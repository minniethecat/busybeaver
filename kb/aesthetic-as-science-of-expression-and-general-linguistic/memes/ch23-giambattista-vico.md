[023-001] "ÆSTHETIC" OF BAUMGARTEN

[023-002] *[[cartesian-revolution|Cartesianism]] and imagination.*

[023-003] The obscure world of wit, taste, imagination, feeling and the *je ne

[023-004] sais quoi* was not selected for examination or even, so to speak,

[023-005] included in the picture of Cartesian philosophy. The [[france|French]] philosopher

[023-006] abhorred imagination, the outcome, according to him, of the agitation

[023-007] of the animal spirits: and though not utterly condemning poetry, he

[023-008] allowed it to exist only in so far as it was guided by intellect, that

[023-009] being the sole faculty able to save men from the caprices of the *folle

[023-010] du logis.* He tolerated it, but that was all; and went so far as not to

[023-011] deny it anything "*qu'un philosophe lui puisse permettre sans offenser

[023-012] sa conscience.*"[[1]](280320132259829826_54618-h-11.htm.html#Footnote_1_138) It has been observed that the æsthetic parallel

[023-013] with Cartesian intellectualism is to be found in Boileau,[[2]](280320132259829826_54618-h-11.htm.html#Footnote_2_139) slave to

[023-014] rigid *raison* ("*Mais nous que la raison à ses règles engage ...*")

[023-015] and enthusiastic partisan of allegory. We have already had occasion

[023-016] to draw attention to the diatribe of Malebranche against imagination.

[023-017] The mathematical spirit fostered in France by Descartes forbade all

[023-018] possibility of a serious consideration of poetry and art. The Italian

[023-019] Antonio Conti, living in that country and witness of the literary

[023-020] disputes raging around him, thus describes the French critics (La

[023-021] Motte, Fontenelle and their followers): "*Ils ont introduit dans les

[023-022] belles lettres l'esprit et la méthode de M. Descartes; et ils jugent

[023-023] de la poésie et de l'éloquence indépendamment des qualités sensibles.

[023-024] De là vient aussi qu'ils confondent le progrès de la philosophie avec

[023-025] celui des arts. Les modernes, dit l'Abbé Terrasson, sont plus grands

[023-026] géomètres que les anciens: donc ils sont plus grands orateurs et plus

[023-027] grands poètes.*"[[3]](280320132259829826_54618-h-11.htm.html#Footnote_3_140) The fight against this mathematical spirit in the

[023-028] matters of art and feeling was still going on in France in the day of

[023-029] the encyclopædists; the din of the battle was heard in [[italy|Italy]], as is

[023-030] shown by the writings of Bettinelli and others. At the time when Du Bos

[023-031] published his daring book there was a counsellor in the parliament of

[023-032] Bordeaux, Jean-Jacques Bel by name, who composed a dissertation (1726)

[023-033] against the doctrine that feeling should be the judge of art.[[4]](280320132259829826_54618-h-11.htm.html#Footnote_4_141)

[023-034] *Crousaz and André.*

[023-035] Cartesianism was incapable of an Æsthetic of imagination. The *Traité

[023-036] du beau* by the eclectic Cartesian J. P. de Crousaz (1715), maintained

[023-037] the dependence of beauty not upon pleasure or feeling, matters about

[023-038] which there can be no difference of opinion, but upon that which can

[023-039] be *approved* and therefore reduced to ideas. He enumerates five such

[023-040] ideas: variety, unity, regularity, order and proportion, observing,

[023-041] "*La variété tempérée par l'unité, la régularité, l'ordre et la

[023-042] proportion, ne sont pas assurément des chimères; elles ne sont pas

[023-043] du ressort de la fantaisie, ce n'est pas le caprice qui en décide*":

[023-044] for him, that is to say, they were real qualities of [[beauty|the beautiful]]

[023-045] founded in nature and truth. He discovered similar characteristics of

[023-046] the beautiful in the individual beauties of the sciences (geometry,

[023-047] algebra, astronomy, physics, history), of virtue, eloquence and

[023-048] religion, finding in each the qualities laid down above.[[5]](280320132259829826_54618-h-11.htm.html#Footnote_5_142) Another

[023-049] Cartesian, the Jesuit André (1742),[[6]](280320132259829826_54618-h-11.htm.html#Footnote_6_143) distinguished between an

[023-050] *essential* beauty, independent of every institution, human and even

[023-051] divine; a *natural* beauty, independent of the opinions of mankind;

[023-052] and, lastly, a beauty to a certain extent *arbitrary* and of human

[023-053] invention: the first composed of regularity, order, proportion and

[023-054] symmetry (here André relied upon Plato and also as an afterthought

[023-055] brought in St. Augustine's definition): the second having its principal

[023-056] measure in the light which generates colours (as a good Cartesian,

[023-057] he took full advantage of Newton's discoveries): the third belonging

[023-058] to fashion and convention, but never at liberty to violate essential

[023-059] beauty. Each of these three forms of beauty was subdivided into

[023-060] *sensible* beauty pertaining to bodies, and *intelligible* beauty of

[023-061] soul.

[023-062] *The [[england|English]]: Locke, Shaftesbury, Hutcheson and the

[023-063] Scottish School.*

[023-064] Like Descartes in France, Locke in England (1690) is an

[023-065] intellectualist, and recognizes no form of spiritual elaboration save

[023-066] reflexion on the senses. None the less he takes over from contemporary

[023-067] literature the distinction between wit and judgement; according to him

[023-068] the former combines ideas with pleasing variety, discovering their

[023-069] similarities and relations and thus grouping them into beautiful

[023-070] pictures which divert and strike the imagination: the latter (judgement

[023-071] or intellect) seeks dissimilarities, guided by the criterion of truth.

[023-072] "The mind, without looking any further, rests satisfied with the

[023-073] agreeableness of the picture, and the gaiety of the [[imagination|fancy]]; and it is

[023-074] a kind of an affront to go about to examine it by the severe rules of

[023-075] truth and good reason; whereby it appears that it consists in something

[023-076] that is not perfectly conformable to them."[[7]](280320132259829826_54618-h-11.htm.html#Footnote_7_144) England produced

[023-077] philosophers who developed an abstract and transcendent Æsthetic,

[023-078] but one more tinged with sensationalism than that of the French

[023-079] Cartesians. Shaftesbury (1709) raises taste to a sense or instinct for

[023-080] the beautiful; a sense of order and proportion identical with moral

[023-081] sense and, with its preconceptions or presentations, anticipating the

[023-082] recognition of reason. Bodies, spirits, God are the three degrees of

[023-083] beauty.[[8]](280320132259829826_54618-h-11.htm.html#Footnote_8_145) Lineal descendant of Shaftesbury was Francis Hutcheson

[023-084] (1723), who succeeded in popularizing the idea of an inward sense of

[023-085] beauty as something intermediate between sense and reason, and adapted

[023-086] to distinguish unity in variety, concord in the manifold, the true,

[023-087] the beautiful and the good in their substantial identity. Hutcheson

[023-088] maintains that from this sense springs the pleasure we take in art,

[023-089] in imitation and in the likeness between copy and original: the last

[023-090] a relative, as distinct from an absolute, beauty.[[9]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_9_146) This view on the

[023-091] whole predominated in England during the eighteenth century and was

[023-092] adopted by Adam Smith as well as by Reid, head of the Scottish school.

[023-093] *Leibniz. Petites perceptions and confused knowledge.*

[023-094] Much more thoroughly and with much greater philosophical vigour Leibniz

[023-095] opened the door to that crowd of psychic facts from which Cartesianism

[023-096] recoiled in horror. In his conception of the real, governed by the law

[023-097] of continuity (*natura non facit saltus*), presenting an uninterrupted

[023-098] scale of existence from the lowest beings to God, imagination, taste,

[023-099] wit and the like found ample room for shelter. The facts now called

[023-100] æsthetic were identified by Leibniz with Descartes' *confused*

[023-101] cognition, which might be *clear* without being *distinct*: scholastic

[023-102] terms borrowed, it would appear, from Duns Scotus, whose works were

[023-103] reprinted and widely read in the seventeenth century.[[10]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_10_147)

[023-104] In his *De cognitione, veritate et ideis* (1684), after dividing

[023-105] *cognitio* into *obscura vel clara,* the *clara* into *confusa vel

[023-106] distincta,* and the *distincta* into *adaequata vel inadaequata,* Leibniz

[023-107] remarks that while painters and other artists are able to judge works

[023-108] of art very fairly they can give no reason for their decisions, and

[023-109] if questioned as to the reason of their condemnation of any work

[023-110] of art, they reply it lacks a *je ne sais quoi*: ("*at iudicii sui

[023-111] rationem reddere saepe non posse, et quaerenti dicere, se in re, quae

[023-112] displicet, desiderare nescio quid*").[[11]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_11_148) They do possess, in fact,

[023-113] clear cognition, but confused and not distinct; what we should call

[023-114] to-day imaginative, not *ratiocinative,* consciousness: and indeed the

[023-115] latter does not exist in the case of art. There are things impossible

[023-116] to define: "*on ne les fait connaître que par des exemples, et, au

[023-117] reste, il faut dire que c'est un je ne sais quoi, jusqu'à ce qu'on

[023-118] en déchiffre la contexture*."[[12]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_12_149) But these *perceptions confuses

[023-119] ou sentiments* have "*plus grande efficacité que l'on ne pense: ce

[023-120] sont elles qui forment ce je ne sais quoi, ces goûts, ces images

[023-121] des qualités des sens.*"[[13]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_13_150) Whence it appears plainly that in his

[023-122] discussion of these perceptions Leibniz reposes upon the æsthetic

[023-123] theories we discussed in the preceding chapter; indeed at one point[[14]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_14_151)

[023-124] he mentions Bouhours' book.

[023-125] *Intellectualism of Leibniz*

[023-126] It might seem that by according *claritas* and denying *distinctio*

[023-127] to æsthetic facts Leibniz recognized that their peculiar character is

[023-128] neither sensuous nor intellectual. He might seem to have distinguished

[023-129] them by their "*claritas*" from pleasure or sense-motions, and from

[023-130] intellect by their lack of "*distinctio.*" But the "*lex continui*"

[023-131] and the Leibnitian intellectualism forbid this interpretation. In this

[023-132] case obscurity and clarity are quantitative degrees of one single

[023-133] consciousness, distinct or intellectual, towards which both converge

[023-134] and with which in the extreme case they unite.

[023-135] To admit that artists judge with confused perceptions, clear but not

[023-136] distinct, does not involve denying that these perceptions may be

[023-137] capable of being connected and verified by intellectual consciousness.

[023-138] The self-same object that is confusedly though clearly recognized by

[023-139] imagination is recognized clearly and distinctly by the intellect;

[023-140] which amounts to saying that a [[art|work of art]] may be perfected by being

[023-141] determined by thought. In the very terminology adopted by Leibniz, who

[023-142] represents sense and imagination as obscure and confused, there is a

[023-143] tinge of contempt, as well as the suggestion of a single form of all

[023-144] cognition. This will help us to understand Leibniz' definition of music

[023-145] as "*exercitium arithmeticae occultum nescientis se numerare animi.*"

[023-146] Elsewhere he says: "*Le but principal de l'histoire, aussi bien que

[023-147] de la poésie, doit être d'enseigner la prudence et la vertu par des

[023-148] exemples, et puis de montrer le vice d'une manière qui en donne

[023-149] l'aversion et qui porte ou serve à l'éviter.*"[[15]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_15_152)

[023-150] The "*claritas*" attributed to æsthetic fact is not specifically

[023-151] different from, but rather a partial anticipation of, the

[023-152] "*distinctio*" of intellect. Undoubtedly this distinction of degree

[023-153] marks a great advance: but careful analysis shows that Leibniz does

[023-154] not differ fundamentally from those who, by inventing the new words

[023-155] and empirical distinctions examined above, called attention to the

[023-156] peculiarities of æsthetic facts.

[023-157] *Speculation on language.*

[023-158] We find the same invincible intellectualism in the speculations on

[023-159] language greatly in vogue at the time. When critics of the [[renaissance|Renaissance]]

[023-160] and sixteenth century tried to rise above merely empirical and

[023-161] practical grammar and strove to reduce grammatical science to a

[023-162] systematic form, they fell into logicism and described grammatical

[023-163] forms by such terms as pleonastic, improper, metaphorical or elliptic.

[023-164] Thus Julius Cæsar Scaliger (1540); thus, too, the most learned of

[023-165] all, Francisco Sanchez (Sanctius or Sanzio), called Brocense, who, in

[023-166] his *Minerva* (1587), asserts that names are attached to things by

[023-167] reason, exclusive of interjections which are not parts of speech but

[023-168] merely sounds expressive of joy or sorrow; he denies the existence of

[023-169] heterogeneous and heteroclitic words, and works out a system of syntax

[023-170] by means of four figures of construction, proclaiming the principle

[023-171] "*doctrinam supplendi esse valde necessarium,*" that is to say, that

[023-172] grammatical diversities must be explained as ellipsis, abbreviation

[023-173] or omission with reference to the typical logical form.[[16]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_16_153) Gaspare

[023-174] Scioppio follows him exactly, abusing the old grammar with his

[023-175] accustomed violence and crying up the "Sanctian" method, at that time

[023-176] still almost unknown, in his *Grammatica philosophica* (1628).[[17]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_17_154)

[023-177] Amongst critics of the seventeenth century, Jacopo Perizonio must not

[023-178] be forgotten; he wrote a commentary on Sanchez' book (1687). Amongst

[023-179] recognized philosophers who studied the philosophy of grammar and

[023-180] noted the merits and defects of various tongues, we find Bacon.[[18]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_18_155) In

[023-181] 1660 Claude Lancelot and Arnauld brought out the *Grammaire générale

[023-182] et raisonnée de Port-Royal,* a work applying the intellectualism

[023-183] of Descartes rigorously to grammatical forms, and dominated by the

[023-184] doctrine of the artificial nature of language. Locke and Leibniz both

[023-185] speculated about language,[[19]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_19_156) but neither succeeded in creating a

[023-186] fresh point of view, although the latter did much to provoke inquiry

[023-187] into the historical origin of languages. All his life Leibniz cherished

[023-188] the notion of a universal language and of an "*ars characteristica

[023-189] universalis*" as a combination likely to result in great scientific

[023-190] discoveries: prior to him, Wilkins had fostered the same hope, nor

[023-191] indeed, in spite of its utter absurdity, is it even yet wholly extinct.

[023-192] *C. Wolff.*

[023-193] In order to correct the æsthetic ideas of Leibniz it was necessary

[023-194] to alter the very foundations of his system, the Cartesianism upon

[023-195] which it rested. This could not be undertaken by disciples of

[023-196] his own personal school, in whom we notice rather an increase of

[023-197] intellectualism. Giving scholastic form to the brilliant observations

[023-198] of the master, Johann Christian Wolff's system began with the theory

[023-199] of knowledge conceived as an "organon" or instrument, followed by

[023-200] systems of natural law, ethics and politics, together constituting

[023-201] the "organon" of practical activity: the remainder was theology

[023-202] and metaphysics, or pneumatology and physics (doctrine of the soul

[023-203] and doctrine of phenomenal nature). Although Wolff distinguishes a

[023-204] productive imagination, ruled by the principle of sufficient reason,

[023-205] from the merely associative and chaotic,[[20]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_20_157) yet a science of

[023-206] imagination considered as a new theoretical value could find no niche

[023-207] in his schematism. Knowledge of a lower order, as such, belonged to

[023-208] Pneumatology and was incapable of possessing its own "organon": at most

[023-209] it could be brought under the organon already existing, which corrected

[023-210] and transcended it by means of logical knowledge in the same way in

[023-211] which Ethics treats the "*facilitas appetitiva inferior.*" As in France

[023-212] the poetics of Boileau corresponded with the philosophy of Descartes,

[023-213] so in [[germany|Germany]] the rationalistic poetics of Gottsched[[21]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_21_158) reflect the

[023-214] Cartesian-Leibnitian theories of Wolff (1729).

[023-215] *Demand for an organon of inferior knowledge.*

[023-216] It was no doubt dimly seen that even in the inferior faculties some

[023-217] distinction was operative between perfect and imperfect, value and

[023-218] non-value. A passage in a book (1725) by the Leibnitian Bülffinger

[023-219] has often been quoted where he says: "*Vellem existerent qui circa

[023-220] facultatem sentiendi, imaginandi, attendendi, abstrahendi et memoriam

[023-221] praestarent quod bonus ille Aristoteles, adeo hodie omnibus sordens,

[023-222] praestitit circa intellectum: hoc est ut in artis formant redigerent

[023-223] quicquid ad illas in suo usu dirigendas et iuvandas pertinet et

[023-224] conducid, quem ad modum Aristoteles in Organo logicam sive facultatem

[023-225] demonstrandi redegit in ordinem.*"[[22]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_22_159) But on reading the extract in

[023-226] its context one recognizes at once that the desired organon would have

[023-227] been merely a series of recipes for strengthening the memory, educating

[023-228] the attention, and so forth: a technique, in a word, not an æsthetic.

[023-229] Similar ideas had been spread in Italy by Trevisano (1708), who, by

[023-230] declaring that the senses might be educated through the mind, asserted

[023-231] the possibility of an *art of feeling* which should "endow manners

[023-232] with prudence and judgement with [[taste|good taste]]."[[23]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_23_160) We notice, moreover,

[023-233] that in his day Bülffinger was counted a depreciator of poetry, so

[023-234] much so that a tract against him was written in order to show that

[023-235] "poetry does not diminish the faculty of clear conception."[[24]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_24_161) Bodmer

[023-236] and Breitinger were ready "to deduce all the parts of eloquence with

[023-237] mathematical precision" (1727), and the latter sketched a Logic of

[023-238] the Imagination (1740) to which he would have assigned the study of

[023-239] similitudes and metaphors; even had he carried out his project, it

[023-240] is difficult to see how it could have differed materially, from a

[023-241] philosophic point of view, from the treatises on the subject written by

[023-242] the Italian rhetoricians of the seventeenth century.

[023-243] *[[alexander-baumgarten|Alexander Baumgarten]]: his "Æsthetic."*

[023-244] These discussions and experiments filled the boyhood and helped to

[023-245] form the intellect of young Alexander Gottlieb Baumgarten of Berlin,

[023-246] a follower of the philosophy of Wolff and, at the same time, student

[023-247] and teacher of Latin rhetoric and poetry; these studies led him to

[023-248] reconsider the problem and search for some method by which the precepts

[023-249] of rhetoricians could be reduced to a rigorous philosophical system.

[023-250] On taking his doctor's degree in September 1735, when twenty-one years

[023-251] old, he published a thesis *Meditationes philosophicae de nonnullis ad

[023-252] poēma pertinentibus*:[[25]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_25_162) in which the word "Æsthetic" appears for the

[023-253] first time as name of a special science.[[26]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_26_163) Baumgarten always remained

[023-254] much attached to his youthful discovery, and in 1742 when called to

[023-255] teach at the university of Frankfort-on-the-Oder, and again in 1749,

[023-256] he gave by request a course of lectures on Æsthetic (*quaedam consilia

[023-257] dirigendarum facultatum inferiorum novam per acroasin exposuit*).[[27]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_27_164)

[023-258] In 1750 he printed a voluminous treatise wherein the word "Æsthetic"

[023-259] attained the honours of a title-page;[[28]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_28_165) in 1758 he published a more

[023-260] slender second part: illness and finally death in 1762 prevented him

[023-261] from completing the work.

[023-262] *Æsthetic as science of sensory consciousness.*

[023-263] What was Æsthetic to Baumgarten? Its objects are sensible facts

[023-264] (ασθητά), carefully distinguished by the ancients from mental objects

[023-265] (νοητά);[[29]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_29_166) hence it becomes *scientia cognitionis sensitivae, theoria

[023-266] liberalium artium, gnoseologia inferior, ars pulcre cogitandi, ars

[023-267] analogi rationis*[[30]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_30_167) [[rhetoric|Rhetoric]] and Poetry constitute two special

[023-268] and interdependent disciplines which are entrusted by Æsthetic with

[023-269] the distinction between the various styles in literature and other

[023-270] small differences,[[31]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_31_168) for the laws she herself investigates are

[023-271] diffused throughout all the arts like guiding-stars for these various

[023-272] subsidiary arts (*quasi cynosura quaedam specialium*)[[32]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_32_169) and must be

[023-273] extracted not from isolated cases only, or from incomplete induction

[023-274] empirically, but from the totality of facts (*falsa regula peior est

[023-275] quant nulla.*)[[33]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_33_170) Nor must Æsthetic be confounded with Psychology,

[023-276] which furnishes its presuppositions only; an independent science, it

[023-277] gives the norm of sensitive cognition (*sensitive quid cognoscendi*)

[023-278] and deals with "*perfectio cognitionis sensitivae, qua talis,*" which is

[023-279] beauty (*pulcritudo)*, just as the opposite, imperfection, is ugliness

[023-280] (*deformitas*)[[34]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_34_171) From the beauty of sensitive cognition (*pulcritudo

[023-281] cognitionis*) we must exclude the beauty of objects and matter

[023-282] (*pulcritudo obiectorum et materiae*) with which it is often confused

[023-283] owing to habits of language, since it is easy to show that ugly things

[023-284] may be thought of in a beautiful manner and beautiful things in an

[023-285] ugly manner (*quacum ob receptam rei significationem saepe sed male

[023-286] confunditur; possunt turpia pulcre cogitare ut talia, et pulcriora

[023-287] turpiter*).[[35]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_35_172) Poetical representations are confused or imaginative:

[023-288] distinctness, that is intellect, is not poetical. The greater the

[023-289] determination, the greater the poetry; individuals "*omnimode

[023-290] determinata*" are highly poetical; poetical also are images or

[023-291] phantasms as well as all that appertains to the senses.[[36]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_36_173) That which

[023-292] judges sensible or imaginary presentations is taste, or "*indicium

[023-293] sensuum.*" These, in brief, are the truths displayed by Baumgarten in

[023-294] his *Meditationes* and, with many distinctions and examples, in his

[023-295] *Æsthetic.*[[37]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_37_174)

[023-296] *Cricisism of judgements based on Baumgarten.*

[023-297] Nearly all German critics[[38]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_38_175) are of opinion that from his own

[023-298] conception of Æsthetic as the science of sensitive cognition Baumgarten

[023-299] should have evolved a species of inductive Logic. But he can be cleared

[023-300] of this accusation: a better philosopher, perhaps, than his critics,

[023-301] he held that an inductive Logic must always be intellectual, since

[023-302] it leads to abstractions and the formation of concepts. The relation

[023-303] existing between "*cognitio confusa*" and the poetical and artistic

[023-304] facts which belong to the realm of taste had been shown before his

[023-305] day, by Leibniz: neither he nor Wolff nor any other of their school

[023-306] ever dreamed of transforming a treatment of the "*cognitio confusa*"

[023-307] or "*petites perceptions*" into an inductive Logic. On the other hand,

[023-308] as a kind of compensation, these critics attribute to Baumgarten

[023-309] a merit he cannot claim, at least to the extent implied by their

[023-310] praises. According to them, he effected a revolution by converting[[39]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_39_176)

[023-311] Leibniz' differences of degree or quantitative distinctions into a

[023-312] specific difference, and turning confused knowledge into something no

[023-313] longer negative but positive[[40]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_40_177) by attributing a "*perfectio*" to

[023-314] sensitive cognition *qua talis*; and by thus destroying the unity of

[023-315] the Leibnitian monad and breaking up the law of continuity, founded

[023-316] the science of Æsthetic. Had he really accomplished such a giant

[023-317] stride, his claim to the title of "father of Æsthetic" would have

[023-318] been placed beyond question. But, in order to win this appellation,

[023-319] Baumgarten ought to have been successful in unravelling all those

[023-320] contradictions in which he was involved no less than Leibniz and all

[023-321] intellectualists. It is not enough to posit a "*perfectio*"; even

[023-322] Leibniz did that when he attributed *claritas* to confused cognition,

[023-323] which, when devoid of clearness, remains obscure, that is to say,

[023-324] imperfect. It was imperative that this perfection "*qua talis*" should

[023-325] be upheld against the "*lex continui,*" and kept uncontaminated by any

[023-326] intellectualistic admixture. Otherwise he was bound to fall back into

[023-327] the pathless labyrinth of the "probable" which is and is not false,

[023-328] of the wit which is and is not intellect, of the taste which is and

[023-329] is not intellectual judgement, of the imagination and feeling which

[023-330] are and are not sensibility and material pleasure. And in that case,

[023-331] notwithstanding the new name: notwithstanding (as we freely admit) the

[023-332] greater insistence than that of Leibniz upon the sensible nature of

[023-333] poetry, Æsthetic, as a science, would not have been born.

[023-334] *Intellectualism of Baumgarten.*

[023-335] Now Baumgarten overcame none of the obstacles above mentioned.

[023-336] Unprejudiced and continued study of his works forces one to this

[023-337] conclusion. Already in his *Meditationes* he does not seem able to

[023-338] distinguish clearly between imagination and intellect, confused and

[023-339] distinct cognition. The law of continuity leads him to set up a scale

[023-340] of more and less: amongst cognitions, the obscure are less poetical

[023-341] than the confused; the distinct are not poetical, but even those

[023-342] of the higher kinds (that is the distinct and intellectual) are to

[023-343] a certain extent poetical in proportion as they are lower in their

[023-344] nature; compound concepts are more poetical than simple; those of

[023-345] larger comprehension are "*extensive clariores.*"[[41]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_41_178) In the *Æsthetic*

[023-346] Baumgarten expounds his thought more fully and thereby exposes its

[023-347] defects. If the introduction of the book leads one to believe that he

[023-348] sees æsthetic truth to consist in consciousness of the individual,

[023-349] the belief is shattered by the explanations which follow. As a good

[023-350] objectivist he asserts that truth in the metaphysical sense has its

[023-351] counterpart in the soul, namely, subjective truth, logical truth in a

[023-352] wide sense, or æsthetico-logical.[[42]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_42_179) And the complete truth lies not

[023-353] in the genus or species, but in the individual. The genus is true,

[023-354] the species more true, the individual most true.[[43]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_43_180) Formal logical

[023-355] truth is acquired "*cum iactura,*" by jettisoning much great material

[023-356] perfection: "*quid enim est abstractio, si iactura non est?*"[[44]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_44_181)

[023-357] So much being granted, logical truth differs from æsthetic in this:

[023-358] metaphysical or objective truth is presented now to the intellect,

[023-359] when it is logical truth in a narrow sense; now to the analogy of

[023-360] reason and the lower cognitive faculties, when it is æsthetic;[[45]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_45_182)

[023-361] a lesser truth in exchange for the greater which man is not always

[023-362] able to attain, thanks to the "*malum metaphysicum.*"[[46]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_46_183) Thus moral

[023-363] truths are comprehended in one fashion by a comic poet, in another

[023-364] by a moral philosopher; an eclipse is described in one way by an

[023-365] astronomer and in another by a shepherd speaking to his friends or

[023-366] his sweetheart.[[47]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_47_184) Universals even are accessible, in part at least,

[023-367] to the inferior faculty.[[48]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_48_185) Take the case of two philosophers, a

[023-368] dogmatic and a sceptic, arguing, with an æsthete listening to them. If

[023-369] the arguments of either party are so balanced that the hearer cannot

[023-370] determine which is true and which false, this appearance is to him

[023-371] æsthetic truth: if one adversary succeed in overbearing the other

[023-372] so that one argument is shown clearly to be wrong, the error just

[023-373] revealed is likewise æsthetic[[49]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_49_186) falsity. Truths strictly æsthetic are

[023-374] (and this is the decisive point) those which appear neither entirely

[023-375] true nor entirely false: probable truths. "*Talia autem de quibus

[023-376] non complete quidem certi sumus, neque tamen falsitatem aliquam in

[023-377] iisdem appercipimus, sunt verisimilia. Est ergo veritas æsthetica,

[023-378] a potiori dicta verisimilitudo, ille veritatis gradus, qui, etiamsi

[023-379] non evectus sit ad completam certitudinem, tamen nihil contineat

[023-380] falsitatis observabilis.*"[[50]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_50_187) And especially the immediate sequel:

[023-381] "*Cujus habent spectator es auditor esve intra animum quum vident

[023-382] audiuntve, quasdam anticipationes, quod plerumque fit, quod fieri

[023-383] solet, quod in opinione positum est, quod habet ad haec in se quandam

[023-384] similitudinem, sive id falsum (logice et latissime), sive verum

[023-385] sit (logice et strictissime), quod non sit facile a nostris sensibus

[023-386] abhorrens: hoc illud* est εἰκός *et verisimile quod, Aristotele et

[023-387] Cicerone assentiente, sectetur æstheticus.*"[[51]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_51_188) The probable embraces

[023-388] that which is true and certain to the intellect and the senses, that

[023-389] which is certain to the senses but not to the intellect, that which

[023-390] is probable logically and æsthetically, or logically improbable but

[023-391] æsthetically probable, or, finally, æsthetically improbable but on the

[023-392] whole probable or that whose improbability is not evident.[[52]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_52_189) So we

[023-393] reach the admission of the impossible and absurd, the *αδύνατον* and

[023-394] *ἄτοπον* of Aristotle.

[023-395] If after reading these paragraphs, highly important as revealing the

[023-396] true thought of Baumgarten, we turn once more to the Introduction to

[023-397] his work, we notice at once his commonplace and erroneous conception of

[023-398] the poetic faculty. To a friend who suggested that there was no need

[023-399] for him to concern himself with confused or inferior consciousness both

[023-400] because "*confusio mater erroris*" and because "*facilitate inferior

[023-401] es, caro, debellandae potius sunt quam excitandae et confirmandae,*"

[023-402] Baumgarten replied that confusion is a condition wherein to find truth:

[023-403] that nature makes no sudden leap from obscurity to clarity: that

[023-404] noonday light is reached from night time through the dawn (*ex node per

[023-405] auroram meridies*): that in the case of the inferior faculties a guide,

[023-406] not a tyrant, is needed (*imperium in facilitates inferiores poscitur,

[023-407] non tyrannis*).[[53]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_53_190) This is still the attitude of Leibniz, Trevisano

[023-408] and Bülffinger. Baumgarten is terrified lest he should be accused of

[023-409] treating subjects unworthy a philosopher. "*Quousque tandem*" (says he

[023-410] to himself), "dost thou, professor of theoretic and moral philosophy,

[023-411] dare to praise lies and mixtures of true and false as though they were

[023-412] noble works?"[[54]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_54_191) And if there is one thing above all others from

[023-413] which he is anxious to guard himself it is sensualism, unbridled and

[023-414] non-moralized. The sensitive perfection of Cartesianism and Wolffianism

[023-415] was liable to be confused with simple pleasure, with the feeling of

[023-416] the perfection of our organism:[[55]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_55_192) but Baumgarten falls into no such

[023-417] confusion. When in 1745 one Quistorp combated his æsthetic theory by

[023-418] saying that if poetry consisted in sensuous perfection it was a thing

[023-419] hurtful to men, Baumgarten answered disdainfully that he did not expect

[023-420] he should ever find time to reply to a critic of such calibre as to

[023-421] mistake his "*oratio perfecta sensitiva*" for an "*oratio perfecte*

[023-422] (that is *omnino) sensitiva.*"[[56]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_56_193)

[023-423] *New names and old meanings.*

[023-424] Save in its title and its first definitions Baumgarten's *Æsthetic* is

[023-425] covered with the mould of antiquity and commonplace. We have seen that

[023-426] he refers back to Aristotle and Cicero for the first principles of his

[023-427] science; in another instance he attaches his Æsthetic to the Rhetoric

[023-428] of antiquity, quoting the truth enunciated by Zeno the Stoic, "*esse

[023-429] duo cogitandi genera, alterum perpetuum et latius, quod Rhetorices sit,

[023-430] alterum concisum et contractius, quod Dialectices,*" and identifying

[023-431] the former with the æsthetic horizon, the latter with the logical.[[57]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_57_194)

[023-432] In his *Meditationes* he rests upon Scaliger and Vossius;[[58]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_58_195) of

[023-433] modern writers beside the philosophers (Leibniz, Wolff, Bülffinger)

[023-434] he quotes Gottsched, Arnold,[[59]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_59_196) Werenfels, Breitinger[[60]](280320132259829826_54618-h-12.htm.html#Footnote_60_197); by means

[023-435] of these latter he is able to make acquaintance with discussions upon

[023-436] taste and imagination, even without direct acquaintance with Addison

[023-437] and Du Bos, as well as the Italians, whose writings had immense vogue

[023-438] in Germany in his day, and with whom his resemblances leap to the

[023-439] eye. Baumgarten always feels himself to be in perfect accord with his

[023-440] predecessors; never at variance with them. He never felt himself to

[023-441] be a revolutionary; and though some have been revolutionaries without

[023-442] knowing it, Baumgarten was not one of them. Baumgarten's works are but

[023-443] another presentation of the problem of Æsthetic still clamouring for

[023-444] solution in a voice so much the stronger as it uttered a commonplace:

[023-445] he proclaims a new science and presents it in conventional scholastic

[023-446] form; the babe about to be born receives the name of Æsthetic by

[023-447] premature baptism at his hands: and the name remains. But the new name

[023-448] is devoid of new matter; the philosophical armour covers no muscular

[023-449] body. Our good Baumgarten, full of ardour and conviction, and often

[023-450] curiously brisk and vivacious in his scholastic Latinism, is a most

[023-451] sympathetic and attractive figure in the history of Æsthetic: of the

[023-452] science in formation, that is to say, not of the science brought to

[023-453] completion: of Æsthetic *condenda* not *condita.*
