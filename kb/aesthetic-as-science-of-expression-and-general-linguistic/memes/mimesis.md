# Mimesis

In Croce's history of aesthetic, mimesis (μίμησις) — the doctrine that art consists in the imitation of nature — represents the first and most persistent deviation from the true concept of art as intuition-expression. Originating in Greek antiquity and surviving through the Renaissance into modern aesthetic theory, the mimetic theory, in Croce's judgment, "oscillates between the most opposite poles, between exact imitation and imaginative imitation" (020-512), never resolving the fundamental question of what precisely art imitates and to what end.

## Definition

Mimesis, from the Greek μίμησις, is the theory that the essence of art lies in its capacity to imitate or copy reality — whether external nature, human action, or ideal forms. Croce identifies this doctrine as the earliest attempt to define art philosophically, dating to the pre-Socratic and classical periods of Greek thought (020-073). The problem, as Croce sees it, is that "art, mimesis, a rational or an irrational fact?" — the question itself reveals the conceptual instability at the doctrine's core (020-100). The mimetic theory cannot decide whether imitation is mechanical reproduction or creative transformation, and this ambiguity, far from being a productive tension, renders the concept incapable of distinguishing art from non-art.

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Other Forms of Spirit

Within Croce's fourfold structure of spirit, the mimetic theory represents a *defective* deviation — one that "denies the existence of a special æsthetic and imaginative activity, or, which amounts to the same thing, denies its autonomy" (020-022). By reducing art to the reproduction of something already given in sensory experience, mimesis subordinates the theoretical activity of intuition to the practical activity of craft or the intellectual activity of concept-formation. This is, for Croce, an error that "mutilates the reality of the spirit" by collapsing the distinction between the individual (intuition) and the universal (concept).

### Key Distinctions

Croce distinguishes **mimesis vs. expression** as two fundamentally opposed theories of artistic production. Expression produces the individual image in its concrete wholeness; imitation reproduces something external and pre-existing. The Aristotelian version of mimesis — which Croce acknowledges as more sophisticated than the Platonic — treats imitation as proper to human nature and productive of knowledge (020-436). Yet even Aristotle's formulation falls short: "Aristotle that mimesis, being proper to man by nature, is a rational activity" (020-434) — but the rationality attributed to mimesis is conceptual, not intuitive, and thus misidentifies the cognitive character of art. Croce further distinguishes **mimesis vs. imagination**: the vagueness of the mimetic concept "reached its apex in those" theories that treat imitation as a mere copy, ignoring the productive and synthetic character of imaginative activity (020-529).

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The mimetic theory originates in Greek philosophy, where it received two classic formulations. Plato, in the *Republic*, condemned art as imitation twice removed from the Forms — a critique that, paradoxically, established mimesis as the defining category of artistic activity for the subsequent tradition. Aristotle, in the *Poetics*, rehabilitated mimesis by treating it as a natural and cognitive human activity: poetry imitates not what is but what could be, thereby achieving a kind of universality. The doctrine persisted through the Renaissance, where "mimesis or imitation was *finis medi*" — the end and measure of artistic achievement, codified in the theory of *ut pictura poesis* (021-209). In the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, the doctrine of imitation of nature remained the dominant framework for aesthetic theory, gradually giving way under pressure from theories of expression and genius.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's treatment of mimesis is historical and critical rather than constructive. He does not improve the mimetic theory but diagnoses it as a systematic error arising from the failure to recognize the autonomy of aesthetic intuition. His innovation lies in tracing "the vagueness of the concept of mimesis" through its entire historical career, showing how it generates the very problems it purports to solve (020-529). For Croce, the entire history of aesthetic from antiquity through the eighteenth century is, in large part, the history of the struggle to overcome the limitations of the mimetic paradigm. The concept of expression, which Croce places at the foundation of aesthetic, is the positive doctrine that emerges when the mimetic theory is finally abandoned.

## Criticism and Debate

> "Is art, mimesis, a rational or an irrational fact?" (020-100). Croce's framing of the question reveals his ultimate judgment: the mimetic theory cannot answer this question coherently because it mistakes the nature of the cognitive act that art is.

Critics have argued that Croce's dismissal of mimesis is too sweeping, neglecting the ways in which representational art does, in fact, involve relations of likeness and reference that are not captured by the expression-intuition model. Defenders respond that Croce does not deny that art may refer to reality; he denies only that such reference constitutes the essence of art. The debate remains live in contemporary aesthetics, particularly in discussions of pictorial representation and literary realism.
