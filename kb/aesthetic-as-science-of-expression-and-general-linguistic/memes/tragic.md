# Tragic

In Croce's aesthetic system, the tragic is not a fundamental category of art but one of the "empirical varieties" that arise from the content of particular expressions.

## Definition

The tragic appears in Croce's work as one term among many that classify kinds of expression: "tragic, comic, pathetic, sublime" (013-010, 013-022). These are not distinct forms of spirit but names we give to expressions with certain emotional qualities.

The varieties of expression — "the comic, the tragic, the humorous" — spring from each other and "from the comic to the tragic and from the tragic to the comic" there is no fixed boundary (013-111, 013-114, 013-129). This fluidity proves that these are empirical classifications, not philosophical distinctions.

## In Croce's System

### Relation to the Beautiful

The tragic is a modification of the beautiful, not a category alongside it. Croce places it among the "secondary, pure beauty, comic and tragic" that derive from the primary fact of expression (032-433). The tragic character of an expression is a quality of its content, not of its form.

### Key Distinctions

Croce distinguishes the tragic from other modifications:

1. **The tragic vs. the sublime** — The sublime involves an overwhelming positive element; the tragic involves the "violated harmony" that "constitutes the tragic" (032-381, 032-391). The sublime elevates; the tragic moves.

2. **The tragic vs. the comic** — Both are modifications, but the comic arises from a contrast between the real and the ideal, while the tragic arises from the collision of values (032-374, 032-439).

3. **The tragic as a kind of expression** — "the tragic, the humorous, etc." are kinds of expression, but for Croce, there are no kinds of expression; there are only individual expressions (019-065).

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The concept of the tragic has ancient roots. Aristotle spoke "obscurely of a tragical catharsis" in the Poetics (020-183). Later theorists "regarded the tragical catharsis as a purification of the passions" (021-239). The tragic has been central to Western aesthetics from Aristotle through Hegel to Nietzsche.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's treatment of the tragic is characteristically reductive: he dissolves it as an independent category. The tragic is not a distinct aesthetic form but a name we give to expressions that evoke certain feelings. This move is consistent with Croce's broader rejection of literary kinds and genres — there are no kinds of art, only individual works.

## Criticism and Debate

> The varieties of the beautiful — the comic, the sublime, the tragic, the humorous — have been the subject of endless aesthetic debate (032-374).

Critics have argued that Croce's dissolution of the tragic as a category fails to do justice to its philosophical depth. Hegel, Schelling, and Nietzsche all saw the tragic as revealing something essential about the nature of art and existence. Croce's response would be that they were doing philosophy of content, not philosophy of art.
