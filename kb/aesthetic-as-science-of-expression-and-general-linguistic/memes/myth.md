# Myth

In Croce's philosophy of spirit, myth represents a form of imaginative cognition that is intimately related to aesthetic intuition, yet frequently mistaken for conceptual thought. Croce approaches myth primarily through the work of [[giambattista-vico|Vico]] and the German idealists, treating it as a spontaneous, pre-reflective vision of truth rather than deliberate fabrication.

## Definition

For Croce, myth occupies an uncertain frontier between art and philosophy. In his historical survey of aesthetic doctrines, he notes that mythology "is proclaimed a necessary condition of all art: mythology which is not allegory, for in the latter the particular signifies only the universal, while the former is already itself the universal" (028-387). Yet the boundary between mythology and art remains "as shadowy as that between art and philosophy" (028-394). Myth is thus neither pure concept nor pure intuition, but hovers between the two — pregnant with universality while still clothed in imagery.

## In Croce's System

### Myth and Intuition

Within Croce's fourfold division of the spirit (intuition, concept, economic, ethical), myth belongs primarily to the theoretical sphere but resists simple classification. Croce follows [[giambattista-vico|Vico]] in recognizing myth as a primordial form of cognition that arises spontaneously rather than through calculation. Vico's great discovery, Croce affirms, is that myth "is no longer an arbitrary calculated invention, but a spontaneous vision of truth as it presented itself to the spirit of primitive man" (024-141). This aligns myth closely with the intuitive, imaginative activity that Croce identifies as the foundation of all art.

However, Croce is careful to distinguish genuine myth (as poetic intuition) from the philosophical misuse of myth. When discussing [[f-w-j-schelling|Schelling]]'s speculations, he observes that some philosophers treated language and mythology as "products of a pre-human consciousness" presented in fantastic allegory (031-037). This betrays the tendency to treat myth as a kind of proto-philosophy rather than as authentic expression.

### Key Distinctions

The most crucial distinction Croce draws is between myth proper and allegory. In the Romantic idealist tradition he critiques, myth is presented as already containing the universal within the particular, whereas allegory merely uses a particular to signify a universal that remains external to it (028-387). This distinction matters for Croce because it touches on the nature of aesthetic intuition itself: if myth is truly a form of intuition, it cannot be reduced to conceptual meaning.

Croce also distinguishes between myth as a living condition of artistic creation and myth as an artificial, retrospective construction. The Romantic tendency to "exaggerate the functions of imagination and to construct upon it a kind of mythology" (028-225) represents, for Croce, a philosophical error that confuses the spontaneous work of imagination with systematic doctrine.

## Historical Context

### Vico's Revolution

Croce credits [[giambattista-vico|Vico]] with the decisive breakthrough in the understanding of myth. Before Vico, mythology was typically dismissed as deliberate falsehood or primitive superstition. Vico demonstrated that myth arises from the natural poetic activity of the human mind — that early peoples "poetically narrated their own history" (024-134). Croce notes that Vico's entire *Scienza nuova* hinges on this discovery of creative imagination, encompassing observations on "the nature of language, mythology, writing, symbolic figures and so forth" (024-348). Even Vico's errors serve as instructive examples: Croce observes that Vico's historical periods "are rather allegories, the mythological expression of his philosophy of the spirit" (024-365), showing how the philosopher himself occasionally lapsed from genuine historical insight into mythologizing.

### German Idealism and Romanticism

The German idealists, particularly [[f-w-j-schelling|Schelling]] and the Romantics, elevated myth to a central position in aesthetic theory. Croce examines how [[f-w-j-schelling|Schelling]]'s system placed mythology as a necessary mediator between the sensible and the intelligible. In the Romantic conception, mythology was made "a necessary condition of all art" (028-387), and both Hellenic and Christian art were understood as possessing their own mythologies — "Christ; the persons of the Trinity; the Virgin mother of God" (028-392). For Croce, however, this Romantic inflation of mythology into a philosophical system risked blurring the vital distinction between aesthetic intuition and conceptual thought.

### Later Developments

By the late nineteenth century, the study of mythology had become entangled with the emerging science of ethnopsychology. Croce criticizes Wundt for having "attached the study of language, mythology and customs to this non-existent science of Ethnopsychology" (036-458), dismissing the attempt to treat collective myth-making as a scientific object distinct from individual expression.

## Criticism and Debate

Croce's treatment of myth is not without tensions. While he celebrates Vico's insight that myth is spontaneous poetic vision rather than calculated falsehood, his own system tends to absorb myth into aesthetic intuition, leaving little room for myth as a distinct spiritual form. The Romantic tradition that Croce partially endorses and partially critiques had insisted on myth's unique cognitive status — its power to present universal truths in sensuous form. Croce's response is to assimilate that power to the general faculty of intuition, thereby dissolving myth's specificity.

> "Mythology is proclaimed a necessary condition of all art: mythology which is not allegory, for in the latter the particular signifies only the universal, while the former is already itself the universal; which explains how easy it is to allegorize, and how fascinating are such poems as those of Homer which lend themselves to such interpretations. Christian, as well as Hellenic, art has its mythology: Christ; the persons of the Trinity; the Virgin mother of God. The fine between mythology and art is as shadowy as that between art and philosophy." (028-387)

This passage captures the central ambiguity: myth is said to be necessary to art, yet its boundary with art is shadowy. For later readers, this raises the question of whether Croce's aesthetic can adequately account for the narrative and symbolic structures that distinguish myth from pure intuition, or whether myth must instead be understood through concepts of language, culture, and history that Croce's idealist framework subordinates to the solitary act of expression.
