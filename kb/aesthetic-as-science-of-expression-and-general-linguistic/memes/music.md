# Music

In Croce's aesthetics, music is not a separate category of spiritual activity but one of the many sensory channels through which the single act of intuition-expression may be physically communicated (002-125, 002-318).

## Definition

A musical motive — "tender or energetic" — is, for Croce, an aesthetic intuition like any other (002-039). The musician who "finds a musical motive" performs the same spiritual act as the painter who conceives a figure or the poet who shapes a lyrical image — the act is intuition-expression, and the sensory medium is a secondary and physical matter (007-109). Musical notes, like "alphabets" and "musical notes," belong to the physical technique of externalization, not to the aesthetic moment itself (014-210).

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Other Forms of Spirit

Music, like all art, occupies the first moment of the theoretical spirit — aesthetic intuition. The distinction between music and other arts is purely physical: "impressions of sight or hearing" supply the raw material for expression, but the expressive act does not differ in kind (012-016). Croce insists that "the deaf man cannot have the intuition of music" — not because music is a different kind of knowing, but because the deaf man lacks the sensory impressions that intuition transforms (012-018). The execution of a musical work — the performance — belongs to the practical order; "the execution of a piece of music is lost" in time, but this does not differentiate the aesthetic nature of music from that of a poem, whose "text" may survive the loss of oral performance (017-198).

### Key Distinctions

Croce rejects the ancient classification of music among the mathematical arts. The medieval quadrivium placed music alongside arithmetic, geometry, and astronomy as a science of proportion — a view that confuses the physical stimulus with the aesthetic expression. Likewise, the question "poetry and music?" is a pseudo-problem if it presupposes two kinds of art (016-110). There is one art — expression — and many physical media. Musical notes, "colours, statues," and all other physical materials are externalizations, not essences (016-165).

> The great error of musical aesthetics has been to treat the physical medium — sound waves, harmonic ratios, instrumental technique — as if it were constitutive of the aesthetic fact. A musical composition is not its performance, not its score, and not the physical laws of acoustics; it is the intuition-expression that these physical things serve to communicate.

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The Pythagorean tradition linked music to cosmic harmony and mathematical proportion, treating the art as a branch of physics. Aristotle's Poetics "determines the use of music in education," subordinating the aesthetic to the practical and thereby obscuring its theoretical nature (020-182). In the Renaissance, the rediscovery of classical musical theory — "quam fictio rhethorica in musicaque posita" (as rhetorical invention is posited in music) — reinforced the connection between music and rhetoric (021-067). The Baroque doctrine of the affections treated music as a language of the passions, a view Croce considers an advance over mathematical formalism but still a confusion of expression with emotional effect.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's treatment of music is radical in its simplicity: he dissolves the entire tradition of musical aesthetics by relocating the question. Instead of asking "What is music?" (which leads to endless disputes about form and content, programme music, absolute music), he asks "What is the spiritual act performed by the musician?" The answer is intuition-expression, the same act performed by every artist, regardless of medium. The "musician, of the painter, of the prose writer" — all are engaged in the same fundamental activity, differentiated only by the physical means they employ to fix and communicate their expressions (002-330). This eliminates the need for a separate aesthetics of music and integrates the art into the general science of expression.

## Criticism and Debate

> The most persistent criticism of Croce's theory of music is that it neglects the temporal and performative dimensions that are essential to musical experience. A symphony is not merely an intuition externalized in sound; the unfolding of the work in time and the interpretive act of the performer are, for many theorists, internal to the aesthetic fact itself. Croce would rejoin that time and performance belong to the practical order of physical realization, not to the theoretical order of expression.
