[019-001] *Summary of the study.*

[019-002] A glance over the path traversed will show that we have completed

[019-003] the entire programme of our treatise. We have studied the nature of

[019-004] intuitive or [[expression|expressive knowledge]], which is the æsthetic or artistic

[019-005] fact (I. and II.), and described the other form of knowledge, the

[019-006] intellectual, and the successive complications of these forms (III.);

[019-007] it thus became possible for us to criticize all erroneous æsthetic

[019-008] theories arising from the confusion between the various forms and from

[019-009] the illicit transference of the characteristics of one form to another

[019-010] (IV.), noting at the same time the opposite errors to be found in the

[019-011] theory of intellectual knowledge and of historiography (V.). Passing

[019-012] on to examine the relations between the æsthetic activity and the

[019-013] other activities of the spirit, no longer theoretic but practical, we

[019-014] indicated the true character of the practical activity and the place

[019-015] which it occupies in respect to the theoretic activity: hence the

[019-016] criticism of the intrusion into æsthetic theory of practical concepts

[019-017] (VI.); we have distinguished the two forms of the practical activity,

[019-018] as economic and ethical (VII.), reaching the conclusion that there are

[019-019] no other forms of the spirit beyond the four which we have analyzed;

[019-020] hence (VIII.) the criticism of every mystical or imaginative Æsthetic.

[019-021] And since there are no other spiritual forms co-ordinate with these,

[019-022] so there are no original subdivisions of the four established, and

[019-023] in particular of Æsthetic. From this arises the impossibility of

[019-024] classes of expressions and the criticism of [[rhetoric|Rhetoric]], that is, of

[019-025] ornate expression distinct from simple expression, and of other similar

[019-026] distinctions and subdistinctions (IX.) But by the law of the unity of

[019-027] the spirit, the æsthetic fact is also a practical fact, and as such,

[019-028] occasions pleasure and pain. This led us to study f the feelings of

[019-029] value in general, and those of æsthetic value or of [[beauty|the beautiful]] in

[019-030] particular (X.), to criticize æsthetic hedonism in all its various

[019-031] manifestations and complications (XI.), and to expel from the system

[019-032] of Æsthetic the long series of psychological concepts which had been

[019-033] introduced into it (XII.). Proceeding from æsthetic production to the

[019-034] facts of reproduction, we began by investigating the external fixing

[019-035] of the æsthetic expression, for the purpose of reproduction. This

[019-036] is called the physically beautiful, whether natural or artificial

[019-037] (XIII.). We derived from this distinction the criticism of the errors

[019-038] which arise from confounding the physical with the æsthetic side of

[019-039] facts (XIV.). We determined the meaning of [[technique|artistic technique]], or that

[019-040] technique which is at the service of reproduction, thus criticizing

[019-041] the divisions, limits and classifications of the individual arts,

[019-042] and establishing the relations of art, economy and morality (XV.).

[019-043] Since the existence of physical objects does not suffice to stimulate

[019-044] æsthetic reproduction to the full, and since, in order to obtain it,

[019-045] we must recall the conditions in which the stimulus first operated,

[019-046] we have also studied the function of historical erudition, directed

[019-047] toward re-establishing the communication between the imagination and

[019-048] the works of the past, and to serve as the basis of the æsthetic

[019-049] judgement (XVI.). We have concluded our treatise by showing how the

[019-050] reproduction thus obtained is afterwards elaborated by the categories

[019-051] of thought, that is to say, by an examination of the method of literary

[019-052] and artistic history (XVII.).

[019-053] The æsthetic fact has in short been considered both in itself and in

[019-054] its relations with the other spiritual activities, with the feelings

[019-055] of pleasure and pain, with what are called physical facts, with

[019-056] memory and with historical treatment. It has passed before us as

[019-057] *subject* until it became *object,* that is to say, from the moment

[019-058] of *its birth* until it becomes gradually changed for the spirit into

[019-059] *subject-matter of history.*

[019-060] Our treatise may appear to be somewhat meagre when externally compared

[019-061] with the great volumes usually dedicated to Æsthetic. But it will not

[019-062] seem so when we perceive that those volumes are nine-tenths full of

[019-063] matter that is not pertinent, such as definitions, psychological or

[019-064] metaphysical, of pseudo-æsthetic concepts ([[sublime|the sublime]], the comic,

[019-065] the tragic, the humorous, etc.), or of the exposition of the supposed

[019-066] Zoology, Botany and Mineralogy of Æsthetic, and of universal history

[019-067] æsthetically judged; that the whole history of concrete art and

[019-068] literature has also been dragged into those Æsthetics and generally

[019-069] mangled, and that they contain judgements upon Homer and Dante, Ariosto

[019-070] and Shakespeare, Beethoven and Rossini, Michæl Angelo and Raphæl. When

[019-071] all this has been deducted from them, we flatter ourselves that our

[019-072] treatise will no longer be held to be too meagre, but, on the contrary,

[019-073] far richer than ordinary treatises, which either omit altogether, or

[019-074] hardly touch at all, the greater part of the difficult problems proper

[019-075] to Æsthetic which we have felt it to be our duty to study.

[019-076] *Identity of linguistic and Æsthetic.*

[019-077] But although Æsthetic as science of expression has been studied by us

[019-078] in its every aspect, it remains to justify the sub-title which we have

[019-079] added to the title of our book, *General Linguistic,* to state and make

[019-080] clear the thesis that the science of art and that of language, Æsthetic

[019-081] and Linguistic, conceived as true sciences, are not two distinct

[019-082] things, but one thing only. Not that there is a special Linguistic;

[019-083] but the much-sought-for science of language, general Linguistic, *in

[019-084] so far as what it contains is reducible to philosophy,* is nothing

[019-085] but Æsthetic. Whoever studies general Linguistic, that is to say,

[019-086] philosophical Linguistic, studies æsthetic problems, and *vice versa.

[019-087] Philosophy of language and philosophy of art are the same thing.*

[019-088] Were Linguistic really a *different* science from Æsthetic it would

[019-089] not have for its object expression, which is the essentially æsthetic

[019-090] fact; that is to say, we must deny that language is expression. But an

[019-091] emission of sounds which expresses nothing is not language. Language

[019-092] is sound articulated, circumscribed and organized for the purposes of

[019-093] expression. If, on the other hand, linguistic were a *special* science

[019-094] in respect to Æsthetic, it would necessarily have for its object a

[019-095] *special class* of expressions. But the non-existence of classes of

[019-096] expression is a point which we have already demonstrated.

[019-097] *Æsthetic formulation of linguistic problems. Nature of

[019-098] language.*

[019-099] The problems which Linguistic tries to solve, and the errors in which

[019-100] Linguistic has been and is involved, are the same that respectively

[019-101] occupy and complicate Æsthetic. If it be not always easy, it is on

[019-102] the other hand always possible to reduce the philosophic questions of

[019-103] Linguistic to their æsthetic formula.

[019-104] The disputes themselves as to the nature of the one find their parallel

[019-105] in those as to the nature of the other. Thus it has been disputed

[019-106] whether Linguistic be a historical or a scientific discipline, and,

[019-107] the scientific having been distinguished from the historical, it

[019-108] has been asked whether it belong to the order of the natural or of

[019-109] the psychological sciences, understanding by these latter empirical

[019-110] Psychology as well as the Sciences of the spirit. The same has happened

[019-111] with Æsthetic, which some have looked upon as a natural science

[019-112] (confusing the æsthetic and the physical sense of the word expression).

[019-113] Others have looked upon it as a psychological science (confusing

[019-114] expression in its universality with the empirical classification of

[019-115] expressions). Others again, denying the very possibility of a science

[019-116] of such a subject, change it into a simple collection of historical

[019-117] facts; not one of these attaining to the consciousness of Æsthetic as a

[019-118] science of activity or of value, a science of the spirit.

[019-119] Linguistic expression, or speech, has often seemed to be a fact of

[019-120] *interjection,* which belongs to the so-called physical expressions

[019-121] of the feelings, common alike to men and animals. But it was soon

[019-122] perceived that an abyss yawns between the "Ah!" which is a physical

[019-123] reflex of pain and a word; as also between that "Ah!" of pain and

[019-124] the "Ah!" employed as a word. The theory of the interjection being

[019-125] abandoned (jocosely termed the "Ah! Ah!" theory by German linguists),

[019-126] the theory of *association* or *convention* appeared. This is liable to

[019-127] the same objection which destroyed æsthetic associationism in general:

[019-128] speech is unity, not multiplicity of images, and multiplicity does

[019-129] not explain, but indeed presupposes the expression to be explained. A

[019-130] variant of linguistic associationism is the imitative, that is to say,

[019-131] the theory of *onomatopœia,* which the same philologists deride under

[019-132] the name of the "bow-wow" theory, from the imitation of the dog's bark,

[019-133] which, according to the onomatopœists, must have given its name to

[019-134] the dog.

[019-135] The most usual theory of our times as regards language (apart from mere

[019-136] crass naturalism) consists of a sort of eclecticism or mixture of the

[019-137] various theories to which we have referred. It is assumed that language

[019-138] is in part the product of interjections and in part of onomatopœia and

[019-139] convention. This doctrine is altogether worthy of the philosophical

[019-140] decadence of the second half of the nineteenth century.

[019-141] *Origin of language and its development.*

[019-142] We must here note an error into which have fallen those very

[019-143] philologists who have best discerned the activistic nature of language,

[019-144] when they maintain that although language was *originally a spiritual

[019-145] creation,* yet that it afterwards increased by *association.* But the

[019-146] distinction does not hold, for origin in this case cannot mean anything

[019-147] but nature or character; and if language be spiritual creation, it must

[019-148] always be creation; if it be association, it must have been so from the

[019-149] beginning. The error has arisen from having failed to grasp the general

[019-150] principle of Æsthetic, known to us: that expressions already produced

[019-151] must descend to the rank of impressions before they can give rise to

[019-152] new impressions. When we utter new words we generally transform the

[019-153] old ones, varying or enlarging their meaning; but this process is not

[019-154] associative, it is *creative,* although the creation has for material

[019-155] the impressions, not of the hypothetical primitive man, but of man who

[019-156] has lived long ages in society, and who has, so to say, stored so many

[019-157] things in his psychic organism, and among them so much language.

[019-158] *Relation between Grammar and Logic.*

[019-159] The question of the distinction between the æsthetic and the

[019-160] intellectual fact appears in Linguistic as that of the relations

[019-161] between Grammar and Logic. This problem has been solved in two

[019-162] partially true ways: the *inseparability* and the *separability* of

[019-163] Logic and Grammar. But the complete solution is this: if the logical

[019-164] form be inseparable from the grammatical (æsthetic), the grammatical is

[019-165] separable from the logical.

[019-166] *Grammatical kinds or parts of speech.*

[019-167] If we look at a picture which for instance portrays a man walking on a

[019-168] country road we may say: "This picture represents a fact of *movement,*

[019-169] which, if conceived as voluntary, is called *action*; and since every

[019-170] movement implies a *material object,* and every action a *being* that

[019-171] acts, this picture also represents a *material object* or *being.*

[019-172] But this movement takes place in a definite place, which is a piece

[019-173] of a definite heavenly body (the Earth), and precisely of a piece of

[019-174] it which is called *terra-firma,* and more precisely of a part of it

[019-175] that is wooded and covered with grass, which is called *country,*

[019-176] cut naturally or artificially into a form called *road.* Now, there

[019-177] is only one example of that star, which is called Earth: the earth

[019-178] is an *individual.* But *terra-firma, country, road* are genera or

[019-179] *universals,* because there are other terra-firmas, other countries,

[019-180] other roads." And it would be possible to continue for a while with

[019-181] similar considerations. By substituting a phrase for the picture that

[019-182] we have imagined, for example one to this effect: "Peter is walking on

[019-183] a country road," and by making the same remarks, we obtain the concepts

[019-184] of *verb* (motion or action), of *noun* (material object or agent), of

[019-185] *proper noun,* of *common noun;* and so on.

[019-186] What have we done in both cases? Neither more nor less than submit to

[019-187] logical elaboration what first presented itself only æsthetically;

[019-188] that is to say, we have destroyed the æsthetic for the logical. But

[019-189] since in general Æsthetic error begins when we wish to return from the

[019-190] logical to the æsthetic and ask what is the *expression* of motion,

[019-191] action, matter, being, of the general, of the individual, etc.; so in

[019-192] the case of language, error begins when motion or action are called

[019-193] *verb,* being or matter, *noun* or *substantive,* and when linguistic

[019-194] categories, or *parts of speech,* are made of all these, noun and verb

[019-195] and so on. The theory of the parts of speech is really identical with

[019-196] that of artistic and literary kinds, already criticized in our Æsthetic.

[019-197] It is false to say that the verb or noun is expressed in definite

[019-198] words, truly distinguishable from others. Expression is an indivisible

[019-199] whole. Noun and verb do not exist in it, but are abstractions made by

[019-200] us, destroying the sole linguistic reality, which is the *sentence.*

[019-201] This last is to be understood, not in the way common to grammars, but

[019-202] as an organism expressive of a complete meaning, which includes alike

[019-203] the simplest exclamation and a great poem. This sounds paradoxical, but

[019-204] is nevertheless the simplest truth.

[019-205] And since in Æsthetic the artistic productions of certain peoples

[019-206] have been looked upon as imperfect, owing to the error above

[019-207] mentioned, because the supposed kinds have seemed not yet to have

[019-208] been discriminated, or to be in part wanting; so in Linguistic, the

[019-209] theory of the parts of speech has caused the analogous error of judging

[019-210] languages as *formed* and *unformed,* according to whether there appear

[019-211] in them or no some of those supposed parts of speech; for example, the

[019-212] verb.

[019-213] *The individuality of speech and the classification of

[019-214] languages.*

[019-215] Linguistic also discovered the irreducible individuality of the

[019-216] æsthetic fact, when it affirmed that the word is what is really

[019-217] spoken, and that two truly identical words do not exist. Thus were

[019-218] synonyms and homonyms destroyed, and thus was shown the impossibility

[019-219] of really translating one word into another, from so-called dialect

[019-220] into so-called language, or from the so-called mother-tongue into the

[019-221] so-called foreign tongue.

[019-222] But the attempt to classify languages ill agrees with this just view.

[019-223] Languages have no reality beyond the propositions and complexes of

[019-224] propositions really written and pronounced by given peoples at definite

[019-225] periods; that is to say, they have no existence outside the works of

[019-226] art (whether little or great, oral or written, soon forgotten or long

[019-227] remembered, does not matter) in which they exist concretely. And what

[019-228] is the art of a given people but the whole of its artistic products?

[019-229] What is the character of an art (for example of Greek art or Provençal

[019-230] literature) but the whole physiognomy of those products? And how can

[019-231] such a question be answered, save by narrating in its particulars the

[019-232] history of the literature, that is to say, of the language in its

[019-233] actuality?

[019-234] It may be thought that this argument, although possessing validity

[019-235] as against many of the usual classifications of languages, yet

[019-236] is without any as regards that queen of classifications, the

[019-237] historico-genealogical, that glory of comparative philology. And this

[019-238] it certainly is; but why? Precisely because that historico-genealogical

[019-239] method is not a mere classification. He who writes history does not

[019-240] classify, and the philologists themselves have hastened to say that

[019-241] languages which can be arranged in historical series (those whose

[019-242] series have hitherto been traced) are not distinct and separate species

[019-243] but a single whole of facts in the various phases of its development.

[019-244] *Impossibility of a normative grammar.*

[019-245] Language has sometimes been regarded as a voluntary or arbitrary act.

[019-246] But at others the impossibility of creating language artificially, by

[019-247] an act of will, has been clearly seen. "*Tu, Caesar, civitatem dare

[019-248] potes homini, verbo non potes*" was once said to a Roman Emperor. And

[019-249] the æsthetic (and therefore theoretic as opposed to practical) nature

[019-250] of expression supplies the method of discovering the scientific error

[019-251] which lies in the conception of a (normative) *Grammar*, establishing

[019-252] the rules of correct speech. Good sense has always rebelled against

[019-253] this error. An example of such rebellion is the "So much the worse for

[019-254] grammar" attributed to Monsieur de Voltaire. But the impossibility

[019-255] of a normative grammar is also recognized by those who teach it,

[019-256] when they confess that to write well cannot be learned by rules, that

[019-257] there are no rules without exceptions, and that the study of Grammar

[019-258] should be conducted practically, by reading and examples, which should

[019-259] form the literary taste. The scientific reason of this impossibility

[019-260] lies in the principle that we have demonstrated: that a technique of

[019-261] the theoretical amounts to a contradiction in terms. And what could

[019-262] a (normative) grammar be, but precisely a technique of linguistic

[019-263] expression, that is to say of a theoretic fact?

[019-264] *Didactic organisms.*

[019-265] The case in which Grammar is understood merely as an empirical

[019-266] discipline, that is to say, as a collection of schemes useful for

[019-267] learning languages, without any claim whatever to philosophic truth, is

[019-268] quite different. Even the abstractions of the parts of speech are in

[019-269] this case both admissible and useful. And we must tolerate as merely

[019-270] didascalic many books entitled "Treatises of Linguistic," where we

[019-271] generally find a little of everything, from the description of the

[019-272] vocal apparatus and of the artificial machines (phonographs) which can

[019-273] imitate it, to summaries of the most important I results obtained by

[019-274] Indo-European, Semitic, Coptic, Chinese, or other philologies; from

[019-275] philosophical generalizations as to the origin or nature of language,

[019-276] to advice on format, calligraphy and the arrangement of notes relating

[019-277] to philological work. But this mass of notions, here administered in

[019-278] a fragmentary and incomplete manner about language in its essence,

[019-279] about language as expression, resolves itself into notions of Æsthetic.

[019-280] Nothing exists outside *Æsthetic,* which gives knowledge of the

[019-281] nature of language, and *empirical Grammar,* which is a pedagogic

[019-282] expedient, save the *History of languages* in their living reality,

[019-283] that is to say, the history of concrete literary productions, which is

[019-284] substantially identical with the *History of literature.*

[019-285] *Elementary linguistic facts or roots.*

[019-286] The same error of taking the physical for the æsthetic, from which the

[019-287] search for the *elementary forms* of the beautiful originates, is made

[019-288] by those who go in search of *elementary linguistic facts,* decorating

[019-289] with that name the divisions of the longer series of physical sounds

[019-290] into shorter series. Syllables, vowels and consonants, and the series

[019-291] of syllables called words, all these elements of speech, which give

[019-292] no definite sense when taken alone, must be called not *facts of

[019-293] language,* but mere sounds, or rather sounds abstracted and classified

[019-294] physically.

[019-295] Another error of the same sort is that of *roots,* to which the most

[019-296] distinguished philologists now accord but small value. Having confused

[019-297] physical with linguistic or expressive facts, and considering that the

[019-298] simple precedes the complex in the order of ideas, they necessarily

[019-299] ended by thinking that the smallest physical facts indicated the

[019-300] simplest linguistic facts. Hence the imaginary necessity that the most

[019-301] ancient primitive languages had a monosyllabic character, and that

[019-302] historical research must always lead to the discovery of monosyllabic

[019-303] roots. But (to follow up the imaginary hypothesis) the first expression

[019-304] that the first man conceived may have had not a phonetic but a mimetic

[019-305] physical reflex; may have been externalized not in a sound but in

[019-306] a gesture. And assuming that it was externalized in a sound, there

[019-307] is no reason to suppose that sound to have been monosyllabic rather

[019-308] than polysyllabic. Philologists readily blame their own ignorance and

[019-309] impotence, when they do not always succeed in reducing polysyllabism to

[019-310] monosyllabism, and rely upon the future to accomplish the reduction.

[019-311] But their faith is without foundation, and their blame of themselves is

[019-312] an act of humility arising from an erroneous presumption.

[019-313] For the rest, the limits of syllables, as those of words, are

[019-314] altogether arbitrary, and distinguished somehow or other by empirical

[019-315] use. Primitive speech, or the speech of uneducated man, is a

[019-316] *continuum,* unaccompanied by any consciousness of divisions of the

[019-317] discourse into words or syllables, imaginary beings created by schools.

[019-318] No true law of Linguistic can be founded on such divisions. Proof of

[019-319] this is to be found in the confession of linguists, that there are

[019-320] no truly phonetic laws of the hiatus, of cacophony, of diæresis or

[019-321] synæresis, but merely laws of taste and convenience; that is to say,

[019-322] *æsthetic* laws. And what are laws of *words* which are not at the same

[019-323] time laws of *style*?

[019-324] *Æsthetic judgement and the model language.*

[019-325] Finally, the search for a *model language,* or for a method of reducing

[019-326] linguistic usage to *unity,* arises from the superstition of a

[019-327] rationalistic measure of the beautiful, from that concept which we have

[019-328] called false æsthetic absoluteness. In [[italy|Italy]] we call this the question

[019-329] of the *unity of the language.*

[019-330] Language is perpetual creation. What has been linguistically expressed

[019-331] is not repeated, save by reproduction of what has already been

[019-332] produced. The ever-new impressions give rise to continuous changes

[019-333] of sound and meaning, that is, to ever-new expressions. To seek the

[019-334] model language, then, is to seek the immobility of motion. Everyone

[019-335] speaks and should speak according to the echoes which things arouse

[019-336] in his soul, that is, according to his impressions. It is not without

[019-337] reason that the most convinced supporter of any one of the solutions of

[019-338] the problem of the unity of language (whether by adopting a standard

[019-339] Italian approximating to Latin, or to fourteenth-century usage, or

[019-340] to the Florentine dialect) feels repugnance in applying his theory,

[019-341] when he is speaking to communicate his thoughts and to make himself

[019-342] understood. The reason is that he feels that in substituting the Latin,

[019-343] fourteenth-century Italian, or Florentine word for that of different

[019-344] origin, but which answers to his natural impressions, he would be

[019-345] falsifying the genuine form of truth. He would become a vain listener

[019-346] to himself instead of a speaker, a pedant in place of a serious man, an

[019-347] actor instead of a sincere person. To write according to a theory is

[019-348] not really to write: at the most, it is making *literature.*

[019-349] The question of the unity of language is always reappearing, because,

[019-350] stated as it is, it is insoluble, being based upon a false conception

[019-351] of what language is. Language is not an arsenal of arms already made,

[019-352] and it is not a *vocabulary,* a collection of abstractions, or a

[019-353] cemetery of corpses more or less well embalmed.

[019-354] Our dismissal of the question of the model language, or of the unity of

[019-355] the language, may seem somewhat abrupt, and yet we would not wish to

[019-356] appear otherwise than respectful towards the long line of literary men

[019-357] who have debated this question in Italy for centuries. But those ardent

[019-358] debates were fundamentally concerned with debates of æstheticity, not

[019-359] of æsthetic science, of literature rather than of literary theory, of

[019-360] effective speaking and writing, not of linguistic science. Their error

[019-361] consisted in transforming the manifestation of a need into a scientific

[019-362] thesis, the desirability, for example, of easier mutual understanding

[019-363] among a people divided by dialects into the philosophic demand for

[019-364] a single, ideal language. Such a search was as absurd as that other

[019-365] search for a *universal language,* a language possessing the immobility

[019-366] of the concept and of abstraction. The social need for a better

[019-367] understanding of one another cannot be satisfied save by the spread of

[019-368] education becoming general, by the increase of communications, and by

[019-369] the interchange of thought among men.

[019-370] *Conclusion.*

[019-371] These scattered observations must suffice to show that all the

[019-372] scientific problems of Linguistic are the same as those of Æsthetic,

[019-373] and that the truths and errors of the one are the truths and errors

[019-374] of the other. If Linguistic and Æsthetic appear to be two different

[019-375] sciences, this arises from the fact that people think of the former

[019-376] as grammar, or as a mixture between philosophy and grammar, that

[019-377] is, an arbitrary mnemonic schematism or a pedagogic medley, and not

[019-378] of a rational science and a pure philosophy of speaking. Grammar,

[019-379] or something not unconnected with grammar, also introduces into the

[019-380] mind the prejudice that the reality of language lies in isolated and

[019-381] combinable words, not in living discourse, in the expressive organisms,

[019-382] rationally indivisible.

[019-383] Those linguists or philologists, philosophically endowed, who have

[019-384] penetrated deepest into the problems of language, find themselves (to

[019-385] employ a trite but effective simile) like workmen piercing a tunnel:

[019-386] at a certain point they must hear the voices of their companions, the

[019-387] philosophers of Æsthetic, who have been at work on the other side. At
