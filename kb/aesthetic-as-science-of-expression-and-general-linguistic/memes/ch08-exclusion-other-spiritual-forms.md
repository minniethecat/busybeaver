[008-001] *The two forms of the practical activity.*

[008-002] The double degree of the theoretical activity, æsthetic and logical,

[008-003] has an important parallel in the practical activity, which has not yet

[008-004] been placed in due relief. The practical activity is also divided into

[008-005] a first and second degree, the second implying the first. The first

[008-006] practical degree is the simply *useful* or *economical* activity; the

[008-007] second the *moral* activity.

[008-008] Economy is, as it were, the Æsthetic of practical life; Morality its

[008-009] Logic.

[008-010] *The economically useful.*

[008-011] If this has not been clearly seen by philosophers; if the correct

[008-012] place in the system of the spirit has not been given to the economic

[008-013] activity, if it has been left to wander about in the prolegomena to

[008-014] treatises on political economy, often vague and but little developed,

[008-015] this is due, among other reasons, to the fact that the useful or

[008-016] economic has been confused, sometimes with the concept of the

[008-017] *technical,* sometimes with that of the *egoistical.*

[008-018] *Distinction between the useful and the technical.*

[008-019] *[[technique|Technique]]* is certainly not a special activity of the spirit.

[008-020] Technique is knowledge; or rather, it is knowledge itself in general

[008-021] which takes this name when it serves as basis, as we have seen it does,

[008-022] for practical action. Knowledge which is not followed, or is supposed

[008-023] not to be easily followed by practical action, is called "pure": the

[008-024] same knowledge, if effectively followed by action, is called "applied";

[008-025] if it is supposed that it can be easily followed by a particular

[008-026] action, it is called "applicable" or "technical." This word, then,

[008-027] indicates a *situation* in which knowledge is, or may easily be, not a

[008-028] special form of knowledge. So true is this, that it would be altogether

[008-029] impossible to establish whether a given order of knowledge were,

[008-030] intrinsically, pure or applied. All knowledge, however abstract and

[008-031] philosophical it may be believed to be, may be a guide to practical

[008-032] acts; a theoretical error in the ultimate principles of morality may be

[008-033] reflected and always in some way is reflected in practical life. One

[008-034] can only speak roughly and unscientifically of certain truths as pure

[008-035] and of others as applied.

[008-036] The same knowledge that is called technical may also be called

[008-037] *useful.* But the word "*useful*" in conformity with the criticism of

[008-038] judgements of value made above, is to be understood as used here in

[008-039] a verbal or metaphorical sense. When we say that water is useful for

[008-040] putting out fire, the word "useful" is used in a non-scientific sense.

[008-041] Water thrown on the fire is the cause of its going out: this is the

[008-042] knowledge that serves for basis to the action, let us say, of firemen.

[008-043] There is a link, not of nature, but of simple succession, between the

[008-044] useful action of the person who extinguishes the conflagration and that

[008-045] knowledge. The technique of the effects of the water is the theoretical

[008-046] activity which precedes; the only useful thing is the *action* of the

[008-047] man who extinguishes the fire.

[008-048] *Distinction of the useful from the egoistic.*

[008-049] Some economists identify utility, that is to say, merely economic

[008-050] action or will, with the *egoistic,* that is to say, with what is

[008-051] profitable to the individual, in so far as individual, without regard

[008-052] to and indeed in complete opposition to the moral law. The egoistic is

[008-053] the immoral. In this case Economics would be a very strange science,

[008-054] standing not beside but opposite Ethics, like the devil facing God, or

[008-055] at least like the *advocatus diaboli* in the processes of canonization.

[008-056] Such a conception is altogether inadmissible: the science of immorality

[008-057] is implied in that of morality, as the science of the false is implied

[008-058] in Logic, science of the true, and a science of unsuccessful expression

[008-059] in Æsthetic, science of successful expression. If, then, Economics were

[008-060] the scientific treatment of egoism, it would be a chapter of Ethics,

[008-061] or Ethics itself; because every moral determination implies, at the

[008-062] same time, a negation of its contrary.

[008-063] Further, conscience tells us that to conduct oneself economically

[008-064] is not to conduct oneself egoistically; that even the most morally

[008-065] scrupulous man must conduct himself usefully (economically), if he

[008-066] does not wish to act at hazard and consequently in a manner quite the

[008-067] reverse of moral. If utility were egoism, how could it be the duty of

[008-068] the altruist to behave like an egoist?

[008-069] *Economic will and moral will.*

[008-070] If we are not mistaken, the difficulty is solved in a manner perfectly

[008-071] analogous to that in which is solved the problem of the relations

[008-072] between expression and concept, Æsthetic and Logic.

[008-073] To will economically is to *will an end;* to will morally is to *will

[008-074] the rational end.* But whoever wills and acts morally, cannot but will

[008-075] and act usefully (economically). How could he will the *rational* end,

[008-076] unless he also willed it *as his particular end*?

[008-077] *Pure economicity.*

[008-078] The converse is not true; as it is not true in æsthetic science that

[008-079] the expressive fact must of necessity be linked with the logical fact.

[008-080] It is possible to will economically without willing morally; and it

[008-081] is possible to conduct oneself with perfect economic coherence, while

[008-082] pursuing an end which is objectively irrational (immoral), or, rather,

[008-083] an end which would be held to be so at a higher grade of consciousness.

[008-084] Examples of the economic, without the moral character, are

[008-085] Machiavelli's hero Cæsar Borgia, or the Iago of Shakespeare. Who can

[008-086] help admiring their strength of will, although their activity is only

[008-087] economic, and is developed in opposition to what we hold moral? Who

[008-088] can help admiring the Ser Ciappelletto of Boccaccio, who pursues and

[008-089] realizes his ideal of the perfect rascal even on his death-bed, making

[008-090] the petty and timid little thieves who are present at his burlesque

[008-091] confession exclaim: "What manner of man is this, whose perversity

[008-092] neither age, nor infirmity, nor the fear of death which he sees at

[008-093] hand, nor the fear of God before whose judgement-seat he must stand in

[008-094] a little while, have been able to remove, nor to make him wish to die

[008-095] otherwise than as he has lived?"

[008-096] *The economic side of morality.*

[008-097] The moral man unites with the pertinacity and fearlessness of a Cæsar

[008-098] Borgia, of an Iago, or of a Ser Ciappelletto, the good will of the

[008-099] saint or of the hero. Or, rather, good will would not be will, and

[008-100] consequently not good, if it did not possess, in addition to the side

[008-101] which makes it *good,* also that which makes it *will.* So a logical

[008-102] thought which does not succeed in expressing itself is not thought, but

[008-103] at the most a confused presentiment of a thought beyond yet to come.

[008-104] It is not correct, then, to conceive of the amoral man as also

[008-105] anti-economical, or to make of morality an element of coherence in

[008-106] the acts of life, and therefore of economicity. Nothing prevents us

[008-107] from conceiving (an hypothesis which is verified at least during

[008-108] certain periods and moments, if not during whole lifetimes) a man

[008-109] altogether without moral conscience. In a man thus organized, what

[008-110] for us is immorality is not so for him, because it is not felt as

[008-111] such. The consciousness of the contradiction between what is desired

[008-112] as a rational end and what is pursued egoistically cannot arise

[008-113] in him. This contradiction is anti-economicity. Immoral conduct

[008-114] becomes also anti-economical only in the man who possesses moral

[008-115] conscience. The moral remorse which is the indication of this, is

[008-116] also economical remorse; that is to say, sorrow at not having known

[008-117] how to will completely and to attain that moral ideal which was

[008-118] willed at first, instead of allowing himself to be led astray by the

[008-119] passions. *Video meliora proboque, deteriora sequor.* The *video*

[008-120] and the *probo* are here an initial *volo* immediately contradicted

[008-121] and overthrown. In the man without moral sense, we must admit a

[008-122] remorse that is *merely economic*; like that of a thief or of an

[008-123] assassin who, when on the point of robbing or of assassinating should

[008-124] abstain from doing so, not owing to a conversion of his being, but

[008-125] to nervousness and bewilderment, or even to a momentary awakening of

[008-126] moral consciousness. When he has come back to himself, such a thief

[008-127] or assassin will regret and be ashamed of his incoherence; his remorse

[008-128] will not be due to having done wrong, but to *not* having done wrong;

[008-129] it is therefore economic, not moral, since the latter is excluded by

[008-130] hypothesis. But since a lively moral consciousness is generally found

[008-131] among the majority of men and its total absence is a rare and perhaps

[008-132] non-existent monstrosity, it may be admitted that morality, in general,

[008-133] coincides with economicity in the conduct of life.

[008-134] *The merely economic and the error of the morally

[008-135] indifferent.*

[008-136] There need be no fear lest the parallelism that we support should

[008-137] introduce afresh into science the category of the *morally

[008-138] indifferent,* of that which is in truth action and volition, but is

[008-139] neither moral nor immoral; the category in short of the *licit* and

[008-140] of the *permissible,* which has always been the cause or reflexion of

[008-141] ethical corruption, as was the case with Jesuitical morality, which it

[008-142] dominated. It remains quite certain that indifferent moral actions do

[008-143] not exist, because moral activity pervades and must pervade every least

[008-144] volitional movement of man. But far from upsetting the established

[008-145] parallelism, this confirms it. Are there by any chance intuitions which

[008-146] science and the intellect do not pervade and analyse, resolving them

[008-147] into universal concepts, or changing them into historical affirmations?

[008-148] We have already seen that true science, philosophy, knows no external

[008-149] limits which bar its way, as happens with the so-called natural

[008-150] sciences. Science and morality entirely dominate, the one the æsthetic

[008-151] intuitions, the other the economic volitions of man, although neither

[008-152] of them can appear in the concrete, save the one in the intuitive, the

[008-153] other in the economic form.

[008-154] *Criticism of utilitarianism and the reform of Ethics and of

[008-155] Economics.*

[008-156] This combined identity and difference of the useful and the moral, of

[008-157] the economic and the ethical, explains the success at the present time

[008-158] and formerly of the utilitarian theory of Ethics. Indeed it is easy to

[008-159] discover and to illustrate a utilitarian side in every moral action; as

[008-160] it is easy to reveal the æsthetic side in every logical proposition.

[008-161] The criticism of ethical utilitarianism cannot begin by denying this

[008-162] truth and seeking out absurd and non-existent examples of *useless*

[008-163] moral actions. It must admit the utilitarian side and explain it as

[008-164] the concrete form of morality, which consists in this, that it is

[008-165] *inside* this form. Utilitarians do not see this inside. This is not

[008-166] the place for the fuller development that such ideas deserve. Ethics

[008-167] and Economics cannot however fail to be gainers (as we have said of

[008-168] Logic and Æsthetic) by a more exact determination of the relations that

[008-169] exist between them. Economic science is now rising to the activistical

[008-170] concept of the useful, as it attempts to surpass the mathematical

[008-171] phase in which it is still entangled; a phase which was in its turn

[008-172] a progress when it superseded historicism, or the confusion of the

[008-173] theoretical with the historical, and destroyed a number of capricious

[008-174] distinctions and false economic theories. With this conception, it will

[008-175] be easy on the one hand to absorb and to verify the semi-philosophical

[008-176] theories of so-called pure economics, and on the other, by the

[008-177] introduction of successive complications and additions, to effect a

[008-178] transition from the philosophical to the empirical or naturalistic

[008-179] method and thus to embrace the particular theories expounded in the

[008-180] so-called political or national economy of the schools.

[008-181] *Phenomenon and noumenon in practical activity.*

[008-182] As æsthetic intuition knows the phenomenon or nature, and the

[008-183] philosophic concept the noumenon or spirit; so the economic activity

[008-184] wills the phenomenon or nature, and the moral activity the noumenon or

[008-185] spirit. *The spirit which wills itself,* its true self, the universal

[008-186] which is in the empirical and finite spirit: that is the formula which

[008-187] perhaps defines the essence of morality with the least impropriety.

[008-188] This will for the true self is *absolute freedom.*
