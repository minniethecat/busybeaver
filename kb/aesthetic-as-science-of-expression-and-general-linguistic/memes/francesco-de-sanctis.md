# Francesco De Sanctis

Francesco De Sanctis (1817–1883) was the most important Italian literary critic of the 19th century. Croce dedicated a chapter to him — "Francesco De Sanctis" (ch33-francesco-de-sanctis) — and regarded him as a major figure in the Italian aesthetic tradition.

## Biography

Born in southern Italy, De Sanctis studied in Naples, participated in the 1848 revolution, was imprisoned, and later became Minister of Public Instruction in the newly unified Italian state. His major work, *History of Italian Literature* (1870–1871), combined Hegelian philosophical framework with passionate critical judgment.

## Contributions to Æsthetic

- **Synthesis of Hegel and Vico**: De Sanctis combined Hegel's dialectical historicism with Vico's insight into the autonomy of poetic imagination, creating a critical method that was both philosophical and literary
- **Form as Living Content**: De Sanctis developed a concept of form as the living, individualized expression of content — not an external container but the very life of the work. This anticipates Croce's identity of intuition and expression
- **The Critical Method**: De Sanctis practiced an immanent criticism that judges works by their internal coherence and expressive success, not by external rules or moral standards — a method Croce explicitly endorses and systematizes
