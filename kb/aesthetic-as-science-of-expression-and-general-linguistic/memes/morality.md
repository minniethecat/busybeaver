# Morality

In Croce's philosophy of spirit, morality (*la moralità*) is the fourth and culminating moment of spiritual activity: the volition of the universal. It completes the fourfold division of spirit — aesthetic (intuition of the individual), logical (concept of the universal), economic (volition of the individual), and moral (volition of the universal). Understanding morality's place in this system is essential to grasping why art, for Croce, is autonomous and not subordinate to ethical judgment.

## Definition

Morality, for Croce, is practical activity directed toward the universal — the will that seeks not individual advantage but the good as such. It is "the concrete form of morality, which consists in this, that the individual subordinates his particular ends to the universal end" (008-164). Croce departs from Kantian formalism: the moral will is not obedience to an abstract law but the concrete willing of the universal within particular circumstances.

Croce observes that morality "entirely dominate[s]" alongside science in rational life (008-150), but insists on their distinctness. Science belongs to the logical sphere; morality to the practical. Neither can absorb the other without damage to the integrity of spirit.

## In Croce's System

### The Economic Side of Morality

Croce devotes sustained attention to "the economic side of morality" (008-096). The economic moment — the volition of the individual — is neither identical with nor opposed to the moral moment. Morality does not negate individual interest but universalizes it: the moral act is that in which individual and universal will coincide. Croce rejects the view that morality is essentially anti-economical; rather, it is "an element of coherence in the individual's economic activity" (008-105).

### Morality and Immorality

Croce argues that "the science of immorality is implied in that of morality, as the science of the false is implied in that of the true" (008-057). Immorality is not a separate spiritual category but the privation of morality — the failure to will the universal. What appears as immorality in one context may not be so in another, for "what for us is immorality is not so for him, because it is not in contradiction with his conscience" (008-110). Croce's historicism here introduces a note of relativism that sits uneasily with his universalism.

### Distinction from the Aesthetic

Morality and art must remain distinct; each is autonomous. Croce repeatedly attacks the confusion of art with moral edification. Art is intuition-expression and is neither moral nor immoral — it is pre-moral. The aesthetic sphere "without the three preceding" (009-008) — that is, without the logical, economic, and moral — would be incomplete as spirit, yet each sphere has its own irreplaceable character. Art cannot be judged by moral criteria without category error.

## Historical Context

The relationship between art and morality is one of the oldest problems in aesthetics. **Plato** banished the poets from the ideal republic on moral grounds; **Aristotle** defended tragedy on the basis of *catharsis* — a quasi-moral effect. In the modern period, **Kant** sought to isolate the aesthetic in the "disinterested" pleasure of taste, while **Schiller** treated art as the education of moral freedom. Croce inherits the Kantian autonomy of the aesthetic but embeds it within a comprehensive philosophy of spirit in which morality has its own distinct and irreducible place. **Jesuitical morality** (008-141), which Croce critiques for reducing moral judgment to casuistic calculation, exemplifies the confusion of the economic and moral spheres that Croce's system is designed to prevent.

## Criticism and Debate

> Croce's insistence on the autonomy of art from morality has been both celebrated and criticized. Celebrated as a liberation of art from didacticism and censorship; criticized as an abdication of art's ethical responsibility. The tension is especially acute in the evaluation of art with manifestly immoral content or intention. Croce would reply that the aesthetic judgment concerns expressive success alone; moral judgment is a separate and legitimate activity, but it must not masquerade as aesthetic judgment. Whether this separation can be maintained in practice remains contested.
