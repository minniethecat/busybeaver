# Scepticism (Aesthetic)

Scepticism enters Croce's aesthetics not as a position he endorses but as a diagnostic symptom. When aesthetic theory goes wrong — when it applies inappropriate criteria to the judgment of art or history — the result is scepticism: the conclusion that genuine knowledge of aesthetic objects is impossible. Croce's response is not to refute scepticism directly but to show that it arises from a category mistake: the demand that aesthetic and historical knowledge conform to the demonstrative standards of science. Once that demand is recognized as illegitimate, scepticism dissolves.

## Definition

Croce distinguishes two forms of scepticism relevant to aesthetics. The first is **historical scepticism**, which arises from the observation that historical knowledge rests on memory and testimony rather than on analysis and demonstration. "Intellectualistic scepticism finds it easy to deny the certainty of any history, for the certainty of history differs from that of science. It is the certainty of memory and of authority, not that of analysis and demonstration" (004-231). The sceptic points out that witnesses may be mistaken, documents may be forged, and memories may be unreliable — and concludes that historical knowledge is therefore impossible.

The second form is what Croce calls the scepticism that follows from the collapse of intellectualist systems. When a philosophical theory that identifies art with a particular conceptual content or metaphysical thesis proves untenable, the disappointed theorist often concludes not that the theory was wrong but that aesthetic truth is unreachable. "If this principle fails, truth is no longer possible; scepticism only remains" (030-234). This is scepticism as intellectual fatigue — the surrender of philosophical inquiry rather than its correction.

## In Croce's System

### Scepticism as Category Mistake

Croce's treatment of scepticism is continuous with his broader argument about the autonomy of aesthetic knowledge. The sceptic demands that aesthetic judgment meet standards appropriate to logical demonstration; when it cannot — because aesthetic intuition is not logical deduction — the sceptic concludes that it is not knowledge at all. But this is to mistake the nature of the activity under examination. The conviction of the art critic, like that of the historian, is "the undemonstrable conviction of the juryman, who has heard the witnesses, listened attentively to the case, and prayed Heaven to inspire him" (004-231). That such conviction is sometimes mistaken does not make it worthless; errors are "in a negligible minority compared with the occasions when he grasps the truth" (004-231).

The aesthetic sceptic, in Croce's analysis, is typically an intellectualist who has discovered that intellectualism fails. The positivist who reduces art to physiological stimulus, the metaphysician who reduces it to an Idea — both, when their reductions are exposed as inadequate, may conclude that art itself is cognitively empty. Croce's countermove is to insist that the failure lies in the reduction, not in the object: art is cognitively robust, but its cognitive character is intuitive, not logical.

### Key Distinctions

The primary distinction is between **scepticism and Croce's philosophy of spirit**. Scepticism treats the limits of demonstrative knowledge as the limits of knowledge itself; Croce treats them as the boundaries between distinct forms of spiritual activity. History and art are not failed sciences; they are successful expressions of intuition and memory, forms of knowledge that answer to their own criteria of adequacy.

A second distinction opposes **historical to intellectualistic scepticism**. Historical scepticism doubts the reliability of testimony; intellectualistic scepticism doubts the possibility of non-demonstrative knowledge. Croce regards the former as a methodological caution — useful when it sharpens critical scrutiny of sources — and the latter as a philosophical error — harmful when it denies the cognitive status of art and history altogether.

## Historical Context

The problem of aesthetic scepticism has ancient roots. Plato's banishment of the poets from the Republic is arguably the founding act of aesthetic scepticism in the Western tradition: because poetry does not yield knowledge in the strict (logical) sense, it is cognitively worthless. Croce's aesthetics is, from one angle, a sustained reply to this Platonic challenge: it argues that intuition is a primary and autonomous form of knowledge, neither reducible to nor inferior to conceptual thought.

In the nineteenth century, scepticism received new impetus from the rise of positivism, whose "hotch-potch of materialistic and idealistic, mechanical and theological theories" was "crowned with scepticism and agnosticism" (036-006). The positivist program of reducing all knowledge to natural science inevitably produced, as its own shadow, the conviction that whatever could not be so reduced was cognitively empty. Croce's system is designed to break this dialectic: by distinguishing the forms of spirit, it grants each its own cognitive dignity.
