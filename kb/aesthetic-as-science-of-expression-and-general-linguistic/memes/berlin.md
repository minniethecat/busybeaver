# Berlin

**Berlin** is the capital city of Brandenburg and the historic center of Prussian intellectual life. In the history of aesthetics, Berlin holds a foundational position as the birthplace of the concept of aesthetics as a distinct philosophical discipline, through the work of [[Alexander Gottlieb Baumgarten]], and as the site where the German idealist and romantic traditions in aesthetic theory reached their fullest expression through figures such as [[J. G. Herder]] and [[Friedrich Schleiermacher]].

## Significance in Æsthetic History

Berlin's role in the history of aesthetics is defined by two epochal developments: the formal invention of aesthetics as a named science by Baumgarten, and the later flourishing of aesthetic theory at the University of Berlin under Schleiermacher and other luminaries of German idealism.

### Birth of the Scientific Discipline of Æsthetic

The word "Æsthetic" appears for the first time as the name of a special philosophical science in the work of Alexander Gottlieb Baumgarten of Berlin. Raised in the city, Baumgarten was formed by the intellectual currents of early 18th-century Berlin, where discussions of rhetoric, poetry, and the nature of sensory cognition were widespread. These discussions "filled the boyhood and helped to form the intellect of young Alexander Gottlieb Baumgarten of Berlin, a follower of the philosophy of Wolff and, at the same time, student and teacher of Latin rhetoric and poetry" (023-244–023-245). His 1735 treatise *Meditationes philosophicae de nonnullis ad poema pertinentibus* introduced the term "Æsthetic" for the first time, inaugurating a new philosophical discipline (023-245–023-248).

### Herder and the Berlin Academy

J. G. Herder, who studied and worked in Berlin, became the most passionate defender of Baumgarten's legacy. Herder had an unbounded admiration for the "old Berlin master," whom he called "the Aristotle of his day" (025-434–025-435). Herder defended Baumgarten warmly against those who described him as "a stupid and obtuse syllogizer" (025-435–025-436). Herder's own thesis on the origin of language (1770), which explored the relation between language and aesthetic intuition, was chosen for discussion by the Berlin Academy (025-587–025-589).

> Herder's passionate cry at the state of German aesthetic theory captures the spirit of the Berlin school: "O Æsthetic! the most fertile, the most beautiful and by far the most novel of all abstract sciences, in what cavern of the Muses is sleeping the youth of my philosophic nation destined to bring thee to perfection?" (025-440–025-442)

### Schleiermacher's Berlin Lectures

Friedrich Schleiermacher, perhaps the most original aesthetic thinker of German romanticism, delivered his first lectures on aesthetics at Berlin University in 1819 (030-057). His aesthetic doctrine is "amongst the least known although it is perhaps the most noteworthy of the day" (030-053–030-055).

> Schleiermacher delivered his first lectures on Æsthetic at Berlin University in 1819, and from that date he began to study the subject seriously with a view to writing a book on it. He repeated his lectures on two occasions, in 1825 and 1832-1833; but his death, which occurred in the following year, prevented him from carrying out his plan, and all we know of his thoughts on Æsthetic comes from his lectures, as collected by his pupils and published in 1842. (030-057–030-063)

## Associated Figures

### Alexander Gottlieb Baumgarten (1714–1762)

Baumgarten is the central figure of Berlin's contribution to aesthetics. As a young man in Berlin, he was immersed in the rhetorical and poetical traditions that would lead him to seek a rigorous philosophical method for the precepts of rhetoric (023-244–023-247). His *Æsthetica* (1750–1758) established the framework that would dominate German aesthetic theory for generations. Baumgarten remained attached to his youthful discovery throughout his career, continuing to refine his system when called to teach at Frankfurt-on-the-Oder in 1742 and again in 1749 (023-245).

### Johann Gottfried Herder (1744–1803)

Herder was the most important disciple of Baumgarten and the principal conduit through which Berlin aesthetic ideas entered the broader stream of German thought. His insistence that language is the "reflexion or consciousness (*Besonnenheit*) of man" (025-589–025-590) laid the groundwork for the expressivist theory of language that would influence the [[Hegelian Movement]] and later thinkers. Herder's *Kritische Wälder* (1769) developed a systematic critique of contemporary aesthetic theory while defending the Berlin master's legacy.

### Friedrich Schleiermacher (1768–1834)

Schleiermacher's aesthetic theory, delivered in his Berlin lectures, represents a synthesis of Kantian and romantic elements. He developed a distinctive view of art as the expression of the individual's relation to the infinite, anticipating later currents in hermeneutics and philosophical anthropology. His lectures, collected and published by his pupils in 1842, were attacked by the Herbartian historian Zimmermann with "real ferocity" (030-065), yet they remain a crucial document of Berlin's golden age of aesthetic philosophy.

### Other Berlin Figures

Berlin was also connected to several other major figures and schools in the history of aesthetics. The Berlin Academy's prize competitions attracted submissions from leading thinkers across Europe. The city's publishing houses—active from the 1780s through the 1840s—produced landmark works such as Eschenburg's *Entwurf einer Theorie und Literatur der schönen Wissenschaften* (Berlin, 1783), Herder's *Sämmtliche Werke* (Berlin, 1878), Heydenreich's *System der Ästhetik*, and the standard edition of Schleiermacher's lectures on aesthetics (030-365) (025-708, 025-715, 025-749, 028-621).

## Institutions and Academies

### Berlin Academy of Sciences (Preussische Akademie der Wissenschaften)

The Berlin Academy was the institutional heart of Prussian intellectual life during the Enlightenment. It was the Academy that selected Herder's essay on the origin of language as a prize topic in 1770 (025-589), thereby stimulating one of the most important debates in 18th-century philosophy of language and aesthetics. The Academy's proceedings circulated throughout Europe and helped establish Berlin as a center of philosophical inquiry competitive with Paris and London.

### Berlin University (Universitat zu Berlin)

Founded in 1810, the University of Berlin became the leading institution for the study of philosophy in the German-speaking world during the 19th century. Schleiermacher's lectures on aesthetics at Berlin University in 1819 (030-057) inaugurated a tradition of systematic aesthetic education that attracted students from across Europe. The university's philosophical faculty included giants of German idealism and provided the institutional framework for the publications of the [[Leibnitian School]], [[Herbartian School]], and [[Hegelian Movement]].
