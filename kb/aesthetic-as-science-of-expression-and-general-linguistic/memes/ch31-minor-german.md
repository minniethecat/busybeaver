[031-001] *Progress of Linguistic.*

[031-002] [[About]] the time when [[friedrich-schleiermacher|Schleiermacher]] was meditating on the nature of the

[031-003] æsthetic fact, a movement of thought was gaining ground in [[germany|Germany]]

[031-004] which, tending as it did to overthrow the old concept of language,

[031-005] might have proved a powerful aid to æsthetic science. But not only had

[031-006] the æsthetic specialists—if we may so call them—no notion of the

[031-007] existence of this movement, the new philosophers of language never

[031-008] brought their ideas into relation with the æsthetic problem, and

[031-009] their discoveries languished imprisoned within the narrow scope of

[031-010] Linguistic, condemned to sterility.

[031-011] *Linguistic speculation at the beginning of the nineteenth

[031-012] century*.

[031-013] Research into the relations between thought and speech, between the

[031-014] unity of logic and the multiplicity of languages, had been promoted,

[031-015] like many other things, by the *Critique of Pure Reason*: the earliest

[031-016] Kantians often tried to apply the Kantian categories of intuition

[031-017] (space and time) and of intellect to language. The first to make the

[031-018] attempt was Roth[[1]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_1_473) in 1795; the same who wrote an essay twenty years

[031-019] later on *Pure Linguistic.* Many other noteworthy books on this subject

[031-020] appeared in quick succession: those of Vater, Bernhardi, Reinbeck and

[031-021] Koch were published one after another in the first ten years of the

[031-022] nineteenth century. In all these treatises the dominating subject is

[031-023] the difference between language and languages; between the universal

[031-024] language, corresponding with Logic, and concrete, historical languages

[031-025] disturbed by feeling and imagination or whatever other name was applied

[031-026] to the psychological element of differentiation. Vater distinguishes a

[031-027] general Linguistic (*all gemeine Sprachlehre*), constructed *a priori*

[031-028] by means of the analysis of the concepts contained in the judgement,

[031-029] from a comparative Linguistic (*vergleichende Sprachlehre*) which

[031-030] attempts by means of induction to reach probable laws through the

[031-031] study of a number of languages. Bemhardi considers language to be an

[031-032] "allegory of intellect" and distinguishes it as functioning either

[031-033] as the organ of poetry or that of science. Reinbeck speaks of an

[031-034] Æsthetic Grammar and a Logical. Koch, more energetic than the others,

[031-035] asserts positively that the character of language is "*non ad Logices

[031-036] sed ad Psychologiae rationem revocanda.*"[[2]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_2_474) Some few philosophers

[031-037] speculated on language and mythology: for example [[f-w-j-schelling|Schelling]] considered

[031-038] them to be the products of a pre-human consciousness (*vormenschliche

[031-039] Bewusstsein,*) presenting them, in a fantastic allegory, as diabolic

[031-040] suggestions which precipitate the ego from the infinite to the

[031-041] finite.[[3]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_3_475)

[031-042] *Wilhelm von Humboldt. Relics of intellectualism.*

[031-043] Even the famous philologist, Wilhelm von Humboldt, was unable to detach

[031-044] himself entirely from the prejudice of the substantial identity and

[031-045] the purely historical, accidental diversity between logical thought

[031-046] and language. His celebrated dissertation, *On the Diversity of

[031-047] Structure of Human Languages* (1836),[[4]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_4_476) is based on the notion of a

[031-048] perfect language split up and distributed amongst particular tongues

[031-049] according to the linguistic or intellectual capacity of various

[031-050] nations. "For," says he, "since disposition towards speech is general

[031-051] in mankind, and all men must necessarily carry within themselves the

[031-052] key to the comprehension of all languages, it follows that the form

[031-053] of all languages must be substantially equal and all must attain the

[031-054] same general end. Diversity can exist solely in the means, and within

[031-055] the bounds permitted by the attainment of the end." Yet this same

[031-056] diversity becomes a real divergence not only in sounds, but in the

[031-057] use of sound made by the linguistic sense in respect to the form of

[031-058] language, or rather, in respect to its own idea of the form of the

[031-059] determinate language. "Languages being merely formal, the operation

[031-060] of the linguistic sense by itself should produce mere uniformity;

[031-061] the linguistic sense must exact from every tongue the same right and

[031-062] legitimate construction that is found in one of them. In practice,

[031-063] however, the facts are quite otherwise, partly owing to the reaction of

[031-064] sounds, and partly by reason of the individual aspect assumed by the

[031-065] same internal meaning in phenomenal reality." Linguistic force "cannot

[031-066] maintain its equality everywhere or show the same intensity, vivacity

[031-067] or regularity; it cannot be supported by an exactly equal tendency

[031-068] towards the symbolic treatment of thought or by exactly equal pleasure

[031-069] in richness and harmony of sound." These, then, are the causes which

[031-070] produce in human languages that diversity which manifests itself in

[031-071] every branch of the civilization of nations. But reflexion on languages

[031-072] "ought to reveal to us a form which of all possible forms best fits the

[031-073] purpose of language" and approaches most closely to its ideal; and "the

[031-074] merits and defects of existing languages must be estimated by their

[031-075] nearness or remoteness from this form." Humboldt finds the nearest

[031-076] approximation to such an ideal in the Sanskrit tongues, which can

[031-077] therefore be used as a standard of comparison. Setting Chinese apart in

[031-078] a class by itself, he proceeds to the division of the possible forms of

[031-079] language into inflective, agglutinative and incorporative; types which

[031-080] are found combined in various proportions in every real language.[[5]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_5_477) He

[031-081] also inaugurated the division of languages into inferior and superior,

[031-082] unformed and formed, according to the way in which verbs are treated.

[031-083] He was never able to rid himself of a second prejudice connected with

[031-084] the first, namely that language exists as something objective outside

[031-085] the talking man, unattached and independent, and waking up when needed

[031-086] for use.

[031-087] *Language an activity. Internal form.*

[031-088] But Humboldt opposes Humboldt: amongst the old dross we detect the

[031-089] brilliant gleams of a wholly new concept of language. Certainly his

[031-090] work is for this very reason not always free from contradictions

[031-091] and from a kind of hesitation and awkwardness which appear

[031-092] characteristically in his literary style and make it at times laboured

[031-093] and obscure. The new man in Humboldt criticizes the old man when he

[031-094] says, "Languages must be considered not as dead products but as an act

[031-095] of production. ... Language in its reality is something continually

[031-096] changing and passing away. Even its preservation in writing is

[031-097] incomplete, a kind of mummification: it is always necessary to render

[031-098] the living speech sensible. Language is not a work, *ergon,* but

[031-099] an activity, *energeia.* ... It is an eternally repeated effort of

[031-100] the spirit in order to make articulated tones capable of expressing

[031-101] thought." Language is the act of speaking. "True and proper language

[031-102] consists in the very act of producing it by means of connected

[031-103] utterance; that is the only thing that must be thought of as the

[031-104] starting-point or the truth in any inquiry which aims at penetrating

[031-105] into the living essence of language. Division into words and rules is a

[031-106] lifeless artifice of scientific analysis."[[6]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_6_478) Language is not a thing

[031-107] arising out of the need of external communication; on the contrary, it

[031-108] springs from the wholly internal thirst for knowledge and the struggle

[031-109] to reach an intuition of things." From its earliest commencement it is

[031-110] entirely human, and extends without intention to all objects of sensory

[031-111] perception or internal elaboration.... Words gush spontaneously from

[031-112] the breast without constraint or intention: there is no nomad tribe in

[031-113] any desert without its songs. Taken as a zoological species, man is a

[031-114] singing animal which connects its thoughts with its utterances."[[7]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_7_479)

[031-115] The new man leads Humboldt to discover a fact hidden from the authors

[031-116] of logico-universal grammars: namely the internal form of language

[031-117] (*innere Sprachform*), which is neither logical concept nor physical

[031-118] sound, but the subjective view of things formed by man, the product

[031-119] of imagination and feeling, the individualization of the concept.

[031-120] Conjunction of the internal form of language with physical sound is

[031-121] the work of an internal synthesis; "and here, more than anywhere else,

[031-122] language by its profound and mysterious operation recalls art. Sculptor

[031-123] and painter also unite the idea with matter, and their efforts are

[031-124] judged praiseworthy or not according as this union, this intimate

[031-125] interpenetration, is the work of true genius, or as the idea is

[031-126] something separate, painfully and laboriously imposed upon the matter

[031-127] by sheer force of brush or chisel."[[8]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_8_480)

[031-128] *Language and art in Humboldt.*

[031-129] But Humboldt was content to regard the procedure of artist and speaker

[031-130] as comparable by analogy, without proceeding to identify them. On the

[031-131] one hand, he was too one-sided in his view of language as a means

[031-132] for the development of thought (logical thought); on the other, his

[031-133] own æsthetic ideas, always vague and not always true, prevented his

[031-134] perception of the identity. Of his two principal writings on Æsthetic,

[031-135] that on *[[beauty|Beauty]] Masculine and Feminine* (1795) seems to be wholly

[031-136] under the influence of [[j-j-winckelmann|Winckelmann]], whose antithesis between beauty

[031-137] and expression is revived, and the opinion expressed that specific

[031-138] sexual characters diminish the beauty of the human body and that beauty

[031-139] asserts itself only by triumphing over differences of sex. His other

[031-140] work, which is inspired by Goethe's *Hermann und Dorothee,* defines

[031-141] art as "representation of nature by means of [[imagination|fancy]]; the representation

[031-142] being beautiful, just because it is the work of fancy," a metamorphosis

[031-143] of nature carried to a higher sphere. The poet reflects the pictures

[031-144] of language, itself a complex of abstractions.[[9]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_9_481) In his dissertation

[031-145] on Linguistic, Humboldt distinguishes poetry and prose, treating

[031-146] the two concepts philosophically, not by the empirical distinction

[031-147] between free and measured or periodic and metric language. "Poetry

[031-148] gives us reality in its sensible appearance, as it is felt internally

[031-149] and externally; but is indifferent to the character which makes it

[031-150] real, and even deliberately ignores that character. It presents the

[031-151] sensuous appearance to fancy and, by this means, leads towards the

[031-152] contemplation of an artistically ideal whole. Prose, on the contrary,

[031-153] looks in reality for the roots which attach it to existence, the cords

[031-154] which bind her to it: hence it fastens fact to fact and concept to

[031-155] concept according to the methods of the intellect, and strives towards

[031-156] the objective union of them all in an idea."[[10]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_10_482) Poetry precedes

[031-157] prose: before producing prose, the spirit necessarily forms itself in

[031-158] poetry.[[11]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_11_483) But, beside these views, some of which are profoundly true,

[031-159] Humboldt looks on poets as perfecters of language, and on poetry as

[031-160] belonging only to certain exceptional moments,[[12]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_12_484) and makes us suspect

[031-161] that after all he never recognized clearly or maintained firmly that

[031-162] language is always poetry, and that prose (science) is a distinction

[031-163] not of æsthetic form but of content, that is, of logical form.

[031-164] *H. Steinthal. The linguistic function independent of the

[031-165] logical.*

[031-166] Humboldt's contradictions about the concept of language lost him his

[031-167] principal follower, Steinthal. With the help of his master, Steinthal

[031-168] restated the position that language belongs not to Logic but to

[031-169] Psychology,[[13]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_13_485) and in 1855 waged a gallant war against the Hegelian

[031-170] Becker, author of *The Organisms of Language,* one of the last logical

[031-171] grammarians, who pledged himself to deduce the entire body of the

[031-172] Sanskrit languages from twelve cardinal concepts. Steinthal declares it

[031-173] is not true that one cannot think without words: the deaf-mute thinks

[031-174] in signs; the mathematician in formulæ. In some languages, as in

[031-175] Chinese, the visual element is as necessary to thought as the phonetic,

[031-176] if not more so.[[14]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_14_486) In this he may have overshot the mark, and failed

[031-177] to establish the autonomy of expression with regard to logical thought;

[031-178] for his examples only confirm the fact that if we can think without

[031-179] words, we cannot think without expressions.[[15]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_15_487) But he successfully

[031-180] demonstrates that concept and word, logical judgement and proposition,

[031-181] are incommensurable. The proposition is not the judgement but the

[031-182] representation (*Darstellung*) of a judgement; and all propositions do

[031-183] not represent logical judgements. It is possible to express several

[031-184] judgements in a single proposition. The logical divisions of judgements

[031-185] (the relations of concepts) find no counterpart in the grammatical

[031-186] divisions of propositions. "A logical form of the proposition is just

[031-187] as much a contradiction as the angle of a circle or the circumference

[031-188] of a triangle." He who talks, in so far as he talks, possesses not

[031-189] thoughts but language.[[16]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_16_488)

[031-190] *Identity of the problems of the origin and the nature of

[031-191] language.*

[031-192] Having thus freed language from all dependence on Logic, having

[031-193] repeatedly proclaimed the principle that language produces its forms

[031-194] independently of Logic and in the fullest autonomy,[[17]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_17_489) and having

[031-195] purified Humboldt's theory from the taint of the logical grammar of

[031-196] Port Royal, Steinthal seeks the origin of language, recognizing, with

[031-197] his master, that the question of its origin is identical with that of

[031-198] nature of language, its psychological genesis or rather the position

[031-199] it occupies in evolution of the spirit. "In the matter of language

[031-200] there is no difference between its original creation (*Urschöpfung*)

[031-201] and the creation which is daily repeated."[[18]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_18_490) Language belongs to the

[031-202] vast class of reflex movements; but to say that is to look at it from

[031-203] one side only and to omit its own essential peculiarity. Animals have

[031-204] reflex movements and sensations like man; but in animals the senses

[031-205] "are wide gates through which external nature rushes to the assault

[031-206] with such impetus as to overwhelm the mind and deprive it of all

[031-207] independence and freedom of movement." In man, however, language can

[031-208] arise because man is resistance to nature, conqueror of his own body,

[031-209] freedom incarnate: "language is liberation: even to-day we feel our

[031-210] mind lightened and freed from a weight when we speak." In the situation

[031-211] immediately preceding the production of speech man must be conceived as

[031-212] "accompanying all his sensations and all the intuitions received by his

[031-213] mind with the most lively contortions of body, attitudes of mimicry,

[031-214] gestures, and above all tones, articulate tones." What element of

[031-215] speech did he lack? One only, but a most important one: the conscious

[031-216] conjunction of reflex bodily movements with the excitations of his

[031-217] mind. If sensuous consciousness is already consciousness, it lacks the

[031-218] consciousness of being conscious; if it is already intuition, it is

[031-219] not intuition of intuition; what it lacks is in a word the internal

[031-220] form of speech. When that arises, there arises too its inseparable

[031-221] accompaniment, words. Man does not select sound: it is given him,

[031-222] and he takes it of necessity, instinctively, without intention or

[031-223] choice.[[19]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_19_491)

[031-224] *Steinthal's mistaken ideas on art: his failure to unite

[031-225] Linguistic and Æsthetic.*

[031-226] This is not the place for detailed examination of the whole of

[031-227] Steinthal's theory and the various phases, not always progressive,

[031-228] through which he travelled, especially after the beginning of

[031-229] his spiritual collaboration with Lazarus, with whom he studied

[031-230] ethnopsychology (*Völkerpsychologie*), of which they both took

[031-231] Linguistic to be a part.[[20]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_20_492) But, while giving him full credit for

[031-232] bringing Humboldt's ideas into coherent order, and for clearly

[031-233] differentiating, as had never before been done, between linguistic

[031-234] activity and the activity of logical thought, it must be noted

[031-235] that Steintha! never recognized the identity of the internal form

[031-236] of language (which he also called the intuition of intuition, or

[031-237] apperception) with the æsthetic imagination. The Herbartian psychology

[031-238] to which he clung afforded him no clue to such a discovery. [[j-f-herbart|Herbart]] and

[031-239] his followers divorced psychology from logic as a normative science

[031-240] and never succeeded in discerning the true connection between feeling

[031-241] and spiritual formation, soul and spirit; they never understood that

[031-242] logical thought is one of these spiritual formations: an activity, not

[031-243] a code of external laws. The domain allotted by them to Æsthetic we

[031-244] already know; for them Æsthetic too was only another code of beautiful

[031-245] formal relations. Under the influence of these doctrines Steinthal

[031-246] was led to regard [[art|Art]] as the embellishment of thoughts, Linguistic as

[031-247] the science of speech, and [[rhetoric|Rhetoric]] or Æsthetic as a thing differing

[031-248] from Linguistic since it is science of fine or beautiful speaking.[[21]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_21_493)

[031-249] In one of his innumerable tracts he says, "Poetics and Rhetoric both

[031-250] differ from Linguistic, since they are obliged to touch on many

[031-251] important topics before reaching language. These sciences therefore

[031-252] have but one section devoted to Linguistic, which is the concluding

[031-253] section of Syntax. Moreover Syntax has a character entirely different

[031-254] from Rhetoric and from Poetics; the former is occupied solely with

[031-255] correctness (*Richtigkeit)* of language; the latter two sciences

[031-256] study beauty or grace of expression (*Schönheit oder Angemessenheit

[031-257] des Ausdrucks*): the principles of the first are merely grammatical,

[031-258] the others must consider matters outside language; for example, the

[031-259] disposition of the orator and so forth. To speak plainly, Syntax is

[031-260] to Stylistic as is the grammatical measure of the quantity of vowels

[031-261] to the theory of metre."[[22]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_22_494) That speaking invariably means good or

[031-262] beautiful speaking, since speech that is neither good nor beautiful is

[031-263] not really speech,[[23]](280320132259829826_54618-h-19.htm.html#Footnote_23_495) and that the radical renewal of the concept of

[031-264] language inaugurated by Humboldt and himself must produce far-reaching

[031-265] effects on the cognate sciences of Poetics, Rhetoric and Æsthetic and,

[031-266] by transforming, unify them, never entered Steinthal's head. After

[031-267] all this labour and all this minute analysis, the identification of

[031-268] language and poetry, and of the science of language with the science of

[031-269] poetry, the identification of Linguistic with Æsthetic, still found its

[031-270] least faulty expression in the prophetic aphorisms of [[giambattista-vico|Giambattista Vico]].
