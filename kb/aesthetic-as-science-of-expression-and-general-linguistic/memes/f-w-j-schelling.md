# F. W. J. Schelling

Friedrich Wilhelm Joseph Schelling (1775–1854) was a German Idealist philosopher who, in his early system, elevated art to the supreme expression of the Absolute. Croce discusses Schelling alongside Solger in his historical survey (P02-351).

## Biography

A prodigy who became professor at Jena at 23, Schelling moved through several philosophical phases — from Fichtean idealism to the philosophy of identity to the later philosophy of revelation. His *System of Transcendental Idealism* (1800) contains his most influential aesthetic theory: art as the "organon of philosophy."

## Contributions to Æsthetic

- **Art as Organon of Philosophy**: Schelling argued that art accomplishes what philosophy can only aspire to — the complete unification of subject and object, freedom and necessity, conscious and unconscious activity. In the work of art, the Absolute becomes objective to itself
- **Aesthetic Intuition as Highest Act**: For Schelling, aesthetic intuition is intellectual intuition become objective — the highest act of spirit, through which the philosopher accesses the identity of nature and mind
- **Influence on Romanticism**: Schelling's elevation of art profoundly shaped the Romantic movement's conception of the artist as a quasi-divine creator and art as a revelation of truth
