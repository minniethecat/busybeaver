[027-001] *[[immanuel-kant|I. Kant]].*

[027-002] Of all these writers, [[j-j-winckelmann|Winckelmann]] and Mengs, Home and Hogarth, [[g-e-lessing|Lessing]]

[027-003] and Goethe, none was a philosopher in the true sense of the word: not

[027-004] even those who like Meier laid claim to the title, nor those who had

[027-005] some gifts for philosophy like Herder or Hamann. After [[giambattista-vico|Vico]], the next

[027-006] European mind of real speculative genius is Immanuel Kant, who now

[027-007] comes before us in his turn.

[027-008] *Kant and Vico.*

[027-009] That Kant took up the problem of philosophy where Vico laid it down

[027-010] (not, of course, in a directly historical, but in an ideal, sense) has

[027-011] already been noted by others.[[1]](280320132259829826_54618-h-16.htm.html#Footnote_1_358) How far he made an advance upon his

[027-012] predecessor and how far he failed to reach the same level it is not

[027-013] here our business to inquire; we must confine ourselves strictly to the

[027-014] consideration of Æsthetic questions.

[027-015] Summarizing the results of such a consideration, we may say at once

[027-016] that though Kant holds an immensely important place in the development

[027-017] of German thought; though the book containing his examination of

[027-018] æsthetic facts is among his most influential works; and though in

[027-019] histories of Æsthetic written from the German point of view, which

[027-020] ignore practically the whole development of European thought from the

[027-021] sixteenth to the eighteenth century, Kant can pose as the man who

[027-022] discovered the problem of Æsthetic or solved it or brought it within

[027-023] sight of solution; yet in an unprejudiced and complete history whose

[027-024] aim is to take broad views and to consider not the popularity of a

[027-025] book or the historical importance of a nation but the intrinsic value

[027-026] of ideas, the judgement passed on Kant must be very different. Like

[027-027] Vico in the serious tenacity with which he reflected upon æsthetic

[027-028] facts, more fortunate than he in having a much larger stock of material

[027-029] gathered from preceding discussion and argument, Kant was at once

[027-030] unlike and less successful than Vico in that he was unable to attain a

[027-031] doctrine substantially true, and unable also to give his thoughts the

[027-032] necessary system and unity.

[027-033] *Identity of the concept of art in Kant and [[alexander-baumgarten|Baumgarten]].*

[027-034] In fact, what was Kant's idea of art? Strange as our reply may

[027-035] seem to those who recollect the explicit and insistent war waged

[027-036] by him against the school of Wolff, and the concept of beauty as a

[027-037] perfection confusedly perceived, we must assert that Kant's idea of

[027-038] art was fundamentally the same as that of Baumgarten and the Wolffian

[027-039] school.[[2]](280320132259829826_54618-h-16.htm.html#Footnote_2_359) In that school his mind had been trained; he always had a

[027-040] great respect for Baumgarten whom in the *Critique of Pure Reason* he

[027-041] calls "that excellent analyst"; he chose the text of Baumgarten for

[027-042] two of his University lectures on Metaphysics, and that of Meier for

[027-043] his lecture on Logic (*Vernunftlehre*). Kant, like them, therefore

[027-044] considered Logic and Æsthetic (or theory of art) as conjoined sciences.

[027-045] They were thus described by him in his *Scheme of Lectures* in 1765,

[027-046] when he proposed, while expounding the critique of reason, to "throw a

[027-047] glance at that of taste, that is to say, at Æsthetic, since the rules

[027-048] of one apply to the other and each throws light upon the other."

[027-049] *Kant's "Lectures."*

[027-050] In his University lectures he distinguished æsthetic truth from logical

[027-051] truth in the style of Meier; even citing the example of [[beauty|the beautiful]]

[027-052] rosy face of a girl which, when seen distinctly, *i.e.* through a

[027-053] microscope, ceases to be beautiful.[[3]](280320132259829826_54618-h-16.htm.html#Footnote_3_360) It is æsthetically true (said

[027-054] he) that a man once dead cannot come to life again, although this

[027-055] is in opposition to logic and moral truth: it is æsthetically true

[027-056] that the sun plunges into the sea, but it is false logically and

[027-057] objectively. To what degree it is necessary to combine logical truth

[027-058] with æsthetic the learned have never yet been able to decide; not even

[027-059] the greatest æstheticians. In order to become accessible, logical

[027-060] concepts must assume æsthetic forms; a garb to be abandoned only in

[027-061] the rational sciences which seek profundity. Æsthetic certainty is

[027-062] subjective: it is content with authority, *i.e.* the citation of the

[027-063] opinions of great men. On account of our weakness, for we are strongly

[027-064] attached to the sensible, æsthetic perfection often helps us to render

[027-065] our thoughts distinct. In this, examples and images co-operate;

[027-066] æsthetic perfection is the vehicle for logical perfection; taste is

[027-067] the analogue of intellect. There are logical truths which are not

[027-068] æsthetic truths: and on the other hand we must exclude from abstract

[027-069] philosophy exclamations and other sentimental commotions proper to the

[027-070] other truth. Poetry is a harmonious play of thoughts and sensations.

[027-071] Poetry and eloquence differ in this: in the former, thoughts adapt

[027-072] themselves to sensations; in the latter the contrary is the case.

[027-073] In these lectures Kant sometimes taught that poetry is anterior to

[027-074] eloquence because sensations come before thoughts; and he observed

[027-075] (perhaps under Herder's influence) that the poetry of Eastern peoples,

[027-076] lacking concepts, is wanting in unity and taste although rich in

[027-077] imaginative detail. Poetry formed out of the pure play of sensibility

[027-078] is doubtless a possibility, *e.g.* love-poems: but true poetry disdains

[027-079] such productions, concerned as they are with sensations which every one

[027-080] knows ought to be expelled from our breasts. True poetry must strive

[027-081] to present virtue and intellectual truth in sensible form, as has been

[027-082] done by Pope in his *Essay on Man,* in which he attempts to vivify

[027-083] poetry by means of reason. On other occasions Kant definitely says that

[027-084] logical perfection is the basis of every other, æsthetic perfection

[027-085] being merely an adornment of the logical; something of the latter may

[027-086] be omitted in order to appeal to the audience, but it must never be

[027-087] disguised or falsified.[[4]](280320132259829826_54618-h-16.htm.html#Footnote_4_361)

[027-088] This is Baumgartenism pure and simple; unless we are prepared to look

[027-089] on these Lectures as representing a pre-critical period of thought,

[027-090] or an exoteric doctrine superseded eventually by Kant's own original

[027-091] esoteric ideas in his *Critique of the Judgment* (1790). Not to open

[027-092] such a controversy, let us put these Lectures on one side (although

[027-093] they often throw no little light on the signification of Kantian

[027-094] phrases and formulæ), and refuse to raise the question what pages

[027-095] of the *Critique of the Judgment* are derived from Baumgarten and

[027-096] Meier; he who reads the works of these disciples of Wolff and passes

[027-097] immediately to the *Critique of Judgment* often has the impression that

[027-098] the atmosphere surrounding him is unchanged. But if the *Critique of

[027-099] Judgment* itself be examined without prejudice it will be seen that

[027-100] Kant always adhered to Baumgarten's conception of art as the sensible

[027-101] and imaginative vesture of an intellectual concept.

[027-102] *[[art|Art]] in the "Critique of Judgment."*

[027-103] According to Kant, art is not pure beauty wholly detached from the

[027-104] concept, it is adherent beauty, which presupposes and attaches

[027-105] itself to a concept.[[5]](280320132259829826_54618-h-16.htm.html#Footnote_5_362) This is the work of genius, the faculty of

[027-106] representing æsthetic ideas. An æsthetic idea is "a representation of

[027-107] the imagination which accompanies a given concept: a representation

[027-108] conjoined with such truthful representation of particulars as to be

[027-109] unable to find for it any expression that may mark a determinate

[027-110] concept, thereby endowing the given concept with something of the

[027-111] ineffable; a feeling which stimulates the cognitive faculties and

[027-112] reinforcing the tongue, which is simply the letter, with the spirit."

[027-113] [[genius|Genius]], then, has two constitutive elements, imagination and intellect;

[027-114] it consists in "that happy disposition, which no science can teach or

[027-115] diligence attain, to find ideas for a given concept and, also, to

[027-116] select the expression by which the subjective commotion it excites

[027-117] as accompaniment to a concept may be communicated to others." No

[027-118] concept is adequate to the æsthetic idea, as no representation of the

[027-119] imagination can ever possibly be adequate to the concept. Examples

[027-120] of æsthetic attributes are found in the eagle of Jupiter with the

[027-121] thunderbolt in its claws, and the peacock of the proud Queen of

[027-122] Heaven: "they do not, like logical attributes, represent that which

[027-123] is contained in our concepts of the [[sublime|sublimity]] or majesty of creation,

[027-124] but something else which gives occasion to the imagination to run

[027-125] riot over a multitude of kindred representations which make us think

[027-126] more than we can express in a given concept by means of words, and

[027-127] give us an æsthetic idea, which serves to this rational idea instead

[027-128] of a logical representation, precisely with the aim of quickening our

[027-129] feelings by throwing open to them a view over a vast field of kindred

[027-130] representations." There are a *modus logicos* and a *modus æstheticus*

[027-131] of expressing our thoughts: the first consists in following determinate

[027-132] principles: the other in the mere feeling of the unity of the

[027-133] representation.[[6]](280320132259829826_54618-h-16.htm.html#Footnote_6_363) To imagination, to intellect and to spirit (*Geist*)

[027-134] we must add taste, the link between imagination and intellect.[[7]](280320132259829826_54618-h-16.htm.html#Footnote_7_364) Art

[027-135] may therefore represent natural ugliness: artistic beauty "is not a

[027-136] beautiful *thing* but a beautiful representation of a thing": although

[027-137] the representation of ugliness has limits varying with the individual

[027-138] arts (a reminiscence of Lessing and Winckelmann), and an absolute limit

[027-139] at the disgusting and nauseating, which kill representation itself.[[8]](280320132259829826_54618-h-16.htm.html#Footnote_8_365)

[027-140] In natural things, too, there is adherent beauty which cannot be judged

[027-141] by the æsthetic judgement alone but demands a concept. Nature thus

[027-142] appears as a work of art, though superhuman art: "the teleological

[027-143] judgement is the basis and condition of the æsthetic." When we say

[027-144] "this is a beautiful woman," we merely mean that "nature beautifully

[027-145] represents in the form of this woman her purpose in the construction

[027-146] of the female body": it is necessary therefore, besides noting simple

[027-147] form, to aim at a concept, "so that the object may be apprehended

[027-148] through an æsthetic judgement logically conditioned."[[9]](280320132259829826_54618-h-16.htm.html#Footnote_9_366) By this

[027-149] means is formed the ideal of beauty in the human face, the expression

[027-150] of moral life.[[10]](280320132259829826_54618-h-16.htm.html#Footnote_10_367) Kant admits that there may also be artistic

[027-151] productions without a concept, comparable with the free beauties of

[027-152] nature, flowers and some birds (parrot, humming-bird, bird of paradise,

[027-153] etc.): ornamental drawings, cornice-mouldings, musical fantasies

[027-154] without words, represent nothing, no object reducible to a determined

[027-155] concept, and must be reckoned among free beauties.[[11]](280320132259829826_54618-h-16.htm.html#Footnote_11_368) But does not

[027-156] this necessitate their exclusion from true and proper art, from the

[027-157] operation of genius in which [[imagination|fancy]] and intellect must both, according

[027-158] to Kant, have a place?

[027-159] *Imagination in Kant's system.*

[027-160] This is Baumgartenism transposed into a higher key, more concentrated,

[027-161] more elaborated, more suggestive, until from moment to moment it seems

[027-162] about to burst into a wholly different conception of art. But it is

[027-163] still Baumgartenism, from whose intellectualistic bonds it never

[027-164] escapes. Nor was escape possible. A profound concept of imagination was

[027-165] entirely lacking to Kant's system and his philosophy of the spirit.

[027-166] Glancing over the table of faculties of the spirit which precedes

[027-167] his *Critique of Judgment,* we see that Kant co-ordinates with it

[027-168] the cognitive faculty, the feeling of pleasure and pain, and the

[027-169] appetitive faculty; to the first corresponds intellect, to the second,

[027-170] judgement (teleological and æsthetic), to the third, reason;[[12]](280320132259829826_54618-h-16.htm.html#Footnote_12_369) he

[027-171] finds no place for imagination amongst powers of the spirit but places

[027-172] it among the facts of sensation. He knows a reproductive imagination

[027-173] and an associative, but he knows nothing of a genuinely productive

[027-174] imagination, imagination in the proper sense.[[13]](280320132259829826_54618-h-16.htm.html#Footnote_13_370) We have seen that, in

[027-175] his doctrine, genius is the co-operation of several faculties.

[027-176] *The forms of intuition and the Transcendental Æsthetic.*

[027-177] Yet sometimes Kant had an inkling that intellectual activity is

[027-178] preceded by something which is not mere sensational material, but

[027-179] is an independent non-intellectual theoretical form. He obtained a

[027-180] glimpse of this latter form not when he was reflecting on art in the

[027-181] strict sense but when he was examining the process of knowledge: he

[027-182] does not treat of it in his *Critique of Judgment,* but in the first

[027-183] section of his *Critique of Pure Reason,* in the first part of the

[027-184] *Transcendental Doctrine of Elements.* He says here that sensations

[027-185] only enter the spirit when the latter itself gives them form; a form

[027-186] not identical with that which intellect gives to sensations, but

[027-187] much simpler, namely pure intuition, the totality of the *a priori*

[027-188] principles of sensibility. There must therefore be "a science which

[027-189] forms the first part of the transcendental doctrine of elements,

[027-190] distinct from that which contains the principles of pure thought and is

[027-191] named transcendental Logic." Now, what name does Kant confer upon this

[027-192] science whose existence he has deduced? None other than Transcendental

[027-193] Æsthetic (*die transcendentale Ästhetik*). In a note he even insists

[027-194] that this is the right name for the new science of which he treats, and

[027-195] censures the Germans for their habit of applying it to the Critique of

[027-196] [[taste|Taste]], which, as he thought at that time, could never become a science.

[027-197] Thus, he concludes, we approach more closely to the usage of the

[027-198] ancients, among whom the distinction between *αἰσθητὰ καὶ νοητά*[[14]](280320132259829826_54618-h-16.htm.html#Footnote_14_371)

[027-199] was well known.

[027-200] Nevertheless, after having so rightly postulated the necessity

[027-201] for a science of the forms of sensation or pure intuition, purely

[027-202] [[intuition|intuitive knowledge]], Kant went on, simply because he had no exact idea

[027-203] of the nature of the æsthetic faculty and of art, to fall into an

[027-204] intellectualistic error by reducing the form of sensibility or pure

[027-205] intuition into the two categories or functions of space and time,

[027-206] and by asserting that the spirit emerges from the chaos of sensation

[027-207] by organizing its sensations in space and time.[[15]](280320132259829826_54618-h-16.htm.html#Footnote_15_372) But space and

[027-208] time as such are very far from being primitive categories; they are

[027-209] relatively late and complex formations.[[16]](280320132259829826_54618-h-16.htm.html#Footnote_16_373) As examples of the matter

[027-210] of sensation Kant quoted hardness, impenetrability, colour and so

[027-211] forth. But the mind only recognizes colour and hardness in so far as it

[027-212] has already given form to its sensations; considered as brute matter,

[027-213] sensations fall outside the cognitive spirit, they are a limit; colour,

[027-214] hardness, impenetrability and so on, when recognized, are already

[027-215] intuitions, spiritual elaborations, the æsthetic activity in its

[027-216] rudimentary manifestation. The characterizing or qualifying imagination

[027-217] which is æsthetic activity ought to have occupied in the *Critique of

[027-218] Pure Reason* the pages devoted to the discussion of space and time,

[027-219] and would thus have constituted a real Transcendental Æsthetic, a real

[027-220] prologue to the transcendental Logic. In this manner Kant would have

[027-221] achieved the truth aimed at by Leibniz and Baumgarten and would have

[027-222] joined hands with Vico.

[027-223] *Theory of Beauty distinguished by Kant from that of Art.*

[027-224] His repeatedly announced opposition to the school of Wolff concerns not

[027-225] the concept of art but that of Beauty; two concepts for Kant entirely

[027-226] distinct. First of all, he did not admit that sensation could be

[027-227] called "confused knowledge," a confused form, that is, of intellectual

[027-228] cognition; rightly judging this to be a false account of sensibility,

[027-229] since a concept, however confused, is always a concept or a rough

[027-230] sketch of a concept, never an intuition.[[17]](280320132259829826_54618-h-16.htm.html#Footnote_17_374) But he further denied that

[027-231] pure beauty contained a concept, and therefore denied that it was a

[027-232] perfection sensibly apprehended. These reflexions have no doubt some

[027-233] connexion with those concerning the nature of art in the *Critique of

[027-234] Judgment;* but the connexion is far from close, still less are they

[027-235] actually fused into a single whole. That Kant was minutely familiar

[027-236] with eighteenth-century writers who had discussed beauty and taste is

[027-237] shown by his Lectures, wherein they are all quoted and used.[[18]](280320132259829826_54618-h-16.htm.html#Footnote_18_375) Of

[027-238] these the greater part, especially the [[england|English]], were sensationalists,

[027-239] others intellectualists; some few, as we have noted, were inclined

[027-240] towards mysticism. Kant began by tending towards sensationalism

[027-241] in æsthetic problems, then became the adversary of sensationalists

[027-242] and intellectualists alike. This development can be traced in his

[027-243] *Observations on the Beautiful and Sublime,* as well as in his

[027-244] Lectures; its final expression is reached in the *Critique of Judgment.*

[027-245] Of the four moments, as he calls them, *i.e.* the four determinations,

[027-246] he accords to Beauty, the two negative are directed, one against the

[027-247] sensationalists, the other against the intellectualists. "That is

[027-248] beautiful which pleases *without interest*": "That is beautiful which

[027-249] pleases *without concepts*."[[19]](280320132259829826_54618-h-16.htm.html#Footnote_19_376) Here he asserts the existence of a

[027-250] spiritual region, distinct on one side from the pleasurable, the useful

[027-251] and the good, and on the other from truth. But this region, as we know

[027-252] very well, is not that of art, which Kant attaches to the concept: it

[027-253] is the region of a special activity of feeling which he calls judgement

[027-254] or, more exactly, æsthetic judgement.

[027-255] *Mystical features in Kant's theory of Beauty.*

[027-256] The other two moments give some kind of a definition of this region:

[027-257] "That is beautiful which has the form of finality without the

[027-258] representation of an end": "That is beautiful which is the object of

[027-259] universal pleasure."[[20]](280320132259829826_54618-h-16.htm.html#Footnote_20_377) What is this mysterious sphere? What this

[027-260] disinterested pleasure we experience in pure colours and tones, in

[027-261] flowers, and even in adherent beauty when we make abstraction from the

[027-262] concept to which it adheres?

[027-263] Our answer is: there is no such sphere; it does not exist; the

[027-264] examples given are instances either of pleasure in general or of

[027-265] facts of artistic expression. Kant, who so emphatically criticizes

[027-266] the sensationalists and the intellectualists, does not show the same

[027-267] severity towards the neo-Platonic line of thought whose revival we

[027-268] remarked in the eighteenth century. Winckelmann in particular exercised

[027-269] strong influence over his mind. In one course of his Lectures we find

[027-270] him making a curious distinction between form and matter: in music

[027-271] melody is matter and harmony form: in a flower the scent is material

[027-272] and the shape (*Gestalt)* is form (*[[form|Form]]*).[[21]](280320132259829826_54618-h-16.htm.html#Footnote_21_378) This reappears

[027-273] slightly modified in the *Critique of Judgment.* "In painting,

[027-274] statuary and all the figurative arts in architecture and gardening,

[027-275] so far as they are fine arts, the drawing is the essential; in which

[027-276] the foundation of taste lies not in what gratifies (*vergnügt*) in

[027-277] sensation, but in that which pleases (*gefällt*) by its form. The

[027-278] colours which illuminate the drawing belong to sensuous stimulus

[027-279] (*Reiz*) and may bring the object more vividly before the senses, but

[027-280] do not render it worthy of contemplation as a thing of beauty; they

[027-281] are, moreover, often limited by the exigencies of the beautiful form,

[027-282] and even where their sensuous stimulus is legitimate, they are ennobled

[027-283] only by the beautiful form."[[22]](280320132259829826_54618-h-16.htm.html#Footnote_22_379) Continuing in pursuit of this phantasm

[027-284] of beauty which is not the beauty of art nor yet the pleasing, and is

[027-285] equally detached from expressiveness and pleasure, Kant loses himself

[027-286] in insoluble contradictions. Little inclined to submit himself to the

[027-287] charm of imagination, abhorring "poetic philosophers" like Herder,[[23]](280320132259829826_54618-h-16.htm.html#Footnote_23_380)

[027-288] he makes statements and refuses to commit himself to them, affirms

[027-289] and immediately criticizes his affirmations, and wraps up Beauty in

[027-290] a mystery which, at bottom, was nothing more than his own individual

[027-291] incertitude and inability to see clearly the existence of an activity

[027-292] of feeling which, in the spirit of his sane philosophy, represented a

[027-293] logical contradiction. "Necessary and universal pleasure" and "finality

[027-294] without the idea of an end" are the organized expression in words of

[027-295] this contradiction.

[027-296] By way of clearing up the contradiction he arrives at the following

[027-297] thought: "The judgement of taste is founded on a concept (the concept

[027-298] of a general foundation of the subjective teleology of nature through

[027-299] judgement); but it is a concept by which it is impossible to know or

[027-300] demonstrate anything of the object, because the object in itself is

[027-301] indeterminable and unsuited to cognition; on the other hand, it has

[027-302] validity for every one (for every one, I say, in so far as it is an

[027-303] individual judgement, immediately accompanying intuition), since its

[027-304] determining reason reposes, perhaps, in the concept of that which may

[027-305] be regarded as the supersensible substrate of mankind." Beauty, then,

[027-306] is a symbol of morality. "The subjective principle alone, that is the

[027-307] indeterminate idea of the supersensible in us, can be considered the

[027-308] only key able to unlock this faculty springing from a source we cannot

[027-309] fathom: excepting by its aid, no comprehension of it can possibly

[027-310] be reached."[[24]](280320132259829826_54618-h-16.htm.html#Footnote_24_381) These cautious words, and all others here used by

[027-311] Kant to conceal his thoughts, do not hide his tendency to mysticism.

[027-312] A mysticism without conviction or enthusiasm, almost in spite of

[027-313] himself, but very evident nevertheless. His inadequate grasp of the

[027-314] æsthetic activity led him to see double, even triple, and caused the

[027-315] unnecessary multiplication of his explanatory principles. Although he

[027-316] was always ignorant of the genuine nature of the æsthetic activity, he

[027-317] was indebted to it for suggesting to him the pure categories of space

[027-318] and time as the Transcendental Æsthetic; it caused him to develop the

[027-319] theory of imaginative embellishment of intellectual concepts by the

[027-320] work of genius; finally it forced him to acknowledge a mysterious

[027-321] faculty of feeling, midway between theoretical and practical activity,

[027-322] cognitive and yet not cognitive, moral and indifferent to morality,

[027-323] pleasing yet wholly detached from the pleasure of the senses. Great

[027-324] use of this power was made by Kant's immediate successors in [[germany|Germany]]

[027-325] who were delighted to find their daring speculations supported by that

[027-326] severe critic of experience, the philosopher of Königsberg.
