# Transcendental

The term "transcendental" in Croce's history of aesthetics carries a double meaning: it names Kant's project of identifying the a priori conditions that make experience possible, and it designates the recurring philosophical temptation to mistake these logical conditions for the intuitive activity that is art. Croce treats the transcendental as a historically necessary concept whose misapplication to the aesthetic sphere produced both the most ambitious and the most erroneous theories of German Idealism.

## Definition

Croce engages the transcendental in two distinct registers. In its scholastic sense, it denotes the universal predicates of being -- the *transcendentalia* of medieval philosophy, invoked by Wolff and Baumgarten when they identified beauty with the perfection that "does not differ from being, nor does being differ from the truth which the schoolmen call transcendental" (025-081). In its Kantian sense, the transcendental names the formal conditions under which the mind organizes sensation into knowledge. Kant correctly saw that sensibility requires its own a priori forms, and he postulated a science -- Transcendental Aesthetic -- to investigate them (027-189). But Croce argues that Kant's execution was fatally flawed: instead of recognizing aesthetic activity as the formative intuition of the spirit, Kant intellectualized sensibility by reducing it to the "two categories or functions of space and time" (027-205).

## In Croce's System

### The Transcendental in Kant and German Idealism

The pivotal moment in Croce's treatment of the transcendental occurs in his analysis of Kant's *Critique of Pure Reason*. Kant deduced the necessity of "a science which forms the first part of the transcendental doctrine of elements, distinct from that which contains the principles of pure thought and is named transcendental Logic" (027-189). He then gave this science its name: "None other than Transcendental Aesthetic (*die transcendentale Asthetik*) (027-192). Croce notes that Kant even "insists that this is the right name for the new science of which he treats, and censures the Germans for their habit of applying it to the Critique of Taste" (027-193).

Yet Kant, having correctly postulated the need for a science of pure intuition, fell into what Croce regards as an intellectualistic error. By identifying the form of sensibility exclusively with space and time, Kant mistook late and complex cognitive constructions for the primitive formative activity of the spirit. Colour, hardness, impenetrability -- Kant's own examples of sensory matter -- are already, Croce insists, "intuitions, spiritual elaborations, the aesthetic activity in its rudimentary manifestation" (027-215). The true subject of a Transcendental Aesthetic should have been the imagination itself as a formative power.

From this critique Croce draws one of his most sweeping historical verdicts:

> The characterizing or qualifying imagination which is aesthetic activity ought to have occupied in the *Critique of Pure Reason* the pages devoted to the discussion of space and time, and would thus have constituted a real Transcendental Aesthetic, a real prologue to the transcendental Logic. In this manner Kant would have achieved the truth aimed at by Leibniz and Baumgarten and would have joined hands with Vico. (027-219)

The reference to Vico is decisive: for Croce, the transcendental project, correctly understood, converges with the Vichian discovery of poetry as autonomous, pre-logical knowledge. Kant's error was not in seeking a priori conditions of intuition but in mistaking those conditions for abstract categories rather than for the living activity of aesthetic expression.

### Key Distinctions

Croce draws several critical distinctions around the transcendental. The first is between the **transcendental and the aesthetic** proper. Kant's Transcendental Aesthetic deals with the conditions of sensible intuition in general, not with art or beauty. Kant himself "censures the Germans for their habit of applying it to the Critique of Taste, which, as he thought at that time, could never become a science" (027-195). Croce agrees that the science of intuition must be kept distinct from a theory of taste, but he rejects Kant's reduction of intuition to spatio-temporal schematism.

The second distinction is between the **transcendental and the speculative**. In Schiller's hands, the transcendental becomes a regulative ideal rather than a constitutive reality: "From transcendental motives, reason here demands that communion be established between formal and material activity; that is to say, there must be an activity of play" (028-171). Schiller thus preserved a critical distance that the Romantic ironists abandoned when they turned the transcendental into a cosmic farce -- Friedrich Schlegel "described art as a perpetual parody of itself and a 'transcendental farce'" (028-249).

The third distinction opposes **transcendental logic to intuition**. Croce's central thesis throughout his *Aesthetic* is that intuition is prior to and independent of conceptual thought. The transcendental, in its Kantian formulation, remains a doctrine of logical conditions; it cannot account for the sui generis character of aesthetic knowledge, which is knowledge of the individual through form, not knowledge of the universal through concepts.

## Historical Context

The trajectory of the transcendental in aesthetic thought runs from Baumgarten through Kant to Schelling and the Romantic epigoni. Baumgarten, working within the Wolffian rationalist framework, had already identified aesthetic knowledge as *cognitio sensitiva* -- sensitive cognition -- but he lacked the transcendental machinery to distinguish its conditions from those of logic. Kant supplied that machinery in the *Critique of Pure Reason* (027-189), yet by confining the aesthetic to spatio-temporal form, he paradoxically blocked the very science he had postulated. Schelling later elevated art to the status of "transcendental intuition become objective" (028-310), making it "not only the organ but the document of philosophy" (028-311) -- a position Croce regards as an overcorrection that dissolves art into metaphysics.

The reaction against transcendental aesthetics came from multiple directions. Francesco De Sanctis, for whom "imagination was never the mystic faculty of transcendental apperception, the intellectual intuition of German metaphysicians, but simply the poet's faculty of synthesis and creation" (034-096), provided Croce with a model of aesthetic theory purged of transcendental excess. In the bibliographical tradition, George Saintsbury excluded "the more transcendental Aesthetic, those ambitious theories of Beauty and artistic pleasure in general" from his history of criticism, judging them to be "but cloud-appearances of Juno" (038-2000). For Croce, however, the transcendental cannot simply be dismissed; it must be corrected from within, by recognizing that the true a priori of art is the expressive activity of the spirit itself.
