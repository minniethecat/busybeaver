# Content

In Benedetto Croce's aesthetic philosophy, content (*contenuto*) is not raw subject matter waiting to be shaped but the **already-formed intuitive material** that arises simultaneously with form in the aesthetic act.

## Definition

Croce rejects the common-sense view that the artist begins with a "content" (a story, a feeling, an idea) and then gives it "form" (words, paint, music). Rather, "Content and form in Æsthetic" (P02-019) are two aspects of a single act. Content is sensation that has been formed; form is the activity that forms it. They are distinguishable only by abstraction, never in experience.

This has important consequences: "Practical innocence of art — Independence of art — Criticism of the theory of the end of art and of the choice of content" (P02-052). If content and form are inseparable, then the artist does not "choose" a content and then "execute" it. The content exists only as the formed work.

## In Croce's System

### Content as Formed Sensation

Content in the aesthetic sphere is sensation that has been taken up into the intuitive-forming activity. Before formation, there is only the "formless matter" of sensation — which the spirit "can never apprehend in itself as simple matter" (002-152). Once formed, sensation becomes content — the what of the intuition, inseparable from its how (the form).

### The Choice of Content

Because content and form are one, questions about "appropriate" content for art are misplaced. Any content can be artistic if it is successfully expressed. Croce's critique of theories that prescribe content (moral, political, religious) is fundamental to his defense of artistic autonomy: art is not answerable to any external standard for its content.

## Key Distinctions

**Content vs Subject Matter.** Subject matter is the external, pre-aesthetic reference (the historical event a painting depicts); content is the formed intuitive material (the event as it appears in the painting). Aesthetic criticism concerns content, not subject matter.

**Content vs Meaning.** Content is what is intuited; meaning is what is conceptually judged. A beautiful image has content; it does not have "meaning" in the logical sense until a concept is applied to it.

**Content in Different Art Forms.** Because content is formed through specific expressive media, the "same" subject matter yields different content in different arts. A Madonna by Raphæl and a Madonna described in poetry share a subject but have different aesthetic contents, because the forming activity differs.

## Historical Context

The content-form problem runs through Western aesthetics. **Plato** subordinated content (the Idea) to its imitation; **Aristotle** integrated form and matter in hylomorphism. **Hegel** saw art as the sensuous appearance of the Idea — content (Idea) seeking adequate form (sensuous appearance). The "Æsthetic of content" tradition (P02-299) emphasized the importance of subject matter, while formalist aesthetics privileged structure over meaning. Croce dissolves the opposition by identifying content and form in the expressive act (P02-252).
