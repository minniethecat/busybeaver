# Hedonistic

The hedonistic (*edonistico*) in Croce's Æsthetic is the dimension of pleasure and pain that necessarily accompanies every form of spiritual activity, including the æsthetic. Unlike the reductive theory of [[hedonism]] — which makes pleasure the essence of art — the hedonistic is a legitimate secondary aspect: the agreeable or disagreeable feeling that attends successful or failed expression.

## Definition

Croce distinguishes between æsthetic hedonism as a theory and the hedonistic as a descriptive fact. The theory is false: it treats the æsthetic as a species of the pleasurable, collapsing the theoretical into the practical. But that does not mean pleasure is absent from æsthetic experience. Every spiritual activity carries a hedonistic accompaniment — a felt quality of satisfaction or dissatisfaction that arises from the activity itself (011-102).

## In Croce's System

### The Hedonistic Accompaniment

In his analysis of the four forms of spirit, Croce insists that each form — intuition, conceptual thought, economic volition, and moral volition — has a hedonistic side. The pleasure of successful expression, the satisfaction of correct reasoning, the gratification of achieved ends: all are hedonistic accompaniments, not the essence of those activities (014-024). The hedonistic thus belongs to the phenomenology of spiritual life, not to its structure.

### Distinction from Hedonism

The hedonistic as accompaniment must not be confused with æsthetic hedonism as doctrine. "The æsthetic-hedonistic point of view has been presented in every imaginable form," Croce observes — but the error lies in treating this accompaniment as primary (012-011). Hedonism makes the hedonistic the whole; Croce makes it a dependent attribute.

### The Rigoristic-Hedonistic Distinction

Croce further differentiates between two modalities: "(a) hedonistic, in so far as it considers art as consisting of sensuous pleasure; (b) rigoristic-hedonistic, in so far as, while admitting the sensuous pleasure of art, it considers it as united with the higher values" (020-030, 020-031). The first is pure hedonism; the second attempts to reconcile pleasure with nobility.

## Historical Context

### Ancient and Modern Hedonistic Theories

The hedonistic view of art has ancient roots. "Hardly has this hedonistic conception of art been formed," Croce notes, "than we see the attempt to temper it" — a pattern visible from Plato through the Renaissance (012-093). The special refinement of hedonistic theory that "used to be pompously called the philosophy of the beautiful" (013-051) dominated eighteenth-century aesthetics before Kant and the idealists offered alternatives.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's innovation is not to deny the hedonistic dimension but to assign it its proper place: as accompaniment, never as essence. The hedonistic side gives rise to "the pleasure and pain of the artist" (014-004) — real and important, but derivative. By contrast, the study of "facts essentially hedonistic (utilitarian)" in art history and psychology does not touch the æsthetic core (012-085).

## Criticism and Debate

> Croce's devaluation of the hedonistic has been questioned by later aestheticians who see the felt quality of art as inseparable from its meaning. The "hedonistic accompaniment" that Croce treats as secondary may, on a more embodied account, be integral to æsthetic experience (014-060).
>
> The forward momentum against "hedonistic and pedagogic Æsthetic" (012-135) also shows Croce aligning against two reductive tendencies at once — making his argument strategic as well as systematic.
