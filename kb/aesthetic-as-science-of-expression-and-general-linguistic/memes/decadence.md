# Decadence

Decadence in art is a historical phenomenon that Croce treats not as a moral judgment but as a structural condition: the exhaustion of a tradition's expressive resources and the consequent failure of new works to achieve aesthetic autonomy. Unlike the moralist who condemns decadence or the aesthete who celebrates it, Croce seeks to understand it as a phase in the life of artistic traditions.

## Definition

Croce defines decadence functionally: "decadence could be the result of employing means already employed" — that is, the reliance on exhausted forms that no longer correspond to living intuitions (018-278). When artists repeat the forms of a previous age without the spiritual content that originally animated them, the result is not art but the simulacrum of art — a dead form parading as expression.

The crucial point is that decadence is not simply the failure of individual artists. "The general decadence of Italian literature at the end of the sixteenth century" was not caused by a sudden scarcity of talent but by a systemic condition: the exhaustion of the Renaissance expressive synthesis and the inability of new artists "to express their own decadence" — that is, to give aesthetic form to their own historical situation (018-282, 018-285).

## In Croce's System

### Relation to Other Forms of Spirit

Decadence belongs, in Croce's system, to the sphere of historical understanding rather than to aesthetic judgment proper. The statement "this period is decadent" is not an aesthetic evaluation of individual works but a historical characterization of a cultural epoch. Aesthetic judgment asks whether a work succeeds or fails as expression; historical judgment asks whether an age is flourishing or declining.

This distinction is crucial because it allows Croce to maintain that genuinely beautiful works can be produced even in periods of historical decadence. "Decadence it presupposes, and thus art can coexist with decadence" (033-154). An individual artist working in a decadent age may still achieve successful expression; the decadence of the age does not determine the aesthetic value of every work produced within it.

### Key Distinctions

Croce distinguishes decadence from several related concepts:

1. **Decadence vs. ugliness** — Ugliness is the failure of expression; decadence is the historical condition in which such failure becomes systematic. A decadent age produces more ugly works, but not every work in a decadent age is ugly.

2. **Decadence vs. transition** — Not every change in artistic style signals decadence. Transition is the normal process by which one expressive synthesis gives way to another; decadence is the abnormal prolongation of forms that have lost their spiritual content.

3. **Decadence vs. primitivism** — The primitive is the inchoate, the not-yet-formed; the decadent is the exhausted, the no-longer-forming. Both stand outside the flourishing of art, but at opposite ends of the historical trajectory.

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

The concept of decadence has a long history in Western thought, from the biblical narrative of the Fall through Gibbon's *Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire*. In aesthetic theory, the concept gained prominence in the nineteenth century through the work of literary historians who sought to periodize the history of art into phases of growth, maturity, and decline.

The Italian tradition to which Croce belongs was particularly concerned with the question of "the decadence of Italian literature in the seventeenth century" (038-352). Francesco De Sanctis, Croce's great predecessor, had made the analysis of literary decadence central to his *History of Italian Literature*. Croce builds upon this tradition while giving it a more rigorous philosophical foundation.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's distinctive contribution is to separate the historical concept of decadence from the moral and aesthetic judgments that usually accompany it. "During the decadence of Italian literature," Croce observes, critics were well "aware that decadence was due to the fact that literature had become a social game" — that is, a matter of conventional rules rather than genuine expression (038-360). This diagnosis is historical, not moralistic: the problem is not that artists were wicked but that the forms they inherited no longer served living intuition.

Croce also describes the "decadence of the ancient world" and the "entire decadence in Southern Italy" as historical facts that must be understood in terms of the exhaustion of creative traditions (038-129, 034-038). But he resists the temptation to draw pessimistic conclusions about the future of art. Decadence is always the decadence of a particular tradition at a particular time; it does not entail that art as such is declining.

## Criticism and Debate

> They have not been able to express their own decadence, they have only repeated the forms of a past greatness. That is the true sign of decadence (018-285).

The question Croce leaves open is whether decadence can itself become the object of successful expression. If an artist can "express their own decadence," then the work that results is not decadent in the pejorative sense — it is a genuine aesthetic achievement that happens to have decadence as its content. This paradox — that the expression of decadence may be the antidote to decadence — is one that Croce does not fully resolve.

A related criticism concerns the historical scope of Croce's concept. By restricting decadence to the exhaustion of particular traditions, Croce may underestimate the possibility of a more general cultural decadence — a decline not of this or that artistic tradition but of the conditions that make art possible at all. Croce's optimism about the permanent vitality of the aesthetic spirit may be philosophically grounded, but it does not answer the historical question of whether a civilization can lose its capacity for aesthetic expression.
