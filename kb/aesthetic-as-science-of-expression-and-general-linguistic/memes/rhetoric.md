# Rhetoric

In Benedetto Croce's aesthetic philosophy, rhetoric (*retorica*) is **not art but a practical technique** — the art of persuasion, which belongs to the economic sphere of spirit, not the aesthetic sphere of intuition-expression.

## Definition

Croce distinguishes rhetoric sharply from poetry and art. Art is the expression of intuition — an end in itself. Rhetoric is the use of language to produce an effect — a means to an end. The orator who moves an audience to action is performing a practical, not aesthetic, act. The poet who expresses an image is performing an aesthetic, not practical, act. A work can be both artistic and rhetorical, but the two dimensions are distinct.

## In Croce's System

### Rhetoric as Economic Activity

Rhetoric belongs to the economic sphere — the pursuit of individual ends through efficient means. The rhetorician's aim is to persuade, to move, to convince. These are practical ends. Art, by contrast, has no external end: "Practical innocence of art" (P02-052) means that art is its own justification.

### The Rhetorization of Art

Croce warns against the tendency to "rhetorize" art — to treat art as if its value lay in its effects rather than in its intrinsic expressive quality. Didactic art (art that teaches), political art (art that agitates), and sentimental art (art that induces emotion) are all forms of rhetoric mistaken for art.

## Key Distinctions

**Rhetoric vs Poetry.** Poetry is expression for its own sake; rhetoric is language for the sake of an effect. This distinction echoes the ancient debate between poetry and rhetoric, but Croce radicalizes it: rhetoric is not a "lower" form of art but a categorially distinct activity.

**Rhetoric vs Technique.** Rhetoric is a practical technique aimed at persuasion; artistic technique is a practical activity aimed at externalizing expression. Both belong to the practical sphere, but they serve different ends.

**Rhetoric vs Aesthetic Criticism.** Genuine aesthetic criticism re-creates the artist's intuition; rhetorical criticism evaluates a work by its effects on an audience. The former is philosophical; the latter is sociological.

## Historical Context

Rhetoric has a long history from **Aristotle**'s *Rhetoric* through **Cicero** and **Quintilian** to the Renaissance, where rhetoric was a cornerstone of education. The Romantic movement devalued rhetoric in favor of spontaneous expression, and Croce continues this tradition while providing a systematic philosophical basis for the distinction: rhetoric is to art as economics is to cognition.
