# Harmony

In Croce's aesthetic system, harmony belongs to the class of empirical aesthetic concepts that describe qualities of expression rather than independent categories of art.

## Definition

Harmony appears in classical aesthetics as one of the fundamental properties of beauty — "order, harmony, proportion, symmetry" (033-100). It is "harmony in variety" (025-344), the pleasing arrangement of parts within a whole.

Croce treats harmony as a term that describes the successful unity of form and content. When expression succeeds, it produces harmony; when it fails, it produces disharmony. "Qualitative disharmony is as such disagreeable" precisely because it signals an aesthetic failure (035-056).

## In Croce's System

### Harmony as Unity of Expression

Harmony is not a property of external objects but of the aesthetic fact itself. When form and content are perfectly fused, the result is harmony. When they are not, the result is "the violated harmony" — the ugly, the failed expression (032-423).

Kant placed beauty in "the perfect harmony of creatures working in harmony with nature" (026-173). Croce relocates harmony from the object to the spirit: it is not the object that is harmonious but the expression.

### Key Distinctions

1. **Harmony vs. proportion** — Proportion is a mathematical relation; harmony is an aesthetic quality. "Likeness, order, proportion, harmony" are all empirical classifications that describe how expressions affect us, not properties of the expressions themselves (026-386).

2. **Harmony vs. disharmony** — "Qualitative disharmony is as such disagreeable" (035-056). The beautiful is harmony; the ugly is disharmony. But this is just another way of saying that the beautiful is successful expression and the ugly is failed expression.

3. **Harmony as form** — "Melody is matter and harmony form" (027-271). Harmony is the formal principle that organizes the material of expression.

## Historical Context

### Predecessors

Harmony has been a central concept in Western aesthetics since the Greeks. The Pythagoreans found harmony in mathematical proportions; Plato saw it in the order of the cosmos. Leibniz described beauty as "complete beauty, accord and harmony" (025-210). The eighteenth century treated harmony as a property of beautiful objects alongside "unity, variety, harmony, symmetry, simplicity."

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's treatment of harmony follows his general strategy of internalizing aesthetic properties. Harmony is not in the object but in the expression. This move eliminates the need for a separate science of harmonic proportions — there is no formula for harmony because harmony is the individual success of each individual expression.
