# Lyric

In Croce's aesthetics, "lyric" carries a weight far beyond its ordinary use as a genre label. Croce identifies the lyrical character with the essence of artistic intuition itself, transforming "lyric" from one literary kind among many into the universal form of all genuine expression.

## Definition

Croce identifies artistic intuition with lyrical expression. The *Æsthetic* asks how the work of art is to be characterised: "as an expression, as a lyrical intuition" (P03-049).

This identification is foundational: to say that art is expression is to say that art is lyrical, and vice versa. The lyrical character belongs to the very nature of aesthetic activity, not as an optional quality but as its defining feature.

## In Croce's System

### The Lyrical Nature of Art

Croce's claim follows directly from his identity thesis. If intuition equals expression, and expression is always individual and feeling-infused, every genuine expression is necessarily lyrical (002-040).

Even the simplest expressive act — a cry, a sigh — already contains the essence of art. Croce later reinforces this in Part II, noting the lyrical character pervades all artistic production (038-897). The early *Æsthetic* located "the lyrical nature of art" as a topic deferred to philosophy of spirit, but it permeates the entire work (001-056).

### Lyric vs. Epic and Dramatic

The traditional tripartite division of poetry into [[epic]], lyric, and dramatic collapses under Croce's analysis. He argues that the distinction between "subjective from objective, lyric from epic" is a false one (005-151).

Since all expression is at once subjective (expression of feeling) and objective (it takes determinate form), every work is both "lyrical" and "epic" in the traditional senses. The late-nineteenth-century elaboration of "rules for lyrical, dramatical and epic poetry" merely codifies a non-existent distinction (025-050).

## Historical Context

### The Genre Tradition

The lyric/epic/dramatic trichotomy originated in Greek poetics and was systematised through the Renaissance and Enlightenment. By Croce's time, an intricate taxonomy of sub-genres and hybrid forms had accumulated.

Nineteenth-century theorists repeatedly attempted to ground this classification in psychology or metaphysics — efforts Croce dismissed as mistaking empirical convenience for philosophical truth.

### Croce's Innovation

Croce's innovation is twofold. First, he dissolves the lyric/epic/dramatic distinction by showing the lyrical character belongs to all genuine art.

Second, he reinterprets what remains of the distinction in terms of feeling-content. The "lyrical" in the narrow sense refers to expressions where feeling-tone is directly emotive, but this is a difference of degree, not of kind.

## Criticism and Debate

> "Every genuine intuition or representation is also expression. What does not objectify itself in expression is not intuition or representation, but sensation and mere natural fact." — Croce, *Æsthetic* (ch. I)

The identification of art with the lyrical has been challenged by theorists who argue that certain art forms — the novel, architecture, abstract painting — resist being characterised as "lyrical." Defenders respond that "lyrical" here names the expressive character of making, not a particular emotional tonality.
