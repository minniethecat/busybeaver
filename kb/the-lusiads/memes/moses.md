## Overview

Moses is the Hebrew prophet who led the Israelites out of Egypt and received the Law at Mount Sinai. In *The Lusiads*, he appears briefly as part of Camões's strategy of comparing Portuguese achievements to both classical and biblical precedents.

## In the Poem

Moses is referenced once in the poem: "Moses the mighty in past centuries" [SaCkni]. This single allusion places Moses within the catalogue of great figures of antiquity — alongside Alexander, Caesar, and Trajan — whose achievements Portuguese navigators are called upon to surpass.

## Biblical Typology

Moses functions typologically in Camões's scheme. Just as Moses led his people through the [[red-sea|Red Sea]] to the Promised Land, [[vasco-da-gama|Vasco da Gama]] leads the Portuguese through the Indian Ocean to India. The parallel is implicit but powerful: Portuguese maritime exploration is framed as a new Exodus, with da [[vasco-da-gama|Gama]] as a new Moses.

## Classical-Biblical Synthesis

The inclusion of Moses alongside classical figures reflects Camões's syncretic method. The poem draws authority from both classical and biblical traditions — Moses represents the Old Testament dimension of Portuguese providential destiny, while the classical figures represent the imperial tradition that [[portugal|Portugal]] inherits and transcends.
