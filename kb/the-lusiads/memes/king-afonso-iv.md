# King Afonso IV of Portugal

## Overview

King Afonso IV (Dom Afonso IV, 1291–1357) was the seventh king of [[portugal|Portugal]], known as "the Brave" for his military campaigns against [[spain|Castile]]. He appears prominently in Canto III of The Lusiads, where Camões narrates the Portuguese wars against [[spain|Castile]] and the culminating battle of the "Afonsos twain." Yet his martial glory is overshadowed in the poem's emotional economy by a single decision: the execution of [[ines-de-castro|Inês de Castro]], his son Pedro's beloved, which transforms the "brave Afonso" into a tragic figure whose legacy is defined by the consequences of that act.

Afonso IV occupies a pivotal position in the dynastic arc of The Lusiads. He is the figure through whom the Burgundian dynasty reaches both its zenith of martial achievement and its moment of irreparable fracture. Camões treats him with a restraint that borders on ambiguity — his virtues are catalogued without irony, yet the Inês episode, narrated immediately after his triumphs, functions as an implicit indictment. The king who won battles could not win the loyalty of his own son, and his reign, for all its bravery, bequeathed a civil war.

## In The Lusiads

### The Brave King (Canto III)

Camões introduces Afonso IV in the martial chronicle of Canto III, distinguishing him from his predecessors with the epithet "Brave," which becomes the defining word attached to his name throughout the poem:

> This, whom the "Brave Afonso" subjects hight, when fenced about with foes on every side… [003-102]

The distinction matters. Afonso IV is not merely another numbered king in the dynastic sequence but a ruler whose personal courage earns him a qualitative epithet. Camões uses this naming strategy to signal that Afonso IV represents a peak of martial kingship — the kind of ruler whose valour translates directly into national strength.

His legitimacy as a true descendant of the first Afonso (Afonso Henriques, the founder of the kingdom) is explicitly affirmed, a genealogical guarantee that the martial virtues of the founding king have passed unbroken through the generations:

> …princes in whom is seen the "brave Afonso's" offspring true and digne… [003-104]

This insistence on "true and digne" offspring is not merely formulaic. In a poem that will later dwell on the bastard birth of [[king-john-i|John I]] and the legitimacy crises that plagued the Portuguese monarchy, Camões is establishing Afonso IV as a point of genealogical certainty — the undisputed son of an undisputed line. The irony, of course, is that this same certainty of legitimacy is what Afonso IV destroys when he orders the death of his own son's beloved.

### Waging War on Castile (Canto III)

The defining military action of Afonso IV's reign is his campaign against [[spain|Castile]], described with the language of bold chivalry that elevates warfare into a moral enterprise:

> …boldly to debel Castile superb, Afonso went to work her weal. [003-107]

Camões's word choice — "debel" (to conquer or subdue in war) — carries the weight of classical epic vocabulary, placing Afonso IV's campaign in the lineage of Roman conquest narratives. The personification of Castile as "superb" (proud, haughty) frames the war as a contest against arrogance, lending moral justification to Portuguese aggression.

Camões paints a broader portrait of the fourth Afonso as a warrior king of unblemished courage, contrasting his martial purity with the moral complexity that surrounds other kings in the poem:

> …of filial duty nidering the fourth Afonso, yet a brave good King. [003-106]

The phrase "brave good King" is significant. It compresses into three words the ideal of medieval kingship — bravery for war, goodness for peace — and awards both qualities to Afonso IV without reservation. This is the highest praise Camões can bestow on a ruler whose later actions will be so catastrophic for his own family.

### The Battle of Tarifa (Canto III)

The confrontation between the Portuguese and Castilian kings reaches its climax at Tarifa, where the two Afonsos meet on a "memorable plain" that Camões invests with the gravity of an epic battlefield:

> In fine when met the Kings, Afonsos twain, upon Tarifa's memorable plain… [003-117]

The repetition of the name — "Afonsos twain" — creates a mirroring effect. These are two kings who share a name and a martial disposition, locked in symmetrical opposition. The plain becomes "memorable" not because of geography but because of the weight of what happened there: two Christian kings of Iberia, whose armies might have been united against the Marinid threat, instead facing each other in a dynastic contest.

And at court, Afonso towers physically as well as symbolically, his stature a visual metaphor for his royal authority:

> …his Royal state among, valiant Afonso, and his tall form towers by his Queen… [003-116]

The image of Afonso towering among his court — by his queen's side — presents a picture of stable, legitimate rule. This is the Afonso that Camões wants his readers to remember: the king in his majesty, the embodiment of order and strength. The contrast with the absent, shadowy authority who orders Inês's death could not be more stark.

### The Shadow of Inês

Though the Inês episode is narrated in Canto III, Afonso IV is present in it only as the absent authority who orders her death — never named directly in the lyrical passage but felt in every line. The "brave Afonso" thus becomes the poem's most tragic figure of royal authority: a king who acts to protect the realm and instead fractures his own dynasty.

Camões's narrative strategy here is masterful. By narrating Afonso IV's martial triumphs in one register and then shifting to the lyric-tragic mode for Inês's death, without explicitly connecting the two, the poet forces his readers to make the connection themselves. The silence around Afonso's name in the Inês passage is louder than any direct condemnation could be. The king who was so boldly present on the battlefield is conspicuously absent from the death chamber.

## Historical Background

### Early Life and Accession

Afonso IV was born in 1291 to King Denis of Portugal and Elizabeth of Aragon. His relationship with his father was troubled: Denis favoured his illegitimate son Afonso Sanches, creating a rivalry that twice erupted into armed conflict between father and son. This personal history — a son in rebellion against a king who favoured an illegitimate heir — prefigures with bitter irony the conflict Afonso IV would later have with his own son [[pedro-i|Pedro]].

Upon Denis's death in 1325, Afonso IV succeeded to the throne and immediately moved against his half-brother Afonso Sanches, confiscating his lands and titles. This ruthlessness in consolidating power would prove characteristic of his reign.

### War with Castile (1336–1339)

Afonso IV waged a fierce war against Alfonso XI of [[spain|Castile]], provoked by Castilian mistreatment of Afonso's daughter Maria, who was married to the Castilian king. Alfonso XI had taken a mistress, Leonor de Guzmán, and was openly slighting his Portuguese queen. Afonso IV responded by forming an alliance with Castile's enemies and invading.

The war culminated in the Battle of Tarifa (also known as the Battle of Río Salado, 1340), where Portuguese and Castilian forces — remarkably — united against Marinid invaders from Morocco. This was a rare moment of Iberian Christian solidarity, with Afonso IV fighting alongside his former enemy Alfonso XI against a common Muslim threat. Camões's poetic treatment compresses the complex diplomatic reversals of this period into a simpler narrative of Portuguese valour.

### The Black Death and Internal Strife

Afonso IV's reign coincided with the arrival of the Black Death in Portugal (1348), which devastated the population and disrupted the economy. The king's response — enforcing labour laws to prevent peasants from exploiting the labour shortage — generated social tensions that simmered throughout the latter half of his reign.

At court, Afonso's circle was divided between supporters of his son Pedro and the faction that feared Galician influence through [[ines-de-castro|Inês de Castro]]'s family. The Castro family had deep connections to the Castilian nobility, and their growing influence at the Portuguese court — channelled through Pedro's attachment to Inês — raised the spectre of Castilian interference in Portuguese succession.

### The Inês Decision (1355)

Fearing that Pedro's relationship with [[ines-de-castro|Inês de Castro]] would draw [[portugal|Portugal]] into Castilian dynastic conflicts through Inês's powerful Galician family, Afonso IV's councillors — led by Pêro Coelho, Álvaro Gonçalves, and Diogo Lopes Pacheco — persuaded him to order her execution. The decision was presented as a matter of state security: Inês's brothers were ambitious nobles with Castilian ties, and her children by Pedro represented a potential alternate line of succession that could destabilise the kingdom.

In January 1355, while Pedro was away from court, the three councillors travelled to Coimbra, where Inês was staying, and killed her. The murder was brutal and public, intended as a demonstration of royal authority. Instead, it triggered a crisis that nearly destroyed the kingdom.

### Civil War and Death

Pedro, enraged by Inês's death, raised an army and launched a rebellion against his father. For over a year, father and son waged war across northern Portugal, with Pedro's forces burning and pillaging towns loyal to the king. The kingdom, which Afonso IV had spent his reign protecting from external threats, was now being torn apart from within.

Through the intervention of Queen Beatrice, a fragile reconciliation was achieved in 1356. Afonso IV, broken by the conflict with his son, died the following year — having preserved his kingdom's territorial integrity but shattered its dynastic unity. His son Pedro would become the "harshly just" king who avenged Inês with spectacular cruelty.

## Significance

In The Lusiads, Afonso IV embodies the painful paradox of medieval kingship: the same courage that makes him "the Brave" leads him to an act — the execution of Inês — that poisons his legacy. Camões treats him with sympathy but not absolution: his martial virtues are recorded without irony, yet the Inês episode, told immediately after his battles, implicitly condemns him through its emotional force.

He is a king who won wars but lost his son — and through that loss, unwittingly set in motion the events that would produce the bastard [[king-john-i|John of Aviz]] and, ultimately, the dynasty of Portuguese greatness that the poem celebrates. This is the deepest irony of Afonso IV's place in The Lusiads: the king who tried to secure a clean dynastic line by eliminating Inês instead ensured that the Portuguese crown would pass through the very bastard line he feared.

Camões's Afonso IV is thus a tragic figure in the classical sense — a ruler whose greatest strength (his determination to protect the realm) becomes the instrument of his greatest failure. The "brave Afonso" is brave against every enemy except the one that matters most: the consequences of his own choices.

## Notes

The "Afonsos twain" passage at Tarifa is historically notable: Afonso IV of [[portugal|Portugal]] and Alfonso XI of [[spain|Castile]] did indeed meet at the Battle of Río Salado (near Tarifa) in 1340, though there they fought as allies against the Marinid Sultanate of Morocco rather than as enemies. Camões compresses and poeticises the complex Iberian politics of the period, transforming a rare moment of Christian cooperation into a more dramatic confrontation between rival kings.

The epithet "Brave Afonso" distinguishes him from his grandfather Afonso III — and, more importantly, from his son Pedro, whose bravery was of a darker, more terrible cast. The contrast between the father's battlefield courage and the son's vengeful cruelty is one of the unspoken dynamics that gives the Inês episode its emotional power.

Afonso IV's decision to execute Inês de Castro has been debated by Portuguese historians for centuries. Some argue he acted out of genuine concern for the realm's stability; others see the influence of courtiers who feared losing their own power to the Castro family. Camões does not resolve this ambiguity — and perhaps the poem is stronger for leaving it unresolved, allowing Afonso IV to remain a figure of genuine, painful complexity rather than a simple villain.
