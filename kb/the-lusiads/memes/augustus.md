## Overview

Augustus (Gaius Octavius, 63 BC – AD 14) was the first Roman emperor, the adopted son of Julius Caesar, and the victor of the civil wars that ended the Roman Republic. In *The Lusiads*, Camões invokes Augustus's naval victory at Actium (31 BC) as a classical type for Portuguese maritime triumph, and references his role in the Second Triumvirate as an exemplar of political alliance.

## In The Lusiads

Augustus appears twice in *The Lusiads*. In Canto II, during the narrative of Portuguese history, the poet compares the Portuguese fleet's destiny to Augustus's victory at Actium — "when to his Actium-fight Augustus came and laid th' injurious Roman captain low" [002-061]. This passage frames Augustus's naval triumph over Mark Antony and Cleopatra as the classical precedent for Portuguese sea power.

In Canto III, Augustus appears in a more political context: King Pedro I of Portugal is said to have made a pact as harsh as that which "Lepidus made with Anth'ony and Augustus" [003-144] — a reference to the Second Triumvirate (43 BC) that divided the Roman world among the three men.

## Actium as Imperial Model

The Battle of Actium holds special significance in *The Lusiads*. As a naval engagement that determined the fate of an empire, Actium is the closest classical parallel to the Portuguese maritime conquests that Camões celebrates. By invoking Augustus at Actium, the poet claims for Vasco da Gama's voyage a historical weight equal to the founding moment of the Roman Empire. The comparison is not merely literary — it is ideological: Portugal, like Augustan Rome, brings order and civilization through military triumph.

## Notes

The Actium reference in Canto II is part of a larger pattern in *The Lusiads*: Camões consistently frames Portuguese naval victories through classical naval precedents (Actium, Salamis, the Argonauts). This is not mere ornament — it is a sustained argument that Portugal has surpassed the ancients in the very domain (maritime power) where the classical world was strongest. The Triumvirate reference in Canto III, by contrast, is darker: it associates Portuguese political violence with Roman precedent, suggesting Camões's awareness that imperial power has a ruthless face.
