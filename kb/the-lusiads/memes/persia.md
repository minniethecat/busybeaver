# Persia

## Overview

Persia appears in The Lusiads as both an ancient rival and a contemporary landmark. In Canto I, its fame — along with Assyria, Greece, and [[rome|Rome]] — is declared subordinate to the Portuguese achievement. In Canto X, its "noble Empire" is displayed in the prophetic vision as a geographical waypoint on the route to India. Camões treats Persia with the respect due to an ancient civilisation — the Persians appear as "noble" rather than barbarous — but the poem's providential scheme is clear: Persia's glory, like [[rome|Rome]]'s, is now eclipsed by [[portugal|Portugal]]'s.

## Geographical Context

In the sixteenth century, Persia was ruled by the Safavid dynasty (1501–1736), a Shia Muslim empire that controlled much of present-day Iran and contested the Persian Gulf with the Portuguese. [[hormuz|Hormuz]], the strategic island at the mouth of the Gulf, was a Portuguese possession from 1515 to 1622 — a key node in the *Estado da Índia*. The Persian silk trade, carried through [[hormuz|Hormuz]] and Aleppo, was a vital commercial interest.

## In The Lusiads

### The Fame Subordinated (Canto I)

Camões opens with a bold claim — the fame of the ancient empires will be overshadowed:

> …shade shall doom the fame of Persia, 'Assyria, Greece, and Rome. [001-032]

### The Noble Persian (Canto X)

In the prophetic vision, the Persian appears as a figure of nobility brought low:

> But see'ing the noble Persian 'slaved and sway'd by pow'er… [010-065]

### The Persian Strait (Canto X)

Persia's geography anchors the cosmic vision:

> …yond yon other Persian Strait, where sight can trace… [010-116]

And the boundary with [[arabia|Arabia]]:

> …parts Araby from fertile Persia's plain. [010-118]

### The Noble Empire (Canto X)

The vision reaches its climax with the display of Persia itself:

> "Great Persia's noble Empire here behold…" [010-119]

### The Patrial Land (Canto IX)

A passing but evocative reference to Persian flora:

> …while from her patrial Persia-land the Pome flourisheth… [009-066]

## Historical Significance

Portuguese relations with Safavid Persia were complex. The Portuguese held [[hormuz|Hormuz]] as a fortress-factory from 1515, controlling access to the Persian Gulf. Persia was simultaneously a potential ally against the Ottoman Turks ([[portugal|Portugal]]'s great rival in the Indian Ocean) and a commercial partner in the silk trade. The epithet "noble Persian" reflects the respect Safavid Persia commanded in [[europe|Europe]]an eyes — unlike the "Moors" of East [[africa|Africa]], Persians were recognised as heirs to an imperial civilisation.

## Notes

Camões's claim that [[portugal|Portugal]] will "doom the fame of Persia" [001-032] is a conventional piece of epic hyperbole — the poem's prophetic voice declares what historical perspective must temper. Yet the ambition is real: by placing Persia in a list with Assyria, Greece, and [[rome|Rome]], Camões claims for [[portugal|Portugal]] a place in the succession of world empires that was central to Renaissance imperial ideology.
