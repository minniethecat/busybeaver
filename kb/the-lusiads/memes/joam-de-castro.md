## Overview

Dom João de Castro (1500–1548) was a Portuguese nobleman, naval officer, and the fourth Viceroy of Portuguese India. He is renowned both for his military leadership — particularly the defense of Diu in 1546 — and for his pioneering scientific contributions as a navigator and cartographer. His *Roteiros* (sailing directions) are among the earliest works of modern hydrography, containing detailed observations of magnetic variation, coastal geography, and ocean currents.

## In The Lusiads

Camões includes Castro among the illustrious Portuguese commanders invoked in the epic's prophetic and historical passages:

> dread Albuquerque, Castro stark and brave, with more, the Decii of Lusian land, whose Roman gravity of soul
> （001-022）

He appears in the prophetic vision of Canto 10 as one of the future governors of Portuguese India:

> Castro[^note-225] succeeds, who Lusian flag shall plant where tepid waves of Erythraean roll
> （010-083）

The endnote specifies:

> D. Joam de Castro (AD 1545) worthily ends the illustrious list of Portuguese governors.
> （012-225）

## The Defense of Diu (1546)

Castro's most celebrated achievement was the second siege of Diu, where he led a Portuguese garrison against a vastly larger Gujarati-Ottoman force. Despite being outnumbered and undersupplied, Castro's tactical skill and personal courage secured a decisive Portuguese victory. The siege cemented Portuguese control of the Gujarati coast and demonstrated the resilience of the *Estado da Índia* against combined Muslim powers.

## Scientific Contributions

Castro's navigational writings mark a turning point in European understanding of the physical world. During his voyage to India in 1538, he made systematic measurements of magnetic declination — the first European to document how the compass needle varies with longitude. His *Roteiro de Lisboa a Goa* (1538) and *Roteiro do Mar Roxo* (1541) combine practical sailing directions with empirical scientific observation, anticipating the methods of modern oceanography.
