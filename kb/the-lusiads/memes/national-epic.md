## Overview

The national epic is the genre of vernacular poetry that celebrates the foundational myths and heroic achievements of a nation. *The Lusiads* belongs to a Renaissance tradition in which poets sought to create vernacular epics rivalling the classical models of Homer and Virgil.

## Renaissance Competition

By the time Camões began composing *The Lusiads* in the 1550s, Italy had already produced Ariosto's *[[orlando-furioso|Orlando Furioso]]* (1516–1532) and Tasso was at work on *Gerusalemme Liberata*. [[portugal|Portugal]], at the height of its maritime empire, lacked a poetic monument commensurate with its achievements [8m8HAc].

## Classical Models

The national epic tradition is rooted in Virgil's *[[the-aeneid|Aeneid]]*, which connected Augustus to Aeneas and provided a template of *pietas* — duty to gods, family, and state — that Camões adapted for the Portuguese maritime empire [C2CFTT]. Like Virgil, Camões wrote with an implicit commission: to legitimize Portuguese imperial power by tracing its origins to divine destiny.

## Camões's Innovation

Camões distinguished his national epic from its classical predecessors by choosing a subject of contemporary history — [[vasco-da-gama|Vasco da Gama]]'s voyage — rather than mythological antiquity. Where Virgil looked back to the Trojan War and Aeneas's legendary journey, Camões claimed that Portuguese deeds in the living present surpassed all ancient fables.

## Cultural Prestige

The creation of a national epic was, in Renaissance [[portugal|Portugal]], a matter of cultural prestige. Camões explicitly asserts [[portugal|Portugal]]'s parity with ancient [[rome|Rome]] and Greece. The Portuguese navigators are the equals of Aeneas and Odysseus, and by writing their epic, Camões positions Portuguese literature alongside the greatest achievements of classical civilization [7IdeGs].

## Ariosto and the Vernacular Epic

*[[orlando-furioso|Orlando Furioso]]* demonstrated that a vernacular poet could achieve the scale of classical epic while incorporating romance elements — love, madness, and enchantment alongside martial heroism. Camões learned from Ariosto how to make an epic feel contemporary, but he rejected what he saw as the Italian poet's frivolity, insisting on a more serious, providential framework [PRCs4P].
