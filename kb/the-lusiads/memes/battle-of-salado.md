# Battle of Salado

## Overview

The Battle of Salado (1340) was a major engagement of the Reconquista, fought near
Tarifa on the banks of the Salado River. A combined Portuguese-Castilian army under
King Afonso IV of Portugal and King Alfonso XI of Castile decisively defeated a
Marinid invasion force from North Africa. The battle is celebrated in Canto III of
*The Lusiads* as a high point of Portuguese chivalry and a demonstration of Christian
solidarity against a common enemy.

## In The Lusiads

Camões presents the battle through the lens of dynastic alliance. Afonso IV had given
his daughter Maria in marriage to Alfonso XI of Castile. When the Marinids invaded,
Maria appealed to her father for aid:

> "Enter'd Maria, fairest of the fair...
> 'Wherefore, O King! of whom for purest fear'"
> — [003-110–113]

Her plea moves Afonso to marshal Portugal's forces and march to Castile's aid.

## The Battle

The combined Christian army confronted the Marinid host, described as a vast multitude
drawn from "whatever various races Earth hath borne":

> "This proud Castile's bravades with equal pride...
> Ne'er did Semiramis such myriads see"
> — [003-107–108]

The battle was fought with ferocious intensity. Camões emphasizes the martial prowess
and courage of the Portuguese knights:

> "Thus do the Moormen, traitor-souls, despise"
> — [003-120]

The Christian victory was total. The Marinid force was routed, and the threat of a new
Muslim invasion of the Iberian Peninsula was decisively ended.

## The Marinid Threat

The Marinid dynasty, based in Fez, represented the last major North African power capable of projecting military force into the Iberian Peninsula. Under Sultan Abu al-Hasan Ali, the Marinids assembled a large invasion force, crossing the Strait of Gibraltar in 1340 with the aim of reversing the Christian advances of the preceding century. The Marinid strategy relied on exploiting divisions among the Christian kingdoms — a calculation that proved fatal when Afonso IV chose dynastic loyalty over political rivalry. The Portuguese mobilization was swift and decisive: Afonso assembled his forces and marched to join Alfonso XI at Seville, from where the combined army moved south to intercept the Marinid host at the Salado River.

## Significance

The Battle of Salado marked the last major North African attempt to reverse the
Reconquista. By placing the battle within Gama's historical narrative to the King of
Melinde, Camões emphasizes the continuity of Portuguese martial valor — the same
spirit that defeated the Moors at Salado now drives the conquest of the Indian Ocean.

The episode also serves to glorify the House of Burgundy (Afonso IV) before the
narrative transitions to the House of Aviz under King John I.

## Camões's Narrative Strategy

Camões's placement of the Battle of Salado within Canto III is carefully calculated. The canto presents a compressed history of Portugal from its founding to the eve of the Age of Discovery. By including Salado among the foundational victories — alongside [[battle-of-ourique|Ourique]] and [[battle-of-aljubarrota|Aljubarrota]] — Camões constructs a narrative of unbroken Portuguese martial excellence. But Salado serves a specific rhetorical function: it is the battle that demonstrates Portuguese willingness to fight alongside [[castile|Castile]] when Christian solidarity demands it, a theme that resonates with Camões's broader vision of Iberian unity under Portuguese leadership.
