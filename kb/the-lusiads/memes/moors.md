## Overview

The Moors (*Mouros*) in Camões' *The Lusiads* represent the Muslim peoples of North Africa and the Iberian Peninsula. They appear throughout the epic as antagonists — both in the historical context of the Reconquista and in the contemporary encounters of Vasco da Gama's fleet along the East African coast. Camões draws on centuries of Portuguese conflict with Moorish powers to construct them as the archetypal "other" against which Portuguese Christian identity is defined.

## Historical Context

The Moorish presence in Iberia began with the Umayyad conquest of 711 CE and lasted nearly eight centuries. The Portuguese Reconquista — completed in 1249 with the capture of the Algarve — was a foundational element of Portuguese national identity. By Camões' time (1572), the struggle had extended across the Strait of Gibraltar into North Africa, where Portugal maintained a string of coastal fortresses. The Moor is thus both a historical memory and a living adversary in the epic's world.

## Role in The Lusiads

### East African Encounters (Cantos 1–2)

The first direct encounter with the Moors occurs at Mozambique, where Gama's fleet seeks a pilot to guide them across the Indian Ocean:

> the shrewd sagacious Moors pretences made to veil from sight their ill intent;
> （002-017）

> Gama hied to greet gladly the Moors that up the bulwarks plied;
> （002-024）

The Moors of Mozambique initially appear hospitable but plot against the Portuguese — a pattern that recurs at Mombasa:

> So fly the Moors; the Loadsman who alone the Ship could pilot,
> （002-036）

### Battle and Conflict (Canto 3)

In the historical narratives of Canto 3, the Moors are the perennial enemy in Portugal's founding struggles:

> front the foes, those haughty Moors that held the glorious land
> （003-050）

> beside, there were an hundred Moors for every one: Judge any sober man what chance
> （003-051）

The Battle of Ourique and the siege of Lisbon are framed as moments when Portuguese valor overcame overwhelming Moorish numbers.

### Prophetic Vision (Cantos 9–10)

In the prophetic episodes, the Moors appear as future adversaries to be vanquished:

> shall slay four hecatombs of Moors abhor'd.
> （010-036）

## Literary Function

The Moors serve multiple functions in the epic's narrative economy. Historically, they justify Portugal's martial identity. Strategically, their plots against Gama create narrative tension during the African coastal episodes. Ideologically, their opposition reinforces the poem's providential framework — Portuguese success is divinely sanctioned, and Moorish resistance is doomed.

Camões does not give the Moors a voice of their own; they function almost entirely as obstacles to be overcome. This reflects the epic's genre conventions and its nationalist project, but it also registers the lived reality of Mediterranean and Indian Ocean conflict in the 16th century.
