# Arabia

## Overview

Arabia appears in The Lusiads in three interconnected registers: as a source of legendary wealth ("rich Arabia"), as the homeland of the Islamic faith that the Portuguese opposed, and as a geographical landmark on the route to India. Camões's Arabia is both a real place — the peninsula whose coasts the Portuguese navigated — and a symbolic one: the land from which Islam came, whose merchants controlled the spice trade the Portuguese sought to seize, and whose "law unright" stood in opposition to Christian truth.

## Geographical Context

Arabia in Camões's usage encompasses the entire Arabian Peninsula — the [[red-sea|Red Sea]] coast with its ports of Jeddah and Aden, the [[persia|Persia]]n Gulf coast with [[hormuz|Hormuz]] at its mouth, and the interior deserts. The Portuguese primarily interacted with the coastal periphery: Aden (which they attempted to capture), [[hormuz|Hormuz]] (which they held), and the Hadhramaut coast (which they raided). Arabia Felix — "[[fortune|Fortuna]]te Arabia" — was the Roman name for the southwestern region, source of frankincense and myrrh.

## In The Lusiads

### Rich Arabia (Canto III)

Arabia is first named as an emblem of wealth:

> "Though rich Arabia, and the brood ferocious Henioches, and Colchis' strand…" [003-080]

The adjective "rich" is automatic in Camões's usage — Arabia's legendary wealth was a classical commonplace stretching back to Herodotus.

### The Arabian's Law (Canto IV)

The religious dimension of Arabia becomes explicit:

> Follows he not th' Arabian's law unright, an thou wouldst have him for a foe most fell? [004-108]

"Arabian's law" is Islam, and "unright" is Camões's unambiguous judgement on it. This is the crusading register of the poem, unapologetic and direct.

### The Tribute from Mecca (Canto IX)

Arabia's role in the spice trade is noted:

> …that th' Armade due from Arabian Meca year by year, is that which freighteth all our argosies… [009-015]

Mecca — the spiritual and commercial centre of Arabia — is the source of the tribute that fills Muslim ships. The Portuguese ambition is to intercept this flow.

## Historical Significance

Arabia was central to Portuguese strategy in the Indian Ocean. Control of the sea routes around Arabia — the [[red-sea|Red Sea]] to the west, the [[persia|Persia]]n Gulf to the east — would, in theory, allow [[portugal|Portugal]] to strangle the Muslim spice trade and redirect [[asia|Asia]]n goods through [[lisbon|Lisbon]]. The capture of [[hormuz|Hormuz]] (1515), the blockade of the [[red-sea|Red Sea]], and the attempted capture of Aden were all aimed at this objective. Arabia thus represents for Camões not a destination but an obstacle — the territory of the rival faith and the rival trading network his poem is designed to defeat.

## Notes

"Arabian's law unright" [004-108] is one of the poem's most direct statements of religious hostility. The phrase occurs in a speech by an [[africa|Africa]]n king contrasting Christian and Muslim morality, and it reflects the crusading ideology that was still central to Portuguese identity in Camões's time. Yet Arabia is not merely the enemy's homeland — it is also "rich," and that richness, as much as the religious difference, is what makes it a target.
