# Mercury's Mission to the Fleet

## Overview

When Venus learns of Bacchus's plot to destroy the Portuguese fleet at Mombasa through
treacherous Moors, she appeals to Jupiter for aid. Jupiter responds by dispatching
Mercury, his swift herald, to descend to Earth and warn Vasco da Gama in a dream.
This episode in Canto II illustrates the poem's central dynamic of divine intervention
protecting the Portuguese enterprise.

## In The Lusiads

The episode unfolds across three movements. First, Venus descends seaward to save
the fleet after learning of Bacchus's scheme:

> "Descending seawards, Venus saves th' Armade"
> — [002-003]

Venus then appeals to Jupiter, who reveals the future fate of the Portuguese and
agrees to send Mercury:

> "She learns our future lot by Jove's good aid"
> — [002-005]

Mercury descends to the fleet anchored near Mombasa and appears in a vision:

> "appeareth Mercury to Lusus' fleet"
> — [002-006]

## Key Passages

Jupiter's command to Mercury is decisive — the messenger god is sent with divine
authority to intervene in human affairs:

> "This said, he sendeth Maia's son divine"
> — [002-064]

Mercury appears to Gama in a dream, delivering his warning with urgency. The
vision is described with the vividness characteristic of epic dream-visitations:

> "When in a vision Maia's son was seen"
> — [002-069]

Upon waking, Gama responds to the vision with renewed resolve. Mercury's message
has roused the fleet from danger:

> "Mercury thus; and rousèd from his dreams"
> — [002-072]

Gama then speaks to the herald, receiving his divine instructions. Mercury's
message is clear — the fleet must depart immediately or face destruction:

> "Unto the Herald straight our Chief replieth:
> 'What mandate, mighty God, dost Thou command?'"
> — [002-013]

## Significance

Mercury's mission exemplifies the epic machinery of divine intervention that
structures *The Lusiads*. Where Bacchus works through human agents (the treacherous
Moors of Mombasa) to destroy the Portuguese, Venus and Jupiter work through
Mercury to illuminate and protect. The dream-vision is a classical epic convention
that Camões adapts to his Christianised framework.

## Notes

The dream-vision is one of the oldest epic conventions, appearing in the *Iliad*
(where Zeus sends a false dream to Agamemnon) and the *Aeneid* (where Mercury
appears to Aeneas at Dido's court). Camões follows Virgil most closely — both
Mercurys descend with divine commands that redirect the hero's course. The
parallel is deliberate: just as Mercury compelled Aeneas to leave Carthage
for Italy, so Camões's Mercury compels Gama to leave Mombasa for India.
