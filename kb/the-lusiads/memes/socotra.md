# Socotra

## Overview

Socotra is an island in the Arabian Sea, off the coast of the Horn of Africa and south of the Arabian Peninsula, known since antiquity for its unique flora — particularly the bitter aloe (Aloë socotrina). In The Lusiads, it appears in the prophetic vision of Canto X, where [[tethys|Tethys]] reveals to [[vasco-da-gama|Gama]] the future course of Portuguese expansion across the Indian Ocean.

## Geographical Context

Socotra lies approximately 240 km east of the Horn of Africa and 380 km south of the Arabian Peninsula. Historically a stopping point on Indian Ocean trade routes, the island was known to Greek, Roman, Arab, and Indian merchants. Its name likely derives from the Sanskrit *Dvīpa Sukhadhara* ("island of bliss"). The island's bitter aloe was a valued medicinal commodity in medieval and Renaissance pharmacology.

At the time of The Lusiads' composition (1572), Socotra had been visited by Portuguese fleets. The Portuguese briefly occupied the island in 1507 under [[afonso-de-albuquerque|Afonso de Albuquerque]] as part of their strategy to control Indian Ocean trade routes, though the occupation was short-lived.

## In The Lusiads

### Prophetic Vision (Canto X, Stanza 137)

Socotra appears in the great prophetic panorama revealed by Tethys on the [[isle-of-love|Isle of Love]]. She foretells the Portuguese exploration of the Red Sea approaches and the African coast, naming Socotra among the islands that will come under Portuguese influence:

> Eke shalt thou see toforn the Red Sea strait / Socotra, famed for Aloë's bitter growth

The island is presented as one of many territories that Portuguese navigators will bring within their sphere — not through conquest in this case (the occupation was abandoned), but through the knowledge and mapping that the Portuguese voyages produced.
