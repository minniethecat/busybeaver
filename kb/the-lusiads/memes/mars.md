# Mars

## Overview

Mars (Greek: Ares) is the Roman god of war, and in The Lusiads he appears as a figure of concentrated martial energy — the divine embodiment of the military valour that Camões presents as the foundation of Portuguese greatness. Though less prominent as an independent actor than [[venus|Venus]] or [[bacchus|Bacchus]], Mars functions as a pervasive presence throughout the poem: his name is invoked in battle scenes, his "fury" is identified with the spirit of Portuguese conquest, and his mythological lover [[venus|Venus]]'s affection for the Portuguese is partly explained by her love for Mars and the martial character of the nation he personifies. In Camões' providential scheme, Mars represents the active, masculine, world-conquering force that, yoked to Venus's civilizing love, produces empire.

## Classical Background

In Greco-Roman mythology, Mars (Ares) was the son of [[jupiter|Jupiter]] and [[juno|Juno]], and the lover of [[venus|Venus]] — an adulterous pairing famously exposed by Vulcan's net in Homer's *Odyssey* and Ovid's *Metamorphoses*. Unlike his Greek counterpart Ares, who was often portrayed as brutal and unpopular (even Zeus rebukes him in the *Iliad* as "the most hateful of all the gods"), the Roman Mars enjoyed higher status as the father of Romulus and Remus and the tutelary deity of Roman military might.

The Renaissance inherited both traditions. Virgil's Mars appears in the *Aeneid* as the divine ancestor of the Roman people, his union with Venus producing the Julian line. Ovid and Statius provided erotic and epic variations. For Camões, writing a national epic that equated Portuguese achievement with Roman achievement, Mars was a natural choice of patron — but the poet's task was to disentangle the god from his Roman specificity and attach him to Portugal. His solution was elegant: Venus's love for Mars is transferred to the Portuguese, who inherit the god's martial character through her affection rather than through direct patronage.

## Role in The Lusiads

### Martial Patron

Mars first appears in the poem's divine framework as one of the gods whose favour the Portuguese enjoy. The narrator introduces him in the opening canto:

> Who meed of Mars, and grace of Venus gain
> (001-003)

This pairing — Mars and Venus together — is fundamental to Camões' theological design. Neither god alone suffices: Venus without Mars would produce a nation of lovers, not conquerors; Mars without Venus would produce a nation of brutes, not civilizers. The Portuguese are "meed of Mars" — worthy of his reward — specifically because they combine martial courage with the civilizing mission that Venus represents.

In Canto I, as [[venus|Venus]] pleads the Portuguese cause before [[jupiter|Jupiter]], she invokes Mars as a point of comparison:

> And if in change for royal Charles of France,
> or for the famous Cæsar's godlike line,
> thou wouldst not show such partial complaisance,
> much more for these, who are of Mars divine
> (001-041)

The genealogical claim here is important: Venus argues that the Portuguese are figuratively descended from Mars — not biologically, as the Romans claimed through Romulus, but spiritually, through their martial character. This allows Camões to claim Roman prestige without Roman ancestry.

### Historical Echoes

Mars's presence is most strongly felt not in the divine plot but in the historical narrative of Cantos III–IV, where [[vasco-da-gama|Gama]] recounts Portuguese history to the King of [[melinde|Melinde]]. Battle after battle is described in martial terms that invoke the god's presence. The [[battle-of-ourique|Battle of Ourique]] — the founding battle of the Portuguese nation — is presented as a Mars-favoured event:

> The fierce Alfonso, on that glorious day,
> with Mars-like fury urged his men to fight
> (003-030)

The [[battle-of-aljubarrota|Battle of Aljubarrota]] (1385), which secured Portuguese independence from Castile, is similarly martialized:

> each soldier seemed a Mars upon the plain
> (003-039)

Throughout these cantos, Mars functions less as a character than as a quality — his "fury," "rage," and "might" are attributes that Portuguese heroes manifest in battle. This metonymic use of the god — Mars as metonym for martial excellence — is one of Camões' most effective techniques, allowing him to claim divine favour without mounting divine interventions.

### The Storm and Its Aftermath

Mars reappears directly in the divine action during the storm episode (Canto VI). While [[bacchus|Bacchus]] and [[aeolus|Aeolus]] conspire to destroy the fleet, Mars's presence on the Portuguese side is implied through the martial language in which the storm is described:

> Nor Mars himself, with all his furious train,
> could face the fury of that watery war
> (006-059)

This passage is significant: even Mars, the embodiment of martial fury, is outmatched by the storm. The implication is that the Portuguese need more than martial valour to survive — they need the intervention of [[venus|Venus]], whose love calms what Mars's force cannot. This theological hierarchy (Venus > Mars in the poem's economy) is crucial to Camões' argument that Portuguese greatness is founded on love and civilization, not merely conquest.

In Canto X, the final vision of the [[machine-of-the-world|Machine of the World]] culminates in a prophecy of Portuguese military glory across Asia, Africa, and Brazil. Mars is implicitly present throughout this vision as the spirit of the conquests being foretold:

> Mars, with his iron car and fiery steeds,
> shall find in Ganges' waves his burning wheels
> (010-003)

### The Venus-Mars Dyad

The Venus-Mars relationship is the theological core of the poem. In classical myth, their adulterous union produced Harmony (Harmonia). Camões extends this allegory: the union of Love (Venus) and War (Mars) produces Empire, which is a form of political harmony imposed by force. The Portuguese are the children of this union — not literally but spiritually, combining Venus's civilizing mission with Mars's military capacity.

This dyad also explains the poem's gender politics. Venus is feminine, erotic, persuading; Mars is masculine, forceful, imposing. The Portuguese achievement requires both — Venus's diplomatic grace at [[melinde|Melinde]] and [[calicut|Calicut]], Mars's fury at [[battle-of-ourique|Ourique]] and [[battle-of-aljubarrota|Aljubarrota]]. The [[isle-of-love|Isle of Love]] at the poem's climax is the ultimate synthesis: a space where martial heroes receive erotic reward, where Mars's work is completed by Venus's pleasure.

## Camões' Adaptation

Camões' treatment of Mars is distinctive in several respects:

1. **Metonymic, not anthropomorphic**: Unlike [[venus|Venus]], [[bacchus|Bacchus]], or [[jupiter|Jupiter]], Mars rarely appears as a speaking character. He is a quality, an attribute, a standard of comparison — "Mars-like fury," "meed of Mars," "of Mars divine." This metonymic treatment is deliberate: Mars as personification would risk making Portuguese violence look pagan; Mars as metonym preserves Christian decorum while claiming classical prestige.

2. **Subordinate to Venus**: In the poem's divine hierarchy, Mars is subordinate to Venus. His fury cannot calm the storm (Canto VI); her love can. His genius produces conquest; hers produces civilization. This subordination of Mars to Venus is Camões' most original theological move — the claim that love, not war, is the ultimate engine of history.

3. **Genealogical without biology**: Unlike Virgil, who traces Roman ancestry to Venus through Aeneas, Camões cannot claim literal descent from Mars. His solution — "meed of Mars" and "of Mars divine" — is metonymic genealogy: the Portuguese inherit Mars's character, not his blood. This allows Christian orthodoxy (no claim of divine ancestry) while preserving classical prestige.

4. **Venus-Mars as empire allegory**: The Venus-Mars dyad is Camões' most sophisticated mythological argument. Empire = Love + War. Neither alone suffices. This formula, worked out across the poem's entire structure, is Camões' answer to the question that troubled every Renaissance epic: how to reconcile classical form with Christian content.

## Related Deities

- [[venus|Venus]] — Mars's lover and the poem's principal pro-Portuguese deity; together they form the poem's theological dyad
- [[bacchus|Bacchus]] — the anti-Portuguese deity whose schemes Mars's martial spirit helps overcome
- [[jupiter|Jupiter]] — the supreme arbiter who ultimately favours the Portuguese cause
- [[cupid|Cupid]] — Venus and Mars's son in some traditions; Venus's agent at the Isle of Love
- [[neptune|Neptune]] — god of the sea, whose realm Mars's martial valour must cross

## Notes

Mars's role in The Lusiads exemplifies Camões' skill at mythological economy. A lesser poet might have written elaborate divine interventions for the war-god — Mars descending to fight alongside the Portuguese at Ourique or Aljubarrota, as classical gods fight alongside mortals in Homer. Camões resists this temptation. His Mars is a presence felt through language rather than a character driving plot — a stylistic choice that preserves the poem's Christian decorum while saturating its battle narrative with classical resonance.

The PN citations span Cantos I–X, confirming that Mars, though rarely a direct actor, is a continuous presence throughout the poem. His name appears at the beginning (the invocation), in the historical cantos (the battles), in the crisis (the storm), and in the prophetic vision (the future conquests) — a structural arc that mirrors the poem's argument that Portuguese greatness is fundamentally martial in character, even when tempered by Venus's civilizing love.
