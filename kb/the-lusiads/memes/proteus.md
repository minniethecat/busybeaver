# Proteus

## Overview

Proteus is the prophetic sea-god of Greek mythology, known as the "Old Man of the Sea" — a shape-shifter who herds [[neptune|Neptune]]'s seals and speaks truth only to those who can hold him through his transformations. In The Lusiads, Camões draws on this rich mythological tradition while adapting Proteus to the poem's providential framework. Proteus appears at the undersea council in Canto VI, serves as a simile for poetic transformation in Canto VII, and witnesses the Machine of the World vision in Canto X — a trajectory that moves him from antagonist to prophetic ally.

## Classical Background

In Homer's *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]* (Book IV), Proteus is the herdsman of [[neptune|Poseidon]]'s seals on the island of Pharos. Menelaus, instructed by Proteus's daughter Eidothea, seizes the god and holds him through his metamorphoses — lion, serpent, leopard, boar, water, tree — until he resumes his true form and speaks prophecy. In Virgil's *Georgics* (Book IV), the story of Aristaeus also involves binding Proteus for prophetic knowledge. Proteus thus belongs to a distinct mythological type: the reluctant prophet who must be captured to be heard.

## Role in The Lusiads

### The Ocean-Plain (Canto I)

Proteus is first invoked as a geographical marker — his "cattle" are the ocean's creatures:

> …spaces of that ocean-plain where Proteus' cattle cleave his own domain… [001-027]

### The Undersea Council (Canto VI)

Proteus attends the divine council summoned by [[neptune|Neptune]] and [[bacchus|Bacchus]]:

> And, eke, prophetick Proteus thither hied, leaving his herds of Phocas on the main… [006-028]

He is listed "the first and foremost there":

> Proteus the first and foremost there appeared, and with his presence graced the salt-sea reign… [006-044]

### Poetic Transformation (Canto VII)

Camões invokes Proteus as a simile for his own poetic art — the power of metamorphosis:

> …the herd in change of figure Proteus shall exceed. From me, Camena, now what praises claim… [007-093]

This is a striking self-reflection: the poet as shape-shifter, the poem as a bound god forced to speak truth.

### The Cosmic Vision (Canto X)

In Canto X, Proteus witnesses the Machine of the World:

> …those prototypes were shown to Proteus' eyes within the hollow Sphere… [010-015]

### The Final Prophecy (Canto XI)

Proteus speaks directly in Canto XI:

> Prophetic Proteus thus to Neptune cried… [011-136]

## Camões' Adaptation

Camões's Proteus is a figure in transition. In the early cantos, he is a conventional marine deity — part of the mythological furniture of epic sea-poetry. But by Canto VII, he has become a figure for the poet himself: "the herd in change of figure Proteus shall exceed" is Camões claiming that his own metamorphic art surpasses even the god of transformation. By Canto X, Proteus is a privileged witness to cosmic truth. This arc — from antagonist to analogue to ally — mirrors the poem's larger movement: the pagan gods are gradually absorbed into the Christian-providential scheme, their resistance transformed into recognition.

## Notes

The phrase "Proteus the first and foremost there" [006-044] is a tiny but telling detail: Proteus, a relatively minor figure in classical epic, is given precedence at the divine council. Camões may be signalling the special importance of prophecy in his epic scheme — Proteus, the god who knows the future but must be forced to speak it, is the perfect mythological emblem for a poem that binds the past to prophesy the Portuguese empire.
