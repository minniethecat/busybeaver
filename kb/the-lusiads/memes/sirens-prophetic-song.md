# The Siren's Prophetic Song

**The Siren's Prophetic Song** is the extended prophetic performance delivered by a sea-nymph (Siren) during the banquet on the [[isle-of-love|Isle of Love]] in [[canto-x|Canto X]] of *The Lusiads*. As [[vasco-da-gama|Vasco da Gama]] and his sailors feast with their [[thetis|Nereid]] companions, a Siren rises and sings — in the future tense — the deeds of Portuguese viceroys, governors, and captains in India over the coming century. The song transforms the poem's final canto into a verse chronicle of the Portuguese [[asia|Asia]]n empire.

## Overview

The Siren's song follows the pattern of prophetic catalogues in classical epic: a divine being reveals future history to a mortal hero. The innovation is that the content is not myth but recent history — the song names real Portuguese commanders and their real achievements, often in startlingly specific detail. Camões, writing in the 1560s–1570s, was recounting events that his readers would recognise as the history of their own lifetimes.

The song moves chronologically through the governorships of Portuguese India:
- **Francisco de Almeida** (1505–1509), the first Viceroy, and his son Lourenço's death at the Battle of Chaul
- **[[afonso-de-albuquerque|Afonso de Albuquerque]]** (1509–1515), the conqueror of [[goa|Goa]], Malacca, and [[hormuz|Ormuz]], the architect of the Portuguese maritime empire
- Successive governors through to **João de Castro** (1545–1548), the last great viceroy before Camões' present day

## In The Lusiads

The Argument to Canto X introduces the event: "[[tethys|Tethys]] inviteth the Navigators: The Siren's prophetick Song, wherein she toucheth upon the principal achievements and conquests of the Portugueze Viceroys, the Governors, and the Captains in India."

The Siren sings in the elevated style of Camões' own epic verse, with the [[thetis|Nereid]] chorus echoing her refrains. The song spans dozens of stanzas and constitutes one of the longest continuous passages in the poem. Each viceroy receives a capsule biography — a miniature epic within the larger epic — recounting battles won, fortresses built, enemies defeated, and the inevitable cost in Portuguese lives.

> Thus sang the Siren, while her sister-choir accompanied the prophecy with harmonies that seemed to come from the spheres themselves.

## Prophetic Framework

The Siren's song exists within a nested structure of prophecies. Earlier in the poem, [[jupiter|Jupiter]] prophesied Portuguese greatness to the [[the-council-of-gods|Council of the Gods]] (Canto II); the King of [[melinde|Melinde]] heard da [[vasco-da-gama|Gama]]'s retrospective history of [[portugal|Portugal]] (Cantos III–V); and now, on the Isle of Love, the Siren foretells the future. This triple revelation — divine, historical, and prophetic — reinforces the poem's central thesis that Portuguese destiny is willed, enacted, and foreknown at every level of the cosmos.

The song also prepares for the greater revelation that follows: after the Siren finishes, [[tethys|Tethys]] leads da [[vasco-da-gama|Gama]] to the mountain to reveal the [[machine-of-the-world|Machine of the World]], which shows not just the future of Portuguese India but the entire cosmos and all future Portuguese dominions.

## Significance

The Siren's song reveals Camões' deep engagement with the history of his own time. The poet had served in India himself and returned to [[portugal|Portugal]] in 1570 after seventeen years abroad. His firsthand knowledge of the Portuguese East informs the specificity of the song: the names, battles, and political dynamics are not literary invention but reportage transposed into verse.

The song also serves a political function. By listing Portuguese achievements in India in prophetic form, Camões argues that the empire is not a series of disconnected events but the unfolding of a providential plan — a history that, properly understood, has the coherence of epic. The Siren's song is thus a work of imperial historiography disguised as mythological entertainment.
