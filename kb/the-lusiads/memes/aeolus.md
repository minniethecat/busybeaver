# Aeolus

## Overview

Aeolus is the divine keeper of the winds, entrusted by [[jupiter|Jupiter]] with the power to imprison or release the storms. In The Lusiads, he appears as a secondary but significant antagonist — the meteorological arm of [[bacchus|Bacchus]]'s conspiracy against the Portuguese in Canto VI. While he does not speak or act with the vivid personality of [[boreas|Boreas]], his presence is felt wherever the poem registers the capricious violence of the sea. He is, in Camões's scheme, the bureaucratic god of bad weather: summoned, petitioned, and deployed by more active divine agents.

## Classical Background

In Greek and Roman mythology, Aeolus was the son of Hippotes and the ruler of the floating island of Aeolia. Homer describes him in the *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]* (Book X) as the keeper of the winds, who gave Odysseus a bag containing all the winds except the favourable west wind — a gift his crew unwisely opened. Virgil, in the *[[the-aeneid|Aeneid]]* (Book I), depicts Aeolus as subordinate to Juno, who bribes him with a nymph to unleash the winds against Aeneas. Camões follows Virgil's model closely: Aeolus is not an autonomous agent but a force others command.

## Role in The Lusiads

### The Storm God Invoked

Aeolus is first named in a simile for poetic ambition — Camões likens his song to a ship braving "Aeolus' winds that moan and howl":

> …the abysmal sea: In thee from Aeolus' winds that moan and howl, we take our refuge… [002-113]

### The Wild Hills of Aeolus (Canto III)

The winds are associated with Aeolus's personal geography — wild, mountainous terrain:

> …and the wild hills where Aeolus loves to blow… [003-016]

### Angry Tyrannies (Canto V)

The maritime dangers the Portuguese face are "angry Aeolus sea-sent tyrannies":

> …caprices of the Main, of angry Aeolus sea-sent tyrannies… [005-023]

### The Divine Conspiracy (Canto VI)

The most developed episode occurs in Canto VI, where the conspiring sea-gods petition Aeolus:

> Now to great Aeolus they send a sign, as 'twere from heaven's high King… [006-043]

And the winds are unleashed against the Portuguese fleet.

### Wrath and Completion (Canto XI)

In the final canto, Aeolus's rage is paired with [[tethys|Tethys]]'s in an epitome of marine fury:

> Through rage of Aeolus and Tethys' wrath. [011-205]

## Camões' Adaptation

Camões's Aeolus follows the Virgilian model closely — the wind-god is an instrument, not an initiator. Unlike [[boreas|Boreas]], who speaks and loves and resents, Aeolus is a mechanism: his winds are "tyrannies" and his rage is "command" — the language of delegated power rather than personal passion. This is consistent with the poem's larger pattern: the higher a deity's cosmological rank, the more abstract and instrumental their role. Aeolus governs the winds, but [[bacchus|Bacchus]] governs Aeolus — and the poem's sympathies are, ultimately, with the ships that survive him.

## Notes

The phrase "Aeolus' winds that moan and howl" [002-113] is typical of Camões's sonic sensitivity — he hears the wind as voice before he names it as force. The epithet "great" in "great Aeolus" [006-043] is conventional but functions ironically: for all his greatness, Aeolus is never more than a functionary in the poem's divine hierarchy, and his winds, however terrible, are always ultimately stilled by a higher power.
