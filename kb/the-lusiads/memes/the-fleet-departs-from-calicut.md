# The Fleet Departs from Calicut

## Overview

The departure from Calicut opens Canto IX of *The Lusiads* and marks the transition from
the fraught diplomatic episode in India to the mythic return voyage. After being detained
by the Catual and navigating the intrigues of the Muslim merchants, Vasco da Gama finally
secures his release and sets sail for Portugal, carrying the news that would transform
European commerce and geopolitics.

## In The Lusiads

Camões frames the departure as a liberation — Gama "fareth from Calecut the Capitayne,
bearing glad tidings of the Orient" ([009-001]). The Portuguese had been "long remainèd
pent" within Calicut, unable to trade because the Muslim merchants "made every trader cease
from trade and gain" ([009-009]). The merchants' strategy was to detain the Portuguese
until "th' expected Fleet of Mecan vessels" could arrive and destroy them ([009-009]).

## The Moorish Conspiracy and Divine Intervention

The Muslim merchants had placed their hopes on the annual fleet from Mecca (Jeddah/Gidà),
which sailed to the Malabar Coast each year carrying spices and goods:

> "Upon these ships firm hopes the Moors had set,
> e'en as their puissance was so much the higher,
> that these who sought their gains so grateful great,
> they might consume with crepitating fire"
> — [009-012]

Their plan was simple: delay the Portuguese until the heavily-armed Mecca fleet arrived,
then destroy them. However, Camões presents the Portuguese escape as an act of divine
providence:

> "But He who rules the Heav'ens and human race,
> who for whatever willèd hath His will,
> the fittest causes from afar doth trace
> which shall His provident effects fulfil"
> — [009-013]

The instrument of divine intervention is Monsaydé, the Moor who had befriended the
Portuguese upon their arrival. Monsaydé warns Gama of the plot, enabling the fleet to slip
away before the Mecca fleet arrives:

> "pitiful accidents of ruth and grace
> dealt to Monsaydé, who, with guarded skill,
> devoted self Da Gama to advise"
> — [009-013]

## Transition to the Mythic Realm

The departure from Calicut serves as the narrative hinge between the realistic — and often
harsh — depiction of Indian Ocean politics in Cantos VII–VIII and the mythic apotheosis
that follows. No sooner has Gama escaped the merchants' plot than [[venus|Venus]] prepares
her reward:

> "To whom, amiddle of the tumid Main,
> Venus displays an Island excellent"
> — [009-003–004]

The [[venus-prepares-isle-of-love|Isle of Love]] that Venus conjures for the weary
mariners represents the poem's turn from history to mythology, from human intrigue to
divine sanction. The departure thus closes the Indian episode and opens the poem's most
celebrated fantastical passage.

## Significance

The departure from Calicut dramatizes the central tension of the Portuguese encounter with
the Indian Ocean: the established Muslim commercial networks recognized the Portuguese as
an existential threat and mobilized to destroy them. Gama's escape — attributed to both
Monsaydé's human loyalty and divine providence — validates the Portuguese enterprise as
divinely favored. The episode also establishes the narrative logic that after suffering
(the torrid zone, the storms, the Catual's detention), the navigators earn their reward
(the Isle of Love and the prophetic vision of Canto X).
