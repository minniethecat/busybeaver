# Mecca

## Overview

Mecca, the holiest city of Islam, appears in The Lusiads not as a destination of Portuguese conquest but as a geographical and religious landmark in the landscape of the Middle East. Its inclusion in the poem's survey of the Red Sea region serves to map the territories of the Islamic world that Portuguese navigation had outflanked — by rounding [[africa|Africa]] and entering the Indian Ocean directly, the Portuguese bypassed the traditional Islamic-controlled overland routes to Asia.

## Geographical Context

Mecca lies in the arid Hijaz region of the Arabian Peninsula, approximately 80 kilometres inland from the Red Sea coast. Its religious significance — as the birthplace of the Prophet Muhammad and the site of the Kaaba, toward which Muslims direct their prayers — made it the single most important city in the Islamic world. The annual pilgrimage (Hajj) brought tens of thousands of travellers through the Red Sea ports, creating a network of trade and communication that the Portuguese sought to intercept.

By the time of [[vasco-da-gama|Vasco da Gama]]'s voyage, Mecca was under the suzerainty of the Mamluk Sultanate of [[egypt|Egypt]], which controlled the [[red-sea|Red Sea]] and the lucrative spice trade that passed through it. The Portuguese strategy of sailing around [[africa|Africa]] to reach India was, in part, a response to Mamluk and Venetian control of this Red Sea route — a route that ran from the Indian Ocean to the Red Sea to [[egypt|Egypt]] and then to [[venice|Venice]] and [[europe|Europe]].

## In The Lusiads

Mecca is not named directly in the poetic text of The Lusiads, but it appears within the poem's geographical frame through two related references. In Canto IX, the poet surveys the Red Sea from [[suez|Suez]] (the ancient Arsinoë) southward, encompassing the Arabian coast. The city's wider cultural and geopolitical context — the Islamic world whose trade the Portuguese disrupted — is present throughout the poem.

In Canto X [010-114], the shore of the [[red-sea|Red Sea]] and the Arabian interior are surveyed in a prophetic vision shown to Gama:

> There, where the City crowns the Red Sea bight
> ...Arsinoë hight, to Suez changèd in our modern day;
> Then mark the lofty Ethiopian height;
> Then the Arabian shore, the Persian bay;
> The coast of Araby the Blest survey,
> whence frankincense and myrrh were sent away.

The "Arabian shore" and "Araby the Blest" frame the territory of which Mecca is the spiritual and commercial heart. The reference to frankincense and myrrh — the ancient trade goods of Arabia — evokes a longue-durée history of Arabian commerce that Portuguese navigation sought to redirect.

## The Portuguese and the Red Sea

The Portuguese ambition to control the Red Sea trade — and thereby deny Mecca and the Islamic world the wealth of the spice route — was a recurring theme of Portuguese Indian Ocean strategy. [[afonso-de-albuquerque|Afonso de Albuquerque]] planned an attack on Mecca itself as part of a broader scheme to destroy Islamic power at its source, though this plan was never realized.

The Portuguese succeeded in blockading the Red Sea for much of the sixteenth century, diverting the spice trade around the Cape and enriching [[lisbon|Lisbon]] at the expense of [[venice|Venice]], [[egypt|Egypt]], and the Red Sea ports. Mecca, while never directly threatened, felt the economic consequences of this shift.
