## Overview

The epic simile — the extended comparison that pauses narrative for elaborate analogy — is one of the most distinctive formal features of *The Lusiads*. Camões inherited this device from Homer and Virgil but transformed it by drawing his analogies from maritime experience and Portuguese history rather than the agricultural and pastoral imagery of his classical models.

## Classical Inheritance

Homer's similes drew on the natural world of the Aegean — lions, boars, storms at sea, shepherds. Virgil adapted the form to Roman material. Camões inherits both traditions but replaces their imagery with the world of Portuguese navigation: ships, storms, sea-monsters, and distant shores.

## Maritime Imagination

Camões's similes draw on the maritime world that defines his epic. Where Homer compares warriors to lions, Camões compares ships to sea-birds and navigators to argonauts. This shift in imagery is not merely decorative — it asserts that the Portuguese maritime experience is a subject worthy of epic treatment on its own terms.

## Structural Function

The epic simile in *The Lusiads* serves multiple functions: it elevates the narrative tone by invoking the classical tradition; it provides moments of reflection within the forward momentum of the voyage; and it connects the particular events of da [[vasco-da-gama|Gama]]'s journey to universal patterns of human experience.

## Camões's Innovation

Camões's most distinctive innovation is the historical simile — comparing Portuguese achievements not to natural phenomena but to events from Portuguese history. This self-referential technique reinforces the poem's central claim that Portuguese history is its own adequate epic subject, requiring no mythological embellishment.
