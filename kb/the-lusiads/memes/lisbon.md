# Lisbon

## Overview

capital of [[portugal|Portugal]] and starting point of [[vasco-da-gama|Vasco da Gama]]'s voyage. Lisbon occupies a commanding position on the north bank of the [[tagus-river|Tagus]] river estuary, where the river widens into the Atlantic Ocean. In the Renaissance imagination, Lisbon was not merely a city but the symbolic gateway between the known European world and the unknown expanses of the maritime frontier (PN 001-009, PN 004-092).

Camoes names the city directly and also by classical allusion: as **Ulyssea** ("Ulysses' city"), invoking the founding legend that the Homeric hero [[ulysses|Ulysses]] built the city after his wanderings (PN 008-013). This mythic genealogy elevates Lisbon beyond a mere capital to the status of an epic city, worthy heir to the traditions of [[troy|Troy]] and [[rome|Rome]] (PN 001-011).

## Geography

Lisbon is situated at 38 degrees 43 minutes north latitude on the western edge of the Iberian Peninsula, at the mouth of the [[tagus-river|Tagus]] river (the "well-loved Tagus" of Canto X, PN 010-008). The city occupies a series of seven hills rising from the riverbank, a topography that Renaissance geographers likened to [[rome|Rome]].

The Restelo anchorage near **Belem** (Bethlehem), west of the city centre, served as the traditional departure point for Portuguese maritime expeditions. From this beach, [[vasco-da-gama|Vasco da Gama]]'s fleet of four vessels cast off in July 1497 (PN 004-095). The Hieronymite monastery of Belem, later built on the site by King [[king-manuel-i|Manuel I]], commemorates the voyage. The Belem Tower, erected as a fortress in the Tagus estuary, became the symbolic face of Lisbon to arriving and departing ships -- the last sight of [[portugal|Portugal]] for outward-bound sailors and its first for returning mariners.

Camoes describes the departure scene with the fleet "in famed Ulyssea's haven man'd" (PN 004-092), the city's waterfront crowded with soldiers, families, and weeping onlookers as the ships prepared to sail into the unknown (PN 004-093, PN 004-096).

## Role in The Lusiads

Lisbon appears in multiple distinct registers throughout *The Lusiads*:

### As Narrative Frame

The poem opens with Camoes invoking not the Muses of classical [[greece|Greece]] but the **Tagian Nymphs** (Tagides) -- the river-spirits of the Tagus (PN 001-012). This deliberate substitution of local for classical deities signals the poem's ambition: to found a Portuguese epic tradition rooted in its own geography rather than borrowed from antiquity. The poet places himself on the banks of the Tagus, and the entire narrative of [[vasco-da-gama|Vasco da Gama]]'s voyage flows outward from this Lisbon-centric perspective (PN 001-009, PN 001-011).

The poem closes symmetrically in Canto X as the fleet returns from [[india|India]]: "Homewards her well-loved Tagus flies to greet" (PN 010-008). This ring composition, beginning and ending at Lisbon's river, underscores the city's role as both point of departure and point of return -- the fixed centre around which the entire epic voyage revolves.

### As Epic Apostrophe

In Canto III, during [[vasco-da-gama|Vasco da Gama]]'s historical narration to the King of [[melinde|Melinde]], Camoes delivers one of the poem's most striking apostrophes:

> And thou, O noble Lisbon! thou encrown'd
> Beyond Germanic Albis and the Rhene,
> (PN 003-065-066)

This rhetorical address belongs to the account of King Afonso Henriques's siege and capture of [[lisbon]] in 1147, when the city was still under Moorish control (PN 003-065-068). The apostrophe -- addressing the city directly as "thou" -- is a moment of intense emotional engagement with place. The siege lasted five months ("Five sequent times her front had Luna veiled," PN 003-067), and the city's capitulation is presented as a foundational event in [[portugal|Portuguese]] national identity: the moment when Lisbon became definitively Christian and Portuguese (PN 003-068).

### As Founding Legend

In Canto VIII, when [[vasco-da-gama|Vasco da Gama]]'s crew examine a series of painted banners depicting Portuguese history, one banner depicts:

> Ulysses 'tis who builds that sacred fane
> To her, the glorious Dardan matron
> (PN 008-013)

This passage explicitly invokes the legend that **Ulysses** ([[ulysses|Odysseus]]) founded Lisbon during his post-Trojan wanderings, building the first temple on what would become the city's hill. The "sacred fane" refers to a shrine to [[athena|Athena]] (Minerva), from which the city's Roman name **Olissipo** was said to derive -- a folk etymology linking **Ulixes** (Ulysses' Latin name) + **Ippo** (city). Camoes' inclusion of this legend in the painted banners of Portuguese history inscribes Lisbon into the genealogy of classical epic, making it not merely the starting point of a voyage but a city founded by one of literature's greatest wanderers.

### As Court and Stage

Lisbon provides the backdrop for key moments in the poem's embedded history. In Canto IV, King [[king-manuel-i|Manuel I]] receives prophetic dreams and deliberates in the palace at Lisbon before commissioning the [[vasco-da-gama|da Gama]] expedition (PN 004-084). The city's significance as seat of royal authority -- the "noble City and sure seat" (PN 003-071) of Portuguese kings -- makes it more than a geographic location: it is the locus of the political and spiritual will that drives the poem's action.

## Historical Context

The settlement that became Lisbon was known to the **Phoenicians** and **Carthaginians** as *Alis Ubbo* ("safe harbour"). The Romans called it **Felicitas Julia Olissipo**, and under Julius [[caesar|Caesar]] it became a *municipium*. The Suevi and Visigoths controlled it before the [[moorish-iberia|Moorish]] conquest in the early 8th century.

In 1147, King **Afonso Henriques** -- the first king of [[portugal|Portugal]] -- captured Lisbon from the Moors with the aid of crusaders en route to the Holy Land (PN 003-065-068). The city became the capital of the Portuguese kingdom in 1255, replacing Coimbra, and grew wealthy through maritime trade.

By the time of [[vasco-da-gama|Vasco da Gama]]'s voyage in 1497, Lisbon was one of Europe's great mercantile cities. Its waterfront at the **Ribeira das Naus** housed shipyards and warehouses serving the African and Indian trades. The city's cosmopolitan character -- Jewish, Muslim, and African communities alongside the Portuguese -- reflected its role as a hub of the early modern global economy.

Camoes himself likely knew the city intimately. He departed from the Restelo anchorage as a young soldier bound for [[india|India]]; after decades in the East, he returned to publish *The Lusiads* at Lisbon in 1572 (PN 001-009).

## Legacy

Lisbon's portrayal in *The Lusiads* helped fix the city in the European literary imagination as the gate to the world. [[luis-de-camoes|Camoes]] invested the city with a double character: it was simultaneously the real, bustling port of the 16th century and a mythic space rooted in Homeric genealogy.

### Literary and Cultural Legacy

The identification of the Tagus with poetic inspiration -- established in the opening invocation (PN 001-012) -- became a durable trope in Portuguese letters. From the **Saudosismo** of [[fernando-pessoa|Fernando Pessoa]] to the modern city-poems of **Alberto de Lacerda** and **Sophia de Mello Breyner Andresen**, Lisbon-as-epic-city remained a productive literary topos. The Belem waterfront, specifically, became the symbolic centre of Portuguese maritime memory: the Jeronimos Monastery -- built on the profits of the spice trade that da Gama inaugurated -- houses the tombs of both [[vasco-da-gama|Vasco da Gama]] and [[luis-de-camoes|Luis de Camoes]].

### Political and Imperial Legacy

The poem's treatment of Lisbon as the seat of a divinely favoured empire (PN 001-009-012) was instrumental in the development of Portuguese imperial ideology. The **Manueline architectural style**, exemplified in the Belem Tower and Jeronimos Monastery, translated the poem's vision into stone: maritime motifs (ropes, armillary spheres, sea monsters) in ecclesiastical architecture, fusing the religious and imperial claims that *The Lusiads* articulated. Under the **Estado Novo** dictatorship (1933-1974), *The Lusiads* was deployed as a national symbol, and the poem's Lisbon-centric vision was selectively mobilised in nationalist propaganda.

### Counter-Current

Yet the poem also contains the counter-voice. The **Old Man of Restelo**, who denounces the imperial enterprise from the beach of Belem (PN 004-095, PN 004-102-104), stands as a permanent internal critic -- a voice spoken from the very sand of Lisbon against what Lisbon had launched. This tension between celebration and critique, departure and return, has given the city's role in the poem a complexity that resists simple patriotic appropriation.

## Notes

Camoes invests this location with both realistic detail and symbolic resonance, and in doing so transforms a physical city into one of the great epic capitals of European literature.
