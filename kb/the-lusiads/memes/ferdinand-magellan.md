# Ferdinand Magellan

## Overview

Ferdinand Magellan (Fernão de Magalhães, c. 1480 – 27 April 1521) was a Portuguese navigator who, after being refused by [[king-manuel-i|King Manuel I]] of [[portugal|Portugal]], offered his services to [[spain|Spain]] and led the expedition that achieved the first circumnavigation of the Earth (1519–1522). Camões mentions Magellan in [[tethys|Tethys]]'s prophetic vision in Canto X, acknowledging his achievement while noting that he sailed under a foreign flag — an ambiguous tribute that reflects Portuguese ambivalence about the navigator who served a rival crown.

## In The Lusiads

### The Prophetic Vision (Canto X)

Magellan appears in the climactic vision where [[tethys|Tethys]] reveals future Portuguese glories to [[vasco-da-gama|Vasco da Gama]]. The reference is pointedly brief:

> ...seeking Earth's extremest bound Magellan who, good sooth, by birth shall be a Portingall, but not in loyalty... [010-156]

Camões acknowledges Magellan's Portuguese birth and his supreme navigational achievement — "Earth's extremest bound" — while carefully noting his defection to [[spain|Spain]]. The phrase "not in loyalty" is the poet's quiet reproach: Magellan's achievement belongs to geography, not to [[portugal|Portugal]].

The endnotes to the Burton translation confirm Magellan's significance:

> Magalhaens (Magellan), Canto X, 138. [012-034]

And reference his circumnavigation:

> Magellan's circumnavigation. [012-282]

## Historical Context

Magellan's biography is a study in the tensions of the Age of [[maritime-exploration|Discovery]]:

1. **Portuguese service**: He served [[portugal|Portugal]] in India under [[afonso-de-albuquerque|Afonso de Albuquerque]], fighting at the Battle of Diu (1509) and the conquest of Malacca (1511).
2. **Fall from favour**: After being wounded in Morocco, Magellan fell into disfavour with [[king-manuel-i|King Manuel I]], who refused his proposal for a westward voyage to the Spice Islands.
3. **Spanish service**: Magellan offered his services to Charles V of [[spain|Spain]], proposing to reach the Moluccas by sailing west — avoiding Portuguese-controlled eastern routes.
4. **The voyage (1519–1522)**: He discovered the Strait of Magellan, crossed the Pacific (which he named), and reached the Philippines, where he was killed at the Battle of Mactan (1521). Juan Sebastián Elcano completed the circumnavigation.
5. **The achievement**: The expedition proved the global extent of the oceans, the true circumference of the Earth, and the feasibility of circumnavigation — achievements that fundamentally reshaped [[europe|Europe]]an understanding of the world.

## Key Passage

**[[tethys|Tethys]]'s ambiguous tribute (Canto X)**:

## Significance

Magellan encapsulates the central tension of *The Lusiads*: the conflict between national loyalty and individual achievement. Camões cannot ignore Magellan — his circumnavigation was the supreme navigational feat of the age, surpassing even [[vasco-da-gama|Gama]]'s — but he cannot fully claim him either, because Magellan sailed for [[spain|Spain]]. The brief, carefully qualified mention is a masterpiece of poetic diplomacy, acknowledging greatness while withholding full national ownership.

## Notes

Magellan's defection to [[spain|Spain]] was a source of lasting Portuguese resentment. Camões's treatment — brief, qualified, but fundamentally respectful — reflects a mature recognition that navigational achievement transcends national rivalry. The phrase "Earth's extremest bound" is, in its way, the highest compliment the poem pays to any navigator: Magellan, not [[vasco-da-gama|Gama]], is the one who reached the literal limit of the world.
