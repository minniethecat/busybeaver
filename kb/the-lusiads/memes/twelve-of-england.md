# The Twelve of England

## Overview

The Twelve of England (*Os Doze de Inglaterra*) is a chivalric episode recounted in Canto VI of *The Lusiads*. During a lull in the storm, a Portuguese soldier tells the tale of twelve Portuguese knights who sailed to England to defend the honour of twelve English ladies insulted by twelve English knights — a story of medieval courtly valour that Camões uses to illustrate Portuguese character across the ages. The episode is one of the poem's few purely medieval chivalric digressions, contrasting with the classical and Renaissance registers that dominate the epic.

## In The Lusiads

### The Narrative Frame (Canto VI)

The episode is told by Fernão Veloso, a soldier aboard [[vasco-da-gama|Gama]]'s fleet, to entertain the crew during a respite from the storm. The storytelling frame echoes Boccaccio and Chaucer — tales told to pass the time during a journey — and gives Camões an opportunity to weave medieval romance into his classical epic.

### The Challenge

Twelve English ladies, ladies-in-waiting to the Duchess of Lancaster, are insulted by twelve English knights who refuse to defend their honour. Unable to find English champions, the ladies appeal to the Duke of Lancaster, who sends a request to [[portugal|Portugal]] for twelve knights to fight on their behalf.

Twelve Portuguese knights volunteer and sail to England. The poem introduces them with the formal roll-call convention of epic:

> ...and of his bravest friends twelve names he quotes... [006-058]

### The Combat

The combat takes place in London before the English court. Camões describes the uneven contest — eleven English knights fall, leaving the twelfth to face the Portuguese champion:

> ...a quaint dissonance, to twelve eleven matched: Begins the cruel fight... [006-069]

The Portuguese triumph, and the twelve ladies' honour is restored. The victorious knights return to [[portugal|Portugal]] with fame and royal favour.

### The Epic Context

The Twelve of England is recalled in the invocation of Canto I as an example of Portuguese valour worthy of epic commemoration:

> ...for the twelve peerless Peers again I cite the deeds of ancient days... [001-020]

This early mention signals Camões's intention to integrate medieval chivalric material into the classical epic framework — part of his broader project of claiming for [[portugal|Portugal]] a heroic tradition equal to those of Greece and [[rome|Rome]].

## Historical and Literary Sources

The Twelve of England is a well-known episode in Portuguese medieval chronicles, particularly Fernão Lopes and Gomes Eanes de Zurara. Camões treats it as authentic history, though modern scholarship questions its literal truth. The episode belongs to the same chivalric tradition as the Arthurian Round Table and Charlemagne's Twelve Peers — Camões explicitly links them in the Canto I invocation.

The episode serves as:
1. **Chivalric interlude**: A narrative break from the voyage's geographical and mythological episodes.
2. **National character portrait**: Portuguese courage is shown to transcend the Age of [[maritime-exploration|Discovery]] — it was already legendary in the Middle Ages.
3. **Generic hybridisation**: Camões demonstrates that medieval romance material can coexist with classical epic within a single poem.

## Significance

The Twelve of England serves multiple functions in the poem's architecture:

1. **Generic range**: It demonstrates Camões's mastery of medieval romance within the classical epic frame — a synthesis unique in Renaissance poetry.
2. **Temporal depth**: Portuguese heroism is shown to extend from ancient [[portugal|Lusitania]] through the Middle Ages to the present voyage — a continuous tradition.
3. **Audience appeal**: The chivalric episode would have pleased a courtly readership familiar with romances of chivalry, balancing the poem's more demanding classical and historical passages.
4. **Moral argument**: The episode reinforces the poem's central claim that Portuguese valour is innate and timeless, manifesting in every era.

## Notes

The episode's placement in Canto VI — inserted as a story-within-a-story during the storm — is a structural echo of the *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]* (Odysseus's tales at the Phaeacian court) and the *[[the-aeneid|Aeneid]]* (Aeneas's narration at Dido's banquet). Camões's innovation is the generic shift: where Homer and Virgil tell stories of war and wandering, Camões tells a story of courtly romance, expanding the epic's generic range.
