# Madeira

## Overview

Madeira is a Portuguese Atlantic island, settled in the early 15th century and renowned for its sugar and wine production. In The Lusiads, it appears as a landmark on the outward voyage of [[vasco-da-gama|Vasco da Gama]]'s fleet — one of the Portuguese Atlantic possessions that demonstrated the nation's mastery of oceanic navigation and colonial enterprise before the fleet turned southward toward the [[cape-of-good-hope|Cape of Good Hope]].

## Geographical Context

Madeira is a volcanic archipelago in the North Atlantic, approximately 700 kilometres off the coast of [[africa|Africa]]. Discovered by Portuguese navigators in 1419 under the patronage of [[prince-henry-the-navigator|Prince Henry the Navigator]], the island was rapidly settled and cultivated. Its volcanic soil and subtropical climate proved ideal for sugar cane — introduced from the Mediterranean — and later for wine production. By the time of Gama's voyage (1497–1499), Madeira was already a prosperous Portuguese settlement and a model for the colonial sugar economy that would later be transplanted to [[brazil|Brazil]].

Madeira's strategic position — off the African coast but firmly Portuguese — made it a natural provisioning stop for ships bound for the [[cape-of-good-hope|Cape of Good Hope]]. The island represented the successful pattern of Portuguese Atlantic colonisation: discovery, settlement, agricultural exploitation, and integration into the maritime network.

## In The Lusiads

Madeira receives a single brief mention in Canto V, as part of Gama's narrative to the King of [[melinde|Melinde]]. The admiral describes the Atlantic leg of the voyage, cataloguing the familiar Portuguese islands the fleet passed before entering the unknown southern seas:

> Hard by the great Madeiran Isle we past
> — [005-013]

The line is part of a geographical sequence that includes the [[azores|Azores]] and references to the North African coast. Its brevity is characteristic of the poem's treatment of already-Portuguese territory: the settled Atlantic islands require no extended description, their prosperity and Portuguese character taken for granted. The word "great" — the only adjective applied to Madeira — acknowledges the island's economic importance and its status as a Portuguese achievement.

The passage situates Madeira in the context of the fleet's departure from the known world into the unknown. After passing Madeira, the [[azores|Azores]], and the coast of North Africa, the fleet sailed into waters that no European ship had charted — the South Atlantic, where the Pole Star disappeared below the horizon and new constellations took its place.

## Historical Significance

Madeira's inclusion in The Lusiads, however brief, reflects its importance in the Portuguese colonial system. The island was the first major success of Portuguese overseas settlement, establishing the economic model — plantation agriculture worked by imported labour — that would define Atlantic colonialism. By the 1490s, Madeira was Europe's leading sugar producer, a role it would cede to [[brazil|Brazil]] in the following century.

The island's most famous product — Madeira wine — emerged slightly later than Gama's voyage, but the wine trade reinforced Madeira's role as a vital node in the Atlantic trading network. Ships bound for the East Indies, Brazil, and the Caribbean all called at Madeira, and the island's wine became a global commodity.
