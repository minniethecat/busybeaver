# Catual

## Overview

The Catual (from Malayalam *kāttal* or Portuguese *catual*, meaning governor or chief magistrate) was the high official of the [[samorim|Samorim]] (Zamorin) of [[calicut|Calicut]] who served as the Portuguese expedition's primary point of contact — and obstruction — during their stay in the Malabar port.

In The Lusiads, the Catual appears in Cantos VII and VIII as a complex antagonist figure: not a warrior-enemy like the [[bacchus|Moorish agents]] who plot against the Portuguese, but a bureaucratic obstacle whose greed and suspicion repeatedly delay Gama's mission. Camões uses the Catual to dramatize the cultural and commercial friction of first contact between Europe and India — the difficulty, as the poet sees it, of dealing with peoples who do not share Christian truth or Portuguese honor.

## In The Lusiads

### First Appearance (Canto VII)

The Catual first appears in the prose argument to Canto VII, which describes his visit to the Portuguese fleet:

> The Catual, or Governor of Calecut, fareth to see the Fleet.

This initial visit is described in detail (007-035). The Catual arrives at the ships with a retinue, inspecting the Portuguese vessels with a mixture of curiosity and suspicion. Gama receives him with diplomatic courtesy, displaying Portuguese wealth and power through gifts and naval display.

The encounter establishes the essential dynamic of the Calicut sequence: Gama represents Christian truth and Portuguese honor, seeking peaceful trade and alliance; the Catual represents a mercantile world where everything — access, favor, justice — has its price.

### The Detention (Canto VIII)

The Catual's most significant appearance comes in Canto VIII (008-001), where he detains Gama on shore while the fleet waits in the harbor. The detention is motivated by greed — the Catual expects gifts and bribes before allowing Gama to return to his ships (008-043):

> where by that greedy Catual 'tis tane

The merchandise that Gama has landed as samples and gifts for the Samorim is seized by the Catual, who uses it as leverage (007-058). Gama's men, [[alvaro|Álvaro]] and [[diogo|Diogo]], are left to guard the goods but cannot prevent the Catual's appropriation.

The detention creates a crisis that is resolved only through the intervention of the Moor [[monsayde|Monsayde]], who negotiates Gama's release. The entire episode serves Camões' larger polemical purpose: to contrast honorable Portuguese conduct with what he portrays as Oriental duplicity and corruption.

### Character Analysis

Camões' portrayal of the Catual is not ethnographic but ideological. The Catual is less an individual than a type — the "greedy" Oriental official who obstructs Christian commerce. This characterization serves several functions in the poem:

1. **Dramatic obstacle**: The Catual provides narrative tension, delaying Gama's mission and creating suspense
2. **Moral contrast**: His greed highlights Portuguese honor and Christian virtue
3. **Political argument**: The obstruction of Portuguese trade by local officials justifies the establishment of Portuguese forts and direct rule — a recurring theme in Camões' imperial vision (007-040)

## Camões' Imperial Vision

The Catual episode is one of the poem's most concentrated expressions of its imperial ideology. Camões presents the Portuguese as envoys of both commerce and Christianity, while the Catual's obstruction is depicted as illegitimate interference with a divinely ordained mission. The episode's resolution — Gama's eventual release and the Portuguese departure from Calicut — leaves the conflict unresolved in narrative terms but settled in ideological ones: the Catual's greed has been exposed, and the moral superiority of the Portuguese has been demonstrated.

This representation served a political purpose in Camões' own time. By the 1570s, Portuguese relations with Calicut had deteriorated into decades of naval warfare, and the Portuguese had established their Indian capital at [[goa|Goa]] rather than on the Malabar coast. Camões' portrait of Calicut's officialdom as corrupt and obstructive provided retrospective justification for this shift — as though Portuguese arms had been forced into Goa by the impossibility of honest dealing at Calicut.

Modern postcolonial scholarship has read the Catual differently: not as evidence of Oriental corruption but as evidence of Camões' participation in what Edward Said would later theorize as Orientalism — the discursive construction of "the East" as irrational, venal, and in need of Western discipline. The Catual, in this reading, is less a portrait of a real Indian official than a projection of Portuguese anxieties about their own precarious position in the Indian Ocean world.

## Historical Context

The historical figure behind the Catual is poorly documented. Portuguese sources refer to the *catual* as a title rather than a personal name — the official who managed the day-to-day administration of Calicut and served as intermediary between foreign traders and the Samorim. The term derives from the Malayalam *kāttal* or *kāttalār*, meaning "guardian" or "overseer."

In the historical record of Gama's first voyage (1498), relations with the Calicut authorities were indeed difficult. The Samorim was accustomed to Arab-dominated trade networks and was unimpressed by the gifts Gama brought — primarily textiles and metalwork that were inferior to the goods already available in the Indian Ocean market. The Portuguese were detained for several days, and Gama had to leave hostages ashore to secure the return of his men.

Camões' account selectively emphasizes the Catual's venality while downplaying the legitimate commercial and political calculations of the Calicut authorities. Modern scholarship views the encounter as a clash of two sophisticated commercial systems rather than a simple morality play.

## Notes

The Catual belongs to a gallery of obstructive foreign officials in Renaissance epic poetry — figures like Ariosto's Moorish kings or Tasso's Egyptian commanders who impede Christian heroes. What distinguishes Camões' treatment is the Catual's banality: he is not a grand villain but a petty bureaucrat whose greed is more annoying than terrifying. This makes him, in some ways, a more modern figure — the kind of obstacle that empire-builders would encounter far more often than heroic enemies.

The Portuguese word *catual* entered the European vocabulary through accounts of Gama's voyage and appears in several 16th-century travel narratives. The title is now obsolete but remains a notable example of early Malayalam-Portuguese linguistic contact.
