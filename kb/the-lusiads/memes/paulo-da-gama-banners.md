# Paulo da Gama Explains the Portuguese Banners

## Overview

At the court of the Samorim of Calicut, the ruler beholds the painted banners displayed
on the Portuguese vessels. Summoning his curiosity, he asks the Gama brothers to explain
their significance. Paulo da Gama, the more eloquent of the brothers, interprets each
banner, each figure and device, weaving them into a comprehensive narrative of Portuguese
history from its mythical origins to the reign of King Afonso V. The episode occupies the
first half of Canto VIII and serves as Camões's vehicle for recounting the glorious
Portuguese past.

## In The Lusiads

Paulo begins his exposition by identifying the founder figure Lusus on the first banner,
providing the etymology of "Lusitania":

> "All of these figures which to thee are shown
>   so bold in bearing, dreadful to behold,
>   and bolder, dreader far, the men were known
>   in mouth of Fame, for words and works of old:
>   Ancients yet moderns are, still brighter grown
>   with names in Genius' highest rank enrol'd:
> This first in sight is Lusus, from whose fame
> our 'Lusitania' gained her royal name."
> — [008-010]

He identifies Lusus as the son or companion of Bacchus, carrying the thyrsus:

> "He was the Theban's son or comrade tried,
>   the God who divers regions overran;
>   it seems he came to hold our Spanish nide,
>   pursuing conquests which his youth began"
> — [008-011]

> "The branch-Device, thou see'st him bear in hand,
>   is the green Thyrsus Bacchus wont to wield,
>   which to our cent'ury doth belief command
>   he was a comrade or belovèd child."
> — [008-012]

Paulo proceeds to Ulysses, the mythical founder of Lisbon:

> "Ulysses 'tis who builds that sacred fane
>   to her, whose favour tongue facund supplies;
>   if there he fired tall Troy on Asian plain
>   here made he mighty Lisbon's walls arise."
> — [008-013]

The banners display Viriatus, the Lusitanian shepherd who defied Rome:

> "Thou see'st a Herdsman who his flock forsook;
>   we know that Viriátus was his name,
>   who aye preferred the Lance before the Crook:
>   He shook and shattered Roman pride and fame"
> — [008-014]

The banner of the progenitor King and of the First Afonso draws the Samorim's
wonder:

> "The First Afonso 'tis," the Gama spake,
>   "by whom the Moor all Portugalia lost;
>   for whom Fame sware her oath by Stygian Lake
>   no more of noble Roman name to boast"
> — [008-019]

## Key Passages

The banners also depict the noble deeds of Egas Moniz, the loyal tutor who offered
his own life as surety for the young king; and the legendary Twelve of England who
fought to defend English ladies' honour. Each painted flag becomes a chapter in
Portugal's collective memory.

## Significance

The episode is one of the most important vehicles for historical narration in the poem.
It transforms a diplomatic audience into a gallery of Portuguese national memory, allowing
Camões to celebrate the great figures of his nation's past while advancing the plot.
The device of painted banners and Paulo's eloquent interpretation creates a visually
and rhetorically compelling celebration of Portuguese identity.
