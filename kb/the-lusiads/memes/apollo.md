# Apollo

Apollo, also called **Phoebus Apollo**, appears in *The Lusiads* in a dual aspect: as the [[syncretism|classical sun god]] whose rays illuminate the world, and as the divine patron of poetry. [[luis-de-camoes|Camoes]] repeatedly invokes him for [[epic-tradition|epic inspiration]], following the convention established by Homer and Virgil, while also making the god's relationship with the poet intensely personal.

## Identity and Domain

Apollo is the Olympian god of the sun, poetry, music, prophecy, and the arts. Son of [[jupiter|Jupiter]] and Latona, twin brother of Diana, he presides over the [[muse-calliope|Muse]]s on Mount Parnassus and drives the solar chariot across the sky each day. In Renaissance poetics, Apollo represented the highest source of artistic inspiration.

In *The Lusiads*, Apollo's domain extends across both his celestial and poetic functions. As **Phoebus** he is the sun personified, his "morning ray" lighting the world. As **Apollo** the patron of song, he is the wellspring from which Camoes draws his creative power — a classical convention the poet both honours and reshapes.

## In The Lusiads

Unlike [[neptune|Neptune]], who actively opposes the Portuguese voyage, Apollo is generally associated with divine favour and artistic blessing. He does not appear as an actor in the poem's mythological machinery but rather as a presiding presence, invoked by the narrator at key moments.

His appearances cluster around the poet's own voice. When Camoes calls upon the [[muse-calliope|Muse]]s or laments the inadequacy of his art, Apollo is never far. The god represents the classical literary tradition that Camoes both inherits and seeks to transcend — a tradition the poet engages with directly, sometimes reverently, sometimes critically.

## Poetic Invocation

Camoes invokes Apollo for poetic inspiration at the most rhetorically charged moments. This follows the epic convention of the *invocatio* — the poet's appeal to a divine source for the power to sing worthily of great deeds.

> "that flows untiring; so shall Apollo for your waves ordain ye" [001-012]

The poet frames his relationship with Apollo as deeply personal. He asks the god to bathe him in the "sovran wave" of inspiration, imagining himself on Pindus-head receiving divine favour. This intimacy distinguishes Camoes' Apollo from the more distant, formal invocations found in classical models.

> "Pindus-head; e'en now I deem Apollo bathes me in that sovran wave" [003-010]

Yet Camoes also acknowledges Apollo's limits. When the subject grows too vast or the poet's powers too frail, even the god's light proves insufficient — a gesture of [[poetic-immortality|poetic humility]] that paradoxically elevates both poet and god by admitting the stakes.

## Classical Background

Apollo's roots lie deep in Greco-Roman mythology. As transmitted through Ovid's *Metamorphoses*, Virgil's *[[the-aeneid|Aeneid]]*, and Homer's *Iliad*, he reached Renaissance poets as the archetype of divine artistry. His association with the [[the-muses|nine Muses]] — "Apollo and the Nine" — made him the natural patron for any poet undertaking an epic.

The Renaissance elevated Apollo to a symbol of enlightenment and rational order. Camoes, writing at the height of [[portugal|Portugal]]'s golden age, draws on this rich tradition while adapting the god to serve a Christian imperial epic. The result is a form of Renaissance [[syncretism]] in which classical deities operate within a providential framework.
