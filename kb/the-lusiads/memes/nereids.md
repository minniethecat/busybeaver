## Overview

The Nereids are the fifty daughters of Nereus (the Old Man of the Sea) and the Oceanid Doris in Greek mythology. They are benevolent sea nymphs who assist sailors and are often depicted riding dolphins or sea-horses. In classical poetry they form the retinue of Poseidon and attend to the sea-goddess Thetis.

## Classical Background

In Greco-Roman tradition, the Nereids personify the friendly aspects of the sea. They are named individually in Hesiod's *Theogony* and appear throughout classical epic — most notably in Homer's *Iliad*, where Thetis (herself a Nereid) mourns for Achilles, and in Virgil's *Aeneid*, where they assist the Trojan fleet. Their primary mythological role is as attendants and intermediaries of the greater sea-gods.

## Role in The Lusiads

Camões invokes the Nereids as active participants in the maritime world of the Portuguese voyage. They appear as both potential obstacles and eventual allies of Vasco da Gama's fleet, reflecting the epic's broader theme of divine and semi-divine forces shaping the journey to India. The Nereids are portrayed as physically present in the Indian Ocean, rising from the waters and interacting with the Portuguese ships.

## Camões' Adaptation

Camões integrates the Nereids into the Lusitanian maritime epic by treating them as real presences in the waters navigated by Gama. Unlike Homer or Virgil, who keep the Nereids largely in the background, Camões gives them direct physical agency — they circle the ships, raise winds, and appear in visionary episodes. This adaptation serves to elevate the Portuguese voyage to the stature of classical epic while rooting it in the tangible geography of the Cape route.
