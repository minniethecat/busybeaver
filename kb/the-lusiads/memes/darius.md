## Overview

Darius I (c. 550–486 BC), called Darius the Great, was the third king of the Achaemenid Persian Empire. In *The Lusiads*, Camões evokes Darius in two distinct contexts: as an exemplar of royal grief over loyal service (the Zopyrus story in Canto III) and as a symbol of imperial power humbled on the battlefield (Marathon in Canto X). Both references serve the poem's larger project of measuring Portuguese achievement against the great empires of antiquity.

## In The Lusiads

Darius first appears in Canto III during the narrative of Portuguese history. When the Portuguese nobleman Egas Moniz offers his own life as surety for his king's word, the poet compares this act of vassal loyalty to the Persian general Zopyrus, who mutilated himself to win Darius's trust. The comparison is explicit: "What did the Persian more for loyalty?" — and Camões answers that Zopyrus earned Darius's grief, just as Egas Moniz earned Portugal's gratitude [003-049].

In Canto X, Darius appears again in the prophetic panorama of world history, where his defeat at Marathon (490 BC) is cited alongside Thermopylae and other famous last stands — all to emphasize that Portuguese feats surpass these classical benchmarks [010-029].

## The Zopyrus Story

The Zopyrus episode, drawn from Herodotus, tells of a Persian nobleman who cut off his own nose and ears to pose as a deserter, gaining entry to rebel-held Babylon and delivering the city to Darius. When Darius learned the truth, he mourned deeply, declaring he would rather have Zopyrus whole than twenty Babylons. Camões adapts this story to frame Egas Moniz's self-sacrifice as equal to the highest examples of loyalty in classical history.

## Key Passages

> What did the Persian more for loyalty
> whose gallant hand his face and nostrils shred?
> When great Darius mourned so grievously
> that he a thousand times deep-sighing said,
> far he prefer'd his Zóp'yrus sound again,
> than lord of twenty Babylons to reign. [003-049]

> Nor he who in the chàmpaign Marathonian,
> Darius' mighty powers piecemeal rendeth [010-029]

## Notes

The Darius references demonstrate Camões's method of classical *exempla*: historical figures are invoked not for their own sake but as comparative benchmarks that elevate Portuguese history. The Zopyrus episode in particular shows how Camões adapts Herodotean material — well known to Renaissance readers — into a Portuguese patriotic framework.
