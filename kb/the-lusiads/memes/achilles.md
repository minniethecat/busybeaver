## Overview

Achilles is the greatest Greek warrior of the Trojan War and the central hero of Homer's *Iliad*. In *The Lusiads*, he appears as the archetype of martial heroism — the figure against whom Portuguese warriors are measured and found equal.

## In the Poem

Achilles appears in Canto X, where the poet compares the Portuguese hero Duarte Pacheco Pereira to "Achilles Lusitan" — the Portuguese Achilles [2alkCk]. This epithet is one of the poem's most direct claims of parity between Portuguese and classical heroism.

## The Achillean Model

The comparison to Achilles serves a specific rhetorical purpose. Achilles represents the highest form of martial heroism in the classical tradition. By calling Pacheco the "Portuguese Achilles," Camões claims that Portuguese courage and military prowess reach the standard set by Homer's greatest hero.

## Thetis and Achilles

[[thetis|Thetis]], Achilles's divine mother, appears independently in *The Lusiads* as a [[thetis|Nereid]]. In Greek mythology, she was of extraordinary beauty, pursued by Zeus and [[neptune|Poseidon]] until a prophecy revealed her son would be greater than his father. Her presence in the poem, alongside her son's invocation, connects the Portuguese epic to the deepest roots of Greek mythology [7m6wSq].
