# The Catual Detains Gama at Calicut

## Overview

After Vasco da Gama's audience with the Samorim (King of Calicut), the Muslim merchants
who dominated the Indian Ocean spice trade recognized the Portuguese as a mortal threat to
their commercial monopoly. Working through the Catual — the Governor of Calicut — they
orchestrated Gama's detention, seeking to prevent him from returning to Portugal with news
of the sea route to India. The episode occupies the latter half of Canto VIII of *The
Lusiads* and illustrates the political intrigue that the Portuguese encountered in their
first contact with the Indian Ocean world.

## In The Lusiads

The detention episode follows Paulo da Gama's explanation of the Portuguese banners to the
Catual. While Paulo's narration impresses the Governor, [[bacchus|Bacchus]] — the divine
antagonist of the Portuguese — intervenes. Disguised as a Muslim elder, Bacchus appears in
a dream to a priest and warns that the Portuguese will destroy the established order:

> "Guard ye, my children, guard ye," — thus he spoke,
> "from these, your foes, a fearfullest of men"
> — [008-056]

The priest relays the warning to the Catual and other officials, stoking fear and
hostility toward the Portuguese visitors.

## The Bribery and Conspiracy

The Muslim merchants, led by Bacchus in disguise, approach the Catual with a practical
argument: the Portuguese must be prevented from returning home, because once Portugal
establishes direct trade with India, the overland routes through the Red Sea and the
merchants' intermediary role will be destroyed:

> "Until this feeble folk full force hath won,
> let naught prevent thy puissant arm from striking"
> — [008-058]

The merchants deploy bribery to secure the cooperation of Calicut's officials:

> "With golden bribe, rich fee, and secret gift,
> how behooves the King, who rules aright"
> — [008-061]

The Catual proves susceptible: "each and ev'ery Cat'ual gross in greed" ([008-064]). He
agrees to detain Gama and prevent the Portuguese fleet from departing.

## Gama's Response

Gama, perceiving the conspiracy, delivers a dignified speech to the Samorim defending the
honesty of the Portuguese mission and the legitimacy of their discoveries:

> "If then, Oh King! this honest truth of mine
> appeareth clear as brightest summer-day..."
> — [008-083]

The Samorim, moved by Gama's eloquence, shows "assured content" ([008-084]) and orders
that Gama be allowed to return to his ships. However, the bribed Catual continues to
obstruct:

> "This Cat'ual also gifts and bribes had tane,
> whence to the Gama's reasons and rough words replieth"
> — [008-089–093]

Gama is detained on shore overnight — "that livelong night in durance vile he lies"
([008-099]) — before the Catual, having secured sufficient bribes, finally permits him to
depart:

> "for gained the goods he let the Gama go"

## Significance

The Catual's detention episode introduces the theme of commercial rivalry that would define
the Portuguese experience in the Indian Ocean. Camões portrays the Muslim merchants not as
simple enemies but as rational actors defending an established economic order against
disruption. The Catual's venality — susceptible to "golden bribe" — serves as a critique
of Eastern governance from the Portuguese perspective, while Gama's patient and eloquent
self-defense models the ideal of Portuguese diplomacy: combining martial readiness with
rhetorical skill.
