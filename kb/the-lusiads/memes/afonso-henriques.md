# Afonso Henriques

## Overview

Afonso Henriques (c. 1109 – 6 December 1185), also known as Afonso I of Portugal
and "the Conqueror" (*o Conquistador*), was the first King of Portugal and the
founder of the Portuguese monarchy. In *The Lusiads*, Camões presents him as the
founding hero of the Portuguese nation — the figure through whom divine providence
first manifested its special favour towards Portugal.

His story occupies a substantial portion of Canto III, where Vasco da Gama recites
Portuguese history to the King of Melinde. Camões traces the birth of the kingdom
through Afonso's miraculous vision, his foundational battles, and the legendary
loyalty of his tutor Egas Moniz.

## Origins

Afonso was the son of Henry of Burgundy (Henrique), a French nobleman who had come
to the Iberian Peninsula to fight in the Reconquista and was rewarded with the
County of Portugal, and Teresa of León, illegitimate daughter of King Alfonso VI
of León and Castile.

> "A King there was in Spain, Afonso hight…
> 'Mid them Henrique, second son, men say"
> — [003-031, 033]

Upon Henry's death, the young Afonso found himself caught between his mother's
ambitions — she sought to rule Portugal in her own right with the support of a
Galician nobleman — and the expectation that Portugal would become an independent
kingdom.

> "Now Prince Afonso (who such style had tane)"
> — [003-038]

## The Founding of the Kingdom

Camões narrates two foundational episodes from Afonso's early reign:

### The Battle of Ourique (1139)

The most celebrated episode of Afonso's reign is the Battle of Ourique, where he
faced five Moorish kings with a vastly outnumbered force. On the eve of battle,
Afonso received a miraculous vision of Christ on the Cross, promising victory:

> "Afonso sees the Christ on Cross elate"
> — [003-004]

The vision transformed a military engagement into a holy crusade. Afonso's victory
over the five kings established the Portuguese monarchy's divine legitimacy. The
five escutcheons (*quinas*) on the Portuguese flag commemorate this victory to the
present day.

> "Order five Moorish Kings the hostile host…
> But Prince Afonso now prepared his band"
> — [003-052, 050]

### The Loyalty of Egas Moniz

The second foundational episode concerns Afonso's tutor and vassal, Egas Moniz,
who had stood as surety for Afonso's oath to the King of León. When Afonso broke
the oath to pursue Portuguese independence, Egas Moniz presented himself — barefoot,
with a rope around his neck, accompanied by his wife and children — to the Leonese
court, offering his family's lives as forfeit for his broken word. The King of León,
moved by such fidelity, pardoned them.

This episode establishes Portuguese loyalty as the moral foundation of the new nation.

## Military Campaigns

After establishing his kingdom, Afonso waged continuous campaigns to expand
Portuguese territory southward into Moorish lands. Camões presents him as a
relentless warrior-king, personally leading his troops into battle:

> "So doth Afonso, sudden seen the foes,
> Victory swift pursuing, rest disdaineth"
> — [003-075–076]

His conquests included Santarém, Lisbon (1147, with the aid of crusaders en route
to the Holy Land), and much of the Alentejo. By his death in 1185, Portugal's
borders extended well south of the Tagus River, establishing the territorial
core of the future maritime empire.

## Appearance in the Poem

Afonso Henriques appears in *The Lusiads* at multiple points:

**Canto III**: The longest treatment — Gama narrates Afonso's life as the founding
chapter of Portuguese history, from his contested succession through the Battle of
Ourique to his death.

**Canto IV**: Brief mentions in the catalogue of Portuguese kings, establishing the
dynastic continuity from Afonso to the present.

**Canto VIII**: In Paulo da Gama's explanation of the Portuguese banners to the
Catual, the "First Afonso" appears on the historical standards:

> "The First Afonso 'tis," the Gama spake
> — [008-019]

## Death and Legacy

> "Afonso dying, straight to him succeedeth"
> — [003-099]

Afonso Henriques died on 6 December 1185 and was succeeded by his son Sancho I.
He was buried at the Monastery of Santa Cruz in Coimbra, which he had founded.
In Portuguese national memory, he occupies the position of founding father —
the figure who transformed a county into a kingdom and set Portugal on its path
to becoming a global maritime power.

Camões's treatment of Afonso Henriques establishes the template for Portuguese
heroism that the poem extends through subsequent kings to Vasco da Gama himself:
divine favour, personal courage, territorial expansion, and the unwavering loyalty
of followers.
