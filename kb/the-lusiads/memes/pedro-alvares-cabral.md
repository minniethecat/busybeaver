# Pedro Álvares Cabral

## Role & Significance

Pedro Álvares Cabral occupies a singular place in Portuguese history: he is the discoverer of Brazil. In 1500, commanding the second Portuguese India Armada — a fleet of thirteen ships following Vasco da Gama's route — Cabral swung wide into the Atlantic to catch favourable winds and sighted the coast of what he initially named *Ilha de Vera Cruz* (Island of the True Cross). The landfall, on 22 April 1500, was not an accident but likely the result of deliberate exploration authorised by the Portuguese Crown, which had reason to believe land lay westward.

Brazil would become Portugal's largest and wealthiest colony, and Cabral's landfall is the foundational event of the Portuguese Atlantic empire. In *The Lusiads*, he receives a brief but significant mention in the prophetic vision of Canto X.

## In The Lusiads

Cabral appears in the prophecy that the sea-goddess Tethys reveals to Vasco da Gama in Canto X — a vision of all the future Portuguese heroes who will carry the empire across the oceans. The reference is embedded in the endnotes of Richard Francis Burton's translation:

> Of Pedr' Alvares Cabral

And later, in a geographical gloss on the lands discovered:

> S. Cruz (= The Brazil) found by Cabral, AD 1500

The brevity of the reference reflects the poem's composition date. *The Lusiads* was published in 1572, during the early decades of Brazil's colonisation — the full magnitude of the discovery was not yet apparent. Cabral's inclusion in the prophetic vision, however brief, affirms his place in Camões' providential narrative: the westward expansion, like the eastward route, is part of Portugal's divinely ordained mission.

## The Discovery of Brazil

Cabral departed Lisbon on 9 March 1500 with thirteen ships and approximately 1,500 men. His fleet included experienced navigators such as [[bartholomeu-dias|Bartholomeu Dias]] and the cosmographer Duarte Pacheco Pereira, who had likely already explored the South American coast.

After sighting land on 22 April, Cabral spent ten days on the Brazilian coast, taking formal possession for Portugal and naming the territory. He then dispatched a ship back to Lisbon with news of the discovery while the remainder of the fleet continued to India — a voyage that would be far more difficult, with only five of the original thirteen ships returning to Lisbon in 1501.

## Camões and Brazil

Camões wrote *The Lusiads* with first-hand knowledge of the Portuguese empire: he spent years in the East (Goa, Macau, Mozambique) and would have been aware of Brazil's emerging importance. Yet the poem is overwhelmingly oriented toward the Indian Ocean — Vasco da Gama's route to India is its geographical and symbolic axis. Brazil, in 1572, was still a frontier: its sugar plantations were just beginning, its gold and diamonds still undiscovered.

Cabral's presence in the Canto X prophecy is thus forward-looking — a seed planted for a future that Camões sensed but could not fully describe. The landfall of 1500 would become, in retrospect, the second great pillar of Portuguese maritime empire, alongside the Indian route.
