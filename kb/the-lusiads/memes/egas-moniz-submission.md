# Egas Moniz's Submission to the King of León

## Overview

Egas Moniz's submission to the King of León is one of the most celebrated episodes of
medieval Portuguese loyalty recounted in Canto III of *The Lusiads*. The nobleman
Egas Moniz had stood as surety for his young prince, Afonso Henriques, promising the
King of León that the prince would acknowledge Leonese suzerainty. When Afonso broke
the oath, Egas Moniz chose to surrender his own life and those of his wife and children
rather than let his word be dishonored.

The episode appears within Vasco da Gama's historical narrative to the King of Melinde,
serving as an exemplum of Portuguese fidelity.

## The Broken Oath

Egas Moniz, a loyal vassal of the young Prince Afonso Henriques (the future first king
of Portugal), had guaranteed to the King of León that his prince would submit to
Leonese authority:

> "This doughty Vassal from that servile horde"
> — [003-034]

When Afonso instead chose to fight for Portuguese independence, the bond of surety was
broken. Egas Moniz, bound by his word, knew what he must do:

> "But when the loyal Vassal well hath known...
> Already came the plighted time and tide"
> — [003-044–045]

## The Act of Submission

Egas Moniz travelled to the Leonese court carrying his wife and children with him.
Dressed not as a nobleman but as a condemned man — barefoot, with a rope around his
neck — he presented himself before the King:

> "He with his children and his wife departeth"
> — [003-046]

He offered his family's lives as forfeit for his broken pledge, declaring that only
their deaths could satisfy the honor of his word:

> "I bring, thou seest here, lives innocent...
> As shrifted wight the hangman stands before"
> — [003-047–048]

## The King's Clemency

The King of León, moved by such extraordinary loyalty, pardoned Egas Moniz and his
family. The act of self-sacrifice itself, even unfulfilled, proved the nobility of
the Portuguese character:

> "Oh the great Portingall-fidelity"
> — [003-049]

## Significance

The story of Egas Moniz was a celebrated *exemplum* in Portuguese chronicle tradition.
Camões includes it to establish the Portuguese national character — loyalty, even unto
death — as the moral foundation of the empire. The episode also provides crucial
historical context for the reign of Afonso Henriques, the founding figure of the
Portuguese monarchy, whose rebellion against León established Portugal as an independent
kingdom.
