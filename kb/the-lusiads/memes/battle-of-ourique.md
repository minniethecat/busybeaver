# The Battle of Ourique

## Overview

The Battle of Ourique (1139) is presented in *The Lusiads* as the foundational event
of Portuguese nationhood. Before the battle, Afonso Henriques receives a miraculous
vision of Christ on the Cross, promising victory. He then defeats five Moorish kings
in a single engagement, establishing the legitimacy of the Portuguese crown and
kingdom. Camões narrates this event in Canto III as part of Vasco da Gama's
historical discourse to the King of Melinde.

## In The Lusiads

The episode is framed within Gama's lengthy recitation of Portuguese history.
After recounting the legendary origins of Lusitania, Gama arrives at the
critical moment of Afonso Henriques's founding battle:

> "Afonso sees the Christ on Cross elate"
> — [003-004]

This visionary moment transforms the military engagement into a sacred crusade.
Camões emphasises the miraculous quality — Christ himself sanctions the
Portuguese cause.

Afonso prepares his warriors against overwhelming odds:

> "But Prince Afonso now prepared his band"
> — [003-050]

The scale of the confrontation is epic — five Moorish kings united against
a single Christian prince:

> "Order five Moorish Kings the hostile host"
> — [003-052]

## Key Passages

The battle itself is swift and decisive. Camões portrays Afonso as an unstoppable
force, cutting through the enemy with divine assistance:

> "So doth Afonso, sudden seen the foes"
> — [003-075]

The victory is total. The Moorish army disintegrates before the Portuguese charge:

> "Th' astonied Moorman in such startled guise"
> — [003-058]

Pursuing the fleeing enemy, Afonso secures the triumph that will define his realm:

> "Victory swift pursuing, rest disdaineth"
> — [003-087]

Camões also invokes the speech Afonso delivers to his men before the engagement,
rallying them with the promise of divine favour and eternal renown:

> "To arms! To arms! for native Land and King!"
> — [003-073]

## Historical Basis

The historical Battle of Ourique took place on 25 July 1139, when Afonso Henriques
defeated an Almoravid force in southern Portugal. The miraculous vision of Christ
became a central element of Portuguese national mythology and is represented on
the Portuguese flag to this day — the five *quinas* (escutcheons) symbolise the
five defeated Moorish kings, and the thirty bezants represent the thirty pieces of
silver for which Christ was betrayed. Camões's account amplifies the providential
dimension, presenting the battle as divine ordination of Portugal's destiny.

## Significance

The Battle of Ourique functions in *The Lusiads* as a foundational myth.
It establishes the pattern — divine favour, heroic courage, victory against
overwhelming odds — that the poem projects onto all subsequent Portuguese
achievements, including Vasco da Gama's voyage.

## Notes

The historical Ourique has been the subject of considerable scholarly dispute.
Some historians question whether five kings were truly present, or whether the
battle was a smaller skirmish elevated to epic proportions by later chroniclers.
Camões follows the traditional account, which by the sixteenth century had become
orthodox national history. The vision of Christ specifically recalls Constantine's
vision of the Cross before the Battle of the Milvian Bridge — a deliberate parallel
that places Afonso Henriques in the lineage of Christian imperial founders.
