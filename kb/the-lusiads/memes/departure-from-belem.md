# Departure from Belém

The **Departure from Belém** is the scene in [[canto-iv|Canto IV]] of *The Lusiads* where [[vasco-da-gama|Vasco da Gama]]'s fleet sets sail from the beach of Belém (Bethlehem), near [[lisbon|Lisbon]], in July 1497. The episode is most famous for the speech of the **Old Man of Restelo** (Velho do Restelo) — an aged figure who stands on the shore and delivers a fierce condemnation of the voyage, denouncing imperial ambition as destructive vanity. This speech is the poem's most powerful moment of internal critique, a counter-voice that complicates the epic's celebration of Portuguese expansion.

## Overview

The fleet of four vessels — the *São Gabriel* (flagship, commanded by da [[vasco-da-gama|Gama]]), the *São Rafael* (commanded by [[paulo-da-gama|Paulo da Gama]]), the *Berrio*, and a storeship — has been prepared at the Restelo anchorage near the Hieronymite monastery of Belém. The scene is one of solemnity and emotion: families gather on the beach to bid farewell; mothers, wives, and sisters weep; and the sailors prepare to leave all they have known for an unknown ocean.

The Argument to Canto IV describes it thus: "Preparations for it: Embarkation and farewells of the navigators upon the Belem beach." Camões devotes the final stanzas of the canto (94–104) to the departure and the Old Man's speech.

## In The Lusiads

As the fleet casts off, an aged man of "venerable presence" rises from the crowd on the beach. His voice carries across the water in what becomes the poem's most famous set-piece speech — a searing indictment of the voyage and of imperial ambition in general. The Old Man denounces the expedition as:

- **Folly born of vainglory**: The desire for fame ([[fame-and-glory|Fama]]) drives men to abandon their homes, families, and peace for uncertain conquests
- **Cruelty disguised as glory**: The suffering that will attend the voyage — shipwreck, disease, death in foreign lands — renders any fame hollow
- **A betrayal of the land**: [[portugal|Portugal]]'s true strength lies in its soil, not in distant conquests

The speech draws on classical and biblical precedents — the anti-imperial rhetoric of Roman historians, the Ecclesiastes theme of vanity, and the Renaissance humanist critique of military expansion. It stands in deliberate tension with the epic's dominant voice, and critics have long debated whether the Old Man represents Camões' own doubts or a foil that the poem ultimately overcomes.

> The Old Man of Restelo denounces the voyage as vainglory born of restless ambition, a prophetic voice against the imperial enterprise the poem otherwise celebrates.

## Historical Basis

The fleet departed from Belém in July 1497. The site — now part of greater [[lisbon|Lisbon]] — is marked by the Jerónimos Monastery and the Belém Tower, both built in the Manueline style that celebrates the Age of [[maritime-exploration|Discovery]]. The monastery was founded by [[prince-henry-the-navigator|Henry the Navigator]] and later became the burial site of [[vasco-da-gama|Vasco da Gama]] and [[luis-de-camoes|Luís de Camões]].

The Old Man of Restelo is almost certainly a fictional figure — a poetic device rather than a historical person. But his name ties him to a real place: Restelo was the anchorage where Portuguese voyages traditionally departed. Camões likely knew the beach well; it was from here that he himself had departed for India as a young soldier.

## Significance

The Old Man's speech is one of the most analysed passages in Portuguese literature. It introduces a note of ambiguity into the poem's imperial ideology that has resonated across centuries — from the nineteenth-century "Saudosismo" of Portuguese romantic nationalism to the twentieth-century anti-colonial readings of the epic.

The speech also functions structurally as the epic's moment of moral complexity. By giving voice to the anti-imperial argument at the moment of departure, Camões ensures that the poem never becomes simple propaganda. The Old Man's words haunt the subsequent narrative: every shipwreck, every battle death, every moment of suffering in the voyage can be read as confirmation of his prophecy.
