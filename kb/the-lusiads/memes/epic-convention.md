## Overview

Epic convention refers to the formal devices inherited from classical epic — invocation to the [[muse-calliope|Muse]], divine machinery, extended similes, catalogues, and prophetic visions — that Camões deploys throughout *The Lusiads*. These conventions signal the poem's membership in the epic tradition while Camões adapts each to his Christian and Portuguese context.

## The Invocation

Camões opens his epic not with a conventional dedication but with a direct address to the young King Sebastian as [[portugal|Portugal]]'s providential hope. This innovation transforms the classical invocation into a nationalist appeal [Ar6qNV].

## Divine Machinery

The supernatural apparatus of *The Lusiads* — the conflict between [[venus|Venus]] and [[bacchus|Bacchus]] — owes a structural debt to both Homer and Ariosto. But Camões Christianises the convention: [[jupiter|Jupiter]]'s decree invokes a providential fate that sounds closer to Christian divine will than to pagan *fatum* [5HVTjQ].

## Prophetic Revelation

The epic convention of prophetic revelation — including the giant [[adamastor|Adamastor]]'s warning and [[tethys|Tethys]]'s vision of future Portuguese glories — allows Camões to telescope Portuguese history into the narrative present [9PvJQV].

## Epic Simile and Catalogue

Camões inherits the extended simile and the catalogue of warriors from Homer and Virgil but adapts them to Portuguese material. The catalogue of Portuguese kings in Canto III replaces the Homeric catalogue of ships; the similes draw on maritime imagery rather than agricultural, reflecting [[portugal|Portugal]]'s oceanic identity.

## Innovation Within Convention

Camões's genius lies in working within epic conventions while subordinating them to a new purpose. The classical form provides legitimacy and scale; the Christian-Portuguese content provides originality. This creative tension defines *The Lusiads*'s place in the epic tradition.
