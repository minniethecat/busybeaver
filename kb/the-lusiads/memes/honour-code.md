# Honour Code

## Definition
The honour code (Portuguese: *honra*) in The Lusiads is the system of aristocratic values — courage, loyalty, religious fidelity, and martial prowess — that defines the Portuguese nobility. Derived from medieval chivalry and infused with Renaissance ideals of personal virtue, it governs the behavior of Camões' heroes from [[vasco-da-gama|Vasco da Gama]] to the historical kings celebrated in the poem's epic catalogue.

## In The Lusiads
Camões opens the poem by announcing its subject as heroic arms:

> The feats of Arms, and famed heroick Host (001-009)

The word "feats" (*feitos*) implies not merely military action but deeds that demonstrate character and earn renown. Throughout the poem, Portuguese nobles define themselves through action rather than birth — though noble lineage remains important.

The historical narrative of Canto III celebrates Portuguese kings who embody martial honour:

> mail, plate of steel, he of Lusus coat (003-059)

In Canto IV, the honour code is linked to national destiny:

> 'Tis heavy travail, hard, heroick, high; which love of me shall make thee light to bear (004-087)

## Historical/Cultural Context
The Portuguese honour code evolved from the fusion of Visigothic military traditions, the ideals of the *Reconquista*, and the chivalric culture of medieval [[europe|Europe]]. The military orders — particularly the [[order-of-christ|Order of Christ]] — institutionalized these values. By Camões' time, the Renaissance concept of *virtù* (Machiavelli's term for active, effective virtue) had enriched the medieval chivalric framework.

## Notes
Camões contrasts genuine honour — earned through action and service — with false honour based on empty titles or flattery. The poem's moral vision aligns earned honour with divine favor, making honour a theological as well as a social category.
