## Overview

Ulysses (Greek: Odysseus) is the legendary Greek hero of Homer's *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]*, known for his cunning intelligence and ten-year journey home from the Trojan War. In *The Lusiads*, he is the Homeric hero most directly relevant to maritime epic — and the figure da [[vasco-da-gama|Gama]] is called upon to surpass.

## In the Poem

Ulysses appears in Canto VIII, where the Ptolemaic cosmos reveals "Ulysses 'tis who builds that sacred fane" [8O6GyB]. This reference connects the Homeric hero to the Portuguese imperial project, suggesting that the spirit of Odyssean exploration finds its ultimate fulfillment in [[portugal|Portugal]]'s maritime empire.

## Da Gama as New Ulysses

The poem's hero is explicitly modeled on Ulysses. Like Odysseus, da [[vasco-da-gama|Gama]] must use cunning as well as courage to survive hostile shores. [[mombasa|Mombasa]], where Muslims plot against him, parallels the Cyclops's cave. And like Odysseus, da [[vasco-da-gama|Gama]] is aided by a protecting goddess — [[venus|Venus]] fulfills Athena's role in the *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]* [9nG3hi].

## Historical Truth

Camões's crucial claim is that da [[vasco-da-gama|Gama]] surpasses Ulysses because his voyage is historical rather than mythological. Where Homer could only imagine the wanderings of a legendary hero, Camões can document the actual journey of a Portuguese navigator. This claim — that truth exceeds fiction — is foundational to *The Lusiads*'s argument for Portuguese exceptionalism.
