# Pompey

## Overview

Pompey the Great (Gnaeus Pompeius Magnus, 106–48 BCE) was one of the most extraordinary figures of the late Roman Republic — a military prodigy who celebrated three triumphs before the age of forty-five, cleared the Mediterranean of pirates in a campaign of breathtaking speed, conquered the eastern kingdoms of Pontus and Armenia, and stood, for a time, as the undisputed master of the Roman world. His defeat by [[julius-caesar|Julius Caesar]] at Pharsalus in 48 BCE, his flight to Egypt, and his murder on an Egyptian beach marked the end of the Roman Republic and the birth of the imperial system that would dominate Europe for five centuries.

For Camões, Pompey serves a specific and carefully calibrated rhetorical function: he is the supreme *exemplum* — the classical figure whose fame and fall together warn that no human power is secure. Invoked in Canto III of The Lusiads, in a single stanza of concentrated poetic argument, Pompey becomes a mirror in which the Portuguese reader is invited to contemplate the fragility of all imperial ambition, including — and especially — [[portugal|Portugal]]'s own.

## Quick Facts

- **Full Name**: Gnaeus Pompeius Magnus
- **Born**: 29 September 106 BCE, Picenum, Roman Italy
- **Died**: 28 September 48 BCE, Pelusium, Egypt (assassinated)
- **Nationality**: Roman
- **Role**: Military commander; consul (three times); member of the First Triumvirate
- **Major Campaigns**: Sulla's civil war (83–82 BCE), Sertorian War in Hispania (76–71 BCE), Third Servile War (71 BCE), pirate war (67 BCE), Third Mithridatic War (66–63 BCE), Civil War against Caesar (49–48 BCE)
- **Cause of Death**: Assassinated on the orders of Ptolemy XIII's advisors upon landing in Egypt

## In The Lusiads

### The Pompey Stanza (Canto III, Stanza 79)

Pompey appears in a single, powerful stanza of Canto III — one of the most compressed and rhetorically charged passages in the entire poem. Camões breaks the narrative of Portuguese history to address Pompey directly, in an apostrophe that mingles pity, warning, and a kind of dark consolation:

> "O famous Pompey! feel thy Wraith no pain
> to see the fate of noble feats like thine;
> nor mourn if all-just Nemesis ordain
> thy bays be torn by sire-in-law indign;
> though Phasis frore and parcht Syéné-plain,
> though hot Bootes and the Mauritanian line
> erst trembled at thy name, yet shalt thou see
> proud Carthage rival thine unhappy sea."

The stanza is a masterpiece of rhetorical compression. Let me unpack its layered argument:

**"O famous Pompey!"** — The direct address (*apostrophe*) establishes an intimate, almost urgent tone. Camões speaks to Pompey as though the dead Roman general could hear him across sixteen centuries. The word "famous" (*famoso*) carries its Latin weight: Pompey is not merely well-known but *famous* in the epic sense, a figure whose deeds demand to be sung.

**"Feel thy Wraith no pain"** — The word "Wraith" (ghost, shade) situates us in the territory of classical epic, where dead heroes continue to care about their posthumous reputations. Camões consoles Pompey's shade: do not grieve that your achievements were undone. This is the poem's characteristic movement from triumph to consolation, from glory to the acceptance of glory's limits.

**"All-just Nemesis"** — The goddess of retribution against hubris is invoked by name. Camões implies that Pompey's fall, like all falls, has a moral dimension: excessive greatness attracts divine correction. "All-just" is crucial: Pompey's destruction is not random or cruel but *just*, part of a cosmic order that punishes greatness precisely because it overreaches. The Portuguese reader is meant to hear this as a caution.

**"Thy bays be torn by sire-in-law indign"** — The laurel wreath ("bays") of victory is torn from Pompey by his "sire-in-law" (*sogro*), Julius Caesar, whose daughter Julia was Pompey's wife. The familial relationship deepens the tragedy: Pompey is destroyed not by an alien enemy but by his own son-in-law, a man bound to him by marriage. The intimacy of the betrayal makes the fall more terrible.

**The geography of empire**: Phasis (in Colchis, the eastern edge of the known world), Syene (Aswan, the southern limit of Egypt), Bootes (the far north), and the Mauritanian line (the western edge of North Africa) — these four geographical markers map the extent of Pompey's once-vast imperium. The whole known world "trembled" at his name. The irony is devastating: the man who commanded the world ends on a beach in Egypt, killed by a boy king's servants.

**"Proud Carthage rival thine unhappy sea"** — The stanza's closing line performs the stanza's central rhetorical move. Pompey, contemplating his own fall, is told he will see [[carthage|Carthage]] — Rome's ancient enemy — meet a fate as terrible as his own. The consolation is competitive: your tragedy is real, but it is not unique. Others have fallen, and others will fall. Portugal, the implied target of this warning, is no exception.

### The Stanza's Function in the Poem

The Pompey stanza occupies a strategic position in Canto III. It follows [[vasco-da-gama|Gama]]'s catalogue of Portuguese kings and battles but precedes the Inês de Castro episode. It serves as a hinge: after the triumphs of Portuguese history, before the tragedy of Inês, Pompey stands as a warning that triumph and tragedy are never far apart.

The stanza is also Camões's most direct engagement with the theme of imperial fragility that haunts the entire poem. The Lusiads was written at the height of Portuguese imperial power — and, paradoxically, at the beginning of its decline. King Sebastian, to whom the poem is dedicated, would die in the sands of Morocco in 1578, and [[portugal|Portugal]] would fall under Spanish rule two years later. Camões, who may have sensed the coming catastrophe, uses Pompey to say what could not be said directly: that all empires are mortal.

## Historical Pompey vs. Camões's Pompey

### The Historical Pompey

Gnaeus Pompeius Magnus was born in 106 BCE to a wealthy equestrian family from Picenum. His rise was meteoric and, by Roman standards, unorthodox: he commanded armies before holding any political office, celebrated triumphs before being old enough to sit in the Senate, and amassed power through military brilliance rather than the slow ascent of the *cursus honorum*.

His career can be divided into four phases:

1. **The Young Butcher (83–71 BCE)**: Pompey fought for Sulla in the civil wars of the 80s BCE, earning the nickname *adulescentulus carnifex* ("the teenage butcher") for his ruthless efficiency. He then campaigned in Sicily and Africa, destroying the remnants of the Marian faction. His demand for a triumph at age twenty-four — a demand Sulla initially resisted but ultimately granted — marked him as a figure of unprecedented early glory.

2. **The Pirate War and the East (67–62 BCE)**: Pompey's greatest achievements came in his forties. In 67 BCE, he was granted extraordinary command over the entire Mediterranean to suppress piracy — a task he accomplished in an astonishing three months. The Lex Gabinia that authorised this command made Pompey, briefly, the most powerful man in Rome. He followed this with the command against Mithridates VI of Pontus (66–63 BCE), conquering the East in a campaign that added vast territories to the Roman sphere and made Pompey fabulously wealthy. His settlement of the East — founding cities, establishing client kingdoms, reorganising provinces — was a masterpiece of imperial administration that would shape the Roman Near East for centuries.

3. **The First Triumvirate and the Slide to Civil War (60–50 BCE)**: Pompey's return to Rome was less triumphant than his conquests. The Senate, fearing his power, delayed ratification of his eastern settlement and refused to grant land to his veterans. In 60 BCE, Pompey formed the First Triumvirate with [[julius-caesar|Julius Caesar]] and Crassus — an informal alliance that dominated Roman politics for a decade. Pompey married Caesar's daughter Julia, and the two men ruled Rome between them. But Julia's death in childbirth in 54 BCE and Crassus's death at Carrhae in 53 BCE removed the bonds that held the alliance together. By 50 BCE, Pompey and [[julius-caesar|Caesar]] were moving inexorably toward war.

4. **The Civil War and Death (49–48 BCE)**: When [[julius-caesar|Caesar]] crossed the Rubicon in 49 BCE, Pompey was charged with defending the Republic. But Pompey's generalship, once brilliant, had grown cautious with age. He abandoned Italy — a decision that shocked his allies — and retreated to Greece to raise an army. The two forces met at Pharsalus in 48 BCE. Pompey's army was larger; Caesar's veterans were better. When the Pompeian cavalry was routed and Caesar's infantry advanced, Pompey left the field, fled to Egypt, and was murdered on the orders of the boy-king Ptolemy XIII's advisors before he could even set foot on land.

### Camões's Transformation

Camões's Pompey is not the historical Pompey but a figure abstracted into moral significance. The poem omits everything that made Pompey controversial or complex — the teenage massacres, the political maneuvering, the vacillation and indecision of his final campaign — and retains only what serves the *exemplum* function: the greatness and the fall.

This is not a failing but a deliberate Renaissance poetic technique. The *exemplum* works by stripping a figure down to its morally salient features. Pompey, for Camões and his readers, is "greatness that fell" — and the historical details that complicate that picture are irrelevant to the poem's purpose.

The transformation is also ideological. By making Pompey's fall an instance of Nemesis's justice — excessive greatness punished — Camões imports a moral framework that the historical Pompey would not have recognised. The real Pompey was destroyed by political forces, military miscalculations, and the structural fragility of the late Republic. Camões's Pompey is destroyed by a goddess, and his destruction serves a moral lesson.

### The Warning to Portugal

This is where Camões's use of Pompey becomes audacious. The Pompey stanza is addressed, nominally, to Pompey's shade. But its real audience is [[portugal|Portugal]]. The poem's readers — and especially its royal dedicatee, King Sebastian — are meant to hear Pompey's story as a cautionary tale about their own empire.

The warning is implicit but unmistakable: [[portugal|Portugal]]'s empire, like Rome's, was built on conquest and maintained by force. Its heroes — [[vasco-da-gama|Gama]], [[francisco-de-almeida|Almeida]], [[afonso-de-albuquerque|Albuquerque]] — are as great as any Roman, and their achievements are as vulnerable to fortune's wheel. The Pompey stanza is Camões's *memento mori* directed at the Portuguese imperium.

This reading is reinforced by the biographical context. Camões wrote The Lusiads in the 1560s and published it in 1572, by which time the Portuguese empire in the East was already under severe pressure from Dutch, English, and Ottoman competition. The poet, who had served in India and seen the empire's fragilities firsthand, knew that the triumphal narrative of Portuguese conquest was only half the story. Pompey is the figure through whom the other half — the half of decline and fall — enters the poem.

## Pompey and the Classical Exemplum Tradition

Camões's use of Pompey belongs to a well-established Renaissance technique: the classical *exemplum*. An exemplum is a figure or story from classical antiquity invoked to make a moral point in a contemporary context. The technique assumes that classical history is a repository of moral truth — that what happened to the Greeks and Romans illuminates what is happening to us.

The logic is comparative. Camões's audience would have known the Pompey story in detail from Plutarch, Lucan, and other classical sources. They would have known Pompey as the greatest Roman of his generation, the man who "made the world tremble," and as the man who ended his life as a headless corpse on an Egyptian beach. By naming Pompey in the context of Portuguese empire, Camões both elevates his subject (Portuguese commanders are as great as Rome's) and warns it (Rome's greatest fell — and so may yours).

This ambivalence — celebration and warning fused in a single stanza — is characteristic of the mature Camões, who wrote The Lusiads with full knowledge that the empire he celebrated was already beginning to fray at the edges.

## Key Themes

### The Fragility of Greatness

Pompey is the supreme example in The Lusiads of a theme that runs through the entire poem: that all human achievement is subject to fortune and time. The invocation of Pompey in Canto III is a *memento mori* addressed to [[portugal|Portugal]] itself — a reminder, couched in the language of classical consolation, that the greatest empires are mortal.

### Nemesis and Hubris

The goddess Nemesis is named in the Pompey stanza: "all-just Nemesis." Camões implies that Pompey's fall, like all great falls, has a moral dimension — excessive greatness attracts divine retribution. The Portuguese reader is meant to hear this as a caution: [[portugal|Portugal]]'s empire, like Rome's, is vulnerable to the same divine logic that brought Pompey down. The poem's triumphalism is shadowed, always, by this awareness of imperial mortality.

### Competitive Consolation

The stanza's final gesture — "yet shalt thou see proud Carthage rival thine unhappy sea" — introduces a distinctive Camonian move: the consolation of shared tragedy. Pompey's fate is not unique; [[carthage|Carthage]], the great rival of Rome, met a similar end. The implicit argument is that greatness and destruction are two sides of the same coin — a coin that every empire must eventually spend. [[portugal|Portugal]]'s turn, the stanza implies without saying, will come.

## Significance

Pompey's significance in The Lusiads is out of all proportion to his single-stanza appearance. He is the poem's most concentrated symbol of the imperial fragility theme — the reminder, placed near the beginning of the historical narrative, that all conquest and glory end.

For Camões, Pompey is the figure through which the epic's triumphalism is shadowed by tragic awareness. The poem celebrates Portuguese achievement with genuine fervour, but it never loses sight of the fact that empires are mortal. Pompey — the greatest Roman of his generation, murdered on an Egyptian beach — is the proof.

The Pompey stanza is also a testament to Camões's ability to compress vast historical and moral argument into the tight space of a single ottava rima. In eight lines, Camões invokes classical history, proposes a moral framework (Nemesis punishes hubris), sketches a geography of empire, and delivers a warning to his Portuguese audience. The density of the stanza is characteristic of Camões at his most concentrated — and it is one of the reasons The Lusiads rewards the kind of close reading that Pompey, more than most figures in the poem, requires.

## Notes

The Pompey stanza has not received as much critical attention as other passages in The Lusiads (the Inês episode, the Giant Adamastor, the Isle of Love). This is unfortunate, as it is one of the poem's most compressed and allusive moments — a miniature treatise on imperial fragility tucked into the middle of Canto III.

Camões's primary source for Pompey's career was likely Lucan's *Pharsalia* (also known as *De Bello Civili*), the great Latin epic of the civil war between Caesar and Pompey. Lucan's Pompey is a tragic figure — a great man whose greatness is already in the past when the poem begins — and Camões's treatment follows Lucan in emphasising the pathos of the fallen hero.

The geographical references in the stanza (Phasis, Syene, Bootes, Mauritania) map the extent of Pompey's power but also, more subtly, the extent of Camões's own geographical imagination. The same places that "trembled" at Pompey's name are places that Portuguese ships would later visit — and, in some cases, places where Portuguese power would also ebb. The geography of Pompey's empire becomes, in the poem's echo chamber, the geography of [[portugal|Portugal]]'s own imperial warning.

The phrase "sire-in-law indign" deserves attention. By emphasising that Pompey's destroyer was his son-in-law, Camões adds a layer of familial tragedy to the political fall. The breaking of kinship bonds — a father-in-law and son-in-law at war — mirrors the dynastic fractures that Camões has just been narrating in Portuguese history (Afonso IV versus Pedro, the crisis of the 1383–1385 interregnum). Pompey's tragedy is both classical and, in the poem's submerged logic, Portuguese.
