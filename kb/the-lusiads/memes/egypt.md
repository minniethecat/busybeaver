# Egypt

## Overview

Egypt appears in The Lusiads in dual aspect: as the land of classical antiquity whose legendary wealth serves as a measure of Portuguese achievement, and as a geographical landmark on the Red Sea route to India. Camões references Egypt's Red Sea coast, its Ptolemaic past, and its proverbial luxury — always in comparison with Portugal's own ambitions.

## In The Lusiads

### The Fair Egyptian (Canto II)

The poem alludes to the classical figure of the Egyptian queen in a moral comparison:

> "that fair Egyptian not so chaste as fair" (002-033)

invoking the story of Cleopatra as a warning against the seductions of luxury.

### Ptolemaic Legacy (Canto IX)

Egypt's Hellenistic past is recalled through its founder:

> "founded by Egypt's royal Ptolemy"

connecting the land to the wider Mediterranean world that Camões's epic seeks to rival (009-003).

### The Heights of Ethiopia (Canto IV)

Egypt's southern boundary marks the limit of the known African world:

> "and climb 'yond Egypt to those Aethiop heights" (004-109)

placing Egypt as the gateway between the Mediterranean and the unknown interior.

### Antique Luxury (Canto X)

In the prophetic vision, Egypt's legendary wealth is surpassed by Portugal's future glory:

> "that ántique Egypt's lux'ury sink to shame" (010-011)

making Egypt the benchmark of opulence that Portuguese triumph will exceed.

## Historical Context

Egypt in Camões's time was part of the Ottoman Empire, conquered in 1517. The Red Sea route past Egypt's coast was strategically vital for Portuguese ambitions to control the spice trade — by blocking the Red Sea, Portugal hoped to force Asian goods around the Cape of Good Hope instead. The Portuguese attacked Suez and patrolled the Red Sea entrance, attempting to sever the traditional trade artery that had enriched Venice and the Muslim world for centuries.

## Notes

Egypt's role in The Lusiads is primarily comparative: its ancient wealth and luxury serve as a foil for Portuguese achievement. The Red Sea coast was also a theatre of Portuguese naval operations against Muslim trade, giving Egypt a practical as well as symbolic importance in the poem's geography.
