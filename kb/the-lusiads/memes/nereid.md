# Nereids

## Overview

The Nereids are the fifty daughters of Nereus (the "[[proteus|Old Man of the Sea]]") and Doris — sea-nymphs who embody the grace and benevolence of the Mediterranean. In The Lusiads, they appear collectively rather than as named individuals (with the notable exception of [[thetis|Thetis]]), forming a divine chorus that assists, adorns, and celebrates the Portuguese voyage. Their most significant appearance is in Canto IX, where the "[[thetis|Nereid]]-choir" populates the Isle of Love, providing the erotic reward that crowns the sailors' labours. They represent the feminine face of the sea — not the threatening abyss of [[adamastor|Adamastor]] but the welcoming waters that receive the Portuguese as lovers.

## Classical Background

The Nereids were sea-nymphs in Greek mythology, often depicted riding dolphins or sea-horses and accompanying [[neptune|Poseidon]]. They were benevolent deities associated with calm seas and safe harbours. In Homer's *Iliad*, [[thetis|Thetis]] is the most famous [[thetis|Nereid]]; her sisters rise from the sea to mourn Patroclus. In art, they were frequently shown in the wedding procession of [[neptune|Poseidon]] and Amphitrite, or attending the birth of [[venus|Venus]].

## Role in The Lusiads

### Propelling the Ships (Canto II)

The Nereids first appear in a striking image of divine propulsion — the sea-nymphs physically push the Portuguese caravel:

> …sternward goes: Her circling Nereids raise and urge afar the threatening prow… [002-030]

This is divine favour made tactile: the Nereids' hands on the hull.

### The Nereids' Wrath (Canto II)

Yet Camões also notes the danger of disturbing them:

> …fearing to rouse the hurrying Nereids' wrath. [002-028]

The sea's benevolence is conditional; the Nereids can turn hostile.

### Adamastor's Vision (Canto V)

In the [[adamastor|Adamastor]] episode, the giant recalls seeing [[thetis|Thetis]] among her sister Nereids:

> One day I saw her with the Nereids rove, all bare and beauteous, on the sandy shore… [005-060]

This image of the Nereids at play — beautiful, unattainable — is the poem's most lyrical vision of marine divinity.

### The Isle of Love Choir (Canto IX)

The climactic [[thetis|Nereid]] appearance is in Canto IX, where they form the welcoming party at the Isle of Love:

> Now the fair Nereid-choir itself enrolled… [009-058]

The verb "enrolled" is suggestive: the Nereids are not random but organised, an ordered company of beauty marshalled for the Portuguese reward.

## Camões' Adaptation

Camões's Nereids are simultaneously classical and Christianised. Their physical assistance to the Portuguese ships — pushing the prow, urging it forward — has no close classical parallel; it is an image of divine favour made almost mechanical, as if the ship were a chariot and the Nereids its horses. At the Isle of Love, they become the handmaidens of [[venus|Venus]]'s reward, their beauty a foretaste of the paradise awaiting the Portuguese. Camões handles the collective Nereids with a lighter touch than he gives to individual deities, but their cumulative effect is substantial: they are the poem's pledge that the sea is not only danger but also welcome, not only [[adamastor|Adamastor]] but also love.

## Notes

The Nereids as a collective are distinct from individual sea-nymphs in the poem. Camões names [[thetis|Thetis]] specifically but keeps the other Nereids as a group — a chorus rather than soloists. This collective treatment is consistent with classical epic practice (Homer does the same with the Nereids mourning Patroclus) and serves a structural purpose: the [[thetis|Nereid]]-choir provides the poem with a register of impersonal divine beauty, a backdrop against which individual deities like [[thetis|Thetis]], [[venus|Venus]], and [[tethys|Tethys]] stand out.
