## Overview

Publius Vergilius Maro (70–19 BCE), known as Virgil, was the greatest poet of ancient [[rome|Rome]] and author of the *[[the-aeneid|Aeneid]]*. In *The Lusiads*, he is the primary classical model — the epic poet whose fusion of national myth and imperial destiny Camões seeks to replicate for [[portugal|Portugal]].

## In the Poem

Camões explicitly invokes the *[[the-aeneid|Aeneid]]* as the template for his own epic project. The structural parallels — a sea voyage, divine intervention, prophetic visions of future glory — are deliberate emulation of Virgil's model. Camões's claim is not to be Virgil's equal but to be [[portugal|Portugal]]'s Virgil, serving the same function for the Portuguese empire that Virgil served for [[rome|Rome]].

## The Aeneid as Structural Model

Virgil's *[[the-aeneid|Aeneid]]* provides Camões with more than a storehouse of epic conventions — it provides the structural argument for national epic itself. Virgil's poem narrates how Aeneas, a defeated Trojan prince, became the founder of the Roman people. The journey from Troy to Italy is simultaneously geographical movement and historical transformation: Aeneas sheds his Trojan identity and becomes Roman. Camões adapts this structure with remarkable fidelity. [[vasco-da-gama|Vasco da Gama]]'s voyage from [[lisbon|Lisbon]] to India is not merely a commercial expedition but a national founding act — the moment when [[portugal|Portugal]] transforms from a peripheral European kingdom into a global maritime empire.

The structural parallels are precise. Aeneas receives prophetic visions of Rome's future glory (Aeneid VI); [[vasco-da-gama|Vasco da Gama]] receives prophetic visions of Portuguese future glory from [[proteus|Proteus]] (Canto X). Aeneas contends with divine opposition (Juno); Gama contends with [[bacchus|Bacchus]]. Aeneas is protected by his divine mother (Venus); Gama is protected by [[venus|Venus]] as the patron of the Portuguese people. These parallels are not coincidental — they constitute Camões' argument that Portuguese achievement recapitulates Roman achievement on a global scale.

## Pietas and National Duty

Virgil's concept of *pietas* — duty to gods, family, and state — provided the template that Camões adapted for the Portuguese maritime empire. Aeneas's dutiful acceptance of his destiny becomes, in *The Lusiads*, [[vasco-da-gama|Vasco da Gama]]'s dutiful navigation toward [[portugal|Portugal]]'s imperial future.

But Camões modifies the Virgilian concept in an important respect. Aeneas's *pietas* is primarily retrospective — he carries his father and household gods from the ruins of Troy, preserving the past for a future he will not see. Gama's duty is prospective — he sails toward an unknown future, founding an empire whose full extent even he cannot imagine. The Portuguese hero does not carry the past on his shoulders; he opens a path into the future.

## Virgil and Homer

Virgil's own relationship to Homer — imitation, veneration, and competition — provided Camões with a model for engaging with classical predecessors. Just as Virgil claimed to surpass Homer by writing a national epic for [[rome|Rome]], Camões claims to surpass both by writing a national epic founded on historical truth rather than mythology.

This three-way relationship — Camões to Virgil to Homer — creates a genealogy of epic that legitimizes the Portuguese poem. Camões does not need to compete directly with Homer because Virgil has already done so; by surpassing Virgil (whose subject, after all, is merely the founding of a Mediterranean city), Camões claims the summit of the epic tradition. The Portuguese voyages, because they are historically true and globally significant, provide material superior to anything available to the ancients.

## Camões' Ambition: Outdoing the Masters

Camões' relationship to Virgil is not merely imitative but competitive. The *Aeneid* narrates the founding of [[rome|Rome]]; The Lusiads narrates the founding of a global maritime empire that exceeds Rome's reach. Where Virgil could only imagine empire within the Mediterranean basin, Camões can describe an empire spanning four continents. Where Virgil's prophetic visions reveal the future of a single city, Camões' prophetic visions (mediated through [[proteus|Proteus]] and the nymph's song in Canto X) reveal the future of a worldwide Portuguese imperium.

This competitive ambition is stated explicitly in the poem's opening stanzas, where Camões claims to sing of achievements greater than those of the ancient heroes. The claim is audacious but central to the poem's rhetorical strategy: Portuguese achievement is not merely equal to classical achievement but surpasses it, and therefore the Portuguese epic must surpass its classical models.

## The Renaissance Virgil

Camões inherited Virgil through the lens of Renaissance humanism, which had transformed the Roman poet from a medieval magician (the legendary "Virgil the Necromancer" of popular tradition) into the supreme model of literary art. Italian critics like Julius Caesar Scaliger had argued for Virgil's superiority over Homer; the *Aeneid* was taught in every humanist school; and every Renaissance epic poet — Ariosto, Tasso, Camões — wrote in Virgil's shadow.

Camões' particular contribution to this Renaissance Virgilian tradition is his insistence on historical truth. Unlike Ariosto's *Orlando Furioso*, which openly embraces romance and fantasy, The Lusiads claims to narrate events that actually happened. This claim to historicity — however embellished — distinguishes Camões from his Italian contemporaries and aligns him more closely with Virgil, whose *Aeneid* also claimed to narrate the historical origins of a people.

## Legacy

Camões' engagement with Virgil established the pattern for subsequent vernacular epics. By demonstrating that a modern national epic could compete with classical models on their own terms, Camões opened the way for Milton, who would perform a similar operation with Christian rather than national history in *Paradise Lost*. The line from Virgil through Camões to Milton is one of the central trajectories of European epic poetry.
