# Orlando Furioso

## Overview

*Orlando Furioso* is Ludovico Ariosto's Italian Renaissance epic, first published in 1516 and expanded to its final form of forty-six cantos in 1532. A continuation of Matteo Maria Boiardo's unfinished *Orlando Innamorato*, the poem weaves together the Carolingian chivalric tradition (the wars of Charlemagne against the Saracens) with Arthurian romance (quests, enchantments, and loves). Its central plot follows the knight Orlando (Roland), who goes mad with love for the pagan princess Angelica — hence the title, "Orlando Mad" — while multiple subplots interlace the adventures of Ruggiero and Bradamante, the mythical founders of the Este family.

Ariosto's poem represents the mature form of the Italian Renaissance epic: ironic in tone, dazzling in its narrative complexity, and audacious in its fusion of martial heroism with erotic enchantment. By Camões' time, *Orlando Furioso* had become one of the most widely read and imitated poems in [[europe|Europe]], setting the standard against which any new vernacular epic would be measured — including *The Lusiads*.

## Relationship to The Lusiads

Camões' relationship to Ariosto is one of competitive rivalry. *Orlando Furioso* represented the dominant Renaissance epic model when Camões began composing *The Lusiads* in the 1550s, and the Portuguese poet was determined to show that [[portugal|Portugal]] — not Italy — would produce the epic worthy of the age. The opening stanzas of *The Lusiads* acknowledge this rivalry directly. Camões tells his patron, King Sebastião, that Portuguese deeds are so great that they make the marvels of chivalric romance seem paltry by comparison:

> exceeding Rodomont, and Ruggiero vain, and Roland haply born of Poet's brain. (001-011)

Rodomont and Ruggiero are characters from *Orlando Furioso*; Roland (Orlando) is the poem's titular hero. By naming them explicitly and dismissing them as "vain" and "haply born of Poet's brain," Camões stakes his claim: *The Lusiads* will deal in historical truth, not poetic fantasy.

This scholarly gesture anchors what would otherwise be a generic boast in a specific intertextual relationship: Camões has read Ariosto, and he wants his readers to know he has surpassed him.

## Key Allusions

**The Supernatural Machinery**: The divine conflict between [[venus|Venus]] and [[bacchus|Bacchus]] that drives the plot of *The Lusiads* owes a structural debt to Ariosto's use of supernatural agents. In *Orlando Furioso*, the conflict between Christian and pagan supernatural forces (angels, demons, enchanters) provides the epic's cosmic frame. Camões adapts this model but replaces Ariosto's Christian-pagan supernatural binary with his own syncretic system: [[venus|Venus]] and [[bacchus|Bacchus]] are classical deities who operate within a providentially ordered Christian cosmos. The result is a divine machinery that has Ariosto's dramatic energy but Virgil's classical dignity.

**[[isle-of-love|The Isle of Love]] (Canto IX)**: The most direct parallel between the two poems is the Isle of Love episode in *The Lusiads* and Alcina's island in *Orlando Furioso* (Cantos VI–VII). In Ariosto's poem, the enchantress Alcina lures the knight Ruggiero to her pleasure-island, where he is seduced and detained until the sorceress Melissa rescues him. In Camões' poem, [[venus|Venus]] prepares an island of love where the Portuguese sailors are rewarded with nymphs and sensual delight after their long voyage.

Camões' treatment, however, transforms Ariosto's erotic enchantment into an allegory of divine reward. Where Alcina's island is a trap of illusory pleasure, [[venus|Venus]]' Isle of Love is a genuine gift from the goddess who has protected the Portuguese throughout their journey. The nymphs are not deceivers but brides, and the union of sailors and sea-nymphs symbolizes the fruitful union of [[portugal|Portugal]] with the oceans it has conquered.

**The Narrator's Voice**: Ariosto's poem is famous for its ironic, self-conscious narrator who comments wryly on the action and addresses the reader directly. Camões inherits this narrative self-consciousness but gives it a darker, more elegiac tone. Where Ariosto's narrator is urbane and amused, Camões' narrator is passionate, sometimes bitter, and increasingly preoccupied with the neglect of poetry in [[portugal|Portugal]] — a theme that reflects the Portuguese poet's own precarious position as a returned veteran seeking royal patronage.

**[[christian-expansion|Christian Expansion]] and Crusade**: Ariosto's poem is set against the backdrop of Charlemagne's wars against the Saracens, giving it an implicit crusading framework. Camões adopts this framework explicitly: *The Lusiads* frames Portuguese maritime expansion as a continuation of the crusading mission, bringing Christianity to the East while breaking the Muslim monopoly on the spice trade. The crusade ideal that runs through both poems reflects the shared ideological context of Mediterranean Christendom confronting Islamic power.

## Literary Significance

*Orlando Furioso* was, along with the *[[the-aeneid|Aeneid]]*, the most important epic model available to Camões. It demonstrated that a vernacular poet could achieve the scale and ambition of classical epic while incorporating the tonal range of romance — love, madness, enchantment, and humor alongside martial heroism. Camões learned from Ariosto how to make an epic feel contemporary, but he rejected what he saw as the Italian poet's frivolity.

The narrator of *The Lusiads* repeatedly insists on the historical truth of his matter, contrasting it with the "fabled, dreamy feats" of chivalric romance. This is a direct challenge to Ariosto's aesthetic: where Ariosto delights in the artificiality of his narrative, Camões stakes his poem's value on its documentary authenticity. The Portuguese sailors are not knights-errant wandering through enchanted forests but historical navigators charting real oceans.

Yet Camões could not escape Ariosto's influence. [[isle-of-love|The Isle of Love]] episode, the sympathetic treatment of pagan beauty, the narrative's multiple registers (heroic, erotic, elegiac, prophetic) — all bear the mark of Ariosto's example. What Camões achieved was not a rejection of the Ariostean model but its transformation: the same narrative energy and sensual richness, disciplined by Virgilian gravity and anchored in historical fact.
