# The Return Voyage to Lisbon

## Overview

The Return Voyage to Lisbon occupies the closing stanzas of Canto X and brings *The
Lusiads* to its formal conclusion. After Tethys has revealed the Machine of the World
and the Nereids have sung the full prophecy of Portuguese imperial destiny, the fleet
departs the Isle of Love laden with gifts, knowledge, and divine favor. The homeward
passage is swift and serene — a marked contrast to the perilous outward voyage.

## Departure from the Isle of Love

Having received Tethys's cosmic revelation and the nymphs' prophetic songs, the fleet
prepares to depart. The goddess has shown Gama everything the ocean encircles, and
the Portuguese now possess foreknowledge of the entire geography of the future empire:

> "Thus far, O Portingalls! to you was given"
> — [010-158]

Tethys bestows favorable winds, and the fleet sets sail with divine blessing:

> "Ye can embark, for fav'ouring blows the Wind"
> — [010-159]

## The Serene Crossing

The return voyage is described with striking brevity. The turbulent storms, the
monsters, the treacherous pilots — all the perils that defined the outward journey —
are absent. The fleet glides across "the Main serene," protected by the favor the
heroes have earned:

> "So fared they, cutting through the Main serene"
> — [010-160]

This serenity is thematically significant: the Portuguese have proven their worth
through suffering, and providence now grants them a peaceful passage. The poet's own
voice returns in these final stanzas, lamenting that his own "years glide downwards"
and his lyre is "out of tune."

## Arrival at the Tagus

Camões does not describe the actual landfall at Lisbon in detail. Instead, he uses
the final lines to address King Sebastian directly, urging the young monarch to favor
the poem and its poet, and to continue the great work of Portuguese expansion:

> "And when his courses pass the midway place"
> — [010-157]

The poem thus closes where it began — with the poet's voice, the dedicatory frame,
and the Tagus River that has borne witness to every Portuguese departure and return
since the age of Ulysses.

## Significance

The Return Voyage fulfills the epic's circular structure. The poem opened with the
fleet already on the Indian Ocean (*in medias res*), and it closes with the same
fleet sailing home. The brevity of the return passage — a few stanzas compared to
the elaborate descriptions of the outward voyage — reflects the epic convention that
the return is the hero's earned rest after the trials of the quest.

The serene homecoming also validates the poem's providential argument: the Portuguese
mission is divinely blessed from beginning to end, and the heroes return transformed
by knowledge, worthy of the empire that awaits them.
