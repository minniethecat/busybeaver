# Ormuz

## Overview

Ormuz (also Hormuz; Persian: *هرمز*) was a small but extraordinarily wealthy island-kingdom located at the mouth of the Persian Gulf, strategically positioned on the maritime trade routes between India, Persia, Arabia, and the Mediterranean. In The Lusiads, Camões presents Ormuz as one of the key prizes of Portuguese eastern expansion — a place that [[afonso-de-albuquerque|Afonso de Albuquerque]] would conquer and subdue, bringing its rich trade under Portuguese control.

The island appears in the prophetic visions of both Canto II (where Jupiter foretells Portuguese victories) and Canto X (where the sea-nymph [[thetis|Thetis]] reveals the future glories of the Portuguese empire). In both cases, Ormuz is presented as a proud kingdom that will be humbled by Portuguese arms — proof of Portugal's divinely ordained mastery of the eastern seas.

## In The Lusiads

### Jupiter's Prophecy (Canto II)

In Canto II, Jupiter convenes the gods on Olympus and declares that the Portuguese are destined to surpass all previous empires. As part of this prophecy, the king of the gods foretells the submission of Ormuz:

> eke shalt thou see the haughty Hormuz-reign
> twice taken, prostrate in their presence lie

The detail "twice taken" is historically accurate — Albuquerque first captured Ormuz in 1507, was forced to withdraw, then returned in 1515 to establish permanent Portuguese control. Camões' Jupiter knows the future with divine precision.

### Thetis' Vision (Canto X)

In the final canto, Thetis unveils the globe to Vasco da Gama and his crew, pointing out the future sites of Portuguese conquest. Ormuz appears prominently:

> yon Hormuz Kingdom strown along the strand,
> whose fame for riches still shall higher soar

Thetis also foretells Albuquerque's role in subduing the island:

> the Hormuz Parsi's heart which be his harm,
> refusing gentle rule as yoke of shame

And the tribute that Portugal will extract:

> he shall chastise high Hormuz' erring horde
> and twofold tribute claim with conq'uering hand

The language is notably harsher here than in other parts of the poem — the Ormuz Persians are described as an "erring horde" who must be "chastised." This reflects Camões' division of the non-European world into friendly kingdoms (like [[melinde|Melinde]]) that accept Portuguese alliance, and hostile ones (like Ormuz) that must be subdued by force.

### Economic Significance

Camões consistently associates Ormuz with wealth and trade. The kingdom is "strown along the strand" — its riches literally spread out for the taking. The "Hormuz Parsi" (Persian merchant) is characterized by his refusal of "gentle rule" — the Portuguese offer of peaceful trade on Portuguese terms — preferring his own commercial networks. The Portuguese conquest of Ormuz is thus framed not only as military victory but as the replacement of an older, non-Christian commercial order with a Portuguese-dominated one.

## Historical Context

The Kingdom of Ormuz was established in the 11th century on the Persian Gulf coast, later moving to the island of Jarun (now Hormuz Island) around 1300. By the 15th century, Ormuz had become one of the wealthiest trading ports in the Indian Ocean world, controlling the flow of spices, silks, pearls, and horses between India and the Middle East.

Afonso de Albuquerque first attacked Ormuz in 1507 with a fleet of seven ships, compelling its ruler to become a Portuguese tributary. However, dissent among his captains forced Albuquerque to withdraw. He returned in 1515, this time with full authority, and established a Portuguese fortress on the island. Portugal maintained control of Ormuz until 1622, when a combined Persian-English force expelled them.

The Portuguese occupation of Ormuz exemplified Albuquerque's strategy of controlling key choke points in Indian Ocean trade rather than attempting to conquer large territories. Together with [[goa|Goa]] (the administrative capital), [[malacca|Malacca]] (the gateway to Southeast Asia), and [[socotra|Socotra]] (guarding the Red Sea entrance), Ormuz formed one of the cornerstones of the Portuguese *Estado da Índia*.

## Notes

Camões' note on Ormuz is characteristically terse: "On Ormuz or Hormuz Isle." The Portuguese spelling "Ormuz" reflects the common European rendering of the Persian name. The island is today part of Iran's Hormozgan Province and remains strategically significant, located near the narrow Strait of Hormuz through which a significant portion of the world's oil passes.

The Portuguese fortress on Hormuz, the Fort of Our Lady of the Conception, was considered one of the most impressive Portuguese fortifications in the East when it was built. Its ruins can still be visited on Hormuz Island today, a silent reminder of the brief but intense period of Portuguese hegemony in the Persian Gulf.
