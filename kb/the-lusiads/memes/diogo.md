# Diogo

## Overview

Diogo was one of [[vasco-da-gama|Vasco da Gama]]'s men stationed ashore at [[calicut|Calicut]] to watch over the Portuguese merchandise after the [[catual|Catual]] had confiscated it. He appears in a single stanza of Canto VIII alongside his companion [[alvaro|Álvaro]], the two men left with the thankless task of guarding goods that had effectively already been seized.

Like many minor figures in The Lusiads, Diogo's brief appearance serves Camões' larger narrative purpose: to dramatize the precariousness of the Portuguese position in India and the quiet courage of ordinary crewmen who remained at their posts even when diplomacy failed.

## In The Lusiads

### The Guard Duty (Canto VIII, Stanza 94)

Diogo appears in the same stanza that names [[alvaro|Álvaro]]. After the merchandise is landed and promptly seized by the Catual, Gama is left with no good options. The two guards — Álvaro and Diogo — are given leave "to truck or vend as best they can," but in practice they can do little more than watch over goods that have already been taken:

> Álvaro and Diogo guard the store, / with leave to truck or vend as best they can.

The stanza captures the futility of their situation: officially they are guards with trading rights, but in reality they are hostages to the Catual's obstruction. Neither man is mentioned again after this brief appearance, yet their presence signals the broader vulnerability of the Portuguese expedition — dependent on the goodwill of local officials and powerless when that goodwill is withdrawn.
