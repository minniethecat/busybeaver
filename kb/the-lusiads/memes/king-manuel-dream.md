# King Manuel's Prophetic Dream

## Overview

King Manuel's Prophetic Dream is a pivotal episode in Canto IV of *The Lusiads*. As
King Manuel I rests on his golden bed, he receives a divine vision in which the rivers
Ganges and Indus appear as venerable elders, foretelling Portuguese sovereignty over
the lands they water. This dream provides the heavenly mandate that launches Vasco da
Gama's expedition to India.

The episode is central to the poem's providential argument: the Portuguese discoveries
are not merely human ambition but the fulfillment of a divine plan revealed through
royal prophecy.

## The Vision

The dream comes to Manuel as he lies "on bed of gold recline'd," a detail that
underscores his royal dignity. He feels himself lifted upward, soaring to a height
from which he surveys the entire world:

> "Here seems the King so high to soar away...
> Birds of the feral kind, and kine, and flocks"
> — [004-077–078]

From this vantage, he beholds two sacred rivers emerging from their sources:

> "From out the Fountains seemed he to behold...
> These hoary Fathers round their foreheads bore"
> — [004-079–080]

## The River Gods Speak

The rivers appear as ancient, venerable figures, "hoary Fathers" crowned with symbolic
garlands. The Ganges speaks first, identifying himself:

> "Illustrious Gange am I, whose farthest fount"
> — [004-082]

The river god declares that the land he waters is destined to submit to Portuguese rule.
The Indus echoes this prophecy. Together, the two great rivers of India effectively
offer the subcontinent to the Portuguese crown through divine dispensation.

> "No more that holy noble River said"
> — [004-083]

## The Royal Response

Awakening, Manuel summons his council and announces his determination to send a fleet
to India. The dream transforms personal conviction into imperial policy:

> "The King in counsel calls his lords to meet"
> — [004-084]

The dream thus serves as the narrative hinge between the history of early Portugal
(covered in Cantos III–IV) and the voyage of da Gama (which begins in earnest in
Canto V).

## Significance

Manuel's dream draws on a long epic tradition of prophetic dreams — from Aeneas's
vision of the Tiber to Alexander's dream of the founding of Alexandria. It positions
Manuel as an epic hero in his own right, one whose authority derives not from martial
prowess alone but from direct divine communication. The dream also provides Camões
with a theologically defensible justification for empire: Portugal's expansion is
not conquest but the fulfillment of a heavenly mandate.
