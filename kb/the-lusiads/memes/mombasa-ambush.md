# The Mombasa Ambush

## Overview

The Mombasa Ambush is a key episode in Canto II of *The Lusiads*, in which Vasco da
Gama's fleet arrives at Mombasa and is received with apparent friendship by its king.
Unknown to the Portuguese, the king has been corrupted by Bacchus and is preparing a
deadly ambush. The episode parallels the earlier Mozambique conspiracy but raises the
stakes — Venus herself must descend from Olympus to save the fleet.

## Arrival at Mombasa

The fleet, having escaped the treachery of Mozambique, sails northward along the East
African coast and anchors at Mombasa — a city celebrated for its wealth and power:

> "Mombasah's King untimely end hath laid
> For famous Gama by his fell deceit"
> — [002-001–002]

The king sends envoys to the ships, offering provisions and promising safe passage.
His emissaries speak with oily courtesy:

> "Unto the Herald straight our Chief replieth"
> — [002-013]

## The Hidden Plot

Behind the screen of diplomacy, Bacchus has already inflamed the king's hostility.
The god recognizes that Mombasa, with its strong fortifications and experienced navy,
is a far more dangerous adversary than Mozambique:

> "'Mid them a villain, who had undertane"
> — [002-010]

The king offers Gama pilots to guide the fleet into the harbor — the same stratagem
that had nearly succeeded at Mozambique. Gama, trusting the king's hospitality,
prepares to enter the port.

## Venus Intervenes

Venus, watching from Olympus, perceives the danger. She descends to the sea and with
her nymphs physically pushes the Portuguese ships away from the harbor entrance:

> "Descending seawards, Venus saves th' Armade,
> and fares on high her sovran Sire to greet"
> — [002-003–004]

The fleet, mysteriously unable to enter the harbor despite favorable winds, drifts
past Mombasa. Gama interprets this as divine providence and gives thanks. Venus then
ascends to Jupiter to learn the future destiny of her people:

> "She learns our future lot by Jove's good aid"
> — [002-005]

## Aftermath

Following the escape from Mombasa, Jupiter sends Mercury to warn Gama directly,
guiding the fleet to the friendly port of Melinde. The Mombasa episode thus sets up
the chain of divine interventions that lead the Portuguese to safety.

## Significance

The Mombasa Ambush deepens the poem's providential theme. The Portuguese are saved not
by their own wisdom — Gama was deceived — but by direct divine action. The episode also
introduces Mombasa as a major geopolitical adversary, foreshadowing Portuguese conflicts
with the Swahili city-states throughout the sixteenth century.
