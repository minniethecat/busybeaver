# Hercules

## Overview

Hercules (Greek: Heracles) is the greatest of the Greek heroes — the son of [[jupiter|Jupiter]] and Alcmena, whose twelve labours took him to the limits of the known world. In The Lusiads, he appears less as a character than as a symbol: his Pillars at the Strait of Gibraltar — inscribed *non plus ultra* ("nothing further beyond") — marked the boundary the ancients dared not cross. The Portuguese, by sailing beyond those pillars into the Atlantic and around [[africa|Africa]], made Hercules's limit their starting point. Camões invokes him repeatedly as the measuring stick of heroic ambition — and as the figure whose achievements the Portuguese have surpassed.

## Classical Background

Hercules was the son of [[jupiter|Jupiter]] (Zeus) and the mortal Alcmena, making him a demigod whose life was defined by his twelve labours — feats of strength and cunning that took him across the Mediterranean world. The tenth labour, the capture of Geryon's cattle, brought him to the western edge of the known world, where he set up the Pillars of Hercules (the Rock of Gibraltar and Jebel Musa) as the boundary between the Mediterranean and the outer Ocean. After his death, he was deified and joined the Olympians. He embodied the Greek ideal of heroic aretē — excellence achieved through suffering and labour.

## Role in The Lusiads

### The Measuring Stick (Canto III)

Hercules appears first as a standard of heroic achievement:

> …than Theseus slew, or vagueing Hercules. [003-145]

The comparison is implicitly competitive: the Portuguese heroes will be measured against Hercules and found not wanting.

### The Pillars Transcended (Canto IV)

The most symbolically charged reference is to "Hercules' Pillars":

> …insignia by renown well known, Hercules' Pillars on their pennons shown… [004-017]

The Portuguese ships fly Hercules's Pillars on their banners — a bold claim that they have not merely honoured Hercules's boundary but have made it their own.

### The Sea-Road Opened (Canto VI)

Hercules is credited with opening the Mediterranean — the first breach of geographical limits:

> …where opened Hercules the broad sea-road. [006-009]

The Portuguese continue what Hercules began: if he opened the Mediterranean, they opened the Atlantic and Indian Oceans.

### The Portals (Canto IX)

> …within the portals oped by Hercules. [009-029]

### Paired with Bacchus (Canto XI)

Hercules is twice paired with [[bacchus|Bacchus]] as a "Theban" pair — both sons of [[jupiter|Jupiter]] with mortal mothers, both boundary-crossers:

> Bacchus and Hercules, that Theban Twain. [011-044]

## Camões' Adaptation

Camões's Hercules is a figure of supersession. Every reference positions Hercules as the forerunner whose achievements the Portuguese have exceeded. This is not denigration — Hercules is genuinely admirable — but it is strategic: by measuring Portuguese exploration against Hercules's labours, Camões claims for his countrymen a heroic status equal to myth. The Pillars of Hercules become not a limit but a launch point. The Portuguese *non plus ultra* becomes *plus ultra*: further beyond.

## Notes

The pairing of [[bacchus|Bacchus]] and Hercules as "that Theban Twain" [011-044] is particularly pointed: both are sons of [[jupiter|Jupiter]] who conquered the East ([[bacchus|Bacchus]] in India, Hercules throughout the Mediterranean), and both serve as mythological forerunners of [[vasco-da-gama|Vasco da Gama]]'s voyage. But where [[bacchus|Bacchus]] actively opposes the Portuguese, Hercules is a silent symbol — a boundary marker the Portuguese have already passed before the poem begins.
