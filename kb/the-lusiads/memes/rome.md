# Rome

## Overview

Rome is the classical pole of The Lusiads — the ancient empire against which Portuguese achievement is measured and through which it is legitimized. Camões constructs [[portugal|Portugal]] as Rome's successor in the West: a nation that has surpassed the ancients in navigation, empire, and poetry. Roman heroes, Caesars, and the Virgilian epic tradition provide the framework within which Portuguese greatness is articulated.

## In The Lusiads

Camões opens the poem by positioning his epic above both the Greek and Roman traditions. The invocation in Canto I explicitly sets aside the classical subjects [001-011]:

> Cease the sage Grecian, and the Man of Troy

This gesture — dismissing Homer and Virgil's heroes to make room for Portuguese ones — is itself deeply Roman: Virgil had made the same move relative to Homer, and Camões claims the succession. The catalogue of ancient empires in the opening canto establishes Rome as the last in a sequence [001-032]:

> Persia, 'Assyria, Greece, and Rome.

Roman achievement serves as the explicit benchmark for Portuguese deeds. In Canto III, a Portuguese commander is measured against Roman standards [003-030]:

> since fame of mighty Rome ne'er did the deed.

The comparison extends to the battlefield carnage that [[portugal|Portugal]] inflicts, which is said to rival Rome's bloodiest days [004-014]:

> of mortal fierceness, such as Rome beheld, done by fierce Marius

The destruction of Roman nobility at Cannae is invoked as a parallel for Portuguese victories [003-124]:

> slain so many Knights of noble Rome, of their gold rings he sent

Roman military heroes serve as the benchmark for Portuguese commanders. In Canto V, [[vasco-da-gama|Vasco da Gama]] argues that [[portugal|Portugal]] produces its own Caesars [005-103]:

> Scipios and Caesars giveth Lusia-land,

The same canto declares Rome's poetic fame as the model for Portuguese epic [005-102]:

> to chaunt Aeneas' name, and Rome's high glories vaunt.

The comparison is repeated with Alexander the Great, as Afonso Henriques is measured against the greatest conquerors of antiquity [008-020]:

> Had Caesar, or King Alexander led

The most explicit Roman parallel appears in Canto XI, where a Portuguese youth is compared to a Roman exemplar [011-067]:

> E'en as the noble Youth of Roman strain

This passage draws on Roman republican history, framing Portuguese virtue through the lens of classical civic duty. The poem goes further in Canto XI, tracing Portuguese lineage to Trojan-Roman origins [011-045]:

> Who founded Troy and Rome for Italy.

## Lisbon as New Rome

A crucial dimension of Rome's presence in The Lusiads is the construction of [[lisbon|Lisbon]] as a "new Rome" — a city destined to succeed the ancient capital as the center of a world empire. In Canto VI, the prophecy declares [006-015]:

> prepare in Lisbon town a novel Rome t' install

This claim — that [[lisbon|Lisbon]] will replace Rome as the imperial capital — is the poem's most audacious argument. It is reinforced by the assertion that Portuguese deeds surpass Roman ones [006-038]:

> a doughtier deed than Rome's high race could claim

The comparison extends to the feminine sphere: in Canto IX, [[venus|Venus]] declares that Portuguese love shall outdo Roman [009-046]:

> shend all feats of rival Rome, I lief devise some mode

And in Canto X, the narrator asks forgiveness of the classical civilizations he claims to surpass [010-027]:

> Illustrious Greece, or Rome, my words condone!

The Portuguese commanders are directly compared to Romans at their peak [010-034]:

> the Roman's in best days of Rome.

Finally, the poem's endnotes document the Portuguese commander who revived the Roman model in the East [010-084]:

> who hath revived the name of Rome, their liege

## Historical Context

The Renaissance was shaped by the rediscovery and emulation of Roman antiquity. For Camões, writing in the shadow of [[the-aeneid|Virgil's Aeneid]], the Roman model was inescapable. [[portugal|Portugal]] claimed to have surpassed Rome in the scope of its empire — reaching India by sea rather than by land. The epic form itself, modeled on the [[the-aeneid|Aeneid]], performs the cultural continuity that the poem's content asserts.

The *translatio imperii* — the transfer of imperial authority from one people to another — was a well-established medieval and Renaissance concept. Camões adapts it for Portuguese purposes: just as Rome inherited Troy's imperial mandate, [[portugal|Portugal]] inherits Rome's. The poem's persistent comparison of Portuguese to Roman achievement is not mere flattery but a sustained argument for [[portugal|Portugal]]'s place in providential history.

## Notes

The poem's engagement with Rome is not merely comparative but competitive. Camões' "Cease the sage Grecian" claims for [[portugal|Portugal]] what Virgil claimed for Rome — that a new people has inherited the imperial destiny of the ancient world. The density of Roman references — spanning nine of the poem's ten cantos — demonstrates how thoroughly the Virgilian model shaped Camões' conception of epic, nation, and empire.
