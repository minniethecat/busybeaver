## Overview

Alexander III of Macedon (356–323 BCE), known as Alexander the Great, was the conqueror who built one of the largest empires of the ancient world. In *The Lusiads*, he is the most frequently invoked classical figure — appearing more often than Caesar, Trajan, or any other ancient conqueror — and serves as the archetype whose achievements Portuguese navigators are repeatedly called upon to surpass.

Camões' treatment of Alexander is characteristically double-edged: the Macedonian is both the supreme exemplar of earthly conquest and, precisely for that reason, the figure whose glory must be exceeded by Portuguese maritime achievement. The poet's relationship to the classical past, like his relationship to [[homer|Homer]], is one of admiring rivalry — acknowledgment of greatness followed by the claim to have transcended it.

## In the Poem

Alexander appears in multiple registers across the poem. In Canto I, Camões explicitly names him in the dedication to King Sebastian (001-003): "an Alexander shall in Thee be shown" — a prophetic promise that the young Portuguese king will equal or exceed the Macedonian's conquests. This direct address sets the poem's political program: Sebastian is to be Portugal's Alexander, and Camões his Homer.

The supersession motif is most explicit in Canto X, where the goddess Thetis foretells Portuguese achievements that will dwarf those of antiquity. Alexander's land empire — vast but bound to a single continent — is contrasted with the Portuguese maritime empire spanning Africa, Asia, and Brazil. The Portuguese "shall give new worlds unto the world," a claim Camões presents as factually establishing Portuguese superiority over all prior conquerors.

In the narrator's reflective passages, Alexander appears alongside other classical figures in catalogues of greatness-to-be-surpassed. Camões commands (001-003): "Cease Alexander, Trajan cease to joy / In the famed triumphs of your sword and spear" — a rhetorical gesture that simultaneously acknowledges and dismisses classical military glory.

## The Poet-Hero Paradigm

Alexander's most significant appearance in the poem concerns his relationship with [[homer|Homer]]. The anecdote — drawn from Cicero and Plutarch — that Alexander kept a copy of the *Iliad* under his pillow and lamented that he had no Homer to immortalize his deeds, becomes in Camões' hands a paradigm for his own relationship with Portuguese royalty.

"What Homer wrote that Alexander read" (001-003) establishes the classical model that Camões seeks to replicate: the great conqueror requires a great poet to immortalize his deeds. By invoking this paradigm, Camões positions himself as [[portugal|Portugal]]'s Homer and his patrons — particularly King Sebastian — as worthy successors to Alexander. The implication is audacious: Portuguese deeds exceed Greek and Roman ones, and *The Lusiads* will therefore surpass the *Iliad* and the *Aeneid*.

The paradigm also contains an implicit warning. Alexander died young, his empire fragmented, and his glory survived only because Homer had preceded him. Camões subtly reminds his Portuguese readers that without the poet, the conqueror is forgotten — a message directed at a court that was not always generous to its epic poet.

## Rhetorical Supersession

Like [[trajan|Trajan]] and [[julius-caesar|Caesar]], Alexander functions rhetorically as a figure to be surpassed — the *superatio* or "outdoing" topos that Renaissance epic inherited from classical rhetoric. Camões's claim is that Portuguese maritime achievement exceeds even Alexander's conquests through three distinct arguments:

First, **geographic scope**: Alexander conquered a single contiguous land empire; the Portuguese circumnavigated the globe, reaching India, China, Japan, and Brazil — territories Alexander never knew existed.

Second, **elemental daring**: Alexander conquered by land, marching where armies had marched before; the Portuguese conquered by sea, venturing into "seas never sailed before" (001-001) — a more terrifying and unprecedented enterprise.

Third, **divine purpose**: Alexander's conquests served personal ambition; Portuguese expansion serves divine providence, spreading Christianity to pagan lands. This theological argument transforms quantitative comparison into qualitative superiority.

These arguments, repeated throughout the poem, constitute Camões' central claim for Portuguese exceptionalism — a claim that requires Alexander as its benchmark precisely because the Macedonian represented the previous summit of human achievement.
