# Atlantis

## Overview

Atlantis in The Lusiads is the mythical island of classical tradition, invoked by Camões as a geographical anchor for the western ocean and a symbol of legendary wealth and antiquity. The name "Atlantis" appears as both a direct reference to the fabled lost continent and as an adjectival qualifier for the Atlantic Ocean itself — the "Atlantis plain" and "Atlantis' breadth" that the Portuguese navigators crossed on their voyage to India.

## In The Lusiads

### The Western Ocean (Canto III)

In the geographical survey of Europe, the Atlantic is described as:

> "the briny billows of Atlantis plain"

marking the western boundary of the European continent and the ocean that Portugal faces.

### The River's Course (Canto V)

Gama's narrative of the voyage notes the Gambia River flowing into the Atlantic:

> "ere in Atlantis' breadth his current sinketh"

identifying the Atlantic as the destination of Africa's westward-flowing rivers (005-018).

### The Treasure of Legend (Canto X)

In the prophetic vision of future Portuguese glory, Atlantis appears as a source of legendary wealth:

> "from far Atlantis-treas'ury hither roll'd"

invoking the classical tradition of Atlantis as a land of immense riches (010-011).

## Classical Context

The Atlantis myth originates with Plato's dialogues *Timaeus* and *Critias*, where it is presented as a powerful island civilisation that sank beneath the ocean. In Renaissance literature, Atlantis served as a byword for lost greatness and fabled wealth. Camões draws on this tradition while also using "Atlantis" as a poetic name for the Atlantic Ocean itself — the sea that defined Portugal's imperial vocation.

## Key Passages

- "the briny billows of Atlantis plain" (003-014)
- "ere in Atlantis' breadth his current sinketh" (005-018)
- "from far Atlantis-treas'ury hither roll'd" (010-011)

## Notes

The triple invocation of Atlantis across Cantos III, V, and X traces an arc from geographical description (the western ocean) through narrative voyage detail (rivers flowing into the Atlantic) to prophetic vision (legendary treasure). This progression mirrors the poem's structure — from history, through journey, to apotheosis.
