# Fernam Veloso

## Overview

Fernam Veloso (Fernão Veloso) was a Portuguese soldier who sailed with Vasco da Gama on
the first voyage to India (1497–1499). He is remembered in *The Lusiads* for a rash
episode on the South African coast: eager to explore the land and meet its inhabitants,
Veloso ventured ashore alone, was ambushed by local people, and barely escaped with his
life. Camões recounts the adventure with characteristic blend of drama and humour,
using Veloso to illustrate both the courage and the foolhardiness of the Portuguese
explorers.

## In The Lusiads

Veloso appears in Canto V, during Gama's extended narration to the King of Melinde. His
story is introduced in the canto's argument as one of the notable events of the African
coasting: "And, eke, Fernam Velloso's hardihood" ([005-004]).

### The Shore Adventure

The episode begins after peaceful first contact with the local inhabitants. The Portuguese
had traded gifts and the locals, "all mother-nude, with night-entinctur'd skin," had
come down to the shore to receive largesse:

> "In crowds they gather'd and so tame were they,
> the show of softness bred much daring in
> Fernam Velloso's brain to see the land,
> and thread the bushes with the barbarous band."
> — [005-038]

Veloso, emboldened by the seemingly friendly reception, ventures inland with the locals.
But when he fails to return, the crew grows anxious:

> "Now doth Velloso on his arm rely
> and, being arr'ogant, weens to wend secure;
> but when already overtime goes by
> wherein no sign of good I can procure"
> — [005-039]

### The Ambush and Rescue

Veloso eventually reappears, running toward the shore "with more of hurry than he showed
before" ([005-039]). A rescue boat under Coelho is dispatched, but before it can reach him,
a local man seizes Veloso:

> "a burly Blackmoor cast a bully hand
> on him, for fear their prisoner evade:
> Others and others coming, soon the band
> grappleth Velloso, who finds none to aid"
> — [005-040]

A skirmish ensues. The rescue party comes under a hail of "shafts and stones," and Gama
himself is wounded — "this hurt of arrow bore" ([005-041]). But Portuguese firepower
prevails, and Veloso is pulled to safety:

> "And, saved Velloso from such imm'inent fate,
> fast to the Squadron both the boats retirèd"
> — [005-042]

### The Joke

The episode concludes with one of the poem's rare moments of humour. Back aboard ship, a
crewmate teases Veloso:

> "'Holá, Velloso! sure that hilly crest
> is hard to climb as easy to descend.'"
> — [005-043]

Veloso's witty reply — that he ran back because he saw so many enemies and feared for his
comrades' safety without him — turns his undignified flight into a display of esprit de
corps:

> "'but when so many curs afar I ken'd
> packing, I hurried, for I 'gan to doubt me
> ill-luck might catch you were ye there without me.'"
> — [005-043]

## Historical Context

The historical Fernão Veloso was one of the soldiers aboard Gama's fleet. The episode on
the South African coast — likely near the Cape of Good Hope or modern Mossel Bay —
occurred in late 1497, before the fleet rounded the Cape. The encounter illustrates the
unpredictability of first contact: initial friendly trade could quickly turn hostile, and
Portuguese explorers navigated these situations with a combination of curiosity, courage,
and overwhelming firepower.

## Significance

In a poem dominated by gods, kings, and epic deeds, Veloso provides a human-scale story.
His rashness is both celebrated (as courage) and gently mocked (as foolhardiness), and the
crew's banter — culminating in his quick-witted retort — offers a rare glimpse of the
social world aboard the expedition. Camões uses the episode to leaven the epic gravity of
Gama's narration and to show that Portuguese heroism encompasses not only grand battles
but also individual acts of daring, however ill-advised.
