# Diu

## Overview

Diu is a small island off the southern coast of Gujarat, western [[india-land|India]], that became one of the most strategically important Portuguese strongholds in the Indian Ocean. In The Lusiads, Diu appears in two registers: as a prophetic vision of future Portuguese glory (Canto II), and as the site of epic naval confrontations celebrated in the poem's triumphant conclusion (Canto X).

For Camões, Diu embodies the Portuguese achievement in the East — a tiny island held against overwhelming odds, defended by valor and divine favor against the combined forces of Muslim powers from Egypt to Gujarat.

## In The Lusiads

### Canto II: Prophetic Vision

Diu enters the poem early, in [[jupiter|Jupiter]]'s speech to the assembled gods at the [[the-council-of-gods|Council of the Gods]]. The king of the gods foretells Portuguese victories in the East, singling out Diu [002-050]:

> Shalt see of Diu th' inexpugnable wall,

This prophecy establishes Diu as one of the destined sites of Portuguese achievement — the "inexpugnable wall" that will testify to Portuguese military engineering and courage.

### Canto X: The Great Sieges

The fullest treatment of Diu comes in Canto X, where the nymph sings of future Portuguese deeds in [[asia|Asia]]. The prophecy describes the fortress of Diu under siege [010-035]:

> of Diu enfamed for siege and battle dread,

The "fierce Armada" of Diu is invoked [010-060]:

> Nor less of Diu the fierce and fere Armade,

The enemy's fear of Portuguese power is registered [010-061]:

> while quakes illustrious Diu his name to hear

The Portuguese capture of Diu from its Muslim defenders [010-062]:

> Diu from the barbarous Rumé-warman rends;
> Diu, which beleaguer'd in his warrior way

The wealth that flows from control of the fortress [010-064]:

> shall yield in wealthy Diu the famous fort,

And the reciprocal relationship between Portuguese builders and the fortress they defend [010-067]:

> that buildeth Diu, this buildèd Diu defendeth.

## Historical Context

The island of Diu was an important trading port long before the Portuguese arrived, connected to the prosperous Sultanate of Gujarat. The Portuguese established a fortress on Diu in 1535, under a treaty with Sultan Bahadur Shah of Gujarat, who sought Portuguese naval support against the expanding Mughal Empire.

Diu became famous for two great sieges:

**The First Siege of Diu (1538)**: An Ottoman-Gujarati fleet under Hadım Suleiman Pasha besieged the Portuguese fortress, which was defended by a small garrison under Captain António da Silveira. The siege was broken when Portuguese reinforcements arrived from [[goa|Goa]].

**The Second Siege of Diu (1546)**: An even larger Gujarati force besieged the fortress. The defense, led by Dom João Mascarenhas and the legendary Dom João de Castro (then Viceroy of Portuguese India), became celebrated in Portuguese chronicles for its heroism. Castro famously pawned his own beard hair to raise money for the relief force.

These sieges — particularly the second — passed into Portuguese national mythology. Diu was the site where a handful of Portuguese soldiers, cut off from reinforcement, held an impregnable fortress against the massed forces of Eastern powers. Camões' prophecy in Canto X draws directly on this mythology, casting the siege defenses as proof of Portuguese martial superiority.

The Portuguese held Diu until 1961, when India annexed the territory along with [[goa|Goa]] and Daman.

## Strategic Significance

Diu's importance in The Lusiads derives from its symbolic value as much as its strategic reality. The fortress represented:
- Portuguese ability to project power thousands of miles from [[lisbon|Lisbon]]
- The triumph of Christian arms over Muslim forces (the "Rumé-warman" — the Rumis, or Ottoman Turks)
- The reciprocal bond between Portuguese soldiers and the empire they built ("that buildeth Diu, this buildèd Diu defendeth")

## Notes

Diu was one of the three key Portuguese strongholds in western India, alongside [[goa|Goa]] and Daman. The Portuguese called these possessions the *Estado da Índia* ("State of India"). In The Lusiads, Diu appears only in prophetic passages (Cantos II and X) rather than in the narrative present (Cantos I–IX), because the sieges that made it famous occurred after [[vasco-da-gama|Gama]]'s voyage. Camões uses Diu as evidence that the destiny initiated by Gama's journey was fulfilled — the "inexpugnable wall" is the poem's proof that Portuguese valor, once planted in the East, could not be uprooted.
