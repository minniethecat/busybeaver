# The Wedding of the Nymphs

## Overview

The Wedding of the Nymphs is the climactic reward episode of *The Lusiads*, occupying
the whole of Canto IX. After the Portuguese have completed their mission in India,
Venus prepares a magnificent island paradise in the midst of the ocean, where she
arranges the marriage of the weary sailors to a chorus of beautiful Nereids. The
episode serves as an allegorical apotheosis — the heroes are granted divine love and
rest as their reward for the trials they have endured.

## Preparation of the Isle

As the fleet sails homeward from Calicut, Venus surveys the suffering her people have
endured and determines they deserve repose:

> "Venus displays an Island excellent:
> Here from all nobly suffered loss and pain,
> Rest and Repose they find convenient"
> — [009-004–006]

The goddess descends in her chariot drawn by swans to the Idalian mountains, where she
enlists Cupid to aid her plan. Cupid's arrows will inflame the Nereids with love for
the Portuguese sailors:

> "Wherefore I pray thee, Son! forthwith go fire...
> 'Tis my good will that in the Neptune-reign"
> — [009-048–050]

## The Isle of Love

The island itself is a vision of surpassing beauty — three hillocks, crystalline streams,
and every kind of fruit-bearing tree:

> "Three fairy hillocks threefold headlets showèd...
> Pomona's choicest gifts spontaneous grow"
> — [009-062–066]

When the Portuguese land, they encounter the nymphs and immediately pursue them through
the forest in a scene that blends classical pastoral with Renaissance courtly love.

## The Chase and Union

The chase is described with playful eroticism. Leonardo, a Portuguese soldier, pursues
a nymph who initially flees but eventually yields:

> "No longer fled the lovely Nymph, to play...
> what hungry kissings wake the wood!"
> — [009-090–091]

The chief nymph, Tethys, leads Gama himself to a palace on the island's summit where she
reveals to him the future glories of the Portuguese empire:

> "One, chiefest She, whose mandate proudly led...
> Taking his hand in hers she guides her guest"
> — [009-094–095]

## Significance

The Wedding of the Nymphs has been interpreted allegorically in multiple ways: as the
union of heroic endeavor with divine favor, as Portugal's marriage to the sea, and as
the Renaissance synthesis of classical mythology with Christian providentialism. The
episode also serves a structural purpose — the peaceful interlude prepares the reader
for the prophetic revelations of Canto X.
