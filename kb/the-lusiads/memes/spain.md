# Spain

## Overview

Spain — often referred to as Castile in the poem — is [[portugal|Portugal]]'s sole land neighbor and the dominant power of the Iberian Peninsula. In The Lusiads, Spain appears as both a rival and a mirror: Camões uses Spain to frame Portuguese identity, defining his nation's greatness partly through contrast with and competition against its larger neighbor. The relationship is complex — ancestral, adversarial, and at times admiring.

## In The Lusiads

Spain is introduced in Canto III as part of the geographical panorama of [[europe|Europe]], described in the narration to the King of [[melinde|Melinde]] [003-025]:

> here her presence showeth noble Spain,

The poem traces the intertwined royal lineages of [[portugal|Portugal]] and Spain. The founding figure of Portuguese independence, Afonso Henriques, is situated within a Spanish dynastic context. The Spanish king Afonso VI is described as [003-027]:

> his star raised high to sway Spain as her saviour

And the Portuguese king is introduced through his Spanish lineage [003-031]:

> A King there was in Spain, Afonso hight,

This Afonso is Afonso VI of León and Castile, whose daughter Teresa married Henry of Burgundy — the union from which the Portuguese royal line descends.

The military rivalry between the two kingdoms is a recurring motif. Castile's martial pride is invoked in battle contexts [003-107]:

> This proud Castile's bravades with equal pride

Spain is portrayed as a mighty adversary bent on conquest [003-111]:

> mighty Monarch, sworn our noble Spain to conquer and command

The Castilian threat is personified [003-042]:

> the superb Castilian 'gins prepare

In Canto XI, Spain appears as the great power whose strength threatens [[portugal|Portugal]] [011-055]:

> All these strong powers uniteth haughty Spain

Against this consolidated might, [[portugal|Portugal]]'s achievement — maintaining independence and building a global empire — is presented as all the more remarkable.

## Spain Beyond Rivalry

While the political and military rivalry dominates the poem's treatment of Spain, Camões also acknowledges the broader Iberian context that the two kingdoms share. In Canto IV, Spain is invoked in a defensive context — the nation must be secured against treachery [004-057]:

> and thus our gen'eral Spain secure from Julian-craft

The reference to "Julian-craft" alludes to Count Julian, whose betrayal opened Iberia to the Moorish invasion — a historical wound shared by all Iberian Christians.

Spain also appears in the poem's geography of the known world. In Canto V, divine aid is extended to Spanish as well as Portuguese warriors [005-017]:

> who often holp the sons of Spain brave slaught'ering of the Moors

The Pyrenees, the mountain boundary, divides the peninsula from France [006-064]:

> of Pyrenee departing Gaul from Spain

Spanish speech is a recognized language in the poem's polyglot world [007-033]:

> He speaketh well the speech of Spain

Spain appears as a distant refuge in the mythological register [007-076]:

> only ken they bide in distant Spain, where bathe my nest and Phoe...

And Spanish warriors are acknowledged for their valor [007-079]:

> when Spain's warlike sons to fight would...

Portuguese heroes who fought in Spain are celebrated [008-034]:

> three in France and Spain who won a name that never sha...

And Spain's distant origins — the Visigothic migration — are noted [008-053]:

> hordes of foreigners from unknown Spain.

The poem closes with a benediction on Spain [011-140]:

> now thou livest safe, O noble Spain!

This final address — "O noble Spain!" — reveals the complexity of Camões' attitude: rivalry, yes, but also recognition of a shared Iberian destiny.

## Historical Context

During the century before The Lusiads' publication, [[portugal|Portugal]] had fought to preserve its independence against Castilian claims. The [[battle-of-aljubarrota|Battle of Aljubarrota]] (1385) secured Portuguese sovereignty under the Aviz dynasty. By 1572, the threat of Spanish absorption was imminent — the Iberian Union would begin just eight years later, in 1580. Camões' emphasis on Portuguese distinctness from Spain carries the weight of this impending crisis.

Yet the relationship was not purely adversarial. The royal houses of [[portugal|Portugal]] and Castile were deeply intermarried; Portuguese and Castilian nobles fought alongside each other in the Reconquista; and the two kingdoms shared a language family, a religion, and a crusading ideology. Camões' treatment of Spain reflects this ambivalence — the poem can move from hostility to admiration within a single canto.

The port of Seville, Spain's gateway to the Americas, was [[lisbon|Lisbon]]'s great commercial rival. The Tordesillas line (1494) had divided the non-[[europe|Europe]]an world between [[portugal|Portugal]] and Spain, making the two Iberian kingdoms the joint arbiters of global empire — a shared monopoly that The Lusiads both celebrates and resents.

## Notes

The poem uses "Castile" and "Spain" somewhat interchangeably, reflecting the political reality of the period — Castile was the dominant kingdom within the Spanish composite monarchy, and its language and political culture set the terms for Iberian competition. The range of Spanish appearances — from adversarial to admiring — makes Spain the most ambivalently treated nation in the poem, simultaneously the enemy against which [[portugal|Portugal]] defines itself and the brother kingdom with which it shares a crusading past.
