# The Reconquista

## Overview

The Reconquista (Spanish and Portuguese for "Reconquest") was the centuries-long military campaign by Christian kingdoms to recapture the Iberian Peninsula from Muslim rule (711–1492). In The Lusiads, Camões presents the Reconquista not as a completed medieval enterprise but as an ongoing sacred mission — one that Portugal carries forward from the Iberian battlefields to the Indian Ocean.

The Reconquista provides the ideological template for Portuguese expansion: the same crusading spirit that drove Christians southward against the Moors in Iberia now drives them eastward against "infidels" in [[asia|Asia]] and [[africa|Africa]].

## In The Lusiads

Camões embeds the Reconquista deeply in the poem's historical narrative. In Canto III, he traces the origins of the Portuguese kingdom from the founding figure of [[afonso-henriques|Afonso Henriques]], whose victories against the Moors established both the nation and its crusading character.

The poem describes the Christian triumph at Ourique (1139), where Afonso Henriques supposedly received divine sanction for Portuguese kingship [003-030], and frames subsequent Portuguese history as a continuation of holy war [003-032]:

References to "Holy War" explicitly connect the Reconquista's crusading past to Portugal's overseas present. The ideology is made explicit at the poem's opening, where the navigators are described as those "Who hied them Holy Faith and Reign to spread" [001-010] — the dual mission of Christianity and empire inherited from the Reconquista.

The [[battle-of-salado|Battle of Salado]] (1340) is the poem's great Reconquista set-piece. Camões presents it as the moment when [[portugal|Portugal]] and [[castile|Castile]] set aside their rivalries to face the common Muslim enemy [003-107]:

Camões frames the battle in explicitly crusading terms — Maria, Queen of Castile, appeals to her father [[king-afonso-iv|Afonso IV]] of Portugal for aid against the Marinid invasion [003-110–111], and the combined Christian victory is presented as providential.

The conquest of [[ceuta|Ceuta]] in 1415, described in the poem, is the direct product of Reconquista momentum — Portuguese crusaders crossing into [[africa|Africa]] for the first time [004-042]. This moment bridges the medieval Reconquista and the age of overseas expansion that The Lusiads celebrates.

## Historical Background

The Reconquista began in 718 with Pelayo's victory at Covadonga and concluded in 1492 with the fall of Granada. Portugal completed its own Reconquista earlier — the Algarve was secured by 1249, giving the kingdom its modern borders well before [[spain|Spain]] finished its southern campaign.

This early completion had profound consequences. With no Muslim frontier left to fight at home, Portugal turned outward — first to [[ceuta|Ceuta]] (1415), then down the African coast, and finally to [[india-land|India]] (1498). Camões presents this outward turn not as mere opportunism but as the logical extension of crusading zeal: the Reconquista did not end at the Algarve; it simply changed theaters.

The crusading orders, especially the [[order-of-christ|Order of Christ]] (successor to the Templars in Portugal), provided both the institutional framework and the ideological vocabulary for this transition. [[prince-henry-the-navigator|Prince Henry the Navigator]], Governor of the Order of Christ, embodied the fusion of crusader and explorer.

## Significance

The Reconquista matters in The Lusiads for three reasons:

1. **National origin**: Portugal was born from the Reconquista — it was a county carved out of León to fight the Moors, and its first king defined himself through victories against Muslim forces.

2. **Ideological continuity**: Camões presents the Indian Ocean voyages as the continuation of Reconquista holy war by other means. The same God who granted victory at Ourique and Salado now guides [[vasco-da-gama|Gama]] to [[calicut|Calicut]].

3. **Providential narrative**: The Reconquista established the template of divine favor that structures the entire poem — Christian forces, outnumbered but righteous, triumph through God's will. This pattern repeats from medieval Iberia to the [[isle-of-love|Isle of Love]].

## Notes

The term "Reconquista" itself is a 19th-century scholarly coinage; medieval Christians spoke of "recovery" (*recuperatio*) or simply of holy war. Camões does not use the word — he invokes the concept through specific battles, providential language, and the recurring motif of Christian victory over Muslim forces. The Reconquista functions in The Lusiads less as a named event than as the deep structure of Portuguese historical consciousness.
