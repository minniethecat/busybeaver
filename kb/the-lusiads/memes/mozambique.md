# Mozambique

## Overview

Mozambique (Moçambique) is an island and coastal region in East Africa where [[vasco-da-gama|Vasco da Gama]]'s fleet made its first landfall after rounding the [[cape-of-good-hope|Cape of Good Hope]]. The episode in Canto I of The Lusiads depicts the encounter between the Portuguese and the Muslim inhabitants of the island — an encounter that begins with apparent hospitality but conceals religious hostility, goaded by [[bacchus|Bacchus]], who fears that Portuguese fame will eclipse his own in the East.

## Geographical Context

Mozambique Island (Ilha de Moçambique) is a small coral island off the coast of present-day northern Mozambique, connected to the mainland by a narrow causeway. The island's deep-water harbour and strategic position along the East African coast made it a vital trading post. By the late 15th century, Mozambique was part of a network of Swahili city-states stretching from [[mombasa|Mombasa]] in the north to Sofala in the south — a network that dominated the Indian Ocean trade in gold, ivory, and slaves.

The island's inhabitants were Muslim, and their culture reflected centuries of contact with Arab, Persian, and Indian traders. The Portuguese arrival in 1498 — the first European ships to appear in these waters — disrupted the established commercial and political order.

## In The Lusiads

Mozambique is the setting for the opening movement of the fleet's Indian Ocean journey, occupying much of Canto I. The episode establishes the pattern of Muslim-Christian encounter that recurs throughout the poem: outward hospitality masking religious antagonism, and divine protection ensuring Portuguese survival.

### The First Contact

The islanders approach the anchored Portuguese ships, their distinctive cotton garments signalling a culture utterly different from the European world:

> They come costumèd all in cotton gear,
>   of hues contrasting, stripèd, chequed, and white;
>   one zone-girt cloth around the waist they wear,
>   other they throw on back in airy plight
> — [001-055]

The exchange of introductions establishes mutual curiosity and mutual suspicion. The islanders enquire in Arabic about the strangers' origins, and Gama's men respond with dignity:

> We are the Occidental Portughuese;
>   And, seeking Orient lands, we sail the seas.
> — [001-058]

The islanders identify their home in one of the poem's most striking place definitions:

> man calls our little island 'Mozambíque.'
> — [001-062]

The description of Mozambique as a refuge for "ev'ery merchantman that stems the tide, / from Quiloa or Sofálah or Mombas" situates the island within the wider commercial geography of the Swahili Coast.

### The Treachery

Beneath the surface of hospitality, the island's governor recognizes the Portuguese as Christians and determines to destroy them. [[bacchus|Bacchus]], the divine antagonist, travels to Mozambique to incite the Muslims, appearing in a dream to warn that the Portuguese will subjugate the entire East African coast.

Gama, unaware of the governor's designs, displays Portuguese arms and armour:

> This said, he bids his armourers diligent
>   bring arms and armour for the Moorman viewer:
>   Come sheeny harness, corselets lucident,
>   the fine-wove mail-coat and plate-armour sure
> — [001-075]

Yet the governor conceals his hatred behind a smiling face, promising friendship while planning destruction. The fleet escapes only through the timely intervention of [[venus|Venus]], who sends warning winds and leads the Portuguese toward the more hospitable port of [[melinde|Melinde]].

### Aftermath

The Portuguese later returned to Mozambique and established it as a major colonial possession. The island became the capital of Portuguese East Africa and remained under Portuguese rule until 1975. Camões, writing in the 1560s, knew Mozambique well — he had lived there for several years — and the island's depiction in The Lusiads is informed by direct personal experience.
