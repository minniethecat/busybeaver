# Christian Expansion

## Definition

Christian expansion refers to the ideology that Portuguese overseas exploration was divinely sanctioned as a mission to spread Christianity. This fusion of crusading zeal with commercial and imperial ambition provided the moral framework within which the Portuguese Crown justified its maritime empire. In The Lusiads, Camões presents the voyage of [[vasco-da-gama|Vasco da Gama]] as simultaneously a feat of navigation, a commercial venture, and a sacred mission.

## In The Lusiads

The poem's opening canto establishes the religious dimension of the Portuguese enterprise from the outset:

> Who hied them Holy Faith and Reign to spread (001-010)

The pairing of "Holy Faith" with "Reign" (empire) encapsulates the dual nature of Portuguese expansion — spiritual and temporal power advancing together. This was not unique to Camões; it reflected the official ideology of the Portuguese Estado da Índia, which operated under the banner of the [[order-of-christ|Order of Christ]].

The poem frames [[portugal|Portugal]] as Christendom's vanguard against Islam, historically rooted in the Reconquista. References to "Christian knaves" (001-087) and "[[crusade-ideal|Holy War]]" (003-032) connect the Indian Ocean voyage to centuries of crusading tradition.

## Historical/Cultural Context

The Portuguese Age of [[maritime-exploration|Discovery]] was inextricably linked to the military orders and crusading ideology. [[prince-henry-the-navigator|Prince Henry the Navigator]] was the Governor of the [[order-of-christ|Order of Christ]], and Portuguese ships sailed with the Cross of the Order on their sails. The *Padroado Real* (Royal Patronage) granted the Portuguese Crown ecclesiastical authority over newly discovered lands.

The Treaty of Tordesillas (1494) and subsequent papal bulls divided the non-Christian world between [[portugal|Portugal]] and [[spain|Spain]], institutionalizing Christian expansion as a legal and spiritual mandate. Camões wrote in the aftermath of these events, when the Portuguese empire was at its height but beginning to show strains.

## Notes & Further Reading

Camões' treatment of non-Christian peoples oscillates between crusading hostility (toward Muslims) and ethnographic curiosity (toward Hindus). This complexity reflects the tension between the official ideology of Christian expansion and the pragmatic realities of Portuguese interaction with diverse cultures across the Indian Ocean.
