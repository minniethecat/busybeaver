# Fortune

## Overview

Fortune (Latin: *Fortuna*) is a personified abstraction in The Lusiads representing the mutable power of fate and chance. Unlike [[venus]] and [[jupiter]], who actively advance Portuguese interests, Fortune operates as an ambivalent force.

Sometimes she aids Portuguese endeavors; sometimes she threatens them. Her presence in the poem reflects the Renaissance preoccupation with the tension between human effort and divine or cosmic will.

## Classical Background

In Roman religion, Fortuna was a major goddess of luck and fate, often depicted with a rudder (steering destiny), a cornucopia (bestowing abundance), and a wheel (signifying mutability).

Boethius's *Consolation of Philosophy* established Fortuna's wheel as the central medieval and Renaissance metaphor for the reversals of fate. Camões inherits this rich tradition and deploys Fortune within the providential framework of his Christian epic.

## The Wheel of Fortune

The wheel of Fortune is the poem's most potent image of mutability. In the classical and medieval tradition, Fortune's wheel turns ceaselessly — those at the top are cast down, and the lowly are raised up, without regard to merit. Camões does not describe the wheel explicitly in the poem, but the concept pervades his treatment of historical reversal. Portuguese kings rise and fall, navigators triumph and perish, and the poet himself — writing from exile and poverty — embodies the wheel's downward turn.

This Boethian framework serves a structural purpose in the epic. By acknowledging Fortune's power over even the greatest human achievements, Camões introduces dramatic tension into a poem that otherwise celebrates Portuguese invincibility. The reader is never allowed to forget that the glory narrated is fragile, dependent on forces beyond human control.

## Role in The Lusiads

Fortune appears most prominently in the later cantos, where the poem's tone shifts from triumphant expansion to somber reflection on the costs of empire. She is invoked in moments of reversal and suffering, reminding readers that Portuguese glory is not immune to the vicissitudes of fate.

In Canto IX, Fortune directs the path of a historical figure [009-084]:

> Here willed his Fortune, he should wing his way

The phrase captures Fortune's ambivalent role — she "wills" but her will may align with or oppose human intention.

The most extended treatment of Fortune comes in Canto XI, where the narrator reflects on her universal power [011-196]:

> Fortune, who vaunteth o'er the world her might

This passage invokes the classical *topos* of Fortune's dominion over mortal affairs, but Camões places it within a Christian framework where divine providence ultimately supersedes Fortune's wheel.

Immediately following, the poet laments Fortune's cruelty [011-219]:

> Oh, force of Fortune grievous, sore to dree,

This is Camões at his most Boethian — Fortune is acknowledged as a real force of suffering, even as the poem's overarching theology insists on providential order.

## Fortune in Renaissance Thought

Camões's treatment of Fortune reflects a broader Renaissance debate about the relationship between human agency and external forces. Machiavelli's *The Prince* (Chapter XXV) famously argued that Fortune controls perhaps half of human affairs, leaving the other half to free will and *virtù*. Camões, writing from within a Christian framework, gives Fortune a more constrained role — she is real but subordinate to divine providence.

Petrarch's *Remedies for Fortune Fair and Foul* provided another influential model, counseling Stoic resignation in the face of Fortune's reversals. The Lusiads' narrator, particularly in the later cantos, adopts a similarly philosophical stance — acknowledging suffering while insisting on the ultimate meaning of Portuguese achievement.

What distinguishes Camões from his Italian predecessors is his national focus. Fortune is not merely an abstract philosophical problem but a force operating within Portuguese history. The poem's catalogue of kings and heroes is, in effect, a demonstration of human agency triumphing over Fortune — Portuguese *virtù* (in the Machiavellian sense) bending Fortune's wheel toward a divinely ordained destiny.

## Relationship with Divine Providence

A central tension in The Lusiads is the relationship between Fortune (pagan caprice) and [[divine-providence|Divine Providence]] (Christian order). Camões generally resolves this by presenting Fortune as the mechanism through which Providence operates — what appears as random chance is ultimately divine will. This synthesis allows him to retain the classical poetic vocabulary of Fortune while affirming Christian theology.

## Fortune and the Poet's Personal Voice

The most distinctive feature of Camões's treatment of Fortune is its autobiographical resonance. The poet wrote The Lusiads after years of hardship — shipwreck, imprisonment, exile in the East — and his invocation of Fortune's cruelty carries the weight of lived experience. When the narrator laments "the force of Fortune grievous," the reader hears not merely a literary convention but the voice of a man who has suffered real reversals.

This personal dimension distinguishes Camões from his classical models. Virgil's narrator is largely impersonal; Homer's is barely present. Camões, by contrast, inserts himself into the poem as a witness to Fortune's operations — a sailor, a soldier, a poet whose own life exemplifies the mutability he describes. The result is a treatment of Fortune that is at once classical in its vocabulary and intensely modern in its subjectivity.

## Related Deities

- [[venus]] — Opposes Fortune's caprice through active divine intervention
- [[jupiter]] — Supreme authority whose will overrides Fortune's wheel
- [[fame]] — Frequently paired with Fortune in Renaissance discourse on human achievement
- [[bacchus]] — Like Fortune, represents opposition to Portuguese destiny

## Notes

Fortune is not a major narrative agent in The Lusiads — she does not appear in the [[the-council-of-gods|Council of the Gods]] or intervene directly in the action. Her role is rhetorical and thematic, invoked by the narrator and characters to make sense of suffering and reversal within the poem's providential framework.
