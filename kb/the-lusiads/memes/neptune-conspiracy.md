# Neptune's Conspiracy with Bacchus

## Overview

After the Portuguese fleet departs from Melinde and sails toward India, Bacchus —
still determined to thwart their mission — descends to the undersea realm to enlist
Neptune's aid. Convincing the sea-god and his court that the Portuguese threaten
their dominion, Bacchus orchestrates a devastating tempest. This episode in Canto VI
represents the climax of divine opposition to the voyage, preceding Venus's
intervention to calm the storm.

## In The Lusiads

The episode begins with Bacchus (Lyaeus) descending beneath the waves to find Neptune:

> "Descends Lyaeus seaward; Neptune haileth"
> — [006-003]

Neptune, initially indifferent, is persuaded by Bacchus's rhetoric. The sea-god
comes to see the Portuguese as a threat to his ancient dominion:

> "Already Neptune, deeming worth his heed"
> — [006-024]

Neptune summons his fellow sea-deities — Ocean, Tritons, Nereids — and rallies
them against the Portuguese:

> "Came from another quarter Neptune's fere"
> — [006-029]

The divine assembly of the waters echoes the earlier Council of the Gods on Olympus,
but here the mood is hostile:

> "And, eke, ye Water-gods, who ne'er endure"
> — [006-036]

## Key Passages

The storm that Neptune unleashes is described with ferocious energy. The winds
battle one another and the waves rise to mountainous heights:

> "Such were the gale-gusts, never Tempest blew"
> — [006-082]

Even as the storm rages, Vasco da Gama recognises its supernatural origin.
The hand of Bacchus is visible in the chaos:

> "Here, certès, Bacchus' handwork I descry"
> — [006-094]

The tempest reaches its terrifying peak when the winds seem to tear the very fabric
of the sky. Camões's description draws on the classical storm-epic tradition:

> "Now rising high, now plunging deep, the prow
> Of each tall ship alternate sinks and soars"
> — [006-078]

## Significance

Neptune's conspiracy with Bacchus represents the climax of divine opposition in
*The Lusiads*. While Bacchus had previously worked through human agents (the
Moorish Sultan, the treacherous pilot), here he escalates to cosmic force.
The episode demonstrates that the Portuguese voyage faces not merely human
enemies but the elemental powers of nature itself — and that these too can
be overcome through divine favour on the side of Portugal.

The episode also serves as a set-piece demonstration of Camões's skill at
combining classical epic machinery (Neptune's court, the storm) with
Christian providentialism (Venus as the force of divine mercy that stills
the waves).

## Notes

Camões's storm scene explicitly imitates the famous tempests of Virgil (*Aeneid* I)
and Lucan (*Pharsalia* V), but with a significant structural difference: where
classical storms typically open an epic (as in the *Aeneid*), Camões places his
at the midpoint, reserving the opening for the more original Council of the Gods.
The Neptune-Bacchus alliance also inverts the typical Homeric pattern — where gods
usually oppose one another, here two deities collaborate against mortals.
