# Lusus and the Mythical Founding of Lusitania

## Overview

The foundation of Lusitania — the ancient name for Portugal — is traced by Camões to a
mythical figure named Lusus (or Lysa), described as the son or companion of the god
Bacchus. This foundation myth appears in two key passages of the poem: first narrated by
Vasco da Gama to the King of Melinde in Canto III, and later elaborated by Paulo da Gama
when interpreting the Portuguese banners before the Samorim of Calicut in Canto VIII.
By linking Portugal's origins to Bacchus, Camões elevates the nation's pedigree to the
level of classical antiquity.

## In The Lusiads

In Canto III, Vasco da Gama recounts the etymology while describing his beloved homeland:

> "This was the Lusitania, name applied
>   by Lusus or by Lysa, sons, they say,
>   of ancient Bacchus, or his boon compeers,
>   eke the first dwellers of her eldest years."
> — [003-029]

This passage situates Lusitania's origins within the Bacchic mythology: after his conquests
in India, Bacchus (Dionysus) journeyed westward, and his companion Lusus settled in the
westernmost part of the Iberian Peninsula, giving the land its name.

In Canto VIII, Paulo da Gama presents the first banner to the Samorim, showing the figure
of Lusus:

> "This first in sight is Lusus, from whose fame
>   our 'Lusitania' gained her royal name."
> — [008-010]

Paulo further clarifies the relationship:

> "He was the Theban's son or comrade tried,
>   the God who divers regions overran;
>   it seems he came to hold our Spanish nide,
>   pursuing conquests which his youth began"
> — [008-011]

The thyrsus — the ivy-wreathed wand carried by Bacchus and his followers — confirms the
connection:

> "The branch-Device, thou see'st him bear in hand,
>   is the green Thyrsus Bacchus wont to wield,
>   which to our cent'ury doth belief command
>   he was a comrade or belovèd child."
> — [008-012]

## Significance

The Lusus foundation myth serves multiple purposes in Camões's epic. It provides the
Portuguese with a classical pedigree comparable to Rome's foundation by Aeneas. It also
establishes a providential view of Portuguese history: the nation was destined from its
mythical origins to carry Bacchic energy — the spirit of conquest, expansion, and
civilisation — across the world. This foundation story sets the stage for every subsequent
triumph narrated in the poem.
