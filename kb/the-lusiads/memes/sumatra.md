# Sumatra

## Overview

Sumatra is the westernmost of the Greater Sunda Islands of Indonesia, a vast and mountainous island that forms the southern shore of the Strait of [[malacca|Malacca]] — one of the world's most strategically important maritime passages. In The Lusiads, Sumatra appears among the islands of the Indonesian archipelago reached by Portuguese navigation, a geographical marker in the poem's catalog of eastern discoveries.

## Geographical Context

Sumatra is the sixth-largest island in the world, stretching over 1,700 kilometres from northwest to southeast. Its position — separated from the Malay Peninsula by the narrow Strait of [[malacca|Malacca]], and from [[java|Java]] by the Sunda Strait — made it an unavoidable landmark for any vessel sailing between India and the spice-producing islands of eastern Indonesia.

At the time of Portuguese arrival, Sumatra was divided among numerous sultanates and kingdoms, the most powerful of which were Aceh (Achem) at the northern tip and Palembang in the southeast. The island's wealth derived from pepper, gold, and its position astride the main shipping routes of Southeast Asian commerce. Aceh, in particular, was a major centre of Islamic learning and a formidable military power that would resist Portuguese expansion for over a century.

The Portuguese established trading contacts with several Sumatran polities but never achieved the kind of territorial control they exercised at [[malacca|Malacca]] (on the opposite shore of the strait). Sumatra's size and political fragmentation made it a different proposition from the city-state of Malacca; Portuguese influence was commercial rather than territorial.

## In The Lusiads

Sumatra appears once in the poem's endnotes, in a geographical gloss to Canto XII:

> We now return westward to Sumatra.
> — [012-262]

The note is part of the long geographical catalog that accompanies the poem — an encyclopedic apparatus that anchors Camões' poetic geography in navigational reality. The phrase "we now return westward" reflects the mental map of Portuguese navigation: Sumatra sits at the western entrance to the Indonesian archipelago, the first major island encountered after leaving India and crossing the Bay of Bengal.

Like [[java|Java]] and the [[spice-islands|Moluccas]], Sumatra appears in the endnotes rather than the poetic text — a product of the poem's temporal frame. Gama's voyage reached India but not the Indonesian archipelago; the places beyond India belong to the later history of Portuguese expansion that Camões chronicles in his endnotes.

## Sumatra and Portuguese Strategy

Sumatra's strategic importance derived from its position controlling the western approaches to the Strait of [[malacca|Malacca]]. The Portuguese capture of Malacca in 1511 made the strait a Portuguese waterway, and the Sumatran shore became the southern boundary of Portuguese naval power. Portuguese relations with the Sumatran sultanates — particularly Aceh — were often hostile, and naval engagements in the strait were frequent.

The island was also a significant source of pepper, second only to the Malabar Coast of India in Portuguese commercial priorities. Sumatran pepper supplemented the spice cargoes from the [[spice-islands|Moluccas]], and the island's ports were regular stops on Portuguese trading voyages.
