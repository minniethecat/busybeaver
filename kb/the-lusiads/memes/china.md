# China

## Overview

China appears in The Lusiads as the limit of the imaginable — the furthest destination named in the poem's geography of ambition. It is invoked in Canto II as the outer boundary of Portuguese navigation ("far as far China"), in Canto VII as a source of exotic trade goods, and in Canto X as a vast empire glimpsed in the prophetic vision. Camões never visited China, and his knowledge of it was mediated by Portuguese travellers' reports, but China occupies an important symbolic role in the poem: it is what lies beyond India, the proof that Portuguese reach exceeds even the limits of the known world.

## Geographical Context

In Camões's time, China was ruled by the Ming dynasty (1368–1644). Portuguese contact began in 1513, when Jorge Álvares reached the Pearl River Delta. By 1557, the Portuguese had established a permanent settlement at Macau, which would become the centre of the China trade for over four centuries. Chinese silk, porcelain, and lacquerware were objects of intense [[europe|Europe]]an desire. The Portuguese were the first [[europe|Europe]]ans to establish a sustained maritime presence on the China coast.

## In The Lusiads

### The Furthest Horizon (Canto II)

The scope of Portuguese destiny is defined by its extremes:

> …Chersonésus'-shore far as far China they shall navigate… [002-062]

"Far as far China" — the repetition of "far" creates a sense of immense distance, almost beyond calculation.

### Exotic Trade (Canto VII)

China is a byword for luxury:

> …in ev'ery style of goods from China sent by sea to Nyle. [007-049]

### The Cosmic Vision (Canto X)

In the Machine of the World, China appears as a vast, shadowed empire:

> …of China runs, and boasts the whole he wealth and matchless might… [010-145]

And:

> This Realm half-shadowed, China's empery afar reflecting… [010-147]

## Historical Significance

The Portuguese relationship with China was unique among [[europe|Europe]]an powers. Unlike in India or [[africa|Africa]], the Portuguese never attempted military conquest of Chinese territory — Macau was leased, not conquered. The China trade, particularly in silk and later tea, was enormously profitable and helped sustain the Portuguese empire after the loss of [[hormuz|Hormuz]] and other Indian Ocean possessions. The phrase "far as far China" captures the Portuguese accomplishment: a nation of barely a million people had projected its power to the furthest shore of the known world.

## Notes

"Half-shadowed" [010-147] is a suggestive epithet. It may refer to China's vastness — half the realm is always in shadow — or to the incomplete state of Portuguese knowledge about it. Either way, China remains for Camões a place at the edge of vision, glimpsed but not fully known, the ultimate horizon of an empire still in the process of becoming.
