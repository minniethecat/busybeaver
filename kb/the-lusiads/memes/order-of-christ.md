# Order of Christ

## Overview

The **Order of Christ** (Ordem de Cristo) was a Portuguese military-religious order founded in 1319 as the successor to the Knights Templar in [[portugal|Portugal]]. Following the suppression of the Templars by Pope Clement V in 1312, King Dinis of [[portugal|Portugal]] negotiated the transfer of Templar properties and personnel in his kingdom to a new order, which received papal approval in 1319. Headquartered initially at Castro Marim and later at Tomar, the Order of Christ inherited both the Templars' assets and their crusading mission — a mission that would be redirected from the Holy Land to the coasts of [[africa|Africa]], India, and beyond.

The Order's most famous member in connection with The Lusiads is [[vasco-da-gama|Vasco da Gama]], who was a knight of the Order. The distinctive Cross of the Order of Christ — a red cross pattée with a white inner cross — was painted on the sails of Portuguese exploration vessels and became the visual emblem of [[portugal|Portugal]]'s maritime empire.

## In The Lusiads

While the Order of Christ is not named directly in the poem, its presence is everywhere implicit. The "[[christian-expansion|Holy Faith]]" that the Portuguese carry overseas is the faith of the crusading orders, and the ships that depart from Restelo sail under the Order's cross.

The poem's opening invocation establishes the religious character of the Portuguese enterprise:

> who hied them Holy Faith and Reign to spread (001-010)

This coupling of "[[christian-expansion|Holy Faith]]" with "Reign" (empire) precisely captures the dual mandate of the Order of Christ — at once spiritual and temporal. The crusading ideology that Camões invokes throughout the poem is the ideology institutionalized and perpetuated by the Order.

The departure of [[vasco-da-gama|Gama]]'s fleet from the shrine at Restelo (Belém) — a site closely associated with the Order, whose grand master [[prince-henry-the-navigator|Prince Henry the Navigator]] had built a chapel there — is rendered with religious gravity:

> Thus we departed from the saintly Shrine (004-095)

The vision of Christ on the Cross that appears to Afonso Henriques before the Battle of Ourique (Canto III) represents the foundational miracle of Portuguese kingship, and the Cross that appears in that vision would become the symbol of the Order:

> Afonso sees the Christ on Cross elate (003-004)

## Historical Background

The Order of Christ was shaped by the peculiar circumstances of Portuguese history. After completing the Reconquista early (1249), [[portugal|Portugal]] lacked a land frontier against Islam, yet its crusading energy — and the institutional apparatus of the military orders — remained intact. Under [[prince-henry-the-navigator|Prince Henry the Navigator]] (Infante D. Henrique), who became the Order's Governor and Administrator in 1420, the Order of Christ became the institutional vehicle for Portuguese overseas expansion.

Henry directed the Order's revenues toward maritime exploration, establishing a center for navigation and cartography at Sagres. The caravels that probed the West [[africa|Africa]]n coast in the fifteenth century sailed under the Order's flag. By the time of [[vasco-da-gama|Gama]]'s voyage, the Order's cross was synonymous with Portuguese maritime ambition.

The Order's influence extended well beyond the period of The Lusiads. It survived the Spanish annexation (1580–1640), the Enlightenment, and the Liberal Wars, eventually being transformed into a civil honorific order in the nineteenth century. Today, the Order of Christ survives as a Portuguese order of merit, and its cross remains a central element of Portuguese national iconography.

## Significance

The Order of Christ bridges two epochs: the medieval crusading world and the early modern Age of [[maritime-exploration|Discovery]]. In institutional terms, it provided the administrative, financial, and ideological framework that made Portuguese expansion possible. The Order's properties generated revenue for expeditions; its network of commanderies supplied personnel; its crusading charter supplied legitimacy.

In the world of The Lusiads, the Order represents the institutional continuity underlying the [[crusade-ideal|Crusade Ideal]]. When Camões invokes crusading imagery — when he describes the Portuguese as soldiers of Christ, their ships as instruments of holy war — he is drawing on traditions preserved and perpetuated by the Order of Christ. The red cross on the Portuguese sail is not decoration but declaration.
