## Overview

Hector is the greatest Trojan warrior and prince in Homer's *Iliad*, the son of King Priam and defender of Troy against the Greek invasion. In *The Lusiads*, Camões invokes Hector as the supreme classical model of martial valor — the standard against which Portuguese feats of arms are measured.

## In The Lusiads

Hector appears in Canto X, where the poet-narrator praises the Portuguese commander Hector da Silveira by directly comparing him to his Homeric namesake. The comparison is doubled: Silveira is "our Hector Portingall" whose fury against the Gujarati foe matches what "Trojan Hector wreakèd on the Greek" [010-076]. This rhetorical move claims parity between Portuguese and classical heroism — the Portuguese Hector equals or surpasses the Trojan original.

## The Hector da Silveira Comparison

Hector da Silveira was a 16th-century Portuguese naval commander who fought in the Indian Ocean campaigns. At the Battle of Diu and the engagement at Chaul, Silveira's forces routed the Gujarati fleet. Camões frames this as a fulfillment of the Achillean-Hectorian heroic pattern: where Homer's Hector fought the Greeks before Troy, Portugal's Hector fights the "Moors" before the walls of Indian fortresses.

## Key Passages

> Nor less of Diu the fierce and fere Armade,
> the dread of Cháúl, daring, proudly man'd,
> with single glance shall fall, till all have fled
> our Hector da Sylveira's heavy hand:
> Our Hector Portingall, of whom 'tis said,
> that o'er yon ever armèd Cambayan strand,
> such wrath on Guzerats 'tis his to wreak
> as Trojan Hector wreakèd on the Greek. [010-076]

## Notes

The Hector da Silveira passage in Canto X forms part of the prophetic vision shown to Gama by the goddess Thetis, in which future Portuguese conquests across Asia are revealed. The doubling of the Hector name — "our Hector da Sylveira" as "our Hector Portingall" — is a deliberate rhetorical strategy of Camões to naturalize classical heroism within Portuguese identity.
