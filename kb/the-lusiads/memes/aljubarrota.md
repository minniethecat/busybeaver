# Battle of Aljubarrota

## Overview

The Battle of Aljubarrota (14 August 1385) was the decisive engagement of the 1383–1385 Portuguese succession crisis. Portuguese forces under the command of [[nuno-alvares-pereira|Nuno Álvares Pereira]], the "Holy Constable," and King [[king-john-i|John I]] of the newly proclaimed House of Aviz, defeated a numerically superior Castilian army that had invaded to claim the Portuguese throne for King John I of [[castile|Castile]].

In The Lusiads, Camões presents Aljubarrota as the foundational battle of Portuguese national identity — the moment when Portugal, outnumbered and underestimated, proved its divine election by defeating the "superb Castilian." The battle receives extended treatment in Canto IV as part of Vasco da Gama's historical narration to the King of [[melinde|Melinde]].

## In The Lusiads

### The Succession Crisis (Canto IV)

The battle narrative in Canto IV is preceded by the political context. After the death of King Fernando of Portugal without a male heir, his widow [[leonor-teles|Leonor Teles]] sought the aid of Castile, threatening Portuguese independence. Camões frames this as a moment of supreme national peril:

> Léonor craveth aidance of Castile

The poet presents the crisis in moral terms — the "weak" king Fernando is contrasted with the providential rise of John, Master of Aviz, the "second Saviour" who will rescue Portugal.

### Nuno Álvares Pereira (Canto IV)

The hero of the Aljubarrota account is Nuno Álvares Pereira, the Constable of Portugal. Camões introduces him with martial epithets:

> Withstandeth Nuno, warrior sans fear

And later:

> "I say Don Nuno, who appearèd here"

The Constable is portrayed as the instrument of divine will — a figure whose military genius and personal sanctity (he would later become a Carmelite friar) demonstrate that God fights on Portugal's side.

Nuno's speech before the battle is a set-piece of heroic oratory, rallying the outnumbered Portuguese troops with appeals to faith, nation, and honor. Camões explicitly compares him to classical heroes — a Portuguese Hector or Aeneas defending a new Troy.

### The Battle Narrative

The battle itself is described in the elevated style of epic warfare, with the Castilian host presented as a vast but disordered force, and the Portuguese as a smaller but divinely favored army. Key elements include:

- The Portuguese defensive position, using the terrain (a tactic historically employed by Nuno)
- The Castilian cavalry charges, repulsed by Portuguese infantry
- The moment of Castilian collapse and rout
- The pursuit and slaughter — presented not as cruelty but as divine justice

Camões emphasizes the numerical disparity to magnify the Portuguese achievement:

> This proud Castile's bravades with equal pride
> and marchèd boldly to debel Castile

### Aftermath and Significance

The victory at Aljubarrota secured the Aviz dynasty, which would rule Portugal for the next two centuries — the dynasty of Henry the Navigator, John II, and Manuel I, under whom the voyages of discovery would be launched. Camões makes this connection explicit: the independence won at Aljubarrota was the necessary precondition for Portugal's maritime destiny.

In Canto I, Camões had already invoked Nuno among the heroes he promises to celebrate:

> For these I give thee a Nuno, fierce in fight

The promise is fulfilled in Canto IV, where Aljubarrota becomes the centerpiece of Portugal's medieval history — the battle that proved the nation was worth the epic Camões now writes.

## Historical Context

The 1383–1385 crisis began with the death of King Fernando I, whose only daughter, Beatriz, was married to King John I of Castile. The prospect of Castilian annexation provoked a nationalist reaction in Portugal, led by the merchant classes of Lisbon and the lower nobility. John, the illegitimate half-brother of Fernando and Master of the military Order of Aviz, emerged as the leader of the resistance.

The actual Battle of Aljubarrota took place near the town of Aljubarrota in central Portugal. Nuno Álvares Pereira, commanding perhaps 6,500 men, faced a Castilian army of approximately 31,000. Using a defensive formation protected by natural obstacles and artificial fieldworks (a tactic adapted from English longbow tactics learned during the Hundred Years' War), the Portuguese repelled the Castilian cavalry charges and counter-attacked, routing the invading army.

The victory was complete. Castilian casualties were enormous; Portuguese casualties were light. John I was confirmed as king, founding the House of Aviz. The Batalha Monastery was built to commemorate the victory and stands as a masterpiece of Portuguese Gothic architecture and a UNESCO World Heritage site.

## Notes

The Battle of Aljubarrota is commemorated in Portuguese national memory as the supreme example of the underdog victory. The famous Portuguese expression "aconteceu em Aljubarrota" ("it happened at Aljubarrota") is used to dismiss improbable claims — because at Aljubarrota, the improbable actually happened. Camões' treatment of the battle in The Lusiads helped cement this place in national mythology.

The battle's modern legacy includes the annual commemoration on 14 August and the continued veneration of Nuno Álvares Pereira, who was canonized as a saint of the Catholic Church in 2009.
