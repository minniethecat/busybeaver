# Hormuz

## Overview

Hormuz (also Ormuz) was the most strategically important Portuguese possession in the [[persia|Persia]]n Gulf — a barren island that commanded the maritime trade routes between India, [[persia|Persia]], and the Ottoman Empire. In The Lusiads, it appears both in early prophecy (Canto II) and in the culminating vision of Canto X, where its conquest by [[afonso-de-albuquerque|Afonso de Albuquerque]] is foretold as one of the defining acts of Portuguese empire. Camões treats Hormuz not as a place of beauty or civilisation but as a pivot of power — the key to the Gulf, and through the Gulf, to the wealth of [[asia|Asia]].

## Geographical Context

Hormuz is a small, arid island at the mouth of the [[persia|Persia]]n Gulf, about 8 km from the Iranian coast. Before the Portuguese arrival, the Kingdom of Hormuz controlled a maritime trading network stretching from the Gulf to East [[africa|Africa]]. The island itself was famously described as having no fresh water and no agriculture — it existed solely as an entrepôt, a city built on trade. [[afonso-de-albuquerque|Albuquerque]] captured it in 1507 and again in 1515, after which the Portuguese built a fortress that held the island until 1622.

## In The Lusiads

### Early Prophecy (Canto II)

The fate of Hormuz is proclaimed early in the poem:

> …shalt thou see the haughty Hormuz-reign twice taken, prostrate at thy feet… [002-057]

"Haughty" is a pointed epithet — Hormuz's wealth made it proud, and its pride made its conquest all the more significant.

### Albuquerque's Conquest (Canto X)

The prophetic vision in Canto X returns to Hormuz with greater detail:

> …Albuquerque's hand shall tame the Hormuz Parsi's heart… [010-048]

The "Parsi" ([[persia|Persia]]n) identification is significant — Camões frames the conquest as Portuguese triumph over [[persia|Persia]]n power.

### Chastisement and Tribute (Canto X)

> …he shall chastise high Hormuz' erring horde and twofold tribute by the right of sword exact… [010-069]

### The Strown Kingdom (Canto X)

> …yon Hormuz Kingdom strown along the strait… [010-117]

## Historical Significance

[[afonso-de-albuquerque|Afonso de Albuquerque]]'s capture of Hormuz (1507, reconquered 1515) was one of the three pillars of his Indian Ocean strategy, alongside [[goa|Goa]] (1510) and Malacca (1511). Hormuz controlled access to the [[persia|Persia]]n Gulf and the overland silk routes to the Mediterranean. The fortress the Portuguese built there was considered impregnable and held for over a century. Its loss in 1622 to a combined [[persia|Persia]]n-English force marked the beginning of the end of Portuguese dominance in the Gulf.

## Notes

The "twice taken" [002-057] refers to the two expeditions — 1507 (brief occupation) and 1515 (permanent fortress) — that secured Hormuz for [[portugal|Portugal]]. Camões's epithet "haughty" for Hormuz captures something of the Portuguese attitude toward the trading kingdoms they conquered: respect for their wealth, contempt for their pride, and confidence in their own divine mandate to rule.
