# Lusitania

## Overview

Lusitania is the ancient Roman name for the western Iberian province that became Portugal, and it serves throughout The Lusiads as the poem's primary designation for its homeland. Far more than a geographical label, "Lusitania" carries the full weight of Camões's patriotic programme — it invokes classical heritage, claims imperial destiny, and transforms a small Atlantic kingdom into the heir of Rome's glory.

## In The Lusiads

### The Founders of the Lusian Race (Canto VIII)

The origin of the name is explained in Paul da Gama's narrative to the Samorim:

> "This first in sight is Lusus, from whose fame / our 'Lusitania' gained her royal name" (008-003)

The legendary founder Lusus (or Lysas), companion of Bacchus, gives the land its classical pedigree — Portugal is not merely a nation but the inheritor of an ancient and heroic lineage.

### The Crown of Europe (Canto III)

In the geographical survey of Europe, Lusitania is described as the continent's crowning glory:

> "See the head-crowning coronet is she, / of general Europe, Lusitania's reign, / where endeth land and where beginneth sea" (003-028)

Portugal's position at Europe's western edge — where land meets ocean — is presented as both geographical fact and providential destiny.

### What Kings and Wars (Canto III)

The poem's historical survey opens with the declaration:

> "What Kings and wars our Lusitania knew" (003-003)

setting the epic's scope as the history of the Portuguese nation from its origins to the present day.

### The Bellic Art (Canto III)

Portugal's martial spirit is praised:

> "our Lusitania, such his bellic art" (003-038)

reinforcing the military virtue that underpins the nation's imperial expansion.

### Castile's Assault (Canto IV)

The dynastic conflict with neighbouring Castile is framed as an assault on Lusitania:

> "Castilia's force on Lusitania fall" (004-008)

marking the existential threat that Portuguese independence faced and overcame.

## Historical Context

The Roman province of Lusitania was established in 27 BCE, encompassing most of modern Portugal and parts of western Spain. The name was revived in the Renaissance as part of the humanist programme of claiming classical antecedents for modern nations. For Camões, "Lusitania" was not an antiquarian curiosity but a living identity — one that connected Portugal to the Roman Empire and asserted its place among the great powers of history. The "Lusian" (Lusíada) of the poem's title is thus both a classical and a national identity.

## Notes

Camões's use of "Lusitania" rather than "Portugal" is a deliberate literary choice. It elevates the vernacular epic to classical stature, connects the Portuguese nation to Roman antiquity, and provides a flexible metrical term for verse. The adjective "Lusitanian" appears even more frequently than the noun, qualifying kings, people, power, and empire throughout the poem.
