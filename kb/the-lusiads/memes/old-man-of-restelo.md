# The Old Man of Restelo's Speech

## Overview

The Old Man of Restelo (*O Velho do Restelo*) is one of the most celebrated and debated figures in *The Lusiads*.
As Vasco da Gama's fleet prepares to depart from the Belém shore in Canto IV, an aged, venerable man
steps forward from the crowd and delivers a solemn and impassioned condemnation of the voyage.
His speech stands as the epic's single most powerful voice of dissent — a counterpoint to the celebration
of heroic exploration that dominates the poem.

## In The Lusiads

The episode unfolds at the port of Ulysséa (Lisbon), where the fleet is being manned and the soldiers
and sailors gather for departure. The scene is one of great public emotion:

> "Now in famed Ulysséa's haven man'd,
> Fast by the foreshore comes the soldiery"
> — [004-092–093]

The city-people gather on what is described as "that saddest day," as families bid farewell
to those embarking on a perilous voyage into unknown seas. Wives lament, mothers weep, and
the company of sailors dare not raise their eyes from the ground.

Then the Old Man appears — "an agèd Sire of reverend mien" — and delivers his famous rebuke.
Addressing the "craving of Command" and "vain Desire," he condemns the expedition as a reckless
pursuit of glory:

> "Thou dour disturber of man's sprite and life,
> What new disaster dost thou here design?"
> — [004-104–105]

## Key Passages

The Old Man's speech builds through several rhetorical movements. He first condemns the lust
for fame that drives men to abandon their homeland and families:

> "Since by this gustful Van'ity led astray,
> Dost leave the foeman breeding at thy gate"
> — [004-107–109]

He then reaches back to classical mythology, invoking Prometheus and Phaethon as archetypes
of the disastrous consequences of human ambition:

> "Oh, curst the Mortal, who the first was found
> …Iápetus' daring Son from Heaven brought
> …Ne'er had the Stripling, miserably brave…"
> — [004-110–112]

The speech culminates in a bitter reflection on the human cost, condemning the "wretched
mothers" who lose their sons to the "shore far-off and frore" of distant lands:

> "What curse on new-found phases still must lie,
> What promised glory, and what promised gain,—
> Vainglory this we call, that Fancy draws"
> — [004-103–106]

## Significance

The Old Man of Restelo has been interpreted by scholars as representing multiple voices:
the conservative wisdom of age, the authentic grief of those left behind, and even Camões's
own ambivalence about the cost of empire. The speech provides the epic with moral complexity,
preventing it from becoming a simple celebration of conquest.

The episode is structurally significant because it is embedded in Vasco da Gama's own
retrospective narrative to the King of Melinde — meaning the hero himself recounts the
words of his most devastating critic.

## Notes

The figure of the Old Man has no clear historical counterpart and appears to be Camões's
own invention. Scholars have long debated whether the poet used this character to voice
his personal misgivings about the Portuguese imperial project — Camões himself had served
in the East and witnessed the human cost of the enterprise firsthand. The speech's
rhetorical structure draws on the classical tradition of the *vetus poeta* (venerable
poet-elder) who speaks truth to power, a device traceable to Homer and Virgil.
