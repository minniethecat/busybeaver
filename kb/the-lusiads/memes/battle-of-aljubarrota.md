# Battle of Aljubarrota

## Overview

The Battle of Aljubarrota, fought on 14 August 1385 near the village of Aljubarrota in central [[portugal|Portugal]], was the pivotal engagement of the 1383–1385 Portuguese interregnum.

Portuguese forces commanded by the Constable of the Kingdom, [[nuno-alvares-pereira|Nuno Alvares Pereira]], and King Joao I decisively defeated a numerically superior Castilian army under King Juan I of [[spain|Castile]].

The victory secured [[portugal|Portugal]]'s independence from Castilian domination, established the House of Avis on the Portuguese throne, and inaugurated an enduring alliance with England.

## In The Lusiads

Camoes devotes a substantial passage of Canto IV (stanzas 14–53) to the Battle of Aljubarrota, structured as a set-piece battle narrative within [[vasco-da-gama|Vasco da Gama]]'s historical discourse to the King of [[melinde|Melinde]].

The Argument to Canto IV frames it as "Military feats of the Constable, D. Nuno Alvares Pereira: Battle and victory of Aljubarrota."

Camoes presents the battle as the providential vindication of Portuguese nationhood, with Nuno depicted as the divinely appointed instrument of national destiny.

The narrative traces the full arc from the succession crisis after King Fernando's death, through the popular acclamation of Joao I, Nuno's rallying of the troops, the ferocity of the battle, and the ultimate rout of the Castilian host.

## Historical Basis

On 14 August 1385, about 6,600 Portuguese (including English longbowmen) faced a Castilian army of roughly 31,000.

Nuno deployed his forces on a defensive rise, using dismounted men-at-arms and archers behind field fortifications. His decisive counter-charge shattered the Castilian line.

King Juan I fled the field. The victory ended [[spain|Castile]]'s claim to the Portuguese throne and led to the Anglo-Portuguese Treaty of Windsor (1386).

Camoes follows the historical outline faithfully but heightens the providential and epic dimensions, presenting Nuno as a lion at bay whose courage transforms the impossible into the inevitable.

## Key Passages

The battle narrative opens with a dawn simile likening Joao I's accession to daylight after a storm:

> "After the horrors of the stormy Night,
> with gloom, and lightning-gleams, and hiss of wind,
> breaks lovely Morning's pure and blessed light,
> with hope of haven and sure rest to find:
> Sol banisheth the dark obscure from sight,
> laying the terror of man's timid mind:
> Thus to the doughty kingdom it befell,
> when King Fernando bade this world farewell."
>
> — [004-001]

The divine legitimation of the new king is sealed by a miraculous sign at Evora:

> "That such accession came from Heaven divine
> proved 'special marvels, God His truth proclaiming,
> when Ev'ora city saw the choicest sign,
> a babe of age unspeech'd the ruler naming;
> and, but to show the Heav'en's supreme design,
> she raised her cradled limbs and voice, exclaiming —
> 'Portugal! Port'ugal!' high uplifting hand,
> 'for the new king, Dom John, who rules the land.'"
>
> — [004-003]

The battle's climax depicts the Castilian host breaking:

> "Here wildest Battle hath its cruel'est will,
> with deaths and shouts, and slash and gory shower;
> the multitud'inous Braves, who're killed and kill,
> rob of their proper hues the bloom and flower:
> At length they fly! they die! now waxeth still
> War's note, while lance and spear have lost their power:
> Castilia's King the fate of pride must own,
> seeing his purpose changed, his host o'erthrown."
>
> — [004-042]

The Castilian king flees the field in panic:

> "The field he leaveth to the Conqueror,
> too glad his life had not been left in fight:
> Follow him all who can; and panick sore
> lends them not feet, but feather'd wings for flight:
> Their breasts are filled with a wild dolour,
> for Deaths, for Treasure waste in wanton plight;
> for woe, disgust, and foul dishonour's soil
> to see the Victor rev'elling in their spoil."
>
> — [004-043]

## Significance

The Battle of Aljubarrota holds dual significance in The Lusiads. Historically, it secured [[portugal|Portugal]]'s existence as an independent kingdom, enabling the overseas expansion the poem celebrates.

The House of Avis founded by Joao I would sponsor the Age of [[maritime-exploration|Discovery]]. Without Aljubarrota, there is no [[vasco-da-gama|Vasco da Gama]].

Poetically, the battle is the climax of the Portuguese historical narrative in Canto IV. Camoes casts national identity as forged in collective sacrifice and divine favour.

The episode also establishes Nuno as the exemplar of Portuguese martial virtue — the "[[nuno-alvares-pereira|Holy Constable]]" whose piety and prowess prefigure the ideal Christian knight.
