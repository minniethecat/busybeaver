# The Aeneid

## Overview

The *Aeneid* is Virgil's Latin epic poem composed between 29 and 19 BC, narrating the journey of the Trojan hero Aeneas from the fallen city of Troy to Italy, where he becomes the legendary ancestor of the Roman people. Written during the reign of Augustus, the poem served to legitimize Roman imperial power by tracing its origins to divine destiny and heroic foundation. Comprising twelve books in dactylic hexameter, the first six follow the model of the *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]* (wanderings by sea), while the latter six follow the *Iliad* (warfare and conquest in Latium). The poem established itself as the definitive epic of the Latin language and the primary classical model for all subsequent [[europe|Europe]]an national epics, including Camões' *The Lusiads*.

Virgil's poem was commissioned, at least implicitly, as a work of national propaganda: it connected Augustus to Aeneas, and Aeneas to [[venus|Venus]], creating a divine genealogy for the Julio-Claudian dynasty. The poem's central themes — *pietas* (duty to gods, family, and state), the cost of empire, and the tension between personal desire and historical destiny — provided a template that Camões would adapt for the Portuguese maritime empire sixteen centuries later.

## Relationship to The Lusiads

The *Aeneid* is the single most important structural and thematic model for *The Lusiads*. Camões explicitly frames his poem as both heir and rival to Virgil's achievement. Where Virgil's Aeneas, guided by fate and the gods, founds the city that will become [[rome|Rome]], Camões' [[vasco-da-gama|Vasco da Gama]], guided by [[venus|Venus]] and divine providence, opens the sea route that will establish [[portugal|Portugal]]'s global empire. The title itself — *Os Lusíadas* — follows the Virgilian pattern of naming an epic after its people (the Lusiads, or sons of [[lusus|Lusus]]), just as the *Aeneid* is named after Aeneas.

The narrator of *The Lusiads* makes the competition explicit in Canto V, where da [[vasco-da-gama|Gama]] himself delivers a speech arguing that his own travails surpass those of Aeneas:

> Dost deem Aeneas, or e'en eloquent Ulysses, fared so far this Earth to tread? (005-094)

Camões follows Virgil in combining the voyage narrative (Cantos I–VI, the journey to India, corresponding to *Aeneid* I–VI) with a historical pageant and prophecy (Cantos III–IV and X, corresponding to *Aeneid* VI's vision of [[rome|Rome]]'s future). The device of having a god ([[jupiter|Jupiter]]) reveal the future destiny of a nation is directly borrowed from Virgil's use of Anchises showing Aeneas the future heroes of [[rome|Rome]].

The poem also follows Virgil's strategy of synthesizing Homeric elements: Virgil had combined the *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]*'s wanderings and the *Iliad*'s warfare into a single continuous epic; Camões integrates both models while adding the new element of documented historical navigation.

## Key Allusions

**The [[the-council-of-gods|Council of the Gods]] (Canto I)** follows the Virgilian pattern of divine assemblies that decide human fates. In *The Lusiads*, [[jupiter|Jupiter]] convenes the gods on Olympus to determine the fate of the Portuguese fleet, just as [[jupiter|Jupiter]] in *Aeneid* X convenes the gods to discuss the war in Latium. Camões' innovation is to split the divine sympathies: [[venus|Venus]] supports the Portuguese as she supported Aeneas, while [[bacchus|Bacchus]] opposes them — a dramatic structure unknown to Virgil but suggested by Juno's opposition to Aeneas.

**The Prophetic Vision (Canto X)** mirrors Aeneid VI. Where Anchises reveals to Aeneas the future heroes of [[rome|Rome]], the sea-nymph [[thetis|Thetis]] reveals to da [[vasco-da-gama|Gama]] the Ptolemaic cosmos and the future sites of Portuguese dominion. Both visions establish that the hero's journey serves a larger historical purpose foreordained by divine will.

**The Storm Episode (Canto VI)** recalls the storm that Juno sends against Aeneas in *Aeneid* I. In Camões' version, [[bacchus|Bacchus]] stirs [[neptune|Neptune]] to raise a storm against the Portuguese fleet, and [[venus|Venus]] intercedes to calm the waters — an inversion of Virgil's pattern where Juno raises the storm and [[neptune|Neptune]] calms it.

**The Lyre of Mantua (Canto V)** is a direct reference to Virgil himself, who was born near Mantua:

> Yes! but that Hero, whose esteem and love crownèd with praise, prize, honours, gifts abounding the Lyre of Mantua, taught her Bard to chaunt Aeneas' name, and Rome's high glories vaunt. (005-102)

The passage acknowledges Virgil's achievement while setting up Camões' argument that [[portugal|Portugal]] lacks a poet of comparable stature — a lack that *The Lusiads* itself is meant to remedy.

## Literary Significance

The *Aeneid* occupies a unique position in Western literature as the bridge between Greek epic and the [[europe|Europe]]an vernacular epic tradition. Written in Latin at the height of Augustan literary culture, it was the one classical epic continuously read and studied throughout the Middle Ages and Renaissance. For Camões, as for Dante before him, Virgil was not merely a literary model but the poet *par excellence* — the standard against which all epic ambition must be measured.

Camões' relationship to Virgil is competitive but not destructive. The narrator of *The Lusiads* argues that Portuguese deeds surpass those of antiquity, and therefore deserve a poet who can surpass Virgil:

> For this, and not for Nature's fault, be sure Virgil nor Homer rise to strike the lyre; nor shall rise ever, an this mode endure, pious Aeneas or Achilles dire. (005-106)

The passage is a lament: [[portugal|Portugal]]'s lack of appreciation for poetry means that even Virgil and Homer could not flourish there. Yet the very act of writing such a passage in an epic that consciously rivals the *Aeneid* is Camões' wager that he *can* be [[portugal|Portugal]]'s Virgil.
