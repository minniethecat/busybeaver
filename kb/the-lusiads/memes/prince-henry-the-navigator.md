# Prince Henry the Navigator

## Overview

Prince Henry the Navigator (Infante Dom Henrique, 1394–1460) was the third son of [[king-john-i|King John I of Portugal]] and the driving force behind the first phase of Portuguese maritime exploration. From his base at Sagres, he sponsored expeditions down the West [[africa|Africa]]n coast, developed navigational techniques, and established the institutional framework — the school of navigation, the caravel design, the systematic mapping of coastlines — that made the Age of [[maritime-exploration|Discovery]] possible. Camões presents Henry as the visionary founder whose work culminates in [[vasco-da-gama|Gama]]'s voyage.

## In The Lusiads

### The Conquest of Ceuta (Canto III)

Henry first appears in [[vasco-da-gama|Gama]]'s historical narration, as a prince who proved his valour in the conquest of [[ceuta|Ceuta]] (1415), [[portugal|Portugal]]'s first overseas possession:

> ...more at home this conqu'ering Henry stood who sacred Hierosol'yma saw... [003-035]

The mention of Jerusalem alludes to Henry's ambition to find a sea route to the Holy Land, a motivating force behind his explorations.

### The Spirit of Exploration (Canto V)

The giant [[adamastor|Adamastor]], in his prophecy, acknowledges Henry's role in initiating the Portuguese thrust into the Atlantic:

> ...which gen'erous Henry had the heart t' explore... [005-012]

This reference — from the mouth of [[portugal|Portugal]]'s mythic antagonist — underscores Henry's foundational importance: even the poem's monsters recognise his achievement.

### The Pilgrim King (Canto VIII)

A third reference presents Henry in a spiritual register:

> ...unto Sanct Sep'ulchre saintly Henry hied that might his kingly train attest... [008-017]

Henry's dual identity — warrior and pilgrim, conqueror and Christian — is central to Camões's portrayal and to the poem's broader argument that Portuguese expansion is simultaneously martial and sacred.

## Historical Context

The historical Prince Henry never sailed on the voyages he sponsored — the nickname "Navigator" is a later English invention. His achievements were organisational and intellectual:

1. **Sagres school**: Established a centre for navigational research, attracting cartographers, astronomers, and shipbuilders.
2. **Caravel development**: Sponsored the design of the caravel, a light, manoeuvrable ship capable of sailing against the wind — the technological key to Atlantic exploration.
3. **West [[africa|Africa]]n exploration**: His captains reached Cape Bojador (1434), Cape Verde (1444), and Sierra Leone (1460), mapping the [[africa|Africa]]n coast and establishing the slave trade.
4. **[[order-of-christ|Order of Christ]]**: As Governor of the [[order-of-christ|Order of Christ]], Henry channelled the military order's resources into exploration, fusing crusading zeal with commercial ambition.

## Significance

Prince Henry stands at the beginning of the Portuguese epic. Without his institutional and technological foundations, [[vasco-da-gama|Gama]]'s voyage would have been impossible. Camões gives him only brief mentions — the poem's focus is [[vasco-da-gama|Gama]], not Henry — but each mention positions Henry as the visionary origin-point of [[portugal|Portugal]]'s maritime destiny. He is the spark; [[vasco-da-gama|Gama]] is the flame.

## Notes

Camões's Henry is more pilgrim and crusader than the secular "Renaissance prince" of modern historiography. This reflects Camões's ideological project: to present Portuguese expansion as a Christian mission, not merely a commercial enterprise. The historical Henry's involvement in the [[africa|Africa]]n slave trade — he personally profited from it — goes unmentioned, consistent with the poem's heroic register.
