## Overview

Scipio Africanus (Publius Cornelius Scipio, 236–183 BC) was the Roman general who defeated Hannibal at the Battle of Zama (202 BC), ending the Second Punic War and establishing Rome as the dominant power of the Mediterranean. In *The Lusiads*, Camões uses Scipio's name as a classical honorific — Nuno Álvares Pereira, the hero of Portuguese independence, is called "Portugal's Scipio."

## In The Lusiads

Scipio appears four times in *The Lusiads*, always as a benchmark of military excellence. In Canto V, the poet ranks Scipio alongside Caesar as figures whose fame Portugal's heroes surpass [005-103]. In Canto VIII, the constable Nuno Álvares Pereira is explicitly named "Portugale's Scipio" — the highest classical compliment Camões could bestow on a Portuguese commander [008-040]. The name recurs in Canto XI, where Scipio's memory is invoked as foundational to the Lusitanian martial tradition [011-052].

## The Nuno Álvares Connection

The epithet "Portugal's Scipio" for Nuno Álvares Pereira (1360–1431) is carefully chosen. Like Scipio, Álvares Pereira was a young commander who defeated a superior invading force — in his case, the Castilian army at the Battle of Aljubarrota (1385), which secured Portuguese independence. Both men combined military genius with deep religious devotion: Scipio was known for his piety toward the gods, while Álvares Pereira later became a Carmelite friar.

## Notes

Camões's use of Scipio reflects the Renaissance humanist practice of *imitatio* — measuring modern achievements against classical models. By calling Álvares Pereira "Portugal's Scipio," Camões not only praises the constable but also claims for Portuguese history a dignity equal to Rome's.
