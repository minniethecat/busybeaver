# Ovid

## Role & Significance

Ovid was the last — and in some ways the most audacious — of the great Augustan poets. His *Metamorphoses*, a fifteen-book mythological epic in hexameters, retells some 250 myths of transformation, from the creation of the world to the deification of Julius Caesar. It became the Renaissance's single most influential handbook of classical mythology.

But his significance for Camões goes beyond mythology. Ovid closes the *Metamorphoses* with an epilogue in which he claims — like Horace — that his work will outlast fire, sword, and the wrath of Jupiter (or Augustus) alike. This boast, the *vivam* passage (*Met.* XV.871–879), is the classical precedent for Camões' most personal assertion: that the poet, not the conqueror, confers true immortality.

## In The Lusiads

Ovid's presence in *The Lusiads* is dual: mythological and metapoetic.

**Mythological.** Ovid's *Metamorphoses* is the deep reservoir from which Renaissance poets drew their knowledge of classical myth. When Camões populates the Isle of Love with [[nymphs|Nereids]] and [[venus|Venus]], or describes transformations and divine epiphanies, he is working in the Ovidian tradition. The *Metamorphoses* taught Camões — as it taught Dante, Chaucer, and Shakespeare — how to move between the human and divine registers.

**Metapoetic.** More specifically, Ovid's epilogue is one of the texts Camões most clearly echoes. The *vivam* passage claims:

> I shall live on, and the better part of me
> shall escape the grave: through all the ages
> I shall grow in fame, ever-renewed.

Camões' equivalent is his insistence that his verse — not the Portuguese ships or forts — will be the enduring monument. The Ovidian boast becomes, in *The Lusiads*, a national boast: Portugal lives on in Camões' poetry.

## Key Themes

### The Poet's Survival

Ovid wrote the *Tristia* in exile at Tomis on the Black Sea, banished by Augustus for reasons still debated. His late poetry is saturated with the tension between imperial power (which could destroy his body and career) and poetic art (which he claimed could survive it). This tension — poet vs. emperor, art vs. power — resonates through Camões' epic, written as the Portuguese empire was already showing signs of decay.

### Myth and Transformation

The *Metamorphoses* is, as its name implies, a poem of change — gods become mortals, mortals become stars, human suffering crystallises into enduring myths. Camões' own handling of transformation — particularly in the Isle of Love episode (Canto IX), where erotic encounter becomes apotheosis — owes much to this Ovidian sensibility.
