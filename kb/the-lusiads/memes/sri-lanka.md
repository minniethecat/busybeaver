# Sri Lanka (Ceylon)

## Overview

Sri Lanka — called Ceylon and Taprobane in The Lusiads — is the large island south of the Indian subcontinent. It appears in the poem as a celebrated landmark of the Indian Ocean, noted for its natural wealth and for Adam's Peak, a mountain venerated in multiple religious traditions.

The island is referenced three times in the epic: once in Canto IX as a source of exotic goods, and twice in Canto X's prophetic survey of [[asia|Asia]]n geography. Camões uses the classical name "Taprobane" alongside the contemporary "Ceylon," reflecting the Renaissance blending of ancient and modern geographical knowledge.

## In The Lusiads

### Canto IX: The Wealth of Ceylon

In Canto IX, the island is invoked among the exotic riches of the East [009-022]:

> He taketh eke some Malabars aboard
> With samples of the wealth, the boast, the beauty of Ceylon.

The passage places Ceylon alongside other sources of Eastern luxury goods, emphasizing the island's reputation for precious commodities — cinnamon, gems, and pearls — that drew Portuguese traders across the Indian Ocean.

### Canto X: The Island Taprobanian

The prophetic vision of Canto X twice references the island. At [010-123], the goddess [[thetis|Thetis]] surveys the Indian coastline:

> See, runs the celebrate seaboard Hindostánian
> ...the Island Taprobanian ('tis now Ceylon) encrowns the fronting sea:

Camões here uses the classical name "Taprobanian" — derived from the Greek "Taprobane" — and glosses it with the contemporary Portuguese "Ceylon." This scholarly annotation reflects the poem's encyclopedic ambition: to map the known world in verse while demonstrating the poet's command of both classical geography and contemporary navigation.

At [010-152], Adam's Peak is singled out as a landmark:

> See in Ceylon that Peak so stark, so gaunt,

Adam's Peak (Sri Pada), a conical mountain sacred to Buddhists, Hindus, Muslims, and Christians, was a well-known navigational landmark for ships crossing the Indian Ocean. Its inclusion in the poem's survey demonstrates Camões's familiarity with the maritime geography of the Portuguese route to India.

## Historical Significance

For the Portuguese, Ceylon was a strategic island on the route to Malacca and the Spice Islands. Though the poem's narrative predates significant Portuguese involvement there — sustained contact began in 1505, after [[vasco-da-gama|Gama]]'s first voyage — Ceylon was well known to Portuguese navigators by the time Camões wrote. The island later became a contested prize between the Portuguese, Dutch, and local kingdoms, producing cinnamon and gems that enriched the Estado da Índia.

## Taprobane: From Ptolemy to Camões

The name Taprobane (Greek: Ταπροβάνη) appears in Ptolemy's Geography and other classical sources as a designation for a large island south of India. Renaissance cartographers debated whether Taprobane referred to Ceylon or Sumatra — a confusion that reflects the gradual refinement of [[europe|Europe]]an geographical knowledge through direct navigation. Camões resolves the ambiguity by explicitly glossing "the Island Taprobanian" as "'tis now Ceylon" [010-123], asserting the superiority of Portuguese navigational experience over classical textual authority.

This glossing gesture is characteristic of the poem's epistemological stance: classical learning provides the vocabulary and the framework, but Portuguese experience provides the verification and the correction. The ancients named Taprobane; the Portuguese have sailed there and know it as Ceylon. The poem thus enacts in miniature the larger argument of The Lusiads — that Portuguese voyages have superseded ancient knowledge, transforming geography from textual tradition into lived experience.

## Notes

The double naming in Canto X — Taprobane/Ceylon — is characteristic of Camões's geographical method. Throughout the epic, classical and contemporary place names are paired, asserting both the poet's humanist learning and the modernity of Portuguese exploration. The gesture says: the ancients knew this place, but we have sailed there.
