# Fame and Glory

## Definition

[[fame|Fame]] and glory (Portuguese: *fama e glória*) represent the Renaissance humanist conviction that heroic achievement, immortalized in poetry, grants its subject a form of eternal life. In the classical tradition inherited by Camões, the epic poet's role was not merely to entertain but to confer undying renown upon the heroes he celebrated. This pact between hero and poet — the hero provides the deeds, the poet provides the memorial — is explicitly thematized throughout The Lusiads.

## In The Lusiads

The opening stanzas of Canto I establish the dual ambition of the poem: to celebrate the Portuguese voyages and to secure eternal fame for their participants:

> The feats of Arms, and famed heroick Host, from occidental Lusitanian strand (001-009)

The word "famed" here is deliberate — Camões announces from the outset that his subject matter is already renowned, and his poem will amplify that renown to rival or surpass the classical epics.

The fragility of fame is a recurring anxiety. In Canto II, the narrator offers the poem itself as the guarantee of eternal memory:

> [grant us] e'erlasting name, eternal glory (002-060)

This transactional understanding of poetic fame becomes most explicit in Canto V, where the poet claims that even the greatest deeds are lost without a worthy chronicler:

> What Homer wrote that Alexander read, we yet remember (005-104)

## Historical/Cultural Context

The Renaissance revived the classical concept of *fama* as a secular form of immortality. Petrarch's *[[africa|Africa]]* and Ariosto's *[[orlando-furioso|Orlando Furioso]]* had established the Italian precedent for Camões' ambition. In [[portugal|Portugal]], the *Cancioneiro Geral* (1516) and the chronicles of João de Barros had begun the work of documenting Portuguese achievements, but no poet had yet attempted a national epic on the scale of Virgil.

The cult of fame was particularly urgent in a seafaring nation whose heroes spent years beyond the reach of [[europe|Europe]]an chronicles. [[vasco-da-gama|Vasco da Gama]]'s voyage (1497–1499) had occurred only some 50 years before Camões began composing, making the threat of oblivion palpable — many participants had already died without their stories recorded.

## Key Passages

**Opening declaration of the poem's ambition**:
> The feats of Arms, and famed heroick Host (001-009)

**The poet's promise of eternal memory**:
> e'erlasting name, eternal glory (002-060)

> What triumphs? what victorious glory? (004-105)

**The poet as guarantor of immortality**:
> Let grateful Gama to my Muse give grace (005-107)

## Notes & Further Reading

The theme of fame in The Lusiads operates on multiple levels: personal ([[vasco-da-gama|Vasco da Gama]]'s individual renown), national ([[portugal|Portugal]]'s imperial glory), and poetic (Camões' own bid for literary immortality). The explicit comparison with Homer and Virgil in Canto V positions The Lusiads as both heir to and competitor with the classical tradition.
