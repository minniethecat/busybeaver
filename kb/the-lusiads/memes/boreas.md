# Boreas

## Overview

Boreas is the Greek god of the north wind, one of the four directional wind-gods (Anemoi) and the bringer of winter storms. In The Lusiads, he appears in two registers: as a meteorological force — the fierce north wind that harries the Portuguese ships — and as a personified divine agent, particularly in Canto VI, where he and his brother-winds conspire to destroy the fleet at [[bacchus|Bacchus]]'s urging. Camões draws on a long poetic tradition of wind-as-divine-actor, from Homer to Virgil, while adapting Boreas to the specifically Portuguese geography of the Atlantic crossing.

## Classical Background

In Greek mythology, Boreas was the winged god of the north wind, son of [[aurora|Eos]] ([[aurora|Dawn]]) and Astraeus. He dwelt in Thrace and was depicted as a bearded, winged figure, often violent and rapacious. His Roman counterpart was Aquilo. In epic poetry, wind-gods frequently intervened at critical moments — [[aeolus|Aeolus]] unleashing the winds in the *[[the-aeneid|Aeneid]]*, Boreas in the *Iliad* — and they represented the dangerous boundary between divine will and natural chaos.

## Role in The Lusiads

### The Storm God

Boreas first appears as a simile for martial fury, establishing his association with violent force:

> As the fierce South, or Boreas in the shade of sylvan upland roars… [001-043]

### The Divine Conspiracy (Canto VI)

Boreas's most developed role comes in Canto VI, where [[bacchus|Bacchus]] descends to the undersea palace and rallies the wind-gods against the Portuguese. Boreas is a central figure in this conspiracy:

> …the wave, much-injured Boreas, with his brother-wind Aquilo… [006-039]

Camões names all four winds — Notus, Auster, Boreas, Aquilo — as they unite to destroy the fleet:

> While Notus, Auster, Boreas, Aquilo the world-machine to shatter all agreed… [006-084]

### A Hint of Romance

In a rare moment of characterisation, Boreas speaks, and Camões gives him a romantic subtext — Boreas is motivated not only by [[bacchus|Bacchus]]'s command but by love for a nymph who commands his breast:

> …meanwhile her Boreas, she who ruled his breast, looked on with favouring eye… [006-096]

And Boreas replies to his brother-wind with wounded dignity:

> "Think not, fere Boreas! e'er 'twas thought of mine…" [006-097]

### A Favouring Breeze (Canto V)

Camões also uses Boreas in a positive sense — a favouring north wind filling the sails:

> …hence while a favouring Boreas fanned the Main, once more we made our way… [005-017]

## Camões' Adaptation

Camões's treatment of Boreas is notably more developed than that of other minor deities. The wind-god receives speech, motivation (love for a sea-nymph), and a distinct personality — proud, easily injured, loyal to his brother-winds. This characterisation owes something to Ovid's *Metamorphoses* (where Boreas abducts Orithyia) but is adapted to the epic's need for antagonists. Boreas and his brother-winds are the meteorological equivalent of [[bacchus|Bacchus]]'s conspiracy: natural forces personified, resisting the Portuguese advance at the divine level.

## Notes

The distinction between Boreas and Aquilo in Camões follows Roman usage — Boreas and Aquilo are sometimes treated as aliases of the north wind, but Camões distinguishes them as brothers, suggesting slightly different directional associations (north vs. northeast). The "much-injured Boreas" [006-039] is an intriguing epithet: it hints at a grievance against the Portuguese that is never fully explained, possibly relating to their violation of the Cape — the boundary between known and unknown seas that Boreas traditionally guarded.
