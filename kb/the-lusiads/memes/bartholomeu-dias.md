# Bartholomeu Dias

## Overview

Bartholomeu Dias (c. 1450–1500) was a Portuguese navigator who achieved one of the pivotal feats of the Age of [[maritime-exploration|Discovery]]: rounding the southern tip of [[africa|Africa]] in 1488. He named it the "Cape of Tempests" (*Cabo das Tormentas*), later renamed the [[cape-of-good-hope|Cape of Good Hope]] (*Cabo da Boa Esperança*) by King John II. Although Dias appears only in the endnotes of The Lusiads, his achievement is everywhere in the poem's geography — the Cape he first rounded is the symbolic threshold of the Portuguese maritime empire.

## In The Lusiads

### A Single Mention

Dias is acknowledged in Burton's endnotes:

> Bartholomeu Dias. [012-122]

This single reference belies the scale of his contribution. The poem itself focuses on [[vasco-da-gama|Vasco da Gama]], the first to reach India, but Dias's earlier rounding of the Cape made [[vasco-da-gama|Gama]]'s voyage possible.

### The Cape He Named (Canto V)

When the Giant [[adamastor|Adamastor]] appears to the Portuguese fleet, he prophesies the dangers of the Cape:

> …hidden mighty Head of Land, the Cape of Tempests fitly named by you… [005-058]

The name "Cape of Tempests" is Dias's own — a testament to the violent storms he encountered there:

> …whose long toils attained the Cape of Tempests… [005-073]

The later renaming to "[[cape-of-good-hope|Cape of Good Hope]]" reflects the optimism Dias's achievement inspired:

> …the farthest mete, the Good Hope Cape, where Austrine shorelands end… [009-024]

## Historical Context

Bartholomeu Dias's voyage (1487–1488) was commissioned by King John II of [[portugal|Portugal]], who sought to verify whether a sea route around [[africa|Africa]] to India existed — a question that had occupied Portuguese exploration for decades:

1. **The Expedition**: Dias sailed from [[lisbon|Lisbon]] in August 1487 with two caravels and a supply ship. After reaching the coast of present-day Namibia, a storm drove his ships southward for thirteen days. When he turned east, he found no land — he had rounded the Cape without seeing it.

2. **The [[maritime-exploration|Discovery]]**: Dias landed at present-day Mossel Bay in February 1488, having passed the southernmost point of [[africa|Africa]]. He continued as far as the Great Fish River before his crew, exhausted, compelled him to turn back.

3. **Naming the Cape**: On the return voyage, Dias sighted the Cape and named it *Cabo das Tormentas* — Cape of Tempests. King John II renamed it *Cabo da Boa Esperança*, reflecting the hope of reaching India.

4. **Later Life**: Dias accompanied [[vasco-da-gama|Vasco da Gama]]'s expedition partway in 1497 and later sailed with Pedro Álvares Cabral's fleet that discovered [[brazil|Brazil]] in 1500. He died when his ship was lost in a storm off the Cape he had first rounded — a tragic symmetry.

## Significance

Though Camões does not name Bartholomeu Dias in the body of the poem, Dias is the indispensable precursor. The Cape — whether called Tempests or Good Hope — is the geographical protagonist of whole cantos, and Dias was the first [[europe|Europe]]an to round it. His achievement transformed [[europe|Europe]]an geography and made the Portuguese empire in the East thinkable. The poem's repeated invocations of the Cape as both obstacle and promise are Dias's monument, even if his name appears only in the scholarly apparatus.

## Notes

The omission of Dias from the poem's main narrative likely reflects Camões's compositional strategy: The Lusiadas is built around [[vasco-da-gama|Vasco da Gama]]'s 1497–1499 voyage, and Dias's earlier achievement is part of its prehistory. The endnote citation in Burton's edition testifies to Dias's continued presence in the scholarly memory of the poem.
