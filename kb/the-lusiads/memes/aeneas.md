## Overview

Aeneas is the legendary Trojan hero, son of the goddess [[venus|Venus]] and the mortal Anchises, whose journey from Troy to Italy forms the subject of Virgil's *[[the-aeneid|Aeneid]]*. In *The Lusiads*, he is the archetypal epic hero against whom [[vasco-da-gama|Vasco da Gama]] is measured — and found equal.

## In the Poem

Aeneas appears as the benchmark of epic heroism. The poem's hero, [[vasco-da-gama|Vasco da Gama]], is explicitly compared to and elevated alongside Aeneas: Camões claims that Portuguese navigators are the equals of the heroes of classical epic [7IdeGs]. [[venus|Venus]], as Aeneas's mother, connects the two heroes through divine lineage.

## Typological Function

Aeneas serves a typological function in Camões's scheme. As Aeneas founded the Roman people, so [[lusus|Lusus]] founded the Portuguese. As Aeneas was guided by [[venus|Venus]] to Italy, so da [[vasco-da-gama|Gama]] is guided by [[venus|Venus]] to India. The parallels establish [[portugal|Portugal]] as [[rome|Rome]]'s true successor — not merely in imperial power but in divine favor.

## Dido and Inês

The *[[the-aeneid|Aeneid]]*'s most famous romantic tragedy — Aeneas's abandonment of Dido — finds an echo in Camões's treatment of [[ines-de-castro|Inês de Castro]]. Both episodes explore the collision between personal love and historical destiny, though Camões inverts Virgil's moral framework: where Aeneas's abandonment of Dido serves Roman destiny, the murder of Inês is presented as a crime against love and justice.
