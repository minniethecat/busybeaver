# Tangier

## Overview

Tangier is the ancient North [[africa|Africa]]n port at the western entrance to the Strait of Gibraltar, a city coveted by Portuguese kings as a foothold for crusading ambition in Morocco. In The Lusiads, it appears in the royal chronicle of Canto III — as a landmark in the geography of Portuguese warfare — and in Canto IV, where Afonso V's expedition to conquer it is described. For Camões, Tangier represents the [[africa|Africa]]n theatre of Portuguese expansion: not the triumphant sea-route to India but the older, bloodier crusade against the Moors that had defined Portuguese identity for centuries.

## Geographical Context

Tangier stands on the North [[africa|Africa]]n coast of the Strait of Gibraltar, about 20 km from the Spanish shore. Founded by Phoenician colonists, it was successively Carthaginian, Roman, Vandal, Byzantine, Arab, Portuguese (1471–1661), and English (1661–1684) before becoming part of Morocco. In the fifteenth century, it was one of several Moroccan port cities — along with [[ceuta|Ceuta]], Alcácer-Ceguer, and Arzila — that Portuguese kings sought to conquer as part of the *Reconquista*'s extension into [[africa|Africa]].

## In The Lusiads

### The Geography of War (Canto III)

Tangier is named among the [[africa|Africa]]n strongholds that define the theatre of Portuguese crusading:

> They flock in thousands from Cape Ampeluse and from Tangier, Antaeus' seat of yore… [003-085]

The mention of Antaeus — the Libyan giant whom [[hercules|Hercules]] defeated — layers classical mythology over the contemporary geography of conflict.

### Afonso V's Expedition (Canto IV)

The Portuguese ambition to take Tangier is described in Canto IV:

> …parted town with arms to guard, Tangier the pop'ulous, and Arzille the walled… [004-063]

"Populous" is a factual but pointed epithet — Tangier was a substantial city, and its conquest was a major military undertaking.

## Historical Significance

Tangier was captured by the Portuguese in 1471, during the reign of Afonso V, and remained Portuguese for nearly two centuries. It was part of the network of Portuguese North [[africa|Africa]]n presidios — fortified coastal outposts — that included [[ceuta|Ceuta]] (captured 1415, the first Portuguese overseas conquest), Alcácer-Ceguer, Arzila, and Mazagan. These outposts served as bases for the Portuguese crown's [[africa|Africa]]n crusade, though their strategic value diminished as the Indian Ocean empire grew. The 1471 capture of Tangier was particularly significant because an earlier attempt in 1437, led by [[prince-henry-the-navigator|Prince Henry the Navigator]]'s brothers, had ended in disastrous defeat.

## Notes

The epithet "Antaeus' seat of yore" [003-085] is a characteristic piece of Camões's mythological geography. Antaeus was a giant of Libyan myth who drew his strength from contact with the earth (Gaia) — and whom [[hercules|Hercules]] defeated by lifting him off the ground. By associating Tangier with Antaeus, Camões suggests that the Portuguese are, like [[hercules|Hercules]], conquering a land of giants — a mythological compliment to the strength of their [[africa|Africa]]n adversaries and, by extension, to their own achievement in defeating them.
