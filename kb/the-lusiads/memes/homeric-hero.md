## Overview

The Homeric hero in *The Lusiads* is the epic archetype that Camões both invokes and transcends. [[vasco-da-gama|Vasco da Gama]] and the Portuguese captains are measured against the heroes of Homer — Odysseus and Achilles — and found not merely equal but superior, because their deeds are historical rather than mythological.

## Camões and Homer

Camões's relationship to Homer was characterized by what his Victorian translator Burton described as "affection and veneration for Homer, whom he loved as a second self" [By5ole]. This devotion was not mere imitation but a conscious claim to poetic lineage: where Homer sang of a mythological voyage, Camões would sing of a historical one, documented in ships' logs and royal chronicles.

## Da Gama as Odysseus

In Canto V, the narrator explicitly measures da [[vasco-da-gama|Gama]] against Odysseus [7GNzoC]. The Portuguese navigator, like the Homeric hero, must use cunning as well as courage to survive hostile shores. [[mombasa|Mombasa]], where the Muslims plot against him, parallels the Cyclops's cave; and like Odysseus, da [[vasco-da-gama|Gama]] is aided by a protecting goddess — [[venus|Venus]] fulfills the role of Athena in the *[[the-odyssey|Odyssey]]* [9nG3hi].

## Nuno as Achilles

The poem's Homeric modelling extends beyond its hero. The poet gives [[nuno-alvares-pereira|Nuno Álvares Pereira]] a speech before the decisive charge at [[battle-of-aljubarrota|Aljubarrota]], a standard epic convention that elevates the Constable to the stature of a Homeric hero [4r4gDD].

## Transcending Homer

Camões's claim is that Portuguese history surpasses Greek mythology. The Portuguese navigators are explicitly compared to and elevated above both Odysseus and Aeneas: the poem argues that real maritime exploration is a greater subject than the legendary wanderings of classical epic [7IdeGs].
