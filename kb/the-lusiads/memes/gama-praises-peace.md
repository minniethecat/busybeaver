# Gama's Praise of Peace Before the Samorim

## Overview

In Canto VII, Vasco da Gama is granted an audience with the Samorim (emperor) of Calicut
on the Malabar Coast. Seated before this potentate of the East, Gama delivers a carefully
crafted diplomatic address. Rather than emphasising military might or threats, Gama's
speech is a praise of peace, friendship, and mutually beneficial commerce. The episode
demonstrates Gama's diplomatic skill and Camões's vision of Portuguese expansion not as
mere conquest but as a civilising mission grounded in peaceful exchange.

## In The Lusiads

Gama enters the Samorim's presence and delivers his message from the King of Portugal.
He opens by establishing the Portuguese monarch's greatness and his desire for friendship:

> "A mighty King there thronèd, whither roll
>   voluble Heavens in eternal round,
>   where Earth by Earth conceals the rays of Sol,
>   tingeing the world he left with gloom profound;
>   hearing the rumours which from distant goal
>   respond to Echo, how on Indic ground
> thine is the sole imperial Majesty,
> the knot of Friendship lief would knit with thee."
> — [007-068]

Gama then enumerates the riches of the Portuguese realm — goods from every corner of the
known world — to establish Portugal as a worthy trading partner:

> "And by long devious courses his command
>   sent me to say, that all things mercantile,
>   which go by ocean or which go by land,
>   supplied by realms betwixt the Tage and Nyle;
>   from foggy Zealand's frore Pole-fronting strand,
>   to the far lands where Sol ne'er changeth style
> of days, that splendid shine on Aethiop shore,
> all these his kingdom holds in mighty store."
> — [007-069]

The core proposal is an alliance of peace, not war or subjugation:

> "And if thou wilt, with pacts and firmest ties
>   of naked, sacred Peace and Friendship rare,
>   allow exchange of superfluities
>   his earth and thine with like abundance bear;
>   making the rents and rev'enues richer rise
>   (wherefore men toil and travail, sweat and fare)
> for both the countries, certès, shall pertain
> to him great glory and to thee great gain."
> — [007-070]

The episode is framed by the Catual's earlier observation — informed by soothsayers —
that the Portuguese excel equally in peaceful and warlike arts:

> "It also saith the Stranger's excellence
>   in Arts of Peace, as in his bellic skill,
>   shall be so puissant, all the world shall know
>   the Conq'ueror's measure by his conquered Foe."
> — [007-064]

## Significance

Gama's speech before the Samorim is the diplomatic centrepiece of the poem. It presents
the Portuguese enterprise in India not as invasion but as a search for peaceful commerce
and Christian fellowship. The deliberate emphasis on peace — "naked, sacred Peace" — serves
Camões's broader argument that Portuguese expansion is divinely sanctioned and differs
from mere conquest. Yet the episode is freighted with dramatic irony: the Samorim's
advisors, bribed by Muslim merchants, will soon conspire against the Portuguese.
