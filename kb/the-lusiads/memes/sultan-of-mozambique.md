# The Sultan of Mozambique's Treachery

## Overview

The arrival of Vasco da Gama's fleet at Mozambique represents the first extended encounter
between the Portuguese and the Muslim world of the East African coast in *The Lusiads*.
The local Sultan, outwardly hospitable, plots to destroy the Portuguese through a scheme
involving a false pilot who is meant to lead the fleet into a trap.

## In The Lusiads

The episode begins with the fleet reaching Mozambique, where the Portuguese make contact
with the Moorish inhabitants. The Sultan receives them and initially speaks with apparent
courtesy:

> "His speech thus spake the Moor, and took his leave"
> — [001-064]

However, the Captain (Gama) soon realises that appearances are deceptive. He requests
pilots to guide the fleet to India, but "th' observant Moorman" has other intentions:

> "The Captain prayed him Pilots to purvey"
> — [001-078]

The Sultan's deceit deepens as he provides a pilot whose true mission is to destroy
the Portuguese:

> "And, know thou further" (quoth the Moor) " 'tis said"
> — [001-087]

The Moorman pursues the fleeing fleet, and the full extent of the conspiracy is revealed:

> "But the false Moorman, skilled in all the snares"
> — [001-105]

## Key Passages

The climactic confrontation occurs when the treacherous pilot's scheme is unmasked.
Camões portrays the Moor as cunning and determined:

> "E'en so that losel Moorman had designèd,"
> — [001-107]

Gama's fleet escapes, and the Sultan's failure is acknowledged in terms that
emphasise his frustration at being outwitted:

> "Now when the caitiff Moor could not but know"
> — [001-109]

The Portuguese crew, now alert to the danger, respond with decisive action. Camões
captures the shift from vulnerability to self-preservation:

> "False was the Pilot whom the King had given"
> — [001-111]

## Historical Basis

The historical Vasco da Gama did encounter hostility at Mozambique in 1498. The
local ruler, suspicious of the Christian newcomers, indeed attempted to sabotage
their voyage. Camões dramatises these events, casting the Moors as antagonists
in a providential narrative where divine favour protects the Portuguese.

## Significance

This episode establishes the central conflict pattern of the voyage: Christian
Portuguese against Muslim opposition. It introduces the theme of treachery that
recurs throughout the poem, and it demonstrates the practical dangers that
Venus and divine providence must protect the fleet against.

## Notes

The Mozambique episode has a dual narrative function: it provides the poem's first
extended cultural encounter between Europe and the Indian Ocean world, and it
establishes an ethnographic pattern that subsequent landfalls (Mombasa, Melinde,
Calicut) will either repeat or invert. The Sultan's duplicity foreshadows the
more serious treacheries that await the fleet in India, while the successful
escape demonstrates that divine providence — working through Portuguese seamanship
— can overcome human malice.
