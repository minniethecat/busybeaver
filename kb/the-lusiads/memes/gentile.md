## Definition

In *The Lusiads*, "Gentile" (Portuguese *gentio*) refers broadly to non-Christian peoples — primarily Muslims ("Moors") and Hindus encountered in Africa and India, but also, by extension, to the pagan peoples of classical antiquity. The term is less a precise theological category than a rhetorical marker of religious and cultural difference, establishing a Christian/Non-Christian axis that structures much of the poem's narrative tension.

## In The Lusiads

The term appears at three key moments in the poem. In Canto I, the poet notes that Vasco da Gama's pilot is "by sire a Gentile and by mother Jew" [001-061] — a detail that immediately establishes the multi-ethnic, multi-faith character of the Portuguese enterprise. In Canto VIII, "the puissant rulers of the Gentile herd" are described as being won over by Portuguese diplomacy [008-064]. In Canto XI, the poet envisions a future where "Christian, Moorman, Turk, and Gentile all, Joinèd in single law" [011-273] — a universalist vision that transcends the very dichotomy the term creates.

## Historical and Cultural Context

The Gentile/Christian dichotomy in *The Lusiads* reflects the 16th-century Portuguese worldview shaped by the Reconquista and the Crusading ideal. Portugal's overseas expansion was framed, in part, as a continuation of the struggle against non-Christian powers. Yet Camões complicates this binary: the poem's sympathetic portrayal of the Sultan of Melinde and its condemnation of treacherous Christian priests in India suggest that moral worth does not align neatly with religious identity.

The 1572 publication of *The Lusiads* came at a moment when Portugal's Asian empire was already under strain. The poem's treatment of Gentiles thus carries both triumphalist and anxious notes — celebrating Christian victory while acknowledging the complexity of cross-cultural encounter.

## Notes

The term "Gentile" in early modern Portuguese usage (*gentio*) carried a broader and less pejorative range than its English cognate. While it could denote "heathen" or "pagan," it was also the standard term for describing newly encountered peoples whose religious status was, from a Christian perspective, undetermined. Camões's usage reflects this ambiguity — moving between condemnation, curiosity, and, in Canto XI, a vision of universal religious unity.
