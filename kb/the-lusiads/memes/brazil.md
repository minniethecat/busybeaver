# Brazil

## Overview

Brazil appears in The Lusiads only in retrospect and prophecy — never as a destination the poem's characters reach, but as a future revealed to [[vasco-da-gama|Vasco da Gama]] through the Machine of the World in Canto X. Camões, writing in 1572, knew that Cabral had discovered Brazil in 1500, thirty years before he was born, but the poem's action is set in 1497–1499, before that discovery. Brazil therefore enters the poem as what it always was for the Portuguese imagination: a land revealed by providence, glimpsed before it was known, and destined for greatness. The single stanza Camões devotes to it carries the weight of a continent.

## Geographical Context

Brazil was claimed for [[portugal|Portugal]] by Pedro Álvares Cabral's fleet in April 1500, during a voyage to India that deviated westward — whether by accident or by design is still debated. Cabral named it *Ilha de Vera Cruz* (Island of the True Cross), later *Terra de Santa Cruz* (Land of the Holy Cross). The name "Brazil" came from the pau-brasil (brazilwood) tree, whose red dye was the colony's first valuable export. By Camões's time, Brazil was already a significant sugar-producing colony, though its vast interior remained unexplored.

## In The Lusiads

### The Prophetic Glimpse (Canto X)

Brazil appears in a single stanza of Canto X, when [[tethys|Tethys]] displays the Machine of the World and points to the lands destined for Portuguese dominion:

> …ready to a high degree by his Brazilian wars, and trophies tane from the fierce Tamoyo… [010-079]

The line is notable for what it foregrounds: not the discovery, but the wars — the conflict with the Tamoyo people — that established Portuguese control. The "Brazilian wars" were already, in 1572, part of the epic of Portuguese expansion.

### The Endnote Context

Burton's edition supplements the poem with endnotes that acknowledge Brazil's importance:

> The glorious Brazil. [012-109]

And:

> S. Cruz (= The Brazil) found by Cabral, AD 1500. [012-268]

## Key Passages

## Historical Significance

Brazil would become, by far, the most significant Portuguese colony — the engine of the sugar economy, the destination of millions of enslaved [[africa|Africa]]ns, and, after 1808, the seat of the Portuguese empire itself when the royal court fled Napoleon. In 1822, Brazil declared independence under [[pedro-i|Dom Pedro I]], the son of the Portuguese king, becoming the only Latin American nation to achieve independence through dynastic continuity rather than revolution. Camões could not have foreseen any of this, but his single stanza acknowledging Brazil as part of the Portuguese providential scheme proved prophetic in ways he never intended: Brazil would outlast and outgrow the empire that discovered it.

## Notes

The scarcity of references to Brazil in The Lusiads — essentially a single stanza — reflects the poem's chronological setting. But the stanza's position within the prophetic climax of Canto X gives it outsized weight. Brazil is the last land named before the vision concludes, the final destination in a poem about destinations. For Camões's Portuguese readers, familiar with the thriving sugar colony, this single line would have resonated as a confirmation that their American possession — distant, vast, still wild — was part of the same providential design that had carried [[vasco-da-gama|Gama]] to India.
