## Overview

Trajan (Marcus Ulpius Traianus, 53–117 CE) was Roman emperor from 98 to 117 CE. In *The Lusiads*, Camões invokes him twice as a benchmark of imperial achievement — a figure of Roman greatness that Portuguese expansion is called upon to surpass.

## In the Poem

Trajan appears in Canto I's invocation, where Camões commands the classical exemplars to yield: "Cease Alexander, Trajan cease to joy" in the glory of their conquests, because Portuguese achievements exceed them [Nog7B0]. The second reference describes Portuguese navigators reaching waters "where daring Trajan never dared tread" [qkNpx5].

## Rhetorical Function

Trajan serves a specific rhetorical function in the poem. By naming him alongside Alexander, Camões targets the two greatest military conquerors of classical antiquity. The argument is explicit: Portuguese maritime exploration represents a form of conquest more daring and more significant than Roman territorial expansion.

## Historical Significance

Trajan presided over the [[rome|Roman Empire]] at its greatest territorial extent. His conquests in Dacia and Parthia made him the archetype of the successful imperial general. For Camões, to claim that Portuguese deeds surpass Trajan's is to claim that [[portugal|Portugal]] has exceeded [[rome|Rome]] itself — the ultimate assertion of national pride.
