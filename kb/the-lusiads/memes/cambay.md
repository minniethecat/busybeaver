# Cambay

## Overview

Cambay (Khambhat) appears in The Lusiads as a significant Indian kingdom — a Muslim-ruled polity in Gujarat that the Portuguese encountered in their expansion along the western Indian coast. The poem characterizes Cambay as "bellicose" and its king as "haughtest Moor," reflecting the adversarial relationship between Portuguese Christian expansion and the Muslim powers of the Indian Ocean.

## In The Lusiads

Cambay makes its most vivid appearance in Canto VII, where the poet catalogues the powers of the Indian subcontinent [007-029]:

> The sovranty of bellicose Cambay,

The adjective "bellicose" — warlike, aggressive — establishes Cambay as a martial power, not merely a geographical region. This characterization reflects Portuguese experience of Gujarati naval power, which contested Portuguese control of the [[arabia|Arabia]]n Sea throughout the sixteenth century.

In Canto X, the prophetic vision names Cambay's ruler directly [010-080]:

> "To him Cambaya's King, that haughtest Moor,"

The phrase "haughtest Moor" condenses multiple layers of Portuguese perception: "Moor" identifies the ruler as Muslim (the term Portuguese used for all Islamic peoples, inherited from the Iberian Reconquista), while "haughtest" (proudest, most arrogant) captures the defiance that Muslim rulers offered to Portuguese claims of dominion. The endnotes identify this ruler as "Bahádur Sháh, King of Cambay" [012-220], anchoring the poetic reference in specific historical personage.

## Historical Context

The Sultanate of Gujarat, with its capital at Cambay (and later Ahmadabad), was one of the major Muslim powers of western India in the sixteenth century. Gujarati ships traded across the Indian Ocean from East [[africa|Africa]] to Malacca, and Gujarati merchants — both Muslim and Hindu — were among the most important commercial communities in the Indian Ocean world.

Bahádur Sháh (r. 1526–1537) ruled Gujarat during the period of Portuguese expansion along the Indian coast. His relations with the Portuguese were complex: periods of conflict alternated with diplomatic negotiation, and Gujarati naval power — supplemented by Ottoman support — posed one of the most serious challenges to Portuguese hegemony in the [[arabia|Arabia]]n Sea.

For Camões, Cambay represents the Muslim "other" that Portuguese expansion must overcome — a bellicose power whose resistance validates Portuguese military achievement. The kingdom's inclusion in the poem's catalog of Indian powers serves both documentary and ideological purposes: it records the political geography of the Indian Ocean while framing that geography as a field for Portuguese conquest.

## Cambay in the Epic's Political Geography

Cambay's characterization as "bellicose" distinguishes it from the passive or friendly Indian polities that populate other parts of the poem. Unlike [[cochin|Cochin]] (an ally) or the generic "Indian" spaces of the narrative, Cambay is an active antagonist — a kingdom whose military power must be acknowledged and overcome.

This adversarial role serves the poem's epic machinery: antagonists are necessary for heroic action, and Cambay — like the Zamorin of [[calicut|Calicut]] — provides the opposition against which Portuguese heroism is defined. The "haughtest Moor" is a necessary figure in the poem's moral geography, the proud pagan whose eventual submission to Christian arms confirms the providential pattern of Portuguese history.

## Notes

Cambay in The Lusiads functions primarily as an emblem of Muslim resistance to Portuguese expansion — a "bellicose" kingdom whose defiance testifies to the scale of Portuguese achievement in overcoming it. The poem's treatment of Cambay reflects the crusading ideology that shaped Portuguese imperial discourse, in which Muslim powers were simultaneously adversaries to be defeated and witnesses to Portuguese greatness.

The historical Bahádur Sháh was a more complex figure than the "haughtest Moor" of Camões' characterization — a ruler who negotiated with the Portuguese as well as fighting them, and whose kingdom was a center of commerce and culture as much as military power. The poem's simplification of Cambay into a martial antagonist is characteristic of its treatment of [[asia|Asia]]n polities, which are rendered visible primarily through their relationship to Portuguese military action.
