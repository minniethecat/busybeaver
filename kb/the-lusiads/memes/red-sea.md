# Red Sea

## Overview

The Red Sea appears in The Lusiads as a narrow passage of enormous strategic significance — the waterway connecting the Mediterranean (via [[suez|Suez]]) to the Indian Ocean, and thus the route by which Muslim shipping carried spices from India to [[europe|Europe]] before the Portuguese discovered the Cape route. In Camões's scheme, the Red Sea is the artery of the old order, and Portuguese naval power in its waters — foretold in the prophetic vision of Canto X — represents the displacement of that order by a new Christian maritime empire.

## Geographical Context

The Red Sea is a narrow, elongated body of water separating [[africa|Africa]] (Egypt, Sudan, Eritrea) from the [[arabia|Arabian Peninsula]]. At its northern end, the Gulf of [[suez|Suez]] leads toward the Mediterranean; at its southern end, the Bab-el-Mandeb strait connects it to the Gulf of Aden and the Indian Ocean. In the sixteenth century, the Red Sea was dominated by Muslim shipping, particularly the spice trade controlled from Jeddah (the port of Mecca) and by the Mamluk Sultanate of Egypt. Portuguese strategy aimed to blockade the Red Sea mouth, cutting the spice supply to [[venice|Venice]] and the Ottoman Empire.

## In The Lusiads

### The Red Sea Bight (Canto IX)

The Red Sea is first located geographically:

> …where the City crowns the Red Sea bight founded by Egypt's royal Ptolemy… [009-010]

And its commercial importance is noted:

> …where the trade of all the Red Sea shorelands flourisht most… [009-011]

### Fear on the Red Sea (Canto X)

The prophetic vision foretells Portuguese naval dominance:

> …Fear's pallid hue to Red Sea waves shall deal. [010-078]

### The Red Sea Mouth (Canto X)

The geography is precisely described:

> …where opes the Red Sea mouth, so wide and deep… [010-113]

### Socotra and the Strait (Canto X)

The island of Socotra, guarding the Red Sea entrance, is noted:

> Eke shalt thou see toforn the Red Sea strait Socotra, famed for Aloes' bitter flow… [010-153]

## Historical Significance

Portuguese strategy in the Indian Ocean under [[afonso-de-albuquerque|Afonso de Albuquerque]] included a plan to blockade the Red Sea at its mouth, cutting the Muslim spice route and capturing Aden. Although Aden was never taken, the Portuguese presence at Socotra and in the Gulf of Aden disrupted Muslim shipping and contributed to the decline of the Mamluk-Venetian spice monopoly. The Red Sea remained a contested space throughout the sixteenth century, with Ottoman naval power challenging Portuguese dominance.

## Notes

"Fear's pallid hue to Red Sea waves shall deal" [010-078] is a resonant line. The "pallid hue" of fear spreading across the "Red" Sea creates a powerful visual paradox — fear is colourless, the Red Sea is red by name — and the collision of the two evokes the transformation of the sea itself by Portuguese power. It is one of Camões's most effective uses of colour symbolism in the prophetic register.
