## Overview

Constantine I (Flavius Valerius Constantinus, c. 272–337 CE) was Roman emperor from 306 to 337 CE, known for legalizing Christianity and founding Constantinople. In *The Lusiads*, he appears as a figure of imperial rule whose greatness was diminished by later events.

## In the Poem

Constantine appears twice in the poem. Camões references the "rule imperial robbed Constantine" — an allusion to the fall of Constantinople and the loss of Christian imperial territory to the Ottoman Turks [E0us7e]. A second reference describes "great injury working to great Constantine," reinforcing the theme of imperial loss [aCc9rs].

## Imperial Loss and Portuguese Mission

Constantine's presence in the poem serves a specific argument. The fall of his eastern empire to Muslim conquest creates a vacuum that Portuguese expansion is called upon to fill. The loss of Constantinople (1453) was a traumatic event for Christian [[europe|Europe]]; Camões positions [[portugal|Portugal]]'s maritime empire as the providential response.

## East-West Symbolism

Constantine represents the Christian empire of the East whose decline opens the way for the Christian empire of the West — [[portugal|Portugal]] — to assume the mantle of providential leadership. This historical arc, from Constantine's founding of Constantinople to its fall, provides the backdrop against which Portuguese expansion takes on eschatological significance.
