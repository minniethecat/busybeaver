# Portuguese Identity

## Definition

Portuguese identity in The Lusiads is constructed around three intertwined elements: descent from the mythical hero [[lusus|Lusus]] (who gives "[[portugal|Lusitania]]" its name), the Christian faith as a defining national characteristic, and maritime exploration as the nation's providential mission. Camões presents these not as separate attributes but as a unified national character that the poem itself serves to articulate and celebrate. The Lusiads is, among other things, a sustained reflection on what it means to be Portuguese — a national epic that does not merely record Portuguese identity but actively creates it.

The concept of Portuguese identity operates at multiple levels in the poem. At the most literal level, it is a matter of naming: the Portuguese are consistently called "Lusians" (*Lusíadas*, the poem's title) or "Lusitanians," terms that invoke the Roman province of Lusitania and its mythical founder [[lusus|Lusus]]. At a deeper level, it is a matter of character: the Portuguese are defined by their courage, their faith, and their hunger for fame — a combination that Camões presents as uniquely Portuguese and providentially ordained. At the deepest level, it is a matter of destiny: to be Portuguese is to be the agent of divine will in history, the nation chosen to expand Christendom and carry European civilisation to the farthest reaches of the globe.

This identity is inherently competitive. Camões defines Portuguese identity against multiple others: against the Spanish (the perennial rival on the Iberian Peninsula), against the Muslim world (the enemy of the Reconquista), against the classical nations of antiquity ([[greece|Greece]] and [[rome|Rome]], whose achievements the Portuguese are shown to surpass), and against the various peoples the Portuguese encounter on their voyages. Portuguese identity in The Lusiads is thus not an isolated self-conception but a relational construct, defined through comparison, contrast, and competition.

## In The Lusiads

### The Lusitanian Name (Canto I)

The poem opens by identifying its subject in terms that immediately invoke the national mythology:

> occidental Lusitanian strand (001-009)

The choice of "[[portugal|Lusitania]]n" rather than "Portuguese" is significant — it invokes the Roman name for the province and connects the modern nation to its classical and mythical origins. The word "occidental" (western) positions [[portugal|Portugal]] at the edge of the known world — the westernmost point of [[europe|Europe]], the place from which expansion into the Atlantic would naturally begin. Throughout the poem, the Portuguese are referred to as "Lusians," "[[portugal|Lusitania]]ns," or "sons of [[lusus|Lusus]]," constructing a continuous lineage from antiquity to the present.

### The Mythical Foundation (Canto III)

The historical narrative of Canto III traces Portuguese identity from its legendary founder through the medieval kings to the Age of [[maritime-exploration|Discovery]]. The foundation myth is presented with scholarly tentativeness that does not diminish its cultural power:

> Lusitania, name applied by Lusus or by Lysa, sons, they say, of Bacchus (003-029)

This mythology of origin — linking [[portugal|Portugal]] to both a classical hero and the god [[bacchus|Bacchus]] — establishes a prestigious genealogy that rivals [[rome|Rome]]'s foundation by [[aeneas|Aeneas]]. The alternative etymology (Lysa) shows Camões's awareness of competing traditions, but the preferred genealogy is clear: the Portuguese descend from a companion of [[bacchus|Bacchus]], the god who conquered India — a genealogy that makes the Portuguese voyage to [[india-land|India]] a return to ancestral territory.

### The Founding Heroes (Canto IV)

Canto IV presents the key figures of Portuguese foundation — [[afonso-henriques|Afonso Henriques]], the first king, and [[nuno-alvares-pereira|Nuno Álvares Pereira]], the hero of the 1385 crisis — as exemplars of national character. The emphasis on military valour, Christian piety, and loyalty to the nation is consistent throughout:

> …the Lusian name, which first brake forth to fame… (004-024)

"Brake forth to fame" captures the sudden emergence of [[portugal|Portugal]] onto the stage of world history — a small kingdom that, against all odds, achieved greatness. The verb "brake" (broke) suggests energy, violence, and irrepressibility: Portuguese identity is not a gradual accretion but an explosive emergence.

### Address to the Nation (Canto VII)

In one of the poem's most direct rhetorical gestures, the poet addresses the Portuguese people as a collective:

> To you, O race from Lusus sprung! (007-010)

The apostrophe "O race from Lusus sprung" transforms the mythical founder into a living presence. The Portuguese are not merely citizens of a kingdom but members of a lineage — a race defined by descent from a single heroic ancestor. This racialised language of national identity, while alien to modern sensibilities, was conventional in Renaissance epic and served to naturalize the nation-state by giving it the authority of blood and inheritance.

### The Founder Revealed (Canto VIII)

In the prophetic vision of Canto VIII, [[lusus|Lusus]] himself appears as a figure in the pageant of Portuguese history:

> This first in sight is Lusus, from whose fame our Lusitania's kingdom takes its name (008-010)

[[lusus|Lusus]] is "first in sight" — the origin point, the founding figure from whom all Portuguese identity flows. The rhyme of "fame" and "name" is significant: the nation's name is inseparable from its renown, and renown is what the poem exists to confer. By placing [[lusus|Lusus]] in the visionary pageant, Camões gives the mythical founder the same ontological status as the historical kings and heroes who follow him — a rhetorical move that collapses the distinction between legend and history in the service of national identity.

### The Destiny of the Nation (Canto X)

In the concluding vision of Canto X, Portuguese identity achieves its fullest expression. The future of the nation — its conquests in [[asia|Asia]], its saints, its martyrs, its viceroys — is revealed as a coherent providential plan:

> …the Lusian heroes, who shall make their names through all the world to shake… (010-009)

"Through all the world to shake" — the phrase captures the global ambition that defines Portuguese identity in the poem. [[portugal|Portugal]] is not a regional power but a world power, and its identity is not local but universal. The poem's conclusion thus transforms Portuguese identity from a matter of descent and character into a matter of destiny: to be Portuguese is to be chosen for world-historical greatness.

## Historical/Cultural Context

Portuguese national identity was forged in the Reconquista, the centuries-long campaign to expel Muslim rule from the Iberian Peninsula. The County of [[portugal|Portugal]] became an independent kingdom in 1139 under [[afonso-henriques|Afonso Henriques]], and the sense of a distinct Portuguese people — separate from the other Iberian kingdoms — was well-established by Camões's time. The Reconquista gave Portuguese identity its militant Christian character, its orientation toward crusade and expansion, and its enduring rivalry with [[spain|Spain]] — a rivalry that the poem negotiates carefully, celebrating Portuguese achievement without directly antagonizing the Habsburg power that ruled both kingdoms after 1580.

The sixteenth century was the apex of Portuguese power and prestige, but also a period of anxiety about national decline. King [[sebastian|Sebastian]] (r. 1557–1578), to whom the poem is dedicated, embodied both the promise and the peril of Portuguese identity. His crusading zeal — he dreamed of conquering [[morocco|Morocco]] and extending Portuguese dominion into North [[africa|Africa]] — was the logical extension of the Reconquista ethos, but it led to the disastrous Battle of Alcácer Quibir (1578), in which [[sebastian|Sebastian]] died and the Portuguese army was destroyed. The resulting succession crisis led to Spanish rule (1580–1640), during which Portuguese identity was preserved largely through cultural memory — and The Lusiads, with its celebration of Portuguese uniqueness, became a central text of that memory.

Camões himself embodies the paradoxes of Portuguese identity. He was a soldier in North [[africa|Africa]] (where he lost an eye), a traveller in [[india-land|India]], and a poet who spent years in obscurity before The Lusiads brought him fame. He died in 1580, the year of the Spanish annexation, reportedly lamenting that he had loved his country too well. His biography — combining military service, overseas adventure, and literary achievement — became itself a model of Portuguese identity, and his death date (June 10) is celebrated as [[portugal|Portugal]]'s national day: Dia de [[portugal|Portugal]], de Camões e das Comunidades Portuguesas.

The poem's construction of Portuguese identity also reflects the broader cultural currents of the Renaissance. The revival of classical antiquity gave [[portugal|Portugal]] a usable past — a genealogy that linked the modern nation to [[rome|Rome]], [[greece|Greece]], and the gods themselves. The humanist emphasis on fame and glory as legitimate human aspirations provided the ideological framework for the poem's celebration of Portuguese achievement. And the expansion of geographical knowledge — the discovery of new continents and peoples — created the conditions for a national identity defined by global reach rather than territorial extent. The Lusiads is thus a product of its moment, but its influence on Portuguese identity has outlasted that moment by centuries.

## Key Passages

**The Occidental Strand (Canto I)**:
> occidental Lusitanian strand (001-009)

The poem's opening self-identification. "Occidental" positions [[portugal|Portugal]] at the western edge of the world; "Lusitanian" connects the modern nation to its classical past. Together they define Portuguese identity in geographical and historical terms.

**Mythical Origins (Canto III)**:
> Lusitania, name applied by Lusus or by Lysa, sons, they say, of Bacchus (003-029)

The dual foundation myth — [[lusus|Lusus]] or Lysa — shows Camões's awareness of competing traditions while preferring the genealogy that links [[portugal|Portugal]] to [[bacchus|Bacchus]], the god who conquered India. A strategically chosen ancestry.

**First Fame (Canto IV)**:
> …the Lusian name, which first brake forth to fame… (004-024)

Portuguese identity as an eruption into world history. "Brake forth" suggests explosive energy; "to fame" defines the measure of national achievement. The passage encapsulates the competitive energy that drives the poem's conception of national identity.

**Race from Lusus (Canto VII)**:
> To you, O race from Lusus sprung! (007-010)

The apostrophe to the Portuguese nation. "Race from Lusus" naturalises the nation by rooting it in biological descent from a single founder. The language of race and lineage is fundamental to the poem's construction of identity.

**The Founder's Fame (Canto VIII)**:
> This first in sight is Lusus, from whose fame our Lusitania's kingdom takes its name (008-010)

[[lusus|Lusus]] as the origin of Portuguese identity. "Fame" and "name" are linked: the nation's identity is inseparable from its renown. The founder appears in the visionary pageant alongside historical kings, collapsing the distinction between legend and history.

**World-Shaking Names (Canto X)**:
> …the Lusian heroes, who shall make their names through all the world to shake… (010-009)

The global ambition of Portuguese identity. "Through all the world" defines the scope; "shake" suggests the impact. Portuguese identity is not merely local or regional but universal — a nation whose deeds will resonate across the globe.

## Related Concepts

- [[fame-and-glory]] — The desire for renown that Camões presents as a defining Portuguese characteristic and a legitimate human aspiration
- [[christian-expansion]] — The fusion of religious identity with national mission, rooted in the Reconquista and extended to global scale
- [[maritime-exploration]] — Seafaring as national character: the Portuguese as a people defined by their relationship to the sea

## Notes & Further Reading

Camões's construction of Portuguese identity in The Lusiads is both descriptive and prescriptive: he describes what [[portugal|Portugal]] has achieved while defining what [[portugal|Portugal]] should be. The poem became so central to Portuguese national identity that Camões's death date (June 10, 1580) is celebrated as [[portugal|Portugal]]'s national day — Dia de [[portugal|Portugal]], de Camões e das Comunidades Portuguesas. No other European nation celebrates its national day on the anniversary of a poet's death, and this fact alone testifies to the poem's unique role in Portuguese culture.

The influence of The Lusiads on Portuguese identity extends far beyond the sixteenth century. During the period of Spanish rule (1580–1640), the poem was a touchstone of Portuguese distinctiveness — proof that [[portugal|Portugal]] had a literary tradition as great as any in [[europe|Europe]]. During the Napoleonic invasions, when the Portuguese court fled to Brazil, the poem's vision of a maritime empire with its capital overseas acquired new resonance. In the twentieth century, the poem was claimed by the Estado Novo regime as a charter of imperial destiny, though it has also been read against the grain as a text that subtly critiques the imperial project it ostensibly celebrates.

The poem's racialised language of national identity — "race from Lusus sprung" — raises questions for modern readers. Camões is writing within a Renaissance framework in which national identity was commonly expressed in the language of blood, lineage, and descent. But his construction of a unitary "Lusian race" also suppresses the internal diversity of Portuguese society (including its Jewish and Muslim populations, expelled or forcibly converted in the decades before the poem was written) and the racial complexity of the Portuguese empire (including the mixed-race populations of [[brazil|Brazil]], [[africa|Africa]], and [[india-land|India]]). A critical reading of the poem must acknowledge both the power of its vision and the exclusions on which that vision depends.

The relationship between Portuguese identity and the sea deserves particular emphasis. Camões presents the Portuguese as a maritime people by nature and destiny — a nation whose identity is inseparable from ships, voyages, and oceanic horizons. This maritime identity distinguishes [[portugal|Portugal]] from the land-based empires of [[spain|Spain]] and [[france|France]] and aligns it with the classical seafaring nations of antiquity (the Phoenicians, the Greeks, the Carthaginians). The sea is not merely the medium of Portuguese achievement but its symbol: to be Portuguese is to be, like [[vasco-da-gama|Vasco da Gama]], a navigator of unknown waters, a discoverer of new worlds, a sailor whose home is the ocean itself.

For further reading on Portuguese identity in the Renaissance, see the works of Luís de Sousa Rebelo on Camões and national identity, and the broader scholarship on early modern nation-building by Anthony D. Smith and Benedict Anderson. The poem's relationship to Portuguese imperial ideology is explored in depth by Helder Macedo and by the post-colonial readings of Eduardo Lourenço.
