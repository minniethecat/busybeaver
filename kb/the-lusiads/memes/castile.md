# Castile

## Overview

Castile (Spanish: *Castilla*) was the dominant Christian kingdom of medieval Iberia and the core of what would become the Kingdom of [[spain|Spain]]. In The Lusiads, Castile appears as the great rival against which [[portugal|Portugal]] defined its identity — a larger, wealthier, and militarily powerful neighbor whose repeated attempts to absorb Portugal shaped the nation's history and self-understanding.

Unlike [[spain|Spain]], which Camões treats with a mixture of rivalry and respect, Castile in the poem is more often the adversary — the power that must be resisted, fought, and defeated for Portugal to fulfill its providential destiny.

## In The Lusiads

Castile first appears in Canto III, where Vasco da Gama narrates the history of Portugal to the King of [[melinde|Melinde]]. The poem describes Afonso VI of León and Castile in elevated terms [003-019]:

> Castilian, whom his star raised high to sway

This is followed by the enumeration of his realm [003-019]:

> Baetis, Leon, Granáda, and Castile.

The Castilian military threat is personified in the figure of the "superb Castilian" who prepares for war [003-034]:

> Lo! the superb Castilian 'gins prepare

In stanza 37, the poem weaves together the dynastic entanglements that bound Portugal to Castile [003-037]:

> when the Castilian Don stood dight to see,
> while still Castile believes him true to be,

The most forceful treatment of Castile comes in Canto III's account of the succession crisis. After the death of King Fernando of Portugal, his widow Leonor Teles sought aid from Castile — a moment Camões presents as a near-fatal threat to Portuguese independence [003-143]:

> Léonor craveth aidance of Castile

The ensuing war produced the defining moment of Portuguese national mythology: the [[battle-of-aljubarrota|Battle of Aljubarrota]] (1385), where Portuguese forces under [[nuno-alvares-pereira|Nuno Álvares Pereira]] and King [[king-john-i|John I]] defeated a superior Castilian army, securing Portugal's independence for two centuries.

At stanza 99, Camões praises Portuguese defiance [003-099]:

> This proud Castile's bravades with equal pride

And the determination to fight [003-099]:

> and marchèd boldly to debel Castile

A savior figure is invoked for the beleaguered kingdom [003-105]:

> a second Saviour to our sad Castile

And the strategic calculus of the conflict [003-112]:

> On this and on his skill Castile relies

The final reference in Canto III has the Castilian forces arriving [003-138]:

> came the Castilian, who came nigh to cause

## Historical Context

The Kingdom of Castile emerged from the 9th-century County of Castile, a frontier territory of the Kingdom of León. Through the 11th to 13th centuries, Castile expanded southward during the Reconquista, absorbing León in 1230 to become the dominant power of Christian Iberia.

For Portugal, which had won its independence from León in 1143 (recognized by the Treaty of Zamora), Castile represented an existential threat throughout the medieval period. Three major succession crises — in 1383–1385, the 1470s, and 1580 — each saw Castile attempt to claim the Portuguese throne. The first was defeated at Aljubarrota; the third succeeded, inaugurating the sixty-year Iberian Union (1580–1640).

Camões wrote The Lusiads in the shadow of the third crisis. When the poem was published in 1572, the young King [[king-sebastian|Sebastian]] was the last hope of the Aviz dynasty. His death at Alcácer Quibir in 1578 would open the door to Philip II of Spain's claim to the Portuguese throne — the very absorption Camões' poem implicitly warns against.

## Notes

Camões uses "Castile" and "Spain" somewhat interchangeably in the poem, reflecting the late-medieval reality that Castile was the hegemonic power within what was increasingly called Spain. However, the tone differs: Spain can be admired, even blessed ("O noble Spain!" at 011-140); Castile is more consistently the adversary that must be resisted. This distinction reflects the specific political threat — it was the Castilian crown, not "Spain" as an abstraction, that sought to absorb Portugal.
