# The Tropics

## Overview

The Tropics — the Tropic of Cancer at 23.5°N and the Tropic of Capricorn at 23.5°S — were, in classical geography, the boundaries of the habitable world. The "Torrid Zone" between them was believed to be uninhabitable due to heat. The Portuguese navigators of the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries proved this belief false, crossing both tropics repeatedly on their way to and from India. In The Lusiads, the Tropics appear as symbols of geographical limits transcended — the old boundaries that Portuguese courage has rendered obsolete.

## Geographical Context

In Ptolemaic geography, which dominated [[europe|Europe]]an knowledge until the Age of [[maritime-exploration|Discovery]], the world was divided into five zones: two frigid zones at the poles, two temperate zones in the middle latitudes, and the Torrid Zone between the Tropics. The Torrid Zone was believed too hot for human habitation — a belief the Portuguese disproved by sailing directly through it. The Tropic of Cancer crosses the Sahara and [[arabia|Arabia]]; the Tropic of Capricorn crosses southern [[africa|Africa]] and [[brazil|Brazil]]. Both were crossed by Portuguese ships on the Carreira da Índia (the India Run).

## In The Lusiads

### The Burning Zone (Canto VIII)

The Portuguese claim to have mastered all climates:

> …in whatever peoples 'neath the Tropick burn. [008-080]

"Burn" is the operative word — the classical fear of the Torrid Zone is acknowledged even as it is dismissed. The Portuguese have not avoided the burning zone; they have marched straight through it.

### From Torrid to Frozen (Canto X)

The prophetic vision encompasses the full climatic range of Portuguese empire:

> To torrid Tropicks, Arcticks frore and grey, the Lusian name shall pass, and pass to stay. [010-163]

The pairing of "torrid Tropicks" with "Arcticks frore" is a poetic expression of totality — the Portuguese empire spans the entire thermal range of the planet.

## Historical Significance

The crossing of the Tropics was one of the quiet revolutions of the Age of [[maritime-exploration|Discovery]]. It required no single heroic act, but it transformed [[europe|Europe]]an geography: the Torrid Zone, once a theoretical barrier, became an experienced reality. The Portuguese were the first [[europe|Europe]]ans to cross the equator by sea (1473, under Lopo Gonçalves), to cross the Tropic of Capricorn (1488, Dias), and to establish a regular navigational route that crossed both tropics twice — outbound and homebound — on every India voyage. This practical refutation of classical geography was as significant, in its way, as the discovery of new lands.

## Notes

The phrase "torrid Tropicks, Arcticks frore and grey" [010-163] is a masterpiece of compression. Three words establish the climate, three more contrast it, and the line as a whole maps the Portuguese empire from the equator to the pole. "Frore" (an archaic word for "frozen") is a characteristic Burton coinage — a word as cold as the thing it describes.
