## Overview

Francis Xavier (1506–1552) was a Navarrese Catholic missionary and co-founder of the Society of Jesus (the Jesuits). He led the first Jesuit mission to Asia, arriving in Goa in 1542, and spent the following decade evangelizing across Portuguese India, Malacca, the Moluccas, and Japan. He died on Shangchuan Island off the coast of China while attempting to enter the Ming Empire. Canonized in 1622, he is venerated as the patron saint of missionaries.

## Connection to The Lusiads

Although Francis Xavier does not appear as a character in *The Lusiads* (which was published in 1572, two decades after his arrival in India), the epic's prophetic passages anticipate the spread of Christianity in the East. The endnote mention of Xavier situates him within the providential framework of the poem — the Portuguese maritime enterprise is presented not merely as conquest or commerce, but as the vehicle for divine revelation to reach pagan lands.

Xavier's mission to India, beginning in 1542, realized the spiritual dimension that Camões' epic celebrates: the extension of Christendom into the Indian Ocean world opened by Gama's voyage.

## Historical Significance

Xavier's arrival in Goa marked the beginning of sustained Jesuit presence in Asia. His missionary methods — adapting to local languages, establishing schools, and working through existing Portuguese colonial structures — set the pattern for Catholic missions in the early modern period. His letters from India were widely circulated in Europe and helped shape European perceptions of Asian societies.
