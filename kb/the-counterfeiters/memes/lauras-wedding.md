# Laura's Wedding

Laura Douviers, pregnant by Vincent Molinier, marries the devoted but dull Félix Douviers in a modest Protestant ceremony at the chapel in Rue Madame — a marriage of convenience that resolves her social crisis at the cost of romantic fulfillment.

## Summary

On November 5th, Laura Douviers is married to Félix Douviers in the little chapel on Rue Madame. The ceremony is attended by the entire Vedel-Azaïs family — Laura's grandfather, parents, two sisters, and young brother — along with numerous uncles, aunts, and cousins. The wedding is notably modest: the traditional wedding breakfast is replaced by "a less costly tea." Edouard attends and records his observations in his journal, noting the melancholy of old La Pérouse at the harmonium. [PN=013-018] [PN=013-039]

## Context

### Narrative Position

This event occurs in **Part I, Chapter 13**, described through Edouard's journal entry dated November 5th. It falls approximately six weeks after the novel's opening and represents a partial resolution of the Laura-Vincent plotline. [PN=013-018] [PN=013-039]

### Preceding Events

Laura became pregnant by Vincent Molinier during their summer affair. Vincent subsequently abandoned her for Lady Griffith (see [[vincents-night-with-lilian]]). Desperate, Laura wrote to Edouard (see [[lauras-letter-to-edouard]]), who returned to Paris. Félix Douviers, a gentle and devoted man who has long loved Laura, has agreed to marry her knowing she carries another man's child. [PN=005-008] [PN=009-003]

## Detailed Account

Edouard's journal records the ceremony with characteristic detachment. The setting is the modest Protestant chapel on Rue Madame. Old La Pérouse, the aged music-master who taught Laura's mother, plays the harmonium — an instrument chosen because "the Protestant service must have seemed a mass to him" (La Pérouse was raised Catholic). [PN=013-018] [PN=013-019]

The whole Vedel-Azaïs clan assembles: Laura's grandfather Azaïs, Pastor Prosper Vedel and his wife, Laura's sisters Sarah and Rachel, her young brother Armand, and extended family. The gathering reveals the close-knit Protestant community that forms the moral background of the novel. [PN=013-018]

The Vedel-Azaïses have substituted a modest tea for the traditional wedding breakfast, reflecting both their financial constraints and their Protestant austerity. The reception is held at the family's boarding school, where the guests mingle in the cramped quarters. Armand, Laura's cynical younger brother, makes sardonic remarks throughout, noting that the best room has been locked to prevent guests from seeing it. [PN=013-039] [PN=013-070]

Edouard records the event with characteristic ambivalence — he is present as family friend and former lover, observing the resolution of Laura's crisis with relief but also with the novelist's analytical eye, already transforming the occasion into material for his journal. [PN=013-018]

## Characters Involved

- **Laura Douviers (now Vedel-Douviers)** — The bride, pregnant with another man's child, marrying for social protection rather than love
- **Félix Douviers** — The groom, a gentle and devoted man who accepts Laura's child as his own
- **Edouard** — The novelist, present as observer and witness, recording the event in his journal
- **Old La Pérouse** — The aged music-master, playing the harmonium with melancholy dignity [PN=013-023]
- **Armand Vedel** — Laura's cynical younger brother, whose sardonic commentary exposes the family's pretensions [PN=013-070] [PN=013-090]
- **The Vedel-Azaïs family** — The extended Protestant clan whose presence symbolizes the social order into which Laura is being absorbed

## Consequences

### Immediate Aftermath

The wedding resolves Laura's immediate social crisis — she now has a husband and a name to give her unborn child. But Edouard's journal entries suggest the resolution is hollow. Laura and Félix depart for their new life, but the marriage is built on a lie of omission: the world knows the child is not Douviers's, but the pretense of legitimacy must be maintained. [PN=015-002]

### Narrative Ripple Effects

The wedding marks Laura's partial exit from the novel's main action — she moves to the background as a married woman. But the compromise of her marriage echoes through the story: it represents the "counterfeit" solution to a real problem, the socially acceptable lie that covers an uncomfortable truth. Laura's story illuminates the limited options available to women in Gide's world, where pregnancy outside marriage can be resolved only through a marriage of convenience or ruin.

## Thematic Significance

Laura's wedding is the novel's clearest example of the **social counterfeit** — a ceremony that publicly declares a fiction (that Douviers is the father) while everyone involved knows the truth. The modest scale of the wedding, the substitution of tea for a wedding breakfast, and the locked rooms all suggest a family papering over its shame. Yet Gide does not condemn Laura or Douviers — their solution, however compromised, is presented as the best available option in a society that punishes female sexuality. The wedding's Protestant austerity, contrasted with La Pérouse's Catholic sensibilities, also highlights the novel's concern with the religious and cultural tensions within French society.

## Literary Technique

Gide presents the wedding entirely through **Edouard's journal**, filtering the public ceremony through private consciousness. This technique transforms communal ritual into individual perception, emphasizing the gap between social performance and inner experience. Edouard's novelist's eye notes details — La Pérouse's sadness, Armand's cynicism — that a conventional wedding narrative would suppress, exposing the anxieties beneath the celebration.
