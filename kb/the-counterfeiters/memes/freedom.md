# Freedom

The supreme value and central paradox of [[André Gide]]'s _The Counterfeiters_ — the liberty that characters long for (024-019), the "spontaneity" that makes authentic life possible (027-009), and the burden that proves difficult to sustain once achieved.

## Definition

In Gide's novel, freedom is defined both positively and negatively. Positively: as the "supreme good" for which [[Bernard Profitendieu|Bernard]] longs (024-019), the quality of those who act "without masters, nor scruples" (027-009). Negatively: as something feared — "she was afraid of liberty, of crime, of ease" (003-101) — and as a state that, once claimed, can turn out to be an illusion: "when I thought I was freeing myself, in reality I was becoming the slave of my pride" (014-036).

## Role in The Counterfeiters

### Thematic Significance

Freedom is the novel's highest ideal and its most difficult problem. Characters pursue freedom — [[Bernard Profitendieu|Bernard]] leaves home, [[Laura Douviers|Laura]] leaves her husband, [[Edouard]] seeks artistic freedom — but each discovers that freedom without purpose becomes emptiness. The novel questions whether freedom is compatible with relationship, morality, or art, without arriving at a simple answer.

> "I stopped putting my freedom first." (024-011) — This admission, spoken by a character who has chosen commitment over absolute liberty, captures the novel's ambivalence: freedom may need to be limited to be meaningful.

## Manifestations in the Text

### Key Scenes

Bernard's departure from the [[Albéric Profitendieu|Profitendieu]] household is the novel's foundational act of freedom. Discovering his illegitimacy, Bernard declares himself "free" and takes "himself for a rebel, an outlaw, who tramples underfoot everything that opposes his desire" (024-019). Yet this declaration proves naive — freedom turns out to require resources, relationships, and eventually, self-limitation.

> "Those who are always saying they want to be free — they never create anything." (023-013) — A pointed critique of freedom as mere negation, suggesting that authentic creation requires constraint.

The [[Saas-Fee]] episodes explore a different model of freedom: the "freedom and spontaneity" that "make the elderly seem younger" in the Alpine setting (027-009). Here, freedom is not escape from society but a natural mode of being that social convention elsewhere suppresses.

## Literary and Philosophical Context

Gide's treatment of freedom reflects his engagement with [[Friedrich Nietzsche|Nietzschean]] individualism — the call to become oneself by shedding inherited morality — as well as his critique of it. The novel shows characters who pursue Nietzschean liberation (Vincent, Lilian) meeting disastrous ends, while those who temper freedom with responsibility (Bernard, Edouard) achieve growth. Gide's own life, marked by his break from Protestant convention and his embrace of homosexual desire, informs the novel's insistence that freedom must be lived, not merely theorized.
