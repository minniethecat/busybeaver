# Hamlet

William Shakespeare's tragedy _Hamlet_ (c. 1600), referenced by [[Bernard Profitendieu]] at the moment of his discovery of his illegitimacy. Hamlet's situation — the son who must confront a hidden paternal truth — provides Bernard with a literary model for his own experience, while the differences between the two figures illuminate Gide's modernist transformation of tragic inheritance into existential freedom.

## Overview

_Hamlet_ is Shakespeare's most celebrated tragedy, the story of the Prince of Denmark who learns from his father's ghost that his uncle Claudius murdered his father and married his mother. Hamlet's dilemma — whether and how to act on this revelation — has made the play the definitive Western treatment of the conflict between knowledge and action, reflection and revenge.

## Appearance in The Counterfeiters

### Context of Mention

[[Bernard Profitendieu|Bernard]] invokes Hamlet in his interior monologue immediately after discovering his mother's love letters and learning he is illegitimate. Reflecting on his situation, he compares himself to the Danish prince:

> "It isn't everyone who can indulge in the luxury of a ghost to reveal them, like Hamlet. Hamlet! It's curious how one's point of view changes according as one is the offspring of crime or legitimacy." (002-004)

The reference is both self-dramatizing and self-mocking. Bernard recognizes the parallel — a hidden truth about a parent revealed, a son forced to reconsider his identity — while also acknowledging the absurdity of the comparison. He has no ghost, no murdered father, no uncle-king. His discovery is sordid rather than tragic: love letters in a drawer, not poison in an ear.

[[Edouard]] later echoes the reference when reflecting on Bernard's reading of his journal: "as I have heard Hamlet remark" (013-108), further weaving Shakespeare's play into the novel's texture of allusion.

> "What doesn't happen to everyone is to find arms underneath — or guilty love-letters. Pooh! The important thing was that I should learn the facts. It isn't everyone who can indulge in the luxury of a ghost to reveal them, like Hamlet." (002-004)

### Narrative Function

The Hamlet reference establishes the novel's method of ironic intertextuality. Bernard invokes the highest tragic precedent for his situation — and immediately deflates it. Where Hamlet's discovery leads to paralysis, feigned madness, and eventual tragedy, Bernard's leads to a decision to leave home, seek out [[Olivier Molinier|Olivier]], and begin a new life. Bernard inverts Hamlet: the discovery of hidden truth is liberating rather than paralyzing.

This inversion is central to Gide's treatment of identity. Hamlet is trapped by his father's legacy — the ghost demands revenge, the son must obey. Bernard, freed from the obligation to resemble any father, can become himself. "There's no better cure for the fear of taking after one's father, than not to know who he is" (002-004). The absence of a ghost — the absence of paternal authority — is Bernard's advantage, not his deprivation.

## Thematic Resonance

| Element | Hamlet | Bernard Profitendieu |
|---------|--------|---------------------|
| **Discovery** | Father's ghost reveals murder | Love letters reveal illegitimacy |
| **Paternal Question** | Must avenge father | Liberated from father |
| **Response to Truth** | Paralysis, feigned madness | Action, departure |
| **Identity** | Defined by inheritance ("I am Hamlet the Dane") | Self-constructed ("I prefer being made a bastard") |
| **Outcome** | Tragedy (death of protagonist) | Open (self remains a work in progress) |

## Gide's Relationship to the Work

Shakespeare was a significant presence in Gide's intellectual formation. Gide translated portions of Shakespeare into French and engaged with the plays throughout his career. The Hamlet reference in _The Counterfeiters_ is typical of Gide's allusive method: the classic text is invoked not for authority but for transformation. Bernard is not Hamlet; he is what Hamlet cannot be — the son who turns the discovery of paternal falsehood into existential opportunity rather than tragic burden.

The reference also aligns _The Counterfeiters_ with the modernist practice of mythic method — the use of classical precedents to give shape and resonance to contemporary experience, while marking the distance between ancient grandeur and modern complexity.
