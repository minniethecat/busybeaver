# The Banquet Scene

Comte [[Comte de Passavant|Robert de Passavant]] organizes a literary banquet for his circle — an event that brings together the novel's competing worlds of art, ambition, and moral compromise in a single social performance. The banquet is the counter-image to [[Edouard]]'s solitary artistic practice, embodying everything about the literary world that Gide's novel critiques. It is the social equivalent of the [[counterfeit-coin]]: false value circulating among people who have agreed to treat it as real.

## Summary

Robert de Passavant, the fashionable novelist and count, hosts a banquet for his literary and social circle. The event gathers various characters from the novel's intersecting worlds — including [[Olivier Molinier]], who is being drawn into Passavant's orbit — and serves as a display of worldly success, literary ambition, and the social mechanisms by which reputations are made and corrupted. The banquet is the counter-image to [[Edouard]]'s solitary artistic practice, embodying everything about the literary world that Gide's novel critiques. [PN=017-036] [PN=016-050] [PN=016-051]

## Context

### Narrative Position

The banquet occupies **Part I, Chapters 16–17**, though it is referenced and prepared for in earlier chapters. It occurs after [[Edouard]]'s return to [[paris]] and during [[Olivier Molinier|Olivier]]'s gradual estrangement from his uncle, as Passavant's influence over the young man grows. [PN=017-036] [PN=016-004]

### Preceding Events

Passavant has been cultivating [[Olivier Molinier]], [[Edouard]]'s nephew, drawing him away from his uncle's austere artistic seriousness toward the glamour of worldly success. Passavant's novel _The Horizontal Bar_ has been a commercial success, and Edouard has been reading it with contempt on the train from Le Havre (see [[lauras-letter-to-edouard]]). The banquet is Passavant's stage — the social mechanism by which he consolidates his influence and displays his power. [PN=009-001] [PN=009-002] [PN=016-050]

## Detailed Account

The banquet is described through [[Edouard]]'s hostile but fascinated observation. Passavant presides with the ease of a man entirely at home in the world of social performance. The gathering includes literary figures, journalists, hangers-on, and young men like [[Olivier Molinier|Olivier]] who are being initiated into the rituals of literary careerism. [PN=017-036] [PN=016-051]

> Passavant holds "an evening paper in his hand" (017-036) — the symbol of his connection to the machinery of publicity that [[Edouard]] despises.

The banquet is a network in visible operation: conversation that is performance, relationships that are investments, praise that is strategic. It is the literary world as [[counterfeit-coin]] — the circulation of false value among people who have agreed to treat it as real. [PN=017-036]

Olivier, uncomfortable but fascinated, is the guest of honor in training. Passavant has given him an inscribed copy of _The Horizontal Bar_, with the dedication marking him as the count's protégé. Olivier "didn't much care for Passavant's book" but finds himself unable to resist the attention. The banquet represents the temptation of worldly success — the easier path that [[Edouard]]'s uncompromising artistic integrity cannot offer. [PN=016-050] [PN=016-054] [PN=016-055]

> The scene is notable for what it lacks: genuine intellectual exchange, authentic feeling, artistic seriousness (017-009). It is a theater of mutual flattery in which everyone plays a part and no one speaks truth.

## Consequences

### Immediate Aftermath

The banquet accelerates [[Olivier Molinier|Olivier]]'s drift toward Passavant's circle. The young man, torn between his uncle's austere example and the count's worldly seductions, begins to spend more time in Passavant's company. This estrangement from [[Edouard]] is one of the novel's central emotional tensions, resolved only when Olivier, after a night of dissolution with Passavant, attempts suicide and returns to Edouard's care. [PN=017-009] [PN=016-057]

### Narrative Ripple Effects

The banquet crystallizes the novel's opposition between two models of the artistic life:

| Model | Representative | Method | Reward |
|-------|---------------|--------|--------|
| **Worldly Careerism** | Passavant | Social performance, publicity, networking | Recognition, influence, money |
| **Artistic Integrity** | Edouard | Solitary labor, journal-keeping, self-scrutiny | Truth to experience |

Throughout the novel, characters are forced to choose between these alternatives — or, more often, to find themselves claimed by one or the other. [[Olivier Molinier|Olivier]]'s ultimate rejection of Passavant and return to [[Edouard]] is the novel's most explicit endorsement of artistic authenticity, but the banquet scene ensures that authenticity is never presented as the easy choice. Passavant's world is seductive precisely because it offers rewards that Edouard's world cannot.

## Thematic Significance

The banquet represents the novel's most concentrated critique of the **counterfeit literary world** — a social system in which artistic value is determined not by the work but by the network, and reputation circulates like counterfeit currency on the strength of collective pretense. Passavant's banquet is the institutional form of what the schoolboy coin ring does in miniature: the creation and circulation of false value. By placing this scene at the heart of the novel, Gide indicts not just Passavant but the entire system of literary reputation that his own novel, however unwillingly, participates in.

## Literary Technique

Gide narrates the banquet through a combination of **Edouard's perspective** and **authorial overview**, maintaining the novel's signature tension between immersion and distance. Passavant is not caricatured — he is presented as genuinely charming and intelligent, which makes his corruption more dangerous than simple villainy. The banquet is a set-piece of social observation, written with the precision of a novelist who knew the Parisian literary world intimately and found it wanting.
