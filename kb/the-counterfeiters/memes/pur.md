# Pur (The Pure Novel)

Edouard's aesthetic ideal in Andre Gide's _The Counterfeiters_ — a conception of the novel purified of all elements not specifically belonging to the genre, representing Gide's modernist ambition to remake the novel form.

## Definition

The concept of the "pure novel" (_roman pur_) appears in Edouard's journal as he travels to Paris on the train. He articulates it as a program: "I should like to strip the novel of every element that does not specifically belong to the novel" (009-025). Just as photography freed painting from the obligation of accurate representation, and the phonograph might free the novel from naturalistic dialogue, the pure novel would shed outward events (to cinema), character description (to the reader's imagination), and everything not essential to the genre's core. Edouard concludes: "in art, as in everything else, purity is the only thing I care about" (009-025).

## Role in The Counterfeiters

### Thematic Significance

The pure novel ideal is the aesthetic counterpart to the novel's moral theme of authenticity. Just as characters struggle to "ring true" and avoid being "counterfeit," the novel itself must be purified of inauthentic elements. The theory informs Gide's own practice: _The Counterfeiters_ abandons linear narration, omniscient narration, and stable characterization in favor of multiple perspectives, journal entries, letters, and the mise-en-abyme of the novel-within-the-novel.

Sophroniska offers the counter-argument: "everything which is created by the intelligence alone is false" (022-058). Her critique suggests that pure formalism, divorced from psychological depth, risks becoming another form of counterfeiting — intellectually elegant but humanly hollow.

### Character Associations

**Edouard** is the theorist of the pure novel, though he has "not yet written a line" of it (009-025). The gap between his theory and practice is itself a subject of the novel. **Bernard** provides the instinctive counterpoint — arguing for starting from facts rather than ideas, for the concrete over the abstract. **Passavant** represents the impure novel — commercially successful, opportunistic, "cut out of a cloth which has no thickness" (027-009).

## Manifestations in the Text

### Key Scenes

The articulation of the pure novel ideal occurs during Edouard's train journey (009-025), where he writes in his notebook: a manifesto for the novel to come. The passage is one of the most frequently cited in Gide criticism, establishing the theoretical framework for the entire work.

The idea resurfaces implicitly throughout the novel. Edouard's journal itself is a "pure" form — unmediated by the conventions of third-person narration. The absence of a traditional plot, the fragmentation of viewpoints, and the refusal of closure all enact the purification Edouard theorizes.

> "I should like to strip the novel of every element that does not specifically belong to the novel." (009-025)

### Symbolic Expressions

The purity metaphor extends through the novel's language: "pure, authentic sound" (024-042), "pure and candid" (describing Olivier). The counterfeit coin is the inverse — the impure, the false, the inauthentic. The novel's formal experiment is a search for literary purity in a world of counterfeit values.

## Literary and Philosophical Context

Gide's pure novel draws on Symbolist and modernist currents. Mallarmé had spoken of "pure poetry"; Valéry pursued "pure thought." Gide extends this purification to the novel, influenced by his reading of Dostoevsky (whose psychological depth he admired) and his rejection of nineteenth-century realism. The idea also resonates with the Protestant ethic of Gide's upbringing — purity as both aesthetic and moral category.

The Goncourts' _Journal_, which Gide references, offered a model of unmediated observation, though Gide complicates this with self-consciousness: "I am never anything but what I think myself — and this varies so incessantly" (009-011).
