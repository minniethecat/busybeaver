# Friendship

In [[André Gide]]'s _The Counterfeiters_, friendship — particularly between young men — is the emotional crucible in which identity is tested, desire is negotiated, and authenticity is forged against the pressures of family, school, and social convention.

## Definition

Friendship in Gide's novel is charged with intensities that exceed the conventional boundaries of the term. It is the site where the novel's deepest questions — about [[sincerity]], about the courage to be oneself, about the costs of intimacy — are dramatized. The friendship between [[Bernard Profitendieu]] and [[Olivier Molinier]] is the novel's emotional core, a relationship that is neither purely platonic nor explicitly sexual, but which carries the full weight of the novel's inquiry into what it means to truly know another person.

## Role in The Counterfeiters

### Thematic Significance

The novel opens with Bernard thinking of Olivier: "Olivier, my friend, the time has come for *me* to put your good-fellowship to the test" (002-007). Friendship is presented not as a static condition but as something that must be tested — and which may fail the test. Bernard's immediate impulse upon discovering his [[illegitimacy]] is not to confide in anyone but to *use* his friendship with Olivier to secure temporary shelter. The "fine thing about our friendship so far," Bernard reflects, "has been that we have never made any use of one another" (002-007). The moment of crisis transforms friendship from a disinterested ideal into a practical necessity, and the novel asks whether friendship can survive this transformation.

Friendship is also a performance. Olivier "took great pains not to show that he liked being with" Bernard; "sometimes he would even pretend not to see him" (002-009). This theatrical concealment of feeling — so at odds with the novel's valorization of [[sincerity]] — suggests that friendship, particularly between adolescent males in the homosocial environment of the lycée, is constrained by codes of emotional display that make authentic connection difficult.

## Manifestations in the Text

### Key Scenes

The night scene in Olivier's room (004-020, 004-021, 004-037) is the novel's most concentrated exploration of friendship's intensities. Bernard, having left home, comes to Olivier. Olivier "admires his friend with immense fervour" but is "stabbed with a pang of terror" at Bernard's resolve (004-020). When Olivier begins a sentence — "Bernard, all the same, you're not thinking of..." — he cannot finish it (004-021). The ellipsis marks the limit of what friendship can say aloud.

> "There was no need to say more for Bernard to understand. He pressed up against his friend." (004-037) — Physical proximity substitutes for speech; the body communicates what language cannot.

### Symbolic Expressions

The novel repeatedly stages friendship in spatial terms: proximity and distance, the shared room, the walk together. The Luxembourg Gardens where Bernard and Olivier usually meet (002-008) is a neutral territory outside both home and school — friendship's own geography. The moonlight scene in the shared bedroom, where Bernard picks up a book (Tocqueville) and drops it, waking Olivier (004-037), renders friendship as the play of attention and accident in the dark.

## Literary and Philosophical Context

Gide's treatment of male friendship draws on the classical tradition of *philia* — the intense bond celebrated in Greek thought as the highest form of love — filtered through the homosocial culture of the French lycée and the codes of the Parisian bourgeoisie. The friendship between Bernard and Olivier is also shaped by Gide's reading of [[Fyodor Dostoevsky|Dostoevsky]], whose young men likewise experience friendship as an existential rather than merely social bond, and of [[Oscar Wilde]], whose aesthetics of male intimacy hover behind the novel's charged silences.

The friendship between Édouard and Laura models a different possibility: friendship that survives the failure of romantic love. Édouard's care for Laura during her crisis of illegitimate pregnancy (009-003) demonstrates friendship as the form of love least likely to be counterfeit — because it demands nothing in return.
