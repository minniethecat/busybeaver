# Edouard Loses His Journal

At the Gare Saint-Lazare, Edouard's suitcase — containing his private journal with Laura's letter and his literary notes — goes missing. Bernard Profitendieu, penniless and desperate, takes it, reads the contents, and gains intimate knowledge of Edouard's inner life.

## Summary

Arriving at the Gare Saint-Lazare from Le Havre, Edouard checks his suitcase at the station consignment office. Bernard, who has been wandering Paris without money after leaving home, sees the suitcase and, in a moment of desperate opportunism, claims it. He discovers it contains not valuables but Edouard's journal — an intimate record of the novelist's thoughts, including Laura's letter and the embryonic plan for a novel called _The Counterfeiters_. [PN=011-007] [PN=011-008] [PN=015-001]

## Context

### Narrative Position

This event occurs in **Part I, Chapters 11-12**, immediately after Edouard's return to Paris. Bernard's theft of the suitcase and his subsequent reading of the journal form a crucial narrative hinge, connecting Bernard's story of escape with Edouard's literary and emotional world. The journal-reading extends into Chapter 15. [PN=011-007] [PN=015-001]

### Preceding Events

Edouard has returned from England, carrying Laura's letter and his journal in his suitcase. Bernard has left home, spent the night on the quays, and discovered he has no money whatsoever — not even the ten centimes needed to retrieve his own belongings from the consignment desk. [PN=007-005] [PN=009-003] [PN=011-007]

## Detailed Account

At the consignment desk, Bernard faces a moment of crisis. The attendant demands ten centimes for retrieving his belongings, but Bernard "had not a single sou left." He sees Edouard's suitcase on the counter — Edouard has momentarily stepped away. In a split-second decision, aided by "the demon," Bernard claims Edouard's suitcase as his own, slips the attendant the tip, and escapes to a nearby hotel in the Rue d'Amsterdam. [PN=011-007] [PN=011-008]

In his hotel room, Bernard opens the suitcase and is disappointed to find no money — only books, papers, and "a notebook filled with handwriting." This is Edouard's journal. Bernard begins to read, and the journal reveals Edouard's intimate thoughts: his feelings for Laura, his rivalry with Passavant, his relationship with Olivier, and most intriguingly, the plan for a novel. [PN=011-010] [PN=011-012]

> "Now, suitcase, a word with you!... A morning coat, certainly not of the latest cut; a few letters; a notebook..." [PN=011-012]

Bernard reads on through the night, consuming Edouard's entries about Laura, his doubts about his novel, and his arrival in Paris. By reading the journal, Bernard gains a privileged intimacy with a man he has never met — including knowledge of Laura's situation, which will shape his conduct when he later encounters her at Saas-Fée. [PN=015-001] [PN=013-132]

## Characters Involved

- **Edouard** — The novelist who loses his journal, a loss that is both a violation of privacy and an unwilling act of self-exposure
- **Bernard Profitendieu** — The runaway who steals the suitcase and reads the journal, gaining knowledge that transforms his understanding of the adult world
- **Laura Douviers** — Present through her letter slipped between the journal's pages; her private distress is now exposed to a stranger [PN=009-025]

## Consequences

### Immediate Aftermath

Bernard, having read the journal, feels a strange intimacy with Edouard and decides to seek him out. He goes to the Vedel-Azaïs boarding school where Laura's family lives, and later becomes Edouard's secretary and traveling companion to Saas-Fée. The theft thus leads directly to Bernard's entry into Edouard's world. [PN=015-037] [PN=026-018]

Edouard is distraught at losing his journal — "There was nothing in it I cared about but my journal. But I cared about that too much" — and is deeply unsettled by the violation. The loss represents an unwilled self-exposure that parallels the novel's broader concern with what is genuine and what is counterfeit in human relationships. [PN=019-001]

### Narrative Ripple Effects

The stolen journal functions as the novel's central unifying device. Bernard's reading of it gives him — and the reader — access to Edouard's inner world, effectively making the journal a novel-within-the-novel. Bernard's subsequent behavior toward Laura and Edouard is shaped by the knowledge he should not possess, creating dramatic irony throughout the Saas-Fée section. The theft also embodies the novel's preoccupation with violated privacy and uninvited intimacy. [PN=015-002]

## Thematic Significance

The theft and reading of the journal dramatize the novel's concern with **voyeurism, privacy, and the ethics of knowledge**. Bernard's violation of Edouard's privacy parallels Gide's own violation of his characters' — and perhaps his readers' — expectations. The journal as stolen object also represents the novel's fascination with the **boundary between public and private**, a boundary that Gide's narrative method constantly erodes. By reading the journal, Bernard becomes a kind of novelist himself, interpreting Edouard's life before having met him.

## Literary Technique

Gide uses the stolen journal as a sophisticated **layering device**. The reader reads Gide's novel, which contains Edouard's journal, which Bernard reads, which contains Laura's letter and the plan for _The Counterfeiters_. This nesting of texts — _mise en abyme_ — creates endless reflections of the novel's central concerns. The suitcase itself becomes a symbol of narrative containment: a locked box that, once opened, releases stories that spill beyond anyone's control.
