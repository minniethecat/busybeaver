# Tuileries Gardens

The Tuileries Gardens appear in The Counterfeiters at a single, crucial moment — as the destination of Vincent's post-coital wandering, a formal space whose geometric order contrasts with his moral chaos.

## Location

The Tuileries Gardens (Jardin des Tuileries) lie between the Louvre and the Place de la Concorde in the 1st arrondissement of Paris. In the novel, Vincent reaches them after walking from the Right Bank quays, crossing the Seine, and continuing "till he reached the part of the Tuileries which lies outside the railings" (006-100). The gardens' position on the Right Bank places them in the world of Passavant, fashion, and worldly success.

## Description in the Novel

Gide's Tuileries scene is one of the novel's most concentrated passages of physical description. Vincent walks through the gardens in a state of moral and physical disorientation, pressing a wet handkerchief to his forehead — an image of shame, fever, and attempted self-comfort. The gardens are nearly empty; it is the early morning after his night with Lilian. The night itself had begun in Lilian's bedroom: "Lilian half sat up and put the tips" of her fingers to her lips (006-001). Later, "Lilian gave a roar of delight" (006-081) at Robert's return, and "Lilian kissed him on the forehead" (006-082) — the closed, erotic world of the bedroom that Vincent has just left contrasts with the open, formal space of the Tuileries. The "part of the Tuileries which lies outside the railings" (006-100) suggests a liminal zone — the gardens' periphery rather than their formal center, matching Vincent's own peripheral moral state.

The novel's climate is also established through this passage: "How hot it is going to be in Paris!" (006-101), Bernard's observation, is juxtaposed with Vincent's morning-after walk, connecting the two brothers through the shared weather of the city.

## Narrative Function

### Scenes Set Here

Vincent's post-coital walk is the gardens' only major scene. After the car drops him off, he walks along the quay, crosses the Seine, and enters the Tuileries in a daze (006-100). He presses a wet handkerchief to his forehead — a gesture that simultaneously suggests physical discomfort (the heat, the hangover) and moral shame (the attempt to cool the fever of guilt).

### Symbolic Significance

The Tuileries Gardens, with their rigidly formal French design, represent the rational order that Vincent has violated. His moral disorientation — he has slept with his friend's mistress, been drawn into the Passavant circle, and begun the slide that will end in madness — is set against the garden's Cartesian geometry. The wet handkerchief is the novel's most condensed image of attempted moral purification: Vincent tries to wash away his sin with a damp cloth.

## Associated Characters

Vincent Molinier is the gardens' principal inhabitant in the novel. He arrives in a state of moral crisis (006-100). The night he has just left behind — Lilian's bedroom with Robert de Passavant (006-001, 006-081, 006-082) — is the garden's implicit antithesis: enclosed, erotic, morally compromised. Bernard is implicitly connected through the shared reference to the Paris heat (006-101). The novel's parallel structure juxtaposes the two brothers' crises — Bernard's ethical awakening, Vincent's moral collapse.

## Real-World Context

The Tuileries Gardens were created by Catherine de' Medici in 1564 and redesigned by André Le Nôtre in the 17th century. In the 1920s, they were a popular public park in the heart of Paris, adjacent to the Louvre and the fashion houses of the Rue de Rivoli. Gide's use of this formal, central Parisian space for Vincent's scene of moral chaos exploits the contrast between classical order and modern disorder.
