# The Schoolboy Counterfeit Coin Ring

George Molinier and his friends from the Vedel-Azaïs boarding school circulate counterfeit ten-franc pieces, turning a childish prank into a criminal enterprise that will attract the attention of examining magistrate Albéric Profitendieu.

## Summary

A group of schoolboys — including George Molinier (Olivier's younger brother) and several of his classmates — obtain counterfeit coins and begin passing them at local shops. A tobacco shop becomes the site of a key transaction. When the shopkeeper detects the false coins, the trail leads back to the boarding school, eventually drawing the attention of examining magistrate Profitendieu, whose investigation uncovers a network that extends beyond mere schoolboy mischief. ## Context

### Narrative Position

This event develops across **Part III, Chapters 16-17**, though references to the counterfeit coins emerge earlier. The schoolboy coin ring is the novel's most literal manifestation of its central metaphor, transforming the abstract "counterfeit" into a concrete criminal investigation. [PN=041-024] [PN=041-042]

### Preceding Events

The counterfeit coin motif has been prepared by Edouard's literary discussion at Saas-Fée (see [[the-counterfeit-coin-discussion]]) and by the novel's ongoing concern with false appearances. George Molinier has been introduced as Olivier's younger brother, a boy of thirteen who has fallen under the influence of older, more cynical schoolmates. Profitendieu, having lost Bernard, has thrown himself into his work as examining magistrate. [PN=023-063] [PN=041-024]

## Detailed Account

The counterfeit coin ring operates through the Vedel-Azaïs boarding school, where a group of boys obtain false ten-franc pieces and pass them at shops in the neighborhood. George Molinier, at thirteen, is one of the younger participants, drawn in by older boys whose cynicism exceeds their years. [PN=041-024]

A critical transaction occurs at a tobacco shop. The schoolboys pass a counterfeit coin there, unaware that the shopkeeper has been warned. The event is recorded with Gide's characteristic restraint — the narrative does not dwell on the mechanics of the crime but on its moral implications. [PN=033-034] [PN=042-008]

The investigation falls to Albéric Profitendieu, the examining magistrate and Bernard's legal father. His inquiry reveals that the counterfeit coins have been "put into circulation for some time past" — the ring is more extensive than a few schoolboy pranks. The investigation threatens to expose not only the boys but also the adults who may have supplied them. [PN=041-024]

## Characters Involved

- **George Molinier** — Olivier's thirteen-year-old brother, the youngest participant in the coin ring, whose innocence is being actively corrupted
- **Albéric Profitendieu** — The examining magistrate whose investigation connects the schoolboy prank to a wider criminal network
- **The schoolboy gang** — George's classmates at the Vedel-Azaïs school, corrupted by boredom and the thrill of transgression
- **The tobacco shopkeeper** — The merchant who detects the counterfeit coins and reports them to the authorities

## Consequences

### Immediate Aftermath

Profitendieu's investigation proceeds alongside — and intersects with — the personal dramas of the novel's characters. The magistrate's pursuit of the counterfeiters brings him into contact with the Vedel-Azaïs world and eventually with Edouard. The investigation threatens to expose George's involvement and bring scandal upon the Molinier family. [PN=041-042]

### Narrative Ripple Effects

The literal counterfeit coin ring gives concrete form to the novel's central metaphor, showing how false values circulate through society — from the schoolboy's counterfeit ten-franc piece to the adult world's counterfeit relationships. The investigation also brings Profitendieu back into the novel's central action, connecting the magistrate's world of law and order with Edouard's world of art and moral ambiguity. The fact that Bernard's legal father is investigating the crime ring that entangles Edouard's nephew (George) weaves the novel's disparate threads into a tighter pattern. [PN=041-032]

## Thematic Significance

The schoolboy coin ring literalizes the novel's metaphor of **circulating falsity**. Counterfeit coins, like counterfeit lives, pass from hand to hand, corrupting everyone who touches them. The schoolboys' crime is particularly resonant because they are still young enough to be genuinely innocent — their corruption represents the loss of something real, not the perfection of something already false. George's participation in the ring parallels Vincent's moral corruption and Passavant's literary charlatanism, suggesting that counterfeiting is not a single act but a social contagion.

## Literary Technique

Gide handles the coin ring elliptically, never providing a full account of its operations. This narrative restraint mirrors the nature of the crime itself — counterfeit coins are designed not to be noticed, and the conspiracy flourishes in the gaps of official knowledge. The investigation scenes are filtered through Profitendieu's perspective, maintaining the novel's commitment to multiple, partial viewpoints rather than omniscient revelation.
