# Coming of Age

The process by which the young characters of [[André Gide]]'s _The Counterfeiters_ move from innocence to experience — not through the acquisition of adult certainties but through the acceptance of uncertainty, moral complexity, and the provisional nature of all identity.

## Definition

Coming of age in Gide's novel is not the traditional *Bildungsroman* trajectory of finding one's place in society. It is instead a process of *unlearning* — of shedding the inherited assumptions of family, class, and religion to discover what, if anything, remains authentically one's own. For [[Bernard Profitendieu]], this means discovering his [[illegitimacy]] and leaving home. For [[Olivier Molinier]], it means navigating his attraction to [[Édouard]] and his entanglement with the [[Comte de Passavant]]. For [[Laura Douviers]], it means facing the consequences of desire outside marriage. Coming of age in this novel is always a crisis — and a crisis that may not resolve.

## Role in The Counterfeiters

### Thematic Significance

The novel opens on the threshold of Bernard's *bachot* — the French baccalaureate examination that marks the official passage from adolescence to adulthood (002-001). But Gide immediately subverts the ritual: Bernard abandons his studies, jettisons his family, and declares his independence. The *bachot* — a test of received knowledge — is replaced by the test of lived experience. Coming of age properly understood is not about passing exams but about asking the questions the exams cannot contain.

The novel's multi-plot structure — following Bernard, Olivier, Vincent, Laura, and the younger boys at the [[Azaïs Pension]] — presents coming of age not as a single arc but as a spectrum of possibilities. Each character must navigate the same fundamental challenges (identity, desire, moral choice) but reaches different destinations, or none at all.

### Character Associations

| Character | Coming-of-Age Arc | Outcome |
|-----------|-------------------|---------|
| **Bernard** | Discovers illegitimacy → leaves home → returns with new self-knowledge | The closest the novel comes to a successful *Bildungsroman* |
| **Olivier** | Shy, repressed → experiences passion → attempts suicide → recovers | Coming of age as crisis survived |
| **Vincent** | Medical student → gambler → murderer in Africa | Coming of age as moral disintegration |
| **Laura** | Respectable wife → adulterous lover → single mother | Coming of age as the cost of desire |
| **George** | Innocent boy → participant in counterfeit coin ring → moral awakening | Coming of age through complicity and shame |

## Manifestations in the Text

### Key Scenes

Bernard's night in the Luxembourg Gardens after discovering his bastardy (003-039) marks the novel's archetypal coming-of-age moment: the protagonist alone, stripped of his past, facing an undefined future. His monologue (007-006) is a coming-of-age in miniature — moving from filial resentment to philosophical reflection, from "my mother's love" to "Theseus must have been about my age when he lifted the stone." The mythological reference signals Bernard's transformation from schoolboy to protagonist of his own story.

> "Seat your youth on that bench, Bernard. What a beautiful morning! There really are days when the sun seems to be kissing the earth. If I could get rid of myself for a little, there's not a doubt but I should write poetry." (007-006)

The coming-of-age moment is also a moment of aesthetic awakening — the discovery that life, properly seen, is material for art.

### Symbolic Expressions

The *bachot* examination (002-001, 004-098) is the novel's primary symbol of official coming-of-age — and Gide's treatment of it is ironic. Bernard, who abandons his studies, grows more than those who remain in school. The open window, the public bench, the streets of Paris: all are spaces of passage, thresholds between the old life and the new. The suitcase — stolen from [[Édouard]], containing his journal — represents the portable identity of the young person in transit.

## Literary and Philosophical Context

Gide's treatment of coming of age draws on the French *roman d'apprentissage* tradition — Stendhal's *The Red and the Black*, Flaubert's *Sentimental Education* — but rejects their teleological structure. There is no final wisdom, no settled identity, no marriage plot resolution. Gide's model is closer to Dostoevsky's adolescents, who oscillate between extremes rather than progressing toward a stable self. The influence of [[Friedrich Nietzsche|Nietzsche]] is also evident: coming of age is self-overcoming, the perpetual becoming that Nietzsche called the "will to power."

The novel's refusal of closure — its open ending, its unresolved plots — is itself a formal statement about coming of age: it never ends. "In irresolution lies the secret of not growing old," Gide writes (as [[Édouard]]) toward the novel's end. The mature self, in Gide's vision, is not the self that has arrived but the self that continues to question.
