# The Fatal Pistol

Boris's suicide at the Pension Vedel-Azaïs is the novel's culminating tragedy — an event prepared by a long chain of foreshadowing, orchestrated by a conspiracy of schoolboys, and rendered with a narrative restraint that intensifies rather than diminishes its horror.

## Summary

Throughout the novel, the aged music master La Pérouse keeps a pair of pistols — a gift from his deceased brother — by his bedside. He has twice contemplated suicide with them and twice lacked the courage to fire. When he takes a position at the Pension Azaïs, the pistols come with him. The schoolboys Ghéridanisol, George Molinier, and Philippe Adamanti discover the weapon and devise a cruel game: they will stage a mock execution of the younger boy Boris, whom they have bullied into submission. The pistol, which they believe to be unloaded, still contains the cartridge La Pérouse had inserted. During the staged "execution," Boris raises the pistol to his temple and fires — and this time, it is not a blank (044-001, 045-001).

## Context

### Narrative Position

The suicide occurs in Part III, Chapter XIX, during the evening preparation period at the pension. It is the climactic event toward which the novel's schoolboy subplot has been building, intersecting with the darker currents of La Pérouse's despair.

### Preceding Events

The pistol first appears in Part II, when La Pérouse shows Edouard the loaded weapon and confesses his failed suicide attempt: "I put the pistol to my forehead. I held it for a long time against my temple. And I didn't fire. I couldn't... I hadn't the courage to fire" (025-001). The pistol reappears when La Pérouse moves to the pension, where the boys discover it during "an indiscreet investigation of his room" (043-001).

In the school, La Pérouse proves incapable of maintaining discipline. His indulgence is mistaken for weakness, and the boys — led by Ghéridanisol — subject him to systematic mockery. Boris, a fragile Polish boy still mourning the death of his friend Bronja, becomes the target of their cruelty after La Pérouse's favored treatment of him fuels their resentment.

## Detailed Account

Ghéridanisol discovers that La Pérouse's pistol still contains the cartridge the old man had loaded but never fired. Concealing this knowledge from the others, he persuades George and Philippe that the weapon is unloaded and that their "game" — a mock execution intended to frighten Boris — will be harmless.

On the appointed evening, during preparation, Ghéridanisol tells Boris: "the time has come" (044-001). Boris, broken by weeks of bullying, walks to the appointed spot "slowly, like an automaton — or rather like a somnambulist" (044-001). He raises the pistol to his temple.

"At first La Pérouse, who was leaning over, did not see. But he suddenly recognized the pistol: Boris had just raised it to his temple. La Pérouse understood and immediately turned icy cold as if the blood were freezing in his veins" (044-001).

The shot is real. Boris falls dead.

In the chaos that follows, George Molinier leaps over his bench and manages to hide the pistol, passing it surreptitiously to Ghéridanisol, who returns it to La Pérouse's room. The police later find the pistol in its case — an oversight that would have exonerated the boys had Ghéridanisol remembered to remove the empty cartridge (045-001).

## Consequences

### Immediate Aftermath

Boris's body is carried to a bed. Ghéridanisol, seized with a visible nervous trembling, is mistaken for showing grief and believed when he protests his innocence. Philippe's note, found under a bench, further supports the pretense that the boys thought the weapon was harmless. George alone knows the full truth.

### Narrative Ripple Effects

The tragedy shatters the Pension Azaïs. "Four families have already removed their children" (045-001). Azaïs is forced to close the school, at least temporarily, and Rachel fears financial ruin. Pauline Molinier withdraws George from the school. Armand Vedel, despite his cynical detachment, offers to help with the schoolwork. Even Olivier is shaken. The reverberations spread through the entire network of characters.

Edouard, the novelist within the novel, reflects on the event with discomfort: "I should not like to put forward any fact which was not accounted for by a sufficiency of motive. And for that reason I shall not make use of little Boris's suicide for my _Counterfeiters_" (045-001). This meta-narrative commentary draws attention to the very artifice of fiction — the demand that events be motivated — while acknowledging the brute, unmotivated reality of certain tragedies.

## Thematic Significance

The fatal pistol embodies several of the novel's central preoccupations. La Pérouse's loaded pistol — prepared for a suicide he could not carry out — becomes a Chekhovian object: once introduced, it must be fired. The transfer of the act from the despairing old man to the innocent child is one of Gide's darkest ironies. The event also dramatizes the novel's concern with counterfeit and authenticity: the boys stage a "pretend execution" that becomes horrifyingly real, and afterward they counterfeit innocence with remarkable success.

## Literary Technique

Gide's narration of the suicide is notable for its restraint and precision. The scene is rendered almost clinically, with the narrator withholding emotional commentary. The effect is to make the event more disturbing, not less — the reader is forced to confront the horror without the buffer of narrative sympathy. The subsequent paragraphs, in which the boys' cover-up is described in matter-of-fact detail, compound the moral shock.
