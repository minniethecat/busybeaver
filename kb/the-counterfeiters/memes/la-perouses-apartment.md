# La Pérouse's Apartment

La Pérouse's apartment on the Faubourg St. Honoré is the novel's most vivid portrait of a domestic hell — a space of prolonged marital resentment, faded artistic promise, and the accumulated bitterness of a life lived in disappointment.

## Location

The apartment is situated on the Faubourg St. Honoré, a fashionable street in the 8th arrondissement of Paris, "in the part of the Faubourg St. Honoré which makes a little square" (014-001). It is on the entresol — the mezzanine floor between the ground floor and the first story, a detail that suggests marginal status within a grand building.

## Description in the Novel

Gide's description of the apartment emphasizes its atmosphere of decay and confinement. Edouard rings the bell, and "La Pérouse opened the door. He was in his shirtsleeves" (014-001) — a detail that conveys the informality of a man who has ceased to care about appearances. The apartment is dominated by the offstage presence of Madame de La Pérouse, whose voice is never heard directly but whose judgments fill the old man's discourse.

"Madame de La Pérouse's maidservant has let the stove go out" (014-005). "Madame de La Pérouse won't understand" (014-018). "Madame de La Pérouse is always reproaching me" (014-022). The wife is invoked as an accusatory presence, a domestic tyrant whose power is expressed through the neglect of the household and the cruelty of her criticisms.

The apartment is also the site of La Pérouse's music lessons — the one remaining connection to his former life as a musician. The piano and the harmonium that he plays at the Rue Madame chapel (013-018) link the apartment to his artistic past.

## Narrative Function

### Scenes Set Here

Edouard visits La Pérouse in his apartment, and the old man recounts the tragedy of his marriage and the loss of his son (014-001–014-048). La Pérouse confesses that he has never known physical love, that his wife despises him, and that his life has been a waste. The apartment is "a long journey away from the Faubourg St. Honoré" (030-005) — a distance measured not in kilometers but in psychological alienation from the world of the living.

### Symbolic Significance

The apartment represents the terminal point of a life lived according to bourgeois conventions — marriage without love, art without achievement, old age without dignity. It is the novel's image of what happens when the counterfeit becomes permanent. La Pérouse's confession to Edouard is the novel's most unsparing portrait of a life lived in bad faith.

## Associated Characters

La Pérouse is the apartment's prisoner. Madame de La Pérouse is its unseen tyrant. Edouard is the rare visitor who hears La Pérouse's confession (014-001). Madame de La Pérouse's maidservant appears as a minor agent of domestic neglect (014-005).

## Real-World Context

The Faubourg St. Honoré was one of Paris's most aristocratic streets in the 18th century and remained a prestigious address in the 1920s. The contrast between the street's grandeur and the shabby entresol apartment reflects the fallen circumstances of the La Pérouses.
