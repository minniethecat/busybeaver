# Edouard's Reading of His Novel

During the stay at Saas-Fée, the novelist Edouard reads portions of his work-in-progress — also titled *The Counterfeiters* — to Bernard and Laura, creating a mirror-within-the-novel that dramatizes Gide's own compositional problems and aesthetic theories.

## Summary

At the Swiss pension in Saas-Fée, Edouard takes the unusual step of reading aloud from his unfinished novel to his traveling companions, Bernard and Laura. The reading becomes an occasion for Edouard to articulate his theory of the novel — his rejection of conventional plot, his desire to capture the "struggle between what reality offers him and what he himself desires to make of it." Bernard, characteristically, challenges Edouard's approach, arguing that a novel should begin from facts rather than ideas (023-001, 024-001).

## Context

### Narrative Position

The reading occurs in Part II (Saas-Fée), the novel's central interlude away from Paris. This section is marked by introspection, intellectual debate, and the temporary suspension of plot — making it the ideal setting for the novel's most extended meta-fictional passage.

### Preceding Events

Edouard, Bernard, and Laura have arrived in Saas-Fée after leaving Paris. The journey has established the triangular tension among them — Edouard's unspoken feelings, Laura's complicated history, Bernard's restless independence. The reading emerges from the peculiar intimacy of their shared isolation (023-001).

## Detailed Account

Edouard's reading is less a performance than an explanation. He reads not to entertain but to clarify — to himself as much as to his audience. His description of his novel-in-progress becomes the novel's most explicit statement of Gide's own aesthetic project: a novel that would include everything, that would refuse the simplifications of conventional plot, that would dramatize the very act of composition.

Bernard's response is crucial. He interrupts Edouard's theoretical exposition with practical impatience: "If I were writing *The Counterfeiters* I should begin by showing the counterfeit coin — the little ten-franc piece you were speaking of just now" (023-001). The interruption exposes the gap between Edouard's abstract aspirations and the concrete reality of fiction-making — a gap that is the novel's central compositional problem.

## Consequences

### Immediate Aftermath

The reading crystallizes the intellectual dynamic among the three characters. Bernard's challenge to Edouard's method marks the beginning of his intellectual independence — he is no longer simply Edouard's secretary and protégé but an interlocutor with views of his own.

### Narrative Ripple Effects

The meta-fictional frame established in this scene — Edouard writing a novel called *The Counterfeiters* within the novel called *The Counterfeiters* — becomes the novel's master trope. Every subsequent event can be read as both happening and as potential material for Edouard's novel, collapsing the distance between experience and representation.

## Thematic Significance

The reading scene is the novel's most concentrated exploration of the relationship between art and life. Edouard's theory — that the novel should dramatize the "struggle between reality and the mind's attempt to shape it" — is both the novel's aesthetic manifesto and its implicit self-critique. Bernard's interruption suggests that this struggle cannot be resolved in theory alone.

## Literary Technique

Gide uses the *mise en abyme* — the mirror within the mirror — to create infinite regress. Edouard reading his novel within Gide's novel makes the act of reading and interpretation itself thematic. The scene invites the reader to consider their own position: are they, like Laura, silent auditors? Or like Bernard, impatient challengers demanding concrete demonstration?
