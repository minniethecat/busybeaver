# Monsieur Molinier

The father of [[Olivier Molinier|Olivier]], [[George Molinier|George]], and [[Vincent Molinier]], whose presence in the novel is indirect — mediated through his wife [[Pauline Molinier]] and his brother [[Edouard]] — and whose paternal role exemplifies the bourgeois authority against which Gide's young characters define themselves.

## Role in the Novel

Monsieur Molinier is a background figure whose function is structural rather than dramatic. He is the absent father whose authority is invoked but never directly depicted — the figure of paternal law from which his sons must distinguish themselves. His absence from the narrative foreground reflects the novel's focus on the younger generation's struggle for self-definition against inherited frameworks.

## Biography

Monsieur Molinier is the husband of Pauline, the half-brother of Edouard, and the father of Olivier, George, and Vincent. Edouard's visits to Pauline's household provide glimpses of the family dynamic: "The two others were never there when I went to see Pauline; the one who was now before me was certainly the youngest" (PN=012-031), referring to the sons he had not yet met. Pauline's concern for Vincent's recovery and her management of the household suggest a family in which the mother is the active presence and the father's authority is assumed rather than exercised.

## Character Analysis

The novel's treatment of Monsieur Molinier exemplifies Gide's broader critique of bourgeois fatherhood — the father as source of constraint rather than guidance, his authority maintained through absence and expectation rather than through engaged relationship. His three sons each negotiate this paternal inheritance differently: Vincent through ambition and catastrophe, Olivier through emotional dependence on Edouard, and George through outright rebellion.
