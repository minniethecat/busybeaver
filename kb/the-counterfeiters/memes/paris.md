# Paris

The principal setting of _The Counterfeiters_, where the novel's bourgeois families, schoolboys, artists, and intellectuals live and circulate. [[paris]] in the novel is both a concrete geographic reality — streets, gardens, apartments — and a symbolic space representing the world of adult moral compromise from which characters like [[Bernard Profitendieu]] seek liberation. It is the city of [[counterfeit-coin|counterfeit values]], contrasted with the alpine clarity of [[saas-fee]].

## Location

Paris, the capital of France, provides the novel's geographic center of gravity. The action is concentrated in the Left Bank neighborhoods around the [[luxembourg-gardens]], the Faubourg St. Honoré, and the Sorbonne quarter. The city is presented as a network of interconnected domestic and public spaces: apartments, gardens, schools, chapels, and salons (001-001). Paris in the 1920s was the center of French intellectual and literary life, and Gide's characters move through a recognizably real cityscape.

## Description in the Novel

Gide depicts Paris not through sustained descriptive passages but through the accumulated detail of his characters' movements. The city appears through precise geographic references: the [[luxembourg-gardens]] where schoolboys gather (002-008), the Rue Madame where [[lauras-wedding|Laura's wedding]] takes place, the Faubourg St. Honoré where [[La Pérouse]] lives, the Sorbonne quadrangle where [[Olivier Molinier]] attends lectures.

The novel opens with [[Bernard Profitendieu]] leaving his family home near the Luxembourg — the opening gesture of the novel is a departure, an escape from the bourgeois household into the wider city. In Part I, Paris is a space of constraint from which characters seek to flee; in Part III, after the Swiss interlude in [[saas-fee]], it becomes the arena where the novel's moral tensions are resolved.

> "How hot it is going to be in Paris! It is time to return to Bernard." (006-101, 009-001) — Edouard, on the train approaching the city.

### Symbolic Significance

Paris represents the world of adult society — its families, institutions, and moral codes. It is the city of fathers (Profitendieu, Molinier, [[La Pérouse]]) against which the sons ([[Bernard Profitendieu|Bernard]], [[Olivier Molinier|Olivier]], [[Vincent Molinier|Vincent]]) rebel. The city's bourgeois respectability is the [[counterfeit-coin|counterfeit]] the novel anatomizes.

> The novel's geography is structured as a moral opposition: Paris is heat, constraint, and compromise; [[saas-fee]] is altitude, clarity, and honesty.

When characters leave Paris for [[saas-fee]] in Part II, they enter a space of relative moral clarity; their return to Paris in Part III marks their re-entry into the world of compromise, but now with greater self-knowledge. This geographic rhythm — departure, retreat, return — mirrors the novel's deeper structure of moral education through experience.

## Associated Characters

Virtually all characters are associated with Paris: [[Bernard Profitendieu]] grows up near the Luxembourg (002-001); the Molinier family lives nearby (003-001); the Vedel-Azaïs family runs their boarding school in the city (006-058); [[Comte de Passavant|Robert de Passavant]] lives in a fashionable apartment (005-001); [[La Pérouse]] lives in the Faubourg St. Honoré (014-001); [[Edouard]] keeps his studio in Paris (037-011). Edouard arrives in Paris from Le Havre (004-099) and [[Laura Douviers|Laura]] comes seeking his help (005-004, 015-006).

## Real-World Context

In the 1920s, Paris was the capital of European modernism — home to Gide, Proust, Valéry, and the expatriate writers of the Lost Generation. Gide's choice of real Parisian locations (the Luxembourg, the Sorbonne, the Faubourg St. Honoré) grounds his fiction in a specific social geography. The Luxembourg Gardens area, in particular, was associated with the Protestant bourgeoisie and intellectual life, both of which are central to the novel's concerns.
