# Rue Madame Chapel

The little Protestant chapel on the Rue Madame is the novel's primary sacred space — the site of Laura's wedding and the setting where the religious character of the Vedel-Azaïs world is most fully displayed.

## Location

The chapel is located on the Rue Madame in the 6th arrondissement of Paris, near the Luxembourg Gardens and the Vedel boarding school. It is described as "the little chapel in Rue Madame, to which I have not been for a long time past" (013-018) — a small, intimate place of Protestant worship in a predominantly Catholic city.

## Description in the Novel

The wedding scene provides the chapel's only sustained description. "The whole of the Vedel-Azaïs families were present" (013-018), filling the small space with the extended clan. Old La Pérouse "was at the harmonium" (013-018), providing music for the ceremony. The chapel's modest scale and Protestant simplicity contrast with Catholic grandeur — it is a space of domestic piety rather than institutional majesty.

Edouard notes that he has not been to the chapel in a long time (013-018), suggesting he has drifted from the religious observance of his youth. The wedding brings him back into this world, where he encounters old La Pérouse at the sacristy door (013-023) and observes the entire Vedel-Azaïs network assembled in one place.

## Narrative Function

### Scenes Set Here

Laura's wedding to Félix Douviers takes place in the chapel, with the full Vedel-Azaïs family in attendance (013-018). La Pérouse plays the harmonium, creating the musical atmosphere. Edouard observes the ceremony from the gallery, a spectator rather than a participant (013-018). After the service, Edouard encounters La Pérouse at the vestry door (013-023), which leads to the conversation that prompts his visit to the old man's apartment.

### Symbolic Significance

The Rue Madame chapel is the novel's emblem of institutional Protestantism — modest, familial, and morally earnest. The wedding that takes place here is a counterfeit in its own way: Laura does not love Félix and is carrying another man's child. The chapel thus becomes the ironic setting for the enactment of a union that conceals its own moral truth.

## Associated Characters

Laura Vedel-Douviers is the bride. Félix Douviers is the groom. The Vedel-Azaïs family fills the chapel (013-018). Pastor Prosper Vedel presumably officiates. La Pérouse plays the harmonium (013-018). Edouard attends as an observer and former lover of Laura's. Olivier, Armand, Sarah, and the younger generation are in the gallery.

## Real-World Context

The Rue Madame is a real street in the 6th arrondissement, historically associated with Protestant institutions in Paris. In the 1920s, the area around the Luxembourg Gardens had a significant Protestant population, including churches, schools, and charitable institutions. Gide was raised Protestant himself and knew this world intimately.
