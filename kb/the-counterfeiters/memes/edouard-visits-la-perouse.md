# Edouard Visits La Pérouse

Edouard calls on the aged music-master La Pérouse at his modest entresol apartment in the Faubourg St. Honoré — a visit that reveals a life of shattered dreams, marital misery, and the desperate hope of caring for a child in his old age.

## Summary

Edouard, prompted by Laura's family to check on the elderly music-master, visits La Pérouse at his home on the Faubourg St. Honoré. The old man lives in an "entresol" — a mezzanine apartment, dark and cramped — with his embittered wife. The visit, ostensibly a courtesy call, becomes the occasion for La Pérouse's confession about his dead son (see [[la-perouses-confession]]) and reveals the poverty, disappointment, and spiritual exhaustion of his long life. [PN=014-001] [PN=014-005] [PN=015-083]

## Context

### Narrative Position

This event is the setting for **Part I, Chapters 14-15**, framed by Edouard's journal entries. The visit follows La Pérouse's melancholy appearance at Laura's wedding and initiates the subplot concerning La Pérouse's hope of caring for Boris in his old age. [PN=013-018] [PN=014-001]

### Preceding Events

At Laura's wedding, Edouard was struck by La Pérouse's sadness while playing the harmonium. Laura's family asked Edouard to visit the old man, who has been a fixture in their world for decades — he taught piano to Laura's mother and has remained connected to the family even as his circumstances have declined. Edouard agreed to the visit out of a mixture of duty and novelist's curiosity. [PN=013-018] [PN=013-023]

## Detailed Account

La Pérouse lives in an entresol apartment in that part of the Faubourg St. Honoré "which makes a little pocket of shadow behind the great modern buildings." The location itself is symbolic — old Paris, hidden away behind the new boulevards, a pocket of the past surviving in the margins of the modern city. [PN=014-001]

Edouard rings the bell and La Pérouse opens the door himself, "in his shirt-sleeves" — a detail that immediately establishes the informality and vulnerability of the old man's existence. The apartment is modest, the furnishings worn. Madame de La Pérouse's maid, whose "store of wine" the old woman has supposedly consumed, is referenced in a fragment of overheard domestic conflict. [PN=014-001] [PN=014-005]

The visit is structured as a gradual revelation. La Pérouse speaks in fragments, interrupted by calls from his wife in the next room. The conversation moves from pleasantries to deeper disclosures: his marriage ("I loved her with innocence — an innocence she was quick to abuse"), his son's death, and his failing religious faith. [PN=014-049] [PN=014-048]

The material conditions of the La Pérouses' life — the cramped entresol, the strained finances, the wife's complaints, the hidden photograph — create a portrait of old age as a slow erosion of dignity. La Pérouse's music, once his vocation and his joy, has been reduced to giving lessons to the children of families like the Vedel-Azaïses, who patronize him with dwindling gratitude. [PN=015-083] [PN=030-005]

Late in the novel, La Pérouse speaks of his hope to care for Boris, saying the journey from the Faubourg St. Honoré feels like "a long journey away" — a measure of the old man's increasing frailty. [PN=030-005]

## Characters Involved

- **La Pérouse** — The aged music-master, whose entresol apartment is both his home and his prison
- **Edouard** — The visiting novelist, whose role as sympathetic listener is complicated by his tendency to convert experience into literary material
- **Madame de La Pérouse** — The embittered wife, present only through her voice from the next room, whose complaints define the old man's domestic hell

## Consequences

### Immediate Aftermath

The visit establishes the bond between Edouard and La Pérouse that persists through the novel. Edouard becomes the old man's confidant and, eventually, the intermediary through whom the possibility of caring for Boris is explored. The visit also yields the confession about La Pérouse's dead son, which becomes part of the novel's deep background of grief. [PN=019-005] [PN=019-006]

### Narrative Ripple Effects

La Pérouse's desperate hope of caring for Boris becomes a minor subplot that culminates in tragedy. The boy, brought from the sanatorium to the Vedel-Azaïs school in Paris, becomes the victim of the older schoolboys' cruelty, manipulated into a suicide that destroys La Pérouse's last reason for living. The entresol apartment, with its hidden photograph and its voice from the next room, becomes a metaphor for the novels' central concern: the concealment of genuine feeling behind the structures of social obligation.

## Thematic Significance

The visit to La Pérouse's apartment embodies the novel's concern with **the consequences of inauthentic choices over a lifetime**. The entresol — neither basement nor first floor, neither public nor private — is the spatial equivalent of La Pérouse's compromised existence. His marriage, undertaken from pity, has become a mutual torment. His faith, once genuine, has decayed into doubt. His music, once his calling, has been reduced to piecework. The visit, like the novel itself, insists that lives lived without authenticity produce suffering that accumulates over decades, visible only to those who, like Edouard, make the effort to look.

## Literary Technique

Gide constructs the visit as a **dramatic scene** within the journal format, using spatial detail (the entresol, the next room, the hidden photograph) to externalize psychological states. The interrupted dialogue — La Pérouse's confessions cut short by his wife's calls — creates a rhythm of revelation and concealment that mirrors the old man's lifetime of suppressed truth. Edouard's perspective, sympathetic but analytical, keeps the reader aware of the tension between genuine compassion and the novelist's appropriative gaze.
