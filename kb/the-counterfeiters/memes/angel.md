# Angel

In Andre Gide's _The Counterfeiters_, the angel represents the ideal of pure, unselfconscious goodness — visible only to the innocent, associated with childhood, and standing in tension with the novel's world of adult moral complexity.

## Definition

Angels appear in the novel primarily through the discourse of children. Bronja, the young Polish girl at the Swiss sanatorium, claims to see angels, and her companion Boris asks whether prayer would allow him to see them too: "I say, Bronja, do you think that, if I were to pray very hard, I should see them too?" (022-023). Bronja's reply — "you aren't naughty; that's why you can see the angels. I shall always be naughty" (022-025) — establishes a moral economy: innocence is the precondition for angelic vision, and Boris, who believes himself naughty, is excluded.

## Role in The Counterfeiters

### Thematic Significance

The angel functions as the novel's symbol of an unattainable moral purity. In a world of counterfeit values, the angel represents the genuine — a goodness that cannot be counterfeited because it is not an achievement but a state of being. The children at the sanatorium (Bronja, Boris) are the novel's moral centers precisely because they do not yet participate in adult hypocrisy.

The angel also connects to the novel's Protestant subtext. Parents consider their children "angels of purity and innocence" (003-004), projecting onto them a goodness the adults themselves have lost. The angel is thus both real (Bronja's vision) and a projection (parental idealization).

### Character Associations

**Bronja** is the angel-seer — her innocence gives her access to a dimension invisible to adults. **Boris** is the angel-seeker — his anxiety about being "naughty" reveals a precocious moral sensitivity. **Bernard** and **Olivier** are neither angels nor devils but the novel's moral middle ground — struggling toward honesty without claiming purity.

## Manifestations in the Text

### Key Scenes

The angel conversation between Bronja and Boris (022-023–022-025) is one of the novel's most tender moments, occurring at the Swiss sanatorium where both children are being treated. The scene juxtaposes childish simplicity with profound theological questions: Who can see angels? What is the relationship between goodness and vision?

The parental projection of angelic qualities onto children (003-004) recurs as an ironic counterpoint — the adults who call children angels are the same adults whose lives are full of the "counterfeit" the novel anatomizes.

> "Bronja, you aren't naughty; that's why you can see the angels. I shall always be naughty." (022-025)

### Symbolic Expressions

The angel is the novel's figure of pure presence — unlike the counterfeit coin, which is all surface with no substance, the angel is substance without surface, visible only to those with the right kind of eyes. Sophie's voice is described as "more angelic than human" (022-001), extending the angelic into the aesthetic register.

## Literary and Philosophical Context

Gide's angels draw on the Christian iconographic tradition but strip it of doctrinal content. The angels are not messengers of God but emblems of a natural goodness available to children. This reflects Gide's ambivalent relationship with Christianity — he retains its moral vocabulary while emptying it of theological content. The angel/naughtiness binary also echoes Dostoevsky's exploration of childhood innocence, particularly in _The Brothers Karamazov_.
