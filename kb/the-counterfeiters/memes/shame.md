# Shame

A recurring emotional register in [[André Gide]]'s _The Counterfeiters_ — the feeling that drives characters to conceal, dissemble, and resist honest confrontation, and the counter-force against the novel's ideal of [[sincerity]].

## Definition

In Gide's novel, shame functions not merely as a private emotion but as a social mechanism. Characters are "ashamed" of joy (010-001), of their past actions (024-019), of their thoughts (024-008), and of revelations about their conduct (022-044). Shame is the barrier between the self one presents and the self one is — a central concern in a novel preoccupied with authenticity and [[counterfeit-coin|counterfeit values]].

## Role in The Counterfeiters

### Thematic Significance

Shame is the antithesis of the novel's moral ideal: the courage to be oneself without concealment. When [[Bernard Profitendieu]] leaves home, he feels "very much ashamed" of having taken the [[Comte de Passavant]]'s suitcase, yet this shame coexists with a "longing for liberty" (024-019). The tension between shame and freedom runs through the novel — characters are trapped by the fear of exposure, and liberation requires confronting what shames them.

> "I don't feel ashamed any more, when I am with you." (015-063) — Laura's words to Edouard suggest that authentic relationship dissolves shame.

### Character Associations

| Character | Experience of Shame | Significance |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Boris** | "Ashamed" and "on the defensive" about his "bad habits" (022-044, 022-046) | The child whose shame is exploited by the schoolboy ring |
| **Bernard** | Ashamed after leaving home, having taken the suitcase (024-019) | Shame as the cost of claiming freedom |
| **Olivier** | Feels "half ashamed" at finding his joy so great (010-001) | Shame even in legitimate happiness |
| **George** | Made "ashamed of having preferred the possession of a few paltry scraps of paper" (025-017) | Shame as a moral awakening |
| **Laura** | Feels no shame with Edouard (015-063) | Authentic relationship transcends shame |

## Manifestations in the Text

### Key Scenes

Boris's situation at the [[Azaïs Pension]] is the novel's most extended exploration of shame. [[Sophroniska]] describes Boris as being "on the defensive" and "ashamed" (022-044), requiring delicate handling before he can make a "complete confession" (022-048). The [[Vedel Boarding School|school]] environment — where the counterfeit coin ring later flourishes — is built on shame's power to silence and control.

> "Oh, shameful? There's no more shame in that than in a purely pathological phenomenon." (022-048) — Sophroniska's medical framing of Boris's condition attempts to neutralize the moral charge of shame, reflecting Gide's own engagement with modern psychology.

The examining magistrate [[Albéric Profitendieu|Profitendieu]]'s interrogation scene reveals shame's institutional dimension. When Boris is asked why he didn't confess sooner, the question — "out of false shame?" (025-009) — exposes how institutions deploy the concept of shame to regulate behavior, even as they participate in the hypocrisy that generates it.

> "At the last moment — it's shameful — I hadn't the courage to..." (031-028) — A character's abortive confession illustrates how shame blocks the truth even at the threshold of revelation.

## Literary and Philosophical Context

Gide's treatment of shame reflects his Protestant upbringing in a culture that, as he wrote, "taught us to be ashamed of our bodies" (013-082). The novel consistently associates shame with religious moralism while positioning honesty — even about transgressive desires — as the higher virtue. This aligns with Gide's broader philosophical project, influenced by [[Friedrich Nietzsche|Nietzsche]] and [[Fyodor Dostoevsky|Dostoevsky]], of liberating the individual from internalized moral constraints.
