# La Pérouse's Confession

In his modest entresol apartment in the Faubourg St. Honoré, the aged music-master La Pérouse tells Edouard the story of his dead son — a confession that reveals decades of marital misery, paternal grief, and spiritual despair.

## Summary

During a visit to La Pérouse's apartment, Edouard learns the old man's most closely guarded secret: he had a son who died young, leaving behind only a photograph and a lock of hair that La Pérouse has kept hidden from his wife for years. The confession opens a window onto a marriage of profound unhappiness — La Pérouse married a woman he pitied rather than loved, and the marriage has been a source of mutual torment ever since. [PN=014-048] [PN=014-052] [PN=014-056] [PN=014-063]

## Context

### Narrative Position

This event is recorded in **Part I, Chapters 14-15**, through Edouard's journal. It follows La Pérouse's appearance at Laura's wedding, where Edouard was struck by the old man's melancholy. The confession occurs during Edouard's first visit to La Pérouse's home. [PN=014-001] [PN=014-048] [PN=013-018]

### Preceding Events

Edouard had promised Laura's family that he would look in on the old music-master, who lives alone with his wife in reduced circumstances. La Pérouse taught Laura's mother piano and has been a fixture in the Vedel-Azaïs world for decades. At Laura's wedding, La Pérouse had played the harmonium with visible sadness, and Edouard felt compelled to visit him. [PN=013-018] [PN=013-023]

## Detailed Account

Edouard finds La Pérouse in his entresol apartment in the Faubourg St. Honoré. The old man, "in his shirt-sleeves," welcomes him with a mixture of dignity and desperation. The conversation turns gradually toward the personal, and La Pérouse begins to speak of things he has "never mentioned" to anyone. [PN=014-001] [PN=014-048]

When Edouard expresses surprise — having always thought the La Pérouses were childless — the old man raises his head and reveals the truth:

> "I had a son," he says simply. He raises his head, which he has been holding in his hands. [PN=014-048]

He produces a hidden photograph: a boy with the "high, prominent forehead" of the La Pérouse family. The photograph, along with a lock of hair, has been concealed from Madame de La Pérouse. The old man admits he "took it from Madame de La Pérouse without her noticing." [PN=014-056] [PN=014-059]

The confession expands to encompass La Pérouse's entire marriage. He married a woman he pitied but never loved — a marriage of duty that has produced only mutual resentment. "Madame de La Pérouse is always reproaching me," he says. The religious faith that might have sustained him has been eroded by a lifetime of forced cohabitation with a woman he cannot love. [PN=014-022] [PN=014-041] [PN=014-063]

> "How can I tell?" cried La Pérouse, in a kind of despair. "They took him from me." [PN=014-063]

The conversation is interrupted by Madame de La Pérouse calling from the next room. The old man masters himself, and the intimate confession is sealed away again behind the public face of a dutiful husband. [PN=014-066]

## Characters Involved

- **La Pérouse** — The aged music-master, whose confession reveals a life of duty without love and grief without expression
- **Edouard** — The listening novelist, whose capacity for empathy is tested by the raw suffering he witnesses
- **Madame de La Pérouse** — The wife, never directly seen but present through her voice from the next room, whose unhappiness is the counterpoint to her husband's
- **La Pérouse's dead son** — Present only as a photograph and a lock of hair, the absent center of the old man's suffering

## Consequences

### Immediate Aftermath

Edouard is deeply affected by the confession. He carries the image of La Pérouse with him through the rest of the novel. The old music-master later becomes a candidate for the guardianship of Boris (Sophroniska's troubled patient), a role he desperately wants but is not suited to fill. [PN=019-005] [PN=019-006]

### Narrative Ripple Effects

La Pérouse's confession establishes the novel's deep background of suffering — the accumulated grief that underlies the younger characters' rebellions and uncertainties. The old man's story of a marriage without love, a child lost, and a faith eroded speaks to the novel's larger concern with the failure of institutions (marriage, religion, family) to sustain genuine human connection. La Pérouse's desperate hope of caring for Boris in his old age will become a minor tragedy in its own right, as the boy's suicide at the novel's end destroys La Pérouse's last purpose for living.

## Thematic Significance

La Pérouse's confession is the novel's most concentrated expression of its concern with **the long-term consequences of inauthentic living**. His marriage — undertaken from pity, endured from duty — is the ultimate counterfeit relationship: a structure maintained for appearances while genuine feeling has been hollowed out. The hidden photograph and lock of hair are the relics of a real love (for his son) that the counterfeit marriage has forced him to conceal. La Pérouse's religious doubt — "They took him from me" — suggests that faith itself has become counterfeit for him, a language of consolation that no longer corresponds to any felt reality.

## Literary Technique

Gide presents the confession through Edouard's journal, filtering La Pérouse's raw grief through the novelist's recording consciousness. This technique creates a double effect: the reader experiences La Pérouse's suffering directly, but also sees Edouard processing it as potential literary material. The interruption by Madame de La Pérouse's voice is a masterly narrative stroke — the public world intrudes on private confession, and La Pérouse's instant self-mastery reveals a lifetime of such concealments.
