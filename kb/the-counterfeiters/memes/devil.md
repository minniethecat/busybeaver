# The Devil

In Andre Gide's _The Counterfeiters_, the devil is not a supernatural entity but a psychological force — the principle of rationalized transgression that provides "reasons for self-approval" to those who have abandoned conventional morality.

## Definition

The devil appears explicitly at crucial moments in the novel, always associated with the rationalization of wrongdoing. At the end of Vincent's night with Lady Griffith, the narrator observes: "night is drawing to an end, and the devil casts up his accounts" (006-084). The devil "watches [Vincent] with amusement" as he walks away, having surrendered to passion and abandoned Laura (006-100). Gide's devil is not the tempter to sin but the provider of justifications: "he continues to be a moral being, and the devil will only get the better of him by furnishing him with reasons for self-approval" (017-006).

## Role in The Counterfeiters

### Thematic Significance

The devil embodies the novel's concern with self-deception. Characters in _The Counterfeiters_ rarely sin out of simple weakness; they construct elaborate moral frameworks to justify their actions. Vincent, having abandoned his pregnant mistress Laura, does not simply acknowledge his cruelty — he invents a "new ethic" based on "immanence" and "totality in the moment" (017-006). The devil's triumph is not in making people do wrong but in making them feel right about doing wrong.

This connects to the novel's central counterfeit metaphor: the devil is the great counterfeiter, replacing genuine moral currency with false equivalents.

### Character Associations

**Vincent** is the devil's primary victim. His intellectual sophistication becomes the tool of his self-deception — theory serves appetite. **Laura** is the victim of Vincent's devil-assisted rationalization. **Edouard** recognizes the devil's method in his own artistic rationalizations — the "novelist's instinct" that turns other people's suffering into material.

## Manifestations in the Text

### Key Scenes

The devil's most explicit appearance is in the dawn scene after Vincent's night with Lilian (006-084). The "ambiguous hour" is both temporal and moral — the boundary between night and day mirrors the boundary between passion and consequence. The devil "casts up his accounts" like a bookkeeper, suggesting that moral debts are being tallied even as Vincent walks away.

In Edouard's journal, the devil appears as a psychological mechanism: "the devil, before attacking [virtue], transplants" his victims — removes them from familiar contexts where their moral habits would protect them (017-011). Vincent and Laura fell because they were "far from their parents, far from their past, far from everything that had given them reasons for resistance."

> "He continues to be a moral being, and the devil will only get the better of him by furnishing him with reasons for self-approval." (017-006)

### Symbolic Expressions

The devil in Gide's novel is never a literal tempter but always a psychological presence — the voice that provides intellectual cover for desire. The "casting up of accounts" metaphor ties the devil to the economic language of the counterfeit coin: moral currency, like financial currency, can be debased.

## Literary and Philosophical Context

Gide's devil draws on Dostoevsky's treatment of rationalized evil — the "Underground Man" who can justify anything — and on the Protestant tradition of self-examination. Gide's own struggle with his Protestant heritage, where moral rules provided both constraint and the material for transgression, informs the devil's role. The devil's method — "furnishing reasons for self-approval" — is a Nietzschean insight: morality can be the servant of the will to power, not its master.
