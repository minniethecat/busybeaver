# Illness

The bodily register of the novel's deeper disturbances — a condition in which physical symptoms disclose moral and psychological realities that characters cannot otherwise articulate.

## Definition

Illness in _The Counterfeiters_ is never merely medical. [[Boris]]'s "nervous illness" (025-009), [[La Pérouse]]'s auditory hallucinations, and the tuberculosis that shadows several minor characters all function as embodied expressions of psychic conflict. Gide inherits from [[Fyodor Dostoevsky|Dostoevsky]] the understanding that the body speaks what the mind cannot admit — that illness is another language, a somatic confession.

## Role in The Counterfeiters

### Thematic Significance

Boris's illness is the novel's central case study. [[Sophroniska]] describes his condition: "He suffers from a number of little troubles, tics, manias, which are the sign of what people call a 'nervous child'" (022-040). The symptoms are real but their meaning is interpretive — Sophroniska reads them as the consequence of repressed "bad habits" and the shame imposed by an older schoolfellow.

The illness, she explains to [[Édouard]], began after Boris tried to free himself from compulsive behavior: "His nervous illness only began after that. It arose no doubt from the constraint Boris was obliged to exercise in order to get free from them" (022-040). This is a psychoanalytic etiology: the illness is not the problem but the solution — a symptom that replaces a more fundamental disturbance.

Yet Gide is characteristically ambivalent about this reading. When Sophroniska announces Boris is "cured," Édouard wonders whether the cure is real or whether Sophroniska "is indulging her own vanity" (025-024). The novel's tragic conclusion — Boris's suicide — suggests the cure was indeed insufficient, that the roots of illness went deeper than analysis could reach.

## Manifestations in the Text

### Key Scenes

[[Édouard]]'s first encounter with Boris is mediated by illness. When he asks about Boris's condition, the response is evasive yet revealing: "Is it the name of his illness you want to know? Oh, you would be very clever if you could give it a name" (022-038). The illness resists naming — it is precisely the unsayable that the body performs.

The scene in which Sophroniska describes Boris's confession (025-004 to 025-009) traces the illness from symptom to cause: the older schoolfellow who taught Boris "bad habits," Boris's attempt to free himself, and the onset of nervous symptoms that followed. The sequence is a model of psychoanalytic reasoning — and the novel's subsequent events question whether such reasoning is adequate to the complexity of a human life.

> "But he clung to it and his illness clung to it as to a last refuge" (022-040) — Sophroniska's description of Boris's attachment to his secret captures the paradoxical function of illness: it is both suffering and shelter.

### Symbolic Expressions

The counterfeiting metaphor extends to illness: just as false coins circulate alongside genuine ones, false illness (malingering, hypochondria) may circulate alongside real suffering. [[La Pérouse]]'s auditory hallucination — "something like a nibbling" — is neither fully real nor fully invented; it occupies the intermediate zone that is illness's native territory.

The sanatorium at Saas-Fee is the spatial correlative of illness: a place of treatment and removal where the ill are gathered away from the healthy, yet where their conditions become visible in ways ordinary life conceals.

## Literary and Philosophical Context

The novel's treatment of illness reflects the influence of Dostoevsky, whose characters' physical afflictions are always also spiritual symptoms, and of the French medical tradition (Zola's naturalism) that understood illness as a social fact. Gide's innovation is to refuse both the purely somatic and the purely symbolic reading — illness in _The Counterfeiters_ is irreducibly both.
