# Sarah Vedel

The youngest sister of Laura Douviers, a girl whose natural boldness and "instinct for revolt" clash with the repressive atmosphere of the Vedel-Azaïs household, leading her to acts of provocative self-assertion that disturb her family's pious conventions.

## Role in the Novel

Sarah Vedel represents the younger generation of the Vedel-Azaïs family in revolt. Where her sister Laura internalized the family's moral strictures to her cost, and Armand masks his rebellion in cynicism, Sarah expresses her defiance through physical boldness and sexual provocation. At Laura's wedding, her behaviour — serving drinks, drinking herself, lying provocatively beside Olivier on a bed — demonstrates a rejection of the modest, pious femininity her family expects. Edouard finds her "unrecognizable" (PN=013-090) from the girl he thought he knew, suggesting how dramatically the family environment has driven her to change.

## Biography

### Background

Sarah is the younger daughter of Pastor Prosper Vedel and his wife, growing up in the Vedel-Azaïs boarding school on Rue Madame near the Luxembourg. She shares a room with an English boarding girl — formerly Laura's room — and helps serve at family functions (PN=013-041). She has been raised in the intensely religious atmosphere of the school, where her grandfather Azaïs has taken over the children's moral education after the pastor and his wife proved inadequate to the task (PN=013-078).

### Key Actions

At the wedding reception for Laura and Douviers, Sarah serves tea with her sister Rachel (PN=013-041). Later, she is reported to have made Olivier drink "six glasses of champagne, in succession" (PN=013-064). She retreats to her room with Olivier, Armand, and the English girl, where they drink and smoke. When Edouard enters, he finds her "sitting on one of the two beds beside Olivier and right up against him" — "her neck and arms were bare. She seemed taller, bolder" (PN=013-090). She lies down at full length beside Olivier, and when Edouard intervenes by pushing aside the screen Armand has placed around them, she shows him her father's private diary — an act of extraordinary indiscretion that she presents with "the faintest touch of irony" (PN=013-112). When Edouard questions the pastor's hypocrisy about giving up smoking, Sarah suggests "it proves that 'smoking' stood for something else" (PN=013-114), displaying a knowingness that shocks Edouard: "Was it really Sarah who spoke in this way? I was struck dumb."

### Relationships

**Pastor Vedel**: Her father, whose private diary she has secretly read and whom she sees with clear-eyed skepticism. **Laura Douviers**: Her older sister, whose disastrous marriage and pregnancy cast a shadow over the family. Sarah's own boldness may be read as a conscious rejection of Laura's path of repressed desire. **Armand Vedel**: Her brother, with whom she shares a sardonic view of their family's pieties. **Olivier Molinier**: Her flirtatious intimacy at the wedding suggests a mutual attraction — though Olivier later tells Edouard he is not in love with her (PN=013-117). **Edouard**: She has always been "exceedingly confidential" with him (PN=013-090), but their encounter at the wedding reveals a new, unsettling dimension of her character.

## Character Analysis

### Motivations

Sarah is driven by a desire to break free of the constraints of her family's religious domesticity. Her behaviour at the wedding is a performance of freedom — drinking, smoking, lying beside a boy, revealing her father's secrets. Each act is a small revolution against the order of the Vedel household. Her "instinct for revolt" reflects a healthy refusal to be suffocated, but Gide leaves open the question of whether her rebellion leads anywhere productive.

### Symbolic Role

Sarah embodies the revolt against religious hypocrisy that runs through the novel. She sees through her father's pieties and is willing to expose them. Yet her revolt is reactive rather than constructive — it defines itself against the family rather than toward anything. In this sense, she represents the danger that rebellion against the counterfeit can itself become a form of counterfeiting.

### Development

Like many of the novel's younger characters, Sarah's trajectory is incomplete. She appears primarily in the single wedding scene, and her future — whether she will find an authentic path or replicate the family patterns she despises — remains uncertain.

## Literary Context

Gide presents Sarah through Edouard's shocked eyes — the older observer unsettled by the younger generation's morals. The scene in her bedroom is structured as a gradual revelation: Edouard enters a room he does not recognize, sees a Sarah he does not recognize, and hears words he cannot believe she speaks. Gide uses the gap between Edouard's memory of Sarah and the present reality to dramatize the generational rupture at the novel's heart.

## Key Passages

> "Was it really Sarah who spoke in this way? I was struck dumb. I looked at her, hardly daring to understand." — Edouard's reaction to Sarah's knowing comment about her father (PN=013-115)

> "Rather touching, isn't it?" asked Sarah with the faintest touch of irony, when I had done reading. — Sarah handing Edouard her father's diary (PN=013-112)
