# Delinquency

The participation of adolescents in systematic wrongdoing — particularly the circulation of counterfeit coins — serves in The Counterfeiters as both a narrative engine and a lens through which Gide examines the transmission of moral corruption across generations.

## Definition

Delinquency in The Counterfeiters refers specifically to the organized misconduct of the schoolboy characters — Georges Molinier, Léon Ghéridanisol, Philippe Adamanti, and their associates — whose trafficking in false coins constitutes the novel's most literal instantiation of its central metaphor. Gide distinguishes between this active delinquency and the passive moral failures of the adult world, yet the novel insists that the two are connected: the boys' criminality is not an aberration but a mirror of the counterfeit values circulating in respectable society. Delinquency thus becomes a diagnostic category, revealing what the adult world prefers not to acknowledge about itself [033-047][042-008][044-071].

## Role in The Counterfeiters

### Thematic Significance

The counterfeit-coin ring literalizes the novel's governing metaphor: the circulation of false value that no one examines too closely. The boys are technically criminals, yet Gide's treatment of their delinquency is notably un-sensational — he presents it as a mundane feature of institutional life, woven into the daily routines of the boarding school. This ordinariness is the point: delinquency is not an exotic pathology but a natural consequence of a social order that substitutes hypocrisy for moral education. When Édouard asks, "What would you think of a man who should offer the public real coins [and] seem to be defrauding them?" — the question collapses the distinction between honest and counterfeit, legitimate and delinquent [040-051].

### Character Associations

Léon Ghéridanisol emerges as the ringleader of the counterfeit operation — a figure of "calm superiority" who manipulates his younger accomplices with practiced detachment [033-031][033-049]. Georges Molinier, Olivier's younger brother, is drawn into the scheme through a combination of bravado and susceptibility to peer influence. Philippe Adamanti ("Phiphi") occupies the lowest rung of the operation, a reluctant participant whose fear and hesitation expose the coercive dynamics underlying the apparent camaraderie [033-047]. The adult characters — particularly Profitendieu, who investigates the counterfeit ring — represent a world of official morality that is itself compromised, having produced the conditions for the delinquency it now condemns.

## Manifestations in the Text

### Key Scenes

The scenes in which the boys negotiate the sale of false coins — Ghéridanisol holding out three francs to his accomplice, the calculation of profits, the hierarchy of risk — constitute an anatomy of petty criminality rendered with documentary precision [033-047][033-048]. The eventual exposure of the ring, and the decision of the adult authorities to handle it discreetly to avoid scandal, reveals the complicity between official morality and the delinquency it officially condemns [044-081][046-020].

> "He had risked his first false coin. He had passed a great many more." [042-008]

### Symbolic Expressions

The false coin itself is the novel's master symbol for delinquency — a small object that carries enormous moral weight. The coin can be held in the hand, passed from pocket to pocket, examined and recognized as counterfeit, yet it continues to circulate because no one wants the trouble of stopping it. The boarding school — an institution designed to produce moral citizens — becomes the very site where delinquency flourishes, transforming the school from symbol of moral formation into symbol of moral failure [044-047].

## Literary and Philosophical Context

Gide's treatment of delinquency engages with the naturalist tradition of representing juvenile crime — from Zola to the Goncourts — but departs from it in refusing to treat the boys' actions as determined by heredity or environment alone. The novel's Protestant framework adds a theological dimension: delinquency as original sin re-described in sociological terms, a condition that no institutional remedy can cure because the institutions themselves are contaminated.
