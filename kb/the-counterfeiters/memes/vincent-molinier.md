# Vincent Molinier

The older brother of Olivier and George Molinier, a young man who abandons his pregnant mistress Laura Douviers for the wealthy and amoral Lady Griffith — the novel's most fully realized portrait of self-deception and moral failure.

## Role in the Novel

Vincent is the novel's primary example of how intelligence can serve self-deception. He does not simply abandon Laura — he constructs a philosophical justification for doing so. Edouard notes that "he continues to be a moral being, and the devil will only get the better of him by furnishing him with reasons for self-approval" (017-006). Vincent invents a "new ethic" of "immanence" and "totality in the moment" to legitimize his conduct.

Vincent's story arc — from promising young man to Lady Griffith's kept lover to something darker (hinted at through his final trajectory) — is the novel's cautionary tale about the cost of unexamined freedom.

## Biography

### Background

Vincent is the eldest son of Oscar and Pauline Molinier, a medical student with intellectual ambitions. He is described as having "rather fine" angles to his character (027-009) — potential that his relationship with Lady Griffith flattens into commonness.

### Key Actions

Vincent begins an affair with Laura Douviers, a married woman, and abandons her when she becomes pregnant. He becomes involved with Lady Lilian Griffith, an American woman of wealth and no scruples. During a night of gambling and passion, Vincent is observed at "that ambiguous hour when night is drawing to an end, and the devil casts up his accounts" (006-084). He drifts into moral dissolution, becoming "commonplace by frequenting her" (027-009).

### Relationships

**Laura Douviers**: Vincent's abandoned mistress — his primary victim. **Lady Griffith (Lilian)**: The woman who "has no soul" (027-009) and into whose orbit Vincent falls. **Olivier Molinier**: Vincent's younger brother, who represents the sensitivity Vincent has lost. **Edouard**: Vincent's uncle, who observes his decline with a novelist's eye.

## Character Analysis

### Motivations

Vincent is motivated by desire and by the need to feel good about himself. His intellectual sophistication becomes the instrument of his self-deception — he cannot simply act badly; he must construct a theory that makes his bad actions appear good.

### Symbolic Role

Vincent represents the counterfeit moral being — someone who uses the language of ethics to justify unethical conduct. He is the novel's demonstration that "the devil" works not through temptation to sin but through the provision of self-justifying reasons.

### Development

Vincent's development is a decline — from complexity to simplification, from moral struggle to moral surrender. The novel charts his progressive loss of "angles" and his absorption into the smooth surface of a life without depth.
