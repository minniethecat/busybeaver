# Moral (Moral Tragedy)

In Andre Gide's _The Counterfeiters_, the moral denotes a specific kind of literary tragedy — the drama of the soul's inward struggle, distinct from the classical tragedy of fate and the modern tragedy of social circumstance.

## Definition

Edouard, in his journal, distinguishes moral tragedy from all previous literary forms: "There is a kind of tragedy, it seems to me, which has hitherto almost entirely eluded literature. The novel has dealt with the contrariness of fate, good or evil fortune, social relationships, the conflicts of passions and of characters — but not with the very essence of man's being" (014-070). He argues that Christianity transferred drama to the moral plane, but that "properly speaking there are no Christian novels" — only edifying ones. The moral tragedy he envisions is dramatized in the Gospel text: "If the salt have lost his flavour wherewith shall it be salted?" (014-071) — the tragedy of a soul that has lost its essential quality.

## Role in The Counterfeiters

### Thematic Significance

Moral tragedy is the aesthetic program of Gide's novel — to write not about what happens to people but about what people become. The novel's characters are all, in different ways, undergoing the drama of the soul: Vincent losing his moral "flavour" through self-justification; Bernard discovering his through honesty; Edouard examining his through endless self-reflection.

The concept also illuminates Gide's critique of the conventional novel. Traditional novels treat characters as finished products; Gide wants to represent them as possibilities. Edouard writes: "I lean with a fearful attraction over the depths of each creature's possibilities and weep for all that lies atrophied under the heavy lid of custom and morality" (013-130). The moral tragedy is the drama of possibility — what a person might become — rather than the drama of actuality.

### Character Associations

**Edouard** is the theorist of moral tragedy, attempting to write the novel that would embody it. **Vincent** is its negative example — a soul losing its salt. **Bernard** is its positive example — a soul in formation. **Laura** and **Profitendieu** represent the condition of those whose moral flavour has already been lost.

## Manifestations in the Text

### Key Scenes

Edouard's journal entries of November 9 (014-070–014-071) contain the novel's most explicit statement of the moral tragedy concept. He contrasts his ambition with all previous novelistic practice and anchors it in the Christian tradition while rejecting Christian edification.

Old Azais's method of extracting "moral teaching" from every event (003-083) represents the degraded form of moral concern — moralizing rather than the moral. The novel's formal innovation — multiple perspectives, refusal of closure, the journal form — is the aesthetic enactment of moral tragedy.

> "If the salt have lost his flavour wherewith shall it be salted?" (014-071, quoting Matthew 5:13)

### Symbolic Expressions

The salt metaphor is the novel's figure for the moral life. Salt that has lost its savour cannot be re-salted — a soul that has lost its essential quality cannot recover it. The counterfeit coin is the more worldly version of the same idea — value that has been hollowed out while retaining its appearance.

## Literary and Philosophical Context

Gide's moral tragedy draws on Dostoevsky, whom he admired as the novelist who brought moral intensity to the psychological novel. It also reflects Gide's engagement with Nietzsche's critique of Christian morality and his attempt to recover a non-Christian ethical seriousness. The Gospel reference anchors moral tragedy in the Christian tradition while refusing the consolations of doctrine.

The concept also engages with the Symbolist ambition to create a "pure" art — Gide's moral tragedy is the novel's equivalent of Mallarmé's pure poetry, purified of everything not essential to the moral drama.
