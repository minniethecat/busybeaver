# Fashion

In _The Counterfeiters_, fashion represents the external cultivation of self-presentation — dress, manners, taste — which Gide treats as both a legitimate mode of self-fashioning and a potential substitute for authentic identity.

## Definition

Fashion in the novel extends beyond clothing to encompass a whole mode of social performance. Characters use fashion to signal belonging, to construct identities, and to exert influence over others. Gide's treatment of fashion is characteristically double-edged: he recognizes the creative potential in self-presentation while exposing its use as a mask for emptiness (PN-005-009, PN-034-001).

## Role in The Counterfeiters

### Thematic Significance

Fashion serves as another register of the novel's central counterfeiting metaphor. Just as false coins substitute appearance for substance, fashion substitutes the performance of identity for its authentic possession. The novel repeatedly asks whether the cultivated surface reveals or conceals the person beneath. This question is especially pointed in relation to the younger characters — Olivier, Bernard, Vincent — who are still in the process of becoming, and for whom fashion can be either a tool of self-discovery or a means of self-betrayal.

### Character Associations

Passavant is the novel's supreme fashioner — he remakes Olivier in his own image through a campaign of sartorial and aesthetic education: "he thought my wretched rags frightful; shirts, ties, socks — nothing I had pleased him... He himself is exquisitely elegant; but above all his taste is very good, and a great many things which I used to think quite bearable now seem odious to me" (PN-029-036). Lady Griffith performs a similar function for Vincent, teaching him "to polish his nails, to part his hair on one side instead of brushing it back" — effectively sculpting him into a more presentable figure (PN-008-034).

## Manifestations in the Text

### Key Scenes

The shopping expedition with Passavant and Olivier in the days before their departure for the South is a sustained dramatization of fashion as seduction. Passavant replaces Olivier's entire wardrobe, and with it, his sense of himself: "naturally everything we bought was sent to his house, for fear of making my mother uncomfortable" (PN-029-036). The detail of hiding the purchases from Olivier's mother reveals fashion's complicity with secrecy and the erosion of filial bonds.

> "He kept repeating that if we were to spend some time together, it would be too painful to him not to see me properly dressed — that is to say, as he likes. Naturally everything we bought was sent to his house, for fear of making my mother uncomfortable." (PN-029-036)

Robert de Passavant's penthouse apartment, decorated and fitted up "in his own fashion," is a physical manifestation of the aesthetic control he exerts over his environment and the people within it (PN-005-009).

## Symbolic Expressions

Gide employs a telling natural metaphor: "The forest fashions the tree. To each one how small a space is given!" (PN-034-001). This image of environmental determinism stands in tension with the novel's celebration of individual agency and self-creation. The forest that fashions the tree is like the social world that shapes its members — fashion in its broadest sense, operating not through choice but through the subtle pressure of context.

## Fashion as Counterfeit Self

The novel's deepest critique of fashion is that it can become a form of self-counterfeiting. When Olivier allows Passavant to remake him, he risks losing himself. When Vincent learns to dress for success, he simultaneously learns to dissemble. Fashion, like the counterfeit coin, circulates in a social economy and can be passed off as the genuine article — but the novel insists that the substitution is ultimately unsustainable.
