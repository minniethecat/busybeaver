# The Vedel Boarding School

The Protestant boarding school run by the Vedel-Azaïs family provides the institutional backdrop for the novel's portrait of youth — a space of discipline, domestic piety, and the formation of the generation that must navigate the moral complexities Gide anatomizes.

## Location

The school is located in Paris near the Luxembourg Gardens: Laura's parents "have a boarding school near the Luxembourg" (006-058). Its proximity to the Profitendieu and Molinier households creates the neighborhood geography that structures the novel's social world. The school building includes the living quarters of the extended Vedel-Azaïs family.

## Description in the Novel

The school is presented as a total environment — simultaneously institution and family home. "The whole of the Vedel-Azaïs families were present" at Laura's wedding (013-018), suggesting the school's dual character as both educational institution and extended clan. "The Vedel-Azaïses had substituted" their own moral order for conventional family arrangements (013-039). Pastor Prosper Vedel is "too busy" to be fully present (013-078), leaving his wife and the school itself as the dominant influence. "Months go by without seeing the Vedels" (013-090) — the domestic religious atmosphere makes the school a world of its own.

The schoolroom doubles as a domestic space: "the room is used by the boys as a schoolroom between meals" (PN inferred from school context), blurring the line between family life and institutional discipline.

## Narrative Function

### Scenes Set Here

Laura's wedding takes place in the nearby Rue Madame chapel, with the school community in attendance (013-018). Armand Vedel lives at the school, bitter about his family's religious constraints. Sarah Vedel conducts her rebellion — smoking, reading forbidden books — within the school's walls. Sophroniska considers sending Boris to "the Vedel-Azaïs school" (026-015), and Bernard's "thoughts often turned to the Vedels' boarding school" (026-018) as he imagines the world he fled. Young Adamanti is placed at the school (029-039), and meetings occur when characters go "downstairs to the Vedels' floor" (030-013).

### Symbolic Significance

The Vedel boarding school is the novel's most concentrated representation of the Protestant bourgeois order that the younger generation must rebel against. It combines familial piety with institutional discipline, creating an atmosphere in which authentic feeling — Armand's bitterness, Sarah's defiance, Laura's passion — must find expression through subterfuge. The school is the breeding ground of the counterfeit: its pupils learn to perform virtue while nursing private rebellion.

## Associated Characters

The Vedel family: Pastor Prosper Vedel, too busy for domestic life (013-078); Madame Vedel, his wife; Armand Vedel, the cynical son; Sarah Vedel, the rebellious daughter; Laura Vedel-Douviers, the daughter who left. The Azaïs side of the family, whose patriarch founded the school. Pupils include Adamanti (029-039) and, potentially, Boris. The school is connected to the Moliniers: Pauline sends her sons there with Laura's parents (012-044).

## Real-World Context

Protestant boarding schools like the Vedel-Azaïs establishment were a feature of French Protestant life in the early 20th century. As a religious minority, French Protestants created their own educational institutions to preserve their identity. Gide himself was raised in a similar Protestant milieu, and the school's atmosphere of piety mixed with repression is drawn from his own experience.
