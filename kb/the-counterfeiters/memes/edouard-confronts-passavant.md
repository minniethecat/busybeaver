# Edouard Confronts Passavant

The morning after Olivier's suicide attempt at the Argonauts banquet, Edouard visits the Comte de Passavant to retrieve Olivier's belongings — a diplomatic errand that becomes a confrontation between the novel's two opposing models of literary and personal authority.

## Summary

After Olivier's breakdown at the Argonauts banquet and his subsequent recovery at Edouard's apartment, Olivier writes to Bernard asking him to collect his belongings from Passavant's residence. Bernard delegates the task to Edouard, who — though reluctant — feels bound by his promise to Olivier. The visit to the Rue de Babylone becomes an exercise in controlled hostility: Passavant, furious at losing Olivier but incapable of acknowledging defeat, affects indifference; Edouard, triumphant but unwilling to show it, maintains a frigid politeness. The two men, each representing an incompatible vision of what literature and life should be, exchange words that barely conceal their mutual contempt (037-001).

## Context

### Narrative Position

The confrontation occurs in Part III, Chapter XII, in the aftermath of the banquet scene. Olivier has survived his suicide attempt and is convalescing under Edouard's care. The visit to Passavant is the final act in the struggle for Olivier's soul — a struggle Edouard has decisively won, though neither man will say so directly.

### Preceding Events

Edouard's dislike of Passavant predates the novel's action. He had been offended by the newspaper campaign around _The Horizontal Bar_, irritated by "everything that Passavant does, and everything that other people do round about him" (009-001). The personal dimension intensified when Passavant took Olivier to Corsica, exploiting the emotional void left by Bernard's departure with Edouard.

At the Argonauts banquet, the tension between the two men had surfaced openly. Passavant had invited Edouard to dinner, and Edouard had accepted — against his better judgment — producing an evening of strained pleasantries that culminated in Olivier's spectacular breakdown.

## Detailed Account

Edouard walks to Passavant's residence, reflecting on the strangeness of his errand. "He told himself that he ought to execrate him; he went over in his mind all his grievances — but they had ceased to sting. This rival, whom only yesterday he had so detested, he could detest no longer — he had ousted him too completely" (037-001).

Passavant, for his part, is a man who cannot bear to be worsted. "In order not to acknowledge his defeats to himself, he always affected to have desired his fate, and whatever happened to him, he pretended that that was what he wished" (037-001). Confronted with Olivier's defection, he has already begun the work of retrospective rationalization: Olivier had been an encumbrance, the boy's talents had been overestimated, the management of a review required more experienced hands.

The interview itself is conducted with exquisite civility. Neither man raises his voice. Passavant makes no attempt to retain Olivier's possessions; Edouard asks for nothing more than what he came for. The transaction is completed without overt hostility — and yet every word is weighted. When Edouard departs, the contest is over, and both men know who has won.

## Consequences

### Immediate Aftermath

Edouard returns with Olivier's trunk and belongings. Olivier, recovering from his breakdown, remains at Edouard's apartment, his rupture with Passavant now physically as well as emotionally complete.

### Narrative Ripple Effects

The confrontation marks the end of Passavant's direct role in Olivier's story — though his influence persists in subtler forms. It also consolidates Edouard's position as Olivier's guardian, setting the stage for the quieter but no less complex dynamics of their relationship in the novel's later chapters.

## Thematic Significance

The confrontation between Edouard and Passavant is the novel's most concentrated dramatization of its central opposition: authenticity versus counterfeit. Passavant's inability to acknowledge defeat — his reflexive transformation of loss into willed choice — is the defining gesture of the counterfeiter. Edouard's refusal to gloat, his coldness and his restraint, reflects a different kind of limitation: the authentic person's difficulty in navigating social situations that demand performance.

## Literary Technique

Gide handles the confrontation with characteristic indirection. The scene is narrated through Edouard's consciousness, and the reader sees Passavant only as Edouard sees him: as a rival who has been vanquished but who is too self-deceived to know it. The narrative commentary on Passavant's psychology — "No one had ever succeeded in catching Passavant out; it was unbearable to him to be worsted" (037-001) — belongs to the novel's essayistic register, in which the novelist within the novel analyzes his characters with clinical detachment.
