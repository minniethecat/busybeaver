# Albéric Profitendieu

A fifty-five-year-old examining magistrate at the Paris law courts, whose discovery of Bernard's illegitimacy and subsequent flight from home sets the novel's central themes of counterfeit identity and bourgeois hypocrisy in motion. A man of rigorous propriety and physical vanity, Profitendieu's world unravels between the opening and closing pages, as the legal and moral certainties he has built his life upon prove inadequate to the complexities of lived experience.

## Role in the Novel

Albéric Profitendieu serves as the novel's first emblem of the established order — the law, the family, the bourgeoisie — whose certainties are systematically undermined. His function is twofold: his discovery of the love letters that reveal Bernard is not his biological son initiates the plot, and his later investigation of the schoolboy prostitution ring dramatizes the tension between legal rectitude and paternal instinct (003-001, 019-007). He is a man who has built his identity on being a judge — both professionally and morally — and finds that identity inadequate when confronted with the truth about his family.

## Biography

### Background

Profitendieu is a respected examining magistrate, a colleague of Oscar Molinier (the *Président de chambre*). At fifty-five, he possesses "a well-developed chest and a brisk gait" and prides himself on his physical soundness, though "an uncomfortable sensation in his right side" betrays a chronic liver complaint (003-001). Through the death of his wife's parents, he has inherited "a very considerable fortune," setting him above colleagues like Molinier who live solely on their civil service salaries (003-001). His household includes his wife, his eldest son Charles (an advocate), his daughter Cécile (a pianist), his youngest son Caloub, and Bernard.

### Key Actions

The crisis of the novel opens when Bernard, having discovered the love letters in a secret drawer of Profitendieu's desk, flees the family home. Profitendieu returns from the law courts to find Bernard's letter, which declares that Bernard knows he is not Profitendieu's biological son (003-004). The letter wounds him deeply — not with anger, but with sorrow localized as pain in his liver: "He feels as if his grief were in his liver" (003-004). He recognizes in Bernard's rawness and defiance a quality he had loved in the boy — "he realizes that it is for those very things that he loved him as he had never loved any of the others" (003-004).

Rather than expose the truth, Profitendieu concocts a cover story: Bernard has gone away with a mathematics tutor. When his wife learns the truth, she breaks down, and Profitendieu attempts to bring her to "a more pious frame of mind," seeking consolation in religious acceptance (003-006). His eldest son Charles senses the truth without being told and offers silent solidarity.

Later in the novel, Profitendieu is assigned to investigate the schoolboy prostitution ring — the "counterfeiting" of sexual morality that mirrors the novel's thematic concerns (019-007). His zeal in the investigation is noted by Molinier, who observes that Profitendieu "charged into the business like a bull, without suspecting that with the first stroke of his horns he ran the risk of sticking his own son" (019-007). When Profitendieu discovers Bernard's possible involvement, his prosecutorial fervor abruptly cools, and the investigation is quietly dropped — a moment that reveals the limits of his commitment to abstract justice when personal interest is at stake.

Near the novel's end, Profitendieu visits Edouard, climbing six flights of stairs to do so, in a state of evident embarrassment. He comes seeking information about Bernard's whereabouts and well-being, the distraught father finally setting aside his dignity (031-001).

### Relationships

Profitendieu's most significant relationship is with **Bernard** — the son who is not his son. Despite knowing Bernard's true parentage, Profitendieu has loved him more than his legitimate children: "he realizes that it is for those very things that he loved him as he had never loved any of the others" (003-004). This love, complicated by the knowledge of his wife's betrayal, is the novel's most sustained examination of fatherhood beyond biology.

His relationship with his **wife** is one of mutual alienation masked by propriety. When the secret of her affair emerges, Profitendieu discovers that their sorrow "should bring them together, but, alas! Profitendieu feels obscurely that their thoughts are travelling by divergent ways" (003-007). With his eldest son **Charles**, there is a wordless understanding — Charles communicates his sympathy through a simple embrace, "the way in which he lays his head upon his shoulder, and leans and lingers there" (003-007).

With **Oscar Molinier**, Profitendieu maintains a collegial rivalry marked by class tension (Profitendieu's inherited wealth versus Molinier's salary-dependent dignity) and professional competition. Molinier regards him as "an honest man, and what's rarer perhaps, a good fellow" (019-007).

## Character Analysis

### Motivations

Profitendieu is driven by a need for order — moral, legal, and domestic. His "particularity as to the proprieties" governs his behavior (003-001), and his prejudices — including his belief that a warm bath on a full stomach is imprudent — are, as the narrator notes, "the props of civilisation" (003-001). Beneath this surface, however, lies a man capable of genuine love, as his attachment to Bernard reveals, and of moral compromise, as his handling of the prostitution investigation demonstrates.

### Symbolic Role

Profitendieu embodies the old order of bourgeois France — law, property, propriety, and conventional paternity — that Gide's novel systematically interrogates. His name itself (from *profit* + *Dieu* — profit and God) suggests the twin pillars of his identity. The revelation that his family is built on a "counterfeit" — Bernard is not his son — makes him the novel's first and most intimate encounter with the theme of falsification. His liver, the organ that registers his grief, is the physical manifestation of a moral order under stress — the body's rebellion against the strictures of respectability.

### Development

Profitendieu undergoes a subtle but significant transformation. He begins as a man who judges — professionally, morally, domestically — and ends as a man who climbs six flights of stairs, hat in hand, to ask a writer about his lost son. The trajectory is from certitude to humility, from the bench to supplication. His decision to quietly drop the investigation that might implicate Bernard is his most consequential moral choice: he chooses fatherhood over law, personal loyalty over abstract justice.

## Literary Context

Gide introduces Profitendieu through the technique of authorial omniscience that slides into ironic distance. The narrator's description of Profitendieu's physical habits — the bath, the liver, the "prejudices are the props of civilisation" maxim — establishes him as a type before revealing him as a man. The chapter that follows the discovery of Bernard's letter is a tour de force of shifting perspective, moving from Profitendieu's consciousness to his wife's, to the children's, to the servant's, each refracting the same event through different angles of understanding and self-interest.

## Key Passages

> "He feels as if his grief were in his liver. He drops into an armchair and rereads Bernard's letter. He shrugs his shoulders sadly. Yes, it is a cruel letter — but there is wounded vanity, defiance — bravado in it, too. Not one of his other children — his real children — would have been capable — any more than he would have been capable himself — of writing it." (003-004)

> "Profitendieu felt better. He had at first been afraid he would be too ill to speak. And yet it was necessary to give an explanation of Bernard's disappearance. He knew now what he must say — however painful it might be. He felt firm and determined." (003-006)

> "He charged into the business like a bull, without suspecting that with the first stroke of his horns... he ran the risk of sticking his own son." (019-007, Molinier to Edouard)
