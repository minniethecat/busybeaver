# Saas-Fée

Saas-Fée is the Swiss mountain village where the novel's Part II unfolds — a site of retreat from [[paris]], where [[Edouard]], [[Laura Douviers|Laura]], [[Bernard Profitendieu|Bernard]], Sophroniska, Bronja, and Boris gather at a sanatorium. Removed from the heat and social pressure of [[paris]], Saas-Fée provides the space where the novel's moral and intellectual tensions crystallize, where [[sincerity]] becomes momentarily possible, and where the children's story — culminating in [[boris-at-the-sanatorium|Boris's tragedy]] — is most fully told.

## Location

Saas-Fée is a real village in the Valais canton of Switzerland, high in the Alps. In the novel, it is described as a remote destination: "at the further end of" the valley (041-044). [[Edouard]] locates it precisely: "I know now where he is. At Saas-Fée. Do you know it? It's in…" (019-040). The village's isolation from [[paris]] is essential to its narrative function — it is a place one must travel to deliberately, a journey that itself marks a moral decision.

## Description in the Novel

Gide presents Saas-Fée as a world apart from the heat and social pressure of [[paris]]. The sanatorium setting provides a community of convalescents and visitors: Bronja and Boris, the Polish children; Sophroniska, the psychoanalyst; [[Laura Douviers|Laura]], seeking refuge; and [[Edouard]], who comes to observe, record, and intervene. The mountain air, the hotel balconies, and the walks through Alpine meadows create a climate of heightened clarity. "Sophroniska joined them, generally on the balcony" (023-006), suggesting the outdoor social space that replaces Parisian interiors.

> The village is small enough that departures are noted: "the keeper says, he must have left Saas-Fée two days before our arrival" (023-087). The mountains make secrets harder to keep.

The entirety of Part II is set in Saas-Fée. Here, [[Edouard]] writes in his [[journal]] and debates the nature of the novel with Sophroniska (023-006, 023-026). The children Bronja and Boris discuss angels (022-033, 022-035). [[Laura Douviers|Laura]] and [[Edouard]] confront each other about their shared past. Boris's story — his secret talisman, his history with Bronja — is told in this setting. The sanatorium community provides the novel's most concentrated display of characters reflecting on their lives.

### Symbolic Significance

Saas-Fée represents the possibility of withdrawal from the [[counterfeit-coin|counterfeit]] world. At altitude, removed from [[paris]], characters speak more honestly. The sanatorium is both a literal health resort and a metaphor for moral convalescence — characters come here to recover, not only physically but emotionally and spiritually.

> The mountain setting suggests clarity, purity, and a kind of moral elevation that contrasts with the stifling bourgeois interiors of [[paris]]. Yet the novel is too honest to make the opposition simple: the tragedy that unfolds from Saas-Fée — Boris's suicide — proves that the counterfeit reaches everywhere.

The novel's geographic structure embodies its moral structure:

## Associated Characters

[[Edouard]] travels to Saas-Fée to find [[Bernard Profitendieu|Bernard]] and rejoin [[Laura Douviers|Laura]] (019-040). Sophroniska brings her daughter Bronja and the boy Boris to the sanatorium (022-035). Laura is already in residence, having fled [[paris]]. Bernard arrives as [[Edouard]]'s secretary. The children Bronja and Boris have their most important scenes here. The local constable appears when the suicide investigation unfolds (041-042).

## Real-World Context

Saas-Fée was a known destination for European health tourists in the early 20th century, part of the Alpine sanatorium culture central to European modernism — Thomas Mann's _The Magic Mountain_ is the most famous literary treatment of this world. Gide himself traveled in Switzerland and was familiar with its sanatorium culture. The choice of a sanatorium setting also allows Gide to gather characters from different backgrounds in a single location, creating the conditions for the novel's most concentrated philosophical exchanges.
