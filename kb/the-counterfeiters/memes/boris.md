# Boris

The young grandson of La Pérouse, living at Sophroniska's sanatorium in Switzerland, whose obsessive anxiety about being "naughty" and consequent inability to see angels — unlike his companion Bronja — becomes the novel's most poignant exploration of childhood moral terror and its fatal consequences.

## Role in the Novel

Boris appears in the novel's Swiss section, a child patient at Sophroniska's sanatorium where Edouard, Bernard, and Laura visit. His psychological fragility — centered on the belief that his naughtiness makes him unworthy of seeing angels — is treated by Sophroniska through psychoanalytic methods. But Boris's story ends in tragedy: he takes his own life at the Vedel-Azaïs school in Paris, a death precipitated by the cruel machinations of his schoolfellows and his own unhealed psychological wounds. His death is the novel's darkest event and its most devastating commentary on the failure of moral education.

## Biography

### Background

Boris is the grandson of La Pérouse, the aged music-master. His father — La Pérouse's son — has been estranged from the family, and "little Boris is being educated in Poland" (PN=014-065) before coming to Sophroniska's care in Switzerland. His mother was an actress who "used to take Boris with her to her dressing-room" (PN=022-035), exposing him to an adult world he was too young to understand. He has been "confided to [Sophroniska] for medical treatment" (PN=022-034) and is being treated for nervous disorders through the "confession" method.

### Key Actions

Boris and Bronja are constant companions at the sanatorium. When Edouard and Bernard first encounter them, Boris is described as being under "careful watch" (PN=022-001). He repeatedly expresses anxiety about his moral state: "Oh, Boris, why are you always saying things like that? You frighten me" (PN=022-020), Bronja says in response to his self-accusations. His central preoccupation is with angels: "Bronja, do you think that, if I were very, very good, I should be able to see them too?" (PN=022-023). Bronja's answer is devastating in its simplicity: "Bronja, you aren't naughty; that's why you can see them" — implying Boris cannot (PN=022-025). Later, after Bronja's death, Boris is brought to the Vedel-Azaïs school in Paris, where he becomes the victim of a cruel prank involving a pistol. "A month later Boris heard of Bronja's death from Madame Sophroniska" (PN=046-001), and the news, combined with the cruelty of his schoolfellows, leads to his suicide.

### Relationships

**La Pérouse**: His grandfather, whose one remaining hope in life is Boris: "Everything I care for... Little Boris" (PN=014-065). **Bronja**: His companion at the sanatorium, a Polish girl who can see angels because she is not naughty. Their relationship is one of love and cruel innocence — Bronja's purity only confirms Boris's sense of his own unworthiness. **Sophroniska**: His doctor, who treats him with psychoanalytic methods and worries about his fragility. **The Schoolfellows at Vedel-Azaïs**: The boys whose cruelty drives Boris to his death, representing the callousness that the school's moral system has produced.

## Character Analysis

### Motivations

Boris is driven by a desperate desire to be good — to be worthy of seeing angels, of being loved, of existing without shame. His tragedy is that this very desire becomes pathological. The religious and moral categories he has internalized ("naughty," "good") have become a prison from which he cannot escape. His obsession with his own moral state — constantly confessing, constantly worrying — is a child's version of the Protestant anxiety that afflicts the Vedel family.

### Symbolic Role

Boris is the novel's purest victim of counterfeit moral education. He has been taught to judge himself by standards he cannot meet, to see his own naughtiness as a permanent condition. The angel he cannot see becomes the novel's most potent symbol of an inaccessible authenticity. His suicide is the logical endpoint of a system that teaches children to despise themselves.

### Development

Boris's trajectory is a downward spiral — from anxious child to psychological patient to suicide victim. He does not develop so much as progressively collapse under the weight of his own moral categories.

## Literary Context

Gide's treatment of Boris is unsparing. The scenes at the sanatorium, rendered through Edouard's journal, have a delicate, observational quality — Boris and Bronja seen through windows, overheard on terraces. The suicide is narrated indirectly, through report rather than scene, which makes it all the more devastating. Boris's death implicates everyone — the school, the family, the moral system — without letting anyone off the hook.
