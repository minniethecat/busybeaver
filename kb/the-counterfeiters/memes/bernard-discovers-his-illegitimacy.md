# Bernard Discovers His Illegitimacy

While cramming for his baccalaureate at home near the [[luxembourg-gardens]], [[Bernard Profitendieu]] discovers a packet of old love letters addressed to his mother, revealing that the man he calls father is not his biological parent. This event opens the novel and functions as its inciting incident — the catalyst that sets [[Bernard Profitendieu|Bernard]] on his journey of self-discovery, triggers [[profitendieus-investigation|Profitendieu's investigation]], and introduces the novel's central theme of [[counterfeit-coin|counterfeit]] versus authentic identity.

## Summary

On a hot [[paris]] afternoon, seventeen-year-old [[Bernard Profitendieu]], alone at home while his family is out, discovers a bundle of love letters dating from seventeen years before his birth, hidden in a secret compartment of a console table. The unsigned letters, tied with pink ribbon and addressed to his mother, make it unmistakably clear that he is not the biological son of Albéric Profitendieu, the examining magistrate who raised him. [PN=002-001] [PN=002-003] [PN=002-005]

## Context

### Narrative Position

This event opens the novel, occurring in **Part I (Paris), Chapter 1**, and serves as the inciting incident that sets the entire plot in motion. It takes place on a single afternoon, between four and six o'clock, before the magistrate returns from the law courts. The novel's famous opening line — "The time has now come for me to hear a step in the passage" (002-001) — begins in Bernard's own voice, immediately establishing the free indirect discourse that characterizes the novel's narrative method. [PN=002-001] [PN=002-007]

### Preceding Events

Bernard has been preparing for his _bachot_ (baccalaureate) exam, with only three weeks remaining. His family has left him alone — his father and elder brother at the law courts, his mother paying visits, his sister at a concert, and his youngest brother Caloub at day-school. Out of boredom or curiosity, Bernard has been amusing himself by repairing a cumbersome clock on a console table, which leads him to discover the hidden drawer beneath it. [PN=002-001] [PN=002-005]

## Detailed Account

Bernard, stripped of his coat in the stifling heat, reads through the packet of twelve letters. The date is conclusive — seventeen years old — and Bernard himself is the only possible subject. The letter is unsigned, with only an initial that could be "V" or "N." [PN=002-003]

> "What can this initial stand for? A V? It might just as well be an N... Would it be becoming to question my mother?... I'm free to imagine he's a prince. It wouldn't advance matters much to know that I was the son of a rapscallion. There's no better cure for the fear of taking after one's father, than not to know who he is." (002-004)

Bernard carefully reassembles the console table, restoring the onyx slab, glass candelabra, and clock to their places. The clock strikes four — he has set it to the right time. The act of reassembly is symbolic: he restores the surface of bourgeois order even as he prepares to abandon it. [PN=002-005] [PN=002-006]

Rather than confront his mother or the magistrate, Bernard decides immediately to leave home. He reflects that he must write a farewell letter, find temporary lodging with his friend [[Olivier Molinier]], and make his escape before six o'clock. [PN=002-007]

> "It's better to sweat than to weep." (002-002) — Bernard's laconic self-command, spoken in the stifling heat of the family home he is about to abandon.

## Consequences

### Immediate Aftermath

Bernard goes to the [[luxembourg-gardens]] to find [[Olivier Molinier|Olivier]] and secure a place to stay for the night. He arranges to sleep in Olivier's room, entering after eleven o'clock when Olivier's mother has locked the family flat. [PN=002-008] [PN=002-022] [PN=002-030]

Back home, Bernard writes a farewell letter to the magistrate and leaves it on the desk. He signs it "Bernard Profitendieu" but adds that he prefers being made a bastard — a relief rather than a grievance. [PN=003-039]

### Narrative Ripple Effects

Bernard's departure is the central rupture that drives the novel's plot:

1. **Triggers Profitendieu's investigation** into the [[counterfeit-coin]] ring — the magistrate, seeking Bernard, stumbles upon the schoolboy coin operation
2. **Brings Bernard into contact with [[Edouard]]** through the stolen suitcase containing Edouard's [[journal]]
3. **Sets in motion the convergence** of the novel's multiple storylines — Bernard becomes the connector between the Profitendieu, Molinier, Vedel, and Edouard-Laura circles
4. **Establishes the thematic key**: the idea that "it is to bastards that the future belongs" echoes through the novel (013-125, 041-032)

## Thematic Significance

The event establishes Gide's central theme of **authenticity versus counterfeit identity**. Bernard's discovery that his parentage is false mirrors the novel's broader concern with [[counterfeit-coin|counterfeit coins]], false appearances, and inauthentic living. Bernard's reaction — welcoming rather than resenting his illegitimacy — embodies Gide's ethic of freedom through the rejection of inherited constraints. The idea that not knowing one's father liberates one from the fear of resembling him speaks to the novel's celebration of self-invention over determined identity.

## Literary Technique

Gide presents this event through a combination of third-person narration and **free indirect discourse**, allowing the reader direct access to Bernard's thoughts. The opening line — "The time has now come for me to hear a step in the passage" (002-001) — begins in Bernard's own voice, blurring the boundary between narrator and character. The motif of heat and sweating creates sensory immediacy, while the clock Bernard repairs becomes a symbol of the temporal order he is about to disrupt. [PN=002-001] [PN=002-002]
