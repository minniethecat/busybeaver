# Bernard Leaves Home

After discovering his illegitimacy, Bernard Profitendieu writes a farewell letter to the man who raised him and walks out of his childhood home, beginning his life of self-invention.

## Summary

On the same afternoon he discovers the love letters, Bernard composes a dignified but cutting farewell letter to Albéric Profitendieu, the examining magistrate, declaring that he has learned he is not his son and that this knowledge brings him "immense relief." He leaves all his possessions behind and departs before anyone returns home, severing all ties with the family that raised him. [PN=003-039] [PN=003-016] [PN=003-017]

## Context

### Narrative Position

This event occurs in **Part I (Paris), Chapter 3**, immediately following the discovery in Chapter 1. The narrative shifts to Profitendieu's perspective, showing the magistrate's return home and his discovery of Bernard's letter. The event spans the same late afternoon and early evening. [PN=003-001] [PN=003-017]

### Preceding Events

Bernard has discovered the love letters revealing his illegitimacy (see [[bernard-discovers-his-illegitimacy]]). He has arranged shelter with Olivier Molinier for the night. After writing the letter, he leaves before the magistrate's return from the law courts at six o'clock. [PN=002-007] [PN=002-030]

## Detailed Account

Profitendieu returns home in a hurry, accompanied by his younger son Caloub and their clerk Antoine. Bernard's absence is immediately noted. Antoine, who has been watching the door, informs the magistrate that Bernard left and that a letter awaits him on the study desk. [PN=003-005] [PN=003-008] [PN=003-017]

Entering his study, Profitendieu finds the letter. Bernard's words are carefully chosen to wound:

> "Sir, Owing to an accidental discovery I happened to make this afternoon, I have become aware that I must cease to regard you as my father. This is an immense relief to me. Realizing as I do how little affection I feel for you, I have long suspected that I was not your son. I prefer to leave you of my own accord." [PN=003-039]

Bernard signs the letter "Bernard Profitendieu," adding that he bears no grudge for being made a bastard and that he prefers not knowing his real father. He leaves behind all his belongings, taking nothing from the household. [PN=003-039] [PN=003-016]

Profitendieu is devastated. He totters to an armchair, unable to process the blow. The narrative reveals his deep, unexpressed love for Bernard — the boy he always suspected was not his own, yet whom he loved more than his legitimate children. [PN=003-040] [PN=003-016]

## Characters Involved

- **Bernard Profitendieu** — The seventeen-year-old who writes the farewell letter and departs
- **Albéric Profitendieu** — The examining magistrate who receives the letter and is devastated by Bernard's departure
- **Antoine** — The family clerk who observes Bernard's absence and reports the letter
- **Caloub Profitendieu** — Bernard's younger half-brother, unaware of the family drama

## Consequences

### Immediate Aftermath

Profitendieu must conceal his devastation from his wife when she returns home for dinner. He struggles to maintain composure, telling her the children are not hungry and sending them away. At dinner, he can barely speak, and afterward summons his wife to his study, but cannot bring himself to reveal what he knows. [PN=003-058] [PN=003-061]

Meanwhile, Bernard spends the night walking along the Seine quays, hungry but exhilarated by his freedom. He has no money and nowhere permanent to go, but feels liberated from all constraints. [PN=007-005] [PN=007-006]

### Narrative Ripple Effects

Bernard's departure is the catalyst for the entire novel. It frees him to meet Edouard (by stealing his suitcase), to travel to Saas-Fée, and to become entangled in the counterfeit coin investigation. For Profitendieu, the loss of Bernard is a wound that never heals — it softens the stern magistrate and eventually leads him to investigate the counterfeit coin ring with unusual sympathy for the young offenders. [PN=002-008] [PN=041-032]

## Thematic Significance

Bernard's departure embodies Gide's central theme of **self-invention through rupture**. By rejecting his legal father and embracing his uncertain origins, Bernard exercises what Gide saw as the essential human freedom: the right to define oneself rather than accept inherited identity. The letter itself is an act of literary self-fashioning — Bernard composes his exit from one life as the first sentence of another. The contrast between Bernard's exhilaration and Profitendieu's grief illustrates the moral complexity of authenticity: one person's liberation is another's devastation.

## Literary Technique

Gide narrates the departure through a shift in perspective — from Bernard's consciousness in Chapter 1 to Profitendieu's in Chapter 3, creating dramatic irony and emotional complexity. The reader sees the same event from both sides: Bernard's sense of liberation and the magistrate's profound grief. The narrative uses free indirect discourse to enter Profitendieu's mind, revealing the love he could never express to the boy he raised but did not father. [PN=003-016] [PN=003-040]
