# Bernard Profitendieu

A schoolboy who discovers at seventeen that he is illegitimate, leaves his family home near the [[luxembourg-gardens]], reads [[Edouard]]'s private [[journal]], and undertakes a journey of moral and psychological self-discovery. Bernard is the novel's experiment in [[sincerity]] — a character who attempts to build an authentic self from scratch after discovering his origins are [[counterfeit-coin|counterfeit]].

## Role in the Novel

Bernard opens the novel: "The time has now come for me to hear a step in the passage" (002-001). He is one of the novel's two central young protagonists (with [[Olivier Molinier]]). His discovery of his illegitimacy — through a seventeen-year-old love letter to his mother — sets in motion his break from family and his entry into the lives of [[Edouard]], [[Laura Douviers|Laura]], and the Vedel family. The event is narrated in [[bernard-discovers-his-illegitimacy|Chapter 1]].

Bernard serves as a moral touchstone. His declaration that honesty is the highest virtue (024-042) articulates the novel's ethical standard. His decision to read [[Edouard]]'s [[journal]] (015-001) raises the question of whether knowledge can be obtained through dishonest means and still serve honest ends.

## Biography

### Background

Bernard has been raised as the son of the examining magistrate Profitendieu, in a comfortable bourgeois household near the [[luxembourg-gardens]] (002-008). At seventeen, he discovers letters revealing that his biological father is not Profitendieu but an unknown man. Rather than confront his mother, he writes his "father" a letter and leaves home.

### Key Actions

Bernard leaves home and stays temporarily with [[Olivier Molinier]]. He reads [[Edouard]]'s lost [[journal]], gaining intimate knowledge of Edouard, Olivier, and Laura (013-132–015-001). He meets [[Laura Douviers|Laura]], to whom he declares his philosophy of honesty (024-042). He becomes [[Edouard]]'s secretary. He visits the [[vedel-boarding-school]] and encounters the [[counterfeit-coin]] ring. Throughout, he navigates his own moral education — learning from Edouard's journal, Laura's suffering, and his own experiences.

### Relationships

**[[Olivier Molinier]]**: Bernard's closest friend, whose complexities Bernard discovers through [[Edouard]]'s [[journal]]. **[[Edouard]]**: Bernard's accidental mentor — he reads Edouard's journal and later works for him, forming a bond based on illicit knowledge. **[[Laura Douviers]]**: Bernard develops a complex attachment to Laura, offering her the [[sincerity|honesty]] he values. **Profitendieu**: Bernard's adoptive father, a judge whose moral authority Bernard both respects and rejects.

## Character Analysis

### Motivations

Bernard is driven by the desire for authenticity. His break from family is not primarily rebellious but existential — he cannot continue to live a life based on a false premise. His pursuit of [[sincerity|honesty]] leads him to value actions over ideas: "If you were to start from a fact and make a good exposition of it, the idea would come of its own accord" (023-063). This formulation directly influences the novel's aesthetic method and its treatment of the [[counterfeit-coin]].

### Symbolic Role

Bernard represents the possibility of honest self-construction. Unlike characters who are trapped by their pasts or their self-deceptions, Bernard attempts to build a self from scratch. He is the novel's experiment in authenticity — and the novel leaves open whether the experiment succeeds. His illegitimacy, rather than being a stigma, becomes a form of freedom: liberated from the obligation to resemble a father, he can become himself.

### Development

Bernard undergoes the novel's most significant character arc. He begins as a boy reacting against his family and ends as a young man who has learned from his experiences. His reading of [[Edouard]]'s [[journal]] is his true education — an illicit curriculum that teaches him more than any school could. The novel traces his moral education without resolving it; like [[Edouard]]'s novel, Bernard's self remains a work in progress.
