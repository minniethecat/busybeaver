# The Argonauts

A Parisian literary circle and review, the established rival to [[Comte de Passavant]]'s new _Vanguard_ review. The Argonauts host a dinner at the Taverne du Panthéon where the novel's literary world gathers — a scene that dramatizes the circulation of [[counterfeit-coin|counterfeit]] reputation and the mechanisms of literary careerism that the novel critiques.

## Overview

The Argonauts are an established literary review with a regular staff, a named editor (the Président des Brousses), and a recognizable social identity within the Parisian literary world. They represent the "elder" generation of literary institutions with which Passavant's upstart _Vanguard_ must negotiate — "He wishes our new review to be on good terms with its elder and not to set up as a rival" (016-065). The Argonauts' existence as an institution shows that the literary world Passavant seeks to dominate is already occupied by established networks with their own rituals and hierarchies.

## Role in the Novel

### Narrative Function

The Argonauts serve as the setting for one of the novel's key set-pieces: the dinner at the Taverne du Panthéon (Part I, Chapter 17). This scene brings together [[Edouard]], [[Bernard Profitendieu|Bernard]], Sarah, Passavant, the Argonauts staff, and various literary figures in a single room. The dinner functions as a concentrated display of the literary world's social mechanics — speeches, toasts, networking, and the manufacture of reputation.

> "Dinner was finished. The table had been cleared, but it was still covered with coffee-cups, bottles and glasses. Everyone was smoking and the atmosphere was stifling." (017-009) — The physical atmosphere mirrors the moral atmosphere of literary society.

The Argonauts dinner also introduces Alfred Jarry, the author of _Ubu Roi_, whose eccentric performance — "that toneless voice of his — a voice without warmth or intonation, or accent or emphasis" (017-035) — serves as a counterpoint to the conventional posturing of the other guests.

### Associated Events

The Argonauts dinner is where [[Edouard]] and [[Bernard Profitendieu|Bernard]] enter the literary world as observers, watching Justinien deliver a speech of "grandiloquent flummery" while Passavant is toasted as if _The Horizontal Bar_ were "another _Iliad_" (017-009). The scene also reveals Jarry playing with a large pistol — "with which the Argonauts had often seen him playing about" (017-120) — a detail that acquires retrospective significance in light of [[boris-at-the-sanatorium|Boris's suicide]].

## Symbolic Significance

The Argonauts represent the established literary order — the institutions that confer and regulate reputation. Their name, drawn from Greek mythology (the Argonauts who sailed with Jason), ironically suggests heroic quest, while their actual activity is the mundane manufacture of literary reputation through dinners, speeches, and toasts. They are the institutional form of the [[counterfeit-coin]] — a system in which value is assigned not by the work itself but by the network that celebrates it.

The contrast with [[Edouard]]'s solitary artistic practice is explicit: where the Argonauts generate reputation through social performance, Edouard seeks artistic truth through solitary reflection and [[journal]]-keeping. The Argonauts are what Passavant aspires to lead and surpass; Edouard is what stands outside the entire system.

## Real-World Parallels

Gide was intimately familiar with the Parisian literary world of reviews, circles, and coteries. The _Nouvelle Revue Française_ (NRF), which Gide co-founded, was itself a literary review that navigated the tensions between artistic integrity and institutional power. The Argonauts dinner may be read as Gide's satirical self-portrait of the literary world he inhabited — a world he both belonged to and critiqued.
