# Bourgeoisie

The French Protestant bourgeoisie — its interlocking institutions of family, school, church, and law — forms the social world that Gide systematically exposes as counterfeit: a system in which the performance of respectability has displaced genuine moral life.

## Definition

In _The Counterfeiters_, the bourgeoisie is less a socioeconomic class than a moral condition — a way of organizing existence around the maintenance of appearances: honourable families whose sons patronize brothels, respectable marriages built on evasion, religious piety that enforces hypocrisy.

The examining magistrate Profitendieu fears investigating a schoolboy prostitution scandal because it would "compromise the respectable families whose children are suspected" [029-046]. "Respectable" operates as both credential and alibi — the counterfeit logic the novel anatomizes.

> "A worthy bourgeois can't understand how one can be worthy in any other fashion than his own." [004-065]

This sentence captures the bourgeoisie's defining feature: the conflation of social conformity with moral worth.

## Role in The Counterfeiters

### Thematic Significance

The bourgeois family is the novel's primary social target. The plot begins with Bernard Profitendieu's discovery of his illegitimacy — the revelation that the respectable Profitendieu household rests on a counterfeit foundation [002-001]. The novel traces how this pattern reproduces through the Azaïs-Vedel school, the law courts, and the Protestant church.

Laura Douviers exemplifies the bourgeoisie's psychological grip: she is among "those loving and docile creatures who make the most devoted wives" but who "had need of the proprieties to lean on" without "the frame of her proper surroundings" [023-001]. The bourgeois "frame" is both her virtue and her prison.

> "I lean with a fearful attraction over the depths of each creature's possibilities and weep for all that lies atrophied under the heavy lid of custom and morality." [013-130]

Edouard's journal entry is the novel's most concentrated indictment of bourgeois order as a stifling of human potential.

### Character Associations

- **Oscar Molinier**: The "respectable, honest, retired bourgeois" whose confession of adultery is rendered grotesque by the "clumsiness and triviality of his words" [029-033].

- **Pastor Vedel and Old Azaïs**: Patriarchs of the Vedel-Azaïs clan, embodying bourgeois Protestantism. Azaïs is "so sincere" that "one hasn't the heart to be ironical" — yet "Azaïs forces one to be hypocritical" [013-077], demanding sincerity while making it "impossible by his own severity" [013-081].

- **Bernard Profitendieu**: The rebel whose discovery that his identity is a bourgeois counterfeit launches the plot.

- **Edouard**: The novelist-observer, born into the same Protestant bourgeoisie he anatomizes, his journal alternating between detachment and self-recognition.

## Manifestations in the Text

### Key Scenes

Laura's wedding reception at the Vedel-Azaïs chapel is the novel's most concentrated portrait of bourgeois society. Edouard records the "odour of Puritanism" peculiar to the exclusively Protestant gathering [013-040]. The chapel's "angular and colourless architecture" and "uncompromising inflexibility" [013-019] become an architectural emblem of the bourgeois spiritual condition.

At the Azaïs school, the patriarch's moral absolutism produces its opposite: the grandchildren lie systematically, not in spite of Azaïs's moral instruction but because of it.

### Symbolic Expressions

Gide represents bourgeois order through spatial imagery: the locked door between rooms at the Vedel-Azaïs reception [013-039], the "heavy lid of custom and morality" [013-130], the "stifling cover of morality and religion" [027-002]. The bourgeoisie is figured as enclosure and suffocation — a world where nothing spontaneous can survive.

The counterfeit coin extends this metaphor: like the false ten-franc piece, bourgeois respectability circulates as genuine, its worth sustained by collective agreement rather than intrinsic truth.

## Literary and Philosophical Context

Gide's treatment of the bourgeoisie reflects his own biography. Born into a wealthy Protestant family, he knew this world from within.

His critique draws on Nietzsche's diagnosis of conventional morality as life-denial and on his own Protestant obsession with sincerity transposed into an aesthetic key. The maxim that "there are no severer Puritans than their freethinkers" [008-007] captures the paradox: bourgeois morality survives even among those who have rejected its theological foundations.
