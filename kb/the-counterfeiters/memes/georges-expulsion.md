# Georges's Expulsion

In the aftermath of Boris's suicide, Pauline Molinier removes her youngest son George from the Pension Azaïs — an act of maternal protection that is also, implicitly, a judgment on the moral environment that made the tragedy possible and on George's own role in it.

## Summary

The suicide of Boris at the Pension Azaïs sends shockwaves through the community of families connected to the school. Four families immediately remove their children; the school's future is thrown into doubt. Pauline Molinier, who had already been troubled by George's behavior — including a suspected theft of a hundred-franc note during the family's summer holiday — decides to bring him home. The expulsion is not formal but is no less real: George is withdrawn from an environment in which he has been, at minimum, complicit in a fatal cruelty (045-001).

## Context

### Narrative Position

The removal occurs in the final chapters of Part III, immediately after the account of Boris's suicide. It is one of several "aftershocks" that Gide traces through the novel's community of characters, showing how a single catastrophic event radiates outward to touch every life it grazes.

### Preceding Events

George's complicity in Boris's death is established in the preceding chapters. He had participated in the conspiracy to frighten Boris, had passed Philippe's anxious note — "Are you quite sure the pistol isn't loaded?" — to Ghéridanisol without acting on his own doubts, and had, in the chaos after the shooting, helped to conceal the weapon. "George was the only one who remained convinced of his entire responsibility" — that is, Ghéridanisol's (045-001).

Earlier in the novel, Pauline had confided to Edouard her anxieties about George: "A hundred-franc note disappeared from a cupboard... I might as well admit that my suspicions fell upon him" (032-001). She had said nothing at the time, paralyzed by the fear of provoking a lie. The suicide crystallizes her unspoken fears into action.

## Detailed Account

In the chapter following Boris's death, the narrator reports the institutional consequences with documentary concision. "Azaïs has been obliged to break up the school — for the time being, he says; but Rachel is afraid of ruin. Four families have already removed their children" (045-001).

Pauline's decision to remove George is reported with similar restraint: "I have not been able to dissuade Pauline from taking George away, so that she may keep him at home with her; especially as the boy has been profoundly shaken by his schoolfellow's death, and seems inclined to reform" (045-001).

This is one of the novel's most morally complex moments. The phrase "seems inclined to reform" registers the ambiguity — is George's shock genuine repentance, or another performance? Pauline's action keeps the question open: by bringing George home, she removes him from the environment that facilitated his worst impulses, but she cannot know whether the reform will hold.

## Consequences

### Immediate Aftermath

George returns to the Molinier household, removed from Ghéridanisol's influence. The novel does not follow him closely in its final pages, leaving his moral trajectory uncertain. The pension itself — the novel's most densely populated social space — dissolves as a center of collective life.

### Narrative Ripple Effects

The scattering of the pension's inhabitants is one of the novel's concluding movements. The school, which had gathered characters from across the novel's social spectrum — the Vedel family, La Pérouse, Boris, the schoolboys, Bernard — disperses under the pressure of tragedy. What had been a concentrated microcosm of the novel's concerns becomes, in its dissolution, an emblem of the fragility of all human communities.

## Thematic Significance

George's removal from the pension raises one of the novel's most persistent questions: can individuals be reformed by changing their environment, or do they carry their capacities for good and evil with them wherever they go? Pauline's decision reflects a mother's hope that the answer is yes — that George, removed from the corrupting influence of Ghéridanisol and the conspiratorial atmosphere of the school, may yet become the person she wants him to be.

The event also underscores the novel's treatment of moral responsibility as distributed and shared rather than individual. George did not load the pistol; he did not tell Boris to fire. But he knew — or should have known — what was happening, and his failure to act makes him, in the novel's moral calculus, neither innocent nor entirely guilty.

## Literary Technique

Gide reports George's removal in a single sentence embedded in a paragraph of aftermath — a technique that subordinates individual events to the larger pattern of consequences. The brevity of the narration is itself a judgment: George's story, at this point in the novel, is no longer central. He is one of many characters whose lives have been altered by Boris's death, and the narrator's attention moves on.
