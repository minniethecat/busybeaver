# The Swiss Sanatorium

The sanatorium at Saas-Fée is the institutional heart of the novel's Part II — a health resort in the Swiss Alps where physical convalescence provides the setting for psychological revelation and moral reckoning.

## Location

The sanatorium is located in Saas-Fée, the Swiss mountain village in the Valais canton (020-001). It is both a medical institution and a residential community, housing patients from across Europe. The Polish psychoanalyst Sophroniska has brought her daughter Bronja and the boy Boris here (022-035). Vincent has been treated at a sanatorium as well (012-045), suggesting a network of such institutions across the Alpine region.

## Description in the Novel

The sanatorium is described through its social arrangements rather than its architecture. Patients and visitors gather on balconies: "Mme. Sophroniska joined them, generally on the balcony" (023-006). The children Boris and Bronja have their own world within the institution — they came from "the same home — a sanatorium, where they had been sent because of their health" (006-052). The sanatorium creates a temporary community of strangers brought together by illness and the search for cure.

The institution has a keeper who notes departures: "the keeper says, he must have left Saas-Fée two days before our arrival" (023-087). A local constable is involved when the suicide occurs (041-042). The sanatorium is both refuge and surveillance — a space of treatment that is also a space of observation.

## Narrative Function

### Scenes Set Here

All of Part II unfolds at the sanatorium. Sophroniska treats Boris and Bronja (022-035). Edouard converses with Sophroniska about psychoanalysis and the novel (022-033, 022-065). Laura seeks refuge from her failed marriage. Bernard arrives as Edouard's secretary. The children discuss angels in one of the novel's most tender scenes. Boris's secret — the talisman given him by Bronja — is revealed. The sanatorium is also the setting for the novel's future tragedy: the suicide that will occur after Boris returns to Paris and enters the Vedel school.

### Symbolic Significance

The sanatorium is the novel's space of suspended action and heightened reflection. Removed from Paris, characters can examine their lives without the distractions of bourgeois routine. The medical framework — treatment, cure, relapse — provides a metaphor for the moral condition: characters are sick with the counterfeit and seek a cure they may not find.

## Associated Characters

Sophroniska brings her daughter Bronja and the boy Boris to the sanatorium (022-035). Edouard arrives as a visitor and stays to observe and record (022-033). Laura is already in residence, recovering from her failed marriage. Bernard comes as Edouard's secretary. Vincent's recovery is reported from another sanatorium (012-045).

## Real-World Context

Alpine sanatoria were a major feature of European medical culture in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, particularly for tuberculosis treatment. They figure prominently in modernist literature — Thomas Mann's The Magic Mountain (1924) is the definitive treatment. Gide participated in this literary tradition, using the sanatorium as a space of moral and psychological exploration.
