# Bernard Becomes Edouard's Secretary

After fleeing his family home and discovering his illegitimacy, Bernard Profitendieu offers himself to the novelist Edouard as a secretary — an arrangement that provides him with both material support and intellectual mentorship, while functioning as a narrative device for Bernard's moral and psychological development.

## Summary

Bernard, having left the Profitendieu household with no resources and no plan beyond defiance, encounters Edouard at the Luxembourg Gardens and later at Olivier's home. Recognizing Edouard's interest in him and needing a means of support, Bernard proposes himself as a secretary. Edouard, who had already been considering taking Olivier as his secretary, accepts the offer in a modified form — inviting Bernard to accompany him and Laura to Switzerland, where Bernard serves as a traveling companion and informal secretary, learning through observation and conversation (002-013, 020-004).

## Context

### Narrative Position

The offer of the secretaryship occurs in Part I, Chapter XIII, shortly after Bernard's flight from home and his initial encounters with the Molinier family. The arrangement matures during the journey to Saas-Fée (Part II) and continues through the novel's remainder, though it strains under Bernard's increasingly independent judgment.

### Preceding Events

Bernard has discovered the love letters revealing he is not Profitendieu's biological son, left home, spent the night with Olivier, and begun navigating Paris on his own. Edouard has been observing Bernard with increasing interest and has already confided to his journal his desire to take Olivier as a secretary — a plan complicated by Olivier's entanglement with the Comte de Passavant (002-004, 002-010).

## Detailed Account

The arrangement begins with Bernard's audacious self-offer. After encountering Edouard and being drawn to his intelligence, Bernard declares: "And now, weren't you in need of a secretary? I can't believe I should fill the post badly — it would be with such joy" (002-013). The gesture is characteristically Bernardian — equal parts need, boldness, and genuine admiration.

Edouard accepts, though the role is initially informal. Rather than office work, Bernard accompanies Edouard and Laura Douviers to Saas-Fée, where he acts as a traveling companion, observer, and sounding board for Edouard's ideas. He educates himself through proximity to Edouard's mind, listening as Edouard discusses his ideas with Laura, taking in ideas without formal instruction (024-054).

## Consequences

### Immediate Aftermath

Bernard gains a patron, a mentor, and a structured alternative to family life. The arrangement takes him to Saas-Fée, where his education accelerates through Edouard's conversation and his exposure to Laura's emotional complexity.

### Narrative Ripple Effects

The secretaryship is not a stable arrangement but a site of continuous friction. Bernard resists Edouard's influence even as he absorbs it: "He could not endure that Edouard should have any ascendancy over him and, rather than yield to his influence, rebelled against it" (023-005). This tension eventually leads Bernard to step aside, recognizing that the role was always meant for Olivier: "It is Olivier who ought to be his secretary. As soon as Olivier is well again, I shall retire. My place is not here" (029-030). The position thus becomes a relay — Bernard holds it temporarily, then passes it to the person for whom it was originally intended, a dynamic later echoed when Olivier takes the secretaryship at Passavant's review (043-033).

## Thematic Significance

The secretaryship embodies the novel's central concern with authenticity versus performance. Bernard's assumption of the role is genuine — he truly wishes to learn — but the position itself is borrowed, destined for Olivier. His eventual recognition that "my place is not here" represents a form of moral clarity: knowing when to cede a counterfeit position is itself an act of authenticity. The secretaryship also functions as a counterpoint to the novel's other employment relationships — Passavant's hiring of Strouvilhou as secretary, for instance — contrasting mentorship with exploitation.

## Literary Technique

Gide presents the secretaryship through multiple perspectives. Edouard's journal records his ambivalence about Bernard's restlessness; Bernard's letters to Olivier reveal his genuine gratitude and intellectual excitement; and the narrator's analysis explicitly frames the arrangement as a failed experiment in influence. The multiplicity of views prevents any single interpretation from dominating.
