# Profitendieu

The examining magistrate whose discovery that Bernard is not his biological son shatters his domestic complacency, and whose professional investigation of the counterfeit coin ring connects the novel's public and private dramas of authenticity and fraud.

## Role in the Novel

Monsieur Albéric Profitendieu serves a double function. In the domestic sphere, he is the deceived husband and adoptive father whose discovery of his wife's infidelity — and of his own sterility — launches Bernard's flight from home and the novel's central plot. In the public sphere, he is the examining magistrate assigned to investigate the counterfeit coin ring that gives the novel its title. His dual role as both victim and investigator of fraud makes him a structural lynchpin connecting the novel's private and public dimensions of counterfeiting.

## Biography

### Background

Profitendieu is an examining magistrate in Paris, a man of established position and reputation. He has raised Bernard as his son (PN=003-001), unaware of his wife's past infidelity. He lives in a comfortable apartment with Bernard and his wife, attended by their maid. His life before the discovery is one of orderly bourgeois respectability — a world of fixed routines, professional authority, and unquestioned paternity.

### Key Actions

The novel opens with Profitendieu returning home early, "in a hurry to get home" (PN=003-001), having made a discovery that will unravel his domestic life. He has found letters proving Bernard is not his biological son. As he enters the house, he is detained by his talkative neighbor Antoine, who "forced Profitendieu, whose sleeve he was holding, to go as slowly as himself" (PN=003-005) — a brilliantly ironic delay that heightens the tension. When Bernard fails to appear for dinner, Profitendieu discovers a letter: "I am leaving all my things behind me, which you will soon have the right to claim, as they belong to your son. Bernard Profitendieu. P.S. I am leaving all my things belonging to Antoine behind me — they would sooner or later bring dishonour to Bernard Profitendieu" (PN=003-039). Profitendieu "totters to an armchair. He would like to think, but a barcarole... makes thinking impossible" (PN=003-040). Later in the novel, Profitendieu appears in his professional capacity, investigating the counterfeit coin scheme: "for some time past a certain number of counterfeit coins have been put into circulation" (PN=041-024).

### Relationships

**Bernard**: The son Profitendieu has raised but who is not biologically his. Bernard's departure note is both cruel and honest — he leaves because he feels he no longer belongs, returning everything that is not rightfully his. **Madame Profitendieu**: His wife, whose infidelity he has discovered. She remains an offstage presence, her guilt implied rather than dramatized. **Antoine**: The garrulous neighbor whose trivial chatter delays Profitendieu at the crucial moment, one of Gide's most effective uses of comic interruption.

## Character Analysis

### Motivations

Profitendieu is driven by the desire for order and justice — values that define him professionally as a magistrate and personally as a bourgeois patriarch. The discovery of his cuckoldry shatters these values at their foundation. His investigation of the counterfeit coins is thus doubly resonant: he pursues literal counterfeiters in his court while confronting the counterfeit of his own paternity. His motivation is a desperate attempt to restore the moral order that has collapsed around him.

### Symbolic Role

Profitendieu represents the counterfeit of bourgeois authority. He is a judge who cannot judge his own life, a guardian of the law whose domestic arrangements are built on a lie. His professional competence is real, but it cannot protect him from the fraud at his own hearth. He embodies the gap between public rectitude and private truth that the novel relentlessly exposes.

### Development

Profitendieu's development is largely negative: he is stripped of his illusions rather than transformed. The novel leaves him suspended between his professional role (investigating counterfeits) and his private crisis (discovering he has been living one).

## Literary Context

Gide opens the novel with Profitendieu's story, establishing the theme of counterfeit identity immediately. The opening chapters are a masterclass in delayed revelation — the reader does not learn exactly what Profitendieu has discovered until Bernard himself finds out. The juxtaposition of the magistrate investigating counterfeit coins with the husband discovering counterfeit paternity is one of Gide's most elegant structural ironies.

## Key Passages

> "Monsieur Profitendieu totters to an armchair. He would like to think, but a barcarole sung by a silly voice in the room near by makes thinking impossible." — Profitendieu's collapse after reading Bernard's note (PN=003-040)
