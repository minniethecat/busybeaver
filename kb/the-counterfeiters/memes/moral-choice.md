# Moral Choice

The central ethical predicament in Gide's novel — a predicament made acute by the absence of any reliable external authority and the recognition that the self is itself a shifting, unreliable guide.

## Definition

Moral choice in _The Counterfeiters_ is never the simple selection of good over evil. Gide's characters confront decisions in which the stakes are unclear, motives are mixed, and the chooser does not fully know what he wants. [[Édouard]] crystallizes this in his journal: "reality interests me inasmuch as it is plastic, and I care more — infinitely more — for what may be than for what has been. I lean with a fearful attraction over the depths of each creature's possibilities and weep for all that lies atrophied under the heavy lid of custom and morality" (013-130). The moral act is not obedience but excavation — uncovering what custom has buried.

## Role in The Counterfeiters

### Thematic Significance

The novel's title signals its central moral problem: how does one distinguish authentic from counterfeit values? The teenage boys passing false coins literalize a deeper question — are the moral codes inherited from parents, church, and school genuine currency or mere tokens?

[[Armand Vedel|Armand]] pushes this to its logical extreme in his meditation on the fragility of moral limits: "The limit of resistance to — well, for instance, to what my father would call temptation. One holds out; the cord on which the devil pulls is stretched to breaking... A tiny bit more, the cord snaps — one is damned" (035-033). Morality, in this view, is not a structure but a tension — a cord that can snap at any point.

[[Bernard Profitendieu|Bernard]]'s self-interrogation after reading his mother's letters is the novel's paradigm of moral reasoning: "Was it wrong of me to read those letters!... No, I should be feeling remorseful! And if I hadn't read the letters, I should have had to go on living in ignorance and falsehood and submission" (007-006). He does not appeal to principle; he consults his own feeling and, finding no remorse, concludes the act was justified. This is Gidean morality: sincerity to oneself over conformity to code.

### Character Associations

| Character | Moral Predicament | Resolution |
|-----------|------------------|------------|
| **Bernard** | Whether to read the letters; whether to leave home; whether to serve Édouard | Acts first, reflects afterward — the Gidean model |
| **La Pérouse** | Whether his lifelong self-denial was virtue or pride | Belated recognition: "God made me take my pride for virtue" (014-036) |
| **Vincent** | Whether to accept Lady Griffith's money and influence | Chooses corruption with full knowledge — the novel's anti-model |
| **Édouard** | Whether his detachment is wisdom or cowardice | Remains suspended — the novelist as permanent observer |

## Manifestations in the Text

### Key Scenes

Bernard's discovery of his mother's letters (002-005) and his subsequent letter of departure (003-039) form the novel's foundational moral sequence. He chooses to know rather than to remain in comfortable ignorance — a choice Gide presents as unequivocally right, even though it causes pain.

The counterfeit coin ring itself dramatizes moral choice as contagion. The boys do not individually decide to commit fraud; they drift into it through a series of small concessions, each one making the next easier. The ring is a study in moral choice as cumulative erosion rather than single decisive act.

> "Nothing that I wrote yesterday is true. Only this remains — that reality interests me inasmuch as it is plastic" (013-130) — Édouard's journal entry captures the Gidean view that moral truth, like aesthetic truth, is never final but always subject to revision.

### Symbolic Expressions

Counterfeit coins are the novel's master symbol for moral choice: the false currency circulates undetected precisely because it resembles the genuine article. The moral life, Gide suggests, is perpetually at risk of passing counterfeit values as authentic ones. [[Sophroniska]]'s therapeutic approach to Boris — making him "ashamed of having preferred the possession of imaginary goods to the real goods which are the reward of effort" (025-017) — extends the metaphor to psychology: moral health consists in learning to prefer the real to the counterfeit.

## Literary and Philosophical Context

Gide's treatment of moral choice reflects his reading of [[Friedrich Nietzsche|Nietzsche]] (the transvaluation of values), [[Fyodor Dostoevsky|Dostoevsky]] (the psychology of transgression), and his own Protestant formation (the imperative of self-examination, stripped of its theological guarantee). The novel's innovation is to present morality as an aesthetic problem — not "what is right?" but "what is authentic?"
