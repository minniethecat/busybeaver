# Counterfeit

The central metaphor of Gide's novel, operating simultaneously at the literal level — a ring of schoolboys passing false coins — and at the figurative level, where it encompasses questions of authenticity in art, morality, identity, and love.

## Definition

Counterfeiting in _The Counterfeiters_ is the production and circulation of something that presents itself as genuine but is not. The concept radiates outward from the false ten-franc piece that young George passes in a tobacco shop to encompass nearly every character and relationship in the novel. It names the gap between what is professed and what is true — in feeling, in art, in moral commitment (PN-023-060, PN-023-063).

## Role in The Counterfeiters

### Thematic Significance

The counterfeit coin is the novel's organizing symbol. Edouard, who is writing a novel also called _The Counterfeiters_, explains: "Has it ever happened to you to hold a counterfeit coin in your hands?" and declares his method: "I should begin by showing the counterfeit coin — the little ten-franc piece" (PN-023-060, PN-023-063). The novel's structure mirrors this ambition — it circulates among multiple perspectives, asking the reader to judge what is authentic at each turn.

### Character Associations

The schoolboy ring — George, Ghéridanisol, Phiphi — operates the literal counterfeiting scheme, passing false coins supplied by Strouvilhou's network (PN-036-063). But the novel suggests that each major character is engaged in their own form of counterfeiting. Passavant counterfeits literary authority through wealth rather than talent. Vincent counterfeits devotion to Laura while planning his desertion. Edouard himself fears his novel may become counterfeit — "too factitious," as his friend X warns, risking "letting go the real subject for the shadow of the subject in my brain" (PN-012-048).

## Manifestations in the Text

### Key Scenes

The conversation at Saas-Fée in which Edouard discusses the novel's title with Bernard, Sophroniska, and Laura is the conceptual center of the theme. Bernard proposes: "If I were writing _The Counterfeiters_ I should begin by showing the counterfeit coin — the little ten-franc piece you were speaking of just now" (PN-023-063).

> "Has it ever happened to you to hold a counterfeit coin in your hands?... I should begin by showing the counterfeit coin — the little ten-franc piece you were speaking of just now." (PN-023-060, PN-023-063)

Monsieur Profitendieu's revelation to Edouard grounds the symbol in criminal reality: "For some time past a certain number of counterfeit coins have been put into circulation. I know, however, that young George — quite innocently, I am willing to believe — is one of those who circulate them" (PN-041-024).

### Symbolic Expressions

The false coin is a portable symbol — an object passed from hand to hand, from scene to scene, accumulating meaning with each transfer. Its smallness (a ten-franc piece) belies the scale of the moral questions it raises. Its circulation among schoolboys links counterfeiting to adolescence as a period of moral formation — and deformation.

> "For some time past a certain number of counterfeit coins have been put into circulation." (PN-041-024)

## Edouard's Novel-within-the-Novel

Edouard's own project — a novel called _The Counterfeiters_ — is the novel's most self-reflexive gesture. He wants a book that admits its artifice rather than passing itself off as transparent representation: "The novel has never known that 'formidable erosion of contours,' as Nietzsche calls it; that deliberate avoidance of life, which gave style to the works of the Greek dramatists" (PN-023-013). His struggle to write authentically about inauthenticity itself becomes the novel's method.
