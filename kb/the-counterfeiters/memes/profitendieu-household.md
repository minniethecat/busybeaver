# The Profitendieu Household

The Profitendieu household is the domestic world that Bernard must leave — the respectable bourgeois home whose moral order is revealed, at the novel's opening, to be built on a lie.

## Location

The home is located near the Luxembourg Gardens in Paris: "The street where Bernard Profitendieu had lived until then was quite close to the Luxembourg Gardens" (002-008). Bernard had lived in this house his entire life until the day he discovers his illegitimacy and walks out.

## Description in the Novel

Gide presents the Profitendieu household primarily through its rhythms of masculine authority and domestic routine. "Monsieur Profitendieu was in a hurry to get home" (003-001) — the father's arrival structures the household's time. Bernard's younger brother, "whose sleeve he was holding," forces Profitendieu to slow his pace (003-005), suggesting the physical claims children make on a distracted father.

The home is a space of ignorance managed by decorum. Bernard's mother has kept the secret of his paternity, and Monsieur Profitendieu "had had every reason to be satisfied" with his household arrangements — until the discovery (003-007). "As Bernard never left home, Profitendieu, whom Antoine was watching out for," was unable to discover the truth (003-017). The household's walls contain secrets that decorum cannot indefinitely suppress.

When the revelation comes, the domestic order shatters: "as Profitendieu entered the room, he saw" the evidence of his wife's betrayal (003-022). "Profitendieu felt his patience giving way" (003-030) — the controlled patriarch loses his composure within his own walls.

## Narrative Function

### Scenes Set Here

The novel opens in and around this household: Bernard stays home "to cram for his baccalaureate" (002-001) while his schoolfellows are at the Luxembourg. He discovers the letters that prove his illegitimacy. He writes his farewell note and leaves. Monsieur Profitendieu returns home, learns of Bernard's discovery, and confronts his wife. The household never recovers its equilibrium.

### Symbolic Significance

The Profitendieu household represents the bourgeois domestic order in its most apparently stable form — a respected magistrate, a beautiful wife, well-raised children, a comfortable home near the Luxembourg. The revelation of Bernard's illegitimacy exposes the counterfeit at the heart of this order: the family that appears most respectable is the one founded on the deepest deception.

## Associated Characters

Bernard Profitendieu is the son who flees. Monsieur Profitendieu is the father whose authority collapses. Madame Profitendieu is the mother whose secret destroys the family's moral claim. Antoine is the younger brother who knows more than anyone suspects. Caloub, the youngest, is an innocent bystander.

## Real-World Context

The Profitendieu household reflects the world of the French Protestant haute bourgeoisie — magistrates, professionals, families of sober respectability — that Gide knew from his own upbringing. The magistrate's family living near the Luxembourg was a recognizable social type in 1920s Paris.
