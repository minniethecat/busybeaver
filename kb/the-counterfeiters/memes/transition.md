# Transition

The underlying condition of Gide's fictional world — a state of perpetual becoming in which no character remains fixed, no belief goes untested, and the novel's open form mirrors its central conviction that identity is a process rather than a destination.

## Definition

Transition in _The Counterfeiters_ is the movement between states — from innocence to experience, from false belief to painful knowledge, from one self-conception to another. Gide does not present characters who are; he presents characters who are becoming.

The novel's very structure — a journal-within-a-novel that refuses closure — enacts this principle at the formal level.

[[Bernard Profitendieu]] tells [[Olivier Molinier|Olivier]]: "I have already changed a great deal; that is, my mind's landscape is not at all what it was the day I left home" (024-011). This self-awareness of transformation, rather than the transformation itself, is the mark of a Gidean character.

## Role in The Counterfeiters

### Thematic Significance

Transition is the engine of the novel's plot. Every significant event — Bernard's discovery of his [[illegitimacy]] (002-005), his departure from the Profitendieu household (003-039), his subsequent service to [[Édouard]], Olivier's fraught relationship with [[Comte de Passavant]], Vincent's corruption by [[Lady Griffith|Lilian]], Laura's pregnancy and retreat — functions as a catalyst for change. The narrator observes midway through the novel: "We have already seen Bernard change; passions may come which will modify him still more" (027-005). This is not merely a remark about one character; it is the novel's operating principle.

The concept of transition also structures the novel's moral vision. Characters who resist change — those who cling to fixed identities or received codes — fare poorly. [[Azaïs]] and the Pension Vedel represent institutional stasis, a "poisonous air" under "the stifling cover of morality and religion" (027-002). [[La Pérouse]], trapped in the prison of his own pride, asks plaintively: "I have only just begun to understand that I have been a dupe during the whole of my life" (014-022). His tragedy is that he understood too late.

## Manifestations in the Text

### Key Scenes

Bernard's discovery of his mother's letters and his subsequent letter to "Monsieur Profitendieu" (003-039) is the novel's archetypal transition scene. He does not merely leave a house; he renounces an identity: "I prefer knowing I am not your son at all." The rest of the novel traces the consequences of this rupture — what fills the void left by the discarded self.

The Saas-Fee interlude (Chapters 19–23) functions as a transitional space — a mountain setting removed from Paris where characters reconfigure their relationships. [[Sophroniska]] tells Édouard that Boris "might have been cured without being frightened" if only "the change of life which was made necessary by his father's death" had been enough (025-017). The therapeutic power of changed circumstances is a recurring Gidean theme.

> "The difficulty in life is to take the same thing seriously for long at a time." (007-006) — Bernard's reflection captures the restless, transitional quality that Gide values: fidelity to one's own becoming trumps fidelity to any fixed principle.

### Symbolic Expressions

The *suitcase* Bernard steals from Édouard at the railway station (011-012) is transition materialized — a portable identity, borrowed and subject to return. The train journey itself is the novel's master symbol for transition: departure, movement between places, the suspension of ordinary life that makes self-reinvention possible. Windows — Bernard sweating by his open window at the novel's opening (002-001), La Pérouse opening and shutting windows in argument with his wife — mark the boundary between stasis and change.

## Literary and Philosophical Context

Gide's treatment of transition owes much to [[Friedrich Nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s concept of self-overcoming and to [[Fyodor Dostoevsky|Dostoevsky]]'s psychology of moral crisis. Unlike the classical *Bildungsroman*, which charts a progression toward maturity, Gide's novel presents transition as potentially endless — the novel closes without resolution because, in the Gidean view, resolution would be falsification. [[Édouard]]'s journal, the novel's internal mirror, captures this: "Was I right to republish this old stuff? It responds to nothing that I care for at present. But it is only at present that I see it does not" (013-010). The writer, like his characters, is always in transit.
