# Examination

A recurring motif in [[André Gide]]'s _The Counterfeiters_ that operates both as a literal event — the French *bachot* (baccalaureate) that multiple characters prepare for — and as a metaphor for the moral testing that defines the novel's vision of human development.

## Definition

Examination in Gide's novel is the institutionalized moment of judgment that marks the boundary between childhood and adult life. For the adolescent characters — [[Bernard Profitendieu]], [[Olivier Molinier]], and their peers — the *bachot* is not merely an academic hurdle but a symbolic gateway: passing it confers entry into the adult world of careers, responsibilities, and social recognition. Gide uses the examination as a structural device to mark time in the narrative, but also as a lens through which to examine (the word's double sense) the authenticity of his characters' aspirations.

## Role in The Counterfeiters

### Thematic Significance

The novel opens with Bernard "cramming for his *bachot*" with "only three more weeks before him" (002-001) — immediately establishing examination as the context within which existential crisis unfolds. Bernard's discovery of his [[illegitimacy]] occurs while he is ostensibly studying, and his decision to leave home transforms the examination from an academic obligation into an existential one. He decides that "he can do without the last lectures and finish preparing for his examination by himself" (004-098), asserting independence from institutional education at the very moment he needs it most.

The examination functions as a measure of sincerity throughout the novel. Characters who pursue examinations earnestly (Bernard, Olivier) are distinguished from those who treat them with contempt. Strouvilhou, the novel's embodiment of nihilistic detachment, "was supposed to be attending lectures, but when he was asked which ones, or what examinations he was studying for, he used to answer negligently" (013-048). The examination becomes a test not of knowledge but of willingness to submit to social form — and the novel's judgment on those who refuse is ambivalent.

## Manifestations in the Text

### Key Scenes

Bernard's examination preparation frames the novel's opening. The heat of his room, the open window, his sweating forehead — these physical details (002-001) establish examination as a bodily ordeal, not just an intellectual one. The contrast between the institutional clock (three weeks until the exam) and the personal clock (discovering one's father is not one's father) creates the novel's characteristic tension between social time and existential time.

[[Albéric Profitendieu]]'s address to his children after Bernard's departure uses the examination metaphor unconsciously: "I don't like to see you standing there as if you were in front of an examiner" (003-067). The judge, an examiner by profession, cannot escape the language of testing even in his most intimate moments.

> "Good luck to you!" said [[Édouard]], raising his glass. "When is the examination?" (010-010) — Édouard's toast to Bernard reduces the uncertain future to a single question, acknowledging the examination as the horizon of adolescent life.

By the novel's midpoint, Olivier has "just passed his *bachot* rather brilliantly" (029-011), and his mother rewards him with permission to travel. The examination passed becomes the credential that unlocks adult freedom — but Olivier's brilliance is ironic, given the moral confusion that awaits him.

### Symbolic Expressions

Gide uses the examination as a metaphor for the novel's own method. The narrative itself "examines" its characters — Édouard's journal is a sustained examination of motive and meaning. The counterfeit coins that circulate through the plot are subject to examination (are they real or false?), just as the characters are. The metaphorical resonance extends to the reader: Gide's open-ended novel is an examination without a final answer key.

## Literary and Philosophical Context

The French *bachot* was, in Gide's time, the definitive rite of passage for the bourgeoisie — the gatekeeper of class reproduction. Gide's treatment of it reflects his ambivalent relationship to institutions: he valued intellectual rigor but distrusted the conformity that examinations enforce. The novel's critique of the examination-as-gatekeeper aligns with [[Friedrich Nietzsche]]'s skepticism toward institutional morality: "The bastard alone has the right to be natural," as the novel puts it (013-125) — the one who is outside the system of legitimate inheritance is also free of its examinations.
