# L'Éducation Sentimentale

Gustave Flaubert's 1869 novel _L'Éducation Sentimentale_ (Sentimental Education), the story of Frédéric Moreau's passage from provincial youth to Parisian disillusionment. Referenced explicitly by [[Edouard]] as an example of the kind of novel whose "story of the work — of its gestation" would be "more interesting than the work itself" (023-026), and appearing as the epigraph to Part III, Flaubert's novel serves as _The Counterfeiters_'s most significant 19th-century intertext — a model of the novel of disenchantment that Gide both inherits and transforms.

## Overview

_L'Éducation Sentimentale_ traces the life of Frédéric Moreau from his arrival in Paris as a young provincial in 1840 through the Revolution of 1848 to middle age. Frédéric's sentimental education — his failed love for Madame Arnoux, his missed opportunities, his drift through a life of compromised ambitions — makes the novel a study in passivity and disillusionment. Flaubert's innovation was to make a protagonist defined not by heroic action but by his inability to act decisively.

The novel is a touchstone of French literary realism and a key work in the tradition of the _Bildungsroman_ (novel of education/formation) that Gide's _The Counterfeiters_ consciously engages and subverts.

## Appearance in The Counterfeiters

### Context of Mention

[[Edouard]] references _L'Éducation Sentimentale_ during his discussion with Sophroniska about his notebook method for writing _The Counterfeiters_. He imagines how valuable it would be to have the notebooks of great novelists: "Just think how interesting such a notebook kept by Dickens or Balzac would be; if we had the diary of the _Education Sentimentale_ or of _The Brothers Karamazov_! — the story of the work — of its gestation! How thrilling it would be — more interesting than the work itself" (023-026).

> "Just think how interesting such a notebook kept by Dickens or Balzac would be; if we had the diary of the _Education Sentimentale_ or of _The Brothers Karamazov_!" (023-026)

The reference positions Flaubert's novel within [[Edouard]]'s aesthetic theory: the novel as a record of its own making, the process of composition as the true subject. [[Edouard]] wants to write the novel that includes its own gestation — and in citing Flaubert, he acknowledges his debt to the 19th-century tradition while claiming to surpass it.

Part III opens with an epigraph from _L'Éducation Sentimentale_: "Flaubert: _L'Education Sentimentale_" (028-001), marking the novel's return to [[paris]] after the [[saas-fee]] interlude under the sign of Flaubertian disillusionment.

### Narrative Function

The reference to Flaubert serves multiple functions. It aligns _The Counterfeiters_ with the tradition of the French novel of disillusionment — the _roman d'apprentissage_ in which the protagonist learns not how to succeed but how thoroughly success is hollow. [[Bernard Profitendieu]]'s journey, like Frédéric Moreau's, is a sentimental education — but where Flaubert's protagonist fails through passivity, Gide's protagonist actively chooses to construct himself. Gide's novel is Flaubert's inverted: an education in authenticity rather than an education in disillusionment.

## Gide's Relationship to the Work

Gide was deeply engaged with Flaubert throughout his career. He admired Flaubert's stylistic rigor — the famous _mot juste_ — while rejecting the realist conventions that Flaubert perfected. Gide's own aesthetic, articulated through [[Edouard]]'s theory of the [[pur]] novel, is in part a response to Flaubert: the novel stripped of elements that do not "specifically belong to the novel" (009-025) is a modernist answer to Flaubert's total representation of social reality.

Gide's choice of _L'Éducation Sentimentale_ — rather than _Madame Bovary_, Flaubert's more famous novel — is significant. _L'Éducation Sentimentale_ was Flaubert's most formally innovative work, poorly received in its time but later recognized as a precursor to the modernist novel. Gide's reference is an act of literary filiation: he claims Flaubert not as a realist forebear but as a proto-modernist ancestor.
