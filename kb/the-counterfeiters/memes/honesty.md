# Honesty

In Andre Gide's _The Counterfeiters_, honesty is not merely truth-telling but a quality of being — the condition of "ringing true" with "a pure, authentic sound," explicitly opposed to the counterfeit values that pervade social life.

## Definition

Bernard articulates honesty as his highest value in a passionate monologue to Laura: "if anyone were to ask me today what virtue I considered the finest, I should answer without hesitation — honesty" (024-042). But Bernard's honesty is not conventional truthfulness. It is an existential quality: "to be worth exactly what one seems to be worth — not to try to seem to be worth more." The problem, as Bernard sees it, is that people are "so much occupied with seeming, that one ends by not knowing what one really is" (024-042). Honesty is the recovery of authentic being beneath the accumulated layers of social performance.

## Role in The Counterfeiters

### Thematic Significance

Honesty is the moral answer to the novel's counterfeit problem. If inauthenticity circulates like false currency — accepted because no one examines it closely — then honesty is the act of refusing to participate in the circulation. Bernard's declaration is provoked by hearing a tourist boast of cheating customs: "Robbing the State is robbing no one" (024-042). The tourist's casual dishonesty, justified by a cynical view of institutions, crystallizes for Bernard everything he wants to reject.

Gide complicates honesty by showing its cost. Honesty requires Bernard to break with his family and class. It requires Edouard to abandon the consolations of self-deception. Laura cannot be honest without acknowledging the failure of her marriage and her affair. Honesty is the hardest virtue because it demands the destruction of comfortable illusions.

### Character Associations

**Bernard** is the novel's theorist and practitioner of honesty — his journey is toward self-knowledge without self-deception. **Laura** struggles with honesty — she wants to be honest but fears its consequences. **Profitendieu**, the examining magistrate, represents institutional dishonesty — the law that claims to seek truth but protects privilege. **Passavant** is honesty's opposite — a man whose artistic and personal life are entirely constructed for effect.

## Manifestations in the Text

### Key Scenes

Bernard's honesty monologue (024-042) is the novel's most explicit moral statement. Speaking to Laura in the Swiss sanatorium, he describes his transformation from anarchist to conservative — not because he has come to love authority but because he has come to value the honesty that authority, at its best, depends on.

The question of women's honesty receives separate treatment: "As if what is called 'honesty' in woman did not always imply resignation!" (039-036). Gide suggests that the social construction of female virtue makes honesty particularly difficult — what is called honesty in women is often compliance dressed in moral language.

> "To be worth exactly what one seems to be worth — not to try to seem to be worth more." (024-042)

### Symbolic Expressions

The counterfeit coin is the negative of Bernard's honest self. Just as the false coin is "not worth two sous" but passes for ten francs as long as undetected, the dishonest person passes for valuable as long as unexamined. Bernard's ideal is to be, as it were, a genuine coin — whose value is intrinsic, not dependent on the credulity of others.

## Literary and Philosophical Context

Gide's treatment of honesty draws on the French moralist tradition (La Rochefoucauld, Pascal) that analyzed self-deception with ruthless precision. It also reflects Gide's reading of Nietzsche, for whom honesty (Redlichkeit) was the cardinal intellectual virtue. The Protestant emphasis on conscience and self-examination provides a third context — Bernard's honesty is a secularized version of the Puritan examination of conscience.
