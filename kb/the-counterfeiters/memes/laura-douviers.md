# Laura Douviers

A married woman abandoned by her lover Vincent Molinier, pregnant with his child, who turns to Edouard for help — her situation catalyzes the novel's exploration of love, fidelity, and the suffering caused by the gap between desire and obligation.

## Role in the Novel

Laura is the novel's primary figure of female suffering and moral complexity. Her letter to Edouard — "To whom can I appeal but you?" (009-003) — sets in motion Edouard's return to Paris and his subsequent involvement in the lives of the other characters. She is the abandoned mistress of Vincent Molinier, the former intimate of Edouard, and the occasion for Bernard's most articulate moral reflections.

Laura's role is to embody the cost of others' freedom. Vincent's self-liberation is purchased at the price of her devastation. The novel never lets the reader forget that the young men's experiments in authenticity have real victims.

## Biography

### Background

Laura is the daughter of the Vedel-Azaïs family, sister to Rachel and Sarah Vedel. She was raised in a devout Protestant household. She married Douviers, a man she does not love, and had an affair with Vincent Molinier.

### Key Actions

Laura writes to Edouard from a sanatorium in the South of France, pregnant and abandoned. Edouard comes to her aid. She travels to the Swiss sanatorium at Saas-Fée where she meets Bernard. Bernard, having read Edouard's journal, knows her situation and offers her his philosophy of honesty (024-042). She asks Edouard for the counterfeit coin as a memento (024-060).

### Relationships

**Vincent Molinier**: Laura's lover who abandoned her when she became pregnant — his moral failure is one of the novel's central judgments. **Edouard**: Laura's former intimate who still cares for her but cannot offer what she needs. **Bernard Profitendieu**: A young man who, knowing her history through Edouard's journal, offers her unexpected understanding. **Douviers**: Laura's husband, a "good" man whom she does not love.

## Character Analysis

### Motivations

Laura is motivated by survival — emotional and physical. She is not a theorist of her own condition but someone who must live through it. Her suffering is not intellectualized but endured.

### Symbolic Role

Laura represents the moral reality that the novel's intellectual characters tend to abstract. Where Edouard and Vincent theorize about freedom and sincerity, Laura experiences their consequences. She is the novel's reminder that moral questions are lived, not merely discussed.

### Development

Laura's development is a movement from desperate dependence toward a fragile independence. Her request for the counterfeit coin as a memento suggests an acceptance of the complexity of her situation — the coin is false, but her feeling for Edouard is real.
