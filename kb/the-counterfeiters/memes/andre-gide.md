# André Gide

The French author (1869–1951) whose novel _The Counterfeiters_ represents the culmination of his experimental literary techniques and his lifelong exploration of sincerity, freedom, and the relationship between art and life.

## Role in the Novel

Gide does not appear directly in _The Counterfeiters_, but his presence permeates every level of the text. The character of [[Edouard]] — a novelist writing a novel also called _The Counterfeiters_ — is widely recognized as Gide's self-portrait as artist. Through Edouard's [[journal]], Gide externalizes his own aesthetic theories while maintaining the ironic distance that allows the reader to judge Edouard's limitations as well as his insights. The novel's formal innovations — the use of multiple narrative perspectives, the incorporation of the author's journal, the mise en abyme of a novel within the novel — are all expressions of Gide's determination to liberate the novel from what he saw as the tyranny of realist convention.

## Relationship to the Novel's Themes

### Sincerity and Authenticity

Gide's lifelong preoccupation with [[sincerity]] — the difficulty of being truthful with oneself and others — is the novel's central moral problem. The concept of the _acte gratuit_ (gratuitous act), though not named in the novel, underlies characters' seemingly unmotivated actions: Bernard's departure from home, Vincent's desertion of Laura, Olivier's suicide attempt. Gide believed that authentic selfhood required liberation from inherited moral frameworks, a conviction that shapes the novel's treatment of [[rebellion]], [[illegitimacy]], and [[coming-of-age]].

### The Counterfeit Motif

Gide chose counterfeiting as his central metaphor to explore the problem of authenticity in modern life. The false coins circulated by the schoolboys literalize a concern that extends to every domain the novel touches: counterfeit emotions, counterfeit art, counterfeit social relations. Gide's Protestant upbringing — with its emphasis on inner truth and its suspicion of outward display — informs this pervasive skepticism about surfaces.

### Narrative Experiment

The novel's formal audacity — Edouard's stated desire to write a "pur" novel stripped "of every element that does not specifically belong to the novel" (PN-023-013) — expresses Gide's ambition to renew the genre. The novel invokes [[Friedrich Nietzsche]]'s concept of the "formidable erosion of contours" as a model for what fiction might achieve: "that deliberate avoidance of life, which gave style to the works of the Greek dramatists" (PN-023-013).

## Literary Context

Gide wrote _The Counterfeiters_ over a period of years, publishing it in 1925 after having established himself as one of France's leading literary figures. The novel represents a departure from his earlier, more conventionally structured works (such as _The Immoralist_ and _Strait Is the Gate_) toward a deliberately fragmentary, polyphonic form. Gide's engagement with [[Fyodor Dostoevsky]]'s novels — he lectured on Dostoevsky and admired his psychological penetration — is evident in the novel's treatment of moral ambiguity and its refusal of simple judgments. The influence of [[Oscar Wilde]], whom Gide met and admired, can be detected in the novel's treatment of the relationship between art and morality.
