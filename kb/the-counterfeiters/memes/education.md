# Education

In Andre Gide's _The Counterfeiters_, education is the institutional and familial project of moral formation — a project that the novel systematically calls into question, showing how conventional upbringing fails to produce the honesty and independence it claims to value.

## Definition

Education in _The Counterfeiters_ operates on multiple levels: the formal education of the boarding school, the moral education provided (or failed) by families, and the self-education undertaken by characters like Bernard who reject the education offered to them. The old generation's confidence in education is expressed by characters like the examining magistrate who believes that "a wholesome education" instills the right values (005-003). The novel shows this confidence to be misplaced — the best-educated young people are the most skilled at hiding their real lives.

## Role in The Counterfeiters

### Thematic Significance

Education is the novel's testing ground for the older generation's values. Pastor Vedel takes charge of his grandchildren's "moral bringing-up, as well as their education" (013-078) — yet his grandchildren (Armand, Sarah) are the most rebellious characters in the novel. The failure of conventional education is not accidental but structural: education that imposes values from without cannot produce the inward honesty the novel values.

Against this failed education, Gide proposes alternative models. Edouard's observation of nature — "an orchard or a garden is an excellent school!" (018-002) — suggests an education based on observation rather than precept. Bernard's self-education through experience and reading represents the novel's preferred model: learning that emerges from life rather than being imposed on it.

### Character Associations

**Bernard** is the novel's exemplary self-educator — he leaves the family and school that would form him and undertakes his own formation. **Pastor Vedel** represents institutional education at its most well-intentioned and ineffective. **Armand** and **Sarah** are the products and critics of Vedel's education. **Profitendieu** represents the law's educational ambition — to correct and reform — which the novel shows to be as counterfeit as the coins it pursues.

## Manifestations in the Text

### Key Scenes

Edouard's meditation on horticulture as pedagogy (018-002) is the novel's most explicit alternative education theory. Observing that only the terminal buds develop naturally while the buds nearest the trunk remain dormant, he draws the analogy: "the most recalcitrant plants, which, if left to themselves, would no doubt have produced nothing but leaves, are induced to bear fruit" through pruning. Education, properly understood, is not the imposition of form but the redirection of energy.

Bernard's reading of Edouard's journal (013-132–015-001) is an act of self-education — illicit, unguided, transformative. He learns about Edouard, Olivier, Laura, and himself through an act that no pedagogue would sanction.

> "An orchard or a garden is an excellent school! and a horticulturist would often make the best of pedagogues!" (018-002)

### Symbolic Expressions

The boarding school is the novel's central institutional space — a place where young people are gathered for education but where the real learning happens outside the curriculum. The counterfeit coin ring operates through the school, making the institution itself the vector of corruption. La Pérouse, the music-master, represents the marginalized educator — one who has something genuine to teach but has been reduced to material poverty.

## Literary and Philosophical Context

Gide's treatment of education reflects his reading of Rousseau (_Émile_) and his critique of institutional religion. The Protestant emphasis on individual conscience informs the novel's preference for self-education over institutional formation. The horticultural metaphor draws on a long tradition (from Plutarch to Montaigne) of comparing education to cultivation.
