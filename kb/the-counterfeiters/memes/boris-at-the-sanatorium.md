# Boris at the Sanatorium

At Sophroniska's sanatorium in Saas-Fée, the troubled boy Boris and the delicate girl Bronja hold conversations about angels and the possibility of seeing them — a childlike mysticism that will end in tragedy.

## Summary

In the rarefied atmosphere of the Swiss sanatorium, Boris — the fragile grandson of old La Pérouse — and Bronja, the doctor Sophroniska's exquisite daughter, engage in intense, whispered conversations about angels, prayer, and the possibility of transcending the material world. Their childlike spirituality exists in poignant contrast to the adult world's corruption and the sanatorium's clinical environment, foreshadowing the novel's tragic conclusion. [PN=022-001] [PN=022-023] [PN=023-006]

## Context

### Narrative Position

This event unfolds across **Part II, Chapters 4-5 (Saas-Fée)** , recorded largely through Edouard's observation and journal entries. The conversations between Boris and Bronja are interleaved with the adult discussions of literature, psychology, and morality that dominate the Saas-Fée sojourn. [PN=022-001] [PN=023-006]

### Preceding Events

Boris has been brought to the sanatorium by Sophroniska, the Polish psychoanalyst, who is treating him for a nervous disorder characterized by compulsive behaviors and what she calls "tics." Boris is the grandson of La Pérouse, the aged music-master, and was educated in Poland after his parents' deaths. Bronja is Sophroniska's young daughter, a child of "exquisite" sensitivity who shares Boris's otherworldly preoccupations. [PN=022-001] [PN=022-034] [PN=014-065]

## Detailed Account

The conversations between Boris and Bronja are reported by Edouard, who observes them from a careful distance. Bronja's voice and presence are described as "more angelic than human." She speaks to Boris in a language of shared fantasy, and he responds with awkward, intense sincerity. [PN=022-001]

Their central preoccupation is angels — whether such beings exist, whether they can be seen, and whether prayer can summon them. In one key exchange, Boris asks:

> "How do you know when it's true? I believed you the other day when you told me about the angels. I say, Bronja, do you think that, if I were to pray very hard, I should see them too?" [PN=022-023]

Bronja's response is telling: she says Boris cannot see the angels because he is "naughty." [PN=022-025]

The sanatorium setting provides an ironic frame. Sophroniska treats Boris's behavior as pathological, yet the adult world surrounding these children is rife with far deeper pathologies — Vincent's madness, Passavant's cynicism, the schoolboy coin ring. Boris's angel-talk, which Sophroniska considers a symptom, may be the sanest response to the world the novel depicts. [PN=023-006]

The conversations are conducted in secrecy, often during walks when Boris and Bronja can escape adult supervision. Their private world exists as a fragile counter-reality, threatened by the clinical and moral structures closing in around them. [PN=023-006]

## Characters Involved

- **Boris** — La Pérouse's grandson, a troubled boy whose compulsive behaviors mask a spiritual sensitivity that the adult world cannot accommodate
- **Bronja** — Sophroniska's daughter, an "exquisite creature" whose voice is "more angelic than human" and who initiates Boris into the world of angelic belief
- **Sophroniska** — Bronja's mother and Boris's doctor, whose clinical perspective cannot fully comprehend the spiritual dimensions of the children's talk
- **Edouard** — The observing novelist, who records the children's conversations with the same analytical attention he gives to adult dramas

## Consequences

### Immediate Aftermath

The angel conversations create a bond between Boris and Bronja that will prove fatal. When Boris later returns to Paris and enters the Vedel-Azaïs school, his memories of Bronja and her angels become a sustaining fantasy — and a vulnerability that the older schoolboys will exploit. The purity of the children's world at the sanatorium makes Boris ill-equipped to survive the moral squalor of the boarding school. [PN=022-035]

### Narrative Ripple Effects

The angel motif returns in the novel's climactic tragedy: Boris, manipulated by the sadistic schoolboy Ghéridanisol into believing that a suicide will allow him to "see the angels" and reunite with Bronja (who has died), shoots himself in the classroom. Thus the children's mystical conversations at Saas-Fée, seemingly innocent, become the psychological groundwork for the novel's darkest moment — a suicide engineered by the cynical manipulation of exactly the angelic belief that Bronja nurtured. [PN=025-024]

## Thematic Significance

The Boris-Bronja conversations represent the novel's counter-image to its dominant theme of **counterfeiting**: the possibility of genuine innocence. The children's angel-talk is not counterfeit — they genuinely believe what they say — but the adult world corrupts it by treating it as pathology and, ultimately, weaponizing it. The tragedy of Boris is that authentic spirituality cannot survive in a world of counterfeit values. The sanatorium itself, designed to cure, becomes a site where a deeper sickness — the impossibility of protecting innocence — is revealed.

## Literary Technique

Gide presents these conversations almost entirely through reported speech, filtered through Edouard's narrative. The effect is one of fragile distance — we never inhabit the children's consciousness directly, only observe them from outside, as Edouard does. This technique preserves the mystery of their inner lives while making their vulnerability palpable. The sanatorium setting functions as a **theatrical space**: a sealed environment where fundamental questions can be explored in isolation from normal social constraints.
