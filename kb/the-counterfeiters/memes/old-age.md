# Old Age

The counterpoint to the novel's dominant concern with [[adolescence]] — a state of retrospective disillusion in which elderly characters reckon with lives built on self-deception, pride, and the slow accumulation of regret.

## Definition

Old age in _The Counterfeiters_ is not a biological condition but an existential one. The novel's elderly characters — [[La Pérouse]], [[Azaïs]], Madame Vedel — have survived into a world whose values have shifted beneath them. They are the living evidence of what happens when the certainties of youth harden into dogma. La Pérouse articulates the old person's predicament with devastating clarity: "Why is it there is so little about old people in books? I suppose it's because old people aren't able to write themselves and young ones don't take any interest in them. No one's interested in an old man" (014-022).

## Role in The Counterfeiters

### Thematic Significance

The novel's elderly characters function as cautionary examples — they embody what [[adolescence]] becomes when it fails to sustain the capacity for self-questioning. The Pension Vedel, run by Azaïs, is the institutional expression of aged rigidity: its "poisonous air" suffocates the young precisely because the old refuse to see what they have become.

[[Édouard]]'s reflection — "I am not far from thinking that in irresolution lies the secret of not growing old" (041-014) — suggests Gide's deeper point: aging is not the accumulation of years but the loss of openness. Those who cling to fixed positions grow old regardless of their age; those who remain in motion, like Bernard, stay perpetually young.

## Manifestations in the Text

### Key Scenes

La Pérouse's confession to Édouard (014-022) is the novel's most extended meditation on old age. In his "yellowish whitish nightcap" — an old stocking tied in a knot (014-001) — he speaks of his discovery that "I have been a dupe during the whole of my life. Madame de La Pérouse has fooled me; my son has fooled me; everybody has fooled me; God has fooled me" (014-022). His tragedy is not simply that he was deceived, but that he discovered the deception too late to do anything about it.

The visit to La Pérouse's new apartment (019-005) deepens the portrait. The old couple's quarrels over open windows become a metaphor for a lifetime of entrenched opposition: "I spend my time shutting the windows and Madame de La Pérouse spends hers opening them again" (019-005).

> "There are certain acts in my past life which I'm only just beginning to understand. Yes, I'm just beginning to understand that they haven't at all the meaning I attached to them in the old days when I did them" (014-022) — La Pérouse describes the peculiar temporality of old age, in which the past is constantly being reinterpreted.

### Symbolic Expressions

Lucien's garden-path fantasy (002-038) provides the novel's most compressed image of old age: at the end of the day, "an old couple" appears, followed by the drumbeat of "Closing time! Everyone goes off. The play is ended." Old age is the final act, the signal that the spectacle is over.

## Literary and Philosophical Context

Gide's treatment of old age inverts the traditional reverence for elderly wisdom found in classical literature. La Pérouse's belated insights do not confer dignity; they deepen his humiliation. The [[Protestantism|Protestant]] emphasis on self-examination, stripped of the possibility of grace, becomes an instrument of torture in old age — the capacity to see one's errors without the time to correct them.
