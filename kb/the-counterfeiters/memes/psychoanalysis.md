# Psychoanalysis

The novel's engagement with the emerging science of the unconscious — at once a narrative method, a theme, and an object of Gide's characteristic skepticism about any system claiming to explain human behavior.

## Definition

Psychoanalysis in _The Counterfeiters_ is embodied primarily in [[Sophroniska]], the Polish doctor who treats young [[Boris]] at the Saas-Fee sanatorium. Her practice — dream analysis, confession, the uncovering of repressed memories — reflects the influence of Freudian ideas on Gide's intellectual milieu in the 1920s. But Gide does not simply endorse psychoanalysis; he subjects it to the same scrutiny he applies to all explanatory systems. The novel's psychological sophistication derives from its recognition that analysis itself can become a form of counterfeiting.

## Role in The Counterfeiters

### Thematic Significance

[[Édouard]]'s journal entry on psychological analysis stands as the novel's methodological manifesto: "Psychological analysis lost all interest for me from the moment that I became aware that men feel what they imagine they feel. From that to thinking that they imagine they feel what they feel was a very short step!" (009-016). This insight — that feeling and the imagination of feeling are indistinguishable — both enables and undermines the psychoanalytic project. If the line between authentic and invented emotion cannot be drawn, what can "cure" mean?

Sophroniska represents psychoanalysis in practice. Her treatment of Boris involves eliciting a full confession of his "bad habits" and interpreting his dreams: "Boris dreams aloud in my presence. Every morning he consents to remain during one hour in that state of semi-somnolence in which the images which present themselves to us escape from the control of our reason" (022-055). Her method is telling: "the ramblings of this child are far more instructive than the most intelligent analysis of the most conscious of minds could be" (022-055).

Yet Gide does not let Sophroniska's claims stand unchallenged. After Boris's treatment, [[Édouard]] notes drily: "Sophroniska goes on repeating that little Boris is cured — a cure which is supposed to corroborate her method; but I am afraid she is indulging her own vanity" (025-024). The psychoanalytic cure may be as counterfeit as the coins the boys pass.

## Manifestations in the Text

### Key Scenes

Sophroniska's account of Boris's confession (025-004) is the novel's most extended depiction of therapeutic practice. Boris's enigmatic response — "But it means nothing — it's magic" (025-007) — when pressed about his behavior resists the analytic framework. The child's appeal to "magic" suggests a realm of experience that psychoanalysis, with its rational categories, cannot reach.

Sophroniska's own analysis traces Boris's "nervous illness" to repression: "It arose no doubt from the constraint Boris was obliged to exercise in order to get free from them" (025-009). Her therapeutic approach — "Far from attempting to blacken his vice, I represented it to him simply as one of the forms of laziness" (025-017) — embodies the psychoanalytic ideal of non-judgmental interpretation. But the novel's subsequent events cast doubt on whether this approach was sufficient.

> "Many things escape the reason, and a person who should attempt to understand life by merely using his reason would be like a man trying to take hold of a flame with the tongs. Nothing remains but a bit of charred wood, which immediately stops flaming" (022-055) — Sophroniska describes the limitation of rational analysis in an image that applies equally to her own method.

### Symbolic Expressions

The Saas-Fee sanatorium itself — a mountain retreat devoted to healing — is the spatial symbol of psychoanalysis: a place removed from ordinary life where the unconscious can be examined. But its remoteness also suggests its limitation; the "cure" achieved there may not survive the return to Paris. The flame-and-tongs metaphor (022-055) recurs as the novel's warning against any system that would grasp the living psyche with the instruments of rational analysis.

## Literary and Philosophical Context

Gide wrote _The Counterfeiters_ during the period when Freud's ideas were being assimilated into French intellectual culture. His engagement with psychoanalysis is characteristically ambivalent: he borrows its techniques (dream narration, the importance of childhood, the talking cure) while resisting its claims to explanatory completeness. The novel's own method — [[Édouard]]'s journal, the multiple points of view — enacts a psychoanalytic principle (the truth emerges through indirection) while refusing psychoanalysis's therapeutic optimism.
