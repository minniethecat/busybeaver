# The Seine

The Seine flows through The Counterfeiters as a recurrent geographic and symbolic presence — a boundary between the novel's moral zones, a stage for solitary walks, and the city's great artery of motion and reflection.

## Location

The Seine bisects Paris, dividing the Right Bank from the Left Bank. In the novel, characters cross it repeatedly: the quays, the bridges, and the riverbanks form a geography of passage and pause. The Tuileries Gardens lie near its Right Bank (006-100), and the Luxembourg is on the Left Bank (007-004).

## Description in the Novel

Gide uses the Seine to track his characters' moral and physical trajectories. After Vincent's night with Lilian and the departure of the car that dropped him off, "Vincent walked a few paces along the quay, crossed the Seine, and went on till he reached the part of the Tuileries" (006-100). The river is crossed at a moment of crisis — Vincent's moral reckoning after his sexual encounter.

Bernard, having run away from home, "reaches the Quays, crosses the Seine. He thinks of the new rule of life he has given himself" (007-004). The crossing of the river coincides with the adoption of a new moral code — Bernard is leaving the bank of his childhood for the bank of his adult life. Later, "Bernard is back again on the quays now. He feels all lightness" (007-005) — the quays have become a space of buoyant solitude in which his ethical project feels viable.

Edouard, too, walks the quays: "I was dawdling back along the quays until it should be time for" his appointment (012-003), the river providing the city's great pedestrian thoroughfare for solitary reflection.

## Narrative Function

### Scenes Set Here

Vincent walks along the quay after his night with Lilian, crosses the Seine, presses a wet handkerchief to his forehead, and enters the Tuileries in a state of moral and physical disorientation (006-100). Bernard crosses the Seine after running away from home, formulating his new "rule of life" — to always do what is most difficult — while crossing the river (007-004).

### Symbolic Significance

The Seine is the novel's river of moral transition. To cross it is to move from one state of being to another — from innocence to experience, from domestic order to personal freedom, from illusion to knowledge. The quays that line the river are thresholds, spaces of walking and thinking, where characters are suspended between destinations and decisions.

## Associated Characters

Vincent Molinier crosses the Seine after his night with Lilian, his moral collapse registered in his physical wandering (006-100). Bernard Profitendieu crosses the Seine after leaving home, using the crossing to formulate his ethical program (007-004, 007-005). Edouard strolls the quays between appointments (012-003).

## Real-World Context

The Seine has been a central symbol in Parisian literature from Villon to the surrealists. In the 1920s, the quays of the Seine were the home of the bouquinistes — the booksellers whose stalls lined the river — and the river was both a working waterway and a site of pedestrian contemplation. Gide's Seine is the river of Baudelaire's flaneur and Apollinaire's Zone — a space of urban solitude.
