# Sincerity

A central ethical and psychological problem in [[André Gide]]'s _The Counterfeiters_ — the question of whether it is possible to be sincere when the self is constantly changing, and whether art demands sincerity or the transcendence of it. Explored most extensively through [[Edouard]]'s journal and [[Bernard Profitendieu]]'s moral declarations, sincerity is the novel's positive counter-value to the [[counterfeit-coin]]'s inauthenticity.

## Definition

In _The Counterfeiters_, sincerity is not a simple virtue but a vexed and unstable concept. [[Edouard]] writes in his journal: "How vexing this question of sincerity is! Sincerity! When I say the word I think only of her. If it is myself that I consider, I cease to understand its meaning. I am never anything but what I think myself — and this varies so incessantly" (009-011). Sincerity, in Gide's treatment, founders on the fluidity of the self — if who one is changes from morning to evening, to which self should one be sincere?

## Role in The Counterfeiters

### Thematic Significance

Sincerity is the ethical twin of the novel's aesthetic concept of the [[pur]] novel. Just as the novel must be stripped of inauthentic elements, the person must resist the temptation to perform a false consistency. But Gide complicates this: sincerity can itself become a performance. [[Edouard]] notes that his eloquence is driven by his "constant desire to instruct, to convince, to captivate" [[Laura Douviers|Laura]] (009-007) — his apparent sincerity is shaped by the desire to please, which introduces an element of performance.

> "I am never anything but what I think myself — and this varies so incessantly, that often, if I were not there to make them acquainted, my morning's self would not recognize my evening's." (009-011) — Edouard's journal, the novel's most concentrated meditation on sincerity.

The problem extends to the older generation. The old pastor Vedel demands sincerity from his grandson but cannot recognize the sincerity of adolescent desire — his demand for sincerity becomes a form of tyranny (013-081). [[Bernard Profitendieu]] articulates the alternative ideal when he says he wants to "ring true, with a pure, authentic sound" — to be "worth exactly what one seems to be worth" (024-042).

> "If anyone were to ask me today what virtue I considered the finest, I should answer without hesitation — honesty." (024-042) — Bernard's moral credo, linking sincerity to the [[counterfeit-coin]]'s language of ringing true.

## Manifestations in the Text

### Key Scenes

[[Edouard]]'s journal entry of October 18 (009-007–009-011) is the novel's most concentrated meditation on sincerity. He examines his own motives toward [[Laura Douviers|Laura]], finding that his apparent devotion is mixed with the pleasure of eloquence. The entry culminates in the admission that sincerity is impossible because the self is in constant flux.

[[Bernard Profitendieu]]'s reflections on "ringing true" (024-042) offer a counter-model: sincerity as a quality of being rather than a quality of speech. He wants his life to produce "a pure, authentic sound" — not to convince others but to be at peace with himself. This formulation directly echoes the [[counterfeit-coin]] metaphor: a genuine coin rings true when struck, a counterfeit does not.

### Symbolic Expressions

The [[counterfeit-coin]] is the negative symbol of sincerity — the false that passes as true. The "pure, authentic sound" of a genuine coin struck against a surface represents the ideal of sincerity: a life that, when tested, "rings true" (024-042). [[Edouard]]'s [[journal]] — a private space where he can record his inconsistencies — is the formal embodiment of the sincerity problem. The act of keeping a journal itself raises the question: is writing for oneself ever truly private, or is the journal always already addressed to a future reader?

## Literary and Philosophical Context

Gide's treatment of sincerity draws on the Protestant tradition of self-examination (Rousseau's _Confessions_, the Puritan diary) while subverting its assumptions. Where the confessional tradition assumes a stable self that can be truthfully reported, Gide insists on the self's multiplicity. This aligns with Nietzsche's critique of the unified subject and anticipates existentialist accounts of authenticity.

The problem also has an aesthetic dimension: if sincerity is impossible, can art be sincere? Gide's answer, implicit in the novel's form, is that art can be true to the experience of inconsistency — not by resolving it but by embodying it. [[Edouard]]'s novel-within-the-novel fails to be written precisely because he cannot resolve the sincerity question; Gide's novel succeeds by making that failure its subject.
