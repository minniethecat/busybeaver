# Sophroniska

A Polish psychoanalyst who runs the sanatorium at [[Saas-Fee]] where she treats [[boris|Boris]] — the grandson of [[la-perouse|La Pérouse]] — and his companion [[bronja|Bronja]]. Through her confession-based therapeutic method and her intellectual exchanges with [[edouard|Edouard]], she represents the novel's engagement with modern psychology as an alternative to the religious and [[moral]] frameworks that dominate the other characters.

## Role in the Novel

Sophroniska appears in the Saas-Fee section (Part II), functioning as a foil to Edouard: where he is the introspective artist who cannot complete his novel, she is the practical scientist confident in her method. Her most important narrative function is as the interpreter of Boris's psychological history. Through her conversations with Edouard, the reader learns of Boris's "magic" practices with Baptistin Kraft, his talisman, and the origin of his nervous tics — a secular, psychoanalytic excavation of childhood trauma in place of religious confession and moral judgment.

She also serves as a critic of Edouard's novel, pressing him on whether his characters "form under his hand" and whether theoretical ambitions can produce living fiction (022-065). Her polite but persistent questioning — "Have you got on far with it?" (023-039), "And the subject of your book is made up?" (023-032) — sharpens the novel's central aesthetic debate. Finally, she acts as a bridge figure, agreeing to send Boris to the Vedel-Azaïs school in Paris at Edouard's suggestion, thereby reconnecting Boris with his grandfather La Pérouse (025-022).

## Biography

### Background

Sophroniska is "a Polish woman doctor, who is in charge of Boris and Bronja" at a sanatorium in the Swiss Alps (022-001). One of the novel's few professionally accomplished women, her status and intellectual confidence distinguish her from characters like Laura, whose identities are defined through domestic and romantic relationships. Before Edouard's arrival, she met Strouvilhou at the sanatorium and gave him Boris's talisman as a souvenir — a decision that later proves significant given Strouvilhou's involvement in the counterfeiting ring (025-021).

### Key Actions

Sophroniska's central action is her treatment of Boris through therapeutic confession. She has worked systematically to bring Boris to "make a complete confession" (022-048), taking apart "the innermost wheels of his mental organism" like "a watchmaker cleaning the works of a clock" (025-004). She discovers that Boris's nervous disorders originated from "magic" practices with his schoolfellow Baptistin Kraft in Warsaw, followed by his mother catching him in the act and his subsequent belief that his father's death was divine punishment. Her frustration is palpable when she learns the mother withheld crucial information "out of false shame": "Boris would be cured already if I had known this from the beginning" (025-013).

She also engages Edouard in sustained aesthetic debate about his novel-in-progress. When the conversation grows tense, she deftly pivots to music, noting that "music is a mathematical art" (023-050). Her agreement to send Boris to Paris marks her willingness to subordinate her own professional judgment to what seems best for the child's future.

### Relationships

**[[boris|Boris]]**: Her patient, whom she describes as "exceedingly pure" (022-058) — she is clinical yet caring, seeking to understand rather than to judge. **[[bronja|Bronja]]**: Boris's companion, whom Sophroniska calls "Boris's real doctor" (022-063), acknowledging the girl's therapeutic power over her own clinical methods. **[[edouard|Edouard]]**: An intellectual sparring partner; their conversations form the novel's most sustained dialogue between the scientific and artistic temperaments. **[[laura-douviers|Laura Douviers]]**: A confidante — Laura "seems to take to her" (022-066). **[[la-perouse|La Pérouse]]**: She knows him through Edouard's account and becomes the agent of Boris's potential reunion with his grandfather.

## Character Analysis

### Motivations

Sophroniska is driven by a professional commitment to her psychoanalytic method and a genuine desire to heal. Unlike the novel's moralists, she does not judge — when she learns of Boris's "magic," she represents it to him "simply as one of the forms of laziness" rather than as sin (025-017). Her goal is psychological liberation, not moral reformation. She also possesses genuine intellectual curiosity: her admission that "without mysticism man can achieve nothing great" (025-002) reveals a breadth of thinking surprising in a scientist.

### Symbolic Role

Sophroniska represents the modern, secular approach to the human soul — a therapeutic alternative to the religious frameworks of Protestant moral seriousness and Catholic mysticism. Yet Gide does not present her as unambiguously right. Edouard doubts Boris is truly cured: "the malady has simply taken refuge in some deeper recess of his being" (025-024). Sophroniska herself is "full of contradictions" (025-024): she disbelieves Church dogma but believes in the "efficacy of faith," speaking "with emotion" of the children's piety. She is a rationalist who acknowledges the limits of science.

### Development

Sophroniska does not develop across the novel — she appears fully formed in the Saas-Fee section and exits unchanged. Her function is to represent a particular intellectual position in the novel's polyphonic argument. Her decision to send Boris to Paris reflects pragmatic acknowledgment of circumstances, not personal growth.

## Literary Context

Gide presents Sophroniska entirely through Edouard's journal, filtering her psychoanalytic method through his ambivalent gaze. The technique is characteristically Gidean: no single perspective is allowed to dominate, and the reader must weigh Sophroniska's confidence against Edouard's skepticism. Her method reflects the intellectual currents of the 1920s, when Freudian psychoanalysis was still new and controversial. Gide neither endorses it as a cure-all nor dismisses it as charlatanism — her method has real value, but its limits are serious.
