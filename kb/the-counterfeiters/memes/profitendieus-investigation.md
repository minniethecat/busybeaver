# Profitendieu's Investigation

Albéric Profitendieu, the examining magistrate who raised Bernard, investigates the circulation of counterfeit coins — an inquiry that brings him into contact with the Vedel-Azaïs boarding school, the young offenders, and the adult world that enabled them.

## Summary

Alerted to counterfeit coins circulating in Paris, examining magistrate Albéric Profitendieu launches an investigation that leads to the Vedel-Azaïs boarding school, where George Molinier and his classmates have been passing false ten-franc pieces. The investigation — conducted by the man who lost his own son to rebellion — becomes a meditation on parental authority, juvenile crime, and the failure of social institutions to guide the young. [PN=041-024] [PN=041-042] [PN=041-032]

## Context

### Narrative Position

This event develops across **Part III**, particularly Chapters 16-17. The investigation runs parallel to the novel's other plotlines — Edouard's literary struggles, Bernard's self-discovery, and the schoolboys' escalating mischief — intersecting with them at critical moments. [PN=041-024] [PN=041-042]

### Preceding Events

Profitendieu, devastated by Bernard's departure (see [[bernard-leaves-home]]), has thrown himself into his work as a judicial magistrate. The counterfeit coin motif was introduced at Saas-Fée (see [[the-counterfeit-coin-discussion]]) as a literary metaphor; in Part III, it materializes as a criminal reality. George Molinier and his friends have been passing counterfeit coins (see [[the-schoolboy-coin-ring]]). [PN=041-024] [PN=023-063]

## Detailed Account

Profitendieu receives reports that "for some time past a certain number of counterfeit coins have been put into circulation." The investigation begins as a routine matter — tracking the source of false currency — but quickly becomes more complex as it leads to the network of schoolboys at the Vedel-Azaïs school. [PN=041-024]

The examining magistrate approaches his task with the professionalism of a career jurist, but the case touches him personally. George Molinier is the younger brother of Olivier, Bernard's best friend — the boy Bernard went to when he ran away. Profitendieu's investigation thus brings him into contact with the world Bernard entered after leaving home. [PN=041-042] [PN=002-022]

The investigation threatens to expose not just the boys but the failures of the adults responsible for them. The Vedel-Azaïs school, run by Laura's family, has allowed a culture of cynicism and petty criminality to flourish. The teachers are distracted, the pastor is ineffectual, and the older boys have been left to corrupt the younger ones with impunity. Profitendieu's investigation exposes what the school has concealed. [PN=041-042]

The narrative does not provide a climactic courtroom scene or a dramatic arrest. Instead, Gide focuses on Profitendieu's inner state — a man carrying out his duty while privately shattered by the loss of his own child, investigating juvenile crime while knowing that the adult world has failed the young more profoundly than the young have failed the law. [PN=003-070] [PN=041-032]

## Characters Involved

- **Albéric Profitendieu** — The examining magistrate whose professional investigation is shadowed by his personal grief over Bernard's departure
- **George Molinier** — Olivier's younger brother, one of the schoolboy offenders whose exposure threatens the Molinier family
- **The schoolboy gang** — The adolescent participants in the coin ring, whose crimes are symptoms of a broader failure of adult supervision
- **The Vedel-Azaïs family** — The proprietors of the school where the coin ring operates, whose neglect of their charges is exposed by the investigation

## Consequences

### Immediate Aftermath

The investigation proceeds without the resolution a conventional novel would provide. There is no grand trial, no definitive punishment. Instead, the threat of exposure hovers over the school and its students, a pressure that contributes to the novel's escalating tension. The investigation's most significant effect may be on Profitendieu himself, who must balance his judicial duty with his understanding — born of personal loss — that punishment alone cannot address the failures revealed by the case. [PN=041-042]

### Narrative Ripple Effects

The investigation connects the novel's three worlds: the judiciary (Profitendieu), the literary-artistic milieu (Edouard, Passavant), and the educational institution (the Vedel-Azaïs school). This convergence is the novel's structural achievement — disparate plotlines that seem independent gradually reveal their interdependence, as the counterfeit coin becomes the object that links the magistrate's investigation, the schoolboy's crime, and the novelist's metaphor. The investigation also reasserts Profitendieu's presence in the narrative, reminding the reader that Bernard's departure left wounds that have not healed. [PN=041-032]

## Thematic Significance

Profitendieu's investigation literalizes the novel's central metaphor: the **search for what is genuine** in a world of counterfeits. As an examining magistrate, Profitendieu's job is to distinguish the false from the true — the counterfeit coin from the genuine currency. But the novel insists on the insufficiency of this legal distinction. The schoolboys who pass false coins are themselves products of counterfeit values — the school's hypocritical moralism, the family's hollow respectability, the culture's commodification of everything, including innocence. The law can catch the counterfeiters but cannot address the deeper counterfeiting. Profitendieu, who lost his son because he could not express genuine love, embodies this insufficiency — he can enforce the law but cannot repair what it fails to protect.

## Literary Technique

Gide presents the investigation elliptically, through fragmentary references rather than a sustained narrative. This technique mirrors the nature of the crime — counterfeit coins are designed to blend in with genuine currency, and detecting them requires the patient examination of fragments rather than dramatic revelation. The investigation scenes also maintain the novel's commitment to **multiple perspectives**: we see the case through Profitendieu's professional eyes, through the schoolboys' fearful whispers, and through Edouard's aestheticized awareness, each perspective revealing different aspects of the same events.
