# Mise en Abyme

The formal device at the heart of Andre Gide's _The Counterfeiters_: a novel that contains within itself a novelist, [[Edouard]], writing a novel also called _The Counterfeiters_ — a structure Gide named _mise en abyme_ after the heraldic practice of placing a smaller shield at the center of a larger one.

## Definition

Gide coined the term _mise en abyme_ to describe a work of art that encloses a smaller copy of itself. The phrase derives from heraldry, where a coat of arms placed at the center (_en abyme_) of a larger shield reproduces the same design in miniature. Gide applied this principle to literature: a novel that stages its own creation within its pages, making the act of composition the subject of the work.

In _The Counterfeiters_, Edouard is a novelist writing a novel called — like Gide's — _The Counterfeiters_. He articulates his method explicitly: "I invent the character of a novelist, whom I make my central figure; and the subject of the book, if you must have one, is just that very struggle between what reality offers him and what he himself desires to make of it" (023-025). This statement is itself the novel's _mise en abyme_: Gide has invented Edouard, the novelist-character, as his central figure, and the subject of Gide's novel is precisely the struggle between reality and artistic shaping that Edouard describes.

## Role in The Counterfeiters

### Thematic Significance

The mise-en-abyme structure is not decorative but thematic. The novel concerns counterfeiting — false coins, false selves, false values. By embedding a novel called _The Counterfeiters_ within _The Counterfeiters_, Gide makes the novel itself participate in its own theme: the book we hold is a representation, a "counterfeit" of reality, just as the coins are counterfeit gold. The distance between Gide-the-author and Edouard-the-character-as-author mirrors the distance between authentic and fraudulent — we are never certain whether Edouard's novel would be a genuine work of art or another kind of counterfeit.

> "If I were writing _The Counterfeiters_ I should begin by showing the counterfeit coin — the little ten-franc piece" (023-063) — [[Bernard Profitendieu]] challenges Edouard to ground his novel in concrete fact rather than abstract idea. This advice applies equally to Gide's novel, which does begin by dramatizing the counterfeit coin and counterfeit identities.

The mise-en-abyme also structures the novel's investigation of [[sincerity]]. If the self is multiple and unstable — "I am never anything but what I think myself — and this varies so incessantly" (009-011) — then the relationship between author, character, and fictional persona becomes another version of the authenticity problem. Gide's Edouard is both Gide and not-Gide, a "counterfeit" author whose presence destabilizes the boundary between fiction and truth.

Edouard envisions his novel formally: "I can't see why what was possible in music should be impossible in literature... I should like something like the art of fugue writing" (023-049). The musical metaphor — multiple voices interweaving, a theme stated and restated at different levels — perfectly describes the mise-en-abyme's effect: the same concerns (counterfeiting, [[sincerity]], creation) appear at every level of the structure.

### Character Associations

**[[Edouard]]** is the embedded novelist, the figure through whom the mise-en-abyme operates. His journal and his novel-in-progress constitute the work-within-the-work. His struggle to complete _The Counterfeiters_ — "not yet written a line of it" at the novel's start (009-025), still working on it in bursts (041-001, 044-079), contemplating using Boris's suicide as material (048-001) — mirrors the reader's own experience of the novel's open-endedness. **[[Bernard Profitendieu|Bernard]]** functions as a critical reader within the fiction, questioning Edouard's method and offering the alternative of starting from facts (023-063). **[[Laura Douviers|Laura]]** identifies the autobiographical dimension: "in this novelist of yours you won't be able to help painting yourself" (023-027).

## Manifestations in the Text

### Key Scenes

The mise-en-abyme is most explicitly articulated in three passages. On the train to [[Paris]], Edouard writes in his notebook about his plans for _The Counterfeiters_ and his theory of the [[pur]] novel (009-025) — this is the structural seed from which the entire mise-en-abyme grows. At the [[saas-fee|Saas-Fee]] sanatorium, Edouard discusses his novel-in-progress with Bernard, Laura, and Sophroniska, explaining his method of inventing a novelist as central figure (023-025). Bernard then challenges him: "If I were writing _The Counterfeiters_ I should begin by showing the counterfeit coin" (023-063) — arguing that the novel should start from a fact, not an idea.

> "I invent the character of a novelist, whom I make my central figure; and the subject of the book... is just that very struggle between what reality offers him and what he himself desires to make of it." (023-025)

The title itself is announced by Bernard: "May I?... _The Counterfeiters_," said Bernard. "But now _you_ must tell me" (023-056). The moment is charged: when Bernard names Edouard's novel, he names Gide's as well, collapsing the boundary between the embedded work and the work that contains it.

Edouard's journal entry about the "deep-lying subject" of his book (025-001) confirms that the struggle between reality and representation — the mise-en-abyme's core dynamic — is not ancillary but central. The novel's final section shows Edouard rereading pages he showed to George (044-079) and contemplating using Boris's suicide as material (048-001), demonstrating that the mise-en-abyme remains active to the end.

### Symbolic Expressions

Edouard's [[journal]] is the mise-en-abyme's material form. He calls it his "pocket-mirror" (019-001): "I cannot feel that anything that happens to me has any real existence until I see it reflected here." The mirror metaphor captures the infinite regress of the mise-en-abyme — a mirror reflecting a mirror reflects itself reflecting itself, just as Gide's novel about a novelist's journal about writing a novel creates a hall of reflective surfaces.

The novel's title — shared by Gide's book and Edouard's — is itself a mise-en-abyme. The words "The Counterfeiters" apply equally to both levels of the structure, making the book's own status as representation its stated subject.

## Literary and Philosophical Context

Gide developed the concept of mise en abyme across his career, most famously in his _Journal_ (1893) where he wrote: "I like finding in a work of art, transposed to the scale of the characters, the very subject of that work." _The Counterfeiters_ (1925) is his most sustained application of the principle.

The device participates in the modernist crisis of representation. Like Proust's novel-about-writing-a-novel in _In Search of Lost Time_ and Pirandello's play-within-a-play in _Six Characters in Search of an Author_, Gide's mise-en-abyme questions the boundary between art and life. But Gide's innovation is to tie the formal device to a thematic concern: the mise-en-abyme is not merely a technical experiment but an enactment of the novel's central question — whether any representation can be authentic, or whether all art is, in some sense, counterfeit.

The concept has been enormously influential in narrative theory and literary criticism. Figures from the French _nouveau roman_ to postmodern fiction have extended Gide's device. But _The Counterfeiters_ remains its paradigmatic instance: the first novel to make the act of its own composition the structural and thematic center of the work.
