# George Molinier

The youngest of the three Molinier brothers, a thirteen-year-old schoolboy who participates in circulating counterfeit coins through the Vedel-Azaïs school network, embodying the novel's theme of moral counterfeiting among the young.

## Role in the Novel

George Molinier serves as a crucial link in the counterfeit coin operation that gives the novel its title. As a pupil at Azaïs's school, he is positioned to pass counterfeit coins among his schoolfellows (PN=013-018), making him a conduit for the moral and literal counterfeiting that spreads through the novel's social world. He represents the younger generation's drift away from moral constraints — a drift that Edouard observes but cannot easily correct, since George "fights shy of me, hardly answers when I speak to him" (PN=012-046).

## Biography

### Background

George is the youngest son of Pauline Molinier (Edouard's half-sister) and her husband, a Paris jurist. He lives with his family on Rue Notre-Dame-des-Champs. He attends the Vedel-Azaïs school, where his older brother Olivier also studies, and is being raised as a Catholic despite his mother's Protestant background (PN=012-044). The family has reorganized their apartment to put Olivier and George together on a lower floor, displacing them to make room for their eldest brother Vincent's medical practice (PN=012-045).

### Key Actions

George first appears in the novel when Edouard catches him attempting to steal a book from a bookseller's stall on the quays. After Edouard buys the book for him, George discovers his uncle's identity and slips him a threatening note: "If you tell my parents the story of the book, I shall say that you solicited me" (PN=012-037). At Laura's wedding at the Vedel chapel, George sits in the gallery with his schoolfellows while Old La Pérouse plays the harmonium (PN=013-018). His most significant off-stage action is his participation in the counterfeit coin ring exploiting the school network — "for some time past a certain number of counterfeit coins have been put into circulation" (PN=041-024), and young George, along with others at the school, is caught up in the scheme.

### Relationships

**Edouard**: George's uncle, who catches him stealing and to whom George responds with defiance and blackmail. Their relationship is one of mutual suspicion — George "cannot forgive me for not having gone to meet him outside the lycée" (PN=012-046). **Olivier Molinier**: George's older brother, with whom he shares a bedroom. Olivier's rebellion is intellectual and emotional; George's is practical and amoral. **Pauline Molinier**: His mother, who appears unaware of his activities. **The School Network**: George participates in a web of schoolboy mischief that escalates into genuine criminality through the counterfeit coins.

## Character Analysis

### Motivations

George is driven less by philosophical rebellion than by petty opportunism and the thrill of transgression. His attempted book theft, his threatening note to Edouard, and his role in the counterfeit coin scheme all suggest a boy testing the limits of authority in an environment where moral guidance has weakened. He belongs to a generation whose moral education — half-Catholic, half-Protestant, largely neglected — has left them adrift.

### Symbolic Role

George embodies the literal dimension of Gide's counterfeiting metaphor. While other characters counterfeit emotions, identities, or beliefs, George handles actual counterfeit coins. His character shows how moral counterfeiting reproduces itself at the most concrete level of social exchange. The yellow ribbon in his buttonhole (PN=012-025) serves as an ironic emblem — a decoration signifying nothing, a counterfeit of honour.

### Development

George does not develop significantly across the novel; he remains what he is: a boy on the edge of delinquency, his fate unresolved. Gide leaves him suspended, much as the counterfeit coin mystery is never fully resolved, suggesting that the problem George represents is ongoing and systemic rather than personal and fixable.

## Literary Context

Gide introduces George obliquely, through Edouard's journal. The street scene with the stolen book is one of the novel's most vividly observed passages, as Edouard notes: "Precision in the reader's imagination should be obtained not by accumulating details but by two or three touches put in exactly the right places" (PN=012-019). George is seen "only sideways — in profile," consistent with Edouard's theory that "nothing is more difficult to observe than creatures in the period of formation" (PN=012-019).

## Key Passages

> "I say... do you often go about picking up schoolboys?" — George to Edouard, his "jeering, insolent voice" turning the encounter on its head (PN=012-028)

> "If you tell my parents the story of the book, I shall say that you solicited me." — George's blackmail note to his uncle, the cross-out of "detest you" revealing a precocious cunning (PN=012-037)
