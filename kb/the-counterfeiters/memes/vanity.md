# Vanity

A form of self-love oriented toward the regard of others, vanity in The Counterfeiters operates as both a social lubricant and a source of moral error, motivating characters toward actions they would otherwise disown.

## Definition

In Gide's novel, vanity denotes an excessive investment in how one appears to others — a concern for reputation, admiration, and social standing that distorts judgment and compromises authenticity. Gide presents vanity not as a rare vice but as a nearly universal human tendency, one that the novel's characters display in forms ranging from the trivial to the catastrophic. The concept is closely linked to the novel's counterfeit theme: vanity produces a false self, a persona constructed for public consumption that may bear little relation to inner truth [003-040][023-012].

## Role in The Counterfeiters

### Thematic Significance

Vanity functions in the novel as a primary engine of self-deception. Characters act not from genuine conviction but from a desire to be seen in a particular light — courageous, generous, sophisticated, or virtuous. This orientation toward external validation makes them manipulable: Passavant exploits vanity to maintain his influence over Olivier and other young writers; the schoolboys' trafficking in counterfeit coins is partly driven by the desire to appear daring and competent before their peers [033-120][037-008]. Gide's critique of vanity extends to the literary world itself, where the desire for recognition corrupts artistic integrity.

### Character Associations

Robert de Passavant is the novel's purest embodiment of vanity — a literary entrepreneur whose entire existence is oriented toward public admiration and influence. Olivier Molinier's susceptibility to Passavant derives partly from his own vanity: the desire to be seen as sophisticated and worldly. Vincent's wounded vanity after his failed medical career and disastrous affair with Laura drives much of his subsequent conduct [003-040]. Even Edouard, who affects indifference to public opinion, is not immune: his journal-keeping, for all its apparent sincerity, contains an element of self-display.

## Manifestations in the Text

### Key Scenes

Bernard's letter to his father is explicitly described as containing "wounded vanity, defiance — bravado" alongside more genuine emotions, illustrating how vanity mingles with and contaminates authentic feeling [003-040]. The party at Passavant's flat stages vanity as spectacle, with characters performing their sophistication for one another's benefit. Vincent's gradual moral deterioration is traced through the lens of vanity — his sensitiveness to slights, his inability to admit error, his construction of a self-justifying narrative [037-008].

> "No; for my part, my vanity consists in never blushing." [017-041]

### Symbolic Expressions

The mirror functions as an implicit symbol of vanity throughout the novel — characters are repeatedly shown seeing themselves through others' eyes, adjusting their self-presentation. The journal form itself can be read as a vanity mechanism: the act of writing about oneself presupposes an audience, even if that audience is only one's future self. The counterfeit coin literalizes the emptiness at the heart of vanity: a value that depends entirely on being taken for something it is not [041-007].

### Edouard's Vanity: The Pervasiveness of Self-Regard

Gide's most subtle treatment of vanity concerns Edouard, who considers himself immune to it. Bernard's presence unsettles Edouard's self-image: "He had the most perfect contempt for the usual fatuity of authors; he snuffed out his own as well as he could; but he was not unwilling to seek a reinforcement of his modesty in other people's consideration; if this consideration failed him, modesty immediately went by the board" (PN-023-012). The passage exposes the paradox at the heart of vanity: the very attempt to transcend it can become its expression. Edouard's self-awareness does not free him from the dynamic — "in spite of all his resolutions, as soon as he was in Bernard's company, he behaved quite differently from what he wished" (PN-023-012). Gide's observation — "a little vanity is quite as effectual in making us pose as a great deal of love" — collapses the distinction between self-regard and regard for others, suggesting that the two emotions may be indistinguishable in their behavioral effects.

### Olivier's Vanity and the Mechanism of Manipulation

Olivier's vanity makes him the novel's most clearly manipulable character. Bernard's uncle, the Comte de Passavant, diagnoses this vulnerability with clinical precision: "his is a tender nature and sensitive to flattery. Everything goes to his head... Sensuality, pique, vanity — to what does not all this lay him open?" (PN-027-008). The structure is clear: vanity creates susceptibility to flattery, which enables control. Olivier himself becomes aware of the mechanism after the fact. Walking to the Argonauts dinner with dread, he recognizes that "what an unfortunate idea his vanity had suggested to him of trying to get Uncle Edouard to come too! There, in the presence of Passavant, surrounded by elder men, by other writers, by the future contributors to the _Vanguard_, he would be obliged to show off" (PN-033-120). In this moment of self-recognition, Olivier sees that his vanity has backed him into a corner from which only humiliation can extract him. The passage captures the essential tragedy of vanity in Gide's world: the vain person is not merely shallow but self-trapping.

## Literary and Philosophical Context

Gide's treatment of vanity draws on the French moralist tradition — particularly La Rochefoucauld, whose maxims repeatedly expose vanity as the hidden motive behind apparently virtuous actions. The theme also connects to the novel's engagement with Protestantism, which traditionally condemns vanity (vanitas) as a form of worldliness incompatible with authentic faith.
