# Bronja

A young Polish girl at Sophroniska's sanatorium, Boris's beloved companion, whose purity — symbolized by her ability to see angels — serves as both a comfort and a torment to Boris, and whose death precipitates the final tragedy of the novel.

## Role in the Novel

Bronja appears almost entirely in the company of Boris during the Swiss chapters of the novel. She functions as Boris's foil: where he is tormented by anxiety about his naughtiness, she is naturally good and untroubled. Her casual statement that she sees angels — because she is not naughty — becomes the psychological pivot of Boris's story. She represents the authenticity that Boris can neither achieve nor accept as unavailable to him. Her death, reported near the novel's end, removes the one figure of genuine innocence from Boris's world, leaving him vulnerable to the cruelty that will destroy him.

## Biography

### Background

Bronja is a Polish girl being treated at Sophroniska's sanatorium in Switzerland. She is the daughter of Sophroniska — described as "an exquisite creature" (PN=022-001). She has been raised by a mother who combines medical expertise with deep psychological insight, and who has observed her daughter's unusual sensitivity. Bronja and Boris sleep with their windows open (PN=024-005) and spend their days together on the sanatorium grounds.

### Key Actions

Bronja first appears when Edouard and his companions arrive at the sanatorium. Her mother Sophroniska keeps her under "careful watch" (PN=022-001) alongside Boris. When Boris speaks of angels, Bronja answers only his second question (PN=022-004) — a detail that suggests her intuitive sense for what matters. She laughs when Boris speaks in "a kind of lyrical ecstasy" (PN=022-019). The pivotal exchange occurs when Boris asks if he could see angels too: "Bronja, you aren't naughty; that's why you can see them" (PN=022-025) — a statement that confirms Boris's fear that he is excluded from angelic vision by his own moral inadequacy. Later, Bronja falls ill: "as soon as she came in, Bronja fell into her mother's arms" (PN=023-072). She receives a letter from Boris (PN=042-035) and her death is reported subsequently: "A month later that Boris heard of Bronja's death from Madame Sophroniska" (PN=046-001).

### Relationships

**Boris**: Her constant companion and the boy who loves her. Her innocence is both the thing he loves and the standard he cannot meet. **Sophroniska**: Her mother, who treats her with medical and psychological expertise. **Edouard**: An observer who records her interactions with Boris in his journal.

## Character Analysis

### Motivations

Bronja's motivation is love — simple, uncomplicated affection for Boris. She does not understand the torment her purity causes him. Her statement about seeing angels is not cruel; it is merely honest, spoken without awareness of its effect. She is what Boris would be if he could stop judging himself.

### Symbolic Role

Bronja represents unfallen innocence — the state before moral self-consciousness corrupts experience. Her ability to see angels is not a supernatural gift but a metaphor for the capacity to perceive goodness without self-doubt. She is the novel's brief, shining image of what authenticity looks like before the fall into moral anxiety. Her death makes the point: in Gide's world, such innocence cannot survive.

### Development

Bronja does not develop. She is what she is — pure, loving, and doomed. Her function in the novel is to be the fixed point of innocence that Boris can neither reach nor abandon, and whose loss destroys him.

## Literary Context

Gide paints Bronja with the lightest of touches — she is glimpsed through windows, heard in fragments of conversation, observed in her mother's arms. She is never given interiority of her own; she exists as Boris sees her, as Edouard sees her. This external treatment is thematically appropriate: innocence is surface, not depth. To give Bronja a complex psychology would be to destroy her symbolic function.

## Key Passages

> "Little Bronja is an exquisite creature; she is almost as delicate and as sickly as he." — Edouard's first impression (PN=022-001)

> "Bronja, you aren't naughty; that's why you can see them." — Boris to Bronja, the line that crystallizes his torment (PN=022-025)
