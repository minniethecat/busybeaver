# Illegitimacy

A condition that [[André Gide]] transforms from a social stigma into a philosophical ideal in _The Counterfeiters_ — the bastard as the figure who, unburdened by legitimate inheritance, is free to invent himself.

## Definition

In its literal sense, illegitimacy in the novel refers to [[Bernard Profitendieu]]'s discovery that he is not the biological son of the man he called father — the result of his mother's brief affair seventeen years before the novel opens (003-039). But Gide immediately elevates the concept: illegitimacy becomes the existential condition of anyone who refuses to be defined by origins, who claims the right to be "natural" rather than socially constructed. The novel's most famous aphorism — "It is to bastards that the future belongs" (013-125) — announces illegitimacy as a revolutionary principle: those not bound by the past are free to shape the future.

## Role in The Counterfeiters

### Thematic Significance

Bernard's discovery of his bastardy is the novel's originating event. His letter to [[Albéric Profitendieu]] (003-039) performs illegitimacy as liberation: "I prefer knowing I am not your son at all." The relief is genuine — Bernard has always felt himself a stranger in the Profitendieu household, and the discovery gives a name to his estrangement. But the novel complicates this liberation: Bernard's subsequent wanderings, his theft of [[Édouard]]'s suitcase, and his eventual return to the paternal home all suggest that freedom from origins is not as simple as a letter of renunciation.

The philosophical case for illegitimacy is made most forcefully by Bernard himself, in a passage (007-006) that moves from personal grievance to existential meditation:

> "The difficulty in life is to take the same thing seriously for long at a time. For instance, my mother's love for the person I used to call my father — I believed in it for fifteen years. I still believed in it yesterday. *She* wasn't able to take her love seriously, either. I wonder whether I despise her or esteem her the more for having made her son a bastard." (007-006)

The question — despise or esteem? — is left unresolved. Illegitimacy is neither innocence nor guilt; it is simply the truth, and the novel's moral logic holds that any truth, however painful, is preferable to a comfortable falsehood.

## Manifestations in the Text

### Key Scenes

Bernard's discovery scene (002-005) is the novel's primal moment: the drawer opened from above, the pink ribbon, the letters that reveal a seventeen-year-old secret. Illegitimacy arrives not as accusation but as accident — Bernard was just trying to mend the clock. The novel insists that the truth of one's origins, however disruptive, is always preferable to the most comfortable falsehood.

The scene in which Bernard declares "I have no parents. That is, I am what the child you are expecting will be — a bastard" (015-027) to Laura is the novel's most compressed statement of illegitimacy's double meaning. The word is both wound and weapon — a stigma Bernard claims as identity.

### Symbolic Expressions

The suitcase Bernard steals from [[Édouard]] (011-012) becomes a symbol of provisional identity — the bastard travels light, unencumbered by inheritance. The open window, the Luxembourg Gardens, the streets Bernard walks: all are spaces of illegitimacy, belonging to no one, open to anyone.

## Literary and Philosophical Context

Gide's celebration of illegitimacy draws on the Romantic tradition of the outlaw-hero, from Byron to Nietzsche, as well as on his own conflicted relationship with bourgeois morality. The novel's epigraph — "We are all bastards" (from a poem cited in the text at 007-006) — universalizes the condition. Gide also draws on Dostoevsky's exploration of illegitimate children in *The Brothers Karamazov* (Smerdyakov) and *The Adolescent*, though Gide's treatment is more celebratory than tragic.

[[Edouard]]'s journal records his ambivalence: "Bernard had hardly ever mentioned his father to me. I had morally accepted his having left his family, ready as I am to consider such desertions natural, and disposed to see in them nothing but what will be to the child's greatest advantage" (041-032). Yet when Profitendieu arrives, grieving, Édouard is "forced to ask myself whether Bernard had done right to leave." Illegitimacy, the novel finally suggests, is not a solution but a condition — and like all conditions in Gide's world, its value depends on what one makes of it.
