# Lady Griffith (Lilian)

A wealthy American woman living in Paris, whose seduction of Vincent Molinier represents the novel's most vivid portrait of worldly corruption — a character who "loved" Vincent but "what she loved was less him than his success" (PN=008-002).

## Role in the Novel

Lady Griffith functions as the novel's central figure of amoral worldly charm. She is introduced through Vincent Molinier's story, having been brought into Vincent's circle by Robert de Passavant. Her seduction of Vincent completes his moral descent — from the idealism of his love affair with Laura to the cynical sophistication of Lady Griffith's world. She represents the counterfeit of love: her interest in Vincent is genuine but predatory, a desire to possess his vitality rather than to know him.

## Biography

### Background

Lady Griffith is an American of considerable wealth who lives between Paris and the French Riviera. She moves in sophisticated, amoral circles that include Comte Robert de Passavant and the gambling world Vincent enters. She speaks Russian, English, and French (PN=006-058), and her cosmopolitanism is part of her rootless, unattached quality. She owns a house where Vincent visits her daily after their introduction (PN=006-045).

### Key Actions

Lady Griffith first appears in conversation with Robert de Passavant, who has just lent Vincent five thousand francs that she predicts Vincent will lose gambling — which she hopes he does, as part of her strategy of possession (PN=006-006). She extracts Vincent's entire story of his affair with Laura Douviers, listening with feigned sympathy while secretly jealous: "I had to pretend at first to be deeply interested in her. I even had to weep with him" (PN=006-052). When Vincent begins winning at gambling, her love intensifies — "what she loved was less him than his success, his victory" (PN=008-002). She takes Vincent to Monaco and later to the South. Her final appearance in the novel comes through a letter: "I have had a letter from Lady Griffith" (PN=040-011), and Laura notes "Lady Griffith doesn't speak of returning" (PN=040-015) — leaving Vincent's fate ambiguous.

### Relationships

**Vincent Molinier**: Her lover and project. She is drawn to his vitality and his vulnerability after the Laura affair, but her love is fundamentally acquisitive. She takes pleasure in reshaping him from a serious, idealistic young medical student into a successful gambler and man of the world. **Robert de Passavant**: Her friend and confidant, who shares her amoral sophistication. Their banter about Vincent (PN=006-001 through PN=006-058) reveals two predators appraising their prey. **Laura Douviers**: The woman Vincent abandoned. Lady Griffith studies Laura's story with the cool curiosity of a rival who has already won.

## Character Analysis

### Motivations

Lady Griffith is driven by the desire for experience and possession. She collects people as she collects sensations — Vincent interests her because he is new, because his story is dramatic, because he represents a conquest. Her restlessness and boredom suggest an inner emptiness that no acquisition can fill. She tells Passavant "the fountains bore me" (PN=017-034), in what may be the novel's most succinct expression of her spiritual condition.

### Symbolic Role

Lady Griffith is the novel's embodiment of counterfeit love and counterfeit vitality. Her charm, her wit, her cosmopolitan glamour are all surfaces covering an absence. She has, as Edouard might say, no soul — she is all style without substance, all performance without authenticity. She is the logical endpoint of the counterfeit principle: a person so thoroughly artificial that even her desires are not her own.

### Development

Lady Griffith does not develop — she is static by design, a fixed point of worldly corruption against which Vincent's moral trajectory can be measured. Her lack of development is itself thematic: the amoral cannot grow because growth requires conscience.

## Literary Context

Gide gives Lady Griffith some of the novel's most brilliant dialogue — her long speech to Passavant about Vincent and Laura (PN=006-052 through PN=006-058) is a tour de force of narrative embedded within dialogue, showing her skill as both listener and storyteller. She tells Passavant "you don't know how to listen" (PN=006-032) while demonstrating her own mastery of the art — an art she deploys seductively, to extract confession and assert control.

## Key Passages

> "It is possible that Lady Griffith loved Vincent; but what she loved was less him than his success, his victory." — Edouard's assessment of Lilian's love (PN=008-002)

> "The fountains bore me. I have a horror of all forced jets of water." — Lady Griffith, expressing her ennui (PN=017-034)
