# Revolt

In Andre Gide's _The Counterfeiters_, revolt is the younger generation's characteristic response to the constraints of family, convention, and hypocrisy — a force that can lead either to liberation or to self-destruction.

## Definition

Revolt in _The Counterfeiters_ is not primarily political but psychological and moral. It is the instinctive refusal of authority — parental, institutional, and conventional — that drives characters like Bernard, Sarah, and Armand to reject the values they have inherited. Sarah Vedel "cultivated a facile contempt for all the domestic virtues as a kind of preventive protest" (036-004); her "instinct for revolt" has been "exasperated" by family life. Revolt is the engine of the novel's generational conflict.

## Role in The Counterfeiters

### Thematic Significance

Revolt is the novel's primary response to the problem of counterfeit values. If the older generation embodies hypocrisy — professing virtues they do not practice — then revolt appears as the young person's path to authenticity. Bernard's revolt against his family, expressed in his decision to leave home and forge his own identity, is the novel's central plot line.

However, Gide complicates revolt by showing its dangers. Sarah's revolt exhausts itself in contempt without building anything. Vincent's revolt against bourgeois morality leads not to liberation but to a different form of self-deception. Even revolt can be counterfeit — a performance of independence that conceals continued dependence.

### Character Associations

**Bernard** embodies productive revolt: his break from his family leads to genuine self-discovery. **Sarah** represents revolt as attitude rather than action — "facile contempt" without positive content. **Armand** channels revolt into cynicism. **Laura's** "sudden outburst of revolt" (039-035) is reactive and self-defeating. **Vincent** revolts against conventional morality only to construct a self-serving alternative.

## Manifestations in the Text

### Key Scenes

Sarah's meditation in her empty room (036-004) crystallizes the psychology of revolt. She sits alone, cultivating contempt for domesticity as "preventive protest" — rebellion as self-protection before the fact of constraint. The scene reveals revolt as both a genuine response to oppression and a potential evasion of engagement.

Bernard's departure from home opens the novel, establishing revolt as the initiating action. His letter to his father — a declaration of independence — sets in motion the events that will test whether his revolt is sustainable.

> "The constraint of family life had intensified her energies and exasperated her instinct for revolt." (036-004)

### Symbolic Expressions

The counterfeit coin itself can be seen as an instrument of revolt — schoolboys passing false coins as a form of rebellion against adult economic order. Bernard's lost suitcase, Edouard's purloined journal — objects that change hands through acts of rebellion — materialize the movement of revolt through the novel's world.

## Literary and Philosophical Context

Gide's treatment of revolt places him within a French tradition running from Rousseau's rejection of civilization to the twentieth-century existentialist embrace of freedom. The novel was published in 1925, in the aftermath of World War I, when the authority of the older generation had been catastrophically delegitimized. Gide anticipates the generational revolt that would become explicit in the literature of the 1930s and 1940s.

The Protestant context is also significant: revolt against religious authority was central to Gide's own biography, and the novel's young characters rebel against a specifically Protestant moral world.
