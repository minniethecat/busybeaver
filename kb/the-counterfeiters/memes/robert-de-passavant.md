# Robert de Passavant

A wealthy young aristocrat, fashionable novelist, and literary entrepreneur — inheritor of the [[comte-de-passavant]] title — Robert de Passavant is the novel's most sustained portrait of a [[counterfeit-coin|counterfeit]] life. He seduces [[Vincent Molinier]] into gambling, draws [[Olivier Molinier]] into his orbit, and stands as [[Edouard]]'s rival in art and affection.

## Role in the Novel

Robert is the novel's antagonist — not a melodramatic villain but a figure whose charm and wealth make counterfeit values attractive.

He is introduced through Vincent: "Robert de Passavant, who now calls himself his friend, is the friend of a great many people" (005-002). He represents the worldly temptation threatening the novel's young characters — a counterfeiter of feeling, an artist who cares more for reputation than for art.

Edouard's journal entry is definitive: "The work of art, as far as Passavant is concerned, is not so much an end as a means" (009-026).

## Biography

### Background

Robert is the son of the old [[comte-de-passavant|Comte de Passavant]], a difficult father whose death he greets with studied indifference: "Yes, about four hours ago. ... I don't want to appear cynical, but I have a horror of reach-me-down sentiments" (005-022).

He inhabits a "sumptuous mansion" on the Rue de Babylone (005-001), drives a car, and moves easily through fashionable [[paris|Parisian]] society. His novel _The Horizontal Bar_ has brought him literary celebrity, though its substance is questionable.

### Key Actions

Robert draws [[Vincent Molinier]] into a gambling club at [[the-argonauts|Pedro's]], then — when Vincent loses everything — lends him five thousand francs, wrapping corruption in friendship (005-007, 005-010).

He writes a pseudonymous manifesto praising himself for Dhurmer's review (005-011). He courts [[Olivier Molinier]] by offering him the editorship of the review, flattering his taste, and giving him an inscribed copy of _The Horizontal Bar_ with an ambiguous motto (016-054--016-057).

He takes Olivier to Corsica, telling Olivier's father the boy is "on the high road to becoming famous" (029-037). He presides over the Argonauts banquet (036-011).

### Relationships

**[[Vincent Molinier]]**: Robert cultivates Vincent as a point of access to [[Olivier Molinier|Olivier]] — "the demon will never rest now till Vincent has sold his brother to that creature of perdition — Passavant" (017-009). Vincent's "admiration for the Comte de Passavant had become much greater since he had ceased to take him seriously" (018-051).

**[[Olivier Molinier]]**: Robert's primary quarry. He offers literary influence, worldly experience, and flattery — everything the sincere [[Edouard]] cannot provide. The relationship culminates in Olivier's crisis after [[the-banquet-scene|the Argonauts banquet]].

**[[Edouard]]**: Robert's antithesis and rival. Edouard struggles for [[sincerity]]; Robert has abandoned the struggle for success. Their competition for Olivier dramatizes the novel's central conflict.

**[[armand-vedel|Armand Vedel]]**: Another young man drawn into Robert's circle at the banquet (036-104).

**[[lady-griffith|Lady Griffith]]**: Robert's lover and co-conspirator. At her apartment he reveals his cynicism about titles: "The Comte de Passavant, who had never had much faith in the authenticity of his friend's title, smiled" (006-073).

## Character Analysis

### Motivations

Robert is driven by the will to power — the desire to dominate, influence, and possess. Art and literature are instruments. He cultivates young talents not to foster them but to attach them. He has no secret artistic exigence; his convictions "are evoked by the passing hour; their _mot d'ordre_ is opportunism" (009-026).

### Symbolic Role

Robert embodies the novel's [[counterfeit-coin|counterfeit]] metaphor in its literary and social dimensions — the false artist, the hollow friend, the counterfeit count. He is "particularly skilful in the art of fathering his own words — and anything else he preferred to disown — on other people" (018-028). This ventriloquism is counterfeiting in essence: nothing he says is authentically his own.

### Development

Robert does not develop. Unlike [[Edouard]], [[bernard-profitendieu|Bernard Profitendieu]], or even [[Vincent Molinier]], he is fully formed when the novel begins — a completed counterfeit. His failure is the novel's judgment: "Nothing spreads more ruin or receives more applause than men of his stamp" (027-008). His work "is in danger of disappearing" with the generation it flatters (009-026).

## Literary Context

Gide constructs Robert as a composite of fashionable literary figures — often read as a partial portrait of Jean Cocteau. The character is revealed through multiple perspectives: the narrator's ironic commentary, Edouard's journal, and his own self-revealing conversations.

His novel _The Horizontal Bar_ and his review serve as instances of [[mise-en-abyme|mise en abyme]] — fictions within the fiction that reflect the novel's artistic concerns. Robert's opportunistic aesthetic is the negative against which Edouard's theory of the [[pur|pure novel]] is defined.

## Key Passages

> "The work of art, as far as Passavant is concerned, is not so much an end as a means. The artistic convictions which he displays are asserted with so much vehemence merely because they lack depth; no secret exigence of his temperament necessitates them; they are evoked by the passing hour; their _mot d'ordre_ is opportunism." (009-026)

> "Passavant was particularly skilful in the art of fathering his own words — and anything else he preferred to disown — on other people. He made a feint of dropping his subject, like an angler who, for fear of startling his trout, makes a long cast with his bait and then draws it in again by imperceptible degrees." (018-028)

> "After which the demon has won the game. ... The demon will never rest now till Vincent has sold his brother to that creature of perdition — Passavant." (017-009)
