# The Sorbonne

The Sorbonne appears in The Counterfeiters as the site of Olivier's academic life — the institutional world of lectures, examinations, and intellectual aspiration that runs parallel to the novel's private dramas.

## Location

The Sorbonne is located in the Latin Quarter of Paris, in the 5th arrondissement. It has been the seat of the University of Paris since the 13th century. In the novel, characters move between the Sorbonne quadrangle and the church of the Sorbonne (042-002, 042-005), anchoring the university in its recognizable architectural geography.

## Description in the Novel

Gide's references to the Sorbonne are economical but precise. "Olivier hastened to the Sorbonne. This was the morning that Bernard was to take his examination" (033-002) — the university is a place of appointment, discipline, and academic obligation. "In the Sorbonne quadrangle, he saw one of his" (042-002) — the courtyard serves as a meeting place, like the Luxembourg Gardens but for an older cohort.

The church of the Sorbonne appears in an extraordinary episode: "towards the church of the Sorbonne, into which the angel passed" (042-005), where the angel — the novel's most mysterious figure — enters the Baroque chapel. The university thus accommodates both the secular and the transcendent.

## Narrative Function

### Scenes Set Here

Olivier attends lectures at the Sorbonne and takes his examinations (033-002). Bernard is seen near the Sorbonne, and Olivier reports: "He saw me this morning near the Sorbonne. I was staying with the Comte" (036-122), revealing his involvement with Passavant. Vincent, on the verge of departure, says: "I knew he would be at the Sorbonne to come and see you" (041-034). The Sorbonne quadrangle is a place of chance encounters: Bernard sees a classmate there (042-002). The angel's passage through the Sorbonne chapel (042-005) brings the supernatural into the university's precincts.

### Symbolic Significance

The Sorbonne represents the institutional life of the mind — examination, lecture, academic hierarchy — that the novel's more rebellious characters chafe against. It is the world of legitimate intellectual ambition, in contrast to the literary salon's fashionable posturing. Bernard's examination at the Sorbonne is a rite of passage: the young man who fled his bourgeois home now re-enters society through academic achievement.

## Associated Characters

Olivier Molinier is the character most associated with the Sorbonne as a student (033-002, 036-122). Bernard takes his examination there (033-002). Vincent studies at the Sorbonne and is known to be found there (041-034). The angel — a figure of mysterious significance — enters the Sorbonne chapel (042-005).

## Real-World Context

The Sorbonne in the 1920s was the center of French academic life. Gide himself had a complex relationship with academic institutions, and the Sorbonne appears in his work as an emblem of established intellectual authority — sometimes respected, sometimes subverted.
