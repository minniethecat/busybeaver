# Childhood

A moral and thematic touchstone in [[André Gide]]'s _The Counterfeiters_ — the state of natural freedom and vulnerability that adult society systematically corrupts, embodied most poignantly in the fates of [[Boris]], [[George Molinier|George]], and the schoolboys of the [[Vedel Boarding School]].

## Definition

In Gide's novel, childhood is not an idealized innocence but a state of unprotected authenticity. Children are portrayed as naturally free from adult hypocrisy — "accustomed to it ever since our childhood" (048-008) describes the shocked recognition that a corrupting influence has always been present. The novel's concern is less with childhood itself than with the process by which adults exploit, corrupt, and betray it.

## Role in The Counterfeiters

### Thematic Significance

Childhood serves as the novel's moral litmus test. The treatment of children — by parents, teachers, and older adolescents — reveals the true ethical character of the adult world. The counterfeit coin ring, in which older boys coerce younger ones like George into passing false coins (033-033), literalizes the metaphor: adults and near-adults circulate false values through children, using them as instruments of corruption.

> "I have been accustomed to it since childhood, that I have not felt all this as I should." (013-019) — The speaker's confession that habituation dulls moral sensitivity points to childhood as the site where ethical numbness is cultivated.

## Manifestations in the Text

### Key Scenes

The novel's most extended treatment of childhood is Boris's tragedy. [[Sophroniska]]'s attempt to cure Boris of his "bad habits" through psychoanalytic confession creates the very vulnerability the schoolboys exploit (022-044 to 025-004). The [[Azaïs Pension]], supposedly a protective institution, becomes the instrument of his destruction — Gide's indictment of a society that "protects" children by controlling them.

> "I listened to in my childhood." (017-020) — Olivier recalls the stories Edouard told him, establishing childhood as a time when narrative — and by extension, art — could shape the self without coercion.

The "magic" of early childhood, "of which he was now ashamed" (046-014), appears in a later passage describing how adult consciousness poisons the natural responses of childhood — a theme Gide explored throughout his work.

### Sophroniska's Treatment and the Adult Gaze

Boris's experience of childhood is mediated almost entirely by adults who claim to know what is best for him. Sophroniska's psychoanalytic method — keeping Boris "in that state of semi-somnolence in which the images which present themselves to us escape from the control of our reason" (PN-022-055) — treats the child's inner life as a problem to be diagnosed and solved. She is confident in her method: "There's no more shame in it than allowing oneself to be sounded. I need to know everything and particularly what is most carefully hidden. I must bring Boris to make a complete confession" (PN-022-048). Yet Gide frames this therapeutic confidence as itself a form of intrusion. The treatment, Sophroniska explains, requires that Boris's mother "leave him entirely to my care; otherwise it would be impossible to answer for his being cured" (PN-022-035). The mother, a concert pianist who "used to take Boris with her to her dressing-room," is blamed for the "artificial atmosphere of the theatre" (PN-022-035) — yet Sophroniska's alternative, total therapeutic control, proves no less damaging. Each adult who takes responsibility for Boris claims to act in his interest while contributing to the chain of circumstances that destroys him.

## Literary and Philosophical Context

Gide's treatment of childhood reflects his engagement with [[Jean-Jacques Rousseau|Rousseau]]'s idea of natural innocence corrupted by society, as well as his own experiences as a child in a strict Protestant household. The novel's critique of institutional education — embodied in the Vedel school — draws on Gide's belief that conventional schooling crushes the individual's natural development, a theme he shared with progressive educational reformers of the early 20th century.
