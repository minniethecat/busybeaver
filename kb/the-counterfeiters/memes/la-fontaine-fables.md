# Fables (La Fontaine)

Jean de La Fontaine's _Fables_ (1668–1694), a collection of verse fables drawn from Aesop and Phaedrus that became a cornerstone of French literary culture. In _The Counterfeiters_, a passage from La Fontaine's "Epistle to Huet" (1687) — the poet's self-portrait as a butterfly flitting from flower to flower — becomes the text for [[bernard-profitendieu|Bernard]]'s baccalaureate essay and sparks a confrontation with [[olivier-molinier|Olivier]] about art, sincerity, and the French spirit.

## Overview

La Fontaine's _Fables_ — over 240 poems in twelve books — use animal stories to deliver moral lessons with wit and psychological acuity. They are among the most memorized works in French, taught to every schoolchild. The passage Gide selects is not from the fables proper but from the "Epistle to Huet," where La Fontaine reflects on his own artistic temperament — a choice that foregrounds the question of the artist's self-portrait.

## Appearance in The Counterfeiters

### Context of Mention

The passage appears in Part III, Chapter V ([[ch30]]), when Bernard meets Olivier after his baccalaureate written examination:

> "Papillon du Parnasse, et semblable aux abeilles   
> A qui le bon Platon compare nos merveilles,   
> Je suis chose légère et vole à tout sujet,   
> Je vais de fleur en fleur et d'objet en objet." (033-009)

Olivier, eager to shine, offers an interpretation borrowed from [[comte-de-passavant|Passavant]]:

> "I should have said that La Fontaine, in painting himself, had painted the portrait of the artist — of the man who consents to take merely the outside of things, their surface, their bloom. ... the truth is the appearance of things, that their secret is their form and that what is deepest in man is his skin." (033-012)

Bernard immediately recognizes the phrase as not Olivier's own. The exchange that follows exposes the novel's central tensions: sincerity versus performance, the genuine versus the counterfeit.

### Narrative Function

Olivier's borrowed interpretation — La Fontaine as artist of surfaces, celebrating lightness — represents the aesthetic of [[comte-de-passavant|Passavant]]: clever, modish, hollow. Bernard's rejoinder is a defense of depth:

> "But, my dear fellow, it isn't La Fontaine either. If he had only had that lightness, which, for that matter, he regretted and apologized for at the end of his life, he would never have been the artist we admire." (033-020)

Bernard insists that La Fontaine's greatness rests not on lightness but on the spirit of "investigation, of logic, of devotedness, of patient thoroughness" (033-020). The lines from the Epistle, taken alone, would reduce the poet to a dilettante; the _Fables_ themselves testify to a more serious art.

The scene dramatizes the novel's method of triangulated sincerity. Olivier speaks words that are not his; Bernard speaks out of genuine conviction but is "himself surprised at what he said — as if his words had preceded his thoughts" (033-016). Neither character is fully transparent to himself.

## Thematic Resonance

La Fontaine's self-description as "chose légère" (a light thing) that flits from subject to subject is a figure for the artist who values mobility over consistency — an image that resonates with Gide's own aesthetic of _disponibilité_ (availability). Yet Bernard's critique exposes the danger: the butterfly-artist can become the dilettante, the "French wit" that "gives us such a deplorable reputation among foreigners" (033-020).

The claim that La Fontaine "in painting himself, had painted the portrait of the artist" (033-012) applies equally to Gide. [[edouard|Edouard]] is Gide's self-portrait; the novel called _The Counterfeiters_ is a portrait of the novelist's condition. Gide's defense, like Bernard's, is that apparent lightness conceals rigorous art — the fragmented form is not evasion but fidelity to complexity.

## Gide's Relationship to the Work

La Fontaine was foundational in Gide's literary education, as for all French writers of his generation. Gide's choice of a lesser-known epistle rather than a famous fable signals his allusive method: the classic is reread, argued with, made to yield contemporary meaning. He recognized in La Fontaine a predecessor in using self-revelation as a literary strategy rather than a transparent confession.
