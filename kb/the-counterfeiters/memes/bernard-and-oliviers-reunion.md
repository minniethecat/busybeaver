# Bernard and Olivier's Reunion

Fresh from the discovery that he is not Profitendieu's biological son, Bernard seeks out his closest schoolfriend Olivier Molinier in the Luxembourg Gardens — a meeting that establishes the novel's most significant friendship and sets in motion the chain of events that will bind both young men to Edouard and to each other.

## Summary

Bernard walks to the Luxembourg Gardens, where his schoolfellows habitually meet on Wednesday afternoons. Through the railings he spots Olivier among a larger-than-usual gathering. The meeting that follows is marked by Bernard's exhilaration at his newfound freedom and Olivier's characteristic reserve — a dynamic that will define their relationship throughout the novel. Bernard, still vibrating from the shock of his discovery, feels liberated; Olivier, unaware of Bernard's circumstances, remains guarded (002-008).

## Context

### Narrative Position

The reunion occurs in Part I, Chapter I, immediately after Bernard's discovery of the love letters and his flight from the Profitendieu household. It is the first external event after the novel's opening crisis.

### Preceding Events

Bernard has discovered the cache of love letters in a secret drawer of Profitendieu's desk, revealing his mother's affair and his own illegitimacy. He has written a defiant farewell letter to Profitendieu and left the family home with nothing but the clothes on his back (002-001, 002-004).

## Detailed Account

The Luxembourg Gardens serve as the regular meeting place for Bernard's circle of schoolfellows. On this particular Wednesday, the gathering is larger than usual owing to fine weather, and includes newcomers Bernard has never seen. The narrator notes how each boy, "as soon as he was in company with the others, lost his naturalness and began to act a part" (002-008) — an observation that establishes the novel's thematic concern with performance and authenticity.

Bernard, by contrast, is unusually uninhibited — his discovery has paradoxically freed him from social constraint. The encounter initiates a pattern: Bernard's boldness and Olivier's reticence complement each other, creating a friendship that will prove central to both characters' development.

> "There, in the path that overlooks the Medici fountains, some of his schoolfellows were in the habit of meeting every Wednesday afternoon, between four and six." (002-008)

## Characters Involved

- **[[bernard-profitendieu]]** — exhilarated by his escape, seeking connection and validation
- **[[olivier-molinier]]** — reserved, unaware of Bernard's crisis, the more cautious half of the friendship
- **[[edouard]]** — not present at this meeting but watching from a distance; his journal records his observations of both boys

## Consequences

### Immediate Aftermath

Bernard spends the night at Olivier's apartment, cementing their bond. Olivier's family becomes Bernard's temporary refuge, and through Olivier, Bernard meets Edouard for the first time (002-010, 010-024).

### Narrative Ripple Effects

The friendship established in this scene becomes the novel's emotional anchor. Bernard and Olivier's relationship — marked by devotion, jealousy, estrangement, and eventual reconciliation — parallels the novel's broader inquiry into authenticity. Olivier's later feeling of abandonment when Bernard leaves without saying goodbye (010-024) and Bernard's eventual return to the same bench in the Luxembourg Gardens (042-003) bookend the friendship's arc.

## Thematic Significance

The reunion scene introduces the novel's preoccupation with the tension between natural feeling and social performance. The schoolboys "acting a part" in the gardens contrast with Bernard's raw, unperformed emotion — he is, in this moment, incapable of pretense. The scene also establishes the Luxembourg Gardens as a symbolic space of freedom and connection, to which Bernard returns at key moments of crisis and resolution.

## Literary Technique

Gide presents the scene through a combination of external description and Bernard's internal consciousness. The narrator's ironic observation about the boys "acting a part" provides thematic commentary while Bernard's perspective drives the emotional immediacy. Edouard's later journal entries retrospectively reframe the scene, adding the complexity of an observer who will become a participant.
