# Edouard's Studio

Edouard's studio is the novel's most important domestic interior — a space that serves simultaneously as living quarters, writer's workshop, and stage for the novel's most intense interpersonal dramas.

## Location

The studio is located in Paris. It is a large room that opens onto Edouard's living space: "His room opened on to a vast studio" (037-011). The apartment includes a spare bedroom where Edouard can accommodate guests (038-001), and a couch that serves as an impromptu bed for Bernard: "The couch in my studio is at your service" (043-007).

## Description in the Novel

The studio is a writer's space, dominated by Edouard's working habits and the equipment of literary production. It is where he sits up late writing: "I was sitting up late in the studio, after having written this" (039-043). The room is large enough to accommodate visitors — Bernard, Olivier, Laura, and others circulate through it — but intimate enough for the intense conversations that define the novel's emotional landscape.

The studio's defining scene occurs when Olivier attempts suicide there. Edouard, hearing Olivier's distress, "took him in his arms, carried him into the studio, laid him on the carpet" (037-014). The same carpet where Olivier lies recovering later becomes the site of reconciliation. When Olivier is moved to the spare room, Edouard continues to watch over him: "he isn't in the studio. I have put him in the spare room" (038-001).

## Narrative Function

### Scenes Set Here

The studio hosts the novel's most dramatic crisis: Olivier's suicide attempt after the Argonauts banquet (037-011–037-014). Edouard nurses Olivier back to consciousness (037-034), and Pauline and George arrive to find him resting (038-039). Edouard and Bernard discuss the nature of sincerity and freedom: "as soon as we were back in the studio" (039-007). Bernard writes at the studio, working as Edouard's secretary late into the night (039-043). Edouard offers Bernard the couch: "The couch in my studio is at your service" (043-007).

### Symbolic Significance

The studio is the novel's space of artistic creation and moral honesty. It is where Edouard writes his novel-within-the-novel, _The Counterfeiters_, and where the boundary between life and art is most permeable. Unlike the bourgeois interiors of the Profitendieu or Molinier households, the studio is a bachelor's space — informal, adaptable, devoted to work rather than display. It is the only interior in the novel where characters can be genuinely vulnerable without social performance.

## Associated Characters

Edouard is the studio's owner and presiding intelligence. Bernard becomes his secretary and lives there (043-007). Olivier attempts suicide there (037-014). Pauline visits to see her son (038-039). Laura consults Edouard there. The studio is the novel's crossroads — characters enter bearing crises and leave transformed.

## Real-World Context

Gide himself lived and worked in a studio apartment in Paris, and Edouard's studio reflects the real living arrangement of the Parisian man of letters in the 1920s. The artist's studio as a space of freedom and unconventional domesticity was a well-established motif in modernist literature and art.
