# Azaïs

The elderly pastor who presides over the Vedel-Azaïs boarding school, where [[Boris]] is sent for his education and where the counterfeit coin ring operates under the cover of institutional piety.

## Role in the Novel

Pastor Azaïs is the moral figurehead of the institution that bears his name — the [[Azaïs Pension]] (Vedel-Azaïs school) — where much of the novel's action concerning the younger characters takes place. His rigid Protestant austerity is meant to provide moral guidance, but the school becomes the site of the novel's most concentrated corruption: the counterfeit coin ring, the bullying and eventual death of Boris, and the moral deformation of children under the guise of their protection.

## Biography

Azaïs is the patriarch of the Vedel-Azaïs family, an elderly pastor whose career and moral reputation give the school its authority. At Laura's wedding, "The old pastor thought it his duty to retrace the whole of the Azaïs family history, beginning with the grandfather, with whom he had been at school in Strasburg" (PN=013-019) — a portrait of institutional self-regard that borders on self-parody. Edouard observes: "Now that he has cut his hair en brosse, old Azaïs doesn't look like Walt Whitman any more" (PN=013-075), a detail that humanizes while it gently mocks. His moral rigidity is noted: "If one's opinions are the least bit different from his, Azaïs forces one to be hypocritical" (PN=013-077).

## Character Analysis

Azaïs embodies the novel's critique of institutional morality — the substitution of formal rectitude for genuine ethical engagement. The school he presides over is described as having "poisonous air" reigning "under the stifling cover of morality and religion" (PN-027-002). Gide's portrait is not hostile but elegiac: "Poor, dear old Azaïs!" (PN-013-088) recognizes the pathos of a man whose moral framework has become an instrument of the corruption it was meant to prevent. The tragedy of Boris — sent to Azaïs's school to be "cured" of "seeking after imaginary goods" (PN-025-024) — is the definitive judgment on the institution Azaïs represents.
