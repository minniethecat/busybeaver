# Rebellion

The instinct of opposition and refusal that drives the moral and narrative energy of [[André Gide]]'s _The Counterfeiters_ — the force that makes characters leave homes, defy parents, and challenge the established order, and the novel's ambivalent verdict on whether rebellion leads to [[freedom]] or merely a new form of bondage.

## Definition

In Gide's novel, rebellion is both a developmental necessity and a potential trap. The "habit of rebellion and opposition" (027-006) can become its own form of determinism — the rebel defined only by what he opposes rather than what he affirms. Gide distinguishes between the liberating rebellion that breaks false constraints and the compulsive rebellion that merely replaces one master with another.

## Role in The Counterfeiters

### Thematic Significance

Rebellion is the novel's engine of plot: [[Bernard Profitendieu|Bernard]] rebels against his family by leaving home (024-019); [[Vincent Molinier|Vincent]] rebels against bourgeois morality; the schoolboys rebel against institutional authority by circulating counterfeit coins. But Gide consistently tests whether rebellion produces genuine transformation or merely inverts the existing order without changing it.

> "Nothing that incites to rebellion is definitively dangerous" (013-120) — A radical claim that provocatively endorses rebellion while qualifying it: "definitively" suggests rebellion may be temporarily dangerous but not ultimately so, because the capacity for rebellion is the capacity for growth.

### Character Associations

| Character | Form of Rebellion | Outcome |
|-----------|-------------------|---------|
| **Bernard** | Leaves home, declares himself "a rebel, an outlaw" (024-019) | Rebellion matures into self-knowledge |
| **Vincent** | Rebels against conventional morality through affairs and abandonment | Rebellion becomes nihilism |
| **George** | Participates in the counterfeit coin ring as rebellion against school authority (033-033) | Rebellion co-opted by older manipulators |
| **Edouard** | Artistic rebellion against conventional novel form | Creative rebellion that generates new possibilities |

## Manifestations in the Text

### Key Scenes

Bernard's self-declaration as a rebel forms the novel's most explicit statement of the theme: "I took myself for a rebel, an outlaw, who tramples underfoot everything that opposes his desire" (024-019). The retrospective irony — "I took myself for" — signals the novel's critical distance from naive rebellion. Bernard's rebellion is real but incomplete; it is a beginning, not an end.

> "The habit he had formed of rebellion and opposition incited him to oppose the laws of nature" (027-006) — This description of a character whose rebellion has become reflexive warns against rebellion as mere habit, emptied of purpose.

The schoolboys' counterfeit coin ring represents rebellion corrupted. What begins as youthful defiance becomes a "shameful traffic" (041-041) in which older figures exploit the boys' rebellious impulses for profit. Gide thus distinguishes between authentic rebellion (Bernard's journey toward selfhood) and inauthentic rebellion (George's manipulation by the coin ring).

[[Edouard]]'s artistic rebellion — his refusal to write a conventional novel, his insistence on the journal form and the novel-within-the-novel — provides the novel's model of creative rebellion. Edouard "rebels against" literary convention (023-005) not to destroy but to discover new forms.

## Literary and Philosophical Context

Gide's treatment of rebellion reflects his engagement with [[Friedrich Nietzsche|Nietzsche]]'s concept of the "transvaluation of values" — the rebellion against inherited morality that creates new values. But Gide is more cautious than Nietzsche: the novel shows rebellion succeeding only when it is accompanied by self-examination (Bernard, Edouard) and failing when it is mere negation (Vincent). The influence of [[Fyodor Dostoevsky|Dostoevsky]] is also evident in the exploration of whether rebellion against moral order leads to freedom or nihilism.
