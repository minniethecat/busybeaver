# Marriage

Marriage in _The Counterfeiters_ is presented not as a sacred bond but as a social arrangement that trades authenticity for security — a form of counterfeit that passes for genuine coin in bourgeois society.

## Definition

In Gide's novel, marriage functions as the institutional framework within which the bourgeois family operates, but it is consistently portrayed as a compromise — sometimes tragic, sometimes merely hollow — between desire and social propriety. The novel examines marriage primarily through the lens of Protestant respectability, where the union is meant to sanctify domestic life but instead becomes a screen for secrets, adulteries, and the slow erosion of affection [009-021]. Gide treats marriage neither as an ideal to be pursued nor as an evil to be condemned outright, but as a social fact whose human cost the novel meticulously documents [023-001].

## Role in The Counterfeiters

### Thematic Significance

Marriage in the novel serves as a structural counterpoint to the ideal of individual authenticity that Gide's young protagonists pursue. Where Bernard Profitendieu seeks self-invention and Edouard pursues artistic freedom, the married characters — Laura Douviers, Profitendieu, Oscar Molinier — live within arrangements they did not choose or can no longer sustain [003-001][023-001]. Marriage becomes the novel's negative case study: it is what authenticity costs, what conformity produces, what happens when social expectation overrides desire.

The decrystallization metaphor, introduced in Edouard's journal, provides the novel's most sustained analysis of marital failure. Edouard observes that "the progressive and reciprocal decrystallization of a husband and wife after fifteen or twenty years of married life" is "an admirable subject for a novel" [009-021]. In courtship, the lover "cannot show himself as he really is, and moreover he does not see the beloved — but instead, an idol whom he decks out, a divinity whom he creates" [009-021]. Marriage, in this account, is the slow stripping away of illusion — not a deepening of knowledge but a disenchantment that reveals two strangers bound together.

### Character Associations

**Laura Douviers** is the novel's central case study in marital compromise. Her marriage to Felix Douviers is described from the outset as a resignation, not a choice: she "had resigned herself" to it, a surrender to the combined pressure of "reason, and her family, and I myself advise her to do" [009-019]. Edouard, who helped persuade her to accept the match, justifies it cynically: in a marriage without initial passion, Laura "is sure to discover in him all the more virtues for having had fewer illusions to begin with" [009-019]. The narrator later describes Laura's marriage as "nothing but a lugubrious barter" [036-004], framing the union as a transaction in which emotional truth is exchanged for social security.

**Albéric Profitendieu** embodies the marriage of convenience sustained by mutual silence. His household is built on a secret — Bernard is not his biological son — that both husband and wife maintain through an unspoken agreement [003-001]. Profitendieu's physical ailment, the pain in his liver, becomes metaphorically linked to this emotional burden: "he feels as if his grief were in his liver" [003-040]. The marriage endures, but at the cost of honesty.

**Oscar and Pauline Molinier** represent the marriage of appearances. Pauline "puts all her efforts into palliating Oscar's insufficiencies and weaknesses, into hiding them from everyone" [034-002]. She "speaks of her husband without contempt, but with a kind of indulgence" [034-002]. Oscar, who "allows himself to be led by his wife" and submits because "he has something to be forgiven him" [029-020], compensates by making "the responsibility of his own shortcomings fall upon his wife's virtue" [029-025]. Their union is a performance of domestic harmony maintained at the price of self-deception on both sides.

**The La Pérouse marriage** presents the darkest version: two elderly partners "tied to each other for life and causing each other abominable suffering" [019-011], each convinced they are the victim and the other the torturer. The narrator observes that "the better I understand them, the more tempered my judgment of them becomes" — a refusal to condemn that nonetheless refuses to excuse [019-011].

**Felix Douviers** is the novel's most fully realized portrait of the well-meaning husband who is, precisely because of his virtues, the wrong partner. Pastor Vedel, though he encouraged the match, confesses his doubts: "he is afraid Douviers may not be the right husband for her" [013-083]. Edouard, more blunt, finds in Douviers "something abstract and simple-minded and credulous" [013-001]. The novel's judgment is that goodness alone cannot sustain a marriage — Douviers' very decency becomes an instrument of Laura's entrapment.

## Manifestations in the Text

### Key Scenes

The wedding ceremony in the Rue Madame chapel is the novel's most concentrated depiction of marriage as social theater. "The whole of the Vedel-Azaïs families were present," along with "quantities of uncles, aunts and cousins," while the Douviers family "was represented by three aunts in deep mourning" [013-018]. The traditional wedding breakfast is replaced by "a less costly tea" [013-039], a detail that underscores the family's straitened Protestant circumstances. The entire scene reads as a ritual of public ratification in which the bride's emotional reality — she is marrying a man she does not love — is irrelevant to the proceedings.

Laura's affair with Vincent, disclosed through Edouard's journal, provides the counter-narrative to the wedding. Vincent "taught her what her husband had never been able to teach" at the sanatorium in Pau [006-052] — the novel's elliptical way of saying that Douviers offered Laura neither passion nor sexual fulfillment. When Laura becomes pregnant by Vincent and is subsequently abandoned, her situation exposes the vulnerability that marriage was supposed to protect her from [005-003].

Bernard Profitendieu's discovery of his illegitimacy — that his mother had an affair and that Albéric Profitendieu is not his biological father — reveals the secret at the heart of the Profitendieu marriage [003-001]. The marriage has functioned as a cover for a transgression, and its stability depends on the maintenance of that cover.

> "People are always talking of the sudden crystallization of love. Its slow _decrystallization_, which I never hear talked of, is a psychological phenomenon which interests me far more. I consider that it can be observed, after a longer or shorter period, in all love marriages." [009-019]

### Symbolic Expressions

The novel repeatedly deploys economic metaphors for marriage, consistent with the counterfeit coin imagery that runs through the text. Laura's marriage is "a lugubrious barter" [036-004]; the wedding substitutes "a less costly tea" for the traditional breakfast [013-039]; the husband's infidelity is rationalized by the wife's "virtue" as a form of moral currency [029-020]. Marriage, like the counterfeit coin, circulates in society at face value while its substance is hollow.

The sanatorium at Pau, where Vincent and Laura's affair begins, functions as a symbolic space outside the normal social order — a place where "as they thought they were doomed to die an early death, they persuaded themselves that nothing they did would be of any consequence" [006-052]. It is the anti-marriage, the space where marital obligation is suspended and desire operates without institutional constraint.

Gide also employs the physical house as a symbol of marital entrapment. Profitendieu's home, entered at the novel's opening, is a space of "solitude" rather than intimacy [003-001]. The La Pérouse apartment is a domestic prison where two people torment each other without escape [019-011]. Pauline remarks that "the youths who are chastest turn into the best husbands — nor even, unfortunately, the most faithful" [039-008], a sardonic observation that separates marital virtue from marital happiness.

## Literary and Philosophical Context

Gide's treatment of marriage reflects his own rejection of bourgeois heterosexual norms — he himself entered a platonic marriage with his cousin Madeleine while pursuing same-sex relationships — but the novel's critique is broader than autobiography. The decrystallization theory articulated in Edouard's journal draws on Stendhal's concept of crystallization in _De l'Amour_ (1822), which described falling in love as the process by which the imagination adorns the beloved with imaginary perfections. Gide inverts Stendhal: marriage is not the fulfillment of crystallization but its undoing [009-021].

The novel's portrayal of marriage also engages with Protestant culture, where marriage carries a particularly heavy moral weight. The Azaïs-Vedel clan's piety does not make their marriages happier; it makes their hypocrisies more elaborate. This aligns with Gide's broader critique — influenced by Nietzsche and Dostoevsky — of institutions that claim moral authority while producing moral deformation. Marriage, in this tradition, is the counterfeit coin's natural habitat: an institution that demands public conformity while generating private falsehood.
