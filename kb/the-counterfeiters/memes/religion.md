# Religion

In Andre Gide's _The Counterfeiters_, religion forms the cultural and moral background against which characters define themselves — primarily through the tension between Protestant seriousness and Catholic difference, between inherited faith and modern unbelief.

## Definition

Religion in _The Counterfeiters_ is less a matter of belief than of atmosphere and inheritance. The novel's world is predominantly Protestant: the Vedel-Azaïs family, the boarding school, the moral seriousness that pervades character judgment. As Edouard notes, "the society was exclusively Protestant. The odour of Puritanism is peculiar to itself" (013-040). Yet individual characters occupy different positions: Pauline's children "have been brought up as Catholics" though she remembers her "early Protestant training" (012-044); young Armand declares "I don't care a damn about any of it. I'm a Catholic" (013-020) — using Catholicism as a gesture of rebellion against his Protestant family.

## Role in The Counterfeiters

### Thematic Significance

The Protestant context provides the moral vocabulary through which the novel's central questions are asked: sincerity, purity, honesty, the counterfeit. The Protestant tradition of self-examination — the anxious scrutiny of one's own motives — is the psychological engine of Edouard's journal and Gide's narrative method.

But religion also functions as a constraint to be escaped. The younger generation's revolt is often expressed as religious rebellion. The old pastor Vedel's moral exhortations represent what the young must break free from (013-078). The novel records the transfer of moral seriousness from religious to aesthetic and psychological domains — the "pure novel" is a secularized Protestant purity.

### Character Associations

**Edouard** embodies the secularized Protestant — retaining the self-scrutiny without the faith. **Armand** performs Catholic difference as rebellion. **Pauline** represents the mother who navigates between traditions. **Pastor Vedel** is the voice of institutional Protestantism, "too busy" for actual pastoral care. **Laura** carries the burden of Protestant guilt into her personal life.

## Manifestations in the Text

### Key Scenes

Laura's wedding at the chapel in Rue Madame (013-018–013-020) gathers the entire Vedel-Azaïs clan and dramatizes the generational relationship to religion. The old pastor's speech, tracing family history through theological institutions, represents religion as inheritance. Armand's muttered "I'm a Catholic" is the generational counter-statement.

The description of "the odour of Puritanism" (013-040) at a gathering of Douviers' colleagues establishes religion as a sensory atmosphere — something in the air rather than a set of propositions. Gide compares it to the emanations of Catholic or Jewish gatherings, suggesting that all religious communities produce their characteristic atmosphere.

> "The odour of Puritanism is peculiar to itself." (013-040)

### Symbolic Expressions

The Protestant atmosphere is the novel's invisible architecture — everywhere present, rarely named. The counterfeit coin can be read as a secularized Eucharist: false value passing for real, appearance substituting for substance. Edouard's journal secularizes the Protestant practice of diary-keeping as spiritual discipline.

## Literary and Philosophical Context

Gide's treatment of religion draws on his own biography: raised Protestant in a Catholic country, he experienced religion as both a moral resource and a constraint to be overcome. His reading of Nietzsche provided the philosophical critique of Christian morality, while Dostoevsky demonstrated how religious themes could animate the psychological novel. The novel's religious landscape also reflects the aftermath of the 1905 French law separating church and state.
