# La Pérouse

The aged music-master whose faded glory, impoverished circumstances, and tragic estrangement from his son represent one of the novel's most affecting portraits of suffering — a man whose dignity persists despite a life of accumulated loss.

## Role in the Novel

La Pérouse is introduced at the periphery — playing the harmonium at Laura Douviers's wedding (PN=013-018) — but Edouard's subsequent visit to him in Chapter 14 transforms him into one of the novel's most memorable characters. His story of his son's estrangement and his wife's decline serves as a kind of negative exemplum: while the young characters are throwing their lives away through restless dissatisfaction, La Pérouse has lost everything through forces beyond his control. He represents authentic suffering in a novel preoccupied with counterfeit emotions.

## Biography

### Background

La Pérouse was once a piano teacher, and among his pupils was the young Edouard — "in the days when he used to give me piano lessons" (PN=013-018). He married and lived with his wife in Paris, but they have been forced by poverty to move repeatedly: "Old Monsieur and Madame de La Pérouse have changed houses again" (PN=014-001). His high, prominent forehead and noble, aged face strike Edouard with their beauty even in decline (PN=014-059).

### Key Actions

At Laura's wedding, La Pérouse plays the harmonium. Edouard observes that "his face has aged, but finer, nobler than ever — though his eye had lost that admirable fire and spirit I found so infectious" (PN=013-018). Their eyes meet and "there was so much sadness in the smile he gave me" that Edouard determines to visit him.

The visit becomes one of the novel's most extended and devastating monologues. La Pérouse reveals his tragic family history: he and his wife had a son, but something — the text suggests a moral failing or betrayal — has estranged them. "Little Boris is being educated in Poland" (PN=014-065), the boy's whereabouts unknown to his grandfather. La Pérouse's wife has descended into senility or mental illness: "Madame de La Pérouse is in a terrible state" (PN=014-041). She reproaches him constantly, misunderstands everything, and has even stolen a letter from him which she denies taking (PN=014-056).

### Relationships

**Madame de La Pérouse**: His wife, now mentally declining, who "won't understand" and "is always reproaching me" (PN=014-018, PN=014-022). La Pérouse married her with "innocence" (PN=014-049) and has remained loyal despite everything. **His Son**: The central tragedy of La Pérouse's life — a son who has abandoned him and whose whereabouts are unknown. **His Grandson Boris**: The one hope in La Pérouse's darkness — "Everything I care for... Little Boris" (PN=014-065). **Edouard**: His former pupil, whose visit prompts the outpouring of a lifetime's grief.

## Character Analysis

### Motivations

La Pérouse's motivation is simple and heartbreaking: he wants to see his grandson Boris, to recover some connection to the future in the wreckage of his past. His visit with Edouard is not a plea for help so much as a release — a need to tell his story to someone who might understand. His dignity consists in not begging, not complaining, but simply presenting the facts of his suffering.

### Symbolic Role

La Pérouse represents authentic tragedy in a novel otherwise dominated by the self-inflicted miseries of the young and restless. His suffering is not chosen, not performative, not counterfeit. He is what happens to a person when life strips away everything and only character remains. His harmonium playing — a modest, unshowy art now faded — embodies the quiet, unambitious authenticity that the novel's more sophisticated characters have lost.

### Development

La Pérouse does not develop; he endures. His role is static by design — a fixed point of genuine pain against which the novel's more restless, self-dramatizing suffering can be measured.

## Literary Context

Gide handles La Pérouse's monologue with extraordinary restraint. The old man's story emerges in fragments, digressions, and silences. The physical detail of the uncarpeted stairs, the "musty smell," the "clean, poor, and sad" apartment (PN=014-005) creates a powerful sense of diminished gentility. The chapter is one of the novel's most direct emotional passages — Edouard's journalistic distance momentarily collapses before the reality of La Pérouse's grief.

## Key Passages
