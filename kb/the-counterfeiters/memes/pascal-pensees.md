# Pensées (Pascal)

Blaise Pascal's _Pensées_ (1670), a posthumous collection of fragments toward an unfinished apology for Christianity, is one of the foundational texts of French literature. In _The Counterfeiters_, Pascal's work is quoted by [[Armand Vedel]] in a hotel-room confession and serves as the epigraph to [[Olivier Molinier|Olivier]]'s convalescence.

## Overview

The _Pensées_ gathers Pascal's notes on the human condition — suspended between angel and beast, reason and faith, greatness and wretchedness. Its central insight, that the self is irreducibly complex, made it the definitive expression of the tragic dimension in French Catholic thought.

Gide read Pascal throughout his life and wrestled with the Pascalian anthropology he both inherited and sought to overcome.

## Appearance in The Counterfeiters

### Context of Mention

The _Pensées_ appears first in a conversation between Armand Vedel and Olivier Molinier. Armand, in a rare moment of unguarded seriousness, unburdens himself of his obsession with limits — the exact threshold where one thing becomes another, where resistance snaps, where damnation begins. He arrives at Pascal:

> "The face of the world would have been changed," says Pascal. But it's not enough for me to think — "if Cleopatra's nose had been shorter." I insist. I ask: shorter, by how much? (035-033)

Armand alludes to Pascal's fragment on Cleopatra's nose — had it been shorter, history would have been different. But Armand radicalizes the thought: he needs to know the precise quantum at which a change occurs. His obsession with "gradation, gradation, and then a sudden leap" (035-033) is a perverse intensification of Pascal's sensitivity to infinitesimal thresholds.

Armand then quotes another Pascal passage — Christ's words from the _Mystère de Jésus_:

> "I shed that very tear for thee..." Olivier remembered Pascal's words; he was even a little put out that his friend had not quoted them exactly. He could not refrain from correcting: "I shed that very drop of blood for thee." (035-036)

Armand misquotes "tear" for "blood," sentimentalizing the theological original. Olivier's correction reveals his precise literary education and the competitive pedantry latent in their friendship.

The _Pensées_ also appears as the epigraph to Part III, Chapter X:

> Rien n'est simple de ce qui s'offre à l'âme; et l'âme ne s'offre jamais simple à aucun sujet. — Pascal

"Nothing is simple that presents itself to the soul; and the soul never presents itself as simple to any object." This frames Olivier's recovery from his suicide attempt as a Pascal-inflected meditation on consciousness — the soul watches itself watch itself, a recursive structure that mirrors the novel's formal method.

Later, Armand dismisses his own prior sincerity: "I played my lament, with a soul like a corkscrew and anguish à la Pascal" (045-031). Here "anguish à la Pascal" becomes something performed rather than suffered — a cultural pose that both invokes and ironizes the tradition.

### Narrative Function

By lodging Pascal's vision in Armand — the novel's most articulate skeptic and its least reliable speaker — Gide both honors its intellectual power and ironizes it. The truth of Pascal's diagnosis of human wretchedness is not denied; it is shown to be a truth that cannot be spoken straight without falsifying it.

Armand's "corkscrew" soul is a Pascalian soul that has learned self-consciousness, and self-consciousness, for Pascal, is itself a mark of the Fall.

The epigraph performs a different function: it invokes Pascal to guarantee psychological seriousness at a moment of inwardness. The soul's non-simplicity is here taken not as evidence of corruption but as the ground of novelistic art — a secularization of Pascal's theological insight.

## Thematic Resonance

Pascal's anthropology — man is fallen, divided, incapable of self-knowledge without grace — is the background against which Gide develops his counter-doctrine of sincerity and self-creation.

Where Pascal saw in the self's multiplicity a wound requiring divine healing, Gide saw a richness requiring aesthetic form. Yet Gide never ceased to feel Pascal's force. The critic Ramon Fernandez observed that Gide's _oeuvre_ could be read as a "dialogue with Pascal" — an attempt to answer the Pascalian critique of the self without resort to supernatural grace.

## Gide's Relationship to the Work

Gide read the _Pensées_ as an adolescent under the influence of his Protestant mother; he returned to them throughout his _Journal_. He called Pascal "the one who taught me to distrust myself."

This admission simultaneously honors Pascal and registers the distance Gide traveled from his conclusions. The _Pensées_ represented the Catholic tradition of French moral seriousness — a tradition Gide could never simply reject, and whose terms shaped even his rebellion against them.
