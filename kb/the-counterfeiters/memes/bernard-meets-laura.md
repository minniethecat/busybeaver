# Bernard Meets Laura

At the Swiss sanatorium of Saas-Fée, Bernard Profitendieu meets Laura Douviers in person — a woman whose private letters and intimate history he has already read in Edouard's stolen journal.

## Summary

Having accompanied Edouard to Saas-Fée as his secretary, Bernard comes face to face with Laura Douviers, the woman whose desperate letter to Edouard he read during his illicit perusal of the novelist's journal. The meeting is charged with dramatic irony: Bernard knows Laura's most intimate secrets — her pregnancy, her abandonment by Vincent, her appeal to Edouard — while Laura knows nothing of his violation of her privacy. Their subsequent friendship is built on this asymmetrical knowledge. [PN=019-040] [PN=020-001] [PN=026-018]

## Context

### Narrative Position

This event occurs in **Part II, Chapters 1-2 (Saas-Fée)** , at the beginning of the novel's middle section. Bernard has traveled with Edouard to the Swiss sanatorium where Sophroniska treats Boris, and where Laura is staying to escape the scandal of her situation in Paris. [PN=020-001] [PN=019-040]

### Preceding Events

Bernard stole Edouard's suitcase at the Gare Saint-Lazare (see [[edouard-loses-his-journal]]) and read his journal, which contained Laura's letter and Edouard's reflections on his affair with her. After meeting Edouard and confessing the theft, Bernard became his traveling companion to Switzerland. Now at Saas-Fée, he encounters the woman he has already met on the page. [PN=015-001] [PN=026-018] [PN=019-040]

## Detailed Account

Laura is staying at a pension in Saas-Fée, near Sophroniska's sanatorium. She has come to Switzerland to escape Paris, where her pregnancy — visible now — would invite scandal. Edouard, who has arranged for her stay, brings Bernard to meet her at the pension. [PN=019-040]

The meeting is characterized by what Bernard does not say. He behaves with careful politeness, concealing everything he knows. He has read Edouard's journal entry about Laura: "Laura does not seem to suspect her power; as for me, who can penetrate to the very depths of my heart, I know that up to this present moment not a word I have written has not been indirectly inspired by her." [PN=009-007]

Laura, for her part, is wary of this unknown young man. She has been through humiliation and uncertainty. Bernard's youth and energy, however, make him welcome company in the isolated Swiss village. Over the following days, they take walks together and talk — Bernard careful never to betray his illicit knowledge, Laura grateful for undemanding companionship. [PN=020-001] [PN=022-035]

## Characters Involved

- **Bernard Profitendieu** — The young runaway who meets Laura already knowing her secrets, and must navigate the ethics of his illicit knowledge
- **Laura Douviers** — The pregnant, abandoned woman who has no idea that Bernard has read her private letter
- **Edouard** — The novelist who has brought Bernard to Saas-Fée but remains unaware of exactly what Bernard knows from the journal

## Consequences

### Immediate Aftermath

Bernard and Laura develop a genuine friendship in the rarified atmosphere of Saas-Fée. Bernard's knowledge of her situation makes him unusually gentle and considerate toward her — he becomes, in effect, the protector that Vincent failed to be and that Edouard cannot quite manage to become. Laura later tells Edouard that Bernard has been "a great comfort" to her in Switzerland. [PN=022-035] [PN=023-087]

### Narrative Ripple Effects

Bernard's relationship with Laura is the novel's most complex example of the tension between knowledge and intimacy. His secret knowledge allows him to be a better friend than he could otherwise be, but it also fundamentally falsifies their relationship. Bernard's decision to stay at Saas-Fée longer than planned — and his growing attachment to Laura — complicates his developing sense of self. The friendship also creates a triangular tension with Edouard, who is both Laura's former lover and Bernard's patron. [PN=026-018]

## Thematic Significance

This meeting dramatizes the novel's central concern with **the ethics of secret knowledge**. Bernard's reading of the journal has given him power over Laura — he knows what she does not know he knows — and his subsequent kindness is tainted by the violation from which it springs. The scene asks uncomfortable questions: Can a relationship built on concealed knowledge ever be authentic? Is the kindness made possible by illicit information a genuine kindness or another form of counterfeiting? Bernard's situation also illuminates the reader's own position, since we too possess knowledge about Laura that she does not have about us.

## Literary Technique

Gide employs **dramatic irony** throughout the Saas-Fée section, creating a constant tension between what Bernard knows and what he reveals. Every conversation between Bernard and Laura operates on two levels — the surface talk and the subtext of Bernard's hidden knowledge. This technique is particularly effective because the reader shares Bernard's guilty knowledge; we are complicit in the violation of Laura's privacy, and our discomfort mirrors Bernard's.
