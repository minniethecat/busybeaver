# Armand Vedel

The son of Pastor Prosper Vedel, a brilliant and cynical young man whose deliberate coarseness masks a sensitive nature, whose rejection of his Protestant heritage and declaration of Catholicism represents an act of rebellion against his family and upbringing.

## Role in the Novel

Armand Vedel functions as a foil to Olivier Molinier — both are intelligent young men contending with a repressive religious upbringing, but where Olivier turns inward and seeks the guidance of Edouard, Armand turns outward in aggressive cynicism. He "has a kind of desire to spoil everything he most cares for" (PN=013-119) and performs vulgarity as a form of protest. His presence at the Vedel-Azaïs school and his sharp observations of his family's hypocrisies make him an important critical voice in the novel's anatomy of Protestant domestic life.

## Biography

### Background

Armand is the son of Pastor Prosper Vedel and his wife, the pastoress, and grandson of old Azaïs, the founder of the boarding school where he lives and studies. He grows up in an intensely religious household where, as Edouard observes, "the deeper the soul plunges into religious devotion, the more it loses all sense of reality" (PN=013-085). His parents intended him for the ministry — "they wanted to make a pastor of him" (PN=013-119) — but he has violently rejected this path.

### Key Actions

At Laura Douviers's wedding, Armand acts as the cynical chorus, making "ridiculous remarks" and clowning with an English boarding girl (PN=013-090). He declares with contempt that Laura should accept Douviers despite his lack of charm because "with only twenty thousand francs for a dot, one could hardly look for anything better" and that as a Christian she should prioritize "spiritual qualities, as our father the pastor would say" (PN=013-095). He locks the door to his mother's sitting room and tells guests to enter "through the chimney" (PN=013-039). Yet Edouard recognizes that Armand's vulgarity is "entirely put on" — he remains "a delicate, sensitive nature" (PN=013-098) who performs coarseness because he is unhappy and misunderstood.

### Relationships

**Olivier Molinier**: Armand's closest friend. Olivier tells Edouard that Armand "hasn't been like that long. I think he's very unhappy and that he jokes in order to hide it" (PN=013-119). The two boys share drunken confidences and cigarettes at the wedding. **Sarah Vedel**: His sister, with whom he trades barbs. When Armand makes his cynical remarks about Laura's marriage, Sarah murmurs "What an idiot! Don't listen to him" (PN=013-096). **Pastor Vedel**: His father, whose hypocrisy Armand sees clearly. **Laura Douviers**: His sister, whose disastrous marriage he comments on with bitter humor. **The English Girl**: A boarder at the school whom Armand tries to entertain with his vulgar antics (PN=013-090).

## Character Analysis

### Motivations

Armand's cynicism is a defense against the suffocating piety of his home. His Catholicism is less a genuine conversion than a rebellion — a way of saying no to everything his family stands for. His desire "to spoil everything he most cares for" suggests a young man who cannot bear sincerity because sincerity has been perverted by the religious language of his upbringing. He represents the self-destructive side of rebellion against bourgeois Protestantism.

### Symbolic Role

Armand embodies the counterfeit of belief. In a family where piety is performance, his own declaration of Catholic faith may be equally performative — a counterfeit conversion that mirrors the counterfeit coins circulating through the school. His character raises the question: in a world of religious hypocrisy, is sincere unbelief more authentic than insincere faith?

### Development

Armand does not develop as much as reveal himself. Edouard's journal peels back layers of performance to glimpse the sensitive, unhappy boy beneath. But whether Armand will ever outgrow his cynicism or find a more authentic mode of being remains an open question.

## Literary Context

Gide presents Armand almost entirely through Edouard's journal, filtering his character through an observing consciousness that is both sympathetic and critical. The wedding scene in Chapter 13 is a tour de force of multi-character staging, with Armand playing a key role as the disruptor of pious convention. His declaration "I'm a Catholic" is one of the novel's many acts of identity-counterfeiting.

## Key Passages

> "I have tried to make them understand that with only twenty thousand francs for a dot, one could hardly look for anything better, and that, as a true Christian, she ought first of all to take into account his spiritual qualities, as our father the pastor would say." — Armand's bitter commentary on Laura's marriage (PN=013-095)

> "He's very proud. His parents don't understand him at all. They wanted to make a pastor of him." — Olivier on Armand (PN=013-119)
