# Music

In Andre Gide's _The Counterfeiters_, music appears as a disruptive emotional force — something that "disturbs" rational thought, associated with the aged music-master La Pérouse and his tragic family history.

## Definition

Music in _The Counterfeiters_ is less a subject of aesthetic theory than a presence that marks emotional intensity. When Bernard's (adoptive) father Profitendieu is trying to think, he tells his daughter: "your music disturbs me" (003-044). The narrator adds that Profitendieu "knew nothing whatever about music" — his aversion is to music's emotional claim, not its content. Music represents what resists intellectual ordering: feeling, memory, the irrational.

## Role in The Counterfeiters

### Thematic Significance

Music functions as the novel's figure for pure emotion — untheorized, unrationalized, irreducible to the intellectual frameworks characters construct. In a novel dominated by self-consciousness and self-justification, music offers an alternative mode of experience: immediate rather than reflective.

The figure of La Pérouse, the old music-master, embodies music's tragic dimension. His son's story — revealed to Edouard in a long confession (014-049) — is the novel's most sustained account of paternal grief. Music teaching is La Pérouse's livelihood and his consolation, but also the scene of his isolation.

### Character Associations

**La Pérouse** is music's primary embodiment — aged, impoverished, living in memory of a lost son. **Bernard** is the character who remembers La Pérouse and prompts Edouard to visit him (015-083), connecting music to the theme of neglected obligations. **Profitendieu** represents music's opposite — the rational mind that finds music "disturbing." **Edouard** visits La Pérouse (019-008) and hears his story, becoming the repository of musical grief.

## Manifestations in the Text

### Key Scenes

Bernard's reminder to Edouard about La Pérouse (015-083) is a brief but significant moment — Bernard, despite his own concerns, remembers the neglected old man. The gesture reveals Bernard's developing moral awareness.

Edouard's visit to La Pérouse's music room (019-008) is the entry point to one of the novel's most emotionally intense sequences — La Pérouse's confession about his son. The room where music lessons are given becomes the room where life's tragedies are told.

> "Your music disturbs me." (003-044)

### Symbolic Expressions

The music room with its two windows looking onto the courtyard (019-008) is a space of enclosure and limited vision — like music itself, which opens emotional vistas within confined physical circumstances. La Pérouse's name — "the perch" — suggests precariousness, and his music is the art of surviving on a perch.

## Literary and Philosophical Context

Gide was not primarily a musical novelist in the manner of Proust or Thomas Mann, but music enters _The Counterfeiters_ through the character system rather than through aesthetic theory. The La Pérouse subplot draws on the nineteenth-century tradition of the artist as marginalized figure, while the association of music with emotional disturbance reflects a broader modernist anxiety about the claims of feeling on the rational mind.
