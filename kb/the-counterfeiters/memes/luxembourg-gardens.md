# Luxembourg Gardens

The Luxembourg Gardens serve as the novel's primary meeting place for its adolescent characters — a liminal space between home and school, family constraint and personal freedom, where the novel's opening action unfolds.

## Location

The Luxembourg Gardens (Jardin du Luxembourg) is a public park in the 6th arrondissement of Paris, created in 1612 by Marie de' Medici. In the novel, it borders the neighborhoods where the Profitendieu, Molinier, and Vedel-Azaïs families live. The street where Bernard grew up "was quite close to the Luxembourg Gardens" (002-008).

## Description in the Novel

Gide uses the Gardens as a recurring spatial anchor. Schoolboys meet "in the path that runs along by the Medici fountains" (002-008). The Gardens are described through the characters' habitual movements: Bernard follows "the railings of the Luxembourg Gardens, goes down the Rue Bonaparte" (007-004) after running away from home. The Gardens are a space of leisurely encounter — Edouard meets a companion who "walked across the Luxembourg Gardens with me" (013-001); later, Bernard enters the Gardens, finds a bench, and sits down with his book (042-003).

The Gardens are neither private nor entirely public — they are where the characters display themselves to each other, where encounters occur that would not take place in the domestic interiors that dominate the novel's other settings.

## Narrative Function

### Scenes Set Here

The novel's opening sequence: Bernard catches sight of Olivier through the railings, and the social world of the schoolboys is first established (002-008). After running away from home, Bernard walks along the Gardens' railings (007-004). Edouard meets a friend there and converses while walking (013-001). Bernard uses the Gardens as a place to sit, read, and reflect (037-008, 042-003). The Gardens also serve as a meeting point: "We went and sat down in the Luxembourg, till it should be time for lunch" (029-007).

### Symbolic Significance

The Luxembourg Gardens represent the threshold between childhood and adulthood, between the domestic order of the family and the open possibilities of the city. They are where boys become visible to each other and to adults outside the family structure. The Gardens are also associated with intellectual life — Bernard reads there, characters discuss ideas while walking — making them the novel's outdoor salon, a space of free circulation and thought.

## Associated Characters

Bernard Profitendieu is the character most closely associated with the Gardens: his home is nearby (002-008), he uses the Gardens as a refuge (042-003), and it is through the railings that he first sees Olivier. Olivier Molinier meets his schoolfellows there (002-008, 002-011). Edouard walks through the Gardens with companions (013-001) and encounters Bercail there (036-045). The novel's youth population circulates through the Gardens after school hours.

## Real-World Context

The Luxembourg Gardens have been a fixture of Parisian intellectual and literary life since the 17th century. In the 1920s, the area around the Gardens was home to much of the Protestant bourgeoisie that Gide himself belonged to. The Gardens appear in the work of many French modernists as a symbol of cultivated public space where private dramas unfold.
