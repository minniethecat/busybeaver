# Fyodor Dostoevsky

The Russian novelist (1821–1881) whose works profoundly influenced [[André Gide]]'s conception of _The Counterfeiters_, particularly in its treatment of moral ambiguity, psychological depth, and narrative multiplicity.

## Role in the Novel

Dostoevsky does not appear as a character, but his influence on the novel's technique and thematic concerns is pervasive. Gide gave a series of influential lectures on Dostoevsky in 1922, just before writing _The Counterfeiters_, and the Russian novelist's practice of presenting characters whose moral positions are genuinely irreconcilable — what Mikhail Bakhtin would later call "polyphony" — directly informs the novel's refusal to adjudicate among its competing perspectives. Edouard's journal entries, Bernard's adventures, Olivier's crisis, and Laura's predicament coexist without a single authoritative narrator to resolve their contradictions.

## Relationship to the Novel's Themes

### Polyphonic Structure

Dostoevsky's novels are characterized by what Gide called a "plurality of consciousnesses" — independent voices that are not subordinated to the author's point of view. _The Counterfeiters_ adopts this principle through its alternation between Edouard's journal, the omniscient narrator's perspective, letters, and dialogue. No single character's understanding is privileged as final, and the reader must navigate among competing accounts of the same events. This structural polyphony is itself an argument about the nature of truth: it cannot be monopolized by a single consciousness.

### Moral Ambiguity

Gide admired Dostoevsky's capacity to present morally compromised characters without condemning them. This influence is visible in Gide's treatment of Vincent, whose abandonment of Laura is presented as morally culpable but psychologically comprehensible, and in the novel's refusal to resolve Olivier's oscillation between Passavant's worldliness and Edouard's idealism into a simple moral choice.

## Literary Context

Gide's engagement with Dostoevsky was part of a broader French reception of Russian literature in the early twentieth century. Gide's 1922 lectures, later published as _Dostoevsky_, argued that the Russian novelist had achieved what French literature had not: a fiction in which ideas are lived rather than merely discussed, and in which psychological truth takes precedence over moral judgment. _The Counterfeiters_ represents Gide's attempt to incorporate these Dostoevskian principles into a distinctively French modernist novel.
