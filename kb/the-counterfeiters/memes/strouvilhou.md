# Strouvilhou

Victor Strouvilhou is the mastermind of the counterfeit coin ring operating through schoolboys, a former boarder at the Vedel household, and cousin of Léon Ghéridanisol — embodying the novel's darkest currents of nihilism, criminality, and the cynical exploitation of respectable society's secrets.

## Role in the Novel

Strouvilhou is the novel's most menacing figure of actual criminal counterfeit — not the metaphorical kind explored through Edouard's novel or Passavant's literary salon, but the literal circulation of false coins through a network of schoolboys.

He appears late and intermittently, yet his influence radiates through the younger characters and culminates in the novel's tragic finale, linking the schoolroom intrigues of George, Ghéridanisol, and Boris with the adult world of Passavant's drawing room.

Strouvilhou is introduced through Laura's childhood memories of the Vedel household, where he was a boarder whose insolence made a lasting impression (013-047). He reappears in Edouard's journal at Saas-Fée, where his name surfaces in the hotel visitors' book (023-087), and he obtains Boris's talisman from Sophroniska (025-021).

His most significant appearances come in the novel's second half: the lunchtime conversation with Ghéridanisol outlining the counterfeit coin scheme (033-051), and his extended interview with the Comte de Passavant, who offers him the editorship of a review (040-050).

## Biography

### Background

Strouvilhou was an independent boarder at the Vedel household ten years before the novel's main action, supposedly attending lectures but answering all questions with "I vary". The pastor tolerated him — Laura speculates — out of "a feeling that was half affection, half pity, for Strouvilhou, and perhaps a vague hope that he might end by persuading — I mean converting — him" (013-050).

His stay ended when the patriarch, enraged by his dinner-table spitefulness, pointed to the door and shouted: "Leave the room!" (013-059).

By the novel's present, Strouvilhou has drifted through "a good many trades" and become far more dangerous. He is the older cousin of Léon Ghéridanisol, a boarder at the Azais-Vedel school.

### Key Actions

**The counterfeit coin operation.** Strouvilhou possesses "fifty-two boxes containing twenty coins each" (033-051) and distributes them through schoolboys, targeting "those who come of good families, because then if rumours get about, their parents do all they can to stifle them" (033-051).

He instructs Ghéridanisol to gather compromising material on families — "the kids must be made to compromise themselves" (033-053) to give them leverage.

**The Saas-Fée interlude.** Strouvilhou stays at Saas-Fée for nearly a month, meets Sophroniska, and obtains Boris's talisman — the magic incantation paper — which he later gives to Ghéridanisol, enabling the psychological torment that drives Boris to suicide (046-013).

**The Passavant interview.** Strouvilhou arrives with a "flunkey's insolence" (040-029) and delivers a philosophical monologue declaring that philanthropy "was never one of my strong points" and "nor egoism either" (040-037, 040-039).

He denounces literature as "compromise and flattery" (040-051) and proposes eugenics: "What we ought to work at is the amelioration of the breed" (040-047). Passavant offers him a review's editorship — the criminal becoming gatekeeper of literary respectability.

**The cover-up.** When Edouard warns George about the false coins, Ghéridanisol dumps the remaining coins down the drains. "That same evening he warned Strouvilhou, who immediately took his precautions" (044-081).

### Relationships

**Léon Ghéridanisol**: Strouvilhou's cousin, groomed as his lieutenant. Strouvilhou praises Léon as "one of the elect" (040-060).

**Comte de Passavant**: Mutual exploitation masked as camaraderie. Passavant "considered himself his match" (040-026); their meeting is a duel of literary and criminal counterfeit ending in a bargain.

**Edouard**: The novelist observes Strouvilhou at a distance, noting: "I should have been curious to see him again" (023-087).

**Sophroniska**: Strouvilhou exploits her trust at Saas-Fée to obtain Boris's talisman, which becomes a weapon leading to Boris's death.

## Character Analysis

### Motivations

Strouvilhou is driven not by greed but by principled nihilism. He systematizes the counterfeit operation, recruits accomplices, and develops a philosophy to justify it. His contempt for "a lot of men" — the conviction that no "addition of a number of sordid units can result in an enchanting total" (040-039) — fuels comprehensive hostility to social bonds.

### Symbolic Role

Strouvilhou represents counterfeit at the street level — the literal false coin mirroring the moral counterfeits of Vincent (self-deception), Passavant (literary fraud), and the Molinier household (bourgeois hypocrisy). His coins pass through George Molinier's hands, creating a circuit between criminal underworld and respectable society — the two counterfeits are, finally, one.

His obtaining of Boris's talisman is the novel's most concrete illustration of adult corruption poisoning childhood. He never harms Boris directly; he provides the instrument and the permission, and the children do the rest.

### Development

Strouvilhou does not develop — he arrives fully formed. His trajectory from Vedel dinner table (insolent) to criminal operation (dangerous) to Passavant salon (literary officialdom) is an arc of revelation, not change.

## Literary Context

Gide constructs Strouvilhou through indirect means — Laura's memories, Edouard's journal, an overheard lunch conversation, a drawing-room duel. The reader never enters his consciousness; this opacity is his power.

The narrator's commentary positions him between character and sociological type — a representative of those who "have a hold on the children, who have a hold on their parents, who have a hold on us" (033-055).

## Key Passages

> "The kids we want, you see, are those who come of good families, because then if rumours get about, their parents do all they can to stifle them. ... We merely want to have a hold on them. Their silence for ours. Let them keep silent and make other people keep silent, and then we'll keep silent too." (033-051)

> "As for me, I maintain that if there's anything more contemptible and more abject than a man, it's a lot of men. No reasoning will ever persuade me that the addition of a number of sordid units can result in an enchanting total." (040-039)

> Strouvilhou liked affecting a kind of flunkey's insolence with Passavant, but Passavant knew him of old. (040-029)

> Then Ghéridanisol pressed his cousin with questions and finally Strouvilhou gave him Boris's talisman and explained its use. (046-013)
