# Protestantism

The Protestant tradition — with its austerity, self-examination, and Puritan inheritance — is the dominant moral atmosphere of Gide's _The Counterfeiters_, underpinning the novel's central concerns with [[sincerity]], authenticity, and the tension between inherited values and personal freedom.

## Definition

In _The Counterfeiters_, Protestantism is less a doctrinal position than a cultural and moral inheritance. It is the air the Vedel-Azais family breathes, the architecture of the chapel in Rue Madame, the habit of self-scrutiny that drives Edouard's journal. The novel's Protestant world is centred on the Azais Pension, founded by a former pastor who "prides himself on keeping his school free from any denominational tendency" (012-044). Protestantism here is not a faith professed but an atmosphere inhabited — what Edouard calls "the odour of Puritanism" (013-040).

Gide's own Protestant upbringing informs the novel's religious landscape. The author was raised in a strict Protestant household, and the novel's central moral vocabulary — [[pur]], [[sincerity]], [[honesty]], the [[counterfeit-coin]] — derives from a Protestant tradition of anxious self-examination that Gide both inherited and struggled to transcend.

## Role in The Counterfeiters

### Thematic Significance

Protestantism provides the moral language through which the novel's central questions are asked. The Protestant insistence on inward sincerity — on the gap between what one is and what one appears to be — is the engine of the novel's plot and its narrative method. Edouard reflects on "the young people's moral bringing-up, as well as their religious" (013-078), identifying the Protestant educational project as both the novel's setting and its target.

Protestantism also functions as the constraint the younger generation must escape. The "heavy lid of custom and morality" (013-130) under which possibilities atrophying is the lid of Protestant convention. The novel dramatises the transfer of moral seriousness from religious to aesthetic domains: Edouard's "pure novel" is a secularised Protestant purity, and his journal secularises the Protestant practice of diary-keeping as spiritual discipline.

The contrast between Protestant austerity and the counterfeit values of Parisian literary society is one of the novel's structuring oppositions. Passavant's salon, with its false literary reputations, represents everything the Protestant conscience distrusts. Olivier's trajectory — from the Protestant boarding school to Passavant's orbit and back — traces this tension in a single character.

### Character Associations

**Edouard** is the secularised Protestant: retaining the habits of self-scrutiny while having lost the faith that animated them. He acknowledges "a certain love of the arduous — a horror of indulgence (towards oneself, I mean) is perhaps the part of my Puritan upbringing which I find it hardest to free myself from" (014-079).

**Armand Vedel** embodies the revolt against Protestant inheritance. His declaration "I don't care a damn about any of it. I'm a Catholic" (013-020) uses Catholicism as a gesture of rebellion, not conviction. His "hatred of everything people call 'Virtue'" and the "incurable resentment" left by "a Puritan bringing-up" (045-065) represents the psychological damage Protestant rigour can inflict.

**Laura Douviers** carries Protestant guilt into her personal life — the affair with Vincent, the forced marriage, the burden of appearances. **Pastor Vedel** is institutional Protestantism: sincere, busy, ineffectual. His wedding address traces family history through theological institutions, presenting religion as inheritance not conviction (013-019). **Olivier Molinier** is caught between traditions: raised Catholic but educated in a Protestant milieu, his whispered "I'm a Catholic" during the pastor's sermon (013-020) signals his vulnerability to Passavant's corruption. **Pauline** bridges both traditions: her children are "brought up as Catholics; but she remembers her early Protestant training" (012-044).

## Manifestations in the Text

### Key Scenes

Laura's wedding in the Rue Madame chapel (013-018–013-020) is the novel's most concentrated dramatisation of Protestantism as atmosphere. Edouard describes "the bare walls, the abstract and chilly light," "the relentless outline of the pulpit," "the straightness of the lines, the rigidity of the columns" — an architecture of "repellent want of grace" and "uncompromising inflexibility." He realises he sees its starkness only because he imagines it through Olivier's Catholic eyes.

> "The society was exclusively Protestant. The odour of Puritanism is peculiar to itself. In a meeting of Catholics or Jews, when they let themselves go in each other's company, the emanation is as strong, and perhaps even more stifling; but among Catholics you find a self-appreciation, and among Jews a self-depreciation, of which Protestants seem to me very rarely capable." (013-040)

The wedding reception (013-040–013-041) extends this portrait: the "less costly tea" substituting for a traditional wedding breakfast, the "suffocating" heat, an atmosphere of restrained propriety — "something ineffably alpine and paradisaical and foolish."

Lilian's observation — "the more emancipated their opinions, the more they cling to their morality; so that there are no severer Puritans than their freethinkers" (008-007) — captures the paradox of secularised Protestantism: the morality survives the loss of faith and becomes more rigid.

### Symbolic Expressions

The Rue Madame chapel is the architectural symbol of Protestantism: bare, linear, parsimonious — the Protestant refusal of sensuous mediation between soul and God. The "odour of Puritanism" (013-040) is Gide's sensory metaphor: not doctrine but smell, something bodily and inescapable. Edouard's journal secularises the Protestant practice of daily self-examination; the novel's form itself, with its multiple perspectives and refusal of omniscience, reflects the Protestant insistence that truth is a matter of individual conscience.

## Literary and Philosophical Context

Gide was raised Protestant in Catholic France — his father a law professor from a Huguenot family, his mother from the wealthy Protestant bourgeoisie of Normandy. The strict moral atmosphere of his upbringing was Gide's own; the "Puritan bringing-up" attributed to Edouard and Armand is autobiographical. The novel's treatment of Protestantism also reflects Gide's engagement with Nietzsche, whose critique of Christian morality as life-denying resonates through Armand's attacks on "Virtue" (045-065) and Edouard's struggle with self-denial. In early twentieth-century France, Protestantism was a minority tradition of the professional bourgeoisie — the Azais-Vedel world of schools, pastors, and modest respectability reflects this social location.
