# Laura's Letter to Edouard

Edouard, on the train from Le Havre to Paris, reads and re-reads Laura Douviers's desperate letter, which prompts him to abandon his literary work and return to the city to help the woman he once loved.

## Summary

While traveling on the Paris express, Edouard takes Laura Douviers's letter from his coat pocket and contemplates its contents. The letter reveals Laura's desperate circumstances — pregnant, abandoned by Vincent Molinier, and uncertain of her future. Edouard, who had a brief affair with Laura the previous summer, feels compelled by duty and residual affection to return to Paris and help her, though he recognizes the complexity of his own motives. [PN=009-003] [PN=009-004] [PN=009-005]

## Context

### Narrative Position

This event occurs in **Part I, Chapter 9**, early in the novel's first section. Edouard is returning from a trip to England, and the letter functions as a narrative hinge, connecting the Paris plot (Vincent's abandonment of Laura) with Edouard's storyline. The chapter alternates between Edouard's journal and the narrator's commentary on his state of mind. [PN=009-006] [PN=009-003]

### Preceding Events

The previous summer, Edouard had a brief romantic involvement with Laura Douviers at her parents' home in the country. Laura subsequently became involved with Vincent Molinier, who impregnated her and then abandoned her for Lady Griffith. Laura, desperate and alone, wrote to Edouard — the one person she believes might help her. Edouard received the letter in England and is now returning to France. [PN=006-042] [PN=012-002] [PN=009-004]

## Detailed Account

On the Paris express, Edouard takes Laura's letter out and reads it repeatedly. The narrator observes that Edouard is not returning to Paris solely to help Laura — he is "glad to have a reason" for returning, since his novel (which would eventually become _The Counterfeiters_) was making no progress in England. [PN=009-004]

Edouard slips the letter between the pages of his journal, establishing the physical link between Laura's story and his literary work. He reflects on the nature of his feelings for her, concluding that "between loving her and imagining that one loves her there is an abyss." The letter becomes a source of creative material as much as a call to moral action. [PN=009-005] [PN=009-016]

> Laura does not seem to suspect her power; as for me, who can penetrate to the very depths of my heart, I know that up to this present moment not a word I have written has not been indirectly inspired by her. [PN=009-007]

Edouard dozes as the train approaches Paris, his thoughts drifting between Laura and his literary ambitions, especially his rivalry with the writer Passavant, whose book _The Horizontal Bar_ he has been reading on the journey. [PN=009-025] [PN=009-026]

## Characters Involved

- **Edouard** — The novelist protagonist who receives the letter; he is torn between genuine concern for Laura and his tendency to aestheticize every experience for his writing
- **Laura Douviers** — The desperate letter-writer, pregnant and abandoned, appealing to Edouard as her last hope
- **Vincent Molinier** — Laura's seducer and abandoner, absent from this scene but the cause of Laura's distress
- **Comte de Passavant** — Edouard's literary rival, whose book he reads on the train, serving as a foil to Edouard's own artistic conscience [PN=009-001] [PN=009-002]

## Consequences

### Immediate Aftermath

Edouard arrives in Paris and meets his nephew Olivier at the station. The joy of their reunion, however, is complicated by Olivier's awkwardness and the knowledge that he has become close to Passavant while Edouard was away. Edouard plans to go to Laura that evening, but first he must deal with the shock of losing his suitcase (see [[edouard-loses-his-journal]]). [PN=010-001] [PN=010-002]

### Narrative Ripple Effects

Laura's letter sets in motion the events of the novel's middle section. Edouard's return to Paris leads directly to his meeting with Bernard at Saas-Fée, where Laura is staying. The letter also establishes the moral dilemma at the heart of Edouard's character: he wants to be a good man and help Laura, but he cannot help viewing her suffering as material for his novel. This tension between life and art haunts the entire narrative.

## Thematic Significance

The letter embodies the novel's concern with **the relationship between art and life**. Edouard's simultaneous genuine concern for Laura and his tendency to aestheticize her suffering raises the question: can the novelist ever be a truly moral person, or does the artistic impulse inevitably transform human suffering into material? The letter also advances the theme of **abandonment and responsibility** — Laura's letter is a cry for help in a world where men (Vincent, and potentially Edouard) evade the consequences of their actions.

## Literary Technique

Gide presents this event through a sophisticated blend of Edouard's journal and third-person narration. The narrator both inhabits Edouard's consciousness and maintains critical distance, noting, for instance, that Edouard is "glad to have a reason" to return to Paris. This dual perspective is the novel's signature technique — the _mise en abyme_ of a novelist writing about a novelist who keeps a journal — and creates an ironic distance that complicates the reader's sympathy for Edouard.
