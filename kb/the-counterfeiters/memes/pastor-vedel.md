# Pastor Vedel

Pastor Prosper Vedel, the head of the Vedel-Azaïs household, whose public piety, private failings, and inability to truly guide his children's moral development make him a central figure in the novel's critique of religious hypocrisy as a form of counterfeiting.

## Role in the Novel

Pastor Vedel presides over the religious and moral atmosphere of the Vedel-Azaïs boarding school. Though he is nominally the spiritual head of the household, the actual moral education of the children has been taken over by old Azaïs, his father-in-law: "the young people's moral bringing-up, as well as their education, has been taken in hand by their grandfather" (PN=013-078). Pastor Vedel is described as "too busy" and too absorbed in his pastoral duties to attend to his own children, while his wife "lives plunged in a religio-poetico daydream, in which she loses all sense of reality" (PN=013-078).

His most significant narrative function is revealed through his private diary, which Sarah shows to Edouard. The diary records his struggle to give up smoking — a struggle rendered in language so extravagantly spiritual that it becomes comic: heartfelt prayers to God for strength, vows broken, renewed resolutions made with operatic intensity. When Edouard later asks the pastor directly whether he has ever tried to give up smoking, the pastor replies that he has, and when asked if he succeeded, answers "Naturally — since I made up my mind to" (PN=013-113) — a statement flatly contradicted by his own diary. This moment crystallizes the theme of religious self-deception that runs through the novel.

## Biography

### Background

Pastor Prosper Vedel is a Protestant minister who started life as a pastor and "prides himself on keeping his school free from any denominational tendency" (PN=012-044). He is married to the pastoress, described by Edouard as "like Lamartine's Elvire" (PN=030-015) — a romantic, vaguely poetical figure ill-suited to the practical demands of running a household or raising children. He is the father of Laura, Rachel, Armand, and Sarah, and son-in-law to old Azaïs, the school's founder.

### Key Actions

At Laura's wedding, "the old pastor thought it his duty to retrace..." (PN=013-019) — he delivers a lengthy address during which Olivier keeps his eyes shut. He presides over the reception, making his study available to guests. His most revealing action is inaction: he has left his private diary lying about where Sarah can find it, and he maintains a public front of spiritual mastery that his children see through. His interactions with Edouard show a man whose religious conviction has paradoxically dulled his perception: "The dazzling light of their faith blinds them to the surrounding world and to their own selves" (PN=013-085).

### Relationships

**Sarah Vedel**: His daughter, who has read his secret diary and recognizes his hypocrisy. **Armand Vedel**: His son, whose cynical rejection of religion is a direct response to his father's example. **Laura Douviers**: His eldest daughter, whose crisis — pregnant by a man who has abandoned her — he seems unable to address. **Madame Vedel**: His wife, the pastoress, who lives in a "religio-poetico daydream" (PN=013-078). **Azaïs**: His father-in-law, who has usurped his role as moral educator of the children.

## Character Analysis

### Motivations

Pastor Vedel's motivations are genuinely religious — he wants to be a good pastor, a good father, a good Christian. His tragedy (or comedy) is that his faith provides him with a language for aspiration but not for self-knowledge. His diary shows a man who earnestly wants to overcome his weaknesses; his public denials show a man who cannot bear to admit those weaknesses exist. His hypocrisy is not cynical but structural — built into a form of religion that prizes spiritual appearance over honest self-assessment.

### Symbolic Role

Pastor Vedel represents the counterfeit of religious sincerity. His faith is real but his performance of it is false — and he cannot tell the difference. The smoking diary, read by Sarah "with the faintest touch of irony" (PN=013-112), becomes the novel's most compressed image of religious self-deception: small private failures wrapped in grand spiritual language, denied in public with an untroubled conscience.

### Development

Pastor Vedel does not develop — he cannot, because development would require the self-awareness his faith has suppressed. He remains fixed in his contradictions, a monument to the failure of religious education in the novel's world.

## Literary Context

Gide's portrait of Pastor Vedel draws on his own Protestant upbringing. The pastor is not a villain but a victim of his own sincerity — a man whose religious language provides cover for self-deception. The smoking diary is a small comic masterpiece within the novel, using bathos (spiritual grandeur applied to a trivial vice) to expose the mechanism of religious hypocrisy. The title Edouard proposes for a chapter — "The Cellular System" after Bourget's "La famille, cette cellule sociale" (PN=013-119) — suggests the pastor's role in the social organism: he is the cell that should provide moral structure but has instead become a space of confusion and deceit.

## Key Passages

> "The deeper the soul plunges into religious devotion, the more it loses all sense of reality, all need, all desire, all love for reality." — Edouard's observation on Pastor Vedel and his kind (PN=013-085)

> "Naturally — since I made up my mind to." — Pastor Vedel's response when Edouard asks if he succeeded in giving up smoking, contradicted by his own diary (PN=013-113)
