# The Molinier Household

The Molinier household is the domestic world that produces three sons — Vincent, Olivier, and George — each of whom embodies a different response to bourgeois family life: disintegration, sensitivity, and delinquency.

## Location

The Molinier apartment is near the Luxembourg Gardens in Paris. Olivier meets his schoolfellows there (002-008). The apartment is on an upper floor, reached by stairs: "on the steps which led to the Moliniers' flat" (005-008). It is close enough to the Profitendieu household that the two families' children move in the same neighborhood geography.

## Description in the Novel

Gide presents the Molinier household through the perspective of visits and the discourse of its matriarch, Pauline. The apartment is described through social detail rather than architectural description. When Edouard visits: "The one who was now before me" (012-031) — George — is encountered on the stairs. "This was the first time I had taken a meal at Pauline's. I was wrong to fight shy of her invitations" (012-032) — the household is a space of hospitality and domestic routine.

Pauline is the household's emotional center: "Pauline presses me to go and see her" (012-044). She "went away with George and left" (013-025), suggesting the constant maternal management of her boys. "Pauline and George are not coming back" from the country until October (029-007) — the household's rhythms of summer absence and autumn return mark the seasonal structure of family life.

Oscar Molinier, the father, is largely absent — he appears as Monsieur Profitendieu's colleague, "keeping him company" (003-001), but his domestic presence is spectral. The household is effectively matriarchal, with Pauline managing her sons with anxious vigilance.

## Narrative Function

### Scenes Set Here

Edouard visits Pauline and meets George, who has been involved in the counterfeit coin ring (012-028). Meals are served, conversations occur, and the household's daily life provides the ground against which the sons' rebellions stand out. Vincent's departure from the family orbit is registered through his absence from the household.

### Symbolic Significance

The Molinier household is the novel's portrait of the bourgeois family in its more benign form — less rigid than the Profitendieu household, less pious than the Vedel school, but still a world of secrets and silences. Pauline's love for her sons is genuine but ineffectual; Oscar's absence leaves a vacuum of paternal authority. The three sons' trajectories — Vincent into madness, Olivier into crisis, George into delinquency — are a collective judgment on the family's failure to provide a viable moral framework.

## Associated Characters

Pauline Molinier is the household's presiding spirit (012-031, 012-044). Oscar Molinier is the largely absent father (003-001). Vincent Molinier, the eldest son, has left the household and is spiraling into moral collapse (005-008). Olivier Molinier, the middle son, is the sensitive one, drawn between Edouard and Passavant. George Molinier, the youngest (012-028), is involved in the counterfeit coin ring. Caloub is the innocent youngest child. Edouard visits as Pauline's half-brother.

## Real-World Context

The Molinier family reflects the world of the French Protestant professional class — magistrates, civil servants, and their families living in the neighborhoods around the Luxembourg. Gide's portrait of Pauline's anxious maternity and Oscar's professional absorption is drawn from his acute observation of bourgeois family life.
