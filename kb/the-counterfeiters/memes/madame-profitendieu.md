# Madame Profitendieu

The wife of Monsieur [[Profitendieu]] and the biological mother of [[Bernard Profitendieu]], whose youthful infidelity is the concealed foundation of the Profitendieu family's apparent stability.

## Role in the Novel

Madame Profitendieu is a largely absent presence — she is away visiting a sick friend when Bernard discovers his illegitimacy and flees the household. Her absence is structurally significant: the revelation that shatters Bernard's world occurs in her absence, and her return is deferred, leaving the consequences of her past action to unfold without her. She represents the unspoken — the truths of bourgeois marriage that are known but never acknowledged.

## Biography

Madame Profitendieu's infidelity, which resulted in Bernard's birth, occurred years before the novel's present. The letters documenting the affair were discovered by her husband, revealing that Bernard is not his biological son. The novel opens with Profitendieu's discovery of these letters: "The time has now come for me to hear a step in the passage" (PN=003-001). Bernard's letter of departure is left on his father's writing table — "Pray excuse me for writing in this way; it is not my object to hurt you" (PN=003-039) — and does not address his mother directly.

## Character Analysis

Madame Profitendieu embodies the novel's theme of concealed truths within the bourgeois family. Her status as a largely offstage character — present only through the effects of her past — underscores the novel's interest in how the unspoken shapes lives more powerfully than the declared. She is the domestic counterpart to the novel's public counterfeiters: her infidelity produced a child raised under false premises, a living "counterfeit" of legitimate paternity.
