The integrated condition of bodily vigour and mental soundness that Wollstonecraft insists is the necessary foundation for the exercise of reason and the cultivation of virtue. Rejecting the fashionable ideal of feminine delicacy — which prized pallor, fragility, and nervous sensitivity as marks of refinement — she argues that "health of body or mind can only be recovered by observing the laws of nature" (013-019), and that women, no less than men, require physical exercise, rational occupation, and freedom from artificial constraint to maintain the health that makes moral and intellectual life possible.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's concept of health is notably holistic for its time. She does not treat bodily health and mental health as separate domains but insists on their interconnection: "the mind is the seat of the understanding" and therefore "health of body and mind" (013-060) must be pursued together. This integration has practical consequences for her educational proposals: the day schools she envisions would include regular physical exercise in the open air, not merely as a concession to bodily needs but as an essential component of intellectual and moral formation.

Her understanding of health is also normative rather than merely descriptive. Health is not simply the absence of illness but the positive condition of vigour that enables a person to fulfil their duties and exercise their faculties. The "healthy temperature" (005-020) she advocates is a state of equilibrium in which neither the passions nor the reason dominate exclusively, and in which the body supports rather than hinders the operations of the mind. This ideal of balanced health stands in deliberate opposition to the extremes of fashionable life: the "fever of love" (002-056) that burns out into indifference, the nervous sensibility that mistakes irritability for refinement, and the physical indolence that enervates both body and character.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument for health is embedded in her broader critique of female education. The system that confines girls to sitting rooms and boarding schools, that prizes a "delicate" complexion over a robust constitution, and that treats physical exertion as unfeminine is, she argues, literally making women sick. "Inattention to health during infancy, and youth" (003-021) produces women whose bodies are too weak to support the demands of motherhood and whose minds are too enervated to sustain the effort of rational thought. The cultivation of "nerves" — that fashionable eighteenth-century complaint — is not a mark of refinement but a symptom of the damage that artificial modes of life inflict on both body and mind.

Her remedy is straightforward and, by the standards of her time, radical. Girls should exercise as boys do: they should run, play outdoors, and develop physical strength and coordination. Physical education should continue throughout youth, not cease at the age when girls are expected to become "young ladies." The health that results from such an education is not merely physical: "health and innocence smile on their sunny brows" (003-055) — the healthy body expresses and supports the healthy mind. This is why Wollstonecraft can claim that "the surest means of preserving health" (012-068) are also the means "of promoting beauty," because genuine beauty, in her view, is the outward expression of inward health and virtue, not the artificial product of cosmetics and constraint.

Her most pointed critique targets the contradiction in contemporary medical and educational advice. Women are told that their health is delicate and must be carefully preserved, yet they are simultaneously discouraged from the very activities — exercise, fresh air, intellectual exertion — that would genuinely strengthen their constitutions. "Why should we injure our health by close study?" (005-127) is the rhetorical question she puts in the mouth of her opponents, exposing the logic that treats women's health as something to be protected from exertion rather than developed through it.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The eighteenth-century cult of sensibility valorised a certain kind of delicacy — pale skin, slender frames, nervous sensitivity — as marks of refinement and femininity. Medical authorities often reinforced this ideal, prescribing rest, seclusion, and the avoidance of "violent" exercise for women. Rousseau, in *Émile*, recommends that Sophie be raised with less physical freedom than Émile, on the grounds that the female body is naturally weaker and requires protection rather than exertion. Wollstonecraft rejects this entire framework. For her, the delicacy that conduct books celebrate is the symptom of oppression, not the mark of refinement. The healthy body — strengthened by exercise, bronzed by the sun, capable of sustained exertion — is the body of a free and rational being.

## Key Passages

> I wish to persuade women to endeavour to strengthen their constitution and abstain from enervating indulgences; for the many diseases that now torture them, I am persuaded, flow from inattention to health during infancy, and youth. (003-021)

> In order to preserve health and beauty, I should earnestly recommend frequent ablutions, and a proper attention to cleanliness, with a strict regard to diet, and regular exercise in the open air. The understanding will expand in proportion as the body is strengthened. (007-025)

> Health of body or mind can only be recovered by observing the laws of nature; and the divine authority of Jesus Christ himself, urges this truth in these emphatic words: 'Sin no more, lest a worse thing come unto thee.' (013-019)
