# Madame de Genlis

## Overview

Stéphanie Félicité du Crest de Saint-Aubin, Comtesse de Genlis (1746–1830), was a prolific French writer and educator whose works on female education circulated widely in the late eighteenth century. Wollstonecraft references her as a contemporary female author engaged with educational questions — a rare acknowledgment of a living woman writer in the *Vindication*.

Genlis's relationship to Wollstonecraft's argument is complex. As a woman who achieved prominence through her educational writings, she provides evidence for Wollstonecraft's central claim about women's intellectual capacity. Yet Genlis's educational philosophy, with its emphasis on accomplishment and social grace, in some respects exemplifies the very approach Wollstonecraft critiques.

## In the Text

Genlis is mentioned briefly but with respect — Wollstonecraft treats her as a serious educational thinker, a contrast to her dismissals of Fordyce and Gregory. The reference situates Genlis within the continental tradition of educational writing that Wollstonecraft surveys in her Introduction.

## Biographical Summary

Madame de Genlis (1746–1830), born Stéphanie Félicité du Crest, was a French writer, harpist, and educator. She served as governess to the children of the Duke of Orléans and wrote over 80 works, including novels, plays, and educational treatises. Her *Adèle et Théodore* (1782) presented an educational programme that influenced pedagogical thought across Europe.
