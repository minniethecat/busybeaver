# Masculine

In _A Vindication_, Wollstonecraft confronts the charge that rational, educated women would become "masculine" — a term of dismissal she dismantles by exposing its incoherence and its function as a tool of social control.

## Definition in Context

"Masculine" was, in 18th-century usage, a term of reproach applied to women who displayed intellectual ambition, learning, or public engagement — qualities coded as male. Wollstonecraft's strategy is not to deny the charge but to question what it means and who benefits from it.

## Usage in the Text

### Reclaiming the Accusation

In the Introduction, she declares: "from every quarter have I heard exclamations against masculine women; but where are they to be found?" (P03-004). If by "masculine" men mean women who hunt and gamble, she has no interest in defending such women. But if the charge is leveled at women who think, who read, who engage in public argument — then the term is merely a stick with which to beat women back into intellectual submission.

### Rousseau's Logic Extended

She explicitly "extend[s] to women" (002-015) Rousseau's opinion that men have been corrupted by "false refinement." Just as men have been drawn from their natural virtues by artificial society, women "have been drawn out of their sphere by false refinement, and not by an endeavour to acquire masculine qualities" (002-015). The real corruption is not that women are becoming masculine, but that both sexes are becoming artificial.

### Masculine as Code for Rational

In Chapter 2, she implicitly equates "masculine qualities" with rational virtues: strength of mind, independence of judgment, moral seriousness. The accusation of masculinity is, in practice, an accusation of rationality — and by exposing this, Wollstonecraft reveals the fear at the heart of anti-feminist rhetoric.

## Theoretical Significance

Wollstonecraft's treatment of "masculine" is foundational for subsequent feminist theory. By refusing to accept the term as an insult, she opens the question of whether the virtues traditionally coded as masculine — reason, courage, independence — are in fact human virtues from which women have been artificially excluded. The term "masculine woman" is exposed as a contradiction maintained by power, not by nature.
