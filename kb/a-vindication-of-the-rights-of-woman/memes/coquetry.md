## Definition

Coquetry, in Wollstonecraft's analysis, is the system of behaviours by which women cultivate male attention through charm, flirtation, and calculated display. She treats coquetry not as a moral failing of individual women but as a structural consequence of a society that denies women access to genuine power, knowledge, and independence. Women practise coquetry because they have been taught that their value lies entirely in male admiration — and they have been given no other arena in which to exercise agency.

## In Context

Wollstonecraft's treatment of coquetry is notably sympathetic in its diagnosis, even as it is uncompromising in its condemnation of the social system that produces it. She argues that "children should be taught to practise the arts of coquetry, when sense and modesty secure their own reward" (P02-016) — that is, young women are actively trained in manipulation because their society offers them no honest path to influence.

In Chapter IV, Wollstonecraft distinguishes between true coquetry — calculated manipulation for advantage — and the "artless impulse of nature" (004-019) that might lead a woman to wish to please. The former, she argues, is a rational adaptation to an irrational system; women learn to wield what power they have. The tragedy is not that women are coquettish, but that coquetry is the only form of power available to them.

Wollstonecraft pairs coquetry with gallantry as twin corruptions of the relation between the sexes. Gallantry is the male offer of flattering but empty deference; coquetry is the female strategy for extracting maximum advantage from that offer. Both rest on a foundation of mutual deception that makes genuine respect and friendship between men and women impossible.

### Coquetry as Education

Wollstonecraft identifies the French educational system as a particularly stark case of coquetry institutionalized through upbringing:

> In France, boys and girls, particularly the latter, are only educated to please, to manage their persons, and regulate their exterior behaviour; and their minds are corrupted at a very early age, by the worldly and pious cautions they receive, to guard them against immodesty. ... At the age of ten or eleven; nay, often much sooner, girls began to coquet, and talked, unreproved, of establishing themselves in the world by marriage.
> (005-015)

### Vanity, Not Nature

Wollstonecraft is careful to argue that coquetry is not a natural female disposition but a learned behaviour produced by a specific social structure. Women are not born coquettes; they are made into them. As she observes:

> The absurdity, in short, of supposing that a girl is naturally a coquette, and that a desire connected with the impulse of nature to propagate the species, should appear even before an improper education has, by heating the imagination, called it forth prematurely, is so unphilosophical.
> (003-014)

She returns to this theme in a pointed passage about the consequences for motherhood:

> The mother will be lost in the coquette, and, instead of making friends of her daughters, view them with eyes askance, for they are rivals — rivals more cruel than any other, because they invite a comparison, and drive her from the throne of beauty, who has never thought of a seat on the bench of reason.
> (003-040)

### Coquetry vs the Artless Impulse

Wollstonecraft draws an important distinction between genuine emotional expression and calculated coquetry:

> With a lover, I grant she should be so, and her sensibility will naturally lead her to endeavour to excite emotion, not to gratify her vanity but her heart. This I do not allow to be coquetry, it is the artless impulse of nature, I only exclaim against the sexual desire of conquest, when the heart is out of the question.
> (004-019)

### Rousseau's Coquette

Wollstonecraft uses Rousseau's description of his ideal woman, [[sophia|Sophie]], to illustrate how the conduct-book tradition itself produces coquetry under the name of modesty. She quotes Rousseau's unsettlingly voyeuristic portrait of Sophie's apparently modest but in fact meticulously calculated dress, and asks pointedly: "Is this modesty? Is this a preparation for immortality?" (005-043). The coquette, Wollstonecraft suggests, is not the aberration of Rousseau's system — she is its intended product.
