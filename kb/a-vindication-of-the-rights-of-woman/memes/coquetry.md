# Coquetry

The deliberate performance of feminine charm and allure, which Wollstonecraft identifies as a socially taught art that women are compelled to practise in lieu of cultivating genuine virtue, modesty, and rational understanding.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft defines coquetry as an acquired art of pleasing men through calculated charm rather than through sincerity or virtue. It is not, in her view, the spontaneous expression of natural affection but a learned set of behaviours inculcated in women from an early age. She explicitly distinguishes "the artless impulse of nature" from coquetry, noting that the former "I do not allow to be coquetry" [004-019]. For Wollstonecraft, coquetry is fundamentally an artefact of women's degraded social position: deprived of opportunities for rational development, women resort to the manipulation of appearances and affections as their only available form of power. She observes that "coquetry more frequently proceeds from vanity than from sensuality" [004-050], linking it to the superficial values instilled by a society that prizes women for their charms rather than their character.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's treatment of coquetry is integral to her larger argument about women's education and the corruption of female character by social expectations. She contends that society itself functions as "a school of coquetry and art" [005-011], beginning this training when girls are scarcely ten or eleven years old. This social education teaches women that their value lies in attracting male attention, and the arts of coquetry become the primary curriculum.

Crucially, Wollstonecraft does not wholly condemn the impulse behind coquetry. She acknowledges that women practise it because they are denied other avenues of agency. The problem is systemic: when women's only route to influence runs through male desire, coquetry becomes a rational — if morally degrading — adaptation. She warns, however, that these arts prove self-defeating in the long term. "Artificial graces and coquetry will likewise pall on the senses" [005-040], meaning that the woman who relies on coquetry to secure affection will find that affection evaporates once novelty fades. Wollstonecraft associates sustained coquetry with "the lovelorn tales of novels" [007-011], suggesting that literary culture reinforces these patterns by presenting coquetry as romantic rather than corrosive.

In her vision of reformed society, Wollstonecraft proposes that education "would shut out gallantry and coquetry, yet allow friendship and love to regulate the intercourse between the sexes" [012-054]. She thus pairs coquetry with gallantry as twin corruptions of genuine relations between men and women — both are artificial performances that prevent the development of rational friendship and mutual respect.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft's stance on coquetry departs sharply from the conventional eighteenth-century view, which often treated coquetry as a natural — even charming — expression of femininity. Conduct-book writers frequently advised women on the proper deployment of coquetry as a tool for securing a husband, treating it as a necessary social skill. Wollstonecraft inverts this logic: she sees the very necessity of coquetry as evidence of women's degradation. Where Rousseau celebrated feminine wiles as nature's compensation for women's physical weakness, Wollstonecraft sees only "the flaunting leaves, after having wasted the strength and usefulness that should form the stem" [P03-001]. She also engages with the notion of "virtuous coquetry" — a term used by sensualists to describe the calculated modesty of a mistress — and dismisses it as a contradiction in terms that reveals the moral bankruptcy of the prevailing sexual culture [005-041].

## Key Passages

> "This I do not allow to be coquetry, it is the artless impulse of nature — the spontaneous effusion of a susceptible heart, that will not be checked by the forms of the world." [004-019]

> "The whole system of society was a school of coquetry and art. At the age of ten or eleven, nay, often much sooner, girls began to be trained for the purpose of attracting men." [005-011]

> "You may employ even the arts of coquetry in the service of virtue, and the artless graces which arise from sensibility will then appear more captivating." [005-038]

> "A rational education would shut out gallantry and coquetry, yet allow friendship and love to regulate the intercourse between the sexes." [012-054]
