The rational and reasoning faculty of the mind, which Wollstonecraft presents as the defining human capacity that distinguishes us from brutes and must be developed through education in both sexes. She argues that intellect "will always govern" (P03-015) and that its expansion is essential to human happiness and virtue.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft locates intellect at the center of her philosophical anthropology. She distinguishes it from mere instinct or sensibility, defining it as the capacity for rational judgment, abstract thought, and the formation of principles. In her view, "what deserves the name of intellect" is "the power of gaining general or abstract ideas, or even intermediate ones" (013-035). This capacity is what separates human beings from animals: brutes operate by unerring instinct, while human beings are meant to govern themselves through reason (002-070). Crucially, Wollstonecraft insists that intellect is not naturally stronger in men — it is simply more cultivated in them through education (002-073).

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's central argument is that the expansion of intellect is the foundation of both individual virtue and social progress. She contends that women have been systematically denied the education necessary to develop their intellectual powers, and that this denial is the root cause of their degradation. "With respect to the culture of the heart," she writes, "it is unanimously allowed that sex is out of the question; but the line of subordination in the mental powers is never to be passed over" (004-002). By denying women's intellect, society reduces them to mere objects of sensual admiration rather than rational companions.

The cultivation of intellect is also Wollstonecraft's answer to the question of happiness. In a pivotal passage, she notes that "men of sensibility doubt whether the expansion of intellect produces a greater portion of happiness or misery" (001-033). Her own answer is that whatever the immediate pains of intellectual growth, the mind gains strength through the exercise, and it is through reason — not sensation — that genuine human flourishing is possible. "The mind gains strength by the exercise, sufficient, perhaps, to comprehend the answers which, in another step of existence, it may receive to the anxious questions it asked" (005-127).

For Wollstonecraft, the proper relationship between the sexes depends on intellectual equality. Marriage without intellectual companionship degrades both partners: only "those who have sufficient intellect to substitute the calm tenderness of friendship, the confidence of respect, instead of blind admiration, and the sensual emotions of fondness" can sustain a meaningful union (002-056). A woman who develops her intellect becomes a true friend to her husband. "Besides, understanding is necessary to give variety and interest to sensual enjoyments, for low, indeed, in the intellectual scale, is the mind that can continue to love when neither virtue nor sense give a human appearance to an animal appetite" (012-082).

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft's position directly challenges Rousseau, who argued that women's education should be designed to please men, not to develop their reason. She also engages with the broader eighteenth-century assumption — shared by writers like Gregory and Fordyce — that intellectual accomplishment in women is unnatural or dangerous. In response, she points to Catherine Macaulay as "an example of intellectual acquirements supposed to be beyond the female reach" (005-114), demonstrating that women's intellectual capacity equals men's when given the same opportunities. Wollstonecraft further notes that "the intellectual world is shut against" women who receive only superficial accomplishments rather than genuine education (004-058).

## Key Passages

> It seems scarcely necessary to say, that I now speak of the sex in general. Many individuals have more sense than their male relatives; and, as nothing preponderates where there is a constant struggle for an equilibrium, without it has naturally more gravity, some women govern their husbands without degrading themselves, because intellect will always govern. (P03-015)

> Men, in general, seem to employ their reason to justify prejudices, which they have imbibed, they cannot trace how, rather than to root them out. The mind must be strong that resolutely forms its own principles; for a kind of intellectual cowardice prevails which makes many men shrink from the task, or only do it by halves. (001-008)

> The being who can think justly in one track, will soon extend its intellectual empire; and she who has sufficient judgment to manage her children, will not submit right or wrong, to her husband, or patiently to the social laws which makes a nonentity of a wife. (012-091)
