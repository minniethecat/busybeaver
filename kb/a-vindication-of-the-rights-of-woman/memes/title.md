Title, in Wollstonecraft's political vocabulary, refers to hereditary distinctions of rank and nobility — the artificial markers of social hierarchy that she identifies as obstacles to both individual virtue and national flourishing. She extends the critique of political titles to the domestic sphere, drawing a structural parallel between hereditary privilege in the state and unearned authority in the family.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft defines titles in opposition to merit and natural ability. She opens her critique with a pointed observation: "whilst rank and titles are held of the utmost importance, before which Genius 'must hide its diminished head,' it is, with a few exceptions, very unfortunate for a nation when a man of abilities, without rank or property, pushes himself forward to notice" (001-012). Titles are not merely decorative; they are institutionally destructive, suppressing genuine talent in favour of inherited status. The invocation of Genius hiding its head signals Wollstonecraft's alignment with Enlightenment meritocracy against the ancien régime's culture of hereditary distinction.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument against titles operates on both political and moral registers. Politically, she asks a devastating rhetorical question: "what but habitual idleness can hereditary wealth and titles produce?" (009-002). Her answer draws on a foundational principle of her philosophy: "man is so constituted that he can only attain a proper use of his faculties by exercising them, and will not exercise them unless necessity, of some kind, first set the wheels in motion." Hereditary titles remove the necessity that drives human development, leaving the title-holder in a state of moral and intellectual inertia.

The moral dimension is inseparable from the political. Wollstonecraft argues that "virtue likewise can only be acquired by the discharge of relative duties" (009-002). The holder of an hereditary title, exempted by birth from the necessity of earning his place, is equally exempted from the duties through which virtue is cultivated. Titles thus produce a class of persons who are morally stunted by the very privileges that define them.

The connection to Wollstonecraft's gender analysis is implicit but powerful. Just as hereditary titles confer unearned authority in the political sphere, patriarchal structures confer unearned authority on men within the domestic sphere. Both systems suppress the development of faculties — in the idle aristocrat and in the woman trained only to please — by removing the necessity that drives genuine growth. The critique of titles is thus integral to the broader argument of A Vindication: artificial distinctions, whether of rank or of sex, are incompatible with the cultivation of virtue.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Where Burke defended hereditary titles as the organic foundation of social order, and where Rousseau accepted rank as a fact of civilised society while critiquing its excesses, Wollstonecraft went further in questioning the very principle of inherited status. Her position aligns more closely with Paine's radical egalitarianism, though she extends the critique beyond the political sphere into the domestic, arguing that the logic that makes hereditary titles indefensible in the state applies with equal force to the unearned authority of husbands and fathers.
