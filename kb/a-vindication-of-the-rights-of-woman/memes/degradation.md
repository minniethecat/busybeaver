# Degradation

"Degradation" is Wollstonecraft's comprehensive term for the condition of women under a system that denies them education, independence, and the exercise of reason — a condition she compares to slavery and polygamy in its dehumanizing effects.

## Definition in Context

Degradation is not merely individual humiliation but a structural condition: the systematic reduction of a class of persons to a status below full humanity. For Wollstonecraft, women's degradation is maintained by custom, law, and above all by an education that trains women to be pleasing rather than rational.

## Usage in the Text

### The Historical Argument

In Chapter 2, she rebuts the argument from historical precedent: "As to the argument respecting the subjection in which the sex has ever been held, it retorts on man" (002-084). That women have always been subjected does not prove they deserve subjection — any more than the historical prevalence of slavery proves that some are born to be slaves.

### Polygamy as Physical Degradation

In Chapter 4, she identifies polygamy as "another physical degradation" (004-078), arguing that it "blasts every domestic virtue" (004-078). The parallel between polygamy in the East and the condition of women in Europe is deliberate: both reduce women from companions to conveniences.

### Economic Degradation

In Chapter 9, she argues that with proper education women could pursue "business of various kinds" and "might save many from common and legal prostitution" (009-032). The degradation of women is perpetuated by their economic dependence — denied the means of self-support, they are forced into marriages that are themselves a form of degradation.

## Theoretical Significance

Wollstonecraft's concept of degradation connects her feminism to the broader Enlightenment critique of arbitrary power. Just as political tyranny degrades citizens by denying them rights, domestic tyranny degrades women by denying them autonomy. The parallel is exact and deliberate: women's degradation and political despotism share a common logic.
