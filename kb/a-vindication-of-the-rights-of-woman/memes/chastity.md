## Definition

In _A Vindication of the Rights of Woman_, chastity is one of the virtues Wollstonecraft most thoroughly re-examines. She critiques the conventional eighteenth-century understanding of chastity as a specifically female obligation — a matter of sexual reputation enforced by social scrutiny rather than cultivated as an inner moral quality. Wollstonecraft argues that chastity, properly understood, is a universal virtue grounded in reason and self-respect, not a gendered code of conduct designed to preserve women's market value in the marriage economy.

## In Context

Wollstonecraft's treatment of chastity is closely tied to her broader argument about the corruption of virtue by social convention. She observes that society demands chastity of women while simultaneously encouraging the very qualities — vanity, love of admiration, intellectual weakness — that make true chastity impossible. A woman trained only to please, she argues, cannot be genuinely chaste because chastity requires the strength of mind to value oneself for reasons other than male approval.

In Chapter VII, Wollstonecraft develops her most sustained analysis. She contends that "the only virtuous support of chastity" is a cultivated mind — "a simplicity of character that leads us to form a just opinion of ourselves" (007-008). False modesty, by contrast, produces only a performative chastity that collapses when social scrutiny is removed. "To render chastity the virtue from which unsophisticated modesty will naturally flow," she writes, "the mind must be strengthened" (007-010).

Wollstonecraft connects chastity to her larger project of educational reform: women cannot be expected to exhibit genuine chastity when their education systematically deprives them of the intellectual resources — reason, self-knowledge, independence of judgment — that make moral virtue possible.

### Chastity and the Modesty Distinction

A crucial move in Wollstonecraft's argument is her distinction between genuine modesty — which she defines as "that soberness of mind which teaches a man not to think more highly of himself than he ought to think" — and the superficial modesty demanded of women as a social performance. She insists:

> In defining modesty, it appears to me equally proper to discriminate that purity of mind, which is the effect of chastity, from a simplicity of character that leads us to form a just opinion of ourselves, equally distant from vanity or presumption, though by no means incompatible with a lofty consciousness of our own dignity.
> (007-003)

### The Double Standard

Wollstonecraft identifies a fundamental hypocrisy in her society's treatment of chastity: "This regard for reputation, independent of its being one of the natural rewards of virtue," gave rise to a system in which "reputation for chastity, became the one thing needful to the sex" (008-011). Men, by contrast, suffer no comparable loss of status for sexual misconduct. The double standard gives women a perverse incentive structure:

> If the honour of a woman, as it is absurdly called, is safe, she may neglect every social duty; nay, ruin her family by gaming and extravagance; yet still present a shameless front — for truly she is an honourable woman!
> (008-021)

> But, in proportion as this regard for the reputation of chastity is prized by women, it is despised by men: and the two extremes are equally destructive to morality.
> (008-023)

### Chastity and Education

Wollstonecraft's analysis of chastity converges with her educational philosophy. She argues that the cultivation of the mind is the only reliable foundation for moral purity:

> The woman who has dedicated a considerable portion of her time to pursuits purely intellectual, and whose affections have been exercised by humane plans of usefulness, must have more purity of mind, as a natural consequence, than the ignorant beings whose time and thoughts have been occupied by gay pleasures or schemes to conquer hearts.
> (007-010)

She extends this argument to men as well, locating the root of the problem in male behaviour rather than female weakness:

> I will venture to assert, that all the causes of female weakness, as well as depravity, which I have already enlarged on, branch out of one grand cause — want of chastity in men.
> (008-027)

The following passage, quoting [[catharine-macaulay|Catharine Macaulay]], reinforces Wollstonecraft's view that women are not inherently more corruptible than men — their moral fragility is the product of circumstance:

> "But no such frail beings come out of the hands of nature. The human mind is built of nobler materials than to be so easily corrupted; and with all their disadvantages of situation and education, women seldom become entirely abandoned till they are thrown into a state of desperation, by the venomous rancour of their own sex."
> (008-022)

## Key Passages

> "I will venture to assert, that their reason will never acquire sufficient strength to enable it to regulate their conduct, whilst the making an appearance in the world is the first wish of the majority of mankind. To this weakness, one very material cause of female follies, I have already alluded — their attachment to dress and pleasure." (007-008)

> "To render chastity the virtue from which unsophisticated modesty will naturally flow, the mind must be strengthened." (007-010)

> "Chastity must more universally prevail, could the energy of the mind be more generally exercised." (P02-007)

> "Purity of mind, or that genuine delicacy, which is the only virtuous support of chastity, is near akin to that refinement of humanity, which never resides in any but cultivated minds. It is something nobler than innocence; it is the delicacy of reflection, and not the coyness of ignorance." (007-008)
