1743 – 1825. English poet, essayist, and children's author, a prominent figure in the Bluestocking Circle and Rational Dissenter community. Wollstonecraft references Barbauld in a footnote, commending her prose essay as an authority on education and moral judgment.

## Life

Anna Laetitia Aikin was born in 1743 in Leicestershire, the daughter of a Dissenting minister and schoolmaster. Her father taught at Warrington Academy, a center of Rational Dissent and Enlightenment thought, where she received an unusually thorough education. She began publishing poetry in the 1760s, and her first collection, _Poems_ (1773), established her reputation.

In 1774 she married Rochemont Barbauld, a former pupil of her father, and the couple moved to Palgrave, Suffolk, where they ran a school for boys. Her _Lessons for Children_ (1778–79) and _Hymns in Prose for Children_ (1781) were pioneering works of children's literature. After leaving teaching, the Barbaulds settled in London, where Anna Laetitia became an influential literary critic and editor, producing a landmark 50-volume edition of _The British Novelists_ (1810).

## Role in the Text

Barbauld appears in _A Vindication_ not in the main text but in a footnote Wollstonecraft appends to her discussion of the contradiction between worldly wisdom and virtue in Chapter V: "See an excellent essay on this subject by Mrs. Barbauld, in Miscellaneous pieces in Prose" (005-153). The footnote directs readers to Barbauld's essay as an authoritative treatment of the conflict between pursuing wealth and cultivating virtue — precisely Wollstonecraft's theme in the surrounding passage.

The reference is significant despite its brevity. By citing Barbauld as an authority, Wollstonecraft aligns herself with the most intellectually respected woman writer of her generation. Barbauld's inclusion in the footnotes — rather than in the polemical main text — may reflect the fact that Barbauld was still living and actively publishing, making her a contemporary ally rather than a historical example.

### Works Referenced

The essay Wollstonecraft cites appears in _Miscellaneous Pieces in Prose_ (1773), a joint publication by Anna Laetitia and her brother John Aikin. The volume contained essays on topics ranging from the pleasure of the imagination to the education of women, and was widely read in Dissenting and reformist circles.

## Key Ideas

Barbauld's writing spans poetry, children's literature, literary criticism, and political pamphleteering. She advocated for religious toleration, the abolition of the slave trade, and educational reform. While more moderate in tone than Wollstonecraft on the question of women's rights — Barbauld did not call for radical restructuring of gender relations — her career demonstrated that a woman could achieve intellectual authority and professional independence through writing.

Wollstonecraft's footnote reference is a gesture of respect from one reformist woman writer to another. Both shared the Rational Dissenter intellectual heritage, and both believed that women's moral and intellectual improvement was essential to social progress, even if they differed on the pace and scope of reform.
