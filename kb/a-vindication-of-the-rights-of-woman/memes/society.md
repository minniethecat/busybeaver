In Wollstonecraft's analysis, **society** is not a neutral backdrop but an active moral agent — the "present state of society" (001-001) that she takes as her point of departure is a system of mutually reinforcing corruptions. The "different ranks of society" (P03-007) each produce characteristic deformations of character, and the "usages of all society, whether savage or civilized" (P01-013), have uniformly subordinated women.

Wollstonecraft's concept of society is thus diagnostic and critical rather than descriptive. She examines society as a causal force — the medium through which "the moral character of women" (P03-007) is produced — and finds it systematically defective.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft uses "society" in three distinct but related senses. First, it denotes the broad social order: "the present state of society" (001-001), the totality of institutions, customs, and relations that constitute eighteenth-century European civilisation. Second, it denotes the specific social circles — "their society" (P03-003) — within which individuals move and by which they are judged. Third, it denotes the normative ideal of a well-ordered community: "society is formed in the wisest manner" when "its constitution is founded on the nature of man" (001-010).

The critical force of the concept lies in the gap Wollstonecraft identifies between the second and third senses. Actual society, governed by "the usages of all society" (P01-013), systematically corrupts the character of both sexes; a genuinely rational society would be organised so as to cultivate virtue rather than undermine it.

The phrase "coercion established in society" (P02-019) captures her core complaint: society as presently constituted is a structure of domination — of women by men, of the poor by the rich, of reason by prejudice — rather than a framework for the mutual development of rational beings.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft opens the *Vindication* with a declaration of method: "In the present state of society, it appears necessary to go back to first principles" (001-001). Society as it stands is so thoroughly corrupted that only a return to fundamental truths — the nature of reason, the character of virtue, the conditions of human flourishing — can provide a standard by which to judge and reform it.

The argument proceeds by tracing the effects of social arrangements on the character of those who inhabit them. Each "rank of society" (001-027) produces its own characteristic vices: the rich are corrupted by idleness, the poor by degradation, women by dependence, men by power. The "misery and disorder which pervaded society" (001-014) are not accidents but the predictable consequences of institutions founded on prejudice rather than principle.

Wollstonecraft's most distinctive contribution is her insistence that society itself — not nature, not divine ordinance — is responsible for the character of women. "It is not individuals but society which must change it" (P01-021), she writes, locating the remedy for women's condition not in better advice to individual women but in the structural transformation of the social order.

This is a remarkably sociological insight for its period. Wollstonecraft grasps that character is socially produced, that the "moral character of women" is "the natural effect of the different circumstances in which they are placed" — an effect so reliable that it can be analysed as systematically as any natural phenomenon.

The political implications are radical. If society produces character, then reforming character requires reforming society. Education, in this analysis, is not merely a private benefit but a public necessity: "instruction through the whole mass of society" (P03-006) is the mechanism by which social transformation becomes possible.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft's analysis of society departs sharply from the dominant eighteenth-century frameworks. Against the conservative tradition represented by Burke — for whom the existing social order was a precious inheritance to be preserved — she insists that society as presently constituted is indefensible by any rational standard.

Against Rousseau, who located the corruption of character in civilisation itself and yearned for a return to nature, Wollstonecraft insists that the remedy is not retreat but reform. Rousseau "became enamoured of solitude" (001-014), "fatigued with jostling against artificial fools"; Wollstonecraft, by contrast, demands that the "artificial" be dismantled through public argument and institutional change.

She shares with the Scottish Enlightenment — particularly Adam Smith and Adam Ferguson — the conviction that society can be analysed as a system of causes and effects, but she brings to this analysis a feminist edge that her male contemporaries lacked. The "different ranks of society" include the rank of "woman" itself, and the effects of this ranking are as analytically significant as those of class or property.

## Key Passages

> In the present state of society, it appears necessary to go back to first principles in search of the most simple truths, and to dispute with some prevailing prejudice every inch of ground. (001-001)

> It is not individuals but society which must change it, and that it will not be done till the education of women is made an affair of the state. (P01-021)

> That the society is formed in the wisest manner, whose constitution is founded on the nature of man, strikes, in the abstract, every thinking being so forcibly, that it looks like presumption to endeavour to bring forward proofs. (001-010)

> Impressed by this view of the misery and disorder which pervaded society, and fatigued with jostling against artificial fools, Rousseau became enamoured of solitude. (001-014)
