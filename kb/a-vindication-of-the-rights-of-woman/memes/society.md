# Society

## Definition

Wollstonecraft uses **society** to refer to the system of social relations,
manners, customs, and hierarchies that structure eighteenth-century European
life. Her usage is predominantly critical: society, as presently constituted,
is a corrupting force that distorts human nature, reinforces irrational
prejudices, and systematically degrades women.

## Society as Corrupting Force

A central claim of the *Vindication* is that the vices and weaknesses attributed
to women (and to a lesser extent, men) are not natural but are produced by the
social order:

The artificial distinctions of society — rank, wealth, birth — substitute
external markers for internal merit. In a society governed by such distinctions,
people learn to value appearance over substance, reputation over virtue, and
the approbation of others over the approval of conscience.

## The Double Standard

Society applies fundamentally different standards to men and women. Men are
expected to be active, rational, and independent; women are expected to be
passive, emotional, and dependent. These expectations are not natural but are
artifacts of a social order designed to maintain male privilege:

Women are "kept in ignorance" and then "ridiculed for their weakness" — a
weakness that society itself has produced. The double standard is enforced
through opinion, reputation, and economic dependence.

## Society and Morality

Wollstonecraft challenges the equation of social respectability with moral
goodness. A person can be impeccably "respectable" — obeying all the rules
of polite society — while being morally worthless. True morality requires
the exercise of reason and the cultivation of principle, both of which are
actively discouraged by a society that rewards conformity and punishes
independent thought.

## Reform, Not Revolution

Despite her critique, Wollstonecraft does not call for the overthrow of
society. Her proposed remedy is reform through education: if individuals
(especially women) are properly educated to reason and virtue, the character
of society itself will be transformed. The "revolution in female manners"
she advocates is an internal, moral revolution — a transformation of how
women think and act — that will in time produce a more rational and just
social order.
