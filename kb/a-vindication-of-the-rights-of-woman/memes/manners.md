# Manners

The social conventions and artificial refinements that Wollstonecraft critiques as a superficial substitute for genuine moral development in women's education.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft distinguishes sharply between manners and morals. "Manners" in her usage refers to the system of social behaviors, polish, and refinement that eighteenth-century society — particularly conduct books — prescribed for women. She describes these as "a polish of manners that injures the substance" (P02-004), creating a surface of propriety that conceals moral emptiness. The "artificial manners and virtues" (001-021) demanded of women are, she argues, essentially theatrical — performances designed to please rather than expressions of genuine character.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

The author argues that women "acquire manners before morals" (002-026), learning to perform social grace before they develop any capacity for moral reasoning. This inversion of priorities produces women who are "polished" but empty — "whose polished manners render vice more dangerous" (001-027) because vice is concealed beneath a veneer of refinement. The "gentleness of manners" (002-067) expected of women is not genuine gentleness but "the submissive demeanour of dependence."

Wollstonecraft connects the cultivation of manners to women's lack of genuine education: "this acquaintance with manners and customs has frequently been confounded with a knowledge of the human heart" (002-021). Women are taught to read social cues rather than to understand moral principles. She predicts that "till the manners of the times are changed," genuine reform in women's condition will be impossible (002-015).

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Edmund Burke's *Reflections* celebrated "the spirit of a gentleman" and "the spirit of religion" as preserved through inherited manners. Wollstonecraft sees only corruption: "factitious and corrupt manners" (P02-005) preserve social hierarchy at the expense of moral truth. Where conservatives saw manners as civilization's safeguard, she sees them as "the flattering veil which hides the defects of character" — a mechanism of deception and self-deception.

## Key Passages

> "artificial manners and virtues, the citizen of Geneva" — on Rousseau's contradictions (001-021)
