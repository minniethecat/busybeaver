# Manners

## Definition

**Manners** in Wollstonecraft's analysis are the outward forms of social behavior — the codes of politeness, deference, and propriety that govern conduct in society. She distinguishes sharply between manners and [[morality]]: manners are conventional and vary by time and place; morality is universal and grounded in [[reason]]. The education of women, she argues, is almost entirely an education in manners rather than in morals, producing women who know how to behave but not how to think.

> "Manners and morals are so nearly allied, that they have often been confounded; but though the former should only be the natural reflection of the latter…" (004-001)

This confusion of manners with morals is not accidental — it serves the interests of those who wish to control women without educating them. A woman with good manners is compliant and pleasing; a woman with genuine morals might be neither.

## In Context

### Manners Consolidated by Time

Wollstonecraft warns that "where manners are consolidated by time, a reformation is barely possible" [006-020]. When social customs acquire the appearance of natural law through long observance, challenging them becomes tantamount to challenging nature itself. This is precisely the situation Wollstonecraft confronts: the subordination of women is defended as natural and customary, and anyone who questions it is accused of indecency.

### The Replacement of Morals by Manners

The tragedy of women's [[education]], in Wollstonecraft's account, is that it substitutes manners for [[morality]] entirely. Women are taught to be polite, to defer, to please — but never to ask whether the social arrangements that demand these performances are [[justice|just]]. The result is a class of people who are exquisitely trained in the forms of virtue but utterly innocent of its substance.
