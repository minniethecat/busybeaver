The foundational structure or nature of a thing — whether the human body, the human mind, or the body politic — that Wollstonecraft examines as both an inherited condition and a malleable foundation capable of improvement through education, exercise, and rational principle. She deploys "constitution" in three interconnected senses: the physical constitution that determines health and vigour; the mental constitution shaped by education and habit; and the political constitution of civil society, which she argues must be founded on reason and must include women within its protections.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft uses "constitution" with deliberate polysemy, moving fluidly between its bodily, mental, and political meanings to construct a unified argument: just as a sound bodily constitution requires exercise and proper care, and a sound mental constitution requires education and the cultivation of reason, so too a sound political constitution must be "founded on reason" (P02-015) rather than on inherited privilege or custom. The term carries particular weight in the revolutionary context of 1792, when France's new constitution was being debated, and Wollstonecraft explicitly addresses Talleyrand-Périgord's report on national education with the demand that "when your constitution is revised, the rights of woman be considered" (P02-022).

The bodily sense appears most frequently in her critique of how women's constitutions are weakened by artificial modes of life: "the constitution of their bodies" (002-035) is undermined by confinement, inactivity, and the pursuit of fashionable delicacy. Yet she insists that bodily constitution is not fixed — it can be strengthened through exercise and rational habits, just as it can be destroyed "by mistaken notions of beauty" (003-010).

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument about constitution operates on three levels simultaneously. On the bodily level, she challenges the prevailing medical and philosophical assumption that women are, "by their very constitution, slaves" (002-083) — naturally weaker, more delicate, and constitutionally incapable of rational exertion. She concedes that "from the constitution of their bodies, men seem to be designed by Providence to attain a greater degree of virtue" (002-035) in one sphere, but immediately qualifies this by insisting that the observed differences are largely the product of education, not nature. A woman who "has a sound constitution" (002-047) and allows it "to retain its natural strength" (002-049) through exercise and rational habits is not the fragile creature that conduct-book writers describe.

On the mental level, she argues that the constitution of the mind is shaped by the same principles as the body: it requires exercise to develop, and it atrophies when confined. Women whose constitutions have been "impaired...by study, or careless inattention to their health" (003-003) are not evidence of female constitutional incapacity but of the damaging effects of the education they receive. The remedy is not to protect women from exertion but to "strengthen her constitution and abstain from enervating indulgences" (003-021).

On the political level, Wollstonecraft's most radical move is to connect individual constitution to the constitution of civil society. "The very constitution of civil governments has put obstacles in the way" (004-011) of women's development, she argues, by excluding them from the rights and responsibilities that would develop their rational capacities. Her address to Talleyrand makes this connection explicit: the new French constitution, if it is to be "the first constitution founded on reason" (P02-015), must include women in its provisions for national education. The constitution of the state, like the constitution of the body and mind, must be founded on rational principles rather than on the accumulated prejudices of custom.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The dominant eighteenth-century view, articulated by Rousseau and absorbed into conduct literature, held that women's constitution was naturally different from men's — weaker, more sensitive, designed for domesticity rather than public life. This "constitutional" argument was used to justify excluding women from education, citizenship, and the rights of man. Wollstonecraft's response is twofold: first, she denies that the observed differences are natural, attributing them instead to the different education and habits imposed on the sexes from infancy; second, she appropriates the language of constitutional soundness to argue that a reformed education would produce women of robust constitution — physical, mental, and moral — who would be better wives, mothers, and citizens than the "artificial" creatures produced by the existing system.

## Key Passages

> If so, on what does your constitution rest? If the abstract rights of man will bear discussion and explanation, those of woman, by a parity of reasoning, will not shrink from the same test. (P02-010)

> But, if women are to be excluded, without having a voice, from a participation of the natural rights of mankind, prove first, to ward off the charge of injustice and inconsistency, that they want reason — else this flaw in your NEW CONSTITUTION, the first constitution founded on reason, will ever shew that man must, in some shape, act like a tyrant. (P02-015)

> I have, probably, had an opportunity of observing more girls in their infancy than J. J. Rousseau. I can recollect my own feelings, and I have looked steadily around me; yet, so far from coinciding with him in opinion respecting the first dawn of the female character, I will venture to affirm, that a girl, whose constitution has not been ruined by the unnatural confinement of a boarding-school, or by an unremitting attention to her person, is often as wild and romping as a boy. (003-020)
