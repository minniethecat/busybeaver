# Reputation

## Definition

**Reputation** in Wollstonecraft's analysis is the central mechanism by which society controls women without genuinely improving them. She observes that women "plumed themselves on their unsullied reputation, as if the whole compass of their duty as wives and mothers was only to preserve it" [008-009], reducing [[virtue|moral virtue]] to a social performance. The opening of Chapter VIII — "It is of the utmost importance to a woman to obtain and preserve a good reputation" [008-001] — states the conventional view with apparent approval, but the chapter that follows is a devastating critique of the proposition.

> "It is of the utmost importance to a woman to obtain and preserve a good reputation." (008-001)

The problem is not reputation per se — Wollstonecraft does not advocate for shamelessness — but the substitution of reputation for [[virtue]]. When women are taught that what matters is what others *think* of their conduct rather than what their conduct actually *is*, they become expert performers of propriety while remaining morally undeveloped.

> "…yet have plumed themselves on their unsullied reputation, as if the whole compass of their duty as wives and mothers was only to preserve it." (008-009)

## In Context

### Reputation vs Virtue

The central analytical move in Chapter VIII is Wollstonecraft's insistence on distinguishing reputation from [[virtue]]. She observes that women "have been taught from their infancy that virtue consists in the preservation of reputation" [008-007] — but this is a catastrophic confusion. A woman may have an unblemished reputation while being thoroughly vicious in character, just as a woman of genuine virtue may lose her reputation through circumstances beyond her control.

> "…they have been taught from their infancy that virtue consists in the preservation of reputation…" (008-007)

This confusion is not accidental but structural: it serves the interests of a society that wishes to control women's behavior without undertaking the difficult work of cultivating their [[reason]]. Reputation is policed by public opinion; virtue requires inner moral development. The first is cheap and effective; the second is demanding and potentially destabilizing.

### The Rousseau Connection

Wollstonecraft engages directly with [[rousseau|Rousseau]] on this point, noting that he "declares, 'that reputation is no less indispensable than chastity'" [008-010] for women. Her response is to characterize this elevation of reputation as a symptom of a weak moral framework: "weak minds are always fond of resting in the ceremonials of duty, but morality offers much simpler motives" [008-010].

> "Rousseau declares, 'that reputation is no less indispensable than chastity.'" (008-010)

By linking reputation to [[chastity]], Rousseau exemplifies the reduction of female [[virtue]] to a single, externally-policed dimension. Wollstonecraft's counter-argument is that genuine virtue — for either sex — requires the development of the whole moral character, not the preservation of a single aspect of reputation.

### The Material Consequences

Reputation is not merely a philosophical error in Wollstonecraft's account — it has material consequences for women's lives. She notes that women "cautiously preserving her reputation free from spot" [003-041] is elevated as the ideal of female conduct, and that women who fail in this regard face social ruin. A woman's entire existence is staked on her reputation, which can be destroyed by a single misstep — a gamble that no man is required to make.

> "…cautiously preserving her reputation free from spot, be reckoned a good woman…" (003-041)

The cruelty of this arrangement is that reputation is subject to forces beyond a woman's control: gossip, slander, the whims of male opinion. To make a woman's moral standing depend on reputation is to place her character at the mercy of others — precisely the opposite of the rational autonomy Wollstonecraft champions.
