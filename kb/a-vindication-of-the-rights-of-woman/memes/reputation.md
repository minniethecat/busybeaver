In Wollstonecraft's moral analysis, **reputation** stands as one of the most pervasive impediments to genuine virtue. She distinguishes sharply between reputation — the external appearance of propriety maintained for social approval — and true moral character grounded in rational principle. Throughout the *Vindication*, she exposes how the relentless social pressure on women to preserve a spotless reputation, particularly for chastity, produces "a kind of hypocritical conformity" (003-049) rather than authentic goodness.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft defines reputation critically, as the external social credit that women are taught to value above all else. She observes that women are systematically trained to be "cautiously preserving her reputation free from spot" (003-049), and that this preservation is "reckoned a good woman" regardless of inward virtue. Reputation, in her analysis, is not virtue itself but its "shadow" (008-014) — a surface image that can be maintained even while genuine moral character deteriorates.

The danger of reputation as a moral standard is precisely that it can be performed. Wollstonecraft notes that women "sometimes obtain a smoother reputation than a wiser or a better man" (008-017), exposing the gap between reputation and desert. For her, reputation is "merely made the stilts of reputation" (005-097) — an artificial prop that substitutes for the genuine moral stature that only reason and virtue can provide.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's most concentrated treatment of reputation appears in Chapter VIII, where she traces how sexual reputation became "the one thing needful" (008-012) in women's moral education. She argues that this fixation on reputation degrades both sexes: women learn to dissemble rather than to be virtuous, while men become "captious" judges of female conduct rather than partners in moral improvement.

The problem, Wollstonecraft insists, is structural rather than individual. "With respect to reputation," she writes, "the attention is confined to a single virtue" (008-021) — chastity — and women are taught that preserving its appearance excuses every other failing. She exposes the hypocrisy of a society that "values a woman's reputation more than her virtue" (008-007) and shows how this inversion of values corrupts the moral education of both sexes.

Wollstonecraft also critiques Rousseau's elevation of reputation, noting that "Rousseau declares, that reputation is no less indispensable than chastity" (008-010). For Wollstonecraft, this conflation is precisely the error: by equating the external marker with the internal quality, Rousseau's philosophy authorises the very system of superficial morality that the *Vindication* seeks to dismantle.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Where conduct-book writers like Dr. Gregory emphasised the preservation of reputation as a woman's primary moral duty, Wollstonecraft inverts the priority: genuine virtue must come first, and a good reputation will follow naturally from it — not the reverse. She challenges the entire premise that women's moral worth should be measured by public perception, arguing instead for an internal standard grounded in reason and conscience. This places her in direct opposition to the eighteenth-century cult of female reputation, which she regards as a mechanism of social control rather than a path to moral improvement.
