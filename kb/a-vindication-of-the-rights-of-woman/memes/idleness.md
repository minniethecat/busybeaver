The state of unproductive inactivity that Wollstonecraft identifies as a principal source of women's vices and follies. She argues that idleness is not a natural feminine trait but the inevitable consequence of denying women access to serious occupations, education, and public life. "But what punishment," she asks, "is reserved for idleness?" (004-012).

## Definition

Wollstonecraft uses "idleness" to describe both a social condition and a moral failing. As a social condition, it is the enforced leisure of women of the upper and middle classes who are excluded from "the vulgar employments of life" (004-061). As a moral failing, it is the habit of mind that results from never having to exert oneself toward any serious purpose. The two are connected: women are rendered idle by their circumstances, and their idleness then becomes a source of the very vices — frivolity, intrigue, vanity — for which they are condemned. "But what punishment," she asks pointedly, "is reserved for idleness?" (004-012), noting that male soldiers and aristocrats also suffer from "active idleness" (001-028).

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's treatment of idleness links gender, class, and moral psychology. She argues that idleness is a structural feature of an unjust society, not a personal failing. Soldiers "may be termed active idleness" (001-028) in their confined existence, just as women suffer from the narrow sphere to which custom confines them. The wealthy, "bred up" to idleness, pursue empty pleasures "which consequent idleness forces the rich to pursue" (009-028).

For women specifically, idleness is both cause and effect of their degradation. "The state of idleness in which women are educated," she writes, is the root of their "enervating vices" (004-082). Barred from serious occupations, women fill their time with trivial pursuits: "gallantry and despotism" are "oftener the effect of idleness than of a lively fancy" (004-094). The solution is not moral exhortation — women are already lectured endlessly about their duties — but structural change: education, employment, and a "civil existence in the state."

Wollstonecraft is particularly critical of the way idleness corrupts the wealthy. "What but habitual idleness can hereditary wealth and titles produce?" (009-002). The idle rich become a drain on society, and their example corrupts those below them. In her educational proposals, she insists that schools should teach habits of industry to both sexes, and that education — not wealth — should determine a person's place in the social order. "It is the pestiferous purple," she writes elsewhere of aristocratic luxury, "which renders the progress of civilization a curse" (001-033).

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Eighteenth-century moralists frequently condemned idleness but rarely connected it to structural causes. Wollstonecraft's innovation is to show that women's idleness is not a moral defect of the sex but a rational response to restricted opportunities. Where conduct books advised women to fill their leisure with "improving" accomplishments, Wollstonecraft demands that they be given access to genuine occupations — medicine, business, farming — that would make idleness impossible and virtue natural.

## Key Passages

> Soldiers, as well as women, acquire the manners of the profession. They may be termed active idleness. More confined to the society of men, the former acquire a fondness for humour and mischievous tricks; whilst the latter, mixing with the world, are rendered weak and artificial by the false respect they meet with. (001-028)

> It were to be wished, that idleness was not allowed to generate, on the rank soil of wealth, most of the frailties and all the vices that undermine private virtue and public happiness. (008-025)

> I have, throughout this work, laboured to shew, that women's various follies and vices arise, in a great degree, from the state of idleness in which women are educated, and from the false refinement which a mistaken civilization produces. (004-082)
