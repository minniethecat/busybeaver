In Wollstonecraft's analysis of social institutions, **custom** is the antagonist of reason — an inertial force that perpetuates arrangements long after their justification has collapsed, and that "supersedes nature" (005-017) by making the artificial appear inevitable. Custom is, for her, the primary mechanism by which women's subordination is maintained: not by argument but by habituation, not by force but by familiarity.

The authority of custom is, in Wollstonecraft's account, a usurped authority. She acknowledges that "long established custom has awarded" superiority to men (P01-018), but the acknowledgment is an indictment, not a concession. Custom is not a source of legitimate norms; it is the obstacle that reason must overcome.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft distinguishes custom from both nature and reason. Nature provides the raw materials of human character — the capacities and tendencies common to the species. Reason provides the standard by which conduct and institutions should be judged. Custom occupies the space between them: it is the accumulated weight of past practice, the "prevailing custom" (007-009) that shapes expectation and behaviour without ever submitting itself to rational scrutiny.

The distinction between custom and principle is fundamental to her argument. Custom operates through habituation — "accustom them early to such confinement" (005-013) — while principle operates through conviction. The child "accustomed" to submission does not choose it; she simply never learns that there is an alternative. Custom, in this sense, is a mode of social control that works by foreclosing thought rather than by persuading it.

Wollstonecraft's critique extends to the language in which custom is defended. She notes the pressure "in compliance with custom, to use synonymous terms" (003-040) — the way customary thinking flattens distinctions that reason would preserve. When custom governs discourse, words lose their precision and argument becomes impossible.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument against custom proceeds from an analysis of its effects on women's character. "Custom has superseded nature" (005-017): the qualities attributed to women as natural — docility, submission, concern for reputation — are in fact the products of a customary education that has "accustomed them early to such confinement" (005-013) that they mistake their chains for ornaments.

The political force of this analysis is that it delegitimises the appeal to custom as a defence of women's subordination. When Wollstonecraft's opponents point to the long history of male authority over women, they are, in her view, citing the crime as its own justification. "I have frequently thought that it would be a noble task to trace the source in nature of any prevailing custom" (007-009) — the implication being that when traced honestly, most prevailing customs will be found to have no source in nature at all.

Custom functions, in her argument, as a kind of epistemic corruption. The mind "accustomed to bow down to power" (003-033) loses the capacity to recognise injustice when it sees it. Custom does not merely dictate behaviour; it reshapes perception, so that what is customary comes to seem natural, and what is natural — women's capacity for reason, for independence, for virtue — comes to seem unnatural.

The remedy Wollstonecraft proposes is the systematic substitution of reason for custom in every domain of life — education, marriage, law, government. A society governed by reason would recognise women's rights; a society governed by custom never will, because custom is the accumulated sediment of past power, and past power was always male.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft's attack on custom places her in direct opposition to the Burkean conservatism emergent in the 1790s, for which custom and prescription were the foundations of legitimate political order. Burke's defence of "prejudice" and "prescription" is, from Wollstonecraft's perspective, a defence of the very mechanisms that keep women in subjection.

She also breaks with the common eighteenth-century rhetorical strategy of appealing to "the customs of all ages and nations" as evidence of a moral truth. For Wollstonecraft, the universality of a custom proves nothing about its justice — it proves only the universality of the power that imposed it.

## Key Passages

> It is time to effect a revolution in female manners — time to restore to them their lost dignity — and make them, as a part of the human species, labour by reforming themselves, to reform the world. It is time to separate unchangeable morals from local customs. (003-040)

> Men have superior strength of body; but were it not for mistaken notions of beauty, women would acquire sufficient to earn their own subsistence, the true definition of independence; and to bear those bodily inconveniences which custom, more than nature, seems to have entailed on them as a sex. (004-078)

> I have frequently thought that it would be a noble task to trace the source in nature of any prevailing custom, and to shew how far it is founded on reason, or arises from casual circumstances. (007-009)

> Girls are from the earliest infancy accustomed to hear conversations about dress, beauty, and accomplishments, and they learn to consider these as the grand business of human life. (005-013)
