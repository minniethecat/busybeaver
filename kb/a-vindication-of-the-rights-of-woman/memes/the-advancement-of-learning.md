## Overview

_The Advancement of Learning_ (1605) is Francis Bacon's systematic survey of the state of human knowledge and his argument for the expansion of learning through empirical method. It is one of the earliest and most influential works in the English language to argue for the progressive nature of knowledge and the importance of systematic inquiry. Wollstonecraft's Vindication shares Bacon's conviction that intellectual progress is both possible and morally urgent.

## Bacon's Quotations in the Vindication

Wollstonecraft quotes Bacon directly at two significant junctures in her argument. In Chapter II, she invokes Bacon's hierarchical vision of human nature to challenge the reductive treatment of women:

> "Certainly," says Lord Bacon, "man is of kin to the beasts by his body: and if he be not of kin to God by his spirit, he is a base and ignoble creature!" (002-005)

Wollstonecraft deploys this quotation to insist that women, like men, possess a spiritual and rational nature that elevates them above mere animal existence. The implication is that denying women intellectual cultivation is tantamount to denying their kinship with the divine — an argument that carries particular weight given the religious framework of late eighteenth-century moral discourse.

In Chapter IV, Wollstonecraft returns to Bacon when discussing the tension between domestic obligations and public duty. She cites his observation that "He that hath wife and children... hath given hostages to fortune; for they are impediments to great enterprises, either of virtue or mischief" (004-053). Wollstonecraft engages this sentiment critically, acknowledging the real constraints of family life while insisting that such constraints should not be wielded as arguments for women's exclusion from intellectual and civic participation.

## In the Vindication

Wollstonecraft does not cite _The Advancement of Learning_ by name, but the work's influence permeates her argument. Bacon's insistence that knowledge must be organised, expanded, and made available to all who can benefit from it — rather than hoarded by a privileged few — is directly extended by Wollstonecraft to women. Where Bacon advanced the frontiers of learning for "mankind," Wollstonecraft insists that this advancement must include womankind.

Bacon's metaphor of knowledge as power, which Wollstonecraft quotes approvingly, originates in the intellectual programme first laid out in _The Advancement of Learning_. Wollstonecraft's own method — arguing from observed facts about women's condition rather than from theological or conventional premises — is deeply Baconian in spirit. Her empiricism, her distrust of received authority, and her faith in the progressive power of reason all echo the Baconian programme.

## Bacon's Epistemological Legacy

_The Advancement of Learning_ was revolutionary in its time for arguing that knowledge should be organised systematically rather than accumulated haphazardly through tradition and authority. Bacon divided human understanding into three domains — history (memory), poetry (imagination), and philosophy (reason) — and argued that each required its own methods of inquiry. This tripartite division, while reflecting early modern categories, established the principle that different forms of knowledge demand different modes of investigation. Wollstonecraft's own approach to the "woman question" is Baconian in precisely this sense: she insists on examining women's actual condition rather than deducing their nature from theological or customary premises.

## Significance for Wollstonecraft's Feminism

Wollstonecraft's use of Bacon serves a strategic rhetorical purpose. By grounding her argument in the authority of a canonical male philosopher — indeed, one of the founding figures of modern science — she preempts the charge that her feminism is merely a sentimental or irrational protest. Bacon provides her with a vocabulary of empiricism, progress, and intellectual ambition that she redirects toward the project of women's emancipation. At the same time, her critical engagement with Bacon's remarks on family life (004-053) demonstrates that she does not simply defer to male authority but actively interrogates it.
