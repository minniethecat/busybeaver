# Conduct

Conduct, in Wollstonecraft's moral vocabulary, is more than mere behaviour: it is the visible manifestation of an inner moral architecture. The quality of a person's conduct reveals the quality of the principles by which they are governed. This makes conduct a central diagnostic tool in the *Vindication* — by observing the conduct of women, Wollstonecraft argues, we can trace its deficiencies back to the educational and social conditions that produce it.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft defines conduct by contrast with its counterfeits. True conduct flows from rational principle; false conduct from the imitation of manners, the pressure of social expectation, or the ungoverned impulse of passion. "The conduct of an accountable being must be regulated by the operations of its own reason," she writes (002-082), establishing that conduct, properly understood, presupposes moral agency. A being incapable of reasoning cannot be said to *conduct* herself — she is merely driven by external forces.

This definition has radical implications for Wollstonecraft's assessment of women's position. If women's conduct is "unstable" and their opinions "wavering" (004-033), the fault lies not in female nature but in an education that denies them the intellectual resources for genuine moral self-governance. The conduct that society condemns in women — coquetry, vanity, inconstancy — is the predictable outcome of a system that teaches them to value appearance over substance and to depend on the approval of others rather than the dictates of their own reason.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

The argument proceeds from first principles. Wollstonecraft establishes early in the *Vindication* that "the conduct of an accountable being must be regulated by the operations of its own reason; or on what foundation rests the throne of God?" (002-082). The theological framing is essential: just as divine governance rests on wisdom rather than arbitrary will, so human conduct must rest on rational understanding rather than blind obedience or unexamined impulse. This analogy between divine and human governance runs throughout the work, giving Wollstonecraft's argument about conduct a metaphysical weight unusual in discussions of manners and morals.

The deficiency of women's conduct is traced to a specific educational failure. "When they should reason, their conduct is unstable, and their opinions are wavering" (004-033) — the instability of conduct is the direct consequence of the absence of rational discipline. Women are taught "a few superficial accomplishments" and encouraged to govern themselves by "the uncertain aid of inequality" (004-031) — by charm and manipulation rather than by principle. The result is a conduct that "gives a trifling turn to the conduct of women in most circumstances" (004-031), rendering them incapable of the steady virtue that alone can sustain domestic happiness and social duty.

Wollstonecraft does not limit her analysis to women. The parent who demands obedience "merely because it is their will" undermines the child's capacity for principled conduct from the earliest age. Children "should only be taught the simple virtues," she argues, "for if they begin too early to make allowance for human passions and manners, they wear off the fine edge of the criterion by which they should regulate their own" (011-019). Conduct is learned, and it is learned through the exercise of reason upon principle, not through the mechanical imitation of approved behaviours.

The political dimension of conduct is equally important. "I have before had occasion to observe," Wollstonecraft writes, "that a right always includes a duty, and I think it may, likewise fairly be inferred, that they forfeit the right, who do not fulfil the duty" (011-017). Conduct is not only a matter of personal morality but of political legitimacy: those who fail to conduct themselves according to reason — whether parents, husbands, or magistrates — forfeit the moral claim to the authority they exercise.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The eighteenth-century conduct-book tradition, exemplified by works such as James Fordyce's *Sermons to Young Women* (1766) and John Gregory's *A Father's Legacy to His Daughters* (1774), treated conduct as a matter of decorous behaviour — the proper management of one's manners, dress, and conversation to secure male approval and social respectability. Wollstonecraft subjects this tradition to devastating critique: conduct-book morality, she argues, teaches women to regulate their external behaviour while leaving their internal principles untouched. The result is the hypocrite who "may be amiable and engaging in her manners" while remaining "mean and contemptible in her heart."

Wollstonecraft's positive model draws on the classical and republican tradition that understood conduct as the expression of civic virtue. The citizen who governs himself by reason contributes to the rational governance of the state; the woman who is denied the cultivation of reason is thereby excluded from full participation in the moral community. This republican framing distinguishes Wollstonecraft's treatment of conduct from both the conservative conduct-book tradition and the radical individualism of some of her Enlightenment contemporaries. For Wollstonecraft, conduct is always social — it is how the individual relates to the community of rational beings — and it can only be improved by improving the social conditions that shape it.

## Key Passages

> In fact, the conduct of an accountable being must be regulated by the operations of its own reason; or on what foundation rests the throne of God? (002-082)

> Taught from their infancy, that beauty is woman's sceptre, the mind shapes itself to the body, and, roaming round its gilt cage, only seeks to adorn its prison. (004-031)

> When they should reason, their conduct is unstable, and their opinions are wavering — not the wavering produced by deliberation or progressive views, but by contradictory emotions. (004-033)

> It is easier, I grant, to command than reason; but it does not follow from hence, that children cannot comprehend the reason why they are made to do certain things habitually; for, from a steady adherence to a few simple principles of conduct flows that salutary power, which a judicious parent gradually gains over a child's mind. (011-018)
