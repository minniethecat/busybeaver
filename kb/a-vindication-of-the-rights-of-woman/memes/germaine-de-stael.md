# Germaine de Stael

## Overview

Germaine de Stael (1766–1817), born Anne Louise Germaine Necker, was a French woman of letters who became one of the most prominent intellectuals of the Revolutionary and Napoleonic eras. In 1792, when Wollstonecraft wrote *A Vindication of the Rights of Woman*, de Stael was twenty-six years old and had recently published her *Letters on Rousseau*, a work of enthusiastic admiration for the Genevan philosopher. Wollstonecraft directly engages this early work in ch05, using de Stael's effusive praise of Rousseau as a text to dissect — and as evidence of how even intelligent women can be seduced into celebrating their own subordination.

## In the Text

Wollstonecraft introduces de Stael at [005-122] as speaking "the same language as the lady just cited" — that is, Mrs. Piozzi — "with more enthusiasm." The critique that follows is one of the most sustained and rhetorically forceful passages in the *Vindication*. She quotes de Stael's own words at length, letting the young author's praise of Rousseau stand as self-incrimination: "Though Rousseau has endeavoured to prevent women from interfering in public affairs... in speaking of them, how much has he done it to their satisfaction!"

Wollstonecraft's rebuttal is devastating. She identifies Rousseau as a "sensualist" who "paid fervent adoration at the shrine of beauty" — not as a defender of women but as a man who "only wished to see it embellished by charms, weaknesses, and errors." The master, she charges, "wished to have a meretricious slave to fondle, entirely dependent on his reason and bounty." The problem with de Stael's encomium is precisely that it mistakes sensual adoration for genuine respect. When de Stael writes that Rousseau's "heart is devotedly theirs," Wollstonecraft retorts: "It is not empire—but equality, that they should contend for."

The passage is significant not only as a critique of Rousseau but as a critique of a contemporary female intellectual. Wollstonecraft treats de Stael not with contempt but with a kind of pained recognition — de Stael represents the class of intelligent women who have been so thoroughly socialized into the gallantry system that they cannot recognize their own oppression. The pathos of the passage lies in Wollstonecraft's awareness that de Stael's "sensibility" — the very quality cultivated in women — makes her vulnerable to exactly this kind of seduction by eloquence.

## Key Quotations

> "The Baroness de Stael speaks the same language as the lady just cited, with more enthusiasm. Her eulogium on Rousseau was accidentally put into my hands, and her sentiments, the sentiments of too many of my sex, may serve as the text for a few comments." [005-122]

> "True!—For never was there a sensualist who paid more fervent adoration at the shrine of beauty. So devout, indeed, was his respect for the person, that excepting the virtue of chastity, for obvious reasons, he only wished to see it embellished by charms, weaknesses, and errors." [005-122]

> "The master wished to have a meretricious slave to fondle, entirely dependent on his reason and bounty; he did not want a companion, whom he should be compelled to esteem..." [005-122]

> "It is not empire—but equality, that they should contend for. Yet, if they only wished to lengthen out their sway, they should not entirely trust to their persons, for though beauty may gain a heart, it cannot keep it." [005-122]

## Biographical Summary

Germaine de Stael was born in Paris, the daughter of the financier and statesman Jacques Necker. Her mother hosted one of the most celebrated salons of the ancien regime, and de Stael grew up immersed in intellectual and political life. She married the Swedish ambassador to France, Baron de Stael-Holstein, in 1786. By 1792, she had published her *Letters on the Works and Character of J.-J. Rousseau* (1788), the work Wollstonecraft critiques. She would go on to become one of the most important European intellectuals of her era, writing major works including *On Literature* (1800) and *On Germany* (1810), and becoming a fierce opponent of Napoleon. She and Wollstonecraft never met, but both were in Paris during the Revolution in 1792–93.
