# Paradise Lost

## Overview

Epic poem by John Milton (1667) on the Fall of Man — cited by Wollstonecraft in discussions of prelapsarian marriage and Eve's moral capacity. Milton's depiction of Adam and Eve before the Fall provides Wollstonecraft with a powerful literary touchstone: she draws on his portrait of companionate marriage while contesting the hierarchical vision encoded in "He for God only, she for God in him." Wollstonecraft declares that "Milton describes our first frail mother" (002-004) in terms that simultaneously grant her rational capacity and subordinate her to Adam's authority. Her strategy is to accept the portrait while rejecting the hierarchy (002-006).

Wollstonecraft's engagement with *Paradise Lost* is part of a broader argumentative strategy. She invokes literary and philosophical authorities — [[john-milton|Milton]], [[rousseau|Rousseau]], the Bible — not to defer to them but to claim them as partial allies. Milton's Eve is a more promising figure for feminist reclamation than [[rousseau|Rousseau]]'s Sophie, because Milton grants her genuine rational capacity even as he subordinates her to Adam. Wollstonecraft's reading extracts the first premise while rejecting the second (P03-014), demonstrating that the very texts used to justify women's subordination contain the resources for their own critique. This method reaches its fullest expression when she quotes Adam's own words back at the patriarchal tradition: "Among unequals what society can sort?" (002-016).

## In the Text

Wollstonecraft engages *Paradise Lost* primarily in Chapters II and IV. In Chapter II, she directly quotes Milton's verses to establish the problem: "Thus Milton describes our first frail mother; though when he tells us that women are formed for softness and sweet attractive grace, I cannot comprehend his meaning, unless, in the true Muhammadan strain, he meant to deprive us of souls" (002-004). This is a characteristic move — she names the enormity of the implication (denying women souls) to force the reader to confront what is at stake in the aestheticized language of female subordination.

She then quotes Eve's own words to Adam: "My author and disposer, what thou bidst / Unargued I obey; so God ordains; / God is thy law, thou mine; to know no more / Is woman's happiest knowledge and her praise" (002-007 through 002-011). Wollstonecraft treats this as a speech she would give to children, not adults: "These are exactly the arguments that I have used to children; but I have added, 'Your reason is now gaining strength'" (002-012). The juxtaposition exposes the infantilization at the heart of the hierarchical model.

Yet she also finds in Milton a more promising vision. She quotes Adam's speech to God: "Among unequals what society / Can sort, what harmony or delight? / Which must be mutual, in proportion due / Given and received" (002-016 through 002-019). This passage, in which Adam himself argues for a companion equal to himself, provides Wollstonecraft with a Miltonic warrant for the egalitarian marriage she advocates.

### Eve as Rational Agent

Milton's depiction of Eve is, for Wollstonecraft, both resource and target. Unlike Rousseau's Sophie — who is educated solely to please — Milton's Eve possesses genuine rational capacity, moral agency, and the ability to deliberate and choose. This makes her a more promising literary precedent for Wollstonecraft's argument than any character in eighteenth-century conduct literature. Yet Milton embeds this rational Eve within a hierarchical frame: "He for God only, she for God in him." Wollstonecraft's reading strategy is to accept the portrait of Eve's rational capacity while rejecting the hierarchical conclusion Milton draws from it — a move that parallels her treatment of [[rousseau|Rousseau]] throughout the *Vindication*.

### Eden as Counter-Model

The prelapsarian state in *Paradise Lost* offers Wollstonecraft a vision of what marriage could be: a relationship of genuine companionship between moral equals who differ in function but not in rational capacity. Wollstonecraft reads the Edenic marriage as the ideal that her own society has corrupted. The Fall, in her implicit reading, is not merely a theological event but a social one: the introduction of hierarchy, domination, and the subordination of women that her own century had raised to the status of [[nature|natural]] law. This reading places her in a tradition of [[reform|radical Protestant]] critique that reads Genesis as a charter for companionate marriage, not patriarchal authority.

### Wollstonecraft's Miltonic Strategy

Wollstonecraft's use of Milton exemplifies her broader rhetorical approach: to invoke and subvert authority simultaneously. By quoting Milton at length — both the hierarchical Eve passages and the egalitarian Adam passages — she shows that the literary canon is not monolithic. The same poet who wrote "He for God only, she for God in him" also wrote Adam's demand for "fellowship fit to participate all rational delight" (002-024). Wollstonecraft's argument is that the second Milton is the better one — more true to [[reason]], more consistent with the nature of a God who would not create souls only to subordinate them. This method of internal critique, of turning an authority's own principles against their conclusions, is the signature move of the *Vindication*.
