# England

The nation whose laws, education, manners, and social hierarchies Wollstonecraft most directly critiques — the immediate context for her argument about women's degradation.

## Location

England in the 1790s was a constitutional monarchy undergoing rapid economic transformation through industrialization. London was Europe's largest city and a center of intellectual and political debate, particularly in response to the French Revolution.

## Relevance to the Text

While Wollstonecraft addresses her Dedication to a French statesman, England is the society she knows intimately and critiques most thoroughly. The English class system, English marriage laws, English educational practices, and English "polished manners" are her primary evidence for the systematic degradation of women. Her references to contemporary English writers — Gregory, Fordyce, Chesterfield — are references to specifically English forms of the conduct-book tradition.

### In the Argument

England provides Wollstonecraft with her most detailed examples of how social institutions corrupt female character. The English system of female education, the English cult of sensibility, and the English double standard of sexual morality are all subjected to sustained critique. Her argument is directed at English readers and English institutions, even when her examples range more widely.

## Historical Context

In 1792, English society was deeply divided over the French Revolution. Radical intellectuals like Thomas Paine and Wollstonecraft supported revolutionary principles, while conservatives like Edmund Burke defended the English constitution and traditional social hierarchy. The "Revolution Controversy" was the immediate context for Wollstonecraft's political writings.
