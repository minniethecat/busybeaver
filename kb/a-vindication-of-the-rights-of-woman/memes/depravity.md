# Depravity

Wollstonecraft uses "depravity" to name the moral corruption that results from a society organized around sensual pleasure rather than rational virtue — a corruption that, she argues, afflicts both sexes, though it takes distinct forms in each.

## Definition in Context

Depravity, for Wollstonecraft, is not innate sinfulness but the acquired corruption of character through habitual indulgence in sensual appetite without the discipline of reason. It is social in origin and structural in its effects.

## Usage in the Text

### Sexual Depravity

In Chapter 7, she condemns "loose behaviour" that "shows such habitual depravity, such weakness of mind" (007-013), arguing that "it is vain to expect much public or private virtue, till both men and women grow more modest" (007-013). Depravity here is the product of a culture that separates sexual conduct from moral character.

### Female Depravity and Reputation

In Chapter 8, she identifies "the grand source of female depravity" as "the impossibility of regaining respectability by a return to virtue" (008-011). The sexual double standard — which condemns a woman permanently for a single lapse while excusing male libertinism — creates depravity by denying women the possibility of moral recovery.

### The Appetite That Brings the Sexes Together

Most pointedly, she writes: "The depravity of the appetite, which brings the sexes together, has had a still more fatal effect" (008-027). The corruption is not in sexuality itself but in the way society has made sexual desire — rather than rational companionship — the foundation of relations between men and women.

## Theoretical Significance

Wollstonecraft's treatment of depravity distinguishes her from both the conservative moralists who saw women as especially prone to sexual sin and the libertines who celebrated sensual indulgence. For her, depravity is a structural problem: a society that educates women to be pleasing rather than rational, and that judges them by a sexual standard it does not apply to men, will inevitably produce moral corruption in both sexes.
