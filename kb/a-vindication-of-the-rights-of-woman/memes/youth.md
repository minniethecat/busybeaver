Youth, in Wollstonecraft's educational philosophy, is not merely a biological stage but the decisive period for character formation — "the first years of youth should be employed to form the mind" (005-009) through rational education rather than the pursuit of superficial accomplishments.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft regards youth as the period during which the foundations of adult character are laid. She emphasizes its plasticity: "in youth the seeds of every affection" (012-003) are sown, and the quality of education received during this period determines the moral trajectory of the entire life.

Youth is also a period of natural energy and ardour. Wollstonecraft observes "the vigour and vivacity of youth" (004-076) as a positive force that, when properly directed, produces intellectual and moral growth. However, when misdirected — as it is for women under the prevailing system — this natural energy becomes mere "youthful ebullitions of animal spirits" (005-119) without lasting substance.

The distinction between the education of male and female youth is central to her critique. While young men "in their youth, are prepared for professions" (004-030), young women are trained only in the arts of pleasing. This asymmetry produces the enduring inequality that Wollstonecraft seeks to remedy.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Youth functions in Wollstonecraft's argument as the causal mechanism that explains how artificial gender difference is produced and reproduced. The different treatment of male and female youth is not a response to natural difference but its manufacturer.

She identifies several specific failures in the education of female youth. First, young women are not given "the natural frankness of youth" (002-047) but are instead taught dissimulation and reserve. Second, their natural curiosity is suppressed rather than cultivated through substantive instruction. Third, the "pretty freshness of youth" (004-074) is treated as their primary asset, with the consequence that when it fades they are left without intellectual resources.

Wollstonecraft's own biography informs her analysis. The biographical sketch notes that "in early youth she exhibited traces of exquisite sensibility" (P01-006), suggesting that natural intellectual gifts require proper cultivation to bear fruit. Her proposal for national education in Chapter 12 places particular emphasis on the shared education of boys and girls during youth, believing that "the exercise of youthful sympathies forms the moral" (012-024) character.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft directly opposes the educational philosophy that treats youth as a period to be filled with accomplishments rather than genuine learning. She criticizes "that hasty premature instruction, which leads them presumptuously" (005-143) to repeat opinions rather than think independently.

Against the Rousseauian view that youthful impulses should be indulged as natural, she insists that "in youth their faculties are not brought into exercise" (002-020) by the prevailing system — nature itself is not the problem; defective education is.

## Key Passages

> In youth the seeds of every affection are sown which are to adorn the future character; and the basis of every virtue is laid during this susceptible period. (012-003)

> The first years of youth should be employed to form the mind, and the heart accustomed to feel the pleasures of benevolence, before the passions are awakened. (005-009)

> It is natural for youth to adorn the first object of its affection with every good quality, and the emulation produced by the consciousness of inferiority may be turned to good account. (005-124)

> When the pretty freshness of youth is worn off, these artless graces become studied airs. (004-074)
