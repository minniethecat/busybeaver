# Morality

## Definition

Wollstonecraft argues throughout the *Vindication* that **morality** must rest on a rational foundation, not on sentiment, custom, or the arbitrary dictates of social convention. She is particularly critical of a "double morality" that holds women to one standard and men to another — a system she diagnoses as fundamentally corrupting because it rewards the appearance of [[virtue]] rather than its substance.

> "Noble morality! and consistent with the cautious prudence of a little soul that cannot extend its views beyond the passing moment." (002-071)

The true foundation of morality, for Wollstonecraft, is the [[character]] of the supreme being — which provides a single, eternal standard available [[equality|equal]]ly to all rational creatures. "When morality shall be settled on a more solid basis," she writes, the artificial distinctions between male and female [[virtue]] will dissolve (002-081).

## In Context

The Dedication establishes the stakes early: without a rational basis, "morality becomes an empty name" (P02-005). Wollstonecraft's critique of [[charles-maurice-de-talleyrand-perigord|Talleyrand]]'s proposal for [[education|female education]] is precisely a moral critique — she charges that his scheme, by denying women the cultivation of [[reason]], undermines morality itself.

Chapter I links morality to the trinity of [[reason]]-[[virtue]]-[[knowledge]]. Chapter II contains the sharpest analysis of how a gendered morality operates: women are trained to value reputation over conscience, and men are permitted vices that women are not — a system that degrades both sexes. Chapter IV intensifies the argument, declaring that "without [[knowledge]] there can be no morality" (004-049).

### The Critique of Double Morality

Wollstonecraft's sharpest moral argument is not against any particular vice but against the structural hypocrisy of a gendered moral code. She observes that women are held to a standard of sexual purity from which men are exempt, while simultaneously being denied the rational [[education]] that would make genuine moral choice possible. The result is a system that produces precisely the moral failings it claims to deplore: women, trained only to guard their reputation rather than their conscience, become duplicitous; men, never held accountable for the vices they encourage in women, become [[tyranny|tyrannical]]. "It is justice, not charity, that is wanting in the world" — her moral vision centres on structural fairness rather than the performance of feminine benevolence that conduct books prescribed.

### The Theological Grounding

Wollstonecraft grounds her moral philosophy in a rational theology: "The only solid foundation for morality appears to be the character of the supreme being" (003-027). This is a strategic and sincere move: by appealing to God's character as the single standard, she undercuts all arguments for sex-specific morality. If God's moral character is one and unchanging, and both men and women are made in God's image with rational souls, then the moral law must be the same for both — and any system that demands different virtues from men and women is not merely unjust but theologically erroneous. "Connected with man as daughters, wives, and mothers, their moral character may be estimated by their manner of fulfilling those simple duties; but the end, the grand end of their exertions should be to unfold their own faculties, and acquire the dignity of conscious virtue" (002-043).

## Further Context

Wollstonecraft's moral philosophy occupies a distinctive position in eighteenth-century thought. She shares with the Rational Dissenters ([[joseph-johnson|Joseph Johnson]]'s circle at Newington Green) the conviction that morality must rest on [[reason]] rather than revelation alone, but she goes further than most of her male contemporaries in insisting that this rational foundation applies to women as fully as to men. The gendered double standard she attacks was not a marginal feature of Enlightenment moral philosophy — it was embedded in the work of its most celebrated figures, from [[rousseau|Rousseau]]'s gendered education in *[[emile|Émile]]* to the conduct-book tradition that prescribed separate moral codes for each sex. Wollstonecraft's insistence on a single, reason-based moral standard for all human beings — regardless of sex — places her at the radical edge of Enlightenment thought, anticipating arguments that would not become mainstream in moral philosophy for another two centuries.
