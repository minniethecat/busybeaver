# Morality

Wollstonecraft's demand for a single, rational standard of morality applicable equally to men and women — grounded in eternal principles, not social fashion.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft insists that "if morality has an eternal foundation," there must be "but one rule of right" for both sexes (002-076). She rejects the double standard by which men's morality is judged by one measure and women's by another. True morality, in her view, is grounded in "the character of the Supreme Being" (003-032), not in social convention or gendered expectations. "Without knowledge there can be no morality" (004-044) — moral understanding requires intellectual cultivation, not passive obedience.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

The author contends that the entire system of gendered morality — in which women are taught "a kind of morality founded on the fashion of the age" (001-026) rather than on rational principles — is "highly injurious to morality" itself (001-025). When women are trained to value reputation over conscience, "morality becomes an empty name" (P02-005), a mere performance rather than a genuine commitment.

Wollstonecraft is withering about the consequences: "Noble morality! and consistent with the cautious wisdom of this world" (002-063), she exclaims sarcastically, describing a system in which women are taught to be virtuous only when watched. She calls for "the language of reason, the morality of life" (002-060), insisting that genuine moral principles must "give a substance to morality" (P02-003) rather than leaving it a hollow shell of social compliance.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Where the "polished manners" of eighteenth-century society "render vice more dangerous" by disguising it as refinement (001-027), Wollstonecraft demands that morality be grounded in immutable reason. She rejects the utilitarian calculus of reputation and social approval in favor of "the only solid foundation for morality" — individual character developed through rational understanding (003-032).
