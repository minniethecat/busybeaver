# Improvement

"Improvement" in Wollstonecraft's vocabulary carries the full weight of Enlightenment optimism about human perfectibility. She uses the term to denote both individual moral and intellectual development and the broader historical progress of society. Crucially, she insists that women must be included in this march of improvement — that excluding half of humanity from education and rational cultivation is not merely unjust but logically incompatible with the very idea of human progress.

## Definition

For Wollstonecraft, improvement is the gradual process by which human faculties — reason, virtue, and knowledge — are cultivated and refined. She draws on the Enlightenment conviction that humanity is capable of progressive advancement, but she radicalises this notion by applying it consistently to women. Improvement is contrasted throughout the *Vindication* with stagnation, which she identifies as the inevitable consequence of denying women access to education and rational pursuits. As she states in the Introduction, works "particularly written for their improvement must not be overlooked" (P03-002), signalling that improvement is an active project requiring deliberate effort and institutional support. Men "have superior opportunities of improvement" (001-029), and it is this inequality of opportunity — not any natural incapacity — that accounts for the difference between the sexes.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument for women's improvement is inseparable from her broader critique of social institutions. She contends that "the progress of human virtue and improvement in knowledge must receive continual checks" so long as women are denied education (003-010). Improvement is not a luxury but a duty: human beings are endowed with "the simple power of improvement" (004-004), and to neglect its cultivation is to violate the divine intention.

The political dimension of improvement is central. Wollstonecraft links the improvement of women to the improvement of society as a whole: "as men become more so; for the improvement must be mutual, or the injustice which one half of the human race are obliged to submit to, retorting on the oppressor, the virtue of man will be worm-eaten by the insect whom he keeps under his feet" (012-084). She envisions "the improvement and emancipation of the whole sex" (012-086) as a collective project benefiting all of humanity.

She also warns that superficial reform is insufficient. Any educational plan that "stops the progress of gradual improvement" by burdening the mind with rote learning rather than cultivating understanding is "abortive" (012-025, 012-027). True improvement requires the active exercise of reason, not passive reception.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Where conservative writers of her time viewed social hierarchy and women's subordination as fixed by nature or divine ordinance, Wollstonecraft insists on the possibility — and necessity — of change. She challenges the notion that women's "improvement" consists in acquiring decorative accomplishments (004-036) and instead demands that they be given access to the same serious education that enables men's intellectual and moral growth. Against the pessimism that sees human nature as irredeemably flawed, she asserts that "human discoveries and improvements may arrive" at unimagined heights once prejudice is cleared away (002-074).

## Key Passages

> The progress of human virtue and improvement in knowledge must receive continual checks, whilst the fetters, which cramp the minds of women, are not broken. (003-010)

> [T]hey have, in a true sense, the simple power of improvement; or, more properly speaking, the power of discerning and judging, which is the basis of all improvement. (004-004)

> I speak of the improvement and emancipation of the whole sex. (012-086)

> [F]or the improvement must be mutual, or the injustice which one half of the human race are obliged to submit to, retorting on the oppressor, the virtue of man will be worm-eaten by the insect whom he keeps under his feet. (012-084)
