# Neglect

Neglect, in Wollstonecraft's vocabulary, is not mere oversight but a systematic failure. When she speaks of the "neglected education of my fellow creatures" (P03-001), she identifies the root cause of women's degradation. Society deliberately withholds education, then blames women for lacking virtues that only education can produce.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft opens the Introduction with her "profound conviction, that the neglected education of my fellow creatures is the grand source of the misery" (P03-001) she deplores. This is the thesis of the entire work: neglect is not passive absence but an active social arrangement.

The education of women is deliberately withheld because an educated woman threatens the gender order. The verb "neglect" appears throughout as a transitive action: men "neglect the duties of humanity" (004-049); women "neglect all the duties of life" (003-022); society causes women's "understandings neglected" (004-033). In each case, neglect is something done to someone.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Parents neglect to educate daughters because society does not reward female intelligence. The "understanding is neglected, whilst accomplishments are cultivated" (004-023). This is not an accident but a deliberate choice about what kind of women society wants.

When women's "understandings [are] neglected; consequently they become" (004-033) weak and frivolous, society cites that weakness as proof of natural inferiority. This is the circular logic Wollstonecraft exposes. "When the seed-time of life is neglected? None" (005-030) — the agricultural metaphor is precise. Neglect in the formative period produces permanently stunted character.

The domestic consequences are clear: "neglecting the duties that reason alone" (004-040) can direct, women become unfit wives and mothers. When "men neglect the duties of humanity, women" (004-049) follow suit, for women learn their morality from the men who govern them.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The conduct-book tradition — Gregory, Fordyce, Rousseau — offered prescriptions for women's behavior while ignoring the conditions that made virtue impossible. They chastised women for frivolity while endorsing the educational neglect that produced it. Wollstonecraft's innovation is to identify neglect as cause rather than symptom.
