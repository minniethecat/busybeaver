# Prescription

The appeal to custom, tradition, or long-established practice as a substitute for rational argument. Wollstonecraft identifies prescription as the central intellectual obstacle to reform — in government, in education, and in relations between the sexes — and devotes her *Vindication* to forcing "the strong hold of prescription" by reason.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft defines prescription by opposition: it is whatever claim to authority rests on the mere fact of long usage rather than on rational justification. She observes "an indolent propensity in man to make prescription always take place of reason, and to place every duty on an arbitrary foundation" (011-001). Prescription is not a doctrine but a habit of mind — the default assumption that what has long been done must be right, that the age of a practice is evidence of its justice.

The concept has a specific legal and political history. In English constitutional thought, "prescription" referred to rights and customs that had been established so long that their origins were lost to memory — the common law's equivalent of Roman *usucapio*. Edmund Burke had elevated prescription to a political principle, arguing that the antiquity of British institutions was itself evidence of their wisdom. Wollstonecraft's use of the term directly engages this tradition, turning prescription from a conservative principle into an object of radical critique.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's attack on prescription structures the *Vindication* from its opening pages. In the Dedication to Talleyrand, she challenges the French revolutionary to examine his own assumptions: "If so, on what does your constitution rest? If on the nature of things, it is a rock that no storm can shake; but if on prescription, it crumbles before reason" (P02-010). The antithesis is absolute: either institutions rest on reason and nature, or they rest on mere habit, and habit offers no defense against rational criticism.

The argument gains force through its application to the family. Prescription justifies not only the rights of kings but parental authority: "The rights of kings are deduced in a direct line from the King of kings; and that of parents from our first parent" (011-001). The theological warrants for patriarchal authority — divine right of kings, Adam's dominion over his descendants — share the same logical structure: they appeal to origins rather than reasons. Wollstonecraft's republican politics and her feminism are two applications of a single principle: no authority is legitimate that cannot give a rational account of itself.

Wollstonecraft is attentive to the psychological appeal of prescription. It spares people the labor of thinking: "vicious or indolent people are always eager to profit by enforcing arbitrary privileges; and generally in the same proportion as they neglect the discharge of the duties which alone render the privileges reasonable" (011-009). Prescription is not merely an intellectual error; it is a moral temptation. Those who benefit from arbitrary privilege have a strong motive to avoid examining its foundations.

Against prescription, Wollstonecraft sets reason as the only legitimate basis of authority. "The strong hold of prescription will never be forced by reason" unless proofs are brought forward (001-010), she acknowledges — the work of rational criticism is difficult, and custom is fortified by centuries of habit. But the difficulty of the task does not excuse anyone from undertaking it. "To urge prescription as an argument to justify the depriving men (or women) of their natural rights, is one of the absurd sophisms which daily insult common sense" (001-010). The parenthetical "(or women)" is characteristic: she will not let her reader forget that the argument against prescription applies as forcefully to gender relations as to political constitutions.

The consequences of allowing prescription to govern women's lives are visible in the intellectual stunting she documents throughout the *Vindication*. "In what a state of inferiority must the female faculties have rusted" (012-088) when the little learning a few exceptional women attained was enough to earn them the sneering label of "learned women"? The rust on female faculties is prescription's work — the accumulated weight of centuries of custom that has convinced even women themselves that intellectual cultivation is unnatural to their sex.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Edmund Burke is the unnamed antagonist throughout this argument. His *Reflections on the Revolution in France* (1790) had made prescription the centerpiece of a conservative political philosophy: British institutions were legitimate because they had evolved over centuries, adapting gradually to circumstances in a process no abstract reason could replicate. Wollstonecraft had already attacked Burke in her *Vindication of the Rights of Men* (1790), and the critique of prescription in the *Vindication of the Rights of Woman* extends that earlier argument into the domain of gender. What Burke defends as the wisdom of tradition, Wollstonecraft exposes as the tyranny of inherited prejudice.

Thomas Paine, in *The Rights of Man* (1791), had also attacked prescription as a basis for government, arguing that each generation had the right to govern itself by its own reason. Wollstonecraft shares Paine's anti-prescriptive politics but goes further: the critique of prescription applies not only to parliaments and monarchies but to nurseries and drawing rooms. The family is a political institution, and its customs require the same rational scrutiny as the state's.

## Key Passages

> That the society is formed in the wisest manner, whose constitution is founded on the nature of man, strikes, in the abstract, every thinking being so forcibly, that it looks like presumption to endeavour to bring forward proofs; though proof must be brought, or the strong hold of prescription will never be forced by reason; yet to urge prescription as an argument to justify the depriving men (or women) of their natural rights, is one of the absurd sophisms which daily insult common sense. (001-010)

> There seems to be an indolent propensity in man to make prescription always take place of reason, and to place every duty on an arbitrary foundation. The rights of kings are deduced in a direct line from the King of kings; and that of parents from our first parent. (011-001)

> Vicious or indolent people are always eager to profit by enforcing arbitrary privileges; and generally in the same proportion as they neglect the discharge of the duties which alone render the privileges reasonable. (011-009)

> In what a state of inferiority must the female faculties have rusted when such a small portion of knowledge as those women attained, who have sneeringly been termed learned women, could be singular? (012-088)
