**Respect** is, for Wollstonecraft, the esteem that rational beings owe to one another in virtue of their shared capacity for reason and virtue. It is distinguished sharply from fondness, tenderness, and the blind admiration that characterise conventional relations between the sexes. Where fondness is a sentiment that may attach to any pleasing object, respect is a judgment — the recognition of moral worth in a being capable of self-governance.

Wollstonecraft argues that women are systematically denied the conditions under which they might earn respect, and then are offered fondness as a substitute. "Fondness is a poor substitute for friendship" (002-056) — and friendship, in her lexicon, is the relation between equals that is grounded in "the confidence of respect, instead of blind admiration" (002-056). A woman who is taught only to please can never be respected, because she can never develop the rational and moral qualities that respect acknowledges.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's treatment of respect draws on Enlightenment conceptions of rational esteem while extending them to women — a move her contemporaries largely declined to make. Respect, in her usage, is the regard owed to a being who exhibits "the dignity of a rational will that only bows to God" (002-078). It is not deference to rank, wealth, or beauty, but the acknowledgment of moral agency.

The foundation of respect is self-respect — "conscious dignity" (003-054) — which can only be cultivated through the exercise of reason and the practice of virtue. A woman who has been educated only to please, whose "understanding has been neglected" (002-065), cannot respect herself, because she has been given no grounds for self-estimation. And without self-respect, she cannot command the respect of others: she "neither acquires wisdom nor respectability of character" (002-059).

Wollstonecraft is clear that respect must be reciprocal. The ideal marriage is one of "friendship" grounded in "the confidence of respect" (002-056), not the asymmetrical relation of a superior condescending to an inferior. A woman who "submits without conviction to a parent or husband" (002-078) forfeits the very capacity for respect — both for herself and from others.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument that women deserve respect rather than mere admiration is one of the central threads of the *Vindication*. It proceeds through a series of linked claims about the nature of respect, the conditions under which it is earned, and the social arrangements that prevent women from meeting those conditions.

First, respect is grounded in reason and virtue, not in charm or beauty. "The being who patiently endures injustice, and silently bears insults, will soon become unjust" (005-020): submission does not earn respect; it destroys the moral capacity on which respect depends. Women must be "taught to respect themselves" (P02-017) — a project that requires education, independence, and the opportunity to exercise their rational faculties.

Second, the existing system of female education is designed to produce objects of fondness, not subjects of respect. Women are taught to cultivate "a pleasing person" (002-044) rather than "to make herself respectable" (002-044). The result is that men have "excited more tenderness than respect" (002-029) — an emotion "similar to what we feel for a pretty animal" (002-029), not the esteem due to a moral equal.

Third, the consequences of denying women respect extend beyond the domestic sphere. When women are not "taught to respect public good" (P02-017), they cannot participate as citizens. The "disregard of order" (002-017) that Wollstonecraft observes in women's conduct is not a natural defect but a predictable consequence of being excluded from the community of those who are taken seriously as moral agents.

Finally, Wollstonecraft insists that respect must be earned through character, not claimed as an entitlement. "Let them attain conscious dignity by feeling themselves only dependent on God" (002-079) — the dignity that grounds self-respect is not a gift from men but an achievement of the rational soul.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft's conception of respect as rational esteem grounded in moral equality contrasts sharply with the eighteenth-century convention of "respect" as deference to social rank. In the hierarchical society she anatomises, respect flows upward — from the lower orders to the higher, from women to men, from the laity to the clergy. Wollstonecraft reverses this current: respect flows toward virtue, regardless of rank or sex.

Rousseau, whom Wollstonecraft engages extensively, advocated for women to be educated to please men and to submit to male authority. In such a scheme, women are objects of desire or tenderness, never subjects of respect. Wollstonecraft's insistence on mutual respect between the sexes as the foundation of marriage is a direct repudiation of the Rousseauian model.

Dr. John Gregory, whose *Legacy to His Daughters* Wollstonecraft critiques at length, advised women to conceal their good sense and learning lest they offend male vanity. Wollstonecraft responds that such concealment makes women unworthy of respect: respect cannot be built on deception. "She who can surrender her reason to the guidance of another, and not feel the degradation, is already too debased to be respected" (002-078) — the sentiment recurs throughout the *Vindication*.

## Key Passages

> Fondness is a poor substitute for friendship. ... The man who can be contented to live with a pretty, useful companion, without a mind, has lost in voluptuous gratifications a taste for more refined enjoyments; he has never felt the calm satisfaction, that refreshes the parched heart, like the silent dew of heaven — of being beloved by one who could understand him. (002-056)

> I wish to persuade women to endeavour to acquire strength, both of mind and body, and to convince them, that the soft phrases, susceptibility of heart, delicacy of sentiment, and refinement of taste, are almost synonymous with epithets of weakness, and that those beings who are only the objects of pity ... will soon become objects of contempt. (004-082)

> Let them attain conscious dignity by feeling themselves only dependent on God. (002-079)

> She who submits without conviction to a parent or husband, will be as little able to fulfil her duty, as she will to attain that degree of respectability of character which a rational being ought to possess. (002-078)
