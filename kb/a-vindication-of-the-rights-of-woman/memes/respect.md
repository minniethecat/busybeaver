# Respect

## Definition

**Respect** in Wollstonecraft's moral vocabulary operates on two distinct levels throughout the *Vindication*. The first is the respect due to persons as rational beings capable of [[virtue]] — a respect grounded in recognition of moral capacity. The second is the hollow performance of deference demanded by social hierarchy, which Wollstonecraft consistently contrasts with genuine moral regard.

The Dedication to [[charles-maurice-de-talleyrand-perigord|Talleyrand]] opens with what appears to be conventional deference — "I carry my respect for your understanding still further" [P02-002] — but quickly transforms into a challenge: Wollstonecraft's respect is contingent on Talleyrand's willingness to *reason*, not on his political office. This rhetorical move establishes the pattern for the entire work: respect is something one earns by exercising [[reason]], not something one claims by virtue of position.

> "But, sir, I carry my respect for your understanding still further…" (P02-002)

Wollstonecraft's argument that "the rights of woman may be respected, if it be fully proved that reason calls for this respect" [P02-018] makes explicit what is implicit throughout the Dedication: respect must be rationally justified. It cannot be demanded as a matter of custom or hierarchy.

> "…the rights of woman may be respected, if it be fully proved that reason calls for this respect, and loudly demands justice for one half of the human race." (P02-018)

## In Context

### Respect and Public Good

Wollstonecraft links the capacity for respect directly to [[education]] and civic participation. She argues that women, "not taught to respect public good, nor allowed any civil rights," become incapable of genuine moral agency [P02-013]. The argument is circular in its diagnosis but radical in its remedy: women cannot respect what they are not taught to value, and they cannot value what they are excluded from.

> "…it will be excusable when, not taught to respect public good, nor allowed any civil rights, they attempt to do themselves justice by retaliation." (P02-013)

The cultivation of respect for public good requires that women be educated to understand their place within a broader moral community, rather than confined to the narrow sphere of domestic pleasing. Without this education, women's moral sentiments remain private and partial — directed toward pleasing individuals rather than contributing to the common good.

### Sacred Respect and Appearance

Wollstonecraft is sharply critical of the way "personal reserve, and sacred respect for cleanliness and delicacy in domestic life" [P02-005] are substituted for genuine moral character in the education of women. This "sacred respect" for appearances is contrasted with the respect that flows from [[virtue]]: one is a matter of outward performance, the other of inward moral reality.

> "…personal reserve, and sacred respect for cleanliness and delicacy in domestic life, do not render women the less ignorant or the more virtuous…" (P02-005)

The critique extends to the way men "respect" women — not as moral equals but as delicate creatures requiring protection, a respect that is indistinguishable from condescension and that perpetuates women's subordinate status.
