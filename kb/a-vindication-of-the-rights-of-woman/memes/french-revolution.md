# French Revolution

1789–1799. The political transformation of France that created the intellectual and political context for Wollstonecraft's argument about women's rights.

## Overview

The French Revolution began in 1789 with the summoning of the Estates-General and the formation of the National Assembly. The Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen (August 1789) proclaimed universal rights — yet, as Wollstonecraft immediately perceived, those rights were not extended to women. The Revolution's early years (1789–1792) were its most optimistic phase, when constitutional monarchy seemed possible and radical reform was on the agenda. By the time Wollstonecraft traveled to Paris in December 1792, the Revolution had entered its more violent phase — the September Massacres had just occurred, and the Terror was imminent.

## Wollstonecraft's Engagement

Wollstonecraft wrote the Vindication during the Revolution's middle period (1792), when revolutionary principles still seemed capable of peaceful realization. She addresses Talleyrand as a fellow "patriot" who shares her commitment to reform, and she frames her argument in revolutionary language — "the rights of woman" deliberately echoes "the rights of man." Yet she also perceives the Revolution's central contradiction: the exclusion of women from the universal rights it proclaimed. Her Dedication to Talleyrand is an intervention in revolutionary politics, demanding that the Revolution extend its principles to the whole human species.

### In the Argument

The Revolution provides Wollstonecraft with both vocabulary and target. The language of "rights," "liberty," and "citizenship" that the Revolution made available becomes the language of her demand for women's equality. At the same time, the Revolution's failure to include women exposes the limits of even the most progressive political movements of her era.

## Aftermath

Wollstonecraft witnessed the Terror firsthand in Paris (1793–1794) and recorded her observations in *An Historical and Moral View of the French Revolution*. The Revolution's descent into violence complicated but did not negate her commitment to its principles. She died in 1797, before the Revolution's conclusion with Napoleon's rise to power.
