**Discontent** in Wollstonecraft's *Vindication* is not a passing mood but a structural condition — the predictable emotional consequence of a life denied purpose, activity, and rational occupation. Women whose "spirits are broken by discontent" (002-044) are not merely unhappy; they are damaged by a social order that denies them the conditions of human flourishing and then blames them for the misery that results.

The concept is central to Wollstonecraft's psychological analysis of oppression. Discontent is what happens to human beings when their capacities are systematically suppressed — when they are denied education, confined to domestic triviality, and reduced to dependence on the will of others. It is not a personal failing but a social product, and Wollstonecraft's task is to trace its causes rather than to condemn its victims.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's conception of discontent draws on the eighteenth-century discourse of the passions, in which discontent was classified among the painful emotions — those arising from the frustration of desire or the obstruction of activity. The classification is morally significant: painful emotions are not vices but afflictions, and the appropriate response to them is not condemnation but investigation of their causes.

Discontent, in Wollstonecraft's usage, is distinguished from related states by its chronic character. Anger is a response to a specific injury; grief is a response to a specific loss. Discontent is a diffuse and persisting dissatisfaction — the emotional colouration of a life that has no adequate object. The woman who is "the prey of discontent" (009-034) is not angry about any particular thing; she is dissatisfied with everything because nothing in her life engages her capacities.

The connection to activity is essential. "How then can the great art of pleasing be expected from a creature who is taught to consider it as the sole business of her life?" (002-044) The rhetorical question links discontent to the absence of meaningful occupation. When the "great art of pleasing" is the only activity permitted to women, discontent is inevitable — because pleasing others is not an activity that can sustain a human life.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument about discontent unfolds through a series of vivid descriptions of women's domestic misery. The descriptions are not condemnations — they are diagnoses. She wants to understand why women are discontented, and her answer is structural: women are discontented because their lives are structured to produce discontent.

The argument has three stages. First, the fact of discontent is established through observation: women are "the prey of discontent" (009-034); their "spirits are broken by discontent" (002-044); they spend "their evenings, discontentedly" (004-060). The repetition of the word across the text creates a cumulative picture of a pervasive emotional condition.

Second, the causes of discontent are identified. Women are denied education, confined to the domestic sphere, and reduced to economic dependence. Each of these conditions is a frustration of a fundamental human capacity: the capacity for knowledge, the capacity for action, the capacity for independence. When capacities are frustrated, discontent is the result.

Third, the consequences of discontent are traced. The discontented woman "becomes the victim of discontent and blind indulgence" (003-045) — discontent drives her to seek relief in sensual pleasure, which only deepens her dissatisfaction. The circle is vicious: confinement produces discontent, discontent produces indulgence, indulgence reinforces the stereotypes that justify confinement.

The structural character of the analysis is what distinguishes Wollstonecraft's treatment from the moralising tradition. Conduct-book writers treated female discontent as a moral failing — a failure of gratitude, of patience, of submission. Wollstonecraft treats it as a rational response to irrational conditions. The woman who is discontented is not a bad woman; she is a woman whose situation is bad.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The eighteenth-century discourse of female discontent was dominated by the language of pathology. Female discontent was "the vapours," "the spleen," "hysteria" — medicalised categories that located the problem in women's bodies rather than in their social conditions. The medicalisation of discontent served a political function: if discontent is a disease, the appropriate response is treatment, not reform.

Wollstonecraft's analysis is a direct challenge to this medicalisation. She does not deny that discontent has bodily manifestations — the "spirits broken" and the "prey of discontent" are embodied states. But she insists that the causes of discontent are social, not somatic. The woman who wastes "life away, the prey of discontent" (009-034) is not ill; she is oppressed.

The contrast with Rousseau is instructive. Rousseau, in *Émile*, treats female discontent as a consequence of civilisation — women in the state of nature were content, and their discontent is a sign of how far society has departed from nature. Wollstonecraft accepts the diagnosis but rejects the prescription. Rousseau's solution is a return to domesticity; Wollstonecraft's is an advance toward equality.

## Key Passages

> How then can the great art of pleasing be expected from a creature who is taught to consider it as the sole business of her life, and whose mind is narrowed and the spirits broken by discontent? (002-044)

> The woman who has only been taught to please will soon find that her graces are but a sort of sunshine which plays on the surface of the waters, whilst the depths are dark and stagnant; or becomes the victim of discontent and blind indulgence. (003-045)

> Their husbands acknowledge that they find more pleasure at the club, or the tavern, than in the company of their wives; yet they return home, and expect to find them in good humour, or, at least their evenings, discontentedly. (004-060)
