# Weakness

Wollstonecraft identifies the cultivation of weakness in women as one of the most pernicious effects of a gendered education. She argues relentlessly that what passes for feminine delicacy is in fact "artificial weakness" [P03-014] — a socially engineered frailty that serves to justify women's subordination while corrupting their moral character.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft does not deny that physical differences exist between the sexes, but she insists that the weakness ascribed to women is overwhelmingly cultural rather than natural. She opens her analysis by observing that "the soft phrases, susceptibility of heart, delicacy of sentiment, and refinement of taste, are almost synonymous with epithets of weakness" [P03-008]. The language of feminine virtue, in other words, is indistinguishable from the language of feminine incapacity. Weakness is presented to women as desirable — an "amiable weakness" [004-040] that attracts male protection — but Wollstonecraft sees through this deception: what is praised as charm is in fact a condition of dependency. Women are "made by this amiable weakness entirely dependent" [004-040], and this dependence is precisely what sustains male dominance.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's treatment of weakness unfolds across several interconnected claims. First, she argues that women's weakness is actively produced rather than passively inherited. From childhood, girls are "kept in a state of perpetual childhood" [003-025], their bodies confined and their minds unchallenged. The result is not natural frailty but "the weakness of character ascribed to woman" [004-023], a weakness that has its "foundation" in social arrangements rather than in nature.

Second, she exposes the hypocrisy of male attitudes toward female weakness. Men "despise the very weakness they cherish" [004-014] — they cultivate dependency in women and then condemn women for being dependent. This double bind is one of Wollstonecraft's most acute psychological observations. The man who "contemptuously insults the very weakness he fostered" [006-018] exemplifies the bad faith at the heart of the gender system.

Third, Wollstonecraft analyses how women themselves come to internalise and exploit weakness. She notes that women "sometimes boast of their weakness, cunningly obtaining power by playing on the weakness of men" [003-009]. This is not a celebration of female strategy but a lament: when weakness is the only form of power available, women will naturally — and degradingly — resort to it. The cultivation of weakness thus corrupts women's character, teaching them manipulation in place of integrity.

Fourth, she links physical weakness to moral weakness. "In proportion to his weakness, he is played upon by designing men" [003-027] applies to women collectively: their cultivated physical and intellectual weakness makes them vulnerable to manipulation, including sexual manipulation. She observes that the law against seduction will "remain in force as long as the weakness of women caused the word seduction to be considered as an excuse" [004-080].

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The dominant eighteenth-century view, articulated most influentially by Rousseau, held that women's weakness was natural and that it served a providential purpose: women's frailty elicited male protection and chivalry, which in turn civilised men. Wollstonecraft regards this as a sentimental fantasy that obscures the reality of exploitation. Where Rousseau sees complementarity, she sees subordination. She also contests the medical discourse of her time, which routinely characterised women as constitutionally weaker. "If it be merely the refuge of weakness or wild fanaticism, and not a sober principle of action" [005-152], she writes of religious devotion, extending her critique of weakness as a substitute for rational conviction into the domain of faith itself.

## Key Passages

> "I wish to persuade women to endeavour to acquire strength, both of mind and body, and to convince them that the soft phrases, susceptibility of heart, delicacy of sentiment, and refinement of taste, are almost synonymous with epithets of weakness." [P03-008]

> "This artificial weakness produces a propensity to tyrannise, and gives birth to cunning, the natural opponent of strength." [P03-014]

> "Made by this amiable weakness entirely dependent, excepting what they can obtain by their charms and weakness." [004-040]

> "Women are every where deplorably ignorant, yet they sometimes boast of their weakness, cunningly obtaining power by playing on the weakness of men." [003-009]
