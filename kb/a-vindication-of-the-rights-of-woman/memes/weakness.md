# Weakness

## Definition

In *A Vindication of the Rights of Woman*, **weakness** is Wollstonecraft's
diagnostic term for the intellectual and moral enfeeblement that results from
women's deficient education. It is not — as her opponents argued — a natural
attribute of the female sex, but an *effect* of a system that denies women the
means to develop their rational faculties.

## Weakness as Artificial

Wollstonecraft emphatically rejects the claim that women are naturally weak.
The appearance of female weakness — in body, mind, and character — is produced
by a social order that values women only for their beauty and delicacy:

Women are "enfeebled by false refinement" (P03-002) — their minds weakened by an
education that substitutes accomplishments for understanding, sensibility for
reason, and the desire to please for the cultivation of virtue.

## The Circular Logic of Weakness

Wollstonecraft identifies a pernicious circular logic in contemporary arguments
about women: women are weak, therefore they should not be educated; but they are
weak precisely *because* they are not educated. Those who cite female weakness as
a justification for denying women rights are pointing to the symptoms of a disease
they themselves have caused and refuse to cure.

## Weakness of Mind

Intellectual weakness manifests as an inability to reason, to form principles, or
to sustain attention on abstract subjects. Wollstonecraft attributes this not to
any natural deficiency but to an education that "relaxes the other powers of the
mind" (004-040) by focusing exclusively on the body, on accomplishment, and on
pleasing.

## Weakness of Character

Moral weakness — what Wollstonecraft calls "a weak elegancy of mind" (P03-009) —
consists in the inability to act on principle. The woman of weak character is
governed by impulse, sensibility, and the desire for approbation rather than by
rational conviction. She may be amiable, graceful, and charming, but she cannot
be genuinely virtuous — for virtue requires the strength to follow reason against
inclination and social pressure.

## Strength as the Antidote

The remedy for weakness is the cultivation of *strength* — intellectual, moral,
and physical — through a reformed education. Wollstonecraft calls for women to
"acquire strength, both of mind and body" (P03-008), arguing that only strong
women can be good wives, mothers, and citizens.
