In Wollstonecraft's analysis, **situation** is the ensemble of social circumstances — economic, educational, and relational — that determines the possibilities available to an individual and, through habituation, shapes their character. The "situation of woman, at present" (P02-007) is, for Wollstonecraft, the central explanatory category: it is not nature but situation that has produced the degraded condition she diagnoses. By insisting on the explanatory power of situation, Wollstonecraft shifts the blame for women's perceived weaknesses from innate deficiency to structural circumstance, opening the door to reform through changed conditions.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft uses "situation" to denote the concrete social position an individual occupies — their economic dependence or independence, the type of education they receive, and the expectations placed upon them by custom. She argues that women's "dependent situation and domestic employments" (002-019) incline them more toward cunning than toward open, rational conduct, because their survival depends on managing the tempers of those on whom they depend. Situation is thus not merely external circumstance but a formative force that penetrates character.

The analytic power of the concept lies in its reversibility: Wollstonecraft insists that men "placed in similar situations have acquired a similar character" (004-101) to that of women, undermining any claim that feminine weakness is natural. If situation produces character, then changing situation — through education, economic independence, and political rights — can produce a different kind of woman, and a different kind of society.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument from situation is the sociological core of the *Vindication*. She repeatedly draws attention to "the education and situation of woman" (P02-007) as the twin determinants of female degradation, insisting that "each of these situations equally retards the progress" (004-011) of reason and virtue. The "equivocal humiliating situation" (004-052) in which women find themselves — dependent on male approval for their very subsistence — produces precisely the qualities that are then cited as proof of women's inferiority. This is, for Wollstonecraft, a cruel circular logic.

The concept has a liberating dimension. Wollstonecraft notes that women who have been forced by circumstance into independence — widows, those managing estates, those whose "local situation" (004-026) demands self-reliance — often display precisely the strength of character that is supposedly reserved to men. This observation is central to her argument: "changing situations" (004-055) can reveal the same human capacities in women that have been cultivated in men, proving that the difference is one of condition, not of nature. The "drapery of situation" (009-002) may hide the man or the woman, but it does not create their essential capacities.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Where conservative writers of her era treated women's subordinate condition as a reflection of divine or natural order, Wollstonecraft treats it as an artifact of "situation" — a contingent, historical arrangement that can and should be changed. Rousseau, whom she directly contests, imagines Sophie's situation as naturally fitted to her sex; Wollstonecraft insists that Sophie's character is the product of the situation Rousseau has constructed for her, not the justification for it. This shift from nature to situation is one of the most radical moves in the *Vindication*.

## Key Passages

> The education and situation of woman, at present, shuts her out from such investigations. (P02-007)

> Led by their dependent situation and domestic employments more than by the bent of their inclination, they acquire the art of pleasing, and suppress the native energy of the mind. (002-019)

> I have been led to remark, that each of these situations equally retards the progress of reason and the cultivation of the heart. (004-011)

> Men placed in similar situations have acquired a similar character. (004-101)
