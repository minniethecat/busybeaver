# Virtue

The foundational moral quality that Wollstonecraft argues must be cultivated through reason and independence, not through passive obedience or sexual stereotypes.

## Definition

For Wollstonecraft, virtue is inseparable from reason and independence. She defines it not as passive purity or mere chastity, but as the active exercise of moral judgment grounded in rational understanding (P02-003). True virtue "can only be produced by liberty" (P02-007), and she repeatedly asserts that without independence, virtue cannot exist (P02-002).

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft contends that women have been systematically denied the conditions necessary for virtue. By confining women to a state of dependence — economically, intellectually, and politically — society renders them incapable of genuine moral development (002-014). She argues that the "minor virtues" expected of women, such as "punctilious politeness," are merely performances that substitute for genuine moral character (002-022).

The author insists that virtue has no sex — "the prejudices that give a sex to virtue" confuse "simple truths with sensual reveries" (P03-013). Both men and women "in the acquirement of virtue, ought to aim at attaining a very different character" (002-001).

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Where Rousseau, in *Émile*, depicts women as naturally modest and designed for domestic submission, Wollstonecraft counters that such "artificial manners and virtues" produce only superficial ornament, not moral strength (001-021). She critiques John Gregory's *A Father's Legacy to His Daughters* for encouraging women to cultivate appearance at "the expense of every solid virtue" (002-015). For Wollstonecraft, virtue is not gendered; it is the universal "basis of every virtue" to be "independent" (P02-002).

## Key Passages

> "taking independence to be the basis of every virtue" (P02-002)

> "an orderly train of virtues spring, can only be produced by liberty" (P02-007)

> "the prejudices that give a sex to virtue, and confound simple truths with sensual reveries" (P03-013)

> "whether they have acquired any virtues in exchange for innocence" (001-011)
