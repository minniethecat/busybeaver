# Virtue

## Definition

For Wollstonecraft, **virtue** is not a set of gendered ornamental qualities — docility, [[sensibility|delicacy]], or submission — but rather the cultivation of [[reason]] and moral excellence that distinguishes the individual and forms the basis of a just society. She defines virtue in explicitly rational terms, arguing that true virtue must flow from the exercise of [[reason]], not from mere adherence to social convention or the performance of "spurious qualities" that have assumed the name.

> "Consequently the perfection of our nature and capability of happiness, must be estimated by the degree of reason, virtue, and knowledge, that distinguish the individual, and direct the laws which bind society: and that from the exercise of reason, knowledge and virtue naturally flow, is equally undeniable, if mankind be viewed collectively." (001-006)

Wollstonecraft's central move is to *de-gender* virtue: she contests the eighteenth-century assumption that men and women ought to acquire different [[character|moral character]]s. Against [[rousseau|Rousseau]] and other thinkers who argued that women's virtues are distinct from men's, she insists that if virtue is a meaningful concept, it must be the same for all rational beings.

> "If women are by nature inferior to men, their virtues must be the same in quality, if not in degree, or virtue is a relative idea; consequently, their conduct should be founded on the same principles, and have the same aim." (002-042)

## In Context

The concept of virtue runs through the entire text, from the Dedication to [[charles-maurice-de-talleyrand-perigord|Charles Maurice de Talleyrand-Périgord]] through the final chapters of the main argument. In the Dedication, Wollstonecraft frames her entire project as an effort in the "cause of virtue" (P02-003). In the Introduction, she explicitly subordinates feminine elegance to virtue, declaring that "the first object of laudable ambition is to obtain a [[character]] as a human being, regardless of the distinction of sex" (P03-009).

Chapter I establishes the philosophical foundation: virtue is linked to [[reason]] and [[knowledge]] as the yardstick of human perfection. Chapter II contains the most concentrated argument about the [[nature]] of virtue, where Wollstonecraft systematically dismantles the idea of sex-specific virtues and argues that "the two sexes, in the acquirement of virtue, ought to aim at attaining a very different [[character]]" is an argument designed merely "to account for, and excuse the tyranny of man" (002-002).

The concept reappears throughout the later chapters in discussions of [[education]], [[modesty]], and social roles, where Wollstonecraft repeatedly exposes how society confuses "convenient" behaviour with genuine moral virtue.

### The Degendering Argument

Wollstonecraft's most original contribution to virtue theory is her insistence that virtue cannot be sex-specific. The prevailing eighteenth-century view, exemplified by [[rousseau|Rousseau]] and the conduct-book tradition of [[john-gregory|Dr. Gregory]] and [[james-fordyce|Dr. Fordyce]], held that men's virtues (courage, rationality, independence) and women's virtues (chastity, [[modesty]], obedience) were fundamentally different in kind. Wollstonecraft identifies this as a logical contradiction: "If women are by nature inferior to men, their virtues must be the same in quality, if not in degree, or virtue is a relative idea" (002-042). If virtue means anything at all, it must mean the same thing for all rational beings. The alternative — that virtue is merely a relative label for whatever qualities a given society happens to praise in a given group — empties the concept of moral content entirely. This argument is central to her broader case for women's [[rights]]: if women cannot develop the same virtues as men, they are being denied the conditions of moral agency itself, not merely some incidental social advantage.

### Virtue and Independence

For Wollstonecraft, virtue is impossible without [[independence]]. A person who is economically dependent, legally subordinate, or socially constrained to please others cannot exercise the free [[judgment]] that virtue requires. "It is vain to expect virtue from women till they are in some degree independent of men" (002-024). This link between virtue and independence is one of her most radical claims: it transforms virtue from an internal disposition into a demand for structural reform. Women cannot be virtuous under conditions of [[tyranny]]; therefore, the reform of social institutions — marriage law, [[education]], professional opportunities — is a precondition of moral life, not merely a political preference.

## Key Passages

> "It is, then, an affection for the whole human race that makes my pen dart rapidly along to support what I believe to be the cause of virtue: and the same motive leads me earnestly to wish to see woman placed in a station in which she would advance, instead of retarding, the progress of those glorious principles that give a substance to morality." (P02-003)

> "I wish to show that elegance is inferior to virtue, that the first object of laudable ambition is to obtain a character as a human being, regardless of the distinction of sex." (P03-009)

> "Why should it be increased by prejudices that give a sex to virtue, and confound simple truths with sensual reveries?" (P03-014)

> "Such deeply rooted prejudices have clouded reason, and such spurious qualities have assumed the name of virtues, that it is necessary to pursue the course of reason as it has been perplexed and involved in error." (001-007)

> "Women are not allowed to have sufficient strength of mind to acquire what really deserves the name of virtue." (002-002)

> "Let their virtues be the same in kind, though they may vainly struggle for the same degree; and the superiority of man will be equally clear, if not clearer; and truth, as it is a simple principle, which admits of no modification, would be common to both." (002-088)
