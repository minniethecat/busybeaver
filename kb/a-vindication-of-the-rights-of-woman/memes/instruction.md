Instruction, in Wollstonecraft's analysis, is the formal component of education — the deliberate transmission of knowledge, principles, and methods — which she argues has been systematically misdirected in the case of women, producing artificial weakness where it should cultivate rational strength.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft draws a careful distinction between instruction and the broader concept of education. Instruction refers to explicit teaching: "the books of instruction, written by men of genius, have tended to narrow the understanding" (P03-002) of women rather than expand it. The problem is not that women receive no instruction, but that "the instruction which women have received has only tended" (P03-013) to render them more artificial.

The purpose of genuine instruction is not to fill the mind with accomplishments but "to adjust instruction as not to narrow the understanding" (004-057). This requires a delicate balance: instruction must provide the materials for reason to work upon without substituting received opinion for independent thought.

Wollstonecraft identifies a specific danger in "that hasty premature instruction, which leads them presumptuously" (005-143) to repeat what they have been told rather than understand it. Premature instruction produces opinionatedness without genuine knowledge — a condition she observes frequently in the women of her time.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

The critique of instruction as currently practiced forms a significant thread in Wollstonecraft's broader case for educational reform. She argues that "the books of instruction, written by men of genius" (P03-002) have been designed to make women pleasing rather than rational.

She is particularly critical of instructional works "professedly written for their instruction, which make the first impression" (006-006) on young female minds. These works, by conditioning women from their earliest reading to value appearance over substance, establish habits of thought that are difficult to undo.

The remedy she proposes is instruction on rational principles. In her scheme for national education, children "receive instruction, in some measure appropriated" (012-051) to their capacities and future stations. Instruction in history, politics, and "the history of man, and politics, might also be taught by conversation" (012-050) — not as rote memorization but as the cultivation of judgment.

Wollstonecraft's own life illustrates the value of self-directed instruction. "She received no literary instructions but such as were to be had" (P01-006) from her own efforts, yet through determined self-cultivation became one of the most formidable minds of her age.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft challenges the educational writers of her time who "endeavour by satire or instruction to improve" (P03-012) women while fundamentally misunderstanding the nature of the problem. Their instruction treats women as deficient creatures to be polished, rather than rational beings to be developed.

She also critiques the assumption "that we expect more from instruction, than mere instruction can produce" (005-122). Instruction alone, without the cultivation of reason and the formation of habits, cannot produce genuine moral agents. This distinguishes her position from the more optimistic Enlightenment view that information alone suffices for improvement.

## Key Passages

> The books of instruction, written by men of genius, have had the same tendency as more frivolous productions; and that, in the true style of Mahometanism, they have tended to narrow the understanding. (P03-002)

> The instruction which has hitherto been addressed to women, has been intended rather to make them alluring mistresses than affectionate wives and rational mothers. (P03-013)

> One difficult task is so to adjust instruction as not to narrow the understanding, whilst it prepares the pupil for a particular walk in life. (004-057)

> We expect more from instruction, than mere instruction can produce. (005-122)
