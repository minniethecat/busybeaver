# Affectation

The artificial and socially enforced performance of feminine delicacy that Wollstonecraft identifies as a core mechanism by which women are prevented from developing genuine virtue, reason, and independence of character.

## Definition

Affectation, in Wollstonecraft's framework, is the systematic production of artificial femininity through social pressure and defective education. She traces it not to any natural female disposition but to "false refinement" that draws women "out of their sphere ... not by an endeavour to acquire masculine qualities" but by a culture that rewards performance over substance (002-015).

Her analysis distinguishes between the natural expression of character and the studied performance demanded of women. When behaviour is governed by rigid codes of decorum rather than by cultivated understanding, what results is not virtue but "rank affectation" (005-080). The very systems intended to render women pleasing—modesty codes, manners instruction, sentimental education—produce the opposite effect, creating beings whose outward show masks inward vacancy.

The concept is inseparable from Wollstonecraft's broader critique of how societies evaluate women. She observes that "a man of sense soon sees through, and despises the affectation" (007-035), yet the social order continues to demand it, revealing that affectation serves not women's interests but the gratification of male expectations.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's indictment of affectation operates on multiple levels. At the most immediate, she criticises the behavioural codes prescribed to women as inherently self-defeating. The endless instruction in manners, deportment, and pleasing address produces not genuine grace but its counterfeit: "studied rules of behaviour only impose on shallow observers" (007-035).

She traces the root cause to women's intellectual deprivation. When women are denied the cultivation of reason, they are "entirely dependent on their senses for employment and amusement," leaving them prey to "the little vanities of the day" (002-048). The question she poses is devastating in its simplicity: "To gain the affections of a virtuous man, is affectation necessary?" (002-048). The implied answer—that genuine virtue, not artificial display, attracts genuine affection—undermines the entire edifice of feminine conduct literature.

At the structural level, Wollstonecraft links affectation to the romantic idealisation of love. The sentimental tradition, she argues, "spread affectation, and take from the dignity of virtue" (004-092). By painting love as a realm of heightened sensibility exempt from rational scrutiny, sentimental literature provides "a plausible excuse to the voluptuary, who disguises sheer sensuality under a sentimental veil" (004-092). The language of elevated feeling becomes a mask for base appetite.

Her alternative is clear: "if men were only anxious to cultivate each virtue, and let it take root firmly in the mind, the grace resulting from it, its natural exteriour mark, would soon strip affectation of its flaunting plumes" (007-036). Authentic virtue produces its own grace; affectation is merely the plumage of those who lack the substance.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Where conduct-book writers like Gregory and Fordyce prescribed elaborate codes of feminine behaviour as the path to social acceptance, Wollstonecraft identifies those very codes as the disease masquerading as the cure. The "starched rules of decorum" (005-080) that conduct literature promoted were, in her analysis, the machinery that manufactured affectation. Rousseau, whom she otherwise admired, contributed to the problem by idealising a feminine modesty that, stripped of rational foundation, became indistinguishable from coquetry.

## Key Passages

> This was Rousseau's opinion respecting men: I extend it to women, and confidently assert that they have been drawn out of their sphere by false refinement, and not by an endeavour to acquire masculine qualities. Still the regal homage which they receive is so intoxicating, that, till the manners of the times are changed, and formed on more reasonable principles, it may be impossible to convince them that the illegitimate power, which they obtain by degrading themselves, is a curse. (002-015)

> Women ought to endeavour to purify their hearts; but can they do so when their uncultivated understandings make them entirely dependent on their senses for employment and amusement, when no noble pursuit sets them above the little vanities of the day, or enables them to curb the wild emotions that agitate a reed over which every passing breeze has power? To gain the affections of a virtuous man, is affectation necessary? (002-048)

> A cultivated understanding, and an affectionate heart, will never want starched rules of decorum, something more substantial than seemliness will be the result; and, without understanding, the behaviour here recommended, would be rank affectation. Decorum, indeed, is the one thing needful! decorum is to supplant nature, and banish simplicity. (005-080)

> Dangerous, because they not only afford a plausible excuse to the voluptuary, who disguises sheer sensuality under a sentimental veil; but as they spread affectation, and take from the dignity of virtue. Virtue, as the very word imports, should have an appearance of seriousness, if not austerity. (004-092)

> Yet, if men were only anxious to cultivate each virtue, and let it take root firmly in the mind, the grace resulting from it, its natural exteriour mark, would soon strip affectation of its flaunting plumes; because, fallacious as unstable, is the conduct that is not founded on truth. (007-036)
