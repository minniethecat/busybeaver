# Perfection

Perfection occupies a foundational role in Wollstonecraft's moral theology and educational philosophy. She grounds her entire argument for women's rights in the conviction that human beings are created in the image of a perfect God and are therefore called to pursue their own perfection — a pursuit that requires the cultivation of reason and virtue, capacities she argues are common to both sexes.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft understands perfection primarily in two interrelated senses. Theologically, it refers to the divine perfection of God, which serves as the model toward which human nature aspires: "I build my belief on the perfection of God" (001-018). Anthropologically, it denotes the fullest possible development of human faculties — reason, virtue, and knowledge — which she terms "the perfection of our nature and capability of happiness" (001-006). She is careful to acknowledge that absolute perfection is unattainable in this life, describing humans as "advancing gradually toward perfection" (004-006), with the recognition that "perfection is found not to be within the reach of mortals" (005-124). Yet the pursuit itself is obligatory and ennobling.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument for women's education is anchored in her theology of perfection. If God is perfect, and human beings are created in God's image with the capacity for reason, then the cultivation of that reason toward perfection is a religious duty — one that applies equally to women and men. She contends that women "must be permitted to acquire human virtues (or perfections) by the SAME means as men" (003-006), for to deny them the means of improvement is to frustrate the divine purpose.

This theological grounding gives her feminism a distinctive character. She does not argue for women's rights on the basis of individual liberty alone but on the basis of a providential order that calls all rational beings to perfection. The failure to educate women is therefore not merely a social injustice but a violation of God's design. "Consequently the perfection of our nature and capability of happiness," she writes, "must be estimated by the degree of reason, virtue, and knowledge, that distinguish the individual, and direct the laws which bind society" (001-006).

She also uses the concept of perfection polemically against those who would sentimentalise women's innocence. True perfection requires struggle, effort, and the exercise of reason — not passive ignorance preserved under the name of modesty. As "we gradually discover the imperfections of our nature, we discover virtues" (005-121), and the honest acknowledgment of imperfection is itself a step toward improvement.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft's concept of perfection stands in sharp contrast to the Rousseauian ideal of woman as naturally perfect in her domestic role. For Rousseau, woman's "perfection" lies in her submission and her adaptation to man's desires. Wollstonecraft rejects this entirely: perfection cannot be achieved through subordination or the suppression of reason. She also distances herself from the sentimental view that women are morally superior by nature — perfection, in her framework, is earned through effort, not bestowed by nature or assigned by social role.

> Consequently the perfection of our nature and capability of happiness, must be estimated by the degree of reason, virtue, and knowledge, that distinguish the individual, and direct the laws which bind society. (001-006)

> I build my belief on the perfection of God. (001-018)

> [M]ust be permitted to acquire human virtues (or perfections) by the SAME means as men, or to be reduced to the condition of the brutes. (003-006)

> [A]dvancing gradually toward perfection. (004-006)

> [W]hen, in the lapse of time, perfection is found not to be within the reach of mortals, we are taught to look beyond the grave for it. (005-124)
