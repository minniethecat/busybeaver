Gravity, in Wollstonecraft's moral vocabulary, denotes a quality of serious, measured character — a steadiness of mind and purpose that she associates with rational self-governance. The term appears at critical junctures in her argument, functioning both as a description of proper moral deportment and as a metaphor drawn from natural philosophy that illuminates her understanding of social order.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft uses "gravity" in two distinct but related senses. The first is metaphorical, drawn from Newtonian natural philosophy: in the Dedication, she writes that "the common law of gravity prevailing, the sexes will fall into their proper places" (P02-019) when coercion is removed from society. Here gravity stands for a natural ordering principle — the idea that when artificial constraints are eliminated, men and women will gravitate toward their appropriate roles through the inherent logic of rational companionship rather than through force or convention.

The second sense is moral-psychological. In the Introduction, Wollstonecraft remarks that a woman "without it has naturally more gravity, some women govern their husbands" (P03-015), using gravity to describe a seriousness and steadiness of character that commands respect and, in some cases, authority. Elsewhere she warns that nature "has never given any specific gravity, neutrality ensues" (004-033), extending the metaphor to suggest that without a definite weight of character — a settled moral substance — a person drifts into moral indeterminacy.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's deployment of gravity is strategic: by borrowing the language of Newtonian science, she lends the authority of natural law to her argument for gender equality. If gravity is a universal principle that governs physical bodies without coercion, then a properly ordered society should allow social relations to be governed by analogous natural principles — reason, virtue, mutual respect — rather than by the artificial constraints of patriarchal custom.

The moral-psychological sense of gravity connects to her broader critique of feminine character. Women, educated to be pleasing rather than serious, lack the gravity that would command genuine respect. The frivolity that society cultivates in women is precisely the absence of gravity — a lightness of character that renders them unfit for the duties of citizenship, motherhood, and rational companionship. Restoring gravity to women's character requires, in Wollstonecraft's framework, the same remedy she prescribes throughout the Vindication: rigorous intellectual and moral education.

The concept also illuminates Wollstonecraft's view of marriage. The "common law of gravity" passage (P02-019) appears in the context of her argument that when men and women are educated equally, marriage will be based on mutual affection and respect rather than on coercion and dependence. Gravity thus functions as a principle of moral physics: in a properly ordered society, people will be drawn together by the weight of genuine virtue and compatibility, not pushed together by economic necessity or social pressure.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft's use of the gravity metaphor is characteristically Enlightenment in its appeal to natural law, but she deploys it to radical ends. Where conservative thinkers invoked natural law to justify existing hierarchies as divinely ordained, Wollstonecraft uses the same conceptual framework to argue that existing hierarchies — including gender hierarchy — are artificial distortions of nature's true order. The "common law of gravity" does not cement women's subordination; it guarantees that when artificial constraints are removed, the sexes will find their proper, complementary places.
