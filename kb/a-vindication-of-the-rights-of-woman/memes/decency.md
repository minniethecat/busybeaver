**Decency** in Wollstonecraft's usage operates at the boundary between genuine moral propriety and superficial social convention. She invokes "attention to decency" (P02-004) as something that even brutes instinctively observe, suggesting that decency, properly understood, is a natural moral sentiment rather than an artificial social code. Yet she is sharply critical of what she calls "insipid decency" (008-010) — the hollow propriety that arises when reputation, not principle, governs conduct.

The concept of decency is central to Wollstonecraft's analysis of modesty, female education, and the gendered double standard. Genuine decency flows from self-respect and the cultivation of virtue; false decency — the "insipid" variety — is manufactured by a social system that prizes women's reputation above their character. The distinction illuminates one of Wollstonecraft's most persistent themes: the corruption of moral language by a social order that substitutes appearance for reality.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's conception of decency is rooted in the natural law tradition, in which certain moral sentiments — modesty, shame, a sense of propriety — are understood as universal features of human nature rather than cultural inventions. "Attention to decency" (P02-004) is something that "brutes instinctively observe," placing decency on a continuum with natural modesty. The implication is that decency is not a specifically female virtue but a human one, and that the social codes that have made it a female burden are a distortion.

The crucial distinction in Wollstonecraft's treatment is between decency grounded in self-respect and decency grounded in the opinion of others. "Respect for the opinion of the world" (008-010) can "only produce a kind of insipid decency" — a decency without moral substance, maintained not because it is right but because it is observed. The adjective "insipid" is telling: insipid decency lacks flavour, character, life. It is the form of virtue without its content.

Wollstonecraft also distinguishes decency from modesty. "Why in the name of decency are sisters, female intimates, or ladies and their waiting women, to be kept in ignorance of these very things?" (007-024) The rhetorical question suggests that decency, properly understood, should not be invoked to justify ignorance. The "care which some modest women take" (007-025) is described as being "on the score of modesty, but decency" — indicating that decency and modesty, though related, are distinct moral categories.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Decency functions in Wollstonecraft's argument as both a standard of moral judgment and an object of critique. As a standard, decency represents the baseline of moral propriety that even a corrupt society cannot wholly extinguish. As an object of critique, the social construction of decency — particularly the gendered double standard — exposes the hypocrisy at the heart of eighteenth-century moral discourse.

The critique of insipid decency is a critique of reputation-based morality. When women are taught that their value resides in their reputation rather than their character, decency becomes a performance — a display of propriety that masks the absence of genuine virtue. "Respect for the opinion of the world has, however, been termed the principal duty of woman" (008-010) — and it is precisely this respect that produces insipid decency. The problem is not decency itself but its detachment from self-respect and its attachment to the judgment of others.

The gendered dimension is explicit. Wollstonecraft observes that men "have also lost a sense of decency in this respect; for they will not be restrained by any regard for decency from indulging their appetites" (008-024). The double standard — in which decency is demanded of women but not of men — corrupts decency itself, transforming it from a universal moral standard into a mechanism of control.

Wollstonecraft's treatment of midwifery illustrates the practical implications. "Midwifery, decency seems to allot to them" (009-030) — the observation that decency assigns certain professions to women is neither an endorsement nor a rejection of the assignment. Rather, it is a recognition that the social organisation of decency has material consequences for women's lives, shaping their access to employment, knowledge, and independence.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The eighteenth-century discourse of decency was shaped by the conduct-book tradition, in which decency was primarily a female virtue concerned with modesty of dress, speech, and behaviour. Writers such as James Fordyce and John Gregory treated decency as a quality that women must cultivate in order to secure male approval and protect their reputation. Decency, in this tradition, was a matter of compliance with social expectation.

Wollstonecraft's treatment is a radical departure. She does not reject decency — she seeks to restore its moral content. Genuine decency, for Wollstonecraft, is an expression of self-respect and rational principle, not of submission to social convention. The conduct-book writers had hollowed decency out, reducing it to a code of behaviour detached from moral character. Wollstonecraft's project is to reattach decency to its foundation in virtue.

The contrast with Rousseau is instructive. Rousseau, in *Émile*, treats female modesty and decency as natural expressions of sexual difference — women are naturally modest, and their decency is a function of their sex. Wollstonecraft rejects this naturalisation: decency is not a sex-specific trait but a moral quality available to all rational beings. The social construction of decency as female is precisely what she seeks to expose and dismantle.

## Key Passages

> There is an attention to decency which brutes instinctively observe. And I will venture to affirm, that a woman of sense and reflection will behave with more propriety than a man who has been only taught to please. (P02-004)

> Why in the name of decency are sisters, female intimates, or ladies and their waiting women, to be kept in ignorance of these very things? (007-024)

> Respect for the opinion of the world has, however, been termed the principal duty of woman — and it is this slavish regard for reputation that can only produce a kind of insipid decency. (008-010)
