# Alexander Pope

## Overview

Alexander Pope (1688–1744) was the dominant English poet of the early eighteenth century, and his couplets provided Wollstonecraft with a ready store of memorable formulations — some she approves, others she contests. Pope's *Moral Essays*, particularly the "Epistle to a Lady: Of the Characters of Women" (1735), offers a characterisation of women as mutable, contradictory, and essentially defined by the ruling passion of the moment — precisely the view Wollstonecraft seeks to dismantle.

Wollstonecraft quotes Pope both as an authority whose observations about human nature can be mobilised for her own argument, and as a representative of the poetic tradition that has naturalised women's supposed moral weakness. The double function reflects her larger strategy: she reads canonical authors against themselves, extracting truths they express despite their own assumptions.

## In the Text

Pope appears primarily as a source of poetic quotations that Wollstonecraft deploys in her analysis of female character. His famous line "Most women have no character at all" becomes, in her treatment, not a statement of natural fact but an indictment of the education that produces the characterlessness it then ridicules.

## Key Quotations

> "Most women have no character at all." (Pope, "Epistle to a Lady" — quoted by Wollstonecraft as evidence of the effect of bad education, not the fact of female nature)

## Biographical Summary

Alexander Pope (1688–1744) was the pre-eminent English poet of the Augustan age. His major works include *The Rape of the Lock* (1712), *An Essay on Man* (1733–1734), and *The Dunciad* (1728–1743). His *Moral Essays* (1731–1735), particularly the epistle on the characters of women, provided Wollstonecraft with a widely recognised characterisation of female nature that she sought to contest.
