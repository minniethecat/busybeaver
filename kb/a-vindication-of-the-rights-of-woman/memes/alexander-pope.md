# Alexander Pope

1688 – 1744. The dominant English poet of the early eighteenth century, whose satirical characterization of women as mutable and characterless is directly challenged by Wollstonecraft — and whose sarcasm she memorably turns back upon men themselves.

## Life

Pope was the preeminent poet of the Augustan age, known for his satirical verse, his translations of Homer, and his moral essays. Works such as *The Rape of the Lock*, *An Essay on Man*, and the *Moral Essays* established him as a master of the heroic couplet. His *Epistle to a Lady* (1735), containing the famous line "Most women have no characters at all," became a touchstone for eighteenth-century assumptions about female nature — and a direct target for Wollstonecraft's rebuttal. Pope's cultural authority was such that his pronouncements on women carried the weight of settled wisdom; to challenge Pope was to challenge the consensus of a literary century.

## Role in the Text

Wollstonecraft engages Pope's characterization of women at three distinct points in the *Vindication*, each time sharpening her critique.

### The Famous Rebuttal

Her opening engagement is the most direct: "Pope has said, in the name of the male sex, 'most women have no characters at all'" (002-037). She turns this observation against the men who made it: if women lack character, it is because their education, social position, and dependence have prevented them from developing any. The "characterlessness" Pope satirizes is, in Wollstonecraft's analysis, not a natural defect but an artificial production — the predictable result of denying women the conditions for character formation.

### Pope Among the Geniuses

She also references Pope in her discussion of literary genius, listing him alongside Cowley and Milton as examples of male poetic achievement — examples that make the exclusion of women from literary greatness seem suspicious rather than natural (004-073).

### Turning the Sarcasm Back

Wollstonecraft's most audacious move comes later in Chapter 4, where she does not merely rebut Pope but appropriates his weapon:

> "I have frequently, with full conviction, retorted Pope's sarcasm on them; or, to speak explicitly, it has appeared to me applicable to the whole human race. A love of pleasure or sway seems to divide mankind, and the husband who lords it in his little harem, thinks only of his pleasure or his convenience." (004-087)

The rhetorical manoeuvre is elegant: Pope's dismissal of women — that they are creatures of appetite without stable character — is, Wollstonecraft argues, *at least as true of men*. The husband in his "little harem," the libertine who marries for convenience, the man of pleasure who "thinks only of his pleasure" — these are Pope's "characterless" creatures in masculine form. What Pope attributed to female nature, Wollstonecraft redistributes across "the whole human race" as a function of power and circumstance, not sex.

### The Concession That Is Not One

In Chapter 6, Wollstonecraft deploys a different strategy — conditional concession — to neutralize Pope's authority:

> "And when all their ingenuity is called forth to adjust their dress, 'a passion for a scarlet coat,' is so natural, that it never surprised me; and, allowing Pope's summary of their character to be just, 'that every woman is at heart a rake,' why should they be bitterly censured for seeking a congenial mind, and preferring a rake to a man of sense?" (006-007)

The logic is devastating in its simplicity. *Even if* Pope were right — even if "every woman is at heart a rake" — the conclusion does not condemn women but the system that made them so. If women prefer rakes to men of sense, it is because "rakes know how to work on their sensibility" (006-008) while reasonable men have been taught to neglect the art of genuine connection. Pope's epigram, intended to expose female shallowness, becomes in Wollstonecraft's hands evidence of male failure — the failure to cultivate in women the rational faculties that would make superficial attraction unnecessary.

### Works Referenced

- *Moral Essays, Epistle II: To a Lady* (1735) — the source of "most women have no characters at all"
- *An Essay on Man* (1734) — philosophical poem on human nature

## Key Ideas

Pope's characterization of women as essentially mutable — "matter too soft a lasting mark to bear" — was one of the most culturally influential formulations of eighteenth-century assumptions about female nature. Wollstonecraft's rebuttal operates on three levels: she denies the naturalness of the character Pope describes, she demonstrates that the same "characterlessness" afflicts men of pleasure, and she argues that even if Pope's description were accurate, it would indict the education that produces it rather than the women who exhibit it. In this, Pope becomes more than a target — he becomes a case study in how literary authority can harden into social dogma, and how a determined critic can turn the master's tools against the master's house.
