# Pleasure

## Definition

Wollstonecraft does not condemn **pleasure** as such; she condemns the
*substitution* of pleasure — particularly sensual and social pleasure — for the
higher satisfactions of reason, virtue, and the performance of duty. Her
critique targets the education that trains women to be "pleasing" rather than
good, to seek the transient gratification of admiration rather than the durable
contentment of self-respect.

## Sensual vs. Rational Pleasure

A key distinction in the *Vindication* is between pleasures of the senses
(including the pleasures of vanity and social admiration) and the pleasures
of reason. The former are transient and dependent on external circumstances;
the latter are enduring and arise from within:

The woman educated only to please "has no resource" when her beauty fades or
when she is alone — her pleasures depend entirely on the attention of others.
The woman of cultivated mind, by contrast, possesses internal resources —
the pleasures of reading, thought, and rational conversation — that do not
diminish with age.

## Pleasure and Virtue

Wollstonecraft rejects the hedonistic reduction of virtue to pleasure. It is
not enough that an action produces pleasure; it must be grounded in rational
principle. Indeed, the pursuit of pleasure as an end in itself is precisely
what produces the vices Wollstonecraft attributes to both sexes: dissipation
in women, and sensuality in men.

True happiness — as distinct from mere pleasure — arises from the exercise
of the rational and moral faculties in the performance of duty. This is the
classical and Christian conception of eudaimonia that Wollstonecraft inherits
and applies to her argument for women's education.

## The Critique of "Pleasing"

The demand that women be "pleasing" is Wollstonecraft's specific target.
Women are educated not to think, not to act, not to govern — but to please:
to please fathers, husbands, and society through beauty, charm, and
accomplishment. This education for pleasing renders women incapable of the
very virtues — fortitude, integrity, independence — that would make them
genuinely good wives, mothers, and citizens.
