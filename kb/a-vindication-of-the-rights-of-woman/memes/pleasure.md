**Pleasure** in Wollstonecraft's argument is a diagnostic category: the kind of pleasure a person pursues reveals the state of their character. She distinguishes sharply between "enervating pleasure" (004-050) — the restless pursuit of sensation that defines fashionable female life — and "the sober pleasures that arise from equality" (004-013). The opposition is not between pleasure and virtue but between pleasure that debases and pleasure that elevates.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft defines enervating pleasure by its consequences: it exhausts without satisfying, demands constant novelty, and renders the mind incapable of sustained thought. "The prevalent fondness for pleasure, which takes place of ambition and those noble passions" (P03-013) — the verb "takes place of" indicates that the taste for pleasure is not merely a harmless preference but an active displacement of worthier pursuits. Pleasure, when made the chief end of life, crowds out ambition, virtue, and public spirit.

The mechanism of enervation is physiological and psychological. "Pleasure—enervating pleasure is, likewise, within woman's reach" (004-050) she writes, the dash emphasizing the qualifier: it is specifically enervating pleasure that corrupts, not pleasure as such. The heart "accustomed to lively emotions" (004-050) loses its capacity for "the calm emotions produced by reason" (004-051). Pleasure thus operates as a kind of addiction: the more one pursues it, the less satisfaction it yields, and the more one needs.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's critique of pleasure is inseparable from her critique of female education. Women are raised to "dream life away in the lap of pleasure, or in the languor of weariness" (002-050). The phrase "lap of pleasure" evokes both infancy and sensuality — women are infantilized by being taught that pleasure is their proper sphere. The alternative, "the languor of weariness," is the inevitable sequel: a life organized around pleasure alternates between overstimulation and boredom, never achieving the steady contentment of purposeful activity.

She argues that the pursuit of pleasure as an end destroys the capacity for genuine happiness. "Reflection shut out by pleasure! Surely it is madness to make the fate of a fellow-creature depend on such a precarious foundation" (001-024). The exclamation mark after "pleasure" registers her astonishment that society has organized women's entire existence around something so unstable. Pleasure is "precarious" because it depends on external circumstance; virtue, by contrast, is self-sustaining.

The structural consequence of the pleasure system is the corruption of marriage and domestic life. "Men will seek for pleasure in variety, and faithless husbands will make faithless wives" (P02-017). When pleasure is the bond between the sexes, that bond dissolves the moment pleasure fades. Wollstonecraft's alternative is "the sober pleasures that arise from equality" (004-013) — pleasures that are the by-product, not the goal, of a life ordered by duty and mutual respect. "True pleasure is the reward of labour" (004-049), she insists, redefining pleasure as the natural consequence of virtuous exertion rather than its competitor.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The hedonistic calculus of eighteenth-century moral philosophy — from Hobbes through Helvétius — made pleasure the sovereign motive of human action. Wollstonecraft does not reject the role of pleasure entirely but reorders it: pleasure is a legitimate reward, never a legitimate goal. This distinguishes her from both the pleasure-as-vice tradition of ascetic Christianity and the pleasure-as-sovereign-good tradition of radical Enlightenment thought. Her position is closest to the classical virtue-ethics tradition, where pleasure is the natural accompaniment of virtuous activity, not its motive.
