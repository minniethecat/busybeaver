# Abject

A condition of moral and social degradation that Wollstonecraft identifies as the inevitable consequence of unequal power relations — particularly those between men and women, masters and dependents, and tyrants and subjects. The term appears throughout the text to describe both the psychological state of the oppressed and the structural character of oppressive institutions.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft uses "abject" to describe a state of profound subordination that degrades both the oppressor and the oppressed. In the Dedication, she observes that unequal power relations end up "degrading the master and the abject dependent" (P02-014), establishing from the outset that the abject condition is not a natural state but a product of social arrangements. The word functions in her lexicon as the opposite of the rational independence she advocates: where reason elevates, abject dependence debases.

The abject condition manifests in multiple domains. It appears in the economic sphere as "abject poverty" (009-008), in the political realm as "abject slavery" (012-010), and in the psychological dimension as "abject fear" (005-020). In each case, Wollstonecraft traces the condition back to a denial of the rights and education that would allow individuals to develop their rational faculties. The abject person is one who has been systematically prevented from exercising independent judgment.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument positions the abject condition as the direct result of denying women equal education and civil rights. When women are reared to value only their physical charms and to depend entirely on male approval, they become psychologically incapable of standing on their own rational footing. This creates a binary trap: "they become either abject slaves or capricious tyrants" (003-029). The alternative to abjection, she warns, is not genuine freedom but a different pathology — the abuse of power by those who have never learned to exercise it justly.

The inherited nature of this degradation is a central concern. Wollstonecraft asks pointedly whether women can be expected to act virtuously when they are "the freed posterity of abject slaves" (004-101). The legacy of subordination does not vanish when formal constraints are lifted; it persists in the habits of mind and character formed under unjust conditions. This argument connects her critique of gender relations to a broader analysis of how social institutions shape individual psychology across generations.

Wollstonecraft extends the analysis to romantic relationships, observing that women who have been taught to value only male attention "become abject wooers, and fond slaves" (006-017) when their lovers' interest wanes. The romantic realm, far from being a refuge from power dynamics, is where the abject condition often reaches its most painful expression. Women who lack independent purpose become emotionally dependent to the point of self-abasement.

The remedy, consistent with her broader argument, is education that cultivates reason and independence. Wollstonecraft counsels women to avoid both extremes of the abject-tyrant binary, "endeavouring to act reasonably" rather than adopting "insolent airs, nor afterward abjectly submit" (005-106). True dignity requires neither domination nor submission but the steady exercise of a mind accustomed to governing itself by rational principles.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Rousseau, in *Émile*, had argued that women's dependent condition was natural and even desirable — that woman was "made specially to please man" and that her submission was part of the natural order. Wollstonecraft directly challenges this framing: what Rousseau calls nature, she calls the product of "the system of tyranny and abject slavery which is established" (012-010). The abject condition is not woman's natural state but the result of systematic denial of education and civil standing.

Where Rousseau saw feminine submission as graceful, Wollstonecraft sees it as psychologically destructive. "Abject fear always inspires contempt" (005-020), she writes — the master may demand submission, but he cannot respect the one who submits. The dynamic degrades both parties, a point Wollstonecraft makes more forcefully than any of her contemporaries. Even liberal writers who advocated improved education for women rarely traced the psychological consequences of subordination with her precision.

## Key Passages

> Women, it is true, obtaining power by unjust means, by practising or fostering vice, evidently lose the rank which reason would assign them, and they become either abject slaves or capricious tyrants. (003-029)

> The being who patiently endures injustice, and silently bears insults, will soon become unjust, or unable to discern right from wrong. Besides, I deny the fact, this is not the true way to form or meliorate the temper; for, as a sex, men have better tempers than women, because they are occupied by pursuits which interest the head as well as the heart; and the steadiness of the head gives a healthy temperature to the heart. Abject fear always inspires contempt; and if you wish to be respected always act reasonably. (005-020)

> Raised sufficiently above abject poverty not to be obliged to weigh the consequence of every farthing they spend, they have only to think of their own comfort and those of their families. (009-008)

> A slavish bondage to parents cramps every faculty of the mind... This strict hand may in some instances produce a degree of mechanick kindness, but it more frequently renders the tempers imperious or abject. (011-014)
