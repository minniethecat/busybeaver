Servants, in Wollstonecraft's analysis, function as more than a description of domestic labour relations. She deploys the master-servant relationship as a structural analogy for understanding the condition of women under patriarchal authority, and simultaneously as a case study in how arbitrary power — even at the scale of the household — corrupts those who wield it and degrades those subjected to it.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft treats the figure of the servant as embodying a particular form of unfreedom: subordination without the governance of fixed rules or principles. She observes that those subjected to arbitrary authority "will, having no fixed rules to square their conduct by, act tyrannically when they have the power" (003-043). The servant is not merely an economic subordinate but a moral category — a person whose character has been shaped by subjection to capricious power. This analysis operates on two levels: the literal treatment of domestic servants in eighteenth-century households, and the structural parallel between servants and women, both groups being subject to the arbitrary will of a master.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's most incisive move is to draw an explicit structural parallel between servants and women. In a striking passage, she describes how a man "lets her, to preserve his own peace, scold the servants, and go to church in clothes made of the very best materials" (004-059), reducing the wife to the status of a superior servant — trusted with household management but denied genuine equality. The analogy cuts deeper: just as servants are trained to obey rather than to reason, women are educated to please rather than to think.

The moral psychology of power is central to her analysis. She observes how children "are allowed to tyrannize over servants" (012-007) and how this early experience of arbitrary authority cultivates a despotic character. The progression is damning: "the transition, as they grow up, from barbarity to brutes to domestic tyranny over wives, children, and servants, is very easy" (012-070). Wollstonecraft traces a direct causal chain from the petty tyranny exercised over servants to the larger structures of domestic oppression.

The educational dimension is equally significant. Wollstonecraft critiques "the manner in which they treat servants in the presence of children, permitting them to suppose, that they ought to wait on them, and bear their humours" (013-064). This observation reveals her concern with how class hierarchy is reproduced through the modelling of behaviour: children who learn to treat servants as instruments of their will grow into adults who treat subordinates — including wives — with the same despotic expectation. Most pointedly, she criticises mothers who "leave their children entirely to the care of servants" (013-065), arguing that this abdication of maternal responsibility damages both the children's moral formation and the mother's own character.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Where eighteenth-century conduct literature typically addressed the management of servants as a matter of domestic economy, Wollstonecraft reframes it as a moral and political question. Her analysis anticipates later critiques of domestic labour as a site of unrecognised power relations. The parallel between servants and women — both governed by arbitrary will rather than by fixed principles of justice — is distinctively Wollstonecraftian, connecting the micro-politics of the household to the macro-politics of gender relations in a way that few of her contemporaries attempted.
