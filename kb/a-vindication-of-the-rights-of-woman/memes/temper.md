In Wollstonecraft's moral psychology, **temper** denotes the settled disposition of mind — not a fixed natural endowment but a quality formed through education, habit, and the exercise of reason. When she criticises the "sweetness of temper" expected of women (005-018), she is objecting not to gentleness itself but to the substitution of a manufactured, submissive disposition for genuine virtue.

Temper, properly understood, is the product of "attention to a child as will slowly sharpen the senses, form the temper, regulate the passions" (002-012). It is an achievement of education, not a gift of nature, and the distinction is central to Wollstonecraft's argument that women's character is shaped by their social situation rather than by innate sexual difference.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft distinguishes between temper as a formed moral disposition and mere "temperature" — the transient fluctuations of feeling that she regards as a poor substitute for genuine character. She observes that "when love to subside, a healthy temperature is thought insipid" (002-056), critiquing the romantic idealisation of emotional volatility, particularly in women.

The concept carries both a descriptive and a normative dimension. Descriptively, temper names the habitual cast of a person's emotional and moral responses — what she calls "the original temperature of the mind" (006-002). Normatively, a good temper is one that has been brought under the governance of reason, producing that "habitual cheerfulness, termed good humour" which she acknowledges as valuable while noting it is "seldom united with great mental powers" (004-070).

Crucially, Wollstonecraft insists that the formation of temper is an educational task. It is not enough to be born with a fortunate disposition; temper must be "meliorated" — improved — through deliberate cultivation. The phrase "true melioration of temper" (002-069) captures her conviction that character is made, not found.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's treatment of temper is inseparable from her critique of women's education. She argues that the conventional cultivation of "softness of temper" and "outward obedience" (002-003) in girls produces not virtue but a "scrupulous slavery" to opinion. What is praised as sweetness of temper is, on inspection, the docility of a being "formed to obey a being so imperfect" (005-018).

The argument takes an explicitly political turn when she contrasts the temper formed by rational education with that produced by the existing system. A woman educated to think will develop a temper characterised by "habitual cheerfulness" grounded in understanding; a woman educated only to please will acquire a temper of "cunning" and "softness" — the "outward obedience" that masks inward resentment.

Education, in Wollstonecraft's account, is literally the "way to form or meliorate the temper" (005-020). She denies that women's allegedly "sweet" temper is natural, attributing it instead to the disciplinary regime that "spoils a child's temper" (004-069) by alternately indulging and coercing. The management of temper is thus a microcosm of her broader argument: what appears to be natural female character is the artifact of a defective education.

Her reference to "intemperate" love of pleasure (004-087) and the "intemperate and contemptuous" character of the biographical sketch subject's father (P01-012) further illustrates the moral weight she assigns to temper. Intemperance, in her vocabulary, is not merely excess but the failure of rational self-governance — the mark of a mind "not restrained by mechanical laws" (003-041).

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft's treatment of temper directly challenges the eighteenth-century commonplace, articulated most influentially by Rousseau, that women possess a natural "sweetness of temper" fitting them for domestic subordination.

Rousseau's Sophie is educated to cultivate a temper that pleases and accommodates; Wollstonecraft's rational woman is educated to cultivate a temper that judges and acts. For Wollstonecraft, the conventional praise of feminine sweetness is a "civil term for weakness" — a phrase she uses of "innocence" (002-006) that applies with equal force to the idealisation of women's submissive temper.

She also contests the medicalised discourse of her period, which treated temper as a matter of humoral "temperature" determined by the body. By insisting that temper is formed through education, she relocates it from physiology to moral psychology, opening the door to the claim that women's temper can and should be improved by the same rational means applied to men.

## Key Passages

> By individual education, I mean — for the sense of the word is not precisely defined — such an attention to a child as will slowly sharpen the senses, form the temper, regulate the passions, as they begin to ferment. (002-012)

> Minds of this rare species see things too much in masses, and seldom, if ever, have a good temper. That habitual cheerfulness, termed good humour, is, perhaps, as seldom united with great mental powers. (004-070)

> Mankind seem to agree, that children should be left under the management of women during their childhood. Now, from all the observation that I have been able to make, women of sensibility are the most unfit to manage a child, because they are perpetually inclined to indulge, and they spoil a child's temper. The management of the temper, the first, and most important branch of education, requires the steady, sober eye of reason. (004-069)

> This is not the true way to form or meliorate the temper; for, as a sex, men have better tempers than women, because they are employed from their infancy in situations that call forth the energies of the mind, whilst women, taught to observe, rather than to act, and always to look up to man for a guide, acquire justness of sentiment, but not strength of character. (005-020)
