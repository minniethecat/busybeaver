## Overview

Stéphanie Félicité, Comtesse de Genlis (1746–1830) was a prolific French writer and educator who served as governess to the children of the Duke of Orléans. She wrote extensively on education and produced moral tales for children. Wollstonecraft acknowledges her work in _A Vindication of the Rights of Woman_ but finds her approach fundamentally limited.

## Role in A Vindication of the Rights of Woman

Wollstonecraft mentions Madame de Genlis in the closing pages of the work, in Chapter V. She acknowledges that "Madame Genlis has written several entertaining books for children; and her letters on Education afford many useful hints, that sensible parents will certainly avail themselves of" [005-124]. However, she immediately qualifies this praise: "but her views are narrow, and her prejudices as unreasonable as strong" [005-124].

This brief characterization places Genlis in the category of writers who, while well-intentioned and practically useful, fail to grasp the foundational principles Wollstonecraft argues for — that women's education must be grounded in reason and aimed at independence, not mere accomplishment or social utility. Genlis's works, like the conduct books of Gregory and Fordyce, offer practical guidance without questioning the hierarchical structure that limits women's development.

## Significance

Genlis serves as a minor but telling figure in Wollstonecraft's argument. By acknowledging the practical value of Genlis's educational writings while rejecting their philosophical premises, Wollstonecraft models her method: engage seriously with existing work on education, then push beyond it toward first principles. Genlis represents the best of the existing paradigm — and its inherent inadequacy.
