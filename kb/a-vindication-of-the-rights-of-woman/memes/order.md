**Order** is one of the most important and most complex concepts in Wollstonecraft's moral and political vocabulary. Throughout the *Vindication*, she deploys "order" in multiple, sometimes conflicting, senses: the divine order of creation, the natural order that reason discerns, the social order that custom has established, and the moral order that virtue both presupposes and sustains. The tension among these senses drives much of the argument: the existing "order of society" (002-080) is not, as its defenders claim, a reflection of the natural order or the divine order; it is a distortion of both, and the task of reform is to bring the social order into conformity with the order that reason and revelation alike attest.

Wollstonecraft's treatment of order is at once conservative and radical. It is conservative in its insistence that order is real — that there is a divinely ordained structure to creation that human beings do not invent but discover. It is radical in its insistence that the existing social order is not that order — that the subordination of women, far from being ordained by God or nature, is a "disregard of order" (002-017) that defies both. "To do every thing in an orderly manner, is a most important precept" (002-018) — and the society that denies women education, independence, and the exercise of reason is doing everything in a disorderly manner.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft inherits her concept of order from multiple sources: the classical tradition of *kosmos* as the beautifully ordered whole of creation; the Christian tradition of the divine ordering of the universe, in which every being has its proper place and purpose; and the Enlightenment tradition of natural order, discernible by reason and expressible in laws that apply universally.

The divine order is the most fundamental. "Instead of disturbing, the order of creation" (003-041) — the task of human beings is not to create order but to discover and conform to the order that God has established. This order is characterised by harmony, proportion, and the subordination of lower to higher: reason governs passion, virtue governs appetite, and justice governs power. When any element of creation is out of its proper place — when passion governs reason, when appetite governs virtue, when power governs justice — the result is disorder, and disorder is the source of all human misery.

The natural order is the order that reason discerns in creation. "The order of nature" (004-049) is not a metaphor but a reality — the reality that every being has a nature, and that flourishing consists in acting in accordance with that nature. Women, as rational beings created in the image of God, have a nature that includes the capacity for reason, virtue, and self-governance. A social order that denies them the exercise of these capacities is "reversing the order of nature" (004-049) — not conforming to nature but defying it.

The social order, by contrast, is the order that human beings have actually constructed — and it is, in Wollstonecraft's analysis, a disorder masquerading as order. "The order of society, as it is at present constituted, renders woman a weak, dependent creature" (002-080). The social order is not the natural order; it is a human artefact that has been naturalised by custom, and the task of the reformer is to expose the gap between what is and what ought to be.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument about order is both diagnostic and prescriptive. Diagnostically, she shows that the existing "order" of gender relations is not an order at all but a disorder — a violation of the principles that ought to govern relations between rational beings. Prescriptively, she argues for the reformation of the social order in accordance with the natural and divine order.

The diagnostic argument begins with the claim that women's subordination is not natural. "I do not wish to invert the order of things" (002-035) — the disclaimer is important because it establishes that Wollstonecraft is not a revolutionary who wishes to destroy all order but a reformer who wishes to restore true order. The "order of things" that she wishes to preserve is the order in which reason governs passion, virtue governs appetite, and justice governs power. The "order" that she wishes to overturn is the merely conventional order that subordinates women to men.

The prescriptive argument identifies "order" as the distinguishing characteristic of virtue. "To give an example of order, the soul of virtue" (004-066) — the phrase is revealing. Order is not merely the arrangement of things but the "soul of virtue" itself: the principle that gives virtue its coherence and its beauty. A virtuous person is an ordered person — a person in whom reason governs passion and principle governs conduct. A virtuous society is an ordered society — a society in which every member is enabled to develop the faculties that their nature includes.

The connection to education is direct. "The cultivation of the understanding" is the means by which order is established in the individual soul; the education of both sexes is the means by which order is established in society. When women are denied education, the result is not merely individual suffering but social disorder — the corruption of marriage, the degradation of citizenship, and the "disregard of order" that Wollstonecraft discerns throughout the society she anatomises.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The eighteenth-century discourse of order was dominated by the Burkean appeal to prescription and inheritance — the claim that the existing order is legitimate because it has endured. Burke's *Reflections on the Revolution in France* (1790) is the classic statement of this position, and Wollstonecraft's response in the *Vindication of the Rights of Men* (1790) and the *Vindication of the Rights of Woman* (1792) is a sustained critique of it. The antiquity of an abuse does not make it less abusive; the fact that women have always been subordinated does not make their subordination just.

The radical Enlightenment — particularly Tom Paine and William Godwin — treated order as something to be constructed by reason on the ruins of inherited institutions. Wollstonecraft is more cautious: order is not constructed but discovered, and the reformer's task is not to invent a new order but to bring the social order into conformity with the natural and divine order that already exists.

Rousseau's treatment of order — particularly the "order of nature" that he invokes to justify Sophie's subordination to Émile — is a persistent target. Wollstonecraft's response is that Rousseau has mistaken convention for nature: the "order" he describes is merely the order of male convenience, and his appeal to nature is an exercise in circular reasoning that finds in nature what he has already placed there.

## Key Passages

> To give an example of order, the soul of virtue, some austere religionists have practised self-denial; but what does this prove? (004-066)

> Instead of disturbing, the order of creation, when, co-operating with the Supreme Being, they labour to reform themselves, they will labour to reform the world. (003-041)

> Nay, the order of society, as it is at present constituted, renders woman a weak, dependent creature. (002-080)

> To do every thing in an orderly manner, is a most important precept, which women, who, generally speaking, receive only a disorderly kind of education, seldom attend to with that degree of exactness that men, who from their infancy are broken into method, can boast of. (002-018)
