**Innocence** is, in Wollstonecraft's argument, one of the most dangerous words in the vocabulary of female education. Under the "specious name of innocence" (002-002), women are deliberately kept in ignorance — denied the knowledge, the experience, and the intellectual cultivation that would enable them to develop genuine virtue. The *Vindication*'s treatment of innocence is therefore an exercise in conceptual reclamation: Wollstonecraft insists that true innocence belongs to childhood alone and that for adults to claim it is to confess that they have never grown up. What is offered to women as a virtue is in fact "ignorance ... courteously termed innocence" (003-024) — and the courtesy conceals a systematic denial of moral agency.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft distinguishes between two senses of innocence that the eighteenth-century discourse of female virtue had systematically conflated. The first is the innocence proper to childhood — the "sportiveness of innocence" (013-043) that is the natural condition of beings who have not yet developed the capacity for moral reflection. This innocence is genuine but transient: it belongs to a stage of development and is properly left behind as the child matures into a morally responsible adult.

The second is the "innocence" that women are instructed to preserve — which is in fact ignorance, "damped by inactivity" (003-020) and sustained by the systematic denial of experience. This is not a moral achievement but a condition of arrested development. Women who have been "taught to look up to man for a relaxation from the discharge of the duties of life" have not been made innocent — they have been made dependent, and their dependence is then misdescribed as moral purity.

The crucial test is whether the state described as innocence can survive contact with experience. True virtue — the consistent disposition to act rightly — is tested and strengthened by experience; it grows through the exercise of judgment in challenging circumstances. The "innocence" that requires protection from experience is not virtue but its counterfeit — a fragile state that "will never grow to the wild luxuriancy of health" (003-020) because it has been "damped" and "tainted by false shame" (003-020) before it could develop.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

The argument unfolds across the *Vindication* in a series of increasingly pointed formulations. In the Introduction, Wollstonecraft frames the problem with characteristic directness: has women's condition been improved by "the homage paid to their beauty and their innocence"? Her answer is unequivocal: "have they gained any virtues in exchange for innocence, equivalent to the misery produced by the system of gallantry?" (001-011). The question is rhetorical — the answer is clearly no — but its structure reveals the argument's architecture. Innocence is not a virtue; it is something women have been given in exchange for virtue, and the exchange has been a bad one.

The second chapter sharpens the critique. Wollstonecraft asks whether women are to be "kept in ignorance under the specious name of innocence" (002-002), and the question is really an accusation. The adjective "specious" — deceptively attractive, plausible but wrong — does the work of the entire argument. What the culture calls innocence is in fact a strategy of control: keep women ignorant and call their ignorance a virtue, and they will not notice that they have been denied the education that would make them free.

The language of dampening and tainting is carefully chosen. "Their youth, and all their waking hours, being spent in acquiring accomplishments, their understandings have been damped by inactivity, or innocence tainted by false shame" (003-020). Inactivity dampens — it suppresses the natural development of the understanding, leaving it weak and undeveloped. False shame taints — it corrupts innocence from within, replacing genuine moral sensibility with a prudishness that mistakes concealment for purity. The combined effect is to produce a being who is neither genuinely innocent (for true innocence is incompatible with the knowingness that false shame implies) nor genuinely virtuous (for virtue requires the exercise of faculties that have been systematically suppressed).

The political consequences are clear. "In order to preserve their innocence, as ignorance is courteously termed" (003-024), women are denied the education that would enable them to participate in public life, to reason about their own interests, and to exercise the rights of citizenship. The "innocence" that is claimed as women's special possession is in fact the mechanism of their subjection.

The physiological and psychological consequences are no less devastating. Women who have been "immured in their families, groping in the dark" (012-073) are not innocent but stunted — their natural capacities for thought and action have never been allowed to develop. Wollstonecraft's alternative is not the abandonment of moral restraint but its transformation: "nor her innocence debauched by knowledge" (012-073) is the false alternative that the champions of female ignorance present. Knowledge does not corrupt; ignorance does. True moral purity is not the absence of experience but the integrity of judgment that experience informed by reason makes possible.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The eighteenth-century discourse of female virtue was saturated with the language of innocence. Conduct books, sermons, and novels alike instructed women that their chief virtue was to preserve their innocence — which meant, in practice, to avoid knowledge of the world, to cultivate a demure ignorance, and to depend on male protection for their moral safety. The ideal woman, in this tradition, was the woman who knew nothing and was therefore "pure."

Wollstonecraft's response is to expose the logic of this discourse. The "established opinion of the innocence of his manners will often lead" (008-013) the libertine to prey on the very innocence he claims to respect — the point being that the cult of female innocence is not a protection against male predation but an invitation to it. A woman who has been taught nothing of the world is defenceless in it; her "innocence" is not a shield but a target.

Rousseau again provides the philosophical framework that Wollstonecraft dismantles. In *Émile*, Sophie is educated to be innocent — which is to say, to be ignorant, dependent, and pleasing. Wollstonecraft's counter-education would produce a woman whose moral character is not the fragile product of protected ignorance but the robust achievement of exercised reason. "It is something nobler than innocence" (007-005), she writes of genuine modesty — and the claim applies equally to virtue. What women need is not the preservation of childhood but the acquisition of moral adulthood.

## Key Passages

> I wish to know whether they have gained any virtues in exchange for innocence, equivalent to the misery produced by the system of gallantry? (001-011)

> How grossly do they insult us who thus advise us only to render ourselves gentle, domestic brutes! For instance, the winning softness, so warmly and frequently recommended, that governs by obeying. What childish expressions, and how insignificant is the being — can it be an immortal one? — who will condescend to govern by such despicable means! (002-002)

> Their youth, and all their waking hours, being spent in acquiring accomplishments, their understandings have been damped by inactivity, or innocence tainted by false shame, will never grow to the wild luxuriancy of health. (003-020)

> To preserve personal beauty, woman's glory! the limbs and faculties are cramped with worse than Chinese bands, and the sedentary life which they are condemned to live, whilst boys frolic in the open air, weakens the muscles and relaxes the nerves. (003-024)

> It is something nobler than innocence; it is the delicacy of reflection, and not the coyness of ignorance. (007-005)
