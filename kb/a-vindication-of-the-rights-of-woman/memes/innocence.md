## Definition

Wollstonecraft mounts a sustained critique of "innocence" as it was applied to women in the eighteenth century. Far from being a [[virtue]], she argues, the innocence expected of women is "but a civil term for weakness" [002-006] — a euphemism that masks the deliberate cultivation of ignorance and dependence. "Women are told from their infancy," she writes, "that a little [[knowledge]] of human weakness, justly termed cunning, softness of temper, [[obedience|outward obedience]], and a scrupulous attention to a puerile kind of propriety, will obtain for them the protection of man" [002-003]. Innocence, in this analysis, is not a [[nature|natural]] state but an artificial production, imposed on women to render them manageable.

Wollstonecraft draws a sharp distinction between the innocence appropriate to children — who have not yet developed [[reason]] — and the "innocence" expected of adult women, which she regards as infantilizing and degrading. "Children, I grant, should be innocent; but when the epithet is applied to men, or women, it is but a civil term for weakness" [002-006]. The problem is not innocence itself but its weaponization as a tool of oppression: "why should they be kept in ignorance under the specious name of innocence?" [002-003].

## In Context

The critique of innocence appears primarily in the early chapters where Wollstonecraft establishes her philosophical framework. In Chapter II, she challenges the prevailing opinion that women should be innocent rather than [[knowledge]]able: "Men complain, and with [[reason]], of the follies and caprices of our sex... Behold, I should answer, the [[nature|natural]] effect of ignorance!" [002-003]. She connects the ideal of female innocence to a broader project of keeping women in "a state of childhood" [002-005], directly linking [[rousseau|Rousseau]]'s [[education]]al philosophy to this pattern.

In Chapter III, Wollstonecraft extends the critique by examining how the [[education]] system enforces innocence at the expense of genuine [[knowledge|understanding]], and in Chapter V she explores the consequences: women kept innocent — that is, ignorant — become incapable of the rational [[friendship]] that should form the basis of marriage.

## Key Passages

> "If then women are not a swarm of ephemeron triflers, why should they be kept in ignorance under the specious name of innocence?" [002-003]

> "Children, I grant, should be innocent; but when the epithet is applied to men, or women, it is but a civil term for weakness." [002-006]

> "Men, indeed, appear to me to act in a very unphilosophical manner, when they try to secure the good conduct of women by attempting to keep them always in a state of childhood." [002-005]

## Related Concepts

- [[education]] — the false [[education]] of women aims at preserving innocence rather than cultivating [[knowledge|understanding]]
- [[ignorance]] — Wollstonecraft repeatedly equates the ideal of female innocence with enforced ignorance
- [[modesty]] — like innocence, [[modesty]] is often invoked as a specious justification for constraint
- [[cunning]] — women kept in a state of "innocence" develop cunning as their only form of agency
