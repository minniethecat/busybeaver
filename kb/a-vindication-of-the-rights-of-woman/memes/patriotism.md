Patriotism, in Wollstonecraft's political philosophy, is not flag-waving sentiment but a rational civic virtue. "The true principle of patriotism" (P02-007) is grounded in the love of mankind and can only flourish when women — as mothers and citizens — are educated to understand their moral and civil duties.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft distinguishes between genuine and counterfeit patriotism.

The latter is "mock patriotism" (001-011) — the use of "liberty" as "a convenient handle" by ambitious men who seek power while oppressing others.

Genuine patriotism, by contrast, is "the pure flame of patriotism" (P02-005) that impels citizens to "labour to improve the morals of their fellow-citizens." It is not a sentiment but a practical commitment to the common good.

Genuine patriotism is inseparable from the "love of mankind, from which an orderly train of virtues spring" (P02-007). Crucially, it requires knowledge: one cannot be a patriot without understanding "the moral and civil interest of mankind" (P02-007).

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's treatment of patriotism is revolutionary in its gendered implications.

The Dedication to Talleyrand-Périgord makes an argument simultaneously about patriotism and women's education. If "children are to be educated to understand the true principle of patriotism, their mother must be a patriot" (P02-007).

This syllogism has radical consequences: women cannot be patriots unless they are educated; children cannot be patriots unless their mothers are; therefore the nation cannot have patriotic citizens unless women receive rational education.

The argument also redefines patriotism as a domestic as well as public virtue. The mother who educates her children in civic duty performs an act of patriotism no less significant than the soldier or the legislator.

In Chapter 1, she deploys "mock patriotism" (001-011) to expose the hypocrisy of male republican discourse. Men who invoke "liberty" while keeping women in "splendid slavery" are not patriots but self-interested actors.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft's patriotism contrasts with Rousseau's civic republicanism in a crucial respect.

Rousseau's patriotism is martial and exclusively masculine: women contribute by bearing and raising male citizens but are not themselves citizens. Wollstonecraft, by insisting that "their mother must be a patriot" (P02-007), grants women the same civic subjectivity as men — they must understand, not merely feel, their patriotic duty.

## Key Passages

> If the pure flame of patriotism have reached their bosoms, they should labour to improve the morals of their fellow-citizens, by teaching men, not only to respect modesty in women, but to acquire it themselves, as the only way to merit their esteem. (P02-005)

> If children are to be educated to understand the true principle of patriotism, their mother must be a patriot; and the love of mankind, from which an orderly train of virtues spring, can only be produced by considering the moral and civil interest of mankind; but the education and situation of woman, at present, shuts her out from such investigations. (P02-007)

> The desire of dazzling by riches, the most certain pre-eminence that man can obtain, the pleasure of commanding flattering sycophants, and many other complicated low calculations of doting self-love, have all contributed to overwhelm the mass of mankind, and make liberty a convenient handle for mock patriotism. (001-011)
