# Learning

In Wollstonecraft's educational philosophy, learning is not the acquisition of ornamental accomplishments but the rigorous cultivation of reason and understanding — "the important task of learning to think and reason" (002-012). She argues that genuine learning, systematically denied to women, is the foundation of moral and intellectual excellence.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft defines learning as the active and sustained exercise of the rational faculties, distinguishing it sharply from the "snatches" of superficial education that women typically receive.

She observes that women's education "is rather by snatches; and as learning is with them, in general, only a secondary thing" (002-019).

True learning, for Wollstonecraft, requires sustained mental effort and the systematic development of the understanding — capacities that the prevailing system of female education systematically withholds.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument for learning is inseparable from her broader case for women's rational equality. She insists that the first step in female education must be "the important task of learning to think and reason" (002-012), treating cognitive development not as a gendered privilege but as a universal human necessity. Without learning, women remain dependent on the opinions of others.

The social prohibition against female learning is one of Wollstonecraft's sharpest targets. She excoriates Dr. Gregory's infamous advice: "if you happen to have any learning keep it a profound secret, especially from the men" (005-081). This injunction to conceal knowledge institutionalises intellectual dishonesty and ensures that women can never develop genuine virtue.

Wollstonecraft extends her argument to the content of learning itself. Genuine learning must include the capacity to distinguish good from evil: "thus learning to know good from evil, by experience" (013-021). She argues that God "never implanted" in women any natural incapacity for learning (005-124); their intellectual limitations are socially constructed.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft's position stands in direct opposition to the dominant educational theories of her time. Where Rousseau's Sophie learns only what is useful for pleasing a husband, and where Gregory advises women to hide their learning, Wollstonecraft insists that learning must be public, rigorous, and equal to men's. She rejects the "advice respecting female learning" that "commonly produces" nothing but intellectual timidity (012-086).

## Key Passages

> "I shall first consider women in the grand light of human creatures, who, in common with men, are placed on this earth to unfold their faculties — and secondly, more particularly, I shall endeavour to point out the important task of learning to think and reason." (002-012)

> "But if you happen to have any learning keep it a profound secret, especially from the men, who generally look with a jealous and malignant eye on a woman of great parts, and a cultivated understanding." (005-081, quoting Gregory)

> "the weak creatures, who are learning to know him, never implanted in them any virtue" (005-124)
