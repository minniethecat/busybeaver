**Cultivation** is the positive term in Wollstonecraft's educational vocabulary. Where refinement, accomplishments, and gallantry name the corruptions she diagnoses, cultivation names the remedy. "Cultivation of mind" (003-022, 004-034, 012-027, 013-046) and "cultivation of the understanding" (002-019, 004-009, 004-011, 004-057) are her most insistently repeated positive phrases — the deliberate, sustained work of developing rational capacity, without which neither virtue nor happiness is possible.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's "cultivation" is distinguished from mere instruction by its object and depth. Instruction transmits facts; cultivation develops faculties. "The true cultivation of the understanding" (004-009) is an exercise that "opens" the mind (004-057), enlarging its capacity rather than merely filling it. She draws on the word's agricultural sense: cultivation is what transforms wild ground into productive soil, and the mind, like the earth, requires not just seeding but sustained tending.

The defining characteristic of genuine cultivation is that it produces independence of judgment. A cultivated mind does not merely know more; it thinks differently — with "enlarged" views (004-059), beyond "confined" horizons (005-092), capable of reasoning from principles rather than from habit. This is why "imperfect cultivation" (002-005) is so dangerous: a mind partially cultivated may acquire the appearance of understanding without the substance, becoming more adept at rationalisation than at reason.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument for cultivation is revolutionary in its scope: she claims that the systematic denial of cultivation to women is not merely an educational failure but a moral crime. "From the imperfect cultivation which their understandings now receive, they are taught to value superficial graces" (002-005). The sequence is causal: deficient cultivation produces women who value the wrong things, which in turn produces a society that justifies deficient cultivation by pointing to the very defects it has created.

She identifies specific obstacles to cultivation: "every thing is in the way to prevent the cultivation of the female understanding" (004-011). These obstacles are not accidental but structural — the entire system of female education, social expectation, and legal subordination is designed to prevent women from developing their rational capacities. "The cultivation of the understanding is always subordinate to the acquirement of some personal accomplishment" (002-019) — the passive construction "is always subordinate" describes not a pedagogical choice but a social law.

Her most radical claim is that cultivation is a universal human need, not a sex-specific luxury. "The one sex is educated to cultivate the understanding, the other to cultivate sensibility" (005-006). This division of cultivation — reason for men, feeling for women — produces the deformed characters she anatomises throughout the *Vindication*. Women who have been denied cultivation become "more useful partners, though not better mothers" (004-058); they can manage households but cannot form minds.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Rousseau's *Émile* presents cultivation as sex-differentiated: Émile cultivates reason; Sophie cultivates charm. Wollstonecraft rejects this division absolutely. "The object of that cultivation is different" (005-006) — in Rousseau's scheme — but for Wollstonecraft, cultivation that does not develop reason is not cultivation at all. Dr. Gregory's *Legacy* to his daughters, which advises women to conceal their learning, represents precisely the anti-cultivation Wollstonecraft attacks: a system that treats the female mind as something to be hidden rather than developed.
