For Wollstonecraft, **birth** is the emblem of everything arbitrary in the social order. That an individual's life chances should be determined by the accident of which family they are born into is, in her analysis, a fundamental injustice — one that corrupts both those elevated by birth and those subordinated by it.

She contrasts the contingent fact of biological birth with the moral concept of "birthright" (004-001): the capacities of reason and self-direction that belong to every human being regardless of the circumstances of their nativity. The structure of hereditary privilege is, in her framework, a denial of this deeper birthright.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft uses "birth" in two distinct but related senses. In its literal sense, it denotes the fact of being born — the entry into a specific family and social position that is, from the infant's perspective, entirely arbitrary.

She notes that women "from their birth" (004-012) are placed in circumstances designed to produce weakness, and that "from their very birth" (005-012) girls are treated to flattery rather than reason, their character shaped by expectations before they can speak.

In its normative sense, "birthright" names what every human being is entitled to simply by virtue of being human. The "birthright of a man" (002-084) includes the capacity for reason, the right to self-direction, and the obligation to develop one's faculties.

When Wollstonecraft writes that women must "assert their birthright" (004-001), she is claiming that the capacities denied to women by custom are in fact their natural inheritance — not a gift to be bestowed by men, but a possession to be claimed.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

The critique of birth runs through Wollstonecraft's argument at multiple levels. On the most straightforward level, she attacks the aristocracy: the notion that "noble birth" confers superiority of character is, she insists, contradicted by the observable fact that hereditary privilege produces idleness, dissipation, and moral vacancy.

The "arbitrary distinction of fortune" (004-059) is, in her view, no more rational than the distinction of birth; both substitute accident for merit.

The argument is extended, with radical implications, to the condition of women. Wollstonecraft contends that the subordination of women is, like the subordination of the poor to the rich, an artifact of birth rather than of nature.

Women "from their birth" (009-002) are "swathed" in constraints that prevent the development of their faculties, just as the aristocrat is "swathed" in privilege that prevents the development of his. The parallel is deliberate and destabilising: if birth does not justify the privileges of the aristocrat, it cannot justify the subordination of women.

This argument culminates in Wollstonecraft's concept of a universal birthright. She insists on "the birth right of man" (011-008) — the right of rational self-determination — as the foundation from which all just social arrangements must proceed.

To offer women "compliments" and "gallantry" in place of this birthright is, she argues, to offer "the shadow instead of the substance" (004-001).

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft's treatment of birth directly challenges the Burkean reverence for hereditary institutions, in which birth is the sacred thread connecting past to future. For Wollstonecraft, birth is not sacred but accidental; what is sacred is the rational capacity that birth arbitrarily distributes.

This aligns her with the radical wing of Enlightenment thought — with Paine rather than Burke — while remaining distinct from the more thoroughgoing egalitarianism of later movements.

## Key Passages

> Instead of asserting their birthright, they quietly lick the dust. (004-001)

> A man, who servilely resigns the birthright of a man, is not a moral agent. (002-084)

> If from their birth men and women are placed in different circumstances, can we expect the same results? (004-012)

> Swathed from their birth, [women] seldom exert the locomotive faculty of body or mind. (009-002)
