In Wollstonecraft's moral psychology, **exertion** is the active principle through which reason and virtue are cultivated. She distinguishes between "mental exertion" (003-027) — the deliberate exercise of the understanding — and the passive reception of impressions that characterises women's degraded education. For Wollstonecraft, virtue is not a passive state but something "obtained by its own exertions" (003-061), making the capacity and opportunity for exertion central to her argument for women's moral equality.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft defines exertion as the deliberate, often difficult application of one's faculties to a worthy object. She acknowledges that "mental as well as bodily exertion is, at first, irksome" (005-144), but insists that this initial difficulty is precisely why the capacity for exertion must be cultivated from childhood. The resistance to exertion — the preference for ease and passive pleasure — is, in her analysis, one of the principal obstacles to women's moral development.

Crucially, Wollstonecraft ties exertion to independence of character. She argues that what is not "obtained by its own exertions" (003-061) cannot form the basis of genuine self-respect. Women who are taught to rely on charm and deference rather than their own intellectual labour are, in her framework, denied the very process through which character is formed. The "vigorous exertion of intellect" (004-026) is not merely an intellectual achievement but a moral necessity.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument for exertion operates within her broader critique of women's education. She contends that the "confined education" she deplores prevents "any vigorous exertions of genius or benevolence" (005-118), effectively starving the mind of the activity it requires to develop. Women are trained for a life of passive reception — receiving male opinions, receiving social approval, receiving fashionable instruction — rather than for the active exertion of their own faculties.

The social consequences of this training are severe. Wollstonecraft observes that women "sink in reality below the brute creation" (003-027) when they make no "mental exertion," becoming creatures of mere instinct rather than rational agents. She contrasts this with the ideal of a life directed by "the exertion of reason" (001-033), in which each individual actively works to understand and improve their condition rather than passively accepting what custom prescribes.

The political dimension of exertion is also significant. Wollstonecraft notes that men "in one station, at least, have an opportunity of exerting themselves with dignity" (004-025) — the military or the professions — while women are denied any arena for the "exertions which really improve a rational creature." Her demand is not merely that women be permitted to exert themselves, but that society be restructured to provide women with objects worthy of their exertion.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Where Rousseau imagined Sophie's education as training in pleasing compliance, Wollstonecraft insists on exertion as the mechanism of moral growth. Rousseau's preference for "the easiest…attainments" (004-016) — those requiring the least exertion — is precisely what Wollstonecraft identifies as the source of women's degradation. She also implicitly counters the aristocratic ideal of leisure, arguing that "hereditary property" (009-002) and inherited station remove the necessity for exertion and thereby corrupt the character of both sexes.

## Key Passages

> Mental as well as bodily exertion is, at first, irksome; so much so, that the great argument against the cultivation of reason has been, that it would be painful. (005-144)

> What is not obtained by its own exertions? (003-061)

> The confined education of women prevents any vigorous exertions of genius or benevolence. (005-118)
