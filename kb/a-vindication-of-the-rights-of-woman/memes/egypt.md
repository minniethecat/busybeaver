# Egypt

Ancient civilization used by Wollstonecraft as a metaphor for the oppressive condition of women and the tyranny of unreasonable authority.

## Location

Egypt, ancient civilization of the Nile Valley in Northeast Africa. In the 18th century, Egypt was part of the Ottoman Empire, known to European readers primarily through biblical and classical sources, as well as travel narratives.

## Relevance to the Text

Wollstonecraft employs Egyptian imagery twice in her argument, each time as a figure for unjust subjection. She draws on the familiar biblical trope of Egyptian bondage to characterize the condition of women under a system that denies them the cultivation of reason.

### In the Argument

In Chapter 6, Wollstonecraft describes women as "educated in worse than Egyptian bondage" (006-006), arguing that women's upbringing — surrounded by false notions of beauty and delicacy — is a form of slavery more insidious than physical captivity. The comparison to Egyptian bondage invokes the Exodus narrative, elevating the plight of women to a spiritual and intellectual captivity.

In the final chapter, she returns to the Egyptian metaphor, warning men not to be "worse than Egyptian task-masters, expecting virtue where nature has not given understanding" (013-080). This passage flips the patriarchal expectation that women should be virtuous without being educated, comparing unreasonable husbands and fathers to the slave drivers of Pharaoh.

## Historical Context

For Wollstonecraft and her readers, Egypt represented ancient despotism and pagan superstition, as filtered through the Bible and classical historians. The Enlightenment critique of arbitrary power frequently drew on these associations — comparing modern tyranny to "Egyptian bondage" was a recognizable rhetorical move in radical and dissenting traditions.
