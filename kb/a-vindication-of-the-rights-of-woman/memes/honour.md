# Honour

Honour in Wollstonecraft's analysis operates as a deeply gendered moral code: for men, it is tied to "hereditary honours" and martial reputation; for women, it is reductively equated with sexual chastity. Both forms, she argues, substitute external reputation for internal virtue.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft distinguishes between the honour code of aristocratic men — "romantic notions of honour, a kind of morality founded on the fashion of the age" (001-026) — and the honour code imposed on women — "the honour of a woman is not made even to depend on her will" (004-083). In both cases, honour is an external standard that bypasses rational moral judgment.

For men, "hereditary honours" and "riches and honours" (004-049) corrupt character by making status independent of merit. Military honour, built on "subordination and rigour" (001-026), demands blind obedience rather than rational virtue. For women, honour is "absurdly" reduced to a "single virtue — chastity" (008-021), a reduction that Wollstonecraft finds morally grotesque: a woman's entire moral worth hangs on one physical fact, leaving no scope for the full range of virtues.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's central argument about honour is that it destroys genuine virtue by externalizing moral judgment. "Riches and hereditary honours have made cyphers of women" (002-027) — the honour code reduces women to blank figures whose value is assigned by others, not cultivated from within. The gendered asymmetry is stark: "A woman who has lost her honour, imagines that she cannot fall lower" (004-082), and society confirms this judgment, treating her as irrecoverable. Yet a man who "had robbed her of her honour" (004-084) suffers no comparable moral annihilation.

She contrasts honour-as-reputation with honour-as-virtue, particularly in discussing marriage: "following the dictates of honour, marries the girl" (005-110) after seduction — here honour is merely reputation-management. True honour, she implies, would be treating women with "tenderness and honour" (005-060) from the start, respecting their rational nature rather than their reputation.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The aristocratic honour code of Wollstonecraft's era — rooted in chivalric tradition — treated honour as a male possession tied to lineage, martial courage, and the sexual purity of female relatives. Wollstonecraft's critique is radical: she argues that this entire framework is morally bankrupt because it substitutes external markers for internal virtue. Her invocation of "the curb, however weak, of honour" (003-023) suggests that even at its best, honour is a weak restraint compared to rational principle.

Rousseau valorized the "honour" of the ancient republics, where citizens sought glory through virtue. Wollstonecraft shares some of this republican critique of hereditary honour, but extends it further: even republican honour, if gendered, fails women.
