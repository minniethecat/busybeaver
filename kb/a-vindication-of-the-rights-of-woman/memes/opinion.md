**Opinion** occupies a central and largely pejorative place in Wollstonecraft's moral epistemology. Throughout the *Vindication*, "opinion" is contrasted with "reason," "principle," and "truth" — it is the shifting, unreliable standard by which women in particular are taught to govern their conduct, and it is the mechanism by which society enforces conformity without conviction. "It is not necessary to dwell on these obvious truths," she writes, "because females have been insulated" from independent judgment and left at the mercy of "the opinions and manners of the society they live in" (002-013).

The critique of opinion is at once epistemological and political. Epistemologically, opinion is belief without foundation — "taking all their opinions on credit" (002-026) rather than arriving at conviction through the exercise of reason. Politically, opinion is the instrument of social control: women are "the modest slaves of opinion" (003-059), taught to care more about what others think of them than about what they owe to themselves.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft distinguishes opinion from knowledge along a spectrum of reliability that she inherits from Enlightenment philosophy. Opinion is belief held on insufficient grounds — "crude opinions" (003-033) that have not been subjected to the discipline of reason. Knowledge, by contrast, is belief grounded in evidence, reflection, and the exercise of one's own faculties.

The distinction matters because women, in Wollstonecraft's analysis, are educated to live entirely within the domain of opinion. "Their conduct is unstable, and their opinions are wavering" (004-033) — not because of any natural deficiency, but because they have been denied the education that would enable them to think for themselves. "The understanding of woman has been so bubbled by this specious homage, that the civilized women of the present century ... are only anxious to inspire love, when they ought to cherish a nobler ambition, and by their abilities and virtues exact respect" (P03-001).

Public opinion is a particularly insidious force. "When once the public opinion is fixed" (001-033), it exercises a tyranny over individual judgment that few have the courage to resist. Women, whose social standing depends entirely on reputation, are especially vulnerable: their whole education is "an education of the opinions" rather than an education of the understanding.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument about opinion unfolds across several dimensions of her broader case for women's education and emancipation.

First, she establishes that opinion is an unreliable foundation for conduct. "Opinions are not deduced from reason, but taken on credit" — and a woman whose conduct is governed by opinion rather than principle "never rises above opinion, or feels the dignity of a rational will" (002-078). The result is a life lived at the mercy of others' judgments, without the internal resources that make moral autonomy possible.

Second, she shows how the tyranny of opinion operates with particular force on women. The "prevailing opinion, that woman was created for man" (002-034) shapes every aspect of female education, teaching girls that their worth is measured by male approbation rather than by their own virtue. "Women are told from their infancy, and taught by the example of their mothers, that a little knowledge of human weakness, justly termed cunning, softness of temper, outward obedience, and a scrupulous attention to a puerile kind of propriety, will obtain for them the protection of man" (002-011). The result is "the modest slaves of opinion" (003-059) — women who have been taught that conformity is virtue.

Third, she identifies the remedy: education that cultivates the capacity for independent judgment. Women must be taught to think, not merely to remember the opinions of others. "The most perfect education, in my opinion, is such an exercise of the understanding as is best calculated to strengthen the body and form the heart" (002-014). The paradox is deliberate: she states her own "opinion" about education while simultaneously arguing that opinion must be transcended by reason.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Rousseau's treatment of opinion provides Wollstonecraft with both a target and a point of departure. "This was Rousseau's opinion respecting men: I extend it to women" (002-015) — she appropriates his framework while reversing his gendered application. Where Rousseau saw women's susceptibility to opinion as a natural trait to be managed, Wollstonecraft sees it as a social construction to be dismantled.

The Scottish Enlightenment tradition — particularly Adam Smith's analysis of the "impartial spectator" — treated opinion as a mechanism of moral regulation. Wollstonecraft shares this interest in the social function of opinion but is more critical: for women, the impartial spectator is always male, and the standards it enforces are not moral but merely conventional.

Edmund Burke, though not named directly in the *Vindication*, represents the conservative elevation of "prejudice" and inherited opinion over abstract reason. Wollstonecraft's insistence that opinion must be tested by reason places her firmly on the rationalist side of this debate.

## Key Passages

> I wish to show that elegance is inferior to virtue, that the first object of laudable ambition is to obtain a character as a human being, regardless of the distinction of sex; and that secondary views should be brought to this simple touchstone. (P02-010)

> It is vain to expect virtue from women till they are, in some degree, independent of men; nay, it is vain to expect that strength of natural affection, which would make them good wives and mothers. Whilst they are absolutely dependent on their husbands, they will be cunning, mean, and selfish. (005-078)

> Strengthen the female mind by enlarging it, and there will be an end to blind obedience. (002-082)

> Why must the sacred majesty of truth be violated to detain a deceitful good? (002-060)
