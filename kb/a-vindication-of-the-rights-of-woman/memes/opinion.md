# Opinion

## Definition

In Wollstonecraft's usage, **opinion** carries a predominantly negative valence.
It refers to beliefs and judgments adopted without rational examination — what she
also calls *prejudices* or *received opinions*. The tyranny of opinion over women's
lives is one of her central critiques: women are taught from childhood to regulate
their conduct not by reason or principle, but by what others will think of them.

## The Tyranny of Opinion

Wollstonecraft argues that women's subjection to opinion is both a cause and a
consequence of their degraded condition:

Women are educated to prize reputation above virtue, and to seek the approbation
of others rather than the approval of their own conscience. This makes them
"the slaves of opinion" — perpetually anxious about how they are perceived,
incapable of independent moral judgment.

The power of opinion over women is reinforced by their economic dependence: since
a woman's security depends on marriage, she must constantly manage her reputation
and attend to the opinions of potential suitors, parents, and society at large.

## Opinion vs. Reason

The fundamental opposition in Wollstonecraft's epistemology is not between true
and false opinions, but between *opinion* (unexamined belief) and *reason*
(examined, principled judgment). Education that merely transmits received opinions
perpetuates error; only education that cultivates the reasoning faculty can liberate
women from subjection to fashionable prejudices.

## Opinion and Reputation

*Reputation* — social estimation of one's character — is the mechanism through
which opinion exerts its power. Wollstonecraft distinguishes reputation sharply
from genuine virtue:

A woman governed by opinion cares above all for her *reputation* — for appearing
virtuous in the eyes of the world. A woman governed by reason cares for *virtue*
itself — for being good, regardless of whether the world acknowledges it. The
former is precarious and inauthentic; the latter is the foundation of moral
integrity.

## The World's Opinion and Female Conduct

Wollstonecraft identifies the desire to make "an appearance in the world" as
a primary motive force in women's lives — and as a primary obstacle to their
moral development (004-091). The respect for "the opinion of the world" has
been cultivated in women to such a degree that it displaces genuine moral
reflection (008-010). A woman who governs her conduct by public opinion cannot
develop authentic virtue, because her standard of right and wrong shifts with
every change in social fashion.

This dependence on external judgment extends even to matters of religious
faith. Wollstonecraft observes that when "a woman is subservient to the public
opinion, her faith in matters" of religion becomes as shallow and performative
as every other aspect of her character (005-041). The tyranny of opinion thus
corrupts not only social conduct but the deepest sources of moral and spiritual
integrity.
