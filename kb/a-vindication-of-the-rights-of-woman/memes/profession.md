# Profession

Wollstonecraft's advocacy for women's access to professions represents one of her most radical proposals — the argument that women should pursue careers beyond domesticity, achieving economic independence and intellectual dignity through meaningful work.

## Definition

In Wollstonecraft's usage, a profession is a respectable occupation that engages the rational faculties and provides economic independence.

She contrasts genuine professions — which require "mind" and yield dignity — with the debased condition of women who are "prepared for professions" but in fact prepared only for marriage (004-030).

Wollstonecraft draws on the Enlightenment recognition that "every profession, in which great subordination of rank constitutes its power, is highly injurious to morality" (001-025), extending this critique of hierarchical professions to the domestic sphere.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft argues that the exclusion of women from professions is a structural injustice that damages both women and society. She observes that "men, in their youth, are prepared for professions, and marriage is not considered as the grand feature in their lives" (004-030), while women face precisely the opposite expectation — marriage as their sole career and source of identity.

The moral dimension of professional exclusion is central to Wollstonecraft's argument. Professions shape character: "a man of sense may only have a cast of countenance that wears off as you trace his individuality" (001-031). The denial of professional opportunity to women shapes them into the artificially weak creatures that society then cites as evidence of female inferiority.

Wollstonecraft identifies the degrading nature of certain professions — "those professions are most debasing whose ladder is intrigue" (012-018) — and extends this insight to the domestic "profession" of wife, structured by total subordination. Her solution: a reformed education where those "designed for particular professions, might attend" appropriate training (012-060).

The biographical dimension reinforces her argument: Wollstonecraft herself "continued in the profession" of teaching and writing (P01-008), demonstrating by example that women could pursue professional vocations.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

While Enlightenment thinkers celebrated professional liberty for men, they almost universally excluded women. Rousseau envisioned Sophie's entire education as preparation for domesticity. Wollstonecraft's radical innovation is to extend the Enlightenment language of professional dignity to women: if "every profession" shapes character (001-025), then women's exclusion from professions shapes them into dependent beings.

## Key Passages

> "every profession, in which great subordination of rank constitutes its power, is highly injurious to morality" (001-025)

> "men, in their youth, are prepared for professions, and marriage is not considered as the grand feature in their lives; whilst women, on the contrary, have no other scheme to sharpen their faculties" (004-030)

> "those professions are most debasing whose ladder is intrigue" (012-018)

> "If women were educated for professions, and allowed to fill more respectable employments, they would not be rendered ridiculous by being placed in situations that do not call forth their faculties" — Wollstonecraft's positive vision
