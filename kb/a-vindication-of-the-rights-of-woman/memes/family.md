# Family

For Wollstonecraft, the family is not merely a private domestic arrangement but a political institution in miniature. She analyzes the family as the site where character is first formed, where women's subordination is enacted daily, and where the logic of despotism is transmitted from one generation to the next. The governance of the family — who rules, how they rule, and what children learn from that rule — is central to her argument for women's education.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft treats the family as a structure of authority that mirrors and supports political despotism. Her own family experience informs this analysis: she notes in the Biographical Sketch that her "father being a despot in his family" (P01-006) shaped her early understanding of arbitrary power. This biographical detail is not incidental — it establishes the conceptual link between domestic and political tyranny that runs through the entire work.

The family, in her analysis, is where women are assigned their role: "considered as the mistress of the family, she is viewed with averted looks" (004-052) when she steps beyond domestic bounds. The phrase "mistress of a family" (004-048) appears repeatedly, denoting a role that is honored in name but circumscribed in practice. Women are expected to govern the household yet are denied the education that would make that governance rational.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

The family is the school of character — and Wollstonecraft finds the curriculum wanting. She asks whether women, with their limited education, can possibly "govern a family, or take care of the poor babes" (003-012), answering implicitly that they cannot. The family requires reason, judgment, and knowledge — the very capacities that women's education neglects.

She critiques the model in which "the father of a family will not then weaken his constitution" (P02-020) by attending to domestic duties, while the mother is expected to manage everything. The asymmetry is structural: the father's authority is absolute, the mother's responsibility is total, yet her authority is merely delegated. Women are "very unfit to manage a family; for by an undue stretch of power" (004-059) they can become domestic tyrants in turn — not because women are naturally unfit, but because their education has given them no principles of rational governance.

The alternative Wollstonecraft envisions is a family governed by reason and mutual respect. She notes that "a master and mistress of a family ought not to continue to love" (002-058) with romantic passion alone — the family requires a steadier foundation. When affection subsides into friendship, the family becomes a partnership of rational equals rather than a hierarchy of command.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Rousseau's *Émile* idealized the family as a natural haven from the corruptions of society, with the father as benevolent patriarch and the mother as devoted nurturer. Wollstonecraft rejects this pastoral picture. For her, the family is not a refuge from politics but politics in miniature — and the patriarchal family teaches tyranny to boys and submission to girls. Where Rousseau saw natural harmony, Wollstonecraft sees "cautious family prudence" (005-092) that stifles women's moral development and confines them to "the regulation of a family" (004-048) as their sole horizon.
