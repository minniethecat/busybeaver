# Blasphemy

One of Wollstonecraft's most striking rhetorical moves in the *Vindication* is her redefinition of blasphemy. Rather than reserving the term for heresy or irreverence toward established doctrine, she applies it to religious doctrines themselves — specifically, to the belief that a just and benevolent God would condemn human beings to eternal punishment. True blasphemy, in her framework, is not the questioning of orthodox theology but the attribution to God of conduct that would be considered unjust in a human magistrate.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft introduces the concept through an observation about theodicy: "men of lively sensibility have almost uttered blasphemy in order to justify the dispensations of providence" (001-013). The paradox is deliberate. Those who rush to defend God's ways by attributing all human misery to divine will come perilously close, in Wollstonecraft's view, to making God the author of evil — a position she regards as genuinely blasphemous. The concept thus operates at the intersection of theology and moral philosophy: what counts as blasphemy depends on what one believes about the character of God.

The strongest formulation appears in the final chapter. To believe that God would create a being "foreseeing, that after fifty or sixty years of feverish existence, it would be plunged into never ending woe — is blasphemy" (013-023). The statement is remarkable for its time — a direct challenge to the doctrine of eternal punishment that was central to much eighteenth-century Christian orthodoxy. Wollstonecraft does not merely disagree with this doctrine; she names it as an affront to the divine character.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument proceeds from her theological rationalism — the conviction that God's attributes, as known through reason, are incompatible with arbitrary punishment. "Considering the attributes of God," she writes, "I believe, that whatever punishment may follow, will tend, like the anguish of disease, to show the malignity of vice, for the purpose of reformation" (013-022). Punishment, on this account, is medicinal rather than retributive — designed to cure rather than to avenge. Any doctrine that presents God as punishing without the "benevolent design of reforming" (013-022) dishonours God more than honest doubt ever could.

This theological argument has direct implications for Wollstonecraft's social critique. The same habit of mind that accepts eternal punishment as divine justice also accepts social hierarchies as divinely ordained. "Many devout people boast of submitting to the Will of God blindly, as to an arbitrary sceptre or rod, on the same principle as the Indians worship the devil" (013-024). The sharpness of the comparison — equating Christian submission to arbitrary divine will with devil-worship — is meant to shock readers into recognizing that their piety may, in fact, be a form of impiety. "Rational religion, on the contrary, is a submission to the will of a being so perfectly wise, that all he wills must be directed by the proper motive — must be reasonable" (013-024).

The second application of the concept targets religious imposture. Wollstonecraft writes that "it is, however, little short of blasphemy to pretend to such power" (013-018) — referring to the magnetisers and miracle-workers who claim to heal by supernatural means. The blasphemy here lies in presuming that God would suspend the natural order to gratify the whims of charlatans. "Can any one so grossly insult the wisdom of God," she asks, "as to suppose, that a miracle will be allowed to disturb his general laws, to restore to health the intemperate and vicious, merely to enable them to pursue the same course with impunity?" (013-018).

Throughout, Wollstonecraft's use of "blasphemy" functions as a rhetorical reversal. The term that orthodox religion reserves for its critics becomes, in her hands, a charge against orthodoxy itself. What is truly blasphemous is not honest inquiry but the projection of human cruelty onto the divine — the insistence that God's justice operates on principles that would be rejected as unjust in any human court.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The doctrine of eternal punishment was defended by many of Wollstonecraft's contemporaries, including William Paley, whose *Principles of Moral and Political Philosophy* (1785) argued that "the pains of hell" were a necessary sanction for moral conduct. Wollstonecraft's position aligns more closely with the rational Dissenting tradition — particularly the Unitarian and Socinian critiques of eternal punishment — though she applies the argument with a polemical force unusual even in those circles. Joseph Priestley, the Unitarian minister and philosopher, had argued against eternal punishment on exegetical grounds; Wollstonecraft adds the philosophical argument that such a doctrine is not merely mistaken but actively impious.

The comparison with Rousseau is also instructive. Rousseau's "Profession of Faith of the Savoyard Vicar" in *Émile* had advanced a rational religion grounded in conscience and natural sentiment, but Rousseau stopped short of naming orthodox doctrines as blasphemous. Wollstonecraft's willingness to do so reflects both her intellectual courage and her conviction that "the more enlightened the human mind becomes, the deeper root will just and simple principles take" (010-004).

## Key Passages

> Such, indeed, has been the wretchedness that has flowed from hereditary honours, riches, and monarchy, that men of lively sensibility have almost uttered blasphemy in order to justify the dispensations of providence. (001-013)

> It is, however, little short of blasphemy to pretend to such power. From the whole tenor of the dispensations of Providence, it appears evident to sober reason, that certain vices produce certain effects: and can any one so grossly insult the wisdom of God, as to suppose, that a miracle will be allowed to disturb his general laws, to restore to health the intemperate and vicious, merely to enable them to pursue the same course with impunity? (013-018)

> To suppose only, that an all-wise and powerful Being, as good as he is great, should create a being, foreseeing, that after fifty or sixty years of feverish existence, it would be plunged into never ending woe — is blasphemy. On what will the worm feed that is never to die? On folly, on ignorance, say ye — I should blush indignantly at drawing the natural conclusion, could I insert it, and wish to withdraw myself from the wing of my God! (013-023)
