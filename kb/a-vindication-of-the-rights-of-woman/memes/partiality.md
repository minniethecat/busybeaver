# Partiality

Wollstonecraft uses "partiality" in the biographical sketch of the author to describe the gradual, almost imperceptible development of parental bias — a natural human tendency that operates beneath the level of conscious reasoning.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

The single direct reference appears in the biographical sketch, where Wollstonecraft describes how "the partiality they conceived for each other grew by the most imperceptible degrees." (P01-017) Though the reference is to the author's parents, the framing is significant: partiality is presented as a natural human disposition, one that develops gradually and outside conscious awareness.

This aligns with Wollstonecraft's broader treatment of the passions — they are not inherently evil but must be subjected to the governance of reason. Partiality, unchecked, becomes the kind of doting parental love she critiques elsewhere as harmful to children's character formation.

### Implications for Education

The concept connects to Wollstonecraft's educational theory through its opposite: impartial judgment. If partiality is the natural disposition of parents toward their children, then education must supply the corrective — teaching both parents and children to evaluate actions and character by rational standards rather than affection alone.
