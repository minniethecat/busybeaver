# Aristocracy

The system of hereditary privilege and rank that Wollstonecraft identifies as the natural first form of government in "the infancy of society" (001-032), but which she argues is fundamentally incompatible with virtue and freedom—and whose domestic analogue, the arbitrary authority of men over women, reproduces the same injustice within the household.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft defines aristocracy in primarily political terms as a system in which power and status descend through birth rather than merit. In tracing its origins, she observes that in early societies "chiefs and priests, touching the most powerful springs of savage conduct—hope and fear—must have had unbounded sway," and that "an aristocracy, of course, is naturally the first form of government" (001-032). Yet her analysis quickly moves beyond the merely descriptive. Aristocracy, for Wollstonecraft, is not simply a historical phase but a persistent logic of unjust hierarchy that "assumes" many forms (004-044). She extends this critique to encompass any arrangement in which arbitrary distinctions—whether of birth, wealth, or sex—determine a person's access to power, education, and moral development. The "aristocracy, founded on property, or sterling talents" (004-071) sweeps before it the possibility of a genuinely virtuous society, precisely because it substitutes inherited position for earned capacity.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft develops her critique of aristocracy along a dual track: political and domestic. Politically, she rejects the argument "frequently heard fall from sensible men in favour of an aristocracy: that the mass of mankind cannot be any thing" (004-001). This claim—that most people are incapable of rational self-governance and therefore require rule by an elite—is, she insists, a self-fulfilling prophecy. When people "patiently allow themselves to be penned up" (004-001), they confirm the prejudice that justified their subordination in the first place. The remedy is not benevolent rule by superiors but the universal diffusion of education, which enables all people to "feel their own consequence, and spurn their chains" (004-001).

The domestic dimension of her critique is equally forceful. Wollstonecraft draws a direct parallel between political aristocracy and the arbitrary authority of men over women. Just as the aristocrat claims superiority by birth rather than merit, so men claim dominion over women by sex rather than demonstrated capacity. She identifies Rousseau as a particularly egregious exponent of this "male aristocracy" (005-034), noting that he "would carry his male aristocracy still further" by insinuating that women should be left "in a state of the most profound ignorance" (005-034) to preserve their chastity. For Wollstonecraft, this is the logic of aristocracy in its purest form: the assertion that some beings are born to rule and others to obey, and that the ruled are best kept in ignorance of their own capacity.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The eighteenth-century debate over aristocracy was dominated by the question of whether hereditary rank could be reconciled with enlightenment principles of natural equality. Conservatives defended aristocracy as a guarantor of social stability; radicals attacked it as the principal obstacle to political reform. Wollstonecraft's contribution to this debate is distinctive in its insistence on the analogy between political and domestic hierarchy. Where her contemporaries treated the "rights of man" and the "condition of woman" as separate questions, Wollstonecraft argued that the same logic of arbitrary privilege underlay both. The man who defends aristocracy in the state while exercising arbitrary authority in the home is, in her analysis, simply inconsistent: both arrangements rest on the assertion that some beings are naturally fit to command and others to obey—an assertion she systematically dismantles.

## Key Passages

> In the infancy of society, when men were just emerging out of barbarism, chiefs and priests, touching the most powerful springs of savage conduct—hope and fear—must have had unbounded sway. An aristocracy, of course, is naturally the first form of government. (001-032)

> This position I shall simply contrast with a conclusion, which I have frequently heard fall from sensible men in favour of an aristocracy: that the mass of mankind cannot be any thing, or the obsequious slaves, who patiently allow themselves to be penned up, would feel their own consequence, and spurn their chains. (004-001)

> In the same strain have I heard men argue against instructing the poor; for many are the forms that aristocracy assumes. "Teach them to read and write," say they, "and you take them out of the station assigned them by nature." (004-044)

> Rousseau would carry his male aristocracy still further, for he insinuates, that he should not blame those, who contend for leaving woman in a state of the most profound ignorance, if it were not necessary, in order to preserve her chastity, and justify the man's choice in the eyes of the world, to give her a little knowledge of men, and the customs produced by human passions. (005-034)

> Unless there be a ballast of understanding, they will never become either virtuous or free: an aristocracy, founded on property, or sterling talents, will ever sweep before it the moderately rich, who fight against its encroachments with such resolution as they can summon. (004-071)

## Related Concepts

- [[Rank]] — the social expression of aristocratic logic, which Wollstonecraft criticises for substituting inherited position for personal merit
- [[Power]] — the arbitrary authority that aristocracy institutionalises and that corrupts both those who exercise it and those who submit to it
- [[Liberty]] — the political condition that aristocracy denies, and whose extension to women and the poor Wollstonecraft demands
- [[Virtue]] — the moral quality that cannot flourish under aristocratic institutions, which reward birth rather than character
