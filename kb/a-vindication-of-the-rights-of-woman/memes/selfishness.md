# Selfishness

A narrow, self-regarding disposition that Wollstonecraft diagnoses as both a product of women's confined social conditioning and a corruption of natural sensibility—fundamentally opposed to the expansive benevolence that characterises rational virtue.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft treats selfishness not as an innate human failing but as a socially produced distortion of character. She identifies "the natural selfishness of sensibility" (004-059) as a tendency that, left unguided by reason, contracts the heart rather than expanding it. Sensibility—the capacity for refined feeling—should in theory connect individuals to the suffering of others.

Without the discipline of reason, however, sensibility degenerates into self-absorption. The education women receive, which cultivates feeling at the expense of understanding, actively produces the selfishness it then condemns as a feminine failing.

In her analysis of boarding schools, she identifies "the cattish appetite of selfishness" (012-010) as the inevitable product of an environment where children are crowded together without moral cultivation. Selfishness in this framework is not a natural vice but a predictable outcome of defective institutions that fail to develop the rational and moral capacities of the young.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's treatment of selfishness operates at three interconnected levels: the individual psychological, the domestic, and the political. At the psychological level, she argues that women's education—focused on personal accomplishment, beauty, and securing a husband—necessarily produces "narrow views and selfishness" (005-021).

A woman taught that her value lies in attracting male admiration cannot develop the disinterested concern for others that constitutes genuine virtue. The woman "who can caress a man, with true feminine softness, the very moment when he treats her tyrannically" (005-021) reveals a heart corrupted by the self-preserving logic of dependency.

At the domestic level, Wollstonecraft observes that selfishness undermines the very relationships it purports to serve. The mother who treats her children as extensions of her own vanity—dressing them for display rather than cultivating their minds—is acting from "cunning selfishness" (012-004), not genuine maternal affection.

At the political level, she connects personal selfishness to broader social corruption: a society that teaches half its members to pursue only personal advantage cannot sustain the public-spiritedness that republican virtue requires.

True parental affection, she contends, "silences the hiss of selfishness or envy" (010-004), suggesting that rational education is the antidote to contracted self-regard.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Conventional conduct literature treated female selfishness as a natural defect of the sex, to be managed through manners rather than remedied through education. Rousseau, while lamenting the corruptions of *amour-propre*, nevertheless designed an education for Sophie that would reinforce precisely the self-regarding preoccupation with appearance and male approval.

Wollstonecraft's innovation is to treat selfishness as a structural effect of unequal education rather than a natural feminine vice, and to prescribe, not repression, but the expansive cultivation of reason as its cure.

## Key Passages

> Of what materials can that heart be composed, which can melt when insulted, and instead of revolting at injustice, kiss the rod? Is it unfair to infer, that her virtue is built on narrow views and selfishness, who can caress a man, with true feminine softness, the very moment when he treats her tyrannically? (005-021)

> But, what boy ever recollected with pleasure the years he spent in close confinement, at an academy near London? ... to devour it with the cattish appetite of selfishness. At boarding schools of every description, the relaxation of the junior boys is mischief; and of the senior, vice. (012-010)

> Convinced that they have a right to what they insist on, they do not fear reason, or dread the sifting of subjects that recur to natural justice: because they firmly believe, that the more enlightened the human mind becomes, the deeper root will just and simple principles take. (010-004)
