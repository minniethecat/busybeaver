Wollstonecraft deploys the concept of **citizen** to mark the boundary between those whose rational and political agency is recognised and those from whom it is withheld.

When she insists that women must be regarded as "rational creatures and free citizens" (012-095), she is making a claim at once descriptive and normative: the category of citizen must be extended to include women, not as a concession but as the logical consequence of their rational nature.

The citizen, in her framework, is defined not by sex, property, or rank but by the capacity for reasoned moral judgment and the duties that accompany it.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's citizen is a moral and political agent who participates in the collective life of the polity through the exercise of reason and the fulfilment of duty. She speaks of "the duties of citizens" (008-036) alongside those of "husbands, wives, fathers, mothers" — a significant move that places civic obligation on the same plane as domestic obligation for both sexes.

The citizen is not merely a subject who obeys law but an agent who contributes to its formation through enlightened understanding.

The concept carries strong republican overtones, drawn from the political vocabulary of the French Revolution and the Enlightenment more broadly. Wollstonecraft's reference to Rousseau as "the citizen of Geneva" (001-021) is pointed: she adopts the language of republican citizenship while contesting Rousseau's exclusion of women from its scope.

For Wollstonecraft, citizenship is not a gendered category; it is the political expression of the rational capacities that both sexes share.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument for women's citizenship operates on two fronts. First, she contends that the duties of citizenship — the obligation to contribute to the common good through informed judgment — cannot be fulfilled by those denied education and independence.

Women who are "kept in ignorance" cannot be "good citizens" (009-039) because they lack the rational equipment that citizenship presupposes. This argument turns the conservative logic on its head: if women are unfit for citizenship, it is because they have been deliberately kept so, and the remedy is not continued exclusion but education.

Second, Wollstonecraft argues that citizenship is the necessary foundation of virtue itself. She insists that a woman who "has no right to that of a citizen" (009-022) — who is excluded from the political community — is denied the very framework within which moral agency acquires meaning.

To "fulfil the duties of a citizen" (009-021) is, in her account, to be fully human; to be excluded from those duties is to be diminished as a moral being. The political and the moral are inseparable: "take away natural rights, and duties become null."

The institutional implications are concrete. Wollstonecraft proposes a national system of education designed "to form citizens" (012-023), not merely to produce obedient subjects. She imagines schools where children of both sexes learn together and develop the habits of rational deliberation that citizenship requires.

This is not a utopian abstraction but a specific policy proposal, grounded in her conviction that "to make men citizens, two natural steps might be taken" (012-008): abolish the distinction of rank in education, and abolish the distinction of sex.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft's treatment of citizenship stands in sharp contrast to Rousseau's, despite their shared republican vocabulary.

Rousseau's citizen is necessarily male — the *citoyen* of the *Social Contract* participates in the general will, while Sophie is educated for domesticity — whereas Wollstonecraft insists that the exclusion of women from citizenship is a contingent artifact of "situation" rather than a requirement of nature.

She also departs from the narrower Lockean tradition, which tied citizenship to property, by grounding it instead in rational capacity — a more universal but also more demanding standard.

## Key Passages

> Women ought to be considered as rational creatures and free citizens, and they will quickly become good wives, good mothers — in a word, better citizens. (012-095)

> Take away natural rights, and duties become null. A woman has no right to that of a citizen. (009-022)

> Thus to make men citizens, two natural steps might be taken: remove the artificial distinctions of rank, and let children be educated together. (012-008)

> Till they become enlightened citizens, till they become free, by being enabled to earn their own subsistence. (012-034)
