**Speculation**, in Wollstonecraft's usage, carries none of the pejorative connotations the term later acquired. It denotes the intellectual operation by which experience is generalised into principles — the movement from particular observations to universal truths. The phrase "experience generalized by speculation" (002-019) is her most compact definition: speculation is the rational faculty that transforms raw observation into knowledge. Without speculation, experience remains desultory, unsystematic, and incapable of yielding genuine understanding.

This concept is central to Wollstonecraft's epistemology and her educational programme. Women's intellectual inferiority, in her account, is not a natural deficiency but a consequence of an education that denies them the opportunity to practise speculative reasoning. Women "attain knowledge more by sheer observations on real life, than from comparing what has been individually observed with the results of experience generalized by speculation" (002-019). The remedy is not more experience but the cultivation of the speculative faculty that makes experience meaningful.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's definition of speculation is carefully distinguished from mere conjecture or idle theorising. Speculation, properly understood, is the rational comparison of "speculation and experience" (002-022) — a dialectical process in which general principles are tested against particular observations and vice versa. The "judgment, formed by comparing speculation and experience" (002-022) is the faculty that Wollstonecraft seeks to cultivate in women through education.

The pejorative sense of speculation — as groundless theorising detached from reality — is not Wollstonecraft's. She uses the term in its eighteenth-century philosophical sense, closer to what we would now call theoretical reasoning or abstract thought. When she writes that "virtue is not to be acquired even by speculation, much less by the negative supineness that wealth naturally generates" (009-024), she is not dismissing speculation but acknowledging its limits: speculation alone, without practical application, is insufficient for the acquisition of virtue. It is necessary but not sufficient.

The contrast between speculation and "sheer observations" (002-019) is fundamental. Observations without speculation remain "desultory" — disconnected, unsystematic, incapable of yielding general truths. Speculation is what transforms a collection of experiences into knowledge properly so called. Women's intellectual disadvantage is precisely that their education denies them access to speculative reasoning, confining them instead to the accumulation of unconnected observations.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Speculation is integral to Wollstonecraft's argument for women's rational education. If knowledge requires the generalisation of experience through speculation, and if women are systematically denied the education that cultivates speculative reasoning, then women's intellectual inferiority is an artifact of social arrangement, not a natural fact.

The passage at 002-019 is the linchpin of this argument. Women of "strong minds" do attain knowledge, but it is "of a more desultory kind than the knowledge of men" because it is acquired "more by sheer observations on real life, than from comparing what has been individually observed with the results of experience generalized by speculation." The deficit is not in women's capacity for observation — women are, if anything, forced into excessive attention to particulars by their domestic confinement — but in their access to the speculative frameworks that make observation intellectually productive.

The political implication is clear. If "experience generalized by speculation" (002-019) is the method of genuine knowledge, and if this method is taught exclusively to men, then the intellectual inequality between the sexes is produced and reproduced by education itself. Wollstonecraft's remedy — rational education for women — is not merely about adding accomplishments but about granting women access to the speculative method that is the foundation of all genuine knowledge.

The relationship between speculation and virtue is also significant. "Virtue is not to be acquired even by speculation, much less by the negative supineness that wealth naturally generates" (009-024). Speculation can yield knowledge of virtue, but virtue itself requires practice. This is not a dismissal of speculation but a recognition of its proper scope. Speculation provides the knowledge; practice provides the virtue. Neither is complete without the other.

The passage at 004-078 deploys speculation in a different register: "to nature apparently reasonable speculations must yield." Here speculation confronts its limit — the facts of nature — and must yield when the evidence contradicts the theory. This is not a weakness of speculation but its strength: properly conducted speculation is self-correcting, always subject to the tribunal of experience. The speculative mind does not cling to its theories in the face of contrary evidence but revises them in light of new observations.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

In the empiricist tradition of Locke and Hume, "speculation" was often distrusted as a source of error — the "speculative" philosopher was one who built systems without adequate empirical foundation. The emphasis was on experience as the source of all knowledge, with speculation treated as a potentially dangerous supplement.

Wollstonecraft's usage is closer to the rationalist tradition, in which speculation — the operation of reason on the materials provided by experience — is essential to the acquisition of knowledge. She does not reject empiricism — her insistence on "experience generalized by speculation" (002-019) grants experience a foundational role — but she insists that experience without speculation is intellectually sterile.

The educational implications are profound. The empiricist tradition, with its distrust of speculation, could be used to justify an education for women that confined them to practical accomplishments and concrete observations. Wollstonecraft's insistence on the necessity of speculation is thus an insistence on women's access to abstract thought — to philosophy, to science, to the generalising operations of reason that transform observation into understanding.

## Key Passages

> The little knowledge which women of strong minds attain, is, from various circumstances, of a more desultory kind than the knowledge of men, and it is acquired more by sheer observations on real life, than from comparing what has been individually observed with the results of experience generalized by speculation. (002-019)

> Where is then the sexual difference, when the education has been the same; all the difference that I can discern, arises from the superior advantage of liberty which enables the former to see more of life. (002-022)

> This appears to be an indication of nature, and to nature apparently reasonable speculations must yield. (004-078)

> Virtue is not to be acquired even by speculation, much less by the negative supineness that wealth naturally generates. (009-024)
