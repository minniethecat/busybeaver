# National Education

## Definition

**National education** is Wollstonecraft's central policy proposal for reforming the social and moral condition of both sexes. She argues that private education will always be insufficient because its effects "will ever be very confined" [012-002] -- only when "education becomes a grand national concern" [012-002] can society hope to produce virtuous citizens. Her proposal goes beyond mere schooling to envision a complete restructuring of how children are prepared for adult life: state-funded day schools where children of both sexes and all classes learn together on terms of equality.

The national education plan is distinguished by several interlocking features. It must be publicly funded so that schoolmasters are "not dependent on the caprice of parents" [012-026]; it must be coeducational so that "boys and girls might be educated together" [012-044]; it must mix social classes so that "the rich and poor should meet together" [012-046]; and it must be day-school-based so that children remain connected to their families and domestic affections. Each of these features serves Wollstonecraft's broader goal of forming citizens whose private virtues support public ones.

## In Context

Chapter XII, titled "On National Education," is the primary locus of this concept. Wollstonecraft opens by arguing that private education cannot achieve the moral transformation she seeks -- it is too confined, too dependent on individual parents, and incapable of producing the social affections that come from mixing children together. She contrasts the ideal of national education with the existing system of boarding schools, which she condemns as "hotbeds of vice and folly" [012-005].

The policy details emerge in the latter part of the chapter. Wollstonecraft proposes day schools for children from five to nine years of age that would be "absolutely free and open to all classes" [012-044], with a curriculum including reading, writing, arithmetic, natural history, and "some simple experiments in natural philosophy" [012-046], alongside gymnastics and outdoor play. After age nine, children would be streamed by aptitude and destination, but the two sexes would continue to study together in the mornings. Her observations on national education are offered as "obviously hints" [012-067], acknowledging the preliminary nature of her proposals while insisting on their importance.

The argument connects national education to the condition of women in particular. Wollstonecraft contends that "the national education of women is of the utmost consequence" [012-079] because women as mothers shape the character of the rising generation. Without enlightened female citizens formed by a national system, no lasting moral improvement is possible.

## Key Passages

> "The good effects resulting from attention to private education will ever be very confined, and the parent who really puts his own hand to the plow, will always, in some degree be disappointed, till education becomes a grand national concern." [012-002]

> "To render this practicable, day schools for particular ages should be established by government, in which boys and girls might be educated together." [012-044]

> "But nothing of this kind could occur in an elementary day-school, where boys and girls, the rich and poor, should meet together." [012-046]

> "My observations on national education are obviously hints; but I principally wish to enforce the necessity of educating the sexes together to perfect both." [012-067]

> "Were only on this account, the national education of women is of the utmost consequence." [012-079]
