# National Education

Wollstonecraft's proposal for a state-organized system of public day schools in which boys and girls would be educated together, developed most fully in Chapter 12. The concept represents the institutional culmination of her argument: if women are to become rational companions to men and competent mothers to children, education must be publicly provided, universally accessible, and conducted on equal terms between the sexes.

## Definition

National education, as Wollstonecraft conceives it, is a publicly funded and universally accessible system of day schools that children of both sexes attend while living at home. It is "national" in three senses: it is organized by the state rather than by private or ecclesiastical bodies; it serves the entire nation regardless of class or sex; and its purpose is the formation of citizens capable of sustaining a just national life.

The concept is introduced in the Dedication, where Wollstonecraft addresses Talleyrand-Périgord, whose pamphlet on national education had proposed a sharply limited curriculum for girls. Wollstonecraft dedicates her entire volume to him, framing the *Vindication* as a direct response to his educational proposals: "Having read with great pleasure a pamphlet, which you have lately published, on National Education, I dedicate this volume to you" (P02-002). The dedication is at once respectful and combative — she honors his interest in education while challenging his exclusion of women from its full benefits.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument for national education develops across the entire *Vindication* before reaching its full expression in Chapter 12. The early chapters establish the diagnosis: women are intellectually and morally stunted by an education that teaches them only to please. The middle chapters trace the consequences across domains of life — marriage, motherhood, social relations. Chapter 12 then presents the remedy: a national system that educates both sexes together, on equal terms, in publicly supported day schools.

The core of the proposal is coeducation. "I principally wish to enforce the necessity of educating the sexes together to perfect both" (012-076), she writes. The argument is both moral and practical. Morally, separation breeds mutual ignorance and the distorted views each sex forms of the other. Practically, children who learn together develop the habits of equality that make just social relations possible. "Only by the jostlings of equality can we form a just opinion of ourselves" (012-076) — the phrase captures her conviction that character is formed in social interaction, not in isolation.

The day-school model is equally deliberate. Children should "sleep at home, that they may learn to love home" (012-076), preserving domestic affections while benefiting from public instruction. The combination of public education and domestic life resolves the dilemma she identified earlier: boarding schools sever children from family feeling, while purely domestic education leaves them without the intellectual stimulation and social equality that only a school of equals can provide.

Wollstonecraft extends the scope of national education beyond the academic curriculum to include moral formation. "Humanity to animals should be particularly inculcated as a part of national education, for it is not at present one of our national virtues" (012-069). The detail is revealing: national education is not merely about literacy and numeracy but about forming the moral sensibilities of a people. Kindness to animals, like kindness to women, is a virtue that a properly organized state should actively cultivate.

She is modest about the specificity of her proposals. "My observations on national education are obviously hints" (012-076), she concedes, positioning herself not as an expert legislator but as a moral critic offering principles for reform. Yet the principles themselves are radical: publicly funded, universally accessible, coeducational, integrated with family life, and oriented toward the formation of citizens rather than the reproduction of social hierarchy. No contemporary European state had implemented anything comparable.

The argument closes with urgency. "If it were only on this account, the national education of women is of the utmost consequence; for what a number of human sacrifices are made to that moloch, prejudice!" (012-089). The image of Moloch — the Canaanite god to whom children were sacrificed — is among the most forceful in the entire *Vindication*. Prejudice, sustained by the denial of education to women, consumes its victims generation after generation. National education is the only way to break the cycle.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Talleyrand-Périgord's 1791 *Rapport sur l'instruction publique* had proposed a national system of education but recommended that girls receive only a domestic curriculum — reading, writing, and household management — on the grounds that women's lives were properly confined to the home. Wollstonecraft's dedication to Talleyrand is a direct challenge to this exclusion. She does not oppose national education; she insists that it must be genuinely national, embracing both sexes on equal terms.

Rousseau's *Émile* had offered the most influential educational philosophy of the century, but it was organized around a single male pupil with a private tutor. Sophie, the female counterpart, received an entirely different education oriented toward pleasing Émile. Wollstonecraft's proposal for coeducational public schools implicitly rejects every premise of Rousseau's model: education must be public, not private; equal, not differentiated by sex; oriented toward citizenship, not toward the performance of gender roles.

## Key Passages

> Having read with great pleasure a pamphlet, which you have lately published, on National Education, I dedicate this volume to you, the first dedication that I have ever written, to induce you to read it with attention; and, because I think that you will understand me, which I do not suppose many pert witlings will, who may ridicule the arguments they are unable to answer. (P02-002)

> I principally wish to enforce the necessity of educating the sexes together to perfect both, and of making children sleep at home, that they may learn to love home; yet to make private support instead of smothering public affections, they should be sent to school to mix with a number of equals, for only by the jostlings of equality can we form a just opinion of ourselves. (012-076)

> Humanity to animals should be particularly inculcated as a part of national education, for it is not at present one of our national virtues. (012-069)

> Indeed, if it were only on this account, the national education of women is of the utmost consequence; for what a number of human sacrifices are made to that moloch, prejudice! (012-089)
