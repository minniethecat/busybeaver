The condition of unfreedom that Wollstonecraft uses as her governing metaphor for women's subjection, operating across political, domestic, and psychological registers. She draws on the language of abolitionist discourse to argue that the "slavery" of women — though distinct from chattel slavery — shares its essential structure: the denial of rights, the degradation of character, and the internalisation of subordination that makes the enslaved complicit in their own subjection. "Slavery of any kind is unfavourable to virtue" (004-101), she insists, and the emancipation she demands is simultaneously political, educational, and psychological.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft deploys "slavery" as a polemical term that gains its force from the late eighteenth-century debates over the abolition of the slave trade. By describing women as "convenient slaves" (P02-014) and marriage as a form of "slavery" (011-014), she appropriates the moral urgency of abolitionism and redirects it toward the condition of women. Yet her usage is not merely rhetorical. She identifies the structural features that women's subjection shares with chattel slavery: the denial of legal personhood, the restriction of freedom of movement and association, the education that trains the enslaved to accept their condition as natural, and the degradation of character that results from having no control over one's own life.

The term appears across the full range of her argument: "splendid slavery" (001-011) describes the gilded cage of aristocratic women who have traded freedom for luxury; "habitual slavery, to first impressions" (006-005) describes the psychological condition of minds trained never to reason; the "slavery of marriage" (011-014) describes the legal and economic subordination of wives; and the "more specious slavery which chains the very soul" (009-013) describes the internalised subjection that persists even when external constraints are loosened.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument proceeds from diagnosis to remedy. The diagnosis is that women are enslaved not primarily by force but by a system of education and social expectation that makes them "voluntary slaves" (004-101). They are "taught from their infancy that beauty is woman's sceptre" (003-025) and trained to value the approval of men above their own rational development. The result is a form of slavery more insidious than the physical kind because the enslaved do not recognise their condition: they mistake the chains of fashion and opinion for ornaments freely chosen.

The remedy is education that emancipates the mind. "Taught from their infancy, that beauty is woman's sceptre, the mind shapes itself to the body, and, roaming round its gilt cage, only seeks to adorn its prison" (003-025). The metaphor of the gilded cage captures the paradox of women's condition: they appear free, even privileged, yet their freedom is entirely circumscribed by male approval. To break free, women must be educated to reason, because only reason can expose the irrationality of submitting to arbitrary power — whether the power of a despot, a husband, or an opinion.

Wollstonecraft is careful to distinguish her argument from a simple call for rebellion. The emancipation she envisions is not the overthrow of all authority but the replacement of arbitrary authority with rational authority — authority founded on reason and mutual respect rather than on force and custom. "Slaves" who have been educated only to obey cannot simply be set free; they must first be educated to govern themselves. This is why education, rather than political agitation, is her primary instrument of emancipation.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Rousseau had famously declared in *The Social Contract* that "man is born free, and everywhere he is in chains" — a formulation that Wollstonecraft both echoes and subverts. Where Rousseau's chains are political and his remedy is the general will, Wollstonecraft insists that the chains binding women are domestic and psychological as well as political, and that no political remedy that excludes half the human race can be legitimate. The Jacobin rhetoric of liberty and the abolitionist rhetoric of emancipation are, in her hands, turned back upon the revolutionaries themselves: if they are sincere in their opposition to slavery, they must extend that opposition to the slavery practised in their own households.

## Key Passages

> Taught from their infancy, that beauty is woman's sceptre, the mind shapes itself to the body, and, roaming round its gilt cage, only seeks to adorn its prison. (003-025)

> Women are voluntary slaves — slavery of any kind is unfavourable to virtue. A being, who is in a state of dependence, is necessarily enfeebled, because his will is subjected to the will of another. (004-101)

> This habitual slavery, to first impressions, has a more baneful effect on the female than the male character, because as girls are left more to themselves, the desultory manner in which their minds are employed, tends to strengthen the prejudices that arise from first impressions. (006-005)
