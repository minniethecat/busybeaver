# Voluptuary

Wollstonecraft uses "voluptuary" and its cognates to designate a specific moral type: the person — typically, though not exclusively, a man — whose character has been corrupted by the habitual pursuit of sensual pleasure. The voluptuary is not merely someone who enjoys pleasure (Wollstonecraft is no ascetic) but someone for whom pleasure has become the organizing principle of life, displacing reason, duty, and the regard for others that virtue requires. In the *Vindication*, the figure of the voluptuary serves as a diagnostic tool for understanding how male libertinism degrades both the men who practise it and the women who are its objects.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft defines the voluptuary by the narrowing of his moral horizon. "The man who can be contented to live with a pretty, useful companion, without a mind," she writes, "has lost in voluptuous gratifications a taste for more refined enjoyments" (005-033). The key word is "lost": the voluptuary is not someone who was born incapable of higher pleasures but someone whose indulgence in lower pleasures has atrophied his capacity for anything better. The process is self-reinforcing — the more one indulges, the less one is capable of the rational enjoyments that would make indulgence unnecessary.

The concept has a specifically gendered dimension. Wollstonecraft argues that the sexual double standard — which condemns female unchastity while winking at male libertinism — creates a class of male voluptuaries whose habits corrupt the institution of marriage. "All the causes of female weakness, as well as depravity, which I have already enlarged upon, branch out of one grand cause — want of chastity in men" (004-050). The voluptuary is not a peripheral figure in Wollstonecraft's social analysis but the hidden cause of much of the misery she documents: the neglected wife, the seduced woman, the children raised without moral guidance, all trace their suffering to the voluptuary's pursuit of pleasure without responsibility.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's treatment of the voluptuary is distinctive in locating the harm of libertinism not primarily in the damage it does to the libertine's soul (though this is real) but in the damage it does to the social fabric. The voluptuary "spreads depravity and misery" (008-031) not because he is uniquely wicked but because his habits are socially sanctioned. Society condemns the fallen woman while tolerating the man who caused her fall — a hypocrisy that Wollstonecraft anatomizes with controlled fury.

The economic dimension is important. The voluptuary's pursuit of pleasure requires resources, and the marriage market is one of the mechanisms by which those resources are secured. A man of fortune may pursue "voluptuous gratifications" outside marriage while demanding that his wife remain "chaste" — a demand that is, in Wollstonecraft's analysis, inseparable from the economic dependence that makes wives the property of their husbands. The critique of the voluptuary thus connects the moral psychology of pleasure to the political economy of marriage.

The effects on women are Wollstonecraft's central concern. Women who are "considered as the property of men" and valued only for their physical charms become, in effect, the instruments of male voluptuousness. "Woman, however, a slave in every situation to prejudice, seldom exerts enlightened maternal affection" (010-006) — and one reason she does not is that the man who should be her companion has been formed by a culture of voluptuousness that makes genuine companionship impossible. The voluptuary who has "lost a taste for more refined enjoyments" cannot value a wife for her mind, because his own mind has been narrowed to the dimensions of his appetites.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The critique of libertinism was a common theme in eighteenth-century moral literature, but it typically took the form of cautionary tales about the ruin of young women seduced by aristocratic rakes. Wollstonecraft's analysis is structurally different: she is less interested in individual seduction narratives than in the systemic conditions that produce and license male voluptuousness. The problem is not that some men are rakes but that the entire culture of relations between the sexes is organized around the assumption that male pleasure is primary and female chastity is its necessary safeguard.

Rousseau, in *Émile*, had warned against the corrupting effects of "voluptuousness" on male character, but his remedy — a natural education that delays and disciplines the sexual passions — was designed for boys alone. Sophie's education, by contrast, was organized around the management of male desire through modesty and chastity. Wollstonecraft's critique of the voluptuary is, in effect, a critique of the Rousseauian division of moral labour: if men are to be cured of voluptuousness, women must be educated as rational beings capable of companionship, not as guardians of a chastity that men themselves refuse to practise.

## Key Passages

> The man who can be contented to live with a pretty, useful companion, without a mind, has lost in voluptuous gratifications a taste for more refined enjoyments. (005-033)

> All the causes of female weakness, as well as depravity, which I have already enlarged upon, branch out of one grand cause — want of chastity in men. This intemperance, so contemptible, even when it is not attended with injustice, gives a tyrannical tendency to the male character, and renders woman the creature of sensation. (004-050)

> He who has rioted among women, spreading depravity and misery, when he wishes to marry, looks out for a chaste partner, to transmit his name to posterity. (008-031)
