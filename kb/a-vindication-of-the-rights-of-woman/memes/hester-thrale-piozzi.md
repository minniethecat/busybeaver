# Hester Thrale Piozzi

## Overview

Hester Thrale Piozzi (1741–1821), born Hester Lynch Salusbury, was a British author, diarist, and literary hostess best known for her close association with Samuel Johnson and her published anecdotes of his life. In *A Vindication of the Rights of Woman*, Wollstonecraft singles her out at [005-118] as one of the women who "repeated by rote what she did not understand" and who "comes forward with Johnsonian periods" — pompous, second-hand moral pronouncements delivered in a borrowed style. Wollstonecraft's critique of Piozzi is sharper than her critique of de Stael: where de Stael is treated as a victim of sensibility, Piozzi is treated as a willing mouthpiece of masculine authority whose advice to women is actively harmful.

## In the Text

Wollstonecraft introduces Piozzi at [005-118] as the first of her illustrative "examples" of women who adopt and amplify the sentiments that "brutalize them." The language is deliberately dismissive: Piozzi is accused of repeating by rote what she does not understand, and her "Johnsonian periods" — a reference to the ponderous Latinate style of Samuel Johnson — suggest borrowed authority rather than independent thought.

The passage Wollstonecraft quotes ([005-119]) is damning in its content. Piozzi advises a newly married man that his wife "will pardon an affront to her understanding much sooner than one to her person" and that "all our attainments, all our arts, are employed to gain and keep the heart of man." The advice reduces women to strategic manipulators whose only currency is physical attractiveness and whose only goal is male approval.

Wollstonecraft's counter-argument is direct. These are "true masculine sentiments" — the phrase is laced with irony, since Piozzi is a woman, but her thinking has been so thoroughly colonized by male perspectives that she voices them as her own. Wollstonecraft then dismantles the logic: if a woman's person is her only claim to attention, and her person inevitably fades, then the advice to seek amends through the attention of other men when neglected by her husband is a prescription for infidelity built on a false premise. Genuine virtue, Wollstonecraft insists, must rest on reason and understanding — qualities Piozzi's advice actively discourages women from cultivating.

## Key Quotations

> "I must illustrate my assertion by a few examples. Mrs. Piozzi, who often repeated by rote, what she did not understand, comes forward with Johnsonian periods." [005-118]

> "'Seek not for happiness in singularity; and dread a refinement of wisdom as a deviation into folly.' Thus she dogmatically addresses a new married man..." [005-119]

> "'All our attainments, all our arts, are employed to gain and keep the heart of man; and what mortification can exceed the disappointment, if the end be not obtained...'" [005-119]

> "These are true masculine sentiments. 'All our arts are employed to gain and keep the heart of man:'—and what is the inference?—if her person... be neglected, she will make herself amends by endeavouring to please other men. Noble morality!" [005-120]

## Biographical Summary

Hester Thrale Piozzi was born in Bodvel, Wales, and married the wealthy brewer Henry Thrale in 1763. Their home at Streatham Park became a centre of London literary life, most notably through her close friendship with Samuel Johnson, who lived for extended periods with the Thrales. After her husband's death in 1781, she married Gabriel Piozzi, an Italian musician, causing a scandal and a permanent rupture with Johnson. She published *Anecdotes of the Late Samuel Johnson* in 1786 and her *Letters to and from the Late Samuel Johnson* in 1788. By 1792, she was a well-known literary figure, exactly the kind of female authority Wollstonecraft felt compelled to challenge.
