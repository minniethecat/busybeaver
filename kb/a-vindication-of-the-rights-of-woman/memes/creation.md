# Creation

The theological and philosophical concept of the natural order that Wollstonecraft critically examines, challenging the claim that women were "created for man" (002-034) and asserting instead that rational capacity—not sexual function—defines human beings' place in creation.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft engages the concept of creation at the intersection of theology, natural philosophy, and gender politics. The term appears throughout the *Vindication* in three interrelated senses.

First, the created order of nature, which establishes humanity's place above "the brute creation" (001-002). Second, the specific claim that woman was created subordinate to man, which Wollstonecraft subjects to rational scrutiny. Third, the broader providential order within which human beings exercise moral agency.

Her opening question—"In what does man's pre-eminence over the brute creation consist?" (001-002)—establishes reason as the defining human characteristic. From this premise, the subordinate creation of women cannot logically follow: if reason defines humanity's elevation above animals, and women possess reason, then women cannot be consigned to a lower rung of the created order.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument concerning creation is a carefully constructed theological-rational critique. She acknowledges that "the prevailing opinion, that woman was created for man, may have taken its rise from Moses's poetical story" (002-034).

Yet she immediately observes that "very few ... who have bestowed any serious thought on the subject, ever supposed that Eve was, literally speaking, one of Adam's ribs." The Genesis account, she implies, is poetry not science.

Having softened the literalist foundation of female subordination, Wollstonecraft mounts a positive argument from the nature of creation itself. If God is rational and benevolent, then the created order must reflect rational benevolence—and a system that denies half of humanity the cultivation of its highest faculty cannot be of divine origin.

She invokes the scriptural precept "be pure as your heavenly father is pure" (003-041) to argue that moral standards are universal, not sex-specific. The claim that women should be denied education because it might "disturb the order established in creation" (013-010) is a self-serving rationalisation of male privilege.

Wollstonecraft extends her argument to the natural world, observing that Nature "has so wisely ordered things" (013-068) that women's bodily capacities would, if honoured rather than suppressed by fashion, naturally space births and preserve maternal health.

When she laments that women have been reduced from "the pride, and ornament of creation" to "a toy" (P01-013), she insists that creation's true order has been inverted by artificial social arrangements.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The dominant theological anthropology of Wollstonecraft's time treated female subordination as a feature of the created order. Woman's auxiliary role was divinely ordained; to challenge it was to challenge God. Wollstonecraft's strategy is not to reject creation theology wholesale but to reinterpret it through the lens of rational religion.

She treats reason—not biblical literalism—as the arbiter of creation's order. This aligns her with the rational Dissenting tradition that emphasised individual conscience, while distinguishing her from more radical critics of Christianity.

## Key Passages

> In what does man's pre-eminence over the brute creation consist? The answer is as clear as that a half is less than the whole; in Reason. (001-002)

> Probably the prevailing opinion, that woman was created for man, may have taken its rise from Moses's poetical story; yet, as very few it is presumed, who have bestowed any serious thought on the subject, ever supposed that Eve was, literally speaking, one of Adam's ribs, the deduction must be allowed to fall to the ground. (002-034)

> It is time to separate unchangeable morals from local manners. If men be demi-gods, why let us serve them! And if the dignity of the female soul be as disputable as that of animals, if their reason does not afford sufficient light to direct their conduct whilst unerring instinct is denied, they are surely of all creatures the most miserable. (003-031)

> This objection has, I grant, some force; but while such a sublime precept exists, as, "be pure as your heavenly father is pure;" it would seem that the virtues of man are not limited by the Being who alone could limit them; and that he may press forward without considering whether he steps out of his sphere. (003-041)

> That it would be absolutely inconsistent with the grand purpose of life, that of rendering human creatures wise and virtuous: and that, were it sanctioned by God, it would disturb the order established in creation. (013-010)
