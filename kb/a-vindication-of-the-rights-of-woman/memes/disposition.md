**Disposition** in Wollstonecraft's lexicon denotes the native temper or cast of character — the emotional and moral tendencies that shape a person's responses to the world. It is a concept she inherits from eighteenth-century moral philosophy and the conduct-book tradition, but which she subjects to radical critique. Where the conduct books treat women's "mild disposition" (005-018) as a natural given to be cultivated through restraint, Wollstonecraft argues that disposition is shaped — and deformed — by social conditions, and that genuine moral character requires the exercise of reason, not the suppression of temperament.

The concept appears across the work in several registers: as a quality attributed to women by the conduct-book writers she critiques, as a component of genuine virtue when shaped by reason, and as a site of ideological contestation over what women "naturally" are. Wollstonecraft's treatment of disposition is thus a case study in her method of taking a conventional moral category and revealing its ideological function.

## Definition

Disposition in the eighteenth-century vocabulary referred to the innate or acquired temper of a person — their characteristic emotional orientation, the "bent" of their nature. It was distinguished from both passion (transient emotion) and principle (rationally adopted rule of conduct). A person's disposition was what they were "disposed" to feel and do, prior to the intervention of reason or the restraint of convention.

Wollstonecraft's use of the term is strategically ambivalent. When she invokes "the triumphs of good dispositions" (002-028), she appears to endorse the conventional valuation of disposition as a moral quality. But the context is ironic: the "good dispositions" she describes are those that Rousseau attributes to his fictional Sophie — dispositions that are the product of a carefully managed education designed to produce precisely the "mildness" that the conduct books prescribe. The "triumph" is not of nature but of artifice.

The critical treatment is most explicit in the discussion of Gregory's advice. Gregory recommends "that she should be of a mild disposition" (005-018), treating disposition as a quality that can be prescribed and cultivated. Wollstonecraft's response is to ask what happens when "mildness of disposition" (005-020) is enforced at the expense of justice: "The being who patiently endures injustice, and silently bears insults, will soon become unjust, or unable to discern right from wrong." Disposition, in this analysis, is not a fixed quality but a product of the conditions under which a person lives.

The alternative conception emerges in the discussion of temper. "People of sensibility have seldom good tempers" (005-020) — here "temper" is closely related to disposition, and Wollstonecraft argues that genuine good temper is the product of a mind that is "occupied by pursuits that interest the head as well as the heart." Disposition, properly understood, is not a passive quality of submission but the active expression of a well-regulated mind.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

The treatment of disposition is integral to Wollstonecraft's argument that what passes for female virtue is, in large part, the product of constraint. The "mild disposition" (005-018) that Gregory prescribes is not a moral achievement but an adaptation to subjection — the "tractableness" of the slave internalised as character.

Wollstonecraft's counter-argument proceeds in two moves. First, she denies that women are naturally of any particular disposition. The "being gentle, whose natural disposition admitted not of such a fine polish" (002-069) suggests that disposition varies across individuals, and that the attempt to impose a uniform "mildness" on all women is both futile and harmful. Second, she argues that genuine good disposition — as opposed to enforced tractability — is the product of liberty and reason. Men "have better tempers than women, because they are occupied by pursuits that interest the head as well as the heart; and the steadiness of the head gives a healthy temperature to the heart" (005-020).

The political implication is radical. If disposition is shaped by the conditions of life — by liberty, by education, by the scope of one's pursuits — then the "mild disposition" of women is not a natural gift but an artifact of oppression. To prescribe mildness is to prescribe subjection. To cultivate genuine virtue requires not the restraint of disposition but the liberation of the conditions under which disposition forms.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The conduct-book tradition — Gregory, Fordyce, Rousseau — treated disposition as a gendered quality. Women were naturally of a "mild disposition"; the task of education was to preserve and refine this natural quality, protecting it from the corruptions of the world. Men's dispositions were more various, requiring different forms of cultivation, but the gender distinction was assumed to be natural.

Wollstonecraft's intervention is to historicise and politicise this assumption. The "mild disposition" of women is not natural but produced — by an education that teaches submission, by a social order that rewards compliance, by the denial of the liberty that would allow disposition to develop in response to genuine moral demands. The conduct books' prescription of mildness is thus a prescription for the perpetuation of subjection.

The contrast with Rousseau is particularly instructive. Rousseau's Sophie is the embodiment of female disposition — gentle, compliant, shaped entirely by her relation to Émile. Wollstonecraft's response is not to propose an alternative female disposition but to deny that disposition should be gendered at all. The qualities that make a good disposition — "the steadiness of the head" that "gives a healthy temperature to the heart" (005-020) — are human qualities, not male or female ones.
