**Justice** is, for Wollstonecraft, the master virtue of the political order — the principle by which all social arrangements, from the constitution of the state to the structure of the family, must be judged. Her celebrated declaration that "it is justice, not charity, that is wanting in the world" (004-081) encapsulates a central argument of the *Vindication*: women do not need benevolence from their superiors; they need the recognition of their rights as equals.

The distinction between justice and charity is foundational. Charity is the virtue of the superior who condescends to the inferior; justice is the obligation of equals to one another. To offer women charity — protection, provision, chivalrous regard — while denying them justice is to confirm their subordination while concealing it under the language of benevolence.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft draws on multiple traditions in her treatment of justice. From the classical and Christian traditions, she inherits the conception of justice as rendering to each what is due — the *suum cuique* of Roman law, the "justice" of the Hebrew prophets who demand that the powerful "do justice, love mercy, and walk humbly" (003-036).

From the Enlightenment, she inherits the conception of justice as the rational foundation of political order — the standard by which laws and institutions are judged legitimate or tyrannical. "If the submission demanded be founded on justice — there is no appealing to a higher power — for God is justice itself" (005-095). The conditional is crucial: submission is only obligatory when it is founded on justice; submission to injustice is not a duty but a corruption.

Wollstonecraft also develops a distinctively gendered conception of justice. The "injustice" she anatomises is not the abstract injustice of philosophers but the concrete, lived injustice of women who are "broken off from society" (004-081), denied education, excluded from citizenship, and reduced to dependence. Justice, in her hands, becomes a feminist principle — the demand that women be included in the community of those to whom obligations are owed.

The theological dimension is not incidental. "God is justice itself" (005-095) — the identification of God with justice means that injustice is not merely a political error but a violation of the divine order. The "angry brow of offended justice" (008-018) is the face of a God who will not indefinitely tolerate the oppression of half of humanity.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument for justice proceeds through a series of linked claims. First, justice is owed to all rational beings — and women are rational beings. The denial of justice to women is therefore not a legitimate exercise of authority but "injustice and inconsistency" (P02-015) — a logical contradiction as well as a moral wrong.

Second, the existing social order is founded on "injustice" rather than justice. The "infamous arts" (005-110) by which fathers control their daughters, the marriage market that reduces women to commodities, the legal doctrine that extinguishes a married woman's independent existence — all these are instances of "justice set at defiance" (005-110) in the name of custom and convenience.

Third, women are taught to "suffer injustice, and to bear the insults of a man" (005-018) as though this were a virtue rather than a degradation. The "being who patiently endures injustice, and silently bears insults, will soon become unjust, or unable to discern right from wrong" (005-020). Submission to injustice does not cultivate virtue; it destroys the very capacity for moral judgment on which virtue depends.

Fourth, the remedy for injustice is not charity but rights. "It is justice, not charity, that is wanting in the world" (004-081) — a sentence that encapsulates Wollstonecraft's entire political philosophy. Charity is a gift from the powerful to the powerless; justice is the recognition of what is owed. Women do not need men's benevolence; they need their own rights.

The argument culminates in a theological warning. "On what principle religion or morality rest, when justice is thus set at defiance?" (005-110). A society that systematically denies justice to women has severed the connection between its professed values and its actual practices. The "injustice of man" (008-016) will be answered by the "offended justice" of God — but Wollstonecraft's primary concern is not the afterlife but the reform of this world.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft's treatment of justice distinguishes her from the dominant traditions of eighteenth-century moral philosophy. Against the utilitarian calculus of Helvétius and Bentham — for whom justice was a matter of maximising aggregate happiness — she insists on the priority of rights: certain claims are due to persons regardless of the consequences of honouring them.

Against the Scottish sentimentalists — Hutcheson, Hume, Smith — who grounded justice in the sentiment of approbation, she insists on its rational foundation. Justice is not a matter of feeling but of "principle," and the capacity to recognise it depends on the cultivation of reason, not the refinement of sentiment.

The most pointed contrast is with the Christian charity tradition, which had long urged the powerful to treat the powerless with kindness while leaving the structures of power intact. Wollstonecraft's insistence on "justice, not charity" is a direct challenge to this tradition: benevolence without justice is complicity in oppression.

## Key Passages

> It is justice, not charity, that is wanting in the world. (004-081)

> If the submission demanded be founded on justice — there is no appealing to a higher power — for God is justice itself. Let us then, as children of the same parent, if not bastardized by being the younger, reason together. (005-095)

> On what principle religion or morality rest, when justice is thus set at defiance? (005-110)

> Of what materials can that heart be composed, which can melt when insulted, and instead of revolting at injustice, kiss the rod? (005-021)
