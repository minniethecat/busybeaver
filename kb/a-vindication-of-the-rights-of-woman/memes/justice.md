# Justice

## Definition

**Justice** in Wollstonecraft's argument is not a legal or procedural concept but a moral demand grounded in the nature of human beings as [[reason|rational]] creatures. Her central claim — that women are entitled to justice, not merely to the indulgence of men — represents a fundamental challenge to the gallantry-based model of gender relations that dominated eighteenth-century discourse.

The word "justice" appears at the climactic moment of the Dedication: Wollstonecraft "loudly demands *justice* for one half of the human race" [P02-018]. The emphasis is hers — the italics underscore that what women need is not protection, not praise, not sentimental regard, but the rigorous moral recognition that only justice provides.

> "…loudly demands *justice* for one half of the human race." (P02-018)

This demand for justice is inseparable from Wollstonecraft's claim that women possess [[reason]]. If women are rational beings, then they are moral beings; if they are moral beings, then they are entitled to justice. The denial of justice to women is thus not merely a political failure but a philosophical contradiction — an admission that the society claiming to be built on [[rights]] is in fact built on force.

## In Context

### Justice and Endurance

Wollstonecraft is sharply critical of the way women are praised for enduring injustice rather than resisting it. She describes the model woman of conventional morality as "the being who patiently endures injustice, and silently bears insults" [005-026], and asks whether such endurance is really a [[virtue]] or merely a symptom of degradation.

> "…the being who patiently endures injustice, and silently bears insults, will soon become unjust, or unable to discern right from wrong." (005-026)

The danger of habituating women to injustice is that it corrupts their moral perception. A person who is taught to accept unfair treatment as normal loses the capacity to recognize unfairness, and this moral blindness is then transmitted to the next generation through the education of children.

### Natural Justice

Wollstonecraft appeals to "natural justice" as a standard against which positive law and social custom must be measured. She notes that those who base their arguments on revelation "recur to natural justice" when it suits them [010-003], suggesting that the concept of justice accessible to unaided [[reason]] is universally acknowledged even by those who claim a higher authority.

> "…subjects that recur to natural justice: because they firmly believe, that there is no virtue founded on utility…" (010-003)

The appeal to natural justice is part of Wollstonecraft's broader strategy of arguing from principles her opponents already accept. If justice is universally binding, and if women are human beings, then justice applies to women — and any social arrangement that denies women justice is by definition unjust.

### Divine Justice

Wollstonecraft invokes divine justice as the ultimate tribunal before which human injustice will be judged. When women appeal "to God from the injustice of man" [008-015], they are not merely expressing pious hope but making a philosophical claim: human laws that contradict divine justice — and divine justice demands the equal treatment of all rational creatures — are illegitimate.

> "…must appeal to God from the injustice of man; and amidst the whining of the self-sufficient, the angry brow of offended justice will be fearfully deprecated…" (008-015)

This theological dimension of Wollstonecraft's argument for justice anticipates her broader insistence that [[virtue]] has an "eternal foundation" and that moral distinctions are not merely matters of social convention.
