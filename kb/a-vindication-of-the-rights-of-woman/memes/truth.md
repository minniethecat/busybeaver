**Truth** is, for Wollstonecraft, the ultimate standard of both knowledge and conduct — the "sacred majesty" (002-060) that must govern every rational being's judgments and actions. She invokes truth throughout the *Vindication* as the antithesis of the artifice, deception, and "false refinement" that characterise the conventional education of women. "I follow truth," she declares, "and still adhering to my first position, I will not depart from it" (003-006).

Wollstonecraft's treatment of truth is at once epistemological, moral, and political. Epistemologically, truth is what reason discovers when it exercises its faculties without the distortions of prejudice, passion, or authority. Morally, truthfulness — speaking "the simple language of truth" (002-039) — is a virtue that women are systematically discouraged from cultivating. Politically, the suppression of truth about women's capacities and condition serves to maintain the existing order of gender subordination.

## Definition

In Wollstonecraft's usage, truth has the simplicity and universality that characterise Enlightenment conceptions of rational knowledge. "Truth, as it is a simple principle, is only to be acquired by the pursuit of simplicity of character" (002-080). The emphasis on simplicity is deliberate: truth is not the product of elaborate systems or learned disputation but of the direct, honest exercise of reason on experience.

The simplicity of truth is contrasted with the artifice that pervades women's education. "Why must the sacred majesty of truth be violated to detain a deceitful good?" (002-060). The question is rhetorical: there is no justification for sacrificing truth to the pursuit of male approbation, yet this is precisely what women are taught to do from childhood.

Wollstonecraft also connects truth to moral character. A person who habitually dissembles — and women, in her analysis, are trained to dissemble from infancy — loses the capacity to recognise truth. "But what a weak barrier is truth when it stands in the way of an hypothesis!" (003-017). The observation is directed at philosophers who sacrifice truth to system-building, but it applies equally to the ordinary deceptions of social life.

The theological dimension is present but subdued. Truth is one of the attributes of God — "the fountain of wisdom, goodness, and power" (003-036) — and to love truth is to love God. But Wollstonecraft's primary emphasis is on the this-worldly consequences of truth-telling and truth-seeking: only those who pursue truth can achieve virtue, and only those who speak truth can participate in genuine human community.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument about truth operates on several levels simultaneously, weaving together claims about knowledge, character, education, and social reform.

The epistemological argument: truth is accessible to reason, but reason must be cultivated through education. "Every individual is in this world a proper judge of truth, if he will but exert his reason" (004-004) — the conditional is crucial. Women are not naturally incapable of discerning truth; they are rendered incapable by an education that teaches them to value pleasing falsehoods over uncomfortable truths.

The moral argument: truthfulness is a virtue that women are actively discouraged from developing. "From the tyranny of man, I firmly believe, the greater number of female follies proceed" (004-070) — and foremost among these follies is the habit of deception. A woman who cannot speak truth to her husband, her father, or her society cannot be a moral agent.

The educational argument: the remedy for the suppression of truth is education that cultivates the understanding rather than the graces. "Strengthen the female mind by enlarging it, and there will be an end to blind obedience" (002-082). The enlarged mind seeks truth; the cramped mind seeks only to avoid giving offence.

The political argument: the suppression of truth about women serves the interests of those who benefit from gender hierarchy. "It is a solemn truth with respect to God" (005-024) that women are rational creatures — but this truth is systematically obscured by those who prefer "the shuffling worldly wisdom of men" (005-153) to the clear light of reason.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft's treatment of truth reflects the influence of Enlightenment rationalism — particularly the conviction, shared with thinkers from Descartes to Condorcet, that truth is accessible to all rational beings and that error is the product of defective education rather than inherent incapacity. But she gives this conviction a distinctively feminist inflection: the systematic production of error about women's nature is not an accident of history but a structure of power.

Against the tradition of "polite" philosophy — Shaftesbury, Chesterfield, and the conduct-book writers — who treated social harmony as a higher value than truthfulness, Wollstonecraft insists that politeness built on deception is a form of corruption. The "false refinement" that substitutes pleasing fictions for uncomfortable truths is not civilisation but its counterfeit.

The contrast with Rousseau is particularly pointed. Rousseau's *Émile* treats Sophie's education in pleasing deceptiveness as natural and appropriate. Wollstonecraft's response is that "nature" in such arguments is merely a name for male convenience: the "truth" that Rousseau claims to find in nature is merely the prejudice he has placed there.
