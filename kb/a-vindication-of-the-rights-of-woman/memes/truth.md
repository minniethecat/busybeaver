## Definition

For Mary Wollstonecraft, truth is a foundational epistemological and moral concept. She presents truth as inherently simple and universally accessible — "truth must be common to all, or it will be inefficacious" [P02-006]. Unlike the complex, ornamental discourse she associates with sentimental literature and artificial manners, truth is "a simple principle" [002-088] that [[reason]] can grasp directly. Wollstonecraft positions the pursuit of truth as the proper object of intellectual effort: she describes herself as one who will "follow truth" wherever it leads [003-006], establishing the concept as both a methodological commitment and a moral ideal.

> "Contending for the rights of women, my main argument is built on this simple principle… for truth must be common to all, or it will be inefficacious with respect to its influence on general practice." (P02-006)

Wollstonecraft's conception of truth is deeply shaped by Enlightenment empiricism — truth is not esoteric or priestly but something that can be arrived at through the patient exercise of [[reason]] upon experience. She insists on the accessibility of truth as a political principle: if truth were the preserve of a learned elite, it could not serve as the foundation for a just society. This democratic epistemology is inseparable from her feminism. Women have been excluded from truth not because they are incapable of grasping it, but because they have been systematically denied the [[education]] and freedom necessary to pursue it.

> "I wish to go; but I follow truth, and still adhering to my first method…" (003-006)

### Truth vs. Sentiment

A central opposition in Wollstonecraft's thought is between truth and sentiment. She consistently contrasts "simple unadorned truth" [P03-011] with the "deluge of false sentiments and overstretched feelings" [P03-011] that characterize the sentimental [[education]] imposed on women. Sentiment, for Wollstonecraft, operates as a kind of epistemological pollutant — it clouds [[reason]], substitutes feeling for thought, and renders women incapable of the clear-eyed judgment that truth requires.

> "…a deluge of false sentiments and overstretched feelings, stifling the natural emotions of the heart, render the domestic pleasures insipid…" (P03-011)

This opposition is not merely descriptive but prescriptive. Wollstonecraft's entire rhetorical strategy in the *Vindication* is to strip away the ornamental language of sentiment and address her readers in what she calls "the simple language of truth" [002-051]. She distinguishes her own method from that of writers like [[rousseau|Rousseau]], who "when he should have reasoned he became impassioned, and reflection inflamed his imagination, instead of enlightening his understanding" [005-056]. The contrast between reason inflaming and reason enlightening captures her conviction that truth requires disciplined thought, not passionate effusion.

> "I wish to speak the simple language of truth, and rather to address the head than the heart." (002-051)

### Truth and Social Convention

Wollstonecraft argues that social conventions are systematically hostile to truth. In a striking passage, she observes that "politeness sport with truth, and eating away the sincerity and humanity natural to man, produce the fine gentleman" [008-003]. The codes of politeness that govern social interaction, particularly between the sexes, are designed to obscure rather than reveal — they produce surfaces rather than substance, and they train both men and women to prize agreeable fictions over uncomfortable truths.

> "…thus does politeness sport with truth, and eating away the sincerity and humanity natural to man, produce the fine gentleman." (008-003)

The consequence of this systematic dishonesty is not merely individual hypocrisy but a collective incapacity for moral judgment. When truth is "lost in a mist of words, virtue in forms, and knowledge rendered a sounding nothing" [001-009], the entire edifice of [[morality]] collapses. Wollstonecraft's analysis here anticipates later feminist critiques of the public/private distinction: the polite fictions that govern drawing-room conduct are not trivial but constitute a form of structural oppression that renders women incapable of grasping their own condition.

> "…till truth is lost in a mist of words, virtue in forms, and knowledge rendered a sounding nothing, by the specious prejudices that assume its name." (001-009)

## In Context

Truth appears throughout the *Vindication*, most concentrated in the Preface and early chapters where Wollstonecraft lays out her philosophical method. In the Preface she declares that she "proceed[s] in search of the most simple truths" [001-002], framing her argument as an investigation rather than a polemic. She describes herself as going "back to first principles in search of the most simple truths, and to dispute with some prevailing prejudice every inch of ground" [001-002] — truth is won through struggle against entrenched error.

> "In the present state of society, it appears necessary to go back to first principles in search of the most simple truths, and to dispute with some prevailing prejudice every inch of ground." (001-002)

The concept recurs in Chapter II when she critiques those who obscure truth through excessive refinement — "till truth is lost in a mist of words" [001-009] — and when she appeals to "the name of truth and common sense" [002-057] against false [[modesty]]. In a particularly pointed passage, she asks: "In the name of truth and common sense, why should not one woman acknowledge that she can take more exercise than another?" [002-057] The appeal to truth here functions as an appeal to the body — to the physical reality of women's capacities that social convention insists on denying.

> "In the name of truth and common sense, why should not one woman acknowledge that she can take more exercise than another?" (002-057)

In Chapter III she contrasts truth with the artificiality of sentimental discourse, noting how women are "turn[ed]... away from simple unadorned truth" by "a deluge of false sentiments" [P03-011]. The chapter also contains one of her most memorable aphorisms: "what a weak barrier is truth when it stands in the way of an hypothesis!" [003-015] — a comment directed at [[rousseau|Rousseau]]'s willingness to sacrifice empirical reality to his theoretical commitments about women's nature.

> "But what a weak barrier is truth when it stands in the way of an hypothesis!" (003-015)

### Truth and Education

Wollstonecraft's argument for women's [[education]] is at its core an argument about truth. Women are denied truth — and therefore denied [[virtue]] — when they are educated only to please. She argues that "[[rousseau|Rousseau]] expected them to be virtuous and constant when reason is neither allowed to be the foundation of their virtue, nor truth the object of their inquiries" [005-055]. The connection between truth and virtue is absolute: without truth as the object of intellectual effort, virtue becomes mere performance. Wollstonecraft's indictment of women's [[education]] is thus not merely that it teaches the wrong things but that it teaches a constitutive disregard for truth itself.

> "…how could Rousseau expect them to be virtuous and constant when reason is neither allowed to be the foundation of their virtue, nor truth the object of their inquiries?" (005-055)

The concept is closely tied to Wollstonecraft's critique of gender hierarchy. She argues that women have been "insulated" from truth by an [[education]] designed to cultivate [[sensibility]] rather than [[knowledge|understanding]] [002-091], and that society's preference for pleasing fictions over uncomfortable truths is a structural barrier to women's rational development. In Chapter XII, she concludes that "so forcibly does this truth strike me, that I would rest the whole tendency of my reasoning upon it; for whatever tends to incapacitate the maternal character, takes woman out of her sphere" [012-080] — the truth about women's capacities is the foundation of her entire argument.

> "So forcibly does this truth strike me, that I would rest the whole tendency of my reasoning upon it…" (012-080)
