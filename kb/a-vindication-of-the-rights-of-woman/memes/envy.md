**Envy** in Wollstonecraft's moral psychology is a destructive passion that flourishes in the artificial social conditions she critiques throughout the work. The vivid phrase "hissing of envy" (008-016) captures envy's character as a surreptitious, venomous social force — one that attacks merit from the shadows rather than engaging it openly. Envy is not treated as a universal or inevitable human failing but as a product of specific social arrangements, particularly those that confine women to competitive self-display rather than cooperative pursuit of virtue.

The concept is deployed across multiple registers: as a moral failing that afflicts both sexes, as a structural consequence of women's restricted sphere, and as a rhetorical weapon used to discredit the few women who attain genuine intellectual distinction. Wollstonecraft's treatment of envy is thus both a diagnosis of a psychological pathology and an indictment of the social conditions that breed it.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft defines envy implicitly through its effects and its opposites. It is the antithesis of the "just and simple principles" (010-004) that enlightened minds embrace. Where reason fosters solidarity and the recognition of merit, envy fosters resentment and the desire to tear down those who excel. The "hissing of envy" (008-016) is set in opposition to "whining candour" — both are distortions of genuine moral response, but envy is the more destructive because it cannot even pretend to virtue.

Envy in Wollstonecraft's framework is closely associated with the artificial construction of female identity. When women are taught to value themselves primarily through the eyes of others, and when their social standing depends on comparison with other women, envy becomes structurally inevitable. "They labour to bring down the possessor, and excite envy in her contemporaries" (012-088) — envy is not merely felt but actively manufactured by a social system that pits women against one another.

The concept is also deployed in a gendered analysis of intellectual achievement. Wollstonecraft observes that women who attain knowledge "superior to that of the rest of their sex" (012-086) are met with "over-bearing" accusations — their achievements, instead of being celebrated, provoke resentment. The "exclamations then" against such women "often arise from envy" (012-086), suggesting that envy functions as a mechanism of social control, punishing women who step outside their prescribed sphere.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Envy plays a strategic role in Wollstonecraft's broader argument for women's education. If envy is produced by artificial social conditions — the confinement of women to a narrow world of competitive display — then the remedy is not moral exhortation against envy but the restructuring of those conditions. Educate women to value reason and virtue, give them meaningful occupations, and the structural basis of envy dissolves.

The passage at 010-004 is instructive: "They do not rest in expedients, or grant that what is metaphysically true can be practically false; but disdaining the shifts of the moment they calmly wait till time, sanctioning innovation, silences the hiss of selfishness or envy." Here envy is paired with selfishness as the characteristic response of those who resist moral and social progress. The enlightened reformer does not engage with envy on its own terms but waits for it to be silenced by the triumph of just principles.

Wollstonecraft deploys the concept in her analysis of women's intellectual emancipation. The "portion of knowledge superior to that of the rest of their sex" (012-086) that some women attain provokes envy not because knowledge is inherently provocative but because it violates the gendered expectations that structure social relations. The women who attain knowledge are accused of being "over-bearing" (012-086) — but Wollstonecraft suggests this accusation is itself a manifestation of envy, a way of punishing female achievement by attributing it to moral failing.

The structural dimension is crucial. Envy is not a random moral failing but a predictable consequence of a social order that gives women no sphere of meaningful action beyond competition for male approval. When "a sensible woman" is "stigmatized as an unnatural mother" (012-088) for deviating from convention, the stigmatization is driven by envy — the resentment of those who remain within the beaten track against those who venture beyond it.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

In the eighteenth-century moral philosophical tradition, envy was typically treated as one of the passions — a universal human failing to be regulated by reason and religion. The conduct-book tradition, exemplified by writers such as James Fordyce and John Gregory, addressed envy primarily as a female vice to be suppressed through modesty and self-effacement. Women were enjoined not to provoke envy in others and not to feel it themselves.

Wollstonecraft's treatment is distinctive in its sociological turn. She does not deny that envy is a moral failing, but she locates its source not in universal human nature but in specific social conditions. Women are not naturally envious; they are made envious by a system that confines them to competitive self-display and denies them access to the broader moral and intellectual goods that would redirect their energies. This is a radical departure from the conduct-book tradition, which treated female envy as a given to be managed rather than a symptom to be diagnosed.

The contrast with Rousseau is instructive. Rousseau, in his treatment of *amour-propre* — the competitive self-love that arises in society — offers a psychological analysis of envy and comparison. But Rousseau naturalises the gender distinction: women's *amour-propre* is, in his account, an expression of their nature. Wollstonecraft's counter-argument is that women's envy is an artifact of their education and social position, not of their sex.

## Key Passages

> There are, it is true, trials when the good man must appeal to God from the injustice of man; and amidst the whining candour or hissing of envy, erect a pavilion in his own mind to retire to, till the rumour be overpast. (008-016)

> They do not rest in expedients, or grant that what is metaphysically true can be practically false; but disdaining the shifts of the moment they calmly wait till time, sanctioning innovation, silences the hiss of selfishness or envy. (010-004)

> The exclamations then which any advice respecting health produces in pretty women, often arise from envy. When they chance to see that the prescription threatens to deface the beauty that has been the principal source of their consequence, they resent the interference. (012-086)

> They labour to bring down the possessor, and excite envy in her contemporaries, and so to level her with the common herd. (012-088)
