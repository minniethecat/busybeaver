The systematic training of the mind, body, and will that Wollstonecraft presents as a prerequisite for the development of reason and virtue. Drawing on classical and Enlightenment ideals of self-mastery, she argues that discipline must be applied equally to both sexes: women no less than men require "a few years of discipline" (002-019) to become rational and virtuous beings.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft employs "discipline" in the broad eighteenth-century sense that encompasses education, training, and the formation of character through regulated practice. She draws on multiple traditions: the classical ideal of Spartan discipline that shaped citizens through rigorous physical and moral training; the Enlightenment concept of mental discipline as the cultivation of reason through systematic study; and the emerging Romantic notion of self-discipline as the individual's mastery over impulse and passion.

Discipline serves a dual function in her argument. It is both diagnostic—the absence of discipline in women's education explains their degradation—and prescriptive—the introduction of genuine discipline is the remedy she proposes. "To bring forward these observations," she writes of her educational plan, children must "submit to the same discipline, or leave the school" (012-050). The universality of the requirement is the point: boys and girls disciplined together learn mutual respect; girls disciplined alone learn only to please.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's case for discipline is inseparable from her critique of existing female education. Where men are trained "to submit to a few years of discipline" (002-019) that builds the capacity for self-governance, women are educated without any systematic training at all—indulged, flattered, and sheltered until they marry, at which point they are expected suddenly to govern a household and raise children. The result is predictable disaster: "they may be well disciplined machines," she writes of men educated without genuine understanding, "but they will seldom exert the reasoning power" (002-024). Women, lacking even this mechanical discipline, fare worse still.

Her educational proposals center on creating institutions where discipline is both rigorous and equitable. The day schools she envisions would have clear rules, equal expectations for both sexes, and consequences for failure. The discipline she advocates is not punitive but formative: it is the structure within which freedom becomes possible. Only the mind that has learned to govern itself through discipline can exercise genuine liberty.

The military metaphor implicit in "discipline" is strategic. By invoking "military discipline" (001-026) and "Spartan discipline" (002-028), Wollstonecraft appropriates a language of masculine virtue and applies it to women. If discipline makes the soldier capable of courage and the citizen capable of self-government, then the same discipline must make women capable of the same virtues. "Is this the man," she asks of Rousseau, "who delights to paint the useful struggles of the Spartan discipline?" (002-028)—and yet refuses to extend its benefits to women.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Rousseau's *Émile* advocates a form of "negative education" that protects the child from corrupting influences until reason develops naturally. Wollstonecraft rejects this model as inadequate for either sex. Children require active discipline, not passive protection. The difference is particularly stark for girls: where Rousseau would shelter Sophie from intellectual exertion lest it make her "unmarriageable," Wollstonecraft demands that girls submit to the same mental and physical discipline as boys, precisely because without it, they cannot become the rational companions and capable mothers that a reformed society requires.

## Key Passages

> Rousseau exerts himself to prove that all was right originally: a crowd of authors that all is now right: and I, that all will be right. But, true to his first position, next to nature, he makes her the slave of man. Is this the man who delights to paint the useful struggles of the Spartan discipline? (002-028)

> The being who has been disciplined to the performance of relative duties, without any conviction of the reason on which they are founded, is only a more useful slave. But the mind which has been expanded by the full exercise of its powers can alone govern the affections. (002-024)

> I should think the formation of the mind more essential than the body, as the former is the seat of the understanding; but both ought to be formed by emulation and discipline; and to bring forward these observations, children ought to be obliged to submit to the same discipline, or leave the school. (012-050)
