# Community

Community in Wollstonecraft's political vocabulary is not a warm word. It does not evoke the cozy solidarities of village or parish but the demanding claims of the common good — the "interest of the whole community" (013-029) against which private, local, and sectarian interests must be measured and found wanting. Her use of community is diagnostic: she asks, in each domain she examines, whether action is directed toward the whole or toward some minute part. Those who act only for themselves, their families, their class, or their sect fail the test of community. Those whose vision is broad enough to embrace the general good pass it. And women, she argues, are systematically prevented from developing this breadth of vision by an education and a social position that confine them to "the minute parts" (013-029).

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's concept of community is both descriptive and normative. Descriptively, community refers to the social whole — the collective body of persons whose lives are interdependent and whose welfare cannot be separated. Normatively, community is the standard by which individual and sectional interests are judged. An action, a policy, or a way of life is virtuous to the extent that it serves the interest of the whole community; it is vicious to the extent that it sacrifices the whole to a part.

This conception draws on republican traditions of the common good and Enlightenment ideals of universal benevolence. Wollstonecraft shares with the republican tradition the conviction that political life should be ordered toward the general welfare, not the private advantage of rulers. She shares with the Enlightenment the conviction that reason enables us to transcend local attachments and identify with the good of humanity as such. But she adds to both traditions a specifically feminist critique: the confinement of women to the private sphere is not merely a personal misfortune but a structural deformation of the community itself. When half of humanity is excluded from concern with the common good, the common good is impoverished.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

### The Weight of Vice and Folly

Wollstonecraft's first significant invocation of community comes in a scathing portrait of idle young men and their corrosive effect on social life. She describes "a set of idle superficial young men, whose only occupation is gallantry" (001-027) and who, by their presence in country towns, corrupt the morals of the inhabitants. These men "whose polished manners render vice more dangerous, by concealing its deformity under gay ornamental drapery" (001-027) are themselves the products of a society that values rank over merit, appearance over substance.

The key theoretical move in this passage is the claim that these individuals are "dead weights of vice and folly on the community" (001-027). The metaphor of dead weight is precise. These men contribute nothing; they merely burden. They are carried by the community rather than carrying their share of its obligations. And the nature of their deadness is important: they are not actively malicious but passively useless. Their "vice and folly" are not crimes but the accumulated weight of a life lived without purpose, sustained by inherited wealth and social position.

Wollstonecraft immediately contrasts the man of rank with the man of merit: "A man of rank or fortune, sure of rising by interest, has nothing to do but to pursue some extravagant freak; whilst the needy GENTLEMAN, who is to rise, as the phrase turns, by his merit, becomes a servile parasite or vile pander" (001-027). The system corrupts both. The rich man, freed from the need to earn his place, dissipates his energies in "extravagant freak[s]." The poor gentleman, forced to depend on patronage rather than merit, becomes morally servile. Neither serves the community. Both are deformed by a social system that rewards birth rather than virtue.

The community, in this analysis, is the victim of a class system that misallocates human capacities. The talents and energies that could serve the common good are instead wasted on gallantry and parasitism. And the waste is not merely individual — a pity for those whose lives are misspent — but collective. The community is poorer, weaker, and more corrupt because of the dead weight it carries.

### Women's Exclusion from Community

The argument about community receives its most concentrated treatment in the final chapter, where Wollstonecraft connects women's political exclusion to the narrowing of their moral vision. She writes: "Females, in fact, denied all political privileges, and not allowed, as married women, excepting in criminal cases, a civil existence, have their attention naturally drawn from the interest of the whole community to that of the minute parts" (013-029).

The phrase "minute parts" is carefully chosen. Women are not excluded from all concern with others — they are encouraged to care intensely about their families, their friends, their immediate circle. But this care is confined to what is small, local, and particular. It does not extend to "the whole community" — the general good that requires the capacity to think in terms of principles rather than persons, of justice rather than affection. Women are denied "political privileges" — the right to participate in the governance of the community — and as a consequence they are denied the education in general welfare that political participation provides.

Wollstonecraft then draws the moral consequence: "the private duty of any member of society must be very imperfectly performed, when not connected with the general good" (013-029). This is a striking claim. Private duty — the duties of family, of friendship, of local care — cannot be adequately performed in isolation from the general good. One must understand how one's private actions relate to the larger structure of society to perform even one's private duties well. The mother who cares only for her own children, without understanding how the education she gives them relates to the education of all children, fails in her duty to her own children as well as to the community.

The confinement to "minute parts" also affects women's moral psychology: "The mighty business of female life is to please, and, restrained from entering into more important concerns by political and civil oppression, sentiments become events, and reflection deepens what it should, and would have effaced, if the understanding had been allowed to take a wider range" (013-029). When action is restricted to the private sphere, feelings swell to fill the available space. "Sentiments become events" — the small dramas of personal relationship assume an importance they would not have if women were permitted to engage with larger concerns. And "reflection deepens what it should...have effaced" — the mind, lacking external objects worthy of its attention, turns inward and magnifies its own minor perturbations. The confinement to the private is not merely a political injustice; it is a psychological deformation.

### Community, Sect, and the Oppression Parallel

In one of the most revealing passages of the final chapter, Wollstonecraft draws an extended parallel between women and religious dissenters. She observes that "the same narrow prejudice for their sect, which women have for their families, prevailed in the dissenting part of the community, however worthy in other respects" (013-077). The structure is the same: a group denied full participation in the political community develops a compensatory attachment to its own "minute part" — the sect for dissenters, the family for women.

She elaborates the parallel: "the same timid prudence, or headstrong efforts, often disgraced the exertions of both. Oppression thus formed many of the features of their character perfectly to coincide with that of the oppressed half of mankind" (013-077). Oppression produces a recognizable character type: timid and headstrong by turns, prudent in small matters and reckless in large ones, incapable of the steady principled action that full participation in community life would cultivate. And this character type is not natural — it is "formed" by oppression. Change the conditions, and the character will change.

The analysis extends to the social behavior of both groups: "is it not notorious, that dissenters were like women, fond of deliberating together, and asking advice of each other, till by a complication of little contrivances, some little end was brought about?" (013-077). The repetition of "little" — "little contrivances," "little end" — drives home the point. Excluded from the large deliberations of the political community, both groups develop an elaborate culture of small-scale consultation and maneuvering. They become expert in the politics of the minute — the management of opinion within a restricted circle — and correspondingly incapable of the politics of the general good.

### The Positive Vision

Wollstonecraft's critique implies a positive vision, though she sketches it only in outline. A community properly ordered would be one in which all members — women and men, dissenters and conformists — participated in the determination of the general good. The education that prepared citizens for such participation would cultivate "the interest of the whole community" rather than attachment to "minute parts." And the character of individuals formed by such a community would be marked by the breadth of their sympathies, the steadiness of their principles, and the rationality of their attachments.

This vision distinguishes Wollstonecraft's feminism from a mere demand for women's inclusion in existing institutions. She does not simply want women to have the vote so that they can vote as their husbands do. She wants the transformation of community life such that both women and men learn to transcend their private interests and identify with the common good. The exclusion of women is not merely an injustice to women; it is a corruption of the community as a whole, which is deprived of the talents, perspectives, and energies of half its members.
