## Overview

Jean-Jacques Rousseau (1712–1778) is the philosopher most extensively engaged with in _A Vindication of the Rights of Woman_. Wollstonecraft critiques Rousseau's views on women's nature, education, and social role — particularly as expressed in _Emile_ (1762) — as emblematic of the prevailing attitudes she seeks to overturn. Rousseau's influence spans at least eleven of the work's thirteen chapters.

## Role in A Vindication of the Rights of Woman

Rousseau serves as Wollstonecraft's primary philosophical antagonist. In the Introduction [001-013], she characterizes his retreat into solitude and celebration of a pre-civilized state as fundamentally misguided. Wollstonecraft argues that had Rousseau "mounted one step higher in his investigation" [001-031], he would have recognized reason as the distinguishing human faculty rather than romanticizing barbarism.

Wollstonecraft's most sustained critique centers on Rousseau's depiction of Sophia in _Emile_. She devotes much of Chapter V to dismantling Rousseau's argument that women's education should be designed solely to please men [005-004]–[005-008]. Wollstonecraft rejects Rousseau's claim that "a woman should never for a moment feel herself independent" [002-041], countering that this renders women "convenient slaves" to masculine caprice.

Despite her criticisms, Wollstonecraft acknowledges Rousseau's intellectual stature, noting that she "respected — almost adored virtue" in his writings [003-015], and that his passionate advocacy for truth makes his errors on women's education all the more dangerous and worthy of refutation.

## Key Concepts Critiqued

| Concept | Rousseau's View | Wollstonecraft's Critique |
|---------|----------------|--------------------------|
| Women's nature | Women exist to please men [002-041] | Women share reason with men; virtue is not sex-dependent |
| Education for women | Training in accomplishments and submission | Women need rigorous intellectual education to be virtuous companions |
| State of nature | Pre-civilized state as ideal [001-017] | Reason, not instinct, distinguishes human excellence [001-031] |
| Modesty | A natural female quality to be cultivated | A human virtue, not a sex-specific attribute |

## Influence on Other Writers

Rousseau's ideas shaped the conduct-book tradition targeted by Wollstonecraft. Dr. John Gregory and James Fordyce, whom Wollstonecraft also critiques, drew on Rousseau's sentimental view of female virtue [002-029]. Wollstonecraft traces a direct line from Rousseau's philosophy to the "weak and artificial" education she opposes.
