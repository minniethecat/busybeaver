# Jean-Jacques Rousseau

[[Jean-Jacques Rousseau]] (1712–1778) is the single most frequently cited author in _[[A Vindication of the Rights of Woman]]_, appearing in over thirty passages across ten chapters. [[Mary Wollstonecraft]] engages Rousseau not as a simple antagonist but as a "respectable visionary" (004-093) whose errors are all the more dangerous for being embedded in an otherwise powerful philosophical system.

Rousseau's _Emilius_ (1762), particularly its fifth book describing the ideal female education of "Sophia," serves as Wollstonecraft's primary foil. She writes that "Rousseau exerts himself to prove, that [woman] was created for the convenience of man" (001-019), a premise she systematically refutes.

## Life

Rousseau was born in Geneva in 1712 and spent much of his career in France, where he became a central figure of the [[Enlightenment]] alongside [[Voltaire]] and [[Diderot]]. His major works — _The Social Contract_, _Emilius_, _Julie_, and the _Confessions_ — established him as both a political philosopher and a theorist of education and sentiment.

Wollstonecraft was writing in the immediate aftermath of the [[French Revolution]], for which Rousseau's ideas about popular sovereignty had provided intellectual fuel. Her dedication of the _Vindication_ to [[Talleyrand-Périgord]] explicitly invokes the Revolution's promise of rights, making Rousseau both ally and adversary.

## Role in the Text

### The Sophia Problem

Wollstonecraft's central objection concerns Rousseau's fictional pupil Sophia, whose education is designed entirely to make her pleasing to men. "Sophia, says Rousseau, should be as perfect a woman as Emilius is a man" (005-003) — but "perfect" for Rousseau means weak, dependent, and intellectually subordinate.

> "Rousseau declares, that a woman should never, for a moment, feel herself independent... she should be early accustomed to restraint." (002-031)

This passage crystallizes Wollstonecraft's outrage: Rousseau's program denies women the very independence she considers the foundation of virtue. She notes that Rousseau would "carry his male aristocracy even to the brute creation" (005-034), extending gender hierarchy to all of nature.

### Respect Amid Disagreement

Wollstonecraft's critique is notable for its intellectual seriousness. She does not dismiss Rousseau as a mere misogynist but engages his arguments point by point.

> "Rousseau respected — almost adored virtue — and yet he degraded woman" (003-017)

This paradox — a philosopher of virtue who systematically degrades half of humanity — structures her entire response.

She identifies the root of Rousseau's error: "All Rousseau's errors in reasoning arose from sensibility, and sensibility to their charms won them [women's] respect" (005-044). His philosophy, she argues, was corrupted by his feelings — precisely the failure mode her critique of [[Sensibility]] diagnoses.

### Natural Man vs. Natural Woman

Wollstonecraft exposes the asymmetry in Rousseau's account of nature. While Rousseau celebrates the "state of nature" as humanity's uncorrupted condition, he simultaneously insists that women should be artificially constrained. "Next to a state of nature, Rousseau celebrates barbarism" (001-020) — a critique that links his gender philosophy to his broader primitivism.

## Key Ideas

| Concept | Rousseau's View | Wollstonecraft's Response |
|---------|----------------|--------------------------|
| Female education | Train for male pleasure | Train for rational independence |
| Independence | Women should never feel it | Foundation of all virtue |
| Reason | Women incapable of abstract thought | Denied education, not capacity |
| Nature | Women naturally subordinate | Social construction, not nature |
| Modesty | Female coyness and reserve | Offspring of reason and sensibility |
