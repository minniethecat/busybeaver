# Immortality

The theological doctrine of the soul's eternal existence, which Wollstonecraft deploys as a rational argument for women's equality: if human beings possess immortal souls, those souls cannot be essentially differentiated by sex, and women must therefore be educated as beings destined for eternity.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft invokes the immortality of the soul as a cornerstone of her argument for women's rational and moral dignity. She introduces the concept early in the *Vindication* through a pointed critique of Rousseau: "How could that energetic advocate for immortality argue so inconsistently?" (001-017). Rousseau, who argued eloquently for the soul's immortality, simultaneously consigned women to a purely temporal existence defined by pleasing men.

For Wollstonecraft, immortality is not merely a theological comfort but a logical principle with revolutionary social implications. She speaks of "the stamina of immortality" as "the perfectibility of human reason" (004-003). If the soul is destined for eternity, then the cultivation of reason—the faculty that distinguishes immortal beings from mortal ones—becomes a universal human obligation, not a sex-specific privilege.

The doctrine provides her with a decisive rebuttal to those who would limit women's education to ornamental accomplishments. "Is this a preparation for immortality?" (005-034) she demands of the trivial curriculum prescribed for women. The rhetorical question admits only one answer: a system that teaches women to charm rather than to think is a system that treats them as creatures of time, not heirs of eternity.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft constructs her argument for immortality as a chain of reasoning that links epistemology, ethics, and social reform. The starting point is epistemological: "every difficulty in morals, that escapes from human discussion, and equally baffles the investigation of profound thinking, and the lightning glance of genius, is an argument on which I build my belief of the immortality of the soul" (004-004). Human reason is limited but improvable; its very incompleteness points toward a state in which its development can continue.

This has immediate consequences for women's education. If "reason is, consequentially, the simple power of improvement; or, more properly speaking, of discerning truth" (004-004), then denying women the cultivation of reason is denying them their nature as immortal beings. The woman who is taught only to please is being prepared for a career that ends at the grave; the woman who is taught to think is being prepared for eternity.

Wollstonecraft sharpens this argument by contrasting the immortality of the soul with the perishable qualities that society prizes in women. She observes that men may be "gratified by this kind of fondness which is confined in a beastly manner to themselves" (012-082), but such gratification belongs to the animal order, not to beings destined for immortality. Physical beauty fades; pleasing manners become irrelevant; only the developed mind persists.

The theological stakes are high. She cites the scriptural injunction "be pure as your heavenly father is pure" to argue that moral standards are universal (003-041). A God who commands purity of all human beings cannot have created women with a different moral nature or a different destiny. "The powers of the soul that are of little use here," she writes, "probably, disturb our animal enjoyments, even while conscious dignity makes us glory in possessing them" (005-128). The very restlessness of the undeveloped mind is evidence of its higher destination.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Eighteenth-century theology generally affirmed the immortality of the soul for both sexes but drew no social consequences from the doctrine. Women were promised heaven on the same terms as men while being denied on earth the education that would fit them for it. Rousseau epitomised this inconsistency: a passionate defender of the soul's immortality who nonetheless designed an education for Sophie that treated her as a creature of sensation, not reason. Wollstonecraft's innovation is to demand consistency: if the doctrine of immortality is true, it must reshape education, and if it does not reshape education, it is professed but not believed.

## Key Passages

> How could that energetic advocate for immortality argue so inconsistently? Had mankind remained for ever in the brutal state of nature, which even his magic pen cannot paint as a state in which a single virtue took root, it would have been clear, though not to the sensitive unreflecting wanderer, that man was born to run the circle of life and death, and adorn God's garden for some purpose which could not easily be reconciled with his attributes. (001-017)

> The stamina of immortality, if I may be allowed the phrase, is the perfectibility of human reason; for, was man created perfect, or did a flood of knowledge break in upon him, when he arrived at maturity, that precluded error, I should doubt whether his existence would be continued after the dissolution of the body. (004-003)

> But in the present state of things, every difficulty in morals, that escapes from human discussion, and equally baffles the investigation of profound thinking, and the lightning glance of genius, is an argument on which I build my belief of the immortality of the soul. Reason is, consequentially, the simple power of improvement; or, more properly speaking, of discerning truth. (004-004)

> The passions also, the winds of life, would be useless, if not injurious, did the substance which composes our thinking being, after we have thought in vain, only become the support of vegetable life, and invigorate a cabbage, or blush in a rose. (005-128)

> She who can discern the dawn of immortality, in the streaks that shoot athwart the misty night of ignorance, promising a clearer day, will respect, as a sacred temple, the body that enshrines such an improvable soul. (007-008)
