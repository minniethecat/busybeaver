fl. 1778–1780. A woman with whom Mary Wollstonecraft lived for two years after leaving her parents' home at the age of nineteen. Known only from a single sentence in the Biographical Sketch, Mrs. Dawson represents the network of female-headed households that provided young women with alternatives to parental authority in the late eighteenth century.

## Life

Nothing is known of Mrs. Dawson beyond the single reference in the Biographical Sketch appended to _A Vindication of the Rights of Woman_. The Sketch records that Wollstonecraft "at the age of nineteen, left her parents, and resided with a Mrs. Dawson for two years; when she returned to the parental roof to give attention to her mother, whose health was declining" (P01-006).

The arrangement suggests Mrs. Dawson was a woman of some independent means who could offer accommodation to a young woman without a formal position. It is possible she was a family friend, a distant relation, or a member of the Dissenting community with which the Wollstonecraft family had connections. The two-year duration of the residence — from approximately 1778 to 1780 — indicates a stable, mutually acceptable arrangement rather than a casual visit.

## Role in the Text

Mrs. Dawson appears in the Biographical Sketch as a way-station in the narrative of Wollstonecraft's early independence. The sentence structure places her between two poles: Wollstonecraft's departure from her parents (a bid for autonomy) and her return to nurse her declining mother (an act of filial duty). Mrs. Dawson's household provided the intermediate space in which the young Wollstonecraft could begin to establish an identity separate from her family of origin.

For readers of _A Vindication_, this biographical detail carries implicit argumentative weight. Wollstonecraft's treatise contends that women must be educated for independence rather than dependence. Her own life — as narrated in the Sketch — provides a case study: she left home, lived with a Mrs. Dawson, and began the process of self-definition that would lead to her career as a writer and reformer.

### Works Referenced

None. Mrs. Dawson is not known to have left any written record.

## Key Ideas

Mrs. Dawson's historical obscurity is thematically resonant with Wollstonecraft's feminist argument. _A Vindication_ insists that women's lives and contributions have been systematically erased from historical record. Mrs. Dawson — a woman who provided a home for one of the eighteenth century's most important thinkers at a critical moment in her development, yet whose full name and circumstances are lost — illustrates the very problem Wollstonecraft diagnosed. She represents the countless women whose domestic and social labor sustained intellectual culture without leaving recoverable traces.
