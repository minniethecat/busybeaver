**Piety** in Wollstonecraft's *Vindication* occupies a delicate and revealing position. She never repudiates piety — the religious devotion that orients the soul toward God — and indeed treats it as a genuine virtue when it is the "nobler fruit of piety and experience" (005-125). But she is sharply critical of the instrumental use of piety by conduct-book writers, particularly John Gregory, who "even recommending piety" (005-063) deploys arguments that reduce religious devotion to a strategy for securing male approval.

The critique is not of piety itself but of its corruption. When piety is recommended to women not as the cultivation of the soul but as a means of making themselves pleasing to men, it ceases to be piety and becomes a species of performance. The distinction between genuine piety — grounded in reason, experience, and the love of God — and instrumental piety — deployed as a social credential — is characteristic of Wollstonecraft's broader method: she seeks not to destroy moral concepts but to restore their integrity.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's understanding of piety draws on the broad Protestant tradition of personal devotion, in which piety is the disposition of the soul toward God — expressed in worship, manifested in conduct, and cultivated through scripture, prayer, and self-examination. The "people distinguished for eminent piety" (013-007) are those whose lives display the integration of religious devotion with moral character.

The definitional challenge for Wollstonecraft is to distinguish genuine piety from its counterfeits. The criterion she applies is the same one she applies to all virtues: does this disposition proceed from reason and lead to the improvement of character, or does it proceed from convention and lead merely to social approval? Piety that is "the nobler fruit of piety and experience" (005-125) passes the test; piety that is recommended as a feminine accomplishment fails it.

The connection between piety and experience is significant. Wollstonecraft does not treat piety as a matter of mere feeling — of religious emotion detached from rational reflection. Piety is the fruit of experience because it grows out of a life lived in accordance with reason and virtue. It is not a substitute for understanding but its culmination.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument about piety unfolds through her critique of Gregory's *Legacy to His Daughters*. Gregory, she observes, "even recommending piety he uses the following argument" (005-063) — an argument that she finds not merely inadequate but corrupting. The problem is not the recommendation of piety but the reasoning that accompanies it.

Gregory's argument, as Wollstonecraft presents it, instrumentalises piety: women should be pious because piety makes them more attractive to men, more patient in suffering, more resigned to their subordinate condition. Piety, in this account, is a consolation for the oppressed rather than a spur to moral improvement. It is the piety of submission, not the piety of aspiration.

Wollstonecraft's alternative is implicit but clear. Piety should be "the nobler fruit of piety and experience" (005-125) — the outcome of a life in which reason, virtue, and religious devotion are integrated. The "nobler fruit" is contrasted with the baser products of unreasoning devotion: superstition, intolerance, passive resignation. Genuine piety, like genuine virtue, requires the exercise of reason; without it, piety degenerates into the instrumental compliance that Gregory recommends.

The closing chapters of the *Vindication* return to piety in a different key. The "example of superior temperance and piety" (013-016) is held up as a model — not of female submission but of human excellence. The "present workers of wickedness" are contrasted with those who, through piety and virtue, labour for the improvement of the world. Piety, in this concluding vision, is not a private consolation but a public force — a motive for social reform grounded in the love of God and neighbour.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The eighteenth-century discourse of piety was shaped by the tension between the enthusiastic and the rational. Evangelical piety — the religion of the heart associated with Wesley, Whitefield, and the Methodist revival — emphasised personal conversion, emotional intensity, and the direct experience of divine grace. Rational piety — the religion of the head associated with Locke, Clarke, and the Latitudinarian tradition — emphasised the compatibility of faith with reason, the importance of moral conduct, and the public utility of religion.

Wollstonecraft's treatment of piety draws on the rational tradition while incorporating elements of the enthusiastic. She values the emotional dimension of religious devotion — piety is the love of God, and love cannot be reduced to intellectual assent. But she insists that piety must be "the fruit of piety and experience" — that it must be integrated with reason and tested by life. Piety without reason is superstition; reason without piety is arid.

The critique of Gregory is, in part, a critique of the gendered deployment of the enthusiastic tradition. Evangelical piety, with its emphasis on feeling and submission, was particularly susceptible to the instrumental use that Gregory exemplifies. Women were encouraged to be pious because piety was compatible with — indeed conducive to — their subordination. Wollstonecraft's insistence that piety must be the fruit of reason is a rejection of this instrumentalisation.

## Key Passages

> Even recommending piety he uses the following argument: "It is of the utmost importance to you, not only to be really pious, but to appear so." A man who advises you to cultivate an appearance of virtue, acknowledges that he has little faith in the reality. (005-063)

> The wisdom of this world, contrasted with the wisdom from above, might make the experiment; and the result would be different from what the shuffling worldly wisdom of men has produced, when it has not been contrasted with the nobler fruit of piety and experience. (005-125)

> The present workers of wickedness, who are distinguished for eminent piety, may be found among those who are crying out against all reformation. (013-016)
