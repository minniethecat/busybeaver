# State

## Definition

In *A Vindication of the Rights of Woman*, Wollstonecraft's references to the
**state** are embedded in a broader argument about political and social reform.
She does not offer a systematic political theory, but her scattered remarks on
government, law, and the political order reveal a consistent Enlightenment
commitment to rational reform, the rule of law, and the extension of rights to
all rational beings.

## The State and Individual Character

A central theme of Wollstonecraft's political thought is the reciprocal
relationship between the character of individuals and the character of the
state. A corrupt state corrupts its citizens; corrupt citizens cannot sustain
a just state:

The reform of society must therefore proceed on both fronts simultaneously —
through the improvement of political institutions (laws, government, education)
and through the moral improvement of individuals (especially women, whose
degraded condition both reflects and perpetuates the corruption of the whole).

## Women and the State

Wollstonecraft challenges the exclusion of women from political life. If women
are rational beings, they are entitled to the rights of citizens — to
representation, to legal protection, to participation in the public sphere.
The state that denies women these rights is, by definition, an unjust state:

Her argument anticipates later feminist political theory in its insistence
that the "rights of man" must be understood to include the rights of woman —
that political justice cannot be sex-selective.

## Education and the State

Wollstonecraft assigns the state a crucial role in education. She advocates
for a national system of co-educational day schools that would educate boys
and girls together, teaching them the same curriculum and thereby forming
citizens capable of rational friendship, mutual respect, and civic virtue.
The reform of education is, in her view, the most direct route to the reform
of both individual character and the political state.

## Women's Exclusion from Civil and Political Rights

Wollstonecraft identifies the systematic denial of civil and political rights
to women as a defining defect of contemporary government. She observes that
"females, in fact, denied all political privileges, and not allowed" any
participation in public life, are reduced to a condition of legal invisibility
(013-034). This exclusion is not accidental but structural: governments that
deny women civil and political rights effectively condemn them to "remain in
the state of perpetual childhood" (P02-010).

The remedy Wollstonecraft proposes is not merely to grant women rights within
the existing political framework but to reconceive the framework itself. A
government that systematically excludes half the human race from participation
is "very defective, and very unlike that which wisdom would dictate" (009-030).

## Civilization, Barbarism, and the State

Wollstonecraft draws a pointed contrast between civilised and barbarous states
to argue that political oppression of women is not a relic of primitive society
but a feature of so-called civilisation. She notes that "females, in fact...
only the men not the women" advance in political standing when society emerges
from barbarism (013-050). The civilised state, which prides itself on the rule
of law and the progress of manners, continues to govern women by force rather
than by consent — a contradiction that reveals the hollowness of its claims to
justice.
