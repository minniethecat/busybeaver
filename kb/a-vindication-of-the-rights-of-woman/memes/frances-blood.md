# Frances Blood

[[Frances Blood]] (c. 1757–1785) was, by the testimony of the Biographical Sketch appended to _[[A Vindication of the Rights of Woman]]_, the person who "gave the first impulse to the formation of [[mary-wollstonecraft|Mary Wollstonecraft]]'s character." Their friendship — intimate, intellectual, and tragically brief — shaped Wollstonecraft's early adulthood more than any other relationship except, perhaps, her later attachment to [[richard-price|Dr. Richard Price]].

## Life

Frances Blood was two years older than Wollstonecraft and, the Biographical Sketch records, "possessing good taste and some knowledge of the fine arts" (P01-006). She and Wollstonecraft met before Mary's sixteenth year, introduced through the clergyman Mr. Clare. In 1783, they established a day school together at Newington Green, the radical dissenting community north of London where Wollstonecraft would encounter the intellectual circle that transformed her thinking.

Their friendship was severed by Frances's marriage and removal to [[lisbon|Lisbon]], Portugal. The Biographical Sketch notes that symptoms of the pulmonary disease that would kill her "were visible before her marriage" (P01-008) — a detail that suggests the marriage may have been undertaken in the shadow of impending mortality. Wollstonecraft travelled to Lisbon to nurse her dying friend, an experience that "expanded" her mind and provided "instructive lessons on the evils of superstition, and intolerance" (P01-008).

## Role in the Text

Frances Blood does not appear in Wollstonecraft's substantive argument; her significance is entirely biographical. Yet the Biographical Sketch's account of this friendship — and of the school they founded together — illuminates the lived experience behind Wollstonecraft's abstract arguments for female education and intellectual companionship.

The mutual regard between Blood and Wollstonecraft, and the devastation of Blood's death, suggest the depth of female friendship that Wollstonecraft's philosophy of rational companionship sought to cultivate. When the _Vindication_ argues for marriage based on friendship rather than passion, and for women's capacity for intellectual partnership, the model is not merely theoretical: it draws on the bond Wollstonecraft had known with Frances Blood.

Wollstonecraft named her first daughter — the child of her relationship with [[gilbert-imlay|Gilbert Imlay]] — Frances, "in commemoration of her early friendship" (P01-014).

## Key Ideas

The friendship between Wollstonecraft and Blood exemplifies the ideal of rational female companionship that the _Vindication_ theorises. Their joint school at Newington Green represented in practice what Wollstonecraft would argue in principle: that women, given education and the opportunity for purposeful work, could sustain the intellectual and emotional bonds that make life meaningful.
