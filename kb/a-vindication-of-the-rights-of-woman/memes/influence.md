Influence, in Wollstonecraft's analysis, is the indirect form of power that women exercise within the existing social order — a power she acknowledges as real but critiques as morally compromised, arguing that it substitutes manipulation for rational participation in public life.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft recognizes influence as a genuine form of social power. Women, excluded from direct political participation, have nonetheless "secured every degree of influence that could conduce to their vanity" (005-070). This influence operates through social channels — conversation, fashion, the management of opinion — rather than through formal institutions.

Yet influence is precisely the form of power that Wollstonecraft finds most problematic. It is exercised through indirection and manipulation rather than through the open exercise of reason. Women "under the influence of their senses" (P03-003) and acting through the senses of others, reproduce the very conditions of their subordination even as they exercise power within them.

The distinction Wollstonecraft draws is between influence as a substitute for rational agency and influence as a byproduct of it. When women are educated to be rational beings, their "influence over the deliberations of men" (005-104) would be grounded in knowledge and virtue rather than in charm and manipulation.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Influence occupies an ambivalent position in Wollstonecraft's social theory. She neither dismisses it as wholly illusory nor celebrates it as an adequate substitute for direct rights. Instead, she subjects it to the same rational scrutiny she applies to all social arrangements.

Her primary objection is that influence, as currently exercised, is morally corrupting for both sexes. Women learn to achieve their ends through indirection, cultivating "the adoration which men, under the influence of their senses, pay them" (P03-003) rather than the rational esteem of equals. Men, meanwhile, are flattered and manipulated rather than engaged as rational interlocutors.

The structural problem is that men "seldom contain men under the influence of strong passions" (002-024) who are capable of exercising sound judgment. When influence operates through passion rather than reason, it degrades the quality of decisions in both the domestic and political spheres.

Wollstonecraft's proposed remedy is not to eliminate women's influence but to transform its basis. If women were educated to reason, their influence would become a force for moral improvement rather than for mutual corruption. "Though they might be influenced, would not be entirely under" (012-002) the sway of passion and prejudice.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft implicitly challenges the common eighteenth-century celebration of women's "indirect influence" as a compensation for their formal exclusion from politics. Where conservative writers praised feminine influence as a softer, more civilizing form of power, Wollstonecraft exposes it as a symptom of the very inequality it purports to remedy.

She also distinguishes her position from those "attempting to diminish their influence over the deliberations of men" (005-104) without addressing the underlying educational deficit that makes that influence pernicious. The problem is not influence per se but influence ungrounded in rational cultivation.

## Key Passages

> They have secured every degree of influence that could conduce to their vanity, but have not obtained the respect which is only paid to the exercise of reason and virtue. (005-070)

> The adoration which men, under the influence of their senses, pay them, is not the respect which a rational mind can accept as satisfactory. (P03-003)

> Though they might be influenced, would not be entirely under the direction of their passions. (012-002)

> Opinions that grow up, and have a great influence on the forming opinion; and when once rooted, are with difficulty eradicated. (001-033)
