# Feminine

In _A Vindication_, Wollstonecraft interrogates the term "feminine" as it was deployed in eighteenth-century discourse — not to celebrate it, but to expose how it functioned to confine women to a narrow, ornamental sphere. The word is contested throughout the text, appearing both in critical quotation marks and in direct repudiation.

## The Language of Condescension

Wollstonecraft opens her argument by dismissing the flattering vocabulary of gender: "Dismissing then those pretty feminine phrases, which the men condescendingly use to soften our slavish dependence" (P03-009). She identifies that "feminine" language — delicacy, sensibility, softness — is a rhetorical veil over women's subordination. Her stated goal is to set aside such language and to treat women as "rational creatures" (P03-008).

## Feminine as Weakness

The term is repeatedly linked to physical and moral debility. In Chapter 3, Wollstonecraft notes that women avoid bodily strength because "it takes from their feminine graces, and from that lovely weakness, the source of their undue power" (003-001). The "power" that comes from feigned weakness is, for Wollstonecraft, no power at all — it is dependence disguised as influence.

## Feminine Affectation

Wollstonecraft is especially harsh on "feminine airs of maturity" (007-034), which she denounces as immodest. These performances — bashfulness that gives way to coquetry, feigned coldness, the manipulation of male desire — are unnatural distortions, not expressions of true femininity. Nature, she insists, does not require women to perform: "let women only acquire knowledge and humanity, and love will teach them modesty" (007-034).

## Satirical Deployment

In a striking passage in Chapter 12, Wollstonecraft uses "feminine" with bitter irony. Describing a woman who neglects her children while lavishing sentimentality on pets, she writes: "No, she was quite feminine, according to the masculine acceptation of the word" (012-073). The phrase "masculine acceptation" makes explicit that femininity is male-defined — a performance script, not a nature.

## A Positive Trace

The one neutral usage appears in the biographical sketch, where Mary Wollstonecraft is described as "in the best and most engaging sense feminine in her manners" (P01-023). This suggests that a genuine, uncoerced femininity — one grounded in character rather than performance — remains theoretically possible, though the text provides few examples.
