# Submission

Wollstonecraft's critical distinction between two forms of submission: the blind submission to arbitrary authority that degrades the human character, and the rational submission to reason and divine will that ennobles it — a distinction at the heart of her case against women's enforced subordination.

## Definition

Submission, in Wollstonecraft's lexicon, is not a single concept but a contested terrain. She draws a sharp line between two fundamentally opposed forms. On one side stands "blind submission" (001-029, 005-108), the unthinking obedience demanded by parents, husbands, social convention, and institutional authority. On the other stands rational submission — "the submission is to reason, and not to man" (002-082) — the free acknowledgment of a legitimate moral authority that the individual's own reason has validated.

The distinction is crucial to her argument because the defenders of female subordination routinely conflate the two. When women are urged to submit to their husbands, the demand is framed as a form of natural deference, parallel to the submission owed to God. Wollstonecraft's strategy is to expose the category error: "Rational religion, on the contrary, is a submission to the will of a being so perfectly wise, that all he wills must be directed by the proper motive — must be reasonable" (013-024). Submission to a perfectly rational being is categorically different from submission to a fallible human authority whose claims may be arbitrary.

The political implications are radical. If submission is only legitimate when directed toward reason, then any system of authority — domestic, educational, political — that demands obedience without rational justification is illegitimate.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft develops her argument about submission through a series of escalating critiques. She begins with the institutional: "The blind submission imposed at college to forms of belief, serves as a noviciate to the curate who most obsequiously respects the opinion of his rector or patron" (001-029). The clergy, who ought to be free thinkers, are trained from youth in intellectual servility — and this training is presented as a model for understanding how women are similarly deformed by the demand for uncritical obedience.

She then moves to the interpersonal. Addressing the conduct literature that instructed women in wifely submission, she quotes with scorn the parent who tells a daughter "accustomed to submission — 'Your husband will instruct you in good time'" (005-035). The assumption that a husband automatically possesses superior wisdom by virtue of his sex is, for Wollstonecraft, an insult to reason itself.

The political dimension emerges most clearly when she writes: "If the submission demanded be founded on justice — there is no appealing to a higher power — for God is justice itself" (005-095). This is a remarkable argument: if the authority demanding submission is just, then the submission is to justice, not to the authority; if the authority is unjust, then submission is not owed. Either way, no human being has a right to unconditional obedience from another.

Wollstonecraft extends this logic to education, arguing that parental authority, like all authority, must be rational to be legitimate. She criticises those who "inculcate not only BLIND submission to parents; but to the opinion of the world" (005-108). A child taught to submit without question to one authority will submit without question to all authorities — including those that deserve resistance.

Her alternative is encapsulated in a single sentence: "I love man as my fellow; but his sceptre real or usurped, extends not to me, unless the reason of an individual demands my homage; and even then the submission is to reason, and not to man" (002-082). This is the core of Wollstonecraft's political philosophy in miniature: all human beings are equals; authority is legitimate only when grounded in reason; and submission, when legitimate, is an act of rational assent, not of self-abnegation.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The conduct-book tradition that Wollstonecraft opposed treated female submission as a natural and divinely ordained virtue. Gregory, Fordyce, and Rousseau all prescribed varying degrees of wifely deference as essential to feminine character. Wollstonecraft does not simply reject submission; she redefines it. By distinguishing rational from blind submission, she preserves the concept of legitimate deference to superior wisdom while demolishing the claim that sex alone confers such superiority.

## Key Passages

> The blind submission imposed at college to forms of belief, serves as a noviciate to the curate who most obsequiously respects the opinion of his rector or patron, if he means to rise in his profession. Perhaps there cannot be a more forcible contrast than between the servile, dependent curate, and the manly, independent minister. (001-029)

> I love man as my fellow; but his sceptre real or usurped, extends not to me, unless the reason of an individual demands my homage; and even then the submission is to reason, and not to man. (002-082)

> If the submission demanded be founded on justice — there is no appealing to a higher power — for God is justice itself. Let us then, as children of the same parent, if not bastardized by being the younger born, reason together, and learn to submit to the authority of reason when her voice is distinctly heard. (005-095)

> For every where does she inculcate not only BLIND submission to parents; but to the opinion of the world. (005-108)

> Rational religion, on the contrary, is a submission to the will of a being so perfectly wise, that all he wills must be directed by the proper motive — must be reasonable. (013-024)
