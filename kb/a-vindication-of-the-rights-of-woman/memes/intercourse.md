# Intercourse

In Wollstonecraft's eighteenth-century usage, "intercourse" carries a broader range of meaning than its modern narrowing to the sexual domain would suggest. It denotes the entire field of social exchange between persons — conversation, commerce, mutual influence, the sharing of ideas and sentiments. When Wollstonecraft writes of "the intercourse between the sexes" (004-050), she is referring not merely to erotic relations but to the totality of how men and women relate to one another in society. The corruption of this intercourse — its reduction from rational exchange to strategic manipulation — is, in her analysis, one of the principal mechanisms by which inequality between the sexes is reproduced.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft defines healthy social intercourse by contrast with the "commerce" of the marriage market. In the Introduction, she observes that "the intercourse of the world" (P03-013) has taught women to value appearance over substance, the opinion of others over the dictates of their own reason. Genuine intercourse, by contrast, is the open exchange between rational beings who meet as equals. It requires, as its precondition, the independence of mind that allows each party to contribute something of their own rather than merely echoing the opinions of the other.

The concept has a specifically philosophical resonance. In the Enlightenment tradition, "intercourse" (like "commerce" and "conversation") was one of the terms through which thinkers imagined the civilizing effects of social exchange. Wollstonecraft draws on this tradition but gives it a feminist inflection: if social intercourse civilizes, then the exclusion of women from the conditions of genuine intercourse — from education, from intellectual exchange, from the public sphere — is not merely unjust to women but destructive of civilization itself.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's most sustained treatment of intercourse appears in her analysis of how courtship corrupts the relations between the sexes. The "intercourse between the sexes, which I have reprobated" (004-050), she writes, is a system in which women are taught to dissemble and men to flatter. Women learn to "assume an artificial character" to attract male approval; men learn to value women for their charms rather than their virtues. The result is a social intercourse that degrades both parties — men become "vain and unprincipled," women become "cunning and mean" — and that destroys the possibility of the rational companionship that alone can sustain lasting affection.

The economic metaphor is pervasive. Wollstonecraft describes the marriage market as a form of "legal prostitution" in which women exchange their persons for maintenance. This is intercourse reduced to transaction — the very opposite of the mutual exchange between equals that genuine social intercourse should be. "Women are told from their infancy," she writes, "and taught by the example of their mothers, that a little knowledge of human weakness, justly termed cunning, softness of temper, outward obedience, and a scrupulous attention to a puerile kind of propriety, will obtain for them the protection of man" (002-003). The intercourse that should be a meeting of minds becomes a negotiation in which women trade compliance for security.

The remedy is education that would enable women to participate in intercourse as rational equals. "Would men but generously snap our chains," she writes, "and be content with rational fellowship instead of slavish obedience, they would find us more observant daughters, more affectionate sisters, more faithful wives, more reasonable mothers — in a word, better citizens." The transformation of intercourse between the sexes is inseparable from the transformation of society as a whole.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The conduct-book tradition treated the intercourse between the sexes as a domain of manners — the art of pleasing, the management of reputation, the delicate negotiation of propriety. James Fordyce's *Sermons to Young Women* is exemplary: he advises women to cultivate "a certain softness and delicacy of manners" because these are the qualities that secure male protection. Wollstonecraft's objection is not that Fordyce's advice is impolite but that it is dishonest. An intercourse conducted on these terms is a systematic deception in which both parties are degraded — women because they are taught to dissemble, men because they are taught to prefer flattery to truth.

Rousseau, more philosophically sophisticated than Fordyce, saw the intercourse between the sexes as a domain of mutual enchantment — men and women pursuing each other through the "delicious" illusions of love. Wollstonecraft's reply is that enchantment, however delicious, is incompatible with virtue. "I wish to see women placed in the grand light of human creatures" — not in the flickering candlelight of romantic illusion. Intercourse between the sexes must be conducted "on the same principles" as intercourse between men: governed by truth, tested by reason, and directed toward mutual improvement rather than mutual flattery.

## Key Passages

> The intercourse between the sexes, which I have reprobated; and all the causes of female weakness, as well as depravity, which I have already enlarged upon, branch out of one grand cause — want of chastity in men. (004-050)

> Taught by the example of their mothers, that a little knowledge of human weakness, justly termed cunning, softness of temper, outward obedience, and a scrupulous attention to a puerile kind of propriety, will obtain for them the protection of man. (002-003)

> The conversation of French women, who are not so rigidly nailed to their chairs, to twist lappets, and knot ribbands, is frequently superficial; but, I contend, that it is not half so insipid as that of those English women, whose time is spent in making caps, bonnets, and the whole mischief of trimmings.
