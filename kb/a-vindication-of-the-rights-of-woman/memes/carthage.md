# Carthage

Ancient North African city-state, invoked by Wollstonecraft through a classical reference to Cato the Elder's relentless campaign for its destruction.

## Location

Carthage was an ancient Phoenician city-state located in present-day Tunisia, on the coast of North Africa. A major Mediterranean power, it was Rome's great rival until its destruction in 146 BCE at the end of the Third Punic War.

## Relevance to the Text

Wollstonecraft references Carthage in a single but striking passage that illuminates her view of obsessive political ambition.

### In the Argument

In Chapter 13, she observes: "He wished to crush Carthage, not to save Rome, but to promote ..." (013-058). The reference is to Cato the Elder, the Roman senator famously said to have ended every speech — regardless of its subject — with the declaration "Carthago delenda est" (Carthage must be destroyed). Wollstonecraft uses this example to illustrate how a narrow, obsessive fixation, even one dressed in patriotic language, can corrupt the character.

The passage appears in the context of her argument about the education of citizens. Just as women's narrow education produces frivolous characters, a purely martial or political education can produce men who are unable to see beyond their obsessions. Cato's fixation on Carthage serves as a cautionary example of the moral deformation that results from an unbalanced character.

## Historical Context

The story of Cato and Carthage was widely known among Wollstonecraft's educated readers through classical education. Plutarch's _Life of Cato the Elder_ records the Carthago delenda est refrain. For Enlightenment thinkers, classical examples provided a shared vocabulary for discussing political and moral questions, and Wollstonecraft draws confidently on this tradition.
