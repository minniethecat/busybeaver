# Sentiment

In Wollstonecraft's usage, sentiment denotes refined feeling or moral emotion — but she consistently subjects it to critical scrutiny, exposing how "sentiment" is often invoked to dignify mere passion, to excuse women from the demands of reason, and to cloak self-interest in the language of elevated sensibility.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft engages with sentiment as a central term in eighteenth-century moral philosophy, but she does so with marked ambivalence. On the one hand, she acknowledges the value of genuine moral sentiment — the capacity to be moved by considerations of justice and virtue. On the other, she repeatedly identifies how the language of sentiment is deployed to evade rigorous thought. She warns against "a deluge of false sentiments and over-stretched feelings, stifling the natural emotions of the heart" [P03-011], indicating that the proliferation of sentimental discourse does not deepen but rather obstructs authentic moral response. She is particularly alert to the way "sentiment" serves as a class and gender marker: "delicacy of sentiment, and refinement of taste, are almost synonymous with epithets of weakness" [P03-008], revealing how the cultivation of sentiment becomes a mechanism for keeping women in a state of dependency.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Throughout the *Vindication*, Wollstonecraft dissects the social function of sentimental language. She observes that women "are more under the influence of sentiments than passions" [004-026], meaning that their moral lives are governed by acquired emotional conventions rather than by either raw desire or rational principle. This makes women susceptible to manipulation — their sentiments can be shaped by novels, conduct books, and flattery, none of which equip them for independent judgment.

A key move in Wollstonecraft's argument is her insistence on distinguishing sentiment from genuine virtue. She notes that "sentiment" is "too often used as a softer phrase for passion" [003-037], a rhetorical substitution that allows people — particularly women — to dignify merely impulsive feeling with the language of moral refinement. She is equally critical of men who deploy sentimental language to seduce: the man who "spreads a sentimental veil" over "sheer sensuality" [004-092] is a recurring target of her indignation. The cult of sentiment, in her analysis, enables both sexes to evade the harder demands of rational virtue.

Wollstonecraft also engages with the philosophical tradition. She borrows Hume's sentiments when comparing French and English character [004-014], yet she fundamentally rejects any moral system that grounds virtue in sentiment alone. "I wish to persuade women to endeavour to acquire strength, both of mind and body, and to convince them that the soft phrases, susceptibility of heart, delicacy of sentiment, and refinement of taste, are almost synonymous with epithets of weakness" [P03-008]. Her project is to replace the authority of sentiment with the authority of reason, without thereby denying the value of properly cultivated moral feeling.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft's treatment of sentiment stands in tension with the Scottish Enlightenment tradition, particularly Adam Smith's *Theory of Moral Sentiments* and the broader culture of sensibility. Where Smith and Hume saw moral sentiment as the foundation of ethical life, Wollstonecraft sees it as dangerously susceptible to corruption when not disciplined by reason. She is closer to Rousseau in her suspicion that sentimental culture is a product of civilisation's corruptions, but she differs from him in insisting that the remedy is not a return to nature but the extension of rational education to women. Her critique of "sentimental cant" [001-028] and her scorn for "most sentimental rant" [005-059] place her at a critical distance from the dominant literary and philosophical modes of her time.
