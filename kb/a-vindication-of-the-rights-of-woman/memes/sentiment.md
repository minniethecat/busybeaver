# Sentiment

## Definition

**Sentiment** in Wollstonecraft's philosophical vocabulary occupies an ambiguous position. She is a writer of the age of sensibility and her prose is charged with feeling — yet she is also one of sentiment's most penetrating critics. The problem is not sentiment itself but sentiment *ungoverned by reason*: feeling that has not been educated, examined, and brought under the discipline of rational judgment.

Wollstonecraft's critique of untutored sentiment is central to her argument about women's [[education]]. Women are encouraged to cultivate their feelings — to be sensitive, tender, responsive — but are denied the [[reason]] that would allow them to judge which feelings are appropriate and which are not. The result is a class of people governed by emotion rather than principle — "the creatures of sensation" that Wollstonecraft so memorably describes.

## In Context

### Sentiment vs Reason

Wollstonecraft does not reject sentiment in favor of cold rationality. Instead, she argues for the proper integration of sentiment and [[reason]]: "the heart should be the pupil of the understanding" [004-040]. Sentiment without reason is blind; reason without sentiment is sterile. The goal of education is to produce beings in whom feeling and thought are harmoniously integrated.

### The Dangers of Untutored Sentiment

When sentiment is not governed by reason, it becomes a source of moral error. Women governed by untutored sentiment are prey to flattery, susceptible to seduction, and incapable of the steady moral commitment that genuine [[virtue]] requires. Their feelings are intense but unstable — "the gusts of passion" that Wollstonecraft contrasts with the steady light of rational principle.
