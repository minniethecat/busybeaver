# Solitude

Solitude occupies a paradoxical position in Wollstonecraft's thought. She regards the capacity for solitude — for being alone with one's thoughts without fleeing into distraction — as a marker of intellectual maturity and moral seriousness. Yet she recognizes that women are systematically excluded from the conditions that make fruitful solitude possible. Denied the education that would furnish the mind for reflection, and socialized to depend on others for their sense of self, women are left either craving company as a narcotic or enduring solitude as a punishment. Wollstonecraft's treatment of solitude thus doubles as a critique of the social conditions that render women incapable of being alone.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's understanding of solitude draws on Enlightenment traditions of reflection and self-examination while departing sharply from Rousseau's romantic idealization of the solitary state. For Rousseau, solitude was a return to nature — man alone with himself, uncorrupted by society. Wollstonecraft accepts that solitude is essential to the development of mind and character, but she insists that true solitude requires a mind already furnished by education: one must have something to think about. Solitude without cultivation is merely emptiness, and emptiness drives the uneducated person back into the distractions of society.

This distinction between cultivated and uncultivated solitude appears throughout the text. Wollstonecraft speaks of "solitude and reflection" as paired conditions (004-027) — solitude is valuable precisely because it makes reflection possible. But reflection requires materials to work upon. The woman whose mind has been left uncultivated finds solitude not enriching but terrifying, and her response is to "fly from solitude in search of sensible objects" (006-002). Solitude for such a person is "joyless" (004-054), not because solitude is inherently joyless but because the uncultivated mind has no inner resources to draw upon.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument about solitude unfolds across several dimensions. She establishes solitude as a necessary condition for the development of passions, character, and reason. She critiques Rousseau's romantic idealization of solitude as philosophically unsound. She reveals how women's education ensures they can never benefit from solitude. And she shows how the dread of solitude perpetuates women's dependence on trivial social pursuits.

### Solitude as a Condition of Character

In one of her most important passages on the subject, Wollstonecraft writes: "Solitude and reflection are necessary to give to wishes the force of passions, and enable the imagination to enlarge the object and make it the most desirable" (004-027). This is a striking formulation. Solitude does not suppress passion — it intensifies it, giving wishes "the force of passions" by allowing the imagination to work upon them without interruption. The same principle applies to the formation of character: "the same may be said of the rich; they do not sufficiently deal in general ideas, collected by impassionate thinking, or calm investigation, to acquire that strength of character, on which great resolves are built" (004-027). Without solitude, without the "calm investigation" that solitude enables, character remains weak and impressionable.

The capacity for solitude is thus tied directly to intellectual and moral development. Those who cannot bear to be alone with their thoughts cannot think rigorously about anything. They remain dependent on the stimulation of company, the "sensible objects" (006-002) that distract but never deepen. Wollstonecraft's analysis here anticipates later psychological insights about the relationship between solitude and creativity, between the capacity to be alone and the capacity to think.

### The Rousseau Critique

Rousseau is Wollstonecraft's central interlocutor on the question of solitude. She acknowledges his argument that man is naturally solitary: "Rousseau became enamoured of solitude, and, being at the same time an optimist, he labours with uncommon eloquence to prove that man was naturally a solitary animal" (001-014). But she immediately identifies the flaw in his reasoning: "Misled by his respect for the goodness of God...he considers evil as positive, and the work of man; not aware that he was exalting one attribute at the expense of another, equally necessary to divine perfection" (001-014). Rousseau's natural man, in her view, is a philosophical fiction — and a dangerous one, because it suggests that civilization itself is the problem rather than specific unjust arrangements within civilization.

She pursues the critique further: "Reared on a false hypothesis, his arguments in favour of a state of nature are plausible, but unsound. I say unsound; for to assert that a state of nature is preferable to civilization in all its possible perfection, is, in other words, to arraign supreme wisdom" (001-015). The argument from design is characteristically eighteenth-century, but the underlying point is important: solitude is not valuable because it returns us to a pre-social state. It is valuable because it enables the reflection and self-examination that make social life more rational and more virtuous.

Wollstonecraft also notes that Rousseau's solitude was not the philosophical contemplation he claimed it to be: "He then sought for solitude, not to sleep with the man of nature; or calmly investigate the causes of things under the shade where Sir Isaac Newton indulged contemplation, but merely to indulge his feelings" (005-045). Rousseau's solitude, in other words, was self-indulgent — a cultivation of sensibility rather than a discipline of reason. The contrast with Newton is pointed: Newton used solitude for investigation; Rousseau used it for feeling. And because Rousseau wrote so eloquently about his feelings, his readers mistake emotional sympathy for intellectual conviction: "they imagine that their understanding is convinced, when they only sympathize with a poetic writer" (005-045).

### Women and the Dread of Solitude

Wollstonecraft's most original contribution to the discourse on solitude is her analysis of its gendered dimensions. Women, she argues, are systematically denied access to fruitful solitude. Their education provides them with no intellectual resources to occupy the mind when alone. Their socialization teaches them to depend on the gaze and approval of others for their sense of self. The result is that solitude becomes a torment rather than an opportunity.

In her discussion of women's education, she observes that accomplishments without understanding produce a mind incapable of self-sustaining activity: "Like the charms of a made-up face, they only strike the senses in a crowd; but at home, wanting mind, they want variety. The consequence is obvious; in gay scenes of dissipation we meet the artificial mind and face, for those who fly from solitude dread next to solitude, the domestic circle; not having it in their power to amuse or interest, they feel their own insignificance, or find nothing to amuse or interest themselves" (012-059). The sequence is devastating: flight from solitude leads to dread of the domestic circle, because both require the capacity for self-generated mental activity that women have been denied.

This dread of solitude has social consequences. Women who cannot bear to be alone pour themselves into trivial company, dissipating their energies in social performance. They become "artificial" — their minds as well as their faces are "made-up." And because they find nothing in themselves to interest them, they demand ever more stimulation from without. Solitude, properly understood, would break this cycle by enabling the self-examination and reflection that build genuine character. But women's education, as currently constituted, ensures they never discover this possibility.

### The Impatient Heart

The absence of solitude also shapes Wollstonecraft's moral psychology of impatience. In a remarkable passage on humility and self-examination, she describes the mind that has learned to examine itself in solitude: "During the still hour of self-collection, the angry brow of offended justice will be fearfully deprecated, or the tie which draws man to the Deity will be recognized in the pure sentiment of reverential adoration, that swells the heart without exciting any tumultuous emotions" (008-018). The "still hour of self-collection" — solitude in its most productive form — enables honest self-appraisal.

But those who lack this capacity for solitary self-examination cannot bear the discomfort of moral scrutiny: "The agonized heart will cry with suffocating impatience" (008-018). Without the discipline of solitude, the heart cannot sit with its own imperfections. It must either flee into distraction or cry out in impatient anguish. The connection between solitude and patience is implicit but clear: solitude teaches patience by forcing the mind to dwell with what it finds in itself.
