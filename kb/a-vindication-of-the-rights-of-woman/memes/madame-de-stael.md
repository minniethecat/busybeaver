# Madame de Staël

1766 – 1817. French-Swiss woman of letters, novelist, and political philosopher whose intellectual salon and writings made her one of the most famous women in Europe — a contemporary of Wollstonecraft whose career demonstrated what female intellect could achieve.

## Life

Germaine de Staël was the daughter of Jacques Necker, Louis XVI's finance minister, and grew up in her mother's celebrated Paris salon. She became one of the most influential intellectual figures of the revolutionary and Napoleonic eras, known for her novels (*Delphine*, *Corinne*) and her political and literary criticism (*De l'Allemagne*, *De la littérature*). Napoleon found her so threatening that he exiled her from Paris. Her salon at Coppet in Switzerland became a center of European intellectual life.

## Role in the Text

De Staël is not directly cited in the Vindication (published in 1792, before her major works), but she represents the generation of women intellectuals — alongside Wollstonecraft and Macaulay — who demonstrated that women's intellectual capacity equaled men's. Wollstonecraft visited Paris during the Revolution and would have been aware of de Staël's prominence. Her existence as a public intellectual provides living evidence for Wollstonecraft's argument that women's intellectual inferiority is not natural but socially produced.

### Works Referenced

- *De la littérature* (1800) — literary and social criticism (published after the Vindication)
- *De l'Allemagne* (1810) — study of German culture (published after the Vindication)

## Key Ideas

De Staël argued for the importance of literature and culture in shaping national character and for women's capacity to contribute to intellectual life. Though her major works postdate the Vindication, her career exemplifies the possibility Wollstonecraft insisted upon: a woman achieving intellectual distinction through education and effort.
