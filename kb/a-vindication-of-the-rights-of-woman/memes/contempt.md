The dismissive scorn directed at women — and internalized by women — that Wollstonecraft identifies as a structural feature of eighteenth-century gender relations. She traces contempt from private domestic life to public institutions, arguing that only rational education can restore the dignity that contempt destroys.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft uses "contempt" to describe both an outward social attitude and an inward psychological state. Outwardly, it is the scorn that men direct at women who lack intellectual cultivation — "what can save such beings from contempt; even though they be soft and fair?" (004-041). Inwardly, contempt is what women learn to feel for themselves when they are "treated like contemptible beings" and consequently "become contemptible" (009-034). The word carries strong moral weight: for Wollstonecraft, contempt is incompatible with genuine respect between rational beings, and its prevalence in male-female relations is a symptom of a corrupt social order.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft traces contempt to its root cause: the systematic denial of education to women. "This contempt of the understanding in early life has more baneful consequences than is commonly supposed," she writes (002-019). When girls are taught that cultivating their appearance matters more than cultivating their minds, they grow into women whom men cannot respect as equals. The contempt is thus self-perpetuating: society denies women education, then despises them for lacking intellect.

This dynamic is not limited to personal relationships. Wollstonecraft extends the analysis to the political sphere, arguing that a government that fails to provide women with "a civil existence in the state" renders them vulnerable to "undeserved contempt" (009-033). The same contempt operates in marriage: a wife who must practise deception to preserve appearances "will render her the most contemptible of human beings" (008-005). And in education, "outward submission and inward contempt" is the poisonous consequence of hierarchical school arrangements (012-049).

Wollstonecraft distinguishes between contempt and legitimate moral judgment. Abject fear and manipulation — the tools women are taught to rely on — "always inspires contempt" (005-020). But genuine virtue, supported by reason, commands esteem: "It is respect for the understanding that keeps alive tenderness for the person" (005-071). The solution to contempt, then, is not to demand that men be more gallant, but to educate women so that they deserve — and receive — rational respect.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Where conduct-book writers like Fordyce and Gregory advised women to cultivate pleasing manners and gentle submission as their path to male approval, Wollstonecraft argues that such behaviour actually produces contempt rather than love. "No, beauty, gentleness, etc. etc. may gain a heart; but esteem, the only lasting affection, can alone be obtained by virtue supported by reason" (005-071). She directly refutes Fordyce's claim that female mildness will "bring a man back to reason," countering that only "the mildness of reason" achieves this, while "abject fear always inspires contempt" (005-020).

## Key Passages

> Fragile in every sense of the word, they are obliged to look up to man for every comfort. In the most trifling dangers they cling to their support, with parasitical tenacity, piteously demanding succour; and their NATURAL protector extends his arm, or lifts up his voice, to guard the lovely trembler — from what? Perhaps the frown of an old cow, or the jump of a mouse; a rat, would be a serious danger. In the name of reason, and even common sense, what can save such beings from contempt; even though they be soft and fair? (004-041)

> the most respectable women are the most oppressed; and, unless they have understandings far superiour to the common run of understandings, taking in both sexes, they must, from being treated like contemptible beings, become contemptible. (009-034)

> What, indeed, can tend to deprave the character more than outward submission and inward contempt? Yet, how can boys be expected to treat an usher with respect when the master seems to consider him in the light of a servant, and almost to countenance the ridicule which becomes the chief amusement of the boys during the play hours? (012-049)
