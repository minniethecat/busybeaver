# Delusion

In Wollstonecraft's lexicon, delusion names the condition of systematic self-deception by which individuals — and especially women — are kept from the exercise of reason. The term appears across multiple domains of her argument: in the psychological register (the "delusions of passion"), the religious register (superstitious enthusiasm mistaken for piety), and the social register (the fashionable deceptions of magnetisers and quacks). In each case, delusion is the opposite of the clear-eyed rational inquiry she advocates as the foundation of virtue.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft uses "delusion" to describe a state in which feeling or social fashion substitutes for rational judgment. The biographical sketch of the author traces this theme to Wollstonecraft's own experience: quoting her expression "Love, dear delusion! Rigorous reason has forced me to abandon thee" (P01-016), the sketch presents the mature writer as one who has learned to see through romantic illusion. The delusion here is not mere error but an emotionally invested false belief — one that reason must actively combat.

The concept acquires sharper analytical force in the body of the *Vindication*. Wollstonecraft describes "the delusions of passion" (004-093) as obstacles to the genuine happiness that can only be achieved through the disciplined exercise of reason. Passions, in her view, are not inherently delusive — they are, indeed, "a strong proof of the immortality of the soul" (004-093). The problem arises when passion is indulged without the governance of understanding, producing romantic reveries that substitute for genuine virtue.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument traces three distinct species of delusion, each afflicting a different domain of human life. The first, romantic delusion, arises from the education of women, which cultivates sensibility at the expense of judgment. Women "subjected by ignorance to their sensations, and only taught to look for happiness in love, refine on sensual feelings, and adopt metaphysical notions respecting that passion" — producing the sentimental cast of mind that Wollstonecraft anatomizes throughout the *Vindication*. This delusion is not a personal failing but the predictable consequence of an educational system that denies women the intellectual resources to distinguish genuine affection from romantic fantasy.

The second species is religious delusion, which Wollstonecraft addresses most directly in the final chapter. She traces a chain of credulity from "these delusions to those still more fashionable deceptions, practised by the whole tribe of magnetisers" (013-011). The target here is not religion itself — Wollstonecraft was a deeply religious thinker — but the superstitious and enthusiastic forms of religiosity that substitute emotional excitement for rational devotion. "The followers of these delusions bear his name," she writes of those who claim Christian sanction for their impostures, "and profess to be the disciples of him, who said, by their works we should know who were the children of God or the servants of sin" (013-019). The delusion consists in believing that miracles or magnetisers can supply what only moral self-discipline can achieve.

The third, social delusion, operates through fashion and the opinion of the world. Women are taught from girlhood to value the appearance of virtue over its substance, to cultivate the arts of pleasing rather than the discipline of reason. This creates what Wollstonecraft calls a "romantic twist of the mind" that leads women "shamefully to neglect the duties of life" (013-026) while they pursue "sublime refinements" that are, in reality, forms of self-indulgence.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Rousseau, in *Émile*, had celebrated the "delicious" illusions of love as natural and even ennobling for women, arguing that "the empire of woman is an empire of softness, of address, and of complaisance; her orders are caresses, her threats are tears." Wollstonecraft turns this romantic idealization on its head: what Rousseau calls nature, she calls delusion — a socially constructed fantasy that keeps women in a state of intellectual infancy. Where Rousseau sees feminine sensibility as a charming complement to masculine reason, Wollstonecraft sees a dangerous asymmetry that makes women the perpetual dupes of their own unexamined feelings.

The evangelical revival of the late eighteenth century provides another context. Writers like Hannah More promoted a form of female piety that emphasized emotional submission and sentimental devotion. Wollstonecraft's critique of religious delusion — her insistence that "rational religion" consists in "submission to the will of a being so perfectly wise, that all he wills must be directed by the proper motive" (013-024) — challenges this enthusiastic model by demanding that religious belief, like all belief, pass the test of reason.

## Key Passages

> They, therefore, who complain of the delusions of passion, do not recollect that they are exclaiming against a strong proof of the immortality of the soul. (004-093)

> From these delusions to those still more fashionable deceptions, practised by the whole tribe of magnetisers, the transition is very natural. (013-011)

> Let them not forget, that the followers of these delusions bear his name, and profess to be the disciples of him, who said, by their works we should know who were the children of God or the servants of sin. (013-019)

> In each other's arms, as in a temple, with its summit lost in the clouds, the world is to be shut out, and every thought and wish, that do not nurture pure affection and permanent virtue. Permanent virtue! alas! Rousseau, respectable visionary! thy paradise would soon be violated by the entrance of some unexpected guest. (004-093)
