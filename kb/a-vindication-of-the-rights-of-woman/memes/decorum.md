# Decorum

Decorum, in Wollstonecraft's critique, is the codified system of external social rules — manners, etiquette, and conventional propriety — that substitutes for genuine moral judgment. She distinguishes the "formulary of decorum" from rational virtue, arguing that decorum without understanding produces mere compliance, not character.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft frames decorum as a system of external regulation that bypasses conscience and reason: "those who study rules of decorum, are, in general termed modest" (007-007). The irony is sharp — what passes for modesty is often mere decorum, an adherence to social rules that requires no internal moral effort. True modesty is a state of mind; decorum is merely its outward performance.

She characterizes decorum as a kind of moral shortcut: "never want starched rules of decorum, something more substantial" (005-080). The adjective "starched" captures the stiffness and artificiality of conduct governed by rules rather than principles. "That formulary of decorum, which treads on the heels of..." (012-036) — decorum follows politeness, adding rigid form to what should be the natural expression of good character.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's critique of decorum is part of her broader argument that women's education emphasizes external compliance over internal development. Women are taught "more out of respect for decorum, than reason" (011-014) — decorum replaces reason as the guide to conduct. This produces women who behave correctly but cannot explain why, whose virtue collapses when social supervision is removed.

She traces decorum to its social function: "great attention to decorum, which was carried to a degree" (013-077) in certain societies, becomes an end in itself. When decorum is the standard, appearance trumps substance — a woman need only seem virtuous, not be virtuous.

Her alternative is implicit: conduct should flow from rational principle, not from "starched rules." A woman who understands why honesty, modesty, and benevolence are virtues needs no rulebook of decorum; her actions will naturally be appropriate because they are grounded in understanding.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The conduct-book tradition — Fordyce's _Sermons to Young Women_, Gregory's _Legacy_ — elevated decorum to the highest female accomplishment. These works provided precise rules for every social situation, treating correct behaviour as the sum of female virtue. Wollstonecraft's argument that decorum is a substitute for, not an expression of, virtue directly challenges this tradition. She anticipates the Romantic critique of artificial social convention while grounding her alternative in Enlightenment rationalism rather than Romantic naturalism.
