# Tyranny

The arbitrary and unjust exercise of power, which Wollstonecraft identifies as operating both in the political sphere and within the domestic relations between men and women.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft uses "tyranny" to describe any exercise of power that rests on force rather than reason and that serves the interests of the powerful at the expense of the governed. She applies the term to political despotism, but most distinctively, she extends it to the domestic sphere, arguing that "tyranny, in whatever part of society it raises its brazen front, will ever undermine morality" ([P02-015]). The tyranny of man over woman within marriage and family is, in her analysis, as damaging to virtue as political tyranny is to liberty.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's deployment of the language of tyranny is rhetorically strategic. By describing men's power over women as "the tyranny of man" ([002-001]), she frames what was commonly accepted as the natural order of the sexes as a form of illegitimate domination analogous to political despotism. She argues that men have constructed "many ingenious arguments" to "account for, and excuse the tyranny of man" ([002-001]), just as apologists for absolutism constructed theories of divine right.

The effects of this domestic tyranny are profound. Men, she observes, "exercise a short lived tyranny" ([002-083]) in their romantic relationships, treating women as subjects to be conquered rather than companions to be respected. This pattern extends from courtship into marriage, where "domestic tyranny over wives, children, and servants" ([012-070]) is the natural consequence of a system that grants men arbitrary power within the household.

Wollstonecraft connects domestic tyranny to the broader system of political oppression. She describes "the system of tyranny and abject slavery which is established among the sexes" ([012-010]), using the language of political critique to expose the injustice of gender relations. The iron "sceptre of tyranny" ([005-094]), she argues, operates as much within the family as within the state. Yet she remains hopeful: "from the tyranny of man, I firmly believe, the greater part of female follies proceed" ([013-077]), implying that the reform of gender relations would liberate both sexes from the corruptions that arbitrary power produces.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Where eighteenth-century political thought typically confined the critique of tyranny to the public sphere, Wollstonecraft's innovation was to apply the same analysis to the private. By equating the husband's power over his wife with the despot's power over his subjects, she challenged the fundamental distinction between public and private that underwrote women's exclusion from rights. This rhetorical move anticipates later feminist critiques of patriarchy as a political system.
