## Definition

### The Expanded Scope of Tyranny

Wollstonecraft uses "tyranny" in an expanded sense that bridges the political and the domestic. While the term conventionally refers to [[despotism|despotic]] government, she insists that "tyranny, in whatever part of society it rears its brazen front, will ever undermine morality" (P02-011). This broadening is central to her argument: tyranny is not confined to the throne but operates wherever [[despotism|arbitrary power]] is exercised without accountability — including within the family. She describes "the iron sceptre of tyranny" as "the original sin of tyrants" (005-111), a striking theological metaphor that equates the will to dominate with a primal corruption of [[nature|human nature]].

Crucially, Wollstonecraft identifies tyranny not only in the overt cruelty of masters and rulers but also in the relationship between men and women. She speaks of "domestic tyranny over wives, children, and servants" (012-062) as a continuum of domination, and in one of her most famous declarations, she writes: "From the tyranny of man, I firmly believe, the greater number of female follies proceed" (013-084). The dedicatory letter to Talleyrand makes the claim even more explicitly political: "In this style, argue tyrants of every denomination from the weak king to the weak father of a family; they are all eager to crush reason" (P02-010), linking the domestic tyrant to the political tyrant through a shared contempt for rational accountability.

### Tyranny's Mechanism: Debasing Others

Wollstonecraft probes the psychological mechanism of tyranny, arguing that the tyrant's power depends on diminishing those subjected to it. The "iron sceptre" metaphor captures this dynamic — tyranny is not merely the exercise of force but the systematic degradation of others to make domination feel natural and necessary. She elaborates this point when she observes that "to account for, and excuse the tyranny of man, many ingenious arguments have been brought forward to prove, that the two sexes, in the acquirement of virtue, ought to aim at attaining a very different character" (002-002). The justification of tyranny, in other words, requires a prior denial that the oppressed are capable of the same moral [[character|development]] as the oppressor. Women "are not allowed to have sufficient strength of mind to acquire what really deserves the name of virtue" (002-002) — a circular logic in which the effects of subjugation are cited as its justification.

## In Context

### The Domestic-Political Continuum

Tyranny is most prominent in Chapters IV, V, XII, and XIII. In Chapter IV, Wollstonecraft argues that the proper conduct of parents should be "equally distant from tyranny and indulgence" (004-069), positioning tyranny as one pole of a spectrum where indulgence is its mirror — both deny the child the conditions for rational development. She observes that "women of sensibility are the most unfit for this task, because they will infallibly, carried away by their feelings, spoil a child's temper" (004-069), identifying unchecked emotion as the substrate of both tyranny and indulgence alike.

In Chapter V, Wollstonecraft critiques the "iron sceptre of tyranny" (005-111) as the root of the tyrant's need to debase others in order to feel powerful, and she extends the analysis through a striking question: "Of what materials can that heart be composed, which can melt when insulted, and instead of revolting at injustice, kiss the rod?" (005-028). Here she exposes the moral damage that tyranny inflicts not only on the tyrant but on those who learn to accommodate it — women who are taught to respond to tyrannical treatment with "true feminine softness" rather than righteous indignation.

### The Chain of Domination

In Chapter XII, Wollstonecraft extends the analysis to a chain of domination: cruelty to animals leads to "domestic tyranny over wives, children, and servants" (012-062), suggesting that the habit of exercising [[despotism|arbitrary power]] over the vulnerable corrupts [[character]] at every level. She extends this observation to the educational institutions themselves, noting that "there is not, perhaps, in the kingdom, a more dogmatical or luxurious set of men, than the pedantic tyrants who reside in colleges and preside at public schools" (012-016), implicating the very institutions meant to cultivate [[reason]] in reproducing the habits of arbitrary rule.

The argument culminates in Chapter XIII with the sweeping claim that "the tyranny of man" is the primary cause of women's supposed follies (013-084) — a direct inversion of the prevailing assumption that women's weaknesses justified their subordination. Wollstonecraft adds a further observation about how tyranny poisons the parent-child relationship: when a mother does not concur with a rational father's educational plans, "the father who restrains will ever be considered as a tyrant" (013-073), demonstrating that the domestic perception of tyranny is shaped by inconsistent authority rather than by any consistent standard of [[reason]].
