# Accomplishment

In Wollstonecraft's critique of women's education, "accomplishments" represent the system of superficial attainments — music, dancing, drawing, conversational charm — that society substituted for genuine intellectual and moral development.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft develops her critique of accomplishments across multiple chapters, arguing that women are "only made to acquire personal accomplishments" while men pursue genuine knowledge. (P02-017) She traces the damage from education through to social consequences: "the acquirement of a few superficial accomplishments have strengthened the prevailing opinion" that women are intellectually inferior. (002-071)

The language she uses is deliberately dismissive — "a smattering of accomplishments" (P03-012), "frivolous accomplishments" (004-028, 004-029) — to underscore the triviality of what passes for women's education. She contrasts this with genuine education: "whilst accomplishments are acquired with sedulous care, the understanding is neglected." (004-023)

Wollstonecraft extends the critique beyond women to aristocratic men, noting that young noblemen are instructed in the same "frivolous accomplishments, supported by his rank." (004-028) This parallel reinforces her argument that the problem is systemic, not biological — both women and the wealthy are educated for display rather than substance.

In the national education chapter, she returns to the theme with a direct moral judgment: "vanity that makes them value accomplishments more than virtues." (012-044)

## Key Passages

> "Women are only made to acquire personal accomplishments, men will seek for pleasure in preference to friendship." (P02-017)

> "These frivolous accomplishments, supported by his rank, and still more by his sex, so changes the nature of things, that the young nobleman is taught to consider himself as a being of a superior order." (004-028)
