# Africa

Africa appears in Wollstonecraft's argument as part of her broader critique of arbitrary power — an analogy between the subjection of women and the enslavement of African peoples.

## Location

The African continent. In the late 18th century, European knowledge of Africa was limited primarily to coastal regions, and the transatlantic slave trade was a subject of intense political debate, particularly in Britain where the abolitionist movement was gaining momentum.

## Relevance to the Text

Wollstonecraft draws a pointed parallel between the treatment of women and the enslavement of Africans, situating both within her critique of arbitrary authority.

### In the Argument

In Chapter 4, Wollstonecraft argues that if "applied to the inhabitants of Africa, it is evident that the men there ..." (004-079), the same logic of natural subordination used to justify women's subjection would also justify African slavery. This rhetorical move exposes the inconsistency of those who condemn slavery yet defend the subordination of women — both rest, she argues, on the same illegitimate foundation of arbitrary power.

In Chapter 9, she extends the analogy, describing "the poor African slaves, to be subject to pre..." (009-015), linking the condition of women and enslaved Africans as parallel victims of a system that denies the rights of reason to those it seeks to control.

## Historical Context

Wollstonecraft was writing during the early years of the British abolitionist movement. The Society for Effecting the Abolition of the Slave Trade was founded in 1787, just five years before the publication of _A Vindication_. Her use of the slavery analogy places her argument within the radical Enlightenment tradition that linked the emancipation of women to the emancipation of enslaved peoples.
