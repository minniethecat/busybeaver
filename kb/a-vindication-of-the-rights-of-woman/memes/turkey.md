# Turkey

The Ottoman Empire, referred to as "Turkey" in Wollstonecraft's text, serves as a rhetorical device for illustrating the degrading effects of despotism on human character.

## Location

The Ottoman Empire, centered on Anatolia and extending across the Middle East, North Africa, and Southeastern Europe. In the late 18th century, the empire was in relative decline but remained a significant power, often depicted in European discourse as the antithesis of enlightened governance.

## Relevance to the Text

Wollstonecraft invokes Turkey as the ultimate example of a society where arbitrary power has corrupted the character of both sexes.

### In the Argument

In Chapter 3, she writes of the "destructive blast which desolates Turkey, and renders the men, as well ..." (003-023). The passage argues that despotism does not merely oppress women — it degrades men too. In a society governed by arbitrary will rather than law and reason, both sexes are corrupted. The Turkish example serves Wollstonecraft's broader argument that the subordination of women is not an isolated injustice but part of a larger system of irrational authority that harms everyone subject to it.

By invoking Turkey, Wollstonecraft draws on the well-established Enlightenment trope of "Oriental despotism" — the idea that absolute monarchy in the East represented the antithesis of European liberty. She turns this trope to feminist purpose, suggesting that the subjection of women is itself a form of domestic despotism.

## Historical Context

The image of Ottoman Turkey in 18th-century British discourse was shaped by travel narratives (such as Lady Mary Wortley Montagu's _Turkish Embassy Letters_), political commentary, and a long tradition of contrasting European "liberty" with Asiatic "despotism." Wollstonecraft's use of this imagery was conventional in form but radical in application — she borrowed the language of political liberty to argue for women's emancipation.
