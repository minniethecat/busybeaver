# Indignation

Indignation is one of Wollstonecraft's signature moral emotions — the righteous anger provoked by witnessing injustice done to others, particularly the systematic wrongs inflicted on women. It is not personal resentment but a principled moral response, and it animates her entire rhetorical project.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft opens her Introduction by confessing "melancholy emotions of sorrowful indignation have depressed my spirits" (P03-001). This is not an apology but a credential: her indignation is evidence of her moral seriousness. The phrase "sorrowful indignation" captures the compound nature of the emotion — grief at the wrong, anger at its perpetrators, both held together.

She distinguishes indignation from mere anger by its object: "indignation always takes place of admiration" (002-028) when she observes how women are flattered rather than respected. Indignation responds to a moral wrong; it is the appropriate reaction when "the plausible epithets which..." (002-070) disguise injustice in the language of praise.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Indignation functions in Wollstonecraft's rhetoric as a moral solvent — it strips away the polite fictions that sustain injustice. "As a philosopher, I read with indignation the plausible epithets" (002-070) used to justify women's subordination. The philosopher's indignation is not emotional excess but clear-sighted moral perception: seeing through flattering language to the injustice beneath.

She experiences indignation at the degradation of women's character: "I cannot recollect without indignation, the jokes and hoiden tricks" (007-027) that pass for female education. This indignation is pedagogical — she wants her readers to feel it too, because feeling it is the first step toward recognizing the wrong.

In a striking passage, she declares: "in support of my virtue, I view with indignation, the mistaken notions" (002-081) that enslave women. Indignation here is not a threat to virtue but its guardian — the moral emotion that defends rational principle against social corruption.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Conduct-book writers like Gregory and Fordyce advised women to suppress anger entirely, cultivating placidity and compliance. Wollstonecraft's valorization of indignation is a direct challenge: a woman who cannot feel indignation at injustice lacks the moral sensibility necessary for virtue. Indignation is not unfeminine — it is the mark of a rational being who recognizes wrong and demands better.
