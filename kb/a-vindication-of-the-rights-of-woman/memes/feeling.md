The concept of **feeling** runs through the *Vindication* in two sharply distinguished registers. In its positive sense, feeling is natural moral responsiveness — the "fellow-feeling" (005-042) that binds human beings to one another and makes virtue possible. In its critical sense, feeling is the ungoverned emotional reactivity into which women have been systematically educated — the "momentary gust of feeling" (004-033) that sweeps away rational judgment and renders its possessor incapable of consistent virtue. Wollstonecraft's argument is not against feeling as such, but against the substitution of feeling for reason in the education of women.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft uses "feeling" to describe a spectrum of emotional responsiveness. At one end lies the natural sensibility that is common to all human beings and essential to moral life — the capacity to be moved by the suffering of others, to respond to beauty and truth, to feel the claims of duty. At the other end lies the cultivated, artificial sensibility into which women are educated — a state in which "their senses are inflamed, and their understandings neglected; consequently they become the prey of their senses, delicately termed sensibility, and are blown about by every momentary gust of feeling" (004-033).

The crucial distinction is between feeling as a companion to reason and feeling as a substitute for it. Wollstonecraft does not advocate the suppression of feeling — she is not a Stoic — but insists that feeling must be "silence[d]" not through repression but through "serious employment" (004-095) that engages the understanding. A woman whose feelings are governed by reason may be moved by compassion without being ruled by it; a woman whose feelings have been cultivated in isolation from reason "will infallibly, carried away by their feelings, spoil a child's temper" (004-069).

The vocabulary of feeling in the *Vindication* is rich and carefully graded: "gust of feeling" (004-033) suggests transience and lack of control; "fine feelings" (002-039) is invariably ironic, marking the affected sensibility Wollstonecraft despises; "strong feelings" (004-070) can be either a mark of greatness or a source of instability; and "fellow-feeling" (005-042) names the moral sympathy that is the foundation of social virtue.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument about feeling is inseparable from her analysis of women's education. The central claim is that "the whole tenor of female education (the education of society) tends to render the best disposed, romantic and inconstant; and the remainder vain and mean" (004-095). The mechanism by which this occurs is the systematic cultivation of feeling at the expense of understanding.

The consequences are both personal and social. Women who are governed by unregulated feeling cannot be reliable mothers, for "the management of the temper, the first and most important branch of education, requires the sober steady eye of reason" (004-069). They cannot be genuine friends, for friendship requires the constancy that only reason-governed affection can provide. They cannot be virtuous, for virtue requires the consistent exercise of rational judgment, not the unpredictable prompting of momentary emotion.

The point is not that feeling is intrinsically bad. Wollstonecraft acknowledges that "minds of a superior class" may possess "strong feelings" (004-070) alongside great mental powers — indeed, she suggests that the two often coexist. The problem is structural: an educational system that "renders women the timid and ferocious slaves of feeling" (004-071) while denying them the "ballast of understanding" (004-071) that would enable them to govern their emotional lives.

The gendered dimension is inescapable. Men's feelings are "wayward" and "fluctuating" (004-086), but men have at least been given the intellectual tools to exercise some control. Women, educated to please rather than to think, are denied even that. Wollstonecraft's famous declaration that she wishes "to speak the simple language of truth, and rather to address the head than the heart" (002-039) is not a rejection of feeling but a diagnosis of its misuse — the heart has been addressed exclusively for so long that women have been taught to value feeling over thought and to mistake emotional intensity for moral insight.

The remedy is not the elimination of feeling but its integration with understanding. "The heart, as well as the understanding, is opened by cultivation" (004-057), Wollstonecraft argues, and the "most difficult task" in education is "so to adjust instruction as not to narrow the understanding, whilst the heart is warmed" — nor "to dry up the feelings by employing the mind in investigations remote from life" (004-057). The ideal is a harmonious development of both faculties, each tempering and informing the other.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft's treatment of feeling must be understood against the background of the eighteenth-century cult of sensibility, in which refined feeling was celebrated as the mark of a superior nature — particularly in women. Writers in this tradition, from Richardson's novels to the conduct books of Fordyce and Gregory, had elevated feminine sensibility to a virtue, teaching women that their moral worth lay in the delicacy of their emotional responses.

Wollstonecraft's critique is devastating. What the sensibility tradition celebrates as refinement, she diagnoses as corruption. "False sentiments and over-stretched feelings, stifling the natural emotions of the heart, render the domestic pleasures insipid" (P03-011). The language of sensibility is "turgid bombast" (P03-010), a literary fashion that mistakes emotional display for moral substance.

Rousseau is a particular target. His treatment of Sophie in *Émile* reduces women to feeling beings who exist to please men: "I particularly object to the lover-like phrases of pumped up passion ... Speak to them the language of truth and soberness, and away with the lullaby strains of condescending endearment!" (005-062). Rousseau's elevation of feminine feeling is, in Wollstonecraft's reading, merely a more sophisticated form of the same reduction of women to the emotional that the conduct-book tradition performs — the difference being that Rousseau's version is philosophically ambitious rather than merely conventional.

Against this tradition, Wollstonecraft insists that women "be taught to respect themselves as rational creatures" (005-062) — not because feeling is worthless, but because feeling without reason is the surest path to subjection.

## Key Passages

> Their senses are inflamed, and their understandings neglected; consequently they become the prey of their senses, delicately termed sensibility, and are blown about by every momentary gust of feeling. (004-033)

> Women of sensibility are the most unfit for this task, because they will infallibly, carried away by their feelings, spoil a child's temper. The management of the temper, the first and most important branch of education, requires the sober steady eye of reason. (004-069)

> Unless there be a ballast of understanding, they will never become either virtuous or free: an aristocracy of genius never has existed, nor ever will exist — they will only be the timid and ferocious slaves of feeling. (004-071)

> I wish to speak the simple language of truth, and rather to address the head than the heart. (002-039)

> It is not against strong, persevering passions; but romantic, wavering feelings, that I wish to guard the female heart by exercising the understanding. (004-094)
