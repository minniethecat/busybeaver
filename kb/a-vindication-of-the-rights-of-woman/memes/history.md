# History

The study of past events and human affairs, which Wollstonecraft treats both as an empirical method for understanding women's condition across societies and as an essential subject in her proposed curriculum for female education.

## Definition

For Wollstonecraft, history serves a dual purpose: it is the record of human experience from which truths about society can be drawn, and it is a discipline of study that cultivates the rational mind. She appeals to "the history of woman" ([002-072]) as evidence for her arguments, treating historical observation as a foundation for reasoning about gender. At the same time, she insists that the study of history—political, natural, and moral—must form part of any serious education, including women's ([012-050]).

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft uses history throughout the *Vindication* as an empirical warrant. She surveys "the history of woman" across nations and ages to demonstrate that women's perceived inferiority is not natural but the product of social conditioning: "if we revert to history, we shall find that the women who have distinguished themselves have neither been the most beautiful nor the most gentle of their sex" ([002-052]). The records of history, she argues, are not filled with instances of female incapacity but with the consequences of denying women education ([003-023]).

In her educational program, Wollstonecraft places history at the centre. She proposes that after acquiring basic literacy and arithmetic, children should study "natural history, and some simple experiments in natural philosophy" ([012-050]), progressing to "the study of history and politics, on a more extensive scale" ([012-052]). She explicitly links historical study to moral and political understanding, arguing that "it is plain from the history of all nations, that women cannot be confined to merely domestic pursuits" ([012-078]).

Wollstonecraft acknowledges that women often find "the reading of history a very dry task" ([013-030]), but attributes this not to any innate incapacity but to the trivial education they receive, which fails to equip them with the intellectual framework to appreciate historical writing. She contrasts the "dignity and matronly grace of history" with the frivolity of the novels women are encouraged to read instead ([013-040]).

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Where Rousseau and other educational writers of the period recommended a curtailed curriculum for women focused on accomplishments and domestic arts, Wollstonecraft insists that women should study "the political history of mankind" ([012-092]) on equal terms with men. She rejects the common view that history is too taxing or unsuitable for the female mind, arguing instead that "the most superficial reader of history" can perceive "the numerous examples of vice and folly" that arise from unjust social arrangements ([012-080]). Her emphasis on history as an empirical tool for social criticism aligns her method with Enlightenment rationalism, but her application of that method to the condition of women was radical for its time.
