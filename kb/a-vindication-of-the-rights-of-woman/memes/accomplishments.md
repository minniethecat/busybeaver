**Accomplishments** in Wollstonecraft's usage refer to the decorative attainments — music, dancing, drawing, modern languages — that constituted the entirety of female education in the eighteenth century. She uses the term with sustained ironic force: what society calls an "accomplishment" she exposes as a mechanism of intellectual impoverishment. "Women are only made to acquire personal accomplishments" (P02-017) — the passive construction "are made to" emphasises that women do not choose this curriculum but are consigned to it by a system that fears their development.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft defines accomplishments by what they are not: they are not understanding, not reason, not virtue. "The cultivation of the understanding is always subordinate to the acquirement of some personal accomplishment" (002-019). The key word is "personal" — accomplishments are directed at the self-as-ornament, not the self-as-moral-agent. They are designed to attract attention, not to develop capacity.

She distinguishes accomplishments from genuine education by their object. "A smattering of accomplishments" (P03-012) is all that women are permitted, while "strength of body and mind are sacrificed to libertine notions of beauty" (P03-012). The verb "smattering" captures the intentional superficiality: women are taught just enough to be pleasing but not enough to be independent. Accomplishments produce a woman who "values accomplishments more than virtues" (012-044), which is for Wollstonecraft the precise inversion of a moral education.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument against accomplishments is structural, not merely moralistic. She does not object to music or drawing in themselves; she objects to a system in which these "frivolous accomplishments" (004-028, 004-029) are the whole of female education while the understanding is neglected. "Whilst accomplishments are acquired with sedulous care, the understanding is neglected" (004-023). The imbalance is the crime — and it is a crime committed deliberately, because a woman with understanding would not submit to the subordinate position assigned her.

She identifies the social function of accomplishments: they prepare women for the marriage market. "Novels, music, poetry and gallantry, all tend to make women the creatures of sensation" (004-036). The arts, which should elevate, are repurposed as seduction techniques. Women "acquire a smattering of accomplishments" while "strength of body and mind are sacrificed" (P03-012) — the language of sacrifice reveals her view that the system is not merely inadequate but violent.

The consequences of an accomplishments-based education extend beyond the individual to corrupt social relations. Men learn to value women for their decorative qualities, and women learn to value themselves by the same measure. "Vanity makes them value accomplishments more than virtues" (012-044) — vanity, for Wollstonecraft, is both cause and effect of the accomplishments system, a feedback loop that entrenches female subordination.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The conduct-book tradition — from Halifax to Gregory — treated accomplishments as the natural and proper content of female education. Rousseau, for all his talk of nature, designed Sophie's education around the same ornamental skills, differing only in the philosophical justification. Wollstonecraft's radicalism lies in rejecting the premise entirely: accomplishments are not a curriculum but a cage. "What important accomplishments is the young nobleman instructed in?" (004-027) she asks with biting sarcasm, exposing the double standard — accomplishments are demanded only of those with no access to power.
