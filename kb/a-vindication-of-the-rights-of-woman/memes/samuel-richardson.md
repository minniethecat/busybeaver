1689 – 1761. English novelist, author of _Pamela_ (1740), _Clarissa_ (1748), and _Sir Charles Grandison_ (1753). Wollstonecraft cites Richardson's _Clarissa_ to expose the inadequacy of conventional notions of female honour — a concept she argues reduces virtue to reputation rather than rational moral agency.

## Life

Samuel Richardson was born in Derbyshire in 1689 and apprenticed as a printer in London, eventually becoming a successful master printer. He began writing fiction late in life: _Pamela; or, Virtue Rewarded_ appeared in 1740 when Richardson was 51, inaugurating the English epistolary novel.

_Pamela_ tells the story of a servant girl whose "virtue" — her chastity — is threatened by her employer, Mr. B, and who is ultimately rewarded with marriage. _Clarissa_ (1748), Richardson's masterpiece, is a tragic eight-volume epistolary novel in which the heroine, Clarissa Harlowe, is abducted and raped by the libertine Robert Lovelace, and dies preserving her moral integrity. The novel was a sensation across Europe and deeply influenced subsequent eighteenth-century fiction, including the works of Rousseau and Diderot.

Richardson's novels are preoccupied with female virtue, but they define virtue almost exclusively in terms of chastity and sexual reputation — a reduction Wollstonecraft forcefully rejects.

## Role in the Text

Wollstonecraft's single reference to Richardson is pointed and critical. In Chapter IV, discussing the destructive effects of reducing female virtue to sexual reputation, she writes: "When Richardson makes Clarissa tell Lovelace that he had robbed her of her honour, he must have had strange notions of honour and virtue. For, miserable beyond all names of misery is the condition of a being, who could be degraded without its own consent!" (004-084).

The critique operates on two levels. First, Wollstonecraft challenges the very premise that virtue resides in the body rather than the will — sexual violation, however traumatic, cannot rob a woman of her moral agency unless she consents to being defined by it. Second, she indicts Richardson, and the culture he represents, for reinforcing this reductive equation of female worth with chastity. Clarissa's tragedy, in Wollstonecraft's reading, is not that she is violated but that she has been taught to believe her violation destroys her.

### Works Referenced

_Clarissa; or, The History of a Young Lady_ (1748) is the novel Wollstonecraft cites. Richardson's other major works, _Pamela_ (1740) and _Sir Charles Grandison_ (1753), are not directly referenced but form part of the eighteenth-century discourse on female virtue that Wollstonecraft contests.

## Key Ideas

Richardson was celebrated in his own time for creating psychologically complex female characters and for insisting on the moral seriousness of the novel. But his fiction reinforces a conception of female virtue that Wollstonecraft finds philosophically incoherent and socially destructive: virtue as a physical state that can be taken by force, rather than a quality of mind that can only be surrendered voluntarily.

Wollstonecraft's critique of Richardson is not merely literary but philosophical: it targets the assumption, pervasive in eighteenth-century moral discourse, that a woman's honour is located in her body rather than her rational will. By challenging Richardson on this point, Wollstonecraft exposes the philosophical poverty of the conventional sexual morality her entire treatise seeks to overturn.

> "When Richardson makes Clarissa tell Lovelace that he had robbed her of her honour, he must have had strange notions of honour and virtue. For, miserable beyond all names of misery is the condition of a being, who could be degraded without its own consent!" (004-084)
