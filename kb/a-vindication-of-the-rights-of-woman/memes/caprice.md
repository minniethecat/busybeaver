**Caprice** in Wollstonecraft's *Vindication* is the principle of arbitrary power — the will ungoverned by reason, the decision undirected by principle. She introduces the concept early and deploys it persistently: "the fate of thousands depend on the caprice of a weak fellow creature" (001-024). The diagnosis is structural. When power is not constrained by reason, law, or virtue, it becomes caprice — and caprice is the enemy of freedom, character, and moral development.

The concept is gendered but not exclusively so. Men exercise caprice over women, parents over children, schoolmasters over pupils, aristocrats over commoners. Caprice is wherever power is unchecked — and Wollstonecraft's political philosophy is, at its core, an argument for replacing caprice with principle. The education of women, the reform of marriage, the restructuring of society — all are directed toward the same end: the elimination of caprice as a governing principle of human relations.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's definition of caprice draws on the classical opposition between reason and will. In the rationalist tradition — Platonic, Stoic, and Christian — reason is the governing faculty, and will is its executive. When reason governs will, decisions are guided by principle; when will escapes the governance of reason, decisions become capricious — determined by impulse, passion, or mere arbitrariness.

Caprice is distinguished from freedom by its irrationality. Freedom is the capacity to act in accordance with reason; caprice is the capacity to act in defiance of reason. The free person chooses the good because she understands it; the capricious person chooses whatever pleases her at the moment, without regard to good or evil. The distinction is fundamental to Wollstonecraft's moral psychology: the goal of education is not to suppress the will but to bring it under the governance of reason.

The social dimension of caprice is equally important. Caprice is not merely an individual failing — it is a structural feature of unjust social relations. When one person has power over another without accountability, that power will be exercised capriciously. The "weak fellow creature" on whose caprice "the fate of thousands" depends is not necessarily malicious; he is merely unconstrained. The absence of constraint is the condition of caprice.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument about caprice operates at the intersection of moral psychology and political critique. Caprice is both a character flaw — the failure of self-governance — and a social pathology — the corruption of power by its insulation from accountability.

The character analysis focuses on the effects of caprice on those who exercise it. The man whose power is unchecked by reason "becomes a prey to childish caprices, and fond jealousies" (002-057). Caprice infantilises: the capricious person, regardless of age, is governed by impulse rather than principle, and impulse is the characteristic of childhood. The connection to Wollstonecraft's critique of female education is direct — women are "educated" to remain children, governed by caprice rather than reason, and the men who exercise power over them are likewise infantilised by their unaccountable authority.

The social analysis focuses on the effects of caprice on those subjected to it. When "school-masters are dependent on the caprice of parents" (012-027), education is corrupted — teachers cannot pursue genuine improvement because they must cater to the whims of those who employ them. When wives are dependent on the caprice of husbands, marriage is corrupted — it becomes a relation of power rather than a partnership of equals. When citizens are dependent on the caprice of rulers, politics is corrupted — it becomes tyranny.

The solution is structural and educational. Structurally, power must be constrained by law, accountability, and the separation of spheres. Educationally, individuals must be taught to govern themselves by reason, so that they neither exercise caprice over others nor submit to the caprice of others. "The suppression of their caprices" (005-013) is not an end in itself — it is the necessary condition for the development of rational self-governance.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The eighteenth-century critique of caprice drew on the classical republican tradition, in which arbitrary power — the *dominium* of the master over the slave — was the defining evil of tyranny. Republican writers from Machiavelli to Montesquieu had argued that freedom consists in being subject to law, not to the will of another; caprice was the mark of the tyrant, and the rule of law was the condition of liberty.

Wollstonecraft extends this republican analysis to the domestic sphere. The husband who exercises caprice over his wife is a tyrant, no less than the monarch who exercises caprice over his subjects. The domestication of the republican critique is one of Wollstonecraft's most radical moves — it transforms marriage from a private relation into a political one, and it subjects the family to the same standards of justice that apply to the state.

The contrast with Rousseau is again significant. Rousseau, in *The Social Contract*, had argued that freedom consists in obedience to the general will — a law one has prescribed to oneself. But he had explicitly excluded women from the general will, reserving citizenship for men. Wollstonecraft's critique of caprice exposes the contradiction: if freedom consists in obedience to law rather than to caprice, then the subjection of women to the caprice of men is tyranny, whatever Rousseau may call it.

## Key Passages

> The fate of thousands depend on the caprice of a weak fellow creature, when a man of abilities, firmness, and principle, would alone be capable of filling such a station with dignity. (001-024)

> I wish to speak with reason, of the follies and caprices of our sex, when they do not proceed from a depraved heart, but from a want of cultivation of the understanding. (002-003)

> The dotard, who has mowed down whole fields of men, is left a prey to childish caprices, and fond jealousies, neglecting the duties of his station. (002-057)
