# Character

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's treatment of **character** is one of the most radical dimensions of her argument. She consistently maintains that character is not innate but formed — by [[education]], by social expectations, by the "station" in which one is placed. This conviction underpins her entire critique: if women appear frivolous, vain, and intellectually feeble, it is because their character has been shaped that way, not because [[nature]] made them so.

The concept of a "sexual character" — the idea that men and women ought to develop fundamentally different moral and intellectual qualities — is her specific target. She opens Chapter II by declaring that "the prevailing opinion of a sexual character discussed" (002-001) is the subject she intends to dismantle. Against this, she argues for a single standard of character applicable to all rational beings. Her project is announced in the Dedication: "In this work I have produced many arguments, which to me were conclusive, to prove that the prevailing notion respecting a sexual character was subversive of morality" (P02-007).

## In Context

Wollstonecraft situates character formation within a broader social analysis. The Introduction surveys how women's character has been systematically degraded across societies. Chapter I provides the philosophical anthropology: "It is of great importance to observe, that the character of every man is, in some degree, formed by his profession" (001-028). If the "profession" assigned to women is to please men, their character will be shaped accordingly. Chapter II is the sustained argument against "sexual character" as a concept, opening with the declaration that "the prevailing opinion of a sexual character discussed" (002-001) is the subject she intends to dismantle. The argument reaches its full development in Chapter V, where Wollstonecraft examines how the [[conduct-book]] tradition — from [[rousseau|Rousseau]]'s Sophie to [[john-gregory|Dr. Gregory]]'s *Legacy* — systematically produces the artificial character she deplores.

### The Formation of Character

Wollstonecraft systematically develops the thesis that character is shaped by external conditions rather than innate disposition. In Chapter I she establishes the principle: "It is of great importance to observe, that the character of every man is, in some degree, formed by his profession" (001-028). If the "profession" assigned to women — pleasing men — governs their character formation, then the resulting traits of [[sensibility]], vanity, and intellectual narrowness are not [[nature|natural]] but manufactured.

The analysis extends beyond gender to encompass all social roles. She observes that "nothing can set the regal character in a more contemptible point of view, than the various crimes that have elevated men to the supreme dignity" (001-020). Power degrades character in rulers just as subordination degrades it in women. The [[tyranny]] of kings and the [[tyranny]] of husbands spring from the same corrupting source: unchecked authority over others.

Wollstonecraft's key insight is that character operates as a feedback loop: society assigns women a subordinate station, forms their character around pleasing men, then points to the resulting frivolity as proof that women are [[nature|natural]]ly inferior. Breaking this loop requires changing the conditions under which character is formed — primarily through [[education]]. The Introduction lays the groundwork by surveying how women's character has been systematically degraded across societies, establishing that the problem is structural rather than individual.

### Character as Moral Foundation

For Wollstonecraft, character is the bridge between [[education]] and [[virtue]]. A well-formed character makes virtuous conduct habitual rather than performative. She declares her ambition directly: "I wish to show that elegance is inferior to virtue, that the first object of laudable ambition is to obtain a character as a human being, regardless of the distinction of sex" (P03-009). This formulation is crucial — the character worth cultivating is a *human* character, not a sexually differentiated one.

The theological dimension surfaces in Chapter III, where she writes that "the only solid foundation for morality appears to be the character of the supreme being" (003-027). Character grounded in divine example transcends the mutable customs of society. Wollstonecraft draws on [[john-milton|Milton]]'s Adam to argue that rational companionship requires equality of character: Adam asks God for a partner with whom he can share "all rational delight" (002-025), not a subordinate whose character is defined by pleasing.

The practical consequence is that [[modesty]], [[chastity]], and the other virtues associated with women's character must be re-examined. Wollstonecraft distinguishes genuine modesty — the sober self-respect of a rational being — from the "sexual" modesty that men demand of women as a condition of desirability. The [[conduct-book]] tradition exemplified by [[john-gregory|Dr. Gregory]] and [[james-fordyce|Dr. Fordyce]] trains women in the latter, producing a character of "artificial weakness" (P03-015) that undermines rather than supports genuine virtue.
