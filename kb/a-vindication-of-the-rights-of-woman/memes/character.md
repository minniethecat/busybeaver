# Character

The moral constitution of the individual — which Wollstonecraft argues must be formed through rational self-cultivation rather than molded to fit gendered social expectations.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft uses "character" in the eighteenth-century sense: the integrated moral and intellectual constitution of a person, encompassing their principles, dispositions, and habits of mind. She distinguishes between character that is "formed" by the individual through rational effort and character that is merely "molded" by external forces. "Independence of character" (003-059) is, for her, the highest form of moral achievement — a character that has been consciously cultivated rather than passively received.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

The author contends that women are systematically prevented from developing genuine character. Instead of being educated to form their own principles, they are trained to "assume the name of virtues" without their substance (001-007). The result is not character but its counterfeit — a "specious" performance of virtue that collapses under scrutiny. Women's "character" as conventionally understood is, she argues, merely the internalization of male expectations — "the softened manners of a polished age" rather than "the hardy virtues of a rational creature."

Wollstonecraft traces this failure of character to women's dependence: "dependence of body naturally produces dependence of mind" (003-021), and a dependent mind cannot form independent character. The cultivation of character requires liberty, education, and the exercise of reason — precisely what women are denied. She insists that "the only solid foundation for morality appears to be the character" of the individual developed through rational understanding (003-032).

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The eighteenth-century cult of "character" — associated with writers like Samuel Johnson and exemplified in Boswell's *Life of Johnson* — celebrated masculine independence and integrity while accepting women's character as naturally formed by domestic relations. Wollstonecraft applies the same standard of character formation to both sexes, demanding that women be given the same conditions for character development that men claim as their natural right.

## Key Passages

> "independence of character; I mean explicitly" — on the foundation of moral selfhood (003-059)

> "dependence of body naturally produces dependence of mind" (003-021)
