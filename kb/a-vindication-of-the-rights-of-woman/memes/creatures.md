# Creatures

Throughout the *Vindication*, Wollstonecraft deploys the word "creatures" with deliberate philosophical precision. When she insists that women be treated as "rational creatures" rather than "pretty soft creatures" or "the creatures of sensation," she is not merely choosing a synonym for "persons." She is staking a theological and philosophical claim: women, like men, are creatures of God endowed with reason, and any social arrangement that denies them the exercise of that reason denies their created nature. The term functions as a polemical bridge between theology and rights discourse, grounding the demand for women's education in the nature of the human creature as such.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft introduces the term in its polemical sense early in the Introduction: "I wish to see women placed in the grand light of human creatures, who, in common with men, are placed on this earth to unfold their faculties" (P03-005). The phrase "human creatures" is carefully chosen. It avoids the gendered particularity of "women" while also avoiding the abstract universalism of "man," which in eighteenth-century usage often silently excluded women. By calling women "human creatures," Wollstonecraft claims for them the full moral status that the language of "rights of man" had ambiguously extended.

The term does double duty as critique. When Wollstonecraft describes women as "the creatures of sensation" (004-036) — beings governed by feeling rather than reason — she is diagnosing the effects of a bad education, not describing a natural condition. The same beings who are capable of becoming "rational creatures" are systematically reduced to mere "creatures of sensation" by an educational regime that cultivates sensibility at the expense of understanding. The ambiguity of "creature" — poised between the dignity of a rational being created in God's image and the passivity of a being merely driven by impulse — captures the tension at the heart of Wollstonecraft's argument.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

The argument proceeds by tracing the deformation of the creature from its created potential to its socially degraded actuality. "If I treat them like rational creatures," Wollstonecraft writes, "instead of flattering their * fascinating graces,* and treating them as if they were in a state of perpetual childhood, they would become more virtuous and useful" (P03-008). The conditional form is crucial: the capacity for rational creaturehood is latent in women, awaiting only the educational conditions that would activate it. Women are not naturally inferior creatures; they are rational creatures whose rationality has been systematically suppressed.

The political stakes emerge most sharply in Wollstonecraft's critique of Rousseau. "Rousseau declares that a woman should never, for a moment, feel herself independent, that she should be governed by fear to exercise her *natural* cunning, and made a coquettish slave in order to render her a more alluring object of desire, a *sweeter* companion to man." Against this, Wollstonecraft insists that women must be treated "as moral, but rational creatures" (003-006) — beings whose moral obligations presuppose rational capacities and whose rational capacities demand the liberty necessary for their exercise.

The figure of the "pretty soft creature" (P01-013) recurs as a negative exemplar. Wollstonecraft's own early life, as narrated in the Biographical Sketch, shows her "surrounded by pretty soft creatures — the spoiled children of her elder brother" (P01-013), an environment against which she rebelled. The "pretty soft creature" is what a woman becomes when she is valued only for her charms and protected from the intellectual and moral demands that forge character. She is a creature, certainly, but a diminished one — a being whose created potential for reason has been traded for the shallow pleasures of admiration.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The contrast with Rousseau is again central. Rousseau's Sophie is a creature designed for man's pleasure — her education cultivates her natural "cunning" and "softness" precisely because these qualities make her a pleasing companion. Wollstonecraft's reply is that such an education produces not a companion but a dependant — a being who cannot be a genuine friend or partner because she lacks the rational independence that friendship requires. The creature Rousseau celebrates is, in Wollstonecraft's analysis, a stunted creature — one whose God-given capacities have been sacrificed to male vanity.

The evangelical tradition, by contrast, emphasized women's creaturehood before God as a ground for spiritual equality while often preserving social subordination. Wollstonecraft radicalizes this framework: if women are creatures of God endowed with reason, then the denial of education is not merely unwise but impious — a frustration of the divine purpose in creation. The creature who is prevented from unfolding her faculties is a creature whose Creator is dishonoured.

## Key Passages

> Contending for the rights of woman, my main argument is built on this simple principle, that if she be not prepared by education to become the companion of man, she will stop the progress of knowledge and virtue; for truth must be common to all, or it will be inefficacious with respect to its influence on general practice. And how can woman be expected to co-operate unless she know why she ought to be virtuous? unless freedom strengthen her reason till she comprehend her duty, and see in what manner it is connected with her real good? If children are to be educated to understand the true principle of patriotism, their mother must be a patriot; and the love of mankind, from which an orderly train of virtues spring, can only be produced by considering the moral and civil interest of mankind. (P02-007)

> I wish to see women placed in the grand light of human creatures, who, in common with men, are placed on this earth to unfold their faculties. (P03-005)

> If I treat them like rational creatures, instead of flattering their *fascinating graces,* and treating them as if they were in a state of perpetual childhood, they would become more virtuous and useful. (P03-008)

> All the causes of female weakness, as well as depravity, which I have already enlarged upon, branch out of one grand cause — want of chastity in men. This intemperance, so contemptible, even when it is not attended with injustice, gives a tyrannical tendency to the male character, and renders woman the creature of sensation. (004-036)
