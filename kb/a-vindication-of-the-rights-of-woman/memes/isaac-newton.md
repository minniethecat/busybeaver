1642 – 1727. English natural philosopher and mathematician, author of _Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica_ (1687). Wollstonecraft invokes Newton as the supreme exemplar of rational contemplation, contrasting his disciplined intellect with the frivolous pursuits prescribed for women.

## Life

Isaac Newton was born in Woolsthorpe, Lincolnshire, in 1642. Educated at Trinity College, Cambridge, he rose to become Lucasian Professor of Mathematics and later President of the Royal Society. His formulation of the laws of motion and universal gravitation established the framework of classical mechanics. Beyond physics, Newton conducted extensive work in optics, mathematics (co-inventing calculus), and theology.

Newton's intellectual method — systematic observation, mathematical reasoning, and refusal to accept unexamined premises — made him the emblematic figure of the Enlightenment's faith in reason. Wollstonecraft's era regarded him not merely as a scientist but as proof of what the human mind, properly cultivated, could achieve.

## Role in the Text

Wollstonecraft references Newton twice in _A Vindication of the Rights of Woman_, both times as a measuring-rod for intellectual capacity.

In Chapter II, she cites "an ingenious conjecture respecting Newton: that he was probably a being of a superior order, accidentally caged in a human body" (002-072). This allusion serves her argument that exceptional intellect is not a product of sexual difference but of cultivation — women, denied education, are artificially prevented from approaching such heights.

In Chapter V, critiquing Rousseau's ideal of feminine sensibility, Wollstonecraft contrasts Rousseau's retreat into emotional solitude with Newton's rational contemplation: "He then sought for solitude, not to sleep with the man of nature; or calmly investigate the causes of things under the shade where Sir Isaac Newton indulged contemplation, but merely to indulge his feelings" (005-045). Newton embodies disciplined inquiry; Rousseau, by contrast, represents self-absorbed sensibility. The juxtaposition reinforces Wollstonecraft's core claim: reason, not feeling, should govern human conduct — and women are as capable of reason as men.

### Works Referenced

Wollstonecraft does not cite specific Newtonian texts, but her references assume the reader's familiarity with Newton as the icon of scientific rationality. The _Principia_ (1687) and _Opticks_ (1704) form the intellectual backdrop against which she measures competing claims about human nature.

## Key Ideas

Newton's methodological legacy — the insistence that knowledge must be derived from observation and reason, not inherited authority — aligns with Wollstonecraft's feminist epistemology. She argues that women, like men, possess the faculty of reason and must be educated to exercise it. Newton's example demonstrates that intellectual achievement is the result of disciplined cultivation, not innate, sex-linked capacity.

Wollstonecraft's invocation of Newton also participates in a broader Enlightenment rhetorical strategy: citing Newton as the benchmark of rationality to expose the irrationality of denying women education. If Newton represents the pinnacle of human reason, and women are systematically denied the conditions that produced him, then their perceived intellectual inferiority is an artifact of oppression, not nature.

> "So few, that the exceptions remind me of an ingenious conjecture respecting Newton: that he was probably a being of a superior order, accidentally caged in a human body." (002-072)
