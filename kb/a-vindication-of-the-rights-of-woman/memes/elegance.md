# Elegance

A quality of style, manner, or language that Wollstonecraft treats ambivalently: she critiques conventional feminine elegance as inferior to moral virtue, yet affirms a restrained "simple elegance of sincerity" as compatible with rational character.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft frames elegance primarily as an aesthetic or stylistic quality that she subordinates to substantive virtue. She explicitly states that "elegance is inferior to virtue" (P03-009), establishing a hierarchy in which ornamental refinement ranks below moral substance. This framing is directed particularly at the conventional education of women, which prized elegant accomplishments over rational development. Wollstonecraft herself adopts a rhetorical posture of disclaiming elegant style — "rather than dazzle by the elegance of my language, I shall not waste my time" (P03-010) — asserting that the seriousness of her argument makes stylistic display inappropriate.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Elegance functions in Wollstonecraft's argument as a diagnostic term: when women are educated to cultivate elegance rather than understanding, the result is a superficial character incapable of virtue. She is skeptical of claims about "innate elegance" (002-046), treating such assertions as evasions of the real work of moral education. The concept thus belongs to her broader critique of the feminine ideal — alongside [[Beauty]], [[Grace]], and [[Delicacy]] — as qualities that substitute aesthetic surface for moral depth.

However, Wollstonecraft does not reject elegance categorically. In a literary context, she acknowledges that "there is a degree of concise elegance conspicuous in many passages" (005-074) in a work she is discussing, indicating that elegance in expression, when concise and serving an argument, is legitimate. More significantly, she reframes elegance in moral terms: the "simple elegance of sincerity" (012-036) represents a chaste, honest quality of character that is the antithesis of artificial feminine polish. This positive usage aligns elegance with [[Sincerity]] and [[Simplicity]] rather than with mere ornament.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Where conduct-book writers of Wollstonecraft's era typically celebrated elegance as a feminine accomplishment to be cultivated, Wollstonecraft demotes it below virtue. Her treatment contrasts with the prevailing educational emphasis on "elegant accomplishments" as markers of a lady's refinement. By distinguishing between decorative elegance and the "simple elegance of sincerity," she redefines the term on rational and moral grounds rather than aesthetic ones (012-036).

## Key Passages

> I wish to show that elegance is inferior to virtue, that the first object of laudable ambition is to obtain a character as a human being, regardless of the distinction of sex. (P03-009)

> Would not this simple elegance of sincerity be a chaste homage to domestic affections? (012-036)
