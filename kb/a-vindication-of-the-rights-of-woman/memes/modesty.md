# Modesty

Wollstonecraft redefines modesty from a gendered sexual virtue into a universal quality of sober self-assessment grounded in reason.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft describes modesty as "the fairest garb of virtue" (P02-004), but immediately distinguishes the genuine article from its counterfeits. True modesty, for her, is not sexual bashfulness or "false notions of modesty" (002-019) but a dignified restraint arising from rational self-knowledge. She defines it carefully: "discriminating modesty from humility in one case" (007-004), insisting that it is a quality of the mind, not of the body or dress.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

In Chapter VII — "Modesty — Comprehensively Considered" — Wollstonecraft devotes sustained attention to dismantling conventional understandings. She argues that "the sexual distinction respecting modesty has proved fatal to virtue and happiness" (007-015), because it teaches women to value appearance over substance. The false modesty demanded of women — blushing, downcast eyes, pretended ignorance — is, she contends, a mechanism of subordination, not a genuine virtue.

She is particularly scathing about Dr. Gregory's advice: "Dr. Gregory's advice respecting modesty, strangely miscalled!" (007-016). For Wollstonecraft, the "want of modesty" she "principally deplores" is not sexual immodesty but the immodesty of those who presume to dictate women's conduct without rational justification (007-017).

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Conduct-book writers like Gregory and Fordyce treat modesty as primarily a sexual virtue — women should be modest to preserve their chastity and reputation. Wollstonecraft inverts this: genuine modesty requires reason and independence. Without these, women's "modesty" is merely performance. "Is this modesty?" she asks of imagination-based conduct training. "Is this a preparation for immortality?" (005-034).
