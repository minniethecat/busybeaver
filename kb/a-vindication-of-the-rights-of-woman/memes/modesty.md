# Modesty

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's treatment of **modesty** is one of the most philosophically sophisticated sections of the *Vindication*. She distinguishes between true modesty — the rational, sober self-assessment that prevents arrogance and is a genuine [[virtue]] for all human beings — and the "romantic species of modesty" imposed on women, which is in fact a sexual performance designed to gratify male pride.

> "For man and woman, truth, if I understand the meaning of the word, must be the same; yet the fanciful female character, so prettily drawn by poets and novelists, demands, in addition to this human quality, a sexual one." (003-048)

True modesty, she argues, is the quality of mind that recognises one's own limitations — a kind of intellectual humility grounded in accurate self-[[knowledge]]. The modesty demanded of women is precisely the opposite: it requires them to pretend to feelings they do not have, to blush at what they should understand, to perform ignorance of what they know.

## In Context

Chapter VII contains the most sustained treatment, where Wollstonecraft develops the distinction between modesty as a rational [[virtue]] and modesty as a sexual performance. Chapter V addresses the related concept of chastity, showing how the obsession with female sexual reputation corrupts the very [[virtue]] it claims to protect. Chapter III frames modesty within the broader argument about how women are trained to dissemble — to appear modest rather than to be modest.

### The Two Modesties

Wollstonecraft's central philosophical move is to split the concept of modesty into two distinct kinds. **True modesty** is a quality of mind: the sober, accurate self-assessment that recognizes the limits of one's own [[knowledge]] and [[virtue]], combined with a commitment to improve. It is a genuinely human virtue, equally applicable to men and women, and it rests on the exercise of [[reason]] rather than the performance of emotion. **False modesty** — "a romantic species of modesty" (003-007) — is the sexualised performance that the conduct-book tradition prescribes for women: blushing, downcast eyes, professions of ignorance, and a studied avoidance of any subject deemed "unfeminine." This performance, Wollstonecraft argues, serves male interests. It "gratifies the pride and libertinism of man" by presenting women as delicate creatures in need of male protection, while simultaneously signalling sexual availability through the very act of denial. It is a game in which women are trained to dissemble and men are trained to interpret dissimulation as [[virtue]].

### Modesty and the Corruption of Character

The moral cost of false modesty is that it systematically corrupts female [[character]]. A woman trained from childhood to perform feelings she does not have, to profess ignorance of what she knows, and to blush at what she should understand cannot develop the integrity that genuine morality requires. She becomes, in Wollstonecraft's analysis, a "convenient slave" whose apparent [[virtue]] is actually a sophisticated form of compliance. The tragedy is that this system rewards women for being less than they could be — a woman who pretends to less [[knowledge|understanding]] than she possesses is praised as modest, while a woman who honestly exercises her [[reason]] is condemned as unfeminine. "For man and woman, truth, if I understand the meaning of the word, must be the same; yet the fanciful female character, so prettily drawn by poets and novelists, demands, in addition to this human quality, a sexual one" (003-048).

## Related Persons

- [[rousseau|Jean-Jacques Rousseau]] — His treatment of female modesty in *[[emile|Émile]]* is a target of Wollstonecraft's critique

Wollstonecraft's analysis of modesty exemplifies her broader method: take a concept conventionally treated as a gendered virtue, expose the structural interests it serves, and reclaim it on a rational, universal basis. The result is not the abolition of modesty but its transformation from a tool of sexual regulation into a genuinely human virtue grounded in accurate self-knowledge and intellectual humility.
