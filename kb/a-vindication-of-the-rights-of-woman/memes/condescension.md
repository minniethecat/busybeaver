# Condescension

Wollstonecraft deploys "condescension" not as a neutral descriptor of social deference but as a term of moral indictment, exposing the power relations disguised within apparently gracious behavior.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's most direct treatment appears in her repudiation of "the insolent condescension of protectorship" — a phrase that compresses her argument into three words. (007-013) Condescension, in her analysis, is insolent precisely because it pretends to benevolence while reinforcing hierarchy. The protector who condescends does not elevate the protected but confirms their inferiority.

She develops this point in an earlier passage: "It is not condescension to bow to an inferior. So ludicrously do rights and duties become confounded, that even the condescending act is mistaken for respect." (004-022) The critique operates on two levels — first, that condescension is not genuine respect, and second, that women are educated to accept and even welcome it.

Wollstonecraft also anticipates the gendered dimension of the critique by acknowledging that women are taught "the winning graces of condescension" as a mode of social performance. (002-067) These graces are winning only within a system that rewards women for self-abasement.

## Key Passages

> "It is not condescension to bow to an inferior. So ludicrously do rights and duties become confounded." (004-022)

> "Neither the language of gallantry, nor the insolent condescension of protectorship." (007-013)
