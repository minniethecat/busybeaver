**Pride** is, for Wollstonecraft, one of the principal moral deformations produced by the existing relations between the sexes. It takes two distinct but complementary forms: the "arrogant pride of man" (002-049), which demands female submission as a tribute to male superiority, and the false pride of woman, which has been cultivated by a system that teaches her to value herself for her power to attract rather than for her virtue. Throughout the *Vindication*, Wollstonecraft subjects both forms of pride to sustained critique, arguing that the pride of power and the pride of vanity are equally incompatible with the rational equality that virtue requires.

The concept is central to Wollstonecraft's diagnosis of the moral psychology of gender. Pride is not merely an individual vice but a structural feature of a society organised around the subordination of women — a society in which "the pride and sensuality of man" (003-025) and the "modesty, which gratifies the pride and libertinism of man" (003-008) are mutually reinforcing.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft distinguishes between several forms of pride. The most fundamental is the pride of power — "the pride of power" (003-028) that leads men to "use the same arguments that tyrants use" (003-028) to justify their dominion over women. This is pride in its political form: the conviction that one's superiority entitles one to command, and that the subordination of others is the natural order of things.

The second form is the pride of vanity — the pride that women are taught to take in their appearance, their accomplishments, and their ability to attract male attention. This pride is, in Wollstonecraft's analysis, not a genuine expression of self-worth but a compensation for the lack of it. A woman who has been denied the opportunity to develop "conscious dignity" (003-054) will grasp at the substitute of vanity — and the substitute, because it is grounded in nothing real, makes her "the poor victim of [man's] pride" (011-011).

The third form is the pride of rank — "over-weening pride" (012-088) — which Wollstonecraft condemns as "disgusting in both men and women" (012-088). This is the pride that attaches to social position rather than personal merit, and it is particularly dangerous because it substitutes the accidents of birth for the achievements of character.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

The core of Wollstonecraft's argument about pride is that it is an obstacle to virtue and, in particular, to the relations of equality that virtue requires. Pride, in whatever form, is always a claim to superiority — and a claim to superiority is incompatible with the recognition of another as an equal. "Let not men then in the pride of power, use the same arguments that tyrants use" (003-028) — the analogy between male pride and political tyranny is deliberate and repeated.

The education of women, Wollstonecraft argues, systematically cultivates a false pride. Women are "considered as the pride and ornament of creation" (P01-013) — the phrase is ironic: to be an "ornament" is to be a decoration, not a person, and the "pride" that attaches to being an ornament is not the pride of self-respect but the pride of display. A woman who prides herself on her beauty or her accomplishments has been taught to value herself for qualities that have no moral worth.

The most damaging effect of male pride is its tendency to prevent men from recognising women's rational equality. "The men who pride themselves upon paying this arbitrary insolent respect" (004-014) to women are, in fact, reinforcing the very system of subordination that they imagine themselves to be transcending. The "lordly caress" (002-049) that gratifies "the arrogant pride of man" (002-049) is not a gesture of respect but a performance of superiority.

Wollstonecraft's remedy for pride is not humility in the conventional sense — which she regards as often merely another form of submission — but the cultivation of genuine self-respect grounded in virtue. A person who knows her own worth because she has earned it through the exercise of reason and virtue has no need of the false pride that feeds on the subordination of others.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The eighteenth-century discourse of pride was dominated by the Christian tradition, which condemned pride as the first of the seven deadly sins, and by the Enlightenment tradition, which re-evaluated pride as a potentially constructive passion. Mandeville's *Fable of the Bees* had argued that "private vices" — including pride — could produce "public benefits"; Hume and Smith had analysed pride as a natural passion that, properly regulated, could motivate virtuous action.

Wollstonecraft is closer to the Christian tradition in her moral condemnation of pride, but her analysis is distinctively social and gendered. Pride is not, for her, merely an individual vice to be overcome by individual effort; it is a structural feature of a society that has made the subordination of women into a source of male self-esteem. The "pride of power" that her male contemporaries celebrate as a mark of independence is, in her analysis, merely the name that tyranny gives to itself when it wishes to appear respectable.
