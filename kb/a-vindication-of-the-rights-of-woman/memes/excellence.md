# Excellence

Wollstonecraft attacks the notion that there are separate standards of excellence for men and women, arguing instead for a single standard rooted in reason and virtue — "human excellence" rather than "female excellence."

## Definition in Context

In the conduct-book tradition that Wollstonecraft opposed, "female excellence" was a distinct category: women were to cultivate modesty, delicacy, and submission, while men pursued courage, wisdom, and independence. Wollstonecraft rejects this division as arbitrary and harmful.

## Usage in the Text

### Female Excellence as Degradation

Early in the Introduction, she identifies the problem: "Women are, in fact, so much degraded by mistaken notions of female excellence" (P03-014). The very phrase "female excellence" is a deception — a code for qualities that keep women weak, dependent, and pleasing to men.

The consequence of this false ideal is paradoxical: "this artificial weakness produces a propensity to tyrannize, and gives birth to cunning, the natural opponent of strength" (P03-014). Women trained to a standard of "excellence" that valorizes weakness become, in practice, manipulative and petty — the very vices that anti-feminists then cite as evidence of women's natural inferiority.

### Human Excellence as the Alternative

Wollstonecraft's alternative is "human excellence" — a single standard of virtue grounded in reason. In Chapter 2, she argues that "monsters who have scarcely shown any discernment of human excellence, have tyrannized over thousands of their fellow-creatures" (002-084). The phrase "discernment of human excellence" implies that the capacity to recognize and cultivate virtue is itself a human capacity, shared by both sexes.

## Theoretical Significance

The distinction between "female excellence" and "human excellence" is at the core of Wollstonecraft's feminist moral philosophy. By insisting on a single standard of virtue, she challenges the entire edifice of gendered morality — the assumption that there are men's virtues and women's virtues, rather than simply human virtues to which all are called.
