In Wollstonecraft's moral vocabulary, **sacrifice** carries a consistently negative charge — it is what society demands of women unjustly, not what virtue demands of women properly. She opens the *Vindication* by lamenting that women's "strength and usefulness are sacrificed to beauty" (P03-001), establishing sacrifice as the mechanism by which women are diminished to fit a male-designed ideal. Throughout the work, the verb "sacrificed" marks the point where social pressure overrides moral principle, where women are required to give up something essential — reason, truth, independence — for something superficial: beauty, propriety, male approval.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft uses "sacrifice" primarily in its transitive sense — something or someone is sacrificed *to* something else. The grammar itself encodes her critique: women are the objects of sacrifice, not its agents. "Strength of body and mind are sacrificed to libertine notions of beauty" (P03-012); "virtue is sacrificed to temporary gratifications" (003-009); "truth and sincerity, virtue, are all to be sacrificed" (003-057) to the demands of romance and propriety. The consistent pattern reveals her structural analysis: sacrifice is not an individual moral failure but a systematic demand that women trade substance for appearance.

She does occasionally use sacrifice in a positive sense, as when she speaks of one who "sacrifices pleasure to the love of virtue" (004-012), but this is the exception that proves the rule. Proper sacrifice — the voluntary renunciation of lesser goods for higher ones — is an act of rational moral agency. The sacrifice society demands of women is precisely the opposite: the involuntary renunciation of higher goods (reason, virtue, independence) for lower ones (beauty, pleasure, male approval).

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's critique of sacrifice operates on multiple levels. Most explicitly, she attacks the demand that women "sacrifice every other consideration to the desire of establishing themselves" (005-005) through marriage — the single-minded pursuit of matrimonial advantage that reduces women's lives to a strategic game. When a woman's "understanding is sacrificed" (005-037) to the project of attracting a husband, the loss is not merely personal but social: society loses the contribution that a developed female mind could make.

The link between sacrifice and virtue is a recurring theme. Wollstonecraft is adamant that "whoever sacrifices virtue, strictly so called, to gain the favour of man, does despite to her own soul" (002-076). This formulation is crucial: sacrifice of virtue is not merely imprudent but morally self-destructive — a "despite to her own soul." The theological language underscores the gravity of the moral injury: to sacrifice virtue for male approval is to damage one's own moral being.

She also critiques the false economy of sacrifice that conduct books promote. When "justice has too often been sacrificed to propriety" (005-109), the apparent gain in social standing is purchased at the cost of genuine moral worth. This critique extends to the sentimental tradition that romanticises female self-sacrifice: Wollstonecraft refuses to idealise what she sees as a systematic degradation.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The most pointed contrast is with the Christian idealisation of female self-sacrifice, particularly as preached by Fordyce and the conduct-book tradition. Where Fordyce presents women's self-abnegation as their highest virtue, Wollstonecraft identifies it as their deepest degradation — not because self-sacrifice is inherently wrong, but because what women are asked to sacrifice (reason, virtue, independence) is precisely what makes them moral beings. She also contests Rousseau's romanticisation of feminine devotion in *Julie*, where the heroine's sacrifices are presented as noble; for Wollstonecraft, such idealisation simply dignifies a system of exploitation.

## Key Passages

> Strength and usefulness are sacrificed to beauty; and the flaunting leaves, after having pleased a fastidious eye, fade, disregarded on the stalk. (P03-001)

> Virtue is sacrificed to temporary gratifications, and the respectability of life to the triumph of an hour. (003-009)

> But what is her understanding sacrificed for? And why is all this preparation necessary, if education be only to cultivate the modesty of virtue? (005-037)

> Justice has too often been sacrificed to propriety. (005-109)
