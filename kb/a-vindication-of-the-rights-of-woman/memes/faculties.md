# Faculties

The innate capacities of the human mind — reason, judgment, imagination, and moral sense — that Wollstonecraft contends are common to both sexes and require active exercise to develop fully. She uses "faculties" to denote the full range of mental powers that distinguish humans from animals, arguing that women's faculties are artificially stunted by a society that values ornamental accomplishments over intellectual cultivation.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft employs "faculties" as a comprehensive term for the mind's capacities: reason, understanding, judgment, imagination, and moral sensibility. These are not merely intellectual endowments but the very essence of human nature — "the power of improving her understanding, and the bent of her disposition" (002-033). For Wollstonecraft, faculties are given by God but must be actively exercised to develop; they atrophy through disuse, and the systematic denial of education to women constitutes a deliberate "cramping" of their rational powers. The exercise of faculties is not only an individual duty but the path to virtue itself (001-029).

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's central argument concerning faculties is that they are sexless — reason, judgment, and moral sense operate identically in men and women. The apparent inferiority of women's intellectual achievements is not a product of natural deficiency but of systematic neglect: "if all the faculties of woman's mind are only to be cultivated as they respect her dependence on man ... let her grovel contentedly" (002-063). She insists that proper education must "unfold" the faculties rather than direct them toward narrow domestic purposes.

The link between exercise and virtue is fundamental to her argument. Drawing on Enlightenment psychology, she argues that faculties strengthen through use: "the exercise of the faculties will produce this fondness" (002-046) — referring to the development of genuine affection through rational engagement rather than sentimental indulgence. Conversely, she warns that "the limbs and faculties are cramped with worse than Chinese bands" (003-015) when women are confined to the trivial pursuits prescribed by fashionable society.

Wollstonecraft extends the argument beyond individual development to social progress. She contends that a society that denies half its members the full exercise of their faculties cannot achieve genuine civilization. The "superior faculties of men" (002-030) are not inherently superior but have been developed through education and public engagement, while women's have been deliberately neglected.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Rousseau, in *Émile*, had argued that women's faculties were naturally different from men's — suited to domesticity and pleasing men rather than independent reasoning. Wollstonecraft directly challenges this: "the limbs and faculties are cramped with worse than Chinese bands" (003-015). Unlike Gregory and Fordyce, who urged women to conceal their intellectual capacities to avoid male disapproval, Wollstonecraft insists that "their faculties have room to unfold, and their virtues to gain strength" (002-073). Her position is closer to the rational Dissenting tradition that valued the cultivation of the mind as a religious duty, but she extends it uniquely to women.

## Key Passages

> "The most perfect education, in my opinion, is such an exercise of the understanding as is best calculated to strengthen the body and form the heart. Or, in other words, to enable the individual to attain such habits of virtue as will render it independent. In fact, it is a farce to call any being virtuous whose virtues do not result from the exercise of its own reason. This was Rousseau's opinion respecting men: I extend it to women." (002-073)

> "I wish to sum up what I have said. Consequently to argue that the woman's reason is not so clear, and her judgment not so solid as the man's, without allowing her the same chance to acquire firmness, is grossly to insult her. Let their faculties have room to unfold, and their virtues to gain strength, and then determine where the whole sex must stand in the intellectual scale." (002-073)

> "if all the faculties of woman's mind are only to be cultivated as they respect her dependence on man; if, when she obtains a husband, she has arrived at her goal, and meanly proud, is satisfied with such a paltry crown, let her grovel contentedly, scarcely raised by her employments above the animal kingdom" (002-063)

> "the blind submission imposed on women ... almost equally cramps their faculties" (001-029)

> "It is not business, extensive plans, or any of the excursive flights of ambition, that engross their attention; no, their thoughts are not employed in rearing such noble structures." (004-030)
