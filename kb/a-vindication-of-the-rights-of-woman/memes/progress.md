**Progress** is the animating conviction of Wollstonecraft's Enlightenment project — the belief that human knowledge, virtue, and social arrangements can and should improve through the exercise of reason. The *Vindication* does not argue for progress as an abstract proposition; it assumes it, and its entire argument is structured around a single urgent claim: that the subjection of women "retards the progress of reason" (004-011) and that their emancipation would accelerate it. The progress of the human mind is inseparable from the condition of women, and neither can advance while the other is held back.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's concept of progress is drawn from the mainstream of Enlightenment thought — the conviction, shared by thinkers from Condorcet to Kant, that human history exhibits a directional movement toward greater knowledge, freedom, and moral refinement. But she gives this conviction a distinctive inflection by insisting that the progress of the whole human race depends on the inclusion of women in its benefits.

"Progress" in the *Vindication* is always qualified by its object: the "progress of knowledge" (P02-006), the "progress of reason" (004-011), the "progress of human virtue and improvement in knowledge" (003-010), the "progress of the human understanding" (012-092). The recurrent formula is significant — progress is never conceived as a merely material or technological phenomenon but always as an advance in the powers of the mind. The "progress of civilization" (001-033) is treated with ambivalence: civilization can be "a curse" when it enriches the few at the expense of the many, when it refines manners while corrupting morals.

The metaphor of motion pervades Wollstonecraft's language of progress. Women are placed in a "station" from which they may either "advance" or "retard" the progress of "glorious principles" (P02-003). The subjection of women "stops the progress of the human mind" (003-027); their emancipation would remove the obstruction. The image is of a natural force — the forward movement of reason — that has been artificially dammed by human institutions. Remove the dam, and the force will resume its course.

Crucially, progress is gradual rather than sudden. "Every thing around us is in a progressive state" (005-125), Wollstonecraft observes, drawing an analogy between the slow growth of a tree and the "majestic marks of time" that the mind acquires through patient cultivation. The mind "in proportion to its dignity advances more slowly towards perfection" (005-125) — a formulation that reconciles the Enlightenment commitment to progress with a realistic acknowledgment of its pace.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

The argument about progress is the scaffolding on which the entire *Vindication* is built. The dedication to Talleyrand establishes the theme at the outset: Wollstonecraft wishes "to see woman placed in a station in which she would advance, instead of retarding, the progress of those glorious principles that give a substance to morality" (P02-003). The French Revolution has opened the question of women's rights; Wollstonecraft's intervention is designed to ensure that the progress it represents is not arrested by the exclusion of half the human race.

The logic is compressed into a single sentence that serves as the argument's fulcrum: "if she be not prepared by education to become the companion of man, she will stop the progress of knowledge, for truth must be common to all, or it will be inefficacious with respect to its influence on general practice" (P02-006). The chain of reasoning is: knowledge is cumulative and collective; its advance depends on the widest possible dissemination; women constitute half of humanity; therefore, to deny women knowledge is to cut off half the sources of its advancement. The progress of knowledge is not a luxury that can be deferred while other problems are addressed — it is the condition on which all other progress depends.

The obstructionist role that the subjection of women plays in human progress is specified with increasing precision. Women who are "not more rationally educated" mean that "the progress of human virtue and improvement in knowledge must receive continual checks" (003-010). The mechanism is twofold. First, uneducated women cannot contribute to the advancement of knowledge; their intellectual potential is wasted. Second, and more insidiously, uneducated women actively retard progress by transmitting their own ignorance to their children. The mother who lacks understanding cannot cultivate it in her offspring, and the cycle of ignorance perpetuates itself across generations.

The political dimension is equally important. "Each of these situations" — the condition of being "either a slave or a despot" — "equally retards the progress of reason" (004-011). Women who are enslaved cannot contribute to the advancement of knowledge; women who exercise despotic power through manipulation and cunning corrupt the social relations on which the progress of reason depends. The "narrowness of mind" that Wollstonecraft identifies as "the grand source of female folly and vice" is not a natural deficiency but the product of "the very constitution of civil governments" that has "put almost insuperable obstacles in the way to prevent the cultivation of the female understanding" (004-011).

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft's treatment of progress must be situated in relation to the two dominant Enlightenment narratives. The first, associated with Condorcet and Turgot, celebrated the progress of the human mind as an inevitable historical process — the gradual but irresistible triumph of reason over superstition and prejudice. Wollstonecraft shares this conviction but qualifies it with an insistence on human agency. Progress is not inevitable; it can be "stopped" by bad institutions, and it requires active effort to sustain.

The second narrative, associated with Rousseau, treated civilization itself as a form of corruption — a fall from a state of natural simplicity into artificiality and vice. Wollstonecraft engages this critique seriously but ultimately rejects its pessimism. The "progress of civilization" (001-033) can indeed be "a curse" when it enriches the few at the expense of the many, but the remedy is not a return to primitive simplicity but a more complete realization of the Enlightenment's own principles — the extension of reason and knowledge to those who have been denied them.

Against the conservative defenders of the existing order, who argued that women's subjection was natural and therefore unchangeable, Wollstonecraft deploys the concept of progress as a critical weapon. If "every thing around us is in a progressive state" (005-125), then the claim that women's condition cannot change is not merely false but incoherent — it contradicts the most fundamental law of nature and history. The question is not whether progress will occur but whether women will participate in it or be left behind by it.

## Key Passages

> Contending for the rights of women, my main argument is built on this simple principle, that if she be not prepared by education to become the companion of man, she will stop the progress of knowledge, for truth must be common to all, or it will be inefficacious with respect to its influence on general practice. (P02-006)

> Till women are more rationally educated, the progress of human virtue and improvement in knowledge must receive continual checks. (003-010)

> Each of these situations equally retards the progress of reason. The grand source of female folly and vice has ever appeared to me to arise from narrowness of mind; and the very constitution of civil governments has put almost insuperable obstacles in the way to prevent the cultivation of the female understanding. (004-011)

> To argue from analogy, every thing around us is in a progressive state; and when an unwelcome knowledge of life produces almost a satiety of life, and we discover by the natural course of things that all that is done under the sun is vanity, we are drawing near the awful close of the drama. (005-125)

> I earnestly wish to see woman placed in a station in which she would advance, instead of retarding, the progress of those glorious principles that give a substance to morality. (P02-003)
