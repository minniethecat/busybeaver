# Merit

Merit, for Wollstonecraft, is genuine personal worth — grounded in knowledge, industry, and virtue — as opposed to the accidental advantages of birth, rank, or wealth. It is the Enlightenment principle that social esteem should track actual contribution, not inherited status.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft deploys "merit" as a critical tool for distinguishing real from apparent worth. She opens her work by submitting it to judgment on its merits: "What are its merits will be decided in the judgment" (P01-013), establishing merit-based evaluation as her normative framework. She addresses Talleyrand with the argument that women must be enabled to "merit their esteem" (P02-005) — esteem, like all social goods, should be earned through demonstrated merit, not granted by courtesy.

The concept is defined in opposition to rank: "as the phrase turns, by his merit, becomes a servile parasite" (001-027) — in a society organized by rank, merit is perverted into servility. True merit is incompatible with flattery and subservience. She observes that "the noble struggles of suffering merit has raised admiration" (002-029), acknowledging that merit often goes unrecognized under corrupt social arrangements.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument for women's education is fundamentally meritocratic: women must be educated so that their merit, not their beauty or compliance, determines their social value. She contrasts the real merit of those who produce public goods — "the best works, and of greatest merit for the public, have proceeded from the unmarried or childless" (004-048) — with the unearned status of the aristocratic.

Her critique of contemporary gender relations frequently invokes merit: men do not value women for their merit but for their charm, and women, denied the opportunity to develop merit, substitute allure. She challenges men: "let them merit love, and they will obtain it" (005-089) — genuine affection, like genuine esteem, must be earned. The critique extends to women's inability to judge merit: "unacquainted with, or to that merit of which she has no idea" (005-036) — without education, women lack the capacity to discern merit in others or cultivate it in themselves.

In a striking passage, she observes that merit is often inversely related to social recognition: "the modest merit of reasonable men has, of course, been overlooked" (006-008), while showy sensibilities attract attention. This is not, she implies, a natural state but a consequence of a society that rewards display over substance.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The aristocratic worldview of Wollstonecraft's era held that merit was hereditary — the nobility possessed it by birth. The Enlightenment challenged this, arguing that merit was individual and achievement-based. Wollstonecraft radicalizes this Enlightenment meritocracy by extending it to women: if merit is individual and achievement-based, then denying women education is denying them the capacity for merit. Rousseau, despite his republicanism, excludes women from the merit economy — Sophie's virtue is compliance, not achievement.
