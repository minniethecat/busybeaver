# Propriety

In Wollstonecraft's critique, propriety denotes the socially prescribed code of female conduct — a "puerile kind of propriety" (002-003) that substitutes conventional decorum for authentic moral virtue. It is a key target of her argument that women are educated for appearance rather than character.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft distinguishes between genuine moral propriety — conduct grounded in rational principle — and the "puerile kind of propriety" that society demands of women. The latter is a system of external rules governing dress, speech, and behaviour, designed to make women pleasing to men rather than virtuous in themselves. She notes that "attention to a puerile kind of propriety, will obtain for them the protection of man" (002-003), exposing the transactional nature of this social code.

The term appears throughout her work as a critical lens: "according to this view of things... with perfect propriety" (002-061) — the irony in "perfect propriety" underscores her challenge to the system that equates conventional decorum with moral worth. She argues that "to speak with strict propriety God, has made all things right" (002-053), invoking a higher standard of propriety rooted in divine order rather than social convention.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's treatment of propriety is consistently critical. She identifies propriety as one of the mechanisms by which women are constrained to superficiality: "they could not then with equal propriety be termed the sweet flowers that smile but for a season" (004-043). The language of propriety justifies women's confinement to the decorative and the ephemeral.

Most devastatingly, she observes that "propriety" functions as a mask for convenience: "too often been sacrificed to propriety; — another word for convenience" (005-109). This equation of propriety with convenience reveals her analysis that social norms around female conduct serve the interests of those in power, not the moral development of women themselves.

She acknowledges a legitimate distinction between modesty and propriety: "produce modesty, though it may propriety of conduct, when it is merely..." (007-011). Modesty, for Wollstonecraft, is an internal virtue; propriety is its external, potentially hollow, manifestation. "But, with respect to propriety of behaviour, excepting one case..." (007-012) — she grants that propriety has some legitimate scope but insists it must be grounded in genuine modesty, not mere appearance.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The conduct-book tradition — represented by Dr. Gregory, Dr. Fordyce, and others — elevated propriety to the supreme female virtue. Gregory advised women to cultivate "propriety of behaviour" as their primary moral duty, without distinguishing between authentic virtue and mere decorum. Wollstonecraft counters that this elevation of propriety over genuine virtue produces precisely the artificiality that the conduct-book writers claim to remedy.

Rousseau's Sophie is educated entirely for propriety — her virtue consists in conforming to social expectations. Wollstonecraft's argument that propriety must be grounded in rational principle directly challenges this model, insisting that women, like men, must understand the reasons for their conduct rather than merely conforming to external rules.
