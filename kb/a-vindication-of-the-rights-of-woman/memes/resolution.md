# Resolution

Wollstonecraft treats resolution as a distinct virtue of character — the capacity for firmness of purpose, particularly in the face of domestic duties and social pressure — and argues that it is a quality women are systematically denied the opportunity to develop.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft presents resolution as necessary for even the most conventional feminine role: "to fulfil domestic duties much resolution is necessary, and a serious kind of perseverance." (004-066) By locating resolution within domestic life, she challenges the assumption that firmness of character is a masculine prerogative.

She extends this argument in a comparative frame: women "have acquired courage and resolution; I only contend that the men have acquired still more." (004-101) The claim is twofold — that women are capable of resolution, and that their relative deficiency is a product of education, not nature.

The moral dimension of resolution is clearest in her observation that people lack "sufficient foresight or resolution, to endure a small evil at the moment, to avoid a greater hereafter." (005-098) Here resolution is not mere stubbornness but a form of practical wisdom — the ability to subordinate immediate impulse to long-term good.

## Key Passages

> "To fulfil domestic duties much resolution is necessary, and a serious kind of perseverance, that requires a more firm support than emotions, however lively and true to nature." (004-066)

> "Women, I allow, may have acquired courage and resolution; I only contend that the men have acquired still more." (004-101)
