In Wollstonecraft's moral psychology, **cunning** is a distinctly female vice produced by the structural conditions of subordination. She identifies it as "the natural opponent of strength" (P03-014), arguing that women, denied legitimate power and rational education, develop cunning as a survival strategy — an "exertion of cunning" that is "only an instinct of nature" (P02-021) under conditions of dependence, not an inherent female trait.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft defines cunning in sharp opposition to wisdom and reason. She is careful to distinguish it from intelligence — cunning is intelligence perverted by social conditions, the "human weakness, justly termed cunning" (002-003) that flourishes when the mind is denied proper objects for its exercise. Her definition is consistently pejorative: cunning is "mean doublings" (012-034), a "pitiful cunning which disgracefully characterises" (012-032) the products of a bad education.

Crucially, Wollstonecraft refuses to naturalise cunning as feminine. When she writes that tyranny "gives birth to cunning" (P03-014), she identifies the causal chain: unjust power → resistance through indirection → cunning. This is a structural analysis, not a characterological one. Cunning, in her framework, is what reason becomes when denied legitimate channels of expression and action.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's most concentrated treatment of cunning appears in Chapter II, where she declares that "cunning should not be opposed to wisdom" (002-036) — meaning that cunning is not merely a lesser version of wisdom but its structural inversion. Wisdom operates openly, through reason; cunning operates covertly, through manipulation. The opposition is not a matter of degree but of kind, and the social conditions that produce the one preclude the development of the other.

She extends this analysis to marriage, observing that when the wife lacks "understanding to make her companion," she has "recourse to cunning to undermine the habitual affection" (004-054) of her husband. This domestic cunning is, for Wollstonecraft, a natural consequence of marriages built on property and appearance rather than rational companionship. The wife who cannot argue openly must manipulate covertly.

Wollstonecraft is equally critical of conduct-book writers who, in her view, effectively teach cunning under the name of propriety. She attacks the "partial moralist" who "recommends cunning systematically and plausibly" (005-023), identifying a tradition — running from Fordyce to Gregory — that instructs women in the arts of strategic compliance while calling it virtue. "Cunning or address" (005-027), she insists, can "never dwell" with a disciplined mind.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Where Rousseau naturalised feminine cunning as a charming aspect of women's "instinct," Wollstonecraft denaturalises it entirely. Rousseau's Sophie is taught to manipulate through apparent submission — precisely the "cunning and dissimulation" (002-075) that Wollstonecraft argues no rational being should be taught. She also directly counters Gregory's *Legacy to His Daughters*, which advises women to conceal their wit and learning; for Wollstonecraft, such advice is a recipe for producing "that pitiful cunning" (012-032) rather than genuine virtue.

## Key Passages

> Tyranny, in whatever part of society it rears its brazen front, will ever undermine morality, and gives birth to cunning, the natural opponent of strength. (P03-014)

> It follows then, that cunning should not be opposed to wisdom, and little arts to great exertions. (002-036)

> The wife, in the mean time, equally without understanding to make her companion, has recourse to cunning to undermine the habitual affection. (004-054)
