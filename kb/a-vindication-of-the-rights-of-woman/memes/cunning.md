## Definition

Wollstonecraft defines cunning as a psychological strategy of the weak — "the [[nature|natural]] opponent of strength" [P03-015] — and traces it directly to the social conditions that deny women access to legitimate forms of power and rational [[education]]. Cunning, in her analysis, is not an innate female trait but a learned behaviour: when women are taught from infancy that their only value lies in beauty and pleasing, and are systematically denied the [[education]] that would cultivate [[reason]] and [[virtue]], they [[nature|natural]]ly develop indirect strategies to achieve their ends.

This diagnosis is central to Wollstonecraft's argument for women's [[education]]. She insists that "artificial weakness produces a propensity to tyrannize, and gives birth to cunning" [P03-015]. The causal chain is clear: artificial weakness → tyranny over those weaker → cunning as the mechanism of control. In a more just society where women were educated to [[reason]] and [[virtue]], cunning would be unnecessary.

## In Context

The concept of cunning appears primarily in the Introduction and early chapters. In the Introduction, Wollstonecraft first names it: "this artificial weakness produces a propensity to tyrannize, and gives birth to cunning, the [[nature|natural]] opponent of strength" [P03-015]. She argues that cunning "leads them to play off those contemptible infantile airs that undermine esteem even whilst they excite [[passion|desire]]."

In Chapter IV, Wollstonecraft extends the analysis: women, having been taught that "the sovereignty of beauty" is their only power, "have, to maintain their power, been obliged to have recourse to low cunning" [004-011]. The word "obliged" is significant — Wollstonecraft presents cunning not as a moral failing of individual women but as the inevitable consequence of a social system that denies them legitimate agency.

Chapter V adds a further dimension: Wollstonecraft critiques the wife who "preserves her [[equality|equal]]ity, and governs him while she affects to obey" [005-033], identifying this as a species of cunning rather than genuine [[equality|equal]]ity. The appearance of submission masking actual manipulation is, for Wollstonecraft, a degraded form of relation that corrupts both parties.

## Related Concepts

- [[tyranny]] — artificial weakness produces tyranny, and tyranny is exercised through cunning
- [[education]] — proper [[education]] would cultivate [[reason]] and [[virtue]], making cunning unnecessary
- [[beauty]] — women taught to value beauty alone inevitably resort to cunning when beauty fades
- [[virtue]] — cunning is incompatible with genuine [[virtue]], which requires honesty and directness
- [[marriage]] — Wollstonecraft critiques marriages built on cunning manipulation rather than rational [[friendship]]
