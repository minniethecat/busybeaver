**Dignity** is, for Wollstonecraft, the quality of worth that belongs to every rational being in virtue of their capacity for reason and virtue. It is both an inherent attribute of human nature — grounded in the soul's relation to God — and an achievement that must be cultivated through education, independence, and the exercise of moral agency. Throughout the *Vindication*, Wollstonecraft argues that women are systematically denied "their proper dignity — their equal rank as rational creatures" (P01-013), and that the recovery of this dignity is the central purpose of female education.

The concept operates at the intersection of Wollstonecraft's theology, her moral psychology, and her social criticism. Theologically, dignity is grounded in the soul's creation by God and its capacity for knowledge of the divine. Psychologically, dignity is experienced as "conscious dignity" (003-054) — the awareness of one's own moral worth that makes submission to unjust authority impossible. Socially, dignity is what the existing order of gender relations denies to women by reducing them to "objects of pity" and "objects of contempt" (004-082).

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's understanding of dignity synthesises several distinct traditions. From the Christian tradition, she inherits the conception of human dignity as a gift of God — beings made "in the image of God" possess a worth that cannot be alienated by any human authority. "The dignity of the female soul" (003-031) is as real and as inviolable as that of the male, whatever the "mistaken notions of dignity" (001-018) that society propagates.

From the Enlightenment, she inherits the conception of dignity as grounded in reason. "The dignity of a rational will that only bows to God" (002-078) is the dignity of a being capable of self-governance — of making moral judgments and acting on principle rather than inclination. This is the "real dignity of man" (002-015) that Wollstonecraft prefers to "childish state" and the trappings of rank.

Crucially, Wollstonecraft develops a distinctively social and gendered conception of dignity. Dignity is not merely an abstract attribute; it is something that social arrangements can either nurture or crush. "Can dignity of mind exist with such trivial cares?" (004-032) — the question is rhetorical. The "trivial cares" of female life — fashion, manners, the endless labour of pleasing — are incompatible with the cultivation of dignity, because they absorb the attention that ought to be directed toward the cultivation of reason and virtue.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument about dignity is inseparable from her critique of female education. The existing system of "female accomplishments" is designed to produce women who are pleasing, not women who are dignified. "They lose all simplicity, all dignity of mind, in acquiring power" (003-029) — the "power" being the power to attract male attention, which is purchased at the cost of everything that makes a human being worthy of respect.

The restoration of dignity requires a thoroughgoing reform of education. "Let them attain conscious dignity by feeling themselves only dependent on God" (002-079) — the phrase "only dependent on God" is the key. Dignity requires independence from human authority, particularly the authority of men over women. A woman who "submits without conviction to a parent or husband" (002-078) has surrendered her dignity along with her judgment.

Wollstonecraft also connects dignity to virtue. "Virtue, as the very word imports, should have its own intrinsic worth" (004-092) — dignity is not something added to virtue from the outside but the quality that virtue itself possesses. A virtuous person is dignified because virtue is the highest expression of human nature. Conversely, vice — particularly the "cunning, mean, and selfish" (005-078) vices that dependence cultivates — is undignified because it degrades the person who practises it.

The social dimension of dignity is crucial. "To restore to them their lost dignity, and make them, as a part of the human species, labour by reforming themselves to reform the world" (003-030) — dignity is not a private possession but a public responsibility. The dignified woman does not merely enjoy her own self-respect; she contributes to the moral improvement of society.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The eighteenth-century discourse of "dignity" was dominated by considerations of rank. Dignity attached to offices, titles, and estates, not to persons as such. Wollstonecraft's insistence that dignity belongs to every rational being as such is a radical democratisation of the concept — a move that aligns her with the levelling impulse of Enlightenment thought against the hierarchical assumptions of the old regime.

Rousseau's treatment of dignity is instructive. In *Émile*, the dignity of the male citizen is grounded in his participation in the general will; Sophie's dignity, by contrast, is grounded in her modesty and her submission to male judgment. Wollstonecraft's response is that dignity grounded in submission is not dignity at all — it is merely the "imaginary dignity" (005-078) that the powerful grant to the powerless to make their subjection more palatable.

The Scottish Enlightenment tradition — particularly Adam Smith and Adam Ferguson — treated dignity as a quality of character cultivated through participation in commercial society. Wollstonecraft shares this interest in the social conditions of dignity but is more critical: commercial society, as it actually exists, has merely created new forms of dependence that deny dignity to women even as it proclaims universal rights.

## Key Passages

> Let them attain conscious dignity by feeling themselves only dependent on God. (002-079)

> Contending for the rights of woman, my main argument is built on this simple principle, that if she be not prepared by education to become the companion of man, she will stop the progress of knowledge and virtue; for truth must be common to all, or it will be inefficacious with respect to its influence on general practice. (P02-006)

> They lose all simplicity, all dignity of mind, in acquiring power, and act as if they thought it a sufficient excuse for their departing from the straight line of truth and virtue, that they have been obliged to do it, and must still continue to do it, in order to preserve their rank. (003-029)

> Can dignity of mind exist with such trivial cares? ... They have always been taught to look up to man as a being of a superior order, and to regard themselves as formed to please him. (004-032)
