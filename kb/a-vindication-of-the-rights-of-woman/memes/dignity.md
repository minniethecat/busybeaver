# Dignity

## Definition

**Dignity**, in Wollstonecraft's moral vocabulary, is the quality of being worthy
of respect — a worth grounded not in rank, wealth, or beauty but in the possession
of reason and the capacity for virtue. To live with dignity is to govern oneself
by rational principle rather than by the opinions of others, and to claim the
rights that belong to every rational creature.

## Dignity and Reason

Dignity is inseparable from reason. Wollstonecraft's argument that women deserve
equal rights rests on the premise that they possess the same rational nature as
men, and therefore the same dignity:

To deny women education and independence is to treat them as less than fully
human — to deny their rational dignity. The "divine spark of reason" that
elevates humanity above the animals is present in women no less than in men.

## The Degradation of Women's Dignity

Wollstonecraft catalogues the many ways in which eighteenth-century society
systematically degrades women's dignity. An education focused on accomplishments
rather than understanding; a social order that values women only for their beauty
and charm; an economic system that makes women dependent on men — all these
conspire to reduce women to "the mere amusement of a tyrant."

Women are taught to value the appearance of virtue over its substance, to seek
approbation rather than self-respect — a substitution that is fatal to genuine
dignity. A woman who governs her conduct by what others will think cannot possess
the inward self-respect that constitutes true dignity.

## Dignity and Independence

Dignity requires independence — the capacity to think and act for oneself.
Wollstonecraft insists that women must be educated for independence, not for
dependence on husbands or fathers. Only an independent woman can be truly
virtuous, because only she can choose virtue freely rather than conform to it
under duress.

## Dignity vs. Rank

Wollstonecraft distinguishes genuine dignity (grounded in rational and moral
excellence) from the false dignity of social rank and title. The aristocrat who
claims dignity by virtue of birth alone has no genuine claim to it; the
individual — man or woman — who cultivates reason and virtue possesses true
dignity regardless of social station.
