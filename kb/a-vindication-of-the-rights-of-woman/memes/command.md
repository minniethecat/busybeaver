# Command

In _A Vindication_, Wollstonecraft distinguishes between command (exercising authority by force) and reason (persuading through rational argument), aligning the former with despotic power and the latter with enlightened governance. The concept bridges her critique of political tyranny and her analysis of domestic relations.

## Command vs. Reason

The antithesis is stated most directly in Chapter 11: "It is easier, I grant, to command than reason; but it does not follow from hence, that children cannot comprehend the reason why they are made to do certain things habitually" (011-018). Command, for Wollstonecraft, represents the lazy exercise of power — the imposition of will without justification. Reason, by contrast, cultivates understanding and judgment.

## Political Command

In the political realm, Wollstonecraft draws on the metaphor of a standing army to illustrate the defects of command: "the main body must be moved by command, like the waves of the sea; for the strong wind of authority pushes the crowd of subalterns forward, they scarcely know or care why, with headlong fury" (001-026). This image of mindless obedience contrasts with the republican ideal of citizens who act from rational conviction.

The pleasure derived from command is likewise condemned. In Chapter 1, she describes "the pleasure of commanding flattering sycophants" as one of the corruptions that "overwhelm the mass of mankind, and make liberty a convenient handle for mock patriotism" (001-011).

## Domestic Command

The critique of command extends into the family. In Chapter 5, Wollstonecraft observes that the nobility, having "always had the command of money, acquire a graceful ease of deportment" (005-058) — but she distinguishes this external grace from the deeper grace of mind that comes from independence and sincerity. Command, even when softened by manners, remains a substitute for genuine virtue.

## Command and Respect

Wollstonecraft allows for a positive form of command only when it arises from earned moral authority rather than brute force. She contrasts religion that is "confounded with irksome ceremonies and unreasonable restraints" with "the sober austere one that commands respect whilst it inspires fear" (012-015). True command, in this sense, is indistinguishable from respect freely given.
