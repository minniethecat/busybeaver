# Parental Affection

## Definition

**Parental affection** is the subject of Wollstonecraft's sustained inquiry in Chapter X, where she develops a distinctive and counterintuitive theory: parental love is not an innate, natural instinct but a moral sentiment that must be cultivated by reason and the habitual exercise of duty. She opens the chapter with a striking claim: "Parental affection is, perhaps, the blindest modification of perverse self-love" [010-002]. This deliberately provocative formulation challenges the sentimental idealization of parental love common in eighteenth-century culture.

Wollstonecraft distinguishes between two kinds of parental feeling. The first, which she calls "brutish" [010-005], is an unreflective attachment that loves children simply because they are one's own -- a form of extended self-love that sacrifices justice, truth, and humanity to narrow family interests. The second is true parental affection, which grows out of "the habitual exercise of a mutual sympathy" [010-008] and is inseparable from the discharge of parental duties. For Wollstonecraft, genuine parental affection is not the cause of good parenting but its consequence -- it "produces filial duty" [010-009] rather than arising automatically from the biological relationship.

## In Context

Chapter X is devoted entirely to this concept, developing the argument through a series of linked propositions. Wollstonecraft begins by noting that the French language usefully distinguishes between a rational and an irrational form of the term, a distinction English lacks [010-002]. She then exposes parental affection as "a pretext to tyrannize where it can be done with impunity" [010-003], arguing that only "good and wise men are content with the respect that will bear discussion" [010-003].

The application to women is particularly pointed. Wollstonecraft observes that woman, "a slave in every situation to prejudice, seldom exerts enlightened maternal affection" [010-005] -- women either neglect their children or spoil them through "improper indulgence" [010-005]. She identifies the root problem as the want of reason: "It is this want of reason in their affections which makes women so often run into extremes" [010-007] between excessive fondness and unnatural carelessness.

Wollstonecraft's most radical claim is that "natural affection, as it is termed, I believe to be a very weak tie" [010-008]. Affections, she insists, "must grow out of the habitual exercise of a mutual sympathy" [010-008]. This position has profound implications for her educational philosophy: mothers who send their infants to nurses and then to schools cannot expect to develop genuine maternal feeling, because the affection is a product of the care itself.

## Key Passages

> "Parental affection is, perhaps, the blindest modification of perverse self-love; for we have not, like the French two terms to distinguish the pursuit of a natural and reasonable desire, from the ignorant calculations of weakness." [010-002]

> "Parental affection, indeed, in many minds, is but a pretext to tyrannize where it can be done with impunity, for only good and wise men are content with the respect that will bear discussion." [010-003]

> "Natural affection, as it is termed, I believe to be a very weak tie, affections must grow out of the habitual exercise of a mutual sympathy." [010-008]

> "Parental affection produces filial duty." [010-009]
