## Definition

Moderation in Wollstonecraft's usage operates on two levels: as a rhetorical stance — the measured, reasonable tone she adopts to persuade her audience of the justice of her claims — and as a moral principle of self-governance. When Wollstonecraft employs the phrase "to speak with moderation" (012-006, 012-027), she signals her commitment to rational argumentation over polemical excess, positioning herself as a reasonable reformer rather than a radical incendiary. This rhetorical moderation is strategic: by framing her demands for women's rights in the language of measured reason, she seeks to make them palatable to an audience that might otherwise dismiss her as immoderate or unwomanly. At the same time, moderation as a virtue of character — the capacity to govern one's passions and appetites through reason — aligns with the broader Enlightenment ideal of rational self-mastery that Wollstonecraft advocates for both sexes.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's deployment of moderation is rhetorically sophisticated. When she writes "to speak with moderation, half of the time" (012-006), she is both practising and performing moderation — the qualifying phrase itself enacts the measured tone it describes, even as it makes a bold claim about the waste of time in current educational arrangements. Similarly, when she asserts that certain tasks in education could be done "to speak with moderation, by the child itself" (012-027), the qualifying phrase cushions what might otherwise seem a radical proposal for child-directed learning.

Beyond its rhetorical function, moderation represents for Wollstonecraft a virtue of character closely related to self-governance. The capacity to moderate one's passions, to exercise restraint, and to govern desire through reason is central to her conception of the virtuous individual. This virtue is, however, unequally distributed by a system that teaches women to cultivate sensibility rather than self-command. Wollstonecraft's advocacy of moderation is thus both a personal ethical prescription and a political argument: women must be educated to govern themselves, and the moderate, reasonable tone of her own writing models the rational character she advocates.

## Key Passages

> (012-006)... to speak with moderation, half of the time...

Wollstonecraft's qualifying phrase introduces a bold claim about the inefficiency of current educational practices. The rhetorical modesty of "to speak with moderation" serves to make the substantive criticism — that half of children's time in school is wasted — more palatable. The passage exemplifies her strategy of advancing radical arguments in moderate language.

> (012-027)... done entirely, to speak with moderation, by the child itself...

Here Wollstonecraft uses the same qualifying phrase to advance a progressive educational proposal: that children are capable of self-directed learning. The moderation of her language contrasts with the relative radicalism of the pedagogical principle she advocates.
