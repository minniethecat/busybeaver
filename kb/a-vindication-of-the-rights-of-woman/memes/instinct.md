The innate, unreflective impulse that operates without deliberation. Wollstonecraft draws a sharp distinction between instinct — which guides animals — and reason — which ought to guide human beings. She argues that denying women education effectively reduces them to the level of creatures governed by instinct rather than intellect.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft defines instinct by contrast with reason. Brutes "instinctively observe" decency (P02-004) and possess an "unerring instinct" (002-070) that guides their conduct without deliberation. Human beings, however, are meant to be governed by reason. When women are denied education, they are left with only "a sort of instinctive common sense, never brought to the test of reason" (002-018). Wollstonecraft's key polemical move is to equate the fashionable ideal of feminine sensibility with mere instinct: Samuel Johnson's definition of sensibility as "quickness of sensation; quickness of perception; delicacy" gives her "no other idea than of the most exquisitely polished instinct" (004-046).

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument about instinct is inseparable from her theory of human nature. Human beings occupy a middle position between brutes and angels: we are not governed by unerring instinct like animals, nor do we possess perfect reason like God. Our dignity consists in the capacity to rise above instinct through the cultivation of reason. When women are educated only to please — when their education consists of accomplishments rather than intellectual development — they are effectively reduced to the level of brutes.

"Has been denied to woman," she writes, "and instinct, sublimated into wit and cunning, for the purposes of life, has been substituted in the place of reason" (004-006). The fashionable cultivation of sensibility is not an elevation of feeling above reason but a degradation of reason into instinct. "I discern not a trace of the image of God in either sensation or matter. Refined seventy times seven, they are still material; intellect dwells not there; nor will fire ever make lead gold!" (004-046).

Wollstonecraft extends this analysis to parental affection, arguing that genuine parental love requires reason to elevate it above mere animal instinct. A mother governed only by instinct "either destroy the embryo in the womb, or cast it off when born" (008-031). True maternal affection is "that ennobles instinct" through reason and moral reflection (008-027). Similarly, filial duty must be founded on rational gratitude, not on "blind obedience to a powerful instinct" (011-002).

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

In the eighteenth-century discourse, "instinct" carried both positive and negative connotations. Sentimental writers sometimes valorized instinct as a natural moral guide uncorrupted by artificial reason. Wollstonecraft rejects this valorization entirely. For her, instinct is never a substitute for reason, and any ethical system that relies on "the instinctive feelings" (005-119) rather than rational principle is building on sand. This places her in opposition to Rousseau's naturalism, which she sees as glorifying a sub-rational state that is beneath human dignity.

## Key Passages

> The great misfortune is this, that they both acquire manners before morals, and a knowledge of life before they have from reflection, any acquaintance with the grand ideal outline of human nature. The consequence is natural; satisfied with common nature, they become a prey to prejudices, and taking all their opinions on credit, they blindly submit to authority. So that if they have any sense, it is a kind of instinctive glance, that catches proportions, and decides with respect to manners; but fails when arguments are to be pursued below the surface, or opinions analyzed. (002-026)

> "Quickness of sensation; quickness of perception; delicacy." Thus is it defined by Dr. Johnson; and the definition gives me no other idea than of the most exquisitely polished instinct. I discern not a trace of the image of God in either sensation or matter. Refined seventy times seven, they are still material; intellect dwells not there; nor will fire ever make lead gold! (004-046)

> The simple definition of the morality of the Mosaic law, is parental affection — all the crimes committed against the children were designed as a punishment to the parents. If, indeed, we try the morality of the Bible by this touchstone, we shall at once see the inconsistency of the system; because they obeyed a powerful instinct? (011-002)
