## Overview

_Ars Amatoria_ (The Art of Love) is a three-book poem by the Roman poet Publius Ovidius Naso (Ovid), composed around 1 BCE to 1 CE. Written in elegiac couplets, the first two books instruct men in the arts of seduction and maintaining a love affair, while the third book offers corresponding advice to women. The work's witty and amoral treatment of romantic pursuit made it both popular and controversial; Augustus banished Ovid in 8 CE, partly citing the _Ars Amatoria_ as contributing to the moral laxity of Rome.

## In A Vindication of the Rights of Woman

Ovid's _Ars Amatoria_ is part of the classical literary heritage that informed eighteenth-century European discourse on love, courtship, and relations between the sexes. Wollstonecraft does not cite the _Ars Amatoria_ directly by name, but her argument engages extensively with the cultural tradition of seduction literature that Ovid's work inaugurated.

In _A Vindication_, Wollstonecraft critiques the entire structure of gallantry, coquetry, and artificial courtship that treats women as objects of male desire rather than as rational beings. The tradition exemplified by the _Ars Amatoria_ — which instructs men in the arts of conquest and women in the arts of pleasing — is precisely the cultural formation that Wollstonecraft's feminist philosophy seeks to dismantle. She argues that such conventions reduce women to "alluring mistresses" rather than "affectionate wives and rational mothers."

## Cultural Context

Wollstonecraft was well-educated in the classics and would have been familiar with Ovid's work. Her argument in _A Vindication_ draws on the classical tradition while also challenging its assumptions about gender. The _Ars Amatoria_ thus represents one element of the classical-erotic literary tradition against which Wollstonecraft's Enlightenment feminism defined itself.
