The earliest stage of human life, extending through childhood, that Wollstonecraft treats as the critical period during which the foundations of character, health, and reason are laid. She uses "infancy" in two distinct but related senses: literally, to refer to the early years of individual development, during which habits of body and mind are formed that determine the trajectory of the entire life; and analogically, to describe the "infancy of society" (001-032) — a historical stage in which humanity was governed by passion and custom rather than reason, and from which civilisation is slowly emerging. In both senses, infancy represents a condition of maximum plasticity and maximum peril: the right interventions at this stage produce a healthy, rational adult; the wrong ones produce a being "corrupted from their infancy" (003-011) who may never recover the capacity for genuine virtue.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's use of "infancy" encompasses more than the modern sense of babyhood. Following eighteenth-century usage, she extends the term to cover the entire period of childhood before the full development of reason — roughly, the first twelve to fifteen years of life. This is the period during which, in her analysis, the most consequential educational decisions are made: whether the child will be encouraged to exercise body and mind or confined and indulged; whether the child will be taught to reason or trained to obey; whether the child's natural "wildness" will be channelled into vigorous health or suppressed into artificial docility.

The concept carries particular weight in her argument because she insists that "women are told from their infancy" (002-003) a set of lessons that determine their entire subsequent development. The gendering of education begins not in adolescence but in the earliest years: "girls are from their earliest infancy fond of dress" (005-005) only because they are taught to be so, and the lesson, once learned, shapes everything that follows. This is why Wollstonecraft's educational proposals focus so intently on the reform of early childhood education: if the first lessons are rational, the subsequent development of reason is natural; if the first lessons are corrupt, the subsequent development is a struggle against a foundation already laid.

The analogical use of "infancy" to describe the infancy of society serves to historicise the condition of women. Just as individual human beings pass through a stage of infancy before reaching the maturity of reason, so human societies pass through stages of development from infancy to civilisation. The implication — radical for its time — is that the subordination of women is a feature of society's infancy, not a permanent condition ordained by nature or God. "In the infancy of society, when men were just emerging from a state of barbarism" (001-032), might made right and women were treated as property; as society matures, reason replaces force as the organising principle of human relations, and the subordination of women becomes increasingly difficult to justify.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument proceeds from the premise that "the infancy of children" (003-014) is the period during which the most consequential educational work is done — and that the current system does this work badly. She reports having "had an opportunity of observing more girls in their infancy than J. J. Rousseau" (003-020) and draws on her own experience as a governess and teacher to challenge Rousseau's claims about the "first dawn of the female character." The girl whose constitution has not been ruined by confinement is, she insists, "as wild and romping as a boy" (003-020) — and it is only the systematic imposition of gender norms from the earliest years that creates the appearance of natural difference.

This observation has profound implications for her educational programme. If the differences between male and female character are the product of education from infancy, then a reformed education — one that treats boys and girls identically in the early years — would produce women capable of the same rational development as men. The "inattention to health during infancy" (003-021) that produces weak, sickly women is not a concession to natural delicacy but a failure of care that damages women for life. The remedy is to attend to infancy — to the health, exercise, and early education of children of both sexes — as the foundation of all subsequent development.

Wollstonecraft also uses the concept of infancy to address the obligations of parents, particularly mothers. "The care of children in their infancy is one of the grand duties" (010-008) of motherhood, and a woman who neglects this duty in favour of fashion or pleasure has failed in the most fundamental obligation of her station. Yet Wollstonecraft is careful not to make this argument a justification for confining women to the nursery. The point is not that infancy is women's exclusive domain but that infancy is too important to be left to women who have been educated only to please. "Proper attention to helpless infancy has a right to require" (011-002) that mothers be educated to reason, because only a rational mother can raise rational children.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Rousseau's *Émile* opens with the declaration that "everything is good as it leaves the hands of the Author of things; everything degenerates in the hands of man." This emphasis on the natural goodness of infancy and the corrupting influence of society is superficially similar to Wollstonecraft's argument, but the differences are decisive. Rousseau's remedy is to isolate the child from society and allow nature to take its course; Wollstonecraft's remedy is to reform society so that the child's natural capacities can be developed within it. Moreover, Rousseau's prescriptions for infancy are sharply gendered: Émile is to be hardened and made independent, Sophie is to be softened and made pleasing. Wollstonecraft insists that infancy is the period during which this gendering must be refused — that boys and girls must be educated together, to the same standard, from the earliest years.

## Key Passages

> Women are told from their infancy, and taught by the example of their mothers, that a little knowledge of human weakness, justly termed cunning, softness of temper, outward obedience, and a scrupulous attention to a puerile kind of propriety, will obtain for them the protection of man. (002-003)

> I have, probably, had an opportunity of observing more girls in their infancy than J. J. Rousseau. I can recollect my own feelings, and I have looked steadily around me; yet, so far from coinciding with him in opinion respecting the first dawn of the female character, I will venture to affirm, that a girl, whose constitution has not been ruined by the unnatural confinement of a boarding-school, or by an unremitting attention to her person, is often as wild and romping as a boy. (003-020)

> Taught from their infancy, that beauty is woman's sceptre, the mind shapes itself to the body, and, roaming round its gilt cage, only seeks to adorn its prison. (003-025)
