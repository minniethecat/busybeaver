# Negligence

Wollstonecraft identifies negligence — particularly parental negligence — as a structural social harm that undermines the formation of rational character in children, placing it alongside indulgence as a twin vice of child-rearing.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft frames negligence as one pole of a harmful binary in child-rearing, the other being excessive indulgence. She poses the question directly: "whether negligence or indulgence is most hurtful" to children's development. (004-068) The answer is not simply that both are bad, but that both produce the same result — failure to cultivate reason and virtue.

She argues that much of what passes for fate or natural disposition is in fact preventable: "is allowed to rise from the negligence of parents; and still these are the parents who complain most bitterly of the ingratitude of their children." (011-008) The circularity is deliberate — negligent parents produce ungrateful children, then blame nature rather than their own failure.

Wollstonecraft extends the concept beyond individual families to social institutions, proposing a system where complaints of negligence could be formally reported: "to whom any complaint of negligence, etc. might be made, if signed by a respectable person." (012-049) This proposal links negligence to her broader plan for national education reform.

The capstone of her argument appears in the final chapter: neither "destroyed by unnatural negligence nor blind fondness," children would develop into rational adults. (013-060) Negligence is thus framed not as a minor parental failing but as a systematic impediment to human improvement.

## Key Passages

> "It has been made questionable, whether negligence or indulgence is most hurtful to the character." (004-068)

> "Neither destroyed by unnatural negligence nor blind fondness, how few would be spoiled by the mother!" (013-060)
