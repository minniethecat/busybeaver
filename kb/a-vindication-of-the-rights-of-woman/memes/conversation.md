**Conversation** in the *Vindication* is more than social exchange — it is the site where the intellectual inequality of the sexes is most visibly enacted and where, Wollstonecraft argues, it can begin to be repaired. Women's conversation betrays the poverty of their education; the conversation they are denied — rational discourse with thoughtful partners of both sexes — is precisely what would develop their minds and render them fit companions rather than decorative amusements.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft distinguishes between several kinds of conversation, each carrying a different moral and intellectual weight. At the lowest level is the "foolish conversation" (005-009) to which girls are condemned — the talk of dolls and trivialities that is the natural product of an education that neglects the understanding. Slightly above this is the "superficial" conversation of accomplished women (004-097), which possesses a certain polish but lacks substance. At the highest level is "rational conversation" (012-087) — discourse in which both sexes participate as intellectual equals, speaking of what they know and think rather than merely of what pleases.

The key concept, however, is not conversation itself but the relationship between conversation and knowledge. Wollstonecraft uses conversation as a diagnostic: "A man speaks of what he knows, a woman of what pleases her; the one requires knowledge, the other taste" (005-031). This is Rousseau's formulation, which Wollstonecraft quotes in order to refute. The fact that women speak only of what pleases is not a fact about women's nature but about their education — given knowledge, they would speak of it. The poverty of women's conversation is an indictment of the system that produces it, not of the beings it produces.

The positive ideal is conversation as mutual cultivation. Women should, "by reading and conversations with both sexes, indiscriminately, improve their mind" (013-067). The adverb "indiscriminately" is crucial — it is the segregation of conversational spheres that preserves intellectual inequality, and the mixing of minds across the sex boundary that promises to dissolve it.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument about conversation operates on three levels simultaneously: the diagnostic, the prescriptive, and the political.

Diagnostically, conversation reveals the intellectual starvation of women. The fact that women "had few topics of conversation, and fewer refinements of sentiment" (013-033) is not a trivial social observation but evidence of systematic deprivation. When women's minds are not furnished with knowledge, their conversation cannot rise above the "muddy current" (002-021) of superficial social exchange — a phrase Wollstonecraft applies to soldiers as well, drawing an analogy between two groups whose education has neglected the understanding in favour of social polish.

The prescriptive argument is that conversation itself can be an instrument of education. Wollstonecraft proposes that in her ideal co-educational school, subjects "might also be taught by conversations, in the socratic form" (012-050). The Socratic method — learning through dialogue rather than passive reception — is presented as superior to the rote instruction that characterizes both male and female education in Wollstonecraft's time. Conversation, properly conducted, is not mere talk but the active exercise of reason.

Politically, the conversation between the sexes is a microcosm of their larger relation. Wollstonecraft observes with contempt the "arts" that "silly women use to interrupt by FLIRTATION ... a rational conversation, which made the men forget that they were pretty women" (012-087). The capitalization of "FLIRTATION" marks it as a technical term of feminine strategy — the weapon by which women, having been denied intellectual resources, exercise power through sexual display. The tragedy is that this strategy works: it makes men forget women's minds, confirming the very prejudice that produced it.

The vision of rational conversation between the sexes is, for Wollstonecraft, inseparable from her broader argument for women's education. If women are to be companions rather than playthings, they must have "subjects to think of, and matter for conversation, that in some degree would exercise their understandings" (004-097). The content of conversation matters — gardening, experimental philosophy, and literature are her recommended topics — because the quality of conversation determines the quality of the minds engaged in it.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The principal foil for Wollstonecraft's treatment of conversation is Rousseau, whose views on women's speech she quotes at length in order to dismantle. Rousseau's claim that women's "tongues are very voluble" and that they "speak earlier, more readily, and more agreeably than the men" (005-031) is presented as a compliment that is actually an insult — it reduces women's speech to a bodily function, like the activity of their "lips and eyes," rather than an intellectual act.

Rousseau's conclusion — "the principal object of a woman is to please" — is the logical endpoint of the view that women's conversation is naturally different from men's. Wollstonecraft's counter-argument is that this difference is not natural but manufactured. Girls who are "forced to sit still, play with dolls, and listen to foolish conversations" (005-009) will inevitably grow into women whose conversation is foolish — not because their nature is limited, but because their education has limited it.

Against the conduct-book tradition that instructed women to be pleasing conversationalists — agreeable rather than challenging, deferential rather than independent — Wollstonecraft insists that genuine conversation requires intellectual equality. A woman who has been taught only to please cannot engage in the mutual exchange of ideas that is the purpose of rational discourse.

## Key Passages

> A man speaks of what he knows, a woman of what pleases her; the one requires knowledge, the other taste; the principal object of a man is to be useful, the principal object of a woman is to please. — Rousseau, as quoted and critiqued by Wollstonecraft (005-031)

> What arts have I not seen silly women use to interrupt by FLIRTATION, (a very significant word to describe such a manoeuvre) a rational conversation, which made the men forget that they were pretty women. (012-087)

> Gardening, experimental philosophy, and literature, would afford them subjects to think of, and matter for conversation, that in some degree would exercise their understandings. (004-097)

> But, fulfilling the duties of a mother, a woman with a sound constitution, may still keep her person scrupulously neat, and assist to maintain her family, if necessary, or by reading and conversations with both sexes, indiscriminately, improve her mind. (013-067)

> Soldiers acquire a little superficial knowledge, snatched from the muddy current of conversation, and, from continually mixing with society, they gain, what is termed a knowledge of the world. (002-021)
