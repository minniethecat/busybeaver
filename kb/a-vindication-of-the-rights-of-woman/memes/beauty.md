## Definition

Wollstonecraft's treatment of beauty is fundamentally critical. She argues that the overvaluation of female beauty — and the [[education]] system built around cultivating it — is one of the primary mechanisms by which women are degraded. "From their infancy," she writes, "beauty is woman's sceptre, the mind shapes itself to the body, and roaming round its gilt cage, only seeks to adorn its prison" [003-021]. Beauty, in this analysis, is not an [[innocence|innocent]] attribute but a social construct that traps women in a cycle of dependence: they are taught to value their appearance above their [[knowledge|understanding]], and society rewards them for doing so — but only for as long as youth lasts.

Against the dominant notion of physical beauty, Wollstonecraft posits "mental beauty" [P02-007] — the attractiveness of a cultivated mind, strong [[character]], and virtuous conduct. She does not deny the existence or appeal of physical beauty, but she insists that it must be subordinate to moral and intellectual qualities. The ideal, she suggests, is "a whole in which strength and beauty would be [[equality|equal]]ly conspicuous" [002-030].

## In Context

Beauty is a recurring theme throughout the *Vindication*, appearing in nearly every chapter. In the Introduction, Wollstonecraft observes that "strength and usefulness are sacrificed to beauty" [P03-001], framing the problem early. She critiques "libertine notions of beauty" that drive women to pursue marriage as their only means of advancement [P03-012], and notes how "artificial notions of beauty, and false descriptions of [[sensibility]], have been the bane of [[education|female education]]" [003-018].

In Chapter II she draws on [[john-milton|Milton]]'s portrait of Eve — "with perfect beauty adorned" [002-007] — to critique the ideal of female attractiveness that serves male [[passion|desire]]. Chapter III contains her most concentrated treatment: she describes how "to preserve personal beauty, woman's glory! the limbs and faculties are cramped with worse than Chinese bands" [003-013], connecting the pursuit of beauty to literal physical confinement. In Chapter IV she observes the paradox that women, having been taught that "the sovereignty of beauty" [004-011] is their only power, "have, to maintain their power, been obliged to have recourse to [[cunning|low cunning]]."

## Key Passages

> "...strength and usefulness are sacrificed to beauty; and the flaunting leaves, after having pleased a fastidious eye, fade, disregarded on the stalk, long before the season when they ought to have arrived at maturity." [P03-001]

> "...the desire of establishing themselves — the only way women can rise in the world — by marriage. And this desire making mere animals of them, when they marry, they act as such children may be expected to act: they dress; they paint, and nickname God's creatures." [P03-012]

> "To preserve personal beauty, woman's glory! the limbs and faculties are cramped with worse than Chinese bands, and the sedentary life which they are condemned to live, whilst boys frolic in the open air, weakens the muscles and relaxes the nerves." [003-013]

> "Taught from their infancy, that beauty is woman's sceptre, the mind shapes itself to the body, and roaming round its gilt cage, only seeks to adorn its prison." [003-021]

> "...the sovereignty of beauty, they have, to maintain their power, been obliged to have recourse to low cunning." [004-011]

## Related Concepts

- [[virtue]] — mental beauty is the outward expression of genuine [[virtue]], not mere propriety
- [[education]] — women's [[education]] is designed to cultivate beauty rather than [[knowledge|understanding]]
- [[sensibility]] — Wollstonecraft distinguishes the "real beauty of [[sensibility]]" [P03-011] from artificial sentiment
- [[strength]] — Wollstonecraft repeatedly pairs strength with beauty, arguing both should be cultivated
- [[cunning]] — women, taught to value beauty over [[reason]], resort to cunning as their only form of power
- [[marriage]] — beauty is the currency by which women are expected to purchase marriage, their only path to social standing
