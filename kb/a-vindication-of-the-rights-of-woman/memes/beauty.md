# Beauty

Wollstonecraft identifies the cultural obsession with female beauty as one of the principal obstacles to women's moral and intellectual development. In her analysis, beauty is not merely an aesthetic preference but a system of value that shapes women's education, social roles, and self-conception — always to their disadvantage.

## Definition

For Wollstonecraft, beauty in the context of women's lives is far more than a physical attribute; it is a social institution. She refers repeatedly to "the sovereignty of beauty" [004-013] and "beauty's empire" [004-016], casting beauty as a regime under which women are governed and to which they must pay tribute. Within this regime, beauty functions as "woman's sceptre" [003-025] — the one instrument of power permitted to women, and consequently the one quality they are encouraged to cultivate at the expense of all others. Wollstonecraft traces how this narrow valuation distorts women's education: "strength and usefulness are sacrificed to beauty" [P03-001], so that girls are trained from infancy to prioritise appearance over substance. The result is that "when the short lived bloom of beauty is over, I presume that RATIONAL prospects may supply its place" [P03-013] — but for most women, no rational prospects have been cultivated, and the loss of beauty leaves them without purpose or power.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's critique of beauty operates on multiple levels. At the most immediate, she observes that the premium placed on female beauty is a form of "libertine notions of beauty" [P03-012] — that is, a standard derived from male desire rather than from any intrinsic worth. Women are valued for their capacity to please men visually, and this valuation shapes every aspect of their upbringing. "Taught from their infancy that beauty is woman's sceptre, the mind shapes itself to the body, and roaming round its gilt cage, only seeks to adorn its prison" [003-025]. The metaphor is devastating: beauty is not a freedom but a cage, and women's mental energies are consumed by its decoration.

She extends this critique to the economic dimensions of beauty, noting that "the respect paid to wealth and beauty is the most certain and unequivocal" [004-024], linking physical appearance to the broader system of property and status that subordinates women. When a woman's beauty fades — and Wollstonecraft pointedly observes that "at twenty the beauty of both sexes is equal; but the libertinism of man leads him to make an arbitrary distinction" [004-076] — she discovers that "in the noon of beauty's power" she was merely "treated like a child" [004-015], indulged but never respected.

Wollstonecraft also engages with aesthetic philosophy, challenging the notion that beauty is an objective quality pertaining to women. She dismisses the idea that "the quality of strength or beauty ought to be confined exclusively to one sex" [005-006] and attacks the male prejudice "which deems beauty the perfection of woman" [004-074]. Her alternative is a vision of human excellence in which "strength and beauty would be equally conspicuous" [002-016] — beauty understood as the harmonious development of the whole person, not as a gendered commodity.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Eighteenth-century conduct literature routinely advised women to cultivate beauty as their primary asset. Edmund Burke's *Philosophical Enquiry into the Origin of Our Ideas of the Sublime and Beautiful* had codified beauty as a feminine quality characterised by smallness, smoothness, and delicacy — precisely the "epithets of weakness" Wollstonecraft rejects. Rousseau, in *Emile*, presents Sophie as naturally inclined to adorn herself and attentive to male judgment of her appearance. Wollstonecraft challenges this entire tradition by insisting that beauty is a social construction that serves male power, not a natural feminine attribute. Her claim that "artificial notions of beauty, and false descriptions of sensibility, have been confounded with the natural principles of the human character" [003-021] is a direct assault on the philosophical foundations of the beauty cult.

## Key Passages

> "Taught from their infancy that beauty is woman's sceptre, the mind shapes itself to the body, and roaming round its gilt cage, only seeks to adorn its prison." [003-025]

> "Strength and usefulness are sacrificed to beauty; and the flaunting leaves, after having wasted the strength and usefulness that should form the stem, drop to the ground." [P03-001]

> "When the short lived bloom of beauty is over, I presume that RATIONAL prospects may supply its place — but, alas! where are they to be found?" [P03-013]

> "The male prejudice, which deems beauty the perfection of woman — mere beauty of features and complexion, the vulgar in all countries hold in contempt." [004-074]
