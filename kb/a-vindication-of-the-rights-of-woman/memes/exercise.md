# Exercise

In Wollstonecraft's philosophy, exercise is not merely physical activity but the active cultivation of all human faculties — bodily and mental. The "exercise of reason, knowledge and virtue" (001-006) and the "exercise of the understanding" (003-059) are central to her case for women's rational equality and the reform of female education.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft defines exercise broadly as the active and sustained use of innate capacities, arguing that faculties left unexercised atrophy.

She extends this principle from the physical domain — "the constitution, strengthened by exercise" (003-053) — to the intellectual and moral domains.

Women must "exercise their own reason" (002-078) rather than relying on the authority of others. Exercise is the mechanism by which potential becomes actual: "from the exercise of reason, knowledge and virtue naturally flow" (001-006).

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument for exercise operates on three levels. First, at the physical level, she insists on the body-mind connection: women's physical weakness is not natural but the product of confinement. A woman who "strengthens her body and exercises her mind" (002-052) will manage her affairs with far greater competence than one whose faculties have been neglected.

Second, at the intellectual level, Wollstonecraft insists that reason develops only through exercise. She defines education as "such an exercise of the understanding as is best calculated to strengthen the body and form the heart" (002-014). She argues that any being "may become virtuous by the exercise of its own reason" (002-013), making the denial of rational exercise a denial of the capacity for virtue itself.

Third, at the moral level, Wollstonecraft argues that virtue requires exercise: "those talents and virtues, the exercise of which ennobles the human character" (P03-004). The absence of exercise produces the very weakness cited as evidence of female inferiority. Women are "not required to exert" themselves (002-029), and from this non-exertion flows dependence and intellectual timidity. Wollstonecraft's radical conclusion: give women "the exercise of their understanding" (003-059) and they will become what nature intended.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Where Rousseau argued that women's physical weakness justified their subordination, Wollstonecraft inverts the causal chain: women are weak because they are confined, not confined because they are weak. Rather than accepting feminine weakness as natural, society should provide women with the exercise that would make them strong.

## Key Passages

> "those talents and virtues, the exercise of which ennobles the human character" (P03-004)

> "from the exercise of reason, knowledge and virtue naturally flow" (001-006)

> "may become virtuous by the exercise of its own reason; for if but one being was created with vicious inclinations, that is positively a mere creature of chance" (002-013)

> "the constitution, strengthened by exercise, has allowed her body to acquire vigour" (003-053)

> "To become respectable, the exercise of their understanding is necessary" (003-059)
