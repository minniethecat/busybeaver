# Jean-Jacques Rousseau

## Overview

Jean-Jacques Rousseau (1712–1778) is the central intellectual figure against whom Wollstonecraft directs the *Vindication of the Rights of Woman*. Though he had died fourteen years before its publication, his influence on educational theory — particularly through *Émile* (1762) — made him the unavoidable interlocutor for any discussion of women's education. Wollstonecraft's relationship to Rousseau is complex: she admires his critique of civilisation and his commitment to reason as the foundation of virtue, but she excoriates his account of female nature and his prescription for Sophie's education in Book V of *Émile*.

Wollstonecraft's strategy is characteristically sharp: she accepts Rousseau's own principles — that virtue must flow from the exercise of reason, that dependence corrupts character — and then demonstrates that his treatment of women violates those very principles. Rousseau, she argues, would deny women the rational education that his own philosophy declares essential to virtue. Her method is immanent critique at its most devastating: she steps inside Rousseau's system, takes his premises seriously, and shows that they lead logically to conclusions he refused to draw.

### Rousseau's Intellectual Stature

Wollstonecraft never dismisses Rousseau lightly. She acknowledges his genius even as she dismantles his arguments — a rhetorical posture that lends her critique greater force. Early in the *Vindication*, she writes of being "fatigued with jostling against artificial fools" and notes that Rousseau "became enamoured of solitude" in response to the same corrupt civilisation she herself despises (001-013). She describes him as labouring "with uncommon eloquence to prove that man was naturally a solitary animal" — a characterisation that conveys respect for his intellectual seriousness even as she disagrees with his conclusions.

The paradox of Rousseau's influence on Wollstonecraft is that she found his diagnosis of civilisation compelling while rejecting his prescriptions for women. She understood that to defeat Rousseau she could not simply denounce him; she had to out-argue him on his own terrain. This is why she repeatedly invokes him by name across ten different chapters — more than any other author in the *Vindication*.

### The Sophie Problem

The specific target of Wollstonecraft's critique is Sophie, the fictional female counterpart to Rousseau's ideal pupil Émile. In Book V of *Émile*, Rousseau constructs an elaborate educational program for Sophie designed entirely around her future role as Émile's companion. Sophie is to be educated not as an autonomous rational being but as a creature of sensibility, modesty, and pleasing subordination — taught to cultivate "cunning" rather than reason, to govern herself through fear of male disapproval, and to accept dependence as her natural condition.

Wollstonecraft finds this project intellectually dishonest and morally dangerous. If Rousseau truly believed that nature had designed women for subordination, why the elaborate artifice of Sophie's education? The very fact that Sophie must be *taught* to be subordinate betrays the constructed character of female dependence. This is the logical trap Wollstonecraft springs repeatedly throughout the *Vindication*.

## In the Text

Rousseau appears throughout the *Vindication*, but the most concentrated engagement occurs in Chapters I, II, and V. Wollstonecraft quotes his description of woman as being "made specially to please man" and subjects it to devastating logical analysis: if women are naturally inferior, why does Rousseau need to construct an elaborate educational system to keep them that way?

### Chapter I: The State of Nature

In Chapter I, Rousseau figures in Wollstonecraft's critique of the "state of nature." She finds his nostalgic primitivism plausible but unsound. She diagnoses his error with precision: "Rousseau exerts himself to prove, that all *was* right originally: a crowd of authors that all *is* now right: and I, that all *will be* right" (001-017). This tripartite schema positions Wollstonecraft as the progressive corrective to both Rousseau's regressive optimism and the complacent conservatism of her contemporaries.

She goes further in diagnosing the roots of his error. Rousseau, she argues, "celebrates barbarism" and "exalts those to demigods, who were scarcely human—the brutal Spartans" (001-018). His romanticisation of primitive virtue blinds him to the possibility that reason and civilisation, properly directed, might genuinely improve the human condition. Wollstonecraft's own position is more nuanced: civilisation has indeed produced corruption, but the answer is not retreat but reform — a position she crystallises in the metaphor of the "pestiferous purple" of wealth that "renders the progress of civilization a curse" unless checked by rational education (001-031).

### Chapter II: The Educational Treatise

The heart of Wollstonecraft's engagement with Rousseau lies in Chapter II, where she confronts his educational theory directly and extends his own logic to its unstated conclusion. Her most celebrated move is to take Rousseau's definition of virtue — as the exercise of one's own reason — and apply it without the gender restriction Rousseau assumed:

> "Consequently, the most perfect education, in my opinion, is such an exercise of the understanding as is best calculated to strengthen the body and form the heart; or, in other words, to enable the individual to attain such habits of virtue as will render it independent. In fact, it is a farce to call any being virtuous whose virtues do not result from the exercise of its own reason. This was Rousseau's opinion respecting men: I extend it to women." (002-028)

This passage is arguably the thesis statement of the entire *Vindication*. Wollstonecraft accepts Rousseau's principle entirely — and then demonstrates that his own reasoning compels its extension to women. She then broadens her indictment to encompass the entire tradition Rousseau inaugurated: "all the writers who have written on the subject of female education and manners, from Rousseau to Dr. Gregory, have contributed to render women more artificial, weaker characters, than they would otherwise have been" (002-029).

Wollstonecraft also deploys a pointed empirical challenge to Rousseau's a priori claims about female nature. She writes with the quiet authority of direct observation: "I have, probably, had an opportunity of observing more girls in their infancy than J. J. Rousseau" (003-017). This is a remarkably modern argumentative gesture — she weighs Rousseau's philosophical speculation against her own lived experience and finds the former wanting. She follows this by insisting that a girl given freedom to play and move, rather than confined to dolls and dressing, will "always be a romp" (003-017) — exactly contrary to Rousseau's assertion of innate female domesticity.

### Chapter V: The Systematic Dismantling

Chapter V contains Wollstonecraft's most sustained and systematic engagement with Rousseau. She opens the chapter by announcing her focus: "I shall begin with Rousseau, and give a sketch of the character of women in his own words, interspersing comments and reflections" (005-004). This is not casual criticism; it is a deliberate, structured rebuttal.

She quotes Rousseau's own description of Sophie — that she should be "as perfect a woman as Emilius is a man" (005-005) — and then demonstrates that the "perfection" Rousseau prescribes is a carefully engineered debility. She exposes the core contradiction: Rousseau demands that women be virtuous and constant, yet simultaneously denies them the rational foundation upon which alone virtue can be built. As she puts it with devastating concision:

> "But, according to the tenor of reasoning by which women are kept from the tree of knowledge, the important years of youth, the usefulness of age, and the rational hopes of futurity, are all to be sacrificed, to render woman an object of desire for a short time. Besides, how could Rousseau expect them to be virtuous and constant when reason is neither allowed to be the foundation of their virtue, nor truth the object of their inquiries?" (005-055)

Wollstonecraft also offers a psychological diagnosis of Rousseau's errors that is at once generous and damning. She traces his mistakes not to malice but to sensibility — a quality that, in her analysis, is itself feminised and thus ironically responsible for his distorted view of women:

> "But all Rousseau's errors in reasoning arose from sensibility, and sensibility to their charms women are very ready to forgive! When he should have reasoned he became impassioned, and reflection inflamed his imagination, instead of enlightening his understanding." (005-056)

This passage performs double work: it explains Rousseau's error while simultaneously demonstrating that the very "sensibility" he prescribed for Sophie is what led him astray. The critique is structural, not merely personal — Rousseau's philosophy of gender is corrupted by the same irrational forces he claims are women's natural domain.

### Later Chapters: Recurring Presence

Rousseau's shadow extends well beyond the early chapters. In Chapter VII, Wollstonecraft returns to the question of modesty, noting that "in the same strain runs Rousseau's and Dr. Gregory's advice respecting modesty, strangely miscalled!" (007-018). The exclamation mark is significant — it signals Wollstonecraft's exasperation at how these male authors have redefined modesty from a genuine virtue into a performance of sexual reticence designed for male consumption.

In Chapter VIII, she critiques Rousseau's elevation of reputation over intrinsic character: "Rousseau declares, 'that reputation is no less indispensable'" (008-010). For Wollstonecraft, this is perverse: making women's moral standing dependent on social opinion rather than their own reason is exactly the kind of "genteel slavery" she is fighting against.

Chapter IX extends the critique to the political domain. Rousseau "and a numerous list of male writers, insist that she should all her life, be subjected to a severe restraint, that of propriety" (009-016). And in Chapter XII, Wollstonecraft offers her most personal reading of Rousseau's motives, suggesting that his degradation of women was an effort to justify his own unequal relationship with Thérèse Levasseur: "Whoever drew a more exalted female character than Rousseau? though in the lump he constantly endeavoured to degrade the sex. And why was he thus anxious? Truly to justify to himself the affection which weakness and virtue had made him cherish for that fool Theresa" (012-071). This biographical analysis reveals Wollstonecraft's sophistication as a critic: she understands that philosophical systems are not purely rational constructs but bear the imprint of their authors' lives and needs.

## Biographical Summary

Jean-Jacques Rousseau (1712–1778) was a Genevan philosopher, writer, and composer whose political philosophy influenced the Enlightenment and the French Revolution. His major works include *The Social Contract* (1762), *Émile* (1762), and the autobiographical *Confessions* (1782). His paradoxes — civilisation corrupts, yet perfectibility is possible — shaped Wollstonecraft's own thinking even as she rejected his conclusions about women. His long-term relationship with Thérèse Levasseur, an uneducated laundress, figures in Wollstonecraft's psychological analysis of his motives: she suggests his degradation of women in theory was in part an attempt to rationalise his own unequal domestic arrangement.
