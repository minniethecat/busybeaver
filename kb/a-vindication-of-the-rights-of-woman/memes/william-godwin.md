# William Godwin

[[William Godwin]] (1756–1836) was the most consequential radical philosopher in England during the 1790s and, through his marriage to [[Mary Wollstonecraft]], the intimate witness to the final year of her life. His _Memoirs of the Author of A Vindication of the Rights of Woman_ (1798) — the Biographical Sketch from which we know the details of Wollstonecraft's private history — is at once an act of love and a document of enduring controversy.

## Life

Godwin was born at Wisbech, Cambridgeshire in 1756, the son of a dissenting minister. Trained for the ministry himself, he lost his faith and turned to political writing. His _Enquiry Concerning Political Justice_ (1793), published in the febrile atmosphere following the [[french-revolution|French Revolution]], argued for the gradual abolition of government, property, and marriage — positions so radical that even in a revolutionary decade Godwin stood at the outermost edge of English political thought. His novel _Caleb Williams_ (1794) dramatised his philosophical convictions in fiction.

## Role in the Text

Godwin's presence in the _Vindication_ is posthumous and editorial. He wrote the Biographical Sketch that was appended to the 1798 posthumous edition of the work, providing readers with an account of Wollstonecraft's life that framed the philosophical argument with biographical fact. The Sketch records their meeting with characteristic restraint:

> "In 1796 she revived or improved an acquaintance which commenced years before with Wm. Godwin, author of 'Political Justice,' and other works of great notoriety." (P01-017)

Their first encounter, years earlier, had not impressed either party. But when they met again in 1796, "circumstances … permitted a mutual and just appreciation of character" (P01-017). Their intimacy, the Sketch records, grew "by regular and almost imperceptible degrees." The Biographical Sketch's description of their love — "In the most refined style of love. It grew with equal advances in the mind of each" (P01-018) — articulates the ideal of rational companionship that the _Vindication_ itself advocates: a union in which "one sex did not take the priority" and neither party "could assume to have been the agent or the patient."

Their marriage, though brief — Wollstonecraft died of puerperal fever eleven days after giving birth to their daughter, the future [[mary-shelley|Mary Shelley]], in September 1797 — was by Godwin's account the fulfilment of the companionate ideal the _Vindication_ theorised.

## Key Ideas

Godwin's _Political Justice_ and Wollstonecraft's _Vindication_ share a common philosophical inheritance: the Enlightenment conviction that reason, properly cultivated, can reform the institutions — marriage, property, government — that perpetuate injustice. Where Wollstonecraft concentrated on the condition of women, Godwin generalised the critique to all hierarchical institutions. Their intellectual convergence in the final year of Wollstonecraft's life represents one of the most remarkable partnerships in the history of political thought.
