# Passion

In _[[A Vindication of the Rights of Woman]]_, **passion** occupies an ambivalent position. It is both a natural force implanted by God for human improvement and, when improperly cultivated, a destructive power that overthrows [[Reason]] and corrupts [[Virtue]].

[[Mary Wollstonecraft]] does not reject passion outright — she insists that "the passions should unfold our reason" (001-016). But she argues that the education of women systematically inflames passion while neglecting the understanding that should govern it.

The term appears in 30 passages across every chapter. Wollstonecraft distinguishes noble passions (ambition, desire for excellence) from destructive ones (vanity, romantic love, craving admiration) and traces the latter to faulty education.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft treats passion as a morally neutral natural endowment — energy that can be channeled toward virtue or vice depending on discipline:

> "For what purpose were the passions implanted? That man by struggling with them might attain a degree of knowledge denied to the brutes." (001-005)

Passion is the raw material of moral development. The struggle with passion — not its elimination — produces knowledge and virtue. This is a developmental view: passion is the engine of growth when governed, and the cause of degradation when unregulated.

Wollstonecraft distinguishes the passion that drives moral ambition from "the romantic passion, which is the concomitant of genius" (002-061). The former elevates; the latter degrades when indulged without discipline.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

### Passion and Reason in Balance

Wollstonecraft argues that passion exists in service to reason, not in opposition. Passions provide motive force; reason provides direction:

> "It is not against strong, persevering passions; but romantic, wavering feelings that I wish to guard the female heart." (002-040)

She does not advocate eliminating passion — neither possible nor desirable. She objects to the "romantic" and "wavering" passions that conduct-book culture encourages.

> "The exercise of the understanding, as life advances, is the only method pointed out by nature to calm the passions." (004-036)

### The Gendering of Passion

Wollstonecraft identifies a double standard. Men's passions are disciplined through education and public life. Women's passions are inflamed by a system that denies them other outlets:

> "The passions of men have thus placed women on thrones." (004-016)

Women are taught to cultivate passions that please men (beauty, sensibility, coquetry) while passions that would make them independent (ambition, intellectual curiosity) are suppressed.

### Artificial Passions and Vanity

Wollstonecraft identifies "artificial passions" — emotions produced by social conditioning rather than nature:

> "Noisy pleasures, and artificial passions, till vanity takes place of every social affection." (003-061)

These are more dangerous than natural passions because they are insatiable. A woman taught to value admiration will never have enough. Artificial passions are the direct product of education that cultivates feeling at the expense of thought.

### Passion, Love, and Marriage

Wollstonecraft argues that romantic passion is a poor foundation for [[Marriage]] because it is impermanent:

> "Love, the common passion, in which chance and sensation take place of choice and reason." (002-056)

She insists married couples "ought not to continue to love each other with passion" (002-058). Passionate love should subside into the calmer affection of friendship. A marriage built on passion alone is doomed to disappointment.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

Passion is the twin and antagonist of [[Reason]]: the two must operate in partnership. When passion overthrows reason, the result is [[Vanity]], [[Sensibility]] without judgment, and conduct governed by impulse.

The management of passion is central to Wollstonecraft's theory of [[Virtue]]. A virtuous person is not without passion but has passions properly ordered. [[Education]] is the mechanism of this ordering — or, in the case of women, its failure.

Passion relates to [[Marriage]]: Wollstonecraft's critique is grounded in how passion, inflamed by courtship culture, leads to unhappy unions. Reform depends on substituting rational [[Affection]] and [[Friendship]] for transient romantic passion.
