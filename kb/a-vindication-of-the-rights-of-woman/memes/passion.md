# Passion

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's treatment of **passion** reflects a sophisticated engagement with Enlightenment moral psychology. She does not reject the passions outright — "For what purpose were the passions implanted?" she asks, answering that it is "that man by struggling with them might attain a degree of [[knowledge]] denied to the brutes" (001-005). The passions are divinely implanted and serve a developmental function: they are the raw material that [[reason]] must work upon.

> "For what purpose were the passions implanted? That man by struggling with them might attain a degree of knowledge denied to the brutes: whispers Experience." (001-005)

This is not the language of repression but of Bildung — the passions are a necessary stage in the unfolding of human capacities. Without passions to struggle against, there would be no occasion for the exercise of [[reason]], and without that exercise, human beings would remain at the level of brutes. The passions, paradoxically, are the engine of our elevation above mere animal existence.

> "It may be so, that the passions should unfold our reason, because he could see that present evil might produce future good." (001-015)

The theodicy is explicit: the passions are part of a providential design. Present evil (the turbulence and disorder of unregulated passion) produces future good (the strengthening of [[reason]] through the struggle to govern it). Wollstonecraft deploys this theodicy not to justify suffering but to establish the developmental framework within which the regulation of the passions — and therefore the [[education]] of women — must be understood.

### The Education of the Passions

But the passions must be regulated — and the [[education]] women receive systematically fails to provide that regulation. Instead of [[education|learning]] to govern their passions through [[reason]], women are encouraged to cultivate them as the source of their social power. Wollstonecraft describes a properly ordered education as one that will "slowly sharpen the senses, form the temper, regulate the passions, as they begin to ferment, and set the understanding to work before the body arrives at maturity" (002-026). The sequence is deliberate: regulation of the passions must begin early, before the body reaches maturity and before the passions acquire their full force.

> "By individual education, I mean — for the sense of the word is not precisely defined — such an attention to a child as will slowly sharpen the senses, form the temper, regulate the passions, as they begin to ferment, and set the understanding to work before the body arrives at maturity; so that the man may only have to proceed, not to begin, the important task of learning to think and reason." (002-026)

The contrast with the education women actually receive could not be sharper. Instead of having their passions regulated as they "begin to ferment," women's passions are left entirely undisciplined — or worse, actively cultivated. The result is what Wollstonecraft describes elsewhere as "strong passions" operating with "very vigorous faculties" but without discipline (002-036), producing caprice, instability, and moral weakness. The problem is not that women have passions — everyone does — but that their passions are never "brought to the test of reason" (002-032).

### Passion, Love, and the Marriage Plot

The climactic argument comes in the discussion of [[love]] and marriage. Wollstonecraft famously argues that love "from its very [[nature]], must be transitory" (002-062) and that building women's entire social existence on the expectation of romantic passion is both philosophically incoherent and practically disastrous. When passion fades — as it must — the woman whose [[character]] was formed around being [[love]]d finds herself without purpose or resources.

> "Love, from its very nature, must be transitory. To seek for a secret that would render it constant, would be as wild a search as for the philosopher's stone, or the grand panacea; and the discovery would be equally useless, or rather pernicious to mankind." (002-062)

Wollstonecraft's insight here is that the transience of passionate love is not a bug but a feature of the moral order. The passions are "spurs to action" that "open the mind" (002-065); they drive the initial movement toward another person. But they cannot sustain a relationship over time, because their intensity depends on novelty, uncertainty, and distance — all of which marriage extinguishes. The "fever of love" must subside, and when it does, what remains must be something other than passion: [[friendship]], esteem, shared purpose.

The practical implication follows: "in order to fulfil the duties of life, and to be able to pursue with vigour the various employments which form the moral character, a master and mistress of a family ought not to continue to love each other with passion" (002-066). This is not a recommendation of coldness but of proportionality. Passion that "engross[es] the thoughts that should be otherwise employed" (002-066) is not a sign of depth but of disorder — it diverts mental energy from the "various employments which form the moral character."

## In Context

Chapter I presents the philosophical framework: "the passions should unfold our [[reason]]" (001-015) — a developmental account in which [[love|passion]] is the motive force that drives the exercise of [[reason]], not its enemy. Chapter II critiques the social arrangements that leave women's passions unregulated while men's are disciplined through [[education]] and public life.

### Noble Passions and the Possibility of Reform

Wollstonecraft does not, however, advocate for the extinguishing of the passions. Her vision is not Stoic but developmental. She distinguishes between the narrow, selfish passions that a corrupt [[education]] cultivates — vanity, coquetry, the "desire of being always women" — and what she calls "nobler passions and motives" that a reformed education would foster:

> "Give their activity of mind a wider range, and nobler passions and motives will govern their appetites and sentiments." (005-019)

The phrase "nobler passions" is significant. Wollstonecraft does not oppose passion to [[reason]] in a simple binary; she opposes degraded passions (cultivated by a [[education]] aimed at pleasing) to nobler passions (cultivated by an education aimed at [[virtue]] and [[knowledge]]). The solution to the problem of passion is not its elimination but its redirection — turning the energy of the passions toward objects worthy of rational beings.

This redirection requires structural change. Women's passions are degraded not because women are naturally frivolous but because their "activity of mind" is confined to a "narrow range." Expand that range — through education, through the cultivation of [[reason]], through the opening of socially valued occupations to women — and the passions themselves will be transformed. The same energy that was wasted on coquetry and romantic fantasy will be available for the pursuit of [[knowledge]], the exercise of [[virtue]], and the enjoyment of [[friendship]].

> "But, leaving superior minds to correct themselves, and pay dearly for their experience, it is necessary to observe, that it is not against strong, persevering passions; but romantic, wavering feelings, that I wish to guard the female heart by exercising the understanding." (004-089)

Here Wollstonecraft draws a crucial distinction. She is not opposed to "strong, persevering passions" — passions that are stable, directed, and integrated with [[reason]]. What she opposes are "romantic, wavering feelings" — the sentimental effusions of an idle imagination that has never been disciplined by the understanding. The remedy for these wavering feelings is not suppression but "exercising the understanding" — giving the mind something substantial to work on so that it does not feed on fantasies.
