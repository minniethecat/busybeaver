The concept of **inclination** occupies a strategic position in Wollstonecraft's argument about human nature and education. Against the theological doctrine that human beings are born with "vicious inclinations" (002-013) — inherently corrupt dispositions that must be suppressed — Wollstonecraft insists that "all our natural inclinations are right and good in themselves" (005-023). The moral quality of an inclination is determined not by its origin but by its cultivation: inclinations that are directed by reason and education become the foundation of virtue; inclinations that are neglected or indulged without discipline become the source of vice. The distinction is crucial to Wollstonecraft's feminist argument, for it is women's inclinations that are systematically misdirected by a defective education.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's treatment of inclination is shaped by the eighteenth-century debate about human nature. The orthodox Christian view, articulated most influentially by the doctrine of original sin, held that human beings are born with corrupt inclinations that must be disciplined into virtue. The Enlightenment counter-tradition — from Locke's *Essay Concerning Human Understanding* to Rousseau's *Discourse on Inequality* — had variously challenged this pessimism, arguing that the mind at birth is a blank slate or that natural man is good until corrupted by society.

Wollstonecraft's position is a distinctive synthesis. She accepts the Enlightenment premise that natural inclinations are not inherently corrupt — "I am persuaded, all our natural inclinations are right and good in themselves" (005-023) — but she insists that this does not mean they can be left to themselves. Inclinations need direction: the task of education is not to suppress or indulge them but to "direct" them toward their proper objects. A woman who is taught only to follow her inclinations — toward pleasure, toward admiration, toward the gratification of vanity — is not following nature but a corrupted version of it, for her inclinations have been shaped by an artificial social environment that rewards frivolity and punishes seriousness.

The key vocabulary distinction is between "inclination" and "duty." Wollstonecraft repeatedly contrasts the two: women are educated to be governed by inclination — by what they happen to want or feel — rather than by duty — by what reason and moral principle demand. "They never think of consulting their inclination, or providing for the comfort of the weak" (011-011) is said of the negligent wealthy, but the larger point is that duty must govern inclination, not the reverse. A life governed by unregulated inclination is, in Wollstonecraft's account, a life of "listless idleness" — not morally neutral but positively corrupt.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

The argument about inclination unfolds across several of the *Vindication*'s central themes. The most important is the question of natural difference between the sexes. Rousseau had declared in *Émile* that "their tastes and inclinations should be different" (005-005) — that women's inclinations are naturally directed toward the domestic and the ornamental, just as men's are naturally directed toward the public and the rational. Wollstonecraft's response is that this supposed difference of inclination is not natural but produced — the effect of an education that systematically cultivates certain dispositions in women and suppresses others.

"If woman was created with vicious inclinations — that is, positively bad — what can save her?" (002-013) The question is rhetorical, but its logic is revealing. If women's inclinations were naturally vicious, then no education could remedy them, and the entire project of the *Vindication* would be futile. But if women's inclinations are naturally good — or at least morally neutral — then the right education can direct them toward virtue. Wollstonecraft's feminism depends on this premise: the problem is not women's nature but their nurture, and the solution is not the suppression of their inclinations but their rational cultivation.

The social mechanism by which inclinations are corrupted is described with precision. "He acts, upon the most indifferent occasions, as if he were disposed to favour all his inclinations" (004-027) — this is Wollstonecraft's description of the man who has never learned to govern his desires, who treats every passing impulse as a legitimate claim on his conduct. The point is not gendered — men are susceptible to the same corruption — but its consequences are, for women are judged more harshly for the same failures of self-governance and given fewer resources for their correction.

The ambition of Wollstonecraft's educational programme is to "so to adjust instruction as not to narrow the understanding, whilst the heart is warmed" — to cultivate the inclinations without allowing them to dominate the understanding. A "strong inclination for external ornaments" (013-044) is not in itself corrupt, but if it is indulged to the exclusion of more serious pursuits, it becomes so. The same inclination, directed by reason toward the cultivation of taste rather than the pursuit of display, might contribute to genuine refinement.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Rousseau's influence on the eighteenth-century understanding of inclination can hardly be overstated, and Wollstonecraft's engagement with him is correspondingly thorough. In *Émile*, Rousseau had argued that women's inclinations are naturally different from men's — that nature itself has fitted women for the domestic sphere and men for the public. The argument is convenient: it naturalises a social arrangement that serves male interests, and it does so in the language of Enlightenment naturalism.

Wollstonecraft's response is to turn the naturalistic argument against itself. If inclinations are natural, and if nature is good, then the inclinations that women actually display — toward vanity, toward frivolity, toward sensual gratification — must be either (a) natural and therefore good, or (b) artificial and therefore correctable. Wollstonecraft chooses (b). The inclinations that Rousseau presents as natural to women are in fact "the effect of an artificial education" — dispositions that have been systematically cultivated in women for the convenience of men. A different education would produce different inclinations, and those inclinations would be no less natural for being cultivated.

The conduct-book tradition — Fordyce, Gregory — had treated inclination as something to be managed rather than directed. Women were advised to conceal their inclinations, to present an appearance of modesty and compliance, while pursuing their real desires through indirection and manipulation. Wollstonecraft's contempt for this advice — "the insinuating nothings of politeness" — is grounded in her conviction that inclination must be governed by reason from within, not constrained by convention from without. A woman who has been taught only to hide her inclinations has not been taught to govern them; she has merely been taught to dissemble.

## Key Passages

> Let us examine this question. Rousseau would persuade us, that "as women have a more delicate frame than men, their tastes and inclinations should be different." (005-005)

> If woman was created with vicious inclinations — that is, positively bad — what can save her? (002-013)

> I am persuaded, all our natural inclinations are right and good in themselves; and that evil, under every shape, arises from a perversion of them. (005-023)

> A strong inclination for external ornaments ever appears to me to indicate a weak mind, because the person who can be pleased with such trifling objects must have little comprehension of the dignity of human nature. (013-044)

> So far is the first inclination carried, that even the hellish yoke of slavery has been found to be lighter under the same circumstance. (013-046)
