# Novels

A form of prose fiction that Wollstonecraft singles out for criticism in the *Vindication*, arguing that the consumption of novels cultivates sensibility at the expense of reason and distorts women's understanding of the world.

## Definition

For Wollstonecraft, "novels" represent a degraded mode of reading that she contrasts with serious literature, history, and philosophy. She is concerned not with novels as a literary form per se but with the effects of novel-reading on the female mind: novels, she argues, feed the imagination with romantic fantasies, cultivate excessive sensibility, and substitute artificial sentiment for genuine moral understanding. Her critique targets both the content of novels—their preoccupation with love and sentimental adventure—and the passive mode of reading they encourage.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's critique of novels is embedded in her broader argument about women's education. She traces a decline in reading habits: taste "has slided from essays into novels, and from novels into familiar letters" ([P03-010]), suggesting a progressive degradation of intellectual culture that especially harms women, who are steered toward the least demanding forms of reading.

The danger of novels, in Wollstonecraft's analysis, is that they substitute artificial emotion for genuine moral development. She notes that "in sermons or novels, however, voluptuousness is a common vehicle" ([005-068]), implying that even texts ostensibly concerned with morality may corrupt through their appeal to sensation. The sentimental novel, with its emphasis on romantic love and heightened feeling, teaches women to value emotion over reason and to measure their lives against fictional ideals that bear no relation to reality.

Wollstonecraft's most sustained discussion of novels appears in Chapter 13, where she acknowledges that she does not condemn all fiction: "when I exclaim against novels, I mean when contrasted with those works which exercise the understanding and regulate the imagination" ([013-030]). Her objection, then, is not to imaginative literature as such but to the displacement of serious reading by frivolous fiction. She observes that some women "of superior talent" have progressed "from reading novels" to more serious literary production ([013-038]), but warns that for most women, excessive novel-reading reinforces the very weaknesses that education should correct.

She also addresses the claim that novel-reading cultivates sensibility, arguing that "the reading of novels makes women, and particularly ladies of fashion, very fond of using strong expressions and superlatives" ([013-040])—a habit that coarsens rather than refines the mind. Her recommended antidote is not censorship but redirection: "to correct a fondness for novels is to ridicule them" ([013-039]), exposing their absurdities and channelling women's desire for reading into more substantial intellectual pursuits.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft's critique of novels participates in a broader eighteenth-century debate about the effects of fiction on readers, particularly female readers. Her position echoes elements of the conservative critique of novel-reading but diverges sharply in its remedy: where conservatives would restrict women's reading altogether, Wollstonecraft argues for replacing novels with better books—history, philosophy, and moral essays—that would cultivate reason rather than sensibility. Her position is thus fundamentally optimistic about women's intellectual capacities, even as it is critical of the reading material to which they are directed.
