Wollstonecraft's treatment of the **private** sphere is notably dialectical: she neither rejects private life nor endorses it as sufficient for moral development. Instead, she argues that "private virtue" (P02-018) is the necessary foundation of public good, but that a purely private education — isolated from the challenges of social life — cannot produce the robust character that genuine virtue requires. Her analysis of the private thus reveals the interdependence of individual cultivation and social reform.

## Definition

For Wollstonecraft, the private encompasses several related domains: private education (as opposed to public schooling), private life (as opposed to public engagement), and private virtue (as opposed to merely public conformity). She is particularly concerned with "private education" (002-013), which she initially favours but increasingly subjects to critical scrutiny throughout the *Vindication*.

Wollstonecraft defines private education not merely as education conducted at home but as an approach that isolates the individual from the corrective influence of diverse social experience. She warns that "if a private education produce self-importance" (012-025) — as it tends to do — it fails in its fundamental purpose. The private, in her analysis, must be complemented by the public; neither sphere alone can produce the fully developed rational being.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument about the private sphere evolves across the *Vindication*. Early in the text, she appears to favour private education, arguing that "I do not believe that a private education can work the wonders" (002-013) that some claim — a qualified endorsement that already signals her reservations. By Chapter XII, her position has sharpened: she now advocates "combining a public and private education" (012-008), recognising that exclusive reliance on either produces characteristic deformations of character.

The critique of purely private education centres on its tendency to produce narrow, self-regarding individuals. Wollstonecraft observes that private education "produce[s] self-importance" (012-025) and fails to prepare individuals for the demands of social life. Children educated exclusively at home never learn to measure themselves against equals, never experience the rough-and-tumble of intellectual debate, and never develop the "public spirit" that alone can sustain genuine virtue.

Yet Wollstonecraft does not simply valorise the public over the private. She insists that "private virtue [is] the cement of public happiness" (009-015), affirming the foundational role of individual moral cultivation. Her ideal is not the abolition of the private but its integration with the public — a vision in which "private virtue [becomes] a public benefit" (009-033) through the rational participation of virtuous individuals in shared social life.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft's evolving position on private education distinguishes her from both Rousseau, who idealised the private tutoring of Emile, and from advocates of convent or boarding-school education, whom she criticises for their own forms of corruption. Her insistence on combining public and private education — allowing the child to benefit from both familial affection and the bracing challenges of peer society — represents a distinctive synthesis that anticipates later progressive educational thought. Where conventional opinion treated private virtue and public life as separate domains, Wollstonecraft insisted on their necessary integration.
