# Reason

The central Enlightenment faculty that Wollstonecraft argues is common to both sexes and must be cultivated through education to achieve genuine virtue.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft defines reason as "the dignified distinction" that elevates humans above animals, an "improvable" faculty that distinguishes the human species (P03-002). It is, she contends, the foundation upon which all moral reasoning is built — "axioms on which reasoning is built" must be treated as unequivocal (001-001). Reason is not a fixed inheritance but a capacity that develops through proper education and liberty (P02-007).

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft argues that women possess the same rational faculties as men but have been systematically denied the education necessary to develop them. She laments that "deep-rooted prejudices have clouded reason" (001-007) and that men "employ their reason to justify prejudices" rather than to examine them critically (001-008). The denial of reason to women is, in her view, a self-serving ideological project — "they are all eager to crush reason; yet always assert that they have the gift of reason" (P02-013).

Crucially, reason is not merely an intellectual capacity but the prerequisite for virtue: "the degree of reason, virtue, and knowledge" distinguishes individuals and nations (001-006). Without freedom to exercise reason, women cannot develop genuine moral character. "Unless freedom strengthen her reason till she comprehend her duty," she writes, women remain in a state of perpetual childhood (P02-007).

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft directly challenges Rousseau's depiction of women as beings of sentiment rather than reason. She rejects the notion that women's "passions should unfold our reason" (001-016), insisting instead that reason must govern passion. Against the "shallow reasoners" who claim that women's rational education is dangerous, she argues that "a set of shallow reasoners are always exclaiming" against women's intellectual development without evidence (001-009).

## Key Passages

> "the degree of reason, virtue, and knowledge, that distinguishes the individual, and guides the laws" (001-006)

> "Unless freedom strengthen her reason till she comprehend her duty" (P02-007)

> "to steal the celestial fire of reason; and the vengeance of heaven" (001-013)

> "when improvable reason is allowed to be the dignified distinction which raises men above the brute creation" (P03-002)
