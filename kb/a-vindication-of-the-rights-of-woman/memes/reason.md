# Reason

## Definition

For Wollstonecraft, **reason** is the defining human faculty — the capacity through which individuals attain [[virtue]], [[knowledge]], and moral independence. She opens Chapter I with an unequivocal declaration that ranks reason above all other human attributes:

> "In what does man's preeminence over the brute creation consist? The answer is as clear as that a half is less than the whole; in Reason." (001-003)

She consistently links reason with human perfection and social progress, arguing that it cannot be the exclusive preserve of one sex. To deny women the cultivation of reason is to deny their humanity.

> "Who made man the exclusive judge, if woman partake with him the gift of reason?" (P02-009)

Reason is not merely intellectual but inherently moral in Wollstonecraft's framework: it is through the exercise of reason that one arrives at genuine [[virtue]], as opposed to the "spurious qualities" that society has falsely labelled virtuous. The degradation of reason — through [[prejudice]], through [[education]] that trains women in superficial accomplishments rather than [[knowledge|understanding]], through institutions that reward flattery rather than thought — is for her the root cause of women's subjugation.

### Reason and the Distinguishing Human Faculty

Wollstonecraft grounds her entire argument on the premise that reason is what separates humanity from brute creation. The memorable opening of Chapter I — "In what does man's preeminence over the brute creation consist? ... in Reason" (001-003) — sets this foundation squarely. Reason, in her view, is not one human capacity among many but the capacity through which all other excellences are realised. Without it, human beings are no different from animals, subject to instinct and appetite rather than to principle.

This premise has immediate political consequences. If reason is the defining human trait, then any social arrangement that denies a group the cultivation of reason is dehumanising. Wollstonecraft draws out this implication in addressing [[charles-maurice-de-talleyrand-perigord|Talleyrand]]:

> "But, if women are to be excluded, without having a voice, from a participation of the natural rights of mankind, prove first, to ward off the charge of injustice and inconsistency, that they want reason, else this flaw in your *new constitution*, the first constitution founded on reason, will ever show that man must, in some shape, act like a tyrant, and tyranny, in whatever part of society it rears its brazen front, will ever undermine morality." (P02-011)

The logic is a direct challenge: either women possess reason, in which case they are entitled to the rights of rational beings; or they do not, in which case the burden of proof lies squarely on those who would deny them those rights. Wollstonecraft anticipates no serious attempt to discharge that burden, because the evidence of women's rational capacity is, for her, as plain as "that a half is less than the whole" (001-003).

### Reason versus Rousseau

[[rousseau|Jean-Jacques Rousseau]] is the central intellectual antagonist of the Vindication, and Wollstonecraft's engagement with him turns almost entirely on the question of reason. Rousseau had argued that virtue must result from the exercise of one's own reason — but only for men. Wollstonecraft takes his principle and extends it to women, exposing a fundamental inconsistency:

> "In fact, it is a farce to call any being virtuous whose virtues do not result from the exercise of its own reason. This was Rousseau's opinion respecting men: I extend it to women, and confidently assert that they have been drawn from their duty by false representations." (002-028)

The rhetorical strategy is devastating: Wollstonecraft does not refute Rousseau's principle; she embraces it and shows that Rousseau himself failed to follow it to its logical conclusion. She repeatedly identifies [[sensibility]] — unchecked feeling operating without the guidance of reason — as the source of Rousseau's philosophical errors:

> "Rousseau's observations, it is proper to remark, were made in a country where the art of pleasing was refined only to extract the grossness of vice. He did not go back to nature, or his ruling appetite disturbed the operations of reason, else he would not have drawn these crude inferences." (005-014)

This diagnosis extends to Rousseau's entire educational scheme for women. By denying women the "tree of knowledge" while expecting virtue from them, Rousseau asks for the impossible:

> "But, according to the tenor of reasoning by which women are kept from the tree of knowledge, the important years of youth, the usefulness of age, and the rational hopes of futurity, are all to be sacrificed, to render woman an object of desire for a short time. Besides, how could Rousseau expect them to be virtuous and constant when reason is neither allowed to be the foundation of their virtue, nor truth the object of their inquiries?" (005-055)

Wollstonecraft's own account of reason, by contrast, is universalist: reason operates identically in men and women. The submission she upholds is to reason itself, not to any human authority:

> "I love man as my fellow; but his sceptre real or usurped, extends not to me, unless the reason of an individual demands my homage; and even then the submission is to reason, and not to man. In fact, the conduct of an accountable being must be regulated by the operations of its own reason; or on what foundation rests the throne of God?" (002-090)

## In Context

The concept of reason pervades every chapter of the Vindication. In the Dedication, Wollstonecraft appeals to [[charles-maurice-de-talleyrand-perigord|Talleyrand]]'s own reason to recognise the inconsistency of denying women rational [[education]] while claiming they share the same Creator:

> "I wish, sir, to set some investigations of this kind afloat in France; and should they lead to a confirmation of my principles, when your constitution is revised, the rights of woman may be respected, if it be fully proved that reason calls for this respect, and loudly demands *justice* for one half of the human race." (P02-018)

Chapter I establishes reason alongside [[virtue]] and [[knowledge]] as the measure of human worth: "Consequently the perfection of our [[nature]] and capability of [[happiness]], must be estimated by the degree of reason, [[virtue]], and [[knowledge]], that distinguish the individual" (001-006). It also diagnoses the central problem: prejudices have "clouded reason" and "spurious qualities have assumed the name of virtues" (001-007), making it necessary to return to first principles.

Chapter II, the most concentrated philosophical argument, repeatedly returns to reason as the standard against which social arrangements must be judged. Wollstonecraft critiques [[rousseau|Rousseau]]'s [[education]]al scheme precisely because it denies women the exercise of their own reason, making [[virtue]] impossible. She is unsparing in her condemnation of any system that expects moral conduct without rational foundation:

> "If, I say, for I would not impress by declamation when reason offers her sober light, if they are really capable of acting like rational creatures, let them not be treated like slaves; or, like the brutes who are dependent on the reason of man, when they associate with him; but cultivate their minds, give them the salutary, sublime curb of principle, and let them attain conscious dignity by feeling themselves only dependent on God." (002-087)

Later chapters extend the argument into specific domains — [[education]], marriage, [[modesty]] — always returning to the same core principle: without reason, there can be no [[virtue]], no independence, no true [[morality]]. By Chapter V, Wollstonecraft has sharpened this to a bitter indictment:

> "Hapless woman! what can be expected from thee, when the beings on whom thou art said naturally to depend for reason and support, have all an interest in deceiving thee! This is the root of the evil that has shed a corroding mildew on all thy virtues; and blighting in the bud thy opening faculties, has rendered thee the weak thing thou art!" (005-092)

### Trajectory Across the Text

Wollstonecraft's treatment of reason follows a discernible arc across the thirteen chapters. The early chapters (Dedication, Introduction, Ch. I–II) establish the philosophical foundations: reason as the distinguishing human faculty, its relationship to virtue and knowledge, and the logical inconsistency of denying it to women. The middle chapters (Ch. III–VII) apply this framework to specific domains — bodily cultivation, sensibility, modesty — showing how the neglect of reason produces specific deformations of female character. The late chapters (Ch. VIII–XIII) expand into institutional critique: education, parental duty, and the structure of society itself, each examined through the lens of whether it cultivates or stifles rational capacity. Throughout this arc, the consistent refrain is that reason must govern conduct: "the being cannot be termed rational or virtuous, who obeys any authority but that of reason" (013-079).

## Key Passages

> "Contending for the rights of women, my main argument is built on this simple principle, that if she be not prepared by education to become the companion of man, she will stop the progress of knowledge, for truth must be common to all, or it will be inefficacious with respect to its influence on general practice." (P02-006)

> "Who made man the exclusive judge, if woman partake with him the gift of reason?" (P02-009)

> "Consequently the perfection of our nature and capability of happiness, must be estimated by the degree of reason, virtue, and knowledge, that distinguish the individual, and direct the laws which bind society: and that from the exercise of reason, knowledge and virtue naturally flow, is equally undeniable, if mankind be viewed collectively." (001-006)

> "Such deeply rooted prejudices have clouded reason, and such spurious qualities have assumed the name of virtues, that it is necessary to pursue the course of reason as it has been perplexed and involved in error." (001-007)

> "In fact, it is a farce to call any being virtuous whose virtues do not result from the exercise of its own reason. This was Rousseau's opinion respecting men: I extend it to women." (002-028)

> "Can they supinely dream life away in the lap of pleasure, or in the languor of weariness, rather than assert their claim to pursue reasonable pleasures, and render themselves conspicuous, by practising the virtues which dignify mankind?" (002-060)
