## Definition

In _A Vindication of the Rights of Woman_, religion is not treated as a separate domain of inquiry but as intertwined with Wollstonecraft's arguments about reason, virtue, and education. She takes for granted the truth of Christianity while insisting that genuine religious faith — as opposed to superstitious observance or conventional piety — requires the full exercise of rational faculties. Women who are denied education cannot, in her view, be genuinely religious, because they lack the intellectual resources to understand the principles of their faith.

## In Context

Wollstonecraft's treatment of religion is strategic. In an era when open irreligion was scandalous, she positions her argument for women's education as consistent with, and indeed required by, sincere Christian faith. If women have immortal souls, she argues, they must be equipped to understand the moral law that governs those souls. A religion reduced to sentimental feeling or unquestioning obedience to male authority is not Christianity but a form of idolatry in which the husband or father replaces God.

In Chapter V, Wollstonecraft engages most directly with the religious dimensions of her argument. She critiques Rousseau's claim that women's religious faith should be determined by male authority, asking: "On what ground can religion or morality rest, when they are founded on the authority of fallible beings?" [005-126]. The implication is clear: a woman whose religious beliefs are simply inherited from her husband or father has no genuine faith at all — she has only second-hand opinion.

Wollstonecraft extends this argument to practical morality. If "reason and religion equally combine to enforce" certain principles of conduct, then women must be capable of reasoning about those principles [005-096]. Otherwise, their moral conduct is mere habit, not virtue.
