Wollstonecraft presents genuine religion as the rational submission to divine authority — the ultimate foundation of morality and virtue. She distinguishes this sharply from the "superstition" (005-111), sentimental piety, and institutional corruption that she saw as characteristic of the religion practised by and prescribed for women.

For Wollstonecraft, religion is inseparable from reason. "Rational religion" (013-024) is a submission of the understanding to truths apprehended by reason, not a surrender of reason to authority or emotion.

This conviction places her at odds with both the institutional church, whose "irksome ceremonies" (012-015) she saw as alienating children from genuine religious feeling, and the conduct-book tradition that recommended religion to women as an ornament of character rather than a principle of action.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft distinguishes between two kinds of religion throughout the *Vindication*. "True religion" or "rational religion" is founded on reason and issues in virtuous action; it is "a submission to the will of God" (013-024) that engages the understanding rather than bypassing it.

"Superstition," by contrast, is religion divorced from reason — a set of observances, emotions, and beliefs that comfort the practitioner without challenging their conduct or transforming their character.

The distinction is crucial to her argument because women, in her analysis, are taught a form of religion indistinguishable from superstition: emotional, sentimental, and disconnected from the rational understanding that alone can make religious belief the foundation of moral action.

Her own religious framework is broadly Christian but inflected with Enlightenment rationalism. She invokes "the divine authority of Jesus Christ" (013-019) and treats the Gospels as a source of moral truth, but she is impatient with theological controversy and ecclesiastical authority.

What matters to her is not doctrinal correctness but whether religion produces virtue. "Religion, pure source of comfort in this vale of tears!" (012-014) is a source of strength, not a set of propositions to be assented to.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's treatment of religion serves two argumentative purposes. First, it establishes the philosophical foundation for her claims about virtue and duty.

If morality is grounded in divine authority, then the path to virtue lies through reason — because only reason can apprehend the divine order and understand the duties it imposes.

This is why the education she proposes must include "the elements of religion" (012-050) taught in a manner that engages the understanding rather than the memory alone. Children who learn religion as a set of rote observances grow into adults for whom religion is merely "a farce" (012-010) — a Sunday performance that has no bearing on Monday's conduct.

Second, it exposes the hypocrisy of a society that claims religious sanction for the subordination of women while systematically denying women the education that would make genuine religious understanding possible.

"On what ground can religion or morality rest," she asks, "when justice is banished?" (005-110). A religion that teaches the equality of souls before God but permits the inequality of the sexes in education and civil society is not true religion but "superstition...mixed with...religion" (005-111).

The women who are praised for their piety are, in her analysis, practising a form of religion "which consists in warming the affections" (005-152) rather than in governing the conduct — and this is not religion at all but a sentimental substitute for it.

Her most controversial claim, for her time, is that institutional religion has contributed to women's degradation. By separating "religion" from "morality" (009-001), the church has treated religious observance as a distinct sphere rather than the foundation of all conduct.

The result is a situation in which "neither religion nor virtue, when they reside in the heart, can restrain the passions" (008-012).

The remedy is an education that integrates religion with reason and morality from the beginning, teaching children to understand their duties to God, to themselves, and to others as a single coherent framework.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Rousseau's treatment of religion in *Émile* presents religious belief as a matter of sentiment and conscience rather than reason. Sophie's religious education is correspondingly limited: she is to accept the religion of her parents without questioning it, because women's minds are not fitted for theological inquiry.

Wollstonecraft's response is implicit but sharp. She insists that women, no less than men, must understand the grounds of their religious beliefs if those beliefs are to serve as genuine moral principles.

## Key Passages

> The religion which consists in warming the affections, and exalting the imagination, without producing the fruits of righteousness, is not that which Jesus Christ came on earth to teach. (005-152)

> On what ground can religion or morality rest, when justice is banished from the domestic hearth, and women are made to believe that they were created for the pleasure of man? (005-110)

> Religion, pure source of comfort in this vale of tears! how has thy clear stream been muddied by the dabblers who have presumptuously attempted to confound thy simple truths with intricate systems and superstitious rites! (012-014)

> Rational religion, on the contrary, is a submission to the will of God, founded on a conviction that his laws are wise and good; and, consequently, that obedience to them must produce happiness. (013-024)
