1727 – 1801. English writer and member of the Bluestocking Circle, author of _Letters on the Improvement of the Mind_ (1773). Wollstonecraft singles Chapone out for rare praise among conduct-book writers, commending her "good sense" and "unaffected humility."

## Life

Hester Mulso was born in 1727 in Northamptonshire. An intellectually precocious child, she taught herself French, Italian, and Latin, and began writing poetry and essays in her teens. Through her friendship with the novelist Samuel Richardson, she entered London literary circles and became a prominent member of the Bluestocking group — an informal network of intellectual women that included Elizabeth Montagu, Elizabeth Carter, and later Anna Laetitia Barbauld.

In 1760 she married John Chapone, an attorney who died within a year. Widowed, Chapone devoted herself to writing. Her major work, _Letters on the Improvement of the Mind_ (1773), addressed to her niece, offered a curriculum of reading and moral instruction for young women. The book was enormously popular, running through numerous editions and influencing the education of generations of English girls.

## Role in the Text

Wollstonecraft's reference to Chapone appears in Chapter V, amid her extended critique of conduct-book writers who reduce female education to the art of pleasing men. After censuring Rousseau, Dr. Gregory, and Dr. Fordyce, Wollstonecraft pauses to acknowledge an exception: "Mrs. Chapone's Letters are written with such good sense, and unaffected humility, and contain so many useful observations, that I only mention them to pay the worthy writer this tribute of respect" (005-112).

This is one of the few moments in _A Vindication_ where Wollstonecraft offers unqualified praise to a writer on female conduct. Chapone's work, unlike that of Fordyce or Gregory, recommends genuine intellectual cultivation — reading history, studying philosophy, and strengthening the rational faculties — rather than mere ornamental accomplishments. Wollstonecraft's endorsement signals that her quarrel is not with all conduct literature but specifically with works that treat women as beings designed solely for male pleasure.

### Works Referenced

_Letters on the Improvement of the Mind_ (1773) is the work Wollstonecraft commends. Chapone's _Letters_ advocates reading in history, literature, and natural philosophy, and emphasizes the formation of character through rational self-discipline — themes that parallel Wollstonecraft's own educational program.

## Key Ideas

Chapone represents a moderate but genuine Enlightenment feminism. Her _Letters_ insists that women are rational beings capable of intellectual improvement, and that education should cultivate the mind rather than merely polish manners. She diverges from Wollstonecraft in tone — her approach is gentle and gradualist where Wollstonecraft's is confrontational — but shares the foundational premise that women's intellectual capacity is equal to men's and that society fails them by denying serious education.

Wollstonecraft's praise of Chapone is strategically significant: it demonstrates that her critique of conduct literature is discriminating rather than indiscriminate, and it aligns her project with the most respectable elements of the Bluestocking tradition.
