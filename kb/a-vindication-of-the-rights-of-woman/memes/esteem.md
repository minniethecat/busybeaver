**Esteem** is, for Wollstonecraft, the form of regard that reason grants to virtue. It is to be sharply distinguished from love, which is "a arbitrary or selfish passion" (005-106), and from desire, which is transient and bodily. Esteem, by contrast, is "the only lasting affection" (005-071) because it is grounded in the recognition of qualities that do not decay — the virtue, wisdom, and strength of character that make a person worthy of rational admiration. Throughout the *Vindication*, Wollstonecraft argues that a marriage not founded on esteem is merely "a selfish bargain" (005-106), and that women are systematically prevented from cultivating the qualities that would make them objects of esteem rather than of desire.

The concept is central to Wollstonecraft's re-imagining of the relations between the sexes. If women were educated to be rational and virtuous rather than merely pleasing, they would be capable of inspiring and receiving esteem — and the relations between men and women would be transformed from the mutual exploitation of gallantry into the mutual respect of friendship.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's understanding of esteem draws on the classical and Enlightenment tradition of distinguishing between the passions and the rational affections. In the Aristotelian tradition, esteem is the appropriate response to *arete* — excellence of character. In the Enlightenment tradition, particularly in the work of Adam Smith and David Hume, esteem is analysed as a form of moral approbation grounded in sympathy. Wollstonecraft synthesises these traditions and gives them a distinctively feminist inflection.

"Love and esteem are very distinct things" (004-042), she insists. Love, as commonly understood in the eighteenth century, is a passion — a feeling that arises unbidden and that can as easily attach to a worthless object as to a worthy one. Esteem, by contrast, is an act of judgment: to esteem someone is to judge that they possess qualities that make them worthy of regard. A woman who has been educated only to please may inspire love (or at least desire), but she cannot inspire esteem, because she has not been given the opportunity to develop the virtues that esteem recognises.

The distinction between "habitual esteem" (005-106) and the fleeting regard that passes for esteem in fashionable society is crucial. Habitual esteem is the settled disposition that arises from long acquaintance with another's character — it is the fruit of "the tender confidence" (005-106) that only sustained intimacy can produce. This is the esteem that should cement marriage, and its absence is what makes most marriages, in Wollstonecraft's view, "mere matrimonial ties" without moral substance.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument about esteem is inseparable from her critique of female education. The existing system of female accomplishments — music, dancing, needlework, the arts of pleasing — is designed to make women attractive, not estimable. "In order to admire or esteem any thing for a continuance, we must have a knowledge of its qualities" (006-010) — but women are systematically denied the education that would give them estimable qualities. They are taught to display rather than to be, and the display may attract admiration but cannot command esteem.

The consequences for marriage are devastating. A marriage founded on desire alone is a marriage destined for misery, because desire fades and leaves nothing in its place. "Before marriage they will not be able to distinguish esteem from love; and after marriage, they will find that they have been deceived" (005-106) — the deception is not the result of bad faith but of a system that prevents women from developing the qualities that would make them estimable and prevents men from valuing those qualities in women.

Wollstonecraft also develops the concept of "filial esteem" (012-003) in her discussion of the parent-child relationship. Children ought to esteem their parents, but esteem cannot be commanded — it must be earned through the parent's demonstrated virtue. "Filial esteem always has a dash of fear mixed" (012-003) with it when the parent governs by authority rather than reason; only when the parent governs by reason can the child's esteem be pure.

The connection between esteem and friendship is one of Wollstonecraft's most important insights. "Esteem, the foundation of friendship" (006-011) — the phrase encapsulates her conviction that the highest form of human relationship is friendship, and that friendship is only possible between persons who esteem one another. This is why the existing relations between the sexes are so impoverished: men and women cannot be friends because women are not educated to be estimable, and men are not educated to value women for anything other than their charms.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The eighteenth-century discourse of esteem was shaped by the tradition of *honnêteté* — the code of honour and politeness that governed aristocratic society. Esteem, in this tradition, attached to rank, manners, and reputation rather than to virtue as such. Wollstonecraft's insistence that esteem is grounded in virtue alone is a radical democratisation of the concept — a move that aligns her with the republican tradition of civic virtue against the aristocratic tradition of honour.

Rousseau's treatment of esteem is instructive. In *Émile*, the tutor cultivates the pupil's *amour-propre* — the desire for the esteem of others — as a motive for virtuous action. But Rousseau confines this mechanism to men; Sophie's virtue is to be private and invisible, and her "esteem" is to be directed entirely toward her husband. Wollstonecraft's response is that a woman who is capable of virtue is capable of being an object of esteem, and a society that denies this is not merely unjust but irrational.
