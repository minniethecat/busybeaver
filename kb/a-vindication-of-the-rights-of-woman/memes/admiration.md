The superficial, often sensual regard directed at women that Wollstonecraft distinguishes from the rational esteem that should characterize relations between equals. She argues that women are educated to inspire admiration rather than to deserve respect, and that this substitution is a central mechanism of their degradation.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft draws a crucial distinction between admiration and esteem. Admiration is a response to surface qualities — beauty, grace, charm — while esteem is a response to moral and intellectual substance. "It is respect for the understanding that keeps alive tenderness for the person," she writes (005-071). When women are educated only to please, they may excite admiration, but they cannot command the lasting respect that alone makes companionship possible. The problem is not admiration itself — Wollstonecraft acknowledges that "the exalted pleasure which intellectual pursuits afford" (005-127) is a worthy object — but the substitution of admiration for esteem as the goal of women's lives.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

The systematized cultivation of admiration is, for Wollstonecraft, one of the most damaging consequences of women's miseducation. "Women are every where in this deplorable state," she observes; "for, in order to preserve their innocence, as ignorance is courteously termed, truth is hidden from them, and they are made to assume an artificial character before their faculties have acquired any strength" (002-028). The result is that indignation — not admiration — is the appropriate response to the women that this system produces.

Wollstonecraft traces how the desire for admiration corrupts relationships between the sexes. A woman "may acquire the public admiration" (004-027) through beauty and accomplishments, but she will not thereby become a fit companion for a rational man. The man who marries a woman for her beauty alone will find, in time, that "familiarity might have turned admiration into disgust" (002-061). Only intellectual companionship can sustain a marriage.

Even in her discussion of modesty, Wollstonecraft notes that the excessive concern with being admired distorts women's character. A woman "intoxicated by the admiration she receives" (009-005) neglects the domestic affections that are her genuine duty. The "savage desire of admiration" (013-046) is a vestige of barbarism that civilized education ought to extinguish.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The conduct-book tradition — Rousseau, Gregory, Fordyce — treated the ability to inspire admiration as women's natural and proper goal. Wollstonecraft's radical innovation is to insist that this goal is itself degrading. She demands that women be educated not to attract admiration but to deserve esteem. The contrast is stark: in the conventional view, a woman's worth is validated by the admiration she receives from men; in Wollstonecraft's view, a woman's worth is validated by the exercise of her own reason and virtue.

## Key Passages

> Love, the common passion, in which chance and sensation take place of choice and reason, is in some degree, felt by the mass of mankind; for it is not necessary to speak, at present, of the emotions that rise above or sink below love. This passion, naturally increased by suspense and difficulties, draws the mind out of its accustomed state, and exalts the affections; but the security of marriage, allowing the fever of love to subside, a healthy temperature is thought insipid, only by those who have not sufficient intellect to substitute the calm tenderness of friendship, the confidence of respect, instead of blind admiration, and the sensual emotions of fondness. (002-056)

> Modesty! Sacred offspring of sensibility and reason! true delicacy of mind! may I unblamed presume to investigate thy nature, and trace to its covert the mild charm, that mellowing each harsh feature of a character, renders what would otherwise only inspire cold admiration — lovely! (007-001)
