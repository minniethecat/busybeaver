# Talents

Natural abilities and acquired skills that Wollstonecraft distinguishes carefully from superficial "accomplishments." While accomplishments are ornamental and aimed at pleasing others (particularly men), talents represent genuine capacity — intellectual, moral, or practical — that contributes to individual dignity and social good. In her framework, talents are divinely bestowed but must be cultivated through education and exercised through virtuous action to have real value.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft uses "talents" to denote genuine abilities — intellectual, moral, and practical — rather than the decorative "accomplishments" that fashionable education cultivated in women. She draws on the biblical parable of the talents (003-056) to argue that abilities are a trust to be developed, not buried: "he gave to one man five talents, and to another one." The implication is clear — those who fail to develop their God-given capacities commit a moral failing. For Wollstonecraft, talents are demonstrated through useful contribution to society, not through display: "talents and virtues" (P03-004) are repeatedly paired, signaling that genuine ability is inseparable from moral character.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's treatment of talents is inseparable from her critique of women's education. She argues that whatever natural talents women possess are systematically suppressed by a society that values only their ornamental qualities. "Men neglect the duties which reason points out, and then boast of their talents" (009-001) — the point being that genuine talent is shown through dutiful action, not self-promotion. She insists that "the middle rank, the one in which talents thrive best" (004-100) is denied to women, who are confined to domestic dependency regardless of their abilities.

The distinction between talents and accomplishments is central to her argument. Accomplishments — music, drawing, superficial language skills — are taught to women to make them pleasing; talents — reasoning, judgment, moral discernment — are neglected because they might make women independent. "When they come to display other talents, whose utility is already apparent, the prior acquisition of some knowledge or strength of mind, makes the task easy" (005-030).

Wollstonecraft also warns against the waste of talent through false modesty. She criticizes women who "hide her talents under a bushel" (005-085), arguing that the world is deprived of women's contributions by a system that teaches them self-effacement. The consequence is not only individual frustration but social loss: "great talents, but great virtues" (005-118) arise from the same conditions of freedom and education.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Rousseau's Sophie is educated only to be useful and agreeable to Émile — her talents, such as they are, exist entirely in the service of male development. Wollstonecraft rejects this instrumental view outright. The biblical framework she employs — "here are five talents" (003-056) — positions women as stewards accountable to God for the development of their abilities, not as accessories to men's projects. Gregory, who advised women to conceal their wit, is the target of her criticism of hiding talents under a bushel.

## Key Passages

> "the attainment of those talents and virtues, the exercise of which ennobles the human character, and which raise mortals in their progress toward perfection" (P03-004)

> "He gave to one man five talents, and to another one. The woman of talents, if she be not absolutely ugly, is the most envied and persecuted of her sex." (012-082)

> "Men neglect the duties which reason points out, and then boast of their talents; they neglect the duties incumbent on man, yet treat with the most supercilious contempt the woman who acts up to the dignity of her nature by respecting herself." (009-001)

> "that middle rank, the one in which talents thrive best, extends not to women; for the same rigid system that oppresses them as human beings, confines them within a narrower sphere" (004-100)

> "I have been led to imagine that the few extraordinary women who have rushed in eccentrical directions out of the orbit prescribed to their sex, were male spirited, and that they had more sense than their forefathers could ever have discovered — they avoided that false modesty, which even the most virtuous women are taught to assume, instead of being determined to hide her talents under a bushel." (005-085)
