Vice, in Wollstonecraft's moral psychology, is not an original sin but a social product: it is "the natural effect of ignorance" (002-003), sustained by the "arbitrary power" (001-021) of hereditary distinctions, and rooted in "the poisoned source of female vices and follies" — the sensual homage paid to beauty (003-040).

## Definition

Wollstonecraft defines vice structurally rather than theologically.

She traces it not to individual moral failure but to institutional causes: the ignorance in which women are kept, the "arbitrary power" of hereditary rank (001-021), and a system of education that substitutes "cunning" and "OUTWARD obedience" for genuine virtue (002-003).

Vice "skulks, with all its native deformity, from close investigation" (001-009); it flourishes only where reason is suppressed and "specious prejudices" take the place of knowledge. This definition is fundamentally optimistic: if vice is the product of corrupt institutions, then reforming those institutions — particularly education — can eradicate it.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

The argument proceeds on two fronts. First, Wollstonecraft insists that women's vices are not natural but produced.

When men complain of women's "headstrong passions and groveling vices," the answer is: "Behold, I should answer, the natural effect of ignorance!" (002-003). Women are systematically denied the education that would give their minds stability.

Second, she traces vice to the structure of power. In her critique of Rousseau in Chapter 1, she observes that the Genevan "saw vice trampling on virtue" but failed to trace "the gigantic mischief up to arbitrary power, up to the hereditary distinctions that clash with the mental superiority that naturally raises a man above his fellows" (001-021).

The argument is radical: vice is not a moral contagion spread by civilization (as Rousseau thought) but a structural consequence of unnatural hierarchy.

The analysis culminates in Chapter 3, where Wollstonecraft identifies "the poisoned source of female vices and follies" as "the sensual homage paid to beauty" (003-040). When women are valued only for their appearance, the entire moral economy is corrupted.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft's structural account of vice departs from both the Christian doctrine of original sin and Rousseau's narrative of civilization as corruption.

For Christian moralists, vice is a consequence of the Fall — an innate tendency requiring grace to overcome. For Rousseau, vice enters with society and the arts. Wollstonecraft rejects both: vice is neither innate nor a generic consequence of civilization, but the specific product of arbitrary power and degraded education.

## Key Passages

> Going back to first principles, vice skulks, with all its native deformity, from close investigation; but a set of shallow reasoners are always exclaiming that these arguments prove too much, and that a measure rotten at the core may be expedient. (001-009)

> He saw vice trampling on virtue, and the semblance of goodness taking place of the reality; he saw talents bent by power to sinister purposes, and never thought of tracing the gigantic mischief up to arbitrary power. (001-021)

> Men complain, and with reason, of the follies and caprices of our sex, when they do not keenly satirize our headstrong passions and groveling vices. Behold, I should answer, the natural effect of ignorance! (002-003)

> It will also require some time to convince women that they act contrary to their real interest on an enlarged scale, when they cherish or affect weakness under the name of delicacy, and to convince the world that the poisoned source of female vices and follies ... has been the sensual homage paid to beauty. (003-040)
