# Knowledge

The intellectual cultivation that Wollstonecraft identifies as inseparable from moral development — she famously declares that "without knowledge there can be no morality."

## Definition

Wollstonecraft defines knowledge not as mere factual accumulation but as the development of the understanding through rational inquiry. It is the fruit of education properly conceived — not the "accomplishments" taught to women but the systematic cultivation of the mind. Knowledge, in her framework, is the bridge between reason (the faculty) and virtue (the moral quality): reason must be filled with knowledge to produce genuine moral judgment. "The degree of reason, virtue, and knowledge" that distinguishes individuals and nations are three aspects of a single developmental process (001-006).

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

The author's most striking claim about knowledge is that "without knowledge there can be no morality" (004-044). This is not merely an epistemological point — it is a direct attack on the educational deprivation of women. If morality requires knowledge, and women are systematically denied knowledge, then women are being systematically denied the capacity for moral action. The entire edifice of gendered virtue — which holds women morally responsible while denying them the intellectual resources for moral judgment — is revealed as incoherent.

Wollstonecraft traces how the denial of knowledge to women produces precisely the intellectual vices that are then cited as proof of women's inferiority: "a set of shallow reasoners" (001-009) are produced by shallow education. Women are "rendered weak and wretched by a variety of concurring causes" (P03-001), then blamed for the weakness that ignorance produces.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The dominant educational theory of the period, most influentially articulated by Rousseau, held that women should be educated only enough to make them pleasing companions to men. Deep knowledge was considered unfeminine, even dangerous. Wollstonecraft's insistence on rigorous intellectual education for women — identical in substance to men's — was one of the most radical positions in eighteenth-century educational thought.

## Key Passages

> "without knowledge there can be no morality" (004-044)

> "the degree of reason, virtue, and knowledge, that distinguishes the individual, and guides the laws" (001-006)

> "a set of shallow reasoners are always exclaiming" — on the products of shallow education (001-009)

> "women in particular, are rendered weak and wretched by a variety of concurring causes" (P03-001)
