# Charles Maurice de Talleyrand-Périgord

## Overview

Charles Maurice de Talleyrand-Périgord (1754–1838) is the named dedicatee of the *Vindication of the Rights of Woman*. Wollstonecraft addresses him not as a personal acquaintance but as a legislator — the author of a 1791 report to the French National Assembly that proposed a system of public education... for boys only. Her Dedication is at once a courtesy and a challenge: she acknowledges France's progress toward liberty while insisting that any liberty that excludes half the human race is incomplete.

The choice of Talleyrand as dedicatee is strategic. As a former bishop who had embraced the Revolution, Talleyrand embodied the progressive forces Wollstonecraft hoped to influence. By addressing him directly, she placed her argument at the centre of the revolutionary debate about the scope of human [[rights]]. She opens with disarming politeness — "Having read with great pleasure a pamphlet, which you have lately published, on National Education, I dedicate this volume to you" (P02-002) — before launching into the substantive challenge.

### The Dedication as Political Argument

The Dedication (pages P02-001 through P02-018) is not merely formal courtesy. It is a carefully constructed argument that uses Talleyrand's own revolutionary principles against his exclusion of women. Wollstonecraft observes that he himself had noted the contradiction: "Consider, Sir, dispassionately, these observations, for a glimpse of this truth seemed to open before you when you observed, 'that to see one half of the human race excluded by the other from all participation of government, was a political phenomenon that, according to abstract principles, it was impossible to explain'" (P02-008). If Talleyrand admits the inexplicability of women's exclusion, then his own report — which perpetuates that exclusion — stands condemned by its author's own logic.

Wollstonecraft's Dedication escalates from polite address to moral demand. The central paragraph is the pivot: "Consider, I address you as a legislator, whether, when men contend for their freedom, and to be allowed to judge for themselves, respecting their own happiness, it be not inconsistent and unjust to subjugate women, even though you firmly believe that you are acting in the manner best calculated to promote their happiness? Who made man the exclusive judge, if woman partake with him the gift of reason?" (P02-009). The rhetorical question "Who made man the exclusive judge?" strikes at the foundational assumption of patriarchal authority — that men are entitled to define women's interests.

She then extends the logic to its conclusion: "But, if women are to be excluded, without having a voice, from a participation of the natural rights of mankind, prove first, to ward off the charge of injustice and inconsistency, that they want reason" (P02-011). The burden of proof is shifted: it is not women who must demonstrate their fitness for [[rights]], but men who must demonstrate women's unfitness. The Dedication closes with a vision of future reform: "I wish, sir, to set some investigations of this kind afloat in France; and should they lead to a confirmation of my principles, when your constitution is revised, the rights of woman may be respected, if it be fully proved that reason calls for this respect, and loudly demands *justice* for one half of the human race" (P02-018).

### Talleyrand in the Vindication

Beyond the Dedication, Chapter I and Chapter II return to the themes raised in the address to Talleyrand, extending the argument beyond its original addressee to encompass all those who would deny women the cultivation of [[reason]]. Wollstonecraft returns to the theme of legislative exclusion in Chapter IX, where she argues that "without rights there cannot be any incumbent duties" (009-017) — a direct rebuttal to the assumption that women can fulfil their social duties without possessing civil rights.

The Dedication's engagement with Talleyrand also exemplifies Wollstonecraft's broader argument about [[education]]. Talleyrand's report proposed a national system of education that would produce citizens — but only male citizens. Wollstonecraft's response is to take the same principle — that [[education]] forms [[character]] and prepares individuals for citizenship — and insist on its universal application. If [[education]] is necessary for men to fulfil their civic [[duty|duties]], it is equally necessary for women to fulfil theirs.

## Biographical Summary

Charles Maurice de Talleyrand-Périgord (1754–1838) was one of the most skilled diplomats in European history. A bishop who supported the French Revolution, he served as Foreign Minister under the Directory, Napoleon, and Louis XVIII. His 1791 *Rapport sur l'instruction publique* to the National Assembly proposed a system of national education that explicitly excluded girls — the immediate catalyst for Wollstonecraft's *Vindication*.
