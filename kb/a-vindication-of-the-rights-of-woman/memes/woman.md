Woman, in Wollstonecraft's argument, is not a fixed natural category but a social product — "the woman who has only been taught to please" (002-043) has been systematically denied the rational education that would make her a genuine moral agent and companion to man.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft approaches "woman" not as an abstract essence but as a historically and socially constructed condition. She opens by observing that women are placed in "silken fetters" (P01-013) — disguised restraints that appear ornamental but function as chains.

She is careful to distinguish between the woman as she appears under existing social arrangements and the woman as she could become under reformed institutions. The present state is one of degradation: "after surveying the history of woman, I cannot help agreeing with the severest satirist" (002-072). Yet this degradation is not natural — it is, she insists, the product of education, custom, and the "prevailing opinion that woman was created for man" (002-034).

Wollstonecraft grounds her analysis in the same rational principles she applies to men. By "a parity of reasoning" (P02-010), if rights belong to man by virtue of his rational nature, they belong equally to woman "if woman partake with him the gift of reason" (P02-012).

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Woman occupies a uniquely central position in Wollstonecraft's argument because the condition of women serves as both evidence of social corruption and the lever for reform. Her case proceeds through several linked claims.

First, the present character of woman is artificial. Women are "placed in a station in which" (P02-003) their faculties are systematically underdeveloped. They are educated not for virtue but for the gratification of male desire: "the person of a woman is not, as it were, idolized" (P02-008) until she loses the capacity for rational independence.

Second, this artificial character is harmful to women themselves. The accomplished woman of received opinion — possessing "a good degree of observation, a moderate quantity of cunning, and a great deal of temper" (002-067) — is a diminished creature. She is denied "the natural frankness of youth" (002-047) and taught dissimulation instead of sincerity.

Third, the degradation of woman harms society as a whole. Because women are the first educators of children, their ignorance is perpetuated across generations. "If all the faculties of woman's mind are only to be cultivated" (002-063) for the purpose of pleasing men, then the whole of society suffers the loss of their rational contribution.

Fourth, the remedy is education on rational principles. Wollstonecraft predicts that when "sound politics diffuse liberty, mankind, including woman, will become more wise and virtuous" (002-086). The liberation of woman is inseparable from the broader Enlightenment project of political and moral improvement.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft engages directly with Rousseau's claim "that a woman should never, for a moment feel herself independent" (002-031). Where Rousseau constructs woman as naturally subordinate and designed for male pleasure, Wollstonecraft insists that "it cannot be demonstrated that woman is essentially inferior to man" (002-084) — only that she has been made to appear so through unequal education.

She also refutes the common argument that giving women rational education would make them masculine or unattractive. "I shall be told that woman would then lose many of her prerogatives" (002-037), she anticipates, but counters that "the woman who strengthens her body and exercises her mind" (002-052) becomes a more, not less, valuable companion.

## Key Passages

> After surveying the history of woman, I cannot help agreeing with the severest satirist, who considers the sex as the weakest as well as the most oppressed half of the species. (002-072)

> It cannot be demonstrated that woman is essentially inferior to man, because she has always been subjugated, and the brutes have been subjugated by man; but in a state of nature, there is no superiority but that of force. (002-084)

> I shall first consider women in the grand light of human creatures, who, in common with men, are placed on this earth to unfold their faculties. (002-005)

> If all the faculties of woman's mind are only to be cultivated as they are subservient to the tyranny of man, I would still ask, for what purpose was the reasoning faculty given? (002-063)
