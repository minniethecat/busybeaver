# Sentimental

A pejorative term in Wollstonecraft's vocabulary, describing a culture and literary mode that privileges feeling over reason, and that she holds responsible for corrupting women's character by teaching them to value emotional display above moral principle.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft uses "sentimental" to name a pervasive cultural pathology: the substitution of emotional indulgence for rational thought, and the veneration of refined feeling as an end in itself. In the Dedication, she identifies "a kind of sentimental lust" as having "prevailed" in attitudes toward women, reducing them to objects of a voluptuary's desire rather than companions worthy of respect (P02-004). Sentimental literature — particularly the novels consumed by middle-class women — spreads this corruption: it teaches "sheer sensuality under a sentimental veil" that corrupts the imagination and weakens the moral faculty (004-092). The sentimental jargon of popular fiction "equally tend[s] to corrupt the taste and the heart" (013-028).

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

The sentimental mode is dangerous precisely because it mimics virtue while undermining it. Women taught to prize "sentimental cant" learn to perform refined feeling rather than to cultivate genuine moral judgment (001-028). Wollstonecraft's critique is sharpest in her analysis of Rousseau, whose eloquence she acknowledges even as she rejects "most sentimental rant" in which he "details his opinions respecting the female character" (005-059). She notes that some of Rousseau's followers have produced writing "so very sentimental" that even "rational men" use it to justify excluding women from serious education (005-060). The sentimental and the rational are opposed: sentimental culture keeps women in intellectual infancy, while rational education would liberate them.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Eighteenth-century culture of sensibility often celebrated refined feeling as a mark of moral superiority, particularly in women. Wollstonecraft breaks sharply with this tradition: for her, sensibility untethered from reason is not a virtue but a source of corruption. The "fine sentimental lady" is, in her analysis, "a more insidious member of society" than the openly vicious woman, because her refinement disguises the absence of genuine principle (004-058). This places her at odds not only with Rousseau but with the entire sentimental novel tradition of Richardson, Sterne, and their imitators.
