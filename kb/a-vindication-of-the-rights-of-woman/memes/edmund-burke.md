# Edmund Burke

[[Edmund Burke]] (1729–1797) is the intellectual catalyst behind [[Mary Wollstonecraft]]'s emergence as a political writer. His _Reflections on the Revolution in France_ (1790) occasioned her first major work, _A Vindication of the Rights of Men_ (1790) — one of the earliest responses to Burke and the work that established her in London's radical circles.

Burke and Wollstonecraft represent the defining intellectual cleavage of the 1790s: tradition versus reason, prescription versus rights, inherited order versus revolutionary possibility. Her critique of "prejudice" throughout the Vindication is a sustained rebuttal of Burke's defense of prejudice as accumulated social wisdom.

## Life

Burke was born in Dublin in 1729 and educated at Trinity College before moving to London, where he became a Member of Parliament and the most eloquent conservative political philosopher in the English-speaking world. His career was defined by a defense of constitutional tradition: he supported the American colonists' grievances while opposing the French Revolution, arguing that reform must respect inherited institutions and gradual change.

The biographical sketch prefacing the Vindication records that Wollstonecraft, "full of sentiments of liberty, and indignant at what she thought subversive of it, seized her pen and produced the first attack upon that famous work" (P01-011). Her response, though "intemperate and contemptuous," was also "vehemently and impetuously eloquent."

> "Though Burke was beloved by the enlightened friends of freedom, they were dissatisfied and disgusted with what they deemed an outrage upon it." (P01-012)

## Role in the Text

### The Vindication of the Rights of Men

Burke appears primarily in Wollstonecraft's biographical sketch rather than the main body of the Vindication, but his intellectual presence is pervasive. Her first response to Burke, the _Rights of Men_, was published mere weeks after his _Reflections_ — making her among the first to publicly challenge his vision of society as a partnership "between those who are living, those who are dead, and those who are to be born."

The success of this response gave Wollstonecraft the confidence to undertake the Vindication, which the sketch reports she "accomplished in six weeks" (P01-013). The compressed timeframe speaks to the urgency of her response to Burke's political conservatism and the broader assumptions about natural hierarchy that he represented.

### The Debate Over Prejudice

Where Burke celebrated the "decent drapery of life" — the manners and customs that give society stability — Wollstonecraft sees only "a polish of manners that injures the substance" (P02-004). Burke's defense of prejudice as accumulated social wisdom stands in direct opposition to Wollstonecraft's rationalist conviction that every custom must justify itself before the tribunal of reason.

Wollstonecraft's insistence that virtue has no sex extends Burke's own principles beyond the domain he was willing to apply them. If rights derive from rational nature rather than inherited station, then the exclusion of women becomes logically unsustainable — a conclusion Burke himself never drew.

### Works Referenced

- _Reflections on the Revolution in France_ (1790) — rebutted by Wollstonecraft in _A Vindication of the Rights of Men_
- _A Philosophical Enquiry into the Origin of Our Ideas of the Sublime and Beautiful_ (1757) — influential on eighteenth-century aesthetic theory

## Key Ideas

| Concept | Burke's Position | Wollstonecraft's Rebuttal |
|---------|-----------------|--------------------------|
| Tradition | Accumulated wisdom to be preserved | Unreasoned prejudice to be examined |
| Reform | Gradual, respecting inherited institutions | Rights-based, answering to reason |
| Society | A partnership across generations | A contract among rational equals |
| Manners | The "decent drapery of life" | A polish that "injures the substance" |
| Rights | Derived from convention and inheritance | Derived from rational nature |

The Burke–Wollstonecraft debate represents the fundamental cleavage of the revolutionary era. Burke argued that social order depends on inherited institutions and gradual reform; Wollstonecraft countered that no institution can claim authority without demonstrating its grounding in reason. Their disagreement extends beyond politics to epistemology — whether wisdom is accumulated through tradition or discovered through rational inquiry.
