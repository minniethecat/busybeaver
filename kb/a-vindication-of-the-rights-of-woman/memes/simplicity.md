**Simplicity** is, for Wollstonecraft, a quality of character that is both the foundation of genuine virtue and the principal casualty of women's education. In a society that prizes refinement, polish, and the art of pleasing above all else, "simplicity of character is rarely to be seen" (007-036). To recover simplicity is not to retreat into ignorance — Wollstonecraft explicitly distinguishes simplicity from folly — but to cultivate a mind so firmly grounded in truth that it has no need of artifice.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's concept of simplicity operates across several related domains. In the moral domain, simplicity names the quality of a character that acts from principle rather than calculation — the person whose behaviour is the "natural exteriour mark" (007-036) of an inwardly cultivated virtue rather than a performance designed to produce a desired effect. In the intellectual domain, simplicity refers to the commitment to "simple truth" (005-032) — the unadorned language of reason that Wollstonecraft sets against the "turgid bombast" of fashionable sensibility. In the affective domain, simplicity describes the natural, unforced quality of genuine love — "the interesting simplicity of affection" (P02-008) that is the ornament of a mind enriched by virtue and sense.

The unifying thread is the opposition between simplicity and artifice. Whatever is simple is natural, sincere, and grounded; whatever is artificial is cultivated, calculated, and unstable. "Simplicity and independence of mind" (005-058) are paired as the twin marks of intellectual virtue — the one denoting freedom from affectation, the other freedom from external authority. Together they describe the ideal of the self-governing rational being that Wollstonecraft holds out to both sexes.

Crucially, simplicity is not ignorance. Wollstonecraft does not valorize the uncultivated mind; she insists that simplicity of character is the product of a proper education, not its absence. The "simple ones" whom she addresses — "will ye love simplicity, and hate knowledge?" (005-093, quoting Proverbs) — are warned that simplicity without understanding is not virtue but vulnerability. The goal is a simplicity that has passed through knowledge and emerged on the other side — not the innocence of the child but the integrity of the wise.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

The argument about simplicity is embedded in Wollstonecraft's broader critique of women's education. The system of female accomplishment — the endless cultivation of manners, graces, and decorative skills — "banish[es] all simplicity and variety of character out of the female world" (005-080). The mechanism is clear: when women are taught that their value lies in the impression they make on others, they become performers rather than persons, and the performance inevitably supplants the character it was meant to express.

The result is a double loss. Women lose the "dignity of mind" (003-029) that comes from acting on principle rather than for effect; society loses the "variety of character" that would enrich it if women were permitted to develop their natural capacities rather than being pressed into a single mould. Wollstonecraft's phrase "all simplicity and variety" is telling — simplicity and variety are not opposed but mutually reinforcing. A society of simple characters would be a society of genuinely diverse individuals; a society of artificial characters is a society of uniform performances.

The political dimension emerges clearly in the analysis of power. Women "obtaining power by unjust means, by practising or fostering vice, evidently lose the rank which reason would assign them, and they become either abject slaves or capricious tyrants. They lose all simplicity, all dignity of mind, in acquiring power" (003-029). The loss of simplicity is not merely an aesthetic or moral misfortune but a political catastrophe — it is what makes women's power, when they exercise it, corrupt and corrupting. The only remedy is a power grounded in reason and virtue, which would be expressed in the "simple elegance of sincerity" (012-036) rather than in manipulation and display.

Wollstonecraft's alternative is a vision of character formed from within. "Lessons of politeness, and that formulary of decorum, which treads on the heels of falsehood, would be rendered useless by habitual propriety of behaviour" (012-036). If the understanding were cultivated and virtue made the object of education, the graces of behaviour would follow naturally — they would be the "natural exteriour mark" of an inward state rather than a mask worn to conceal its absence. This is the "simple elegance of sincerity" that Wollstonecraft opposes to the "meretricious compliments that shine with false lustre in the heartless intercourse of fashionable life" (012-036).

The educational ideal Wollstonecraft describes — a "model of that grand simplicity, of those concurring energies, which arrest our attention and command our reverence" (012-066) — is not a return to primitive innocence but the achievement of a unified character in which all faculties work together under the guidance of reason. It is, in its way, a classical ideal: the harmony of the soul that is the condition of genuine freedom.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The conduct-book tradition — Fordyce, Gregory, and the innumerable writers of manuals on female deportment — is Wollstonecraft's immediate target. These writers elevated decorum to the supreme feminine virtue, producing elaborate codes of behaviour that were meant to govern every aspect of a woman's public presentation. Wollstonecraft's response is uncompromising: "Decorum, indeed, is the one thing needful! decorum is to supplant nature, and banish all simplicity and variety of character out of the female world. Yet what good end can all this serve?" (005-080).

The rhetorical structure is characteristic — the exclamation mark after "needful" signals irony, and the question at the end exposes the vacuity of the whole enterprise. Decorum serves no good end because it addresses the surface rather than the substance. A woman who has been taught only how to appear virtuous will never be virtuous; she will only be skilled in the performance of virtue, which is a different thing entirely.

Rousseau's version of simplicity is more complex and more dangerous. Rousseau celebrated simplicity as a natural virtue, particularly in his account of Sophie, but Wollstonecraft recognized that the simplicity Rousseau praised was the simplicity of ignorance — the simplicity of a being who has been deliberately kept from knowledge in order to preserve her charm. True simplicity, for Wollstonecraft, is compatible with knowledge and indeed requires it. The "simple ones" who love simplicity and hate knowledge are not virtuous but vulnerable.

## Key Passages

> Decorum is to supplant nature, and banish all simplicity and variety of character out of the female world. Yet what good end can all this serve? (005-080)

> They lose all simplicity, all dignity of mind, in acquiring power, and act as men are observed to act when they have been exalted by the same means. (003-029)

> This mental grace, not noticed by vulgar eyes, often flashes across a rough countenance, and irradiating every feature, shows simplicity and independence of mind. (005-058)

> In fact, behaviour in most circumstances is now so much thought of, that simplicity of character is rarely to be seen; yet, if men were only anxious to cultivate each virtue, and let it take root firmly in the mind, the grace resulting from it, its natural exteriour mark, would soon strip affectation of its flaunting plumes. (007-036)

> I, who love simplicity, would gladly give up politeness for a quarter of the virtue that has been sacrificed to an equivocal quality, which, at best, should only be the polish of virtue. (005-032)
