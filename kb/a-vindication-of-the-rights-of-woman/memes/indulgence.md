Indulgence appears throughout A Vindication as a term of moral condemnation, applied to parenting, self-governance, and social arrangements alike. For Wollstonecraft, indulgence is not kindness but its corruption: the refusal to impose rational discipline where it is needed, motivated by sentimentality rather than genuine care.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft defines indulgence by its effects. She asks "whether negligence or indulgence is most hurtful" (004-068), pairing the two as cognate forms of parental failure. Negligence withholds attention; indulgence withholds discipline. Both produce the same result: a child whose character is formed by appetite rather than by reason. The mother who indulges her children is guilty of a subtler form of negligence — she attends to their desires but not to their moral development.

The concept operates across multiple domains. In the realm of parenting, indulgence is the error of "too much indulgence" that corrupts rather than cultivates virtue (005-013). In the realm of self-governance, it describes the "enervating indulgences" (003-021) that weaken moral fibre. In the realm of social critique, it names the "unbridled indulgence" (008-024) characteristic of a society that has severed pleasure from principle. The consistency of usage across these domains reveals indulgence as a structural concept in Wollstonecraft's moral philosophy: wherever reason ought to govern, indulgence names the failure to govern.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument against indulgence is simultaneously pedagogical and political. Pedagogically, she insists that children require rational discipline, not the "improper indulgence" (010-006) that "spoils them." The indulgent parent — typically the mother in Wollstonecraft's analysis — mistakes sentiment for virtue, believing that giving children what they want is the same as giving them what they need. This error has lasting consequences: children raised on indulgence enter adulthood without the capacity for self-denial, and a society composed of such adults cannot sustain republican institutions.

The political dimension is clearest in her observation that "even the duties of humanity are too often sacrificed to the indulgence of vanity and selfishness" (004-088). Indulgence is not merely a private failing; it is a public danger. When individuals prioritise their own gratification over duty, the social fabric unravels. Wollstonecraft's remedy is education: "to prevent this abuse, we should" — and here she pauses, the conditional structure itself underscoring that prevention is possible but not guaranteed (005-013). Education that cultivates reason rather than sensibility is the only reliable antidote to the culture of indulgence.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The eighteenth-century cult of sensibility elevated feeling as the foundation of moral life, making indulgence — the gratification of tender feeling — appear virtuous. Wollstonecraft's critique is sharp: she identifies the sentimental indulgence of children and women as a form of moral negligence dressed in the language of affection. Where Rousseau's Émile recommended a form of "negative education" that deferred to nature, Wollstonecraft insisted on active rational discipline. Her position is closer to Locke's emphasis on habit formation, but with a distinctive feminist edge: indulgence is gendered in her analysis, with women simultaneously the objects of indulgence (treated as creatures of feeling rather than reason) and its agents (as mothers who spoil rather than educate).
