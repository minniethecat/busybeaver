## Definition

Wollstonecraft's treatment of "taste" is consistently critical. She classes "refinement of taste" alongside "susceptibility of heart" and "[[sensibility|delicacy]] of sentiment" as "almost synonymous with epithets of weakness" [P03-008]. Taste, as it was celebrated in eighteenth-century aesthetic discourse, is for Wollstonecraft a distraction from what matters: the cultivation of [[reason]], [[virtue]], and strength of [[character]]. The woman who has been taught to value fine taste has been taught to value the wrong thing.

This is not to say that Wollstonecraft is indifferent to aesthetic judgment. She critiques "[[refinement|false refinement]]" and the corruption of taste — "vitiate the taste" [P03-011] — not taste itself. The problem is an [[education]] system that cultivates taste at the expense of [[knowledge|understanding]], producing women who can judge a painting or a poem but cannot judge their own conduct or cultivate their own minds. "She thought a distinguishing taste and puny appetite the height of refinement," Wollstonecraft observes sarcastically of a certain type of educated woman [003-019].

## In Context

Taste appears in the Introduction as one of the qualities cultivated in women at the expense of more substantive [[virtue|virtues]] [P03-008, P03-011]. In Chapter III, Wollstonecraft elaborates on how "a distinguishing taste" becomes a substitute for genuine accomplishment [003-019], and in Chapter V she connects the cultivation of taste to the broader critique of [[sensibility]] and refinement that runs through the *Vindication*. For Wollstonecraft, fine taste without [[judgment|sound judgment]] is merely another form of artificiality.

## Key Passages

> "...the soft phrases, susceptibility of heart, delicacy of sentiment, and refinement of taste, are almost synonymous with epithets of weakness." [P03-008]

> "These pretty nothings, these caricatures of the real beauty of sensibility, dropping glibly from the tongue, vitiate the taste, and create a kind of sickly delicacy that turns away from simple unadorned truth." [P03-011]

> "She thought a distinguishing taste and puny appetite the height of refinement." [003-019]
