**Taste** occupies a deeply ambivalent position in Wollstonecraft's moral vocabulary. On the one hand, she acknowledges taste as a genuine faculty of discrimination — the capacity to judge what is beautiful, fitting, and proportionate in both art and conduct. On the other hand, she is sharply critical of the conventional cultivation of taste as a substitute for the cultivation of reason and virtue, particularly in the education of women. "Without a foundation of principles, taste is superficial; and grace must arise from something deeper than the surface" (004-061).

The ambivalence reflects Wollstonecraft's broader strategy of appropriating eighteenth-century aesthetic discourse while subjecting it to moral critique. Taste, in the tradition of Shaftesbury, Hutcheson, and Burke, was understood as a faculty that united reason and sensibility — a "moral sense" that enabled the perception of beauty and virtue alike. Wollstonecraft accepts this unification in principle but argues that, as actually cultivated in women, taste is reduced to a merely conventional accomplishment — "a distinguishing taste and puny appetite" (003-022) that substitutes for genuine moral discrimination.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's treatment of taste is shaped by the eighteenth-century discourse of aesthetic judgment. Taste, in this tradition, is the faculty by which we perceive and judge the beautiful, the sublime, and the fitting. It is not merely a matter of subjective preference but a cultivated capacity for discrimination — "the power of receiving pleasure or pain from the beauties or deformities of nature and art" (as contemporaries defined it).

But Wollstonecraft is more interested in what taste becomes when it is cultivated in isolation from reason and virtue. "Refinement of taste, are almost synonymous with epithets of weakness" (004-082) — the claim is deliberately provocative. Taste, when it is the only faculty a woman is permitted to exercise, becomes not a strength but a weakness: it makes her exquisitely sensitive to trivialities while leaving her incapable of moral judgment.

The remedy is not to abandon taste but to ground it in principle. "Without a foundation of principles, taste is superficial" (004-061). True taste — the taste that can distinguish not merely the beautiful from the ugly but the virtuous from the vicious — requires the cultivation of reason. "The intellectual world is shut against women" (004-058) — and a taste formed in ignorance of that world can never be more than a "puny appetite" (003-022).

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument about taste proceeds on two fronts simultaneously: a critique of the conventional cultivation of taste in women, and a reclamation of taste as a genuinely moral faculty when grounded in reason and principle.

The critique is detailed and unsparing. Women are taught to "cultivate their taste" — by which is meant the ability to dress fashionably, to decorate a drawing-room, to judge a novel or a poem by the standards of polite society (005-056). This is not taste in any serious sense; it is the acquisition of conventional preferences that signal social status. "They have been taught to look up to man as a being of a superior order" (004-032), and their taste is formed accordingly — it is the taste of those who exist to please, not of those who exist to judge.

The consequences are both intellectual and moral. Intellectually, the cultivation of "mere taste" — taste without knowledge — leaves women incapable of "greatness of mind" (004-058). Morally, it makes them "objects of pity" and eventually "objects of contempt" (004-082), because taste that has been trained only on the surfaces of things cannot discern the difference between appearance and reality, between the pleasing and the good.

The reclamation of taste proceeds through the connection to principle. "Grace must arise from something deeper than the surface" (004-061) — the grace that taste perceives is the outward expression of inward virtue. A person who has cultivated reason and virtue will develop a taste that is genuinely discriminating; a person who has cultivated only taste will have neither reason, nor virtue, nor genuine taste.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft's treatment of taste engages directly with the aesthetic theory of her contemporaries. Edmund Burke's *Philosophical Enquiry into the Origin of Our Ideas of the Sublime and Beautiful* (1757) had established taste as a faculty common to all mankind, grounded in the senses and refined by education. Wollstonecraft accepts the universality of taste but rejects Burke's gendering of the sublime (male) and the beautiful (female): the "beauty" that women are taught to cultivate is not the beauty of virtue but the beauty of pleasingness.

The Scottish Enlightenment tradition — particularly Lord Kames and Adam Smith — treated taste as a faculty that could be refined through commerce and polite society. Wollstonecraft shares the interest in refinement but is more critical of the social conditions under which refinement occurs: a society that confines women to the domestic sphere cannot produce genuinely refined taste, because refinement requires the full exercise of all human faculties.

Dr. Gregory's *Legacy to His Daughters* advises women to "avoid cultivating their taste, lest their husbands should occasionally find them less agreeable" (002-065). Wollstonecraft quotes this advice with contempt: the suppression of taste in the name of pleasing men is the perfect expression of everything wrong with female education.
