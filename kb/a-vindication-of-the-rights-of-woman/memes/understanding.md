# Understanding

In _[[A Vindication of the Rights of Woman]]_, **understanding** is the rational faculty that distinguishes the human mind from mere sensation and impulse. [[Mary Wollstonecraft]] argues that women's understanding has been deliberately neglected — "bubbled" by a system that prefers them ornamental to thoughtful — and that the cultivation of the understanding is the necessary foundation of both individual [[Virtue]] and social progress.

The term appears across virtually every chapter, most concentrated in Chapters 2 through 4. Wollstonecraft pairs "understanding" with "reason" as the highest human faculty, and contrasts it with "sensibility," "passion," and the "accomplishments" that conduct-book education promotes at the understanding's expense.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft treats the understanding as the active, improving faculty of mind — the capacity to grasp principles, weigh evidence, and form sound judgments. Unlike mere "sense" or instinct, understanding must be cultivated through exercise:

> "The exercise of the understanding, as life advances, is the only method pointed out by nature to calm the passions." (004-036)

This definition is significant because it positions the understanding as the governing faculty: it is not merely intellectual but moral. A cultivated understanding restrains passion, guides conduct, and makes virtue possible. By implication, to neglect the understanding is to disable moral agency.

Wollstonecraft insists that the understanding is not sexually differentiated — women possess it in equal measure to men — but it requires cultivation, which society systematically withholds: "the understanding of the sex has been so bubbled by this specious homage" (P03-001).

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

### The Systematic Neglect of Women's Understanding

Wollstonecraft's central complaint is that women's education, such as it is, trains the senses and the body while leaving the understanding entirely uncultivated. She writes of this contempt for the understanding in early life:

> "This contempt of the understanding in early life has more baneful consequences than is commonly supposed." (002-019)

The consequences are not merely intellectual but moral. A woman without a cultivated understanding becomes "the prey of her senses" (002-040), governed by passion rather than principle. She cannot distinguish genuine virtue from its appearance, nor true duty from social convention.

> "Their senses are inflamed, and their understandings neglected; consequently they become the prey of their senses." (004-033)

### Understanding as the Foundation of Character

For Wollstonecraft, stability of character — the capacity to act on principle rather than impulse — depends directly on the cultivation of the understanding:

> "By the exercise of their understandings, that stability of character is acquired." (002-006)

This argument reverses the standard assumption of her era: women were thought to lack stability of character by nature, justifying their subordination. Wollstonecraft responds that the instability is an artifact of neglected understanding, not a natural deficiency.

### The Emancipation of the Understanding

Wollstonecraft calls explicitly for the emancipation of women's understanding from the "slavery" of social expectation:

> "But was their understanding once emancipated from the slavery to which the pride and sensuality of man and their short-sighted desire... has subjected them, we should probably read of their weaknesses with surprise." (003-025)

The emancipation of the understanding is, for Wollstonecraft, the first step toward women's broader liberation. Before women can claim [[Rights]], before they can practice [[Virtue]], their understanding must be freed from the artificial constraints that have "bubbled" and confined it.

### Understanding and Marriage

Wollstonecraft connects the cultivation of understanding directly to the reform of [[Marriage]]. She argues that a wife without understanding cannot be a true companion to her husband:

> "The more understanding women acquire, the more they will be attached to their duty — comprehending it." (P02-014)

A woman who understands her duty through reason, rather than merely complying from fear or habit, is a moral agent rather than a mechanical subordinate. This distinction is central to Wollstonecraft's vision of reformed marriage as a friendship between equals.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

Understanding is the faculty through which [[Reason]] operates and upon which [[Virtue]] depends. Without a cultivated understanding, [[Education]] becomes mere ornament rather than genuine cultivation. The neglect of understanding is the mechanism by which women are denied [[Rights]] — they are rendered incapable of exercising rights they have never been taught to comprehend.

Understanding stands in direct opposition to the "sensibility" celebrated by [[Jean-Jacques Rousseau]] and the conduct-book tradition critiqued in Chapter 5. Where [[Sensibility]] reacts, understanding judges. Where sensibility feels, understanding reasons. Wollstonecraft does not reject sensibility wholesale — she seeks its proper subordination to understanding.

The cultivation of understanding is also inseparable from [[Independence]]: a dependent woman who must please to survive cannot afford to exercise her understanding freely. Economic and intellectual independence advance together.
