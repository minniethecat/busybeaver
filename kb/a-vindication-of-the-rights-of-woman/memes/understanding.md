# Understanding

## Definition

**Understanding** in Wollstonecraft's lexicon is the capacious term for the rational faculty in its broadest sense — encompassing [[reason]], [[judgment]], comprehension, and the capacity for intellectual development. When she insists that women's understanding must be cultivated, she is making a claim about the full range of mental powers, not merely the acquisition of polite accomplishments.

The cultivation of the understanding is, for Wollstonecraft, the fundamental purpose of [[education]] and the necessary precondition of [[virtue]]. A being whose understanding has not been developed cannot be said to act morally, because moral action requires comprehension of moral principles.

## In Context

### Understanding as Moral Prerequisite

Wollstonecraft's argument that the understanding must be cultivated before [[virtue]] is possible is one of her most radical claims. The conventional view held that women could be virtuous without being educated — indeed, that too much education might corrupt female virtue. Wollstonecraft reverses this: ignorance is the enemy of virtue, and only a developed understanding can produce genuine moral character.

### The Equality of Understanding

Implicit throughout the *Vindication* — and explicit in its most forceful passages — is the claim that the female understanding is equal in capacity to the male. The apparent inferiority of women's intellectual achievements is not evidence of inferior capacity but of inferior opportunity: "the understanding, strictly speaking, has not been called into action" [005-030] in women. Give women the same education as men, and their understanding will prove equally capable.
