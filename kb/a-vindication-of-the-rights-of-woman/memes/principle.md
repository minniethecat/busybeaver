**Principle** is the cornerstone of Wollstonecraft's moral epistemology. To act on principle is to be governed by reason rather than by impulse, custom, or the opinions of others. Women's degradation, in her analysis, stems fundamentally from a lack of principle: they have been educated to obey, not to reason; to please, not to judge.

The *Vindication* opens with a methodological declaration that sets principle at the centre of the entire argument: "it is necessary to go back to first principles in search of the most simple truths" (001-001). Principle is both the starting point of her inquiry and its ultimate object — the formation of women capable of governing themselves by principle rather than by the arbitrary authority of men.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft uses "principle" in several interrelated senses. The most fundamental is the epistemological sense of "first principles" (001-001) — the foundational truths from which reasoning must begin and to which it must return. These are "simple principles" (002-020) that admit of no modification.

The moral sense of principle denotes the internalised rational standard by which conduct is governed. A woman who acts on principle "forms its own principles" (001-008) through the exercise of reason rather than receiving them ready-made from authority. The absence of principle produces dependence on opinion; its presence produces independence of character.

The political sense extends principle to the organisation of society. Wollstonecraft appeals to "those glorious principles that give a substance to morality" (P02-003) and insists that women's education and rights must be "founded on the same principles" (002-032) as men's. The "true principle of patriotism" (P02-007) is not loyalty to a particular ruler but adherence to rational principles of justice.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument about principle is structured as a diagnosis and a prescription. The diagnosis: women are "not fortified by principles" (002-021); their conduct is governed by "a weak substitute for simple principles" (002-020) — namely, custom, opinion, and the desire to please. The consequences are "similar to what we feel when children are playing" (002-029) — women's lives are a form of play rather than serious moral existence.

The prescription follows from the diagnosis. Women must be educated to understand "the true principle" (P02-007) on which their duties rest. They must acquire "the salutary, sublime curb of principle" (002-079) that replaces external authority with internal governance. And they must learn that "truth, as it is a simple principle, admits of no modification" (002-080) — moral truth is not relative to sex, station, or convenience.

The theological dimension of principle is significant. Wollstonecraft invokes "the Father of spirits" (003-041) as the source of the rational principles by which the moral world is governed. When she insists that women should be educated on "the same immutable principles as those of man" (P02-014), she is claiming that the moral law is universal — that God, reason, and nature speak with one voice.

The method of "going back to first principles" (001-009) is Wollstonecraft's distinctive intellectual procedure. It is both an analytic method — stripping away custom and prejudice to reach the underlying truth — and a critical method — exposing the contradictions between professed principles and actual practice. When she observes that "vice skulks, with all its native deformity, from the rigorous search of sober principles" (001-009), she is claiming that principle itself has a reforming power.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft's emphasis on principle engages critically with the tradition of moral sense philosophy. Against those who would ground morality in sentiment or sympathy, she insists that feeling without principle is unstable and unreliable. The "severer principles of reason and philosophy" (003-011) must discipline the softer promptings of the heart.

Against Rousseau's educational theory, which she treats at length, Wollstonecraft argues that the principles on which Sophie is educated are fundamentally different from those on which Émile is educated — and that this difference is the source of women's degradation. "According to abstract principles, it was impossible to explain" (P02-009) the exclusion of women from political participation — the principles of justice, consistently applied, demand women's inclusion.

## Key Passages

> It is necessary to go back to first principles in search of the most simple truths. (001-001)

> Going back to first principles, vice skulks, with all its native deformity, from the rigorous search of sober principles. (001-009)

> A weak substitute for simple principles. (002-020)

> Let women be taught the salutary, sublime curb of principle, and let them attain conscious dignity by feeling themselves responsible only to God and to reason. (002-079)

> Truth, as it is a simple principle, admits of no modification. (002-080)
