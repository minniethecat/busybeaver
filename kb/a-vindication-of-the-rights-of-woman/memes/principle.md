# Principle

## Definition

For Wollstonecraft, a **principle** is a fixed, rationally grounded rule of
conduct that governs moral action. Principles stand in opposition to *prejudices*
(opinions adopted without examination), *custom* (habitual practices justified
only by tradition), and *sensibility* (emotional impulse). The capacity to form
and act upon principles is what distinguishes the virtuous person from one who
merely follows convention.

## Principles vs. Prejudices

The central distinction in Wollstonecraft's epistemology of morals is between
principles and prejudices:

> "The mind will ever be unstable that has only prejudices to rest on" (002-003)

Prejudices are opinions inherited from custom and authority, accepted without
rational examination. Principles, by contrast, are conclusions reached through
the independent exercise of reason. A mind governed by prejudices is at the mercy
of fashion and social pressure; a mind governed by principles possesses moral
autonomy.

## The Formation of Principles

Principles are not innate — they must be cultivated through education and the
exercise of reason. Wollstonecraft argues that women have been denied the
education necessary to form principles, leaving them dependent on the opinions
of others:

> "The mind must be strong that resolutely forms its own principles" (001-008)

This strength is acquired through the study of abstract ideas, the habit of
logical reasoning, and exposure to diverse perspectives — all of which were
systematically denied to women in the eighteenth century.

## Principle and Virtue

Genuine virtue, for Wollstonecraft, is impossible without principle. Acting
virtuously from instinct, sentiment, or social pressure is not virtue at all —
it is merely conformity. Only actions guided by rationally held principles
qualify as truly moral:

Women who have been taught only to please "have no fixed principles" and
consequently "yield to the first impulse" (005-019). Without principles, moral
conduct is unstable — dependent on mood, circumstance, and the opinions of
others rather than on internal conviction.

## Political Dimension

Wollstonecraft extends the concept of principle to political life. Just as
individuals should be governed by rational principles rather than prejudice,
so too should society be organized around principles of justice and equality
rather than inherited privilege and arbitrary power. The demand for women's
rights is, at bottom, a demand that the principles of reason be applied
consistently to all human beings regardless of sex.
