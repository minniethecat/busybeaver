# Association of Ideas

The psychological mechanism—derived from Locke and Hartley—by which Wollstonecraft explains how the content and structure of women's education systematically produce the very defects of character that contemporaries attributed to female nature, offering a causal account in which "the association of our ideas is either habitual or instantaneous" (006-002) and the former, shaped by environment, determines moral development.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft introduces the concept of the association of ideas at the opening of Chapter VI, dedicating an entire chapter to what she calls "a determinate effect an early association of ideas has on the character" (006-001). Drawing on the associationist psychology of John Locke and David Hartley, she distinguishes between two modes of mental connection: the "habitual" and the "instantaneous." The latter "seems rather to depend on the original temperature of the mind than on the will" (006-002), but the former—the habitual association—"has a great effect on the moral character of mankind; and by which a turn is given to the mind, that commonly remains throughout life" (006-003). For Wollstonecraft, this distinction is not merely academic. It is the key to understanding why women appear intellectually and morally inferior to men: their habitual associations are systematically distorted by an education that teaches them to value appearance over substance and compliance over principle.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's deployment of associationist psychology is strategically brilliant. Her contemporaries typically explained women's intellectual and moral limitations as the consequence of innate female nature—a weaker rational faculty, a more delicate constitution, or a divinely ordained domestic destiny. By invoking the association of ideas, Wollstonecraft offers a competing causal account that requires no appeal to innate difference. Women are not born intellectually inferior; they are made so by "the first associations, forced on them by every surrounding object" (006-006).

The argument proceeds through a series of linked claims. First, "the associations which depend on adventitious circumstances, during the period that the body takes to arrive at maturity, can seldom be disentangled by reason" (006-004). The plasticity of the developing mind means that early impressions are exceptionally durable—"one idea calls up another, its old associate, and memory, faithful to the first impressions, retraces them" (006-004). Second, this "habitual slavery, to first impressions, has a more baneful effect on the female than the male character" (006-005), because men's engagement with "business and other dry employments of the understanding" tends "to deaden the feelings and break associations that do violence to reason" (006-005). Women, by contrast, are "made women of when they are mere children, and brought back to childhood when they ought to leave the go-cart forever" (006-005)—their associations are never challenged, never broken, and therefore never outgrown.

The practical consequence is that women's mental life is structured by "false notions of beauty and delicacy" that "stop the growth of their limbs and produce a sickly soreness, rather than delicacy of organs" (006-006). Every object a woman encounters—"every thing that they see or hear"—"serves to fix impressions, call forth emotions, and associate ideas, that give a sexual character to the mind" (006-006). The result is not merely intellectual limitation but the thoroughgoing sexualisation of feminine consciousness.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Associationist psychology was a mainstream Enlightenment doctrine, developed by Locke in the *Essay Concerning Human Understanding* (1689) and systematised by Hartley in *Observations on Man* (1749). What distinguishes Wollstonecraft's use of the theory is her application of it to the gendered formation of character. Where Locke and Hartley were primarily concerned with epistemology—how the mind acquires knowledge through the association of simple ideas into complex ones—Wollstonecraft deploys associationism as a tool of social critique. The doctrine becomes, in her hands, an argument about how unjust social arrangements produce distorted minds. This application is original to her and marks one of her most significant contributions to Enlightenment thought.

## Key Passages

> Educated in the enervating style recommended by the writers on whom I have been animadverting; and not having a chance, from their subordinate state in society, to recover their lost ground, is it surprising that women every where appear a defect in nature? Is it surprising, when we consider what a determinate effect an early association of ideas has on the character, that they neglect their understandings? (006-001)

> The association of our ideas is either habitual or instantaneous; and the latter mode seems rather to depend on the original temperature of the mind than on the will. When the ideas, and matters of fact, are once taken in, they lie by for use, till some fortuitous circumstance makes the information dart into the mind with illustrative force. (006-002)

> So ductile is the understanding, and yet so stubborn, that the associations which depend on adventitious circumstances, during the period that the body takes to arrive at maturity, can seldom be disentangled by reason. One idea calls up another, its old associate, and memory, faithful to the first impressions, particularly when the intellectual powers are not employed to cool our sensations, retraces them. (006-004)

> This habitual slavery, to first impressions, has a more baneful effect on the female than the male character, because business and other dry employments of the understanding, tend to deaden the feelings and break associations that do violence to reason. But females, who are made women of when they are mere children, and brought back to childhood when they ought to leave the go-cart forever, have not sufficient strength of mind to efface what has been so deeply inscribed. (006-005)

> Every thing that they see or hear serves to fix impressions, call forth emotions, and associate ideas, that give a sexual character to the mind. False notions of beauty and delicacy stop the growth of their limbs and produce a sickly soreness, rather than delicacy of organs; and thus weakened by being employed in unfolding instead of examining the first associations, forced on them by every surrounding object. (006-006)
