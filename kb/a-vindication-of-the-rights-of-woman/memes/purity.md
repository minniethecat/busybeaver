# Purity

A moral and mental quality that Wollstonecraft carefully distinguishes from mere bodily chastity, locating true purity in the disciplined understanding and habitual self-governance rather than in external compliance with social codes of female conduct.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's treatment of purity is distinguished by her insistence that it is primarily a quality of mind, not of body. She explicitly seeks "to discriminate that purity of mind, which is the effect of chastity" (007-002) from the mere appearance of virtue that social convention demands. For Wollstonecraft, genuine purity arises from a cultivated understanding that governs the passions and directs conduct toward worthy ends. It is "the effect of chastity" in a deeper sense—not the chastity of repressed desire but the chastity of a mind that has not been corrupted by the "voluptuous" (007-002) preoccupations that a superficial education instills. This mental purity is inseparable from modesty, which she defines as "that soberness of mind which teaches a man not to think more highly of himself than he ought to think" (007-002)—a quality of accurate self-assessment rather than of sexual propriety alone.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft develops her concept of purity through a sustained critique of the double standard that governs eighteenth-century sexual morality. She observes that women are held to an external standard of chastity while being systematically denied the education that would cultivate genuine purity of mind. "Women only dress" (007-030) for display, she notes, because they have been taught that their value lies in attracting male admiration through their persons. This produces, not purity, but its opposite: a mind "entirely occupied by the body" (007-033) and therefore incapable of the rational self-governance that constitutes true moral purity.

Her argument culminates in the claim that the entire system of female education and morality operates on a fundamental confusion between appearance and reality. Women are "forced to be chaste to preserve their reputation" (013-040) while being given no rational basis for valuing purity for its own sake. The result is a society in which "purity of taste is vitiated" (013-075) because women have been taught to prize the appearance of virtue over its substance. Wollstonecraft's remedy is a revolution in female education that would make purity a natural expression of rational character rather than an anxious performance demanded by male opinion. She envisions a future in which "purity" returns as "the natural reflection" (013-077) of minds cultivated by knowledge and governed by principle.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The conduct-book tradition—represented by writers such as Gregory, Fordyce, and Rousseau—treated female chastity as a delicate possession to be guarded by seclusion, ignorance, and constant vigilance. Purity, in this framework, was synonymous with sexual inexperience and modest deportment. Wollstonecraft fundamentally reorients the concept: purity is not the absence of sexual knowledge but the presence of rational self-command. She does not dismiss chastity as unimportant; rather, she insists that bodily chastity without mental purity is worthless—a "specious homage" (007-033) to virtue that leaves the character fundamentally unchanged. Her position anticipates later feminist critiques of the sexual double standard while grounding its argument in the Enlightenment language of rational self-cultivation.

## Key Passages

> In speaking of the association of our ideas, I have noticed two distinct modes; and in defining modesty, it appears to me equally proper to discriminate that purity of mind, which is the effect of chastity, from a simplicity of character that leads us to form a just opinion of ourselves, equally distant from vanity or presumption. (007-002)

> I have then philosophically pursued these reflections till I inferred, that those women who have most improved their reason must have the most modesty—though a dignified sedateness of deportment may have succeeded the playful, bewitching bashfulness of youth. (007-007)

> A Christian has still nobler motives to incite her to preserve her chastity and acquire modesty, for her body has been called the Temple of the living God; of that God who requires more than modesty of body. (007-033)

> That the most salutary effects tending to improve mankind, might be expected from a REVOLUTION in female manners, appears at least, with a face of probability, to rise out of the observation. (013-075)
