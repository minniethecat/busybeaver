# Rational

The defining capacity of human beings, shared equally by both sexes, which Wollstonecraft places at the center of her argument for women's education, moral agency, and political rights.

## Definition

For Wollstonecraft, to be rational is to be capable of reasoning — of exercising judgment, understanding moral principles, and directing one's own conduct by reflection rather than impulse or external command. She insists from the opening biographical sketch that women must be regarded in their "equal rank as rational beings" (P01-013). Rationality is not a male attribute that women lack by nature; it is a universal human capacity that education must develop. She pointedly addresses her readers as "rational creatures" rather than flattering their vanity (P03-008), signaling that her argument is directed at their reason, not their sensibility. The concept anchors her claim that women should be "considered not only as moral, but rational creatures" who "ought to endeavour to acquire human virtues" by the same means as men (003-006).

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

The assertion that women are rational beings is the logical fulcrum of Wollstonecraft's entire argument. If women are rational, then denying them education is unjust; if they are not rational, it is only because they have been denied the conditions in which reason develops. She argues that "till women are more rationally educated, the progress of human virtue and improvement in knowledge must receive continual checks" (003-010). The denial of rationality to women is exposed as self-serving: men simultaneously claim women lack reason and prevent them from acquiring it. Wollstonecraft demands that women be allowed "to feel the dignity of a rational will that only bows to God" (002-078), asserting that they are "really capable of acting like rational creatures" and must not "be treated like a kind of subordinate beings" (002-079).

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Rousseau's Emile presents Sophie as a complement to the rational male, her education directed at pleasing rather than reasoning. Wollstonecraft rejects this division explicitly: women possess the same rational faculty as men and require the same cultivation. Against the common eighteenth-century view that women's reason is inferior or differently constituted, she maintains that where women appear less rational, it is the product of education — women are taught to value sensibility, sentiment, and pleasing over judgment and reflection (003-020). The few women who have "acted like rational creatures" demonstrate that the capacity exists wherever it is permitted to develop (003-020).

## Key Passages

> I shall first consider women in the grand light of rational creatures, who, in common with men, are placed on this earth to unfold their faculties (P03-008)

> till women are more rationally educated, the progress of human virtue and improvement in knowledge must receive continual checks (003-010)

> let them not be treated like a kind of subordinate beings, and the improvement of their understanding will keep pace with our own (002-079)

> considered not only as moral, but rational creatures, [women] ought to endeavour to acquire human virtues (or perfections) by the same means as men (003-006)
