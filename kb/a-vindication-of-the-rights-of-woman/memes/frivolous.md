# Frivolous

Wollstonecraft's indictment of the frivolity that society cultivates in women — a systematic reduction of female existence to "frivolous pursuits" (012-028), "frivolous accomplishments" (004-028), and "frivolous fashion" (009-023). She uses "frivolous" not as a moral condemnation of individual women but as a sociological diagnosis: women are made frivolous by a system that denies them access to serious occupation, rational education, and civic participation.

## Definition

In Wollstonecraft's usage, "frivolous" describes the condition to which women are reduced by a society that values them only for ornamental and pleasing qualities. She acknowledges that women "are still reckoned a frivolous sex" (P03-012), but insists that this is an effect, not a cause — women are not naturally frivolous but are made so by the denial of serious education and occupation. Frivolity is the symptom of a deeper structural problem: when "the limbs and faculties are cramped with worse than Chinese bands" (003-015), the mind turns to trifles because it has no scope for anything greater.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

The argument proceeds in three steps. First, Wollstonecraft identifies frivolity as the artificial product of women's social condition. "These frivolous accomplishments, supported by deportment, constitute the sum of female perfection" (004-028) — the irony is cutting: what society calls "perfection" is actually a carefully curated emptiness.

Second, she traces the mechanism: denied access to serious activity, women's energies are channeled into triviality. "What can be a more melancholy sight ... than a woman, who has been taught to look up to man as a being of a superior order, wasting her time in childish pursuits, in order to please this lord?" (009-023). The "childish pursuits" are not a preference but a rational adaptation to a system that rewards women for being decorative rather than competent.

Third, Wollstonecraft argues that frivolity is self-perpetuating across generations. Frivolous mothers raise frivolous daughters because they have no intellectual substance to transmit: "Unable to grasp any thing great, is continually restless in search of some gratification, or to invent some frivolous fashion" (009-023). The only escape from this cycle is education, which gives women "some other scheme to sharpen their faculties" (004-030).

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Conduct-book writers like Gregory and Fordyce accepted women's frivolity as natural feminine character and counseled women to manage it charmingly. Wollstonecraft's radical move is to treat frivolity as socially produced — and therefore changeable. The "more frivolous productions" (P03-002) she mentions include the very conduct books and novels that reinforced women's preoccupation with triviality. She positions her own work as the antithesis: a serious philosophical treatise addressed to women as rational beings.

## Key Passages

> "These pretty nothings, these caricatures of the real beauty of sensibility, are not allowed to be wanting in a lady's face. But I still advise the women not to let the graces be so much engrossed by the cultivation of those arts, which are the fruit of indolence and vanity, when they might acquire a stock of ideas that would enlarge their minds." (004-028)

> "What can be a more melancholy sight, than a woman, who has been taught to look up to man as a being of a superior order, wasting her time in childish pursuits, in order to please this lord? or to invent some frivolous fashion?" (009-023)

> "So far from it, that these frivolous pursuits might comprehensively be termed the science of coquetry; the whole tendency of female education tends to debase the sex." (012-028)

> "But, I presuppose, that the understanding has been strengthened by proper exercise; for a weak and vain mind, perpetually employed in frivolous pursuits, will never be able to comprehend the simple truths of morality." (012-054)

> "I wish to persuade women to endeavor to acquire strength, both of mind and body, and to convince them that the soft phrases, susceptibility of heart, delicacy of sentiment, and refinement of taste, are almost synonymous with epithets of weakness, and that those beings who are only the objects of pity, and that kind of love which has been termed its sister, will soon become objects of contempt." (004-028)
