The virtue of truthful, unaffected self-expression that Wollstonecraft identifies as foundational to genuine morality and a casualty of the artificial manners imposed on women. She contrasts sincerity with the "insincerity and falsehood" promoted by conduct books and worldly advice, arguing that the social cultivation of feminine artifice undermines the possibility of virtue itself (005-027).

## Definition

Wollstonecraft treats sincerity as a moral-epistemic virtue: it is the disposition to express one's genuine thoughts and feelings without dissimulation. She presents it as the opposite of the "polished manners" that conduct literature recommends to women, which she regards as a system of organized deception. "Sincerity will render me unaffected," she declares in her own Introduction, distinguishing her authorial method from "the bar of opinion" that silences honest speech (P03-010).

The concept has a political dimension as well as a personal one. The gallantry that structures male-female relations is for Wollstonecraft "hunting sincerity out of society" (P02-004)—a system in which both sexes perform prescribed roles rather than engaging as rational equals. Sincerity is thus not merely a personal virtue but a precondition for the rational society she envisions.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument for sincerity unfolds across several fronts. First, she identifies the social mechanisms that systematically suppress it: gallantry, which rewards flattery and punishes honest speech; conduct literature, which trains women to dissemble their intelligence and desires; and the institution of marriage, which incentivizes women to trade sincerity for security. "Sincerity is not expected," she observes, "and is too frequently out of countenance" (P02-004).

Second, she argues that without sincerity, virtue itself becomes impossible. If women are taught from childhood that their social survival depends on pleasing others rather than speaking truthfully, they cannot develop the moral integrity that virtue requires. "Virtue becomes a relative idea," she writes, "having no other foundation than the opinions of men" (003-057). The sacrifice of sincerity is thus the sacrifice of virtue itself.

Third, sincerity is central to her rhetorical strategy. By adopting the voice of a sincere, plain-speaking advocate, Wollstonecraft models the very virtue she recommends to women. She refuses to "cull my phrases, or polish my stile" (P03-010) to please a fashionable audience, preferring the "simple elegance of sincerity" (012-036) to the corruptions of artifice.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The conduct-book tradition, exemplified by writers like Dr. Gregory and Lord Chesterfield, treated social polish as a woman's primary virtue. Gregory advised his daughters to conceal their intelligence and learning; Chesterfield's letters to his son elevated manners over sincerity. Wollstonecraft systematically inverts this hierarchy: manners without sincerity are "falsehood" (005-027), and a wife who deceives her husband about her true character is a participant in a corrupt system, not a model of feminine propriety.

## Key Passages

> Addressing my sex in a firmer tone, I pay particular attention to those in the middle class, because they appear to be in the most natural state—I aim at being useful, and sincerity will render me unaffected; for wishing rather to persuade by the force of my arguments, than dazzle by the elegance of my language, I shall not waste my time to cull my phrases, or polish my stile. (P03-010)

> The man who had some virtue whilst he was struggling for a crown, often becomes a voluptuous tyrant when it graces his brow; and, when the lover is not lost in the husband, the delusion which allowed him to be the lover, becomes an idolater who worships the graven image, and hunts sincerity out of society. (P02-004)

> Virtue becomes a relative idea, having no other foundation than the opinions of men, and the sacrifice of truth and sincerity, which a blind compliance with them exacts, poisons the very source of morality. (003-057)
