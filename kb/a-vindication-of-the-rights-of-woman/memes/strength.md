## Overview

Strength is a recurring and foundational concept in _A Vindication of the Rights of Woman_. Wollstonecraft treats it as a prerequisite for virtue — arguing that without strength of both body and mind, women cannot be truly moral agents. She rejects the conventional view that female delicacy and physical weakness are desirable, insisting instead that women must "endeavour to acquire strength, both of mind and body" [P03-008].

## Physical Strength

Wollstonecraft directly challenges the assumption that men's superior bodily strength justifies female subordination. She acknowledges that "superiority with respect to bodily strength" may give men certain advantages [P03-014], but argues this makes the cultivation of women's physical strength all the more necessary. The contemporary ideal of feminine delicacy, she contends, sacrifices "strength of body and mind" to "libertine notions of beauty" [P03-012].

Her prescription is practical: girls should engage in physical activity, play outdoors, and develop bodily vigor rather than being confined to sedentary indoor pursuits. This physical strength is not an end in itself but a foundation for the more important strength of mind.

## Strength of Mind

Wollstonecraft's concept of mental strength encompasses both intellectual capacity and moral fortitude. Reason, she argues, gains "strength" through exercise and freedom [P02-012]. Women denied education are prevented from developing "sufficient strength of mind to discharge the duties of a rational creature" [001-022].

Without strength of mind, women cannot be truly virtuous — they may follow rules by rote, but cannot grasp the principles behind them. Wollstonecraft traces women's supposed intellectual inferiority not to nature but to the systematic denial of the conditions that develop mental strength: freedom to inquire, education that challenges, and the expectation of rational accountability.

## Relationship to Virtue

Strength is inseparable from Wollstonecraft's conception of virtue. A weak mind, she argues, cannot be virtuous because it cannot independently grasp moral principles. Women raised to be physically and mentally weak may appear well-behaved, but this is mere conformity — not the rational virtue Wollstonecraft champions. "Unless freedom strengthen her reason," she writes, a woman cannot truly "comprehend" virtue [P02-006].
