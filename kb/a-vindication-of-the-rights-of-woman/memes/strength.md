# Strength

Wollstonecraft places the cultivation of strength — mental and physical — at the centre of her programme for women's emancipation. Where the prevailing culture praises feminine weakness, she insists that only through the deliberate development of strength can women attain the virtue, independence, and rational character that befit them as human beings.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's conception of strength is comprehensive. She speaks repeatedly of "strength of mind" [002-001, 002-032, 002-080, 003-003] as the capacity for independent thought, moral resolution, and intellectual perseverance — qualities that the conventional education of women systematically suppresses. But she also insists on the importance of bodily strength. Her call for women "to endeavour to acquire strength, both of mind and body" [P03-008] reflects her conviction that physical and mental vigour are mutually reinforcing. A body weakened by confinement and inactivity produces a mind equally enfeebled; conversely, a body strengthened by exercise and fresh air supports the development of a robust understanding. This integrated view of strength — at once physical, intellectual, and moral — distinguishes Wollstonecraft's feminist philosophy from the more narrowly intellectualist positions of some of her successors.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft builds her case for strength through a sequence of arguments that engage with, and ultimately dismantle, the prevailing assumptions about sex difference. She begins by conceding the observable fact of male physical superiority: "I will allow that bodily strength seems to give man a natural superiority over woman" [003-006]. But she immediately qualifies this concession. Bodily strength is a distinction, not a moral warrant, and "if strength of body be, with some show of reason, the boast of men, why are women so infatuated as to be proud of a defect?" [003-007]. She inverts the customary valuation: if men are stronger, that is an argument for women to become stronger too, not to luxuriate in their relative weakness as if it were a virtue.

The cultivation of strength, for Wollstonecraft, is inseparable from the cultivation of liberty. "Liberty is the mother of virtue," she writes, and "absolute monarchies" — including the domestic monarchy of husband over wife — "destroy all strength of character" [002-083]. Women cannot develop strength of mind while they live under the arbitrary authority of men, any more than citizens can develop civic virtue under a despot. This political dimension of strength is crucial to Wollstonecraft's argument: she is not merely recommending exercise and study but calling for a transformation of the power relations that make women weak.

She also links strength to the development of genuine virtue. "Women are not allowed to have sufficient strength of mind to acquire what really deserves the name of virtue" [002-001] — the denial of strength is simultaneously a denial of moral agency. Only a mind that has been strengthened by rational education can consistently choose virtue over expediency. The woman who has been raised to be weak will, when tested, resort to cunning and manipulation rather than principled action.

Wollstonecraft offers herself as an example: "I have a soul that revolts against all the bonds of custom, and gave me sufficient strength of mind to dare to exert my own reason" [002-081]. Strength of mind here is precisely the courage to think independently — to challenge received opinion, to question authority, to form one's own judgments. This is the strength she wishes for all women.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The contrast with Rousseau is stark and deliberate. In *Emile*, Rousseau presents Sophie as physically weaker than Emile and argues that this weakness is providential: it makes woman dependent on man and thereby binds the sexes together in complementary relation. Wollstonecraft rejects this entire framework. She does not deny the fact of average physical difference, but she denies that this difference should dictate women's social role or education. Where Rousseau sees women's weakness as the foundation of their charm and their moral influence, Wollstonecraft sees it as the foundation of their degradation. Her insistence that "the woman who strengthens her body and exercises her mind will, by thus managing her constitution, become the mother of a more vigorous progeny" [002-052] turns Rousseau's argument on its head: female strength benefits society, including men, while female weakness corrupts both sexes.

## Key Passages

> "I wish to persuade women to endeavour to acquire strength, both of mind and body, and to convince them that the soft phrases, susceptibility of heart, delicacy of sentiment, and refinement of taste, are almost synonymous with epithets of weakness." [P03-008]

> "Women are not allowed to have sufficient strength of mind to acquire what really deserves the name of virtue." [002-001]

> "Liberty is the mother of virtue, and if women be, by their very constitution, slaves, and not allowed to breathe the sharp invigorating air of freedom, they must ever languish like exotics, and be reckoned beautiful flaws in nature. Absolute monarchies destroy all strength of character." [002-083]
