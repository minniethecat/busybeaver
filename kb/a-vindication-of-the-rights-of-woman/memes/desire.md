**Desire** is a central term in Wollstonecraft's moral psychology, and her treatment of it reveals the complexity of her engagement with Enlightenment philosophy of the passions. She does not condemn desire as such — desire is a natural passion, given by God and essential to human flourishing. What she condemns is the systematic distortion of desire by a social order that educates women to be "objects of desire" (P03-013) rather than subjects capable of governing their own desires through reason.

The distinction between natural and artificial desire runs throughout the *Vindication*. Natural desires — the "desire of establishing themselves" (P03-012) in virtuous independence, the "desire to improve the understanding" (005-093) — are the engines of moral and intellectual progress. Artificial desires — the "ignoble desire" of pleasing men (002-083), the "desire of conquest" (004-019), the "desire of present enjoyment" (004-086) — are the products of a corrupt education that directs women's energies toward trivial and degrading ends. "This desire of being always women," she writes with exasperation, "is the very consciousness that degrades the sex" (005-087).

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's analysis of desire draws on the eighteenth-century discourse of the passions — the rich tradition, running from Hobbes and Spinoza through Hume and Smith, that treated the passions as the primary motives of human action and asked how they could be governed, educated, and directed toward virtuous ends. She accepts the premise that desire is ineradicable and, in its proper form, good. "The passions are neither good nor evil in themselves; they are the materials out of which virtue or vice is formed" (005-131).

But she adds a distinctively gendered analysis. Women's desires are not left to develop naturally; they are "stimulated" — artificially excited and then condemned. The "desire of pleasing" (002-043) is inculcated from infancy, and then women are blamed for the vanity and coquetry that this desire produces. "Women are told from their infancy, and taught by the example of their mothers, that a little knowledge of human weakness, justly termed cunning ... will obtain for them the protection of man" (002-011). The desire for male approbation, once cultivated, becomes the master-passion that subordinates all others.

The theological dimension is significant. Wollstonecraft's God is not the ascetic's God who demands the extinction of desire but the Creator who gave desire as a good to be governed by reason. "The appetites would answer every earthly purpose, and produce more moderate and permanent happiness" (005-128) if they were left to their natural course. It is society — and particularly the social construction of gender — that perverts desire into an instrument of corruption.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument about desire operates at three levels: the philosophical, the educational, and the political.

Philosophically, she argues for the natural goodness of desire when governed by reason. "The regulation of the passions is not always wisdom. On the contrary, it should seem that one reason why men have superiour judgment and more fortitude than women is undoubtedly this, that they give a freer scope to the grand passions" (005-131). The argument is deliberately paradoxical: the suppression of desire does not produce virtue; it produces weakness. Women are taught to suppress their desires, and the result is not purity but "cunning, mean, and selfish" (005-078) behaviour.

Educationally, she argues that the remedy is not to extinguish desire but to direct it toward worthy objects. "Were the desire of pleasing to be more strongly implanted, and the desire of improving the understanding to be more encouraged, women would not be the insignificant objects of desire they are now" (005-087). The distinction between the "desire of pleasing" and the "desire of improving the understanding" is crucial: both are desires, but one degrades while the other ennobles. Education should cultivate the latter and redirect the former.

Politically, she argues that the manipulation of desire is a mechanism of social control. Women are "made to desire" what men want them to desire — admiration, protection, a wealthy husband — and then are punished for desiring these things too openly or too skilfully. "This desire of being always women, is the very consciousness that degrades the sex" (005-087). The desire to be a "woman" — in the socially constructed sense of that term — is a desire that has been implanted by a society that benefits from women's subordination.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Rousseau's treatment of desire in *Émile* provides the most direct contrast. Rousseau argues that desire is natural but must be carefully managed — in boys, by being directed toward useful objects; in girls, by being directed toward pleasing men. Wollstonecraft's response is that directing desire toward pleasing others rather than improving oneself is the definition of moral corruption.

The tradition of Christian asceticism — which treated desire, and particularly sexual desire, as a consequence of the Fall — is a background presence in Wollstonecraft's argument. She does not reject the Christian suspicion of desire outright, but she relocates the problem: it is not desire itself that corrupts but the social arrangements that distort it. "Love, considered as an animal appetite, cannot long feed on itself" (004-088) — the problem is not that desire exists but that it is denied the rational and spiritual dimensions that would make it fully human.

The emerging discourse of political economy — Mandeville's paradox that private vices produce public benefits, Smith's analysis of the desire for approbation — provides another context. Wollstonecraft shares the interest in the social function of desire but is more critical: the "desire of dazzling by riches" (001-011) that commercial society cultivates is, in her view, a corruption of both individual character and social order.
