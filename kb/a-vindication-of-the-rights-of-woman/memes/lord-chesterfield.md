# Lord Chesterfield

1694 – 1773. English statesman and man of letters whose posthumously published *Letters to His Son* (1774) Wollstonecraft cites as an example of education reduced to the cultivation of social performance rather than moral character.

## Life

Philip Stanhope, 4th Earl of Chesterfield, served as a diplomat and statesman, holding the position of Lord Lieutenant of Ireland (1745–46) and Secretary of State for the Northern Department (1746–48). His diplomatic career was marked by the same attention to social performance that characterizes his *Letters* — he was known as a master of the art of pleasing, a reputation that Wollstonecraft would later turn against him.

His *Letters to His Son*, written almost daily over thirty years to his illegitimate son Philip, were published posthumously in 1774 and became enormously influential as a guide to manners, social conduct, and worldly success. The *Letters* counsel the young man in everything from the art of conversation to the management of sexual liaisons, always with the aim of social advancement rather than moral improvement. Samuel Johnson famously described the Letters as teaching "the morals of a whore and the manners of a dancing-master" — a judgment Wollstonecraft would have endorsed.

| Chesterfield's worldly letters | Young men | Social advancement through strategic performance | *Letters to His Son* (1774) |
| Conduct books for women | Young women | Pleasing men through cultivated sensibility | Fordyce's *Sermons*, Gregory's *Legacy* |
| **Wollstonecraft's critique** | **Both sexes** | **Education for moral character, not social performance** | **_A Vindication of the Rights of Woman_** |

## Role in the Text

Wollstonecraft references Chesterfield in her discussion of how the cultivation of manners corrupts genuine virtue. She quotes his admission that he "endeavoured to gain the hearts of twenty women" through studied attention to their vanity:

> "That the plan of life which enables us to carry some knowledge into our intercourse with the world, according to the advice of Lord Chesterfield, must be in some measure pursued, I am well convinced; but I am also convinced that it requires a degree of resolution which no woman, educated as women generally are, can be expected to possess." (004-020)

The passage captures the double bind Wollstonecraft identifies. Chesterfield's method requires "resolution" — the very quality women's education systematically denies them. Men are trained to deploy strategic knowledge in social situations; women are trained merely to be pleasing objects within them. Chesterfield thus becomes an unwitting witness for Wollstonecraft's prosecution: the education he recommends for men is precisely what women are denied, and women are then blamed for lacking the qualities that education would produce.

She also notes, in discussing education, that "Lord Chesterfield's Letters must not be silently passed over" (005-116), acknowledging their cultural influence while rejecting their moral premises. His advice represents the masculine counterpart to the conduct-book tradition — training men in the arts of manipulation while conduct books train women in the arts of pleasing:

> "Lord Chesterfield's Letters … are written with such a degree of art, that they may be said to contain the quintessence of worldly wisdom; and it is necessary to observe, that they are calculated to chill the best affections of the heart, by representing the intercourse of society as a game of skill." (005-116)

Together, Chesterfield and the conduct-book writers form a closed system: men learn to deceive, women learn to be deceived, and both mistake the performance for the thing itself.

The symmetry Wollstonecraft draws between Chesterfield and the conduct-book writers — [[james-fordyce|James Fordyce]], [[john-gregory|John Gregory]], [[hester-chapone|Hester Chapone]] — is one of her most incisive structural arguments. The same society that produces "Lord Chesterfield's Letters" also produces "Fordyce's Sermons"; the gallant and the coquette are products of the same educational logic, differentiated only by the specific arts each is taught. To reform women's education without reforming men's would be to address only half the equation; both sexes must be educated for sincerity rather than performance, for moral character rather than social advantage.

## Key Ideas

Chesterfield's worldly philosophy represents, for Wollstonecraft, the corruption of genuine moral education by the cult of social performance. His advice treats human relationships as strategic interactions governed by manners rather than by moral principle — precisely the system that, when applied to women's education, produces "the minor virtues with punctilious politeness" rather than genuine virtue (002-022). Chesterfield is not merely an example of bad education; he is the logical endpoint of any education that values appearance over substance, performance over principle, pleasing over being good.

What makes Wollstonecraft's engagement with Chesterfield distinctive is her refusal to treat his *Letters* as merely a problem for men. By drawing the structural parallel between Chesterfield's advice to young men and the conduct books' advice to young women, she reveals the underlying unity of a social system that appears to treat the sexes differently but in fact subjects both to the same logic — the logic of performance, artifice, and strategic self-presentation. The solution is not a better Chesterfield or a better conduct book but the abolition of the entire genre: an education that cultivates reason and virtue rather than the arts of pleasing and manipulating.
