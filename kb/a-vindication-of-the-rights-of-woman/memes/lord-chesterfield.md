# Lord Chesterfield

## Overview

Philip Dormer Stanhope, 4th Earl of Chesterfield (1694–1773), was an English statesman and man of letters whose posthumously published *Letters to His Son* (1774) became a manual of worldly wisdom for the eighteenth-century gentleman. Wollstonecraft cites Chesterfield as an example of the kind of education that produces external polish — manners, address, the art of pleasing — without cultivating genuine moral character.

For Wollstonecraft, Chesterfield's advice represents the masculine counterpart to the conduct books addressed to women: both reduce morality to performance, virtue to reputation, and education to the art of managing others' impressions. The worldly wisdom Chesterfield imparts is, in her analysis, merely a more sophisticated form of the dissimulation that Gregory and Fordyce recommend to women.

## In the Text

Chesterfield appears in the context of Wollstonecraft's broader critique of an education that values appearance over substance. She contrasts the "Chesterfieldian" ideal of the gentleman — polished, agreeable, calculating — with her own ideal of the virtuous citizen whose character is grounded in reason rather than social performance.

## Biographical Summary

Philip Stanhope, 4th Earl of Chesterfield (1694–1773), was an English statesman, diplomat, and man of letters. He served as Lord Lieutenant of Ireland and Secretary of State. His *Letters to His Son* (1774), written to his illegitimate son Philip, became a classic of eighteenth-century courtesy literature, valued for its prose style but critiqued for its worldly cynicism.
