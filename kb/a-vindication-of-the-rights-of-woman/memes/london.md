# London

The capital of Britain and the center of the radical intellectual circle in which Wollstonecraft established herself as a professional writer.

## Location

London in the 1790s was Europe's largest city, with a population approaching one million. It was the political capital of Britain, the financial center of a growing empire, and home to a vibrant publishing industry that made it the natural center for English-language intellectual life.

## Relevance to the Text

London was where Wollstonecraft lived and worked during the composition of the Vindication. She was part of the circle around publisher Joseph Johnson, which included Thomas Paine, William Godwin, Henry Fuseli, and other leading radicals. This intellectual community provided her with books, conversation, and professional support — the conditions that made her career possible.

### In Wollstonecraft's Life

Wollstonecraft moved to London in 1787 after leaving her position as governess to the Kingsborough family in Ireland. Joseph Johnson, recognizing her talent, encouraged her writing and published her works. The London radical circle was her intellectual home, and the Vindication emerged directly from the debates and conversations of this community.

## Historical Context

London in the 1790s was convulsed by the "Revolution Controversy" — the debate over the meaning and legitimacy of the French Revolution. Burke's *Reflections* was published there in 1790, Wollstonecraft's *Vindication of the Rights of Men* followed within weeks, and Paine's *Rights of Man* appeared in 1791–92. London was the epicenter of English-language radical thought.
