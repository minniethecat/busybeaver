## Definition

The "[[rights|rights of woman]]" is the organizing concept of Wollstonecraft's entire *Vindication*. She declares her purpose directly: "Contending for the rights of women, my main argument is built on this simple principle, that if she be not prepared by [[education]] to become the companion of man, she will stop the progress of [[knowledge]] and [[virtue]]" (P02-006). The rights Wollstonecraft claims are not special privileges but the universal rights of rational beings — rights to [[education]], to moral agency, to the development of [[reason]] and [[virtue]].

Wollstonecraft's argument for women's rights is grounded in her theological anthropology: if women have souls, they must have the capacity for [[reason]] and [[virtue]]; if they have that capacity, they have a [[duty]] to develop it; and if they have a [[duty]], they must have the right to the means of fulfilling it. "Let woman share the rights," she concludes in the final chapter, "and she will emulate the [[virtue|virtues]] of man" (013-087). The rights she claims are not an end in themselves but the necessary condition for women to fulfil their moral [[duty|obligation]]s as rational creatures.

## In Context

The concept of women's rights is introduced in the Preface and developed throughout the *Vindication*. Wollstonecraft frames her argument carefully: she is not "violently to agitate the contested question respecting the [[equality|equal]]ity and inferiority of the sex" (P03-003) but rather to demonstrate that the denial of rights produces the very weakness that is then cited as justification for that denial.

In Chapter IV, Wollstonecraft connects the denial of rights to the degradation of women's [[character]]: "Pleasure is the business of a woman's life, according to the present modification of society" (004-011), because women are denied the rights that would give them access to meaningful occupation. In Chapter IX, she argues that "without rights there cannot be any incumbent [[duty|duties]]" (009-017) — rights and [[duty|duties]] are reciprocal, and the denial of rights excuses women from the [[duty|duties]] that would cultivate [[virtue]].

The argument culminates in Chapter XIII: "Let woman share the rights, and she will emulate the [[virtue|virtues]] of man; for she must grow more perfect when emancipated" (013-087). [[rights|Rights]] are the mechanism of moral progress.

### The Theological Foundation

Wollstonecraft's argument for women's rights rests on a theological premise that her contemporaries could not easily dismiss. If women have immortal souls, they must possess the faculties — [[reason]] and moral agency — that distinguish humans from animals. If they possess those faculties, they have a divine mandate to develop them. And if they have that mandate, society has no authority to obstruct it. This chain of reasoning — souls → reason → duty → rights — allows Wollstonecraft to claim the high ground of religious orthodoxy while advancing a radically egalitarian argument. "Let woman share the rights," she writes, "and she will emulate the virtues of man; for she must grow more perfect when emancipated, or justify the authority that chains such a weak being to her duty" (013-087).

### Rights and Duties: The Reciprocal Pair

One of Wollstonecraft's most philosophically sophisticated moves is her insistence that rights and [[duty|duties]] are reciprocal. "Without rights there cannot be any incumbent duties" (009-017). This is not the claim that the powerless have no moral obligations — Wollstonecraft would reject that as moral nihilism — but rather that the social denial of rights undermines the moral framework that makes duties binding. If women are denied the right to [[education]], they cannot be held fully accountable for the ignorance that results. If they are denied the right to [[independence]], they cannot be blamed for the servility that dependence produces. Duties presuppose rights, because duties presuppose the capacity for free moral choice, and that capacity requires the social conditions — education, economic independence, legal standing — that only rights can secure.

- [[virtue]] — rights are necessary for the cultivation of [[virtue]]; without rights, there can be no genuine moral agency

## The Rights Claim in Full Voice

> "Consider, I address you as a legislator, whether, when men contend for their freedom, and to be allowed to judge for themselves, respecting their own happiness, it be not inconsistent and unjust to subjugate women, even though you firmly believe that you are acting in the manner best calculated to promote their happiness? Who made man the exclusive judge, if woman partake with him the gift of reason?" (P02-009)

> "But, if women are to be excluded, without having a voice, from a participation of the natural rights of mankind, prove first, to ward off the charge of injustice and inconsistency, that they want reason, else this flaw in your *new constitution*, the first constitution founded on reason, will ever show that man must, in some shape, act like a tyrant, and tyranny, in whatever part of society it rears its brazen front, will ever undermine morality." (P02-011)

> "I wish, sir, to set some investigations of this kind afloat in France; and should they lead to a confirmation of my principles, when your constitution is revised, the rights of woman may be respected, if it be fully proved that reason calls for this respect, and loudly demands *justice* for one half of the human race." (P02-018)
