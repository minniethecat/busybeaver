Throughout the *Vindication*, Wollstonecraft critically deploys the eighteenth-century concept of **station** — one's assigned place in the social hierarchy — to expose the tension between social convention and moral worth. She consistently insists that the duties of one's station, properly understood, require the exercise of reason and virtue, not merely passive conformity to inherited expectations. In her analysis, station is a social fact that must be moralised through the cultivation of character, not a natural order to be accepted without question.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft uses "station" in two distinct but related senses. In its descriptive sense, station refers to the social position an individual occupies — "an inferior station" (004-027), "a useful station" (002-027), or "a subordinate, yet respectable station in life" (P03-014). In its normative sense, station implies the duties and obligations appropriate to one's position — the responsibilities that must be discharged if one is to merit the respect that station claims.

Crucially, Wollstonecraft does not accept station as fixed by nature. She observes that individuals are "thrown out of a useful station by the unnatural distinctions of civil society" (002-027), identifying station as a product of convention rather than inherent desert. This allows her to critique both the unjust distribution of stations — women denied access to the stations that would exercise their faculties — and the failure of those in elevated stations to discharge their attendant duties worthily.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument about station operates on two fronts. First, she challenges the equation of social station with moral worth. She notes that there are those "filling an important station, who are not respectable" (003-045), and that a person's character is not determined by the station they occupy but by how they discharge its duties. This decoupling of station from desert is central to her broader critique of hereditary privilege and the arbitrary distribution of social goods.

Second, she argues that the duties of any station require the full exercise of reason and virtue. Whether one occupies a high station or a low one, "discharging the duties of her station" (009-007) demands moral capacities that can only be cultivated through education. The implication is radical: if women are assigned to domestic stations, they require the same rational cultivation as men assigned to public stations, because the duties of any station — properly understood — demand the exercise of virtue.

The political dimension is explicit. Wollstonecraft observes that men "in one station, at least, have an opportunity of exerting themselves with dignity" (004-025), while women are denied any station that provides such an arena. Her demand is not for women to abandon their stations but for society to recognise that all stations require the cultivation of reason — and that women, like men, must therefore be educated accordingly.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Where conservative moralists treated station as a divinely ordained hierarchy to be accepted with gratitude, Wollstonecraft treated it as a social arrangement to be judged by the standard of reason. She does not call for the abolition of social distinctions — her radicalism is more measured than that — but she insists that station must be moralised: those who occupy stations must be educated to discharge their duties virtuously, and the assignment of stations must not permanently foreclose the development of human capacities. This distinguishes her both from radical levellers and from defenders of the status quo.

## Key Passages

> There are many who, though filling an important station, are not respectable; she must follow the maxims they inculcate — or be considered eccentric. (003-045)

> Men have thus, in one station, at least, an opportunity of exerting themselves with dignity, and of rising by the exertions which really improve a rational creature. (004-025)

> The man who discharges the duties of his station with propriety is respected by all who judge with candour. (009-006)
