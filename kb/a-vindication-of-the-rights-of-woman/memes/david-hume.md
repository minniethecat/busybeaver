# David Hume

[[David Hume]] (1711–1776), the Scottish philosopher, historian, and essayist, was a central figure of the [[Enlightenment]] whose empiricism and scepticism profoundly shaped eighteenth-century thought. In _[[A Vindication of the Rights of Woman]]_, Wollstonecraft invokes him not as a philosophical authority but as an observer whose description of women's social condition inadvertently exposes its absurdity.

## Life

Hume was born in Edinburgh in 1711 and educated at the University of Edinburgh. His major philosophical works — _A Treatise of Human Nature_ (1739–40), _An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding_ (1748), and _An Enquiry Concerning the Principles of Morals_ (1751) — established him as the foremost British philosopher of his generation. His _History of England_ (1754–62) made him one of the most widely read historians of the century, and his essays on political and moral subjects were standard references for writers across Europe, including Wollstonecraft.

Hume's moral philosophy, grounded in the concept of sympathy as the foundation of ethical judgment, deeply influenced his friend and fellow Scot [[adam-smith|Adam Smith]], whose _Theory of Moral Sentiments_ Wollstonecraft engages more directly. Hume's scepticism about the scope of reason — his famous dictum that "reason is, and ought only to be the slave of the passions" — placed him in tension with the rationalist feminism Wollstonecraft advances, though she does not engage this dimension of his thought directly.

## Role in the Text

Wollstonecraft's single extended reference to Hume appears in Chapter 4, in the midst of her argument against the "arbitrary insolent respect" that men pay to women — a gallantry that flatters while it subordinates:

> "Often do they repeat Mr. Hume's sentiments; when comparing the French and Athenian character, he alludes to women. 'But what is more singular in this whimsical nation, say I to the Athenians, is, that a frolic of yours during the Saturnalia, when the slaves are served by their masters, is seriously continued by them through the whole year … The women, though without virtue, are their masters and sovereigns.'" (004-014)

The quotation, drawn from one of Hume's essays, draws a parallel between the festive inversion of the Roman Saturnalia — when masters served their slaves for a day — and the permanent inversion produced by gallantry, where "those whom nature has subjected to them" are exalted as "masters and sovereigns." Wollstonecraft's deployment is characteristically double-edged: Hume supplies the observation, but the critical force is hers. She immediately addresses women directly, asking why they "condescend to receive a degree of attention and respect from strangers" that is merely a "hollow respect," a gilded cage.

The Saturnalia comparison is especially pointed. In the Roman festival, the inversion was temporary — a licensed release that, by its very boundedness, reinforced the normal order. Gallantry, by contrast, makes the inversion permanent, creating a social order in which women reign without ruling, sovereigns without sovereignty. This is not liberation but a more insidious form of captivity: the cage gilded so attractively that its occupant forgets it is a cage.

Wollstonecraft extends this logic by asking women to examine the basis of their "sovereignty":

> "Why do they condescend to receive a degree of attention and respect from strangers, different from that reciprocation of civility which the dictates of humanity, and the politeness of civilization, authorise between man and man?" (004-015)

The contrast between "reciprocation of civility" — the mutual respect that governs relations between equals — and the "degree of attention" women receive from gallant strangers exposes the asymmetry at the heart of gallantry. What presents itself as deference is in fact a refusal of equality: the gallant man does not honour the woman as an equal but as a superior of a different kind, a sovereign whose rule is ceremonial rather than real.

## Key Ideas

Hume's analysis of female social power as a Saturnalian inversion — power without virtue, sovereignty without merit — serves Wollstonecraft's argument that what passes for female privilege is in fact a sophisticated form of subjection. The "queens" of the drawing-room are, in her extension of Hume's logic, "confined … in cages, like the feathered race," their apparent elevation concealing their actual captivity.

Wollstonecraft's use of Hume reveals her skill at enlisting unlikely allies. Hume was no feminist; his own essays on love and marriage treat women with the conventional gallantry of his age. Yet his empirical observation — that the condition of French and Athenian women resembles a Saturnalian inversion — supplies Wollstonecraft with precisely the critical leverage she needs. She does not need Hume to be a feminist; she needs only his disinterested observation, which, once detached from the gallant frame in which he presented it, becomes devastating evidence against the system he himself participated in.
