# David Hume

## Overview

David Hume (1711–1776) was a Scottish Enlightenment philosopher, historian, and essayist whose empiricist philosophy profoundly shaped eighteenth-century thought. In *A Vindication of the Rights of Woman*, Wollstonecraft quotes Hume's observation on the peculiar relation between men and women in French society, drawn from his essay comparing French and Athenian character. She uses Hume's own words to expose the contradiction at the heart of the gallantry system: men who pay extravagant homage to women are, in truth, reinforcing their own superiority by treating women as perpetual inferiors elevated only through male indulgence.

## In the Text

Hume appears in a single extended passage in ch04 ([004-011]), where Wollstonecraft uses his comparison of French and Athenian customs to illuminate the false position of women. She introduces the quotation with the observation that men "often repeat Mr. Hume's sentiments" when comparing national character — and then lets Hume's own words do the rhetorical work. Hume describes with satirical wonder how the French "gravely exalt those whom nature has subjected to them, and whose inferiority and infirmities are absolutely incurable. The women, though without virtue, are their masters and sovereigns."

Wollstonecraft's strategy in citing Hume is characteristically subversive. She does not disagree with his observation; rather, she draws out its implication — that the system of gallantry Hume describes is fundamentally unjust. The very men who profess admiration for women, she argues in the paragraphs that follow, are those "most inclined to tyrannize over, and despise the very weakness they cherish." Hume's neutral, even amused tone becomes, in Wollstonecraft's hands, an indictment.

The passage also serves as an entry point into Wollstonecraft's broader argument in ch04 about the structural analogy between women and the aristocracy. Just as courtiers flatter a king while despising his weakness, men flatter women while denying them the rational equality that would make genuine respect possible.

## Key Quotations

> "Often do they repeat Mr. Hume's sentiments; when comparing the French and Athenian character, he alludes to women. 'But what is more singular in this whimsical nation, say I to the Athenians, is, that a frolic of yours during the Saturnalia, when the slaves are served by their masters, is seriously continued by them through the whole year, and through the whole course of their lives... The women, though without virtue, are their masters and sovereigns.'" [004-011]

> "And why do they not discover, when 'in the noon of beauty's power,' that they are treated like queens only to be deluded by hollow respect, till they are led to resign, or not assume, their natural prerogatives?" [004-012]

## Biographical Summary

David Hume was born in Edinburgh and became one of the most influential philosophers of the Scottish Enlightenment. His *Treatise of Human Nature* (1739–40) and *Enquiries* developed a comprehensive empiricist philosophy. His six-volume *History of England* (1754–1762) was the standard work on the subject for generations. By 1792, Hume had been dead for sixteen years, but his essays on politics and national character remained widely read. Wollstonecraft draws on his essay "Of National Characters" or a similar comparative essay for the French/Athenian comparison.
