**Sympathy** is, for Wollstonecraft, the natural moral capacity that makes social virtue possible — the "fellow-feeling with all the emotions of our own breast" (005-042) that enables human beings to understand one another, to care about one another's welfare, and to act on that care. Yet sympathy, like every natural capacity, can be corrupted by a defective education. The *Vindication* traces both the promise of sympathy — as the foundation of marriage, parenthood, and social life — and its perversion into the enervating "sensibility" that renders women manipulable and morally unreliable.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft inherits the concept of sympathy from the eighteenth-century tradition of moral sense philosophy — particularly Adam Smith's *Theory of Moral Sentiments*, which she cites approvingly. In this tradition, sympathy is the capacity to enter into the feelings of another, to experience a version of their emotional state, and to be moved by it to action. It is the mechanism by which self-interest is transcended and moral community becomes possible.

But Wollstonecraft adds a crucial distinction that the moral sense tradition had largely neglected: the distinction between sympathy governed by reason and sympathy cultivated in isolation from it. The former is the foundation of genuine virtue — "the child of exercised sympathy and reason" (011-014). The latter is what the eighteenth century called "sensibility" — a heightened emotional responsiveness that substitutes for moral judgment rather than informing it.

The vocabulary is precise. "Fellow-feeling" (005-042) names the natural capacity for sympathetic response. "Mutual sympathy" (010-011) describes the reciprocity that characterizes genuine moral relations — between husband and wife, parent and child, citizen and citizen. "Sensibility" — when used critically, as it most often is — names the corrupted form: sympathy that has been cultivated as an end in itself, detached from the understanding that would give it moral direction.

The key test is whether sympathy leads to action or exhausts itself in feeling. Wollstonecraft's contempt is reserved for those who feel deeply and do nothing — who experience the pleasures of sympathetic identification without accepting the obligations it entails. Sympathy without action is not virtue but self-indulgence.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

The argument about sympathy is woven through Wollstonecraft's treatment of marriage, parenthood, and social relations. In each domain, she identifies a natural sympathy that has been distorted by artificial social arrangements, and she proposes reforms that would restore sympathy to its proper function.

In marriage, the ideal is a union founded on "mutual sympathy" — the partners understanding and responding to one another's minds. The reality, Wollstonecraft argues, is typically a marriage in which the wife's sympathy is demanded as a service while the husband's is never cultivated. "The man who can be contented to live with a pretty useful companion without a mind, has lost in voluptuous gratifications a taste for more refined enjoyments; he has never felt the calm satisfaction ... of being beloved by one who could understand him" (005-042). The wife who lacks understanding cannot truly sympathize, because sympathy requires the capacity to enter into another's mental state — and a mind that has not been cultivated cannot enter into a cultivated mind.

The gendered critique is sharp. "The power of women," some author had declared, "is her sensibility" (004-050). Wollstonecraft's response is to expose the hollowness of this supposed power. If women's only claim to influence is their emotional responsiveness, then they are dependent on the feelings of others for their social existence — they are not agents but mirrors. "Those who constantly employ their sensibility will have most," she notes drily, citing the example of poets and painters whose professional cultivation of feeling makes them professionally unfit for anything else.

In parenthood, sympathy finds its most natural expression. "A mutual care produces a new mutual sympathy" (010-011) between parent and child — a bond that Wollstonecraft presents as a "natural substitute for love, when the lover becomes only a friend." The parent-child relation is, in its ideal form, the model of how natural sympathy can be the foundation of lasting moral relations. But even this natural sympathy can be corrupted: "they who do their duty by proxy should not murmur if they miss the reward of duty — parental affection produces filial duty" (010-011). Sympathy, like every moral capacity, must be exercised to be maintained.

The political dimension of sympathy emerges in Wollstonecraft's critique of the existing social order. The "common sympathy" (008-027) that should unite the sexes in mutual respect has been degraded into "the depravity of the appetite" — a merely physical attraction that "brings the sexes together" without engaging the moral faculties that would make their union virtuous. The restoration of sympathy to its proper function requires, in Wollstonecraft's account, nothing less than a revolution in the relations between the sexes — the replacement of the marriage of convenience with the marriage of "congenial souls."

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The cult of sensibility — the eighteenth-century celebration of refined feeling as the mark of a superior nature — is Wollstonecraft's principal target in her treatment of sympathy. The sensibility tradition, from the novels of Richardson to the philosophy of Rousseau, had elevated "fine feelings" to the status of a moral achievement. Wollstonecraft's response is to diagnose this elevation as a symptom of the very corruption it claims to transcend.

Rousseau is again the central antagonist. His celebration of natural pity in the *Discourse on Inequality* might seem to align with Wollstonecraft's treatment of sympathy, but she recognizes a fundamental divergence. Rousseau's pity is pre-rational — it operates before reason and is, in his account, corrupted by the development of rational capacities. Wollstonecraft's sympathy, by contrast, requires reason for its proper functioning. The "child of exercised sympathy and reason" (011-014) is not a primitive impulse but a cultivated capacity — the product of education, reflection, and moral discipline.

The conduct-book tradition — Fordyce, Gregory — had made a virtue of women's sympathetic nature, instructing them to cultivate the emotional responsiveness that would make them pleasing companions. Wollstonecraft's reply is that this "sympathy" is not a virtue but a servitude. Women who are taught only to feel what others feel are denied the moral autonomy that is the precondition of genuine virtue. They become, in her phrase, the "timid and ferocious slaves of feeling" — creatures of reflected emotion rather than sources of moral action.

## Key Passages

> "The charm of life," says a grave philosophical reasoner, is "sympathy; nothing pleases us more than to observe in other men a fellow-feeling with all the emotions of our own breast." (005-042)

> A mutual care produces a new mutual sympathy. But a child, though a pledge of affection, will not enliven it, if both father and mother are content to transfer the charge to hirelings; for they who do their duty by proxy should not murmur if they miss the reward of duty. (010-011)

> "The power of women," says some author, "is her sensibility;" and men not aware of the consequence, do all they can to make this power swallow up every other. (004-050)

> Nature, by making the gratification of an appetite, in this respect, as well as every other, a natural and imperious law to preserve the species, exalts the appetite, and mixes a little mind and affection with a sensual gust. The feelings of a parent mingling with an instinct merely animal, give it dignity. (008-027)
