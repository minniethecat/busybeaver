Licentiousness, in Wollstonecraft's vocabulary, names the endpoint of moral degradation: the condition in which desire has entirely displaced principle. She applies the term both to the corruption of women's character — "their character is degraded, and licentiousness spread through the whole aggregate" (003-010) — and to the intellectual productions that rationalise such corruption, denouncing "the reveries of fancy and refined licentiousness" (005-041) that pass for moral philosophy.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's use of "licentiousness" carries both descriptive and diagnostic force. Descriptively, it names a social condition in which sexual appetite governs conduct without the restraint of reason or virtue. Diagnostically, it identifies the cause of this condition: the systematic denial of rationality to women, which makes them incapable of the self-governance that alone can resist licentiousness.

The term's force derives from its contrast with her positive ideal. Wollstonecraft envisions a society in which relations between the sexes are governed by rational friendship and mutual respect. Licentiousness is the antithesis of this vision: it reduces women to objects of appetite and men to slaves of desire. When she writes that "their character is degraded" (003-010), the degradation is not merely of individual women but of the "whole aggregate" — licentiousness is a systemic, not merely personal, corruption.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument against licentiousness is framed as a rebuttal to the conduct-book tradition. The writers who claim to defend female virtue — who speak of delicacy, modesty, and propriety — are, in her analysis, complicit in promoting the very licentiousness they purport to oppose. By addressing women as sexual beings rather than rational beings, they embed licentiousness in the structure of social relations. Her condemnation of "refined licentiousness" (005-041) is particularly sharp because "refined" here means intellectualised, given a veneer of philosophical sophistication: it is licentiousness dressed in the language of sensibility and taste.

The political stakes are high. Licentiousness is not merely a private failing but a threat to the republican virtues Wollstonecraft seeks to cultivate. A licentious people cannot be a free people, because licentiousness — the rule of appetite over reason — reproduces in the individual soul the same structure of arbitrary power that despotism imposes on the body politic. Her critique thus links the corruption of women's character to the corruption of the state: both result from the failure to ground social arrangements in rational principle.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The eighteenth-century discourse on licentiousness was typically directed at men — the "libertine" who indulged his appetites at the expense of female virtue. Wollstonecraft's innovation is to redirect the critique toward the social structures that produce licentiousness in both sexes: women become licentious because they are taught to value only their sexual function; men become licentious because they are taught that women exist for their gratification. Her analysis is structural rather than merely moralistic, anticipating later feminist critiques of how gender relations are systematically reproduced through education and social convention.
