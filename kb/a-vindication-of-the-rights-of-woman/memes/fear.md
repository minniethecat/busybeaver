**Fear** is, in Wollstonecraft's analysis, one of the primary instruments by which women are governed — and one of the principal obstacles to their moral development. Throughout the *Vindication*, she argues that women are "governed by fear to exercise their natural cunning" (002-031), that "fear in girls, instead of being cherished, ought to be combated" (004-043), and that a morality founded on fear rather than reason is no morality at all. "Such slaves are we to hope and fear!" (005-135) — the exclamation is not merely rhetorical; it captures the psychological mechanism by which dependence corrupts the capacity for moral autonomy.

Wollstonecraft's treatment of fear draws on multiple intellectual traditions. From the Enlightenment critique of religious superstition — the "fear of the devil" (005-005) that Hobbes and Spinoza had taught Europeans to diagnose — she borrows the argument that fear-based morality is essentially childish and incompatible with rational adulthood. From the republican tradition of Machiavelli and Montesquieu, she borrows the argument that fear is the passion of subjects, not citizens — the emotion that tyrants cultivate in those they rule. And from her own experience as a governess and educator, she draws the observation that girls are systematically taught to be fearful, and that this fearfulness is then mistaken for a natural feminine trait.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft distinguishes fear as a natural passion from fear as a social instrument. Fear as a natural passion is a response to genuine danger — the "fears, when not affected" (004-042) that any rational being experiences in the presence of a threat. Fear as a social instrument is something else entirely: it is the cultivated timidity that makes women dependent on male protection, the "fear of departing from a supposed sexual character" (004-006) that enforces conformity, and the "fear of the world" (005-022) that substitutes for genuine moral restraint.

The distinction matters because the two kinds of fear have opposite effects on moral development. Natural fear, like any passion, can be governed by reason and integrated into a virtuous character. But social fear — fear cultivated as a mechanism of control — corrupts the very capacity for moral judgment. "The being who patiently endures injustice, and silently bears insults, will soon become unjust, or unable to discern right from wrong" (005-020). A woman who has been taught to fear male disapproval more than she fears moral wrong has been deprived of the foundation of virtue.

The theological dimension is present but subdued. Wollstonecraft distances herself from the tradition of "fear of God" as the beginning of wisdom — not because she rejects it, but because she insists that fear of God must take the form of rational reverence, not superstitious terror. "Having no fear of the devil before mine eyes," she writes with deliberate irreverence, "I venture to call this a mere sexual feature" (005-005).

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument about fear is inseparable from her critique of female education and the social construction of femininity. She identifies three distinct mechanisms by which fear is cultivated in women, and three corresponding harms that result.

The first mechanism is the cultivation of physical timidity. Girls are taught to fear everything — "fear in girls, instead of being cherished" (004-043) should be discouraged — and this physical timidity is then generalised into intellectual and moral timidity. A woman who has been taught to fear a spider will be taught to fear an opinion; the habit of fear, once established, extends to every domain of life.

The second mechanism is the cultivation of reputational fear. Women's conduct is governed not by principle but by "the fear of the world, or of hell" (005-022) — by what others will think or by what punishment may follow. This is not morality but prudence: the calculation of consequences that leaves the character untouched. A woman who refrains from adultery only because she fears disgrace is not virtuous; she is merely cautious.

The third mechanism is the cultivation of fear of male authority. "She should be governed by fear to exercise her natural cunning" (002-031) — Wollstonecraft quotes this view only to reject it. The woman who is governed by fear of her husband or father learns not virtue but deception; she becomes "cunning, mean, and selfish" (005-078), precisely the vices that the fear-based system claims to prevent.

The remedy, as always in Wollstonecraft, is education that cultivates reason. "If fear in girls, instead of being cherished, was not once instilled, we should soon see women with more dignified characters" (004-043). The dignified character is the character that governs itself by reason rather than being governed by fear.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Rousseau's treatment of fear in *Émile* provides Wollstonecraft with both a model and a target. Rousseau argues that fear is a natural passion that education should manage rather than extinguish. Wollstonecraft agrees that fear is natural but insists that the fear cultivated in women is not natural at all — it is "affected" (004-042), produced by an education designed to make women dependent.

The tradition of Christian moral theology — particularly the emphasis on "fear of the Lord" as the beginning of wisdom — is one that Wollstonecraft handles carefully. She does not reject the fear of God outright, but she resists its reduction to superstitious terror. The God she invokes is "the fountain of wisdom, goodness, and power" (003-036), not the tyrant who rules through fear.

The political dimension is equally important. Fear is the passion of subjects under despotism; hope and fear together are the mechanisms by which absolute rulers control their populations. "Such slaves are we to hope and fear!" (005-135) — the political analogy is explicit. Women are governed as despots govern their subjects: through the manipulation of hope and fear rather than through the appeal to reason and justice.
