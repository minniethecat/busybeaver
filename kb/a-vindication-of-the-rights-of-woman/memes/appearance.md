# Appearance

The socially constructed priority given to outward display over inward character, which Wollstonecraft identifies as a fundamental corruption of moral judgment — particularly damaging to women, who are systematically trained to value how they appear over what they are.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's concept of appearance encompasses the entire apparatus of social display that substitutes surface for substance in the evaluation of human beings. She uses the term in multiple registers: the "appearance of weakness" that education gives to females (002-021); the "appearance of seriousness" that virtue should carry (004-092); and "the making an appearance in the world" that becomes "the first wish of the majority of mankind" (004-096).

The unifying thread is the distinction between being and seeming. In a properly ordered society, appearance would follow from character — virtue would naturally display itself with gravity, understanding would manifest as grace. In the society Wollstonecraft critiques, the relationship is inverted: the management of appearance becomes an end in itself, consuming the energy that should be devoted to the cultivation of character.

This inversion bears most heavily on women, whose social existence is defined almost entirely by the imperative to appear pleasing. "Men look for beauty and the simper of good humoured docility" (006-012), and women, trained to supply what men demand, become expert in the management of surfaces at the expense of substance.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's treatment of appearance operates as a sustained critique of a society that has lost the ability to distinguish the real from the performed. She begins with the observation that education itself gives women "this appearance of weakness" (002-021), and she draws a pointed analogy with military men, who "are, like them, sent into the world before their minds have been stored with knowledge or fortified by principles" (002-021). Both soldiers and women acquire a superficial polish — "what is termed a knowledge of the world" — that passes for competence while masking intellectual emptiness.

The social priority of appearance corrupts the most intimate human relations. Women marry "merely to BETTER THEMSELVES" (004-096), selecting partners based on the appearance of prosperity rather than genuine compatibility. The result is a union of surfaces: two people who have learned to perform their respective roles without developing the capacities that would make companionship meaningful.

Wollstonecraft is particularly acute on the moral psychology of appearance-management. When women's social survival depends on maintaining appearances, they develop what she calls "the convenient cloak of marriage" (008-005) — a system of strategic dissimulation that corrodes integrity from within. The married woman who preserves appearances while conducting intrigues is, in Wollstonecraft's judgment, more degraded than the "innocent girl" who "become a prey to love" (008-005), because the former has made hypocrisy a way of life.

Ultimately, Wollstonecraft traces the tyranny of appearance to a failure of reason. "Their reason will never acquire sufficient strength to enable it to regulate their conduct, whilst the making an appearance in the world is the first wish of the majority of mankind" (004-096). The cultivation of reason and the management of appearance are zero-sum competitors for human attention; a society that prioritises the latter will inevitably fail at the former.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The eighteenth-century cult of sensibility elevated appearance to a moral category: the capacity to display refined feeling was taken as evidence of virtuous character. Wollstonecraft inverts this equation, arguing that the performance of sensibility is precisely what "spread affectation, and take from the dignity of virtue" (004-092). Where her contemporaries saw polish as proof of cultivation, she saw it as evidence of superficiality. True virtue, she insists, "should have an appearance of seriousness, if not austerity" (004-092) — not because virtue is grim, but because depth of character manifests differently from theatrical display.

## Key Passages

> As a proof that education gives this appearance of weakness to females, we may instance the example of military men, who are, like them, sent into the world before their minds have been stored with knowledge or fortified by principles. The consequences are similar; soldiers acquire a little superficial knowledge, snatched from the muddy current of conversation. (002-021)

> Virtue, as the very word imports, should have an appearance of seriousness, if not austerity; and to endeavour to trick her out in the garb of pleasure, because the epithet has been used as another name for beauty, was to say the least a very needless expedient. (004-092)

> Their reason will never acquire sufficient strength to enable it to regulate their conduct, whilst the making an appearance in the world is the first wish of the majority of mankind. To this weak wish the natural affections and the most useful virtues are sacrificed. (004-096)

> Men look for beauty and the simper of good humoured docility: women are captivated by easy manners: a gentleman-like man seldom fails to please them, and their thirsty ears eagerly drink the insinuating nothings of politeness, whilst they turn from the unintelligible sounds of the charmer — reason, charm he never so wisely. (006-012)

> Low, indeed, in the intellectual scale, is the mind that can continue to love when neither virtue nor sense give a human appearance to an animal appetite. (012-082)
