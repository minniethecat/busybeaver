# Adam Smith

[[Adam Smith]] (1723–1790), the Scottish moral philosopher and political economist, appears in _[[A Vindication of the Rights of Woman]]_ not as a target of critique but as a supplier of conceptual tools. Wollstonecraft draws on his _Theory of Moral Sentiments_ (1759) to construct one of her most original arguments: that the character of "people of rank and fortune" described by Smith is, in every essential respect, the same character artificially imposed upon women.

## Life

Smith was born in Kirkcaldy, Scotland in 1723 and educated at Glasgow and Oxford. As Professor of Moral Philosophy at Glasgow, he published _The Theory of Moral Sentiments_ (1759), a work that established his reputation before the more famous _Wealth of Nations_ (1776). His moral philosophy centres on the concept of sympathy — the imaginative capacity to enter into the feelings of others — as the foundation of ethical judgment. Smith died in Edinburgh in 1790, just two years before Wollstonecraft published the _Vindication_.

## Role in the Text

### The Character of the Rich, Applied to Women

In Chapter 4, Wollstonecraft makes a strategic move that would have startled Smith's readers. Quoting a passage from the _Theory of Moral Sentiments_ that describes the advantages sought by the wealthy, she declares:

> "In Dr. Smith's Theory of Moral Sentiments, I have found a general character of people of rank and fortune, that in my opinion, might with the greatest propriety be applied to the female sex." (004-026)

She then quotes Smith's analysis: "'To be observed, to be attended to, to be taken notice of with sympathy, complacency, and approbation, are all the advantages which they seek'" (004-026). The observation — originally about rich men — maps with precision onto the socially prescribed ambitions of women. Both groups, Wollstonecraft argues, are "localized" by their position: the rich man by rank, the woman by courtesy.

The implication is radical. If Smith's analysis of how "local situation swallowed up the man" applies to women, then what is presented as natural feminine character is in fact an artefact of social position — no more innate to women than the vices of the rich are innate to aristocrats.

### Moral Sentiments and Reputation

In Chapter 8, wrestling with the relationship between genuine virtue and public reputation, Wollstonecraft twice invokes Smith's authority. First, she quotes his account of moral luck at length:

> "Speaking of the general laws of morality, Dr. Smith observes — 'That by some very extraordinary and unlucky circumstance, a good man may come to be suspected of a crime of which he was altogether incapable … Accidents of the first kind, however, are perhaps still more rare … and it still remains true, that the practice of truth, justice and humanity, is a certain and almost infallible method of acquiring what those virtues chiefly aim at, the confidence and love of those we live with.'" (008-013)

Smith's confidence in the general reliability of moral reputation supports Wollstonecraft's argument that a person's true character will "still work its way to light" despite the distortions of "weak affection, or ingenious malice" (008-017).

However, she does not fully endorse Smith's bystander-based theory of moral judgment. After citing Smith's argument that we form our moral self-assessment by viewing ourselves as others view us, she demurs: "Because each bystander may have his own prejudices, besides the prejudices of his age or country. We should rather endeavour to view ourselves, as we suppose that Being views us, who seeth each thought ripen into action" (008-017). For Wollstonecraft, the ultimate tribunal is not the impartial spectator but the divine eye — a theological corrective to Smith's secular moral psychology.

## Key Ideas

| Aspect | Smith's View | Wollstonecraft's Use |
|--------|-------------|---------------------|
| Sympathy | Foundation of moral judgment | Accepted as descriptive, but supplemented by divine perspective |
| Social position | Shapes moral character | Applied to women: their character is socially constructed, not natural |
| Reputation | Generally reliable guide to virtue | Qualified: bystanders have prejudices; divine judgment is the true standard |
| Moral luck | Rare but real; virtue generally rewarded | Endorsed as warrant for believing genuine character prevails |
