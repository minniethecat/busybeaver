# Adam Smith

## Overview

Adam Smith (1723–1790) was a Scottish moral philosopher and economist, best known for *The Wealth of Nations* (1776) and *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* (1759). Wollstonecraft cites Smith as a "cool reasoner" and "acute observer" whose analysis of how rank and wealth shape character provides her with a powerful framework for understanding the degraded condition of women. She does not critique Smith; instead, she repurposes his description of the rich to illuminate the female condition, arguing that women, like the aristocracy, are "localized" by their social position and thus prevented from developing the virtues that arise from necessity and struggle.

## In the Text

Wollstonecraft invokes Adam Smith in two chapters. In ch04, she quotes extensively from *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* to argue that the character of women mirrors that of the rich: both are corrupted by unearned privilege, flattery, and the absence of necessity. The extended quotation from Smith ([004-026]), which she introduces at [004-025], forms one of the longest block quotations in the entire work and serves as the structural centerpiece of her argument that the degradation of women is caused by social conditions, not by nature.

In ch08, she returns to Smith as an authority on the general laws of morality. She cites his observation that while an innocent person may be misrepresented in a particular action, the general tenor of a virtuous life tends to earn the confidence and love of others. She deploys this to support her argument that reputation, as distinct from genuine virtue, is a "specious poison" that eats away at morality.

Wollstonecraft's use of Smith is strategic: by borrowing the authority of a respected male philosopher to describe the rich, and then applying his own categories to women, she turns patriarchal assumptions against themselves. If Smith's diagnosis of the rich is correct, she argues, then the same diagnosis must apply to women, who share the same structural position.

## Key Quotations

> "In Dr. Smith's Theory of Moral Sentiments, I have found a general character of people of rank and fortune, that in my opinion, might with the greatest propriety be applied to the female sex." [004-025]

> "Do the great seem insensible of the easy price at which they may acquire the public admiration? or do they seem to imagine, that to them, as to other men, it must be the purchase either of sweat or of blood?" [004-026]

> "Speaking of the general laws of morality, Dr. Smith observes—'That by some very extraordinary and unlucky circumstance, a good man may come to be suspected of a crime of which he was altogether incapable...'" [008-012]

## Biographical Summary

Adam Smith was born in Kirkcaldy, Scotland, and became one of the central figures of the Scottish Enlightenment. He held the Chair of Moral Philosophy at the University of Glasgow and published *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* in 1759, a work that established his reputation across Europe. His *Wealth of Nations* (1776) laid the foundations of classical economics. By the time Wollstonecraft wrote in 1792, Smith had recently died (1790), but his works were at the height of their influence in British intellectual life.
