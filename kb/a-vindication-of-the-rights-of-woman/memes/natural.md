# Natural

The state or condition in which human beings develop according to their inherent capacities, guided by reason and virtue, rather than being shaped by the artificial conventions, hierarchies, and expectations that society imposes — particularly upon women.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft uses "natural" as a normative concept grounded in Enlightenment assumptions about human nature. For her, the natural state is not a primitive condition but the condition in which reason, knowledge, and virtue can flourish without artificial impediments (P03-006). She argues that the "natural rights of mankind" extend to women, who "may be confined in gilded cages" but are nevertheless entitled to participate in those rights (P02-015). The natural is consistently opposed to the artificial: men "endeavour to sink us still lower" not satisfied with their "natural pre-eminence" (P03-003), while women are taught to cultivate "cunning, the natural opponent of strength" as their only recourse in a world that denies them genuine power (P03-014).

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's entire case rests on the claim that the degradation of women is not natural but artificial — a product of "unnatural" social arrangements. From the opening of the Dedication, she asserts that the vindication "seems to flow so naturally" from simple principles of justice (P02-003). She contends that reason, knowledge, and virtue "naturally flow" from the same source for both sexes (001-006). The central political claim follows: depriving "men (or women) of their natural rights" is "one of the absurd sophisms" that sustains arbitrary power (001-010). Throughout, she traces how "unnatural distinctions" — hereditary honours, riches, and monarchy — have produced "misery" (001-013), and how aristocracy itself emerges "naturally" as the first form of government before degenerating into corruption (001-032).

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Where Rousseau imagined a solitary natural man uncorrupted by society, Wollstonecraft turns the natural-state argument toward the condition of women (001-014). She explicitly rejects the Rousseauian notion that woman's "natural" character is fundamentally different from man's: the differences she observes are produced by education, not nature. Against the commonplace that feminine weakness and sensibility are "natural," she insists these are cultivated traits — the "natural effect of ignorance" (002-003). Her use of natural rights discourse directly challenges Burkean traditionalism and the argument that existing social hierarchies reflect a natural order.

## Key Passages

> seems to flow so naturally from these simple principles, that it seems almost unnecessary to illustrate the obvious subject (P02-003)

> the natural effect of ignorance! The mind will ever be unstable that has only prejudices to rest on (002-003)

> depriving men (or women) of their natural rights, is one of the absurd sophisms which daily insult common sense. (001-010)

> Riches and hereditary honours have made cyphers of women, and mere artificial beings. (002-027)

> it may be impossible to convince them that the illegitimate power, which they obtain, by degrading themselves, is a curse, and that they must return to nature and equality, if they wish to secure the placid satisfaction that unsophisticated affections impart. (004-059)
