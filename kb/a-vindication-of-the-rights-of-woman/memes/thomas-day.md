1748 – 1789. English author and radical, best known for _The History of Sandford and Merton_ (1783–89), a children's book promoting Rousseau-inspired educational principles. Wollstonecraft cites Day's work approvingly in her argument that most conduct literature fails to address women as rational beings.

## Life

Thomas Day was born in London in 1748. Educated at Corpus Christi College, Oxford, he became associated with the Lunar Society, a circle of Midlands intellectuals and industrialists that included Erasmus Darwin, Joseph Priestley, and Josiah Wedgwood. Day was an ardent follower of Jean-Jacques Rousseau and attempted to live according to Rousseau's principles of simplicity and natural virtue.

Day's personal life was marked by eccentric experiments in education. He attempted to train two orphan girls according to Rousseau's prescriptions in _Émile_, hoping one would become his ideal wife — a project that failed and has become a notorious example of Enlightenment educational theory taken to absurd extremes. He later married an heiress who shared his ideals of rural simplicity.

His literary reputation rests on _The History of Sandford and Merton_, a children's narrative contrasting the virtuous, active Tommy Merton with the spoiled, indolent Harry Sandford. The book was enormously popular and remained in print throughout the nineteenth century.

## Role in the Text

Wollstonecraft invokes Day in a footnote to Chapter III, where she is arguing that most educational writing addressed to women treats them as mere ornaments rather than rational agents. She writes: "I wish also to steer clear of an error, which many respectable writers have fallen into; for the instruction which has hitherto been addressed to women, has rather been applicable to LADIES, if the little indirect advice, that is scattered through Sandford and Merton, be excepted" (P03-006).

The specific quotation from Day appears in the main text of Chapter III: "'But, Selene's education was regulated by different views, and conducted upon severer principles; if that can be called severity which opens the mind to a sense of moral and religious duties, and most effectually arms it against the inevitable evils of life.' — Mr. Day's 'Sandford and Merton,' Volume 3" (003-011). Wollstonecraft deploys Day's words to support her argument that true education "opens the mind" rather than merely decorating it — a principle Day applied to his fictional Selene but which Wollstonecraft demands for all women.

### Works Referenced

_The History of Sandford and Merton_ (1783–89), particularly Volume 3, which contains the story of Selene. Day also wrote _The Dying Negro_ (1773), an abolitionist poem, and _Reflections upon the Present State of England_ (1782).

## Key Ideas

Day's educational philosophy, like Wollstonecraft's, was profoundly shaped by Rousseau — but both ultimately departed from Rousseau on the question of female education. Where Rousseau's _Émile_ prescribes a domestic, dependent education for Sophie, Day's Selene receives "severer principles" that cultivate moral and rational autonomy. Wollstonecraft seizes on this exception within the Rousseauian tradition to argue that even Rousseau's followers recognized the inadequacy of his prescriptions for women.

Day represents the best impulse within late-eighteenth-century radicalism toward educational reform: a willingness to challenge inherited assumptions, faith in the power of rational cultivation, and an insistence — however inconsistently applied — that character should be formed by principle rather than custom.
