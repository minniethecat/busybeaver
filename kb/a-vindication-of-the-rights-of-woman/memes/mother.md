# Mother

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's treatment of the **mother** transforms a conventional domestic
role into a rational vocation. Motherhood, in her analysis, is not primarily a
matter of sentiment and instinct but of duty, reason, and education. A mother
is the first educator of the next generation; her fitness for this role depends
on the cultivation of her own mind.

## Motherhood as Rational Vocation

The conventional eighteenth-century view of motherhood emphasized tenderness,
patience, and domestic affection — sentimental qualities that required no
formal education. Wollstonecraft challenges this view directly:

A mother who is herself ignorant cannot educate her children. She may love
them, indulge them, or neglect them — but she cannot form their minds, shape
their characters, or prepare them for the duties of adult life. For this, she
needs reason, knowledge, and independence of judgment — precisely the qualities
that women's education systematically fails to cultivate.

## The Educated Mother

Wollstonecraft's positive vision of motherhood centers on the educated woman who
approaches child-rearing as a rational enterprise. Such a mother:

- Teaches her children to reason rather than merely to obey
- Cultivates their virtues through example and instruction
- Prepares daughters for independence rather than dependence
- Raises sons who respect women as rational equals

This conception of motherhood elevates the role from mere domestic management
to a form of public service: "the care of children in their infancy is one of
the grand duties annexed to the female character by nature."

## Sentiment vs. Reason in Motherhood

Wollstonecraft does not deny the importance of maternal affection, but she
insists that affection alone is insufficient. Sentimental motherhood —
governed by impulse and feeling rather than principle — produces spoiled,
ill-disciplined children. Rational motherhood — governed by principle and
informed by knowledge — produces virtuous citizens. The distinction is central
to her argument for women's education: if women are to be mothers, they must
first be rational beings.
