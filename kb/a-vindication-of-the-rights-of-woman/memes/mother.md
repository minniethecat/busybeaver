# Mother

For Wollstonecraft, the mother is not a sentimental icon but a rational agent whose intellectual cultivation is the precondition of her children's moral education. Throughout the *Vindication*, the figure of the mother serves as both the strongest argument for women's education and the most damning evidence of its neglect. The mother who has not been taught to reason cannot teach her children to reason; the mother who values appearance over substance will raise daughters who do the same. The reform of motherhood is thus central to Wollstonecraft's vision of social regeneration.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft defines the mother not by biology — the mere fact of bearing children — but by the rational discharge of maternal duty. "The mother, who wishes to give true dignity to her character," she writes, "must endeavour to acquire sufficient strength of mind to be able to judge for herself" (003-013). True motherhood, on this account, requires the exercise of independent judgment; the mother who merely follows custom or the opinion of others has not yet become a mother in the full moral sense of the term.

This definition has far-reaching implications. It means that the quality of motherhood in any society is a direct function of the quality of women's education. "To be a good mother," Wollstonecraft argues, "a woman must have sense, and that independence of mind which few women possess who are taught to depend entirely on their husbands" (002-059). The dependent wife cannot be a competent mother because the same intellectual submission that makes her a pleasing wife renders her incapable of the independent judgment that maternal duty demands. "The wife is, in general, the best mother" (002-059) — not because wifehood and motherhood are naturally connected, but because a marriage grounded in rational companionship cultivates the very qualities that maternal duty requires.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument places the mother at the centre of the social order. "The first care of those mothers or fathers, who really attend to the education of their children, should be to strengthen their bodies and sharpen their senses" (003-010), but the mother who has never been taught to think cannot provide this education. She becomes, instead, a vector for the transmission of prejudice: "taught by the example of their mothers, that a little knowledge of human weakness, justly termed cunning, softness of temper, outward obedience, and a scrupulous attention to a puerile kind of propriety, will obtain for them the protection of man" (002-003), daughters learn from their mothers the arts of pleasing that substitute for the cultivation of virtue.

The psychological damage is reciprocal. The mother who lacks rational resources becomes emotionally dependent on her children, using them as instruments of her own vanity. "The affection of some women for their children is," she writes, "frequently very brutish; for it eradicates every spark of humanity. Justice, truth, every thing is sacrificed by these Rebekahs, and for the sake of their own children they violate the most sacred duties" (010-006). This is Wollstonecraft at her most astringent: maternal love, untutored by principle, becomes a form of tribal selfishness that destroys the very virtues it purports to nurture.

The remedy is education — but an education directed specifically at the duties of motherhood. "The mother, who wishes to give true dignity to her character, must not rest on the authority of her husband, or the opinion of the world" (003-013). She must cultivate her own understanding so that she can "form the principles and enlarge the understanding of her children" (003-045). Wollstonecraft envisions a domestic education in which "both father and mother" participate in forming the character of their children, with the mother's contribution being not merely affectionate but intellectual — the transmission of reasoned principles rather than the inculcation of unexamined habits.

The political stakes are high. "Liberty is the mother of virtue," Wollstonecraft declares (002-083) — a formulation that links the domestic and the political in a single image. The mother who is herself unfree cannot raise free citizens; the mother who has been taught to submit cannot teach her children the principled independence that republican citizenship requires. The figure of the mother thus becomes, in Wollstonecraft's hands, an argument for the transformation of society as a whole.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Rousseau's *Émile* had presented Sophie's education as preparation for motherhood — but a motherhood understood as the exercise of "natural" feminine instinct rather than rational judgment. "The whole education of women," Rousseau wrote, "ought to be relative to men. To please them, to be useful to them, to make themselves loved and honoured by them, to educate them when young, to care for them when grown, to counsel them, to console them, and to make life agreeable and sweet to them — these are the duties of women at all times, and what should be taught them from their infancy." Wollstonecraft's reply is an inversion: the mother cannot fulfil even these limited duties unless she is first educated as a rational being. "How can she be a good wife or mother," she asks, "the greater part of whose time is employed in the pursuit of pleasure, or the management of her person?" (003-021).

The evangelical model of motherhood, exemplified by Hannah More's *Strictures on the Modern System of Female Education* (1799), promoted female education as a means of producing pious wives and mothers — but within a framework that emphasized submission and domesticity. Wollstonecraft shares More's conviction that mothers are educators, but she radicalizes the claim: the mother must be educated not to transmit piety but to cultivate reason. The Wollstonecraftian mother teaches her children to think for themselves, even if that thinking leads them to question the very conventions their mother has been taught to uphold.

## Key Passages

> Taught by the example of their mothers, that a little knowledge of human weakness, justly termed cunning, softness of temper, outward obedience, and a scrupulous attention to a puerile kind of propriety, will obtain for them the protection of man. (002-003)

> The mother, who wishes to give true dignity to her character, must not rest on the authority of her husband, or the opinion of the world. She must endeavour to acquire sufficient strength of mind to be able to judge for herself. (003-013)

> How can she be a good wife or mother, the greater part of whose time is employed in the pursuit of pleasure, or the management of her person? (003-021)

> Liberty is the mother of virtue, and if women are, by their very constitution, slaves, and not allowed to breathe the sharp invigorating air of freedom, they must ever languish like exotics, and be reckoned beautiful flaws in nature. (002-083)

> The mother will be lost in the coquette, and, instead of making her children the first object of her attention, she will only consider them as a part of her suite. (003-047)
