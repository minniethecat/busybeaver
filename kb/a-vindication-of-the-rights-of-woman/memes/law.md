Wollstonecraft's concept of **law** operates on multiple registers — natural, civil, moral, and divine — and her argument gains much of its force from the way she moves between them. The "law of nature" is the standard by which all merely conventional laws must be judged; the "laws which bind society" (001-006) are legitimate only insofar as they conform to it.

When Wollstonecraft invokes "the common law of gravity" (P02-019) as a metaphor for the moral order, she signals her intellectual allegiance to the Newtonian vision of a universe governed by universal, discoverable laws — and her conviction that the moral universe should be governed by laws no less universal, no less rational, and no less indifferent to the accidents of sex and rank.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft distinguishes between several kinds of law. First, there is the **law of nature** — the rational order discoverable by the exercise of reason, which governs both the physical universe and, properly understood, the moral relations among human beings. This is the standard she invokes when she writes that women should not be treated as "beautiful flaws in nature" (002-083) but as creatures equally subject to, and equally entitled to the protection of, natural law.

Second, there is **civil law** — the "laws which bind society" (001-006), the positive enactments of political authority. These are legitimate only when they "direct" rather than contravene the law of nature. Wollstonecraft's critique of existing civil law is that it systematically subordinates women — a subordination that cannot be justified by appeal to nature, since nature makes no distinction between the rational capacities of the sexes.

Third, there is **moral law** — the internalised principles that govern individual conduct. Wollstonecraft's educational project is, at bottom, the project of bringing women under the governance of moral law rather than the tyranny of custom and opinion. To be governed by moral law is to be free; to be governed by the arbitrary will of another is to be enslaved, whatever the legal forms.

A fourth register appears in her references to "mechanical laws" (003-041) — the deterministic regularities of the physical universe that she distinguishes from the freedom of rational beings. The contrast is significant: rational beings are "not restrained by mechanical laws" but are capable of "struggling to free" themselves, a capacity that women, no less than men, possess.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument about law proceeds from a foundational premise: that the "law of nature" is universal, applying equally to both sexes, and that any civil law inconsistent with it is illegitimate. The "law of nature" she invokes is not the traditional conception — which had often been used to justify women's subordination by reference to their alleged natural inferiority — but a rationalised, Enlightenment conception in which nature is identified with reason itself.

When she challenges Talleyrand-Périgord to "prove first, to ward off the charge of injustice and inconsistency, that they want reason" (P02-015), she is demanding that the exclusion of women from the rights of citizenship meet the standard of rational justification that natural law requires. The existing "flaw in your new constitution" is not a mere political oversight but a violation of the most fundamental law.

The argument extends to the domestic sphere. Men who exercise "lawless power" (003-023) over their wives — who treat them as subjects rather than equals — violate the same natural law that kings violate when they rule tyrannically. The analogy between domestic and political tyranny is central to Wollstonecraft's argument: both are instances of "lawless" power, power unconstrained by the rational principles that alone can justify authority.

Wollstonecraft's treatment of the divine law — "God is thy law, thou mine" (002-006) — is characteristically sharp. She quotes Milton's Adam addressing Eve only to expose the presumption: the chain of command from God to man to woman is a human invention dressed in theological language, not a requirement of divine law properly understood.

The remedy Wollstonecraft proposes is not the abolition of law but its rationalisation. "Let not men then in the pride of power, use the same arguments that tyrannic kings and venal ministers have used, and fallaciously assert, that woman ought to be subjected because she has always been so" (003-028). The historical fact of subjection is not a justification for its continuance — a principle as revolutionary in the domestic sphere as it was in the political sphere of 1792.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft's concept of law places her in direct opposition to the conservative legal philosophy of her period, most influentially articulated by William Blackstone, for whom the common law's treatment of women — the doctrine of coverture, under which a married woman's legal existence was "suspended" — was justified by custom and divine ordinance.

Against this, Wollstonecraft insists that law must be judged by reason, not by pedigree. A law that has existed for centuries but violates the law of nature is no law at all, properly speaking — a claim that aligns her with the natural law tradition while radicalising its implications for women.

She also distinguishes her position from the Hobbesian conception of law as the command of the sovereign, backed by force. For Wollstonecraft, law without reason is mere "lawless power" — tyranny, whether exercised by a king, a husband, or a father. The legitimacy of law depends not on its source but on its conformity to rational principle.

## Key Passages

> Consequently the perfection of our nature and capability of happiness, must be estimated by the degree of reason, virtue, and knowledge, that distinguish the individual, and direct the laws which bind society. (001-006)

> But, if women are to be excluded, without having a voice, from a participation of the natural rights of mankind, prove first, to ward off the charge of injustice and inconsistency, that they want reason — else this flaw in your NEW CONSTITUTION, the first constitution founded on reason, will show that man must, in some shape, act like a tyrant. (P02-015)
