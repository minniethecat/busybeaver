# Courage

A moral and intellectual virtue that Wollstonecraft argues women have been systematically denied through their education and social conditioning, yet which she considers essential for rational independence and resistance to injustice.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft treats courage not merely as physical bravery but as moral and intellectual fortitude — the strength to think independently, to resist social pressure, and to pursue truth regardless of consequence. She directly addresses the fear that "women will acquire too much courage or fortitude" (P03-013), dismissing it as a pretext for keeping women in a state of dependent weakness. In her framework, courage is inseparable from reason: to think clearly about one's condition requires the courage to face uncomfortable truths about social arrangements.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

The denial of courage to women is central to Wollstonecraft's critique of feminine education. She observes that women are systematically "encouraged to remain with listless inactivity" (004-038) — a condition that cultivates timidity rather than the moral courage required for virtuous action. This is not a natural deficiency but a product of social conditioning: where women have received a "manly education," they "have acquired courage and resolution" (004-101), demonstrating that the capacity for courage is universal and its absence in women is manufactured.

Courage in Wollstonecraft's argument also has a political dimension. She links the absence of courage to servitude: a population without courage cannot resist oppression. The implicit argument is that women's lack of courage — deliberately cultivated through education that prizes submission — serves to maintain their subordination. When she rhetorically asks her reader whether "the attempt does not surpass your courage" (005-038), she is challenging women to exercise the very virtue she argues they have been denied.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Contemporary conduct literature typically did not list courage among feminine virtues; women were instead encouraged to cultivate modesty, submission, and delicacy. Wollstonecraft rejects this gendered distribution of virtue, treating courage as a human rather than masculine quality. Her position implicitly challenges Rousseau's view of the sexes as naturally different in character.

## Key Passages

> I am aware of an obvious inference: from every quarter have I heard exclamations against masculine women; but where are they to be found? If by this appellation men mean to inveigh against their ardour in hunting, shooting, and gaming, I shall most cordially join in the cry; but if it be against the imitation of manly virtues, or, more properly speaking, the acquisition of those talents and virtues, the exercise of which ennobles the human character, and which raise females in the scale of animal being, when they are comprehensively termed mankind; — all those who view them with a philosophical eye must, I should think, wish with me, that they may every day grow more and more masculine. (P03-013)

> Let an enlightened nation then try what effect reason would have to bring them back to nature, and their duty; and allowing them to share the advantages of education and government with man, see whether they will become better, as they grow wiser and become free. They cannot be injured by the experiment; for it is not in the power of man to render them more insignificant than they are at present. (004-101)
