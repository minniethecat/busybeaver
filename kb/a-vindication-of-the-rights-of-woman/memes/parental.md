# Parental

Parental authority and parental affection are recurrent themes in Wollstonecraft's *Vindication*, particularly in the later chapters where she turns from diagnosing the problem of women's education to prescribing remedies. She treats the parent-child relationship as the first and most formative context for moral and intellectual development, and she subjects both the abuse and the neglect of parental duty to sustained critique.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft distinguishes between two dimensions of the parental role: "parental authority" and "parental affection." Parental authority, properly exercised, is the legitimate guidance parents provide to children who have not yet developed the full use of reason. She warns, however, against "the absurd manner of making the parental authority supplant reason" (005-108) — when parents demand obedience on the basis of authority alone, rather than cultivating the child's own rational faculties, they undermine the very purpose of education. Parental affection, in her view, is a natural instinct — but one that must be refined by reason to become a genuine moral duty. Without the guidance of reason, parental affection degenerates into mere indulgence or, worse, into the selfish desire to possess and control (010-010, 010-011).

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's treatment of the parental theme is notable for its insistence that parental duty is a rational obligation, not merely a sentimental impulse. She argues that "parental affection produces filial duty" (010-011) — that the bond between parent and child is reciprocal and that children's obligation to honour their parents is grounded in the quality of care they have received, not in mere biological fact. This is a striking departure from the patriarchal view that filial obedience is owed unconditionally.

She extends this reasoning to a critique of how parental authority is exercised over daughters in particular. The "irregular exercise of parental authority" (011-019) — by which daughters are married off without their consent or forced into roles that stifle their development — is presented as a violation of both natural justice and rational duty. She observes that many husbands are "so devoid of sense and parental affection, that during the first effervescence of passion they neglect their children" (004-088), linking the failure of parental duty to the broader corruption of character produced by a society that values pleasure over virtue.

Wollstonecraft also uses the parent-child relationship as a model for social relations more broadly. She speaks of "the parental affection of humanity" (011-005) as a metaphor for the benevolent regard that all rational beings should feel for one another, suggesting that the natural tenderness of the parent-child bond can serve as a template for a more just and compassionate society.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft's conception of parental duty challenges the patriarchal model in which the father's authority is absolute and unquestionable. She insists that authority must be exercised reasonably and directed toward the child's development as an independent rational being, not toward the perpetuation of parental power. Her view also contrasts with the sentimental idealisation of motherhood, which she saw as reducing women's maternal role to instinct rather than elevating it to rational duty.

## Key Passages

> [T]he absurd manner of making the parental authority supplant reason. For all the poverty of education consists in substituting prejudice for principle. (005-108)

> [P]arental affection produces filial duty. (010-011)

> [T]he parental design of nature is forgotten, and the mere animal being is cherished. (008-030)

> [H]usbands so devoid of sense and parental affection, that during the first effervescence of passion they neglect their children. (004-088)

> [T]he parental affection of humanity ... leaves reason in its full force. (011-005)
