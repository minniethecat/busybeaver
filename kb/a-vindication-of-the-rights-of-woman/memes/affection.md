# Affection

In _[[A Vindication of the Rights of Woman]]_, **affection** is the emotional bond that grounds moral life. [[Mary Wollstonecraft]] distinguishes carefully between authentic affection — rooted in [[Reason]] and [[Virtue]] — and the transient, sensual "love" celebrated in fashionable society. The term appears in 25 passages across the work, most prominently in the Dedication and Chapter 2.

Affection is central to Wollstonecraft's argument for companionate marriage: only rational affection can sustain a lifelong union; sensual passion inevitably cools, leaving contempt or indifference in its place.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft defines affection as the natural human capacity for attachment to others, but she insists that natural affection alone is insufficient. Affection must be cultivated by reason and strengthened by virtue:

> "It is, then, an affection for the whole human race that makes my pen dart rapidly along." (P02-003)

This declaration from the Dedication establishes affection as Wollstonecraft's motivating principle — not personal grievance or partisan loyalty but a universal human concern. It is both personal feeling and moral principle.

The dedication to [[Charles-Maurice de Talleyrand-Perigord]] frames affection as a political force: the reform of women's condition is motivated by affection for humanity itself, not merely for one's own sex.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

### Affection in Marriage

Wollstonecraft argues that marriage should be founded on rational affection, not sensual passion. She urges men to "choose wives from motives of affection" (P02-019) — not from lust or vanity or convenience. A marriage based on affection survives the inevitable cooling of physical desire:

> "In order to fulfil the duties of life... to gain the affections of a virtuous man, is affectation necessary?" (002-048)

The rhetorical question exposes the absurdity of courtship conventions that require women to feign ignorance to attract husbands. Genuine affection cannot be built on pretense; a wife should not "conceal her affection, nor pretend to an unnatural coldness of constitution" (002-052).

### Affection vs. Sensual Passion

Wollstonecraft distinguishes affection (rational, enduring, moral) from sensual passion (irrational, transient, physical). A marriage based on the latter is doomed:

> "A blind unsettled affection may, like human passions, occupy the mind." (003-036)

The adjective "blind" is significant — affection without reason is indistinguishable from mere passion. Wollstonecraft's educational program aims to give women the rational capacity to love wisely, making affection a moral achievement rather than a mere feeling.

### Parental Affection

Wollstonecraft extends her analysis to parental affection. A mother's genuine affection "gives a sacred heroic cast to her maternal solicitude" (003-054) — but only when grounded in understanding. Mere instinctual attachment is insufficient for the duties of motherhood; parental affection, like marital affection, requires cultivation.

### Social and Universal Affection

Wollstonecraft's vision extends beyond domestic bonds. "An affection for the whole human race" (P02-003) is the highest form — the moral sentiment that grounds the demand for justice. This universal affection is the opposite of the narrow self-interest that she identifies as characteristic of aristocratic society.

The "unsophisticated affections" (002-015) of natural feeling are superior to the artificial passions cultivated by fashionable education. Authentic affection connects the individual to others; artificial sensibility isolates her in self-regarding emotion.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

Affection is the emotional foundation of [[Virtue]] and the practical expression of [[Duty]]. It stands in opposition to [[Vanity]] — which displaces social affection — and to purely sensual [[Passion]], which burns out where affection endures.

Affection connects to [[Marriage]] (its primary domain), [[Education]] (which should cultivate rather than distort it), and [[Sensibility]] (which is affection refined by cultivation, when properly directed). It is the opposite of the cold self-interest that characterizes aristocratic and commercial society.
