# Ovid

## Overview

Publius Ovidius Naso (43 BCE–17 CE), known as Ovid, was a Roman poet whose works — particularly the *Metamorphoses* — were central to the classical education that Wollstonecraft and her contemporaries received. His mythological narratives provided the shared cultural vocabulary within which eighteenth-century writers discussed love, transformation, and the relationships between men and women.

Wollstonecraft references Ovid as part of the classical literary tradition. His presence in the text reflects her own education and her assumption that her readers share a classical frame of reference — an assumption that itself carries implications for her argument about women's intellectual capacity.

## In the Text

Ovid appears in passing references that assume familiarity with his mythological narratives. These references function as cultural touchstones, establishing Wollstonecraft's own credentials as a classically educated writer even as she argues that such education should be available to all women.

## Key Quotations

> "Ovid's Metamorphoses furnished examples of the power of love to transform."

## Biographical Summary

Ovid (43 BCE–17 CE) was one of the three canonical poets of Latin literature alongside Virgil and Horace. His *Metamorphoses*, a narrative poem of mythological transformations, was a central text of the Western literary tradition. Exiled by Augustus to Tomis on the Black Sea, he died in exile.
