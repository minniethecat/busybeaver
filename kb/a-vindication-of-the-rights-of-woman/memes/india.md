# India

The subcontinent whose colonization by Britain was accelerating during Wollstonecraft's lifetime — providing a context for her discussions of empire, commerce, and national character.

## Location

In the 1790s, the British East India Company was consolidating its control over large territories in India, transforming from a trading enterprise into a territorial power. The impeachment of Warren Hastings (1788–1795) made India a subject of intense political debate in Britain.

## Relevance to the Text

Wollstonecraft's indirect references to empire and commerce connect to her broader critique of the corruption produced by power and wealth. The British engagement with India — and the fortunes made there — represented, for radicals, the corrupting influence of imperial wealth on British society and character. Her argument about the "artificial" character produced by luxury and power applies to imperial as well as to gender relations.

## Historical Context

British India was the most significant imperial possession of the era. The debates over Hastings's conduct and the nature of British rule in India were central to British politics in the 1780s and 1790s, engaging Burke, Sheridan, and other figures Wollstonecraft knew or referenced.
