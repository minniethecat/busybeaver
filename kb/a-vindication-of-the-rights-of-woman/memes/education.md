# Education

## Definition

**Education** is the central practical concern of the *Vindication*. Wollstonecraft uses the term broadly — encompassing not only formal schooling but the entire process by which individuals' [[character]]s are formed: the books they read, the company they keep, the expectations society places upon them, and most importantly, whether they are trained to exercise their own [[reason]] or merely to please others.

> "The most perfect education, in my opinion, is such an exercise of the understanding as is best calculated to strengthen the body and form the heart; or, in other words, to enable the individual to attain such habits of virtue as will render it independent." (002-028)

She distinguishes between public education (in mixed-gender day schools, where children learn through interaction with [[equality|equal]]s) and private education (at home, where children absorb the [[prejudice|prejudices]] of their parents). Her ideal is a national system of co-educational day schools — remarkably progressive for 1792.

Wollstonecraft's educational philosophy is grounded in [[knowledge|epistemological]] premises: if [[reason]] is the faculty that distinguishes humans from animals, and if women possess that faculty equally with men, then denying women access to education is both irrational and unjust. This Lockean framework — that the mind is formed by experience — underpins her entire argument against inherited [[prejudice]] and for the cultivation of [[virtue]] through trained [[judgment]].

## In Context

The Introduction surveys the existing literature on female education and finds it wanting: "After considering the historic page, and viewing the living world with anxious solicitude, the most melancholy [[passion|emotion]]s of sorrowful indignation have depressed my spirits" (P03-001). The problem, she argues, is that all previous writers on the subject — from [[rousseau|Rousseau]] to [[john-gregory|Dr. Gregory]] — have aimed to make women "pleasing" rather than virtuous, rational, and independent.

Chapter II offers the most sustained philosophical argument about education, critiquing the "disorderly kind of education" (002-032) that women receive and proposing an alternative grounded in [[reason]]. Chapter XII returns to the theme with concrete proposals for a national education system.

Wollstonecraft's educational vision has two complementary dimensions. The **individual** dimension concerns the cultivation of [[reason]], [[virtue]], and [[independence]] in each person — what she calls forming "the heart" and "the understanding." The **social** dimension concerns the institutional structures that make such cultivation possible: co-educational day schools funded by the state, where children of both sexes learn together and develop [[friendship]] and mutual [[respect]] rather than the artificial [[gallantry]] that corrupts relations between the sexes.

## Historical Context

### Enlightenment Educational Debates

Wollstonecraft wrote in the wake of the French Revolution (1789), when the question of "the rights of man" dominated European intellectual life. The revolutionary National Assembly's debates about public education — culminating in [[charles-maurice-de-talleyrand-perigord|Talleyrand]]'s 1791 *Rapport sur l'instruction publique*, which proposed state-funded education for boys only — provided the immediate occasion for the *Vindication*. Wollstonecraft's dedication to Talleyrand is both respectful and pointed: she urges him to extend his principles to women.

Her educational thinking engages critically with three major Enlightenment traditions. Against [[rousseau|Rousseau]]'s *[[emile|Émile]]* (1762), she rejects the claim that women's education should aim at pleasing men and serving the domestic sphere — Book V of *Émile*, describing Sophie's education, is her primary target. Wollstonecraft declares: "I now principally allude to Rousseau, for his character of Sophia is, undoubtedly, a captivating one, though it appears to me grossly unnatural" (002-039).

From Locke's *Some Thoughts Concerning Education* (1693), she adopts the empiricist premise that the mind is a blank slate shaped by experience, but she extends its application to women far beyond what Locke himself contemplated. From the Dissenting academy tradition (associated with her publisher [[johnson|Joseph Johnson]] and the Rational Dissenters at Newington Green), she inherits the ideal of education as the formation of independent [[judgment]] rather than rote obedience to authority.

Her proposal for national co-educational day schools in Chapter XII draws on the model of the *écoles primaires* proposed by the French Revolution's Talleyrand report and Condorcet's more egalitarian *Rapport sur l'organisation générale de l'instruction publique* (1792), though she radicalizes both by insisting on genuinely mixed-sex instruction.

### The Gendered Consequences of Neglect

Wollstonecraft does not merely argue that women *deserve* education — she traces the concrete harms of its denial. She observes that the "disorderly kind of education" (002-032) given to women produces precisely the weakness that is then cited as proof of female inferiority: "As a proof that education gives this appearance of weakness to females, we may instance the example of military men, who are, like them, sent into the world before their minds have been stored with knowledge" (002-034). Education that reduces women to ornaments — focused on dress, [[beauty]], and [[sensibility]] — literally "cramps" their faculties: "To preserve personal beauty, woman's glory! the limbs and faculties are cramped with worse than Chinese bands" (003-013). Her target is not women themselves but the system that shapes them: "Youth is the season for [[love]] in both sexes; but in those days of thoughtless enjoyment, provision should be made for the more important years of life, when reflection takes place of sensation" (002-052).

## Key Passages

> "Contending for the rights of women, my main argument is built on this simple principle, that if she be not prepared by education to become the companion of man, she will stop the progress of knowledge." (P02-006)

> "The education of women has, of late, been more attended to than formerly; yet they are still reckoned a frivolous sex, and ridiculed or pitied by the writers who endeavour by satire or instruction to improve them." (P03-012)

> "Consequently, the most perfect education, in my opinion, is such an exercise of the understanding as is best calculated to strengthen the body and form the heart; or, in other words, to enable the individual to attain such habits of virtue as will render it independent." (002-028)

> "I do not believe that a private education can work the wonders which some sanguine writers have attributed to it." (002-027)

> "A mistaken education, a narrow, uncultivated mind, and many sexual prejudices, tend to make women more constant than men." (002-067)

> "That a proper education; or, to speak with more precision, a well-stored mind, would enable a woman to support a single life with dignity." (002-073)

- [[reason]] — The faculty education must cultivate rather than suppress

- [[rights]] — Education is among the rights Wollstonecraft claims for women
- [[equality]] — Education must be equal for both sexes to be just
- [[independence]] — A properly educated person achieves independence of mind and circumstance
- [[prejudice]] — Education rooted in [[reason]] is the antidote to inherited prejudice
