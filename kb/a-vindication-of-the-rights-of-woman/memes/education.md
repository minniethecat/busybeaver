# Education

Wollstonecraft's primary prescription for the emancipation of women: a rational, national system of education that treats both sexes as intellectual equals.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft distinguishes between the superficial "accomplishments" taught to women and genuine education — the systematic cultivation of reason, virtue, and knowledge (002-012). She advocates for "national education" that would place boys and girls together in day schools, receiving the same curriculum (P02-002). A "proper education" is one that develops the understanding rather than merely adorning the person (002-065).

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

The author argues that the entire system of female education in her time — from Rousseau's *Émile* to Dr. Gregory's *Legacy* — is designed to render women pleasing rather than rational (002-015). "The education of women has, of late, been more attended to than formerly," she acknowledges, but "the education of the rich tends to render them vain and helpless" (P03-012, P03-006). She contends that the cultivation of the understanding has been neglected in favor of accomplishments: "in the education of women the cultivation of the understanding is always subordinate" (002-019).

Wollstonecraft's radical proposal is that "the most perfect education, in my opinion, is such an exercise of the understanding as is best calculated to strengthen the body and form the heart" (002-014). She insists that education must be public and mixed: "a private education can[not] work the wonders which some sanguine writers have attributed to it" (002-013).

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Where Rousseau, in *Émile*, designs a separate education for Sophie — training her to please and serve Émile — Wollstonecraft demands identical intellectual formation for both sexes. She sharply criticizes writers who claim that "the whole tendency of female education ought to be directed to one point" — making women pleasing to men (002-042). For Wollstonecraft, education's purpose is not social ornament but moral and intellectual independence.

## Key Passages

> "the most perfect education, in my opinion, is such an exercise of the understanding as is best calculated to strengthen the body and form the heart" (002-014)

> "the education and situation of woman, at present, shuts her out from such investigations" (P02-007)

> "the whole tendency of female education ought to be directed to one point" — her critique of contemporary pedagogy (002-042)

> "a mistaken education, a narrow, uncultivated mind" (002-059)
