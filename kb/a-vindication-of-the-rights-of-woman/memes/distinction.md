# Distinction

The system of artificial social and sexual differentiation that Wollstonecraft identifies as the primary obstacle to women's moral and intellectual development—a system she contends must be "confounded in society" (004-023) if women are ever to be treated as rational beings rather than as ornamental accessories to men.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's analysis of distinction operates at the intersection of two Enlightenment preoccupations: the critique of hereditary rank and the emerging discourse of universal human rights. She opens the *Vindication* by invoking "the dignified distinction, which raises men above the brute creation" (P03-002)—namely, reason—and immediately contrasts it with the degraded "distinction of sex" (P03-009) that reduces women to "a part of the human species" only in the most grudging sense. This opposition between authentic distinction (based on rational capacity) and artificial distinction (based on sex, birth, or wealth) structures her entire argument. Genuine distinction is earned through the exercise of reason and virtue; false distinction is inherited or ascribed, and it systematically obstructs the development of the very qualities it pretends to recognise.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's case against the distinction of sex unfolds across multiple chapters and registers. Her central claim is that the sexual distinction, as it operates in eighteenth-century society, is not a recognition of natural difference but an artificial construction that manufactures the inferiority it purports merely to describe. "I do earnestly wish to see the distinction of sex confounded in society," she declares, "unless where love animates the behaviour" (004-023). The qualifying clause is crucial: she does not deny that sexual difference exists or that it has a proper place in erotic life. What she contests is the extension of that distinction into every domain of social existence, where it becomes "the foundation of the weakness of character ascribed to woman" and "the cause why the understanding is neglected, whilst accomplishments are acquired with sedulous care" (004-023).

The argument proceeds by identifying the mechanisms through which the sexual distinction is produced and maintained. Wollstonecraft notes that "the unnatural distinctions established in civilized life" have "made cyphers of women to give consequence to the numerical figure" (002-027)—women exist not as independent agents but as markers of male status. The same logic operates in the parallel case of military officers, who "do not lose their rank in the distinction of sexes, for they are still reckoned superior to women" (002-025), despite sharing with women a life organised around gallantry and display rather than rational occupation. In both cases, distinction serves to naturalise hierarchy by dressing arbitrary social arrangements in the language of essential difference.

Wollstonecraft is particularly attentive to the pernicious effects of the sexual distinction on the cultivation of reason. "The soul of woman is not allowed to have this distinction" of rationality, she observes, because women are "outwardly ornamented with elaborate care, and so adorned to delight man" (004-005). The distinction of sex thus operates as a kind of intellectual disenfranchisement: women are systematically denied access to the very quality—reason—that Wollstonecraft identifies as the defining human capacity. The result is that women are "only considered as females, and not as a part of the human species" (P03-009)—a formulation that captures with precision the dehumanising logic of sexual distinction.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The eighteenth-century discourse on sexual difference was dominated by the "separate spheres" ideology that Wollstonecraft contests. Rousseau's *Emile* (1762) had given philosophical articulation to the view that men and women possess fundamentally different natures requiring fundamentally different educations. Wollstonecraft's argument that the distinction of sex should be "confounded in society" (004-023) is a direct rejoinder to this tradition. While she does not deny biological difference, she insists that it should have no bearing on the cultivation of reason and virtue, which are capacities common to all human beings "regardless of the distinction of sex" (P03-009). This position places her at the radical edge of Enlightenment feminism, anticipating arguments that would not become mainstream for another two centuries.

## Key Passages

> In a treatise, therefore, on female rights and manners, the works which have been particularly written for their improvement must not be overlooked; especially when it is asserted, in direct terms, that the minds of women are enfeebled by false refinement; that the books of instruction, written by men of genius, have had the same tendency as more frivolous productions; and that, in the true style of Mahometanism, they are only considered as females, and not as a part of the human species, when improvable reason is allowed to be the dignified distinction, which raises men above the brute creation. (P03-002)

> Dismissing then those pretty feminine phrases, which the men condescendingly use to soften our slavish dependence ... I wish to show that elegance is inferior to virtue, that the first object of laudable ambition is to obtain a character as a human being, regardless of the distinction of sex; and that secondary views should be brought to this simple touchstone. (P03-009)

> I do earnestly wish to see the distinction of sex confounded in society, unless where love animates the behaviour. For this distinction is, I am firmly persuaded, the foundation of the weakness of character ascribed to woman; is the cause why the understanding is neglected, whilst accomplishments are acquired with sedulous care. (004-023)

> May not the same remark be applied to women? Nay, the argument may be carried still further, for they are both thrown out of a useful station by the unnatural distinctions established in civilized life. Riches and hereditary honours have made cyphers of women to give consequence to the numerical figure. (002-027)

> Yet outwardly ornamented with elaborate care, and so adorned to delight man, "that with honour he may love," the soul of woman is not allowed to have this distinction, and man, ever placed between her and reason, she is always represented as only created to see through a gross medium. (004-005)
