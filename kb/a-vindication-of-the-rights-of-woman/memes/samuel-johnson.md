# Samuel Johnson

[[Samuel Johnson]] (1709–1784), often referred to as Dr. Johnson, was the pre-eminent English literary figure of the later eighteenth century — a lexicographer, poet, essayist, and moralist whose _A Dictionary of the English Language_ (1755) and critical writings shaped the intellectual landscape that [[Mary Wollstonecraft]] inhabited. She cites him as an authority on language, morality, and practical wisdom across multiple chapters of _[[A Vindication of the Rights of Woman]]_.

## Life

Johnson was born in Lichfield, Staffordshire in 1709 and rose from provincial obscurity to become the dominant literary personality of his age. His _Dictionary_ (1755) was a monumental work of English lexicography, standardising definitions and usage for generations of writers including Wollstonecraft. His _Lives of the Poets_ (1779–81), essays in _The Rambler_ and _The Idler_, and his edition of [[william-shakespeare|Shakespeare]]'s works established him as the foremost critic of his time.

James Boswell's _Life of Samuel Johnson_ (1791), published just one year before the _Vindication_, was the most celebrated biography of the century. Its influence is directly traceable: when Wollstonecraft discusses the difficulty of forming an accurate public character of a great person, she footnotes "I allude to various biographical writings, but particularly to Boswell's Life of Johnson" (008-017).

## Role in the Text

### The Dictionary Definition of Sensibility

In Chapter 4, Wollstonecraft quotes Johnson's _Dictionary_ definition in order to challenge it:

> "And what is sensibility? Quickness of sensation; quickness of perception; delicacy." Thus is it defined by Dr. Johnson; and the definition gives me no other idea than of the most exquisitely polished instinct. I discern not a trace of the image of God in either sensation or matter. (004-046)

For Wollstonecraft, Johnson's definition inadvertently exposes the problem: a faculty described in purely sensory terms — however "refined" — remains material, not intellectual. She insists that true virtue requires reason, not the "polished instinct" that Johnson's definition circumscribes.

### Johnsonian Prose and Female Pretension

In Chapter 5, Wollstonecraft invokes Johnson's distinctive periodic style to satirise the pretensions of [[hester-lynch-piozzi|Hester Lynch Piozzi]], who "often repeated by rote, what she did not understand" and "comes forward with Johnsonian periods" (005-100). The critique is double-edged: Johnson's style, when borrowed by a woman who has absorbed its manner without its substance, becomes hollow ornamentation — precisely the kind of superficial female accomplishment Wollstonecraft condemns.

### Practical Industry Versus Idle Refinement

Twice Wollstonecraft enlists Johnson as an ally in her argument for female industry. In Chapter 9, contemplating the idle women of London, she aligns herself with his practical turn of mind:

> "What can be a more melancholy sight to a thinking mind, than to look into the numerous carriages that drive helter-skelter about this metropolis in a morning, full of pale-faced creatures who are flying from themselves. I have often wished, with Dr. Johnson, to place some of them in a little shop, with half a dozen children looking up to their languid countenances for support." (009-023)

And in Chapter 11, discussing the bonds between parents and attentive children, she notes: "I never knew a parent who had paid more than common attention to his children, disregarded (Dr. Johnson makes the same observation.)" (011-003). Johnson serves here as a corroborating moral authority on the natural reciprocity between parental care and filial respect.

## Key Ideas

| Aspect | Johnson's View | Wollstonecraft's Use |
|--------|---------------|---------------------|
| Sensibility | "Quickness of sensation; quickness of perception; delicacy" | Cited to show that sensibility, so defined, is mere instinct — not reason |
| Language | Monumental prose style | Satirised when mindlessly imitated (Piozzi's "Johnsonian periods") |
| Work | Practical industry preferred to idle refinement | Enlisted as authority for female engagement in productive labour |
| Parent-child bonds | Attentive parents earn their children's regard | Corroboration of natural moral reciprocity |
