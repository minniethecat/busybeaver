# Indifference

In Wollstonecraft's moral vocabulary, indifference is not mere neutrality but a positive vice: the failure to care when care is demanded. It appears in the text as the antithesis of active virtue, whether in marriage, in politics, or in the cultivation of the self.

## Indifference in Marriage

Wollstonecraft describes a trajectory that undermines romantic love: "This is, must be, the course of nature — friendship or indifference inevitably succeeds love" (002-057). The passage frames indifference not as a tragic accident but as the structural successor to passion. When love is founded on sensation rather than rational esteem, its cooling leaves not friendship but blank disregard — a state in which the husband becomes "the dotard a prey to childish caprices, and fond jealousies" (002-057).

In Chapter 2, the cycle is restated: passion fades into "disgust; or, at least, into indifference" (002-061), after which "the imagination is allowed to wander." Indifference, in this framework, licenses infidelity and dissolves domestic bonds.

## Public Indifference

The political dimension of indifference is most sharply drawn in Chapter 12. Wollstonecraft quotes an "eloquent moralist" who contrasts "that rough and repulsing reason, which always finds in indifference for the public good, the first obstacle to whatever would promote it" (012-062) with the zeal of a "truly humane heart." Indifference to the public good is here aligned with cold reason — a reason severed from benevolence — and presented as the primary barrier to social reform.

## Indifference and Male Vision

In Chapter 3, indifference takes on a gendered dimension. Wollstonecraft observes that intellectual beauty — the "fine woman, who inspires more sublime emotions by displaying intellectual beauty" — "may be overlooked or observed with indifference, by those men who find their happiness in the gratification of their appetites" (003-040). Here, indifference is specifically male indifference to female intellect: a refusal to see what does not gratify appetite.

## Indifference as Moral Failure

Throughout the text, indifference appears not as a passive absence but as an active refusal of moral obligation. The wife indifferent to her husband, the citizen indifferent to the common good, the man indifferent to women's intellectual worth — each represents a failure of the rational benevolence Wollstonecraft places at the centre of virtue.
