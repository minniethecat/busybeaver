## Overview

_Théâtre d'Éducation_ (1779–1780) is a four-volume collection of plays for children by Stéphanie Félicité, Comtesse de Genlis (Madame de Genlis). The plays were designed to teach moral lessons to young people, particularly girls, through dramatic performance. The work was widely known in late eighteenth-century Europe and formed part of Genlis's larger educational project, which included _Adelaide and Theodore_ (1782) and _Letters on Education_.

## Wollstonecraft's Assessment

Mary Wollstonecraft discusses Madame de Genlis's educational writings in _A Vindication of the Rights of Woman_ (1792). In Chapter V, Wollstonecraft acknowledges that "Madame Genlis has written several entertaining books for children; and her letters on Education afford many useful hints, that sensible parents will certainly avail themselves of" (005-124). However, she immediately qualifies this praise: "her views are narrow, and her prejudices as unreasonable as strong" (005-124).

Wollstonecraft's ambivalent assessment reflects her broader critique of contemporary female educators. While she respects Genlis's literary accomplishments and practical pedagogical contributions, she regards Genlis's educational philosophy as fundamentally constrained by the same gender prejudices that _A Vindication_ seeks to dismantle. Genlis advocated for a form of female education that, in Wollstonecraft's view, remained oriented toward producing accomplished and marriageable women rather than rational, independent beings.

## Significance

The inclusion of Genlis in Wollstonecraft's argument is strategic. By engaging with a celebrated female author, Wollstonecraft demonstrates that even the most accomplished women of her era were limited by the narrow conceptions of female capability prevailing in eighteenth-century educational thought. _Théâtre d'Éducation_ thus functions in the text as an example of how even well-intentioned educational works by women could perpetuate rather than challenge gender constraints.
