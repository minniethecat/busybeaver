1797 – 1851. English novelist, best known as the author of _Frankenstein; or, The Modern Prometheus_ (1818). Mary Shelley was the daughter of Mary Wollstonecraft and William Godwin, born eleven days before Wollstonecraft's death from puerperal fever. The Biographical Sketch appended to _A Vindication_ notes her parentage and her marriage to the poet Percy Bysshe Shelley.

## Life

Mary Wollstonecraft Godwin was born on 30 August 1797 in London. Her mother died of complications from childbirth on 10 September 1797. She was raised by her father, William Godwin, and a stepmother, Mary Jane Clairmont. Godwin ensured she received a broad education, and the family home was a gathering place for London's literary and radical circles.

At age sixteen she eloped with the married poet Percy Bysshe Shelley, beginning a partnership marked by creative collaboration, financial hardship, and personal tragedy — three of their four children died in infancy. In 1816, during the famous gathering at the Villa Diodati on Lake Geneva with Lord Byron and John Polidori, she conceived the story that would become _Frankenstein_. Published anonymously in 1818, the novel was an immediate sensation and has never been out of print.

After Shelley's death by drowning in 1822, Mary Shelley supported herself and her surviving son through writing, producing additional novels (_The Last Man_, _Lodore_, _Falkner_), travel writing, biographical essays, and extensive editorial work on her husband's poetry. She died in 1851.

## Role in the Text

Mary Shelley does not appear in Wollstonecraft's own text — she was born five years after _A Vindication_ was published. Her sole reference in the work is in the posthumous Biographical Sketch: "Mary lived but a few months after her marriage, and died in child-bed; having given birth to a daughter who is now known to the literary world as Mrs. Shelly, the widow of Percy Bysche Shelly" (P01-019).

The Biographical Sketch's mention of Mary Shelley serves two purposes: it records the tragic circumstances of Wollstonecraft's death, and it gestures toward the literary legacy that survived her. The daughter who killed the mother in birth would grow up to write one of the defining works of Romantic literature — a biographical irony that has fascinated readers and scholars for two centuries.

### Works Referenced

_Frankenstein_ (1818) was published over two decades after _A Vindication_. The Biographical Sketch references Mary Shelley's literary identity but not her specific works.

## Key Ideas

Mary Shelley's relationship to _A Vindication_ is genealogical rather than philosophical. Yet her life and work extend Wollstonecraft's themes in ways that invite retrospective reading. _Frankenstein_'s exploration of creation, responsibility, and the consequences of unchecked ambition resonates with Wollstonecraft's insistence on rational self-governance. Mary Shelley's career as a professional woman writer also vindicates her mother's argument that women are capable of intellectual achievement when given access to education and literary culture.
