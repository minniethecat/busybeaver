Wollstonecraft's treatment of the **domestic** sphere is notably ambivalent: she affirms the moral seriousness of domestic duties while simultaneously critiquing the confinement of women to domestic concerns as a denial of their rational potential. She does not reject domestic life but insists that "to fulfil domestic duties much resolution is necessary" (004-066) — elevating domestic obligation from mere habit to a demanding moral vocation requiring the full exercise of reason.

## Definition

For Wollstonecraft, the domestic is not a natural sphere but a socially constructed confinement. She observes that women "cannot, by force, be confined to domestic concerns" (P02-016) because their rational nature exceeds the narrow bounds assigned to them. The domestic sphere, as she analyses it, becomes problematic precisely when it is treated as women's exclusive domain rather than one arena among many for the exercise of virtue.

Wollstonecraft distinguishes between domestic duties properly understood — the moral obligations of household management, child-rearing, and familial care — and domestic confinement, which reduces women to "gentle, domestic brutes" (002-005). The former she affirms as requiring genuine virtue; the latter she condemns as an insult to women's rational nature. Her careful discrimination between these two meanings of "domestic" is central to understanding her refusal to either simply endorse or simply reject domesticity.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument operates through a series of linked claims. First, she insists that the domestic sphere, when made women's exclusive arena, becomes intellectually stifling. Women whose minds are confined to "domestic trifles" (002-029) cannot develop the breadth of understanding that genuine virtue requires. The domestic becomes a prison when it forecloses intellectual pursuits rather than providing a foundation for them.

Second, she argues that even within the domestic sphere, virtue requires the exercise of reason. "To fulfil domestic duties," she writes, "much resolution is necessary" (004-066) — a claim that elevates domestic labour above mechanical habit by insisting that it demands the same moral faculties as public life. The woman who manages a household without understanding why is, in Wollstonecraft's view, no more virtuous than the soldier who obeys without questioning.

Third, she exposes the hypocrisy of a society that confines women to domesticity while simultaneously devaluing domestic labour. Men who "laboured to domesticate women" (004-048) have done so not to honour domestic virtue but to secure male prerogative. Wollstonecraft's critique thus targets not domestic life itself but the instrumental use of domestic ideology to deny women access to the full range of human capacities.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Where Rousseau idealised the domestic woman as the cornerstone of republican virtue, Wollstonecraft exposed this idealisation as a mask for subordination. Rousseau's Sophie is domesticated in order to serve Emile's needs; Wollstonecraft's vision of domestic life, by contrast, requires that both partners bring developed reason to their shared obligations. She also distinguishes her position from that of conservative moralists who reduced women's virtue entirely to domestic performance, insisting instead that genuine domestic virtue requires intellectual and moral capacities that must be cultivated through education.

## Key Passages

> To fulfil domestic duties much resolution is necessary, and a serious kind of perseverance that requires a more firm support than emotions. (004-066)

> They cannot, by force, be confined to domestic concerns; for they will, however ignorant, intermeddle with the weighty affairs of state. (P02-016)

> The domestic trifles of the day have afforded matter for cheerful converse. (002-029)
