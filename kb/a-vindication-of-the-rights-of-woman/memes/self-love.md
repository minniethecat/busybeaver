# Self-Love

A central concept in Wollstonecraft's moral psychology, self-love denotes the instinctive concern for one's own interests that can manifest either as a reasonable natural desire or as a perverse, calculating selfishness. Wollstonecraft draws explicitly on the French distinction between *amour de soi* (natural self-regard) and *amour-propre* (vanity-driven self-love) to diagnose how distorted self-love corrupts parental affection, social relations, and the education of women.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft opens her discussion of parental affection with a precise lexical distinction: "we have not, like the French two terms (L'amour propre, L'amour de soi meme) to distinguish the pursuit of a natural and reasonable desire, from the ignorant calculations of weakness" (010-001). This absence of linguistic precision in English reflects, she implies, a conceptual confusion that allows selfishness to masquerade as natural affection. Properly understood, self-love in its natural form is the reasonable care for one's own wellbeing, aligned with virtue and the exercise of reason. In its corrupted form — what she elsewhere calls "doting self-love" (001-011) — it becomes a narrow, calculating selfishness that sacrifices every relative duty to personal advancement.

The concept operates at both the individual and the civilizational level. Wollstonecraft traces the "misery and disorder which pervaded society" to "many other complicated low calculations of doting self-love" (001-011), linking personal selfishness to the systemic corruption of social institutions. The desire for riches, rank, and flattery are all expressions of self-love run amok, producing structures of inequality that degrade both masters and dependents.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's most sustained treatment of self-love appears in her analysis of parental affection, which she calls "perhaps, the blindest modification of perverse self-love" (010-001). The apparent paradox — that love for one's children could be a form of selfishness — is central to her argument. Parents who sacrifice "every relative duty to promote their advancement in the world" (010-001) are not truly loving their children but using them as instruments of their own ambition. The parent who tyrannizes over a child, demanding unquestioning obedience, exercises a "despotic stretch of power" (010-001) that serves the parent's ego rather than the child's development.

The educational implications are severe. Wollstonecraft observes that "young people, to whose education particular attention has been paid, have, in general, been very superficial and conceited" (005-143), attributing this to "hasty premature instruction" driven by parental vanity rather than genuine concern for the child's intellectual growth. The cautionary craft she calls "ignorant self-love" (005-143) — the prudence that seeks to spare children difficulty rather than cultivate their capacity for independent judgment — produces adults who are "all their lives the slaves of prejudices" (005-143).

In the domestic sphere, self-love undermines the parent-child relationship at its foundation. When parents "indolently availing themselves of a supposed privilege, damp the first faint glimmering of reason" (011-014), they destroy the very basis on which filial duty can rest securely. Duty not founded on knowledge "cannot gain sufficient strength to resist the squalls of passion, or the silent sapping of self-love" (011-014). The phrase "silent sapping" captures Wollstonecraft's insight that self-love operates not always as loud ambition but as a quiet erosion of genuine relationship — the parent who demands submission "merely because it is their will" (011-014) feeds their own self-love at the expense of the child's rational development.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Rousseau's distinction between *amour de soi* and *amour-propre* in *Émile* is the explicit philosophical backdrop to Wollstonecraft's analysis. Rousseau had argued that *amour de soi* was the natural, pre-social instinct for self-preservation, while *amour-propre* was the artificial, comparative form of self-regard that arose only in society and was the source of all human misery. Wollstonecraft borrows the distinction but gives it a distinctly feminist inflection: where Rousseau saw women as particularly susceptible to *amour-propre* by nature, Wollstonecraft argues that women's education systematically cultivates this perverse self-love. Women are taught from girlhood to value the admiration of others above their own rational self-assessment, making them perpetual slaves to the opinions of the world.

Where Rousseau located the remedy in a carefully managed natural education for boys (Sophie, in his scheme, receives a fundamentally different education), Wollstonecraft insists that the same rational education that corrects self-love in men must be extended to women. The parent who "sets a good example, patiently lets that example work" (011-014) cultivates in children of both sexes a genuine self-regard rooted in reason rather than vanity. For Wollstonecraft, the correction of self-love is not a return to a pre-social state but a forward movement toward the rational independence that alone can ground genuine affection and moral duty.

## Key Passages

> Parental affection is, perhaps, the blindest modification of perverse self-love; for we have not, like the French two terms (L'amour propre, L'amour de soi meme) to distinguish the pursuit of a natural and reasonable desire, from the ignorant calculations of weakness. Parents often love their children in the most brutal manner, and sacrifice every relative duty to promote their advancement in the world. (010-001)

> The desire of dazzling by riches, the most certain pre-eminence that man can obtain, the pleasure of commanding flattering sycophants, and many other complicated low calculations of doting self-love, have all contributed to overwhelm the mass of mankind, and make liberty a convenient handle for mock patriotism. (001-011)

> I very much doubt whether any knowledge can be attained without labour and sorrow; and those who wish to spare their children both, should not complain if they are neither wise nor virtuous. They only aimed at making them prudent; and prudence, early in life, is but the cautious craft of ignorant self-love. (005-143)

> Unless it be founded on knowledge, it cannot gain sufficient strength to resist the squalls of passion, or the silent sapping of self-love. But it is not the parents who have given the surest proof of their affection for their children, who most vehemently insist on their children submitting to their will, merely because it is their will. (011-014)
