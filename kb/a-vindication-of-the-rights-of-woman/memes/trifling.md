## Definition

"Trifling" in Wollstonecraft's usage denotes the trivial, insignificant quality of women's occupations and character under a system of education that denies them serious intellectual and moral cultivation. A "trifling turn" of mind is the predictable outcome of an education that teaches women to value appearance over substance, accomplishment over understanding, and amusement over virtue. Wollstonecraft uses the term to characterise both the specific employments to which women are confined — needlework, fashionable visiting, novel-reading — and the broader orientation of character that results from a lifetime of such trivial pursuits. The concept encapsulates her argument that women are not naturally trivial but are made so by a social system that gives them nothing serious to do.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's critique of trifling is central to her diagnosis of women's degraded condition. She argues that the "trifling turn" given to women's conduct by their education (004-031) is not an accident but a systematic consequence of denying them access to serious intellectual cultivation. When women are excluded from the pursuits that exercise rational faculties, they necessarily turn to trifles — and are then condemned for being trifling. This circular logic is, for Wollstonecraft, one of the chief injustices of women's situation.

The concept extends from individual character to social structure. Wollstonecraft argues that "trifling employments have rendered women a trifler" (004-101), suggesting a causal relationship between the narrow scope of women's permitted activities and the quality of their minds. The solution, she implies, is not to berate women for being trifling but to give them serious employments and a rational education that would make trifling pursuits unsatisfying. She further warns that the taste for trifling gratification undermines moral character (005-136), making women incapable of the self-denial necessary for genuine virtue. The "idle bustle of morning trifling" (013-069) draws women away from their duties, creating a cycle in which trivial pursuits consume the time and energy that should be devoted to intellectual and moral improvement.

## Key Passages

> (004-031)... the trifling turn given to the conduct of women by their education...

Wollstonecraft identifies the "trifling turn" as a direct product of education, not an innate feminine characteristic. The phrase captures her argument that women's triviality is socially constructed rather than natural, and that changing education would change character.

> (004-101)... trifling employments have rendered women a trifler...

This passage states the causal argument explicitly: the triviality of women's permitted occupations produces triviality of mind. Wollstonecraft's use of the singular "a trifler" emphasises that the individual woman is shaped by the social conditions that constrain her.

> (005-136) What! renounce the most trifling gratification to be applauded...

Wollstonecraft dramatises the moral psychology of trifling: when women are taught to value trivial gratifications, they become incapable of the self-denial that genuine virtue requires. The rhetorical question captures her indignation at a system that makes moral seriousness impossible for women.

> (013-069)... the idle bustle of morning trifling, draw women from their duty...

Here trifling is presented as a positive distraction from duty — the "idle bustle" of trivial social engagements consumes time and energy that should be directed toward moral and intellectual improvement. The passage appears in Wollstonecraft's concluding chapter, reinforcing the argument's centrality to her project.
