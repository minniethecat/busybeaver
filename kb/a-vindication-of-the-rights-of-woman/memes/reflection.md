**Reflection** is, for Wollstonecraft, the defining capacity of the human mind — "the noble distinction of man" (005-137) that raises human beings above the animals and makes moral agency possible. It is the power of the mind to turn back upon itself, to examine its own operations, and to form judgments on the basis of that examination. Throughout the *Vindication*, Wollstonecraft argues that women are systematically denied the cultivation of reflection — "reflection shut out by pleasure" (001-024) — and that this denial is the core of their degradation.

The concept is foundational to Wollstonecraft's epistemology and moral psychology. Reflection is what transforms "sensation" into "knowledge" and "experience" into "principle." Without it, a person remains at the level of immediate feeling — capable of being pleased or pained, but incapable of understanding why, and therefore incapable of governing herself according to reason.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's conception of reflection draws on the Lockean tradition of the mind's capacity to observe its own operations. Locke had distinguished between sensation — the mind's reception of ideas from without — and reflection — the mind's perception of its own activities. Wollstonecraft takes this distinction and gives it a distinctly moral and gendered inflection.

Reflection is the bridge between experience and wisdom. Experience provides the raw material of knowledge, but "the dictates of experience and reflection" (P03-010) together are required to transform that raw material into usable insight. "Experience and reflection have led me to draw" (002-015) her conclusions, Wollstonecraft says — the pairing is significant: experience without reflection is blind, and reflection without experience is empty.

Crucially, reflection requires conditions that women are systematically denied. "Solitude and reflection are necessary" (004-027) to give "wishes the force of passions" — that is, to transform vague desires into determinate volitions. But women are never permitted solitude; their lives are filled with the "trivial cares" (004-032) of domestic management and social performance, leaving no space for the mind to turn inward and examine itself.

The most important product of reflection is self-knowledge — and self-knowledge is the precondition of self-government. A person who does not understand her own motives cannot govern them, and a person who cannot govern herself cannot be virtuous. This is why the denial of reflection to women is not merely an intellectual deprivation but a moral catastrophe.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument about reflection proceeds through a series of contrasts between the conditions of men's lives and the conditions of women's. Men are given "the sharp invigorating air of freedom" (002-083); they are encouraged to think, to read, to debate, to form judgments — all activities that exercise the power of reflection. Women, by contrast, are confined to the drawing-room, where conversation never rises above the trivial and where "reflection shut out by pleasure" (001-024) is the normal condition.

The consequences are devastating for women's moral development. "Wanting a due proportion of reflection and self-government" (004-062), women are incapable of the sustained attention to principle that virtue requires. They act on impulse rather than judgment, and their impulses have been shaped by a system that rewards pleasingness and punishes independence. The result is a character that is "very unfit to manage a family" (004-059) — let alone to participate in the moral improvement of society.

Wollstonecraft also develops the connection between reflection and intellectual independence. "Reflection inflamed his imagination" (005-044) — the phrase, describing Rousseau, captures the way in which reflection transforms passive reception into active creation. Rousseau's imagination was not the product of mere fancy but of "reflection" — of the mind's active engagement with its own materials. Women, denied this activity, are condemned to the passive reproduction of what they have been taught.

The remedy, as always in Wollstonecraft, is education. "Strengthen our minds by reflection, till our heads become a balance" (005-051) — the image of the mind as a balance captures the ideal of reflective equilibrium that Wollstonecraft inherits from the Enlightenment and extends to women. A mind strengthened by reflection is a mind capable of weighing evidence, comparing principles, and arriving at judgments that are genuinely its own.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The eighteenth-century discourse of reflection was shaped by the empiricist tradition of Locke and the rationalist tradition of Descartes. For Locke, reflection is the mind's observation of its own operations — a secondary source of ideas, less vivid than sensation but more reliable. For Descartes, reflection — in the form of the *cogito* — is the foundation of all knowledge. Wollstonecraft draws on both traditions but gives the concept a social and political dimension that neither Locke nor Descartes had anticipated.

The gendered dimension of Wollstonecraft's argument is original. Neither Locke nor Descartes had considered that the capacity for reflection might be differentially distributed by social arrangements rather than by nature. Wollstonecraft's insight is that reflection — like reason, like virtue — is a capacity that must be cultivated, and that the cultivation of reflection requires conditions — solitude, leisure, education — that women are systematically denied.

## Key Passages

> Reflection — the noble distinction of man, which raises him above the brute creation. (005-137)

> Solitude and reflection are necessary to give to wishes the force of passions. (004-027)

> Strengthen our minds by reflection, till our heads become a balance to weigh our own feelings and the feelings of others. (005-051)

> Wanting a due proportion of reflection and self-government, they only seek for amusement. (004-062)
