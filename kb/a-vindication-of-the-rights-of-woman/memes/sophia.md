# Sophia

## Overview

Sophia (or Sophie) is the fictional ideal woman created by Jean-Jacques Rousseau in Book V of *Emile* (1762). She is designed as the perfect companion for Emile, Rousseau's ideal man: where Emile is educated for autonomy, reason, and citizenship, Sophia is educated for dependence, modesty, and the art of pleasing. Wollstonecraft makes Sophia — and the educational philosophy she embodies — the central target of her critique in the second half of the *Vindication*. More than any other single figure in the text, Sophia represents everything Wollstonecraft believes is wrong with the eighteenth-century construction of womanhood.

## In the Text

Sophia appears across two chapters. In ch02, Wollstonecraft introduces her as the culminating example of how male writers have distorted female education. At [002-039], she acknowledges that Rousseau's "character of Sophia is, undoubtedly, a captivating one," but immediately declares that "it appears to me grossly unnatural." Wollstonecraft's objection is not that Sophia is unattractive — on the contrary, Rousseau has made her seductive — but that her character is built on a foundation of principles that deny women reason, independence, and moral agency. "It is not the superstructure, but the foundation of her character, the principles on which her education was built, that I mean to attack."

In ch05, Wollstonecraft returns to Sophia in a sustained, page-by-page dissection of Rousseau's educational prescriptions. At [005-005], she quotes Rousseau's maxim: "Sophia... should be as perfect a woman as Emilius is a man, and to render her so, it is necessary to examine the character which nature has given to the sex." The irony Wollstonecraft exposes is that Rousseau's "nature" is really convention — the character he ascribes to women is precisely the product of the education he recommends, not its justification.

The most pointed critique comes at [005-049], where Wollstonecraft quotes Rousseau's direct address to Sophia: "Emilius, in becoming your husband, is become your master, and claims your obedience. Such is the order of nature." She notes the paradox: Rousseau then claims that "when a man is married... to such a wife as Sophia, it is proper he should be directed by her," which Wollstonecraft dismisses as "unintelligible paradoxes." The woman is to obey, but also to direct — but her direction is not rational authority, only the covert influence of cunning and charm.

The figure of Sophia thus serves as a synecdoche for the entire system of female education Wollstonecraft opposes. Sophia is educated to be beautiful rather than virtuous, pleasing rather than wise, and dependent rather than free. Her "modesty" is coquetry in disguise; her "dress is extremely modest in appearance, and yet very coquettish in fact" ([005-043]). In Wollstonecraft's reading, Sophia is not a model of feminine excellence but a cautionary tale of what happens when women are educated only for the gratification of men.

## Biographical Summary

Sophia is not a historical person but a fictional character created by Rousseau in Book V of *Emile, or On Education* (1762). She is the designated wife of Emile, the book's protagonist, and her education forms the work's account of proper female development. Rousseau's *Emile* was one of the most influential educational treatises of the eighteenth century, widely read across Europe, and its prescriptions for female education — embodied in Sophia — shaped conduct literature for decades. Wollstonecraft's decision to make Sophia a central target reflects the enormous cultural authority of Rousseau's educational model and her recognition that attacking it was essential to advancing her own alternative vision.
