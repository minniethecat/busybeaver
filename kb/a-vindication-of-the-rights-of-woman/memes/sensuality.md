**Sensuality** occupies a central place in Wollstonecraft's diagnosis of the corruption of relations between the sexes. The term names more than mere physical appetite — it describes a comprehensive moral condition in which the gratification of the senses has displaced the cultivation of the understanding as the governing principle of conduct. Women are educated to be sensual beings — to value their bodies over their minds, to seek admiration rather than respect, to cultivate the arts of pleasing rather than the habits of virtue — and men are encouraged to value women for their sensual qualities rather than their rational ones. The result is a society in which "the very essence of sensuality has been extracted" (P02-004) and the moral possibilities of human relation have been systematically degraded.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's deployment of "sensuality" draws on a long tradition of moral critique while giving it a distinctively feminist inflection. The term had been a staple of Christian moral discourse, where it named the excessive indulgence of bodily appetites — particularly sexual appetite — and the consequent neglect of spiritual goods. The Enlightenment had added a rationalist critique: sensuality was the state in which reason is subordinated to passion, and the person governed by sensuality was incapable of the consistent moral judgment that virtue requires.

Wollstonecraft adopts both traditions but reorients them around the condition of women. The sensuality she critiques is not primarily a personal failing — though it is that as well — but a structural condition produced by the education of women. When women are "taught to look up to man for a relaxation from the discharge of the duties of life" and denied the cultivation of their rational faculties, they become what their education has made them: beings whose only claim to attention is their power to excite sensual interest. "It follows then, I think, that from their infancy women are either shut up like eastern princes, or taught to speak in such a way as to inflame the passions" (003-037).

The vocabulary is precise. "Sensuality" names the corrupt state; "sensualist" or "voluptuary" names the person who inhabits it; "sensual gust" (008-027) describes the momentary impulse of appetite that sensuality makes the governing principle of conduct. The "voluptuary, who disguises sheer sensuality under a sentimental veil" (004-092) is Wollstonecraft's most damning portrait — the man whose refinement of manner conceals a reduction of women to objects of sensual gratification. The "sentimental veil" is the language of romantic love, of gallantry, of the cult of sensibility, all of which Wollstonecraft exposes as disguises for the same underlying reduction of women to the sensual.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

The argument about sensuality unfolds across several of the *Vindication*'s most important passages. The Dedication to Talleyrand sets the terms: "in France the very essence of sensuality has been extracted to regale the voluptuary" (P02-004). The reference is to the sexual politics of the ancien régime — the culture of the court, the system of aristocratic libertinage — but the diagnosis applies with equal force to Britain. Sensuality, in this account, is not a peripheral vice but a structural feature of societies in which women are valued for their bodies rather than their minds.

The mechanism by which sensuality is produced and sustained is the education of women. "From the tyranny of man, I firmly believe, the greater number of female follies proceed; and the cunning, which I allow makes at present a part of their character, arises from their being taught to look up to man as their master" (003-025). Women are taught sensuality — taught to value their bodies, to cultivate their powers of attraction, to measure their worth by the desire they excite — and then condemned for being sensual. The hypocrisy is structural: a society that educates women for sensuality punishes them for displaying the qualities it has cultivated.

The alternative is the cultivation of reason. Wollstonecraft's insistence on the education of women's rational faculties is not motivated by a rejection of the body — she is no ascetic — but by the conviction that reason is the only faculty capable of governing the sensual appetites. A woman whose understanding has been cultivated will still experience sensual desire, but she will not be governed by it; a woman whose education has been limited to the cultivation of her person will have no resources with which to resist the appetites that her education has inflamed.

The political consequences are far-reaching. A society in which relations between the sexes are governed by sensuality rather than reason is a society in which "the fumes which pride and sensuality have thus spread over the subject" (002-032) obscure the possibility of genuine moral community. Women cannot be citizens — cannot exercise the rights and responsibilities of political membership — so long as they are valued primarily as objects of sensual gratification. The "slavery to which the pride and sensuality of man and their short sighted desire" (003-025) has reduced women is a political condition as well as a personal one: sensuality is the mechanism by which women are excluded from the public sphere and confined to the private.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The eighteenth century had developed an elaborate discourse of "sensibility" that Wollstonecraft diagnoses as the ideological cover for sensuality. The cult of sensibility celebrated refined feeling — particularly in women — as the mark of a superior nature. But the "sensibility" that the culture celebrated was, in Wollstonecraft's analysis, merely "a softer phrase for sensuality" (003-037). The language of feeling provided a vocabulary in which the reduction of women to emotional and sensual beings could be described as an elevation rather than a degradation.

Rousseau is again the central target. His treatment of Sophie in *Émile* reduces women to beings whose sole function is to please men — and the pleasure they provide is, ultimately, sensual. Wollstonecraft's response is to expose the logic of this reduction: "Rousseau would persuade us that as women have a more delicate frame than men, their tastes and inclinations should be different." But the "difference" he claims to find in nature is in fact the product of the education he recommends — an education that cultivates women's sensuality while suppressing their rationality.

The novel of sensibility — Richardson, Sterne, and their imitators — is an additional target. These works had made refined sensuality the subject of literary celebration, teaching women to value themselves for their capacity to feel and to be moved rather than to think and to act. Wollstonecraft's critique of this tradition is that it "inflames the imagination" (003-037) while neglecting the understanding, producing women who are emotionally responsive but morally unreliable — capable of intense feeling but incapable of consistent virtue.

## Key Passages

> In France the very essence of sensuality has been extracted to regale the voluptuary, and a kind of sentimental lust has prevailed, which, together with the system of duplicity that the whole tenor of their political and civil government taught, have given a sinister sort of sagacity to the French character. (P02-004)

> I shall first consider women in the grand light of human creatures, who in common with men, are placed on this earth to unfold their faculties; but I must be allowed to remove the fumes which pride and sensuality have thus spread over the subject. (002-032)

> From the tyranny of man, I firmly believe, the greater number of female follies proceed; and the cunning, which I allow makes at present a part of their character, and which is commonly termed the instinct of their sex, I am persuaded, arises from their being taught to look up to man as their master. (003-025)

> All their thoughts, all their passions, turned on the pleasures of the imagination, which terminate in vanity and sensuality. (003-011)

> The voluptuary, who disguises sheer sensuality under a sentimental veil, is not the man to direct the studies of women. (004-092)
