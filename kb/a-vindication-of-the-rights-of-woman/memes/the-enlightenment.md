# The Enlightenment

1685–1815. The intellectual movement that championed reason, science, and human progress — providing Wollstonecraft with both the philosophical tools she deploys and the contradiction she exposes in its treatment of women.

## Overview

The Enlightenment was an intellectual movement spanning Europe and the Atlantic world, characterized by faith in human reason, skepticism toward traditional authority, and a commitment to progress through education and reform. Key figures included Voltaire, Rousseau, Diderot, Hume, and Kant. The movement produced the *Encyclopédie*, new political theories of rights and social contract, and a vast literature on education and human improvement.

## Wollstonecraft's Engagement

Wollstonecraft is a child of the Enlightenment — her faith in reason, her commitment to education as the engine of human improvement, and her willingness to challenge received authority are all characteristically Enlightenment commitments. Yet she also exposes the Enlightenment's deepest contradiction: the exclusion of women from the universal reason it championed. Rousseau, the Enlightenment's most influential educational theorist, explicitly denied women the full education he prescribed for men. Wollstonecraft's Vindication can be read as the Enlightenment's self-critique — the demand that Enlightenment principles be applied consistently to both sexes.

### In the Argument

The Enlightenment's core commitments — reason over prejudice, education over ignorance, liberty over tyranny — are Wollstonecraft's commitments. Her argument is that these commitments, consistently applied, require the emancipation of women. The failure of male Enlightenment thinkers to recognize this is, in her analysis, a failure of consistency, not of principle.
