# Francis Bacon

[[Francis Bacon]] (1561–1626), Viscount St. Alban, was the English philosopher, statesman, and essayist whose articulation of the empirical method established him as a founding figure of modern science. In _[[A Vindication of the Rights of Woman]]_, Wollstonecraft invokes Bacon twice — each time as an unimpeachable authority whose aphorisms frame her argument about the relationship between reason, nature, and the condition of women.

## Life

Bacon rose through the ranks of Elizabethan and Jacobean politics to become Lord Chancellor of England before his fall from office on charges of corruption in 1621. His philosophical legacy, however, proved far more enduring than his political career. The _Novum Organum_ (1620) and _The Advancement of Learning_ (1605) laid the epistemological foundations for the scientific revolution, while his _Essays_ (1597, expanded 1625) distilled a lifetime of worldly observation into compact moral and practical wisdom. Wollstonecraft, writing nearly two centuries later, found in Bacon's aphoristic style a reservoir of authority that even her adversaries could not dismiss.

## Role in the Text

### Man Between Beast and God

Wollstonecraft's first appeal to Bacon comes in Chapter 2, where she is dismantling the argument that women should be educated only to be pleasing:

> "How grossly do they insult us, who thus advise us only to render ourselves gentle, domestic brutes! … 'Certainly,' says Lord Bacon, 'man is of kin to the beasts by his body: and if he be not of kin to God by his spirit, he is a base and ignoble creature!'" (002-005)

The quotation, drawn from Bacon's _Essays_, serves Wollstonecraft's argument with perfect economy. Bacon's tripartite schema — body, spirit, and the elevating capacity of reason — becomes a weapon against those who would deny women access to the very faculty that makes humanity "of kin to God." If women are denied the cultivation of reason, they are condemned to the condition of "domestic brutes" — kin only to the beasts, never to the divine.

### Wives, Children, and Hostages to Fortune

In Chapter 4, discussing the "peculiar duties of women," Wollstonecraft deploys Bacon's most famous aphorism on domestic life:

> "'He that hath wife and children,' says Lord Bacon, 'hath given hostages to fortune; for they are impediments to great enterprises, either of virtue or mischief. Certainly the best works, and of greatest merit for the public, have proceeded from the unmarried or childless men.' I say the same of women." (004-048)

Her addition — "I say the same of women" — is a small phrase that carries a large claim. By extending Bacon's observation from men to women, Wollstonecraft insists that women, like men, are capable of "great enterprises" of intellect and public merit, and that domestic bonds are impediments — not exclusive to one sex — to the highest exertions. She immediately qualifies this with a defence of domestic duty: "the welfare of society is not built on extraordinary exertions." But the rhetorical work is done: Bacon's authority has been invoked to dismantle the assumption that domesticity is woman's natural and exclusive sphere.

## Key Ideas

Bacon's foundational insight — that knowledge comes through observation and reason, not authority or tradition — aligns him, in Wollstonecraft's handling, with the Enlightenment project she brings to bear on the condition of women. His aphorisms supply her with a language of human nature universal enough to include women, at a time when most authorities on human nature had women firmly excepted.
