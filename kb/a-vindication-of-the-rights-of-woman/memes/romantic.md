"Romantic" functions in A Vindication as a term of epistemic and moral critique: it names beliefs, expectations, and dispositions that are shaped by feeling and imagination rather than by reason and experience. Wollstonecraft applies the epithet to notions of honour, to passions mistaken for love, to expectations of constant affection, and to the "twist of the mind" produced by confined education.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's usage of "romantic" is consistently negative. A "spirit inspired by romantic notions of honour" (001-026) is one that has substituted a code of feeling for a code of reason. The term describes not the content of a belief but its epistemic status: romantic beliefs are those held on the authority of sentiment rather than on the authority of rational investigation. When she distances herself from "romantic ground" (003-045), she is asserting her own commitment to rational argument over the seductions of sentimental prose.

The term appears most frequently in connection with love and sexual relations. Wollstonecraft is careful to distinguish "the romantic passion" (002-061) — love as it is represented in novels and sentimental discourse — from the rational affection that she endorses. The "romantic unnatural delicacy of feeling" (002-065) that some women cultivate is, in her analysis, neither natural nor delicate but a product of the same defective education that produces all feminine vices: an education that addresses women as creatures of feeling rather than as rational beings.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's critique of the romantic is inseparable from her critique of women's education. The "romantic twist of the mind" (013-026) is produced "by a confined education" — it is not innate but manufactured. Women who read novels rather than works of serious philosophy, who are taught to value feeling over thought, inevitably develop romantic expectations that reality cannot satisfy. The result is disappointment, inconstancy, and a character formed by "wavering feelings" rather than by "persevering passions" (004-094).

The political dimension of this critique emerges in her treatment of the epithet itself. She notes with some bitterness that "the damning epithet — romantic" (012-062) is used to dismiss proposals for women's education and political reform. The charge of being "romantic" — unrealistic, utopian, unworldly — is deployed as a rhetorical weapon against those who advocate for women's rights. Wollstonecraft's response is to turn the accusation back on her accusers: the real romanticism, she argues, lies not in demanding rational education for women but in expecting women to be virtuous while denying them the means of virtue.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The Romantic movement, in its early phase, was contemporaneous with Wollstonecraft's writing, and her use of the term predates its later valorisation by Wordsworth and Coleridge. Her critique is directed not at Romanticism as a literary movement but at a broader cultural disposition — the elevation of feeling over reason that characterised the culture of sensibility. In this she is closer to Johnson's commonsensical critique of "romantic virtue" than to the Romantic poets' celebration of imagination and feeling. Her target is not imagination per se but imagination ungoverned by reason, and her alternative is not the suppression of feeling but its rational cultivation.

## Key Passages

> A spirit inspired by romantic notions of honour, a kind of honour that is but the child of sentiment (001-026)

> I do not mean to allude to the romantic passion, which is the concomitant of genius (002-061)

> Gregory confined his remark to romantic expectations of constant love and a uniform character (002-064)

> women who have fostered a romantic unnatural delicacy of feeling, waste their lives in preparing for a romantic drama (002-065)

> we are not treading on romantic ground, though he may think he is (003-045)

> romantic, wavering feelings, that I wish to see converted into persevering passions (004-094)

> render the best disposed, romantic and inconstant; and the remainder of their lives is spent in vain regret (004-095)

> virtue, and a romantic kind of delicacy, made him prefer the artificial to the natural (005-044)

> the damning epithet — romantic; the force of which I shall endeavour to counteract (012-062)

> a romantic twist of the mind, which has been very properly termed *sentimental* (013-026)
