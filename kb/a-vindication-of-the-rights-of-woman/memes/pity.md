# Pity

In _A Vindication_, pity is a volatile moral category. Wollstonecraft recognizes it as a natural human response to suffering, yet she repeatedly warns that pity without respect degrades both its object and its subject. Pity must be distinguished from the rational regard owed to fellow human beings.

## Pity vs. Respect

The distinction is drawn most sharply in the Introduction. Wollstonecraft declares her determination to treat women "like rational creatures" and warns that "those beings who are only the objects of pity and that kind of love, which has been termed its sister, will soon become objects of contempt" (P03-008). Pity, when it substitutes for recognition of a person's rational capacities, is not benevolence but a form of contempt.

The women of the leisured class, educated only in "artificial manners" (P03-006), have "the strongest claim to pity" — but this is a damning observation. Their claim to pity is evidence of their degradation, not their deserving.

## Pity in Moral Judgment

In Chapter 5, Wollstonecraft offers a more complex account of pity's role in moral life. Observing how follies "swell into a vice, by almost imperceptible degrees," she notes that we "pity while we blame" (005-121). Here pity tempers judgment, reminding the moral critic of shared human imperfection. This is pity in its legitimate function — as a brake on the presumption of righteousness.

## Pity and the Divine

The capacity for pity is treated in Chapter 8 as a mark of moral sensibility. Wollstonecraft asks: "shall not I pity those who are stamped with less vigour, or who have stamped themselves with characters, which, with difficulty, they efface?" (008-018). The question is rhetorical: pity for the morally deformed is an obligation, linked to the recognition that virtue depends partly on circumstances beyond individual control.

## A Skeptical Note

Yet Wollstonecraft remains wary. In Chapter 9, she observes: "I doubt whether pity and love are so near a-kin as the poet who painted the cave" (009-034). The literary tradition that merges pity with love — the knightly pity for the distressed damsel — is, in her view, a source of confusion rather than moral clarity. Pity may soften the heart, but it does not necessarily produce just action.
