Flattery, in Wollstonecraft's critique, is not merely insincere praise but a systematic discursive practice that sustains women's subordination. By addressing women in the language of adoration rather than the language of reason, flattery keeps them intellectually infantile and morally stunted, substituting the gratification of vanity for the cultivation of virtue.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft defines flattery through its effects rather than its form. She observes that "the feelings of a man are stifled by flattery, and reflection shut out by pleasure" (001-024), identifying flattery as an obstacle to the exercise of reason. The mechanism is precise: flattery produces pleasure, and pleasure displaces reflection. This analysis applies with particular force to women, who are systematically addressed through flattery rather than through the "language of truth and soberness" (005-062) that would engage their rational faculties.

The term is closely associated with "artful" and "delusive" — adjectives that underscore Wollstonecraft's view of flattery as a calculated instrument of control rather than an innocent social convention. When she asks "what can such delusive flattery lead to, but vanity and folly?" (005-067), the rhetorical question carries the weight of her entire argument: flattery is not harmless gallantry but a mechanism of moral corruption.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's critique of flattery operates at the intersection of gender, education, and political philosophy. She objects most strenuously to the use of flattery in contexts where women should be addressed as rational beings. Her attack on conduct-book writers who "cajole women into virtue by artful flattery and sexual compliments" (005-062) is particularly pointed: if virtue must be coaxed through flattery, it is not virtue at all but a performance designed to please.

The political dimension emerges in her analysis of how flattery corrupts those in power. Describing how a man is "cheated out of his humanity by the flattery of sycophants" (009-002), Wollstonecraft draws a parallel between the courtier who flatters the prince and the suitor who flatters the woman. In both cases, flattery isolates the powerful from reality, leaving them incapable of self-knowledge or genuine virtue. This structural parallel connects the critique of flattery to Wollstonecraft's broader republican politics: arbitrary power, whether political or domestic, generates flattery, and flattery in turn reinforces arbitrary power.

The antidote is sincerity. Wollstonecraft contrasts "the rant of flattery" with "the simple accents of sincerity" (012-040), aligning flattery with artifice and performance, and sincerity with nature and truth. This opposition maps onto her broader argument about women's education: women must be taught to value sincere rational discourse over the flattering compliments that have been the currency of their social existence.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Where the eighteenth-century culture of gallantry treated flattery as a harmless — even desirable — component of mixed-sex sociability, Wollstonecraft identified it as a form of intellectual violence. Rousseau, who lamented the corrupting effects of flattery on political life, nevertheless endorsed a form of gallantry toward women that Wollstonecraft would have recognised as flattery by another name. Her innovation was to apply the republican critique of courtly flattery — developed by writers from Machiavelli to Montesquieu — to the domestic sphere, revealing the drawing-room as a site of the same corrupting dynamics that plagued the throne room.
