# Compassion

Compassion, for Wollstonecraft, is a natural moral sentiment — the spontaneous emotional response to the suffering of others. She affirms its value while insisting that it must be governed by reason and distinguished from mere sensibility, which can be indulged without producing moral action.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft treats compassion as an innate moral capacity: "compassion to every living creature" is part of what cultivates "humanity" (012-068). She experiences it herself — "I have seldom seen much compassion excited by the helplessness of..." (009-034) — presenting compassion as a marker of moral seriousness. Yet she distinguishes genuine compassion from the performative sensibility she critiques in contemporary culture.

The concept appears in her discussion of marriage and friendship, where passion should be "subsiding into friendship or compassionate tenderness" (002-060). This framing — compassion as the mature form of romantic passion — is characteristic of her argument that rational affection, not romantic intensity, is the proper foundation for lasting relationships.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Compassion functions in Wollstonecraft's moral psychology as a bridge between feeling and action. She notes that "when the heart is opened by compassion, and the head active in arranging" (009-008), genuine moral response becomes possible. Compassion opens the heart; reason directs the response. Without reason, compassion risks becoming mere sentiment — felt but not acted upon.

She demonstrates her own compassion in her analysis of women's condition: one cannot "avoid feeling the most lively compassion for those unfortunate females" (004-081) who have been ruined by society's double standard. This compassion is not sentimental pity but a moral response that demands structural critique.

She advocates "more compassionate forbearance" (005-142) in moral judgment — the recognition that circumstances shape character. This is not moral relativism but a call for nuanced understanding: "the world can... observe it with more compassionate forbearance" (005-142).

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Rousseau and the culture of sensibility elevated compassion as the supreme moral sentiment, but Wollstonecraft critiques the version that stops at feeling. Sentimental compassion — the tear shed at a tragedy while ignoring real suffering — is worse than indifference because it mistakes feeling for virtue. Genuine compassion must lead to action and be governed by rational judgment about what truly alleviates suffering.
