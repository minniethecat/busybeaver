# Devotion

A quality of intense commitment, whether religious or emotional, that Wollstonecraft treats with characteristic ambivalence: she critiques irrational devotion as a failure of reason, yet recognises its proximity to the moral sentiments she values.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft uses "devotion" in two interrelated senses. In its primary, religious sense, devotion denotes pious commitment — which she insists "ought to rest upon" the same principle as reason (003-037), not upon mere feeling or inherited habit. Devotion without understanding is, in her framework, indistinguishable from superstition. In its secondary, emotional sense, devotion describes an intensity of attachment or tenderness — "so near a-kin to devotion" (012-013) — that can either elevate moral character or, when directed toward unworthy objects, degrade it.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's treatment of religious devotion is consistently rationalist. She critiques "irrational devotion" (013-009) and the "solemn devotional parade" (013-008) of religious ceremonies that impress the senses without engaging the understanding. The danger of devotion in this sense is that "like devotion, make it absorb every meaner" (004-092) — it can consume the whole person, crowding out the rational faculties that should govern religious sentiment. Women, educated to feel rather than to reason, are particularly susceptible to this species of devotional excess.

In its emotional sense, devotion is more ambivalently valued. Wollstonecraft notes its proximity to tenderness — a quality she does not reject wholesale — but warns that devotion directed toward a husband or lover, rather than toward moral principle, reproduces the dynamic of dependence she critiques throughout the work. Women who are "devotional" without understanding "neither acquired ideas" (013-031) nor developed independent moral judgment.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft's rationalist treatment of devotion aligns her with Enlightenment critiques of religious enthusiasm, but she departs from male rationalists by connecting the problem of irrational devotion specifically to women's education. Where a writer like Hume might critique religious enthusiasm in general, Wollstonecraft identifies the mechanism by which women's exclusion from rational education makes them particularly vulnerable to it.

## Key Passages

> Reason ought to rest upon the same principle that devotion ought to rest upon. No other foundation can secure either from the danger of enthusiasm. (003-037)

> There is a tenderness, so near a-kin to devotion. I do not say, that these devotional feelings are to be eradicated. (012-013)
