# Mary Wollstonecraft

[[Mary Wollstonecraft]] (1759–1797) wrote _[[A Vindication of the Rights of Woman]]_ in six weeks — and in doing so, produced the foundational text of modern feminist philosophy. Her central argument is as simple as it is radical: women are rational beings whose apparent intellectual and moral inferiority is the product of inadequate education and systematic constraint, not of nature. To deny women the cultivation of reason is to deny them the capacity for [[Virtue]] itself.

Wollstonecraft wrote at the intersection of the [[Enlightenment]] and the [[French Revolution]], drawing on the language of universal rights while exposing its limits. She addresses [[Talleyrand-Périgord]] directly in her Dedication, challenges [[Jean-Jacques Rousseau]]'s educational philosophy across ten chapters, and builds her argument on Lockean empiricist foundations — all while maintaining the urgent, first-person voice of a woman who had achieved intellectual independence against considerable odds.

## Life

Born in 1759, Wollstonecraft endured an unsettled childhood marked by her father's financial imprudence. The biographical sketch records that "in early youth she exhibited traces of exquisite sensibility, soundness of understanding, and decision of character" (P01-006). She worked as a lady's companion, a schoolmistress, and a governess before establishing herself as a professional writer — a rare achievement for a woman in the eighteenth century.

At Newington Green she became acquainted with Dr. [[Richard Price]], the dissenting minister and radical thinker, to whom "she became strongly attached; the regard was mutual" (P01-007). This connection placed her at the centre of London's dissenting intellectual circles, where she encountered the ideas of [[John Locke]], [[Joseph Priestley]], and the Revolutionary politics that would shape her writing.

After her close friend [[Frances Blood]] died in Lisbon, Wollstonecraft resolved to devote herself to literature. In 1787 she began writing for the publisher Joseph Johnson, and "during the three subsequent years, she was actively engaged" in literary work (P01-009). The biographical sketch notes that despite her father's financial troubles, "Mary practised a rigid economy" and used her savings to support her siblings (P01-010) — a testament to the independence of character she would champion in her writing.

The [[French Revolution]] galvanized her political writing. Her _Vindication of the Rights of Men_ (1790) was among the first responses to [[Edmund Burke]]'s _Reflections on the Revolution in France_. The success of this work gave her the confidence to begin the Vindication, which the sketch reports she "accomplished in six weeks" (P01-013).

She traveled to Paris during the Revolution, where she witnessed the Terror firsthand. Her relationship with the American adventurer [[Gilbert Imlay]] produced a daughter, Fanny, and ended in personal crisis. She later married [[William Godwin]], the radical philosopher, and died in 1797 from complications following the birth of their daughter Mary — later [[Mary Shelley]], author of _Frankenstein_.

## Role in the Text

### The Authorial Voice

Wollstonecraft writes in the first person throughout, addressing [[Talleyrand-Périgord]] in the Dedication and maintaining a personal, urgent voice. This is not abstract philosophy — it is an argument conducted in the heat of Revolutionary conviction, informed by lived experience. Her tone ranges from righteous indignation to cool analytical rigor, from intimate confession to sweeping political declaration.

> "I plead for my sex — not for myself. Independence I have long considered as the grand blessing of life, the basis of every virtue." (P02-005)

### Against Rousseau

Wollstonecraft's primary intellectual antagonist is Rousseau, whose _Emilius_ she engages across ten chapters. Her critique is notable for its intellectual seriousness: she does not dismiss him as a mere misogynist but engages his arguments point by point. "Rousseau respected — almost adored virtue — and yet he degraded woman" (003-017). This paradox structures her entire response: a philosopher of virtue who systematically degrades half of humanity.

### Educational Philosophy

Her educational philosophy is grounded in Lockean empiricism — the premise that character is formed by experience, not innate disposition — and extends it to the domain that Locke himself left unexamined. If women appear frivolous, it is because they have been educated for frivolity. If they appear intellectually weak, it is because their reason has never been cultivated.

> "Strengthen the female mind by enlarging it, and there will be an end to blind obedience." (003-058)

### The Virtue Argument

Wollstonecraft's most radical claim is that [[Virtue]] has no sex. If virtue requires reason, and women possess reason, then women are capable of virtue — and any social arrangement that denies them the cultivation of reason is not merely unjust but morally destructive, producing the very vices it claims to guard against.

### Works Referenced

- _Thoughts on the Education of Daughters_ (1787) — early educational treatise
- _A Vindication of the Rights of Men_ (1790) — response to Burke; established her reputation
- _A Vindication of the Rights of Woman_ (1792) — the present work
- _An Historical and Moral View of the French Revolution_ (1794) — eyewitness account of the Terror
- _Maria: or, The Wrongs of Woman_ (1798, posthumous) — fictional treatment of similar themes

## Key Ideas

| Theme | Wollstonecraft's Position | Opponents' View |
|-------|--------------------------|-----------------|
| Reason | Women possess reason equally — denied cultivation, not capacity | Women are creatures of feeling, unfit for abstract thought |
| Education | Must form women for independence and virtue, not pleasing | Train for domestic subordination and male gratification |
| Virtue | Has no sex — grounded in rational self-governance | Male virtue is public; female virtue is private chastity |
| Independence | "The grand blessing of life, the basis of every virtue" | Women should never feel independent (Rousseau) |
| Nature | Sex differences are products of education, not innate disposition | Women are naturally subordinate to men |

Wollstonecraft's philosophical method is grounded in Enlightenment rationalism but applied to a domain — gender relations — that most Enlightenment thinkers had left unexamined. Her greatest originality lies not in any single argument but in the systematic application of rationalist principles to the condition of women, exposing the inconsistency at the heart of the age of reason.
