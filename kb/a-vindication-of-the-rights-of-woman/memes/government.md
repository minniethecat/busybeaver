**Government** is, in Wollstonecraft's vocabulary, a concept that operates on three distinct but interrelated levels: the political government of the state, the moral self-government of the individual, and the providential government of the world. Her argument for women's rights draws on all three, moving between the critique of existing political institutions and the demand for a form of government that would include women in its benefits and its deliberations.

The Dedication to Talleyrand-Périgord establishes the political frame: "the exclusion of women from all participation of government was a political phenomenon that, perhaps, no human being has ever attempted to explain" (P02-009). Government, in this primary sense, is the domain from which women have been unjustly barred — and whose reform is the central political demand of the *Vindication*.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's most capacious use of the term invokes the analogy between the government of the world and the government of the self. "In the government of the physical world, it is observable that the female in point of strength is, in general, inferior to the male" (P03-003) — but this physical fact, she argues, has no bearing on the government of the moral world, where reason, not strength, is the ordering principle.

The concept of self-government is central to her educational theory. Women must acquire "a portion of reflection and self-government" (004-062) if they are to be capable of virtue. Self-government is the internalisation of rational order — the capacity to govern one's passions by reason rather than being governed by them. It is the moral analogue of political liberty.

Political government, in Wollstonecraft's analysis, is defective in two related ways. First, it excludes women from participation, treating half of humanity as though they were not parties to the social contract. Second, it fails to govern — it leaves the domestic sphere as a domain of arbitrary power, a "tyranny" that exists alongside and beneath the formal equality of citizens.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument about government begins with the claim that existing civil governments have "put almost insuperable obstacles in the way" of women's improvement (004-011). The very "constitution of civil governments" (004-011) is founded on the exclusion of women — an exclusion that is not accidental but structural, built into the legal and institutional framework of the state.

The argument extends to the domestic analogy between political and familial government. Wollstonecraft observes that "the system of government which prevails in the moral world" (002-057) is one in which reason ought to rule — but in the actual government of the family, arbitrary will has replaced rational authority. The husband governs the wife not by reason but by power; the father governs the children not by principle but by caprice.

The remedy she proposes is twofold: the reform of political government to include women, and the reform of domestic government to replace arbitrary authority with rational order. National education, established "by government" (012-049), is the institutional mechanism by which both reforms can be advanced simultaneously.

Her claim that "the laws, which ought to be the government of society" (012-093) should govern "not those who execute them" is a radical assertion of the rule of law over the rule of persons — a principle she extends to the domestic sphere, where the arbitrary will of the husband must give way to the rational authority of shared principles.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft's treatment of government engages critically with the social contract tradition. Locke had argued that political government arises from consent and exists to protect natural rights; Wollstonecraft points out that women have never been asked for their consent and that their natural rights remain unprotected.

Against the Rousseauian ideal of the patriarchal family as a model for the state, she argues that the family as presently constituted is a model of tyranny, not of legitimate government. The reform of political government requires the simultaneous reform of domestic government; one cannot be achieved without the other.

## Key Passages

> The exclusion of women from all participation of government, was a political phenomenon that, perhaps, no human being has ever attempted to explain, on any principle worthy of discussion. (P02-009)

> The very constitution of civil governments has put almost insuperable obstacles in the way of female improvement. (004-011)

> Let woman share the advantages of education and government with man, see whether they will become better, as they grow wiser and freer. (012-048)

> Day schools, for particular ages, should be established by government, in which boys and girls might be educated together. (012-049)
