# Jonathan Swift

1667 – 1745. Anglo-Irish satirist and clergyman, author of *Gulliver's Travels*, whose literary treatment of women Wollstonecraft references in her broader critique of how male authors have shaped conceptions of female nature.

## Life

Swift was the foremost satirist in the English language, known for works including *A Tale of a Tub*, *Gulliver's Travels*, and *A Modest Proposal*. As Dean of St. Patrick's Cathedral in Dublin, he was also a prominent figure in Irish politics. His complex relationships with women — particularly "Stella" (Esther Johnson) and "Vanessa" (Esther Vanhomrigh) — have been the subject of extensive biographical speculation.

## Role in the Text

Wollstonecraft references Swift in her discussion of how male writers have misrepresented female nature. His satirical treatment of women — particularly in poems like "The Lady's Dressing Room" — represents one strain of the literary misogyny she critiques. Swift's savage satire of female vanity, while brilliantly executed, participates in the broader cultural project of treating women as objects of ridicule rather than as rational moral agents.

Wollstonecraft's engagement with Swift is less extensive than her treatment of Rousseau or Gregory, but he represents an important current in eighteenth-century literary culture: the satirical tradition that made women the butt of jokes about vanity, affectation, and sexual duplicity. Her rebuttal to this tradition is not to deny the phenomena Swift satirizes — she acknowledges that women's education produces vanity — but to insist that the fault lies in the education, not in the nature of women.

### Works Referenced

- *Gulliver's Travels* (1726) — satirical masterpiece
- Various poems on women — satirical treatments of female vanity

## Key Ideas

Swift's satire of female vanity, while artistically accomplished, represents the literary tradition that treats women's faults as natural rather than as products of their education and social position. Wollstonecraft's argument reframes the phenomena Swift mocks: women's vanity is not a natural defect but the predictable consequence of an education that teaches them to value appearance over substance.
