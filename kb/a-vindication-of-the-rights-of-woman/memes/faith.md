# Faith

Faith in Wollstonecraft's work operates on two planes: theological faith — trust in divine wisdom and order — and interpersonal faith, particularly marital faithfulness. She critiques blind faith in authority while affirming faith grounded in reason, and exposes the gendered double standard in expectations of conjugal fidelity.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft distinguishes between rational faith and blind credulity. When she warns that "the state is in danger, if faith in the wisdom of antiquity is to be the criterion of truth" (001-024), she targets unthinking deference to tradition — the same deference that keeps women subordinate. Faith, properly understood, must be compatible with reason: "her faith in matters of religion, should not be founded on the authority of man" (005-033). A woman's religious convictions, like her moral convictions, must be her own.

In the marital context, faith becomes "faithfulness" — fidelity to one's spouse. Wollstonecraft is withering about the double standard: "faithless husbands will make faithless wives" (P02-017). She insists that marital faith must be reciprocal: "she and her lord were equally faithless" (008-007). Her analysis anticipates modern feminist critiques of the sexual double standard, arguing that unfaithfulness is produced by a system that values women for charm rather than character.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's treatment of faith is consistent with her broader argument for women's rational autonomy. Religious faith must not be imposed by male authority — priest, father, or husband — but must arise from each woman's own reason and conscience. "Her faith in matters of religion, should not be founded on the authority of man" (005-033) is a radical claim in an era when women's religious lives were directed by male clergy and male relatives.

On marital faith, she is equally radical. Rather than simply condemning unfaithful wives, she analyzes the structural conditions that produce infidelity: women educated only to please will, when pleased by someone new, transfer their "faith." "The woman who is faithful to the father of her children... demands the same fidelity in return" (004-080) — faithfulness must be mutual, grounded in rational friendship rather than romantic passion.

She critiques the sentimentalization of faith: "memory, faithful to the first impressions" (006-004) — the language of faithfulness is deployed to describe early associations that shape character, suggesting that faith, like all moral dispositions, is formed through education and habit.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Eighteenth-century conduct literature demanded female chastity and fidelity as absolute, non-negotiable virtues while tacitly excusing male infidelity. Wollstonecraft's insistence on reciprocity — "who is faithful to her husband, and neither desires nor seeks to be more" (009-022) — reframes faithfulness as a mutual obligation of rational friendship. She anticipates the argument, developed more fully in her later work _Maria_, that women's fidelity is undermined by a marriage system that gives them no meaningful choice and no equality within marriage.

Her theological views — faith grounded in reason rather than authority — align her with rational Dissent and Enlightenment critiques of institutional religion, while her insistence on divine order as the foundation of moral law distinguishes her from more secular Enlightenment thinkers.
