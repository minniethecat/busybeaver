For Wollstonecraft, **employment** is not merely an economic concern but a moral and psychological necessity. She argues that "the employment of the thoughts shapes the character" (004-099) and that women's confinement to "trifling employments" (004-101) systematically degrades their intellectual and moral capacities. Meaningful employment — work that engages the understanding — is, in her framework, essential to the development of virtue.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft uses "employment" in a broad sense encompassing both paid work and purposeful activity that engages the mind. She contrasts "various employments and pursuits which engage the attention" of men (003-025) with the narrow, repetitive tasks assigned to women. Her definition is functional: an employment is valuable to the degree that it exercises reason and contributes to the formation of "the moral character" (002-058). Trifling employments, conversely, are those that "contract the faculties" (004-096) by demanding only mechanical repetition.

She also distinguishes between employment as occupation and employment as purposeful use of time. When she observes that women "have seldom sufficient serious employment to silence their feelings" (004-095), she identifies the psychological harm of idleness — the mind, finding "no employment" (004-058), turns inward to morbid sensibility. Employment, in this therapeutic sense, is the outward direction of mental energy toward rational objects.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's case for women's employment is built on the principle that "the character has ever been formed by the employments the individual, or class pursues" (003-060). This is a materialist argument avant la lettre: character is not innate but shaped by the activities that fill one's days. If women are confined to needlework and dressing, their characters will be correspondingly narrow; if they are given "various employments" that engage understanding, their characters will expand.

She repeatedly contrasts the intellectual effects of different kinds of work. Needlework, the quintessential female employment, "contracts their faculties more than any other" and "renders women a trifler" (004-101). By contrast, "business and other dry employments of the understanding" (006-005) are praised precisely because they demand sustained rational attention. The gendered division of labour is, for Wollstonecraft, a primary mechanism by which intellectual inequality is produced and reproduced.

The connection between employment and independence is central to her argument. She insists that women should "pursue with vigour the various employments which form the moral character" (002-058) and advocates for women's entry into professions — medicine, business, politics — that would give them economic independence and thus the material basis for moral autonomy. Without "sufficient serious employment" (004-095), women remain dependent on men not only economically but psychologically, their minds shaped by the need to please rather than the discipline of useful work.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Rousseau's *Emile* consigns Sophie to domestic employments that cultivate charm rather than understanding; Wollstonecraft directly counters this by insisting that employment must engage reason to be morally formative. She also challenges the conduct-book tradition — Gregory, Fordyce, and others — which assumed that women's natural sphere was domestic and decorative. For Wollstonecraft, this assumption is circular: women appear fitted only for domestic employment because they have been given no other, and their characters have been shaped by that confinement.

## Key Passages

> Men have various employments and pursuits which engage the attention, and give a character to the opening mind. (003-025)

> The employment of the thoughts shapes the character, if I may be allowed the expression; and trifling employments have rendered women a trifler. (004-099, 004-101)

> Few are the modes of earning a subsistence, and those very humiliating. Perhaps it is the very want of a proper spirit to pursue with vigour the various employments which form the moral character, that makes the generality of women so much more insignificant than they otherwise would be. (002-058)
