# Prejudice

The entrenched, unexamined beliefs that Wollstonecraft argues systematically obstruct reason, virtue, and the progress of women's rights.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft defines prejudice as belief without examination — convictions "imbibed" from custom rather than derived from reason (001-008). Prejudices are not merely individual errors but social structures: "deeply rooted prejudices have clouded reason" (001-007) across entire cultures and historical periods. She distinguishes prejudice from genuine principle, noting that principle "restrained by reason" is stable, while what "has only prejudices to rest on" will always be unstable (002-003).

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

The author argues that prejudice is the primary mechanism by which women's subordination is maintained. Men "employ their reason to justify prejudices, which they have imbibed" rather than to examine them (001-008). The "prevailing prejudice" about women's nature — that they are intellectually and morally inferior — is not a conclusion drawn from evidence but a pre-existing commitment that distorts all subsequent reasoning (001-001).

Wollstonecraft is particularly concerned with "prejudices that give a sex to virtue" (P03-013), the assumption that different moral standards apply to men and women. She calls on her readers to "not foster these prejudices, and they will naturally fall" (P03-014). The "firmly rooted prejudices which sensualists have planted" (003-040) must be actively eradicated through education and rational inquiry.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Where Edmund Burke and other conservatives celebrated "prejudice" as accumulated social wisdom, Wollstonecraft sees only intellectual laziness and self-interest. Her treatment anticipates later Enlightenment critiques of unreasoned tradition, but she uniquely applies this analysis to gender relations, showing how "sexual prejudices" (002-059) specifically harm women's intellectual and moral development.
