# Prejudice

## Definition

**Prejudice** is, for Wollstonecraft, the primary epistemological enemy of [[reason]] and the chief mechanism by which women's subordination is perpetuated. She uses the term not in the modern sense of personal bias or bigotry (though that is included) but in its Enlightenment sense: a prejudice is an opinion adopted without examination — a conclusion reached before (pre-) the exercise of judgment.

> "Such deeply rooted prejudices have clouded reason, and such spurious qualities have assumed the name of virtues." (001-007)

The insidious power of prejudice lies in its invisibility to those who hold it. People "seem to employ their [[reason]] to justify prejudices, which they have imbibed, they cannot trace how, rather than to root them out" (001-008) — a penetrating psychological observation that anticipates modern concepts of motivated [[reason]]ing. Prejudice operates as a closed loop: it pre-empts the rational inquiry that would expose it as unfounded.

## In Context

Prejudice is mobilised throughout the text as both an explanatory concept and a polemical target. In the Introduction, Wollstonecraft identifies prejudices about female [[nature]] as the obstacle her argument must overcome. Chapter I establishes the epistemological critique: prejudices "cloud" [[reason]] and make genuine [[knowledge]] impossible. Chapter II shows how prejudices about women's [[nature]] produce the very behaviour they claim merely to describe — a closed loop in which prejudice manufactures the evidence that appears to confirm it. Crucially, Wollstonecraft does not exempt women themselves from holding prejudices. Women internalise the prejudices of their culture — about their own [[nature]], their proper sphere, their [[duty|duties]] — and transmit them to their children, perpetuating the cycle across generations.

### The Epistemology of Prejudice

Wollstonecraft develops a systematic account of how prejudices function. Chapter I provides the theoretical framework. She begins with first principles — "The rights and duties of man thus simplified, it seems almost impertinent to attempt to illustrate truths that appear so incontrovertible" (001-007) — and then diagnoses the cognitive mechanisms that obscure those principles. Prejudices are not mere errors; they are actively maintained by "a kind of intellectual cowardice" that "makes many men shrink from the task" of forming their own principles (001-008).

The critique is not limited to explicit doctrines about women. Wollstonecraft identifies a broader pattern: "Thus expediency is continually contrasted with simple principles, till truth is lost in a mist of words, virtue in forms, and knowledge rendered a sounding nothing, by the specious prejudices that assume its name" (001-009). Prejudice is a systemic phenomenon, embedded in institutions, language, and the unexamined assumptions of an entire culture. The [[conduct-book]] tradition represents prejudice codified into a programme of [[education]].

Wollstonecraft's analysis has a dialectical quality. She shows how prejudice and [[nature]] function as mutually reinforcing concepts: a prejudice about women's nature (that they are intellectually weak) produces an [[education]] that keeps them weak, and the resulting weakness is then cited as proof that the prejudice was accurate — that nature itself ordained the difference. Breaking this cycle requires that "the mind must be strong that resolutely forms its own principles" (001-008).

### Prejudice Embodied in Social Practice

Prejudice is mobilised throughout the text as both an explanatory concept and a polemical target. In the Introduction, Wollstonecraft identifies prejudices about female [[nature]] as the obstacle her argument must overcome. Chapter I establishes the epistemological critique: prejudices "cloud" [[reason]] and make genuine [[knowledge]] impossible. Chapter II shows how prejudices about women's [[nature]] produce the very behaviour they claim merely to describe.

The Introduction also sounds a note of warning: "Why should it be increased by prejudices that give a sex to virtue, and confound simple truths with sensual reveries?" (P03-014). The phrase "give a sex to virtue" is central — it identifies the specific prejudice that Wollstonecraft considers most damaging. Once [[virtue]] itself is gendered, the claim that women lack certain virtues becomes self-fulfilling, because the virtues assigned to women — [[sensibility]], delicacy, sweetness — are defined in opposition to the rational virtues of courage, fortitude, and judgment.

Wollstonecraft does not exempt women themselves from holding prejudices. Women internalise the prejudices of their culture — about their own [[nature]], their proper sphere, their [[duty|duties]] — and transmit them to their children, perpetuating the cycle. "Women are, in fact, so much degraded by mistaken notions of female excellence" (P03-015) that they become agents of their own subordination. The remedy is not merely to expose individual prejudices but to restructure the conditions — above all, [[education]] — under which prejudices form and reproduce themselves.

## Key Passages

> "Such deeply rooted prejudices have clouded reason, and such spurious qualities have assumed the name of virtues." (001-007)

> "Men, in general, seem to employ their reason to justify prejudices, which they have imbibed, they cannot trace how, rather than to root them out." (001-008)

> "Thus expediency is continually contrasted with simple principles, till truth is lost in a mist of words, virtue in forms, and knowledge rendered a sounding nothing, by the specious prejudices that assume its name." (001-009)

> "I wish to persuade women to endeavour to acquire strength, both of mind and body, and to convince them that the soft phrases, susceptibility of heart, delicacy of sentiment, and refinement of taste, are almost synonymous with epithets of weakness." (P03-011)

> "Why should it be increased by prejudices that give a sex to virtue, and confound simple truths with sensual reveries?" (P03-014)

> "Women are, in fact, so much degraded by mistaken notions of female excellence, that I do not mean to add a paradox when I assert, that this artificial weakness produces a propensity to tyrannize." (P03-015)
