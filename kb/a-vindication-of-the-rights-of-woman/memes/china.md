# China

The ancient empire that Wollstonecraft invokes as an example of a civilization frozen by custom — a society where reverence for tradition has prevented the progress of reason and liberty.

## Location

Eighteenth-century Europeans knew China primarily through Jesuit missionaries' accounts and the reports of traders. China was admired by some Enlightenment thinkers as a model of rational governance and moral philosophy (Confucianism), while others saw it as an example of "Oriental despotism" — a static civilization unable to progress.

## Relevance to the Text

Wollstonecraft references China as part of her argument about the corrupting effects of unexamined tradition. A society that never questions its customs, she suggests, cannot progress — and the subjection of women in such societies is the most visible symptom of this stagnation. China serves as the extreme case of a civilization governed by "prejudices" rather than by reason, demonstrating the consequences of the intellectual servitude she critiques in European society.

### In the Argument

The reference to China supports Wollstonecraft's broader argument that customs which go unexamined become tyrannical. If European women's subjection is justified by custom rather than reason, European society risks the same stagnation she attributes to China. The solution — rational examination of all customs, including those governing gender — is the Enlightenment project she champions.

## Historical Context

Eighteenth-century European attitudes toward China were complex and contradictory. Voltaire admired Chinese civilization; Montesquieu used it as an example of despotism. Wollstonecraft's usage follows the critical tradition, treating China as a warning rather than a model.
