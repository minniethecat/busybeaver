**Politeness** is, for Wollstonecraft, the name of a systematic moral failure — the substitution of "punctilious politeness" (002-022) for genuine virtue, of external decorum for internal principle, of "the insinuating nothings of politeness" (006-012) for the direct language of truth. The *Vindication*'s critique of politeness is not a rejection of civility — Wollstonecraft is no advocate of rudeness — but an exposure of the moral economy by which the forms of respect are preserved while the substance is abandoned. Women are the principal victims of this economy, for they are taught to value politeness above sincerity and to mistake the ability to please for the capacity for virtue.

## Definition

Eighteenth-century Britain had developed an elaborate culture of politeness, theorised most influentially by the third Earl of Shaftesbury and Joseph Addison, in which refined manners were understood as the outward expression of inward virtue. The polite person was the person whose external conduct manifested internal moral cultivation — "politeness" was, in this tradition, not merely a set of behavioural conventions but a moral ideal.

Wollstonecraft's critique exposes the gap between this ideal and its social reality. In practice, she argues, politeness functions not as the expression of virtue but as its substitute. "Thus does politeness sport with truth" (008-002) — the verb "sport" is carefully chosen, suggesting a game, a diversion, a form of play that has no serious moral purpose. Politeness, in this usage, is what permits people to say what they do not mean and to mean what they do not say — a system of systematic insincerity that undermines the very virtues it purports to express.

The "lessons of politeness" (012-036) that form the core of women's education are lessons in concealment. A woman who has been taught politeness has been taught to hide her thoughts, to suppress her feelings, to present to the world a surface that bears no relation to her interior. This is not moral education but "that formulary of decorum" (012-036) — a training in the techniques of social performance that leaves the character untouched.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

The argument against politeness is inseparable from Wollstonecraft's larger argument about the corruption of language. Throughout the *Vindication*, she exposes the gap between words and their referents — between what is said and what is meant. "Innocence" means ignorance. "Sensibility" means sensuality. "Modesty" means prudery. And "politeness" means — not virtue, not civility, not respect — but the social performance of these things in their absence.

The gendered dimension of this critique is devastating. Women are "taught to please, and they only live to please" (002-022), and the art of pleasing is the art of politeness — the skill of making oneself agreeable regardless of what one thinks or feels. The "punctilious politeness" that women are taught is not a moral achievement but a form of servitude: it requires the constant suppression of the self in favour of the social performance that others demand. A woman who is always polite is a woman who is never herself.

Wollstonecraft contrasts politeness with sincerity — the virtue of saying what one means and meaning what one says. "I wish to speak the simple language of truth, and rather to address the head than the heart" (002-039) is her own rhetorical manifesto, and it is also her moral one. Sincerity is not compatible with politeness when politeness requires insincerity — and in Wollstonecraft's analysis, the politeness demanded of women always requires insincerity, for it demands that they present themselves as content with a condition that no rational being could accept.

The political dimension of the critique emerges in Wollstonecraft's analysis of the relation between politeness and power. "Every corps is a chain of despots and slaves" (001-027) — the observation is made in the context of military discipline, but it applies with equal force to the social world that politeness governs. Politeness is the lubricant of hierarchy: it makes subjection palatable by giving it the form of voluntary deference. The woman who is taught to be polite to her social superiors is being taught to accept her subordination as a matter of manners — and to mistake compliance for virtue.

The remedy is not rudeness — Wollstonecraft is no advocate of social disruption for its own sake — but sincerity governed by reason. The person who speaks "the simple language of truth" may sometimes offend, but the offence is committed in the service of a higher obligation: the obligation to be truthful rather than pleasing, to cultivate virtue rather than its appearance. "One would gladly give up politeness for a quarter of the virtue that has been sacrificed" (005-032) — the proportion is deliberately absurd, underscoring the disproportion between what politeness offers (the appearance of virtue) and what it demands (virtue itself).

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The eighteenth-century cult of politeness had its most influential expression in the periodical essays of Addison and Steele, whose *Spectator* papers had made the cultivation of polite manners a central project of Enlightenment civility. Politeness, in this tradition, was the mechanism by which the rough energies of commercial society were refined into the mutual respect that makes social life possible. It was a Whig virtue — the virtue of the coffeehouse and the drawing room, the virtue that distinguished the civilised from the barbarous.

Wollstonecraft's critique is not a rejection of this tradition but an exposure of its limits. The politeness that Addison celebrated was a politeness among equals — or at least among those who could imagine themselves as equals. The politeness demanded of women was politeness to superiors — the deference of the subordinate to the dominant. What the *Spectator* tradition presented as the universal language of civility was in fact the specific language of male sociability, and its extension to women transformed it from an expression of respect into an instrument of control.

Chesterfield's *Letters to His Son* — the most notorious eighteenth-century manual of politeness — is an implicit target. Chesterfield had taught that politeness was the art of pleasing, and that pleasing required the strategic concealment of one's true thoughts and feelings. Wollstonecraft's response is that this "art" is morally corrupting: it teaches the practitioner to value appearance over reality and to treat other people as audiences to be managed rather than as moral equals to be engaged. The "polite" man, in Chesterfield's sense, is a man who has learned to dissemble — and a society in which dissimulation is the price of social acceptance is a society in which virtue has been systematically undermined.

## Key Passages

> Every corps is a chain of despots and slaves. This servile obedience to the caprices of a superior, destroys, in the breast of inferiors, all sense of justice, which ought to regulate the relations they may hold with each other. (001-027)

> Where is then the sexual difference, when the education has been the same? All the difference that I can discern, arises from the superior advantage of liberty which enables the former to see more of life. (002-022)

> One would gladly give up politeness for a quarter of the virtue that has been sacrificed to it. (005-032)

> He who listens to the insinuating nothings of politeness, whilst they turn from the unabashed countenance of truth, will find no comfort in the upstart race. (006-012)

> Thus does politeness sport with truth, and eating away the substance, leaves only the shadow to mock the eager hand. (008-002)
