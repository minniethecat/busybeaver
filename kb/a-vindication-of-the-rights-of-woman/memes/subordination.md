**Subordination** in Wollstonecraft's *Vindication* is a structural concept — it names the principle of hierarchical ordering that governs relations between ranks, sexes, and institutions. She uses the term with deliberate ambivalence: subordination is sometimes a necessary feature of organised social life, but it is more often the mechanism by which power is concentrated and freedom is suppressed. The "great subordination of rank" (001-025) that constitutes the power of the military profession is her paradigmatic example of a hierarchical system that sacrifices individual development to collective order.

The concept is central to Wollstonecraft's analysis of gender relations. The subordination of women to men is not, she argues, a natural arrangement but a social construction — one that is "incompatible with freedom" (001-026) and that "cramps" the faculties of those subjected to it. Yet she also acknowledges that some forms of subordination — the "reasonable subordination" (012-075) of children to parents, or of individual interest to the common good — are necessary for moral and social life.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft draws on the political vocabulary of eighteenth-century radicalism, in which "subordination" was a term of contestation. Conservatives defended subordination as the natural foundation of social order — the great chain of being extended from heaven to earth. Radicals, drawing on Locke and the republican tradition, challenged subordination as an artifact of conquest and custom rather than a dictate of nature or reason.

Wollstonecraft's definition operates through a series of contrasts. Subordination is set against freedom: "subordination and rigour are the very sinews of" military discipline, and yet they are "incompatible with freedom" (001-026). Subordination is set against improvement: "subordination almost equally cramps their faculties" (001-029) — the subordination of soldiers, like that of women, prevents the full development of rational and moral capacities. Subordination is set against equality: "the line of subordination in the mental powers is never to be settled" (004-002) — any claim that one sex is naturally subordinate to the other in intellectual capacity is unfounded.

The distinction between necessary and arbitrary subordination is crucial. Necessary subordination — the obedience of a child to a parent, or of a citizen to just laws — is compatible with freedom because it serves the development of the subordinate party. Arbitrary subordination — the subjection of women to men, or of commoners to aristocrats — serves only the interests of the powerful.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument about subordination operates on two levels. On the first level, she offers a critique of specific forms of subordination — military, political, domestic — showing how each corrupts the character of both the subordinate and the superordinate. On the second level, she offers a philosophical analysis of subordination as such, asking what conditions must be met for hierarchical relations to be compatible with freedom and virtue.

The military example is the most fully developed. "In what respect are the soldiers of a standing army superior to women?" (001-029) The question is deliberately provocative. Soldiers, like women, are subjected to a system of subordination that "cramps their faculties"; they are taught obedience rather than reason, submission rather than independence. The military is Wollstonecraft's model of what subordination does to human beings: it produces a "servile" character, incapable of the independent judgment that virtue requires.

The domestic application follows directly. If the subordination of soldiers is incompatible with the development of free and virtuous citizens, then the subordination of women — which is more comprehensive and more intimate — must be even more destructive. Women are subordinated not merely in their public roles but in their private persons; their subordination extends to their bodies, their affections, their minds.

Yet Wollstonecraft does not reject subordination altogether. "Reasonable subordination" (012-075) — the loving deference of a child to wise parents, or the willing cooperation of citizens in a common project — is compatible with freedom because it is grounded in reason rather than force. The distinction between reasonable and unreasonable subordination is the distinction between authority that serves the development of the subordinate and authority that serves only itself.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The eighteenth-century defence of subordination drew on multiple traditions: the patriarchal theory of Filmer, the divine right of kings, the natural law theory of Pufendorf, and the emerging discourse of political economy that treated hierarchy as essential to productivity. In each tradition, subordination was naturalised — presented as a feature of the created order rather than a product of human choice.

Wollstonecraft challenges this naturalisation at its root. Subordination is not a feature of nature but of "rank" — a human institution, not a divine decree. "The line of subordination in the mental powers is never to be settled" (004-002): the claim that any group is naturally subordinate to any other in intellectual capacity is an assertion of power, not a finding of fact.

The contrast with Edmund Burke is implicit but significant. Burke's defence of "the decent drapery of life" — the hierarchical orderings that give society its shape and stability — is precisely the target of Wollstonecraft's critique. She does not deny that hierarchy can provide stability; she denies that stability is sufficient justification for the suppression of human capacities. A stable tyranny is still a tyranny.

## Key Passages

> Standing armies can never consist of resolute, robust men; they may be well disciplined machines, but they will seldom contain men under the influence of strong passions, or with very vigorous faculties. And as for any depth of understanding, or any extent of science, I will venture to affirm, that they are as rarely to be found in the army as amongst women. (001-025)

> Subordination and rigour are the very sinews of military discipline; but they are incompatible with freedom, because subordination, when it becomes a habit, narrows the mind, and the man who is taught to obey will seldom have the courage to think for himself. (001-026)

> It is of great importance to the happiness of mankind, that parents should teach their children to love with reasonable subordination their whole family, from the bosom of which they are never to be torn. (012-075)
