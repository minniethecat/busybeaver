Contentment, in Wollstonecraft's framework, is not the passive quiescence she associates with Swift's degraded Yahoos but an active moral achievement: the "sublime contentment which only morality can diffuse" through education and the harmonious exercise of duty and affection.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft distinguishes between two forms of contentment.

The first, which she rejects, is a degraded state of passivity — "making man rest in contentment" (005-139) without the exercise of reason or the cultivation of generous feelings. This she associates with Swift's satirical depiction of the Yahoos: creatures stripped of passion and reduced to mere instinct.

The second, which she affirms, is contentment as the product of moral agency — "true happiness, I mean all the contentment, and virtuous satisfaction that can be snatched in this imperfect state" (009-005). This positive contentment arises from the proper discharge of duty and the cultivation of "well regulated affections" (009-005).

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Contentment functions in Wollstonecraft's broader argument as a criterion for distinguishing genuine moral achievement from artificial social accommodation.

She insists that contentment cannot be achieved by conforming to the artificial expectations placed upon women. Rather, it requires the active exercise of reason and the fulfillment of duty.

When women are educated only to please, "natural and artificial duties clash" (009-005), making genuine contentment impossible. The woman sacrifices "the comfort and respectability of a woman's life to voluptuous notions of beauty" (009-005).

The political dimension emerges most clearly in her educational proposals. In Chapter 12, she envisions education producing "the general diffusion of that sublime contentment which only morality can diffuse" (012-096). This contentment arises when individuals become "more like moral agents" (012-096) through institutions that cultivate reason and virtue.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft departs sharply from the Stoic tradition of resignation, critiquing it through her reference to Swift's Houyhnhnms — the rational horses whose insipid contentment she finds philosophically inadequate (005-139).

She also implicitly counters Rousseau's vision of female contentment within domestic subordination. Where Rousseau would have women find satisfaction in pleasing men, Wollstonecraft insists that contentment must be grounded in rational self-cultivation and the discharge of duty understood as moral obligation.

## Key Passages

> Who that has read Dean Swift's disgusting description of the Yahoos, and insipid one of Houyhnhnm with a philosophical eye, can avoid seeing the futility of degrading the passions, or making man rest in contentment? (005-139)

> True happiness, I mean all the contentment, and virtuous satisfaction that can be snatched in this imperfect state, must arise from well regulated affections; and an affection includes a duty. (009-005)

> When I wish to see my sex become more like moral agents, my heart bounds with the anticipation of the general diffusion of that sublime contentment which only morality can diffuse. (012-096)
