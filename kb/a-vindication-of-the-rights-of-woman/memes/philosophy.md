Philosophy, for Wollstonecraft, encompasses both natural philosophy (what we would now call science) and moral philosophy (the rational investigation of duty and virtue). In both senses, it is the antidote to the "useless arts" (003-011) to which women's education is typically confined.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft uses "philosophy" in the broad eighteenth-century sense.

It includes "experimental philosophy" (004-097) — the empirical investigation of the natural world — and "the severer principles of reason and philosophy" (003-011) that discipline the mind in moral reasoning. In her educational programme, "some simple experiments in natural philosophy" (012-050) are part of the daily curriculum for both sexes.

Philosophy is thus not a specialised academic discipline but a general habit of rational inquiry accessible to all — including, crucially, women.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

The absence of philosophical education for women is, in Wollstonecraft's analysis, a structural cause of their degradation.

Instead of "hardening their minds by the severer principles of reason and philosophy, we breed them to useless arts, which terminate in vanity and sensuality" (003-011). The verb "harden" is significant: philosophical study is not decorative but disciplinary.

The positive case for philosophy appears in Chapter 4, where Wollstonecraft contrasts the empty occupations of English women with the more substantial conversation of French women. She recommends that "gardening, experimental philosophy, and literature, would afford them subjects to think of" (004-097).

Her concrete educational proposals in Chapter 12 return to philosophy as curriculum: in her coeducational day-school, "reading, writing, arithmetic, natural history, and some simple experiments in natural philosophy, might fill up the day" (012-050). The phrase "simple experiments" is deliberately democratic.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft's insistence on philosophy for women was radical.

Rousseau's Sophie, the model woman of *Émile*, is explicitly denied philosophical education: her mind is cultivated only enough to make her pleasing to men.

Wollstonecraft inverts this: it is precisely because women's "domestic comforts and the education of our children must depend" (003-011) on their character that they require philosophy. The domestic sphere, far from exempting women from rational inquiry, demands it.

## Key Passages

> Instead of hardening their minds by the severer principles of reason and philosophy, we breed them to useless arts, which terminate in vanity and sensuality. (003-011)

> Gardening, experimental philosophy, and literature, would afford them subjects to think of, and matter for conversation, that in some degree would exercise their understandings. (004-097)

> Reading, writing, arithmetic, natural history, and some simple experiments in natural philosophy, might fill up the day; but these pursuits should never encroach on gymnastic plays in the open air. (012-050)
