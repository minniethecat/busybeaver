The virtue of diligent, sustained effort directed toward productive ends. Wollstonecraft presents industry as both a moral virtue—essential to the formation of character—and an economic necessity for women who would otherwise be "hanging their heads surcharged with the dew of sensibility" (009-034). She contrasts the productive industry that builds independence with the decorative "arts of industry"—needlework and accomplishments—that keep women occupied but dependent (005-030).

## Definition

Wollstonecraft uses "industry" in two distinct but related senses. In its moral sense, industry is the habit of active exertion that builds character and prevents the vices that arise from idleness. "Unremitting industry will mostly reach its goal in all that is valuable," she writes, placing industry among the cardinal virtues (008-015). In its economic sense, industry is the capacity to earn one's own living—the practical competence that makes women independent of male support and therefore capable of genuine moral choice.

Both senses converge in her educational program. The schools she proposes would teach girls "the business of life" alongside boys, cultivating habits of industry that serve both moral and economic ends. Girls who learn to support themselves acquire not merely a trade but the psychological independence that virtue requires; they are no longer "obliged to marry for a support" (009-034).

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument for industry proceeds from a diagnosis of women's economic dependency. As long as women's only respectable career is marriage, they will cultivate the arts of pleasing rather than the virtues of productive citizenship. "How many women," she asks, "thus waste life away the prey of discontent?" (009-034). The solution is not moral exhortation but economic opportunity: "I wish to see women placed in a station in which they would advance, instead of retarding, the progress of those glorious principles that give a substance to morality."

She distinguishes productive industry from the decorative "industry" of the needle. Women "confined to the arts of industry and the needle" (005-030) are not truly industrious but merely occupied—their efforts produce ornament, not independence. True industry contributes to the public good and builds the character of the agent. It is through "knowledge, industry, patience, by self-denial" (004-027) that women, like men, cultivate the virtues of citizenship. "Knowledge, industry, valour, and beneficence," she writes, "treated with the same respect in both sexes" (004-028) is her vision of a just society.

The class dimension is significant: Wollstonecraft's advocacy for women's industry is directed primarily at middle-class women who, unlike the laboring poor, are educated into idleness. She notes that "the reward of the individual's own industry" (005-147) is a principle recognized for men but denied to women of her class.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Eighteenth-century moralists praised industry but applied it selectively. For working-class women, industry meant manual labor—a necessity, not a virtue. For middle-class women, "industry" meant decorative accomplishment—samplers and embroidery that signified refinement without generating independence. Wollstonecraft cuts through this class-gender knot by insisting on a single standard: industry must produce genuine independence of mind and circumstance. Neither the drudgery of the poor nor the "accomplishments" of the rich qualifies as true industry in her sense.

## Key Passages

> How many women thus waste life away the prey of discontent, who might have practiced as physicians, regulated a farm, managed a shop, and stood erect, supported by their own industry, instead of hanging their heads surcharged with the dew of sensibility. (009-034)

> But the treatment of women, or the care which they receive, sufficiently proves how far this is the case. The arguments they use are as void of consistency as they: founded on prejudices, that knowledge, industry, valour, and beneficence, treated with the same respect in both sexes, would only render women more masculine. (004-028)

> Unremitting industry will mostly reach its goal in all that is valuable; and, if we do not reach the goal, it is because we desist from the pursuit. (008-015)
