# Corruption

The systemic moral and social decay that Wollstonecraft identifies as the inevitable consequence of unjust power relations—particularly the subordination of women—which spreads "through the whole mass of society" (P03-006) and undermines both individual virtue and political health.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's concept of corruption operates at a structural rather than merely individual level. When she warns that the denial of education and rational autonomy to women will "spread corruption through the whole mass of society" (P03-006), she is articulating a causal chain: unequal institutions produce distorted characters, which in turn reproduce the inequalities that formed them. Corruption, in her analysis, is not a discrete moral failing but a systemic condition in which the very structures of social life—marriage, education, politics—are organised around principles contrary to virtue. She observes that those who benefit from unjust power are "compelled, to make covert corruption hold fast the power" (001-033) they possess, suggesting that inequality generates its own self-perpetuating logic. The corrupt individual does not simply make bad choices; she or he is formed by corrupt institutions and in turn reinforces them.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft develops her analysis of corruption along two intersecting axes: the corruption of women by their dependent condition, and the corruption of the broader social order by the injustice of gender hierarchy. Women are corrupted, she argues, not because they are innately morally weaker than men but because they are systematically denied the education that would cultivate rational virtue. Taught to value beauty, charm, and compliance over understanding and principle, women become "the slaves of casual lust" (008-019)—not through innate depravity but through the operation of a system that rewards their moral diminishment.

Crucially, Wollstonecraft insists that this corruption is symmetrical: men are also corrupted by the power they exercise over women. The man who demands flattery rather than rational companionship from his wife "holds fast the power" (001-033) that the system grants him, but at the cost of his own moral development. A being who never encounters rational opposition, who is surrounded by "obsequious" (008-019) inferiors, cannot develop the habits of justice and self-examination that genuine virtue requires. Wollstonecraft's treatment of corruption thus anticipates later structural analyses of oppression: the master is deformed by mastery no less than the slave is deformed by servitude.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The dominant moral discourse of Wollstonecraft's time treated corruption primarily as a problem of individual vice—the result of weak character, bad influences, or (in women's case) natural moral frailty. Rousseau's account of the corruptions of civilisation in the *Discourse on Inequality* shares Wollstonecraft's concern with systemic decay but locates its origin in the invention of property rather than in gender hierarchy. Wollstonecraft's distinctive contribution is to identify the subordination of women as a primary vector of social corruption, arguing that no society can be virtuous while half its members are systematically denied the conditions of moral agency. Her analysis thus links the reform of female education directly to the project of political regeneration.

## Key Passages

> I wish also to steer clear of an error, which many respectable writers have fallen into; for the instruction which has hitherto been addressed to women, has rather been applicable to LADIES ... and the surest route for the exercise of all the social virtues is to do good to all God's creatures from the foundation of virtue, and spread corruption through the whole mass of society. (P03-006)

> But clashing interests soon losing their equipoise, a monarchy and hierarchy break out of the confusion of ambitious struggles, and the foundation of both is secured by feudal tenures. This appears to be the origin of monarchial and priestly power, and the dawn of civilization. But such combustible materials cannot long be pent up. (001-033)

> This sympathy extends still further, till a man well pleased observes force in arguments that do not carry conviction to his own bosom, and he gladly places in the fairest light to himself, the shows of reason that have led others astray, rejoiced to find some reason in all the errors of man. (008-019)
