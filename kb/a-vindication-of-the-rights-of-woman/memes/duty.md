# Duty

## Definition

**Duty** in Wollstonecraft's framework is not a matter of [[obedience|blind obedience]] to social convention but a rational moral obligation that can only be properly discharged when the agent understands *why* it is binding. "Unless [[liberty|freedom]] strengthen her [[reason]] till she comprehend her duty, and see in what manner it is connected with her real good," a woman cannot be said to act morally — she merely conforms.

> "Unless freedom strengthen her reason till she comprehend her duty, and see in what manner it is connected with her real good." (P02-006)

This is a radical redefinition: duty is not what society tells you to do (obey your husband, please your father, be modest, be quiet) but what [[reason]] reveals as the moral course of action. The "duties" imposed on women — what Wollstonecraft calls the "convenient" duties — are often at odds with genuine moral duty, because they demand compliance rather than [[knowledge|understanding]].

Wollstonecraft grounds her conception of duty in a universal moral law accessible to reason. She insists that "there can be but one rule of right, if morality has an eternal foundation" [002-082], and that anyone "whose duty it is to act in such a manner, lives only for the passing day, and cannot be an accountable creature" [002-082]. The universality of moral law is essential to her argument: if duties vary by sex, then [[morality]] itself is relative, and the concept of [[virtue]] loses all meaning. The radical implication is that the duties assigned to women by custom — obedience, pleasing, self-concealment — have no moral force whatsoever unless they can be justified by [[reason]].

> "Surely there can be but one rule of right, if morality has an eternal foundation, and whoever sacrifices virtue, strictly so called, to present convenience, or whose duty it is to act in such a manner, lives only for the passing day, and cannot be an accountable creature." (002-082)

### The Rational Foundation of Duty

Wollstonecraft's most direct challenge to the conventional morality of her time comes in the form of a question addressed to [[charles-maurice-de-talleyrand-perigord|Talleyrand]]: "you will not assert, that a duty can be binding which is not founded on reason?" [P02-010]. This is not merely a rhetorical question but a philosophical gauntlet. If duties must be founded on [[reason]], then every duty imposed on women must be subjected to rational scrutiny. The duties of obedience to husbands, of pleasing men, of accepting exclusion from public life — all of these must either be justified by reason or discarded as illegitimate impositions.

> "…you will not assert, that a duty can be binding which is not founded on reason?" (P02-010)

The test of rational foundation is connected to Wollstonecraft's broader critique of prescription — the notion that duties are binding simply because they have always been observed. She observes "an indolent propensity in man to make prescription always take place of reason, and to place every duty on an arbitrary foundation" [011-002]. The parallel she draws between patriarchal and political authority is deliberate: just as "the rights of kings are deduced in a direct line from the King of kings," so the duties of women are justified by appeal to tradition rather than reason. Both justifications collapse under rational scrutiny.

> "There seems to be an indolent propensity in man to make prescription always take place of reason, and to place every duty on an arbitrary foundation." (011-002)

### Reciprocity of Rights and Duties

A crucial element of Wollstonecraft's theory is the reciprocity between rights and duties. She argues that "a right always includes a duty, and I think it may, likewise fairly be inferred, that they forfeit the right, who do not fulfil the duty" [011-017]. This principle cuts both ways: parents who fail in their duty to educate their children forfeit their right to obedience; husbands who tyrannize over their wives forfeit the right to their service; society that denies women their [[rights]] cannot legitimately demand that they perform their duties.

> "…a right always includes a duty, and I think it may, likewise fairly be inferred, that they forfeit the right, who do not fulfil the duty." (011-017)

The reciprocity principle has profound implications for Wollstonecraft's political argument. If women have duties — and Wollstonecraft insists they do, particularly the duty of motherhood — then they must have the corresponding rights, including the right to [[education]] and the right to develop their [[reason]]. To demand the performance of duties while denying the rights that make their performance possible is not merely inconsistent but tyrannical.

## In Context

The Dedication sets up the duty question by asking [[charles-maurice-de-talleyrand-perigord|Talleyrand]] to consider whether "a duty can be binding which is not founded on [[reason]]" [P02-010]. The implication is that the duties assigned to women — domestic subordination, sexual [[modesty]] defined by reputation, the obligation to please — fail this test. Wollstonecraft pointedly notes that women, "whilst they are only made to acquire personal accomplishments," will neglect "private duties only to disturb, by cunning tricks, the orderly plans of reason which rise above their comprehension" [P02-012]. The neglect of duty is not a failure of female character but a predictable consequence of an [[education]] that never teaches women what genuine duty requires.

> "…women cannot, by force, be confined to domestic concerns; for they will however ignorant, intermeddle with more weighty affairs, neglecting private duties…" (P02-012)

Chapter III develops the argument around motherhood: "A double duty devolves on her; to educate their children, and to form their principles" [003-038]. Being a mother is a genuine duty, Wollstonecraft argues, but one that can only be performed properly by a woman whose own [[reason]] has been cultivated. She elaborates this point in Chapter X: "the care of children in their infancy is one of the grand duties annexed to the female character by nature, this duty would afford many forcible arguments for strengthening the female understanding, if it were properly considered" [010-006]. The duty of motherhood, far from being an argument for keeping women in ignorance, is the strongest argument for educating them.

> "As the care of children in their infancy is one of the grand duties annexed to the female character by nature, this duty would afford many forcible arguments for strengthening the female understanding, if it were properly considered." (010-006)

The contradictory demands on women — be a good mother but don't think too much, educate your children but remain ignorant yourself — expose the incoherence of conventional duty discourse.

### Duty Beyond Reputation

Wollstonecraft devotes sustained attention to the way "duty" is reduced to mere reputation among women. She describes women "who, if they did not love their husbands, loved nobody else, giving themselves entirely up to vanity and dissipation, neglecting every domestic duty" [008-009], yet who "plumed themselves on their unsullied reputation, as if the whole compass of their duty as wives and mothers was only to preserve it" [008-009]. This reduction of duty to reputation is a recurring theme: when women are taught that their worth lies in what others think of them rather than in what they actually are, they substitute the appearance of duty for its substance.

> "…neglecting every domestic duty; nay, even squandering away all the money which should have been saved for their helpless younger children, yet have plumed themselves on their unsullied reputation…" (008-009)

The elevation of reputation over genuine duty is reinforced by male writers, including [[rousseau|Rousseau]], who "declares, 'that reputation is no less indispensable than chastity'" [008-010] for women. Wollstonecraft's response is to insist that "weak minds are always fond of resting in the ceremonials of duty, but morality offers much simpler motives" [008-010]. The ceremonial of duty — the outward performance of propriety — is a substitute for the real thing, and an [[education]] that prizes appearances over substance produces women who are incapable of distinguishing between them.

> "Weak minds are always fond of resting in the ceremonials of duty, but morality offers much simpler motives…" (008-010)
