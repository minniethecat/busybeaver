# Duty

In _[[A Vindication of the Rights of Woman]]_, **duty** is radically redefined by [[Mary Wollstonecraft]] as moral obligation grounded in rational comprehension, not blind compliance with social convention.

This reframing opposes the conduct-book tradition that prescribed women's duties as obedience and domestic service. Wollstonecraft insists that "a duty can[not] be binding which is not founded on reason" (P02-013) — a principle that elevates duty from mechanical performance to an act of moral agency requiring [[Reason]] and [[Virtue]].

The concept pervades the entire work: "duty" appears in over fifty passages across all thirteen chapters. Wollstonecraft's treatment of duty connects directly to her arguments for [[Education]], [[Independence]], and her critique of [[Sensibility]] as an inadequate moral guide.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft distinguishes genuine duty from mere social prescription through a rationalist criterion: a duty is binding only if its bearer can comprehend why. She writes:

> "Unless freedom strengthen her reason till she comprehend her duty, and see in what manner it is connected with her real good, she will never become a virtuous woman" (P02-007)

This formulation ties duty inextricably to reason. Without understanding, compliance is mere obedience — not morality. Wollstonecraft insists that "you will not assert, that a duty can be binding which is not founded on reason" (P02-013). The implication is revolutionary: women must be educated to reason before they can be held morally accountable for their duties.

The author further argues that comprehension creates attachment: "unless they comprehend their duty, they cannot be attached to it" (P02-014). This psychological insight grounds duty in cognitive engagement rather than passive acceptance.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

### The Double Duty

Wollstonecraft is particularly concerned with the "double duty" imposed on women. She observes that a mother bears responsibility for the moral education of her children while being denied education herself:

> "A double duty devolves on her; to educate their children" (003-045)

Women are expected "to fulfil the double duty of being the father as well as the mother" (003-054) in shaping their children's moral character, yet are given none of the intellectual resources that fathers possess.

This contradiction renders women's performance of duty merely mechanical rather than genuinely moral. The double duty becomes a structural injustice: society demands what it has made impossible.

### Duty as Self-Cultivation

Wollstonecraft insists that duty is not self-sacrifice for its own sake but the rational exercise of moral capacity. She declares that "the practice of every duty be subordinate to the grand one of improving our own faculties" (005-051).

This priority — self-improvement over social performance — challenges the domestic ideology that defined women's primary duty as service to others.

She critiques the reduction of women's duty to domestic trivia. The woman who believes that, by "mending children's clothes, she does her duty" (004-096) has mistaken housekeeping for moral life. Genuine duty requires intellectual engagement, not a checklist of domestic tasks.

### Duty vs. Proxy Performance

Wollstonecraft observes that "in the superiour ranks of life, every duty is done by deputies, as if duties could be waived" (009-028). The wealthy delegate child-rearing to nurses, evading "the indispensable duty of a mother" (009-005).

This proxy culture empties duty of its moral content, reducing it to a transaction — a critique applicable to contemporary debates about parental responsibility as much as to eighteenth-century aristocratic life.

### Parental Duty

The later chapters develop duty in the context of parenting. Wollstonecraft argues that parental duty must be founded on reason, not arbitrary authority: "if parents discharge their duty they have a strong hold" (011-002) on their children's moral development.

She rejects "the absurd duty, too often inculcated, of obeying a parent only on account of his being a parent" (011-003), insisting that duty flows from rational obligation, not brute hierarchy.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

Duty occupies a central position in Wollstonecraft's moral architecture. It is the bridge between [[Reason]] (which comprehends obligation) and [[Virtue]] (which duty serves). Without reason, duty becomes superstition; without virtue, it becomes empty ritual.

The concept stands in productive tension with [[Sensibility]]: Wollstonecraft critiques the assumption that women's "natural" sensibility equips them for moral duty. Feeling alone cannot discharge a duty — only reason can. She explicitly warns that "men of sensibility" risk substituting emotional response for moral obligation (008-032).

Duty also connects to [[Education]]: women cannot fulfill duties they have not been taught to understand. The entire argument for female education rests on the premise that rational beings must comprehend their obligations.
