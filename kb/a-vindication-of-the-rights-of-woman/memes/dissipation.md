## Definition

Dissipation in Wollstonecraft's moral vocabulary refers to the dispersal of mental and moral energies in frivolous social pursuits — the round of fashionable visiting, entertainments, and amusements that consume women's time and attention, leaving no space for serious reflection or self-improvement. The term carries eighteenth-century connotations of moral dissolution and waste: to be dissipated is not merely to be busy with trivialities but to have one's character progressively eroded by them. Wollstonecraft uses dissipation as a key diagnostic concept for understanding how the social organisation of women's lives systematically prevents the cultivation of virtue and reason.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft presents dissipation as both a cause and a consequence of women's degraded condition. Women are driven to dissipation by an education that gives them no resources for serious occupation, and dissipation in turn further erodes whatever capacity for rational thought and moral seriousness they might possess. She describes the woman who "gives herself entirely up to vanity and dissipation, neglecting every domestic duty" (008-009), framing dissipation as the abandonment of responsibility in favour of empty pleasure-seeking.

The corporeal dimension of dissipation is significant in Wollstonecraft's analysis. She describes the dissipated woman as having "nerveless limbs" (009-002), suggesting that dissipation has physical as well as moral consequences — the body, like the mind, is weakened by frivolous pursuits. This aligns with her broader body-mind philosophy: just as physical exercise strengthens the body and thereby the mind, dissipation weakens both. In her discussion of national education, Wollstonecraft critiques the "total dissipation and beastly indulgence" (012-006) that characterises leisure time under current social arrangements, contrasting it with the rational recreation that a reformed education would provide. The "immoderate fondness for dress and dissipation" (012-045) that she attributes to mothers distracts from the serious work of child-rearing, perpetuating the cycle of degraded education across generations.

## Key Passages

> (008-009)... gives herself entirely up to vanity and dissipation, neglecting every domestic duty...

Wollstonecraft pairs dissipation with vanity as the twin corrupters of female character. The woman who surrenders to dissipation abandons her domestic responsibilities, undermining the very role that society claims to value in women. The passage appears in her discussion of how sexual notions undermine morality.

> (009-002)... dragging from one scene of dissipation to another the nerveless limbs...

Wollstonecraft's corporeal language — "nerveless limbs" — emphasises the physical debilitation produced by dissipation. The dissipated woman is not merely frivolous but physically weakened, her body as degraded as her character by a life of empty amusements.

> (012-006)... when they arrive they are spent in total dissipation and beastly indulgence.

In her chapter on national education, Wollstonecraft contrasts the healthy, rational recreation that a reformed education would provide with the dissipation and indulgence that characterise the leisure of the uneducated. The phrase "beastly indulgence" links dissipation to the animal appetites that reason should govern.

> (012-045)... immoderate fondness for dress and dissipation carries the mother of a family...

Wollstonecraft argues that a mother's absorption in dress and dissipation prevents her from fulfilling the educational duties of parenthood. Dissipation is not merely a personal failing but a social harm, as it corrupts the education of the next generation.
