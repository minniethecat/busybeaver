**Reformation** in Wollstonecraft's usage denotes the thoroughgoing transformation of individuals, manners, and institutions — the process by which moral and social corruption is corrected and genuine virtue restored. It is a concept poised between the religious discourse of moral reformation and the Enlightenment discourse of social and political reform. Wollstonecraft's deployment of the term reflects her conviction that the condition of women requires not incremental improvement but fundamental reformation — of education, of manners, of the social relations between the sexes.

The concept carries a distinctive urgency in the work. Reformation is "barely possible" (006-019) once corrupt habits have been consolidated by time — yet it is precisely this reformation that Wollstonecraft demands. The tension between the difficulty of reformation and its necessity is one of the work's driving rhetorical energies.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's concept of reformation operates on multiple levels. At the individual level, reformation is the moral transformation of character — the process by which a person corrupted by bad education and artificial manners is restored to genuine virtue. At the social level, reformation is the restructuring of institutions, customs, and relations that produce and reproduce corruption. These levels are interdependent: individual reformation requires social reformation as its condition of possibility, and social reformation requires individual reformation as its agent.

The concept draws on the Protestant discourse of "reformation of manners" — the campaign to restore moral discipline to society through the reform of individual conduct. But Wollstonecraft inflects this discourse in a radical direction. The reformation she demands is not the restoration of a lost moral order but the creation of a new one — an order in which women are educated to reason and virtue rather than to the "system of dissimulation" (005-086) that passes for female propriety.

The passage at 006-019 is crucial for understanding Wollstonecraft's conception of the difficulty of reformation. "When the habits of weak people are consolidated by time, a reformation is barely possible; and actually makes the beings miserable who have not sufficient mind to be amused by innocent pleasure." Reformation is "barely possible" not because it is intrinsically impossible but because the consolidation of habit creates a powerful inertial resistance. The "weak people" whose habits have been "consolidated by time" are not morally culpable — they are victims of a bad education whose effects cannot easily be undone.

The theological dimension appears at 013-022, where Wollstonecraft discusses punishment as a vehicle of reformation. "Whatever punishment may follow, will tend, like the anguish of disease, to show the malignity of vice, for the purpose of reformation." Here reformation is a divine purpose — the benevolent design of a Deity who punishes not for retribution but for correction. The analogy with disease is significant: punishment, like the "anguish of disease," is diagnostic and curative rather than punitive. Reformation is not imposed from without but elicited from within — the natural response of a rational being to the recognition of error.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Reformation is the work's ultimate horizon. Wollstonecraft's critique of the condition of women is not merely diagnostic — it is directed toward the reformation of that condition. The specific reforms she advocates — rational education, the opening of respectable professions to women, the restructuring of marriage — are instances of the broader reformation she envisions: a reformation of the relations between the sexes grounded in the recognition of women's rational and moral equality.

The political stakes are made explicit in the discussion of resistance to reform. "These are the people who most strenuously insist on the will of the founder being observed, crying out against all reformation, as if it were a violation of justice" (012-011). The targets are those who defend established institutions — in this passage, endowed charities — against reform by invoking the sanctity of the founder's will. Wollstonecraft's response is that the "will of the founder" is not sacred; institutions exist to serve present purposes, not to memorialise past intentions. The "apprehensive timidity of indolent slugs" who resist reformation is a moral failing — the refusal to discharge present duties in deference to obsolete arrangements.

This passage illuminates Wollstonecraft's broader theory of reformation. Institutions, customs, and social arrangements are human creations, not natural facts. They are subject to rational criticism and, when they fail to serve genuine human goods, to reformation. The resistance to reformation — the "fear of innovation" (012-011) — is not prudence but cowardice, the preference for the familiar over the good.

The relationship between individual and social reformation is dialectical. Individual reformation — the cultivation of reason and virtue in particular persons — is the means by which social reformation is achieved. But social reformation — the restructuring of the conditions under which individuals are formed — is the condition of individual reformation's success. A vicious circle: corrupt institutions produce corrupt individuals, who in turn perpetuate corrupt institutions. Wollstonecraft's project is to break this circle — to begin the reformation of institutions through the reformation of the individuals who inhabit them, and to make possible the reformation of individuals through the reformation of the institutions that shape them.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The eighteenth century witnessed a vigorous discourse of "reformation" — of manners, of morals, of institutions. The Society for the Reformation of Manners, founded in the 1690s, had sought to enforce moral discipline through legal prosecution. The broader Enlightenment discourse of reform — associated with figures such as Beccaria, Bentham, and the French *philosophes* — treated social institutions as amenable to rational redesign.

Wollstonecraft's contribution to this discourse is distinctive in its focus on gender. The reformation she demands is not merely of legal institutions but of the most intimate relations of social life — the education of children, the conduct of marriage, the manners that govern the interaction of the sexes. And it is distinctive in its insistence that reformation must be grounded in the recognition of women's rational equality. A reformation that leaves the subordination of women intact is not a genuine reformation.

The contrast with Burke is particularly illuminating. Burke's *Reflections on the Revolution in France* (1790) had defended established institutions against revolutionary reform, arguing that gradual, organic change was superior to wholesale reformation. Wollstonecraft's insistence on the necessity of reformation — even when it makes "the beings miserable who have not sufficient mind" (006-019) — is a direct rejoinder to Burkean conservatism. The preservation of corrupt institutions is not prudence but complicity in the corruption they produce.
