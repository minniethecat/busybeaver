## Definition

Cleanliness in Wollstonecraft's usage extends beyond mere physical tidiness to encompass a moral and rational quality of mind. She treats bodily cleanliness as a habitual practice that cultivates order, self-respect, and mental clarity — a physical virtue that parallels and supports the cultivation of reason. Drawing on Enlightenment associations between bodily discipline and mental order, Wollstonecraft presents cleanliness as a visible sign of rational self-governance, particularly important for women whose domestic roles make them responsible for the physical environment of the household.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft advocates for cleanliness as an integral component of women's education, linking it directly to the cultivation of reason and self-respect. In a striking passage, she declares that she would prescribe cleanliness, neatness, and personal reserve as the foundation of female virtue (007-028), elevating what might seem a mundane domestic concern to a moral imperative. She distinguishes the "luxury of cleanliness" from the corrupting luxury of wealth and fashion (009-007), arguing that a clean and orderly domestic environment contributes to dignity and rational living rather than superficial display. Wollstonecraft further connects cleanliness to mental order through the metaphor of "cleanliness of mind" (012-036), suggesting that the discipline of maintaining bodily and domestic cleanliness cultivates analogous habits of intellectual order. She pairs cleanliness with exercise as the fundamental means of preserving health (012-068), grounding her argument in the body-mind connection that runs throughout her educational philosophy.

Wollstonecraft also employs cleanliness as a point of critique against religious sects that overvalue ritual purity while neglecting genuine moral cultivation (007-025). For Wollstonecraft, cleanliness is not a matter of superstitious observance but a rational practice that reflects and reinforces the dignity of the reasoning being. Her inclusion of "sacred respect for cleanliness" alongside delicacy in domestic life (P02-005) in the dedication to Talleyrand signals the seriousness with which she treats this apparently minor virtue.

## Key Passages

> (007-028) I should instantly exclaim, cleanliness, neatness, and personal reserve...

Wollstonecraft's most direct statement on the moral importance of cleanliness, prescribing it as foundational to female character alongside neatness and personal reserve. The context is her argument that women should cultivate virtues grounded in reason rather than the superficial graces demanded by social convention.

> (012-036)... the sober effect of cleanliness of mind...

Here Wollstonecraft extends the concept of cleanliness from the physical to the mental domain, arguing that the discipline of bodily cleanliness produces a corresponding orderliness of thought. This passage appears in her discussion of national education, reinforcing the idea that physical habits shape intellectual character.

> (012-068) Exercise and cleanliness appear to be not only the surest means of preserving health...

Wollstonecraft pairs cleanliness with exercise as the twin pillars of physical education, arguing that both are essential for preserving the health that underlies all intellectual and moral development. This passage reflects her broader argument that the body must be properly cared for if the mind is to flourish.

> (009-007)... with only the luxury of cleanliness, to receive her husband...

Wollstonecraft distinguishes the "luxury of cleanliness" from the corrupting luxuries of fashion and display. Cleanliness represents a legitimate and virtuous form of domestic comfort, one that contributes to the dignity of the household rather than to vain display.
