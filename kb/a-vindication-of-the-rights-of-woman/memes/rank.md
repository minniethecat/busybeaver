# Rank

## Definition

**Rank** in Wollstonecraft's social analysis is a system of hierarchical distinction that corrupts those it elevates and degrades those it subordinates. She draws an explicit parallel between hereditary aristocracy and the gendered hierarchy: both are systems in which position is inherited rather than earned, and both produce the same moral deformities — pride without merit, [[power]] without [[virtue]], and the systematic confusion of social status with moral worth.

The critique of rank serves a strategic purpose in Wollstonecraft's argument. By linking the subordination of women to the broader critique of hereditary privilege — a critique widely shared among Enlightenment thinkers — she positions feminism as a natural extension of progressive political thought rather than a departure from it.

## In Context

### The Parallel Between Sex and Rank

Wollstonecraft's most powerful analytical move in discussing rank is the parallel she draws between the condition of women and the condition of the hereditary aristocracy. Both are born into a station they did not choose; both are educated to value appearance over substance; both are corrupted by the absence of genuine [[duty]] and the presence of unearned privilege. The critique of rank thus becomes a critique of gender: to oppose hereditary aristocracy while defending male supremacy is to be guilty of a fundamental inconsistency.

### Rank and Character

Wollstonecraft argues that rank — like gender — is a system that substitutes external position for internal character. A person of rank is treated with deference regardless of personal merit, just as a man is accorded authority regardless of his actual [[wisdom]]. This substitution of position for virtue degrades everyone involved: the powerful are denied the incentive to develop genuine merit, and the subordinate are denied the recognition that merit deserves.
