# Rank

In _[[A Vindication of the Rights of Woman]]_, **rank** is the social hierarchy that organizes society by birth rather than merit. [[Mary Wollstonecraft]] argues that rank is incompatible with [[Virtue]]: "whilst rank and titles are held of the utmost importance" (001-012), moral worth is displaced by inherited status. The term appears in 25 passages, concentrated in Chapters 1, 2, 4, and 5.

Rank is the structural expression of the same arbitrary authority she attacks in despotism and domestic tyranny. It corrupts those at the top with pride and those at the bottom with servility.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft defines rank as the principle of social distinction based on birth and title rather than talent and virtue. It is the foundation of aristocracy — a system fundamentally opposed to Enlightenment values of equality and merit:

> "Whilst rank and titles are held of the utmost importance, the virtues necessary to support the dignity of society will be neglected." (001-012)

The adjective "whilst" is conditional: rank is not an eternal truth but a contingent social arrangement. Wollstonecraft's critique is not merely political but moral — rank makes virtue impossible by substituting inherited status for cultivated merit.

For women, rank operates through the same logic as sex-based subordination. Both systems assign worth by category rather than character: a titled woman has "rank" but not "the rank that reason assigned her" (002-080).

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

### Rank and Moral Corruption

Rank corrupts both the high and the low. Those of high rank have no incentive to cultivate virtue — they are honored regardless of merit. Those of lower rank learn servility rather than independence:

> "Great subordination of rank constitutes its power, is highly unfavorable to morality." (001-025)

A "man of rank or fortune, sure of rising by his interest" (001-027), has no need of excellence. Rank is structurally anti-virtuous — it rewards birth, not effort, and the resulting character is weak regardless of social position.

### The Middle Rank as Moral Ideal

Wollstonecraft locates virtue in "the middle rank of life" (004-030), where neither inherited wealth nor desperate poverty corrupt character. The middle rank is "the one in which talents thrive best" (004-100):

> "In the middle rank of life, to continue the comparison, men appear to be most virtuous." (004-030)

This is not mere class prejudice but a structural argument: the middle rank must earn its living through industry, which cultivates habits of discipline and foresight. Neither privilege nor desperation distorts moral development.

### Women and Rank

Women occupy an ambiguous position in the rank hierarchy. Their status derives from male relations — fathers and husbands — rather than from their own achievements. A woman who marries up acquires rank without earning it. A woman who marries down loses it without deserving to.

Wollstonecraft's demand that women have "the rank that reason assigned her" (002-080) is radical: it proposes that women's social position should be determined by their own merit, not by marriage or birth. This is simultaneously a feminist and an Enlightenment argument.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

Rank is the social-structural dimension of Wollstonecraft's critique of hierarchy. It connects to [[Power]] — rank is the institutional form of arbitrary power — and to [[Property]] — hereditary property is the economic basis of rank. Both corrupt [[Virtue]] by substituting birth for merit.

The concept is closely linked to [[Equality]], which is the positive principle rank violates. It also connects to [[Education]]: proper national education would reward talent, not birth. [[Independence]] is impossible for those defined by inherited rank rather than achieved merit.
