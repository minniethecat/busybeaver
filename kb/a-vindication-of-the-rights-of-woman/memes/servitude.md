# Servitude

A condition of enforced dependence and subordination that Wollstonecraft uses to describe both political tyranny and the domestic situation of women, arguing that servitude corrupts the moral character of those subjected to it.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft employs "servitude" as a strong political term, drawing on Enlightenment discourse about liberty and despotism to diagnose women's condition. She links "private misery, and public servitude" (003-011) as parallel states — just as political subjects under tyranny are degraded by their lack of freedom, so women under domestic tyranny are degraded by their dependence. Servitude in her usage is not merely external constraint but an internalised condition that shapes character: "servitude not only debases the individual" (005-016) but perpetuates itself through the habits of mind it creates.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Servitude is the antithesis of the rational independence Wollstonecraft champions. She argues that women's education and social position place them in a state analogous to political servitude — denied the liberty to develop their reason, kept dependent on male authority, and taught to value the very qualities that make them pliable. The term appears in her argument at moments of particular intensity, when she wishes to stress the severity of women's condition beyond mere inequality.

Crucially, Wollstonecraft does not treat servitude as women's natural state but as one imposed by social arrangement. She notes that women "escape in this pitiful way from servitude" (009-033) only through strategies of manipulation — coquetry, cunning, and flattery — which are themselves symptoms of the servile condition rather than genuine freedom. The remedy is not better masters but the abolition of the condition itself through education and equal rights.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft's use of "servitude" is more politically charged than the contemporary language of "subordination" or "dependence" common in conduct literature. By framing women's condition in the language of political tyranny, she aligns her feminist argument with the broader Enlightenment critique of despotism — a rhetorical strategy that gives her argument radical political implications.

## Key Passages

> Private misery, and public servitude... But, Selene's education was not directed to form a reasonable woman. (003-011)

> This is begging the question; for servitude not only debases the individual, but its effects seem to be transmitted to posterity. (005-016)
