The concept of **melancholy** appears throughout the *Vindication* in a double register that reveals much about Wollstonecraft's rhetorical strategy. In its critical deployment, melancholy names the emotional consequence of women's confinement to trivial pursuits — the "melancholy emotions of sorrowful indignation" (P03-001) provoked by the spectacle of wasted human potential. In its affirmative deployment, melancholy is an elevated emotional state, "a sublime gloom of tender melancholy" (007-008), associated with the capacity for deep reflection that distinguishes genuine moral seriousness from superficial cheerfulness. Wollstonecraft's handling of melancholy thus enacts the same distinction that structures the *Vindication* as a whole: between the corrupted emotional life produced by a defective education and the morally significant emotional life that reason-governed cultivation makes possible.

## Definition

Eighteenth-century culture had a complex vocabulary of melancholy. The term could denote a medical condition — the "constitutional melancholy" (002-061) associated with an excess of black bile — an aesthetic category associated with the sublime, or a moral-psychological state of reflective sadness. Wollstonecraft draws on all these registers while subjecting them to her characteristic critical analysis.

The "constitutional melancholy" that she occasionally references is the medicalised version — a temperamental predisposition to sadness that, in her account, is often the product of circumstances rather than nature. "The fancy has hovered round" (002-061) the melancholy person, Wollstonecraft writes, suggesting that melancholy is as much cultivated as inherited. The critical edge is unmistakable: if melancholy is produced by the conditions of life rather than innate constitution, then it is subject to moral evaluation and capable of reform.

The aesthetic melancholy of the sublime tradition — "that awful melancholy, that sublime tenderness" (012-013) — is, by contrast, treated with greater respect. This is melancholy as a mode of elevated perception, the emotional correlate of serious thought. When Wollstonecraft describes "a sublime gloom of tender melancholy, that allows the mind for a moment to stand still" (007-008), she is drawing on the language of Burkean and Kantian sublimity to claim melancholy as a state of moral and intellectual significance. The mind that can experience melancholy of this kind is a mind capable of reflection — and reflection, in Wollstonecraft's vocabulary, is the precondition of virtue.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's treatment of melancholy is inseparable from her analysis of women's social condition. The "most melancholy emotions of sorrowful indignation" (P03-001) with which she opens the *Introduction* are not merely personal feelings — they are the appropriate emotional response of a rational being to the spectacle of systematic injustice. Melancholy, in this usage, is a form of moral knowledge: to be melancholy about the condition of women is to perceive that condition correctly.

The argument extends to the domestic sphere. Wollstonecraft observes that "it is a melancholy thing for a father of a family" (005-036) to see his daughters educated for display rather than for virtue. The adjective "melancholy" here carries moral weight — it is not merely sad but also wrong, a state of affairs that ought not to be and that calls for correction. The marriage of moral judgment and emotional response is characteristic of Wollstonecraft's rhetorical method: she does not suppress feeling in favour of reason but demonstrates how properly cultivated feeling is itself a form of rational response.

The gendered dimension of melancholy is particularly revealing. Wollstonecraft notes, with characteristic irony, that it is considered "masculine for a woman to be melancholy" (002-068). The observation cuts in two directions. On the one hand, it exposes the gendered policing of emotional expression: women are permitted to be cheerful, frivolous, and ornamental but denied access to the serious emotional states associated with intellectual depth. On the other hand, it implicitly claims melancholy — and the capacity for reflection it signifies — for women as well as men. Wollstonecraft's own rhetorical practice, in which a tone of "melancholy tenderness" (005-074) alternates with rational argument and impassioned critique, models the integration of feeling and reason that she advocates for her sex.

The "melancholy truth" (009-034) to which Wollstonecraft repeatedly returns is that the corruption of women's character is not natural but artificial — produced by an educational system whose effects are so pervasive as to appear inevitable. The melancholy serves a rhetorical function: it is the emotional register of moral seriousness, the tone appropriate to a subject whose gravity demands more than merely rational analysis.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The eighteenth century had made melancholy fashionable. The "graveyard poets" — Young, Gray, Blair — had elevated melancholy to a literary mode, and the cult of sensibility had made refined sadness a marker of superior feeling. Wollstonecraft's treatment of melancholy engages this tradition critically. Where the poets of sensibility treated melancholy as an end in itself — an intrinsically valuable state of refined emotion — Wollstonecraft treats it as an index of something else: of moral perception, of intellectual seriousness, of the capacity to be moved by considerations beyond self-interest.

Rousseau is again an implicit target. The Rousseau of the *Reveries of the Solitary Walker* had celebrated melancholy as the emotional signature of the authentic self, the feeling-state in which the individual most fully possesses his own existence. Wollstonecraft's melancholy is different: it is not self-regarding but world-regarding, directed outward toward the injustices that produce it rather than inward toward the self that experiences it. The "melancholy train of reflection" (011-019) into which she is led is not a reverie but an analysis — feeling in the service of understanding rather than in flight from it.

## Key Passages

> I have turned over various books written on the subject of education, and patiently observed the conduct of parents and the management of schools; but what has been the result? — a profound conviction that the neglected education of my fellow-creatures is the grand source of the misery I deplore. (P03-001)

> The fancy has hovered round a form of beauty dimly seen — but familiarity might have turned it into an every-day substance. Besides, I have led a solitary life, and from constitutional melancholy, which perhaps is increased by disappointment, am inclined to be contemplative. (002-061)

> It is a melancholy truth; yet such is the blessed effect of civilization, that the most respectable women are the most oppressed. (009-034)

> These may be termed the indirect effects of a system of government which saps the very foundation of morality. What can be a more melancholy sight to a thinking mind, than to see such a number of immortal beings sinking into the most abject state of degradation? (009-023)
