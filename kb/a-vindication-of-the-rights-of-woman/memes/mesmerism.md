# Mesmerism

## Definition

**Mesmerism** (or animal magnetism) was a controversial medical and quasi-spiritual practice developed by Franz Anton Mesmer in the 1770s-80s, based on the theory that an invisible magnetic fluid pervades the universe and that illness results from its blockage in the human body. By the time Wollstonecraft wrote in 1792, mesmerism had become a fashionable craze in Paris and London, attracting wealthy patrons who paid considerable sums to be "magnetised" by practitioners claiming to cure diseases that had resisted conventional medicine.

Wollstonecraft treats mesmerism as a particularly egregious example of the "delusions" [013-016] and "fashionable deceptions" [013-016] that flourish when women are denied a rational [[education]]. Her critique is at once medical, moral, and theological: she argues that the magnetisers' claims are scientifically baseless, that their practices exploit the credulous for profit, and that their pretensions to supernatural healing constitute "little short of blasphemy" [013-022]. She identifies the magnetisers as "priests of quackery" [013-020] who prey on the same female credulity that sustains fortune-tellers and other impostors.

## In Context

The mesmerism critique occupies Section 13.1 of Chapter XIII, the opening of Wollstonecraft's catalogue of "follies...peculiar to women" that flow "from ignorance or prejudice" [013-002]. She begins with fortune-tellers and cunning-men before transitioning to the magnetisers as "still more fashionable deceptions" [013-016] -- the progression underscoring her contention that these are not isolated aberrations but expressions of a systemic failure of female education.

Wollstonecraft deploys a Socratic question-and-answer method in this section, directly interrogating the women who patronize magnetisers: "Do you know anything of the construction of the human frame?" [013-017]. She contrasts the slow, natural methods of genuine medicine -- "regimen, another word for temperance, air, exercise, and a few medicines prescribed by persons who have studied the human body" [013-017] -- with the magnetisers' pretended ability to work instant cures. The core of her objection is that mesmerism offers an easy substitute for the difficult self-discipline that genuine health requires: "I allow that it is easier to touch the body of a saint, or to be magnetised, than to restrain our appetites or govern our passions" [013-024].

Her theological argument is particularly sharp. She contends that to believe God would "work a miracle to authorize confusion by sanctioning an error" [013-029] is itself impious, and that the magnetisers' claims implicitly insult divine wisdom by suggesting that "a miracle will be allowed to disturb his general laws, to restore to health the intemperate and vicious" [013-023]. Wollstonecraft thus repositions the skeptical, rational position as the truly religious one.

## Key Passages

> "From these delusions to those still more fashionable deceptions, practised by the whole tribe of magnetisers, the transition is very natural." [013-016]

> "These are the priests of quackery, though it be true they have not the convenient expedient of selling masses for souls in purgatory." [013-020]

> "It is, however, little short of blasphemy to pretend to such power." [013-022]

> "I allow that it is easier to touch the body of a saint, or to be magnetised, than to restrain our appetites or govern our passions; but health of body or mind can only be recovered by these means, or we make the Supreme Judge partial and revengeful." [013-024]

## Related Concepts

- [[religion]] -- the genuine faith that mesmerism blasphemes by claiming supernatural powers
- [[folly]] -- the broad category of errors produced by female ignorance, of which mesmerism is a prime instance
