# Dissimulation

## Definition

**Dissimulation** in Wollstonecraft's analysis refers to the concealment of one's true thoughts, feelings, and character -- a practice she identifies as a systemic feature of the education and social conditioning imposed on women. Rather than treating it as an individual moral failing, she exposes dissimulation as a "system" [005-100] deliberately cultivated by conduct-book writers and social convention. The core of her objection is that dissimulation severs the connection between inner character and outward behavior, making it impossible for women to develop genuine [[virtue]], which requires the honest exercise of reason.

The concept is closely linked to Wollstonecraft's broader critique of the artificial manners imposed on women. When women are taught that their social survival depends on pleasing others rather than acting from principle, dissimulation becomes a rational strategy for the powerless. The result is a corruption of character that Wollstonecraft sees as far more damaging than open vice, because it destroys the very capacity for moral integrity by making truth-telling seem dangerous and inexpedient.

## In Context

The most pointed treatment of dissimulation appears in Chapter II, where Wollstonecraft critiques Dr. Gregory's advice to his daughters. She charges that Gregory "actually recommends dissimulation, and advises an innocent girl to give the lie to her feelings" [002-057]. This passage is central to her argument that the conduct-book tradition actively teaches women to be deceitful -- not through malicious intent, but through a misguided belief that concealment protects feminine delicacy.

In Chapter V, Wollstonecraft broadens the critique, identifying "this system of dissimulation, throughout the volume" [005-100] as the organizing principle of a certain class of writings on female education. Here she connects dissimulation to the wider [[cunning]] that women are forced to develop when denied honest means of influence. The theme also appears in Chapter XIII, where she observes that wherever arbitrary power "curbs the free spirit of man, dissimulation is practised, and the various..." [013-085], suggesting that dissimulation is a universal response to tyranny, not a gendered vice.

## Key Passages

> "He actually recommends dissimulation, and advises an innocent girl to give the lie to her feelings, and not dance with spirit, when gaiety of heart would make her feet eloquent, without intending to be coquettish." [002-057]

> "It is this system of dissimulation, throughout the volume, that I despise." [005-100]

> "Wherever...arbitrary power curbs the free spirit of man, dissimulation is practised, and the various..." [013-085]
