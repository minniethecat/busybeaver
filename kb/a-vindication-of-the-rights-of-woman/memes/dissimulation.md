**Dissimulation** — the concealment of one's true thoughts, feelings, or intentions — is in Wollstonecraft's moral vocabulary one of the gravest charges she levels against the conduct-book tradition. When she writes that John Gregory "actually recommends dissimulation" (002-047) and that his advice amounts to "cunning and dissimulation" (002-075), she is not using hyperbole. She is identifying what she takes to be the logical consequence of an education that subordinates truth to propriety, principle to reputation, and virtue to the opinion of others.

The concept is central to Wollstonecraft's diagnosis of the moral corruption produced by women's subordinate social position. Dissimulation is not a female vice — it is a vice systematically cultivated in women by a social order that rewards concealment and punishes candour. The critique of dissimulation is thus inseparable from the critique of the social conditions that make it necessary.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's treatment of dissimulation draws on a long tradition of moral discourse in which dissimulation — the intentional concealment of truth — is distinguished from both lying (active falsehood) and discretion (the judicious withholding of information). In the classical and Christian traditions, dissimulation was generally condemned as a violation of the duty of truthfulness, though some moralists allowed it in cases of necessity.

Wollstonecraft's usage sharpens the concept into a specifically gendered critique. Dissimulation, in her analysis, is not an occasional moral lapse but a "system of dissimulation" (005-086) — a comprehensive mode of conduct that the conduct-book tradition actively inculcates in women. Gregory's advice that women should conceal their feelings, moderate their candour, and manage their self-presentation for the purpose of securing male approval is, in Wollstonecraft's reading, a systematic education in dissimulation.

The pairing of "cunning and dissimulation" (002-075) is significant. Cunning is the strategic intelligence that deploys dissimulation for advantage; dissimulation is the practice of concealment that cunning directs. Together they constitute a moral posture that is antithetical to the "virtues of humanity" (002-075) — the frankness, candour, and integrity that Wollstonecraft takes to be the marks of genuine moral character.

Wollstonecraft contrasts dissimulation with "the natural frankness of youth" (002-047). The "sensible mother" should not "restrain" this frankness by "instilling such indecent cautions." The adjective "indecent" is pointed: what the conduct books present as propriety is, in Wollstonecraft's moral framework, a violation of decency itself — the decency that consists in the honest expression of a virtuous character.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Dissimulation functions in Wollstonecraft's argument as the diagnostic symptom of a deeper pathology. The conduct-book tradition, by making women's social standing dependent on male opinion, creates a structural incentive for dissimulation. Women must conceal their true thoughts because those thoughts, if expressed, would threaten the approval on which their social existence depends. Dissimulation is not a character flaw but a survival strategy.

The critique of Gregory is the focal point. Gregory "actually recommends dissimulation, and advises an innocent girl to give the lie to her feelings" (002-047). The phrase "give the lie" is carefully chosen: dissimulation is not merely concealment but a form of self-betrayal, a lie told not to others but to oneself. The "innocent girl" who follows Gregory's advice learns to distrust her own feelings, to subordinate her inner life to the demands of social performance.

The political dimension is explicit. "It is this system of dissimulation, throughout the volume, that I despise" (005-086). The "volume" is Gregory's *Legacy to His Daughters*, but the "system" extends beyond Gregory to encompass the entire conduct-book tradition and, more broadly, the social order that makes such advice plausible. Dissimulation is not an individual failing but a structural feature of women's condition — the moral posture required of those who are permitted no independent moral standing.

Wollstonecraft's alternative is candour grounded in virtue. A woman whose character is formed by reason and principle has no need of dissimulation because her conduct is governed by standards that are independent of the opinions of others. The "frankness" (002-047) that Gregory would restrain is, in Wollstonecraft's framework, a moral good — the natural expression of a character that has nothing to conceal. The cultivation of dissimulation is thus not merely morally objectionable but practically unnecessary: a woman of genuine virtue has no need of it.

The connection to broader social critique is underscored at 013-077, where dissimulation is linked to the social conditions that produce it: "A similar attention to preserve their reputation was conspicuous in the dissenting and female world, and was produced by a similar cause." Dissimulation flourishes wherever reputation, rather than principle, is the basis of social standing — and women's social standing, like that of religious dissenters, depends precariously on the opinion of those in power.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The conduct-book tradition treated dissimulation — or, in its preferred vocabulary, "reserve" and "discretion" — as a female virtue. Gregory's advice that women should conceal their feelings and moderate their candour was presented not as an education in deception but as a counsel of prudence. In a world where women's reputation was their most valuable asset, reserve was a form of self-protection.

Wollstonecraft's counter-argument is that this "prudence" is morally corrupting. By teaching women to subordinate truth to reputation, the conduct books produce precisely the moral deformity — the divorce of inner character from outer conduct — that they purport to prevent. The "system of dissimulation" (005-086) is not a safeguard of female virtue but its destruction.

The contrast with the classical republican tradition is also significant. In that tradition, candour and frankness were virtues of the citizen — the qualities that enabled free men to speak truth to power. Women, excluded from citizenship, were excluded from candour as well. Wollstonecraft's demand that women be educated to frankness rather than dissimulation is thus a demand for women's moral citizenship — their inclusion in the community of those who may speak truthfully because they are answerable only to their own reason and conscience.

## Key Passages

> He actually recommends dissimulation, and advises an innocent girl to give the lie to her feelings, and not dance with spirit, when she finds herself observed. (002-047)

> He will not, when he treats of the education of women, assert, that they ought never to have the free use of reason, nor would he recommend cunning and dissimulation to beings who are acquiring, in like manner as himself, the virtues of humanity. (002-075)
