# Sentimental Novel

## Definition

The **sentimental novel** is a target of sustained criticism in Wollstonecrafts work, particularly in Section 13.2 of Chapter XIII, where she identifies novel-reading as a major source of womens intellectual and moral corruption. Her critique is not of all fiction, but specifically of the sentimental genre that dominated late eighteenth-century literary culture -- works that "work up stale tales, and describe meretricious scenes, all retailed in a sentimental jargon" [013-033]. She argues that such novels "equally tend to corrupt the taste, and draw the heart aside from its daily duties" [013-033].

Wollstonecrafts objection operates on multiple levels. Epistemologically, sentimental novels substitute fantasy for knowledge, giving women a distorted picture of human nature and social relations. Morally, they cultivate a "romantic twist of the mind" [013-031] that inflames the passions while leaving the [[understanding]] unexercised. Practically, they absorb the time and attention that should be devoted to domestic duties and intellectual improvement. Crucially, Wollstonecraft does not advocate the complete prohibition of novels but rather their displacement by more substantial reading: "when I exclaim against novels, I mean when contrasted with those works which exercise the understanding and regulate the imagination" [013-035].

## In Context

The novel critique in Chapter XIII follows immediately after her attack on fortune-tellers and magnetisers (Section 13.1), establishing a progression from superstition to sentimental delusion as two forms of the same feminine weakness produced by [[ignorance]]. Wollstonecraft identifies the sentimental disposition as "another instance of that feminine weakness of character, often produced by a confined [[education]]" [013-031].

Her analysis is nuanced. She acknowledges that "any kind of reading I think better than leaving a blank still a blank" [013-035], because even novels exercise the mind to some degree. She recounts the case of a man who allowed his less able daughter to read novels on the grounds that "erroneous opinions were better than none at all" [013-042]. Yet she also observes that "from reading novels some women of superior talents learned to despise them" [013-043], suggesting that novels can serve as a gateway to better reading for those with natural ability.

The practical remedy Wollstonecraft proposes is instructive: "The best method, I believe, that can be adopted to correct a fondness for novels is to ridicule them" [013-044] -- not indiscriminately but through judicious comparison with "pathetic incidents and heroic characters in history" [013-044]. This approach reflects her broader pedagogical principle that the understanding must be led, not forced, from error to truth.

Her most specific complaint concerns the effect on language and self-presentation: "the reading of novels makes women, and particularly ladies of fashion, very fond of using strong expressions and superlatives in conversation" [013-046]. The sentimental idiom, she argues, produces a false and theatrical emotionality that substitutes for genuine feeling.
