# Tenderness

Tenderness occupies an ambivalent position in Wollstonecraft's moral lexicon. She acknowledges its value as a natural human sentiment while subjecting the cultural idealisation of feminine tenderness to sharp critique. For Wollstonecraft, the problem is not tenderness itself but its reduction to a gendered performance — a substitute for the rational respect and genuine virtue that women are denied the opportunity to cultivate.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft distinguishes between two kinds of tenderness. The first is a genuine moral sentiment — "the calm tenderness of friendship" (002-056) that arises between equals who respect one another's rational nature. The second, and the one she relentlessly critiques, is a "vapid tenderness" (002-069) manufactured by a society that educates women to be pleasing rather than virtuous. This latter form of tenderness is performative, rooted in weakness rather than strength, and ultimately corrosive to both the woman who deploys it and the man who is manipulated by it. It "easily degenerates into contempt" (002-069), undermining the very affection it purports to express.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's critique of tenderness is inseparable from her analysis of power. She observes that "weakness may excite tenderness, and gratify the arrogant pride of man" (002-049), but this is not genuine affection — it is a transaction in which the woman's vulnerability purchases the man's protection, at the cost of her dignity and autonomy. The tenderness that men feel for women under this arrangement is not respect but the condescension of the powerful toward the dependent.

Against this degraded form of tenderness, Wollstonecraft proposes a reformed ideal. Instead of "the familiar tenderness which frequently prolongs the empire of the senses" (007-029), she advocates for the tenderness that flows from genuine intimacy grounded in mutual respect: "That intimacy from which tenderness should flow, will not, cannot subsist between the vicious" (013-076). True tenderness, in her view, is the emotional expression of a relationship between rational equals — not the sentimental glue of an unequal partnership.

She also connects the critique of tenderness to her educational philosophy. The tenderness that mothers feel for their children is natural and valuable, but it must be guided by reason to become a genuine moral force. When parental affection is reduced to mere tenderness — "sacrificing to lasciviousness the parental affection, that ennobles instinct" (008-031) — it fails both parent and child. Tenderness must be elevated by principle, or it remains no more than animal instinct.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The conduct-book tradition that Wollstonecraft contests consistently prescribes tenderness as a feminine virtue. Dr. Gregory advises his daughters to cultivate a tender manner to attract and retain male affection. Wollstonecraft rejects this instrumentalisation of tenderness outright. For her, tenderness is not a tool of seduction or a marker of feminine delicacy but a moral quality that, when rightly understood, belongs equally to both sexes and is grounded in reason and respect, not weakness.

## Key Passages

> Weakness may excite tenderness, and gratify the arrogant pride of man; but the lordly caresses of a protector will not gratify a noble mind. (002-049)

> [S]ubstitute the calm tenderness of friendship, the confidence of respect, instead of blind admiration, and the sensual emotions of fondness. (002-056)

> [A] vapid tenderness, which easily degenerates into contempt. (002-069)

> [R]espectful, than the familiar tenderness which frequently prolongs the empire of the senses. (007-029)

> That intimacy from which tenderness should flow, will not, cannot subsist between the vicious. (013-076)
