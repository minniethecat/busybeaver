Bashfulness occupies a distinctive place in Wollstonecraft's moral vocabulary: it is the appearance of modesty without its substance. She carefully distinguishes bashfulness — a socially conditioned, transient quality of youth — from genuine modesty, which she understands as a mature virtue grounded in reason and self-respect.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft opens her analysis with a crucial distinction: "I do not mean to confound it with bashfulness" (007-004), referring to modesty. Bashfulness, she explains, is a surface phenomenon — "the playful, bewitching bashfulness of youth" (007-007) — that may or may not indicate genuine modesty beneath. The distinction is not merely terminological; it carries the weight of her critique of how women's education cultivates appearances rather than virtues.

The trajectory she traces is from bashfulness to something less innocent. "After their maidenish bashfulness is once lost," she observes, women "in fact, have no resource" (007-025), implying that bashfulness serves as a temporary substitute for genuine modesty. When the bloom of youth fades, nothing substantial remains to govern conduct — precisely because education has produced only the appearance of virtue, not its reality.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument proceeds by mapping bashfulness onto a developmental psychology of virtue. In a properly constituted education, "modesty will take place of bashfulness" (007-031): the transient, socially performed quality of youth is progressively replaced by a mature, rationally grounded virtue. This substitution should be natural and inevitable, but Wollstonecraft's critique is that society arrests this development, keeping women in a state of perpetual bashfulness — or worse, allowing bashfulness to decay into the "manners of maturity, which succeed bashfulness, to which truth is sacrificed" (007-034).

The educational dimension is clearest in her observation that without proper cultivation, young persons "will infallibly lose that decent bashfulness, which might have ripened into" genuine modesty (012-033). The verb "ripened" is significant: bashfulness is not worthless but preparatory. It contains the potential for modesty, but only if education provides the conditions for maturation. Without those conditions, bashfulness either persists as a permanent infantilism or decays into something more damaging — the worldly manners that sacrifice truth to appearance.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The conduct-book tradition — represented by James Fordyce and John Gregory — tended to conflate bashfulness with modesty, treating the "maidenly blush" as evidence of virtue. Wollstonecraft's innovation is to insist on a developmental distinction: bashfulness may be a natural phase in the formation of character, but it is not the endpoint. Her position is more psychologically sophisticated than that of Rousseau, who valorised a kind of natural shyness in women as essential to their sexual character. For Wollstonecraft, bashfulness belongs to a stage of life, not to a sex, and its persistence into adulthood is a sign of failed education, not of preserved innocence.
