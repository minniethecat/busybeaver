Zeal, in Wollstonecraft's analysis, appears as a morally ambiguous passion: capable of motivating action, yet prone to corrupting judgment when it outruns reason. She consistently pairs the term with "blind," signalling that zeal without reflection is not virtue but its counterfeit.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft uses "zeal" not as a neutral descriptor of enthusiasm but as a term requiring qualification. The phrase "mistaken fondness or blind zeal" (003-014) places zeal alongside misguided affection as forces that obscure rather than illuminate moral judgment. Her critique is not of passionate commitment as such but of commitment severed from rational scrutiny: zeal becomes "blind" precisely when it refuses to subject its certainties to the test of reason.

The recurring adjective "blind" is essential to her definition. When Wollstonecraft warns against being led "with blind zeal to usurp the character of omnipotence" (005-121), she identifies zeal as a temptation to overreach — to mistake the intensity of one's convictions for their correctness. This diagnostic move is characteristic of her broader method: moral psychology in service of political and educational critique.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's treatment of zeal reveals a nuanced position on moral motivation. She is not hostile to zeal per se — indeed, she praises a "truly humane heart, whose zeal renders every thing easy" (012-062) — but she insists that zeal must be anchored in humane sentiment and rational understanding rather than in dogmatic certainty. The zeal that "renders every thing easy" is a productive, enabling force; the zeal that is "blind" is destructive.

This distinction carries institutional implications. Her observation that the established church inspires zeal that "never makes them lose sight of" practical concerns (012-011) suggests that institutional structures can channel zeal toward constructive or destructive ends. The critique is not anti-religious but anti-dogmatic: zeal becomes dangerous when institutional authority protects it from the corrective of experience and reason.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Eighteenth-century moral philosophy frequently celebrated zeal as a mark of sincere religious conviction. Wollstonecraft's innovation is to detach zeal from sincerity and attach it instead to reason: zeal is legitimate only when it passes through the filter of rational reflection. This position aligns her more closely with the Enlightenment critique of enthusiasm — shared by Locke and Hume — than with the evangelical tradition that valorised passionate religious feeling. Yet she extends the critique beyond religion into the domain of parental affection and education, where "blind zeal" (003-014) can damage children as surely as religious fanaticism can damage souls.
