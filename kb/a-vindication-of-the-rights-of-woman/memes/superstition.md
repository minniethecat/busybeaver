Superstition, in Wollstonecraft's analysis, is not merely religious error but a political and social poison: it is "the sure dregs of ambition" (001-033), a tool by which despotic power and priestly authority maintain their hold over an uneducated populace.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft defines superstition through its effects rather than by abstract theological criteria.

It is the irrational element that "is mixed with her religion" (005-111) in even well-intentioned writing, the force that alongside luxury "is most quickly spread" to corrupt society (001-033). It is also the prejudice she herself overcame through direct experience of Catholic Portugal, where she took "instructive lessons on the evils of superstition, and intolerance" (P01-008).

Superstition is thus linked to intolerance: both arise from unreasoning deference to authority rather than the independent exercise of rational judgment.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Superstition functions in Wollstonecraft's argument as a structural obstacle to women's emancipation.

In Chapter 1, she traces how "monarchial and priestly power" (001-033) arose from feudal arrangements and maintained themselves through superstition. The implication for women is direct: if superstition sustains political despotism, it also sustains domestic despotism, since both depend on unexamined submission.

Her critique extends to the conduct of educated women. In Chapter 5, examining a contemporary female writer, Wollstonecraft acknowledges "sentiments that do honour to her head and heart" but warns that "so much superstition is mixed with her religion" that she "should not let a young person read her works" without critical guidance (005-111).

This example illustrates Wollstonecraft's core pedagogical conviction: religion, when not purified by reason, becomes superstition, and superstition, however well-intentioned, is educationally harmful — particularly to young women whose rational faculties are already under-cultivated.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Where many Enlightenment thinkers treated superstition as a problem of uneducated masses, Wollstonecraft locates it specifically in women's education.

Rousseau, whom she critiques throughout, would confine women to a religion of sentiment and domestic piety. Wollstonecraft sees in such sentimental religion the very seed of superstition.

Her own experience in Portugal (P01-008) gave her a comparative perspective: Catholic superstition and Protestant bigotry are two faces of the same unreasoning deference, and only rational education can inoculate against both.

## Key Passages

> This baneful lurking gangrene is most quickly spread by luxury and superstition, the sure dregs of ambition. The indolent puppet of a court first becomes a luxurious monster, or fastidious sensualist, and then makes the contagion which his unnatural state spreads, the instrument of tyranny. (001-033)

> Yet so much superstition is mixed with her religion, and so much worldly wisdom with her morality, that I should not let a young person read her works, unless I could afterwards converse on the subjects, and point out the contradictions. (005-111)

> Her mind was expanded by this residence in a foreign country, and though clear of religious bigotry before, she took some instructive lessons on the evils of superstition, and intolerance. (P01-008)
