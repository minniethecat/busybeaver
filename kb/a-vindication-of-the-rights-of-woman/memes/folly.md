In Wollstonecraft's framework, **folly** is not an innate female trait but the predictable product of a society that denies women rational education and cultivates vanity instead of virtue. She identifies folly as the "grand source of female…vice" (004-011), arguing that women's degraded condition produces foolish behaviour as inevitably as any system produces its designed outcomes.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft defines folly primarily in opposition to reason and wisdom. For her, folly is not mere silliness but a moral category — the failure to act rationally that results from stunted intellectual development. She speaks of "the bondage of folly" (006-020) and describes women as becoming "dead weights of vice and folly on the community" (001-027) when denied education. Her usage consistently ties folly to the social construction of femininity: women are made foolish by a system that prizes "accomplishments" over understanding.

Crucially, Wollstonecraft distinguishes folly from innate incapacity. When she writes of "unnatural instances of folly and cruelty" (003-023), the word "unnatural" signals her core argument — that such behaviour is not inherent to women but artificially produced by their conditioned dependence. Folly, in this analysis, is the behavioural expression of a mind denied the exercise of reason.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Throughout the *Vindication*, Wollstonecraft traces folly to its structural causes. The "grand source of female folly and vice has ever appeared to me to arise from a confined education" (004-011) — this statement encapsulates her causal chain: confined education → undeveloped reason → folly → vice. She does not exempt women from moral responsibility, but she insists that the conditions producing folly must be addressed before women can be expected to behave wisely.

The progression from folly to vice is a recurring theme. Wollstonecraft observes that "folly swell into a vice, by almost imperceptible gradations" (005-121), warning that trivial foolishness, left uncorrected by reason, hardens into genuine moral failing. She also critiques the sentimental tradition that romanticises folly, noting how sensibility can drive a woman "into folly — into vice" (007-018) when not governed by rational principle.

Wollstonecraft's tone can be sharp — she calls certain behaviours "a mixture of madness and folly" (004-034) and refers to "more egregious folly" (005-137) in the context of misplaced devotion. Yet her harshness serves a rhetorical purpose: she intends to shock readers into recognising that what polite society calls feminine charm is, in her analysis, a form of cultivated irrationality.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Where Rousseau romanticised feminine weakness as natural and charming, Wollstonecraft refused to sentimentalise folly. Rousseau's idealisation of Sophia — pleasing, submissive, and intellectually limited — represents precisely the "example of folly, not to say vice" (003-047) that Wollstonecraft warns against. She also implicitly counters Dr. Gregory's advice that women should cultivate delicacy of sentiment, arguing that such cultivation leads not to virtue but to a "deviation into folly" (005-100) when not grounded in rational understanding.
