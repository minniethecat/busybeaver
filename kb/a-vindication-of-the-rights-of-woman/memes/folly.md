# Folly

## Definition

**Folly** in Wollstonecraft's moral vocabulary is not mere foolishness but a specific kind of moral and intellectual error produced by a corrupt [[education]]. When women are described as foolish — as they frequently are in eighteenth-century discourse — Wollstonecraft insists on asking *why* they are foolish. Her answer is that folly is not natural to women but is produced by an education that cultivates [[vanity]], suppresses [[reason]], and substitutes appearance for substance.

The strategic importance of this redefinition is that it shifts the blame from women's nature to women's circumstances. If folly is a product of education rather than of nature, then the remedy for folly is not to condemn women but to educate them differently.

## In Context

### Folly and Education

Wollstonecraft directly connects folly to the deficiencies of female [[education]]: women are foolish because they have been taught to be foolish. An education that prizes accomplishments over understanding, that rewards pleasing over thinking, and that cultivates [[vanity]] rather than [[virtue]] cannot produce anything other than folly — and to blame women for the folly that this education produces is the height of injustice.

### Folly as a Social Product

The follies that Wollstonecraft catalogues — the obsession with fashion, the cultivation of trivial accomplishments, the susceptibility to flattery — are not individual failings but social products. They are what happens when human beings are denied the opportunity to develop their [[reason]] and are offered admiration as a substitute for respect. The remedy is not moral exhortation but structural change — an education that cultivates the understanding rather than the appearance.

### The Escalation from Folly to Vice

Wollstonecraft traces a disturbing trajectory in which folly — if left uncorrected — does not remain mere foolishness but escalates into something far more serious. "We see a folly swell into a vice, by almost imperceptible gradations" [005-139], she warns. The point is not merely that foolish behaviour is unattractive but that it is morally dangerous: the habits of mind cultivated by a frivolous education — intellectual laziness, moral complacency, the preference for appearance over reality — are the same habits that enable genuine wrongdoing. A woman who has never been taught to distinguish truth from pleasing falsehood cannot be expected to distinguish virtue from its counterfeit.

> "We see a folly swell into a vice, by almost imperceptible gradations." [005-139]

### The Folly of Flattery

A particularly revealing example of folly in Wollstonecraft's analysis is women's susceptibility to flattery. "To see a mortal adorn an object with ideal perfection, and then fall down and worship the idol which his own hands have made — the weakness of the mortal is indeed pitiable" [005-154]. This passage captures the tragic absurdity of the folly-flattery cycle: men create an impossible ideal of feminine perfection through flattery, women strive to embody that ideal, and both parties mistake the resulting performance for reality. The folly is not merely the woman's for believing the flattery but also the man's for offering it — a mutual self-deception that degrades both parties.

> "To see a mortal adorn an object with ideal perfection, and then fall down and worship the idol which his own hands have made — the weakness of the mortal is indeed pitiable." [005-154]
