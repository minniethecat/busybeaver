# Children

## Definition

In the *Vindication*, **children** appear primarily as the objects of education
and the beneficiaries (or victims) of parental conduct. Wollstonecraft's interest
is not in childhood as a developmental stage but in the principles that should
govern the rearing of the next generation — principles that, she argues, can
only be adequately fulfilled by educated, rational mothers.

## Children as Rational Beings in Potential

Wollstonecraft challenges the view — common in eighteenth-century educational
writing — that children are merely creatures of instinct and appetite to be
managed through authority and habit. Children possess reason in potential, and
education should cultivate this latent capacity from the earliest possible age:

A child treated as a rational being will begin to exercise reason; a child
treated as a mere animal will develop only cunning and appetite. The method
of education determines the outcome.

## The Duty of Mothers

The education of children is, for Wollstonecraft, the central duty of motherhood
— and the strongest argument for women's education. An ignorant, frivolous
mother cannot educate her children properly; she can only indulge or neglect
them. A rational, educated mother, by contrast, can form her children's
characters from infancy:

> "to be a good mother, a woman must have sense, and that independence of mind
> which few women possess who are taught to depend entirely on their husbands."

If women are to fulfill their maternal duties, they must be educated for them
— not in the superficial accomplishments of the boarding school, but in the
serious cultivation of reason and virtue.

## Children and the State

Wollstonecraft extends the argument for children's education to the public
sphere. The character of the rising generation determines the character of
the future state; therefore, the education of children is a matter of public
concern, not merely private preference. Her advocacy of national day schools
reflects this conviction that the rearing of children is too important to be
left to uneducated parents alone.
