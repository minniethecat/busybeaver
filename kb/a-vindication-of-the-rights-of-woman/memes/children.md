**Children** occupy a strategic position in Wollstonecraft's argument: they are the reason women must be educated, the measure of women's current degradation, and the means by which social reform will be transmitted across generations. The claim that uneducated women cannot properly educate their children is one of the most practically compelling arguments the *Vindication* advances.

Women themselves, Wollstonecraft observes, have been reduced to "overgrown children" (002-016) by an education that cultivates neither reason nor principle. The consequence is a cycle of degradation: "spoiled children" (P01-013) grow up to become spoiled women, who in turn spoil their own children, perpetuating the very system that produced them.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft uses the figure of the child in two distinct but related ways. The first is literal: children are the objects of education, and the quality of their education determines the character of the adults they will become. The mother's duty is to educate her children — but she cannot perform this duty unless she herself has been educated.

The second is metaphorical: women who have not been educated to reason are, morally and intellectually, children. "Thanks to early debauchery and a systematic neglect of understanding," she writes, men "are often only overgrown children" (002-016). The same diagnosis applies to women, whose education has been even more systematically neglected.

The concept of "spoiled children" runs through the text as a diagnosis of the mechanism by which bad education reproduces itself. Children who are indulged rather than instructed, praised for charm rather than character, grow into adults who expect indulgence and praise — and who are incapable of the self-discipline that virtue requires.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument about children is at once a critique of contemporary motherhood and a vision of its transformation. The critique is sharp: women "attend to their children... to dress them in a becoming manner" (004-059) rather than to cultivate their understanding. Maternal affection, untutored by reason, becomes a form of indulgence that harms rather than benefits the child.

The vision is equally sharp. The educated mother "surrounded by her children, reaping the reward of her care" (003-055) is Wollstonecraft's image of redeemed domesticity. She will not neglect her children for "the arts of coquetry" (P02-020) because she will have been educated to understand that the cultivation of rational beings is a higher calling than the cultivation of personal charm.

The argument extends to fathers as well. The father who "lavishes on the overgrown children" (002-057) the confidence that should be reserved for rational equals perpetuates a system in which women are treated as charming dependents rather than as partners in the moral education of the young.

The economic dimension is significant. Women "encumbered with children" (003-045) are rendered economically dependent, and this dependence — like all dependence — corrupts character. Wollstonecraft's insistence that women should be capable of economic independence is inseparable from her argument about children: the mother who can support herself can educate her children in independence; the mother who cannot, cannot.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Against the Rousseauian idealisation of the mother as nature's educator, Wollstonecraft insists that untutored maternal instinct is insufficient. The mother who educates by "mere habit" (005-015) rather than by rational engagement transmits the defects of her own education to the next generation.

Against the political theory that excluded women from citizenship on the grounds that their role was domestic, Wollstonecraft argues that the domestic role — the education of children — is itself a form of citizenship and that it requires the same rational cultivation as any other public function. "If children are to be educated to understand the true principle of patriotism, their mother must be a patriot" (P02-007).
