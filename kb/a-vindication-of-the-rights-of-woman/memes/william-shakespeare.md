# William Shakespeare

1564 – 1616. England's greatest playwright, referenced by Wollstonecraft as an exemplar of natural genius — a topic she treats cautiously, since the cult of "natural genius" was often used to argue that women need not be educated.

## Life

Shakespeare was the preeminent dramatist of the English Renaissance, author of some thirty-eight plays and over one hundred fifty sonnets. By the eighteenth century, he had been elevated to the status of national poet, his works central to English cultural identity. The debate over whether Shakespeare's genius was "natural" (untaught) or "cultivated" (the product of learning) was a live question in eighteenth-century criticism.

## Role in the Text

Wollstonecraft's reference to Shakespeare is brief but strategically significant. The eighteenth-century cult of Shakespeare as "natural genius" — the untutored prodigy who produced masterpieces by sheer innate power — was sometimes deployed to argue against the necessity of education. If genius is natural, why educate? Wollstonecraft resists this implication: even Shakespeare's genius, she suggests, required cultivation, and the "natural genius" argument should not be used to justify denying education to women.

Her treatment of Shakespeare reflects her broader strategy: she does not challenge the cultural authority of canonical male writers but reinterprets their significance in ways that support rather than undermine her arguments for women's education.

## Key Ideas

The debate over Shakespeare's "natural genius" was, for Wollstonecraft, directly relevant to the question of women's education. If even Shakespeare's genius benefited from cultivation, how much more do ordinary minds — male and female — require education to develop their capacities? The fetishization of "natural genius" serves, in her analysis, to justify educational neglect by pretending that intellectual achievement is a gift rather than a product of cultivation.
