In Wollstonecraft's framework, **ignorance** is not a natural female deficiency but a condition deliberately cultivated by a society that "keep[s] them in ignorance under the specious name of innocence" (002-002). She relentlessly attacks the conflation of ignorance with feminine virtue, arguing that the "night of sensual ignorance" (001-033) serves to maintain women's subordination by denying them the rational faculty that would enable them to claim their rights.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft defines ignorance primarily in opposition to reason and knowledge. For her, ignorance is the foundational condition from which all female vices spring — "the natural effect of ignorance" (002-003) is a mind that "will ever be unstable" because it lacks the grounding of rational understanding. She repeatedly uses the imagery of darkness and night to characterise ignorance: the "night of sensual ignorance" (001-033), the "night of ignorance" (010-002), and "the misty night of ignorance" (007-008) — all suggesting a state of intellectual blindness from which women must be led.

Crucially, Wollstonecraft distinguishes between innocent ignorance and culpable ignorance. When she denounces the "scrupulosity of ignorance" (008-012), she targets those who wilfully maintain ignorance as a supposed safeguard of virtue, arguing that "neither religion nor virtue" requires such intellectual impoverishment. Women are "kept in ignorance" (002-002) not by nature but by design — a key distinction that underpins her entire educational argument.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's treatment of ignorance runs through every chapter of the *Vindication*. She opens by declaring that superficial accomplishments have "plastered over unsightly ignorance" (001-011), establishing ignorance as the hidden reality beneath the veneer of female polish. This metaphor — ignorance plastered over — recurs throughout: society teaches women to appear accomplished precisely to conceal that they have not been permitted to develop genuine understanding.

The structural function of ignorance is clearest in her critique of the innocence-ignorance equivalence. She mocks those who "preserve their innocence, as ignorance is courteously termed" (003-024), exposing the linguistic trick by which intellectual deprivation is rebranded as moral purity. This critique reaches its sharpest point in Chapter VII, where she challenges those who insist "on keeping women in a state of ignorance, and yet so vehemently to insist on their resisting temptation" (007-015) — an impossible double standard that sets women up to fail.

Wollstonecraft also traces ignorance to its material conditions. When she observes that men "continually undermine" virtue "through ignorance or pride" (009-002), she identifies ignorance as a systemic force, not merely an individual failing. The "bondage of ignorance" (009-013) is, in her analysis, the deepest obstacle to women's emancipation — deeper even than legal inequality — because it operates at the level of consciousness, preventing women from recognising their own oppression.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Where Rousseau celebrated the "amiable ignorance" of his ideal Sophie, Wollstonecraft saw only cultivated incapacity. Rousseau's educational programme for women explicitly limits their intellectual development, arguing that female curiosity should be "easily satisfied." Wollstonecraft directly counters this tradition by insisting that "the right use of reason" is impossible even in an "independent state of even harmless ignorance" (006-020) — even benign ignorance, she argues, is incompatible with moral agency. She also contests the commonplace view, echoed by Gregory and Fordyce, that a woman's ignorance of the world preserves her modesty; for Wollstonecraft, true modesty is a product of knowledge, not its absence, and the "instinctive timidity of ignorance" (007-004) is no substitute for the "reserve of reason" (007-005).

## Key Passages

> Why should they be kept in ignorance under the specious name of innocence? (002-002)

> The mind will ever be unstable that has only prejudices to lean on, and the current of its feelings is continually interrupted by the gusts of passion, unless it be strengthened by reason. The natural effect of ignorance! (002-003)

> It is vain to expect virtue from women till they are, in some degree, independent of men; nay, it is vain to expect that strength of natural affection, which would make them good wives and mothers. Whilst they are absolutely dependent on their husbands, they will be cunning, mean, and selfish. (009-013)
