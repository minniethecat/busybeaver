# Countenance

In eighteenth-century discourse, countenance carried meanings that exceed its modern sense of "facial expression." The countenance was understood as the visible register of character — the face as a text in which moral qualities could be read. Wollstonecraft draws on this tradition, using "countenance" to connect physical appearance, moral psychology, and social critique.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft uses "countenance" in a physiognomic sense: the face reveals the mind that shaped it. She describes how "that character of countenance, which the French term a physionomie" (004-074) is formed by the habitual exercise of the understanding. The countenance is not a passive surface but the cumulative inscription of mental activity — or its absence. A mind that reasons produces one kind of face; a mind confined to trivial pursuits produces another.

This is not a simple determinism. Wollstonecraft allows that "good sense may only have a cast of countenance that wears off as you trace" (001-030) the superficial features — genuine character may be obscured by surface appearance. But the fundamental logic remains: the countenance is an index of the mind.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

The countenance functions in Wollstonecraft's argument as visible evidence for her moral claims. When she advocates for women's intellectual cultivation, she can point to the countenance as proof: "give character to the countenance; that is, they trace the operations of the mind" (004-076). The educated mind produces a countenance of character; the neglected mind produces vacancy.

This argument has political force. If the countenance reveals mental cultivation, then women's allegedly inferior countenances are not natural but produced — they are the visible effects of educational neglect. To demand that women be beautiful without being educated is to demand an impossibility: genuine beauty of countenance requires the activity of mind.

She also notes the social dimension: "the only way to acquire that countenance from their fellow creatures" (009-003) is through the exercise of virtue. The countenance is not merely an individual attribute but a social signal — it is how one is read by others, and therefore how one acquires respect or contempt. Women are taught to cultivate a pleasing countenance rather than a virtuous one, substituting surface for substance.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Lavater's *Physiognomische Fragmente* (1775–1778) had made physiognomy a popular science, claiming to read character from fixed facial features. Wollstonecraft is more interested in countenance as formed rather than given — the face as shaped by mental habits rather than determined by birth. Her countenance argument is a social one: change the education, and you change the face.
