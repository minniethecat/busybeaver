# Temperance

Temperance, for Wollstonecraft, is a classical virtue of self-governance — the rational regulation of appetite, passion, and desire. She invokes it as part of the traditional moral vocabulary while extending its application to gendered critique: intemperance in men produces oppression of women.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft lists temperance among the core virtues: "Modesty, temperance, and self-denial, are the sober... " (005-015). She draws on the classical and Christian tradition that treats temperance as one of the cardinal virtues — the rational soul's governance over bodily appetite. But she does not merely repeat this tradition; she deploys it critically.

Her most distinctive use of the concept is in her critique of male behaviour: "the oppression that his intemperance naturally produced" (008-024). Male intemperance — in drink, in passion, in the exercise of power — produces women's suffering. She observes that "this intemperance, so prevalent, depraves the appetite" (008-030) — intemperance is not merely a personal failing but a social force that corrupts relationships and institutions.

She references "superior temperance and piety" (013-016) as marks of moral seriousness, contrasting genuine temperance with the performative self-denial that seeks social approval rather than moral improvement.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Temperance functions in Wollstonecraft's moral psychology as the virtue that makes other virtues possible. Without temperance — the capacity to govern impulse by reason — no sustained moral effort is possible. This is why she includes it among the foundational virtues alongside modesty and self-denial.

Her application of temperance to gender relations is pointed: she observes the effects of male "intemperance" on women and argues that men who cannot govern themselves will inevitably govern others badly. The intemperate husband is a domestic tyrant; intemperance in the powerful produces suffering in the dependent.

She also extends temperance to education: "improvement abortive which includes temperance" (012-025) — any educational programme that omits training in self-governance is incomplete. Women must be taught to govern themselves, not merely to submit to external control. This is a radical claim: women's education, in her view, should produce autonomous moral agents, not compliant dependents.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Classical and Christian traditions treated temperance as a universal virtue, but conduct literature for women translated it into a specifically female duty of sexual restraint. Wollstonecraft resists this narrowing: temperance is the governance of all appetite by reason, not merely chastity. Her critique of male intemperance as a cause of women's suffering is particularly striking — she redirects the moral demand for self-governance toward those who hold power.
