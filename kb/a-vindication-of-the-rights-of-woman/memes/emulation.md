# Emulation

Emulation — the desire to equal or excel others — runs through Wollstonecraft's educational and moral psychology as a force of uncertain value. She is neither a straightforward defender of emulation as a spur to virtue, nor a simple critic of it as a corruption of the soul. Instead, she distinguishes between forms of emulation based on their objects and origins. Emulation directed toward genuine excellence and grounded in respect for the qualities one seeks to match can be ennobling. Emulation rooted in ignorance, inexperience, or the mere desire for display is corrupting. The distinction matters because it bears directly on her proposals for education: whether emulation should be cultivated as a motive in schools, and whether women — whose education currently channels emulation into trivial objects — can be redirected toward worthier aims.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft uses "emulation" in the eighteenth-century sense of competitive striving, a meaning that sits between the older classical sense of virtuous rivalry and the emerging modern sense of mere ambition. The term retains for her a connection to *aemulatio* — the Latin concept of imitation-as-competition, the effort not merely to copy excellence but to match or surpass it. But she is acutely aware of how easily emulation slides into vanity, how the desire to excel can become indistinguishable from the desire merely to be seen to excel.

She identifies two distinct species of emulation. The first is "the emulation produced by ignorance, or, to speak with more propriety, by inexperience" (005-124) — a youthful, uncritical desire to match whatever one admires, without the judgment to distinguish worthy objects from unworthy ones. The second is what might be called mature emulation: the "emulous panting after perfection which ever glows in a noble mind" (005-124), a striving directed not at surpassing others but at approaching an ideal standard. The transition from the first to the second is, for Wollstonecraft, one of the central tasks of moral education.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

### Emulation and Women's Education

Wollstonecraft's most pointed treatment of emulation appears in her critique of women's education. She observes that "in youth their faculties are not brought forward by emulation; and having no serious scientific study, if they have natural sagacity it is turned too soon on life and manners" (002-020). The absence of emulation in girls' education is not, for her, a blessing — it is a deprivation. Emulation is a natural motive that can be harnessed for intellectual development. When it is denied a worthy object, it does not disappear; it redirects itself toward "life and manners" — the social arts of pleasing, the competition for admiration that Wollstonecraft elsewhere condemns as coquetry.

The passage is significant because it runs counter to a certain reading of Wollstonecraft as simply hostile to competition. She is not. She recognizes that the desire to excel is a powerful natural motive, and her complaint is that women's education neither cultivates nor directs it. Boys are permitted to compete in serious intellectual pursuits; girls are left to compete only for attention. The fault lies not in emulation itself but in the objects toward which it is directed.

The educational consequence is clear: "They dwell on effects, and modifications, without tracing them back to causes; and complicated rules to adjust behaviour are a weak substitute for simple principles" (002-020). Without the discipline of emulation in serious study, women learn only the surface of things — effects rather than causes, rules of behavior rather than principles of conduct. They become adept at the social performance of intelligence without possessing the substance of it.

### The Purification of Emulation

Wollstonecraft's most extended analysis of emulation comes in a passage that traces its maturation from youthful infatuation to mature virtue. She observes that "it is natural for youth to adorn the first object of its affection with every good quality, and the emulation produced by ignorance, or, to speak with more propriety, by inexperience, brings forward the mind capable of forming such an affection" (005-124). Early emulation is blind — it idealizes its object and strives to match an imagined perfection that does not exist. But this blindness, she suggests, is not simply a defect; it "brings forward the mind," drawing it out of itself and toward something higher than its present state.

The process of maturation involves disappointment and its productive consequences: "when, in the lapse of time, perfection is found not to be within the reach of mortals, virtue, abstractly, is thought beautiful, and wisdom sublime" (005-124). The failure of particular objects of emulation — the discovery that no human being is perfect — redirects the emulous impulse toward abstract ideals. One no longer strives to be like a particular admired person; one strives to be virtuous, to be wise. This is emulation purified of its personal and competitive elements, transformed into what she calls "that emulous panting after perfection which ever glows in a noble mind" (005-124).

The theological dimension of this transformation is important to Wollstonecraft: "the being walks alone only dependent on heaven for that emulous panting after perfection" (005-124). Mature emulation is not competition with others but aspiration toward a divine standard. It isolates the individual — "walks alone" — while connecting the individual to the highest possible object of striving. This is emulation as a form of devotion, the desire to excel understood as a desire to approach God.

### Emulation and Discipline

Wollstonecraft does not share the romantic faith that emulation alone, unguided by discipline, will produce excellence. In her discussion of national education, she concedes that "a few good scholars, I grant, may have been formed by emulation and discipline; but, to bring forward these clever boys, the health and morals of a number have been sacrificed" (012-020). The pairing of "emulation and discipline" is deliberate: emulation provides the motive force, discipline provides the structure. Neither alone is sufficient.

But she is also attentive to the costs of emulation-based education. The competitive pressure that produces a few "clever boys" sacrifices the health and morals of the rest — a critique that anticipates later progressive objections to competitive schooling. Her proposed solution is not to abolish emulation but to universalize the conditions under which it can be directed toward worthy objects. If all children — girls as well as boys, the poor as well as the rich — were educated together under a system that valued genuine intellectual achievement, emulation would serve the public good rather than private ambition.

### Vanity's Triumph Over Emulation

The most damning treatment of emulation in the text is also the briefest. Describing the effects of women's restricted education, Wollstonecraft writes that ignorance and cunning "render women very fond of dress, and produce all the vanity which such a fondness may naturally be expected to generate, to the exclusion of emulation and magnanimity" (013-041). The formula is stark: vanity excludes emulation and magnanimity. The more energy is invested in the competition for appearance, the less is available for the competition for excellence.

The pairing of emulation with magnanimity — greatness of soul — is telling. For Wollstonecraft, these are the virtues of a mind enlarged by education and directed toward public goods. Vanity is the virtue of a mind narrowed by restriction, directed toward private display. The exclusion is structural: the same limited education that makes women vain also makes them incapable of the emulation that would lead them out of vanity. It is a closed loop, and only the radical reform of education can break it.
