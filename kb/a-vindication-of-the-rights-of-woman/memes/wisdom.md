# Wisdom

## Definition

**Wisdom** occupies a distinct place in Wollstonecraft's conceptual architecture. Where [[reason]] is the faculty, [[knowledge]] the content, and [[virtue]] the practice, [[knowledge|wisdom]] is the integration of all three into the capacity for sound practical judgment. It is the quality that enables a person to navigate life's complexities without being swayed by [[love|passion]], [[prejudice]], or social pressure.

> "It is impossible for any man... to acquire sufficient knowledge and strength of mind to discharge the duties of a king, entrusted with uncontrolled power; how then must they be violated when his very elevation is an insuperable bar to the attainment of either wisdom or virtue?" (001-022)

The example of the king is telling: power without [[knowledge|wisdom]] produces tyranny. And just as absolute power makes [[knowledge|wisdom]] impossible for the monarch, Wollstonecraft implies, domestic power over women makes [[knowledge|wisdom]] about women's [[nature]] impossible for men. Neither the [[despotism|despot]] nor the master can be wise, because their position insulates them from the corrective of reality.

## In Context

Wisdom appears most often in negative contexts — Wollstonecraft identifies the *absence* of [[knowledge|wisdom]] as the consequence of social arrangements that prevent its cultivation. Kings lack [[knowledge|wisdom]] because flattery prevents self-[[knowledge]]. Women are denied [[knowledge|wisdom]] because their [[education]] trains them in accomplishments rather than judgment. Men lack [[knowledge|wisdom]] about women because their power over women prevents honest observation.

The positive account of [[knowledge|wisdom]] emerges in the [[education]]al proposals: a properly ordered [[education]] would cultivate [[knowledge|wisdom]] by training the judgment, exposing the mind to varied experience, and subordinating [[love|passion]] to [[reason]].

### Wisdom versus Cunning

Wollstonecraft draws a sharp distinction between wisdom and cunning — a distinction with gendered implications. "Cunning should not be opposed to wisdom, little cares to great exertions" (002-046). Cunning, in her analysis, is what women develop when denied access to genuine wisdom: the skill of manipulating others through indirection, flattery, and strategic compliance. It is the degraded form of intelligence available to the powerless — a way of achieving one's ends without ever openly asserting them. Wollstonecraft diagnoses cunning not as a female vice but as a structural consequence of women's subordination: when direct exercise of [[reason]] and open pursuit of one's goals are denied, indirect manipulation becomes the only available strategy. True wisdom, by contrast, requires the freedom to think and act for oneself — conditions that women's social position systematically denies them.

### The Structural Conditions of Wisdom

A recurring theme in Wollstonecraft's treatment of wisdom is that it cannot be cultivated in isolation. Wisdom requires exposure to varied experience, honest feedback from equals, and the opportunity to exercise [[judgment]] in consequential matters. These conditions are social and institutional, not merely individual: a society structured around hierarchy and dependence — whether the political hierarchy of monarchy or the domestic hierarchy of marriage — makes wisdom impossible for those at both the top and the bottom of the hierarchy. "It is impossible for any man to acquire sufficient knowledge and strength of mind to discharge the duties of a king, entrusted with uncontrolled power" (001-022). The same logic applies to husbands, fathers, and all who exercise unchallenged authority: their position cuts them off from the "jostlings of equality" (012-067) that alone can form sound judgment. This is one of Wollstonecraft's most radical claims: wisdom is not a private achievement but a social product, and unjust social arrangements make wisdom structurally impossible.

## Related Persons

- [[rousseau|Jean-Jacques Rousseau]] — Wollstonecraft implies that [[rousseau|Rousseau]], for all his intelligence, lacks [[knowledge|wisdom]] about women because his social position blinds him

Wollstonecraft's concept of wisdom thus completes her moral epistemology: [[reason]] is the faculty, [[knowledge]] the content, [[judgment]] the trained capacity, and wisdom the mature integration of all three into a well-lived life. Her insistence that wisdom requires equal social conditions — the "jostlings of equality" — makes it not merely an individual virtue but a political demand.
