**Wisdom** occupies a complex and ambivalent place in Wollstonecraft's moral vocabulary. On the one hand, she endorses the biblical injunction that "wisdom is the principal thing: THEREFORE get wisdom; and with all thy gettings get understanding" (005-093), treating wisdom as the highest attainment of the rational soul. On the other hand, she is sharply critical of what she calls "worldly wisdom" (005-153) — the prudential calculation that substitutes expediency for principle and that she identifies as a defining vice of her age.

The distinction between true wisdom and worldly wisdom is central to Wollstonecraft's argument. True wisdom is the fruit of reason, reflection, and the pursuit of virtue; it is "desirable on its own account" (005-051) and is inseparable from the love of God, who is "the fountain of wisdom, goodness, and power" (003-036). Worldly wisdom, by contrast, is the cunning of those who have learned to navigate a corrupt society without challenging its premises — "the wisdom of this world, contrasted with the wisdom from above" (005-125).

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's conception of true wisdom draws on multiple sources: the wisdom literature of the Hebrew Bible, the classical tradition of *sophia* as the integration of knowledge and virtue, and the Enlightenment ideal of the philosopher who subjects all claims to the test of reason. Wisdom, in her usage, is not merely knowledge — it is knowledge united with virtue and directed toward the good.

"Wisdom is desirable on its own account" (005-051) — the phrase is significant because Wollstonecraft rarely concedes that anything other than virtue has intrinsic value. Wisdom shares with virtue the quality of being its own reward, because the exercise of reason in pursuit of truth is itself a form of human flourishing.

The theological dimension is important. "To love God as the fountain of wisdom, goodness, and power, appears the only worship useful to a being who wishes to acquire either virtue or knowledge" (003-036). Wisdom is not a human achievement independent of God; it is participation in the divine nature through the cultivation of the rational soul.

Worldly wisdom is the counterfeit. "I should dread a refinement of wisdom as a deviation into folly" (005-100) — the fear is that intellectual sophistication unmoored from moral principle is worse than ignorance, because it equips the vicious with the tools of rationalisation. The "shuffling worldly wisdom of men" (005-153) is the wisdom of those who "forget that the only true God is love" and substitute calculation for charity.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument about wisdom functions as both a philosophical claim and a rhetorical strategy. Philosophically, she argues that wisdom — the integration of reason, virtue, and experience — is the proper goal of education for both sexes. Rhetorically, she uses the charge of "worldly wisdom" to expose the hypocrisy of those who profess virtue while practising expediency.

The philosophical argument begins with the claim that wisdom requires the cultivation of reason. "If wisdom is desirable on its own account, if virtue, to deserve the name, must be founded on knowledge" (005-051) — the conditional structure is deliberate. The pursuit of wisdom presupposes the pursuit of knowledge, and the pursuit of knowledge presupposes education. Women, who are denied education, are therefore denied the conditions under which wisdom can be acquired.

The connection between wisdom and virtue is essential. "To acquire wisdom and virtue by the exercise of reason" (005-122) — the three terms are inseparable. Reason is the instrument, wisdom is the understanding that results from its exercise, and virtue is the character that wisdom informs. A person who possesses knowledge without virtue is not wise but merely clever; a person who possesses virtue without knowledge is not wise but merely well-intentioned.

The critique of worldly wisdom is a critique of the social order. Worldly wisdom is the characteristic vice of those who benefit from existing arrangements and who deploy their intelligence to justify them. "The prudent voluptuary might enjoy the good things of this world, whilst he rioted in the follies of the next" (005-126) — the worldly wise man is one who has constructed a moral philosophy that leaves his pleasures undisturbed.

For women, the situation is different but equally damaging. Women are not taught worldly wisdom — they are taught no wisdom at all. "Women are told from their infancy ... that a little knowledge of human weakness, justly termed cunning ... will obtain for them the protection of man" (002-011). What is offered to women as wisdom is in fact "cunning" — the debased substitute for wisdom that dependence cultivates.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The eighteenth-century discourse of wisdom was shaped by the tension between classical and Christian conceptions. The classical tradition — Aristotle, Cicero, the Stoics — treated wisdom (*sophia*, *prudentia*) as the intellectual virtue that enables the exercise of all other virtues. The Christian tradition — Augustine, Aquinas, the devotional writers — treated wisdom as a gift of the Holy Spirit, distinct from and superior to merely human knowledge.

Wollstonecraft draws on both traditions while subjecting them to a gendered critique. Neither the classical philosopher nor the Christian divine had contemplated the possibility that women might be capable of wisdom; both had assumed, explicitly or implicitly, that wisdom was a male attainment. Wollstonecraft's insistence that women can and must pursue wisdom is a radical extension of both traditions.

The contrast with the "worldly wisdom" of commercial society is equally important. Adam Smith, Bernard Mandeville, and the tradition of political economy that Wollstonecraft implicitly engages treated prudence — the rational pursuit of self-interest — as a virtue. Wollstonecraft rejects this reduction of wisdom to calculation: the truly wise person does not merely calculate means to ends but questions the ends themselves.

## Key Passages

> Wisdom is desirable on its own account. If virtue, to deserve the name, must be founded on knowledge, let us endeavour to strengthen our minds by reflection, till our ideas are fixed and our judgments are rendered clear and consistent. (005-051)

> To love God as the fountain of wisdom, goodness, and power, appears the only worship useful to a being who wishes to acquire either virtue or knowledge. (003-036)

> I should dread a refinement of wisdom as a deviation into folly. (005-100)

> The shuffling worldly wisdom of men, who, forgetting that the only true God is love, substitute charity for justice, and imagine that they purchase heaven by giving that which they do not want, while they oppress those who have a claim on their justice. (005-153)
