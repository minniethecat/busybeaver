# Natural Affection

An instinctive emotional bond — particularly between parents and children — that Wollstonecraft acknowledges as a foundation of human virtue but argues must be cultivated by reason and education to achieve its full moral potential. The concept bridges her arguments about family life, education, and the development of civic character.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft distinguishes between mere instinctive affection and its cultivated, rational form. She acknowledges that "natural affections" exist as a universal human endowment — the bond between parent and child, the ties of family — but she insists that these instincts alone cannot sustain a just society. In their raw state, natural affections are vulnerable to being overridden by vanity and social ambition: "the natural affections and the most useful virtues are sacrificed" to the "weak wish" of making an appearance in the world (004-096).

The concept thus operates on two levels. At the descriptive level, natural affection names the instinctive emotional bonds that exist prior to education. At the normative level, it names what those bonds become when refined by reason — a moral capacity that "leaves instinctive natural affection far behind" (011-005). The distance between these two levels is the space where education and moral cultivation do their work.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's treatment of natural affection is inseparable from her critique of how society corrupts it. She charges that the fashionable education of women actively weakens natural affection by directing their attention away from domestic duties and toward public display. When girls are taught to value admiration above all else, "the natural affections and the most useful virtues are sacrificed" (004-096). The problem is not that natural affection is weak by nature — it is that society systematically undermines it.

The argument reaches its sharpest edge in her discussion of parental duty. Wollstonecraft insists that parental love must be more than instinct: "the parent who sedulously endeavours to form the heart and enlarge the understanding of his child, has given that dignity to the discharge of a duty, common to the whole animal world, that only reason can give" (011-005). Animals feel natural affection; only rational beings can transform it into moral education. This is the foundation of her argument for national education — if parents are to fulfill their duty, they must themselves be educated.

The absence of natural affection in women who have been raised to value only superficial accomplishments is, for Wollstonecraft, evidence of social corruption. She observes "the want of natural affection in many women, who are drawn from their nursery to acquire personal accomplishments" (012-089), tracing the defect not to female nature but to a system that prizes display over substance. The remedy is education that strengthens rather than severs the bonds of natural affection.

Wollstonecraft's vision extends from the family to the nation. "The charm, which natural affections, and unsophisticated feelings spread round the human character" (012-041) is not a private luxury but a public good. Citizens who have been raised with cultivated natural affections are prepared for the mutual obligations of a just society. Private virtue and public order share the same foundation.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Rousseau had celebrated natural sentiment in *Émile* but confined its cultivation to a domestic sphere that excluded women from public life. Wollstonecraft accepts the importance of natural affection but rejects the artificial separation between domestic feeling and public reason. Where Rousseau saw natural affection as a feminine quality best expressed in the home, Wollstonecraft argues that it must be cultivated in both sexes and brought to bear on public institutions, particularly through national education.

Edmund Burke's celebration of "natural" social bonds in his *Reflections on the Revolution in France* also stands in implicit contrast. Burke invoked natural affection to defend traditional hierarchies; Wollstonecraft invokes it to attack them, arguing that genuine natural affection flourishes only where individuals are free and equal. The parent who rules by prescription rather than reason does not foster natural affection — he extinguishes it.

## Key Passages

> But I will venture to assert, that their reason will never acquire sufficient strength to enable it to regulate their conduct, whilst the making an appearance in the world is the first wish of the majority of mankind. To this weak wish the natural affections and the most useful virtues are sacrificed. (004-096)

> The parent who sedulously endeavours to form the heart and enlarge the understanding of his child, has given that dignity to the discharge of a duty, common to the whole animal world, that only reason can give. This is the parental affection of humanity, and leaves instinctive natural affection far behind. (011-005)

> Men of the first genius, and most cultivated minds, have appeared to have the highest relish for the simple beauties of nature; and they must have forcibly felt, what they have so well described, the charm, which natural affections, and unsophisticated feelings spread round the human character. (012-041)

> Indeed, if it were only on this account, the national education of women is of the utmost consequence; for what a number of human sacrifices are made to that moloch, prejudice! (012-089)
