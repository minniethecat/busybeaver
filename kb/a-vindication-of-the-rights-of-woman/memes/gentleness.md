# Gentleness

A feminine ideal that Wollstonecraft distinguishes into two opposed forms: the authentic gentleness that arises from moral reflection and benevolence, and the counterfeit "insipid softness, varnished over with the name of gentleness" (002-036) that she argues is produced by women's subordination and serves to disguise their moral diminishment.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's treatment of gentleness is characteristically dialectical: she refuses either to endorse the quality uncritically or to dismiss it wholesale. "To recommend gentleness, indeed, on a broad basis is strictly philosophical," she concedes—"a frail being should labour to be gentle" (002-069). Yet she immediately qualifies this concession by warning that "when forbearance confounds right and wrong, it ceases to be a virtue" (002-069). The distinction she draws is between gentleness as a free moral disposition and gentleness as a survival strategy imposed by dependence. The woman who is gentle because she has been taught that compliance is her only path to security exhibits not virtue but the "vapid tenderness, which easily degenerates into contempt" (002-069). Genuine gentleness, by contrast, requires the moral autonomy to choose forbearance freely—a capacity that can only be developed through education and rational independence.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

The core of Wollstonecraft's argument about gentleness is that the quality, as conventionally prescribed to women, is not a virtue at all but a symptom of subordination. She associates the false gentleness of dependent women with a broader pattern of moral confusion in which "cunning should not be opposed to wisdom, little cares to great exertions, nor insipid softness, varnished over with the name of gentleness, to that fortitude which grand views alone can inspire" (002-036). The metaphor of "varnish" is crucial: the gentleness demanded of women is a surface polish that conceals rather than expresses character. It is manufactured by a system that rewards women for being agreeable and punishes them for being principled.

Wollstonecraft extends this critique to the sentimental discourse of courtship and marriage. She cites Dr. Fordyce's recommendation that women deploy gentleness as a strategy for winning and retaining male affection, observing drily that "beauty, gentleness, etc. etc. may gain a heart; but esteem, the only lasting affection, can alone be obtained by virtue supported by reason" (005-071). The invocation of "etc. etc." is itself a rhetorical dismissal: gentleness is merely one item in a catalogue of superficial charms, interchangeable with beauty and equally incapable of securing genuine regard. For Wollstonecraft, this instrumental gentleness—deployed to please rather than arising from principle—is the antithesis of the moral fortitude she champions.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Eighteenth-century conduct literature consistently prescribed gentleness as the cardinal feminine virtue. Writers from James Fordyce to John Gregory urged women to cultivate softness of manner, pliability of temper, and a yielding disposition. Wollstonecraft's intervention is radical not because she rejects gentleness outright but because she refuses to accept it as a self-evident good. By insisting that gentleness must be judged by its moral content rather than its social effects, she transforms the question from "are women gentle?" to "what kind of gentleness do women exhibit, and what conditions produce it?" This reframing is characteristic of her broader method: she takes a conventional virtue, subjects it to rational analysis, and reveals that what passes for virtue in women is too often the mark of their degradation.

## Key Passages

> It follows then, that cunning should not be opposed to wisdom, little cares to great exertions, nor insipid softness, varnished over with the name of gentleness, to that fortitude which grand views alone can inspire. (002-036)

> To recommend gentleness, indeed, on a broad basis is strictly philosophical. A frail being should labour to be gentle. But when forbearance confounds right and wrong, it ceases to be a virtue; and, however convenient it may be found in a companion, that companion will ever be considered as an inferior, and only inspire a vapid tenderness, which easily degenerates into contempt. (002-069)

> No, beauty, gentleness, etc. etc. may gain a heart; but esteem, the only lasting affection, can alone be obtained by virtue supported by reason. It is respect for the understanding that keeps alive tenderness for the person. (005-071)
