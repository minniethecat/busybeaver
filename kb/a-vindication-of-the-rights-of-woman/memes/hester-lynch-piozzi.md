# Hester Lynch Piozzi

[[Hester Lynch Piozzi]] (1741–1821), born Hester Lynch Salusbury and known for much of her life as Mrs. Thrale, was a Welsh-born writer, diarist, and prominent member of the Bluestocking circle — the informal network of intellectual women that flourished in late eighteenth-century London. In _[[A Vindication of the Rights of Woman]]_, Wollstonecraft singles her out for satirical treatment, making her the exemplar of a specifically female intellectual failing: the parroting of learned style without the substance of independent thought.

## Life

Hester Lynch Salusbury married the wealthy brewer Henry Thrale in 1763, entering a social world that included the most celebrated literary figure of the age, [[samuel-johnson|Samuel Johnson]]. Johnson became a frequent guest at the Thrales' Streatham Park, and Mrs. Thrale's detailed diaries and _Anecdotes of the Late Samuel Johnson_ (1786) remain among the most vivid records of Johnson's conversation and character.

After Thrale's death, Hester married the Italian musician Gabriel Piozzi in 1784 — a match that scandalised Johnson's circle and led to a break with Johnson himself. She spent much of her later life in Wales and Italy, producing poetry, travel writing, and the historical work _Retrospection_ (1801).

## Role in the Text

Wollstonecraft's single reference to Piozzi occurs in Chapter 5, in a passage on the intellectual pretensions encouraged by superficial female education:

> "Mrs. Piozzi, who often repeated by rote, what she did not understand, comes forward with Johnsonian periods." (005-100)

The charge is precise and devastating. Piozzi — who had listened to Johnson's conversation for years and absorbed his cadences — deploys his stylistic manner ("Johnsonian periods") without having internalised his critical method. She "repeated by rote what she did not understand": the very definition of the mechanical imitation that Wollstonecraft's educational philosophy is designed to eliminate.

The passage that follows — an extended quotation from Piozzi's advice to a newly married man — serves as exhibit A: a cascade of Johnsonian antitheses that, stripped of Johnson's intellectual pressure, collapse into platitude. For Wollstonecraft, Piozzi is not a villain but a symptom: the product of an educational system that teaches women to ornament their speech rather than to think.

## Key Ideas

Piozzi's role in the _Vindication_ is representative rather than personal. She stands for the Bluestocking phenomenon in its most compromised form — women who had access to intellectual society but were never trained to participate in it as equals. Wollstonecraft's critique of Piozzi is thus not an attack on female intellectual ambition but a demonstration of its corruption when denied the foundation of rigorous education. The tragedy is not that Piozzi wrote but that she was taught to write before she was taught to think.
