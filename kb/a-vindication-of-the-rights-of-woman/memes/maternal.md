# Maternal

Wollstonecraft's treatment of the maternal role — encompassing maternal duties, maternal affection, and the maternal character — redefines motherhood as a rational and civic responsibility rather than mere biological instinct. She elevates maternal duty to a "sacred heroic cast" (003-054), arguing that the mother's role in educating children makes her central to the formation of virtuous citizens and the progress of civilization.

## Definition

For Wollstonecraft, "maternal" is not reducible to biological motherhood or sentimental attachment. It is a moral and intellectual office — the mother as first educator, character-former, and civic actor. "The maternal solicitude of a reasonable affectionate woman is very interesting" (009-006): the qualifying adjective "reasonable" is essential, distinguishing Wollstonecraft's vision from the sentimental idealization of motherhood common in her era. True maternal affection, in her account, is "enlightened" (010-006) — informed by reason and directed toward the child's long-term development as a rational and virtuous being, not merely indulged as instinct.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument proceeds along two tracks. First, she insists that maternal duty is a form of civic virtue: the mother who educates her children well contributes directly to the health of the nation. She "thinks that not only the eye of the parent, but of the legislator ought to be open" (003-054), linking the domestic sphere to the political order. This is a radical claim — the mother's work is not merely private but public in its consequences.

Second, she argues that women cannot fulfill even their maternal duties properly without education. The uneducated mother "seldom exerts enlightened maternal affection; for she either neglects her children, or spoils them by improper indulgence" (010-006). The practical consequence is clear: denying women education harms not only women but the next generation. "The active principle which gives a sacred heroic cast to her maternal duties" (003-054) is only possible when women themselves have been educated to reason.

Wollstonecraft is particularly critical of the way fashionable society "incapacitates the maternal character" (012-090) by encouraging women to prioritize social display over child-rearing. She argues that the qualities needed for good mothering — patience, fortitude, judgment — "united with the fortitude which distinguishes true activity" (012-094) — are precisely the qualities that fashionable education suppresses.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Rousseau's *Émile* idealized the mother as a tender caregiver led by nature and sentiment, not reason. Wollstonecraft explicitly counters this with her insistence on "enlightened maternal affection" (010-006) — affection shaped by understanding, not mere instinct. She also challenges the prevalent double standard by which mothers were sentimentalized in literature while being denied the education that would make them effective educators of their children.

## Key Passages

> "The active principle which gives a sacred heroic cast to her maternal duties. She thinks that not only the eye of the parent, but of the legislator ought to be open to perceive the consequences of the present system." (003-054)

> "A woman of feeling must respect herself to such a degree as never to behave indecently even to him she loves the best. The maternal solicitude of a reasonable affectionate woman is very interesting, and the chastened dignity with which she fulfills the duties of her station adorns her character." (009-006)

> "The parent who sedulously endeavors to form the heart and enlarge the understanding of his child, has given a much greater proof of affection, than he who only, governed by blind instinct, provides for its animal wants." (010-006)

> "A fine lady seldom exerts enlightened maternal affection; for she either neglects her children, or spoils them by improper indulgence, to make them amends for the apparent neglect." (010-006)

> "The being who is thus dependent is obliged to have recourse to low cunning, and cunning ever tends to incapacitate the maternal character, takes woman out of her sphere, and makes her an intriguing being." (012-090)
