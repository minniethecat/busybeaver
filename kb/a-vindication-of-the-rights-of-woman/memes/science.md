# Science

The systematic pursuit of knowledge through reason and empirical investigation, which Wollstonecraft presents as an essential component of women's education and a counterforce to the artificial accomplishments that eighteenth-century society prescribed for the female sex.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft uses "science" in the broad eighteenth-century sense encompassing systematic knowledge across all domains—moral, political, and natural. She regards the "science of politics" as still "in its infancy" (002-085), suggesting that the rational investigation of governance and human relations remains undeveloped. This aligns with her Enlightenment conviction that all fields of human inquiry can and should be subjected to rational analysis. Science, in her framework, is not a specialised male pursuit but the foundation of sound judgment, which women are equally capable of acquiring if granted access to education. She contrasts genuine scientific understanding with superficial accomplishments, arguing that women have been denied "the elements of science" (012-052) in favour of decorative attainments that leave the mind uncultivated.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft integrates the concept of science into her broader argument for women's rational education at multiple levels. First, she observes that the exclusion of women from scientific knowledge is a deliberate mechanism of subordination: women are taught "the whole science of wantonness" (004-082) rather than genuine knowledge, reducing them to objects of male desire. Second, she insists that scientific education is indispensable for women's practical roles—as nurses, mothers, and domestic managers—arguing that "women, to guard against the errors of ignorance, should be taught the elements of anatomy and medicine" (012-092). Third, she connects scientific understanding to moral development: the habit of patient, systematic inquiry that science cultivates is precisely the mental discipline that women need to escape the "cramping" (005-036) of their minds by superficial social expectations.

Wollstonecraft's advocacy for women's scientific education is also tied to her political vision. She argues that "the science of politics is in its infancy" (002-085) precisely because half of humanity has been excluded from rational public life. A truly enlightened society would recognise that women's participation in scientific and political discourse is essential to progress. In her educational proposals, she envisions co-educational schools where girls and boys alike study "the elements of science, and continue the study of history and politics, on a more extensive scale" (012-052), treating intellectual development as a universal human right rather than a male prerogative.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft's insistence on women's scientific education stood in stark contrast to the prevailing educational philosophy of her time. Rousseau, in *Émile*, had argued that women's education should be entirely relational—designed to please and serve men—and explicitly excluded them from abstract inquiry. Wollstonecraft repudiates this view, contending that any being capable of reason has a right to cultivate it. While conduct-book writers like Gregory and Fordyce emphasised "female accomplishments"—music, drawing, ornamental needlework—Wollstonecraft insisted that such pursuits were a "science of wantonness" (004-082) that degraded rather than elevated the female character. She acknowledges that men "wasted the lamp of life" (003-005) in solitary study, but her remedy is not to exclude women from science; rather, it is to bring women into the intellectual community so that both sexes might benefit from mutual rational engagement.

## Key Passages

> Brutal force has hitherto governed the world, and that the science of politics is in its infancy, is evident from philosophers scrupling to give the knowledge most useful to man that determinate distinction. (002-085)

> The young people of superior abilities, or fortune, might now be taught, in another school, the dead and living languages, the elements of science, and continue the study of history and politics, on a more extensive scale, which would not exclude polite literature. Girls and boys still together? I hear some readers ask: yes. (012-052)

> In public schools women, to guard against the errors of ignorance, should be taught the elements of anatomy and medicine, not only to enable them to take proper care of their own health, but to make them rational nurses of their infants, parents, and husbands. (012-092)

> A woman who has lost her honour, imagines that she cannot fall lower, and as for recovering her former station, it is impossible; no exertion can wash this stain away. Losing thus every spur, and having no other means of support, prostitution becomes her only refuge, and the character is quickly depraved by circumstances over which the poor victim has little power, unless she possesses an uncommon portion of sense. (004-082)
