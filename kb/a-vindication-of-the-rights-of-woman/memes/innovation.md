# Innovation

The progressive transformation of institutions and practices that Wollstonecraft defends against the entrenched "fear of innovation" (012-011) she identifies as the most stubborn obstacle to the reform of female education and the advancement of women's rights.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's concept of innovation is inseparable from her Enlightenment conviction that social institutions should be judged by reason rather than precedent. Innovation, in her usage, does not mean change for its own sake but the deliberate reform of practices that have failed the test of rational scrutiny. She opens her argument by asserting that "the divine right of husbands, like the divine right of kings, may, it is to be hoped, in this enlightened age, be contested without danger" (003-012), framing her call for innovation in women's condition as continuous with the broader Enlightenment assault on arbitrary authority. The "narrow-minded" who "rail with thoughtless vehemence at innovation" (003-012) are, in her analysis, not defending genuine tradition but protecting their own unearned privileges from rational examination.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's defence of innovation operates on two levels: a principled argument for the necessity of reform, and a tactical rejoinder to those who counsel patience. On the principled level, she insists that reason and justice are not bound by custom. Those who trust in the power of enlightenment "do not rest in expedients, or grant that what is metaphysically true can be practically false" (010-004). Instead, "disdaining the shifts of the moment they calmly wait till time, sanctioning innovation, silences the hiss of selfishness or envy" (010-004). The organic metaphor is carefully chosen: innovation is not a violent rupture but a natural maturation, sanctioned by time and the progressive unfolding of rational principles.

On the tactical level, Wollstonecraft confronts the argument that innovation in female education should be deferred until public opinion is ready. Her response is scorching. "The fear of innovation, in this country, extends to every thing," she writes, characterising its proponents as "indolent slugs, who guard, by sliming it over, the snug place, which they consider in the light of an hereditary estate" (012-011). This is the language of moral indictment, not scholarly debate. The opponents of innovation are not prudent conservatives but selfish obstructionists, protecting their comfort at the expense of justice. Wollstonecraft extends this critique to the universities in particular, where she finds "this barbarous aversion to innovation carried still further" (012-088), noting with bitter irony that "a small portion of knowledge as those women attained, who have sneeringly been termed learned women, could be singular" only because institutions devoted to learning had so thoroughly resisted reform (012-088).

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Edmund Burke's *Reflections on the Revolution in France* (1790) had made the case against innovation the centrepiece of conservative political philosophy, arguing that gradual organic change was preferable to rationalist schemes of reform. Wollstonecraft's position—articulated in her *Vindication of the Rights of Men* (1790) and carried forward into the *Vindication of the Rights of Woman*—directly contests this Burkean conservatism. Where Burke saw inherited institutions as reservoirs of collective wisdom, Wollstonecraft saw them as fortifications of inherited injustice. Her insistence that innovation be judged by its conformity to rational principles rather than its compatibility with existing arrangements places her firmly in the radical Enlightenment tradition. Yet her invocation of "time, sanctioning innovation" (010-004) suggests that she was not insensitive to the conservative insight that lasting reform requires cultural as well as institutional change.

## Key Passages

> The divine right of husbands, like the divine right of kings, may, it is to be hoped, in this enlightened age, be contested without danger, and though conviction may not silence many boisterous disputants, yet, when any prevailing prejudice is attacked, the wise will consider, and leave the narrow-minded to rail with thoughtless vehemence at innovation. (003-012)

> They do not rest in expedients, or grant that what is metaphysically true can be practically false; but disdaining the shifts of the moment they calmly wait till time, sanctioning innovation, silences the hiss of selfishness or envy. (010-004)

> But the fear of innovation, in this country, extends to every thing. This is only a covert fear, the apprehensive timidity of indolent slugs, who guard, by sliming it over, the snug place, which they consider in the light of an hereditary estate; and eat, drink, and enjoy themselves, instead of fulfilling the duties, excepting a few empty forms, for which it was endowed. (012-011)

> Nay, has not a little rationality exposed many women to the severest censure? I advert to well known-facts, for I have frequently heard women ridiculed, and every little weakness exposed, only because they adopted the advice of a medical author, and deviated from the beaten track in their mode of treating their infants. (012-088)
