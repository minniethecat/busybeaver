fl. 1775. A clergyman who became acquainted with Mary Wollstonecraft before her sixteenth year, mentioned in a single sentence of the Biographical Sketch as part of Wollstonecraft's early intellectual circle.

## Life

Almost nothing is known of Mr. Clare beyond the single mention in the Biographical Sketch appended to posthumous editions of _A Vindication of the Rights of Woman_. He appears to have been a clergyman who moved in the same provincial circles as the young Wollstonecraft. His name is recorded alongside that of Frances Blood — Wollstonecraft's closest friend and formative influence — suggesting he was part of the small intellectual network that nurtured Wollstonecraft's early development.

The surname "Clare" is not uncommon in eighteenth-century English clerical records, but no specific identification has been firmly established by Wollstonecraft's biographers. His obscurity is itself historically significant: it marks the limits of what can be known about the formative relationships of young women in the period, whose early lives were rarely documented with the care accorded to their male contemporaries.

## Role in the Text

The Biographical Sketch records: "Before her sixteenth year she became acquainted with Mr. Clare a clergyman, and Miss Frances Blood; the latter, two years older than herself, was the most intimate friend she ever had" (P01-006). Mr. Clare appears alongside Frances Blood as one of the two significant acquaintances of Wollstonecraft's adolescence.

The mention is brief but structurally significant. The Biographical Sketch — written by Wollstonecraft's widower, William Godwin, or an anonymous editor — assembles a narrative of Wollstonecraft's intellectual formation. Mr. Clare, as a clergyman, may have provided the young Wollstonecraft with access to books, theological discussion, or social connections that furthered her education. His pairing with Frances Blood in the same sentence suggests both were part of the circle that sustained Wollstonecraft's precocious intellectual development before her departure from home.

### Works Referenced

None. Mr. Clare is not known to have published any writings.

## Key Ideas

Mr. Clare exemplifies the minor but meaningful figures who populate the margins of intellectual biography. His brief appearance in the Biographical Sketch testifies to the informal, relationship-based networks through which eighteenth-century women — excluded from universities and formal intellectual institutions — accessed education and intellectual community. Like Frances Blood, he belongs to the pre-history of Wollstonecraft's public career, a figure whose influence can only be inferred from the trajectory of the life he briefly touched.
