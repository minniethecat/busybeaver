# Docility

The prescribed feminine virtue of compliant meekness that Wollstonecraft exposes as a socially manufactured substitute for genuine moral character — demanded of women by an educational system designed to produce obedience rather than excellence.

## Definition

Docility occupies a distinctive place in Wollstonecraft's catalogue of false feminine virtues. It is, she observes, systematically "recommended as the cardinal virtues of the sex" (002-068) alongside gentleness and a "spaniel-like affection." The choice of the canine simile is deliberate and devastating: the qualities demanded of women are those of a well-trained animal, not a rational being.

Wollstonecraft's analysis of docility appears at the very opening of the *Vindication*, in her introduction, where she announces her intention to dismiss "those pretty feminine phrases, which the men condescendingly use to soften our slavish dependence, and despising that weak elegancy of mind, exquisite sensibility, and sweet docility of manners, supposed to be the sexual characteristics of the weaker vessel" (P03-009). From the outset, docility is framed not as a virtue to be cultivated but as a rhetorical device employed by men to dignify women's subordination.

What makes docility particularly insidious, in Wollstonecraft's analysis, is its superficial resemblance to genuine moral qualities. Gentleness, patience, and humility are real virtues; docility is their counterfeit, distinguished by the absence of rational agency. The genuinely gentle person chooses forbearance from strength; the docile person complies from weakness.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft constructs her critique of docility around the relationship between character and social circumstance. She does not deny that docility exists as a female trait; she denies that it is natural or virtuous. Her argument is environmental: "Gentleness, docility, and a spaniel-like affection are, on this ground, consistently recommended as the cardinal virtues of the sex" (002-068). The passive construction is significant — these qualities are "recommended," not discovered. They are prescribed by a social order that benefits from female compliance.

The mechanism by which docility is produced is defective education. Wollstonecraft observes that "women are told from their infancy, and taught by the example of their mothers, that a little knowledge of human weakness, justly termed cunning, softness of temper, outward obedience, and a scrupulous attention to a puerile kind of propriety, will obtain for them the protection of man" (004-026). Docility is not an innate disposition but a survival strategy, rationally adopted by beings who have been denied the resources for genuine independence.

The moral psychology of docility is Wollstonecraft's deepest concern. She warns that "the being who patiently endures injustice, and silently bears insults, will soon become unjust, or unable to discern right from wrong" (005-020). Docility is not morally neutral; it corrodes the capacity for moral judgment. A person trained to accept whatever treatment she receives loses the ability to distinguish justice from injustice, virtue from vice.

Wollstonecraft draws a crucial distinction between docility and genuine gentleness. The latter arises from "constitutional good humour" and a cultivated moral sense; the former is "that docility, which fear stamps on the beholder" (005-020). One is the expression of character; the other is the suppression of it. The entire apparatus of feminine education, she argues, is designed to produce the second while calling it the first.

The consequence of universal docility is the erasure of moral individuality. "All women are to be levelled, by meekness and docility, into one character of yielding softness" (005-068). A system that produces identical docility in all women is a system that has destroyed the diversity of human character that nature intended.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The conduct literature of Wollstonecraft's era universally praised docility as a feminine excellence. Gregory called it "amiable"; Fordyce celebrated the "sweet docility of manners" that Wollstonecraft's introduction scorned. Rousseau made Sophie's docility the centrepiece of her education. Wollstonecraft's innovation is to reframe docility not as a virtue but as a symptom — the visible sign of a mind that has been prevented from developing its own principles and must therefore borrow them from others.

## Key Passages

> Dismissing then those pretty feminine phrases, which the men condescendingly use to soften our slavish dependence, and despising that weak elegancy of mind, exquisite sensibility, and sweet docility of manners, supposed to be the sexual characteristics of the weaker vessel, I wish to show that elegance is inferior to virtue, that the first object of laudable ambition is to obtain a character as a human being, regardless of the distinction of sex. (P03-009)

> Gentleness, docility, and a spaniel-like affection are, on this ground, consistently recommended as the cardinal virtues of the sex; and, disregarding the arbitrary economy of nature, the writers of male morality have exclusively restricted female excellence to this single virtue, though order has been disturbed by such a narrow, and I may say, partial interpretation of the divine law. (002-068)

> The being who patiently endures injustice, and silently bears insults, will soon become unjust, or unable to discern right from wrong. Besides, I deny the fact, this is not the true way to form or meliorate the temper; for, as a sex, men have better tempers than women, because they are occupied by pursuits that interest the head as well as the heart. (005-020)

> Men are allowed by moralists to cultivate, as nature directs, different qualities, and assume the different characters, that the same passions, modified almost to infinity, give to each individual. A virtuous man may have a choleric or a sanguine constitution, be gay or grave, unreproved; be firm till be is almost over-bearing, or, weakly submissive, have no will or opinion of his own; but all women are to be levelled, by meekness and docility, into one character of yielding softness. (005-068)

> Men look for beauty and the simper of good humoured docility: women are captivated by easy manners: a gentleman-like man seldom fails to please them, and their thirsty ears eagerly drink the insinuating nothings of politeness, whilst they turn from the unintelligible sounds of the charmer — reason, charm he never so wisely. (006-012)
