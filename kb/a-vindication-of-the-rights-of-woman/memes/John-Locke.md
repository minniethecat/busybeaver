## Overview

John Locke (1632–1704) was an English philosopher and physician, widely regarded as one of the most influential Enlightenment thinkers. His work on epistemology, particularly the theory that the mind is a *tabula rasa* at birth and that all knowledge derives from experience, laid the foundation for eighteenth-century empiricism. His *Essay Concerning Human Understanding* (1689) and *Some Thoughts Concerning Education* (1693) were widely read and discussed throughout the period.

## Role in the *Vindication*

Wollstonecraft cites Locke once in the *Vindication*, and the reference is approving. In Chapter XI, she writes that "Mr. Locke very judiciously observes" [011-015] — a rare straightforward endorsement in a text more often critical than laudatory. The context is her discussion of the association of ideas: Wollstonecraft follows Locke in arguing that habitual associations, formed through experience, shape character. This Lockean framework underlies her central argument that women's apparent weaknesses are not innate but the product of the associations cultivated by a degrading education.

Though Locke is cited only once directly, his influence on Wollstonecraft's thought runs deeper than the single reference suggests. Her empiricist method — appealing to observation and experience rather than abstract principle — is Lockean in spirit, as is her conviction that the mind is shaped by education and environment rather than fixed by nature.
