# Gilbert Imlay

[[Gilbert Imlay]] (1754?–1828?) was an American adventurer, land speculator, and writer whose relationship with [[Mary Wollstonecraft]] during the French Revolution became one of the most consequential episodes of her life. His treatment of her, recorded in the Biographical Sketch appended to the _Vindication_, illustrates with painful clarity the vulnerability that even the most intellectually independent woman of the age could suffer through the affections.

## Life

Imlay served as a captain in the American Revolutionary War before turning to land speculation and writing. His _Topographical Description of the Western Territory of North America_ (1792) promoted emigration to the American frontier. By 1792 he was in [[france|Paris]], moving in the circles of British and American expatriates drawn to Revolutionary France. It was there, in the same city where Wollstonecraft had gone to witness the Revolution at first hand, that their acquaintance began.

The Biographical Sketch records the meeting plainly: "In 1792 she removed to Paris, where she became acquainted with Gilbert Imlay, of the United States. And from this acquaintance grew an attachment, which brought the parties together, without legal formalities" (P01-014). This arrangement — a union outside marriage, which Wollstonecraft defended on principle given Imlay's alleged "family embarrassments" — reflected her willingness to live by her philosophical convictions, however costly the consequence.

## Role in the Text

Imlay does not appear in Wollstonecraft's substantive argument; he belongs entirely to the biographical record that frames the work. Yet his presence haunts the _Vindication_ in a different sense — the emotional devastation he caused would become the subject of Wollstonecraft's later _Letters Written During a Short Residence in Sweden, Norway, and Denmark_ (1796).

The Biographical Sketch records the arc of estrangement with restrained but devastating concision:

> "Before she went to England, she had some gloomy forebodings that the affections of Imlay, had waned, if they were not estranged from her; on her arrival, those forebodings were sorrowfully confirmed." (P01-015)

The reunion in London brought no reconciliation. Imlay's "attentions were too formal and constrained to pass unobserved by her penetration" (P01-016). The Biographical Sketch records that "she had planned her own destruction, from which Imlay prevented her" — a first suicide attempt — followed by a second: "She conceived the idea of suicide a second time, and threw herself into the Thames; she remained in the water, until consciousness forsook her, but she was taken up and resuscitated" (P01-016).

After two years of "divers attempts to revive the affections of Imlay, with sundry explanations and professions on his part," Wollstonecraft "resolved finally to forgo all hope of reclaiming him" (P01-016). The final private interview "did not produce any painful emotions" — a detail that marks, as much as any philosophical passage in the _Vindication_, the disciplined mastery of feeling that Wollstonecraft considered the office of reason.

## Key Ideas

Wollstonecraft's own words on resigning her attachment crystallise the argument running through the _Vindication_: that reason must govern the passions, however painfully:

> "Love, dear delusion! Rigorous reason has forced me to resign; and now my rational prospects are blasted, just as I have learned to be contented with rational enjoyments." (P01-016)

The experience with Imlay tested the very philosophy of rational self-command that the _Vindication_ advocates for women. That Wollstonecraft emerged from the crisis — and went on to write some of her finest work — is testimony to the resilience that her philosophy of reason aimed to cultivate.
