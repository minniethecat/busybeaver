# Despotism

Despotism in Wollstonecraft's analysis denotes arbitrary, unchecked power — exercised in the state, the military, and the household. Her key insight is the structural connection between forms of despotism: political despotism and domestic despotism mutually reinforce one another.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft identifies despotism as a mode of power characterized by the absence of rational accountability. In the military context, "despotism is necessary to give vigour to..." (001-026) — a claim she makes descriptively, observing how hierarchical institutions require arbitrary power to function. But this observation is also a critique: institutions built on despotism deform the character of those within them.

In gender relations, she identifies "a mixture of gallantry and despotism in society" (002-027) — men exercise despotic power over women while cloaking it in the language of protection and gallantry. This is the core of her analysis: despotism disguised as benevolence is more insidious than overt tyranny because it resists critique.

Most tellingly, she declares that "one kind of despotism supports another" (010-001) — political tyranny and domestic tyranny are not separate phenomena but manifestations of the same principle. A man who is a despot in his household cannot be a genuine republican in the state.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft traces the effects of despotism on character: "when a destructive system of despotism had formed their character" (004-030), people become incapable of freedom. This is a psychological argument — despotism does not merely constrain action; it deforms the capacity for self-governance. This explains why women, long subjected to domestic despotism, may appear to consent to their subordination.

She anticipates the "gloom of despotism subsides" (002-074) with the progress of Enlightenment, expressing her hope that rational principles will displace arbitrary power. Yet she is clear-eyed about the mechanisms: despotism "kills virtue and genius" (003-023) — it is not merely a political arrangement but a moral poison.

The metaphor of the "artful chain of despotism" (004-018) captures her analysis of how despotic power is maintained: not through overt force alone but through a system of interlocking constraints — legal, social, educational — that bind women to subordination.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Montesquieu and Enlightenment political theory distinguished despotism from monarchy and republicanism as forms of government. Wollstonecraft extends this political analysis into the domestic sphere, arguing that the family is the nursery of political character: children raised under domestic despotism will grow into citizens fit only for political despotism.
