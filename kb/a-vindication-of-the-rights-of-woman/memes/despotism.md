## Definition

Despotism, for Wollstonecraft, is not merely a political category describing corrupt monarchies — it is a relational dynamic that operates wherever power is exercised without accountability. She identifies [[tyranny|despotism]] in the state ("the sacred majesty of Kings" [001-023]), in the military ("every corps is a chain of despots, who, submitting and tyrannizing without exercising their [[reason]]" [001-025]), and, crucially, in the household. Her innovation is to connect these domains: the same logic of arbitrary power that corrupts political life also corrupts domestic life.

Wollstonecraft's analysis of [[tyranny|despotism]] is psychological as well as political. Despotism "is necessary to give vigour to enterprises that one will directs" [001-024], but this efficiency comes at a moral cost: those who exercise arbitrary power are themselves corrupted by it, and those who submit to it are reduced to "dead weights of vice and folly on the community" [001-025]. The despot and the slave are locked in a mutually degrading relationship — a theme that runs through her critique of marriage as well as her political philosophy.

## In Context

Despotism features most prominently in Chapter I, where Wollstonecraft critiques the institutional structures of power in European society. After attacking "the sacred majesty of Kings" [001-023], she turns to the military as an example of how "subordination and rigour are the very sinews of military discipline; and [[tyranny|despotism]] is necessary to give vigour to enterprises that one will directs" [001-024]. The critique extends to "every profession, in which great subordination of rank constitutes its power" [001-023], which she argues "is highly injurious to [[morality]]."

In Chapters III and IV, Wollstonecraft extends the analysis of [[tyranny|despotism]] to the domestic sphere, arguing that the arbitrary authority of husbands over wives replicates the structure of political [[tyranny|despotism]] in miniature. The wife who must submit to her husband's will without recourse to [[reason]] is in the position of a subject under a despot — and the moral effects are identical.

## Key Passages

> "...every profession, in which great subordination of rank constitutes its power, is highly injurious to morality." [001-023]

> "A standing army, for instance, is incompatible with freedom; because subordination and rigour are the very sinews of military discipline; and despotism is necessary to give vigour to enterprises that one will directs." [001-024]

> "Every corps is a chain of despots, who, submitting and tyrannizing without exercising their reason, become dead weights of vice and folly on the community." [001-025]
