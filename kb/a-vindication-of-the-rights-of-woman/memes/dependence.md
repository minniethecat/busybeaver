For Wollstonecraft, **dependence** is the structural condition that defines women's social existence and the primary obstacle to their moral and intellectual development. She opens the *Dedication* by declaring, "Independence I have long considered as the grand blessing of life" (P02-002), establishing the binary of independence and dependence as the work's organising axis. Dependence, in her analysis, is neither natural nor inevitable — it is a condition manufactured by education, law, and custom.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft uses "dependence" in multiple registers: economic dependence on men for subsistence, intellectual dependence produced by deficient education, and moral dependence — the habit of looking to others for approval rather than to one's own rational judgment. She argues these forms are mutually reinforcing: "dependence of body naturally produces dependence of mind" (003-021). The phrase "slavish dependence" (P03-009, 003-027) recurs throughout the *Vindication*, linking women's condition to the language of political tyranny and slavery.

She defines true independence as a quality of character rather than mere economic self-sufficiency: "the true definition of independence" (005-028) involves bearing "bodily inconveniences" with fortitude and thinking for oneself. Dependence, conversely, is the abdication of moral and intellectual agency — "to act according to the will of another" (003-042) rather than one's own reason.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's central argument about dependence is causal: women's vices and weaknesses are not innate but the result of a system that "rendered them dependent — dependence is called natural" (003-014). The circular logic, whereby dependence is imposed and then naturalised, is a key target of her critique. She repeatedly exposes the sophistry of arguments that treat socially constructed dependence as biological destiny.

The material dimension matters. Wollstonecraft describes the "bitter bread of dependence" (004-056) with visceral force, recognising that women who must marry for subsistence cannot exercise moral judgment freely. Economic dependence corrupts character: women are "educated for dependence, that is, to act according to the will of another" (003-042). The solution is neither charity nor chivalry but "independence of character" founded on "independence of mind" (005-057).

She also identifies a subtler form of dependence in the sentimental tradition. "The submissive demeanour of dependence" and "the support of weakness" (002-067) are taught to women as virtues, reinforcing the very condition that degrades them. Gregory's advice that a wife should "soften our slavish dependence" (P03-009) exemplifies the complicity of conduct literature in perpetuating the dependence it purports to ease.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Rousseau, in *Émile*, naturalised female dependence as the design of nature: woman is made "to please man" and is thus naturally dependent. Wollstonecraft directly counters this, arguing that "a state of dependence being natural to the sex" (005-015) is a claim made to justify existing power relations, not a fact observed in nature. She also challenges the common eighteenth-century metaphor of man as the oak and woman as the ivy — the ivy that "clasps the oak" is a being without independent structure, and for Wollstonecraft, that is a condition to be resisted, not romanticised.
