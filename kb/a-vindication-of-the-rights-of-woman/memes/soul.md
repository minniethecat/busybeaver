# Soul

## Definition

In the *Vindication*, **soul** carries a double significance. In its theological
sense, it is the immortal essence that relates each human being to God, and
that guarantees the equality of all souls — male and female — before their
Creator. In its philosophical sense, it is the seat of reason, conscience, and
moral capacity — what Wollstonecraft also calls the "understanding" or "mind."

## The Soul and Equality

The theological argument for women's equality is one of Wollstonecraft's most
powerful rhetorical weapons. If women have immortal souls — and no Christian
theologian denied this — then they must possess the same rational and moral
faculties as men. God would not endow half the human race with souls and then
deny them the means to cultivate those souls:

> "if she be allowed to have an immortal soul, she must have an employment
> suited to it" (004-052).

A soul requires cultivation — through education, through the exercise of reason,
through the performance of duty. To deny women education is, in effect, to
condemn their souls to stagnation.

## The Soul and Reason

Wollstonecraft identifies the soul closely with reason. The "divine spark" that
elevates humanity above the animals is the rational faculty — the capacity to
understand, to judge, and to choose. The soul is not a passive receptacle of
divine grace but an active power that must be exercised and developed. This is
why education matters in the ultimate sense: it is the cultivation of the soul.

## Body and Soul

The body-soul distinction serves Wollstonecraft's argument against the reduction
of women to their physical attributes. If women are valued only for their bodies
— for beauty, for childbearing, for sexual pleasure — their souls are neglected.
A society that treats women as "mere animals" or "playthings" denies their
spiritual nature. The reform of women's condition is, at its deepest level, a
spiritual imperative — a demand that women be recognized and treated as beings
with immortal souls.

## The Soul and Moral Independence

Wollstonecraft goes beyond the simple assertion of the soul's immortality to
argue for its active cultivation through moral independence. A soul that is
never permitted to exercise reason remains dormant, no matter its immortal
nature. She insists that women must be allowed to "exercise their own reason"
and become "independent" (002-086); without this, the soul's potential is
squandered. The "dignity of conscious virtue" — a state in which the rational
soul governs conduct — is Wollstonecraft's ideal for all human beings (002-043).

This emphasis on the active soul sets Wollstonecraft apart from more
conventional religious writers of her time. She does not merely appeal to the
soul as a passive recipient of salvation but as a faculty requiring disciplined
exercise. The being who "impressed them on my soul, and gave me sufficient
strength" (002-089) demands that the strength be used — that reason, virtue, and
knowledge be cultivated as acts of spiritual obedience.
