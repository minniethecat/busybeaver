The concept of the **soul** is the theological linchpin of Wollstonecraft's argument for sexual equality. If women have souls — and Wollstonecraft takes this as a given of Christian doctrine — then they possess the same immortal, rational nature as men, and any education or social arrangement that treats them as merely bodily beings is not only irrational but impious.

The point is made with characteristic directness early in the *Vindication*: "allowing them to have souls, there is but one way appointed by Providence to lead mankind to either virtue or happiness" (002-001). The inference is immediate and devastating: if souls are sexless, then the path to virtue must be the same for both sexes, and the elaborate apparatus of differentiated female education collapses.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's concept of the soul draws on the Christian Platonic tradition — the soul is the immortal, rational essence that distinguishes human beings from animals and connects them to the divine. She speaks of "the soul" not as a metaphor but as a theological reality with direct moral and educational implications.

The soul is, in her account, the seat of reason and the capacity for virtue. Those "nobler passions that open and enlarge the soul" (P03-013) are contrasted with the "prevalent fondness for pleasure" that contracts and diminishes it. The soul's greatness is measured by its capacity for rational aspiration, not by its sensitivity to pleasure or pain.

The theological stake is explicit: "Surely she has not an immortal soul who can loiter life away, merely employed to adorn her person" (002-051). This is not a rhetorical question but a logical demonstration. If women have immortal souls — and Wollstonecraft assumes no Christian can deny this — then an education devoted entirely to the body and its adornment is a contradiction of the most fundamental religious truth.

The concept of the soul also grounds Wollstonecraft's argument against the sexual double standard in virtue. If "the soul is not immortal" (002-060), then a different standard might be justified; but since it is, the same standard must apply. The "inferiority" of women "must either arise from a weak understanding or a defect of soul" (002-072) — and since no one seriously claims women lack souls, the only remaining explanation is defective education.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft deploys the concept of the soul strategically, using it to turn the religious arguments of her conservative opponents against them. If God created both sexes with immortal souls, then any attempt to deny women the cultivation of those souls — through education, through participation in public life, through the exercise of reason — is not merely unjust but impious.

This argument has particular force against the religious writers — Fordyce, Gregory, and the conduct-book tradition — whom Wollstonecraft subjects to sustained critique. These writers claimed divine authority for women's subordination; Wollstonecraft counters that the very doctrine of the soul they profess entails women's equality.

The rhetoric intensifies when she contemplates the alternative. To deny women souls is to "deprive us of souls" (002-004) — an implication she attributes to Rousseau's argument that women exist only for the pleasure of men. This is not merely an intellectual error but, in Wollstonecraft's telling, a blasphemy: "he meant to deprive us of souls, and insinuate that we were beings only designed by the Creator" for male gratification.

The political stakes of the theological argument become clear in her treatment of "congenial souls" (002-044) — the ideal of companionship between the sexes that Wollstonecraft sets against the marriage of convenience. A marriage founded on "congenial souls" meeting as rational equals is the model of what relations between the sexes could be; the existing marriage market, in which women's bodies are traded while their souls are neglected, is the model of what they are.

Wollstonecraft's own faith is invoked not as a dogmatic system but as a source of "strength of mind" (002-081): "Thanks to that Being who impressed them on my soul, and gave me sufficient strength of mind to dare to exert my own reason." The soul is, in this personal testament, the ground of intellectual independence — the capacity to think for oneself that is the precondition of all genuine virtue.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft's treatment of the soul places her in an ambiguous relation to the Christian theological tradition she inherits. She accepts the doctrine of the immortal soul but draws from it conclusions — women's intellectual equality, the necessity of identical education — that the tradition had largely resisted.

Rousseau's position is Wollstonecraft's primary target. In *Émile*, Rousseau had argued that Sophie must be educated differently from Émile because her soul, though immortal, is housed in a female body whose nature determines its proper virtues. Wollstonecraft's reply is devastating: "But what a weak barrier is truth when it stands in the way of an hypothesis!" (003-017). Rousseau "argued so warmly, and so well, for the immortality of the soul" yet "gave a sex to mind" — a contradiction that Wollstonecraft exposes as the foundational incoherence of his educational theory.

Against the materialist philosophers of the French Enlightenment — who questioned the immortality of the soul altogether — Wollstonecraft takes a different tack. She acknowledges the hypothetical possibility that "the soul is not immortal" (002-060) but insists that even on this assumption, virtue in this life is best served by the cultivation of reason, and women should not be excluded from it.

## Key Passages

> To account for, and excuse the tyranny of man, many ingenious arguments have been brought forward to prove, that the two sexes, in the acquirement of virtue, ought to aim at attaining a very different character: or, to speak explicitly, allowing them to have souls, that there is but one way appointed by Providence to lead mankind to either virtue or happiness. (002-001)

> Surely she has not an immortal soul who can loiter life away, merely employed to adorn her person, that she may amuse the languid hours, and soften the cares of a fellow-creature who is willing to be enlivened. (002-051)

> Supposing for a moment, that the soul is not immortal, and that man was only created for this life — still, virtue is the best support of human life. (002-060)

> Thanks to that Being who impressed them on my soul, and gave me sufficient strength of mind to dare to exert my own reason, till becoming dependent only on him for the approbation of my conduct. (002-081)
