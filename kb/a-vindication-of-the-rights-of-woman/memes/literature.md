# Literature

The body of written works, including essays, history, philosophy, and polite letters, which Wollstonecraft champions as essential to women's intellectual development while distinguishing it from the trivial reading she critiques.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft uses "literature" in the broad eighteenth-century sense, encompassing not only imaginative writing but also philosophy, history, moral essays, and works of natural philosophy. She treats literature as a vehicle for the cultivation of reason and virtue, arguing that "a taste for literature" ([009-008]) is one of the few resources available to women seeking to escape the narrowness of their prescribed sphere. She carefully distinguishes the serious literature she recommends from the "novels" and "familiar letters" she criticises as degrading to the female mind.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Literature occupies a central place in Wollstonecraft's educational vision. She presents her own turn to authorship in the biographical sketch as a considered choice: having "decided to apply herself to literature, she now resolved to commence an author" ([P01-009]). Literature is thus framed not as a pastime but as a vocation demanding serious commitment.

In her educational program, Wollstonecraft insists that girls should study "polite literature" ([012-052]) alongside boys, arguing that literary study expands the mind and furnishes it with material for reflection. She observes that many middle-class women, "finding no employment," discover that "literature affords a fund of amusement" ([004-058]), but she wants literature to be more than amusement—it should be a means of intellectual and moral development. She proposes that women should acquire "at least a smattering of literature" ([004-100]) and should engage in conversation with men on literary and intellectual subjects rather than being confined to domestic trivialities.

Wollstonecraft confronts the objection that literary pursuits might distract women from their domestic duties. She argues that women have not been "led from female duties by literature, nor her innocence debauched by books" ([012-073]). On the contrary, literary study strengthens the mind and fits women for the rational performance of their duties. She acknowledges that some women "have not a taste for literature" ([013-047]), but attributes this to their defective education: "women need not shut them out from literature, nor prevent their attaching themselves to it with ardour" ([013-068]).

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft's position contrasts with both the conservative view that women should be protected from intellectual pursuits and the sentimental tradition that valued literature primarily for its capacity to evoke feeling. Against the former, she insists that women are capable of serious literary engagement; against the latter, she argues that literature should cultivate reason, not merely sensibility. Her distinction between "polite literature" and the "novels" she critiques mirrors a broader eighteenth-century anxiety about the effects of reading on female character.
