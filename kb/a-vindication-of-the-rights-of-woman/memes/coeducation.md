# Coeducation

## Definition

**Coeducation**, the education of boys and girls together in the same schools, is one of Wollstonecraft's most forward-looking proposals. While the term itself does not appear in the text -- she writes of educating the sexes "together" or "after the same model" -- the concept is the logical culmination of her argument that the moral improvement of society requires the equal development of both sexes' rational faculties. She asserts this principle explicitly: "to improve both sexes they ought, not only in private families, but in public schools, to be educated together" [012-033].

Wollstonecraft's case for coeducation rests on several interconnected arguments. First, she contends that marriage is "the cement of society" [012-033], and that husbands and wives cannot be true companions if they have been formed by entirely different educational regimes. Second, she argues that the separation of the sexes in education fosters the very vices it claims to prevent -- girls shut up together acquire "bad habits" [012-033], and boys in all-male schools lose the "decent bashfulness" that might ripen into genuine modesty [012-032]. Third, she insists that the masculine and feminine virtues are not fundamentally different but are both founded on reason, and therefore the education that cultivates them should be the same.

## In Context

The coeducation argument unfolds primarily in Chapter XII, where it is embedded within the broader proposal for national education. Wollstonecraft first raises the idea when reflecting on the corrupting effects of single-sex schooling: "I have already animadverted on the bad habits which females acquire when they are shut up together; and I think that the observation may fairly be extended to the other sex, till the natural inference is drawn which I have had in view throughout -- that to improve both sexes they ought...to be educated together" [012-033].

She then translates this principle into concrete institutional design: "day schools for particular ages should be established by government, in which boys and girls might be educated together" [012-044]. The plan is remarkably specific: an elementary day school for children aged five to nine where "boys and girls, the rich and poor, should meet together" [012-046], dressed alike and subject to the same discipline. After age nine, the sexes would continue to study together in the mornings, with afternoons devoted to sex-differentiated practical instruction [012-047].

Wollstonecraft anticipates and addresses objections. To the concern about early romantic attachments between boys and girls educated together, she responds that such attachments would have "the best effect on the moral character of the young people" [012-048] and would promote early marriages, from which "the most salutary physical and moral effects naturally flow" [012-049]. She argues that under her proposed system, "the constitution of boys would not be ruined by the early debaucheries, which now make men so selfish, nor girls rendered weak and vain, by indolence and frivolous pursuits" [012-050].

## Key Passages

> "To improve both sexes they ought, not only in private families, but in public schools, to be educated together." [012-033]

> "If marriage be the cement of society, mankind should all be educated after the same model, or the intercourse of the sexes will never deserve the name of fellowship." [012-033]

> "To render this practicable, day schools for particular ages should be established by government, in which boys and girls might be educated together." [012-044]

> "In this plan of education, the constitution of boys would not be ruined by the early debaucheries, which now make men so selfish, nor girls rendered weak and vain, by indolence and frivolous pursuits." [012-050]
