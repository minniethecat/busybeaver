# Sparta

The ancient Greek city-state that Wollstonecraft cites favorably as an example of a society that recognized the importance of physical education for women.

## Location

Sparta was a dominant military power in ancient Greece, located in the Peloponnese peninsula. Unlike Athens, which confined women to the domestic sphere, Sparta was known in the ancient world for the relative freedom and physical training of its women.

## Relevance to the Text

Wollstonecraft references Sparta as a counter-example to contemporary European practices. Spartan women received physical education and were expected to be strong and healthy — in stark contrast to the "delicacy" and physical weakness cultivated in eighteenth-century European women. By invoking Sparta, Wollstonecraft demonstrates that the confinement of women to domestic passivity is not a universal practice but a historically specific choice.

Sparta serves her argument about the importance of physical education: if women are to be healthy mothers of healthy citizens, they must be physically educated, not encased in corsets and confined to sofas. The Spartan example shows that a society can value women's physical strength without compromising their virtue.

## Historical Context

Classical antiquity provided eighteenth-century writers with a rich store of examples and counter-examples. Sparta, in particular, was celebrated by republicans and radicals — including Rousseau — for its austere virtue and civic spirit. Wollstonecraft's use of Sparta participates in this tradition while redirecting it toward feminist ends.
