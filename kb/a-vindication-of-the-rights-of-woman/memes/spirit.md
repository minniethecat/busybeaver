**Spirit** in Wollstonecraft's *Vindication* operates across several registers: the physiological notion of "animal spirits" inherited from eighteenth-century medical discourse, the moral-spiritual capacity that elevates humanity above mere animal existence, and the civic virtue of "public spirit."

The Dedication opens with an appeal to the highest register: "I plead for my sex, not for myself... dictated by a disinterested spirit" (P02-002). This framing establishes spirit as the capacity for self-transcending moral action — the opposite of the selfishness attributed to women educated only for pleasure.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft inherits from eighteenth-century physiology the concept of "animal spirits" — a fluid or vapour believed to flow through the nerves and mediate between mind and body. She uses the term both literally and critically: "mere animal spirits have no claim to the kindness of our nature" (005-088), denying that physical liveliness constitutes moral worth.

Against this merely physical sense, she posits a moral and theological conception of spirit. Citing Milton, she invokes the idea that humanity's kinship with God is through spirit: "if he be not of kin to God by his spirit, he is a base and ignoble creature" (002-005). Spirit in this register is the seat of reason and virtue.

The civic-political meaning appears throughout the text. Wollstonecraft appeals to a "disinterested spirit" (P02-002) as the motive force behind genuine reform, and she diagnoses the "chivalrous spirit" (005-078) as having "unnerved" women, replacing genuine moral capacity with sentimental deference.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument about spirit is fundamentally about education. She observes that "a girl, whose spirits have not been damped by inactivity" (003-020) exhibits natural liveliness — but this vital energy, if not directed toward reason, dissipates into mere "animal spirits" without moral content.

The critique of contemporary gender norms turns on the distinction between true and false spirit. Wollstonecraft famously remarks that some women of forceful character "were MALE spirits, confined by mistake in a female body" (002-072) — a satirical observation exposing the absurdity of attributing intellectual and moral qualities to bodily sex.

The "depression of spirits" that Wollstonecraft experienced (P01-009) is not merely biographical; it enters the argument as evidence that women's emotional lives have moral and intellectual content that the culture refuses to acknowledge. When she speaks of "the spirits broken by discontent" (002-044), she is diagnosing the psychological damage of exclusion from rational pursuits.

The theological dimension anchors her claim that women possess the same moral capacity as men. If "the Father of spirits" (003-041) created all rational beings with the same spiritual nature, then no human authority can legitimately deny women the education this nature requires.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Against the medical materialism that reduced spirit to a physical fluid, Wollstonecraft insists on the irreducibly moral dimension of spirit. "Flesh and spirit, they make the most perfect" being (004-045) — spirit and body are complementary; but the education of women neglects spirit in favour of the body.

Against the chivalric tradition that celebrated a debased form of "spirit" in women — making them objects of male devotion while denying rational agency — Wollstonecraft demands that women's spirit be cultivated for its own sake, not for the gratification of men. The "loftiness of spirit" (004-082) she admires is the independence of a mind governed by principle.
