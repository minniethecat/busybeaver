1683 – 1765. English poet, best known for _The Complaint, or Night Thoughts on Life, Death, and Immortality_ (1742–45). The Biographical Sketch of Mary Wollstonecraft references Young in connection with the death and burial of Frances Blood, Wollstonecraft's closest friend.

## Life

Edward Young was born in 1683 in Upham, Hampshire. Educated at Winchester College and Oxford, he took holy orders and served as a royal chaplain. He published poetry throughout his life but achieved lasting fame only with _Night Thoughts_, a nine-part blank-verse meditation on mortality, grief, and Christian consolation. The poem was immensely popular across Europe — translated into multiple languages and admired by figures as diverse as Samuel Johnson, Immanuel Kant, and the young Johann Wolfgang von Goethe.

Young's poetry belongs to the "Graveyard School" of eighteenth-century verse, characterized by nocturnal settings, meditations on death, and an aesthetic of sublime melancholy. His influence extended well into the Romantic period.

## Role in the Text

Young appears not in Wollstonecraft's own words but in the Biographical Sketch appended to posthumous editions of _A Vindication_. The Sketch records that during Wollstonecraft's time in Lisbon attending the dying Frances Blood, "She aided, as did Dr. Young, in 'Stealing Narcissa a grave'" (P01-008).

The allusion is to a famous episode in Young's life: after his stepdaughter, Elizabeth Temple (the "Narcissa" of _Night Thoughts_), died in France in 1736, Young was denied permission to bury her in a Protestant cemetery. He allegedly exhumed her body and secretly reburied it in a Protestant graveyard — "stealing Narcissa a grave." Wollstonecraft, similarly, navigated Catholic burial restrictions in Lisbon after Blood's death in 1785.

The parallel drawn by the Biographical Sketch casts Wollstonecraft in a Young-like role: a sensitive soul defying institutional obstacles to honor the dead. It is one of several literary allusions the Sketch uses to frame Wollstonecraft's life within eighteenth-century conventions of sensibility and mourning.

### Works Referenced

_Night Thoughts_ (1742–45) is the source of the "Narcissa" episode. Young's other works include _The Universal Passion_ (1725–28) and _Conjectures on Original Composition_ (1759).

## Key Ideas

Young's presence in the Biographical Sketch is emblematic rather than argumentative. He represents the culture of sensibility — the elevation of deep feeling, particularly grief — that Wollstonecraft both inhabits and critiques. While Wollstonecraft's own text subjects sensibility to rigorous rational scrutiny, the Biographical Sketch presents her life in the idiom of Young-like sentimental mourning, illustrating the tension between Wollstonecraft's rationalist project and her reception by contemporaries.
