# Louis XIV

## Overview

Louis XIV (1638–1715), known as the Sun King, reigned over France for seventy-two years and epitomized the system of absolute monarchy that Wollstonecraft and her radical contemporaries opposed. In *A Vindication of the Rights of Woman*, Louis XIV appears as the paradigmatic despot whose reign demonstrates how the concentration of power and the cultivation of artificial manners corrupt society as a whole — and women in particular. He is invoked both directly by Wollstonecraft and indirectly through the extended quotation from Adam Smith that she deploys in ch04.

## In the Text

Wollstonecraft introduces Louis XIV at [004-018] as the agent who "spread factitious manners, and caught in a specious way, the whole nation in his toils." Her language is unmistakably that of political radicalism: Louis established "an artful chain of despotism" that made it in the interest of the people to support his power. His treatment of women — "whom he flattered by a puerile attention to the whole sex" — is presented not as genuine respect but as a mechanism of control. Under Louis, women "obtained in his reign that prince-like distinction so fatal to reason and virtue."

The figure of Louis XIV returns in the extended Smith quotation at [004-026], where Smith (writing well before the French Revolution) describes Louis as having been "regarded, not only in France, but over all Europe, as the most perfect model of a great prince" — yet what were his virtues? "It was by none of these qualities," Smith observes. The king's reputation rested not on justice, knowledge, or valour, but on "the gracefulness of his shape, and the majestic beauty of his features." Wollstonecraft immediately follows this quotation by observing that woman, "also thus 'in herself complete,' by possessing all these frivolous accomplishments," occupies the same structural position as the despotic king.

This double invocation of Louis XIV — as a historical actor who corrupted French society and as Smith's example of a ruler whose power rested on appearance rather than substance — allows Wollstonecraft to build a devastating analogy: the system that places women on pedestals is structurally identical to the system that places kings on thrones. Both are built on illusion, and both are fatal to reason and virtue.

## Key Quotations

> "Louis XIV, in particular, spread factitious manners, and caught in a specious way, the whole nation in his toils; for establishing an artful chain of despotism, he made it the interest of the people at large, individually to respect his station, and support his power. And women, whom he flattered by a puerile attention to the whole sex, obtained in his reign that prince-like distinction so fatal to reason and virtue." [004-018]

> "Louis XIV, during the greater part of his reign, was regarded, not only in France, but over all Europe, as the most perfect model of a great prince. But what were the talents and virtues, by which he acquired this great reputation? ... It was by none of these qualities." [004-026]

> "Woman, also, thus 'in herself complete,' by possessing all these frivolous accomplishments, so changes the nature of things..." [004-027]

## Biographical Summary

Louis XIV became king of France at the age of four and ruled from 1643 until his death in 1715, the longest recorded reign of any European monarch. He centralized power in the monarchy, built the Palace of Versailles as the seat of his court, and pursued an aggressive foreign policy that made France the dominant European power. His court culture set the standard for aristocratic behaviour across Europe, emphasizing elaborate etiquette, gallantry toward women, and the performance of refinement. By 1792, when Wollstonecraft wrote, the French Revolution had dramatically overturned the Bourbon monarchy, and Louis XIV's great-great-great-grandson Louis XVI was a constitutional monarch soon to be deposed.
