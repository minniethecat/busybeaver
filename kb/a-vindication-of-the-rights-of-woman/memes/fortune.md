In Wollstonecraft's moral vocabulary, **fortune** denotes both wealth and the capricious distribution of material advantage — the "arbitrary distinction of fortune" (004-059) that determines social position without reference to merit.

Fortune is, in her analysis, a corrosive force: it substitutes inherited wealth for earned virtue, enables the corruption of both sexes through idleness, and structures marriage as an economic transaction in which women are the commodity. To pin one's hopes on fortune rather than on the "exertion of reason" is, for Wollstonecraft, to surrender moral agency.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft distinguishes between fortune as mere wealth and fortune as the principle of arbitrary distribution that governs eighteenth-century society. A "man of rank or fortune" (001-027) advances by interest rather than merit, and a "girl of fortune" (005-109) is pursued for her property rather than her character.

Fortune thus names a system in which social standing is disconnected from personal virtue — a system that degrades both those who possess fortune and those who lack it.

The concept's critical force lies in its opposition to merit. Wollstonecraft repeatedly contrasts what is obtained through fortune with what is obtained through exertion, insisting that only the latter produces genuine character.

Those who inherit "fortune and rank" (004-026) are denied the very struggles through which moral personality is formed, while those who lack fortune — particularly women — are forced into humiliating dependence.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's treatment of fortune is woven through her analysis of women's economic vulnerability. She describes with sharp clarity how a woman "who has been taught to look for happiness in a man of fortune" (004-096) is trained from childhood to commodify herself, learning only those "accomplishments" that will attract a purchaser.

The language is deliberately commercial: women "give hostages to fortune" (004-048) in marriage, surrendering their autonomy for material security.

The critique extends beyond gender to the general corruption of character by unearned wealth. Wollstonecraft observes that "those whom fortune has thrown down" (004-014) reveal the fragility of character built on circumstance rather than principle.

She contrasts the "man of superior fortune" (004-096) who can afford to wait for a wife with the woman who cannot afford to wait at all — an asymmetry that structures the entire marriage market.

Her proposed remedy is not the abolition of property but the creation of conditions in which women can earn their own subsistence, freeing them from dependence on fortune, whether inherited or married.

The term "misfortune" recurs with telling frequency. Wollstonecraft uses it not merely to denote bad luck but to describe the structural condition of women: "the great misfortune is this" (002-026), she writes, that both sexes acquire the vices of their station.

Misfortune is the predictable consequence of a system that distributes advantage by accident rather than desert, and women are its principal victims.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft's critique of fortune sets her apart from both the aristocratic celebration of inherited wealth and the emerging capitalist faith in its meritocratic distribution. She does not idealise poverty — she is acutely aware of its degradations — but she insists that fortune must be disentangled from virtue.

This distinguishes her from writers like Edmund Burke, for whom inherited fortune was the foundation of social order, and aligns her with Enlightenment critiques of unearned privilege.

## Key Passages

> A man of rank or fortune, sure of rising by interest, has nothing to do but to pursue the pleasures of life. (001-027)

> Women give hostages to fortune, for they are impediments to great enterprises. (004-048)

> The arbitrary distinction of fortune is sometimes more conspicuous than the distinction of sex. (004-059)

> Till a man with a superior fortune offers — on this subject I mean to dwell. (004-096)
