# Generosity

A moral quality of liberal and magnanimous conduct that Wollstonecraft examines critically: she challenges both the self-serving "chivalrous generosity" of men toward women and the claim that women lack the capacity for genuine generosity.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft treats generosity as a virtue of character involving liberality of spirit and disinterested benevolence toward others. However, she is sharply critical of the way generosity is deployed in gender relations — specifically, the "chivalrous generosity" (004-045) with which men claim to protect and provide for women, which she sees as a disguised form of domination rather than genuine moral liberality. True generosity, in her framework, must be grounded in respect for the equality and rational capacity of the recipient.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's treatment of generosity operates on two fronts. First, she critiques male "generosity" toward women as condescending and self-serving. When men claim to act from "a great effort of generosity" (004-054) in attending to women's welfare, they simultaneously assert women's incapacity to manage their own affairs — generosity becomes an instrument of subordination rather than liberation.

Second, she confronts the claim "that women have seldom so much generosity as men" (013-057), which she attributes to narrow social circumstance rather than natural deficiency. Women denied education, property, and independence have few opportunities to exercise the kind of public generosity that earns recognition. Wollstonecraft also notes the "generosity of bravery" (007-015) as a quality admired in men but denied to women by the same logic that denies them courage — a circular argument in which women's lack of opportunity to display virtue is taken as proof of incapacity.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The eighteenth-century discourse of chivalry celebrated male generosity toward women as a mark of civilisation and gentility. Wollstonecraft inverts this valuation, treating chivalrous generosity as a form of insult — it presupposes women's weakness and dependence. Her treatment aligns generosity with justice rather than condescension, anticipating later feminist critiques of benevolent sexism.

## Key Passages

> I have endeavoured to prove, with chivalrous generosity, that the sexes ought not to be compared; man was made to deliberate, woman to feel. (004-045)

> It has been asserted that women have seldom so much generosity as men; and that their narrow souls are incapable of it. (013-057)
