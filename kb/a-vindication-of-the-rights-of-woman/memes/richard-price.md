# Richard Price

[[Richard Price]] (1723–1791) was a Welsh dissenting minister, moral philosopher, and radical political theorist whose influence on [[Mary Wollstonecraft]] was personal as well as intellectual. His celebrated sermon _A Discourse on the Love of Our Country_ (1789) — defending the French Revolution and arguing for political liberty as a divine gift — provoked [[edmund-burke|Edmund Burke]]'s _Reflections on the Revolution in France_, the work to which Wollstonecraft's own _A Vindication of the Rights of Men_ (1790) was the first published reply. Price died in 1791, the year before the _Vindication of the Rights of Woman_ appeared.

## Life

Price was born at Tynton, Glamorganshire in 1723 and educated at dissenting academies in Wales and London. He served as minister to the congregation at Newington Green from 1758, where his preaching attracted a circle of radical intellectuals that would become Wollstonecraft's intellectual family. His philosophical contributions include _A Review of the Principal Questions in Morals_ (1758), which developed a rationalist ethical theory influential among Unitarians and radicals — a theory that grounded moral obligation in the deliverances of reason rather than the promptings of sentiment, providing a philosophical alternative to the sensibility-based ethics of Hume and Smith. He was elected a Fellow of the Royal Society in 1765 for his work on probability and actuarial mathematics — his _Observations on Reversionary Payments_ (1771) became the standard textbook on life insurance and pensions for a century.

It was at Newington Green that Wollstonecraft, then in her late twenties and running a school with her sisters and [[frances-blood|Frances Blood]], came under Price's influence. The Biographical Sketch records simply: "At this place she became acquainted with Dr. Price, to whom she became strongly attached; the regard was mutual" (P01-007). This mutual regard — between the aging dissenter and the young schoolmistress — would shape Wollstonecraft's intellectual development more than any formal education could have done. Price introduced her to a world of radical politics, rational religion, and philosophical debate; his conviction that reason, not rank, was the proper foundation of moral and political judgment became the bedrock of her feminism.

## Role in the Text

Price appears only once in the body of the _Vindication_, but the passage is among the work's most eloquent:

> "These are bitter calumnies, yet they reached one of the best of men, (Dr. Price.) whose ashes still preach peace, and whose memory demands a respectful pause, when subjects are discussed that lay so near his heart." (001-024)

The context is Wollstonecraft's defence of radical political principles against the charge of impiety. She invokes Price — recently dead, "whose ashes still preach peace" — as a martyr to calumny, a man whose character refutes the accusation that those who "dare to attack human authority" are "despisers of God, and enemies of man" (001-024).

That Wollstonecraft pauses her argument to pay this tribute reveals the depth of personal loss. The "respectful pause" she demands is not merely rhetorical; it is the gesture of a disciple honouring a teacher whose memory authorises the radicalism that follows. Price — the man who provoked Burke, who defended revolution, who taught that patriotism meant "the love of our country as a community of rational beings" — stands behind the _Vindication_ as an absent presence, his "ashes" preaching the peace his living voice had proclaimed.

The tribute is also strategically placed. By invoking Price at this early point in the _Vindication_ — Chapter 1, before the sustained argument about women's education has fully unfolded — Wollstonecraft establishes her radical credentials and her personal connection to the dissenting tradition. The reader who knows that Price was Burke's target, and that Wollstonecraft had already defended Price in her _Vindication of the Rights of Men_, understands that the _Vindication of the Rights of Woman_ is a continuation of the same political project: the extension of rationalist, dissenting principles from the rights of men to the rights of woman.

## Key Ideas

Price's moral philosophy — grounding ethical obligation in reason rather than sentiment — supplied Wollstonecraft with an alternative to the [[Sensibility|sensibility]]-based ethics she critiques throughout the _Vindication_. Where Hume and Smith located moral judgment in the operations of sympathy and the "impartial spectator," Price insisted that moral truths were accessible to reason alone, independent of feeling. This rationalist ethics gave Wollstonecraft the philosophical language to argue that women's moral capacities were not dependent on their cultivated "sensibility" — the very quality that conduct-book writers sought to refine — but on their rational nature, which was identical to men's.

His political sermon on the love of country, arguing that patriotism properly understood means "the love of our country as a community of rational beings," prefigures Wollstonecraft's own insistence that rational citizenship, not sentimental loyalty, is the proper bond between the individual and the state. Both Price and Wollstonecraft extended Enlightenment rationalism into radical politics, and both paid the price of calumny for doing so.
