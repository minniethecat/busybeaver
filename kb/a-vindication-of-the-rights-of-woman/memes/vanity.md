# Vanity

In _[[A Vindication of the Rights of Woman]]_, **vanity** is one of the most frequently cited moral failings of women's character — not as an innate female trait but as a predictable consequence of an education that values appearance over virtue. [[Mary Wollstonecraft]] uses the term in 25 passages, arguing that women are systematically trained into vanity by a society that makes their worth depend on male admiration.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft defines vanity as the excessive concern with external admiration, rooted in an education that "terminates in vanity and sensuality" (003-011). It is not a natural female disposition but a learned behavior: women are taught that their value lies in appearance, and vanity is the internalization of that lesson.

> "Those pretty nothings, those less arts, which terminate in vanity and sensuality." (003-011)

Vanity is the opposite of [[Virtue]]. Where virtue seeks moral excellence for its own sake, vanity seeks admiration for its own sake — regardless of merit. This makes vanity both a moral failure and a social product.

Vanity also displaces authentic social feeling: "till vanity takes place of every social affection" (003-061). The vain woman cannot form genuine relationships because she values others only as sources of admiration.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

### Vanity as the Product of Education

Wollstonecraft insists that vanity is not natural to women but manufactured by their upbringing:

> "When her habits are fixed, and vanity has long ruled her chaotic mind." (005-022)

The phrase "chaotic mind" is significant: it is not that women are naturally vain but that their untrained minds — denied the discipline of reason — are easily captured by vanity. Education that teaches girls to value appearance creates the very vanity it then condemns as feminine weakness.

### Vanity and Sensibility

Wollstonecraft distinguishes vanity from genuine [[Sensibility]]. In fashionable society, "vanity is oftener fostered than sensibility" (004-050). Sensibility — authentic emotional responsiveness — is displaced by the shallower appetite for admiration. The woman of sensibility feels for others; the woman of vanity feels only for herself.

This distinction is crucial to Wollstonecraft's argument: she is not condemning emotion but its corruption into self-regard. Vanity is sensibility degraded into self-display.

### Vanity and Marriage

Vanity corrupts marriage by making wives seek admiration rather than build genuine partnership. Wollstonecraft observes that women motivated by "simply vanity" (004-097) approach marriage as a stage for performance rather than a school for virtue:

> "Their motive is simply vanity." (004-097)

The wife who marries to display herself cannot be a companion to her husband. Her affections are too shallow to anchor a rational friendship. Vanity thus undermines the marriage ideal Wollstonecraft advocates — a union of equals bound by mutual respect.

### Vanity and Social Rank

Vanity operates across all social classes but in different forms. Among the wealthy, "vanity and dissipation" (008-009) produce neglect of duty; among those aspiring to status, "the same vanity and extravagance" (012-017) hollow out family life. Vanity is the mechanism by which social hierarchy corrupts character.

### Vanity and Religion

Wollstonecraft uses Ecclesiastes to reinforce her critique: "that is done under the sun is vanity" (005-125). This biblical echo adds gravity to her social analysis — vanity is not merely a fashion but a spiritual condition. The remedy is not suppression but redirection: toward "soberness of mind" (007-036) and the cultivation of reason.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

Vanity is the corruption of [[Sensibility]] into self-regard. It is closely linked to [[Beauty]], which provides vanity its currency, and to [[Coquetry]], which is vanity in action. The concept opposes [[Virtue]] and [[Modesty]] — both require the subordination of self-display to duty.

Vanity is produced by an [[Education]] that values ornament over understanding. It undermines [[Marriage]] by substituting performance for partnership. The concept is connected to [[Rank]] — vanity takes different shapes across social classes but corrupts all.
