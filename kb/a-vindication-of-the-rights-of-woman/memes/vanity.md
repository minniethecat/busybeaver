# Vanity

## Definition

**Vanity** in Wollstonecraft's moral psychology is the inevitable product of an [[education]] that teaches women to value themselves only through the eyes of others. When women are "taught to look to the opinion of the world alone for approbation" [002-041], they develop vanity — an excessive concern with appearance and admiration — rather than [[virtue]], which is grounded in inner moral worth.

Wollstonecraft's analysis of vanity is structural rather than merely moralistic. She does not simply condemn vain women; she traces vanity to its causes in the social system that produces it. Vanity is what happens when human beings, denied the opportunity to develop genuine [[character]], are offered admiration as a substitute for respect.

## In Context

### The Education That Produces Vanity

Wollstonecraft directly links vanity to the deficiencies of women's [[education]]: "the whole tendency of female education is to foster vanity and to repress every noble exertion of the mind" [002-043]. An education that prizes accomplishments over understanding, appearance over substance, and pleasing over reasoning cannot produce anything other than vain women — and to blame women for the vanity that this education produces is to compound the injustice.

The solution is not to lecture women about vanity but to offer them an education that makes vanity unnecessary. A woman who has cultivated her [[reason]] and developed genuine [[virtue]] has no need of the empty admiration that vanity craves — she possesses something real.

### Vanity and Reputation

Vanity is closely tied to [[reputation]] in Wollstonecraft's analysis. The woman who "plumes herself on her unsullied reputation" is driven by vanity — the need for the admiration and approval of others — rather than by genuine moral commitment. The pursuit of reputation is thus a species of vanity, and both are symptoms of a character that has been shaped by external opinion rather than internal principle.

Wollstonecraft contrasts vanity with the "sober contentment" that comes from the exercise of [[reason]] and the performance of genuine [[duty]]. The contrast is between a life oriented toward the gaze of others and a life oriented toward moral truth — and only the latter, she insists, can produce anything worthy of the name virtue.
