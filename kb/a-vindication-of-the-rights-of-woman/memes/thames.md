# River Thames

The River Thames, flowing through London, appears in the biographical sketch as the site of Wollstonecraft's suicide attempt after Gilbert Imlay's rejection — a dramatic episode that reveals the emotional cost behind her feminist philosophy.

## Location

The River Thames is the principal river of southern England, flowing through London to the North Sea. In the late 18th century, it was the commercial artery of the British Empire, lined with docks, wharves, and the teeming life of Georgian London.

## Relevance to the Text

The Thames appears in a single, harrowing passage in the biographical sketch that illuminates the personal suffering behind Wollstonecraft's philosophical commitments.

### In Wollstonecraft's Life

The biographical sketch records that after realizing Imlay's affections had irrevocably cooled, Wollstonecraft "threw herself into the Thames; she remained in the water, until ..." (P01-016). She was rescued, but the episode reveals the depth of her despair. This biographical detail is significant for readers of the _Vindication_ because it demonstrates that Wollstonecraft's critique of women's emotional dependency was grounded in painful personal experience. She knew firsthand the danger of investing one's entire happiness in the affections of another.

The Thames suicide attempt stands as a biographical counterpoint to the philosophical arguments of the _Vindication_: Wollstonecraft advocated for rational independence not from a position of detachment, but from the hard-won knowledge of what emotional dependence can cost.

## Historical Context

The Thames in the 1790s was London's lifeline — a working river crowded with vessels, its bridges (only London Bridge and Westminster Bridge at the time) connecting the growing metropolis. The river was also, tragically, a common site of suicide, particularly for women. Wollstonecraft's near-death in the Thames places her personal story within the broader social reality of Georgian London.
