# Adoration

In _A Vindication_, "adoration" names the gallant but deceptive reverence that men offer women — a reverence that Wollstonecraft diagnoses as the prelude to scorn, not the foundation of respect.

## Definition in Context

For Wollstonecraft, adoration is the language of romantic gallantry: the lover's exaltation of his beloved as a goddess or angel. But this language, she argues, is a form of flattery that serves male interest by keeping women content with ornamental rather than rational status.

## Usage in the Text

In Chapter 4, she delivers one of her most aphoristic judgments: "But the adoration comes first, and the scorn is not anticipated" (004-017). The gallant lover who worships his mistress today will despise her tomorrow, because his adoration was never based on respect for her rational capacities — it was a sensual idealization that no real woman can sustain.

In Chapter 5, she elaborates: "The lover, it is true, has a poetic licence to exalt his mistress; his reason is the bubble of his passion" (005-067). The lover's adoration is explicitly contrasted with reason — it is a product of passion, not judgment, and therefore inherently unstable. When passion fades, so does the adoration, and what remains is often contempt.

## Theoretical Significance

Wollstonecraft's critique of adoration is directed at the entire tradition of courtly and romantic love, which she sees as a mechanism for keeping women satisfied with flattery rather than with rights. The Rousseauian ideal of the beloved woman — worshipped for her sensibility, her delicacy, her weakness — is exposed as a trap: the same qualities that inspire adoration also justify subordination.
