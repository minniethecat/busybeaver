# Resignation

Wollstonecraft treats "resignation" not as a virtuous acceptance of fate but as a symptom of the degradation of women's character — a melancholy passivity that substitutes for the active exercise of reason and virtue.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

The single direct reference appears in the context of a mother's grief: "softened sorrow into melancholy resignation, her heart turns to her child." (003-054) The phrasing is telling — sorrow is "softened" into resignation, suggesting a process of emotional dilution rather than genuine resolution. The mother turns to her child not from active maternal love but from a resignation that has displaced her capacity for other responses.

Wollstonecraft's treatment of resignation is consistent with her broader critique of feminine emotional culture. Where men are permitted anger, ambition, and action, women are trained into "melancholy resignation" — a passive acceptance of circumstances that forecloses the possibility of change. This resignation is not virtue but its counterfeit: it mimics the serenity of genuine fortitude while actually signifying defeat.

### Distinction from Fortitude

The concept gains clarity through contrast: Wollstonecraft's "resolution" (004-066) is active, requiring "a more firm support than emotions," while resignation is passive, a surrender to emotion dressed as acceptance. The distinction maps onto her larger argument that women's virtues must be active and rational, not passive and emotional.
