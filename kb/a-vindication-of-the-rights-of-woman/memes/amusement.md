Amusement, in Wollstonecraft's framework, denotes the superficial entertainments and pastimes that society prescribes for women in lieu of rational pursuits and intellectual cultivation. She treats amusement not as innocent leisure but as a structural mechanism that keeps women intellectually stunted and socially dependent.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft defines amusement primarily through negation — as that which displaces genuine employment and intellectual engagement. In the Introduction, she warns that women who are valued only for their charms "soon only afford barren amusement" (P03-006), framing amusement as a hollow substitute for meaningful companionship. The term is repeatedly contrasted with rational employment: when "no noble pursuit sets the mind in motion," women turn to their senses "for employment and amusement" (002-048). Amusement thus operates as the default state of the uneducated mind — a filler for the void left by the absence of serious intellectual cultivation. The gendered dimension is explicit: women are systematically directed toward amusement rather than toward the development of reason and virtue.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's critique of amusement runs through her broader argument about women's education and social role. She observes that the very structure of women's upbringing channels them toward trivial pastimes: "the doll is the peculiar amusement of the females; from whence we may trace the love of dress and of personal beauty" (005-008). This early conditioning establishes a pattern in which women's identity is tied to ornamental self-presentation rather than intellectual achievement.

The gendering of amusement extends to adult social life. Wollstonecraft acknowledges that "both sexes have a great many amusements in common; and so they ought to have" (005-007), suggesting that shared rational recreations are not inherently problematic. The harm arises when amusement becomes women's primary occupation rather than an occasional respite. She notes that "when they are let to pursue their amusements at pleasure, they care very little about the means" (005-005), indicating that the pursuit of pleasure, unchecked by higher aims, corrupts character.

The educational dimension is central. Wollstonecraft argues that "literature affords a fund of amusement, which they have never sought to perpetuate" (004-058), observing that even when women have access to intellectually enriching material, their education has not equipped them to engage with it substantively. The consequence is a life structured around frivolity: women become "dependent on the novelist for amusement" (013-030), consuming shallow entertainment rather than cultivating their rational faculties. In the most damning formulation, she warns against "flying for amusement to the wanton, from the unsophisticated charms of virtue" (013-040), drawing a direct line from the pursuit of amusement to moral degradation.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Where Rousseau and other Enlightenment thinkers valorised women's cultivation of pleasing arts as natural to their sex, Wollstonecraft rejected the premise entirely. Rousseau's Sophie is educated to amuse and please; Wollstonecraft's ideal woman is educated to think and to act as a rational moral agent. The "pleasing amusement of making O's" that Wollstonecraft dismisses (003-018) — a reference to the ornamental accomplishments expected of women — represents precisely the kind of education she sought to replace with rigorous intellectual training. Gregory's advice that women cultivate "a certain degree of delicacy and reserve" to hold male attention is, in Wollstonecraft's framework, merely another form of reducing women to a source of amusement for men.
