# Divine

Wollstonecraft's theological reference point: she invokes divine perfection, divine law, and divine attributes to ground human dignity in a transcendent moral order, while simultaneously attacking the appropriation of divine authority to justify patriarchal and political tyranny.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's concept of the divine serves two distinct argumentative functions. Positively, she appeals to divine perfection as the model toward which rational beings should strive: the cultivation of reason and virtue is a participation in the "divine perfection" that characterizes the creator (001-014). The "divine, indefeasible, earthly sovereignty" of reason within each individual grounds human dignity and moral responsibility (013-079). Negatively, she exposes the abuse of divine language to sanctify unjust human authority: "the right of husbands, like the divine right of kings, may, it is to be feared, be in danger of being equally exploded" (003-012). The parallel between patriarchal and monarchical claims to divine sanction is central to her political critique.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's theological framework is rationalist and Enlightenment-inflected: she treats divine perfection as accessible to reason rather than requiring revelation alone. The capacity for reason is itself a divine gift, and developing it is a religious duty. This allows her to argue that denying women education is not merely politically unjust but theologically indefensible — it prevents them from fulfilling their divinely-ordained nature as rational and moral beings. Her critique of the "divine right of kings" extends seamlessly to the "divine right of husbands": both are human inventions that appropriate sacred language to sanctify mere power (003-012). True respect for the divine requires challenging, not obeying, unjust human authority (008-004).

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft writes within a Christian framework but departs from the conservative Anglican establishment that treated existing social hierarchy as divinely ordained. Where Burke and others saw the existing order as a reflection of divine will, Wollstonecraft distinguishes sharply between divine perfection — the moral standard — and the corrupt human institutions that claim divine warrant. Her theological argument anticipates the rational Dissenting tradition: God is a God of reason, and irrational social arrangements cannot claim divine sanction.
