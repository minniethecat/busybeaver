# Friendship

In _[[A Vindication of the Rights of Woman]]_, **friendship** serves as [[Mary Wollstonecraft]]'s normative ideal for human relationships — the standard against which [[Marriage]], parenting, and social bonds should be measured. She argues that friendship, grounded in reason and mutual respect, is morally superior to the transient fever of romantic passion.

Friendship appears in over twenty-five passages. Wollstonecraft's treatment of the concept challenges the eighteenth-century assumption that women are capable only of sexual love, not the steady affection of rational friendship.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft defines friendship as a bond of reason, not passion. She insists that genuine friendship requires equality: one cannot be friends with a being one dominates or a being one must please for survival.

> "Friendship is a serious affection; the most sublime of all affections, because it is founded on principle, and cemented by time" (002-057)

This definition contrasts friendship with the "fever of love" that Wollstonecraft critiques in her analysis of [[Sensibility]]. Love is transient and emotionally volatile; friendship is durable and rationally grounded. The former characterizes degraded marriage; the latter should characterize reformed marriage.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

### Friendship as the Model for Marriage

Wollstonecraft's most radical claim about friendship is that it — not romantic love — should be the foundation of marriage:

> "Friendship or indifference inevitably succeeds love" (002-057)

Given that passionate love must fade, the only question is what replaces it. Friendship offers the answer: a bond grounded in mutual respect, shared interests, and rational companionship. But friendship of this kind is impossible between unequals — which is why women must be educated before they can be wives.

### Friendship Between the Sexes

Wollstonecraft argues that men and women should be capable of friendship outside marriage — a claim that challenged contemporary assumptions about cross-sex relations being inevitably sexual:

> "The man who can be contented to live with a pretty, useful companion, without a mind, has lost in voluptuous gratifications a taste for more refined enjoyments" (005-092)

She contends that men who see women only as sexual objects have rendered themselves incapable of friendship — and have impoverished their own moral lives in the process.

### Parental Friendship

Wollstonecraft extends the ideal of friendship to the parent-child relationship. She argues that parents who govern by arbitrary power forfeit the friendship of their children:

> "A parent acquires all the rights of the most sacred friendship" (011-005)

This is the culmination of her argument about [[Duty]]: when parental authority is grounded in reason rather than mere position, it earns the friendship — not just the obedience — of children.

### Friendship vs. Love in the Vindication

| Dimension | Romantic Love | Friendship |
|-----------|--------------|------------|
| Foundation | Passion, sensation | Reason, principle |
| Duration | Transient | Durable, cemented by time |
| Requires equality | No (flourishes in domination) | Yes (requires mutual respect) |
| Moral value | Morally neutral or dangerous | "The most sublime of all affections" |
| Role in marriage | Fades inevitably | Should succeed and replace love |

## Relationship to Other Concepts

Friendship is the relationship form that [[Reason]] makes possible and [[Equality]] requires. It stands in opposition to the power dynamics Wollstonecraft critiques in [[Marriage]] and to the emotional volatility she diagnoses in [[Sensibility]].

The concept also connects to [[Duty]]: when parents govern by reason, they earn the friendship of their children — duty fulfilled becomes friendship. And to [[Virtue]]: friendship is the social expression of virtue, the bond that forms between beings who respect each other's rational nature.
