# Friendship

## Definition

In Wollstonecraft's moral vision, **friendship** represents the highest form of human relationship — superior to [[love|romantic love]], to the parent-child bond in its immature forms, and certainly to the relations of dependency and patronage that [[character]]ised most women's social connections in her era. Friendship, as she conceives it, is the steady [[love|affection]] that arises between rational beings who know and respect each other's [[character]].

> "Friendship is a serious affection; the most sublime of all affections, because it is founded on principle, and cemented by time." (004-087)

This is not a pious commonplace but a radical philosophical claim. To call friendship "the most sublime of all affections" is to rank it above romantic love, above filial piety, above the passions that eighteenth-century culture celebrated as the summit of human experience. Wollstonecraft elevates friendship precisely because it is _not_ a passion — because it is the one affection that is "founded on principle" rather than on impulse, beauty, or proximity.

> "Friendship or indifference inevitably succeeds love." (002-065)

This is not a pessimistic claim but a developmental one: the "fever of [[love]]" must give way to something more durable, and that something is friendship — provided both parties have cultivated the qualities ([[reason]], [[virtue]], [[independence|independence of mind]]) that make friendship possible. A woman trained only to please and to be [[love]]d will find herself without resources when the transition comes.

### Friendship and the Rehabilitation of Women

The concept of friendship does enormous argumentative work in Wollstonecraft's text. Because she defines friendship as a relation between equals — between beings who respect each other's rational [[character]] — the demand that women become capable of friendship is also, implicitly, the demand that women become capable of [[equality]]. You cannot be friends with a dependent, she implies, any more than you can be friends with a pet. This is why the argument about friendship runs directly into the argument about [[education]]: if women are to be friends to their husbands rather than "humble dependents," they must be educated to think, to judge, and to sustain an independent moral life.

> "Besides, the woman who strengthens her body and exercises her mind will, by managing her family and practising various virtues, become the friend, and not the humble dependent of her husband." (002-061)

The conditionals here are precise: strengthening the body, exercising the mind, practising virtues — these are the prerequisites of friendship. Wollstonecraft is not saying that husbands and wives happen to be friends; she is saying that friendship is what marriage becomes only when both parties are intellectually and morally developed. Without that development, marriage reduces to a relation of dependence, and dependence is the opposite of friendship.

### Esteem as the Foundation

Wollstonecraft distinguishes friendship from love by identifying their different foundations. Love, she argues, "is often excited by evanescent beauties and graces" — it is drawn to surfaces — whereas friendship rests on **esteem**, which is a judgement of [[character]] arrived at through [[reason]]:

> "Love is, in a great degree, an arbitrary passion, and will reign like some other stalking mischiefs, by its own authority, without deigning to reason; and it may also be easily distinguished from esteem, the foundation of friendship, because it is often excited by evanescent beauties and graces." (006-011)

This distinction between love and esteem is central to her moral psychology. Esteem cannot be commanded or seduced; it must be earned by demonstrating qualities that [[reason]] can recognise and approve. That is why friendship is "cemented by time" — it grows as the parties come to know each other's [[character]] through sustained observation. The implication for women is stark: if you have been trained only to attract love, you have not been trained to earn esteem, and when the "evanescent beauties" fade, you will be left with neither.

## In Context

Chapter II establishes the [[love]]-friendship developmental arc as the [[nature|natural]] course of human relationships. Wollstonecraft's argument proceeds by analogy: just as the virtuous man who struggled for a crown often becomes a "voluptuous tyrant" once it is won, so the lover who has secured his object finds his passion subsiding into indifference — or, if the conditions are right, into friendship. The moral world, she insists, operates by the same logic as the political one.

Chapter IV elaborates the most consequential claim: "in a great degree, [[love]] and friendship cannot subsist in the same bosom" (004-087) — they are not complementary but sequential, and the social order that tries to make them simultaneous corrupts both. When a wife is expected to be simultaneously a lover and a friend, she is set an impossible task, because the qualities that sustain love (mystery, novelty, distance) are incompatible with the qualities that sustain friendship (familiarity, transparency, proximity).

> "Personal attachment is a very happy foundation for friendship; yet, when even two virtuous young people marry, it would, perhaps, be happy if some circumstance checked their passion." (004-086)

The passage reveals Wollstonecraft's subtlety. She does not deny that personal attachment — what we might call chemistry or affection — is a "very happy foundation" for friendship. But she is wary of unchecked passion, which she sees as potentially consuming the very qualities on which durable friendship depends. Even for "two virtuous young people," she suggests, it might be better if passion were restrained, so that esteem could provide the "plan to regulate a friendship which only death ought to dissolve."

Chapter V returns to the theme in the context of marriage, arguing that marriages based on mutual respect and friendship are more durable and more genuinely satisfying than those built on romantic [[love|passion]]. The implication is radical: if friendship is the goal, then women must be educated to be friends — which means they must be educated to be the intellectual [[equality|equals]] of their husbands.

> "When I treat of friendship, love, and marriage, it will be found that the two latter have been most insidiously employed to sacrifice the former." (005-108)

### The Politics of Friendship

Wollstonecraft's argument about friendship extends beyond the domestic sphere. Throughout the _Vindication_, she uses friendship as a model for ethical relations more broadly. When she imagines the properly educated woman of the future, she imagines her as a friend — to her husband, to her children, to her fellow women. This is a political vision as much as a domestic one, because it presupposes a reorganisation of social relations away from hierarchy and toward mutuality:

> "In this plan of education, the constitution of boys would not be ruined by the early debaucheries, which now make men so selfish, nor girls rendered weak and vain, by indolence and frivolous pursuits. But, I presuppose, that such a degree of equality should be established between the sexes as would shut out gallantry and coquetry, yet allow friendship and love to temper the heart for the discharge of higher duties." (012-050)

Friendship and love, in this vision, are not enemies but allies — so long as equality is their precondition. Without equality, love becomes gallantry (a performance of devotion that masks condescension) and friendship becomes impossible. The picture Wollstonecraft draws is of a world in which men "will wish to converse at their fireside with a friend, after they cease to play with a mistress" (012-072).

Wollstonecraft even extends the model of friendship to the parent-child relationship, arguing that the highest form of parental affection "acquires all the rights of the most sacred friendship" (011-007) when it is grounded in the cultivation of the child's understanding rather than mere instinct. Friendship, in this sense, is not one relationship among others but the regulative ideal toward which all human relationships should tend.
