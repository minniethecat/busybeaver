For Wollstonecraft, **fashion** is far more than a matter of dress — it is a symbol and instrument of women's subjugation. She describes "an air of fashion" as "but a badge of slavery" (001-027), identifying the pursuit of fashion as both symptom and cause of women's degraded condition. Throughout the *Vindication*, fashion functions as a metonym for the entire system of superficial values that Wollstonecraft seeks to overturn.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft defines fashion critically as a system of external standards that substitutes for genuine virtue. She speaks of "a kind of morality founded on the fashion of the age" (001-026), emphasising that fashion is not merely aesthetic but normative — it establishes what conduct, appearance, and character are valued, and by doing so displaces the moral standards grounded in reason.

The "air of fashion" (005-085) that many young people are taught to cultivate is, for Wollstonecraft, precisely the wrong object of education. Where young women should be developing their understanding and judgment, they are instead trained to perform fashionable graces that are "but a badge of slavery" (001-027) — the external sign of their internal subordination to arbitrary social opinion.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument about fashion operates on two fronts. First, she critiques fashion as a diversion of female energy: instead of cultivating their minds, women are taught to devote their attention to "frivolous fashion" (009-023), spending their lives in pursuit of the ephemeral rather than the enduring. The woman of fashion, in Wollstonecraft's portrayal, is a tragic figure — her days consumed by "following the fashionable vagaries of dress" (013-068) while her reason lies fallow.

Second, and more profoundly, Wollstonecraft exposes fashion as a mechanism of social control. The "heartless intercourse of fashionable life" (012-036) is not merely empty but actively corrupting, training women to value appearance over substance and to judge themselves and others by external standards. She extends this critique to the aristocracy, observing that middle-class women "ape the fashions of the nobility" (004-096) without sharing their advantages, implicating fashion in the reproduction of class hierarchy as well as gender subordination.

The transition from girlhood to fashionable life is particularly damning in Wollstonecraft's analysis. She asks: "what can be more than a girl's coming out in the fashionable world?" (012-060) — a question whose answer is that such an entry into fashionable society marks the end of any possibility of genuine intellectual development, substituting the pursuit of admiration for the cultivation of virtue.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Where eighteenth-century conduct literature treated fashion as a matter of appropriate feminine taste — advising women on how to dress and present themselves without impropriety — Wollstonecraft refuses to engage fashion on its own terms. She does not offer advice on modest dress or appropriate adornment; instead, she treats the entire preoccupation with fashion as a symptom of women's degraded condition. Her radical alternative is not reformed fashion but the cultivation of reason, which she believes will make women indifferent to the "ornamental drapery" (001-027) that currently defines their social value.

## Key Passages

> An air of fashion, which is but a badge of slavery, and the affectation of the manners of the great, have taken the place of the useful qualities of the heart. (001-027)

> The air of fashion, which many young people are so anxious to acquire, is nothing more than the art of pleasing, and the desire of pleasing is only another name for the desire of being loved. (005-085)

> What can be a more melancholy sight, than a woman, whose mind has never been stored with useful knowledge, left to the dissipation of the fashionable world? (009-023)
