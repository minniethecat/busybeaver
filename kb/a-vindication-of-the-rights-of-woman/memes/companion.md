# Companion

The figure of the "companion" is Wollstonecraft's positive ideal for relations between the sexes. Against the prevailing models of marriage — the wife as dependant, the wife as ornament, the wife as household manager — she proposes a model in which husband and wife are rational companions, united by shared understanding rather than by economic necessity or sexual attraction alone. The companionate ideal is not merely domestic; it is political, for "if she be not prepared by education to become the companion of man, she will stop the progress of knowledge and virtue" (P02-007).

## Definition

Wollstonecraft defines companionship by contrast with its counterfeits. The woman who has been educated only to please is not a companion but a dependant — "a sweeter companion to man, whenever he chooses to relax himself," in Rousseau's revealing phrase, but incapable of the rational exchange that genuine companionship requires. True companionship requires equality of understanding: "the woman who strengthens her body and exercises her mind will, by managing her family and practising various virtues, become the friend, and not the humble dependent of her husband" (002-059).

The definition is demanding. A companion must possess independent judgment, for one cannot be the companion of another if one's opinions are merely the echo of his. She must possess sufficient knowledge to engage in "subjects that exercise the understanding" rather than merely "the tittle-tattle of the day." And she must possess the moral independence — what Wollstonecraft calls "a rational affection" — that allows her to be a genuine friend rather than a flattering mirror. The companion, in short, is a rational being in her own right who chooses to share her life with another rational being.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument for companionate marriage is both prudential and principled. The prudential argument observes that marriages founded on sexual attraction alone inevitably decay as beauty fades and novelty wears off. "When the lover is become the husband," she writes, "he has no longer any motive to disguise his real character; and the woman, who rested all her consequence on her personal charms, finds herself reduced to a mere household drudge" — or worse, to the humiliating position of a neglected wife competing with mistresses for her husband's waning attention. The companionate marriage, by contrast, is "founded on friendship" and therefore "the tie will not grow weaker with years."

The principled argument is more radical. Companionship between the sexes is not merely a private good but a condition of social progress. Wollstonecraft opens the Dedication with the claim that "if she be not prepared by education to become the companion of man, she will stop the progress of knowledge and virtue" (P02-007). The claim is striking: women's education is not a favour to be granted to women but a necessity for the advancement of the species. "Truth must be common to all, or it will be inefficacious with respect to its influence on general practice." A society in which half the population cannot reason cannot be a society governed by reason.

The connection between companionship and citizenship is explicit. "If children are to be educated to understand the true principle of patriotism, their mother must be a patriot" (P02-007). The mother who cannot think cannot raise citizens; the wife who cannot reason cannot be the companion of a husband who would himself be a citizen. The companionate marriage is thus the domestic correlate of republican citizenship — the institution through which the virtues necessary for self-government are cultivated and transmitted.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Rousseau's Sophie was designed to be a "companion" to Émile, but companionship in Rousseau's sense meant pleasing, soothing, and entertaining — not engaging as an intellectual equal. Wollstonecraft seizes on this contradiction: Rousseau wants women to be companions but denies them the education that would make companionship possible. A woman who has been taught only to please cannot be a companion; she can only be a pleasing object.

John Milton's Eve, whom Wollstonecraft discusses at length, represents another failed model of companionship. Milton describes Eve as Adam's "fair large front and eye sublime declared absolute rule," yet Eve is also "she who was formed for softness and sweet attractive grace" — a figure who adores rather than reasons. Wollstonecraft's critique is pointed: "How could a rational being, created in the image of God, be formed merely to obey?" A companion who merely obeys is not a companion at all.

> Contending for the rights of woman, my main argument is built on this simple principle, that if she be not prepared by education to become the companion of man, she will stop the progress of knowledge and virtue; for truth must be common to all, or it will be inefficacious with respect to its influence on general practice. (P02-007)

> The woman who strengthens her body and exercises her mind will, by managing her family and practising various virtues, become the friend, and not the humble dependent of her husband. (002-059)

> How could a rational being, created in the image of God, be formed merely to obey? (002-004)

> In fact, it is a farce to call any being virtuous whose virtues do not result from the exercise of its own reason. This was Rousseau's opinion respecting men: I extend it to women. (002-032)
