**Refinement** is for Wollstonecraft almost always a term of critique. When she writes of "false refinement" (P03-002, 002-015), "refinement of taste" (P03-008), or "refinements of luxury" (009-027), she diagnoses a civilisational disease: the point at which polish becomes poison, and the cultivation of surface eclipses the cultivation of mind. Refinement, in her usage, is the mechanism by which a society mistakes elegance for virtue and complexity for wisdom.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's "refinement" is distinguished from genuine cultivation by its object and effect. True cultivation strengthens the understanding; refinement merely polishes the surface. "Women are enfeebled by false refinement" (P03-002) — the adjective "false" is not incidental but definitional: refinement that does not serve the development of reason is not refinement at all, but a form of weakening. She groups "delicacy of sentiment, and refinement of taste" as "almost synonymous with epithets of weakness" (P03-008), explicitly linking the aesthetic category to moral enfeeblement.

The term carries a spatial metaphor: refinement operates on the surface, at the point of social contact — manners, taste, conversation — while leaving the depths untouched. Women "who are only depraved by the refinements which prevail in the society they live in" (012-069) have been corrupted not by gross vice but by an excess of polish that has no rational substance beneath it.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument about refinement follows her characteristic pattern of denaturalisation. What society praises as refinement — elegant manners, delicate taste, polished conversation — is in fact a system of constraints that keeps women intellectually stunted. "The books of instruction written by men of genius have probably had the same effect as more frivolous productions; they teach women to value refinement above strength" (P03-002).

The danger of refinement is that it is seductive. Unlike open tyranny, which provokes resistance, refinement presents itself as an accomplishment and an elevation. But its elevation is illusory: "the seeds of false refinement, immorality, and vanity have been fostered by external circumstances" (P03-006). The triplet is instructive — refinement, immorality, vanity — placing refinement not in opposition to vice but as its precursor and partner.

Wollstonecraft extends the critique beyond women to civilisation itself. "Luxury has introduced a refinement in eating that destroys the constitution" (008-024). Here refinement is not a gendered failing but a civilisational pathology: the over-development of appetite that paradoxically weakens what it elaborates. When she describes "the refinements of luxury, or the vicious repinings of enervated sensibility" (009-027), she maps refinement onto the same moral territory as the artificial feelings she condemns.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The eighteenth-century discourse of politeness treated refinement as an unambiguous good — the mark of civilised society, the triumph of manners over brutality. Wollstonecraft challenges this consensus at its root. Where Addison and Steele's *Spectator* celebrated the refining influence of women on male manners, Wollstonecraft sees only a mutual corruption: men become "voluptuaries" and women become ornaments. She "dreads a refinement of wisdom as a deviation into folly" (005-100) — the fear that even wisdom, when refined past its substance, becomes indistinguishable from its opposite.
