Wollstonecraft's treatment of **ambition** is distinctive for its careful differentiation between a "laudable ambition" (P03-009) directed toward moral and intellectual excellence, and a "false ambition" (002-046) that serves only vanity and social display. She argues that women's ambition has been systematically misdirected — channelled into the pursuit of beauty and admiration rather than the cultivation of character — and that redirecting this powerful passion is essential to women's moral regeneration.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft defines ambition as a natural passion that, like all passions, requires the guidance of reason to become a force for good. She speaks of "a nobler ambition" (P03-001) that women "ought to cherish," indicating that ambition itself is not the problem — its object is. When ambition aims at "obtaining a character as a human being" (P03-009), it is laudable; when it aims at being "fair" and "raising emotion" (002-083), it is degrading.

The distinction between true and false ambition parallels Wollstonecraft's broader distinction between reason-governed and passion-governed conduct. "False ambition in men," she writes, arises "from a love of power" (002-046), suggesting that ambition becomes corrupt when its goal is domination rather than excellence. For women, false ambition takes a different but equally corrupting form — the ambition "to be fair, to raise emotion" (002-083) rather than to be wise and virtuous.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument about ambition functions as both diagnosis and prescription. As diagnosis, she exposes how women's "sole ambition is to be fair" (002-083) — a goal that, when achieved, brings only the transient admiration of others rather than the enduring satisfaction of self-respect. She observes that "pleasure takes place of ambition and those nobler passions that open and enlarge the soul" (P03-013), tracing how the pursuit of immediate gratification supplants the striving for excellence that characterises a fully developed human being.

As prescription, Wollstonecraft insists that women's ambition must be redirected toward intellectual and moral objects. The "first object of laudable ambition," she declares, "is to obtain a character as a human being" (P03-009) — a formulation that places moral self-cultivation above all social rewards. She envisions a future in which women "cherish a nobler ambition" (P03-001) and "by their abilities and virtues exact respect" — earning through merit what they currently seek through charm.

The political dimension of this argument is explicit. Wollstonecraft observes that "when the sole ambition of woman centres in beauty" (013-050), women become complicit in their own subordination, investing their energy in the very qualities that mark them as objects rather than subjects. Redirecting female ambition toward intellectual and moral excellence is, in her framework, not merely a matter of personal improvement but a necessary condition of women's emancipation.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft's treatment of ambition departs sharply from the eighteenth-century consensus that ambition was a masculine passion, unsuitable for women. Where Rousseau and others treated female desires for admiration as natural and appropriate, Wollstonecraft refuses to naturalise the misdirection of female ambition. She does not counsel women to suppress their ambition — she counsels them to elevate its object. This subtle but crucial distinction places her in opposition both to those who would deny women ambition entirely and to those who would channel it into the pursuit of male approval. Her alternative — ambition directed toward intellectual and moral self-cultivation — represents a fundamentally different vision of what women can and should desire.

## Key Passages

> They ought to cherish a nobler ambition, and by their abilities and virtues exact respect. (P03-001)

> The first object of laudable ambition is to obtain a character as a human being, regardless of the distinction of sex. (P03-009)

> Have women so little ambition as to be satisfied with such a condition? (002-050)
