# Delicacy

## Definition

**Delicacy** in Wollstonecraft's critique is the artificial refinement of feeling and manner that eighteenth-century society demanded of women — and that women were taught to cultivate as their highest accomplishment. She distinguishes between genuine sensitivity, which is a natural human capacity, and the exaggerated delicacy that is inculcated by a corrupt [[education]] as a mark of feminine refinement.

The problem with delicacy, as Wollstonecraft analyzes it, is that it is a form of cultivated weakness. Women are taught to be delicate — to shrink from robust experience, to affect a fine sensibility that is offended by reality — as a way of signaling their refinement. But this delicacy renders them unfit for the genuine exercise of [[virtue]], which requires strength, independence, and the capacity to confront difficult truths.

## In Context

### Delicacy and False Refinement

Wollstonecraft connects delicacy to the broader culture of "false refinement" that she sees as corrupting both sexes. Delicacy is the performance of a sensitivity one does not genuinely possess — a social signal rather than a moral quality. The woman who affects delicacy is like the aristocrat who affects refinement: both are substituting the appearance of superiority for its substance.

### The Anecdote of the Woman of Fashion

Wollstonecraft illustrates her argument with a devastating portrait of a woman who embodies everything wrong with the cult of delicacy:

> I once knew a weak woman of fashion, who was more than commonly proud of her delicacy and sensibility. She thought a distinguishing taste and puny appetite the height of all human perfection, and acted accordingly. I have seen this weak sophisticated being neglect all the duties of life, yet recline with self-complacency on a sofa, and boast of her want of appetite as a proof of delicacy that extended to, or, perhaps, arose from, her exquisite sensibility: for it is difficult to render intelligible such ridiculous jargon.
> (003-019)

The anecdote is carefully crafted: the woman's "delicacy" is not a moral achievement but a social performance, and it coexists with actual moral failure — she "insult[s] a worthy old gentlewoman" dependent on her charity. The point is clear: delicacy is not merely useless as a substitute for virtue; it is actively corrosive of it.

### Sickly Delicacy and Marriage

Wollstonecraft shifts to the marriage market to expose the structural function of delicacy. Women are counseled to affect weakness because it appeals to male pride:

> Nature has given woman a weaker frame than man; but, to ensure her husband's affections, must a wife ... condescend, to use art, and feign a sickly delicacy, in order to secure her husband's affection? Weakness may excite tenderness, and gratify the arrogant pride of man; but the lordly caresses of a protector will not gratify a noble mind that pants for and deserves to be respected.
> (002-059)

### Delicacy as a Barrier to Virtue

Genuine [[virtue]], for Wollstonecraft, requires the capacity to act in the world — to confront injustice, to discharge [[duty]], to exercise [[reason]] in difficult circumstances. Delicacy, by teaching women to retreat from the world into a private sphere of refined feeling, makes genuine virtue impossible. The delicate woman may be pleasing, but she cannot be good — because goodness requires a strength that delicacy systematically undermines.

She extends this argument to physical health, connecting enforced delicacy to the sedentary confinement of women's lives:

> Sedentary employments render the majority of women sickly, and false notions of female excellence make them proud of this delicacy, though it be another fetter, that by calling the attention continually to the body, cramps the activity of the mind.
> (004-093)

### Wollstonecraft's Own Delicacy

Notably, Wollstonecraft does not reject delicacy wholesale. She affirms "natural delicacy" in the context of genuine affection — the kind that distinguishes the caresses of the beloved individual from mere sensual gratification:

> Without this natural delicacy, love becomes a selfish personal gratification that soon degrades the character.
> (005-103)

And when a man takes liberties with a woman he has just met: "she will consider such an impertinent freedom in the light of an insult, if she have any true delicacy, instead of being flattered by this unmeaning homage to beauty" (005-104). The distinction is crucial: natural delicacy is a quality of moral perception; artificial delicacy is a social performance that replaces moral perception with mere squeamishness.
