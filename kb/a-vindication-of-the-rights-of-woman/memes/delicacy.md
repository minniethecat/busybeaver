In Wollstonecraft's analysis, **delicacy** is a key term in the eighteenth-century vocabulary of feminine virtue — and one she subjects to sustained critique. What conduct writers praised as "delicacy of sentiment" she exposes as a cultivated weakness: "that weakness which we falsely call delicacy" (003-011). The word appears throughout the *Vindication* in two opposed senses: the false delicacy imposed on women as a fetter, and the genuine delicacy of a refined and rational mind.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft distinguishes between two forms of delicacy. The first, which she condemns, is "a sickly delicacy that turns away from simple unadorned truth" (P03-011) — an affectation produced by sentimental education that renders women incapable of confronting reality. The second, which she would endorse, is the delicacy that accompanies genuine refinement of mind and moral perception — what Dr. Johnson defined as "quickness of perception" (004-046).

The false delicacy Wollstonecraft critiques functions as a social performance. Women are taught to cultivate "a romantic unnatural delicacy of feeling" (002-065) that wastes their lives "in the pursuit of an imaginary perfection." This delicacy is not a natural feminine quality but an artefact of education: "women are made to assume an artificial character before their faculties have acquired any strength" (003-024).

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

The core of Wollstonecraft's argument is that false delicacy serves as a mechanism of social control. It is "another fetter" (004-099) that restricts women's freedom while flattering their vanity: "women…are made proud of this delicacy, though it be another fetter" (004-099). The system works because women internalise the ideal, taking pride in the very quality that limits them.

Wollstonecraft traces this ideal to conduct literature, particularly Dr. John Gregory's *A Father's Legacy to His Daughters*. Gregory's "advice respecting delicacy of sentiment" (002-062) recommends that women cultivate a refined sensibility — precisely the counsel Wollstonecraft finds pernicious. She argues that Gregory's version of delicacy, far from producing virtue, encourages women to "feign a sickly delicacy in order to secure her husband's affection" (002-049), making sincerity and genuine moral development impossible.

The alternative Wollstonecraft proposes is not coarseness but strength. Instead of "hardening their minds by the severities of science and philosophy" — the false choice posed by defenders of delicacy — women should develop the fortitude to bear truth without the protective veil of sentimental refinement (003-011).

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The contrast with Rousseau is instructive. Rousseau's Sophie embodies "delicacy of sentiment" as a natural feminine attribute; for Wollstonecraft, this is precisely the "romantic unnatural delicacy" (002-065) that must be dismantled. Gregory's advice occupies a middle ground — more pragmatic than Rousseau's idealism but equally complicit in perpetuating an ideal that weakens women. Wollstonecraft's position is radical: true delicacy, like true modesty, can only be achieved by a mind trained in reason, not one taught to perform refinement.
