## Definition

The conduct book was a popular eighteenth-century literary genre that prescribed rules of behaviour, manners, and morality — particularly for women. These works, typically written by men, instructed women on how to be pleasing wives, modest companions, and proper members of society. Wollstonecraft identifies the conduct book tradition as a central obstacle to women's intellectual development, arguing that it reduces women to objects of male gratification rather than cultivating them as rational beings.

## In Context

Wollstonecraft engages with the conduct book tradition from the very opening of _A Vindication of the Rights of Woman_. In the Introduction, she declares that she has "turned over various books written on the subject of education" and concluded that "the neglected education of my fellow creatures is the grand source of the misery I deplore" (P03-001). She argues that these instructional works share a fundamental flaw: they address women "rather as women than human creatures" and are "more anxious to make them alluring mistresses than rational wives" (P03-001).

In Chapter II, Wollstonecraft intensifies her critique, asserting that "the books of instruction, written by men of genius, have had the same tendency as more frivolous productions" (P03-002). She draws a direct line from conduct literature to the intellectual enfeeblement of women, arguing that these works — like the broader system of female education — treat women "only as females, and not as a part of the human species" (P03-002).

### The Sweeping Indictment

Wollstonecraft's critique is not limited to individual authors but extends to the entire genre:

> All the writers who have written on the subject of female education and manners, from Rousseau to Dr. Gregory, have contributed to render women more artificial, weaker characters, than they would otherwise have been; and, consequently, more useless members of society.
> (002-029)

### Fordyce's Sentimental Sermons

The specific targets of Wollstonecraft's critique include James Fordyce, whose _Sermons to Young Women_ were widely read. She objects to their affected style and degrading sentimentalism:

> Dr. Fordyce's sermons have long made a part of a young woman's library; nay, girls at school are allowed to read them; but I should instantly dismiss them from my pupil's, if I wished to strengthen her understanding.
> (005-071)

> These discourses are written in such an affected style, that were it only on that account, and had I nothing to object against his *mellifluous* precepts, I should not allow girls to peruse them, unless I designed to hunt every spark of nature out of their composition, melting every human quality into female weakness and artificial grace.
> (005-072)

She quotes Fordyce directly to expose the absurdity of his rhetoric, in a passage she declines even to comment on seriously:

> "Behold these smiling innocents, whom I have graced with my fairest gifts, and committed to your protection; behold them with love and respect; treat them with tenderness and honour. They are timid and want to be defended. They are frail; O do not take advantage of their weakness!"
> (005-075)

### Gregory's Legacy to His Daughters

Wollstonecraft reserves some of her sharpest criticism for [[john-gregory|John Gregory]]'s _A Father's Legacy to His Daughters_. Despite respecting Gregory's heart, she "entirely disapprove[s] of his celebrated Legacy" (002-055). She objects to his recommendation that women cultivate a fondness for dress, his advice to conceal their feelings, and his counsels that treat women as beings whose entire moral universe revolves around the husband they hope to secure:

> Dr. Gregory goes much further; he actually recommends dissimulation, and advises an innocent girl to give the lie to her feelings, and not dance with spirit, when gaiety of heart would make her feet eloquent, without making her gestures immodest.
> (002-057)

> I now allude to that part of Dr. Gregory's treatise, where he advises a wife never to let her husband know the extent of her sensibility or affection. Voluptuous precaution; and as ineffectual as absurd.
> (002-062)

> Of the same complexion is Dr. Gregory's advice respecting delicacy of sentiment, which he advises a woman not to acquire, if she has determined to marry.
> (002-070)

Wollstonecraft's final assessment of Gregory's work is damning: "The question is, whether it procures most pain or pleasure? The answer will decide the propriety of Dr. Gregory's advice, and show how absurd and tyrannic it is thus to lay down a system of slavery" (002-074).

## Key Passages

> "I have turned over various books written on the subject of education, and patiently observed the conduct of parents and the management of schools; but what has been the result? a profound conviction, that the neglected education of my fellow creatures is the grand source of the misery I deplore; and that women in particular, are rendered weak and wretched by a variety of concurring causes, originating from one hasty conclusion." (P03-001)

> "One cause of this barren blooming I attribute to a false system of education, gathered from the books written on this subject by men, who, considering females rather as women than human creatures, have been more anxious to make them alluring mistresses than rational wives." (P03-001)

> "The books of instruction, written by men of genius, have had the same tendency as more frivolous productions; and that, in the true style of Muhammadanism, they are only considered as females, and not as a part of the human species." (P03-002)

> "All the writers who have written on the subject of female education and manners, from Rousseau to Dr. Gregory, have contributed to render women more artificial, weaker characters, than they would otherwise have been; and, consequently, more useless members of society." (002-029)
