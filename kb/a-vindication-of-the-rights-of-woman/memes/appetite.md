**Appetite** is, in Wollstonecraft's moral vocabulary, the bodily and sub-rational dimension of human desire — the "animal appetite" (004-088) that human beings share with other creatures and that, when not governed by reason and elevated by affection, reduces persons to objects and relationships to transactions. Throughout the *Vindication*, she distinguishes appetite sharply from the higher forms of attachment — friendship, rational affection, the love that is grounded in esteem — and argues that a society that reduces relations between the sexes to "the personal intercourse of appetite" (005-088) degrades both men and women.

The concept is central to Wollstonecraft's critique of the sexual double standard. Men are permitted to indulge their appetites while condemning women for satisfying them; women are educated to stimulate male appetite while being denied any appetite of their own. "The depravity of the appetite, which brings the sexes together, has occasioned all the miseries which the world is full of" (008-027) — the claim is characteristically sweeping, and it identifies appetite as not merely a personal failing but a social catastrophe.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's treatment of appetite draws on the long philosophical tradition — running from Plato through Augustine to the Enlightenment — that distinguishes reason, spirit, and appetite as the three elements of the human soul. Appetite, in this tradition, is the lowest element: the desires of the body for food, drink, sex, and comfort. It is not evil in itself — it is part of God's creation — but it must be governed by the higher faculties if the soul is to be well-ordered.

The distinctive contribution of the *Vindication* is to give this philosophical analysis a gendered inflection. Appetite, in Wollstonecraft's account, is not distributed equally between the sexes — or rather, the social treatment of appetite is not. Men are permitted to indulge their appetites; women are permitted only to stimulate male appetite. "The appetite that draws him to them" (004-062) is a male appetite; women are its objects, not its subjects.

This asymmetry has far-reaching consequences. Because women are denied the status of subjects of appetite — because they are not permitted to desire, only to be desired — they are denied the education that would enable them to govern desire through reason. A woman who has been taught only to stimulate male appetite has been taught nothing about how to govern her own; the result is not purity but "cunning" — the debased substitute for rational self-governance that dependence cultivates.

The connection to virtue is essential. "Love, considered as an animal appetite, cannot long feed on itself without degenerating into caprice or disgust" (004-088). Appetite that is not transformed by reason and affection into something higher exhausts itself and leaves behind only "caprice or disgust." The cultivation of virtue requires the elevation of appetite into rational affection — and this elevation is precisely what the existing education of women prevents.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument about appetite unfolds across the linked domains of moral psychology, social criticism, and educational reform.

In moral psychology, she establishes the distinction between appetite and higher forms of attachment. "They sink into mere appetites, become a personal momentary gratification" (002-057) — the verb "sink" is significant. Appetite is not a different kind of thing from love; it is love that has sunk, that has been deprived of the rational and spiritual dimensions that would make it fully human. The task of education is to prevent this sinking, to cultivate the higher dimensions of attachment so that appetite is governed by reason rather than the reverse.

In social criticism, she anatomises the damage that the reign of appetite does to relations between the sexes. "The depravity of the appetite, which brings the sexes together, has occasioned all the miseries which the world is full of" (008-027). The claim is deliberately extreme, but the argument that supports it is precise: when the relation between men and women is governed by appetite rather than reason, women are reduced to commodities, marriage becomes prostitution, and children are raised by parents who have never learned to govern their own desires.

In educational reform, she prescribes the remedy: education that cultivates reason and virtue in both sexes, so that appetite is governed by higher faculties. "Were the understanding improved, the passions would be governed, and the appetites would not be indulged merely to gratify the senses" (005-042). The remedy is not to suppress appetite — suppression only drives it underground, where it becomes "cunning" — but to educate the faculties that can govern it.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The treatment of appetite in eighteenth-century moral philosophy was dominated by the question of whether human beings are naturally benevolent or naturally selfish. Hobbes had argued that appetite — the "perpetual and restless desire of power after power" — was the fundamental motive of human action. Shaftesbury and Hutcheson had responded that benevolence was as natural as appetite, and that the moral sense could govern the lower passions.

Wollstonecraft sides with the benevolists against Hobbes but adds a gendered analysis that neither side had contemplated. The question is not simply whether appetite is natural — it is — but whether the social arrangements that govern appetite are just. A society that permits men to indulge their appetites while condemning women for doing the same is not a society governed by reason but a society governed by power.

Rousseau's treatment of appetite is consistently opposed. In *Émile*, the tutor carefully manages Émile's appetites so that they develop naturally; Sophie's appetites are managed so that they develop into the desire to please. Wollstonecraft's response is that this asymmetry is the root of the corruption she diagnoses: when appetite is governed differently in men and women, the result is not complementary virtue but mutual degradation.
