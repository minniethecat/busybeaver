While Wollstonecraft's *Vindication* is primarily a work of gender analysis, the concept of **class** runs through it as a critical secondary axis. She attends "to those in the middle class, because they appear to be in the most natural state" (P03-006), and her argument frequently distinguishes between the corruptions of the aristocracy and the degraded condition of the labouring poor.

Class, for Wollstonecraft, is not merely an economic category but a moral environment: each class produces characteristic deformations of character through the circumstances and expectations it imposes.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft uses "class" to denote the broad social groupings — from the "superior class" (004-100) of the aristocracy to the "lower class" (012-069) of servants and labourers — that structure eighteenth-century British society. Her usage is flexible, encompassing both rank-based hierarchy and the emerging language of economic stratification.

She observes that "one class presses on another" (009-001) in a system of competitive emulation that corrupts each in turn.

The middle class occupies a privileged position in Wollstonecraft's moral geography. Because they are neither corrupted by hereditary wealth nor brutalised by extreme poverty, the middle class are, she argues, the natural bearers of the virtues — industry, modesty, and domestic affection — that a reformed society requires.

Her focus on the middle class is not mere snobbery but a strategic selection of the social group she believes most capable of rational improvement.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft builds her class analysis around the relationship between circumstance and character. She contends that the "superior class" are "not to be considered" (004-071) as representative of women's potential because their wealth insulates them from the very exertions that produce virtue.

The aristocracy, in her account, is morally hollow — its members have their wants met without effort and therefore never develop the "vigorous exertions of intellect" that form character.

At the other end of the social scale, Wollstonecraft acknowledges the brutalisation of poverty. She notes that "amongst the lower class" (012-069) domestic affection is often "oftener to be found" than moral refinement, but she does not sentimentalise this observation.

Her political demand — that "every class of people" (012-018) should receive national education, that schools be "absolutely free and open to all classes" (012-049) — is radical in its scope, extending the promise of rational improvement across the social spectrum.

The intersection of gender and class is particularly revealing in Wollstonecraft's analysis. She notes that "those of the superior class, by catching, at least a smattering of literature" (004-100), have occasionally produced women of talent, but that "women have never yet started out of a class" (004-101) in significant numbers because the structural barriers are too great.

Class position and gender subordination reinforce each other; changing one without the other is insufficient.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft's class analysis departs from both the aristocratic conservatism that treated hierarchy as natural and the emerging radicalism that would soon call for its abolition.

Her project is reformist: she seeks to extend the virtues she associates with the middle class — reason, modesty, industry — across the social spectrum through education, rather than to eliminate class distinctions entirely. This distinguishes her from later socialist feminists while maintaining her radicalism in the context of 1792.

## Key Passages

> I pay particular attention to those in the middle class, because they appear to be in the most natural state. (P03-006)

> One class presses on another; for all are aiming at respectability. (009-001)

> Those of the superior class, by catching, at least a smattering of literature, have sometimes appeared to advantage. (004-100)

> A sufficient number of masters should be chosen, and schools absolutely free and open to all classes. (012-049)
