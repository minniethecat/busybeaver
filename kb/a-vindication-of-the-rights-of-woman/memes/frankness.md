# Frankness

Wollstonecraft treats frankness — honest, direct openness — as a natural virtue that society, particularly through the education of women, systematically extinguishes.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft deploys frankness strategically in the opening of her work. Addressing Talleyrand-Périgord directly, she writes: "Pardon my frankness, but I must observe, that you treat the subject in too cursory a manner." (P02-002) This performance of frankness is itself an argument — she models the intellectual directness she believes women should be entitled to exercise.

The explicit theoretical treatment comes in her observation that "the natural frankness of youth" is restrained by parents who "instill such false notions of propriety." (002-047) Frankness, like reason, is a natural endowment that corrupt education destroys. The mechanism is gender-specific: girls are taught that frankness is unbecoming, that feminine virtue requires concealment and indirection.

The concept thus functions as a bridge between Wollstonecraft's educational theory and her gender critique: frankness is natural, education should preserve and direct it, but current education extinguishes it — especially in women.

## Key Passages

> "Pardon my frankness, but I must observe, that you treat the subject in too cursory a manner." (P02-002)

> "The natural frankness of youth will be restrained by instilling such false notions of propriety." (002-047)
