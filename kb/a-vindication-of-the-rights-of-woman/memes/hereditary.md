# Hereditary

A political and social category that Wollstonecraft subjects to sustained critique: the transmission of wealth, titles, and power by birth rather than by merit, which she identifies as a primary source of corruption in both the state and the character of individuals who inherit unearned advantage.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft uses "hereditary" almost exclusively in a critical sense, associating it with the artificial inequalities that deform both individual character and the body politic. "Hereditary honours, riches, and monarchy" are gathered together as sources of "misery" that have "flowed" through society (001-013). Hereditary distinctions are presented as incompatible with natural merit: they "clash with the mental superiority that naturally raises a man above his fellows" (001-021). The transmission of wealth and status without effort corrupts the recipient: "habitual idleness can hereditary wealth and titles produce" nothing but vice (009-002). For Wollstonecraft, hereditary privilege is the structural opposite of the merit-based social order that reason and virtue demand.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

The critique of hereditary privilege runs through the entire Vindication, connecting her analysis of political corruption with her analysis of women's condition. Hereditary power "chokes the affections" and prevents the development of genuine virtue, because those who inherit status never learn to earn it (004-015). She links hereditary wealth to the condition of women: "riches and hereditary honours have made cyphers of women" by reducing them to "mere artificial beings" valued for ornament rather than character (002-027). The solution is not to abolish property but to ensure that possessions are earned: "till hereditary possessions are spread abroad, the monster will ever exist in some shape or other" (004-050). The hereditary principle is fundamentally at odds with her vision of a society organized around rational merit.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft's position places her in the radical wing of Enlightenment thought, aligned with Paine and Godwin rather than Burke. Where Burke defended hereditary succession and the "wisdom of the ages" embedded in inherited institutions, Wollstonecraft sees only accumulated injustice. However, her critique of hereditary privilege is more focused on its moral effects — how it corrupts character — than on proposing specific constitutional alternatives, distinguishing her approach from more programmatic republican writers. Her argument that women should "throw off their gaudy hereditary trappings" (002-015) applies the critique of inherited status directly to gender, an extension absent from most male radical writing of the period.

## Key Passages

> the misery that has flowed from hereditary honours, riches, and monarchy (001-013)

> arbitrary power, up to the hereditary distinctions that clash with the mental superiority that naturally raises a man above his fellows (001-021)

> Riches and hereditary honours have made cyphers of women, and mere artificial beings. (002-027)

> what but habitual idleness can hereditary wealth and titles produce? (009-002)

> it is vain to expect it whilst hereditary power chokes the affections (004-015)
