# Russia

The Russian Empire appears in Wollstonecraft's text through two contrasting allusions: the example of an enlightened female sovereign, and a satirical remark about commercial exploitation.

## Location

The Russian Empire, stretching across Eastern Europe and Northern Asia. In the 18th century under Catherine II (Catherine the Great, reigned 1762–1796), Russia was a major European power and a subject of Enlightenment interest.

## Relevance to the Text

Wollstonecraft's references to Russia reflect her ambivalence about female rulers who achieve prominence without necessarily advancing the condition of women as a class.

### In the Argument

In Chapter 4, Wollstonecraft lists "Mrs. Macaulay, the Empress of Russia, Madame d'Eon, etc." (004-101) among examples of exceptional women who have distinguished themselves. Catherine the Great appears alongside the historian Catharine Macaulay and the diplomat Chevalier d'Éon as evidence that women are capable of intellectual and political achievement when circumstances permit. However, Wollstonecraft's larger argument insists that isolated examples of female greatness do not excuse systematic denial of education to women as a whole.

In Chapter 13, she delivers a satirical jab: "want to open a fresh trade with Russia for whips; a present which a father ..." (013-079), mocking the eagerness of tyrannical fathers and husbands to acquire instruments of punishment. The reference to Russian whips evokes the stereotype of Russian despotism and corporal punishment.

## Historical Context

Catherine the Great was a complex figure for Enlightenment thinkers. While she corresponded with Voltaire and Diderot and presented herself as an enlightened monarch, her actual reforms were limited, and serfdom persisted. For Wollstonecraft, Catherine represented the paradox of a woman wielding power in a system that denied women as a sex the education and independence needed for genuine virtue.
