The concept of the **artificial** is one of Wollstonecraft's most powerful analytical tools. Throughout the *Vindication*, she uses "artificial" to designate qualities, behaviours, and social structures that are presented as natural but are in fact manufactured by education, custom, and power. "Artificial weakness" (P03-014, 002-015), "artificial graces" (002-083, 005-040), "artificial notions of beauty" (003-021), and "artificial feelings" (P03-010, 005-060) — in each case, the adjective does the work of denaturalisation, exposing what is culturally constructed as contingent and alterable.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft's "artificial" operates in explicit opposition to the natural. Where Rousseau and other writers claimed feminine weakness as nature's design, Wollstonecraft insists it is "artificial" — produced by "enervating indulgences" (003-021) and a system of education that prevents women from developing their rational faculties. The term functions as a critique of reification: what is artificial is not inevitable, and what is constructed can be reconstructed.

She extends the concept beyond individual character to social forms. "Artificial manners and virtues" (001-021) disgust her as much as "artificial fools" (001-014), and she identifies "an artificial mode of behaviour" (008-003) produced by "supposed necessity." The artificial, for Wollstonecraft, is the totality of performances that a corrupt society demands — performances that deform those who enact them.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument about the artificial has a characteristic structure: identify a quality presented as natural → trace it to its social causes → reveal it as artificial → argue that different conditions would produce different outcomes. This pattern recurs across the *Vindication* and constitutes one of her most distinctly philosophical contributions to eighteenth-century feminism.

The most important application of this argument concerns female character itself. Women "are made to assume an artificial character before their faculties have acquired any strength" (003-024). This artificial character — weak, pleasing, dependent — is then mistaken for the real thing. The result is a circular system: society creates artificial women, observes their artificial behaviour, and concludes that artificiality is female nature.

She also diagnoses the emotional register of the artificial. "The turgid bombast of artificial feelings, which, coming from the head, never reach the heart" (P03-010) is, for Wollstonecraft, a symptom of a broader cultural disease. Sentimental literature and conduct books teach women to express feelings they have not genuinely experienced, producing a society in which "there is a display of cold, artificial feelings, and that parade of sensibility which convinces without touching the heart" (005-060).

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Rousseau is Wollstonecraft's primary foil on this point. "Disgusted with artificial manners and virtues, the citizen of Geneva" (001-021) retreated to an ideal of natural simplicity — but one that, Wollstonecraft argues, merely relocated artificiality to a different register. Rousseau's praise of natural womanhood is itself a construction, and a damaging one: "Rousseau became enamoured of a state of nature…and in his zeal to render women more natural, he contributed to render women more artificial, weaker characters, than they would otherwise have been" (002-015). This is a subtle and devastating critique: the very attempt to recover the natural, when guided by fantasy rather than observation, produces a new form of artificiality.
