# Sexes

The phrase "the two sexes" appears throughout the *Vindication* as the organising category of Wollstonecraft's social analysis. She does not deny that men and women differ, but she denies that their differences justify different educations, different moral standards, or different access to the rights and duties of rational beings. Her argument that "the two sexes, in the acquirement of virtue, ought to aim at the same standard" (002-001) is the foundation on which the entire *Vindication* rests, and her analysis of the "intercourse between the sexes" (004-050) maps the social mechanisms by which inequality is reproduced.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft opens Chapter 2 with a clear methodological statement: "I shall first consider women in the grand light of human creatures, who, in common with men, are placed on this earth to unfold their faculties; and afterwards I shall more particularly point out their peculiar designation" (002-001). The order is significant. The common humanity of the sexes — their shared status as rational creatures — must be established before any discussion of sexual difference can proceed. To begin with difference, as Rousseau and the conduct-book writers do, is to prejudge the question in favour of inequality.

Wollstonecraft's definition of the sexes is thus procedural rather than essential: what the sexes are cannot be determined in advance of educating both equally. "I wish to see women placed in the grand light of human creatures," she writes in the Introduction; only after they have been so placed — after they have received the education that would allow their capacities to develop freely — can any reliable judgment be made about the natural differences between the sexes. Until then, "it is vain to expect virtue from women till they are, in some degree, independent of men" (002-059).

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

The argument proceeds through three connected claims. The first is epistemological: we do not know what women's natural capacities are because women have never been educated in conditions of freedom. The "prevailing opinion of a sexual character" (the title of Chapter 2) is a social construction mistaken for a natural fact. Men have "kept women in the dark" and then "ridiculed them for having weak eyes." The subordination of the sexes is not observed; it is produced — and then naturalized by a philosophical tradition that treats the effects of education as evidence of innate inferiority.

The second claim is moral: "the two sexes, in the acquirement of virtue, ought to aim at the same standard" (002-001). Virtue cannot be sex-specific because virtue is nothing other than the conformity of conduct to reason, and reason is the same for all rational beings. "If women are rational creatures," Wollstonecraft argues, "they should be incited to acquire virtues which they may call their own" (003-061). The "sexual virtues" celebrated by the conduct-book tradition — modesty, chastity, compliance — are not virtues at all but strategies of survival in conditions of dependence.

The third claim is social: the inequality between the sexes corrupts both. "The social relations of the sexes are indeed truly admirable," Wollstonecraft observes with deliberate irony (005-033). Because men are taught to value women for their charms rather than their virtues, and women are taught to value themselves for the admiration they can command, neither sex develops the steady principles that make genuine relationship — friendship, companionship, rational affection — possible. "The man who can be contented to live with a pretty, useful companion, without a mind, has lost in voluptuous gratifications a taste for more refined enjoyments" (005-033). The degradation of women entails the degradation of men.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Rousseau's *Émile* had naturalized the difference between the sexes as the foundation of social order: "The man should be strong and active; the woman should be weak and passive." From this axiom, Rousseau derived entirely different educations for Émile and Sophie — one cultivating reason and independence, the other cultivating compliance and charm. Wollstonecraft's reply is that Rousseau's axiom is not an observation but a prescription — a statement about how Rousseau wants the sexes to be, not about how they are. The circular logic — women are weak, therefore they must be educated to be weak, which proves they are naturally weak — is precisely the "false hypothesis" on which, she argues, the entire edifice of female subordination rests.

John Gregory's *A Father's Legacy to His Daughters* (1774) offers a more moderate but equally problematic version: Gregory advises his daughters to cultivate "softness" and "gentleness" because these are the qualities men value in women. Wollstonecraft's objection is not that Gregory's advice is insincere but that it accepts the existing relations between the sexes as natural and immutable rather than as the product of unequal power. The father who advises his daughters to please men is training them for a market in which they are the commodity, not the agent.

## Key Passages

> I shall first consider women in the grand light of human creatures, who, in common with men, are placed on this earth to unfold their faculties; and afterwards I shall more particularly point out their peculiar designation. (002-001)

> Let it not be concluded, that I wish to invert the order of things; I have already granted, that, from the constitution of their bodies, men seem to be designed by Providence to attain a greater degree of virtue. I speak collectively of the whole sex; but I see not the shadow of a reason to conclude that their virtues should differ in respect to their nature. In fact, how can they, if virtue has only one eternal standard? I must therefore, if I reason consequentially, as strenuously maintain, that they must be allowed to have the same direction, as that there is a God. (002-032)

> The social relations of the sexes are indeed truly admirable: from their intimacy, the most pure and exalted happiness is supposed to flow; but, it is a lotterie. (005-033)

> Liberty is the mother of virtue, and if women are, by their very constitution, slaves, and not allowed to breathe the sharp invigorating air of freedom, they must ever languish like exotics, and be reckoned beautiful flaws in nature. (002-083)
