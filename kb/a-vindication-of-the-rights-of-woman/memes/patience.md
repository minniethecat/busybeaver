# Patience

Wollstonecraft's treatment of patience reveals the critical method she applies to every virtue traditionally assigned to women. She asks: patience for what, and on whose terms? When patience is demanded of women as a concession to their supposed weakness — when it appears alongside "docility, good-humour, and flexibility" (004-026) in the catalogue of negative virtues expected of the powerless — it becomes an instrument of subjection. But when patience is understood as the moral discipline required for genuine intellectual and ethical development — the patience to think carefully, to examine one's own motives, to persist in the face of difficulty — it becomes indispensable. The task, as Wollstonecraft frames it, is to rescue patience from the contempt into which it has fallen as a "feminine" virtue and to reestablish it as a universal component of rational character.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft distinguishes between two kinds of patience without always naming them explicitly. The first is the patience demanded of subordinates: the patience to endure injustice without complaint, to accept one's lot without ambition, to wait quietly while others decide. This patience is not a virtue at all but a posture of submission — "the negative virtues only are expected, when any virtues are expected, patience, docility, good-humour, and flexibility; virtues incompatible with any vigorous exertion of intellect" (004-026). The grouping is damning: patience here belongs to a class of virtues defined by their negativity, their absence of active force, their incompatibility with intellectual vigor.

The second patience is the patience required for genuine moral and intellectual work. It is the patience to undergo "calm investigation" (004-027), to persist through difficulty, to examine one's own heart honestly rather than crying out in "suffocating impatience" (008-018). This patience is not passive but active — a discipline rather than a submission. It belongs to the same family as "industry" and "self-denial" (004-027), the virtues of self-governance that Wollstonecraft associates with masculine character but insists should be universal.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

### The Negative Virtues Critique

The passage in which Wollstonecraft lists patience among women's expected virtues is one of her most concentrated attacks on the ideology of sexual character. Drawing on Adam Smith's *Theory of Moral Sentiments*, she observes that the character of people of rank and fortune might "with the greatest propriety be applied to the female sex" (004-026). Both groups — the rich and women — are "LOCALIZED" by their social position, prevented from exerting "any manual strength," and expected to display only the negative virtues: "patience, docility, good-humour, and flexibility; virtues incompatible with any vigorous exertion of intellect" (004-026).

The word "LOCALIZED" — which Wollstonecraft capitalizes and apologizes for as a coinage — captures her analysis precisely. Women are confined to a local, particular sphere rather than being permitted to operate on the universal plane of reason and principle. The virtues assigned to them are correspondingly local and particular: patience with one's husband, docility toward one's parents, good-humour in company, flexibility to adapt to others' wishes. These are not the virtues of a rational being who acts on principle; they are the accommodations of a dependent being who must please to survive.

Wollstonecraft sharpens the point by noting that women, "by living more with each other, and to being seldom absolutely alone, they are more under the influence of sentiments than passions" (004-026). This observation connects lack of solitude to a specific deformation of character. Sentiments — the shallow, reactive feelings of social life — flourish in constant company. Passions — the deep, sustained commitments that give force to character — require solitude to develop. Women, denied solitude, are also denied passion. They are left with patience-as-endurance rather than patience-as-discipline.

### The Impatient Heart

The most psychologically acute treatment of patience in the text appears not in direct argument but in a passage on humility and self-examination. Describing the individual who has learned to examine his own heart honestly, Wollstonecraft writes: "During the still hour of self-collection, the angry brow of offended justice will be fearfully deprecated, or the tie which draws man to the Deity will be recognized in the pure sentiment of reverential adoration" (008-018). The "still hour of self-collection" — solitude transformed into moral discipline — requires patience. One must sit with uncomfortable truths about oneself without fleeing.

The alternative is what she calls "suffocating impatience" — the cry of a heart that cannot bear its own imperfection: "The agonized heart will cry with suffocating impatience--I too am a man! and have vices, hid, perhaps, from human eye, that bend me to the dust before God" (008-018). This is impatience not as mere restlessness but as a moral crisis — the inability to tolerate the gap between what one is and what one ought to be. True patience, by contrast, would enable the calm acknowledgment of imperfection that makes moral progress possible.

### Patience, Knowledge, and Industry

Wollstonecraft also addresses patience in the context of intellectual development. Quoting Adam Smith's rhetorical questions about the great, she asks: "By what important accomplishments is the young nobleman instructed to support the dignity of his rank...Is it by knowledge, by industry, by patience, by self-denial, or by virtue of any kind?" (004-027). The answer, of course, is no — the nobleman achieves his station not through these virtues but through the "arts" of graceful deportment. But the list itself is significant: knowledge, industry, patience, self-denial. These are the virtues of the self-made mind, the mind that has earned its development through sustained effort. They are precisely the virtues that women's education — which teaches "accomplishments" rather than genuine knowledge — fails to cultivate.

The biographical sketch provides a glimpse of the alternative. Wollstonecraft herself, we are told, would have "had more patience with rude, brute coercion, than with the elegant tyranny" (P01-013) of polite oppression. This is patience of a different kind — not submission to injustice but a preference for honest conflict over deceptive gentleness. It suggests a writer who could endure hardship when the terms were clear but who refused to accept injury dressed as civility. The biographical patience is the patience of a reformer who accepts the slow work of persuasion but will not be placated by false courtesy.
