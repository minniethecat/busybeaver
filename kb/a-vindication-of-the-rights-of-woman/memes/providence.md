**Providence** appears in Wollstonecraft's argument as a theological warrant and a critical target — sometimes invoked to authorise her claims about the natural equality of the sexes, sometimes exposed as a rhetorical device by which the defenders of women's subordination clothe contingent customs in the language of divine decree. Her treatment of providence reflects the broader tension in Enlightenment thought between the appeal to divine order and the insistence on rational autonomy.

When Wollstonecraft writes that "there is but one way appointed by providence to lead mankind to either virtue or happiness" (002-001), she invokes providence to unify the moral world under a single standard. The implication is radical: if providence has appointed one way for all of humanity, then the separate and inferior education prescribed for women cannot be justified by appeal to providential design.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft uses providence in the sense common to eighteenth-century rational theology: the divine ordering of the world, manifest in the laws of nature and the constitution of the human mind. Providence is not, in her usage, a matter of miraculous intervention but of the rational structure of creation — the "ways of providence" (003-031) that reason can discern and that philosophy must "justify."

This rationalised conception of providence is crucial to her argument. If providence operates through natural laws that reason can discover, then the appeal to providence cannot justify arrangements that contradict the deliverances of reason. A providential order that required the subjection of half of humanity would be a contradiction in terms — a divine decree at war with the rational nature God had given to women as well as men.

The concept also carries a critical function in her rhetoric. When she speaks of "justifying the ways of providence respecting them" (003-031) — "them" being women — she is ironically echoing theodicy (the justification of God's ways to man) while redirecting it toward a feminist end. The thing that needs justifying is not God's treatment of women but man's; the appeal to providence is often a disguise for the appeal to power.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument about providence operates on two fronts. Negatively, she exposes the misuse of providential language to naturalise contingent social arrangements. When men claim that women's subordination is ordained by providence, they are, in her analysis, attributing to God what is in fact the work of custom, prejudice, and power.

Positively, she deploys providence as a critical standard against which existing arrangements can be judged. If providence has given women the same rational faculties as men, then the denial of education to women is not a compliance with providential order but a violation of it. The "one way appointed by providence" (002-001) is the way of reason and virtue — and this way is open to women as much as to men.

The argument reaches its sharpest edge in the suggestion that women should "break loose from his providence" (001-016) — that is, from the false providentialism that tells them their subordination is divinely ordained — and "boldly learn to know good" through the exercise of their own reason. This is not atheism but a demand for intellectual autonomy within a theistic framework: women must distinguish between the genuine providence that governs the natural order and the counterfeit providence invoked by those who benefit from their subjection.

The reference to "providence" having "furnished women with a natural" tenderness toward children (010-011) illustrates her constructive use of the concept. She does not deny that women have distinctive natural endowments; she denies that these endowments justify their exclusion from the full exercise of reason and the full enjoyment of rights. Providence gives nature; it does not give the social arrangements that pervert nature.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft's treatment of providence engages the tradition of natural theology from William Paley to Joseph Butler, for whom the evidence of design in nature was the foundation of religious belief. She accepts the general framework — the universe is providentially ordered — while insisting that the specific content of that order must be discovered by reason, not read off from existing social hierarchies.

Her position is closest to that of the rational Dissenters — Price, Priestley — who combined a strong doctrine of providence with a commitment to political reform. For these thinkers, as for Wollstonecraft, the recognition of providential order was not an argument for conservatism but a demand for the reform of institutions that contradicted that order.

## Key Passages

> Man has been held out as independent of his power, who made him, and woman has been considered as the mere creature of man, formed to gratify his appetites. It is time to break loose from his providence, and boldly learn to know good. (001-016)

> There is but one way appointed by providence to lead mankind to either virtue or happiness. (002-001)

> But to justify the ways of providence respecting them, by pointing out some irrefragable reason for thus keeping the two sexes asunder, is a task I have by no means attempted. (003-031)

> Nature, in these respects, may safely be left to herself; let women only acquire knowledge and humanity, and love will teach them modesty. There is no need of falsehoods, or of artificial sentiments, to guard the natural feelings; providence has furnished women with a natural modesty. (010-011, adapted)
