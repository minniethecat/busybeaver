# Notion

Notion is one of Wollstonecraft's most frequently deployed critical terms. When she speaks of "prevailing notions," "mistaken notions," or "false notions," she identifies ideas that have acquired social authority without rational warrant. Notions circulate as common sense, shaping behavior while evading scrutiny.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft targets "the prevailing notion respecting a sexual character" (P02-008) — the idea that men and women possess fundamentally different natures requiring different educations. This is the master notion that her entire work aims to dismantle.

A notion, in her usage, is not a reasoned conclusion but a received idea. It is "imbibed" rather than examined, "instilled" rather than argued. She repeatedly modifies "notions" with adjectives of error: "mistaken notions of female excellence" (P03-014), "artificial notions of beauty" (003-021), "false notions of modesty" (002-019). Each targets a domain where unexamined ideas produce real harm.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Women "who have early imbibed notions of passive obedience" (002-072) cannot develop the independence that virtue requires. The verb "imbibe" suggests absorption rather than learning — notions are taken in with the cultural atmosphere, not chosen through deliberation.

She writes with "indignation, the mistaken notions that enslave my sex" (002-081), making explicit the political function. Notions about beauty are particularly pernicious: when women are "sacrificed to libertine notions of beauty" (P03-012), their entire being is reduced to appearance.

These "false notions of beauty and delicacy stop" (006-006) the development of character, substituting aesthetic for moral judgment. Wollstonecraft extends the analysis to religion: without rational understanding, women cannot "form any true notions of religion" (005-033). Those who "only instill certain notions" (005-149) without encouraging examination are guilty of intellectual tyranny.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

The empiricist tradition — Locke, Hume — treated ideas as deriving from experience, but Wollstonecraft adds a sociological dimension her predecessors lacked. Notions are not merely individual errors but social products, produced by institutions, circulated through culture, internalized by the people they harm. Her analysis anticipates the concept of ideology.
