# Gratitude

Gratitude in Wollstonecraft's moral vocabulary is a sentiment with teeth. It is not merely a pleasant feeling of thankfulness but a bond that creates real and binding obligations — and one that, precisely because it creates obligations, can be perverted into an instrument of domination. Her treatment of gratitude moves from historical example to domestic relations to the parent-child bond, in each case asking the same question: is the gratitude demanded by the powerful a genuine moral claim, or is it a disguise for the demand of submission? The answer depends on whether the benefit that supposedly grounds gratitude was freely given, whether it genuinely benefited the recipient, and whether the obligation it creates is reciprocal or unilateral.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft approaches gratitude through the lens of justice rather than sentiment. Gratitude is not, for her, a spontaneous warmth of feeling but a response to a real benefit — and a response that carries determinate moral weight. To be genuinely grateful is to acknowledge a debt that must, in some form, be repaid. This makes gratitude a species of justice: the recognition that one has received something to which one was not entitled, and the corresponding recognition that one owes something in return.

But because gratitude creates obligation, it can be falsely claimed. The powerful can demand gratitude from the powerless as a cover for extracting submission. Parents can demand gratitude from children as a justification for tyranny. The sentimental language of gratitude — "after all I have done for you" — can conceal the reality that what was "done" was either not beneficial or was done from motives that merit no gratitude at all. Wollstonecraft's task is to distinguish genuine gratitude from its counterfeits by examining the conditions under which gratitude is properly owed.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

### Gratitude and Justice: The Spartan Example

Wollstonecraft's first significant reference to gratitude is also her most dramatic. Critiquing Rousseau's celebration of Sparta, she writes that he exalts "the brutal Spartans, who in defiance of justice and gratitude, sacrificed, in cold blood, the slaves that had shown themselves men to rescue their oppressors" (001-020). The phrase "in defiance of justice and gratitude" pairs the two concepts as coordinate demands of morality. The slaves who fought to rescue their Spartan masters had performed an act that created a claim of gratitude — they had "shown themselves men" by an act of courage that benefited those who oppressed them. The Spartans' response — sacrificing those same slaves "in cold blood" — was not merely cruel but unjust in a specific way: it violated the bond of gratitude that the slaves' actions had created.

This example establishes gratitude as something more than politeness or personal feeling. It belongs to the same moral order as justice. To violate gratitude is to violate a claim that another person holds against you — a claim grounded not in abstract right but in concrete benefit received. The Spartans owed those slaves something because of what the slaves had done for them. Their failure to acknowledge that debt was not ingratitude in the trivial sense of forgetfulness or rudeness; it was a species of injustice.

### Domestic Ingratitude

Wollstonecraft brings the analysis of gratitude into the domestic sphere through an extended anecdote. She describes "a weak woman of fashion, who was more than commonly proud of her delicacy and sensibility" (003-022) — a woman who performed refinement while neglecting genuine duty. This woman, Wollstonecraft recounts, would "recline with self-complacency on a sofa, and boast of her want of appetite as a proof of delicacy that extended to, or, perhaps, arose from, her exquisite sensibility" (003-022). The performance of delicacy — the cultivated inability to eat, to exert oneself, to engage with the world — is presented as a mark of refinement.

But Wollstonecraft immediately exposes the moral reality beneath the performance: "at the moment, I have seen her insult a worthy old gentlewoman, whom unexpected misfortunes had made dependent on her ostentatious bounty, and who, in better days, had claims on her gratitude" (003-022). The old gentlewoman "had claims" — the language of right and obligation, not of sentiment. She had provided benefits to the woman of fashion "in better days," and those benefits had created a debt that the woman of fashion now dishonored. The woman's "ostentatious bounty" — her conspicuous display of charity — was not generosity but the opposite: a refusal to acknowledge a genuine debt by dressing it as a free gift.

The passage reveals the structure of false gratitude. The woman of fashion presents her support of the old gentlewoman as an act of spontaneous benevolence, when in fact it is the repayment — and an inadequate one — of a debt she already owes. By refusing to acknowledge the debt, she transforms the old gentlewoman from a creditor with claims into a dependent who must be grateful. The asymmetry of power enables the inversion of the moral relationship: the debtor poses as the benefactor, and the real benefactor is reduced to the posture of a supplicant.

### Parental Gratitude and Filial Duty

Wollstonecraft's most systematic treatment of gratitude appears in her discussion of the parent-child relationship. She opens the chapter "Duty to Parents" with a characteristic move: "Why do we thus go back for principles that should always rest on the same base, and have the same weight to-day that they had a thousand years ago — and not a jot more?" (011-002). The appeal to antiquity, to tradition, to "what has always been believed" — these are not arguments but evasions. The duty of children to parents must be established on rational principles, not on custom.

She then states the principle: "If parents discharge their duty they have a strong hold and sacred claim on the gratitude of their children; but few parents are willing to receive the respectful affection of their offspring on such terms" (011-002). The condition is crucial. Parents have a claim on their children's gratitude *if* they discharge their duty — if they provide genuine care, education, and moral guidance. Gratitude is earned, not inherited. It is a response to benefit actually conferred, not a tribute owed to mere biological relationship.

But most parents, Wollstonecraft charges, are "not willing to receive the respectful affection of their offspring on such terms" (011-002). They do not want gratitude that is freely given in response to genuine benefit; they want obedience that is demanded as a right. "They demand blind obedience, because they do not merit a reasonable service: and to render these demands of weakness and ignorance more binding, a mysterious sanctity is spread round the most arbitrary principle" (011-002). The language of filial gratitude is deployed to mystify what is in fact a demand for submission. "Blind obedience" is dressed in the language of "sacred claim" — but the claim is not sacred at all; it is an assertion of power without justification.

Wollstonecraft's alternative is a model of reciprocal obligation: "The parent who pays proper attention to helpless infancy has a right to require the same attention when the feebleness of age comes upon him" (011-002). This is gratitude as reciprocity, not gratitude as submission. The parent cares for the child when the child is helpless; the child cares for the parent when the parent is helpless. The obligation is real, but it is grounded in the actual exchange of benefits over a lifetime, not in an abstract duty of obedience. And it is limited: "to subjugate a rational being to the mere will of another, after he is of age to answer to society for his own conduct, is a most cruel and undue stretch of power" (011-002). Gratitude does not extend to the surrender of one's rational autonomy. The parent may be owed care, but the parent is not owed obedience.

### Gratitude and the Structure of Oppression

Wollstonecraft's scattered treatments of gratitude converge on a single structural insight. Gratitude is one of the moral sentiments through which power relations are naturalized. The powerful demand gratitude from the powerless not because they have earned it but because the posture of gratitude reinforces the posture of submission. The woman must be grateful to the husband who provides for her; the child must be grateful to the parent who "gave him life"; the poor must be grateful to the rich who dispense charity. In each case, the demand for gratitude conceals the prior question: was the benefit genuinely given, or was it extracted? Does it create a real obligation, or does it merely fortify an existing hierarchy?

Her response is not to reject gratitude but to insist on its conditions. Gratitude is owed only when a genuine benefit has been freely given. It cannot be demanded as a tribute to power. And the obligation it creates is always reciprocal — the beneficiary owes acknowledgment and, where possible, return, but the benefactor owes the honesty to recognize that what was given was a gift, not a purchase of subordination. Gratitude, in Wollstonecraft's hands, becomes not a conservative virtue that reinforces hierarchy but a critical tool for exposing the false claims that hierarchy generates.
