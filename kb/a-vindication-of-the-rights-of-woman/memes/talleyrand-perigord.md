# Charles-Maurice de Talleyrand-Périgord

[[Charles-Maurice de Talleyrand-Périgord]] (1754–1838) is the dedicatee of _[[A Vindication of the Rights of Woman]]_ and, in a crucial sense, its immediate occasion. His 1791 _Rapport sur l'instruction publique_, presented to the [[National Assembly]] of revolutionary France, proposed a system of public education that excluded women from civic instruction — confining them instead to domestic education under parental supervision.

Wollstonecraft's Dedication is a direct challenge to Talleyrand's report. She addresses him with the respect due a fellow advocate of liberty, but her opening words cut to the contradiction at the heart of Revolutionary gender politics: a nation proclaiming universal rights while denying them to half its citizens.

## Life

Talleyrand was born into the French aristocracy in 1754 and, despite a childhood lameness that barred him from military service, rose to become Bishop of Autun before the Revolution. During the early Revolutionary period, he aligned himself with the reformers, proposing the nationalization of Church property and serving as president of the National Assembly. His 1791 report on public education was an expression of Revolutionary optimism — the conviction that a newly free nation must educate its citizens.

His subsequent career would make him one of the most famous diplomats in European history, serving under the Directory, Napoleon, and the restored Bourbon monarchy. Talleyrand's political survival across regimes made him both legendary and notorious — but in 1792, when Wollstonecraft wrote, he was still the Revolutionary bishop who might yet be persuaded.

## Role in the Text

### The Dedication

Wollstonecraft opens the Vindication with a Dedication addressed directly to Talleyrand, invoking the principles of the [[French Revolution]] even as she calls out its shortcomings. Her tone is respectful but pointed:

> "Sir — Having read with great pleasure a pamphlet which you have lately published, on the subject of National Education, I dedicate this volume to you." (P02-001)

The Dedication is a rhetorical masterstroke. By addressing a leading Revolutionary statesman, Wollstonecraft positions her argument within the central political debates of the age. She invokes the very principles Talleyrand claims to champion — liberty, equality, reason — and demands that they be applied consistently.

> "If woman be not prepared by education to become the companion of man, she will stop the progress of knowledge; for truth must be common to all." (P02-003)

This argument — that national progress depends on women's education — directly answers Talleyrand's report in its own terms. If public education is the foundation of civic virtue, then excluding women undermines the very project of national regeneration that Talleyrand claims to serve.

### The Rights Argument

Wollstonecraft does not merely ask Talleyrand to reconsider; she insists that the logic of rights admits no exception. Her Dedication establishes the argumentative framework for the entire Vindication: rights derived from reason cannot be sex-limited.

> "I plead for my sex — not for myself. I have long since resigned the hope of seeing a more rational system of education established for women. But the claims I make are on behalf of all women." (P02-005)

The Dedication does not name Talleyrand after its opening address, but his presence is implicit throughout. Every argument Wollstonecraft makes about women's capacity for reason, their fitness for citizenship, and the necessity of equal education is a reply to the premise of his report — that women's education need only prepare them for private domestic life.

## Key Ideas

| Issue | Talleyrand's Report | Wollstonecraft's Response |
|-------|-------------------|--------------------------|
| Women's education | Domestic, under parental supervision | Public, rational, equal to men's |
| Civic participation | For men only | Rights derive from reason, which has no sex |
| National progress | Requires educated male citizens | Requires educated citizens of both sexes |
| Women's role | Private, domestic, supportive | Public, rational, independent |
| The Revolution's promise | Universal in rhetoric, male in practice | Must be universal in both rhetoric and practice |

Talleyrand's report crystallized a contradiction that Wollstonecraft's Vindication was designed to expose: that the language of universal rights, proclaimed by revolutionaries, was in practice restricted to men. By dedicating her work to him, Wollstonecraft makes Talleyrand the representative of this contradiction — and by implication, the test case for whether Revolutionary principles can survive their own logic.
