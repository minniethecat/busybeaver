## Definition

In _A Vindication of the Rights of Woman_, imagination occupies an ambivalent position in Wollstonecraft's faculty psychology. It is not inherently suspect — she recognises that imagination is essential to sympathy, moral vision, and intellectual creativity. But she argues that imagination becomes dangerous when it is cultivated at the expense of reason, particularly in women's education, where novels, romantic expectations, and the "heating" of the imagination through sensibility training produce women incapable of sound judgment.

## In Context

Wollstonecraft's critique of imagination follows from her analysis of women's education. She observes that girls are systematically taught to live in their imaginations — through novels that depict unrealistic romantic scenarios, through accomplishments that prize aesthetic sensibility over understanding, and through social conventions that reward fantasy over reality. The result is a woman whose "imagination is kept alive at the expense of reason" [002-072], making her unfit for the genuine duties of life.

In Chapter III, Wollstonecraft turns this critique on men as well. She observes that even a man of intellect, "whose imagination had been allowed to run wild" [003-007], can be led into erroneous thinking when passion overpowers reason. The imagination, unchecked, leads both sexes astray — but women are systematically deprived of the rational training that would enable them to govern it.

Wollstonecraft does not propose eliminating imagination from education. Her target is the imbalance: "the imagination is heated" [004-065] while the understanding is starved. A properly educated person — of either sex — possesses a lively imagination governed by a strong reason. This is the ideal she holds up in contrast to the "overstretched sensibility" that characterises the women of her time.

## Key Passages

> "The imagination is kept alive at the expense of reason." [002-072]

> "A man, whose imagination had been allowed to run wild, and whose passions had assumed the reins, would think and act very differently from what the voice of reason, strengthened by experience, dictates." [003-007]

> "The imagination, however, is heated, and the fancy quickly runs over the scenes it has sketched." [004-065]
