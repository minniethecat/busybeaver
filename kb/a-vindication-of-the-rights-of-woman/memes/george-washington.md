1732 – 1799. Commander of the Continental Army during the American Revolution and first President of the United States. Wollstonecraft invokes Washington twice in _A Vindication of the Rights of Woman_ as a model of genuine modesty and republican citizenship — a standard against which she measures both male and female virtue.

## Life

George Washington was born in Westmoreland County, Virginia, in 1732. He served as a surveyor and militia officer in the French and Indian War before emerging as a leader of the colonial resistance to British rule. Appointed Commander-in-Chief of the Continental Army in 1775, he led the American forces through an eight-year war of independence. After victory, he resigned his military commission — an act widely celebrated in Europe as proof of his republican virtue — and later served as the first President of the United States (1789–1797).

By the time Wollstonecraft published _A Vindication_ in 1792, Washington was an international symbol of republican leadership: a citizen-soldier who wielded power without becoming corrupted by it. His reputation for modesty, self-discipline, and civic devotion made him a natural reference point for Enlightenment arguments about virtue.

## Role in the Text

Wollstonecraft references Washington in two strategic contexts.

In Chapter VII, "Modesty — Comprehensively Considered," she cites Washington as proof that modesty is compatible with masculine achievement and public action: "nor was General Washington when he accepted of the command of the American forces. The latter has always been characterized as a modest man" (007-002). This reference is crucial to Wollstonecraft's redefinition of modesty: if Washington — a military commander and head of state — can be called modest, then modesty cannot mean the self-effacing timidity prescribed for women. It must mean something closer to sober self-knowledge, the quality of knowing one's own worth without vanity.

In Chapter IX, she invokes Washington as a model of republican citizenship: "when a citizen fought for his country like a Fabricius or a Washington, and then returned to his farm to let his virtuous fervour run in a more placid, but not a less salutary stream" (009-010). Washington, alongside the Roman hero Fabricius, exemplifies the citizen who serves the republic without seeking personal power — a model Wollstonecraft implicitly extends to women, who should be educated as citizens rather than ornaments.

### Works Referenced

No specific Washington text is cited. Wollstonecraft draws on Washington's public reputation — disseminated in Europe through newspapers, pamphlets, and biographies — rather than his writings.

## Key Ideas

Washington's significance in Wollstonecraft's argument is exemplary rather than philosophical. He embodies a republican conception of virtue that is active, public, and compatible with modesty — precisely the combination Wollstonecraft argues should be available to women. By associating Washington with the Roman Fabricius, she places him in a classical tradition of civic virtue that predates and transcends the gendered restrictions of eighteenth-century conduct literature.

The Washington references also reflect the pro-American sympathies of British radicals in the 1790s. Wollstonecraft's circle — which included Richard Price, Joseph Priestley, and William Godwin — saw the American Revolution as a vindication of Enlightenment principles. Citing Washington aligned _A Vindication_ with that revolutionary tradition.
