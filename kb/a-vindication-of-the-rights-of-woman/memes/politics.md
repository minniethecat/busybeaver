Politics, in Wollstonecraft's argument, is not merely the science of government but the domain in which the principles of reason and liberty find their public expression — and from which the exclusion of women reveals a fundamental inconsistency in the Enlightenment project of political reform.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft approaches politics as both a descriptive and a normative category. Descriptively, she acknowledges that "the whole system of British politics, if system it may courteously be called" (009-011), is characterized by inconsistency and corruption. The exclusion of women from formal political participation is one manifestation of this broader failure of rational principle.

Normatively, she insists that "sound politics diffuse liberty" (002-086) — and that this diffusion must include women. The "science of politics is in its infancy" (002-085) precisely because it has not yet recognized that the principles of rights apply universally to all rational beings regardless of sex.

Wollstonecraft makes a distinctive move by linking domestic and political tyranny. The arbitrary power exercised by husbands within marriage and by fathers within the family is structurally continuous with the arbitrary power of despotic government. Reform in one sphere cannot succeed without reform in the other.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Politics enters Wollstonecraft's argument at several strategic points, each reinforcing her central claim that the condition of women is a political question, not merely a matter of private manners.

First, she establishes the epistemological connection between politics and the condition of women. The argument that "the science of politics is in its infancy" (002-085) is supported by the observation that women's rights have been systematically ignored. A mature political science would recognize that "as sound politics diffuse liberty, mankind, including woman, will become more wise and virtuous" (002-086).

Second, she identifies the educational remedy. In her proposal for national education, she envisions that young people "might also study politics, and settle their benevolence on the broadest basis" (009-031). Political education is not a luxury but a necessity for the formation of moral citizens. She further specifies that "the history of man, and politics, might also be taught by conversation" (012-050) in the common schools she proposes.

Third, she extends her analysis to the critique of existing political culture. Women are denied the education that would make them capable of political judgment, and this denial is then cited as evidence of their incapacity. "They find politics dry, because they have not acquired" (013-047) the habits of mind that political reasoning requires.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft positions herself against the mainstream of eighteenth-century British political thought, which treated women's exclusion from politics as natural and unproblematic. She implicitly challenges Edmund Burke's organic conception of political society by insisting that inherited arrangements must be judged by the standard of reason.

Her reference to "the whole system of British politics, if system it may courteously be called" (009-011) signals her alignment with the radical critique of British constitutional arrangements. The corruption she diagnoses in gender relations is not accidental to the political system but structurally embedded within it.

## Key Passages

> That the science of politics is in its infancy, is evident from the arbitrary distinctions which still subsist, and the gross ignorance which prevails respecting the rights of woman. (002-085)

> As sound politics diffuse liberty, mankind, including woman, will become more wise and virtuous. (002-086)

> They might also study politics, and settle their benevolence on the broadest basis. (009-031)

> The whole system of British politics, if system it may courteously be called, consists in multiplying dependents and contriving taxes which eat into the vitals of the state. (009-011)
