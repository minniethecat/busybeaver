# Émile, or On Education

## Overview

*Émile, or On Education* (1762) is Jean-Jacques Rousseau's major treatise on education. Structured as a narrative following the education of an imaginary pupil, Émile, from infancy to adulthood, the work presents Rousseau's philosophy of "negative education" — allowing the child to learn from nature and experience rather than from books and formal instruction. The first four books trace Émile's development; Book V, "Sophie, or the Woman," presents Rousseau's programme for the education of Émile's future wife.

It is Book V that makes *Émile* the primary target of Wollstonecraft's *Vindication*. Rousseau argues that Sophie should be educated to please and be useful to Émile — to be modest, charming, and dependent, with just enough reason to appreciate her husband's superior intellect. Wollstonecraft systematically dismantles this programme.

## In the Text

Wollstonecraft engages *Émile* primarily in Chapters I, II, and V. Her strategy is to accept Rousseau's own principles — that virtue must flow from the exercise of one's own reason, that dependence corrupts character — and then demonstrate that his treatment of Sophie violates those very principles. "This was Rousseau's opinion respecting men," she writes; "I extend it to women" (002-028).

### The Immanent Critique

Wollstonecraft's method in engaging *Émile* is a form of immanent critique: she grants Rousseau his premises and then shows that his conclusions about women do not follow from them. Rousseau argues that virtue requires the free exercise of one's own [[reason]]; Wollstonecraft agrees. Rousseau argues that dependence on others' judgment corrupts [[character]]; Wollstonecraft agrees. But where Rousseau concludes that women — being "naturally" dependent and intellectually inferior — should receive an education suited to their subordinate role, Wollstonecraft points out that this conclusion contradicts his own principles. If dependence corrupts, then educating women for dependence corrupts them. If virtue requires the exercise of one's own reason, then denying women the cultivation of reason denies them the possibility of virtue. "This was Rousseau's opinion respecting men," she writes; "I extend it to women" (002-028).

### The Sophie Problem

Book V of *Émile* presents Sophie as the ideal woman: modest, charming, skilled in domestic arts, and educated just enough to appreciate Émile's superior intellect without challenging it. Wollstonecraft finds this portrait "grossly unnatural" (002-039). Sophie is a creature designed entirely for male consumption — her education aims at making her pleasing rather than virtuous, dependent rather than independent. Wollstonecraft's critique centres on the psychological consequences: a Sophie educated according to Rousseau's principles would be incapable of genuine [[friendship]], rational [[love|affection]], or moral [[judgment]]. She would be, in Wollstonecraft's devastating phrase, a "convenient slave" dressed in the language of compliment.

## Bibliographic Information

*Émile, ou De l'éducation*. Published 1762 in French. Wollstonecraft read it in the original French or in one of the many English translations circulating in the 1780s. The work was condemned by the Parliament of Paris and publicly burned in 1762; Rousseau fled France to avoid arrest. Its influence on educational theory, however, proved enduring — it inspired Pestalozzi, Froebel, and the progressive education movement of the nineteenth century, even as its gendered programme for Sophie's education drew sustained criticism from Wollstonecraft and later feminist thinkers.
