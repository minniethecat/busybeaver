# Domestic Affections

The emotional bonds of family and household life that Wollstonecraft identifies as the first opening of the human heart to moral feeling and the necessary foundation of civic virtue. Unlike natural affection, which is instinctive, domestic affections must be deliberately cultivated through the structure of education and home life.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft defines domestic affections as the ties of feeling that bind members of a household — between spouses, between parents and children, and among siblings. These affections are not merely private sentiments; they have a public function. She argues that "the domestic affections, that first open the heart to the various modifications of humanity" (012-008) are the seedbed of broader social virtue. The capacity to feel for one's family is the template for feeling for one's community and, ultimately, for humanity.

Yet domestic affections do not flourish automatically. The same social arrangements that Wollstonecraft criticizes throughout the *Vindication* — fashionable education, the separation of children from parents, the reduction of marriage to economic transaction — all work against the cultivation of genuine domestic feeling. The health of domestic affections is, in her analysis, a reliable index of the health of the society that surrounds them. Where domestic affections are weak or absent, she traces the defect to corrupting social institutions rather than to individual moral failure.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument for domestic affections cuts against the grain of both contemporary convention and radical critique. She insists that the domestic sphere matters — not as a place of feminine confinement, but as the foundation of moral education for both sexes. Her criticism of existing schools is precisely that they undermine domestic feeling: "boys become gluttons and slovens, and, instead of cultivating domestic affections, very early rush into the libertinism which destroys the constitution before it is formed; hardening the heart as it weakens the understanding" (012-005).

The solution she proposes is not the abolition of domestic life but its reform through a system of combined public and private education. Children should live at home while attending day schools, so that "the domestic affections, that first open the heart to the various modifications of humanity would be cultivated" (012-008) alongside formal instruction. The school teaches the mind; the home teaches the heart. Neither alone is sufficient, and the separation of the two — children sent away to boarding schools, or kept at home without education — produces either cold rationalists or sentimental weaklings.

Wollstonecraft is particularly attentive to the economic conditions that undermine domestic affections. When wealth and status become the primary motives for marriage, the emotional bonds that sustain family life atrophy. She describes how "to preserve their beauty, and wear the flowery crown of the day" (009-006), fashionable women spurn the natural nurturance of children — breastfeeding, daily presence, emotional availability — that cements domestic bonds. The critique is structural, not personal: a society organized around display will produce mothers who display rather than nurture.

The vision she offers in response is of domestic life grounded in sincerity rather than performance. "Would not this simple elegance of sincerity be a chaste homage paid to domestic affections, far surpassing the meretricious compliments that shine with false lustre in the heartless intercourse of fashionable life?" (012-036). The metaphor is revealing: domestic affections, rightly cultivated, are a form of worship — a "chaste homage" that replaces the false worship of wealth, status, and sensual gratification.

Her recommendation that "vacations, which do not foster domestic affections, continually disturb the course of study" (012-025) shows the practical weight she gives to the concept. Educational reform is not just about what happens in the classroom; it is about the entire rhythm of a child's life, the integration of learning and love, reason and affection. Domestic affections are not an ornament to education — they are one of its primary objects.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Jean-Jacques Rousseau had, in *Émile*, celebrated domestic sentiment but confined its cultivation to women, whom he tasked with creating an emotional haven for men returning from the rigors of public life. Wollstonecraft radicalizes Rousseau's insight: domestic affections are not a gendered specialty but a universal human capacity that education must cultivate in both sexes. Men who are raised without domestic feeling become "libertines" and "gluttons" — emotionally and morally stunted, however well they may reason.

The contrast with John Gregory is equally instructive. Gregory's *A Father's Legacy to His Daughters* had advised women to cultivate domestic virtues as a strategy for attracting and keeping a husband. Wollstonecraft finds this instrumental approach degrading. Domestic affections, in her account, are not a means to matrimonial security — they are an end in themselves, constitutive of a well-lived human life.

## Key Passages

> At school, boys become gluttons and slovens, and, instead of cultivating domestic affections, very early rush into the libertinism which destroys the constitution before it is formed; hardening the heart as it weakens the understanding. (012-005)

> Thus to make men citizens, two natural steps might be taken, which seem directly to lead to the desired point; for the domestic affections, that first open the heart to the various modifications of humanity would be cultivated, whilst the children were nevertheless allowed to spend great part of their time, on equal terms, with other children. (012-008)

> The vacations, which do not foster domestic affections, continually disturb the course of study, and render any plan of improvement abortive which includes temperance; still, were they abolished, children would be entirely separated from their parents. (012-025)

> Would not this simple elegance of sincerity be a chaste homage paid to domestic affections, far surpassing the meretricious compliments that shine with false lustre in the heartless intercourse of fashionable life? (012-036)
