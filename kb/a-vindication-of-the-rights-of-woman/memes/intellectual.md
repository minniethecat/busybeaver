# Intellectual

Wollstonecraft deploys "intellectual" throughout the *Vindication* as both a descriptive term for the rational faculties and a critical tool to expose how women are systematically denied their cultivation. She argues that the mind's intellectual powers are not sex-specific but universal, and that women's apparent intellectual inferiority is the product of education, not nature.

## Definition

In Wollstonecraft's usage, "intellectual" refers to the rational and cognitive capacities of the human mind — the power to form principles, pursue knowledge, and exercise judgment independently. She contrasts intellectual endeavour with the mere cultivation of sensibility, which she saw as the prescribed and limiting domain of women. The intellectual faculty is what elevates humans above mere sensation (001-008), and its exercise through "intellectual pursuits" is essential to moral character and genuine virtue (002-029). Wollstonecraft insists that women's position "in the intellectual scale" cannot be fairly assessed until they are granted the same education and freedom to develop their faculties as men (002-073).

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's central claim is that intellectual capacity is a human universal, not a male prerogative. She systematically dismantles the prevailing assumption that women are naturally inferior in intellect by arguing that any observed difference is the consequence of education and social conditioning rather than innate deficiency. She observes that the "intellectual world is shut against" women, who are "told from their infancy, and taught by the example of their mothers, that a little knowledge of human weakness, justly termed cunning, softness of temper, outward obedience, and a scrupulous attention to a puerile kind of propriety, will obtain for them the protection of man" (004-058).

She further argues that even sublime emotional experience can be enriched by intellectual development: "sublime emotions by displaying intellectual beauty, may be overlooked or observed with indifference" by those whose minds remain uncultivated (003-040). The alternative to intellectual cultivation is not innocence but a degraded form of character — women become "only anxious to inspire love, when they ought to cherish a nobler ambition, and by their abilities and virtues exact respect" (004-048).

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Wollstonecraft directly challenges Rousseau's assertion that women's education should be oriented entirely toward pleasing men. Where Rousseau advocates cultivating sensibility and modesty in women, Wollstonecraft insists that "the most serious intellectual pursuit" yields emotional rewards that surpass those of frivolous accomplishments (004-048). She also takes aim at the broader cultural assumption — exemplified by Dr. Gregory's advice to his daughters — that women should conceal their intellectual abilities to avoid intimidating men. For Wollstonecraft, this "intellectual cowardice" (001-008) is not a feminine virtue but a moral failing that pervades both sexes.

## Key Passages

> The mind must be strong that resolutely forms its own principles; for a kind of intellectual cowardice prevails which makes many men shrink from the task, or only do it by halves. (001-008)

> Let their faculties have room to unfold, and their virtues to gain strength, and then determine where the whole sex must stand in the intellectual scale. (002-073)

> The intellectual world is shut against them; take them out of their sphere of pleasure, and they have not sufficient strength of mind to enjoy the contemplation of a rational prospect. (004-058)

> [I]s not the mind low, indeed, in the intellectual scale, that can continue to admire trick, or to be pleased with the unmeaning grimaces of a buffoon? (012-082)
