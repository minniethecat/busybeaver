# Independence

The foundational principle of Wollstonecraft's moral philosophy: the freedom from external control that alone makes genuine virtue and reason possible.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft declares independence the "basis of every virtue" (P02-002), a statement that anchors her entire argument. For her, independence is not merely political or economic autonomy but a quality of mind — "independence of mind" (005-058) and "independence of character" (003-059) that enables genuine moral judgment. She defines it in practical terms: "the true definition of independence" involves bearing hardship without becoming servile (005-028).

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

The author contends that women's systematic dependence — economic, intellectual, and emotional — is the root cause of their degraded moral condition. "Educated in slavish dependence, and enervated by luxury and sloth" (003-027), women cannot develop the strength of character necessary for virtue. Dependence is a self-perpetuating cycle: "dependence of body naturally produces dependence of mind" (003-021).

Wollstonecraft traces this dependence to women's education and social position. "If women are educated for dependence, that is, to act according to the will of another" (003-042), they will never develop independent judgment. She contrasts the "submissive demeanour of dependence" with the "simple dignity" of an independent mind (002-067). True independence means that women should "act with consonant independence and dignity" rather than seeking male approval (005-075).

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Where contemporary conduct books celebrated women's "amiable weakness" and "pleasing dependence," Wollstonecraft sees only degradation. Rousseau's Sophie, trained to depend on Émile's reason, embodies everything Wollstonecraft opposes. She insists that women must be given "the first lesson of independence" so they may "practically be taught to think and act for themselves," developing the same independence of judgment that men claim as their birthright.
