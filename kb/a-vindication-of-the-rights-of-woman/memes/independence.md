## Definition

### Reason as the Foundation

Independence, for Wollstonecraft, is not merely a political or economic condition but the essential foundation of [[character|moral character]]. She defines it explicitly: "there is no other foundation for independence of character; I mean explicitly to say, that they must only bow to the authority of reason" (003-050), linking independence directly to the rational faculty. This formulation is significant — she rejects independence as mere wilfulness or rebellion and instead anchors it in the discipline of [[reason]]. True independence is the capacity to act according to one's own [[judgment|rational judgment]] rather than being governed by external opinion, [[passion|emotion]], or the will of others.

The epistemological dimension of independence is critical: "it is the right use of reason alone which makes us independent of everything — excepting the unclouded Reason — 'Whose service is perfect freedom'" (006-021). Wollstonecraft draws here on a Christian-Platonic tradition that identifies freedom not with the absence of constraint but with alignment to rational and divine order. A related dimension is what Wollstonecraft calls "independence of mind" (010-008), which she identifies as a quality few women possess because their [[education]] trains them to defer to male authority rather than to think for themselves.

### The Material Dimension

Wollstonecraft also insists on the economic and bodily dimensions of independence. She argues that "were it not for mistaken notions of beauty, women would acquire sufficient to enable them to earn their own subsistence, the true definition of independence" (005-034), making physical strength and earning capacity constitutive of genuine autonomy. This is not a secondary concern — in her view, economic dependence on men is the structural mechanism that keeps women from developing virtuous [[character]]. "It is vain to expect virtue from women till they are, in some degree, independent of men" (009-005), she declares, directly linking moral [[virtue]] to material self-sufficiency. Without independence, women become "cunning, mean, and selfish" (009-005), their apparent affection reduced to the "fawning fondness, of spaniel-like affection" that dependence breeds.

She elaborates the positive vision: "The being who discharges the duties of its station, is independent; and, speaking of women at large, their first duty is to themselves as rational creatures" (009-018). This assertion radicalizes conventional duty ethics by making self-development into a rational creature the primary obligation — prior even to the duties of a mother.

## In Context

### Rousseau and the Denial of Independence

The concept of independence appears across multiple chapters, each emphasizing a different facet. In Chapter II, Wollstonecraft directly confronts Rousseau who "declares, that a woman should never, for a moment feel herself independent" (002-041). She recognizes this as the central obstacle to women's moral development: if women are "never to be independent, never to rise above opinion, or to feel the dignity of a rational will that only bows to God" (002-086), then they are bound by what she calls an "adamantine chain of destiny" — not a natural law but a social prescription disguised as one.

In Chapter III, Wollstonecraft argues for "independence of character" grounded in [[reason]] as the alternative to the dependent, pleasing disposition cultivated in women (003-050). She critiques the physical constraints that produce dependency from infancy: "the limbs and faculties are cramped with worse than Chinese bands, and the sedentary life which they are condemned to live, whilst boys frolic in the open air, weakens the muscles and relaxes the nerves" (003-013). Independence, she insists, must be physically cultivated as well as intellectually.

### The Practical Horizon

In Chapter V, Wollstonecraft critiques male authors who "check" women's "natural independence and dignity" (005-090) through restrictive [[education]], and she notes that "true grace arises from some kind of independence of mind" (005-072) — a direct inversion of the conduct-book assumption that grace requires feminine submission. The most practical application appears in Chapter IX, where Wollstonecraft provocatively suggests that women should pursue "extensive plans of usefulness and independence" (009-023) — a direct challenge to the domestic confinement expected of her sex. She specifies that a just government must provide "for honest, independent women, by encouraging them to fill respectable stations" (009-030), recognizing that independence requires not merely individual virtue but institutional support and civil existence.

In Chapter XII, she extends the critique to the institutions that shape character, asking of tutors: "can they be expected to inspire independent sentiments, whose conduct must be regulated by the cautious prudence that is ever on the watch for preferment?" (012-018). In Chapter XIII, she returns to the theme with a call for women's [[education]] to begin with "the first lesson of independence" (013-071), reinforcing that independence must be taught and cultivated from childhood.

## Key Passages

> "...there is no other foundation for independence of character; I mean explicitly to say, that they must only bow to the authority of reason, instead of being the *modest* slaves of opinion." (003-050)

> "It is vain to expect virtue from women till they are, in some degree, independent of men." (009-005)

> "...were it not for mistaken notions of beauty, women would acquire sufficient to enable them to earn their own subsistence, the true definition of independence." (005-034)

> "Rousseau declares, that a woman should never, for a moment feel herself independent." (002-041)

> "Grace arises from some kind of independence of mind." (005-072)

> "...for it is the right use of reason alone which makes us independent of everything — excepting the unclouded Reason." (006-021)

> "...women might certainly study the art of healing, and be physicians as well as nurses. And midwifery, decency seems to allot to them... they might, also, study politics, and settle their benevolence on the broadest basis... Business of various kinds, they might likewise pursue." (009-023)

> "The being who discharges the duties of its station, is independent; and, speaking of women at large, their first duty is to themselves as rational creatures." (009-018)

> "...a man must have sense, and that independence of mind which few women possess who are taught to depend entirely on their husbands." (010-008)

> "...as the first lesson of independence, they should practically be taught to exert their own understanding." (013-071)
