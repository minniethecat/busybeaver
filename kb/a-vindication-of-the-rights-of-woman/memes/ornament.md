# Ornament

In Wollstonecraft's critique, "ornament" is a central metaphor for the degradation of women. To treat woman as "the pride and ornament of creation" (P01-013) is not to honor her but to "degrade" her — reducing a rational being to a decorative object. Throughout the *Vindication*, ornament and its cognates (ornamental, ornaments) signify the systematic reduction of women's value to appearance, surface, and display, at the expense of their intellectual and moral development.

## Definition

Wollstonecraft uses "ornament" to describe both the literal trappings of fashion — "mirrors, trinkets, and all the tribe of ornaments" (005-008) — and, more significantly, the metaphorical condition of women as decorative accessories to men's lives. The "strong inclination for external ornaments" (013-044) that she observes in women is not innate but taught: women are educated to value themselves only as objects of visual appreciation. The term operates as a critique of a society that substitutes surface decoration for substance, and appearance for virtue.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft's argument about ornament unfolds through a series of linked claims. First, she identifies the ornamental ideal itself as degrading: "to consider woman as the pride, and ornament of creation, degrades her to a mere toy" (P01-013). The language of "pride" and "ornament" — ostensibly complimentary — masks a fundamental denial of women's rational nature.

Second, she traces the mechanism by which women internalize this valuation. Fashionable society teaches women to invest their identity in "clothes, necessary or ornamental" (004-096), and "are continually talking of the elegance of their dress" — conversations that substitute for any engagement with ideas. The woman who "considers her person as the chief object of attention" becomes incapable of considering anything else.

Third, Wollstonecraft connects ornament to broader civilizational decline. "A strong inclination for external ornaments ever appears in barbarous states" (013-044) — the more a society values ornament, the less it values rational development. This point inverts the self-congratulation of "polished" European society: the obsession with ornament is not a sign of refinement but of barbarism.

Finally, she offers a qualified alternative. While acknowledging that "I know how many ornaments to human nature have been engrafted by civilization" (013-077), she distinguishes between genuine cultural achievements and the superficial ornamentation that masks intellectual emptiness. The proper ambition is not to be an ornament to society but to contribute substantively to it.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Edmund Burke's aesthetic philosophy had celebrated ornament and beauty as civilizing forces. Wollstonecraft implicitly challenges this tradition, arguing that ornament — particularly when imposed on women as their primary value — is not civilization but its parody. Rousseau, despite his critique of luxury, continued to value women primarily for their ornamental and pleasing qualities. Wollstonecraft's insistence that external ornament signals barbarism is a direct reversal of the prevailing association between ornament and refinement.

## Key Passages

> "Dismissing, then, those pretty feminine phrases, which the men condescendingly use to soften our slavish dependence, and to consider woman as the pride, and ornament of creation, degrades her to a mere toy — a pretty bauble, to be viewed, to be played with, and to be broken." (P01-013, paraphrased)

> "But, what have women to do in society? I am sure, if they have any duty to perform, they have some serious employment, and not merely to spend their time in adorning their persons. They, of course, must be more rationally educated, or their knowledge will only be a more specious kind of ignorance." (004-005)

> "Yet the youth, the gaiety, the beauty of women are perpetually talked of — their outward graces are the theme of every pen — but very few think of the human heart that beats within. And what is woman? A fair defect of nature — an angel in feature — but, why need I add, something less than man? An ornament to society!" (004-005)

> "outwardly ornamented with elaborate care, and so dazzling to the eye, that it conceals the want of every internal grace" (004-005)

> "A strong inclination for external ornaments ever appears in barbarous states, only the men not the women, adorn themselves there — for wherever women are allowed to be on an equality with men, they are modestly and rationally dressed." (013-044)
