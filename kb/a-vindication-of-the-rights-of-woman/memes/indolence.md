A state of habitual mental passivity and resistance to exertion that Wollstonecraft identifies as one of the most destructive consequences of women's confined education. More than mere laziness, indolence represents the internalization of a social order that discourages female intellectual effort—"a vicious indolence and inactivity, which we falsely call delicacy" (003-011).

## Definition

Wollstonecraft distinguishes indolence from simple idleness by its active dimension: it is a cultivated refusal to exert the mind, taught to women under the name of delicacy and refinement. "We encourage a vicious indolence and inactivity," she writes, "which we falsely term delicacy" (003-011). This indolence is not natural to women but is systematically produced by an education that prizes bodily ease over mental vigor. The body is pampered while the mind is starved, creating what she calls "the hot-bed of luxurious indolence" (004-071)—a metaphor that emphasizes artificial cultivation.

The concept carries both a physical and mental dimension. Physically, women are denied the exercise and activity that strengthen the body and, by extension, the mind. "To preserve personal beauty, woman's glory," she observes, "the limbs and faculties are cramped with worse than Chinese bands" (004-071). Mentally, indolence manifests as "a benumbing indolence of mind, which he has seldom afterwards resolution to shake off" (012-002)—a description applied to boys in boarding schools but relevant to her broader argument about how soft education ruins character.

## Wollstonecraft's Argument

Wollstonecraft connects indolence to her broader thesis about women's degradation. The cult of feminine delicacy, far from protecting women, enfeebles them. Women raised in luxurious indolence become "the prey of their senses" (008-026) because they have never been trained to exert reason or self-discipline. "The shameful indolence of many married women, and others a little advanced in life, is too frequently an insuperable bar to improvement" (008-026)—indolence once established becomes self-perpetuating.

The remedy is education that demands active exertion. She advocates for physical exercise and mental discipline as mutually reinforcing: both train the capacity for sustained effort that virtue requires. Her educational proposals consistently pair bodily activity with intellectual work, arguing that women must be "roused from the lethargy" that indolence produces. Parents who favor boarding schools "only to favour the indolence of parents" (011-016) perpetuate the cycle, preferring to outsource their children's education rather than undertake the demanding work of character formation.

### Contrast with Contemporary Views

Eighteenth-century conduct literature treated female delicacy as a virtue—proof of refinement and class status. Wollstonecraft inverts this valuation: delicacy is indolence in disguise, a mark of degradation rather than elevation. Rousseau, whom she engages throughout, celebrates Sophie's "natural modesty" and aversion to strenuous thought; Wollstonecraft sees the same traits as symptoms of a corrupt education. Where traditional moralists prescribed leisure as a sign of gentility, Wollstonecraft demands industry as the foundation of virtue.

## Key Passages

> We encourage a vicious indolence and inactivity, which we falsely term delicacy; instead of hardening their minds by the severer principles of reason and philosophy, we breed them to useless arts, which terminate in vanity and sensuality. (003-011)

> To preserve personal beauty, woman's glory, the limbs and faculties are cramped with worse than Chinese bands, and the sedentary life which they are condemned to live, whilst boys frolic in the open air, weakens the muscles and relaxes the nerves. (004-071)

> The shameful indolence of many married women, and others a little advanced in life, is too frequently an insuperable bar to improvement; and the mind, uncultivated, preys on itself. (008-026)
