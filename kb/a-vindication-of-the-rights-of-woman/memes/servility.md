# Servility

Wollstonecraft's use of "servility" in _A Vindication_ extends a political concept into the psychological and aesthetic realms. Servility is not merely the condition of being a servant; it is the internal corruption produced by subordination — a deformation of character that mimics the external posture even in voluntary action.

## Political Servility

The political analogy is drawn explicitly in Chapter 2. Women, stripped of the virtues appropriate to humanity, have been "decked with artificial graces, that enable them to exercise a short lived tyranny." Their ambition is merely to please, "and this ignoble desire, like the servility in absolute monarchies, destroys all strength of character" (002-083). The parallel is precise: just as absolute monarchy produces subjects incapable of independent political action, the sexual system produces women incapable of moral independence. "Liberty is the mother of virtue" — and servility, its opposite, is the mother of vice.

## Marital Servility

The domestic form of servility appears in Chapter 3. Wollstonecraft considers the case of a woman "trained up to obedience" and married to a sensible man "who directs her judgment, without making her feel the servility of her subjection" (003-044). The qualifier is crucial: even when subjection is gentle, it remains subjection. The woman who reasons "by this reflected light" — at second hand — is morally fragile, incapable of standing alone should her husband die.

The passage exposes the benevolent patriarch as still a patriarch: no amount of kindness transforms command into equality.

## Aesthetic Servility

In Chapter 5, Wollstonecraft extends the critique to taste. She dismisses "the studied attitudes of some modern prints, copied with tasteless servility after the antiques; the soul is left out, and none of the parts are tied together by what may properly be termed character" (005-085). Aesthetic servility — the unthinking imitation of classical models — is homologized with moral servility: both substitute external form for internal substance, both lack what Wollstonecraft calls "character."

## The Psychology of Subjection

Across these three domains, servility describes the same phenomenon: the internalized posture of the subordinate. Whether the sovereign is a monarch, a husband, or a classical model, the servile mind ceases to exercise its own judgment. For Wollstonecraft, this is the deepest injury that systems of subordination inflict — not material deprivation, but the destruction of the capacity for moral independence.
