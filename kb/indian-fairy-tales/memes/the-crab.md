## Overview

The Crab is the sole survivor and avenger in "[[the-cruel-crane|The Cruel Crane]] Outwitted" (Chapter 7), a Jataka tale in which a deceitful crane devours all the fish in a pond by promising to transport them to a larger lake. When the crane tries to eat the Crab as well, the Crab's wariness and powerful grip allow him to turn the tables: he seizes the crane's neck, forces it to return him to the pond, and then kills the crane (007-019–007-034). The Crab thus represents the archetype of the small but clever creature who defeats a larger, more powerful adversary through wit and caution.

This tale is of ancient Buddhist origin — it appears as the Baka-Jātaka (No. 38) and spread through the Bidpai literature into numerous European traditions (030-045).

## Role in the Tale

### The Crane's Deception

After the crane has eaten all the fish in the shrinking pond by pretending to carry them to a beautiful lake, only the Crab remains. The crane calls out to him with the same false promise (007-019–007-020). Unlike the trusting fish, however, the Crab is immediately suspicious:

> "But how will you take hold of me to carry me along?" (007-021)

When the crane offers to grip him with his beak, the Crab refuses, saying he would slip off and fall. The crane, growing impatient, promises to hold tight — but the Crab has his own plan (007-023–007-025).

### The Crab's Stratagem

The Crab proposes an alternative: rather than the crane holding the Crab, the Crab will hold the crane — by the neck, with his claws (007-026):

> "If you let me catch hold of you round the neck with my claws, I shall be glad to go with you." (007-026)

The crane, eager to eat the last remaining creature, agrees without realizing the danger. The Crab catches hold of the crane's neck with his claws "as securely as with a pair of blacksmith's pincers" (007-027).

### Discovery and Vengeance

As they fly, the crane veers away from the pond toward the Varana-tree where he has devoured all the fish. The Crab immediately protests (007-029):

> "Uncle! The pond lies that way, but you are taking me this way!" (007-029)

The crane reveals his true intent: "Just as I have eaten those fish, every one of them, just so I will devour you as well!" (007-030). The Crab responds by tightening his grip like a vice (007-031). In pain, the crane begs for his life and agrees to deliver the Crab to the pond (007-032–007-033).

Once safely at the pond's edge, the Crab does not release the crane — instead, he "cut through its neck as clean as one would cut a lotus-stalk with a hunting-knife" before entering the water (007-034). The Genius of the Varana-tree applauds the Crab's deed, proclaiming that cleverness without virtue leads to destruction (007-035–007-036).

## Key Deeds

- **Caution**: Unlike the fish who trusted the crane blindly, the Crab questions the crane's method of transport and refuses to be carried by the beak, recognizing the danger (007-021–007-023).
- **Cunning Reversal**: Instead of letting the crane hold him, the Crab proposes the reverse — that he hold the crane by the neck — turning the predator's own plan against him (007-026).
- **Execution**: When the crane reveals his treachery, the Crab tightens his grip with crushing force, compelling the crane to deliver him safely to the pond (007-029–007-033).
- **Retributive Justice**: The Crab kills the crane by severing its neck, exacting justice for all the fish the crane devoured and ensuring no more creatures fall victim to his deceit (007-034).

## Relationships

- **[[the-crane|The Crane]]** (the-crane) — The antagonist: a deceitful bird who devours all the fish in the pond. The Crab outwits him by using his own predatory nature against him, ultimately killing him (007-019–007-034).
- **[[the-fish|The Fish]]** — The crane's previous victims, who trusted the crane's false promises and were eaten one by one. The Crab avenges their deaths (007-017–007-018).
- **The Genius of the Varana-tree** — A supernatural witness who applauds the Crab's cleverness and moral victory, uttering a verse about the fate of the clever but unrighteous (007-035–007-036).

## Cultural Significance

The Crab embodies a central theme of the Jataka tales: intelligence and caution triumph over brute force and deceit. The crab, a small and seemingly vulnerable creature, defeats the large predatory crane not through strength but through wariness and strategic reversal — he turns the crane's method (gripping) against him. This story is one of the most widely travelled in world folklore: originating in the Buddhist Baka-Jātaka, it entered the Bidpai (Panchatantra) collection and spread through Persian, Arabic, Hebrew, and Latin translations into medieval European literature, including the earliest English translation by Sir Thomas North (030-045). The Grimm Brothers' tale "Doctor Know-All" even preserves a distant echo of the crab motif (030-057). The Genius of the Tree's closing verse — praising the Crab's justice while deploring the crane's clever villainy — reinforces the Jataka's moral lesson that cleverness must be paired with righteousness.
