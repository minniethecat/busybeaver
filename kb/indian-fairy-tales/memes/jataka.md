# Jataka Tales

## Definition

The Jatakas (Sanskrit: *Jātaka*; Pali: *Jātaka-aṭṭhakathā*) are a body of approximately 547 birth-stories recounting the previous lives of Gautama Buddha, in both human and animal form. They constitute one of the oldest and most extensive collections of folklore in the world, forming Book 10 of the *Khuddaka Nikāya* in the Pali Buddhist canon. In Joseph Jacobs' *Indian Fairy Tales* (1892), the Jatakas provide the narrative core from which many of the collected tales derive.

## Examples in the Tales

The Jataka framework explicitly underlies several tales in this collection:

- **Preface acknowledgment**: Jacobs notes that some of the tales "were utilised in the Jatakas, or Birth-stories of Buddha" (P01-004), and that his collection draws "from the Jatakas, from the Bidpai, and from the various other sources that have come down to us" (P01-007), establishing the Jataka corpus as a primary source.

- **Notes and References**: The scholarly apparatus of the collection devotes extensive attention to the Jataka provenance. Jacobs explains that "a corpus of the Jatakas, or Birth-Tales of the Buddha" was gradually compiled (030-002), and that "these Jatakas, as we now have them, are en[[the-temple|shrine]]d in the Pali canon" (030-003). He further observes that "the vast bulk of the Jatakas does not contain a single example of a story properly so-called which cannot be paralleled in European folklore" (030-017), highlighting their universal appeal.

- **Moral framing**: Each Jataka is structured as a teaching device. As Jacobs notes, the Buddha "summed up the Jataka thus: 'At that time, the Lion was Devadatta, and the Crane was I myself'" (030-004), illustrating how the tales map characters onto the Buddhist moral universe with the Bodhisatta as the virtuous protagonist.

## Cultural Context

The Jatakas are "probably the oldest collection of folk-tales in the world" (030-011), predating both the *Panchatantra* and Aesop's fables. Their diffusion from India followed multiple routes — through the *Bidpai* literature into Persian and Arabic, then into European medieval romance; and through Buddhist missionaries into Southeast Asia, where Jataka scenes adorn temple walls from Borobudur to Angkor Wat.

The Jataka form — a prose narrative framing a verse moral — influenced the structure of folk tale collections across Eurasia. Jacobs notes "the similarity of the Jataka form with that familiar to us in *The Arabian Nights*" (030-025), tracing a direct lineage from Buddhist didactic literature to the world's most famous story collection. Some Jatakas "bear a remarkable resemblance to some of the most famous Fables of Aesop" (030-005), suggesting either common Indo-European origins or westward transmission along trade routes.
