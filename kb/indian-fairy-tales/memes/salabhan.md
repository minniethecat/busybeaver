## Overview

Raja Salabhan is a great king who rules in [[raja-rasalu|Chapter 19]]. He and his Queen [[lona]] long for a child, and when a son is finally promised, a [[fate-and-prophecy|prophecy]] warns them that looking upon the child's face before twelve years will bring death. Salabhan obeys the [[fate-and-prophecy|prophecy]], hiding his son [[rasalu|Raja Rasalu]] in an underground palace — a decision that shapes the entire tale.

## Role in the Tale

Salabhan's role is defined by his obedience to fate. When three [[the-jogi|Jogis]] prophesy that he will die if he sees his son before twelve years, he immediately orders the infant hidden away (019-002). For eleven years he does not look upon the boy. When the young [[rasalu|Prince Rasalu]] emerges early and comes to the city, breaking the women's water-pitchers, Salabhan immediately recognises his son (019-009):

> Now, as soon as Rajah Salabhan heard this, he guessed at once that it was Prince Rasalu come forth before the time, and, mindful of the Jogis' words that he would die if he looked on his son's face before twelve years were past, he did not dare to send his guards to seize the offender and bring him to be judged. (019-009)

Rather than confront his son, Salabhan pays the women for their broken pitchers and gives them vessels of iron and brass (019-009). Even when [[rasalu|Rasalu]] strides into the audience hall, Salabhan turns his back and says nothing (019-011):

> …but Raja Salabhan, in fear of his life, turned his back hastily and said never a word in reply. (019-011)

## Key Deeds

- **Rules as a great [[king|Raja]]** alongside [[lona|Queen Lona]] (019-001)
- **Receives the [[fate-and-prophecy|prophecy]]** from three Jogis and accepts its terms (019-002)
- **Hides the infant [[rasalu|Rasalu]]** in an underground palace with nurses, servants, and a colt (019-003)
- **Recognises his son immediately** when reports reach him of a prince breaking pitchers at the well (019-009)
- **Refuses to look upon [[rasalu|Rasalu]]'s face**, even when the prince enters his hall and salutes him (019-011)

## Relationships

- **[[lona|Queen Lona]]** — his wife, who shares his longing for a child and his obedience to the [[fate-and-prophecy|prophecy]]
- **[[rasalu|Raja Rasalu]]** — his son, whom he never sees face-to-face until after the twelve years, by which point [[rasalu|Rasalu]] has already left the palace
- **[[the-jogi|The Jogis]]** — the three wandering ascetics who deliver the [[fate-and-prophecy|prophecy]] that governs Salabhan's actions

## Moral and Cultural Significance

Salabhan embodies the folktale motif of **the [[fate-and-prophecy|prophecy]]-bound king** — a ruler who must choose between paternal love and obedience to fate. His willingness to sacrifice seeing his own son for twelve years reflects the tale's deeper theme: that fate (and the words of holy men) cannot be evaded. The image of a king turning his back on his own son in the audience hall is one of the tale's most poignant moments, showing the cost of [[fate-and-prophecy|prophecy]] on human relationships.
