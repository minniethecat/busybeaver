# Bengal

## Location

Bengal is the great eastern region of the Indian subcontinent. In this collection it appears within the editorial apparatus as both a source-region and a comparative reference-point, chiefly through the Rev. Lal Behari Day's *Folk-Tales of Bengal* (030-035, 030-074). Its people are noted directly: among a household described in the notes, the servants were "all of them Bengalese" (030-013). The region is treated as one of the eastern poles of the collection's geographic range, set against the Deccan and the northwestern territories when the editor traces "the tale from East to West" (030-036).

## Appearances in Tales

- [[notes-and-references]] — Bengal recurs as a documented source and comparison. The notes describe servants who were "all of them Bengalese" (030-013). A tale is traced across regions "in the Deccan ('[[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]]'); in Bengal (Day, *Folk-Tales of Bengal*)" (030-035). The editor follows "the tale from East to West," observing that "in Bengal we have the soul 'in a necklace'" as a local variant of the external-soul motif (030-036). Day's *Folk-Tales of Bengal* is again cited as a source among the references for a tale (030-074).

## Significance

Bengal's role in the collection is comparative and bibliographic, illuminating how a single story could take different shapes in different regions. In tracing the external-soul motif, the editor contrasts the Bengali version — in which the hidden soul lies "in a necklace" — with forms found elsewhere, using Bengal as one endpoint of the tale's journey "from East to West" (030-036). Lal Behari Day's *Folk-Tales of Bengal* stands among the key nineteenth-century collections on which the editor draws for parallels and sources (030-035, 030-074). Bengal thus helps define the eastern edge of the subcontinental story-map that the collection's notes survey, complementing the northern sources such as [[kashmir]] and the Panjab (030-013).

## Cultural or Historical Notes

Bengal, in the northeast of the subcontinent along the Ganges delta, has one of South Asia's richest literary and folk traditions. The Rev. Lal Behari Day's *Folk-Tales of Bengal* (1883) — the volume repeatedly cited in this collection's notes — was among the earliest and most influential English-language gatherings of Bengali oral stories, and it remained a standard reference for later folklorists. Bengal's prominence in the comparative notes reflects both the depth of its own storytelling and the pioneering documentary work of Victorian-era collectors who recorded its tales.
