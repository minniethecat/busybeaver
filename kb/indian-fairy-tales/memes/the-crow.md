## Overview

The Crow is the central negative character in "[[the-pigeon|The Pigeon]] and the Crow" (Chapter 29), a Jataka tale in which the Bodhisatta is born as a Pigeon. The Crow embodies greed and self-destructive [[folly-and-foolishness|foolishness]]: he befriends the Pigeon solely to gain access to a rich man's kitchen, ignores all warnings, and is ultimately plucked, spiced, and left to die (029-001).

## Role in the Tale

The Crow is introduced as a [[greed-in-folklore|greedy]] bird who, flying near a rich man's kitchen and seeing "all sorts of delicate food," determines to get some by any means. He approaches the Pigeon and pretends friendship, following him everywhere (029-002).

[[the-pigeon|The Pigeon]] is wary but charitable, asking "What do you want, Mr. Crow? You and I don't feed alike" (029-003). The Crow insists on being companions, and they begin feeding together — though the Crow secretly gorges himself on fat worms from cow-dung while the Pigeon takes his time (029-005).

The Crow's undoing comes when a great purchase of fish arrives at the kitchen. Unable to contain his greed, he pretends to have indigestion to avoid flying out with the Pigeon (029-008). [[the-pigeon|The Pigeon]] warns him directly:

> "Nonsense! Crows never have indigestion... don't behave in this way just for seeing a little fish." (029-011)

The Crow ignores the warning. As soon as the cook steps away, he alights on a dish of dainty food. The cook catches him, plucks all the feathers from his head (leaving one tuft), rubs him with powdered ginger, cummin, and butter-milk, and throws him into a basket (029-014).

The tale ends with a poetic exchange in which the Pigeon mocks the Crow and the Crow admits his fault:

> "I'm nothing but a greedy crow. / I would not do as I was told, / So now I'm plucked, as you behold." (029-022)

> "You'll come to grief again, I know — / It is your nature to do so." (029-027)

[[the-pigeon|The Pigeon]] flies away, refusing to live with the Crow any longer, and the Crow lies groaning until he dies (029-031).

## Relationships

- **[[the-pigeon|The Pigeon]] (Bodhisatta)** — the Crow's would-be friend and mentor; the Pigeon tries to warn and guide the Crow but is ultimately betrayed by the Crow's nature (029-003)
- **The Cook** — the human agent of the Crow's punishment; catches and humiliates him (029-014)

## Moral and Cultural Significance

The Crow is a classic "foolish greed" figure in the Jataka tradition. The tale's moral is stated explicitly at the end: the Crow's nature — [[greed-in-folklore|greedy]], dishonest, and impulsive — cannot be changed, and it will always bring him to ruin (029-027). [[the-prince-of-five-weapons|The Bodhisatta]] (as the Pigeon) represents wisdom and moderation, while the Crow embodies the opposite.

The Crow's punishment is both physical and social: he is stripped of his feathers (the visible sign of his disgrace), publicly humiliated, and abandoned by his only friend. His death at the end — "the Crow lay there groaning till he died" (029-031) — serves as the fable's unambiguous warning against allowing greed to override judgment.

Jacobs notes this tale is a Jataka (No. 42) featuring [[the-pigeon|the Bodhisatta as a Pigeon]], part of the extensive animal-fable tradition in Buddhist literature.
