## Overview

The White Hind is the primary antagonist of "[[the-son-of-seven-mothers|The Son of Seven Queens]]" (Chapter 16), a shape-shifting enchantress who first appears as a beautiful, uncatchable creature to lure the King away from his hunting party, then transforms into a maiden of bewitching beauty. Through her magical glamour, she compels the King to blind his seven Queens and install her in their place (016-006–016-010). The White Hind embodies the tale's central tension between outward beauty and inner cruelty — a motif recurring across Indian folklore, where shape-shifting female [[supernatural-beings|spirits]] often test or destroy kings.

## Role in the Tale

### The Chase

The White Hind first appears as a creature of extraordinary beauty — "a white hind with golden horns and silver hoofs" — that flashes past the King during a hunt (016-006). She deliberately leads him away from his attendants, deep into the mountains, to a narrow ravine with a wretched hovel. This is no ordinary animal: the chase is a deliberate trap.

### The Transformation

Inside the hovel, the King finds an old woman at a spinning-wheel. At her call, a maiden emerges — "so lovely and charming, so white-skinned and golden-haired, that the King was transfixed by astonishment" (016-007). The text immediately reveals the connection:

> "She held the vessel of water to the King's lips, and as he drank he looked into her eyes, and then it became clear to him that the girl was no other than the white hind with the golden horns and silver feet he had chased so far." (016-007)

### The Demand

When the King begs the maiden to return as his bride, she sets a cruel condition:

> "Give me the eyes of your seven Queens, and then perhaps I may believe you mean what you say." (016-008)

[[king|The King]], "carried away by the glamour of the white hind's magical beauty," complies. He has the seven Queens blinded and thrown into a dungeon, then delivers their fourteen eyes to the White Hind. She threads them into a necklace and mockingly gives it to her mother: "Wear that, little mother, as a keepsake, whilst I am away in the King's palace" (016-009).

### The Usurpation

The White Hind takes the seven Queens' palace, clothes, jewels, and slaves, living "in wicked splendour" — the narrator's phrase explicitly condemning her rule (016-010, 016-013). She remains the antagonist throughout the tale until the son of the first Queen eventually defeats her.

## Key Deeds

- **Luring the King**: Transforms into a magical white hind to lead the King away from his retinue into a trap (016-006).
- **Bewitchment**: Uses her supernatural beauty to cloud the King's judgment, causing him to forget his seven wives and his own moral compass (016-007–016-008).
- **Cruelty**: Demands the eyes of the seven Queens as a condition of marriage, then wears them as a mocking necklace (016-008–016-009).
- **Usurpation**: Displaces the seven Queens entirely, taking their home, possessions, and status (016-010).

## Relationships

- **[[king|The King]]** — Her victim and husband. She bewitches him with her magical beauty, manipulating him into horrific acts against his own wives (016-007–016-010).
- **The Seven Queens** — Her rivals, whom she has blinded and imprisoned. She treats their suffering with contemptuous mockery (016-008–016-009).
- **[[the-old-woman|The Old Woman]] (her mother)** — An accomplice who lives in the hovel in the ravine. The White Hind gives her the seven Queens' eyes as a "keepsake" (016-009).
- **[[the-son-of-seven-mothers|The Son of Seven Queens]]** — The hero who ultimately defeats her, born to the first Queen after the fakir's blessing (016-001–016-002).

## Cultural Significance

The White Hind belongs to a widespread folklore motif: the **shape-shifting enchantress** who uses beauty to ensnare and destroy powerful men (ATU 403, "The Black and the White Bride," and related tale-types). In the Indian context, she shares features with *rakshasis* (female [[supernatural-beings|demons]]) who assume beautiful forms to deceive their victims, though the text does not explicitly identify her as a rakshasi.

Her demand for the Queens' eyes carries specific symbolic weight: in Indian folklore, the eye is associated with both fertility and the evil eye (*nazar*), and the act of blinding rival wives is a grotesque inversion of the polygynous household's normal hierarchy. Her ultimate defeat by the son born despite her usurpation reinforces the tale's underlying moral: that cruelty and deception cannot permanently suppress rightful lineage.
