## Overview

The Gardener's Daughter is the heroine of "The Boy Who Had a [[moon|Moon]] on His Forehead and a Star on His Chin" (Chapter 22). A poor girl who plays daily under the trees in the King's garden, she confidently prophesies to her playfellows that when she marries, she will bear a son "with a moon on his forehead and a star on his chin" (022-002). Her [[fate-and-prophecy|prophecy]] reaches the King, who — having four Queens but no child — marries her in hope of an heir (022-003). What follows is a tale of persecution, resilience, and vindication, as the Gardener's Daughter endures the machinations of four jealous co-wives, the loss of her miraculous son, and years of servitude before the truth is finally revealed.

## Role in the Tale

### The Prophecy

The Gardener's Daughter is introduced as one of "seven daughters of poor parents" who play daily with the gardener's daughter in the King's garden. Her repeated boast — "When I am married I shall have a son. Such a beautiful boy as he will be has never been seen. He will have a moon on his forehead and a star on his chin" — is initially mocked by her playfellows (022-002). But when the King overhears, he resolves to marry her, hoping to obtain the prophesied heir (022-003).

### Persecution by the Four Queens

[[king|The King]]'s four existing Queens, jealous and threatened, conspire against the Gardener's Daughter. They trick her into beating a magic kettle-drum that summons the King home from his hunt, making her appear frivolous and demanding (022-004–022-010). When she falls ill, they drug her into unconsciousness, and when she gives birth to the miraculous boy, they substitute a stone and throw the infant into a potter's field (022-012–022-017).

> "The King was very angry and disappointed to hear that his youngest wife, the gardener's daughter, had given him a stone instead of the beautiful little son she had promised him." (022-017)

[[king|The King]] casts her out, and she becomes a servant in the palace, working unrecognised for years (022-017, 022-078).

### Vindication

Years later, when the young prince — now grown and bearing the distinctive moon and star — arrives at the palace, he recognises his mother by instinct, making her "a great many salaams" and giving her the finest presents (022-087). When the King asks why he honours a servant, the prince reveals the truth:

> "She is no servant, but the gardener's daughter, and she is my mother." (022-092)

[[king|The King]] is overcome with remorse and restores the Gardener's Daughter to her rightful position, punishing the four Queens for their cruelty (022-092–022-093).

## Key Deeds

- **The Prophecy**: Boldly and repeatedly declares her future son's miraculous nature, a declaration that sets the entire narrative in motion (022-002).
- **Endurance**: Survives years of unjust punishment — cast out, stripped of status, reduced to servitude — without losing her essential goodness (022-017, 022-078).
- **Grace Under Persecution**: Unlike many folk-tale heroines who plot revenge, the Gardener's Daughter simply endures; her vindication comes not through her own action but through her son's recognition (022-087, 022-092).

## Relationships

- **[[king|The King]]** — Her husband, who marries her for her [[fate-and-prophecy|prophecy]] but abandons her when deceived by the four Queens. His remorse and restoration of her status closes the tale (022-003, 022-017, 022-093).
- **The Boy with the [[moon|Moon]] and Star** — Her miraculous son, whose birth fulfills her [[fate-and-prophecy|prophecy]]. He is the agent of her vindication, recognising and honouring his mother when no one else will (022-087, 022-092).
- **The Four Queens** — Her antagonists. Jealous of her prophesied son and her youth, they drug her, steal her infant, and convince the King she has borne a stone (022-012–022-017).
- **[[the-gardener|The Gardener]] and His Wife** — Her parents, delighted when the King offers to marry their daughter (022-003).
- **[[the-potter|The Potter]] and His Wife** — The couple who find and raise the abandoned infant prince; they are not directly related to the Gardener's Daughter but serve as her son's foster-parents (022-018–022-020).

## Cultural Significance

The Gardener's Daughter embodies a recurring figure in Indian folklore: the **low-born woman whose innate virtue and prophetic [[fate-and-prophecy|destiny]] elevate her above her station**, only to be pulled down by the jealousy of those who hold power by convention rather than merit. Her story belongs to the widespread tale-type of the "Calumniated Wife" (ATU 707, "The Three Golden Children"), in which a woman's extraordinary children are stolen and she is falsely accused, only to be vindicated years later.

The class dimension is significant: the Gardener's Daughter is explicitly "poor" and of low birth, while the four Queens represent established aristocratic power. Her triumph is not merely personal but carries a subtle social critique — that **virtue and [[fate-and-prophecy|prophecy]] can arise from the humblest origins**, while aristocratic privilege is no guarantee of goodness. This theme resonates with the broader Indian folk tradition's sympathy for the underdog and its recurring motif of the low-born hero or heroine whose worth is ultimately recognised.
