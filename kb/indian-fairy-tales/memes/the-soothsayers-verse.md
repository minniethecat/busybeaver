# The Soothsayer's Inherited Verse

## Summary

In the tenth tale, a soothsayer "on his deathbed wrote out the horoscope of his second son," [[soothsayers-son|Gangazara]], "and bequeathed it to him as his only property, leaving the whole of his estate to his eldest son" (010-001). The horoscope is a terse four-part [[fate-and-prophecy|prophecy]]: "'From my birth poverty!'... 'For ten years, imprisonment'... 'Death on [[the-sea|the sea-shore]]'... 'have some happiness afterwards!'" (010-002). Poor and friendless, [[soothsayers-son|Gangazara]] sets out for [[benares|Benares]] carrying only this verse — but it is precisely his absolute faith in it that saves him at the story's first crisis. Trapped dying of thirst in a waterless desert, he reasons: "Surely my father's [[fate-and-prophecy|prophecy]] never proved untrue. I must survive this calamity to find my death on some sea-coast" (010-003), and this conviction gives him the strength of mind to press on and find a ruined well.

> "Surely my father's prophecy never proved untrue. I must survive this calamity to find my death on some sea-coast." (010-003)

## Context

The dying soothsayer's "sayings never failed"; [[soothsayers-son|Gangazara]] has "seen them prove true to the last word while he was living" (010-002), so the inherited verse carries the full authority of proven [[fate-and-prophecy|prophecy]]. Disinherited of all estate, [[soothsayers-son|Gangazara]] possesses nothing but these lines, and their bleakness — poverty, a decade of imprisonment, death far from home — might have crushed him. Instead he treats the [[fate-and-prophecy|prophecy]] as a shield: because it promises he will die "on [[the-sea|the sea-shore]]," he concludes that nothing else can kill him first. This logic is immediately tested when, in the Vindhya desert with "not a morsel in his hand to eat; nor a drop of water to drink," he must choose whether to trust the verse or despair (010-003).

## Participants

- [[soothsayers-son]] — [[soothsayers-son|Gangazara]], the disinherited second son whose sole legacy is the horoscope, and whose unwavering belief in it becomes his courage and his salvation (010-001, 010-003).
- **The soothsayer (his father)** — the dying seer whose sayings "never failed" and who leaves his son nothing but the prophetic verse that will govern his whole life (010-001, 010-002).
- **The king of tigers** — the first creature [[soothsayers-son|Gangazara]] dares to rescue from the well, precisely because the verse convinces him he "must die on a sea coast, and not by a tiger" (010-004).
- **The king of serpents (Nagaraja)** — the "son of Adisesha" whom [[soothsayers-son|Gangazara]] lifts out next, again calling "to mind the 'death on [[the-sea|the sea-shore]]' of the [[fate-and-prophecy|prophecy]]" as he acts (010-005).

## Outcome or Consequence

The verse's power is proved at once. Reaching [[the-well|the ruined well]], [[soothsayers-son|Gangazara]] hears the trapped king of tigers beg for rescue; where another traveller might flee a tiger, he lifts it out, reasoning from the [[fate-and-prophecy|prophecy]] that no tiger can be his death (010-004). True to its word, the grateful tiger-king vows lifelong aid — "Whenever you are in any difficulty just think of me" — and warns him against ever trusting the goldsmith below (010-004). Emboldened by the same faith, [[soothsayers-son|Gangazara]] then rescues the king of serpents, who likewise pledges to "help you throughout life" (010-005). Thus the inherited verse, by removing his fear of death, secures him the two grateful animal kings whose loyalty will later feed him through his foretold imprisonment and empower his rise. Every clause of the [[fate-and-prophecy|prophecy]] — poverty, the ten years' imprisonment, death on [[the-sea|the sea-shore]], and the "happiness afterwards" — ultimately comes literally true, "and so the horoscope was fully fulfilled" (later in the tale).

## Cultural or Moral Significance

The event dramatizes the folk conviction that fate is fixed and inescapable, and that faith in it can be a source of strength rather than paralysis. [[soothsayers-son|Gangazara]]'s inheritance is not gold but a [[fate-and-prophecy|prophecy]], and the tale's irony is that this seemingly worthless bequest proves more valuable than his brother's estate: it makes him fearless. Because he "knows" the manner of his death, he faces tiger and serpent without terror, and that fearlessness — really trust in [[fate-and-prophecy|destiny]] — wins him the allies who carry him to a throne. The verse also embodies the Indian astrological tradition of the horoscope cast at birth, treated here as literally binding: its riddling final promise of "happiness afterwards" supplies the story's suspense, since the reader, like [[soothsayers-son|Gangazara]], must wait to see how so grim a [[fate-and-prophecy|prophecy]] can end in joy. The episode thus frames the whole tale as a meditation on [[fate-and-prophecy]] and the courage that faith in [[fate-and-prophecy|destiny]] can give.
