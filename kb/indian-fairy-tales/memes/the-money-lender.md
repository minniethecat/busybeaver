# The Money-lender

## Overview

The money-lender is the antagonist of the twenty-first tale, [[the-farmer-and-the-money-lender]]. He opens the story as the prosperous half of a fixed contest: "Good harvests, or bad, the farmer was always poor, the money-lender rich" (021-001). Never named, he is defined wholly by his function and his vice — usury and greed — and he serves as a compact folk portrait of the grasping creditor. His downfall is one of the collection's clearest illustrations of [[greed-in-folklore]]: offered a share in a magic windfall, he is destroyed not by poverty or misfortune but by his own compulsion to have twice as much as anyone else.

## Role in the Tale

The money-lender is the [[greed-in-folklore|greedy]] exploiter who cannot leave well enough alone. Having bled the farmer dry, he is asked the secret of wealth and answers with pious hypocrisy, deflecting the question onto the god Ram: "'My friend,' returned the money-lender, piously, 'riches come from Ram—ask *him*'" (021-002). When the farmer returns from that errand transformed and fortunate, the money-lender's role becomes that of the parasite who insists on attaching himself to another's magic. His scheming drives the plot's second half, and his fate delivers the tale's verdict on [[greed-in-folklore|avarice]]: he engineers a bargain to profit from the farmer's [[magical-objects|magic conch]] and is undone by the very doubling he demanded.

## Key Deeds and Appearances

- **The hypocritical deflection (021-001, 021-002).** After years of keeping the farmer poor while growing rich himself, the money-lender responds to the ruined farmer's plea for "the secret of becoming rich" by piously redirecting him to Ram — a hypocrisy that ironically sets the whole plot in motion (021-002).

- **Prying out the secret (021-009).** Noticing the farmer's high [[supernatural-beings|spirits]] on his return — "Some good fortune must have befallen the stupid fellow, to make him hold his head so jauntily" — the money-lender congratulates him "in such cunning words, pretending to have heard all about it," that he coaxes the farmer into telling nearly the whole story (021-009).

- **Stealing the conch (021-010).** Determined "to have the conch by hook or by crook," and "villain enough not to stick at trifles," he simply steals the magic conch — only to find, after "nearly bursting himself with blowing" it, that he cannot work its secret (021-010, 021-011).

- **The doubling bargain (021-011 to 021-014).** Stalemated, he forces a deal on the farmer: he will return the conch and let the farmer use it, but "whatever you get from it, I am to get double" (021-011). Waving away the farmer's protest with "don't be a dog in the manger" (021-013), he binds his victim to enrich him twofold with every wish.

- **His self-made death (021-015).** When the farmer wishes for a well in a drought, the money-lender gets two wells; when the farmer, in a flash of desperate cunning, wishes to be "blind of one eye," the money-lender is blinded in both, and, "in trying to steer his way between the two new wells, he fell into one, and was drowned" (021-015).

## Relationships

- **The farmer** — his lifelong debtor and victim, "the simple farmer" who is always poor while the money-lender prospers (021-001); the man he cheats, robs, and finally binds to a doubling bargain, and who at last destroys him by turning that greed against him (021-011, 021-015).
- **Ram** — the god the money-lender hypocritically invokes to fob off the farmer (021-002); it is Ram who then gives the farmer the conch and warns that "even magic is not proof against" a money-lender's wiles (021-008), a warning the story's ending vindicates.

## Moral or Cultural Significance

The money-lender is the archetypal usurer of Indian folk tales, and his story is a pointed parable of greed defeating itself. His fatal flaw is not merely wanting wealth but requiring *more* than his neighbour — the demand for "double" that makes another man's blessing his own curse. Ram's caution that "even magic is not proof against their wiles" (021-008) frames him as a figure of proverbial cunning, yet the tale insists that cunning without limit is its own trap: to ruin such a man, the farmer need only give him twice of a self-inflicted wound. The closing moral — that "a farmer once got the better of a money-lender—but only by losing one of his eyes" (021-016) — measures both the depth of the usurer's grip and the terrible price of escaping it, sealing the money-lender's place as a cautionary emblem of [[greed-in-folklore|avarice]] punished.
