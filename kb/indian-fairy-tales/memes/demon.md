Demons in Indian fairy tales encompass a range of malevolent supernatural beings, most prominently the Rakshas (or Rakshasa) — shape-shifting creatures of Hindu and Buddhist mythology who haunt forests, guard treasures, and threaten human protagonists. They appear as obstacles that heroes must overcome through cunning, courage, or divine aid, and are distinct from other supernatural entities like ghosts (bhuta) or spirits (preta) in their tangible, physically threatening nature (002-067, 008-027).

## Definition

In the Indian folk tradition, demons are supernatural beings of formidable power and generally malevolent intent. The Rakshasa (rendered as "Rakshas" in these tales) is the most frequently encountered type: a creature capable of assuming different forms, dwelling in wild or desolate places, and preying upon humans. Unlike the asura (anti-gods of Vedic mythology), the demons of fairy tales are individually encountered rather than cosmic forces. They serve as guardians of treasure or territory, and defeating them often forms the climactic test of a hero's journey.

## Examples in the Tales

Demons appear as fearsome obstacles in several key tales:

- **The two demons guarding the princess** (002-067–002-073): In *How the Raja's Son Won the Princess Labam*, the prince must first fight and kill two demons before he can approach the princess. The king warns him of this requirement, and when the prince hears of the demons he is "very sad" — until he invokes divine aid in the form of tigers who fight on his behalf (002-068, 002-069). After the demons are slain, the prince reflects, "I had his demons killed by the tigers" (002-073)

- **The eighteen thousand demons of the garden** (016-030–016-041): In *The Son of Seven Queens*, the hero must pass through a garden guarded by eighteen thousand demons — later multiplied to eighteen million — who "kept watch and ward over the rice" (016-030). These demons are "really frightful to look at" (016-031), and the hero is repeatedly warned against attempting to pass them (016-040)

- **The Rakshas within** (008-027–008-035): In *Loving Laili*, a character is tormented by the belief that a Rakshas inhabits their body or their food. The crane warns of a Rakshas in the stomach (008-011), and later Mirza Lajbun is "quite sure she was a Rakshas, or a demon, or some such horrible thing" (008-030). The tale plays with the boundary between real demonic possession and superstitious fear — "if it had been a Rakshas, it would not have eaten you" (008-028)

## Cultural Context

The Rakshasa in Indian tradition predates the fairy tale collections and has deep roots in Vedic and Puranic literature. In the *Ramayana*, Ravana — the ten-headed king of Lanka — is the archetypal Rakshasa: powerful, wealthy, learned, yet ultimately doomed by his own excess. The fairy-tale demons are scaled-down versions of this epic archetype: they guard specific sites rather than ruling kingdoms, and they can be defeated by heroism or trickery rather than requiring divine incarnation.

The multiplication of demon numbers (from eighteen thousand to eighteen million) in *The Son of Seven Queens* reflects a folkloric pattern of hyperbolic escalation — the storyteller amplifies the danger with each retelling to heighten the hero's achievement. The scholarly notes observe that the "well-bred demon" motif — where demons follow supernatural etiquette — is a characteristically Indian touch (030-099).
