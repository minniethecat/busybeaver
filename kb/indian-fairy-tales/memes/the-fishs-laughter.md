# The Fish's Laughter and the Riddle Solved

## Summary

When a fisherwoman brings a great fish to the palace, the queen asks whether it is male or female, and "on hearing this the fish laughed aloud" (024-003). The queen, annoyed, tells the king, who orders his vizier to explain the fish's laughter within six months "on pain of death" (024-011). No sage or magician can solve it, and the despairing vizier sends his son away to travel until the king's anger cools (024-011). On his journey the young man lodges with an old farmer whose sharp daughter alone can interpret the youth's cryptic speech (024-031). She finally reads the true meaning of the riddle: "The laughing of the fish... indicates that there is a man in the palace who is plotting against the king's life" (024-048). The son races home, and a test devised to expose the intruder confirms it.

> "The laughing of the fish," said the girl, "which seems to have been the cause of all this trouble, indicates that there is a man in the palace who is plotting against the king's life." (024-048)

## Context

The tale opens with the queen at her window, asking the fisherwoman whether the fish is "a he or a she" because she wishes to purchase "a she fish" (024-001, 024-002). The fish's rude laughter sends the queen "to her room in a great rage" (024-005), and her account provokes the king's disbelief — "A fish laugh! Impossible!" — before he resolves to inquire (024-008, 024-010). The vizier labours for five months in vain, consulting "the wise and learned, and they who were skilled in magic and in all manner of trickery," and finding no answer, prepares for death (024-011). His clever son, travelling under Kismat's guidance, tests every companion with riddling words and deeds, all of which the farmer's daughter unravels one by one (024-032 to 024-041), proving herself the true solver of mysteries before she turns to the fish itself.

## Participants

- **The vizier's son** — the clever, handsome young man banished to save his life, who carries the riddle to the farmer's household and, once it is solved, cries "There is yet time for me to return and save my father" (024-012, 024-049).
- **The farmer's daughter** — "a very sharp and wise girl" who interprets the youth's every cryptic act and finally divines that the fish laughed because a man is hidden in the palace plotting against the king (024-031, 024-048).
- **The vizier** — the faithful minister condemned to death for a riddle he cannot answer, who proposes the pit-jumping test that proves his son's report (024-011, 024-052).
- **The queen** — at whose window the fish laughed, and whose concealed male attendant the laughter exposes (024-001, 024-054).
- **The king** — who threatens his vizier with death, disbelieves the accusation, and orders the test that unmasks the impostor (024-011, 024-051, 024-053).

## Outcome or Consequence

The vizier's son "hastened back to his own country, taking with him the farmer's daughter," and reports what he has learned to his father, who is carried, "now almost dead from the expectation of death," before the king (024-050). Though the king cries "Never!", the vizier insists and proposes a test: "call together all the maids in your palace, and order them to jump over a pit... We'll soon find out whether there is any man there" (024-051, 024-052). The pit is dug, all the maids attempt the leap, "but only one succeeded. That one was found to be a man!!" (024-053). Thus the fish's laughter is vindicated: "Thus was the queen satisfied, and the faithful old vizier saved" (024-054). In reward, "the vizier's son married the old farmer's daughter; and a most happy marriage it was" (024-055).

## Cultural or Moral Significance

The episode is a classic riddle-and-wisdom tale in which apparent nonsense conceals hidden truth. The fish — an emblem of nature bearing witness — laughs at the queen's question because the palace harbours a man [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]]d among her women, a scandal the laughter silently accuses. The tale exalts the intelligence of a humble farmer's daughter over the learned sages and magicians who fail: it is her plain wit, not court magic, that reads both the young man's parables and the fish's meaning, embodying the folk conviction that true wisdom is practical and often found in unexpected people. Her string of interpretations — debt, sticks-for-horses, city-as-cemetery — trains the reader to hear double meanings before the central riddle is unlocked, and her reward, a happy marriage, affirms that cleverness and virtue are rightly matched.
