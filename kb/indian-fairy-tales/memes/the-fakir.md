The **Fakir** is the central figure in the tale of [[the-prince-and-the-fakir]] (Chapter 23 of Joseph Jacobs's *Indian Fairy Tales*, 1892). A wandering [[ascetic]] who arrives at a crossroads where a childless [[King]] lies seeking divine mercy, the Fakir offers a bargain: he will pray for the King to have two sons, but one of them will belong to him (023-002). When the Fakir returns a year later to claim his prize, he becomes the antagonist whose supernatural powers and eventual defeat by the [[Prince]] drive the narrative's central conflict. The Fakir illustrates the dual role of the ascetic in Indian folklore — at once a holy man capable of granting boons and a dangerous figure who must be outwitted.

## Overview

The Fakir appears at a crossroads where a childless King has laid himself down so that passersby might step over him, hoping thereby to be absolved of his sins and granted a son (023-002). Unlike the thousand men who passed before, the Fakir stops and questions the King. Learning of the King's desire for children, he offers a supernatural bargain: two sweetmeats, two sons — but one son will belong to the Fakir (023-006).

> "Oh King! If you have children, what will you give me?" ... The Fakir said, "Of goods and gold I have no lack, but I will say a prayer for you, and you will have two sons; one of those sons will be mine." (023-006, 023-008)

A year later, the Fakir returns to claim his due. [[king|The King]] attempts to deceive him with slave-children, but the Fakir possesses supernatural perception — he overhears an ant's conversation that reveals the true sons are eating in an underground cellar (023-016). The Fakir takes the eldest Prince as his pupil and sets out on a journey.

What follows is a deadly test of wits. The Fakir orders the Prince to gather fuel, then prepares a great pot of boiling oil — a hundred gallons, with fire burning beneath — and commands the Prince to approach (023-021). Three times the Prince refuses, each time replying "Master first, and pupil after" (023-022). The Fakir makes a dash to seize the Prince and throw him into the caldron, but the Prince lifts the Fakir and throws him in instead, killing him instantly (023-022). The Fakir is revealed posthumously as a captor of many — his house holds imprisoned men, horses, greyhounds, simurgs, and tigers, all freed by the Prince after the Fakir's death.

## Role in the Tale

The Fakir serves three intertwined roles in "[[the-prince|The Prince]] and the Fakir":

**The Boon-Giver.** The Fakir initiates the tale's action by offering the King the supernatural means to have children. His power to grant fertility through prayer and sweetmeats establishes him as a figure of genuine occult ability, not a charlatan (023-006).

**The Claimant-Trickster.** When he returns to collect one son, the Fakir operates within the letter of a freely-made bargain. His supernatural perception — detecting the King's deception through the speech of ants — [[supernatural-beings|demons]]trates powers beyond the ordinary, reinforcing the motif that one cannot cheat a holy man (023-016).

**The Would-Be Destroyer.** The Fakir's true nature emerges when he attempts to boil the Prince alive in a caldron of oil. This reveals him as a predatory figure who lures victims, imprisons them, and ultimately destroys them. [[the-prince|The Prince]]s he collected in the past did not survive; this Prince is the first to turn the tables (023-022).

## The Fakir's Household

After the Fakir's death, the Prince discovers the extent of his captive collection:

- **Imprisoned men** — previous victims locked in the Fakir's house
- **Two horses** — kept in a stable-hut
- **Two greyhounds** — tied up
- **Two simurgs** — mythical birds, imprisoned
- **Two tigers** — caged

Each pair of creatures becomes part of the Prince's retinue, and the tigers and hounds later assist him in his adventures. The simurgs, drawn from Persian mythology, signal the Fakir's reach into supernatural domains beyond ordinary asceticism (023-022).

## Relationships

- **[[the-prince-and-the-fakir|The Prince]]** — the Fakir's chosen pupil and eventual destroyer. Their conflict is a classic reversal: the predator becomes prey when the pupil refuses to submit (023-022).
- **[[King]]** — the childless monarch who bargains with the Fakir. Though the King attempts to deceive, the Fakir's supernatural perception defeats the ruse (023-016).
- **[[the-princes-test-by-the-fakir|The Prince's Test by the Fakir]]** — the event of the caldron confrontation, which marks both the Fakir's death and the Prince's emergence as a hero.

## Moral and Cultural Significance

The Fakir embodies a recurring figure in Indian folklore: the ascetic whose spiritual power is morally ambivalent. He can grant blessings — fertility to a childless King — but his motives are predatory. The tale's resolution carries a clear message: cleverness and courage can defeat even a supernaturally-gifted opponent.

The Fakir's role also reflects the broader [[trickster-figure]] motif and the [[jataka]] tradition's interest in rightful succession and the testing of princes. His death-by-caldron mirrors the folkloric pattern of the villain destroyed by his own trap — a motif related to [[folly-and-foolishness]], though here the Fakir is cunning rather than foolish, and his defeat comes through the Prince's greater cleverness.

The Fakir's clothing — which the Prince later wears — functions as a [[disguise-and-deception|transformation]]al [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]], a motif explored further in [[disguise-and-deception]]. [[the-princess|The Princess]] at the husband-choosing ceremony sees through the Fakir's attire to the royal marks beneath, recognizing the Prince's true identity (023-033).
