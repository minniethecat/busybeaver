# The Cruel Crane

## Overview

The cruel crane is the villain of the tale [[the-cruel-crane-outwitted]] (Chapter 7). Eyeing a pond of fish stranded by the dry season, he resolves at once, "I must outwit these fish somehow or other and make a prey of them" (007-003). Posing as their friend and rescuer, he offers to carry them one by one to a finer pond — and instead flies each to a tree, kills it, and eats it. His treachery succeeds against every fish in the pond, but it meets its match in a single wary crab.

This crane is the moral opposite of the noble white crane of Chapter 1: where that bird is a self-sacrificing form of the Bodhisatta, this one is a "villain, though exceeding clever" (007-036), and the tale is a Jataka lesson in how deceit destroys the deceiver.

## Role in the Tale

The crane's whole plot is a confidence trick. He sits brooding at the water's edge until the fish ask what troubles him, then feigns concern for their plight in the shrinking pond and promises a "fine large pond, covered with all the kinds of lotuses" (007-010). When the fish object that "a crane should take thought for [[the-fish|the fishes]] is a thing unheard of" (007-011), he disarms them by offering to fly a scout to see the pond for itself (007-012). Won over, the fish surrender themselves one at a time. The crane carries each to a Varana-tree, where "he threw it into a fork of the tree, struck it with his beak, and killed it; and then ate its flesh, and threw its bones away at the foot of the tree" (007-016), returning each time to call for the next victim — "till he came back and found no more!" (007-018).

His greed then overreaches. A crab remains, and the crane, meaning to eat him too (007-019), offers the same false ferry. But the crab insists on gripping the crane's neck with his claws rather than being carried in the beak (007-026). When the crane veers toward the tree of bones and gloats, "just as I have eaten those fish... just so I will devour you as well!" (007-030), the crab tightens his pincer-grip and forces the crane down to the pond. Set safely at the water's edge, the crab "cut through its neck as clean as one would cut a lotus-stalk" (007-034), and the deceiver is destroyed.

## Key Deeds and Appearances

- **Plots against the fish** — resolves to "outwit these fish... and make a prey of them" (007-003).
- **Feigns friendship** — pretends to grieve over [[the-fish|the fishes]]' shrinking pond and promises a fine lotus-covered pond (007-008, 007-010).
- **Wins their trust** — offers to fly a one-eyed scout-fish to see the pond, which convinces the rest (007-012, 007-014).
- **Devours them one by one** — kills each fish in a Varana-tree and piles the bones at its root (007-016, 007-018).
- **Turns on the crab** — offers the same false ferry, intending to eat him too (007-019, 007-020).
- **Boasts too soon** — gloats that he will devour the crab as he did the fish (007-030).
- **Beheaded** — pleads for his life as the crab grips his neck, then is forced to the pond and decapitated (007-032, 007-034).

## Relationships

- **The fish** — the trusting victims he lures from their pond and eats one by one; their credulity is his opportunity (007-011, 007-016).
- **The one-eyed fish** — the scout the fish send with him, whom he shows the pond and then kills first (007-013, 007-016).
- **The crab** — the wary creature who sees through the ruse, grips the crane's neck "as with a vice," and beheads him (007-026, 007-031, 007-034).
- **The Tree-Genius (Bodhisatta)** — the spirit of the Varana-tree who witnesses the deed and pronounces the closing verse on the villain's fate (007-035). See [[bodhisatta]].

## Moral or Cultural Significance

The cruel crane is the emblem of clever villainy that ruins itself. The tale's own verdict, spoken by the tree-spirit, is that "the villain, though exceeding clever, / Shall prosper not by his villainy" (007-036, 007-038) — he may win for a while by deceit, "but only as the Crane here from the Crab!" (007-039). The bird's treachery answers the fable's question about the fate of the deceiver: cunning turned to cruelty invites a cunning of its own to undo it.

As a Jataka, the story travelled far. The notes trace it into the [[bidpai]] literature and its many offshoots, including North's early English version, "where the crane becomes 'a great Paragone of India'" and the crab, before all the fish are devoured, destroys him "as in the Jataka" (030-045). The crane thus stands as a fixed type in world folklore — the false protector whose betrayal of trust is repaid in kind. Within this collection he is the dark mirror of the noble crane of [[the-lion-and-the-crane]], setting predatory deceit against self-sacrificing virtue.
