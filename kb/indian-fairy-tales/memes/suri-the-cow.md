# Suri the Cow

## Overview

Suri is the King's cow in [[the-boy-who-had-a-moon-on-his-forehead-and-a-star-on-his-chin]] (Chapter 22), a talking animal who becomes the second guardian in the chain that preserves the life of the stolen infant [[the-moon-prince]]. When the dog Shankar is shot by the jealous Queens, he brings the child to Suri with the plea: "Suri, I want you to take care of this child for me" (022-022). Suri accepts without hesitation — "Let me see what it is. I will take care of it if I can" (022-023) — and swallows the child into her stomach, where he lives for two full years (022-025).

Suri's guardianship bridges the gap between Shankar's short nursing period (six months) and [[katar-the-horse]]'s final two years. She is the longest-serving guardian in terms of continuous care, and her fate — killed by the King on the Queens' persuasion — establishes the pattern of sacrifice that defines all three animal protectors in the story.

## Role in the Tale

Suri enters the narrative after the dog Shankar has been shot with an arrow for concealing the child (022-027). Having witnessed the wonderful child that Shankar carries within him, Suri agrees to take over guardianship. The child now "lived in Suri's stomach" (022-025), and for two years — roughly the child's second and third years of life — she hides him from the Queens who seek his death.

[[the-queen|The Queen]]s, however, remain watchful. A cowherd follows Suri and witnesses "the wonderful child" (022-027), confirming their suspicion that the infant survived Shankar's death. They go to the King and persuade him — as they did with Shankar — to have Suri killed. Suri overhears the King's order, just as Shankar had heard the order that doomed him, and just as [[katar-the-horse|Katar]] will later hear his own death sentence (022-029).

Rather than flee as [[katar-the-horse|Katar]] eventually does, Suri brings the child up from her stomach and shows him to Shankar, who has survived his arrow wound. She says to Shankar, "show me what it is," and Shankar presents the child to her one final time (022-030). Suri then passes the child to [[katar-the-horse|Katar]] the horse — the third and last guardian — before meeting her own death at the King's hands (022-045). Years later, when the [[moon|Moon]] Prince returns in triumph, the King is forced to remember his cow Suri "and how you had her killed to please your wives" (022-095), a reproach that names Suri as an innocent whose death demands acknowledgment.

## Key Deeds and Appearances

- **Accepting the charge** — receives the child from Shankar with the promise "I will take care of it if I can" (022-022, 022-023).
- **Two years of concealment** — the child lives inside Suri's stomach for two full years, the longest unbroken guardianship of any animal protector in the tale (022-025).
- **Witnessed by the cowherd** — her secret is discovered when the cowherd follows her and sees the wonderful child she carries (022-027).
- **Hearing the death sentence** — overhears the King ordering her to be killed, as Shankar before her and [[katar-the-horse|Katar]] after her would each hear their own executions ordered (022-029).
- **Passing the child to [[katar-the-horse|Katar]]** — brings the child up from her stomach and shows him to Shankar before transferring guardianship to [[katar-the-horse|Katar]] the horse (022-030).
- **Remembered in accusation** — at the story's climax the King is confronted with the memory of Suri, killed "to please your wives" (022-095).

## Relationships

- **[[the-moon-prince]]** — the child she swallows and protects for two years, from roughly age two to four.
- **Shankar the Dog** — the first guardian, who brings the child to Suri and with whom she shares a final exchange before her death (022-022, 022-030).
- **[[katar-the-horse]]** — the third guardian to whom Suri passes the child; [[katar-the-horse|Katar]] inherits the protective chain and completes it by fleeing with the boy (022-030).
- **The Four Queens** — the murderous co-wives who orchestrate Suri's death after discovering her secret through the cowherd's report (022-027).
- **[[king|The King]]** — her owner, who orders her killed on the Queens' persuasion and is later made to remember the injustice (022-029, 022-095).

## Moral or Cultural Significance

Suri represents the sacrificial guardian in its purest form. Unlike Shankar, who survives his arrow wound, and [[katar-the-horse|Katar]], who escapes to freedom, Suri accepts the child and then accepts her own death. Her two years of service are longer than Shankar's six months, and her steady, silent protection — the child simply "lived in Suri's stomach" (022-025) — lacks the drama of [[katar-the-horse|Katar]]'s magical [[disguise-and-deception|transformation]]s but carries a quiet [[faithfulness-and-loyalty|fidelity]] that the tale honours by recalling her at the end. In the King's final confrontation with his past, Suri's name is spoken alongside Shankar's as one of the innocents whose deaths were bought by the Queens' malice. Within Hindu cultural contexts, the cow (go-mata) is sacred and inviolable — the King's command to kill his own cow, a creature that had done nothing but protect a child, compounds the crime with sacrilege, and deepens the moral weight of the Queens' deception.
