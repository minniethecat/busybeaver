# The Parrot

## Overview

The parrot of the tale [[punchkin]] (Chapter 4) is "a little green parrot" (004-061) in which the wicked magician [[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]] has hidden his life. It never speaks or acts, yet it is the hinge on which the whole story turns: as [[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]] himself confesses, "on the life of the parrot depends my life; and if the parrot is killed I must die" (004-061). To reach it, [[balna|Balna]]'s son must cross "hundreds of thousands of miles" of desolate jungle to a circle of palm trees, where beneath six stacked water-pots the parrot is kept "in a small cage" guarded by "many thousand genii" (004-061).

The bird is the physical vessel of the "external soul" motif — the life-token kept outside the body — and its destruction is the exact undoing of the magician's power.

## Role in the Tale

The parrot is the secret weakness at the heart of [[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]]'s seeming invincibility. When [[balna|Balna]], coached by her son, flatters the magician into revealing whether he can ever die, he discloses the hidden parrot and boasts that it is safe forever, both because the country is unreachable and because the guardian genii "kill all who approach the place" (004-061). [[balna|Balna]]'s son nonetheless reaches it: carried by grateful eagles, he overturns the six water-pots and "seized the little green parrot, which he rolled up in his cloak" while the genii wake too late to stop him (004-065).

The climax binds the parrot's body to the magician's, limb for limb. As the boy plays with the bird at the palace door, the frightened magician bargains for it, first freeing his petrified victims (004-072). Then the boy destroys the parrot piece by piece, and [[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]]'s body fails in step:

> Then the boy took hold of the parrot, and tore off one of its wings; and as he did so the Magician's right arm fell off. (004-075)

Wing by wing and leg by leg the parrot is dismembered, each loss echoed in [[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]]'s body, until "he wrung the bird's neck, and threw it at the Magician; and, as he did so, [[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]]'s head twisted round, and, with a fearful groan, he died!" (004-078).

## Key Deeds and Appearances

- **Holds the magician's life** — [[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]] reveals that "on the life of the parrot depends my life" (004-061).
- **Hidden and guarded** — kept in a cage beneath six water-pots in a palm circle, ringed by thousands of genii (004-061).
- **Seized by the hero** — [[balna|Balna]]'s son overturns the pots and rolls the parrot up in his cloak (004-065).
- **Used as ransom** — playing with the parrot, the boy forces [[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]] to free his victims (004-067, 004-072).
- **Destroyed limb by limb** — each wing and leg torn off fells the corresponding limb of the magician (004-075, 004-076, 004-077).
- **Killed** — its neck wrung, and with it [[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]] dies (004-078).

## Relationships

- **[[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]] the magician** — the parrot is the hidden seat of his life and soul; its fate is his fate. See [[punchkin-magician]] (004-061, 004-078).
- **[[balna|Balna]]'s son** — the hero who journeys to find the parrot, seizes it, and destroys it to free his family (004-065, 004-075).
- **The eagles** — the grateful birds who carry the boy to the guarded palm circle where the parrot is hidden (004-065).

## Moral or Cultural Significance

The little green parrot is the clearest instance in the collection of the "external soul" or "[[the-external-soul|life-index]]" — the folklore motif of a being whose life is stored in an object or creature apart from itself, so that the hero who destroys the token destroys the enemy; see [[the-external-soul]]. The notes to the collection discuss this pattern under "The Philosophy of [[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]]" as a widespread feature of Indian and world tales (030-034, 030-035).

That the life-vessel is a caged parrot — beautiful, exotic, and helpless — sharpens the tale's moral geometry: the magician who imprisons others in stone has in turn imprisoned his own life in a cage, and the freeing of his victims and the destroying of the parrot are one and the same act. Parrots recur elsewhere in the collection as prized companions — the talking parrot [[hiraman|Hiraman]] in [[how-the-rajas-son-won-the-princess-labam]] (002-004, 002-058) and the parrot that rides on [[rasalu|Rasalu]]'s wrist in [[raja-rasalu]] (019-008, 019-027) — but only here does a parrot carry a man's very life.
