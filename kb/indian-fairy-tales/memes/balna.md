# Balna

## Overview

Balna is the youngest of the seven princesses in the tale [[punchkin]] (Chapter 4). Of the seven daughters of a [[king|Raja]], all "good girls," she is singled out from the first as the one who "was more clever than the rest" (004-001). She marries the youngest and handsomest of seven princely brothers, who "took the beautiful Balna" as his bride (004-038), and it is her cleverness — and later her son's courage — that undoes the wicked magician who tears her family apart.

Her story moves from the domestic quarrels of seven sisters to a tale of abduction, imprisonment, and rescue. Carried off by the magician [[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]] and shut in a high tower for refusing to marry him, Balna endures for fourteen years until the son she bore delivers her and breaks the enchantment that had turned her husband and his brothers to stone.

## Role in the Tale

In the tale's opening Balna is the sharp-tongued, perceptive sister, quick to distrust the [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]]d step-mother's daughter — "Balna saw her first" and warned the others of danger (004-023), though her sisters "thought Balna unnecessarily suspicious" (004-024). This early wariness marks her as the clever one whose judgement, had it been heeded, might have spared them grief.

After her marriage, tragedy strikes. [[About]] a year later Balna bears a little son (004-040), but soon the magician [[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]], wandering through the palace as a fakir, comes to the room where "Balna sat singing beside her little" child (004-045) and carries her away, keeping her "shut up in the tower because she refused to marry" him (004-054). Her petrified husband and his brothers are turned to stone. Balna's role then becomes that of the counselling mother: she tells her grown son what [[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]] had said, yet begs him "on no account to endanger his life by trying to rescue her" (004-054, 004-062). When her son at last conquers the magician, she is set free from the tower (004-079).

## Key Deeds and Appearances

- **The clever sister** — named the youngest yet "more clever than the rest" of the seven princesses (004-001).
- **Suspicious of the intruder** — the first to see and mistrust the step-mother's [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]]d daughter, though her sisters dismiss her caution (004-023, 004-024).
- **Married to the youngest prince** — the handsomest of the seven brothers "took the beautiful Balna" (004-038).
- **Mother of the hero** — about a year after her marriage she bears a little son (004-040), whom she is found singing beside when [[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]] comes (004-045).
- **Abducted and imprisoned** — [[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]] shuts her in a high tower for refusing to marry him (004-054).
- **The counselling mother** — she believes her son's account of his long search and tells him what [[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]] said, but warns him not to risk his life (004-054, 004-056, 004-062).
- **Freed at last** — after the magician's death "they let Balna out of the tower," and she, her son, and the seven Princes depart together (004-079).

## Relationships

- **Her son** — the boy who searches for her for years, whose courage frees her and destroys [[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]]; she trusts his story and takes his advice (004-047, 004-055, 004-056).
- **Her husband, the seventh Prince** — the youngest of the brothers, turned to stone by [[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]] and restored when the magician's power is broken (004-041, 004-072).
- **[[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]]** — the magician who abducts and imprisons her; see [[punchkin-magician]] (004-045, 004-054).
- **Her six sisters** — the elder princesses who marry the other six brothers and whom she tried to warn (004-016, 004-023).

## Moral or Cultural Significance

Balna embodies two virtues prized in these tales: the cleverness that perceives danger before others do, and the maternal love that would rather remain a prisoner than see her child endangered. Her refusal to marry [[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]] through fourteen years of captivity is a quiet, stubborn constancy — the counterpart to her husband's frozen helplessness in stone. Yet the tale gives the decisive act not to Balna herself but to the son she raised, so that her true legacy is the courageous child who completes the rescue. Her restoration, together with her husband and his brothers "in their natural shapes" (004-072), closes the story on the reunion of a family that magic had scattered.
