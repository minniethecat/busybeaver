## Overview

The Ass is the central character of "The Ass in the Lion's Skin" (Chapter 20), a Jataka tale set in [[benares|Benares]] during the reign of Brahma-datta. The story belongs to the "foolish deception exposed" motif: a hawker dresses his ass in a lion's skin to scare away field watchmen, but the ass's own bray reveals its true nature, leading to its death (020-001).

## Role in the Tale

The Ass is a passive figure — it does not choose to deceive; the deception is imposed upon it by its owner, a traveling hawker. At each stop, the hawker clothes the ass in a lion's skin and turns it loose into rice and barley fields, where watchmen dare not approach, mistaking it for a lion (020-002).

The turning point comes when [[the-village|the villagers]], determined to confront the "lion," approach with weapons, blowing conch shells and beating drums. Terrified, the ass brays — and the sound instantly exposes it (020-003). The [[bodhisatta|future Buddha]] (born as a peasant) pronounces the moral in verse:

> "This is not a lion's roaring, / Nor a tiger's, nor a panther's; / Dressed in a lion's skin, / 'Tis a wretched ass that roars!" (020-005)

The villagers then beat the ass until its bones break, and it dies on the spot (020-015).

## Moral and Cultural Significance

The Ass embodies the theme of false appearance versus true nature. The tale teaches that outward [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]] cannot change inner identity — a lesson the [[bodhisatta|future Buddha]] articulates by contrasting the ass's bray with the genuine roars of lion, tiger, and panther (020-005).

The hawker's closing verse reinforces the practical wisdom: the ass could have lived well by keeping silent, but its nature betrayed it (020-010). This aligns with the broader Jataka tradition's emphasis on self-knowledge and the inevitability of one's true character emerging under pressure.

The story is a variant of the widespread "Ass in the Lion's Skin" fable (ATU 214B), found in both the Panchatantra and Aesop traditions. Jacobs notes the Jataka version is one of the earliest recorded examples of this motif (030-112).
