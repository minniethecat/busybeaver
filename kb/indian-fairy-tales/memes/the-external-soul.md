# The External Soul (Life-Index)

## Definition

The external soul — also called the "life-index" — is the motif in which a powerful villain's life is concealed outside his own body, lodged in a distant object or a living creature. So long as that hidden life is safe, the villain cannot be harmed by any ordinary means; but if the object is found and destroyed, he dies instantly. The motif furnishes a story with both an apparently invincible enemy and a hidden weakness that the hero must discover, and it corresponds to tale-type ATU 302, "The Ogre's Heart in the Egg."

## Examples in the Tales

- **[[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]]'s life in a parrot** in [[punchkin]]: the wicked magician [[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]] (004-044) reveals that his life is bound to a little green parrot hidden far away — "on the life of the parrot depends my life; and if the parrot is killed I must die" (004-061). When the hero at last seizes the bird and [[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]] frantically pleads for it, the boy answers "Take your parrot, then" (004-078) and destroys it, whereupon the magician is killed and his spell over his many prisoners is broken. This is the collection's textbook instance of the external-soul motif.

## Cultural Context

The external soul is one of the most widely distributed motifs in world folklore, and the notes to Jacobs's *Indian Fairy Tales* treat it at length. They identify in "[[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]]" the incident of an "external soul" or "Life Index" (030-019) and note that scholars have studied "the external soul of the ogre" as a distinct type (030-034). The notes observe that this conception of an "external soul or 'life-index'" links the Indian tales to a broad comparative tradition (030-035), reaching back to "the oldest Egyptian" stories and westward through many European variants (030-036) — in [[bengal|Bengal]] the soul is hidden "in a necklace, in a box," and in other versions in an egg inside a bird. The motif's antiquity and geographic spread make [[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]]'s parrot a local expression of a truly global folk idea.
