# Bodhisatta (Bodhisattva)

## Definition

[[the-prince-of-five-weapons|The Bodhisatta]] (Pali; Sanskrit: *Bodhisattva*) is a being who has resolved to attain Buddhahood and, motivated by great compassion (*karuṇā*), delays entry into nirvana in order to guide other beings toward liberation. In the context of the *Indian Fairy Tales* collection by Joseph Jacobs, the Bodhisatta is the recurring protagonist of the Jataka tales — stories of the Buddha's previous births in various forms (animal, human, and divine) — through which Buddhist moral lessons are conveyed.

## Examples in the Tales

[[the-prince-of-five-weapons|The Bodhisatta]] appears in at least five tales in this collection, each time reborn in a different form to teach a moral lesson:

- **As a White Crane** in [[the-lion-and-the-crane]] (001-001): [[the-prince-of-five-weapons|The Bodhisatta]] is born as a crane in the [[the-himalayas|Himavanta]] region. This tale illustrates gratitude and the danger of aiding the wicked, as the crane helps a lion remove a bone from its throat but must outwit [[gratitude-and-ingratitude|the ungrateful]] beast.

- **As Prince Five-Weapons** in [[the-demon-with-the-matted-hair]] (025-004): [[the-prince-of-five-weapons|The Bodhisatta]] is born as the son of [[king-brahmadatta|King Brahmadatta]]'s chief queen. Armed with five weapons and great self-confidence, he confronts the Demon of the Matted Hair. When ensnared by the demon, the Bodhisatta declares he has "the weapon of knowledge" within him (025-020), [[supernatural-beings|demons]]trating that wisdom and fearlessness conquer brute force.

- **As a Minister's Son** in [[the-talkative-tortoise]] (013-001): [[the-prince-of-five-weapons|The Bodhisatta]] is born into a minister's family and becomes the king's advisor. He skillfully cures a talkative king of his excessive speech by teaching a lesson through the story of a tortoise who fell to its death because it could not keep silent (013-002).

- **As a Prince and Heir** in [[a-lesson-for-kings]] (017-001): [[the-prince-of-five-weapons|The Bodhisatta]] returns to life as the son of [[king-brahmadatta|King Brahmadatta]]. When he ascends the throne, he rules so righteously that disputes cease entirely, teaching the lesson that just governance eliminates the need for judgment (017-002).

- **As a Pigeon** in [[the-pigeon-and-the-crow]] (029-001): [[the-prince-of-five-weapons|The Bodhisatta]] is born as a pigeon living in a rich man's kitchen, where he observes and teaches a moral lesson about greed and contentment.

## Cultural Context

[[the-prince-of-five-weapons|The Bodhisatta]] concept is fundamental to the Jataka tradition — a vast collection of approximately 547 birth-stories that form a cornerstone of Buddhist literature across South and Southeast Asia. In the Pali canon, the *Jātaka-aṭṭhakathā* (commentary on the [[jataka|Jatakas]]) presents each story with the Bodhisatta in a different incarnation, always exemplifying the *pāramitās* (perfections) that lead to Buddhahood.

Joseph Jacobs drew extensively from the Jataka corpus for his *Indian Fairy Tales* (1892), selecting tales that had migrated from Buddhist scripture into the wider Indian folk tradition. As the preface notes, these tales were "utilised in the [[jataka|Jatakas]], or [[jataka|Birth-stories of Buddha]]" (P01-004), and later spread from India through the *Panchatantra*, the *Bidpai* literature, and the *Arabian Nights* into European folklore (P01-007). [[the-prince-of-five-weapons|The Bodhisatta]] thus serves as the unifying thread connecting the collection's Buddhist moral framework to its universal folk-tale appeal.
