## Overview

The Old Woman is a key supporting character in "How the [[prince|Raja's Son]] Won the [[princess-labam|Princess Labam]]" (Chapter 2). Living alone in the country of the [[princess-labam|Princess Labam]], she initially refuses to host the young [[king|Raja]]'s son because the king has forbidden sheltering foreigners — yet she relents out of compassion, calling the prince her nephew (002-035–002-036). Over the following days, she becomes the prince's safe harbour, intermediary, and witness, enabling him to court the princess from the shadows and survive the king's deadly trials. Her role embodies the folk-tale archetype of the humble but indispensable helper whose kindness unlocks the hero's success.

## Role in the Tale

### A Reluctant Host

When the [[king|Raja]]'s son arrives in the [[princess-labam|Princess Labam]]'s country, he comes to a house where he finds the Old Woman. Her first response is to refuse him shelter: "No, I cannot let you stay with me; for our king has ordered that men from other countries may not stay in his country" (002-034). But when the prince calls her "aunty" and pleads that wild beasts will eat him if she turns him away (002-035), her compassion overcomes her fear:

> "Well," said the old woman, "you may stay here to-night; but to-morrow morning you must go away, for if the king hears you have passed the night in my house, he will have me seized and put into prison." (002-036)

### Witness to Wonders

That first evening, the Old Woman witnesses the prince's magical possessions firsthand. When she begins preparing dinner, he stops her and produces a "delicious dinner, served up on two gold plates" from his magic bag (002-037). When she offers to fetch water, he fills his magic bowl instantly (002-039). These [[supernatural-beings|demons]]trations subtly shift their relationship: the Old Woman moves from reluctant host to intrigued ally.

She also serves as the prince's informant, explaining why no lamps are lit in the country — because the [[princess-labam|Princess Labam]] herself "shines so that she lights up all the country and our houses, and we can see to do our work as if it were day" (002-041).

### Guardian of the Secret

Each morning, the Old Woman urges the prince to leave, fearing the king's wrath (002-048, 002-054). And each morning, the prince pleads illness, and she relents — "Good," said the old woman. So he stayed (002-050) — until days stretch into a longer stay. She hides him in her house "so that no one may see me" (002-055), becoming his silent co-conspirator.

When the prince finally declares his intention to marry the princess, the Old Woman attempts to dissuade him with dire warnings: "A great many [[king|Raja]]s and [[king|Raja]]s' sons have come here to marry her, and her father has had them all killed... You will be killed too, if you try. Do go away" (002-062). Yet when the prince persists, she does not betray him — she remains his steadfast ally.

### The Anchoring Place

Throughout the king's escalating trials — crushing mustard seed for oil, fighting [[supernatural-beings|demons]], beating a kettle-drum in the sky, and cutting a tree trunk with a wax hatchet — the Old Woman's house remains the prince's base of operations. After each ordeal, he returns to her: "The [[king|Raja]]'s son went back to the old woman's house" (002-073). Her simple dwelling becomes the still point around which the tale's magical action revolves.

## Key Deeds

- **Compassion Over Fear**: Despite the king's prohibition and the threat of imprisonment, she shelters the stranger — called by the universal claim of hospitality (002-035–002-036).
- **Practical Care**: She prepares to cook dinner, fetches water, and provides a bed — the ordinary domestic acts that sustain the extraordinary hero (002-037–002-039).
- **Loyal Discretion**: Though she warns the prince of danger and urges him to leave, she never betrays his presence to the authorities, even as his stay extends from one night to many (002-048, 002-050, 002-054–002-055).
- **Truthful Counsel**: She honestly tells the prince the fatal history of previous suitors, giving him the information he needs to make an informed choice (002-062).
- **Silent Witness**: She observes the prince's magical powers, the princess's radiant beauty, and the king's demands — a humble vantage point from which the entire tale is witnessed (002-037–002-073).

## Relationships

- **[[the-prince|The Raja's Son]]** — The prince who calls her "aunty" and whom she shelters. He respects her enough to heed her warnings even as he persists in his quest; her house is his safe harbour throughout the tale (002-031–002-073).
- **[[the-princess|The Princess]] [[princess-labam|Labam]]** — Though they never directly interact, the Old Woman is the one who first tells the prince about the princess's nightly radiance, setting the stage for his infatuation (002-041).
- **[[king|The King]] ([[princess-labam|Princess Labam]]'s Father)** — The unseen threat whose prohibition hangs over every moment of the Old Woman's hospitality. Her courage lies in defying this shadow-authority for the sake of kindness (002-034, 002-036, 002-062).

## Moral and Cultural Significance

The Old Woman embodies the **ethics of hospitality** (*atithi dharma*) that runs through Indian folk narrative. The conflict she faces — between obeying the king's law and honouring the stranger's need — is a moral archetype. Her choice of compassion over compliance aligns her with a venerable tradition of humble figures ([[the-forest|the wood]]cutter, the ferryman, the old hermit-woman) whose kindness to strangers proves providential.

Her role also illustrates the **gender economy of folk-tale helpers**: while male helpers (the Ant-[[king|Raja]], the Tiger) provide active, magical intervention, the Old Woman provides the domestic infrastructure — food, shelter, information, concealment — without which the hero's magical tools would be useless. Her contribution is no less essential for being quiet and domestic.

In the broader context of the Joseph Jacobs collection, she is one of several elderly female figures who serve as protectors or counsellors to young protagonists, contrasting with the more frequent portrayal of old women as magical threats or supernatural beings (cf. the transformed fairy in Chapter 8, 008-031).
