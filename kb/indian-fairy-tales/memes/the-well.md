# The Well

## Location

The well as a place is a deep shaft sunk in the countryside — sometimes disused and "ruined," sometimes fitted with a "well-wheel" for drawing irrigation water. Its defining feature is depth and enclosure: things that fall in cannot easily climb out, and someone at the bottom depends on those above for rescue. In [[the-soothsayers-son]] a "ruined well" thrown in the hero's path becomes the tale's central trap, a shaft from which the tiger reaches "the top of the well and felt himself on safe ground" only by the hero's aid (010-004, 010-007). Elsewhere a well is a working agricultural fixture — a buffalo is seen "turning a well-wheel" to raise water (009-008). A well may be freshly dug, and two new wells set side by side create a hazard a careless man can stumble into (021-015).

## Appearances in Tales

- [[the-soothsayers-son]] — A "ruined well" is thrown in the wandering hero's way; a fleeing goldsmith "fell into this well," and the hero descends to rescue those trapped inside, fearing that "some one might remain in the well and demand his assistance." The tiger climbs to "the top of the well" to safety, and the hero later returns to "the ruined well where he had released" the captives; a secret passage even runs "from the inside of a ruined well to the dungeon" (010-004, 010-005, 010-007, 010-008, 010-012).
- [[the-tiger-the-brahman-and-the-jackal]] — On his wanderings the Brahman sees "a buffalo turning a well-wheel," a working well that gives him no comfort (009-008).
- [[the-charmed-ring]] — A man "walked to the well and prepared to jump in," treating the well as the site of a fatal bargain (012-008).
- [[the-farmer-and-the-money-lender]] — Between "the two new wells" a man "fell into one, and was drowned," the well serving as the instrument of his death (021-015).

## Significance

The well is one of the collection's most reliable plot devices, and nearly every appearance turns on its depth as a trap. As a pit of entrapment, the ruined well in [[the-soothsayers-son]] gathers a whole cast — a goldsmith, a tiger, and others — who fall in and can escape only through the hero's help, and gratitude or ingratitude for that rescue then drives the rest of the tale (010-005, 010-007). As an instrument of death, a well can drown the unwary (021-015) or serve as the place where a man prepares to leap to his doom (012-008). As a symbol of hidden connection, a secret passage runs from the bottom of a ruined well to an underground dungeon, linking the world above to the prison below (010-012). In its working form, the well-wheel stands for the toil of ordinary rural life (009-008).

## Cultural or Historical Notes

The well is a central feature of rural Indian life, both as the community's water source and, through the ox- or buffalo-driven well-wheel (the *rahat* or Persian wheel), as the means of lifting water for irrigation — exactly the scene the Brahman witnesses in [[the-tiger-the-brahman-and-the-jackal]] (009-008). Abandoned or "ruined" wells were common features of the landscape, and their open shafts made them natural traps for animals and travellers alike, which is why they recur so often as the pivot of a tale's action. The motif of the creature trapped in a well and released by a passer-by — only to threaten its rescuer — is a widespread Indian folk pattern, and the well's role as a hidden vertical passage between the surface and an underground chamber reflects the same fascination with concealed subterranean spaces seen in the collection's cellars and dungeons.
