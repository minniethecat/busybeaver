## Overview

The Crane is one of the two central characters in "[[the-lion|The Lion]] and the Crane" (Chapter 1), a fable drawn from the Jataka tradition of Buddhist birth-stories. When the Lion gets a bone stuck in his throat and no other animal dares to approach, the Crane — described as having "a long neck and a long bill" — volunteers to help, extracting the bone at great personal risk (001-007–001-011). Unlike the antagonist crane of Chapter 7 ([[the-cruel-crane]]), this Crane is portrayed as a helpful and trusting figure who is ultimately rewarded only with ingratitude, serving as a vehicle for one of the collection's most direct moral lessons.

## Role in the Tale

### A Service Rendered

[[the-lion|The Lion]], unable to eat and in great pain, promises "anything you like to ask" to anyone who will remove the bone from his throat (001-007). While other animals hesitate, the Crane steps forward. Using his long bill, the Crane reaches into the Lion's mouth and extracts the bone (001-008–001-010). He then asks the Lion for a reward, but the Lion refuses with a notorious reply:

> "A service have we done thee such as never beast did before to another, in that we have taken a bone from thy throat, and thou askest a reward of us!" (001-002)

The Crane's verses (spoken in the original Pali-derived form) retort that the Lion's ingratitude is itself the crane's true protection, for no other beast would serve a lion thus (001-011–001-019).

### The Departure

After delivering his reply, the Crane simply flies away — choosing dignified withdrawal over confrontation (001-020). This departure marks the end of the fable and reinforces its moral through understatement rather than vengeance.

## Key Deeds

- **Courage**: Approaches the Lion when all other animals fear for their lives, trusting in the Lion's promise (001-007–001-008).
- **Skill**: Successfully extracts the bone from the Lion's throat using his specialized bill, performing a service no other creature could (001-010).
- **Wit**: Responds to the Lion's ingratitude with a verse-reply that exposes the moral bankruptcy of the Lion's position, turning the Lion's own logic against him (001-011–001-019).
- **Dignity**: Departs peacefully rather than escalating the conflict, embodying the patience of the bodhisatta-figure in Jataka tales (001-020).

## Relationships

- **[[the-lion|The Lion]]** ([[the-lion]]) — The Crane's foil in this fable. [[the-lion|The Lion]] promises a reward, accepts the Crane's help, then denies any obligation through sophistry. Their dynamic illustrates the Jataka theme of the powerful exploiting the weak (001-001–001-019).
- **[[the-cruel-crane|The Cruel Crane]]** ([[the-cruel-crane]]) — A distinct crane character appearing in Chapter 7. Where the Crane of Chapter 1 is helpful and trusting, the cruel crane of Chapter 7 is predatory and deceitful — together they form a complementary pair in the collection's crane-related fables.

## Moral and Cultural Significance

The Crane's story is a direct Jataka parable on ingratitude (Pali: *akataññutā*). In the Buddhist framework, the Crane represents the bodhisatta (the Buddha-to-be in a former life), while the Lion represents those who receive kindness and return evil. The fable's moral operates on two levels:

1. **The danger of trusting the powerful**: The Crane's willingness to put himself at the mercy of the Lion — even with a promise — is portrayed as naive but noble.
2. **The sufficiency of moral clarity**: The Crane's verse-reply shows that simply naming ingratitude for what it is can be a form of justice, even when no material reward is forthcoming.

This tale appears across multiple Asian folklore traditions. The notes identify a parallel in the *Jātaka* collection (No. 308, the Javasakuṇa Jātaka), where a woodpecker performs a similar service for a lion and is similarly denied thanks (030-045). A North American Indigenous parallel — where a crane extracts a bone from a wolf's throat — is also noted, making this one of the collection's most widely diffused tale-types (030-044–030-045).
