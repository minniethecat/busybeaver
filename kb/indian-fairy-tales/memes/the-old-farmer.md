## Overview

The Old Farmer is a supporting character in "Why the Fish Laughed" (Chapter 24). He is a simple country farmer who encounters the young vizier's son during the latter's journey to seek his fortune. Throughout their interactions, the Old Farmer serves as a foil to the extraordinary events unfolding around him — repeatedly bewildered by the young man's cryptic remarks, yet ultimately rewarded when his daughter marries the vizier's son (024-055). His role embodies the folk-tale motif of the humble host who unknowingly entertains greatness.

## Role in the Tale

### The Encounter

The Old Farmer first meets the young man on the road while he is "on a journey to a certain village" (024-012). Observing the young man's strange behaviour — such as talking about a fish laughing — the Old Farmer reacts with the plain-spoken incredulity of a practical man:

> "What a fool the man is!" thought the old farmer. (024-014)

Despite his bewilderment, the Old Farmer remains a generous host. When they arrive near his house, he walks about with the young man, listening to his increasingly puzzling remarks (024-019). The young man's declaration that a "largely populated city" is visible in the distance — when only the farmer's modest dwelling stands there — leaves the farmer once again astonished:

> "What does the man mean," thought the old farmer, "calling this largely populated city, when I see only my own little house?" (024-021)

### Patience and Reward

The Old Farmer's patience is tested repeatedly. At a stream crossing, the young man makes yet another bewildering observation, and the farmer thinks he "must surely be demented" (024-024). Nevertheless, the Old Farmer continues to assist him — taking off his shoes and paijamas to wade through the deep water (024-025). Eventually, however, his patience wears thin:

> The old farmer left him in despair, and entered his house. (024-029)

Yet the Old Farmer's hospitality is not forgotten. When the truth of the young man's identity is revealed, the farmer is brought before the court alongside the vizier's son, and "great attention was shown to him" (024-047). In the dénouement, the vizier's son marries the Old Farmer's daughter in "a most happy marriage" (024-055), transforming the simple farmer into the father-in-law of a high-ranking nobleman.

## Key Deeds

- **Hospitality**: Despite his confusion at the young man's strange words, the Old Farmer provides lodging and companionship to the traveller, fulfilling the traditional duty of a host (024-012, 024-019).
- **Plain-Spoken Honesty**: His repeated internal monologues ("What a fool the man is!", "surely be demented!") represent the voice of common sense confronting the inexplicable (024-014, 024-021, 024-024).
- **Patience**: Endures the young man's eccentricities through multiple episodes before finally withdrawing in despair (024-029).
- **Reward by Proxy**: Though he does not actively pursue advancement, his daughter's marriage to the vizier's son elevates his entire family (024-055).

## Relationships

- **[[the-viziers-son|The Vizier's Son]]** — The young traveller whom the Old Farmer hosts. The farmer's repeated bafflement at the young man's remarks provides the tale's comic relief, but his hospitality is ultimately repaid handsomely (024-012–024-055).
- **The Old Farmer's Daughter** — Her marriage to the vizier's son closes the tale on a note of resolution and reward for the farmer's household (024-055).

## Moral and Cultural Significance

The Old Farmer represents the **dignity of the common man** in Indian folk narrative. He is not heroic, clever, or especially wise — he is ordinary, and his ordinariness is precisely the point. His character anchors the tale's more fantastical elements (talking animals, mysterious prophecies) in the recognisable world of village life.

His story also illustrates a recurring folk-tale pattern: **the reward of hospitality to strangers**. Though the Old Farmer does not know his guest's true identity, his willingness to offer shelter and company — however grudgingly at times — is repaid beyond anything he could have anticipated. This theme resonates across the broader Indian folk tradition, where the unknown guest is often a [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]]d prince, a god in mortal form, or a bearer of hidden fortune.
