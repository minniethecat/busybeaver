## Overview

King Mallika is the ruler of Kosala and a central figure in "A Lesson for Kings" (Chapter 17), a Jataka-derived tale about royal virtue and humility. When his chariot meets that of King Brahma-datta of [[benares|Benares]] on a narrow cart-track — where only one can pass — the ensuing standoff becomes a test not of power but of righteousness. Mallika is revealed to be Brahma-datta's precise equal: equally aged, equally powerful, equally wealthy, and equally virtuous. His role in the narrative is that of the **mirror-king** — a peer whose identical excellence forces Brahma-datta to confront his own imperfections and learn the lesson of humility that gives the tale its name.

## Role in the Tale

### The Standoff

The tale's inciting incident is elegantly simple: two royal chariots meet on a narrow road. At that time "the king of Kosala, Mallika by name, was also ruling his kingdom" (017-006), and his charioteer issues the challenge:

> "Then the charioteer of Mallika the king said to the charioteer of Brahma-datta, 'Take thy carriage out of the way!'" (017-007)

The charioteer's demand escalates into a formal declaration of his master's greatness:

> "In the kingdom of Kosala, the great king Mallika. Take thy carriage out of the way!" (017-009)

### The Mirror of Virtue

What follows is unique in the Jacobs collection: a confrontation resolved not by combat but by comparison. The two charioteers — each defending his own king — list their masters' attributes: age, caste, wealth, kingdom, army, and moral conduct. In every category, Brahma-datta and Mallika prove to be identical.

The tale deploys a formal verse to capture Mallika's defining quality:

> "The mild by mildness, does Mallika" (017-015)

This line — part of a longer Pali verse retained in Jacobs' translation — characterises Mallika as a king who governs through gentleness rather than force. The alliterative construction ("mild by mildness") suggests a ruler whose power is expressed through restraint, echoing the Buddhist ethical framework that underlies the Jataka source.

### The Resolution

When both kings are revealed as equals in every measurable dimension, the standoff cannot be resolved by supremacy — neither can claim superior right. The tale's resolution comes when Brahma-datta, confronted by a mirror-image of his own virtue, realises that the lesson applies to both kings equally:

> "Both Mallika the king and his charioteer acknowledged the justice of what had been said." (017-031)

Mallika's role is complete: he has served as the instrument of Brahma-datta's moral education simply by being exactly who he is — a righteous king whose existence proves that virtue is not the monopoly of any single ruler.

## Key Deeds

- **The Standoff**: His chariot meets Brahma-datta's on the narrow track, creating the narrative friction that drives the tale's moral inquiry (017-006–017-007).
- **The Declaration of Equality**: Through his charioteer, Mallika's attributes are publicly declared and measured against Brahma-datta's — revealing perfect parity (017-009).
- **The Virtue of Mildness**: Characterised by the tale's verse as a ruler who governs through gentleness ("The mild by mildness"), embodying the Buddhist ideal of the righteous king (017-015).
- **Acknowledgement of Justice**: Accepts the resolution with grace, [[supernatural-beings|demons]]trating that humility is a virtue practised, not merely preached (017-031).

## Relationships

- **King Brahma-datta of [[benares|Benares]]** — Mallika's mirror and equal. Their encounter is a philosophical duel rather than a martial one, and both emerge wiser. Brahma-datta is the nominal protagonist, but Mallika is the indispensable catalyst — without his equal virtue, there would be no lesson (017-006, 017-031).
- **Mallika's Charioteer** — His spokesman in the confrontation. The charioteer articulates Mallika's greatness, but also — crucially — acknowledges when the contest has reached its natural conclusion, accepting the justice of the resolution (017-007, 017-031).

## Moral and Cultural Significance

King Mallika is one of the few named historical or semi-historical kings in the Jacobs collection, and his inclusion reflects the tale's Jataka origins. In Buddhist literature, the kingdom of Kosala (with its capital at Savatthi) was a major political power during the Buddha's lifetime, and King Pasenadi of Kosala appears in numerous suttas as a royal patron. The name "Mallika" — associated with Queen Mallika, Pasenadi's chief queen in Buddhist texts — may represent a conflation or adaptation of these historical figures into folk-narrative form.

The tale's central device — two kings of equal virtue meeting on a narrow road — is a **philosophical parable in narrative form**. Mallika's function is to prove, by his very existence, that righteousness is not unique. If two kings can be equally good, then goodness itself is a standard to which all must aspire, not a possession any single ruler can claim as his own. This is the "lesson for kings" of the tale's title: humility is not weakness but the recognition of a universal moral order to which even monarchs are subject.

In the broader Indian narrative tradition, Mallika represents the ideal of the **dharmic king** — a ruler whose authority derives not from conquest or birth but from conformity to moral law (dharma). His confrontation with Brahma-datta is, in essence, a dramatisation of the principle that "dhamma protects those who protect dhamma" — a concept central to Buddhist political philosophy and, in adapted form, to the wider Indian understanding of righteous kingship.
