## Overview

The Charioteer is a significant supporting character in "A Lesson for Kings" (Chapter 17), a Jataka tale in which the [[bodhisatta|future Buddha]] is reborn as Prince Brahma-datta of [[benares|Benares]]. When King Brahma-datta — ruling with such perfect justice that the Hall of Justice stands empty — goes searching the countryside in [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]] for someone to tell him his faults, his charioteer drives him. At a narrow cart-track, they meet the chariot of [[king-mallika|King Mallika]] of Kosala, and the two charioteers engage in a contest of words to determine whose king is more righteous (017-007–017-031). The Charioteer's eloquent proclamation of his master's virtues not only wins right-of-way but articulates the tale's central moral philosophy.

## Role in the Tale

### The Narrow Road

The Charioteer drives King Brahma-datta's chariot as the [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]]d monarch searches the countryside for a fault-finder. At "a low cart-track with precipitous sides, where there was no space for a chariot to get out of the way," they encounter another chariot — that of [[king-mallika|King Mallika]] of Kosala, likewise travelling in [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]] (017-006). [[king-mallika|Mallika]]'s charioteer demands right-of-way:

> "Take thy chariot out of the way!" (017-007)

The Charioteer replies with dignity, naming his master: "In this chariot sitteth the lord over the kingdom of [[benares|Benares]], the great king Brahma-datta" (017-008). But the other charioteer counters with an identical claim for [[king-mallika|King Mallika]].

### The Wisdom Contest

Realising both chariots carry kings, the Charioteer devises a systematic method for resolving the impasse. He inquires about age, kingdom extent, army, wealth, renown, country, caste, tribe, and family — and finds that "in respect of army and wealth and renown, and the countries in which they lived, and their caste and their tribe and their family, they were just on a par!" (017-011). His final criterion is righteousness, and he asks [[king-mallika|Mallika]]'s charioteer to describe his king's virtues.

[[king-mallika|Mallika]]'s charioteer proclaims: "The strong he overthrows by strength, the mild by mildness... the good he conquers by goodness, and the wicked by wickedness too" (017-014–017-019). The Charioteer, unimpressed, delivers a devastating rebuttal: "If these are his *virtues*, where are then his faults?" (017-022).

Then, called upon to proclaim his own king's virtues, the Charioteer delivers the tale's moral centrepiece:

> "Anger he conquers by calmness,
> And by goodness the wicked;
> The stingy he conquers by gifts,
> And by truth the speaker of lies.
> Such is the nature of *this* king!
> Move out of the way, O charioteer!" (017-025–017-030)

### The Victory

The Charioteer's words are so compelling that [[king-mallika|King Mallika]] and his charioteer do not merely yield right-of-way — they alight from their chariot, take out the horses, and physically remove their chariot to make way (017-031). The Charioteer has not argued or threatened; he has simply articulated a superior moral philosophy, and that articulation itself is victorious.

## Key Deeds

- **Methodical Judgment**: When confronted with an impasse between equals, the Charioteer systematically compares every measurable attribute — age, kingdom, army, wealth, renown, country, caste, tribe, and family — before arriving at the one that truly matters: righteousness (017-010–017-011).
- **The Defining Question**: His retort — "If these are his *virtues*, where are then his faults?" — exposes the amoral pragmatism of [[king-mallika|Mallika]]'s rule in a single line (017-022).
- **The Verse Proclamation**: He delivers a four-line stanza that distils the Buddhist ethical ideal into memorable poetic form, each line a paradox of moral victory through non-retaliation (017-025–017-030).

## Relationships

- **King Brahma-datta (the Future Buddha)** — The Charioteer's master. Though the king is the tale's protagonist, the Charioteer is his voice in the world — the one who articulates and defends his master's virtue to outsiders. Their relationship [[supernatural-beings|demons]]trates how a righteous ruler's influence radiates outward, shaping even the speech and conduct of his servants (017-005, 017-007–017-031).
- **The Charioteer of [[king-mallika|Mallika]]** — His counterpart and opponent. Where [[king-mallika|Mallika]]'s charioteer boasts of his king's pragmatic ruthlessness (overthrowing strong, mild, good, and wicked alike), the Charioteer of [[benares|Benares]] counters with a philosophy of transformative virtue (017-014–017-030).
- **[[king-mallika|King Mallika]]** — Though they never speak directly, the Charioteer's words move [[king-mallika|Mallika]] to voluntary submission — a [[supernatural-beings|demons]]tration that moral authority can persuade where force cannot (017-031).

## Moral and Cultural Significance

The Charioteer is more than a servant — he is the **moral spokesman** of the tale. In the Jataka framework, his eloquence and wisdom are extensions of his master's righteousness, suggesting that a just king creates a just society, right down to the words of his charioteer. This is a distinctly Buddhist vision of kingship: the king's *dhamma* (righteousness) is not a private virtue but a radiating force that shapes all who serve him.

The Charioteer's role also challenges assumptions about **class and wisdom** in folk narrative. He is not a minister, a sage, or a brahman — he is a driver of chariots, a man of practical skill. Yet it is he, not the kings, who designs the contest of virtues and delivers its decisive judgment. This inversion — the servant as moral arbiter between kings — is characteristic of the democratic impulse in Jataka storytelling, where wisdom is distributed by karma and character, not by birth.

The fourfold verse he recites (017-025–017-030) is one of the collection's most concentrated expressions of [[buddhist-framework|Buddhist ethics]]: anger met with calmness, wickedness with goodness, stinginess with gifts, and lies with truth. It echoes the *Dhammapada*'s teaching that "hatred is not overcome by hatred; it is overcome by love" — a principle the Charioteer [[supernatural-beings|demons]]trates not by preaching, but by winning a roadside argument with poetry.
