# Loving Laili

## Overview

Laili is the beautiful princess at the heart of the tale [[loving-laili]] (Chapter 8), the daughter of a king (008-003) whose single-minded devotion to [[majnun|Prince Majnun]] drives the whole story. Before she has ever seen him, an angel sent by [[khuda|Khuda]] comes to her in a dream in the form of a fakir and commands her whom she is to marry (008-004); waking, she tells her father, "[[khuda|Khuda]] has ordered me to marry no one but [[majnun|Majnun]]" (008-005), and in her longing she is described as "half mad" (008-005).

Her love is a devotion that outlasts death itself. Reduced to a heap of ashes, transformed into a dog, a doe, and other forms, Laili is again and again restored, until at last she is reunited with [[majnun|Majnun]] and made "young again" (008-016). Her name gives the tale its title — "Loving Laili" — and her constancy is its central virtue.

## Role in the Tale

Laili is the pattern of the faithful lover. From the moment of her dream she can speak of nothing else, keeping up the ceaseless cry, "[[majnun|Majnun]], [[majnun|Majnun]]; I want [[majnun|Majnun]]" (008-007), a refrain repeated so insistently that even a [[the-fish|Rohu fish]] and the creatures around her come to hear it (008-010, 008-024). Her longing sets the plot in motion, drawing the fakir and the angel to carry word across many rivers to [[majnun|Majnun]]'s country (008-009, 008-011).

Once united with [[majnun|Majnun]] amid great rejoicings (008-017), her trials are only beginning. When her husband leaves her a "little heap of ashes" in the jungle (008-020), Laili does not perish; the fakir gathers her up and hides her in [[king-dantal|King Dantal]]'s garden (008-023, 008-025). There she survives through a sequence of [[disguise-and-deception|transformation]]s, changing into "a pretty little dog" (008-029) and other shapes to remain near her beloved, until she can step forth "young and far more beautiful" than before (008-036).

## Key Deeds and Appearances

- **Commanded in a dream** — [[khuda|Khuda]] sends an angel as a fakir to tell her she must marry no one but [[majnun|Majnun]] (008-004, 008-005).
- **The ceaseless cry** — half mad with longing, she repeats "[[majnun|Majnun]], [[majnun|Majnun]]; I want [[majnun|Majnun]]" until her devotion reaches across the world (008-007, 008-009).
- **Emerges from the fish** — carried across many rivers, she is at last cut free and "out jumped Laili" (008-013).
- **Marriage to [[majnun|Majnun]]** — united with the prince amid great rejoicings, the two stay together happily for a time (008-017).
- **Survives as ashes** — turned into "a little heap of ashes" in the jungle, she is rescued and hidden by the fakir in [[king-dantal|King Dantal]]'s garden (008-020, 008-023, 008-025).
- **The [[disguise-and-deception|transformation]]s** — she becomes "a pretty little dog" and other creatures to stay near [[majnun|Majnun]] (008-029), then steps out "young and far more beautiful" than before (008-036).
- **Made young again** — she prays to [[khuda|Khuda]] to "make us both young again, and then we shall be married" (008-016), and the reunion restores them both (008-026).

## Relationships

- **[[majnun|Prince Majnun]]** — her destined husband, whom she is commanded to marry and for whom she endures every [[disguise-and-deception|transformation]]; when reunited he cries, "I see you are Laili come back to me" (008-040). See [[loving-laili]].
- **[[khuda|Khuda]]** — the divine authority whose command through an angel binds her love to [[majnun|Majnun]] and who restores her youth (008-004, 008-016).
- **[[king-dantal|King Dantal]]** — [[majnun|Majnun]]'s father, in whose garden the fakir conceals the transformed Laili until the lovers can be reunited (008-023, 008-026).

## Moral or Cultural Significance

Laili — her name echoing the celebrated Laila of the Laila-[[majnun|Majnun]] romance — is the exemplar of undying, faithful love. Where many heroines are won through the deeds of a prince, Laili wins her own reunion by the sheer persistence of devotion: her love is stronger than madness, than death, and than the repeated undoing of her very body. The pattern of the lover reduced to ashes and restored through [[disguise-and-deception|transformation]] gives the tale a note of resurrection, framing steadfast love as a force that even death cannot finally break — the very ideal of [[faithfulness-and-loyalty]] embodied in a heroine. The closing refrain of the collection remembers her as one who "became young again" (008-050) — love rewarded with renewed life.
