# Gratitude and Ingratitude

## Definition

Gratitude — the obligation to repay a service or an act of kindness — and its opposite, ingratitude, form one of the most frequently repeated moral axes of the *Indian Fairy Tales* collection. Tale after tale contrasts the creature or person who faithfully returns a good turn with the villain who accepts help and then betrays or forgets the helper. The theme is inherited directly from the Buddhist Jataka birth-stories, where a wrong done to the Bodhisatta is often revealed to be the work of Devadatta, the Buddha's treacherous cousin, in a former life.

## Examples in the Tales

- **The ungrateful lion** in [[the-lion-and-the-crane]]: after a crane risks its life to pull a bone from the lion's throat, the lion refuses any reward, and the crane pronounces that "In him there is no gratitude" (001-014). The tale closes with the revelation that the lion "was Devadatta the Traitor, but the white crane was I myself" (001-021), tying ingratitude to the archetypal betrayer.

- **The ungrateful tiger** in [[the-tiger-the-brahman-and-the-jackal]]: freed from a cage by a kind Brahman, the tiger promptly resolves to eat him, scoffing, "You are a fool to expect gratitude!" (009-008) — a blunt statement of the theme's dark side.

- **The grateful serpent** in [[the-gold-giving-serpent]]: a Brahman feeds milk to a serpent living in an ant-hill, and each day afterward finds a gold denar left in the bowl (015-002), the beast repaying kindness with steady generosity.

- **The grateful serpent-friend** in [[the-soothsayers-son]]: a serpent saved from danger will not depart "without showing its gratitude," addressing its rescuer as "life of my life" (010-006) and later commanding all serpents to aid him.

- **Grateful animals, ungrateful man** in [[the-charmed-ring]]: a merchant's son spends his money to rescue a cat and a dog (012-008), and their master longs "to repay you for your great kindness to me" (012-005); when the boy delivers a raja's son, the raja shows "gratitude to him for having delivered his son" (012-012), while a human thief betrays him.

- **Repented ingratitude** in [[the-ivory-city-and-its-fairy-princess]]: a princess who has wronged her benefactor is finally asked "whether she had repented of her ingratitude," and weeps in acknowledgement (026-109).

## Cultural Context

The pairing of gratitude and ingratitude is a hallmark of the Jataka tradition, the Buddhist birth-stories in which the Bodhisatta (the [[bodhisatta|future Buddha]]) exemplifies the moral perfections across successive lives. In these stories the animal or human who betrays a kindness is frequently identified in the closing frame as Devadatta, the Buddha's envious cousin, while the wronged party is the Bodhisatta himself. Joseph Jacobs, compiling his *Indian Fairy Tales* (1892), drew heavily on this corpus, and the notes to the collection remark on the migration of the "grateful animals" motif through the folk literature (030-028). The persistent moral — that beasts often prove more faithful than men — reflects the tales' didactic origins as vehicles for [[buddhist-framework|Buddhist ethics]].
