# The Gold-giving Serpent

## Overview

The gold-giving serpent is the supernatural figure at the heart of the fifteenth tale, [[the-gold-giving-serpent]]. It is "a great hooded snake" that the poor Brahman [[the-farmer|Haridatta]] sees "creeping out of an ant-hill" in his field, and which he takes to be "the guardian deity of the field" (015-001). As one of the collection's [[supernatural-beings]], the serpent is a benevolent field-spirit whose favour is real but conditional: it rewards devotion generously and punishes greed absolutely. Its story is a tightly drawn fable of [[greed-in-folklore]], in which a source of steady blessing is destroyed by the human craving to seize everything at once.

## Role in the Tale

The serpent is both benefactor and judge. To the reverent Brahman who offers it milk, it gives a daily gold coin, embodying the reward of humble, patient worship. But when that worship curdles into greed — first in the son, then in the father — the serpent becomes the agent of consequence, killing the attacker and severing the relationship for good. It thus functions as a moral fulcrum: its generosity measures the Brahman's piety, and its withdrawal measures his fall. The serpent's dwelling in an ant-hill and its identity as a "guardian deity" tie it to the folk tradition of the naga, the sacred serpent-guardian of place and treasure.

## Key Deeds and Appearances

- **Accepting worship and giving gold (015-001, 015-002).** Believing his poor harvests are due to neglecting the field's guardian, [[the-farmer|Haridatta]] brings milk to the ant-hill and begs forgiveness: "O Guardian of this Field!... pray forgive me" (015-002). The next morning he finds "a gold denar in the bowl," and thereafter "every day the same thing occurred: he gave milk to the serpent and found a gold denar" (015-002).

- **Killing the [[greed-in-folklore|greedy]] son (015-003).** When [[the-farmer|Haridatta]] sends his son with the milk, the son reasons, "This ant-hill is surely full of golden denars; I'll kill the serpent, and take them all for myself," and strikes it on the head with a cudgel (015-003). The serpent "escaped death by the will of fate, and in a rage bit the Brahman's son with its sharp fangs, and he fell down dead at once" (015-003).

- **Severing the bond (015-004).** When the grieving father returns and praises it, the serpent appears "only with its head out of the opening of the ant-hill" and pronounces the relationship over:

  > "'Tis greed that brings you here, and makes you even forget the loss of your son. From this time forward friendship between us is impossible. Your son struck me in youthful ignorance, and I have bitten him to death. How can I forget the blow with the cudgel? And how can you forget the pain and grief at the loss of your son?" (015-004)

  It gives him a single "costly pearl," commands "Come back no more," and disappears, ending the daily gift forever (015-004).

## Relationships

- **[[the-farmer|Haridatta]]** — the poor Brahman farmer who first worships the serpent and receives its daily gold; a devotee whose renewed approach after his son's death the serpent reads as greed, and to whom it grants one final pearl before breaking off all friendship (015-001, 015-002, 015-004).
- **[[the-farmer|Haridatta]]'s son** — the youth who, sent in his father's place, covets the whole hoard and attacks the serpent with a cudgel, and is bitten dead for it; his greed shatters the bond of worship and reward (015-003).

## Moral or Cultural Significance

The gold-giving serpent is a distilled emblem of blessing forfeited through greed. Its logic is exact and unsentimental: patient devotion earns a daily coin, but the grasp for the entire treasure ends in death and estrangement. The serpent's own words draw the moral with painful clarity — the blow with the cudgel and the grief of a lost son are wounds neither party can forget, so "friendship between us is impossible" (015-004). As a guardian naga of the field, the serpent belongs to a deep Indian tradition of serpent-deities who bestow fortune on the reverent and ruin on the rapacious, and its tale stands beside the collection's other studies of [[greed-in-folklore|avarice]] as a warning that the desire to have everything at once destroys even a gift that would have given freely forever.
