## Overview

Sthuladatta is a wealthy householder (grihapati) who appears at the opening of the tale "[[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]]" (Chapter 11). He employs the poor and foolish Brahman [[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]] as a servant — a decision that sets in motion the entire chain of events that follow. Sthuladatta functions as the catalyst figure: by giving the incompetent [[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]] a place in his household, he unwittingly enables the Brahman's rise to fame through a series of lucky coincidences.

## Role in the Tale

Sthuladatta is introduced in the very first sentence of "[[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]]" as the employer who takes in the titular character:

> There was a certain Brahman in a certain village, named Harisarman. He was poor and foolish and in the constant habit of lounging about, which got him the name of "the idle fellow." His wealthy patron, a householder named Sthuladatta, employed him to look after the house and fields. (011-001)

When [[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]]'s wife complains that her husband has not received the respect she believes he deserves, Sthuladatta's household becomes the arena for [[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]]'s scheme. Acting on his wife's urging, [[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]] steals the horse that was to be part of a wedding procession, hides it, and then "discovers" it through pretended divination — all within Sthuladatta's domain (011-002).

Sthuladatta's role in the narrative is brief but structurally essential. He represents the established social order — a prosperous householder who provides the stage upon which the [[trickster-figure|trickster]] figure ([[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]]) can perform. Without Sthuladatta's initial act of employment, the tale's central irony — that a fool can rise to royal favor through luck alone — could not unfold.

Sthuladatta's sole direct mention occurs in the tale's opening lines. After [[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]] is summoned to the king's court (011-003), the narrative shifts entirely away from the householder, and Sthuladatta is not mentioned again. This pattern — a named patron who vanishes after the protagonist departs — is characteristic of the frame-establishing figures in Indian folk narratives.

## Relationships

- **[[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]]** — his employee; a poor, foolish Brahman whom Sthuladatta employs as a household servant (011-001). The relationship is hierarchical: Sthuladatta is the patron, [[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]] the dependent.
- **[[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]]'s Wife** — his servant's wife, who first voices discontent with [[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]]'s treatment and pushes him to demand more respect from Sthuladatta's household (011-002).

## Moral and Cultural Significance

Sthuladatta embodies the figure of the wealthy *grihapati* (householder) in classical Indian society — a landowner and patron who employs Brahmans and servants within his domestic sphere. His role illustrates the [[caste-in-folklore|social hierarchy]] of village life: even a Brahman (by caste) could fall into servitude under a wealthy commoner. The tale's silence about Sthuladatta after the opening also reflects a narrative economy common in Indian folklore — once the protagonist's journey is launched, the domestic anchor figure is no longer needed. In this sense, Sthuladatta represents the stable, static world that the [[trickster-figure|trickster]]-hero leaves behind.
