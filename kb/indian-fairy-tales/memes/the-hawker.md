The **Hawker** is the human antagonist of "[[the-ass-in-the-lions-skin|The Ass in the Lion's Skin]]" (Chapter 20 of Joseph Jacobs's *Indian Fairy Tales*, 1892), a Jataka fable set in the reign of Brahma-datta in [[benares|Benares]]. A travelling trader who uses an ass to carry his goods from village to village, the Hawker devises a cunning deception: at each stop, he drapes his ass in a lion's skin and turns it loose in the rice and barley fields, knowing the watchmen will mistake it for a lion and keep their distance (020-002). His scheme succeeds — until the ass brays.

The Hawker is not a cruel master; he is a pragmatist exploiting a loophole in local vigilance. His punishment is not moral opprobrium but economic loss: when [[the-village|the villagers]] discover the deception and beat the ass to death, the Hawker arrives too late to save either his animal or his livelihood. In the Jataka moral economy, he is both deceiver and deceived — the ass's inability to maintain the [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]] is as much a failure of the Hawker's plan as it is of the ass's nature.

## Overview

The Hawker is introduced as an itinerant trader "who used to go from place to place, trafficking in goods carried by an ass" (020-002). His life is one of routine and repetition — unpack at each village, cook breakfast, release the [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]]d ass, collect it in the evening. The lion-skin [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]] is described matter-of-factly, as a tool of the trade rather than a grand deceit:

> "At each place he came to, when he took the pack down from the ass's back, he used to clothe him in a lion's skin, and turn him loose in the rice and barley fields." (020-002)

The scheme works because the village watchmen, seeing what they take to be a lion, "dared not go near him" (020-002). The Hawker's success rests entirely on the gap between appearance and reality — a gap that the ass, through its own nature, eventually closes.

One day the Hawker stops in a village, dresses the ass, and goes to cook his breakfast (020-003). The watchmen, this time, sound the alarm — they return with the entire village, armed, blowing conch shells and beating drums. The noise terrifies the ass, who forgets its [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]] and brays — "the bray of an ass!" (020-003). The [[bodhisatta|future Buddha]], born as a peasant in that village, pronounces the verdict in verse:

> "This is not a lion's roaring,
> Nor a tiger's, nor a panther's;
> Dressed in a lion's skin,
> 'Tis a wretched ass that roars!" (020-005–020-008)

The villagers beat the ass until its bones break and carry off the lion's skin. The Hawker arrives to find his animal dying, and speaks the tale's closing verse:

> "Long might the ass,
> Clad in a lion's skin,
> Have fed on the barley green.
> But he brayed!
> And that moment he came to ruin." (020-010–020-014)

The ass dies on the spot — and the Hawker, his deception exposed and his means of transport destroyed, is left with nothing.

## Role in the Tale

The Hawker serves as the tale's instigator and its economic counterpoint. His role is defined by three movements:

**The Deviser.** The Hawker creates the deception — the lion skin — and profits from it repeatedly. The fable does not moralise about this; the deception is presented as clever, not wicked. The Hawker is simply a man of commerce solving a logistical problem (grazing his ass for free).

**The Absent Master.** The Hawker is offstage during the tale's climax. While [[the-village|the villagers]] surround the ass, he is "getting his own breakfast cooked" (020-003). This absence is structural: the deception cannot be maintained without the deceiver present to manage it. Left to its own devices, the ass reveals its true nature.

**The Ruined Trader.** The Hawker's closing verse is not a lament for the ass but a statement of economic logic — the ass *could* have fed indefinitely on the barley if it had only kept silent. The Hawker is the tale's voice of worldly wisdom, recognising that the scheme failed not because it was wrong but because it was unsustainable.

## Relationships

- **[[the-ass|The Ass]]** — The Hawker's beast of burden and the instrument of his scheme. Their relationship is purely economic — the ass is never described with affection or concern — but it is also symbiotic. When the ass dies, the Hawker's livelihood dies with it (020-015).
- **[[the-prince-of-five-weapons|The Bodhisatta]]** — The [[bodhisatta|future Buddha]], born as a peasant, is the Hawker's undoing. His verses expose the deception, but he acts as revealer of truth, not punisher. The Hawker is not named or shamed; he simply arrives to find his scheme in ruins.

## Moral Significance

The Hawker's story is a Jataka fable about the limits of deception. The deception is successful as long as the ass remains silent — as long as the costume is not tested. But the costume *will* be tested, because it is the ass's nature to bray when frightened. The Hawker's error is not moral but practical: he built a scheme that depended on an animal not behaving like an animal.

The tale pairs the Hawker's pragmatic wisdom with the [[bodhisatta|Bodisat]]'s moral clarity. The Hawker sees that the scheme *could* have worked; the [[bodhisatta|Bodisat]] sees that it *had* to fail. Between them, the fable makes its point: nature cannot be [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]]d indefinitely. The lion's skin is a costume; the bray is the truth.
