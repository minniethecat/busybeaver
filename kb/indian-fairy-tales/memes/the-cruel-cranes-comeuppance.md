# The Cruel Crane's Comeuppance

## Summary

A crane had been deceiving the fish of a drying pond, offering to carry them one by one to a fine large pond covered with lotuses — and instead eating each on a Varana-tree and heaping their bones at its foot (007-016, 007-018). One crab remained. Suspecting treachery, the crab agreed to be carried only if it could hold the crane's neck with its claws, boasting that "we crabs have a famous grip" (007-026). Aloft, the crab saw the heap of fish-bones and understood, and when the crane tried to shake it off, the crab tightened its hold and forced the bird down to the pond's edge — where it "cut through its neck as clean as one would cut a lotus-stalk with a hunting-knife" (007-034).

> So the crab caught hold of his neck with his claws as securely as a smith… (007-027)

## Context

The tale is a Jataka: "Long ago the [[bodhisatta|Bodisat]] was born to a forest life" as the tree-spirit standing near the lotus pond, who witnesses the whole affair (007-001). The crane had schemed, "I must outwit these fish somehow" (007-003), and won [[the-fish|the fishes]]' trust by ferrying one as proof before betraying them all. The crab is the crane's intended final victim; the confrontation between them is the climax and reversal of the story.

## Participants

- **[[the-cruel-crane]]** — the treacherous bird who devours the trusting fish and then attempts the same trick on the crab, only to be undone.
- **[[the-crab|The Crab]]** — the wary intended victim whose "famous grip" turns the tables; upon seeing the bone-heap it resolves that the crane "shall lose his life this day."
- **[[the-fish|The Fish]]** — the crane's earlier victims, whose bones by the Varana-tree betray the deception to the crab.

The event is the turning point of [[the-cruel-crane-outwitted]], the seventh tale of the collection.

## Outcome or Consequence

The crane pays with its life for its treachery, beheaded by the very creature it meant to eat. As the crab realized the danger, it reasoned that if the crane once got hold of fish "he would never let them go" (007-025), and acted first. The crane's dying plea for mercy goes unanswered, and the crab, once returned to safety, executes it. Poetic justice is complete: the deceiver is destroyed by his intended prey.

## Cultural or Moral Significance

The episode is a model of the "biter bit" motif — treachery recoiling fatally upon the one who practises it. It pairs with the collection's other crane tale, [[the-lion-and-the-crane]], but inverts its lesson: here the crane is the villain rather than the wronged benefactor. The story warns against betraying trust for greed, and celebrates the wit of the weak who, by caution and courage, overcome a stronger and craftier foe.
