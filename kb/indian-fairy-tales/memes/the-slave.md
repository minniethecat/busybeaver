## Overview

The Slave appears in the *Indian Fairy Tales* collection as a figure at the absolute bottom of the [[caste-in-folklore|social hierarchy]], yet one who is frequently present at crucial moments in the narrative. From the slaves of King Sarkap who carry poisoned trays to [[rasalu|Raja Rasalu]] (019-098), to the slave-girl whose sons are presented as royal heirs in "[[the-prince|The Prince]] and the Fakir" (023-015), slaves in these tales illuminate the extreme power dynamics of the courts in which the stories unfold.

## Role in the Tales

### Raja Rasalu (Chapter 19) — The Slaves of Sarkap

King Sarkap's slaves appear at several critical moments. When [[rasalu|Rasalu]] arrives, "Sarkap sent slaves to him with trays of sweetmeats" (019-098) — but the offering is poisoned, as [[rasalu|Rasalu]]'s dog dies after following the slaves (019-100). [[rasalu|Rasalu]] sends the slaves back with a defiant message: "Go back to Sarkap, slaves! and tell him that [[rasalu|Rasalu]] does not accept his favours on such terms" (019-101). Later, when Sarkap is defeated at chaupur, he "bade his slaves remove Bhaunr, the Arab steed" (019-115). The slaves function here as extensions of the king's will — instruments of both hospitality and treachery.

### The Prince and the Fakir (Chapter 23) — Slave-Girls' Sons

In a striking plot detail, the King brings "two slave-girls' sons" and presents them as if they were the Princess's own children (023-015). The handmaiden who correctly identifies the Princess's true child is herself called a "slave-girl" by the people — "The slave-girl has made a mistake" (023-035) — to which she replies with dignity: "The slave-girl has made no mistake" (023-036). This exchange highlights the blurred line between slave and servant in the tales, and the way low-status figures can possess moral clarity.

### Other Tales

In "[[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]]" (Chapter 4), slaves serve as constant attendants: "the Magician or one of his slaves was always in the room" (004-054), creating an atmosphere of surveillance and menace. In "The Charmed Ring" (Chapter 12), the ring's power is [[supernatural-beings|demons]]trated by its ability to compel a terrible death "whose slave it therefore was" (012-012). In "The Talkative Tortoise" (Chapter 13), [[the-jungle|the wild]] ducks dismiss the tortoise's cry with contempt: "What is that to you, you wretched slaves!" (013-009).

The most vivid episode comes when the slaves deliver poisoned sweetmeats to [[rasalu|Rasalu]]:

> "Sarkap sent slaves to him with trays of sweetmeats" (019-098). Now when the slaves brought the trays to Raja Rasalu, he gave a portion to his dog, which had followed the slave, and lo! the dog died. (019-099)

## The Aesop Connection

The collection's preface notes that fables "under the name of the Samian slave, Æsop, were derived from India" (P01-004). This connection places the slave figure at the very origin of the fable tradition: one of the world's most famous storytellers was himself a slave, and his tales traveled from India westward through trade routes. The slave in Indian folklore is thus not only a character within the stories but also, in a sense, their original teller.

## Relationships

- **[[king|The King]] / Master** — the slave exists at the master's absolute mercy, an instrument of royal will with no independent status
- **The Magician / Sorcerer** — in tales like "[[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]]," slaves serve supernatural masters, adding an element of magical menace to their surveillance role (004-054)
- **The Hero** — slaves often serve as messengers between king and hero, particularly in "[[rasalu|Raja Rasalu]]," where they become vehicles for both poisoned offerings and defiant replies

## Moral and Cultural Significance

The presence of slaves throughout these tales reflects historical social structures while also serving a narrative purpose: the slave represents the extreme of powerlessness, against which the hero's courage and the handmaiden's quiet dignity are measured. The "slave-girl" who insists she has made no mistake (023-036) embodies a recurring message in Indian folklore — that moral authority does not derive from social status. The slave who speaks truth to power, however quietly, is a figure of moral weight.
