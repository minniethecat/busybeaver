# Princess Labam

## Overview

Princess Labam is the beautiful and radiant princess of the tale [[how-the-rajas-son-won-the-princess-labam]] (Chapter 2). She is famed far and wide; a prince first hears of "the beautiful Princess Labam" from his father's parrots and is at once seized with a longing to see her (002-002). So powerful is his desire that he resolves, "I must go," and sets out to find her country though he does not know where it lies (002-007, 002-010).

Labam's defining trait is a supernatural radiance. Each night she comes out and "sits on her roof, and she shines so that she lights up all" the country around (002-041); adorned with pearls, "she shone like the moon, and her beauty made night" as bright as day (002-042). To win her, the prince must accomplish the [[marriage-and-courtship|impossible tasks]] and defeat the [[supernatural-beings|demons]] set by her father — and it is only after these trials that the princess becomes his bride.

## Role in the Tale

Labam is the unseen goal that drives the quest, and then the reward that crowns it. For much of the story she is spoken of rather than seen — the distant, shining princess whose very name the prince repeats as he journeys through strange lands toward "the Princess Labam's country" (002-030, 002-031). Her nightly appearance on the roof, luminous as the moon (002-041, 002-042), makes her the radiant object of the whole adventure.

When the prince at last reaches her, their meeting is delicate and startling: he takes "the Princess Labam's hand gently to put on the ring, and she started up very much" alarmed (002-056). But before he can truly win her, her father imposes the trials that guard her — a task of sorting eighty pounds of mustard seed (002-061, 002-063) and single combat against the king's two [[supernatural-beings|demons]], for "kings' sons who wanted to marry the Princess Labam had to fight with these [[supernatural-beings|demons]]" (002-067). Only when these are overcome does Labam pass from unattainable ideal to wedded princess, departing at last for the prince's own country, "where they lived happily" (002-078).

## Key Deeds and Appearances

- **Her fame spreads** — the prince hears of "the beautiful Princess Labam" from the parrots and vows to see her (002-002, 002-005).
- **The radiant princess** — each night she sits on her roof and "shines so that she lights up all" the land (002-041).
- **Bright as the moon** — decked in pearls, "she shone like the moon, and her beauty made night" like day (002-042).
- **The startled meeting** — when the prince gently takes her hand to place a ring on it, she starts up in alarm (002-056).
- **The impossible task** — her father sets the prince eighty pounds of mustard seed to sort (002-061, 002-063).
- **The demon-guardians** — suitors who would marry her must first fight and kill the king's two [[supernatural-beings|demons]] (002-067).
- **The happy departure** — after the trials, Labam leaves with the prince for his own country, where they live happily (002-078).

## Relationships

- **The [[king|Raja]]'s son** — the prince who crosses the world drawn by her fame, endures her father's trials, and wins her as his bride; see [[how-the-rajas-son-won-the-princess-labam]] (002-002, 002-056).
- **Her father, the king** — the ruler who guards his daughter with [[marriage-and-courtship|impossible tasks]] and demon-combat, hoping each suitor "will kill him" instead (002-061, 002-067).

## Moral or Cultural Significance

Princess Labam is a classic figure of the folk quest: the peerless, radiant maiden whose worth is measured by the trials a hero must survive to win her. Her moon-like brilliance marks her as more than mortal, an almost celestial prize, while her father's deadly conditions — the sorted seed, the demon-guardians — belong to the widespread "bride won through [[marriage-and-courtship|impossible tasks]]" pattern of [[marriage-and-courtship]]. The notes to the collection observe that the particular forms of these tests "as given in 'Princess Labam' are not known in Europe" (030-028), marking the tale as a distinctively Indian variation on a universal theme. Labam herself remains largely passive, the shining goal of the story; its virtues of persistence and courage belong to the suitor, while she embodies the beauty that makes the whole endeavour worth its cost.
