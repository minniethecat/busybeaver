# Fear in Folklore

**Fear in Folklore** is a pervasive narrative motif in the Indian Fairy Tales collected by Joseph Jacobs (1892). Fear functions as both a survival instinct — driving characters to avoid literal death — and a moral force that exposes cowardice, tests loyalty, and ultimately rewards courage. Unlike [[greed-in-folklore|greed]], which is uniformly condemned, fear in these tales exists on a moral spectrum: the fear of dishonouring one's word is virtuous, while the fear that leads to abandoning family is contemptible.

## Definition

In the context of this corpus, fear manifests in three primary narrative patterns:

1. **Primal Fear of Death** — animals and humans alike face the immediate threat of being killed and eaten. The Crane who fears the Lion's jaws (001-001), the Lambikin who fears being devoured (003-001), and the fish who fear the Crane's deception (007-032) all act from this most basic fear.

2. **Fear of Royal Authority** — the king's power of life and death hangs over many tales. The Raja's son in "How the Raja's Son Won the Princess Labam" must repeatedly face tasks set by the king, knowing failure means execution (002-063, 002-065). This fear drives the entire narrative arc from obedience to triumph.

3. **Fear and the Supernatural** — demons, magicians, and enchanted beings inspire terror that tests courage. In "Punchkin," the princess fears the magician Punchkin's power to maintain her imprisonment indefinitely (004-045). In "Loving Laili," Prince Majnun trembles with fear before cutting the enchanted fruit that holds his beloved (008-036).

These three patterns establish fear as the emotional engine that powers the tales' narrative logic: characters must overcome fear to achieve their goals, and those who succumb are punished.

## Examples in the Tales

### The Lion and the Crane: Fear as Survival

In the opening tale, a Crane approaches a Lion who roars in pain. The Crane is initially paralyzed by fear: "I dare not enter thy mouth for fear thou mightest eat me" (001-001). The Lion's reassurance does not eliminate the Crane's fear but channels it into a calculated risk — extracting the bone that has lodged in the Lion's throat. The crane's courage is rewarded, though the tale's later turn to ingratitude complicates the moral.

### Punchkin: Fear of the Magician

The captive princesses in "Punchkin" live under the constant terror of the magician's power. The narrative is explicit: "they feared that their kind husbands must die" (004-043) and the Princess of the seventh kingdom "feared for her son's safety" (004-045). Fear defines every relationship in the tale — between the magician and his prisoners, between the imprisoned wives and their husbands, and between the mother and her endangered child. The tale's climax is the overcoming of this fear through the discovery of the magician's secret vulnerability (004-078).

### The Cruel Crane Outwitted: Fear of Death

When the Crab catches the Crane in its deception, the Crane's reaction is described with visceral intensity: "his eyes standing out, and trembling with the fear of death, the crane beseeched the Crab" (007-032). The hunter becomes the hunted, and the fear that the Crane inflicted on the fish is now visited upon himself — a karmic inversion that the tale frames as poetic justice.

### Harisarman: Fear as Social Control

Fear operates differently in the tale of [[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]]. When Harisarman hides in fear after his boasting is challenged (011-004), he inadvertently discovers the thieves' hiding place. His fear-driven flight produces what appears to be supernatural divination, transforming a cowardly moment into social advancement. The tale offers a rare comedic treatment of fear: the emotion that destroys characters in other tales here becomes, through accident, the foundation of success.

### The Charmed Ring: Fear of Loss

In "The Charmed Ring," a merchant's son faces the fear of losing his inheritance and his beloved: "He feared lest his son should die and his family line be extinguished" (012-013). This fear motivates the acquisition of the magical ring, which in turn generates its own complications. The tale traces how fear of loss leads to magical intervention, which leads to new dangers — a spiral that only the hero's courage can break.

## Cultural Context

Fear in Indian folklore is inseparable from the [[buddhist-framework|Buddhist]] understanding of *bhaya* (fear) as both an obstacle and a teacher. In the [[jataka]] tradition, the [[bodhisatta]] repeatedly faces terrifying situations — demons, wild animals, hostile kings — and overcomes them through wisdom (*paññā*) rather than martial prowess. This pattern contrasts with heroic traditions that valorise fearlessness in battle.

The *Panchatantra* tradition similarly treats fear as a rational response to genuine danger, not a moral failing. The wise character calculates risk; the foolish character ignores danger signals. Fear, when rightly directed, is a form of intelligence.

Jacobs's selection foregrounds tales where fear drives narrative rather than merely colouring it. This is partly a legacy of the collection's composite sources: the Buddhist tales (via the Jataka) treat fear as a spiritual test, while the folk tales from the Punjab and Kashmir treat it as a practical problem of survival in a dangerous world.
