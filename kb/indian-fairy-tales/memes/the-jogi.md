The **Jogi** is a recurring character type in the *Indian Fairy Tales* collection, appearing most prominently as the wandering ascetic who discovers and crafts the magical fiddle in "[[the-magic-fiddle|The Magic Fiddle]]" (Chapter 6). The Jogi is a renunciant — a holy man who lives by begging — but in these tales he is also a craftsman, an owner of supernatural animals, and an unwitting participant in magical [[disguise-and-deception|transformation]]s. His name is an Anglicisation of *yogi* (Sanskrit: योगी), a practitioner of yoga and ascetic discipline, though Jacobs's Jogis are folk figures rather than philosophical adepts.

The Jogi of Chapter 6 is the most developed of this type. Passing by the tank where the [[the-bonga-girl|Bonga Girl]] drowned, he sees an enormous bamboo stalk and decides to make a fiddle from it. When the bamboo speaks and tries to bargain — "Do not cut at the root, cut higher up" — the Jogi, angered by what he takes to be a Bonga spirit's trickery, cuts it down anyway and shapes it into an instrument of extraordinary beauty. He loses the fiddle to thieves while drunk, and exits the tale as abruptly as he entered. In "[[the-son-of-seven-mothers|The Son of Seven Queens]]" (Chapter 16), a different Jogi owns a wonderful white cow whose milk possesses magical properties. In "[[rasalu|Raja Rasalu]]" (Chapter 19), three Jogis at a palace gate foretell the prince's doom if he goes above ground before a certain age.

The Jogi in these tales occupies a liminal role — part holy man, part unwitting agent of supernatural events. He is never the hero; he is the catalyst who sets [[disguise-and-deception|transformation]]s in motion.

## Role in the Tales

### The Magic Fiddle (Chapter 6)

The Jogi appears midway through the tale as a wandering beggar who passes the tank where the Bonga Girl drowned. He spots a bamboo growing to "an immense size" on the embankment and decides to fashion a fiddle from it (006-018). The bamboo protests:

> "Do not cut at the root, cut higher up." When he lifted his axe to cut high up the stem, the bamboo cried out, "Do not cut near the top, cut at the root." (006-018)

The Jogi, "feeling sure that a Bonga was trying to frighten him," becomes angry and cuts the bamboo down at the root, unintentionally trapping the Bonga Girl's spirit inside the resulting fiddle. The instrument possesses a "superior tone" that "delighted all who heard it," and the Jogi uses it to beg, returning home each evening "with a full wallet" (006-018). He later visits the Bonga girl's brothers' house, where the fiddle's music moves them to tears — "the fiddle seemed to wail as one in bitter anguish" (006-019).

The Jogi's final scene is his undoing. At a village chief's house, he is plied with liquor until intoxicated, and his fiddle is stolen and replaced with an old one. When he recovers and protests, the household denies the theft. Bereft of his means of livelihood, he departs — and is never mentioned again (006-020). The Jogi's role is instrumental but not heroic: he creates the fiddle, carries it to the places where its story will unfold, and then loses it, clearing the stage for the [[the-chiefs-son|chief's son]].

### The Son of Seven Queens (Chapter 16)

A different Jogi appears in Chapter 16 as the owner of a magical cow. When the princess, working as a servant in the King's garden, needs a cow's milk to perform a task for the gardener's daughter, she calls on the Jogi:

> She went to the Jogi's hut and asked her for the Jogi's cow, she could not refuse, and let her take it. (016-030)

The cow is described as "tall, white, and beautiful" (016-031). The Jogi here is a peripheral figure — a guardian of supernatural livestock rather than an active agent — but the cross-tale recurrence of the Jogi-as-owner-of-magical-things reinforces the type.

## Relationships

- **[[the-bonga-girl|The Bonga Girl]]** — The Jogi is the unwitting captor of her spirit. He does not know she is inside the bamboo; he acts out of anger at what he perceives as a [[trickster-figure|trickster]] spirit. His fiddle-making is both an act of creation and an imprisonment (006-018).
- **[[the-chiefs-son|The Chief's Son]]** — The Jogi's loss is the chief's son's gain. The theft of the fiddle transfers the Bonga Girl from one man's instrument to another man's household, where she will eventually be discovered and freed (006-021).
- **[[the-princess|The Princess]]** (Ch 16) — The Jogi's cow enables the princess to perform her [[marriage-and-courtship|impossible tasks]].

## Cultural Significance

The Jogi in the *Indian Fairy Tales* is a folk appropriation of the yogi archetype. Unlike the philosophical ascetic of Sanskrit literature — a figure of renunciation, meditation, and supernatural attainment — Jacobs's Jogis are earthy, fallible, and often comically limited. The Jogi of Chapter 6 is a drunkard who loses his most precious possession to thieves; the Jogis of Chapter 19 are gate-beggars who speak prophecies casually.

This demystification of the holy man is a recurring feature of Indian folk narrative. The Jogi may possess or create magical objects, but he does not control them. He is a conduit, not a master. The fiddle's true power — the Bonga Girl within — operates independently of him, emerging to cook and to court while the household sleeps. The Jogi's marginal status as a beggar — dependent on others for food and shelter — mirrors his marginal narrative role: he appears, he creates, he loses, he vanishes.
