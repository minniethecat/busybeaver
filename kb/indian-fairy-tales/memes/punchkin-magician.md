# Punchkin (the Magician)

## Overview

Punchkin is the wicked magician and chief villain of the tale [[punchkin]] (Chapter 4). Wandering through the palace [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]]d as a fakir (004-045), he abducts the princess [[balna|Balna]] and works his dark power upon her family. His name gives the tale its title, and the preface of the collection singles out "that fine story, 'Punchkin'" as one of its memorable myths (P01-008).

His defining feature is the classic motif of the "external soul": his life does not reside in his body but is hidden far away inside a little green parrot. As he himself confesses, "on the life of the parrot depends my life" (004-061). This secret is both the source of his seeming invincibility and the single vulnerability through which he is at last destroyed.

## Role in the Tale

Punchkin is the enchanter who scatters and petrifies a family. He turns his victims to stone and tree — "all the rocks and trees" that fill the landscape are the people he has bewitched, "turned into stones and trees" (004-050) — and among them are [[balna|Balna]]'s husband and his six brothers. Having carried [[balna|Balna]] off, he shuts her in a high tower "because she refused to marry him" (004-054), holding her captive for fourteen years while pressing his suit in vain.

His undoing comes through [[balna|Balna]]'s grown son, who tricks him into revealing the secret of the parrot. The magician keeps his soul in "a small cage which contains a little green parrot," set in the centre of a circle of palm trees in the midst of a distant jungle (004-061). When the boy seizes the parrot, Punchkin is helpless: he begs and bargains, first offering to restore his victims — "by a stroke of his wand, [[balna|Balna]]'s husband and his brothers resumed their natural shapes" (004-072) — then pleading, "Give me my parrot!" (004-075). But as the boy tears off the bird's wings and destroys it, the magician's body fails limb by limb, until at last "Punchkin's head twisted round, and, with a fearful groan, he died!" (004-076, 004-078).

## Key Deeds and Appearances

- **The [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]]d fakir** — wanders through the palace as "Punchkin Fakir" and comes upon [[balna|Balna]] singing beside her child (004-045).
- **Turns victims to stone** — his power transforms people into "stones and trees," which fill the surrounding land (004-050).
- **Imprisons [[balna|Balna]]** — shuts her in a high tower for fourteen years because she refuses to marry him (004-054).
- **The external soul** — reveals that his life is bound to a little green parrot hidden in a distant jungle: "on the life of the parrot depends my life" (004-061).
- **Cornered by the boy** — when [[balna|Balna]]'s son plays with and then seizes the parrot, the frightened magician bargains for its return (004-067, 004-071, 004-072).
- **Restores his victims** — a stroke of his wand returns [[balna|Balna]]'s husband and his brothers to "their natural shapes" (004-072).
- **His death** — as the boy destroys the parrot wing by wing, Punchkin's body twists and fails until he dies with a fearful groan (004-075, 004-076, 004-078).

## Relationships

- **[[balna|Balna]]** — the princess he abducts and imprisons in the tower, whom he vainly presses to marry; see [[balna]] (004-045, 004-054).
- **[[balna|Balna]]'s son** — the young hero who discovers the secret of the parrot and destroys it, bringing about the magician's death (004-062, 004-075).
- **His petrified victims** — [[balna|Balna]]'s husband and the six other princes, turned to stone until his defeat restores them (004-050, 004-072).
- **The green parrot** — the bird that holds his external soul, and thus his life and death (004-061, 004-065).

## Moral or Cultural Significance

Punchkin is the archetypal fairy-tale sorcerer whose life is kept outside his own body — the "external soul" or "[[the-external-soul|life-index]]" motif that recurs across world folklore; see [[the-external-soul]]. The notes to the collection treat this tale as a key witness to the motif, discussing "The Philosophy of Punchkin" and tracing the pattern through [[bengal|Bengal]] and the Deccan (030-034, 030-035). His seeming invincibility, resting on a secret hidden a world away, dramatises a moral common to such tales: that even the mightiest evil harbours a single fatal weakness, and that cunning — here the persistence of a child — can find it where brute force cannot. His petrifying magic makes him the frozen opposite of the love and courage that finally free his victims, and his death by the destruction of the parrot is the exact and satisfying reversal of the spell he cast on others.
