## Overview

The Gardener appears in "The Boy Who Had a [[moon|Moon]] on His Forehead and a Star on His Chin" (Chapter 22). He is a servant in [[the-garden|the king's garden]] and the father of a beautiful daughter who secretly meets and marries a banished prince. Through his daughter, the gardener becomes grandfather to the wonder-child — a boy born with a moon on his forehead and a star on his chin (022-002).

## Role in the Tale

The gardener is a loyal royal servant whose life is transformed when a [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]]d prince — the banished seventh son of a king — asks to marry his daughter. The gardener, unaware of the suitor's royal identity, grants permission (022-003).

His daughter, once married, lives with the prince in a house the prince magically conjures. When the other six queens become envious of the gardener's daughter's apparent wealth, they visit her repeatedly and eventually plot against her (022-006). The gardener's daughter falls gravely ill during the queens' third visit (022-013), setting up one of the tale's major crises.

The gardener's most significant connection to the plot is through his grandson, the miraculous child. When the boy is born with a moon on his forehead and a star on his chin, and the jealous queens substitute a puppy and cast the infant away, it is the gardener's daughter — not the gardener himself — who suffers the consequences of being accused of giving birth to an animal (022-015).

## Moral and Cultural Significance

The gardener represents the virtuous commoner whose humble station is no barrier to connection with royal [[fate-and-prophecy|destiny]]. His role is modest — he never actively drives the plot — but his willing consent to his daughter's marriage to a stranger enables the story's central union.

The tale reinforces a pattern common in Indian folklore: the humble parent whose child marries into royalty through virtue rather than status. The gardener's unquestioning permission to the [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]]d prince (022-003) contrasts with the scheming of the six queens, positioning simple trust above courtly intrigue.
