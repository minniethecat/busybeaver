## Overview

The Granny is the grandmother of the [[lambikin|Lambikin]] in the cumulative beast-fable [[the-lambikin]] (Chapter 3). She lives at the end of [[lambikin|Lambikin]]'s outward journey, and when he arrives — having talked his way past a jackal, a vulture, a tiger, a wolf, a dog, and an eagle — she does exactly what he asks: she puts him in the corn-bin to fatten for seven days, and when he is "fat enough for anything" (003-020), she builds him a little drumikin out of his dead brother's skin so he can roll home hidden inside. The Granny is the tale's enabler: without her corn-bin and her sewing, [[lambikin|Lambikin]]'s trick would be impossible.

## Role in the Tale

### The Corn-Bin

When [[lambikin|Lambikin]] arrives at her house "all in a great hurry," he announces: "Granny, dear, I've promised to get very fat; so, as people ought to keep their promises, please put me into the corn-bin at once" (003-019). The Granny "said he was a good boy, and put him into the corn-bin," where the [[greed-in-folklore|greedy]] [[lambikin|Lambikin]] "stayed for seven days, and ate, and ate, and ate, until he could scarcely waddle" (003-020). Her response is entirely uncritical — she accepts his transparent excuse about "keeping promises" at face value and indulges his gluttony without question.

### The Drumikin

When [[lambikin|Lambikin]] declares himself too plump to walk home safely — "some animal would be sure to eat him on the way back, he was so plump and tender" (003-020) — he proposes the drum solution. The Granny complies:

> "So his Granny made a nice little drumikin out of his brother's skin, with the wool inside, and Lambikin curled himself up snug and warm in the middle, and trundled away gaily." (003-022)

The drumikin is the vehicle of [[lambikin|Lambikin]]'s deception on the return journey, and the Granny — by building it — is his accomplice. Whether she knows the full danger he faces is left unsaid; her role is simply to help.

## Key Deeds

- **The corn-bin**: She puts [[lambikin|Lambikin]] in the corn-bin at his request and leaves him to eat for seven days, believing him to be "a good boy" keeping a promise (003-019, 003-020).
- **The drumikin**: She fashions a drum from the skin of [[lambikin|Lambikin]]'s dead brother, creating the [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]] that carries him past every predator on the road home — until the jackal recognises his voice (003-022).

## Relationships

- **[[lambikin|Lambikin]]** — her grandson, the "wee wee [[lambikin|Lambikin]]" whose visit sets the tale in motion. She indulges his schemes without hesitation, from the corn-bin to the drumikin (003-019, 003-022).
- **[[lambikin|Lambikin]]'s brother** — dead, his skin becomes the material of the drumikin. The Granny's use of it is matter-of-fact, reflecting the tale's unsentimental folk logic (003-022).

## Moral and Cultural Significance

The Granny occupies a small but structurally essential role. She is the **helper-figure** in a tale that otherwise consists entirely of predators and one clever victim. Without her corn-bin there would be no fattening; without her drumikin there would be no [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]]; without both there would be no story. Her unquestioning indulgence of her grandson — she calls him "a good boy" even as he schemes — reflects the folk-tale archetype of the grandmother as a benign, enabling figure, whose love takes the form of food and shelter rather than caution.

In the larger context of [[folly-and-foolishness]], the Granny is not foolish herself but the instrument of [[lambikin|Lambikin]]'s own foolish overconfidence. She makes him fat and she makes him invisible, but she cannot protect him from his own voice — the one thing the drum cannot hide. Her role thus carries a gentle irony: a grandmother's love equips her grandson perfectly for the journey, but even the best equipment cannot save someone determined to speak when silence would keep them safe.
