# The Brahman

## Overview

The Brahman is the central human figure of the ninth tale, [[the-tiger-the-brahman-and-the-jackal]]. He enters as "a poor Brahman" who happens by a cage in which a tiger is trapped (009-002), and his defining trait — a tender-heartedness that overrides prudence — sets the whole story in motion. When the tiger begs for release, the Brahman's compassion undoes him: his kindness is answered with treachery, and he is saved only by the wit of a jackal. He stands as the collection's clearest embodiment of misplaced mercy and the peril of expecting [[gratitude-and-ingratitude|gratitude]] from the wicked.

## Role in the Tale

The Brahman is the merciful victim whose plight frames the tale's central question: is it just to devour one's rescuer? He is cautious at first — "you would probably eat me if I did" (009-003) — but his "pious" heart is worn down by the tiger's tears and oaths until "at last he consented to open the door of the cage" (009-005). Thereafter he is the story's moral surveyor, wandering the countryside to ask a *pipal* tree, a buffalo, and the road whether the tiger's betrayal is just (009-007 to 009-010). Each answers with its own bitter tale of ingratitude, deepening the story's indictment of a thankless world. His helplessness makes room for the [[trickster-figure|jackal]], whose cleverness ultimately resolves what the Brahman's goodness cannot.

## Key Deeds and Appearances

- **Freeing the tiger out of pity (009-002 to 009-005).** Passing the cage, the Brahman is implored, "Let me out of this cage, oh pious one!" (009-002). He hesitates, but the tiger swears "with many oaths" to "serve you as a slave" (009-004), and "when the tiger sobbed and sighed and wept and swore, the pious Brahman's heart softened" until he opens the door (009-005). The tiger at once seizes him: "What a fool you are! What is to prevent my eating you now...?" (009-005).

- **Bargaining for a hearing (009-006).** Pleading for his life in vain, the most the Brahman can win is "a promise to abide by the decision of the first three things he chose to question as to the justice of the tiger's action" (009-006).

- **Seeking three judgments (009-007 to 009-010).** The Brahman consults a *pipal* tree, which rebukes him ("Don't whimper—be a man!" 009-007); a buffalo turning a [[the-well|well-wheel]], which complains it is repaid for its milk with "refuse as fodder" (009-008); and the road, which laments that all "trample on me as they go past" (009-010). Each confirms that ingratitude is the way of the world, leaving him more sorrowful.

- **Recounting the tale to the jackal (009-011 to 009-019).** Meeting the jackal, who asks "what's the matter, Mr. Brahman? You look as miserable as a fish out of water!" (009-011), the Brahman tells his story again and again as the jackal feigns confusion, buying the time and the pretext the rescue will require (009-012 to 009-019).

- **Rescued by the ruse (009-035, 009-036).** As the Brahman narrates yet again, the exasperated tiger leaps back into the cage to [[supernatural-beings|demons]]trate how he was trapped — "This way! Now do you understand how it was?" (009-035) — whereupon the jackal "dexterously shut the door" and the Brahman is saved (009-036).

## Relationships

- **The tiger** — the trapped beast the Brahman frees out of pity, who repays him with immediate treachery and nearly devours him; the living proof of the ingratitude the Brahman's ordeal exposes (009-002 to 009-005).
- **The jackal** — the clever creature who takes up the Brahman's cause, feigns stupidity, and tricks the tiger back into the cage, delivering the rescue the Brahman could not achieve for himself (009-011, 009-036).
- **The pipal tree, the buffalo, and the road** — the three witnesses the Brahman questions; each recounts its own experience of thanklessness and rules against him, embodying the world's indifference to kindness (009-007 to 009-010).

## Moral or Cultural Significance

The Brahman personifies the danger of mercy freely given to the merciless. His goodness is genuine and even admirable — he cannot bear to leave a weeping creature caged — yet the tale makes plain that virtue untempered by judgment invites disaster, a lesson underscored by the [[talking-animals]] and objects who each testify that good deeds are met with abuse. The story sets the Brahman's soft heart against the jackal's sharp head, and it is intellect, not piety, that finally rights the wrong: the pious man supplies the moral stakes, but the [[trickster-figure|trickster]] supplies the solution. As a widely travelled folk motif, the Brahman's near-fatal charity has become proverbial for the naivety of expecting [[gratitude-and-ingratitude|gratitude]] from the wicked, and his rescue for the folk faith that cleverness can redeem even the gravest error of good intentions.
