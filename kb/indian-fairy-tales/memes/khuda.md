## Overview

Khuda — the Persian and Urdu word for "God" — appears across four tales in the *Indian Fairy Tales* collection as the divine force invoked by characters in moments of crisis, gratitude, and supplication. As Joseph Jacobs notes in his editorial commentary, he has "replaced 'God' by 'Khuda,' the word originally used" (030-027), reflecting the Indo-Persian linguistic and cultural context of the oral sources from which these tales were collected. Khuda functions not as a character with dialogue or physical presence, but as the ultimate agency behind miracles, [[disguise-and-deception|transformation]]s, and the restoration of life — the force to whom characters turn when human effort has reached its limit.

## Role in the Tales

### How the Raja's Son Won the Princess Labam (Chapter 2)

In this tale, Khuda appears as the source of magical gifts. When the princess first receives a beautiful shawl and a splendid horse, she attributes them directly to divine intervention:

> "See, mother," she said; "Khuda must have given me this shawl and this horse, for these things are not of this world." And the mother replied, "Yes, my child; Khuda must have given you this splendid shawl and this horse." (002-052–002-053)

This attribution frames the raja's son's magical acquisitions — the result of the fakir's aid — as ultimately sanctioned by divine will. Khuda here serves as the explanatory force for wonders beyond ordinary comprehension.

### Loving Laili (Chapter 8)

Khuda's most active role occurs in "[[laili|Loving Laili]]," where the divine appears repeatedly as an agent of [[disguise-and-deception|transformation]] and restoration. The tale opens with Khuda sending an angel to [[laili|Laili]] in a dream, commanding her to marry [[majnun|Prince Majnun]]:

> As she slept, Khuda sent to her an angel in the form of a man, who said, "You must not marry anyone but Prince Majnun." (008-004)

Later, when [[laili|Laili]] and [[majnun|Majnun]] are turned to ashes by a jealous rival, it is Khuda who restores them:

> But Laili had never done any wrong to anyone, and when she was burned to ashes she had regained her human form, and Khuda sent life into it. (008-023)

The fakir who destroys them acknowledges Khuda's primacy, asking, "Of what use is it that this woman should be dead?" (008-024). When the couple is restored, [[majnun|Majnun]] declares: "I became a heap of ashes. But Khuda gave me my life again, and brought me here" (008-030). Khuda functions here as the power of resurrection — the force that can undo death itself when the victim is innocent.

### The Boy Who Had a Moon on His Forehead and a Star on His Chin (Chapter 22)

In this tale, Khuda is invoked in a moment of parental anxiety. When the seventh queen gives birth to a son with miraculous markings — a moon on his forehead and a star on his chin — the king's initial fear that the child might be a demon is calmed by his delight:

> The king was delighted and said, "If it pleases Khuda that this child should live, I will make him my heir." (022-018)

Here Khuda represents fate and divine pleasure — the child's survival is placed in divine hands, the king's own will subordinate to a higher power.

### The Ivory City and Its Fairy Princess (Chapter 26)

A passing reference appears when a ghoul describes her vigil: "I have been here by this tomb worshipping Khuda" (026-102). The mention underscores Khuda's role as the object of devotion even for supernatural beings, situating all creatures — human and non-human — within a shared framework of divine worship.

## Relationships

- **[[laili|Laili]]** — the heroine of Chapter 8, whose fate Khuda directly orchestrates through dreams, [[disguise-and-deception|transformation]]s, and resurrection (008-004, 008-023). [[laili|Laili]] prays to Khuda to restore sight to her blinded parents (008-017) and appeals to Khuda's command when refusing the fakir's advances (008-044).
- **[[majnun|Prince Majnun]]** — [[laili|Laili]]'s beloved; Khuda decrees their union, and restores [[majnun|Majnun]] from ashes (008-030)

- **The Seventh Queen's Son** — the moon-forehead boy whose survival is placed in Khuda's hands (022-018)

## Moral and Cultural Significance

Khuda's presence across the collection reflects the deep Islamic-Persian cultural overlay on Indian oral tradition. The tales in Jacobs' collection were collected in the late 19th century from oral sources in northern India, where centuries of Persianate court culture had infused the vernacular with Perso-Arabic religious vocabulary. "Khuda" (خدا) — rather than the Sanskrit-derived "Ishwar" or "Bhagavan" — signals the syncretic religious landscape from which these tales emerged.

Theologically, Khuda in these tales operates as a distant but responsive divine power — not a character who speaks directly, but a force whose will is executed through angels, dreams, and miraculous interventions. This conception aligns with the Islamic understanding of divine transcendence, yet the tales' use of resurrection and dream-commands also echoes Sufi devotional narrative traditions, where God (Khuda) communicates through visions and restores the faithful after trials.

Notably, Khuda appears most prominently in tales of romantic love — "[[laili|Loving Laili]]" especially — suggesting that the divine was understood as particularly invested in the union of destined lovers, a theme shared across both Persian romance (the *Layla and [[majnun|Majnun]]* tradition) and Hindu devotional poetry.
