## Overview

The Step-Mother is the primary domestic antagonist in "[[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]]" (Chapter 4). As the [[king|Raja]]'s second wife — or, in the terminology of folk-tale scholarship, the "envious co-wife" — she systematically persecutes the seven Princesses born of the [[king|Raja]]'s first marriage. Her cruelty escalates from starvation to the destruction of supernatural gifts, and culminates in a false plea of mortal illness that demands the blood of the seven Princesses as the only cure (004-012–004-027). She is a classic embodiment of the "envious step-mother" archetype that appears across Indian and European folk traditions (030-075).

## Role in the Tale

### Starvation of the Princesses

The Step-Mother's cruelty is established from the outset. The seven Princesses cry out in hunger, lamenting to their dead mother's spirit (004-012):

> "Oh mother, mother, cannot you see your poor children, how unhappy we are, and how we are starved by our cruel step-mother?" (004-012)

Their mother's spirit responds by causing a beautiful pomelo tree to grow from her grave, and the Princesses secretly eat its fruit each day instead of the bad food their step-mother gives them (004-013).

### Discovery and Destruction

Suspecting that someone is feeding the Princesses, the Step-Mother sends her own daughter — the Prudhan's daughter — to spy on them (004-014–004-015). Though the Princesses try to keep their secret, they give the Prudhan's daughter some of the pomelo fruit, and she reports everything to her mother (004-016–004-018).

The Step-Mother then feigns a headache and tells the [[king|Raja]] that the only cure is water boiled with the pomelo tree from his dead wife's grave. The [[king|Raja]] complies, and the tree is destroyed (004-019). The seven Princesses are left weeping (004-020).

### The Final Scheme

Despite the destruction of the tree, the dead mother's spirit provides more help: a cream-like cake rises from the grave, which the Princesses eat each day (004-021). The Step-Mother again sends her daughter to spy, and again the secret is betrayed (004-022–004-024).

This time, the Step-Mother escalates to murder. She pretends to be at the point of death and tells the [[king|Raja]] (004-025):

> "Only one thing can save my life... To save my life, you must kill the seven daughters of your first wife, and put some of their blood on my forehead and on the palms of my hands, and their death will be my life." (004-025)

The [[king|Raja]], bound by his promise but unable to kill his own daughters, instead abandons them in the jungle — a decision driven entirely by his fear of the Step-Mother (004-027).

## Key Deeds

- **Starvation and Neglect**: Provides the seven Princesses with such poor food that they go hungry each day, forcing them to seek supernatural aid from their dead mother (004-012–004-013).
- **Destruction of the Pomelo Tree**: Through manipulation of the [[king|Raja]], destroys the miraculous tree that sustained the Princesses, eliminating the first gift from their mother's grave (004-018–004-019).
- **Espionage**: Twice sends her daughter to spy on the Princesses, uncovering their secret sources of sustenance and enabling the Step-Mother to destroy them (004-014–004-015, 004-022–004-024).
- **Demand for Blood Sacrifice**: Fabricates a mortal illness and manipulates the [[king|Raja]] into promising to kill his own daughters, demanding their blood as the only cure — the cruelest escalation of her persecution (004-025).

## Relationships

- **The [[king|Raja]]** — The Step-Mother's husband, whom she manipulates through feigned illness and emotional coercion to destroy the pomelo tree and ultimately to agree to kill his own daughters (004-019, 004-025–004-027).
- **The Seven Princesses** — Her step-daughters, the children of the [[king|Raja]]'s first wife. She starves, spies on, and ultimately attempts to have them murdered (004-012–004-027). The eldest, [[balna|Balna]], is particularly wary of her schemes.
- **The Prudhan's Daughter** — The Step-Mother's own daughter, whom she uses as a spy to uncover the Princesses' secrets (004-014–004-015, 004-022–004-024).
- **The First Wife (Dead Mother)** — The Step-Mother's predecessor, whose spirit continues to protect her children from beyond the grave, creating the pomelo tree and cream-cake that the Step-Mother repeatedly destroys (004-013, 004-021).

## Cultural Significance

The Step-Mother in "[[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]]" exemplifies the "envious step-mother" (or "envious co-wife") motif that is one of the most widespread archetypes in world folklore. As folklorist Emmanuel Cosquin has noted, this figure appears across Indian and European traditions, often representing tensions within polygamous households where co-wives competed for resources and their children's inheritance (030-075). In the Sicilian variant documented by Gonzenbach, the envious witch transforms her seven co-queens into seven step-daughters whose eyes are put out — a parallel that Cosquin argues derives directly from the Indian original (030-075). The Step-Mother's actions drive the entire plot of "[[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]]": without her persecution, there would be no exile of the Princesses, no marriage of the youngest to the son of a neighboring [[king|Raja]], and no quest to find and destroy [[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]]'s soul. She is thus the catalyst of the tale's central conflict, embodying the domestic cruelty that forces the heroines into their adventure.
