**The Prince's Test by the Fakir** is the climactic event of Chapter 23 of Joseph Jacobs's *Indian Fairy Tales* (1892), "[[the-prince|The Prince]] and the Fakir." After the [[the-fakir|Fakir]] claims the King's eldest son as payment for granting the King children, he takes the Prince to his home and attempts to kill him by throwing him into a boiling caldron of oil. [[the-prince|The Prince]] reverses the attempt, throwing the Fakir into his own caldron and freeing the Fakir's many prisoners. This event marks the tale's turning point — the predator becomes prey, and the Prince proves his worthiness through cleverness and courage.

## Summary

After the Fakir takes the King's eldest son from the palace, he brings the Prince to his home and orders him to gather fuel (023-019). When the Prince returns with cow-dung for the fire, the Fakir puts on a great pot containing about a hundred gallons of oil and lights a fire beneath it. He then commands the Prince to approach:

> "Come round here, my pupil." But the King's son said, "Master first, and pupil after." (023-021)

[[the-fakir|The Fakir]] commands the Prince three times; each time the Prince gives the same reply. On the third refusal, the Fakir makes a dash to seize the Prince and throw him into the boiling oil. [[the-prince|The Prince]], instead, lifts the Fakir and throws him into the caldron, where the Fakir is burnt to death (023-022).

After the Fakir's death, the Prince discovers a key on his body. Using it, he opens the Fakir's house and finds:

- Many imprisoned men, now freed
- Two horses in a stable-hut
- Two greyhounds, tied up
- Two [[simurgh|simurgs]], imprisoned
- Two tigers, caged

[[the-prince|The Prince]] releases all the captives — men, animals, and mythical creatures — and takes them with him as companions. These creatures become his retinue and assist him in subsequent adventures (023-022).

## Context

The event is the culmination of a bargain struck at the tale's beginning. The childless King, lying at a crossroads for penance, had agreed to give the Fakir one of his future sons in exchange for a prayer that would grant him children (023-006–023-008). A year later, when the Fakir returns to claim his due, the King attempts to deceive him with slave-children, but the Fakir's supernatural perception — he overhears an ant's conversation revealing the true sons' location — defeats the ruse (023-016).

[[the-fakir|The Fakir]] takes the eldest Prince ostensibly as a pupil, but the caldron reveals the Fakir's true intention: he has been collecting princes to kill them. [[the-prince|The Prince]] is not the first; he is merely the first to survive.

Before the caldron scene, the Prince has already [[supernatural-beings|demons]]trated his character. When ordered to gather fuel, he complies without complaint — showing obedience but not passivity. His repeated response "Master first, and pupil after" is neither defiance nor submission; it is a strategic delay that forces the Fakir to reveal his hostile intent.

## Outcome and Consequence

**Immediate outcome**: [[the-fakir|The Fakir]] is dead, burned in his own caldron. [[the-prince|The Prince]] has not merely survived — he has destroyed his would-be destroyer and inherited his resources (house, animals, captives). The reversal is total.

**Narrative consequence**: [[the-prince|The Prince]] now possesses a supernatural retinue (tigers, simurgs, greyhounds) that marks him as a hero. He continues his journey and arrives at a new kingdom, where he will eventually win a Princess through a husband-choosing ceremony — attending it in the Fakir's clothes as a [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]] (023-031–023-037).

**Motif**: This event exemplifies the folkloric pattern of the villain destroyed by his own trap — a motif related to the [[trickster-figure|trickster]]-tricked theme. [[the-fakir|The Fakir]], who uses trickery and supernatural power to collect victims, is himself tricked by a victim who refuses to play the expected role. [[the-prince|The Prince]]'s victory is achieved not through superior strength but through verbal cleverness ("Master first, and pupil after") and decisive action when the moment comes.

## Cultural and Moral Significance

The caldron test is a trial by ordeal in reverse — the villain sets up the ordeal, but the hero turns it against him. In the broader context of Indian folklore, this pattern recurs: the [[trickster-figure|trickster]] who lives by deception dies by it. The tale's moral is not merely "cleverness wins" but "evil contains the seed of its own destruction."

[[the-prince|The Prince]]'s liberation of the Fakir's captives — men, horses, hounds, simurgs, and tigers — transforms the event from personal survival into an act of universal justice. He does not merely escape; he dismantles the Fakir's entire operation. This aligns with the [[buddhist-framework|Buddhist]] narrative pattern in which the [[bodhisatta]]-figure not only defeats evil but restores moral order.

The recurrence of the number two — two horses, two hounds, two simurgs, two tigers — suggests a folkloric structure of paired magical helpers, a pattern common in Indo-European fairy tales (compare the two ravens, two wolves of Odin in Norse mythology).
