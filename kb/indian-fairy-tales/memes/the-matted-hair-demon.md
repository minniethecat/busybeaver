The **Demon with the Matted Hair** is the antagonist of Chapter 25 of Joseph Jacobs's *Indian Fairy Tales* (1892), a [[jataka]] tale recounting a former birth of the [[Buddha]]. Described as a towering creature as tall as a palm tree, with a head the size of a pagoda, saucer-sized eyes, knobbed tusks, a hawk's face, a variegated belly, and blue hands and feet (025-012), the Demon kills every traveller who enters his forest. He is ultimately subdued not by weapons but by the fearless wisdom of the [[bodhisatta]], who is born as the [[the-prince-of-five-weapons|Prince of the Five Weapons]]. The tale concludes with the Demon's moral reformation — he renounces wickedness and is established in the five kinds of virtue (025-023).

## Overview

The Demon with the Matted Hair inhabits a forest on the road between [[Takkasila]] and [[benares|Benares]] (025-011). He is a maneater — the men at the forest's edge warn the young Prince: "There's a Demon in it called he of the Matted Hair: he kills every man he sees!" (025-012). No one who enters the forest emerges alive.

[[the-prince-of-five-weapons|The Prince of the Five Weapons]] — the [[bodhisatta]] in this birth — enters the forest undeterred, trusting in his skill with sword, spear, bow, battle-axe, and shield. The Demon confronts him mid-forest, a monstrous apparition:

> "He made himself as tall as a palm tree; his head was the size of a pagoda, his eyes as big as saucers, and he had two tusks all over knobs and bulbs; he had the face of a hawk, a variegated belly, and blue hands and feet." (025-012)

Each of the Prince's five weapons — arrows, sword, spear, club — sticks fast in the Demon's matted hair. When the Prince strikes with his right hand, left hand, right foot, left foot, and finally his head, all five limbs also stick fast, leaving him suspended from the Demon's hair in five places. Yet the Prince feels no fear (025-019).

The Demon, astonished by the Prince's fearlessness, asks why he is not afraid. [[the-prince|The Prince]] replies that in his belly is a thunderbolt — the weapon of knowledge — which will destroy the Demon from within if eaten (025-021). The Demon, convinced, releases the Prince, and the Prince in turn preaches to him the disadvantages of wickedness and the benefits of virtue, converting the Demon to a righteous path (025-023).

## Role in the Tale

The Demon serves as both antagonist and convert in this Jataka narrative:

**The Obstacle.** The Demon's forest is a physical barrier on the Prince's journey home, representing the dangers that test a hero's worthiness. Local people warn all travellers away; the Demon is an established terror (025-011).

**The Unwinnable Foe.** Conventional weapons — arrows, sword, spear, club — cannot harm the Demon; they only adhere to his matted hair. The five-fold snaring of the Prince's body (right hand, left hand, right foot, left foot, head) creates a total impasse that physical force cannot resolve (025-019).

**The Convert.** The tale's climax is not violent but dialogic. [[the-prince|The Prince]]'s fearlessness and his "thunderbolt of knowledge" argument persuade the Demon to release him. [[the-prince|The Prince]] then delivers a moral discourse, establishing the Demon in the five kinds of virtue and making him the guardian of the forest [[the-temple|shrine]] (025-023). This conversion arc is characteristic of Jataka tales, where the Bodhisatta reforms rather than destroys antagonists.

## Physical Description

The text provides a detailed portrait of the Demon, rare among the corpus's antagonists:

| Feature | Description |
|---------|------------|
| Height | As tall as a palm tree |
| Head | Size of a pagoda (temple tower) |
| Eyes | As big as saucers |
| Tusks | Two, covered in knobs and bulbs |
| Face | Like a hawk |
| Belly | Variegated (multi-colored) |
| Hands and feet | Blue |
| Hair | Matted — weapons and limbs adhere to it |

This elaborate physiognomy reflects the demonic iconography of Indian folklore, blending animal features (hawk face), exaggerated human proportions (palm-tree height), and supernatural coloring (blue extremities). The matted hair that traps all weapons and limbs is the Demon's defining supernatural attribute — it creates the narrative's central impossibility, which only wisdom can resolve (025-012, 025-017, 025-019).

## Moral and Cultural Significance

The Demon tale is a quintessential [[jataka]] narrative, framed by the Teacher (the [[Buddha]]) addressing a monk who has ceased striving after righteousness (025-001). The story's message is explicit: effort in the right place leads to attainment. [[the-prince-of-five-weapons|The Prince of the Five Weapons]] trusts not in his physical armaments but in the "weapon of knowledge" — a metaphor for wisdom (paññā) in Buddhist doctrine.

The Demon's conversion from maneater to [[the-temple|shrine]]-guardian embodies the Buddhist principle that even the most wicked being can be reformed through right teaching. [[the-prince|The Prince]]'s discourse enumerates the consequences of wickedness — rebirth as an animal, a [[Preta]] (hungry ghost), or an [[Asura]] — and the benefits of the five kinds of virtue (025-023). This moral taxonomy is a core element of Buddhist cosmology as transmitted through the Jataka collection.

The tale also contains the striking metaphor of release "as the moon is disgorged from the jaws of Rāhu after the eclipse" (025-021), connecting the Demon's forest to the cosmic drama of Indian mythology, where the serpent Rāhu periodically swallows the moon.

The Jacobs note (030-098) identifies the five-fold attack and the "tar baby" motif — objects sticking to a creature's body — as a widespread folkloric pattern, comparing it to the Cherokee story of the Tar Baby and the Rabbit.
