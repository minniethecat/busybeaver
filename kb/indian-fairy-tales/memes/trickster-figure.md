# Trickster Figure

## Definition

The trickster is a universal archetype in world folklore — a character who uses cleverness, cunning, and deception to overcome more powerful opponents. In the Indian fairy tale tradition, the trickster most frequently takes the form of the jackal, a small but shrewd animal who intervenes in disputes and outwits larger predators. Unlike the European trickster (Reynard the Fox, Br'er Rabbit), the Indian trickster often serves a moral function — exposing folly, punishing greed, and restoring justice through cleverness rather than force.

## Examples in the Tales

- **[[the-tiger|The Tiger]], the Brahman, and the Jackal** (the-tiger-the-brahman-and-the-jackal): A Brahman releases a tiger from a trap, only for the tiger to threaten to eat him. On the road, he meets a jackal who calls out, "Why, what's the matter, Mr. Brahman?" (009-011). The jackal, pretending not to understand the situation, repeatedly asks the tiger to explain until the frustrated beast re-enters the trap to [[supernatural-beings|demons]]trate — "How very confusing!" said the jackal (009-012) — at which point the jackal closes the door. The jackal's feigned stupidity is its greatest weapon, exemplifying the trickster's use of cunning to defeat brute strength.

- **[[the-cruel-crane|The Cruel Crane]] Outwitted** (the-cruel-crane-outwitted): A crane deceives fish by promising to carry them to a beautiful lake, eating them instead. When the crane tries the same trick on a crab, the crab says to himself: "If this fellow wants to eat me, I will outwit him" (007-025), and agrees to be carried while secretly gripping the crane's neck with its claws. The crab's famous grip proves fatal — "we crabs have a famous grip" (007-026) — and the crane is killed. The tale presents a double trickster structure: the crane as false benefactor, the crab as righteous avenger.

- **[[the-lion|The Lion]] and the Crane** (the-lion-and-the-crane): After the crane removes a bone from the lion's throat, [[gratitude-and-ingratitude|the ungrateful]] lion refuses to reward the crane. The crane, using wit rather than confrontation, escapes and exposes the lion's ingratitude. The crane embodies the trickster-as-survivor, outwitting a predator through intelligence.

- **[[the-pigeon|The Pigeon]] and the Crow** (the-pigeon-and-the-crow): A [[greed-in-folklore|greedy]] crow attempts to usurp the pigeon's comfortable position in the rich man's kitchen, but is caught and killed. The pigeon, content and clever, survives while the crow's cunning fails — illustrating that the true trickster succeeds through wisdom, not mere greed.

## Cultural Context

The jackal as trickster has deep roots in Indian literature, appearing in the *Panchatantra* as the figures Damanaka and Karataka — two jackals whose political machinations drive the frame story. This tradition influenced Persian and Arabic literature through the *Kalila wa Dimna* translation, and eventually reached European folklore.

Joseph Jacobs' collection preserves the Indian trickster in its distinctive form. Unlike the European trickster who often acts from pure mischief, the Indian trickster typically serves a didactic purpose — the jackal does not merely fool the tiger for sport but to save the Brahman and teach a lesson about gratitude, trust, and the danger of releasing evil.
