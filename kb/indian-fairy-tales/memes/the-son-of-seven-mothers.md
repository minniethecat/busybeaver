## Overview

The Son of Seven Mothers — also called the Son of Seven Queens — is the unnamed protagonist of "The Son of Seven Queens" (Chapter 16). Born in a dungeon prison to seven queens who were blinded and imprisoned by a jealous rival, he scrapes through the prison wall to bring food to his starving mothers, survives the treachery of the white hind, outwits the Jogi king of [[supernatural-beings|demons]], and ultimately restores his mothers' eyesight. His story is a classic "hidden prince triumphs over adversity" narrative, combining elements of the miraculous birth, the witch-figure antagonist, and the hero's acquisition of magical aid.

## Role in the Tale

### Birth and Imprisonment

The hero is born under extraordinary circumstances: his seven mothers — all queens of a single king — are blinded and cast into prison by a co-wife who "took out the eyes of the seven Queens, and put them in a box" (016-009). The boy is born in darkness, the child of seven mothers who share a single husband, a circumstance the tale's narrator notes with wonder:

> "As the son of seven Queens could not read, he took thought with himself..." (016-021)

### Escape and Provision

Growing strong within the prison, the young prince scrapes through the prison wall — a feat of physical determination — and emerges into the outside world to gather food for his starving mothers. His escape marks the transition from passive victim to active agent.

### The Witch's Test

While foraging, the hero encounters the white hind, a beautiful but treacherous creature who lures him into her service. The hind, secretly a witch, attempts to trap him, but the hero's innate cleverness allows him to perceive her true nature.

The white hind declares that she will "only marry one who was the son of seven mothers" (016-022) — a condition that the hero, uniquely among all men, fulfills. Yet when the white hind proposes marriage, "the son of seven Queens said he would not marry her" (016-025), recognising the danger behind the fair form.

### The Jogi King and the Magical Cow

The hero's path leads him to the realm of the Jogi, a demon king who possesses a magical cow capable of producing endless nourishment. The hero infiltrates the Jogi's domain, and through a combination of daring and quick thinking, steals the cow — a theft that provides for his mothers and represents the triumph of human cleverness over demonic power.

> "The son of seven Queens started with a light heart" (016-036)

### Restoration

In the tale's climax, the hero kills the white hind, acquiring the means to restore his mothers' eyes:

> "Blew on it, and instantly the son of seven Queens started up as well as ever" (016-043)

Returning to his mothers, "the son of seven Queens returned joyfully" (016-045) and restores their sight, reuniting the broken family.

## Key Deeds

- **Prison Escape**: Scrapes through the dungeon wall to bring food to his seven blind mothers, [[supernatural-beings|demons]]trating filial devotion and physical determination (016-021).
- **Resistance to Temptation**: Refuses the white hind's marriage proposal, perceiving the danger beneath her beautiful exterior (016-022, 016-025).
- **Resourcefulness**: Consults the Prince in a neighbouring kingdom for guidance when confronted with the white hind's trap (016-030).
- **Theft of the Magical Cow**: Outwits the Jogi king of [[supernatural-beings|demons]] to obtain the life-sustaining cow (016-035–016-036).
- **Killing the White Hind**: Defeats the witch-figure, securing the means to heal his mothers (016-041–016-043).
- **Restoration of Sight**: Returns his seven mothers' eyes, completing his filial quest (016-045).

## Relationships

- **The Seven Queens** — His mothers, blinded and imprisoned. The hero's entire quest is motivated by love and duty toward them. Their restoration is his triumph (016-009, 016-045).
- **[[the-white-hind|The White Hind]]** — A treacherous witch in animal form. She tests, deceives, and attempts to entrap the hero, serving as the tale's primary antagonist (016-022, 016-025).
- **[[the-jogi|The Jogi]] King of Demons** — The possessor of the magical cow. The hero outwits him, proving that mortal cleverness can overcome supernatural power (016-035).
- **The Neighbouring Prince** — A helpful figure whom the hero consults at a crucial juncture, providing counsel that aids his quest (016-030).

## Moral and Cultural Significance

The Son of Seven Mothers embodies the archetype of the **filial hero** — a protagonist whose heroism is defined not by conquest or glory, but by devotion to family. His quest is not for personal power but for the restoration of those he loves, making him a figure of moral rather than martial virtue.

The tale's central motif — a child born of seven mothers — is noted by Jacobs as "a characteristic Indian idea" arising from a culture where polygamy was practised and the concept of shared motherhood held narrative fascination (030-034, 030-074). The hero's unusual parentage marks him as extraordinary from birth, a common pattern in folk-tale hero narratives worldwide.

The story also participates in the widespread folk-tale pattern of the **witch-slaying hero**, where the antagonist is a beautiful but deadly female figure who must be seen through and defeated. The hero's resistance to the white hind's seduction — "he would not marry her" — is a moral victory before it is a physical one.
