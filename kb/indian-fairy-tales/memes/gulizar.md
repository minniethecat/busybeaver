## Overview

Gulizar is the princess of the [[ivory-city|Ivory City]] and the romantic heroine of "The [[ivory-city|Ivory City]] and Its Fairy Princess" (Chapter 26). Her name, as the Notes reveal, is "Persian for rosy-cheeked" (030-102), signalling the cross-cultural Persian infusion into this Indian tale. Gulizar is not a passive princess awaiting rescue — she actively facilitates her relationship with the prince, obtaining a charmed swing for secret meetings, betrothing herself to him of her own volition, and ultimately enduring the consequences of her father's rage when their secret marriage is discovered (026-029–026-114).

## Role in the Tale

### A Name That Carries Weight

Gulizar is introduced not in person but through the parrot [[hiraman|Hiraman]], who first speaks her name to the prince: "This is none other than Gulizar of the Ivory City. I know this garden and the princess" (026-029). Later, when the prince asks the old woman in the [[ivory-city|Ivory City]] about the princess, she replies: "The name of the princess is Gulizar, and the name of the queen——" (026-053) — the name alone carries a weight of significance that the old woman dares not finish the sentence.

### Courtship and the Charmed Swing

Gulizar first becomes aware of the prince through her servant, who reports the old woman's encounter with the travellers (026-059). Rather than waiting passively, Gulizar takes initiative: "Now Gulizar had obtained a charmed swing, and by means of this swing she flew to the old woman's hut" (026-069). The charmed swing — a magical object under her control — symbolises her agency: she is not merely visited; she visits.

When she and the prince finally meet face to face, her response is immediate and emotionally direct:

> "At last," said Gulizar, "I have seen my beloved, my heart." (026-075)

### The Secret Betrothal

Gulizar does not defer to her father, her mother, or any chaperone. She and the prince "betrothed themselves to one another" (026-077) — a mutual act that bypasses parental authority entirely. From that point forward, the prince "visited Gulizar every day and returned to the old woman's hut every night" (026-078), a clandestine courtship sustained over an extended period.

Yet Gulizar is not reckless. When the prince's increasingly frequent visits risk detection, she counsels caution: "Gulizar for the time assented, but she began to fear for the prince's safety" (026-079). Her love is passionate but not heedless — she understands the political stakes better than the prince does.

### The Aftermath

The consequences of their secret love are severe. When the prince and the vizier's son are forced to flee, Gulizar is left behind and — in the morning — is found in a state of collapse: "In the morning, when Gulizar had returned to her senses, she was inconsolable" (026-089). Her grief testifies to the depth of her attachment.

Yet even in separation, Gulizar remains the emotional centre around which the resolution revolves. The prince sends a letter to "Gulizar's father" explaining everything (026-110), and though her father is "much enraged with his daughter for having acted without his consent," he ultimately agrees: "with the vizier's son's persuasion, he settled to arrange for his marriage with Gulizar as soon as possible" (026-112). The tale closes with the prince returning with "his beautiful bride Gulizar" (026-114), the romance consummated and legitimised.

## Key Deeds

- **Active Courtship**: Rather than being wooed passively, Gulizar uses her charmed swing to fly to the old woman's hut, initiating the face-to-face meeting that begins the romance (026-069, 026-075).
- **The Mutual Betrothal**: She and the prince betroth themselves to one another — a bold act of self-determination that bypasses royal protocol and parental authority (026-077).
- **Sustained Resistance**: Though she "assents" outwardly to caution, she continues the secret relationship, prioritising love over safety (026-078–026-079).
- **Emotional Authenticity**: Her collapse upon the prince's departure and her subsequent grief [[supernatural-beings|demons]]trate that her love is not frivolous but profound (026-089).

## Relationships

- **[[the-prince|The Prince]]** — Her beloved and eventual husband. Their relationship is characterised by mutual passion, secrecy, and self-determination — they betroth themselves without waiting for parental or political approval (026-075, 026-077, 026-114).
- **Gulizar's Father** — The king of the [[ivory-city|Ivory City]]. Initially enraged by his daughter's secret betrothal, he is ultimately persuaded to accept the marriage — a trajectory from patriarchal anger to pragmatic acceptance (026-110–026-112).
- **[[the-old-woman|The Old Woman]]** — The intermediary who carries messages between Gulizar and the prince. Gulizar's servant first encounters the old woman, establishing the chain of communication that enables the romance (026-059, 026-068).
- **[[the-viziers-son|The Vizier's Son]]** — Though their direct interaction is minimal, it is his persuasion that helps convince Gulizar's father to consent to the marriage (026-112).

## Moral and Cultural Significance

Gulizar represents a distinctive type of heroine in the Indian fairy-tale tradition: **the self-determining princess**. Unlike many folk-tale heroines who are prizes to be won through trials (cf. the [[princess-labam|Princess Labam]] in Chapter 2), Gulizar is an active participant in her own romantic fate. She possesses magical agency (the charmed swing), emotional agency (the mutual betrothal), and — ultimately — the power to move her father from rage to acceptance through the sheer persistence of her attachment.

Her Persian name ("rosy-cheeked") is not incidental. The notes identify the tale as "a 'Sirá and [[king-mallika|Mallika]]' story" from the region of the Deccan, and the Persian linguistic element confirms the **cross-cultural hybridity** of Indian courtly romance, where Persian literary traditions (the *Shahnama*, the *Arabian Nights* cycle) intermingled with indigenous Indian storytelling. Gulizar is thus not merely a character but a cultural crossroads — her very name encoding the Persianate influence on Deccan narrative tradition.

The tale's resolution — in which the princess's secret love is eventually legitimised through marriage — reflects a recurring folk-tale pattern in which **youthful passion, however transgressive, is ultimately vindicated** if it is sincere. Gulizar is not punished for her self-determination; she is rewarded with the marriage she chose. This distinguishes her tale from moralistic narratives that punish female agency and aligns her with the more romantic, Persian-influenced strand of Indian storytelling.
