# The Broken Pot Daydream

## Summary

This brief cautionary event follows a poor Brahman who, resting beside a pot of rice he has begged, loses himself in a daydream of the fortune the rice might one day bring him — a wife, a child, and a household. Carried away by an imagined fit of temper within the fantasy, he lashes out with his foot in reality, shatters the very pot, and spills the rice that was the whole basis of his dream. The tale exemplifies the "castle in the air" motif and the folly of building on riches not yet possessed, a close relative of the [[greed-in-folklore|greed and covetousness]] warnings the collection returns to again and again.

> …with his foot, and broke the pot. All the rice fell over him… (005-002)

## Context

[[the-brahman|The Brahman]] is destitute: he "had collected a quantity of rice by begging, and after having dined off it," he saves what remains, filling "a pot with what was left over" (005-001). Hanging the pot up near where he lies, he begins to imagine what would happen if famine came and rice grew dear — how he would sell his store, buy goats, then cattle, then land, and grow rich enough to marry.

> …he filled a pot with what was left over. (005-001)

## Outcome or Consequence

The daydream escalates to a domestic scene. [[the-brahman|The Brahman]] imagines a wife and a baby, and pictures himself so provoked by the child that "full of anger, I shall call to my wife, 'Take the baby; take him!'" (005-002). Warming to the fantasy, he imagines that if she is too busy at the hearth to obey, "I get up, and give her such a kick with my foot" (005-002).

At that very instant, acting out the imaginary kick, he strikes the real pot: he lashed out "with his foot, and broke the pot" (005-002). All the rice tumbles down over him and "made him quite white" (005-002), leaving him poorer than before — his entire remaining store lost to a blow struck at a wife who never existed.

## Cultural or Moral Significance

The broken pot is one of the collection's compact moral fables, and Jacobs closes it with an explicit proverb-lesson: "He who makes" such castles in the air ruins himself. The story is the Indian ancestor of a tale-type found across world literature — the milkmaid or dreamer who spills the very goods on which the fantasy is built. Jacobs's notes trace exactly this line of transmission, connecting the proverb "count your chickens before they are hatched" (030-038) to the tales of "Alnaschar, the Barber's fifth brother" in the *Arabian Nights* and to La Fontaine's "La Perette, who counted her chickens before they were hatched" (030-039), a theme he singles out "as showing the *literary* transmission of tales" from India westward (030-040). Its target is not wealth as such but the folly of reckoning on gains not yet in hand and being ruled by imagined passions. [[the-brahman|The Brahman]]'s whitening with spilled rice is a vivid emblem of self-inflicted ruin: the daydreamer is undone not by any outside enemy but by the temper and greed he indulges within his own mind.
