# The Tiger

## Overview

The tiger is the antagonist of the tale [[the-tiger-the-brahman-and-the-jackal]] (Chapter 9), the embodiment of ingratitude. Caught in a trap and unable to escape, it "rolled and bit with rage and grief when he failed" (009-001), until a passing Brahman is moved to pity. The tiger wins its freedom with tearful oaths of gratitude — and then, the moment it is loose, tries to eat the very man who saved it.

Its cruelty is matched only by its arrogance, and it is this arrogance that destroys it. Too proud to tolerate a stupid audience, the tiger is tricked by a clever jackal into climbing back into its own cage, and the door is shut upon it for good.

## Role in the Tale

The tiger drives the plot from captivity to reversal. Trapped and desperate, it pleads with the Brahman: "Let me out of this cage, oh pious one!" (009-002). When the Brahman hesitates, fearing to be eaten, the tiger swears otherwise:

> "Not at all!" swore the tiger with many oaths; "on the contrary, I should be for ever grateful, and serve you as a slave!" (009-004)

But no sooner is it free than it seizes the Brahman and reveals its true nature:

> "What a fool you are! What is to prevent my eating you now, for after being cooped up so long I am just terribly hungry!" (009-005)

The tiger grants only a grudging reprieve: the Brahman may put his case to the first three things he questions (009-006). While the Brahman is away consulting them, the tiger waits by the cage "sharpening his teeth and claws" (009-015), and greets his return with a chilling, "now let us begin our dinner" (009-016). Its downfall comes through impatience: goaded by the jackal's feigned confusion and finally "in a rage at the jackal's stupidity" (009-023), the tiger leaps back into the cage to [[supernatural-beings|demons]]trate how it was first caught — whereupon the jackal shuts the door (009-035, 009-036).

## Key Deeds and Appearances

- **Trapped in the cage** — caught in a trap, it rages and bites at the bars in vain (009-001).
- **Begs for release** — implores the passing Brahman, "Let me out of this cage, oh pious one!" (009-002).
- **Swears false gratitude** — vows with many oaths to be "for ever grateful, and serve you as a slave" (009-004).
- **Betrays its rescuer** — the instant it is free, it seizes the Brahman and announces its hunger (009-005).
- **Grants a grudging trial** — agrees to abide by the judgment of the first three things the Brahman questions (009-006).
- **Waits to feast** — lurks by the cage sharpening its teeth and claws, then calls for "our dinner" (009-015, 009-016).
- **Trapped again** — in a fury at the jackal, it jumps back into the cage to prove its point and is locked in (009-035, 009-036).

## Relationships

- **[[the-brahman|The Brahman]]** — the "pious one" who takes pity and frees the tiger, and whom the tiger then tries to devour (009-002, 009-005).
- **The jackal** — the clever beast whose feigned stupidity provokes the tiger into re-caging itself; the tiger dismisses it as a fool, "in a rage at the jackal's stupidity" (009-021, 009-023). See [[the-jackal]].
- **The false judges** — the *pipal* tree, buffalo, and road, whose testimony (that ingratitude is universal) at first seems to vindicate the tiger's treachery (009-007, 009-008, 009-010).

## Moral or Cultural Significance

The tiger is the archetype of [[gratitude-and-ingratitude|the ungrateful]] creature [[gratitude-and-ingratitude|repaying kindness]] with malice — the fixed point against which the tale weighs the question of whether good deeds are ever rewarded; see [[gratitude-and-ingratitude]]. Its false oaths and swift betrayal make the Brahman's dilemma concrete, while the answers of tree, buffalo, and road build a bleak chorus insisting that ingratitude is the way of the world. That the tiger is finally defeated not by strength but by its own pride and impatience delivers the fable's counter-lesson: the powerful undo themselves through arrogance, and cunning can restore justice where mercy was abused. This story of [[gratitude-and-ingratitude|the ungrateful]] beast belongs to the widely travelled Indian fable stock that reached Europe through the [[bidpai]] literature (P01-007, 030-039), where the same plot recurs across many languages.
