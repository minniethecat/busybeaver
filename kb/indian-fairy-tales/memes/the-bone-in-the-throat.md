# The Bone in the Throat

## Summary

While a lion was devouring meat, a bone stuck fast in his throat, which swelled until he could neither eat nor rest (001-001). A white crane — the Bodhisatta born into the region of [[the-himalayas|Himavanta]] — agreed to help, but only after taking precautions against the lion's jaws. Propping the mouth open with a stick, the crane reached its head inside and worked the bone loose before darting free. When the crane afterwards asked for a reward, the lion replied that the crane should count itself fortunate to have escaped with its life, and the crane flew away (001-020).

> …as a lion was eating meat a bone stuck in his throat. The throat became swollen, he could not take food, his suffering was terrible. (001-001)

## Context

The tale is a Jataka birth-story: "Long ago the [[bodhisatta|Bodisat]] was born to a forest life," here in the shape of a white crane in the [[the-himalayas|Himavanta]], during the reign of [[king-brahmadatta|Brahmadatta]] (001-001). The event opens the story directly — the lion's distress is the crisis that draws the crane into rendering its dangerous service. Wary of the beast, the crane declares it dares not enter the lion's mouth for fear of being eaten (001-001), and so devises the trick of the propping stick before performing the extraction.

## Participants

- **[[the-lion|The Lion]]** — [[gratitude-and-ingratitude|the ungrateful]] beast whose life is saved; he later insists that having spared the crane is reward enough.
- **The White Crane** — the [[bodhisatta]] in crane form, who removes the bone and afterwards asks in vain for recompense. The crane's plea and the lion's refusal are framed by the crane's opening verses, "A service have we done thee" (001-002).

The event is dramatized within [[the-lion-and-the-crane]], the first tale of the collection.

## Outcome or Consequence

The lion recovers, but repays the crane with contempt rather than gratitude. In the verses that follow, the crane observes of the lion that "In him there is no gratitude" (001-014), and, "having thus spoken the crane flew away" (001-020). The frame closes with the identification that "the Lion was Devadatta the Traitor, but the white crane was I myself" (001-021) — spoken by the Buddha, marking the lion as an emblem of treachery.

## Cultural or Moral Significance

The episode is the collection's opening lesson on the folly of aiding the wicked and expecting thanks. It belongs to the widespread motif of [[gratitude-and-ingratitude|the ungrateful]] beast: a benefactor who rescues a dangerous creature and receives ingratitude in return, a theme that recurs across the tales (see [[gratitude-and-ingratitude]]). As a Jataka, the story also teaches that virtue lies in doing good without counting on reward — the crane performs its service and, meeting only ingratitude, simply departs.
