## Overview

The Goldsmith (Manikkasari) is the human antagonist of "[[soothsayers-son|The Soothsayer's Son]]" (Chapter 10). Rescued by [[soothsayers-son|Gangazara]] from the fourth ledge of a ruined well — after the hero has already pulled out a tiger, a serpent, and a rat — the Goldsmith repays his deliverance with calculated betrayal. When [[soothsayers-son|Gangazara]] later entrusts him with a jewelled crown to be broken down into gold and gems, the Goldsmith denounces him to the prince as the murderer of the king, seizing half the kingdom as reward (010-008–010-010). His character serves as the tale's moral pivot: three wild beasts prove faithful, while the one human being — and a fellow townsman at that — proves treacherous.

## Role in the Tale

### The Rescue

The Goldsmith is first encountered trapped at the bottom of a ruined well in the Vindhya mountains. [[soothsayers-son|Gangazara]], having already released a tiger-king, a serpent-king, and a rat-king — each of whom warned him urgently against the goldsmith — nevertheless decides to pull him up:

> "What wrong would there be in my assisting him? Why should I not release him also?" (010-007)

The Goldsmith introduces himself as Manikkasari, living in the East main street of [[ujjain|Ujjaini]], and invites [[soothsayers-son|Gangazara]] to visit him on his return journey (010-007). The three animal kings had each warned: "Never trust a goldsmith. Do not release him; and if you do, you shall surely repent of it one day or other" (010-004).

### The Betrayal

Ten years later, [[soothsayers-son|Gangazara]] passes through [[ujjain|Ujjaini]] on his way home and seeks out the Goldsmith. He shows him a magnificent jewelled crown — a gift from the grateful Tiger-King — and asks him to separate the gold from the diamonds for easier transport (010-008). The Goldsmith, recognising the crown as belonging to the king of [[ujjain|Ujjaini]] (who had been killed by a tiger a week earlier), conceives a scheme. Instead of helping, he hides the crown under his garments and flies to the palace.

> The goldsmith knew full well that it was a tiger that killed the king, and not any hunter's hands, since he had heard from Gangazara how he obtained the crown. Still, he resolved to denounce Gangazara as the king's murderer. (010-010)

He presents the crown to the prince, claims to have caught the assassin, and receives half the kingdom. [[soothsayers-son|Gangazara]], bathing innocently at the river, is seized by armed soldiers and thrown into a death-cell dungeon (010-010).

### The Reckoning

For ten years, [[soothsayers-son|Gangazara]] survives in the dungeon through the aid of his faithful animal friends — the very creatures the Goldsmith had advised against trusting. When [[soothsayers-son|Gangazara]] is finally freed and reveals the truth before the assembled populace, the Goldsmith flees for his life. He is caught by the king's soldiers but — in a final [[supernatural-beings|demons]]tration of [[soothsayers-son|Gangazara]]'s magnanimity — is pardoned (010-022).

## Key Deeds

- **The Broken Promise**: After being saved from certain death, the Goldsmith invites [[soothsayers-son|Gangazara]] to visit him — then repays that debt with the gravest possible betrayal (010-007, 010-010).
- **The False Accusation**: Knowing the tiger killed the king, the Goldsmith deliberately redirects blame onto his benefactor to claim half the kingdom for himself (010-010).
- **The Coward's Flight**: When exposed, the Goldsmith does not confess or seek forgiveness — he runs, and must be captured by soldiers (010-022).

## Relationships

- **[[soothsayers-son|Gangazara]] (the Soothsayer's Son)** — His rescuer and victim. The Goldsmith owes [[soothsayers-son|Gangazara]] his life, yet betrays him for political gain. Their relationship forms the tale's central moral contrast: human ingratitude versus animal [[faithfulness-and-loyalty|fidelity]] (010-007–010-022).
- **[[the-tiger|The Tiger]]-King, Serpent-King, and Rat-King** — The three animals whose warnings about the Goldsmith [[soothsayers-son|Gangazara]] ignores. They collectively represent the tale's subversive proposition that "you can place more faith in me, a tiger... than in a goldsmith" (010-004).
- **[[the-prince|The Prince]] of [[ujjain|Ujjaini]]** — The ruler whom the Goldsmith manipulates. By presenting the crown and a false accusation, the Goldsmith uses the prince's grief and desire for justice as instruments of his own ambition (010-010).

## Moral and Cultural Significance

The Goldsmith is perhaps the most explicit example of a **professional stereotype** in the Indian Fairy Tales collection. The tiger's warning — "Goldsmiths are never to be trusted" (010-004) — is reiterated by the serpent and the rat, and borne out by the narrative. This reflects a deep-seated folk prejudice against goldsmiths in traditional Indian society, where they were stereotyped as [[greed-in-folklore|greedy]], dishonest, and willing to melt down stolen goods without questions.

Beyond the specific professional caricature, the Goldsmith embodies the **universal theme of ingratitude**. His betrayal of [[soothsayers-son|Gangazara]] mirrors the Lion's ingratitude toward the Crane in Chapter 1, but with a crucial difference: while the Lion's ingratitude is naked and philosophical ("thou askest a reward of us!"), the Goldsmith's is calculated, duplicitous, and socially weaponised. The tale's moral structure is unusually sophisticated: [[the-jungle|the wild]] animals (tiger, serpent, rat) display honour and gratitude, while the civilised human (a townsman, a craftsman) displays treachery — inverting the expected hierarchy of moral reliability.

The Notes identify this tale as deriving from the *Katha Sarit Sagara* and related to European collections (*Sindbad*, *Pentamerone*), where "the list of animals is sometimes different, but the goldsmith always appears" (030-075–030-080).
