## Overview

The Farmer appears in two tales in the *Indian Fairy Tales* collection, embodying contrasting aspects of the peasant archetype. In "[[the-golden-serpent|The Gold-giving Serpent]]" (Chapter 15), he is Haridatta, a poor farmer whose life is transformed by a serpent's daily gift of gold — and whose son's greed destroys that blessing. In "The Farmer and the Money-lender" (Chapter 21), he is a simple cultivator who suffers under a money-lender's exploitation but ultimately triumphs through an unexpected legal loophole.

## Role in the Tales

### The Gold-giving Serpent

Haridatta is introduced as "a farmer, but poor was the return his land gave him" (015-001). Despite his poverty, he is pious and leaves offerings for the village deity. One day a serpent emerges from an anthill on his land and reveals itself as the deity, rewarding Haridatta's devotion with a gold coin each day.

The farmer's virtue is his contentment — he accepts the single daily coin without demanding more. The tragedy comes from his son, who attempts to kill the serpent for its presumed hoard of gold, thereby ending the blessing. The farmer's role in this tale is to represent the virtue of modest acceptance contrasted with destructive greed.

### The Farmer and the Money-lender

Here the farmer "suffered much at the hands of a money-lender" (021-001). Desperate, he sets out to find the god Ram to ask for justice. On his journey he meets a Jogi who explains that Ram has already appeared to him:

> "That was Ram himself who appeared to you!" said the Jogi (021-008)

The farmer returns home and, armed with the Jogi's counsel, outwits the money-lender in court, reclaiming his property. This tale casts the farmer as a folk hero whose apparent simplicity masks practical wisdom. The moral is explicit:

> "this true story shows that a farmer once got the better of a money-lender" (021-016)

## Relationships

- **[[the-golden-serpent|The Gold-giving Serpent]]** — the farmer's benefactor, a serpent who rewards devotion with daily gold (015-001); the relationship is devotional, not transactional
- **The Farmer's Son** — whose greed destroys the blessing (015-001); the generational contrast between contented father and grasping son is central to the tale's moral
- **[[the-money-lender|The Money-lender]]** — the farmer's antagonist, an exploiter who is ultimately outwitted (021-001)
- **Ram / [[the-jogi|The Jogi]]** — divine figures who guide the farmer to justice (021-008); the farmer's piety is rewarded by supernatural intervention

## Moral and Cultural Significance

The farmer in these tales represents the Indian folk ideal of the *kisan* (peasant) as both humble devotee and resilient survivor. In "[[the-golden-serpent|The Gold-giving Serpent]]," the farmer embodies the virtue of *santosha* (contentment) — he is blessed not because he asks, but because he offers. The tale's tragedy is not the farmer's but his son's: greed (*lobha*) destroys what devotion built.

In "The Farmer and the Money-lender," the same archetype takes on a different moral register: the farmer is a *bhola* (simple one) whose very simplicity becomes a form of strength. Like many folk heroes, he succeeds not through sophisticated strategy but through sincerity and divine aid. Together, these tales present the farmer as a moral center in a world of predators — a figure whose connection to the land and to the divine offers the only reliable path through hardship.
