## Overview

Robbers appear as antagonists in at least three tales in the *Indian Fairy Tales* collection. Their most developed role is in "Pride Goeth Before a Fall" (Chapter 18), where three notorious robbers are defeated not by force but by a clever merchant's verbal trick that exploits their lack of education. They also feature in "[[soothsayers-son|The Soothsayer's Son]]" (Chapter 10) as opportunistic criminals and in "The [[ivory-city|Ivory City]]" (Chapter 26) as an organised family of tower-dwelling bandits.

## Role in the Tales

In "Pride Goeth Before a Fall," three robbers terrorise a village and intercept a group of ten cloth merchants returning home. The robbers seize the merchants' goods, and the ten men sit in despair — all except one clever merchant who devises a verbal trap (018-002). He tells the robbers that there were ten of them while the robbers are only three, reasoning that if three could overpower ten, it proves nothing of their strength. When the robbers, uneducated and unable to follow the logic (018-010), are persuaded to sit passively while their hands and feet are bound, they are captured and humbled (018-015).

In "[[soothsayers-son|The Soothsayer's Son]]," robbers are briefly mentioned as lurking on the road, their presence used by [[soothsayers-son|Gangazara]] to [[supernatural-beings|demons]]trate his supposed prophetic powers (010-008).

In "The [[ivory-city|Ivory City]]," the robbers form a more organised threat — a family of eleven (ten brothers and an elder sister) living in a tower with strong-rooms and a corpse pit, who prey on travellers (026-031). The prince and vizier's son are lured to their tower but escape through the vizier's quick thinking (026-038).

The robbers' defeat in Chapter 18 is memorably complete:

> "The robbers were all uneducated, and thought that they were ten men, the robbers only three, that if three could overcome ten... they tied the hands and feet of the robbers. These were entirely humbled." (018-014)

## Relationships

- **The Clever Cloth Merchant** — outwits the robbers in Ch. 18 with a linguistic trick
- **[[the-prince|The Prince]] and the Vizier's Son** — escape from the robber family's tower in Ch. 26
- **The Elder Sister** — the robber family's cook and housekeeper, who helps the captives escape on promise of marriage (026-038)
- **[[soothsayers-son|Gangazara]] ([[soothsayers-son|The Soothsayer's Son]])** — uses the robbers' presence to [[supernatural-beings|demons]]trate his "prophetic" skill (010-008)

## Moral and Cultural Significance

Robbers in these tales are consistently portrayed as physically powerful but intellectually weak. Their defeat in Chapter 18 — through a clever verbal trick rather than combat — reinforces a common Indian folk theme: intelligence and education triumph over brute force. The phrase "Pride goeth before a fall" applies as much to the robbers' overconfidence as to anyone else's.

The [[ivory-city|Ivory City]] robbers (Chapter 26), with their organised tower operation, represent a more dangerous, systemic threat — but they too are ultimately outwitted through quick thinking and the help of an insider, the elder sister who betrays them for the promise of marriage (026-038).
