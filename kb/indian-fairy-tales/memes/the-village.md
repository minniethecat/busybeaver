# The Village

## Location

The village is the rural settlement that forms the everyday backdrop of many tales — the home of Brahmans, farmers, cloth merchants, and other common folk, as opposed to the courtly world of the palace. Tales repeatedly open by placing a character "in a certain village": [[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]] the poor Brahman lives so (011-001), and ten cloth merchants dwell together "in a certain village" (018-001). The village is a modest, self-contained community: it is led by a "village chief" at whose house a wandering musician performs (006-020), and it is small enough that "there is not another village within several miles" (026-032). Villages are the units of rural wealth and administration — a king rewards a favourite with "the revenue of many villages" (011-004).

## Appearances in Tales

- [[harisarman]] — [[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]] is introduced as "a certain Brahman in a certain village," poor and in service; he is later granted "the revenue of many villages" as a reward (011-001, 011-004).
- [[the-charmed-ring]] — The wandering merchant "reached a village, where some folk were quarrelling" over a possession, prompting his intervention (012-001).
- [[pride-goeth-before-a-fall]] — "In a certain village there lived ten cloth merchants," whose homecoming to their village frames the tale (018-001, 018-016).
- [[the-ass-in-the-lions-skin]] — A hawker "stopped in a village"; when "the villagers knew the creature to be an ass," the [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]]d donkey is exposed and beaten (020-003, 020-009).
- [[the-farmer-and-the-money-lender]] — The farmer "went back to his village rejoicing" after his dealings with the money-lender (021-009).
- [[why-the-fish-laughed]] — Travellers journey to "a certain village" and arrive "at a big village," where a clever exchange of riddling remarks unfolds (024-012, 024-018).
- [[the-ivory-city-and-its-fairy-princess]] — Travellers reach "a certain village" where they "determined to spend the night," one of several rural stops on their journey (026-018, 026-032).

## Significance

The village grounds the tales in ordinary rural life and supplies the humble origins from which many protagonists rise. It is the starting point of the classic rags-to-riches arc: the poor village Brahman [[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]], by luck and cunning, wins the revenue of many villages and a place at court (011-001, 011-004). It is a community of collective judgment — the villagers together recognise the ass beneath the lion's skin and punish the imposture (020-009). It also serves as a way-station on journeys, a place where travellers halt for the night and where riddling test-conversations occur (024-018, 026-018). Set against the palace, the village represents the world of common sense, labour, and shared knowledge, and its people frequently see through the pretensions that fool their betters.

## Cultural or Historical Notes

The village is the fundamental social unit of traditional India, and the tales faithfully reflect its structure: a headman or "village chief" (006-020), resident Brahmans who serve as priests and teachers, farmers who work the land, and merchants who trade cloth and goods. The granting of "the revenue of many villages" (011-004) mirrors the historical practice by which rulers rewarded servants and favourites with the tax income of specified villages rather than with coin alone. This everyday rural world — of quarrels settled among neighbours, of travellers seeking a night's lodging, and of communities quick to unmask a fraud — provides the realistic counterweight to the collection's kings, magicians, and talking animals, and reflects the folk origins of the tales that Joseph Jacobs gathered from Indian oral and literary tradition.
