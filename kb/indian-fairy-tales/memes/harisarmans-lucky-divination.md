# Harisarman's Lucky Divination

## Summary

In the eleventh tale, the poor and foolish Brahman [[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]], slighted at his patron's feast, resolves to feign "a knowledge of magic" to win respect (011-002). He himself steals his master's son-in-law's horse and hides it, then "recovers" it by pretended divination, and so is "honoured by all men as a sage" (011-002). His reputation soon brings him before the king to name a palace thief. Trapped by his own pretence, he laments aloud to his own tongue — "O Tongue, what is this that you have done through your greediness?" — just as the thieving maid, who is named [[the-maid|Jihva]] ("Tongue"), listens at the door; terrified that she has been discovered, she confesses and reveals where the treasure is buried (011-003). Tested a third time with a covered pitcher, [[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]], thinking his last hour has come, cries out his childhood pet-name "[[harisarman-the-brahman|Froggie]]" — and the pitcher happens to contain a frog (011-005). Each fraud succeeds purely by luck.

> "Fate brings about, as if in sport, things impossible, for when calamity was so near, who would have thought chance would have brought us success? While I was blaming my jihva, the thief Jihva suddenly flung herself at my feet." (011-004)

## Context

[[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]] had entered the service of the rich householder [[sthuladatta|Sthuladatta]], but at the wedding feast of [[sthuladatta|Sthuladatta]]'s daughter "no one thought of him" and he was left unfed (011-001). Humiliated, he devised his artifice of false magic and instructed his wife to advertise his powers, laying the trap of the hidden horse (011-002). The success of that first deception — the horse "found" exactly where he had concealed it — made him famous as a diviner and drew the king's summons when treasure was stolen from the palace (011-002, 011-003). Thus each escalating test grows out of the last: a self-manufactured miracle, then a genuine theft he cannot possibly solve, then a deliberate trial by the sceptical minister Devajnanin (011-005).

## Participants

- [[harisarman-the-brahman]] — the poor, foolish Brahman who fakes magical knowledge and is saved from exposure three times by coincidence, ending rich and honoured (011-002, 011-004, 011-005).
- **[[the-maid|Jihva]]** — the palace maid "named [[the-maid|Jihva]] (which means Tongue)" who, with her brother, stole the king's treasure and, overhearing [[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]] blame his own "tongue," believes herself discovered and confesses (011-003).
- **[[sthuladatta|Sthuladatta]]** — [[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]]'s wealthy patron, whose stolen-then-recovered horse establishes the impostor's reputation (011-002).
- **Devajnanin** — the king's minister who doubts [[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]] and devises the frog-in-the-pitcher test to expose him (011-005).
- **The king** — who summons [[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]] to find the palace thief and, convinced by each lucky success, rewards him lavishly (011-003, 011-005).

## Outcome or Consequence

The maid [[the-maid|Jihva]]'s confession lets [[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]] lead the king to the buried treasure "under the pomegranate tree," claiming the thief had escaped with part of it; the pleased king "gave him the revenue of many villages" (011-004). When Devajnanin persuades the king to test him further with the hidden frog, [[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]]'s despairing cry of "[[harisarman-the-brahman|Froggie]]" chimes so exactly with the pitcher's contents that the onlookers applaud — "Ah! a great sage, he knows even about the frog!" — and the king, "thinking that this was all due to knowledge of divination," rewards him with still more villages, "gold, an umbrella, and state carriages of all kinds" (011-005). [[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]] keeps [[the-maid|Jihva]]'s own gold in exchange for sparing her (011-004), and the tale closes: "So [[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]] prospered in the world" (011-005).

## Cultural or Moral Significance

The episode is a comic [[trickster-figure|trickster]] tale in which fraud triumphs not through skill but through repeated, improbable coincidence — the pun on "[[the-maid|Jihva]]/Tongue," the accident of the pet-name "[[harisarman-the-brahman|Froggie]]," the horse the diviner hid himself. [[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]]'s own reflection supplies the moral commentary: "Secret crimes manifest themselves by means of fear" (011-004) — the thief betrays herself, and luck rewards the bold pretender. The tale gently satirizes credulous belief in astrology and magic, for the "great sage" is a hungry fool whose every success is chance dressed as [[fate-and-prophecy|prophecy]], while the sceptical minister who alone sees through him is overruled by the king's wonder. It belongs to the wide family of "lucky impostor" stories, celebrating quick wit and audacity even as it exposes the hollowness of pretended supernatural knowledge.
