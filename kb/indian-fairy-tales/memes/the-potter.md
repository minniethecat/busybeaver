## Overview

The Potter is a minor but pivotal character in "A Lac of Rupees for a Bit of Advice," where he appears at a critical juncture in the prince's journey (014-024-014-029). He is an artisan who is simultaneously laughing and crying — caught between the absurd honour and the deadly consequence of his son being summoned to marry a princess whose husbands all die on their wedding night. His plight provides the [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]]d prince with a path back to royal status.

## Role in the Tale

The prince, having been banished by his father the king and having narrowly escaped death multiple times, encounters the Potter while walking along a road (014-024). The Potter is sitting with his wife and children, alternately laughing and crying. When the prince asks why, the Potter initially tries to dismiss him but eventually explains: the king's daughter must be married every day, and all her husbands die on the first night. Nearly all the young men of the land have perished, and now the Potter's own son is to be called (014-027).

The prince volunteers to take the son's place, asking only for suitable garments and ornaments (014-028). The Potter provides beautiful raiment and the prince goes to the palace in his stead (014-029). This substitution enables the prince to face and slay the two Shahmar snakes that emerge from the princess's nostrils each night, ending the cycle of deaths and winning both the princess and eventually the kingdom (014-029-014-032).

## Key Deeds

- **Revealing the princess's secret**: The Potter explains to the prince the deadly pattern of the princess's marriages, providing the knowledge the prince needs to prepare (014-027).
- **Providing the [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]]**: He gives the prince beautiful raiment and ornaments, enabling him to enter the palace as the Potter's son (014-029).

## Relationships

- **[[the-prince|The Prince]]** — The banished prince who volunteers to marry the princess in the Potter's son's stead, thereby saving the son's life and advancing his own fortune (014-028-014-029).
- **The Potter's Wife and Children** — His family shares in his paradoxical laughter and weeping over their impending tragedy (014-024).
- **[[the-princess|The Princess]]** — The king's daughter whose lethal condition is the indirect cause of the Potter's distress (014-027).

## Moral and Cultural Significance

The Potter exemplifies the archetype of the humble commoner caught in a cruel royal decree — his son is to be sacrificed to a princess's curse simply because the pool of eligible young men has been exhausted. His simultaneous laughter and weeping captures the absurdity of [[caste-in-folklore|social hierarchy]]: it is ludicrous that a potter's son should marry a princess, yet the "honour" carries a death sentence (014-027). The Potter's willingness to accept the prince's substitution shows the desperate pragmatism of those at the mercy of royal power. He also serves as a narrative device illustrating the third piece of advice the prince had purchased: "If a man has to do any work, he must do it himself, and do it with might and without fear" (014-007) — the prince takes direct action rather than letting fate run its course.
