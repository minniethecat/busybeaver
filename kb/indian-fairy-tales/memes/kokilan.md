## Overview

Kokilan is the princess at the end of [[raja-rasalu]] (Chapter 19), whose fate is tied to a mango tree. The victorious [[rasalu|Raja Rasalu]], after defeating the giant Sarkap, goes to the Murti Hills and places the newborn Kokilan "in an underground palace" beneath the earth, planting a mango branch at the entrance with a vow: "In twelve years the mango tree will blossom; then will I return and marry Kokilan" (019-142). When the twelve years have passed and the mango tree flowers as promised, [[rasalu|Rasalu]] returns and marries her (019-143). Kokilan's story distills the motif of the **hidden bride** — the woman placed in suspended time, waiting underground for a destined husband.

## Role in the Tale

Kokilan appears only in the tale's closing verses, yet her placement is structurally significant. The entire narrative of [[rasalu|Rasalu]]'s exploits — his birth and concealment, his conquest of giants, his chess-game with Sarkap — culminates not in a throne but in a marriage. [[rasalu|Rasalu]]'s planting of the mango branch and his twelve-year wait mirror the folkloric pattern of the **orchard bridegroom**: the hero who sets a natural timer and returns when it ripens. Kokilan, sequestered in the underground palace, is both the prize and the proof of [[rasalu|Rasalu]]'s mastery over time itself.

Her underground palace also echoes [[rasalu|Rasalu]]'s own origin: he too was hidden in an underground palace with nurses and a colt born the same day (019-003). The symmetry suggests that Kokilan is not simply [[rasalu|Rasalu]]'s bride but his narrative double — a second hidden child whose emergence marks the tale's true conclusion.

## Key Appearances

- **Placed underground**: [[rasalu|Rasalu]] carries the newborn Kokilan to the Murti Hills, places her in an underground palace, and seals the entrance with a mango branch timed to bloom in twelve years (019-142).
- **Married after twelve years**: When the mango tree flowers as foretold, [[rasalu|Rasalu]] returns and marries Princess Kokilan (019-143).
- **Noted by the editor**: The notes section observes that the marriage to Kokilan belongs to the [[rasalu|Rasalu]] cycle and appears "at the end of our part of it" (030-080).

## Relationships

- **[[raja-rasalu|Raja Rasalu]]** — the legendary hero who places Kokilan underground as a newborn, then returns to claim her as his wife when she comes of age (019-142, 019-143).
- **Sarkap** — the giant king whom [[rasalu|Rasalu]] defeats at chaupur before claiming Kokilan; his defeat makes [[rasalu|Rasalu]]'s kingship secure and the marriage possible (019-143).

## Moral and Cultural Significance

Kokilan represents a recurring figure in Indian heroic legend: the woman whose life is set on a fixed trajectory by a powerful male agent, her growth marked by natural signs rather than personal choice. The mango tree functions as a botanical calendar, and Kokilan's twelve-year wait in the underground palace — like [[rasalu|Rasalu]]'s own concealed childhood — reflects the folkloric belief in underground spaces as a kind of suspended time, where the normal passage of years does not age the body in the same way.

The compressed narrative of her marriage in a single couplet also reflects the conventions of the *qissa* (oral verse-tale) tradition from which the [[rasalu|Rasalu]] cycle originates: details of courtship and domestic life are subordinated to the hero's exploits, and the bride's appearance signals closure rather than the beginning of a new story.
