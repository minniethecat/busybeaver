# The Abduction of Balna

## Summary

This event marks the turn from domestic happiness to captivity in the tale of [[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]]. [[balna]], the cleverest and most beautiful of seven sisters, has married one of seven princes and borne a son. While her husband is away, the magician [[punchkin-magician]], entering the palace in the guise of a holy man, seizes her and carries her off to a distant tower, where she is held prisoner for years.

> …last of all, took the beautiful Balna. (004-038)

## Context

[[balna|Balna]] is singled out from the story's opening as exceptional: "the youngest, named [[balna|Balna]], was more clever than the rest" (004-001). She and her sisters are married to seven neighbouring princes, and "last of all" the marriages, the beautiful [[balna|Balna]] is wedded (004-038). In time "[[balna|Balna]] had a little son" (004-040), and the family lives happily until her husband, "the seventh Prince ([[balna|Balna]]'s husband)," announces he will go out hunting (004-041) and departs.

> About a year after this Balna had a little son… (004-040)

## Participants

- [[balna]] — the youngest and cleverest queen, taken from her home and infant son.
- [[punchkin-magician]] — the wicked magician who covets [[balna|Balna]] and abducts her.

## Outcome or Consequence

With the prince gone, the magician strikes. "As [[balna|Balna]] was rocking her baby's cradle, and whilst her sisters were working in the room below" (004-044), [[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]] gains entry by claiming a sacred exemption — "I am a holy man, you must let me in" (004-044) — and passes through the palace "till at last he reached the room where [[balna|Balna]] sat singing beside her little" child (004-045). She is carried off with no chance "of making an escape, or of letting her sisters know what had become of her" (004-045).

The family returns to find the baby "all alone, and [[balna|Balna]] nowhere to be seen" (004-046). Years pass in this grief until "[[balna|Balna]]'s son was fourteen years old" (004-047). The magician holds her imprisoned in a high tower "because she refused to marry him, and she was so closely guarded" (004-054) that rescue seems impossible — setting up the long quest that will end in [[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]]'s death.

## Cultural or Moral Significance

[[balna|Balna]]'s abduction supplies the tale's engine: the wrong that the entire narrative exists to redress. It combines two motifs the collection returns to often — the coveted bride seized by a magical villain, and the false ascetic who exploits the sanctity owed to holy men. Jacobs elsewhere remarks that "unholy fakirs are rather rare" in the tradition (030-092), which makes [[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]]'s [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]] as a "holy man" a pointed inversion of expected piety. Crucially, [[balna|Balna]] does not merely suffer; her cleverness, established at the outset, later becomes the means of the villain's defeat, so that the victim of the abduction ultimately authors the rescue.
