## Overview

The Elephant appears throughout the Indian Fairy Tales as a symbol of royal prestige, power, and wealth. As a grand mount for princesses and kings, and as a prized gift bestowed upon favoured individuals, the elephant reflects the material splendour of Indian courtly life.

Unlike many animal characters who speak or act with agency, the elephant serves primarily as an emblem of status. It is a living treasure that marks its rider or recipient as a person of the highest rank.

## Role in the Tales

In "[[the-boy-who-had-a-moon-on-his-forehead-and-a-star-on-his-chin]]" (Chapter 22), the elephant plays its most prominent role. The youngest princess orders "a grand elephant to be ready for her" to ride the next day (022-049).

After discovering the boy with the moon on his forehead, she places him upon her elephant and escorts him to the palace (022-052). The elephant thus serves as the vehicle of the boy's elevation from obscurity to royal recognition.

When the boy recounts his story, he describes how the princess "sat on an elephant and went round" looking at the garden (022-072). At his departure, the prince receives "a great many horses, and elephants, and all sorts of presents" as gifts befitting his new station (022-076).

In "[[the-ivory-city-and-its-fairy-princess]]" (Chapter 26), elephants feature in a dramatic ruse. A servant bursts in announcing that the king's elephants "had gone mad, and were rushing about the city" (026-068).

The old woman, fearing "lest the elephants should go and push down her hut" (026-069), begs to leave at once (026-070). At the tale's conclusion, the protagonists receive "presents of horses and elephants, and jewels and rich cloths" (026-113).

In "[[a-lac-of-rupees-for-a-bit-of-advice]]" (Chapter 14), the elephant appears twice as a gift. The king gives the prince "elephants, horses, jewels, and abundant money" (014-032). The prince later bestows "two elephants, two beautiful horses" and other treasures upon his sister (014-033).

## Key Appearances

**The Grand Mount.** The youngest princess orders a "grand elephant" readied (022-049). It carries the moon-prince to the palace (022-052).

**[[the-garden|The Garden]] Inspection.** The princess rides an elephant as she tours the garden and discovers the boy (022-072).

**Royal Gifts.** Elephants are given as the ultimate gift alongside horses and jewels (014-032)(014-033)(022-076)(026-113).

**The Stampede Ruse.** A servant claims the king's elephants have run mad through the city (026-068). The ruse sends the old woman fleeing in terror (026-069)(026-070).

## Moral or Cultural Significance

The elephant in these tales is never merely an animal — it is a unit of royal currency, a measure of a ruler's generosity and wealth. To give elephants was to confer the highest honour; to ride an elephant was to display one's rank before all.

The elephant's recurring role as a gift underscores the storytelling tradition's emphasis on royal largesse and the material trappings of kingship. Even the stampeding elephants of Chapter 26, though momentarily dangerous, remain "royal elephants" whose very presence testifies to the king's possession of such powerful beasts.
