# Rasalu's Game of Chaupur with Sarkap

## Summary

In the climactic episode of the nineteenth tale, [[rasalu]] sits down to play *chaupur* — a dice-and-board game — against the tyrant-king Sarkap, whose custom was that "every one who came to play chaupur with the King had to beat in the game, or lose his head" (019-085). The stakes rise across three games, with each player wagering his kingdom, his wealth, and finally his own head (019-105). Sarkap cheats by loosing his famous rat, Dhol [[king|Raja]], to upset the pieces on the sly, so that [[rasalu|Rasalu]] loses the first two games and gives up his armour and his horse (019-106, 019-107). Only when a grateful kitten in his pocket begins to struggle does [[rasalu|Rasalu]] remember a warning to distrust Sarkap's dice; he insists on playing with his own dice, and the kitten watches the window to frighten off the cheating rat (019-119, 019-121). With the cheat neutralised, [[rasalu|Rasalu]] wins back his arms and horse and then wins the final game, so that Sarkap loses "first his kingdom, then the wealth of the whole world, and lastly his head" (019-133).

> Then they began to play, whilst the women stood round in a circle, and the kitten watched Dhol Raja from the window. Then Sarkap lost, first his kingdom, then the wealth of the whole world, and lastly his head. (019-133)

## Context

[[rasalu|Rasalu]] had "started off to play chaupur with King Sarkap" (019-034) despite being warned along the way. A talking corpse advised him to make his dice from the bones of dead men in a graveyard so as to counter the enchantment of the game (019-054, 019-055). On the road [[rasalu|Rasalu]] also performed acts of mercy — most importantly restoring a mother cat's kittens from an unbaked potter's pot (019-102, 019-103) — that would later save him. On reaching Sarkap's city, seventy maidens, daughters of kings the tyrant had beheaded, greeted him (019-059), a grim measure of the toll of Sarkap's game. Before play began, Sarkap fixed his stakes on kingdom, world-wealth, and head, and [[rasalu|Rasalu]] matched him with his arms, his horse, and his own head (019-105).

## Participants

- [[rasalu]] — the hero-prince who accepts the fatal wager, survives Sarkap's cheating through the grateful kitten's aid, and wins all three games.
- **Sarkap** — the tyrant-king who beheads every guest who loses his game, and who cheats with his rat Dhol [[king|Raja]] before finally losing his own head (019-091, 019-106, 019-133).
- **The kitten** — one of the kittens [[rasalu|Rasalu]] saved from the potter's kiln; hidden in his pocket, it rouses his memory and then guards the window so Sarkap's rat dares not upset the pieces (019-119, 019-121, 019-122).
- **Bhaunr, the Arab steed** — [[rasalu|Rasalu]]'s faithful sea-born horse, staked and nearly lost, who cries out the reminder to "place hand in your pocket" that recalls the kitten and the warning (019-107, 019-114).

## Outcome or Consequence

Having won the third game and Sarkap's head, [[rasalu|Rasalu]] shows mercy. When a servant announces the birth of a daughter to Sarkap and the despairing king orders the ill-omened infant killed, [[rasalu|Rasalu]] intervenes: "Not so, oh king! She has done no evil. Give me this child to wife; and if you will vow... never again to play chaupur for another's head, I will spare yours now!" (019-135). Sarkap swears the solemn vow, ending his reign of beheadings, and gives [[rasalu|Rasalu]] the newborn, [[kokilan|Kokilan]], on a golden dish (019-136). [[rasalu|Rasalu]] also frees a band of Sarkap's prisoners on his way out (019-141). He places the infant [[kokilan|Kokilan]] in an underground palace with a mango branch, vowing to return when the tree blossoms; and "after twelve years, the mango tree began to flower, and [[rasalu|Raja Rasalu]] married the [[kokilan|Princess Kokilan]], whom he won from Sarkap when he played chaupur with the King" (019-142, 019-143).

## Cultural or Moral Significance

The game dramatizes the folk motif of the deadly wager won by the hero through gratitude repaid. [[rasalu|Rasalu]]'s early kindness to the mother cat returns at the decisive moment, so that the kitten — not brute strength — undoes Sarkap's cheating; the episode thus binds the story of [[gratitude-and-ingratitude]] to the triumph of the just over the tyrannical. Sarkap's insistence on playing "for another's head" marks him as an abuser of power, and his fall enacts a form of [[royal-justice]]: the king who lived by beheading his guests is himself brought to lose his head. Yet [[rasalu|Rasalu]] tempers victory with mercy, sparing both the tyrant and his innocent daughter in exchange for a binding vow, so that the event closes not on vengeance but on the reform of a cruel custom and the promise of a marriage.
