## Overview

The Thief appears across multiple tales in the Indian Fairy Tales collection as a stock antagonist whose crimes set plots in motion.

Whether as a solitary palace thief or as a member of an organized band, the thief represents the ever-present threat to wealth and order in the tale world. The thief also serves as a test of cleverness, wisdom, and sometimes simple luck for the protagonists.

## Role in the Tales

In "[[harisarman]]" (Chapter 11), a thief steals jewels from [[the-palace|the king's palace]]. The theft is attributed to "a thief from the palace" who has taken the queen's jewels (011-003).

This crime creates the crisis that [[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]], the tale's unlikely hero, must resolve. The thief is personified with a name — "the thief [[the-maid|Jihva]]" — playing on the double meaning of *jihva* (tongue), as [[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]]'s accidental muttering exposes the culprit (011-004).

In "[[pride-goeth-before-a-fall]]" (Chapter 18), thieves feature as a band of robbers dividing their spoils. When "each party pounced upon a thief," only "the remaining one — the leader" escapes (018-015). Their internal conflict provides the mechanism for the tale's resolution.

In "[[how-the-wicked-sons-were-duped]]" (Chapter 28), the thief motif extends metaphorically into the behaviour of the wicked sons, whose deception and trickery mirror the thief's craft.

## Key Appearances

**[[the-palace|The Palace]] Theft.** A thief steals the queen's jewels from the palace, triggering the investigation that [[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]] must solve (011-003).

**[[the-maid|Jihva]] the Thief.** The thief is named [[the-maid|Jihva]] ("tongue"), and [[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]]'s muttering about his own *jihva* accidentally exposes the culprit (011-004).

**The Band of Thieves.** A group of thieves divides their loot, but their internal quarrel leads to their capture — each party seizes one thief, though the leader escapes (018-015).

**Deception and Duping.** The motif of thievery extends into Chapter 28, where the wicked sons' trickery mirrors the thief's craft.

## Moral or Cultural Significance

The thief serves a dual narrative function. On one level, the thief is a straightforward villain whose crimes must be resolved — a problem-solving device that tests the wit of the protagonist.

On a deeper level, the thief embodies a wider cultural anxiety about the insecurity of wealth. The tales consistently reward cleverness over brute force: [[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]] triumphs through accidental wordplay, and the bandits are undone by their own greed. The implicit message is that thieves carry the seeds of their own destruction.
