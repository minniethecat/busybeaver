## Overview

Moon is one of the three celestial siblings in the etiological myth "How [[sun|Sun]], Moon, and [[wind|Wind]] went out to Dinner" (Chapter 27). Unlike her selfish brothers [[sun|Sun]] and [[wind|Wind]], Moon remembers her mother Star during a great feast, saving a portion of every dainty dish under her fingernails (027-002). Her filial piety is rewarded with a blessing that makes her light "ever cool, and calm, and bright," while her brothers are cursed for their neglect (027-008). The story is a classic explanatory myth accounting for the perceived qualities of the three celestial bodies in Indian folklore.

## Role in the Tale

### The Feast

One day [[sun|Sun]], Moon, and [[wind|Wind]] are invited to dine with their uncle and aunts Thunder and Lightning. Their mother, Star (described as "one of the most distant Stars you see far up in the sky"), waits alone at home for her children's return (027-001). While [[sun|Sun]] and [[wind|Wind]] enjoy the feast greedily and selfishly without a thought of bringing anything home, Moon alone remembers her mother:

> "But the gentle Moon did not forget her. Of every dainty dish that was brought round, she placed a small portion under one of her beautiful long finger-nails, that Star might also have a share in the treat." (027-002)

### The Return and Judgement

When the siblings return home, Star asks each what they have brought. [[sun|Sun]] and [[wind|Wind]] confess they brought nothing, each justifying their selfishness with indifference (027-003). Moon alone produces a feast:

> "Mother, fetch a plate, see what I have brought you." And shaking her hands she showered down such a choice dinner as never was seen before. (027-003)

### The Blessing

Star blesses Moon for her filial devotion:

> "Daughter, because you remembered your mother, and kept for her a share in your own enjoyment, from henceforth you shall be ever cool, and calm, and bright. No noxious glare shall accompany your pure rays, and men shall always call you 'blessed.'" (027-008)

The narrator adds: "And that is why the moon's light is so soft, and cool, and beautiful even to this day" (027-009).

## Key Deeds

- **Filial Piety**: Unlike her brothers, Moon thinks of her mother even while enjoying a feast, saving portions of every dish (027-002).
- **Selflessness**: Moon's action requires effort — hiding food under her fingernails throughout the meal — yet she does so without hesitation or complaint (027-002).
- **Generosity**: Upon returning home, Moon immediately and joyfully shares her saved feast with her mother (027-003).

## Relationships

- **Star** — Moon's mother, described as "one of the most distant Stars." Star waits alone while her children feast, and it is she who pronounces the blessings and curses that define the celestial bodies' natures (027-001, 027-004–027-008).
- **[[sun|Sun]]** ([[sun]]) — Moon's elder brother, portrayed as selfish and [[greed-in-folklore|greedy]]. [[sun|Sun]] brings nothing home and is cursed to have "hot and scorching" rays that burn all they touch (027-003–027-005).
- **[[wind|Wind]]** ([[wind]]) — Moon's brother, also selfish. He too brings nothing home and is cursed to "parch and shrivel all living things" in hot weather (027-006–027-007).
- **Thunder and Lightning** — Moon's uncle and aunts, who host the feast (027-001).

## Moral and Cultural Significance

Moon's story is an etiological myth — a narrative that explains a natural phenomenon (the moon's cool, gentle light) through a moral drama. The tale belongs to a widespread folklore pattern in which natural forces are personified as a family and their qualities are explained through reward and punishment for moral conduct.

The core value celebrated is **filial piety** (*matteyya* in the Buddhist framework): the duty of children to remember and care for their parents. Moon's gentleness is presented as the direct result of her moral virtue, while [[sun|Sun]]'s harshness and [[wind|Wind]]'s destructiveness result from selfishness. This aligns the story with broader Indian folk traditions, where celestial bodies frequently serve as moral exemplars.

The contrast between Moon and her brothers also illustrates the Buddhist principle that **gentleness and remembrance** produce lasting blessings, while greed and self-absorption produce lasting curses — a theme that echoes throughout the Jacobs collection.
