## Overview

The Teacher appears in the *Indian Fairy Tales* collection in two distinct guises: as the mortal tutor employed by kings to educate their sons, and as the far-famed master of Takkasila (Taxila) — the ancient Indian centre of learning — who instructs the Bodhisatta in the Jataka-derived tale "[[the-matted-hair-demon|The Demon with the Matted Hair]]." Though a minor figure in terms of page count, the Teacher represents the high value placed on learning and mentorship in Indian storytelling tradition, connecting the fairy tales to the broader Buddhist and Brahminical educational heritage.

## Role in the Tales

### The Royal Tutor (Chapters 2 and 8)

In two tales, the teacher appears as a background figure in the education of princes. In "[[laili|Loving Laili]]," [[king-dantal|King Dantal]] employs a teacher for [[majnun|Prince Majnun]] and the vizier's son: "[[king-dantal|King Dantal]] had a teacher for them to teach them to read and write" (008-001). In "[[the-prince|The Raja's Son]] and [[princess-labam|Princess Labam]]," four fakirs are introduced as disciples whose "teacher and master had died" (002-027), leaving them in search of a successor — a detail that sets the prince's journey in motion when they offer him magical gifts. These brief mentions establish the teacher as an expected and respected presence in the upbringing of royal children.

### The Teacher of Takkasila (Chapter 25)

The most substantive teacher figure appears in "[[the-matted-hair-demon|The Demon with the Matted Hair]]," a tale drawn from the Jataka collection. When a young lad — the Bodhisatta in a previous birth — asks "Who shall be my teacher?" (025-008), he is directed to the far-famed teacher of Takkasila. His parents tell him: "In the kingdom of Takkasila, is a far-famed teacher from whom I wish you to learn" (025-009). The lad travels to Takkasila and "was educated by this teacher; he received the Five Weapons from him" (025-010).

This teacher equips the Bodhisatta with the Five Weapons — a symbolic armament representing the spiritual and practical tools needed to face the demon of the forest. The teacher's role, though brief in the narrative, is pivotal: without his instruction, the Bodhisatta would lack the means to confront and pacify the Demon with the Matted Hair.

### The Great Teacher (Preface)

Joseph Jacobs, in his Preface, notes how the Indian fairy tales migrated along with Buddhism: "the stories of India round the great teacher" (030-001). This reference situates the Teacher archetype within the broader history of the Jataka tales — stories of the Buddha's previous births — which form the ultimate source for several tales in the collection, including "[[the-matted-hair-demon|The Demon with the Matted Hair]]" and "[[the-ass|The Ass]] in the Lion's Skin."

## Relationships

- **[[majnun|Prince Majnun]]** — his pupil in "[[laili|Loving Laili]]," taught to read and write alongside the vizier's son (008-001)
- **[[the-prince-of-five-weapons|The Bodhisatta]]** — his most significant pupil; the teacher of Takkasila arms him with the Five Weapons (025-010) that prove essential in confronting the Demon
- **The Four Fakirs** — disciples of a deceased teacher in Chapter 2, whose search for a successor sets the prince's adventure in motion (002-027)
- **[[king-dantal|King Dantal]]** — the royal employer who retains a teacher for his son's education (008-001)

## Moral and Cultural Significance

The Teacher in *Indian Fairy Tales* reflects the deep-rooted Indian institution of the guru-shishya (teacher-disciple) tradition. The far-famed teacher of Takkasila (025-009) is not merely a schoolmaster but a transmitter of sacred knowledge — the Five Weapons he bestows are both literal arms and spiritual preparedness. Takkasila itself was the most celebrated centre of learning in ancient India, attracting students from across the subcontinent, and its appearance in a Jataka tale grounds the fairy story in historical geography.

The teacher's role as a background figure — rarely central to the plot but always present at moments of preparation and education — mirrors the cultural position of the guru: essential to the hero's formation, yet standing outside the adventure itself. The teacher arms the hero and sends him forth; the hero's triumph is, in part, the teacher's vindication.

The Preface's reference to the Buddha as "the great teacher" (030-001) completes the arc: from the mortal tutors of princes, through the far-famed master of Takkasila, to the Buddha himself — the supreme teacher whose Jataka tales form the wellspring of the entire collection. In this light, every story in *Indian Fairy Tales* is, at some level, a teacher's tale.
