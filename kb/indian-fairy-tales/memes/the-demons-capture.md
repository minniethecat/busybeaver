**The Demon's Capture** is the central event of Chapter 25 of Joseph Jacobs's *Indian Fairy Tales* (1892), "[[the-matted-hair-demon|The Demon with the Matted Hair]]" — a [[jataka]] tale recounting a former birth of the [[bodhisatta|Bodhisatta]]. The young [[the-prince-of-five-weapons|Prince of the Five Weapons]], travelling home to [[benares|Benares]] after completing his education at [[Takkasila]], enters a forest inhabited by a man-eating [[the-matted-hair-demon|Demon with the Matted Hair]]. All five of his weapons — and then all four of his limbs and his head — stick fast to the Demon's matted hair. Suspended and helpless, the Prince nonetheless feels no fear, and through the "weapon of knowledge" (wisdom), he persuades the Demon to release him and then converts the Demon to a life of virtue.

## Summary

### The Approach

[[the-prince-of-five-weapons|The Prince of the Five Weapons]] — so named because he has mastered the sword, spear, bow, battle-axe, and shield — completes his education at Takkasila and sets out for [[benares|Benares]], armed with all five weapons (025-007). On his way, he reaches a forest. Men at its edge warn him:

> "Hullo, young sir, keep clear of that wood! There's a Demon in it called he of the Matted Hair: he kills every man he sees!" (025-012)

[[the-prince|The Prince]], "having confidence in himself, went straight on, fearless as a maned lion" (025-012).

### The Demon's Manifestation

At mid-forest, the Demon reveals himself in terrifying form:

> "He made himself as tall as a palm tree; his head was the size of a pagoda, his eyes as big as saucers, and he had two tusks all over knobs and bulbs; he had the face of a hawk, a variegated belly, and blue hands and feet." (025-012)

The Demon shouts: "Where are you going? Stop! You'll make a meal for me!" (025-013).

### The Weapon Assault

[[the-prince|The Prince]] responds with a threat, then fires a poisoned arrow. It sticks fast in the Demon's hair. He fires fifty arrows in succession — all stick. The Demon snaps them off and throws them down. [[the-prince|The Prince]] strikes with his three-and-thirty-inch sword — it sticks. His spear — it sticks. His club — it sticks (025-016–025-017).

### The Five-Fold Snaring

Seeing his weapons fail, the Prince strikes with his body: right hand (sticks), left hand (sticks), right foot (sticks), left foot (sticks), and finally his head (sticks). He is now suspended from the Demon's hair in five places — completely immobilized, "caught fast in five places, hanging suspended" (025-019).

> "Thus the Bodhisatta was five times snared, caught fast in five places, hanging suspended: yet he felt no fear — was not even nervous." (025-019)

### The Thunderbolt Argument

The Demon, astonished by the Prince's fearlessness, asks why he is not afraid. [[the-prince|The Prince]] replies:

> "Why should I fear, Demon? In one life a man can die but once. Besides, in my belly is a thunderbolt; if you eat me, you will never be able to digest it; this will tear your inwards into little bits, and kill you: so we shall both perish. That is why I fear nothing." (025-021)

The narrator explains: "By this, the Bodhisatta meant the weapon of knowledge which he had within him" (025-021). The Demon, convinced by this argument, releases the Prince:

> "Young sir, you are a lion of a man! I will not eat you up. I set you free from my hands, as the moon is disgorged from the jaws of Rāhu after the eclipse." (025-021)

### The Demon's Conversion

Rather than fleeing, the Prince delivers a moral discourse. He tells the Demon that his wickedness — killing and devouring travellers — is the result of evil deeds in former lives, and that continuing in wickedness will lead "from darkness to darkness": rebirth as an animal, a [[Preta]] (hungry ghost), an [[Asura]], or a short-lived human (025-023).

[[the-prince|The Prince]] establishes the Demon in the five kinds of virtue, makes him the guardian of the forest [[the-temple|shrine]], and departs. The Demon, formerly a maneater, becomes a protector of travellers.

## Context

The tale is framed as a Jataka story told by the [[Buddha|Teacher]] to a monk who has "ceased striving after righteousness" (025-001). The frame establishes the story's explicit purpose: to [[supernatural-beings|demons]]trate that effort in the right place leads to attainment. [[the-prince-of-five-weapons|The Prince of the Five Weapons]] is the [[bodhisatta]] in a former birth, and his encounter with the Demon models the Buddhist ideal of fearless wisdom confronting and converting ignorance and violence.

[[the-prince|The Prince]]'s education at Takkasila — the great ancient university city of Gandhara (modern Taxila, Pakistan) — places the tale in a recognizable geography of the Buddhist world. The road from Takkasila to [[benares|Benares]] was a major artery of the Mauryan and pre-Mauryan periods.

## Outcome and Consequence

**Immediate outcome**: The Demon releases the Prince, then undergoes moral reformation. [[the-prince|The Prince]] continues to [[benares|Benares]], becomes king, rules righteously, and "after giving alms and doing good he passed away according to his deeds" (025-023).

**Cosmic consequence**: The Demon, established in the five kinds of virtue, ceases his predation. The forest road becomes safe for travellers — a concrete improvement in the world's moral geography resulting from a single act of fearless wisdom.

**Motif connections**: The event combines several widespread folkloric motifs:
- **The Tar Baby** (objects sticking to a sticky surface) — identified by Jacobs in his Notes (030-098) as having Cherokee parallels
- **The Thunderbolt in the Belly** — a bluff that defeats a physically superior opponent
- **The Conversion of the Demon** — the reform rather than destruction of the antagonist, characteristic of Buddhist narratives
- **Rāhu and the Eclipse** — the Demon's own simile connects his release of the Prince to the cosmic serpent Rāhu disgorging the moon after an eclipse (025-021)

## Cultural and Moral Significance

This event is the tale's moral center and illustrates the Buddhist doctrine that *paññā* (wisdom) conquers where physical force cannot. [[the-prince|The Prince]]'s five weapons — sword, spear, bow, battle-axe, shield — represent conventional power; all prove useless against the Demon. The "weapon of knowledge," invisible and indestructible, is the only one that works.

The five-fold snaring — right hand, left hand, right foot, left foot, head — creates a total impasse that mirrors the five weapons' failure. The number five recurs: five weapons, five snared points, five kinds of wickedness, five kinds of virtue. This numerological structure is characteristic of Buddhist didactic narrative, where enumerated lists (the Five Precepts, the Five Aggregates, the Five Hindrances) organize moral teaching.

The Demon's conversion rather than destruction is significant. In the Jataka framework, the Bodhisatta liberates beings not only from physical danger but from spiritual ignorance. The Demon is not merely defeated — he is **saved**. This distinguishes the tale from the folkloric pattern in "[[the-prince|The Prince]] and the Fakir" (Ch. 23), where the antagonist is destroyed without the possibility of reform.

The Jacobs note (030-098) identifies the five-fold attack as having Cherokee parallels (the Tar Baby story), and the encounter between a hero and a sticky-haired demon appears in variant forms across South and Southeast Asian folklore.
