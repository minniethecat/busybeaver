The **Deccan** is a large plateau region in southern India, bounded by the Western and Eastern Ghats, mentioned as a source region for several tales in Joseph Jacobs's *Indian Fairy Tales* (1892). Unlike [[benares|Benares]] or [[the-himalayas|the Himalayas]], the Deccan does not appear as an in-story setting within the tales themselves; it appears exclusively in Jacobs's scholarly apparatus — the Preface and Notes and References — as a bibliographic source region. This distinction is critical: the Deccan's significance to the collection is ethnographic and textual, not narrative.

## Location

The Deccan Plateau covers approximately 1.9 million square kilometers of peninsular India, encompassing parts of the modern Indian states of Maharashtra, Karnataka, Telangana, Andhra Pradesh, Tamil Nadu, and Kerala. In the late 19th century, when Jacobs compiled his collection (1892), the Deccan was a region of intense British ethnographic and folkloristic activity. Colonial administrators and missionaries working in the Deccan — notably Mary Frere (*Old Deccan Days*, 1868) — collected and published oral tales that entered the comparative folklore corpus Jacobs drew upon.

The Deccan is bounded by:
- **North**: the Vindhya and Satpura mountain ranges, which historically separated North India (Hindustan) from South India (the Deccan)
- **West**: the Western Ghats (Sahyadri range), running parallel to the Arabian Sea coast
- **East**: the Eastern Ghats, running parallel to the Bay of [[bengal|Bengal]] coast
- **South**: the meeting point of the Western and Eastern Ghats at the Nilgiri Hills

## Appearances in Tales

The Deccan does not appear as a narrative setting within any of the twenty-nine tales themselves. Its presence in the collection is through Jacobs's Notes and References (Chapter 30), where he identifies the Deccan as the provenance of tales collected by Mary Frere and other 19th-century folklorists working in the Bombay Presidency and surrounding regions.

Jacobs draws on Deccan sources for comparative notes, tracing parallels between the tales in his collection and variants recorded in the Deccan oral tradition. For example:

- The "Demon with the Matted Hair" tale (Ch. 25) is noted as having Deccan analogues in the Frere collection (030-098)
- Several [[talking-animals|animal fables]] in the collection share motifs with Deccan oral tales documented by British ethnographers

## Significance

The Deccan's significance to the collection is **ethnographic rather than narrative**. It represents one pole of Jacobs's source geography: tales flow from the Himalayan north ([[kashmir|Kashmir]], collected by J. H. Knowles), the Gangetic plain ([[benares|Benares]]), and the Deccan south (collected by Mary Frere). This tripartite source geography — North, Center, South — gave Jacobs's collection a pan-Indian scope despite containing only twenty-nine tales.

For readers and researchers using this wiki, the Deccan page serves as a **navigational anchor**: it documents a source region that, while absent from the tales' diegetic worlds, shaped which stories reached Jacobs and thus which stories appear in this collection. The Deccan's folkloric tradition — rich in [[talking-animals|animal fables]], [[trickster-figure|trickster]] tales, and local goddess narratives — contributed motifs that appear throughout the corpus, even when individual tales are not explicitly set there.

## Cultural and Historical Notes

The Deccan has been a distinct cultural region of India since at least the Satavahana period (c. 200 BCE–200 CE). Its linguistic boundary — the divide between Indo-Aryan languages of the north (Hindi, Gujarati) and Dravidian languages of the south (Marathi, Kannada, Telugu, Tamil) — also marks a folkloric boundary. Deccan oral tales often feature different stock characters, different caste configurations, and different supernatural beings than their northern counterparts.

Key reference works from the Deccan region:
- Mary Frere, *Old Deccan Days; or, Hindoo Fairy Legends Current in Southern India* (London, 1868) — collected from the oral narratives of Anna Liberata de Souza, a Deccan storyteller
- The tale "[[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]]" (Ch. 4) has Deccan analogues in the Frere collection, though Jacobs's version is sourced from a different collector
