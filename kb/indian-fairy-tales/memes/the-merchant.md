## Overview

The Merchant is a recurring commoner figure in the *Indian Fairy Tales* collection, appearing as a backdrop figure of urban commerce. In "The [[ivory-city|Ivory City]] and its Fairy Princess" (Chapter 26), he serves as the catalyst for the entire narrative: his wife is accidentally struck by the prince's arrow, triggering the prince's banishment and subsequent quest for the fairy princess [[gulizar|Gulizar]] (026-001).

## Role in the Tales

The merchant's most significant role is in "The [[ivory-city|Ivory City]] and its Fairy Princess," where his wife is grazed by a stray arrow while standing at an upstairs window. The merchant, believing his wife dead, cries out for help (026-002). He then reports the incident to the king, setting in motion the chain of events that leads to the prince's exile and the revelation of the beautiful fairy [[gulizar|Gulizar of the Ivory City]].

In "The Charmed Ring," the merchant is a background figure: a merchant gives his son a start in life, and the merchant's son uses a magical ring (012-001). In "A Lac of Rupees for a Bit of Advice," a generous merchant advises a poor Brahman to seek the king's help (014-002).

The pivotal moment is the merchant's wife's identification of the prince and the vizier's son:

> "As soon as these two appeared in front of the merchant's window they were recognised by the merchant's wife, and at once reported to the king." (026-008)

## Relationships

- **The Merchant's Wife** — central to the plot of "The [[ivory-city|Ivory City]]"; her injury and identification of the prince set the story in motion (026-001)
- **[[king|The King]]** — the merchant appeals to the king for justice after his wife is injured (026-004)
- **[[the-prince|The Prince]]** — the inadvertent cause of his wife's injury; the merchant's complaint leads to the prince's banishment (026-001)
- **[[the-brahman|The Brahman]]** — in Ch. 14, a recipient of the merchant's charity and guidance (014-002)

## Moral and Cultural Significance

The merchant figure represents the urban commoner class in Indian folklore — neither royal nor peasant, but a middling figure whose household becomes the accidental intersection with royal affairs. In "The [[ivory-city|Ivory City]]," the merchant's role is that of the wronged citizen who seeks justice from the king, a pattern that reinforces the ideal of royal accountability: even the king's own son is not above the law when a commoner is harmed (026-004).

The merchant provides a narrative function familiar across folk traditions: the ordinary person whose misfortune unwittingly launches the hero's extraordinary journey.
