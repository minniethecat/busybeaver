## Overview

The Lion is the central antagonist of the Jataka tale "The Lion and the Crane" (001-001-001-021). He is explicitly identified as Devadatta the Traitor in a previous birth — the cousin and rival of the Buddha who repeatedly tried to kill him (001-021). When a bone becomes lodged in his throat, the Lion begs a crane (the Bodhisatta) to save him, promising not to harm him. Once saved, he refuses any reward, declaring that the crane is fortunate merely to have survived being between his teeth (001-007-001-010).

## Role in the Tale

The story opens with the Lion suffering terribly after a bone sticks in his throat while eating meat. His throat swells and he cannot take food (001-001). A white crane, who is the Bodhisatta in a previous birth, sees the Lion's suffering and offers to help, but hesitates — "dare not enter thy mouth for fear thou mightest eat me" (001-001). The Lion swears he will not harm the crane, pleading "only save my life" (001-001).

The crane devises a clever precaution: he places a small stick between the Lion's jaws to keep the mouth propped open, then inserts his head and strikes the bone with his beak until it falls out. He quickly withdraws, knocking the stick away, and settles safely on a branch (001-001).

After recovering, the Lion kills a buffalo and feasts. The crane, wishing to test the Lion's gratitude, asks what return he will receive for his service (001-002-001-005). The Lion's infamous reply reveals his true nature:

> "As I feed on blood, / And always hunt for prey, / 'Tis much that thou art still alive / Having once been between my teeth." (001-007-001-010)

The crane responds with two verses condemning ingratitude and wisely departs, "neither envying nor abusing" (001-011-001-020). The Buddha later reveals that the Lion was Devadatta the Traitor and the crane was the Buddha himself (001-021).

## Key Deeds

- **Pleading for help**: The suffering Lion begs the crane to save his life, swearing not to harm him (001-001).
- **Refusing gratitude**: Once saved, the Lion dismisses the crane's request for a reward, arguing that not eating the crane is reward enough (001-007-001-010).
- **Embodying ingratitude**: His famous lines about feeding on blood and hunting for prey become the archetypal expression of the ingrate who feels no obligation to those who help him (001-007-001-010).

## Relationships

- **[[the-crane|The Crane]]** (the-cruel-crane) — [[the-prince-of-five-weapons|The Bodhisatta]] who saves the Lion's life by removing the bone from his throat. Their relationship exemplifies the asymmetry between benefactor and ungrateful recipient (001-001-001-020). Despite the crane's service, the Lion feels no debt.
- **Devadatta the Traitor** — The Lion is Devadatta in a previous birth, the Buddha's cousin who became his mortal enemy (001-021). The Lion's ingratitude prefigures Devadatta's later betrayals.

## Moral and Cultural Significance

The Lion embodies *akataññutā* (ingratitude), one of the gravest moral failings in [[buddhist-framework|Buddhist ethics]]. His declaration that the crane is lucky to be alive — having once been between his teeth — is a classic expression of the predator's logic: might makes right, and mercy is a gift that creates no obligation. The tale belongs to the extensive Jataka cycle and illustrates the recurring conflict between the Bodhisatta and Devadatta across multiple lifetimes.

> "Ungrateful, doing no good, / Not doing as he would be done by, / In him there is no gratitude, / To serve him is useless." (001-012-001-015)

The crane's response — to withdraw quietly "neither envying nor abusing" — models the Buddhist ideal of responding to ingratitude not with anger but with dignified detachment (001-019).
