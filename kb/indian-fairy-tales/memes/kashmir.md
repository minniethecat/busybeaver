# Kashmir

## Location

Kashmir is a northern Himalayan region of the Indian subcontinent that appears in this collection chiefly as a source-region for its tales rather than as a setting within them. In the editorial apparatus it is repeatedly invoked as the home of a distinct folk-tale tradition, cited through the Rev. J. Hinton Knowles' *Folk-Tales of Kashmir* (P01-008, 030-014). The editor groups it among the northern collecting-grounds, noting that after other regions "the Panjab and the Kashmir then had their turn" as fields of folklore gathering (030-013). Kashmir occupies the far north of the region covered by the collection's sources.

## Appearances in Tales

- [[preface]] — Kashmir is named among the Oriental collections drawn upon, citing "Mr. Knowles' 'Folk-tales of Kashmir,' in their Oriental Library" (P01-008).
- [[notes-and-references]] — Kashmir recurs throughout the notes as a documented source. The editor observes that "the Panjab and the Kashmir then had their turn" in the history of Indian folklore collecting (030-013), and cites *Folk-Tales of Kashmir* (Trübner's Oriental Library) as the source for several tales (030-014, 030-058, 030-064, 030-093, 030-102, 030-107). He also notes "the existence of the tale in Kashmir" as evidence of a story's spread (030-109).

## Significance

Kashmir's importance in this collection is bibliographic and comparative. Rather than serving as the stage for any single story, it functions as one of the principal wellsprings from which the tales were drawn: multiple entries in [[notes-and-references]] trace their tales directly to Knowles' *Folk-Tales of Kashmir* (030-058, 030-064, 030-093, 030-102, 030-107). By repeatedly pointing to a tale's parallel "in Kashmir," the editor uses the region as evidence for how stories migrated and recurred across the subcontinent (030-109). Kashmir thus marks one corner of the geographic range — alongside the Panjab, [[bengal|Bengal]], and the Deccan — that the collection's comparative notes survey (030-013).

## Cultural or Historical Notes

Kashmir, in the northwestern Himalayas, has long been a distinct cultural region with its own languages, literature, and rich narrative tradition; the medieval Sanskrit *Kathāsaritsāgara* ("Ocean of the Streams of Story"), one of the great story-compendia of the world, was itself composed in Kashmir. In the nineteenth century the Rev. J. Hinton Knowles gathered and published Kashmiri oral tales as *Folk-Tales of Kashmir* (Trübner's Oriental Library), the very volume repeatedly cited in this collection's notes. Kashmir's prominence as a source therefore reflects both its ancient literary heritage and the active work of Victorian-era folklorists in the region.
