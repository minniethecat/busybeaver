## Overview

The Prince appears as a central or significant figure in at least six tales of the *Indian Fairy Tales* collection, making him one of the most frequently recurring character archetypes in the corpus. He appears in diverse roles: as a determined suitor winning a distant princess (002-002), as the lovelorn [[majnun|Majnun]] destined by [[khuda|Khuda]] (008-001), as a clever youth who outwits a cannibal Fakir (023-019), as an exiled prince who falls in love with a fairy (026-001), and as a husband chosen by the youngest princess (022-043). Across these tales, the Prince embodies the classic hero's journey — departure, trial, and triumphant return — a pattern that runs through the Joseph Jacobs collection.

## Role in the Tales

### The Raja's Son and Princess Labam (Chapter 2)

The young prince listens to his mother's counsel and sets out to seek [[princess-labam|Princess Labam]] (002-002). Despite the parrot's mocking and the difficulties of the journey, he persists: "Perhaps [[khuda|God]] will show me the way" (002-011). With magical aids — a bag, bowl, and bed — he wins the princess through a series of [[marriage-and-courtship|impossible tasks]], including felling a giant tree with a wax hatchet (002-072) and identifying the princess among identical attendants on her wedding night (002-074). He succeeds through a combination of the princess's secret aid and his own persistence.

### Prince Majnun and Laili (Chapter 8)

[[majnun|Prince Majnun]] is introduced as the handsome only son of [[king-dantal|King Dantal]] — "a handsome boy with white teeth, red lips, blue eyes, red cheeks, red hair, and a white skin" (008-001). His love for [[laili|Laili]] is divinely ordained: [[khuda|Khuda]] sends an angel to [[laili|Laili]] declaring she shall marry no one but [[majnun|Majnun]] (008-004). The tale follows their separation across kingdoms, [[majnun|Majnun]]'s prayer to [[khuda|Khuda]] to restore [[laili|Laili]]'s youth (008-016), and their eventual reunion. His story is one of destined love overcoming distance and deception, though it ends tragically when [[laili|Laili]]'s jealous sister beheads him (008-044).

### The Prince and the Fakir (Chapter 23)

Born as the elder son of a childless king who lay at a crossroads seeking forgiveness (023-005), this Prince is claimed by a Fakir in exchange for the prayer that granted his birth (023-008). When the Fakir attempts to throw him into a boiling cauldron, the Prince responds with the words "Master first, and pupil after" (023-020), then hurls the Fakir into the pot instead (023-022). He liberates the Fakir's captives — men, horses, tigers, hounds, and simurgs — and sets out as a traveller. Disguised in the Fakir's clothes, he is recognised by a princess who sees his "royal marks" (023-028) and chooses him as her husband over all the assembled nobles (023-037). He proves his worth by hunting prowess and reveals his true identity, eventually inheriting his father-in-law's kingdom (023-043).

### The Ivory City and Its Fairy Princess (Chapter 26)

A young prince practising archery accidentally wounds a merchant's wife (026-001), leading to his banishment by his own father the king (026-013). Joined by the loyal vizier's son, he journeys into exile and discovers a fairy — [[gulizar|Princess Gulizar]] of the [[ivory-city|Ivory City]] — reflected in a lotus-covered lake (026-019). The prince falls into a swoon at the sight of her beauty (026-019). Through the vizier's son's elaborate scheme involving a fake fakir, a poisoned pilaw, and a stolen necklace, the prince eventually marries [[gulizar|Gulizar]] (026-113). After razing the robbers' tower and returning home with treasure and a beautiful bride, he is reconciled with his father and succeeds to the throne (026-116).

### The Boy with the Moon on His Forehead (Chapter 22)

The youngest princess chooses the moon-forehead boy — a prince in all but title — as her husband over all other suitors, declaring to her father: "My husband is not a handsome man, but he has a moon on his forehead and a star on his chin" (022-071). Through her steadfast loyalty and his magical birthmarks, he is ultimately revealed and accepted.

### The Soothsayer's Son (Chapter 10)

Though not a prince by birth, [[soothsayers-son|Gangazara]] fulfills the archetypal prince role: he rescues a princess from death, marries her, and succeeds to the throne (010-027). His story mirrors the Prince archetype's journey of trial, rescue, and royal ascension.

The Prince's most famous moment of cleverness comes in his confrontation with the cannibal Fakir:

> "Master first, and pupil after." (023-020)

Three times the Fakir commands him to approach the boiling cauldron, and three times the Prince responds with this formula before hurling the Fakir in instead (023-022).

## Relationships

- **[[the-princess|The Princess]]** — his counterpart and bride across multiple tales; in Chapter 2 she secretly aids him through the [[marriage-and-courtship|impossible tasks]] (002-075); in Chapter 23 she recognises his royal nature beneath his [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]] (023-028); in Chapter 26 [[gulizar|Gulizar]] is the fairy princess he pursues across kingdoms (026-019)
- **[[the-fakir|The Fakir]]** — antagonist in Chapter 23, the cannibal who claims him at birth and whom the Prince ultimately destroys (023-022)
- **[[the-viziers-son|The Vizier's Son]]** — his loyal companion in Chapter 26, who follows him into exile, saying "We have lived together, we will be exiled together, and we will die together" (026-014)
- **[[king|The King]]** — his father in multiple tales; sometimes a source of exile and punishment (026-013), sometimes a figure to be reconciled with after triumph (026-116)
- **[[the-teacher|The Teacher]]** — the Fakir's role before his true nature is revealed in Chapter 23; also the prince's tutor in Chapter 8 (008-001)

## Moral and Cultural Significance

The Prince archetype in *Indian Fairy Tales* embodies the tension between fate and initiative. In some tales, his [[fate-and-prophecy|destiny]] is divinely ordained — [[majnun|Majnun]] is chosen by [[khuda|Khuda]] (008-004), the Fakir's prince is born through a holy man's prayer (023-008). In others, he succeeds through his own persistence and cleverness — refusing to enter the boiling pot (023-020), persevering through [[marriage-and-courtship|impossible tasks]] (002-065). This duality reflects a core theme in Indian folklore: the hero is both blessed by [[fate-and-prophecy|destiny]] and tested by it. The recurring exile motif — banishment from the father's kingdom followed by a triumphant return (026-116) — mirrors the archetypal royal succession narrative found across Indo-European folklore, where the prince must leave home to prove his worth before he can inherit the throne.
