# Faithfulness and Loyalty

## Definition

Faithfulness — unwavering devotion to a friend, master, or beloved — is one of the collection's most warmly praised virtues, and it is very often embodied by animals rather than by human beings. Again and again the *Indian Fairy Tales* set the constancy of a loyal beast or a true-hearted companion against the treachery of men, so that the moral rewards those who keep faith and shames those who break it. Fidelity in love, the steadfastness of animal helpers, and the contrast between loyal creatures and faithless people together form a persistent ethical thread.

## Examples in the Tales

- **Faithful [[animal-helpers|animal companions]]** in [[the-soothsayers-son]]: a tiger, a serpent, and a rat keep their word to their rescuer, and the hero is "extremely delighted at the faithfulness with which the brute beasts behaved" (010-008); at his lowest moment he resolves, "let me think of my faithful brute friends" (010-011) — the beasts prove loyal where a human goldsmith proves treacherous.

- **Loyal animals against a faithless man** in [[the-charmed-ring]]: the grateful dog and cat wait for their master at the spring and set off to recover his lost ring (012-012), their devotion contrasted with the human thief who has robbed him.

- **Fidelity in love** in [[loving-laili]]: [[laili|Laili]] is so faithful to her destined husband that she will "marry no one but [[majnun|Majnun]]" (008-005), and when he lies dead she goes "to [[majnun|Majnun]]'s dead body" and cuts her own finger in her grief and devotion (008-051) — a portrait of loyalty unto death.

- **The dog who kept watch** in [[a-lac-of-rupees-for-a-bit-of-advice]]: "the faithful dog, however, knew what was going" on (014-017), the loyal animal alert to danger its master cannot see.

- **A faithful steed** in [[raja-rasalu]]: [[rasalu|Rasalu]]'s devotion to his horse is such that when he loses it at play he "could not" bear to let the faithful steed be led away (019-115).

## Cultural Context

The praise of the faithful animal and the loyal friend is a staple of the Indian didactic tale, inherited from the Jataka and *Panchatantra* traditions in which beasts routinely model the virtues that humans neglect. The recurring lesson — that a dog, a cat, or a serpent may keep faith more truly than a fellow human — carries a pointed moral force, and the notes to Jacobs's collection remark that the loyal animal who recovers a lost token is a widely diffused folk element (030-060). Fidelity in these stories is not merely sentimental; it is a tested virtue that the plot rewards and its absence that the plot punishes.
