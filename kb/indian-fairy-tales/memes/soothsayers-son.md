# Gangazara

## Overview

Gangazara is the hero of the tenth tale, [[the-soothsayers-son]]. He is "the second son" of a soothsayer who, on his deathbed, writes out the boy's horoscope "and bequeathed it to him as his only property, leaving the whole of his estate to his eldest son" (010-001). The horoscope is bleak, [[fate-and-prophecy|foretelling]] "'From my birth poverty!'", "'For ten years, imprisonment'", and "'Death on [[the-sea|the sea-shore]]'", followed by the enigma of "'some happiness afterwards!'" (010-002, 010-003). Gangazara's life becomes the unfolding of these predictions, and his story is one of the collection's fullest treatments of [[fate-and-prophecy]]: every calamity he suffers, and his eventual glory, are already written, and the tale's suspense lies not in whether the horoscope is true but in how its riddles will resolve.

## Role in the Tale

Gangazara is the fated protagonist whose unwavering trust in his father's [[fate-and-prophecy|prophecy]] carries him through disaster. Believing he is destined to "die on a sea coast, and not by a tiger" (010-004), he acts with a fearlessness that is really faith in fate — a faith that shapes his defining act of mercy and the [[animal-helpers]] who repay it. He is also a devout Brahman, "very observant of his religious ceremonies" (010-008), whose ten years in a dungeon give his body "a sort of lustre" until the people take him for a god (010-016, 010-020). His arc dramatizes the tension between [[fate-and-prophecy|destiny]] and free will: he releases the treacherous goldsmith against all advice, and that single free choice delivers exactly the imprisonment the horoscope foretold.

## Key Deeds and Appearances

- **Rescuing the three animal kings (010-004 to 010-006).** Dying of thirst in the desert, Gangazara lowers his water-vessel into a ruined well and successively hauls out the king of tigers, the king of serpents (the Nagaraja, "son of Adisesha"), and the king of rats. Each swears lifelong gratitude — "Whenever you are in any difficulty just think of me" (010-005) — and each warns him, in the tiger's words, "never assist that wretched man... Goldsmiths are never to be trusted" (010-004).

- **Freeing the goldsmith against all warnings (010-007).** Reasoning "What wrong would there be in my assisting him?", Gangazara lifts the goldsmith [[the-goldsmith|Manikkasari]] from the well too, ignoring the animals' repeated counsel — the fateful choice that seals his coming ruin.

- **Betrayal and imprisonment (010-008 to 010-011).** Ten years later the grateful tiger-king brings Gangazara a jewelled crown taken from the slain king of [[ujjain|Ujjaini]] (010-008, 010-009). When Gangazara asks [[the-goldsmith|Manikkasari]] to melt it down, the goldsmith instead denounces him to the prince as the king's murderer; Gangazara, bathing in the river, is seized "without any knowledge of the fate that hung over him" and thrown into an underground dungeon (010-010, 010-011) — the "ten years' imprisonment" of the [[fate-and-prophecy|prophecy]].

- **Sustained and freed by the animals (010-012 to 010-016).** The rat, serpent, and tiger kings tunnel to his cell, feed him with stolen bread and water-soaked cloth, and unleash a plague of tiger- and snake-attacks across the kingdom to force his release (010-013, 010-014). After a serpent finally kills the king's daughter, Gangazara is remembered and brought forth; the king "no sooner saw Gangazara than he fell on the ground," struck by the lustre of his person (010-016).

- **Reviving the dead (010-017, 010-018).** Gangazara demands "all the corpses of men and cattle" and revives them by sprinkling water while thinking of his snake-king and tiger-king; "the princess, too, was restored to life" (010-018).

- **Fulfilling the last [[fate-and-prophecy|prophecy]] (010-024 to 010-027).** Journeying home, Gangazara takes "a wrong road" and passes "near a sea-coast," where he meets his long-lost elder brother; "the pleasure of Gangazara was so great that he died of joy" (010-024) — the foretold "death on [[the-sea|the sea-shore]]." His brother, a worshipper of Ganesa, obtains a living Gangazara from the god (010-025, 010-026), and Gangazara returns to [[ujjain|Ujjaini]], marries the princess, and gains the throne, "and so the horoscope was fully fulfilled" (010-027).

## Relationships

- **The soothsayer (his father)** — the dying seer who leaves Gangazara nothing but the horoscope that governs his entire life (010-001).
- **The elder brother** — heir to the family estate; a devout worshipper of Ganesa whose meeting with Gangazara triggers the "death on [[the-sea|the sea-shore]]," and whose devotion to the god later restores him to life (010-023 to 010-026).
- **The tiger-king, serpent-king, and rat-king** — the three grateful beasts Gangazara saves from the well; loyal [[animal-helpers]] who feed him in prison, engineer his freedom, and empower his miracles (010-004 to 010-006, 010-013, 010-018).
- **[[the-goldsmith|Manikkasari]] the goldsmith** — the treacherous man Gangazara mercifully rescues, who repays him by stealing the crown and denouncing him as a murderer (010-007, 010-010); his ingratitude embodies the theme of [[gratitude-and-ingratitude]].
- **The king and princess of [[ujjain|Ujjaini]]** — the ruler who wrongly imprisons Gangazara and later begs his forgiveness, and the princess whom Gangazara revives and eventually marries (010-011, 010-016, 010-027).

## Moral or Cultural Significance

Gangazara's tale is a sustained argument for the inescapability of [[fate-and-prophecy|destiny]]: "Surely my father's [[fate-and-prophecy|prophecy]] never proved untrue" is his refrain (010-003), and each stage of the horoscope comes literally true, down to the pun-like precision of dying of joy on a sea-coast. Yet the story also weaves in a moral about trust: the beasts, though predators, prove utterly faithful, while the human goldsmith betrays his rescuer — an inversion that dramatizes [[faithfulness-and-loyalty]] against human [[greed-in-folklore]]. The tiger-king's rebuke of the "stupid king" who imprisons an innocent on a goldsmith's word turns the tale toward [[royal-justice]] as well, making Gangazara's suffering an indictment of hasty rulers. As a poor second son who ends on a throne, Gangazara embodies the folk conviction that fate, faithfully borne, delivers its promised "happiness afterwards."
