# Greed in Folklore

## Definition

Greed (*lobha* in Pali/Sanskrit) is one of the three root poisons (*kilesas*) in [[buddhist-framework|Buddhist teaching]], alongside hatred (*dosa*) and delusion (*moha*). In Indian fairy tales, greed functions as the primary moral failing that drives the narrative toward its cautionary conclusion. Unlike European tales where greed is sometimes rewarded (the giant's gold in *Jack and the Beanstalk*), the Indian tradition consistently portrays excessive desire as self-destructive, reflecting the Buddhist emphasis on contentment (*santuṭṭhi*) and non-attachment (*anattā*).

## Examples in the Tales

- **The Broken Pot** (the-broken-pot): A Brahman named [[the-brahman-of-the-broken-pot|Svabhavakripana]] — "a born miser" — fills a pot with rice obtained by begging and, while resting, fantasizes about the wealth he will accumulate by selling it (005-001). He imagines buying goats, then cattle, then a wife, and eventually becoming so rich that he will kick his servants. In his excitement, he actually kicks the pot, shattering it (005-002). This tale illustrates how greed-fueled fantasy destroys what one already possesses — a moral echoed in the European fable of the milkmaid and her pail.

- **[[the-pigeon|The Pigeon]] and the Crow** (the-pigeon-and-the-crow): A greedy crow, envious of the pigeon's comfort in the rich man's kitchen, attempts to take the pigeon's place. The cook catches and kills the crow. The tale contrasts the pigeon's contentment with the crow's destructive envy, teaching that greed leads to ruin while contentment brings security (029-001).

- **[[the-cruel-crane|The Cruel Crane]] Outwitted** (the-cruel-crane-outwitted): A crane deceives fish by offering to transport them to a beautiful lake, but instead eats them one by one. When the crane's greed leads it to try the same trick on a crab, the crab outwits and kills the crane. The tale illustrates how greed ultimately destroys the greedy — a karmic justice embedded in the narrative structure.

- **[[the-golden-serpent|The Gold-giving Serpent]]** (the-gold-giving-serpent): A serpent dwelling in an ant-hill rewards a respectful Brahman who offers milk with a gold coin (015-002). When the Brahman's son, driven by greed upon seeing the coin, decides to kill the serpent for more gold, he strikes it with a stick and is fatally bitten (015-003). The tale explicitly contrasts respectful devotion with greedy aggression — the outcome is fatal for the greedy, and the father returns to find his son dead (015-004).

- **A Lac of Rupees for a Bit of Advice** (a-lac-of-rupees-for-a-bit-of-advice): The tension between wealth and wisdom drives this tale, where a king learns that a piece of good advice is worth more than material riches. The tale questions whether the pursuit of rupees can coexist with the pursuit of virtue.

## Cultural Context

The condemnation of greed in Indian folklore is rooted in both Buddhist and Hindu ethical traditions. Buddhism identifies *lobha* as a root cause of suffering (*dukkha*), while Hindu *dharma* teaching emphasizes *aparigraha* (non-possessiveness). The tales collected by Joseph Jacobs reflect this deep cultural consensus: greed is not merely a character flaw but a spiritual poison that leads inevitably to destruction.

The "Broken Pot" tale in particular has a long literary history in India, appearing in both the *Panchatantra* and the *Hitopadesha*. Jacobs notes in his notes and references that such tales "bear a remarkable resemblance to some of the most famous Fables of Aesop" (030-005), confirming the shared Indo-European moral vocabulary around greed and its consequences.
