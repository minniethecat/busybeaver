## Overview

The Nurse appears as a supporting figure in two tales from the *Indian Fairy Tales* collection. In "[[rasalu|Raja Rasalu]]" (Chapter 19), nurses collectively raise the young prince in an underground palace, teaching him "all things needful" (019-004). In "The Boy who had a [[moon|Moon]] on his Forehead and a Star on his Chin" (Chapter 22), a specific nurse is ordered by the jealous queens to kill the newborn prince, but instead she places him in a box and sets him adrift on a river — a pivotal act of mercy that allows the tale's hero to survive (022-015, 022-018).

## Role in the Tales

### In "Raja Rasalu"

The nurses appear in the background of [[rasalu|Raja Rasalu]]'s childhood. The young prince is hidden away in an underground palace, attended by nurses and servants who provide for his every need (019-003). As he grows, the nurses teach him all things needful and warn him about the dangers of the upper world (019-004, 019-006). When he eventually desires to leave, the nurses remind him of their warnings:

> Mindful of what his nurses had oft told him, when he came to the top of the palace stairs, he stopped, for he remembered that he had been told never to go beyond the top step. (019-007)

### In "The Boy who had a Moon on his Forehead and a Star on his Chin"

The nurse in this tale plays a far more consequential role. The six jealous queens, envious of the seventh queen who bears the king a son marked with a moon on his forehead and a star on his chin, hatch a plot (022-014). They take the boy to the nurse with a deadly command:

> "Now you must kill the boy, and bring us his eyes and his heart as proof that you have done so." (022-015)

The nurse, however, cannot bring herself to commit the murder. Instead, she places the infant in a box and sets him adrift:

> In the middle of the night the nurse took the box in which was the boy, and carried it to the river, and put it in the water. (022-018)

She then kills a young dog and presents its eyes and heart to the queens as false proof, deceiving them into believing the prince is dead (022-018).

Much later in the tale, after the boy has grown and been restored to the palace, the nurse's box becomes evidence of his identity. The possessions he had left in it — discovered by Shankar and [[suri-the-cow|Suri]] — confirm his origins (022-076).

## Key Deeds

- **Raising [[rasalu|Raja Rasalu]]**: Along with other nurses, she teaches the young prince the knowledge he needs to survive (019-004).
- **Warning the Prince**: The nurses caution [[rasalu|Raja Rasalu]] about going beyond the underground palace, advice he remembers at a critical moment (019-006–019-007).
- **Defying the Queens**: In a pivotal act of mercy, the nurse spares the moon-forehead boy's life despite the queens' orders (022-015, 022-018).
- **The Deception**: By substituting a dog's eyes and heart, the nurse protects the child while satisfying the queens' demand for proof of death (022-018).

## Relationships

- **[[rasalu|Raja Rasalu]]** — The prince whom the nurses collectively raise and prepare for his [[fate-and-prophecy|destiny]] (019-003–019-007).
- **The Seven Queens** — The nurse's employers in Chapter 22; the six jealous queens order the murder, while the seventh queen is the boy's true mother (022-014–022-015).
- **The [[moon|Moon]]-Forehead Boy** — The infant prince whose life the nurse spares, setting in motion the entire second half of the tale (022-015, 022-018).
- **Shankar and [[suri-the-cow|Suri]]** — The cobblers who later find the boy's possessions in the nurse's box, providing the evidence that reunites him with his family (022-076).

## Character Analysis

The nurse in Chapter 22 embodies a common folk-tale archetype: the merciful servant who disobeys a cruel command. Her refusal to kill the child — and her clever substitution of a dog's heart and eyes — echoes similar figures across world folklore (cf. Snow White's huntsman). Unlike the collective, nameless nurses of "[[rasalu|Raja Rasalu]]," the Chapter 22 nurse makes an individual moral choice with far-reaching consequences, transforming from a passive instrument of the queens' jealousy into an active agent of the boy's survival.
