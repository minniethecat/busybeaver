## Overview

The Fish appears in two significant tales in the *Indian Fairy Tales* collection, each illustrating a different aspect of the character type. In "[[the-cruel-crane|The Cruel Crane]] Outwitted" (Chapter 7), the fish are gullible victims of a predatory crane's deception. In "Why the Fish Laughed" (Chapter 24), a single fish becomes the catalyst for a kingdom-spanning mystery when it laughs at a remark made in the palace. Together, these tales show fish as creatures whose innocence and unexpected wisdom drive narrative turns.

## Role in the Tales

### The Cruel Crane Outwitted

In this tale, the fish inhabit a pool that is drying up. A crane, seeing an easy meal, pretends to be their savior:

> "I must outwit these fish somehow or other and make a prey of them" (007-003)

The crane tells the fish of a beautiful large lake and offers to carry them there one by one. The fish, trusting the crane's false benevolence, ask him what he is thinking:

> "I am sitting thinking about you," said he. (007-006)

The crane carries each fish away — but instead of the promised lake, he takes them to a varana-tree where he eats them, leaving a pile of fish bones beneath (007-018). The fish's collective gullibility leads to their destruction, until a crab finally sees through the crane's trick and kills him.

### Why the Fish Laughed

In this tale, a fisherwoman tries to sell a fish at the palace. When the queen asks to buy "a she fish," the fish laughs aloud — because it is male (024-003). This seemingly trivial moment sets off a chain of inquiry: the king demands to know the reason for the laughter, and a young man's quest to discover the answer drives the entire plot.

> "It's a he," replied the fisherwoman (024-004)

- **[[the-crane|The Crane]]** — predator and deceiver of the fish (007-003); the relationship illustrates the predator-prey dynamic common in fable literature
- **[[the-crab|The Crab]]** — the one creature who avenges the fish by killing the crane (007-025)

## Moral and Cultural Significance

The fish in "[[the-cruel-crane|The Cruel Crane]] Outwitted" represents the theme of blind trust and its consequences. The fish accept the crane's story without scrutiny — a cautionary tale about credulity. Their fate is sealed by the crane's reflection:

> "those fishes got eaten through their own stupidity" (007-031)

In "Why the Fish Laughed," the fish serves a different function: it is a truth-teller whose spontaneous reaction exposes the gap between surface appearances and reality. The laugh is a moment of cosmic irony — a creature with no power in the human world momentarily reveals a truth that the powerful cannot see. This aligns with the broader folk motif of the wise fool or the lowly creature who possesses knowledge denied to kings.
