## Overview

The Maid appears across the *Indian Fairy Tales* collection in multiple forms: as a named palace attendant with her own agency (Jihva in "[[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]]"), as a handmaiden who carries crucial messages ("[[the-prince|The Prince]] and the Fakir"), and as the collective seventy maidens of King Sarkap's court who test — and are defeated by — [[rasalu|Raja Rasalu]]'s wit and charm ("[[rasalu|Raja Rasalu]]"). While often appearing in subordinate roles, maids in these tales are frequently the characters who recognize hidden truths and drive the plot through their observations and actions.

## Role in the Tales

### Harisarman (Chapter 11) — Jihva the Named Maid

The most fully realized maid character in the collection is Jihva (whose name means "Tongue") in the palace of the Brahman [[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]] (011-003). She is the first person [[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]] confides in about his supposed prophetic powers, telling her he has "a secret" to share. Jihva consents to hear him (011-004) and becomes the conduit through which he learns of thefts in the palace — enabling his false reputation as a seer. Jihva's role as the palace's information hub makes her, rather than [[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]] himself, the true source of the "prophecies."

### The Prince and the Fakir (Chapter 23) — The Handmaiden

[[the-princess|The Princess]] commands her handmaiden to "Take this dish of henna, go to that traveller dressed like a Fakir, and sprinkle it on him" (023-033). The handmaiden obeys "the Princess's order" (023-034) without question, and when the people accuse her of making a mistake, she replies with quiet confidence: "The slave-girl has made no mistake, it is the Princess who has ordered this" (023-035). This handmaiden's loyalty and composure under public scrutiny [[supernatural-beings|demons]]trate the quiet strength of the maid figure.

### Raja Rasalu (Chapter 19) — The Seventy Maidens

In one of the collection's most vivid episodes, seventy maidens — daughters of King Sarkap — test [[rasalu|Raja Rasalu]] (019-059). One maiden looks into his face and sees his true worth (019-073), but the other sixty-nine "being jealous, laughed scornfully" (019-074). The seventy maidens eventually climb into a single swing which [[rasalu|Rasalu]] breaks, sending them all "headlong" to the ground (019-084). This episode blends comedy with the theme of discernment: the maid who sees truly is rewarded, while the scornful suffer.

In "The Magic Fiddle" (Chapter 6), a maiden emerges from a magical pitcher and is given to the hero: "you may keep the maiden as your own" (006-001). This maiden's appearance from an enchanted object connects the maid archetype to the broader folklore motif of supernatural brides.

## Relationships

- **[[king|The King]] / Master** — maids serve royal households and are bound by palace hierarchy
- **[[the-princess|The Princess]]** — the maid's closest relationship is often with the princess she serves, acting as confidante and messenger
- **The Hero / Traveller** — maids are often the first to recognize a [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]]d hero's true identity, serving as narrative revealers

The maid's position within the household provides her with **privileged access to information** — she hears what others do not, sees what is meant to be hidden, and can act on that knowledge in ways that higher-ranking characters cannot.

## Moral and Cultural Significance

The maid figure in Indian folklore embodies a tension between powerlessness and insight. Though at the bottom of the palace hierarchy, maids frequently possess the knowledge that drives the plot — Jihva's information about thefts, the handmaiden's accurate delivery of the henna, the discerning maiden who sees [[rasalu|Rasalu]]'s true worth. This reflects a broader folkloric truth: wisdom and perception are not correlated with social rank. The maid who is overlooked by the powerful may see more clearly than they do.
