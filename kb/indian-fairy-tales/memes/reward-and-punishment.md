Indian fairy tales consistently affirm a moral universe in which actions bring consequences. The generous are rewarded with wealth, marriage, or supernatural aid; the cruel and ungrateful face punishment — sometimes immediate, sometimes deferred but always certain. This pattern, rooted in the doctrine of karma (action and its fruit) and the Jataka teaching tradition, pervades tales from *The Lion and the Crane* (001-014) to *The Ivory City and Its Fairy Princess* (026-109).

## Definition

Reward and punishment in Indian fairy tales operates as a narrative structure and a moral principle. Unlike the capricious fortunes of some European tales, where luck or magic can save the undeserving, Indian tales tend to follow a logic of desert: what a character receives is tied to what they have done. This reflects the Buddhist and Hindu concept of karma — the law of moral cause and effect that governs all beings across lifetimes. In the tales, however, the karmic frame is simplified: good deeds receive immediate or episode-level rewards, while evil deeds bring swift punishment.

## Examples in the Tales

The motif manifests across many stories:

- **The ungrateful lion's punishment** (001-014): In *The Lion and the Crane*, the crane helps a lion by removing a bone from its throat, but the lion refuses to show gratitude. The tale explicitly states: "In him there is no gratitude" (001-014) — and the lion's treachery is condemned as the moral of the story

- **The grateful serpent's reward** (012-007, 012-012): In *The Charmed Ring*, a character who helps another is told: "He will wish to reward you, too" (012-007), and indeed the recipient of aid shows "gratitude to him for having delivered him" (012-012), demonstrating the positive side of the reward pattern

- **The pigeon's reward for service** (016-029): In *The Son of Seven Queens*, a pigeon promises that if helped, "she would reward him with the Jogi's wonderful cow" — creatures repay kindness with magical gifts (016-029)

- **The wicked witch punished** (016-045): In the same tale, after the hero's trials are complete, the narrative closes with "punished the wicked white witch" (016-045), pairing reward for the virtuous with punishment for the malevolent

- **The princess's ingratitude and repentance** (026-109): In *The Ivory City and Its Fairy Princess*, the princess must acknowledge and repent of her ingratitude before harmony is restored. "The princess wept, and acknowledged that he was her greatest helper and friend" (026-109) — punishment is not mere vengeance but a path to correction

- **Rewarding the wise man** (014-021): In *A Lac of Rupees for a Bit of Advice*, the conclusion is both moral and practical: "he went away, after rewarding the man" (014-021) — good advice is repaid with material reward

## Cultural Context

The reward-and-punishment pattern in Indian folklore draws from two related sources: the Jataka tradition of moral exempla and the broader Indian understanding of karma. In the Jatakas, each tale illustrates a specific virtue or vice, with the Buddha (in a past life) modeling right action and receiving its reward, while antagonists model wrong action and receive its consequences.

The scholarly notes observe that "the story of the ingratitude of man compared with the gratitude of beasts" (030-054) is a cross-cultural motif found from India to Europe, but the Indian version ties it explicitly to karmic consequence rather than mere narrative symmetry. The pattern serves a dual purpose: entertainment (the satisfaction of seeing villains punished) and instruction (the teaching that actions have moral weight).
