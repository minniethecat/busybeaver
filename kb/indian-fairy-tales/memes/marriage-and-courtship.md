# Marriage and Courtship

## Definition

Marriage and the courtship that precedes it are among the strongest structural engines of the *Indian Fairy Tales*. A great many of the romances turn on a suitor who must prove himself — most often a prince who is set a series of impossible tasks before a king will grant his daughter's hand — or, conversely, on a princess who insists on choosing her own husband. The quest to win a bride carries the hero across kingdoms, into contests with [[supernatural-beings|demons]], and through trials that test courage, cunning, and endurance; the wedding itself, celebrated with lavish pomp, marks the resolution of the tale.

## Examples in the Tales

- **Impossible tasks for the bride** in [[how-the-rajas-son-won-the-princess-labam]]: the princess's father decrees that any suitor "must first do whatever I bid him. If he fails" (002-061), and that the penalty for failure is death — "if he cannot, the king will have him killed" (002-062). Only after the prince accomplishes his tasks does "the wedding" take place before all the [[king|Raja]]s (002-078).

- **Fated marriage** in [[loving-laili]]: [[laili|Laili]] declares that "[[khuda|Khuda]] has ordered me to marry no one but [[majnun|Majnun]]" (008-005), so that her union is decreed by [[fate-and-prophecy|destiny]] rather than won by contest — courtship here is the fulfilment of fate.

- **A princess who chooses** in [[the-boy-who-had-a-moon-on-his-forehead-and-a-star-on-his-chin]]: the youngest princess announces, "I wish to be married; but I must choose my husband myself" (022-047), and her father consents; she selects the [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]]d hero, and "the princess and the boy were married with great pomp and splendour" (022-053).

- **A bride under guard** in [[punchkin]]: the captured princess is confined in a tower "because she refused to marry" the magician who holds her (004-054), the courtship inverted into imprisonment until a rescuer arrives.

- **Marriage as the hero's reward** in [[raja-rasalu]]: [[rasalu|Rasalu]] at last "married the [[kokilan|Princess Kokilan]]" (019-143), the union crowning his long cycle of adventures.

## Cultural Context

The bride-quest is a near-universal folk-tale pattern, and Jacobs's collection presents its distinctively Indian inflections: the swayamvara-like scene in which a princess openly selects her own husband, the royal father who imposes deadly ordeals on suitors, and the framing of some matches as divinely ordained. Weddings in the tales are occasions of great public splendour, attended by assembled rajas and marked by [[food-and-feasting|feasting]] and gift-giving. The notes to the collection observe that certain task-and-taboo forms found in "[[princess-labam|Princess Labam]]" are "not known in Europe" (030-028), underscoring the local colour of these courtship plots even as the broader bride-winning structure remains cross-cultural.
