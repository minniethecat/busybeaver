## Overview

King Brahmadatta is a framing figure in two Jataka tales within the *Indian Fairy Tales* collection: "[[the-lion|The Lion]] and the Crane" (Chapter 1) and "[[the-matted-hair-demon|The Demon with the Matted Hair]]" (Chapter 25). He functions not as an active participant in the narratives but as the historical anchor — the reigning monarch of [[benares|Benares]] (Varanasi) during the time when the Bodhisatta's past-life stories are set. His name establishes the temporal-spiritual framework of the Jataka tradition, in which the Buddha's previous births occurred during specific reigns in India's mythic past.

## Role in the Tales

### The Lion and the Crane (Chapter 1)

Brahmadatta is mentioned in the opening sentence of the very first tale in the collection:

> In a previous birth, the Bodhisatta was born in the region of Himavanta as a white crane; now Brahmadatta was at that time reigning in Benares. (001-001)

This frame — "now Brahmadatta was at that time reigning in [[benares|Benares]]" — is the classic opening formula of Pali Jataka stories, situating the *atita-vatthu* (story of the past) in a specific historical-mythic context. Brahmadatta himself does not appear in the crane-and-lion drama that follows, but his reign provides the temporal container for the Bodhisatta's moral action.

The tale closes by returning to this frame, identifying the characters' present-life equivalents:

> The lion was Devadatta the Traitor, but the white crane was I myself. (001-021)

The Buddha — "I myself" — was the crane in a past life during Brahmadatta's reign, making the king an unwitting witness to the karmic prehistory of the Buddha's eventual triumph over his traitorous cousin Devadatta.

### The Demon with the Matted Hair (Chapter 25)

Brahmadatta appears again in the opening of Chapter 25:

> Once upon a time, when Brahmadatta was King of Benares, the Bodhisatta was born as a Brahman's son. (025-004)

This time, the Bodhisatta's birth is human — a young Brahman who travels to Takkasila (Taxila) to study under a world-renowned teacher. The tale that follows concerns the Bodhisatta's encounter with a demon in the forest, whom he defeats not through force but through the power of truth and the recitation of a protective charm.

As in Chapter 1, Brahmadatta's role is entirely framing. He is the named king whose reign marks the time, but he does not interact with the Bodhisatta's adventure. The pattern is consistent across hundreds of Jataka tales: King Brahmadatta of [[benares|Benares]] serves as the standard temporal marker, much as "once upon a time" functions in European fairy tales — but with the specificity of a named historical figure that roots the story in the Buddhist cosmos.

## Moral and Cultural Significance

Brahmadatta's presence in the collection serves a specific literary function inherited from the Pali Jataka tradition. In the canonical Jataka tales (part of the Khuddaka Nikaya in the Pali Canon), nearly every story is introduced with the formula: *"Atīte Bārāṇasiyaṃ Brahmadatte rajjaṃ kārente..."* — "In the past, when Brahmadatta was reigning in [[benares|Benares]]..." The name "Brahmadatta" literally means "given by Brahma," suggesting divine sanction for his rule.

By including this framing figure, the tales anchor themselves in Buddhist sacred history. For the Buddhist audience, the mention of Brahmadatta signals that what follows is not mere entertainment but a *jataka* — a story of the Buddha's past life that carries moral and karmic significance. The king himself is a neutral figure: neither virtuous nor villainous, simply the ruler in whose time the Bodhisatta's moral dramas unfold.

The recurrence of Brahmadatta across the collection also highlights the Jataka roots of Indian fairy tales. Joseph Jacobs selected several Jataka-derived stories for this collection, and the Brahmadatta frame — present in Chapters 1 and 25 — marks these tales as belonging to the Buddhist narrative tradition that predates and informs the broader Indian folk corpus.
