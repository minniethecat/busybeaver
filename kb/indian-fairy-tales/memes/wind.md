## Overview

Wind is one of the three celestial siblings in the etiological myth "How [[sun|Sun]], [[moon|Moon]], and Wind went out to Dinner" (Chapter 27). Together with his elder brother [[sun|Sun]], Wind feasts greedily at a banquet given by Thunder and Lightning, giving no thought to bringing food home to their mother Star (027-002). When Star asks what they have brought, Wind answers with callous indifference and is cursed to "parch and shrivel all living things" in hot weather (027-006–027-007). The story is an explanatory myth accounting for why hot winds are so unpleasant — a question of daily experience in the Indian subcontinent.

## Role in the Tale

### The Feast

Wind accompanies his brother [[sun|Sun]] and sister [[moon|Moon]] to dine with their uncle and aunts Thunder and Lightning. While [[moon|Moon]] secretly saves portions of every dish under her fingernails for their mother, Wind — like [[sun|Sun]] — "enjoyed the great feast that had been prepared for them, without a thought of saving any of it to take home to their mother" (027-002).

### The Confession

Upon returning home, when Star asks what they have brought, Wind answers with brusque selfishness:

> "Neither have I brought anything home for you, mother. You could hardly expect me to bring a collection of good things for you, when I merely went out for my own pleasure." (027-003)

### The Curse

Star pronounces Wind's doom in direct response to his selfishness:

> "You also who forgot your mother in the midst of your selfish pleasures — hear your doom. You shall always blow in the hot dry weather, and shall parch and shrivel all living things. And men shall detest and avoid you from this very time." (027-006)

The narrator adds: "And that is why the Wind in the hot weather is still so disagreeable" (027-007). This directly connects the mythological narrative to a lived environmental reality in India, where the hot *loo* winds of summer are famously harsh.

## Key Deeds

- **Selfishness**: Attends a feast and enjoys it fully, but refuses to save any food for his waiting mother (027-002).
- **Callousness**: When questioned, Wind does not express regret or apology; instead he justifies his neglect by asserting that he went out "for my own pleasure" — as if filial duty were incompatible with personal enjoyment (027-003).
- **Punishment**: Unlike [[moon|Moon]], who is blessed, Wind is cursed for all time, his punishment directly mirroring his crime: just as he brought no comfort to his mother, his winds shall bring no comfort to the world (027-006–027-007).

## Relationships

- **Star** — Wind's mother, "one of the most distant Stars you see far up in the sky." She pronounces the curse that defines Wind's nature (027-001, 027-006–027-007).
- **[[sun|Sun]]** ([[sun]]) — Wind's elder brother, similarly selfish and similarly cursed. [[sun|Sun]] receives a parallel punishment: his rays shall "burn all that they touch" and "men shall hate you, and cover their heads when you appear" (027-004–027-005).
- **[[moon|Moon]]** ([[moon]]) — Wind's sister, whose gentle virtue serves as his foil. Where Wind is cursed for his selfishness, [[moon|Moon]] is blessed for her filial devotion (027-002, 027-008–027-009).
- **Thunder and Lightning** — Wind's uncle and aunts, hosts of the fateful feast (027-001).

## Moral and Cultural Significance

Wind's story serves a dual function in the collection. As an **etiological myth**, it explains a natural phenomenon — the harshness of hot winds — through a moral narrative, a pattern common across world folklore traditions. As a **moral exemplum**, Wind represents the consequences of selfishness elevated to cosmic scale: his failure of filial piety is punished not with a temporary setback but with a permanent alteration of his essential nature.

The parallel between Wind and [[sun|Sun]]'s punishments is carefully structured: both are cursed to be harmful rather than beneficial, and both are cursed to be hated rather than loved. This symmetry reinforces the tale's central moral: that **filial remembrance** is the quality that separates the blessed from the cursed, the gentle from the harsh, the loved from the detested.

The tale's resonance with the Indian climate — where the hot *loo* wind is a genuine threat to crops and comfort — gives this mythological explanation a particular cultural immediacy that purely abstract moral fables lack.
