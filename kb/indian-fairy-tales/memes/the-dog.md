## Overview

The Dog is one of the three [[animal-helpers|animal companions]] purchased by the merchant's son in "The Charmed Ring" (Chapter 12). Saved from being killed by herdsmen for one hundred rupees, the Dog becomes a faithful companion — sleeping by the young man's head at night and following him throughout the day (012-001, 012-004). When the charmed ring is stolen and the master's wife and house vanish, the Dog joins the Cat on a quest to recover them. However, the Dog's jealousy and carelessness repeatedly jeopardize the mission: twice the Dog demands to carry the ring and twice drops it, forcing the Cat to retrieve it from a fish and then a kite (012-027–012-031).

## Role in the Tale

### Rescue and Early Companionship

The Dog is the first animal rescued by the merchant's son, found among herdsmen quarrelling over whether to kill it. The young man pays one hundred rupees to spare its life (012-001). Along with the Cat and the Snake, the Dog accompanies the hero to the snake-king's underground realm and waits faithfully at the spring for his return (012-008, 012-012).

### The Quest for the Ring

When the [[supernatural-beings|ogres]]s steals the charmed ring and everything vanishes, the Dog and Cat together volunteer for the month-long recovery mission (012-016). During the journey, the Cat takes the lead — climbing into the palace, interrogating the princess, and devising the plan with the rats — while the Dog waits outside (012-018).

### The Jealousy and the Stream

After the Cat successfully recovers the ring from the [[supernatural-beings|ogres]]s, the companions must cross a stream. The Dog, carrying the Cat on its back, reveals its fatal flaw:

> Now the dog was jealous of the cat, so he asked for the ring, and threatened to throw the cat into the water if it did not give it up; whereupon the cat gave up the ring. Sorry moment, for the dog at once dropped it, and a fish swallowed it. (012-027)

[[the-cat|The Cat]] devises an elaborate plan — hiding inside a dead lamb to catch a nadhar bird, which then commands the king of fishes to find the ring — and recovers the prize (012-028–012-031).

### The Second Failure

Immediately after the ring is recovered, the Dog's jealousy flares again:

> "No, I will not," said the dog, "unless you let me have the ring. I can carry it as well as you. Let me have it or I will kill you." So the cat was obliged to give up the ring. The careless dog very soon dropped it again. This time it was picked up and carried off by a kite. (012-031)

[[the-cat|The Cat]] must then climb a tree, kill the kite, and recover the ring yet again. After this third recovery, the Dog is finally ashamed and begs the Cat's pardon, allowing the ring to be delivered safely to their master (012-031).

## Key Deeds

- **Loyalty**: Faithfully serves the merchant's son, waiting for him at the spring and volunteering for the dangerous quest (012-008, 012-016).
- **Physical Strength**: Carries the Cat across [[the-river|the stream]], providing the transport necessary for their mission (012-027).
- **Obedience in Tasks**: Follows the Cat's instructions — killing a lamb and fetching it, staying quiet when told — showing that despite its flaws, the Dog contributes to the quest's success (012-030).
- **Final Repentance**: After the second failure, the Dog is "thoroughly ashamed of itself" and begs the Cat's pardon, enabling them to complete their mission together (012-031).

## Relationships

- **[[the-cat|The Cat]]** (the-cat) — Fellow companion and the superior strategist of the pair. The Dog's jealousy of the Cat's cleverness causes the quest's major setbacks, but the Cat forgives the Dog and they succeed together (012-027–012-031).
- **[[the-snake|The Snake]]** (the-snake) — The third animal companion, whose father [[king|Raja]] Indrasha provides the charmed ring that the Dog and Cat must later recover (012-005–012-012).
- **[[the-merchant|The Merchant]]'s Son** — The Dog's master, who saved its life from the herdsmen. The Dog repays this with loyal service, though its carelessness nearly costs the master everything (012-001, 012-016).

## Character Analysis

The Dog's role in the tale serves a dual narrative purpose. As a helper, it provides physical transport and support for the more intelligent Cat. As a flawed character, the Dog embodies the dangers of jealousy and pride — each time it insists on carrying the ring out of envy, disaster follows. The Dog's arc from jealousy to shame mirrors the tale's broader moral framework, in which loyalty and humility are ultimately rewarded and stubborn pride must be overcome before the happy ending can be achieved.
