# The Farmer's Daughter

## Overview

The Farmer's Daughter is the unnamed but pivotal figure in [[why-the-fish-laughed]] (Chapter 24), described as "a very sharp and wise girl" (024-031). When her father brings home a traveling stranger who speaks entirely in riddles, she alone deciphers every coded utterance — proving that the stranger is no fool but a man of hidden wisdom. Her insight then solves the central mystery of the tale: [[the-fish|the laughing fish]], she declares, indicates "that there is a man in the palace who is plotting against the king's life" (024-048).

Her role in the story is brief but structurally decisive. Without her riddle-solving, the vizier's son would remain a baffling wanderer, his father would be executed, and the king would remain ignorant of the conspiracy in his own palace. She is the story's interpreter — the one who translates cryptic speech into actionable knowledge — and her marriage to the vizier's son at the tale's close (024-055) rewards her intelligence with a rise in station that the story presents as entirely earned.

## Role in the Tale

The Farmer's Daughter enters the story after her father has spent a long journey in the company of the vizier's son, a young man who speaks entirely in proverbial riddles. The old farmer, baffled and amused, brings his guest home with the complaint that "the fellow is such a fool that I cannot make anything out of him" (024-030). The daughter immediately corrects her father: "this man, whosoever he is, is no fool, as you deem him. He only wishes to know if you can afford to entertain him" (024-031).

What follows is a systematic decryption. The farmer recounts each of the stranger's strange utterances, and the daughter interprets each in turn: the request to "carry one another" means telling stories to pass the time (024-033); the question "is this eaten or not" asks whether the corn-field's owner is in debt (024-035); the clasp-knife meant for "two horses" is a request to cut two walking sticks (024-037); calling the city a "cemetery" and the cemetery a "city" comments on the inhospitality of the living versus the charity of the dead (024-039); and wading through [[the-river|the stream]] with shoes on shows wisdom, not folly, since the stones are sharp and the current swift (024-041).

Her most critical contribution comes after the vizier's son reveals his true identity and the predicament that sent him traveling — his father the vizier faces execution unless someone can explain why a fish laughed at the queen (024-047). The Farmer's Daughter answers without hesitation: [[the-fish|the laughing fish]] "indicates that there is a man in the palace who is plotting against the king's life" (024-048). Her insight proves correct: a pit is dug, the palace maids are made to jump across it, and one is discovered to be a man in [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]] — the queen's secret lover and would-be assassin (024-053). At the story's end she marries the vizier's son in "a most happy marriage" (024-055).

## Key Deeds and Appearances

- **Correcting her father** — immediately recognizes the vizier's son as wise rather than foolish, interpreting his first riddle about the beam of the house (024-031).
- **Decoding the riddles** — systematically interprets all five of the stranger's cryptic utterances, each time [[supernatural-beings|demons]]trating a mind more subtle than her father's (024-033, 024-035, 024-037, 024-039, 024-041).
- **The coded message** — sends the vizier's son a present of ghee, chapatis, and milk with the message "the moon is full; twelve months make a year, and the sea is overflowing with water" — a test of his own wit, which he passes by replying that "the moon is new, and that I can only find eleven months in the year" (024-044, 024-046).
- **Solving [[the-fish|the laughing fish]] mystery** — declares that [[the-fish|the laughing fish]] signals a man [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]]d in the palace, a deduction that saves the vizier's life and the king's throne (024-048).
- **Marriage to the vizier's son** — her wisdom is rewarded with a rise from farmer's daughter to the wife of the vizier's son, in a marriage described as "most happy" (024-055).

## Relationships

- **[[the-old-farmer|The Old Farmer]] (her father)** — a good-hearted but literal-minded man who cannot see past the surface of words; he brings the stranger home thinking him a fool, only to be shown by his daughter that the fool is himself (024-029, 024-030, 024-031).
- **[[the-viziers-son]]** — the riddling traveler whose coded speech she alone can decipher; their mutual understanding — she decodes his words, he decodes her coded gift — makes them intellectual equals, and they marry at the story's close (024-046, 024-055).

## Moral or Cultural Significance

The Farmer's Daughter belongs to a recognizable folktale type: the clever peasant girl who outthinks her social superiors and earns a rise in station through intelligence alone. Her wisdom is not book-learned but innate — she has never met the vizier's son before, yet she sees through his riddles at once because she shares his cast of mind. The story makes this mutuality explicit through the exchange of coded gifts: she tests him with a riddle of her own ("the moon is full; twelve months make a year"), and he answers correctly, proving that they speak the same language of layered meaning (024-044, 024-046). Her solution to the central mystery — that a laughing fish means a man in the palace — requires an intuitive leap that no learned vizier or magician in five months of searching could make (024-011), and positions native wit above institutional wisdom as the true source of insight.
