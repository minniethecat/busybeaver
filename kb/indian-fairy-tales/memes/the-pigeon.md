## Overview

The Pigeon is the central character of the tale "The Pigeon and the Crow" and is an incarnation of the Bodhisatta (the Buddha in a previous life) (029-001). He lives in a nest-basket hung by a rich man's cook in the kitchen, where he is cared for as an act of merit-making (029-001). Unlike the [[greed-in-folklore|greedy]] Crow, the Pigeon embodies patience, wisdom, and moderation, following the natural order of his kind rather than grasping for what does not belong to him (029-003).

## Role in the Tale

The Pigeon lives peacefully in the rich man's kitchen when a [[greed-in-folklore|greedy]] Crow, seeing the abundant food, schemes to gain access (029-001). [[the-crow|The Crow]] follows the Pigeon and asks to become his companion, despite their different feeding habits (029-003). The Pigeon generously agrees, and the cook accommodates the new guest by hanging a second basket (029-005, 029-007).

[[the-crow|The Crow]] secretly gorges on worms from cow-dung while pretending to feed alongside the Pigeon (029-005). When a large purchase of fish arrives, the Crow feigns indigestion to avoid foraging, hoping to steal the fish instead (029-008, 029-010). The Pigeon sees through the ruse, knowing that crows digest everything quickly except lamp-wicks (029-011). Despite the Pigeon's warning, the Crow steals from a dish, is caught by the cook, and is severely punished — his feathers plucked out and his body rubbed with spiced buttermilk (029-014).

When the Pigeon returns and finds the Crow in agony, he recites verses mocking the Crow's pretensions and delivers the moral lesson: "If people make a dish of meat, 'tis not for little birds to eat" (029-016, 029-027-029-030). The Pigeon then flies away, declaring he can no longer live with such a creature, leaving the Crow to groan until his death (029-031).

## Key Deeds

- **Offering companionship**: The Pigeon agrees to share his home and feeding grounds with the Crow despite their differences (029-005).
- **Warning against greed**: He warns the Crow that "Crows never have indigestion" and not to misbehave just for the sight of fish (029-011).
- **Delivering the moral**: After the Crow's punishment, the Pigeon recites three verses of poetry that encapsulate the fable's lesson about knowing one's place and the consequences of greed (029-016-029-030).
- **Choosing to depart**: Recognizing the Crow's incorrigible nature, the Pigeon wisely severs the relationship and leaves (029-031).

## Relationships

- **[[the-crow|The Crow]]** (the-crow) — A [[greed-in-folklore|greedy]] and deceitful companion whom the Pigeon initially befriends but ultimately abandons after the Crow's theft and punishment (029-002-029-031). The contrast between the two birds drives the entire fable.
- **The Cook** — The rich man's cook who hung the Pigeon's nest-basket to earn merit, and later punishes the Crow for ruining the master's meal (029-001, 029-014-029-015).
- **[[the-prince-of-five-weapons|The Bodhisatta]]** — The Pigeon is an incarnation of the Bodhisatta, linking this character to the broader Jataka tradition of the Buddha's previous lives (029-001).

## Moral and Cultural Significance

The Pigeon in this Jataka tale represents the ideal of wisdom (*paññā*) and right conduct. [[the-prince-of-five-weapons|The Bodhisatta]], even in animal form, [[supernatural-beings|demons]]trates the virtues of generosity (sharing his nest), discernment (seeing through the Crow's deception), and appropriate detachment (leaving when the Crow proves incorrigible). The closing verses, which the Pigeon recites, distill the [[buddhist-framework|Buddhist teaching]] that suffering arises from craving (*taṇhā*): the Crow's desire for food that is not his leads directly to his painful demise (029-027-029-031).
