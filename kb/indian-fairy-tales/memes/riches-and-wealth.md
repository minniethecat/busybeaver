Riches and wealth pervade Indian fairy tales as both desire and danger. Gold plates at a princess's feast, hidden treasure stolen by thieves, a serpent that gives gold coins in exchange for milk, and a miser who dreams his way to ruin — these tales map the moral geography of wealth in a society where prosperity was recognized as a blessing but material attachment was understood as a spiritual hazard.

## Definition

In the narrative economy of Indian fairy tales, wealth appears in multiple registers: as **reward** for virtue or cleverness (the hero who wins a kingdom), as **temptation** that leads to downfall (the miser whose greed destroys him), as **test** of character (the money-lender who claims "riches come from Ram"), and as **narrative device** — the magical ring, the wishing-pot, the gold-giving serpent that drives the plot forward. Gold (*suvarna*), the most frequently mentioned form of wealth, carries alchemical symbolism in Indian tradition, associated with purity, divine favor, and transformation (030-070).

## Examples in the Tales

Wealth appears as a driving force across many tales:

- **The gold plates of Princess Labam** (002-037): When the hero finally reaches Princess Labam's palace, she serves him "a most delicious dinner, served up on two gold plates" (002-037). The gold plates signify both royal status and the otherworldly luxury of the fairy princess's domain. The tale is full of golden objects — the hero's bed, his weapons, the coats worn by demons — all marking the threshold between the ordinary world and the realm of wonders.

- **The miser's shattered dream** (005-001): In *The Broken Pot*, a "born miser" (Sanskrit *kripana*) plans his future wealth in elaborate detail while carrying a pot of rice on his head: "when I sell them, plenty of gold. With that gold I shall get a wife..." (005-001). He grows so excited by his imaginary prosperity that he kicks the pot, shattering it — and his dreams — in an instant. The tale is a cautionary fable about the folly of counting future gains before they materialize, a theme the Bidpai fables frequently explore.

- **The goldsmith's betrayal** (010-004, 010-022): In *The Soothsayer's Son*, Gangazara meets a goldsmith trapped in a well and frees him. The goldsmith — whose very profession is the working of wealth — turns out to be the murderer of Gangazara's father (010-010). Gangazara later "beat his head, tore his hair, and... uttered cries of sorrow" (010-022) for having "believed in the word of a goldsmith" (010-022). The tale links wealth-manufacture with moral corruption: those who work with gold cannot be trusted with truth.

- **The stolen royal treasure** (011-003): In *Harisarman*, "much treasure, both of gold and jewels, had been stolen" (011-003) from the Raja's palace, setting up the premise that allows the poor Brahman Harisarman to pretend to be a diviner. The hidden treasure — its theft and eventual recovery — structures the entire narrative arc, with Harisarman's pretended magical powers all revolving around the location and recovery of wealth.

- **The gold-giving serpent** (015-001): The serpent who gives gold coins to a farmer in exchange for daily offerings of milk represents wealth as a supernatural gift contingent on ritual reciprocity. When the farmer's son greedily attacks the serpent, the gift is withdrawn, and the son pays with his life (015-003). The tale is explicit: wealth is a blessing, not an entitlement.

## Cultural Context

Indian folklore treats wealth within a framework shaped by both Hindu and Buddhist ethics. The concept of *artha* (material prosperity) is recognized in Hindu thought as one of the four legitimate aims of life (*purusharthas*), alongside *dharma* (duty), *kama* (pleasure), and *moksha* (liberation). But *artha* must be pursued within the bounds of *dharma* — righteous conduct. Tales like *The Gold-Giving Serpent* dramatize exactly this tension: wealth received through proper ritual practice is blessed; wealth seized through violence brings destruction.

The Buddhist Jataka tales add a further dimension. In Buddhist ethics, attachment (*upadana*) to wealth is a form of craving (*tanha*), one of the root causes of suffering. The miser who dreams of gold, the goldsmith who kills for profit, the thieves who steal royal treasure — all illustrate how attachment to riches generates suffering for self and others (030-098). At the same time, the tales do not condemn wealth itself: the prosperous king, the rewarded hero, the generous merchant — these figures appear as often as the greedy miser, suggesting that wealth is morally neutral and only the disposition toward it determines its spiritual effect.
