# The Vizier's Son

## Overview

The Vizier's Son is the unnamed yet indispensable companion of the exiled prince in [[the-ivory-city-and-its-fairy-princess]] (Chapter 26). When the jealous Queen-mother has the King's son driven from the palace, the Vizier's Son does not merely accompany him — he voluntarily joins the prince in exile, bringing four bags of muhrs for their survival, and in the course of their journey proves himself the story's true strategist (026-014). He outwits robbers, identifies the fairy princess from the prince's description, devises the plan to brand [[gulizar|Gulizar]] and retrieve the necklace, and eventually secures the prince's marriage.

A shorter version of the Vizier's Son also appears in [[why-the-fish-laughed]] (Chapter 24) as a riddle-solving traveler. There his cleverness extends beyond practical stratagems to the interpretation of enigmatic speech — he speaks in riddles that only the farmer's daughter can unravel, and in doing so exposes a hidden scandal in the [[king|Raja]]'s palace (024-049, 024-055).

## Role in the Tale

In *The [[ivory-city|Ivory City]] and its Fairy Princess*, the Vizier's Son is introduced alongside the King's son as one of the youths whose fathers vow that, should the King drive out his son, their own sons will go with him into exile (026-007). When the Queen-mother's scheme succeeds, the Vizier's Son makes good on that pledge, overtaking the prince at the last custom-house with four bags of muhrs (026-014). From this point forward every significant advance in the story is his doing.

He procures grain and oil from the baniya while the prince guards their goods, only to return and find the fire extinguished and the grain stolen (026-018, 026-020). After the prince falls into despair over a vision of the fairy princess, the Vizier's Son keeps him grounded — frowning slightly "in token of disapproval" at the prince's melodramatic grief, and taking charge: "I will climb to the window, and see whether she is a human being or a fairy" (026-034, 026-037). He persuades the prince to sell their horses and goods at the ivory city, making the horses deliberately thin to command better prices and refusing the [[king|Raja]]'s offer of an elephant in exchange (026-042, 026-045). Throughout their sojourn in the old woman's hut, he pretends ignorance while drawing out information about [[gulizar|Gulizar]], the princess he correctly deduces is the prince's beloved (026-045, 026-049, 026-050). When the old woman arranges for the princess to brand her servant girl instead, the Vizier's Son detects the deception, warns the prince, and devises the counter-plan to retrieve the necklace (026-071, 026-073). Even after a near-fatal poisoning attempt, he remains wary: "[[khuda|God]] be praised," he exclaims, "who has preserved me from death" (026-082).

In *Why the Fish Laughed*, the Vizier's Son appears as a traveler who speaks in riddles — asking whether guests can "carry one another" (tell stories), whether the house is "eaten" (in debt), and whether the "beam is strong" (can the host afford guests). The old farmer's daughter alone understands his coded inquiries (024-046). He later marries her, and together they solve the mystery of [[the-fish|the laughing fish]] — a man [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]]d as a woman in the [[king|Raja]]'s palace (024-055).

## Key Deeds and Appearances

- **Voluntary exile** — overtakes the prince at the last custom-house with four bags of muhrs, choosing exile over safety (026-014).
- **Procurement and loss** — buys grain and oil from the baniya while the prince guards their goods; returns to find both fire and grain lost (026-018, 026-020).
- **Identifying the princess** — hears the prince's description of the fairy woman he glimpsed through a palace window and correctly identifies her as [[gulizar|Gulizar]], the [[ivory-city|Ivory City]] princess (026-029, 026-050).
- **The branding ruse** — climbs to the palace window to examine the brand-mark on [[gulizar|Gulizar]]'s neck, and later detects the old woman's substitution of a servant girl with a pomegranate-juice stain (026-037, 026-071).
- **The horse trick** — makes the horses deliberately thin to command higher prices, then refuses the [[king|Raja]]'s elephant offer (026-042, 026-045).
- **Poisoning attempt and survival** — narrowly escapes death when the old woman, bribed by the princess, sends him poisoned food (026-079, 026-080, 026-082).
- **Riddle-speaking traveler** (Ch 24) — encodes his questions in proverbial speech and, with the farmer's daughter, deciphers the mystery of [[the-fish|the laughing fish]] (024-049, 024-055).

## Relationships

- **[[king|The King]]'s Son (the Prince)** — the exiled heir whose fortunes the Vizier's Son transforms from despair to triumph; he follows the Vizier's Son's counsel at every turn, ultimately declaring he would "do everything as the vizier's son had advised him" (026-087).
- **[[gulizar|Gulizar]]** — the fairy princess of the [[ivory-city|Ivory City]], whom the Vizier's Son identifies, schemes to meet, and ultimately secures as the prince's bride (026-029, 026-050).
- **[[the-old-woman|The Old Woman]]** — their host in the [[ivory-city|Ivory City]], whom the Vizier's Son plays against her own cunning, drawing information from her while pretending ignorance (026-045, 026-054).
- **[[the-farmers-daughter|The Farmer's Daughter]]** — in Ch 24, the only person capable of interpreting his riddle-speech; she becomes his wife (024-046, 024-055).

## Moral or Cultural Significance

The Vizier's Son embodies a distinct folktale type: the clever companion whose intelligence outstrips his master's. He is not royal, not magical, not divinely favoured — he succeeds through resourcefulness, patience, and a keen eye for deception. His pattern of pretending ignorance while collecting information (with the old woman, with the baniya) recurs across both chapters, and marks him as a forerunner of the vizier-as-strategist type that appears across Indian and Persian narrative traditions. His riddling mode of speech in Ch 24 further aligns him with the figure of the wise fool or coded messenger — one whose words must be untangled by an equally clever listener.
