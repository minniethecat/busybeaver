# The Garden

## Location

The garden is the cultivated pleasure-ground attached to a king's palace, a recurring built setting throughout the tales. It is typically walled and "very large and full" of fruit and flowers — [[king-dantal|King Dantal]]'s garden in [[loving-laili]] is described this way (008-001), and it is a "beautiful garden" through which the prince walks (008-020). The garden lies immediately beside the royal residence: in [[the-charmed-ring]] a magic building stands "in the midst of the palace garden" (012-015), and in [[harisarman]] treasure is buried "in the earth in a garden behind the palace, under a pomegranate tree" (011-003). Tended by a gardener whose family lives on the grounds (022-002, 022-003), the garden is a place of fruit-gathering, shade trees, and secluded corners where a person can be "hidden in the tall grass" (008-026).

## Appearances in Tales

- [[loving-laili]] — [[king-dantal|King Dantal]]'s garden, "very large and full" of fruit, is the setting where the prince and the Wazir's son walk, gather fruit, and where [[laili|Laili]] hides in the tall grass (008-001, 008-020, 008-026, 008-027).
- [[punchkin]] — When the Magician walks "in his garden" he sees the little girl and is deceived by her; later the whole garden becomes "suddenly alive" as the enchanter's victims are freed (004-052, 004-074).
- [[harisarman]] — Stolen treasure is buried "in a garden behind the palace, under a pomegranate tree," and [[harisarman-the-brahman|Harisarman]] leads the king "into the garden" to the hidden hoard (011-003, 011-004).
- [[the-charmed-ring]] — A magic palace appears "in the midst of the palace garden," which the prince enters (012-015).
- [[the-boy-who-had-a-moon-on-his-forehead-and-a-star-on-his-chin]] — The king walks "under the shady trees in the King's garden with the gardener's daughter," whom he courts and marries (022-002).
- [[the-soothsayers-son]] — Opposing armies encamp "at a garden near the dungeon" during the siege (010-012).

## Significance

The garden is the tales' privileged space of encounter and concealment. As a meeting-place it hosts courtship and romance: it is where a king woos the gardener's daughter beneath the shade trees (022-002), and where princes wander in search of the beloved. As a place of hiding, its tall grass and dense foliage conceal [[laili|Laili]] after her rescue (008-026) and shelter buried treasure beneath a pomegranate tree (011-003). As a stage for enchantment, it is where [[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]] the Magician is deceived and where his victims spring back to life when the spell breaks (004-052, 004-074), and where a magic palace materialises for the prince to enter (012-015). The garden thus repeatedly serves as the threshold between the guarded formality of the palace and the freer, transformative events of the tale.

## Cultural or Historical Notes

The walled pleasure-garden is a hallmark of Indian and wider Persianate royal culture, where the enclosed *bagh* — irrigated, planted with fruit trees and flowers, and reserved for the court — was an emblem of kingly refinement and paradise on earth. In the tales this ideal appears as the vast, fruit-laden garden of a raja, tended by a hereditary gardener whose daughter can herself rise to become a queen (022-002, 022-017). The garden's association with love, secrecy, and magical [[disguise-and-deception|transformation]] is a stock feature of the Indian wonder-tale; through the same transmission routes Joseph Jacobs describes for the collection — the *Panchatantra* and the *Ocean of the Streams of Story* — such enchanted-garden motifs also entered the storytelling traditions of the wider world.
