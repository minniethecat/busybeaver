# The Jungle

## Location

The jungle is the collection's most pervasive wilderness setting: dense, uncultivated scrub and thicket lying just beyond the edge of villages and cities, through which travellers must pass and in which they are repeatedly lost. It is characterised as wild and dangerous — "if I go into the jungle, then the wild beasts will eat" the traveller (002-035) — and often as impassably thick, "a white country covered with thick jungle. In the midst of the jungle" stands a hidden place (004-061). Unlike a bounded wood a traveller crosses in a day, the jungle is a chain of successive wildernesses: a hero goes "on and on till he came to a jungle," then "to another jungle," then "to a third jungle" (002-014, 002-015, 002-027).

## Appearances in Tales

- [[how-the-rajas-son-won-the-princess-labam]] — The prince's whole quest is a passage through jungles: setting out, "he found himself in a jungle, and nothing in the jungle but" wilderness (002-003); he comes successively to a jungle with a tank (002-014), "another jungle, and there he saw a tiger" (002-015), and "a third jungle" where four fakirs live (002-027), and there lets his horse loose in the jungle (002-030).
- [[punchkin]] — The seven princes are lured "out into the jungle" (004-027) and, finding "themselves all alone in the thick jungle," are much frightened (004-029); later a [[king|Raja]] hunting "in that same jungle" (004-030) rescues one abandoned princess, and the hero pursues his quest "far, far away over the thick jungles" (004-065).
- [[loving-laili]] — The grieving prince "went off into the jungle, and wandered from jungle to jungle" (008-007); [[laili|Laili]] herself is left "living in the jungle where her husband had left her" (008-023) and finally "disappeared into the jungle" (008-027), making the jungle the emblem of separation and loss.
- [[the-boy-who-had-a-moon-on-his-forehead-and-a-star-on-his-chin]] — The wronged queen is taken "out to a broad plain in the jungle" (022-018), and the faithful dog goes "by night to the jungle" (022-019) and is later "taken to the jungle and shot" (022-024) — the jungle serving as the place of exile and execution.

## Significance

Where a court or village supplies the tales' social order, the jungle supplies its opposite: a zone without law where anything may happen. It is where the [[talking-animals|animal fables]] belong — the tiger of [[how-the-rajas-son-won-the-princess-labam]] (002-015) rules his own patch of it — and where human society expels its victims: abandoned princes (004-029), a slandered queen and her loyal dog (022-018, 022-024), a lost bride (008-023). Crossing the jungle is the standard ordeal by which a hero proves himself, and its repetition ("from jungle to jungle," 008-007) measures the depth of a character's wandering or grief. The jungle is thus both a physical wilderness and a narrative engine of trial, danger, and [[disguise-and-deception|transformation]].

## Cultural or Historical Notes

The English word *jungle* entered the language from Hindi/Urdu *jaṅgal* (itself from Sanskrit *jāṅgala*), meaning wild, uncultivated land — the sense preserved throughout these translations, where the jungle is scrub-and-thicket country rather than specifically tropical rainforest. Its constant presence at the margins of the tales reflects the geography of the north Indian and Deccan settings from which Jacobs drew, where cultivated land gave way abruptly to tiger-haunted waste.
