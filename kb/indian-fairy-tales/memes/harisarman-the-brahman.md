# Harisarman

## Overview

Harisarman is the protagonist of the eleventh tale, [[harisarman]]. He is introduced as "a certain Brahman in a certain village, named Harisarman," who "was poor and foolish and in evil case for want of employment," burdened with "very many children, that he might reap the fruit of his misdeeds in a former life" (011-001). Reduced to begging with his family, he takes service with a rich householder named [[sthuladatta|Sthuladatta]], where his sons herd cattle and his wife becomes a servant (011-001). From this lowly station Harisarman rises, through a chain of accidents rather than any real skill, to become honoured by a king as a great sage — a comic study of how reputation, luck, and bluff can substitute for genuine wisdom. He belongs to the wider tradition of the [[trickster-figure]], though his triumphs owe far more to fate than to cunning.

## Role in the Tale

Harisarman is the false diviner. Slighted and left unfed at his patron's daughter's wedding feast, he resolves to win respect by pretence: "I will pretend by means of an artifice to possess a knowledge of magic, so that I may become an object of respect to this [[sthuladatta|Sthuladatta]]" (011-002). Every episode that follows tests this imposture, and each time blind luck — not learning — rescues him. The tale turns on [[disguise-and-deception]]: Harisarman never actually acquires magical knowledge, yet the world persists in treating his lucky guesses as proof of it. His repeated private terror ("he was sad because he had pretended to have knowledge," 011-003) supplies the story's irony, while the crowd's applause supplies its lesson about how easily fame outruns merit.

## Key Deeds and Appearances

- **Staging the first "miracle" (011-002).** To manufacture a problem only he can solve, Harisarman secretly hides the bridegroom's horse "at some distance" while the household sleeps. When the horse cannot be found, his wife advertises him as "a wise man, skilled in astrology and magical sciences," and [[sthuladatta|Sthuladatta]], having earlier forgotten him, now begs his pardon: "I forgot you, forgive me" (011-002). Harisarman draws "all kinds of pretended diagrams" and directs the searchers to the very spot where he himself hid the animal, and is thereafter "honoured by all men as a sage" (011-002).

- **The lucky lament over "Tongue" (011-003, 011-004).** Summoned by the king to name the thief of stolen palace treasure, Harisarman stalls for a day and, alone and despairing, blames his own tongue aloud:

  > "O Tongue, what is this that you have done through your greediness? Wicked one, you will soon receive punishment in full." (011-003)

  By pure chance the actual thief is a palace maid named [[the-maid|Jihva]] — which means Tongue — who, overhearing him, believes herself discovered, falls at his feet, and confesses where the treasure is buried "under a pomegranate tree" (011-003). Harisarman coolly accepts the credit — "Depart, I know all this; I know the past, present and future" (011-004) — and marvels privately that "Fate brings about, as if in sport, things impossible" (011-004). He leads the king to the treasure and is rewarded with the revenue of many villages.

- **The frog in the pitcher (011-005).** The sceptical minister Devajnanin persuades the king to test Harisarman with a covered pitcher secretly containing a frog. Certain "that his last hour had come," Harisarman recalls the pet name "Froggie" his father gave him in childhood and cries out to himself:

  > "This is a fine pitcher for you, Froggie; it will soon become the swift destroyer of your helpless self." (011-005)

  The words seem to name the hidden frog exactly, the onlookers shout that he "knows even about the frog," and the delighted king heaps on him "the revenue of more villages, with gold, an umbrella, and state carriages of all kinds" (011-005). "So Harisarman prospered in the world" (011-005).

## Relationships

- **[[sthuladatta|Sthuladatta]]** — the rich householder Harisarman serves; the patron who neglects him at the wedding feast and then, after the staged theft of the horse, propitiates him and honours him as a sage (011-001, 011-002).
- **Harisarman's wife** — a servant in [[sthuladatta|Sthuladatta]]'s house who plays a knowing part in the fraud, presenting her husband to the household as a master of "astrology and magical sciences" (011-002).
- **[[the-maid|Jihva]] ("Tongue")** — the palace maid who, with her brother, stole the king's treasure; her terror at Harisarman's overheard lament makes her confess, handing him his greatest apparent triumph (011-003).
- **Devajnanin** — the king's minister, the one sceptic who suspects Harisarman of "a secret intelligence with thieves" and devises the frog-pitcher test that Harisarman survives only by luck (011-005).
- **The king** — the ruler who summons Harisarman over the stolen treasure and, convinced by the frog episode, showers him with lands and honours (011-003, 011-005).

## Moral or Cultural Significance

Harisarman is the archetype of the lucky impostor, and his tale is a wry meditation on the gap between reputation and reality. He possesses no magic whatsoever; his rise depends entirely on coincidence, most pointedly the double meaning of *jihva* ("tongue") and the childhood nickname "Froggie." His own reflection supplies the story's philosophy: "Secret crimes manifest themselves by means of fear" (011-004) — it is the guilty maid's fear, not any occult sight, that betrays her. As a poor Brahman who wins wealth by bluff, Harisarman also illustrates the fluid interplay of [[caste-in-folklore]] and cleverness in the Indian tale tradition, where wit and fortune can lift a beggar to a king's favour. The tale is finally comic rather than cautionary: the fraud is never exposed, fate rewards the pretender, and the reader is left smiling at a world so ready to mistake luck for wisdom.
