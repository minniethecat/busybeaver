# Lambikin's Drum Escape

## Summary

Having fattened for seven days at his [[the-granny|Granny]]'s house (003-020), [[lambikin]] devised a way to get home past the hungry beasts who lay in wait for him. He instructed that a little drum — a "drumikin" — be made from the skin of his brother, curled himself up snug inside it with the wool turned in, and had himself set rolling along the road (003-021, 003-022). As each beast demanded whether it had seen [[lambikin|Lambikin]], the lamb answered from within the drum in a taunting rhyme, and the [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]] carried him safely past — until the last predator recognized the hidden voice, tore the drum open, and gobbled him up (003-045, 003-047).

> "…you must make a little drumikin out of the skin of my little brother…" (003-021)

## Context

The tale is a cumulative beast-fable. On his outward journey the plump little [[lambikin|Lambikin]] escaped a succession of hungry animals by promising he would return far fatter after [[food-and-feasting|feasting]] at his grandmother's: "Where I shall fatter grow" (003-005), a plea the beasts found reasonable and so let him pass (003-007, 003-013). The drum escape is his scheme for the return trip, when the same beasts are lying in wait to collect on that promise. The [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]] is the story's clever centrepiece and its turning point.

## Participants

- **[[lambikin]]** — the frolicking lamb whose wit nearly saves him; he authors the drum trick and voices the rhyme from inside.
- **[[lambikin|Lambikin]]'s [[the-granny|Granny]]** — who fattens him and fashions the drumikin from the brother-lamb's skin (003-022).
- **The Beasts of the Road (jackal and others)** — the predators he must pass; the last of them sees through the [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]].

The event is central to [[the-lambikin]], the third tale of the collection.

## Outcome or Consequence

The trick succeeds against every beast but the last. [[lambikin|Lambikin]] trundled along "laughing to himself" (003-029), curled up snug in his warm nest (003-025) and calling out his taunting rhyme from within the drum. The final predator recognized the hidden voice, "whereupon he tore open [[lambikin|Drumikin]] and gobbled up [[lambikin|Lambikin]]" (003-047). The lamb's cleverness wins him several reprieves but not, in the end, his life.

## Cultural or Moral Significance

The drum escape belongs to the cumulative "flight" pattern of animal tales, in which a small creature repeatedly outwits larger ones through cunning and rhyme. Yet [[lambikin|Lambikin]]'s fate carries a cautionary edge: cleverness delays but does not finally avert danger, and over-confidence — singing his triumph aloud once too often — betrays him. Jacobs notes the closeness of the [[lambikin|Drumikin]] motif to the churning-hill escapes of European nursery tales (030-032), marking [[lambikin|Lambikin]] as a distinctively Indian cousin of that widespread type.
