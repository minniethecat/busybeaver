The **Chief's Son** is the rescuer and eventual husband of the [[the-bonga-girl|Bonga Girl]] in "[[the-magic-fiddle|The Magic Fiddle]]" (Chapter 6 of Joseph Jacobs's *Indian Fairy Tales*, 1892). A young man living in his father's household — a village chief's compound — he is a musician who plays the stolen fiddle with skill and delight. Unlike the [[the-jogi|Jogi]] who created the instrument, the Chief's Son is curious enough to discover its secret: he hides in a woodpile, watches the Bonga Girl emerge from the fiddle, and catches her in his arms, declaring them one from that day forward (006-021).

The Chief's Son is the tale's agent of resolution. He transforms the fiddle from a stolen magical object into a home — and the Bonga Girl from a hidden spirit into a visible wife. His role is the closing arc of the Bonga Girl's long metamorphosis: drowned by sisters-in-law → transformed to a Bonga → imprisoned in bamboo → trapped in a fiddle → discovered and married. He is the ending.

## Overview

The Chief's Son enters the story when his household steals the Jogi's fiddle. Unlike the Jogi, who used the instrument for profit, the Chief's Son plays it for pleasure — "the music it gave forth delighted the ears of all who heard it" (006-020). He is a natural musician, and his relationship to the fiddle is aesthetic rather than transactional.

When food begins appearing daily under his bed, the Chief's Son grows suspicious. Other household members assume "some girl friend of theirs was in this manner showing her interest in the young man," but he is angry rather than flattered: "I will catch her to-day, and give her a sound beating; she is causing me to be ashamed before the others" (006-021). His determination to solve the mystery — hiding in a corner in a pile of firewood — is the pivot on which the entire tale turns.

What he discovers changes everything. [[the-bonga-girl|The Bonga Girl]] emerges from the fiddle, "began to dress her hair," cooked the meal, ate, and prepared to re-enter the instrument (006-021). The Chief's Son runs from his hiding-place and catches her. When she protests — "Fie! Fie! you may be a Dom, or you may be a Hadi of some other caste with whom I cannot marry" — he answers simply: "No. But from to-day, you and I are one" (006-021). The wedding is immediate, verbal, and permanent. The household, returning from the fields, "saw that she was both a human being and a Bonga, and they rejoiced exceedingly" (006-021).

## Role in the Tale

The Chief's Son plays three roles, each building on the last:

**The Musician.** He is the first person to play the fiddle purely for its music. [[the-jogi|The Jogi]] used it to fill his wallet; the Chief's Son plays for joy. This aesthetic relationship matters: it is the music that first draws the household to the fiddle, and it is the fiddle's silent, secret life — the Bonga Girl within — that the music conceals (006-020).

**The Detective.** His decision to hide and watch is motivated by shame — he does not want to be seen accepting food from an unknown girl — but it requires patience and nerve. He watches the full sequence: emergence, grooming, cooking, eating, departure. He waits for the right moment — "when she was about to enter the fiddle again" — and then acts (006-021).

**The Husband.** His declaration — "from to-day, you and I are one" — is striking for its simplicity. There is no negotiation, no bride-price, no ceremony. He catches her, and by catching her, marries her. [[the-bonga-girl|The Bonga Girl]]'s caste objection ("Dom," "Hadi" — both low-caste/untouchable designations) is dismissed with a single word: "No." The marriage is both romantic and transactional, a capture and a liberation.

## Relationships

- **[[the-bonga-girl|The Bonga Girl]]** — His wife. Their relationship begins with capture but matures into acceptance. The household's joy that she is both human and Bonga (006-021) suggests that the Chief's Son does not try to diminish her dual nature — he marries all of her.
- **[[the-jogi|The Jogi]]** — The previous owner of the fiddle. The Chief's Son never meets the Jogi; the fiddle is stolen by the household, not by him personally. He is the beneficiary of someone else's theft, which complicates any simple reading of him as a hero.
- **[[the-village|The Village]] Chief** (his father) — Mentioned only as the head of the household. The father's role is to provide the domestic setting in which the discovery can occur — a house large enough to hold a hidden fiddle, empty enough during the day for the Bonga Girl to emerge unseen.

## Cultural Significance

The Chief's Son is a folk-tale example of the "capture bridegroom" — a male counterpart to the "capture bride" motif. He gains his wife not through courtship or arrangement but through observation and seizure. This asymmetry is typical of Indian folk narratives in which supernatural brides are won by mortal men who witness their secret [[disguise-and-deception|transformation]]s.

The caste reference in the Bonga Girl's protest — "you may be a Dom, or you may be a Hadi" — grounds the marriage in the social reality of the Santal world, where inter-caste unions were fraught. By dismissing the objection with a simple "No," the Chief's Son asserts the primacy of personal bond over caste regulation. The household's rejoicing — rather than outrage — makes this a utopian resolution, a folk vision of social acceptance that real Santal villages might not have matched.
