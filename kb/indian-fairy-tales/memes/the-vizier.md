## Overview

The Vizier (Wazir) appears as a significant character in two tales of the *Indian Fairy Tales* collection: as a loyal but imperiled advisor in "Why the Fish Laughed" and as the father of the prince's devoted companion in "The [[ivory-city|Ivory City]] and Its Fairy Princess." In both tales, the vizier embodies the tension between serving absolute royal authority and protecting one's own family — a recurring theme in the folklore of courtly life.

## Role in the Tales

### Why the Fish Laughed (Chapter 24)

The vizier in this tale is caught between a king's jealous suspicion and a wife's cunning plot. When the queen tells the king a secret that causes a dead fish to laugh, the king threatens the vizier with death unless he discovers why the fish laughed within six months (024-011). The vizier searches desperately across the land, growing "almost dead from the exposure" (024-050), until his own son — having married a clever farmer's daughter — returns with the explanation. The story ends with the king satisfied and "the faithful old vizier saved" (024-052), and his son married to the farmer's daughter (024-055).

### The Ivory City and Its Fairy Princess (Chapter 26)

In this tale, the chief vizier is the father of the prince's loyal companion — the vizier's son who accompanies the prince on his adventures (026-001). When the two young men accidentally shoot an arrow that strikes the [[ivory-city|Ivory City]], it is the vizier who counsels the angry king: "I beseech you. Let the facts be examined before you give an order" (026-010). Throughout the tale, the vizier appears as a moderating voice, having "a long and earnest talk" with the king (026-013) about how to handle the situation. His son goes on to be a key figure in the adventure, assisting the prince at every turn.

The vizier's most dramatic moment comes when, near death from his desperate search, he receives the answer from his son:

> "Joy, joy!" exclaimed the vizier's son. "There is yet time for the vizier to say why the fish laughed." (024-049)

## Relationships

- **[[king|The King]]** — his master; the vizier serves loyally but lives at the king's mercy, as shown when the king threatens him with death over the fish's secret (024-011)
- **[[the-viziers-son|The Vizier's Son]]** — his son, who saves him in "Why the Fish Laughed" and is the prince's key ally in "The [[ivory-city|Ivory City]]" (026-014)
- **[[the-prince|The Prince]]** — in "The [[ivory-city|Ivory City]]," his son accompanies and aids the prince on the adventure

## Moral and Cultural Significance

The vizier represents the precarious position of royal servants in Indian courtly culture. No matter how high-ranking or faithful, the vizier's life ultimately depends on the king's pleasure — a single unexplained mystery can mean death (024-052). Yet the vizier is not portrayed as a victim; he is resourceful and, through his son, ultimately vindicated. In both tales, the vizier's survival depends on the next generation — a son who either discovers the answer or proves his worth. This reflects the broader theme of intergenerational wisdom in Indian folklore, where the young supplement the wisdom of the old.
