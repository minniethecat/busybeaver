# The Death of Punchkin

## Summary

The climax of the tale of [[punchkin-magician]] turns on the secret of the magician's life, which he keeps hidden far from his own body inside a little green parrot. When [[balna]]'s son gains possession of the bird and destroys it, the magician dies instantly and all his victims are released. This is the classic "external-soul" resolution, in which a seemingly invincible sorcerer is undone the moment his separated life is found.

> …which contains a little green parrot; on the life of the parrot depends… (004-061)

## Context

[[balna|Balna]], held captive in a tower, coaxes [[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]] into revealing where his life is stored, and learns it lies not in his body but in a distant place guarded by beasts, in a cage "which contains a little green parrot" (004-061). Her son undertakes the journey, passing every guardian, and "seized [[the-parrot|the little green parrot]], which he rolled up in his cloth" (004-065). Returning to the palace, the boy openly sits "and began playing with the parrot" (004-067) until [[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]] sees him.

> Punchkin saw him, and came to him quickly… (004-067)

## Participants

- [[punchkin-magician]] — the wicked magician whose entire existence is bound to the parrot's life.
- [[the-parrot]] — [[the-parrot|the little green parrot]]; not a character who acts, but the fragile vessel in which the magician's soul is hidden.
- [[balna]] — the captive queen whose questioning first uncovers the secret, and whose son carries out the deed.

## Outcome or Consequence

Terrified, [[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]] begs for the bird — "Give me my parrot!" (004-074) — and in a frantic attempt to bargain offers to release his prisoners: "Let the seven [[king|Raja]]'s sons whom you turned into rocks and trees be instantly liberated" (004-071). By a stroke of his wand, "[[balna|Balna]]'s husband and his brothers resumed" their human forms (004-072), and the enchanted garden "became suddenly alive: where rocks, and stones, and trees had been" (004-074).

But the boy does not relent. Dismembering the bird limb by limb as [[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]]'s own body mirrors each loss, he at last "wrung the bird's neck, and threw it" down; "[[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]]'s head twisted round, and, with a fearful groan, he died!" (004-078). [[balna|Balna]] is then let out of the tower (004-079), reunited with her husband and the freed princes.

## Cultural or Moral Significance

[[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]]'s death is Jacobs's showcase instance of the "[[the-external-soul|life-index]]" or external-soul motif, which he singles out in his notes as spread "in the Deccan ('[[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]]'); in [[bengal|Bengal]]" and beyond (030-035). The story dramatizes a deep folk intuition: that great power concealed and externalized becomes great vulnerability. [[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]] is unkillable by sword or force, yet a child destroys him by finding the one hidden thing that holds his soul. The episode also completes the tale's moral arithmetic — the innocent turned to stone are restored, and the tyrant is repaid in kind — affirming the collection's recurring promise that cruelty carries the seed of its own undoing.
