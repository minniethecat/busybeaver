# The Tortoise's Fatal Fall

## Summary

A tortoise living in a pond in the Himalaya mountains befriended two young hamsas, or wild ducks (013-003). Wishing to carry their friend home to their own dwelling, the ducks had the tortoise bite firmly onto the middle of a stick, which they then took up by the ends and bore aloft through the air (013-008). As the strange sight passed over a city, children below cried out that two wild ducks were carrying a tortoise along on a stick (013-009). Stung by the taunt, the tortoise let go the stick to make an angry reply — and, its jaws no longer gripping, it fell to its death (013-019).

> And making the tortoise bite hold of a stick, they thus went through the air. (013-008)

## Context

The event is the moral exemplum inside a frame story: the Bodhisatta, born as a king's counsellor, seeks a way to cure a talkative king of his endless speech (013-010, 013-011). Seeing the fallen tortoise, the king asks the [[bodhisatta|Bodisat]] how it came to grief, and the Bodhisatta tells this tale of the tortoise and the ducks to illustrate the danger. The bird-borne tortoise is thus a story-within-a-story, aimed squarely at the king's own failing.

## Participants

- **[[the-tortoise|The Tortoise]]** — the talkative creature who cannot hold its tongue even when its life depends on silence.
- **The Two Wild Ducks (Hamsas)** — the tortoise's friends, who devise the stick-carriage and warn, in effect, that the plan depends on the tortoise keeping its mouth shut.
- **[[the-prince-of-five-weapons|The Bodhisatta]]** — the [[bodhisatta]] as royal counsellor, who narrates the fall to teach the king.

The event unfolds within [[the-talkative-tortoise]], the thirteenth tale of the collection.

## Outcome or Consequence

The tortoise is killed by its own inability to keep silent. [[the-prince-of-five-weapons|The Bodhisatta]] drives home the point in verse: "Verily the tortoise killed himself" (013-012), for "The tortoise fell into this wretched plight!" (013-019) precisely because it could not stay quiet. The lesson lands on its intended target: hearing it, the talkative king reforms and "became a man of few words" (013-022).

## Cultural or Moral Significance

The episode is a classic Jataka parable on the perils of unbridled speech. It warns that "chatter-boxes — people whose words have no end — come to grief" (013-011), counselling instead to "speak wise words, not out of season" (013-017). The motif of the tortoise carried by birds and killed by its own talk is ancient and widely travelled, appearing in the Panchatantra and its many descendants, and here serving the collection's broader interest in the discipline of the tongue.
