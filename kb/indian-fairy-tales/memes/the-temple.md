# The Temple

## Location

The temple (and its smaller counterpart, the shrine) is a place of worship dedicated to a deity, appearing in the tales as the setting for prayer, offering, and divine intervention. Temples in the collection are named for the god they house: the "nearest Ganesa temple" is where a devotee carries a corpse to call upon the elephant-headed god Ganesa (010-025). A temple may be a newly built and richly endowed foundation — a king has "a golden temple" built and staffs it with a Brahman (014-002), and "every morning at the golden temple a golden rupee" is dispensed as charity in the king's name (014-003). The shrine is the humbler place of devotion where the childless go to pray, as when a queen "wept and prayed at many a shrine" longing for a child (019-001).

## Appearances in Tales

- [[the-soothsayers-son]] — A devout worshipper of Ganesa takes a corpse "to the nearest Ganesa temple and called upon him," and the god himself answers, ashamed at the worshipper's reproach (010-025, 010-026).
- [[a-lac-of-rupees-for-a-bit-of-advice]] — A king has "a golden temple" newly built and appoints a Brahman to look after it; each morning "a golden rupee was given in the king's name" to any beggar there (014-002, 014-003).
- [[raja-rasalu]] — The childless queen Lona "wept and prayed at many a shrine" but had never a child, framing the tale's opening plea for a son (019-001).

## Significance

Though it appears less often than the palace or garden, the temple carries concentrated religious weight in the tales as the meeting-point of the human and the divine. As a place of petition, the shrine is where the barren queen prays for the child whose miraculous birth sets [[raja-rasalu]] in motion (019-001). As a site of miracle, the Ganesa temple is where a devotee's bold reproach actually moves the god to act, shaming Ganesa into restoring a corpse (010-025, 010-026). As an instrument of royal piety and charity, the golden temple channels a king's wealth to beggars each morning, linking devotion to public generosity (014-003). In each case the temple marks the point where prayer, offering, or reproach reaches directly into the world of the gods.

## Cultural or Historical Notes

The temple and shrine reflect the living Hindu devotional culture that underlies much of the collection. Ganesa (Ganesha), the elephant-headed remover of obstacles invoked at the start of undertakings, has his own temple where worship is tied to auspicious days — the tale notes that the appeal was made on "a Friday, a day very" favourable to him (010-025). The daily gift of a golden rupee "in the king's name" at the golden temple (014-003) mirrors the historical role of royal temple endowments, through which rulers gained religious merit and dispensed charity to the poor. The childless woman praying at many a shrine for offspring (019-001) is a recurring motif of Indian religious life and folk narrative alike. This religious setting is thinly but distinctively represented across the tales, appearing in only a handful of stories.
