## Overview

Hiraman is a talking parrot and the king of the parrots in the jungle where the young [[king|Raja]]'s son goes hunting against his mother's warning (002-003). When the prince shoots at the flock and all the other parrots fly away, Hiraman remains — and his refusal to be abandoned is the catalyst for the entire tale. He alone knows the name and the whereabouts of the [[princess-labam|Princess Labam]], and though he never directly reveals her location, it is his mention of her that launches the prince's quest. Hiraman thus occupies the classic folkloric role of the **supernatural informant**: the creature whose casual words set a hero on his journey.

## Role in the Tale

### The Hunting Encounter

The young [[king|Raja]]'s son, forbidden by his mother from hunting on the fourth side of the kingdom, disobeys and enters a jungle "where nothing lived but a quantity of parrots" (002-003). He shoots at them, and all fly away except their king, Hiraman. Left alone, the parrot threatens his flock:

> "Don't fly away and leave me alone when the Raja's son shoots. If you desert me like this, I will tell the Princess Labam." (002-004)

The parrots immediately fly back, chattering. The prince is astonished that the birds can talk, and demands to know who [[princess-labam|Princess Labam]] is and where she lives. But the parrots refuse to give away her location, saying only: "You can never get to the [[princess-labam|Princess Labam]]'s country" (002-005).

### The Messenger

Though Hiraman and his parrots refuse to tell the prince *where* [[princess-labam|Princess Labam]] lives, the name alone is enough. The prince returns home "sad and lonely" with love, and when he finally rallies enough to leave his bed, he tells the princess:

> "Hiraman parrot, who lives in the jungle where I went to hunt, told me your name, and then I left my father and mother, and came to see you." (002-058)

Hiraman's function is thus precise and limited: he supplies the *name* that gives the quest its object, while withholding the *location* that makes the quest necessary. Without him the tale would have no beginning.

## Key Deeds

- **Stands his ground**: When the prince shoots, all other parrots flee skyward; Hiraman stays, and his threat to "tell [[princess-labam|Princess Labam]]" is the first mention of the tale's central figure (002-004).
- **Commands the flock**: Hiraman's authority over the other parrots is absolute — they obey his call to return without hesitation, chattering in deference (002-005).
- **Guards the secret**: He and the flock know [[princess-labam|Princess Labam]]'s country but will not disclose it, telling the prince only "you can never get there" — a refusal that preserves the story's mystery and tests the hero's resolve (002-005).

## Relationships

- **[[the-rajas-son|The Raja's Son]]** — the prince who shoots at the parrots and, through Hiraman, learns of [[princess-labam|Princess Labam]]'s existence. Hiraman is the prince's unwitting informant (002-003, 002-058).
- **[[princess-labam|Princess Labam]]** — never seen directly by Hiraman in the tale, but the parrot invokes her name with such authority that the parrots immediately return to him (002-004).
- **[[the-parrot|The Parrot]] Flock** — Hiraman's subjects, who obey his commands without question and share in the keeping of [[princess-labam|Labam]]'s secret (002-005).

## Moral and Cultural Significance

Hiraman belongs to the widespread tradition of **talking birds as otherworldly messengers** in Indian folklore. Unlike the hostile or deceptive animals in some tales, Hiraman is morally neutral: he neither helps nor hinders the prince, but simply *knows*. His function is to mark the boundary between the ordinary world and the world of quest — to name what lies beyond without making [[the-road|the path]] easy. The parrot as a speaker of human language is a recurring motif in Indian narrative, from the *Śukasaptati* (Seventy Tales of the Parrot) onward, and Hiraman carries that tradition into the folk-tale register.
