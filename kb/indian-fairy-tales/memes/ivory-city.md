# Ivory City

## Location

The Ivory City is a remote, marvellous city belonging to the world of fairies rather than to any real geography. It is the home of the [[gulizar|Princess Gulizar]] — "none other than [[gulizar|Gulizar of the Ivory City]]" (026-029) — and lies so far from ordinary lands that it can only be found by asking repeatedly of every traveller along the way: the prince and his companion "were off again, and asked for Ivory City from every passer-by" until at last they got word of it (026-044). When the prince finally hears the name spoken aloud by someone who knows it, he "gave a deep sigh," so charged is the place with longing and distance (026-048, 026-049).

## Appearances in Tales

- [[the-ivory-city-and-its-fairy-princess]] — The Ivory City is named as the home of the fairy princess [[gulizar|Gulizar]], identified from the sign of an ivory box she carried (026-028, 026-029). It becomes the object of the prince's long search, sought "from every passer-by" (026-044), and is finally located when a stranger asks incredulously, "Ivory City. Don't you know that?" (026-048), at which the prince sighs at hearing his goal named (026-049).

## Significance

The Ivory City is the destination that structures the entire quest of [[the-ivory-city-and-its-fairy-princess]]. Its very name is a clue: the prince first learns the princess's identity because she showed him an ivory box, and it is from that box that her origin in the Ivory City is deduced (026-028, 026-029). The city's inaccessibility drives the plot — because no one can simply travel there, the prince must journey and inquire endlessly, and the difficulty of merely finding the place measures the depth of his devotion to [[gulizar|Gulizar]] (026-044, 026-049). As the seat of a fairy princess who possesses a "charmed swing" for travelling between worlds (026-069), the Ivory City belongs to the enchanted geography typical of Indian fairy romance.

## Cultural or Historical Notes

The Ivory City is a fictional, enchanted locale with no real-world counterpart; it is a "mythical" place in the sense used by this collection for realms that exist only within the fairy-tale world. Its name evokes the prized material of ivory — long associated in Indian art and craft with palaces, thrones, and objects of great value — and so signals a city of rare beauty and wealth suited to a fairy princess. The motif of a hero who must seek out a distant, magical city inhabited by a supernatural bride is common to the Indo-Persian romance tradition from which this tale, with its Persian-styled name "[[gulizar|Gulizar]]," appears to descend.
