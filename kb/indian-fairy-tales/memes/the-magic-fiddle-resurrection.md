# The Magic Fiddle Resurrection

## Summary

In this event a young girl, murdered by drowning at the hands of her jealous sisters-in-law, does not stay dead. Through the power of a [[supernatural-beings|Bonga]] spirit she passes through a chain of [[disguise-and-deception|transformation]]s — into a bamboo and then into a fiddle that becomes one of the tale's chief [[magical-objects|magical objects]] — until at last, recognizing her own impoverished family, she resumes human shape and is restored to them.

> The Bonga then transformed her into a Bamboo… (006-017)

## Context

The tale opens with seven married brothers and their unmarried sister (006-001); the jealous wives resent the girl, complaining "She does not go out to the fields to work, but remains quietly" at home (006-001). Invoking their Bonga and "vowing vows unto him" (006-001), the sisters-in-law engineer the girl's death. Lured to the water, "she sank and was drowned" (006-017) — but the murder does not end her existence.

> They then called upon their Bonga, and vowing vows unto him… (006-001)

## Participants

This event has no individual person pages yet; its actors are the unmarried sister (the future Bonga girl), her seven brothers and their jealous wives, and a wandering Jogi. Related pages:

- [[supernatural-beings]] — the Bonga, "the presiding spirit of a certain kind of rice land" (030-041), whose power drives every [[disguise-and-deception|transformation]].
- [[magical-objects]] — the bamboo fiddle, the enchanted instrument through which the girl continues to live and act.

## Outcome or Consequence

After drowning, the Bonga transforms the girl "into a Bamboo" growing on the embankment (006-017). A passing Jogi, judging "This will make a splendid fiddle," cuts the bamboo and fashions the instrument (006-018), whose strains deeply affect all who hear them (006-019). Thereafter, whenever the Jogi is away, "the Bonga girl used to come out of the bamboo fiddle, and prepared the family meal" (006-021), living a hidden half-human life.

The turning point comes when the fiddle is brought to the very "house of the Bonga girl's brothers" (006-019). In time her family, fallen "very poor" (006-022), unknowingly host her; but "the Bonga girl recognised them at once" (006-023). Setting cooked rice before them and sitting down, she is at last restored and reunited with her own kin (006-023).

## Cultural or Moral Significance

The magic fiddle resurrection is a striking example of the [[disguise-and-deception|transformation]]-and-rebirth motif that runs through Indian folk narrative, in which a murdered innocent is preserved by supernatural means until justice — or reunion — can be achieved. Jacobs's notes identify the climactic scene as "the denunciation of the magic fiddle of the murderous sisters" (030-042), linking the tale to a wider family of stories in which a slain victim's voice sings out from a plant or instrument to reveal the crime. The Bonga, explained as the tutelary spirit of the rice land (030-041), roots this Santal tale in a specifically animist worldview, where the boundary between the human, the vegetable, and the spirit world is fluid, and death is not necessarily final.
