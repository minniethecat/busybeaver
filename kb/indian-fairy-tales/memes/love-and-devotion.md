# Love and Devotion

**Love and Devotion** constitutes one of the most powerful narrative forces in the Indian Fairy Tales collected by Joseph Jacobs (1892). Unlike European fairy tales where love often operates at the level of enchantment — a single glance and the prince is smitten — the Indian tales portray love as an active, sustained commitment that must be proven through trial. Devotion, whether romantic or filial, is not a passive state but a discipline.

## Definition

Love in this corpus operates across three distinct modes:

1. **Romantic Love as Quest** — the male protagonist sees or hears of a beautiful princess and undertakes an arduous journey to win her, often facing lethal tests imposed by the princess's father. The Raja's son in "How the Raja's Son Won the Princess Labam" embodies this pattern: he declares his intention to marry the princess after glimpsing her beauty, then systematically overcomes each royal challenge (002-043, 002-056, 002-063).

2. **Marital Devotion Under Trial** — love between married partners is tested by separation, transformation, or supernatural interference. "Loving Laili" is the definitive example: Prince Majnun's love for Laili is so absolute that "he loved her dearly; he could not do without her even for a moment" (008-060), and this devotion drives him through her transformation into a mango tree, a pool, and other forms.

3. **Filial Love and Loyalty** — the love of a mother for her child, or a son for his parents, appears as a moral anchor. In "The Son of Seven Queens," the prince's devotion to his seven blind mothers — "as he loved to call the seven blind Queens" (016-012) — motivates his entire journey and his eventual restoration of their sight.

## Examples in the Tales

### How the Raja's Son Won the Princess Labam: Love as Quest

The prince's motivation is established early: upon seeing the princess in her beautiful clothes and jewels, "he said to himself, 'How lovely she is!'" (002-043). This single moment of admiration launches an extended quest that involves multiple tests of worthiness imposed by the king (002-063). The princess reciprocates: she puts on her "lovely clothes and jewels, and sat at her window" (002-056) to see the prince, and her mother becomes a secret ally. Love in this tale is not instantaneous magic — it is desire that must be proven through action, a pattern that distinguishes the Indian tradition from European "love at first sight" narratives.

### Loving Laili: Devotion Through Transformation

"Loving Laili" presents the most intense portrait of marital devotion in the collection. Laili falls in love with Prince Majnun the moment she sees him (008-006), but her stepmother's jealousy subjects their love to a series of supernatural ordeals. The prince's devotion transcends physical form: when Laili is transformed into a mango tree, a pool, and other shapes, he recognizes and loves her in each incarnation. The tale's central question — "Would he be very sorry if she died?" (008-019) — is answered by Majnun's willingness to endure any trial. The climax, in which he cuts the "large, lovely red fruit" (008-034) despite trembling with fear, demonstrates that love and fear coexist: devotion is not the absence of terror but the willingness to act despite it.

### The Charmed Ring: Love Across Distance

In "The Charmed Ring," love is tested by geographical separation. The merchant's son acquires a magical ring that produces "a beautiful house and a lovely princess with golden hair" (012-013) — but love cannot be manufactured by magic alone. The son must pursue the princess across kingdoms, and his "intense love" (012-015) is chronicled as a driving force equal to any magical object in the tale.

### The Ivory City and Its Fairy Princess: Love Conquering Danger

The prince's journey to the Ivory City is fundamentally a love quest, though the dangers he faces — the [[the-demon-with-the-matted-hair|Demon of the Matted Hair]], the robbers' den (026-031) — rival any martial adventure. Love provides the motivation that makes the supernatural dangers surmountable.

## Cultural Context

The Indian treatment of love in folklore reflects the tension between the *kama* (desire) tradition — celebrated in Sanskrit poetry and the *Kama Sutra* — and the ascetic traditions that counsel detachment. Jacobs's tales navigate this tension by making love a test of character: the protagonist who loves faithfully is rewarded, while the one who loves selfishly (or yields to lust rather than devotion) is punished.

The *Jataka* tales, which form the substrate of several stories in this collection, treat love as a bond that persists across rebirths — the [[bodhisatta]]'s wife in one life may appear as his companion in the next. This [[karma-and-rebirth|rebirth]] framework gives love a cosmic dimension absent from European folklore, where "happily ever after" is bounded by a single lifespan.

The tale of "Loving Laili" draws on Persian and Sufi love-narrative traditions, where the lover's devotion to the beloved becomes a metaphor for the soul's devotion to the divine. Jacobs's version, collected from oral sources in the Punjab, retains the emotional intensity while secularizing the frame.
