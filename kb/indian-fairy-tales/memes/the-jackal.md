# The Jackal

## Overview

The jackal is the clever animal judge in the tale [[the-tiger-the-brahman-and-the-jackal]] (Chapter 9). He is the last of several "judges" the desperate Brahman consults after a tiger he has kindly released turns to devour him — and the only one who takes the Brahman's side. Where a *pipal* tree, a buffalo, and the road all coldly tell the Brahman that ingratitude is the way of the world (009-007, 009-008, 009-010), the jackal alone saves him, not by strength but by cunning.

His trademark is deliberate stupidity. Meeting the Brahman on the road, he greets him with the words:

> "Why, what's the matter, Mr. Brahman? You look as miserable as a fish out of water!" (009-011)

Behind this comic bewilderment lies a sharp mind: as the story tells, he was "for all his sorry looks as sharp as a needle" (003-040, in the parallel tale of the [[lambikin|Lambikin]], where a jackal is likewise the shrewdest of the beasts). In Chapter 9 he plays the fool so convincingly that the tiger, in a rage at his slowness, condemns itself.

## Role in the Tale

The jackal enters when the Brahman, having failed to win a favourable "judgment" from tree, beast, and road, turns back "sorrowfully" toward the cage and the waiting tiger (009-011). The jackal offers to give a ruling but claims he cannot follow the story: "How very confusing!... would you mind telling me over again, for everything has got so mixed up?" (009-012). He hears it twice and still shakes his head, "in a distracted sort of way" (009-013), insisting, "it all seems to go in at one ear and out at the other! I will go to the place where it all happened" (009-014).

This apparent dullness is the whole of his strategy. Back at the cage, he keeps muddling the facts — pretending to believe he himself was in the cage, then that "the tiger was in the Brahman, and the cage came walking by" (009-022) — until the exasperated tiger tries to [[supernatural-beings|demons]]trate exactly how it was trapped. When the beast leaps back into the cage to prove the point, the jackal springs the door shut and delivers his verdict.

## Key Deeds and Appearances

- **Meets the grieving Brahman** — hails him on the road and asks why he looks "as miserable as a fish out of water" (009-011).
- **Feigns confusion** — claims the tale is too tangled to follow and asks to hear it again and again (009-012, 009-013).
- **Proposes a re-enactment** — insists on going "to the place where it all happened" so he can give judgment (009-014).
- **Muddles the facts on purpose** — pretends to mix up who was in the cage, provoking the tiger's fury (009-020, 009-022).
- **Traps the tiger** — when the tiger jumps into the cage to [[supernatural-beings|demons]]trate, "the jackal, as he dexterously shut the door" (009-036), locks it in.
- **Delivers the verdict** — grins that "matters will remain as they were!" (009-036), leaving the tiger caged and the Brahman free.

## Relationships

- **[[the-brahman|The Brahman]]** — the kindly man the jackal rescues; the Brahman explains "matters to the jackal here, who is somewhat slow in his wits" (009-018), never guessing the slowness is an act.
- **The tiger** — [[gratitude-and-ingratitude|the ungrateful]] beast the jackal outwits; the tiger, "in a rage at the jackal's stupidity" (009-023), destroys itself trying to correct him. See [[the-tiger]].
- **The false judges** — the *pipal* tree, buffalo, and road, whose cynical rulings the jackal's cunning overturns (009-007, 009-008, 009-010).

## Moral or Cultural Significance

The jackal embodies the [[trickster-figure|trickster]] who defeats brute power through wit rather than force — a figure at the heart of the Indian and Buddhist fable tradition. This tale of [[gratitude-and-ingratitude|the ungrateful]] beast and its clever undoing belongs to the great migratory stock of [[talking-animals|animal fables]] that spread from India through the [[bidpai]] literature into Europe (P01-007, 030-039); the jackal is the Indian counterpart of the fox in Western beast-epic. Two jackals, "Karataka and Damanaka... who figure in the earlier stories" of the *Panchatantra*, are named in the notes as the ancestors of this type (030-039).

Within the collection, the jackal's ruse answers the tale's central question — whether kindness must be repaid with ingratitude — by proving that although the world may be ungrateful, cleverness can still turn the tables and see justice done; see [[gratitude-and-ingratitude]]. His method, letting a proud enemy defeat itself by its own impatience, is a recurring lesson of the fables: the strong are undone not by greater strength but by their own folly.
