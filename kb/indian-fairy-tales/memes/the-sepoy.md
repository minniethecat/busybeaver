## Overview

The Sepoy appears as a recurring figure of the armed guard in "[[the-boy-who-had-a-moon-on-his-forehead-and-a-star-on-his-chin]]" (Chapter 22). Sepoys — native Indian soldiers — are depicted as the king's enforcement arm, ordered to shoot, guard, and march with the royal procession.

Though the sepoys act collectively rather than as individuals, their presence underscores the military power at the king's disposal and the atmosphere of danger surrounding the tale's young protagonist.

## Role in the Tale

The sepoys are first introduced when the King, suspicious of his daughter's behaviour, sends servants "to bid his troop of sepoys shoot" the mysterious boy at the stables (022-033). This order reveals the dark underside of royal authority.

The following day, "the King placed his sepoys all round the stable," surrounding it to capture the boy (022-034). Yet "the sepoys tried in vain to shoot the horse" carrying the boy, who remained indistinct and unreachable (022-035). Mortal weapons prove powerless against those protected by supernatural forces.

When the prince departs after his marriage, he receives "a great many sepoys to guard them" on the journey (022-076). Here the sepoys shift from antagonists to protectors.

In the tale's closing, the prince summons "all the children, fakirs, and sepoys" — and all "men of all castes, fakirs, and sepoys, in his country" — to gather at his palace (022-077)(022-078). The entire social order, from holy men to soldiers, comes together in celebration.

## Key Appearances

**Ordered to Shoot.** [[king|The King]] commands his sepoys to shoot the mysterious boy at the stables (022-033).

**The Ambush.** Sepoys surround the stable but cannot hit their target, their bullets proving futile (022-034)(022-035).

**Guard Duty.** Sepoys are assigned to escort the prince on his journey home (022-076).

**The Grand Summons.** Sepoys are included among the diverse assembly called to the prince's palace (022-077)(022-078).

## Moral or Cultural Significance

The sepoys represent the dual nature of royal power: they are instruments of both destruction and protection, depending on whose authority they serve.

Their inability to harm the moon-foreheaded boy, despite their numbers and the king's orders, reinforces a central theme: those marked by [[fate-and-prophecy|destiny]] are beyond mortal reach. The inclusion of sepoys in the final assembly alongside fakirs and children reflects the Indian social imagination, where the military holds a recognized place within the broader community.
