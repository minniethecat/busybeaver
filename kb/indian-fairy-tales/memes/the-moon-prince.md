# The Moon Prince

## Overview

The Moon Prince is the prophesied hero of [[the-boy-who-had-a-moon-on-his-forehead-and-a-star-on-his-chin]] (Chapter 22), a child foretold by the gardener's daughter before her marriage to the King: "He will have a moon on his forehead and a star on his chin" (022-002). When the [[fate-and-prophecy|prophecy]] is fulfilled and a beautiful son is born bearing those celestial marks (022-015), the King's four jealous Queens steal the infant, bury him alive, and substitute a stone — but a series of animal guardians preserves his life across five years of concealment.

Raised in succession by the dog Shankar, the cow [[suri-the-cow|Suri]], and the horse [[katar-the-horse|Katar]], the boy grows into a "grand young prince" (022-059) and escapes on [[katar-the-horse|Katar]]'s back to a distant kingdom. There, magically [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]]d as an ugly commoner, he works for a grain merchant and wins the youngest princess by singing songs that reveal his true identity — a classic "hidden prince" folktale pattern culminating in recognition and marriage (022-085, 022-087).

## Role in the Tale

The Moon Prince's [[fate-and-prophecy|destiny]] is spoken before he is conceived: the gardener's daughter, playing under the King's trees, declares she will have a son "with a moon on his forehead and a star on his chin" (022-002). [[king|The King]], childless despite four Queens, marries her for that promise (022-003). When the child is born exactly as foretold, the four Queens conspire — they bury the infant in a hole, place a stone in his cradle, and tell the King his wife has given birth to a lifeless lump (022-020, 022-023).

But the dog Shankar digs up the child "quite alive and well" (022-027), and for six months nurses him. When discovered by the Queens, the dog is shot with an arrow, and the child passes to the cow [[suri-the-cow|Suri]], who swallows and protects him for two years (022-042, 022-045). [[the-queen|The Queen]]s kill [[suri-the-cow|Suri]] in turn, and the child passes to the horse [[katar-the-horse|Katar]], who swallows him for a further two years, keeping him through his fourth and fifth years until [[katar-the-horse|Katar]] too is discovered and runs away with the boy (022-050, 022-052, 022-055).

Arriving in a new kingdom, [[katar-the-horse|Katar]] magically transforms both himself and the prince into donkeys, then into an ugly commoner and an ordinary horse, so that the prince may work incognito for a grain merchant (022-058). The Moon Prince sings nightly of his identity, and the youngest princess, hearing him, falls in love. When the King summons all his subjects in search of the singer, the prince is found, and the King recognizes him — "a grand young prince with a moon on his forehead and a star on his chin" (022-085, 022-087). He marries the princess, and together they return to his father's kingdom to reveal the Queens' crime and reunite the family.

## Key Deeds and Appearances

- **The [[fate-and-prophecy|prophecy]] fulfilled** — born with the promised celestial marks, "the loveliest child" (022-015, 022-020).
- **Buried alive and rescued** — the four Queens bury the infant in a hole and substitute a stone; the dog Shankar digs him up alive (022-023, 022-027).
- **Raised by animal guardians** — survives five years through the succession of Shankar (six months), [[suri-the-cow|Suri]] the cow (two years), and [[katar-the-horse|Katar]] the horse (two years) (022-027, 022-042, 022-050).
- **The escape on [[katar-the-horse|Katar]]** — when the Queens discover [[katar-the-horse|Katar]] hiding the child, the horse runs away with the boy to a distant kingdom (022-055).
- **Magical [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]]** — [[katar-the-horse|Katar]] transforms the prince into an ugly commoner and himself into an ordinary horse, allowing the prince to work incognito (022-058).
- **Singing his identity** — as a grain merchant's servant, the prince sings nightly of his origins; his voice betrays his royal nature to the youngest princess (022-061, 022-063).
- **Recognition and marriage** — the King summons all his subjects, finds the prince, recognizes his celestial marks, and gives him his youngest daughter in marriage (022-085, 022-087).

## Relationships

- **[[the-gardeners-daughter|The Gardener's Daughter]] (his mother)** — the woman who foretold his birth and bore him; she is imprisoned as a madwoman after the Queens' deception, and is only vindicated years later when her son returns (022-002, 022-015, 022-023).
- **[[king|The King]] (his father)** — childless until he marries the gardener's daughter for her [[fate-and-prophecy|prophecy]]; he is deceived by the four Queens and believes his wife gave birth to a stone (022-003, 022-020).
- **The Four Queens** — the jealous co-wives who steal the infant, bury him, kill his animal guardians, and perpetuate the fraud for five years (022-020, 022-023, 022-027).
- **Shankar the Dog** — the first guardian, who digs the child from the earth, nurses him six months, and is shot by the Queens (022-027).
- **[[suri-the-cow]]** — the second guardian, who swallows and protects the child for two years before being killed (022-042, 022-045).
- **[[katar-the-horse]]** — the third guardian and ultimate enabler of escape, who swallows the child for two years and then flees with him to the distant kingdom (022-050, 022-055).
- **[[the-princess|The Youngest Princess]]** — the girl who hears his singing, falls in love, and becomes his wife (022-061, 022-087).

## Moral or Cultural Significance

The Moon Prince's story is a near-perfect example of the "hidden prince" folktale motif (ATU 707, The Three Golden Children). The celestial marks — moon and star — function as both the cause of his persecution (the Queens' jealousy at a child more beautiful than any they could produce) and the proof of his identity at the story's climax. His passage through three animal guardians, each sacrificed in turn, builds a chain of protective love that stands in stark contrast to the Queens' murderous envy. The prince himself is notably passive through most of the tale: he is buried, dug up, swallowed, transformed, and hidden — his agency emerges only at the end, when he chooses to sing and thereby reclaims his identity. It is the faithful guardians, and ultimately his own voice, that restore him to his birthright.
