# The Palace

## Location

The palace is the royal residence that recurs as a setting throughout the collection: the fortified home of the king (raja), queen (rani), and their court, standing at the centre of the kingdom's capital. In the tales it is described as a grand, multi-storeyed structure — a princess sits "on the roof of her palace" (002-042), and the building is sometimes crowned by a "high tower" (004-048). When a king wishes to reward or honour someone, the highest gift is to "build a splendid palace" for them, complete with grounds and gardens (008-060). Beneath the palace may lie hidden chambers: a "dark cellar underground, built with strong stone walls" serves as a prison in [[the-soothsayers-son]] (010-011). The palace is not tied to a single named place but functions as the generic royal seat wherever a raja reigns.

## Appearances in Tales

- [[how-the-rajas-son-won-the-princess-labam]] — [[princess-labam|Princess Labam]] sits on the roof of her palace, which the questing prince must reach (002-042).
- [[punchkin]] — After the enchanter is destroyed, the rescued daughter comes "to live in the palace" (004-010), and the Fakir who arrives at "the palace door" wanders through its many beautiful rooms (004-044, 004-045).
- [[loving-laili]] — Events unfold at [[king-dantal|King Dantal]]'s palace, where the Wazir's son runs "quickly to the palace" (008-027) and [[laili|Laili]] later comes into the palace (008-029); the king finally builds "a splendid palace" as a reward (008-060).
- [[harisarman]] — A jewel is "stolen by a thief from the palace of the king," setting the plot in motion (011-003).
- [[the-charmed-ring]] — The prince "rushed off to the palace" and locked himself in his room; a bee later carries the magic ring back "to the palace" where he lies dying (012-013, 012-015).
- [[a-lac-of-rupees-for-a-bit-of-advice]] — There is "great consternation in the palace" when the queen loses her jewel; the clever prince is later invited to "abide in his palace" and made the king's successor (014-023, 014-032).
- [[the-son-of-seven-queens]] — A false queen lives "luxuriously in a splendid palace, attended by hundreds of female slaves" (016-004).

## Significance

The palace is the narrative anchor of royal power in the tales, and its threshold marks the boundary between the ordinary world and the sphere of kings. It serves several recurring functions. As a prize, it is the reward a hero attains at a tale's happy ending — a splendid palace built for a bride and her son signals the restoration of order (008-060). As a place of confinement and secrecy, its towers and underground cellars hold guarded princesses and prisoners (004-048, 010-011). As a stage for intrigue, it is where thieves strike (011-003), where a queen's jewel goes missing amid great consternation (014-023), and where a false queen usurps the true one's place (016-004). Movement toward the palace — a prince rushing to it, a Fakir arriving at its door — repeatedly signals a turning point in the plot.

## Cultural or Historical Notes

The palace of these tales reflects the courtly world of pre-modern India, where the raja's residence was simultaneously a home, a fortress, a treasury, and the administrative heart of the realm. Many of the collection's palaces belong to the recurring figure of [[king-brahmadatta|King Brahmadatta]] reigning in [[benares]], the setting for several of the Jataka-derived stories (013-001, 017-001). The motif of the guarded princess in a high tower, and of the hidden underground chamber, are stock features of the Indian wonder-tale that also travelled westward into European fairy-tale tradition through the same channels — the *Panchatantra*, the *Ocean of the Streams of Story*, and the *Arabian Nights* — that Joseph Jacobs identifies as the routes by which Indian tales spread.
