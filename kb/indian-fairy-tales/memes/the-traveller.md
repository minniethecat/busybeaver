## Overview

The Traveller appears across the *Indian Fairy Tales* collection as a figure of mobility and [[disguise-and-deception|transformation]]. Unlike the palace-bound king or the village-rooted farmer, the traveller crosses boundaries — geographical, social, and narrative. In "[[the-prince|The Prince]] and the Fakir," the prince adopts the traveller's guise to win his bride; in "Why the Fish Laughed," two travellers arrive at a village where a fateful meal awaits them; in "The [[ivory-city|Ivory City]]," two young travellers face bandits and a mysterious old woman on the road to adventure. The traveller is both a narrative device for moving between settings and a symbol of the human journey toward wisdom.

## Role in the Tales

### The Prince and the Fakir (Chapter 23) — The Traveller Prince

The prince arrives at the assembly of kings "dressed in the habit of a traveller" (023-031), sitting among the gathered rulers as an unknown stranger. [[the-princess|The Princess]] notices "that the traveller Prince was sitting in the assembly" (023-032) and sends her handmaiden to sprinkle henna on him — marking him as her chosen husband among all the assembled royalty. The traveller Prince later commands: "Let all the Kings be ready with their armies" (023-039), revealing that beneath the traveller's cloak lies royal authority.

### Why the Fish Laughed (Chapter 24) — Two Travellers

"After a little while the two travellers arrived at a big village" (024-018), where [[the-village|the villagers]] beckon to them and "gave them as much as they could eat of the most delicious Pulaw" (024-022). This seemingly generous act conceals a sinister purpose — [[the-village|the villagers]] are fattening strangers to eat them, a practice the travellers narrowly escape. The episode exemplifies how the traveller's vulnerability to the unknown is both their danger and their opportunity for discovery.

### The Ivory City (Chapter 26) — Travellers on the Road

The prince and the vizier's son travel through dangerous country, facing "the people of the country, robbing those travellers who could not resist them" (026-031). Trapped in a hut, the "two travellers bemoaned their fate" (026-036) before the vizier's son climbs to the window and discovers their destination — the [[ivory-city|Ivory City]]. Later, the old woman describes them: "two young travellers, a prince and the son of some great vizier, have taken up their abode in my poor hut" (026-062).

The traveller's state is captured in the scene where the two travellers are trapped:

> "The two travellers bemoaned their fate" (026-036). But the vizier's son said, "I will climb to the window and see what is outside" (026-037).

This movement from despair to action is characteristic of the traveller figure — vulnerability gives way to resourcefulness.

## The Traveller in Indian Narrative Tradition

The collection's preface acknowledges the traveller as the vehicle through which stories themselves spread: fables traveled "by Buddhists, by Jews, by traders, by travellers" (P01-002). The traveller is thus both a character within the tales and the historical agent who carried Indian stories westward. The image of the traveller at the fire in "[[rasalu|Raja Rasalu]]" — "Oh, traveller! for [[khuda|God]]'s sake save me from this fire!" (019-056) — evokes the ancient tradition of travellers sharing stories around roadside fires.

## Relationships

- **[[the-princess|The Princess]]** — in "[[the-prince|The Prince]] and the Fakir," the Princess recognizes the traveller's true identity when others see only a stranger
- **[[the-village|The Village]]rs** — the traveller's relationship with settled communities is ambivalent: hospitality may conceal danger, as in the village that fattens travellers (024-018)
- **Fellow Travellers** — the traveller rarely journeys alone; the bond between travelling companions (the prince and the vizier's son, the two travellers facing the cannibal village) is a recurring theme

## Moral and Cultural Significance

The traveller embodies the Indian folkloric principle that wisdom comes through journey. The prince who travels in [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]] learns what the palace-bound king never can; the travellers who encounter danger develop resourcefulness that the settled villager never needs. This reflects the broader Indian cultural value placed on *yatra* (pilgrimage/journey) as a form of spiritual education. The traveller is the figure through whom the static world of kings and villages is connected to the wider world of adventure, danger, and [[disguise-and-deception|transformation]].
