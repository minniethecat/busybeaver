## Overview

The Brahmani is a pivotal though briefly appearing character in "A Lac of Rupees for a Bit of Advice" (Chapter 14). She is the wife of a blind Brahman who leaves home to seek his fortune. When she follows her husband to a neighbouring kingdom, a series of events leads her to receive a paper containing three cryptic pieces of advice — which she then sells to a desperate prince for a lac of rupees. Her transaction sets the entire resolution of the tale in motion: without her intervention, the prince would have perished in any of the three deadly trials that follow. She is the story's hidden mechanism — a figure whose single commercial act saves a royal life.

## Role in the Tale

### The Search

Two months after her husband departs to seek his fortune, "the Brahman's wife, not having heard any news of her husband" (014-003), resolves to find him. Unlike her passive husband — who is blind and dependent on the charity of the potter whose home he stumbles into — the Brahmani takes active steps. She journeys to the neighbouring kingdom where he has settled.

### The Paper of Advice

When she arrives, she discovers that her husband has been living with a potter. In a twist of miscommunication or misdirected generosity, the potter gives the Brahmani a paper upon which three pieces of advice are written — advice originally intended for her husband but now in her hands.

### The Transaction

The Brahmani's defining moment comes when she encounters the prince of that kingdom. Recognising the value of what she carries — or simply seizing an opportunity — she sells the paper to the prince:

> "The first piece of advice that the Brahmani gave me. I will not sleep to-night." (014-011)

The prince pays "a lac of rupees" — an enormous sum — for three pieces of advice that seem almost absurdly simple: do not sleep, do not drink, do not enter. Yet each proves to be the precise survival instruction needed for a mortal trial the prince immediately faces.

### Aftermath

When the prince survives all three trials, the king demands to know how. The prince explains that "he had purchased from the Brahmani" (014-035) the advice that saved him. The king, recognising the wisdom behind the transaction, expresses gratitude — and the Brahmani's role as the tale's quiet saviour is confirmed.

## Key Deeds

- **Active Agency**: Unlike her blind husband who waits passively, the Brahmani takes the initiative to find him — a rare example of female agency in the Jacobs collection (014-003).
- **The Paper's Reception**: Receives the three pieces of advice from the potter, becoming their custodian — whether by accident or design, the tale leaves ambiguous (014-008).
- **The Lac of Rupees**: Sells the paper of advice to the prince for a lac of rupees, a transaction that proves to be the tale's turning point (014-011).
- **Indirect Heroism**: Without ever knowing it, her commercial act saves the prince from death by snake, poison, and a falling millstone (014-011, 014-035).

## Relationships

- **The Blind Brahman** — Her husband, whose departure triggers her journey. The Brahmani's active search contrasts sharply with his passive acceptance of fate (014-003).
- **[[the-potter|The Potter]]** — The intermediary who gives her the paper of advice, though the advice was likely meant for her husband. His role is a classic folk-tale misdirection (014-008).
- **[[the-prince|The Prince]]** — The buyer of the advice. He is the visible hero of the tale, surviving the trials; but the Brahmani is the invisible one, providing the means of survival (014-011).
- **[[king|The King]]** — The prince's father, who ultimately acknowledges the value of the advice his son purchased from the Brahmani (014-035).

## Moral and Cultural Significance

The Brahmani represents a fascinating structural role in Indian folk narrative: the **unwitting benefactor**. She does not intend to save a prince's life; she intends to sell a piece of paper for money. Yet her commercial instinct — the recognition that information has value — proves to be the mechanism of salvation. The tale thus embeds a subtle argument about the value of knowledge and the dignity of commerce, themes that resonate within the broader Indian narrative tradition where merchants and traders frequently appear as agents of fortune.

Her character also subverts the expected gender dynamic. In a collection where women are often prizes to be won or passive sufferers of fate, the Brahmani acts. She travels alone, negotiates independently, and profits from her transaction. The tale does not moralise about her commercial acumen — it rewards it, making her the silent architect of a prince's survival and a kingdom's gratitude.
