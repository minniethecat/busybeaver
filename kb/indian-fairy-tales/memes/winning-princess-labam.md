# The Winning of Princess Labam

## Summary

This event traces the quest of a [[king|Raja]]'s son who, forbidden to hunt in one particular jungle, disobeys and there overhears a parrot named [[hiraman|Hiraman]] speak of the beautiful [[princess-labam]]. Seized by the resolve to marry a woman he has never seen, the prince crosses vast distances with the aid of magic gifts and finally wins her hand by surviving a series of lethal trials set by her father.

> "Who is the Princess Labam? Where does she live?" (002-005)

## Context

The prince's father owns a jungle he has forbidden his son ever to enter (002-002). When the young man at last disobeys and hunts there, he finds it inhabited only by parrots, whose [[king|Raja]] is called [[hiraman|Hiraman]] (002-003). Overhearing the parrot's talk of the [[princess-labam|Princess Labam]], the prince becomes fixed on the idea of finding her, declaring "I must go; I must go and see the [[princess-labam|Princess Labam]]" (002-007), though he does not even know where her country lies.

> When Hiraman parrot found himself left alone, he… (002-004)

The distance is so great that ordinary travel is impossible. The prince is aided by a series of [[magical-objects|enchanted objects]], chief among them a magic bed that "carried him to the room where she lay" (002-044) and later "carried him to the princess" (002-051), allowing him to reach and secretly court [[princess-labam|Labam]] where she sits shining upon her palace roof (002-041).

## Participants

- [[princess-labam]] — the hidden princess whose beauty, reported by the parrot, sets the whole quest in motion and who ultimately consents to the marriage.

## Outcome or Consequence

Once the prince declares his wish, [[princess-labam|Labam]]'s father imposes conditions before any suitor may marry her: "if this [[king|Raja]]'s son wishes to marry my daughter, he must first do…" (002-061). The final and deadliest trial requires the prince to fight the king's two [[supernatural-beings|demons]]: "you must first fight with my two [[supernatural-beings|demons]] and kill them" (002-067). The prince, sorrowful at the prospect (002-068), turns to a tiger he had earlier befriended, asking "May these tigers fight your [[supernatural-beings|demons]] for me?" (002-069) — and the [[supernatural-beings|demons]] are slain in his stead.

With every task fulfilled, the king relents: "Now you can marry my daughter," and the wedding is held (002-078). Afterward the prince proposes to take his bride home: "Let us go to my father's country" (002-078).

## Cultural or Moral Significance

The winning of [[princess-labam|Princess Labam]] is a textbook example of the folk-tale "impossible-tasks" pattern, in which a hero must accomplish a graded sequence of trials to earn a bride. Jacobs's notes observe that "the tasks are also equally common" across the tradition (030-028) and that certain motifs given "in '[[princess-labam|Princess Labam]]' are not known in Europe" (030-028), marking the tale as distinctively Indian. The prince's success rests less on his own strength than on gratitude repaid: the tiger he had befriended fights the [[supernatural-beings|demons]] for him, so that the marriage is won through alliance and kindness rather than raw heroism — a recurring moral thread in the collection.
