# Ujjain

## Location

Ujjain — spelled *Ujjaini* in the tale — is an ancient city of central India that appears as a distinct kingdom with its own ruler in [[the-soothsayers-son]]. Its position is given precisely relative to the road to [[benares|Benares]]: a house stands "in the East main street of Ujjaini, which is twenty kas to the south" of a wayside point on that road (010-007). The city has "a king of Ujjaini" of its own (010-009), and it lies close enough to the hero's route that travellers pass through it on journeys to and from [[benares|Benares]] (010-008). The city corresponds to the modern city of Ujjain in Madhya Pradesh.

## Appearances in Tales

- [[the-soothsayers-son]] — Ujjaini is where the hero [[soothsayers-son|Gangazara]] is directed to go, its location fixed as "twenty kas to the south" of the [[benares|Benares]] road, with a specific house on "the East main street" (010-007). A companion resolves, "I will go now to Ujjaini on my way" (010-008). The city's ruler, "the king of Ujjaini," had "a week before gone" on an expedition (010-009), and the king later addresses the sufferings of "these poor subjects of Ujjaini" (010-021). At the tale's close the two heroes "both went to Ujjaini," where [[soothsayers-son|Gangazara]] marries (010-027).

## Significance

Ujjain is the setting for the resolution of [[the-soothsayers-son]]. The tale treats it as a real, navigable place, giving its distance and direction from the [[benares|Benares]] highway and even naming a particular street and house within it (010-007). This precise geography contrasts with the collection's more purely mythical locales and reflects the tale's origin outside the Jataka cycle. Ujjaini's king and its people become central to the plot's climax: the king intervenes on behalf of "these poor subjects of Ujjaini" (010-021), and the city is where [[soothsayers-son|Gangazara]] at last settles and marries, bringing his wandering adventures to an end (010-027).

## Cultural or Historical Notes

Ujjain is one of the most storied cities of ancient India, a great center of learning, astronomy, and trade on the Malwa plateau in what is now Madhya Pradesh. In classical Sanskrit literature it was the celebrated capital of King Vikramaditya and the seat of the poet Kalidasa, and it long served as the reference meridian for Hindu astronomers. It is also one of the seven sacred cities of Hinduism and a site of the Kumbh Mela. The tale's use of a *kas* (a traditional South Asian unit of distance) to fix Ujjaini's position, and its naming of a real and famous city, ground the story in recognizable central-Indian geography.
