# The Golden Serpent's Broken Bond

## Summary

In the fifteenth tale, a poor Brahman farmer wins the daily favour of a hooded serpent he takes to be the guardian deity of his field: each morning, after he leaves an offering of milk at the ant-hill, he finds "a gold denar in the bowl, and from that time onward every day the same thing occurred" (015-002). When the Brahman must travel, he sends his son with the milk. The son, seeing a single denar again, reasons "This ant-hill is surely full of golden denars; I'll kill the serpent, and take them all for myself" (015-003). Greed drives him to strike the serpent on the head with a cudgel; but the blow fails, and the serpent "in a rage bit the Brahman's son with its sharp fangs, and he fell down dead at once" (015-003).

> "This ant-hill is surely full of golden denars; I'll kill the serpent, and take them all for myself." (015-003)

## Context

[[the-brahman|The Brahman]] [[the-farmer|Haridatta]], whose "labour brought" him little (015-001), one day sees "a great hooded snake creeping out of an ant-hill" and concludes it is the neglected guardian deity of his field, whose worship will end his poverty (015-001). He offers milk with an apology — "all this while I did not know that you dwelt here" — and is rewarded the next morning with gold, establishing a steady, reverent exchange between man and serpent (015-002). It is into this bond of daily piety and reward that the son intrudes, substituting for his father's humility a calculation of how to seize the whole hoard at once. The serpent's power to give is thus tested against human greed at the very moment the ritual is entrusted to the next generation (015-003).

## Participants

- [[the-golden-serpent]] — the hooded guardian of the ant-hill who rewards daily milk with a gold denar, kills the son who tries to slay it, and then declares the friendship at an end (015-002, 015-003, 015-004).
- **[[the-brahman|The Brahman]]'s son** — the young man who, coveting the whole treasure, strikes the serpent with a cudgel and is bitten to death for his greed (015-003).
- **[[the-farmer|Haridatta]] the Brahman** — the pious farmer who began the exchange and who, returning to mourn his son, still comes back to praise the serpent, only to be told the bond is broken (015-001, 015-004).

## Outcome or Consequence

The son's people burn his body "to ashes" on a funeral pyre near the field (015-003). Two days later the grieving Brahman returns, takes the bowl of milk, and praises the serpent as before; but the serpent now shows only its head from the ant-hill and pronounces the end of the relationship: "From this time forward friendship between us is impossible... Your son struck me in youthful ignorance, and I have bitten him to death. How can I forget the blow with the cudgel? And how can you forget the pain and grief at the loss of your son?" (015-004). Before vanishing, it gives the Brahman "a costly pearl" and commands, "Come back no more"; the Brahman departs, "cursing the folly of his son" (015-004). The daily stream of gold is ended forever by a single act of greed.

## Cultural or Moral Significance

The event is a compact fable on the self-destroying nature of greed. The serpent's steady gift of one denar a day is more than the son can bear; his wish to possess "all" at once costs him both the treasure and his life, illustrating the folk maxim that grasping for the whole destroys the part one already enjoys. The serpent's parting speech makes the moral explicit and mutual: a bond of trust, once broken by violence and grief on both sides, cannot be restored by renewed offerings — remembered injury outweighs remembered kindness. The tale also preserves the ancient Indian motif of the serpent as guardian of hidden wealth, whose favour depends on reverence rather than exploitation, so that the story stands as a warning against betraying a benefactor out of [[greed-in-folklore|covetousness]].
