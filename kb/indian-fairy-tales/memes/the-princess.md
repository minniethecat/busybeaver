## Overview

The Princess appears as a female counterpart to the Prince across at least seven tales in the *Indian Fairy Tales* collection. Her roles span the full spectrum of agency: from the passive but divinely destined [[laili|Laili]], to the secretly resourceful [[princess-labam|Princess Labam]] who coaches her suitor through deadly trials (002-075), to the independent youngest princess who chooses her own husband against her father's expectations (022-071). She is at once the object of the hero's quest and, in the most memorable tales, an active agent who shapes her own fate.

## Role in the Tales

### Princess Labam (Chapter 2)

[[princess-labam|Princess Labam]] is the distant beloved whom the [[king|Raja]]'s son seeks across kingdoms. When the king sets three [[marriage-and-courtship|impossible tasks]] for her suitor — crushing oil from mustard seed, felling a giant tree with a wax hatchet, and identifying her among identical attendants — it is the princess herself who secretly ensures his success. She tells him: "Do not be afraid; do as I bid you, and you will be safe" (002-075). She gives him a strand of her hair, and when all the attendants appear identical, "the princess put on her lovely clothes and her jewels" and shook her head so that the prince could identify her (002-056). She is the hidden architect of her own marriage, acting through the prince rather than openly defying her father.

### Laili (Chapter 8)

[[laili|Laili]] is "very beautiful; she had brown eyes and black hair" (008-003). Her love for [[majnun|Prince Majnun]] is divinely ordained: an angel appears to her in sleep declaring that [[khuda|Khuda]] has commanded her to marry [[majnun|Majnun]] and no one else (008-004). From that moment she repeats nothing but "[[majnun|Majnun]], [[majnun|Majnun]]; I want [[majnun|Majnun]]" (008-004), to her father's vexation. Her obsessive devotion borders on madness — "she was half mad" (008-005) — yet it is precisely this unwavering [[faithfulness-and-loyalty|fidelity]], sanctioned by divine will, that drives the tale forward. She embodies the theme of destined love that transcends kingdoms and human opposition.

### The Princess of the Soothsayer's Son (Chapter 10)

A princess who falls victim to a serpent that enters her bed-chamber and "sucked her life" (010-015). Rescued and restored to life by [[soothsayers-son|Gangazara]], she becomes his bride — her restoration marking the hero's transition from wandering soothsayer's son to royal successor (010-027).

### The Beautiful Princess of the Charmed Ring (Chapter 12)

A princess with golden hair who appears magically when a charmed ring is turned (012-013). She is imprisoned by an [[supernatural-beings|ogres]]s who tricks her into removing her protective charm (012-015), and must be rescued through the ring's power. Her story exemplifies the magical bride motif — a supernatural being bound to a mortal through an enchanted object.

### The Henna Princess (Chapter 23)

A king's daughter who sits on the palace roof and sees the traveller Prince's "royal marks" (023-028). She declares to her father: "When I marry, I will marry him and no other" (023-028). In the assembly of all the land's nobles, she sends her handmaiden to sprinkle scent on the [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]]d Prince in Fakir's clothes (023-033). When the crowd protests that the slave-girl has made a mistake, the handmaiden replies: "The slave-girl has made no mistake, 'tis her mistress has made the mistake" (023-036). The princess's act of choosing — against all social expectation — is the decisive moment of the tale.

### The Youngest Princess (Chapter 22)

The youngest of seven sisters, she alone visits the moon-forehead boy in the garden each night to ask why he weeps (022-043). When her father arranges a husband-choosing ceremony, she rides a grand elephant past all the assembled nobles and chooses the disfigured boy (022-049). She defends her choice: "My husband is not a handsome man, but he has a moon on his forehead and a star on his chin" (022-071). Her loyalty to inner worth over outer appearance is the tale's central moral moment.

### Princess Gulizar of the Ivory City (Chapter 26)

A fairy princess — "[[gulizar|Gulizar of the Ivory City]]" (026-029) — who is revealed to the exiled prince as a reflection in a lotus-covered lake (026-019). After their secret betrothal (026-077), she attempts to poison the vizier's son out of possessive jealousy (026-079). The vizier's son detects the poison and engineers an elaborate counter-scheme, branding her back with a hot shovel and accusing her of being a ghoul (026-102). Her eventual repentance — "she wept, and acknowledged that he was her greatest helper and friend" (026-109) — completes her arc from jealous fairy to grateful bride.

## Relationships

- **[[the-prince|The Prince]]** — her counterpart and husband across multiple tales; in Chapter 2 she secretly aids him; in Chapter 23 she chooses him from the crowd; in Chapter 26 [[gulizar|Gulizar]] is the object of his quest and eventually his bride
- **[[king|The King]] (Father)** — her father, who typically arranges the husband-choosing ceremony and whose authority she either works around (Chapter 2) or openly defies (Chapter 23)
- **The [[the-maid|Handmaiden]] / Servant** — her intermediary; in Chapter 23 the handmaiden carries the henna dish to the [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]]d prince and speaks the famous line about the mistress's choice (023-034)
- **[[the-viziers-son|The Vizier's Son]]** — antagonist-turned-ally in Chapter 26; [[gulizar|Gulizar]] attempts to poison him (026-079), but later weeps in repentance and acknowledges him as "her greatest helper and friend" (026-109)
- **[[the-fakir|The Fakir]] / Ogress / Serpent** — the supernatural threats from which she must be rescued or against which she must protect herself

## Moral and Cultural Significance

The Princess in *Indian Fairy Tales* is far more than a passive prize. Across the tales, Jacobs's selection reveals a spectrum of female agency that complicates the simple damsel-in-distress narrative. [[princess-labam|Princess Labam]] coaches her suitor in secret — she cannot openly defy her father, but she can manipulate the trial from within. The Henna Princess openly chooses her husband in public assembly, risking social humiliation when she picks a man in Fakir's rags. The youngest princess of Chapter 22 articulates the collection's most explicit moral statement about judging by inner worth rather than appearance.

At the same time, the tales do not romanticise this agency unconditionally. [[gulizar|Gulizar]]'s jealousy leads her to attempted murder (026-079); [[laili|Laili]]'s divine obsession borders on madness (008-005). The Princess figure thus embodies both the virtues and the dangers of feminine will within the patriarchal structures of Indian courtly life — resourceful within constraints, dangerous when those constraints are removed.
