The **Bonga Girl** is the protagonist of "[[the-magic-fiddle|The Magic Fiddle]]" (Chapter 6 of Joseph Jacobs's *Indian Fairy Tales*, 1892), a Santal folk tale of [[disguise-and-deception|transformation]], music, and quiet revenge. She begins as the unmarried sister of seven brothers, cooking for the household while her sisters-in-law resent her. Through a curse directed at a Bonga — a presiding spirit of [[the-jungle|the wild]] — she is drowned, transformed, and carried off into the spirit world. She then undergoes two further metamorphoses: first into a bamboo stalk, and then into a fiddle whose music wails with her sorrow, before the discovery by a young chief restores her to human society (006-001–006-023).

The Bonga Girl's story is unusual among the collection's female characters. She is neither a passive princess awaiting rescue nor a dangerous enchantress — she is a victim of domestic cruelty whose supernatural [[disguise-and-deception|transformation]] paradoxically becomes the vehicle for her liberation and eventual marriage. Her tale is one of the few in the *Indian Fairy Tales* that centres a female perspective from beginning to end.

## Overview

The Bonga Girl is introduced as the sister of seven brothers, living in a household where she performs all the cooking while her brothers work the fields and their wives openly despise her (006-001). The wives, conspiring to oust her from her domestic role, call upon a Bonga — a spirit of the Santal world — and offer the maiden in exchange for supernatural assistance:

> "At midday when our sister-in-law goes to bring water, cause it thus to happen, that on seeing her pitcher the water shall vanish, and again slowly re-appear. In this way she will be delayed. Let the water not flow into her pitcher, and you may keep the maiden as your own." (006-001)

At the tank, the water rises in stages — ankles, knees, waist, neck — as she cries out to her absent brother in a rhythmic, song-like lament structured as a folk ballad (006-003–006-013). When the water reaches a man's height, the pitcher finally fills — but it pulls her down with it. She drowns, and the Bonga transforms her into one of his own kind (006-017).

Her death is not the end. She reappears as a bamboo stalk on the embankment of the tank where she drowned (006-018). A wandering Jogi cuts the bamboo despite its protests and fashions it into a fiddle of extraordinary beauty and sorrowful tone — an instrument that seems to "wail as one in bitter anguish" (006-019). This fiddle is later stolen by a village chief's household, and the Bonga Girl emerges from it daily to cook the family's meal (006-021). Hidden in a woodpile, the chief's son watches the [[disguise-and-deception|transformation]] and catches her — restoring her to visible, human society through marriage (006-021).

Her story concludes with quiet dignity rather than violent revenge. When her impoverished brothers visit the chief's household years later, she serves them water and rice, then upbraids them in "wailing tones" for their failure to protect her — and that, the narrator notes, "was all the revenge she took" (006-023).

## Role in the Tale

The Bonga Girl occupies three distinct roles in sequence, each corresponding to a [[disguise-and-deception|transformation]]:

**The Domestic Victim.** As the unmarried sister in a joint-family household, she is hardworking but vulnerable — her sisters-in-law resent her and conspire against her, while her brothers are absent or indifferent. The Bonga curse is merely the supernatural instrument; the real violence originates in domestic jealousy (006-001).

**The Imprisoned Spirit.** In the Bonga realm, she is neither fully dead nor fully alive. Her second [[disguise-and-deception|transformation]] — into bamboo — is an accident of location (she grows where she drowned), but it traps her consciousness in vegetative form: the bamboo can speak, protest, and bargain with the Jogi's axe (006-018).

**The Spirit Bride.** Her third [[disguise-and-deception|transformation]] — into the fiddle — is the most profound. She lives inside the instrument, emerging only when the household is empty to perform domestic labour. Her eventual discovery by the chief's son, who catches her mid-[[disguise-and-deception|transformation]] and declares her his wife, restores her to human visibility: "from to-day, you and I are one" (006-021). The marriage is simultaneously a rescue and an integration — the household rejoices that "she was both a human being and a Bonga" (006-021), accepting her dual nature.

## Relationships

- **The Seven Brothers** — Her brothers are background figures, working in the fields, blind to the household cruelty. Their wives act while they are absent; the girl's drowning lament is addressed to "my brother" — a cry that goes unheard. When she upbraids them years later, they do not even recognise her (006-023).
- **The Seven Sisters-in-Law** — The antagonists. Their resentment is domestic and economic: they resent the sister's indoor role because it grants her authority over food. They enlist the Bonga to destroy her, trading her body for supernatural favour (006-001).
- **The Bonga** — The spirit who drowns and transforms her. The Bonga is both executioner and creator — he kills the girl and simultaneously remakes her. Jacobs's Notes define a Bonga as "the presiding spirit of a certain natural feature" in Santal belief (030-041).
- **[[the-jogi|The Jogi]]** — The wandering ascetic who cuts the bamboo and makes the fiddle. He is unwitting — he does not know the spirit within — but his craft gives the Bonga Girl a new body of wood and music. He later loses the fiddle through theft while intoxicated (006-020).
- **[[the-chiefs-son|The Chief's Son]]** — Her rescuer and husband. Unlike the Jogi, who used the fiddle for profit, the chief's son watches, discovers her secret, and catches her. His marriage to her restores her to human visibility — but notably, she remains "both a human being and a Bonga" (006-021).

## Cultural Significance

The Bonga Girl's story embodies several motifs central to Indian folklore:

**The Transformed Bride.** Like the Swan Maiden of European folklore, the Bonga Girl is a supernatural woman captured by a mortal man who observes her secret [[disguise-and-deception|transformation]]. Unlike the Swan Maiden, however, her spirit nature is not a birthright — it is an acquired condition, imposed by cruelty rather than descent.

**The Voice of the Instrument.** The fiddle that "wailed as one in bitter anguish" (006-019) is a powerful instance of the widespread motif in which a musical instrument made from a murdered person retains their voice and tells their story. The bamboo's protests — "Do not cut at the root, cut higher up" — are the last articulate speech of the girl before she is silenced into wood, and then into music.

**Quiet Revenge.** The tale's ending is striking for its restraint. Reunited with the brothers who failed her, the Bonga Girl does not curse them, expose them, or punish them. She serves them food, tells them what happened, and leaves it at that: "And that was all the revenge she took" (006-023). In a collection filled with violent retribution (the Demon boiled in oil, [[punchkin-magician|Punchkin]]'s parrot destroyed, the Fakir thrown into his own cauldron), this quiet confrontation is a distinctive moral register.

**Santal Cosmology.** The Bonga spirit — defined by Jacobs as a "presiding spirit" of natural features (030-041) — anchors the tale in the Santal (Santhal) tribal world of eastern India. The Bonga is not a demon or an abstraction but a specific kind of spirit-being embedded in the landscape: the tank, the bamboo grove, the household. The tale's supernatural logic — drowning → [[disguise-and-deception|transformation]] → bamboo → fiddle → emergence — is a chain of Santal beliefs about spirit metamorphosis, not a generic fairy-tale enchantment.
