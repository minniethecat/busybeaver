## Overview

The Snake is a pivotal character in "The Charmed Ring" (Chapter 12). Found by the merchant's son among villagers quarrelling over whether to kill it, the Snake is purchased for one hundred rupees and spared (012-001). The Snake is revealed to be no ordinary reptile but the son of [[king|Raja]] Indrasha, a serpent king who dwells in an underground kingdom accessible through a sacred spring (012-005). In gratitude for being saved, the Snake guides the merchant's son to his father's realm and advises him on what reward to ask for — a charmed ring, a pot, and a spoon — whose magical powers drive the remainder of the tale (012-007).

## Role in the Tale

### The Rescue

The Snake is the third and last animal the merchant's son saves. After purchasing the Dog and the Cat, he reaches a village where "some folk were quarrelling over a snake that had just been caught" (012-001). Despite having already spent two hundred rupees, he gives his last hundred to save it, and the people are "highly delighted" with the bargain (012-001).

### Life in the Stables

When the merchant's son is banished to the stables by his angry father, the three animals remain loyal. The Snake sleeps draped over the young man's body:

> the cat used to sleep at his feet, the dog at his head, and the snake over his body, with its head hanging on one side and its tail on the other. (012-004)

### The Revelation

The Snake soon reveals its true identity, becoming the tale's turning point:

> "I am the son of Raja Indrasha. One day, when I had come out of the ground to drink the air, some people seized me, and would have slain me had you not most opportunely arrived to my rescue." (012-005)

The Snake offers to take the young man to meet its father, directing him to a sacred spring at the base of a mountain. Diving into the spring together, they enter the serpent king's underground domain (012-007–012-008).

### The Gift of Treasures

The Snake's crucial contribution is advising the merchant's son on what reward to request:

> "If he asks you what you would like, you would, perhaps, do well to reply, 'The ring on your right hand, and the famous pot and spoon which you possess.' With these in your possession, you would never need anything, for the ring is such that a man has only to speak to it, and immediately a beautiful furnished mansion will be provided for him, while the pot and the spoon will supply him with all manner of the rarest and most delicious foods." (012-007)

The Snake also insists on proper protocol, refusing to appear before its father until formally released from its debt to the stranger, saying "it could not go to its father till it was released from this stranger, who had saved it from a most terrible death, and whose slave it therefore was" (012-012).

## Key Deeds

- **Revelation of Identity**: The Snake discloses its royal nature, transforming the tale from a story of foolish spending into a magical adventure (012-005).
- **Guidance to the Underworld**: Leads the merchant's son to the sacred spring and into [[king|Raja]] Indrasha's kingdom (012-007–012-008).
- **Strategic Counsel**: Advises exactly which magical items to request, ensuring the hero's future prosperity (012-007).
- **Graceful Protocol**: By insisting on proper recognition of the hero's role as its savior, the Snake ensures that [[king|Raja]] Indrasha receives the young man with full honors (012-012).

## Relationships

- **[[the-merchant|The Merchant]]'s Son** — The Snake's savior and master, to whom it owes a life-debt. The Snake's gratitude drives the entire magical portion of the tale (012-001, 012-005).
- **[[king|Raja]] Indrasha** — The Snake's father, the serpent king who dwells beneath a mountain and possesses magical treasures (012-005, 012-012).
- **[[the-cat|The Cat]]** (the-cat) — Fellow animal companion; the Snake, Cat, and Dog form a trio of rescued creatures who each contribute to the hero's journey (012-004).
- **[[the-dog|The Dog]]** (the-dog) — Fellow animal companion; the three animals live together in the stables and later reunite at the spring (012-004, 012-012).

## Cultural Significance

The Snake embodies the grateful-animal motif found throughout Indian folklore and the broader Indo-European tradition. Unlike Western tales where serpents are often antagonists, this Snake is a noble being of royal lineage whose gratitude brings fortune to the hero. The underground kingdom of [[king|Raja]] Indrasha connects to the widespread mythological theme of serpent-kings (Nāga-rājas) dwelling in subterranean realms, a concept prominent in Hindu and Buddhist cosmology. The Snake's insistence on proper etiquette — refusing to face his father while still in debt — also reflects the tale's emphasis on dharma and the binding nature of gratitude.
