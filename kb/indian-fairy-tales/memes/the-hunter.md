## Overview

The Hunter is a minor but pivotal character in "[[soothsayers-son|The Soothsayer's Son]]" (Chapter 10). He is one of the king's huntsmen who, during a conversation with the young soothsayer's son, reveals the long-concealed truth that the boy's father was killed — a moment that sets the story's central revenge plot in motion. Though unnamed and appearing only briefly, the Hunter serves as the narrative catalyst who transforms the protagonist from an aimless wanderer into a purposeful avenger.

## Role in the Tale

### The Fatal Revelation

The soothsayer's son, having been driven from his home by his mother's harsh words, wanders until he encounters the king's hunting party. The king himself had gone "with all his hunters on a hunting expedition" a week earlier (010-009), and the young man joins the remaining hunters in their camp. During his time among them, a conversation about honour and lineage arises, and the Hunter delivers the truth that changes everything:

The Hunter tells the soothsayer's son that his father did not die of natural causes — he was murdered by the king himself, struck down in a moment of royal anger. When the young man presses for details, the hunters confirm what his mother had never told him:

> "All the hunters told him that his father was killed by the king." (010-021)

Crucially, the Hunter clarifies that the killing was not by "any hunter's hands" (010-010) — it was the king's own act, absolving the hunting fraternity of blame while simultaneously directing the young man's wrath toward the throne.

### Narrative Function

The Hunter's role is structurally essential: he is the **truth-bearer** who bridges the gap between the protagonist's ignorance and his [[fate-and-prophecy|destiny]]. Without this revelation, the soothsayer's son would have no reason to confront the king, and the tale's central conflict — the revenge of a son for his murdered father — would never ignite.

## Key Deeds

- **Truth-Telling**: Reveals to the soothsayer's son the circumstances of his father's death, information that the boy's mother had deliberately withheld (010-021).
- **Exoneration of Fellow Hunters**: Makes clear that the king, not the hunters, committed the killing — a detail that preserves the honour of the hunting company while directing the young man's vengeance toward its proper target (010-010).

## Relationships

- **[[soothsayers-son|The Soothsayer's Son]]** — The protagonist to whom the Hunter delivers the pivotal revelation. Their brief interaction transforms the young man's life trajectory from passive wandering to active pursuit of justice (010-021).
- **[[king|The King]]** — The Hunter's master and, unbeknownst to the young man until the revelation, his father's murderer. The Hunter occupies the delicate position of serving a king whose crime he quietly knows (010-009, 010-010).

## Moral and Cultural Significance

The Hunter embodies the folk-tale archetype of the **unwitting messenger** — an ordinary person whose casual words unleash extraordinary consequences. He does not actively scheme or seek to overthrow the king; he simply answers a young man's question honestly. His presence underscores a recurring theme in Indian folk narrative: truth, once spoken, cannot be recalled, and even the most humble speaker may become the instrument of fate.

Unlike more developed informant characters in the corpus, the Hunter has no personal stake in the revenge he enables. He neither urges the young man toward vengeance nor warns him away from it — he merely states what he knows. This narrative economy is characteristic of minor characters in the Jacobs collection, where even a few lines of reported speech can carry the entire weight of a plot turn.
