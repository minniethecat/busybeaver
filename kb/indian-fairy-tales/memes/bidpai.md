The Fables of Bidpai represent one of the most consequential story collections in world literature — an ancient Indian compendium of animal fables that, through successive translations, carried Indian narrative material from Sanskrit into Middle Persian, Arabic, and eventually nearly every European vernacular. The collection is a primary source for many of the tales in this volume, second only to the Jatakas as a reservoir of Indian folk narrative.

## Definition

The Fables of Bidpai (also known as the *Fables of Pilpay*, or *Kalila and Dimna* in its Arabic form) originated as the Sanskrit *Panchatantra*, a collection of animal fables framed within a larger story of a king seeking to educate his foolish sons. The work was translated into Pahlavi (Middle Persian) in the sixth century CE, then into Arabic by Ibn al-Muqaffaʿ around 750 CE under the title *Kalila wa Dimna*, named after the two jackals who serve as the frame story's narrators. From Arabic, the collection radiated outward — into Syriac, Hebrew, Greek, Latin, Spanish, and eventually most European languages (030-006, 030-039). The name "Bidpai" (or "Pilpay") derives from the Sanskrit *Vidyapati*, the wise Brahman philosopher who narrates the fables in the frame story.

## Examples in the Tales

The Bidpai corpus does not appear directly as a tale in this volume, but its influence permeates the collection:

- **The Broken Pot** (Tale 5, 005-001): This cautionary fable of the "born miser" who builds castles in the air — imagining the wealth from selling rice, the cattle purchased, the wife and children produced — only to shatter his pot and his dreams — appears in the Bidpai literature. The story illustrates the folly of counting future gains before they materialize, a theme the Bidpai fables frequently explore.

- **The Talkative Tortoise** (Tale 13): The story of a tortoise who cannot hold its tongue and falls to its death appears in the Bidpai tradition as well as the Jataka. The Bidpai version and the Jataka version share the same narrative core, illustrating the fluid boundaries between these two great story-reservoirs (030-045). The Jataka version specifically entered the Bidpai literature and occurs in all major recensions of the collection (030-045).

- **The Pigeon and the Crow** (Tale 29): Though recorded in this volume from a Kashmiri oral source, the tale of the treacherous crow who receives a just punishment belongs to a widely distributed Bidpai fable type about false friendship and its consequences (030-039).

- **The transmission lineage** (P01-003, P01-007): Joseph Jacobs himself identifies the Bidpai as one of the three great channels through which Indian tales entered Western consciousness — alongside the Jatakas and modern oral collections from contact zones like the Punjab and Kashmir (P01-003, P01-007). The Bidpai served as the "literary" transmission pathway, complementing the "religious" pathway of the Jatakas.

## Cultural Context

The significance of the Bidpai fables lies not only in their literary quality but in their role as a cultural bridge. The migration of these tales from India to Europe has been called "the travels of the Fables of Bidpai" — a journey that took them through Persian court culture, Abbasid Baghdad, medieval Spain under both Muslim and Christian rule, and Renaissance Italy and England (030-039). By the time the tales reached Europe, they had been layered with Persian, Arabic, and Hebrew accretions, yet the core Indian narrative material remained intact.

Prof. Benfey, the great nineteenth-century Sanskritist, contended that the Bidpai collection was of Buddhist origin, derived from Jataka material (030-006, 030-007). While subsequent scholarship has complicated this claim — the *Panchatantra* proper is a Brahmanical work, not Buddhist — the overlap between Bidpai fables and Jataka tales is nonetheless extensive. Many individual stories appear in both traditions, and distinguishing which version is earlier remains a matter of scholarly debate.

The Bidpai fables also shaped European literary form. The "frame story" technique — in which stories nest within stories, each character telling a tale to make a point — entered European literature largely through the Bidpai tradition, influencing Boccaccio's *Decameron*, Chaucer's *Canterbury Tales*, and the broader medieval story-collection genre (030-017).
