## Overview

The Brahman of the Broken Pot, whose proper name is **Svabhavakripana** — meaning "a born miser" (005-001) — is the protagonist of the short cautionary tale [[the-broken-pot]] (Chapter 5). He has collected a pot of rice by begging and, lying beneath it through the night, daydreams an elaborate chain of investments that will bring him from a single pot of rice to a grand house, a beautiful wife, and a son named Somasarman. The fantasy grows so vivid that he physically acts it out — and kicks the pot to pieces. He is the archetype of the **castle-builder**, the dreamer whose imagination so outstrips reality that it destroys what little he actually has.

## Role in the Tale

Svabhavakripana is both the tale's only character and its cautionary subject. His function is to embody a single proverb, stated at the story's close: "He who makes [[folly-and-foolishness|foolish plans]] for the future will be white all over, like the father of Somasarman" (005-002). The tale itself is a frame for this proverb, and the Brahman exists purely to illustrate it.

His daydream follows a strict economic logic — rice to goats to cows to buffaloes to horses to gold to a house to a wife to a son — each step plausible in isolation, absurd only in the assumption of uninterrupted success. The kick is the punchline, but also the truth: his real life contains no goats, no cows, no wife, no son — only a pot of cold rice and the delusion of greatness.

## Key Appearance

The entire tale is Svabhavakripana's soliloquy, reported in a single block of free indirect discourse (005-001, 005-002):

- **The pot**: He hangs his pot of leftover rice on a peg, lies beneath it, and stares at it "all the night" (005-001).
- **The chain of speculation**: Rice → hundred rupees → goats → herd of goats → cows → calves → buffaloes → mares → horses → gold → house → Brahman's daughter with dowry → son named Somasarman (005-001).
- **The fantasy of anger**: In his imagined future, the boy will toddle too near a horse's hoof; Svabhavakripana, engrossed in a book, will call to his wife to take the child; she, "distracted by some domestic work," will not hear; he will rise in fury and kick her — and at this very thought, his real foot swings out and shatters the real pot (005-002).
- **The aftermath**: The rice pours over him, making him "quite white," and the narrator delivers the moral (005-002).

## Relationships

Svabhavakripana has no actual relationships in the tale — every person he interacts with (the wife, the son Somasarman, the Brahman who brings the dowry) exists only in his imagination. This absence of real human connection is itself the story's point: his fantasy is a surrogate for a life he has not lived.

## Moral and Cultural Significance

The Broken Pot is one of the most widely distributed tales in world folklore — classified under the **"counting chickens before they hatch"** motif — and the Brahman's version is its Indian expression. The tale belongs firmly under [[folly-and-foolishness]], but it carries a specifically economic dimension: Svabhavakripana is not merely a fool but a miser who, through grasping at future riches, destroys his present wealth.

The name *Svabhavakripana* ("born miser") signals that his flaw is innate, a matter of character rather than circumstance. In the Hindu social context, a Brahman living by begging is already a figure of diminished dignity; the tale compounds this by making him a miser who cannot even enjoy the rice he has, because he is already spending it — in his mind — a hundred times over. The story is thus a commentary on [[greed-in-folklore]] as much as on [[folly-and-foolishness|foolishness]]: it is not the planning that is wrong but the unwillingness to be content with what one already has.
