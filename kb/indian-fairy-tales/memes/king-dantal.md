## Overview

King Dantal is the ruler of the Phalana country and the father of [[majnun|Prince Majnun]] in the tale "[[laili|Loving Laili]]" (Chapter 8). He appears throughout the narrative as the patriarchal figure whose decisions — first to marry his son to a new bride, then to accept [[laili|Laili]] as his daughter-in-law — shape the tale's dramatic arc. King Dantal embodies the archetype of the aging monarch whose kingdom becomes the stage for the next generation's adventures and trials.

## Role in the Tale

### Wealth and Generosity

The tale opens by establishing King Dantal's prosperity: "There was a king called King Dantal, who had a great many rupees and soldiers and horses" (008-001). He is a figure of established power, ruling over a stable kingdom — a stability that his son [[majnun|Majnun]]'s love for [[laili|Laili]] will eventually disrupt and then restore.

### The Second Marriage and Conflict

After [[majnun|Majnun]]'s first wife dies, King Dantal attempts to arrange a second marriage for his son:

> King Dantal wished his son to marry again, and tried to arrange a match, but Majnun refused. He said he would never marry again unless Laili could be found. (008-020)

This establishes the central tension: the king's paternal desire to secure his son's future through marriage conflicts with [[majnun|Majnun]]'s devotion to [[laili|Laili]]. King Dantal's authority — though absolute — cannot compel his son's heart.

### Skepticism and Acceptance

When [[majnun|Majnun]] and [[husain-mahamat|Husain Mahamat]] report seeing a beautiful woman in the garden, King Dantal is initially dismissive:

> King Dantal did not believe Majnun had seen a woman at all. (008-028)

This skepticism reveals the king as a practical, perhaps weary ruler — one who has seen enough wonders in his long life to doubt the extravagant claims of young men. Yet when [[laili|Laili]] is eventually brought before him, King Dantal becomes a protective father figure to her as well.

### The Resolution

In the tale's resolution, King Dantal is confronted with the truth of [[laili|Laili]]'s ordeal. When [[laili|Laili]] — restored to life — calls him and asks sadly "Why are you doing this?" after he wishes her dead, King Dantal answers simply: "It is because you are not worthy of [[majnun|Majnun]]" (008-038). This moment captures the king's fundamental motivation: protecting his son's honor and happiness. Once [[laili|Laili]]'s innocence is established, King Dantal accepts her fully, and the tale ends with celebration:

> They were all very happy, and King Dantal had all the drums in the place beaten, and all the guns fired off, to celebrate Majnun's wedding to Laili. (008-040)

### The Kingdom's Protection

In the tale's final lines, King Dantal's concern for [[laili|Laili]]'s safety becomes explicit — and surprisingly modern:

> King Dantal said, "Laili is so beautiful, that perhaps some one may come and try to take her away, so I will not have any other men but myself and Majnun live in the palace." (008-060)

This possessive protectiveness reflects both the king's recognition of [[laili|Laili]]'s extraordinary beauty and the patriarchal anxiety characteristic of royal households in folklore.

## Relationships

- **[[majnun|Prince Majnun]]** — his son and heir; King Dantal tries to arrange [[majnun|Majnun]]'s second marriage, and ultimately celebrates his union with [[laili|Laili]] (008-020, 008-040)
- **[[laili|Laili]]** — his daughter-in-law; initially skeptical of her existence (008-028), then protective of her to the point of restricting palace access (008-060)
- **[[husain-mahamat|Husain Mahamat]]** — his companion figure who walks with the aging king in the garden (008-026)
- **[[the-fakir|The Fakir]]** — the antagonist who abducts [[laili|Laili]] and places her in King Dantal's garden (008-025), setting up the final confrontation

## Moral and Cultural Significance

King Dantal represents the patriarchal monarch in Indian folklore — a figure whose authority is absolute but whose wisdom is tested by the passions of the younger generation. His arc from skepticism to acceptance mirrors a broader cultural theme: the older generation's eventual recognition that love and [[fate-and-prophecy|destiny]] operate beyond the scope of royal decree.

The king's final act — restricting the palace to protect [[laili|Laili]]'s beauty — reflects the *purdah* (seclusion) motif common in Indo-Persian courtly culture, where female beauty was both celebrated and guarded as a form of family and royal honor. Yet the tale also subtly undermines this patriarchal control: [[laili|Laili]]'s beauty and worth are validated not by the king's protection but by [[khuda|Khuda]]'s direct intervention throughout the narrative.
