# Lambikin

## Overview

Lambikin is the [[trickster-figure|trickster]] hero of the tale [[the-lambikin]] (Chapter 3), "a wee wee Lambikin, who frolicked about on his little tottery legs, and enjoyed himself amazingly" (003-001). Setting off to visit his [[the-granny|Granny]], he meets a whole procession of predators — a jackal, a vulture, a tiger, a wolf, a dog, and an eagle — each of whom wants to eat him. With a nimble wit far larger than his little body, he talks his way past every one of them, feasts at his [[the-granny|Granny]]'s, and starts home [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]]d inside a drum.

He is the classic small, clever creature who survives by cunning where he cannot survive by strength. But his cleverness is not perfect: in the end his own voice gives him away, and the tale closes on a sharp reversal of fortune.

## Role in the Tale

Lambikin's strategy is a promise of greater reward later. To every beast that cries "Lambikin! Lambikin! I'll eat YOU!" he replies with the same frisking rhyme:

> "To Granny's house I go, / Where I shall fatter grow, / Then you can eat me so." (003-004, 003-005, 003-006)

Each predator "thought this reasonable, and let Lambikin pass" (003-007). Safe at [[the-granny|Granny]]'s, he has himself shut in the corn-bin and eats for seven days "until he could scarcely waddle" (003-020). Knowing he is now too plump to walk home safely, "cunning little Lambikin" (003-020) devises his masterstroke: a little drum, or "drumikin," made from a skin, inside which he curls up "snug and warm" and trundles away unseen (003-021, 003-022).

As "Drumikin," he fools the same beasts a second time. When each calls out, "Drumikin! Drumikin! Have you seen Lambikin?" he answers from inside, "Fallen into the fire, and so will you / On little Drumikin. Tum-pa, tum-too!" (003-023, 003-026, 003-027), and every animal sighs over the tender morsel it thinks it has missed (003-039). His luck holds until the jackal comes by. Halfway through the rhyme, the jackal "recognised his voice at once" (003-046), tore open the drum, "and gobbled up Lambikin" (003-047).

## Key Deeds and Appearances

- **Sets off to [[the-granny|Granny]]'s** — a wee lamb frolicking on tottery legs, bound for his grandmother's house (003-001).
- **Outtalks the predators** — meets a jackal, vulture, tiger, wolf, dog, and eagle, and puts off each with the "fatter grow" rhyme (003-002, 003-008, 003-014).
- **Fattens in the corn-bin** — stays seven days eating until he can scarcely waddle (003-020).
- **Invents the drumikin** — has [[the-granny|Granny]] make a drum of skin so he can roll home hidden inside (003-021, 003-022).
- **Fools the beasts again** — passes as "Drumikin," singing "Tum-pa, tum-too" and taunting each animal (003-023, 003-036).
- **Caught at last** — the jackal recognises his voice, tears open the drum, and eats him (003-046, 003-047).

## Relationships

- **The jackal** — the first beast Lambikin tricks and the last to catch him; sharp "as a needle," it alone recognises his voice and ends his trickery (003-002, 003-040, 003-046).
- **[[the-granny|Granny]]** — his grandmother, who fattens him in the corn-bin and builds the drumikin at his request (003-019, 003-022).
- **The hungry beasts** — the vulture, tiger, wolf, dog, and eagle, all outwitted twice, who sigh over the morsel they let slip (003-014, 003-039).

## Moral or Cultural Significance

Lambikin is the hero of a cumulative "escaping-animal" tale, a nursery type in which a small creature evades a string of predators by a repeated formula before a final twist. The notes to the collection compare the story with European analogues, observing that "the [[lambikins-drum-escape|Lambikin in the Drum]]ikin can scarcely be accidental" as an echo of a like tale where a creature rolls downhill in a churn (030-031, 030-032). His device — hiding his identity and putting off danger with a promise — makes him kin to the [[trickster-figure|trickster]] figures of the wider fable tradition, though his eventual capture sets him apart from luckier [[trickster-figure|trickster]]s like the jackal of [[the-tiger-the-brahman-and-the-jackal]].

The tale carries a double edge. Lambikin's success celebrates the survival of the quick-witted weak, yet his downfall — undone by the very singing that expressed his glee — warns that overconfidence and a wagging tongue can betray even the cleverest. He is at once "[[greed-in-folklore|greedy]]" (003-020), "cunning" (003-020), and, in the storyteller's fond phrase, a "little slyboots" (003-035) whose luck finally runs out.
