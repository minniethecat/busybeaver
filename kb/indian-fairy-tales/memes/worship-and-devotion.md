Worship and devotion appear throughout Indian fairy tales not as mere background but as narrative turning points — the moment a character prays, makes an offering, or sits absorbed in meditation is often the hinge on which the plot turns. These tales reflect the religious pluralism of India itself, with scenes of Hindu, Buddhist, and Islamic devotion coexisting within the same story collection.

## Definition

In the context of these tales, worship (Sanskrit *puja*, Persian *bandagi*) encompasses a range of devotional acts: the offering of prayers to a deity, the performance of religious rituals at sacred sites, the sitting in absorbed meditation at a tomb or shrine, and the public demonstration of piety. Devotion, in these narratives, is not merely private feeling — it is a visible act that often draws the attention of both divine and human observers. The tales depict worship as a source of divine favor, a test of sincerity, and occasionally a mask for deception.

## Examples in the Tales

The motif of worship surfaces across multiple tales, spanning Hindu, Buddhist, and Islamic contexts:

- **The elder brother's devotion to Ganesa** (010-025): In *The Soothsayer's Son*, Gangazara's elder brother is "a devout worshipper of Ganesa" (010-025), the elephant-headed god of wisdom and remover of obstacles. On a Friday — sacred to Ganesa — the brother performs his worship at a temple, and it is through this act of devotion that Gangazara is able to substitute himself for a corpse and escape his prophesied death (010-025). Worship here serves as the mechanism of deliverance.

- **The man absorbed in worship before the Raja** (014-022): In *A Lac of Rupees for a Bit of Advice*, a clever protagonist pretends "to be absorbed in worship" (014-022) when the Raja passes by. His conspicuous piety earns him the Raja's attention and eventually a reward, illustrating how the appearance of devotion could be strategically deployed in courtly life. The tale does not condemn this — it celebrates the wit that uses worship as both genuine practice and social performance.

- **The queen's prayer to God for her husband** (022-073): In *The Boy Who Had a Moon on His Forehead and a Star on His Chin*, a devoted queen, believing she has found her destined husband, "worshipped God and thanked him for letting her find her husband" (022-073). The spontaneous prayer of gratitude marks the tale's romantic climax — devotion as the natural response to divine blessing.

- **Worshipping Khuda at the tomb** (026-102): In *The Ivory City and Its Fairy Princess*, the hero and his companion are found "sitting here by this tomb worshipping Khuda" (026-102) when a disguised ghoul approaches. The tale uses the Arabic-Urdu name for God, "Khuda," reflecting the Islamic cultural layer in tales collected from the Punjab region. The act of worship at a saint's tomb places the action within the Sufi tradition of *dargah* pilgrimage.

- **Feigned worship as a trap** (025-023): In *The Demon with the Matted Hair*, the captured Bodhisatta is made to "worship the deity to whom offerings were made" (025-023) by the demon, who uses the ritual as bait — luring travelers into worshipping a sacred tree before consuming them. Worship here takes on a darker role: the demon has corrupted a genuine religious practice into a deadly ambush.

## Cultural Context

The coexistence of Hindu, Buddhist, and Islamic worship practices within a single collection of tales reflects India's layered religious history. Ganesa worship appears in tales from the Deccan and western India, reflecting the god's widespread popularity as a remover of obstacles. The Buddhist Jataka tales frame worship within the context of *dana* (generosity) and *sila* (moral conduct) — the Bodhisatta's acts of devotion are often directed toward ascetics and holy men rather than deities per se (030-003).

The invocation of "Khuda" in *The Ivory City* (026-102) is particularly noteworthy: this tale was collected in the Punjab, a region with a long Islamic presence, and the use of the Persian-derived divine name situates the story within a Muslim narrative world. The tomb (*dargah*) worship further anchors the tale in Sufi devotional practice, where saints' tombs serve as sites of prayer, meditation, and miraculous intervention.
