# Raja Rasalu

## Overview

Raja Rasalu is the heroic prince at the centre of the tale [[raja-rasalu]] (Chapter 19), a legendary figure of the Punjab whose traditional seat is placed at Sialkot (030-081). Destined for greatness from birth, he is proclaimed a child who "shall be known far and" wide, but a condition is laid upon his parents: "for twelve years you must not look upon his face" (019-002). To fulfil this, they hide him away in an underground palace with nurses and servants (019-003).

Tender-hearted and strong, Rasalu grows into a gallant prince who journeys forth into the world seeking adventure, and whose renown spreads before him (019-098). His story is a chain of trials — separating seed from sand, swinging the seventy maidens, and finally the great game of chaupur against the tyrant King Sarkap — in each of which his courage and the help of humble creatures carry the day.

## Role in the Tale

Rasalu embodies the wandering warrior-hero. Even as a boy he shows his bold, headstrong nature, flinging stones at the earthen water-vessels of the village women (019-008), an act that reveals his identity as "Prince Rasalu come forth before the time" (019-009). Once grown, he sets out to play chaupur with King Sarkap (019-034), a journey that becomes the spine of the tale.

Along the way he meets a headless corpse in an old graveyard and, being tender-hearted, sits and talks with it through the dark and dreary night (019-054); moved by pity he restores the dead man, though its voice "was the voice of a tiny cricket" (019-056, 019-057). At the city of Sarkap he is met by the seventy maidens, daughters of the king (019-059), who set him a series of tasks. His crowning trial is the game of chaupur itself, on which his own head is staked — for "every one who came to play chaupur with the King had to beat" him or forfeit their life (019-085).

## Key Deeds and Appearances

- **Prophesied and hidden at birth** — named Raja Rasalu and concealed in an underground palace so no one might look upon his face for twelve years (019-002, 019-003).
- **Reveals himself as a boy** — flings stones at the women's water-pots, betraying that the prince has "come forth before the time" (019-008, 019-009).
- **Compassion in the graveyard** — sits through the night talking with a headless corpse, then tender-heartedly restores the tiny creature it has become (019-054, 019-057).
- **Separates the seed** — set the task of dividing a hundredweight of millet seed mixed with sand, he calls on the crickets, whose flight completes it in one night (019-076, 019-078, 019-080).
- **Swings the seventy maidens** — performs the maidens' task, grumbling that he will not "spend my life swinging girls" yet accomplishing it in his shining armour (019-082, 019-083).
- **Plays chaupur with King Sarkap** — journeys forth to play, staking his horse, his arms, and finally his own head against the tyrant (019-102, 019-105).
- **Wins with the kitten's help** — when Sarkap's rat upsets the pieces on the sly, Rasalu's kitten watches from the window and unnerves it, so that Sarkap "lost, first his kingdom, then the wealth of the whole" of his realm (019-106, 019-133).

## Relationships

- **King Sarkap** — the tyrant chaupur-king whom Rasalu defeats, ending his practice of taking the heads of losing players (019-104, 019-133). When Sarkap "vowed a solemn vow never to play for another's head" again, Rasalu spares him (019-135, 019-136).
- **[[kokilan|Princess Kokilan]]** — Rasalu marries the [[kokilan|Princess Kokilan]], "whom he won from Sarkap when he played chaupur" (019-142, 019-143).
- **Bhaunr, the Arab steed** — his faithful horse, staked and lost during the game (019-107, 019-115).
- **The kitten** — a humble helper whose vigilance at the window wins the decisive game (019-119, 019-121).

## Moral or Cultural Significance

Raja Rasalu belongs to the living folk-tradition of the Punjab: the notes record his traditional seat "in the neighbourhood of Sialkot" (030-081). His character fuses two folk ideals — the fearless warrior who stakes his own head, and the tender-hearted man whose kindness to a corpse and to helpless creatures is repaid in his hour of need. The victory over Sarkap is won not by brute strength but through compassion and the loyalty of small helpers, echoing the theme of [[gratitude-and-ingratitude]] that runs through the collection. His marriage to [[kokilan|Kokilan]] opens onward into further legend, for "[[kokilan|Kokilan]], when she grows up," is "married" into the next cycle of story (030-080).
