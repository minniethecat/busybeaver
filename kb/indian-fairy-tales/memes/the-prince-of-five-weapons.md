## Overview

The Prince of the Five Weapons is the Bodhisatta (a previous incarnation of the Buddha), born as the son of [[king-brahmadatta|King Brahmadatta]] of [[benares|Benares]] in the Jataka tale [[the-demon-with-the-matted-hair]] (Chapter 25). At his name-day, eight hundred Brahmans prophesy that he "shall be famous and renowned for his skill with the five weapons, and shall be the chief man in all India" (025-005). Armed with sword, spear, bow, battle-axe, and shield, he journeys to Takkasila for his education and later confronts the forest Demon with the Matted Hair — not primarily with his weapons, but with the "weapon of knowledge" within him (025-020).

## Role in the Tale

### Education and Prophecy

Born as the son of [[king-brahmadatta|Brahmadatta]]'s chief queen, the prince receives his name from the Brahmans who divine his "lucky marks." They name him the Prince of the Five Weapons — sword, spear, bow, battle-axe, and shield — and promise future greatness (025-005). He is sent to Takkasila, the ancient centre of learning, where his teacher gives him the five weapons as a parting gift, and the prince begins his return journey to [[benares|Benares]] "armed with the Five Weapons" (025-010).

### Confrontation with the Demon

On the road to [[benares|Benares]], travellers warn the prince to avoid a forest inhabited by the Demon with the Matted Hair, "who kills every man he sees" (025-012). But the Bodhisatta, "having confidence in himself, went straight on, fearless as a maned lion" (025-012). When the Demon appears — enormous, with a face "the size of a palm-leaf umbrella" and eyes burning like bowls of oil — the prince fires fifty poisoned arrows, but every one sticks in the Demon's hair. He strikes with his sword, spear, and club — all stick fast. He strikes with his right hand, left hand, right foot, left foot, and finally butts with his head — and is caught in five places, hanging suspended from the Demon's hair (025-016, 025-017).

> "Thus the Bodhisatta was five times snared, caught fast in five places, hanging suspended: yet he felt no fear — was not even nervous." (025-018)

### The Weapon of Knowledge

The Demon, astonished, asks why the prince does not fear death. The Bodhisatta replies:

> "In one life a man can die but once. Besides, in my belly is a thunderbolt; if you eat me, you will never be able to digest it; this will tear your inwards into little bits, and kill you: so we shall both perish. That is why I fear nothing." (025-020)

The narrator explains: "By this, the Bodhisatta meant the weapon of knowledge which he had within him" (025-020). The Demon, terrified, releases the prince, and the Bodhisatta converts him from wickedness, "established him in the five kinds of virtue," and makes him the guardian spirit of the forest (025-023).

## Key Deeds

- **Prophecy at birth**: Eight hundred Brahmans divine his skill with the five weapons and prophesy his future greatness (025-005).
- **Education at Takkasila**: Receives the five weapons as a gift from his teacher and departs for [[benares|Benares]] (025-010).
- **The forest confrontation**: Walks into the Demon's wood despite warnings, "fearless as a maned lion" (025-012).
- **The fivefold capture**: All five physical weapons fail and he is caught by five limbs in the Demon's hair — yet "felt no fear" (025-016, 025-017, 025-018).
- **The thunderbolt within**: Defeats the Demon not by force but by the weapon of knowledge, the inner truth that makes him unafraid (025-020).
- **Conversion of the Demon**: Discourses to the Demon on the "disadvantage of the five kinds of wickedness, and the profit of the five kinds of virtue," transforming him from predator to protector (025-023).

## Relationships

- **[[king-brahmadatta|Brahmadatta]]** — the King of [[benares|Benares]] and the Bodhisatta's father in this birth (025-004).
- **[[the-matted-hair-demon|The Demon with the Matted Hair]]** ([[the-demon-with-the-matted-hair]]) — the forest-dwelling monster who captures the prince with his sticky hair, but is ultimately converted by the prince's fearlessness (025-012, 025-023).
- **[[the-teacher|The Teacher]] at Takkasila** — the master who educates the prince and gives him the five weapons as a parting gift (025-010).
- **The Eight Hundred Brahmans** — the diviners at the prince's name-day who prophesy his [[fate-and-prophecy|destiny]] (025-005).

## Moral and Cultural Significance

The Prince of the Five Weapons is a **Jataka figure** — the Bodhisatta in a previous birth — and his tale is structurally a lesson about the limits of physical force. The narrative sets up an expectation of martial prowess (eight hundred Brahmans prophesy his skill, he is named for his weapons, he is trained at Takkasila) and then systematically dismantles it. Every physical weapon fails; what succeeds is the interior quality of fearlessness grounded in wisdom.

The "weapon of knowledge" is not a hidden knife but a philosophical truth: that death can only come once, and that a mind free of fear is itself impervious. This is the Jataka's characteristic fusion of folk-tale action with Buddhist doctrine. The Demon's conversion — from "cruel, blood-bibbing, devourer of the flesh" to a self-denying guardian — makes the tale an allegory of the Buddha's teaching mission, where even the most violent being can be brought to virtue by the right words and the right example (025-023).

The tale's frame — the Demon as a being "born a Demon... because you did wickedly in former lives" (025-023) — embeds the whole confrontation within the Buddhist doctrine of karma and rebirth, making the Prince's victory not a triumph of arms but of the Dhamma itself.
