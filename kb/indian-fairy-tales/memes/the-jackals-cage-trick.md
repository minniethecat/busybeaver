# The Jackal's Cage Trick

## Summary

A tiger caught in a trap begged a passing Brahman to release it, swearing with many oaths that it meant no harm (009-002, 009-004). No sooner was the cage opened than the tiger sprang out and announced it would eat its rescuer (009-005). Granted the right to consult three witnesses, the Brahman found the pipal tree, the buffalo, and the road all siding against mankind (009-007 to 009-009). At last a jackal offered to judge — but pretended, again and again, to be unable to grasp how the affair began, until the exasperated tiger leapt back into the cage to [[supernatural-beings|demons]]trate its former position, whereupon the jackal shut the door (009-035, 009-036).

> "Perfectly!" grinned the jackal, as he dexterously shut the door… (009-036)

## Context

The tale is a beast-fable on ingratitude. [[the-brahman|The Brahman]] had freed the tiger out of pity, and in vain "pleaded for his life" once betrayed (009-006). The three appeals to nature only confirmed the tiger's case — one witness even scoffing, "You are a fool to expect gratitude!" (009-008). The jackal is the Brahman's last hope; its arrival and studied confusion set up the story's rescue and reversal.

## Participants

- **[[the-jackal]]** — the clever judge who, feigning a slow wit, manoeuvres the tiger into re-entering the cage.
- **[[the-tiger]]** — [[gratitude-and-ingratitude|the ungrateful]] beast whose impatience and vanity are its undoing; in a rage at the jackal's "stupidity" it re-enters the trap to prove its point (009-023, 009-035).
- **[[the-brahman]]** — the merciful rescuer whose life hangs on the jackal's ruse; his knees knock together as he awaits being made the tiger's dinner (009-017).

The event resolves [[the-tiger-the-brahman-and-the-jackal]], the ninth tale of the collection.

## Outcome or Consequence

The tiger is trapped anew and the Brahman saved. Playing the fool, the jackal repeatedly muddled the account — "the tiger was in the Brahman, and the cage came walking by" (009-022) — until the tiger, losing all patience, "jumping into the cage, cried, 'This way! Now do you understand?'" (009-035). The jackal instantly latched the door, leaving the ingrate confined and the deliverer free to go on his way.

## Cultural or Moral Significance

The episode is among the most famous of Indian fables on the theme that the wicked deserve no pity and that cunning can rescue where mercy has failed. It dramatizes the motif of [[gratitude-and-ingratitude|the ungrateful]] beast returned to captivity, a lesson echoed elsewhere in the collection (see [[gratitude-and-ingratitude]]). The jackal's feigned dullness is a [[trickster-figure|trickster]]'s masterstroke — wisdom [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]]d as folly — and the tale's structure, with its chorus of witnesses condemning humankind, gives its final reversal both comic and moral force.
