## Overview

The Queen appears as a royal female figure in multiple tales of the Indian Fairy Tales collection. She is not a single character but a recurring archetype of the royal consort, sometimes portrayed as a figure of palace authority, at other times as a character whose curiosity drives the plot forward.

Her most prominent appearances are in "[[a-lac-of-rupees-for-a-bit-of-advice]]" (Chapter 14), where the loss of her pearl rosary leads to dramatic consequences, and "[[why-the-fish-laughed]]" (Chapter 24), where her inquiry about a fish sets off a chain of events that nearly costs a vizier his life.

## Role in the Tales

In "[[a-lac-of-rupees-for-a-bit-of-advice]]", the Queen is central to a palace crisis. Her lost pearl rosary leads to the false accusation of a holy man — the [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]]d prince (014-023). The Queen's pearl rosary represents both royal wealth and the fragility of material possessions.

In "[[why-the-fish-laughed]]", the Queen's interaction with a fisherwoman sets the story in motion. When she asks whether a fish is male or female and the fish laughs at her, she becomes enraged and reports the incident to the King (024-001)(024-007).

Her indignation triggers the King's demand that the vizier explain the fish's laughter within six months (024-011). This near-impossible task drives the vizier's son to embark on a journey of discovery.

A briefer mention occurs in "[[the-ivory-city-and-its-fairy-princess]]", where the Queen is referenced in the context of [[gulizar|Gulizar]] and the royal household (026-053).

## Key Appearances

**The Pearl Rosary Incident.** The Queen loses her pearl rosary, causing "great consternation in the palace" (014-023). The rosary is found near the jogi ([[disguise-and-deception|disguise]]d prince), leading the King to order the jogi's execution — though the prince escapes.

**[[the-fishs-laughter|The Laughing Fish]].** The Queen beckons a fisherwoman to her window and asks, "Is it a he or a she? I wish to purchase a she fish" (024-001)(024-002). When the fish laughs, she is deeply annoyed (024-007).

**The Queen's Satisfaction.** After the vizier's son uncovers a [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]]d man among the palace maids, "the queen was satisfied, and the faithful old vizier saved" (024-054).

## Moral or Cultural Significance

The Queen figure illustrates the dynamics of courtly life in traditional Indian storytelling. Her concerns — a lost rosary, the proper purchase of a fish — reflect domestic palace affairs, yet the consequences extend far beyond the harem walls.

The tales show how royal women, though confined to the inner palace, could inadvertently set events in motion that tested the wisdom and loyalty of the King's advisors. The Queen's character also embodies the theme of curiosity: her simple question about a fish reveals a deeper truth about deception within the palace itself.
