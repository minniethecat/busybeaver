## Overview

Queen Lona is the wife of [[salabhan|Raja Salabhan]] and the mother of [[rasalu|Raja Rasalu]] in [[raja-rasalu|Chapter 19]]. She is introduced as a queen who long endured childlessness — "though she wept and prayed at many a [[the-temple|shrine]], had never a child to gladden her eyes" (019-001) — before her prayers are finally answered. Her story intertwines the joy of motherhood with the grief of separation, as a [[fate-and-prophecy|prophecy]] compels her to hide her son for twelve years.

## Role in the Tale

Lona's role is that of the **yearning mother**, a recurring figure in folktales. After years of barrenness, she receives the promise of a son. When the child is born, she consults three [[the-jogi|Jogis]] about his fate:

> Queen Lona returned to the palace, and when the time for the birth of the promised son drew nigh, she inquired of three Jogis who came begging to her gate, what the child's fate would be, and the youngest of them answered and said, "Oh, Queen! the child will be a boy, and he will live to be a great man. But for twelve years you must not look upon his face, for if either you or his father see it before the twelve years are past, you will surely die!" (019-002)

Bound by this [[fate-and-prophecy|prophecy]], she sends her newborn son to an underground palace, where he is raised by nurses. For twelve years she does not see his face — a profound maternal sacrifice.

When [[rasalu|Rasalu]] departs on his adventures, Lona's grief surfaces:

> And Queen Lona answered through her tears:
> "Yea! mother am I, though I weep…" (019-022–019-023)

And when she finally watches him leave:

> …and Queen Lona, when she saw them going, watched until they disappeared (019-028)

## Key Deeds

- **Prays at many [[the-temple|shrine]]s** for a child, weeping for years without answer (019-001)
- **Receives the promise of a son** after long devotion (019-001)
- **Consults the three Jogis** and learns of the twelve-year [[fate-and-prophecy|prophecy]] (019-002)
- **Sends her infant son away** to the underground palace, obeying the [[fate-and-prophecy|prophecy]] at great personal cost (019-003)
- **Speaks to [[rasalu|Rasalu]] through tears** when he returns and must depart again (019-022)
- **Watches her son go** until he disappears from sight (019-028)

## Relationships

- **[[salabhan|Raja Salabhan]]** — her husband, who shares the burden of the [[fate-and-prophecy|prophecy]]
- **[[rasalu|Raja Rasalu]]** — her son, the promised child for whom she waited and wept
- **[[the-jogi|The Jogis]]** — the three ascetics who foretell [[rasalu|Rasalu]]'s greatness and the fatal condition

## Moral and Cultural Significance

Queen Lona represents the **suffering mother** archetype in Indian folklore — a woman whose devotion to the gods is rewarded with a child, only to be tested by a [[fate-and-prophecy|prophecy]] that demands separation. Her tears at [[rasalu|Rasalu]]'s departure (019-022) resonate with the universal theme of maternal love contending with fate. Unlike Salabhan, who acts out of fear of death, Lona's obedience to the [[fate-and-prophecy|prophecy]] seems driven more by faith in the holy men's words — a subtle distinction that deepens her character.
