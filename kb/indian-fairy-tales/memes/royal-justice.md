# Royal Justice

## Definition

Royal justice — the ideal of a king who governs so wisely and righteously that wrongdoing withers and quarrels cease — is a recurring standard against which rulers are measured in the *Indian Fairy Tales*. The theme has two faces. In one, a virtuous monarch's justice is so perfect that the machinery of law falls idle for want of disputes. In the other, a ruler's (or a court's) discernment is put to the test by a riddle or a clever challenge, and the tale exposes whether authority truly possesses the wisdom it claims. Both faces descend from the Jataka tradition's preoccupation with the righteous king.

## Examples in the Tales

- **The king whose justice leaves nothing to judge** in [[a-lesson-for-kings]]: the Bodhisatta-king rules so well that "he thus reigned with justice, with justice also his ministers administered the law" (017-001); the lawsuits dry up so completely that "though the judges sat all day in the court, they had to leave" without a single case to hear (017-001). Perfect justice makes the judge's bench redundant.

- **Testing the ruler's discernment** in [[why-the-fish-laughed]]: a fish "laughed aloud" (024-003) at the king's court, and the mystery is resolved only when it emerges that the laughter mocked a failure of discernment — the fish "laughed most rudely" over the question of whether a [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]]d person "was a male or female" (024-007). It is a "sharp and wise girl" (024-031), not the king or his officers, who unravels the riddle, so that the tale quietly tests the wisdom of those in authority.

- **Questioning the justice of a verdict** in [[the-tiger-the-brahman-and-the-jackal]]: when the tiger would devour the Brahman, the dispute is submitted to a series of judges "as to the justice of the tiger's action" (009-006), dramatizing the search for a fair ruling.

- **Righteous rule as the hero's crown** in [[the-demon-with-the-matted-hair]]: the Bodhisatta at last "became king, and ruled righteously" (025-024), giving alms and doing good — the reward of virtue being just governance.

## Cultural Context

The figure of the just king (Sanskrit *dharma-rāja*) is central to Indian didactic literature, and the Jataka birth-stories repeatedly present the Bodhisatta as an exemplary ruler whose righteousness benefits all his subjects. Jacobs's collection preserves this ideal alongside the widespread folk pattern of the wisdom-test, in which a humble but clever figure — often a young woman — succeeds where kings and learned men fail, implicitly holding rulers to account. Together the two strands express a moral vision in which the legitimacy of power rests on justice and discernment rather than on force.
