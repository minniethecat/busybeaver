# Katar the Horse

## Overview

Katar is the King's horse in [[the-boy-who-had-a-moon-on-his-forehead-and-a-star-on-his-chin]] (Chapter 22), described as "very wicked, and quite untameable" — so dangerous that no groom dares approach him, and he "was never allowed to go" free (022-029, 022-031). Yet this ferocity conceals a deeper nature: Katar is a fairy-horse, "and came from the fairies' country" (022-035). He becomes the third and final animal guardian of the [[the-moon-prince]], swallowing the child and concealing him for two full years before fleeing with him to safety in a distant kingdom.

Katar is more than a beast of burden. He can speak, reason, transform himself and his rider, and produce princely clothes and weapons from thin air. His magical [[disguise-and-deception|transformation]]s — turning both himself and the prince into donkeys, then into an ugly commoner and an ordinary horse — are the means by which the hidden prince survives and eventually reclaims his identity (022-058, 022-059). At the story's end Katar does not return to the fairies' country but remains in the King's stables, his role as guardian complete (022-102).

## Role in the Tale

Katar enters the story as the King's most feared possession — a horse so dangerous that the King, on hearing a rumour that something strange lives inside the animal, casually resolves: "I will have Katar shot to-morrow" (022-033). But when Shankar the dog shows Katar the child, the horse is "very glad," and the groom hears the child laughing and playing inside the horse's body — the same sound that had been heard from inside the cow [[suri-the-cow|Suri]] (022-031, 022-032).

Katar's guardianship is the longest of the three animal protectors. After [[suri-the-cow|Suri]] is killed, the child lives inside Katar for two years — the fourth and fifth years of the prince's life (022-050). When the Queens, hearing the same mysterious laughter from inside Katar, persuade the King to have the horse shot as well, Katar hears the King's order delivered to the groom and acts decisively. He instructs the boy to mount, and "galloped away, on, and on, and on" until they reach a jungle-plain far from the kingdom (022-035, 022-036).

In the new land Katar takes on an additional role: that of the enchanter and strategist. He transforms himself into a donkey and the prince into a groom (022-058). Later, entering the capital, he transforms into an ordinary horse and the prince into "an ugly commoner" so the boy can find work with a grain merchant (022-058). When the prince's singing draws the King's attention and a summons is issued, Katar instructs the boy to twist his left ear, producing princely clothes and weapons so the prince may present himself in his true form (022-059). At the story's close, Katar lives in the King's stables, his violent past subsumed into his identity as the prince's protector, and he does not return to the fairy country (022-102).

## Key Deeds and Appearances

- **The untameable beast** — introduced as the King's "very wicked" horse whom no one can approach, yet he is "very glad to see the child" (022-029, 022-031).
- **Swallowing the child** — takes the [[moon|Moon]] Prince into his body for two years, the longest of the three guardianships, after the cow [[suri-the-cow|Suri]] is killed (022-032, 022-050).
- **The escape** — on hearing the King's order to have him shot, Katar orders the boy to mount and gallops away to a distant jungle-plain (022-035, 022-036).
- **Magical [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]]** — transforms himself and the prince into donkeys (022-058), then into an ordinary horse and an ugly commoner, enabling the prince to work incognito for a grain merchant (022-058).
- **Revealing the prince** — instructs the prince to twist his left ear to produce princely garments and weapons, restoring him to his true appearance for the King's summons (022-059).
- **Recognition scene** — the King of the [[moon|Moon]] Prince's homeland later identifies Katar as his own lost horse, confirming the prince's true identity (022-095, 022-096).
- **Earthly retirement** — at the story's end Katar does not return to the fairies' country but remains in the King's stables (022-102).

## Relationships

- **[[the-moon-prince]]** — his ward, swallowed and protected for two years, then carried to safety and magically [[disguise-and-deception|disguise]]d; Katar's actions make the prince's eventual recognition and marriage possible.
- **Shankar the Dog** — the first guardian, who brings the child to Katar before being shot; Katar is the third link in the chain begun by Shankar (022-031).
- **[[suri-the-cow]]** — the second guardian, killed by the Queens; Katar inherits the child from her and continues her protective work (022-045, 022-050).
- **[[king|The King]] of the [[moon|Moon]] Prince's homeland** — Katar's original owner, who twice orders him shot (022-033, 022-055); yet at the story's end the King recognizes Katar as his own horse (022-096).

## Moral or Cultural Significance

Katar belongs to the widespread folktale type of the magical horse — an animal that is both mount and mentor, capable of speech and [[disguise-and-deception|transformation]]. His initial description as "very wicked, and quite untameable" (022-029) sets up a deliberate misdirection: the horse that no groom dares approach is the gentlest guardian the child will ever have. Katar's violence is directed outward, against those who would harm the child, while his inner nature proves loyal, strategic, and self-sacrificing. The detail that he does not return to the fairies' country (022-102) is significant — unlike a purely magical helper who vanishes when the quest is done, Katar chooses to remain, his fairy origin subordinated to the earthly bond he has formed with the prince. He is the tale's most fully realized example of the "helper animal" motif, and the only guardian who survives to see the story's happy ending.
