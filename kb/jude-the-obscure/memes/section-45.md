[045-001] “Apart from ourselves, and our unhappy peculiarities, it is foreign to

[045-002] a man’s nature to go on loving a person when he is told that he must

[045-003] and shall be that person’s lover. There would be a much likelier chance

[045-004] of his doing it if he were told not to love. If the marriage ceremony

[045-005] consisted in an oath and signed contract between the parties to cease

[045-006] loving from that day forward, in consideration of personal possession

[045-007] being given, and to avoid each other’s society as much as possible in

[045-008] public, there would be more loving couples than there are now. Fancy

[045-009] the secret meetings between the perjuring husband and wife, the denials

[045-010] of having seen each other, the clambering in at bedroom windows, and

[045-011] the hiding in closets! There’d be little cooling then.”

[045-012] “Yes; but admitting this, or something like it, to be true, you are not

[045-013] the only one in the world to see it, dear little Sue. People go on

[045-014] marrying because they can’t resist natural forces, although many of

[045-015] them may know perfectly well that they are possibly buying a month’s

[045-016] pleasure with a life’s discomfort. No doubt my father and mother, and

[045-017] your father and mother, saw it, if they at all resembled us in habits

[045-018] of observation. But then they went and married just the same, because

[045-019] they had ordinary passions. But you, Sue, are such a phantasmal,

[045-020] bodiless creature, one who—if you’ll allow me to say it—has so little

[045-021] animal passion in you, that you can act upon reason in the matter, when

[045-022] we poor unfortunate wretches of grosser substance can’t.”

[045-023] “Well,” she sighed, “you’ve owned that it would probably end in misery

[045-024] for us. And I am not so exceptional a woman as you think. Fewer women

[045-025] like marriage than you suppose, only they enter into it for the dignity

[045-026] it is assumed to confer, and the social advantages it gains them

[045-027] sometimes—a dignity and an advantage that I am quite willing to do

[045-028] without.”

[045-029] Jude fell back upon his old complaint—that, intimate as they were, he

[045-030] had never once had from her an honest, candid declaration that she

[045-031] loved or could love him. “I really fear sometimes that you cannot,” he

[045-032] said, with a dubiousness approaching anger. “And you are so reticent. I

[045-033] know that women are taught by other women that they must never admit

[045-034] the full truth to a man. But the highest form of affection is based on

[045-035] full sincerity on both sides. Not being men, these women don’t know

[045-036] that in looking back on those he has had tender relations with, a man’s

[045-037] heart returns closest to her who was the soul of truth in her conduct.

[045-038] The better class of man, even if caught by airy affectations of dodging

[045-039] and parrying, is not retained by them. A Nemesis attends the woman who

[045-040] plays the game of elusiveness too often, in the utter contempt for her

[045-041] that, sooner or later, her old admirers feel; under which they allow

[045-042] her to go unlamented to her grave.”

[045-043] Sue, who was regarding the distance, had acquired a guilty look; and

[045-044] she suddenly replied in a tragic voice: “I don’t think I like you

[045-045] to-day so well as I did, Jude!”

[045-046] “Don’t you? Why?”

[045-047] “Oh, well—you are not nice—too sermony. Though I suppose I am so bad

[045-048] and worthless that I deserve the utmost rigour of lecturing!”

[045-049] “No, you are not bad. You are a dear. But as slippery as an eel when I

[045-050] want to get a confession from you.”

[045-051] “Oh yes, I am bad, and obstinate, and all sorts! It is no use your

[045-052] pretending I am not! People who are good don’t want scolding as I do…

[045-053] But now that I have nobody but you, and nobody to defend me, it is very

[045-054] hard that I mustn’t have my own way in deciding how I’ll live with you,

[045-055] and whether I’ll be married or no!”

[045-056] “Sue, my own comrade and sweetheart, I don’t want to force you either

[045-057] to marry or to do the other thing—of course I don’t! It is too wicked

[045-058] of you to be so pettish! Now we won’t say any more about it, and go on

[045-059] just the same as we have done; and during the rest of our walk we’ll

[045-060] talk of the meadows only, and the floods, and the prospect of the

[045-061] farmers this coming year.”

[045-062] After this the subject of marriage was not mentioned by them for

[045-063] several days, though living as they were with only a landing between

[045-064] them it was constantly in their minds. Sue was assisting Jude very

[045-065] materially now: he had latterly occupied himself on his own account in

[045-066] working and lettering headstones, which he kept in a little yard at the

[045-067] back of his little house, where in the intervals of domestic duties she

[045-068] marked out the letters full size for him, and blacked them in after he

[045-069] had cut them. It was a lower class of handicraft than were his former

[045-070] performances as a cathedral mason, and his only patrons were the poor

[045-071] people who lived in his own neighbourhood, and knew what a cheap man

[045-072] this “Jude Fawley: Monumental Mason” (as he called himself on his front

[045-073] door) was to employ for the simple memorials they required for their

[045-074] dead. But he seemed more independent than before, and it was the only

[045-075] arrangement under which Sue, who particularly wished to be no burden on

[045-076] him, could render any assistance.

[045-077] II

[045-078] It was an evening at the end of the month, and Jude had just returned

[045-079] home from hearing a lecture on ancient history in the public hall not

[045-080] far off. When he entered, Sue, who had been keeping indoors during his

[045-081] absence, laid out supper for him. Contrary to custom she did not speak.

[045-082] Jude had taken up some illustrated paper, which he perused till,

[045-083] raising his eyes, he saw that her face was troubled.

[045-084] “Are you depressed, Sue?” he said.

[045-085] She paused a moment. “I have a message for you,” she answered.

[045-086] “Somebody has called?”

[045-087] “Yes. A woman.” Sue’s voice quavered as she spoke, and she suddenly sat

[045-088] down from her preparations, laid her hands in her lap, and looked into

[045-089] the fire. “I don’t know whether I did right or not!” she continued. “I

[045-090] said you were not at home, and when she said she would wait, I said I

[045-091] thought you might not be able to see her.”

[045-092] “Why did you say that, dear? I suppose she wanted a headstone. Was she

[045-093] in mourning?”

[045-094] “No. She wasn’t in mourning, and she didn’t want a headstone; and I

[045-095] thought you couldn’t see her.” Sue looked critically and imploringly at

[045-096] him.

[045-097] “But who was she? Didn’t she say?”

[045-098] “No. She wouldn’t give her name. But I know who she was—I think I do!

[045-099] It was Arabella!”

[045-100] “Heaven save us! What should Arabella come for? What made you think it

[045-101] was she?”

[045-102] “Oh, I can hardly tell. But I know it was! I feel perfectly certain it

[045-103] was—by the light in her eyes as she looked at me. She was a fleshy,

[045-104] coarse woman.”

[045-105] “Well—I should not have called Arabella coarse exactly, except in

[045-106] speech, though she may be getting so by this time under the duties of

[045-107] the public house. She was rather handsome when I knew her.”

[045-108] “Handsome! But yes!—so she is!”

[045-109] “I think I heard a quiver in your little mouth. Well, waiving that, as

[045-110] she is nothing to me, and virtuously married to another man, why should

[045-111] she come troubling us?”

[045-112] “Are you sure she’s married? Have you definite news of it?”

[045-113] “No—not definite news. But that was why she asked me to release her.

[045-114] She and the man both wanted to lead a proper life, as I understood.”

[045-115] “Oh Jude—it was, it _was_ Arabella!” cried Sue, covering her eyes with

[045-116] her hand. “And I am so miserable! It seems such an ill omen, whatever

[045-117] she may have come for. You could not possibly see her, could you?”

[045-118] “I don’t really think I could. It would be so very painful to talk to

[045-119] her now—for her as much as for me. However, she’s gone. Did she say she

[045-120] would come again?”

[045-121] “No. But she went away very reluctantly.”

[045-122] Sue, whom the least thing upset, could not eat any supper, and when

[045-123] Jude had finished his he prepared to go to bed. He had no sooner raked

[045-124] out the fire, fastened the doors, and got to the top of the stairs than

[045-125] there came a knock. Sue instantly emerged from her room, which she had

[045-126] but just entered.

[045-127] “There she is again!” Sue whispered in appalled accents.

[045-128] “How do you know?”

[045-129] “She knocked like that last time.”

[045-130] They listened, and the knocking came again. No servant was kept in the

[045-131] house, and if the summons were to be responded to one of them would

[045-132] have to do it in person. “I’ll open a window,” said Jude. “Whoever it

[045-133] is cannot be expected to be let in at this time.”

[045-134] He accordingly went into his bedroom and lifted the sash. The lonely

[045-135] street of early retiring workpeople was empty from end to end save of

[045-136] one figure—that of a woman walking up and down by the lamp a few yards

[045-137] off.

[045-138] “Who’s there?” he asked.

[045-139] “Is that Mr. Fawley?” came up from the woman, in a voice which was

[045-140] unmistakably Arabella’s.

[045-141] Jude replied that it was.

[045-142] “Is it she?” asked Sue from the door, with lips apart.

[045-143] “Yes, dear,” said Jude. “What do you want, Arabella?” he inquired.

[045-144] “I beg your pardon, Jude, for disturbing you,” said Arabella humbly.

[045-145] “But I called earlier—I wanted particularly to see you to-night, if I

[045-146] could. I am in trouble, and have nobody to help me!”

[045-147] “In trouble, are you?”

[045-148] “Yes.”

[045-149] There was a silence. An inconvenient sympathy seemed to be rising in

[045-150] Jude’s breast at the appeal. “But aren’t you married?” he said.

[045-151] Arabella hesitated. “No, Jude, I am not,” she returned. “He wouldn’t,

[045-152] after all. And I am in great difficulty. I hope to get another

[045-153] situation as barmaid soon. But it takes time, and I really am in great

[045-154] distress because of a sudden responsibility that’s been sprung upon me

[045-155] from Australia; or I wouldn’t trouble you—believe me I wouldn’t. I want

[045-156] to tell you about it.”

[045-157] Sue remained at gaze, in painful tension, hearing every word, but

[045-158] speaking none.

[045-159] “You are not really in want of money, Arabella?” he asked, in a

[045-160] distinctly softened tone.

[045-161] “I have enough to pay for the night’s lodging I have obtained, but

[045-162] barely enough to take me back again.”

[045-163] “Where are you living?”

[045-164] “In London still.” She was about to give the address, but she said, “I

[045-165] am afraid somebody may hear, so I don’t like to call out particulars of

[045-166] myself so loud. If you could come down and walk a little way with me

[045-167] towards the Prince Inn, where I am staying to-night, I would explain

[045-168] all. You may as well, for old time’s sake!”

[045-169] “Poor thing! I must do her the kindness of hearing what’s the matter, I

[045-170] suppose,” said Jude in much perplexity. “As she’s going back to-morrow

[045-171] it can’t make much difference.”

[045-172] “But you can go and see her to-morrow, Jude! Don’t go now, Jude!” came

[045-173] in plaintive accents from the doorway. “Oh, it is only to entrap you, I

[045-174] know it is, as she did before! Don’t go, dear! She is such a

[045-175] low-passioned woman—I can see it in her shape, and hear it in her

[045-176] voice!

[045-177] “But I shall go,” said Jude. “Don’t attempt to detain me, Sue. God

[045-178] knows I love her little enough now, but I don’t want to be cruel to

[045-179] her.” He turned to the stairs.

[045-180] “But she’s not your wife!” cried Sue distractedly. “And I—”

[045-181] “And you are not either, dear, yet,” said Jude.

[045-182] “Oh, but are you going to her? Don’t! Stay at home! Please, please stay

[045-183] at home, Jude, and not go to her, now she’s not your wife any more than

[045-184] I!”
