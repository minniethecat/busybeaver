# Arabella Donn

Arabella Donn is [[jude-fawley|Jude Fawley]]'s first wife and the novel's embodiment of earthy, pragmatic sensuality. Introduced as "Arabella Donn. I'm living here" (007-008) — a terse self-introduction that captures her directness — she entraps [[jude-fawley|Jude]] into marriage by feigning pregnancy, abandons him for Australia, and returns years later to remarry him on his deathbed. As a foil to [[sue-bridehead|Sue Bridehead]], Arabella represents the claims of the body over the mind, and of survival over idealism.

## Character Overview

Arabella is the daughter of a pig-farmer near [[marygreen|Marygreen]]. She is physically robust, sexually confident, and entirely unsentimental. Her courtship of [[jude-fawley|Jude]] begins with a crude gesture — throwing a pig's pizzle at him — and proceeds through calculated flirtation. Her father calls out crudely, encouraging the match: "Arabella! Here's your young man come coorting! Mizzle, my girl!" (007-143). When she fears she may be pregnant, she pressures [[jude-fawley|Jude]] into marriage; when the pregnancy proves false, she expresses no guilt. The narrator captures her pragmatic indifference: "Arabella Donn. During the whole bygone week he had been resolving to..." (007-091).

After abandoning [[jude-fawley|Jude]], Arabella marries [[cartlett|Cartlett]] in Australia and lives comfortably. Upon [[cartlett|Cartlett]]'s death, she returns to England and, finding [[jude-fawley|Jude]] dying, seduces him into remarrying her — partly from possessiveness and partly from the practical calculation that his death will free her. She is, in many ways, the novel's most successful survivor.

## Role in the Narrative

### First Appearance

Arabella first appears bathing in a stream near [[marygreen|Marygreen]], where she catches [[jude-fawley|Jude]]'s attention. "Arabella's personality, held Jude to the spot against his intention" (007-016) — the narrator immediately establishes her physical power over him. Their meeting — crude, physical, and charged with animal energy — establishes the novel's contrast between physical [[love|passion]] (Arabella) and intellectual companionship ([[sue-bridehead|Sue]]). Jude finds her face a "useful lamp" (007-169) that illuminates his world, though even the narrator's phrasing suggests a purely sensory attraction rather than genuine understanding.

## Character Analysis

### Motivations & Desires

Arabella's motivations are simple and clear: security, possession, and sensual satisfaction. Unlike [[jude-fawley|Jude]] and [[sue-bridehead|Sue]], she does not aspire beyond her class or question social conventions — she uses them. Her pragmatism makes her the novel's most adaptable character: she survives where the idealists perish. Hardy gives her the grimly comic line "I suppose we can have some beer" (008-020) at a moment of high emotional tension, underlining her inability — or refusal — to take anything too seriously.

### Development Arc

Arabella does not develop so much as she persists. She begins and ends the novel as herself — sensual, calculating, and unkillable. Her reappearance at the novel's close, triumphantly remarried to the dying [[jude-fawley|Jude]] while [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] is exiled to a loveless marriage with [[richard-phillotson|Phillotson]], is Hardy's bitterest structural irony: the woman who feels least wins most. Her reply to the dying Jude — "In a few moments Arabella replied in a curiously low, hungry tone" (008-135) — could describe her mode of being throughout the entire novel: hungry, low, and utterly self-interested.
