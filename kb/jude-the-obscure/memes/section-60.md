[060-001] “Solemnity?” Jude looked at her with some surprise, and grew conscious

[060-002] that she was not the Sue of their earlier time.

[060-003] “Yes,” she said, with a little quiver in her words, “I have had

[060-004] dreadful fears, a dreadful sense of my own insolence of action. I have

[060-005] thought—that I am still his wife!”

[060-006] “Whose?”

[060-007] “Richard’s.”

[060-008] “Good God, dearest!—why?”

[060-009] “Oh I can’t explain! Only the thought comes to me.”

[060-010] “It is your weakness—a sick fancy, without reason or meaning! Don’t let

[060-011] it trouble you.”

[060-012] Sue sighed uneasily.

[060-014] improvement in their pecuniary position, which earlier in their

[060-015] experience would have made them cheerful. Jude had quite unexpectedly

[060-016] found good employment at his old trade almost directly he arrived, the

[060-017] summer weather suiting his fragile constitution; and outwardly his days

[060-018] went on with that monotonous uniformity which is in itself so grateful

[060-019] after vicissitude. People seemed to have forgotten that he had ever

[060-020] shown any awkward aberrancies, and he daily mounted to the parapets and

[060-021] copings of colleges he could never enter, and renewed the crumbling

[060-022] freestones of mullioned windows he would never look from, as if he had

[060-023] known no wish to do otherwise.

[060-024] There was this change in him; that he did not often go to any service

[060-025] at the churches now. One thing troubled him more than any other; that

[060-026] Sue and himself had mentally travelled in opposite directions since the

[060-027] tragedy: events which had enlarged his own views of life, laws,

[060-028] customs, and dogmas, had not operated in the same manner on Sue’s. She

[060-029] was no longer the same as in the independent days, when her intellect

[060-031] at that time respected, though he did not now.

[060-032] On a particular Sunday evening he came in rather late. She was not at

[060-033] home, but she soon returned, when he found her silent and meditative.

[060-034] “What are you thinking of, little woman?” he asked curiously.

[060-035] “Oh I can’t tell clearly! I have thought that we have been selfish,

[060-036] careless, even impious, in our courses, you and I. Our life has been a

[060-038] We should mortify the flesh—the terrible flesh—the curse of Adam!”

[060-039] “Sue!” he murmured. “What has come over you?”

[060-040] “We ought to be continually sacrificing ourselves on the altar of duty!

[060-041] But I have always striven to do what has pleased me. I well deserved

[060-043] out of me, and all my monstrous errors, and all my sinful ways!”

[060-044] “Sue—my own too suffering dear!—there’s no evil woman in you. Your

[060-045] natural instincts are perfectly healthy; not quite so impassioned,

[060-046] perhaps, as I could wish; but good, and dear, and pure. And as I have

[060-047] often said, you are absolutely the most ethereal, least sensual woman I

[060-048] ever knew to exist without inhuman sexlessness. Why do you talk in such

[060-049] a changed way? We have not been selfish, except when no one could

[060-050] profit by our being otherwise. You used to say that human nature was

[060-051] noble and long-suffering, not vile and corrupt, and at last I thought

[060-053] “I want a humble heart; and a chastened mind; and I have never had them

[060-054] yet!”

[060-055] “You have been fearless, both as a thinker and as a feeler, and you

[060-056] deserved more admiration than I gave. I was too full of narrow dogmas

[060-057] at that time to see it.”

[060-058] “Don’t say that, Jude! I wish my every fearless word and thought could

[060-059] be rooted out of my history. Self-renunciation—that’s everything! I

[060-060] cannot humiliate myself too much. I should like to prick myself all

[060-064] remorse is not for you, my sensitive plant, but for the wicked ones of

[060-068] “Why not?”

[060-069] “It is indulgence.”

[060-070] “Still on the same tack! But is there anything better on earth than

[060-072] “Yes. It depends on the sort of love; and yours—ours—is the wrong.”

[060-073] “I won’t have it, Sue! Come, when do you wish our marriage to be signed

[060-086] “I have been to the service at St. Silas’, and I was in the fumes of

[060-089] “Yes. I go there sometimes.”

[060-090] “Indeed. You go there!”

[060-098] “Well—a sceptic, to be plain.”

[060-099] “How can you pain me so, dear Jude, in my trouble! Yet I know you

[060-101] “I won’t. But I am much surprised!”

[060-114] “I don’t think I am!”

[060-115] “Not? But suppose we _had_ gone through the ceremony? Would you feel

[060-117] “No. I should not feel even then that I was. I should feel worse than I

[060-120] “Because I am Richard’s.”

[060-121] “Ah—you hinted that absurd fancy to me before!”

[060-122] “It was only an impression with me then; I feel more and more convinced

[060-124] “My good heavens—how we are changing places!”

[060-125] “Yes. Perhaps so.”

[060-127] sitting in the same small room downstairs, when a knock came to the

[060-128] front door of the carpenter’s house where they were lodging, and in a

[060-129] few moments there was a tap at the door of their room. Before they

[060-130] could open it the comer did so, and a woman’s form appeared.

[060-131] “Is Mr. Fawley here?”

[060-132] Jude and Sue started as he mechanically replied in the affirmative, for

[060-133] the voice was Arabella’s.

[060-134] He formally requested her to come in, and she sat down in the window

[060-135] bench, where they could distinctly see her outline against the light;

[060-136] but no characteristic that enabled them to estimate her general aspect

[060-137] and air. Yet something seemed to denote that she was not quite so

[060-138] comfortably circumstanced, nor so bouncingly attired, as she had been

[060-139] during Cartlett’s lifetime.

[060-140] The three attempted an awkward conversation about the tragedy, of which

[060-141] Jude had felt it to be his duty to inform her immediately, though she

[060-142] had never replied to his letter.

[060-143] “I have just come from the cemetery,” she said. “I inquired and found

[060-144] the child’s grave. I couldn’t come to the funeral—thank you for

[060-148] manner, fumbled with iterations. “But I am glad I found the grave. As

[060-152] “He was my child, and naturally I feel for him.”

[060-153] “I hope so. We all did.”

[060-154] “The others that weren’t mine I didn’t feel so much for, as was

[060-155] natural.”

[060-156] “Of course.”
