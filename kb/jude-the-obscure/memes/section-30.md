[030-001] “What’s the matter, Jude?” she said suddenly.

[030-002] He was leaning with his elbows on the table and his chin on his hands,

[030-003] looking into a futurity which seemed to be sketched out on the

[030-004] tablecloth.

[030-005] “Oh—nothing!”

[030-006] “You are ‘father’, you know. That’s what they call the man who gives

[030-007] you away.”

[030-008] Jude could have said “Phillotson’s age entitles him to be called that!”

[030-009] But he would not annoy her by such a cheap retort.

[030-010] She talked incessantly, as if she dreaded his indulgence in reflection,

[030-011] and before the meal was over both he and she wished they had not put

[030-012] such confidence in their new view of things, and had taken breakfast

[030-013] apart. What oppressed Jude was the thought that, having done a wrong

[030-014] thing of this sort himself, he was aiding and abetting the woman he

[030-015] loved in doing a like wrong thing, instead of imploring and warning her

[030-016] against it. It was on his tongue to say, “You have quite made up your

[030-017] mind?”

[030-018] After breakfast they went out on an errand together moved by a mutual

[030-019] thought that it was the last opportunity they would have of indulging

[030-020] in unceremonious companionship. By the irony of fate, and the curious

[030-021] trick in Sue’s nature of tempting Providence at critical times, she

[030-022] took his arm as they walked through the muddy street—a thing she had

[030-023] never done before in her life—and on turning the corner they found

[030-024] themselves close to a grey perpendicular church with a low-pitched

[030-025] roof—the church of St. Thomas.

[030-026] “That’s the church,” said Jude.

[030-027] “Where I am going to be married?”

[030-028] “Yes.”

[030-029] “Indeed!” she exclaimed with curiosity. “How I should like to go in and

[030-030] see what the spot is like where I am so soon to kneel and do it.”

[030-031] Again he said to himself, “She does not realize what marriage means!”

[030-032] He passively acquiesced in her wish to go in, and they entered by the

[030-033] western door. The only person inside the gloomy building was a

[030-034] charwoman cleaning. Sue still held Jude’s arm, almost as if she loved

[030-035] him. Cruelly sweet, indeed, she had been to him that morning; but his

[030-036] thoughts of a penance in store for her were tempered by an ache:

[030-037] … I can find no way

[030-038] How a blow should fall, such as falls on men,

[030-039] Nor prove too much for your womanhood!

[030-040] They strolled undemonstratively up the nave towards the altar railing,

[030-041] which they stood against in silence, turning then and walking down the

[030-042] nave again, her hand still on his arm, precisely like a couple just

[030-043] married. The too suggestive incident, entirely of her making, nearly

[030-044] broke down Jude.

[030-045] “I like to do things like this,” she said in the delicate voice of an

[030-046] epicure in emotions, which left no doubt that she spoke the truth.

[030-047] “I know you do!” said Jude.

[030-048] “They are interesting, because they have probably never been done

[030-049] before. I shall walk down the church like this with my husband in about

[030-050] two hours, shan’t I!”

[030-051] “No doubt you will!”

[030-052] “Was it like this when you were married?”

[030-053] “Good God, Sue—don’t be so awfully merciless! … There, dear one, I

[030-054] didn’t mean it!”

[030-055] “Ah—you are vexed!” she said regretfully, as she blinked away an access

[030-056] of eye moisture. “And I promised never to vex you! … I suppose I ought

[030-057] not to have asked you to bring me in here. Oh, I oughtn’t! I see it

[030-058] now. My curiosity to hunt up a new sensation always leads me into these

[030-059] scrapes. Forgive me! … You will, won’t you, Jude?”

[030-060] The appeal was so remorseful that Jude’s eyes were even wetter than

[030-061] hers as he pressed her hand for Yes.

[030-062] “Now we’ll hurry away, and I won’t do it any more!” she continued

[030-063] humbly; and they came out of the building, Sue intending to go on to

[030-064] the station to meet Phillotson. But the first person they encountered

[030-065] on entering the main street was the schoolmaster himself, whose train

[030-066] had arrived sooner than Sue expected. There was nothing really to demur

[030-067] to in her leaning on Jude’s arm; but she withdrew her hand, and Jude

[030-068] thought that Phillotson had looked surprised.

[030-069] “We have been doing such a funny thing!” said she, smiling candidly.

[030-070] “We’ve been to the church, rehearsing as it were. Haven’t we, Jude?”

[030-071] “How?” said Phillotson curiously.

[030-072] Jude inwardly deplored what he thought to be unnecessary frankness; but

[030-073] she had gone too far not to explain all, which she accordingly did,

[030-074] telling him how they had marched up to the altar.

[030-075] Seeing how puzzled Phillotson seemed, Jude said as cheerfully as he

[030-076] could, “I am going to buy her another little present. Will you both

[030-077] come to the shop with me?”

[030-078] “No,” said Sue, “I’ll go on to the house with him”; and requesting her

[030-079] lover not to be a long time she departed with the schoolmaster.

[030-080] Jude soon joined them at his rooms, and shortly after they prepared for

[030-081] the ceremony. Phillotson’s hair was brushed to a painful extent, and

[030-082] his shirt collar appeared stiffer than it had been for the previous

[030-083] twenty years. Beyond this he looked dignified and thoughtful, and

[030-084] altogether a man of whom it was not unsafe to predict that he would

[030-085] make a kind and considerate husband. That he adored Sue was obvious;

[030-086] and she could almost be seen to feel that she was undeserving his

[030-087] adoration.

[030-088] Although the distance was so short he had hired a fly from the Red

[030-089] Lion, and six or seven women and children had gathered by the door when

[030-090] they came out. The schoolmaster and Sue were unknown, though Jude was

[030-091] getting to be recognized as a citizen; and the couple were judged to be

[030-092] some relations of his from a distance, nobody supposing Sue to have

[030-093] been a recent pupil at the training school.

[030-094] In the carriage Jude took from his pocket his extra little

[030-095] wedding-present, which turned out to be two or three yards of white

[030-096] tulle, which he threw over her bonnet and all, as a veil.

[030-097] “It looks so odd over a bonnet,” she said. “I’ll take the bonnet off.”

[030-098] “Oh no—let it stay,” said Phillotson. And she obeyed.

[030-099] When they had passed up the church and were standing in their places

[030-100] Jude found that the antecedent visit had certainly taken off the edge

[030-101] of this performance, but by the time they were half-way on with the

[030-102] service he wished from his heart that he had not undertaken the

[030-103] business of giving her away. How could Sue have had the temerity to ask

[030-104] him to do it—a cruelty possibly to herself as well as to him? Women

[030-105] were different from men in such matters. Was it that they were, instead

[030-106] of more sensitive, as reputed, more callous, and less romantic; or were

[030-107] they more heroic? Or was Sue simply so perverse that she wilfully gave

[030-108] herself and him pain for the odd and mournful luxury of practising

[030-109] long-suffering in her own person, and of being touched with tender pity

[030-110] for him at having made him practise it? He could perceive that her face

[030-111] was nervously set, and when they reached the trying ordeal of Jude

[030-112] giving her to Phillotson she could hardly command herself; rather,

[030-113] however, as it seemed, from her knowledge of what her cousin must feel,

[030-114] whom she need not have had there at all, than from self-consideration.

[030-115] Possibly she would go on inflicting such pains again and again, and

[030-116] grieving for the sufferer again and again, in all her colossal

[030-117] inconsistency.

[030-118] Phillotson seemed not to notice, to be surrounded by a mist which

[030-119] prevented his seeing the emotions of others. As soon as they had signed

[030-120] their names and come away, and the suspense was over, Jude felt

[030-121] relieved.

[030-122] The meal at his lodging was a very simple affair, and at two o’clock

[030-123] they went off. In crossing the pavement to the fly she looked back; and

[030-124] there was a frightened light in her eyes. Could it be that Sue had

[030-125] acted with such unusual foolishness as to plunge into she knew not what

[030-126] for the sake of asserting her independence of him, of retaliating on

[030-127] him for his secrecy? Perhaps Sue was thus venturesome with men because

[030-128] she was childishly ignorant of that side of their natures which wore

[030-129] out women’s hearts and lives.

[030-130] When her foot was on the carriage-step she turned round, saying that

[030-131] she had forgotten something. Jude and the landlady offered to get it.

[030-132] “No,” she said, running back. “It is my handkerchief. I know where I

[030-133] left it.”

[030-134] Jude followed her back. She had found it, and came holding it in her

[030-135] hand. She looked into his eyes with her own tearful ones, and her lips

[030-136] suddenly parted as if she were going to avow something. But she went

[030-137] on; and whatever she had meant to say remained unspoken.

[030-139] Jude wondered if she had really left her handkerchief behind; or

[030-140] whether it were that she had miserably wished to tell him of a love

[030-141] that at the last moment she could not bring herself to express.

[030-142] He could not stay in his silent lodging when they were gone, and

[030-143] fearing that he might be tempted to drown his misery in alcohol he went

[030-144] upstairs, changed his dark clothes for his white, his thin boots for

[030-145] his thick, and proceeded to his customary work for the afternoon.

[030-146] But in the cathedral he seemed to hear a voice behind him, and to be

[030-147] possessed with an idea that she would come back. She could not possibly

[030-148] go home with Phillotson, he fancied. The feeling grew and stirred. The

[030-149] moment that the clock struck the last of his working hours he threw

[030-150] down his tools and rushed homeward. “Has anybody been for me?” he

[030-151] asked.

[030-152] Nobody had been there.

[030-153] As he could claim the downstairs sitting-room till twelve o’clock that

[030-154] night he sat in it all the evening; and even when the clock had struck

[030-155] eleven, and the family had retired, he could not shake off the feeling

[030-156] that she would come back and sleep in the little room adjoining his own

[030-157] in which she had slept so many previous days. Her actions were always

[030-158] unpredictable: why should she not come? Gladly would he have compounded

[030-159] for the denial of her as a sweetheart and wife by having her live thus

[030-160] as a fellow-lodger and friend, even on the most distant terms. His

[030-161] supper still remained spread, and going to the front door, and softly

[030-162] setting it open, he returned to the room and sat as watchers sit on

[030-163] Old-Midsummer eves, expecting the phantom of the Beloved. But she did

[030-164] not come.

[030-165] Having indulged in this wild hope he went upstairs, and looked out of

[030-166] the window, and pictured her through the evening journey to London,

[030-167] whither she and Phillotson had gone for their holiday; their rattling

[030-168] along through the damp night to their hotel, under the same sky of

[030-169] ribbed cloud as that he beheld, through which the moon showed its

[030-170] position rather than its shape, and one or two of the larger stars made

[030-171] themselves visible as faint nebulæ only. It was a new beginning of

[030-172] Sue’s history. He projected his mind into the future, and saw her with

[030-173] children more or less in her own likeness around her. But the

[030-174] consolation of regarding them as a continuation of her identity was

[030-175] denied to him, as to all such dreamers, by the wilfulness of Nature in

[030-176] not allowing issue from one parent alone. Every desired renewal of an

[030-177] existence is debased by being half alloy. “If at the estrangement or

[030-178] death of my lost love, I could go and see her child—hers solely—there

[030-179] would be comfort in it!” said Jude. And then he again uneasily saw, as

[030-180] he had latterly seen with more and more frequency, the scorn of Nature

[030-181] for man’s finer emotions, and her lack of interest in his aspirations.

[030-182] The oppressive strength of his affection for Sue showed itself on the

[030-183] morrow and following days yet more clearly. He could no longer endure

[030-184] the light of the Melchester lamps; the sunshine was as drab paint, and

[030-185] the blue sky as zinc. Then he received news that his old aunt was

[030-186] dangerously ill at Marygreen, which intelligence almost coincided with

[030-187] a letter from his former employer at Christminster, who offered him

[030-188] permanent work of a good class if he would come back. The letters were

[030-189] almost a relief to him. He started to visit Aunt Drusilla, and resolved

[030-190] to go onward to Christminster to see what worth there might be in the

[030-191] builder’s offer.
