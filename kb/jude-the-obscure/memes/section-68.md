[068-001] He never said another word to her about his wish or his conjecture. A

[068-002] silent, undiscerned resolve grew up in him, which gave him, if not

[068-003] strength, stability and calm. One midday when, after an absence of two

[068-004] hours, she came into the room, she beheld the chair empty.

[068-005] Down she flopped on the bed, and sitting, meditated. “Now where the

[068-006] devil is my man gone to!” she said.

[068-007] A driving rain from the north-east had been falling with more or less

[068-008] intermission all the morning, and looking from the window at the

[068-009] dripping spouts it seemed impossible to believe that any sick man would

[068-010] have ventured out to almost certain death. Yet a conviction possessed

[068-011] Arabella that he had gone out, and it became a certainty when she had

[068-012] searched the house. “If he’s such a fool, let him be!” she said. “I can

[068-013] do no more.”

[068-014] Jude was at that moment in a railway train that was drawing near to

[068-015] Alfredston, oddly swathed, pale as a monumental figure in alabaster,

[068-016] and much stared at by other passengers. An hour later his thin form, in

[068-017] the long great-coat and blanket he had come with, but without an

[068-018] umbrella, could have been seen walking along the five-mile road to

[068-019] Marygreen. On his face showed the determined purpose that alone

[068-020] sustained him, but to which has weakness afforded a sorry foundation.

[068-021] By the up-hill walk he was quite blown, but he pressed on; and at

[068-022] half-past three o’clock stood by the familiar well at Marygreen. The

[068-023] rain was keeping everybody indoors; Jude crossed the green to the

[068-024] church without observation, and found the building open. Here he stood,

[068-025] looking forth at the school, whence he could hear the usual sing-song

[068-026] tones of the little voices that had not learnt Creation’s groan.

[068-027] He waited till a small boy came from the school—one evidently allowed

[068-028] out before hours for some reason or other. Jude held up his hand, and

[068-029] the child came.

[068-030] “Please call at the schoolhouse and ask Mrs. Phillotson if she will be

[068-031] kind enough to come to the church for a few minutes.”

[068-032] The child departed, and Jude heard him knock at the door of the

[068-033] dwelling. He himself went further into the church. Everything was new,

[068-034] except a few pieces of carving preserved from the wrecked old fabric,

[068-035] now fixed against the new walls. He stood by these: they seemed akin to

[068-036] the perished people of that place who were his ancestors and Sue’s.

[068-037] A light footstep, which might have been accounted no more than an added

[068-038] drip to the rainfall, sounded in the porch, and he looked round.

[068-039] “Oh—I didn’t think it was you! I didn’t—Oh, Jude!” A hysterical catch

[068-040] in her breath ended in a succession of them. He advanced, but she

[068-041] quickly recovered and went back.

[068-042] “Don’t go—don’t go!” he implored. “This is my last time! I thought it

[068-043] would be less intrusive than to enter your house. And I shall never

[068-044] come again. Don’t then be unmerciful. Sue, Sue! We are acting by the

[068-045] letter; and ‘the letter killeth’!”

[068-046] “I’ll stay—I won’t be unkind!” she said, her mouth quivering and her

[068-047] tears flowing as she allowed him to come closer. “But why did you come,

[068-048] and do this wrong thing, after doing such a right thing as you have

[068-049] done?”

[068-050] “What right thing?”

[068-051] “Marrying Arabella again. It was in the Alfredston paper. She has never

[068-052] been other than yours, Jude—in a proper sense. And therefore you did so

[068-053] well—Oh so well!—in recognizing it—and taking her to you again.”

[068-054] “God above—and is that all I’ve come to hear? If there is anything more

[068-055] degrading, immoral, unnatural, than another in my life, it is this

[068-056] meretricious contract with Arabella which has been called doing the

[068-057] right thing! And you too—you call yourself Phillotson’s wife! _His_

[068-058] wife! You are mine.”

[068-059] “Don’t make me rush away from you—I can’t bear much! But on this point

[068-060] I am decided.”

[068-061] “I cannot understand how you did it—how you think it—I cannot!”

[068-062] “Never mind that. He is a kind husband to me—And I—I’ve wrestled and

[068-063] struggled, and fasted, and prayed. I have nearly brought my body into

[068-064] complete subjection. And you mustn’t—will you—wake—”

[068-065] “Oh you darling little fool; where is your reason? You seem to have

[068-066] suffered the loss of your faculties! I would argue with you if I didn’t

[068-067] know that a woman in your state of feeling is quite beyond all appeals

[068-068] to her brains. Or is it that you are humbugging yourself, as so many

[068-069] women do about these things; and don’t actually believe what you

[068-070] pretend to, and only are indulging in the luxury of the emotion raised

[068-071] by an affected belief?”

[068-072] “Luxury! How can you be so cruel!”

[068-073] “You dear, sad, soft, most melancholy wreck of a promising human

[068-074] intellect that it has ever been my lot to behold! Where is your scorn

[068-075] of convention gone? I _would_ have died game!”

[068-076] “You crush, almost insult me, Jude! Go away from me!” She turned off

[068-077] quickly.

[068-078] “I will. I would never come to see you again, even if I had the

[068-079] strength to come, which I shall not have any more. Sue, Sue, you are

[068-080] not worth a man’s love!”

[068-081] Her bosom began to go up and down. “I can’t endure you to say that!”

[068-082] she burst out, and her eye resting on him a moment, she turned back

[068-083] impulsively. “Don’t, don’t scorn me! Kiss me, oh kiss me lots of times,

[068-084] and say I am not a coward and a contemptible humbug—I can’t bear it!”

[068-085] She rushed up to him and, with her mouth on his, continued: “I must

[068-086] tell you—oh I must—my darling Love! It has been—only a church

[068-087] marriage—an apparent marriage I mean! He suggested it at the very

[068-088] first!”

[068-089] “How?”

[068-090] “I mean it is a nominal marriage only. It hasn’t been more than that at

[068-091] all since I came back to him!”

[068-092] “Sue!” he said. Pressing her to him in his arms, he bruised her lips

[068-093] with kisses. “If misery can know happiness, I have a moment’s happiness

[068-094] now! Now, in the name of all you hold holy, tell me the truth, and no

[068-095] lie. You do love me still?”

[068-096] “I do! You know it too well! … But I _mustn’t_ do this! I mustn’t kiss

[068-097] you back as I would!”

[068-098] “But do!”

[068-099] “And yet you are so dear!—and you look so ill—”

[068-100] “And so do you! There’s one more, in memory of our dead little

[068-101] children—yours and mine!”

[068-102] The words struck her like a blow, and she bent her head. “I _mustn’t_—I

[068-103] _can’t_ go on with this!” she gasped presently. “But there, there,

[068-104] darling; I give you back your kisses; I do, I do! … And now I’ll _hate_

[068-105] myself for ever for my sin!”

[068-106] “No—let me make my last appeal. Listen to this! We’ve both remarried

[068-107] out of our senses. I was made drunk to do it. You were the same. I was

[068-108] gin-drunk; you were creed-drunk. Either form of intoxication takes away

[068-109] the nobler vision… Let us then shake off our mistakes, and run away

[068-110] together!”

[068-111] “No; again no! … Why do you tempt me so far, Jude! It is too merciless!

[068-112] … But I’ve got over myself now. Don’t follow me—don’t look at me. Leave

[068-113] me, for pity’s sake!”

[068-114] She ran up the church to the east end, and Jude did as she requested.

[068-115] He did not turn his head, but took up his blanket, which she had not

[068-116] seen, and went straight out. As he passed the end of the church she

[068-117] heard his coughs mingling with the rain on the windows, and in a last

[068-118] instinct of human affection, even now unsubdued by her fetters, she

[068-119] sprang up as if to go and succour him. But she knelt down again, and

[068-120] stopped her ears with her hands till all possible sound of him had

[068-121] passed away.

[068-122] He was by this time at the corner of the green, from which the path ran

[068-123] across the fields in which he had scared rooks as a boy. He turned and

[068-124] looked back, once, at the building which still contained Sue; and then

[068-125] went on, knowing that his eyes would light on that scene no more.

[068-126] There are cold spots up and down Wessex in autumn and winter weather;

[068-127] but the coldest of all when a north or east wind is blowing is the

[068-128] crest of the down by the Brown House, where the road to Alfredston

[068-129] crosses the old Ridgeway. Here the first winter sleets and snows fall

[068-130] and lie, and here the spring frost lingers last unthawed. Here in the

[068-131] teeth of the north-east wind and rain Jude now pursued his way, wet

[068-132] through, the necessary slowness of his walk from lack of his former

[068-133] strength being insufficent to maintain his heat. He came to the

[068-134] milestone, and, raining as it was, spread his blanket and lay down

[068-135] there to rest. Before moving on he went and felt at the back of the

[068-136] stone for his own carving. It was still there; but nearly obliterated

[068-137] by moss. He passed the spot where the gibbet of his ancestor and Sue’s

[068-138] had stood, and descended the hill.

[068-139] It was dark when he reached Alfredston, where he had a cup of tea, the

[068-140] deadly chill that began to creep into his bones being too much for him

[068-141] to endure fasting. To get home he had to travel by a steam tram-car,

[068-142] and two branches of railway, with much waiting at a junction. He did

[068-143] not reach Christminster till ten o’clock.

[068-144] IX

[068-145] On the platform stood Arabella. She looked him up and down.

[068-146] “You’ve been to see her?” she asked.

[068-147] “I have,” said Jude, literally tottering with cold and lassitude.

[068-148] “Well, now you’d best march along home.”

[068-149] The water ran out of him as he went, and he was compelled to lean

[068-150] against the wall to support himself while coughing.

[068-151] “You’ve done for yourself by this, young man,” said she. “I don’t know

[068-152] whether you know it.”

[068-153] “Of course I do. I meant to do for myself.”

[068-154] “What—to commit suicide?”

[068-155] “Certainly.”

[068-156] “Well, I’m blest! Kill yourself for a woman.”

[068-157] “Listen to me, Arabella. You think you are the stronger; and so you

[068-158] are, in a physical sense, now. You could push me over like a nine-pin.

[068-159] You did not send that letter the other day, and I could not resent your

[068-160] conduct. But I am not so weak in another way as you think. I made up my

[068-161] mind that a man confined to his room by inflammation of the lungs, a

[068-162] fellow who had only two wishes left in the world, to see a particular

[068-163] woman, and then to die, could neatly accomplish those two wishes at one

[068-164] stroke by taking this journey in the rain. That I’ve done. I have seen

[068-165] her for the last time, and I’ve finished myself—put an end to a

[068-166] feverish life which ought never to have been begun!”

[068-167] “Lord—you do talk lofty! Won’t you have something warm to drink?”

[068-168] “No thank you. Let’s get home.”

[068-169] They went along by the silent colleges, and Jude kept stopping.

[068-170] “What are you looking at?”

[068-171] “Stupid fancies. I see, in a way, those spirits of the dead again, on

[068-172] this my last walk, that I saw when I first walked here!”

[068-173] “What a curious chap you are!”

[068-174] “I seem to see them, and almost hear them rustling. But I don’t revere

[068-175] all of them as I did then. I don’t believe in half of them. The

[068-176] theologians, the apologists, and their kin the metaphysicians, the

[068-177] high-handed statesmen, and others, no longer interest me. All that has

[068-178] been spoilt for me by the grind of stern reality!”

[068-179] The expression of Jude’s corpselike face in the watery lamplight was

[068-180] indeed as if he saw people where there was nobody. At moments he stood

[068-181] still by an archway, like one watching a figure walk out; then he would

[068-182] look at a window like one discerning a familiar face behind it. He

[068-183] seemed to hear voices, whose words he repeated as if to gather their

[068-184] meaning.
