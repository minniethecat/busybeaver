# Remembrance Day

The Remembrance Day (Commemoration Day) ceremony at [[Christminster]] is the novel's culminating scene of exclusion — a university ritual celebrating the academic community to which [[Jude Fawley]] has spent his life aspiring, and which he watches from the margins as a dying man. It is the structural bookend to the novel's opening: Jude first saw Christminster as a distant vision of hope; he last sees it as an inaccessible ceremony of belonging.

## Background

Remembrance Day (also called Commemoration Day or Encaenia at Oxford) is an annual ceremony at Christminster honoring the university's founders and benefactors. It is a ritual of institutional memory — the recalling of the "religious school" whose founders "most real to Jude Fawley were" (013-159). The ceremony includes a procession of dignitaries, Latin orations, and the awarding of honorary degrees.

For Jude, the ceremony represents everything he has been denied: membership in a scholarly community, recognition of intellectual achievement, and a place within the institutional structures that define the city. He attends it not as a participant but as a spectator — and not even as a healthy spectator, but as a man so ill he can barely stand.

## In *Jude the Obscure*

### The Scene

The Remembrance Day scene occurs in Part Sixth, the novel's final section. Jude, separated from Sue and dying of consumption, has returned to Christminster — the city that once promised everything. He takes lodging near the colleges and, on the day of the ceremony, drags himself to the theater where the rites are performed.

The scene unfolds in precise sensory detail. Jude "hastened round into Chief Street, and when near the theatre could hear the notes of the organ" (070-133) — the organ, that signature Christminster instrument, playing a rehearsal for a coming concert. The music draws him forward into the ceremony's orbit. "Almost as soon as they had arrived the grand procession" (071-045) of dignitaries begins, and the crowd presses close.

Arabella identifies what is happening: "Oh, that's the Doctors in the theatre, conferring Honorary degrees" (071-116). The Latin orations float down from the open windows: "An occasional word, as from some one making a speech, floated from the open windows of the theatre across to this quiet corner" (071-120). Jude, who once taught himself Latin by candlelight in Marygreen, can understand every word — and this comprehension is the cruelest irony. The language that was to be his passport has become a foreign tongue spoken behind walls.

The earlier commencement scene provides the template for this final exclusion. At the "old round theatre of Wren a cheer rose high" (057-032) as "the procession of heads of houses and new Doctors emerged" (057-028). A bystander explains: "you may hear a few words of the Latin speech by staying here; the windows are open" (057-041). The open window — Hardy's recurring image of permeable exclusion — lets the sound through while keeping the listener out.

Earlier, the rain falls on the spectators: "The rain came on more heavily" (057-018) — a Hardyan detail that strips the ceremony of its dignity and reveals it as a human ritual, contingent and indifferent. The same rain falls on Jude and the Doctors alike, but only the Doctors proceed under the theatre's shelter.

The scene is structured as a triple exclusion: Jude is outside the ceremony physically (he cannot enter the theatre), socially (he is not a member of the university), and existentially (he is dying). Arabella, now remarried to him, watches with detached pragmatism. Sue, now remarried to Phillotson, is absent — but the ceremony's language of memory and belonging makes Jude's isolation absolute.

### Narrative Significance

The Remembrance Day scene is Hardy's most concentrated use of irony. Every element of the ceremony — the Latin Jude once taught himself, the founders he once imagined as companions, the community he once dreamed of joining — is present but inaccessible. The ceremony remembers those who built the institution; Jude, who repaired its stonework with his hands, will be forgotten.

Hardy's staging is precise: the open windows, the floating speech, the rain, the cheers. These details construct a world in which Jude can witness everything and participate in nothing. The scene's power lies in its refusal of melodrama — Jude dies quietly, offstage from the ceremony, acknowledged by no one in the theatre.

## Critical Interpretation

The Remembrance Day scene has been read as Hardy's most autobiographical passage: the working-class autodidact attending (and excluded from) the rituals of institutional learning. The open windows — through which Latin speech "floated" (071-120) to Jude's ears — are a precise metaphor for the partial access that defines his entire life. He can hear, he can understand, but he cannot enter.

The scene anticipates modernist treatments of ritual and alienation — T.S. Eliot's "The Waste Land" and Joyce's "Ulysses" would explore similar territory two decades later. But Hardy's version is characteristically democratic: the ceremony is not mocked but simply shown to be irrelevant to the human suffering that unfolds at its margins.
