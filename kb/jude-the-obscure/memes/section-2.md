[002-001] The local washerwoman replied that it was perhaps a very good plan of

[002-002] Miss or Mrs. Fawley’s (as they called her indifferently) to have him

[002-003] with her—“to kip ’ee company in your loneliness, fetch water, shet the

[002-004] winder-shetters o’ nights, and help in the bit o’ baking.”

[002-005] Miss Fawley doubted it. … “Why didn’t ye get the schoolmaster to take

[002-006] ’ee to Christminster wi’ un, and make a scholar of ’ee,” she continued,

[002-007] in frowning pleasantry. “I’m sure he couldn’t ha’ took a better one.

[002-008] The boy is crazy for books, that he is. It runs in our family rather.

[002-009] His cousin Sue is just the same—so I’ve heard; but I have not seen the

[002-010] child for years, though she was born in this place, within these four

[002-011] walls, as it happened. My niece and her husband, after they were

[002-012] married, didn’ get a house of their own for some year or more; and then

[002-013] they only had one till—Well, I won’t go into that. Jude, my child,

[002-014] don’t you ever marry. ’Tisn’t for the Fawleys to take that step any

[002-015] more. She, their only one, was like a child o’ my own, Belinda, till

[002-016] the split come! Ah, that a little maid should know such changes!”

[002-017] Jude, finding the general attention again centering on himself, went

[002-018] out to the bakehouse, where he ate the cake provided for his breakfast.

[002-019] The end of his spare time had now arrived, and emerging from the garden

[002-020] by getting over the hedge at the back he pursued a path northward, till

[002-021] he came to a wide and lonely depression in the general level of the

[002-022] upland, which was sown as a corn-field. This vast concave was the scene

[002-023] of his labours for Mr. Troutham the farmer, and he descended into the

[002-024] midst of it.

[002-025] The brown surface of the field went right up towards the sky all round,

[002-026] where it was lost by degrees in the mist that shut out the actual verge

[002-027] and accentuated the solitude. The only marks on the uniformity of the

[002-028] scene were a rick of last year’s produce standing in the midst of the

[002-029] arable, the rooks that rose at his approach, and the path athwart the

[002-030] fallow by which he had come, trodden now by he hardly knew whom, though

[002-031] once by many of his own dead family.

[002-032] “How ugly it is here!” he murmured.

[002-033] The fresh harrow-lines seemed to stretch like the channellings in a

[002-034] piece of new corduroy, lending a meanly utilitarian air to the expanse,

[002-035] taking away its gradations, and depriving it of all history beyond that

[002-036] of the few recent months, though to every clod and stone there really

[002-037] attached associations enough and to spare—echoes of songs from ancient

[002-038] harvest-days, of spoken words, and of sturdy deeds. Every inch of

[002-039] ground had been the site, first or last, of energy, gaiety, horse-play,

[002-040] bickerings, weariness. Groups of gleaners had squatted in the sun on

[002-041] every square yard. Love-matches that had populated the adjoining hamlet

[002-042] had been made up there between reaping and carrying. Under the hedge

[002-043] which divided the field from a distant plantation girls had given

[002-044] themselves to lovers who would not turn their heads to look at them by

[002-045] the next harvest; and in that ancient cornfield many a man had made

[002-046] love-promises to a woman at whose voice he had trembled by the next

[002-047] seed-time after fulfilling them in the church adjoining. But this

[002-048] neither Jude nor the rooks around him considered. For them it was a

[002-049] lonely place, possessing, in the one view, only the quality of a

[002-050] work-ground, and in the other that of a granary good to feed in.

[002-051] The boy stood under the rick before mentioned, and every few seconds

[002-052] used his clacker or rattle briskly. At each clack the rooks left off

[002-053] pecking, and rose and went away on their leisurely wings, burnished

[002-054] like tassets of mail, afterwards wheeling back and regarding him

[002-055] warily, and descending to feed at a more respectful distance.

[002-056] He sounded the clacker till his arm ached, and at length his heart grew

[002-057] sympathetic with the birds’ thwarted desires. They seemed, like

[002-058] himself, to be living in a world which did not want them. Why should he

[002-059] frighten them away? They took upon more and more the aspect of gentle

[002-060] friends and pensioners—the only friends he could claim as being in the

[002-061] least degree interested in him, for his aunt had often told him that

[002-062] she was not. He ceased his rattling, and they alighted anew.

[002-063] “Poor little dears!” said Jude, aloud. “You _shall_ have some

[002-064] dinner—you shall. There is enough for us all. Farmer Troutham can

[002-065] afford to let you have some. Eat, then my dear little birdies, and make

[002-066] a good meal!”

[002-067] They stayed and ate, inky spots on the nut-brown soil, and Jude enjoyed

[002-068] their appetite. A magic thread of fellow-feeling united his own life

[002-069] with theirs. Puny and sorry as those lives were, they much resembled

[002-070] his own.

[002-071] His clacker he had by this time thrown away from him, as being a mean

[002-072] and sordid instrument, offensive both to the birds and to himself as

[002-073] their friend. All at once he became conscious of a smart blow upon his

[002-074] buttocks, followed by a loud clack, which announced to his surprised

[002-075] senses that the clacker had been the instrument of offence used. The

[002-076] birds and Jude started up simultaneously, and the dazed eyes of the

[002-077] latter beheld the farmer in person, the great Troutham himself, his red

[002-078] face glaring down upon Jude’s cowering frame, the clacker swinging in

[002-079] his hand.

[002-080] “So it’s ‘Eat my dear birdies,’ is it, young man? ‘Eat, dear birdies,’

[002-081] indeed! I’ll tickle your breeches, and see if you say, ‘Eat, dear

[002-082] birdies,’ again in a hurry! And you’ve been idling at the

[002-083] schoolmaster’s too, instead of coming here, ha’n’t ye, hey? That’s how

[002-084] you earn your sixpence a day for keeping the rooks off my corn!”

[002-085] Whilst saluting Jude’s ears with this impassioned rhetoric, Troutham

[002-086] had seized his left hand with his own left, and swinging his slim frame

[002-087] round him at arm’s-length, again struck Jude on the hind parts with the

[002-088] flat side of Jude’s own rattle, till the field echoed with the blows,

[002-089] which were delivered once or twice at each revolution.

[002-090] “Don’t ’ee, sir—please don’t ’ee!” cried the whirling child, as

[002-091] helpless under the centrifugal tendency of his person as a hooked fish

[002-092] swinging to land, and beholding the hill, the rick, the plantation, the

[002-093] path, and the rooks going round and round him in an amazing circular

[002-094] race. “I—I sir—only meant that—there was a good crop in the ground—I

[002-095] saw ’em sow it—and the rooks could have a little bit for dinner—and you

[002-096] wouldn’t miss it, sir—and Mr. Phillotson said I was to be kind to

[002-097] ’em—oh, oh, oh!”

[002-098] This truthful explanation seemed to exasperate the farmer even more

[002-099] than if Jude had stoutly denied saying anything at all, and he still

[002-100] smacked the whirling urchin, the clacks of the instrument continuing to

[002-101] resound all across the field and as far as the ears of distant

[002-102] workers—who gathered thereupon that Jude was pursuing his business of

[002-103] clacking with great assiduity—and echoing from the brand-new church

[002-104] tower just behind the mist, towards the building of which structure the

[002-105] farmer had largely subscribed, to testify his love for God and man.

[002-106] Presently Troutham grew tired of his punitive task, and depositing the

[002-107] quivering boy on his legs, took a sixpence from his pocket and gave it

[002-108] him in payment for his day’s work, telling him to go home and never let

[002-109] him see him in one of those fields again.

[002-110] Jude leaped out of arm’s reach, and walked along the trackway

[002-111] weeping—not from the pain, though that was keen enough; not from the

[002-112] perception of the flaw in the terrestrial scheme, by which what was

[002-113] good for God’s birds was bad for God’s gardener; but with the awful

[002-114] sense that he had wholly disgraced himself before he had been a year in

[002-115] the parish, and hence might be a burden to his great-aunt for life.

[002-116] With this shadow on his mind he did not care to show himself in the

[002-117] village, and went homeward by a roundabout track behind a high hedge

[002-118] and across a pasture. Here he beheld scores of coupled earthworms lying

[002-119] half their length on the surface of the damp ground, as they always did

[002-120] in such weather at that time of the year. It was impossible to advance

[002-121] in regular steps without crushing some of them at each tread.

[002-122] Though Farmer Troutham had just hurt him, he was a boy who could not

[002-123] himself bear to hurt anything. He had never brought home a nest of

[002-124] young birds without lying awake in misery half the night after, and

[002-125] often reinstating them and the nest in their original place the next

[002-126] morning. He could scarcely bear to see trees cut down or lopped, from a

[002-127] fancy that it hurt them; and late pruning, when the sap was up and the

[002-128] tree bled profusely, had been a positive grief to him in his infancy.

[002-129] This weakness of character, as it may be called, suggested that he was

[002-130] the sort of man who was born to ache a good deal before the fall of the

[002-131] curtain upon his unnecessary life should signify that all was well with

[002-132] him again. He carefully picked his way on tiptoe among the earthworms,

[002-133] without killing a single one.

[002-134] On entering the cottage he found his aunt selling a penny loaf to a

[002-135] little girl, and when the customer was gone she said, “Well, how do you

[002-136] come to be back here in the middle of the morning like this?”

[002-137] “I’m turned away.”

[002-138] “What?”

[002-139] “Mr. Troutham have turned me away because I let the rooks have a few

[002-140] peckings of corn. And there’s my wages—the last I shall ever hae!”

[002-141] He threw the sixpence tragically on the table.

[002-142] “Ah!” said his aunt, suspending her breath. And she opened upon him a

[002-143] lecture on how she would now have him all the spring upon her hands

[002-144] doing nothing. “If you can’t skeer birds, what can ye do? There! don’t

[002-145] ye look so deedy! Farmer Troutham is not so much better than myself,

[002-146] come to that. But ’tis as Job said, ‘Now they that are younger than I

[002-147] have me in derision, whose fathers I would have disdained to have set

[002-148] with the dogs of my flock.’ His father was my father’s journeyman,

[002-149] anyhow, and I must have been a fool to let ’ee go to work for ’n, which

[002-150] I shouldn’t ha’ done but to keep ’ee out of mischty.”

[002-151] More angry with Jude for demeaning her by coming there than for

[002-152] dereliction of duty, she rated him primarily from that point of view,

[002-153] and only secondarily from a moral one.

[002-154] “Not that you should have let the birds eat what Farmer Troutham

[002-155] planted. Of course you was wrong in that. Jude, Jude, why didstn’t go

[002-156] off with that schoolmaster of thine to Christminster or somewhere? But,

[002-157] oh no—poor or’nary child—there never was any sprawl on thy side of the

[002-158] family, and never will be!”

[002-159] “Where is this beautiful city, Aunt—this place where Mr. Phillotson is

[002-160] gone to?” asked the boy, after meditating in silence.

[002-161] “Lord! you ought to know where the city of Christminster is. Near a

[002-162] score of miles from here. It is a place much too good for you ever to

[002-163] have much to do with, poor boy, I’m a-thinking.”

[002-164] “And will Mr. Phillotson always be there?”

[002-165] “How can I tell?”

[002-166] “Could I go to see him?”

[002-167] “Lord, no! You didn’t grow up hereabout, or you wouldn’t ask such as

[002-168] that. We’ve never had anything to do with folk in Christminster, nor

[002-169] folk in Christminster with we.”

[002-170] Jude went out, and, feeling more than ever his existence to be an

[002-171] undemanded one, he lay down upon his back on a heap of litter near the

[002-172] pig-sty. The fog had by this time become more translucent, and the

[002-173] position of the sun could be seen through it. He pulled his straw hat

[002-174] over his face, and peered through the interstices of the plaiting at

[002-175] the white brightness, vaguely reflecting. Growing up brought

[002-176] responsibilities, he found. Events did not rhyme quite as he had

[002-177] thought. Nature’s logic was too horrid for him to care for. That mercy

[002-178] towards one set of creatures was cruelty towards another sickened his

[002-179] sense of harmony. As you got older, and felt yourself to be at the

[002-180] centre of your time, and not at a point in its circumference, as you

[002-181] had felt when you were little, you were seized with a sort of

[002-182] shuddering, he perceived. All around you there seemed to be something

[002-183] glaring, garish, rattling, and the noises and glares hit upon the

[002-184] little cell called your life, and shook it, and warped it.
