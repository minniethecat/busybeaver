# Pig-Killing

The pig-killing in *Jude the Obscure* is a short, brutal domestic episode early in Jude and Arabella's marriage that Hardy uses to dramatise the collapse of Jude's intellectual ambitions into rural manual labour. The scene, in which Jude must slaughter the family pig after the professional pig-killer fails to arrive, is one of the novel's most concentrated metaphors for the way marriage and poverty combine to extinguish the life of the mind.

## Context

The pig-killing occurs shortly after Jude and Arabella's hasty marriage in Part First. Jude, who had dreamed of studying at Christminster and reading the Church Fathers, finds himself instead living in a cottage at Marygreen, tending to livestock with a wife whose interests extend no further than the pig-sty. The pig has been fattened for slaughter, a routine event in the rural economy, but one that Jude — a stonemason and would-be scholar — is temperamentally unsuited to perform.

### Preceding Events

Jude has married Arabella after being trapped by the belief that she is pregnant. The marriage has already cost him his books (sold for saucepans) and his plans for Christminster. The wedding itself (see [[wedding]]) was conducted with a haste that foreshadows its later dissolution. Now, in the weeks following the marriage, Jude finds himself progressively absorbed into the rhythms of peasant life — feeding pigs, repairing the cottage, and facing the grim necessity of slaughter.

## Key Details

### The Failed Pig-Killer

The episode begins with Jude's reluctant admission that the pig is ready for slaughter. He has arranged for a professional pig-killer to come and do the work, sparing himself the task. But on the appointed morning the pig-killer sends word that he cannot come: "'I'm afraid the pig-killer won't be able to come,' he said, coming in from the pig-sty with a troubled face" (011-005). The pig has already been starved in preparation; the slaughter cannot be delayed.

### The Killing

Jude must perform the killing himself. He is incompetent at the task. The pig's squeals fill the cottage; the blood spreads across the floor. Arabella, raised in a pig-breeding family, finds the scene unremarkable. Jude is shaken. The animal's cry changes register as Jude's knife fails to strike cleanly: "The animal's note changed its quality. It was not now rage, but the cry of despair; long-drawn, slow and hopeless" (011-050).

The killing is messy, prolonged, and undignified — the antithesis of the scholarly life Jude had imagined for himself. Afterwards, Arabella remarks casually that Jude's squeamishness is laughable, asking what he would have done "with such a messy job as a pig-killing" if he had no wife to manage the practical side of life (011-101).

### Location

The killing takes place at the pig-sty attached to the cottage Jude and Arabella share near Marygreen. The pig-sty, mentioned earlier in the novel as a detail of the rural landscape (002-172), is here transformed from pastoral backdrop into the site of Jude's mortification.

## Narrative Significance

The pig-killing is, in miniature, the tragedy of Jude's first marriage. Jude, who began the novel reading Greek in the cart-track to Christminster, ends Part First with his hands soaked in pig's blood. The scene is Hardy's bluntest statement on the incompatibility of intellectual aspiration and peasant necessity: Arabella's world is one of "pigs' chitterlings" (006-121) and pig-sties; Jude's was one of Greek texts and the Church Fathers. The slaughter forces Jude to confront the material realities that his books could never prepare him for.

The episode also foreshadows the novel's larger structure of entrapment. The pig, fattened and penned, awaiting a death it cannot escape, is an analogue for Jude himself — a creature whose fate is determined by forces beyond its understanding or control.
