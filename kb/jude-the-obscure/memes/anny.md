# Anny

Anny is a recurring minor character in *[[jude-fawley|Jude the Obscure]]* whose role bridges the novel's two central female figures. A friend of both [[Arabella-Donn]] and [[Sue-Bridehead]], she appears first as a young woman in the [[marygreen|Marygreen]]-Alfredston district and later as a married woman whose letters and visits keep [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] connected to the world outside her troubled domestic life. Anny's presence serves as a barometer of the changes time brings to ordinary country lives.

## Character Overview

Anny is introduced in the novel's early chapters as one of several young women in the rural community where [[Jude-Fawley]] and [[arabella-donn|Arabella]] first meet. She is part of a trio of girls — alongside [[arabella-donn|Arabella]] and Sarah — who work at labelling packages and engage in the kind of banter and gossip that Hardy uses to establish the texture of village life (010-037).

Her early characterisation is sharp and knowing. "Oh, Anny, how can you!" exclaims one of her companions, suggesting a penchant for provocative speech (006-131). She is described as speaking "judicially" on matters of courtship and reputation — a young woman already worldly in the ways of the marriage market (008-134).

## Role in the Narrative

### Early Appearances

Anny's early scenes establish her as a foil to [[arabella-donn|Arabella]]: where [[arabella-donn|Arabella]] is calculating and strategic in her pursuit of [[jude-fawley|Jude]], Anny is direct and humorous. When [[arabella-donn|Arabella]]'s schemes become the subject of discussion, it is Anny who voices the cynical perspective — "'Tis clear she don't!" she says of [[arabella-donn|Arabella]]'s professed indifference to [[jude-fawley|Jude]] (008-149).

### Reappearance as Arabella's Confidante

Anny resurfaces in the novel's later sections as the friend of [[arabella-donn|Arabella]]'s girlhood (051-038). Now a married woman herself, Anny provides [[arabella-donn|Arabella]] with companionship and a sounding board for her schemes. When [[arabella-donn|Arabella]] recounts her dealings with [[jude-fawley|Jude]] and [[cartlett|Cartlett]], "Anny had burst out in hearty laugh" — her earthy humour unchanged by the passage of years (051-039). It is Anny who jokes that [[arabella-donn|Arabella]]'s description of her marriage situation sounds like a comedy: "That's you, [[arabella-donn|Arabella]]" (051-058).

### Friend to Sue

Anny also serves as a friend and source of news for [[sue-bridehead|Sue]]. When [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] is living in Alfredston, she visits with Anny and receives letters from her (054-185, 065-035). Through Anny, [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] learns of the wider community's judgment on her marriage to [[jude-fawley|Jude]] — "by everybody's account, so Anny says" (065-043). In the novel's closing sections, [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] expresses a wish to "see Anny, who'll be sure to have heard" the news (065-067), making Anny a conduit for the gossip and moral commentary of the wider world.
