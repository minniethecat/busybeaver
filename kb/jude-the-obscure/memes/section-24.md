[024-001] This they did, and entered the house, admitted by an old woman without

[024-002] a single tooth, to whom they were as civil as strangers can be when

[024-003] their only chance of rest and shelter lies in the favour of the

[024-004] householder.

[024-005] “A nice little cottage,” said Jude.

[024-006] “Oh, I don’t know about the niceness. I shall have to thatch it soon,

[024-007] and where the thatch is to come from I can’t tell, for straw do get

[024-008] that dear, that ’twill soon be cheaper to cover your house wi’ chainey

[024-009] plates than thatch.”

[024-010] They sat resting, and the shepherd came in. “Don’t ’ee mind I,” he said

[024-011] with a deprecating wave of the hand; “bide here as long as ye will. But

[024-012] mid you be thinking o’ getting back to Melchester to-night by train?

[024-013] Because you’ll never do it in this world, since you don’t know the lie

[024-014] of the country. I don’t mind going with ye some o’ the ways, but even

[024-015] then the train mid be gone.”

[024-016] They started up.

[024-017] “You can bide here, you know, over the night—can’t ’em, Mother? The

[024-018] place is welcome to ye. ’Tis hard lying, rather, but volk may do

[024-019] worse.” He turned to Jude and asked privately: “Be you a married

[024-020] couple?”

[024-021] “Hsh—no!” said Jude.

[024-022] “Oh—I meant nothing ba’dy—not I! Well then, she can go into Mother’s

[024-023] room, and you and I can lie in the outer chimmer after they’ve gone

[024-024] through. I can call ye soon enough to catch the first train back.

[024-025] You’ve lost this one now.”

[024-026] On consideration they decided to close with this offer, and drew up and

[024-027] shared with the shepherd and his mother the boiled bacon and greens for

[024-028] supper.

[024-029] “I rather like this,” said Sue, while their entertainers were clearing

[024-030] away the dishes. “Outside all laws except gravitation and germination.”

[024-031] “You only think you like it; you don’t: you are quite a product of

[024-032] civilization,” said Jude, a recollection of her engagement reviving his

[024-033] soreness a little.

[024-034] “Indeed I am not, Jude. I like reading and all that, but I crave to get

[024-035] back to the life of my infancy and its freedom.”

[024-036] “Do you remember it so well? You seem to me to have nothing

[024-037] unconventional at all about you.”

[024-038] “Oh, haven’t I! You don’t know what’s inside me.”

[024-039] “What?”

[024-040] “The Ishmaelite.”

[024-041] “An urban miss is what you are.”

[024-042] She looked severe disagreement, and turned away.

[024-043] The shepherd aroused them the next morning, as he had said. It was

[024-044] bright and clear, and the four miles to the train were accomplished

[024-045] pleasantly. When they had reached Melchester, and walked to the Close,

[024-046] and the gables of the old building in which she was again to be immured

[024-047] rose before Sue’s eyes, she looked a little scared. “I expect I shall

[024-048] catch it!” she murmured.

[024-049] They rang the great bell and waited.

[024-050] “Oh, I bought something for you, which I had nearly forgotten,” she

[024-051] said quickly, searching her pocket. “It is a new little photograph of

[024-052] me. Would you like it?”

[024-053] “_Would_ I!” He took it gladly, and the porter came. There seemed to be

[024-054] an ominous glance on his face when he opened the gate. She passed in,

[024-055] looking back at Jude, and waving her hand.

[024-056] III

[024-057] The seventy young women, of ages varying in the main from nineteen to

[024-058] one-and-twenty, though several were older, who at this date filled the

[024-059] species of nunnery known as the Training-School at Melchester, formed a

[024-060] very mixed community, which included the daughters of mechanics,

[024-061] curates, surgeons, shopkeepers, farmers, dairy-men, soldiers, sailors,

[024-062] and villagers. They sat in the large school-room of the establishment

[024-063] on the evening previously described, and word was passed round that Sue

[024-064] Bridehead had not come in at closing-time.

[024-065] “She went out with her young man,” said a second-year’s student, who

[024-066] knew about young men. “And Miss Traceley saw her at the station with

[024-067] him. She’ll have it hot when she does come.”

[024-068] “She said he was her cousin,” observed a youthful new girl.

[024-069] “That excuse has been made a little too often in this school to be

[024-070] effectual in saving our souls,” said the head girl of the year, drily.

[024-071] The fact was that, only twelve months before, there had occurred a

[024-072] lamentable seduction of one of the pupils who had made the same

[024-073] statement in order to gain meetings with her lover. The affair had

[024-074] created a scandal, and the management had consequently been rough on

[024-075] cousins ever since.

[024-076] At nine o’clock the names were called, Sue’s being pronounced three

[024-077] times sonorously by Miss Traceley without eliciting an answer.

[024-078] At a quarter past nine the seventy stood up to sing the “Evening Hymn,”

[024-079] and then knelt down to prayers. After prayers they went in to supper,

[024-080] and every girl’s thought was, Where is Sue Bridehead? Some of the

[024-081] students, who had seen Jude from the window, felt that they would not

[024-082] mind risking her punishment for the pleasure of being kissed by such a

[024-083] kindly-faced young man. Hardly one among them believed in the

[024-084] cousinship.

[024-085] Half an hour later they all lay in their cubicles, their tender

[024-086] feminine faces upturned to the flaring gas-jets which at intervals

[024-087] stretched down the long dormitories, every face bearing the legend “The

[024-088] Weaker” upon it, as the penalty of the sex wherein they were moulded,

[024-089] which by no possible exertion of their willing hearts and abilities

[024-090] could be made strong while the inexorable laws of nature remain what

[024-091] they are. They formed a pretty, suggestive, pathetic sight, of whose

[024-092] pathos and beauty they were themselves unconscious, and would not

[024-093] discover till, amid the storms and strains of after-years, with their

[024-094] injustice, loneliness, child-bearing, and bereavement, their minds

[024-095] would revert to this experience as to something which had been allowed

[024-096] to slip past them insufficiently regarded.

[024-097] One of the mistresses came in to turn out the lights, and before doing

[024-098] so gave a final glance at Sue’s cot, which remained empty, and at her

[024-099] little dressing-table at the foot, which, like all the rest, was

[024-100] ornamented with various girlish trifles, framed photographs being not

[024-101] the least conspicuous among them. Sue’s table had a moderate show, two

[024-102] men in their filigree and velvet frames standing together beside her

[024-103] looking-glass.

[024-104] “Who are these men—did she ever say?” asked the mistress. “Strictly

[024-105] speaking, relations’ portraits only are allowed on these tables, you

[024-106] know.”

[024-107] “One—the middle-aged man,” said a student in the next bed—“is the

[024-108] schoolmaster she served under—Mr. Phillotson.”

[024-109] “And the other—this undergraduate in cap and gown—who is he?”

[024-110] “He is a friend, or was. She has never told his name.”

[024-111] “Was it either of these two who came for her?”

[024-112] “No.”

[024-113] “You are sure ’twas not the undergraduate?”

[024-114] “Quite. He was a young man with a black beard.”

[024-115] The lights were promptly extinguished, and till they fell asleep the

[024-116] girls indulged in conjectures about Sue, and wondered what games she

[024-117] had carried on in London and at Christminster before she came here,

[024-118] some of the more restless ones getting out of bed and looking from the

[024-119] mullioned windows at the vast west front of the cathedral opposite, and

[024-120] the spire rising behind it.

[024-121] When they awoke the next morning they glanced into Sue’s nook, to find

[024-122] it still without a tenant. After the early lessons by gas-light, in

[024-123] half-toilet, and when they had come up to dress for breakfast, the bell

[024-124] of the entrance gate was heard to ring loudly. The mistress of the

[024-125] dormitory went away, and presently came back to say that the

[024-126] principal’s orders were that nobody was to speak to Bridehead without

[024-127] permission.

[024-128] When, accordingly, Sue came into the dormitory to hastily tidy herself,

[024-129] looking flushed and tired, she went to her cubicle in silence, none of

[024-130] them coming out to greet her or to make inquiry. When they had gone

[024-131] downstairs they found that she did not follow them into the dining-hall

[024-132] to breakfast, and they then learnt that she had been severely

[024-133] reprimanded, and ordered to a solitary room for a week, there to be

[024-134] confined, and take her meals, and do all her reading.

[024-135] At this the seventy murmured, the sentence being, they thought, too

[024-136] severe. A round robin was prepared and sent in to the principal, asking

[024-137] for a remission of Sue’s punishment. No notice was taken. Towards

[024-138] evening, when the geography mistress began dictating her subject, the

[024-139] girls in the class sat with folded arms.

[024-140] “You mean that you are not going to work?” said the mistress at last.

[024-141] “I may as well tell you that it has been ascertained that the young man

[024-142] Bridehead stayed out with was not her cousin, for the very good reason

[024-143] that she has no such relative. We have written to Christminster to

[024-144] ascertain.”

[024-145] “We are willing to take her word,” said the head girl.

[024-146] “This young man was discharged from his work at Christminster for

[024-147] drunkenness and blasphemy in public-houses, and he has come here to

[024-148] live, entirely to be near her.”

[024-149] However, they remained stolid and motionless, and the mistress left the

[024-150] room to inquire from her superiors what was to be done.

[024-151] Presently, towards dusk, the pupils, as they sat, heard exclamations

[024-152] from the first-year’s girls in an adjoining classroom, and one rushed

[024-153] in to say that Sue Bridehead had got out of the back window of the room

[024-154] in which she had been confined, escaped in the dark across the lawn,

[024-155] and disappeared. How she had managed to get out of the garden nobody

[024-156] could tell, as it was bounded by the river at the bottom, and the side

[024-157] door was locked.

[024-158] They went and looked at the empty room, the casement between the middle

[024-159] mullions of which stood open. The lawn was again searched with a

[024-160] lantern, every bush and shrub being examined, but she was nowhere

[024-161] hidden. Then the porter of the front gate was interrogated, and on

[024-162] reflection he said that he remembered hearing a sort of splashing in

[024-163] the stream at the back, but he had taken no notice, thinking some ducks

[024-164] had come down the river from above.

[024-165] “She must have walked through the river!” said a mistress.

[024-166] “Or drownded herself,” said the porter.

[024-167] The mind of the matron was horrified—not so much at the possible death

[024-168] of Sue as at the possible half-column detailing that event in all the

[024-169] newspapers, which, added to the scandal of the year before, would give

[024-170] the college an unenviable notoriety for many months to come.

[024-171] More lanterns were procured, and the river examined; and then, at last,

[024-172] on the opposite shore, which was open to the fields, some little

[024-173] boot-tracks were discerned in the mud, which left no doubt that the too

[024-174] excitable girl had waded through a depth of water reaching nearly to

[024-175] her shoulders—for this was the chief river of the county, and was

[024-176] mentioned in all the geography books with respect. As Sue had not

[024-177] brought disgrace upon the school by drowning herself, the matron began

[024-178] to speak superciliously of her, and to express gladness that she was

[024-179] gone.

[024-180] On the self-same evening Jude sat in his lodgings by the Close Gate.

[024-181] Often at this hour after dusk he would enter the silent Close, and

[024-182] stand opposite the house that contained Sue, and watch the shadows of

[024-183] the girls’ heads passing to and fro upon the blinds, and wish he had

[024-184] nothing else to do but to sit reading and learning all day what many of

[024-185] the thoughtless inmates despised. But to-night, having finished tea and

[024-186] brushed himself up, he was deep in the perusal of the Twenty-ninth

[024-187] Volume of Pusey’s Library of the Fathers, a set of books which he had

[024-188] purchased of a second-hand dealer at a price that seemed to him to be

[024-189] one of miraculous cheapness for that invaluable work. He fancied he

[024-190] heard something rattle lightly against his window; then he heard it

[024-191] again. Certainly somebody had thrown gravel. He rose and gently lifted

[024-192] the sash.

[024-193] “Jude!” (from below).

[024-194] “Sue!”

[024-195] “Yes—it is! Can I come up without being seen?”
