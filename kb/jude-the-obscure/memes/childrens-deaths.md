# The Children's Deaths

The children's deaths — the murder-suicide in which Little Father Time hangs his two half-siblings and then himself in a Christminster lodging-house, leaving the note *"Done because we are too menny"* — is the fulcrum on which *Jude the Obscure* turns from a novel of social realism into one of existential horror. The event, occurring in Part Sixth, Chapter 2, is among the most notorious scenes in Victorian literature: it provoked outrage upon the novel's publication and remains, over a century later, one of the most powerful representations of childhood despair in English fiction.

## Context

### Preceding Events

Jude, Sue, and their three children have returned to Christminster on Remembrance Day (see [[remembrance-day]]). Jude is ill; Sue is pregnant with a fourth child. They have been turned away from multiple lodgings because landlords refuse to accommodate children — "'we don't let where there are children'" (057-088). The family is at the end of its resources: evicted, exhausted, and socially outcast because Jude and Sue cannot legally marry (see [[divorce]]).

The night before the tragedy, Jude delivers an impromptu sermon in the rain outside the college gates, lamenting the futility of his scholarly ambitions. The family then finds shelter at a lodging-house where Sue and the children sleep in one room while Jude stays at a rough inn nearby. That evening, Sue has a fateful conversation with Little Father Time, the melancholy boy Jude fathered with Arabella.

### The Conversation with Sue

Sue, trying to be honest with the precociously gloomy child, tells him she is pregnant again. His reaction is one of horror: "'Oh God, Mother, you've never a-sent for another; and such trouble with what you've got!'" (058-036). Sue confirms the pregnancy, and the boy breaks into bitter weeping: "How _ever_ could you, Mother, be so wicked and cruel as this, when you needn't have done it till we was better off, and Father well!" (058-041).

The exchange that follows is one of the most philosophically charged dialogues in the novel. Little Father Time delivers a monologue that reads like a child's untutored engagement with Schopenhauer: "I think that whenever children be born that are not wanted they should be killed directly, before their souls come to 'em, and not allowed to grow big and walk about!" (058-024). Sue, exhausted and emotionally depleted, offers only weak reassurance. The boy retreats to the closet where the children sleep, muttering: "If we children was gone there'd be no trouble at all!" (058-057).

## Key Details

### The Discovery

The following morning, Sue rises early to meet Jude at his inn for breakfast. They return to the lodging-house about half-past eight. Jude stays to boil eggs while Sue goes to wake the children. The moment is rendered with devastating narrative economy:

> "He saw that the door of the room, or rather closet — which had seemed to go heavily upon its hinges as she pushed it back — was open, and that Sue had sunk to the floor just within it. Hastening forward to pick her up he turned his eyes to the little bed spread on the boards; no children were there." (058-092)

At the back of the door, on two hooks meant for hanging garments, "the forms of the two youngest children were suspended, by a piece of box-cord round each of their necks, while from a nail a few yards off the body of little Jude was hanging in a similar manner. An overturned chair was near the elder boy, and his glazed eyes were slanted into the room; but those of the girl and the baby boy were closed" (058-098).

Jude, "half-paralyzed by the strange and consummate horror of the scene," cuts the cords with his pocket-knife and throws the three bodies on the bed, but "the feel of their bodies in the momentary handling seemed to say that they were dead" (058-103). A surgeon is summoned but his presence is superfluous: the children had been hanging more than an hour.

### The Note

On the floor, written in the boy's hand with a bit of lead pencil, is the phrase that became one of the most famous lines in Hardy's fiction:

> *"Done because we are too menny."* (058-123)

The misspelling — "menny" for "many" — intensifies the horror by reminding the reader that these are the words of a child barely old enough to write. At the sight of the note, Sue's nerves "utterly gave way, an awful conviction that her discourse with the boy had been the main cause of the tragedy" (058-124).

### The Aftermath

Sue, pregnant and convulsed with self-reproach, descends into a state of religious mania. She becomes convinced that the children's deaths are divine punishment for her "sin" of living with Jude outside marriage. Jude, attempting to console her, offers the novel's bleakest assessment of their situation: "It was in his nature to do it. The Doctor says there are such boys springing up amongst us — boys of a sort unknown in the last generation — the outcome of new views of life. They seem to see all its terrors before they are old enough to have staying power to resist them. He says it is the beginning of the coming universal wish not to live" (058-139).

As they wait for the coroner's inquest (see [[inquest]]), the sound of an organ practising in a college chapel drifts through the walls — the anthem from the seventy-third Psalm: "Truly God is loving unto Israel" (058-161). The irony is savage. Sue breaks: "There is something external to us which says, 'You shan't!' First it said, 'You shan't learn!' Then it said, 'You shan't labour!' Now it says, 'You shan't love!'" (058-174).

### Location

The tragedy occurs in a cramped lodging-house in Christminster, the city of Jude's lifelong dreams. The children sleep in a windowless closet — a detail that underscores the family's poverty and the city's indifference. The contrast between the grandeur of the colleges and the squalor of the room where three children die is one of Hardy's most pointed architectural ironies.

### Chronology

The tragedy unfolds across approximately twelve hours:

1. **Evening** — Jude preaches in the rain at the Remembrance Day procession; the family searches for lodging; Sue has her conversation with Little Father Time
2. **Night** — Little Father Time, "thrown into a fit of aggravated despondency," hangs his siblings and himself (058-118)
3. **Morning** — Sue meets Jude for breakfast; they return to the lodging-house
4. **Half-past eight** — Sue discovers the bodies; Jude cuts them down; the surgeon confirms death
5. **Aftermath** — Sue's religious conversion begins; the inquest is scheduled; the family's dissolution accelerates

## Narrative Significance

The children's deaths are the narrative's hinge. Before this event, *Jude the Obscure* is fundamentally a social novel about class barriers to education and the injustice of marriage law. After it, the novel becomes something darker — an inquiry into whether existence itself is bearable. The event transforms every character:

- **Sue** retreats from her intellectual rebellion into religious orthodoxy, concluding that her suffering is divine punishment. She remarries Phillotson in an act of self-mortification.
- **Jude** loses the will to struggle and descends into illness and despair. His final return to Christminster, dying on Remembrance Day, closes the novel's cycle of aspiration and defeat.
- **The novel itself** becomes a work of such unrelenting bleakness that Hardy would never write another novel. The outcry over the children's deaths contributed directly to his abandonment of fiction for poetry.

Hardy's point, made explicit through Jude's conversation with the Doctor, is that Little Father Time is not an aberration but a harbinger: "the beginning of the coming universal wish not to live" (058-143). The boy who sees "all the terrors of life before he is old enough to have staying power to resist them" is Hardy's most radical critique of the very premise of human existence.

## Critical Interpretation

Contemporary reviewers were almost unanimous in their condemnation of the children's deaths scene. The *Pall Mall Gazette* called it "a study in the pathology of suicide" that crossed the bounds of literary decency. The *New York World* headlined its review "Jude the Obscene." Bishop William Walsham How, the Bishop of Wakefield, claimed to have thrown his copy of the novel into the fire — an act of censorship that Hardy never forgot.

But the scene has also been defended as one of the most philosophically courageous in English fiction. D. H. Lawrence, writing in his *Study of Thomas Hardy* (1914), argued that Little Father Time represents "the little, noiseless, justification-proof arsenal of the death-wish that Hardy saw coming into the world." Modern critics have read the episode through the lens of the Malthusian anxiety about overpopulation that pervaded late-Victorian social thought, as well as through the Darwinian pessimism that colours Hardy's late fiction.

The phrase "Done because we are too menny" has become one of the most quoted lines in Hardy's oeuvre — seven words that compress the novel's entire argument about the cruelty of a world that produces children it cannot support and consciousness it cannot console.
