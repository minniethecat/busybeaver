[062-001] He went to the bed, removed one of the pair of pillows thereon, and

[062-002] flung it to the floor.

[062-003] Sue looked at him, and bending over the bed-rail wept silently. “You

[062-004] don’t see that it is a matter of conscience with me, and not of dislike

[062-005] to you!” she brokenly murmured. “Dislike to you! But I can’t say any

[062-006] more—it breaks my heart—it will be undoing all I have begun!

[062-007] Jude—good-night!”

[062-008] “Good-night,” he said, and turned to go.

[062-009] “Oh but you shall kiss me!” said she, starting up. “I can’t—bear—!”

[062-010] He clasped her, and kissed her weeping face as he had scarcely ever

[062-011] done before, and they remained in silence till she said, “Good-bye,

[062-012] good-bye!” And then gently pressing him away she got free, trying to

[062-013] mitigate the sadness by saying: “We’ll be dear friends just the same,

[062-014] Jude, won’t we? And we’ll see each other sometimes—yes!—and forget all

[062-015] this, and try to be as we were long ago?”

[062-016] Jude did not permit himself to speak, but turned and descended the

[062-017] stairs.

[062-018] IV

[062-019] The man whom Sue, in her mental _volte-face_, was now regarding as her

[062-020] inseparable husband, lived still at Marygreen.

[062-021] On the day before the tragedy of the children, Phillotson had seen both

[062-022] her and Jude as they stood in the rain at Christminster watching the

[062-023] procession to the theatre. But he had said nothing of it at the moment

[062-024] to his companion Gillingham, who, being an old friend, was staying with

[062-025] him at the village aforesaid, and had, indeed, suggested the day’s trip

[062-026] to Christminster.

[062-027] “What are you thinking of?” said Gillingham, as they went home. “The

[062-028] university degree you never obtained?”

[062-029] “No, no,” said Phillotson gruffly. “Of somebody I saw to-day.” In a

[062-030] moment he added, “Susanna.”

[062-031] “I saw her, too.”

[062-032] “You said nothing.”

[062-033] “I didn’t wish to draw your attention to her. But, as you did see her,

[062-034] you should have said: ‘How d’ye do, my dear-that-was?’”

[062-035] “Ah, well. I might have. But what do you think of this: I have good

[062-036] reason for supposing that she was innocent when I divorced her—that I

[062-037] was all wrong. Yes, indeed! Awkward, isn’t it?”

[062-038] “She has taken care to set you right since, anyhow, apparently.”

[062-039] “H’m. That’s a cheap sneer. I ought to have waited, unquestionably.”

[062-040] At the end of the week, when Gillingham had gone back to his school

[062-041] near Shaston, Phillotson, as was his custom, went to Alfredston market;

[062-042] ruminating again on Arabella’s intelligence as he walked down the long

[062-043] hill which he had known before Jude knew it, though his history had not

[062-044] beaten so intensely upon its incline. Arrived in the town he bought his

[062-045] usual weekly local paper; and when he had sat down in an inn to refresh

[062-046] himself for the five miles’ walk back, he pulled the paper from his

[062-047] pocket and read awhile. The account of the “strange suicide of a

[062-048] stone-mason’s children” met his eye.

[062-049] Unimpassioned as he was, it impressed him painfully, and puzzled him

[062-050] not a little, for he could not understand the age of the elder child

[062-051] being what it was stated to be. However, there was no doubt that the

[062-052] newspaper report was in some way true.

[062-053] “Their cup of sorrow is now full!” he said: and thought and thought of

[062-054] Sue, and what she had gained by leaving him.

[062-055] Arabella having made her home at Alfredston, and the schoolmaster

[062-056] coming to market there every Saturday, it was not wonderful that in a

[062-057] few weeks they met again—the precise time being just after her return

[062-058] from Christminster, where she had stayed much longer than she had at

[062-059] first intended, keeping an interested eye on Jude, though Jude had seen

[062-060] no more of her. Phillotson was on his way homeward when he encountered

[062-061] Arabella, and she was approaching the town.

[062-062] “You like walking out this way, Mrs. Cartlett?” he said.

[062-063] “I’ve just begun to again,” she replied. “It is where I lived as maid

[062-064] and wife, and all the past things of my life that are interesting to my

[062-065] feelings are mixed up with this road. And they have been stirred up in

[062-066] me too, lately; for I’ve been visiting at Christminster. Yes; I’ve seen

[062-067] Jude.”

[062-068] “Ah! How do they bear their terrible affliction?”

[062-069] “In a ve-ry strange way—ve-ry strange! She don’t live with him any

[062-070] longer. I only heard of it as a certainty just before I left; though I

[062-071] had thought things were drifting that way from their manner when I

[062-072] called on them.”

[062-073] “Not live with her husband? Why, I should have thought ’twould have

[062-074] united them more.”

[062-075] “He’s not her husband, after all. She has never really married him

[062-076] although they have passed as man and wife so long. And now, instead of

[062-077] this sad event making ’em hurry up, and get the thing done legally,

[062-078] she’s took in a queer religious way, just as I was in my affliction at

[062-079] losing Cartlett, only hers is of a more ’sterical sort than mine. And

[062-080] she says, so I was told, that she’s your wife in the eye of Heaven and

[062-081] the Church—yours only; and can’t be anybody else’s by any act of man.”

[062-082] “Ah—indeed? … Separated, have they!”

[062-083] “You see, the eldest boy was mine—”

[062-084] “Oh—yours!”

[062-085] “Yes, poor little fellow—born in lawful wedlock, thank God. And perhaps

[062-086] she feels, over and above other things, that I ought to have been in

[062-087] her place. I can’t say. However, as for me, I am soon off from here.

[062-088] I’ve got Father to look after now, and we can’t live in such a hum-drum

[062-089] place as this. I hope soon to be in a bar again at Christminster, or

[062-090] some other big town.”

[062-091] They parted. When Phillotson had ascended the hill a few steps he

[062-092] stopped, hastened back, and called her.

[062-093] “What is, or was, their address?”

[062-094] Arabella gave it.

[062-095] “Thank you. Good afternoon.”

[062-096] Arabella smiled grimly as she resumed her way, and practised

[062-097] dimple-making all along the road from where the pollard willows begin

[062-098] to the old almshouses in the first street of the town.

[062-099] Meanwhile Phillotson ascended to Marygreen, and for the first time

[062-100] during a lengthened period he lived with a forward eye. On crossing

[062-101] under the large trees of the green to the humble schoolhouse to which

[062-102] he had been reduced he stood a moment, and pictured Sue coming out of

[062-103] the door to meet him. No man had ever suffered more inconvenience from

[062-104] his own charity, Christian or heathen, than Phillotson had done in

[062-105] letting Sue go. He had been knocked about from pillar to post at the

[062-106] hands of the virtuous almost beyond endurance; he had been nearly

[062-107] starved, and was now dependent entirely upon the very small stipend

[062-108] from the school of this village (where the parson had got ill-spoken of

[062-109] for befriending him). He had often thought of Arabella’s remarks that

[062-110] he should have been more severe with Sue, that her recalcitrant spirit

[062-111] would soon have been broken. Yet such was his obstinate and illogical

[062-112] disregard of opinion, and of the principles in which he had been

[062-113] trained, that his convictions on the rightness of his course with his

[062-114] wife had not been disturbed.

[062-115] Principles which could be subverted by feeling in one direction were

[062-116] liable to the same catastrophe in another. The instincts which had

[062-117] allowed him to give Sue her liberty now enabled him to regard her as

[062-118] none the worse for her life with Jude. He wished for her still, in his

[062-119] curious way, if he did not love her, and, apart from policy, soon felt

[062-120] that he would be gratified to have her again as his, always provided

[062-121] that she came willingly.

[062-122] But artifice was necessary, he had found, for stemming the cold and

[062-123] inhumane blast of the world’s contempt. And here were the materials

[062-124] ready made. By getting Sue back and remarrying her on the respectable

[062-125] plea of having entertained erroneous views of her, and gained his

[062-126] divorce wrongfully, he might acquire some comfort, resume his old

[062-127] courses, perhaps return to the Shaston school, if not even to the

[062-128] Church as a licentiate.

[062-129] He thought he would write to Gillingham to inquire his views, and what

[062-130] he thought of his, Phillotson’s, sending a letter to her. Gillingham

[062-131] replied, naturally, that now she was gone it were best to let her be,

[062-132] and considered that if she were anybody’s wife she was the wife of the

[062-133] man to whom she had borne three children and owed such tragical

[062-134] adventures. Probably, as his attachment to her seemed unusually strong,

[062-135] the singular pair would make their union legal in course of time, and

[062-136] all would be well, and decent, and in order.

[062-137] “But they won’t—Sue won’t!” exclaimed Phillotson to himself.

[062-138] “Gillingham is so matter of fact. She’s affected by Christminster

[062-139] sentiment and teaching. I can see her views on the indissolubility of

[062-140] marriage well enough, and I know where she got them. They are not mine;

[062-141] but I shall make use of them to further mine.”

[062-142] He wrote a brief reply to Gillingham. “I know I am entirely wrong, but

[062-143] I don’t agree with you. As to her having lived with and had three

[062-144] children by him, my feeling is (though I can advance no logical or

[062-145] moral defence of it, on the old lines) that it has done little more

[062-146] than finish her education. I shall write to her, and learn whether what

[062-147] that woman said is true or no.”

[062-148] As he had made up his mind to do this before he had written to his

[062-149] friend, there had not been much reason for writing to the latter at

[062-150] all. However, it was Phillotson’s way to act thus.

[062-151] He accordingly addressed a carefully considered epistle to Sue, and,

[062-152] knowing her emotional temperament, threw a Rhadamanthine strictness

[062-153] into the lines here and there, carefully hiding his heterodox feelings,

[062-154] not to frighten her. He stated that, it having come to his knowledge

[062-155] that her views had considerably changed, he felt compelled to say that

[062-156] his own, too, were largely modified by events subsequent to their

[062-157] parting. He would not conceal from her that passionate love had little

[062-158] to do with his communication. It arose from a wish to make their lives,

[062-159] if not a success, at least no such disastrous failure as they

[062-160] threatened to become, through his acting on what he had considered at

[062-161] the time a principle of justice, charity, and reason.

[062-162] To indulge one’s instinctive and uncontrolled sense of justice and

[062-163] right, was not, he had found, permitted with impunity in an old

[062-164] civilization like ours. It was necessary to act under an acquired and

[062-165] cultivated sense of the same, if you wished to enjoy an average share

[062-166] of comfort and honour; and to let crude loving kindness take care of

[062-167] itself.

[062-168] He suggested that she should come to him there at Marygreen.

[062-169] On second thoughts he took out the last paragraph but one; and having

[062-170] rewritten the letter he dispatched it immediately, and in some

[062-171] excitement awaited the issue.

[062-172] A few days after a figure moved through the white fog which enveloped

[062-173] the Beersheba suburb of Christminster, towards the quarter in which

[062-174] Jude Fawley had taken up his lodging since his division from Sue. A

[062-175] timid knock sounded upon the door of his abode.

[062-176] It was evening—so he was at home; and by a species of divination he

[062-177] jumped up and rushed to the door himself.

[062-178] “Will you come out with me? I would rather not come in. I want to—to

[062-179] talk with you—and to go with you to the cemetery.”

[062-180] It had been in the trembling accents of Sue that these words came. Jude

[062-181] put on his hat. “It is dreary for you to be out,” he said. “But if you

[062-182] prefer not to come in, I don’t mind.”

[062-183] “Yes—I do. I shall not keep you long.”

[062-184] Jude was too much affected to go on talking at first; she, too, was now

[062-185] such a mere cluster of nerves that all initiatory power seemed to have

[062-186] left her, and they proceeded through the fog like Acherontic shades for

[062-187] a long while, without sound or gesture.
