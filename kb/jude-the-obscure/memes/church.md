# Church

The Church of England saturates the world of *Jude the Obscure* — not merely as a religious institution but as an economic employer, a gatekeeper of education, a sanctifier of marriage, and a source of architectural employment for the stonemason protagonist [[Jude-Fawley]]. Hardy presents the church as an ambivalent force: beautiful in its material fabric, oppressive in its social function.

## Overview

The church appears in the novel in multiple registers. It is a physical presence — the medieval churches and cathedrals whose stonework Jude repairs and whose architecture he loves. It is an institutional gatekeeper — Christminster's colleges are religious foundations, and university admission was tied to Anglican orthodoxy until 1871. And it is a moral authority — the force that condemns Jude and Sue's unmarried cohabitation and that Sue ultimately embraces as a form of self-punishment.

Hardy's own relationship to Christianity was complex: raised Anglican, he lost his faith but retained a deep attachment to church architecture and ritual. This double vision — the church as both beautiful and destructive — shapes the novel.

## In *Jude the Obscure*

### Narrative Significance

The church functions as a structuring institution throughout the novel:

- **As employer**: Jude repairs and restores church stonework, most significantly the Ten Commandments in a country church — an ironic task for a man living in what the church considers sin.
- **As gatekeeper**: Jude's dream of Christminster is inseparable from its identity as a religious foundation. The "founders of the religious school" that "most real to Jude Fawley were" (013-159) are churchmen.
- **As moral judge**: The church's condemnation of Jude and Sue's relationship drives their social ostracism. Sue's eventual return to the church — taking communion, remarrying Phillotson in a religious ceremony — is the novel's most devastating capitulation.

## Description in the Text

> "...most real to Jude Fawley were the founders of the religious school at Christminster..." (013-159)

> The church and its architecture are present in the novel's very texture — as the stonemason's material, the worshipper's sanctuary, and the moralist's weapon.

## Critical Interpretation

Hardy's treatment of the church reflects the Victorian crisis of faith. The novel was published in 1895, decades after Darwin's *Origin of Species* (1859) had destabilized biblical literalism, yet the church remained a powerful social institution. Contemporary reviewers attacked the novel's "paganism" — Sue's preference for Venus over the Virgin Mary was cited as evidence of the book's immorality.
