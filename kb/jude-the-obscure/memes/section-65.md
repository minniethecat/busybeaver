[065-001] “Then you turn me away?”

[065-002] “I’ll give you enough to get food and lodging for a few days.”

[065-003] “Oh, but can’t you have the kindness to take me in? I cannot endure

[065-004] going to a public house to lodge; and I am so lonely. Please, Jude, for

[065-005] old times’ sake!”

[065-006] “No, no,” said Jude hastily. “I don’t want to be reminded of those

[065-007] things; and if you talk about them I shall not help you.”

[065-008] “Then I suppose I must go!” said Arabella. She bent her head against

[065-009] the doorpost and began sobbing.

[065-010] “The house is full,” said Jude. “And I have only a little extra room to

[065-011] my own—not much more than a closet—where I keep my tools, and

[065-012] templates, and the few books I have left!”

[065-013] “That would be a palace for me!”

[065-014] “There is no bedstead in it.”

[065-015] “A bit of a bed could be made on the floor. It would be good enough for

[065-016] me.”

[065-017] Unable to be harsh with her, and not knowing what to do, Jude called

[065-018] the man who let the lodgings, and said this was an acquaintance of his

[065-019] in great distress for want of temporary shelter.

[065-020] “You may remember me as barmaid at the Lamb and Flag formerly?” spoke

[065-021] up Arabella. “My father has insulted me this afternoon, and I’ve left

[065-022] him, though without a penny!”

[065-023] The householder said he could not recall her features. “But still, if

[065-024] you are a friend of Mr. Fawley’s we’ll do what we can for a day or

[065-025] two—if he’ll make himself answerable?”

[065-026] “Yes, yes,” said Jude. “She has really taken me quite unawares; but I

[065-027] should wish to help her out of her difficulty.” And an arrangement was

[065-028] ultimately come to under which a bed was to be thrown down in Jude’s

[065-029] lumber-room, to make it comfortable for Arabella till she could get out

[065-030] of the strait she was in—not by her own fault, as she declared—and

[065-031] return to her father’s again.

[065-032] While they were waiting for this to be done Arabella said: “You know

[065-033] the news, I suppose?”

[065-034] “I guess what you mean; but I know nothing.”

[065-035] “I had a letter from Anny at Alfredston to-day. She had just heard that

[065-036] the wedding was to be yesterday: but she didn’t know if it had come

[065-037] off.”

[065-038] “I don’t wish to talk of it.”

[065-039] “No, no: of course you don’t. Only it shows what kind of woman—”

[065-040] “Don’t speak of her I say! She’s a fool! And she’s an angel, too, poor

[065-041] dear!”

[065-042] “If it’s done, he’ll have a chance of getting back to his old position,

[065-043] by everybody’s account, so Anny says. All his well-wishers will be

[065-044] pleased, including the bishop himself.”

[065-045] “Do spare me, Arabella.”

[065-046] Arabella was duly installed in the little attic, and at first she did

[065-047] not come near Jude at all. She went to and fro about her own business,

[065-048] which, when they met for a moment on the stairs or in the passage, she

[065-049] informed him was that of obtaining another place in the occupation she

[065-050] understood best. When Jude suggested London as affording the most

[065-051] likely opening in the liquor trade, she shook her head. “No—the

[065-052] temptations are too many,” she said. “Any humble tavern in the country

[065-053] before that for me.”

[065-054] On the Sunday morning following, when he breakfasted later than on

[065-055] other days, she meekly asked him if she might come in to breakfast with

[065-056] him, as she had broken her teapot, and could not replace it

[065-057] immediately, the shops being shut.

[065-058] “Yes, if you like,” he said indifferently.

[065-059] While they sat without speaking she suddenly observed: “You seem all in

[065-060] a brood, old man. I’m sorry for you.”

[065-061] “I am all in a brood.”

[065-062] “It is about her, I know. It’s no business of mine, but I could find

[065-063] out all about the wedding—if it really did take place—if you wanted to

[065-064] know.”

[065-065] “How could you?”

[065-066] “I wanted to go to Alfredston to get a few things I left there. And I

[065-067] could see Anny, who’ll be sure to have heard all about it, as she has

[065-068] friends at Marygreen.”

[065-069] Jude could not bear to acquiesce in this proposal; but his suspense

[065-070] pitted itself against his discretion, and won in the struggle. “You can

[065-071] ask about it if you like,” he said. “I’ve not heard a sound from there.

[065-072] It must have been very private, if—they have married.”

[065-073] “I am afraid I haven’t enough cash to take me there and back, or I

[065-074] should have gone before. I must wait till I have earned some.”

[065-075] “Oh—I can pay the journey for you,” he said impatiently. And thus his

[065-076] suspense as to Sue’s welfare, and the possible marriage, moved him to

[065-077] dispatch for intelligence the last emissary he would have thought of

[065-078] choosing deliberately.

[065-079] Arabella went, Jude requesting her to be home not later than by the

[065-080] seven o’clock train. When she had gone he said: “Why should I have

[065-081] charged her to be back by a particular time! She’s nothing to me—nor

[065-082] the other neither!”

[065-083] But having finished work he could not help going to the station to meet

[065-084] Arabella, dragged thither by feverish haste to get the news she might

[065-085] bring, and know the worst. Arabella had made dimples most successfully

[065-086] all the way home, and when she stepped out of the railway carriage she

[065-087] smiled. He merely said “Well?” with the very reverse of a smile.

[065-088] “They are married.”

[065-089] “Yes—of course they are!” he returned. She observed, however, the hard

[065-090] strain upon his lip as he spoke.

[065-091] “Anny says she has heard from Belinda, her relation out at Marygreen,

[065-092] that it was very sad, and curious!”

[065-093] “How do you mean sad? She wanted to marry him again, didn’t she? And he

[065-094] her!”

[065-095] “Yes—that was it. She wanted to in one sense, but not in the other.

[065-096] Mrs. Edlin was much upset by it all, and spoke out her mind at

[065-097] Phillotson. But Sue was that excited about it that she burnt her best

[065-098] embroidery that she’d worn with you, to blot you out entirely. Well—if

[065-099] a woman feels like it, she ought to do it. I commend her for it, though

[065-100] others don’t.” Arabella sighed. “She felt he was her only husband, and

[065-101] that she belonged to nobody else in the sight of God A’mighty while he

[065-102] lived. Perhaps another woman feels the same about herself, too!”

[065-103] Arabella sighed again.

[065-104] “I don’t want any cant!” exclaimed Jude.

[065-105] “It isn’t cant,” said Arabella. “I feel exactly the same as she!”

[065-106] He closed that issue by remarking abruptly: “Well—now I know all I

[065-107] wanted to know. Many thanks for your information. I am not going back

[065-108] to my lodgings just yet.” And he left her straightway.

[065-109] In his misery and depression Jude walked to well-nigh every spot in the

[065-110] city that he had visited with Sue; thence he did not know whither, and

[065-111] then thought of going home to his usual evening meal. But having all

[065-112] the vices of his virtues, and some to spare, he turned into a public

[065-113] house, for the first time during many months. Among the possible

[065-114] consequences of her marriage Sue had not dwelt on this.

[065-115] Arabella, meanwhile, had gone back. The evening passed, and Jude did

[065-116] not return. At half-past nine Arabella herself went out, first

[065-117] proceeding to an outlying district near the river where her father

[065-118] lived, and had opened a small and precarious pork-shop lately.

[065-119] “Well,” she said to him, “for all your rowing me that night, I’ve

[065-120] called in, for I have something to tell you. I think I shall get

[065-121] married and settled again. Only you must help me: and you can do no

[065-122] less, after what I’ve stood ’ee.”

[065-123] “I’ll do anything to get thee off my hands!”

[065-124] “Very well. I am now going to look for my young man. He’s on the loose

[065-125] I’m afraid, and I must get him home. All I want you to do to-night is

[065-126] not to fasten the door, in case I should want to sleep here, and should

[065-127] be late.”

[065-128] “I thought you’d soon get tired of giving yourself airs and keeping

[065-129] away!”

[065-130] “Well—don’t do the door. That’s all I say.”

[065-131] She then sallied out again, and first hastening back to Jude’s to make

[065-132] sure that he had not returned, began her search for him. A shrewd guess

[065-133] as to his probable course took her straight to the tavern which Jude

[065-134] had formerly frequented, and where she had been barmaid for a brief

[065-135] term. She had no sooner opened the door of the “Private Bar” than her

[065-136] eyes fell upon him—sitting in the shade at the back of the compartment,

[065-137] with his eyes fixed on the floor in a blank stare. He was drinking

[065-138] nothing stronger than ale just then. He did not observe her, and she

[065-139] entered and sat beside him.

[065-140] Jude looked up, and said without surprise: “You’ve come to have

[065-141] something, Arabella? … I’m trying to forget her: that’s all! But I

[065-142] can’t; and I am going home.” She saw that he was a little way on in

[065-143] liquor, but only a little as yet.

[065-144] “I’ve come entirely to look for you, dear boy. You are not well. Now

[065-145] you must have something better than that.” Arabella held up her finger

[065-146] to the barmaid. “You shall have a liqueur—that’s better fit for a man

[065-147] of education than beer. You shall have maraschino, or curaçao dry or

[065-148] sweet, or cherry brandy. I’ll treat you, poor chap!”

[065-149] “I don’t care which! Say cherry brandy… Sue has served me badly, very

[065-150] badly. I didn’t expect it of Sue! I stuck to her, and she ought to have

[065-151] stuck to me. I’d have sold my soul for her sake, but she wouldn’t risk

[065-152] hers a jot for me. To save her own soul she lets mine go damn! … But it

[065-153] isn’t her fault, poor little girl—I am sure it isn’t!”

[065-154] How Arabella had obtained money did not appear, but she ordered a

[065-155] liqueur each, and paid for them. When they had drunk these Arabella

[065-156] suggested another; and Jude had the pleasure of being, as it were,

[065-157] personally conducted through the varieties of spirituous delectation by

[065-158] one who knew the landmarks well. Arabella kept very considerably in the

[065-159] rear of Jude; but though she only sipped where he drank, she took as

[065-160] much as she could safely take without losing her head—which was not a

[065-161] little, as the crimson upon her countenance showed.

[065-162] Her tone towards him to-night was uniformly soothing and cajoling; and

[065-163] whenever he said “I don’t care what happens to me,” a thing he did

[065-164] continually, she replied, “But I do very much!” The closing hour came,

[065-165] and they were compelled to turn out; whereupon Arabella put her arm

[065-166] round his waist, and guided his unsteady footsteps.

[065-167] When they were in the streets she said: “I don’t know what our landlord

[065-168] will say to my bringing you home in this state. I expect we are

[065-169] fastened out, so that he’ll have to come down and let us in.”

[065-170] “I don’t know—I don’t know.”

[065-171] “That’s the worst of not having a home of your own. I tell you, Jude,

[065-172] what we had best do. Come round to my father’s—I made it up with him a

[065-173] bit to-day. I can let you in, and nobody will see you at all; and by

[065-174] to-morrow morning you’ll be all right.”

[065-175] “Anything—anywhere,” replied Jude. “What the devil does it matter to

[065-176] me?”

[065-177] They went along together, like any other fuddling couple, her arm still

[065-178] round his waist, and his, at last, round hers; though with no amatory

[065-179] intent; but merely because he was weary, unstable, and in need of

[065-180] support.

[065-181] “This—is th’ Martyrs’—burning-place,” he stammered as they dragged

[065-182] across a broad street. “I remember—in old Fuller’s _Holy State_—and I

[065-183] am reminded of it—by our passing by here—old Fuller in his _Holy State_

[065-184] says, that at the burning of Ridley, Doctor Smith—preached sermon, and

[065-185] took as his text _‘Though I give my body to be burned, and have not

[065-186] charity, it profiteth me nothing.’_—Often think of it as I pass here.

[065-187] Ridley was a—”
