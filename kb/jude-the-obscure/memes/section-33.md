[033-001] “Jude—don’t talk about _me_—I wish you wouldn’t!” she entreated. “It

[033-002] distresses me, rather. Forgive my saying it! … Where did you stay last

[033-003] night?”

[033-004] She had asked the question in perfect innocence, to change the topic.

[033-005] He knew that, and said merely, “At an inn,” though it would have been a

[033-006] relief to tell her of his meeting with an unexpected one. But the

[033-007] latter’s final announcement of her marriage in Australia bewildered him

[033-008] lest what he might say should do his ignorant wife an injury.

[033-009] Their talk proceeded but awkwardly till they reached Alfredston. That

[033-010] Sue was not as she had been, but was labelled “Phillotson,” paralyzed

[033-011] Jude whenever he wanted to commune with her as an individual. Yet she

[033-012] seemed unaltered—he could not say why. There remained the five-mile

[033-013] extra journey into the country, which it was just as easy to walk as to

[033-014] drive, the greater part of it being uphill. Jude had never before in

[033-015] his life gone that road with Sue, though he had with another. It was

[033-016] now as if he carried a bright light which temporarily banished the

[033-017] shady associations of the earlier time.

[033-018] Sue talked; but Jude noticed that she still kept the conversation from

[033-019] herself. At length he inquired if her husband were well.

[033-020] “O yes,” she said. “He is obliged to be in the school all the day, or

[033-021] he would have come with me. He is so good and kind that to accompany me

[033-022] he would have dismissed the school for once, even against his

[033-023] principles—for he is strongly opposed to giving casual holidays—only I

[033-024] wouldn’t let him. I felt it would be better to come alone. Aunt

[033-025] Drusilla, I knew, was so very eccentric; and his being almost a

[033-026] stranger to her now would have made it irksome to both. Since it turns

[033-027] out that she is hardly conscious I am glad I did not ask him.”

[033-028] Jude had walked moodily while this praise of Phillotson was being

[033-029] expressed. “Mr. Phillotson obliges you in everything, as he ought,” he

[033-030] said.

[033-031] “Of course.”

[033-032] “You ought to be a happy wife.”

[033-033] “And of course I am.”

[033-034] “Bride, I might almost have said, as yet. It is not so many weeks since

[033-035] I gave you to him, and—”

[033-036] “Yes, I know! I know!” There was something in her face which belied her

[033-037] late assuring words, so strictly proper and so lifelessly spoken that

[033-038] they might have been taken from a list of model speeches in “The Wife’s

[033-039] Guide to Conduct.” Jude knew the quality of every vibration in Sue’s

[033-040] voice, could read every symptom of her mental condition; and he was

[033-041] convinced that she was unhappy, although she had not been a month

[033-042] married. But her rushing away thus from home, to see the last of a

[033-043] relative whom she had hardly known in her life, proved nothing; for Sue

[033-044] naturally did such things as those.

[033-045] “Well, you have my good wishes now as always, Mrs. Phillotson.”

[033-046] She reproached him by a glance.

[033-047] “No, you are not Mrs. Phillotson,” murmured Jude. “You are dear, free

[033-048] Sue Bridehead, only you don’t know it! Wifedom has not yet squashed up

[033-049] and digested you in its vast maw as an atom which has no further

[033-050] individuality.”

[033-051] Sue put on a look of being offended, till she answered, “Nor has

[033-052] husbandom you, so far as I can see!”

[033-053] “But it has!” he said, shaking his head sadly.

[033-054] When they reached the lone cottage under the firs, between the Brown

[033-055] House and Marygreen, in which Jude and Arabella had lived and

[033-056] quarrelled, he turned to look at it. A squalid family lived there now.

[033-057] He could not help saying to Sue: “That’s the house my wife and I

[033-058] occupied the whole of the time we lived together. I brought her home to

[033-059] that house.”

[033-060] She looked at it. “That to you was what the school-house at Shaston is

[033-061] to me.”

[033-062] “Yes; but I was not very happy there as you are in yours.”

[033-063] She closed her lips in retortive silence, and they walked some way till

[033-064] she glanced at him to see how he was taking it. “Of course I may have

[033-065] exaggerated your happiness—one never knows,” he continued blandly.

[033-066] “Don’t think that, Jude, for a moment, even though you may have said it

[033-067] to sting me! He’s as good to me as a man can be, and gives me perfect

[033-068] liberty—which elderly husbands don’t do in general… If you think I am

[033-069] not happy because he’s too old for me, you are wrong.”

[033-070] “I don’t think anything against him—to you dear.”

[033-071] “And you won’t say things to distress me, will you?”

[033-072] “I will not.”

[033-073] He said no more, but he knew that, from some cause or other, in taking

[033-074] Phillotson as a husband, Sue felt that she had done what she ought not

[033-075] to have done.

[033-076] They plunged into the concave field on the other side of which rose the

[033-077] village—the field wherein Jude had received a thrashing from the farmer

[033-078] many years earlier. On ascending to the village and approaching the

[033-079] house they found Mrs. Edlin standing at the door, who at sight of them

[033-080] lifted her hands deprecatingly. “She’s downstairs, if you’ll believe

[033-081] me!” cried the widow. “Out o’ bed she got, and nothing could turn her.

[033-082] What will come o’t I do not know!”

[033-083] On entering, there indeed by the fireplace sat the old woman, wrapped

[033-084] in blankets, and turning upon them a countenance like that of

[033-085] Sebastiano’s Lazarus. They must have looked their amazement, for she

[033-086] said in a hollow voice:

[033-087] “Ah—sceered ye, have I! I wasn’t going to bide up there no longer, to

[033-088] please nobody! ’Tis more than flesh and blood can bear, to be ordered

[033-089] to do this and that by a feller that don’t know half as well as you do

[033-090] yourself! … Ah—you’ll rue this marrying as well as he!” she added,

[033-091] turning to Sue. “All our family do—and nearly all everybody else’s. You

[033-092] should have done as I did, you simpleton! And Phillotson the

[033-093] schoolmaster, of all men! What made ’ee marry him?”

[033-094] “What makes most women marry, Aunt?”

[033-095] “Ah! You mean to say you loved the man!”

[033-096] “I don’t meant to say anything definite.”

[033-097] “Do ye love un?”

[033-098] “Don’t ask me, Aunt.”

[033-099] “I can mind the man very well. A very civil, honourable liver; but

[033-100] Lord!—I don’t want to wownd your feelings, but—there be certain men

[033-101] here and there that no woman of any niceness can stomach. I should have

[033-102] said he was one. I don’t say so _now_, since you must ha’ known better

[033-103] than I—but that’s what I _should_ have said!”

[033-104] Sue jumped up and went out. Jude followed her, and found her in the

[033-105] outhouse, crying.

[033-106] “Don’t cry, dear!” said Jude in distress. “She means well, but is very

[033-107] crusty and queer now, you know.”

[033-108] “Oh no—it isn’t that!” said Sue, trying to dry her eyes. “I don’t mind

[033-109] her roughness one bit.”

[033-110] “What is it, then?”

[033-111] “It is that what she says is—is true!”

[033-112] “God—what—you don’t like him?” asked Jude.

[033-113] “I don’t mean that!” she said hastily. “That I ought—perhaps I ought

[033-114] not to have married!”

[033-115] He wondered if she had really been going to say that at first. They

[033-116] went back, and the subject was smoothed over, and her aunt took rather

[033-117] kindly to Sue, telling her that not many young women newly married

[033-118] would have come so far to see a sick old crone like her. In the

[033-119] afternoon Sue prepared to depart, Jude hiring a neighbour to drive her

[033-120] to Alfredston.

[033-121] “I’ll go with you to the station, if you’d like?” he said.

[033-122] She would not let him. The man came round with the trap, and Jude

[033-123] helped her into it, perhaps with unnecessary attention, for she looked

[033-124] at him prohibitively.

[033-125] “I suppose—I may come to see you some day, when I am back again at

[033-126] Melchester?” he half-crossly observed.

[033-127] She bent down and said softly: “No, dear—you are not to come yet. I

[033-128] don’t think you are in a good mood.”

[033-129] “Very well,” said Jude. “Good-bye!”

[033-130] “Good-bye!” She waved her hand and was gone.

[033-131] “She’s right! I won’t go!” he murmured.

[033-132] He passed the evening and following days in mortifying by every

[033-133] possible means his wish to see her, nearly starving himself in attempts

[033-134] to extinguish by fasting his passionate tendency to love her. He read

[033-135] sermons on discipline, and hunted up passages in Church history that

[033-136] treated of the Ascetics of the second century. Before he had returned

[033-137] from Marygreen to Melchester there arrived a letter from Arabella. The

[033-138] sight of it revived a stronger feeling of self-condemnation for his

[033-139] brief return to her society than for his attachment to Sue.

[033-140] The letter, he perceived, bore a London postmark instead of the

[033-141] Christminster one. Arabella informed him that a few days after their

[033-142] parting in the morning at Christminster, she had been surprised by an

[033-143] affectionate letter from her Australian husband, formerly manager of

[033-144] the hotel in Sydney. He had come to England on purpose to find her; and

[033-145] had taken a free, fully-licensed public, in Lambeth, where he wished

[033-146] her to join him in conducting the business, which was likely to be a

[033-147] very thriving one, the house being situated in an excellent, densely

[033-148] populated, gin-drinking neighbourhood, and already doing a trade of

[033-149] £200 a month, which could be easily doubled.

[033-150] As he had said that he loved her very much still, and implored her to

[033-151] tell him where she was, and as they had only parted in a slight tiff,

[033-152] and as her engagement in Christminster was only temporary, she had just

[033-153] gone to join him as he urged. She could not help feeling that she

[033-154] belonged to him more than to Jude, since she had properly married him,

[033-155] and had lived with him much longer than with her first husband. In thus

[033-156] wishing Jude good-bye she bore him no ill-will, and trusted he would

[033-157] not turn upon her, a weak woman, and inform against her, and bring her

[033-158] to ruin now that she had a chance of improving her circumstances and

[033-159] leading a genteel life.

[033-160] X

[033-161] Jude returned to Melchester, which had the questionable recommendation

[033-162] of being only a dozen and a half miles from his Sue’s now permanent

[033-163] residence. At first he felt that this nearness was a distinct reason

[033-164] for not going southward at all; but Christminster was too sad a place

[033-165] to bear, while the proximity of Shaston to Melchester might afford him

[033-166] the glory of worsting the Enemy in a close engagement, such as was

[033-167] deliberately sought by the priests and virgins of the early Church,

[033-168] who, disdaining an ignominious flight from temptation, became even

[033-169] chamber-partners with impunity. Jude did not pause to remember that, in

[033-170] the laconic words of the historian, “insulted Nature sometimes

[033-171] vindicated her rights” in such circumstances.

[033-172] He now returned with feverish desperation to his study for the

[033-173] priesthood—in the recognition that the single-mindedness of his aims,

[033-174] and his fidelity to the cause, had been more than questionable of late.

[033-175] His passion for Sue troubled his soul; yet his lawful abandonment to

[033-176] the society of Arabella for twelve hours seemed instinctively a worse

[033-177] thing—even though she had not told him of her Sydney husband till

[033-178] afterwards. He had, he verily believed, overcome all tendency to fly to

[033-179] liquor—which, indeed, he had never done from taste, but merely as an

[033-180] escape from intolerable misery of mind. Yet he perceived with

[033-181] despondency that, taken all round, he was a man of too many passions to

[033-182] make a good clergyman; the utmost he could hope for was that in a life

[033-183] of constant internal warfare between flesh and spirit the former might

[033-184] not always be victorious.

[033-185] As a hobby, auxiliary to his readings in Divinity, he developed his

[033-186] slight skill in church-music and thorough-bass, till he could join in

[033-187] part-singing from notation with some accuracy. A mile or two from

[033-188] Melchester there was a restored village church, to which Jude had

[033-189] originally gone to fix the new columns and capitals. By this means he

[033-190] had become acquainted with the organist, and the ultimate result was

[033-191] that he joined the choir as a bass voice.
