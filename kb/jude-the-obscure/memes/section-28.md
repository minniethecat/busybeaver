[028-001] The interior of the room—the books, the furniture, the schoolmaster’s

[028-002] loose coat, his attitude at the table, even the flickering of the fire,

[028-003] bespoke the same dignified tale of undistracted research—more than

[028-004] creditable to a man who had had no advantages beyond those of his own

[028-005] making. And yet the tale, true enough till latterly, was not true now.

[028-006] What he was regarding was not history. They were historic notes,

[028-007] written in a bold womanly hand at his dictation some months before, and

[028-008] it was the clerical rendering of word after word that absorbed him.

[028-009] He presently took from a drawer a carefully tied bundle of letters,

[028-010] few, very few, as correspondence counts nowadays. Each was in its

[028-011] envelope just as it had arrived, and the handwriting was of the same

[028-012] womanly character as the historic notes. He unfolded them one by one

[028-013] and read them musingly. At first sight there seemed in these small

[028-014] documents to be absolutely nothing to muse over. They were

[028-015] straightforward, frank letters, signed “Sue B—”; just such ones as

[028-016] would be written during short absences, with no other thought than

[028-017] their speedy destruction, and chiefly concerning books in reading and

[028-018] other experiences of a training school, forgotten doubtless by the

[028-019] writer with the passing of the day of their inditing. In one of

[028-020] them—quite a recent note—the young woman said that she had received his

[028-021] considerate letter, and that it was honourable and generous of him to

[028-022] say he would not come to see her oftener than she desired (the school

[028-023] being such an awkward place for callers, and because of her strong wish

[028-024] that her engagement to him should not be known, which it would

[028-025] infallibly be if he visited her often). Over these phrases the

[028-026] school-master pored. What precise shade of satisfaction was to be

[028-027] gathered from a woman’s gratitude that the man who loved her had not

[028-028] been often to see her? The problem occupied him, distracted him.

[028-029] He opened another drawer, and found therein an envelope, from which he

[028-030] drew a photograph of Sue as a child, long before he had known her,

[028-031] standing under trellis-work with a little basket in her hand. There was

[028-032] another of her as a young woman, her dark eyes and hair making a very

[028-033] distinct and attractive picture of her, which just disclosed, too, the

[028-034] thoughtfulness that lay behind her lighter moods. It was a duplicate of

[028-035] the one she had given Jude, and would have given to any man. Phillotson

[028-036] brought it half-way to his lips, but withdrew it in doubt at her

[028-037] perplexing phrases: ultimately kissing the dead pasteboard with all the

[028-038] passionateness, and more than all the devotion, of a young man of

[028-039] eighteen.

[028-040] The schoolmaster’s was an unhealthy-looking, old-fashioned face,

[028-041] rendered more old-fashioned by his style of shaving. A certain

[028-042] gentlemanliness had been imparted to it by nature, suggesting an

[028-043] inherent wish to do rightly by all. His speech was a little slow, but

[028-044] his tones were sincere enough to make his hesitation no defect. His

[028-045] greying hair was curly, and radiated from a point in the middle of his

[028-046] crown. There were four lines across his forehead, and he only wore

[028-047] spectacles when reading at night. It was almost certainly a

[028-048] renunciation forced upon him by his academic purpose, rather than a

[028-049] distaste for women, which had hitherto kept him from closing with one

[028-050] of the sex in matrimony.

[028-051] Such silent proceedings as those of this evening were repeated many and

[028-052] oft times when he was not under the eye of the boys, whose quick and

[028-053] penetrating regard would frequently become almost intolerable to the

[028-054] self-conscious master in his present anxious care for Sue, making him,

[028-055] in the grey hours of morning, dread to meet anew the gimlet glances,

[028-056] lest they should read what the dream within him was.

[028-057] He had honourably acquiesced in Sue’s announced wish that he was not

[028-058] often to visit her at the training school; but at length, his patience

[028-059] being sorely tried, he set out one Saturday afternoon to pay her an

[028-060] unexpected call. There the news of her departure—expulsion as it might

[028-061] almost have been considered—was flashed upon him without warning or

[028-062] mitigation as he stood at the door expecting in a few minutes to behold

[028-063] her face; and when he turned away he could hardly see the road before

[028-064] him.

[028-065] Sue had, in fact, never written a line to her suitor on the subject,

[028-066] although it was fourteen days old. A short reflection told him that

[028-067] this proved nothing, a natural delicacy being as ample a reason for

[028-068] silence as any degree of blameworthiness.

[028-069] They had informed him at the school where she was living, and having no

[028-070] immediate anxiety about her comfort, his thoughts took the direction of

[028-071] a burning indignation against the training school committee. In his

[028-072] bewilderment Phillotson entered the adjacent cathedral, just now in a

[028-073] direly dismantled state by reason of the repairs. He sat down on a

[028-074] block of freestone, regardless of the dusty imprint it made on his

[028-075] breeches; and his listless eyes following the movements of the workmen

[028-076] he presently became aware that the reputed culprit, Sue’s lover Jude,

[028-077] was one amongst them.

[028-078] Jude had never spoken to his former hero since the meeting by the model

[028-079] of Jerusalem. Having inadvertently witnessed Phillotson’s tentative

[028-080] courtship of Sue in the lane there had grown up in the younger man’s

[028-081] mind a curious dislike to think of the elder, to meet him, to

[028-082] communicate in any way with him; and since Phillotson’s success in

[028-083] obtaining at least her promise had become known to Jude, he had frankly

[028-084] recognized that he did not wish to see or hear of his senior any more,

[028-085] learn anything of his pursuits, or even imagine again what excellencies

[028-086] might appertain to his character. On this very day of the

[028-087] schoolmaster’s visit Jude was expecting Sue, as she had promised; and

[028-088] when therefore he saw the schoolmaster in the nave of the building,

[028-089] saw, moreover, that he was coming to speak to him, he felt no little

[028-090] embarrassment; which Phillotson’s own embarrassment prevented his

[028-091] observing.

[028-092] Jude joined him, and they both withdrew from the other workmen to the

[028-093] spot where Phillotson had been sitting. Jude offered him a piece of

[028-094] sackcloth for a cushion, and told him it was dangerous to sit on the

[028-095] bare block.

[028-096] “Yes; yes,” said Phillotson abstractedly, as he reseated himself, his

[028-097] eyes resting on the ground as if he were trying to remember where he

[028-098] was. “I won’t keep you long. It was merely that I have heard that you

[028-099] have seen my little friend Sue recently. It occurred to me to speak to

[028-100] you on that account. I merely want to ask—about her.”

[028-101] “I think I know what!” Jude hurriedly said. “About her escaping from

[028-102] the training school, and her coming to me?”

[028-103] “Yes.”

[028-104] “Well”—Jude for a moment felt an unprincipled and fiendish wish to

[028-105] annihilate his rival at all cost. By the exercise of that treachery

[028-106] which love for the same woman renders possible to men the most

[028-107] honourable in every other relation of life, he could send off

[028-108] Phillotson in agony and defeat by saying that the scandal was true, and

[028-109] that Sue had irretrievably committed herself with him. But his action

[028-110] did not respond for a moment to his animal instinct; and what he said

[028-111] was, “I am glad of your kindness in coming to talk plainly to me about

[028-112] it. You know what they say?—that I ought to marry her.”

[028-113] “What!”

[028-114] “And I wish with all my soul I could!”

[028-115] Phillotson trembled, and his naturally pale face acquired a corpselike

[028-116] sharpness in its lines. “I had no idea that it was of this nature! God

[028-117] forbid!”

[028-118] “No, no!” said Jude aghast. “I thought you understood? I mean that were

[028-119] I in a position to marry her, or someone, and settle down, instead of

[028-120] living in lodgings here and there, I should be glad!”

[028-121] What he had really meant was simply that he loved her.

[028-122] “But—since this painful matter has been opened up—what really

[028-123] happened?” asked Phillotson, with the firmness of a man who felt that a

[028-124] sharp smart now was better than a long agony of suspense hereafter.

[028-125] “Cases arise, and this is one, when even ungenerous questions must be

[028-126] put to make false assumptions impossible, and to kill scandal.”

[028-127] Jude explained readily; giving the whole series of adventures,

[028-128] including the night at the shepherd’s, her wet arrival at his lodging,

[028-129] her indisposition from her immersion, their vigil of discussion, and

[028-130] his seeing her off next morning.

[028-131] “Well now,” said Phillotson at the conclusion, “I take it as your final

[028-132] word, and I know I can believe you, that the suspicion which led to her

[028-133] rustication is an absolutely baseless one?”

[028-134] “It is,” said Jude solemnly. “Absolutely. So help me God!”

[028-135] The schoolmaster rose. Each of the twain felt that the interview could

[028-136] not comfortably merge in a friendly discussion of their recent

[028-137] experiences, after the manner of friends; and when Jude had taken him

[028-138] round, and shown him some features of the renovation which the old

[028-139] cathedral was undergoing, Phillotson bade the young man good-day and

[028-140] went away.

[028-141] This visit took place about eleven o’clock in the morning; but no Sue

[028-142] appeared. When Jude went to his dinner at one he saw his beloved ahead

[028-143] of him in the street leading up from the North Gate, walking as if no

[028-144] way looking for him. Speedily overtaking her he remarked that he had

[028-145] asked her to come to him at the cathedral, and she had promised.

[028-146] “I have been to get my things from the college,” she said—an

[028-147] observation which he was expected to take as an answer, though it was

[028-148] not one. Finding her to be in this evasive mood he felt inclined to

[028-149] give her the information so long withheld.

[028-150] “You have not seen Mr. Phillotson to-day?” he ventured to inquire.

[028-151] “I have not. But I am not going to be cross-examined about him; and if

[028-152] you ask anything more I won’t answer!”

[028-153] “It is very odd that—” He stopped, regarding her.

[028-154] “What?”

[028-155] “That you are often not so nice in your real presence as you are in

[028-156] your letters!”

[028-157] “Does it really seem so to you?” said she, smiling with quick

[028-158] curiosity. “Well, that’s strange; but I feel just the same about you,

[028-159] Jude. When you are gone away I seem such a coldhearted—”

[028-160] As she knew his sentiment towards her Jude saw that they were getting

[028-161] upon dangerous ground. It was now, he thought, that he must speak as an

[028-162] honest man.

[028-163] But he did not speak, and she continued: “It was that which made me

[028-164] write and say—I didn’t mind your loving me—if you wanted to, much!”

[028-165] The exultation he might have felt at what that implied, or seemed to

[028-166] imply, was nullified by his intention, and he rested rigid till he

[028-167] began: “I have never told you—”

[028-168] “Yes you have,” murmured she.

[028-169] “I mean, I have never told you my history—all of it.”

[028-170] “But I guess it. I know nearly.”

[028-171] Jude looked up. Could she possibly know of that morning performance of

[028-172] his with Arabella; which in a few months had ceased to be a marriage

[028-173] more completely than by death? He saw that she did not.

[028-174] “I can’t quite tell you here in the street,” he went on with a gloomy

[028-175] tongue. “And you had better not come to my lodgings. Let us go in

[028-176] here.”

[028-177] The building by which they stood was the market-house; it was the only

[028-178] place available; and they entered, the market being over, and the

[028-179] stalls and areas empty. He would have preferred a more congenial spot,

[028-180] but, as usually happens, in place of a romantic field or solemn aisle

[028-181] for his tale, it was told while they walked up and down over a floor

[028-182] littered with rotten cabbage-leaves, and amid all the usual squalors of

[028-183] decayed vegetable matter and unsaleable refuse. He began and finished

[028-184] his brief narrative, which merely led up to the information that he had

[028-185] married a wife some years earlier, and that his wife was living still.
