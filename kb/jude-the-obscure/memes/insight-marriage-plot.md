# The Marriage Plot: Marriage as Institutional Antagonist

Marriage in *Jude the Obscure* is not a relationship — it is a legal, religious, and social institution that systematically destroys everyone it touches. Jude marries Arabella through deception, Sue marries Phillotson through conformity, and both marriages become prisons from which there is no legal escape. This insight article examines how Hardy weaponizes the marriage plot against the Victorian novel's most cherished convention — the happy ending — to argue that the institution of marriage is fundamentally incompatible with love.

## Thesis

*Jude the Obscure* inverts the traditional marriage plot of the nineteenth-century novel. Where Austen and Dickens use marriage as resolution, Hardy uses it as catastrophe. The novel traces not the path *toward* marriage but the impossibility of escape *from* it. Every marriage in the book is a trap; every attempt to escape one is punished; and the only character who treats marriage purely as a strategic instrument — Arabella — is the only one who survives.

## The Three Marriages

### Jude + Arabella: The Trap

Jude's first marriage is engineered by Arabella through the false claim of pregnancy. The deception is crude but effective: in a society where pregnancy outside marriage means social death, Jude has no choice. The marriage is consummated in haste and collapses almost immediately — Arabella departs for Australia, and Jude is left legally bound to a woman he despises.

Arabella's approach to marriage is entirely instrumental. She uses it for security, abandons it when a better prospect appears (Cartlett in Australia), and reclaims it when that prospect dies. Her final act — remarrying the dying Jude — is the novel's blackest joke: she acquires marriage as one acquires property, and at the end, she has everything while Jude and Sue have nothing.

### Sue + Phillotson: The Cage

Sue's marriage to Phillotson is the novel's most painful because it is the most conventional. She marries him not from passion or calculation but from a kind of exhausted compliance — a surrender to the social expectation that a woman of her age and position must marry. Phillotson is not cruel; he is kind and well-meaning. But kindness within a cage is still imprisonment.

Sue's physical revulsion at Phillotson's touch, her escape through the window, and her eventual return to him after the children's deaths form the novel's most brutal arc. Marriage claims her, releases her, and re-claims her — not through force but through the internalized guilt of religious conversion. The tragedy is not that Sue cannot escape Phillotson but that she cannot escape the belief that she *should* be with him.

### The Cohabitation That Cannot Be

Jude and Sue's years together — unmarried, in defiance of law and convention — represent the novel's utopian alternative. Yet their relationship is systematically destroyed from without: they are denied housing, employment, and social existence. The children's deaths are the catastrophic endpoint of this exclusion: Little Father Time kills himself and his siblings because "we are too many" — a child's brutal paraphrase of what society has been telling his parents for years.

## Marriage and Law

Victorian marriage law was, for Hardy's purposes, perfectly calibrated to produce tragedy. Divorce required an Act of Parliament for most of the nineteenth century; even after the Matrimonial Causes Act of 1857, it remained prohibitively expensive and socially ruinous. Jude cannot divorce Arabella because he cannot prove her adultery in England; Sue cannot divorce Phillotson without destroying her reputation.

The novel documents the gap between law and justice with forensic precision. Arabella's bigamous return to Jude (she married Cartlett without divorcing Jude) is technically illegal but practically unpunishable — the law cannot reach her in Australia. Phillotson's release of Sue — the most morally admirable act in the book — destroys his career. The law punishes virtue and rewards cunning.

## Marriage and Religion

The religious dimension compounds the legal one. Sue's reconversion after the children's deaths drives her back to Phillotson not because she loves him but because she believes her suffering is divine punishment for living outside marriage. Her return is not a choice but a penance — a self-inflicted wound dressed in theological language.

The irony is brutal: the church that Jude once dreamed of serving as a clergyman becomes the instrument of his final destruction. The marriage ceremony Sue undergoes with Phillotson at the novel's end is not a resolution but an execution — the death of everything Jude and Sue fought for.

## Conclusion

Hardy's marriage plot is an anti-marriage plot. It takes the nineteenth-century novel's most reliable source of narrative closure and transforms it into an engine of tragedy. Marriage in *Jude the Obscure* does not resolve conflict — it *is* the conflict. The novel's final image — Arabella watching Jude's funeral procession while planning her next conquest — is Hardy's verdict on the institution: marriage survives, and love does not.
