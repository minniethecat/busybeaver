[019-001] “You ought to have told me,” she gasped petulantly, “that one of the

[019-002] inspector’s surprise-visits was imminent! Oh, what shall I do! Now

[019-003] he’ll write and tell the managers that I am no good, and I shall be

[019-004] disgraced for ever!”

[019-005] “He won’t do that, my dear little girl. You are the best teacher ever I

[019-006] had!”

[019-007] He looked so gently at her that she was moved, and regretted that she

[019-008] had upbraided him. When she was better she went home.

[019-009] Jude in the meantime had been waiting impatiently for Friday. On both

[019-010] Wednesday and Thursday he had been so much under the influence of his

[019-011] desire to see her that he walked after dark some distance along the

[019-012] road in the direction of the village, and, on returning to his room to

[019-013] read, found himself quite unable to concentrate his mind on the page.

[019-014] On Friday, as soon as he had got himself up as he thought Sue would

[019-015] like to see him, and made a hasty tea, he set out, notwithstanding that

[019-016] the evening was wet. The trees overhead deepened the gloom of the hour,

[019-017] and they dripped sadly upon him, impressing him with

[019-018] forebodings—illogical forebodings; for though he knew that he loved her

[019-019] he also knew that he could not be more to her than he was.

[019-020] On turning the corner and entering the village the first sight that

[019-021] greeted his eyes was that of two figures under one umbrella coming out

[019-022] of the vicarage gate. He was too far back for them to notice him, but

[019-023] he knew in a moment that they were Sue and Phillotson. The latter was

[019-024] holding the umbrella over her head, and they had evidently been paying

[019-025] a visit to the vicar—probably on some business connected with the

[019-026] school work. And as they walked along the wet and deserted lane Jude

[019-027] saw Phillotson place his arm round the girl’s waist; whereupon she

[019-028] gently removed it; but he replaced it; and she let it remain, looking

[019-029] quickly round her with an air of misgiving. She did not look absolutely

[019-030] behind her, and therefore did not see Jude, who sank into the hedge

[019-031] like one struck with a blight. There he remained hidden till they had

[019-032] reached Sue’s cottage and she had passed in, Phillotson going on to the

[019-033] school hard by.

[019-034] “Oh, he’s too old for her—too old!” cried Jude in all the terrible

[019-035] sickness of hopeless, handicapped love.

[019-036] He could not interfere. Was he not Arabella’s? He was unable to go on

[019-037] further, and retraced his steps towards Christminster. Every tread of

[019-038] his feet seemed to say to him that he must on no account stand in the

[019-039] schoolmaster’s way with Sue. Phillotson was perhaps twenty years her

[019-040] senior, but many a happy marriage had been made in such conditions of

[019-041] age. The ironical clinch to his sorrow was given by the thought that

[019-042] the intimacy between his cousin and the schoolmaster had been brought

[019-043] about entirely by himself.

[019-044] VI

[019-045] Jude’s old and embittered aunt lay unwell at Marygreen, and on the

[019-046] following Sunday he went to see her—a visit which was the result of a

[019-047] victorious struggle against his inclination to turn aside to the

[019-048] village of Lumsdon and obtain a miserable interview with his cousin, in

[019-049] which the word nearest his heart could not be spoken, and the sight

[019-050] which had tortured him could not be revealed.

[019-051] His aunt was now unable to leave her bed, and a great part of Jude’s

[019-052] short day was occupied in making arrangements for her comfort. The

[019-053] little bakery business had been sold to a neighbour, and with the

[019-054] proceeds of this and her savings she was comfortably supplied with

[019-055] necessaries and more, a widow of the same village living with her and

[019-056] ministering to her wants. It was not till the time had nearly come for

[019-057] him to leave that he obtained a quiet talk with her, and his words

[019-058] tended insensibly towards his cousin.

[019-059] “Was Sue born here?”

[019-060] “She was—in this room. They were living here at that time. What made

[019-061] ’ee ask that?”

[019-062] “Oh—I wanted to know.”

[019-063] “Now you’ve been seeing her!” said the harsh old woman. “And what did I

[019-064] tell ’ee?”

[019-065] “Well—that I was not to see her.”

[019-066] “Have you gossiped with her?”

[019-067] “Yes.”

[019-068] “Then don’t keep it up. She was brought up by her father to hate her

[019-069] mother’s family; and she’ll look with no favour upon a working chap

[019-070] like you—a townish girl as she’s become by now. I never cared much

[019-071] about her. A pert little thing, that’s what she was too often, with her

[019-072] tight-strained nerves. Many’s the time I’ve smacked her for her

[019-073] impertinence. Why, one day when she was walking into the pond with her

[019-074] shoes and stockings off, and her petticoats pulled above her knees,

[019-075] afore I could cry out for shame, she said: ‘Move on, Aunty! This is no

[019-076] sight for modest eyes!’”

[019-077] “She was a little child then.”

[019-078] “She was twelve if a day.”

[019-079] “Well—of course. But now she’s older she’s of a thoughtful, quivering,

[019-080] tender nature, and as sensitive as—”

[019-081] “Jude!” cried his aunt, springing up in bed. “Don’t you be a fool about

[019-082] her!”

[019-083] “No, no, of course not.”

[019-084] “Your marrying that woman Arabella was about as bad a thing as a man

[019-085] could possibly do for himself by trying hard. But she’s gone to the

[019-086] other side of the world, and med never trouble you again. And there’ll

[019-087] be a worse thing if you, tied and bound as you be, should have a fancy

[019-088] for Sue. If your cousin is civil to you, take her civility for what it

[019-089] is worth. But anything more than a relation’s good wishes it is stark

[019-090] madness for ’ee to give her. If she’s townish and wanton it med bring

[019-091] ’ee to ruin.”

[019-092] “Don’t say anything against her, Aunt! Don’t, please!”

[019-093] A relief was afforded to him by the entry of the companion and nurse of

[019-094] his aunt, who must have been listening to the conversation, for she

[019-095] began a commentary on past years, introducing Sue Bridehead as a

[019-096] character in her recollections. She described what an odd little maid

[019-097] Sue had been when a pupil at the village school across the green

[019-098] opposite, before her father went to London—how, when the vicar arranged

[019-099] readings and recitations, she appeared on the platform, the smallest of

[019-100] them all, “in her little white frock, and shoes, and pink sash”; how

[019-101] she recited “Excelsior,” “There was a sound of revelry by night,” and

[019-102] “The Raven”; how during the delivery she would knit her little brows

[019-103] and glare round tragically, and say to the empty air, as if some real

[019-104] creature stood there—

[019-105] “Ghastly, grim, and ancient Raven,

[019-106] wandering from the Nightly shore,

[019-107] Tell me what thy lordly name is

[019-108] on the Night’s Plutonian shore!”

[019-109] “She’d bring up the nasty carrion bird that clear,” corroborated the

[019-110] sick woman reluctantly, “as she stood there in her little sash and

[019-111] things, that you could see un a’most before your very eyes. You too,

[019-112] Jude, had the same trick as a child of seeming to see things in the

[019-113] air.”

[019-114] The neighbour told also of Sue’s accomplishments in other kinds:

[019-115] “She was not exactly a tomboy, you know; but she could do things that

[019-116] only boys do, as a rule. I’ve seen her hit in and steer down the long

[019-117] slide on yonder pond, with her little curls blowing, one of a file of

[019-118] twenty moving along against the sky like shapes painted on glass, and

[019-119] up the back slide without stopping. All boys except herself; and then

[019-120] they’d cheer her, and then she’d say, ‘Don’t be saucy, boys,’ and

[019-121] suddenly run indoors. They’d try to coax her out again. But ’a wouldn’t

[019-122] come.”

[019-123] These retrospective visions of Sue only made Jude the more miserable

[019-124] that he was unable to woo her, and he left the cottage of his aunt that

[019-125] day with a heavy heart. He would fain have glanced into the school to

[019-126] see the room in which Sue’s little figure had so glorified itself; but

[019-127] he checked his desire and went on.

[019-128] It being Sunday evening some villagers who had known him during his

[019-129] residence here were standing in a group in their best clothes. Jude was

[019-130] startled by a salute from one of them:

[019-131] “Ye’ve got there right enough, then!”

[019-132] Jude showed that he did not understand.

[019-133] “Why, to the seat of l’arning—the ‘City of Light’ you used to talk to

[019-134] us about as a little boy! Is it all you expected of it?”

[019-135] “Yes; more!” cried Jude.

[019-136] “When I was there once for an hour I didn’t see much in it for my part;

[019-137] auld crumbling buildings, half church, half almshouse, and not much

[019-138] going on at that.”

[019-139] “You are wrong, John; there is more going on than meets the eye of a

[019-140] man walking through the streets. It is a unique centre of thought and

[019-141] religion—the intellectual and spiritual granary of this country. All

[019-142] that silence and absence of goings-on is the stillness of infinite

[019-143] motion—the sleep of the spinning-top, to borrow the simile of a

[019-144] well-known writer.”

[019-145] “Oh, well, it med be all that, or it med not. As I say, I didn’t see

[019-146] nothing of it the hour or two I was there; so I went in and had a pot

[019-147] o’ beer, and a penny loaf, and a ha’porth o’ cheese, and waited till it

[019-148] was time to come along home. You’ve j’ined a college by this time, I

[019-149] suppose?”

[019-150] “Ah, no!” said Jude. “I am almost as far off that as ever.”

[019-151] “How so?”

[019-152] Jude slapped his pocket.

[019-153] “Just what we thought! Such places be not for such as you—only for them

[019-154] with plenty o’ money.”

[019-155] “There you are wrong,” said Jude, with some bitterness. “They are for

[019-156] such ones!”

[019-157] Still, the remark was sufficient to withdraw Jude’s attention from the

[019-158] imaginative world he had lately inhabited, in which an abstract figure,

[019-159] more or less himself, was steeping his mind in a sublimation of the

[019-160] arts and sciences, and making his calling and election sure to a seat

[019-161] in the paradise of the learned. He was set regarding his prospects in a

[019-162] cold northern light. He had lately felt that he could not quite satisfy

[019-163] himself in his Greek—in the Greek of the dramatists particularly. So

[019-164] fatigued was he sometimes after his day’s work that he could not

[019-165] maintain the critical attention necessary for thorough application. He

[019-166] felt that he wanted a coach—a friend at his elbow to tell him in a

[019-167] moment what sometimes would occupy him a weary month in extracting from

[019-168] unanticipative, clumsy books.

[019-169] It was decidedly necessary to consider facts a little more closely than

[019-170] he had done of late. What was the good, after all, of using up his

[019-171] spare hours in a vague labour called “private study” without giving an

[019-172] outlook on practicabilities?

[019-173] “I ought to have thought of this before,” he said, as he journeyed

[019-174] back. “It would have been better never to have embarked in the scheme

[019-175] at all than to do it without seeing clearly where I am going, or what I

[019-176] am aiming at… This hovering outside the walls of the colleges, as if

[019-177] expecting some arm to be stretched out from them to lift me inside,

[019-178] won’t do! I must get special information.”

[019-179] The next week accordingly he sought it. What at first seemed an

[019-180] opportunity occurred one afternoon when he saw an elderly gentleman,

[019-181] who had been pointed out as the head of a particular college, walking

[019-182] in the public path of a parklike enclosure near the spot at which Jude

[019-183] chanced to be sitting. The gentleman came nearer, and Jude looked

[019-184] anxiously at his face. It seemed benign, considerate, yet rather

[019-185] reserved. On second thoughts Jude felt that he could not go up and

[019-186] address him; but he was sufficiently influenced by the incident to

[019-187] think what a wise thing it would be for him to state his difficulties

[019-188] by letter to some of the best and most judicious of these old masters,

[019-189] and obtain their advice.
