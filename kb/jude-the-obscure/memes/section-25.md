[025-001] “Oh yes!”

[025-002] “Then don’t come down. Shut the window.”

[025-003] Jude waited, knowing that she could enter easily enough, the front door

[025-004] being opened merely by a knob which anybody could turn, as in most old

[025-005] country towns. He palpitated at the thought that she had fled to him in

[025-006] her trouble as he had fled to her in his. What counterparts they were!

[025-007] He unlatched the door of his room, heard a stealthy rustle on the dark

[025-008] stairs, and in a moment she appeared in the light of his lamp. He went

[025-009] up to seize her hand, and found she was clammy as a marine deity, and

[025-010] that her clothes clung to her like the robes upon the figures in the

[025-011] Parthenon frieze.

[025-012] “I’m so cold!” she said through her chattering teeth. “Can I come by

[025-013] your fire, Jude?”

[025-014] She crossed to his little grate and very little fire, but as the water

[025-015] dripped from her as she moved, the idea of drying herself was absurd.

[025-016] “Whatever have you done, darling?” he asked, with alarm, the tender

[025-017] epithet slipping out unawares.

[025-018] “Walked through the largest river in the county—that’s what I’ve done!

[025-019] They locked me up for being out with you; and it seemed so unjust that

[025-020] I couldn’t bear it, so I got out of the window and escaped across the

[025-021] stream!” She had begun the explanation in her usual slightly

[025-022] independent tones, but before she had finished the thin pink lips

[025-023] trembled, and she could hardly refrain from crying.

[025-024] “Dear Sue!” he said. “You must take off all your things! And let me

[025-025] see—you must borrow some from the landlady. I’ll ask her.”

[025-026] “No, no! Don’t let her know, for God’s sake! We are so near the school

[025-027] that they’ll come after me!”

[025-028] “Then you must put on mine. You don’t mind?”

[025-029] “Oh no.”

[025-030] “My Sunday suit, you know. It is close here.” In fact, everything was

[025-031] close and handy in Jude’s single chamber, because there was not room

[025-032] for it to be otherwise. He opened a drawer, took out his best dark

[025-033] suit, and giving the garments a shake, said, “Now, how long shall I

[025-034] give you?”

[025-035] “Ten minutes.”

[025-036] Jude left the room and went into the street, where he walked up and

[025-037] down. A clock struck half-past seven, and he returned. Sitting in his

[025-038] only arm-chair he saw a slim and fragile being masquerading as himself

[025-039] on a Sunday, so pathetic in her defencelessness that his heart felt big

[025-040] with the sense of it. On two other chairs before the fire were her wet

[025-041] garments. She blushed as he sat down beside her, but only for a moment.

[025-042] “I suppose, Jude, it is odd that you should see me like this and all my

[025-043] things hanging there? Yet what nonsense! They are only a woman’s

[025-044] clothes—sexless cloth and linen… I wish I didn’t feel so ill and sick!

[025-045] Will you dry my clothes now? Please do, Jude, and I’ll get a lodging by

[025-046] and by. It is not late yet.”

[025-047] “No, you shan’t, if you are ill. You must stay here. Dear, dear Sue,

[025-048] what can I get for you?”

[025-049] “I don’t know! I can’t help shivering. I wish I could get warm.” Jude

[025-050] put on her his great-coat in addition, and then ran out to the nearest

[025-051] public-house, whence he returned with a little bottle in his hand.

[025-052] “Here’s six of best brandy,” he said. “Now you drink it, dear; all of

[025-053] it.”

[025-054] “I can’t out of the bottle, can I?” Jude fetched the glass from the

[025-055] dressing-table, and administered the spirit in some water. She gasped a

[025-056] little, but gulped it down, and lay back in the armchair.

[025-057] She then began to relate circumstantially her experiences since they

[025-058] had parted; but in the middle of her story her voice faltered, her head

[025-059] nodded, and she ceased. She was in a sound sleep. Jude, dying of

[025-060] anxiety lest she should have caught a chill which might permanently

[025-061] injure her, was glad to hear the regular breathing. He softly went

[025-062] nearer to her, and observed that a warm flush now rosed her hitherto

[025-063] blue cheeks, and felt that her hanging hand was no longer cold. Then he

[025-064] stood with his back to the fire regarding her, and saw in her almost a

[025-065] divinity.

[025-066] IV

[025-067] Jude’s reverie was interrupted by the creak of footsteps ascending the

[025-068] stairs.

[025-069] He whisked Sue’s clothing from the chair where it was drying, thrust it

[025-070] under the bed, and sat down to his book. Somebody knocked and opened

[025-071] the door immediately. It was the landlady.

[025-072] “Oh, I didn’t know whether you was in or not, Mr. Fawley. I wanted to

[025-073] know if you would require supper. I see you’ve a young gentleman—”

[025-074] “Yes, ma’am. But I think I won’t come down to-night. Will you bring

[025-075] supper up on a tray, and I’ll have a cup of tea as well.”

[025-076] It was Jude’s custom to go downstairs to the kitchen, and eat his meals

[025-077] with the family, to save trouble. His landlady brought up the supper,

[025-078] however, on this occasion, and he took it from her at the door.

[025-079] When she had descended he set the teapot on the hob, and drew out Sue’s

[025-080] clothes anew; but they were far from dry. A thick woollen gown, he

[025-081] found, held a deal of water. So he hung them up again, and enlarged his

[025-082] fire and mused as the steam from the garments went up the chimney.

[025-083] Suddenly she said, “Jude!”

[025-084] “Yes. All right. How do you feel now?”

[025-085] “Better. Quite well. Why, I fell asleep, didn’t I? What time is it? Not

[025-086] late surely?”

[025-087] “It is past ten.”

[025-088] “Is it really? What _shall_ I do!” she said, starting up.

[025-089] “Stay where you are.”

[025-090] “Yes; that’s what I want to do. But I don’t know what they would say!

[025-091] And what will you do?”

[025-092] “I am going to sit here by the fire all night, and read. To-morrow is

[025-093] Sunday, and I haven’t to go out anywhere. Perhaps you will be saved a

[025-094] severe illness by resting there. Don’t be frightened. I’m all right.

[025-095] Look here, what I have got for you. Some supper.”

[025-096] When she had sat upright she breathed plaintively and said, “I do feel

[025-097] rather weak still. I thought I was well; and I ought not to be here,

[025-098] ought I?” But the supper fortified her somewhat, and when she had had

[025-099] some tea and had lain back again she was bright and cheerful.

[025-100] The tea must have been green, or too long drawn, for she seemed

[025-101] preternaturally wakeful afterwards, though Jude, who had not taken any,

[025-102] began to feel heavy; till her conversation fixed his attention.

[025-103] “You called me a creature of civilization, or something, didn’t you?”

[025-104] she said, breaking a silence. “It was very odd you should have done

[025-105] that.”

[025-106] “Why?”

[025-107] “Well, because it is provokingly wrong. I am a sort of negation of it.”

[025-108] “You are very philosophical. ‘A negation’ is profound talking.”

[025-109] “Is it? Do I strike you as being learned?” she asked, with a touch of

[025-110] raillery.

[025-111] “No—not learned. Only you don’t talk quite like a girl—well, a girl who

[025-112] has had no advantages.”

[025-113] “I have had advantages. I don’t know Latin and Greek, though I know the

[025-114] grammars of those tongues. But I know most of the Greek and Latin

[025-115] classics through translations, and other books too. I read Lemprière,

[025-116] Catullus, Martial, Juvenal, Lucian, Beaumont and Fletcher, Boccaccio,

[025-117] Scarron, De Brantôme, Sterne, De Foe, Smollett, Fielding, Shakespeare,

[025-118] the Bible, and other such; and found that all interest in the

[025-119] unwholesome part of those books ended with its mystery.”

[025-120] “You have read more than I,” he said with a sigh. “How came you to read

[025-121] some of those queerer ones?”

[025-122] “Well,” she said thoughtfully, “it was by accident. My life has been

[025-123] entirely shaped by what people call a peculiarity in me. I have no fear

[025-124] of men, as such, nor of their books. I have mixed with them—one or two

[025-125] of them particularly—almost as one of their own sex. I mean I have not

[025-126] felt about them as most women are taught to feel—to be on their guard

[025-127] against attacks on their virtue; for no average man—no man short of a

[025-128] sensual savage—will molest a woman by day or night, at home or abroad,

[025-129] unless she invites him. Until she says by a look ‘Come on’ he is always

[025-130] afraid to, and if you never say it, or look it, he never comes.

[025-131] However, what I was going to say is that when I was eighteen I formed a

[025-132] friendly intimacy with an undergraduate at Christminster, and he taught

[025-133] me a great deal, and lent me books which I should never have got hold

[025-134] of otherwise.”

[025-135] “Is your friendship broken off?”

[025-136] “Oh yes. He died, poor fellow, two or three years after he had taken

[025-137] his degree and left Christminster.”

[025-138] “You saw a good deal of him, I suppose?”

[025-139] “Yes. We used to go about together—on walking tours, reading tours, and

[025-140] things of that sort—like two men almost. He asked me to live with him,

[025-141] and I agreed to by letter. But when I joined him in London I found he

[025-142] meant a different thing from what I meant. He wanted me to be his

[025-143] mistress, in fact, but I wasn’t in love with him—and on my saying I

[025-144] should go away if he didn’t agree to _my_ plan, he did so. We shared a

[025-145] sitting-room for fifteen months; and he became a leader-writer for one

[025-146] of the great London dailies; till he was taken ill, and had to go

[025-147] abroad. He said I was breaking his heart by holding out against him so

[025-148] long at such close quarters; he could never have believed it of woman.

[025-149] I might play that game once too often, he said. He came home merely to

[025-150] die. His death caused a terrible remorse in me for my cruelty—though I

[025-151] hope he died of consumption and not of me entirely. I went down to

[025-152] Sandbourne to his funeral, and was his only mourner. He left me a

[025-153] little money—because I broke his heart, I suppose. That’s how men

[025-154] are—so much better than women!”

[025-155] “Good heavens!—what did you do then?”

[025-156] “Ah—now you are angry with me!” she said, a contralto note of tragedy

[025-157] coming suddenly into her silvery voice. “I wouldn’t have told you if I

[025-158] had known!”

[025-159] “No, I am not. Tell me all.”

[025-160] “Well, I invested his money, poor fellow, in a bubble scheme, and lost

[025-161] it. I lived about London by myself for some time, and then I returned

[025-162] to Christminster, as my father— who was also in London, and had started

[025-163] as an art metal-worker near Long-Acre—wouldn’t have me back; and I got

[025-164] that occupation in the artist-shop where you found me… I said you

[025-165] didn’t know how bad I was!”

[025-166] Jude looked round upon the arm-chair and its occupant, as if to read

[025-167] more carefully the creature he had given shelter to. His voice trembled

[025-168] as he said: “However you have lived, Sue, I believe you are as innocent

[025-169] as you are unconventional!”

[025-170] “I am not particularly innocent, as you see, now that I have

[025-171] ‘twitched the robe

[025-172] From that blank lay-figure your fancy draped,’”

[025-173] said she, with an ostensible sneer, though he could hear that she was

[025-174] brimming with tears. “But I have never yielded myself to any lover, if

[025-175] that’s what you mean! I have remained as I began.”

[025-176] “I quite believe you. But some women would not have remained as they

[025-177] began.”

[025-178] “Perhaps not. Better women would not. People say I must be

[025-179] cold-natured—sexless—on account of it. But I won’t have it! Some of the

[025-180] most passionately erotic poets have been the most self-contained in

[025-181] their daily lives.”

[025-182] “Have you told Mr. Phillotson about this university scholar friend?”

[025-183] “Yes—long ago. I have never made any secret of it to anybody.”

[025-184] “What did he say?”

[025-185] “He did not pass any criticism—only said I was everything to him,

[025-186] whatever I did; and things like that.”
