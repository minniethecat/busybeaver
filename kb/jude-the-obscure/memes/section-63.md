[063-001] “I want to tell you,” she presently said, her voice now quick, now

[063-002] slow, “so that you may not hear of it by chance. I am going back to

[063-003] Richard. He has—so magnanimously—agreed to forgive all.”

[063-004] “Going back? How can you go—”

[063-005] “He is going to marry me again. That is for form’s sake, and to satisfy

[063-006] the world, which does not see things as they are. But of course I _am_

[063-007] his wife already. Nothing has changed that.”

[063-008] He turned upon her with an anguish that was well-nigh fierce.

[063-009] “But you are _my_ wife! Yes, you are. You know it. I have always

[063-010] regretted that feint of ours in going away and pretending to come back

[063-011] legally married, to save appearances. I loved you, and you loved me;

[063-012] and we closed with each other; and that made the marriage. We still

[063-013] love—you as well as I—_know_ it, Sue! Therefore our marriage is not

[063-014] cancelled.”

[063-015] “Yes; I know how you see it,” she answered with despairing

[063-016] self-suppression. “But I am going to marry him again, as it would be

[063-017] called by you. Strictly speaking you, too—don’t mind my saying it,

[063-018] Jude!—you should take back—Arabella.”

[063-019] “I should? Good God—what next! But how if you and I had married

[063-020] legally, as we were on the point of doing?”

[063-021] “I should have felt just the same—that ours was not a marriage. And I

[063-022] would go back to Richard without repeating the sacrament, if he asked

[063-023] me. But ‘the world and its ways have a certain worth’ (I suppose),

[063-024] therefore I concede a repetition of the ceremony… Don’t crush all the

[063-025] life out of me by satire and argument, I implore you! I was strongest

[063-026] once, I know, and perhaps I treated you cruelly. But Jude, return good

[063-027] for evil! I am the weaker now. Don’t retaliate upon me, but be kind. Oh

[063-028] be kind to me—a poor wicked woman who is trying to mend!”

[063-029] He shook his head hopelessly, his eyes wet. The blow of her bereavement

[063-030] seemed to have destroyed her reasoning faculty. The once keen vision

[063-031] was dimmed. “All wrong, all wrong!” he said huskily. “Error—perversity!

[063-032] It drives me out of my senses. Do you care for him? Do you love him?

[063-033] You know you don’t! It will be a fanatic prostitution—God forgive me,

[063-034] yes—that’s what it will be!”

[063-035] “I don’t love him—I must, must, own it, in deepest remorse! But I shall

[063-036] try to learn to love him by obeying him.”

[063-037] Jude argued, urged, implored; but her conviction was proof against all.

[063-038] It seemed to be the one thing on earth on which she was firm, and that

[063-039] her firmness in this had left her tottering in every other impulse and

[063-040] wish she possessed.

[063-041] “I have been considerate enough to let you know the whole truth, and to

[063-042] tell it you myself,” she said in cut tones; “that you might not

[063-043] consider yourself slighted by hearing of it at second hand. I have even

[063-044] owned the extreme fact that I do not love him. I did not think you

[063-045] would be so rough with me for doing so! I was going to ask you…”

[063-046] “To give you away?”

[063-047] “No. To send—my boxes to me—if you would. But I suppose you won’t.”

[063-048] “Why, of course I will. What—isn’t he coming to fetch you—to marry you

[063-049] from here? He won’t condescend to do that?”

[063-050] “No—I won’t let him. I go to him voluntarily, just as I went away from

[063-051] him. We are to be married at his little church at Marygreen.”

[063-052] She was so sadly sweet in what he called her wrong-headedness that Jude

[063-053] could not help being moved to tears more than once for pity of her. “I

[063-054] never knew such a woman for doing impulsive penances, as you, Sue! No

[063-055] sooner does one expect you to go straight on, as the one rational

[063-056] proceeding, than you double round the corner!”

[063-057] “Ah, well; let that go! … Jude, I must say good-bye! But I wanted you

[063-058] to go to the cemetery with me. Let our farewell be there—beside the

[063-059] graves of those who died to bring home to me the error of my views.”

[063-060] They turned in the direction of the place, and the gate was opened to

[063-061] them on application. Sue had been there often, and she knew the way to

[063-062] the spot in the dark. They reached it, and stood still.

[063-063] “It is here—I should like to part,” said she.

[063-064] “So be it!”

[063-065] “Don’t think me hard because I have acted on conviction. Your generous

[063-066] devotion to me is unparalleled, Jude! Your worldly failure, if you have

[063-067] failed, is to your credit rather than to your blame. Remember that the

[063-068] best and greatest among mankind are those who do themselves no worldly

[063-069] good. Every successful man is more or less a selfish man. The devoted

[063-070] fail… ‘Charity seeketh not her own.’”

[063-071] “In that chapter we are at one, ever beloved darling, and on it we’ll

[063-072] part friends. Its verses will stand fast when all the rest that you

[063-073] call religion has passed away!”

[063-074] “Well—don’t discuss it. Good-bye, Jude; my fellow-sinner, and kindest

[063-075] friend!”

[063-076] “Good-bye, my mistaken wife. Good-bye!”

[063-077] V

[063-078] The next afternoon the familiar Christminster fog still hung over all

[063-079] things. Sue’s slim shape was only just discernible going towards the

[063-080] station.

[063-081] Jude had no heart to go to his work that day. Neither could he go

[063-082] anywhere in the direction by which she would be likely to pass. He went

[063-083] in an opposite one, to a dreary, strange, flat scene, where boughs

[063-084] dripped, and coughs and consumption lurked, and where he had never been

[063-085] before.

[063-086] “Sue’s gone from me—gone!” he murmured miserably.

[063-087] She in the meantime had left by the train, and reached Alfredston Road,

[063-088] where she entered the steam-tram and was conveyed into the town. It had

[063-089] been her request to Phillotson that he should not meet her. She wished,

[063-090] she said, to come to him voluntarily, to his very house and

[063-091] hearthstone.

[063-092] It was Friday evening, which had been chosen because the schoolmaster

[063-093] was disengaged at four o’clock that day till the Monday morning

[063-094] following. The little car she hired at the Bear to drive her to

[063-095] Marygreen set her down at the end of the lane, half a mile from the

[063-096] village, by her desire, and preceded her to the schoolhouse with such

[063-097] portion of her luggage as she had brought. On its return she

[063-098] encountered it, and asked the driver if he had found the master’s house

[063-099] open. The man informed her that he had, and that her things had been

[063-100] taken in by the schoolmaster himself.

[063-101] She could now enter Marygreen without exciting much observation. She

[063-102] crossed by the well and under the trees to the pretty new school on the

[063-103] other side, and lifted the latch of the dwelling without knocking.

[063-104] Phillotson stood in the middle of the room, awaiting her, as requested.

[063-105] “I’ve come, Richard,” said she, looking pale and shaken, and sinking

[063-106] into a chair. “I cannot believe—you forgive your—wife!”

[063-107] “Everything, darling Susanna,” said Phillotson.

[063-108] She started at the endearment, though it had been spoken advisedly

[063-109] without fervour. Then she nerved herself again.

[063-110] “My children—are dead—and it is right that they should be! I am

[063-111] glad—almost. They were sin-begotten. They were sacrificed to teach me

[063-112] how to live! Their death was the first stage of my purification. That’s

[063-113] why they have not died in vain! … You will take me back?”

[063-114] He was so stirred by her pitiful words and tone that he did more than

[063-115] he had meant to do. He bent and kissed her cheek.

[063-116] Sue imperceptibly shrank away, her flesh quivering under the touch of

[063-117] his lips.

[063-118] Phillotson’s heart sank, for desire was renascent in him. “You still

[063-119] have an aversion to me!”

[063-120] “Oh no, dear—I have been driving through the damp, and I was chilly!”

[063-121] she said, with a hurried smile of apprehension. “When are we going to

[063-122] have the marriage? Soon?”

[063-123] “To-morrow morning, early, I thought—if you really wish. I am sending

[063-124] round to the vicar to let him know you are come. I have told him all,

[063-125] and he highly approves—he says it will bring our lives to a triumphant

[063-126] and satisfactory issue. But—are you sure of yourself? It is not too

[063-127] late to refuse now if—you think you can’t bring yourself to it, you

[063-128] know?”

[063-129] “Yes, yes, I can! I want it done quick. Tell him, tell him at once! My

[063-130] strength is tried by the undertaking—I can’t wait long!”

[063-131] “Have something to eat and drink then, and go over to your room at Mrs.

[063-132] Edlin’s. I’ll tell the vicar half-past eight to-morrow, before anybody

[063-133] is about—if that’s not too soon for you? My friend Gillingham is here

[063-134] to help us in the ceremony. He’s been good enough to come all the way

[063-135] from Shaston at great inconvenience to himself.”

[063-136] Unlike a woman in ordinary, whose eye is so keen for material things,

[063-137] Sue seemed to see nothing of the room they were in, or any detail of

[063-138] her environment. But on moving across the parlour to put down her muff

[063-139] she uttered a little “Oh!” and grew paler than before. Her look was

[063-140] that of the condemned criminal who catches sight of his coffin.

[063-141] “What?” said Phillotson.

[063-142] The flap of the bureau chanced to be open, and in placing her muff upon

[063-143] it her eye had caught a document which lay there. “Oh—only a—funny

[063-144] surprise!” she said, trying to laugh away her cry as she came back to

[063-145] the table.

[063-146] “Ah! Yes,” said Phillotson. “The licence… It has just come.”

[063-147] Gillingham now joined them from his room above, and Sue nervously made

[063-148] herself agreeable to him by talking on whatever she thought likely to

[063-149] interest him, except herself, though that interested him most of all.

[063-150] She obediently ate some supper, and prepared to leave for her lodging

[063-151] hard by. Phillotson crossed the green with her, bidding her good-night

[063-152] at Mrs. Edlin’s door.

[063-153] The old woman accompanied Sue to her temporary quarters, and helped her

[063-154] to unpack. Among other things she laid out a night-gown tastefully

[063-155] embroidered.

[063-156] “Oh—I didn’t know _that_ was put in!” said Sue quickly. “I didn’t mean

[063-157] it to be. Here is a different one.” She handed a new and absolutely

[063-158] plain garment, of coarse and unbleached calico.

[063-159] “But this is the prettiest,” said Mrs. Edlin. “That one is no better

[063-160] than very sackcloth o’ Scripture!”

[063-161] “Yes—I meant it to be. Give me the other.”

[063-162] She took it, and began rending it with all her might, the tears

[063-163] resounding through the house like a screech-owl.

[063-164] “But my dear, dear!—whatever....”

[063-165] “It is adulterous! It signifies what I don’t feel—I bought it long

[063-166] ago—to please Jude. It must be destroyed!”

[063-167] Mrs. Edlin lifted her hands, and Sue excitedly continued to tear the

[063-168] linen into strips, laying the pieces in the fire.

[063-169] “You med ha’ give it to me!” said the widow. “It do make my heart ache

[063-170] to see such pretty open-work as that a-burned by the flames—not that

[063-171] ornamental night-rails can be much use to a’ ould ’ooman like I. My

[063-172] days for such be all past and gone!”

[063-173] “It is an accursed thing—it reminds me of what I want to forget!” Sue

[063-174] repeated. “It is only fit for the fire.”

[063-175] “Lord, you be too strict! What do ye use such words for, and condemn to

[063-176] hell your dear little innocent children that’s lost to ’ee! Upon my

[063-177] life I don’t call that religion!”

[063-178] Sue flung her face upon the bed, sobbing. “Oh, don’t, don’t! That kills

[063-179] me!” She remained shaken with her grief, and slipped down upon her

[063-180] knees.

[063-181] “I’ll tell ’ee what—you ought not to marry this man again!” said Mrs.

[063-182] Edlin indignantly. “You are in love wi’ t’ other still!”

[063-183] “Yes I must—I am his already!”

[063-184] “Pshoo! You be t’ other man’s. If you didn’t like to commit yourselves

[063-185] to the binding vow again, just at first, ’twas all the more credit to

[063-186] your consciences, considering your reasons, and you med ha’ lived on,

[063-187] and made it all right at last. After all, it concerned nobody but your

[063-188] own two selves.”
