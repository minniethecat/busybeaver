# Mr. Cartlett

Mr. Cartlett is a minor figure in *[[jude-fawley|Jude the Obscure]]* whose significance lies entirely in his relationship to [[Arabella-Donn]]. As Arabella's second husband — married after her [[divorce|separation]] from [[Jude-Fawley]] — Cartlett is a publican whose death frees [[arabella-donn|Arabella]] to pursue [[jude-fawley|Jude]] once more. He never appears directly in narrated scenes but is evoked through [[arabella-donn|Arabella]]'s references and the narrator's accounting of events.

## Character Overview

Cartlett is introduced obliquely: "was married over again to Cartlett last Tuesday," the narrator reports of [[arabella-donn|Arabella]], placing the event with matter-of-fact brevity (047-102). He is a publican — the keeper of a public house — and his social position represents a practical, unromantic match for [[arabella-donn|Arabella]], whose first marriage to [[jude-fawley|Jude]] had been driven by a combination of youthful [[love|passion]] and entrapment.

[[arabella-donn|Arabella]]'s calculation regarding Cartlett is transparent: after years of [[divorce|separation]] from [[jude-fawley|Jude]], she has found a man who can provide for her. Yet Cartlett's presence complicates her relations with [[jude-fawley|Jude]]. "Naturally Cartlett might think him in the way," [[arabella-donn|Arabella]] observes when [[jude-fawley|Jude]] reappears (047-122).

## Role in the Narrative

Cartlett's narrative function is structural: his marriage to [[arabella-donn|Arabella]] keeps her and [[jude-fawley|Jude]] apart for a time, creating an obstacle that heightens the novel's central romantic tension. [[arabella-donn|Arabella]]'s behaviour around him reveals her calculating nature — she wishes her husband not to see [[jude-fawley|Jude]] (048-052), and her conversations with Cartlett during their travels expose the gap between her worldly pragmatism and [[jude-fawley|Jude]]'s idealism.

### Cartlett's Death

Cartlett dies between Parts 5 and 6, an event reported retrospectively: "After Cartlett's death I..." [[arabella-donn|Arabella]] says, making clear that his passing has again altered her circumstances (054-180). His death is handled with the same offhand narrative economy as his introduction — a structural event rather than a dramatic one. [[arabella-donn|Arabella]] Cartlett is described in mourning: "[[arabella-donn|Arabella]] Cartlett and her weeds" (054-196), the black clothing signalling her widowed status and availability.

### Arabella's Second Widowhood

When [[arabella-donn|Arabella]] reappears as "[[arabella-donn|Mrs. Cartlett]]" to [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] (054-087), she is a widow. The narrator observes that "losing Cartlett" is a different experience from her loss of [[jude-fawley|Jude]] (062-079). Cartlett's death restores [[arabella-donn|Arabella]]'s freedom at a moment when [[jude-fawley|Jude]]'s relationship with [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] is faltering, and she begins manoeuvring to reclaim him.
