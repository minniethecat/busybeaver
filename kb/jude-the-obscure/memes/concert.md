# Concert

The concert in *Jude the Obscure* is a recurring emblem of cultural exclusion — the music that Jude can hear but not enter, the refinement that belongs to the university city but not to the working-class stonemason who repairs its walls. Concerts and organ music appear at key moments as auditory thresholds between Jude's world and the world of learning he aspires to join.

## Overview

Christminster (Hardy's Oxford) was a city of music. College chapels maintained choirs; the university hosted concerts and recitals; and public halls offered promenade concerts — informal musical events where the audience could walk about while listening. For the working-class residents of the city, the music was both omnipresent and inaccessible: they could hear it through open doors and windows, but they were not the audience for whom it was performed.

The organ is the novel's signature instrument. Jude first enters Christminster to the sound of "the pedal notes of an organ" (022-006) — the music of the city greeting him before he has even seen its walls. The organ, housed in college chapels and cathedrals, is an instrument of institutional religion and institutional learning alike. Jude the stonemason works on the very buildings that house the organs he will never be permitted to play.

## Key Details

### The Promenade Concert (Part Second)

Jude's first encounter with a Christminster concert occurs when he enters "a public hall, where a promenade concert was in progress" (020-148). The scene establishes the concert as a space of cultural participation from which Jude is subtly excluded — he can physically enter the hall but does not belong to the social world it represents.

### The Organ Rehearsal (Part Sixth)

Near the novel's end, the concert motif returns with greater symbolic weight. Jude, dying and separated from Sue, wanders the Christminster streets as an organ rehearsal is underway: "a rehearsal for a coming concert being in progress" (070-134). Carriages draw up outside, and Sue moves on as the "concert began" (070-143). The "powerful notes of that concert rolled forth through the swinging doors" (070-144) — music that once represented aspiration now sounds like a verdict. "No water came, and the organ notes, faint as a bee's hum, rolled in" (070-156), and then the Remembrance Day "hurrahs were repeated, drowning the faint organ notes" (070-162). The academic celebration's noise overwhelms the music, just as institutional exclusion overwhelms Jude's life.

Jude's face, at this moment, is the face of a man who has heard the music of Christminster all his life and will never be inside the room where it is played.

### The Organist and Jude's Musical Aspiration

Jude's relationship to music is more than passive listening. He befriends a cathedral organist, and "the ultimate result was that he had become acquainted with the organist" (033-190) — a connection that briefly seems to offer access to the musical world. Jude "once tried to get the cathedral organ when the post was vacant" (034-015), a failed ambition that mirrors his failed university hopes. The organ, like the college, is an institution that admits some and excludes others.

The "organist practising" in the college chapel (058-161) is a figure Jude observes from outside — the music floating through stone walls that he himself helped repair. The "pedal notes" (022-006) that greet his arrival become, by the novel's end, the "faint" (070-162) accompaniment to his death.

### The Soundscape of Exclusion

Hardy uses music throughout the novel to construct what might be called an acoustic architecture of exclusion. The "organ changing to a pathetic Gregorian tune as the singers gave" (015-170) voice creates a barrier of beauty between Jude and the liturgical world. Beyond "the peals of the organ, and the shouts and hurrahs" (057-042) of academic celebration, Jude remains an auditor, never a participant.

The concert functions as the secular counterpart to church music — a cultural ritual that is supposedly open to all but in practice reserved for those who belong to Christminster's academic and social elite.

## Narrative Significance

The concert scenes function as acoustic barriers. Jude hears the music but cannot cross the threshold into the world that produces it. The progression from the early promenade concert (where Jude is still hopeful) to the final organ rehearsal (where Jude is dying) traces the arc of his relationship with Christminster: from eager outsider to broken witness.
