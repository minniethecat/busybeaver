[035-001] It was to this breezy and whimsical spot that Jude ascended from the

[035-002] nearest station for the first time in his life about four o’clock one

[035-003] afternoon, and entering on the summit of the peak after a toilsome

[035-004] climb, passed the first houses of the aerial town; and drew towards the

[035-005] school-house. The hour was too early; the pupils were still in school,

[035-006] humming small, like a swarm of gnats; and he withdrew a few steps along

[035-007] Abbey Walk, whence he regarded the spot which fate had made the home of

[035-008] all he loved best in the world. In front of the schools, which were

[035-009] extensive and stone-built, grew two enormous beeches with smooth

[035-010] mouse-coloured trunks, as such trees will only grow on chalk uplands.

[035-011] Within the mullioned and transomed windows he could see the black,

[035-012] brown, and flaxen crowns of the scholars over the sills, and to pass

[035-013] the time away he walked down to the level terrace where the abbey

[035-014] gardens once had spread, his heart throbbing in spite of him.

[035-015] Unwilling to enter till the children were dismissed he remained here

[035-016] till young voices could be heard in the open air, and girls in white

[035-017] pinafores over red and blue frocks appeared dancing along the paths

[035-018] which the abbess, prioress, subprioress, and fifty nuns had demurely

[035-019] paced three centuries earlier. Retracing his steps he found that he had

[035-020] waited too long, and that Sue had gone out into the town at the heels

[035-021] of the last scholar, Mr. Phillotson having been absent all the

[035-022] afternoon at a teachers’ meeting at Shottsford.

[035-023] Jude went into the empty schoolroom and sat down, the girl who was

[035-024] sweeping the floor having informed him that Mrs. Phillotson would be

[035-025] back again in a few minutes. A piano stood near—actually the old piano

[035-026] that Phillotson had possessed at Marygreen—and though the dark

[035-027] afternoon almost prevented him seeing the notes Jude touched them in

[035-028] his humble way, and could not help modulating into the hymn which had

[035-029] so affected him in the previous week.

[035-030] A figure moved behind him, and thinking it was still the girl with the

[035-031] broom Jude took no notice, till the person came close and laid her

[035-032] fingers lightly upon his bass hand. The imposed hand was a little one

[035-033] he seemed to know, and he turned.

[035-034] “Don’t stop,” said Sue. “I like it. I learnt it before I left

[035-035] Melchester. They used to play it in the training school.”

[035-036] “I can’t strum before you! Play it for me.”

[035-037] “Oh well—I don’t mind.”

[035-038] Sue sat down, and her rendering of the piece, though not remarkable,

[035-039] seemed divine as compared with his own. She, like him, was evidently

[035-040] touched—to her own surprise—by the recalled air; and when she had

[035-041] finished, and he moved his hand towards hers, it met his own half-way.

[035-042] Jude grasped it—just as he had done before her marriage.

[035-043] “It is odd,” she said, in a voice quite changed, “that I should care

[035-044] about that air; because—”

[035-045] “Because what?”

[035-046] “I am not that sort—quite.”

[035-047] “Not easily moved?”

[035-048] “I didn’t quite mean that.”

[035-049] “Oh, but you _are_ one of that sort, for you are just like me at

[035-050] heart!”

[035-051] “But not at head.”

[035-052] She played on and suddenly turned round; and by an unpremeditated

[035-053] instinct each clasped the other’s hand again.

[035-054] She uttered a forced little laugh as she relinquished his quickly. “How

[035-055] funny!” she said. “I wonder what we both did that for?”

[035-056] “I suppose because we are both alike, as I said before.”

[035-057] “Not in our thoughts! Perhaps a little in our feelings.”

[035-058] “And they rule thoughts… Isn’t it enough to make one blaspheme that the

[035-059] composer of that hymn is one of the most commonplace men I ever met!”

[035-060] “What—you know him?”

[035-061] “I went to see him.”

[035-062] “Oh, you goose—to do just what I should have done! Why did you?”

[035-063] “Because we are not alike,” he said drily.

[035-064] “Now we’ll have some tea,” said Sue. “Shall we have it here instead of

[035-065] in my house? It is no trouble to get the kettle and things brought in.

[035-066] We don’t live at the school you know, but in that ancient dwelling

[035-067] across the way called Old-Grove Place. It is so antique and dismal that

[035-068] it depresses me dreadfully. Such houses are very well to visit, but not

[035-069] to live in—I feel crushed into the earth by the weight of so many

[035-070] previous lives there spent. In a new place like these schools there is

[035-071] only your own life to support. Sit down, and I’ll tell Ada to bring the

[035-072] tea-things across.”

[035-073] He waited in the light of the stove, the door of which she flung open

[035-074] before going out, and when she returned, followed by the maiden with

[035-075] tea, they sat down by the same light, assisted by the blue rays of a

[035-076] spirit-lamp under the brass kettle on the stand.

[035-077] “This is one of your wedding-presents to me,” she said, signifying the

[035-078] latter.

[035-079] “Yes,” said Jude.

[035-080] The kettle of his gift sang with some satire in its note, to his mind;

[035-081] and to change the subject he said, “Do you know of any good readable

[035-082] edition of the uncanonical books of the New Testament? You don’t read

[035-083] them in the school I suppose?”

[035-084] “Oh dear no!—’twould alarm the neighbourhood… Yes, there is one. I am

[035-085] not familiar with it now, though I was interested in it when my former

[035-086] friend was alive. Cowper’s _Apocryphal Gospels_.”

[035-087] “That sounds like what I want.” His thoughts, however reverted with a

[035-088] twinge to the “former friend”—by whom she meant, as he knew, the

[035-089] university comrade of her earlier days. He wondered if she talked of

[035-090] him to Phillotson.

[035-091] “The Gospel of Nicodemus is very nice,” she went on to keep him from

[035-092] his jealous thoughts, which she read clearly, as she always did. Indeed

[035-093] when they talked on an indifferent subject, as now, there was ever a

[035-094] second silent conversation passing between their emotions, so perfect

[035-095] was the reciprocity between them. “It is quite like the genuine

[035-096] article. All cut up into verses, too; so that it is like one of the

[035-097] other evangelists read in a dream, when things are the same, yet not

[035-098] the same. But, Jude, do you take an interest in those questions still?

[035-099] Are you getting up _Apologetica_?”

[035-100] “Yes. I am reading Divinity harder than ever.”

[035-101] She regarded him curiously.

[035-102] “Why do you look at me like that?” said Jude.

[035-103] “Oh—why do you want to know?”

[035-104] “I am sure you can tell me anything I may be ignorant of in that

[035-105] subject. You must have learnt a lot of everything from your dear dead

[035-106] friend!”

[035-107] “We won’t get on to that now!” she coaxed. “Will you be carving out at

[035-108] that church again next week, where you learnt the pretty hymn?”

[035-109] “Yes, perhaps.”

[035-110] “That will be very nice. Shall I come and see you there? It is in this

[035-111] direction, and I could come any afternoon by train for half an hour?”

[035-112] “No. Don’t come!”

[035-113] “What—aren’t we going to be friends, then, any longer, as we used to

[035-114] be?”

[035-115] “No.”

[035-116] “I didn’t know that. I thought you were always going to be kind to me!”

[035-117] “No, I am not.”

[035-118] “What have I done, then? I am sure I thought we two—” The _tremolo_ in

[035-119] her voice caused her to break off.

[035-120] “Sue, I sometimes think you are a flirt,” said he abruptly.

[035-121] There was a momentary pause, till she suddenly jumped up; and to his

[035-122] surprise he saw by the kettle-flame that her face was flushed.

[035-123] “I can’t talk to you any longer, Jude!” she said, the tragic contralto

[035-124] note having come back as of old. “It is getting too dark to stay

[035-125] together like this, after playing morbid Good Friday tunes that make

[035-126] one feel what one shouldn’t! … We mustn’t sit and talk in this way any

[035-127] more. Yes—you must go away, for you mistake me! I am very much the

[035-128] reverse of what you say so cruelly—Oh, Jude, it _was_ cruel to say

[035-129] that! Yet I can’t tell you the truth—I should shock you by letting you

[035-130] know how I give way to my impulses, and how much I feel that I

[035-131] shouldn’t have been provided with attractiveness unless it were meant

[035-132] to be exercised! Some women’s love of being loved is insatiable; and

[035-133] so, often, is their love of loving; and in the last case they may find

[035-134] that they can’t give it continuously to the chamber-officer appointed

[035-135] by the bishop’s licence to receive it. But you are so straightforward,

[035-136] Jude, that you can’t understand me! … Now you must go. I am sorry my

[035-137] husband is not at home.”

[035-138] “Are you?”

[035-139] “I perceive I have said that in mere convention! Honestly I don’t think

[035-140] I am sorry. It does not matter, either way, sad to say!”

[035-141] As they had overdone the grasp of hands some time sooner, she touched

[035-142] his fingers but lightly when he went out now. He had hardly gone from

[035-143] the door when, with a dissatisfied look, she jumped on a form and

[035-144] opened the iron casement of a window beneath which he was passing in

[035-145] the path without. “When do you leave here to catch your train, Jude?”

[035-146] she asked.

[035-147] He looked up in some surprise. “The coach that runs to meet it goes in

[035-148] three-quarters of an hour or so.”

[035-149] “What will you do with yourself for the time?”

[035-150] “Oh—wander about, I suppose. Perhaps I shall go and sit in the old

[035-151] church.”

[035-152] “It does seem hard of me to pack you off so! You have thought enough of

[035-153] churches, Heaven knows, without going into one in the dark. Stay

[035-154] there.”

[035-155] “Where?”

[035-156] “Where you are. I can talk to you better like this than when you were

[035-157] inside… It was so kind and tender of you to give up half a day’s work

[035-158] to come to see me! … You are Joseph the dreamer of dreams, dear Jude.

[035-159] And a tragic Don Quixote. And sometimes you are St. Stephen, who, while

[035-160] they were stoning him, could see Heaven opened. Oh, my poor friend and

[035-161] comrade, you’ll suffer yet!”

[035-162] Now that the high window-sill was between them, so that he could not

[035-163] get at her, she seemed not to mind indulging in a frankness she had

[035-164] feared at close quarters.

[035-165] “I have been thinking,” she continued, still in the tone of one brimful

[035-166] of feeling, “that the social moulds civilization fits us into have no

[035-167] more relation to our actual shapes than the conventional shapes of the

[035-168] constellations have to the real star-patterns. I am called Mrs. Richard

[035-169] Phillotson, living a calm wedded life with my counterpart of that name.

[035-170] But I am not really Mrs. Richard Phillotson, but a woman tossed about,

[035-171] all alone, with aberrant passions, and unaccountable antipathies… Now

[035-172] you mustn’t wait longer, or you will lose the coach. Come and see me

[035-173] again. You must come to the house then.”

[035-174] “Yes!” said Jude. “When shall it be?”

[035-175] “To-morrow week. Good-bye—good-bye!” She stretched out her hand and

[035-176] stroked his forehead pitifully—just once. Jude said good-bye, and went

[035-177] away into the darkness.

[035-178] Passing along Bimport Street he thought he heard the wheels of the

[035-179] coach departing, and, truly enough, when he reached the Duke’s Arms in

[035-180] the Market Place the coach had gone. It was impossible for him to get

[035-181] to the station on foot in time for this train, and he settled himself

[035-182] perforce to wait for the next—the last to Melchester that night.

[035-183] He wandered about awhile, obtained something to eat; and then, having

[035-184] another half-hour on his hands, his feet involuntarily took him through

[035-185] the venerable graveyard of Trinity Church, with its avenues of limes,

[035-186] in the direction of the schools again. They were entirely in darkness.

[035-187] She had said she lived over the way at Old-Grove Place, a house which

[035-188] he soon discovered from her description of its antiquity.

[035-189] A glimmering candlelight shone from a front window, the shutters being

[035-190] yet unclosed. He could see the interior clearly—the floor sinking a

[035-191] couple of steps below the road without, which had become raised during

[035-192] the centuries since the house was built. Sue, evidently just come in,

[035-193] was standing with her hat on in this front parlour or sitting-room,

[035-194] whose walls were lined with wainscoting of panelled oak reaching from

[035-195] floor to ceiling, the latter being crossed by huge moulded beams only a

[035-196] little way above her head. The mantelpiece was of the same heavy

[035-197] description, carved with Jacobean pilasters and scroll-work. The

[035-198] centuries did, indeed, ponderously overhang a young wife who passed her

[035-199] time here.
