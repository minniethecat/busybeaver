[053-001] The visitors gave one more glance, as if to see whether Jude and Sue

[053-002] had left the “nots” out likewise, and then severally left the church,

[053-003] even the old woman at last. Sue and Jude, who had not stopped working,

[053-004] sent back the child to school, and remained without speaking; till,

[053-005] looking at her narrowly, he found she had been crying silently.

[053-006] “Never mind, comrade!” he said. “I know what it is!”

[053-007] “I can’t _bear_ that they, and everybody, should think people wicked

[053-008] because they may have chosen to live their own way! It is really these

[053-009] opinions that make the best intentioned people reckless, and actually

[053-010] become immoral!”

[053-011] “Never be cast down! It was only a funny story.”

[053-012] “Ah, but we suggested it! I am afraid I have done you mischief, Jude,

[053-013] instead of helping you by coming!”

[053-014] To have suggested such a story was certainly not very exhilarating, in

[053-015] a serious view of their position. However, in a few minutes Sue seemed

[053-016] to see that their position this morning had a ludicrous side, and

[053-017] wiping her eyes she laughed.

[053-018] “It is droll, after all,” she said, “that we two, of all people, with

[053-019] our queer history, should happen to be here painting the Ten

[053-020] Commandments! You a reprobate, and I—in my condition… O dear!” … And

[053-021] with her hand over her eyes she laughed again silently and

[053-022] intermittently, till she was quite weak.

[053-023] “That’s better,” said Jude gaily. “Now we are right again, aren’t we,

[053-024] little girl!”

[053-025] “Oh but it is serious, all the same!” she sighed as she took up the

[053-026] brush and righted herself. “But do you see they don’t think we are

[053-027] married? They _won’t_ believe it! It is extraordinary!”

[053-028] “I don’t care whether they think so or not,” said Jude. “I shan’t take

[053-029] any more trouble to make them.”

[053-030] They sat down to lunch—which they had brought with them not to hinder

[053-031] time—and having eaten it, were about to set to work anew when a man

[053-032] entered the church, and Jude recognized in him the contractor Willis.

[053-033] He beckoned to Jude, and spoke to him apart.

[053-034] “Here—I’ve just had a complaint about this,” he said, with rather

[053-035] breathless awkwardness. “I don’t wish to go into the matter—as of

[053-036] course I didn’t know what was going on—but I am afraid I must ask you

[053-037] and her to leave off, and let somebody else finish this! It is best, to

[053-038] avoid all unpleasantness. I’ll pay you for the week, all the same.”

[053-039] Jude was too independent to make any fuss; and the contractor paid him,

[053-040] and left. Jude picked up his tools, and Sue cleansed her brush. Then

[053-041] their eyes met.

[053-042] “How could we be so simple as to suppose we might do this!” said she,

[053-043] dropping to her tragic note. “Of course we ought not—I ought not—to

[053-044] have come!”

[053-045] “I had no idea that anybody was going to intrude into such a lonely

[053-046] place and see us!” Jude returned. “Well, it can’t be helped, dear; and

[053-047] of course I wouldn’t wish to injure Willis’s trade-connection by

[053-048] staying.” They sat down passively for a few minutes, proceeded out of

[053-049] the church, and overtaking the boy pursued their thoughtful way to

[053-050] Aldbrickham.

[053-051] Fawley had still a pretty zeal in the cause of education, and, as was

[053-052] natural with his experiences, he was active in furthering “equality of

[053-053] opportunity” by any humble means open to him. He had joined an

[053-054] Artizans’ Mutual Improvement Society established in the town about the

[053-055] time of his arrival there; its members being young men of all creeds

[053-056] and denominations, including Churchmen, Congregationalists, Baptists,

[053-057] Unitarians, Positivists, and others—Agnostics had scarcely been heard

[053-058] of at this time—their one common wish to enlarge their minds forming a

[053-059] sufficiently close bond of union. The subscription was small, and the

[053-060] room homely; and Jude’s activity, uncustomary acquirements, and, above

[053-061] all, singular intuition on what to read and how to set about

[053-062] it—begotten of his years of struggle against malignant stars—had led to

[053-063] his being placed on the committee.

[053-064] A few evenings after his dismissal from the church repairs, and before

[053-065] he had obtained any more work to do, he went to attend a meeting of the

[053-066] aforesaid committee. It was late when he arrived: all the others had

[053-067] come, and as he entered they looked dubiously at him, and hardly

[053-068] uttered a word of greeting. He guessed that something bearing on

[053-069] himself had been either discussed or mooted. Some ordinary business was

[053-070] transacted, and it was disclosed that the number of subscriptions had

[053-071] shown a sudden falling off for that quarter. One member—a really

[053-072] well-meaning and upright man—began speaking in enigmas about certain

[053-073] possible causes: that it behoved them to look well into their

[053-074] constitution; for if the committee were not respected, and had not at

[053-075] least, in their differences, a common standard of _conduct_, they would

[053-076] bring the institution to the ground. Nothing further was said in Jude’s

[053-077] presence, but he knew what this meant; and turning to the table wrote a

[053-078] note resigning his office there and then.

[053-079] Thus the supersensitive couple were more and more impelled to go away.

[053-080] And then bills were sent in, and the question arose, what could Jude do

[053-081] with his great-aunt’s heavy old furniture, if he left the town to

[053-082] travel he knew not whither? This, and the necessity of ready money,

[053-083] compelled him to decide on an auction, much as he would have preferred

[053-084] to keep the venerable goods.

[053-085] The day of the sale came on; and Sue for the last time cooked her own,

[053-086] the child’s, and Jude’s breakfast in the little house he had furnished.

[053-087] It chanced to be a wet day; moreover Sue was unwell, and not wishing to

[053-088] desert her poor Jude in such gloomy circumstances, for he was compelled

[053-089] to stay awhile, she acted on the suggestion of the auctioneer’s man,

[053-090] and ensconced herself in an upper room, which could be emptied of its

[053-091] effects, and so kept closed to the bidders. Here Jude discovered her;

[053-092] and with the child, and their few trunks, baskets, and bundles, and two

[053-093] chairs and a table that were not in the sale, the two sat in meditative

[053-094] talk.

[053-095] Footsteps began stamping up and down the bare stairs, the comers

[053-096] inspecting the goods, some of which were of so quaint and ancient a

[053-097] make as to acquire an adventitious value as art. Their door was tried

[053-098] once or twice, and to guard themselves against intrusion Jude wrote

[053-099] “Private” on a scrap of paper, and stuck it upon the panel.

[053-100] They soon found that, instead of the furniture, their own personal

[053-101] histories and past conduct began to be discussed to an unexpected and

[053-102] intolerable extent by the intending bidders. It was not till now that

[053-103] they really discovered what a fools’ paradise of supposed unrecognition

[053-104] they had been living in of late. Sue silently took her companion’s

[053-105] hand, and with eyes on each other they heard these passing remarks—the

[053-106] quaint and mysterious personality of Father Time being a subject which

[053-107] formed a large ingredient in the hints and innuendoes. At length the

[053-108] auction began in the room below, whence they could hear each familiar

[053-109] article knocked down, the highly prized ones cheaply, the unconsidered

[053-110] at an unexpected price.

[053-111] “People don’t understand us,” he sighed heavily. “I am glad we have

[053-112] decided to go.”

[053-113] “The question is, where to?”

[053-114] “It ought to be to London. There one can live as one chooses.”

[053-115] “No—not London, dear! I know it well. We should be unhappy there.”

[053-116] “Why?”

[053-117] “Can’t you think?”

[053-118] “Because Arabella is there?”

[053-119] “That’s the chief reason.”

[053-120] “But in the country I shall always be uneasy lest there should be some

[053-121] more of our late experience. And I don’t care to lessen it by

[053-122] explaining, for one thing, all about the boy’s history. To cut him off

[053-123] from his past I have determined to keep silence. I am sickened of

[053-124] ecclesiastical work now; and I shouldn’t like to accept it, if offered

[053-125] me!”

[053-126] “You ought to have learnt classic. Gothic is barbaric art, after all.

[053-127] Pugin was wrong, and Wren was right. Remember the interior of

[053-128] Christminster Cathedral—almost the first place in which we looked in

[053-129] each other’s faces. Under the picturesqueness of those Norman details

[053-130] one can see the grotesque childishness of uncouth people trying to

[053-131] imitate the vanished Roman forms, remembered by dim tradition only.”

[053-132] “Yes—you have half-converted me to that view by what you have said

[053-133] before. But one can work, and despise what one does. I must do

[053-134] something, if not church-gothic.”

[053-135] “I wish we could both follow an occupation in which personal

[053-136] circumstances don’t count,” she said, smiling up wistfully. “I am as

[053-137] disqualified for teaching as you are for ecclesiastical art. You must

[053-138] fall back upon railway stations, bridges, theatres, music-halls,

[053-139] hotels—everything that has no connection with conduct.”

[053-140] “I am not skilled in those… I ought to take to bread-baking. I grew up

[053-141] in the baking business with aunt, you know. But even a baker must be

[053-142] conventional, to get customers.”

[053-143] “Unless he keeps a cake and gingerbread stall at markets and fairs,

[053-144] where people are gloriously indifferent to everything except the

[053-145] quality of the goods.”

[053-146] Their thoughts were diverted by the voice of the auctioneer: “Now this

[053-147] antique oak settle—a unique example of old English furniture, worthy

[053-148] the attention of all collectors!”

[053-149] “That was my great-grandfather’s,” said Jude. “I wish we could have

[053-150] kept the poor old thing!”

[053-151] One by one the articles went, and the afternoon passed away. Jude and

[053-152] the other two were getting tired and hungry, but after the conversation

[053-153] they had heard they were shy of going out while the purchasers were in

[053-154] their line of retreat. However, the later lots drew on, and it became

[053-155] necessary to emerge into the rain soon, to take on Sue’s things to

[053-156] their temporary lodging.

[053-157] “Now the next lot: two pairs of pigeons, all alive and plump—a nice pie

[053-158] for somebody for next Sunday’s dinner!”

[053-159] The impending sale of these birds had been the most trying suspense of

[053-160] the whole afternoon. They were Sue’s pets, and when it was found that

[053-161] they could not possibly be kept, more sadness was caused than by

[053-162] parting from all the furniture. Sue tried to think away her tears as

[053-163] she heard the trifling sum that her dears were deemed to be worth

[053-164] advanced by small stages to the price at which they were finally

[053-165] knocked down. The purchaser was a neighbouring poulterer, and they were

[053-166] unquestionably doomed to die before the next market day.

[053-167] Noting her dissembled distress Jude kissed her, and said it was time to

[053-168] go and see if the lodgings were ready. He would go on with the boy, and

[053-169] fetch her soon.

[053-170] When she was left alone she waited patiently, but Jude did not come

[053-171] back. At last she started, the coast being clear, and on passing the

[053-172] poulterer’s shop, not far off, she saw her pigeons in a hamper by the

[053-173] door. An emotion at sight of them, assisted by the growing dusk of

[053-174] evening, caused her to act on impulse, and first looking around her

[053-175] quickly, she pulled out the peg which fastened down the cover, and went

[053-176] on. The cover was lifted from within, and the pigeons flew away with a

[053-177] clatter that brought the chagrined poulterer cursing and swearing to

[053-178] the door.

[053-179] Sue reached the lodging trembling, and found Jude and the boy making it

[053-180] comfortable for her. “Do the buyers pay before they bring away the

[053-181] things?” she asked breathlessly.

[053-182] “Yes, I think. Why?”

[053-183] “Because, then, I’ve done such a wicked thing!” And she explained, in

[053-184] bitter contrition.

[053-185] “I shall have to pay the poulterer for them, if he doesn’t catch them,”

[053-186] said Jude. “But never mind. Don’t fret about it, dear.”

[053-187] “It was so foolish of me! Oh why should Nature’s law be mutual

[053-188] butchery!”

[053-189] “Is it so, Mother?” asked the boy intently.

[053-190] “Yes!” said Sue vehemently.

[053-191] “Well, they must take their chance, now, poor things,” said Jude. “As

[053-192] soon as the sale-account is wound up, and our bills paid, we go.”
