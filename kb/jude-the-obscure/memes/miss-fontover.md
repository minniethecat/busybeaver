# Miss Fontover

Miss Fontover is a minor character in *[[jude-fawley|Jude the Obscure]]* who employs [[Sue-Bridehead]] in her religious ornament shop in [[christminster|Christminster]]. An elderly, pious woman of conventional church sympathies, Miss Fontover represents the respectable, orthodox Christianity that dominates [[christminster|Christminster]] — and against which [[sue-bridehead|Sue]]'s freethinking spirit quietly rebels. Though she appears only briefly, she is one of the novel's most precisely drawn embodiments of institutional religion's suffocating effects on intellectual and spiritual independence, particularly for women.

## Character Overview

Miss Fontover is introduced as "the mistress of the house" and proprietor of a shop dealing in religious articles (016-089). Her establishment is characterised by its ecclesiastical atmosphere — "Gothic-framed prints of saints" and devotional objects that signal her devout and conventional character (016-106). She is described as "elderly" and clearly belongs to the established church community that forms [[christminster|Christminster]]'s institutional backbone.

Her piety is conventional, commercial, and unquestioning. She takes [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] to church "perforce" (016-175), expecting her young employee to conform to the same outward observances. But beneath this respectable surface, Miss Fontover is also a figure of surveillance: she keeps a watchful eye on [[sue-bridehead|Sue]]'s private reading, and [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] must conceal the pagan authors she has brought with her, knowing that Miss Fontover remained unaware of the subversive book hidden in her box (016-125). The shop that sells the material trappings of faith is also a place where genuine intellectual inquiry — particularly inquiry into non-Christian thought — must be conducted in secret.

## Role in the Narrative

Miss Fontover functions primarily as a foil to [[sue-bridehead|Sue Bridehead]]. Where [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] is intellectually curious, spiritually heterodox, and resistant to institutional authority, Miss Fontover embodies the settled, unexamined piety of conventional [[christminster|Christminster]]. The contrast between employer and employee dramatises the constraints that the city — for all its reputation as a seat of [[education|learning]] — imposes on independent-minded women. Miss Fontover's shop is a microcosm of [[christminster|Christminster]] itself: a place where the forms of faith and learning are preserved in Gothic stone and framed prints, but where genuine intellectual and spiritual inquiry is quietly suppressed.

### First Appearance

Miss Fontover first appears when [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] arrives at her new lodgings and employment in [[christminster|Christminster]]. The household is described through its religious ornamentation — the "Gothic-framed prints of saints" and ecclesiastical atmosphere that signal Miss Fontover's devout and conventional character (016-106). The narrator introduces her as "the mistress of the house, Miss Fontover," an "elderly lady" whose authority over the young [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] is immediately established (016-089).

### Key Moments

1. **Sue's Concealed Book** — [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] has brought with her a volume of pagan literature that she must keep hidden from her employer. The narrator notes that she removed a "book she had taken from her box, which Miss Fontover knew nothing of" (016-125). This small act of concealment is loaded with significance: it establishes the secret inner life that [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] must maintain behind the facade of compliance, and it identifies Miss Fontover — however benign her intentions — as an obstacle to intellectual freedom. The book she must hide is not merely private reading; it is a token of the classical, pre-Christian world that [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] instinctively prefers to the Gothic piety of her surroundings.

2. **The Enforced Church Attendance** — Miss Fontover compels [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] to accompany her to church — "who had perforce accompanied the elderly Miss Fontover thither" (016-175). This ritual attendance, performed without evident spiritual feeling, represents exactly the kind of institutional religion that [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] instinctively distrusts: religion as social obligation rather than genuine conviction. The word "perforce" is crucial — it signals compulsion, the absence of choice, the reduction of worship to a disciplinary practice.

3. **Sue's Departure and Judgment** — When [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] decides to leave the shop, she explains to [[jude-fawley|Jude]]:

> "Well — I must go," she continued. "Miss Fontover, one of the partners in the establishment, is a very churchy woman, and I don't like her." (018-007)

The judgment is characteristically candid — [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] makes no effort to soften her dislike or to find diplomatic language. The word "churchy" is a [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] coinage, condensing her critique of institutional religion into a single dismissive adjective. Miss Fontover is not wicked or cruel; she is "churchy" — defined entirely by her institutional affiliation, incapable of imagining a spirituality outside its bounds.

4. **Later Mockery** — The novel preserves a memory of [[sue-bridehead|Sue]]'s time with Miss Fontover long after she has left the shop. In a later scene, the narrator recalls how [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] "mimicked Miss Fontover when she" adopted pious airs (061-093). The mimicry is significant: [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] has internalised her former employer sufficiently to perform her, to transform her sanctimony into comedy. The mockery is an act of retrospective liberation — having escaped Miss Fontover's surveillance, [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] can now defuse her power through imitation. The passage also links Miss Fontover explicitly to [[christminster|Christminster]] — the narrator asks rhetorically how [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] "came into this most Christian city" (061-093), framing her presence as an intrusion of pagan vitality into a space defined by Miss Fontover's kind of Christianity.

## Character Analysis

### The Commercialisation of Faith

Miss Fontover's shop is one of Hardy's most economical satirical devices. She trades in the *things* of religion — Gothic-framed prints, ecclesiastical furnishings, devotional objects — while her own religiosity seems to consist of little more than church attendance and the surveillance of others' reading. The implication is clear: in [[christminster|Christminster]], faith has been reduced to a commodity, something that can be bought and sold and displayed on a wall. The "Gothic-framed prints of saints" (016-106) are simulacra of sanctity, mass-produced images that substitute for genuine spiritual experience. Miss Fontover is not a hypocrite in the ordinary sense — she genuinely believes in the religion she purveys — but her faith is entirely external, a matter of objects and observances rather than inward conviction.

### The Gender of Surveillance

Miss Fontover's surveillance of [[sue-bridehead|Sue]]'s reading is not merely a character detail but a pointed commentary on the social regulation of women's intellectual lives in Victorian England. The university city of [[christminster|Christminster]] is ostensibly a place of learning, but that learning is reserved for men; women who seek knowledge must do so in secret, under the watchful eye of guardians like Miss Fontover. The paradox is sharp: Miss Fontover is herself a woman, and yet she functions as an enforcer of the patriarchal order, policing the boundaries of acceptable female behaviour. Her surveillance of [[sue-bridehead|Sue]]'s reading — her ignorance of the pagan book in [[sue-bridehead|Sue]]'s box — is a small but telling instance of how women could be enlisted in the project of their own intellectual containment.

### A Miniature of Christminster

Miss Fontover is a structural synecdoche for [[christminster|Christminster]] itself. Her shop, with its "Gothic-framed prints" and ecclesiastical atmosphere, reproduces in miniature the city's defining characteristics: antiquity, orthodoxy, the commodification of tradition, and the suppression of independent thought. [[sue-bridehead|Sue]]'s flight from Miss Fontover's establishment prefigures her larger flight from the constraints of institutional religion and conventional morality. The question the narrator poses — how did [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] "come into this most Christian city?" (061-093) — applies equally to the shop and to [[christminster|Christminster]] as a whole. In both cases, the answer is the same: she came seeking something she could not find there, and she left because the cost of staying — the concealment, the "perforce" church attendance, the suppression of her intellectual life — was too high.

### Sue's First Rebellion

Miss Fontover's significance in the novel's larger architecture is that she represents [[sue-bridehead|Sue]]'s first act of rebellion against institutional authority. Before [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] rebels against [[Richard-Phillotson]], before she rebels against [[marriage]] itself, before she defies the college heads of [[christminster|Christminster]], she rebels against Miss Fontover — by concealing her book, by mocking her piety, and finally by leaving her employ with the devastating judgment: "a very churchy woman, and I don't like her" (018-007). The rebellion is small in scale but large in significance: it establishes the pattern of resistance that will define [[sue-bridehead|Sue]]'s entire character arc. Miss Fontover is the first authority figure against whom [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] defines her independence, and the terms of that definition — intellectual freedom, spiritual heterodoxy, resistance to institutional compulsion — will remain constant throughout the novel.

### The Limits of Satire

For all her satirical function, Miss Fontover is not a villain. She is an "elderly lady" (016-089) whose piety, however conventional, is sincere. She takes [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] to church "perforce" not out of malice but because she genuinely believes it is good for her — and in the novel's final movement, when [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] undergoes her catastrophic religious conversion and returns to [[Richard-Phillotson]], the novel suggests that Miss Fontover's kind of enforced piety was, in some terrible sense, prophetic. The religion that [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] mocked and fled will ultimately claim her. Miss Fontover is thus a figure of both satire and tragic irony: the churchy woman [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] despised becomes the figure of the fate she cannot escape.
