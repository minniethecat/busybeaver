[059-001] Jude dreaded her dull apathetic silences almost more than her

[059-002] paroxysms. “Why don’t you speak to me, Jude?” she cried out, after one

[059-003] of these. “Don’t turn away from me! I can’t _bear_ the loneliness of

[059-004] being out of your looks!”

[059-005] “There, dear; here I am,” he said, putting his face close to hers.

[059-006] “Yes… Oh, my comrade, our perfect union—our two-in-oneness—is now

[059-007] stained with blood!”

[059-008] “Shadowed by death—that’s all.”

[059-009] “Ah; but it was I who incited him really, though I didn’t know I was

[059-010] doing it! I talked to the child as one should only talk to people of

[059-011] mature age. I said the world was against us, that it was better to be

[059-012] out of life than in it at this price; and he took it literally. And I

[059-013] told him I was going to have another child. It upset him. Oh how

[059-014] bitterly he upbraided me!”

[059-015] “Why did you do it, Sue?”

[059-016] “I can’t tell. It was that I wanted to be truthful. I couldn’t bear

[059-017] deceiving him as to the facts of life. And yet I wasn’t truthful, for

[059-018] with a false delicacy I told him too obscurely.—Why was I half-wiser

[059-019] than my fellow-women? And not entirely wiser! Why didn’t I tell him

[059-020] pleasant untruths, instead of half-realities? It was my want of

[059-021] self-control, so that I could neither conceal things nor reveal them!”

[059-022] “Your plan might have been a good one for the majority of cases; only

[059-023] in our peculiar case it chanced to work badly perhaps. He must have

[059-024] known sooner or later.”

[059-025] “And I was just making my baby darling a new frock; and now I shall

[059-026] never see him in it, and never talk to him any more! … My eyes are so

[059-027] swollen that I can scarcely see; and yet little more than a year ago I

[059-028] called myself happy! We went about loving each other too much—indulging

[059-029] ourselves to utter selfishness with each other! We said—do you

[059-030] remember?—that we would make a virtue of joy. I said it was Nature’s

[059-031] intention, Nature’s law and _raison d’être_ that we should be joyful in

[059-032] what instincts she afforded us—instincts which civilization had taken

[059-033] upon itself to thwart. What dreadful things I said! And now Fate has

[059-034] given us this stab in the back for being such fools as to take Nature

[059-035] at her word!”

[059-036] She sank into a quiet contemplation, till she said, “It is best,

[059-037] perhaps, that they should be gone.—Yes—I see it is! Better that they

[059-038] should be plucked fresh than stay to wither away miserably!”

[059-039] “Yes,” replied Jude. “Some say that the elders should rejoice when

[059-040] their children die in infancy.”

[059-041] “But they don’t know! … Oh my babies, my babies, could you be alive

[059-042] now! You may say the boy wished to be out of life, or he wouldn’t have

[059-043] done it. It was not unreasonable for him to die: it was part of his

[059-044] incurably sad nature, poor little fellow! But then the others—my _own_

[059-045] children and yours!”

[059-046] Again Sue looked at the hanging little frock and at the socks and

[059-047] shoes; and her figure quivered like a string. “I am a pitiable

[059-048] creature,” she said, “good neither for earth nor heaven any more! I am

[059-049] driven out of my mind by things! What ought to be done?” She stared at

[059-050] Jude, and tightly held his hand.

[059-051] “Nothing can be done,” he replied. “Things are as they are, and will be

[059-052] brought to their destined issue.”

[059-053] She paused. “Yes! Who said that?” she asked heavily.

[059-054] “It comes in the chorus of the _Agamemnon_. It has been in my mind

[059-055] continually since this happened.”

[059-056] “My poor Jude—how you’ve missed everything!—you more than I, for I did

[059-057] get you! To think you should know that by your unassisted reading, and

[059-058] yet be in poverty and despair!”

[059-059] After such momentary diversions her grief would return in a wave.

[059-060] The jury duly came and viewed the bodies, the inquest was held; and

[059-061] next arrived the melancholy morning of the funeral. Accounts in the

[059-062] newspapers had brought to the spot curious idlers, who stood apparently

[059-063] counting the window-panes and the stones of the walls. Doubt of the

[059-064] real relations of the couple added zest to their curiosity. Sue had

[059-065] declared that she would follow the two little ones to the grave, but at

[059-066] the last moment she gave way, and the coffins were quietly carried out

[059-067] of the house while she was lying down. Jude got into the vehicle, and

[059-068] it drove away, much to the relief of the landlord, who now had only Sue

[059-069] and her luggage remaining on his hands, which he hoped to be also clear

[059-070] of later on in the day, and so to have freed his house from the

[059-071] exasperating notoriety it had acquired during the week through his

[059-072] wife’s unlucky admission of these strangers. In the afternoon he

[059-073] privately consulted with the owner of the house, and they agreed that

[059-074] if any objection to it arose from the tragedy which had occurred there

[059-075] they would try to get its number changed.

[059-076] When Jude had seen the two little boxes—one containing little Jude, and

[059-077] the other the two smallest—deposited in the earth he hastened back to

[059-078] Sue, who was still in her room, and he therefore did not disturb her

[059-079] just then. Feeling anxious, however, he went again about four o’clock.

[059-080] The woman thought she was still lying down, but returned to him to say

[059-081] that she was not in her bedroom after all. Her hat and jacket, too,

[059-082] were missing: she had gone out. Jude hurried off to the public house

[059-083] where he was sleeping. She had not been there. Then bethinking himself

[059-084] of possibilities he went along the road to the cemetery, which he

[059-085] entered, and crossed to where the interments had recently taken place.

[059-086] The idlers who had followed to the spot by reason of the tragedy were

[059-087] all gone now. A man with a shovel in his hands was attempting to earth

[059-088] in the common grave of the three children, but his arm was held back by

[059-089] an expostulating woman who stood in the half-filled hole. It was Sue,

[059-090] whose coloured clothing, which she had never thought of changing for

[059-091] the mourning he had bought, suggested to the eye a deeper grief than

[059-092] the conventional garb of bereavement could express.

[059-093] “He’s filling them in, and he shan’t till I’ve seen my little ones

[059-094] again!” she cried wildly when she saw Jude. “I want to see them once

[059-095] more. Oh Jude—please Jude—I want to see them! I didn’t know you would

[059-096] let them be taken away while I was asleep! You said perhaps I should

[059-097] see them once more before they were screwed down; and then you didn’t,

[059-098] but took them away! Oh Jude, you are cruel to me too!”

[059-099] “She’s been wanting me to dig out the grave again, and let her get to

[059-100] the coffins,” said the man with the spade. “She ought to be took home,

[059-101] by the look o’ her. She is hardly responsible, poor thing, seemingly.

[059-102] Can’t dig ’em up again now, ma’am. Do ye go home with your husband, and

[059-103] take it quiet, and thank God that there’ll be another soon to swage yer

[059-104] grief.”

[059-105] But Sue kept asking piteously: “Can’t I see them once more—just once!

[059-106] Can’t I? Only just one little minute, Jude? It would not take long! And

[059-107] I should be so glad, Jude! I will be so good, and not disobey you ever

[059-108] any more, Jude, if you will let me? I would go home quietly afterwards,

[059-109] and not want to see them any more! Can’t I? Why can’t I?”

[059-110] Thus she went on. Jude was thrown into such acute sorrow that he almost

[059-111] felt he would try to get the man to accede. But it could do no good,

[059-112] and might make her still worse; and he saw that it was imperative to

[059-113] get her home at once. So he coaxed her, and whispered tenderly, and put

[059-114] his arm round her to support her; till she helplessly gave in, and was

[059-115] induced to leave the cemetery.

[059-116] He wished to obtain a fly to take her back in, but economy being so

[059-117] imperative she deprecated his doing so, and they walked along slowly,

[059-118] Jude in black crape, she in brown and red clothing. They were to have

[059-119] gone to a new lodging that afternoon, but Jude saw that it was not

[059-120] practicable, and in course of time they entered the now hated house.

[059-121] Sue was at once got to bed, and the Doctor sent for.

[059-122] Jude waited all the evening downstairs. At a very late hour the

[059-123] intelligence was brought to him that a child had been prematurely born,

[059-124] and that it, like the others, was a corpse.

[059-125] III

[059-126] Sue was convalescent, though she had hoped for death, and Jude had

[059-127] again obtained work at his old trade. They were in other lodgings now,

[059-128] in the direction of Beersheba, and not far from the Church of

[059-129] Ceremonies—Saint Silas.

[059-130] They would sit silent, more bodeful of the direct antagonism of things

[059-131] than of their insensate and stolid obstructiveness. Vague and quaint

[059-132] imaginings had haunted Sue in the days when her intellect scintillated

[059-133] like a star, that the world resembled a stanza or melody composed in a

[059-134] dream; it was wonderfully excellent to the half-aroused intelligence,

[059-135] but hopelessly absurd at the full waking; that the First Cause worked

[059-136] automatically like a somnambulist, and not reflectively like a sage;

[059-137] that at the framing of the terrestrial conditions there seemed never to

[059-138] have been contemplated such a development of emotional perceptiveness

[059-139] among the creatures subject to those conditions as that reached by

[059-140] thinking and educated humanity. But affliction makes opposing forces

[059-141] loom anthropomorphous; and those ideas were now exchanged for a sense

[059-142] of Jude and herself fleeing from a persecutor.

[059-143] “We must conform!” she said mournfully. “All the ancient wrath of the

[059-144] Power above us has been vented upon us, His poor creatures, and we must

[059-145] submit. There is no choice. We must. It is no use fighting against

[059-146] God!”

[059-147] “It is only against man and senseless circumstance,” said Jude.

[059-148] “True!” she murmured. “What have I been thinking of! I am getting as

[059-149] superstitious as a savage! … But whoever or whatever our foe may be, I

[059-150] am cowed into submission. I have no more fighting strength left; no

[059-151] more enterprise. I am beaten, beaten! … ‘We are made a spectacle unto

[059-152] the world, and to angels, and to men!’ I am always saying that now.”

[059-153] “I feel the same!”

[059-154] “What shall we do? You are in work now; but remember, it may only be

[059-155] because our history and relations are not absolutely known… Possibly,

[059-156] if they knew our marriage had not been formalized they would turn you

[059-157] out of your job as they did at Aldbrickham!”

[059-158] “I hardly know. Perhaps they would hardly do that. However, I think

[059-159] that we ought to make it legal now—as soon as you are able to go out.”

[059-160] “You think we ought?”

[059-161] “Certainly.”

[059-162] And Jude fell into thought. “I have seemed to myself lately,” he said,

[059-163] “to belong to that vast band of men shunned by the virtuous—the men

[059-164] called seducers. It amazes me when I think of it! I have not been

[059-165] conscious of it, or of any wrongdoing towards you, whom I love more

[059-166] than myself. Yet I am one of those men! I wonder if any other of them

[059-167] are the same purblind, simple creatures as I? … Yes, Sue—that’s what I

[059-168] am. I seduced you… You were a distinct type—a refined creature,

[059-169] intended by Nature to be left intact. But I couldn’t leave you alone!”

[059-170] “No, no, Jude!” she said quickly. “Don’t reproach yourself with being

[059-171] what you are not. If anybody is to blame it is I.”

[059-172] “I supported you in your resolve to leave Phillotson; and without me

[059-173] perhaps you wouldn’t have urged him to let you go.”

[059-174] “I should have, just the same. As to ourselves, the fact of our not

[059-175] having entered into a legal contract is the saving feature in our

[059-176] union. We have thereby avoided insulting, as it were, the solemnity of

[059-177] our first marriages.”
