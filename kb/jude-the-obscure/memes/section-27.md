[027-001] “Now a dew-bit to eat with it,” he said; “and off we go. You can have a

[027-002] regular breakfast when you get there.”

[027-003] They went quietly out of the house, Jude accompanying her to the

[027-004] station. As they departed along the street a head was thrust out of an

[027-005] upper window of his lodging and quickly withdrawn. Sue still seemed

[027-006] sorry for her rashness, and to wish she had not rebelled; telling him

[027-007] at parting that she would let him know as soon as she got re-admitted

[027-008] to the training-school. They stood rather miserably together on the

[027-009] platform; and it was apparent that he wanted to say more.

[027-010] “I want to tell you something—two things,” he said hurriedly as the

[027-011] train came up. “One is a warm one, the other a cold one!”

[027-012] “Jude,” she said. “I know one of them. And you mustn’t!”

[027-013] “What?”

[027-014] “You mustn’t love me. You are to like me—that’s all!”

[027-015] Jude’s face became so full of complicated glooms that hers was agitated

[027-016] in sympathy as she bade him adieu through the carriage window. And then

[027-017] the train moved on, and waving her pretty hand to him she vanished

[027-018] away.

[027-019] Melchester was a dismal place enough for Jude that Sunday of her

[027-020] departure, and the Close so hateful that he did not go once to the

[027-021] cathedral services. The next morning there came a letter from her,

[027-022] which, with her usual promptitude, she had written directly she had

[027-023] reached her friend’s house. She told him of her safe arrival and

[027-024] comfortable quarters, and then added:—

[027-025] What I really write about, dear Jude, is something I said to you at

[027-026] parting. You had been so very good and kind to me that when you were

[027-027] out of sight I felt what a cruel and ungrateful woman I was to say it,

[027-028] and it has reproached me ever since. _If you want to love me, Jude, you

[027-029] may_: I don’t mind at all; and I’ll never say again that you mustn’t!

[027-030] Now I won’t write any more about that. You do forgive your

[027-031] thoughtless friend for her cruelty? and won’t make her miserable by

[027-032] saying you don’t?—Ever,

[027-033] SUE.

[027-034] It would be superfluous to say what his answer was; and how he thought

[027-035] what he would have done had he been free, which should have rendered a

[027-036] long residence with a female friend quite unnecessary for Sue. He felt

[027-037] he might have been pretty sure of his own victory if it had come to a

[027-038] conflict between Phillotson and himself for the possession of her.

[027-039] Yet Jude was in danger of attaching more meaning to Sue’s impulsive

[027-040] note than it really was intended to bear.

[027-041] After the lapse of a few days he found himself hoping that she would

[027-042] write again. But he received no further communication; and in the

[027-043] intensity of his solicitude he sent another note, suggesting that he

[027-044] should pay her a visit some Sunday, the distance being under eighteen

[027-045] miles.

[027-046] He expected a reply on the second morning after despatching his

[027-047] missive; but none came. The third morning arrived; the postman did not

[027-048] stop. This was Saturday, and in a feverish state of anxiety about her

[027-049] he sent off three brief lines stating that he was coming the following

[027-050] day, for he felt sure something had happened.

[027-051] His first and natural thought had been that she was ill from her

[027-052] immersion; but it soon occurred to him that somebody would have written

[027-053] for her in such a case. Conjectures were put an end to by his arrival

[027-054] at the village school-house near Shaston on the bright morning of

[027-055] Sunday, between eleven and twelve o’clock, when the parish was as

[027-056] vacant as a desert, most of the inhabitants having gathered inside the

[027-057] church, whence their voices could occasionally be heard in unison.

[027-058] A little girl opened the door. “Miss Bridehead is up-stairs,” she said.

[027-059] “And will you please walk up to her?”

[027-060] “Is she ill?” asked Jude hastily.

[027-061] “Only a little—not very.”

[027-062] Jude entered and ascended. On reaching the landing a voice told him

[027-063] which way to turn—the voice of Sue calling his name. He passed the

[027-064] doorway, and found her lying in a little bed in a room a dozen feet

[027-065] square.

[027-066] “Oh, Sue!” he cried, sitting down beside her and taking her hand. “How

[027-067] is this! You couldn’t write?”

[027-068] “No—it wasn’t that!” she answered. “I did catch a bad cold—but I could

[027-069] have written. Only I wouldn’t!”

[027-070] “Why not?—frightening me like this!”

[027-071] “Yes—that was what I was afraid of! But I had decided not to write to

[027-072] you any more. They won’t have me back at the school—that’s why I

[027-073] couldn’t write. Not the fact, but the reason!”

[027-074] “Well?”

[027-075] “They not only won’t have me, but they gave me a parting piece of

[027-076] advice—”

[027-077] “What?”

[027-078] She did not answer directly. “I vowed I never would tell you, Jude—it

[027-079] is so vulgar and distressing!”

[027-080] “Is it about us?”

[027-081] “Yes.”

[027-082] “But do tell me!”

[027-083] “Well—somebody has sent them baseless reports about us, and they say

[027-084] you and I ought to marry as soon as possible, for the sake of my

[027-085] reputation! … There—now I have told you, and I wish I hadn’t!”

[027-086] “Oh, poor Sue!”

[027-087] “I don’t think of you like that means! It did just _occur_ to me to

[027-088] regard you in the way they think I do, but I hadn’t begun to. I _have_

[027-089] recognized that the cousinship was merely nominal, since we met as

[027-090] total strangers. But my marrying you, dear Jude—why, of course, if I

[027-091] had reckoned upon marrying you I shouldn’t have come to you so often!

[027-092] And I never supposed you thought of such a thing as marrying me till

[027-093] the other evening; when I began to fancy you did love me a little.

[027-094] Perhaps I ought not to have been so intimate with you. It is all my

[027-095] fault. Everything is my fault always!”

[027-096] The speech seemed a little forced and unreal, and they regarded each

[027-097] other with a mutual distress.

[027-098] “I was so blind at first!” she went on. “I didn’t see what you felt at

[027-099] all. Oh, you have been unkind to me—you have—to look upon me as a

[027-100] sweetheart without saying a word, and leaving me to discover it myself!

[027-101] Your attitude to me has become known; and naturally they think we’ve

[027-102] been doing wrong! I’ll never trust you again!”

[027-103] “Yes, Sue,” he said simply; “I am to blame—more than you think. I was

[027-104] quite aware that you did not suspect till within the last meeting or

[027-105] two what I was feeling about you. I admit that our meeting as strangers

[027-106] prevented a sense of relationship, and that it was a sort of subterfuge

[027-107] to avail myself of it. But don’t you think I deserve a little

[027-108] consideration for concealing my wrong, very wrong, sentiments, since I

[027-109] couldn’t help having them?”

[027-110] She turned her eyes doubtfully towards him, and then looked away as if

[027-111] afraid she might forgive him.

[027-112] By every law of nature and sex a kiss was the only rejoinder that

[027-113] fitted the mood and the moment, under the suasion of which Sue’s

[027-114] undemonstrative regard of him might not inconceivably have changed its

[027-115] temperature. Some men would have cast scruples to the winds, and

[027-116] ventured it, oblivious both of Sue’s declaration of her neutral

[027-117] feelings, and of the pair of autographs in the vestry chest of

[027-118] Arabella’s parish church. Jude did not. He had, in fact, come in part

[027-119] to tell his own fatal story. It was upon his lips; yet at the hour of

[027-120] this distress he could not disclose it. He preferred to dwell upon the

[027-121] recognized barriers between them.

[027-122] “Of course—I know you don’t—care about me in any particular way,” he

[027-123] sorrowed. “You ought not, and you are right. You belong to—Mr.

[027-124] Phillotson. I suppose he has been to see you?”

[027-125] “Yes,” she said shortly, her face changing a little. “Though I didn’t

[027-126] ask him to come. You are glad, of course, that he has been! But I

[027-127] shouldn’t care if he didn’t come any more!”

[027-128] It was very perplexing to her lover that she should be piqued at his

[027-129] honest acquiescence in his rival, if Jude’s feelings of love were

[027-130] deprecated by her. He went on to something else.

[027-131] “This will blow over, dear Sue,” he said. “The training-school

[027-132] authorities are not all the world. You can get to be a student in some

[027-133] other, no doubt.”

[027-134] “I’ll ask Mr. Phillotson,” she said decisively.

[027-135] Sue’s kind hostess now returned from church, and there was no more

[027-136] intimate conversation. Jude left in the afternoon, hopelessly unhappy.

[027-137] But he had seen her, and sat with her. Such intercourse as that would

[027-138] have to content him for the remainder of his life. The lesson of

[027-139] renunciation it was necessary and proper that he, as a parish priest,

[027-140] should learn.

[027-141] But the next morning when he awoke he felt rather vexed with her, and

[027-142] decided that she was rather unreasonable, not to say capricious. Then,

[027-143] in illustration of what he had begun to discern as one of her redeeming

[027-144] characteristics there came promptly a note, which she must have written

[027-145] almost immediately he had gone from her:

[027-146] Forgive me for my petulance yesterday! I was horrid to you; I know it,

[027-147] and I feel perfectly miserable at my horridness. It was so dear of you

[027-148] not to be angry! Jude, please still keep me as your friend and

[027-149] associate, with all my faults. I’ll try not to be like it again.

[027-150] I am coming to Melchester on Saturday, to get my things away from

[027-151] the T. S., &c. I could walk with you for half an hour, if you would

[027-152] like?—Your repentant

[027-153] SUE.

[027-154] Jude forgave her straightway, and asked her to call for him at the

[027-155] cathedral works when she came.

[027-156] VI

[027-157] Meanwhile a middle-aged man was dreaming a dream of great beauty

[027-158] concerning the writer of the above letter. He was Richard Phillotson,

[027-159] who had recently removed from the mixed village school at Lumsdon near

[027-160] Christminster, to undertake a large boys’ school in his native town of

[027-161] Shaston, which stood on a hill sixty miles to the south-west as the

[027-162] crow flies.

[027-163] A glance at the place and its accessories was almost enough to reveal

[027-164] that the schoolmaster’s plans and dreams so long indulged in had been

[027-165] abandoned for some new dream with which neither the Church nor

[027-166] literature had much in common. Essentially an unpractical man, he was

[027-167] now bent on making and saving money for a practical purpose—that of

[027-168] keeping a wife, who, if she chose, might conduct one of the girls’

[027-169] schools adjoining his own; for which purpose he had advised her to go

[027-170] into training, since she would not marry him offhand.

[027-171] About the time that Jude was removing from Marygreen to Melchester, and

[027-172] entering on adventures at the latter place with Sue, the schoolmaster

[027-173] was settling down in the new school-house at Shaston. All the furniture

[027-174] being fixed, the books shelved, and the nails driven, he had begun to

[027-175] sit in his parlour during the dark winter nights and re-attempt some of

[027-176] his old studies—one branch of which had included Roman-Britannic

[027-177] antiquities—an unremunerative labour for a national school-master but a

[027-178] subject, that, after his abandonment of the university scheme, had

[027-179] interested him as being a comparatively unworked mine; practicable to

[027-180] those who, like himself, had lived in lonely spots where these remains

[027-181] were abundant, and were seen to compel inferences in startling contrast

[027-182] to accepted views on the civilization of that time.

[027-183] A resumption of this investigation was the outward and apparent hobby

[027-184] of Phillotson at present—his ostensible reason for going alone into

[027-185] fields where causeways, dykes, and tumuli abounded, or shutting himself

[027-186] up in his house with a few urns, tiles, and mosaics he had collected,

[027-187] instead of calling round upon his new neighbours, who for their part

[027-188] had showed themselves willing enough to be friendly with him. But it

[027-189] was not the real, or the whole, reason, after all. Thus on a particular

[027-190] evening in the month, when it had grown quite late—to near midnight,

[027-191] indeed—and the light of his lamp, shining from his window at a salient

[027-192] angle of the hill-top town over infinite miles of valley westward,

[027-193] announced as by words a place and person given over to study, he was

[027-194] not exactly studying.
