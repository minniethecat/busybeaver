# Marriage

Marriage in *Jude the Obscure* is not a romantic ideal but a social institution under sustained critique — a legal contract that, in Hardy's vision, entraps individuals in bonds they cannot escape, punishes those who attempt to live outside it, and systematically destroys love rather than sheltering it.

## Definition

In Victorian England, marriage was a near-indissoluble legal contract. The Matrimonial Causes Act of 1857 had made divorce theoretically possible, but it remained inaccessible to the working class — requiring an Act of Parliament for remarriage, with costs that put it beyond reach. For characters like Jude and Sue, legal marriage was effectively permanent, and the only escape was separation without remarriage, which carried severe social penalties.

Hardy presents marriage as a tragic inheritance — the Fawley family carries a generational curse: "marriage is killing work for our family," as Drusilla Fawley warns. This is not superstition but Hardy's shorthand for a structural reality: the institution of marriage, as legally constituted, is incompatible with human happiness for his characters.

## In *Jude the Obscure*

### Manifestation

The novel presents three marriages, each a variation on the theme of institutional failure:

1. **Jude and Arabella**: A marriage of deception — Arabella feigns pregnancy to trap Jude. It is a union of physical convenience and manipulation, ended by Arabella's departure for Australia but never legally dissolved for Jude.
2. **Sue and Phillotson**: A marriage of gratitude — Sue marries Phillotson because she feels responsible for his professional ruin. She is physically repelled by him, and the marriage is never consummated in any emotional sense.
3. **Jude and Sue's common-law union**: Not a legal marriage at all — the couple lives together unmarried, and this "irregular" arrangement brings them their greatest happiness and their most devastating punishment.

### Narrative Trajectory

The marriage theme follows a tightening spiral. Jude's first marriage to Arabella (Part First) is a mistake born of youth and deception. Sue's marriage to Phillotson (Part Third) is a rational choice that proves emotionally catastrophic. The couple's attempt to live outside marriage (Parts Fourth and Fifth) produces their brief happiness — but social ostracism, poverty, and the children's deaths destroy it. By Part Sixth, Sue has returned to Phillotson in a remarriage that Hardy presents as a form of living death, and Jude dies remarried to Arabella in a union devoid of love.

## Critical Interpretation

Hardy's treatment of marriage made *Jude the Obscure* notorious. Victorian critics accused Hardy of advocating free love and attacking the sanctity of marriage. Contemporary feminist criticism reads the novel as a structural analysis of how marriage law functions as an instrument of patriarchal control — Sue's body, once legally given to Phillotson, can never fully be her own again, even after she leaves him.

The novel anticipates twentieth-century debates about divorce reform. Hardy himself separated from his first wife Emma during the novel's composition, and the bitterness of the marriage plot reflects personal experience as much as social critique.
