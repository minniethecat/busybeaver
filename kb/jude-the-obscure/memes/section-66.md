[066-001] “Yes. Exactly. Very thoughtful of you, deary, even though it hasn’t

[066-002] much to do with our present business.”

[066-003] “Why, yes it has! I’m giving my body to be burned! But—ah you don’t

[066-004] understand!—it wants Sue to understand such things! And I was her

[066-005] seducer—poor little girl! And she’s gone—and I don’t care about myself!

[066-006] Do what you like with me! … And yet she did it for conscience’ sake,

[066-007] poor little Sue!”

[066-008] “Hang her!—I mean, I think she was right,” hiccuped Arabella. “I’ve my

[066-009] feelings too, like her; and I feel I belong to you in Heaven’s eye, and

[066-010] to nobody else, till death us do part! It is—hic—never too late—hic to

[066-011] mend!”

[066-012] They had reached her father’s house, and she softly unfastened the

[066-013] door, groping about for a light within.

[066-014] The circumstances were not altogether unlike those of their entry into

[066-015] the cottage at Cresscombe, such a long time before. Nor were perhaps

[066-016] Arabella’s motives. But Jude did not think of that, though she did.

[066-017] “I can’t find the matches, dear,” she said when she had fastened up the

[066-018] door. “But never mind—this way. As quiet as you can, please.”

[066-019] “It is as dark as pitch,” said Jude.

[066-020] “Give me your hand, and I’ll lead you. That’s it. Just sit down here,

[066-021] and I’ll pull off your boots. I don’t want to wake him.”

[066-022] “Who?”

[066-023] “Father. He’d make a row, perhaps.”

[066-024] She pulled off his boots. “Now,” she whispered, “take hold of me—never

[066-025] mind your weight. Now—first stair, second stair—”

[066-026] “But—are we out in our old house by Marygreen?” asked the stupefied

[066-027] Jude. “I haven’t been inside it for years till now! Hey? And where are

[066-028] my books? That’s what I want to know?”

[066-029] “We are at my house, dear, where there’s nobody to spy out how ill you

[066-030] are. Now—third stair, fourth stair—that’s it. Now we shall get on.”

[066-031] VII

[066-032] Arabella was preparing breakfast in the downstairs back room of this

[066-033] small, recently hired tenement of her father’s. She put her head into

[066-034] the little pork-shop in front, and told Mr. Donn it was ready. Donn,

[066-035] endeavouring to look like a master pork-butcher, in a greasy blue

[066-036] blouse, and with a strap round his waist from which a steel dangled,

[066-037] came in promptly.

[066-038] “You must mind the shop this morning,” he said casually. “I’ve to go

[066-039] and get some inwards and half a pig from Lumsdon, and to call

[066-040] elsewhere. If you live here you must put your shoulder to the wheel, at

[066-041] least till I get the business started!”

[066-042] “Well, for to-day I can’t say.” She looked deedily into his face. “I’ve

[066-043] got a prize upstairs.”

[066-044] “Oh? What’s that?”

[066-045] “A husband—almost.”

[066-046] “No!”

[066-047] “Yes. It’s Jude. He’s come back to me.”

[066-048] “Your old original one? Well, I’m damned!”

[066-049] “Well, I always did like him, that I will say.”

[066-050] “But how does he come to be up there?” said Donn, humour-struck, and

[066-051] nodding to the ceiling.

[066-052] “Don’t ask inconvenient questions, Father. What we’ve to do is to keep

[066-053] him here till he and I are—as we were.”

[066-054] “How was that?”

[066-055] “Married.”

[066-056] “Ah… Well it is the rummest thing I ever heard of—marrying an old

[066-057] husband again, and so much new blood in the world! He’s no catch, to my

[066-058] thinking. I’d have had a new one while I was about it.”

[066-059] “It isn’t rum for a woman to want her old husband back for

[066-060] respectability, though for a man to want his old wife back—well,

[066-061] perhaps it is funny, rather!” And Arabella was suddenly seized with a

[066-062] fit of loud laughter, in which her father joined more moderately.

[066-063] “Be civil to him, and I’ll do the rest,” she said when she had

[066-064] recovered seriousness. “He told me this morning that his head ached fit

[066-065] to burst, and he hardly seemed to know where he was. And no wonder,

[066-066] considering how he mixed his drink last night. We must keep him jolly

[066-067] and cheerful here for a day or two, and not let him go back to his

[066-068] lodging. Whatever you advance I’ll pay back to you again. But I must go

[066-069] up and see how he is now, poor deary.”

[066-070] Arabella ascended the stairs, softly opened the door of the first

[066-071] bedroom, and peeped in. Finding that her shorn Samson was asleep she

[066-072] entered to the bedside and stood regarding him. The fevered flush on

[066-073] his face from the debauch of the previous evening lessened the

[066-074] fragility of his ordinary appearance, and his long lashes, dark brows,

[066-075] and curly back hair and beard against the white pillow completed the

[066-076] physiognomy of one whom Arabella, as a woman of rank passions, still

[066-077] felt it worth while to recapture, highly important to recapture as a

[066-078] woman straitened both in means and in reputation. Her ardent gaze

[066-079] seemed to affect him; his quick breathing became suspended, and he

[066-080] opened his eyes.

[066-081] “How are you now, dear?” said she. “It is I—Arabella.”

[066-082] “Ah!—where—oh yes, I remember! You gave me shelter… I am

[066-083] stranded—ill—demoralized—damn bad! That’s what I am!”

[066-084] “Then do stay here. There’s nobody in the house but father and me, and

[066-085] you can rest till you are thoroughly well. I’ll tell them at the

[066-086] stoneworks that you are knocked up.”

[066-087] “I wonder what they are thinking at the lodgings!”

[066-088] “I’ll go round and explain. Perhaps you had better let me pay up, or

[066-089] they’ll think we’ve run away?”

[066-090] “Yes. You’ll find enough money in my pocket there.”

[066-091] Quite indifferent, and shutting his eyes because he could not bear the

[066-092] daylight in his throbbing eye-balls, Jude seemed to doze again.

[066-093] Arabella took his purse, softly left the room, and putting on her

[066-094] outdoor things went off to the lodgings she and he had quitted the

[066-095] evening before.

[066-096] Scarcely half an hour had elapsed ere she reappeared round the corner,

[066-097] walking beside a lad wheeling a truck on which were piled all Jude’s

[066-098] household possessions, and also the few of Arabella’s things which she

[066-099] had taken to the lodging for her short sojourn there. Jude was in such

[066-100] physical pain from his unfortunate break-down of the previous night,

[066-101] and in such mental pain from the loss of Sue and from having yielded in

[066-102] his half-somnolent state to Arabella, that when he saw his few chattels

[066-103] unpacked and standing before his eyes in this strange bedroom,

[066-104] intermixed with woman’s apparel, he scarcely considered how they had

[066-105] come there, or what their coming signalized.

[066-106] “Now,” said Arabella to her father downstairs, “we must keep plenty of

[066-107] good liquor going in the house these next few days. I know his nature,

[066-108] and if he once gets into that fearfully low state that he does get into

[066-109] sometimes, he’ll never do the honourable thing by me in this world, and

[066-110] I shall be left in the lurch. He must be kept cheerful. He has a little

[066-111] money in the savings bank, and he has given me his purse to pay for

[066-112] anything necessary. Well, that will be the licence; for I must have

[066-113] that ready at hand, to catch him the moment he’s in the humour. You

[066-114] must pay for the liquor. A few friends, and a quiet convivial party

[066-115] would be the thing, if we could get it up. It would advertise the shop,

[066-116] and help me too.”

[066-117] “That can be got up easy enough by anybody who’ll afford victuals and

[066-118] drink… Well yes—it would advertise the shop—that’s true.”

[066-119] Three days later, when Jude had recovered somewhat from the fearful

[066-120] throbbing of his eyes and brain, but was still considerably confused in

[066-121] his mind by what had been supplied to him by Arabella during the

[066-122] interval—to keep him, jolly, as she expressed it—the quiet convivial

[066-123] gathering, suggested by her, to wind Jude up to the striking point,

[066-124] took place.

[066-125] Donn had only just opened his miserable little pork and sausage shop,

[066-126] which had as yet scarce any customers; nevertheless that party

[066-127] advertised it well, and the Donns acquired a real notoriety among a

[066-128] certain class in Christminster who knew not the colleges, nor their

[066-129] works, nor their ways. Jude was asked if he could suggest any guest in

[066-130] addition to those named by Arabella and her father, and in a saturnine

[066-131] humour of perfect recklessness mentioned Uncle Joe, and Stagg, and the

[066-132] decayed auctioneer, and others whom he remembered as having been

[066-133] frequenters of the well-known tavern during his bout therein years

[066-134] before. He also suggested Freckles and Bower o’ Bliss. Arabella took

[066-135] him at his word so far as the men went, but drew the line at the

[066-136] ladies.

[066-137] Another man they knew, Tinker Taylor, though he lived in the same

[066-138] street, was not invited; but as he went homeward from a late job on the

[066-139] evening of the party, he had occasion to call at the shop for trotters.

[066-140] There were none in, but he was promised some the next morning. While

[066-141] making his inquiry Taylor glanced into the back room, and saw the

[066-142] guests sitting round, card-playing, and drinking, and otherwise

[066-143] enjoying themselves at Donn’s expense. He went home to bed, and on his

[066-144] way out next morning wondered how the party went off. He thought it

[066-145] hardly worth while to call at the shop for his provisions at that hour,

[066-146] Donn and his daughter being probably not up, if they caroused late the

[066-147] night before. However, he found in passing that the door was open, and

[066-148] he could hear voices within, though the shutters of the meat-stall were

[066-149] not down. He went and tapped at the sitting-room door, and opened it.

[066-150] “Well—to be sure!” he said, astonished.

[066-151] Hosts and guests were sitting card-playing, smoking, and talking,

[066-152] precisely as he had left them eleven hours earlier; the gas was burning

[066-153] and the curtains drawn, though it had been broad daylight for two hours

[066-154] out of doors.

[066-155] “Yes!” cried Arabella, laughing. “Here we are, just the same. We ought

[066-156] to be ashamed of ourselves, oughtn’t we? But it is a sort of

[066-157] housewarming, you see; and our friends are in no hurry. Come in, Mr.

[066-158] Taylor, and sit down.”

[066-159] The tinker, or rather reduced ironmonger, was nothing loath, and

[066-160] entered and took a seat. “I shall lose a quarter, but never mind,” he

[066-161] said. “Well, really, I could hardly believe my eyes when I looked in!

[066-162] It seemed as if I was flung back again into last night, all of a

[066-163] sudden.”

[066-164] “So you are. Pour out for Mr. Taylor.”

[066-165] He now perceived that she was sitting beside Jude, her arm being round

[066-166] his waist. Jude, like the rest of the company, bore on his face the

[066-167] signs of how deeply he had been indulging.

[066-168] “Well, we’ve been waiting for certain legal hours to arrive, to tell

[066-170] look as much like a maiden blush as possible. “Jude and I have decided

[066-171] to make up matters between us by tying the knot again, as we find we

[066-172] can’t do without one another after all. So, as a bright notion, we

[066-173] agreed to sit on till it was late enough, and go and do it off-hand.”

[066-174] Jude seemed to pay no great heed to what she was announcing, or indeed

[066-175] to anything whatever. The entrance of Taylor infused fresh spirit into

[066-176] the company, and they remained sitting, till Arabella whispered to her

[066-177] father: “Now we may as well go.”

[066-178] “But the parson don’t know?”

[066-179] “Yes, I told him last night that we might come between eight and nine,

[066-180] as there were reasons of decency for doing it as early and quiet as

[066-181] possible; on account of it being our second marriage, which might make

[066-182] people curious to look on if they knew. He highly approved.”

[066-183] “Oh very well, I’m ready,” said her father, getting up and shaking

[066-184] himself.

[066-185] “Now, old darling,” she said to Jude. “Come along, as you promised.”

[066-186] “When did I promise anything?” asked he, whom she had made so tipsy by

[066-188] him sober again—or to seem so to those who did not know him.
