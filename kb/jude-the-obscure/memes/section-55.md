[055-001] It was two hours later on the same day that Anny and Mrs. Cartlett,

[055-002] having had tea at the Temperance Hotel, started on their return journey

[055-003] across the high and open country which stretches between Kennetbridge

[055-004] and Alfredston. Arabella was in a thoughtful mood; but her thoughts

[055-005] were not of the new chapel, as Anny at first surmised.

[055-006] “No—it is something else,” at last said Arabella sullenly. “I came here

[055-007] to-day never thinking of anybody but poor Cartlett, or of anything but

[055-008] spreading the Gospel by means of this new tabernacle they’ve begun this

[055-009] afternoon. But something has happened to turn my mind another way

[055-010] quite. Anny, I’ve heard of un again, and I’ve seen _her_!”

[055-011] “Who?”

[055-012] “I’ve heard of Jude, and I’ve seen his wife. And ever since, do what I

[055-013] will, and though I sung the hymns wi’ all my strength, I have not been

[055-014] able to help thinking about ’n; which I’ve no right to do as a chapel

[055-015] member.”

[055-016] “Can’t ye fix your mind upon what was said by the London preacher

[055-017] to-day, and try to get rid of your wandering fancies that way?”

[055-018] “I do. But my wicked heart will ramble off in spite of myself!”

[055-019] “Well—I know what it is to have a wanton mind o’ my own, too! If you

[055-020] on’y knew what I do dream sometimes o’ nights quite against my wishes,

[055-021] you’d say I had my struggles!” (Anny, too, had grown rather serious of

[055-022] late, her lover having jilted her.)

[055-023] “What shall I do about it?” urged Arabella morbidly.

[055-024] “You could take a lock of your late-lost husband’s hair, and have it

[055-025] made into a mourning brooch, and look at it every hour of the day.”

[055-026] “I haven’t a morsel!—and if I had ’twould be no good… After all that’s

[055-027] said about the comforts of this religion, I wish I had Jude back

[055-028] again!”

[055-029] “You must fight valiant against the feeling, since he’s another’s. And

[055-030] I’ve heard that another good thing for it, when it afflicts volupshious

[055-031] widows, is to go to your husband’s grave in the dusk of evening, and

[055-032] stand a long while a-bowed down.”

[055-033] “Pooh! I know as well as you what I should do; only I don’t do it!”

[055-034] They drove in silence along the straight road till they were within the

[055-035] horizon of Marygreen, which lay not far to the left of their route.

[055-036] They came to the junction of the highway and the cross-lane leading to

[055-037] that village, whose church-tower could be seen athwart the hollow. When

[055-038] they got yet farther on, and were passing the lonely house in which

[055-039] Arabella and Jude had lived during the first months of their marriage,

[055-040] and where the pig-killing had taken place, she could control herself no

[055-041] longer.

[055-042] “He’s more mine than hers!” she burst out. “What right has she to him,

[055-043] I should like to know! I’d take him from her if I could!”

[055-044] “Fie, Abby! And your husband only six weeks gone! Pray against it!”

[055-045] “Be damned if I do! Feelings are feelings! I won’t be a creeping

[055-046] hypocrite any longer—so there!”

[055-047] Arabella had hastily drawn from her pocket a bundle of tracts which she

[055-048] had brought with her to distribute at the fair, and of which she had

[055-049] given away several. As she spoke she flung the whole remainder of the

[055-050] packet into the hedge. “I’ve tried that sort o’ physic and have failed

[055-051] wi’ it. I must be as I was born!”

[055-052] “Hush! You be excited, dear! Now you come along home quiet, and have a

[055-053] cup of tea, and don’t let us talk about un no more. We won’t come out

[055-054] this road again, as it leads to where he is, because it inflames ’ee

[055-055] so. You’ll be all right again soon.”

[055-056] Arabella did calm herself down by degrees; and they crossed the

[055-057] ridge-way. When they began to descend the long, straight hill, they saw

[055-058] plodding along in front of them an elderly man of spare stature and

[055-059] thoughtful gait. In his hand he carried a basket; and there was a touch

[055-060] of slovenliness in his attire, together with that indefinable something

[055-061] in his whole appearance which suggested one who was his own

[055-062] housekeeper, purveyor, confidant, and friend, through possessing nobody

[055-063] else at all in the world to act in those capacities for him. The

[055-064] remainder of the journey was down-hill, and guessing him to be going to

[055-065] Alfredston they offered him a lift, which he accepted.

[055-066] Arabella looked at him, and looked again, till at length she spoke. “If

[055-067] I don’t mistake I am talking to Mr. Phillotson?”

[055-068] The wayfarer faced round and regarded her in turn. “Yes; my name is

[055-069] Phillotson,” he said. “But I don’t recognize you, ma’am.”

[055-070] “I remember you well enough when you used to be schoolmaster out at

[055-071] Marygreen, and I one of your scholars. I used to walk up there from

[055-072] Cresscombe every day, because we had only a mistress down at our place,

[055-073] and you taught better. But you wouldn’t remember me as I should

[055-074] you?—Arabella Donn.”

[055-075] He shook his head. “No,” he said politely, “I don’t recall the name.

[055-076] And I should hardly recognize in your present portly self the slim

[055-077] school child no doubt you were then.”

[055-078] “Well, I always had plenty of flesh on my bones. However, I am staying

[055-079] down here with some friends at present. You know, I suppose, who I

[055-080] married?”

[055-081] “No.”

[055-082] “Jude Fawley—also a scholar of yours—at least a night scholar—for some

[055-083] little time, I think? And known to you afterwards, if I am not

[055-084] mistaken.”

[055-085] “Dear me, dear me,” said Phillotson, starting out of his stiffness.

[055-086] “_You_ Fawley’s wife? To be sure—he had a wife! And he—I understood—”

[055-087] “Divorced her—as you did yours—perhaps for better reasons.”

[055-088] “Indeed?”

[055-089] “Well—he med have been right in doing it—right for both; for I soon

[055-090] married again, and all went pretty straight till my husband died

[055-091] lately. But you—you were decidedly wrong!”

[055-092] “No,” said Phillotson, with sudden testiness. “I would rather not talk

[055-093] of this, but—I am convinced I did only what was right, and just, and

[055-094] moral. I have suffered for my act and opinions, but I hold to them;

[055-095] though her loss was a loss to me in more ways than one!”

[055-096] “You lost your school and good income through her, did you not?”

[055-097] “I don’t care to talk of it. I have recently come back here—to

[055-098] Marygreen. I mean.”

[055-099] “You are keeping the school there again, just as formerly?”

[055-100] The pressure of a sadness that would out unsealed him. “I am there,” he

[055-101] replied. “Just as formerly, no. Merely on sufferance. It was a last

[055-102] resource—a small thing to return to after my move upwards, and my long

[055-103] indulged hopes—a returning to zero, with all its humiliations. But it

[055-104] is a refuge. I like the seclusion of the place, and the vicar having

[055-105] known me before my so-called eccentric conduct towards my wife had

[055-106] ruined my reputation as a schoolmaster, he accepted my services when

[055-107] all other schools were closed against me. However, although I take

[055-108] fifty pounds a year here after taking above two hundred elsewhere, I

[055-109] prefer it to running the risk of having my old domestic experiences

[055-110] raked up against me, as I should do if I tried to make a move.”

[055-111] “Right you are. A contented mind is a continual feast. She has done no

[055-112] better.”

[055-113] “She is not doing well, you mean?”

[055-114] “I met her by accident at Kennetbridge this very day, and she is

[055-115] anything but thriving. Her husband is ill, and she anxious. You made a

[055-116] fool of a mistake about her, I tell ’ee again, and the harm you did

[055-117] yourself by dirting your own nest serves you right, excusing the

[055-118] liberty.”

[055-119] “How?”

[055-120] “She was innocent.”

[055-121] “But nonsense! They did not even defend the case!”

[055-122] “That was because they didn’t care to. She was quite innocent of what

[055-123] obtained you your freedom, at the time you obtained it. I saw her just

[055-124] afterwards, and proved it to myself completely by talking to her.”

[055-125] Phillotson grasped the edge of the spring-cart, and appeared to be much

[055-126] stressed and worried by the information. “Still—she wanted to go,” he

[055-127] said.

[055-128] “Yes. But you shouldn’t have let her. That’s the only way with these

[055-129] fanciful women that chaw high—innocent or guilty. She’d have come round

[055-130] in time. We all do! Custom does it! It’s all the same in the end!

[055-131] However, I think she’s fond of her man still—whatever he med be of her.

[055-132] You were too quick about her. _I_ shouldn’t have let her go! I should

[055-133] have kept her chained on—her spirit for kicking would have been broke

[055-134] soon enough! There’s nothing like bondage and a stone-deaf taskmaster

[055-135] for taming us women. Besides, you’ve got the laws on your side. Moses

[055-136] knew. Don’t you call to mind what he says?”

[055-137] “Not for the moment, ma’am, I regret to say.”

[055-138] “Call yourself a schoolmaster! I used to think o’t when they read it in

[055-139] church, and I was carrying on a bit. ‘Then shall the man be guiltless;

[055-140] but the woman shall bear her iniquity.’ Damn rough on us women; but we

[055-141] must grin and put up wi’ it! Haw haw! Well; she’s got her deserts now.”

[055-142] “Yes,” said Phillotson, with biting sadness. “Cruelty is the law

[055-143] pervading all nature and society; and we can’t get out of it if we

[055-144] would!”

[055-145] “Well—don’t you forget to try it next time, old man.”

[055-146] “I cannot answer you, madam. I have never known much of womankind.”

[055-147] They had now reached the low levels bordering Alfredston, and passing

[055-148] through the outskirts approached a mill, to which Phillotson said his

[055-149] errand led him; whereupon they drew up, and he alighted, bidding them

[055-150] good-night in a preoccupied mood.

[055-151] In the meantime Sue, though remarkably successful in her cake-selling

[055-152] experiment at Kennetbridge fair, had lost the temporary brightness

[055-153] which had begun to sit upon her sadness on account of that success.

[055-154] When all her “Christminster” cakes had been disposed of she took upon

[055-155] her arm the empty basket, and the cloth which had covered the standing

[055-156] she had hired, and giving the other things to the boy left the street

[055-157] with him. They followed a lane to a distance of half a mile, till they

[055-158] met an old woman carrying a child in short clothes, and leading a

[055-159] toddler in the other hand.

[055-160] Sue kissed the children, and said, “How is he now?”

[055-161] “Still better!” returned Mrs. Edlin cheerfully. “Before you are

[055-162] upstairs again your husband will be well enough—don’t ’ee trouble.”

[055-163] They turned, and came to some old, dun-tiled cottages with gardens and

[055-164] fruit-trees. Into one of these they entered by lifting the latch

[055-165] without knocking, and were at once in the general living-room. Here

[055-166] they greeted Jude, who was sitting in an arm-chair, the increased

[055-167] delicacy of his normally delicate features, and the childishly

[055-168] expectant look in his eyes, being alone sufficient to show that he had

[055-169] been passing through a severe illness.

[055-170] “What—you have sold them all?” he said, a gleam of interest lighting up

[055-171] his face.

[055-172] “Yes. Arcades, gables, east windows and all.” She told him the

[055-173] pecuniary results, and then hesitated. At last, when they were left

[055-174] alone, she informed him of the unexpected meeting with Arabella, and

[055-175] the latter’s widowhood.

[055-176] Jude was discomposed. “What—is she living here?” he said.

[055-177] “No; at Alfredston,” said Sue.

[055-178] Jude’s countenance remained clouded. “I thought I had better tell you?”

[055-179] she continued, kissing him anxiously.

[055-180] “Yes… Dear me! Arabella not in the depths of London, but down here! It

[055-181] is only a little over a dozen miles across the country to Alfredston.

[055-182] What is she doing there?”

[055-183] She told him all she knew. “She has taken to chapel-going,” Sue added;

[055-184] “and talks accordingly.”

[055-185] “Well,” said Jude, “perhaps it is for the best that we have almost

[055-186] decided to move on. I feel much better to-day, and shall be well enough

[055-187] to leave in a week or two. Then Mrs. Edlin can go home again—dear

[055-188] faithful old soul—the only friend we have in the world!”
