# Stoke-Barehills

Stoke-Barehills is a small market town in *[[jude-fawley|Jude the Obscure]]* where [[jude-fawley|Jude Fawley]] works as an itinerant stonemason in Part Fifth, after his life with [[sue-bridehead|Sue Bridehead]] has collapsed. Its defining feature — the cemetery that Hardy calls its "most familiar object" — makes Stoke-Barehills the novel's most concentrated emblem of mortality and diminishment. It is a place where [[jude-fawley|Jude]]'s trade becomes his sole remaining identity, stripped of the scholarly ambition and romantic hope that once defined him.

## Overview

Stoke-Barehills is a modest town of "gaunt" aspect (050-076), set in the [[wessex|Wessex]] landscape between [[christminster|Christminster]] and the rural districts. It has no pretensions to culture or [[education|learning]] — Hardy notes that few in Stoke-Barehills "are now even aware" that life once held other possibilities (050-088). The town is defined by its cemetery, which is presented as its central landmark, a fact that carries grim thematic weight in a novel about the death of aspiration.

[[jude-fawley|Jude]] is drawn to Stoke-Barehills by the practical demand for stonemasons — the town's churches and monuments require repair and carving, and [[jude-fawley|Jude]]'s skill as a craftsman is his only portable asset. But the town's cemetery-centric identity makes it a place where [[jude-fawley|Jude]]'s craft services death rather than creation, a bitter inversion of the stonework he once performed on the living colleges of [[christminster|Christminster]].

## In the Novel

### Narrative Significance

Stoke-Barehills appears only in Part Fifth, but its brief presence is thematically loaded. [[jude-fawley|Jude]] arrives there after the dissolution of his partnership with [[sue-bridehead|Sue]], at a point in the novel where his inner life has contracted to the routines of manual labor. The town's description is among Hardy's most compressed pieces of symbolic geography: three sentences establish a landscape of gauntness, cultural oblivion, and death.

"The most familiar object in Stoke-Barehills nowadays is its cemetery" (050-091) — the sentence is a distillation of the novel's preoccupation with mortality and the failure of enlightenment. Earlier in the novel, [[jude-fawley|Jude]] also traveled to Stoke-Barehills "sometimes" during his years of wandering (054-026), suggesting that the town was one station in a career of itinerant labor that spanned much of [[wessex|Wessex]].

## Real-World Basis

| Fictional Name | Real Location | County |
|---------------|---------------|--------|
| Stoke-Barehills | Basingstoke | Hampshire |

Hardy based Stoke-Barehills on Basingstoke, a market town in Hampshire whose Victorian cemetery was a notable civic feature. Basingstoke's reputation as a practical, unromantic market town — a place of trade rather than culture — matches Hardy's description of Stoke-Barehills. The name itself is a Hardy coinage: "Stoke" from the Old English for "place," combined with "Barehills" to suggest the exposed, treeless downs on which the town sits.
