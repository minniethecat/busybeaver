[026-001] Jude felt much depressed; she seemed to get further and further away

[026-002] from him with her strange ways and curious unconsciousness of gender.

[026-003] “Aren’t you _really_ vexed with me, dear Jude?” she suddenly asked, in

[026-004] a voice of such extraordinary tenderness that it hardly seemed to come

[026-005] from the same woman who had just told her story so lightly. “I would

[026-006] rather offend anybody in the world than you, I think!”

[026-007] “I don’t know whether I am vexed or not. I know I care very much about

[026-008] you!”

[026-009] “I care as much for you as for anybody I ever met.”

[026-010] “You don’t care _more_! There, I ought not to say that. Don’t answer

[026-011] it!”

[026-012] There was another long silence. He felt that she was treating him

[026-013] cruelly, though he could not quite say in what way. Her very

[026-014] helplessness seemed to make her so much stronger than he.

[026-015] “I am awfully ignorant on general matters, although I have worked so

[026-016] hard,” he said, to turn the subject. “I am absorbed in theology, you

[026-017] know. And what do you think I should be doing just about now, if you

[026-018] weren’t here? I should be saying my evening prayers. I suppose you

[026-019] wouldn’t like—”

[026-020] “Oh no, no,” she answered, “I would rather not, if you don’t mind. I

[026-021] should seem so—such a hypocrite.”

[026-022] “I thought you wouldn’t join, so I didn’t propose it. You must remember

[026-023] that I hope to be a useful minister some day.”

[026-024] “To be ordained, I think you said?”

[026-025] “Yes.”

[026-026] “Then you haven’t given up the idea?—I thought that perhaps you had by

[026-027] this time.”

[026-028] “Of course not. I fondly thought at first that you felt as I do about

[026-029] that, as you were so mixed up in Christminster Anglicanism. And Mr.

[026-030] Phillotson—”

[026-031] “I have no respect for Christminster whatever, except, in a qualified

[026-032] degree, on its intellectual side,” said Sue Bridehead earnestly. “My

[026-033] friend I spoke of took that out of me. He was the most irreligious man

[026-034] I ever knew, and the most moral. And intellect at Christminster is new

[026-035] wine in old bottles. The mediævalism of Christminster must go, be

[026-036] sloughed off, or Christminster itself will have to go. To be sure, at

[026-037] times one couldn’t help having a sneaking liking for the traditions of

[026-038] the old faith, as preserved by a section of the thinkers there in

[026-039] touching and simple sincerity; but when I was in my saddest, rightest

[026-040] mind I always felt,

[026-041] ‘O ghastly glories of saints, dead limbs of gibbeted Gods!’”…

[026-042] “Sue, you are not a good friend of mine to talk like that!”

[026-043] “Then I won’t, dear Jude!” The emotional throat-note had come back, and

[026-044] she turned her face away.

[026-045] “I still think Christminster has much that is glorious; though I was

[026-046] resentful because I couldn’t get there.” He spoke gently, and resisted

[026-047] his impulse to pique her on to tears.

[026-048] “It is an ignorant place, except as to the townspeople, artizans,

[026-049] drunkards, and paupers,” she said, perverse still at his differing from

[026-050] her. “_They_ see life as it is, of course; but few of the people in the

[026-051] colleges do. You prove it in your own person. You are one of the very

[026-052] men Christminster was intended for when the colleges were founded; a

[026-053] man with a passion for learning, but no money, or opportunities, or

[026-054] friends. But you were elbowed off the pavement by the millionaires’

[026-055] sons.”

[026-056] “Well, I can do without what it confers. I care for something higher.”

[026-057] “And I for something broader, truer,” she insisted. “At present

[026-058] intellect in Christminster is pushing one way, and religion the other;

[026-059] and so they stand stock-still, like two rams butting each other.”

[026-060] “What would Mr. Phillotson—”

[026-061] “It is a place full of fetishists and ghost-seers!”

[026-062] He noticed that whenever he tried to speak of the schoolmaster she

[026-063] turned the conversation to some generalizations about the offending

[026-064] university. Jude was extremely, morbidly, curious about her life as

[026-065] Phillotson’s _protégée_ and betrothed; yet she would not enlighten him.

[026-066] “Well, that’s just what I am, too,” he said. “I am fearful of life,

[026-067] spectre-seeing always.”

[026-068] “But you are good and dear!” she murmured.

[026-069] His heart bumped, and he made no reply.

[026-070] “You are in the Tractarian stage just now, are you not?” she added,

[026-071] putting on flippancy to hide real feeling, a common trick with her.

[026-072] “Let me see—when was I there? In the year eighteen hundred and—”

[026-073] “There’s a sarcasm in that which is rather unpleasant to me, Sue. Now

[026-074] will you do what I want you to? At this time I read a chapter, and then

[026-075] say prayers, as I told you. Now will you concentrate your attention on

[026-076] any book of these you like, and sit with your back to me, and leave me

[026-077] to my custom? You are sure you won’t join me?”

[026-078] “I’ll look at you.”

[026-079] “No. Don’t tease, Sue!”

[026-080] “Very well—I’ll do just as you bid me, and I won’t vex you, Jude,” she

[026-081] replied, in the tone of a child who was going to be good for ever

[026-082] after, turning her back upon him accordingly. A small Bible other than

[026-083] the one he was using lay near her, and during his retreat she took it

[026-084] up, and turned over the leaves.

[026-085] “Jude,” she said brightly, when he had finished and come back to her;

[026-086] “will you let me make you a _new_ New Testament, like the one I made

[026-087] for myself at Christminster?”

[026-088] “Oh yes. How was that made?”

[026-089] “I altered my old one by cutting up all the Epistles and Gospels into

[026-090] separate _brochures_, and rearranging them in chronological order as

[026-091] written, beginning the book with Thessalonians, following on with the

[026-092] Epistles, and putting the Gospels much further on. Then I had the

[026-093] volume rebound. My university friend Mr.—but never mind his name, poor

[026-094] boy—said it was an excellent idea. I know that reading it afterwards

[026-095] made it twice as interesting as before, and twice as understandable.”

[026-096] “H’m!” said Jude, with a sense of sacrilege.

[026-097] “And what a literary enormity this is,” she said, as she glanced into

[026-098] the pages of Solomon’s Song. “I mean the synopsis at the head of each

[026-099] chapter, explaining away the real nature of that rhapsody. You needn’t

[026-100] be alarmed: nobody claims inspiration for the chapter headings. Indeed,

[026-101] many divines treat them with contempt. It seems the drollest thing to

[026-102] think of the four-and-twenty elders, or bishops, or whatever number

[026-103] they were, sitting with long faces and writing down such stuff.”

[026-104] Jude looked pained. “You are quite Voltairean!” he murmured.

[026-105] “Indeed? Then I won’t say any more, except that people have no right to

[026-106] falsify the Bible! I _hate_ such hum-bug as could attempt to plaster

[026-107] over with ecclesiastical abstractions such ecstatic, natural, human

[026-108] love as lies in that great and passionate song!” Her speech had grown

[026-109] spirited, and almost petulant at his rebuke, and her eyes moist. “I

[026-110] _wish_ I had a friend here to support me; but nobody is ever on my

[026-111] side!”

[026-112] “But my dear Sue, my very dear Sue, I am not against you!” he said,

[026-113] taking her hand, and surprised at her introducing personal feeling into

[026-114] mere argument.

[026-115] “Yes you are, yes you are!” she cried, turning away her face that he

[026-116] might not see her brimming eyes. “You are on the side of the people in

[026-117] the training-school—at least you seem almost to be! What I insist on

[026-118] is, that to explain such verses as this: ‘Whither is thy beloved gone,

[026-119] O thou fairest among women?’ by the note: ‘_The Church professeth her

[026-120] faith_,’ is supremely ridiculous!”

[026-121] “Well then, let it be! You make such a personal matter of everything! I

[026-122] am—only too inclined just now to apply the words profanely. You know

[026-123] _you_ are fairest among women to me, come to that!”

[026-124] “But you are not to say it now!” Sue replied, her voice changing to its

[026-125] softest note of severity. Then their eyes met, and they shook hands

[026-126] like cronies in a tavern, and Jude saw the absurdity of quarrelling on

[026-127] such a hypothetical subject, and she the silliness of crying about what

[026-128] was written in an old book like the Bible.

[026-129] “I won’t disturb your convictions—I really won’t!” she went on

[026-130] soothingly, for now he was rather more ruffled than she. “But I did

[026-131] want and long to ennoble some man to high aims; and when I saw you, and

[026-132] knew you wanted to be my comrade, I—shall I confess it?—thought that

[026-133] man might be you. But you take so much tradition on trust that I don’t

[026-134] know what to say.”

[026-135] “Well, dear; I suppose one must take some things on trust. Life isn’t

[026-136] long enough to work out everything in Euclid problems before you

[026-137] believe it. I take Christianity.”

[026-138] “Well, perhaps you might take something worse.”

[026-139] “Indeed I might. Perhaps I have done so!” He thought of Arabella.

[026-140] “I won’t ask what, because we are going to be _very_ nice with each

[026-141] other, aren’t we, and never, never, vex each other any more?” She

[026-142] looked up trustfully, and her voice seemed trying to nestle in his

[026-143] breast.

[026-144] “I shall always care for you!” said Jude.

[026-145] “And I for you. Because you are single-hearted, and forgiving to your

[026-146] faulty and tiresome little Sue!”

[026-147] He looked away, for that epicene tenderness of hers was too harrowing.

[026-148] Was it that which had broken the heart of the poor leader-writer; and

[026-149] was he to be the next one? … But Sue was so dear! … If he could only

[026-150] get over the sense of her sex, as she seemed to be able to do so easily

[026-151] of his, what a comrade she would make; for their difference of opinion

[026-152] on conjectural subjects only drew them closer together on matters of

[026-153] daily human experience. She was nearer to him than any other woman he

[026-154] had ever met, and he could scarcely believe that time, creed, or

[026-155] absence, would ever divide him from her.

[026-156] But his grief at her incredulities returned. They sat on till she fell

[026-157] asleep again, and he nodded in his chair likewise. Whenever he aroused

[026-158] himself he turned her things, and made up the fire anew. About six

[026-159] o’clock he awoke completely, and lighting a candle, found that her

[026-160] clothes were dry. Her chair being a far more comfortable one than his

[026-161] she still slept on inside his great-coat, looking warm as a new bun and

[026-162] boyish as a Ganymede. Placing the garments by her and touching her on

[026-163] the shoulder he went downstairs, and washed himself by starlight in the

[026-164] yard.

[026-165] V

[026-166] When he returned she was dressed as usual.

[026-167] “Now could I get out without anybody seeing me?” she asked. “The town

[026-168] is not yet astir.”

[026-169] “But you have had no breakfast.”

[026-170] “Oh, I don’t want any! I fear I ought not to have run away from that

[026-171] school! Things seem so different in the cold light of morning, don’t

[026-172] they? What Mr. Phillotson will say I don’t know! It was quite by his

[026-173] wish that I went there. He is the only man in the world for whom I have

[026-174] any respect or fear. I hope he’ll forgive me; but he’ll scold me

[026-175] dreadfully, I expect!”

[026-176] “I’ll go to him and explain—” began Jude.

[026-177] “Oh no, you shan’t. I don’t care for him! He may think what he likes—I

[026-178] shall do just as I choose!”

[026-179] “But you just this moment said—”

[026-180] “Well, if I did, I shall do as I like for all him! I have thought of

[026-181] what I shall do—go to the sister of one of my fellow-students in the

[026-182] training-school, who has asked me to visit her. She has a school near

[026-183] Shaston, about eighteen miles from here—and I shall stay there till

[026-184] this has blown over, and I get back to the training-school again.”

[026-185] At the last moment he persuaded her to let him make her a cup of

[026-186] coffee, in a portable apparatus he kept in his room for use on rising

[026-187] to go to his work every day before the household was astir.
