[056-001] “Where do you think to go to?” Sue asked, a troublousness in her tones.

[056-002] Then Jude confessed what was in his mind. He said it would surprise

[056-003] her, perhaps, after his having resolutely avoided all the old places

[056-004] for so long. But one thing and another had made him think a great deal

[056-005] of Christminster lately, and, if she didn’t mind, he would like to go

[056-006] back there. Why should they care if they were known? It was

[056-007] oversensitive of them to mind so much. They could go on selling cakes

[056-008] there, for that matter, if he couldn’t work. He had no sense of shame

[056-009] at mere poverty; and perhaps he would be as strong as ever soon, and

[056-010] able to set up stone-cutting for himself there.

[056-011] “Why should you care so much for Christminster?” she said pensively.

[056-012] “Christminster cares nothing for you, poor dear!”

[056-013] “Well, I do, I can’t help it. I love the place—although I know how it

[056-014] hates all men like me—the so-called self-taught—how it scorns our

[056-015] laboured acquisitions, when it should be the first to respect them; how

[056-016] it sneers at our false quantities and mispronunciations, when it should

[056-017] say, I see you want help, my poor friend! … Nevertheless, it is the

[056-018] centre of the universe to me, because of my early dream: and nothing

[056-019] can alter it. Perhaps it will soon wake up, and be generous. I pray so!

[056-020] … I should like to go back to live there—perhaps to die there! In two

[056-021] or three weeks I might, I think. It will then be June, and I should

[056-022] like to be there by a particular day.”

[056-023] His hope that he was recovering proved so far well grounded that in

[056-024] three weeks they had arrived in the city of many memories; were

[056-025] actually treading its pavements, receiving the reflection of the

[056-026] sunshine from its wasting walls.

[056-033] I

[056-034] On their arrival the station was lively with straw-hatted young men,

[056-035] welcoming young girls who bore a remarkable family likeness to their

[056-036] welcomers, and who were dressed up in the brightest and lightest of

[056-037] raiment.

[056-038] “The place seems gay,” said Sue. “Why—it is Remembrance Day!—Jude—how

[056-039] sly of you—you came to-day on purpose!”

[056-040] “Yes,” said Jude quietly, as he took charge of the small child, and

[056-041] told Arabella’s boy to keep close to them, Sue attending to their own

[056-042] eldest. “I thought we might as well come to-day as on any other.”

[056-043] “But I am afraid it will depress you!” she said, looking anxiously at

[056-044] him up and down.

[056-045] “Oh, I mustn’t let it interfere with our business; and we have a good

[056-046] deal to do before we shall be settled here. The first thing is

[056-047] lodgings.”

[056-048] Having left their luggage and his tools at the station they proceeded

[056-049] on foot up the familiar street, the holiday people all drifting in the

[056-050] same direction. Reaching the Fourways they were about to turn off to

[056-051] where accommodation was likely to be found when, looking at the clock

[056-052] and the hurrying crowd, Jude said: “Let us go and see the procession,

[056-053] and never mind the lodgings just now. We can get them afterwards.”

[056-054] “Oughtn’t we to get a house over our heads first?” she asked.

[056-055] But his soul seemed full of the anniversary, and together they went

[056-056] down Chief Street, their smallest child in Jude’s arms, Sue leading her

[056-057] little girl, and Arabella’s boy walking thoughtfully and silently

[056-058] beside them. Crowds of pretty sisters in airy costumes, and meekly

[056-059] ignorant parents who had known no college in their youth, were under

[056-060] convoy in the same direction by brothers and sons bearing the opinion

[056-061] written large on them that no properly qualified human beings had lived

[056-062] on earth till they came to grace it here and now.

[056-063] “My failure is reflected on me by every one of those young fellows,”

[056-064] said Jude. “A lesson on presumption is awaiting me to-day!—Humiliation

[056-065] Day for me! … If you, my dear darling, hadn’t come to my rescue, I

[056-066] should have gone to the dogs with despair!”

[056-067] She saw from his face that he was getting into one of his tempestuous,

[056-068] self-harrowing moods. “It would have been better if we had gone at once

[056-069] about our own affairs, dear,” she answered. “I am sure this sight will

[056-070] awaken old sorrows in you, and do no good!”

[056-071] “Well—we are near; we will see it now,” said he.

[056-072] They turned in on the left by the church with the Italian porch, whose

[056-073] helical columns were heavily draped with creepers, and pursued the lane

[056-074] till there arose on Jude’s sight the circular theatre with that

[056-075] well-known lantern above it, which stood in his mind as the sad symbol

[056-076] of his abandoned hopes, for it was from that outlook that he had

[056-077] finally surveyed the City of Colleges on the afternoon of his great

[056-078] meditation, which convinced him at last of the futility of his attempt

[056-079] to be a son of the university.

[056-080] To-day, in the open space stretching between this building and the

[056-081] nearest college, stood a crowd of expectant people. A passage was kept

[056-082] clear through their midst by two barriers of timber, extending from the

[056-083] door of the college to the door of the large building between it and

[056-084] the theatre.

[056-085] “Here is the place—they are just going to pass!” cried Jude in sudden

[056-086] excitement. And pushing his way to the front he took up a position

[056-087] close to the barrier, still hugging the youngest child in his arms,

[056-088] while Sue and the others kept immediately behind him. The crowd filled

[056-089] in at their back, and fell to talking, joking, and laughing as carriage

[056-090] after carriage drew up at the lower door of the college, and solemn

[056-091] stately figures in blood-red robes began to alight. The sky had grown

[056-092] overcast and livid, and thunder rumbled now and then.

[056-093] Father Time shuddered. “It do seem like the Judgment Day!” he

[056-094] whispered.

[056-095] “They are only learned Doctors,” said Sue.

[056-096] While they waited big drops of rain fell on their heads and shoulders,

[056-097] and the delay grew tedious. Sue again wished not to stay.

[056-098] “They won’t be long now,” said Jude, without turning his head.

[056-099] But the procession did not come forth, and somebody in the crowd, to

[056-100] pass the time, looked at the façade of the nearest college, and said he

[056-101] wondered what was meant by the Latin inscription in its midst. Jude,

[056-102] who stood near the inquirer, explained it, and finding that the people

[056-103] all round him were listening with interest, went on to describe the

[056-104] carving of the frieze (which he had studied years before), and to

[056-105] criticize some details of masonry in other college fronts about the

[056-106] city.

[056-107] The idle crowd, including the two policemen at the doors, stared like

[056-108] the Lycaonians at Paul, for Jude was apt to get too enthusiastic over

[056-109] any subject in hand, and they seemed to wonder how the stranger should

[056-110] know more about the buildings of their town than they themselves did;

[056-111] till one of them said: “Why, I know that man; he used to work here

[056-112] years ago—Jude Fawley, that’s his name! Don’t you mind he used to be

[056-113] nicknamed Tutor of St. Slums, d’ye mind?—because he aimed at that line

[056-114] o’ business? He’s married, I suppose, then, and that’s his child he’s

[056-115] carrying. Taylor would know him, as he knows everybody.”

[056-116] The speaker was a man named Jack Stagg, with whom Jude had formerly

[056-117] worked in repairing the college masonries; Tinker Taylor was seen to be

[056-118] standing near. Having his attention called the latter cried across the

[056-119] barriers to Jude: “You’ve honoured us by coming back again, my friend!”

[056-120] Jude nodded.

[056-121] “An’ you don’t seem to have done any great things for yourself by going

[056-122] away?”

[056-123] Jude assented to this also.

[056-124] “Except found more mouths to fill!” This came in a new voice, and Jude

[056-125] recognized its owner to be Uncle Joe, another mason whom he had known.

[056-126] Jude replied good-humouredly that he could not dispute it; and from

[056-127] remark to remark something like a general conversation arose between

[056-128] him and the crowd of idlers, during which Tinker Taylor asked Jude if

[056-129] he remembered the Apostles’ Creed in Latin still, and the night of the

[056-130] challenge in the public house.

[056-131] “But Fortune didn’t lie that way?” threw in Joe. “Yer powers wasn’t

[056-132] enough to carry ’ee through?”

[056-133] “Don’t answer them any more!” entreated Sue.

[056-134] “I don’t think I like Christminster!” murmured little Time mournfully,

[056-135] as he stood submerged and invisible in the crowd.

[056-136] But finding himself the centre of curiosity, quizzing, and comment,

[056-137] Jude was not inclined to shrink from open declarations of what he had

[056-138] no great reason to be ashamed of; and in a little while was stimulated

[056-139] to say in a loud voice to the listening throng generally:

[056-140] “It is a difficult question, my friends, for any young man—that

[056-141] question I had to grapple with, and which thousands are weighing at the

[056-142] present moment in these uprising times—whether to follow uncritically

[056-143] the track he finds himself in, without considering his aptness for it,

[056-144] or to consider what his aptness or bent may be, and re-shape his course

[056-145] accordingly. I tried to do the latter, and I failed. But I don’t admit

[056-146] that my failure proved my view to be a wrong one, or that my success

[056-147] would have made it a right one; though that’s how we appraise such

[056-148] attempts nowadays—I mean, not by their essential soundness, but by

[056-149] their accidental outcomes. If I had ended by becoming like one of these

[056-150] gentlemen in red and black that we saw dropping in here by now,

[056-151] everybody would have said: ‘See how wise that young man was, to follow

[056-152] the bent of his nature!’ But having ended no better than I began they

[056-153] say: ‘See what a fool that fellow was in following a freak of his

[056-154] fancy!’

[056-155] “However it was my poverty and not my will that consented to be beaten.

[056-156] It takes two or three generations to do what I tried to do in one; and

[056-157] my impulses—affections—vices perhaps they should be called—were too

[056-158] strong not to hamper a man without advantages; who should be as

[056-159] cold-blooded as a fish and as selfish as a pig to have a really good

[056-160] chance of being one of his country’s worthies. You may ridicule me—I am

[056-161] quite willing that you should—I am a fit subject, no doubt. But I think

[056-162] if you knew what I have gone through these last few years you would

[056-163] rather pity me. And if they knew”—he nodded towards the college at

[056-164] which the dons were severally arriving—“it is just possible they would

[056-165] do the same.”

[056-166] “He do look ill and worn-out, it is true!” said a woman.

[056-167] Sue’s face grew more emotional; but though she stood close to Jude she

[056-168] was screened.

[056-169] “I may do some good before I am dead—be a sort of success as a

[056-170] frightful example of what not to do; and so illustrate a moral story,”

[056-171] continued Jude, beginning to grow bitter, though he had opened serenely

[056-172] enough. “I was, perhaps, after all, a paltry victim to the spirit of

[056-173] mental and social restlessness that makes so many unhappy in these

[056-174] days!”

[056-175] “Don’t tell them that!” whispered Sue with tears, at perceiving Jude’s

[056-176] state of mind. “You weren’t that. You struggled nobly to acquire

[056-177] knowledge, and only the meanest souls in the world would blame you!”

[056-178] Jude shifted the child into a more easy position on his arm, and

[056-179] concluded: “And what I appear, a sick and poor man, is not the worst of

[056-180] me. I am in a chaos of principles—groping in the dark—acting by

[056-181] instinct and not after example. Eight or nine years ago when I came

[056-182] here first, I had a neat stock of fixed opinions, but they dropped away

[056-183] one by one; and the further I get the less sure I am. I doubt if I have

[056-184] anything more for my present rule of life than following inclinations

[056-185] which do me and nobody else any harm, and actually give pleasure to

[056-186] those I love best. There, gentlemen, since you wanted to know how I was

[056-187] getting on, I have told you. Much good may it do you! I cannot explain

[056-188] further here. I perceive there is something wrong somewhere in our

[056-189] social formulas: what it is can only be discovered by men or women with

[056-190] greater insight than mine—if, indeed, they ever discover it—at least in

[056-191] our time. ‘For who knoweth what is good for man in this life?—and who

[056-192] can tell a man what shall be after him under the sun?’”
