[037-001] “I have only been married a month or two!” she went on, still remaining

[037-002] bent upon the table, and sobbing into her hands. “And it is said that

[037-003] what a woman shrinks from—in the early days of her marriage—she shakes

[037-004] down to with comfortable indifference in half a dozen years. But that

[037-005] is much like saying that the amputation of a limb is no affliction,

[037-006] since a person gets comfortably accustomed to the use of a wooden leg

[037-007] or arm in the course of time!”

[037-008] Jude could hardly speak, but he said, “I thought there was something

[037-009] wrong, Sue! Oh, I thought there was!”

[037-010] “But it is not as you think!—there is nothing wrong except my own

[037-011] wickedness, I suppose you’d call it—a repugnance on my part, for a

[037-012] reason I cannot disclose, and what would not be admitted as one by the

[037-013] world in general! … What tortures me so much is the necessity of being

[037-014] responsive to this man whenever he wishes, good as he is morally!—the

[037-015] dreadful contract to feel in a particular way in a matter whose essence

[037-016] is its voluntariness! … I wish he would beat me, or be faithless to me,

[037-017] or do some open thing that I could talk about as a justification for

[037-018] feeling as I do! But he does nothing, except that he has grown a little

[037-019] cold since he has found out how I feel. That’s why he didn’t come to

[037-020] the funeral… Oh, I am very miserable—I don’t know what to do! … Don’t

[037-021] come near me, Jude, because you mustn’t. Don’t—don’t!”

[037-022] But he had jumped up and put his face against hers—or rather against

[037-023] her ear, her face being inaccessible.

[037-024] “I told you not to, Jude!”

[037-025] “I know you did—I only wish to—console you! It all arose through my

[037-026] being married before we met, didn’t it? You would have been my wife,

[037-027] Sue, wouldn’t you, if it hadn’t been for that?”

[037-028] Instead of replying she rose quickly, and saying she was going to walk

[037-029] to her aunt’s grave in the churchyard to recover herself, went out of

[037-030] the house. Jude did not follow her. Twenty minutes later he saw her

[037-031] cross the village green towards Mrs. Edlin’s, and soon she sent a

[037-032] little girl to fetch her bag, and tell him she was too tired to see him

[037-033] again that night.

[037-034] In the lonely room of his aunt’s house, Jude sat watching the cottage

[037-035] of the Widow Edlin as it disappeared behind the night shade. He knew

[037-036] that Sue was sitting within its walls equally lonely and disheartened;

[037-037] and again questioned his devotional motto that all was for the best.

[037-038] He retired to rest early, but his sleep was fitful from the sense that

[037-039] Sue was so near at hand. At some time near two o’clock, when he was

[037-040] beginning to sleep more soundly, he was aroused by a shrill squeak that

[037-041] had been familiar enough to him when he lived regularly at Marygreen.

[037-042] It was the cry of a rabbit caught in a gin. As was the little

[037-043] creature’s habit, it did not soon repeat its cry; and probably would

[037-044] not do so more than once or twice; but would remain bearing its torture

[037-045] till the morrow when the trapper would come and knock it on the head.

[037-046] He who in his childhood had saved the lives of the earthworms now began

[037-047] to picture the agonies of the rabbit from its lacerated leg. If it were

[037-048] a “bad catch” by the hind-leg, the animal would tug during the ensuing

[037-049] six hours till the iron teeth of the trap had stripped the leg-bone of

[037-050] its flesh, when, should a weak-springed instrument enable it to escape,

[037-051] it would die in the fields from the mortification of the limb. If it

[037-052] were a “good catch,” namely, by the fore-leg, the bone would be broken

[037-053] and the limb nearly torn in two in attempts at an impossible escape.

[037-054] Almost half an hour passed, and the rabbit repeated its cry. Jude could

[037-055] rest no longer till he had put it out of its pain, so dressing himself

[037-056] quickly he descended, and by the light of the moon went across the

[037-057] green in the direction of the sound. He reached the hedge bordering the

[037-058] widow’s garden, when he stood still. The faint click of the trap as

[037-059] dragged about by the writhing animal guided him now, and reaching the

[037-060] spot he struck the rabbit on the back of the neck with the side of his

[037-061] palm, and it stretched itself out dead.

[037-062] He was turning away when he saw a woman looking out of the open

[037-063] casement at a window on the ground floor of the adjacent cottage.

[037-064] “Jude!” said a voice timidly—Sue’s voice. “It is you—is it not?”

[037-065] “Yes, dear!”

[037-066] “I haven’t been able to sleep at all, and then I heard the rabbit, and

[037-067] couldn’t help thinking of what it suffered, till I felt I must come

[037-068] down and kill it! But I am so glad you got there first… They ought not

[037-069] to be allowed to set these steel traps, ought they!”

[037-070] Jude had reached the window, which was quite a low one, so that she was

[037-071] visible down to her waist. She let go the casement-stay and put her

[037-072] hand upon his, her moonlit face regarding him wistfully.

[037-073] “Did it keep you awake?” he said.

[037-074] “No—I was awake.”

[037-075] “How was that?”

[037-076] “Oh, you know—now! I know you, with your religious doctrines, think

[037-077] that a married woman in trouble of a kind like mine commits a mortal

[037-078] sin in making a man the confidant of it, as I did you. I wish I hadn’t,

[037-079] now!”

[037-080] “Don’t wish it, dear,” he said. “That may have _been_ my view; but my

[037-081] doctrines and I begin to part company.”

[037-082] “I knew it—I knew it! And that’s why I vowed I wouldn’t disturb your

[037-083] belief. But—I am _so glad_ to see you!—and, oh, I didn’t mean to see

[037-084] you again, now the last tie between us, Aunt Drusilla, is dead!”

[037-085] Jude seized her hand and kissed it. “There is a stronger one left!” he

[037-086] said. “I’ll never care about my doctrines or my religion any more! Let

[037-087] them go! Let me help you, even if I do love you, and even if you…”

[037-088] “Don’t say it!—I know what you mean; but I can’t admit so much as that.

[037-089] There! Guess what you like, but don’t press me to answer questions!”

[037-090] “I wish you were happy, whatever I may be!”

[037-091] “I _can’t_ be! So few could enter into my feeling—they would say ’twas

[037-092] my fanciful fastidiousness, or something of that sort, and condemn me…

[037-093] It is none of the natural tragedies of love that’s love’s usual tragedy

[037-094] in civilized life, but a tragedy artificially manufactured for people

[037-095] who in a natural state would find relief in parting! … It would have

[037-096] been wrong, perhaps, for me to tell my distress to you, if I had been

[037-097] able to tell it to anybody else. But I have nobody. And I _must_ tell

[037-098] somebody! Jude, before I married him I had never thought out fully what

[037-099] marriage meant, even though I knew. It was idiotic of me—there is no

[037-100] excuse. I was old enough, and I thought I was very experienced. So I

[037-101] rushed on, when I had got into that training school scrape, with all

[037-102] the cock-sureness of the fool that I was! … I am certain one ought to

[037-103] be allowed to undo what one had done so ignorantly! I daresay it

[037-104] happens to lots of women, only they submit, and I kick… When people of

[037-105] a later age look back upon the barbarous customs and superstitions of

[037-106] the times that we have the unhappiness to live in, what _will_ they

[037-107] say!”

[037-108] “You are very bitter, darling Sue! How I wish—I wish—”

[037-109] “You must go in now!”

[037-110] In a moment of impulse she bent over the sill, and laid her face upon

[037-111] his hair, weeping, and then imprinting a scarcely perceptible little

[037-112] kiss upon the top of his head, withdrawing quickly, so that he could

[037-113] not put his arms round her, as otherwise he unquestionably would have

[037-114] done. She shut the casement, and he returned to his cottage.

[037-115] III

[037-116] Sue’s distressful confession recurred to Jude’s mind all the night as

[037-117] being a sorrow indeed.

[037-118] The morning after, when it was time for her to go, the neighbours saw

[037-119] her companion and herself disappearing on foot down the hill path which

[037-120] led into the lonely road to Alfredston. An hour passed before he

[037-121] returned along the same route, and in his face there was a look of

[037-122] exaltation not unmixed with recklessness. An incident had occurred.

[037-123] They had stood parting in the silent highway, and their tense and

[037-124] passionate moods had led to bewildered inquiries of each other on how

[037-125] far their intimacy ought to go; till they had almost quarrelled, and

[037-126] she said tearfully that it was hardly proper of him as a parson in

[037-127] embryo to think of such a thing as kissing her even in farewell as he

[037-128] now wished to do. Then she had conceded that the fact of the kiss would

[037-129] be nothing: all would depend upon the spirit of it. If given in the

[037-130] spirit of a cousin and a friend she saw no objection: if in the spirit

[037-131] of a lover she could not permit it. “Will you swear that it will not be

[037-132] in that spirit?” she had said.

[037-133] No: he would not. And then they had turned from each other in

[037-134] estrangement, and gone their several ways, till at a distance of twenty

[037-135] or thirty yards both had looked round simultaneously. That look behind

[037-136] was fatal to the reserve hitherto more or less maintained. They had

[037-137] quickly run back, and met, and embracing most unpremeditatedly, kissed

[037-138] close and long. When they parted for good it was with flushed cheeks on

[037-139] her side, and a beating heart on his.

[037-140] The kiss was a turning-point in Jude’s career. Back again in the

[037-141] cottage, and left to reflection, he saw one thing: that though his kiss

[037-142] of that aerial being had seemed the purest moment of his faultful life,

[037-143] as long as he nourished this unlicensed tenderness it was glaringly

[037-144] inconsistent for him to pursue the idea of becoming the soldier and

[037-145] servant of a religion in which sexual love was regarded as at its best

[037-146] a frailty, and at its worst damnation. What Sue had said in warmth was

[037-147] really the cold truth. When to defend his affection tooth and nail, to

[037-148] persist with headlong force in impassioned attentions to her, was all

[037-149] he thought of, he was condemned _ipso facto_ as a professor of the

[037-150] accepted school of morals. He was as unfit, obviously, by nature, as he

[037-151] had been by social position, to fill the part of a propounder of

[037-152] accredited dogma.

[037-153] Strange that his first aspiration—towards academical proficiency—had

[037-154] been checked by a woman, and that his second aspiration—towards

[037-155] apostleship—had also been checked by a woman. “Is it,” he said, “that

[037-156] the women are to blame; or is it the artificial system of things, under

[037-157] which the normal sex-impulses are turned into devilish domestic gins

[037-158] and springs to noose and hold back those who want to progress?”

[037-159] It had been his standing desire to become a prophet, however humble, to

[037-160] his struggling fellow-creatures, without any thought of personal gain.

[037-161] Yet with a wife living away from him with another husband, and himself

[037-162] in love erratically, the loved one’s revolt against her state being

[037-163] possibly on his account, he had sunk to be barely respectable according

[037-164] to regulation views.

[037-165] It was not for him to consider further: he had only to confront the

[037-166] obvious, which was that he had made himself quite an impostor as a

[037-167] law-abiding religious teacher.

[037-168] At dusk that evening he went into the garden and dug a shallow hole, to

[037-169] which he brought out all the theological and ethical works that he

[037-170] possessed, and had stored here. He knew that, in this country of true

[037-171] believers, most of them were not saleable at a much higher price than

[037-172] waste-paper value, and preferred to get rid of them in his own way,

[037-173] even if he should sacrifice a little money to the sentiment of thus

[037-174] destroying them. Lighting some loose pamphlets to begin with, he cut

[037-175] the volumes into pieces as well as he could, and with a three-pronged

[037-176] fork shook them over the flames. They kindled, and lighted up the back

[037-177] of the house, the pigsty, and his own face, till they were more or less

[037-178] consumed.
