[003-001] If he could only prevent himself growing up! He did not want to be a

[003-002] man.

[003-003] Then, like the natural boy, he forgot his despondency, and sprang up.

[003-004] During the remainder of the morning he helped his aunt, and in the

[003-005] afternoon, when there was nothing more to be done, he went into the

[003-006] village. Here he asked a man whereabouts Christminster lay.

[003-007] “Christminster? Oh, well, out by there yonder; though I’ve never bin

[003-008] there—not I. I’ve never had any business at such a place.”

[003-009] The man pointed north-eastward, in the very direction where lay that

[003-010] field in which Jude had so disgraced himself. There was something

[003-011] unpleasant about the coincidence for the moment, but the fearsomeness

[003-012] of this fact rather increased his curiosity about the city. The farmer

[003-013] had said he was never to be seen in that field again; yet Christminster

[003-014] lay across it, and the path was a public one. So, stealing out of the

[003-015] hamlet, he descended into the same hollow which had witnessed his

[003-016] punishment in the morning, never swerving an inch from the path, and

[003-017] climbing up the long and tedious ascent on the other side till the

[003-018] track joined the highway by a little clump of trees. Here the ploughed

[003-019] land ended, and all before him was bleak open down.

[003-020] III

[003-021] Not a soul was visible on the hedgeless highway, or on either side of

[003-022] it, and the white road seemed to ascend and diminish till it joined the

[003-023] sky. At the very top it was crossed at right angles by a green

[003-024] “ridgeway”—the Ickneild Street and original Roman road through the

[003-025] district. This ancient track ran east and west for many miles, and down

[003-026] almost to within living memory had been used for driving flocks and

[003-027] herds to fairs and markets. But it was now neglected and overgrown.

[003-028] The boy had never before strayed so far north as this from the nestling

[003-029] hamlet in which he had been deposited by the carrier from a railway

[003-030] station southward, one dark evening some few months earlier, and till

[003-031] now he had had no suspicion that such a wide, flat, low-lying country

[003-032] lay so near at hand, under the very verge of his upland world. The

[003-033] whole northern semicircle between east and west, to a distance of forty

[003-034] or fifty miles, spread itself before him; a bluer, moister atmosphere,

[003-035] evidently, than that he breathed up here.

[003-036] Not far from the road stood a weather-beaten old barn of reddish-grey

[003-037] brick and tile. It was known as the Brown House by the people of the

[003-038] locality. He was about to pass it when he perceived a ladder against

[003-039] the eaves; and the reflection that the higher he got, the further he

[003-040] could see, led Jude to stand and regard it. On the slope of the roof

[003-041] two men were repairing the tiling. He turned into the ridgeway and drew

[003-042] towards the barn.

[003-043] When he had wistfully watched the workmen for some time he took

[003-044] courage, and ascended the ladder till he stood beside them.

[003-045] “Well, my lad, and what may you want up here?”

[003-046] “I wanted to know where the city of Christminster is, if you please.”

[003-047] “Christminster is out across there, by that clump. You can see it—at

[003-048] least you can on a clear day. Ah, no, you can’t now.”

[003-049] The other tiler, glad of any kind of diversion from the monotony of his

[003-050] labour, had also turned to look towards the quarter designated. “You

[003-051] can’t often see it in weather like this,” he said. “The time I’ve

[003-052] noticed it is when the sun is going down in a blaze of flame, and it

[003-053] looks like—I don’t know what.”

[003-054] “The heavenly Jerusalem,” suggested the serious urchin.

[003-055] “Ay—though I should never ha’ thought of it myself. … But I can’t see

[003-056] no Christminster to-day.”

[003-057] The boy strained his eyes also; yet neither could he see the far-off

[003-058] city. He descended from the barn, and abandoning Christminster with the

[003-059] versatility of his age he walked along the ridge-track, looking for any

[003-060] natural objects of interest that might lie in the banks thereabout.

[003-061] When he repassed the barn to go back to Marygreen he observed that the

[003-062] ladder was still in its place, but that the men had finished their

[003-063] day’s work and gone away.

[003-064] It was waning towards evening; there was still a faint mist, but it had

[003-065] cleared a little except in the damper tracts of subjacent country and

[003-066] along the river-courses. He thought again of Christminster, and wished,

[003-067] since he had come two or three miles from his aunt’s house on purpose,

[003-068] that he could have seen for once this attractive city of which he had

[003-069] been told. But even if he waited here it was hardly likely that the air

[003-070] would clear before night. Yet he was loth to leave the spot, for the

[003-071] northern expanse became lost to view on retreating towards the village

[003-072] only a few hundred yards.

[003-073] He ascended the ladder to have one more look at the point the men had

[003-074] designated, and perched himself on the highest rung, overlying the

[003-075] tiles. He might not be able to come so far as this for many days.

[003-076] Perhaps if he prayed, the wish to see Christminster might be forwarded.

[003-077] People said that, if you prayed, things sometimes came to you, even

[003-078] though they sometimes did not. He had read in a tract that a man who

[003-079] had begun to build a church, and had no money to finish it, knelt down

[003-080] and prayed, and the money came in by the next post. Another man tried

[003-081] the same experiment, and the money did not come; but he found

[003-082] afterwards that the breeches he knelt in were made by a wicked Jew.

[003-083] This was not discouraging, and turning on the ladder Jude knelt on the

[003-084] third rung, where, resting against those above it, he prayed that the

[003-085] mist might rise.

[003-086] He then seated himself again, and waited. In the course of ten or

[003-087] fifteen minutes the thinning mist dissolved altogether from the

[003-088] northern horizon, as it had already done elsewhere, and about a quarter

[003-089] of an hour before the time of sunset the westward clouds parted, the

[003-090] sun’s position being partially uncovered, and the beams streaming out

[003-091] in visible lines between two bars of slaty cloud. The boy immediately

[003-092] looked back in the old direction.

[003-093] Some way within the limits of the stretch of landscape, points of light

[003-094] like the topaz gleamed. The air increased in transparency with the

[003-095] lapse of minutes, till the topaz points showed themselves to be the

[003-096] vanes, windows, wet roof slates, and other shining spots upon the

[003-097] spires, domes, freestone-work, and varied outlines that were faintly

[003-098] revealed. It was Christminster, unquestionably; either directly seen,

[003-099] or miraged in the peculiar atmosphere.

[003-100] The spectator gazed on and on till the windows and vanes lost their

[003-101] shine, going out almost suddenly like extinguished candles. The vague

[003-102] city became veiled in mist. Turning to the west, he saw that the sun

[003-103] had disappeared. The foreground of the scene had grown funereally dark,

[003-104] and near objects put on the hues and shapes of chimaeras.

[003-105] He anxiously descended the ladder, and started homewards at a run,

[003-106] trying not to think of giants, Herne the Hunter, Apollyon lying in wait

[003-107] for Christian, or of the captain with the bleeding hole in his forehead

[003-108] and the corpses round him that remutinied every night on board the

[003-109] bewitched ship. He knew that he had grown out of belief in these

[003-110] horrors, yet he was glad when he saw the church tower and the lights in

[003-111] the cottage windows, even though this was not the home of his birth,

[003-112] and his great-aunt did not care much about him.

[003-113] Inside and round about that old woman’s “shop” window, with its

[003-114] twenty-four little panes set in lead-work, the glass of some of them

[003-115] oxidized with age, so that you could hardly see the poor penny articles

[003-116] exhibited within, and forming part of a stock which a strong man could

[003-117] have carried, Jude had his outer being for some long tideless time. But

[003-118] his dreams were as gigantic as his surroundings were small.

[003-119] Through the solid barrier of cold cretaceous upland to the northward he

[003-120] was always beholding a gorgeous city—the fancied place he had likened

[003-121] to the new Jerusalem, though there was perhaps more of the painter’s

[003-122] imagination and less of the diamond merchant’s in his dreams thereof

[003-123] than in those of the Apocalyptic writer. And the city acquired a

[003-124] tangibility, a permanence, a hold on his life, mainly from the one

[003-125] nucleus of fact that the man for whose knowledge and purposes he had so

[003-126] much reverence was actually living there; not only so, but living among

[003-127] the more thoughtful and mentally shining ones therein.

[003-128] In sad wet seasons, though he knew it must rain at Christminster too,

[003-129] he could hardly believe that it rained so drearily there. Whenever he

[003-130] could get away from the confines of the hamlet for an hour or two,

[003-131] which was not often, he would steal off to the Brown House on the hill

[003-132] and strain his eyes persistently; sometimes to be rewarded by the sight

[003-133] of a dome or spire, at other times by a little smoke, which in his

[003-134] estimate had some of the mysticism of incense.

[003-135] Then the day came when it suddenly occurred to him that if he ascended

[003-136] to the point of view after dark, or possibly went a mile or two

[003-137] further, he would see the night lights of the city. It would be

[003-138] necessary to come back alone, but even that consideration did not deter

[003-139] him, for he could throw a little manliness into his mood, no doubt.

[003-140] The project was duly executed. It was not late when he arrived at the

[003-141] place of outlook, only just after dusk, but a black north-east sky,

[003-142] accompanied by a wind from the same quarter, made the occasion dark

[003-143] enough. He was rewarded; but what he saw was not the lamps in rows, as

[003-144] he had half expected. No individual light was visible, only a halo or

[003-145] glow-fog over-arching the place against the black heavens behind it,

[003-146] making the light and the city seem distant but a mile or so.

[003-147] He set himself to wonder on the exact point in the glow where the

[003-148] schoolmaster might be—he who never communicated with anybody at

[003-149] Marygreen now; who was as if dead to them here. In the glow he seemed

[003-150] to see Phillotson promenading at ease, like one of the forms in

[003-151] Nebuchadnezzar’s furnace.

[003-152] He had heard that breezes travelled at the rate of ten miles an hour,

[003-153] and the fact now came into his mind. He parted his lips as he faced the

[003-154] north-east, and drew in the wind as if it were a sweet liquor.

[003-155] “You,” he said, addressing the breeze caressingly “were in

[003-156] Christminster city between one and two hours ago, floating along the

[003-157] streets, pulling round the weather-cocks, touching Mr. Phillotson’s

[003-158] face, being breathed by him; and now you are here, breathed by me—you,

[003-159] the very same.”

[003-160] Suddenly there came along this wind something towards him—a message

[003-161] from the place—from some soul residing there, it seemed. Surely it was

[003-162] the sound of bells, the voice of the city, faint and musical, calling

[003-163] to him, “We are happy here!”

[003-164] He had become entirely lost to his bodily situation during this mental

[003-165] leap, and only got back to it by a rough recalling. A few yards below

[003-166] the brow of the hill on which he paused a team of horses made its

[003-167] appearance, having reached the place by dint of half an hour’s

[003-168] serpentine progress from the bottom of the immense declivity. They had

[003-169] a load of coals behind them—a fuel that could only be got into the

[003-170] upland by this particular route. They were accompanied by a carter, a

[003-171] second man, and a boy, who now kicked a large stone behind one of the

[003-172] wheels, and allowed the panting animals to have a long rest, while

[003-173] those in charge took a flagon off the load and indulged in a drink

[003-174] round.
