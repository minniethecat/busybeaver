[051-001] The pair with their charge passed through the turnstiles, Arabella and

[051-002] her husband not far behind them. When inside the enclosure the

[051-003] publican’s wife could see that the two ahead began to take trouble with

[051-004] the youngster, pointing out and explaining the many objects of

[051-005] interest, alive and dead; and a passing sadness would touch their faces

[051-006] at their every failure to disturb his indifference.

[051-007] “How she sticks to him!” said Arabella. “Oh no—I fancy they are not

[051-008] married, or they wouldn’t be so much to one another as that… I wonder!”

[051-009] “But I thought you said he did marry her?”

[051-010] “I heard he was going to—that’s all, going to make another attempt,

[051-011] after putting it off once or twice… As far as they themselves are

[051-012] concerned they are the only two in the show. I should be ashamed of

[051-013] making myself so silly if I were he!”

[051-014] “I don’t see as how there’s anything remarkable in their behaviour. I

[051-015] should never have noticed their being in love, if you hadn’t said so.”

[051-016] “You never see anything,” she rejoined. Nevertheless Cartlett’s view of

[051-017] the lovers’ or married pair’s conduct was undoubtedly that of the

[051-018] general crowd, whose attention seemed to be in no way attracted by what

[051-019] Arabella’s sharpened vision discerned.

[051-020] “He’s charmed by her as if she were some fairy!” continued Arabella.

[051-021] “See how he looks round at her, and lets his eyes rest on her. I am

[051-022] inclined to think that she don’t care for him quite so much as he does

[051-023] for her. She’s not a particular warm-hearted creature to my thinking,

[051-024] though she cares for him pretty middling much—as much as she’s able to;

[051-025] and he could make her heart ache a bit if he liked to try—which he’s

[051-026] too simple to do. There—now they are going across to the cart-horse

[051-027] sheds. Come along.”

[051-028] “I don’t want to see the cart-horses. It is no business of ours to

[051-029] follow these two. If we have come to see the show let us see it in our

[051-030] own way, as they do in theirs.”

[051-031] “Well—suppose we agree to meet somewhere in an hour’s time—say at that

[051-032] refreshment tent over there, and go about independent? Then you can

[051-033] look at what you choose to, and so can I.”

[051-034] Cartlett was not loath to agree to this, and they parted—he proceeding

[051-035] to the shed where malting processes were being exhibited, and Arabella

[051-036] in the direction taken by Jude and Sue. Before, however, she had

[051-037] regained their wake a laughing face met her own, and she was confronted

[051-038] by Anny, the friend of her girlhood.

[051-039] Anny had burst out in hearty laughter at the mere fact of the chance

[051-040] encounter. “I am still living down there,” she said, as soon as she was

[051-041] composed. “I am soon going to be married, but my intended couldn’t come

[051-042] up here to-day. But there’s lots of us come by excursion, though I’ve

[051-043] lost the rest of ’em for the present.”

[051-044] “Have you met Jude and his young woman, or wife, or whatever she is? I

[051-045] saw ’em by now.”

[051-046] “No. Not a glimpse of un for years!”

[051-047] “Well, they are close by here somewhere. Yes—there they are—by that

[051-048] grey horse!”

[051-049] “Oh, that’s his present young woman—wife did you say? Has he married

[051-050] again?”

[051-051] “I don’t know.”

[051-052] “She’s pretty, isn’t she!”

[051-053] “Yes—nothing to complain of; or jump at. Not much to depend on, though;

[051-054] a slim, fidgety little thing like that.”

[051-055] “He’s a nice-looking chap, too! You ought to ha’ stuck to un,

[051-056] Arabella.”

[051-057] “I don’t know but I ought,” murmured she.

[051-058] Anny laughed. “That’s you, Arabella! Always wanting another man than

[051-059] your own.”

[051-060] “Well, and what woman don’t I should like to know? As for that body

[051-061] with him—she don’t know what love is—at least what I call love! I can

[051-062] see in her face she don’t.”

[051-063] “And perhaps, Abby dear, you don’t know what she calls love.”

[051-064] “I’m sure I don’t wish to! … Ah—they are making for the art department.

[051-065] I should like to see some pictures myself. Suppose we go that way?—

[051-066] Why, if all Wessex isn’t here, I verily believe! There’s Dr. Vilbert.

[051-067] Haven’t seen him for years, and he’s not looking a day older than when

[051-068] I used to know him. How do you do, Physician? I was just saying that

[051-069] you don’t look a day older than when you knew me as a girl.”

[051-070] “Simply the result of taking my own pills regular, ma’am. Only two and

[051-071] threepence a box—warranted efficacious by the Government stamp. Now let

[051-072] me advise you to purchase the same immunity from the ravages of time by

[051-073] following my example? Only two-and-three.”

[051-074] The physician had produced a box from his waistcoat pocket, and

[051-075] Arabella was induced to make the purchase.

[051-076] “At the same time,” continued he, when the pills were paid for, “you

[051-077] have the advantage of me, Mrs.— Surely not Mrs. Fawley, once Miss Donn,

[051-078] of the vicinity of Marygreen?”

[051-079] “Yes. But Mrs. Cartlett now.”

[051-080] “Ah—you lost him, then? Promising young fellow! A pupil of mine, you

[051-081] know. I taught him the dead languages. And believe me, he soon knew

[051-082] nearly as much as I.”

[051-083] “I lost him; but not as you think,” said Arabella dryly. “The lawyers

[051-084] untied us. There he is, look, alive and lusty; along with that young

[051-085] woman, entering the art exhibition.”

[051-086] “Ah—dear me! Fond of her, apparently.”

[051-087] “They _say_ they are cousins.”

[051-088] “Cousinship is a great convenience to their feelings, I should say?”

[051-089] “Yes. So her husband thought, no doubt, when he divorced her… Shall we

[051-090] look at the pictures, too?”

[051-091] The trio followed across the green and entered. Jude and Sue, with the

[051-092] child, unaware of the interest they were exciting, had gone up to a

[051-093] model at one end of the building, which they regarded with considerable

[051-094] attention for a long while before they went on. Arabella and her

[051-095] friends came to it in due course, and the inscription it bore was:

[051-096] “Model of Cardinal College, Christminster; by J. Fawley and S. F. M.

[051-097] Bridehead.”

[051-098] “Admiring their own work,” said Arabella. “How like Jude—always

[051-099] thinking of colleges and Christminster, instead of attending to his

[051-100] business!”

[051-101] They glanced cursorily at the pictures, and proceeded to the

[051-102] band-stand. When they had stood a little while listening to the music

[051-103] of the military performers, Jude, Sue, and the child came up on the

[051-104] other side. Arabella did not care if they should recognize her; but

[051-105] they were too deeply absorbed in their own lives, as translated into

[051-106] emotion by the military band, to perceive her under her beaded veil.

[051-107] She walked round the outside of the listening throng, passing behind

[051-108] the lovers, whose movements had an unexpected fascination for her

[051-109] to-day. Scrutinizing them narrowly from the rear she noticed that

[051-110] Jude’s hand sought Sue’s as they stood, the two standing close together

[051-111] so as to conceal, as they supposed, this tacit expression of their

[051-112] mutual responsiveness.

[051-113] “Silly fools—like two children!” Arabella whispered to herself

[051-114] morosely, as she rejoined her companions, with whom she preserved a

[051-115] preoccupied silence.

[051-116] Anny meanwhile had jokingly remarked to Vilbert on Arabella’s hankering

[051-117] interest in her first husband.

[051-118] “Now,” said the physician to Arabella, apart; “do you want anything

[051-119] such as this, Mrs. Cartlett? It is not compounded out of my regular

[051-120] pharmacopœia, but I am sometimes asked for such a thing.” He produced a

[051-121] small phial of clear liquid. “A love-philtre, such as was used by the

[051-122] ancients with great effect. I found it out by study of their writings,

[051-123] and have never known it to fail.”

[051-124] “What is it made of?” asked Arabella curiously.

[051-125] “Well—a distillation of the juices of doves’ hearts—otherwise

[051-126] pigeons’—is one of the ingredients. It took nearly a hundred hearts to

[051-127] produce that small bottle full.”

[051-128] “How do you get pigeons enough?”

[051-129] “To tell a secret, I get a piece of rock-salt, of which pigeons are

[051-130] inordinately fond, and place it in a dovecot on my roof. In a few hours

[051-131] the birds come to it from all points of the compass—east, west, north,

[051-132] and south—and thus I secure as many as I require. You use the liquid by

[051-133] contriving that the desired man shall take about ten drops of it in his

[051-134] drink. But remember, all this is told you because I gather from your

[051-135] questions that you mean to be a purchaser. You must keep faith with

[051-136] me?”

[051-137] “Very well—I don’t mind a bottle—to give some friend or other to try it

[051-138] on her young man.” She produced five shillings, the price asked, and

[051-139] slipped the phial in her capacious bosom. Saying presently that she was

[051-140] due at an appointment with her husband, she sauntered away towards the

[051-141] refreshment bar, Jude, his companion, and the child having gone on to

[051-142] the horticultural tent, where Arabella caught a glimpse of them

[051-143] standing before a group of roses in bloom.

[051-144] She waited a few minutes observing them, and then proceeded to join her

[051-145] spouse with no very amiable sentiments. She found him seated on a stool

[051-146] by the bar, talking to one of the gaily dressed maids who had served

[051-147] him with spirits.

[051-148] “I should think you had enough of this business at home!” Arabella

[051-149] remarked gloomily. “Surely you didn’t come fifty miles from your own

[051-150] bar to stick in another? Come, take me round the show, as other men do

[051-151] their wives! Dammy, one would think you were a young bachelor, with

[051-152] nobody to look after but yourself!”

[051-153] “But we agreed to meet here; and what could I do but wait?”

[051-154] “Well, now we have met, come along,” she returned, ready to quarrel

[051-155] with the sun for shining on her. And they left the tent together, this

[051-156] pot-bellied man and florid woman, in the antipathetic, recriminatory

[051-157] mood of the average husband and wife of Christendom.

[051-158] In the meantime the more exceptional couple and the boy still lingered

[051-159] in the pavilion of flowers—an enchanted palace to their appreciative

[051-160] taste—Sue’s usually pale cheeks reflecting the pink of the tinted roses

[051-161] at which she gazed; for the gay sights, the air, the music, and the

[051-162] excitement of a day’s outing with Jude had quickened her blood and made

[051-163] her eyes sparkle with vivacity. She adored roses, and what Arabella had

[051-164] witnessed was Sue detaining Jude almost against his will while she

[051-165] learnt the names of this variety and that, and put her face within an

[051-166] inch of their blooms to smell them.

[051-167] “I should like to push my face quite into them—the dears!” she had

[051-168] said. “But I suppose it is against the rules to touch them—isn’t it,

[051-169] Jude?”

[051-170] “Yes, you baby,” said he: and then playfully gave her a little push, so

[051-171] that her nose went among the petals.

[051-172] “The policeman will be down on us, and I shall say it was my husband’s

[051-173] fault!”

[051-174] Then she looked up at him, and smiled in a way that told so much to

[051-175] Arabella.

[051-176] “Happy?” he murmured.

[051-177] She nodded.

[051-178] “Why? Because you have come to the great Wessex Agricultural Show—or

[051-179] because _we_ have come?”

[051-180] “You are always trying to make me confess to all sorts of absurdities.

[051-181] Because I am improving my mind, of course, by seeing all these

[051-182] steam-ploughs, and threshing-machines, and chaff-cutters, and cows, and

[051-183] pigs, and sheep.”

[051-184] Jude was quite content with a baffle from his ever evasive companion.

[051-185] But when he had forgotten that he had put the question, and because he

[051-186] no longer wished for an answer, she went on: “I feel that we have

[051-187] returned to Greek joyousness, and have blinded ourselves to sickness

[051-188] and sorrow, and have forgotten what twenty-five centuries have taught

[051-189] the race since their time, as one of your Christminster luminaries

[051-190] says… There is one immediate shadow, however—only one.” And she looked

[051-191] at the aged child, whom, though they had taken him to everything likely

[051-192] to attract a young intelligence, they had utterly failed to interest.

[051-193] He knew what they were saying and thinking. “I am very, very sorry,

[051-194] Father and Mother,” he said. “But please don’t mind!—I can’t help it. I

[051-195] should like the flowers very very much, if I didn’t keep on thinking

[051-196] they’d be all withered in a few days!”
