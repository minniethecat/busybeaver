# Wedding

The wedding ceremonies in *Jude the Obscure* are not celebrations but ominous thresholds — moments when characters cross into legal bonds that the novel presents as forms of imprisonment. Each wedding in the narrative marks a turning point away from possibility and toward tragedy.

## Background

In Victorian England, marriage was both a religious sacrament and a civil contract, governed by the Marriage Act of 1836 which permitted both church weddings and civil ceremonies at register offices. For the working-class characters of Hardy's novel, the form of the ceremony — church or civil — carried social significance. The novel's weddings take place in a social world where marriage is effectively permanent: divorce was practically unavailable to the poor, and remarriage after separation was legally impossible without an Act of Parliament.

## Key Weddings in the Novel

### Jude and Arabella (Part First)

Jude's first wedding is an act of entrapment. Arabella Donn, the daughter of a pig-farmer, feigns pregnancy to pressure Jude into marriage. The ceremony is described minimally — Hardy emphasizes not the ritual but the consequences. Jude, who had been walking toward Christminster and learning, is suddenly "Mrs. Jude Fawley was walking in the streets of Alfredston" — a domestic possession rather than a scholar (010-088).

This wedding establishes the novel's pattern: marriage as a trap that springs closed before the victim knows what has happened. Arabella soon departs for Australia, leaving Jude legally bound but practically abandoned.

### Sue and Phillotson (Part Third)

Sue's wedding to Phillotson is a marriage of gratitude and obligation, not love. Phillotson has lost his position at Shaston for allowing Sue to leave his house to live with Jude; Sue marries him partly from guilt and partly from a sense that she owes him recompense. The marriage is never consummated in any meaningful sense — Sue is physically repelled by Phillotson — but it is legally binding, and its existence blocks her union with Jude.

### Jude and Sue's Marriage Notice (Part Fifth)

Jude and Sue's decision to formalize their union — "Jude Fawley signed the form of notice, Sue looking over his shoulder" (048-171) — is the novel's most hopeful wedding scene, but it remains incomplete. Social pressure and Sue's ambivalence prevent the actual ceremony from taking place. Their common-law union, unsanctified by church or state, is the novel's experiment in love without institutional form.

### Sue's Remarriage to Phillotson and Jude's Remarriage to Arabella (Part Sixth)

The novel ends with two remarriages, both acts of negation. Sue remarries Phillotson as penance — a religious self-punishment for her life with Jude. Arabella seduces the dying Jude into remarrying her, motivated by pragmatism and possessiveness. These twin ceremonies close the novel's marriage plot in complete darkness: love destroyed, convention triumphant.

## Key Passages

> "Mrs. Jude Fawley was walking in the streets of Alfredston one Saturday evening..." (010-088)

> "Jude Fawley signed the form of notice, Sue looking over his shoulder and watching his hand as it traced his name." (048-171)

## Critical Interpretation

Hardy's wedding scenes are notably sparse — he refuses to describe them as moments of celebration or beauty. The absence of ceremonial detail is itself a critique: weddings in the novel are bureaucratic events, the signing of documents that transfer ownership. For Sue, the wedding night with Phillotson is a scene of quiet horror; for Jude, the wedding to Arabella is a moment of confused capitulation.

The novel was condemned for its treatment of marriage, with one reviewer writing that Hardy "makes war on the institution of marriage." Hardy's response was that he was not attacking marriage itself but "the inconsistent position of its laws" — the gap between what the law permits and what human happiness requires.
