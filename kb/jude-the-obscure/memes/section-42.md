[042-001] He repeated simply! “I thought—what I naturally thought. But if we are

[042-002] not lovers, we are not. Phillotson thought so, I am sure. See, here is

[042-003] what he has written to me.” He opened the letter she had brought, and

[042-004] read:

[042-005] “I make only one condition—that you are tender and kind to her. I know

[042-006] you love her. But even love may be cruel at times. You are made for

[042-007] each other: it is obvious, palpable, to any unbiased older person. You

[042-008] were all along ‘the shadowy third’ in my short life with her. I repeat,

[042-009] take care of Sue.”

[042-010] “He’s a good fellow, isn’t he!” she said with latent tears. On

[042-011] reconsideration she added, “He was very resigned to letting me go—too

[042-012] resigned almost! I never was so near being in love with him as when he

[042-013] made such thoughtful arrangements for my being comfortable on my

[042-014] journey, and offering to provide money. Yet I was not. If I loved him

[042-015] ever so little as a wife, I’d go back to him even now.”

[042-016] “But you don’t, do you?”

[042-017] “It is true—oh so terribly true!—I don’t.”

[042-018] “Nor me neither, I half-fear!” he said pettishly. “Nor anybody perhaps!

[042-019] Sue, sometimes, when I am vexed with you, I think you are incapable of

[042-020] real love.”

[042-021] “That’s not good and loyal of you!” she said, and drawing away from him

[042-022] as far as she could, looked severely out into the darkness. She added

[042-023] in hurt tones, without turning round: “My liking for you is not as some

[042-024] women’s perhaps. But it is a delight in being with you, of a supremely

[042-025] delicate kind, and I don’t want to go further and risk it by—an attempt

[042-026] to intensify it! I quite realized that, as woman with man, it was a

[042-027] risk to come. But, as me with you, I resolved to trust you to set my

[042-028] wishes above your gratification. Don’t discuss it further, dear Jude!”

[042-029] “Of course, if it would make you reproach yourself… but you do like me

[042-030] very much, Sue? Say you do! Say that you do a quarter, a tenth, as much

[042-031] as I do you, and I’ll be content!”

[042-032] “I’ve let you kiss me, and that tells enough.”

[042-033] “Just once or so!”

[042-034] “Well—don’t be a greedy boy.”

[042-035] He leant back, and did not look at her for a long time. That episode in

[042-036] her past history of which she had told him—of the poor Christminster

[042-037] graduate whom she had handled thus, returned to Jude’s mind; and he saw

[042-038] himself as a possible second in such a torturing destiny.

[042-039] “This is a queer elopement!” he murmured. “Perhaps you are making a

[042-040] cat’s paw of me with Phillotson all this time. Upon my word it almost

[042-041] seems so—to see you sitting up there so prim!”

[042-042] “Now you mustn’t be angry—I won’t let you!” she coaxed, turning and

[042-043] moving nearer to him. “You did kiss me just now, you know; and I didn’t

[042-044] dislike you to, I own it, Jude. Only I don’t want to let you do it

[042-045] again, just yet—considering how we are circumstanced, don’t you see!”

[042-046] He could never resist her when she pleaded (as she well knew). And they

[042-047] sat side by side with joined hands, till she aroused herself at some

[042-048] thought.

[042-049] “I can’t possibly go to that Temperance Inn, after your telegraphing

[042-050] that message!”

[042-051] “Why not?”

[042-052] “You can see well enough!”

[042-053] “Very well; there’ll be some other one open, no doubt. I have sometimes

[042-054] thought, since your marrying Phillotson because of a stupid scandal,

[042-055] that under the affectation of independent views you are as enslaved to

[042-056] the social code as any woman I know!”

[042-057] “Not mentally. But I haven’t the courage of my views, as I said before.

[042-058] I didn’t marry him altogether because of the scandal. But sometimes a

[042-059] woman’s _love of being loved_ gets the better of her conscience, and

[042-060] though she is agonized at the thought of treating a man cruelly, she

[042-061] encourages him to love her while she doesn’t love him at all. Then,

[042-062] when she sees him suffering, her remorse sets in, and she does what she

[042-063] can to repair the wrong.”

[042-064] “You simply mean that you flirted outrageously with him, poor old chap,

[042-065] and then repented, and to make reparation, married him, though you

[042-066] tortured yourself to death by doing it.”

[042-067] “Well—if you will put it brutally!—it was a little like that—that and

[042-068] the scandal together—and your concealing from me what you ought to have

[042-069] told me before!”

[042-070] He could see that she was distressed and tearful at his criticisms, and

[042-071] soothed her, saying: “There, dear; don’t mind! Crucify me, if you will!

[042-072] You know you are all the world to me, whatever you do!”

[042-073] “I am very bad and unprincipled—I know you think that!” she said,

[042-074] trying to blink away her tears.

[042-075] “I think and know you are my dear Sue, from whom neither length nor

[042-076] breadth, nor things present nor things to come, can divide me!”

[042-077] Though so sophisticated in many things, she was such a child in others

[042-078] that this satisfied her, and they reached the end of their journey on

[042-079] the best of terms. It was about ten o’clock when they arrived at

[042-080] Aldbrickham, the county town of North Wessex. As she would not go to

[042-081] for another; and a youth who volunteered to find one wheeled their

[042-082] luggage to the George farther on, which proved to be the inn at which

[042-083] Jude had stayed with Arabella on that one occasion of their meeting

[042-084] after their division for years.

[042-085] Owing, however, to their now entering it by another door, and to his

[042-086] preoccupation, he did not at first recognize the place. When they had

[042-087] engaged their respective rooms they went down to a late supper. During

[042-088] Jude’s temporary absence the waiting-maid spoke to Sue.

[042-089] “I think, ma’am, I remember your relation, or friend, or whatever he

[042-090] is, coming here once before—late, just like this, with his wife—a lady,

[042-091] at any rate, that wasn’t you by no manner of means—jest as med be with

[042-092] you now.”

[042-093] “Oh do you?” said Sue, with a certain sickness of heart. “Though I

[042-094] think you must be mistaken! How long ago was it?”

[042-095] “About a month or two. A handsome, full-figured woman. They had this

[042-096] room.”

[042-097] When Jude came back and sat down to supper Sue seemed moping and

[042-098] miserable. “Jude,” she said to him plaintively, at their parting that

[042-099] night upon the landing, “it is not so nice and pleasant as it used to

[042-100] be with us! I don’t like it here—I can’t bear the place! And I don’t

[042-101] like you so well as I did!”

[042-102] “How fidgeted you seem, dear! Why do you change like this?”

[042-103] “Because it was cruel to bring me here!”

[042-104] “Why?”

[042-105] “You were lately here with Arabella. There, now I have said it!”

[042-106] “Dear me, why—” said Jude looking round him. “Yes—it is the same! I

[042-107] really didn’t know it, Sue. Well—it is not cruel, since we have come as

[042-108] we have—two relations staying together.”

[042-109] “How long ago was it you were here? Tell me, tell me!”

[042-110] “The day before I met you in Christminster, when we went back to

[042-111] Marygreen together. I told you I had met her.”

[042-112] “Yes, you said you had met her, but you didn’t tell me all. Your story

[042-113] was that you had met as estranged people, who were not husband and wife

[042-114] at all in Heaven’s sight—not that you had made it up with her.”

[042-115] “We didn’t make it up,” he said sadly. “I can’t explain, Sue.”

[042-116] “You’ve been false to me; you, my last hope! And I shall never forget

[042-117] it, never!”

[042-118] “But by your own wish, dear Sue, we are only to be friends, not lovers!

[042-119] It is so very inconsistent of you to—”

[042-120] “Friends can be jealous!”

[042-121] “I don’t see that. You concede nothing to me and I have to concede

[042-122] everything to you. After all, you were on good terms with your husband

[042-123] at that time.”

[042-124] “No, I wasn’t, Jude. Oh how can you think so! And you have taken me in,

[042-125] even if you didn’t intend to.” She was so mortified that he was obliged

[042-126] to take her into her room and close the door lest the people should

[042-127] hear. “Was it this room? Yes it was—I see by your look it was! I won’t

[042-128] have it for mine! Oh it was treacherous of you to have her again! _I_

[042-129] jumped out of the window!”

[042-130] “But Sue, she was, after all, my legal wife, if not—”

[042-131] Slipping down on her knees Sue buried her face in the bed and wept.

[042-132] “I never knew such an unreasonable—such a dog-in-the-manger feeling,”

[042-133] said Jude. “I am not to approach you, nor anybody else!”

[042-134] “Oh don’t you _understand_ my feeling? Why don’t you? Why are you so

[042-135] gross? _I_ jumped out of the window?”

[042-136] “Jumped out of window?”

[042-137] “I can’t explain!”

[042-138] It was true that he did not understand her feelings very well. But he

[042-139] did a little; and began to love her none the less.

[042-140] “I—I thought you cared for nobody—desired nobody in the world but me at

[042-141] that time—and ever since!” continued Sue.

[042-142] “It is true. I did not, and don’t now!” said Jude, as distressed as

[042-143] she.

[042-144] “But you must have thought much of her! Or—”

[042-145] “No—I need not—you don’t understand me either—women never do! Why

[042-146] should you get into such a tantrum about nothing?”

[042-147] Looking up from the quilt she pouted provokingly: “If it hadn’t been

[042-148] for that, perhaps I would have gone on to the Temperance Hotel, after

[042-149] all, as you proposed; for I was beginning to think I did belong to

[042-150] you!”

[042-151] “Oh, it is of no consequence!” said Jude distantly.

[042-152] “I thought, of course, that she had never been really your wife since

[042-153] she left you of her own accord years and years ago! My sense of it was,

[042-154] that a parting such as yours from her, and mine from him, ended the

[042-155] marriage.”

[042-156] “I can’t say more without speaking against her, and I don’t want to do

[042-157] that,” said he. “Yet I must tell you one thing, which would settle the

[042-158] matter in any case. She has married another man—really married him! I

[042-159] knew nothing about it till after the visit we made here.”

[042-160] “Married another? … It is a crime—as the world treats it, but does not

[042-161] believe.”

[042-162] “There—now you are yourself again. Yes, it is a crime—as you don’t

[042-163] hold, but would fearfully concede. But I shall never inform against

[042-164] her! And it is evidently a prick of conscience in her that has led her

[042-165] to urge me to get a divorce, that she may remarry this man legally. So

[042-166] you perceive I shall not be likely to see her again.”

[042-167] “And you didn’t really know anything of this when you saw her?” said

[042-168] Sue more gently, as she rose.

[042-169] “I did not. Considering all things, I don’t think you ought to be

[042-170] angry, darling!”

[042-171] “I am not. But I shan’t go to the Temperance Hotel!”

[042-172] He laughed. “Never mind!” he said. “So that I am near you, I am

[042-173] comparatively happy. It is more than this earthly wretch called Me

[042-174] deserves—you spirit, you disembodied creature, you dear, sweet,

[042-175] tantalizing phantom—hardly flesh at all; so that when I put my arms

[042-176] round you I almost expect them to pass through you as through air!

[042-177] Forgive me for being gross, as you call it! Remember that our calling

[042-178] cousins when really strangers was a snare. The enmity of our parents

[042-179] gave a piquancy to you in my eyes that was intenser even than the

[042-180] novelty of ordinary new acquaintance.”

[042-181] “Say those pretty lines, then, from Shelley’s ‘Epipsychidion’ as if

[042-182] they meant me!” she solicited, slanting up closer to him as they stood.

[042-183] “Don’t you know them?”

[042-184] “I know hardly any poetry,” he replied mournfully.
