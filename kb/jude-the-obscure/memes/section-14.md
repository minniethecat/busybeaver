[014-001] Jude found himself speaking out loud, holding conversations with them

[014-002] as it were, like an actor in a melodrama who apostrophizes the audience

[014-003] on the other side of the footlights; till he suddenly ceased with a

[014-004] start at his absurdity. Perhaps those incoherent words of the wanderer

[014-005] were heard within the walls by some student or thinker over his lamp;

[014-006] and he may have raised his head, and wondered what voice it was, and

[014-007] what it betokened. Jude now perceived that, so far as solid flesh went,

[014-008] he had the whole aged city to himself with the exception of a belated

[014-009] townsman here and there, and that he seemed to be catching a cold.

[014-010] A voice reached him out of the shade; a real and local voice:

[014-011] “You’ve been a-settin’ a long time on that plinth-stone, young man.

[014-012] What med you be up to?”

[014-013] It came from a policeman who had been observing Jude without the latter

[014-014] observing him.

[014-015] Jude went home and to bed, after reading up a little about these men

[014-016] and their several messages to the world from a book or two that he had

[014-017] brought with him concerning the sons of the university. As he drew

[014-018] towards sleep various memorable words of theirs that he had just been

[014-019] conning seemed spoken by them in muttering utterances; some audible,

[014-020] some unintelligible to him. One of the spectres (who afterwards mourned

[014-021] Christminster as “the home of lost causes,” though Jude did not

[014-022] remember this) was now apostrophizing her thus:

[014-023] “Beautiful city! so venerable, so lovely, so unravaged by the fierce

[014-024] intellectual life of our century, so serene! … Her ineffable charm

[014-025] keeps ever calling us to the true goal of all of us, to the ideal, to

[014-026] perfection.”

[014-027] Another voice was that of the Corn Law convert, whose phantom he had

[014-028] just seen in the quadrangle with a great bell. Jude thought his soul

[014-029] might have been shaping the historic words of his master-speech:

[014-030] “Sir, I may be wrong, but my impression is that my duty towards a

[014-031] country threatened with famine requires that that which has been the

[014-032] ordinary remedy under all similar circumstances should be resorted to

[014-033] now, namely, that there should be free access to the food of man from

[014-034] whatever quarter it may come… Deprive me of office to-morrow, you can

[014-035] never deprive me of the consciousness that I have exercised the powers

[014-036] committed to me from no corrupt or interested motives, from no desire

[014-037] to gratify ambition, for no personal gain.”

[014-038] Then the sly author of the immortal Chapter on Christianity: “How shall

[014-039] we excuse the supine inattention of the Pagan and philosophic world, to

[014-040] those evidences [miracles] which were presented by Omnipotence? … The

[014-041] sages of Greece and Rome turned aside from the awful spectacle, and

[014-042] appeared unconscious of any alterations in the moral or physical

[014-043] government of the world.”

[014-044] Then the shade of the poet, the last of the optimists:

[014-045] How the world is made for each of us!

[014-046] * * * * * * * * *

[014-047] And each of the Many helps to recruit

[014-048] The life of the race by a general plan.

[014-049] Then one of the three enthusiasts he had seen just now, the author of

[014-050] the _Apologia_:

[014-051] “My argument was … that absolute certitude as to the truths of natural

[014-052] theology was the result of an assemblage of concurring and converging

[014-053] probabilities … that probabilities which did not reach to logical

[014-054] certainty might create a mental certitude.”

[014-055] The second of them, no polemic, murmured quieter things:

[014-056] Why should we faint, and fear to live alone,

[014-057] Since all alone, so Heaven has will’d, we die?

[014-058] He likewise heard some phrases spoken by the phantom with the short

[014-059] face, the genial Spectator:

[014-060] “When I look upon the tombs of the great, every motion of envy dies in

[014-061] me; when I read the epitaphs of the beautiful, every inordinate desire

[014-062] goes out; when I meet with the grief of parents upon a tombstone, my

[014-063] heart melts with compassion; when I see the tombs of the parents

[014-064] themselves, I consider the vanity of grieving for those whom we must

[014-065] quickly follow.”

[014-066] And lastly a gentle-voiced prelate spoke, during whose meek, familiar

[014-067] rhyme, endeared to him from earliest childhood, Jude fell asleep:

[014-068] Teach me to live, that I may dread

[014-069] The grave as little as my bed.

[014-070] Teach me to die…

[014-071] He did not wake till morning. The ghostly past seemed to have gone, and

[014-072] everything spoke of to-day. He started up in bed, thinking he had

[014-073] overslept himself and then said:

[014-074] “By Jove—I had quite forgotten my sweet-faced cousin, and that she’s

[014-075] here all the time! … and my old schoolmaster, too.” His words about his

[014-076] schoolmaster had, perhaps, less zest in them than his words concerning

[014-077] his cousin.

[014-078] II

[014-079] Necessary meditations on the actual, including the mean

[014-080] bread-and-cheese question, dissipated the phantasmal for a while, and

[014-081] compelled Jude to smother high thinkings under immediate needs. He had

[014-082] to get up, and seek for work, manual work; the only kind deemed by many

[014-083] of its professors to be work at all.

[014-084] Passing out into the streets on this errand he found that the colleges

[014-085] had treacherously changed their sympathetic countenances: some were

[014-086] pompous; some had put on the look of family vaults above ground;

[014-087] something barbaric loomed in the masonries of all. The spirits of the

[014-088] great men had disappeared.

[014-089] The numberless architectural pages around him he read, naturally, less

[014-090] as an artist-critic of their forms than as an artizan and comrade of

[014-091] the dead handicraftsmen whose muscles had actually executed those

[014-092] forms. He examined the mouldings, stroked them as one who knew their

[014-093] beginning, said they were difficult or easy in the working, had taken

[014-094] little or much time, were trying to the arm, or convenient to the tool.

[014-095] What at night had been perfect and ideal was by day the more or less

[014-096] defective real. Cruelties, insults, had, he perceived, been inflicted

[014-097] on the aged erections. The condition of several moved him as he would

[014-098] have been moved by maimed sentient beings. They were wounded, broken,

[014-099] sloughing off their outer shape in the deadly struggle against years,

[014-100] weather, and man.

[014-101] The rottenness of these historical documents reminded him that he was

[014-102] not, after all, hastening on to begin the morning practically as he had

[014-103] intended. He had come to work, and to live by work, and the morning had

[014-104] nearly gone. It was, in one sense, encouraging to think that in a place

[014-105] of crumbling stones there must be plenty for one of his trade to do in

[014-106] the business of renovation. He asked his way to the workyard of the

[014-107] stone-mason whose name had been given him at Alfredston; and soon heard

[014-108] the familiar sound of the rubbers and chisels.

[014-109] The yard was a little centre of regeneration. Here, with keen edges and

[014-110] smooth curves, were forms in the exact likeness of those he had seen

[014-111] abraded and time-eaten on the walls. These were the ideas in modern

[014-112] prose which the lichened colleges presented in old poetry. Even some of

[014-113] those antiques might have been called prose when they were new. They

[014-114] had done nothing but wait, and had become poetical. How easy to the

[014-115] smallest building; how impossible to most men.

[014-116] He asked for the foreman, and looked round among the new traceries,

[014-117] mullions, transoms, shafts, pinnacles, and battlements standing on the

[014-118] bankers half worked, or waiting to be removed. They were marked by

[014-119] precision, mathematical straightness, smoothness, exactitude: there in

[014-120] the old walls were the broken lines of the original idea; jagged

[014-121] curves, disdain of precision, irregularity, disarray.

[014-122] For a moment there fell on Jude a true illumination; that here in the

[014-123] stone yard was a centre of effort as worthy as that dignified by the

[014-124] name of scholarly study within the noblest of the colleges. But he lost

[014-125] it under stress of his old idea. He would accept any employment which

[014-126] might be offered him on the strength of his late employer’s

[014-127] recommendation; but he would accept it as a provisional thing only.

[014-128] This was his form of the modern vice of unrest.

[014-129] Moreover he perceived that at best only copying, patching and imitating

[014-130] went on here; which he fancied to be owing to some temporary and local

[014-131] cause. He did not at that time see that mediævalism was as dead as a

[014-132] fern-leaf in a lump of coal; that other developments were shaping in

[014-133] the world around him, in which Gothic architecture and its associations

[014-134] had no place. The deadly animosity of contemporary logic and vision

[014-135] towards so much of what he held in reverence was not yet revealed to

[014-136] him.

[014-137] Having failed to obtain work here as yet he went away, and thought

[014-138] again of his cousin, whose presence somewhere at hand he seemed to feel

[014-139] in wavelets of interest, if not of emotion. How he wished he had that

[014-140] pretty portrait of her! At last he wrote to his aunt to send it. She

[014-141] did so, with a request, however, that he was not to bring disturbance

[014-142] into the family by going to see the girl or her relations. Jude, a

[014-143] ridiculously affectionate fellow, promised nothing, put the photograph

[014-144] on the mantel-piece, kissed it—he did not know why—and felt more at

[014-145] home. She seemed to look down and preside over his tea. It was

[014-146] cheering—the one thing uniting him to the emotions of the living city.

[014-147] There remained the schoolmaster—probably now a reverend parson. But he

[014-148] could not possibly hunt up such a respectable man just yet; so raw and

[014-149] unpolished was his condition, so precarious were his fortunes. Thus he

[014-150] still remained in loneliness. Although people moved round him he

[014-151] virtually saw none. Not as yet having mingled with the active life of

[014-152] the place it was largely non-existent to him. But the saints and

[014-153] prophets in the window-tracery, the paintings in the galleries, the

[014-154] statues, the busts, the gargoyles, the corbel-heads—these seemed to

[014-155] breathe his atmosphere. Like all newcomers to a spot on which the past

[014-156] is deeply graven he heard that past announcing itself with an emphasis

[014-157] altogether unsuspected by, and even incredible to, the habitual

[014-158] residents.

[014-159] For many days he haunted the cloisters and quadrangles of the colleges

[014-160] at odd minutes in passing them, surprised by impish echoes of his own

[014-161] footsteps, smart as the blows of a mallet. The Christminster

[014-162] “sentiment,” as it had been called, ate further and further into him;

[014-163] till he probably knew more about those buildings materially,

[014-164] artistically, and historically, than any one of their inmates.

[014-165] It was not till now, when he found himself actually on the spot of his

[014-166] enthusiasm, that Jude perceived how far away from the object of that

[014-167] enthusiasm he really was. Only a wall divided him from those happy

[014-168] young contemporaries of his with whom he shared a common mental life;

[014-169] men who had nothing to do from morning till night but to read, mark,

[014-170] learn, and inwardly digest. Only a wall—but what a wall!

[014-171] Every day, every hour, as he went in search of labour, he saw them

[014-172] going and coming also, rubbed shoulders with them, heard their voices,

[014-173] marked their movements. The conversation of some of the more thoughtful

[014-174] among them seemed oftentimes, owing to his long and persistent

[014-175] preparation for this place, to be peculiarly akin to his own thoughts.

[014-176] Yet he was as far from them as if he had been at the antipodes. Of

[014-177] course he was. He was a young workman in a white blouse, and with

[014-178] stone-dust in the creases of his clothes; and in passing him they did

[014-179] not even see him, or hear him, rather saw through him as through a pane

[014-180] of glass at their familiars beyond. Whatever they were to him, he to

[014-181] them was not on the spot at all; and yet he had fancied he would be

[014-182] close to their lives by coming there.

[014-183] But the future lay ahead after all; and if he could only be so

[014-184] fortunate as to get into good employment he would put up with the

[014-185] inevitable. So he thanked God for his health and strength, and took

[014-186] courage. For the present he was outside the gates of everything,

[014-187] colleges included: perhaps some day he would be inside. Those palaces

[014-188] of light and leading; he might some day look down on the world through

[014-189] their panes.
