[001-065] PREFACE

[001-066] The history of this novel (whose birth in its present shape has been

[001-067] much retarded by the necessities of periodical publication) is briefly

[001-068] as follows. The scheme was jotted down in 1890, from notes made in 1887

[001-069] and onward, some of the circumstances being suggested by the death of a

[001-070] woman in the former year. The scenes were revisited in October, 1892;

[001-071] the narrative was written in outline in 1892 and the spring of 1893,

[001-072] and at full length, as it now appears, from August, 1893, onward into

[001-073] the next year; the whole, with the exception of a few chapters, being

[001-074] in the hands of the publisher by the end of 1894. It was begun as a

[001-075] serial story in HARPER’S MAGAZINE at the end of November, 1894, and was

[001-076] continued in monthly parts.

[001-077] But, as in the case of _Tess of the D’Urbervilles_, the magazine

[001-078] version was, for various reasons, abridged and modified in some degree,

[001-079] the present edition being the first in which the whole appears as

[001-080] originally written. And in the difficulty of coming to an early

[001-081] decision in the matter of a title, the tale was issued under a

[001-082] provisional name—two such titles having, in fact, been successively

[001-083] adopted. The present and final title, deemed on the whole the best, was

[001-084] one of the earliest thought of.

[001-085] For a novel addressed by a man to men and women of full age, which

[001-086] attempts to deal unaffectedly with the fret and fever, derision and

[001-087] disaster, that may press in the wake of the strongest passion known to

[001-088] humanity, and to point, without a mincing of words, the tragedy of

[001-089] unfulfilled aims, I am not aware that there is anything in the handling

[001-090] to which exception can be taken.

[001-091] Like former productions of this pen, _Jude the Obscure_ is simply an

[001-092] endeavor to give shape and coherence to a series of seemings, or

[001-093] personal impressions, the question of their consistency or their

[001-094] discordance, of their permanence or their transitoriness, being

[001-095] regarded as not of the first moment.

[001-096] T.H.

[001-097] _August_, 1895.

[001-098] Part First AT MARYGREEN

[001-099] _“Yea, many there be that have run out of their wits for women, and

[001-100] become servants for their sakes. Many also have perished, have erred,

[001-101] and sinned, for women… O ye men, how can it be but women should be

[001-102] strong, seeing they do thus?”_—ESDRAS.

[001-103] I

[001-104] The schoolmaster was leaving the village, and everybody seemed sorry.

[001-105] The miller at Cresscombe lent him the small white tilted cart and horse

[001-106] to carry his goods to the city of his destination, about twenty miles

[001-107] off, such a vehicle proving of quite sufficient size for the departing

[001-108] teacher’s effects. For the schoolhouse had been partly furnished by the

[001-109] managers, and the only cumbersome article possessed by the master, in

[001-110] addition to the packing-case of books, was a cottage piano that he had

[001-111] bought at an auction during the year in which he thought of learning

[001-112] instrumental music. But the enthusiasm having waned he had never

[001-113] acquired any skill in playing, and the purchased article had been a

[001-114] perpetual trouble to him ever since in moving house.

[001-115] The rector had gone away for the day, being a man who disliked the

[001-116] sight of changes. He did not mean to return till the evening, when the

[001-117] new school-teacher would have arrived and settled in, and everything

[001-118] would be smooth again.

[001-119] The blacksmith, the farm bailiff, and the schoolmaster himself were

[001-120] standing in perplexed attitudes in the parlour before the instrument.

[001-121] The master had remarked that even if he got it into the cart he should

[001-122] not know what to do with it on his arrival at Christminster, the city

[001-123] he was bound for, since he was only going into temporary lodgings just

[001-124] at first.

[001-125] A little boy of eleven, who had been thoughtfully assisting in the

[001-126] packing, joined the group of men, and as they rubbed their chins he

[001-127] spoke up, blushing at the sound of his own voice: “Aunt have got a

[001-128] great fuel-house, and it could be put there, perhaps, till you’ve found

[001-129] a place to settle in, sir.”

[001-130] “A proper good notion,” said the blacksmith.

[001-131] It was decided that a deputation should wait on the boy’s aunt—an old

[001-132] maiden resident—and ask her if she would house the piano till Mr.

[001-133] Phillotson should send for it. The smith and the bailiff started to see

[001-134] about the practicability of the suggested shelter, and the boy and the

[001-135] schoolmaster were left standing alone.

[001-136] “Sorry I am going, Jude?” asked the latter kindly.

[001-137] Tears rose into the boy’s eyes, for he was not among the regular day

[001-138] scholars, who came unromantically close to the schoolmaster’s life, but

[001-139] one who had attended the night school only during the present teacher’s

[001-140] term of office. The regular scholars, if the truth must be told, stood

[001-141] at the present moment afar off, like certain historic disciples,

[001-142] indisposed to any enthusiastic volunteering of aid.

[001-143] The boy awkwardly opened the book he held in his hand, which Mr.

[001-144] Phillotson had bestowed on him as a parting gift, and admitted that he

[001-145] was sorry.

[001-146] “So am I,” said Mr. Phillotson.

[001-147] “Why do you go, sir?” asked the boy.

[001-148] “Ah—that would be a long story. You wouldn’t understand my reasons,

[001-149] Jude. You will, perhaps, when you are older.”

[001-150] “I think I should now, sir.”

[001-151] “Well—don’t speak of this everywhere. You know what a university is,

[001-152] and a university degree? It is the necessary hallmark of a man who

[001-153] wants to do anything in teaching. My scheme, or dream, is to be a

[001-154] university graduate, and then to be ordained. By going to live at

[001-155] Christminster, or near it, I shall be at headquarters, so to speak, and

[001-156] if my scheme is practicable at all, I consider that being on the spot

[001-157] will afford me a better chance of carrying it out than I should have

[001-158] elsewhere.”

[001-159] The smith and his companion returned. Old Miss Fawley’s fuel-house was

[001-160] dry, and eminently practicable; and she seemed willing to give the

[001-161] instrument standing-room there. It was accordingly left in the school

[001-162] till the evening, when more hands would be available for removing it;

[001-163] and the schoolmaster gave a final glance round.

[001-164] The boy Jude assisted in loading some small articles, and at nine

[001-165] o’clock Mr. Phillotson mounted beside his box of books and other

[001-166] _impedimenta_, and bade his friends good-bye.

[001-167] “I shan’t forget you, Jude,” he said, smiling, as the cart moved off.

[001-168] “Be a good boy, remember; and be kind to animals and birds, and read

[001-169] all you can. And if ever you come to Christminster remember you hunt me

[001-170] out for old acquaintance’ sake.”

[001-171] The cart creaked across the green, and disappeared round the corner by

[001-172] the rectory-house. The boy returned to the draw-well at the edge of the

[001-173] greensward, where he had left his buckets when he went to help his

[001-174] patron and teacher in the loading. There was a quiver in his lip now

[001-175] and after opening the well-cover to begin lowering the bucket he paused

[001-176] and leant with his forehead and arms against the framework, his face

[001-177] wearing the fixity of a thoughtful child’s who has felt the pricks of

[001-178] life somewhat before his time. The well into which he was looking was

[001-179] as ancient as the village itself, and from his present position

[001-180] appeared as a long circular perspective ending in a shining disk of

[001-181] quivering water at a distance of a hundred feet down. There was a

[001-182] lining of green moss near the top, and nearer still the hart’s-tongue

[001-183] fern.

[001-184] He said to himself, in the melodramatic tones of a whimsical boy, that

[001-185] the schoolmaster had drawn at that well scores of times on a morning

[001-186] like this, and would never draw there any more. “I’ve seen him look

[001-187] down into it, when he was tired with his drawing, just as I do now, and

[001-188] when he rested a bit before carrying the buckets home! But he was too

[001-189] clever to bide here any longer—a small sleepy place like this!”

[001-190] A tear rolled from his eye into the depths of the well. The morning was

[001-191] a little foggy, and the boy’s breathing unfurled itself as a thicker

[001-192] fog upon the still and heavy air. His thoughts were interrupted by a

[001-193] sudden outcry:

[001-194] “Bring on that water, will ye, you idle young harlican!”

[001-195] It came from an old woman who had emerged from her door towards the

[001-196] garden gate of a green-thatched cottage not far off. The boy quickly

[001-197] waved a signal of assent, drew the water with what was a great effort

[001-198] for one of his stature, landed and emptied the big bucket into his own

[001-199] pair of smaller ones, and pausing a moment for breath, started with

[001-200] them across the patch of clammy greensward whereon the well

[001-201] stood—nearly in the centre of the little village, or rather hamlet of

[001-202] Marygreen.

[001-203] It was as old-fashioned as it was small, and it rested in the lap of an

[001-204] undulating upland adjoining the North Wessex downs. Old as it was,

[001-205] however, the well-shaft was probably the only relic of the local

[001-206] history that remained absolutely unchanged. Many of the thatched and

[001-207] dormered dwelling-houses had been pulled down of late years, and many

[001-208] trees felled on the green. Above all, the original church, hump-backed,

[001-209] wood-turreted, and quaintly hipped, had been taken down, and either

[001-210] cracked up into heaps of road-metal in the lane, or utilized as pig-sty

[001-211] walls, garden seats, guard-stones to fences, and rockeries in the

[001-212] flower-beds of the neighbourhood. In place of it a tall new building of

[001-213] modern Gothic design, unfamiliar to English eyes, had been erected on a

[001-214] new piece of ground by a certain obliterator of historic records who

[001-215] had run down from London and back in a day. The site whereon so long

[001-216] had stood the ancient temple to the Christian divinities was not even

[001-217] recorded on the green and level grass-plot that had immemorially been

[001-218] the churchyard, the obliterated graves being commemorated by

[001-219] eighteen-penny cast-iron crosses warranted to last five years.

[001-220] II

[001-221] Slender as was Jude Fawley’s frame he bore the two brimming

[001-222] house-buckets of water to the cottage without resting. Over the door

[001-223] was a little rectangular piece of blue board, on which was painted in

[001-224] yellow letters, “Drusilla Fawley, Baker.” Within the little lead panes

[001-225] of the window—this being one of the few old houses left—were five

[001-226] bottles of sweets, and three buns on a plate of the willow pattern.

[001-227] While emptying the buckets at the back of the house he could hear an

[001-228] animated conversation in progress within-doors between his great-aunt,

[001-229] the Drusilla of the sign-board, and some other villagers. Having seen

[001-230] the school-master depart, they were summing up particulars of the

[001-231] event, and indulging in predictions of his future.

[001-232] “And who’s he?” asked one, comparatively a stranger, when the boy

[001-233] entered.

[001-234] “Well ye med ask it, Mrs. Williams. He’s my great-nephew—come since you

[001-235] was last this way.” The old inhabitant who answered was a tall, gaunt

[001-236] woman, who spoke tragically on the most trivial subject, and gave a

[001-237] phrase of her conversation to each auditor in turn. “He come from

[001-238] Mellstock, down in South Wessex, about a year ago—worse luck for ’n,

[001-239] Belinda” (turning to the right) “where his father was living, and was

[001-240] took wi’ the shakings for death, and died in two days, as you know,

[001-241] Caroline” (turning to the left). “It would ha’ been a blessing if

[001-242] Goddy-mighty had took thee too, wi’ thy mother and father, poor useless

[001-243] boy! But I’ve got him here to stay with me till I can see what’s to be

[001-244] done with un, though I am obliged to let him earn any penny he can.

[001-245] Just now he’s a-scaring of birds for Farmer Troutham. It keeps him out

[001-246] of mischty. Why do ye turn away, Jude?” she continued, as the boy,

[001-247] feeling the impact of their glances like slaps upon his face, moved

[001-248] aside.
