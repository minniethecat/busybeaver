[021-001] In the evening the frequenters of the house dropped in one by one, Jude

[021-002] still retaining his seat in the corner, though his money was all spent,

[021-003] and he had not eaten anything the whole day except a biscuit. He

[021-004] surveyed his gathering companions with all the equanimity and

[021-005] philosophy of a man who has been drinking long and slowly, and made

[021-006] friends with several: to wit, Tinker Taylor, a decayed

[021-007] church-ironmonger who appeared to have been of a religious turn in

[021-008] earlier years, but was somewhat blasphemous now; also a red-nosed

[021-009] auctioneer; also two Gothic masons like himself, called Uncle Jim and

[021-010] Uncle Joe. There were present, too, some clerks, and a gown- and

[021-011] surplice-maker’s assistant; two ladies who sported moral characters of

[021-012] various depths of shade, according to their company, nicknamed “Bower

[021-013] o’ Bliss” and “Freckles”; some horsey men “in the know” of betting

[021-014] circles; a travelling actor from the theatre, and two devil-may-care

[021-015] young men who proved to be gownless undergraduates; they had slipped in

[021-016] by stealth to meet a man about bull-pups, and stayed to drink and smoke

[021-017] short pipes with the racing gents aforesaid, looking at their watches

[021-018] every now and then.

[021-019] The conversation waxed general. Christminster society was criticized,

[021-020] the dons, magistrates, and other people in authority being sincerely

[021-021] pitied for their shortcomings, while opinions on how they ought to

[021-022] conduct themselves and their affairs to be properly respected, were

[021-023] exchanged in a large-minded and disinterested manner.

[021-024] Jude Fawley, with the self-conceit, effrontery, and _aplomb_ of a

[021-025] strong-brained fellow in liquor, threw in his remarks somewhat

[021-026] peremptorily; and his aims having been what they were for so many

[021-027] years, everything the others said turned upon his tongue, by a sort of

[021-028] mechanical craze, to the subject of scholarship and study, the extent

[021-029] of his own learning being dwelt upon with an insistence that would have

[021-030] appeared pitiable to himself in his sane hours.

[021-031] “I don’t care a damn,” he was saying, “for any provost, warden,

[021-032] principal, fellow, or cursed master of arts in the university! What I

[021-033] know is that I’d lick ’em on their own ground if they’d give me a

[021-034] chance, and show ’em a few things they are not up to yet!”

[021-035] “Hear, hear!” said the undergraduates from the corner, where they were

[021-036] talking privately about the pups.

[021-037] “You always was fond o’ books, I’ve heard,” said Tinker Taylor, “and I

[021-038] don’t doubt what you state. Now with me ’twas different. I always saw

[021-039] there was more to be learnt outside a book than in; and I took my steps

[021-040] accordingly, or I shouldn’t have been the man I am.”

[021-041] “You aim at the Church, I believe?” said Uncle Joe. “If you are such a

[021-042] scholar as to pitch yer hopes so high as that, why not give us a

[021-043] specimen of your scholarship? Canst say the Creed in Latin, man? That

[021-044] was how they once put it to a chap down in my country.”

[021-045] “I should think so!” said Jude haughtily.

[021-046] “Not he! Like his conceit!” screamed one of the ladies.

[021-047] “Just you shut up, Bower o’ Bliss!” said one of the undergraduates.

[021-048] “Silence!” He drank off the spirits in his tumbler, rapped with it on

[021-049] the counter, and announced, “The gentleman in the corner is going to

[021-050] rehearse the Articles of his Belief, in the Latin tongue, for the

[021-051] edification of the company.”

[021-052] “I won’t!” said Jude.

[021-053] “Yes—have a try!” said the surplice-maker.

[021-054] “You can’t!” said Uncle Joe.

[021-055] “Yes, he can!” said Tinker Taylor.

[021-056] “I’ll swear I can!” said Jude. “Well, come now, stand me a small Scotch

[021-057] cold, and I’ll do it straight off.”

[021-058] “That’s a fair offer,” said the undergraduate, throwing down the money

[021-059] for the whisky.

[021-060] The barmaid concocted the mixture with the bearing of a person

[021-061] compelled to live amongst animals of an inferior species, and the glass

[021-062] was handed across to Jude, who, having drunk the contents, stood up and

[021-063] began rhetorically, without hesitation:

[021-064] “_Credo in unum Deum, Patrem omnipotentem, Factorem coeli et terrae,

[021-065] visibilium omnium et invisibilium._”

[021-066] “Good! Excellent Latin!” cried one of the undergraduates, who, however,

[021-067] had not the slightest conception of a single word.

[021-068] A silence reigned among the rest in the bar, and the maid stood still,

[021-069] Jude’s voice echoing sonorously into the inner parlour, where the

[021-070] landlord was dozing, and bringing him out to see what was going on.

[021-071] Jude had declaimed steadily ahead, and was continuing:

[021-072] “_Crucifixus etiam pro nobis: sub Pontio Pilato passus, et sepultus

[021-073] est. Et resurrexit tertia die, secundum Scripturas._”

[021-074] “That’s the Nicene,” sneered the second undergraduate. “And we wanted

[021-075] the Apostles’!”

[021-076] “You didn’t say so! And every fool knows, except you, that the Nicene

[021-077] is the most historic creed!”

[021-078] “Let un go on, let un go on!” said the auctioneer.

[021-079] But Jude’s mind seemed to grow confused soon, and he could not get on.

[021-080] He put his hand to his forehead, and his face assumed an expression of

[021-081] pain.

[021-082] “Give him another glass—then he’ll fetch up and get through it,” said

[021-083] Tinker Taylor.

[021-084] Somebody threw down threepence, the glass was handed, Jude stretched

[021-085] out his arm for it without looking, and having swallowed the liquor,

[021-086] went on in a moment in a revived voice, raising it as he neared the end

[021-087] with the manner of a priest leading a congregation:

[021-088] “_Et in Spiritum Sanctum, Dominum et vivificantem, qui ex Patre

[021-089] Filioque procedit. Qui cum Patre et Filio simul adoratur et

[021-090] conglorificatur. Qui locutus est per prophetas._

[021-091] “_Et unam Catholicam et Apostolicam Ecclesiam. Confiteor unum Baptisma

[021-092] in remissionem peccatorum. Et exspecto Resurrectionem mortuorum. Et

[021-093] vitam venturi saeculi. Amen._”

[021-094] “Well done!” said several, enjoying the last word, as being the first

[021-095] and only one they had recognized.

[021-096] Then Jude seemed to shake the fumes from his brain, as he stared round

[021-097] upon them.

[021-098] “You pack of fools!” he cried. “Which one of you knows whether I have

[021-099] said it or no? It might have been the Ratcatcher’s Daughter in double

[021-100] Dutch for all that your besotted heads can tell! See what I have

[021-101] brought myself to—the crew I have come among!”

[021-102] The landlord, who had already had his license endorsed for harbouring

[021-103] queer characters, feared a riot, and came outside the counter; but

[021-104] Jude, in his sudden flash of reason, had turned in disgust and left the

[021-105] scene, the door slamming with a dull thud behind him.

[021-106] He hastened down the lane and round into the straight broad street,

[021-107] which he followed till it merged in the highway, and all sound of his

[021-108] late companions had been left behind. Onward he still went, under the

[021-109] influence of a childlike yearning for the one being in the world to

[021-110] whom it seemed possible to fly—an unreasoning desire, whose ill

[021-111] judgement was not apparent to him now. In the course of an hour, when

[021-112] it was between ten and eleven o’clock, he entered the village of

[021-113] Lumsdon, and reaching the cottage, saw that a light was burning in a

[021-114] downstairs room, which he assumed, rightly as it happened, to be hers.

[021-115] Jude stepped close to the wall, and tapped with his finger on the pane,

[021-116] saying impatiently, “Sue, Sue!”

[021-117] She must have recognized his voice, for the light disappeared from the

[021-118] apartment, and in a second or two the door was unlocked and opened, and

[021-119] Sue appeared with a candle in her hand.

[021-120] “Is it Jude? Yes, it is! My dear, dear cousin, what’s the matter?”

[021-121] “Oh, I am—I couldn’t help coming, Sue!” said he, sinking down upon the

[021-122] doorstep. “I am so wicked, Sue—my heart is nearly broken, and I could

[021-123] not bear my life as it was! So I have been drinking, and blaspheming,

[021-124] or next door to it, and saying holy things in disreputable

[021-125] quarters—repeating in idle bravado words which ought never to be

[021-126] uttered but reverently! Oh, do anything with me, Sue—kill me—I don’t

[021-127] care! Only don’t hate me and despise me like all the rest of the

[021-128] world!”

[021-129] “You are ill, poor dear! No, I won’t despise you; of course I won’t!

[021-130] Come in and rest, and let me see what I can do for you. Now lean on me,

[021-131] and don’t mind.” With one hand holding the candle and the other

[021-132] supporting him, she led him indoors, and placed him in the only easy

[021-133] chair the meagrely furnished house afforded, stretching his feet upon

[021-134] another, and pulling off his boots. Jude, now getting towards his sober

[021-135] senses, could only say, “Dear, dear Sue!” in a voice broken by grief

[021-136] and contrition.

[021-137] She asked him if he wanted anything to eat, but he shook his head. Then

[021-138] telling him to go to sleep, and that she would come down early in the

[021-139] morning and get him some breakfast, she bade him good-night and

[021-140] ascended the stairs.

[021-141] Almost immediately he fell into a heavy slumber, and did not wake till

[021-142] dawn. At first he did not know where he was, but by degrees his

[021-143] situation cleared to him, and he beheld it in all the ghastliness of a

[021-144] right mind. She knew the worst of him—the very worst. How could he face

[021-145] her now? She would soon be coming down to see about breakfast, as she

[021-146] had said, and there would he be in all his shame confronting her. He

[021-147] could not bear the thought, and softly drawing on his boots, and taking

[021-148] his hat from the nail on which she had hung it, he slipped noiselessly

[021-149] out of the house.

[021-150] His fixed idea was to get away to some obscure spot and hide, and

[021-151] perhaps pray; and the only spot which occurred to him was Marygreen. He

[021-152] called at his lodging in Christminster, where he found awaiting him a

[021-153] note of dismissal from his employer; and having packed up he turned his

[021-154] back upon the city that had been such a thorn in his side, and struck

[021-155] southward into Wessex. He had no money left in his pocket, his small

[021-156] savings, deposited at one of the banks in Christminster, having

[021-157] fortunately been left untouched. To get to Marygreen, therefore, his

[021-158] only course was walking; and the distance being nearly twenty miles, he

[021-159] had ample time to complete on the way the sobering process begun in

[021-160] him.

[021-161] At some hour of the evening he reached Alfredston. Here he pawned his

[021-162] waistcoat, and having gone out of the town a mile or two, slept under a

[021-163] rick that night. At dawn he rose, shook off the hayseeds and stems from

[021-164] his clothes, and started again, breasting the long white road up the

[021-165] hill to the downs, which had been visible to him a long way off, and

[021-166] passing the milestone at the top, whereon he had carved his hopes years

[021-167] ago.

[021-168] He reached the ancient hamlet while the people were at breakfast. Weary

[021-169] and mud-bespattered, but quite possessed of his ordinary clearness of

[021-170] brain, he sat down by the well, thinking as he did so what a poor

[021-171] Christ he made. Seeing a trough of water near he bathed his face, and

[021-172] went on to the cottage of his great-aunt, whom he found breakfasting in

[021-173] bed, attended by the woman who lived with her.

[021-174] “What—out o’ work?” asked his relative, regarding him through eyes

[021-175] sunken deep, under lids heavy as pot-covers, no other cause for his

[021-176] tumbled appearance suggesting itself to one whose whole life had been a

[021-177] struggle with material things.

[021-178] “Yes,” said Jude heavily. “I think I must have a little rest.”

[021-179] Refreshed by some breakfast, he went up to his old room and lay down in

[021-180] his shirt-sleeves, after the manner of the artizan. He fell asleep for

[021-181] a short while, and when he awoke it was as if he had awakened in hell.

[021-182] It _was_ hell—“the hell of conscious failure,” both in ambition and in

[021-183] love. He thought of that previous abyss into which he had fallen before

[021-184] leaving this part of the country; the deepest deep he had supposed it

[021-185] then; but it was not so deep as this. That had been the breaking in of

[021-186] the outer bulwarks of his hope: this was of his second line.
