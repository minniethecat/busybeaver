[012-001] “Then I must do it to-morrow morning. It won’t keep.”

[012-002] “Very well—do.”

[012-003] XI

[012-004] Next morning, which was Sunday, she resumed operations about ten

[012-005] o’clock; and the renewed work recalled the conversation which had

[012-006] accompanied it the night before, and put her back into the same

[012-007] intractable temper.

[012-008] “That’s the story about me in Marygreen, is it—that I entrapped ’ee?

[012-009] Much of a catch you were, Lord send!” As she warmed she saw some of

[012-010] Jude’s dear ancient classics on a table where they ought not to have

[012-011] been laid. “I won’t have them books here in the way!” she cried

[012-012] petulantly; and seizing them one by one she began throwing them upon

[012-013] the floor.

[012-014] “Leave my books alone!” he said. “You might have thrown them aside if

[012-015] you had liked, but as to soiling them like that, it is disgusting!” In

[012-016] the operation of making lard Arabella’s hands had become smeared with

[012-017] the hot grease, and her fingers consequently left very perceptible

[012-018] imprints on the book-covers. She continued deliberately to toss the

[012-019] books severally upon the floor, till Jude, incensed beyond bearing,

[012-020] caught her by the arms to make her leave off. Somehow, in going so, he

[012-021] loosened the fastening of her hair, and it rolled about her ears.

[012-022] “Let me go!” she said.

[012-023] “Promise to leave the books alone.”

[012-024] She hesitated. “Let me go!” she repeated.

[012-025] “Promise!”

[012-026] After a pause: “I do.”

[012-027] Jude relinquished his hold, and she crossed the room to the door, out

[012-028] of which she went with a set face, and into the highway. Here she began

[012-029] to saunter up and down, perversely pulling her hair into a worse

[012-030] disorder than he had caused, and unfastening several buttons of her

[012-031] gown. It was a fine Sunday morning, dry, clear and frosty, and the

[012-032] bells of Alfredston Church could be heard on the breeze from the north.

[012-033] People were going along the road, dressed in their holiday clothes;

[012-034] they were mainly lovers—such pairs as Jude and Arabella had been when

[012-035] they sported along the same track some months earlier. These

[012-036] pedestrians turned to stare at the extraordinary spectacle she now

[012-037] presented, bonnetless, her dishevelled hair blowing in the wind, her

[012-038] bodice apart, her sleeves rolled above her elbows for her work, and her

[012-039] hands reeking with melted fat. One of the passers said in mock terror:

[012-040] “Good Lord deliver us!”

[012-041] “See how he’s served me!” she cried. “Making me work Sunday mornings

[012-042] when I ought to be going to my church, and tearing my hair off my head,

[012-043] and my gown off my back!”

[012-044] Jude was exasperated, and went out to drag her in by main force. Then

[012-045] he suddenly lost his heat. Illuminated with the sense that all was over

[012-046] between them, and that it mattered not what she did, or he, her husband

[012-047] stood still, regarding her. Their lives were ruined, he thought; ruined

[012-048] by the fundamental error of their matrimonial union: that of having

[012-049] based a permanent contract on a temporary feeling which had no

[012-050] necessary connection with affinities that alone render a lifelong

[012-051] comradeship tolerable.

[012-052] “Going to ill-use me on principle, as your father ill-used your mother,

[012-053] and your father’s sister ill-used her husband?” she asked. “All you be

[012-054] a queer lot as husbands and wives!”

[012-055] Jude fixed an arrested, surprised look on her. But she said no more,

[012-056] and continued her saunter till she was tired. He left the spot, and,

[012-057] after wandering vaguely a little while, walked in the direction of

[012-058] Marygreen. Here he called upon his great-aunt, whose infirmities daily

[012-059] increased.

[012-060] “Aunt—did my father ill-use my mother, and my aunt her husband?” said

[012-061] Jude abruptly, sitting down by the fire.

[012-062] She raised her ancient eyes under the rim of the by-gone bonnet that

[012-063] she always wore. “Who’s been telling you that?” she said.

[012-064] “I have heard it spoken of, and want to know all.”

[012-065] “You med so well, I s’pose; though your wife—I reckon ’twas she—must

[012-066] have been a fool to open up that! There isn’t much to know after all.

[012-067] Your father and mother couldn’t get on together, and they parted. It

[012-068] was coming home from Alfredston market, when you were a baby—on the

[012-069] hill by the Brown House barn—that they had their last difference, and

[012-070] took leave of one another for the last time. Your mother soon

[012-071] afterwards died—she drowned herself, in short, and your father went

[012-072] away with you to South Wessex, and never came here any more.”

[012-073] Jude recalled his father’s silence about North Wessex and Jude’s

[012-074] mother, never speaking of either till his dying day.

[012-075] “It was the same with your father’s sister. Her husband offended her,

[012-076] and she so disliked living with him afterwards that she went away to

[012-077] London with her little maid. The Fawleys were not made for wedlock: it

[012-078] never seemed to sit well upon us. There’s sommat in our blood that

[012-079] won’t take kindly to the notion of being bound to do what we do readily

[012-080] enough if not bound. That’s why you ought to have hearkened to me, and

[012-081] not ha’ married.”

[012-082] “Where did Father and Mother part—by the Brown House, did you say?”

[012-083] “A little further on—where the road to Fenworth branches off, and the

[012-084] handpost stands. A gibbet once stood there not onconnected with our

[012-085] history. But let that be.”

[012-086] In the dusk of that evening Jude walked away from his old aunt’s as if

[012-087] to go home. But as soon as he reached the open down he struck out upon

[012-088] it till he came to a large round pond. The frost continued, though it

[012-089] was not particularly sharp, and the larger stars overhead came out slow

[012-090] and flickering. Jude put one foot on the edge of the ice, and then the

[012-091] other: it cracked under his weight; but this did not deter him. He

[012-092] ploughed his way inward to the centre, the ice making sharp noises as

[012-093] he went. When just about the middle he looked around him and gave a

[012-094] jump. The cracking repeated itself; but he did not go down. He jumped

[012-095] again, but the cracking had ceased. Jude went back to the edge, and

[012-096] stepped upon the ground.

[012-097] It was curious, he thought. What was he reserved for? He supposed he

[012-098] was not a sufficiently dignified person for suicide. Peaceful death

[012-099] abhorred him as a subject, and would not take him.

[012-100] What could he do of a lower kind than self-extermination; what was

[012-101] there less noble, more in keeping with his present degraded position?

[012-102] He could get drunk. Of course that was it; he had forgotten. Drinking

[012-103] was the regular, stereotyped resource of the despairing worthless. He

[012-104] began to see now why some men boozed at inns. He struck down the hill

[012-105] northwards and came to an obscure public-house. On entering and sitting

[012-106] down the sight of the picture of Samson and Delilah on the wall caused

[012-107] him to recognize the place as that he had visited with Arabella on that

[012-108] first Sunday evening of their courtship. He called for liquor and drank

[012-109] briskly for an hour or more.

[012-110] Staggering homeward late that night, with all his sense of depression

[012-111] gone, and his head fairly clear still, he began to laugh boisterously,

[012-112] and to wonder how Arabella would receive him in his new aspect. The

[012-113] house was in darkness when he entered, and in his stumbling state it

[012-114] was some time before he could get a light. Then he found that, though

[012-115] the marks of pig-dressing, of fats and scallops, were visible, the

[012-116] materials themselves had been taken away. A line written by his wife on

[012-117] the inside of an old envelope was pinned to the cotton blower of the

[012-118] fireplace:

[012-119] “_Have gone to my friends. Shall not return._”

[012-120] All the next day he remained at home, and sent off the carcase of the

[012-121] pig to Alfredston. He then cleaned up the premises, locked the door,

[012-122] put the key in a place she would know if she came back, and returned to

[012-123] his masonry at Alfredston.

[012-124] At night when he again plodded home he found she had not visited the

[012-125] house. The next day went in the same way, and the next. Then there came

[012-126] a letter from her.

[012-127] That she had gone tired of him she frankly admitted. He was such a slow

[012-128] old coach, and she did not care for the sort of life he led. There was

[012-129] no prospect of his ever bettering himself or her. She further went on

[012-130] to say that her parents had, as he knew, for some time considered the

[012-131] question of emigrating to Australia, the pig-jobbing business being a

[012-132] poor one nowadays. They had at last decided to go, and she proposed to

[012-133] go with them, if he had no objection. A woman of her sort would have

[012-134] more chance over there than in this stupid country.

[012-135] Jude replied that he had not the least objection to her going. He

[012-136] thought it a wise course, since she wished to go, and one that might be

[012-137] to the advantage of both. He enclosed in the packet containing the

[012-138] letter the money that had been realized by the sale of the pig, with

[012-139] all he had besides, which was not much.

[012-140] From that day he heard no more of her except indirectly, though her

[012-141] father and his household did not immediately leave, but waited till his

[012-142] goods and other effects had been sold off. When Jude learnt that there

[012-143] was to be an auction at the house of the Donns he packed his own

[012-144] household goods into a waggon, and sent them to her at the aforesaid

[012-145] homestead, that she might sell them with the rest, or as many of them

[012-146] as she should choose.

[012-147] He then went into lodgings at Alfredston, and saw in a shopwindow the

[012-148] little handbill announcing the sale of his father-in-law’s furniture.

[012-149] He noted its date, which came and passed without Jude’s going near the

[012-150] place, or perceiving that the traffic out of Alfredston by the southern

[012-151] road was materially increased by the auction. A few days later he

[012-152] entered a dingy broker’s shop in the main street of the town, and amid

[012-153] a heterogeneous collection of saucepans, a clothes-horse, rolling-pin,

[012-154] brass candlestick, swing looking-glass, and other things at the back of

[012-155] the shop, evidently just brought in from a sale, he perceived a framed

[012-156] photograph, which turned out to be his own portrait.

[012-157] It was one which he had had specially taken and framed by a local man

[012-158] in bird’s-eye maple, as a present for Arabella, and had duly given her

[012-159] on their wedding-day. On the back was still to be read, “_Jude to

[012-160] Arabella_,” with the date. She must have thrown it in with the rest of

[012-161] her property at the auction.

[012-162] “Oh,” said the broker, seeing him look at this and the other articles

[012-163] in the heap, and not perceiving that the portrait was of himself, “It

[012-164] is a small lot of stuff that was knocked down to me at a cottage sale

[012-165] out on the road to Marygreen. The frame is a very useful one, if you

[012-166] take out the likeness. You shall have it for a shilling.”

[012-167] The utter death of every tender sentiment in his wife, as brought home

[012-168] to him by this mute and undesigned evidence of her sale of his portrait

[012-169] and gift, was the conclusive little stroke required to demolish all

[012-170] sentiment in him. He paid the shilling, took the photograph away with

[012-171] him, and burnt it, frame and all, when he reached his lodging.

[012-172] Two or three days later he heard that Arabella and her parents had

[012-173] departed. He had sent a message offering to see her for a formal

[012-174] leave-taking, but she had said that it would be better otherwise, since

[012-175] she was bent on going, which perhaps was true. On the evening following

[012-176] their emigration, when his day’s work was done, he came out of doors

[012-177] after supper, and strolled in the starlight along the too familiar road

[012-178] towards the upland whereon had been experienced the chief emotions of

[012-179] his life. It seemed to be his own again.
