[052-001] VI

[052-002] The unnoticed lives that the pair had hitherto led began, from the day

[052-003] of the suspended wedding onwards, to be observed and discussed by other

[052-004] persons than Arabella. The society of Spring Street and the

[052-005] neighbourhood generally did not understand, and probably could not have

[052-006] been made to understand, Sue and Jude’s private minds, emotions,

[052-007] positions, and fears. The curious facts of a child coming to them

[052-008] unexpectedly, who called Jude “Father,” and Sue “Mother,” and a hitch

[052-009] in a marriage ceremony intended for quietness to be performed at a

[052-010] registrar’s office, together with rumours of the undefended cases in

[052-011] the law-courts, bore only one translation to plain minds.

[052-012] Little Time—for though he was formally turned into “Jude,” the apt

[052-013] nickname stuck to him—would come home from school in the evening, and

[052-014] repeat inquiries and remarks that had been made to him by the other

[052-015] boys; and cause Sue, and Jude when he heard them, a great deal of pain

[052-016] and sadness.

[052-017] The result was that shortly after the attempt at the registrar’s the

[052-018] pair went off—to London it was believed—for several days, hiring

[052-019] somebody to look to the boy. When they came back they let it be

[052-020] understood indirectly, and with total indifference and weariness of

[052-021] mien, that they were legally married at last. Sue, who had previously

[052-022] been called Mrs. Bridehead now openly adopted the name of Mrs. Fawley.

[052-023] Her dull, cowed, and listless manner for days seemed to substantiate

[052-024] all this.

[052-025] But the mistake (as it was called) of their going away so secretly to

[052-026] do the business, kept up much of the mystery of their lives; and they

[052-027] found that they made not such advances with their neighbours as they

[052-028] had expected to do thereby. A living mystery was not much less

[052-029] interesting than a dead scandal.

[052-030] The baker’s lad and the grocer’s boy, who at first had used to lift

[052-031] their hats gallantly to Sue when they came to execute their errands, in

[052-032] these days no longer took the trouble to render her that homage, and

[052-033] the neighbouring artizans’ wives looked straight along the pavement

[052-034] when they encountered her.

[052-035] Nobody molested them, it is true; but an oppressive atmosphere began to

[052-036] encircle their souls, particularly after their excursion to the show,

[052-037] as if that visit had brought some evil influence to bear on them. And

[052-038] their temperaments were precisely of a kind to suffer from this

[052-039] atmosphere, and to be indisposed to lighten it by vigorous and open

[052-040] statements. Their apparent attempt at reparation had come too late to

[052-041] be effective.

[052-042] The headstone and epitaph orders fell off: and two or three months

[052-043] later, when autumn came, Jude perceived that he would have to return to

[052-044] journey-work again, a course all the more unfortunate just now, in that

[052-045] he had not as yet cleared off the debt he had unavoidably incurred in

[052-046] the payment of the law-costs of the previous year.

[052-047] One evening he sat down to share the common meal with Sue and the child

[052-048] as usual. “I am thinking,” he said to her, “that I’ll hold on here no

[052-049] longer. The life suits us, certainly; but if we could get away to a

[052-050] place where we are unknown, we should be lighter hearted, and have a

[052-051] better chance. And so I am afraid we must break it up here, however

[052-052] awkward for you, poor dear!”

[052-053] Sue was always much affected at a picture of herself as an object of

[052-054] pity, and she saddened.

[052-055] “Well—I am not sorry,” said she presently. “I am much depressed by the

[052-056] way they look at me here. And you have been keeping on this house and

[052-057] furniture entirely for me and the boy! You don’t want it yourself, and

[052-058] the expense is unnecessary. But whatever we do, wherever we go, you

[052-059] won’t take him away from me, Jude dear? I could not let him go now! The

[052-060] cloud upon his young mind makes him so pathetic to me; I do hope to

[052-061] lift it some day! And he loves me so. You won’t take him away from me?”

[052-062] “Certainly I won’t, dear little girl! We’ll get nice lodgings, wherever

[052-063] we go. I shall be moving about probably—getting a job here and a job

[052-064] there.”

[052-065] “I shall do something too, of course, till—till— Well, now I can’t be

[052-066] useful in the lettering it behoves me to turn my hand to something

[052-067] else.”

[052-068] “Don’t hurry about getting employment,” he said regretfully. “I don’t

[052-069] want you to do that. I wish you wouldn’t, Sue. The boy and yourself are

[052-070] enough for you to attend to.”

[052-071] There was a knock at the door, and Jude answered it. Sue could hear the

[052-072] conversation:

[052-073] “Is Mr. Fawley at home? … Biles and Willis, the building contractors,

[052-074] sent me to know if you’ll undertake the relettering of the ten

[052-075] commandments in a little church they’ve been restoring lately in the

[052-076] country near here.”

[052-077] Jude reflected, and said he could undertake it.

[052-078] “It is not a very artistic job,” continued the messenger. “The

[052-079] clergyman is a very old-fashioned chap, and he has refused to let

[052-080] anything more be done to the church than cleaning and repairing.”

[052-081] “Excellent old man!” said Sue to herself, who was sentimentally opposed

[052-082] to the horrors of over-restoration.

[052-083] “The Ten Commandments are fixed to the east end,” the messenger went

[052-084] on, “and they want doing up with the rest of the wall there, since he

[052-085] won’t have them carted off as old materials belonging to the contractor

[052-086] in the usual way of the trade.”

[052-087] A bargain as to terms was struck, and Jude came indoors. “There, you

[052-088] see,” he said cheerfully. “One more job yet, at any rate, and you can

[052-089] help in it—at least you can try. We shall have all the church to

[052-090] ourselves, as the rest of the work is finished.”

[052-091] Next day Jude went out to the church, which was only two miles off. He

[052-092] found that what the contractor’s clerk had said was true. The tables of

[052-093] the Jewish law towered sternly over the utensils of Christian grace, as

[052-094] the chief ornament of the chancel end, in the fine dry style of the

[052-095] last century. And as their framework was constructed of ornamental

[052-096] plaster they could not be taken down for repair. A portion, crumbled by

[052-097] damp, required renewal; and when this had been done, and the whole

[052-098] cleansed, he began to renew the lettering. On the second morning Sue

[052-099] came to see what assistance she could render, and also because they

[052-100] liked to be together.

[052-101] The silence and emptiness of the building gave her confidence, and,

[052-102] standing on a safe low platform erected by Jude, which she was

[052-103] nevertheless timid at mounting, she began painting in the letters of

[052-104] the first Table while he set about mending a portion of the second. She

[052-105] was quite pleased at her powers; she had acquired them in the days she

[052-106] painted illumined texts for the church-fitting shop at Christminster.

[052-107] Nobody seemed likely to disturb them; and the pleasant twitter of

[052-108] birds, and rustle of October leafage, came in through an open window,

[052-109] and mingled with their talk.

[052-110] They were not, however, to be left thus snug and peaceful for long.

[052-111] About half-past twelve there came footsteps on the gravel without. The

[052-112] old vicar and his churchwarden entered, and, coming up to see what was

[052-113] being done, seemed surprised to discover that a young woman was

[052-114] assisting. They passed on into an aisle, at which time the door again

[052-115] opened, and another figure entered—a small one, that of little Time,

[052-116] who was crying. Sue had told him where he might find her between

[052-117] school-hours, if he wished. She came down from her perch, and said,

[052-118] “What’s the matter, my dear?”

[052-119] “I couldn’t stay to eat my dinner in school, because they said—” He

[052-120] described how some boys had taunted him about his nominal mother, and

[052-121] Sue, grieved, expressed her indignation to Jude aloft. The child went

[052-122] into the churchyard, and Sue returned to her work. Meanwhile the door

[052-123] had opened again, and there shuffled in with a businesslike air the

[052-124] white-aproned woman who cleaned the church. Sue recognized her as one

[052-125] who had friends in Spring Street, whom she visited. The church-cleaner

[052-126] looked at Sue, gaped, and lifted her hands; she had evidently

[052-127] recognized Jude’s companion as the latter had recognized her. Next came

[052-128] two ladies, and after talking to the charwoman they also moved forward,

[052-129] and as Sue stood reaching upward, watched her hand tracing the letters,

[052-130] and critically regarded her person in relief against the white wall,

[052-131] till she grew so nervous that she trembled visibly.

[052-132] They went back to where the others were standing, talking in

[052-133] undertones: and one said—Sue could not hear which—“She’s his wife, I

[052-134] suppose?”

[052-135] “Some say Yes: some say No,” was the reply from the charwoman.

[052-136] “Not? Then she ought to be, or somebody’s—that’s very clear!”

[052-137] “They’ve only been married a very few weeks, whether or no.”

[052-138] “A strange pair to be painting the Two Tables! I wonder Biles and

[052-139] Willis could think of such a thing as hiring those!”

[052-140] The churchwarden supposed that Biles and Willis knew of nothing wrong,

[052-141] and then the other, who had been talking to the old woman, explained

[052-142] what she meant by calling them strange people.

[052-143] The probable drift of the subdued conversation which followed was made

[052-144] plain by the churchwarden breaking into an anecdote, in a voice that

[052-145] everybody in the church could hear, though obviously suggested by the

[052-146] present situation:

[052-147] “Well, now, it is a curious thing, but my grandfather told me a strange

[052-148] tale of a most immoral case that happened at the painting of the

[052-149] Commandments in a church out by Gaymead—which is quite within a walk of

[052-150] this one. In them days Commandments were mostly done in gilt letters on

[052-151] a black ground, and that’s how they were out where I say, before the

[052-152] owld church was rebuilded. It must have been somewhere about a hundred

[052-153] years ago that them Commandments wanted doing up just as ours do here,

[052-154] and they had to get men from Aldbrickham to do ’em. Now they wished to

[052-155] get the job finished by a particular Sunday, so the men had to work

[052-156] late Saturday night, against their will, for overtime was not paid then

[052-157] as ’tis now. There was no true religion in the country at that date,

[052-158] neither among pa’sons, clerks, nor people, and to keep the men up to

[052-159] their work the vicar had to let ’em have plenty of drink during the

[052-160] afternoon. As evening drawed on they sent for some more themselves;

[052-161] rum, by all account. It got later and later, and they got more and more

[052-162] fuddled, till at last they went a-putting their rum-bottle and rummers

[052-163] upon the communion table, and drawed up a trestle or two, and sate

[052-164] round comfortable and poured out again right hearty bumpers. No sooner

[052-165] had they tossed off their glasses than, so the story goes, they fell

[052-166] down senseless, one and all. How long they bode so they didn’t know,

[052-167] but when they came to themselves there was a terrible thunder-storm

[052-168] a-raging, and they seemed to see in the gloom a dark figure with very

[052-169] thin legs and a curious voot, a-standing on the ladder, and finishing

[052-170] their work. When it got daylight they could see that the work was

[052-171] really finished, and couldn’t at all mind finishing it themselves. They

[052-172] went home, and the next thing they heard was that a great scandal had

[052-173] been caused in the church that Sunday morning, for when the people came

[052-174] and service began, all saw that the Ten Commandments wez painted with

[052-175] the ‘nots’ left out. Decent people wouldn’t attend service there for a

[052-176] long time, and the Bishop had to be sent for to reconsecrate the

[052-177] church. That’s the tradition as I used to hear it as a child. You must

[052-178] take it for what it is wo’th, but this case to-day has reminded me o’t,

[052-179] as I say.”
