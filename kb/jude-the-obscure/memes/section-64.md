[064-001] “Richard says he’ll have me back, and I’m bound to go! If he had

[064-002] refused, it might not have been so much my duty to—give up Jude. But—”

[064-003] She remained with her face in the bed-clothes, and Mrs. Edlin left the

[064-004] room.

[064-005] Phillotson in the interval had gone back to his friend Gillingham, who

[064-006] still sat over the supper-table. They soon rose, and walked out on the

[064-007] green to smoke awhile. A light was burning in Sue’s room, a shadow

[064-008] moving now and then across the blind.

[064-009] Gillingham had evidently been impressed with the indefinable charm of

[064-010] Sue, and after a silence he said, “Well: you’ve all but got her again

[064-011] at last. She can’t very well go a second time. The pear has dropped

[064-012] into your hand.”

[064-013] “Yes! … I suppose I am right in taking her at her word. I confess there

[064-014] seems a touch of selfishness in it. Apart from her being what she is,

[064-015] of course, a luxury for a fogey like me, it will set me right in the

[064-016] eyes of the clergy and orthodox laity, who have never forgiven me for

[064-017] letting her go. So I may get back in some degree into my old track.”

[064-018] “Well—if you’ve got any sound reason for marrying her again, do it now

[064-019] in God’s name! I was always against your opening the cage-door and

[064-020] letting the bird go in such an obviously suicidal way. You might have

[064-021] been a school inspector by this time, or a reverend, if you hadn’t been

[064-022] so weak about her.”

[064-023] “I did myself irreparable damage—I know it.”

[064-024] “Once you’ve got her housed again, stick to her.”

[064-025] Phillotson was more evasive to-night. He did not care to admit clearly

[064-026] that his taking Sue to him again had at bottom nothing to do with

[064-027] repentance of letting her go, but was, primarily, a human instinct

[064-028] flying in the face of custom and profession. He said, “Yes—I shall do

[064-029] that. I know woman better now. Whatever justice there was in releasing

[064-030] her, there was little logic, for one holding my views on other

[064-031] subjects.”

[064-032] Gillingham looked at him, and wondered whether it would ever happen

[064-033] that the reactionary spirit induced by the world’s sneers and his own

[064-034] physical wishes would make Phillotson more orthodoxly cruel to her than

[064-035] he had erstwhile been informally and perversely kind.

[064-036] “I perceive it won’t do to give way to impulse,” Phillotson resumed,

[064-037] feeling more and more every minute the necessity of acting up to his

[064-038] position. “I flew in the face of the Church’s teaching; but I did it

[064-039] without malice prepense. Women are so strange in their influence that

[064-040] they tempt you to misplaced kindness. However, I know myself better

[064-041] now. A little judicious severity, perhaps…”

[064-042] “Yes; but you must tighten the reins by degrees only. Don’t be too

[064-043] strenuous at first. She’ll come to any terms in time.”

[064-044] The caution was unnecessary, though Phillotson did not say so. “I

[064-045] remember what my vicar at Shaston said, when I left after the row that

[064-046] was made about my agreeing to her elopement. ‘The only thing you can do

[064-047] to retrieve your position and hers is to admit your error in not

[064-048] restraining her with a wise and strong hand, and to get her back again

[064-049] if she’ll come, and be firm in the future.’ But I was so headstrong at

[064-050] that time that I paid no heed. And that after the divorce she should

[064-051] have thought of doing so I did not dream.”

[064-052] The gate of Mrs. Edlin’s cottage clicked, and somebody began crossing

[064-053] in the direction of the school. Phillotson said “Good-night.”

[064-054] “Oh, is that Mr. Phillotson,” said Mrs. Edlin. “I was going over to see

[064-055] ’ee. I’ve been upstairs with her, helping her to unpack her things; and

[064-056] upon my word, sir, I don’t think this ought to be!”

[064-057] “What—the wedding?”

[064-058] “Yes. She’s forcing herself to it, poor dear little thing; and you’ve

[064-059] no notion what she’s suffering. I was never much for religion nor

[064-060] against it, but it can’t be right to let her do this, and you ought to

[064-061] persuade her out of it. Of course everybody will say it was very good

[064-062] and forgiving of ’ee to take her to ’ee again. But for my part I

[064-063] don’t.”

[064-064] “It’s her wish, and I am willing,” said Phillotson with grave reserve,

[064-065] opposition making him illogically tenacious now. “A great piece of

[064-066] laxity will be rectified.”

[064-067] “I don’t believe it. She’s his wife if anybody’s. She’s had three

[064-068] children by him, and he loves her dearly; and it’s a wicked shame to

[064-069] egg her on to this, poor little quivering thing! She’s got nobody on

[064-070] her side. The one man who’d be her friend the obstinate creature won’t

[064-071] allow to come near her. What first put her into this mood o’ mind, I

[064-072] wonder!”

[064-073] “I can’t tell. Not I certainly. It is all voluntary on her part. Now

[064-074] that’s all I have to say.” Phillotson spoke stiffly. “You’ve turned

[064-075] round, Mrs. Edlin. It is unseemly of you!”

[064-076] “Well, I knowed you’d be affronted at what I had to say; but I don’t

[064-077] mind that. The truth’s the truth.”

[064-078] “I’m not affronted, Mrs. Edlin. You’ve been too kind a neighbour for

[064-079] that. But I must be allowed to know what’s best for myself and Susanna.

[064-080] I suppose you won’t go to church with us, then?”

[064-081] “No. Be hanged if I can… I don’t know what the times be coming to!

[064-082] Matrimony have growed to be that serious in these days that one really

[064-083] do feel afeard to move in it at all. In my time we took it more

[064-084] careless; and I don’t know that we was any the worse for it! When I and

[064-085] my poor man were jined in it we kept up the junketing all the week, and

[064-086] drunk the parish dry, and had to borrow half a crown to begin

[064-087] housekeeping!”

[064-088] When Mrs. Edlin had gone back to her cottage Phillotson spoke moodily.

[064-089] “I don’t know whether I ought to do it—at any rate quite so rapidly.”

[064-090] “Why?”

[064-091] “If she is really compelling herself to this against her

[064-092] instincts—merely from this new sense of duty or religion—I ought

[064-093] perhaps to let her wait a bit.”

[064-094] “Now you’ve got so far you ought not to back out of it. That’s my

[064-095] opinion.”

[064-096] “I can’t very well put it off now; that’s true. But I had a qualm when

[064-097] she gave that little cry at sight of the licence.”

[064-098] “Now, never you have qualms, old boy. I mean to give her away to-morrow

[064-099] morning, and you mean to take her. It has always been on my conscience

[064-100] that I didn’t urge more objections to your letting her go, and now

[064-101] we’ve got to this stage I shan’t be content if I don’t help you to set

[064-102] the matter right.”

[064-103] Phillotson nodded, and seeing how staunch his friend was, became more

[064-104] frank. “No doubt when it gets known what I’ve done I shall be thought a

[064-105] soft fool by many. But they don’t know Sue as I do. Though so elusive,

[064-106] hers is such an honest nature at bottom that I don’t think she has ever

[064-107] done anything against her conscience. The fact of her having lived with

[064-108] Fawley goes for nothing. At the time she left me for him she thought

[064-109] she was quite within her right. Now she thinks otherwise.”

[064-110] The next morning came, and the self-sacrifice of the woman on the altar

[064-111] of what she was pleased to call her principles was acquiesced in by

[064-112] these two friends, each from his own point of view. Phillotson went

[064-113] across to the Widow Edlin’s to fetch Sue a few minutes after eight

[064-114] o’clock. The fog of the previous day or two on the low-lands had

[064-115] travelled up here by now, and the trees on the green caught armfuls,

[064-116] and turned them into showers of big drops. The bride was waiting,

[064-117] ready; bonnet and all on. She had never in her life looked so much like

[064-118] the lily her name connoted as she did in that pallid morning light.

[064-119] Chastened, world-weary, remorseful, the strain on her nerves had preyed

[064-120] upon her flesh and bones, and she appeared smaller in outline than she

[064-121] had formerly done, though Sue had not been a large woman in her days of

[064-122] rudest health.

[064-123] “Prompt,” said the schoolmaster, magnanimously taking her hand. But he

[064-124] checked his impulse to kiss her, remembering her start of yesterday,

[064-125] which unpleasantly lingered in his mind.

[064-126] Gillingham joined them, and they left the house, Widow Edlin continuing

[064-127] steadfast in her refusal to assist in the ceremony.

[064-128] “Where is the church?” said Sue. She had not lived there for any length

[064-129] of time since the old church was pulled down, and in her preoccupation

[064-130] forgot the new one.

[064-131] “Up here,” said Phillotson; and presently the tower loomed large and

[064-132] solemn in the fog. The vicar had already crossed to the building, and

[064-133] when they entered he said pleasantly: “We almost want candles.”

[064-134] “You do—wish me to be yours, Richard?” gasped Sue in a whisper.

[064-135] “Certainly, dear; above all things in the world.”

[064-136] Sue said no more; and for the second or third time he felt he was not

[064-137] quite following out the humane instinct which had induced him to let

[064-138] her go.

[064-139] There they stood, five altogether: the parson, the clerk, the couple,

[064-140] and Gillingham; and the holy ordinance was resolemnized forthwith. In

[064-141] the nave of the edifice were two or three villagers, and when the

[064-142] clergyman came to the words, “What God hath joined,” a woman’s voice

[064-143] from among these was heard to utter audibly:

[064-144] “God hath jined indeed!”

[064-145] It was like a re-enactment by the ghosts of their former selves of the

[064-146] similar scene which had taken place at Melchester years before. When

[064-147] the books were signed the vicar congratulated the husband and wife on

[064-148] having performed a noble, and righteous, and mutually forgiving act.

[064-149] “All’s well that ends well,” he said smiling. “May you long be happy

[064-150] together, after thus having been ‘saved as by fire.’”

[064-151] They came down the nearly empty building, and crossed to the

[064-152] schoolhouse. Gillingham wanted to get home that night, and left early.

[064-153] He, too, congratulated the couple. “Now,” he said in parting from

[064-154] Phillotson, who walked out a little way, “I shall be able to tell the

[064-155] people in your native place a good round tale; and they’ll all say

[064-156] ‘Well done,’ depend on it.”

[064-157] When the schoolmaster got back Sue was making a pretence of doing some

[064-158] housewifery as if she lived there. But she seemed timid at his

[064-159] approach, and compunction wrought on him at sight of it.

[064-160] “Of course, my dear, I shan’t expect to intrude upon your personal

[064-161] privacy any more than I did before,” he said gravely. “It is for our

[064-162] good socially to do this, and that’s its justification, if it was not

[064-163] my reason.” Sue brightened a little.

[064-166] Christminster—far from the precincts of St. Silas’ where he had

[064-167] formerly lived, which saddened him to sickness. The rain was coming

[064-168] down. A woman in shabby black stood on the doorstep talking to Jude,

[064-169] who held the door in his hand.

[064-170] “I am lonely, destitute, and houseless—that’s what I am! Father has

[064-171] turned me out of doors after borrowing every penny I’d got, to put it

[064-172] into his business, and then accusing me of laziness when I was only

[064-173] waiting for a situation. I am at the mercy of the world! If you can’t

[064-174] take me and help me, Jude, I must go to the workhouse, or to something

[064-175] worse. Only just now two undergraduates winked at me as I came along.

[064-176] ’Tis hard for a woman to keep virtuous where there’s so many young

[064-177] men!”

[064-178] The woman in the rain who spoke thus was Arabella, the evening being

[064-179] that of the day after Sue’s remarriage with Phillotson.

[064-180] “I am sorry for you, but I am only in lodgings,” said Jude coldly.
