[015-001] At length he did receive a message from the stone-mason’s yard—that a

[015-002] job was waiting for him. It was his first encouragement, and he closed

[015-003] with the offer promptly.

[015-004] He was young and strong, or he never could have executed with such zest

[015-005] the undertakings to which he now applied himself, since they involved

[015-006] reading most of the night after working all the day. First he bought a

[015-007] shaded lamp for four and six-pence, and obtained a good light. Then he

[015-008] got pens, paper, and such other necessary books as he had been unable

[015-009] to obtain elsewhere. Then, to the consternation of his landlady, he

[015-010] shifted all the furniture of his room—a single one for living and

[015-011] sleeping—rigged up a curtain on a rope across the middle, to make a

[015-012] double chamber out of one, hung up a thick blind that nobody should

[015-013] know how he was curtailing the hours of sleep, laid out his books, and

[015-014] sat down.

[015-015] Having been deeply encumbered by marrying, getting a cottage, and

[015-016] buying the furniture which had disappeared in the wake of his wife, he

[015-017] had never been able to save any money since the time of those

[015-018] disastrous ventures, and till his wages began to come in he was obliged

[015-019] to live in the narrowest way. After buying a book or two he could not

[015-020] even afford himself a fire; and when the nights reeked with the raw and

[015-021] cold air from the Meadows he sat over his lamp in a great-coat, hat,

[015-022] and woollen gloves.

[015-023] From his window he could perceive the spire of the cathedral, and the

[015-024] ogee dome under which resounded the great bell of the city. The tall

[015-025] tower, tall belfry windows, and tall pinnacles of the college by the

[015-026] bridge he could also get a glimpse of by going to the staircase. These

[015-027] objects he used as stimulants when his faith in the future was dim.

[015-028] Like enthusiasts in general he made no inquiries into details of

[015-029] procedure. Picking up general notions from casual acquaintance, he

[015-030] never dwelt upon them. For the present, he said to himself, the one

[015-031] thing necessary was to get ready by accumulating money and knowledge,

[015-032] and await whatever chances were afforded to such an one of becoming a

[015-033] son of the University. “For wisdom is a defence, and money is a

[015-034] defence; but the excellency of knowledge is, that wisdom giveth life to

[015-035] them that have it.” His desire absorbed him, and left no part of him to

[015-036] weigh its practicability.

[015-037] At this time he received a nervously anxious letter from his poor old

[015-038] aunt, on the subject which had previously distressed her—a fear that

[015-039] Jude would not be strong-minded enough to keep away from his cousin Sue

[015-040] Bridehead and her relations. Sue’s father, his aunt believed, had gone

[015-041] back to London, but the girl remained at Christminster. To make her

[015-042] still more objectionable, she was an artist or designer of some sort in

[015-043] what was called an ecclesiastical warehouse, which was a perfect

[015-044] seed-bed of idolatry, and she was no doubt abandoned to mummeries on

[015-045] that account—if not quite a Papist. (Miss Drusilla Fawley was of her

[015-046] date, Evangelical.)

[015-047] As Jude was rather on an intellectual track than a theological, this

[015-048] news of Sue’s probable opinions did not much influence him one way or

[015-049] the other, but the clue to her whereabouts was decidedly interesting.

[015-050] With an altogether singular pleasure he walked at his earliest spare

[015-051] minutes past the shops answering to his great-aunt’s description; and

[015-052] beheld in one of them a young girl sitting behind a desk, who was

[015-053] suspiciously like the original of the portrait. He ventured to enter on

[015-054] a trivial errand, and having made his purchase lingered on the scene.

[015-055] The shop seemed to be kept entirely by women. It contained Anglican

[015-056] books, stationery, texts, and fancy goods: little plaster angels on

[015-057] brackets, Gothic-framed pictures of saints, ebony crosses that were

[015-058] almost crucifixes, prayer-books that were almost missals. He felt very

[015-059] shy of looking at the girl in the desk; she was so pretty that he could

[015-060] not believe it possible that she should belong to him. Then she spoke

[015-061] to one of the two older women behind the counter; and he recognized in

[015-062] the accents certain qualities of his own voice; softened and sweetened,

[015-063] but his own. What was she doing? He stole a glance round. Before her

[015-064] lay a piece of zinc, cut to the shape of a scroll three or four feet

[015-065] long, and coated with a dead-surface paint on one side. Hereon she was

[015-066] designing or illuminating, in characters of Church text, the single

[015-067] word

[015-068] [A L L E L U J A]

[015-069] “A sweet, saintly, Christian business, hers!” thought he.

[015-070] Her presence here was now fairly enough explained, her skill in work of

[015-071] this sort having no doubt been acquired from her father’s occupation as

[015-072] an ecclesiastical worker in metal. The lettering on which she was

[015-073] engaged was clearly intended to be fixed up in some chancel to assist

[015-074] devotion.

[015-075] He came out. It would have been easy to speak to her there and then,

[015-076] but it seemed scarcely honourable towards his aunt to disregard her

[015-077] request so incontinently. She had used him roughly, but she had brought

[015-078] him up: and the fact of her being powerless to control him lent a

[015-079] pathetic force to a wish that would have been inoperative as an

[015-080] argument.

[015-081] So Jude gave no sign. He would not call upon Sue just yet. He had other

[015-082] reasons against doing so when he had walked away. She seemed so dainty

[015-083] beside himself in his rough working-jacket and dusty trousers that he

[015-084] felt he was as yet unready to encounter her, as he had felt about Mr.

[015-085] Phillotson. And how possible it was that she had inherited the

[015-086] antipathies of her family, and would scorn him, as far as a Christian

[015-087] could, particularly when he had told her that unpleasant part of his

[015-088] history which had resulted in his becoming enchained to one of her own

[015-089] sex whom she would certainly not admire.

[015-090] Thus he kept watch over her, and liked to feel she was there. The

[015-091] consciousness of her living presence stimulated him. But she remained

[015-092] more or less an ideal character, about whose form he began to weave

[015-093] curious and fantastic day-dreams.

[015-094] Between two and three weeks afterwards Jude was engaged with some more

[015-095] men, outside Crozier College in Old-time Street, in getting a block of

[015-096] worked freestone from a waggon across the pavement, before hoisting it

[015-097] to the parapet which they were repairing. Standing in position the head

[015-098] man said, “Spaik when he heave! He-ho!” And they heaved.

[015-099] All of a sudden, as he lifted, his cousin stood close to his elbow,

[015-100] pausing a moment on the bend of her foot till the obstructing object

[015-101] should have been removed. She looked right into his face with liquid,

[015-102] untranslatable eyes, that combined, or seemed to him to combine,

[015-103] keenness with tenderness, and mystery with both, their expression, as

[015-104] well as that of her lips, taking its life from some words just spoken

[015-105] to a companion, and being carried on into his face quite unconsciously.

[015-106] She no more observed his presence than that of the dust-motes which his

[015-107] manipulations raised into the sunbeams.

[015-108] His closeness to her was so suggestive that he trembled, and turned his

[015-109] face away with a shy instinct to prevent her recognizing him, though as

[015-110] she had never once seen him she could not possibly do so; and might

[015-111] very well never have heard even his name. He could perceive that though

[015-112] she was a country-girl at bottom, a latter girlhood of some years in

[015-113] London, and a womanhood here, had taken all rawness out of her.

[015-114] When she was gone he continued his work, reflecting on her. He had been

[015-115] so caught by her influence that he had taken no count of her general

[015-116] mould and build. He remembered now that she was not a large figure,

[015-117] that she was light and slight, of the type dubbed elegant. That was

[015-118] about all he had seen. There was nothing statuesque in her; all was

[015-119] nervous motion. She was mobile, living, yet a painter might not have

[015-120] called her handsome or beautiful. But the much that she was surprised

[015-121] him. She was quite a long way removed from the rusticity that was his.

[015-122] How could one of his cross-grained, unfortunate, almost accursed stock,

[015-123] have contrived to reach this pitch of niceness? London had done it, he

[015-124] supposed.

[015-125] From this moment the emotion which had been accumulating in his breast

[015-126] as the bottled-up effect of solitude and the poetized locality he dwelt

[015-127] in, insensibly began to precipitate itself on this half-visionary form;

[015-128] and he perceived that, whatever his obedient wish in a contrary

[015-129] direction, he would soon be unable to resist the desire to make himself

[015-130] known to her.

[015-131] He affected to think of her quite in a family way, since there were

[015-132] crushing reasons why he should not and could not think of her in any

[015-133] other.

[015-134] The first reason was that he was married, and it would be wrong. The

[015-135] second was that they were cousins. It was not well for cousins to fall

[015-136] in love even when circumstances seemed to favour the passion. The

[015-137] third: even were he free, in a family like his own where marriage

[015-138] usually meant a tragic sadness, marriage with a blood-relation would

[015-139] duplicate the adverse conditions, and a tragic sadness might be

[015-140] intensified to a tragic horror.

[015-141] Therefore, again, he would have to think of Sue with only a relation’s

[015-142] mutual interest in one belonging to him; regard her in a practical way

[015-143] as some one to be proud of; to talk and nod to; later on, to be invited

[015-144] to tea by, the emotion spent on her being rigorously that of a kinsman

[015-145] and well-wisher. So would she be to him a kindly star, an elevating

[015-146] power, a companion in Anglican worship, a tender friend.

[015-147] III

[015-148] But under the various deterrent influences Jude’s instinct was to

[015-149] approach her timidly, and the next Sunday he went to the morning

[015-150] service in the Cathedral church of Cardinal College to gain a further

[015-151] view of her, for he had found that she frequently attended there.

[015-152] She did not come, and he awaited her in the afternoon, which was finer.

[015-153] He knew that if she came at all she would approach the building along

[015-154] the eastern side of the great green quadrangle from which it was

[015-155] accessible, and he stood in a corner while the bell was going. A few

[015-156] minutes before the hour for service she appeared as one of the figures

[015-157] walking along under the college walls, and at sight of her he advanced

[015-158] up the side opposite, and followed her into the building, more than

[015-159] ever glad that he had not as yet revealed himself. To see her, and to

[015-160] be himself unseen and unknown, was enough for him at present.

[015-161] He lingered awhile in the vestibule, and the service was some way

[015-162] advanced when he was put into a seat. It was a louring, mournful, still

[015-163] afternoon, when a religion of some sort seems a necessity to ordinary

[015-164] practical men, and not only a luxury of the emotional and leisured

[015-165] classes. In the dim light and the baffling glare of the clerestory

[015-166] windows he could discern the opposite worshippers indistinctly only,

[015-167] but he saw that Sue was among them. He had not long discovered the

[015-168] exact seat that she occupied when the chanting of the 119th Psalm in

[015-169] which the choir was engaged reached its second part, _In quo corriget_,

[015-170] the organ changing to a pathetic Gregorian tune as the singers gave

[015-171] forth:

[015-172] Wherewithal shall a young man cleanse his way?

[015-173] It was the very question that was engaging Jude’s attention at this

[015-174] moment. What a wicked worthless fellow he had been to give vent as he

[015-175] had done to an animal passion for a woman, and allow it to lead to such

[015-176] disastrous consequences; then to think of putting an end to himself;

[015-177] then to go recklessly and get drunk. The great waves of pedal music

[015-178] tumbled round the choir, and, nursed on the supernatural as he had

[015-179] been, it is not wonderful that he could hardly believe that the psalm

[015-180] was not specially set by some regardful Providence for this moment of

[015-181] his first entry into the solemn building. And yet it was the ordinary

[015-182] psalm for the twenty-fourth evening of the month.
