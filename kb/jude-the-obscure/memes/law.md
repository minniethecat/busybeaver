# Law

Law in *Jude the Obscure* is not an abstract concept but a concrete institutional force that governs the characters' most intimate lives. Marriage law, in particular, functions as the structural antagonist — trapping characters in unwanted unions, preventing legitimate divorce, defining the boundaries of acceptable domestic arrangement, and ultimately contributing to the novel's catastrophic climax. Hardy presents law not as a neutral arbiter of justice but as an instrument of social control that systematically subordinates human happiness to institutional order.

## Definition

In the novel, "law" operates on three interconnected levels. The primary level is Victorian marriage and divorce law — the legal system that binds Jude to Arabella, Sue to Phillotson, and prevents Jude and Sue from achieving a legitimate union. The secondary level is the unwritten but equally binding "laws of nature" (024-090) that Hardy uses to interrogate whether human institutions align with or violate natural impulses. The tertiary level is social convention functioning as quasi-legal force — "the screens and subtleties of convention" (022-150) that punish transgression as surely as any court.

The novel opens with a cosmic framing that establishes the law theme at its deepest register. Young Jude, punished by Farmer Troutham for letting birds eat his crops, experiences "the perception of the flaw in the terrestrial scheme, by which what was good for God's birds was bad for God's gardener" (002-112). This early intuition — that the world is governed by conflicting and unjust laws — becomes the novel's governing insight. What Jude perceives as a child about the natural order, he will spend his adult life discovering about the legal order.

## In *Jude the Obscure*

### Manifestation

The novel presents marriage law as a trap. Arabella exploits the legal bond of marriage to ensnare Jude — a union entered under the false pretense of pregnancy. Jude and Sue's later attempt to live outside legal marriage is punished by social ostracism, poverty, and ultimately tragedy. Phillotson remains legally entitled to Sue despite her clear wish to leave him. And the near-impossibility of divorce in Victorian England means that none of these bonds can be dissolved.

Sue articulates the novel's most direct legal critique in her confrontation with Phillotson:

> "But I do ask it! Domestic laws should be made according to temperaments, which should be classified. If people are at all peculiar in character they have to suffer from the very rules that produce" (038-170)

When Phillotson protests that her request is "irregular" (038-169), Sue's response cuts to the novel's core argument:

> "What is the use of thinking of laws and ordinances," she burst out (038-175)

Jude's relationship with Sue is permanently shadowed by his legal bond to Arabella, vividly rendered as a form of imprisonment:

> "There was not the least doubt that from his own orthodox point of view the situation was growing immoral. For Sue to be the loved one of a man who was licensed by the laws of his country to love Arabella and none other unto his life's end, was a pretty bad second beginning" (017-014)

Sue's most devastating critique compares legal marriage to a physical trap, deliberately echoing the novel's recurring imagery of the rabbit trap:

> "What Arabella has been saying to me has made me feel more than ever how hopelessly vulgar an institution legal marriage is — a sort of trap to catch a man — I can't bear to think of it." (047-029)

The trap metaphor extends to Jude's professional life as well. The aborted rabbit caught in a steel gin becomes a symbol for all the novel's trapped characters. Jude himself meditates on cruelty when he hears "the faint click of the trap" (037-058) and reflects that it is wrong of farmers "to be allowed to set these steel traps, ought they!" (037-069). The law, in Hardy's vision, is exactly such a trap — set by society, sprung on the unwary, and inescapable once caught.

### The Fawley Family Curse

The novel deepens its legal critique by giving it a hereditary, almost supernatural dimension. The Fawley family is afflicted by what Jude recognizes as a biological as well as legal curse:

> "The third: even were he free, in a family like his own where marriage usually meant a tragic sadness, marriage with a blood-relation would duplicate the adverse conditions, and a tragic sadness might be" (015-137)

This "curse" is not truly supernatural — it is Hardy's shorthand for the structural reality that the Fawleys, as a working-class family with limited means, are especially vulnerable to the legal trap of marriage. They cannot afford divorce, they cannot weather scandal, and their marriages — like Jude's own — are entered rashly under pressure of circumstance rather than chosen freely. Jude explicitly reflects on his own family's unhappy history with matrimony: "How could one of his cross-grained, unfortunate, almost accursed stock" (015-122) ever hope for happiness within the institution of marriage?

### Sue's Legal Identity

Sue Bridehead occupies a uniquely ambiguous legal position throughout the novel. Married to Phillotson, she is legally his property; living with Jude, she is legally nothing — neither wife nor widow nor single woman. She articulates this legal non-existence with painful clarity:

> "I am called Mrs. Richard Phillotson, living a calm wedded life with my counterpart of that name. But I am not really Mrs. Richard Phillotson, but a woman tossed about, all alone, with aberrant passions, and unaccountable antipathies" (035-170)

The law constructs an identity for Sue — "Mrs. Richard Phillotson" — that has no correspondence to her actual existence. She is simultaneously bound by a legal identity she does not inhabit and denied the legal identity she needs to live with Jude. Phillotson, in a moment of painful recognition, acknowledges the law's absurdity:

> "But Sue, she was, after all, my legal wife, if not —" (042-130)

The unfinished sentence captures the gap between legal form and human reality that the novel relentlessly exposes. Sue is Phillotson's "legal wife" — but the sentence cannot complete itself because the "if not" contains everything that matters: if not in love, if not in fact, if not in any sense that the law should recognize.

### Narrative Trajectory

Law enters the novel through Jude's marriage to Arabella (Part First), a union entered under false pretenses that legally binds him for life. Hardy frames this first marriage as a fundamental error of legal design:

> "The immediate reason of his marriage had proved to be non-existent. But the marriage remained." (010-186)

This devastating sentence — the marriage remains despite the reason for it having vanished — encapsulates the novel's entire critique of matrimonial law. The marriage is a legal fact that persists regardless of the facts that produced it.

The law intensifies in Parts Third and Fourth as Sue marries Phillotson — a legal act she immediately regrets, precipitated by that "unhappy visit to her on that sad night" (023-086). It becomes the central obstacle in Part Fifth, preventing Jude and Sue from marrying and rendering their children legally illegitimate. Their attempt to signal compliance by publishing banns captures the absurdity of legal form without substance:

> "Jude Fawley signed the form of notice, Sue looking over his shoulder" (048-171)

By Part Sixth, the law's full destructive force is realized. The children's deaths are partially attributable to the family's social and legal vulnerability — Little Father Time, the child who asks "why do people have 'em?" (058-016) and is told "because it is a law of nature" (058-016), enacts a tragedy that is also a legal tragedy. Sue's final return to Phillotson represents the law's ultimate victory over personal desire — she becomes "legally married, to save appearances" (063-011) in a union Jude insists has no moral validity:

> "We still love — you as well as I — know it, Sue! Therefore our marriage is not." (063-013)

### Law and Nature

Hardy develops a crucial tension between human law and natural law. When Sue declares herself "Outside all laws except gravitation and germination" (024-030), she articulates a fantasy of escape from human legal systems into a purely natural order. But the novel demonstrates the impossibility of this escape. The "inexorable laws of nature" (024-090) are no kinder than the laws of man — they are merely indifferent. Little Father Time's suicide and the deaths of the children represent the novel's bleakest statement about the intersection of law and nature: the children are too many because the legal system has made their parents' situation unsustainable.

The novel's final irony is that Arabella, who has manipulated legal marriage throughout, triumphs over it in the end. Her return to Jude's bedside and their remarriage — "It is true religion! Ha—ha—ha!" (067-091) — is a mockery of the legal institution that has destroyed everyone it was supposed to protect.

## Critical Interpretation

Hardy wrote *Jude the Obscure* in the context of the Matrimonial Causes Act of 1857, which had created civil divorce but maintained a gendered double standard: men could divorce for adultery alone; women had to prove adultery plus cruelty, desertion, or additional grounds. The costs — approximately forty to fifty pounds for a full divorce suit — placed the remedy permanently beyond the reach of working-class characters like Jude. The novel's legal critique was radical for its time: Sue's argument for domestic law reform anticipates twentieth-century no-fault divorce debates by nearly a century.

The novel's treatment of law as a force that "licenses" love while denying its reality reveals the gap between legal form and human experience that Hardy saw as fundamental to social injustice. Contemporary reviewers attacked the novel for its critique of marriage; the *Pall Mall Gazette* accused Hardy of advocating free love. But the novel does not argue against marriage as such — it argues against a legal system that makes marriage permanent regardless of circumstance, that punishes those born into the wrong family or the wrong class, and that protects the legal form of marriage at the expense of its human substance.

Modern legal and feminist criticism has read the novel as a structural analysis of how marriage law functions as an instrument of patriarchal control. Sue's body, once legally transferred to Phillotson, can never fully be her own again — even when she leaves him, her legal identity remains defined by her marital status. The novel's insistence that "a marriage is dissolved when it is dissolved" (044-147), regardless of what the law says, is Hardy's most radical legal proposition: that human relationships exist independently of legal recognition and should not be destroyed by legal fiction.
