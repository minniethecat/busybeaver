[018-001] “Why must you leave Christminster?” he said regretfully. “How can you

[018-002] do otherwise than cling to a city in whose history such men as Newman,

[018-003] Pusey, Ward, Keble, loom so large!”

[018-004] “Yes—they do. Though how large do they loom in the history of the

[018-005] world? … What a funny reason for caring to stay! I should never have

[018-006] thought of it!” She laughed.

[018-007] “Well—I must go,” she continued. “Miss Fontover, one of the partners

[018-008] whom I serve, is offended with me, and I with her; and it is best to

[018-009] go.”

[018-010] “How did that happen?”

[018-011] “She broke some statuary of mine.”

[018-012] “Oh? Wilfully?”

[018-013] “Yes. She found it in my room, and though it was my property she threw

[018-014] it on the floor and stamped on it, because it was not according to her

[018-015] taste, and ground the arms and the head of one of the figures all to

[018-016] bits with her heel—a horrid thing!”

[018-017] “Too Catholic-Apostolic for her, I suppose? No doubt she called them

[018-018] popish images and talked of the invocation of saints.”

[018-019] “No… No, she didn’t do that. She saw the matter quite differently.”

[018-020] “Ah! Then I am surprised!”

[018-021] “Yes. It was for quite some other reason that she didn’t like my

[018-022] patron-saints. So I was led to retort upon her; and the end of it was

[018-023] that I resolved not to stay, but to get into an occupation in which I

[018-024] shall be more independent.”

[018-025] “Why don’t you try teaching again? You once did, I heard.”

[018-026] “I never thought of resuming it; for I was getting on as an

[018-027] art-designer.”

[018-028] “_Do_ let me ask Mr. Phillotson to let you try your hand in his school?

[018-029] If you like it, and go to a training college, and become a first-class

[018-030] certificated mistress, you get twice as large an income as any designer

[018-031] or church artist, and twice as much freedom.”

[018-032] “Well—ask him. Now I must go in. Good-bye, dear Jude! I am so glad we

[018-033] have met at last. We needn’t quarrel because our parents did, need we?”

[018-034] Jude did not like to let her see quite how much he agreed with her, and

[018-035] went his way to the remote street in which he had his lodging.

[018-036] To keep Sue Bridehead near him was now a desire which operated without

[018-037] regard of consequences, and the next evening he again set out for

[018-038] Lumsdon, fearing to trust to the persuasive effects of a note only. The

[018-039] school-master was unprepared for such a proposal.

[018-040] “What I rather wanted was a second year’s transfer, as it is called,”

[018-041] he said. “Of course your cousin would do, personally; but she has had

[018-042] no experience. Oh—she has, has she? Does she really think of adopting

[018-043] teaching as a profession?”

[018-044] Jude said she was disposed to do so, he thought, and his ingenious

[018-045] arguments on her natural fitness for assisting Mr. Phillotson, of which

[018-046] Jude knew nothing whatever, so influenced the schoolmaster that he said

[018-047] he would engage her, assuring Jude as a friend that unless his cousin

[018-048] really meant to follow on in the same course, and regarded this step as

[018-049] the first stage of an apprenticeship, of which her training in a normal

[018-050] school would be the second stage, her time would be wasted quite, the

[018-051] salary being merely nominal.

[018-052] The day after this visit Phillotson received a letter from Jude,

[018-053] containing the information that he had again consulted his cousin, who

[018-054] took more and more warmly to the idea of tuition; and that she had

[018-055] agreed to come. It did not occur for a moment to the schoolmaster and

[018-056] recluse that Jude’s ardour in promoting the arrangement arose from any

[018-057] other feelings towards Sue than the instinct of co-operation common

[018-058] among members of the same family.

[018-059] V

[018-060] The schoolmaster sat in his homely dwelling attached to the school,

[018-061] both being modern erections; and he looked across the way at the old

[018-062] house in which his teacher Sue had a lodging. The arrangement had been

[018-063] concluded very quickly. A pupil-teacher who was to have been

[018-064] transferred to Mr. Phillotson’s school had failed him, and Sue had been

[018-065] taken as stop-gap. All such provisional arrangements as these could

[018-066] only last till the next annual visit of H.M. Inspector, whose approval

[018-067] was necessary to make them permanent. Having taught for some two years

[018-068] in London, though she had abandoned that vocation of late, Miss

[018-069] Bridehead was not exactly a novice, and Phillotson thought there would

[018-070] be no difficulty in retaining her services, which he already wished to

[018-071] do, though she had only been with him three or four weeks. He had found

[018-072] her quite as bright as Jude had described her; and what

[018-073] master-tradesman does not wish to keep an apprentice who saves him half

[018-074] his labour?

[018-075] It was a little over half-past eight o’clock in the morning and he was

[018-076] waiting to see her cross the road to the school, when he would follow.

[018-077] At twenty minutes to nine she did cross, a light hat tossed on her

[018-078] head; and he watched her as a curiosity. A new emanation, which had

[018-079] nothing to do with her skill as a teacher, seemed to surround her this

[018-080] morning. He went to the school also, and Sue remained governing her

[018-081] class at the other end of the room, all day under his eye. She

[018-082] certainly was an excellent teacher.

[018-083] It was part of his duty to give her private lessons in the evening, and

[018-084] some article in the Code made it necessary that a respectable, elderly

[018-085] woman should be present at these lessons when the teacher and the

[018-086] taught were of different sexes. Richard Phillotson thought of the

[018-087] absurdity of the regulation in this case, when he was old enough to be

[018-088] the girl’s father; but he faithfully acted up to it; and sat down with

[018-089] her in a room where Mrs. Hawes, the widow at whose house Sue lodged,

[018-090] occupied herself with sewing. The regulation was, indeed, not easy to

[018-091] evade, for there was no other sitting-room in the dwelling.

[018-092] Sometimes as she figured—it was arithmetic that they were working

[018-093] at—she would involuntarily glance up with a little inquiring smile at

[018-094] him, as if she assumed that, being the master, he must perceive all

[018-095] that was passing in her brain, as right or wrong. Phillotson was not

[018-096] really thinking of the arithmetic at all, but of her, in a novel way

[018-097] which somehow seemed strange to him as preceptor. Perhaps she knew that

[018-098] he was thinking of her thus.

[018-099] For a few weeks their work had gone on with a monotony which in itself

[018-100] was a delight to him. Then it happened that the children were to be

[018-101] taken to Christminster to see an itinerant exhibition, in the shape of

[018-102] a model of Jerusalem, to which schools were admitted at a penny a head

[018-103] in the interests of education. They marched along the road two and two,

[018-104] she beside her class with her simple cotton sunshade, her little thumb

[018-105] cocked up against its stem; and Phillotson behind in his long dangling

[018-106] coat, handling his walking-stick genteelly, in the musing mood which

[018-107] had come over him since her arrival. The afternoon was one of sun and

[018-108] dust, and when they entered the exhibition room few people were present

[018-109] but themselves. The model of the ancient city stood in the middle of

[018-110] the apartment, and the proprietor, with a fine religious philanthropy

[018-111] written on his features, walked round it with a pointer in his hand,

[018-112] showing the young people the various quarters and places known to them

[018-113] by name from reading their Bibles; Mount Moriah, the Valley of

[018-114] Jehoshaphat, the City of Zion, the walls and the gates, outside one of

[018-115] which there was a large mound like a tumulus, and on the mound a little

[018-116] white cross. The spot, he said, was Calvary.

[018-117] “I think,” said Sue to the schoolmaster, as she stood with him a little

[018-118] in the background, “that this model, elaborate as it is, is a very

[018-119] imaginary production. How does anybody know that Jerusalem was like

[018-120] this in the time of Christ? I am sure this man doesn’t.”

[018-121] “It is made after the best conjectural maps, based on actual visits to

[018-122] the city as it now exists.”

[018-123] “I fancy we have had enough of Jerusalem,” she said, “considering we

[018-124] are not descended from the Jews. There was nothing first-rate about the

[018-125] place, or people, after all—as there was about Athens, Rome,

[018-126] Alexandria, and other old cities.”

[018-127] “But my dear girl, consider what it is to us!”

[018-128] She was silent, for she was easily repressed; and then perceived behind

[018-129] the group of children clustered round the model a young man in a white

[018-130] flannel jacket, his form being bent so low in his intent inspection of

[018-131] the Valley of Jehoshaphat that his face was almost hidden from view by

[018-132] the Mount of Olives. “Look at your cousin Jude,” continued the

[018-133] schoolmaster. “He doesn’t think we have had enough of Jerusalem!”

[018-134] “Ah—I didn’t see him!” she cried in her quick, light voice. “Jude—how

[018-135] seriously you are going into it!”

[018-136] Jude started up from his reverie, and saw her. “Oh—Sue!” he said, with

[018-137] a glad flush of embarrassment. “These are your school-children, of

[018-138] course! I saw that schools were admitted in the afternoons, and thought

[018-139] you might come; but I got so deeply interested that I didn’t remember

[018-140] where I was. How it carries one back, doesn’t it! I could examine it

[018-141] for hours, but I have only a few minutes, unfortunately; for I am in

[018-142] the middle of a job out here.”

[018-143] “Your cousin is so terribly clever that she criticizes it

[018-144] unmercifully,” said Phillotson, with good-humoured satire. “She is

[018-145] quite sceptical as to its correctness.”

[018-146] “No, Mr. Phillotson, I am not—altogether! I hate to be what is called a

[018-147] clever girl—there are too many of that sort now!” answered Sue

[018-148] sensitively. “I only meant—I don’t know what I meant—except that it was

[018-149] what you don’t understand!”

[018-150] “_I_ know your meaning,” said Jude ardently (although he did not). “And

[018-151] I think you are quite right.”

[018-152] “That’s a good Jude—I know _you_ believe in me!” She impulsively seized

[018-153] his hand, and leaving a reproachful look on the schoolmaster turned

[018-154] away to Jude, her voice revealing a tremor which she herself felt to be

[018-155] absurdly uncalled for by sarcasm so gentle. She had not the least

[018-156] conception how the hearts of the twain went out to her at this

[018-157] momentary revelation of feeling, and what a complication she was

[018-158] building up thereby in the futures of both.

[018-159] The model wore too much of an educational aspect for the children not

[018-160] to tire of it soon, and a little later in the afternoon they were all

[018-161] marched back to Lumsdon, Jude returning to his work. He watched the

[018-162] juvenile flock in their clean frocks and pinafores, filing down the

[018-163] street towards the country beside Phillotson and Sue, and a sad,

[018-164] dissatisfied sense of being out of the scheme of the latters’ lives had

[018-165] possession of him. Phillotson had invited him to walk out and see them

[018-166] on Friday evening, when there would be no lessons to give to Sue, and

[018-167] Jude had eagerly promised to avail himself of the opportunity.

[018-168] Meanwhile the scholars and teachers moved homewards, and the next day,

[018-169] on looking on the blackboard in Sue’s class, Phillotson was surprised

[018-170] to find upon it, skilfully drawn in chalk, a perspective view of

[018-171] Jerusalem, with every building shown in its place.

[018-172] “I thought you took no interest in the model, and hardly looked at it?”

[018-173] he said.

[018-174] “I hardly did,” said she, “but I remembered that much of it.”

[018-175] “It is more than I had remembered myself.”

[018-176] Her Majesty’s school-inspector was at that time paying

[018-177] “surprise-visits” in this neighbourhood to test the teaching unawares;

[018-178] and two days later, in the middle of the morning lessons, the latch of

[018-179] the door was softly lifted, and in walked my gentleman, the king of

[018-180] terrors—to pupil-teachers.

[018-181] To Mr. Phillotson the surprise was not great; like the lady in the

[018-182] story, he had been played that trick too many times to be unprepared.

[018-183] But Sue’s class was at the further end of the room, and her back was

[018-184] towards the entrance; the inspector therefore came and stood behind her

[018-185] and watched her teaching some half-minute before she became aware of

[018-186] his presence. She turned, and realized that an oft-dreaded moment had

[018-187] come. The effect upon her timidity was such that she uttered a cry of

[018-188] fright. Phillotson, with a strange instinct of solicitude quite beyond

[018-189] his control, was at her side just in time to prevent her falling from

[018-190] faintness. She soon recovered herself, and laughed; but when the

[018-191] inspector had gone there was a reaction, and she was so white that

[018-192] Phillotson took her into his room, and gave her some brandy to bring

[018-193] her round. She found him holding her hand.
