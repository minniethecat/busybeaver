[031-001] Jude found his aunt even worse than the communication from the Widow

[031-002] Edlin had led him to expect. There was every possibility of her

[031-003] lingering on for weeks or months, though little likelihood. He wrote to

[031-004] Sue informing her of the state of her aunt, and suggesting that she

[031-005] might like to see her aged relative alive. He would meet her at

[031-006] Alfredston Road, the following evening, Monday, on his way back from

[031-007] Christminster, if she could come by the up-train which crossed his

[031-008] down-train at that station. Next morning, according, he went on to

[031-009] Christminster, intending to return to Alfredston soon enough to keep

[031-010] the suggested appointment with Sue.

[031-011] The city of learning wore an estranged look, and he had lost all

[031-012] feeling for its associations. Yet as the sun made vivid lights and

[031-013] shades of the mullioned architecture of the façades, and drew patterns

[031-014] of the crinkled battlements on the young turf of the quadrangles, Jude

[031-015] thought he had never seen the place look more beautiful. He came to the

[031-016] street in which he had first beheld Sue. The chair she had occupied

[031-017] when, leaning over her ecclesiastical scrolls, a hog-hair brush in her

[031-018] hand, her girlish figure had arrested the gaze of his inquiring eyes,

[031-019] stood precisely in its former spot, empty. It was as if she were dead,

[031-020] and nobody had been found capable of succeeding her in that artistic

[031-021] pursuit. Hers was now the city phantom, while those of the intellectual

[031-022] and devotional worthies who had once moved him to emotion were no

[031-023] longer able to assert their presence there.

[031-024] However, here he was; and in fulfilment of his intention he went on to

[031-025] his former lodging in “Beersheba,” near the ritualistic church of St.

[031-026] Silas. The old landlady who opened the door seemed glad to see him

[031-027] again, and bringing some lunch informed him that the builder who had

[031-028] employed him had called to inquire his address.

[031-029] Jude went on to the stone-yard where he had worked. But the old sheds

[031-030] and bankers were distasteful to him; he felt it impossible to engage

[031-031] himself to return and stay in this place of vanished dreams. He longed

[031-032] for the hour of the homeward train to Alfredston, where he might

[031-033] probably meet Sue.

[031-034] Then, for one ghastly half-hour of depression caused by these scenes,

[031-035] there returned upon him that feeling which had been his undoing more

[031-036] than once—that he was not worth the trouble of being taken care of

[031-037] either by himself or others; and during this half-hour he met Tinker

[031-038] Taylor, the bankrupt ecclesiastical ironmonger, at Fourways, who

[031-039] proposed that they should adjourn to a bar and drink together. They

[031-040] walked along the street till they stood before one of the great

[031-041] palpitating centres of Christminster life, the inn wherein he formerly

[031-042] had responded to the challenge to rehearse the Creed in Latin—now a

[031-043] popular tavern with a spacious and inviting entrance, which gave

[031-044] admittance to a bar that had been entirely renovated and refitted in

[031-045] modern style since Jude’s residence here.

[031-046] Tinker Taylor drank off his glass and departed, saying it was too

[031-047] stylish a place now for him to feel at home in unless he was drunker

[031-048] than he had money to be just then. Jude was longer finishing his, and

[031-049] stood abstractedly silent in the, for the minute, almost empty place.

[031-050] The bar had been gutted and newly arranged throughout, mahogany

[031-051] fixtures having taken the place of the old painted ones, while at the

[031-052] back of the standing-space there were stuffed sofa-benches. The room

[031-053] was divided into compartments in the approved manner, between which

[031-054] were screens of ground glass in mahogany framing, to prevent topers in

[031-055] one compartment being put to the blush by the recognitions of those in

[031-056] the next. On the inside of the counter two barmaids leant over the

[031-057] white-handled beer-engines, and the row of little silvered taps inside,

[031-058] dripping into a pewter trough.

[031-059] Feeling tired, and having nothing more to do till the train left, Jude

[031-060] sat down on one of the sofas. At the back of the barmaids rose

[031-061] bevel-edged mirrors, with glass shelves running along their front, on

[031-062] which stood precious liquids that Jude did not know the name of, in

[031-063] bottles of topaz, sapphire, ruby and amethyst. The moment was enlivened

[031-064] by the entrance of some customers into the next compartment, and the

[031-065] starting of the mechanical tell-tale of monies received, which emitted

[031-066] a ting-ting every time a coin was put in.

[031-067] The barmaid attending to this compartment was invisible to Jude’s

[031-068] direct glance, though a reflection of her back in the glass behind her

[031-069] was occasionally caught by his eyes. He had only observed this

[031-070] listlessly, when she turned her face for a moment to the glass to set

[031-071] her hair tidy. Then he was amazed to discover that the face was

[031-072] Arabella’s.

[031-073] If she had come on to his compartment she would have seen him. But she

[031-074] did not, this being presided over by the maiden on the other side. Abby

[031-075] was in a black gown, with white linen cuffs and a broad white collar,

[031-076] and her figure, more developed than formerly, was accentuated by a

[031-077] bunch of daffodils that she wore on her left bosom. In the compartment

[031-078] she served stood an electro-plated fountain of water over a

[031-079] spirit-lamp, whose blue flame sent a steam from the top, all this being

[031-080] visible to him only in the mirror behind her; which also reflected the

[031-081] faces of the men she was attending to—one of them a handsome,

[031-082] dissipated young fellow, possibly an undergraduate, who had been

[031-083] relating to her an experience of some humorous sort.

[031-084] “Oh, Mr. Cockman, now! How can you tell such a tale to me in my

[031-085] innocence!” she cried gaily. “Mr. Cockman, what do you use to make your

[031-086] moustache curl so beautiful?” As the young man was clean shaven, the

[031-087] retort provoked a laugh at his expense.

[031-088] “Come!” said he, “I’ll have a curaçao; and a light, please.”

[031-089] She served the liqueur from one of the lovely bottles and striking a

[031-090] match held it to his cigarette with ministering archness while he

[031-091] whiffed.

[031-092] “Well, have you heard from your husband lately, my dear?” he asked.

[031-093] “Not a sound,” said she.

[031-094] “Where is he?”

[031-095] “I left him in Australia; and I suppose he’s there still.”

[031-096] Jude’s eyes grew rounder.

[031-097] “What made you part from him?”

[031-098] “Don’t you ask questions, and you won’t hear lies.”

[031-099] “Come then, give me my change, which you’ve been keeping from me for

[031-100] the last quarter of an hour; and I’ll romantically vanish up the street

[031-101] of this picturesque city.”

[031-102] She handed the change over the counter, in taking which he caught her

[031-103] fingers and held them. There was a slight struggle and titter, and he

[031-104] bade her good-bye and left.

[031-105] Jude had looked on with the eye of a dazed philosopher. It was

[031-106] extraordinary how far removed from his life Arabella now seemed to be.

[031-107] He could not realize their nominal closeness. And, this being the case,

[031-108] in his present frame of mind he was indifferent to the fact that

[031-109] Arabella was his wife indeed.

[031-110] The compartment that she served emptied itself of visitors, and after a

[031-111] brief thought he entered it, and went forward to the counter. Arabella

[031-112] did not recognize him for a moment. Then their glances met. She

[031-113] started; till a humorous impudence sparkled in her eyes, and she spoke.

[031-114] “Well, I’m blest! I thought you were underground years ago!”

[031-115] “Oh!”

[031-116] “I never heard anything of you, or I don’t know that I should have come

[031-117] here. But never mind! What shall I treat you to this afternoon? A

[031-118] Scotch and soda? Come, anything that the house will afford, for old

[031-119] acquaintance’ sake!”

[031-120] “Thanks, Arabella,” said Jude without a smile. “But I don’t want

[031-121] anything more than I’ve had.” The fact was that her unexpected presence

[031-122] there had destroyed at a stroke his momentary taste for strong liquor

[031-123] as completely as if it had whisked him back to his milk-fed infancy.

[031-124] “That’s a pity, now you could get it for nothing.”

[031-125] “How long have you been here?”

[031-126] “About six weeks. I returned from Sydney three months ago. I always

[031-127] liked this business, you know.”

[031-128] “I wonder you came to this place!”

[031-129] “Well, as I say, I thought you were gone to glory, and being in London

[031-130] I saw the situation in an advertisement. Nobody was likely to know me

[031-131] here, even if I had minded, for I was never in Christminster in my

[031-132] growing up.”

[031-133] “Why did you return from Australia?”

[031-134] “Oh, I had my reasons… Then you are not a don yet?”

[031-135] “No.”

[031-136] “Not even a reverend?”

[031-137] “No.”

[031-138] “Nor so much as a rather reverend dissenting gentleman?”

[031-139] “I am as I was.”

[031-140] “True—you look so.” She idly allowed her fingers to rest on the pull of

[031-141] the beer-engine as she inspected him critically. He observed that her

[031-142] hands were smaller and whiter than when he had lived with her, and that

[031-143] on the hand which pulled the engine she wore an ornamental ring set

[031-144] with what seemed to be real sapphires—which they were, indeed, and were

[031-145] much admired as such by the young men who frequented the bar.

[031-146] “So you pass as having a living husband,” he continued.

[031-147] “Yes. I thought it might be awkward if I called myself a widow, as I

[031-148] should have liked.”

[031-149] “True. I am known here a little.”

[031-150] “I didn’t mean on that account—for as I said I didn’t expect you. It

[031-151] was for other reasons.”

[031-152] “What were they?”

[031-153] “I don’t care to go into them,” she replied evasively. “I make a very

[031-154] good living, and I don’t know that I want your company.”

[031-155] Here a chappie with no chin, and a moustache like a lady’s eyebrow,

[031-156] came and asked for a curiously compounded drink, and Arabella was

[031-157] obliged to go and attend to him. “We can’t talk here,” she said,

[031-158] stepping back a moment. “Can’t you wait till nine? Say yes, and don’t

[031-159] be a fool. I can get off duty two hours sooner than usual, if I ask. I

[031-160] am not living in the house at present.”

[031-161] He reflected and said gloomily, “I’ll come back. I suppose we’d better

[031-162] arrange something.”

[031-163] “Oh, bother arranging! I’m not going to arrange anything!”

[031-164] “But I must know a thing or two; and, as you say, we can’t talk here.

[031-165] Very well; I’ll call for you.”

[031-166] Depositing his unemptied glass he went out and walked up and down the

[031-167] street. Here was a rude flounce into the pellucid sentimentality of his

[031-168] sad attachment to Sue. Though Arabella’s word was absolutely

[031-169] untrustworthy, he thought there might be some truth in her implication

[031-170] that she had not wished to disturb him, and had really supposed him

[031-171] dead. However, there was only one thing now to be done, and that was to

[031-172] play a straightforward part, the law being the law, and the woman

[031-173] between whom and himself there was no more unity than between east and

[031-174] west, being in the eye of the Church one person with him.

[031-175] Having to meet Arabella here, it was impossible to meet Sue at

[031-176] Alfredston as he had promised. At every thought of this a pang had gone

[031-177] through him; but the conjuncture could not be helped. Arabella was

[031-178] perhaps an intended intervention to punish him for his unauthorized

[031-179] love. Passing the evening, therefore, in a desultory waiting about the

[031-180] town wherein he avoided the precincts of every cloister and hall,

[031-181] because he could not bear to behold them, he repaired to the tavern bar

[031-182] while the hundred and one strokes were resounding from the Great Bell

[031-183] of Cardinal College, a coincidence which seemed to him gratuitous

[031-184] irony. The inn was now brilliantly lighted up, and the scene was

[031-185] altogether more brisk and gay. The faces of the barmaidens had risen in

[031-186] colour, each having a pink flush on her cheek; their manners were still

[031-187] more vivacious than before—more abandoned, more excited, more sensuous,

[031-188] and they expressed their sentiments and desires less euphemistically,

[031-189] laughing in a lackadaisical tone, without reserve.
