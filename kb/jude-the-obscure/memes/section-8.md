[008-001] Arabella said she would like some tea, and they entered an inn of an

[008-002] inferior class, and gave their order. As it was not for beer they had a

[008-003] long time to wait. The maid-servant recognized Jude, and whispered her

[008-004] surprise to her mistress in the background, that he, the student “who

[008-005] kept hisself up so particular,” should have suddenly descended so low

[008-006] as to keep company with Arabella. The latter guessed what was being

[008-007] said, and laughed as she met the serious and tender gaze of her

[008-008] lover—the low and triumphant laugh of a careless woman who sees she is

[008-009] winning her game.

[008-010] They sat and looked round the room, and at the picture of Samson and

[008-011] Delilah which hung on the wall, and at the circular beer-stains on the

[008-012] table, and at the spittoons underfoot filled with sawdust. The whole

[008-013] aspect of the scene had that depressing effect on Jude which few places

[008-014] can produce like a tap-room on a Sunday evening when the setting sun is

[008-015] slanting in, and no liquor is going, and the unfortunate wayfarer finds

[008-016] himself with no other haven of rest.

[008-017] It began to grow dusk. They could not wait longer, really, for the tea,

[008-018] they said. “Yet what else can we do?” asked Jude. “It is a three-mile

[008-019] walk for you.”

[008-020] “I suppose we can have some beer,” said Arabella.

[008-021] “Beer, oh yes. I had forgotten that. Somehow it seems odd to come to a

[008-022] public-house for beer on a Sunday evening.”

[008-023] “But we didn’t.”

[008-024] “No, we didn’t.” Jude by this time wished he was out of such an

[008-025] uncongenial atmosphere; but he ordered the beer, which was promptly

[008-026] brought.

[008-027] Arabella tasted it. “Ugh!” she said.

[008-028] Jude tasted. “What’s the matter with it?” he asked. “I don’t understand

[008-029] beer very much now, it is true. I like it well enough, but it is bad to

[008-030] read on, and I find coffee better. But this seems all right.”

[008-031] “Adulterated—I can’t touch it!” She mentioned three or four ingredients

[008-032] that she detected in the liquor beyond malt and hops, much to Jude’s

[008-033] surprise.

[008-034] “How much you know!” he said good-humouredly.

[008-035] Nevertheless she returned to the beer and drank her share, and they

[008-036] went on their way. It was now nearly dark, and as soon as they had

[008-037] withdrawn from the lights of the town they walked closer together, till

[008-038] they touched each other. She wondered why he did not put his arm round

[008-039] her waist, but he did not; he merely said what to himself seemed a

[008-040] quite bold enough thing: “Take my arm.”

[008-041] She took it, thoroughly, up to the shoulder. He felt the warmth of her

[008-042] body against his, and putting his stick under his other arm held with

[008-043] his right hand her right as it rested in its place.

[008-044] “Now we are well together, dear, aren’t we?” he observed.

[008-045] “Yes,” said she; adding to herself: “Rather mild!”

[008-046] “How fast I have become!” he was thinking.

[008-047] Thus they walked till they reached the foot of the upland, where they

[008-048] could see the white highway ascending before them in the gloom. From

[008-049] this point the only way of getting to Arabella’s was by going up the

[008-050] incline, and dipping again into her valley on the right. Before they

[008-051] had climbed far they were nearly run into by two men who had been

[008-052] walking on the grass unseen.

[008-053] “These lovers—you find ’em out o’ doors in all seasons and

[008-054] weathers—lovers and homeless dogs only,” said one of the men as they

[008-055] vanished down the hill.

[008-056] Arabella tittered lightly.

[008-057] “Are we lovers?” asked Jude.

[008-058] “You know best.”

[008-059] “But you can tell me?”

[008-060] For answer she inclined her head upon his shoulder. Jude took the hint,

[008-061] and encircling her waist with his arm, pulled her to him and kissed

[008-062] her.

[008-063] They walked now no longer arm in arm but, as she had desired, clasped

[008-064] together. After all, what did it matter since it was dark, said Jude to

[008-065] himself. When they were half-way up the long hill they paused as by

[008-066] arrangement, and he kissed her again. They reached the top, and he

[008-067] kissed her once more.

[008-068] “You can keep your arm there, if you would like to,” she said gently.

[008-069] He did so, thinking how trusting she was.

[008-070] Thus they slowly went towards her home. He had left his cottage at

[008-071] half-past three, intending to be sitting down again to the New

[008-072] Testament by half-past five. It was nine o’clock when, with another

[008-073] embrace, he stood to deliver her up at her father’s door.

[008-074] She asked him to come in, if only for a minute, as it would seem so odd

[008-075] otherwise, and as if she had been out alone in the dark. He gave way,

[008-076] and followed her in. Immediately that the door was opened he found, in

[008-077] addition to her parents, several neighbours sitting round. They all

[008-078] spoke in a congratulatory manner, and took him seriously as Arabella’s

[008-079] intended partner.

[008-080] They did not belong to his set or circle, and he felt out of place and

[008-081] embarrassed. He had not meant this: a mere afternoon of pleasant

[008-082] walking with Arabella, that was all he had meant. He did not stay

[008-083] longer than to speak to her stepmother, a simple, quiet woman without

[008-084] features or character; and bidding them all good night plunged with a

[008-085] sense of relief into the track over the down.

[008-086] But that sense was only temporary: Arabella soon re-asserted her sway

[008-087] in his soul. He walked as if he felt himself to be another man from the

[008-088] Jude of yesterday. What were his books to him? what were his

[008-089] intentions, hitherto adhered to so strictly, as to not wasting a single

[008-090] minute of time day by day? “Wasting!” It depended on your point of view

[008-091] to define that: he was just living for the first time: not wasting

[008-092] life. It was better to love a woman than to be a graduate, or a parson;

[008-093] ay, or a pope!

[008-094] When he got back to the house his aunt had gone to bed, and a general

[008-095] consciousness of his neglect seemed written on the face of all things

[008-096] confronting him. He went upstairs without a light, and the dim interior

[008-097] of his room accosted him with sad inquiry. There lay his book open,

[008-098] just as he had left it, and the capital letters on the title-page

[008-099] regarded him with fixed reproach in the grey starlight, like the

[008-100] unclosed eyes of a dead man:

[008-101] Η ΚΑΙΝΗ ΔΙΑΘΗΚΗ.

[008-102] * * * * * * * * *

[008-103] Jude had to leave early next morning for his usual week of absence at

[008-104] lodgings; and it was with a sense of futility that he threw into his

[008-105] basket upon his tools and other necessaries the unread book he had

[008-106] brought with him.

[008-107] He kept his impassioned doings a secret almost from himself. Arabella,

[008-108] on the contrary, made them public among all her friends and

[008-109] acquaintances.

[008-110] Retracing by the light of dawn the road he had followed a few hours

[008-111] earlier under cover of darkness, with his sweetheart by his side, he

[008-112] reached the bottom of the hill, where he walked slowly, and stood

[008-113] still. He was on the spot where he had given her the first kiss. As the

[008-114] sun had only just risen it was possible that nobody had passed there

[008-115] since. Jude looked on the ground and sighed. He looked closely, and

[008-116] could just discern in the damp dust the imprints of their feet as they

[008-117] had stood locked in each other’s arms. She was not there now, and “the

[008-118] embroidery of imagination upon the stuff of nature” so depicted her

[008-119] past presence that a void was in his heart which nothing could fill. A

[008-120] pollard willow stood close to the place, and that willow was different

[008-121] from all other willows in the world. Utter annihilation of the six days

[008-122] which must elapse before he could see her again as he had promised

[008-123] would have been his intensest wish if he had had only the week to live.

[008-124] An hour and a half later Arabella came along the same way with her two

[008-125] companions of the Saturday. She passed unheedingly the scene of the

[008-126] kiss, and the willow that marked it, though chattering freely on the

[008-127] subject to the other two.

[008-128] “And what did he tell ’ee next?”

[008-129] “Then he said—” And she related almost word for word some of his

[008-130] tenderest speeches. If Jude had been behind the fence he would have

[008-131] felt not a little surprised at learning how very few of his sayings and

[008-132] doings on the previous evening were private.

[008-133] “You’ve got him to care for ’ee a bit, ’nation if you han’t!” murmured

[008-134] Anny judicially. “It’s well to be you!”

[008-135] In a few moments Arabella replied in a curiously low, hungry tone of

[008-136] latent sensuousness: “I’ve got him to care for me: yes! But I want him

[008-137] to more than care for me; I want him to have me—to marry me! I must

[008-138] have him. I can’t do without him. He’s the sort of man I long for. I

[008-139] shall go mad if I can’t give myself to him altogether! I felt I should

[008-140] when I first saw him!”

[008-141] “As he is a romancing, straightfor’ard, honest chap, he’s to be had,

[008-142] and as a husband, if you set about catching him in the right way.”

[008-143] Arabella remained thinking awhile. “What med be the right way?” she

[008-144] asked.

[008-145] “Oh you don’t know—you don’t!” said Sarah, the third girl.

[008-146] “On my word I don’t!—No further, that is, than by plain courting, and

[008-147] taking care he don’t go too far!”

[008-148] The third girl looked at the second. “She _don’t_ know!”

[008-149] “’Tis clear she don’t!” said Anny.

[008-150] “And having lived in a town, too, as one may say! Well, we can teach

[008-151] ’ee som’at then, as well as you us.”

[008-152] “Yes. And how do you mean—a sure way to gain a man? Take me for an

[008-153] innocent, and have done wi’ it!”

[008-154] “As a husband.”

[008-155] “As a husband.”

[008-156] “A countryman that’s honourable and serious-minded such as he; God

[008-157] forbid that I should say a sojer, or sailor, or commercial gent from

[008-158] the towns, or any of them that be slippery with poor women! I’d do no

[008-159] friend that harm!”

[008-160] “Well, such as he, of course!”

[008-161] Arabella’s companions looked at each other, and turning up their eyes

[008-162] in drollery began smirking. Then one went up close to Arabella, and,

[008-163] although nobody was near, imparted some information in a low tone, the

[008-164] other observing curiously the effect upon Arabella.

[008-165] “Ah!” said the last-named slowly. “I own I didn’t think of that way! …

[008-166] But suppose he _isn’t_ honourable? A woman had better not have tried

[008-167] it!”

[008-168] “Nothing venture nothing have! Besides, you make sure that he’s

[008-169] honourable before you begin. You’d be safe enough with yours. I wish I

[008-170] had the chance! Lots of girls do it; or do you think they’d get married

[008-171] at all?”

[008-172] Arabella pursued her way in silent thought. “I’ll try it!” she

[008-173] whispered; but not to them.

[008-174] VIII

[008-175] One week’s end Jude was as usual walking out to his aunt’s at Marygreen

[008-176] from his lodging in Alfredston, a walk which now had large attractions

[008-177] for him quite other than his desire to see his aged and morose

[008-178] relative. He diverged to the right before ascending the hill with the

[008-179] single purpose of gaining, on his way, a glimpse of Arabella that

[008-180] should not come into the reckoning of regular appointments. Before

[008-181] quite reaching the homestead his alert eye perceived the top of her

[008-182] head moving quickly hither and thither over the garden hedge. Entering

[008-183] the gate he found that three young unfattened pigs had escaped from

[008-184] their sty by leaping clean over the top, and that she was endeavouring

[008-185] unassisted to drive them in through the door which she had set open.

[008-186] The lines of her countenance changed from the rigidity of business to

[008-187] the softness of love when she saw Jude, and she bent her eyes

[008-188] languishingly upon him. The animals took advantage of the pause by

[008-189] doubling and bolting out of the way.
