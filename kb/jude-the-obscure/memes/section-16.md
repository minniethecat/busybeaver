[016-001] The girl for whom he was beginning to nourish an extraordinary

[016-002] tenderness was at this time ensphered by the same harmonies as those

[016-003] which floated into his ears; and the thought was a delight to him. She

[016-004] was probably a frequenter of this place, and, steeped body and soul in

[016-005] church sentiment as she must be by occupation and habit, had, no doubt,

[016-006] much in common with him. To an impressionable and lonely young man the

[016-007] consciousness of having at last found anchorage for his thoughts, which

[016-008] promised to supply both social and spiritual possibilities, was like

[016-009] the dew of Hermon, and he remained throughout the service in a

[016-010] sustaining atmosphere of ecstasy.

[016-011] Though he was loth to suspect it, some people might have said to him

[016-012] that the atmosphere blew as distinctly from Cyprus as from Galilee.

[016-013] Jude waited till she had left her seat and passed under the screen

[016-014] before he himself moved. She did not look towards him, and by the time

[016-015] he reached the door she was half-way down the broad path. Being dressed

[016-016] up in his Sunday suit he was inclined to follow her and reveal himself.

[016-017] But he was not quite ready; and, alas, ought he to do so with the kind

[016-018] of feeling that was awakening in him?

[016-019] For though it had seemed to have an ecclesiastical basis during the

[016-020] service, and he had persuaded himself that such was the case, he could

[016-021] not altogether be blind to the real nature of the magnetism. She was

[016-022] such a stranger that the kinship was affectation, and he said, “It

[016-023] can’t be! I, a man with a wife, must not know her!” Still Sue _was_ his

[016-024] own kin, and the fact of his having a wife, even though she was not in

[016-025] evidence in this hemisphere, might be a help in one sense. It would put

[016-026] all thought of a tender wish on his part out of Sue’s mind, and make

[016-027] her intercourse with him free and fearless. It was with some heartache

[016-028] that he saw how little he cared for the freedom and fearlessness that

[016-029] would result in her from such knowledge.

[016-030] Some little time before the date of this service in the cathedral the

[016-031] pretty, liquid-eyed, light-footed young woman, Sue Bridehead, had an

[016-032] afternoon’s holiday, and leaving the ecclesiastical establishment in

[016-033] which she not only assisted but lodged, took a walk into the country

[016-034] with a book in her hand. It was one of those cloudless days which

[016-035] sometimes occur in Wessex and elsewhere between days of cold and wet,

[016-036] as if intercalated by caprice of the weather-god. She went along for a

[016-037] mile or two until she came to much higher ground than that of the city

[016-038] she had left behind her. The road passed between green fields, and

[016-039] coming to a stile Sue paused there, to finish the page she was reading,

[016-040] and then looked back at the towers and domes and pinnacles new and old.

[016-041] On the other side of the stile, in the footpath, she beheld a foreigner

[016-042] with black hair and a sallow face, sitting on the grass beside a large

[016-043] square board whereon were fixed, as closely as they could stand, a

[016-044] number of plaster statuettes, some of them bronzed, which he was

[016-045] re-arranging before proceeding with them on his way. They were in the

[016-046] main reduced copies of ancient marbles, and comprised divinities of a

[016-047] very different character from those the girl was accustomed to see

[016-048] portrayed, among them being a Venus of standard pattern, a Diana, and,

[016-049] of the other sex, Apollo, Bacchus, and Mars. Though the figures were

[016-050] many yards away from her the south-west sun brought them out so

[016-051] brilliantly against the green herbage that she could discern their

[016-052] contours with luminous distinctness; and being almost in a line between

[016-053] herself and the church towers of the city they awoke in her an oddly

[016-054] foreign and contrasting set of ideas by comparison. The man rose, and,

[016-055] seeing her, politely took off his cap, and cried, “I-i-i-mages!” in an

[016-056] accent that agreed with his appearance. In a moment he dexterously

[016-057] lifted upon his knee the great board with its assembled notabilities

[016-058] divine and human, and raised it to the top of his head, bringing them

[016-059] on to her and resting the board on the stile. First he offered her his

[016-060] smaller wares—the busts of kings and queens, then a minstrel, then a

[016-061] winged Cupid. She shook her head.

[016-062] “How much are these two?” she said, touching with her finger the Venus

[016-063] and the Apollo—the largest figures on the tray.

[016-064] He said she should have them for ten shillings.

[016-065] “I cannot afford that,” said Sue. She offered considerably less, and to

[016-066] her surprise the image-man drew them from their wire stay and handed

[016-067] them over the stile. She clasped them as treasures.

[016-068] When they were paid for, and the man had gone, she began to be

[016-069] concerned as to what she should do with them. They seemed so very large

[016-070] now that they were in her possession, and so very naked. Being of a

[016-071] nervous temperament she trembled at her enterprise. When she handled

[016-072] them the white pipeclay came off on her gloves and jacket. After

[016-073] carrying them along a little way openly an idea came to her, and,

[016-074] pulling some huge burdock leaves, parsley, and other rank growths from

[016-075] the hedge, she wrapped up her burden as well as she could in these, so

[016-076] that what she carried appeared to be an enormous armful of green stuff

[016-077] gathered by a zealous lover of nature.

[016-078] “Well, anything is better than those everlasting church fallals!” she

[016-079] said. But she was still in a trembling state, and seemed almost to wish

[016-080] she had not bought the figures.

[016-081] Occasionally peeping inside the leaves to see that Venus’s arm was not

[016-082] broken, she entered with her heathen load into the most Christian city

[016-083] in the country by an obscure street running parallel to the main one,

[016-084] and round a corner to the side door of the establishment to which she

[016-085] was attached. Her purchases were taken straight up to her own chamber,

[016-086] and she at once attempted to lock them in a box that was her very own

[016-087] property; but finding them too cumbersome she wrapped them in large

[016-088] sheets of brown paper, and stood them on the floor in a corner.

[016-089] The mistress of the house, Miss Fontover, was an elderly lady in

[016-090] spectacles, dressed almost like an abbess; a dab at Ritual, as become

[016-091] one of her business, and a worshipper at the ceremonial church of St.

[016-092] Silas, in the suburb of Beersheba before-mentioned, which Jude also had

[016-093] begun to attend. She was the daughter of a clergyman in reduced

[016-094] circumstances, and at his death, which had occurred several years

[016-095] before this date, she boldly avoided penury by taking over a little

[016-096] shop of church requisites and developing it to its present creditable

[016-097] proportions. She wore a cross and beads round her neck as her only

[016-098] ornament, and knew the Christian Year by heart.

[016-099] She now came to call Sue to tea, and, finding that the girl did not

[016-100] respond for a moment, entered the room just as the other was hastily

[016-101] putting a string round each parcel.

[016-102] “Something you have been buying, Miss Bridehead?” she asked, regarding

[016-103] the enwrapped objects.

[016-104] “Yes—just something to ornament my room,” said Sue.

[016-105] “Well, I should have thought I had put enough here already,” said Miss

[016-106] Fontover, looking round at the Gothic-framed prints of saints, the

[016-107] Church-text scrolls, and other articles which, having become too stale

[016-108] to sell, had been used to furnish this obscure chamber. “What is it?

[016-109] How bulky!” She tore a little hole, about as big as a wafer, in the

[016-110] brown paper, and tried to peep in. “Why, statuary? Two figures? Where

[016-111] did you get them?”

[016-112] “Oh—I bought them of a travelling man who sells casts—”

[016-113] “Two saints?”

[016-114] “Yes.”

[016-115] “What ones?”

[016-116] “St. Peter and St.—St. Mary Magdalen.”

[016-117] “Well—now come down to tea, and go and finish that organ-text, if

[016-118] there’s light enough afterwards.”

[016-119] These little obstacles to the indulgence of what had been the merest

[016-120] passing fancy created in Sue a great zest for unpacking her objects and

[016-121] looking at them; and at bedtime, when she was sure of being

[016-122] undisturbed, she unrobed the divinities in comfort. Placing the pair of

[016-123] figures on the chest of drawers, a candle on each side of them, she

[016-124] withdrew to the bed, flung herself down thereon, and began reading a

[016-125] book she had taken from her box, which Miss Fontover knew nothing of.

[016-126] It was a volume of Gibbon, and she read the chapter dealing with the

[016-127] reign of Julian the Apostate. Occasionally she looked up at the

[016-128] statuettes, which appeared strange and out of place, there happening to

[016-129] be a Calvary print hanging between them, and, as if the scene suggested

[016-130] the action, she at length jumped up and withdrew another book from her

[016-131] box—a volume of verse—and turned to the familiar poem—

[016-132] Thou hast conquered, O pale Galilean:

[016-133] The world has grown grey from thy breath!

[016-134] which she read to the end. Presently she put out the candles,

[016-135] undressed, and finally extinguished her own light.

[016-136] She was of an age which usually sleeps soundly, yet to-night she kept

[016-137] waking up, and every time she opened her eyes there was enough diffused

[016-138] light from the street to show her the white plaster figures, standing

[016-139] on the chest of drawers in odd contrast to their environment of text

[016-140] and martyr, and the Gothic-framed Crucifix-picture that was only

[016-141] discernible now as a Latin cross, the figure thereon being obscured by

[016-142] the shades.

[016-143] On one of these occasions the church clocks struck some small hour. It

[016-144] fell upon the ears of another person who sat bending over his books at

[016-145] a not very distant spot in the same city. Being Saturday night the

[016-146] morrow was one on which Jude had not set his alarm-clock to call him at

[016-147] his usually early time, and hence he had stayed up, as was his custom,

[016-148] two or three hours later than he could afford to do on any other day of

[016-149] the week. Just then he was earnestly reading from his Griesbach’s text.

[016-150] At the very time that Sue was tossing and staring at her figures, the

[016-151] policeman and belated citizens passing along under his window might

[016-152] have heard, if they had stood still, strange syllables mumbled with

[016-153] fervour within—words that had for Jude an indescribable enchantment:

[016-154] inexplicable sounds something like these:—

[016-155] “_All hemin heis Theos ho Pater, ex hou ta panta, kai hemeis eis

[016-156] auton:_”

[016-157] Till the sounds rolled with reverent loudness, as a book was heard to

[016-158] close:—

[016-159] “_Kai heis Kurios Iesous Christos, di hou ta panta kai hemeis di

[016-160] autou!_”

[016-161] IV

[016-162] He was a handy man at his trade, an all-round man, as artizans in

[016-163] country-towns are apt to be. In London the man who carves the boss or

[016-164] knob of leafage declines to cut the fragment of moulding which merges

[016-165] in that leafage, as if it were a degradation to do the second half of

[016-166] one whole. When there was not much Gothic moulding for Jude to run, or

[016-167] much window-tracery on the bankers, he would go out lettering monuments

[016-168] or tombstones, and take a pleasure in the change of handiwork.

[016-169] The next time that he saw her was when he was on a ladder executing a

[016-170] job of this sort inside one of the churches. There was a short morning

[016-171] service, and when the parson entered Jude came down from his ladder,

[016-172] and sat with the half-dozen people forming the congregation, till the

[016-173] prayer should be ended, and he could resume his tapping. He did not

[016-174] observe till the service was half over that one of the women was Sue,

[016-175] who had perforce accompanied the elderly Miss Fontover thither.

[016-176] Jude sat watching her pretty shoulders, her easy, curiously nonchalant

[016-177] risings and sittings, and her perfunctory genuflexions, and thought

[016-178] what a help such an Anglican would have been to him in happier

[016-179] circumstances. It was not so much his anxiety to get on with his work

[016-180] that made him go up to it immediately the worshipers began to take

[016-181] their leave: it was that he dared not, in this holy spot, confront the

[016-182] woman who was beginning to influence him in such an indescribable

[016-183] manner. Those three enormous reasons why he must not attempt intimate

[016-184] acquaintance with Sue Bridehead, now that his interest in her had shown

[016-185] itself to be unmistakably of a sexual kind, loomed as stubbornly as

[016-186] ever. But it was also obvious that man could not live by work alone;

[016-187] that the particular man Jude, at any rate, wanted something to love.

[016-188] Some men would have rushed incontinently to her, snatched the pleasure

[016-189] of easy friendship which she could hardly refuse, and have left the

[016-190] rest to chance. Not so Jude—at first.
