[049-001] During the interval before the issuing of the certificate, Sue, in her

[049-002] housekeeping errands, sometimes walked past the office, and furtively

[049-003] glancing in saw affixed to the wall the notice of the purposed clinch

[049-004] to their union. She could not bear its aspect. Coming after her

[049-005] previous experience of matrimony, all the romance of their attachment

[049-006] seemed to be starved away by placing her present case in the same

[049-007] category. She was usually leading little Father Time by the hand, and

[049-008] fancied that people thought him hers, and regarded the intended

[049-009] ceremony as the patching up of an old error.

[049-010] Meanwhile Jude decided to link his present with his past in some slight

[049-011] degree by inviting to the wedding the only person remaining on earth

[049-012] who was associated with his early life at Marygreen—the aged widow Mrs.

[049-013] Edlin, who had been his great-aunt’s friend and nurse in her last

[049-014] illness. He hardly expected that she would come; but she did, bringing

[049-015] singular presents, in the form of apples, jam, brass snuffers, an

[049-016] ancient pewter dish, a warming-pan, and an enormous bag of goose

[049-017] feathers towards a bed. She was allotted the spare room in Jude’s

[049-018] house, whither she retired early, and where they could hear her through

[049-019] the ceiling below, honestly saying the Lord’s Prayer in a loud voice,

[049-020] as the Rubric directed.

[049-021] As, however, she could not sleep, and discovered that Sue and Jude were

[049-022] still sitting up—it being in fact only ten o’clock—she dressed herself

[049-023] again and came down, and they all sat by the fire till a late

[049-024] hour—Father Time included; though, as he never spoke, they were hardly

[049-025] conscious of him.

[049-026] “Well, I bain’t set against marrying as your great-aunt was,” said the

[049-027] widow. “And I hope ’twill be a jocund wedding for ye in all respects

[049-028] this time. Nobody can hope it more, knowing what I do of your families,

[049-029] which is more, I suppose, than anybody else now living. For they have

[049-030] been unlucky that way, God knows.”

[049-031] Sue breathed uneasily.

[049-032] “They was always good-hearted people, too—wouldn’t kill a fly if they

[049-033] knowed it,” continued the wedding guest. “But things happened to thwart

[049-034] ’em, and if everything wasn’t vitty they were upset. No doubt that’s

[049-035] how he that the tale is told of came to do what ’a did—if he _were_ one

[049-036] of your family.”

[049-037] “What was that?” said Jude.

[049-038] “Well—that tale, ye know; he that was gibbeted just on the brow of the

[049-039] hill by the Brown House—not far from the milestone between Marygreen

[049-040] and Alfredston, where the other road branches off. But Lord, ’twas in

[049-041] my grandfather’s time; and it medn’ have been one of your folk at all.”

[049-042] “I know where the gibbet is said to have stood, very well,” murmured

[049-043] Jude. “But I never heard of this. What—did this man—my ancestor and

[049-044] Sue’s—kill his wife?”

[049-045] “’Twer not that exactly. She ran away from him, with their child, to

[049-046] her friends; and while she was there the child died. He wanted the

[049-047] body, to bury it where his people lay, but she wouldn’t give it up. Her

[049-048] husband then came in the night with a cart, and broke into the house to

[049-049] steal the coffin away; but he was catched, and being obstinate,

[049-050] wouldn’t tell what he broke in for. They brought it in burglary, and

[049-051] that’s why he was hanged and gibbeted on Brown House Hill. His wife

[049-052] went mad after he was dead. But it medn’t be true that he belonged to

[049-053] ye more than to me.”

[049-054] A small slow voice rose from the shade of the fireside, as if out of

[049-055] the earth: “If I was you, Mother, I wouldn’t marry Father!” It came

[049-056] from little Time, and they started, for they had forgotten him.

[049-057] “Oh, it is only a tale,” said Sue cheeringly.

[049-058] After this exhilarating tradition from the widow on the eve of the

[049-059] solemnization they rose, and, wishing their guest good-night, retired.

[049-060] The next morning Sue, whose nervousness intensified with the hours,

[049-061] took Jude privately into the sitting-room before starting. “Jude, I

[049-062] want you to kiss me, as a lover, incorporeally,” she said, tremulously

[049-063] nestling up to him, with damp lashes. “It won’t be ever like this any

[049-064] more, will it? I wish we hadn’t begun the business. But I suppose we

[049-065] must go on. How horrid that story was last night! It spoilt my thoughts

[049-066] of to-day. It makes me feel as if a tragic doom overhung our family, as

[049-067] it did the house of Atreus.”

[049-068] “Or the house of Jeroboam,” said the quondam theologian.

[049-069] “Yes. And it seems awful temerity in us two to go marrying! I am going

[049-070] to vow to you in the same words I vowed in to my other husband, and you

[049-071] to me in the same as you used to your other wife; regardless of the

[049-072] deterrent lesson we were taught by those experiments!”

[049-073] “If you are uneasy I am made unhappy,” said he. “I had hoped you would

[049-074] feel quite joyful. But if you don’t, you don’t. It is no use

[049-075] pretending. It is a dismal business to you, and that makes it so to

[049-076] me!”

[049-077] “It is unpleasantly like that other morning—that’s all,” she murmured.

[049-078] “Let us go on now.”

[049-079] They started arm in arm for the office aforesaid, no witness

[049-080] accompanying them except the Widow Edlin. The day was chilly and dull,

[049-081] and a clammy fog blew through the town from “Royal-tower’d Thame.” On

[049-082] the steps of the office there were the muddy foot-marks of people who

[049-083] had entered, and in the entry were damp umbrellas. Within the office

[049-084] several persons were gathered, and our couple perceived that a marriage

[049-085] between a soldier and a young woman was just in progress. Sue, Jude,

[049-086] and the widow stood in the background while this was going on, Sue

[049-087] reading the notices of marriage on the wall. The room was a dreary

[049-088] place to two of their temperament, though to its usual frequenters it

[049-089] doubtless seemed ordinary enough. Law-books in musty calf covered one

[049-090] wall, and elsewhere were post-office directories, and other books of

[049-091] reference. Papers in packets tied with red tape were pigeon-holed

[049-092] around, and some iron safes filled a recess, while the bare wood floor

[049-093] was, like the door-step, stained by previous visitors.

[049-094] The soldier was sullen and reluctant: the bride sad and timid; she was

[049-095] soon, obviously, to become a mother, and she had a black eye. Their

[049-096] little business was soon done, and the twain and their friends

[049-097] straggled out, one of the witnesses saying casually to Jude and Sue in

[049-098] passing, as if he had known them before: “See the couple just come in?

[049-099] Ha, ha! That fellow is just out of gaol this morning. She met him at

[049-100] the gaol gates, and brought him straight here. She’s paying for

[049-101] everything.”

[049-102] Sue turned her head and saw an ill-favoured man, closely cropped, with

[049-103] a broad-faced, pock-marked woman on his arm, ruddy with liquor and the

[049-104] satisfaction of being on the brink of a gratified desire. They jocosely

[049-105] saluted the outgoing couple, and went forward in front of Jude and Sue,

[049-106] whose diffidence was increasing. The latter drew back and turned to her

[049-107] lover, her mouth shaping itself like that of a child about to give way

[049-108] to grief:

[049-109] “Jude—I don’t like it here! I wish we hadn’t come! The place gives me

[049-110] the horrors: it seems so unnatural as the climax of our love! I wish it

[049-111] had been at church, if it had to be at all. It is not so vulgar there!”

[049-112] “Dear little girl,” said Jude. “How troubled and pale you look!”

[049-113] “It must be performed here now, I suppose?”

[049-114] “No—perhaps not necessarily.”

[049-115] He spoke to the clerk, and came back. “No—we need not marry here or

[049-116] anywhere, unless we like, even now,” he said. “We can be married in a

[049-117] church, if not with the same certificate with another he’ll give us, I

[049-118] think. Anyhow, let us go out till you are calmer, dear, and I too, and

[049-119] talk it over.”

[049-120] They went out stealthily and guiltily, as if they had committed a

[049-121] misdemeanour, closing the door without noise, and telling the widow,

[049-122] who had remained in the entry, to go home and await them; that they

[049-123] would call in any casual passers as witnesses, if necessary. When in

[049-124] the street they turned into an unfrequented side alley where they

[049-125] walked up and down as they had done long ago in the market-house at

[049-126] Melchester.

[049-127] “Now, darling, what shall we do? We are making a mess of it, it strikes

[049-128] me. Still, _anything_ that pleases you will please me.”

[049-129] “But Jude, dearest, I am worrying you! You wanted it to be there,

[049-130] didn’t you?”

[049-131] “Well, to tell the truth, when I got inside I felt as if I didn’t care

[049-132] much about it. The place depressed me almost as much as it did you—it

[049-133] was ugly. And then I thought of what you had said this morning as to

[049-134] whether we ought.”

[049-135] They walked on vaguely, till she paused, and her little voice began

[049-136] anew: “It seems so weak, too, to vacillate like this! And yet how much

[049-137] better than to act rashly a second time… How terrible that scene was to

[049-138] me! The expression in that flabby woman’s face, leading her on to give

[049-139] herself to that gaol-bird, not for a few hours, as she would, but for a

[049-140] lifetime, as she must. And the other poor soul—to escape a nominal

[049-141] shame which was owing to the weakness of her character, degrading

[049-142] herself to the real shame of bondage to a tyrant who scorned her—a man

[049-143] whom to avoid for ever was her only chance of salvation… This is our

[049-144] parish church, isn’t it? This is where it would have to be, if we did

[049-145] it in the usual way? A service or something seems to be going on.”

[049-146] Jude went up and looked in at the door. “Why—it is a wedding here too,”

[049-147] he said. “Everybody seems to be on our tack to-day.”

[049-148] Sue said she supposed it was because Lent was just over, when there was

[049-149] always a crowd of marriages. “Let us listen,” she said, “and find how

[049-150] it feels to us when performed in a church.”

[049-151] They stepped in, and entered a back seat, and watched the proceedings

[049-152] at the altar. The contracting couple appeared to belong to the

[049-153] well-to-do middle class, and the wedding altogether was of ordinary

[049-154] prettiness and interest. They could see the flowers tremble in the

[049-155] bride’s hand, even at that distance, and could hear her mechanical

[049-156] murmur of words whose meaning her brain seemed to gather not at all

[049-157] under the pressure of her self-consciousness. Sue and Jude listened,

[049-158] and severally saw themselves in time past going through the same form

[049-159] of self-committal.

[049-160] “It is not the same to her, poor thing, as it would be to me doing it

[049-161] over again with my present knowledge,” Sue whispered. “You see, they

[049-162] are fresh to it, and take the proceedings as a matter of course. But

[049-163] having been awakened to its awful solemnity as we have, or at least as

[049-164] I have, by experience, and to my own too squeamish feelings perhaps

[049-165] sometimes, it really does seem immoral in me to go and undertake the

[049-166] same thing again with open eyes. Coming in here and seeing this has

[049-167] frightened me from a church wedding as much as the other did from a

[049-168] registry one… We are a weak, tremulous pair, Jude, and what others may

[049-169] feel confident in I feel doubts of—my being proof against the sordid

[049-170] conditions of a business contract again!”

[049-171] Then they tried to laugh, and went on debating in whispers the

[049-172] object-lesson before them. And Jude said he also thought they were both

[049-173] too thin-skinned—that they ought never to have been born—much less have

[049-174] come together for the most preposterous of all joint ventures for

[049-175] _them_—matrimony.

[049-176] His betrothed shuddered; and asked him earnestly if he indeed felt that

[049-177] they ought not to go in cold blood and sign that life-undertaking

[049-178] again? “It is awful if you think we have found ourselves not strong

[049-179] enough for it, and knowing this, are proposing to perjure ourselves,”

[049-180] she said.
