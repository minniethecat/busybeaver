[046-001] “Well, she is, rather more than you, come to that,” he said, taking his

[046-002] hat determinedly. “I’ve wanted you to be, and I’ve waited with the

[046-003] patience of Job, and I don’t see that I’ve got anything by my

[046-004] self-denial. I shall certainly give her something, and hear what it is

[046-005] she is so anxious to tell me; no man could do less!”

[046-006] There was that in his manner which she knew it would be futile to

[046-007] oppose. She said no more, but, turning to her room as meekly as a

[046-008] martyr, heard him go downstairs, unbolt the door, and close it behind

[046-009] him. With a woman’s disregard of her dignity when in the presence of

[046-010] nobody but herself, she also trotted down, sobbing articulately as she

[046-011] went. She listened. She knew exactly how far it was to the inn that

[046-012] Arabella had named as her lodging. It would occupy about seven minutes

[046-013] to get there at an ordinary walking pace; seven to come back again. If

[046-014] he did not return in fourteen minutes he would have lingered. She

[046-015] looked at the clock. It was twenty-five minutes to eleven. He _might_

[046-016] enter the inn with Arabella, as they would reach it before closing

[046-017] time; she might get him to drink with her; and Heaven only knew what

[046-018] disasters would befall him then.

[046-019] In a still suspense she waited on. It seemed as if the whole time had

[046-020] nearly elapsed when the door was opened again, and Jude appeared.

[046-021] Sue gave a little ecstatic cry. “Oh, I knew I could trust you!—how good

[046-022] you are!”—she began.

[046-023] “I can’t find her anywhere in this street, and I went out in my

[046-024] slippers only. She has walked on, thinking I’ve been so hard-hearted as

[046-025] to refuse her requests entirely, poor woman. I’ve come back for my

[046-026] boots, as it is beginning to rain.”

[046-027] “Oh, but why should you take such trouble for a woman who has served

[046-028] you so badly!” said Sue in a jealous burst of disappointment.

[046-029] “But, Sue, she’s a woman, and I once cared for her; and one can’t be a

[046-030] brute in such circumstances.”

[046-031] “She isn’t your wife any longer!” exclaimed Sue, passionately excited.

[046-032] “You _mustn’t_ go out to find her! It isn’t right! You _can’t_ join

[046-033] her, now she’s a stranger to you. How can you forget such a thing, my

[046-034] dear, dear one!”

[046-035] “She seems much the same as ever—an erring, careless, unreflecting

[046-036] fellow-creature,” he said, continuing to pull on his boots. “What those

[046-037] legal fellows have been playing at in London makes no difference in my

[046-038] real relations to her. If she was my wife while she was away in

[046-039] Australia with another husband, she’s my wife now.”

[046-040] “But she wasn’t! That’s just what I hold! There’s the absurdity!—

[046-041] Well—you’ll come straight back, after a few minutes, won’t you, dear?

[046-042] She is too low, too coarse for you to talk to long, Jude, and was

[046-043] always!”

[046-044] “Perhaps I am coarse too, worse luck! I have the germs of every human

[046-045] infirmity in me, I verily believe—that was why I saw it was so

[046-046] preposterous of me to think of being a curate. I have cured myself of

[046-047] drunkenness I think; but I never know in what new form a suppressed

[046-048] vice will break out in me! I do love you, Sue, though I have danced

[046-049] attendance on you so long for such poor returns! All that’s best and

[046-050] noblest in me loves you, and your freedom from everything that’s gross

[046-051] has elevated me, and enabled me to do what I should never have dreamt

[046-052] myself capable of, or any man, a year or two ago. It is all very well

[046-053] to preach about self-control, and the wickedness of coercing a woman.

[046-054] But I should just like a few virtuous people who have condemned me in

[046-055] the past, about Arabella and other things, to have been in my

[046-056] tantalizing position with you through these late weeks!—they’d believe,

[046-057] I think, that I have exercised some little restraint in always giving

[046-058] in to your wishes—living here in one house, and not a soul between us.”

[046-059] “Yes, you have been good to me, Jude; I know you have, my dear

[046-060] protector.”

[046-061] “Well—Arabella has appealed to me for help. I must go out and speak to

[046-062] her, Sue, at least!”

[046-063] “I can’t say any more!—Oh, if you must, you must!” she said, bursting

[046-064] out into sobs that seemed to tear her heart. “I have nobody but you,

[046-065] Jude, and you are deserting me! I didn’t know you were like this—I

[046-066] can’t bear it, I can’t! If she were yours it would be different!”

[046-067] “Or if you were.”

[046-068] “Very well then—if I must I must. Since you will have it so, I agree! I

[046-069] will be. Only I didn’t mean to! And I didn’t want to marry again,

[046-070] either! … But, yes—I agree, I agree! I do love you. I ought to have

[046-071] known that you would conquer in the long run, living like this!”

[046-072] She ran across and flung her arms round his neck. “I am not a

[046-073] cold-natured, sexless creature, am I, for keeping you at such a

[046-074] distance? I am sure you don’t think so! Wait and see! I do belong to

[046-075] you, don’t I? I give in!”

[046-076] “And I’ll arrange for our marriage to-morrow, or as soon as ever you

[046-077] wish.”

[046-078] “Yes, Jude.”

[046-079] “Then I’ll let her go,” said he, embracing Sue softly. “I do feel that

[046-080] it would be unfair to you to see her, and perhaps unfair to her. She is

[046-081] not like you, my darling, and never was: it is only bare justice to say

[046-082] that. Don’t cry any more. There; and there; and there!” He kissed her

[046-083] on one side, and on the other, and in the middle, and rebolted the

[046-084] front door.

[046-085] The next morning it was wet.

[046-086] “Now, dear,” said Jude gaily at breakfast; “as this is Saturday I mean

[046-087] to call about the banns at once, so as to get the first publishing done

[046-088] to-morrow, or we shall lose a week. Banns will do? We shall save a

[046-089] pound or two.”

[046-090] Sue absently agreed to banns. But her mind for the moment was running

[046-091] on something else. A glow had passed away from her, and depression sat

[046-092] upon her features.

[046-093] “I feel I was wickedly selfish last night!” she murmured. “It was sheer

[046-094] unkindness in me—or worse—to treat Arabella as I did. I didn’t care

[046-095] about her being in trouble, and what she wished to tell you! Perhaps it

[046-096] was really something she was justified in telling you. That’s some more

[046-097] of my badness, I suppose! Love has its own dark morality when rivalry

[046-098] enters in—at least, mine has, if other people’s hasn’t… I wonder how

[046-099] she got on? I hope she reached the inn all right, poor woman.”

[046-100] “Oh yes: she got on all right,” said Jude placidly.

[046-101] “I hope she wasn’t shut out, and that she hadn’t to walk the streets in

[046-102] the rain. Do you mind my putting on my waterproof and going to see if

[046-103] she got in? I’ve been thinking of her all the morning.”

[046-104] “Well—is it necessary? You haven’t the least idea how Arabella is able

[046-105] to shift for herself. Still, darling, if you want to go and inquire you

[046-106] can.”

[046-107] There was no limit to the strange and unnecessary penances which Sue

[046-108] would meekly undertake when in a contrite mood; and this going to see

[046-109] all sorts of extraordinary persons whose relation to her was precisely

[046-110] of a kind that would have made other people shun them was her instinct

[046-111] ever, so that the request did not surprise him.

[046-112] “And when you come back,” he added, “I’ll be ready to go about the

[046-113] banns. You’ll come with me?”

[046-114] Sue agreed, and went off under cloak and umbrella letting Jude kiss her

[046-115] freely, and returning his kisses in a way she had never done before.

[046-116] Times had decidedly changed. “The little bird is caught at last!” she

[046-117] said, a sadness showing in her smile.

[046-118] “No—only nested,” he assured her.

[046-119] She walked along the muddy street till she reached the public house

[046-120] mentioned by Arabella, which was not so very far off. She was informed

[046-121] that Arabella had not yet left, and in doubt how to announce herself so

[046-122] that her predecessor in Jude’s affections would recognize her, she sent

[046-123] up word that a friend from Spring Street had called, naming the place

[046-124] of Jude’s residence. She was asked to step upstairs, and on being shown

[046-125] into a room found that it was Arabella’s bedroom, and that the latter

[046-126] had not yet risen. She halted on the turn of her toe till Arabella

[046-127] cried from the bed, “Come in and shut the door,” which Sue accordingly

[046-128] did.

[046-129] Arabella lay facing the window, and did not at once turn her head: and

[046-130] Sue was wicked enough, despite her penitence, to wish for a moment that

[046-131] Jude could behold her forerunner now, with the daylight full upon her.

[046-132] She may have seemed handsome enough in profile under the lamps, but a

[046-133] frowsiness was apparent this morning; and the sight of her own fresh

[046-134] charms in the looking-glass made Sue’s manner bright, till she

[046-135] reflected what a meanly sexual emotion this was in her, and hated

[046-136] herself for it.

[046-137] “I’ve just looked in to see if you got back comfortably last night,

[046-138] that’s all,” she said gently. “I was afraid afterwards that you might

[046-139] have met with any mishap?”

[046-140] “Oh—how stupid this is! I thought my visitor was—your friend—your

[046-141] husband—Mrs. Fawley, as I suppose you call yourself?” said Arabella,

[046-142] flinging her head back upon the pillows with a disappointed toss, and

[046-143] ceasing to retain the dimple she had just taken the trouble to produce.

[046-144] “Indeed I don’t,” said Sue.

[046-145] “Oh, I thought you might have, even if he’s not really yours. Decency

[046-146] is decency, any hour of the twenty-four.”

[046-147] “I don’t know what you mean,” said Sue stiffly. “He is mine, if you

[046-148] come to that!”

[046-149] “He wasn’t yesterday.”

[046-150] Sue coloured roseate, and said, “How do you know?”

[046-151] “From your manner when you talked to me at the door. Well, my dear,

[046-152] you’ve been quick about it, and I expect my visit last night helped it

[046-153] on—ha-ha! But I don’t want to get him away from you.”

[046-154] Sue looked out at the rain, and at the dirty toilet-cover, and at the

[046-155] detached tail of Arabella’s hair hanging on the looking-glass, just as

[046-156] it had done in Jude’s time; and wished she had not come. In the pause

[046-157] there was a knock at the door, and the chambermaid brought in a

[046-158] Arabella opened it as she lay, and her ruffled look disappeared.

[046-159] “I am much obliged to you for your anxiety about me,” she said blandly

[046-160] when the maid had gone; “but it is not necessary you should feel it. My

[046-161] man finds he can’t do without me after all, and agrees to stand by the

[046-162] promise to marry again over here that he has made me all along. See

[046-163] Sue to read, but Sue did not take it. “He asks me to come back. His

[046-164] little corner public in Lambeth would go to pieces without me, he says.

[046-165] But he isn’t going to knock me about when he has had a drop, any more

[046-166] after we are spliced by English law than before! … As for you, I should

[046-167] coax Jude to take me before the parson straight off, and have done with

[046-168] it, if I were in your place. I say it as a friend, my dear.”

[046-169] “He’s waiting to, any day,” returned Sue, with frigid pride.

[046-170] “Then let him, in Heaven’s name. Life with a man is more businesslike

[046-171] after it, and money matters work better. And then, you see, if you have

[046-172] rows, and he turns you out of doors, you can get the law to protect

[046-173] you, which you can’t otherwise, unless he half-runs you through with a

[046-174] knife, or cracks your noddle with a poker. And if he bolts away from

[046-175] you—I say it friendly, as woman to woman, for there’s never any knowing

[046-176] what a man med do—you’ll have the sticks o’ furniture, and won’t be

[046-177] looked upon as a thief. I shall marry my man over again, now he’s

[046-178] willing, as there was a little flaw in the first ceremony. In my

[046-179] made it up with Jude; and that frightened him, I expect! Perhaps I

[046-180] should quite have done it if it hadn’t been for you,” she said

[046-181] laughing; “and then how different our histories might have been from

[046-182] to-day! Never such a tender fool as Jude is if a woman seems in

[046-183] trouble, and coaxes him a bit! Just as he used to be about birds and

[046-184] things. However, as it happens, it is just as well as if I had made it

[046-185] up, and I forgive you. And, as I say, I’d advise you to get the

[046-186] business legally done as soon as possible. You’ll find it an awful

[046-187] bother later on if you don’t.”
