[038-001] Though he was almost a stranger here now, passing cottagers talked to

[038-002] him over the garden hedge.

[038-003] “Burning up your awld aunt’s rubbidge, I suppose? Ay; a lot gets heaped

[038-004] up in nooks and corners when you’ve lived eighty years in one house.”

[038-005] It was nearly one o’clock in the morning before the leaves, covers, and

[038-006] binding of Jeremy Taylor, Butler, Doddridge, Paley, Pusey, Newman and

[038-007] the rest had gone to ashes, but the night was quiet, and as he turned

[038-008] and turned the paper shreds with the fork, the sense of being no longer

[038-009] a hypocrite to himself afforded his mind a relief which gave him calm.

[038-010] He might go on believing as before, but he professed nothing, and no

[038-011] longer owned and exhibited engines of faith which, as their proprietor,

[038-012] he might naturally be supposed to exercise on himself first of all. In

[038-013] his passion for Sue he could not stand as an ordinary sinner, and not

[038-014] as a whited sepulchre.

[038-015] Meanwhile Sue, after parting from him earlier in the day, had gone

[038-016] along to the station, with tears in her eyes for having run back and

[038-017] let him kiss her. Jude ought not to have pretended that he was not a

[038-018] lover, and made her give way to an impulse to act unconventionally, if

[038-019] not wrongly. She was inclined to call it the latter; for Sue’s logic

[038-020] was extraordinarily compounded, and seemed to maintain that before a

[038-021] thing was done it might be right to do, but that being done it became

[038-022] wrong; or, in other words, that things which were right in theory were

[038-023] wrong in practice.

[038-024] “I have been too weak, I think!” she jerked out as she pranced on,

[038-025] shaking down tear-drops now and then. “It was burning, like a

[038-026] lover’s—oh, it was! And I won’t write to him any more, or at least for

[038-027] a long time, to impress him with my dignity! And I hope it will hurt

[038-028] him very much—expecting a letter to-morrow morning, and the next, and

[038-029] the next, and no letter coming. He’ll suffer then with suspense—won’t

[038-030] he, that’s all!—and I am very glad of it!”—Tears of pity for Jude’s

[038-031] approaching sufferings at her hands mingled with those which had surged

[038-032] up in pity for herself.

[038-033] Then the slim little wife of a husband whose person was disagreeable to

[038-034] her, the ethereal, fine-nerved, sensitive girl, quite unfitted by

[038-035] temperament and instinct to fulfil the conditions of the matrimonial

[038-036] relation with Phillotson, possibly with scarce any man, walked fitfully

[038-037] along, and panted, and brought weariness into her eyes by gazing and

[038-038] worrying hopelessly.

[038-039] Phillotson met her at the arrival station, and, seeing that she was

[038-040] troubled, thought it must be owing to the depressing effect of her

[038-041] aunt’s death and funeral. He began telling her of his day’s doings, and

[038-042] how his friend Gillingham, a neighbouring schoolmaster whom he had not

[038-043] seen for years, had called upon him. While ascending to the town,

[038-044] seated on the top of the omnibus beside him, she said suddenly and with

[038-045] an air of self-chastisement, regarding the white road and its bordering

[038-046] bushes of hazel:

[038-047] “Richard—I let Mr. Fawley hold my hand a long while. I don’t know

[038-048] whether you think it wrong?”

[038-049] He, waking apparently from thoughts of far different mould, said

[038-050] vaguely, “Oh, did you? What did you do that for?”

[038-051] “I don’t know. He wanted to, and I let him.”

[038-052] “I hope it pleased him. I should think it was hardly a novelty.”

[038-053] They lapsed into silence. Had this been a case in the court of an

[038-054] omniscient judge, he might have entered on his notes the curious fact

[038-055] that Sue had placed the minor for the major indiscretion, and had not

[038-056] said a word about the kiss.

[038-057] After tea that evening Phillotson sat balancing the school registers.

[038-058] She remained in an unusually silent, tense, and restless condition, and

[038-059] at last, saying she was tired, went to bed early. When Phillotson

[038-060] arrived upstairs, weary with the drudgery of the attendance-numbers, it

[038-061] was a quarter to twelve o’clock. Entering their chamber, which by day

[038-062] commanded a view of some thirty or forty miles over the Vale of

[038-063] Blackmoor, and even into Outer Wessex, he went to the window, and,

[038-064] pressing his face against the pane, gazed with hard-breathing fixity

[038-065] into the mysterious darkness which now covered the far-reaching scene.

[038-066] He was musing, “I think,” he said at last, without turning his head,

[038-067] “that I must get the committee to change the school-stationer. All the

[038-068] copybooks are sent wrong this time.”

[038-069] There was no reply. Thinking Sue was dozing he went on:

[038-070] “And there must be a rearrangement of that ventilator in the

[038-071] class-room. The wind blows down upon my head unmercifully and gives me

[038-072] the ear-ache.”

[038-073] As the silence seemed more absolute than ordinarily he turned round.

[038-074] The heavy, gloomy oak wainscot, which extended over the walls upstairs

[038-075] and down in the dilapidated “Old-Grove Place,” and the massive

[038-076] chimney-piece reaching to the ceiling, stood in odd contrast to the new

[038-077] and shining brass bedstead, and the new suite of birch furniture that

[038-078] he had bought for her, the two styles seeming to nod to each other

[038-079] across three centuries upon the shaking floor.

[038-080] “Soo!” he said (this being the way in which he pronounced her name).

[038-081] She was not in the bed, though she had apparently been there—the

[038-082] clothes on her side being flung back. Thinking she might have forgotten

[038-083] some kitchen detail and gone downstairs for a moment to see to it, he

[038-084] pulled off his coat and idled quietly enough for a few minutes, when,

[038-085] finding she did not come, he went out upon the landing, candle in hand,

[038-086] and said again “Soo!”

[038-087] “Yes!” came back to him in her voice, from the distant kitchen quarter.

[038-088] “What are you doing down there at midnight—tiring yourself out for

[038-089] nothing!”

[038-090] “I am not sleepy; I am reading; and there is a larger fire here.”

[038-091] He went to bed. Some time in the night he awoke. She was not there,

[038-092] even now. Lighting a candle he hastily stepped out upon the landing,

[038-093] and again called her name.

[038-094] She answered “Yes!” as before, but the tones were small and confined,

[038-095] and whence they came he could not at first understand. Under the

[038-096] staircase was a large clothes-closet, without a window; they seemed to

[038-097] come from it. The door was shut, but there was no lock or other

[038-098] fastening. Phillotson, alarmed, went towards it, wondering if she had

[038-099] suddenly become deranged.

[038-100] “What are you doing in there?” he asked.

[038-101] “Not to disturb you I came here, as it was so late.”

[038-102] “But there’s no bed, is there? And no ventilation! Why, you’ll be

[038-103] suffocated if you stay all night!”

[038-104] “Oh no, I think not. Don’t trouble about me.”

[038-105] “But—” Phillotson seized the knob and pulled at the door. She had

[038-106] fastened it inside with a piece of string, which broke at his pull.

[038-107] There being no bedstead she had flung down some rugs and made a little

[038-108] nest for herself in the very cramped quarters the closet afforded.

[038-109] When he looked in upon her she sprang out of her lair, great-eyed and

[038-110] trembling.

[038-111] “You ought not to have pulled open the door!” she cried excitedly. “It

[038-112] is not becoming in you! Oh, will you go away; please will you!”

[038-113] She looked so pitiful and pleading in her white nightgown against the

[038-114] shadowy lumber-hole that he was quite worried. She continued to beseech

[038-115] him not to disturb her.

[038-116] He said: “I’ve been kind to you, and given you every liberty; and it is

[038-117] monstrous that you should feel in this way!”

[038-118] “Yes,” said she, weeping. “I know that! It is wrong and wicked of me, I

[038-119] suppose! I am very sorry. But it is not I altogether that am to blame!”

[038-120] “Who is then? Am I?”

[038-121] “No—I don’t know! The universe, I suppose—things in general, because

[038-122] they are so horrid and cruel!”

[038-123] “Well, it is no use talking like that. Making a man’s house so unseemly

[038-124] at this time o’ night! Eliza will hear if we don’t mind.” (He meant the

[038-125] servant.) “Just think if either of the parsons in this town was to see

[038-126] us now! I hate such eccentricities, Sue. There’s no order or regularity

[038-127] in your sentiments! … But I won’t intrude on you further; only I would

[038-128] advise you not to shut the door too tight, or I shall find you stifled

[038-129] to-morrow.”

[038-130] On rising the next morning he immediately looked into the closet, but

[038-131] Sue had already gone downstairs. There was a little nest where she had

[038-132] lain, and spiders’ webs hung overhead. “What must a woman’s aversion be

[038-133] when it is stronger than her fear of spiders!” he said bitterly.

[038-134] He found her sitting at the breakfast-table, and the meal began almost

[038-135] in silence, the burghers walking past upon the pavement—or rather

[038-136] roadway, pavements being scarce here—which was two or three feet above

[038-137] the level of the parlour floor. They nodded down to the happy couple

[038-138] their morning greetings, as they went on.

[038-139] “Richard,” she said all at once; “would you mind my living away from

[038-140] you?”

[038-141] “Away from me? Why, that’s what you were doing when I married you. What

[038-142] then was the meaning of marrying at all?”

[038-143] “You wouldn’t like me any the better for telling you.”

[038-144] “I don’t object to know.”

[038-145] “Because I thought I could do nothing else. You had got my promise a

[038-146] long time before that, remember. Then, as time went on, I regretted I

[038-147] had promised you, and was trying to see an honourable way to break it

[038-148] off. But as I couldn’t I became rather reckless and careless about the

[038-149] conventions. Then you know what scandals were spread, and how I was

[038-150] turned out of the training school you had taken such time and trouble

[038-151] to prepare me for and get me into; and this frightened me and it seemed

[038-152] then that the one thing I could do would be to let the engagement

[038-153] stand. Of course I, of all people, ought not to have cared what was

[038-154] said, for it was just what I fancied I never did care for. But I was a

[038-155] coward—as so many women are—and my theoretic unconventionality broke

[038-156] down. If that had not entered into the case it would have been better

[038-157] to have hurt your feelings once for all then, than to marry you and

[038-158] hurt them all my life after… And you were so generous in never giving

[038-159] credit for a moment to the rumour.”

[038-160] “I am bound in honesty to tell you that I weighed its probability and

[038-161] inquired of your cousin about it.”

[038-162] “Ah!” she said with pained surprise.

[038-163] “I didn’t doubt you.”

[038-164] “But you inquired!”

[038-165] “I took his word.”

[038-166] Her eyes had filled. “_He_ wouldn’t have inquired!” she said. “But you

[038-167] haven’t answered me. Will you let me go away? I know how irregular it

[038-168] is of me to ask it—”

[038-169] “It is irregular.”

[038-170] “But I do ask it! Domestic laws should be made according to

[038-171] temperaments, which should be classified. If people are at all peculiar

[038-172] in character they have to suffer from the very rules that produce

[038-173] comfort in others! … Will you let me?”

[038-174] “But we married—”

[038-175] “What is the use of thinking of laws and ordinances,” she burst out,

[038-176] “if they make you miserable when you know you are committing no sin?”

[038-177] “But you are committing a sin in not liking me.”

[038-178] “I _do_ like you! But I didn’t reflect it would be—that it would be so

[038-179] much more than that… For a man and woman to live on intimate terms when

[038-180] one feels as I do is adultery, in any circumstances, however legal.

[038-181] There—I’ve said it! … Will you let me, Richard?”

[038-182] “You distress me, Susanna, by such importunity!”

[038-183] “Why can’t we agree to free each other? We made the compact, and surely

[038-184] we can cancel it—not legally of course; but we can morally, especially

[038-185] as no new interests, in the shape of children, have arisen to be looked

[038-186] after. Then we might be friends, and meet without pain to either. Oh

[038-187] Richard, be my friend and have pity! We shall both be dead in a few

[038-188] years, and then what will it matter to anybody that you relieved me

[038-189] from constraint for a little while? I daresay you think me eccentric,

[038-190] or super-sensitive, or something absurd. Well—why should I suffer for

[038-191] what I was born to be, if it doesn’t hurt other people?”
