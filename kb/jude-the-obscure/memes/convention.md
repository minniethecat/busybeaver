# Convention

Convention — the unwritten social rules and moral expectations of Victorian England — functions throughout *Jude the Obscure* as a pervasive, suffocating force against which the principal characters struggle and by which they are ultimately destroyed. Thomas Hardy presents convention not as a neutral social framework but as a destructive mechanism that punishes deviation with scandal, poverty, and death. The novel traces the arc of convention from background condition to active antagonist, showing how social norms acquire the force of law without law's formal processes or protections.

## Sue Bridehead and the Revolt Against Convention

Sue Bridehead is the novel's most articulate critic of social convention and its most tragic victim. She describes herself as someone whose rebellion was intellectual but whose courage failed when tested. Her account of falling from principle into conformity is the novel's most detailed anatomy of conventional enforcement:

> "I became rather reckless and careless about the conventions. Then you know what scandals were spread, and how I was turned out of the training school" (038-148)

The consequences are immediate and devastating — expulsion, loss of livelihood, social death. Sue continues:

> "Of course I, of all people, ought not to have cared what was said, for it was just what I fancied I never did care for. But I was a coward — as so many women are — and my theoretic unconventionality broke down." (038-154)

This passage is crucial to understanding convention in the novel. Sue's "theoretic unconventionality" — her intellectual rejection of social norms — is genuine but insufficient. Theory cannot withstand the material consequences of transgression: the training school expels her, her reputation is destroyed, her future as a teacher is foreclosed. The phrase "as so many women are" is partly Sue's self-recrimination and partly Hardy's acknowledgment that women bear the heaviest burden of conventional enforcement. A man's unconventionality may be excused as originality; a woman's is punished as immorality.

Sue's early relationship with Jude is characterized by a deliberate disregard for convention. They live together unmarried, and for a time, this freedom seems sustainable:

> "The screens and subtleties of convention had likewise disappeared." (022-150)

But this disappearance is temporary and illusory. The "screens" of convention have not vanished — they have simply become invisible to the lovers. Convention operates most effectively not when it is directly enforced but when it is internalized, when the characters begin to police themselves.

Jude suspects that Sue's withdrawal is not lack of love but the victory of convention over authentic feeling:

> "On reflection his brow showed perplexity. 'But perhaps it is that you don't love me — not that you have become conventional! Much as, under your teaching, I hate convention, I hope it _is_ that, not the other.'" (041-173)

Jude's implicit acknowledgment — "under your teaching, I hate convention" — reveals how much of his own unconventionality was learned from Sue. When she abandons her principles, he is left defending a position she taught him and then deserted. The tragedy is doubled: convention destroys Sue's integrity before it destroys her life.

By the novel's end, Jude confronts Sue with the full measure of her surrender:

> "'Where is your scorn of convention gone? I _would_ have died game!'" (068-075)

The hunting metaphor — "died game" — is telling. Convention is the hunter; Sue is the prey that once fought and now submits. Jude's question has no answer because Sue's scorn of convention has not "gone" anywhere — it has been beaten out of her by the accumulated pressure of social punishment.

## Convention as Social Violence

Hardy portrays convention as a form of institutional violence, operating through the mechanisms of scandal, gossip, and opinion. When Phillotson agrees to let Sue leave him — an act of personal decency — he is immediately punished by the community:

> "They have requested me to send in my resignation on account of my scandalous conduct in giving my tortured wife her liberty — or, as they call it, condoning her adultery." (043-065)

The school committee's language — "scandalous conduct," "condoning her adultery" — transforms Phillotson's act of compassion into a moral crime. The phrase "my tortured wife" is Phillotson's own; it registers his awareness of Sue's suffering even as the community insists on seeing only the violation of conventional norms.

Phillotson defends himself by arguing that "he did not see how an act of natural charity could injure morals" (043-095). But the community's response is unanimous and devastating:

> "All the respectable inhabitants and well-to-do fellow-natives of the town were against Phillotson to a man." (043-096)

Respectability — that most Victorian of virtues — is here exposed as the mechanism of persecution. To be "respectable" is to enforce convention against those who deviate from it. The "well-to-do" exercise their class privilege through moral condemnation; Phillotson, already socially precarious as a mere schoolmaster, has no defense against their collective judgment.

The novel repeatedly connects convention to violence and death. Sue's children die partly because the family's unconventional status has made them vulnerable — unable to find decent housing, unable to command social respect, unable to protect themselves from the slow grinding of public opinion. Sue makes this connection explicit:

> "I can't _bear_ that they, and everybody, should think people wicked because they may have chosen to live their own way! It is really these opinions that make the best intentioned people reckless, and actually become immoral!" (053-009)

This is the novel's sharpest critique of convention: that convention does not merely punish immorality but creates it. The "opinions" of others — the gossip, the scandal, the social death — push the "best intentioned people" into desperation. Convention is not the cure for immorality; it is the cause.

## Sue's Rejection of Respectability

Sue's rebellion against convention is crystallized in her rejection of "respectability" — the Victorian code of moral and social propriety that governed every aspect of women's lives:

> "I know you mean my good. But I don't want to be respectable!" (039-054)

This is a remarkable statement for a Victorian fictional heroine. To "not want to be respectable" is to reject the entire framework of feminine virtue upon which Victorian society was built. Sue does not want to be respectable because she understands respectability as a form of control — the internalized surveillance that makes external enforcement unnecessary.

The gap between Phillotson's natural charity and the community's respectability reveals the chasm between human decency and social convention — and the novel leaves no doubt about which prevails. Phillotson's defense that his act was charitable falls on deaf ears; against "the respectable inhabitants and well-to-do fellow-natives" (043-096), an individual's conscience counts for nothing.

Even practical economic life is governed by convention. Sue recognizes that her intellectual freedom cannot survive without economic independence:

> "'But even a baker must be conventional, to get customers.'" (053-142)

This is convention at its most insidious: not the dramatic scandal of a Phillotson being driven from his post, but the daily, grinding requirement that everyone — even a baker — must perform conventionality to survive. Convention is not optional; it is a condition of economic existence.

## Convention and Religious Orthodoxy

Convention in the novel extends to and is reinforced by religious orthodoxy. Sue's intellectual questioning of established belief is treated as scandalous, and her eventual submission to religious convention — remarrying Phillotson in a church ceremony she does not believe in — is presented as a form of self-annihilation:

> "Sue and himself had mentally travelled in opposite directions since the tragedy: events which had enlarged his own views of life, laws, customs, and dogmas, had not operated in the same manner on Sue's. She was no longer the same as in the independent days, when her intellect played like lambent lightning over conventions and formalities which he at that time respected, though he did not now." (060-028)

The image of Sue's intellect as "lambent lightning" — a flickering, beautiful, destructive force — is one of Hardy's most striking characterizations. Convention extinguishes this lightning. Sue, who once bought pagan statues and debated theological questions with fearless intelligence, becomes a woman who speaks of mortifying the flesh and submits to a marriage she abhors.

The "conventional garb of bereavement" (059-092) becomes a metaphor for the death of authentic feeling under the weight of social expectation. Sue does not merely wear mourning clothes; she wears convention itself as a costume that conceals the self-destruction beneath.

> "'We all do! Custom does it! It's all the same in the end!'" (055-130)

This fatalistic acceptance — spoken by a minor character about the inexorable power of social custom — is the novel's epigraph for convention. Custom does what it will; resistance is temporary; in the end, convention always wins.

## Convention as Mechanism of Social Control

Throughout the novel, Hardy anatomizes convention as a system of enforcement that operates through specific social mechanisms:

**Scandal** — the rapid circulation of moral judgment that turns private acts into public crimes. When the church's Ten Commandments are repaired by Jude while he lives unmarried with Sue, "a great scandal had been caused in the church that Sunday morning" (052-172). Scandal is convention's enforcement arm: it transforms individual choices into collective condemnations.

**Gossip** — the informal network through which conventional judgment circulates. Arabella's mother and neighbors engage in discussions that shape community opinion (009-101). Even the novel's narrator participates in this economy of information, noting how news travels through rural communities.

**Opinion** — the collective moral judgment that acquires quasi-legal force. Phillotson is asked about "the morals of the town; and, to ordinary opinion, your position is" (043-074). The phrase "ordinary opinion" is chilling: it is not law, not justice, not even articulated principle — merely what ordinary people think — that has the power to destroy a man's livelihood and a woman's freedom.

**Respectability** — the ultimate conventional value, against which every character is measured and most are found wanting. Arabella, the least respectable character by any moral standard, deploys the language of respectability most effectively. She indignantly protests: "Calling a respectable married woman by the name of that —" (070-025), wielding respectability as a weapon even as her own actions violate every principle it represents.

## Critical Interpretation

Hardy's treatment of convention in *Jude the Obscure* was among the most politically radical aspects of a novel notorious for its radicalism. By identifying convention — rather than individual moral failing — as the source of tragedy, Hardy shifted the burden of blame from character to society. The novel anticipates twentieth-century critiques of social control: Michel Foucault's concept of "discipline" — the internalized surveillance that makes external enforcement unnecessary — finds a literary precursor in Sue's struggle with the conventional expectations she has absorbed despite herself.

The novel's treatment of convention also intersects with feminist criticism. Sue Bridehead is one of nineteenth-century fiction's most complex portraits of a woman destroyed by the social enforcement of feminine propriety. Her "theoretic unconventionality" is not weakness but a rational response to a system in which women who deviate from convention are punished with social death. Her eventual submission — remarrying Phillotson in "the conventional garb of bereavement" — is not a failure of character but a demonstration of how thoroughly convention can break even its most articulate critics.

The convention theme extends to the novel's formal structure. Hardy's narrator repeatedly adopts the language of conventional morality only to undermine it — describing Jude and Sue's relationship in terms that echo social condemnation while the narrative's sympathies clearly lie with the condemned. This ironic use of conventional discourse is one of the novel's most powerful critical tools: convention is not merely described but performed and subverted in the novel's own language.
