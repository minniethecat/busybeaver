[009-001] “They were only put in this morning!” she cried, stimulated to pursue

[009-002] in spite of her lover’s presence. “They were drove from Spaddleholt

[009-003] Farm only yesterday, where Father bought ’em at a stiff price enough.

[009-004] They are wanting to get home again, the stupid toads! Will you shut the

[009-005] garden gate, dear, and help me to get ’em in. There are no men folk at

[009-006] home, only Mother, and they’ll be lost if we don’t mind.”

[009-007] He set himself to assist, and dodged this way and that over the potato

[009-008] rows and the cabbages. Every now and then they ran together, when he

[009-009] caught her for a moment and kissed her. The first pig was got back

[009-010] promptly; the second with some difficulty; the third a long-legged

[009-011] creature, was more obstinate and agile. He plunged through a hole in

[009-012] the garden hedge, and into the lane.

[009-013] “He’ll be lost if I don’t follow ’n!” said she. “Come along with me!”

[009-014] She rushed in full pursuit out of the garden, Jude alongside her,

[009-015] barely contriving to keep the fugitive in sight. Occasionally they

[009-016] would shout to some boy to stop the animal, but he always wriggled past

[009-017] and ran on as before.

[009-018] “Let me take your hand, darling,” said Jude. “You are getting out of

[009-019] breath.” She gave him her now hot hand with apparent willingness, and

[009-020] they trotted along together.

[009-021] “This comes of driving ’em home,” she remarked. “They always know the

[009-022] way back if you do that. They ought to have been carted over.”

[009-023] By this time the pig had reached an unfastened gate admitting to the

[009-024] open down, across which he sped with all the agility his little legs

[009-025] afforded. As soon as the pursuers had entered and ascended to the top

[009-026] of the high ground it became apparent that they would have to run all

[009-027] the way to the farmer’s if they wished to get at him. From this summit

[009-028] he could be seen as a minute speck, following an unerring line towards

[009-029] his old home.

[009-030] “It is no good!” cried Arabella. “He’ll be there long before we get

[009-031] there. It don’t matter now we know he’s not lost or stolen on the way.

[009-032] They’ll see it is ours, and send un back. Oh dear, how hot I be!”

[009-033] Without relinquishing her hold of Jude’s hand she swerved aside and

[009-034] flung herself down on the sod under a stunted thorn, precipitately

[009-035] pulling Jude on to his knees at the same time.

[009-036] “Oh, I ask pardon—I nearly threw you down, didn’t I! But I am so

[009-037] tired!”

[009-038] She lay supine, and straight as an arrow, on the sloping sod of this

[009-039] hill-top, gazing up into the blue miles of sky, and still retaining her

[009-040] warm hold of Jude’s hand. He reclined on his elbow near her.

[009-041] “We’ve run all this way for nothing,” she went on, her form heaving and

[009-042] falling in quick pants, her face flushed, her full red lips parted, and

[009-043] a fine dew of perspiration on her skin. “Well—why don’t you speak,

[009-044] deary?”

[009-045] “I’m blown too. It was all up hill.”

[009-046] They were in absolute solitude—the most apparent of all solitudes, that

[009-047] of empty surrounding space. Nobody could be nearer than a mile to them

[009-048] without their seeing him. They were, in fact, on one of the summits of

[009-049] the county, and the distant landscape around Christminster could be

[009-050] discerned from where they lay. But Jude did not think of that then.

[009-051] “Oh, I can see such a pretty thing up this tree,” said Arabella. “A

[009-052] sort of a—caterpillar, of the most loveliest green and yellow you ever

[009-053] came across!”

[009-054] “Where?” said Jude, sitting up.

[009-055] “You can’t see him there—you must come here,” said she.

[009-056] He bent nearer and put his head in front of hers. “No—I can’t see it,”

[009-057] he said.

[009-058] “Why, on the limb there where it branches off—close to the moving

[009-059] leaf—there!” She gently pulled him down beside her.

[009-060] “I don’t see it,” he repeated, the back of his head against her cheek.

[009-061] “But I can, perhaps, standing up.” He stood accordingly, placing

[009-062] himself in the direct line of her gaze.

[009-063] “How stupid you are!” she said crossly, turning away her face.

[009-064] “I don’t care to see it, dear: why should I?” he replied looking down

[009-065] upon her. “Get up, Abby.”

[009-066] “Why?”

[009-067] “I want you to let me kiss you. I’ve been waiting to ever so long!”

[009-068] She rolled round her face, remained a moment looking deedily aslant at

[009-069] him; then with a slight curl of the lip sprang to her feet, and

[009-070] exclaiming abruptly “I must mizzle!” walked off quickly homeward. Jude

[009-071] followed and rejoined her.

[009-072] “Just one!” he coaxed.

[009-073] “Shan’t!” she said.

[009-074] He, surprised: “What’s the matter?”

[009-075] She kept her two lips resentfully together, and Jude followed her like

[009-076] a pet lamb till she slackened her pace and walked beside him, talking

[009-077] calmly on indifferent subjects, and always checking him if he tried to

[009-078] take her hand or clasp her waist. Thus they descended to the precincts

[009-079] of her father’s homestead, and Arabella went in, nodding good-bye to

[009-080] him with a supercilious, affronted air.

[009-081] “I expect I took too much liberty with her, somehow,” Jude said to

[009-082] himself, as he withdrew with a sigh and went on to Marygreen.

[009-083] On Sunday morning the interior of Arabella’s home was, as usual, the

[009-084] scene of a grand weekly cooking, the preparation of the special Sunday

[009-085] dinner. Her father was shaving before a little glass hung on the

[009-086] mullion of the window, and her mother and Arabella herself were

[009-087] shelling beans hard by. A neighbour passed on her way home from morning

[009-088] service at the nearest church, and seeing Donn engaged at the window

[009-089] with the razor, nodded and came in.

[009-090] She at once spoke playfully to Arabella: “I zeed ’ee running with

[009-091] ’un—hee-hee! I hope ’tis coming to something?”

[009-092] Arabella merely threw a look of consciousness into her face without

[009-093] raising her eyes.

[009-094] “He’s for Christminster, I hear, as soon as he can get there.”

[009-095] “Have you heard that lately—quite lately?” asked Arabella with a

[009-096] jealous, tigerish indrawing of breath.

[009-097] “Oh no! But it has been known a long time that it is his plan. He’s

[009-098] on’y waiting here for an opening. Ah well: he must walk about with

[009-099] somebody, I s’pose. Young men don’t mean much now-a-days. ’Tis a sip

[009-100] here and a sip there with ’em. ’Twas different in my time.”

[009-101] When the gossip had departed Arabella said suddenly to her mother: “I

[009-102] want you and Father to go and inquire how the Edlins be, this evening

[009-103] after tea. Or no—there’s evening service at Fensworth—you can walk to

[009-104] that.”

[009-105] “Oh? What’s up to-night, then?”

[009-106] “Nothing. Only I want the house to myself. He’s shy; and I can’t get un

[009-107] to come in when you are here. I shall let him slip through my fingers

[009-108] if I don’t mind, much as I care for ’n!”

[009-109] “If it is fine we med as well go, since you wish.”

[009-110] In the afternoon Arabella met and walked with Jude, who had now for

[009-111] weeks ceased to look into a book of Greek, Latin, or any other tongue.

[009-112] They wandered up the slopes till they reached the green track along the

[009-113] ridge, which they followed to the circular British earth-bank

[009-114] adjoining, Jude thinking of the great age of the trackway, and of the

[009-115] drovers who had frequented it, probably before the Romans knew the

[009-116] country. Up from the level lands below them floated the chime of church

[009-117] bells. Presently they were reduced to one note, which quickened, and

[009-118] stopped.

[009-119] “Now we’ll go back,” said Arabella, who had attended to the sounds.

[009-120] Jude assented. So long as he was near her he minded little where he

[009-121] was. When they arrived at her house he said lingeringly: “I won’t come

[009-122] in. Why are you in such a hurry to go in to-night? It is not near

[009-123] dark.”

[009-124] “Wait a moment,” said she. She tried the handle of the door and found

[009-125] it locked.

[009-126] “Ah—they are gone to church,” she added. And searching behind the

[009-127] scraper she found the key and unlocked the door. “Now, you’ll come in a

[009-128] moment?” she asked lightly. “We shall be all alone.”

[009-129] “Certainly,” said Jude with alacrity, the case being unexpectedly

[009-130] altered.

[009-131] Indoors they went. Did he want any tea? No, it was too late: he would

[009-132] rather sit and talk to her. She took off her jacket and hat, and they

[009-133] sat down—naturally enough close together.

[009-134] “Don’t touch me, please,” she said softly. “I am part egg-shell. Or

[009-135] perhaps I had better put it in a safe place.” She began unfastening the

[009-136] collar of her gown.

[009-137] “What is it?” said her lover.

[009-138] “An egg—a cochin’s egg. I am hatching a very rare sort. I carry it

[009-139] about everywhere with me, and it will get hatched in less than three

[009-140] weeks.”

[009-141] “Where do you carry it?”

[009-142] “Just here.” She put her hand into her bosom and drew out the egg,

[009-143] which was wrapped in wool, outside it being a piece of pig’s bladder,

[009-144] in case of accidents. Having exhibited it to him she put it back, “Now

[009-145] mind you don’t come near me. I don’t want to get it broke, and have to

[009-146] begin another.”

[009-147] “Why do you do such a strange thing?”

[009-148] “It’s an old custom. I suppose it is natural for a woman to want to

[009-149] bring live things into the world.”

[009-150] “It is very awkward for me just now,” he said, laughing.

[009-151] “It serves you right. There—that’s all you can have of me”

[009-152] She had turned round her chair, and, reaching over the back of it,

[009-153] presented her cheek to him gingerly.

[009-154] “That’s very shabby of you!”

[009-155] “You should have catched me a minute ago when I had put the egg down!

[009-156] There!” she said defiantly, “I am without it now!” She had quickly

[009-157] withdrawn the egg a second time; but before he could quite reach her

[009-158] she had put it back as quickly, laughing with the excitement of her

[009-159] strategy. Then there was a little struggle, Jude making a plunge for it

[009-160] and capturing it triumphantly. Her face flushed; and becoming suddenly

[009-161] conscious he flushed also.

[009-162] They looked at each other, panting; till he rose and said: “One kiss,

[009-163] now I can do it without damage to property; and I’ll go!”

[009-164] But she had jumped up too. “You must find me first!” she cried.

[009-165] Her lover followed her as she withdrew. It was now dark inside the

[009-166] room, and the window being small he could not discover for a long time

[009-167] what had become of her, till a laugh revealed her to have rushed up the

[009-168] stairs, whither Jude rushed at her heels.

[009-169] IX

[009-170] It was some two months later in the year, and the pair had met

[009-171] constantly during the interval. Arabella seemed dissatisfied; she was

[009-172] always imagining, and waiting, and wondering.

[009-173] One day she met the itinerant Vilbert. She, like all the cottagers

[009-174] thereabout, knew the quack well, and she began telling him of her

[009-175] experiences. Arabella had been gloomy, but before he left her she had

[009-176] grown brighter. That evening she kept an appointment with Jude, who

[009-177] seemed sad.

[009-178] “I am going away,” he said to her. “I think I ought to go. I think it

[009-179] will be better both for you and for me. I wish some things had never

[009-180] begun! I was much to blame, I know. But it is never too late to mend.”

[009-181] Arabella began to cry. “How do you know it is not too late?” she said.

[009-182] “That’s all very well to say! I haven’t told you yet!” and she looked

[009-183] into his face with streaming eyes.

[009-184] “What?” he asked, turning pale. “Not…?”

[009-185] “Yes! And what shall I do if you desert me?”

[009-186] “Oh, Arabella—how can you say that, my dear! You _know_ I wouldn’t

[009-187] desert you!”

[009-188] “Well then—”

[009-189] “I have next to no wages as yet, you know; or perhaps I should have

[009-190] thought of this before… But, of course if that’s the case, we must

[009-191] marry! What other thing do you think I could dream of doing?”
