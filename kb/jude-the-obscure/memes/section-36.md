[036-001] She had opened a rosewood work-box, and was looking at a photograph.

[036-002] Having contemplated it a little while she pressed it against her bosom,

[036-003] and put it again in its place.

[036-004] Then becoming aware that she had not obscured the windows she came

[036-005] forward to do so, candle in hand. It was too dark for her to see Jude

[036-006] without, but he could see her face distinctly, and there was an

[036-007] unmistakable tearfulness about the dark, long-lashed eyes.

[036-008] She closed the shutters, and Jude turned away to pursue his solitary

[036-009] journey home. “Whose photograph was she looking at?” he said. He had

[036-010] once given her his; but she had others, he knew. Yet it was his,

[036-011] surely?

[036-012] He knew he should go to see her again, according to her invitation.

[036-013] Those earnest men he read of, the saints, whom Sue, with gentle

[036-014] irreverence, called his demi-gods, would have shunned such encounters

[036-015] if they doubted their own strength. But he could not. He might fast and

[036-016] pray during the whole interval, but the human was more powerful in him

[036-017] than the Divine.

[036-018] II

[036-019] However, if God disposed not, woman did. The next morning but one

[036-020] brought him this note from her:

[036-021] Don’t come next week. On your own account don’t! We were too free,

[036-022] under the influence of that morbid hymn and the twilight. Think no more

[036-023] than you can help of

[036-024] SUSANNA FLORENCE MARY.

[036-025] The disappointment was keen. He knew her mood, the look of her face,

[036-026] when she subscribed herself at length thus. But, whatever her mood, he

[036-027] could not say she was wrong in her view. He replied:

[036-028] I acquiesce. You are right. It is a lesson in renunciation which I

[036-029] suppose I ought to learn at this season.

[036-030] JUDE.

[036-031] He despatched the note on Easter Eve, and there seemed a finality in

[036-032] their decisions. But other forces and laws than theirs were in

[036-033] operation. On Easter Monday morning he received a message from the

[036-034] Widow Edlin, whom he had directed to telegraph if anything serious

[036-035] happened:

[036-036] Your aunt is sinking. Come at once.

[036-037] He threw down his tools and went. Three and a half hours later he was

[036-038] crossing the downs about Marygreen, and presently plunged into the

[036-039] concave field across which the short cut was made to the village. As he

[036-040] ascended on the other side a labouring man, who had been watching his

[036-041] approach from a gate across the path, moved uneasily, and prepared to

[036-042] speak. “I can see in his face that she is dead,” said Jude. “Poor Aunt

[036-043] Drusilla!”

[036-044] It was as he had supposed, and Mrs. Edlin had sent out the man to break

[036-045] the news to him.

[036-046] “She wouldn’t have knowed ’ee. She lay like a doll wi’ glass eyes; so

[036-047] it didn’t matter that you wasn’t here,” said he.

[036-048] Jude went on to the house, and in the afternoon, when everything was

[036-049] done, and the layers-out had finished their beer, and gone, he sat down

[036-050] alone in the silent place. It was absolutely necessary to communicate

[036-051] with Sue, though two or three days earlier they had agreed to mutual

[036-052] severance. He wrote in the briefest terms:

[036-053] Aunt Drusilla is dead, having been taken almost suddenly. The funeral

[036-054] is on Friday afternoon.

[036-055] He remained in and about Marygreen through the intervening days, went

[036-056] out on Friday morning to see that the grave was finished, and wondered

[036-057] if Sue would come. She had not written, and that seemed to signify

[036-058] rather that she would come than that she would not. Having timed her by

[036-059] her only possible train, he locked the door about mid-day, and crossed

[036-060] the hollow field to the verge of the upland by the Brown House, where

[036-061] he stood and looked over the vast prospect northwards, and over the

[036-062] nearer landscape in which Alfredston stood. Two miles behind it a jet

[036-063] of white steam was travelling from the left to the right of the

[036-064] picture.

[036-065] There was a long time to wait, even now, till he would know if she had

[036-066] arrived. He did wait, however, and at last a small hired vehicle pulled

[036-067] up at the bottom of the hill, and a person alighted, the conveyance

[036-068] going back, while the passenger began ascending the hill. He knew her;

[036-069] and she looked so slender to-day that it seemed as if she might be

[036-070] crushed in the intensity of a too passionate embrace—such as it was not

[036-071] for him to give. Two-thirds of the way up her head suddenly took a

[036-072] solicitous poise, and he knew that she had at that moment recognized

[036-073] him. Her face soon began a pensive smile, which lasted till, having

[036-074] descended a little way, he met her.

[036-075] “I thought,” she began with nervous quickness, “that it would be so sad

[036-076] to let you attend the funeral alone! And so—at the last moment—I came.”

[036-077] “Dear faithful Sue!” murmured Jude.

[036-078] With the elusiveness of her curious double nature, however, Sue did not

[036-079] stand still for any further greeting, though it wanted some time to the

[036-080] burial. A pathos so unusually compounded as that which attached to this

[036-081] hour was unlikely to repeat itself for years, if ever, and Jude would

[036-082] have paused, and meditated, and conversed. But Sue either saw it not at

[036-083] all, or, seeing it more than he, would not allow herself to feel it.

[036-084] The sad and simple ceremony was soon over, their progress to the church

[036-085] being almost at a trot, the bustling undertaker having a more important

[036-086] funeral an hour later, three miles off. Drusilla was put into the new

[036-087] ground, quite away from her ancestors. Sue and Jude had gone side by

[036-088] side to the grave, and now sat down to tea in the familiar house; their

[036-089] lives united at least in this last attention to the dead.

[036-090] “She was opposed to marriage, from first to last, you say?” murmured

[036-091] Sue.

[036-092] “Yes. Particularly for members of our family.”

[036-093] Her eyes met his, and remained on him awhile.

[036-094] “We are rather a sad family, don’t you think, Jude?”

[036-095] “She said we made bad husbands and wives. Certainly we make unhappy

[036-096] ones. At all events, I do, for one!”

[036-097] Sue was silent. “Is it wrong, Jude,” she said with a tentative tremor,

[036-098] “for a husband or wife to tell a third person that they are unhappy in

[036-099] their marriage? If a marriage ceremony is a religious thing, it is

[036-100] possibly wrong; but if it is only a sordid contract, based on material

[036-101] convenience in householding, rating, and taxing, and the inheritance of

[036-102] land and money by children, making it necessary that the male parent

[036-103] should be known—which it seems to be—why surely a person may say, even

[036-104] proclaim upon the housetops, that it hurts and grieves him or her?”

[036-105] “I have said so, anyhow, to you.”

[036-106] Presently she went on: “Are there many couples, do you think, where one

[036-107] dislikes the other for no definite fault?”

[036-108] “Yes, I suppose. If either cares for another person, for instance.”

[036-109] “But even apart from that? Wouldn’t the woman, for example, be very

[036-110] bad-natured if she didn’t like to live with her husband; merely”—her

[036-111] voice undulated, and he guessed things—“merely because she had a

[036-112] personal feeling against it—a physical objection—a fastidiousness, or

[036-113] whatever it may be called—although she might respect and be grateful to

[036-114] him? I am merely putting a case. Ought she to try to overcome her

[036-115] pruderies?”

[036-116] Jude threw a troubled look at her. He said, looking away: “It would be

[036-117] just one of those cases in which my experiences go contrary to my

[036-118] dogmas. Speaking as an order-loving man—which I hope I am, though I

[036-119] fear I am not—I should say, yes. Speaking from experience and unbiased

[036-120] nature, I should say, no. … Sue, I believe you are not happy!”

[036-121] “Of course I am!” she contradicted. “How can a woman be unhappy who has

[036-122] only been married eight weeks to a man she chose freely?”

[036-123] “‘Chose freely!’”

[036-124] “Why do you repeat it? … But I have to go back by the six o’clock

[036-125] train. You will be staying on here, I suppose?”

[036-126] “For a few days to wind up Aunt’s affairs. This house is gone now.

[036-127] Shall I go to the train with you?”

[036-128] A little laugh of objection came from Sue. “I think not. You may come

[036-129] part of the way.”

[036-130] “But stop—you can’t go to-night! That train won’t take you to Shaston.

[036-131] You must stay and go back to-morrow. Mrs. Edlin has plenty of room, if

[036-132] you don’t like to stay here?”

[036-133] “Very well,” she said dubiously. “I didn’t tell him I would come for

[036-134] certain.”

[036-135] Jude went to the widow’s house adjoining, to let her know; and

[036-136] returning in a few minutes sat down again.

[036-137] “It is horrible how we are circumstanced, Sue—horrible!” he said

[036-138] abruptly, with his eyes bent to the floor.

[036-139] “No! Why?”

[036-140] “I can’t tell you all my part of the gloom. Your part is that you ought

[036-141] not to have married him. I saw it before you had done it, but I thought

[036-142] I mustn’t interfere. I was wrong. I ought to have!”

[036-143] “But what makes you assume all this, dear?”

[036-144] “Because—I can see you through your feathers, my poor little bird!”

[036-145] Her hand lay on the table, and Jude put his upon it. Sue drew hers

[036-146] away.

[036-147] “That’s absurd, Sue,” cried he, “after what we’ve been talking about! I

[036-148] am more strict and formal than you, if it comes to that; and that you

[036-149] should object to such an innocent action shows that you are

[036-150] ridiculously inconsistent!”

[036-151] “Perhaps it was too prudish,” she said repentantly. “Only I have

[036-152] fancied it was a sort of trick of ours—too frequent perhaps. There, you

[036-153] may hold it as much as you like. Is that good of me?”

[036-154] “Yes; very.”

[036-155] “But I must tell him.”

[036-156] “Who?”

[036-157] “Richard.”

[036-158] “Oh—of course, if you think it necessary. But as it means nothing it

[036-159] may be bothering him needlessly.”

[036-160] “Well—are you sure you mean it only as my cousin?”

[036-161] “Absolutely sure. I have no feelings of love left in me.”

[036-162] “That’s news. How has it come to be?”

[036-163] “I’ve seen Arabella.”

[036-164] She winced at the hit; then said curiously, “When did you see her?”

[036-165] “When I was at Christminster.”

[036-166] “So she’s come back; and you never told me! I suppose you will live

[036-167] with her now?”

[036-168] “Of course—just as you live with your husband.”

[036-169] She looked at the window pots with the geraniums and cactuses, withered

[036-170] for want of attention, and through them at the outer distance, till her

[036-171] eyes began to grow moist. “What is it?” said Jude, in a softened tone.

[036-172] “Why should you be so glad to go back to her if—if what you used to say

[036-173] to me is still true—I mean if it were true then! Of course it is not

[036-174] now! How could your heart go back to Arabella so soon?”

[036-175] “A special Providence, I suppose, helped it on its way.”

[036-176] “Ah—it isn’t true!” she said with gentle resentment. “You are teasing

[036-177] me—that’s all—because you think I am not happy!”

[036-178] “I don’t know. I don’t wish to know.”

[036-179] “If I were unhappy it would be my fault, my wickedness; not that I

[036-180] should have a right to dislike him! He is considerate to me in

[036-181] everything; and he is very interesting, from the amount of general

[036-182] knowledge he has acquired by reading everything that comes in his way.

[036-183] … Do you think, Jude, that a man ought to marry a woman his own age, or

[036-184] one younger than himself—eighteen years—as I am than he?”

[036-185] “It depends upon what they feel for each other.”

[036-186] He gave her no opportunity of self-satisfaction, and she had to go on

[036-187] unaided, which she did in a vanquished tone, verging on tears:

[036-188] “I—I think I must be equally honest with you as you have been with me.

[036-189] Perhaps you have seen what it is I want to say?—that though I like Mr.

[036-190] Phillotson as a friend, I don’t like him—it is a torture to me to—live

[036-191] with him as a husband!—There, now I have let it out—I couldn’t help it,

[036-192] although I have been—pretending I am happy.—Now you’ll have a contempt

[036-193] for me for ever, I suppose!” She bent down her face upon her hands as

[036-194] they lay upon the cloth, and silently sobbed in little jerks that made

[036-195] the fragile three-legged table quiver.
