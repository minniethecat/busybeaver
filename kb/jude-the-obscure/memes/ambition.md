# Ambition

Ambition is the motor of *Jude the Obscure* — the force that propels Jude from the obscurity of Marygreen toward Christminster, drives his self-education, and ultimately produces his tragedy. Hardy presents ambition not as a sin but as a natural human impulse that Victorian social structure systematically punishes in those not born to privilege.

## Definition

In the novel, "ambition" refers primarily to Jude's intellectual and social aspirations: his desire for a university education, his dream of entering the Church, and his hope of transcending his class position through learning. Hardy distinguishes ambition from mere vanity or greed — Jude's ambition is genuine and noble, which makes its defeat more tragic. The novel also explores the absence of ambition (Sue's post-tragedy resignation) and its corrupt forms (Arabella's social climbing).

## In *Jude the Obscure*

### Manifestation

Ambition is introduced as Jude's defining trait: as a boy, he dreams of "a university degree" and "to be ordained" (001-152–154) — ambitions planted by Phillotson's example. Throughout Part First and Second, ambition structures Jude's daily life: he studies Latin while driving the baker's cart, walks miles to consult grammars, and measures every action against his goal of reaching Christminster.

The novel tracks the progressive defeat of ambition. Jude initially frames his aspiration as selfless: "to gratify ambition, for no personal gain" (014-037). Phillotson later diagnoses the problem: "a mundane ambition masquerading in a surplice" (022-049) — the university and Church are not paths of pure learning but institutions of class advancement. By Part Fifth, Jude and Sue have "lost all ambition, and were never so happy" (054-161) — a bitter observation that happiness and ambition are inversely related in a world where ambition cannot be fulfilled.

### Key Characters

| Character | Relation to Ambition |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Jude Fawley | Ambition defines him — his academic aspiration, his desire for Sue, his hope for a better life; the novel traces ambition's transformation from noble drive to source of suffering |
| Richard Phillotson | Represents achievable, modest ambition — the schoolmaster's career is the ceiling for a man of his class; his example both inspires and limits Jude |
| Sue Bridehead | Initially ambitious in an intellectual sense; her ambition is defeated differently than Jude's — through gender constraints rather than class barriers |

### Narrative Trajectory

Ambition is planted in the novel's opening pages (Part First), flourishes in Jude's self-directed study, is tested and rejected at Christminster (Part Second), enters a period of dormancy during the domestic sections (Parts Third through Fifth), and is finally extinguished in Part Sixth. Jude's final reflections on his failed ambition (062-028, 069-023) confirm that the aspiration that gave his life meaning also destroyed it — not because the aspiration was wrong, but because the gates it sought were barred.

## Critical Interpretation

Hardy's treatment of ambition in *Jude* is notably different from the Victorian success narrative (e.g., *David Copperfield*, *The Way of All Flesh*). In those novels, ambition tempered by virtue leads to modest fulfillment; in Jude, ambition regardless of virtue leads to catastrophe. The novel's radical claim is that the system is rigged — that "meritocracy" is an ideological cover for class reproduction. Modern criticism reads Jude's ambition through the lens of cultural capital (Bourdieu): Jude masters the content of elite culture (Latin, Greek, theology) but lacks the social and economic capital that makes that mastery legible to institutions.
