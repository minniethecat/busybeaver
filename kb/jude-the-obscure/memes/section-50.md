[050-075] There is in Upper Wessex an old town of nine or ten thousand souls; the

[050-076] town may be called Stoke-Barehills. It stands with its gaunt,

[050-077] unattractive, ancient church, and its new red brick suburb, amid the

[050-080] Wintoncester, and the important military station of Quartershot. The

[050-082] the road branches into two, merely to unite again some twenty miles

[050-083] further westward. Out of this bifurcation and reunion there used to

[050-084] arise among wheeled travellers, before railway days, endless questions

[050-085] of choice between the respective ways. But the question is now as dead

[050-086] as the scot-and-lot freeholder, the road waggoner, and the mail

[050-087] coachman who disputed it; and probably not a single inhabitant of

[050-088] Stoke-Barehills is now even aware that the two roads which part in his
