[032-001] The bar had been crowded with men of all sorts during the previous

[032-002] hour, and he had heard from without the hubbub of their voices; but the

[032-003] customers were fewer at last. He nodded to Arabella, and told her that

[032-004] she would find him outside the door when she came away.

[032-005] “But you must have something with me first,” she said with great good

[032-006] humour. “Just an early night-cap: I always do. Then you can go out and

[032-007] wait a minute, as it is best we should not be seen going together.” She

[032-008] drew a couple of liqueur glasses of brandy; and though she had

[032-009] evidently, from her countenance, already taken in enough alcohol either

[032-010] by drinking or, more probably, from the atmosphere she had breathed for

[032-011] so many hours, she finished hers quickly. He also drank his, and went

[032-012] outside the house.

[032-013] In a few minutes she came, in a thick jacket and a hat with a black

[032-014] feather. “I live quite near,” she said, taking his arm, “and can let

[032-015] myself in by a latch-key at any time. What arrangement do you want to

[032-016] come to?”

[032-017] “Oh—none in particular,” he answered, thoroughly sick and tired, his

[032-018] thoughts again reverting to Alfredston, and the train he did not go by;

[032-019] the probable disappointment of Sue that he was not there when she

[032-020] arrived, and the missed pleasure of her company on the long and lonely

[032-021] climb by starlight up the hills to Marygreen. “I ought to have gone

[032-022] back really! My aunt is on her deathbed, I fear.”

[032-023] “I’ll go over with you to-morrow morning. I think I could get a day

[032-024] off.”

[032-025] There was something particularly uncongenial in the idea of Arabella,

[032-026] who had no more sympathy than a tigress with his relations or him,

[032-027] coming to the bedside of his dying aunt, and meeting Sue. Yet he said,

[032-028] “Of course, if you’d like to, you can.”

[032-029] “Well, that we’ll consider… Now, until we have come to some agreement

[032-030] it is awkward our being together here—where you are known, and I am

[032-031] getting known, though without any suspicion that I have anything to do

[032-032] with you. As we are going towards the station, suppose we take the

[032-033] nine-forty train to Aldbrickham? We shall be there in little more than

[032-034] half an hour, and nobody will know us for one night, and we shall be

[032-035] quite free to act as we choose till we have made up our minds whether

[032-036] we’ll make anything public or not.”

[032-037] “As you like.”

[032-038] “Then wait till I get two or three things. This is my lodging.

[032-039] Sometimes when late I sleep at the hotel where I am engaged, so nobody

[032-040] will think anything of my staying out.”

[032-041] She speedily returned, and they went on to the railway, and made the

[032-042] half-hour’s journey to Aldbrickham, where they entered a third-rate inn

[032-043] near the station in time for a late supper.

[032-045] On the morrow between nine and half-past they were journeying back to

[032-046] Christminster, the only two occupants of a compartment in a third-class

[032-047] railway-carriage. Having, like Jude, made rather a hasty toilet to

[032-048] catch the train, Arabella looked a little frowsy, and her face was very

[032-049] far from possessing the animation which had characterized it at the bar

[032-050] the night before. When they came out of the station she found that she

[032-051] still had half an hour to spare before she was due at the bar. They

[032-052] walked in silence a little way out of the town in the direction of

[032-053] Alfredston. Jude looked up the far highway.

[032-054] “Ah … poor feeble me!” he murmured at last.

[032-055] “What?” said she.

[032-056] “This is the very road by which I came into Christminster years ago

[032-057] full of plans!”

[032-058] “Well, whatever the road is I think my time is nearly up, as I have to

[032-059] be in the bar by eleven o’clock. And as I said, I shan’t ask for the

[032-060] day to go with you to see your aunt. So perhaps we had better part

[032-061] here. I’d sooner not walk up Chief Street with you, since we’ve come to

[032-062] no conclusion at all.”

[032-063] “Very well. But you said when we were getting up this morning that you

[032-064] had something you wished to tell me before I left?”

[032-065] “So I had—two things—one in particular. But you wouldn’t promise to

[032-066] keep it a secret. I’ll tell you now if you promise? As an honest woman

[032-067] I wish you to know it… It was what I began telling you in the

[032-068] night—about that gentleman who managed the Sydney hotel.” Arabella

[032-069] spoke somewhat hurriedly for her. “You’ll keep it close?”

[032-070] “Yes—yes—I promise!” said Jude impatiently. “Of course I don’t want to

[032-071] reveal your secrets.”

[032-072] “Whenever I met him out for a walk, he used to say that he was much

[032-073] taken with my looks, and he kept pressing me to marry him. I never

[032-074] thought of coming back to England again; and being out there in

[032-075] Australia, with no home of my own after leaving my father, I at last

[032-076] agreed, and did.”

[032-077] “What—marry him?”

[032-078] “Yes.”

[032-079] “Regularly—legally—in church?”

[032-080] “Yes. And lived with him till shortly before I left. It was stupid, I

[032-081] know; but I did! There, now I’ve told you. Don’t round upon me! He

[032-082] talks of coming back to England, poor old chap. But if he does, he

[032-083] won’t be likely to find me.”

[032-084] Jude stood pale and fixed.

[032-085] “Why the devil didn’t you tell me last, night!” he said.

[032-086] “Well—I didn’t… Won’t you make it up with me, then?”

[032-087] “So in talking of ‘your husband’ to the bar gentlemen you meant him, of

[032-088] course—not me!”

[032-089] “Of course… Come, don’t fuss about it.”

[032-090] “I have nothing more to say!” replied Jude. “I have nothing at all to

[032-091] say about the—crime—you’ve confessed to!”

[032-092] “Crime! Pooh. They don’t think much of such as that over there! Lots of

[032-093] ’em do it… Well, if you take it like that I shall go back to him! He

[032-094] was very fond of me, and we lived honourable enough, and as respectable

[032-095] as any married couple in the colony! How did I know where you were?”

[032-096] “I won’t go blaming you. I could say a good deal; but perhaps it would

[032-097] be misplaced. What do you wish me to do?”

[032-098] “Nothing. There was one thing more I wanted to tell you; but I fancy

[032-099] we’ve seen enough of one another for the present! I shall think over

[032-100] what you said about your circumstances, and let you know.”

[032-101] Thus they parted. Jude watched her disappear in the direction of the

[032-102] hotel, and entered the railway station close by. Finding that it wanted

[032-103] three-quarters of an hour of the time at which he could get a train

[032-104] back to Alfredston, he strolled mechanically into the city as far as to

[032-105] the Fourways, where he stood as he had so often stood before, and

[032-106] surveyed Chief Street stretching ahead, with its college after college,

[032-107] in picturesqueness unrivalled except by such Continental vistas as the

[032-108] Street of Palaces in Genoa; the lines of the buildings being as

[032-109] distinct in the morning air as in an architectural drawing. But Jude

[032-110] was far from seeing or criticizing these things; they were hidden by an

[032-111] indescribable consciousness of Arabella’s midnight contiguity, a sense

[032-112] of degradation at his revived experiences with her, of her appearance

[032-113] as she lay asleep at dawn, which set upon his motionless face a look as

[032-114] of one accurst. If he could only have felt resentment towards her he

[032-115] would have been less unhappy; but he pitied while he contemned her.

[032-116] Jude turned and retraced his steps. Drawing again towards the station

[032-117] he started at hearing his name pronounced—less at the name than at the

[032-118] voice. To his great surprise no other than Sue stood like a vision

[032-119] before him—her look bodeful and anxious as in a dream, her little mouth

[032-120] nervous, and her strained eyes speaking reproachful inquiry.

[032-121] “Oh, Jude—I am so glad—to meet you like this!” she said in quick,

[032-122] uneven accents not far from a sob. Then she flushed as she observed his

[032-123] thought that they had not met since her marriage.

[032-124] They looked away from each other to hide their emotion, took each

[032-125] other’s hand without further speech, and went on together awhile, till

[032-126] she glanced at him with furtive solicitude. “I arrived at Alfredston

[032-127] station last night, as you asked me to, and there was nobody to meet

[032-128] me! But I reached Marygreen alone, and they told me Aunt was a trifle

[032-129] better. I sat up with her, and as you did not come all night I was

[032-130] frightened about you—I thought that perhaps, when you found yourself

[032-131] back in the old city, you were upset at—at thinking I was—married, and

[032-132] not there as I used to be; and that you had nobody to speak to; so you

[032-133] had tried to drown your gloom—as you did at that former time when you

[032-134] were disappointed about entering as a student, and had forgotten your

[032-135] promise to me that you never would again. And this, I thought, was why

[032-136] you hadn’t come to meet me!”

[032-137] “And you came to hunt me up, and deliver me, like a good angel!”

[032-138] “I thought I would come by the morning train and try to find you—in

[032-139] case—in case—”

[032-140] “I did think of my promise to you, dear, continually! I shall never

[032-141] break out again as I did, I am sure. I may have been doing nothing

[032-142] better, but I was not doing that—I loathe the thought of it.”

[032-143] “I am glad your staying had nothing to do with that. But,” she said,

[032-144] the faintest pout entering into her tone, “you didn’t come back last

[032-145] night and meet me, as you engaged to!”

[032-146] “I didn’t—I am sorry to say. I had an appointment at nine o’clock—too

[032-147] late for me to catch the train that would have met yours, or to get

[032-148] home at all.”

[032-149] Looking at his loved one as she appeared to him now, in his tender

[032-150] thought the sweetest and most disinterested comrade that he had ever

[032-151] had, living largely in vivid imaginings, so ethereal a creature that

[032-152] her spirit could be seen trembling through her limbs, he felt heartily

[032-153] ashamed of his earthliness in spending the hours he had spent in

[032-154] Arabella’s company. There was something rude and immoral in thrusting

[032-155] these recent facts of his life upon the mind of one who, to him, was so

[032-156] uncarnate as to seem at times impossible as a human wife to any average

[032-157] man. And yet she was Phillotson’s. How she had become such, how she

[032-158] lived as such, passed his comprehension as he regarded her to-day.

[032-159] “You’ll go back with me?” he said. “There’s a train just now. I wonder

[032-160] how my aunt is by this time… And so, Sue, you really came on my account

[032-161] all this way! At what an early time you must have started, poor thing!”

[032-162] “Yes. Sitting up watching alone made me all nerves for you, and instead

[032-163] of going to bed when it got light I started. And now you won’t frighten

[032-164] me like this again about your morals for nothing?”

[032-165] He was not so sure that she had been frightened about his morals for

[032-166] nothing. He released her hand till they had entered the train,—it

[032-167] seemed the same carriage he had lately got out of with another—where

[032-168] they sat down side by side, Sue between him and the window. He regarded

[032-169] the delicate lines of her profile, and the small, tight, applelike

[032-170] convexities of her bodice, so different from Arabella’s amplitudes.

[032-171] Though she knew he was looking at her she did not turn to him, but kept

[032-172] her eyes forward, as if afraid that by meeting his own some troublous

[032-173] discussion would be initiated.

[032-175] such a hurry that we have not said a word about it!”

[032-176] “There’s no necessity,” she quickly returned.
