[034-001] He walked out to this parish twice every Sunday, and sometimes in the

[034-002] week. One evening about Easter the choir met for practice, and a new

[034-003] hymn which Jude had heard of as being by a Wessex composer was to be

[034-004] tried and prepared for the following week. It turned out to be a

[034-005] strangely emotional composition. As they all sang it over and over

[034-006] again its harmonies grew upon Jude, and moved him exceedingly.

[034-007] When they had finished he went round to the organist to make inquiries.

[034-008] The score was in manuscript, the name of the composer being at the

[034-009] head, together with the title of the hymn: “The Foot of the Cross.”

[034-010] “Yes,” said the organist. “He is a local man. He is a professional

[034-011] musician at Kennetbridge—between here and Christminster. The vicar

[034-012] knows him. He was brought up and educated in Christminster traditions,

[034-013] which accounts for the quality of the piece. I think he plays in the

[034-014] large church there, and has a surpliced choir. He comes to Melchester

[034-015] sometimes, and once tried to get the cathedral organ when the post was

[034-016] vacant. The hymn is getting about everywhere this Easter.”

[034-017] As he walked humming the air on his way home, Jude fell to musing on

[034-018] its composer, and the reasons why he composed it. What a man of

[034-019] sympathies he must be! Perplexed and harassed as he himself was about

[034-020] Sue and Arabella, and troubled as was his conscience by the

[034-021] complication of his position, how he would like to know that man! “He

[034-022] of all men would understand my difficulties,” said the impulsive Jude.

[034-023] If there were any person in the world to choose as a confidant, this

[034-024] composer would be the one, for he must have suffered, and throbbed, and

[034-025] yearned.

[034-026] In brief, ill as he could afford the time and money for the journey,

[034-027] Fawley resolved, like the child that he was, to go to Kennetbridge the

[034-028] very next Sunday. He duly started, early in the morning, for it was

[034-029] only by a series of crooked railways that he could get to the town.

[034-030] About mid-day he reached it, and crossing the bridge into the quaint

[034-031] old borough he inquired for the house of the composer.

[034-032] They told him it was a red brick building some little way further on.

[034-033] Also that the gentleman himself had just passed along the street not

[034-034] five minutes before.

[034-035] “Which way?” asked Jude with alacrity.

[034-036] “Straight along homeward from church.”

[034-037] Jude hastened on, and soon had the pleasure of observing a man in a

[034-038] black coat and a black slouched felt hat no considerable distance

[034-039] ahead. Stretching out his legs yet more widely, he stalked after. “A

[034-040] hungry soul in pursuit of a full soul!” he said. “I must speak to that

[034-041] man!”

[034-042] He could not, however, overtake the musician before he had entered his

[034-043] own house, and then arose the question if this were an expedient time

[034-044] to call. Whether or not he decided to do so there and then, now that he

[034-045] had got here, the distance home being too great for him to wait till

[034-046] late in the afternoon. This man of soul would understand scant

[034-047] ceremony, and might be quite a perfect adviser in a case in which an

[034-048] earthly and illegitimate passion had cunningly obtained entrance into

[034-049] his heart through the opening afforded for religion.

[034-050] Jude accordingly rang the bell, and was admitted.

[034-051] The musician came to him in a moment, and being respectably dressed,

[034-052] good-looking, and frank in manner, Jude obtained a favourable

[034-053] reception. He was nevertheless conscious that there would be a certain

[034-054] awkwardness in explaining his errand.

[034-055] “I have been singing in the choir of a little church near Melchester,”

[034-056] he said. “And we have this week practised ‘The Foot of the Cross,’

[034-057] which I understand, sir, that you composed?”

[034-058] “I did—a year or so ago.”

[034-059] “I—like it. I think it supremely beautiful!”

[034-060] “Ah well—other people have said so too. Yes, there’s money in it, if I

[034-061] could only see about getting it published. I have other compositions to

[034-062] go with it, too; I wish I could bring them out; for I haven’t made a

[034-063] five-pound note out of any of them yet. These publishing people—they

[034-064] want the copyright of an obscure composer’s work, such as mine is, for

[034-065] almost less than I should have to pay a person for making a fair

[034-066] manuscript copy of the score. The one you speak of I have lent to

[034-067] various friends about here and Melchester, and so it has got to be sung

[034-068] a little. But music is a poor staff to lean on—I am giving it up

[034-069] entirely. You must go into trade if you want to make money nowadays.

[034-070] The wine business is what I am thinking of. This is my forthcoming

[034-071] list—it is not issued yet—but you can take one.”

[034-072] He handed Jude an advertisement list of several pages in booklet shape,

[034-073] ornamentally margined with a red line, in which were set forth the

[034-074] various clarets, champagnes, ports, sherries, and other wines with

[034-075] which he purposed to initiate his new venture. It took Jude more than

[034-076] by surprise that the man with the soul was thus and thus; and he felt

[034-077] that he could not open up his confidences.

[034-078] They talked a little longer, but constrainedly, for when the musician

[034-079] found that Jude was a poor man his manner changed from what it had been

[034-080] while Jude’s appearance and address deceived him as to his position and

[034-081] pursuits. Jude stammered out something about his feelings in wishing to

[034-082] congratulate the author on such an exalted composition, and took an

[034-083] embarrassed leave.

[034-084] All the way home by the slow Sunday train, sitting in the fireless

[034-085] waiting-rooms on this cold spring day, he was depressed enough at his

[034-086] simplicity in taking such a journey. But no sooner did he reach his

[034-087] Melchester lodging than he found awaiting him a letter which had

[034-088] arrived that morning a few minutes after he had left the house. It was

[034-089] a contrite little note from Sue, in which she said, with sweet

[034-090] humility, that she felt she had been horrid in telling him he was not

[034-091] to come to see her, that she despised herself for having been so

[034-092] conventional; and that he was to be sure to come by the

[034-093] eleven-forty-five train that very Sunday, and have dinner with them at

[034-094] half-past one.

[034-095] Jude almost tore his hair at having missed this letter till it was too

[034-096] late to act upon its contents; but he had chastened himself

[034-097] considerably of late, and at last his chimerical expedition to

[034-098] Kennetbridge really did seem to have been another special intervention

[034-099] of Providence to keep him away from temptation. But a growing

[034-100] impatience of faith, which he had noticed in himself more than once of

[034-101] late, made him pass over in ridicule the idea that God sent people on

[034-102] fools’ errands. He longed to see her; he was angry at having missed

[034-103] her: and he wrote instantly, telling her what had happened, and saying

[034-104] he had not enough patience to wait till the following Sunday, but would

[034-105] come any day in the week that she liked to name.

[034-106] Since he wrote a little over-ardently, Sue, as her manner was, delayed

[034-107] her reply till Thursday before Good Friday, when she said he might come

[034-108] that afternoon if he wished, this being the earliest day on which she

[034-109] could welcome him, for she was now assistant-teacher in her husband’s

[034-110] school. Jude therefore got leave from the cathedral works at the

[034-111] trifling expense of a stoppage of pay, and went.

[034-112] Part Fourth AT SHASTON

[034-113] _“Whoso prefers either Matrimony or other Ordinance before the Good of

[034-114] Man and the plain Exigence of Charity, let him profess Papist, or

[034-115] Protestant, or what he will, he is no better than a Pharisee.”_—J.

[034-116] MILTON.

[034-117] I

[034-118] Shaston, the ancient British Palladour,

[034-119] From whose foundation first such strange reports arise,

[034-120] (as Drayton sang it), was, and is, in itself the city of a dream. Vague

[034-121] imaginings of its castle, its three mints, its magnificent apsidal

[034-122] abbey, the chief glory of South Wessex, its twelve churches, its

[034-123] shrines, chantries, hospitals, its gabled freestone mansions—all now

[034-124] ruthlessly swept away—throw the visitor, even against his will, into a

[034-125] pensive melancholy, which the stimulating atmosphere and limitless

[034-126] landscape around him can scarcely dispel. The spot was the burial-place

[034-127] of a king and a queen, of abbots and abbesses, saints and bishops,

[034-128] knights and squires. The bones of King Edward “the Martyr,” carefully

[034-129] removed hither for holy preservation, brought Shaston a renown which

[034-130] made it the resort of pilgrims from every part of Europe, and enabled

[034-131] it to maintain a reputation extending far beyond English shores. To

[034-132] this fair creation of the great Middle-Age the Dissolution was, as

[034-133] historians tell us, the death-knell. With the destruction of the

[034-134] enormous abbey the whole place collapsed in a general ruin: the

[034-135] Martyr’s bones met with the fate of the sacred pile that held them, and

[034-136] not a stone is now left to tell where they lie.

[034-137] The natural picturesqueness and singularity of the town still remain;

[034-138] but strange to say these qualities, which were noted by many writers in

[034-139] ages when scenic beauty is said to have been unappreciated, are passed

[034-140] over in this, and one of the queerest and quaintest spots in England

[034-141] stands virtually unvisited to-day.

[034-142] It has a unique position on the summit of a steep and imposing scarp,

[034-143] rising on the north, south, and west sides of the borough out of the

[034-144] deep alluvial Vale of Blackmoor, the view from the Castle Green over

[034-145] three counties of verdant pasture—South, Mid, and Nether Wessex—being

[034-146] as sudden a surprise to the unexpectant traveller’s eyes as the

[034-147] medicinal air is to his lungs. Impossible to a railway, it can best be

[034-148] reached on foot, next best by light vehicles; and it is hardly

[034-149] accessible to these but by a sort of isthmus on the north-east, that

[034-150] connects it with the high chalk table-land on that side.

[034-151] Such is, and such was, the now world-forgotten Shaston or Palladour.

[034-152] Its situation rendered water the great want of the town; and within

[034-153] living memory, horses, donkeys and men may have been seen toiling up

[034-154] the winding ways to the top of the height, laden with tubs and barrels

[034-155] filled from the wells beneath the mountain, and hawkers retailing their

[034-156] contents at the price of a halfpenny a bucketful.

[034-157] This difficulty in the water supply, together with two other odd facts,

[034-158] namely, that the chief graveyard slopes up as steeply as a roof behind

[034-159] the church, and that in former times the town passed through a curious

[034-160] period of corruption, conventual and domestic, gave rise to the saying

[034-161] that Shaston was remarkable for three consolations to man, such as the

[034-162] world afforded not elsewhere. It was a place where the churchyard lay

[034-163] nearer heaven than the church steeple, where beer was more plentiful

[034-164] than water, and where there were more wanton women than honest wives

[034-165] and maids. It is also said that after the Middle Ages the inhabitants

[034-166] were too poor to pay their priests, and hence were compelled to pull

[034-167] down their churches, and refrain altogether from the public worship of

[034-168] God; a necessity which they bemoaned over their cups in the settles of

[034-169] their inns on Sunday afternoons. In those days the Shastonians were

[034-170] apparently not without a sense of humour.

[034-171] There was another peculiarity—this a modern one—which Shaston appeared

[034-172] to owe to its site. It was the resting-place and headquarters of the

[034-173] proprietors of wandering vans, shows, shooting-galleries, and other

[034-174] itinerant concerns, whose business lay largely at fairs and markets. As

[034-175] strange wild birds are seen assembled on some lofty promontory,

[034-176] meditatively pausing for longer flights, or to return by the course

[034-177] they followed thither, so here, in this cliff-town, stood in stultified

[034-178] silence the yellow and green caravans bearing names not local, as if

[034-179] surprised by a change in the landscape so violent as to hinder their

[034-180] further progress; and here they usually remained all the winter till

[034-181] they turned to seek again their old tracks in the following spring.
