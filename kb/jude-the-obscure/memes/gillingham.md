# Gillingham

Gillingham (referred to familiarly as "Dick") is a schoolmaster and the closest friend of [[Richard-Phillotson]] in *[[jude-fawley|Jude the Obscure]]*. He functions as a foil to [[richard-phillotson|Phillotson]]'s idealism: where [[richard-phillotson|Phillotson]] is ruled by conscience and feeling, Gillingham is governed by reason, convention, and worldly practicality. His counsel — always sensible, sometimes cold — helps shape [[richard-phillotson|Phillotson]]'s decisions about his disastrous marriage to [[Sue-Bridehead]].

## Character Overview

Gillingham is introduced as "his friend Gillingham, a neighbouring schoolmaster" (038-042). He is the person [[richard-phillotson|Phillotson]] turns to when his marriage to [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] begins to unravel. Gillingham's demeanour is grave, measured, and direct — "Now," said Gillingham, "let us take it quietly" (040-112). He genuinely cares for his friend's welfare but is constitutionally unable to endorse [[richard-phillotson|Phillotson]]'s radical willingness to release [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] from their marriage.

Hardy presents Gillingham as a representative of conventional wisdom. His advice is never wrong in a worldly sense — and never adequate to the depth of feeling [[richard-phillotson|Phillotson]] experiences. "Gillingham is so matter of fact," [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] observes dismissively (062-138).

## Role in the Narrative

Gillingham appears at critical junctures in [[richard-phillotson|Phillotson]]'s marriage. When [[richard-phillotson|Phillotson]] first confides that [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] is unhappy and wishes to leave him, Gillingham listens with sympathy but counsels against any "weakness" — by which he means any concession to [[sue-bridehead|Sue]]'s extraordinary request for freedom (040-061).

When [[richard-phillotson|Phillotson]] does release [[sue-bridehead|Sue]], Gillingham witnesses the consequences: his friend becomes a social outcast, and the schoolmaster visits to offer what support he can (043-031, 043-082). The narrator records that "Gillingham saw that his rather headstrong" friend was suffering deeply (043-082), and later "Gillingham was so interested in [[richard-phillotson|Phillotson]]'s" fate that he reflected at length on the case (044-044).

### Later Appearances

Gillingham reappears in the novel's closing sections, joining [[richard-phillotson|Phillotson]] on a visit to [[marygreen|Marygreen]]. The two old friends discuss [[jude-fawley|Jude]] and [[sue-bridehead|Sue]]'s situation with the distance of men observing a moral puzzle — "What are you thinking of?" Gillingham asks as they walk home (062-027). His practical, earthbound perspective provides a counterweight to [[richard-phillotson|Phillotson]]'s continued emotional involvement with [[sue-bridehead|Sue]]'s fate.

When [[richard-phillotson|Phillotson]] at last reunites with [[sue-bridehead|Sue]], Gillingham joins them — "Gillingham now joined them from his room" (063-147), a quiet witness to the painful resolution of a story he had advised against from the beginning.
