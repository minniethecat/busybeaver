[058-001] “I don’t know!” said Sue despondently. “I am afraid this will trouble

[058-002] your father.”

[058-003] “I wish Father was quite well, and there had been room for him! Then it

[058-004] wouldn’t matter so much! Poor Father!”

[058-005] “It wouldn’t!”

[058-006] “Can I do anything?”

[058-007] “No! All is trouble, adversity, and suffering!”

[058-008] “Father went away to give us children room, didn’t he?”

[058-009] “Partly.”

[058-010] “It would be better to be out o’ the world than in it, wouldn’t it?”

[058-011] “It would almost, dear.”

[058-012] “’Tis because of us children, too, isn’t it, that you can’t get a good

[058-013] lodging?”

[058-014] “Well—people do object to children sometimes.”

[058-015] “Then if children make so much trouble, why do people have ’em?”

[058-016] “Oh—because it is a law of nature.”

[058-017] “But we don’t ask to be born?”

[058-018] “No indeed.”

[058-019] “And what makes it worse with me is that you are not my real mother,

[058-020] and you needn’t have had me unless you liked. I oughtn’t to have come

[058-021] to ’ee—that’s the real truth! I troubled ’em in Australia, and I

[058-022] trouble folk here. I wish I hadn’t been born!”

[058-023] “You couldn’t help it, my dear.”

[058-024] “I think that whenever children be born that are not wanted they should

[058-025] be killed directly, before their souls come to ’em, and not allowed to

[058-026] grow big and walk about!”

[058-027] Sue did not reply. She was doubtfully pondering how to treat this too

[058-028] reflective child.

[058-029] She at last concluded that, so far as circumstances permitted, she

[058-030] would be honest and candid with one who entered into her difficulties

[058-031] like an aged friend.

[058-032] “There is going to be another in our family soon,” she hesitatingly

[058-033] remarked.

[058-034] “How?”

[058-035] “There is going to be another baby.”

[058-036] “What!” The boy jumped up wildly. “Oh God, Mother, you’ve never a-sent

[058-037] for another; and such trouble with what you’ve got!”

[058-038] “Yes, I have, I am sorry to say!” murmured Sue, her eyes glistening

[058-039] with suspended tears.

[058-040] The boy burst out weeping. “Oh you don’t care, you don’t care!” he

[058-041] cried in bitter reproach. “How _ever_ could you, Mother, be so wicked

[058-042] and cruel as this, when you needn’t have done it till we was better

[058-043] off, and Father well! To bring us all into _more_ trouble! No room for

[058-044] us, and Father a-forced to go away, and we turned out to-morrow; and

[058-045] yet you be going to have another of us soon! … ’Tis done o’

[058-046] purpose!—’tis—’tis!” He walked up and down sobbing.

[058-047] “Y-you must forgive me, little Jude!” she pleaded, her bosom heaving

[058-048] now as much as the boy’s. “I can’t explain—I will when you are older.

[058-049] It does seem—as if I had done it on purpose, now we are in these

[058-050] difficulties! I can’t explain, dear! But it—is not quite on purpose—I

[058-051] can’t help it!”

[058-052] “Yes it is—it must be! For nobody would interfere with us, like that,

[058-053] unless you agreed! I won’t forgive you, ever, ever! I’ll never believe

[058-054] you care for me, or Father, or any of us any more!”

[058-055] He got up, and went away into the closet adjoining her room, in which a

[058-056] bed had been spread on the floor. There she heard him say: “If we

[058-057] children was gone there’d be no trouble at all!”

[058-058] “Don’t think that, dear,” she cried, rather peremptorily. “But go to

[058-059] sleep!”

[058-060] The following morning she awoke at a little past six, and decided to

[058-061] get up and run across before breakfast to the inn which Jude had

[058-062] informed her to be his quarters, to tell him what had happened before

[058-063] he went out. She arose softly, to avoid disturbing the children, who,

[058-064] as she knew, must be fatigued by their exertions of yesterday.

[058-065] She found Jude at breakfast in the obscure tavern he had chosen as a

[058-066] counterpoise to the expense of her lodging: and she explained to him

[058-067] her homelessness. He had been so anxious about her all night, he said.

[058-068] Somehow, now it was morning, the request to leave the lodgings did not

[058-069] seem such a depressing incident as it had seemed the night before, nor

[058-070] did even her failure to find another place affect her so deeply as at

[058-071] first. Jude agreed with her that it would not be worth while to insist

[058-072] upon her right to stay a week, but to take immediate steps for removal.

[058-073] “You must all come to this inn for a day or two,” he said. “It is a

[058-074] rough place, and it will not be so nice for the children, but we shall

[058-075] have more time to look round. There are plenty of lodgings in the

[058-076] suburbs—in my old quarter of Beersheba. Have breakfast with me now you

[058-077] are here, my bird. You are sure you are well? There will be plenty of

[058-078] time to get back and prepare the children’s meal before they wake. In

[058-079] fact, I’ll go with you.”

[058-080] She joined Jude in a hasty meal, and in a quarter of an hour they

[058-081] started together, resolving to clear out from Sue’s too respectable

[058-082] lodging immediately. On reaching the place and going upstairs she found

[058-083] that all was quiet in the children’s room, and called to the landlady

[058-084] in timorous tones to please bring up the tea-kettle and something for

[058-085] their breakfast. This was perfunctorily done, and producing a couple of

[058-086] eggs which she had brought with her she put them into the boiling

[058-087] kettle, and summoned Jude to watch them for the youngsters, while she

[058-088] went to call them, it being now about half-past eight o’clock.

[058-089] Jude stood bending over the kettle, with his watch in his hand, timing

[058-090] the eggs, so that his back was turned to the little inner chamber where

[058-091] the children lay. A shriek from Sue suddenly caused him to start round.

[058-092] He saw that the door of the room, or rather closet—which had seemed to

[058-093] go heavily upon its hinges as she pushed it back—was open, and that Sue

[058-094] had sunk to the floor just within it. Hastening forward to pick her up

[058-095] he turned his eyes to the little bed spread on the boards; no children

[058-096] were there. He looked in bewilderment round the room. At the back of

[058-097] the door were fixed two hooks for hanging garments, and from these the

[058-098] forms of the two youngest children were suspended, by a piece of

[058-099] box-cord round each of their necks, while from a nail a few yards off

[058-100] the body of little Jude was hanging in a similar manner. An overturned

[058-101] chair was near the elder boy, and his glazed eyes were slanted into the

[058-102] room; but those of the girl and the baby boy were closed.

[058-103] Half-paralyzed by the strange and consummate horror of the scene, he

[058-104] let Sue lie, cut the cords with his pocket-knife and threw the three

[058-105] children on the bed; but the feel of their bodies in the momentary

[058-106] handling seemed to say that they were dead. He caught up Sue, who was

[058-107] in fainting fits, and put her on the bed in the other room, after which

[058-108] he breathlessly summoned the landlady and ran out for a doctor.

[058-109] When he got back Sue had come to herself, and the two helpless women,

[058-110] bending over the children in wild efforts to restore them, and the

[058-111] triplet of little corpses, formed a sight which overthrew his

[058-112] self-command. The nearest surgeon came in, but, as Jude had inferred,

[058-113] his presence was superfluous. The children were past saving, for though

[058-114] their bodies were still barely cold it was conjectured that they had

[058-115] been hanging more than an hour. The probability held by the parents

[058-116] later on, when they were able to reason on the case, was that the elder

[058-117] boy, on waking, looked into the outer room for Sue, and, finding her

[058-118] absent, was thrown into a fit of aggravated despondency that the events

[058-119] and information of the evening before had induced in his morbid

[058-120] temperament. Moreover a piece of paper was found upon the floor, on

[058-121] which was written, in the boy’s hand, with the bit of lead pencil that

[058-122] he carried:

[058-123] _Done because we are too menny._

[058-124] At sight of this Sue’s nerves utterly gave way, an awful conviction

[058-125] that her discourse with the boy had been the main cause of the tragedy,

[058-126] throwing her into a convulsive agony which knew no abatement. They

[058-127] carried her away against her wish to a room on the lower floor; and

[058-128] there she lay, her slight figure shaken with her gasps, and her eyes

[058-129] staring at the ceiling, the woman of the house vainly trying to soothe

[058-130] her.

[058-131] They could hear from this chamber the people moving about above, and

[058-132] she implored to be allowed to go back, and was only kept from doing so

[058-133] by the assurance that, if there were any hope, her presence might do

[058-134] harm, and the reminder that it was necessary to take care of herself

[058-135] lest she should endanger a coming life. Her inquiries were incessant,

[058-136] and at last Jude came down and told her there was no hope. As soon as

[058-137] she could speak she informed him what she had said to the boy, and how

[058-138] she thought herself the cause of this.

[058-139] “No,” said Jude. “It was in his nature to do it. The Doctor says there

[058-140] are such boys springing up amongst us—boys of a sort unknown in the

[058-141] last generation—the outcome of new views of life. They seem to see all

[058-142] its terrors before they are old enough to have staying power to resist

[058-143] them. He says it is the beginning of the coming universal wish not to

[058-144] live. He’s an advanced man, the Doctor: but he can give no consolation

[058-145] to—”

[058-146] Jude had kept back his own grief on account of her; but he now broke

[058-147] down; and this stimulated Sue to efforts of sympathy which in some

[058-148] degree distracted her from her poignant self-reproach. When everybody

[058-149] was gone, she was allowed to see the children.

[058-150] The boy’s face expressed the whole tale of their situation. On that

[058-151] little shape had converged all the inauspiciousness and shadow which

[058-152] had darkened the first union of Jude, and all the accidents, mistakes,

[058-153] fears, errors of the last. He was their nodal point, their focus, their

[058-154] expression in a single term. For the rashness of those parents he had

[058-155] groaned, for their ill assortment he had quaked, and for the

[058-156] misfortunes of these he had died.

[058-157] When the house was silent, and they could do nothing but await the

[058-158] coroner’s inquest, a subdued, large, low voice spread into the air of

[058-159] the room from behind the heavy walls at the back.

[058-160] “What is it?” said Sue, her spasmodic breathing suspended.

[058-161] “The organ of the college chapel. The organist practising I suppose.

[058-162] It’s the anthem from the seventy-third Psalm; ‘Truly God is loving unto

[058-163] Israel.’”

[058-164] She sobbed again. “Oh, oh my babies! They had done no harm! Why should

[058-165] they have been taken away, and not I!”

[058-166] There was another stillness—broken at last by two persons in

[058-167] conversation somewhere without.

[058-168] “They are talking about us, no doubt!” moaned Sue. “‘We are made a

[058-169] spectacle unto the world, and to angels, and to men!’”

[058-170] Jude listened—“No—they are not talking of us,” he said. “They are two

[058-171] clergymen of different views, arguing about the eastward position. Good

[058-172] God—the eastward position, and all creation groaning!”

[058-173] Then another silence, till she was seized with another uncontrollable

[058-174] fit of grief. “There is something external to us which says, ‘You

[058-175] shan’t!’ First it said, ‘You shan’t learn!’ Then it said, ‘You shan’t

[058-176] labour!’ Now it says, ‘You shan’t love!’”

[058-177] He tried to soothe her by saying, “That’s bitter of you, darling.”

[058-178] “But it’s true!”

[058-179] Thus they waited, and she went back again to her room. The baby’s

[058-180] frock, shoes, and socks, which had been lying on a chair at the time of

[058-181] his death, she would not now have removed, though Jude would fain have

[058-182] got them out of her sight. But whenever he touched them she implored

[058-183] him to let them lie, and burst out almost savagely at the woman of the

[058-184] house when she also attempted to put them away.
