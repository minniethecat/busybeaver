# Aldbrickham

Aldbrickham is the county town of North Wessex where [[jude-fawley|Jude Fawley]] and [[sue-bridehead|Sue Bridehead]] attempt to live together as a couple without marrying — the novel's most sustained experiment in unconventional domestic life. Larger than [[alfredston|Alfredston]] and more worldly than [[marygreen|Marygreen]], Aldbrickham is a town big enough for anonymity but too small to escape the social surveillance that ultimately drives [[jude-fawley|Jude]] and [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] from its streets. Part Fifth of the novel, titled "At Aldbrickham and Elsewhere," places the town at the center of the couple's struggle to exist beyond convention.

## Overview

Aldbrickham is described as "a much bigger town — sixty or seventy thousand inhabitants" (041-152), a substantial county seat with railway connections to [[london|London]]. [[jude-fawley|Jude]] and [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] settle there after leaving [[melchester|Melchester]], taking a small house where they live with [[sue-bridehead|Sue]]'s unusual child, later known as [[Little-Father-Time]]. The town provides [[jude-fawley|Jude]] with stonemasonry work and [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] with a degree of distance from her past, but it is here that their domestic experiment faces its most persistent challenge: the refusal of their neighbors, landlords, and employers to accept a couple living together outside [[marriage|wedlock]].

## In the Novel

### Narrative Significance

Aldbrickham represents the middle ground of the novel's geography — neither the rural isolation of [[marygreen|Marygreen]] nor the institutional power of [[christminster|Christminster]], but a site of ordinary provincial life where [[jude-fawley|Jude]] and [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] attempt to prove that a man and woman can live in dignity without the sanction of church or state. Their failure is not dramatic but cumulative: whispered gossip, a landlord's refusal to renew their lease, the steady closing of social doors. The town's size works against them — too large to grant them the tolerance that anonymity might provide, too small to let them disappear.

Aldbrickham is also where [[arabella-donn|Arabella Donn]], now remarried as [[arabella-donn|Mrs. Cartlett]], reappears to complicate [[jude-fawley|Jude]]'s life. The town thus becomes the stage for the novel's intertwined domestic triangles, past and present.

## Real-World Basis

| Fictional Name | Real Location | County |
|---------------|---------------|--------|
| Aldbrickham | Reading | Berkshire |

Hardy modeled Aldbrickham on Reading, the largest town in Berkshire and a major railway junction. Reading in the 1890s was a prosperous county town undergoing rapid expansion, with the kind of mixed population — tradespeople, railway workers, small shopkeepers — that could both accommodate and ostracize a couple like [[jude-fawley|Jude]] and [[sue-bridehead|Sue]]. Hardy knew Reading well, having lived there briefly, and its combination of provincial self-regard and social conservatism is precisely what he captures in Aldbrickham.
