[004-001] They were elderly men, and had genial voices. Jude addressed them,

[004-002] inquiring if they had come from Christminster.

[004-003] “Heaven forbid, with this load!” said they.

[004-004] “The place I mean is that one yonder.” He was getting so romantically

[004-005] attached to Christminster that, like a young lover alluding to his

[004-006] mistress, he felt bashful at mentioning its name again. He pointed to

[004-007] the light in the sky—hardly perceptible to their older eyes.

[004-008] “Yes. There do seem a spot a bit brighter in the nor’-east than

[004-009] elsewhere, though I shouldn’t ha’ noticed it myself, and no doubt it

[004-010] med be Christminster.”

[004-011] Here a little book of tales which Jude had tucked up under his arm,

[004-012] having brought them to read on his way hither before it grew dark,

[004-013] slipped and fell into the road. The carter eyed him while he picked it

[004-014] up and straightened the leaves.

[004-015] “Ah, young man,” he observed, “you’d have to get your head screwed on

[004-016] t’other way before you could read what they read there.”

[004-017] “Why?” asked the boy.

[004-018] “Oh, they never look at anything that folks like we can understand,”

[004-019] the carter continued, by way of passing the time. “On’y foreign tongues

[004-020] used in the days of the Tower of Babel, when no two families spoke

[004-021] alike. They read that sort of thing as fast as a night-hawk will whir.

[004-022] ’Tis all learning there—nothing but learning, except religion. And

[004-023] that’s learning too, for I never could understand it. Yes, ’tis a

[004-024] serious-minded place. Not but there’s wenches in the streets o’ nights…

[004-025] You know, I suppose, that they raise pa’sons there like radishes in a

[004-026] bed? And though it do take—how many years, Bob?—five years to turn a

[004-027] lirruping hobble-de-hoy chap into a solemn preaching man with no

[004-028] corrupt passions, they’ll do it, if it can be done, and polish un off

[004-029] like the workmen they be, and turn un out wi’ a long face, and a long

[004-030] black coat and waistcoat, and a religious collar and hat, same as they

[004-031] used to wear in the Scriptures, so that his own mother wouldn’t know un

[004-032] sometimes. … There, ’tis their business, like anybody else’s.”

[004-033] “But how should you know”

[004-034] “Now don’t you interrupt, my boy. Never interrupt your senyers. Move

[004-035] the fore hoss aside, Bobby; here’s som’at coming… You must mind that I

[004-036] be a-talking of the college life. ’Em lives on a lofty level; there’s

[004-037] no gainsaying it, though I myself med not think much of ’em. As we be

[004-038] here in our bodies on this high ground, so be they in their

[004-039] minds—noble-minded men enough, no doubt—some on ’em—able to earn

[004-040] hundreds by thinking out loud. And some on ’em be strong young fellows

[004-041] that can earn a’most as much in silver cups. As for music, there’s

[004-042] beautiful music everywhere in Christminster. You med be religious, or

[004-043] you med not, but you can’t help striking in your homely note with the

[004-044] rest. And there’s a street in the place—the main street—that ha’n’t

[004-045] another like it in the world. I should think I did know a little about

[004-046] Christminster!”

[004-047] By this time the horses had recovered breath and bent to their collars

[004-048] again. Jude, throwing a last adoring look at the distant halo, turned

[004-049] and walked beside his remarkably well-informed friend, who had no

[004-050] objection to telling him as they moved on more yet of the city—its

[004-051] towers and halls and churches. The waggon turned into a cross-road,

[004-052] whereupon Jude thanked the carter warmly for his information, and said

[004-053] he only wished he could talk half as well about Christminster as he.

[004-054] “Well, ’tis oonly what has come in my way,” said the carter

[004-055] unboastfully. “I’ve never been there, no more than you; but I’ve picked

[004-056] up the knowledge here and there, and you be welcome to it. A-getting

[004-057] about the world as I do, and mixing with all classes of society, one

[004-058] can’t help hearing of things. A friend o’ mine, that used to clane the

[004-059] boots at the Crozier Hotel in Christminster when he was in his prime,

[004-060] why, I knowed un as well as my own brother in his later years.”

[004-061] Jude continued his walk homeward alone, pondering so deeply that he

[004-062] forgot to feel timid. He suddenly grew older. It had been the yearning

[004-063] of his heart to find something to anchor on, to cling to—for some place

[004-064] which he could call admirable. Should he find that place in this city

[004-065] if he could get there? Would it be a spot in which, without fear of

[004-066] farmers, or hindrance, or ridicule, he could watch and wait, and set

[004-067] himself to some mighty undertaking like the men of old of whom he had

[004-068] heard? As the halo had been to his eyes when gazing at it a quarter of

[004-069] an hour earlier, so was the spot mentally to him as he pursued his dark

[004-070] way.

[004-071] “It is a city of light,” he said to himself.

[004-072] “The tree of knowledge grows there,” he added a few steps further on.

[004-073] “It is a place that teachers of men spring from and go to.”

[004-074] “It is what you may call a castle, manned by scholarship and religion.”

[004-075] After this figure he was silent a long while, till he added:

[004-076] “It would just suit me.”

[004-077] IV

[004-078] Walking somewhat slowly by reason of his concentration, the boy—an

[004-079] ancient man in some phases of thought, much younger than his years in

[004-080] others—was overtaken by a light-footed pedestrian, whom,

[004-081] notwithstanding the gloom, he could perceive to be wearing an

[004-082] extraordinarily tall hat, a swallow-tailed coat, and a watch-chain that

[004-083] danced madly and threw around scintillations of sky-light as its owner

[004-084] swung along upon a pair of thin legs and noiseless boots. Jude,

[004-085] beginning to feel lonely, endeavoured to keep up with him.

[004-086] “Well, my man! I’m in a hurry, so you’ll have to walk pretty fast if

[004-087] you keep alongside of me. Do you know who I am?”

[004-088] “Yes, I think. Physician Vilbert?”

[004-089] “Ah—I’m known everywhere, I see! That comes of being a public

[004-090] benefactor.”

[004-091] Vilbert was an itinerant quack-doctor, well known to the rustic

[004-092] population, and absolutely unknown to anybody else, as he, indeed, took

[004-093] care to be, to avoid inconvenient investigations. Cottagers formed his

[004-094] only patients, and his Wessex-wide repute was among them alone. His

[004-095] position was humbler and his field more obscure than those of the

[004-096] quacks with capital and an organized system of advertising. He was, in

[004-097] fact, a survival. The distances he traversed on foot were enormous, and

[004-098] extended nearly the whole length and breadth of Wessex. Jude had one

[004-099] day seen him selling a pot of coloured lard to an old woman as a

[004-100] certain cure for a bad leg, the woman arranging to pay a guinea, in

[004-101] instalments of a shilling a fortnight, for the precious salve, which,

[004-102] according to the physician, could only be obtained from a particular

[004-103] animal which grazed on Mount Sinai, and was to be captured only at

[004-104] great risk to life and limb. Jude, though he already had his doubts

[004-105] about this gentleman’s medicines, felt him to be unquestionably a

[004-106] travelled personage, and one who might be a trustworthy source of

[004-107] information on matters not strictly professional.

[004-108] “I s’pose you’ve been to Christminster, Physician?”

[004-109] “I have—many times,” replied the long thin man. “That’s one of my

[004-110] centres.”

[004-111] “It’s a wonderful city for scholarship and religion?”

[004-112] “You’d say so, my boy, if you’d seen it. Why, the very sons of the old

[004-113] women who do the washing of the colleges can talk in Latin—not good

[004-114] Latin, that I admit, as a critic: dog-Latin—cat-Latin, as we used to

[004-115] call it in my undergraduate days.”

[004-116] “And Greek?”

[004-117] “Well—that’s more for the men who are in training for bishops, that

[004-118] they may be able to read the New Testament in the original.”

[004-119] “I want to learn Latin and Greek myself.”

[004-120] “A lofty desire. You must get a grammar of each tongue.”

[004-121] “I mean to go to Christminster some day.”

[004-122] “Whenever you do, you say that Physician Vilbert is the only proprietor

[004-123] of those celebrated pills that infallibly cure all disorders of the

[004-124] alimentary system, as well as asthma and shortness of breath. Two and

[004-125] threepence a box—specially licensed by the government stamp.”

[004-126] “Can you get me the grammars if I promise to say it hereabout?”

[004-127] “I’ll sell you mine with pleasure—those I used as a student.”

[004-128] “Oh, thank you, sir!” said Jude gratefully, but in gasps, for the

[004-129] amazing speed of the physician’s walk kept him in a dog-trot which was

[004-130] giving him a stitch in the side.

[004-131] “I think you’d better drop behind, my young man. Now I’ll tell you what

[004-132] I’ll do. I’ll get you the grammars, and give you a first lesson, if

[004-133] you’ll remember, at every house in the village, to recommend Physician

[004-134] Vilbert’s golden ointment, life-drops, and female pills.”

[004-135] “Where will you be with the grammars?”

[004-136] “I shall be passing here this day fortnight at precisely this hour of

[004-137] five-and-twenty minutes past seven. My movements are as truly timed as

[004-138] those of the planets in their courses.”

[004-139] “Here I’ll be to meet you,” said Jude.

[004-140] “With orders for my medicines?”

[004-141] “Yes, Physician.”

[004-142] Jude then dropped behind, waited a few minutes to recover breath, and

[004-143] went home with a consciousness of having struck a blow for

[004-144] Christminster.

[004-145] Through the intervening fortnight he ran about and smiled outwardly at

[004-146] his inward thoughts, as if they were people meeting and nodding to

[004-147] him—smiled with that singularly beautiful irradiation which is seen to

[004-148] spread on young faces at the inception of some glorious idea, as if a

[004-149] supernatural lamp were held inside their transparent natures, giving

[004-150] rise to the flattering fancy that heaven lies about them then.

[004-151] He honestly performed his promise to the man of many cures, in whom he

[004-152] now sincerely believed, walking miles hither and thither among the

[004-153] surrounding hamlets as the Physician’s agent in advance. On the evening

[004-154] appointed he stood motionless on the plateau, at the place where he had

[004-155] parted from Vilbert, and there awaited his approach. The road-physician

[004-156] was fairly up to time; but, to the surprise of Jude on striking into

[004-157] his pace, which the pedestrian did not diminish by a single unit of

[004-158] force, the latter seemed hardly to recognize his young companion,

[004-159] though with the lapse of the fortnight the evenings had grown light.

[004-160] Jude thought it might perhaps be owing to his wearing another hat, and

[004-161] he saluted the physician with dignity.

[004-162] “Well, my boy?” said the latter abstractedly.

[004-163] “I’ve come,” said Jude.

[004-164] “You? who are you? Oh yes—to be sure! Got any orders, lad?”

[004-165] “Yes.” And Jude told him the names and addresses of the cottagers who

[004-166] were willing to test the virtues of the world-renowned pills and salve.

[004-167] The quack mentally registered these with great care.

[004-168] “And the Latin and Greek grammars?” Jude’s voice trembled with anxiety.

[004-169] “What about them?”

[004-170] “You were to bring me yours, that you used before you took your

[004-171] degree.”

[004-172] “Ah, yes, yes! Forgot all about it—all! So many lives depending on my

[004-173] attention, you see, my man, that I can’t give so much thought as I

[004-174] would like to other things.”

[004-175] Jude controlled himself sufficiently long to make sure of the truth;

[004-176] and he repeated, in a voice of dry misery, “You haven’t brought ’em!”

[004-177] “No. But you must get me some more orders from sick people, and I’ll

[004-178] bring the grammars next time.”

[004-179] Jude dropped behind. He was an unsophisticated boy, but the gift of

[004-180] sudden insight which is sometimes vouchsafed to children showed him all

[004-181] at once what shoddy humanity the quack was made of. There was to be no

[004-182] intellectual light from this source. The leaves dropped from his

[004-183] imaginary crown of laurel; he turned to a gate, leant against it, and

[004-184] cried bitterly.

[004-185] The disappointment was followed by an interval of blankness. He might,

[004-186] perhaps, have obtained grammars from Alfredston, but to do that

[004-187] required money, and a knowledge of what books to order; and though

[004-188] physically comfortable, he was in such absolute dependence as to be

[004-189] without a farthing of his own.
