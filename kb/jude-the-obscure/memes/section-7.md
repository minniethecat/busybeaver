[007-001] “What made either of the others throw it, I wonder?” Jude asked,

[007-002] politely accepting her assertion, though he had very large doubts as to

[007-003] its truth.

[007-004] “Impudence. Don’t tell folk it was I, mind!”

[007-005] “How can I? I don’t know your name.”

[007-006] “Ah, no. Shall I tell it to you?”

[007-007] “Do!”

[007-008] “Arabella Donn. I’m living here.”

[007-009] “I must have known it if I had often come this way. But I mostly go

[007-010] straight along the high-road.”

[007-011] “My father is a pig-breeder, and these girls are helping me wash the

[007-012] innerds for black-puddings and such like.”

[007-013] They talked a little more and a little more, as they stood regarding

[007-014] each other and leaning against the hand-rail of the bridge. The

[007-015] unvoiced call of woman to man, which was uttered very distinctly by

[007-016] Arabella’s personality, held Jude to the spot against his

[007-017] intention—almost against his will, and in a way new to his experience.

[007-018] It is scarcely an exaggeration to say that till this moment Jude had

[007-019] never looked at a woman to consider her as such, but had vaguely

[007-020] regarded the sex as beings outside his life and purposes. He gazed from

[007-021] her eyes to her mouth, thence to her bosom, and to her full round naked

[007-022] arms, wet, mottled with the chill of the water, and firm as marble.

[007-023] “What a nice-looking girl you are!” he murmured, though the words had

[007-024] not been necessary to express his sense of her magnetism.

[007-025] “Ah, you should see me Sundays!” she said piquantly.

[007-026] “I don’t suppose I could?” he answered

[007-027] “That’s for you to think on. There’s nobody after me just now, though

[007-028] there med be in a week or two.” She had spoken this without a smile,

[007-029] and the dimples disappeared.

[007-030] Jude felt himself drifting strangely, but could not help it. “Will you

[007-031] let me?”

[007-032] “I don’t mind.”

[007-033] By this time she had managed to get back one dimple by turning her face

[007-034] aside for a moment and repeating the odd little sucking operation

[007-035] before mentioned, Jude being still unconscious of more than a general

[007-036] impression of her appearance. “Next Sunday?” he hazarded. “To-morrow,

[007-037] that is?”

[007-038] “Yes.”

[007-039] “Shall I call?”

[007-040] “Yes.”

[007-041] She brightened with a little glow of triumph, swept him almost tenderly

[007-042] with her eyes in turning, and retracing her steps down the brookside

[007-043] grass rejoined her companions.

[007-044] Jude Fawley shouldered his tool-basket and resumed his lonely way,

[007-045] filled with an ardour at which he mentally stood at gaze. He had just

[007-046] inhaled a single breath from a new atmosphere, which had evidently been

[007-047] hanging round him everywhere he went, for he knew not how long, but had

[007-048] somehow been divided from his actual breathing as by a sheet of glass.

[007-049] The intentions as to reading, working, and learning, which he had so

[007-050] precisely formulated only a few minutes earlier, were suffering a

[007-051] curious collapse into a corner, he knew not how.

[007-052] “Well, it’s only a bit of fun,” he said to himself, faintly conscious

[007-053] that to common sense there was something lacking, and still more

[007-054] obviously something redundant in the nature of this girl who had drawn

[007-055] him to her which made it necessary that he should assert mere

[007-056] sportiveness on his part as his reason in seeking her—something in her

[007-057] quite antipathetic to that side of him which had been occupied with

[007-058] literary study and the magnificent Christminster dream. It had been no

[007-059] vestal who chose _that_ missile for opening her attack on him. He saw

[007-060] this with his intellectual eye, just for a short fleeting while, as by

[007-061] the light of a falling lamp one might momentarily see an inscription on

[007-062] a wall before being enshrouded in darkness. And then this passing

[007-063] discriminative power was withdrawn, and Jude was lost to all conditions

[007-064] of things in the advent of a fresh and wild pleasure, that of having

[007-065] found a new channel for emotional interest hitherto unsuspected, though

[007-066] it had lain close beside him. He was to meet this enkindling one of the

[007-067] other sex on the following Sunday.

[007-068] Meanwhile the girl had joined her companions, and she silently resumed

[007-069] her flicking and sousing of the chitterlings in the pellucid stream.

[007-070] “Catched un, my dear?” laconically asked the girl called Anny.

[007-071] “I don’t know. I wish I had thrown something else than that!”

[007-072] regretfully murmured Arabella.

[007-073] “Lord! he’s nobody, though you med think so. He used to drive old

[007-074] Drusilla Fawley’s bread-cart out at Marygreen, till he ’prenticed

[007-075] himself at Alfredston. Since then he’s been very stuck up, and always

[007-076] reading. He wants to be a scholar, they say.”

[007-077] “Oh, I don’t care what he is, or anything about ’n. Don’t you think it,

[007-078] my child!”

[007-079] “Oh, don’t ye! You needn’t try to deceive us! What did you stay talking

[007-080] to him for, if you didn’t want un? Whether you do or whether you don’t,

[007-081] he’s as simple as a child. I could see it as you courted on the bridge,

[007-082] when he looked at ’ee as if he had never seen a woman before in his

[007-083] born days. Well, he’s to be had by any woman who can get him to care

[007-084] for her a bit, if she likes to set herself to catch him the right way.”

[007-086] The next day Jude Fawley was pausing in his bedroom with the sloping

[007-087] ceiling, looking at the books on the table, and then at the black mark

[007-088] on the plaster above them, made by the smoke of his lamp in past

[007-089] months.

[007-090] It was Sunday afternoon, four-and-twenty hours after his meeting with

[007-091] Arabella Donn. During the whole bygone week he had been resolving to

[007-092] set this afternoon apart for a special purpose,—the re-reading of his

[007-093] Greek Testament—his new one, with better type than his old copy,

[007-094] following Griesbach’s text as amended by numerous correctors, and with

[007-095] variorum readings in the margin. He was proud of the book, having

[007-096] obtained it by boldly writing to its London publisher, a thing he had

[007-097] never done before.

[007-098] He had anticipated much pleasure in this afternoon’s reading, under the

[007-099] quiet roof of his great-aunt’s house as formerly, where he now slept

[007-100] only two nights a week. But a new thing, a great hitch, had happened

[007-101] yesterday in the gliding and noiseless current of his life, and he felt

[007-102] as a snake must feel who has sloughed off its winter skin, and cannot

[007-103] understand the brightness and sensitiveness of its new one.

[007-104] He would not go out to meet her, after all. He sat down, opened the

[007-105] book, and with his elbows firmly planted on the table, and his hands to

[007-106] his temples, began at the beginning:

[007-107] Η ΚΑΙΝΗ ΔΙΑΘΗΚΗ.

[007-108] Had he promised to call for her? Surely he had! She would wait indoors,

[007-109] poor girl, and waste all her afternoon on account of him. There was a

[007-110] something in her, too, which was very winning, apart from promises. He

[007-111] ought not to break faith with her. Even though he had only Sundays and

[007-112] week-day evenings for reading he could afford one afternoon, seeing

[007-113] that other young men afforded so many. After to-day he would never

[007-114] probably see her again. Indeed, it would be impossible, considering

[007-115] what his plans were.

[007-116] In short, as if materially, a compelling arm of extraordinary muscular

[007-117] power seized hold of him—something which had nothing in common with the

[007-118] spirits and influences that had moved him hitherto. This seemed to care

[007-119] little for his reason and his will, nothing for his so-called elevated

[007-120] intentions, and moved him along, as a violent schoolmaster a schoolboy

[007-121] he has seized by the collar, in a direction which tended towards the

[007-122] embrace of a woman for whom he had no respect, and whose life had

[007-123] nothing in common with his own except locality.

[007-124] Η ΚΑΙΝΗ ΔΙΑΘΗΚΗ was no more heeded, and the predestinate Jude sprang up

[007-125] and across the room. Foreseeing such an event he had already arrayed

[007-126] himself in his best clothes. In three minutes he was out of the house

[007-127] and descending by the path across the wide vacant hollow of corn-ground

[007-128] which lay between the village and the isolated house of Arabella in the

[007-129] dip beyond the upland.

[007-130] As he walked he looked at his watch. He could be back in two hours,

[007-131] easily, and a good long time would still remain to him for reading

[007-132] after tea.

[007-133] Passing the few unhealthy fir-trees and cottage where the path joined

[007-134] the highway he hastened along, and struck away to the left, descending

[007-135] the steep side of the country to the west of the Brown House. Here at

[007-136] the base of the chalk formation he neared the brook that oozed from it,

[007-137] and followed the stream till he reached her dwelling. A smell of

[007-138] piggeries came from the back, and the grunting of the originators of

[007-139] that smell. He entered the garden, and knocked at the door with the

[007-140] knob of his stick.

[007-141] Somebody had seen him through the window, for a male voice on the

[007-142] inside said:

[007-143] “Arabella! Here’s your young man come coorting! Mizzle, my girl!”

[007-144] Jude winced at the words. Courting in such a businesslike aspect as it

[007-145] evidently wore to the speaker was the last thing he was thinking of. He

[007-146] was going to walk with her, perhaps kiss her; but “courting” was too

[007-147] coolly purposeful to be anything but repugnant to his ideas. The door

[007-148] was opened and he entered, just as Arabella came downstairs in radiant

[007-149] walking attire.

[007-150] “Take a chair, Mr. What’s-your-name?” said her father, an energetic,

[007-151] black-whiskered man, in the same businesslike tones Jude had heard from

[007-152] outside.

[007-153] “I’d rather go out at once, wouldn’t you?” she whispered to Jude.

[007-154] “Yes,” said he. “We’ll walk up to the Brown House and back, we can do

[007-155] it in half an hour.”

[007-156] Arabella looked so handsome amid her untidy surroundings that he felt

[007-157] glad he had come, and all the misgivings vanished that had hitherto
