# Suicide

Suicide in *Jude the Obscure* is not merely a plot event but the novel's thematic fulcrum — the moment at which all of Hardy's social critique converges into irreversible tragedy. The murder-suicide of Little Father Time ("because we are too menny") remains one of the most shocking scenes in Victorian literature, and its reverberations determine every character's fate thereafter.

## Definition

In the novel, "suicide" operates at two levels: as a specific act (Little Father Time's killing of his half-siblings and himself) and as a thematic presence — the specter of self-destruction that haunts Jude's despair and Sue's eventual religious self-annihilation. Hardy treats suicide not as individual pathology but as the logical endpoint of social conditions that make life unlivable.

## In *Jude the Obscure*

### Manifestation

Suicide first appears as an abstract possibility — Jude, in his despair after Arabella's departure, is described as "a sufficiently dignified person for suicide" (012-098), though the narrator quickly adds that he chooses "peaceful death" by other means. This early reference establishes suicide as the horizon of the novel's emotional landscape: it is always a possibility, waiting to be actualized.

The actualization comes through Little Father Time — the eerily aged child who, upon hearing Sue's despairing reflection that another child would mean more suffering, hangs his half-siblings and himself with the note "Done because we are too menny." The newspaper describes it as "the strange suicide of a child" (062-047). In the novel's final pages, Arabella mockingly asks Jude "What — to commit suicide?" (068-154), reducing the novel's most sacred tragedy to a joke — a gesture that captures Hardy's vision of a universe without moral coherence.

### Key Characters

| Character | Relation to Suicide |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Little Father Time | The perpetrator and victim — a child whose act of murder-suicide crystallizes the novel's argument about the cruelty of social conditions |
| Jude Fawley | Contemplates suicide as an alternative to meaningless suffering; his actual death, while not suicide, is a kind of self-abandonment to despair |
| Sue Bridehead | The unintended catalyst — her words to Little Father Time about the burden of children trigger the tragedy; her subsequent religious reconversion is a form of spiritual suicide |

### Narrative Trajectory

Suicide is introduced as a distant possibility (Part First), recedes during the hopeful domestic sections (Parts Third through Fifth), and erupts with annihilating force in Part Sixth. The murder-suicide scene divides the novel into a before and after — everything that follows (Sue's reconversion, Jude's death, Arabella's triumph) is consequence. The novel never recovers from this event, and neither do its characters.

## Critical Interpretation

The murder-suicide scene was the primary target of the vicious critical response that greeted *Jude the Obscure* upon publication — the *New York World* called it "Jude the Obscene," and Hardy was so wounded by the reception that he abandoned novel-writing entirely. The scene's power lies in its refusal of redemptive meaning: unlike the deaths of Little Nell or Paul Dombey in Dickens, Little Father Time's suicide offers no moral lesson, no tearful reconciliation, no suggestion that suffering ennobles. Modern criticism reads the scene through trauma theory and the history of childhood — the child as bearer of social truth that adults cannot face.
