# Phillotson's Dismissal

Richard Phillotson's dismissal from the Shaston village school is one of the novel's starkest demonstrations of the cost of moral independence. Phillotson commits an act of personal decency — releasing his wife Sue from a marriage she finds intolerable — and is punished by the institutional machinery of Victorian respectability. The dismissal is swift, formal, and public: a "formal letter" followed by a "public meeting" in which Phillotson defends his actions and loses.

## Context（或 Background）

Phillotson is the schoolmaster at Shaston, a position that carries with it the obligation to model moral rectitude for the community. When he permits Sue to leave his household and live with Jude Fawley, he violates the community's understanding of a husband's duty. The school managers — local worthies who govern the school — view his action not as private generosity but as public scandal. In the Victorian moral economy, a man who cannot control his wife cannot control a classroom.

## Key Details

### The Letter of Dismissal

The dismissal arrives with bureaucratic speed: "in a quarter of an hour — the formal letter of dismissal arrived, the managers having remained behind to write it after Phillotson's withdrawal" (035-170). The speed is damning — there is no deliberation, no weighing of circumstances. The managers had already made their decision.

### The Public Meeting

Rather than accept the dismissal quietly, Phillotson calls "a public meeting, which he attended, although he looked so weak and ill that his friend implored him to stay at home" (035-170). At the meeting, he argues that the matter of his marriage is "a domestic theory which did not concern them." The managers overrule him, "insisting that the private matter had become a public scandal."

### Consequences

Phillotson loses his position, his income, and his standing in the community. The dismissal triggers a downward trajectory: he moves from Shaston to a smaller, poorer position, and never recovers professionally. The act of generosity that cost him everything also becomes the basis for Sue's guilt — she feels responsible for his ruin, a guilt that contributes to her eventual return to him.

## Narrative Significance

The dismissal crystallizes the novel's critique of institutional morality. Phillotson acts ethically — he refuses to compel his wife to stay in a marriage she finds physically repugnant — and for this he is destroyed. The school managers, representing respectable society, insist that private morality must conform to public expectation. Phillotson's counter-argument — that his marriage is a "domestic theory which did not concern them" — is a radical claim of personal sovereignty that Victorian society could not accommodate.
