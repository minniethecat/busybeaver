[029-001] “Why didn’t you tell me before!”

[029-002] “I couldn’t. It seemed so cruel to tell it.”

[029-003] “To yourself, Jude. So it was better to be cruel to me!”

[029-004] “No, dear darling!” cried Jude passionately. He tried to take her hand,

[029-005] but she withdrew it. Their old relations of confidence seemed suddenly

[029-006] to have ended, and the antagonisms of sex to sex were left without any

[029-007] counter-poising predilections. She was his comrade, friend, unconscious

[029-008] sweetheart no longer; and her eyes regarded him in estranged silence.

[029-009] “I was ashamed of the episode in my life which brought about the

[029-010] marriage,” he continued. “I can’t explain it precisely now. I could

[029-011] have done it if you had taken it differently!”

[029-012] “But how can I?” she burst out. “Here I have been saying, or writing,

[029-013] that—that you might love me, or something of the sort!—just out of

[029-014] charity—and all the time—oh, it is perfectly damnable how things are!”

[029-015] she said, stamping her foot in a nervous quiver.

[029-016] “You take me wrong, Sue! I never thought you cared for me at all, till

[029-017] quite lately; so I felt it did not matter! Do you care for me, Sue?—you

[029-018] know how I mean?—I don’t like ‘out of charity’ at all!”

[029-019] It was a question which in the circumstances Sue did not choose to

[029-020] answer.

[029-021] “I suppose she—your wife—is—a very pretty woman, even if she’s wicked?”

[029-022] she asked quickly.

[029-023] “She’s pretty enough, as far as that goes.”

[029-024] “Prettier than I am, no doubt!”

[029-025] “You are not the least alike. And I have never seen her for years… But

[029-026] she’s sure to come back—they always do!”

[029-027] “How strange of you to stay apart from her like this!” said Sue, her

[029-028] trembling lip and lumpy throat belying her irony. “You, such a

[029-029] religious man. How will the demi-gods in your Pantheon—I mean those

[029-030] legendary persons you call saints—intercede for you after this? Now if

[029-031] I had done such a thing it would have been different, and not

[029-032] remarkable, for I at least don’t regard marriage as a sacrament. Your

[029-033] theories are not so advanced as your practice!”

[029-034] “Sue, you are terribly cutting when you like to be—a perfect Voltaire!

[029-035] But you must treat me as you will!”

[029-036] When she saw how wretched he was she softened, and trying to blink away

[029-037] her sympathetic tears said with all the winning reproachfulness of a

[029-038] heart-hurt woman: “Ah—you should have told me before you gave me that

[029-039] idea that you wanted to be allowed to love me! I had no feeling before

[029-040] that moment at the railway-station, except—” For once Sue was as

[029-041] miserable as he, in her attempts to keep herself free from emotion, and

[029-042] her less than half-success.

[029-043] “Don’t cry, dear!” he implored.

[029-044] “I am—not crying—because I meant to—love you; but because of your want

[029-045] of—confidence!”

[029-046] They were quite screened from the market-square without, and he could

[029-047] not help putting out his arm towards her waist. His momentary desire

[029-048] was the means of her rallying. “No, no!” she said, drawing back

[029-049] stringently, and wiping her eyes. “Of course not! It would be hypocrisy

[029-050] to pretend that it would be meant as from my cousin; and it can’t be in

[029-051] any other way.”

[029-052] They moved on a dozen paces, and she showed herself recovered. It was

[029-053] distracting to Jude, and his heart would have ached less had she

[029-054] appeared anyhow but as she did appear; essentially large-minded and

[029-055] generous on reflection, despite a previous exercise of those narrow

[029-056] womanly humours on impulse that were necessary to give her sex.

[029-057] “I don’t blame you for what you couldn’t help,” she said, smiling. “How

[029-058] should I be so foolish? I do blame you a little bit for not telling me

[029-059] before. But, after all, it doesn’t matter. We should have had to keep

[029-060] apart, you see, even if this had not been in your life.”

[029-061] “No, we shouldn’t, Sue! This is the only obstacle.”

[029-062] “You forget that I must have loved you, and wanted to be your wife,

[029-063] even if there had been no obstacle,” said Sue, with a gentle

[029-064] seriousness which did not reveal her mind. “And then we are cousins,

[029-065] and it is bad for cousins to marry. And—I am engaged to somebody else.

[029-066] As to our going on together as we were going, in a sort of friendly

[029-067] way, the people round us would have made it unable to continue. Their

[029-068] views of the relations of man and woman are limited, as is proved by

[029-069] their expelling me from the school. Their philosophy only recognizes

[029-070] relations based on animal desire. The wide field of strong attachment

[029-071] where desire plays, at least, only a secondary part, is ignored by

[029-072] them—the part of—who is it?—Venus Urania.”

[029-073] Her being able to talk learnedly showed that she was mistress of

[029-074] herself again; and before they parted she had almost regained her

[029-075] vivacious glance, her reciprocity of tone, her gay manner, and her

[029-076] second-thought attitude of critical largeness towards others of her age

[029-077] and sex.

[029-078] He could speak more freely now. “There were several reasons against my

[029-079] telling you rashly. One was what I have said; another, that it was

[029-080] always impressed upon me that I ought not to marry—that I belonged to

[029-081] an odd and peculiar family—the wrong breed for marriage.”

[029-082] “Ah—who used to say that to you?”

[029-083] “My great-aunt. She said it always ended badly with us Fawleys.”

[029-084] “That’s strange. My father used to say the same to me!”

[029-085] They stood possessed by the same thought, ugly enough, even as an

[029-086] assumption: that a union between them, had such been possible, would

[029-087] have meant a terrible intensification of unfitness—two bitters in one

[029-088] dish.

[029-089] “Oh, but there can’t be anything in it!” she said with nervous

[029-090] lightness. “Our family have been unlucky of late years in choosing

[029-091] mates—that’s all.”

[029-092] And then they pretended to persuade themselves that all that had

[029-093] happened was of no consequence, and that they could still be cousins

[029-094] and friends and warm correspondents, and have happy genial times when

[029-095] they met, even if they met less frequently than before. Their parting

[029-096] was in good friendship, and yet Jude’s last look into her eyes was

[029-097] tinged with inquiry, for he felt that he did not even now quite know

[029-098] her mind.

[029-099] VII

[029-100] Tidings from Sue a day or two after passed across Jude like a withering

[029-101] blast.

[029-102] Before reading the letter he was led to suspect that its contents were

[029-103] of a somewhat serious kind by catching sight of the signature—which was

[029-104] in her full name, never used in her correspondence with him since her

[029-105] first note:

[029-106] MY DEAR JUDE,—I have something to tell you which perhaps you will not

[029-107] be surprised to hear, though certainly it may strike you as being

[029-108] accelerated (as the railway companies say of their trains). Mr.

[029-109] Phillotson and I are to be married quite soon—in three or four weeks.

[029-110] We had intended, as you know, to wait till I had gone through my course

[029-111] of training and obtained my certificate, so as to assist him, if

[029-112] necessary, in the teaching. But he generously says he does not see any

[029-113] object in waiting, now I am not at the training school. It is so good

[029-114] of him, because the awkwardness of my situation has really come about

[029-115] by my fault in getting expelled.

[029-116] Wish me joy. Remember I say you are to, and you mustn’t

[029-117] refuse!—Your affectionate cousin,

[029-118] SUSANNA FLORENCE MARY BRIDEHEAD.

[029-119] Jude staggered under the news; could eat no breakfast; and kept on

[029-120] drinking tea because his mouth was so dry. Then presently he went back

[029-121] to his work and laughed the usual bitter laugh of a man so confronted.

[029-122] Everything seemed turning to satire. And yet, what could the poor girl

[029-123] do? he asked himself, and felt worse than shedding tears.

[029-124] “O Susanna Florence Mary!” he said as he worked. “You don’t know what

[029-125] marriage means!”

[029-126] Could it be possible that his announcement of his own marriage had

[029-127] pricked her on to this, just as his visit to her when in liquor may

[029-128] have pricked her on to her engagement? To be sure, there seemed to

[029-129] exist these other and sufficient reasons, practical and social, for her

[029-130] decision; but Sue was not a very practical or calculating person; and

[029-131] he was compelled to think that a pique at having his secret sprung upon

[029-132] her had moved her to give way to Phillotson’s probable representations,

[029-133] that the best course to prove how unfounded were the suspicions of the

[029-134] school authorities would be to marry him off-hand, as in fulfilment of

[029-135] an ordinary engagement. Sue had, in fact, been placed in an awkward

[029-136] corner. Poor Sue!

[029-137] He determined to play the Spartan; to make the best of it, and support

[029-138] her; but he could not write the requested good wishes for a day or two.

[029-139] Meanwhile there came another note from his impatient little dear:

[029-140] Jude, will you give me away? I have nobody else who could do it so

[029-141] conveniently as you, being the only married relation I have here on the

[029-142] spot, even if my father were friendly enough to be willing, which he

[029-143] isn’t. I hope you won’t think it a trouble? I have been looking at the

[029-144] marriage service in the prayer-book, and it seems to me very

[029-145] humiliating that a giver-away should be required at all. According to

[029-146] the ceremony as there printed, my bridegroom chooses me of his own will

[029-147] and pleasure; but I don’t choose him. Somebody _gives_ me to him, like

[029-148] a she-ass or she-goat, or any other domestic animal. Bless your exalted

[029-149] views of woman, O churchman! But I forget: I am no longer privileged to

[029-150] tease you.—Ever,

[029-151] SUSANNA FLORENCE MARY BRIDEHEAD.

[029-152] Jude screwed himself up to heroic key; and replied:

[029-153] MY DEAR SUE,—Of course I wish you joy! And also of course I will give

[029-154] you away. What I suggest is that, as you have no house of your own, you

[029-155] do not marry from your school friend’s, but from mine. It would be more

[029-156] proper, I think, since I am, as you say, the person nearest related to

[029-157] you in this part of the world.

[029-158] I don’t see why you sign your letter in such a new and terribly

[029-159] formal way? Surely you care a bit about me still!—Ever your

[029-160] affectionate,

[029-161] JUDE.

[029-162] What had jarred on him even more than the signature was a little sting

[029-163] he had been silent on—the phrase “married relation”—What an idiot it

[029-164] made him seem as her lover! If Sue had written that in satire, he could

[029-165] hardly forgive her; if in suffering—ah, that was another thing!

[029-166] His offer of his lodging must have commended itself to Phillotson at

[029-167] any rate, for the schoolmaster sent him a line of warm thanks,

[029-168] accepting the convenience. Sue also thanked him. Jude immediately moved

[029-169] into more commodious quarters, as much to escape the espionage of the

[029-170] suspicious landlady who had been one cause of Sue’s unpleasant

[029-171] experience as for the sake of room.

[029-172] Then Sue wrote to tell him the day fixed for the wedding; and Jude

[029-173] decided, after inquiry, that she should come into residence on the

[029-174] following Saturday, which would allow of a ten days’ stay in the city

[029-175] prior to the ceremony, sufficiently representing a nominal residence of

[029-176] fifteen.

[029-177] She arrived by the ten o’clock train on the day aforesaid, Jude not

[029-178] going to meet her at the station, by her special request, that he

[029-179] should not lose a morning’s work and pay, she said (if this were her

[029-180] true reason). But so well by this time did he know Sue that the

[029-181] remembrance of their mutual sensitiveness at emotional crises might, he

[029-182] thought, have weighed with her in this. When he came home to dinner she

[029-183] had taken possession of her apartment.

[029-184] She lived in the same house with him, but on a different floor, and

[029-185] they saw each other little, an occasional supper being the only meal

[029-186] they took together, when Sue’s manner was something like that of a

[029-187] scared child. What she felt he did not know; their conversation was

[029-188] mechanical, though she did not look pale or ill. Phillotson came

[029-189] frequently, but mostly when Jude was absent. On the morning of the

[029-190] wedding, when Jude had given himself a holiday, Sue and her cousin had

[029-191] breakfast together for the first and last time during this curious

[029-192] interval; in his room—the parlour—which he had hired for the period of

[029-193] Sue’s residence. Seeing, as women do, how helpless he was in making the

[029-194] place comfortable, she bustled about.
