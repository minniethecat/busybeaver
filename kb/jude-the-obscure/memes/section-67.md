[067-001] “Why!” said Arabella, affecting dismay. “You’ve promised to marry me

[067-002] several times as we’ve sat here to-night. These gentlemen have heard

[067-003] you.”

[067-004] “I don’t remember it,” said Jude doggedly. “There’s only one woman—but

[067-005] I won’t mention her in this Capharnaum!”

[067-006] Arabella looked towards her father. “Now, Mr. Fawley be honourable,”

[067-007] said Donn. “You and my daughter have been living here together these

[067-008] three or four days, quite on the understanding that you were going to

[067-009] marry her. Of course I shouldn’t have had such goings on in my house if

[067-010] I hadn’t understood that. As a point of honour you must do it now.”

[067-011] “Don’t say anything against my honour!” enjoined Jude hotly, standing

[067-012] up. “I’d marry the W–––– of Babylon rather than do anything

[067-013] dishonourable! No reflection on you, my dear. It is a mere rhetorical

[067-014] figure—what they call in the books, hyperbole.”

[067-015] “Keep your figures for your debts to friends who shelter you,” said

[067-016] Donn.

[067-017] “If I am bound in honour to marry her—as I suppose I am—though how I

[067-018] came to be here with her I know no more than a dead man—marry her I

[067-019] will, so help me God! I have never behaved dishonourably to a woman or

[067-020] to any living thing. I am not a man who wants to save himself at the

[067-021] expense of the weaker among us!”

[067-022] “There—never mind him, deary,” said she, putting her cheek against

[067-023] Jude’s. “Come up and wash your face, and just put yourself tidy, and

[067-024] off we’ll go. Make it up with Father.”

[067-025] They shook hands. Jude went upstairs with her, and soon came down

[067-026] looking tidy and calm. Arabella, too, had hastily arranged herself, and

[067-027] accompanied by Donn away they went.

[067-028] “Don’t go,” she said to the guests at parting. “I’ve told the little

[067-029] maid to get the breakfast while we are gone; and when we come back

[067-030] we’ll all have some. A good strong cup of tea will set everybody right

[067-031] for going home.”

[067-032] When Arabella, Jude, and Donn had disappeared on their matrimonial

[067-033] errand the assembled guests yawned themselves wider awake, and

[067-034] discussed the situation with great interest. Tinker Taylor, being the

[067-035] most sober, reasoned the most lucidly.

[067-036] “I don’t wish to speak against friends,” he said. “But it do seem a

[067-037] rare curiosity for a couple to marry over again! If they couldn’t get

[067-038] on the first time when their minds were limp, they won’t the second, by

[067-039] my reckoning.”

[067-040] “Do you think he’ll do it?”

[067-041] “He’s been put upon his honour by the woman, so he med.”

[067-042] “He’d hardly do it straight off like this. He’s got no licence nor

[067-043] anything.”

[067-044] “She’s got that, bless you. Didn’t you hear her say so to her father?”

[067-045] “Well,” said Tinker Taylor, relighting his pipe at the gas-jet. “Take

[067-046] her all together, limb by limb, she’s not such a bad-looking

[067-047] piece—particular by candlelight. To be sure, halfpence that have been

[067-048] in circulation can’t be expected to look like new ones from the mint.

[067-049] But for a woman that’s been knocking about the four hemispheres for

[067-050] some time, she’s passable enough. A little bit thick in the flitch

[067-051] perhaps: but I like a woman that a puff o’ wind won’t blow down.”

[067-052] Their eyes followed the movements of the little girl as she spread the

[067-053] breakfast-cloth on the table they had been using, without wiping up the

[067-054] slops of the liquor. The curtains were undrawn, and the expression of

[067-055] the house made to look like morning. Some of the guests, however, fell

[067-056] asleep in their chairs. One or two went to the door, and gazed along

[067-057] the street more than once. Tinker Taylor was the chief of these, and

[067-058] after a time he came in with a leer on his face.

[067-059] “By Gad, they are coming! I think the deed’s done!”

[067-060] “No,” said Uncle Joe, following him in. “Take my word, he turned rusty

[067-061] at the last minute. They are walking in a very unusual way; and that’s

[067-062] the meaning of it!”

[067-063] They waited in silence till the wedding-party could be heard entering

[067-064] the house. First into the room came Arabella boisterously; and her face

[067-065] was enough to show that her strategy had succeeded.

[067-066] “Mrs. Fawley, I presume?” said Tinker Taylor with mock courtesy.

[067-067] “Certainly. Mrs. Fawley again,” replied Arabella blandly, pulling off

[067-068] her glove and holding out her left hand. “There’s the padlock, see…

[067-069] Well, he was a very nice, gentlemanly man indeed. I mean the clergyman.

[067-070] He said to me as gentle as a babe when all was done: ‘Mrs. Fawley, I

[067-071] congratulate you heartily,’ he says. ‘For having heard your history,

[067-072] and that of your husband, I think you have both done the right and

[067-073] proper thing. And for your past errors as a wife, and his as a husband,

[067-074] I think you ought now to be forgiven by the world, as you have forgiven

[067-075] each other,’ says he. Yes; he was a very nice, gentlemanly man. ‘The

[067-076] Church don’t recognize divorce in her dogma, strictly speaking,’ he

[067-077] says: ‘and bear in mind the words of the service in your goings out and

[067-078] your comings in: What God hath joined together let no man put asunder.’

[067-079] Yes; he was a very nice, gentlemanly man… But, Jude, my dear, you were

[067-080] enough to make a cat laugh! You walked that straight, and held yourself

[067-081] that steady, that one would have thought you were going ’prentice to a

[067-082] judge; though I knew you were seeing double all the time, from the way

[067-083] you fumbled with my finger.”

[067-084] “I said I’d do anything to—save a woman’s honour,” muttered Jude. “And

[067-085] I’ve done it!”

[067-086] “Well now, old deary, come along and have some breakfast.”

[067-087] “I want—some—more whisky,” said Jude stolidly.

[067-088] “Nonsense, dear. Not now! There’s no more left. The tea will take the

[067-089] muddle out of our heads, and we shall be as fresh as larks.”

[067-090] “All right. I’ve—married you. She said I ought to marry you again, and

[067-091] I have straightway. It is true religion! Ha—ha—ha!”

[067-092] VIII

[067-093] Michaelmas came and passed, and Jude and his wife, who had lived but a

[067-094] short time in her father’s house after their remarriage, were in

[067-095] lodgings on the top floor of a dwelling nearer to the centre of the

[067-096] city.

[067-097] He had done a few days’ work during the two or three months since the

[067-098] event, but his health had been indifferent, and it was now precarious.

[067-099] He was sitting in an arm-chair before the fire, and coughed a good

[067-100] deal.

[067-101] “I’ve got a bargain for my trouble in marrying thee over again!”

[067-102] Arabella was saying to him. “I shall have to keep ’ee entirely—that’s

[067-103] what ’twill come to! I shall have to make black-pot and sausages, and

[067-104] hawk ’em about the street, all to support an invalid husband I’d no

[067-105] business to be saddled with at all. Why didn’t you keep your health,

[067-106] deceiving one like this? You were well enough when the wedding was!”

[067-107] “Ah, yes!” said he, laughing acridly. “I have been thinking of my

[067-108] foolish feeling about the pig you and I killed during our first

[067-109] marriage. I feel now that the greatest mercy that could be vouchsafed

[067-110] to me would be that something should serve me as I served that animal.”

[067-111] This was the sort of discourse that went on between them every day now.

[067-112] The landlord of the lodging, who had heard that they were a queer

[067-113] couple, had doubted if they were married at all, especially as he had

[067-114] seen Arabella kiss Jude one evening when she had taken a little

[067-115] cordial; and he was about to give them notice to quit, till by chance

[067-116] overhearing her one night haranguing Jude in rattling terms, and

[067-117] ultimately flinging a shoe at his head, he recognized the note of

[067-118] genuine wedlock; and concluding that they must be respectable, said no

[067-119] more.

[067-120] Jude did not get any better, and one day he requested Arabella, with

[067-121] considerable hesitation, to execute a commission for him. She asked him

[067-122] indifferently what it was.

[067-123] “To write to Sue.”

[067-124] “What in the name—do you want me to write to her for?”

[067-125] “To ask how she is, and if she’ll come to see me, because I’m ill, and

[067-126] should like to see her—once again.”

[067-127] “It is like you to insult a lawful wife by asking such a thing!”

[067-128] “It is just in order not to insult you that I ask you to do it. You

[067-129] know I love Sue. I don’t wish to mince the matter—there stands the

[067-130] fact: I love her. I could find a dozen ways of sending a letter to her

[067-131] without your knowledge. But I wish to be quite above-board with you,

[067-132] and with her husband. A message through you asking her to come is at

[067-133] least free from any odour of intrigue. If she retains any of her old

[067-134] nature at all, she’ll come.”

[067-135] “You’ve no respect for marriage whatever, or its rights and duties!”

[067-136] “What _does_ it matter what my opinions are—a wretch like me! Can it

[067-137] matter to anybody in the world who comes to see me for half an

[067-138] hour—here with one foot in the grave! … Come, please write, Arabella!”

[067-139] he pleaded. “Repay my candour by a little generosity!”

[067-140] “I should think _not_!”

[067-141] “Not just once?—Oh do!” He felt that his physical weakness had taken

[067-142] away all his dignity.

[067-143] “What do you want _her_ to know how you are for? She don’t want to see

[067-144] ’ee. She’s the rat that forsook the sinking ship!”

[067-145] “Don’t, don’t!”

[067-146] “And I stuck to un—the more fool I! Have that strumpet in the house

[067-147] indeed!”

[067-148] Almost as soon as the words were spoken Jude sprang from the chair, and

[067-149] before Arabella knew where she was he had her on her back upon a little

[067-150] couch which stood there, he kneeling above her.

[067-151] “Say another word of that sort,” he whispered, “and I’ll kill you—here

[067-152] and now! I’ve everything to gain by it—my own death not being the least

[067-153] part. So don’t think there’s no meaning in what I say!”

[067-154] “What do you want me to do?” gasped Arabella.

[067-155] “Promise never to speak of her.”

[067-156] “Very well. I do.”

[067-157] “I take your word,” he said scornfully as he loosened her. “But what it

[067-158] is worth I can’t say.”

[067-159] “You couldn’t kill the pig, but you could kill me!”

[067-160] “Ah—there you have me! No—I couldn’t kill you—even in a passion. Taunt

[067-161] away!”

[067-162] He then began coughing very much, and she estimated his life with an

[067-163] appraiser’s eye as he sank back ghastly pale. “I’ll send for her,”

[067-164] Arabella murmured, “if you’ll agree to my being in the room with you

[067-165] all the time she’s here.”

[067-166] The softer side of his nature, the desire to see Sue, made him unable

[067-167] to resist the offer even now, provoked as he had been; and he replied

[067-168] breathlessly: “Yes, I agree. Only send for her!”

[067-169] In the evening he inquired if she had written.

[067-170] “Yes,” she said; “I wrote a note telling her you were ill, and asking

[067-171] her to come to-morrow or the day after. I haven’t posted it yet.”

[067-172] The next day Jude wondered if she really did post it, but would not ask

[067-173] her; and foolish Hope, that lives on a drop and a crumb, made him

[067-174] restless with expectation. He knew the times of the possible trains,

[067-175] and listened on each occasion for sounds of her.

[067-176] She did not come; but Jude would not address Arabella again thereon. He

[067-177] hoped and expected all the next day; but no Sue appeared; neither was

[067-178] there any note of reply. Then Jude decided in the privacy of his mind

[067-179] that Arabella had never posted hers, although she had written it. There

[067-180] was something in her manner which told it. His physical weakness was

[067-181] such that he shed tears at the disappointment when she was not there to

[067-182] see. His suspicions were, in fact, well founded. Arabella, like some

[067-183] other nurses, thought that your duty towards your invalid was to pacify

[067-184] him by any means short of really acting upon his fancies.
