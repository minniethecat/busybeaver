# Farmer Troutham

Farmer Troutham is a minor but sharply drawn figure in the opening chapters of *[[jude-fawley|Jude the Obscure]]*: the farmer who employs the young [[Jude-Fawley]] to drive birds away from his cornfield, then beats him when the boy's natural sympathy for the birds overcomes his sense of duty (001-245). The episode encapsulates the conflict between natural feeling and economic necessity that defines [[jude-fawley|Jude]]'s life.

## Character Overview

Troutham is a practical, unsentimental man of the land. When he discovers that [[jude-fawley|Jude]] has been allowing the rooks to feed rather than driving them off, he delivers a harsh punishment — "a-scaring of birds for Farmer Troutham" having become a nightmarish memory for the boy (001-245). Troutham represents the hard, utilitarian world of agricultural labor that young [[jude-fawley|Jude]] must inhabit, a world that has no room for the boy's tender-heartedness or his bookish dreams.

He appears only briefly in the novel, but his presence looms as an archetype of the rural employing class: those who demand labor and obedience without acknowledging the humanity of those who serve them.

## Role in the Narrative

Troutham's sole extended scene is one of the novel's most memorable early episodes. [[jude-fawley|Jude]], dispatched to Troutham's field to act as a human scarecrow, finds himself unable to perform his task. The rooks, he reasons, have as much right to eat as anyone — "a weakness," as the narrator drily puts it, "more in keeping with the schoolmaster's character than with the farmer's" (001-245).

Troutham's response is swift, physical punishment. He beats [[jude-fawley|Jude]] and dismisses him, an act that sends the boy home in humiliation and reinforces his sense that the world of work is a world of cruelty. The episode serves as [[jude-fawley|Jude]]'s first real encounter with the economic order that will crush his aspirations.

### First Appearance

Troutham first appears when he discovers [[jude-fawley|Jude]] has allowed the rooks to feed freely in his cornfield. The confrontation — a boy caught between sympathy for nature and the demands of paid labor — establishes the novel's central tension between feeling and necessity (001-245).
