[044-001] “I am glad to know you think so,” said Phillotson huskily. “No. I don’t

[044-002] hate you!”

[044-003] It grew dusk quickly in the gloomy room during their intermittent chat,

[044-004] and when candles were brought and it was time to leave she put her hand

[044-005] in his or rather allowed it to flit through his; for she was

[044-006] significantly light in touch. She had nearly closed the door when he

[044-007] said, “Sue!” He had noticed that, in turning away from him, tears were

[044-008] on her face and a quiver in her lip.

[044-009] It was bad policy to recall her—he knew it while he pursued it. But he

[044-010] could not help it. She came back.

[044-011] “Sue,” he murmured, “do you wish to make it up, and stay? I’ll forgive

[044-012] you and condone everything!”

[044-013] “Oh you can’t, you can’t!” she said hastily. “You can’t condone it

[044-014] now!”

[044-015] “_He_ is your husband now, in effect, you mean, of course?”

[044-016] “You may assume it. He is obtaining a divorce from his wife Arabella.”

[044-017] “His wife! It is altogether news to me that he has a wife.”

[044-018] “It was a bad marriage.”

[044-019] “Like yours.”

[044-020] “Like mine. He is not doing it so much on his own account as on hers.

[044-021] She wrote and told him it would be a kindness to her, since then she

[044-022] could marry and live respectably. And Jude has agreed.”

[044-023] “A wife… A kindness to her. Ah, yes; a kindness to her to release her

[044-024] altogether… But I don’t like the sound of it. I can forgive, Sue.”

[044-025] “No, no! You can’t have me back now I have been so wicked—as to do what

[044-026] I have done!”

[044-027] There had arisen in Sue’s face that incipient fright which showed

[044-028] itself whenever he changed from friend to husband, and which made her

[044-029] adopt any line of defence against marital feeling in him. “I _must_ go

[044-030] now. I’ll come again—may I?”

[044-031] “I don’t ask you to go, even now. I ask you to stay.”

[044-032] “I thank you, Richard; but I must. As you are not so ill as I thought,

[044-033] I _cannot_ stay!”

[044-034] “She’s his—his from lips to heel!” said Phillotson; but so faintly that

[044-035] in closing the door she did not hear it. The dread of a reactionary

[044-036] change in the schoolmaster’s sentiments, coupled, perhaps, with a faint

[044-037] shamefacedness at letting even him know what a slipshod lack of

[044-038] thoroughness, from a man’s point of view, characterized her transferred

[044-039] allegiance, prevented her telling him of her, thus far, incomplete

[044-040] relations with Jude; and Phillotson lay writhing like a man in hell as

[044-041] he pictured the prettily dressed, maddening compound of sympathy and

[044-042] averseness who bore his name, returning impatiently to the home of her

[044-043] lover.

[044-044] Gillingham was so interested in Phillotson’s affairs, and so seriously

[044-045] concerned about him, that he walked up the hill-side to Shaston two or

[044-046] three times a week, although, there and back, it was a journey of nine

[044-047] miles, which had to be performed between tea and supper, after a hard

[044-048] day’s work in school. When he called on the next occasion after Sue’s

[044-049] visit his friend was downstairs, and Gillingham noticed that his

[044-050] restless mood had been supplanted by a more fixed and composed one.

[044-051] “She’s been here since you called last,” said Phillotson.

[044-052] “Not Mrs. Phillotson?”

[044-053] “Yes.”

[044-054] “Ah! You have made it up?”

[044-055] “No… She just came, patted my pillow with her little white hand, played

[044-056] the thoughtful nurse for half an hour, and went away.”

[044-057] “Well—I’m hanged! A little hussy!”

[044-058] “What do you say?”

[044-059] “Oh—nothing!”

[044-060] “What do you mean?”

[044-061] “I mean, what a tantalizing, capricious little woman! If she were not

[044-062] your wife—”

[044-063] “She is not; she’s another man’s except in name and law. And I have

[044-064] been thinking—it was suggested to me by a conversation I had with

[044-065] her—that, in kindness to her, I ought to dissolve the legal tie

[044-066] altogether; which, singularly enough, I think I can do, now she has

[044-067] been back, and refused my request to stay after I said I had forgiven

[044-068] her. I believe that fact would afford me opportunity of doing it,

[044-069] though I did not see it at the moment. What’s the use of keeping her

[044-070] chained on to me if she doesn’t belong to me? I know—I feel absolutely

[044-071] certain—that she would welcome my taking such a step as the greatest

[044-072] charity to her. For though as a fellow-creature she sympathizes with,

[044-073] and pities me, and even weeps for me, as a husband she cannot endure

[044-074] me—she loathes me—there’s no use in mincing words—she loathes me, and

[044-075] my only manly, and dignified, and merciful course is to complete what I

[044-076] have begun… And for worldly reasons, too, it will be better for her to

[044-077] be independent. I have hopelessly ruined my prospects because of my

[044-078] decision as to what was best for us, though she does not know it; I see

[044-079] only dire poverty ahead from my feet to the grave; for I can be

[044-080] accepted as teacher no more. I shall probably have enough to do to make

[044-081] both ends meet during the remainder of my life, now my occupation’s

[044-082] gone; and I shall be better able to bear it alone. I may as well tell

[044-083] you that what has suggested my letting her go is some news she brought

[044-084] me—the news that Fawley is doing the same.”

[044-085] “Oh—he had a spouse, too? A queer couple, these lovers!”

[044-086] “Well—I don’t want your opinion on that. What I was going to say is

[044-087] that my liberating her can do her no possible harm, and will open up a

[044-088] chance of happiness for her which she has never dreamt of hitherto. For

[044-089] then they’ll be able to marry, as they ought to have done at first.”

[044-090] Gillingham did not hurry to reply. “I may disagree with your motive,”

[044-091] he said gently, for he respected views he could not share. “But I think

[044-092] you are right in your determination—if you can carry it out. I doubt,

[044-093] however, if you can.”

[044-094] Part Fifth AT ALDBRICKHAM AND ELSEWHERE

[044-095] _“Thy aerial part, and all the fiery parts which are mingled in thee,

[044-096] though by nature they have an upward tendency, still in obedience to

[044-097] the disposition of the universe they are over-powered here in the

[044-098] compound mass the body.”_—M. ANTONINUS (Long).

[044-099] I

[044-100] How Gillingham’s doubts were disposed of will most quickly appear by

[044-101] passing over the series of dreary months and incidents that followed

[044-102] the events of the last chapter, and coming on to a Sunday in the

[044-103] February of the year following.

[044-104] Sue and Jude were living in Aldbrickham, in precisely the same

[044-105] relations that they had established between themselves when she left

[044-106] Shaston to join him the year before. The proceedings in the law-courts

[044-107] had reached their consciousness, but as a distant sound and an

[044-108] occasional missive which they hardly understood.

[044-109] They had met, as usual, to breakfast together in the little house with

[044-110] Jude’s name on it, that he had taken at fifteen pounds a year, with

[044-111] three-pounds-ten extra for rates and taxes, and furnished with his

[044-112] aunt’s ancient and lumbering goods, which had cost him about their full

[044-113] value to bring all the way from Marygreen. Sue kept house, and managed

[044-114] everything.

[044-115] As he entered the room this morning Sue held up a letter she had just

[044-116] received.

[044-117] “Well; and what is it about?” he said after kissing her.

[044-118] “That the decree _nisi_ in the case of Phillotson _versus_ Phillotson

[044-119] and Fawley, pronounced six months ago, has just been made absolute.”

[044-120] “Ah,” said Jude, as he sat down.

[044-121] The same concluding incident in Jude’s suit against Arabella had

[044-122] occurred about a month or two earlier. Both cases had been too

[044-123] insignificant to be reported in the papers, further than by name in a

[044-124] long list of other undefended cases.

[044-125] “Now then, Sue, at any rate, you can do what you like!” He looked at

[044-126] his sweetheart curiously.

[044-127] “Are we—you and I—just as free now as if we had never married at all?”

[044-128] “Just as free—except, I believe, that a clergyman may object personally

[044-129] to remarry you, and hand the job on to somebody else.”

[044-130] “But I wonder—do you think it is really so with us? I know it is

[044-131] generally. But I have an uncomfortable feeling that my freedom has been

[044-132] obtained under false pretences!”

[044-133] “How?”

[044-134] “Well—if the truth about us had been known, the decree wouldn’t have

[044-135] been pronounced. It is only, is it, because we have made no defence,

[044-136] and have led them into a false supposition? Therefore is my freedom

[044-137] lawful, however proper it may be?”

[044-138] “Well—why did you let it be under false pretences? You have only

[044-139] yourself to blame,” he said mischievously.

[044-140] “Jude—don’t! You ought not to be touchy about that still. You must take

[044-141] me as I am.”

[044-142] “Very well, darling: so I will. Perhaps you were right. As to your

[044-143] question, we were not obliged to prove anything. That was their

[044-144] business. Anyhow we are living together.”

[044-145] “Yes. Though not in their sense.”

[044-146] “One thing is certain, that however the decree may be brought about, a

[044-147] marriage is dissolved when it is dissolved. There is this advantage in

[044-148] being poor obscure people like us—that these things are done for us in

[044-149] a rough and ready fashion. It was the same with me and Arabella. I was

[044-150] afraid her criminal second marriage would have been discovered, and she

[044-151] punished; but nobody took any interest in her—nobody inquired, nobody

[044-152] suspected it. If we’d been patented nobilities we should have had

[044-153] infinite trouble, and days and weeks would have been spent in

[044-154] investigations.”

[044-155] By degrees Sue acquired her lover’s cheerfulness at the sense of

[044-156] freedom, and proposed that they should take a walk in the fields, even

[044-157] if they had to put up with a cold dinner on account of it. Jude agreed,

[044-158] and Sue went up-stairs and prepared to start, putting on a joyful

[044-159] coloured gown in observance of her liberty; seeing which Jude put on a

[044-160] lighter tie.

[044-161] “Now we’ll strut arm and arm,” he said, “like any other engaged couple.

[044-162] We’ve a legal right to.”

[044-163] They rambled out of the town, and along a path over the low-lying lands

[044-164] that bordered it, though these were frosty now, and the extensive

[044-165] seed-fields were bare of colour and produce. The pair, however, were so

[044-166] absorbed in their own situation that their surroundings were little in

[044-167] their consciousness.

[044-168] “Well, my dearest, the result of all this is that we can marry after a

[044-169] decent interval.”

[044-170] “Yes; I suppose we can,” said Sue, without enthusiasm.

[044-171] “And aren’t we going to?”

[044-172] “I don’t like to say no, dear Jude; but I feel just the same about it

[044-173] now as I have done all along. I have just the same dread lest an iron

[044-174] contract should extinguish your tenderness for me, and mine for you, as

[044-175] it did between our unfortunate parents.”

[044-176] “Still, what can we do? I do love you, as you know, Sue.”

[044-177] “I know it abundantly. But I think I would much rather go on living

[044-178] always as lovers, as we are living now, and only meeting by day. It is

[044-179] so much sweeter—for the woman at least, and when she is sure of the

[044-180] man. And henceforward we needn’t be so particular as we have been about

[044-181] appearances.”

[044-182] “Our experiences of matrimony with others have not been encouraging, I

[044-183] own,” said he, with some gloom; “either owing to our own dissatisfied,

[044-184] unpractical natures, or by our misfortune. But we two—”

[044-185] “Should be two dissatisfied ones linked together, which would be twice

[044-186] as bad as before… I think I should begin to be afraid of you, Jude, the

[044-187] moment you had contracted to cherish me under a Government stamp, and I

[044-188] was licensed to be loved on the premises by you—Ugh, how horrible and

[044-189] sordid! Although, as you are, free, I trust you more than any other man

[044-190] in the world.”

[044-191] “No, no—don’t say I should change!” he expostulated; yet there was

[044-192] misgiving in his own voice also.
