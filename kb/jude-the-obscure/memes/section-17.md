[017-001] But as the days, and still more particularly the lonely evenings,

[017-002] dragged along, he found himself, to his moral consternation, to be

[017-003] thinking more of her instead of thinking less of her, and experiencing

[017-004] a fearful bliss in doing what was erratic, informal, and unexpected.

[017-005] Surrounded by her influence all day, walking past the spots she

[017-006] frequented, he was always thinking of her, and was obliged to own to

[017-007] himself that his conscience was likely to be the loser in this battle.

[017-008] To be sure she was almost an ideality to him still. Perhaps to know her

[017-009] would be to cure himself of this unexpected and unauthorized passion. A

[017-010] voice whispered that, though he desired to know her, he did not desire

[017-011] to be cured.

[017-012] There was not the least doubt that from his own orthodox point of view

[017-013] the situation was growing immoral. For Sue to be the loved one of a man

[017-014] who was licensed by the laws of his country to love Arabella and none

[017-015] other unto his life’s end, was a pretty bad second beginning when the

[017-016] man was bent on such a course as Jude purposed. This conviction was so

[017-017] real with him that one day when, as was frequent, he was at work in a

[017-018] neighbouring village church alone, he felt it to be his duty to pray

[017-019] against his weakness. But much as he wished to be an exemplar in these

[017-020] things he could not get on. It was quite impossible, he found, to ask

[017-021] to be delivered from temptation when your heart’s desire was to be

[017-022] tempted unto seventy times seven. So he excused himself. “After all,”

[017-023] he said, “it is not altogether an _erotolepsy_ that is the matter with

[017-024] me, as at that first time. I can see that she is exceptionally bright;

[017-025] and it is partly a wish for intellectual sympathy, and a craving for

[017-026] loving-kindness in my solitude.” Thus he went on adoring her, fearing

[017-027] to realize that it was human perversity. For whatever Sue’s virtues,

[017-028] talents, or ecclesiastical saturation, it was certain that those items

[017-029] were not at all the cause of his affection for her.

[017-030] On an afternoon at this time a young girl entered the stone-mason’s

[017-031] yard with some hesitation, and, lifting her skirts to avoid draggling

[017-032] them in the white dust, crossed towards the office.

[017-033] “That’s a nice girl,” said one of the men known as Uncle Joe.

[017-034] “Who is she?” asked another.

[017-035] “I don’t know—I’ve seen her about here and there. Why, yes, she’s the

[017-036] daughter of that clever chap Bridehead who did all the wrought ironwork

[017-037] at St. Silas’ ten years ago, and went away to London afterwards. I

[017-038] don’t know what he’s doing now—not much I fancy—as she’s come back

[017-039] here.”

[017-040] Meanwhile the young woman had knocked at the office door and asked if

[017-041] Mr. Jude Fawley was at work in the yard. It so happened that Jude had

[017-042] gone out somewhere or other that afternoon, which information she

[017-043] received with a look of disappointment, and went away immediately. When

[017-044] Jude returned they told him, and described her, whereupon he exclaimed,

[017-045] “Why—that’s my cousin Sue!”

[017-046] He looked along the street after her, but she was out of sight. He had

[017-047] no longer any thought of a conscientious avoidance of her, and resolved

[017-048] to call upon her that very evening. And when he reached his lodging he

[017-049] found a note from her—a first note—one of those documents which, simple

[017-050] and commonplace in themselves, are seen retrospectively to have been

[017-051] pregnant with impassioned consequences. The very unconsciousness of a

[017-052] looming drama which is shown in such innocent first epistles from women

[017-053] to men, or _vice versa_, makes them, when such a drama follows, and

[017-054] they are read over by the purple or lurid light of it, all the more

[017-055] impressive, solemn, and in cases, terrible.

[017-056] Sue’s was of the most artless and natural kind. She addressed him as

[017-057] her dear cousin Jude; said she had only just learnt by the merest

[017-058] accident that he was living in Christminster, and reproached him with

[017-059] not letting her know. They might have had such nice times together, she

[017-060] said, for she was thrown much upon herself, and had hardly any

[017-061] congenial friend. But now there was every probability of her soon going

[017-062] away, so that the chance of companionship would be lost perhaps for

[017-063] ever.

[017-064] A cold sweat overspread Jude at the news that she was going away. That

[017-065] was a contingency he had never thought of, and it spurred him to write

[017-066] all the more quickly to her. He would meet her that very evening, he

[017-067] said, one hour from the time of writing, at the cross in the pavement

[017-068] which marked the spot of the Martyrdoms.

[017-069] When he had despatched the note by a boy he regretted that in his hurry

[017-070] he should have suggested to her to meet him out of doors, when he might

[017-071] have said he would call upon her. It was, in fact, the country custom

[017-072] to meet thus, and nothing else had occurred to him. Arabella had been

[017-073] met in the same way, unfortunately, and it might not seem respectable

[017-074] to a dear girl like Sue. However, it could not be helped now, and he

[017-075] moved towards the point a few minutes before the hour, under the

[017-076] glimmer of the newly lighted lamps.

[017-077] The broad street was silent, and almost deserted, although it was not

[017-078] late. He saw a figure on the other side, which turned out to be hers,

[017-079] and they both converged towards the crossmark at the same moment.

[017-080] Before either had reached it she called out to him:

[017-081] “I am not going to meet you just there, for the first time in my life!

[017-082] Come further on.”

[017-083] The voice, though positive and silvery, had been tremulous. They walked

[017-084] on in parallel lines, and, waiting her pleasure, Jude watched till she

[017-085] showed signs of closing in, when he did likewise, the place being where

[017-086] the carriers’ carts stood in the daytime, though there was none on the

[017-087] spot then.

[017-088] “I am sorry that I asked you to meet me, and didn’t call,” began Jude

[017-089] with the bashfulness of a lover. “But I thought it would save time if

[017-090] we were going to walk.”

[017-091] “Oh—I don’t mind that,” she said with the freedom of a friend. “I have

[017-092] really no place to ask anybody in to. What I meant was that the place

[017-093] you chose was so horrid—I suppose I ought not to say horrid—I mean

[017-094] gloomy and inauspicious in its associations… But isn’t it funny to

[017-095] begin like this, when I don’t know you yet?” She looked him up and down

[017-096] curiously, though Jude did not look much at her.

[017-097] “You seem to know me more than I know you,” she added.

[017-098] “Yes—I have seen you now and then.”

[017-099] “And you knew who I was, and didn’t speak? And now I am going away!”

[017-100] “Yes. That’s unfortunate. I have hardly any other friend. I have,

[017-101] indeed, one very old friend here somewhere, but I don’t quite like to

[017-102] call on him just yet. I wonder if you know anything of him—Mr.

[017-103] Phillotson? A parson somewhere about the county I think he is.”

[017-104] “No—I only know of one Mr. Phillotson. He lives a little way out in the

[017-105] country, at Lumsdon. He’s a village schoolmaster.”

[017-106] “Ah! I wonder if he’s the same. Surely it is impossible! Only a

[017-107] schoolmaster still! Do you know his Christian name—is it Richard?”

[017-108] “Yes—it is; I’ve directed books to him, though I’ve never seen him.”

[017-109] “Then he couldn’t do it!”

[017-110] Jude’s countenance fell, for how could he succeed in an enterprise

[017-111] wherein the great Phillotson had failed? He would have had a day of

[017-112] despair if the news had not arrived during his sweet Sue’s presence,

[017-113] but even at this moment he had visions of how Phillotson’s failure in

[017-114] the grand university scheme would depress him when she had gone.

[017-115] “As we are going to take a walk, suppose we go and call upon him?” said

[017-116] Jude suddenly. “It is not late.”

[017-117] She agreed, and they went along up a hill, and through some prettily

[017-118] wooded country. Presently the embattled tower and square turret of the

[017-119] church rose into the sky, and then the school-house. They inquired of a

[017-120] person in the street if Mr. Phillotson was likely to be at home, and

[017-121] were informed that he was always at home. A knock brought him to the

[017-122] school-house door, with a candle in his hand and a look of inquiry on

[017-123] his face, which had grown thin and careworn since Jude last set eyes on

[017-124] him.

[017-125] That after all these years the meeting with Mr. Phillotson should be of

[017-126] this homely complexion destroyed at one stroke the halo which had

[017-127] surrounded the school-master’s figure in Jude’s imagination ever since

[017-128] their parting. It created in him at the same time a sympathy with

[017-129] Phillotson as an obviously much chastened and disappointed man. Jude

[017-130] told him his name, and said he had come to see him as an old friend who

[017-131] had been kind to him in his youthful days.

[017-132] “I don’t remember you in the least,” said the school-master

[017-133] thoughtfully. “You were one of my pupils, you say? Yes, no doubt; but

[017-134] they number so many thousands by this time of my life, and have

[017-135] naturally changed so much, that I remember very few except the quite

[017-136] recent ones.”

[017-137] “It was out at Marygreen,” said Jude, wishing he had not come.

[017-138] “Yes. I was there a short time. And is this an old pupil, too?”

[017-139] “No—that’s my cousin… I wrote to you for some grammars, if you

[017-140] recollect, and you sent them?”

[017-141] “Ah—yes!—I do dimly recall that incident.”

[017-142] “It was very kind of you to do it. And it was you who first started me

[017-143] on that course. On the morning you left Marygreen, when your goods were

[017-144] on the waggon, you wished me good-bye, and said your scheme was to be a

[017-145] university man and enter the Church—that a degree was the necessary

[017-146] hall-mark of one who wanted to do anything as a theologian or teacher.”

[017-147] “I remember I thought all that privately; but I wonder I did not keep

[017-148] my own counsel. The idea was given up years ago.”

[017-149] “I have never forgotten it. It was that which brought me to this part

[017-150] of the country, and out here to see you to-night.”

[017-151] “Come in,” said Phillotson. “And your cousin, too.”

[017-152] They entered the parlour of the school-house, where there was a lamp

[017-153] with a paper shade, which threw the light down on three or four books.

[017-154] Phillotson took it off, so that they could see each other better, and

[017-155] the rays fell on the nervous little face and vivacious dark eyes and

[017-156] hair of Sue, on the earnest features of her cousin, and on the

[017-157] schoolmaster’s own maturer face and figure, showing him to be a spare

[017-158] and thoughtful personage of five-and-forty, with a thin-lipped,

[017-159] somewhat refined mouth, a slightly stooping habit, and a black frock

[017-160] coat, which from continued frictions shone a little at the

[017-161] shoulder-blades, the middle of the back, and the elbows.

[017-162] The old friendship was imperceptibly renewed, the schoolmaster speaking

[017-163] of his experiences, and the cousins of theirs. He told them that he

[017-164] still thought of the Church sometimes, and that though he could not

[017-165] enter it as he had intended to do in former years he might enter it as

[017-166] a licentiate. Meanwhile, he said, he was comfortable in his present

[017-167] position, though he was in want of a pupil-teacher.

[017-168] They did not stay to supper, Sue having to be indoors before it grew

[017-169] late, and the road was retraced to Christminster. Though they had

[017-170] talked of nothing more than general subjects, Jude was surprised to

[017-171] find what a revelation of woman his cousin was to him. She was so

[017-172] vibrant that everything she did seemed to have its source in feeling.

[017-173] An exciting thought would make her walk ahead so fast that he could

[017-174] hardly keep up with her; and her sensitiveness on some points was such

[017-175] that it might have been misread as vanity. It was with heart-sickness

[017-176] he perceived that, while her sentiments towards him were those of the

[017-177] frankest friendliness only, he loved her more than before becoming

[017-178] acquainted with her; and the gloom of the walk home lay not in the

[017-179] night overhead, but in the thought of her departure.
