[057-001] “Hear, hear,” said the populace.

[057-002] “Well preached!” said Tinker Taylor. And privately to his neighbours:

[057-003] “Why, one of them jobbing pa’sons swarming about here, that takes the

[057-004] services when our head reverends want a holiday, wouldn’t ha’

[057-005] discoursed such doctrine for less than a guinea down. Hey? I’ll take my

[057-006] oath not one o’ ’em would! And then he must have had it wrote down for

[057-007] ’n. And this only a working-man!”

[057-008] As a sort of objective commentary on Jude’s remarks there drove up at

[057-009] this moment with a belated Doctor, robed and panting, a cab whose horse

[057-010] failed to stop at the exact point required for setting down the hirer,

[057-011] who jumped out and entered the door. The driver, alighting, began to

[057-012] kick the animal in the belly.

[057-013] “If that can be done,” said Jude, “at college gates in the most

[057-014] religious and educational city in the world, what shall we say as to

[057-015] how far we’ve got?”

[057-016] “Order!” said one of the policemen, who had been engaged with a comrade

[057-017] in opening the large doors opposite the college. “Keep yer tongue

[057-018] quiet, my man, while the procession passes.” The rain came on more

[057-019] heavily, and all who had umbrellas opened them. Jude was not one of

[057-020] these, and Sue only possessed a small one, half sunshade. She had grown

[057-021] pale, though Jude did not notice it then.

[057-022] “Let us go on, dear,” she whispered, endeavouring to shelter him. “We

[057-023] haven’t any lodgings yet, remember, and all our things are at the

[057-024] station; and you are by no means well yet. I am afraid this wet will

[057-025] hurt you!”

[057-026] “They are coming now. Just a moment, and I’ll go!” said he.

[057-027] A peal of six bells struck out, human faces began to crowd the windows

[057-028] around, and the procession of heads of houses and new Doctors emerged,

[057-029] their red and black gowned forms passing across the field of Jude’s

[057-030] vision like inaccessible planets across an object-glass.

[057-031] As they went their names were called by knowing informants, and when

[057-032] they reached the old round theatre of Wren a cheer rose high.

[057-033] “Let’s go that way!” cried Jude, and though it now rained steadily he

[057-034] seemed not to know it, and took them round to the theatre. Here they

[057-035] stood upon the straw that was laid to drown the discordant noise of

[057-036] wheels, where the quaint and frost-eaten stone busts encircling the

[057-037] building looked with pallid grimness on the proceedings, and in

[057-038] particular at the bedraggled Jude, Sue, and their children, as at

[057-039] ludicrous persons who had no business there.

[057-040] “I wish I could get in!” he said to her fervidly. “Listen—I may catch a

[057-041] few words of the Latin speech by staying here; the windows are open.”

[057-042] However, beyond the peals of the organ, and the shouts and hurrahs

[057-043] between each piece of oratory, Jude’s standing in the wet did not bring

[057-044] much Latin to his intelligence more than, now and then, a sonorous word

[057-045] in _um_ or _ibus_.

[057-046] “Well—I’m an outsider to the end of my days!” he sighed after a while.

[057-047] “Now I’ll go, my patient Sue. How good of you to wait in the rain all

[057-048] this time—to gratify my infatuation! I’ll never care any more about the

[057-049] infernal cursed place, upon my soul I won’t! But what made you tremble

[057-050] so when we were at the barrier? And how pale you are, Sue!”

[057-051] “I saw Richard amongst the people on the other side.”

[057-052] “Ah—did you!”

[057-053] “He is evidently come up to Jerusalem to see the festival like the rest

[057-054] of us: and on that account is probably living not so very far away. He

[057-055] had the same hankering for the university that you had, in a milder

[057-056] form. I don’t think he saw me, though he must have heard you speaking

[057-057] to the crowd. But he seemed not to notice.”

[057-058] “Well—suppose he did. Your mind is free from worries about him now, my

[057-059] Sue?”

[057-060] “Yes, I suppose so. But I am weak. Although I know it is all right with

[057-061] our plans, I felt a curious dread of him; an awe, or terror, of

[057-062] conventions I don’t believe in. It comes over me at times like a sort

[057-063] of creeping paralysis, and makes me so sad!”

[057-064] “You are getting tired, Sue. Oh—I forgot, darling! Yes, we’ll go on at

[057-065] once.”

[057-066] They started in quest of the lodging, and at last found something that

[057-067] seemed to promise well, in Mildew Lane—a spot which to Jude was

[057-068] irresistible—though to Sue it was not so fascinating—a narrow lane

[057-069] close to the back of a college, but having no communication with it.

[057-070] The little houses were darkened to gloom by the high collegiate

[057-071] buildings, within which life was so far removed from that of the people

[057-072] in the lane as if it had been on opposite sides of the globe; yet only

[057-073] a thickness of wall divided them. Two or three of the houses had

[057-074] notices of rooms to let, and the newcomers knocked at the door of one,

[057-075] which a woman opened.

[057-076] “Ah—listen!” said Jude suddenly, instead of addressing her.

[057-077] “What?”

[057-078] “Why the bells—what church can that be? The tones are familiar.”

[057-079] Another peal of bells had begun to sound out at some distance off.

[057-080] “I don’t know!” said the landlady tartly. “Did you knock to ask that?”

[057-081] “No; for lodgings,” said Jude, coming to himself.

[057-082] The householder scrutinized Sue’s figure a moment. “We haven’t any to

[057-083] let,” said she, shutting the door.

[057-084] Jude looked discomfited, and the boy distressed. “Now, Jude,” said Sue,

[057-085] “let me try. You don’t know the way.”

[057-086] They found a second place hard by; but here the occupier, observing not

[057-087] only Sue, but the boy and the small children, said civilly, “I am sorry

[057-088] to say we don’t let where there are children”; and also closed the

[057-089] door.

[057-090] The small child squared its mouth and cried silently, with an instinct

[057-091] that trouble loomed. The boy sighed. “I don’t like Christminster!” he

[057-092] said. “Are the great old houses gaols?”

[057-093] “No; colleges,” said Jude; “which perhaps you’ll study in some day.”

[057-094] “I’d rather not!” the boy rejoined.

[057-095] “Now we’ll try again,” said Sue. “I’ll pull my cloak more round me…

[057-096] Leaving Kennetbridge for this place is like coming from Caiaphas to

[057-097] Pilate! … How do I look now, dear?”

[057-098] “Nobody would notice it now,” said Jude.

[057-099] There was one other house, and they tried a third time. The woman here

[057-100] was more amiable; but she had little room to spare, and could only

[057-101] agree to take in Sue and the children if her husband could go

[057-102] elsewhere. This arrangement they perforce adopted, in the stress from

[057-103] delaying their search till so late. They came to terms with her, though

[057-104] her price was rather high for their pockets. But they could not afford

[057-105] to be critical till Jude had time to get a more permanent abode; and in

[057-106] this house Sue took possession of a back room on the second floor with

[057-107] an inner closet-room for the children. Jude stayed and had a cup of

[057-108] tea; and was pleased to find that the window commanded the back of

[057-109] another of the colleges. Kissing all four he went to get a few

[057-110] necessaries and look for lodgings for himself.

[057-111] When he was gone the landlady came up to talk a little with Sue, and

[057-112] gather something of the circumstances of the family she had taken in.

[057-113] Sue had not the art of prevarication, and, after admitting several

[057-114] facts as to their late difficulties and wanderings, she was startled by

[057-115] the landlady saying suddenly:

[057-116] “Are you really a married woman?”

[057-117] Sue hesitated; and then impulsively told the woman that her husband and

[057-118] herself had each been unhappy in their first marriages, after which,

[057-119] terrified at the thought of a second irrevocable union, and lest the

[057-120] conditions of the contract should kill their love, yet wishing to be

[057-121] together, they had literally not found the courage to repeat it, though

[057-122] they had attempted it two or three times. Therefore, though in her own

[057-123] sense of the words she was a married woman, in the landlady’s sense she

[057-124] was not.

[057-125] The housewife looked embarrassed, and went downstairs. Sue sat by the

[057-126] window in a reverie, watching the rain. Her quiet was broken by the

[057-127] noise of someone entering the house, and then the voices of a man and

[057-128] woman in conversation in the passage below. The landlady’s husband had

[057-129] arrived, and she was explaining to him the incoming of the lodgers

[057-130] during his absence.

[057-131] His voice rose in sudden anger. “Now who wants such a woman here? and

[057-132] perhaps a confinement! … Besides, didn’t I say I wouldn’t have

[057-133] children? The hall and stairs fresh painted, to be kicked about by

[057-134] them! You must have known all was not straight with ’em—coming like

[057-135] that. Taking in a family when I said a single man.”

[057-136] The wife expostulated, but, as it seemed, the husband insisted on his

[057-137] point; for presently a tap came to Sue’s door, and the woman appeared.

[057-138] “I am sorry to tell you, ma’am,” she said, “that I can’t let you have

[057-139] the room for the week after all. My husband objects; and therefore I

[057-140] must ask you to go. I don’t mind your staying over to-night, as it is

[057-141] getting late in the afternoon; but I shall be glad if you can leave

[057-142] early in the morning.”

[057-143] Though she knew that she was entitled to the lodging for a week, Sue

[057-144] did not wish to create a disturbance between the wife and husband, and

[057-145] she said she would leave as requested. When the landlady had gone Sue

[057-146] looked out of the window again. Finding that the rain had ceased she

[057-147] proposed to the boy that, after putting the little ones to bed, they

[057-148] should go out and search about for another place, and bespeak it for

[057-149] the morrow, so as not to be so hard-driven then as they had been that

[057-150] day.

[057-151] Therefore, instead of unpacking her boxes, which had just been sent on

[057-152] from the station by Jude, they sallied out into the damp though not

[057-153] unpleasant streets, Sue resolving not to disturb her husband with the

[057-154] news of her notice to quit while he was perhaps worried in obtaining a

[057-155] lodging for himself. In the company of the boy she wandered into this

[057-156] street and into that; but though she tried a dozen different houses she

[057-157] fared far worse alone than she had fared in Jude’s company, and could

[057-158] get nobody to promise her a room for the following day. Every

[057-159] householder looked askance at such a woman and child inquiring for

[057-160] accommodation in the gloom.

[057-161] “I ought not to be born, ought I?” said the boy with misgiving.

[057-162] Thoroughly tired at last Sue returned to the place where she was not

[057-163] welcome, but where at least she had temporary shelter. In her absence

[057-164] Jude had left his address; but knowing how weak he still was she

[057-165] adhered to her determination not to disturb him till the next day.

[057-166] II

[057-167] Sue sat looking at the bare floor of the room, the house being little

[057-168] more than an old intramural cottage, and then she regarded the scene

[057-169] outside the uncurtained window. At some distance opposite, the outer

[057-170] walls of Sarcophagus College—silent, black, and windowless—threw their

[057-171] four centuries of gloom, bigotry, and decay into the little room she

[057-172] occupied, shutting out the moonlight by night and the sun by day. The

[057-173] outlines of Rubric College also were discernible beyond the other, and

[057-174] the tower of a third farther off still. She thought of the strange

[057-175] operation of a simple-minded man’s ruling passion, that it should have

[057-176] led Jude, who loved her and the children so tenderly, to place them

[057-177] here in this depressing purlieu, because he was still haunted by his

[057-178] dream. Even now he did not distinctly hear the freezing negative that

[057-179] those scholared walls had echoed to his desire.

[057-180] The failure to find another lodging, and the lack of room in this house

[057-181] for his father, had made a deep impression on the boy—a brooding

[057-182] undemonstrative horror seemed to have seized him. The silence was

[057-183] broken by his saying: “Mother, _what_ shall we do to-morrow!”
