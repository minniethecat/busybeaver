[023-001] Sue came out into the town with him, and they walked and talked with

[023-002] tongues centred only on the passing moments. Jude said he would like to

[023-003] buy her a little present of some sort, and then she confessed, with

[023-004] something of shame, that she was dreadfully hungry. They were kept on

[023-005] very short allowances in the college, and a dinner, tea, and supper all

[023-006] in one was the present she most desired in the world. Jude thereupon

[023-007] took her to an inn and ordered whatever the house afforded, which was

[023-008] not much. The place, however, gave them a delightful opportunity for a

[023-009] _tête-à-tête_, nobody else being in the room, and they talked freely.

[023-010] She told him about the school as it was at that date, and the rough

[023-011] living, and the mixed character of her fellow-students, gathered

[023-012] together from all parts of the diocese, and how she had to get up and

[023-013] work by gas-light in the early morning, with all the bitterness of a

[023-014] young person to whom restraint was new. To all this he listened; but it

[023-015] was not what he wanted especially to know—her relations with

[023-016] Phillotson. That was what she did not tell. When they had sat and

[023-017] eaten, Jude impulsively placed his hand upon hers; she looked up and

[023-018] smiled, and took his quite freely into her own little soft one,

[023-019] dividing his fingers and coolly examining them, as if they were the

[023-020] fingers of a glove she was purchasing.

[023-021] “Your hands are rather rough, Jude, aren’t they?” she said.

[023-022] “Yes. So would yours be if they held a mallet and chisel all day.”

[023-023] “I don’t dislike it, you know. I think it is noble to see a man’s hands

[023-024] subdued to what he works in… Well, I’m rather glad I came to this

[023-025] training-school, after all. See how independent I shall be after the

[023-026] two years’ training! I shall pass pretty high, I expect, and Mr.

[023-027] Phillotson will use his influence to get me a big school.”

[023-028] She had touched the subject at last. “I had a suspicion, a fear,” said

[023-029] Jude, “that he—cared about you rather warmly, and perhaps wanted to

[023-030] marry you.”

[023-031] “Now don’t be such a silly boy!”

[023-032] “He has said something about it, I expect.”

[023-033] “If he had, what would it matter? An old man like him!”

[023-034] “Oh, come, Sue; he’s not so very old. And I know what I saw him doing—”

[023-035] “Not kissing me—that I’m certain!”

[023-036] “No. But putting his arm round your waist.”

[023-037] “Ah—I remember. But I didn’t know he was going to.”

[023-038] “You are wriggling out if it, Sue, and it isn’t quite kind!”

[023-039] Her ever-sensitive lip began to quiver, and her eye to blink, at

[023-040] something this reproof was deciding her to say.

[023-041] “I know you’ll be angry if I tell you everything, and that’s why I

[023-042] don’t want to!”

[023-043] “Very well, then, dear,” he said soothingly. “I have no real right to

[023-044] ask you, and I don’t wish to know.”

[023-045] “I shall tell you!” said she, with the perverseness that was part of

[023-046] her. “This is what I have done: I have promised—I have promised—that I

[023-047] will marry him when I come out of the training-school two years hence,

[023-048] and have got my certificate; his plan being that we shall then take a

[023-049] large double school in a great town—he the boys’ and I the girls’—as

[023-050] married school-teachers often do, and make a good income between us.”

[023-051] “Oh, Sue! … But of course it is right—you couldn’t have done better!”

[023-052] He glanced at her and their eyes met, the reproach in his own belying

[023-053] his words. Then he drew his hand quite away from hers, and turned his

[023-054] face in estrangement from her to the window. Sue regarded him passively

[023-055] without moving.

[023-056] “I knew you would be angry!” she said with an air of no emotion

[023-057] whatever. “Very well—I am wrong, I suppose! I ought not to have let you

[023-058] come to see me! We had better not meet again; and we’ll only correspond

[023-059] at long intervals, on purely business matters!”

[023-060] This was just the one thing he would not be able to bear, as she

[023-061] probably knew, and it brought him round at once. “Oh yes, we will,” he

[023-062] said quickly. “Your being engaged can make no difference to me

[023-063] whatever. I have a perfect right to see you when I want to; and I

[023-064] shall!”

[023-065] “Then don’t let us talk of it any more. It is quite spoiling our

[023-066] evening together. What does it matter about what one is going to do two

[023-067] years hence!”

[023-068] She was something of a riddle to him, and he let the subject drift

[023-069] away. “Shall we go and sit in the cathedral?” he asked, when their meal

[023-070] was finished.

[023-071] “Cathedral? Yes. Though I think I’d rather sit in the railway station,”

[023-072] she answered, a remnant of vexation still in her voice. “That’s the

[023-073] centre of the town life now. The cathedral has had its day!”

[023-074] “How modern you are!”

[023-075] “So would you be if you had lived so much in the Middle Ages as I have

[023-076] done these last few years! The cathedral was a very good place four or

[023-077] five centuries ago; but it is played out now… I am not modern, either.

[023-078] I am more ancient than mediævalism, if you only knew.”

[023-079] Jude looked distressed.

[023-080] “There—I won’t say any more of that!” she cried. “Only you don’t know

[023-081] how bad I am, from your point of view, or you wouldn’t think so much of

[023-082] me, or care whether I was engaged or not. Now there’s just time for us

[023-083] to walk round the Close, then I must go in, or I shall be locked out

[023-084] for the night.”

[023-085] He took her to the gate and they parted. Jude had a conviction that his

[023-086] unhappy visit to her on that sad night had precipitated this marriage

[023-087] engagement, and it did anything but add to his happiness. Her reproach

[023-088] had taken that shape, then, and not the shape of words. However, next

[023-089] day he set about seeking employment, which it was not so easy to get as

[023-090] at Christminster, there being, as a rule, less stone-cutting in

[023-091] progress in this quiet city, and hands being mostly permanent. But he

[023-092] edged himself in by degrees. His first work was some carving at the

[023-093] cemetery on the hill; and ultimately he became engaged on the labour he

[023-094] most desired—the cathedral repairs, which were very extensive, the

[023-095] whole interior stonework having been overhauled, to be largely replaced

[023-096] by new. It might be a labour of years to get it all done, and he had

[023-097] confidence enough in his own skill with the mallet and chisel to feel

[023-098] that it would be a matter of choice with himself how long he would

[023-099] stay.

[023-100] The lodgings he took near the Close Gate would not have disgraced a

[023-101] curate, the rent representing a higher percentage on his wages than

[023-102] mechanics of any sort usually care to pay. His combined bed and

[023-103] sitting-room was furnished with framed photographs of the rectories and

[023-104] deaneries at which his landlady had lived as trusted servant in her

[023-105] time, and the parlour downstairs bore a clock on the mantelpiece

[023-106] inscribed to the effect that it was presented to the same

[023-107] serious-minded woman by her fellow-servants on the occasion of her

[023-108] marriage. Jude added to the furniture of his room by unpacking

[023-109] photographs of the ecclesiastical carvings and monuments that he had

[023-110] executed with his own hands; and he was deemed a satisfactory

[023-111] acquisition as tenant of the vacant apartment.

[023-112] He found an ample supply of theological books in the city book-shops,

[023-113] and with these his studies were recommenced in a different spirit and

[023-114] direction from his former course. As a relaxation from the Fathers, and

[023-115] such stock works as Paley and Butler, he read Newman, Pusey, and many

[023-116] other modern lights. He hired a harmonium, set it up in his lodging,

[023-117] and practised chants thereon, single and double.

[023-119] “To-morrow is our grand day, you know. Where shall we go?”

[023-120] “I have leave from three till nine. Wherever we can get to and come

[023-121] back from in that time. Not ruins, Jude—I don’t care for them.”

[023-122] “Well—Wardour Castle. And then we can do Fonthill if we like—all in the

[023-123] same afternoon.”

[023-124] “Wardour is Gothic ruins—and I hate Gothic!”

[023-125] “No. Quite otherwise. It is a classic building—Corinthian, I think;

[023-126] with a lot of pictures.”

[023-127] “Ah—that will do. I like the sound of Corinthian. We’ll go.”

[023-128] Their conversation had run thus some few weeks later, and next morning

[023-129] they prepared to start. Every detail of the outing was a facet

[023-130] reflecting a sparkle to Jude, and he did not venture to meditate on the

[023-131] life of inconsistency he was leading. His Sue’s conduct was one lovely

[023-132] conundrum to him; he could say no more.

[023-133] There duly came the charm of calling at the college door for her; her

[023-134] emergence in a nunlike simplicity of costume that was rather enforced

[023-135] than desired; the traipsing along to the station, the porters’ “B’your

[023-136] leave!,” the screaming of the trains—everything formed the basis of a

[023-137] beautiful crystallization. Nobody stared at Sue, because she was so

[023-138] plainly dressed, which comforted Jude in the thought that only himself

[023-139] knew the charms those habiliments subdued. A matter of ten pounds spent

[023-140] in a drapery-shop, which had no connection with her real life or her

[023-141] real self, would have set all Melchester staring. The guard of the

[023-142] train thought they were lovers, and put them into a compartment all by

[023-143] themselves.

[023-144] “That’s a good intention wasted!” said she.

[023-145] Jude did not respond. He thought the remark unnecessarily cruel, and

[023-146] partly untrue.

[023-147] They reached the park and castle and wandered through the

[023-148] picture-galleries, Jude stopping by preference in front of the

[023-149] devotional pictures by Del Sarto, Guido Reni, Spagnoletto,

[023-150] Sassoferrato, Carlo Dolci, and others. Sue paused patiently beside him,

[023-151] and stole critical looks into his face as, regarding the Virgins, Holy

[023-152] Families, and Saints, it grew reverent and abstracted. When she had

[023-153] thoroughly estimated him at this, she would move on and wait for him

[023-154] before a Lely or Reynolds. It was evident that her cousin deeply

[023-155] interested her, as one might be interested in a man puzzling out his

[023-156] way along a labyrinth from which one had one’s self escaped.

[023-157] When they came out a long time still remained to them and Jude proposed

[023-158] that as soon as they had had something to eat they should walk across

[023-159] the high country to the north of their present position, and intercept

[023-160] the train of another railway leading back to Melchester, at a station

[023-161] about seven miles off. Sue, who was inclined for any adventure that

[023-162] would intensify the sense of her day’s freedom, readily agreed; and

[023-163] away they went, leaving the adjoining station behind them.

[023-164] It was indeed open country, wide and high. They talked and bounded on,

[023-165] Jude cutting from a little covert a long walking-stick for Sue as tall

[023-166] as herself, with a great crook, which made her look like a shepherdess.

[023-167] About half-way on their journey they crossed a main road running due

[023-168] east and west—the old road from London to Land’s End. They paused, and

[023-169] looked up and down it for a moment, and remarked upon the desolation

[023-170] which had come over this once lively thoroughfare, while the wind

[023-171] dipped to earth and scooped straws and hay-stems from the ground.

[023-172] They crossed the road and passed on, but during the next half-mile Sue

[023-173] seemed to grow tired, and Jude began to be distressed for her. They had

[023-174] walked a good distance altogether, and if they could not reach the

[023-175] other station it would be rather awkward. For a long time there was no

[023-176] cottage visible on the wide expanse of down and turnip-land; but

[023-177] presently they came to a sheepfold, and next to the shepherd, pitching

[023-178] hurdles. He told them that the only house near was his mother’s and

[023-179] his, pointing to a little dip ahead from which a faint blue smoke

[023-180] arose, and recommended them to go on and rest there.
