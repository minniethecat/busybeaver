[047-001] “I have told you he is asking me to marry him—to make our natural

[047-002] marriage a legal one,” said Sue, with yet more dignity. “It was quite

[047-003] by my wish that he didn’t the moment I was free.”

[047-004] “Ah, yes—you are a oneyer too, like myself,” said Arabella, eyeing her

[047-005] visitor with humorous criticism. “Bolted from your first, didn’t you,

[047-006] like me?”

[047-007] “Good morning!—I must go,” said Sue hastily.

[047-008] “And I, too, must up and off!” replied the other, springing out of bed

[047-009] so suddenly that the soft parts of her person shook. Sue jumped aside

[047-010] in trepidation. “Lord, I am only a woman—not a six-foot sojer! … Just a

[047-011] moment, dear,” she continued, putting her hand on Sue’s arm. “I really

[047-012] did want to consult Jude on a little matter of business, as I told him.

[047-013] I came about that more than anything else. Would he run up to speak to

[047-014] me at the station as I am going? You think not. Well, I’ll write to him

[047-015] about it. I didn’t want to write it, but never mind—I will.”

[047-016] III

[047-017] When Sue reached home Jude was awaiting her at the door to take the

[047-018] initial step towards their marriage. She clasped his arm, and they went

[047-019] along silently together, as true comrades oft-times do. He saw that she

[047-020] was preoccupied, and forbore to question her.

[047-021] “Oh Jude—I’ve been talking to her,” she said at last. “I wish I hadn’t!

[047-022] And yet it is best to be reminded of things.”

[047-023] “I hope she was civil.”

[047-024] “Yes. I—I can’t help liking her—just a little bit! She’s not an

[047-025] ungenerous nature; and I am so glad her difficulties have all suddenly

[047-026] ended.” She explained how Arabella had been summoned back, and would be

[047-027] enabled to retrieve her position. “I was referring to our old question.

[047-028] What Arabella has been saying to me has made me feel more than ever how

[047-029] hopelessly vulgar an institution legal marriage is—a sort of trap to

[047-030] catch a man—I can’t bear to think of it. I wish I hadn’t promised to

[047-031] let you put up the banns this morning!”

[047-032] “Oh, don’t mind me. Any time will do for me. I thought you might like

[047-033] to get it over quickly, now.”

[047-034] “Indeed, I don’t feel any more anxious now than I did before. Perhaps

[047-035] with any other man I might be a little anxious; but among the very few

[047-036] virtues possessed by your family and mine, dear, I think I may set

[047-037] staunchness. So I am not a bit frightened about losing you, now I

[047-038] really am yours and you really are mine. In fact, I am easier in my

[047-039] mind than I was, for my conscience is clear about Richard, who now has

[047-040] a right to his freedom. I felt we were deceiving him before.”

[047-041] “Sue, you seem when you are like this to be one of the women of some

[047-042] grand old civilization, whom I used to read about in my bygone, wasted,

[047-043] classical days, rather than a denizen of a mere Christian country. I

[047-044] almost expect you to say at these times that you have just been talking

[047-045] to some friend whom you met in the Via Sacra, about the latest news of

[047-046] Octavia or Livia; or have been listening to Aspasia’s eloquence, or

[047-047] have been watching Praxiteles chiselling away at his latest Venus,

[047-048] while Phryne made complaint that she was tired of posing.”

[047-049] They had now reached the house of the parish clerk. Sue stood back,

[047-050] while her lover went up to the door. His hand was raised to knock when

[047-051] she said: “Jude!”

[047-052] He looked round.

[047-053] “Wait a minute, would you mind?”

[047-054] He came back to her.

[047-055] “Just let us think,” she said timidly. “I had such a horrid dream one

[047-056] night! … And Arabella—”

[047-057] “What did Arabella say to you?” he asked.

[047-058] “Oh, she said that when people were tied up you could get the law of a

[047-059] man better if he beat you—and how when couples quarrelled… Jude, do you

[047-060] think that when you must have me with you by law, we shall be so happy

[047-061] as we are now? The men and women of our family are very generous when

[047-062] everything depends upon their goodwill, but they always kick against

[047-063] compulsion. Don’t you dread the attitude that insensibly arises out of

[047-064] legal obligation? Don’t you think it is destructive to a passion whose

[047-065] essence is its gratuitousness?”

[047-066] “Upon my word, love, you are beginning to frighten me, too, with all

[047-067] this foreboding! Well, let’s go back and think it over.”

[047-068] Her face brightened. “Yes—so we will!” said she. And they turned from

[047-069] the clerk’s door, Sue taking his arm and murmuring as they walked on

[047-070] homeward:

[047-071] Can you keep the bee from ranging,

[047-072] Or the ring-dove’s neck from changing?

[047-073] No! Nor fetter’d love…

[047-074] They thought it over, or postponed thinking. Certainly they postponed

[047-075] action, and seemed to live on in a dreamy paradise. At the end of a

[047-076] fortnight or three weeks matters remained unadvanced, and no banns were

[047-077] announced to the ears of any Aldbrickham congregation.

[047-078] Whilst they were postponing and postponing thus a letter and a

[047-079] newspaper arrived before breakfast one morning from Arabella. Seeing

[047-080] the handwriting Jude went up to Sue’s room and told her, and as soon as

[047-081] she was dressed she hastened down. Sue opened the newspaper; Jude the

[047-082] letter. After glancing at the paper she held across the first page to

[047-083] him with her finger on a paragraph; but he was so absorbed in his

[047-084] letter that he did not turn awhile.

[047-085] “Look!” said she.

[047-086] He looked and read. The paper was one that circulated in South London

[047-087] only, and the marked advertisement was simply the announcement of a

[047-088] marriage at St. John’s Church, Waterloo Road, under the names,

[047-089] “CARTLETT——DONN”; the united pair being Arabella and the inn-keeper.

[047-090] “Well, it is satisfactory,” said Sue complacently. “Though, after this,

[047-091] it seems rather low to do likewise, and I am glad. However, she is

[047-092] provided for now in a way, I suppose, whatever her faults, poor thing.

[047-093] It is nicer that we are able to think that, than to be uneasy about

[047-094] her. I ought, too, to write to Richard and ask him how he is getting

[047-095] on, perhaps?”

[047-096] But Jude’s attention was still absorbed. Having merely glanced at the

[047-097] announcement he said in a disturbed voice: “Listen to this letter. What

[047-098] shall I say or do?”

[047-099] THE THREE HORNS, LAMBETH.

[047-100] DEAR JUDE (I won’t be so distant as to call you Mr. Fawley),—I send

[047-101] to-day a newspaper, from which useful document you will learn that I

[047-102] was married over again to Cartlett last Tuesday. So that business is

[047-103] settled right and tight at last. But what I write about more particular

[047-104] is that private affair I wanted to speak to you on when I came down to

[047-105] Aldbrickham. I couldn’t very well tell it to your lady friend, and

[047-106] should much have liked to let you know it by word of mouth, as I could

[047-107] have explained better than by letter. The fact is, Jude, that, though I

[047-108] have never informed you before, there was a boy born of our marriage,

[047-109] eight months after I left you, when I was at Sydney, living with my

[047-110] father and mother. All that is easily provable. As I had separated from

[047-111] you before I thought such a thing was going to happen, and I was over

[047-112] there, and our quarrel had been sharp, I did not think it convenient to

[047-113] write about the birth. I was then looking out for a good situation, so

[047-114] my parents took the child, and he has been with them ever since. That

[047-115] was why I did not mention it when I met you in Christminster, nor at

[047-116] the law proceedings. He is now of an intelligent age, of course, and my

[047-117] mother and father have lately written to say that, as they have rather

[047-118] a hard struggle over there, and I am settled comfortably here, they

[047-119] don’t see why they should be encumbered with the child any longer, his

[047-120] parents being alive. I would have him with me here in a moment, but he

[047-121] is not old enough to be of any use in the bar nor will be for years and

[047-122] years, and naturally Cartlett might think him in the way. They have,

[047-123] however, packed him off to me in charge of some friends who happened to

[047-124] be coming home, and I must ask you to take him when he arrives, for I

[047-125] don’t know what to do with him. He is lawfully yours, that I solemnly

[047-126] swear. If anybody says he isn’t, call them brimstone liars, for my

[047-127] sake. Whatever I may have done before or afterwards, I was honest to

[047-128] you from the time we were married till I went away, and I remain,

[047-129] yours, &c.,

[047-130] ARABELLA CARTLETT.

[047-131] Sue’s look was one of dismay. “What will you do, dear?” she asked

[047-132] faintly.

[047-133] Jude did not reply, and Sue watched him anxiously, with heavy breaths.

[047-134] “It hits me hard!” said he in an under-voice. “It _may_ be true! I

[047-135] can’t make it out. Certainly, if his birth was exactly when she says,

[047-136] he’s mine. I cannot think why she didn’t tell me when I met her at

[047-137] Christminster, and came on here that evening with her! … Ah—I do

[047-138] remember now that she said something about having a thing on her mind

[047-139] that she would like me to know, if ever we lived together again.”

[047-140] “The poor child seems to be wanted by nobody!” Sue replied, and her

[047-141] eyes filled.

[047-142] Jude had by this time come to himself. “What a view of life he must

[047-143] have, mine or not mine!” he said. “I must say that, if I were better

[047-144] off, I should not stop for a moment to think whose he might be. I would

[047-145] take him and bring him up. The beggarly question of parentage—what is

[047-146] it, after all? What does it matter, when you come to think of it,

[047-147] whether a child is yours by blood or not? All the little ones of our

[047-148] time are collectively the children of us adults of the time, and

[047-149] entitled to our general care. That excessive regard of parents for

[047-150] their own children, and their dislike of other people’s, is, like

[047-151] class-feeling, patriotism, save-your-own-soul-ism, and other virtues, a

[047-152] mean exclusiveness at bottom.”

[047-153] Sue jumped up and kissed Jude with passionate devotion. “Yes—so it is,

[047-154] dearest! And we’ll have him here! And if he isn’t yours it makes it all

[047-155] the better. I do hope he isn’t—though perhaps I ought not to feel quite

[047-156] that! If he isn’t, I should like so much for us to have him as an

[047-157] adopted child!”

[047-158] “Well, you must assume about him what is most pleasing to you, my

[047-159] curious little comrade!” he said. “I feel that, anyhow, I don’t like to

[047-160] leave the unfortunate little fellow to neglect. Just think of his life

[047-161] in a Lambeth pothouse, and all its evil influences, with a parent who

[047-162] doesn’t want him, and has, indeed, hardly seen him, and a stepfather

[047-163] who doesn’t know him. ‘Let the day perish wherein I was born, and the

[047-164] night in which it was said, There is a man child conceived!’ That’s

[047-165] what the boy—_my_ boy, perhaps, will find himself saying before long!”

[047-166] “Oh no!”

[047-167] “As I was the petitioner, I am really entitled to his custody, I

[047-168] suppose.”

[047-169] “Whether or no, we must have him. I see that. I’ll do the best I can to

[047-170] be a mother to him, and we can afford to keep him somehow. I’ll work

[047-171] harder. I wonder when he’ll arrive?”

[047-172] “In the course of a few weeks, I suppose.”

[047-173] “I wish—When shall we have courage to marry, Jude?”

[047-174] “Whenever you have it, I think I shall. It remains with you entirely,

[047-175] dear. Only say the word, and it’s done.”

[047-176] “Before the boy comes?”

[047-177] “Certainly.”

[047-178] “It would make a more natural home for him, perhaps,” she murmured.
