[022-001] If he had been a woman he must have screamed under the nervous tension

[022-002] which he was now undergoing. But that relief being denied to his

[022-003] virility, he clenched his teeth in misery, bringing lines about his

[022-004] mouth like those in the Laocoön, and corrugations between his brows.

[022-005] A mournful wind blew through the trees, and sounded in the chimney like

[022-006] the pedal notes of an organ. Each ivy leaf overgrowing the wall of the

[022-007] churchless church-yard hard by, now abandoned, pecked its neighbour

[022-008] smartly, and the vane on the new Victorian-Gothic church in the new

[022-009] spot had already begun to creak. Yet apparently it was not always the

[022-010] outdoor wind that made the deep murmurs; it was a voice. He guessed its

[022-011] origin in a moment or two; the curate was praying with his aunt in the

[022-012] adjoining room. He remembered her speaking of him. Presently the sounds

[022-013] ceased, and a step seemed to cross the landing. Jude sat up, and

[022-014] shouted “Hoi!”

[022-015] The step made for his door, which was open, and a man looked in. It was

[022-016] a young clergyman.

[022-017] “I think you are Mr. Highridge,” said Jude. “My aunt has mentioned you

[022-018] more than once. Well, here I am, just come home; a fellow gone to the

[022-019] bad; though I had the best intentions in the world at one time. Now I

[022-020] am melancholy mad, what with drinking and one thing and another.”

[022-021] Slowly Jude unfolded to the curate his late plans and movements, by an

[022-022] unconscious bias dwelling less upon the intellectual and ambitious side

[022-023] of his dream, and more upon the theological, though this had, up till

[022-024] now, been merely a portion of the general plan of advancement.

[022-025] “Now I know I have been a fool, and that folly is with me,” added Jude

[022-026] in conclusion. “And I don’t regret the collapse of my university hopes

[022-027] one jot. I wouldn’t begin again if I were sure to succeed. I don’t care

[022-028] for social success any more at all. But I do feel I should like to do

[022-029] some good thing; and I bitterly regret the Church, and the loss of my

[022-030] chance of being her ordained minister.”

[022-031] The curate, who was a new man to this neighbourhood, had grown deeply

[022-032] interested, and at last he said: “If you feel a real call to the

[022-033] ministry, and I won’t say from your conversation that you do not, for

[022-034] it is that of a thoughtful and educated man, you might enter the Church

[022-035] as a licentiate. Only you must make up your mind to avoid strong

[022-036] drink.”

[022-037] “I could avoid that easily enough, if I had any kind of hope to support

[022-038] me!”

[022-039] Part Third AT MELCHESTER

[022-040] _“For there was no other girl, O bridegroom, like her!”_

[022-041] —SAPPHO (H. T. Wharton).

[022-042] I

[022-043] It was a new idea—the ecclesiastical and altruistic life as distinct

[022-044] from the intellectual and emulative life. A man could preach and do

[022-045] good to his fellow-creatures without taking double-firsts in the

[022-046] schools of Christminster, or having anything but ordinary knowledge.

[022-047] The old fancy which had led on to the culminating vision of the

[022-048] bishopric had not been an ethical or theological enthusiasm at all, but

[022-049] a mundane ambition masquerading in a surplice. He feared that his whole

[022-050] scheme had degenerated to, even though it might not have originated in,

[022-051] a social unrest which had no foundation in the nobler instincts; which

[022-052] was purely an artificial product of civilization. There were thousands

[022-053] of young men on the same self-seeking track at the present moment. The

[022-054] sensual hind who ate, drank, and lived carelessly with his wife through

[022-055] the days of his vanity was a more likable being than he.

[022-056] But to enter the Church in such an unscholarly way that he could not in

[022-057] any probability rise to a higher grade through all his career than that

[022-058] of the humble curate wearing his life out in an obscure village or city

[022-059] slum—that might have a touch of goodness and greatness in it; that

[022-060] might be true religion, and a purgatorial course worthy of being

[022-061] followed by a remorseful man.

[022-062] The favourable light in which this new thought showed itself by

[022-063] contrast with his foregone intentions cheered Jude, as he sat there,

[022-064] shabby and lonely; and it may be said to have given, during the next

[022-065] few days, the _coup de grâce_ to his intellectual career—a career which

[022-066] had extended over the greater part of a dozen years. He did nothing,

[022-067] however, for some long stagnant time to advance his new desire,

[022-068] occupying himself with little local jobs in putting up and lettering

[022-069] headstones about the neighbouring villages, and submitting to be

[022-070] regarded as a social failure, a returned purchase, by the half-dozen or

[022-071] so of farmers and other country-people who condescended to nod to him.

[022-072] The human interest of the new intention—and a human interest is

[022-073] indispensable to the most spiritual and self-sacrificing—was created by

[022-074] a letter from Sue, bearing a fresh postmark. She evidently wrote with

[022-075] anxiety, and told very little about her own doings, more than that she

[022-076] had passed some sort of examination for a Queen’s Scholarship, and was

[022-077] going to enter a training college at Melchester to complete herself for

[022-078] the vocation she had chosen, partly by his influence. There was a

[022-079] theological college at Melchester; Melchester was a quiet and soothing

[022-080] place, almost entirely ecclesiastical in its tone; a spot where worldly

[022-081] learning and intellectual smartness had no establishment; where the

[022-082] altruistic feeling that he did possess would perhaps be more highly

[022-083] estimated than a brilliancy which he did not.

[022-084] As it would be necessary that he should continue for a time to work at

[022-085] his trade while reading up Divinity, which he had neglected at

[022-086] Christminster for the ordinary classical grind, what better course for

[022-087] him than to get employment at the further city, and pursue this plan of

[022-088] reading? That his excessive human interest in the new place was

[022-089] entirely of Sue’s making, while at the same time Sue was to be regarded

[022-090] even less than formerly as proper to create it, had an ethical

[022-091] contradictoriness to which he was not blind. But that much he conceded

[022-092] to human frailty, and hoped to learn to love her only as a friend and

[022-093] kinswoman.

[022-094] He considered that he might so mark out his coming years as to begin

[022-095] his ministry at the age of thirty—an age which much attracted him as

[022-096] being that of his exemplar when he first began to teach in Galilee.

[022-097] This would allow him plenty of time for deliberate study, and for

[022-098] acquiring capital by his trade to help his aftercourse of keeping the

[022-099] necessary terms at a theological college.

[022-100] Christmas had come and passed, and Sue had gone to the Melchester

[022-101] Normal School. The time was just the worst in the year for Jude to get

[022-102] into new employment, and he had written suggesting to her that he

[022-103] should postpone his arrival for a month or so, till the days had

[022-104] lengthened. She had acquiesced so readily that he wished he had not

[022-105] proposed it—she evidently did not much care about him, though she had

[022-106] never once reproached him for his strange conduct in coming to her that

[022-107] night, and his silent disappearance. Neither had she ever said a word

[022-108] about her relations with Mr. Phillotson.

[022-109] Suddenly, however, quite a passionate letter arrived from Sue. She was

[022-110] quite lonely and miserable, she told him. She hated the place she was

[022-111] in; it was worse than the ecclesiastical designer’s; worse than

[022-112] anywhere. She felt utterly friendless; could he come

[022-113] immediately?—though when he did come she would only be able to see him

[022-114] at limited times, the rules of the establishment she found herself in

[022-115] being strict to a degree. It was Mr. Phillotson who had advised her to

[022-116] come there, and she wished she had never listened to him.

[022-117] Phillotson’s suit was not exactly prospering, evidently; and Jude felt

[022-118] unreasonably glad. He packed up his things and went to Melchester with

[022-119] a lighter heart than he had known for months.

[022-120] This being the turning over a new leaf he duly looked about for a

[022-121] temperance hotel, and found a little establishment of that description

[022-122] in the street leading from the station. When he had had something to

[022-123] eat he walked out into the dull winter light over the town bridge, and

[022-124] turned the corner towards the Close. The day was foggy, and standing

[022-125] under the walls of the most graceful architectural pile in England he

[022-126] paused and looked up. The lofty building was visible as far as the

[022-127] roofridge; above, the dwindling spire rose more and more remotely, till

[022-128] its apex was quite lost in the mist drifting across it.

[022-129] The lamps now began to be lighted, and turning to the west front he

[022-130] walked round. He took it as a good omen that numerous blocks of stone

[022-131] were lying about, which signified that the cathedral was undergoing

[022-132] restoration or repair to a considerable extent. It seemed to him, full

[022-133] of the superstitions of his beliefs, that this was an exercise of

[022-134] forethought on the part of a ruling Power, that he might find plenty to

[022-135] do in the art he practised while waiting for a call to higher labours.

[022-136] Then a wave of warmth came over him as he thought how near he now stood

[022-137] to the bright-eyed vivacious girl with the broad forehead and pile of

[022-138] dark hair above it; the girl with the kindling glance, daringly soft at

[022-139] times—something like that of the girls he had seen in engravings from

[022-140] paintings of the Spanish school. She was here—actually in this Close—in

[022-141] one of the houses confronting this very west façade.

[022-142] He went down the broad gravel path towards the building. It was an

[022-143] ancient edifice of the fifteenth century, once a palace, now a

[022-144] training-school, with mullioned and transomed windows, and a courtyard

[022-145] in front shut in from the road by a wall. Jude opened the gate and went

[022-146] up to the door through which, on inquiring for his cousin, he was

[022-147] gingerly admitted to a waiting-room, and in a few minutes she came.

[022-148] Though she had been here such a short while, she was not as he had seen

[022-149] her last. All her bounding manner was gone; her curves of motion had

[022-150] become subdued lines. The screens and subtleties of convention had

[022-151] likewise disappeared. Yet neither was she quite the woman who had

[022-152] written the letter that summoned him. That had plainly been dashed off

[022-153] in an impulse which second thoughts had somewhat regretted; thoughts

[022-154] that were possibly of his recent self-disgrace. Jude was quite overcome

[022-155] with emotion.

[022-156] “You don’t—think me a demoralized wretch—for coming to you as I was—and

[022-157] going so shamefully, Sue?”

[022-158] “Oh, I have tried not to! You said enough to let me know what had

[022-159] caused it. I hope I shall never have any doubt of your worthiness, my

[022-160] poor Jude! And I am glad you have come!”

[022-161] She wore a murrey-coloured gown with a little lace collar. It was made

[022-162] quite plain, and hung about her slight figure with clinging

[022-163] gracefulness. Her hair, which formerly she had worn according to the

[022-164] custom of the day was now twisted up tightly, and she had altogether

[022-165] the air of a woman clipped and pruned by severe discipline, an

[022-166] under-brightness shining through from the depths which that discipline

[022-167] had not yet been able to reach.

[022-168] She had come forward prettily, but Jude felt that she had hardly

[022-169] expected him to kiss her, as he was burning to do, under other colours

[022-170] than those of cousinship. He could not perceive the least sign that Sue

[022-171] regarded him as a lover, or ever would do so, now that she knew the

[022-172] worst of him, even if he had the right to behave as one; and this

[022-173] helped on his growing resolve to tell her of his matrimonial

[022-174] entanglement, which he had put off doing from time to time in sheer

[022-175] dread of losing the bliss of her company.
