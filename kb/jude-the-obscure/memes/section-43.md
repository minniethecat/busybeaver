[043-002] A seraph of Heaven, too gentle to be human,

[043-003] Veiling beneath that radiant form of woman…

[043-004] Oh it is too flattering, so I won’t go on! But say it’s me! Say it’s

[043-005] me!”

[043-006] “It is you, dear; exactly like you!”

[043-007] “Now I forgive you! And you shall kiss me just once there—not very

[043-008] long.” She put the tip of her finger gingerly to her cheek; and he did

[043-009] as commanded. “You do care for me very much, don’t you, in spite of my

[043-010] not—you know?”

[043-011] “Yes, sweet!” he said with a sigh; and bade her good-night.

[043-012] VI

[043-013] In returning to his native town of Shaston as schoolmaster Phillotson

[043-014] had won the interest and awakened the memories of the inhabitants, who,

[043-015] though they did not honour him for his miscellaneous acquirements as he

[043-016] would have been honoured elsewhere, retained for him a sincere regard.

[043-017] When, shortly after his arrival, he brought home a pretty

[043-018] wife—awkwardly pretty for him, if he did not take care, they said—they

[043-019] were glad to have her settle among them.

[043-020] For some time after her flight from that home Sue’s absence did not

[043-021] excite comment. Her place as monitor in the school was taken by another

[043-022] young woman within a few days of her vacating it, which substitution

[043-023] also passed without remark, Sue’s services having been of a provisional

[043-024] nature only. When, however, a month had passed, and Phillotson casually

[043-025] admitted to an acquaintance that he did not know where his wife was

[043-026] staying, curiosity began to be aroused; till, jumping to conclusions,

[043-027] people ventured to affirm that Sue had played him false and run away

[043-028] from him. The schoolmaster’s growing languor and listlessness over his

[043-029] work gave countenance to the idea.

[043-030] Though Phillotson had held his tongue as long as he could, except to

[043-031] his friend Gillingham, his honesty and directness would not allow him

[043-032] to do so when misapprehensions as to Sue’s conduct spread abroad. On a

[043-033] Monday morning the chairman of the school committee called, and after

[043-034] attending to the business of the school drew Phillotson aside out of

[043-035] earshot of the children.

[043-036] “You’ll excuse my asking, Phillotson, since everybody is talking of it:

[043-037] is this true as to your domestic affairs—that your wife’s going away

[043-038] was on no visit, but a secret elopement with a lover? If so, I condole

[043-039] with you.”

[043-040] “Don’t,” said Phillotson. “There was no secret about it.”

[043-041] “She has gone to visit friends?”

[043-042] “No.”

[043-043] “Then what has happened?”

[043-044] “She has gone away under circumstances that usually call for condolence

[043-045] with the husband. But I gave my consent.”

[043-046] The chairman looked as if he had not apprehended the remark.

[043-047] “What I say is quite true,” Phillotson continued testily. “She asked

[043-048] leave to go away with her lover, and I let her. Why shouldn’t I? A

[043-049] woman of full age, it was a question of her own conscience—not for me.

[043-050] I was not her gaoler. I can’t explain any further. I don’t wish to be

[043-051] questioned.”

[043-052] The children observed that much seriousness marked the faces of the two

[043-053] men, and went home and told their parents that something new had

[043-054] happened about Mrs. Phillotson. Then Phillotson’s little maidservant,

[043-055] who was a schoolgirl just out of her standards, said that Mr.

[043-056] Phillotson had helped in his wife’s packing, had offered her what money

[043-057] she required, and had written a friendly letter to her young man,

[043-058] telling him to take care of her. The chairman of committee thought the

[043-059] matter over, and talked to the other managers of the school, till a

[043-060] request came to Phillotson to meet them privately. The meeting lasted a

[043-061] long time, and at the end the school-master came home, looking as usual

[043-062] pale and worn. Gillingham was sitting in his house awaiting him.

[043-063] “Well; it is as you said,” observed Phillotson, flinging himself down

[043-064] wearily in a chair. “They have requested me to send in my resignation

[043-065] on account of my scandalous conduct in giving my tortured wife her

[043-066] liberty—or, as they call it, condoning her adultery. But I shan’t

[043-067] resign!”

[043-068] “I think I would.”

[043-069] “I won’t. It is no business of theirs. It doesn’t affect me in my

[043-070] public capacity at all. They may expel me if they like.”

[043-071] “If you make a fuss it will get into the papers, and you’ll never get

[043-072] appointed to another school. You see, they have to consider what you

[043-073] did as done by a teacher of youth—and its effects as such upon the

[043-074] morals of the town; and, to ordinary opinion, your position is

[043-075] indefensible. You must let me say that.”

[043-076] To this good advice, however, Phillotson would not listen.

[043-077] “I don’t care,” he said. “I don’t go unless I am turned out. And for

[043-078] this reason; that by resigning I acknowledge I have acted wrongly by

[043-079] her; when I am more and more convinced every day that in the sight of

[043-080] Heaven and by all natural, straightforward humanity, I have acted

[043-081] rightly.”

[043-082] Gillingham saw that his rather headstrong friend would not be able to

[043-083] maintain such a position as this; but he said nothing further, and in

[043-084] due time—indeed, in a quarter of an hour—the formal letter of dismissal

[043-085] arrived, the managers having remained behind to write it after

[043-086] Phillotson’s withdrawal. The latter replied that he should not accept

[043-087] dismissal; and called a public meeting, which he attended, although he

[043-088] looked so weak and ill that his friend implored him to stay at home.

[043-089] When he stood up to give his reasons for contesting the decision of the

[043-090] managers he advanced them firmly, as he had done to his friend, and

[043-091] contended, moreover, that the matter was a domestic theory which did

[043-092] not concern them. This they over-ruled, insisting that the private

[043-093] eccentricities of a teacher came quite within their sphere of control,

[043-094] as it touched the morals of those he taught. Phillotson replied that he

[043-095] did not see how an act of natural charity could injure morals.

[043-096] All the respectable inhabitants and well-to-do fellow-natives of the

[043-097] town were against Phillotson to a man. But, somewhat to his surprise,

[043-098] some dozen or more champions rose up in his defence as from the ground.

[043-099] It has been stated that Shaston was the anchorage of a curious and

[043-100] interesting group of itinerants, who frequented the numerous fairs and

[043-101] markets held up and down Wessex during the summer and autumn months.

[043-102] Although Phillotson had never spoken to one of these gentlemen they now

[043-103] nobly led the forlorn hope in his defence. The body included two cheap

[043-104] Jacks, a shooting-gallery proprietor and the ladies who loaded the

[043-105] guns, a pair of boxing-masters, a steam-roundabout manager, two

[043-106] travelling broom-makers, who called themselves widows, a

[043-107] gingerbread-stall keeper, a swing-boat owner, and a

[043-108] “test-your-strength” man.

[043-109] This generous phalanx of supporters, and a few others of independent

[043-110] judgment, whose own domestic experiences had been not without

[043-111] vicissitude, came up and warmly shook hands with Phillotson; after

[043-112] which they expressed their thoughts so strongly to the meeting that

[043-113] issue was joined, the result being a general scuffle, wherein a black

[043-114] board was split, three panes of the school windows were broken, an

[043-115] inkbottle was spilled over a town-councillor’s shirt front, a

[043-116] churchwarden was dealt such a topper with the map of Palestine that his

[043-117] head went right through Samaria, and many black eyes and bleeding noses

[043-118] were given, one of which, to everybody’s horror, was the venerable

[043-119] incumbent’s, owing to the zeal of an emancipated chimney-sweep, who

[043-120] took the side of Phillotson’s party. When Phillotson saw the blood

[043-121] running down the rector’s face he deplored almost in groans the

[043-122] untoward and degrading circumstances, regretted that he had not

[043-123] resigned when called upon, and went home so ill that next morning he

[043-124] could not leave his bed.

[043-125] The farcical yet melancholy event was the beginning of a serious

[043-126] illness for him; and he lay in his lonely bed in the pathetic state of

[043-127] mind of a middle-aged man who perceives at length that his life,

[043-128] intellectual and domestic, is tending to failure and gloom. Gillingham

[043-129] came to see him in the evenings, and on one occasion mentioned Sue’s

[043-130] name.

[043-131] “She doesn’t care anything about me!” said Phillotson. “Why should

[043-132] she?”

[043-133] “She doesn’t know you are ill.”

[043-134] “So much the better for both of us.”

[043-135] “Where are her lover and she living?”

[043-136] “At Melchester—I suppose; at least he was living there some time ago.”

[043-137] When Gillingham reached home he sat and reflected, and at last wrote an

[043-138] anonymous line to Sue, on the bare chance of its reaching her, the

[043-139] letter being enclosed in an envelope addressed to Jude at the diocesan

[043-140] capital. Arriving at that place it was forwarded to Marygreen in North

[043-141] Wessex, and thence to Aldbrickham by the only person who knew his

[043-142] present address—the widow who had nursed his aunt.

[043-143] Three days later, in the evening, when the sun was going down in

[043-144] splendour over the lowlands of Blackmoor, and making the Shaston

[043-145] windows like tongues of fire to the eyes of the rustics in that vale,

[043-146] the sick man fancied that he heard somebody come to the house, and a

[043-147] few minutes after there was a tap at the bedroom door. Phillotson did

[043-148] not speak; the door was hesitatingly opened, and there entered—Sue.

[043-149] She was in light spring clothing, and her advent seemed ghostly—like

[043-150] the flitting in of a moth. He turned his eyes upon her, and flushed;

[043-151] but appeared to check his primary impulse to speak.

[043-152] “I have no business here,” she said, bending her frightened face to

[043-153] him. “But I heard you were ill—very ill; and—and as I know that you

[043-154] recognize other feelings between man and woman than physical love, I

[043-155] have come.”

[043-156] “I am not very ill, my dear friend. Only unwell.”

[043-157] “I didn’t know that; and I am afraid that only a severe illness would

[043-158] have justified my coming!”

[043-159] “Yes… yes. And I almost wish you had not come! It is a little too

[043-160] soon—that’s all I mean. Still, let us make the best of it. You haven’t

[043-161] heard about the school, I suppose?”

[043-162] “No—what about it?”

[043-163] “Only that I am going away from here to another place. The managers and

[043-164] I don’t agree, and we are going to part—that’s all.”

[043-165] Sue did not for a moment, either now or later, suspect what troubles

[043-166] had resulted to him from letting her go; it never once seemed to cross

[043-167] her mind, and she had received no news whatever from Shaston. They

[043-168] talked on slight and ephemeral subjects, and when his tea was brought

[043-169] up he told the amazed little servant that a cup was to be set for Sue.

[043-170] That young person was much more interested in their history than they

[043-171] supposed, and as she descended the stairs she lifted her eyes and hands

[043-172] in grotesque amazement. While they sipped Sue went to the window and

[043-173] thoughtfully said, “It is such a beautiful sunset, Richard.”

[043-174] “They are mostly beautiful from here, owing to the rays crossing the

[043-175] mist of the vale. But I lose them all, as they don’t shine into this

[043-176] gloomy corner where I lie.”

[043-177] “Wouldn’t you like to see this particular one? It is like heaven

[043-178] opened.”

[043-179] “Ah yes! But I can’t.”

[043-180] “I’ll help you to.”

[043-181] “No—the bedstead can’t be shifted.”

[043-182] “But see how I mean.”

[043-183] She went to where a swing-glass stood, and taking it in her hands

[043-184] carried it to a spot by the window where it could catch the sunshine,

[043-185] moving the glass till the beams were reflected into Phillotson’s face.

[043-186] “There—you can see the great red sun now!” she said. “And I am sure it

[043-187] will cheer you—I do so hope it will!” She spoke with a childlike,

[043-188] repentant kindness, as if she could not do too much for him.

[043-189] Phillotson smiled sadly. “You are an odd creature!” he murmured as the

[043-190] sun glowed in his eyes. “The idea of your coming to see me after what

[043-191] has passed!”

[043-192] “Don’t let us go back upon that!” she said quickly. “I have to catch

[043-193] the omnibus for the train, as Jude doesn’t know I have come; he was out

[043-194] when I started; so I must return home almost directly. Richard, I am so

[043-195] very glad you are better. You don’t hate me, do you? You have been such

[043-196] a kind friend to me!”
