# Despair

Despair is the emotional terminus of *Jude the Obscure* — the state toward which every character's arc tends. More than a passing mood, despair in Hardy's novel is a philosophically grounded condition: the logical response of a sensitive consciousness to a world in which aspiration is systematically punished and love is foreclosed by institution.

## Definition

In the novel, "despair" operates on a spectrum from acute disappointment to existential hopelessness. It encompasses Jude's early experience of "long-drawn, slow and hopeless" despair (011-051), Phillotson's professional desolation, Sue's collapse after the children's deaths, and Jude's final conviction that "I would have gone to the dogs with despair" (056-066). Hardy distinguishes despair from mere sadness by its totality — despair is the recognition that no avenue of escape or improvement remains.

## In *Jude the Obscure*

### Manifestation

Despair appears at every narrative junction as the consequence of blocked hope. Jude's first experience of it follows his failed marriage to Arabella — "the stereotyped resource of the despairing worthless" (012-103). It intensifies with each institutional rejection: the university, the Church, the law. Sue, who initially resists despair through intellectual rationalization, succumbs entirely after Little Father Time's murder-suicide: "she answered with despairing" finality (063-015). By the novel's end, despair has become the shared condition of both protagonists — Jude dying in bitter hopelessness, Sue living in its shadow through her enforced return to Phillotson.

### Key Characters

| Character | Relation to Despair |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Jude Fawley | The novel's primary subject of despair — his trajectory from hope to hopelessness is the novel's emotional structure |
| Sue Bridehead | Initially avoids despair through intellectual detachment; collapses into it after the children's deaths in the novel's most dramatic reversal |
| Richard Phillotson | Experiences despair as quiet resignation — his ruined career and loveless remarriage to Sue represent despair sublimated into duty |

### Narrative Trajectory

Despair is introduced early as a foreboding undercurrent (Part First) and deepens with each of Jude's institutional rejections (Parts Second through Fourth). It reaches its catastrophic climax in Part Sixth with the children's deaths — an event so absolute in its despair that it reverses Sue's entire philosophical orientation. The novel closes in despair: Jude dead, Sue trapped, and Arabella's cynical laughter providing the narrative's final note.

## Critical Interpretation

Despair in *Jude* has been read as the culmination of Hardy's philosophical pessimism — a worldview shaped by Schopenhauer, Darwin, and the erosion of Victorian religious certainty. Unlike the redemptive suffering of Christian narrative, Hardy's despair offers no transcendence. The novel's despair is emphatically material: it is produced by institutions (the university, the Church, marriage law) that could be otherwise, making the tragedy political as much as philosophical. D.H. Lawrence noted that Jude's despair is "not weakness but the final courage — to see things as they are."
