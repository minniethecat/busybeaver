[070-001] Jude was absolutely indifferent to what she said, and indeed, often

[070-002] regarded her abuse in a humorous light. Sometimes his mood was more

[070-003] earnest, and as he lay he often rambled on upon the defeat of his early

[070-004] aims.

[070-005] “Every man has some little power in some one direction,” he would say.

[070-006] “I was never really stout enough for the stone trade, particularly the

[070-007] fixing. Moving the blocks always used to strain me, and standing the

[070-008] trying draughts in buildings before the windows are in always gave me

[070-009] colds, and I think that began the mischief inside. But I felt I could

[070-010] do one thing if I had the opportunity. I could accumulate ideas, and

[070-011] impart them to others. I wonder if the founders had such as I in their

[070-012] minds—a fellow good for nothing else but that particular thing? … I

[070-013] hear that soon there is going to be a better chance for such helpless

[070-014] students as I was. There are schemes afoot for making the university

[070-015] less exclusive, and extending its influence. I don’t know much about

[070-016] it. And it is too late, too late for me! Ah—and for how many worthier

[070-017] ones before me!”

[070-018] “How you keep a-mumbling!” said Arabella. “I should have thought you’d

[070-019] have got over all that craze about books by this time. And so you

[070-020] would, if you’d had any sense to begin with. You are as bad now as when

[070-021] we were first married.”

[070-022] On one occasion while soliloquizing thus he called her “Sue”

[070-023] unconsciously.

[070-024] “I wish you’d mind who you are talking to!” said Arabella indignantly.

[070-025] “Calling a respectable married woman by the name of that—” She

[070-026] remembered herself and he did not catch the word.

[070-027] But in the course of time, when she saw how things were going, and how

[070-028] very little she had to fear from Sue’s rivalry, she had a fit of

[070-029] generosity. “I suppose you want to see your—Sue?” she said. “Well, I

[070-030] don’t mind her coming. You can have her here if you like.”

[070-031] “I don’t wish to see her again.”

[070-032] “Oh—that’s a change!”

[070-033] “And don’t tell her anything about me—that I’m ill, or anything. She

[070-034] has chosen her course. Let her go!”

[070-035] One day he received a surprise. Mrs. Edlin came to see him, quite on

[070-036] her own account. Jude’s wife, whose feelings as to where his affections

[070-037] were centred had reached absolute indifference by this time, went out,

[070-038] leaving the old woman alone with Jude. He impulsively asked how Sue

[070-039] was, and then said bluntly, remembering what Sue had told him: “I

[070-040] suppose they are still only husband and wife in name?”

[070-041] Mrs. Edlin hesitated. “Well, no—it’s different now. She’s begun it

[070-042] quite lately—all of her own free will.”

[070-043] “When did she begin?” he asked quickly.

[070-044] “The night after you came. But as a punishment to her poor self. He

[070-045] didn’t wish it, but she insisted.”

[070-046] “Sue, my Sue—you darling fool—this is almost more than I can endure! …

[070-047] Mrs. Edlin—don’t be frightened at my rambling—I’ve got to talk to

[070-048] myself lying here so many hours alone—she was once a woman whose

[070-049] intellect was to mine like a star to a benzoline lamp: who saw all _my_

[070-050] superstitions as cobwebs that she could brush away with a word. Then

[070-051] bitter affliction came to us, and her intellect broke, and she veered

[070-052] round to darkness. Strange difference of sex, that time and

[070-053] circumstance, which enlarge the views of most men, narrow the views of

[070-054] women almost invariably. And now the ultimate horror has come—her

[070-055] giving herself like this to what she loathes, in her enslavement to

[070-056] forms! She, so sensitive, so shrinking, that the very wind seemed to

[070-057] blow on her with a touch of deference… As for Sue and me when we were

[070-058] at our own best, long ago—when our minds were clear, and our love of

[070-059] truth fearless—the time was not ripe for us! Our ideas were fifty years

[070-060] too soon to be any good to us. And so the resistance they met with

[070-061] brought reaction in her, and recklessness and ruin on me! … There—this,

[070-062] Mrs. Edlin, is how I go on to myself continually, as I lie here. I must

[070-063] be boring you awfully.”

[070-064] “Not at all, my dear boy. I could hearken to ’ee all day.”

[070-065] As Jude reflected more and more on her news, and grew more restless, he

[070-066] began in his mental agony to use terribly profane language about social

[070-067] conventions, which started a fit of coughing. Presently there came a

[070-068] knock at the door downstairs. As nobody answered it Mrs. Edlin herself

[070-069] went down.

[070-070] The visitor said blandly: “The Doctor.” The lanky form was that of

[070-071] Physician Vilbert, who had been called in by Arabella.

[070-072] “How is my patient at present?” asked the physician.

[070-073] “Oh bad—very bad! Poor chap, he got excited, and do blaspeam terribly,

[070-074] since I let out some gossip by accident—the more to my blame. But

[070-075] there—you must excuse a man in suffering for what he says, and I hope

[070-076] God will forgive him.”

[070-077] “Ah. I’ll go up and see him. Mrs. Fawley at home?”

[070-078] “She’s not in at present, but she’ll be here soon.”

[070-079] Vilbert went; but though Jude had hitherto taken the medicines of that

[070-080] skilful practitioner with the greatest indifference whenever poured

[070-081] down his throat by Arabella, he was now so brought to bay by events

[070-082] that he vented his opinion of Vilbert in the physician’s face, and so

[070-083] forcibly, and with such striking epithets, that Vilbert soon scurried

[070-084] downstairs again. At the door he met Arabella, Mrs. Edlin having left.

[070-085] Arabella inquired how he thought her husband was now, and seeing that

[070-086] the Doctor looked ruffled, asked him to take something. He assented.

[070-087] “I’ll bring it to you here in the passage,” she said. “There’s nobody

[070-088] but me about the house to-day.”

[070-089] She brought him a bottle and a glass, and he drank.

[070-090] Arabella began shaking with suppressed laughter. “What is this, my

[070-091] dear?” he asked, smacking his lips.

[070-092] “Oh—a drop of wine—and something in it.” Laughing again she said: “I

[070-093] poured your own love-philtre into it, that you sold me at the

[070-094] agricultural show, don’t you re-member?”

[070-095] “I do, I do! Clever woman! But you must be prepared for the

[070-096] consequences.” Putting his arm round her shoulders he kissed her there

[070-097] and then.

[070-098] “Don’t don’t,” she whispered, laughing good-humouredly. “My man will

[070-099] hear.”

[070-100] She let him out of the house, and as she went back she said to herself:

[070-101] “Well! Weak women must provide for a rainy day. And if my poor fellow

[070-102] upstairs do go off—as I suppose he will soon—it’s well to keep chances

[070-103] open. And I can’t pick and choose now as I could when I was younger.

[070-104] And one must take the old if one can’t get the young.”

[070-105] XI

[070-106] The last pages to which the chronicler of these lives would ask the

[070-107] reader’s attention are concerned with the scene in and out of Jude’s

[070-108] bedroom when leafy summer came round again.

[070-109] His face was now so thin that his old friends would hardly have known

[070-110] him. It was afternoon, and Arabella was at the looking-glass curling

[070-111] her hair, which operation she performed by heating an umbrella-stay in

[070-112] the flame of a candle she had lighted, and using it upon the flowing

[070-113] lock. When she had finished this, practised a dimple, and put on her

[070-114] things, she cast her eyes round upon Jude. He seemed to be sleeping,

[070-115] though his position was an elevated one, his malady preventing him

[070-116] lying down.

[070-117] Arabella, hatted, gloved, and ready, sat down and waited, as if

[070-118] expecting some one to come and take her place as nurse.

[070-119] Certain sounds from without revealed that the town was in festivity,

[070-120] though little of the festival, whatever it might have been, could be

[070-121] seen here. Bells began to ring, and the notes came into the room

[070-122] through the open window, and travelled round Jude’s head in a hum. They

[070-123] made her restless, and at last she said to herself: “Why ever doesn’t

[070-124] Father come?”

[070-125] She looked again at Jude, critically gauged his ebbing life, as she had

[070-126] done so many times during the late months, and glancing at his watch,

[070-127] which was hung up by way of timepiece, rose impatiently. Still he

[070-128] slept, and coming to a resolution she slipped from the room, closed the

[070-129] door noiselessly, and descended the stairs. The house was empty. The

[070-130] attraction which moved Arabella to go abroad had evidently drawn away

[070-131] the other inmates long before.

[070-132] It was a warm, cloudless, enticing day. She shut the front door, and

[070-133] hastened round into Chief Street, and when near the theatre could hear

[070-134] the notes of the organ, a rehearsal for a coming concert being in

[070-135] progress. She entered under the archway of Oldgate College, where men

[070-136] were putting up awnings round the quadrangle for a ball in the hall

[070-137] that evening. People who had come up from the country for the day were

[070-138] picnicking on the grass, and Arabella walked along the gravel paths and

[070-139] under the aged limes. But finding this place rather dull she returned

[070-140] to the streets, and watched the carriages drawing up for the concert,

[070-141] numerous Dons and their wives, and undergraduates with gay female

[070-142] companions, crowding up likewise. When the doors were closed, and the

[070-143] concert began, she moved on.

[070-144] The powerful notes of that concert rolled forth through the swinging

[070-145] yellow blinds of the open windows, over the housetops, and into the

[070-146] still air of the lanes. They reached so far as to the room in which

[070-147] Jude lay; and it was about this time that his cough began again and

[070-148] awakened him.

[070-149] As soon as he could speak he murmured, his eyes still closed: “A little

[070-150] water, please.”

[070-151] Nothing but the deserted room received his appeal, and he coughed to

[070-152] exhaustion again—saying still more feebly: “Water—some

[070-153] water—Sue—Arabella!”

[070-154] The room remained still as before. Presently he gasped again:

[070-155] “Throat—water—Sue—darling—drop of water—please—oh please!”

[070-156] No water came, and the organ notes, faint as a bee’s hum, rolled in as

[070-157] before.

[070-158] While he remained, his face changing, shouts and hurrahs came from

[070-159] somewhere in the direction of the river.

[070-160] “Ah—yes! The Remembrance games,” he murmured. “And I here. And Sue

[070-161] defiled!”

[070-162] The hurrahs were repeated, drowning the faint organ notes. Jude’s face

[070-163] changed more: he whispered slowly, his parched lips scarcely moving:

[070-164] _“Let the day perish wherein I was born, and the night in which it was

[070-165] said, There is a man-child conceived.”_

[070-166] (“Hurrah!”)

[070-167] _“Let that day be darkness; let not God regard it from above, neither

[070-168] let the light shine upon it. Lo, let that night be solitary, let no

[070-169] joyful voice come therein.”_

[070-170] (“Hurrah!”)

[070-171] _“Why died I not from the womb? Why did I not give up the ghost when I

[070-172] came out of the belly? … For now should I have lain still and been

[070-173] quiet. I should have slept: then had I been at rest!”_

[070-174] (“Hurrah!”)

[070-175] _“There the prisoners rest together; they hear not the voice of the

[070-176] oppressor… The small and the great are there; and the servant is free

[070-177] from his master. Wherefore is light given to him that is in misery, and

[070-178] life unto the bitter in soul?”_

[070-179] Meanwhile Arabella, in her journey to discover what was going on, took

[070-180] a short cut down a narrow street and through an obscure nook into the

[070-181] quad of Cardinal. It was full of bustle, and brilliant in the sunlight

[070-182] with flowers and other preparations for a ball here also. A carpenter

[070-183] nodded to her, one who had formerly been a fellow-workman of Jude’s. A

[070-184] corridor was in course of erection from the entrance to the hall

[070-185] staircase, of gay red and buff bunting. Waggon-loads of boxes

[070-186] containing bright plants in full bloom were being placed about, and the

[070-187] great staircase was covered with red cloth. She nodded to one workman

[070-188] and another, and ascended to the hall on the strength of their

[070-189] acquaintance, where they were putting down a new floor and decorating

[070-190] for the dance.
