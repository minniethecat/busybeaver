# Mrs. Edlin

Mrs. Edlin (often called the Widow Edlin) is a warm, pragmatic figure in *[[jude-fawley|Jude the Obscure]]*: a [[marygreen|Marygreen]] widow who provides practical help and emotional support to [[Jude-Fawley]] and [[Sue-Bridehead]] at crucial moments in their lives. Unlike the novel's more judgmental characters, Mrs. Edlin offers acceptance without moralizing — making her one of the few steady, sympathetic presences in the narrative.

## Character Overview

Mrs. Edlin is a widow living in [[marygreen|Marygreen]] who had served as nurse or attendant to [[Jude-Fawley]]'s great-aunt, [[aunt-drusilla|Drusilla Fawley]] (009-102). After the aunt's death, Mrs. Edlin remains connected to [[jude-fawley|Jude]] and later to [[sue-bridehead|Sue]], providing shelter, counsel, and practical assistance. She is characterised by a cheerful, no-nonsense disposition — "Still better!" she returns cheerfully when told of a difficult domestic arrangement (055-161).

She embodies the traditional village woman: unschooled in intellectual matters but rich in human wisdom. Her [[love|affection]] for both [[jude-fawley|Jude]] and [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] is genuine and uncomplicated — she does not share the wider community's moral objections to their relationship, instead offering practical support when others withdraw.

## Role in the Narrative

Mrs. Edlin first enters the story as a figure in [[jude-fawley|Jude]]'s childhood village: [[jude-fawley|Jude]]'s aunt sends word to "go and inquire how the Edlins be" (009-102). She becomes more significant after the death of [[jude-fawley|Jude]]'s great-aunt, when she helps [[jude-fawley|Jude]] with arrangements and later provides a home for [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] when needed (036-034).

Her most prominent role comes in the novel's later sections, where she accompanies [[jude-fawley|Jude]] and [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] on their journey to be married — "accompanying them except the Widow Edlin," the narrator notes, emphasising her singular presence (049-080). After the tragedy of the children's deaths, Mrs. Edlin is one of the few who remains by their side, crossing the [[christminster|Christminster]] road to assist (069-033).

At the novel's close, Mrs. Edlin appears at the deathbed scene alongside [[arabella-donn|Arabella]] — a pairing that underscores the contrast between authentic care and self-interest (071-107).
