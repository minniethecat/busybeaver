[006-001] As another outcome of this change of groove he visited on Sundays all

[006-002] the churches within a walk, and deciphered the Latin inscriptions on

[006-003] fifteenth-century brasses and tombs. On one of these pilgrimages he met

[006-004] with a hunch-backed old woman of great intelligence, who read

[006-005] everything she could lay her hands on, and she told him more yet of the

[006-006] romantic charms of the city of light and lore. Thither he resolved as

[006-007] firmly as ever to go.

[006-008] But how live in that city? At present he had no income at all. He had

[006-009] no trade or calling of any dignity or stability whatever on which he

[006-010] could subsist while carrying out an intellectual labour which might

[006-011] spread over many years.

[006-012] What was most required by citizens? Food, clothing, and shelter. An

[006-013] income from any work in preparing the first would be too meagre; for

[006-014] making the second he felt a distaste; the preparation of the third

[006-015] requisite he inclined to. They built in a city; therefore he would

[006-016] learn to build. He thought of his unknown uncle, his cousin Susanna’s

[006-017] father, an ecclesiastical worker in metal, and somehow mediæval art in

[006-018] any material was a trade for which he had rather a fancy. He could not

[006-019] go far wrong in following his uncle’s footsteps, and engaging himself

[006-020] awhile with the carcases that contained the scholar souls.

[006-021] As a preliminary he obtained some small blocks of freestone, metal not

[006-022] being available, and suspending his studies awhile, occupied his spare

[006-023] half-hours in copying the heads and capitals in his parish church.

[006-024] There was a stone-mason of a humble kind in Alfredston, and as soon as

[006-025] he had found a substitute for himself in his aunt’s little business, he

[006-026] offered his services to this man for a trifling wage. Here Jude had the

[006-027] opportunity of learning at least the rudiments of freestone-working.

[006-028] Some time later he went to a church-builder in the same place, and

[006-029] under the architect’s direction became handy at restoring the

[006-030] dilapidated masonries of several village churches round about.

[006-031] Not forgetting that he was only following up this handicraft as a prop

[006-032] to lean on while he prepared those greater engines which he flattered

[006-033] himself would be better fitted for him, he yet was interested in his

[006-034] pursuit on its own account. He now had lodgings during the week in the

[006-035] little town, whence he returned to Marygreen village every Saturday

[006-036] evening. And thus he reached and passed his nineteenth year.

[006-037] VI

[006-038] At this memorable date of his life he was, one Saturday, returning from

[006-039] Alfredston to Marygreen about three o’clock in the afternoon. It was

[006-040] fine, warm, and soft summer weather, and he walked with his tools at

[006-041] his back, his little chisels clinking faintly against the larger ones

[006-042] in his basket. It being the end of the week he had left work early, and

[006-043] had come out of the town by a round-about route which he did not

[006-044] usually frequent, having promised to call at a flour-mill near

[006-045] Cresscombe to execute a commission for his aunt.

[006-046] He was in an enthusiastic mood. He seemed to see his way to living

[006-047] comfortably in Christminster in the course of a year or two, and

[006-048] knocking at the doors of one of those strongholds of learning of which

[006-049] he had dreamed so much. He might, of course, have gone there now, in

[006-050] some capacity or other, but he preferred to enter the city with a

[006-051] little more assurance as to means than he could be said to feel at

[006-052] present. A warm self-content suffused him when he considered what he

[006-053] had already done. Now and then as he went along he turned to face the

[006-054] peeps of country on either side of him. But he hardly saw them; the act

[006-055] was an automatic repetition of what he had been accustomed to do when

[006-056] less occupied; and the one matter which really engaged him was the

[006-057] mental estimate of his progress thus far.

[006-058] “I have acquired quite an average student’s power to read the common

[006-059] ancient classics, Latin in particular.” This was true, Jude possessing

[006-060] a facility in that language which enabled him with great ease to

[006-061] himself to beguile his lonely walks by imaginary conversations therein.

[006-062] “I have read two books of the _Iliad_, besides being pretty familiar

[006-063] with passages such as the speech of Phœnix in the ninth book, the fight

[006-064] of Hector and Ajax in the fourteenth, the appearance of Achilles

[006-065] unarmed and his heavenly armour in the eighteenth, and the funeral

[006-066] games in the twenty-third. I have also done some Hesiod, a little scrap

[006-067] of Thucydides, and a lot of the Greek Testament… I wish there was only

[006-068] one dialect all the same.

[006-069] “I have done some mathematics, including the first six and the eleventh

[006-070] and twelfth books of Euclid; and algebra as far as simple equations.

[006-071] “I know something of the Fathers, and something of Roman and English

[006-072] history.

[006-073] “These things are only a beginning. But I shall not make much farther

[006-074] advance here, from the difficulty of getting books. Hence I must next

[006-075] concentrate all my energies on settling in Christminster. Once there I

[006-076] shall so advance, with the assistance I shall there get, that my

[006-077] present knowledge will appear to me but as childish ignorance. I must

[006-078] save money, and I will; and one of those colleges shall open its doors

[006-079] to me—shall welcome whom now it would spurn, if I wait twenty years for

[006-080] the welcome.

[006-081] “I’ll be D.D. before I have done!”

[006-082] And then he continued to dream, and thought he might become even a

[006-083] bishop by leading a pure, energetic, wise, Christian life. And what an

[006-084] example he would set! If his income were £5000 a year, he would give

[006-085] away £4500 in one form and another, and live sumptuously (for him) on

[006-086] the remainder. Well, on second thoughts, a bishop was absurd. He would

[006-087] draw the line at an archdeacon. Perhaps a man could be as good and as

[006-088] learned and as useful in the capacity of archdeacon as in that of

[006-089] bishop. Yet he thought of the bishop again.

[006-090] “Meanwhile I will read, as soon as I am settled in Christminster, the

[006-091] books I have not been able to get hold of here: Livy, Tacitus,

[006-092] Herodotus, Æschylus, Sophocles, Aristophanes—”

[006-093] “Ha, ha, ha! Hoity-toity!” The sounds were expressed in light voices on

[006-094] the other side of the hedge, but he did not notice them. His thoughts

[006-095] went on:

[006-096] “—Euripides, Plato, Aristotle, Lucretius, Epictetus, Seneca, Antoninus.

[006-097] Then I must master other things: the Fathers thoroughly; Bede and

[006-098] ecclesiastical history generally; a smattering of Hebrew—I only know

[006-099] the letters as yet—”

[006-100] “Hoity-toity!”

[006-101] “—but I can work hard. I have staying power in abundance, thank God!

[006-102] and it is that which tells… Yes, Christminster shall be my Alma Mater;

[006-103] and I’ll be her beloved son, in whom she shall be well pleased.”

[006-104] In his deep concentration on these transactions of the future Jude’s

[006-105] walk had slackened, and he was now standing quite still, looking at the

[006-106] ground as though the future were thrown thereon by a magic lantern. On

[006-107] a sudden something smacked him sharply in the ear, and he became aware

[006-108] that a soft cold substance had been flung at him, and had fallen at his

[006-109] feet.

[006-110] A glance told him what it was—a piece of flesh, the characteristic part

[006-111] of a barrow-pig, which the countrymen used for greasing their boots, as

[006-112] it was useless for any other purpose. Pigs were rather plentiful

[006-113] hereabout, being bred and fattened in large numbers in certain parts of

[006-114] North Wessex.

[006-115] On the other side of the hedge was a stream, whence, as he now for the

[006-116] first time realized, had come the slight sounds of voices and laughter

[006-117] that had mingled with his dreams. He mounted the bank and looked over

[006-118] the fence. On the further side of the stream stood a small homestead,

[006-119] having a garden and pig-sties attached; in front of it, beside the

[006-120] brook, three young women were kneeling, with buckets and platters

[006-121] beside them containing heaps of pigs’ chitterlings, which they were

[006-122] washing in the running water. One or two pairs of eyes slyly glanced

[006-123] up, and perceiving that his attention had at last been attracted, and

[006-124] that he was watching them, they braced themselves for inspection by

[006-125] putting their mouths demurely into shape and recommencing their rinsing

[006-126] operations with assiduity.

[006-127] “Thank you!” said Jude severely.

[006-128] “I _didn’t_ throw it, I tell you!” asserted one girl to her neighbour,

[006-129] as if unconscious of the young man’s presence.

[006-130] “Nor I,” the second answered.

[006-131] “Oh, Anny, how can you!” said the third.

[006-132] “If I had thrown anything at all, it shouldn’t have been _that_!”

[006-133] “Pooh! I don’t care for him!” And they laughed and continued their

[006-134] work, without looking up, still ostentatiously accusing each other.

[006-135] Jude grew sarcastic as he wiped his face, and caught their remarks.

[006-136] “_You_ didn’t do it—oh no!” he said to the up-stream one of the three.

[006-137] She whom he addressed was a fine dark-eyed girl, not exactly handsome,

[006-138] but capable of passing as such at a little distance, despite some

[006-139] coarseness of skin and fibre. She had a round and prominent bosom, full

[006-140] lips, perfect teeth, and the rich complexion of a Cochin hen’s egg. She

[006-141] was a complete and substantial female animal—no more, no less; and Jude

[006-142] was almost certain that to her was attributable the enterprise of

[006-143] attracting his attention from dreams of the humaner letters to what was

[006-144] simmering in the minds around him.

[006-145] “That you’ll never be told,” said she deedily.

[006-146] “Whoever did it was wasteful of other people’s property.”

[006-147] “Oh, that’s nothing.”

[006-148] “But you want to speak to me, I suppose?”

[006-149] “Oh yes; if you like to.”

[006-150] “Shall I clamber across, or will you come to the plank above here?”

[006-151] Perhaps she foresaw an opportunity; for somehow or other the eyes of

[006-152] the brown girl rested in his own when he had said the words, and there

[006-153] was a momentary flash of intelligence, a dumb announcement of affinity

[006-154] _in posse_ between herself and him, which, so far as Jude Fawley was

[006-155] concerned, had no sort of premeditation in it. She saw that he had

[006-156] singled her out from the three, as a woman is singled out in such

[006-157] cases, for no reasoned purpose of further acquaintance, but in

[006-158] commonplace obedience to conjunctive orders from headquarters,

[006-159] unconsciously received by unfortunate men when the last intention of

[006-160] their lives is to be occupied with the feminine.

[006-161] Springing to her feet, she said: “Bring back what is lying there.”

[006-162] Jude was now aware that no message on any matter connected with her

[006-163] father’s business had prompted her signal to him. He set down his

[006-164] basket of tools, picked up the scrap of offal, beat a pathway for

[006-165] himself with his stick, and got over the hedge. They walked in parallel

[006-166] lines, one on each bank of the stream, towards the small plank bridge.

[006-167] As the girl drew nearer to it, she gave without Jude perceiving it, an

[006-168] adroit little suck to the interior of each of her cheeks in succession,

[006-169] by which curious and original manœuvre she brought as by magic upon its

[006-170] smooth and rotund surface a perfect dimple, which she was able to

[006-171] retain there as long as she continued to smile. This production of

[006-172] dimples at will was a not unknown operation, which many attempted, but

[006-173] only a few succeeded in accomplishing.

[006-174] They met in the middle of the plank, and Jude, tossing back her

[006-175] missile, seemed to expect her to explain why she had audaciously

[006-176] stopped him by this novel artillery instead of by hailing him.

[006-177] But she, slyly looking in another direction, swayed herself backwards

[006-178] and forwards on her hand as it clutched the rail of the bridge; till,

[006-179] moved by amatory curiosity, she turned her eyes critically upon him.

[006-180] “You don’t think _I_ would shy things at you?”

[006-181] “Oh no.”

[006-182] “We are doing this for my father, who naturally doesn’t want anything

[006-183] thrown away. He makes that into dubbin.” She nodded towards the

[006-184] fragment on the grass.
