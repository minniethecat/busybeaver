# Divorce

Divorce in *Jude the Obscure* is the escape that never comes — the legal remedy that Victorian society nominally provided but that remained, in practice, inaccessible to the working-class characters who most needed it. The near-impossibility of dissolving a marriage is not merely background to the novel's plot but its engine: every major tragedy in the narrative traces back to the fact that characters cannot legally leave the spouses they have chosen in error.

## Background

The legal framework of divorce in Victorian England was established by the Matrimonial Causes Act of 1857, which created a secular Court for Divorce and Matrimonial Causes. Before 1857, divorce required a private Act of Parliament — a process so expensive that only the wealthiest could pursue it. The 1857 Act made divorce theoretically more accessible, but it retained a fundamental gender asymmetry: a husband could divorce his wife for adultery alone, while a wife had to prove adultery *plus* an additional ground (cruelty, desertion, incest, or sodomy).

Moreover, the costs remained prohibitive for the working class. A full divorce suit cost approximately £40–50 in the 1890s — roughly a year's wages for a stonemason like Jude. Legal separation (a *mensa et thoro*, "from bed and board") was cheaper but did not permit remarriage. Characters who separated remained legally bound to their spouses for life.

## In *Jude the Obscure*

### Jude and Arabella

Jude's first marriage to Arabella ends not in divorce but in abandonment — Arabella simply leaves for Australia with her parents. This leaves Jude in legal limbo: he is married to a woman who has vanished across the globe. When he later wishes to marry Sue, he cannot, because Arabella is still his legal wife. The novel makes clear that Jude could theoretically pursue divorce on grounds of desertion, but the cost and complexity are beyond his means.

Arabella's return later in the novel reveals the asymmetry of the situation: she has no difficulty remarrying (she marries Cartlett in Australia), presumably because her second marriage is technically bigamous — a risk that a woman of Arabella's pragmatism is willing to take, and that the novel's lax enforcement makes possible.

### Sue and Phillotson

Sue's marriage to Phillotson produces the novel's most explicit engagement with divorce law. Phillotson, genuinely caring for Sue, agrees to let her leave him to live with Jude. He tells the school authorities, in effect, that he is permitting his wife to commit adultery — a remarkable act of generosity that costs him his position. But even this "friendly" separation does not constitute a legal divorce. Sue cannot remarry Jude; she can only live with him in a state the law calls concubinage.

When Sue later decides to remarry Phillotson (as an act of religious penance after the children's deaths), the novel's bitter irony is complete: divorce was unavailable when it would have liberated her, but her return to a legal marriage she loathes is effortless.

### The Central Tragedy

The novel's central tragedy — that Jude and Sue cannot be legally married to each other because Jude is already married to Arabella, and Sue to Phillotson — is entirely a product of divorce law. Had divorce been accessible, Jude could have dissolved his marriage to Arabella, Sue could have dissolved hers to Phillotson, and the couple could have married legally. Their social ostracism, their poverty (partly caused by their inability to settle legally), and ultimately the psychological pressure that drives Sue back to Phillotson — all flow from a legal system that trapped people in failed marriages.

## Critical Interpretation

Hardy's treatment of divorce was among the most controversial aspects of the novel. The *Pall Mall Gazette* accused him of advocating "free love" and attacking the sanctity of marriage. But Hardy's target was not marriage as such; it was the divorce law that made marriage a trap.

The novel was published in 1895, just two decades before significant divorce reform. The Matrimonial Causes Act of 1923 finally equalized the grounds for divorce between men and women, and the Matrimonial Causes Act of 1937 expanded the grounds beyond adultery to include cruelty, desertion, and incurable insanity. Hardy did not live to see these reforms (he died in 1928), but *Jude the Obscure* helped create the cultural conditions in which reform became possible.
