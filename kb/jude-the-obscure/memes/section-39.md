[039-001] “But it does—it hurts _me_! And you vowed to love me.”

[039-002] “Yes—that’s it! I am in the wrong. I always am! It is as culpable to

[039-003] bind yourself to love always as to believe a creed always, and as silly

[039-004] as to vow always to like a particular food or drink!”

[039-005] “And do you mean, by living away from me, living by yourself?”

[039-006] “Well, if you insisted, yes. But I meant living with Jude.”

[039-007] “As his wife?”

[039-008] “As I choose.”

[039-009] Phillotson writhed.

[039-010] Sue continued: “She, or he, ‘who lets the world, or his own portion of

[039-011] it, choose his plan of life for him, has no need of any other faculty

[039-012] than the apelike one of imitation.’ J. S. Mill’s words, those are. I

[039-013] have been reading it up. Why can’t you act upon them? I wish to,

[039-014] always.”

[039-015] “What do I care about J. S. Mill!” moaned he. “I only want to lead a

[039-016] quiet life! Do you mind my saying that I have guessed what never once

[039-017] occurred to me before our marriage—that you were in love, and are in

[039-018] love, with Jude Fawley!”

[039-019] “You may go on guessing that I am, since you have begun. But do you

[039-020] suppose that if I had been I should have asked you to let me go and

[039-021] live with him?”

[039-022] The ringing of the school bell saved Phillotson from the necessity of

[039-023] replying at present to what apparently did not strike him as being such

[039-024] a convincing _argumentum ad verecundiam_ as she, in her loss of courage

[039-025] at the last moment, meant it to appear. She was beginning to be so

[039-026] puzzling and unstateable that he was ready to throw in with her other

[039-027] little peculiarities the extremest request which a wife could make.

[039-028] They proceeded to the schools that morning as usual, Sue entering the

[039-029] class-room, where he could see the back of her head through the glass

[039-030] partition whenever he turned his eyes that way. As he went on giving

[039-031] and hearing lessons his forehead and eyebrows twitched from

[039-032] concentrated agitation of thought, till at length he tore a scrap from

[039-033] a sheet of scribbling paper and wrote:

[039-034] Your request prevents my attending to work at all. I don’t know what I

[039-035] am doing! Was it seriously made?

[039-036] He folded the piece of paper very small, and gave it to a little boy to

[039-037] take to Sue. The child toddled off into the class-room. Phillotson saw

[039-038] his wife turn and take the note, and the bend of her pretty head as she

[039-039] read it, her lips slightly crisped, to prevent undue expression under

[039-040] fire of so many young eyes. He could not see her hands, but she changed

[039-041] her position, and soon the child returned, bringing nothing in reply.

[039-042] In a few minutes, however, one of Sue’s class appeared, with a little

[039-043] note similar to his own. These words only were pencilled therein:

[039-044] I am sincerely sorry to say that it was seriously made.

[039-045] Phillotson looked more disturbed than before, and the meeting-place of

[039-046] his brows twitched again. In ten minutes he called up the child he had

[039-047] just sent to her, and dispatched another missive:

[039-048] God knows I don’t want to thwart you in any reasonable way. My whole

[039-049] thought is to make you comfortable and happy. But I cannot agree to

[039-050] such a preposterous notion as your going to live with your lover. You

[039-051] would lose everybody’s respect and regard; and so should I!

[039-052] After an interval a similar part was enacted in the class-room, and an

[039-053] answer came:

[039-054] I know you mean my good. But I don’t want to be respectable! To produce

[039-055] “Human development in its richest diversity” (to quote your Humboldt)

[039-056] is to my mind far above respectability. No doubt my tastes are low—in

[039-057] your view—hopelessly low! If you won’t let me go to him, will you grant

[039-058] me this one request—allow me to live in your house in a separate way?

[039-059] To this he returned no answer.

[039-060] She wrote again:

[039-061] I know what you think. But cannot you have pity on me? I beg you to; I

[039-062] implore you to be merciful! I would not ask if I were not almost

[039-063] compelled by what I can’t bear! No poor woman has ever wished more than

[039-064] I that Eve had not fallen, so that (as the primitive Christians

[039-065] believed) some harmless mode of vegetation might have peopled Paradise.

[039-066] But I won’t trifle! Be kind to me—even though I have not been kind to

[039-067] you! I will go away, go abroad, anywhere, and never trouble you.

[039-068] Nearly an hour passed, and then he returned an answer:

[039-069] I do not wish to pain you. How well you _know_ I don’t! Give me a

[039-070] little time. I am disposed to agree to your last request.

[039-071] One line from her:

[039-072] Thank you from my heart, Richard. I do not deserve your kindness.

[039-073] All day Phillotson bent a dazed regard upon her through the glazed

[039-074] partition; and he felt as lonely as when he had not known her.

[039-075] But he was as good as his word, and consented to her living apart in

[039-076] the house. At first, when they met at meals, she had seemed more

[039-077] composed under the new arrangement; but the irksomeness of their

[039-078] position worked on her temperament, and the fibres of her nature seemed

[039-079] strained like harp-strings. She talked vaguely and indiscriminately to

[039-080] prevent his talking pertinently.

[039-082] Phillotson was sitting up late, as was often his custom, trying to get

[039-083] together the materials for his long-neglected hobby of Roman

[039-084] antiquities. For the first time since reviving the subject he felt a

[039-085] return of his old interest in it. He forgot time and place, and when he

[039-086] remembered himself and ascended to rest it was nearly two o’clock.

[039-087] His preoccupation was such that, though he now slept on the other side

[039-088] of the house, he mechanically went to the room that he and his wife had

[039-089] occupied when he first became a tenant of Old-Grove Place, which since

[039-090] his differences with Sue had been hers exclusively. He entered, and

[039-091] unconsciously began to undress.

[039-092] There was a cry from the bed, and a quick movement. Before the

[039-093] schoolmaster had realized where he was he perceived Sue starting up

[039-094] half-awake, staring wildly, and springing out upon the floor on the

[039-095] side away from him, which was towards the window. This was somewhat

[039-096] hidden by the canopy of the bedstead, and in a moment he heard her

[039-097] flinging up the sash. Before he had thought that she meant to do more

[039-098] than get air she had mounted upon the sill and leapt out. She

[039-099] disappeared in the darkness, and he heard her fall below.

[039-100] Phillotson, horrified, ran downstairs, striking himself sharply against

[039-101] the newel in his haste. Opening the heavy door he ascended the two or

[039-102] three steps to the level of the ground, and there on the gravel before

[039-103] him lay a white heap. Phillotson seized it in his arms, and bringing

[039-104] Sue into the hall seated her on a chair, where he gazed at her by the

[039-105] flapping light of the candle which he had set down in the draught on

[039-106] the bottom stair.

[039-107] She had certainly not broken her neck. She looked at him with eyes that

[039-108] seemed not to take him in; and though not particularly large in general

[039-109] they appeared so now. She pressed her side and rubbed her arm, as if

[039-110] conscious of pain; then stood up, averting her face, in evident

[039-111] distress at his gaze.

[039-112] “Thank God—you are not killed! Though it’s not for want of trying—not

[039-113] much hurt I hope?”

[039-114] Her fall, in fact, had not been a serious one, probably owing to the

[039-115] lowness of the old rooms and to the high level of the ground without.

[039-116] Beyond a scraped elbow and a blow in the side she had apparently

[039-117] incurred little harm.

[039-118] “I was asleep, I think!” she began, her pale face still turned away

[039-119] from him. “And something frightened me—a terrible dream—I thought I saw

[039-120] you—” The actual circumstances seemed to come back to her, and she was

[039-121] silent.

[039-122] Her cloak was hanging at the back of the door, and the wretched

[039-123] Phillotson flung it round her. “Shall I help you upstairs?” he asked

[039-124] drearily; for the significance of all this sickened him of himself and

[039-125] of everything.

[039-126] “No thank you, Richard. I am very little hurt. I can walk.”

[039-127] “You ought to lock your door,” he mechanically said, as if lecturing in

[039-128] school. “Then no one could intrude even by accident.”

[039-129] “I have tried—it won’t lock. All the doors are out of order.”

[039-130] The aspect of things was not improved by her admission. She ascended

[039-131] the staircase slowly, the waving light of the candle shining on her.

[039-132] Phillotson did not approach her, or attempt to ascend himself till he

[039-133] heard her enter her room. Then he fastened up the front door, and

[039-134] returning, sat down on the lower stairs, holding the newel with one

[039-135] hand, and bowing his face into the other. Thus he remained for a long

[039-136] long time—a pitiable object enough to one who had seen him; till,

[039-137] raising his head and sighing a sigh which seemed to say that the

[039-138] business of his life must be carried on, whether he had a wife or no,

[039-139] he took the candle and went upstairs to his lonely room on the other

[039-140] side of the landing.
