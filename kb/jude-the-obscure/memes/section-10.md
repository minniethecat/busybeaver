[010-001] “I thought—I thought, deary, perhaps you would go away all the more for

[010-002] that, and leave me to face it alone!”

[010-003] “You knew better! Of course I never dreamt six months ago, or even

[010-004] three, of marrying. It is a complete smashing up of my plans—I mean my

[010-005] plans before I knew you, my dear. But what are they, after all! Dreams

[010-006] about books, and degrees, and impossible fellowships, and all that.

[010-007] Certainly we’ll marry: we must!”

[010-008] That night he went out alone, and walked in the dark self-communing. He

[010-009] knew well, too well, in the secret centre of his brain, that Arabella

[010-010] was not worth a great deal as a specimen of womankind. Yet, such being

[010-011] the custom of the rural districts among honourable young men who had

[010-012] drifted so far into intimacy with a woman as he unfortunately had done,

[010-013] he was ready to abide by what he had said, and take the consequences.

[010-014] For his own soothing he kept up a factitious belief in her. His idea of

[010-015] her was the thing of most consequence, not Arabella herself, he

[010-016] sometimes said laconically.

[010-017] The banns were put in and published the very next Sunday. The people of

[010-018] the parish all said what a simple fool young Fawley was. All his

[010-019] reading had only come to this, that he would have to sell his books to

[010-020] buy saucepans. Those who guessed the probable state of affairs,

[010-021] Arabella’s parents being among them, declared that it was the sort of

[010-022] conduct they would have expected of such an honest young man as Jude in

[010-023] reparation of the wrong he had done his innocent sweetheart. The parson

[010-024] who married them seemed to think it satisfactory too. And so, standing

[010-025] before the aforesaid officiator, the two swore that at every other time

[010-026] of their lives till death took them, they would assuredly believe,

[010-027] feel, and desire precisely as they had believed, felt, and desired

[010-028] during the few preceding weeks. What was as remarkable as the

[010-029] undertaking itself was the fact that nobody seemed at all surprised at

[010-030] what they swore.

[010-031] Fawley’s aunt being a baker she made him a bride-cake, saying bitterly

[010-032] that it was the last thing she could do for him, poor silly fellow; and

[010-033] that it would have been far better if, instead of his living to trouble

[010-034] her, he had gone underground years before with his father and mother.

[010-035] Of this cake Arabella took some slices, wrapped them up in white

[010-036] note-paper, and sent them to her companions in the pork-dressing

[010-037] business, Anny and Sarah, labelling each packet “_In remembrance of

[010-038] good advice_.”

[010-039] The prospects of the newly married couple were certainly not very

[010-040] brilliant even to the most sanguine mind. He, a stone-mason’s

[010-041] apprentice, nineteen years of age, was working for half wages till he

[010-042] should be out of his time. His wife was absolutely useless in a

[010-043] town-lodging, where he at first had considered it would be necessary

[010-044] for them to live. But the urgent need of adding to income in ever so

[010-045] little a degree caused him to take a lonely roadside cottage between

[010-046] the Brown House and Marygreen, that he might have the profits of a

[010-047] vegetable garden, and utilize her past experiences by letting her keep

[010-048] a pig. But it was not the sort of life he had bargained for, and it was

[010-049] a long way to walk to and from Alfredston every day. Arabella, however,

[010-050] felt that all these make-shifts were temporary; she had gained a

[010-051] husband; that was the thing—a husband with a lot of earning power in

[010-052] him for buying her frocks and hats when he should begin to get

[010-053] frightened a bit, and stick to his trade, and throw aside those stupid

[010-054] books for practical undertakings.

[010-055] So to the cottage he took her on the evening of the marriage, giving up

[010-056] his old room at his aunt’s—where so much of the hard labour at Greek

[010-057] and Latin had been carried on.

[010-058] A little chill overspread him at her first unrobing. A long tail of

[010-059] hair, which Arabella wore twisted up in an enormous knob at the back of

[010-060] her head, was deliberately unfastened, stroked out, and hung upon the

[010-061] looking-glass which he had bought her.

[010-062] “What—it wasn’t your own?” he said, with a sudden distaste for her.

[010-063] “Oh no—it never is nowadays with the better class.”

[010-064] “Nonsense! Perhaps not in towns. But in the country it is supposed to

[010-065] be different. Besides, you’ve enough of your own, surely?”

[010-066] “Yes, enough as country notions go. But in town the men expect more,

[010-067] and when I was barmaid at Aldbrickham—”

[010-068] “Barmaid at Aldbrickham?”

[010-069] “Well, not exactly barmaid—I used to draw the drink at a public-house

[010-070] there—just for a little time; that was all. Some people put me up to

[010-071] getting this, and I bought it just for a fancy. The more you have the

[010-072] better in Aldbrickham, which is a finer town than all your

[010-073] Christminsters. Every lady of position wears false hair—the barber’s

[010-074] assistant told me so.”

[010-075] Jude thought with a feeling of sickness that though this might be true

[010-076] to some extent, for all that he knew, many unsophisticated girls would

[010-077] and did go to towns and remain there for years without losing their

[010-078] simplicity of life and embellishments. Others, alas, had an instinct

[010-079] towards artificiality in their very blood, and became adepts in

[010-080] counterfeiting at the first glimpse of it. However, perhaps there was

[010-081] no great sin in a woman adding to her hair, and he resolved to think no

[010-082] more of it.

[010-083] A new-made wife can usually manage to excite interest for a few weeks,

[010-084] even though the prospects of the household ways and means are cloudy.

[010-085] There is a certain piquancy about her situation, and her manner to her

[010-086] acquaintance at the sense of it, which carries off the gloom of facts,

[010-087] and renders even the humblest bride independent awhile of the real.

[010-088] Mrs. Jude Fawley was walking in the streets of Alfredston one

[010-089] market-day with this quality in her carriage when she met Anny her

[010-090] former friend, whom she had not seen since the wedding.

[010-091] As usual they laughed before talking; the world seemed funny to them

[010-092] without saying it.

[010-093] “So it turned out a good plan, you see!” remarked the girl to the wife.

[010-094] “I knew it would with such as him. He’s a dear good fellow, and you

[010-095] ought to be proud of un.”

[010-096] “I am,” said Mrs. Fawley quietly.

[010-097] “And when do you expect?”

[010-098] “Ssh! Not at all.”

[010-099] “What!”

[010-100] “I was mistaken.”

[010-101] “Oh, Arabella, Arabella; you be a deep one! Mistaken! well, that’s

[010-102] clever—it’s a real stroke of genius! It is a thing I never thought o’,

[010-103] wi’ all my experience! I never thought beyond bringing about the real

[010-104] thing—not that one could sham it!”

[010-105] “Don’t you be too quick to cry sham! ’Twasn’t sham. I didn’t know.”

[010-106] “My word—won’t he be in a taking! He’ll give it to ’ee o’ Saturday

[010-107] nights! Whatever it was, he’ll say it was a trick—a double one, by the

[010-108] Lord!”

[010-109] “I’ll own to the first, but not to the second… Pooh—he won’t care!

[010-110] He’ll be glad I was wrong in what I said. He’ll shake down, bless

[010-111] ’ee—men always do. What can ’em do otherwise? Married is married.”

[010-112] Nevertheless it was with a little uneasiness that Arabella approached

[010-113] the time when in the natural course of things she would have to reveal

[010-114] that the alarm she had raised had been without foundation. The occasion

[010-115] was one evening at bedtime, and they were in their chamber in the

[010-116] lonely cottage by the wayside to which Jude walked home from his work

[010-117] every day. He had worked hard the whole twelve hours, and had retired

[010-118] to rest before his wife. When she came into the room he was between

[010-119] sleeping and waking, and was barely conscious of her undressing before

[010-120] the little looking-glass as he lay.

[010-121] One action of hers, however, brought him to full cognition. Her face

[010-122] being reflected towards him as she sat, he could perceive that she was

[010-123] amusing herself by artificially producing in each cheek the dimple

[010-124] before alluded to, a curious accomplishment of which she was mistress,

[010-125] effecting it by a momentary suction. It seemed to him for the first

[010-126] time that the dimples were far oftener absent from her face during his

[010-127] intercourse with her nowadays than they had been in the earlier weeks

[010-128] of their acquaintance.

[010-129] “Don’t do that, Arabella!” he said suddenly. “There is no harm in it,

[010-130] but—I don’t like to see you.”

[010-131] She turned and laughed. “Lord, I didn’t know you were awake!” she said.

[010-132] “How countrified you are! That’s nothing.”

[010-133] “Where did you learn it?”

[010-134] “Nowhere that I know of. They used to stay without any trouble when I

[010-135] was at the public-house; but now they won’t. My face was fatter then.”

[010-136] “I don’t care about dimples. I don’t think they improve a

[010-137] woman—particularly a married woman, and of full-sized figure like you.”

[010-138] “Most men think otherwise.”

[010-139] “I don’t care what most men think, if they do. How do you know?”

[010-140] “I used to be told so when I was serving in the tap-room.”

[010-141] “Ah—that public-house experience accounts for your knowing about the

[010-142] adulteration of the ale when we went and had some that Sunday evening.

[010-143] I thought when I married you that you had always lived in your father’s

[010-144] house.”

[010-145] “You ought to have known better than that, and seen I was a little more

[010-146] finished than I could have been by staying where I was born. There was

[010-147] not much to do at home, and I was eating my head off, so I went away

[010-148] for three months.”

[010-149] “You’ll soon have plenty to do now, dear, won’t you?”

[010-150] “How do you mean?”

[010-151] “Why, of course—little things to make.”

[010-152] “Oh.”

[010-153] “When will it be? Can’t you tell me exactly, instead of in such general

[010-154] terms as you have used?”

[010-155] “Tell you?”

[010-156] “Yes—the date.”

[010-157] “There’s nothing to tell. I made a mistake.”

[010-158] “What?”

[010-159] “It was a mistake.”

[010-160] He sat bolt upright in bed and looked at her. “How can that be?”

[010-161] “Women fancy wrong things sometimes.”

[010-162] “But—! Why, of course, so unprepared as I was, without a stick of

[010-163] furniture, and hardly a shilling, I shouldn’t have hurried on our

[010-164] affair, and brought you to a half-furnished hut before I was ready, if

[010-165] it had not been for the news you gave me, which made it necessary to

[010-166] save you, ready or no… Good God!”

[010-167] “Don’t take on, dear. What’s done can’t be undone.”

[010-168] “I have no more to say!”

[010-169] He gave the answer simply, and lay down; and there was silence between

[010-170] them.

[010-171] When Jude awoke the next morning he seemed to see the world with a

[010-172] different eye. As to the point in question he was compelled to accept

[010-173] her word; in the circumstances he could not have acted otherwise while

[010-174] ordinary notions prevailed. But how came they to prevail?

[010-175] There seemed to him, vaguely and dimly, something wrong in a social

[010-176] ritual which made necessary a cancelling of well-formed schemes

[010-177] involving years of thought and labour, of foregoing a man’s one

[010-178] opportunity of showing himself superior to the lower animals, and of

[010-179] contributing his units of work to the general progress of his

[010-180] generation, because of a momentary surprise by a new and transitory

[010-181] instinct which had nothing in it of the nature of vice, and could be

[010-182] only at the most called weakness. He was inclined to inquire what he

[010-183] had done, or she lost, for that matter, that he deserved to be caught

[010-184] in a gin which would cripple him, if not her also, for the rest of a

[010-185] lifetime? There was perhaps something fortunate in the fact that the

[010-186] immediate reason of his marriage had proved to be non-existent. But the

[010-187] marriage remained.
