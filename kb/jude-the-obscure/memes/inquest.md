# Inquest

The coroner's inquest following the deaths of the children is the novel's most devastating scene of public reckoning — the mechanism by which Little Father Time's murder of his siblings and his own suicide are converted from private horror into legal fact. The inquest marks the point at which the tragedy becomes irreversible: after this, Jude and Sue's relationship cannot recover, and their separate trajectories toward Sue's religious mania and Jude's death are set in motion.

## Context（或 Background）

In Victorian England, any violent or unexplained death required a coroner's inquest — a public inquiry to establish the cause of death. The coroner, an elected official, convened a jury of local men who viewed the body and heard testimony. The inquest's purpose was factual (determining cause of death) rather than punitive, but the process exposed private tragedy to public scrutiny. For Jude and Sue, already ostracized for their unconventional union, the inquest adds the weight of official inquiry to their grief.

## Key Details

### The Discovery

The children are discovered in the morning: Little Father Time has hanged his younger brother and sister and then hanged himself, leaving a note: "Done because we are too menny." Sue, pregnant, miscarries upon discovering the bodies.

### The Inquest Proceedings

The inquest is conducted with procedural calm, a stark contrast to the emotional devastation. A "subdued, large, low voice" (058-158) delivers the coroner's findings. The jury views the bodies: "the inquest was held" (059-060). The verdict — murder-suicide — is a legal formality that captures nothing of the tragedy's meaning.

### Aftermath

The inquest does not assign blame, but its effects are corrosive. Sue, already unmoored by the children's deaths, interprets the tragedy as divine punishment for her relationship with Jude. This religious interpretation — "the time for the removal of herself and Jude from the scene of this disaster" (058-158) — leads her to return to Phillotson as an act of penitence. The inquest thus functions not as closure but as the mechanism that formalizes the tragedy and accelerates Sue's psychological disintegration.

## Narrative Significance

The inquest is Hardy's most concentrated use of institutional process as a foil to human suffering. The legal machinery — coroner, jury, verdict — operates with perfect efficiency while being perfectly inadequate to the reality it addresses. The "subdued, large, low voice" of the coroner is the sound of the state processing what the heart cannot.

The scene also marks the novel's structural pivot toward its darkest third act. Before the inquest, there is still a fragile possibility that Jude and Sue's love can sustain them. After it, that possibility is foreclosed. Sue's turn to religious orthodoxy, Jude's physical decline, and their eventual separation all flow from this moment.

## Key Passages

> "The coroner's inquest, a subdued, large, low voice, stated that the time for the removal of herself and Jude from the scene of this disaster had arrived." (058-158)
