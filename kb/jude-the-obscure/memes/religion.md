# Religion

Religion in *Jude the Obscure* functions as both a personal aspiration and an institutional force — one that Jude initially embraces with fervent piety, then questions under Sue's freethinking influence, and finally rejects in disillusionment. Hardy uses the religious theme to critique Victorian orthodoxy while exploring the possibility of alternative, non-institutional forms of spiritual meaning. The novel's religious trajectory — from faith through doubt to something bleaker than either — mirrors the Victorian "crisis of faith" and Hardy's own loss of religious belief.

## Definition

In the novel, "religion" operates on multiple levels: as institutional Christianity (embodied in the Anglican Church, Christminster's theological colleges, and clerical career paths), as personal faith and doubt (Jude's evolving beliefs and Sue's skeptical intelligence), and as a symbolic system that Hardy subjects to sustained critique. The term encompasses both orthodox doctrine and the characters' attempts to construct meaning outside it — including Sue's attraction to pagan art and Jude's late appeal to a humanistic "religion" of love.

## In *Jude the Obscure*

### Manifestation

Jude's relationship with religion follows a trajectory from devout aspiration to bitter disillusionment. As a boy, he associates Christminster's religious significance with its academic prestige and frames his life's ambition in religious terms:

> "You know what a university is, and a university degree? It is the necessary hallmark of a man who wants to do anything in teaching. My scheme, or dream, is to be a university graduate, and then to be ordained." (001-152)

This entanglement of scholarship and religion defines Jude's early ambitions — the Church is not merely a spiritual vocation but a career path, a form of class mobility, and an intellectual destination. Christminster represents all of these fused into a single gleaming image.

Jude's early piety is genuine but rooted in imagination rather than theological conviction. He pictures "the founders of the religious school at Christminster" (013-159) as living companions who will welcome him — not the remote historical figures they actually are. His dream of entering holy orders is, like his dream of university education, a fantasy of belonging to a world that was never designed to include him.

> "I'll be D.D. before I have done! And then he continued to dream, and thought he might become even a bishop by leading a pure, energetic, wise, Christian life... Well, on second thoughts, a bishop was absurd... Yet he thought of the bishop again." (006-086)

This oscillation between grand ambition and self-mockery captures Jude's religious imagination — sincere, naive, and ultimately doomed by the institutions it aspires to enter. His dream of becoming a bishop (006-083) is simultaneously a genuine spiritual aspiration and a class fantasy, revealing how thoroughly religion and social ambition were fused in the Victorian imagination.

### Sue Bridehead: Pagan Skepticism

Sue Bridehead introduces the novel's most explicit religious countercurrent: a deliberate, intellectual paganism that challenges Christianity at its foundations. Her purchase of pagan statuary is an act of cultural rebellion as much as personal taste. She selects from a tray of figures "among them being a Venus of standard pattern, a Diana, and, of the other sex, Apollo, Bacchus, and Mars" (016-048):

> "How much are these two?" she said, touching with her finger the Venus and the Apollo — the largest figures on the tray. (016-062)

Sue carries her Venus and Apollo home, "occasionally peeping inside the leaves to see that Venus's arm was not broken" (016-081). The image of Sue — a young woman in Victorian England — carrying pagan gods through the streets of Christminster is one of the novel's sharpest visual statements about the conflict between classical culture and Christian orthodoxy.

The novel repeatedly describes Sue's intellectual style in terms that oppose religious convention. Her mind "played like lambent lightning over conventions and formalities" (060-030). This is not simply skepticism but an active, creative intelligence that cannot be contained within doctrinal boundaries. She directly challenges the Christian valuation of celibacy and the denigration of sexual love:

> "He was as long as he nourished this unlicensed tenderness it was glaringly inconsistent for him to pursue the idea of becoming the soldier and servant of a religion in which sexual love was regarded as at its best a frailty, and at its worst damnation." (037-145)

Sue's alternative values draw on a classical, pre-Christian ethos in which love — including physical love — is not inherently sinful. But the novel is careful to show that Sue's paganism is not a solution to the problems Christianity creates. Her rebellion is intellectual and aesthetic, not institutional; it cannot protect her from the social consequences of religious transgression.

### The Crisis and Jude's Loss of Faith

Jude's loss of faith is gradual, cumulative, and finally decisive. The turning point comes when Aunt Drusilla dies, severing the last family tie that might have restrained his relationship with Sue:

> "Jude seized her hand and kissed it. 'There is a stronger one left!' he said. 'I'll never care about my doctrines or my religion any more! Let them go! Let me help you, even if I do love you, and even if you...'" (037-086)

This declaration is not a philosophical argument but an emotional renunciation. Jude abandons religion not because he has been intellectually convinced of its falsehood but because his love for Sue has made religious prohibition impossible to sustain. The novel implies that this is a more honest mode of religious change than doctrinal debate: Jude's faith dissolves under the pressure of lived experience rather than rational argument.

The novel traces how Jude and Sue's religious trajectories diverge after the children's deaths — in opposite directions that Hardy presents as equally tragic:

> "Sue and himself had mentally travelled in opposite directions since the tragedy: events which had enlarged his own views of life, laws, customs, and dogmas, had not operated in the same manner on Sue's. She was no longer the same as in the independent days, when her intellect played like lambent lightning over conventions and formalities which he at that time respected, though he did not now." (060-028)

This passage captures the novel's central religious irony: Jude moves toward Sue's former skepticism while Sue retreats into the orthodoxy Jude has abandoned. They cross paths in opposite directions, their religious trajectories forming an X that marks the death of their shared intellectual life.

### Sue's Traumatic Reconversion

Sue's return to Christianity after the children's deaths is the novel's most devastating religious event — not a genuine conversion but a psychological collapse dressed in religious language:

> "We should mortify the flesh — the terrible flesh — the curse of Adam!" (060-038)

This is not the Sue who bought pagan statues and debated theological questions with intellectual freedom. This is Sue in the grip of trauma, reaching for the religious framework she had rejected because it provides a vocabulary for self-punishment. She tells Phillotson that she is remarrying him "merely from this new sense of duty or religion" (064-092) — the "merely" betraying the hollowness of the conversion even as she insists upon it.

Jude recognizes the inauthenticity of Sue's religious turn and confronts her with a different vision of faith — one rooted not in doctrine but in human love:

> "'In that chapter we are at one, ever beloved darling, and on it we'll part friends. Its verses will stand fast when all the rest that you call religion has passed away!'" (063-073)

Jude's appeal to a religion beyond religion — a humanistic faith in love over institution — is the novel's most hopeful religious statement and its most defeated. Sue cannot hear it; she is already in the grip of the orthodoxy that will destroy her.

### Arabella's Mockery: The Final Religious Irony

The novel's final religious word is given to Arabella, whose response to Jude's death is the bitterest irony Hardy permits himself. Having remarried Jude on his deathbed, Arabella declares:

> "She said I ought to marry you again, and I have straightway. It is true religion! Ha—ha—ha!" (067-091)

The laughter that ends the sentence — "Ha—ha—ha!" — is the novel's verdict on institutional religion. The church that should sanctify marriage and comfort the dying is reduced to a joke told by a manipulative widow over her husband's corpse. Arabella, who has no religious conviction whatsoever, is the character who most successfully deploys religious forms — marriage rites, deathbed ceremonies — to her advantage. The faithful are destroyed; the faithless prosper.

### Narrative Trajectory

Religion appears early as Jude's idealized vocation (Part First), fused with academic aspiration. Jude's boyhood dream of ordination — "to be a university graduate, and then to be ordained" (001-152) — sets the terms of a religious ambition that the novel will systematically dismantle.

At Christminster (Part Second), Jude reads the Greek Testament alongside Homer and discovers that "there seemed little harmony between this pagan literature and the medieval colleges at Christminster, that ecclesiastical romance in stone" (005-164). The tension between classical learning and Christian orthodoxy that will later destroy Sue is already present in Jude's own intellectual formation.

Sue's influence challenges Jude's faith (Parts Third and Fourth). Her purchase of pagan statuary in Christminster — "How much are these two?" she said, touching with her finger the Venus and the Apollo" (016-062) — is a deliberate symbolic rebellion. Jude attends "service in the Cathedral church of Cardinal College" (015-150) to catch a glimpse of Sue, his religious observance already secondary to his romantic obsession.

In Part Fifth, religion becomes an explicit point of conflict. Jude renounces his doctrines (037-086) in the same breath that he declares his love for Sue. The irony — that he gives up religion for a love that will itself be destroyed by religious convention — is the novel's bitterest theological insight.

The crisis comes in Part Sixth when Sue undergoes her traumatic reconversion. She speaks of mortifying the flesh (060-038) and returns to Phillotson — and to the Church — out of "duty or religion" (064-092). Jude counters with his vision of a love that will endure "when all the rest that you call religion has passed away" (063-073). The novel's final religious word is Arabella's mockery of "true religion" (067-091) over Jude's body — a benediction that is also a curse upon the institution that has failed every character it was supposed to serve.

Christminster's bells ring for Remembrance Week as Jude dies, and they are described in the novel's famous closing as continuing to ring while "the body of Jude" was carried out. The bells are religious, institutional, and indifferent — they toll for the dead scholar who never was, the bishop who never was, the licentiate who never was.

## Critical Interpretation

Hardy's treatment of religion reflects the Victorian "crisis of faith" precipitated by Darwinism, Higher Criticism of the Bible, and geological discoveries that challenged literal Genesis. The novel's religious trajectory parallels Hardy's own loss of faith — raised Anglican, he lost his belief but retained a deep attachment to church architecture, ritual, and the cultural inheritance of Christianity. This double vision — the church as both beautiful and destructive — shapes the novel at every level.

The classical-pagan countercurrent that Sue introduces was especially controversial upon publication. Contemporary reviewers attacked what they called the novel's "paganism" — Sue's preference for Venus over the Virgin Mary was cited as evidence of the book's immorality. But Hardy's paganism is not a celebration of pre-Christian religion so much as a deployment of classical culture as a critical tool: the Greek and Roman texts that Jude studies are themselves evidence that Christianity is not the only framework for understanding human experience.

The novel's closing irony — Arabella's "true religion" — has been read as Hardy's most nihilistic statement. But it can also be read as his most radical: if institutional religion is a mockery, then the only authentic religion is the humanistic faith in love and companionship that Jude articulates in his final conversations with Sue. This "religion" has no church, no doctrine, and no future in the novel's world — but it is the only faith the novel refuses to mock.
