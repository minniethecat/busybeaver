[040-001] Though well-trained and even proficient masters, they occasionally used

[040-002] a dialect-word of their boyhood to each other in private.

[040-003] “I’ve come, George, to explain to you my reasons for taking a step that

[040-004] I am about to take, so that you, at least, will understand my motives

[040-005] if other people question them anywhen—as they may, indeed certainly

[040-006] will… But anything is better than the present condition of things. God

[040-007] forbid that you should ever have such an experience as mine!”

[040-008] “Sit down. You don’t mean—anything wrong between you and Mrs.

[040-009] Phillotson?”

[040-010] “I do… My wretched state is that I’ve a wife I love who not only does

[040-011] not love me, but—but— Well, I won’t say. I know her feeling! I should

[040-012] prefer hatred from her!”

[040-013] “Ssh!”

[040-014] “And the sad part of it is that she is not so much to blame as I. She

[040-015] was a pupil-teacher under me, as you know, and I took advantage of her

[040-016] inexperience, and toled her out for walks, and got her to agree to a

[040-017] long engagement before she well knew her own mind. Afterwards she saw

[040-018] somebody else, but she blindly fulfilled her engagement.”

[040-019] “Loving the other?”

[040-020] “Yes; with a curious tender solicitude seemingly; though her exact

[040-021] feeling for him is a riddle to me—and to him too, I think—possibly to

[040-022] herself. She is one of the oddest creatures I ever met. However, I have

[040-023] been struck with these two facts; the extraordinary sympathy, or

[040-024] similarity, between the pair. He is her cousin, which perhaps accounts

[040-025] for some of it. They seem to be one person split in two! And with her

[040-026] unconquerable aversion to myself as a husband, even though she may like

[040-027] me as a friend, ’tis too much to bear longer. She has conscientiously

[040-028] struggled against it, but to no purpose. I cannot bear it—I cannot! I

[040-029] can’t answer her arguments—she has read ten times as much as I. Her

[040-030] intellect sparkles like diamonds, while mine smoulders like brown

[040-031] paper… She’s one too many for me!”

[040-032] “She’ll get over it, good-now?”

[040-033] “Never! It is—but I won’t go into it—there are reasons why she never

[040-034] will. At last she calmly and firmly asked if she might leave me and go

[040-035] to him. The climax came last night, when, owing to my entering her room

[040-036] by accident, she jumped out of window—so strong was her dread of me!

[040-037] She pretended it was a dream, but that was to soothe me. Now when a

[040-038] woman jumps out of window without caring whether she breaks her neck or

[040-039] no, she’s not to be mistaken; and this being the case I have come to a

[040-040] conclusion: that it is wrong to so torture a fellow-creature any

[040-041] longer; and I won’t be the inhuman wretch to do it, cost what it may!”

[040-042] “What—you’ll let her go? And with her lover?”

[040-043] “Whom with is her matter. I shall let her go; with him certainly, if

[040-044] she wishes. I know I may be wrong—I know I can’t logically, or

[040-045] religiously, defend my concession to such a wish of hers, or harmonize

[040-046] it with the doctrines I was brought up in. Only I know one thing:

[040-047] something within me tells me I am doing wrong in refusing her. I, like

[040-048] other men, profess to hold that if a husband gets such a so-called

[040-049] preposterous request from his wife, the only course that can possibly

[040-050] be regarded as right and proper and honourable in him is to refuse it,

[040-051] and put her virtuously under lock and key, and murder her lover

[040-052] perhaps. But is that essentially right, and proper, and honourable, or

[040-053] is it contemptibly mean and selfish? I don’t profess to decide. I

[040-054] simply am going to act by instinct, and let principles take care of

[040-055] themselves. If a person who has blindly walked into a quagmire cries

[040-056] for help, I am inclined to give it, if possible.”

[040-057] “But—you see, there’s the question of neighbours and society—what will

[040-058] happen if everybody—”

[040-059] “Oh, I am not going to be a philosopher any longer! I only see what’s

[040-060] under my eyes.”

[040-061] “Well—I don’t agree with your instinct, Dick!” said Gillingham gravely.

[040-062] “I am quite amazed, to tell the truth, that such a sedate, plodding

[040-063] fellow as you should have entertained such a craze for a moment. You

[040-064] said when I called that she was puzzling and peculiar: I think you

[040-065] are!”

[040-066] “Have you ever stood before a woman whom you know to be intrinsically a

[040-067] good woman, while she has pleaded for release—been the man she has

[040-068] knelt to and implored indulgence of?”

[040-069] “I am thankful to say I haven’t.”

[040-070] “Then I don’t think you are in a position to give an opinion. I have

[040-071] been that man, and it makes all the difference in the world, if one has

[040-072] any manliness or chivalry in him. I had not the remotest idea—living

[040-073] apart from women as I have done for so many years—that merely taking a

[040-074] woman to church and putting a ring upon her finger could by any

[040-075] possibility involve one in such a daily, continuous tragedy as that now

[040-076] shared by her and me!”

[040-077] “Well, I could admit some excuse for letting her leave you, provided

[040-078] she kept to herself. But to go attended by a cavalier—that makes a

[040-079] difference.”

[040-080] “Not a bit. Suppose, as I believe, she would rather endure her present

[040-081] misery than be made to promise to keep apart from him? All that is a

[040-082] question for herself. It is not the same thing at all as the treachery

[040-083] of living on with a husband and playing him false… However, she has not

[040-084] distinctly implied living with him as wife, though I think she means

[040-085] to... And, to the best of my understanding, it is not an ignoble,

[040-086] merely animal, feeling between the two: that is the worst of it;

[040-087] because it makes me think their affection will be enduring. I did not

[040-088] mean to confess to you that in the first jealous weeks of my marriage,

[040-089] before I had come to my right mind, I hid myself in the school one

[040-090] evening when they were together there, and I heard what they said. I am

[040-091] ashamed of it now, though I suppose I was only exercising a legal

[040-092] right. I found from their manner that an extraordinary affinity, or

[040-093] sympathy, entered into their attachment, which somehow took away all

[040-094] flavour of grossness. Their supreme desire is to be together—to share

[040-095] each other’s emotions, and fancies, and dreams.”

[040-096] “Platonic!”

[040-097] “Well no. Shelleyan would be nearer to it. They remind me of—what are

[040-098] their names—Laon and Cythna. Also of Paul and Virginia a little. The

[040-099] more I reflect, the more _entirely_ I am on their side!”

[040-100] “But if people did as you want to do, there’d be a general domestic

[040-101] disintegration. The family would no longer be the social unit.”

[040-102] “Yes—I am all abroad, I suppose!” said Phillotson sadly. “I was never a

[040-103] very bright reasoner, you remember. … And yet, I don’t see why the

[040-104] woman and the children should not be the unit without the man.”

[040-105] “By the Lord Harry!—Matriarchy! … Does _she_ say all this too?”

[040-106] “Oh no. She little thinks I have out-Sued Sue in this—all in the last

[040-107] twelve hours!”

[040-108] “It will upset all received opinion hereabout. Good God—what will

[040-109] Shaston say!”

[040-110] “I don’t say that it won’t. I don’t know—I don’t know! … As I say, I am

[040-111] only a feeler, not a reasoner.”

[040-112] “Now,” said Gillingham, “let us take it quietly, and have something to

[040-113] drink over it.” He went under the stairs, and produced a bottle of

[040-114] cider-wine, of which they drank a rummer each. “I think you are rafted,

[040-115] and not yourself,” he continued. “Do go back and make up your mind to

[040-116] put up with a few whims. But keep her. I hear on all sides that she’s a

[040-117] charming young thing.”

[040-118] “Ah yes! That’s the bitterness of it! Well, I won’t stay. I have a long

[040-119] walk before me.”

[040-120] Gillingham accompanied his friend a mile on his way, and at parting

[040-121] expressed his hope that this consultation, singular as its subject was,

[040-122] would be the renewal of their old comradeship. “Stick to her!” were his

[040-123] last words, flung into the darkness after Phillotson; from which his

[040-124] friend answered “Aye, aye!”

[040-125] But when Phillotson was alone under the clouds of night, and no sound

[040-126] was audible but that of the purling tributaries of the Stour, he said,

[040-127] “So Gillingham, my friend, you had no stronger arguments against it

[040-128] than those!”

[040-129] “I think she ought to be smacked, and brought to her senses—that’s what

[040-130] I think!” murmured Gillingham, as he walked back alone.

[040-131] The next morning came, and at breakfast Phillotson told Sue:

[040-132] “You may go—with whom you will. I absolutely and unconditionally

[040-133] agree.”

[040-134] Having once come to this conclusion it seemed to Phillotson more and

[040-135] more indubitably the true one. His mild serenity at the sense that he

[040-136] was doing his duty by a woman who was at his mercy almost overpowered

[040-137] his grief at relinquishing her.

[040-138] Some days passed, and the evening of their last meal together had

[040-139] come—a cloudy evening with wind—which indeed was very seldom absent in

[040-140] this elevated place. How permanently it was imprinted upon his vision;

[040-141] that look of her as she glided into the parlour to tea; a slim flexible

[040-142] figure; a face, strained from its roundness, and marked by the pallors

[040-143] of restless days and nights, suggesting tragic possibilities quite at

[040-144] variance with her times of buoyancy; a trying of this morsel and that,

[040-145] and an inability to eat either. Her nervous manner, begotten of a fear

[040-146] lest he should be injured by her course, might have been interpreted by

[040-147] a stranger as displeasure that Phillotson intruded his presence on her

[040-148] for the few brief minutes that remained.

[040-149] “You had better have a slice of ham or an egg, or something with your

[040-150] tea? You can’t travel on a mouthful of bread and butter.”

[040-151] She took the slice he helped her to; and they discussed as they sat

[040-152] trivial questions of housekeeping, such as where he would find the key

[040-153] of this or that cupboard, what little bills were paid, and what not.

[040-154] “I am a bachelor by nature, as you know, Sue,” he said, in a heroic

[040-155] attempt to put her at her ease. “So that being without a wife will not

[040-156] really be irksome to me, as it might be to other men who have had one a

[040-157] little while. I have, too, this grand hobby in my head of writing ‘The

[040-158] Roman Antiquities of Wessex,’ which will occupy all my spare hours.”

[040-159] “If you will send me some of the manuscript to copy at any time, as you

[040-160] used to, I will do it with so much pleasure!” she said with amenable

[040-161] gentleness. “I should much like to be some help to you still—as

[040-162] a—f-f-friend.”

[040-163] Phillotson mused, and said: “No, I think we ought to be really

[040-164] separate, if we are to be at all. And for this reason, that I don’t

[040-165] wish to ask you any questions, and particularly wish you not to give me

[040-166] information as to your movements, or even your address… Now, what money

[040-167] do you want? You must have some, you know.”

[040-168] “Oh, of course, Richard, I couldn’t think of having any of your money

[040-169] to go away from you with! I don’t want any either. I have enough of my

[040-170] own to last me for a long while, and Jude will let me have—”

[040-171] “I would rather not know anything about him, if you don’t mind. You are

[040-172] free, absolutely; and your course is your own.”

[040-173] “Very well. But I’ll just say that I have packed only a change or two

[040-174] of my own personal clothing, and one or two little things besides that

[040-175] are my very own. I wish you would look into my trunk before it is

[040-176] closed. Besides that I have only a small parcel that will go into

[040-177] Jude’s portmanteau.”
