# Little Father Time

Little Father Time is the son of [[Jude-Fawley]] and [[Arabella-Donn]], conceived in [[jude-fawley|Jude]]'s first, brief marriage and raised in Australia by [[arabella-donn|Arabella]]'s parents. A child of unnerving gravity and premature world-weariness, he is nicknamed "Little Father Time" for his aged, sorrowful demeanor (048-149). His arrival in England — and his subsequent suicide-murder of [[jude-fawley|Jude]] and [[Sue-Bridehead]]'s children — marks the irreversible turning point that sends the novel plunging into its final tragedy.

## Character Overview

Little Father Time is one of the most unsettling characters in Victorian fiction: a child who seems never to have been young. When he first appears, descending from a train at Aldbrickham station, he is described as "a small, pale child's face" staring out from the gloom of a third-class carriage (048-008). His fellow passengers laugh at the kitten's antics, but the solitary boy regards the animal with "saucer eyes" and seems "mutely to say: 'All laughing comes from misapprehension. Rightly looked at, there is no laughable thing under the sun'" (048-019, 048-021). The boy would answer "'Yes,' without animation, would try to smile, and fail" (048-025).

> He was Age masquerading as Juvenility, and doing it so badly that his real self showed through crevices. A ground-swell from ancient years of night seemed now and then to lift the child in this his morning-life, when his face took a back view over some great Atlantic of Time, and appeared not to care about what it saw. (048-027, 048-028, 048-029, 048-030)

Hardy's narrator elevates the boy to near-mythical stature. When all other travelers — "even the kitten curling itself up in the basket, weary of its too circumscribed play" — fall asleep, the boy remains awake:

> He then seemed to be doubly awake, like an enslaved and dwarfed divinity, sitting passive and regarding his companions as if he saw their whole rounded lives rather than their immediate figures. (048-034, 048-030, 048-035)

The image of an "enslaved and dwarfed divinity" is one of the novel's most arresting conceits: a child who is in some sense a god — all-knowing, all-sorrowing — but trapped in diminutive form, powerless to alter the universe he comprehends. [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] herself describes his face as "the tragic mask of Melpomene" (048-147), invoking the Muse of Tragedy to describe a child's features. When asked whether he has been christened, the boy replies with chilling logic: "Because, if I died in damnation, 'twould save the expense of a Christian funeral" (048-156, 048-157). He has never heard of his father's name: "Never heerd on it" (048-160).

His personality is described as "mysterious" by those who encounter him, "a subject which" fascinates the Christminster community (053-106). [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] notes that "these preternaturally old boys almost always come from new countries" (048-152), linking his condition to a kind of colonial anomie — born in the antipodes of the British world, he seems to belong to no place at all.

## Role in the Narrative

Little Father Time appears late in the novel — Part 5 — but catalyzes its most dramatic event. [[arabella-donn|Arabella]], "with her usual carelessness," had postponed telling [[jude-fawley|Jude]] about the boy's existence until his arrival was imminent, having "looked him over with an" air of dismissive appraisal at the Three Horns inn before dispatching him onward (048-038, 048-048, 048-051). She sends him to [[jude-fawley|Jude]] by train, washing her hands of parental responsibility.

The boy is accepted into [[jude-fawley|Jude]] and [[sue-bridehead|Sue]]'s household, where [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] is "usually leading little Father Time by the hand" (049-007). She calls him "Juey" (054-099) and attempts to treat him kindly, but the boy remains spectral and unreachable. The narrator observes that "as he never spoke, they were hardly" conscious of him as a full presence (049-024). Even so, [[jude-fawley|Jude]] resolves to "educate and train him with a view to the university" (048-111) — inheriting his own impossible dream for the next generation. [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] calls him a "dreamer" (048-114).

### First Appearance

Little Father Time is first seen arriving at Aldbrickham station on the evening train, a solitary child carrying a "key-basket" — a key "suspended round his neck by a piece of common string" and a half-ticket stuck in his hat-band (048-010, 048-011, 048-013). [[jude-fawley|Jude]] meets him at the station and brings him home, where [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] scrutinizes his features and suddenly recognizes [[jude-fawley|Jude]] in him: "What Arabella says is true — true! I see you in him!" (048-105). The boy's arrival disturbs [[jude-fawley|Jude]] deeply, for "the advent of the child disturbed him" (048-164) — it is the return of his first marriage, the past he had hoped to bury.

### Key Moments

1. **Arrival in England** — [[arabella-donn|Arabella]] sends the boy to [[jude-fawley|Jude]] without warning. He arrives alone at Aldbrickham station — a pale, silent child bearing "a small, pale child's face" and carrying a key on a string, the mark of his premature self-sufficiency (048-007, 048-008, 048-010).

2. **Integration into the Household** — The boy lives quietly in [[jude-fawley|Jude]] and [[sue-bridehead|Sue]]'s home, a spectral presence. [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] is "usually leading little Father Time by the hand" (049-007), yet she never truly connects with him. His "quaint and weird face set, and his eyes resting on things they did not see in the substantial world" (048-144, 048-145, 048-146).

3. **The Christminster Proclamation Scene** — In Part 6, during Remembrance Day, Father Time watches the academic procession through [[Christminster]] and shudders at the spectacle: "It do seem like the Judgment Day!" (056-093). The scholarly pageantry that inspired [[jude-fawley|Jude]] strikes the boy as apocalyptic — a reversal that underlines how far the novel has journeyed from its hopeful beginnings.

4. **Rejection from Lodgings** — When [[jude-fawley|Jude]] and [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] are turned away from lodgings because of their children, the boy sighs "I don't like Christminster!" (057-091). The repeated rejection — another landlady saying "I am sorry to say we don't let where there are children" (057-088) — drives home the boy's conviction that his very existence is a burden.

5. **"I Ought Not to Be Born"** — In the most fateful exchange in the novel, the boy asks [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] a question that seals all their fates: "I ought not to be born, ought I?" (057-161). The question is not a child's bid for reassurance but a metaphysical inquiry, and [[sue-bridehead|Sue]]'s inability to offer a full-throated denial confirms his worst fears.

6. **The Dialogue That Precipitates Tragedy** — In the rented room on the morning of the murder-suicide, the boy engages [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] in a devastating Socratic exchange:

> "It would be better to be out o' the world than in it, wouldn't it?"
> "It would almost, dear."
> "'Tis because of us children, too, isn't it, that you can't get a good lodging?"
> "Well — people do object to children sometimes."
> "Then if children make so much trouble, why do people have 'em?"
> "Oh — because it is a law of nature."
> "But we don't ask to be born?"
> "No indeed."
> "I think that whenever children be born that are not wanted they should be killed directly, before their souls come to 'em, and not allowed to grow big and walk about!" (058-010, 058-011, 058-012, 058-014, 058-015, 058-016, 058-017, 058-018, 058-024, 058-025, 058-026)

[[sue-bridehead|Sue]], "doubtfully pondering how to treat this too reflective child" (058-027, 058-028), cannot find the words to contradict him. He speaks "like an aged friend" (058-031), and her honesty — her philosophical commitment to truth-telling — becomes the instrument of destruction.

7. **The Murder-Suicide** — On waking to find [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] absent, the boy is "thrown into a fit of aggravated despondency" (058-118) and hangs himself and the two younger children from garment hooks with box-cord. The note left behind — "_Done because we are too menny_" (058-123) — has become one of the most quoted and devastating sentences in Victorian fiction. Its misspelling underscores the child's incomplete education and the pathos of his attempt to articulate an adult despair with a child's limited tools.

8. **The Doctor's Verdict** — The attending physician offers a chilling diagnosis that places the boy's act in a broader social context: "there are such boys springing up amongst us — boys of a sort unknown in the last generation — the outcome of new views of life. They seem to see all its terrors before they are old enough to have staying power to resist them. He says it is the beginning of the coming universal wish not to live" (058-140, 058-141, 058-142, 058-143, 058-144). The boy is not an aberration but a harbinger — the first of a new human type shaped by the erosion of religious consolation.

## Character Analysis

### A Symbol of Hereditary Despair

Little Father Time is Hardy's most audacious symbolic creation: a child who functions less as a realistic character than as an embodiment of what the novel calls "the coming universal wish not to live" (058-143). He represents the accumulated weight of ancestral misery — the sins of the fathers visited upon the children — and his tragedy is that he understands this inheritance with a clarity that no child should possess. The "ground-swell from ancient years of night" (048-028) that seems to lift him is the tide of inherited suffering, the biological transmission of despair across generations.

### The Failure of Adult Honesty

The boy's tragedy is also [[sue-bridehead|Sue]]'s tragedy. Her intellectual honesty — her refusal to offer false comfort, her determination to "be honest and candid with one who entered into her difficulties like an aged friend" (058-030, 058-031) — becomes the instrument of destruction. [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] treats the boy as a fellow adult, a philosophical interlocutor, and in doing so she fails to give him the one thing a child most needs: the reassuring lie that the world is safe and that he is wanted. The novel suggests that certain truths — the truth that children are a burden, that life is suffering, that existence is not inherently justified — are too terrible for children to bear, yet the boy already knows them. He does not need to be told; he needs to be saved from what he already knows, and [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] cannot save him.

### The Christminster Reversal

The boy's shudder at the Christminster procession — "It do seem like the Judgment Day!" (056-093) — is a crucial moment of structural irony. [[jude-fawley|Jude]] came to [[christminster|Christminster]] as a young man filled with hope, staring up at the college spires as "a city of light." Little Father Time sees the same city as a landscape of judgment, a place that rejects him and his kind. The academic pageantry that once inspired [[jude-fawley|Jude]] now strikes his son as a vision of apocalypse. This reversal marks the novel's transformation from a story of aspiration into a story of annihilation.

### The "Enslaved and Dwarfed Divinity"

Hardy's comparison of the boy to "an enslaved and dwarfed divinity" (048-035) is the interpretive key to the character. He is not merely a sad child; he is a god trapped in miniature — omniscient but powerless, seeing "whole rounded lives rather than their immediate figures" (048-030) but unable to do anything with that knowledge except destroy. The image fuses Greek tragedy (the chorus who sees all but cannot intervene) with Darwinian pessimism (the human reduced to a biological organism, driven by forces it comprehends but cannot control).

### Narrative Function

Little Father Time is the mechanism by which the novel's thematic concerns — [[marriage]], [[education]], religion, class, the suffering of children — are converted into irreversible action. Before his arrival, [[jude-fawley|Jude]] and [[sue-bridehead|Sue]]'s struggles are reversible in principle: they could marry, they could find work, they could make peace with society. After his act, nothing can be undone. The boy functions as the novel's tragic hinge, the point where possibility collapses into fate. [[sue-bridehead|Sue]]'s subsequent religious conversion and return to [[Richard-Phillotson]], and [[jude-fawley|Jude]]'s decline and death, are both set in motion by this single, unbearable act.
