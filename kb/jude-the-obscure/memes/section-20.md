[020-001] During the next week or two he accordingly placed himself in such

[020-002] positions about the city as would afford him glimpses of several of the

[020-003] most distinguished among the provosts, wardens, and other heads of

[020-004] houses; and from those he ultimately selected five whose physiognomies

[020-005] seemed to say to him that they were appreciative and far-seeing men. To

[020-006] these five he addressed letters, briefly stating his difficulties, and

[020-007] asking their opinion on his stranded situation.

[020-008] When the letters were posted Jude mentally began to criticize them; he

[020-009] wished they had not been sent. “It is just one of those intrusive,

[020-010] vulgar, pushing, applications which are so common in these days,” he

[020-011] thought. “Why couldn’t I know better than address utter strangers in

[020-012] such a way? I may be an impostor, an idle scamp, a man with a bad

[020-013] character, for all that they know to the contrary… Perhaps that’s what

[020-014] I am!”

[020-015] Nevertheless, he found himself clinging to the hope of some reply as to

[020-016] his one last chance of redemption. He waited day after day, saying that

[020-017] it was perfectly absurd to expect, yet expecting. While he waited he

[020-018] was suddenly stirred by news about Phillotson. Phillotson was giving up

[020-019] the school near Christminster, for a larger one further south, in

[020-020] Mid-Wessex. What this meant; how it would affect his cousin; whether,

[020-021] as seemed possible, it was a practical move of the schoolmaster’s

[020-022] towards a larger income, in view of a provision for two instead of one,

[020-023] he would not allow himself to say. And the tender relations between

[020-024] Phillotson and the young girl of whom Jude was passionately enamoured

[020-025] effectually made it repugnant to Jude’s tastes to apply to Phillotson

[020-026] for advice on his own scheme.

[020-027] Meanwhile the academic dignitaries to whom Jude had written vouchsafed

[020-028] no answer, and the young man was thus thrown back entirely on himself,

[020-029] as formerly, with the added gloom of a weakened hope. By indirect

[020-030] inquiries he soon perceived clearly what he had long uneasily

[020-031] suspected, that to qualify himself for certain open scholarships and

[020-032] exhibitions was the only brilliant course. But to do this a good deal

[020-033] of coaching would be necessary, and much natural ability. It was next

[020-034] to impossible that a man reading on his own system, however widely and

[020-035] thoroughly, even over the prolonged period of ten years, should be able

[020-036] to compete with those who had passed their lives under trained teachers

[020-037] and had worked to ordained lines.

[020-038] The other course, that of buying himself in, so to speak, seemed the

[020-039] only one really open to men like him, the difficulty being simply of a

[020-040] material kind. With the help of his information he began to reckon the

[020-041] extent of this material obstacle, and ascertained, to his dismay, that,

[020-042] at the rate at which, with the best of fortune, he would be able to

[020-043] save money, fifteen years must elapse before he could be in a position

[020-044] to forward testimonials to the head of a college and advance to a

[020-045] matriculation examination. The undertaking was hopeless.

[020-046] He saw what a curious and cunning glamour the neighbourhood of the

[020-047] place had exercised over him. To get there and live there, to move

[020-048] among the churches and halls and become imbued with the _genius loci_,

[020-049] had seemed to his dreaming youth, as the spot shaped its charms to him

[020-050] from its halo on the horizon, the obvious and ideal thing to do. “Let

[020-051] me only get there,” he had said with the fatuousness of Crusoe over his

[020-052] big boat, “and the rest is but a matter of time and energy.” It would

[020-053] have been far better for him in every way if he had never come within

[020-054] sight and sound of the delusive precincts, had gone to some busy

[020-055] commercial town with the sole object of making money by his wits, and

[020-056] thence surveyed his plan in true perspective. Well, all that was clear

[020-057] to him amounted to this, that the whole scheme had burst up, like an

[020-058] iridescent soap-bubble, under the touch of a reasoned inquiry. He

[020-059] looked back at himself along the vista of his past years, and his

[020-060] thought was akin to Heine’s:

[020-061] Above the youth’s inspired and flashing eyes

[020-062] I see the motley mocking fool’s-cap rise!

[020-063] Fortunately he had not been allowed to bring his disappointment into

[020-064] his dear Sue’s life by involving her in this collapse. And the painful

[020-065] details of his awakening to a sense of his limitations should now be

[020-066] spared her as far as possible. After all, she had only known a little

[020-067] part of the miserable struggle in which he had been engaged thus

[020-068] unequipped, poor, and unforeseeing.

[020-069] He always remembered the appearance of the afternoon on which he awoke

[020-070] from his dream. Not quite knowing what to do with himself, he went up

[020-071] to an octagonal chamber in the lantern of a singularly built theatre

[020-072] that was set amidst this quaint and singular city. It had windows all

[020-073] round, from which an outlook over the whole town and its edifices could

[020-074] be gained. Jude’s eyes swept all the views in succession, meditatively,

[020-075] mournfully, yet sturdily. Those buildings and their associations and

[020-076] privileges were not for him. From the looming roof of the great

[020-077] library, into which he hardly ever had time to enter, his gaze

[020-078] travelled on to the varied spires, halls, gables, streets, chapels,

[020-079] gardens, quadrangles, which composed the ensemble of this unrivalled

[020-080] panorama. He saw that his destiny lay not with these, but among the

[020-081] manual toilers in the shabby purlieu which he himself occupied,

[020-082] unrecognized as part of the city at all by its visitors and

[020-083] panegyrists, yet without whose denizens the hard readers could not read

[020-084] nor the high thinkers live.

[020-085] He looked over the town into the country beyond, to the trees which

[020-086] screened her whose presence had at first been the support of his heart,

[020-087] and whose loss was now a maddening torture. But for this blow he might

[020-088] have borne with his fate. With Sue as companion he could have renounced

[020-089] his ambitions with a smile. Without her it was inevitable that the

[020-090] reaction from the long strain to which he had subjected himself should

[020-091] affect him disastrously. Phillotson had no doubt passed through a

[020-092] similar intellectual disappointment to that which now enveloped him.

[020-093] But the schoolmaster had been since blest with the consolation of sweet

[020-094] Sue, while for him there was no consoler.

[020-095] Descending to the streets, he went listlessly along till he arrived at

[020-096] an inn, and entered it. Here he drank several glasses of beer in rapid

[020-097] succession, and when he came out it was night. By the light of the

[020-098] flickering lamps he rambled home to supper, and had not long been

[020-099] sitting at table when his landlady brought up a letter that had just

[020-100] arrived for him. She laid it down as if impressed with a sense of its

[020-101] possible importance, and on looking at it Jude perceived that it bore

[020-102] the embossed stamp of one of the colleges whose heads he had addressed.

[020-103] “_One_—at last!” cried Jude.

[020-104] The communication was brief, and not exactly what he had expected;

[020-105] though it really was from the master in person. It ran thus:

[020-106] BIBLIOLL COLLEGE.

[020-107] SIR,—I have read your letter with interest; and, judging from your

[020-108] description of yourself as a working-man, I venture to think that you

[020-109] will have a much better chance of success in life by remaining in your

[020-110] own sphere and sticking to your trade than by adopting any other

[020-111] course. That, therefore, is what I advise you to do. Yours faithfully,

[020-112] T. TETUPHENAY.

[020-113] To Mr. J. FAWLEY, Stone-mason.

[020-114] This terribly sensible advice exasperated Jude. He had known all that

[020-115] before. He knew it was true. Yet it seemed a hard slap after ten years

[020-116] of labour, and its effect upon him just now was to make him rise

[020-117] recklessly from the table, and, instead of reading as usual, to go

[020-118] downstairs and into the street. He stood at a bar and tossed off two or

[020-119] three glasses, then unconsciously sauntered along till he came to a

[020-120] spot called The Fourways in the middle of the city, gazing abstractedly

[020-121] at the groups of people like one in a trance, till, coming to himself,

[020-122] he began talking to the policeman fixed there.

[020-123] That officer yawned, stretched out his elbows, elevated himself an inch

[020-124] and a half on the balls of his toes, smiled, and looking humorously at

[020-125] Jude, said, “You’ve had a wet, young man.”

[020-126] “No; I’ve only begun,” he replied cynically.

[020-127] Whatever his wetness, his brains were dry enough. He only heard in part

[020-128] the policeman’s further remarks, having fallen into thought on what

[020-129] struggling people like himself had stood at that crossway, whom nobody

[020-130] ever thought of now. It had more history than the oldest college in the

[020-131] city. It was literally teeming, stratified, with the shades of human

[020-132] groups, who had met there for tragedy, comedy, farce; real enactments

[020-133] of the intensest kind. At Fourways men had stood and talked of

[020-134] Napoleon, the loss of America, the execution of King Charles, the

[020-135] burning of the Martyrs, the Crusades, the Norman Conquest, possibly of

[020-136] the arrival of Caesar. Here the two sexes had met for loving, hating,

[020-137] coupling, parting; had waited, had suffered, for each other; had

[020-138] triumphed over each other; cursed each other in jealousy, blessed each

[020-139] other in forgiveness.

[020-140] He began to see that the town life was a book of humanity infinitely

[020-141] more palpitating, varied, and compendious than the gown life. These

[020-142] struggling men and women before him were the reality of Christminster,

[020-143] though they knew little of Christ or Minster. That was one of the

[020-144] humours of things. The floating population of students and teachers,

[020-145] who did know both in a way, were not Christminster in a local sense at

[020-146] all.

[020-147] He looked at his watch, and, in pursuit of this idea, he went on till

[020-148] he came to a public hall, where a promenade concert was in progress.

[020-149] Jude entered, and found the room full of shop youths and girls,

[020-150] soldiers, apprentices, boys of eleven smoking cigarettes, and light

[020-151] women of the more respectable and amateur class. He had tapped the real

[020-152] Christminster life. A band was playing, and the crowd walked about and

[020-153] jostled each other, and every now and then a man got upon a platform

[020-154] and sang a comic song.

[020-155] The spirit of Sue seemed to hover round him and prevent his flirting

[020-156] and drinking with the frolicsome girls who made advances—wistful to

[020-157] gain a little joy. At ten o’clock he came away, choosing a circuitous

[020-158] route homeward to pass the gates of the college whose head had just

[020-159] sent him the note.

[020-160] The gates were shut, and, by an impulse, he took from his pocket the

[020-161] lump of chalk which as a workman he usually carried there, and wrote

[020-162] along the wall:

[020-163] “_I have understanding as well as you; I am not inferior to you: yea,

[020-164] who knoweth not such things as these?_”—Job xii. 3.

[020-165] VII

[020-166] The stroke of scorn relieved his mind, and the next morning he laughed

[020-167] at his self-conceit. But the laugh was not a healthy one. He re-read

[020-168] the letter from the master, and the wisdom in its lines, which had at

[020-169] first exasperated him, chilled and depressed him now. He saw himself as

[020-170] a fool indeed.

[020-171] Deprived of the objects of both intellect and emotion, he could not

[020-172] proceed to his work. Whenever he felt reconciled to his fate as a

[020-173] student, there came to disturb his calm his hopeless relations with

[020-174] Sue. That the one affined soul he had ever met was lost to him through

[020-175] his marriage returned upon him with cruel persistency, till, unable to

[020-176] bear it longer, he again rushed for distraction to the real

[020-177] Christminster life. He now sought it out in an obscure and low-ceiled

[020-178] tavern up a court which was well known to certain worthies of the

[020-179] place, and in brighter times would have interested him simply by its

[020-180] quaintness. Here he sat more or less all the day, convinced that he was

[020-181] at bottom a vicious character, of whom it was hopeless to expect

[020-182] anything.
