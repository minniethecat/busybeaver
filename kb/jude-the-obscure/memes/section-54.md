[054-001] “Where do we go to?” asked Time, in suspense.

[054-002] “We must sail under sealed orders, that nobody may trace us… We mustn’t

[054-003] go to Alfredston, or to Melchester, or to Shaston, or to Christminster.

[054-004] Apart from those we may go anywhere.”

[054-005] “Why mustn’t we go there, Father?”

[054-006] “Because of a cloud that has gathered over us; though ‘we have wronged

[054-007] no man, corrupted no man, defrauded no man!’ Though perhaps we have

[054-008] ‘done that which was right in our own eyes.’”

[054-009] VII

[054-010] From that week Jude Fawley and Sue walked no more in the town of

[054-011] Aldbrickham.

[054-012] Whither they had gone nobody knew, chiefly because nobody cared to

[054-013] know. Any one sufficiently curious to trace the steps of such an

[054-014] obscure pair might have discovered without great trouble that they had

[054-015] taken advantage of his adaptive craftsmanship to enter on a shifting,

[054-016] almost nomadic, life, which was not without its pleasantness for a

[054-017] time.

[054-018] Wherever Jude heard of free-stone work to be done, thither he went,

[054-019] choosing by preference places remote from his old haunts and Sue’s. He

[054-020] laboured at a job, long or briefly, till it was finished; and then

[054-021] moved on.

[054-022] Two whole years and a half passed thus. Sometimes he might have been

[054-023] found shaping the mullions of a country mansion, sometimes setting the

[054-024] parapet of a town-hall, sometimes ashlaring an hotel at Sandbourne,

[054-025] sometimes a museum at Casterbridge, sometimes as far down as Exonbury,

[054-026] sometimes at Stoke-Barehills. Later still he was at Kennetbridge, a

[054-027] thriving town not more than a dozen miles south of Marygreen, this

[054-028] being his nearest approach to the village where he was known; for he

[054-029] had a sensitive dread of being questioned as to his life and fortunes

[054-030] by those who had been acquainted with him during his ardent young

[054-031] manhood of study and promise, and his brief and unhappy married life at

[054-032] that time.

[054-033] At some of these places he would be detained for months, at others only

[054-034] a few weeks. His curious and sudden antipathy to ecclesiastical work,

[054-035] both episcopal and noncomformist, which had risen in him when suffering

[054-036] under a smarting sense of misconception, remained with him in cold

[054-037] blood, less from any fear of renewed censure than from an

[054-038] ultra-conscientiousness which would not allow him to seek a living out

[054-039] of those who would disapprove of his ways; also, too, from a sense of

[054-040] inconsistency between his former dogmas and his present practice,

[054-041] hardly a shred of the beliefs with which he had first gone up to

[054-042] Christminster now remaining with him. He was mentally approaching the

[054-043] position which Sue had occupied when he first met her.

[054-044] On a Saturday evening in May, nearly three years after Arabella’s

[054-045] recognition of Sue and himself at the agricultural show, some of those

[054-046] who there encountered each other met again.

[054-047] It was the spring fair at Kennetbridge, and, though this ancient

[054-048] trade-meeting had much dwindled from its dimensions of former times,

[054-049] the long straight street of the borough presented a lively scene about

[054-050] midday. At this hour a light trap, among other vehicles, was driven

[054-051] into the town by the north road, and up to the door of a temperance

[054-052] inn. There alighted two women, one the driver, an ordinary country

[054-053] person, the other a finely built figure in the deep mourning of a

[054-054] widow. Her sombre suit, of pronounced cut, caused her to appear a

[054-055] little out of place in the medley and bustle of a provincial fair.

[054-056] “I will just find out where it is, Anny,” said the widow-lady to her

[054-057] companion, when the horse and cart had been taken by a man who came

[054-058] forward: “and then I’ll come back, and meet you here; and we’ll go in

[054-059] and have something to eat and drink. I begin to feel quite a sinking.”

[054-060] “With all my heart,” said the other. “Though I would sooner have put up

[054-061] at the Chequers or The Jack. You can’t get much at these temperance

[054-062] houses.”

[054-063] “Now, don’t you give way to gluttonous desires, my child,” said the

[054-064] woman in weeds reprovingly. “This is the proper place. Very well: we’ll

[054-065] meet in half an hour, unless you come with me to find out where the

[054-066] site of the new chapel is?”

[054-067] “I don’t care to. You can tell me.”

[054-068] The companions then went their several ways, the one in crape walking

[054-069] firmly along with a mien of disconnection from her miscellaneous

[054-070] surroundings. Making inquiries she came to a hoarding, within which

[054-071] were excavations denoting the foundations of a building; and on the

[054-072] boards without one or two large posters announcing that the

[054-073] foundation-stone of the chapel about to be erected would be laid that

[054-074] afternoon at three o’clock by a London preacher of great popularity

[054-075] among his body.

[054-076] Having ascertained thus much the immensely weeded widow retraced her

[054-077] steps, and gave herself leisure to observe the movements of the fair.

[054-078] By and by her attention was arrested by a little stall of cakes and

[054-079] ginger-breads, standing between the more pretentious erections of

[054-080] trestles and canvas. It was covered with an immaculate cloth, and

[054-081] tended by a young woman apparently unused to the business, she being

[054-082] accompanied by a boy with an octogenarian face, who assisted her.

[054-083] “Upon my—senses!” murmured the widow to herself. “His wife Sue—if she

[054-084] is so!” She drew nearer to the stall. “How do you do, Mrs. Fawley?” she

[054-085] said blandly.

[054-086] Sue changed colour and recognized Arabella through the crape veil.

[054-087] “How are you, Mrs. Cartlett?” she said stiffly. And then perceiving

[054-088] Arabella’s garb her voice grew sympathetic in spite of herself.

[054-089] “What?—you have lost—”

[054-090] “My poor husband. Yes. He died suddenly, six weeks ago, leaving me none

[054-091] too well off, though he was a kind husband to me. But whatever profit

[054-092] there is in public-house keeping goes to them that brew the liquors,

[054-093] and not to them that retail ’em… And you, my little old man! You don’t

[054-094] know me, I expect?”

[054-095] “Yes, I do. You be the woman I thought wer my mother for a bit, till I

[054-096] found you wasn’t,” replied Father Time, who had learned to use the

[054-097] Wessex tongue quite naturally by now.

[054-098] “All right. Never mind. I am a friend.”

[054-099] “Juey,” said Sue suddenly, “go down to the station platform with this

[054-100] tray—there’s another train coming in, I think.”

[054-101] When he was gone Arabella continued: “He’ll never be a beauty, will he,

[054-102] poor chap! Does he know I am his mother really?”

[054-103] “No. He thinks there is some mystery about his parentage—that’s all.

[054-104] Jude is going to tell him when he is a little older.”

[054-105] “But how do you come to be doing this? I am surprised.”

[054-106] “It is only a temporary occupation—a fancy of ours while we are in a

[054-107] difficulty.”

[054-108] “Then you are living with him still?”

[054-109] “Yes.”

[054-110] “Married?”

[054-111] “Of course.”

[054-112] “Any children?”

[054-113] “Two.”

[054-114] “And another coming soon, I see.”

[054-115] Sue writhed under the hard and direct questioning, and her tender

[054-116] little mouth began to quiver.

[054-117] “Lord—I mean goodness gracious—what is there to cry about? Some folks

[054-118] would be proud enough!”

[054-119] “It is not that I am ashamed—not as you think! But it seems such a

[054-120] terribly tragic thing to bring beings into the world—so

[054-121] presumptuous—that I question my right to do it sometimes!”

[054-122] “Take it easy, my dear… But you don’t tell me why you do such a thing

[054-123] as this? Jude used to be a proud sort of chap—above any business

[054-124] almost, leave alone keeping a standing.”

[054-125] “Perhaps my husband has altered a little since then. I am sure he is

[054-126] not proud now!” And Sue’s lips quivered again. “I am doing this because

[054-127] he caught a chill early in the year while putting up some stonework of

[054-128] a music-hall, at Quartershot, which he had to do in the rain, the work

[054-129] having to be executed by a fixed day. He is better than he was; but it

[054-130] has been a long, weary time! We have had an old widow friend with us to

[054-131] help us through it; but she’s leaving soon.”

[054-132] “Well, I am respectable too, thank God, and of a serious way of

[054-133] thinking since my loss. Why did you choose to sell gingerbreads?”

[054-134] “That’s a pure accident. He was brought up to the baking business, and

[054-135] it occurred to him to try his hand at these, which he can make without

[054-136] coming out of doors. We call them Christminster cakes. They are a great

[054-137] success.”

[054-138] “I never saw any like ’em. Why, they are windows and towers, and

[054-139] pinnacles! And upon my word they are very nice.” She had helped

[054-140] herself, and was unceremoniously munching one of the cakes.

[054-141] “Yes. They are reminiscences of the Christminster Colleges. Traceried

[054-142] windows, and cloisters, you see. It was a whim of his to do them in

[054-143] pastry.”

[054-144] “Still harping on Christminster—even in his cakes!” laughed Arabella.

[054-145] “Just like Jude. A ruling passion. What a queer fellow he is, and

[054-146] always will be!”

[054-147] Sue sighed, and she looked her distress at hearing him criticized.

[054-148] “Don’t you think he is? Come now; you do, though you are so fond of

[054-149] him!”

[054-150] “Of course Christminster is a sort of fixed vision with him, which I

[054-151] suppose he’ll never be cured of believing in. He still thinks it a

[054-152] great centre of high and fearless thought, instead of what it is, a

[054-153] nest of commonplace schoolmasters whose characteristic is timid

[054-154] obsequiousness to tradition.”

[054-155] Arabella was quizzing Sue with more regard of how she was speaking than

[054-156] of what she was saying. “How odd to hear a woman selling cakes talk

[054-157] like that!” she said. “Why don’t you go back to school-keeping?”

[054-158] She shook her head. “They won’t have me.”

[054-159] “Because of the divorce, I suppose?”

[054-160] “That and other things. And there is no reason to wish it. We gave up

[054-161] all ambition, and were never so happy in our lives till his illness

[054-162] came.”

[054-163] “Where are you living?”

[054-164] “I don’t care to say.”

[054-165] “Here in Kennetbridge?”

[054-166] Sue’s manner showed Arabella that her random guess was right.

[054-167] “Here comes the boy back again,” continued Arabella. “My boy and

[054-168] Jude’s!”

[054-169] Sue’s eyes darted a spark. “You needn’t throw that in my face!” she

[054-170] cried.

[054-171] “Very well—though I half-feel as if I should like to have him with me!

[054-172] … But Lord, I don’t want to take him from ’ee—ever I should sin to

[054-173] speak so profane—though I should think you must have enough of your

[054-174] own! He’s in very good hands, that I know; and I am not the woman to

[054-175] find fault with what the Lord has ordained. I’ve reached a more

[054-176] resigned frame of mind.”

[054-177] “Indeed! I wish I had been able to do so.”

[054-178] “You should try,” replied the widow, from the serene heights of a soul

[054-179] conscious not only of spiritual but of social superiority. “I make no

[054-180] boast of my awakening, but I’m not what I was. After Cartlett’s death I

[054-181] was passing the chapel in the street next ours, and went into it for

[054-182] shelter from a shower of rain. I felt a need of some sort of support

[054-183] under my loss, and, as ’twas righter than gin, I took to going there

[054-184] regular, and found it a great comfort. But I’ve left London now, you

[054-185] know, and at present I am living at Alfredston, with my friend Anny, to

[054-186] be near my own old country. I’m not come here to the fair to-day.

[054-187] There’s to be the foundation-stone of a new chapel laid this afternoon

[054-188] by a popular London preacher, and I drove over with Anny. Now I must go

[054-189] back to meet her.”

[054-190] Then Arabella wished Sue good-bye, and went on.

[054-191] VIII

[054-192] In the afternoon Sue and the other people bustling about Kennetbridge

[054-193] fair could hear singing inside the placarded hoarding farther down the

[054-194] street. Those who peeped through the opening saw a crowd of persons in

[054-195] broadcloth, with hymn-books in their hands, standing round the

[054-196] excavations for the new chapel-walls. Arabella Cartlett and her weeds

[054-197] stood among them. She had a clear, powerful voice, which could be

[054-198] distinctly heard with the rest, rising and falling to the tune, her

[054-199] inflated bosom being also seen doing likewise.
