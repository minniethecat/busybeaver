[013-001] He could not realize himself. On the old track he seemed to be a boy

[013-002] still, hardly a day older than when he had stood dreaming at the top of

[013-003] that hill, inwardly fired for the first time with ardours for

[013-004] Christminster and scholarship. “Yet I am a man,” he said. “I have a

[013-005] wife. More, I have arrived at the still riper stage of having disagreed

[013-006] with her, disliked her, had a scuffle with her, and parted from her.”

[013-007] He remembered then that he was standing not far from the spot at which

[013-008] the parting between his father and his mother was said to have

[013-009] occurred.

[013-010] A little further on was the summit whence Christminster, or what he had

[013-011] taken for that city, had seemed to be visible. A milestone, now as

[013-012] always, stood at the roadside hard by. Jude drew near it, and felt

[013-013] rather than read the mileage to the city. He remembered that once on

[013-014] his way home he had proudly cut with his keen new chisel an inscription

[013-015] on the back of that milestone, embodying his aspirations. It had been

[013-016] done in the first week of his apprenticeship, before he had been

[013-017] diverted from his purposes by an unsuitable woman. He wondered if the

[013-018] inscription were legible still, and going to the back of the milestone

[013-019] brushed away the nettles. By the light of a match he could still

[013-020] discern what he had cut so enthusiastically so long ago:

[013-021] [THITHER—J. F. [with a pointing finger]]

[013-022] The sight of it, unimpaired, within its screen of grass and nettles,

[013-023] lit in his soul a spark of the old fire. Surely his plan should be to

[013-024] move onward through good and ill—to avoid morbid sorrow even though he

[013-025] did see uglinesses in the world? _Bene agere et lætari_—to do good

[013-026] cheerfully—which he had heard to be the philosophy of one Spinoza,

[013-027] might be his own even now.

[013-028] He might battle with his evil star, and follow out his original

[013-029] intention.

[013-030] By moving to a spot a little way off he uncovered the horizon in a

[013-031] north-easterly direction. There actually rose the faint halo, a small

[013-032] dim nebulousness, hardly recognizable save by the eye of faith. It was

[013-033] enough for him. He would go to Christminster as soon as the term of his

[013-034] apprenticeship expired.

[013-035] He returned to his lodgings in a better mood, and said his prayers.

[013-036] Part Second AT CHRISTMINSTER

[013-037] _“Save his own soul he hath no star.”_—SWINBURNE.

[013-038] _“Notitiam primosque gradus vicinia fecit;

[013-039] Tempore crevit amor.”_—OVID.

[013-040] I

[013-041] The next noteworthy move in Jude’s life was that in which he appeared

[013-042] gliding steadily onward through a dusky landscape of some three years’

[013-043] later leafage than had graced his courtship of Arabella, and the

[013-044] disruption of his coarse conjugal life with her. He was walking towards

[013-045] Christminster City, at a point a mile or two to the south-west of it.

[013-046] He had at last found himself clear of Marygreen and Alfredston: he was

[013-047] out of his apprenticeship, and with his tools at his back seemed to be

[013-048] in the way of making a new start—the start to which, barring the

[013-049] interruption involved in his intimacy and married experience with

[013-050] Arabella, he had been looking forward for about ten years.

[013-051] Jude would now have been described as a young man with a forcible,

[013-052] meditative, and earnest rather than handsome cast of countenance. He

[013-053] was of dark complexion, with dark harmonizing eyes, and he wore a

[013-054] closely trimmed black beard of more advanced growth than is usual at

[013-055] his age; this, with his great mass of black curly hair, was some

[013-056] trouble to him in combing and washing out the stone-dust that settled

[013-057] on it in the pursuit of his trade. His capabilities in the latter,

[013-058] having been acquired in the country, were of an all-round sort,

[013-059] including monumental stone-cutting, gothic free-stone work for the

[013-060] restoration of churches, and carving of a general kind. In London he

[013-061] would probably have become specialized and have made himself a

[013-062] “moulding mason,” a “foliage sculptor”—perhaps a “statuary.”

[013-063] He had that afternoon driven in a cart from Alfredston to the village

[013-064] nearest the city in this direction, and was now walking the remaining

[013-065] four miles rather from choice than from necessity, having always

[013-066] fancied himself arriving thus.

[013-067] The ultimate impulse to come had had a curious origin—one more nearly

[013-068] related to the emotional side of him than to the intellectual, as is

[013-069] often the case with young men. One day while in lodgings at Alfredston

[013-070] he had gone to Marygreen to see his old aunt, and had observed between

[013-071] the brass candlesticks on her mantlepiece the photograph of a pretty

[013-072] girlish face, in a broad hat with radiating folds under the brim like

[013-073] the rays of a halo. He had asked who she was. His grand-aunt had

[013-074] gruffly replied that she was his cousin Sue Bridehead, of the inimical

[013-075] branch of the family; and on further questioning the old woman had

[013-076] replied that the girl lived in Christminster, though she did not know

[013-077] where, or what she was doing.

[013-078] His aunt would not give him the photograph. But it haunted him; and

[013-079] ultimately formed a quickening ingredient in his latent intent of

[013-080] following his friend the school master thither.

[013-081] He now paused at the top of a crooked and gentle declivity, and

[013-082] obtained his first near view of the city. Grey-stoned and dun-roofed,

[013-083] it stood within hail of the Wessex border, and almost with the tip of

[013-084] one small toe within it, at the northernmost point of the crinkled line

[013-085] along which the leisurely Thames strokes the fields of that ancient

[013-086] kingdom. The buildings now lay quiet in the sunset, a vane here and

[013-087] there on their many spires and domes giving sparkle to a picture of

[013-088] sober secondary and tertiary hues.

[013-089] Reaching the bottom he moved along the level way between pollard

[013-090] willows growing indistinct in the twilight, and soon confronted the

[013-091] outmost lamps of the town—some of those lamps which had sent into the

[013-092] sky the gleam and glory that caught his strained gaze in his days of

[013-093] dreaming, so many years ago. They winked their yellow eyes at him

[013-094] dubiously, and as if, though they had been awaiting him all these years

[013-095] in disappointment at his tarrying, they did not much want him now.

[013-096] He was a species of Dick Whittington whose spirit was touched to finer

[013-097] issues than a mere material gain. He went along the outlying streets

[013-098] with the cautious tread of an explorer. He saw nothing of the real city

[013-099] in the suburbs on this side. His first want being a lodging he

[013-100] scrutinized carefully such localities as seemed to offer on inexpensive

[013-101] terms the modest type of accommodation he demanded; and after inquiry

[013-102] took a room in a suburb nicknamed “Beersheba,” though he did not know

[013-103] this at the time. Here he installed himself, and having had some tea

[013-104] sallied forth.

[013-105] It was a windy, whispering, moonless night. To guide himself he opened

[013-106] under a lamp a map he had brought. The breeze ruffled and fluttered it,

[013-107] but he could see enough to decide on the direction he should take to

[013-108] reach the heart of the place.

[013-109] After many turnings he came up to the first ancient mediæval pile that

[013-110] he had encountered. It was a college, as he could see by the gateway.

[013-111] He entered it, walked round, and penetrated to dark corners which no

[013-112] lamplight reached. Close to this college was another; and a little

[013-113] further on another; and then he began to be encircled as it were with

[013-114] the breath and sentiment of the venerable city. When he passed objects

[013-115] out of harmony with its general expression he allowed his eyes to slip

[013-116] over them as if he did not see them.

[013-117] A bell began clanging, and he listened till a hundred-and-one strokes

[013-118] had sounded. He must have made a mistake, he thought: it was meant for

[013-119] a hundred.

[013-120] When the gates were shut, and he could no longer get into the

[013-121] quadrangles, he rambled under the walls and doorways, feeling with his

[013-122] fingers the contours of their mouldings and carving. The minutes

[013-123] passed, fewer and fewer people were visible, and still he serpentined

[013-124] among the shadows, for had he not imagined these scenes through ten

[013-125] bygone years, and what mattered a night’s rest for once? High against

[013-126] the black sky the flash of a lamp would show crocketed pinnacles and

[013-127] indented battlements. Down obscure alleys, apparently never trodden now

[013-128] by the foot of man, and whose very existence seemed to be forgotten,

[013-129] there would jut into the path porticoes, oriels, doorways of enriched

[013-130] and florid middle-age design, their extinct air being accentuated by

[013-131] the rottenness of the stones. It seemed impossible that modern thought

[013-132] could house itself in such decrepit and superseded chambers.

[013-133] Knowing not a human being here, Jude began to be impressed with the

[013-134] isolation of his own personality, as with a self-spectre, the sensation

[013-135] being that of one who walked but could not make himself seen or heard.

[013-136] He drew his breath pensively, and, seeming thus almost his own ghost,

[013-137] gave his thoughts to the other ghostly presences with which the nooks

[013-138] were haunted.

[013-139] During the interval of preparation for this venture, since his wife and

[013-140] furniture’s uncompromising disappearance into space, he had read and

[013-141] learnt almost all that could be read and learnt by one in his position,

[013-142] of the worthies who had spent their youth within these reverend walls,

[013-143] and whose souls had haunted them in their maturer age. Some of them, by

[013-144] the accidents of his reading, loomed out in his fancy

[013-145] disproportionately large by comparison with the rest. The brushings of

[013-146] the wind against the angles, buttresses, and door-jambs were as the

[013-147] passing of these only other inhabitants, the tappings of each ivy leaf

[013-148] on its neighbour were as the mutterings of their mournful souls, the

[013-149] shadows as their thin shapes in nervous movement, making him comrades

[013-150] in his solitude. In the gloom it was as if he ran against them without

[013-151] feeling their bodily frames.

[013-152] The streets were now deserted, but on account of these things he could

[013-153] not go in. There were poets abroad, of early date and of late, from the

[013-154] friend and eulogist of Shakespeare down to him who has recently passed

[013-155] into silence, and that musical one of the tribe who is still among us.

[013-156] Speculative philosophers drew along, not always with wrinkled foreheads

[013-157] and hoary hair as in framed portraits, but pink-faced, slim, and active

[013-158] as in youth; modern divines sheeted in their surplices, among whom the

[013-159] most real to Jude Fawley were the founders of the religious school

[013-160] called Tractarian; the well-known three, the enthusiast, the poet, and

[013-161] the formularist, the echoes of whose teachings had influenced him even

[013-162] in his obscure home. A start of aversion appeared in his fancy to move

[013-163] them at sight of those other sons of the place, the form in the

[013-164] full-bottomed wig, statesman, rake, reasoner, and sceptic; the smoothly

[013-165] shaven historian so ironically civil to Christianity; with others of

[013-166] the same incredulous temper, who knew each quad as well as the

[013-167] faithful, and took equal freedom in haunting its cloisters.

[013-168] He regarded the statesmen in their various types, men of firmer

[013-169] movement and less dreamy air; the scholar, the speaker, the plodder;

[013-170] the man whose mind grew with his growth in years, and the man whose

[013-171] mind contracted with the same.

[013-172] The scientists and philologists followed on in his mind-sight in an odd

[013-173] impossible combination, men of meditative faces, strained foreheads,

[013-174] and weak-eyed as bats with constant research; then official

[013-175] characters—such men as governor-generals and lord-lieutenants, in whom

[013-176] he took little interest; chief-justices and lord chancellors, silent

[013-177] thin-lipped figures of whom he knew barely the names. A keener regard

[013-178] attached to the prelates, by reason of his own former hopes. Of them he

[013-179] had an ample band—some men of heart, others rather men of head; he who

[013-180] apologized for the Church in Latin; the saintly author of the Evening

[013-181] Hymn; and near them the great itinerant preacher, hymn-writer, and

[013-182] zealot, shadowed like Jude by his matrimonial difficulties.
