# Beersheba

Beersheba is a working-class suburb of [[christminster|Christminster]] in *[[jude-fawley|Jude the Obscure]]*, the district where [[jude-fawley|Jude Fawley]] takes his lodgings during his years as a stonemason and aspiring scholar. Its biblical name — a mocking local nickname — encapsulates the novel's central irony: a place named for the wells of the patriarchs is, in Hardy's [[christminster|Christminster]], a quarter of cramped rooms and exhausted labor.

## Overview

Beersheba lies at the periphery of [[christminster|Christminster]], physically close to the colleges yet socially remote from them. [[jude-fawley|Jude]] takes a room in the suburb soon after his arrival in the city, "though he did not know why it was called so" (013-102). The name itself is a local jest — Beersheba, in the Old Testament, is the place of wells and oaths; in [[christminster|Christminster]], it is the quarter of artisans and laborers who build and maintain the very buildings from which they are excluded.

The suburb is described as being "near the ritualistic church" of St. Silas (031-025), and it is from Beersheba that [[jude-fawley|Jude]] walks to his work as a stonemason on the college buildings. The district recurs throughout the novel as [[jude-fawley|Jude]]'s base in [[christminster|Christminster]] — a constant reminder that he belongs to the city's working periphery, not its learned center.

## In the Novel

### Narrative Significance

Beersheba first appears when [[jude-fawley|Jude]] arrives in [[christminster|Christminster]] and finds a room in the suburb (013-102). The lodging is described through the address of [[jude-fawley|Jude]]'s neighbor, "Silas, in the suburb of Beersheba" (016-092) — a detail that roots the district in the specific geography of tradesmen and artisans. The suburb's biblical name, bestowed ironically, mirrors [[jude-fawley|Jude]]'s own condition: a man of deep spiritual and intellectual aspiration consigned to the margins of the institution that could fulfill them.

When [[jude-fawley|Jude]] returns to [[christminster|Christminster]] in Part Sixth, he again stays in Beersheba. [[arabella-donn|Arabella]] finds him there in "his former lodging in Beersheba" (031-025), and her visit to "my old quarter of Beersheba" (058-076) in Part Fifth marks the suburb as a site of return — [[jude-fawley|Jude]]'s life circles back to the same narrow streets even as his ambitions have been defeated. The novel closes with [[arabella-donn|Arabella]] watching [[jude-fawley|Jude]]'s funeral from the direction of "the Beersheba suburb of [[christminster|Christminster]]" (062-173), the district becoming the final vantage point on his life.

## Real-World Basis

| Fictional Name | Real Location | County |
|---------------|---------------|--------|
| Beersheba | Jericho (suburb of [[christminster|Oxford]]) | [[christminster|Oxford]]shire |

Hardy based Beersheba on the Jericho district of [[christminster|Oxford]] — a working-class suburb just outside the city walls, historically home to artisans, laborers, and workers at the [[christminster|Oxford]] University Press. Jericho, like Beersheba, sits near a ritualistic church (St. Barnabas) and was known in Hardy's time for its modest terraced housing. The biblical nickname is Hardy's own sardonic invention, but it captures the real Jericho's character as a district named with scriptural grandeur but lived in with industrial labor.
