[005-001] At this date Mr. Phillotson sent for his pianoforte, and it gave Jude a

[005-002] lead. Why should he not write to the schoolmaster, and ask him to be so

[005-003] kind as to get him the grammars in Christminster? He might slip a

[005-004] letter inside the case of the instrument, and it would be sure to reach

[005-005] the desired eyes. Why not ask him to send any old second-hand copies,

[005-006] which would have the charm of being mellowed by the university

[005-007] atmosphere?

[005-008] To tell his aunt of his intention would be to defeat it. It was

[005-009] necessary to act alone.

[005-010] After a further consideration of a few days he did act, and on the day

[005-011] of the piano’s departure, which happened to be his next birthday,

[005-012] clandestinely placed the letter inside the packing-case, directed to

[005-013] his much-admired friend, being afraid to reveal the operation to his

[005-014] aunt Drusilla, lest she should discover his motive, and compel him to

[005-015] abandon his scheme.

[005-016] The piano was despatched, and Jude waited days and weeks, calling every

[005-017] morning at the cottage post office before his great-aunt was stirring.

[005-018] At last a packet did indeed arrive at the village, and he saw from the

[005-019] ends of it that it contained two thin books. He took it away into a

[005-020] lonely place, and sat down on a felled elm to open it.

[005-021] Ever since his first ecstasy or vision of Christminster and its

[005-022] possibilities, Jude had meditated much and curiously on the probable

[005-023] sort of process that was involved in turning the expressions of one

[005-024] language into those of another. He concluded that a grammar of the

[005-025] required tongue would contain, primarily, a rule, prescription, or clue

[005-026] of the nature of a secret cipher, which, once known, would enable him,

[005-027] by merely applying it, to change at will all words of his own speech

[005-028] into those of the foreign one. His childish idea was, in fact, a

[005-029] pushing to the extremity of mathematical precision what is everywhere

[005-030] known as Grimm’s Law—an aggrandizement of rough rules to ideal

[005-031] completeness. Thus he assumed that the words of the required language

[005-032] were always to be found somewhere latent in the words of the given

[005-033] language by those who had the art to uncover them, such art being

[005-034] furnished by the books aforesaid.

[005-035] When, therefore, having noted that the packet bore the postmark of

[005-036] Christminster, he cut the string, opened the volumes, and turned to the

[005-037] Latin grammar, which chanced to come uppermost, he could scarcely

[005-038] believe his eyes.

[005-039] The book was an old one—thirty years old, soiled, scribbled wantonly

[005-040] over with a strange name in every variety of enmity to the letterpress,

[005-041] and marked at random with dates twenty years earlier than his own day.

[005-042] But this was not the cause of Jude’s amazement. He learnt for the first

[005-043] time that there was no law of transmutation, as in his innocence he had

[005-044] supposed (there was, in some degree, but the grammarian did not

[005-045] recognize it), but that every word in both Latin and Greek was to be

[005-046] individually committed to memory at the cost of years of plodding.

[005-047] Jude flung down the books, lay backward along the broad trunk of the

[005-048] elm, and was an utterly miserable boy for the space of a quarter of an

[005-049] hour. As he had often done before, he pulled his hat over his face and

[005-050] watched the sun peering insidiously at him through the interstices of

[005-051] the straw. This was Latin and Greek, then, was it this grand delusion!

[005-052] The charm he had supposed in store for him was really a labour like

[005-053] that of Israel in Egypt.

[005-054] What brains they must have in Christminster and the great schools, he

[005-055] presently thought, to learn words one by one up to tens of thousands!

[005-056] There were no brains in his head equal to this business; and as the

[005-057] little sun-rays continued to stream in through his hat at him, he

[005-058] wished he had never seen a book, that he might never see another, that

[005-059] he had never been born.

[005-060] Somebody might have come along that way who would have asked him his

[005-061] trouble, and might have cheered him by saying that his notions were

[005-062] further advanced than those of his grammarian. But nobody did come,

[005-063] because nobody does; and under the crushing recognition of his gigantic

[005-064] error Jude continued to wish himself out of the world.

[005-065] V

[005-066] During the three or four succeeding years a quaint and singular vehicle

[005-067] might have been discerned moving along the lanes and by-roads near

[005-068] Marygreen, driven in a quaint and singular way.

[005-069] In the course of a month or two after the receipt of the books Jude had

[005-070] grown callous to the shabby trick played him by the dead languages. In

[005-071] fact, his disappointment at the nature of those tongues had, after a

[005-072] while, been the means of still further glorifying the erudition of

[005-073] Christminster. To acquire languages, departed or living in spite of

[005-074] such obstinacies as he now knew them inherently to possess, was a

[005-075] herculean performance which gradually led him on to a greater interest

[005-076] in it than in the presupposed patent process. The mountain-weight of

[005-077] material under which the ideas lay in those dusty volumes called the

[005-078] classics piqued him into a dogged, mouselike subtlety of attempt to

[005-079] move it piecemeal.

[005-080] He had endeavoured to make his presence tolerable to his crusty maiden

[005-081] aunt by assisting her to the best of his ability, and the business of

[005-082] the little cottage bakery had grown in consequence. An aged horse with

[005-083] a hanging head had been purchased for eight pounds at a sale, a

[005-084] creaking cart with a whity-brown tilt obtained for a few pounds more,

[005-085] and in this turn-out it became Jude’s business thrice a week to carry

[005-086] loaves of bread to the villagers and solitary cotters immediately round

[005-087] Marygreen.

[005-088] The singularity aforesaid lay, after all, less in the conveyance itself

[005-089] than in Jude’s manner of conducting it along its route. Its interior

[005-090] was the scene of most of Jude’s education by “private study.” As soon

[005-091] as the horse had learnt the road and the houses at which he was to

[005-092] pause awhile, the boy, seated in front, would slip the reins over his

[005-093] arm, ingeniously fix open, by means of a strap attached to the tilt,

[005-094] the volume he was reading, spread the dictionary on his knees, and

[005-095] plunge into the simpler passages from Caesar, Virgil, or Horace, as the

[005-096] case might be, in his purblind stumbling way, and with an expenditure

[005-097] of labour that would have made a tender-hearted pedagogue shed tears;

[005-098] yet somehow getting at the meaning of what he read, and divining rather

[005-099] than beholding the spirit of the original, which often to his mind was

[005-100] something else than that which he was taught to look for.

[005-101] The only copies he had been able to lay hands on were old Delphin

[005-102] editions, because they were superseded, and therefore cheap. But, bad

[005-103] for idle schoolboys, it did so happen that they were passably good for

[005-104] him. The hampered and lonely itinerant conscientiously covered up the

[005-105] marginal readings, and used them merely on points of construction, as

[005-106] he would have used a comrade or tutor who should have happened to be

[005-107] passing by. And though Jude may have had little chance of becoming a

[005-108] scholar by these rough and ready means, he was in the way of getting

[005-109] into the groove he wished to follow.

[005-110] While he was busied with these ancient pages, which had already been

[005-111] thumbed by hands possibly in the grave, digging out the thoughts of

[005-112] these minds so remote yet so near, the bony old horse pursued his

[005-113] rounds, and Jude would be aroused from the woes of Dido by the stoppage

[005-114] of his cart and the voice of some old woman crying, “Two to-day, baker,

[005-115] and I return this stale one.”

[005-116] He was frequently met in the lanes by pedestrians and others without

[005-117] his seeing them, and by degrees the people of the neighbourhood began

[005-118] to talk about his method of combining work and play (such they

[005-119] considered his reading to be), which, though probably convenient enough

[005-120] to himself, was not altogether a safe proceeding for other travellers

[005-121] along the same roads. There were murmurs. Then a private resident of an

[005-122] adjoining place informed the local policeman that the baker’s boy

[005-123] should not be allowed to read while driving, and insisted that it was

[005-124] the constable’s duty to catch him in the act, and take him to the

[005-125] police court at Alfredston, and get him fined for dangerous practices

[005-126] on the highway. The policeman thereupon lay in wait for Jude, and one

[005-127] day accosted him and cautioned him.

[005-128] As Jude had to get up at three o’clock in the morning to heat the oven,

[005-129] and mix and set in the bread that he distributed later in the day, he

[005-130] was obliged to go to bed at night immediately after laying the sponge;

[005-131] so that if he could not read his classics on the highways he could

[005-132] hardly study at all. The only thing to be done was, therefore, to keep

[005-133] a sharp eye ahead and around him as well as he could in the

[005-134] circumstances, and slip away his books as soon as anybody loomed in the

[005-135] distance, the policeman in particular. To do that official justice, he

[005-136] did not put himself much in the way of Jude’s bread-cart, considering

[005-137] that in such a lonely district the chief danger was to Jude himself,

[005-138] and often on seeing the white tilt over the hedges he would move in

[005-139] another direction.

[005-140] On a day when Fawley was getting quite advanced, being now about

[005-141] sixteen, and had been stumbling through the “Carmen Sæculare,” on his

[005-142] way home, he found himself to be passing over the high edge of the

[005-143] plateau by the Brown House. The light had changed, and it was the sense

[005-144] of this which had caused him to look up. The sun was going down, and

[005-145] the full moon was rising simultaneously behind the woods in the

[005-146] opposite quarter. His mind had become so impregnated with the poem

[005-147] that, in a moment of the same impulsive emotion which years before had

[005-148] caused him to kneel on the ladder, he stopped the horse, alighted, and

[005-149] glancing round to see that nobody was in sight, knelt down on the

[005-150] roadside bank with open book. He turned first to the shiny goddess, who

[005-151] seemed to look so softly and critically at his doings, then to the

[005-152] disappearing luminary on the other hand, as he began:

[005-153] “Phœbe silvarumque potens Diana!”

[005-154] The horse stood still till he had finished the hymn, which Jude

[005-155] repeated under the sway of a polytheistic fancy that he would never

[005-156] have thought of humouring in broad daylight.

[005-157] Reaching home, he mused over his curious superstition, innate or

[005-158] acquired, in doing this, and the strange forgetfulness which had led to

[005-159] such a lapse from common sense and custom in one who wished, next to

[005-160] being a scholar, to be a Christian divine. It had all come of reading

[005-161] heathen works exclusively. The more he thought of it the more convinced

[005-162] he was of his inconsistency. He began to wonder whether he could be

[005-163] reading quite the right books for his object in life. Certainly there

[005-164] seemed little harmony between this pagan literature and the mediæval

[005-165] colleges at Christminster, that ecclesiastical romance in stone.

[005-166] Ultimately he decided that in his sheer love of reading he had taken up

[005-167] a wrong emotion for a Christian young man. He had dabbled in Clarke’s

[005-168] Homer, but had never yet worked much at the New Testament in the Greek,

[005-169] though he possessed a copy, obtained by post from a second-hand

[005-170] bookseller. He abandoned the now familiar Ionic for a new dialect, and

[005-171] for a long time onward limited his reading almost entirely to the

[005-172] Gospels and Epistles in Griesbach’s text. Moreover, on going into

[005-173] Alfredston one day, he was introduced to patristic literature by

[005-174] finding at the bookseller’s some volumes of the Fathers which had been

[005-175] left behind by an insolvent clergyman of the neighbourhood.
