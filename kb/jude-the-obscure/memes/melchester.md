# Melchester

Melchester is the fictional cathedral city where Part Third of *[[jude-fawley|Jude the Obscure]]* -- titled "At Melchester" (022-039) -- principally unfolds. It is the site of the Training [[college|College]] where [[sue-bridehead|Sue Bridehead]] enrolls to become a teacher, and the setting for the intensifying relationship between [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] and [[jude-fawley|Jude]] -- a relationship conducted in the shadow of the cathedral close. "Melchester was a quiet and soothing place" (022-079): an apparently gentle cathedral city that proves, for [[sue-bridehead|Sue]], to be an apparatus of surveillance and punishment. It is religious [[wessex|Wessex]] at its most institutional -- a city of bells, rules, and enclosure, where personal [[love|desire]] runs directly into ecclesiastical discipline.

## Overview

Melchester is home to both a theological college and a teacher-training school -- two institutions that embody the respectable professions open, in Hardy's fiction, to those without independent means. It is here that [[sue-bridehead|Sue]], having left her work as a designer of ecclesiastical ornaments in [[christminster|Christminster]], attempts to conform to the expectations placed upon young women of her class by entering a respectable profession. The novel describes her admission: "Christmas had come and passed, and Sue had gone to the Melchester Normal School" (022-100).

The city's ecclesiastical architecture and rhythms dominate its atmosphere. The "vast west front of the cathedral opposite" (024-119) is a constant visual presence; the mullioned windows of the training-school look out upon it. The Training-School itself is described as "a species of nunnery" (024-059) -- the phrase capturing both the institution's religious foundation and its function as a place where women's bodies are enclosed and regulated. The seventy young women who "filled the species of nunnery known as the Training-School at Melchester" (024-059) "formed a very mixed community, which included the daughters of mechanics, curates, surgeons, shopkeepers, farmers, dairy-men, soldiers, sailors, and villagers" (024-060, 024-062) -- a social cross-section that reflects the contemporary expansion of women's professional education.

For [[sue-bridehead|Sue]], Melchester becomes both a refuge from her past and a prison of regulation. She is confined to the college grounds, visited by [[jude-fawley|Jude]] under strict rules, and ultimately expelled for an infraction that reveals the institution's cruelty beneath its pious surface. [[jude-fawley|Jude]], who "packed up his things and went to Melchester with an unreasonably glad heart" (022-118), finds work as a stonemason on the cathedral repairs -- a detail that places him, physically, at the very heart of the ecclesiastical establishment that has rejected his university aspirations.

## In the Novel

### Narrative Significance

Melchester is the setting for Part Third, and the city's role is to bring [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] and [[jude-fawley|Jude]] into close proximity under conditions of institutional surveillance. The Training [[college|College]] functions as a disciplinary apparatus: its rules about visiting hours, its walls, and its moral code all conspire to make [[sue-bridehead|Sue]]'s and [[jude-fawley|Jude]]'s connection illicit by definition.

The tension between institutional and personal life is dramatised in the architecture itself. The training-school, "with mullioned and transomed windows, and a courtyard" (022-144), is a building designed for observation. Signs of the cathedral's ongoing presence are everywhere: "timber and stone were lying about, which signified that the cathedral was undergoing repairs" (022-131). When [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] deflects [[jude-fawley|Jude]]'s suggestion that they sit in the cathedral -- "'Cathedral? Yes. Though I think I'd rather sit in the railway station,' she said, 'that's the centre of the town life now. The cathedral has had its day!'" (023-071) -- she voices a theme that cuts across the novel: the displacement of religious authority by modern mobility and individual desire.

The cathedral repairs, "which were very extensive, the most desired" (023-094) work that [[jude-fawley|Jude]] could hope for as a stonemason, place him in an ambiguous relationship to the church. He is simultaneously inside and outside it: his hands restore its stones, but his mind and heart are excluded from its community. When [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] is caught returning from an evening with [[jude-fawley|Jude]], having spent the night sheltering in a shepherd's cottage after missing her train -- "'Don't 'ee mind I,' he said with a deprecating wave of the hand; 'bide here as long as ye will. But mid you be thinking o' getting back to Melchester to-night by train?'" (024-011, 024-012) -- the college's punishment, expulsion, dramatises the novel's critique of the way institutional religion polices human [[love|affection]].

After [[sue-bridehead|Sue]]'s expulsion, Melchester becomes a way station. [[jude-fawley|Jude]], who had been working nearby, takes her to the railway station and sees her off. The city, which had promised [[sue-bridehead|Sue]] professional stability, instead becomes the scene of her social undoing -- a pattern that repeats for female characters throughout Hardy's fiction. "Melchester was a dismal place enough for Jude that Sunday" (027-019) after her departure: the city emptied of her presence becomes merely another provincial town.

## Real-World Basis

| Fictional Name | Real Location | County |
|---------------|---------------|--------|
| Melchester | Salisbury | Wiltshire |

Hardy based Melchester on Salisbury, the cathedral city in Wiltshire whose spire is the tallest in England. The Training [[college|College]] corresponds to the Salisbury Diocesan Training College (now part of Salisbury's university provision). Salisbury's close -- the walled precinct around the cathedral -- is one of the largest and best-preserved in England, and its atmosphere of enclosure and surveillance is precisely what Hardy draws on for [[sue-bridehead|Sue]]'s experience.

The cathedral undergoing repairs in the novel reflects the historical reality: Salisbury Cathedral underwent extensive Victorian restoration, and the presence of stonemasons and scaffolding around its fabric would have been a familiar sight to Hardy's contemporary readers. [[jude-fawley|Jude]]'s work on these repairs places him, literally, at the intersection of medieval craft and Victorian modernity -- restoring an ancient institution whose present-day gatekeepers would never admit him.
