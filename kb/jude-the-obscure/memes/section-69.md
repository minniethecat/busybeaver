[069-001] “They seem laughing at me!”

[069-002] “Who?”

[069-003] “Oh—I was talking to myself! The phantoms all about here, in the

[069-004] college archways, and windows. They used to look friendly in the old

[069-005] days, particularly Addison, and Gibbon, and Johnson, and Dr. Browne,

[069-006] and Bishop Ken—”

[069-007] “Come along do! Phantoms! There’s neither living nor dead hereabouts

[069-008] except a damn policeman! I never saw the streets emptier.”

[069-009] “Fancy! The Poet of Liberty used to walk here, and the great Dissector

[069-010] of Melancholy there!”

[069-011] “I don’t want to hear about ’em! They bore me.”

[069-012] “Walter Raleigh is beckoning to me from that

[069-013] lane—Wycliffe—Harvey—Hooker—Arnold—and a whole crowd of Tractarian

[069-014] Shades—”

[069-015] “I _don’t want_ to know their names, I tell you! What do I care about

[069-016] folk dead and gone? Upon my soul you are more sober when you’ve been

[069-017] drinking than when you have not!”

[069-018] “I must rest a moment,” he said; and as he paused, holding to the

[069-019] railings, he measured with his eye the height of a college front. “This

[069-020] is old Rubric. And that Sarcophagus; and up that lane Crozier and

[069-021] Tudor: and all down there is Cardinal with its long front, and its

[069-022] windows with lifted eyebrows, representing the polite surprise of the

[069-023] university at the efforts of such as I.”

[069-024] “Come along, and I’ll treat you!”

[069-025] “Very well. It will help me home, for I feel the chilly fog from the

[069-026] meadows of Cardinal as if death-claws were grabbing me through and

[069-027] through. As Antigone said, I am neither a dweller among men nor ghosts.

[069-028] But, Arabella, when I am dead, you’ll see my spirit flitting up and

[069-029] down here among these!”

[069-030] “Pooh! You mayn’t die after all. You are tough enough yet, old man.”

[069-031] It was night at Marygreen, and the rain of the afternoon showed no sign

[069-032] of abatement. About the time at which Jude and Arabella were walking

[069-033] the streets of Christminster homeward, the Widow Edlin crossed the

[069-034] green, and opened the back door of the schoolmaster’s dwelling, which

[069-035] she often did now before bedtime, to assist Sue in putting things away.

[069-036] Sue was muddling helplessly in the kitchen, for she was not a good

[069-037] housewife, though she tried to be, and grew impatient of domestic

[069-038] details.

[069-039] “Lord love ’ee, what do ye do that yourself for, when I’ve come o’

[069-040] purpose! You knew I should come.”

[069-041] “Oh—I don’t know—I forgot! No, I didn’t forget. I did it to discipline

[069-042] myself. I have scrubbed the stairs since eight o’clock. I _must_

[069-043] practise myself in my household duties. I’ve shamefully neglected

[069-044] them!”

[069-045] “Why should ye? He’ll get a better school, perhaps be a parson, in

[069-048] “Don’t talk of my pretty hands, Mrs. Edlin. This pretty body of mine

[069-049] has been the ruin of me already!”

[069-050] “Pshoo—you’ve got no body to speak of! You put me more in mind of a

[069-051] sperrit. But there seems something wrong to-night, my dear. Husband

[069-052] cross?”

[069-053] “No. He never is. He’s gone to bed early.”

[069-054] “Then what is it?”

[069-055] “I cannot tell you. I have done wrong to-day. And I want to eradicate

[069-056] it… Well—I will tell you this—Jude has been here this afternoon, and I

[069-057] find I still love him—oh, grossly! I cannot tell you more.”

[069-058] “Ah!” said the widow. “I told ’ee how ’twould be!”

[069-059] “But it shan’t be! I have not told my husband of his visit; it is not

[069-060] necessary to trouble him about it, as I never mean to see Jude any

[069-061] more. But I am going to make my conscience right on my duty to

[069-062] Richard—by doing a penance—the ultimate thing. I must!”

[069-063] “I wouldn’t—since he agrees to it being otherwise, and it has gone on

[069-064] three months very well as it is.”

[069-065] “Yes—he agrees to my living as I choose; but I feel it is an indulgence

[069-066] I ought not to exact from him. It ought not to have been accepted by

[069-067] me. To reverse it will be terrible—but I must be more just to him. O

[069-068] why was I so unheroic!”

[069-069] “What is it you don’t like in him?” asked Mrs. Edlin curiously.

[069-070] “I cannot tell you. It is something… I cannot say. The mournful thing

[069-071] is, that nobody would admit it as a reason for feeling as I do; so that

[069-072] no excuse is left me.”

[069-073] “Did you ever tell Jude what it was?”

[069-074] “Never.”

[069-075] “I’ve heard strange tales o’ husbands in my time,” observed the widow

[069-076] in a lowered voice. “They say that when the saints were upon the earth

[069-077] devils used to take husbands’ forms o’ nights, and get poor women into

[069-078] all sorts of trouble. But I don’t know why that should come into my

[069-079] head, for it is only a tale… What a wind and rain it is to-night!

[069-080] Well—don’t be in a hurry to alter things, my dear. Think it over.”

[069-081] “No, no! I’ve screwed my weak soul up to treating him more

[069-082] courteously—and it must be now—at once—before I break down!”

[069-083] “I don’t think you ought to force your nature. No woman ought to be

[069-084] expected to.”

[069-085] “It is my duty. I will drink my cup to the dregs!”

[069-086] Half an hour later when Mrs. Edlin put on her bonnet and shawl to

[069-087] leave, Sue seemed to be seized with vague terror.

[069-088] “No—no—don’t go, Mrs. Edlin,” she implored, her eyes enlarged, and with

[069-089] a quick nervous look over her shoulder.

[069-090] “But it is bedtime, child.”

[069-091] “Yes, but—there’s the little spare room—my room that was. It is quite

[069-092] ready. Please stay, Mrs. Edlin!—I shall want you in the morning.”

[069-093] “Oh well—I don’t mind, if you wish. Nothing will happen to my four old

[069-094] walls, whether I be there or no.”

[069-095] She then fastened up the doors, and they ascended the stairs together.

[069-096] “Wait here, Mrs. Edlin,” said Sue. “I’ll go into my old room a moment

[069-097] by myself.”

[069-099] been hers exclusively since her arrival at Marygreen, and pushing to

[069-100] the door knelt down by the bed for a minute or two. She then arose, and

[069-101] taking her night-gown from the pillow undressed and came out to Mrs.

[069-102] Edlin. A man could be heard snoring in the room opposite. She wished

[069-103] Mrs. Edlin good-night, and the widow entered the room that Sue had just

[069-104] vacated.

[069-105] Sue unlatched the other chamber door, and, as if seized with faintness,

[069-106] sank down outside it. Getting up again she half opened the door, and

[069-109] The snoring had quite ceased for some time, but he did not reply. Sue

[069-110] seemed relieved, and hurried back to Mrs. Edlin’s chamber. “Are you in

[069-111] bed, Mrs. Edlin?” she asked.

[069-112] “No, dear,” said the widow, opening the door. “I be old and slow, and

[069-113] it takes me a long while to un-ray. I han’t unlaced my jumps yet.”

[069-114] “I—don’t hear him! And perhaps—perhaps—”

[069-115] “What, child?”

[069-116] “Perhaps he’s dead!” she gasped. “And then—I should be _free_, and I

[069-117] could go to Jude! … Ah—no—I forgot _her_—and God!”

[069-118] “Let’s go and hearken. No—he’s snoring again. But the rain and the wind

[069-119] is so loud that you can hardly hear anything but between whiles.”

[069-120] Sue had dragged herself back. “Mrs. Edlin, good-night again! I am sorry

[069-121] I called you out.” The widow retreated a second time.

[069-122] The strained, resigned look returned to Sue’s face when she was alone.

[069-123] “I must do it—I must! I must drink to the dregs!” she whispered.

[069-124] “Richard!” she said again.

[069-125] “Hey—what? Is that you, Susanna?”

[069-126] “Yes.”

[069-127] “What do you want? Anything the matter? Wait a moment.” He pulled on

[069-128] some articles of clothing, and came to the door. “Yes?”

[069-129] “When we were at Shaston I jumped out of the window rather than that

[069-132] in.”

[069-133] “Perhaps you only think you ought to do this? I don’t wish you to come

[069-135] “But I beg to be admitted.” She waited a moment, and repeated, “I beg

[069-143] “Oh—the old story!”

[069-146] “A good many. I don’t know. I am horrified to look back on it, and the

[069-148] “Come—this is pretty bad, after what I’ve done! Anything else to

[069-150] “No.” She had been intending to say: “I called him my darling Love.”
