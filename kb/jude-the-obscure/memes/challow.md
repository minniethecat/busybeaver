# Mr. Challow

Mr. Challow is a minor character in *[[jude-fawley|Jude the Obscure]]* who appears in a single, vividly realised scene early in the novel: the slaughter of [[Arabella-Donn]]'s pig. A professional pig-killer and scalder — the man who makes "the water hot" and scrapes the bristles from the carcass — Challow's late arrival forces [[Jude-Fawley]] into a role for which he is temperamentally unsuited, creating one of the novel's most memorable episodes of dark rural comedy.

## Character Overview

Challow is known by his trade. He arrives with a "basket o' knives and things" (011-035) — the tools of the pig-killing profession — and his physical presence is that of a man built for the work: a "burly form" seen "leaning over the gate, critically surveying their performance" (011-108). He is a figure from the old rural economy, skilled in the bloody practicalities of country life that [[jude-fawley|Jude]], for all his origins in [[marygreen|Marygreen]], has never fully mastered.

[[arabella-donn|Arabella]]'s preference for Challow is stated plainly: "Though as it is such a big pig I had rather Challow had done it" (011-035). The remark is a small but telling slight against [[jude-fawley|Jude]], whose competence in the masculine rituals of rural life is implicitly questioned.

## Role in the Narrative

Challow's narrative function is to highlight [[jude-fawley|Jude]]'s inadequacy in the world of physical, practical men. The pig-killing scene is one of Hardy's set-pieces of rustic realism, and Challow's late arrival — and his subsequent critical appraisal of [[jude-fawley|Jude]] and [[arabella-donn|Arabella]]'s amateur efforts — provides the comic deflation that follows the episode's visceral intensity.

### Key Moments

1. **The Expected Arrival** — The scene begins with [[arabella-donn|Arabella]] asking, "Is Challow come?" (011-017). The question establishes Challow as the expected professional, the man whose skills are needed for the messy work ahead. His absence forces [[jude-fawley|Jude]] into an unfamiliar and unwelcome role.

2. **The Belated Appearance** — After [[jude-fawley|Jude]] and [[arabella-donn|Arabella]] have struggled through the killing themselves, "the burly form of Mr. Challow" appears at the garden gate, "critically surveying their performance" (011-108). The image is comic: the professional arriving after the amateurs have done the job, offering what can only be belated judgment.

3. **Challow's Contrition** — Challow "expressed his contrition" and tells them, "You should have waited a bit" (011-113). The advice is both reasonable and useless — they could not wait, and his lateness has made their amateur efforts a necessity.

4. **Zeal in the Scalding** — Challow "made up for his neglect of the killing by zeal in the scalding and scraping" (011-119). The professional, having failed in his primary duty, compensates with excessive diligence in the secondary work — a small moment of character comedy that Hardy renders with [[love|affection]]ate precision.

## Character Analysis

### Significance in the Novel

The Challow episode, though brief, is not incidental. It belongs to a pattern in the early chapters of the novel in which [[jude-fawley|Jude]]'s masculinity is measured against rural working-class norms and found wanting. [[arabella-donn|Arabella]]'s preference for Challow's professional competence — "I had rather Challow had done it" — is a small but pointed moment of comparison, and [[jude-fawley|Jude]]'s "dissatisfied with himself as a man" (011-120) at the episode's close is a feeling that will recur throughout the novel.

Challow himself is barely individuated — he is his trade, his "burly form," his "basket o' knives." But this is precisely the point: he represents a mode of masculine competence that [[jude-fawley|Jude]], the scholar-dreamer, cannot access. The pig must be killed; Challow is the man who kills pigs. [[jude-fawley|Jude]], who aspires to the life of the mind, finds himself messy and inadequate in the life of the body.
