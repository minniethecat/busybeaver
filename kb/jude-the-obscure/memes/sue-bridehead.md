# Sue Bridehead

Sue Bridehead is the magnetic and contradictory co-protagonist of *[[jude-fawley|Jude the Obscure]]* — an intellectually adventurous, sexually withholding "woman of the feminist type" (as Hardy described her) whose relationship with her cousin [[Jude-Fawley]] forms the volatile emotional center of the novel. Described as "light-footed" and possessed of an "inimical branch of the family" character (013-074), Sue embodies the collision between Victorian social convention and the emerging New Woman.

## Character Overview

Sue is introduced through [[jude-fawley|Jude]]'s first glimpse of her photograph — before they ever meet, her image exerts a fascination that unsettles him. When she appears in person, she is working as a designer and artist in [[Christminster]], living independently and reading pagan authors banned by her strictly religious upbringing. Her intellectual boldness — she purchases statues of Venus and Apollo, reads Gibbon and Swinburne — coexists with a terror of physical intimacy and an almost superstitious dread of committing herself to any man.

She is small, quick-moving, and possessed of what Hardy calls "curious unconsciousness of gender" — a quality that alternately attracts and perplexes [[jude-fawley|Jude]]. Her character arc is the novel's most complex: she moves from defiant free-thinker to broken penitent, repudiating every principle she once held.

## Role in the Narrative

### First Appearance

[[jude-fawley|Jude]] first learns of Sue when he sees her photograph in his great-aunt's house. The aunt describes Sue as belonging to "the inimical branch of the family" (013-074), framing her from the start as both kin and dangerous stranger. When [[jude-fawley|Jude]] and Sue finally meet in [[christminster|Christminster]], the connection is immediate and unsettling — [[jude-fawley|Jude]] finds "his interest in her" growing against his will (016-184).

## Character Analysis

### Motivations & Desires

Sue's central conflict is between intellectual freedom and emotional security. She [[love|desire]]s independence — she supports herself, reads forbidden books, refuses to be owned — yet she also craves the protection of a man who does not demand physical intimacy. Her flight from the Training [[college|College]], climbing through a window into [[jude-fawley|Jude]]'s arms, dramatizes this paradox: she escapes institutional control only to enter another form of dependence.

### Development Arc

Sue's trajectory is an inverted V: she rises from conventional girlhood into a brief, brilliant period of intellectual and emotional freedom with [[jude-fawley|Jude]], then collapses under social pressure. The children's deaths — which she interprets as divine punishment for her unconventional life — trigger a complete reversal. She returns to [[richard-phillotson|Phillotson]], to the church, and to a doctrine of sexual renunciation that Hardy presents as a form of psychological self-destruction. "To keep Sue Bridehead near him was now a [[love|desire]]" that [[jude-fawley|Jude]] could not abandon (018-036), but Sue's retreat into orthodoxy makes her unreachable.

## Critical Reception

Sue Bridehead has divided critics since the novel's publication. Victorian reviewers saw her as unnatural and repellent; D.H. Lawrence celebrated her as Hardy's greatest creation — a portrait of the modern woman "before her time." Feminist criticism reads Sue as a case study in how patriarchy disciplines female intellect: her punishment for thinking freely is a life of sexual servitude to a man she cannot bear to touch. Her arc from pagan free-thinker to Christian penitent remains one of literature's most debated character transformations.
