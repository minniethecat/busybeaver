[048-001] Jude thereupon wrote in purely formal terms to request that the boy

[048-002] should be sent on to them as soon as he arrived, making no remark

[048-003] whatever on the surprising nature of Arabella’s information, nor

[048-004] vouchsafing a single word of opinion on the boy’s paternity, nor on

[048-005] whether, had he known all this, his conduct towards her would have been

[048-006] quite the same.

[048-007] In the down train that was timed to reach Aldbrickham station about ten

[048-008] o’clock the next evening, a small, pale child’s face could be seen in

[048-009] the gloom of a third-class carriage. He had large, frightened eyes, and

[048-010] wore a white woollen cravat, over which a key was suspended round his

[048-011] neck by a piece of common string: the key attracting attention by its

[048-012] occasional shine in the lamplight. In the band of his hat his

[048-013] half-ticket was stuck. His eyes remained mostly fixed on the back of

[048-014] the seat opposite, and never turned to the window even when a station

[048-015] was reached and called. On the other seat were two or three passengers,

[048-016] one of them a working woman who held a basket on her lap, in which was

[048-017] a tabby kitten. The woman opened the cover now and then, whereupon the

[048-018] kitten would put out its head, and indulge in playful antics. At these

[048-019] the fellow-passengers laughed, except the solitary boy bearing the key

[048-020] and ticket, who, regarding the kitten with his saucer eyes, seemed

[048-021] mutely to say: “All laughing comes from misapprehension. Rightly looked

[048-022] at, there is no laughable thing under the sun.”

[048-023] Occasionally, at a stoppage, the guard would look into the compartment

[048-024] and say to the boy, “All right, my man. Your box is safe in the van.”

[048-025] The boy would say, “Yes,” without animation, would try to smile, and

[048-026] fail.

[048-027] He was Age masquerading as Juvenility, and doing it so badly that his

[048-028] real self showed through crevices. A ground-swell from ancient years of

[048-029] night seemed now and then to lift the child in this his morning-life,

[048-030] when his face took a back view over some great Atlantic of Time, and

[048-031] appeared not to care about what it saw.

[048-032] When the other travellers closed their eyes, which they did one by

[048-033] one—even the kitten curling itself up in the basket, weary of its too

[048-034] circumscribed play—the boy remained just as before. He then seemed to

[048-035] be doubly awake, like an enslaved and dwarfed divinity, sitting passive

[048-036] and regarding his companions as if he saw their whole rounded lives

[048-037] rather than their immediate figures.

[048-038] This was Arabella’s boy. With her usual carelessness, she had postponed

[048-039] writing to Jude about him till the eve of his landing, when she could

[048-040] absolutely postpone no longer, though she had known for weeks of his

[048-041] approaching arrival, and had, as she truly said, visited Aldbrickham

[048-042] mainly to reveal the boy’s existence and his near home-coming to Jude.

[048-043] This very day on which she had received her former husband’s answer at

[048-044] some time in the afternoon, the child reached the London Docks, and the

[048-045] family in whose charge he had come, having put him into a cab for

[048-046] Lambeth and directed the cabman to his mother’s house, bade him

[048-047] good-bye, and went their way.

[048-048] On his arrival at the Three Horns, Arabella had looked him over with an

[048-049] expression that was as good as saying, “You are very much what I

[048-050] expected you to be,” had given him a good meal, a little money, and,

[048-051] late as it was getting, dispatched him to Jude by the next train,

[048-052] wishing her husband Cartlett, who was out, not to see him.

[048-053] The train reached Aldbrickham, and the boy was deposited on the lonely

[048-054] platform beside his box. The collector took his ticket, and, with a

[048-055] meditative sense of the unfitness of things, asked him where he was

[048-056] going by himself at that time of night.

[048-057] “Going to Spring Street,” said the little one impassively.

[048-058] “Why, that’s a long way from here; a’most out in the country; and the

[048-059] folks will be gone to bed.”

[048-060] “I’ve got to go there.”

[048-061] “You must have a fly for your box.”

[048-062] “No. I must walk.”

[048-063] “Oh well: you’d better leave your box here and send for it. There’s a

[048-064] ‘bus goes half-way, but you’ll have to walk the rest.”

[048-065] “I am not afraid.”

[048-066] “Why didn’t your friends come to meet ’ee?”

[048-067] “I suppose they didn’t know I was coming.”

[048-068] “Who is your friends?”

[048-069] “Mother didn’t wish me to say.”

[048-070] “All I can do, then, is to take charge of this. Now walk as fast as you

[048-071] can.”

[048-072] Saying nothing further the boy came out into the street, looking round

[048-073] to see that nobody followed or observed him. When he had walked some

[048-074] little distance he asked for the street of his destination. He was told

[048-075] to go straight on quite into the outskirts of the place.

[048-076] The child fell into a steady mechanical creep which had in it an

[048-077] impersonal quality—the movement of the wave, or of the breeze, or of

[048-078] the cloud. He followed his directions literally, without an inquiring

[048-079] gaze at anything. It could have been seen that the boy’s ideas of life

[048-080] were different from those of the local boys. Children begin with

[048-081] detail, and learn up to the general; they begin with the contiguous,

[048-082] and gradually comprehend the universal. The boy seemed to have begun

[048-083] with the generals of life, and never to have concerned himself with the

[048-084] particulars. To him the houses, the willows, the obscure fields beyond,

[048-085] were apparently regarded not as brick residences, pollards, meadows;

[048-086] but as human dwellings in the abstract, vegetation, and the wide dark

[048-087] world.

[048-088] He found the way to the little lane, and knocked at the door of Jude’s

[048-089] house. Jude had just retired to bed, and Sue was about to enter her

[048-090] chamber adjoining when she heard the knock and came down.

[048-091] “Is this where Father lives?” asked the child.

[048-092] “Who?”

[048-093] “Mr. Fawley, that’s his name.”

[048-094] Sue ran up to Jude’s room and told him, and he hurried down as soon as

[048-095] he could, though to her impatience he seemed long.

[048-096] “What—is it he—so soon?” she asked as Jude came.

[048-097] She scrutinized the child’s features, and suddenly went away into the

[048-098] little sitting-room adjoining. Jude lifted the boy to a level with

[048-099] himself, keenly regarded him with gloomy tenderness, and telling him he

[048-100] would have been met if they had known of his coming so soon, set him

[048-101] provisionally in a chair whilst he went to look for Sue, whose

[048-102] supersensitiveness was disturbed, as he knew. He found her in the dark,

[048-103] bending over an arm-chair. He enclosed her with his arm, and putting

[048-104] his face by hers, whispered, “What’s the matter?”

[048-105] “What Arabella says is true—true! I see you in him!”

[048-106] “Well: that’s one thing in my life as it should be, at any rate.”

[048-107] “But the other half of him is—_she_! And that’s what I can’t bear! But

[048-108] I ought to—I’ll try to get used to it; yes, I ought!”

[048-109] “Jealous little Sue! I withdraw all remarks about your sexlessness.

[048-110] Never mind! Time may right things… And Sue, darling; I have an idea!

[048-111] We’ll educate and train him with a view to the university. What I

[048-112] couldn’t accomplish in my own person perhaps I can carry out through

[048-113] him? They are making it easier for poor students now, you know.”

[048-114] “Oh you dreamer!” said she, and holding his hand returned to the child

[048-115] with him. The boy looked at her as she had looked at him. “Is it you

[048-116] who’s my _real_ mother at last?” he inquired.

[048-117] “Why? Do I look like your father’s wife?”

[048-118] “Well, yes; ’cept he seems fond of you, and you of him. Can I call you

[048-119] Mother?”

[048-120] Then a yearning look came over the child and he began to cry. Sue

[048-121] thereupon could not refrain from instantly doing likewise, being a harp

[048-122] which the least wind of emotion from another’s heart could make to

[048-123] vibrate as readily as a radical stir in her own.

[048-124] “You may call me Mother, if you wish to, my poor dear!” she said,

[048-125] bending her cheek against his to hide her tears.

[048-126] “What’s this round your neck?” asked Jude with affected calmness.

[048-127] “The key of my box that’s at the station.”

[048-128] They bustled about and got him some supper, and made him up a temporary

[048-129] bed, where he soon fell asleep. Both went and looked at him as he lay.

[048-130] “He called you Mother two or three times before he dropped off,”

[048-131] murmured Jude. “Wasn’t it odd that he should have wanted to!”

[048-132] “Well—it was significant,” said Sue. “There’s more for us to think

[048-133] about in that one little hungry heart than in all the stars of the sky…

[048-134] I suppose, dear, we must pluck up courage, and get that ceremony over?

[048-135] It is no use struggling against the current, and I feel myself getting

[048-136] intertwined with my kind. Oh Jude, you’ll love me dearly, won’t you,

[048-137] afterwards? I do want to be kind to this child, and to be a mother to

[048-138] him; and our adding the legal form to our marriage might make it easier

[048-139] for me.”

[048-140] IV

[048-141] Their next and second attempt thereat was more deliberately made,

[048-142] though it was begun on the morning following the singular child’s

[048-143] arrival at their home.

[048-144] Him they found to be in the habit of sitting silent, his quaint and

[048-145] weird face set, and his eyes resting on things they did not see in the

[048-146] substantial world.

[048-147] “His face is like the tragic mask of Melpomene,” said Sue. “What is

[048-148] your name, dear? Did you tell us?”

[048-149] “Little Father Time is what they always called me. It is a nickname;

[048-150] because I look so aged, they say.”

[048-151] “And you talk so, too,” said Sue tenderly. “It is strange, Jude, that

[048-152] these preternaturally old boys almost always come from new countries.

[048-153] But what were you christened?”

[048-154] “I never was.”

[048-155] “Why was that?”

[048-156] “Because, if I died in damnation, ’twould save the expense of a

[048-157] Christian funeral.”

[048-158] “Oh—your name is not Jude, then?” said his father with some

[048-159] disappointment.

[048-160] The boy shook his head. “Never heerd on it.”

[048-161] “Of course not,” said Sue quickly; “since she was hating you all the

[048-162] time!”

[048-163] “We’ll have him christened,” said Jude; and privately to Sue: “The day

[048-164] we are married.” Yet the advent of the child disturbed him.

[048-165] Their position lent them shyness, and having an impression that a

[048-166] marriage at a superintendent registrar’s office was more private than

[048-167] an ecclesiastical one, they decided to avoid a church this time. Both

[048-168] Sue and Jude together went to the office of the district to give

[048-169] notice: they had become such companions that they could hardly do

[048-170] anything of importance except in each other’s company.

[048-171] Jude Fawley signed the form of notice, Sue looking over his shoulder

[048-172] and watching his hand as it traced the words. As she read the

[048-173] four-square undertaking, never before seen by her, into which her own

[048-174] and Jude’s names were inserted, and by which that very volatile

[048-175] essence, their love for each other, was supposed to be made permanent,

[048-176] her face seemed to grow painfully apprehensive. “Names and Surnames of

[048-177] the Parties”—(they were to be parties now, not lovers, she thought).

[048-178] “Condition”—(a horrid idea)—“Rank or Occupation”—“Age”—“Dwelling

[048-179] at”—“Length of Residence”—“Church or Building in which the Marriage is

[048-180] to be solemnized”—“District and County in which the Parties

[048-181] respectively dwell.”

[048-182] “It spoils the sentiment, doesn’t it!” she said on their way home. “It

[048-183] seems making a more sordid business of it even than signing the

[048-184] contract in a vestry. There is a little poetry in a church. But we’ll

[048-185] try to get through with it, dearest, now.”

[048-186] “We will. ‘For what man is he that hath betrothed a wife and hath not

[048-187] taken her? Let him go and return unto his house, lest he die in the

[048-188] battle, and another man take her.’ So said the Jewish law-giver.”

[048-189] “How you know the Scriptures, Jude! You really ought to have been a

[048-190] parson. I can only quote profane writers!”
