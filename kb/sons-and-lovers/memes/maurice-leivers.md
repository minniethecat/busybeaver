## Introduction

Maurice Leivers is one of the sons of the Leivers family at [[Willey Farm]], brother to [[Miriam Leivers]], [[Agatha Leivers]], Edgar, and Geoffrey. A "big lad" of about twelve or thirteen when [[paul-morel|Paul]] first visits the farm (006-470), Maurice embodies the rough, physical, unreflective world of agricultural labour — a world that [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]], with her spiritual intensities, finds both alien and oppressive.

## Background and Family

Maurice is one of five Leivers brothers who work the farm alongside their father. The family, though not wealthy, maintains a small mixed farm with crops and livestock. Maurice, like his brothers, is absorbed in the physical work of the farm and possesses the boisterous, teasing manner typical of country boys. His [[intellect-and-intellectualism|education]], if any, is minimal — the farm is his world, and he seems content within it.

## Role in the Novel

Maurice appears only in a few brief scenes, but his function is clear: he represents the earthy, physical, unselfconscious life that [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] is simultaneously drawn to and repelled by. His teasing of her — pulling her hand towards a hen-coop, mocking her fear — crystallises the tension between [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]]'s spiritual yearnings and the brute facts of farm life. Maurice is not cruel; he is simply unaware that there might be a world beyond the physical, and his incomprehension of [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]]'s nature is, in its way, as wounding as deliberate cruelty would be.

## Relationships

**[[Miriam Leivers]]** — Maurice's relationship with [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] is defined by rough teasing and mutual incomprehension. He pulls her hand towards a hen-coop to make her touch the chickens, mocking her hesitation: "Now, [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]], you come an' 'ave a look" (006-486). When she refuses to climb a haystack, Geoffrey and Maurice both taunt her: "She niver durst do anything" (006-492). For Maurice, [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]]'s delicacy is absurd; for [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]], his roughness is an assault on her very being.

**The Leivers Brothers** — Maurice is part of a cohort of brothers — Edgar, Geoffrey, and the younger ones — who together constitute the masculine, physical world of [[willey-farm|Willey Farm]]. Their collective teasing of [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] (006-492–496) suggests a family dynamic in which the sensitive sister is perpetually outnumbered and outvoiced.

**[[Paul Morel]]** — Maurice's interactions with [[paul-morel|Paul]] are minimal but telling. During the excursion to Wingfield Manor (009-1264), Maurice is named among those who "stood aside" — a gesture of deference or exclusion that marks the social distance between the Leivers farm lads and the more cultivated [[paul-morel|Paul]].

## Key Scenes

- **The Hen-Coop Episode** (006-470–496): Maurice, described as a "big lad of twelve and thirteen," takes [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s hand and leads him to see the chickens at [[willey-farm|Willey Farm]]. He then turns on [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]], insisting she come and touch the birds. When she hesitates, the brothers mock her. Maurice's cry of "'The Lady of the Lake!' Yah!" (006-496) is a devastating parody of [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]]'s romantic aspirations, reducing her inner life to a schoolgirl fantasy.

- **The Pantry Raid** (007-169): Maurice, with characteristic directness, announces what food is "left in th' pantry" — the farm lad's perpetual concern with physical sustenance, contrasted implicitly with [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]]'s more rarified preoccupations.

- **Wingfield Manor** (009-1264): Maurice is among the group that visits the old manor, but he "stood aside" with the other farm lads as [[paul-morel|Paul]] and Clara move ahead. The scene marks the class and cultural boundary between the Leivers brothers and the more sophisticated [[paul-morel|Paul]].

## Character Analysis

Maurice, though barely sketched, is an important element in the novel's emotional ecology. He represents the world from which [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] is trying to escape — a world of physical labour, rough teasing, and the reduction of all experience to the tangible. His mockery of her as "The Lady of the Lake" is cruel in its accuracy: it names precisely the romantic idealism that isolates [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] from her family and, ultimately, from [[paul-morel|Paul]].

Lawrence, who grew up in a mining village with brothers and neighbours not unlike the Leivers lads, renders Maurice with neither sentimentality nor contempt. The farm boys are what they are: healthy, unreflective, absorbed in the physical world. Their teasing of [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] is not malice but a form of social policing — the enforcement of a norm from which [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]], by her very nature, deviates. The tragedy is not that Maurice is cruel, but that the world he represents has no place for the [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]]s of this world.
