# Paul and Baxter Dawes Fight

The fight between [[paul-morel|Paul Morel]] and [[baxter-dawes|Baxter Dawes]] is a dramatic confrontation in Chapter 13 of *Sons and Lovers*, in which [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s affair with [[clara-dawes|Clara Dawes]] erupts into physical violence with her estranged husband.

## Background

[[baxter-dawes|Baxter Dawes]] is Clara's husband, from whom she is separated. Though estranged, [[baxter-dawes|Dawes]] remains possessive and resentful of [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s relationship with Clara. The two men had a long-standing mutual hostility:

> Paul often thought of Baxter Dawes, often wanted to get at him and be friends with him. He knew that Dawes often thought about him, and that the man was drawn to him by some bond or other. And yet the two never looked at each other save in hostility. (013-014)

## The Pub Confrontation

The immediate trigger occurs at the Punch Bowl, where [[paul-morel|Paul]] is drinking with friends. [[baxter-dawes|Dawes]] enters and, recognizing [[paul-morel|Paul]] as the man seen leaving the theatre with Clara, begins taunting him publicly:

> "Oh, him in a bob-tailed evening suit, on the lardy-da!" sneered Dawes, jerking his head contemptuously at Paul. (013-034)

[[baxter-dawes|Dawes]] insults Clara, calling her a "tart" (013-044), and implies [[paul-morel|Paul]] is merely the latest in a line of her lovers. [[paul-morel|Paul]], humiliated and enraged, throws beer in [[baxter-dawes|Dawes]]'s face. [[baxter-dawes|Dawes]] then strikes [[paul-morel|Paul]], knocking him across the table. The pub confrontation ends with [[paul-morel|Paul]] being thrown out, but the conflict is far from over.

## The Fight

Days later, [[paul-morel|Paul]] encounters [[baxter-dawes|Dawes]] on a lonely path near the fields. The two men fight savagely. [[baxter-dawes|Dawes]], older and heavier, initially overpowers [[paul-morel|Paul]], but [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s desperate fury drives him on. The fight ends with [[baxter-dawes|Dawes]] falling and striking his head:

> He lay still, with tiny bits of snow tickling his face. It was pleasant to lie quite, quite still. (013-923)

[[paul-morel|Paul]], badly injured himself — his mouth bleeding, his body aching — crawls to a pond to wash, then staggers home:

> Walking blindly, every step making him sick with pain, he went back to the pond and washed his face and hands. The icy water hurt, but helped to bring him back to himself. (013-947)

## Aftermath

The fight marks a turning point for both men. [[baxter-dawes|Dawes]] is hospitalized with a damaged shoulder, and [[paul-morel|Paul]], though victorious, is shaken by the violence. [[gertrude-morel|Mrs. Morel]], already seriously ill, is distressed to learn of the brawl. The event forces [[paul-morel|Paul]] to confront the consequences of his relationship with Clara and his own capacity for violence. Later, [[paul-morel|Paul]] and [[baxter-dawes|Dawes]] achieve a hard-won reconciliation during [[baxter-dawes|Dawes]]'s convalescence.

## Significance

The fight represents the collision between [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s sensitive, artistic nature and the raw physicality of working-class masculinity. It also dramatizes the destructive consequences of the [[paul-morel|Paul]]-Clara relationship, and sets the stage for [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s eventual break with Clara and partial reconciliation with [[baxter-dawes|Dawes]].
