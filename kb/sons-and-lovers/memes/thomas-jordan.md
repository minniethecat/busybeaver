## Introduction

Thomas Jordan is the owner of [[Jordan's Surgical Appliance Factory]], the [[nottingham|Nottingham]] firm where [[Paul Morel]] secures his first employment and where [[Clara Dawes]] later returns to work. A small, ageing man of volcanic temperament, Jordan embodies the paternalistic industrial capitalism of the late-Victorian era — quick to anger, physically diminished, yet wielding absolute authority over his employees. His collision with an electric lamp becomes one of the novel's early moments of dark workplace comedy.

## Background and Family

Little is disclosed about Thomas Jordan's personal history beyond his long proprietorship of the surgical appliance business. He is old enough that [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s mother [[Gertrude Morel]] can recall him once asking _her_ for a reference for one of his employees (010-306). The firm bears his name — "Thomas Jordan and Son" — displayed ominously on a brass plate at the entrance (005-396). The name suggests a family business, though no "Son" ever appears in the narrative.

## Role in the Novel

Jordan functions as an emblem of the old industrial order against which [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s artistic sensibilities must struggle. He hires [[paul-morel|Paul]] as a clerk at fourteen, setting in motion the youth's entry into the working world. More significantly, [[jordans-surgical-appliance-factory|Jordan's]] factory becomes the crucible where [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s relationships with [[clara-dawes|Clara Dawes]] and [[Fanny]] (the hunchback) develop. Jordan himself appears primarily in moments of crisis: barking orders, colliding with machinery, and embo[[death|dying]] the brittle authority of a [[death|dying]] generation of mill-owners.

## Relationships

**[[Paul Morel]]** — Jordan is [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s first employer. [[paul-morel|Paul]] dreads his initial interview, searching anxiously for "Thomas Jordan and Son" among the [[nottingham|Nottingham]] factories (005-375–396). Over time, [[paul-morel|Paul]] becomes a "superior employee" (013-018), though he remains subject to [[jordans-surgical-appliance-factory|Jordan's]] explosive moods.

**[[Clara Dawes]]** — Jordan is Clara's employer both before and after her separation from [[Baxter Dawes]]. He holds a dim view of her husband, and Clara returns to [[jordans-surgical-appliance-factory|Jordan's]] having previously worked there before her marriage. Their relationship is purely professional, defined by the hierarchy of proprietor and employee.

**The Factory Workers** — Jordan rules his workforce through fear and caprice. His signature mode of address is to "snap" at his employees (013-272, 013-275, 013-290). The workers tolerate rather than respect him, and his authority is undercut by his physical frailty.

## Key Scenes

- **[[paul-morel|Paul]]'s Interview** (005-375–396): The young [[paul-morel|Paul]], seeking his first job, searches anxiously for Thomas Jordan's firm among the [[nottingham|Nottingham]] factories. The "ominous" brass plate reading "Thomas Jordan and Son" looms before him.

- **The Collision** (013-266–303): In one of the novel's most vivid workplace scenes, Jordan storms out of his glass office to confront [[paul-morel|Paul]]. Before anyone can intervene, the half-blind old man collides with the electric lamp — "a little man with grey hair and a little white moustache, thin, very erect in his bearing" — and crashes to the floor, shaken and bruised but not seriously hurt. The scene is both farcical and pitiful, revealing [[jordans-surgical-appliance-factory|Jordan's]] physical vulnerability beneath his bluster.

- **[[baxter-dawes|Dawes]]'s Taunt** (014-095): [[baxter-dawes|Dawes]] sneeringly asks [[paul-morel|Paul]] why he does not ask "Thomas Jordan" to lend him his wife — a crude remark that links [[jordans-surgical-appliance-factory|Jordan's]] name to the simmering sexual rivalry between [[baxter-dawes|Dawes]] and [[paul-morel|Paul]] over Clara.

## Character Analysis

Thomas Jordan belongs to a literary tradition of irascible, physically frail employers whose authority is simultaneously absolute and absurd. Lawrence renders him with satirical precision: the glass-panelled office from which he "pops" like a jack-in-the-box, the hair-trigger temper, the comic collision with the very machinery his money has purchased. Yet the satire carries an undercurrent of pathos. Jordan is not merely ridiculous; he is old, his eyesight failing, his body no longer answering to his will. He represents an industrial order that is itself stumbling towards obsolescence, colliding with the modernity it cannot quite see.

The factory that bears his name will outlast him, but the world it represents — of absolute proprietorial authority, of "superior employees" who know their place — is being eroded by forces Jordan can neither understand nor control. [[paul-morel|Paul]], the sensitive artist-clerk, is the herald of that new order.
