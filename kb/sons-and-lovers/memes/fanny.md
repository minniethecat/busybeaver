## Introduction

Fanny is a young woman employed in the finishing-off room at [[Jordan's Surgical Appliance Factory]], where [[Paul Morel]] also works. A hunchback, she is physically fragile and emotionally sensitive, often reduced to tears by the casual cruelties of factory life. Despite her suffering, she possesses a gentle spirit and a fine singing voice, and she develops a tender, protective affection for [[paul-morel|Paul]].

## Background and Family

Little is revealed about Fanny's family or origins. She appears only in the context of [[jordans-surgical-appliance-factory|the factory]], and her personal life outside [[jordans-surgical-appliance-factory|Jordan's]] remains largely unknown. Her physical disability — described as a "long-legged hunchback" (005-901) — sets her apart from the other girls and makes her a target for mockery. She works on a high stool in the finishing-off room, where [[paul-morel|Paul]] first encounters her when he begins work at [[jordans-surgical-appliance-factory|Jordan's]].

## Role in the Novel

Fanny serves as a foil to the more assertive factory girls and provides a window into the emotional world of the working-class women at [[jordans-surgical-appliance-factory|Jordan's]]. Her relationship with [[paul-morel|Paul]] is marked by a quiet, yearning tenderness: she sings for him, confides in him, and half-jokingly tells him she will "haunt" him when she dies (005-1080). Through Fanny, Lawrence portrays the vulnerability of those whom industrial society marginalises — the physically different, the emotionally exposed.

## Relationships

**[[Paul Morel]]** — Fanny's most significant relationship. [[paul-morel|Paul]] is kind to her when others are cruel, and she responds with deep, unspoken affection. She sings for him in the finishing-off room and treasures his company. Her feelings for [[paul-morel|Paul]], though never openly declared, are evident in her tears, her blushes, and her half-serious comment about haunting him after death.

**The Factory Girls** — Fanny's relationships with the other girls at [[jordans-surgical-appliance-factory|Jordan's]] are defined by the casual bullying she endures. They tease her, criticise her singing, and mock her sensitivity. One girl dismisses her emotional songs with the cry: "Don't be so soft, Fanny!" (005-1059). Yet some, like the girl who calls for Fanny to "put it down for him," recognise her talent.

**[[Clara Dawes]]** — Clara treats Fanny with unfailing compassion, distinguishing herself from other factory workers who are less considerate. The narrator notes that "towards Fanny, the poor, overstrung hunchback, Clara was unfailingly compassionate" (010-419).

## Key Scenes

- **First Appearance** (005-896–912): [[paul-morel|Paul]] meets the hunchback woman on a high stool, who turns and reveals a "worn" but sweet face. She is almost in tears at his initial, unintentional slight.

- **Singing in the Finishing-off Room** (005-1050–1085): Fanny sings "Arthur's Seat" for [[paul-morel|Paul]], her voice described as having "a certain woodland poetry, the poetry of a wild thing." Afterward, she tells [[paul-morel|Paul]] she will haunt him when she dies — a remark that reveals her deep, unspoken attachment.

- **The Night-Shift Episode** (010-591–646): Fanny comes to [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s room at night during a work emergency, flushed and emotional. When she blurts out personal remarks about [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s private life, he cuts her off sharply: "No, Fanny" (010-638), and she is overcome with emotion.

- **The Accident** (013-257–303): Fanny is involved in the crisis when [[thomas-jordan|Thomas Jordan]] collides with the electric light. She relays urgent messages between [[paul-morel|Paul]] and others on [[jordans-surgical-appliance-factory|the factory]] floor.

## Character Analysis

Fanny represents a recurring Lawrentian theme: the wounded, sensitive individual crushed by an indifferent world. Her hunchback is both a literal physical deformity and a metaphor for the emotional vulnerability that industrial society exploits and discards. Her singing — "the poetry of a wild thing" — suggests a soul that remains untouched by the ugliness of factory life, a natural beauty that industrialism cannot fully destroy.

Lawrence uses Fanny sparingly but effectively: she appears only in a handful of scenes, yet each appearance deepens the novel's emotional register. Her unrequited tenderness for [[paul-morel|Paul]] mirrors the larger pattern of women who love [[paul-morel|Paul]] without being able to possess him, and her fragility underscores the cost of a world that values utility over sensitivity.
