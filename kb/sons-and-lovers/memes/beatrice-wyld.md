## Introduction

Beatrice Wyld is a young factory worker at [[jordans-surgical-appliance-factory|Jordan's Surgical Appliance Factory]], described as "a young woman of about twenty-two, small and pale, hollow-eyed, yet with a relentless look about her" (008-873). She is a friend of the [[walter-morel|Morel]] family and a vivid presence in [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s social circle. With her sharp tongue, teasing manner, and earthy working-class humor, Beatrice functions as a foil to the intense, spiritually serious [[miriam-leivers|Miriam Leivers]]. She also appears in Chapter 9 as one of the "Spiral" girls, providing a glimpse of [[jordans-surgical-appliance-factory|the factory]]'s social world.

## Role in the Novel

Beatrice's most significant scene occurs in Chapter 8, when she arrives unexpectedly at the [[walter-morel|Morel]] house while [[paul-morel|Paul]] and [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] are alone together. She immediately targets [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] with wicked humor, demanding to see her muddy boots: "Glory! You're a positive muck-heap" (008-882). Her teasing has a cruel edge — "Every one of [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s friends delighted in taking sides against her, and he left her in the lurch" (008-894) — but it also reveals the social dynamics that isolate [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] from [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s working-class world.

Beatrice's physical ease with [[paul-morel|Paul]] contrasts sharply with [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]]'s intense, wordless devotion. She playfully kisses him on the cheek, calling him "Sweet boy!" (008-949), and he responds with equal flirtation. When the bread [[paul-morel|Paul]] has been baking burns during their flirtatious exchange, Beatrice takes charge practically, grating the burnt crust away while delivering sardonic commentary: "I know why King Alfred burned the cakes. Now I see it!" (008-957). Her quick thinking saves [[paul-morel|Paul]] from his mother's displeasure, demonstrating that beneath the teasing exterior lies practical competence and genuine friendship.

She also appears briefly in Chapter 9 at [[jordans-surgical-appliance-factory|Jordan's]], where she is referred to as a "Spiral" girl (009-1159), part of the group of young women who work under [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s supervision. [[paul-morel|Paul]] "had always been fond of Beatrice Wyld," suggesting an ongoing, uncomplicated affection that contrasts with the emotional complexity of his relationships with [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] and Clara.

## Relationships

**[[paul-morel|Paul Morel]]** — They share an easy, flirtatious friendship. [[paul-morel|Paul]] is fond of her, and their banter has a natural, unforced quality. Beatrice is one of the few women in the novel with whom [[paul-morel|Paul]] interacts without the weight of romantic or spiritual expectation.

**[[miriam-leivers|Miriam Leivers]]** — Beatrice instinctively targets [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] with sarcasm and mockery. Her teasing of [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]]'s muddy boots and her mock-innocent questions about the nature of love expose the class and temperament gap between [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]]'s spiritual intensity and the working-class world of [[bestwood|Bestwood]].

**[[annie-morel|Annie Morel]]** — Beatrice is a friend of the [[walter-morel|Morel]] household and interacts comfortably with Annie, who arrives home to find her covering for [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s burnt bread.
