## Introduction

Dr. Ansell is the physician who attends [[gertrude-morel|Gertrude Morel]] during her final illness in [[sheffield|Sheffield]], where she collapses while visiting her daughter Annie. He is described as "a shrewd, lovable man" (013-1132) who handles the difficult diagnosis of [[gertrude-morel|Mrs. Morel]]'s tumour with professional candor and human warmth. In Chapter 14, he plays a surprising role as a catalyst for reconciliation between [[paul-morel|Paul Morel]] and his rival [[baxter-dawes|Baxter Dawes]].

## Role in the Novel

Dr. Ansell first appears in Chapter 13, called urgently when [[gertrude-morel|Mrs. Morel]] collapses at Annie's house in [[sheffield|Sheffield]]. He diagnoses a large tumour — likely cancerous — that the family's [[nottingham|Nottingham]] physician, Dr. Jameson, had missed, having "treated her for heart and indigestion" (013-1211) because [[gertrude-morel|Mrs. Morel]] "never told Dr. Jameson about the lump" (013-1211). Dr. Ansell's forthright diagnosis shatters [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s world, forcing the family to confront the reality of [[gertrude-morel|Mrs. Morel]]'s terminal condition. His honesty, though painful, is depicted as a form of compassion: he does not conceal the severity of the situation from the young man who questions him with desperate intensity.

In Chapter 14, Dr. Ansell re-emerges in an unexpected role. Visiting [[paul-morel|Paul]] in [[sheffield|Sheffield]], he mentions that a man from [[nottingham|Nottingham]] — [[baxter-dawes|Baxter Dawes]] — is in the fever hospital with typhoid, noting that [[baxter-dawes|Dawes]] "doesn't seem to have many belongings in this world" (014-002). This casual revelation sets in motion the novel's most significant reconciliation. Dr. Ansell acts as an intermediary, conveying [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s offer to visit [[baxter-dawes|Dawes]] and later providing [[paul-morel|Paul]] with the hospital card that gains him entry. His medical authority and quiet humanity create the conditions for the two rivals to meet not as enemies but as two men confronting suffering and [[death|mortality]].

Dr. Ansell also serves as a practical advisor to the [[walter-morel|Morel]] family: he recommends a convalescent home at Seathorpe for [[baxter-dawes|Dawes]]'s recovery, and suggests the examination with Dr. Jameson for [[gertrude-morel|Mrs. Morel]]'s definitive diagnosis. His presence in the novel, though brief, is pivotal — he is the figure who enables the truth to be spoken about [[gertrude-morel|Mrs. Morel]]'s cancer and who opens the door to the novel's culminating act of human connection between [[paul-morel|Paul]] and [[baxter-dawes|Dawes]].

## Relationships

**[[gertrude-morel|Gertrude Morel]]** — His patient. Dr. Ansell treats [[gertrude-morel|Mrs. Morel]] with professional competence and the respect due to her dignity, even as he must deliver a devastating prognosis.

**[[paul-morel|Paul Morel]]** — Dr. Ansell responds to [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s anxious questioning with direct honesty, neither shielding him from the truth nor delivering it with unnecessary brutality. Later, he trusts [[paul-morel|Paul]] enough to facilitate the hospital visit to [[baxter-dawes|Dawes]].

**[[baxter-dawes|Baxter Dawes]]** — Dr. Ansell is [[baxter-dawes|Dawes]]'s attending physician at the fever hospital. He describes [[baxter-dawes|Dawes]] as having been "a fine fellow, physically" (014-002) and is frustrated by the patient's sullen withdrawal, but continues to advocate for his recovery.

**[[annie-morel|Annie Morel]]** — He works with Annie, who had been caring for [[gertrude-morel|Mrs. Morel]], and communicates the medical situation to the family through her.
