## Introduction

Miss Limb is a young woman who lives in the countryside near [[willey-farm|Willey Farm]], the daughter of Limb, "a thin, swarthy man of forty, tenant" (009-691) of a farm in the district. She appears briefly in Chapter 9 during a walk with [[miriam-leivers|Miriam Leivers]] and [[paul-morel|Paul Morel]], extending a generous invitation that illuminates the social geography of the farming community surrounding the Leivers' land. Her presence is minor but offers a glimpse of the wider rural world beyond the intense [[paul-morel|Paul]]-[[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] dyad (009-734).

## Role in the Novel

Miss Limb enters the narrative when [[paul-morel|Paul]] and [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] encounter her father "at the wood's edge" (009-691). Limb pulls up before them and offers to show them the way, pointing out features of the local landscape (009-704, 009-710). When they reach the Limb household, "Miss Limb looked up" (009-734) from her work and joins the conversation.

Miss Limb is immediately warm and welcoming. Of a farm dog, she reassures them: "'He's quite gentle,' said Miss Limb" (009-743). When [[paul-morel|Paul]] expresses interest in fishing, she extends an open invitation: "'Because if you do you might come and fish any time,' said Miss Limb" (009-754). This spontaneous hospitality reflects the easy sociability of the farming community. [[paul-morel|Paul]] later reports the offer to [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]]: "'Miss Limb was just saying we could come when'" (009-768), and Miss Limb adds a practical qualification: "'Of course there's the farm-hands,' said Miss Limb" (009-770) — an acknowledgment that even generous hospitality exists within the constraints of a working farm's daily life.

[[miriam-leivers|Miriam]], characteristically self-conscious, asks [[paul-morel|Paul]]: "'You mean Miss Limb?'" (009-776) — needing to identify the woman by name before engaging with the substance of the invitation, a small instance of [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]]'s difficulty with spontaneous social warmth.

Miss Limb's function in the novel is primarily that of a foil: her easy, uncomplicated friendliness contrasts with [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]]'s intense self-awareness and [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s restless dissatisfaction. She represents a mode of rural social life — open, generous, unneurotic — that the Leivers household, for all its hospitality, cannot match because of the emotional currents that run through it (009-754).

## Relationships

**Limb** — Her father, a tenant farmer, "a thin, swarthy man of forty" (009-691). He guides [[paul-morel|Paul]] and [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] through the woods before they encounter his daughter.

**[[miriam-leivers|Miriam Leivers]]** — Miss Limb extends hospitality to [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] as part of the local farming network. [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] receives the invitation with characteristic hesitation, asking [[paul-morel|Paul]] to clarify "'You mean Miss Limb?'" (009-776) rather than simply accepting the offered warmth.

**[[paul-morel|Paul Morel]]** — Miss Limb's interaction with [[paul-morel|Paul]] centres on the promise of fishing — a masculine recreation that appeals to [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s love of outdoor activity. Her uncomplicated offer contrasts with the emotional demands of [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s relationship with [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] (009-754).
