# Moon and Stars

The Moon and Stars was a public house in [[bestwood|Bestwood]] where [[walter-morel|Walter Morel]] and other miners gathered to drink, socialize, and escape the hardships of mining life. It appears across multiple chapters of *Sons and Lovers* as a recurring social setting.

## Walter Morel and the Moon and Stars

The Moon and Stars is first mentioned early in the novel, when [[gertrude-morel|Mrs. Morel]] learns that her husband is not at work but instead "helping to wait" at the pub. A neighbour reports seeing him through the window:

> "He's helping to wait at the Moon and Stars. I seed him through that black tin stuff wi' holes in, on the window, wi' his sleeves rolled up." (001-148)

[[walter-morel|Morel]]'s work at the pub — likely in exchange for drink — reflects his prioritization of social drinking over family responsibilities. After a day there, he returns home "extraordinarily pleased with himself" (001-235), chattering away while his exhausted wife retreats to bed.

## As a Social Hub

The Moon and Stars serves as a gathering place for the mining community. [[walter-morel|Morel]] and his companions begin their drinking excursions there:

> At the Moon and Stars they had their first drink, then on to the Old Spot. (001-728)

This pattern — starting at the Moon and Stars before moving on to other establishments — establishes it as the central pub in the [[bestwood|Bestwood]] drinking circuit.

## Paul and the Moon and Stars

Later in the novel, [[paul-morel|Paul Morel]] follows in his father's footsteps by frequenting the same establishment. He plays billiards there with his friend Newton, and uses the pub as a social sphere separate from his relationships with [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] and his mother:

> he called and had a game of billiards with him in the Moon and Stars. If he gave to Miriam the excuse of his men friends, he felt quite justified. (011-547)

## Significance

The Moon and Stars represents the masculine, working-class social world of the mining community — a world that [[gertrude-morel|Mrs. Morel]] despises and from which she tries to distance her sons, yet one that [[paul-morel|Paul]] ultimately finds himself drawn into. It functions as a counterpoint to the domestic sphere dominated by [[gertrude-morel|Mrs. Morel]], and to the intellectual and spiritual world represented by [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]].
