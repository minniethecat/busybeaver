## Introduction

Pride operates as a double-edged force in D.H. Lawrence's *Sons and Lovers*. It is at once a source of strength — [[gertrude-morel|Mrs. Morel]]'s pride in [[paul-morel|Paul]] sustains her, and [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s pride in his art gives him identity — and a source of [[loneliness|isolation]], as wounded pride drives characters apart. "Her pride had been wounded inside her" [^1], Lawrence writes of Clara, capturing the way pride is experienced as a wound that must be healed. Pride in the novel is not the sin of arrogance but the vulnerable assertion of self-worth in a world that offers little validation.

## Maternal Pride

[[gertrude-morel|Mrs. Morel]]'s pride is focused entirely on her children, especially [[paul-morel|Paul]]. Her "heart was firm with pride and joy" [^2] at his successes, and this maternal pride is the emotional engine of the novel: it drives [[paul-morel|Paul]] to achieve but also chains him to his mother's expectations. [[gertrude-morel|Mrs. Morel]]'s pride is a survival mechanism — faced with a disappointing husband and a constricted life, she invests her self-worth in her children's futures. The pride is fierce, protective, and ultimately possessive.

## Artistic Pride

[[paul-morel|Paul]]'s pride is expressed through his painting. His artistic identity gives him a sense of worth that transcends his class origins. But his pride is also a form of defense — against his father's coarseness, against the mining world's indifference to beauty, against the women who demand more of him than he can give. When he speaks of his work, "his pride" is palpable [^3], and his art becomes the one arena where he can assert himself without guilt.

## Wounded Pride

The novel pays close attention to the aftermath of wounded pride. [[clara-dawes|Clara Dawes]], after being set aside by [[paul-morel|Paul]], experiences a pride that is both hurt and healed in the same gesture: "It healed her; it made her proud again" [^4]. The rhythm of pride wounded and restored is central to the novel's treatment of romantic relationships. [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]]'s pride, wounded by [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s rejection, turns into a spiritual bitterness; Clara's wounded pride finds healing in [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s renewed attention.
