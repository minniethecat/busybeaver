## Introduction

The courtship and marriage of Gertrude Coppard and [[walter-morel|Walter Morel]], recounted in the novel's opening chapters, is the founding event of *Sons and Lovers* — the origin from which all subsequent events flow. Lawrence presents this union with the compressed intensity of a prologue to tragedy: we see the attraction, the class difference, the early happiness, and the seeds of destruction, all within a few dozen pages. The [[walter-morel|Morel]]s' courtship is not merely backstory but a miniature of the novel's larger concerns: class mobility, sexual attraction, and the irreconcilable differences that destroy love.

## Narrative Context

Gertrude Coppard is twenty-three, from a respectable Congregationalist family of "good old burgher" stock (001-238). She meets [[walter-morel|Walter Morel]] at a Christmas party (001-298) when he is twenty-seven — a handsome, vigorous young miner with "wavy black hair" and a laugh that is "so often and so heartily" (001-303) given. She is drawn to his vitality, his difference from her own class, his physical presence.

## The Event

The courtship is swift and intense. Gertrude is struck by Walter's sensuality — "a certain subtlety" (001-328) in the young miner, a physical magnetism that her own class has trained out of its men. She is also warned: Walter's mother and sisters try to dissuade her, but she is willful and confident.

The marriage follows at the next Christmas (001-386). The early months are happy — Gertrude is "proud of him" (001-524), and Walter is tender, calling her "my beauty" (003-107). But the discovery of Walter's financial dishonesty — the unpaid bills, the lies about the house ownership — marks the first breach. "After this she scarcely [[passion|desire]]d him" (003-061), and the long decline of the marriage begins.

The children come quickly: William, then Annie, then [[paul-morel|Paul]], then Arthur. With each child, Gertrude's emotional investment shifts further from husband to offspring. Walter, excluded and resentful, turns to the pub. The pattern of the novel's family life is established before the end of the second chapter.

## Long-Term Consequences

The [[walter-morel|Morel]]s' failed marriage is the psychological foundation of everything that follows. [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s Oedipal attachment, his inability to love other women, his artistic ambition as escape — all trace back to the parents' union and its failure. The marriage is the novel's original wound, and every subsequent relationship is an attempt to heal or repeat it.

## Associated Characters

**[[gertrude-morel|Gertrude Morel]]**: She marries down, hoping to lift Walter up, and learns that class mobility is not a one-person project.
**[[walter-morel|Walter Morel]]**: He marries up, briefly, and spends the rest of his life falling back to where he started.
**The [[walter-morel|Morel]] children**: Products of a marriage that was failing before they were born.

## Critical Significance

The [[walter-morel|Morel]]s' courtship has been read as a class allegory — the respectable lower-middle-class woman drawn to working-class vitality, only to be repelled by working-class coarseness. But Lawrence resists schematic reading: Gertrude's attraction to Walter is genuine, and her revulsion is gradual and complex. The marriage fails not because of class difference alone but because of the specific personalities involved and the structural pressures — economic, social, psychological — that bear down on them.
