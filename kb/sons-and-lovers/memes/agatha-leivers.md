## Introduction

Agatha Leivers is the elder sister of [[Miriam Leivers]] and a school-teacher by profession. Fair-haired where [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] is dark, worldly where [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] is spiritual, Agatha represents everything [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] despises and secretly envies. The two sisters exist in a state of quiet, corrosive rivalry, their relationship defined by cutting remarks and mutual incomprehension. Through Agatha, Lawrence illustrates the tensions within the Leivers household and, by extension, the conflict between worldly practicality and romantic idealism that runs throughout the novel.

## Background and Family

Agatha is one of the Leivers children at [[Willey Farm]]; she is the eldest sister, older than [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]]. She works as a teacher at the British School, a position that gives her a measure of independence and worldliness unusual for a young woman of her class and era. The Leivers family, presided over by [[mrs-leivers|Mrs. Leivers]], is characterised by a peculiar blend of intellectual aspiration and rural [[loneliness|isolation]], and Agatha embodies its more pragmatic, outward-facing aspect.

## Role in the Novel

Agatha appears primarily as a foil to [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]]. Their sibling dynamic — Agatha's dismissive worldliness versus [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]]'s intense spiritual yearning — mirrors the broader thematic conflict between the material and the transcendent that structures the novel. Agatha's presence at [[willey-farm|Willey Farm]] also contributes to the atmosphere of quiet tension that characterises the Leivers household, where each member seems locked in a private drama of misunderstanding.

## Relationships

**[[Miriam Leivers]]** — Agatha's relationship with her younger sister is the most fully drawn sibling conflict in the novel. [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] considers Agatha "worldly" and resents her (007-1044), while Agatha regards [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] with cutting condescension. Their exchanges are brief but charged: Agatha's "Aren't you glad?" is delivered "cuttingly" (007-1076), and [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] parries her remarks "with much vehemence" (007-1118). The narrator notes that the sisters "did not talk much to each other" (007-1055), a silence more eloquent than speech.

**[[Paul Morel]]** — Agatha's opinion of [[paul-morel|Paul]] is filtered through [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]]'s devotion. She tells [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] that [[paul-morel|Paul]] is "as good as any tennis player" (008-912), a remark that, in its casualness, diminishes the intensity of [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]]'s feelings. Agatha seems to view [[paul-morel|Paul]] with the same detached worldliness she brings to all things, seeing him as a young man among other young men rather than as the transcendent figure [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] perceives.

**The Leivers Family** — As the eldest sister and a wage-earner, Agatha occupies a distinct position within the family. She is the first to dress and go downstairs (007-1094), suggesting a practical efficiency that sets her apart from the dreamier, more introspective [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]]. Her mother's attitudes are not directly explored, but the two elder Leivers daughters — Agatha and [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] — seem to have divided between them the competing impulses of the household: Agatha the practical, [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] the spiritual.

## Key Scenes

- **Preparing for the Party** (007-1046–1118): Agatha and [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] dress together in their bedroom before a social gathering. Their conversation is sparse and edged with hostility. Agatha has bought a new mirror while [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] uses an old one; Agatha dresses first and departs downstairs, leaving [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] alone. The scene captures the sisters' rivalry in miniature — the competition over mirrors, the cutting remarks, the charged silences.

- **The Tennis Match** (008-912): Agatha's offhand comment that [[paul-morel|Paul]] is "as good as any tennis player" reaches [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]], who receives it as a slight against the man she reveres. The remark demonstrates Agatha's role as a deflating force in [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]]'s romantic imagination.

## Character Analysis

Agatha is a minor but structurally crucial character. She represents, in compressed form, the "world" that [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] cannot abide and from which she retreats into mystical communion with nature. The fact that Agatha is a teacher — an educator of children, a participant in public life — underscores her difference from [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]], who is largely confined to [[willey-farm|Willey Farm]] and its private intensities.

Lawrence draws the sisters' relationship with his characteristic economy: a few scenes, a handful of cutting remarks, and a pervasive atmosphere of mutual incomprehension. Agatha is not a villain — she is simply equipped for a world that [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] finds intolerable. Her fair hair, her new mirror, her brisk efficiency are all indices of a temperament that accepts the material world on its own terms, and in this acceptance lies the implicit critique of [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]]'s otherworldliness that she embodies.

The Leivers sisters thus externalise a conflict that in other contexts is internal: the war between the soul's yearning for transcendence and the body's (and society's) demand for practical accommodation. Agatha is the champion of accommodation, and Lawrence, who understood both sides of this war, renders her with a sympathy that is all the more effective for being understated.
