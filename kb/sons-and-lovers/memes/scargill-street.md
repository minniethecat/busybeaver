## Introduction

Scargill Street is the address of the [[walter-morel|Morel]] family's second home in [[Bestwood]], to which they move from [[the-bottoms|the Bottoms]] when [[Paul Morel]] is still a child. Unlike the cramped, valley-bottom terrace of the Bottoms, the Scargill Street house offers "openness" and a view across the landscape towards the [[Derbyshire]] hills (004-768). It represents the family's modest but meaningful ascent — not out of the working class, but to its more respectable edge.

## Description in the Novel

Lawrence's description of the Scargill Street house emphasises space and light. The children "loved the Scargill Street house for its openness, for the great scallop of the view" (004-768). At night, they could look out across the valley and see "a tangle of lights below where [[minton|Minton]] pit lay, and another far away for [[selby|Selby]]" (004-728). The view, which encompasses both industrial lights and the dark mass of the [[derbyshire|Derbyshire]] hills, condenses the novel's symbolic geography: industry and nature, enclosure and freedom, visible from the same window.

The house is "an old one" (007-786), suggesting a dwelling that predates the industrial expansion that created the Bottoms. Its age and its position — higher on the hill, with a view — distinguish it from the company housing below. The [[walter-morel|Morel]]s do not own it, but they have earned it, and the move to Scargill Street is a marker of [[Gertrude Morel]]'s determination that her children will not grow up in the Bottoms.

## Symbolic Significance

Scargill Street occupies a crucial position in the novel's symbolic geography. If the Bottoms represents industrial entrapment and the [[derbyshire|Derbyshire]] hills represent transcendence, Scargill Street is the middle ground — the place where one can see both the mine and the hills, the industrial past and the possible future. The house's "openness" is literal (more windows, more space) and metaphorical (more possibility, more light).

The fact that the children "loved" the Scargill Street house (004-768) contrasts sharply with Gertrude's "sick" response to the Bottoms (001-057). The second home is a place of relative happiness, associated with the winter evenings when "Annie and [[paul-morel|Paul]] and Arthur loved the winter evenings" (004-722). It is the house of [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s adolescence, the house from which he ventures into [[nottingham|Nottingham]] and to [[willey-farm|Willey Farm]], the house where his artistic sensibility begins to form.

## Key Events

- **The Move** (004-093): The family moves from the Bottoms to Scargill Street, marking a new chapter in domestic life.

- **Winter Evenings** (004-722–768): The children gather in the Scargill Street kitchen, drawing, reading, and listening to their mother's stories — some of the novel's warmest domestic scenes.

- **William's Telegram** (006-830): A second, more urgent telegram about [[William Morel]]'s illness arrives at the Scargill Street house.

- **[[miriam-leivers|Miriam]]'s Visits** (007-786): [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] visits the Scargill Street house, and the contrast between the [[walter-morel|Morel]]s' working-class home and the Leivers' farm cottage becomes a subtle register of class difference.
