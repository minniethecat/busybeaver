## Introduction

The Christmas party described in the opening chapter of D.H. Lawrence's *Sons and Lovers* is the novel's foundational event: it is here that [[walter-morel|Walter Morel]], a young miner of twenty-seven, meets Gertrude Coppard, a woman from a higher social class. Their courtship at this dance leads to a marriage that will produce the novel's protagonist, [[paul-morel|Paul Morel]], and whose tragic estrangement will shape every character's emotional life.

## Narrative Context

Before the Christmas party, Lawrence sketches the prehistory of the [[walter-morel|Morel]] family with remarkable economy. The [[nottingham|Nottinghamshire]] coalfields have recently been discovered, drawing young laborers to the mining villages. Gertrude Coppard, "came of a good old burgher family" (001-249), is living in reduced circumstances after her father's engineering business failed. She is twenty-three, intelligent, and disappointed — a woman whose aspirations exceed her prospects.

## The Event

The Christmas party brings together the novel's two founding characters under the sign of celebration and misrecognition. [[walter-morel|Walter Morel]], at twenty-seven, is "a fine figure of a man" — vigorous, dancing, laughing, his physical vitality a stark contrast to the dour religious atmosphere of Gertrude's home life. Gertrude, reserved but drawn to his warmth, accepts his attentions.

Lawrence emphasizes the sensory appeal of Walter: his dancing, his laugh, his hands. Gertrude, who "loved dancing" and had been starved of physical joy, is undone by it. The party becomes a scene of seduction in which class, [[passion|desire]], and self-deception are inextricably entangled.

## Immediate Aftermath

The courtship proceeds rapidly after the Christmas party. Walter visits Gertrude at her home, and within months they are married. The first year of marriage brings a brief period of happiness: a cottage at [[bestwood|Bestwood]], the birth of their first child (William), and the excitement of a new life together. But the discovery that Walter has lied about owning their house — and about his financial status more generally — triggers the first rupture in their relationship:

> "She was to the miner that side of the family which she represented, but he was everything else. And she realized that she had married beneath her, and that he was a stranger to her."
> (001-322)

## Long-Term Consequences

The Christmas party is the seed from which the entire novel grows. The marriage it initiates produces [[paul-morel|Paul]], William, Annie, and Arthur; the estrangement that follows shapes [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s Oedipal bond with his mother and his inability to sustain relationships with either [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] or [[clara-dawes|Clara]]. The novel can be read as an extended working-through of the consequences of that single evening's dance.
