## Introduction

Jealousy is one of the most persistent emotional currents in D.H. Lawrence's *Sons and Lovers*, present from the novel's earliest chapters and intensifying through its central relationships. [[walter-morel|Walter Morel]] is jealous of his children's attachment to their mother; [[gertrude-morel|Gertrude Morel]] is jealous of any woman who claims [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s love; [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] and Clara are jealous of each other and of the mother who holds [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s deepest loyalty. Lawrence traces jealousy not as a simple moral failing but as a structural feature of love itself — the shadow side of attachment, the fear of being replaced.

## Manifestation in the Novel

The novel's first instance of jealousy is between father and son: "the father was jealous" of the child (001-502) — jealous of the mother's attention, of the bond that excludes him. This primal jealousy establishes a pattern that repeats throughout: love creates triangles, and triangles breed jealousy.

The mother's jealousy is the novel's most powerful instance. [[gertrude-morel|Gertrude Morel]] is jealous of [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] — [[paul-morel|Paul]] feels "her stiffen" when he mentions [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]]'s jealousy of her (010-742). And [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]], for her part, is "watchful and jealous" (007-1005) of anyone who claims [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s attention. [[gertrude-morel|Mrs. Morel]] "sat jealously in her own chair" (007-1229), guarding her position as [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s primary emotional attachment.

Between the brothers, jealousy is more muted but present. William and [[paul-morel|Paul]] are "unconsciously jealous" of each other (004-445, 004-446) — the rivalry of siblings for parental attention, complicated by affection and guilt.

The jealousy between the women in [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s life is the novel's most overt: [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] jealous of Clara, Clara jealous of [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]], both jealous of the mother who will not release her son. When Clara accuses [[paul-morel|Paul]] — "I believe you're jealous" (012-501) — the accusation rebounds: everyone in this novel is jealous, because love in this novel is a zero-sum game.

## Key Passages

- (001-502): "the father was jealous" — the primal jealousy.
- (004-445, 004-446): William and [[paul-morel|Paul]] "unconsciously jealous" — sibling rivalry.
- (007-1005): [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] "watchful and jealous" — possessive love.
- (007-1229): [[gertrude-morel|Mrs. Morel]] "sat jealously in her own chair" — maternal possessiveness.
- (010-742, 010-743): "They're jealous of you" — the triangle of jealousy.
- (012-501): "I believe you're jealous" — the accusation that rebounds.

## Character Perspectives

**[[gertrude-morel|Gertrude Morel]]**: Her jealousy is the structural center of the novel's emotional conflicts. She cannot bear to share [[paul-morel|Paul]], and her jealousy drives the wedge between him and every woman who might claim him.

**[[walter-morel|Walter Morel]]**: His jealousy of his children is the jealousy of a man who has lost his wife's love and sees it redirected to others. It is pathetic and destructive in equal measure.

**[[miriam-leivers|Miriam Leivers]]**: Her jealousy is spiritual — she is jealous not just of [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s time but of his soul, of the parts of him that belong to others and not to her.

**[[paul-morel|Paul Morel]]**: He is the object of everyone's jealousy and, in his own way, jealous of those who can love freely — his father, who does not feel the claims that torment [[paul-morel|Paul]].

## Critical Interpretation

Jealousy in *Sons and Lovers* has been read through psychoanalytic frameworks as a manifestation of the Oedipal triangle: the son is jealous of the father (who possesses the mother), the mother is jealous of the son's lovers (who threaten to take him away), and the lovers are jealous of the mother (who will not release him). The novel's jealousy is thus structural, built into the family romance that is its subject.

But jealousy in Lawrence is also a philosophical problem. If love is possession — and Lawrence suggests it often is — then jealousy is love's natural consequence. The novel's critique of jealousy is inseparable from its critique of possessive love, and [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s final act of walking away is an escape from the whole jealous economy.
