## Introduction

Lincoln appears in *Sons and Lovers* not as a setting for sustained narrative action but as a symbolically freighted destination — a place that [[Paul Morel]] hopes to take his mother, a city associated with the Lincolnshire coast he loves, and a landscape that embodies his artistic vision of escape and beauty. Lincoln and Lincolnshire are woven through [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s imaginative life: the "flat Lincoln coast" that inspired his sketches, the eternal quality of the Lincolnshire landscape, and the abortive plan to take [[Gertrude Morel]] to Lincoln when her health is failing. In each instance, Lincoln represents a world beyond the mining village — cultured, coastal, open — that [[paul-morel|Paul]] can yearn for but not, in the end, inhabit with his mother.

## Description in the Novel

The Lincolnshire landscape is introduced through [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s art. His "beloved sketches of the flat Lincoln coast" (007-252) are among his earliest artistic works, and the adjective "beloved" suggests that this is not merely a subject but a personal attachment. The flatness of the Lincolnshire coast — so different from the hilly [[derbyshire|Derbyshire]] landscape around [[Bestwood]] — captures something about [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s artistic sensibility: his attraction to openness, to horizons, to spaces uncluttered by the industrial detritus of the mining district.

Lincolnshire "meant to him the eternal" (007-1324), a word of unusual weight in Lawrence's lexicon. The coast's flatness, its exposure to sea and sky, evokes a timeless quality that contrasts with the historically specific and socially constrained world of the mining village. "He loved the Lincolnshire coast, and she loved the sea" (013-550) links [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s geographical passion with his mother's — a shared longing for maritime horizons that the landlocked Midlands cannot satisfy.

The journey that nearly happens — [[paul-morel|Paul]] taking his mother to Lincoln (009-901) — is charged with hope and pathos. "She was bright and" eager, but the trip does not materialise. Instead, when Gertrude's illness worsens, she is taken not to Lincoln or "the seaside place" first planned (013-1033) but to [[Sheffield]], to Annie's house, where she dies. The substitution — [[sheffield|Sheffield]] for Lincoln, industrial city for cathedral town, daughter's home for holiday destination — is one of the novel's quiet tragedies.

## Symbolic Significance

Lincoln and the Lincolnshire coast function as symbols of an escape that never quite happens. The flat coast that [[paul-morel|Paul]] sketches is the aesthetic correlative of his [[passion|desire]] for openness — a landscape without the vertical stratification of the mines (deep underground) and the hills (above). Lincolnshire offers a horizontal world, a world of sea-level vision and unbroken horizons. When [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s mother asks for the sea (013-550), she is asking for the same release her son seeks through art — and the novel's geography conspires to deny it.

The "eternal" quality [[paul-morel|Paul]] associates with Lincolnshire is also a spiritual claim. In a novel where religious faith is largely absent, the Lincolnshire landscape serves as a kind of secular sublime — a place where the self dissolves into sky and sea, a release from the insistent particularity of character and social relation that defines the domestic drama of [[bestwood|Bestwood]].

## Key Events

- **[[paul-morel|Paul]]'s Sketches** (007-252): [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s "beloved sketches of the flat Lincoln coast" are mentioned as part of his artistic development; the landscape is an early and enduring subject.

- **The Planned Lincoln Trip** (009-901): [[paul-morel|Paul]] takes his mother to Lincoln for a day trip, and "she was bright and" pleased — a brief moment of shared happiness in a relationship increasingly shadowed by illness.

- **The Substitution** (013-1033–1052): When Gertrude's illness worsens, the seaside holiday that was planned is replaced by [[sheffield|Sheffield]] — a symbolic downward revision of hope into necessity.

- **The Shared Longing** (013-550): [[paul-morel|Paul]] reflects that "He loved the Lincolnshire coast, and she loved the sea" — the mother-son bond expressed through a shared geographical imagination.

## Real-World Context

Lincoln is a historic cathedral city approximately forty miles east of Eastwood, situated on the River Witham. Its medieval cathedral — one of the finest in England — was a destination for day trips and excursions from the Midlands mining towns. The Lincolnshire coast lies further east, stretching from the Humber estuary to the Wash, and was accessible by rail from [[nottingham|Nottinghamshire]]. Lawrence's own knowledge of the Lincolnshire landscape came from trips to the coast during his youth; the "flat" quality that [[paul-morel|Paul]] sketches is an accurate descriptor of the low-lying coastal plain. The substitution of [[sheffield|Sheffield]] for Lincoln in the novel's closing chapters may reflect Lawrence's own understanding that the seaside holiday — that most English of working-class dreams — was often promised and rarely realised.
