## Introduction

Mr. Pappleworth is [[paul-morel|Paul Morel]]'s immediate supervisor in the spiral department at [[jordans-surgical-appliance-factory|Jordan's Surgical Appliance Factory]]. When [[paul-morel|Paul]] begins his employment at fourteen, Pappleworth is assigned to train him as a clerk. He is a man of fussy habits, short-lived authority, and routine commuter rhythms — a figure who embodies the trivial hierarchies and comic indignities of office life in a provincial manufacturing town. Pappleworth appears exclusively in Chapter 5 of the novel, functioning as a bridge between [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s protected home life and the world of adult work (005-720).

## Role in the Novel

Pappleworth is the gatekeeper of [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s initiation into working life at [[jordans-surgical-appliance-factory|Jordan's]]. On [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s first day, the cashier introduces him: "'Pappleworth. He's your boss, but he's not — '" (005-720), an ambiguous recommendation that sets the tone for Pappleworth's distracted, unthreatening authority.

Pappleworth arrives each morning "chewing a chlorodyne gum, at about twenty to ten" (005-747), establishing his habitual lateness and mild neuroticism. He gives [[paul-morel|Paul]] a stick of the gum — "Mr. Pappleworth gave a chew to his gum" (005-756) — in a gesture that mingles mentorship with self-absorption. His instruction methods are haphazard: he demonstrates copying letters and addresses, then "Mr. Pappleworth disappeared" (005-783), leaving [[paul-morel|Paul]] to manage on his own. When he "reappeared" (005-786), he fusses over the letters [[paul-morel|Paul]] has written (005-792).

Pappleworth's speech alternates between gruff authority and comic ineffectuality. He tells a factory girl, "'you'd better'" (005-799) — the broken phrase suggesting an authority that cannot complete itself. When asked about [[jordans-surgical-appliance-factory|the factory]] girls' noise, he tries "to be impressive for [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s benefit" (005-899) but fails, the girls mocking him by addressing [[paul-morel|Paul]] in a "contralto voice" (005-897). The girls below laugh at him "looking down on them from the far end" (005-892), exposing the thinness of his hierarchical position.

His daily departure, dictated by the suburban train schedule, mirrors his arrival: "Mr. Pappleworth disappeared to catch his train: he lived in the suburbs" (005-927). He returns "at twenty minutes to three" (005-938), a commuter whose life is defined by railway timetables and the mechanical repetition of office routine. Throughout the chapter, Pappleworth remains a figure of faint absurdity — not cruel, not kind, but absorbed in the small rituals of his own existence (005-880).

## Relationships

**[[paul-morel|Paul Morel]]** — Pappleworth is [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s first boss and trainer. He shows [[paul-morel|Paul]] the mechanical tasks of a clerk — copying, addressing envelopes, handling the tube system — with distracted indifference rather than genuine mentorship. [[paul-morel|Paul]] observes him with the sharp eye of a newcomer, noting his chlorodyne habit, his fussiness, and his failure to command respect from [[jordans-surgical-appliance-factory|the factory]] girls (005-756, 005-899).

**The Spiral Girls** — Pappleworth has an antagonistic relationship with [[jordans-surgical-appliance-factory|the factory]] women working below the clerks' office. He scolds them for noise but is ignored and parodied by them. A girl mimics him in a "contralto voice" (005-897), and the women laugh at his attempts at authority, underscoring his powerlessness within [[jordans-surgical-appliance-factory|the factory]]'s informal social order (005-892).
