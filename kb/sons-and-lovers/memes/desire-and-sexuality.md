## Introduction

Desire and [[passion|sexuality]] in D.H. Lawrence's *Sons and Lovers* represent the body's claim against the spirit's dominion. Where [[miriam-leivers|Miriam Leivers]] offers spiritual love that refuses the physical, where [[gertrude-morel|Gertrude Morel]] offers maternal love that claims the soul, [[clara-dawes|Clara Dawes]] offers [[passion|desire]] — raw, physical, and demanding. Lawrence's treatment of [[passion|sexuality]] was radical for its time and remains central to the novel's power: he insists that the body's [[passion|desire]]s are real, legitimate, and irreducible to spiritual or social categories.

## Manifestation in the Novel

The novel's treatment of [[passion|desire]] is most concentrated in the [[paul-morel|Paul]]-Clara relationship. "Sex [[passion|desire]] was a sort of" force (010-868) that [[paul-morel|Paul]] did not fully understand, and his relationship with Clara is his [[intellect-and-intellectualism|education]] in the body. He "did not positively [[passion|desire]] her" at first (010-872) — the [[passion|desire]] grows gradually, through proximity and physical intimacy, rather than through spiritual affinity or emotional connection.

The language of [[passion|desire]] in the novel is intensely physical. [[paul-morel|Paul]] is "obsessed by the [[passion|desire]] to kiss" (012-1149), Clara is "mad with [[passion|desire]] of him" (013-515), and [[passion|desire]] is a "dark, impersonal fire" (011-253) that burns through social convention. This "impersonal" quality is crucial — [[passion|desire]] in Lawrence is not about personality or compatibility but about the body's impersonal demand.

The tension between [[passion|desire]] and other forms of love structures the novel. [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s [[passion|desire]] for Clara is set against his spiritual bond with [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] and his emotional bond with his mother. Desire is the force that cuts through both: it cannot be sublimated into spiritual communion (as [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] would wish) or absorbed into maternal devotion (as Gertrude would wish). It is, for Lawrence, a separate faculty of the self, with its own logic and its own rights.

The novel also traces the failure of [[passion|desire]] to sustain relationship. [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s [[passion|desire]] for Clara burns hotly but does not last; their relationship ends not because they stop wanting each other but because [[passion|desire]] alone cannot build a life. "He was cured of his [[passion|desire]] for the lesser things" (010-886) — the language of cure suggests that [[passion|desire]] is something to be outgrown, a phase in [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s development rather than a permanent home.

Gertrude's marriage to [[walter-morel|Walter Morel]] provides the novel's cautionary tale about [[passion|desire]]. Their early attraction — the young miner "laughing so often and so heartily" (001-303), Gertrude drawn to his vitality — was genuine but insufficient. After marriage, "she scarcely [[passion|desire]]d him" (003-061), and [[passion|desire]]'s failure becomes the foundation of the novel's family tragedy.

## Key Passages

- (010-868): "Sex [[passion|desire]] was a sort of" — [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s conceptualization of physical [[passion|desire]].
- (010-872): "he did not positively [[passion|desire]] her" — the gradual awakening of [[passion|desire]].
- (011-022): "strong [[passion|desire]] battling with a still stronger" — [[passion|desire]] as internal conflict.
- (011-253): "the dark, impersonal fire of [[passion|desire]]" — [[passion|desire]] as impersonal force.
- (011-467): "and his [[passion|desire]]" — [[passion|desire]] as claim.
- (012-1149): "obsessed by the [[passion|desire]] to kiss" — [[passion|desire]] as obsession.
- (013-515): "she was mad with [[passion|desire]] of him" — [[passion|desire]] consuming Clara.
- (013-917): "the [[passion|desire]] was anguish in" — [[passion|desire]] as pain.
- (003-061): "she scarcely [[passion|desire]]d him" — the death of [[passion|desire]] in marriage.

## Character Perspectives

**[[paul-morel|Paul Morel]]**: His [[passion|desire]] is a discovery — first abstract ("Sex [[passion|desire]] was a sort of"), then personal, then overwhelming. His relationship with Clara teaches him that [[passion|desire]] has its own logic, irreducible to the spiritual love he shares with [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] or the maternal love that defines him.

**[[clara-dawes|Clara Dawes]]**: She is the novel's most fully embodied figure of [[passion|desire]]. Married, experienced, physically confident, she offers [[paul-morel|Paul]] a relationship defined by the body. Her [[passion|desire]] for him is consuming — "mad with [[passion|desire]]" — and her tragedy is that his [[passion|desire]], once awakened, cannot be contained by her.

**[[miriam-leivers|Miriam Leivers]]**: Her relationship to [[passion|desire]] is one of fear and sublimation. She wants [[paul-morel|Paul]] but cannot admit the physical dimension of her wanting; her love is spiritualized past the point where [[passion|desire]] can survive.

## Critical Interpretation

Lawrence's treatment of [[passion|sexuality]] was the primary reason for the novel's notoriety and its initial difficulties with publishers. The novel's frank treatment of "sex [[passion|desire]]" — including the infamous scene of [[paul-morel|Paul]] and Clara's lovemaking by the river — violated Edwardian conventions and established Lawrence as a serious writer willing to explore what other novelists evaded.

Psychoanalytic critics have read the novel's treatment of [[passion|desire]] through the Oedipus complex: [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s [[passion|desire]] for Clara is a displacement of his forbidden [[passion|desire]] for his mother. Feminist critics have offered more ambivalent readings, noting that Clara, for all her physical confidence, remains an instrument of [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s development — [[passion|desire]] teaches him something about himself, but what it teaches her is less clear.
