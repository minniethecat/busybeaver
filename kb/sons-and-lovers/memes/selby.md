## Introduction

Selby is a mining settlement in the brook valley near [[Bestwood]], established as one of the satellite communities of the [[carston-waite-and-co|Carston, Waite and Co.]] mining operations. The novel's opening geographical survey situates Selby precisely within the industrial landscape: it lies in "the valleys of the brooks from Selby and [[nuttall|Nuttall]]" (001-025), where new mines were sunk in the boom years of the [[nottingham|Nottinghamshire]] coalfield. Smaller and less narratively prominent than [[bestwood|Bestwood]], Selby nonetheless appears at telling moments — a place name that anchors conversations about work, obligation, and the circumscribed geography of the mining community.

## Description in the Novel

Lawrence introduces Selby as part of the panoramic opening chapter, mapping the industrial landscape with the precision of a surveyor and the sensibility of a native. The brook valley stretches "north to Beggarlee and Selby, that looks over at Crich and the hills of [[derbyshire|Derbyshire]]" (001-031), and "the climb of the valley towards Selby" (001-042) is part of the natural topography that the mines have reshaped.

The village makes its most memorable appearance in a moment of domestic comedy and tension. [[William Morel]], chafing against his father's authority, receives a sharp retort from [[Gertrude Morel]]: "If I _wanted_ you to go to Selby on Friday night, I can imagine what you'd say" (008-1175). The line is revealing: Selby is invoked as the kind of inconvenient errand a parent might ask of a child — a place close enough to be plausible, distant enough to be an imposition. It is a measure of the [[walter-morel|Morel]]s' world that Selby, barely a few miles distant, constitutes "going somewhere."

From the heights above [[bestwood|Bestwood]], "the farthest tiny lights" visible "opposite for Selby" (004-729) mark the village at night, a distant constellation of domesticity. The "stark houses" of Selby on the hill (007-623) present an image of exposed, unsoftened working-class habitation — no romantic haze here, just the hard fact of miners' dwellings against the sky.

## Symbolic Significance

Selby functions as a measure of distance — geographical and psychological — in the novel's emotional economy. When [[gertrude-morel|Mrs. Morel]] uses Selby as an example of an inconvenient request, she is mapping the boundaries of the family's world in terms that everyone understands. Selby is not a place of escape (like [[Sheffield]]) or culture (like [[Nottingham]]); it is the next village over, a place of routine, obligation, and the unglamorous everyday. In a novel preoccupied with flight — to [[london|London]], to the Continent, to the sea — Selby is the destination one does not choose, the errand one resents.

## Key Events

- **The Opening Survey** (001-025–042): Selby is situated in the novel's opening geographical exposition, part of the brook valley system that defined the mining industry's expansion.

- **[[gertrude-morel|Mrs. Morel]]'s Reproach** (008-1175): Gertrude invokes Selby in a moment of domestic tension with William, using the nearby village as shorthand for filial obligation and inconvenience.

- **Night View from [[bestwood|Bestwood]]** (004-729): The lights of Selby are visible from the heights, a re[[intellect-and-intellectualism|mind]]er of the interconnected mining communities that surround [[bestwood|Bestwood]].

## Real-World Context

Selby is likely a fictional composite based on one of the several mining villages in the Erewash Valley near Eastwood, Lawrence's birthplace. The name itself — shared with a Yorkshire town — is applied here to a [[nottingham|Nottinghamshire]] colliery settlement, suggesting Lawrence's freedom in fictionalising the geography of his youth. The pattern of brook-valley mining settlements described in the novel's opening accurately reflects the historical development of the [[nottingham|Nottinghamshire]] coalfield in the mid-nineteenth century, when [[carston-waite-and-co|Carston, Waite and Co.]] (a fictional stand-in for Barber, Walker and Co.) sank pits along the watercourses.
