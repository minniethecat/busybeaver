## Introduction

Strelley Mill Farm is the farmstead run by the Leivers family, situated in the countryside near [[Bestwood]]. It is closely associated with [[Miriam Leivers]] and her family, and functions as a pastoral counterpoint to the industrial landscape of the mining village. For [[Paul Morel]], Strelley Mill is a place of escape — from the cramped domesticity of the [[walter-morel|Morel]] household, from the grime of [[the-coal-mine|the pit]] — and a setting where his relationship with [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] unfolds against a backdrop of agricultural life and natural beauty.

## Description in the Novel

Strelley Mill appears in connection with a walk [[paul-morel|Paul]] and [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] take together. "They set off down to Strelley Mill Farm" (009-663) — the preposition "down" suggesting the farm lies in the valley below the village. The farm is described as a working agricultural enterprise, "which he ran as a cattle-raiser" (009-692), anchoring it in the real economy of the countryside rather than in pastoral cliché.

The farm's name — Strelley Mill — evokes a vanished watermill, suggesting a longer agricultural history that predates the mining industry. In a landscape dominated by colliery headstocks and railway lines, the mill farm represents continuity with an older, rural England. This temporal layering — the mill that was, the farm that is, the mine that has taken over the valley — is part of Lawrence's broader depiction of the industrial Midlands as a palimpsest of successive ways of life.

## Symbolic Significance

Strelley Mill embodies the pastoral alternative that [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] represents for [[paul-morel|Paul]]. Where [[Bestwood]] is coal, darkness, and domestic tension, Strelley Mill is fields, cattle, and the rhythms of agricultural labour. The farm is the world [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] belongs to — a world of religious devotion, natural beauty, and sexual reticence — and [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s visits there are a negotiation between his attraction to that world and his growing sense that it cannot contain him.

The mill in the farm's name — a structure that harnesses natural forces (water) for productive work — is an apt metaphor for the relationship Lawrence depicts between [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] and [[paul-morel|Paul]]. She offers him a form of spiritual and emotional sustenance, but the mill's wheel turns too slowly for his restless energy.

## Key Events

- **The Walk to Strelley Mill** (009-663): [[paul-morel|Paul]] and [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]] set off together to the farm, a walk that is both a rural excursion and a stage in their developing intimacy.

- **The Cattle Farm** (009-692): The farm is described as a cattle-raising operation, grounding it in agricultural reality rather than sentimental pastoralism.

## Associated Characters

- [[Miriam Leivers]] — the farm is her family home and the world she belongs to

- The Leivers family — the family who run the farm, including [[Edgar Leivers]], [[Geoffrey Leivers]], and [[Agatha Leivers]]

## Real-World Context

Strelley Mill is likely based on one of the farms in the countryside near Eastwood. The name "Strelley" survives as a [[nottingham|Nottinghamshire]] village name, and Lawrence's use of it for the fictional farm follows his practice of borrowing real place names for fictional locations. The combination of arable farming and cattle-raising described in the novel was typical of [[nottingham|Nottinghamshire]] mixed agriculture in the early twentieth century, and the proximity of working farms to mining villages — with miners' children often working on farms during harvest — was a feature of the industrial Midlands landscape that Lawrence knew intimately.
