## Introduction

Jordan's Surgical Appliance Factory is the [[nottingham|Nottingham]] workplace where [[paul-morel|Paul Morel]] is employed as a clerk and designer, and where he meets and pursues [[clara-dawes|Clara Dawes]]. Owned by [[thomas-jordan|Thomas Jordan]], the factory produces surgical appliances and serves as the backdrop for much of the novel's middle section — the site of flirtation, class tension, and the daily rhythms of Edwardian industrial life.

## Description in the Novel

Lawrence depicts Jordan's with precise industrial realism. Clara works at a spinning jenny producing elastic stockings; [[paul-morel|Paul]] designs and corresponds in the office upstairs. The factory's hierarchy — [[thomas-jordan|Thomas Jordan]] in his glass office, the clerks, the factory girls at their benches — maps the class structure of Edwardian [[nottingham|Nottingham]]. The noise of machinery, the smell of materials, and the social dynamics of the factory floor are rendered with the authority of Lawrence's own experience working at a similar establishment.

## Role in the Narrative

Jordan's serves several narrative functions. It is where [[paul-morel|Paul]] establishes himself as a working man distinct from his miner father, earning wages through skill rather than physical labor. It is also the setting for his pursuit of Clara — their charged conversations across the factory floor, [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s approach to her bench (010-550), and the gossip their relationship generates among the other workers (013-546).

The factory also provides the setting for the conflict between [[paul-morel|Paul]] and [[baxter-dawes|Baxter Dawes]], Clara's estranged husband, who works as a smith at Jordan's. Their rivalry culminates in a physical fight that originates in the tensions of the workplace.

## Associated Characters

## Symbolic Significance

Jordan's represents the intermediate space of [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s class position — not [[the-coal-mine|the pit]] where his father works, not the art school he aspires to, but a place of respectable clerical labor that enables his artistic ambitions. The factory's name — "Surgical Appliance" — carries an ironic echo of the novel's concern with bodies in damage and repair.
