## Introduction

Polly is one of the spiral girls — the young working-class women employed in the spiral department of [[jordans-surgical-appliance-factory|Jordan's Surgical Appliance Factory]] — who populate [[paul-morel|Paul Morel]]'s early working life. Described as "the little brisk" girl (005-1006), with "her dark eyes flashing" (005-828), Polly possesses a quick, combative energy that enlivens [[jordans-surgical-appliance-factory|the factory]] floor. She is one of the girls to whom [[paul-morel|Paul]] feels a genuine belonging, a representative of the unpretentious working-class camaraderie that offers him an alternative to both his mother's fierce love and [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]]'s spiritual intensity (005-1022).

## Role in the Novel

Polly appears exclusively in Chapter 5, during the extended sequence that narrates [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s initiation into working life at [[jordans-surgical-appliance-factory|Jordan's]]. She is first heard before she is seen: "'there's Polly crying out'" (005-804), Pappleworth tells [[paul-morel|Paul]] — her voice presaging her impetuous personality. When Pappleworth delivers the finished work to the girls below, "Polly, flying at him, her dark eyes flashing" (005-828), she attacks the package with characteristic energy, her physicality conveying the restless vitality of [[jordans-surgical-appliance-factory|the factory]]'s female workforce.

Polly's verbal style is sharp and combative. When Pappleworth uses the phrase "'Here y'are!'" she retorts: "'I think it is "here you are"!' exclaimed Polly" (005-823) — mocking his diction with the precision of someone who has heard it too many times. She repeats phrases with ironic emphasis: "'As we did the last!' repeated Polly" (005-831), and when re[[intellect-and-intellectualism|mind]]ed that work hours have begun, she fires back: "'It was time for work some time back,' said Polly, marching away with" (005-836) a flourish of offended dignity. Her exchanges with authority have the rhythm of a well-rehearsed comedy routine, suggesting years of factory-floor banter.

The narrator offers a compact summary of her place in [[paul-morel|Paul]]'s emotional landscape. The girls in the spiral room "were uninteresting" (005-1006) to him, "[but] Polly, the little brisk" girl, was different — "Polly he felt he belonged to" (005-1022). This sense of belonging, rare for [[paul-morel|Paul]], places Polly in a select group of people with whom he feels an uncomplicated, unanalyzed connection. Later, when conflicts arise among the girls, [[paul-morel|Paul]] finds himself mediating: "he had to plead her case with Polly" (005-1086), suggesting that Polly's sharpness could cut both ways and that [[paul-morel|Paul]] had learned to navigate the social dynamics of [[jordans-surgical-appliance-factory|the factory]] floor.

## Relationships

**Pappleworth** — Polly's interactions with the supervisor are defined by good-natured insubordination. She corrects his grammar, parodies his manner, and refuses to be intimidated by his authority (005-823, 005-828).

**[[paul-morel|Paul Morel]]** — [[paul-morel|Paul]] feels a sense of belonging to Polly that is rare for him (005-1022). Unlike his relations with the women who dominate his emotional life — [[gertrude-morel|Mrs. Morel]], [[miriam-leivers|Miriam]], Clara — his connection with Polly requires nothing of him beyond presence and participation in the shared rhythms of factory work.

**The Spiral Girls** — Polly is one of a collective of working-class women who form the social foundation of the spiral department. Alongside [[louie-travers|Louie Travers]] and others, she represents the energetic, unselfconscious femininity that contrasts with [[clara-dawes|Clara Dawes]]'s self-conscious striving for refinement (005-1006).
