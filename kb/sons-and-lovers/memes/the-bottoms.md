## Introduction

The Bottoms is the terrace of miners' cottages in [[Bestwood]] where [[Walter Morel]] and [[Gertrude Morel]] raise their family during the novel's early chapters. Built by the mining company to house its workers, the Bottoms is a place of cramped domesticity, neighbourly proximity, and industrial ugliness — "twelve dwellings, a block" (001-037) — that Gertrude, with her middle-class aspirations, finds almost intolerable. The [[walter-morel|Morel]]s' eventual move "up" to a house on [[scargill-street|Scargill Street]] marks a small but symbolically significant rise in the family's fortunes.

## Description in the Novel

Lawrence's descriptions of the Bottoms are among the novel's most visceral evocations of industrial landscape. The houses are "erected" rather than built — the verb suggesting the mechanical, impersonal force of industrial development (001-037). The terrace crouches in a valley, "almost blindingly" filled with sunlight or "shone black with wet" depending on the season (002-600). The ash-pits, the alleyways, the slate roofs, the constant presence of the mine — all contribute to an atmosphere of enclosure from which the sensitive soul (Gertrude, and later [[paul-morel|Paul]]) must struggle to escape.

The Bottoms is also defined by its sounds: the "bang, bang" of the colliers' hobnailed boots, the "shrill voice" of quarrelling women, the "bang of the gate" as the men return from [[the-coal-mine|the pit]] (002-187). It is a world of communal noise, where privacy is scarce and domestic tensions are audible to the neighbours.

## Symbolic Significance

The Bottoms functions as the novel's primary symbol of industrial entrapment. Its very name suggests lowness — a place at the bottom of the valley, at the bottom of the social hierarchy, at the bottom of aspiration. Gertrude's "sick" feeling about moving there (001-057) is a judgment not just on the house but on the life it represents: a life of miners' wives, of communal washing and shared ash-pits, of children who grow up to go down [[the-coal-mine|the pit]].

When the [[walter-morel|Morel]]s move from the Bottoms to [[scargill-street|Scargill Street]] (004-093), the move is freighted with symbolic significance. They are moving "up" — up the hill, up in the world, up and away from the life Gertrude most fears. Yet the escape is only partial. The mine remains visible, the colliers remain neighbours, and the Bottoms remains part of the family's psychic geography long after they have physically left it.

## Key Events

- **Gertrude's Arrival** (001-057): Gertrude, pregnant and reluctant, moves into the Bottoms. Her "sick" response to the house and its neighbours establishes the emotional tone of the family's early years.

- **The Wakes** (001-727–864): The Bottoms empties for the wakes (fair), and the narrative lingers over the deserted alleyways, the "intolerable" afternoon heat, and the return of the revellers.

- **William's Death** (002-093–126): News of [[William Morel]]'s fatal illness reaches the Bottoms by telegram, and the community's collective response — "a murmur of voices" heard "from one end of the Bottoms to the other" — illustrates the inescapable proximity of neighbour to neighbour.

- **The Move** (004-093): The family moves from the Bottoms to a house on [[scargill-street|Scargill Street]], marking the end of the novel's first phase and the beginning of a new chapter in the [[walter-morel|Morel]]s' domestic life.

## Real-World Context

The Bottoms is based on the mining terraces of Eastwood, [[nottingham|Nottinghamshire]], where D.H. Lawrence was born and raised. The terraced housing provided by mining companies was a standard feature of British industrial communities in the late nineteenth century: cheaply built, crowded, and tied to employment at [[the-coal-mine|the pit]]. Lawrence's own family moved from such a terrace to a better house — just as the [[walter-morel|Morel]]s do — and the mixture of shame, aspiration, and attachment that the Bottoms evokes in the novel draws directly on his own experience.
