## Overview

The Pacific Ocean functions in *The Absolute at Large* as the geographical origin point of the global conflict that eventually engulfs Europe. The war between Japan and China — both equipped with Karburator technology — begins in the Pacific theater before its consequences ripple westward across Asia and into Central Europe.

## Significance in the Novel

### The Pacific Theater

The novel's apocalyptic chronology traces the Greatest War's origins to the Far East. Japan and China, both industrialized and Karburator-equipped, clash over control of East Asia, with the Pacific serving as both battlefield and supply route. The Japanese, having "thrust the Chinese out of Eastern Europe in the year 1951", establish a brief Chinese-dominated "Middle Kingdom" in the former Austria-Hungary — a direct consequence of military campaigns that originated in Pacific waters.

### Global Reach

The Pacific's presence in the novel's geography emphasizes the truly global scale of the Karburator catastrophe. The technology that Marek invented in a Břevnov cellar cannot be contained within Bohemia, or Europe, or any single continent. The Pacific — the largest geographic feature on Earth — serves as a reminder that the Absolute respects no borders, no oceans, and no cultural boundaries. What begins as an industrial innovation in a Prague suburb ends as a world war fought across the planet's largest ocean.

## Thematic Resonance

The Pacific represents the final scale of the novel's geographic expansion. Čapek's narrative moves from a cellar in Břevnov, to the city of Prague, to the Bohemian countryside, to all of Europe, and finally to the global stage where the Pacific becomes a theater of war. This geographic progression mirrors the spiritual progression: from one man's invention, to local miracles, to mass religious movements, to a world war fought with divine power. The Pacific is the endpoint of this expansion — the point at which the Absolute has literally filled the entire world.
