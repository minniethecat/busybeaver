# Canonization of the Absolute

The **canonization of the Absolute** is the Catholic Church's formal process — described in Chapter XIX of Čapek's novel — to recognize the released Absolute as divine, culminating in its declaration not as a Saint but as a God on the twelfth of December.

## Admission to the Church

Before the canonization process could begin, the Absolute was first admitted to the bosom of the Church by Papal Brief. The College of Cardinals, faced with the accomplished fact, deliberated whether the sacrament of baptism should be conferred upon the Absolute. It was decided to dispense with this: while there was precedent for the baptism of a God (vide John the Baptist), the candidate must be present in person. The Sacred Congregation recommended instead that the Holy Father pray for the new member of the Church at the next Pontifical Mass, which was carried out in solemn form.

A new body of Church doctrine was also established: in addition to baptism and baptism by blood, the Church now recognized "baptism through works of piety, meritoriousness, and virtue".

## The Deification Commission

After the Absolute's widespread manifestations, the Church initiated a summary beatification under the rule *super cultu immemorabili*, declaring the Absolute "Blessed." A regular but expedited process of canonization was arranged, with one crucial innovation:

> "The Absolute was to be declared, not a Saint, but a God. A Deification Commission was immediately appointed from among the best of the Church's scholars and pastors."

Cardinal Archbishop Varesi of Venice served as Procurator Dei, while Monsignor Cullatti acted as Advocatus Diaboli — the Devil's Advocate.

## Evidence of Miracles

Cardinal Varesi presented seventeen thousand testimonies to miracles performed, signed by nearly all the cardinals, patriarchs, primates, metropolitans, princes of the Church, archbishops, principals of Orders, and abbots. Each testimonial was appended with expert reports from medical authorities, professors of natural sciences, technicians, and economists, as well as eyewitness signatures and legal authentications. These seventeen thousand documents, Varesi stated, represented "but an insignificant fraction" of the miracles actually performed by the Absolute, their number having already at a conservative estimate exceeded thirty millions.

The Procurator Dei also secured detailed expert opinions from the world's greatest scientific specialists. Professor Gardien, Rector of the medical faculty in Paris, examined cases from a medical standpoint "completely hopeless and scientifically incurable," including paralysis, cancer of the throat, blindness after surgical removal of both eyes, lameness following amputation of both lower extremities, death after decapitation, and strangulation in a subject hanged two days before. The Sorbonne concluded these cures could only be ascribed either to "complete ignorance" of medical science — "or, a possibility we do not wish to exclude, to the interference of higher powers not limited by the laws of nature".

Professor Meadow of Glasgow, a psychologist, found the Absolute to be "a thinking being, capable of association, memory, and even of logical judgment" operating without a brain or nervous system — which he cited as corroboration of his "crushing criticism of the psychophysical parallelism". Professor Lupen of Brno stated simply that "from the standpoint of effective performance, the Absolute is a force deserving of the highest respect," while Professor Willibald of Tübingen declared it "in admirable conformity with Einstein's theory of Relativity".

## Theological Foundation

A committee of eminent authorities on dogma and exegesis established the identity of the Absolute with the Third Person of the Trinity, based on the Scriptures and the writings of the Church Fathers. This theological innovation — equating a physically released entity with the Holy Spirit — represents Čapek's satirical treatment of institutional religion's adaptability.

## Competing Claims

Before the ceremony of deification took place, a theological dispute erupted. The Patriarch of Constantinople declared, as head of the Eastern Church, the identity of the Absolute with the First Person of the Trinity, the Creator. This "undeniably heretical teaching" was espoused by the Old Catholics, the circumcised Christians of Abyssinia, the Evangelicals of the Helvetian Confession, the Nonconformists, and several larger American sects.

Meanwhile, a secret doctrine spread among the Jews to the effect that the Absolute was Baal of old, with Liberal Jews announcing they would in that case recognize Baal. The Free Thought Society, assembled in Basle with two thousand delegates, proclaimed the Absolute the God of the Freethinkers, attacking clerics of all denominations who, as stated in their resolution, "are eager to seek their own advantage with the one scientific God and to drag Him down into the filthy cage of ecclesiastical dogma". The German Monist Association laid the foundation-stone of the future Cathedral of the Atomic God in Leipzig, a ceremony at which sixteen persons were injured and the physicist Lüttgen had his spectacles smashed.

## The Deification Ceremony

The deification of the Absolute was at length accomplished in Rome on the twelfth of December.

Seven thousand priests with lighted candles escorted the Holy Father to St. Peter's, where the largest twelve-ton Karburator — a gift from the M.E.C. to the Holy See — had been erected behind the high altar. The ceremony lasted five hours, and twelve hundred of the faithful and spectators were crushed to death. At the stroke of noon the Pope intoned the "*In nomine Dei Deus*," and at the same moment the bells of all the Catholic churches in the world were set ringing as all the bishops and priests turned from the altars and announced to the world of believers: "*Habemus Deum*".

## Čapek's Satirical Commentary

G.H. Bondy's role in the process underscores the novel's satire: three days before the Papal Brief, the Pope gave a lengthy audience to Bondy, who afterwards conferred with Monsignor Cullatti for twenty-four hours. The implication — that business interests shaped theological doctrine — is a recurring theme in Čapek's critique of institutional power.
