## Overview

The First Blow Struck, narrated in Chapter XI of *[[the-absolute|The Absolute]] at Large*, marks the novel's pivot from the comedy of personal religious experience to the tragedy of sectarian violence. After Jan Binder's stolen [[the-karburator|Karburator]]-powered merry-go-round establishes a rival congregation near [[jan-binder-kuzenda|Kuzenda]]'s dredge at Stechovice, religious competition escalates into physical confrontation. The brawl — fought with fists, a gunstock, a knife, and the broken antlers of a merry-go-round deer — is Čapek's miniature of the Greatest War to come.

## The Stolen Karburator

The chapter opens with a mystery: the original Marek [[the-karburator|Karburator]] is stolen from its guarded premises at [[brevnov|1651 Mixa Street]], [[brevnov|Břevnov]]. Despite an active police search, "not a trace of the stolen machine was found." The theft goes unsolved — but the machine resurfaces when Jan Binder, a merry-go-round proprietor, purchases an unusual copper cylinder from a dealer in old iron for three hundred crowns.

> [(011-002)] Jan Binder was seized with a strange, almost blind faith in the copper cylinder, and he bought it for three hundred crowns. Then he hauled it away on a truck with his own hands to his merry-go-round, which was standing out of action near Zlichov.

Binder connects the cylinder to his merry-go-round's axle. The machine begins to turn — and with it, the [[the-absolute|Absolute]]'s influence begins to radiate from a carnival attraction.

## The Merry-Go-Round Ministry

The merry-go-round becomes an instrument of conversion. Binder discovers that when the machine runs, the earth itself seems to spin while the merry-go-round remains a "lovely island of quiet and repose." Passersby — children, grandmothers, working men, couples on evening walks — are overcome by dizziness, "brought to safety in the embrace of the merry-go-round," and "strengthened for their future life by the apt exhortations of Jan Binder."

> [(011-015)] Holding tightly to his rod, Binder stooped down to them and shouted, "Here, people, this way!" And the people seeing the shining merry-go-round calmly uplifted above the reeling earth, staggered towards it.

Binder, a tall figure in his sleeveless striped jersey, preaches in "unpractised style": "Well, good people, here we have landed out of the whirl and confusion of the world." The merry-go-round becomes a mobile church, roaming from Zlichov up the Vltava to Chuchle, Zbraslav, and finally Stechovice.

## The Confrontation

At Stechovice, Binder's ministry collides with [[jan-binder-kuzenda|Kuzenda]]'s dredge congregation. After four days of "tremendous power," a black and silent procession approaches Binder one night. At its head is [[jan-binder-kuzenda|Kuzenda]], the bearded dredge-priest.

> [(011-013)] "Now then," he said, trying to master his excitement, "pack up at once, or —"

The theological dispute is brief and devastating:

- **[[jan-binder-kuzenda|Kuzenda]]**: "Binder, you are carrying on foul witchcraft here, and we'll not put up with it so close to the sacred shrine of our dredge."
- **Binder**: "Your dredge is a fraud!"

[[jan-binder-kuzenda|Kuzenda]] is "cut to the quick." The ensuing brawl involves: a baker's fist, a gamekeeper's gunstock, a postman's throttling hands, a girl's teeth (she bites Binder's Bohemian lion tattoo), a knife, and the broken antlers of a merry-go-round deer. The merry-go-round itself "gave a deep groan and heeled over, its roof falling right upon the struggling mob."

> [(011-023)] What happened next it is hard to disentangle into any logical sequence. It seems that the first blow was struck by the baker from Kuzenda's camp, but Binder landed him a blow on the head with his fist.

The casualties: Binder with a broken collarbone, [[jan-binder-kuzenda|Kuzenda]] unconscious from a pole blow, [[mr-brych|Brych]] "spitting out teeth and blood," and a girl from Stechovice "sobbing hysterically." The rest have fled.

## Key Participants

- **Jan Binder**: The merry-go-round proprietor turned preacher, whose stolen [[the-karburator|Karburator]] creates a rival congregation. A tall, physically imposing figure in a striped jersey, whose theology is as improvised as his machinery.
- **[[jan-binder-kuzenda|Kuzenda]]**: The dredge-priest of Stechovice, whose followers form the "black and silent body of people" confronting Binder. His accusation of "foul witchcraft" reflects the territorial nature of even the most enlightened religion.
- **[[mr-brych|Brych]]**: [[jan-binder-kuzenda|Kuzenda]]'s stoker, quick to escalate the conflict — "Just let him try!" — and one of the brawl's casualties.
- **[[mr-hudec|Mr. Hudec]]**: A moderating voice in [[jan-binder-kuzenda|Kuzenda]]'s camp who tries to restrain his leader.

## Narrative Significance

The chapter is the novel's thesis statement on religious violence in miniature. The theological differences between Binder's and [[jan-binder-kuzenda|Kuzenda]]'s congregations are essentially non-existent — both worship the [[the-absolute|Absolute]], both perform miracles, both attract devoted followers. Their conflict is purely territorial: two prophets cannot occupy the same spiritual marketplace. The farcical nature of the brawl (deer antlers as weapons, a merry-go-round collapsing on the combatants) underscores the absurdity of sectarian violence while simultaneously demonstrating how easily illumination tips into aggression.

The chapter's title — "The First Blow Struck" — positions this petty brawl as the inaugural act of the [[the-greatest-war|Greatest War]]. What begins with a fistfight between two charismatic leaders in a riverside town ends, nine years later, with 198 million dead and civilization in ruins. The causal chain is Čapek's darkest satirical argument: the distance between religious enthusiasm and mass slaughter is shorter than anyone cares to admit.
