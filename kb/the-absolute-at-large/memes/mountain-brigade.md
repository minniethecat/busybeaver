## Overview

The Mountain Brigade is a Czechoslovak military unit whose personnel become swept up in the Absolute's religious epidemic. Its mention in the novel illustrates the reach of the Karburator's influence into the armed forces — an institution whose core function (organized violence) stands in stark tension with the Absolute's message of universal love and martyrdom.

## Role in the Novel

### Military Conversion

The Mountain Brigade appears as one of many military formations affected by the Absolute. Like the Prague garrison — whose soldiers were exhorted by the transformed Minister for War to "become martyrs" — the Mountain Brigade's troops experience the spiritual awakening that follows Karburator deployment. The brigade's name suggests a specialized mountain warfare unit, perhaps stationed in the border regions where Karburator-equipped factories and installations were most concentrated.

### The Paradox of Armed Believers

The Mountain Brigade embodies one of the novel's central ironies: the Absolute, which inspires universal love and pacifism in individual believers, also fuels the most destructive war in human history. The brigade's soldiers, individually transformed, collectively become combatants in a global religious war where "No quarter was given". The same divine force that makes them seek transcendence also makes them deadly.

## Thematic Significance

The Mountain Brigade represents the paradox of militarized spirituality. The Absolute cannot be confined to peaceful manifestations; once released into the armed forces, it becomes a weapon. The brigade's soldiers are sincere in their devotion and merciless in their fighting — a combination that Čapek presents not as hypocrisy but as the logical endpoint of unleashing divine power into human institutions.
