**Prince Trivelino** is the Italian Minister who attends the secret Conference of the Supreme Council of the Great Powers on St. Kilda in Karel Čapek's *The Absolute at Large*. As the representative of Italy — a nation uniquely positioned between the Catholic Church and the secular state — Trivelino embodies the contradictions of a country where the divine simultaneously serves the Vatican and the Quirinal [(020-016)].

## Role and Significance

Prince Trivelino occupies a distinctive position in the St. Kilda Conference's nationalist-religious landscape. Unlike [[baron-yanato|Baron Yanato]]'s serene claim that the Absolute is Japanese, or General Buchtin's bullish insistence on Orthodox primacy, Trivelino speaks from a nation where the divine is institutionally split — between the Papacy's universal claims and the Italian state's secular authority.

> "That's because He's showing Himself such an anti-militarist in your country," said Prince Trivelino with a certain malicious pleasure. [(020-016)]

His needling of the French Premier Dudieu — pointing out that the Absolute has become an anti-militarist in France — reveals his own nation's more complicated relationship with the divine. As Dudieu retorts, in Italy the Absolute "works for the Vatican with one hand and for the Quirinal with the other," simultaneously serving the Church and the secular Italian state [(020-017)].

Trivelino also plays a role in the Conference's sub-plot of Catholic Karburator smuggling. The German Chancellor Dr. Wurm sarcastically thanks him "for the Catholic Karburators that you are smuggling over to us" — adding that "fortunately they are poorly made, like all Italian products" — revealing that beneath the theological debates, the powers are engaged in a religious arms race using Karburator technology [(020-022)].

> "Even in your Catholic provinces?" asked Prince Trivelino with a smile, challenging Dr. Wurm's claim of German unity under a "State Absolute" [(020-021)].

When the Conference collapses, Trivelino leaves "looking very pale" — his composure shattered where Yanato's smile endures — suggesting that Italy's ambivalent position between sacred and secular leaves it more vulnerable to the Absolute's destabilizing effects than nations with cleaner ideological lines [(020-035)].
