## Who

Professor Meadow was a psychologist at the University of Glasgow who studied the
phenomenon of [[the-absolute|the Absolute]] from a scientific perspective. He is one of several
academic authorities cited in the novel who attempted to understand the nature
of the Absolute through the lens of their respective disciplines.

## Role in the Novel

Professor Meadow appears in Chapter XIX, where his psychological analysis of the
Absolute is quoted. He identified the Absolute as a thinking entity that performed
cognitive operations — association, memory, and logical judgment — without the
physical medium of a brain or nervous system:

> Professor Meadow of Glasgow, the psychologist, wrote: "…Since in these activities
> there is manifest what is obviously a thinking being, capable of association, memory,
> and even of logical judgment, a being which performs these psychic operations without
> the medium of a brain and nervous system, it affords a striking…" [[XIX-009]]

## Significance

Professor Meadow represents the scientific establishment's attempt to grapple with
the Absolute phenomenon. His observation that the Absolute is a thinking entity
operating without biological infrastructure anticipates and reinforces the novel's
central metaphysical conceit — that consciousness and intelligence can exist
independently of material form. Alongside other academic experts cited in the novel,
Meadow embodies Čapek's satirical treatment of the limits of scientific inquiry when
confronted with the genuinely supernatural.
