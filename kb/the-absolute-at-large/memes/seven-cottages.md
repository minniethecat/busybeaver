## Overview

Seven Cottages is a minor rural settlement in the Bohemian countryside, mentioned in connection with the gamekeeper Rys — one of the early converts to Kuzenda's dredge-based religious community on the Vltava. The name appears in the context of the network of believers that radiates outward from the sacred dredge at Štěchovice into the surrounding landscape.

## Location

The settlement is located in the Vltava River valley region, within traveling distance of the M.E.28 dredge moored above Štěchovice. Its name suggests a hamlet of seven dwellings — a typical small Bohemian rural settlement — located in or near the forested hillsides where a gamekeeper would work.

## Significance in the Novel

### Connection to Kuzenda's Community

Seven Cottages enters the narrative through the gamekeeper Rys, one of the followers drawn to Kuzenda's dredge. Like the baker, the postman, and the "sweethearts from Štěchovice," the gamekeeper represents the ordinary rural population transformed by proximity to the Absolute. His residence at Seven Cottages places the dredge's spiritual influence within a named settlement, however small, extending the geography of the novel's religious movement from the river itself into the surrounding countryside.

The settlement's modest scale — seven cottages, a gamekeeper's station — embodies the democratic, anti-institutional character of Kuzenda's ministry. The Absolute reaches not just cities and cathedrals but the smallest hamlets, transforming gamekeepers and bakers into mystics and martyrs.

## Thematic Resonance

Seven Cottages functions as a synecdoche for rural Bohemia — a landscape of small, named places whose inhabitants are drawn, without institutional mediation, into direct contact with the divine. The settlement's name, specifying neither a saint nor a patron but simply a number of dwellings, reflects Čapek's vision of a religious movement rooted in the ordinary: not in cathedrals or monasteries but in cottages, dredges, and merry-go-rounds.
