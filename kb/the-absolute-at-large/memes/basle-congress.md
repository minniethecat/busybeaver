## Overview

The Basle Congress was a major assembly of the international [[free-thought|Free Thought Society]],
held in Basle (Basel), Switzerland, during the period of [[the-absolute|the Absolute]]'s growing influence.
At the congress, two thousand delegates formally recognized the Absolute as a scientific deity
and used it to attack traditional religious institutions.

## The Proclamation

In the presence of two thousand delegates, the Absolute was proclaimed as the God of the
Freethinkers. This declaration was accompanied by an denunciation of religious authorities:

> The Free Thought Society assembled in Basle. In the presence of two thousand delegates
> the Absolute was proclaimed as the God of the Freethinkers, and this was followed by
> an incredibly violent attack upon clerics of all denominations, who, in the terms of
> the resolution, "are eager to seek their own advantage with the one scientific God…" [[XIX-017]]

## The Free Religion

The congress asserted that the Free Thought Association alone was the legitimate congregation
of the Absolute, and that only the Basle Congress had the authority to establish [[doctrine|doctrine]] and
ritual for the emerging "Free Religion":

> "…has nothing to do with the medieval traffic of these Pharisees; the Free Thought
> Association alone is His congregation, and only the Basle Congress has the right to
> set forth the doctrine and the ritual of the Free Religion." [[XIX-017]]

## Significance

The Basle Congress represents a key moment in the novel's satire of religious appropriation
of the Absolute. Just as Catholic, Protestant, and other denominations claimed the Absolute
for their own traditions, the Freethinkers — ostensibly advocates of rationalism and [[science|science]] —
proved equally eager to institutionalize the Absolute into a formal religious structure
with its own doctrines, rituals, and attacks on rival denominations. The congress
illustrates Čapek's ironic thesis that the Absolute, whatever its true nature,
inevitably becomes a mirror for humanity's pre-existing ideological divisions.
