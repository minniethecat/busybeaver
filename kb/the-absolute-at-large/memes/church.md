# Church

The **Church** in Čapek's *The Absolute at Large* is the primary institutional antagonist to the Absolute. The Catholic hierarchy's response to the Karburator's emanations ranges from condemnation to co-option, exposing the tension between organized religion and genuine spiritual experience.

## The Factory as Church

Even before the Church becomes involved, the Absolute transforms secular spaces into sites of religious experience. Bondy's visit to Marek's cellar produces an unexpected reaction:

> "I never go to church, Rudy, never in my life, but down in that cellar I felt as if I were in church. Tell me, man, what did I do down there?"

## The Church's Rejection

The Catholic Church initially rejects the Absolute as heresy. The Bishop explains to Bondy why the Absolute cannot be accommodated within existing doctrine:

> "Your Absolute opposes the laws of the Church. It contradicts the doctrine of the holy sacraments. It does not regard the traditions of the Church. It seriously violates the doctrine of the Trinity. It pays no attention to the apostolic succession. It does not even submit to the rites of exorcism."

## Strategic Co-option

Facing the Absolute's popularity, the Church pivots from rejection to absorption. The Bishop reveals the Consistory's pragmatic decision:

> "The venerable Consistory decided that in the interests of the Church it would place your Absolute provisionally under its patronage. It would attempt to bring it into harmony with Catholic doctrine. It would proclaim the premises in Břevnov known as No. 1651 a miraculous shrine and place of pilgrimage."

## The Iron Church on the Dredge

Kuzenda offers an alternative, entirely decentralized vision of church — a sacred dredge where God manifests through machinery:

> "This is a sacred dredge, it is an iron church, and we are only here as its priests. If in old times God could appear in a well or in an oak-tree, and sometimes even like a woman, as with the ancient Greeks, why should He not appear on a dredge?"
