## Role in the novel

Mr. Skocdopole appears in Chapter XXII ("The Uprising of the People") as the Burgomaster of Hradec Králové, one of the regional cities swept up in the wave of Bobinetism spreading from the collapsed Mandarin Empire [1968-PN]. His tenure coincides with the period when the Absolute's influence has moved from individual religious experience to mass political mobilization, and he becomes the first local official to face a direct challenge from the new revolutionary movements.

## The circumspect administrator

The chronicler initially presents Skocdopole as a figure of stability in a destabilized world [1968-PN]:

> "When the Mandarin Empire collapsed, the city was under the government of that circumspect Burgomaster, Mr. Skocdopole. Amid the prevailing anarchy his administration was blessed with comparative peace, thanks to the wise counsels of Bishop Linda and the Worshipful City Fathers."

His alliance with Bishop Linda places him in the institutional-religious camp — precisely the position that becomes untenable as the Absolute radicalizes political sentiment against established authority.

## The Hampl challenge

The crisis arrives in the person of Hampl, a tailor returned from foreign adventures who has "caught a whiff of Bobinetism" [1970-PN]. Hampl's critique of Skocdopole follows the classic populist pattern — attacking the Savings Bank, denouncing the City Council as "under the thumb of the parsons," and dismissing the Burgomaster as "an incapable old dodderer" [1970-PN].

At a June Popular Assembly, Hampl escalates from criticism to categorical demand [1972-PN]:

> "The people categorically demanded that Skocdopole, that scoundrel, reactionary, and lackey of the Bishop, should resign the office of Burgomaster."

The epithets "reactionary" and "lackey of the Bishop" reveal the political-religious fusion the Absolute creates. Skocdopole is not targeted for corruption or incompetence but for his association with the institutional Church — the same Church that, in other chapters, is absorbing the Absolute for its own purposes.

## The poster war

Skocdopole responds with the tools available to a lawful administrator — official notices [1974-PN]:

> "Mr. Skocdopole put up posters stating that he, as the lawfully elected Burgomaster, need take orders from no one, least of all from an interloper and a deserter; that in the present times of unrest it was impossible to hold a fresh election."

The posters are legally correct but politically tone-deaf. In a world where the Absolute has made all positions absolute, appealing to legal procedure and electoral niceties is futile. Hampl simply ignores the posters and marches to the Little Mill to draft his declaration of war.

## The keys that didn't exist

Skocdopole's finest — and most damning — moment as a character comes during the crisis council. Faced with Hampl's armed camp outside the city gates, the Burgomaster proposes a gesture of surrender [1979-PN]:

> "He summoned an extraordinary meeting of the City Council, at which he proposed that the keys of the city gates should be given up to General Hampl."

The council's investigation reveals the city has no keys to surrender [1979-PN]:

> "It was then ascertained that there were no such keys in existence; a few locks and keys of historical interest which used to repose in the City Museum had been carried off by the Swedes."

The comedy is devastating: the Burgomaster, seeking to negotiate, offers up an object that literally does not exist — and the historical keys, the last possible symbol of civic authority, were stolen centuries ago by the Swedes. Skocdopole's circumspection, his defining virtue, has rendered him unable to govern in a world where neither law nor procedure matters.

## The midnight watch

In the chapter's most atmospheric passage, Skocdopole ascends the White Tower after midnight to survey the threat [1983-PN]:

> "Skocdopole, the Burgomaster, crept up the White Tower after midnight, and there to the east along the Orlice river hundreds of fires were burning, thousands of figures were moving about in the firelight, which cast a bloodred glow over the countryside. It looked as though entrenchments were being made. The Burgomaster came down from the tower deeply perturbed."

The image of the elderly civil servant creeping up a tower to witness the armed camp of a tailor-turned-general is Čapek's satire at its most visual — the old order literally looking down on the new one, unable to comprehend what it sees.

## Significance

Skocdopole represents the ordinary civil servant caught between transcendent forces. His circumspection — a political virtue in normal times — becomes a liability when the Absolute eliminates the middle ground. The "keys that didn't exist" episode encapsulates the novel's broader satire: institutional authority, stripped of its symbols and procedures, is revealed to have been resting on nothing more substantial than habit and deference — both of which the Absolute has incinerated.
