# Mr. Rejzek

Mr. Rejzek is an editor at the Czechoslovak Press Bureau who appears alongside reporter [[cyril-keval|Cyril Keval]]. He processes incoming international reports and engages in discussions about the increasingly strange worldwide effects attributed to the Absolute.

## Life at the Press Bureau

Rejzek is introduced in Chapter IX at the Czechoslovak Press Bureau, where he and Keval sift through the day's news from around the world. The atmosphere is one of weary routine, punctuated by the bizarre reports arriving from countries affected by the Absolute:

> "Mhm," answered old Rejzek, turning over the papers from the Czechoslovak Press Bureau. (009-005)

His characterisation as "old Rejzek" establishes him as a veteran newsman, experienced enough to handle unusual stories with professional detachment — at least initially.

## The Beards Discussion

One of the most notable scenes involving Rejzek is the conversation about the sudden growth of beards among men worldwide — one of the stranger physical manifestations of the Absolute's influence:

> "What makes their beards grow so long?" Mr. Keval ruminated. "I say, Rejzek, I do believe the Absolute has something to do with that as well." (009-010)

Rejzek's response reflects the pragmatic skepticism of a career journalist:

> "Golly, Rejzek, I'm afraid of something of the sort growing on me. Just imagine." (009-012)

This exchange captures the blend of professional curiosity and personal unease that characterizes the press's response to the Absolute phenomenon. Rejzek and Keval process reports of the impossible, caught between journalistic duty and the creeping fear that the impossible might affect them personally.

## Processing the News

As an editor, Rejzek's primary function is to filter and interpret incoming reports. The fact that he is shown "turning over the papers" suggests that the volume of strange reports has increased dramatically — the Absolute has become a major news story, generating international coverage that must be sorted, verified, and packaged for the Czechoslovak public.

## Significance

Mr. Rejzek represents the journalistic establishment's attempt to process the Absolute phenomenon through conventional news frameworks. His presence, along with Keval's, shows how the media served as both transmitter and interpreter of the Absolute's global spread — turning miraculous events into newspaper copy. The beard conversation in particular exemplifies Čapek's satirical technique: using absurd physical details to undercut the gravity of metaphysical events.
