## Summary

The Greatest War — officially designated "The So-Called Greatest War" in Chapter XXV — is the catastrophic climax of *[[the-absolute|The Absolute]] at Large*. Spanning from February 12, 1944 to the autumn of 1953, the war consumes 198 million combatants and reduces civilization to ruins, leaving only thirteen survivors. Capek's satirical treatment is pointed: the chapter (025-001) is framed not as military history but as a meditation on humanity's need for superlatives — the observation that people find "peculiar satisfaction" in having lived through "the biggest" disaster of its kind constituting the novel's most sustained piece of irony. (025-003)

> [(025-003)] Well, the war which lasted from February 12, 1944 to the autumn of 1953, was in all truthfulness and without any exaggeration (on my honour!) the Greatest War. Do not let us rob those who lived through it of this one solitary and well-earned satisfaction. 198,000,000 men took part in the fighting, and all but thirteen of them fell.

## Context

### The Proliferation of the Absolute

The war emerges from the gradual worldwide spread of the Absolute, released by [[g-h-bondy|G.H. Bondy]]'s Karburator at the M.E.C. factory in [[brevnov|Brevnov]]. After the [[the-ceremony-at-stvanice|Stvanice ceremony]] (Chapter IX) exposes the Absolute's spiritual power to a mass public audience , the phenomenon spreads via commercial Karburators exported globally. By Chapter XIV, "The Land of Plenty," the world economy has been disrupted by abundance — factories powered by atomic motors produce without cost, wages collapse, and social order frays. (014-001)

Religious fervor becomes the dominant social force. As the Absolute fills those near the Karburators, societies fracture along sectarian lines. Catholic regions, Protestant strongholds, and entirely new cults like Kuzenda's dredge fellowship  and the Binderians of the merry-go-round (013-009) compete for followers. The Prussian army, described in Chapter XV, marches on Bohemia because the Absolute in the brewery at [[hradec-kralove|Hradec Kralove]] produces "plentiful and ardently Catholic beer" (028-004) — a comically precise illustration of how the spiritual contaminates the material world. (015-001)

### Bobinet's Rise

The military dimension is set in motion by [[emperor-bobinet|Toni Bobinet]], the twenty-two-year-old lieutenant of mountain artillery stationed at Annecy in Haute Savoie (026-001). Bored and stationed high in the Alps above the reach of the Absolute's atmospheric influence, Bobinet reads a two-week-old newspaper calling for "the strong hand of a helmsman" and launches a one-man coup. (026-002)

> [(026-002)] Lieutenant Toni Bobinet sat on a boulder and tugged at his tiny moustache, first because he was bored, and secondly because he had read a newspaper two weeks old right through for the fifth time, and was now thinking things over.

Bobinet deposes his captain, declares war on the Absolute, and systematically destroys Karburators across southeastern France. Within a week he commands 15,000 men; within months, 40,000. By July he is proclaimed First Consul with the title Marshal, and on August 14th he crowns himself Emperor of the French. (026-004) His military doctrine — state atheism enforced by execution — is the precise inverse of the religious fervor that drives every other faction. (026-004)

### The Global Configuration

Chapter XXV chronicles the war's sprawling absurdity. The chronicler refuses to provide a conventional military history — no maps, no casualty tables, no strategic analysis — instead offering a catalogue of surreal juxtapositions that mirrors the war's essential irrationality. (025-004)

> [(025-004)] Bobinet's victorious regiments flew, so to speak, in one dazzling swoop in the footprints of Alexander the Great across India to China; but meanwhile the Yellow invasion swept over Siberia and Russia into France and Spain, thus cutting off from their native land the Muslims who were operating in Sweden.

### The Local Microcosm: Czechoslovakia

Czechoslovakia is held in succession by Swedes, French, Turks, Russians, and Chinese. Each invasion kills off the native population "to the last man" (025-005) — a statistic the chronicler delivers without comment, its impossibility being the point. The Stavovsky Theatre is "invariably full, being used for the purposes of an army store" — one of the few "gratifying changes" (025-005). The Church of St. Vitus sees services held by "a pastor, a solicitor, an Imam, an Archimandrite and a bonze, none of them enjoying any permanent success" (025-005).

> [(025-005)] Czechoslovakia was held by the Swedes, French, Turks, Russians and Chinese in succession; each of these invasions killed off the native population to the last man.

### The Middle Kingdom Interlude

A particularly absurd episode: when the Japanese thrust the Chinese out of Eastern Europe in 1951, "a new Middle Kingdom ... fell precisely within the frontiers of the old empire of Austria-Hungary" (025-006). A 106-year-old mandarin, Jaja Wir Weana, is enthroned at Schonbrunn. Chinese becomes the official language, "which at one sweep did away with all nationalistic rivalries" (025-006). The State god is Buddha. Stubborn Catholics of Bohemia and Moravia become "victims of Chinese dragonades and confiscations" — while "several prominent and prudent Czech patriots were exalted to mandarin rank" (025-006).

The Middle Kingdom collapses not from military defeat but because "the supply of lead necessary for munitions ran out, and all authority thereupon collapsed" (025-006) — a punchline that reduces all imperial ambition to a logistical footnote.

### Bobinet's Fate

The chronicler offers two versions of Bobinet's end. Learning of an Amazon Empire in the unexplored river-territory of the Irawaddy, Salwin, and Mekong, Bobinet sets out with his Old Guard and never returns:

> [(025-007)] According to one version he married and settled there. According to another, Amalia, the Queen of the Amazons, cut off his head in battle and flung it into a skin filled with blood, saying, "Satia te sanguine, quem tantum sitisti." This second version is doubtless the milder.

## Key Participants

### Emperor Toni Bobinet
The twenty-two-year-old lieutenant who becomes the "Napoleon of the Mountain Brigade" (026-001). Bobinet's career arc — from bored garrison officer to First Consul to Emperor to vanished adventurer — parodies the Napoleonic myth. His foreign policy is expressed entirely in terms of "keys": key to the Mediterranean (Gibraltar), key to Europe (Danube valley), key to the North Sea (Denmark). By New Year's Eve 1944, "the Emperor Bobinet had all his keys in the pocket of his artillery breeches" (026-006). See [[emperor-bobinet|Emperor Bobinet]].

### Sergei Nikolayevich Zlocin
Russian commander who, retreating before Chinese numerical superiority, establishes a short-lived Czardom in North Africa. He is soon murdered because "his Bavarian generals conspired against his Prussian hetmans" (025-004) — a joke about the impossibility of multi-ethnic empire.

### General Hampl
The tailor-adventurer who brings Bobinet's methods home to [[hradec-kralove|Hradec Kralove]]. A deserter from Bobinet's army, Hampl models his coup on Bobinet's own methods — writing a declaration of war in Bobinet's name, inventing an army, and seizing power through bluff. (028-010) See [[general-hampl|General Hampl]].

### The Chronicler
The narrator of Chapter XXV, who explicitly refuses to write conventional military history. He protests that he "would far rather depict the arrival of the Bedouins in their white burnouses at Geneva" or "the love adventures of a French poilu in Tibet" (025-004). This narrative refusal is itself the chapter's argument: the war is too vast and too absurd for coherent narration.

## The Collapse of Civilization

### Europe

The war's final phase is described as too grim for detail (025-008). Europe becomes a battleground between Black African and Mongolian forces. The chronicler offers a catalogue of collapse whose comic specificity makes the horror bearable:

- Bears multiply on Hradcany to the point that [[prague-in-the-novel|Prague]]'s last inhabitants destroy all bridges, "even the Charles Bridge" (025-008)
- The Vysehrad Chapter "died out both on the male and female side" (025-008)
- A football championship match between Sparta Club and Victoria Zizkov is "witnessed by only one hundred and ten people" (025-008)

> [(025-008)] The last traces of civilization vanished. For instance, the bears multiplied on Hradcany to such an extent that the last inhabitants of Prague destroyed all the bridges, even the Charles Bridge, to save the right bank of the Vltava from these bloodthirsty beasts. The population shrank to an insignificant little group; the Vysehrad Chapter died out both on the male and female side; the championship match between the Sparta Club and the Victoria Zizkov was witnessed by only one hundred and ten people.

### The Rest of the World

The global situation is no better. North America, after "fearful ravages of the murderous struggles between the Prohibitionists and the 'Wets'," becomes a Japanese colony (025-009). South America sees a revolving door of empires: Uruguay, Chile, Peru, Brandenburg, and Patagonia. Australia's "Ideal State" transforms the continent into an uninhabited desert. In Africa, "over two million white men had been eaten" (025-009). The Congo basin negroes hurl themselves upon Europe while the rest of Africa endures "the fluctuating conflicts of its one hundred and eighty-six different Emperors, Sultans, Kings, Chiefs and Presidents" (025-009).

The chronicler then steps back from this litany of devastation to reflect on the human cost beneath the statistics:

> [(025-010)] Each one of those hundred millions of warring pygmies had had his childhood, his loves, his plans; he was often afraid, he was frequently a hero, but usually he was tired to death and would have been glad to lie down on his bed in peace; and if he died, it was certainly against his will. And from all of this, one can take only a handful of arid events: a battle here or there, losses so-and-so, result this or that — and, after all, the result never brought about any real decision.

## Aftermath

### The End of the War

The war ends not with a treaty but with exhaustion. [[the-last-battle|The Last Battle]] (Chapter XXIX) documents the war's comic epitaph. Historians cannot agree on which engagement was the last. The only reliable evidence comes from a collection of pre-war prophecies, all of which mention the number thirteen and a birch tree (029-015). The last thirteen soldiers in the world simply stop fighting, lie down in the shade of a birch, and decide to go home (029-027).

> [(029-027)] And such, one can well imagine, was the end of the Greatest War.

### The Epilogue

The novel's epilogue (Chapter XXX) finds the surviving characters — [[mr-brych|Brych]] the stoker, Canon [[father-jost|Jost]], the journalist [[mr-rejzek|Rejzek]], and the innkeeper Binder — reunited years later in a quiet tavern, reading the [[the-peoples-journal|People's Journal]] and eating liver sausages (030-001). The war is over, [[the-absolute|the Absolute]] has dissipated, and ordinary life has resumed its quiet rhythms. Yet the final scene also includes the policeman reporting that "Karburator dens" still exist in the cellars of Prague — religious ecstasy now criminalized as a breach of the peace .

## Narrative Significance

### Satirical Method

Chapter XXV is Capek's most ambitious narrative experiment. The framing — a chronicler who protests his own inadequacy to the task — dissolves the boundary between history-writing and storytelling. The chapter's organizing principle is not chronology or causation but association, digression, and the accumulating weight of absurd juxtaposition. This is historiography as comedy: the narrator's "poor memory for figures" (025-003) and stated preference for romance over military history enact the very thesis the chapter argues — that war cannot be narrated coherently because it is itself incoherent.

> [(025-011)] Therefore I say: Do not rob the people of that time of their only boast — that what they went through was the Greatest War. We, however, know that in a few decades we shall succeed in arranging an even greater war, for in this respect also the human race is progressing ever upward and on.

### The War as Theology

The Greatest War literalizes what the novel has been developing thematically: that the Absolute's promise of total knowledge, total conviction, total righteousness is indistinguishable from the logic of holy war. Each faction believes it possesses the one true God; each faction is willing to kill for that belief. The war is not incidental to the Absolute but its logical conclusion. Capek's innovation is to treat this not as tragedy but as farce — the war's horror is inseparable from its absurdity, and the satire aims not at any particular religion but at the structure of absolute certainty itself.

### The Anti-Epic

The chapter is structured as an anti-epic. Where conventional war narratives catalogue heroic feats and strategic genius, Capek catalogues bureaucratic absurdities (the city council discovering there are no keys to the city gates), statistical impossibilities ("each of these invasions killed off the native population to the last man"), and the collapse of all distinctions between combatant and civilian, victor and vanquished, sacred and profane. The war's only "gratifying change" is that the Stavovsky Theatre remains full — as an army store. (025-005)
