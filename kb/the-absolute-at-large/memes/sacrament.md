## Definition

In Catholic theology, a **sacrament** is a visible sign of invisible grace, instituted by Christ. Within Čapek's novel, the sacraments — particularly baptism — function as a satirical device revealing the Church's institutional logic: when faced with the Absolute's competition for believers, the Church responds not with theological discernment but with administrative absorption, deliberating whether the Absolute itself needs to be baptized.

## Bishop Linda's objection

The first significant mention of sacraments comes from Bishop Linda in Chapter III, during his meeting with Bondy and Marek [452-PN]. The Bishop lists the Absolute's theological offenses in rapid succession:

> "It contradicts the doctrine of the holy sacraments. It does not regard the traditions of the Church. It seriously violates the doctrine of the Trinity." [452-PN]

Here sacraments are presented as one item in a bureaucratic checklist of grievances — the Bishop treats theological categories as items on a legal indictment. The comedy arises from the juxtaposition: a transcendent entity that performs miracles is being evaluated for compliance with ecclesiastical regulations.

## Bondy's baptism

The sacrament of baptism becomes a media event in Chapter XIX when Bishop Linda orchestrates the Absolute's absorption into the Church. His instructions to the newspaper staff include a personal detail about Bondy [1495-PN]:

> "Kostal, announce among the local news items that Mr. G. H. Bondy will on Sunday next receive the sacrament of baptism at the hands of the Archbishop, and add a few words of hearty welcome."

This is layered satire: Bondy, the industrial capitalist who manufactured the Karburator for profit, is now being publicly baptized — not out of conviction but as a PR maneuver by the Church hierarchy. The sacrament is reduced to a newspaper announcement, a "local news item" alongside the Apostolic Letter.

## The Absolute's baptism debate

The novel's most theologically sophisticated sacrament discussion occurs when the College of Cardinals debates whether the Absolute itself should be baptized [1514-PN]:

> "The College of Cardinals, being faced with the accomplished fact, merely deliberated whether the sacrament of baptism should be conferred upon the Absolute. It was decided to dispense with this."

The Cardinals create a new theological category — "baptism through works of piety, meritoriousness, and virtue" — to resolve the procedural dilemma [1514-PN]. This is Čapek at his most satirical: the Church invents doctrine to paper over an administrative gap, treating the divine with the same bureaucratic logic applied to any institutional problem.

## Satirical function

The sacrament motif serves three satirical purposes in the novel:

| Function | Example |
|----------|---------|
| **Institutional logic** | The Church evaluates the Absolute by checklist, not revelation |
| **Media spectacle** | Bondy's baptism is announced as a newspaper item |
| **Theological improvisation** | New sacramental categories are invented to close procedural gaps |

Through these episodes, Čapek suggests that institutional religion processes the genuinely sacred through the same mechanisms it uses for everything else: committees, legal opinions, and press releases.
