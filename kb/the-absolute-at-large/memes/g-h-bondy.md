## Overview

G. H. Bondy is the Chairman of the Board of Directors of the [[m-e-c|M.E.C.]] (Metallo-Electrical Company), a major industrial conglomerate with ten factories and 34,000 employees (001-011). A pragmatic businessman shaped by thirty years of industrial leadership, Bondy reconnects with his old schoolfellow [[rudy-marek|R. Marek]] after discovering a cryptic newspaper advertisement in the *[[the-peoples-journal|People's Journal]]* (001-002) and becomes the commercial catalyst for the world-changing consequences of the [[the-karburator|Karburator]]. His defining philosophy — "From the business point of view, it's a matter of indifference. If He wants to exist, in Heaven's name, let Him" (003-032) — captures the collision between industrial capitalism and the metaphysical that drives the novel's central satire.

## Role and Significance

Bondy serves as the reader's entry point into Capek's satirical world. His transformation from a crisis-preoccupied industrialist to someone who experiences religious ecstasy, and finally to a figure grappling with the catastrophic consequences of his business decisions, mirrors the novel's critique of industrial capitalism and its relationship with spiritual matters.

> (001-002) On New Year's Day, 1943, G. H. Bondy, head of the great Metallo-Electrical Company, was sitting as usual reading his paper. He skipped the news from the theatre of war rather disrespectfully, avoided the Cabinet crisis, then crowded on sail ... for the Finance and Commerce section.

The opening portrait establishes Bondy's defining traits: his focus on business over politics, his irritation with economic stagnation ("The Coal Crisis! ... Heavens above, it's a sheer disaster!" (001-002)), and his susceptibility to a certain romantic sentimentality beneath the industrialist's exterior.

Bondy's decision to visit Marek's Brevnov factory is motivated by a mixture of nostalgia and curiosity — but it quickly becomes a hard-headed business negotiation:

> (004-031) Bondy: "I'm not afraid to manufacture Karburators wholesale. Karburators for trains and ships. Karburators for central heating, for houses, offices, factories, and schools. In ten years' time all the heating in the world will be done by Karburators. I'll give you three percent of the gross profits."

The phrase "three percent of the gross profits" encapsulates Bondy's worldview: everything, including the divine, can be reduced to a licensing agreement. When Marek warns him that commercializing the Karburator means "letting God loose upon this world," Bondy responds with chilling commercial logic:

> (004-033) Bondy: "Then He will be personally indebted to me to that extent. And I hope that He won't show me any ill-feeling."

## Background and Origins

Bondy is introduced as a figure of established industrial power. The M.E.C. under his leadership is "the leading producer of iron" with "no competitor as regards boilers" and "world-famous" grates (001-011). His thirty-year career has built an empire of ten factories and 34,000 employees, yet Bondy feels the weight of that achievement as insufficiency: "after thirty years' hard work, gracious Heavens, surely one would have got bigger results elsewhere" (001-011).

His connection to Marek dates to their youth at the Technical School, where Marek was the "red-haired" dreamer and Bondy the practical one. Reflecting on the advertisement, Bondy remembers: "Marek had a head for science. He was a bit of a talker, but there was a touch of genius about the lad. He had ideas. In other respects he was a fearfully unpractical fellow. An absolute fool, in fact" (001-014).

The reunion is framed by Bondy's sentimental imagination — he pictures Marek as a "fallen inventor" in a garret, "horribly shaggy and dishevelled," and rehearses a scene of noble charity: "he takes a leather wallet from his pocket and lays on the table one, two ... three thousand-crown notes" (001-015). The reality — Marek as owner of a prosperous electric lamp factory — deflates this fantasy, but Bondy's mixture of sentiment and self-interest remains the engine of his decisions throughout.

## Business Decisions and the M.E.C.

### The Karburator Purchase

Bondy's negotiation with Marek in Chapters III and IV establishes the pattern for all his subsequent dealings with the Absolute. He initially dismisses the Absolute's effects as "poisonous gases" and seeks a practical solution:

> (003-018) Bondy: "That business in the cellar. That queer psychical condition. It's some form of poisoning, isn't it?"

When Marek insists on the theological reality, Bondy pivots to commercial logic without missing a beat. His worldview is expressed most concisely in his response to the question of God's existence:

> (003-032) Bondy: "From the business point of view, it's a matter of indifference. If He wants to exist, in Heaven's name, let Him. We aren't mutually exclusive."

He even proposes a formal legal contract with the Absolute:

> (004-025) Bondy: "Perhaps you could come to some agreement with Him. A proper formal contract, in something like this style: 'We guarantee to produce You discreetly and continuously to an extent to be fixed by mutual agreement; in return for which You pledge yourself to refrain from any divine manifestations within such and such a radius from the place of origin.'"

### The Church Negotiation

Bondy's business instincts extend to ecclesiastical politics. He arranges the meeting between Marek and Bishop Linda, serves as intermediary, and even purchases a baroque church on the White Mountain for three hundred thousand to offer the Absolute "more dignified quarters" (005-034). His approach to the Church is indistinguishable from his approach to any commercial counterparty:

> (005-040) Bondy introduced Marek to him as the owner of the ill-omened cellar at Brevnov. The Bishop rubbed his hands with delight while the wrathful engineer spluttered out something about being "delighted to have the honour."

### The Board Meeting

At the M.E.C. board meeting (Chapter VI), Bondy confronts his directors as the Absolute begins affecting them. His authority is challenged when board members experience religious ecstasy, and he responds with the full force of his industrial authority:

> (006-044) "Silence!" shouted Bondy, pale and with gleaming eyes, as he rose with the whole authority of his massive frame. "Gentlemen, if the factory for Karburators does not suit your fancy, I will take it over under my own personal charge."

## Reactions to the Absolute

### Initial Ecstasy

Bondy's first encounter with the Absolute in Marek's cellar is transformative, though he resists the implications:

> (002-048) G. H. Bondy fell on his knees, lost in a bewildering, shining ecstasy. He felt as if he must shout and sing, he seemed to hear about him the rustle of unceasing and innumerable wings.

Yet Bondy's characteristic pragmatism immediately reasserts itself. He processes the ecstatic experience through the language of industrial chemistry, retreating to the hypothesis of "poisonous gases" before Marek demolishes it.

### Mounting Crisis

As the Absolute's effects spread, Bondy tracks the escalating chaos with a businessman's ledger-book mentality. At the meeting with Bishop Linda, he reports:

> (005-018) Bondy: "Do you know that we had to have Mixa Street closed? ... People kept going there to pray. Whole processions of them. The police tried to disperse them, and seven people lost their lives."

He struggles between the commercial opportunity and the mounting disaster:

> (010-003) Bondy: "Are we to throw the whole thing up? Orders worth twenty-three millions came in today."

### Confession to Ellen

Bondy's encounter with Ellen, the thought-reader, forces him to confront the moral weight of his actions in a way no business ledger could:

> (010-028) "Then listen to me, Ellen," said Bondy. "I can tell you everything, for you'd read it in me in any case. I could never marry you, Ellen … neither business nor society is possible without thoughts that are not disclosed."

### Torment and Prayer

At moments of extreme crisis, Bondy's industrialist carapace cracks to reveal genuine spiritual torment. Before the Stvanice ceremony:

> (007-046) G. H. Bondy arose in great torment of spirit. "God! oh God!" he whispered, "if it be possible, remove this cup..."

## Appearances

Bondy appears throughout the novel as both actor and observer of the unfolding catastrophe:

- **Chapter I — The Advertisement** (001-002)–(001-029): Bondy discovers Marek's newspaper ad, reminisces about their school days, and drives to Brevnov. The chapter establishes his character through sharp satirical detail: his irritation with the syllable "tion," his imagination of Marek as a starving inventor, his surprise at finding a functioning factory.

- **Chapter II — The Karburator** (002-047)–(002-048): Bondy experiences the Absolute in Marek's cellar — the defining moment of his conversion from skeptic to believer as he falls to his knees in ecstasy.

- **Chapters III–IV — Pantheism and God in the Cellar** (003-001)–(004-033): Bondy negotiates the purchase of the Karburator patent, dismisses the Absolute as "poisonous gases" before Marek forces him to confront the truth, and declares his intention to mass-produce the device.

- **Chapter V — Bishop Linda** (005-001)–(005-064): Bondy arranges the meeting between Marek and Bishop Linda, attempts to negotiate a "franchise" for God with [[the-catholic-church|the Catholic Church]], purchases a baroque church for the Absolute, and hears Bishop Linda's grim prophecy.

- **Chapter VI — The Board Meeting** (006-001)–(006-048): Bondy confronts his own Board of Directors as the M.E.C. begins mass production of Karburators. His authority is challenged when board members begin experiencing religious ecstasy during the meeting.

- **Chapter VII — Developments** (007-046): Bondy wrestles with the mounting chaos, including the religious strike at the Industrial Bank and his prayer of torment.

- **Chapter IX — The Ceremony** (009-015): Bondy attends the opening of the Stvanice power station, representing the M.E.C. as the contracting firm, "biting his lips."

- **Chapter X — Saint Ellen** (010-001)–(010-030): Bondy wanders the streets of Prague, reflecting on the crisis, and encounters Ellen — a thought-reader who sees the state of his soul.

- **Chapters XIV–XXX**: Bondy navigates the escalating global crisis, including the Pacific Islands investment worth one hundred and twenty millions (022-030). He receives audience with the Pope before the Papal Brief (019-003), and his final appearances show a man broken by the forces he commercialized.

## Relationships

- **[[rudy-marek|R. Marek]]** (Engineer): Old schoolfellow from the Technical School and inventor of the Karburator. Bondy's foil — where Marek represents scientific conscience, Bondy represents commercial appetite:

> (001-014) Bondy: "It's very surprising that he isn't a Professor ... I haven't set eyes on him for twenty years. ... Yes, he must be down and out, living away over in Brevnov, poor chap ... and getting a living out of inventions! What an awful finish!"

- **Bishop Linda**: Religious authority who negotiates with Bondy over the miraculous cellar at Brevnov. Bondy treats him as a business counterparty, proposing a formal contract with God (005-040).

- **The Board of Directors**: M.E.C. leadership who approve Karburator production but soon fall under the Absolute's influence. Bondy's struggle to maintain commercial discipline against religious ecstasy is one of the novel's richest satirical sequences.

- **Ellen**: A young thought-reader Bondy encounters in [[prague-in-the-novel|Prague]] who reads the contents of his soul, forcing him to confront the moral weight of his actions (010-028).

- **The Pope**: Bondy receives a lengthy papal audience before the publication of the Papal Brief admitting the Absolute to the Church (019-003), representing the ultimate merger of industrial capitalism and organized religion.

## Fate and Ending

Bondy's arc in the novel's final chapters traces the dissolution of his commercial empire and his personal world. The Karburator-powered economy he created collapses under the weight of its own miraculous productivity — goods produced so abundantly and given away so freely that markets cease to function. His investment of one hundred and twenty millions in Pacific Islands real estate (022-030) represents his continued attempt to apply business logic to an apocalyptic situation.

Bondy's audience with the Pope (019-003) marks the convergence of his commercial enterprise with the highest levels of ecclesiastical power — the M.E.C. has, in effect, supplied God to the Vatican in the form of a twelve-ton Karburator. His final appearances in the closing chapters show him as a witness to the world he helped destroy, the reluctant agent of a catastrophe he never intended but could not prevent.

## Business Philosophy

Bondy's worldview is expressed most concisely in his response to the question of God's existence:

> (003-032) Bondy: "From the business point of view, it's a matter of indifference. If He wants to exist, in Heaven's name, let Him. We aren't mutually exclusive."

This statement captures the novel's central satirical insight: industrial capitalism is so totalizing that even God becomes just another production input. Bondy does not deny God's existence; he simply finds it irrelevant — until the Absolute begins interfering with share prices.

## Significance

Bondy represents Capek's satirical portrait of twentieth-century industrial capitalism: a system so confident in its own logic that it attempts to process the divine through the same mechanisms as iron production. His arc — from bored businessman to unwitting apostle of catastrophe — mirrors the novel's warning about technological hubris. Bondy never acts with malice; he simply applies the only logic he knows, and the world burns as a result.

The character also serves as Capek's vehicle for exploring the relationship between commerce and conscience. Bondy is not a villain in any conventional sense — he is decent, sentimental, even kindly in his way — but his very normalcy makes the catastrophe he enables more disturbing. The novel suggests that the greatest dangers come not from evil intentions but from the routine operation of systems that cannot accommodate consequences beyond their own logic.
