## Overview

Brno is the capital of Moravia and the second city of Czechoslovakia. In Čapek's novel, it appears in two distinct phases: first as a participant in the M.E.C.'s scientific testing program (Karburator No. 3 at the Technical Institute), and later as a site of the Absolute's autonomous industrial activity, when the atomic boilers begin operating factories without human direction.

## Location

Brno lies in southern Moravia, at the confluence of the Svitava and Svratka rivers, approximately 200 kilometers southeast of Prague. Its Technical Institute, mentioned in the novel, represents the scientific and engineering establishment of interwar Czechoslovakia.

## Significance in the Novel

### Scientific Testing

In the M.E.C. chairman's presentation of Karburator test results, Brno holds a distinguished position: "No. 3 is in the testing laboratory of the Brno Technical Institute". Unlike the industrial deployments in Prague, Hradec Králové, and Slaný, Brno's Karburator serves a scientific purpose — it is an instrument of study, not production. Professor Lupen of the Institute later provides an expert endorsement: "From the standpoint of effective performance, the Absolute is a force deserving of the highest respect".

### Industrial Takeover

As the Absolute's influence spreads, Brno's industrial infrastructure becomes a site of autonomous machine operation. The Absolute "has captured Liberec and the Brno cotton works, Trutnov, twenty sugar factories, sawmills, the City Brewery in Pilsen", operating factories without human labor — a phenomenon described as "insane overproduction" that threatens economic collapse. Brno's textile industry, central to the city's economic identity, becomes a theater of the Absolute's drive to work through machinery.

## Thematic Resonance

Brno embodies the dual character of the Karburator revolution: rational investigation and irrational proliferation. The Technical Institute's laboratory represents the Enlightenment ideal of dispassionate scientific study — the conviction that any force, however mysterious, can be understood and controlled through systematic inquiry. But the Absolute's subsequent capture of the Brno cotton works — operating machines beyond human command — demonstrates that such confidence is misplaced. The same city that housed the most scientific of the early Karburator installations becomes one of the most dramatic sites of its industrial autonomy.
