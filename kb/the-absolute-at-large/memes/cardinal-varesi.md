# Cardinal Varesi

Cardinal Varesi is the Archbishop of Venice and a senior figure in the Catholic Church's hierarchy, appointed as Procurator Dei — God's Advocate — in the expedited canonization and deification process of the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] during Chapter XIX.

## Appointment as Procurator Dei

Following the Papal Brief that admitted the Absolute into the Church, a Deification Commission was established to declare the Absolute not merely a Saint but a God. Cardinal Varesi was selected for the crucial role of Procurator Dei:

> A Deification Commission was immediately appointed from among the best of the Church's scholars and pastors. Varesi, the Cardinal Archbishop of Venice, was appointed Procurator Dei, while Monsignor Cullatti was to act as Advocatus Diaboli. (019-004)

This appointment placed Varesi in the role of arguing *for* the Absolute's divinity, a position that inverted the traditional canonization process where the Advocatus Diaboli (Devil's Advocate) would argue against sainthood and the promoter of the cause would argue for it. In this case, Cullatti as Advocatus Diaboli was tasked with challenging the deification, while Varesi as Procurator Dei was responsible for proving it.

## The Seventeen Thousand Testimonies

Varesi's primary contribution was the assembly and presentation of an overwhelming body of evidence supporting the Absolute's miraculous nature:

> Cardinal Varesi presented seventeen thousand testimonies to miracles performed, signed by nearly all the cardinals, patriarchs, primates, metropolitans, princes of the Church, archbishops, principals of Orders, and abbots. To each testimonial were appended expert reports by medical authorities and members of faculties, opinions from professors of natural sciences, technicians, and economists, as well as the signatures of eyewitnesses and legal authentications. (019-005)

The scale of this documentation — seventeen thousand signed and authenticated testimonies — reflects the bureaucratic machinery of the Church brought to bear on what is essentially a satirical inversion of the canonization process. Varesi acknowledged that even this monumental dossier barely scratched the surface:

> These seventeen thousand documents, Monsignor Varesi stated, represented but an insignificant fraction of the miracles actually performed by the Absolute, their number having already at a conservative estimate exceeded thirty millions. (019-005)

The claim that over thirty million miracles had been performed underscores Capek's satirical portrayal of the Absolute phenomenon as having achieved a scale of influence that overwhelms any institutional attempt to catalogue or control it.

## Role in the Deification Process

The canonization process proceeded rapidly under Varesi's advocacy. He also secured detailed expert opinions from the world's leading scientific authorities — including Professors Gardien of the Sorbonne, Meadow of Glasgow, Lupen of Brno, and the chemist Willibald of Tubingen — whose testimonies were published in the Acts of the Holy See. The process culminated successfully, though a theological dispute soon erupted when the Patriarch of Constantinople declared the Absolute to be the First Person of the Trinity rather than the Third, splintering the Christian world.

## Satirical Significance

Cardinal Varesi embodies Capek's satire of institutional religion confronted with industrial modernity. The figure of a Prince of the Church diligently compiling seventeen thousand dossiers to prove that a factory byproduct is God represents the collision between bureaucratic rationality and religious faith that runs throughout the novel. Varesi's earnestness in the role of Procurator Dei — a position whose very name inverts the traditional canonization procedure — underscores the absurdity of the Church's attempt to juridically process the divine.
