## Overview

Mrs. Prouzova (also referred to as "Aunt Prouzova") is a minor character in Karel Čapek's *The Absolute at Large*, a neighbour of the Blahous family in the rural hamlet of Seven Cottages. She appears in Chapter XXVIII as part of the trio of elderly villagers whose simple, quotidian conversation forms a comic counterpoint to the apocalyptic events of the Greatest War (028-001).

## Role & Significance

Mrs. Prouzova functions as an embodiment of the ordinary person's bafflement at world events. While nations clash and ideologies consume one another, she leans on the fence and asks the simple, unanswerable question: "what's all this awful to-do about nowadays?" Her response to Grandfather Blahous's elaborate theories about the war — a yawn and the remark "What's the use of it all?" — represents Čapek's deflationary humour at its finest, puncturing grand narratives with the irreducible reality of human indifference (028-003).

## Appearances

Mrs. Prouzova appears in Chapter XXVIII ("At Seven Cottages"), where she is introduced alongside her neighbours:

> And while the world shook with the clash of armies... their neighbour, Mrs. Prouzova, was leaning on the fence, repeating meditatively, "Yes, yes." (028-001)

She raises the central question that none of the villagers can answer: "And what I want to know is what's all this awful to-do about nowadays?" (028-003). When Grandfather Blahous offers his elaborate theory about the Jews, the Pope, and the Karburators, she responds with characteristic simplicity:

> "Yes, yes," said Aunt Prouzova, yawning. "What's the use of it all?" (028-005)

Her late husband is mentioned as having been a spiritualist or medium. According to Grandfather Blahous, a falling-out over a joke about summoning an evil spirit caused the two neighbours to never speak again — a piece of village history that illustrates the petty conflicts that, in Blahous's view, mirror the larger ones consuming the world (028-004).
