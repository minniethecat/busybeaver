## Overview

The coral island of Hereheretua is the setting of the novel's final chapter ("A Coral Island in the Pacific"). Purchased by G. H. Bondy as a private retreat before the outbreak of the Greatest War, the island becomes the last refuge of the man who commercialized the Karburator — and the stage for the novel's concluding meditation on technology, responsibility, and escape.

## Location

Hereheretua is a coral island in the Pacific Ocean. Its name, which does not correspond to any real island in the Tuamotu archipelago or elsewhere, is Čapek's invention. The island's isolation — accessible only by sea, surrounded by "mangroves and bananas" — represents the maximum possible distance from the Břevnov cellar where the Karburator was invented.

## Significance in the Novel

### Bondy's Refuge

The chapter opens with Bondy on the veranda of his bungalow:

> Bondy still looked anything but tranquil. He sat droopingly on the veranda of his bungalow on the coral island of Hereheretua, which he had purchased just before the outbreak of the Greatest War. Captain Trouble was glowering suspiciously at the thicket of mangroves and bananas which surrounded the bungalow.

The image is rich with irony: the industrial magnate who brought the Karburator to the world now sits on a remote Pacific island, unable to find peace. The island's name — "Hereheretua" — sounds vaguely Polynesian but is itself a fiction, like Bondy's attempted escape from the consequences of his actions.

### The Final Chapter

The coral island serves as the novel's closing geography, the end point of a trajectory that began in a cellar in Břevnov. From the most intimate domestic space (Marek's workshop) to the most remote location on Earth (a Pacific atoll), the Absolute has followed every human being who encountered it. Bondy's flight to the ends of the earth cannot outrun what he helped unleash.

## Thematic Resonance

The coral island is the novel's final geographic symbol: the end of the earth, the last refuge, the place you go when there is nowhere left to run. Bondy's purchase of the island "just before the outbreak of the Greatest War" suggests both foresight and futility — he saw the catastrophe coming and secured his escape, but the escape offers no real sanctuary. The novel ends not with resolution but with Bondy drooping on his veranda, the Pacific stretching before him, and the question of human responsibility for technological creation hanging unanswered in the tropical air.
