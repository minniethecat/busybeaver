## Overview

Hradec Králové is the first city outside Prague to be explicitly cited as a Karburator success story in the novel. The M.E.C.'s technical report lists it among the early testing deployments: "No. 5 is supplying the city of Hradec Králové with light. That is the ten-kilo pattern". This brief but significant mention establishes Hradec Králové as a milestone in the Karburator's spread across Czechoslovakia.

## Location

Hradec Králové lies in eastern Bohemia, at the confluence of the Elbe and Orlice rivers, approximately 100 kilometers east of Prague. Historically known by its German name Königgrätz — site of the decisive 1866 battle of the Austro-Prussian War — the city represents the reach of the M.E.C.'s industrial network beyond the capital.

## Significance in the Novel

### Early Karburator Deployment

Hradec Králové appears in the M.E.C. chairman's presentation of Karburator test results, alongside deployments on the Sázava River, the Upper Vltava, the Brno Technical Institute, and a mill at Slaný. The ten-kilo pattern Karburator assigned to Hradec Králové represents a mid-range model, smaller than the fifty-kilo "monster" installed at Stvanice for all of Prague, but substantial enough to power a city's lighting infrastructure.

The deployment is part of a broader pattern: the M.E.C. first proves the Karburator in industrial and municipal settings before the Absolute's spiritual effects become public knowledge. Hradec Králové, like other early adopters, receives the technology as a purely material innovation — clean, limitless energy — before anyone understands what else the atomic boilers release.

### Implied Consequences

Though the novel never returns to Hradec Králové explicitly, the global pattern of Karburator-induced religious mania would inevitably reach the city, as it reaches every location with an operating atomic boiler. The city's fate mirrors that of all Karburator-equipped communities: technological triumph followed by spiritual upheaval.

## Thematic Resonance

Hradec Králové exemplifies the novel's pattern of provincial cities being drawn into the Karburator revolution — and its consequences — without agency or warning. The city's brief, technical mention contrasts starkly with the spiritual upheavals that follow Karburator deployment elsewhere, implying that every location receiving the technology, no matter how prosaically documented, becomes a site of transformation beyond human control.
