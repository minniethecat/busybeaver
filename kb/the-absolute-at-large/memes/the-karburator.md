The **Karburator** is the central technological device in Karel Čapek's *The Absolute at Large* (1922) — a machine invented by engineer [[rudy-marek|R. Marek]] that achieves "the complete utilization of atomic energy" (002-005) through perfect combustion of matter. Unlike conventional engines that leave ash and waste, the Karburator disintegrates matter entirely, releasing enormous mechanical power. Its catastrophic side effect — the liberation of [[the-absolute|the Absolute]], God imprisoned in every atom — drives the novel's entire plot from industrial triumph to global catastrophe.

Marek describes the Karburator's mechanism to Bondy with a grain-milling analogy: "when the grinding is perfect, there's nothing or next to nothing left of the matter we burn. It's ground up completely. It is used up. It returns to its original nothingness" (002-015). One kilogramme of coal, under complete combustion, would run a good-sized factory for several hundred hours (002-016). The enormous copper cylinder in Marek's cellar, "closed on all sides except at the top, where there was a grating bedecked with seals" (002-020), represents a revolution in energy technology — and an existential threat to human civilization.

## In the Novel

### Conception and Design

Marek worked on the Karburator for twenty years before perfecting it (002-008). His first three working models failed; the fourth succeeded (003-019). The device's physical form — a massive copper cylinder on cement supports, driving a heavy flywheel through smooth, regular piston motion (002-020) — conveys industrial solidity belying its metaphysical consequences.

Marek's theory draws on Spinoza, Fechner, and Leibniz: "Fechner teaches that everything, everything that is, is penetrated with the divine, that God fills with His being the whole of the matter in the world" (003-001). The Karburator operationalizes pantheism into an industrial process:

> (003-003) Marek: "As a result of its complete disintegration of matter, my Perfect Karburator manufactures a byproduct: pure and unconfined Absolute, God in a chemically pure form. At one end, so to speak, it emits mechanical power, and at the other, the divine principle. Just as when you split water up into hydrogen and oxygen, only on an immensely larger scale."

### The Karburator's Effects

The Karburator runs continuously for six weeks in Marek's cellar, saturating the space with the Absolute:

> (003-020) Marek: "In a single day the cellar was chock-full of the Absolute, ready to burst with it; and it began to spread all over the house. The pure Absolute penetrates all matter, you know, but it takes a little longer with solid substances."

The effects are immediate and dramatic. Marek's porter and his wife — previously "thoroughly hardheaded" freethinkers — begin performing miracles and uttering prophecies (003-021). Marek himself experiences levitation, clairvoyance, and prophetic visions: "I could read people's thoughts, light emanated from me, I had a desperate struggle not to become absorbed in prayer" (003-022).

> (003-023) Marek: "I tried to clog the Karburator up with sand, but I was seized with a bout of levitation. That machine won't let anything stop it."

### Commercialization

Despite Marek's warnings about the Absolute — "it hurls God as a byproduct into the world" (003-024) — Bondy sees only commercial opportunity: "I'll give you three percent of the gross profits. The first year it will only be a few millions, perhaps" (003-030). He commissions mass production of Karburators:

> (003-029) Bondy: "I'm not afraid to manufacture Karburators wholesale. Karburators for trains and ships. Karburators for central heating, for houses, offices, factories, and schools. In ten years' time all the heating in the world will be done by Karburators."

### Global Proliferation

Within the novel's timeline, "several thousand Karburators" are operating worldwide (014-008), each continuously emitting the Absolute. The consequences spiral beyond anyone's control:

| Stage | Scale | Effect |
|-------|-------|--------|
| Laboratory | One Karburator in Marek's cellar | Porter's conversion, Marek's levitation |
| Local | Břevnov district | Religious manifestations in neighboring houses |
| National | Czechoslovakia | Industrial banks, factories "captured" by the Absolute |
| Global | Thousands of units worldwide | [[the-greatest-war|The Greatest War]] (Chapter XXV) |

Bondy's industrial strategy weaponizes the Karburator's byproduct: "We'll flood the Germans and the French with them; then we'll bombard England with the Absolute" (014-013).

## Interpretations

### Technology as Theology

The Karburator literalizes a metaphysical proposition: what if God were not a matter of faith but a measurable physical substance? Marek's "factory for the Absolute" (003-018) reduces divine creation to an industrial process. Čapek satirizes technological hubris — the conviction that any phenomenon, including God, can be engineered, scaled, and commodified.

### Unintended Consequences

The Karburator exemplifies the law of unintended consequences in technology. Marek's goal was to solve the energy crisis; the result was global religious war. The novel prefigures twentieth-century anxieties about technologies (nuclear fission, industrial chemistry) whose byproducts prove as consequential as their intended outputs.

### The Faustian Bargain

Bondy knowingly accepts the Absolute's dangers in exchange for profit: "it will lay a frightful load upon your conscience. Make up your mind!" Marek warns (003-006). Bondy's choice — commercial gain over spiritual caution — positions the Karburator deal as a modern Faustian pact, with the Absolute playing Mephistopheles.

### Class and Immunity

Marek notes that his third fitter, "a drinker," proved "to some extent immune" to the Absolute's effects (003-020). This detail — the inebriated proletarian resisting divine illumination — is a characteristic Čapek aside: spiritual experience may be a luxury of the sober, or conversely, the working class may be too grounded for metaphysical intoxication.
