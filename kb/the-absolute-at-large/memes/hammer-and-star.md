## Overview

The Hammer and Star is a Masonic lodge of the French obedience operating in Prague, with G. H. Bondy as its Worshipful Master. The lodge and its extraordinary meeting form the subject of Chapter XVII — one of the novel's most densely satirical set-pieces, in which a secret society dedicated to reason and enlightenment grapples with the eruption of the irrational and the divine into its ordered world.

## Role in the Novel

### The Extraordinary Meeting

Bondy convenes the lodge at an unusual hour. The setting is portentous:

> The Brothers of the Free French Masonic Lodge, "The Hammer and Star," took their seats, never taking their eyes off the Worshipful Master, G. H. Bondy, who had called them together at such an unusual time. The lodge was as quiet as a church between the black-draped walls with the maxims of the craft woven in the hangings.

The description "as quiet as a church" is a deliberate inversion: the Masonic lodge, a temple of Enlightenment rationalism, now confronts a phenomenon that is indistinguishable from religion. The black-draped walls and woven maxims become, in this context, as ritualistic as any cathedral's vestments.

### The Debate and the Vote

The brothers debate the Absolute with all the procedural solemnity of Masonic tradition. One brother, speaking from personal experience, declares the phenomena "praiseworthy, uplifting, and moral" and moves that "our Lodge should declare its neutrality with respect to all these manifestations of the divine presence".

The motion for neutrality is the novel's sharpest satire of institutional evasion. Rather than take a position — for or against the divine force reshaping their world — the Masons vote to abstain. The Worshipful Master then adds a worldly postscript: "the Industrial Federation has elected the Absolute as its honorary President" and "a donor who wishes to remain unknown has contributed one thousand shares to the Benevolent Fund of our Lodge". The spiritual and the financial are inextricably entangled.

### The Lodge's Endorsement

Later in the novel, it is reported that "the Masonic Lodges commend the Absolute to the support of their members", indicating that the Hammer and Star's neutrality was always a temporary posture. Like every other institution exposed to the Absolute — the Industrial Bank, the Ministry of War, the wireless station — the lodge eventually capitulates.

## Thematic Significance

The Hammer and Star chapter is Čapek's most sustained satire of institutional reason in the face of the irrational. Freemasonry — with its elaborate rituals, its Enlightenment heritage, its claim to rational brotherhood — should be the institution best equipped to analyze and respond to the Absolute. Instead, it votes for neutrality, accepts the anonymous donation, and eventually joins the general capitulation. The lodge's "quiet as a church" is the novel's verdict: in the presence of the divine, all institutions look the same.
