# Grandfather Blahous

Grandfather Blahous is the elderly father of Dr. Blahous (the youthful university lecturer introduced in Chapter XII). He appears only in Chapter XXX, "At Seven Cottages," where he lives in quiet retirement as the world around him descends into the cataclysmic religious wars sparked by the Absolute.

## In the Novel

Grandfather Blahous appears in a brief domestic scene at Seven Cottages (Sedm Chalup), the rural hamlet where he lives with his wife, old Mrs. Blahous, and their neighbour Mrs. Prouzova [2147-PPP]. The scene is starkly juxtaposed against the global warfare raging elsewhere: while armies clash and nations crumble, old Mrs. Blahous peels potatoes, Grandfather Blahous sits on the doorstep smoking beech-leaves, and their neighbour leans on the fence making small talk [2147-PPP].

The conversation among the three elderly residents is deliberately banal and reiterative — consisting of variations on "Yes, yes" and "My word, yes" — contrasting with the apocalyptic events described in surrounding chapters [2149-PPP][2151-PPP].

When Grandfather Blahous finally introduces a news item — "They say the Italians got a good hiding" [2159-PPP] — the comment is followed by an ambiguous response: "Aye, who knows?" [2161-PPP], suggesting the remoteness of world events from the daily experience of rural life.

## Significance

The Blahous household at Seven Cottages represents one of Čapek's narrative devices for contrasting the grand sweep of history with ordinary domestic life. Grandfather Blahous, smoking beech-leaves on his doorstep, embodies an almost stoic detachment — whether from wisdom, ignorance, or simple exhaustion is left deliberately ambiguous. The scene also ties the Blahous family name to two registers of the novel: the intellectual ambition of Dr. Blahous in Prague and the rustic endurance of his parents in the countryside.
