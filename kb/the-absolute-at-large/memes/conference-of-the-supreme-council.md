## Overview

The Conference of the Supreme Council, held in strict secrecy on the uninhabited Hebridean island of St. Kilda, represents the world's first diplomatic attempt to address the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] crisis. Summoned by British Premier Sir W. O'Patterney, the gathering brings together the ambassadors and premiers of the great powers — only to demonstrate, with devastating satire, that the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] has already been co-opted into every nation's self-interest, making international cooperation impossible.

## The Setting

St. Kilda, a barren rock of pliocene tufa far west of the Hebrides, was chosen precisely for its isolation — far from any [[the-karburator|Karburator]]'s influence. Within three days, carpenters and upholsterers transform the uninhabited island into a luxurious diplomatic venue. Sixteen torpedo-boats enforce a total news blackout, though the *New York Herald* correspondent I. Sawitt infiltrates the conference disguised as a waiter.

> [(020-009)] St. Kilda is a little island, practically nothing more than a rock of pliocene tufa far to the west of the Hebrides. A few stunted birches, a handful of heather and dry grass, flocks of nesting seagulls and semi-arctic butterflies of the order *Polyommatus* are all that lives on this lost outpost of our hemisphere.

## The Nationalist Gods

The conference's central revelation is that the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] has no universal character — it has adopted the national temperament of every country it touches. M. Dudieu provides the first inventory:

> [(020-025)] "Yes, gentlemen, *c'est ça*. We cannot remain indifferent to Him. We must either recognize Him or deny Him. We Frenchmen are in favour of the latter course!"

Dudieu catalogs the [[the-absolute|Absolute]]'s political incarnations: in France an anti-militarist and radical; in Italy a nationalist dreaming of the Roman Empire; in America a sportsman and Prohibition-dodger; in Britain a Conservative clergyman. Each delegate then confirms the pattern:

> [(020-033)] Baron Yanato then said smilingly: "In my country He is quite at home. A very, very nice God. He has adapted Himself very well. Indeed, a very great Japanese."

[[general-buchtin|General Buchtin]] insists He is Russian, an Orthodox Slav of "the great Russian soul." [[dr-wurm|Dr. Wurm]] describes a "State [[the-absolute|Absolute]]" developed into "the higher consciousness of the State." Mr. Kei, fingering his Buddhist rosary, says only: "Buddha."

## The Collapse of Diplomacy

The attempt to find a "common line of conduct with regard to [[the-absolute|God]]" collapses immediately. Each power insists its own [[the-absolute|Absolute]] is the true one. The session ends in acrimony:

> [] Sir W. O'Patterney, regardless of polite usage, came out with his hat on his head: his face was deep red; M. Dudieu followed him in silence. Prince Trivelino walked away, looking very pale, Baron Yanato at his heels with his perpetual smile. The last to leave was Mr. Kei, with downcast eyes, an exceedingly long black rosary sliding through his fingers.

No official communiqué is issued except one about colonial spheres of interest. The conference's failure is total: the world's diplomats cannot agree on [[the-absolute|God]] because each nation has already made [[the-absolute|God]] in its own image.

## Narrative Significance

The St. Kilda Conference is Čapek's most concentrated satire of international politics. [[the-absolute|The Absolute]], supposedly a universal divine essence, reveals itself to be a mirror of human tribalism — it becomes whatever each nation already is. The scene's dark comedy lies in the diplomats' complete inability to recognize the irony: they argue about whose [[the-absolute|God]] is truest, never suspecting that the [[the-absolute|God]] they are experiencing is their own collective projection. The failed conference foreshadows the religious world war to come.

## Aftermath

No decision of value emerges from St. Kilda. "Unforeseen events were shaping themselves in the womb of history." Within months, the rival national [[the-absolute|Absolute]]s would drive their host nations into the apocalyptic Greatest War.
