In Karel Čapek's *The Absolute at Large*, the discovery of the [[the-karburator|Karburator]] — a machine that achieves total atomic combustion and releases God as an industrial byproduct — triggers a three-way struggle among the novel's most powerful institutions. [[g-h-bondy|G. H. Bondy]] and his [[m-e-c|M.E.C.]] seek to commodify the Absolute as a limitless energy source. [[bishop-linda|Bishop Linda]] and the [[the-catholic-church|Catholic Church]] move to canonize and regulate it as a licensed miracle. And the apparatus of the State — from the President and Cabinet to the [[the-peoples-journal|Press Bureau]] — scrambles to manage the cascading political consequences, culminating in the Greatest War [(025-003)]. Each institution fails, and as Čapek shows, they fail for the same fundamental reason: the Absolute cannot be controlled because it operates on a logic utterly incompatible with institutional power. This pattern extends from the [[the-board-meeting|M.E.C. boardroom]] to the [[conference-of-the-supreme-council|St. Kilda conference table]] to the battlefields of the [[the-greatest-war|Greatest War]].

## The Industrial Capture

G. H. Bondy's approach to the Absolute is that of the pure capitalist. When engineer Marek warns him that manufacturing Karburators means "letting God loose upon this world," Bondy replies with chilling commercial confidence: "Then He will be personally indebted to me to that extent. And I hope that He won't show me any ill-feeling" [(004-033)]. Bondy envisions a vast industrial empire: "Karburators for trains and ships. Karburators for central heating, for houses, offices, factories, and schools. In ten years' time all the heating in the world will be done by Karburators" [(004-031)]. The M.E.C. raises its share capital to a billion and a half, builds a factory complex at Vysocany with seven thousand bricklayers, and soon has orders pouring in from every corner of the globe [(007-003)].

The industrial capture fails for two reasons.

### The Conversion of the Workforce

First, the Absolute is not a passive energy source — it is a conscious, proselytizing divine presence that converts everyone who comes near it. Bondy's own Board of Directors dissolves into religious mania: Rosenthal becomes an orthodox Jewish mystic, Hubka joins the Bohemian Brethren, and Machat proposes handing over the factory to the poor [(007-031)–(007-033)]. As Bondy's manager reports: "It was terrible; they talked religion until morning ... They were like men possessed" [(007-033)]. The Sugar Bank officials, infected by their Karburator central heating, "opened the safes and gave away the money to anyone who came. They finished by burning bundles of banknotes on a bonfire in the main hall" [(007-037)]. Bondy cannot staff his own enterprise — the Absolute has turned every employee into a saint.

### The Destruction of the Market

Second, the Absolute's infinite productivity shatters the economic framework that makes industrial capitalism possible. The Absolute "simply squandered its activity. It had found a quantitative expression for its own infinitude: overproduction" [(014-016)]. Goods become valueless. The market collapses. "Thus there ruled in the world a state of boundless plenty of all that men could need. But men need everything, everything but boundless plenty" [(014-016)]. Bondy's attempt to harness God as a source of profit ends not in wealth but in economic catastrophe.

> "The Absolute wants work. It clings furiously to life. Once it created the earth; now it has flung itself into manufacture." — G. H. Bondy's internal monologue [(010-005)]

## The Church's Response

Where Bondy sees a commodity, Bishop Linda sees a jurisdictional threat. Summoned to inspect the miracle-working cellar at Břevnov, the Bishop undergoes the full supernatural experience — "illumination, miraculous powers, ecstasy, everything" [(005-030)] — yet emerges unmoved in his institutional loyalty. He tells Bondy "that from the Catholic standpoint God cannot be brought into the matter, that the Church absolutely rejects and forbids the pantheistic hypothesis as heresy. In short, that this isn't any legal, duly recognized God, supported by the authority of the Church" [(005-028)].

The Church's strategy pivots from rejection to appropriation. The Consistory decides to place the Absolute "provisionally under its patronage" and proclaim the Břevnov cellar a "miraculous shrine and place of pilgrimage" — but with strict conditions: diluted Absolute only, coal exclusively from a district with an existing Marian shrine, and a monopoly on all production [(005-050)–(005-056)]. It is a breathtakingly cynical attempt at regulatory capture of the divine, reducing God to a franchise operation.

The Vatican escalates this strategy to its logical conclusion. A Papal Brief admits the Absolute to the bosom of the Church. A Deification Commission is appointed, with Cardinal Varesi as Procurator Dei and Monsignor Cullatti as Advocatus Diaboli [(019-004)]. Seventeen thousand testimonies to miracles are collected [(019-005)]. A theological committee establishes "the identity of the Absolute with the Third Person of the Trinity" on the basis of Scripture and the Fathers [(019-011)]. On the twelfth of December, the Pope intones the "In nomine Dei Deus" from St. Peter's, and the bells of all Catholic churches ring out: "Habemus Deum" — We have a God [(019-016)].

> "We cannot permit the rise of some new God or possibly a new religion." — Bishop Linda [(005-048)]

But the Church's attempt to monopolize the Absolute collapses immediately. The Patriarch of Constantinople declares the Absolute to be the First Person of the Trinity instead [(019-012)]. The Free Thought Society proclaims the Absolute the God of the Freethinkers [(019-013)]. The German Monists lay the foundation stone of a Cathedral of the Atomic God [(019-014)]. Negroes in the Congo "slaughtered and ate the missionaries and bowed down to a new idol which they called Ato or Alolto" — atomic motors, as it turned out, supplied by German agents [(019-015)]. The Church's effort to make the Absolute a Catholic God instead unleashes a centrifugal explosion of competing theologies that no institution can contain.

## The State's Intervention

The State is the third and most hapless competitor. Unlike Industry and Church, which at least have coherent strategies, the State's response is pure damage control. When the Minister for War "saw the light" and assembled the Prague garrison to preach eternal peace, he had to resign at once [(005-036)]. When the Karburator at Stvanice — a fifty-kilo monster meant to light all Prague — is ceremonially inaugurated in the presence of the President, the Burgomaster, and the diplomatic corps, the dignitaries fall to the earth and worship God. Bondy flees in terror [(009-022)].

The Press Bureau's communiqué is a masterpiece of institutional denial:

> "Contrary to the reports issued by a local publication which have obtained some currency abroad, we are able to state on the very best authority that no improper incidents of any kind took place on the occasion of the formal opening of the Karburator Central Power Station ... It is in any case decidedly improper to connect the Chief of State with regrettable incidents occasioned only by insufficient ventilation, combined with excessive strain upon the nerves." [(009-029)]

The State's instrumentalities are progressively overwhelmed. The Absolute takes possession of the Government mints and printing establishments, "and every day it flung out upon the world hundreds of millions of banknotes, coins, and securities" [(015-012)]. The Ministries of Supply, Commerce, Social Welfare, and Railways are paralyzed by religious ecstasy — "the personnel of each of the Ministries were the victims of grace in unusual power, and spent their office hours in joyful prayer" [(015-014)]. The Cabinet sits in permanent session and threatens to proclaim a state of siege, but is powerless [(007-027)].

The ultimate expression of State failure is the St. Kilda Conference, where the Great Powers assemble on a remote island precisely "in order to eliminate any direct influence of the Absolute on its decisions" [(020-005)]. The diplomats discover they cannot agree on anything — each nation claims the Absolute as its own. General Buchtin insists "He's a Russian, a genuine Russian, a Slav" [(020-016)]. Baron Yanato counters that "He is a great Japanese. The whole world can adopt the Japanese faith" [(020-023)]. The conference collapses without result, and the Greatest War — a religious war fought with atomic weapons over competing theologies — consumes the globe [(025-003)].

> "We have to agree upon a common line of conduct with regard to God." — Dr. Wurm, German Imperial Chancellor [(020-028)]

## The Pattern of Failure

The three institutions fail in structurally identical ways, which is Čapek's central satirical insight.

| Institution | Strategy | Instrument | Failure Mode | Outcome |
|-------------|----------|------------|--------------|---------|
| **Industry** (Bondy/M.E.C.) | Commodification | Mass-produced Karburators | The Absolute converts the workforce and destroys the market through infinite overproduction | Economic collapse; Board of Directors descends into religious mania |
| **Church** (Bishop Linda/Vatican) | Canonization and regulatory capture | Papal Brief, Deification Commission, Procurator Dei | The Absolute inspires competing theologies — Orthodox, Monist, Freethinker, animist — that shatter ecclesiastical monopoly | Theological fragmentation; Antipope elected; Catholic vs. Protestant Holy War |
| **State** (President/Cabinet/Press Bureau) | Containment and denial | Press communiqués, state of siege, St. Kilda Conference | The Absolute infiltrates every state apparatus — army, mint, ministries — and nationalizes God into mutually incompatible forms | The Greatest War: thirteen nations destroy each other under a birch tree |

Each institution approaches the Absolute as something to be *managed* — as a resource, a doctrine, or a public order problem. But the Absolute is not manageable. It is infinite, protean, and radically democratic: it manifests to everyone in their own cultural idiom. As Dr. Wurm observes at St. Kilda, "He knows just how to appeal to the people. In every case, He adopts the mentality of the country He is in" [(020-017)]. An entity that can be simultaneously an Orthodox Slav, a Japanese national deity, an American sportsman, and a Communist radical simply cannot be enclosed within any single institutional framework.

What makes the satire so devastating is that none of the institutions is defeated by superior external force. Each is undone *by its own logic*. Industry pursues infinite production and discovers that infinite production is economic ruin. The Church pursues theological monopoly and discovers that a real God inspires infinite theological diversity. The State pursues order through national sovereignty and discovers that a universal God turns every nation's claim into a casus belli.

## Čapek's Satire

Bishop Linda delivers the novel's most prescient line before a single Karburator has been commercially deployed: "Because mankind, whether believers or unbelievers, cannot do with a real and active God. We simply cannot, gentlemen. It is out of the question" [(005-062)]. This is the thesis that the entire novel proceeds to prove.

Čapek's target is not religion, capitalism, or the state in isolation — it is the human habit of *institutionalizing* the transcendent. Every institution in the novel attempts to make the Absolute serve its existing purposes: profit, orthodoxy, sovereignty. The Absolute refuses all of them, not out of malice but out of its own nature. It is genuinely divine — which is to say, it is what it is, and what it is cannot be reduced to a boiler, a dogma, or a diplomatic communiqué.

> "The greater the things are in which a man believes, the more fiercely he despises those who do not believe in them. And yet the greatest of all beliefs would be belief in one's fellow-men." — Father (Canon) Jost [(030-044)]

Čapek's conclusion is gently radical: the only way to live with the divine is to stop trying to control it. As [[mr-brych|Brych]] puts it: "A man may certainly think that another religion is a bad one, but he oughtn't to think that the man who follows it is a low, vile, and treacherous fellow. And the same applies to politics and everything" [(030-043)]. Years after the Greatest War has burned itself out, former sect leaders Brych, Kuzenda, Binder, and Canon Jost sit in a tavern eating liver sausages and discussing sauerkraut. "It's just the same with sauerkraut as it is with religion. One man can't understand how another can believe anything different" [(030-037)]. The Canon, who once defended orthodoxy with ferocity, now asks to try Moravian sauerkraut — a different way of preparing an old dish [(030-046)]. And the [[the-karburator|last Karburator]] is discovered in a cellar, "forbidden by the police" as a breach of the peace — the divine reduced to a contraband narcotic [(030-050)]. The institutions that sought to capture the Absolute all destroyed themselves and much of the world with them. What endures is not any institution's claim to God but the simple human capacity to believe in other people.
