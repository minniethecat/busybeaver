## Definition

In the novel, **enlightenment** operates on two levels: (1) the intellectual illumination that accompanies the Absolute's presence, granting sudden clarity and insight, and (2) the satirical inversion of Enlightenment rationalism — the idea that ultimate truth arrives not through reason but through an industrial accident.

## Marek's illumination

Marek describes his inventive process in terms of revelatory insight [174-PN]:

> "I had moments of astounding illumination. I saw, for instance, quite clearly, things that would happen in the future."

The word "illumination" carries both scientific and mystical connotations. Marek is an engineer, but his description of discovery mirrors the language of religious visionaries. The novel suggests that the boundary between scientific insight and mystical revelation is thinner than rationalists admit — both involve seeing what was previously invisible.

## Mass enlightenment

The chronicle records enlightenment alongside ecstasy as a mass phenomenon [1487-PN]:

> "Of wonders, ecstasy, enlightenment, inspiration and belief!"

The grouping is deliberate: enlightenment is placed between ecstasy (emotional transport) and inspiration (creative impulse), suggesting a spectrum of cognitive effects produced by the Absolute. Enlightenment, in this taxonomy, is the intellectual component of divine experience — the sudden understanding, the clarity of vision.

## Enlightenment inverted

Čapek's satire targets the Enlightenment project itself. The 18th-century philosophes promised that reason would liberate humanity from superstition. In Čapek's world, the liberation comes — but it's produced by a machine, not by rational inquiry. The Absolute provides what reason promised: universal truth, clarity, moral insight. And it's delivered as a Karburator byproduct, rendering the entire rationalist project redundant.

The novel thus suggests a paradox: the Enlightenment sought to replace religion with reason, but the ultimate rational achievement (atomic energy) accidentally brings religion back — and the religion that returns is more powerful and more disruptive than the one reason tried to banish.
