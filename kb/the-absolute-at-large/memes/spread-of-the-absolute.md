The **Spread of the Absolute** traces the geographic and temporal trajectory by which the divine essence — released as a byproduct of Marek's Karburator — radiates from a single concrete cellar in the Prague suburb of Břevnov to every corner of the globe. Capek's novel maps this expansion with remarkable geographic precision, tracing an arc that moves from laboratory to capital city, from capital to waterways, and from waterways to every industrialized nation on earth. The pattern reveals Capek's central satirical metaphor: industrial infrastructure functions as the vehicle of spiritual contagion, with factories, power stations, dredges, and shipping lanes serving as "arteries of the divine" — a darkly comic vision in which the spread of God operates on precisely the same logic as the spread of capitalism, technology, and global trade.

## Phase 1: The Laboratory (Břevnov)

The Absolute's earthly career begins in the most unassuming of settings: the cellar of Marek's small factory at 1651 Mixa Street, Břevnov, a working-class suburb west of Prague's center. Here the engineer R. Marek has spent twenty years perfecting the Karburator, a machine that achieves complete atomic combustion — and which, as an unintended consequence, releases God from matter.

The cellar itself is described with deliberate industrial plainness: "a huge thick reinforced door just like an armour-plated safe in a bank," concrete walls, a copper cylinder on cement supports, the rattle of a ventilator. There is nothing sacred about the space. Yet within six weeks of the Karburator's activation, the cellar has become the most theologically significant location in the world. Marek describes the initial outbreak:

> In a single day the cellar was chock-full of the Absolute, ready to burst with it; and it began to spread all over the house. The pure Absolute penetrates all matter, you know, but it takes a little longer with solid substances. In the air it spreads as swiftly as light.

The first casualties are the porter and his wife, living directly above the cellar. A "thoroughly hardheaded chap, a monist and a freethinker," the porter suddenly acquires the gift of healing by laying on of hands and begins delivering religious addresses. His wife utters prophecies. Both are removed to a sanatorium. Next, Marek himself begins to experience "visions of gigantic, swampy primeval forests, overgrown with mosses and inhabited by weird monsters" — the God of the Carboniferous Age, presumably released from the Upper Silesian coal feeding the machine.

The Břevnov phase establishes the pattern: the Absolute does not respect boundaries. "You can't shut it up, you can't soak it up. It's mischief let loose," Marek laments. Nothing — "ashes, sand, metal walls," even the collected works of positivist philosophers — can contain it.

## Phase 2: The Capital (Prague)

From Břevnov, the Absolute radiates outward through metropolitan Prague. The spread follows no single vector but rather leaches through the city's districts with the apparent randomness of an epidemic — yet always along the pathways of the new industrial infrastructure.

Bondy reports the first wave to Marek with barely suppressed alarm: cases of ecstasy appear at No. 457, just steps from a baroque church that remains mysteriously untouched. Two more cases break out in Vokovice and Košiře, while at the Petřín wireless station there is "practically an epidemic of religion" — the wireless operators broadcasting "a sort of new gospel to the world at large: God coming down again to the earth to ransom it".

The most politically destabilizing outbreak occurs in Dejvice, the villa district favored by government officials:

> The Minister for War. He "saw the light" all of a sudden in his villa at Dejvice. The following morning he assembled the garrison of Prague, talked to them about eternal peace, and exhorted the troops to become martyrs. Of course he had to resign at once.

The crowning moment of Prague's saturation comes with the ceremonial activation of the Central Karburator at Stvanice, a "fifty-kilo monster" built to light the whole of Prague. The ceremony, attended by the President of Czechoslovakia, the Burgomaster, the diplomatic corps, and Chairman Bondy, unfolds with all the civic pomp of an imperial occasion — bands, fireworks, a hundred-and-one-gun salute, the Castle illuminated. But the moment the President presses the lever that lights the city, the Absolute surges through the assembled dignitaries with catastrophic force. The Burgomaster resigns and retreats to the country. The doyen of the Diplomatic Corps quietly expires on his feet. Bondy flees the building "as pale as death".

By this point, the city is saturated. Karburators have been installed in the Houses of Parliament, in every government department, in the Industrial Bank, in residential blocks in the New Town. The infrastructure of the modern capital has become the infrastructure of divine transmission. "It's terrible, Bondy, the way it's spreading," Marek groans — and the journalist Cyril Keval reports that "things are fairly seething everywhere".

## Phase 3: The Waterways

The transition from Prague to the wider world occurs not over land but along water. The M.E.C.'s first Karburator deployments after the prototype cluster are riverine: "Karburator No. 1 drives the suction pump on the Sazava River, and has been running without attention for fourteen days. No. 2, the dredge on the Upper Vltava, is working splendidly". No. 5 lights the city of Hradec Králové on the Elbe. The deployment pattern follows the Vltava-Elbe river system — the historic trade artery of Central Europe — suggesting that Capek deliberately maps the Absolute's spread onto the geography of industrial shipping.

The dredge M.E.28 on the Vltava above Štěchovice becomes the most vivid embodiment of this riverine phase. Captain Kuzenda transforms the floating industrial platform into "an iron church," a site of pilgrimage where "several hundred people" gather on Sundays, "even sitting on both banks of the river, and behold, our coffee multiplied itself so that there was enough for everybody". The Vltava serves not merely as a setting but as a medium: the fish gather to listen, the water itself seems to participate in worship, and the spiritual radius of the dredge's Karburator extends outward along the river corridor — "on the bank He only does inspirations and conversions, and in Stechovice, with a favourable wind, you only notice a kind of holy fragrance".

The waterways function as the crucial bridge between the local and the global. Marek's boast that "with half a hundredweight of coal in the Karburator you can sail right round the world in a steamship" foreshadows the mechanism of global spread. The M.E.C. establishes a department for "ships' engines" within weeks of beginning serial production, and the Sazava-Vltava-Elbe corridor — connecting inland Bohemia to Hamburg and the North Sea — becomes the primary route by which Karburator-powered vessels carry the Absolute into international waters.

## Phase 4: Global Dissemination

Once the Karburator enters mass production, the spread becomes exponential and uncontrollable. The M.E.C. reports "thirteen thousand orders" within months, including two hundred for Russia, nine hundred for Japan, and over five thousand for Germany. General agencies are established in forty-eight capitals. An atomotive flies "from Prague to Melbourne, Australia, without a stop". The infrastructure of globalization — trade networks, shipping lanes, international banking, multinational industrial combines — becomes the infrastructure of divine contagion.

By the time Bondy walks the streets of Prague reflecting on the situation, the scope is already planetary: "There are several thousand Karburators at work in the world already, every one of them pouring forth the Absolute day and night". His proposed solution — "we'll flood the Germans and the French with them; then we'll bombard England with the Absolute... We'll infect the whole industrial and financial world with God, and preserve only our own country as an island of civilization" — makes explicit what Capek's geography has implied all along: the Absolute spreads on exactly the same channels as industrial capitalism. God is a byproduct of trade.

The climax of global dissemination is the so-called Greatest War (1944--1953), in which the Absolute, having saturated every industrialized nation, transforms geopolitical conflict into religious warfare on an unprecedented scale. Armies fight under crosses of different colors. "Supernatural powers" intervene on both sides. Karburators become military targets — their destruction triggering "a frightful explosion which felled every tree and structure within a radius of twelve kilometres and scooped out a crater over two hundred metres deep". A Japanese fleet lands in California; the Emperor Bobinet occupies keys to every continent; 198,000,000 men take part in the fighting, "and all but thirteen of them fell".

The chronicler concedes midway through the narrative that "the Absolute has already flooded the world, and has become a mass-phenomenon", and by the end the geographic trajectory is complete: from the cellar at 1651 Mixa Street to every battlefield, factory floor, and coral atoll on Earth.

## Phase 5: The Geography of Belief

Capek's geographic mapping of the Absolute's spread is not incidental to the novel's argument — it is the argument. By tracing the divine contagion along the pathways of industrial capitalism, Capek constructs a satire in which the geography of production and the geography of salvation are indistinguishable. The Absolute does not appear in churches (Bondy notes with bemusement that the baroque church near Břevnov remains unaffected, while the factories erupt in ecstasy). It does not follow routes of pilgrimage. It follows supply chains.

The river system is the novel's master metaphor for this convergence. The Vltava and Elbe are simultaneously industrial arteries (carrying coal, dredging sand, powering pumps) and spiritual arteries (carrying the Absolute, hosting Kuzenda's floating congregation, transmitting grace downstream). When Kuzenda preaches that God "is about six hundred metres in diameter, and even then is weak towards the edges", he is describing the emission radius of an industrial boiler — divinity measured in the same units as steam pressure.

The ultimate irony is that the spread cannot be stopped precisely because the economy cannot be stopped. Bondy, contemplating whether to halt production, faces orders worth twenty-three million and concludes, "It can't be stopped now. It means the end of the world, or something." The profit motive drives the global dissemination of God even when God's global presence is demonstrably catastrophic. Industry, having become religion's infrastructure, cannot be dismantled without dismantling civilization itself.

## Map of the Spread

Read cartographically, the novel traces a shape that begins as a point, expands into concentric circles, then follows a branching riverine network outward to the globe:

- **Point of origin**: 1651 Mixa Street, Břevnov. A single concrete cellar.
- **First radius** (~1 km): Marek's house and factory — the porter, the workmen, Marek himself.
- **Second radius** (~5 km): Prague's western suburbs — Vokovice, Košiře, Petřín, Dejvice.
- **Third radius** (~10 km): Central Prague — the New Town, Stvanice, the government quarter, the banks.
- **River corridor** (~100--300 km): The Vltava south to Štěchovice; the Vltava-Elbe corridor northward to Hradec Králové, toward Hamburg and the North Sea.
- **Continental spread**: Germany (5,000+ orders), Russia (200), France, Italy, Sweden — every major European industrial nation.
- **Global spread**: Japan (900 orders), the Americas (Karburator bombardment from airships), Australia (transcontinental atomotive flights), the Pacific (coral island colonies).
- **Final contraction**: As Karburators are destroyed in the Greatest War, the Absolute recedes until it lingers only in isolated pockets — the Seven Cottages, remote mountain hamlets, places where the industrial infrastructure never fully penetrated.

This geographic pattern — expansion along industrial corridors, contraction as those corridors are destroyed — mirrors the lifecycle of a technological product, a viral epidemic, and (in Capek's satire) a religious awakening. The fact that all three maps are identical is the novel's deepest joke.
