## Overview

Břevnov, a district on the western edge of Prague, serves as the physical origin point of the entire catastrophe in Capek's *The Absolute at Large*. At 1651 Mixa Street stands [[rudy-marek|R. Marek]]'s electric lamp factory — a modest industrial building whose cellar houses the original [[the-karburator|Karburator]], the machine that achieves complete atomic combustion and releases [[the-absolute|the Absolute]] as its unintended byproduct. From this cellar, God spreads outward through [[prague-in-the-novel|Prague]] and eventually across the globe, transforming a quiet manufacturing district into the epicenter of a metaphysical earthquake.

> [(001-022)] "Marek, Marek," said the inspector, searching his memory. "That must be Marek the engineer, of Marek and Co., the electric lamp factory, 1651, Mixa Street."

## Location

Břevnov is situated west of Prague's historic center, beyond Hradčany and the White Mountain. In the novel's geography, it represents the industrial periphery — far enough from the centers of power that Marek can work in obscurity for twenty years, yet close enough that when the Absolute escapes, it reaches the Minister for War's villa in nearby [[dejvice|Dejvice]] within days.

Bondy's journey from the city center to Břevnov is described with the irritation of a businessman venturing into unfamiliar territory:

> [(001-021)] He had rather a boring time while his car was gliding all over Břevnov in search of a mean hovel bearing the number 1651. They had to inquire at the police-station.

Bondy's expectation — a "mean hovel" — is confounded by the reality: "quite a pretty little factory" with flowerbeds and creeper on the walls [(001-028)]. [[rudy-marek|Marek]]'s success as an electric lamp manufacturer belies the desperation of his advertisement.

## The Factory

### Marek and Co., 1651 Mixa Street

The factory is described through Bondy's eyes as he arrives:

> [(001-028)] Bondy was soon ringing at the residential part of quite a pretty little factory.

> [(001-029)] It's all very nice and clean here ... Flowerbeds in the yard, creeper on the walls. Humph! There always was a touch of the philanthropist and reformer about that confounded Marek.

The factory's dual identity — a legitimate manufacturing business that also houses the world's most dangerous machine — is one of Capek's characteristic ironies. [[rudy-marek|Marek]] built his fortune on electric lamp filaments, a byproduct of his real obsession: the complete combustion of matter.

### The Cellar

The true significance of Břevnov lies beneath the factory, in "the arched concrete cellar, clean as a monastery cell" [(002-047)] where Marek installed the full-scale Karburator. The cellar's description emphasizes its resemblance to a sacred space — the "monastery cell" comparison is no accident in a novel about the industrialization of God.

> [(002-047)] And there in the middle of the arched concrete cellar, clean as a monastery cell, lay a gigantic copper cylinder resting on cement supports. It was closed on all sides except at the top, where there was a grating bedecked with seals.

The cellar is accessed through "a huge thick reinforced door just like an armour-plated safe in a bank" [(002-047)] — a detail that renders the building simultaneously a factory, a bank vault, and a shrine. Capek's architecture here is deliberately overdetermined: a place where industrial production, capital accumulation, and divine revelation collapse into one another.

The cellar is sealed, ventilated, and monitored. Yet the Absolute escapes through all barriers:

> [(004-012)] In a single day the cellar was chock-full of the Absolute, ready to burst with it; and it began to spread all over the house. The pure Absolute penetrates all matter, you know, but it takes a little longer with solid substances.

## Significance in the Novel

### Ground Zero

Břevnov functions as the novel's spatial origin point — the place where the chain of cause and effect begins. Everything that follows — the religious epidemics, the economic chaos, [[the-greatest-war|the Greatest War]] — radiates outward from 1651 Mixa Street. The geography is precise: [[dejvice|Dejvice]], Vokovice, and Košiře are the first districts affected, then the [[petrin|Petřín]] wireless station, then all of [[prague-in-the-novel|Prague]].

> [(005-034)] Marek," Bondy added in a whisper, "I'll tell you something, but it's a dead secret. A week ago it attacked our Minister for War."

> [(005-037)] "In Dejvice already!" groaned the engineer. "It's terrible, Bondy, the way it's spreading."

The spread pattern — from industrial Břevnov to the fashionable Dejvice villa of the Minister for War — traces the social geography of the city. Capek's Absolute respects no boundaries of class or district.

### The First Miraculous Shrine

As the Absolute's effects become public knowledge, Břevnov is transformed from industrial site to pilgrimage destination. Processions of worshippers converge on Mixa Street:

> [(005-018)] Bondy: "Do you know that we had to have Mixa Street closed? ... People kept going there to pray. Whole processions of them. The police tried to disperse them, and seven people lost their lives. They let themselves be knocked over like sheep."

The authorities' response — barbed wire, police cordons, the evacuation of neighboring houses — militarizes the sacred space:

> [(005-022)] Bondy: "We've blocked the street with barbed wire ... We had to clear the people out of the neighbouring houses — religious manifestations all over them, you know. A commission of the Ministries of Health and Education is occupying them now."

[[bishop-linda|Bishop Linda]]'s proposal to the Holy See formalizes Břevnov's new status:

> [(005-050)] Bishop Linda: "It would proclaim the premises in Břevnov known as No. 1651 a miraculous shrine and place of pilgrimage..."

The Church even negotiates to use coal from Male Svantovice, site of an existing Marian shrine, to fuel the Karburator at Břevnov:

> [(005-054)] Bishop Linda: "As you know, there is a miraculous shrine of the Virgin in that district, so that with the aid of this particular coal we might establish at No. 1651 Břevnov a centre for the worship of Our Lady."

### Contagion

The Břevnov cellar's most alarming property is its infectiousness. Objects moved from the infected zone carry the Absolute with them:

> [(005-038)] Bondy: "It's amazing ... The other day a man shifted his piano from the infected Mixa Street area out to Pankrác. In twenty-four hours the whole house was down with it."

This detail transforms Břevnov from a location into a vector — a source of spiritual contamination that spreads through the material infrastructure of urban life, as unstoppable as the gas Marek initially mistakes the Absolute for.

### The Factory for the Absolute

The narrator, looking back from a later perspective, refers to the Břevnov operation as "the Factory for the Absolute" [(013-002)] — a phrase that captures both Marek's description of his Karburator ("It acts, you might say, as a factory for the Absolute" [(004-006)]) and the novel's central satirical conceit. Břevnov is the place where God became a manufactured product.

## The Theft

Despite being "garrisoned by detectives and surrounded by a cordon of police," the original Marek Karburator is stolen:

> [(011-002)] Exactly how it happened has not yet been established, but just at the very time when the little factory belonging to R. Marek, Engineer, 1651 Mixa Street, Břevnov, was garrisoned by detectives and surrounded by a cordon of police, unknown malefactors stole the original Marek Karburator.

The theft — impossible to prevent, impossible to explain — reenacts the novel's central argument: no force, whether police cordons or Positivist philosophy, can contain the Absolute once it is released. The original machine disappears into the world it has already transformed.

## Thematic Resonance

Břevnov embodies several of Capek's key satirical themes:

1. **The Industrialization of the Sacred**: A factory that manufactures God transforms its district into a shrine. The police cordon, the barbed wire, the "Ministries of Health and Education" commission — the apparatus of the modern state deployed to manage a divine manifestation — renders the sacred as a public health problem.

2. **Center and Periphery**: Břevnov's location on Prague's western edge is thematically significant. The world-changing invention emerges not from a university laboratory or a government institute but from a modest factory in an obscure district, built by a man [[g-h-bondy|Bondy]] dismisses as likely "down and out" [(001-014)].

3. **Containment and Contagion**: The failure of every physical barrier — the armoured door, the ventilator, the barbed wire, the police cordon — to contain the Absolute reflects Capek's skepticism about humanity's ability to control the consequences of its technological creations.

4. **Memory and Origin**: As the novel progresses and the global catastrophe unfolds, Břevnov recedes into the background but remains the remembered origin point. The narrator's retrospective reference to "the Factory for the Absolute" [(013-002)] preserves Břevnov's status as the place where it all began.
