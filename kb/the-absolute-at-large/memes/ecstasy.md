## Definition

**Ecstasy** in Čapek's novel is not the culmination of spiritual discipline but an involuntary side effect of proximity to the Karburator. Characters fall into states of religious rapture without preparation, intention, or theological framework — the ecstasy is as mechanical as the energy production that causes it.

## Bondy's ecstasy

The most vivid ecstatic episode occurs when Bondy first encounters the Absolute in Marek's shed [339-PN]:

> "G. H. Bondy fell on his knees, lost in a bewildering, shining ecstasy. He felt as if he must shout and sing, he seemed to hear abysmal music, and he was seized now by a scorching fever, now by a pleasing coolness."

The physical language — "scorching fever," "pleasing coolness," "abysmal music" — presents ecstasy as a sensory overload rather than a spiritual state. Bondy, the pragmatic industrialist, is reduced to kneeling and shouting by something he cannot understand or control. The comedy lies in the contrast between his calculating business persona and this involuntary submission.

## Ecstasy as industrial byproduct

Čapek's satirical innovation is to recast religious ecstasy as a manufacturing externality. The Karburator produces two things: free energy and the Absolute. The energy powers factories; the Absolute produces ecstasy in anyone nearby. Neither product requires belief, ritual, or moral preparation — they are automatic outputs of a machine that Marek built in his shed.

This mechanization of the sacred is the novel's central joke: humanity spends millennia building religions to access divine experience, and then an engineer accidentally produces it as exhaust.

## Ecstasy in the chronicle

The chronicle records ecstasy as a widespread phenomenon during the Absolute's spread [1487-PN]:

> "Of wonders, ecstasy, enlightenment, inspiration and belief!"

The list form — wonders, ecstasy, enlightenment, inspiration, belief — suggests these experiences have become so common as to be enumerable, almost commodity-like. What was once the rarest of spiritual attainments is now so abundant it can be listed alongside "wonders" as a bullet point.
