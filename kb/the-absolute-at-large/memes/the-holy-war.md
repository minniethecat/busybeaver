## Overview

The Holy War is not a single conflict but a cascading sequence of religious wars that engulfs the world after the [[the-absolute|Absolute]]'s release. Čapek depicts the progression from absurd local skirmishes — a brawl in an Augsburg pencil factory — to continent-spanning crusades, demonstrating how divine certainty, combined with human tribalism, makes violence inevitable and total.

## Origins: The Augsburg Imbroglio

The war begins with a dispute of absurd triviality. In the Scholler lead-pencil factory at Augsburg, Catholic workmen beat a Protestant foreman over "some dispute relating to the worship of the Virgin." The conflict escalates with terrifying speed:

> [(023-005)] Scholler, the owner of the factory, was killed, and two directors were stabbed. The clergy were compelled by force to carry the monstrance at the head of the procession. The Archbishop, Dr. Lenz, who came out to pacify the demonstrators, was thrown into the River Lech.

Within twenty-four hours, the synagogue is dynamited, the City Council declares the Immaculate Conception, and the Bavarian Government proclaims a Holy War on Prussia. The Imperial Chancellor dispatches ten thousand bayonets; the troop trains are blown up at the frontier.

## Escalation: The Crusades

The Bavarian Holy War rapidly draws in the entire European continent. The conflict fractures along denominational lines — Catholics versus Protestants — but the religious certainty conferred by the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] ensures that neither side can compromise:

> [(023-024)] No quarter was given in the fighting. Cities were razed to the ground, the men killed, and women up to the age of fifty violated. But the first things destroyed in every case were the enemy Karburators.

[[the-absolute|The Absolute]] fights on both sides simultaneously, introducing supernatural weapons — earthquakes, cyclones, showers of sulphur, plagues of locusts — until conventional military strategy becomes meaningless. Soldiers abandon formations and fight individually, man against man, distinguished only by the colour of the cross on their chest.

## Global Reach

By February, the war has spread worldwide: a general revolt in India, a Muslim Holy War on Christians, the Protestant Union proclaimed at Stettin. Japan's [[the-absolute|Absolute]], the "very great Japanese" [[the-absolute|God]], backs its own imperial ambitions. Each nation's [[the-absolute|Absolute]] fights for its host, making every conflict a holy war:

> [(023-026)] It was apparent that while the Absolute attacked on one side, it also defended itself on the other.

## The Absolute as Combatant

The war's most grotesque feature is the [[the-absolute|Absolute]]'s direct participation as a supernatural combatant. Contemporaries report invisible hands seizing aircraft and dashing them to the ground, fifty-four-centimetre projectiles intercepted mid-flight and hurled back. The destruction of enemy [[the-karburator|Karburator]]s triggers invisible cyclones that scatter buildings "like someone blowing on a heap of feathers," followed by detonations carving craters two hundred metres deep.

> [(023-027)] Suffocating gases spread over a radius of three hundred kilometres, utterly blasting all vegetation; however, as these creeping clouds several times turned back upon their own ranks — through the strategical intervention of supernatural powers — this very unreliable method of warfare was abandoned.

## Narrative Significance

The Holy War sequence is Čapek's most thorough demolition of religious nationalism. [[the-absolute|The Absolute]], supposedly the source of universal love and truth, becomes the ultimate accelerant of human violence — not because it is evil, but because it amplifies whatever certainty humans already possess. Every combatant is absolutely convinced [[the-absolute|God]] is on their side, and that conviction makes compromise not merely difficult but theologically impossible. The satire's target is not religion per se but the human capacity to weaponize belief.

## Aftermath

The Holy War consumes itself. After the Greatest War and the Last Battle, civilization collapses. The final irony is that the war ends not through victory but through exhaustion — the last thirteen soldiers in the world simply stop fighting and fall asleep under a birch tree.
