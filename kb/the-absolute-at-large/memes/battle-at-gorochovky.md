## Overview

The Battle at Gorochovky is recorded by the historian Usinski as one of the final engagements of the Greatest War. Its significance lies less in military detail than in its historiographical function: it is one of several battles whose chroniclers each claim to represent the "last battle," illustrating how the war's chaotic, decentralized nature rendered objective history impossible.

## Context

By the time of the Greatest War's final phase (Chapter XXVIII), conventional military structures have disintegrated entirely. The war is no longer fought between armies but between individuals, each soldier carrying a knife, cartridges, and bombs, killing anyone whose chest-cross is a different colour. In this atomized battlefield, even basic facts — which battle was last, who won, how many died — become contested.

## Competing Claims

Čapek presents the historiographical problem directly. Usinski records the battle at Gorochovky; Leblond records a skirmish at Les Batignolles; Van Goo records a fight near Nieuport. The narrator refuses to adjudicate:

> [(028-007)] "The historian Usinski records a similar battle at Gorochovky, Leblond a skirmish at Les Batignolles, and Van Goo a fight near Nieuport; but it would seem that local patriotism influenced them more directly than genuinely historical motives. In short, no one knows which was the last battle of the Greatest War."

The irony is pointed: even the historians have been captured by the [[the-absolute|Absolute]]'s fragmenting logic. Each nation's chronicler claims primacy for their own local engagement, transforming historiography into yet another theater of holy war.

## The Prophecy Paradox

The narrator notes a further irony: while historians cannot agree on which battle was last, the series of prophecies that appeared *before* the Greatest War are "striking in their agreement." Prophecy, supposedly the most unreliable form of knowledge, proves more consistent than historical record — a reversal that underscores the epistemological chaos the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] has unleashed.

## Narrative Significance

The Battle at Gorochovky functions as a meta-historical commentary on the novel's larger themes. [[the-absolute|The Absolute]], by making every person divinely certain of their own truth, has destroyed the possibility of shared fact. If historians cannot agree on which battle ended the war, what hope is there for any common understanding? The battle's obscurity — it is named, located, and attributed to a chronicler, but never described — is itself the point: in the world the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] has made, even war becomes unknowable.

## Aftermath

The battle at Gorochovky, whether real or inflated by "local patriotism," joins the constellation of final engagements that mark the war's exhaustion. The definitive end comes not through any single battle but through the Last Battle under the birch tree, where the final thirteen soldiers simply stop fighting.
