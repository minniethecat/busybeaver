## Overview

Emperor Bobinet, born Toni Bobinet, is a twenty-two-year-old lieutenant of mountain artillery in the Czechoslovak army, stationed at Annecy in Haute Savoie, who becomes the most dramatic military manifestation of the Absolute's disruptive power. Starting from a remote garrison in the French Alps — where the altitude places him above the Absolute's atmospheric reach, allowing him to "think clearly and freely" (026-002) — Bobinet transforms himself from a bored junior officer into Emperor of France and then a global conqueror whose campaigns span from India to China. His trajectory from obscure lieutenant to world-historical figure parodies the Napoleonic myth, while his subsequent disappearance into an Amazon Empire of women provides the novel's most surreal denouement.

> [(026-002)] Bobinet, if you please, Toni Bobinet, the twenty-two-year-old lieutenant of mountain artillery, attached to the garrison of Annecy (Haute Savoie), but at present on six weeks' manoeuvres on the Needles (Les Aiguilles), from which on a clear day one can see in the west the lakes of Annecy and Geneva, and in the east the blunted ridge of the Bonne Montagne and the peaks of Mont Blanc.

## Role and Significance

### From Boredom to Empire

Bobinet's rebellion originates in boredom and altitude. Stationed over two thousand metres above sea level on Les Aiguilles, where "the air is pure and free from the Absolute, so that one can think clearly and freely" (026-002), he reads a two-week-old copy of the Annecy *Moniteur* containing an editorial by "M. Babillard" calling for "the strong hand of a helmsman who will steer the good ship France out of the raging storm toward new power and glory" (026-002). This newspaper editorial, combined with the panoramic view of a "miniature world which could easily be shot to pieces from where he sat" (026-002), triggers his transformation.

> [(026-002)] Lieutenant Toni Bobinet sat on a boulder and tugged at his tiny moustache, first because he was bored, and secondly because he had read a newspaper two weeks old right through for the fifth time, and was now thinking things over.

The temporal detail of the "two weeks old" newspaper is significant — Bobinet is so removed from the world that even his news is stale, yet this very isolation gives him the clarity to act when others are paralyzed by the Absolute's influence.

### The Campaign

Bobinet's rise is swift and largely bloodless — at first. He assembles his battery, deposes "the incompetent old captain," takes the military post at Sallanches, and declares war on the Absolute "with Napoleonic brevity" (026-003). The following day he shoots to pieces the Karburator in the bakery at Thones, occupies the railway station at Bonneville, and seizes command at Annecy with three thousand men. Within a week he has destroyed over two hundred Karburators and leads fifteen thousand bayonets and sabres against Grenoble. (026-003)

From Grenoble he commands forty thousand men, descends into the Rhone valley, and systematically clears the territory of atomic motors with long-range guns. His capture of the Minister for War — who had been driving to "put Bobinet back in his place" but is instead "captivated and convinced by Bobinet's plans" — epitomizes the absurd logic of his ascent. (026-003)

> [(026-003)] Within a week he had destroyed over two hundred Karburators and was leading fifteen thousand bayonets and sabres against Grenoble. He was proclaimed commandant of Grenoble, and now had a small army of forty thousand men at his back, with which he descended into the valley of the Rhone and busied himself in painstakingly clearing the surrounding territory of all atomic motors by means of his long-range guns.

By July, Parliament proclaims that Bobinet "had deserved well of his country" and raises him to First Consul with the title of Marshal. France is consolidated. Bobinet introduces State atheism — "any sort of religious demonstration was punishable by court-martial with death" (026-004). On August 14th he proclaims himself Emperor.

### The Napoleonic Anecdotes

Capek includes three satirical vignettes — "Bobinet and his Mother," "Bobinet and Love of Country," and "Bobinet and the Old Pensioner" — that caricature the genre of Great Man anecdote. In the first, Bobinet interrupts a military council to point out his aged mother warming herself in the Versailles park, moving hardened generals to tears (026-005). In the second, when a passing army motor splashes mud on his cloak, Bobinet restrains Marshal Jollivet from punishing the driver, saying, "Let him alone, Marshal. After all, this is the mud of France!" (026-006). In the third, Bobinet, moved by an old one-legged pensioner's story, sends him a personal courier bearing — the Bronze Medal (026-007). Each anecdote follows the exact syntax of Napoleonic hagiography while delivering a deflating punchline.

> [(026-007)] "Marshal, take this man's name," said Bobinet, deeply affected. And sure enough a week later there came a knock at the door of the old pensioner's hut; it was Bobinet's personal courier, who handed the hapless cripple a packet "from the First Consul." Who can describe the surprise and delight of the old soldier when upon opening the packet he found inside it the *Bronze Medal*!

### Imperial Strategy: The Keys

Bobinet's strategic vision is expressed entirely in the language of keys:

> [(026-006)] Bobinet's plan was the following: 1. To occupy Spain, and by taking Gibraltar secure the key to the Mediterranean Sea. 2. To occupy the Danube valley as far as Budapest as the key to the interior of Europe. 3. To occupy Denmark as the key to the North Sea area.

A fourth army occupies Asia Minor as "the key to the East," a fifth takes the mouth of the St. Lawrence as "the key to America," a sixth goes down in the naval battle off the English coast, and a seventh lays siege to Sebastopol. By New Year's Eve 1944, "the Emperor Bobinet had all his keys in the pocket of his artillery breeches" (026-006). The metaphor collapses under its own weight — keys, after all, need locks.

### The Amazon Fate

Bobinet's end is deliberately ambiguous. Stationed in Simla, India, he learns of an Amazon Empire of women in the unexplored river-territory of the Irawaddy, Salwin, and Mekong. He sets out with his Old Guard and never returns (025-007). The chronicler offers two versions:

> [(025-007)] According to one version he married and settled there. According to another, Amalia, the Queen of the Amazons, cut off his head in battle and flung it into a skin filled with blood, saying, "Satia te sanguine, quem tantum sitisti." This second version is doubtless the milder.

The chronicler's preference for the decapitation over the marriage — the second version is "doubtless the milder" — is one of the novel's darkest and funniest jokes.

- **[[general-hampl|General Hampl]]** — The tailor-adventurer who models his coup on Bobinet's methods. Hampl is a deserter from Bobinet's army who "had caught a whiff of Bobinetism" (028-010) and brings Bobinet's template of bluff-and-declaration to [[hradec-kralove|Hradec Kralove]].
- **Marshal Jollivet** — Bobinet's loyal subordinate who appears in the Napoleonic anecdotes, eager to punish the driver who splashed Bobinet's cloak. (026-006)
- **[[g-h-bondy|G.H. Bondy]]** — The industrialist whose Karburator invention creates the conditions Bobinet exploits. Bobinet's state atheism is a direct response to Bondy's unintended release of the Absolute.

## Thematic Significance

Bobinet embodies the novel's argument that absolute clarity of purpose — whether religious or secular — is indistinguishable from the will to power. Stationed above the Absolute's range, Bobinet represents pure rationality untempered by any spiritual dimension — and the result is empire-building, conquest, and mass death. His state atheism, enforced by execution, is the mirror image of the religious fanaticism he opposes. Both positions are founded on the same absolute certainty that Capek identifies as the root of violence.

The Napoleonic anecdotes are particularly telling. By reproducing the exact conventions of Great Man hagiography (filial devotion, patriotic sentiment, generosity to veterans) and then undermining each with a bathetic conclusion, Capek exposes the machinery of charismatic authority. Bobinet is not Napoleon — he is a twenty-two-year-old with a tiny moustache who happened to be in the right place at the right time with the right newspaper. And that, Capek suggests, is all it takes.
