## Summary

The Battle of Hradec Králové, depicted in Chapter XXVI of *[[the-absolute|The Absolute]] at Large*, is the novel's central satirical set-piece on war. Rather than describing the global conflict in sweeping military terms, Capek narrows the lens to a single provincial Bohemian town — the chronicler's own boyhood home — and stages a mock-battle fought with theatre props, brass bands, and bureaucratic farce. The chapter opens with the chronicler invoking "a drop of water" as a mirror of world events, then proceeds to show how a tailor-turned-adventurer seizes the city without firing a shot. The chapter's humour underscores the novel's darkest thesis: that the "Greatest War" is a species of collective madness, and that its heroes are, on close inspection, absurd.

> [(026-001)] The Battle of Hradec Králové

> [(026-003)] Well, then, the drop of water entitled Hradec Králové is memorable to the chronicler because he used to race about in it like a tiny organism, as an infusorian of the grammar school there, and not surprisingly thought it the universe; but enough of that.

## Context

### The Global Situation

By Chapter XXVI, [[the-absolute|the Absolute]] has spread globally through [[the-karburator|Karburator]]-powered factories, and multiple religious sects have formed around competing interpretations of the Absolute experience. The "Napoleon of the Mountain Brigade," [[emperor-bobinet|Toni Bobinet]], has risen from a bored lieutenant in the French Alps to Emperor of the French, destroying Karburators across Europe with his mountain artillery. The war that began on February 12, 1944 has consumed the globe for nearly a decade.

> [(025-003)] Well, the war which lasted from February 12, 1944 to the autumn of 1953, was in all truthfulness and without any exaggeration (on my honour!) the Greatest War.

> [(024-001)] The Napoleon of the Mountain Brigade

### The Chronicler's Method

Capek deliberately places the chronicler's boyhood town at the centre of this world conflagration, not to honour it but to demonstrate that even the great events of history, when inspected at close range, dissolve into petty local quarrels, theatrical gestures, and the sheer inertia of bureaucracy. The chapter is a sustained exercise in comic deflation. The chronicler invokes professional historians to justify this focus on the particular:

> [(026-002)] The present chronicler now appeals to August Sedlacek, Joseph Pekar, and other authorities on the writing of history, in support of the statement that a vast amount of historical knowledge can be drawn from purely local happenings. They mirror world events as in a drop of water.

## The Battle Itself

### The Armed Brewery

[[hradec-kralove|Hradec Králové]] enters the war armed with a single [[the-karburator|Karburator]] housed in the brewery behind the Church of the Holy Spirit. The narrator notes drily that [[the-absolute|the Absolute]] in this "hallowed environment" produces "a plentiful and ardently Catholic beer." The Prussians soon destroy the Karburator "in their Lutheran fury," but the town's real ordeal begins with the arrival of [[general-hampl|Hampl]], the tailor.

> [(026-004)] The Greatest War found Hradec Králové armed with only a single Karburator, and that was in the brewery still standing to this day behind the Church of the Holy Spirit, which is next to the residence of the Canons.

### Hampl's Coup

[[general-hampl|Hampl]] — a native of Hradec who served with the Foreign Legion, marched with [[emperor-bobinet|Bobinet]]'s troops to India, and deserted near Baghdad — returns home and founds the "Social-Revolutionary Party." His coup follows a script of pure farce:

> [(026-010)] In the name of His Highness the Emperor Bobinet, I hereby summon the royal City of Hradec Králové to place the keys of the city gates in my hands. If this is not done by sunset, I will put into effect the military measures I have prepared and will attack the city at dawn with artillery, cavalry and bayonets.

The demand produces an immediate bureaucratic crisis: the city gates have no keys. "A few locks and keys of historical interest which used to repose in the City Museum had been carried off by the Swedes." When [[general-hampl|Hampl]]'s forces march on the city — several thousand men in civilian clothes, accompanied by Mr. Cerveny's brass band playing "The Girls along the Highway" — they find the city entirely empty. The Burgomaster has fled. On the Council Chamber table, sheets of paper await the conqueror, each bearing the inscription "In the name of His Majesty the [[emperor-bobinet|Emperor Bobinet]]" — a preemptive surrender carefully prepared in advance.

> [(026-005)] Nevertheless, mindful of its historical continuity, Hradec maintained an agreeable religious temperature, especially when the enlightened Bishop Linda took over the diocese. And even when the Bobinets, the Turks, and the Chinese came, Hradec did not lose its proud consciousness that (I) it had the best amateur theatre in all Eastern Bohemia; (II) it had the tallest steeple in Eastern Bohemia; (III) the pages of its local history contained the greatest battle in Eastern Bohemia.

[[general-hampl|Hampl]] declares victory without a single casualty and is installed as Mayor. The chapter concludes with the wry observation that his administration "also was blessed with comparative peace, thanks to the wise counsels of [[bishop-linda|Bishop Linda]] and the Worshipful City Fathers" — a perfect circle of absurdity.

## Key Participants

- **[[general-hampl|Hampl]]** [(026-006)]: The tailor-adventurer who seizes Hradec Králové. A deserter from [[emperor-bobinet|Bobinet]]'s army, he models his coup on Bobinet's own methods — a chain of imitation that reduces military glory to farce.
- **[[mr-skocdopole|Burgomaster Skocdopole]]** [(026-006)]: The "circumspect" mayor whose response to the crisis is to discover that the city gates have no keys, and who later flees, leaving behind pre-written declarations of submission.
- **[[bishop-linda|Bishop Linda]]** [(026-005)]: The "enlightened" bishop who laughs at Skocdopole's fears and whose wise counsels manage to keep the city peaceful regardless of which faction claims power.
- **[[emperor-bobinet|Toni Bobinet]]** [(024-001)]: The "Napoleon of the Mountain Brigade" whose rise from bored lieutenant to Emperor provides the template that Hampl imitates. His trajectory from the French Alps to global empire sets the whole war in motion.
- **[[the-chronicle|The Chronicler]]** [(026-003)]: The narrator, who admits his attachment to Hradec Králové as his boyhood home and uses it as the "drop of water" in which to view world history.

## The Greatest War

### The Global Scale

The battle is only one fragment of a global catastrophe. Chapter XXV provides the scale: 198 million men take part in the fighting, and all but thirteen of them fall. The chapter catalogues the collapse of civilization with the same deadpan irony — Czechoslovakia is held in succession by "Swedes, French, Turks, Russians and Chinese," the Vysehrad Chapter "died out both on the male and female side," and the only reliable marker of the apocalypse is that a football championship is witnessed by "only one hundred and ten people."

> [(025-011)] Therefore I say: Do not rob the people of that time of their only boast — that what they went through was the Greatest War. We, however, know that in a few decades we shall succeed in arranging an even greater war, for in this respect also the human race is progressing ever upward and on.

### The War's Epitaph

Chapter XXIX ("[[the-last-battle|The Last Battle]]") provides the war's comic epitaph. No one can agree on which engagement was the last, and the only reliable evidence comes from a collection of pre-war prophecies, all of which mention the number thirteen and a birch tree. The last thirteen soldiers in the world, we are told, simply stop fighting, lie down in the shade of a birch, and decide to go home.

> [(029-027)] And such, one can well imagine, was the end of the Greatest War.

## Aftermath

The Battle of Hradec Králové itself has no military consequences whatsoever — [[general-hampl|Hampl]] simply becomes the next in a line of interchangeable administrators. But the war as a whole reduces civilization to ruins. Chapter XXVII finds [[g-h-bondy|G. H. Bondy]], the industrialist who began the whole chain of events, hiding on a remote Pacific island where cannibals have taken up the practice "more out of religion" — a final satirical echo of [[the-absolute|the Absolute]]'s promise of universal communion.

The novel's epilogue (Chapter XXX) shows the surviving characters — [[mr-brych|Brych]] the stoker, Canon [[father-jost|Jost]], the journalist [[mr-rejzek|Rejzek]], and the innkeeper Binder — reunited years later in a quiet tavern, reading [[the-peoples-journal|the People's Journal]] and eating liver sausages. The war is over, [[the-absolute|the Absolute]] has dissipated, and ordinary life has resumed its quiet rhythms.

## Narrative Significance

The Battle of Hradec Králové is Capek's definitive statement on the relationship between grand historical narrative and lived experience. By compressing a world war into the frame of a single small town — and rendering that war as a bloodless comedy of bureaucracy, imitation, and civic pride — Capek argues that the "great events" of history are, when examined closely, accumulations of petty human vanities. [[general-hampl|Hampl]]'s coup is a perfect miniature of [[emperor-bobinet|Bobinet]]'s career, which is itself a parody of Napoleonic ambition — a recursive chain of imitation that drains military glory of all meaning.

The chapter also completes the novel's satirical arc: [[the-absolute|the Absolute]], which began as a promise of infinite energy and spiritual transcendence, ends by reducing human conflict to its most absurd form — a war fought over the keys to gates that have no locks, with weapons that harm no one, by leaders who are interchangeable. [[the-chronicle|The chronicler]]'s choice to narrate the world war through the "drop of water" of his boyhood town is the book's final, most radical thesis: that history is not a grand narrative but an accumulation of local stories, each as important — and as absurd — as every other.
