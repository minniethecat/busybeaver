## Overview

The Sugar Bank is a Prague financial institution in *The Absolute at Large* notable as the first bank to install a Karburator (atomic boiler). The installation triggers a dramatic episode in which the bank's officials, seized by the Absolute's spiritual influence, give away the bank's money and burn bundles of banknotes — an incident characterized as "Religious Bolshevism" (007-037).

## Role in the Novel

### The Karburator Installation

The Sugar Bank was the first bank to install a Karburator, acquiring one for central heating. The consequences were immediate and catastrophic from a business perspective:

> It is indeed. Did you hear about the Sugar Bank? All the officials there were seized with it at one fell swoop. They opened the safes and gave away the money to anyone who came. They finished by burning bundles of banknotes on a bonfire in the main hall. Religious Bolshevism, I should call it. (007-037)

> Yes. For central heating. The Sugar Bank was the first to install one. Now the police have closed the Bank. Even the confidential clerks and the directors were affected. (007-039)

### Aftermath

The incident prompts G. H. Bondy, chairman of the M.E.C. (the company manufacturing Karburators), to issue an immediate directive:

> Send word round that the sale of Karburators to banks is forbidden. (007-040)

This ban represents one of the earliest attempts to contain the social disruption caused by the Absolute — a recognition that financial institutions are particularly vulnerable to its effects, as the logic of divine charity is fundamentally incompatible with the logic of banking.

### Industrial Bank Parallel

The Sugar Bank's fate prefigures a similar incident at the Industrial Bank, where officials declare the bank's property "a sacred trust for the poor". Both incidents illustrate the same satirical point: that the Absolute's moral influence makes capitalist institutions impossible to operate.

## Thematic Significance

The Sugar Bank episode introduces the term "Religious Bolshevism" — a satirical coinage that captures the novel's central anxiety: that divine influence, when unleashed into modern industrial society, produces effects indistinguishable from revolutionary politics. The bank officials' behavior (giving away money, destroying capital) mirrors Bolshevik rhetoric about the abolition of private property, but the motivation is religious rather than political. This blurring of the sacred and the revolutionary is a recurring theme in Čapek's satire.
