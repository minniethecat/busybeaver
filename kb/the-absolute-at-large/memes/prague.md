## Overview

Prague is the capital of Czechoslovakia and the central geographic stage of *The Absolute at Large*. The city serves as the hub of the [[g-h-bondy|Metallo-Electrical Company]]'s operations and the site of the first large-scale [[the-karburator|Karburator]] deployment, making it ground zero for both the technological triumph and the subsequent religious epidemic that engulfs the world (003-025). From the industrial suburb of [[brevnov|Břevnov]] to the ceremonial power station at [[stvanice|Stvanice]], Prague's districts each play a distinct role in the unfolding catastrophe that Capek chronicles.

## Location

Prague lies on the Vltava River in Central Bohemia. Within the novel, the action radiates outward from the industrial suburb of Břevnov (site of Marek's laboratory) through the city's districts — [[dejvice|Dejvice]], [[stvanice|Stvanice]], the New Town — and eventually encompasses the entire metropolitan area as the Karburator infrastructure expands. The Vltava itself serves as both a literal and symbolic artery: it carries the dredges powered by Karburators, witnesses the ecstatic gatherings along its banks, and in the novel's apocalyptic conclusion, sees its bridges destroyed in a desperate attempt to quarantine the chaos (025-008).

## Significance in the Novel

### Industrial Epicenter

The M.E.C., headquartered in Prague under G. H. Bondy's leadership, manufactures and deploys Karburators throughout the city. Marek boasts to Bondy that "with half a hundredweight of coal in the Karburator you can sail right round the world in a steamship, you can light the whole city of Prague, you can supply power for the whole of a huge factory" (003-025). The city becomes the proving ground for the atomic technology, and within weeks the M.E.C. has installed atomic boilers in "the Houses of Parliament and in all the Government departments" (007-043).

The speed of industrial transformation is staggering: the M.E.C. reports "thirteen thousand stationary atomic boilers. Eight thousand of the central heating apparatus. Nearly ten thousand atomobiles" (007-021), with much of this infrastructure concentrated in and around Prague. The New Town district receives a five-kilo Karburator for central heating in a residential block, and the company's administrative headquarters becomes a nerve center of the new atomic economy.

### The Stvanice Ceremony

The Central Karburator at Stvanice, a "fifty-kilo monster, a magnificent machine," is built "to light the whole of Prague" (007-043). Its ceremonial activation — attended by the President of Czechoslovakia, the Burgomaster of Greater Prague, and Chairman Bondy — marks the apex of technological optimism before the Absolute's effects manifest catastrophically. The ceremony culminates in a city-wide illumination:

> Then, in an instant, the whole of Prague shone out as a vast expanse of light, the crowds cheered, the bells in all the steeples began to swing, and from the Marianske fort there sounded the first boom of the cannon. (009-020)

The festivities are elaborate: "Flaming rockets shot up from Střelecky Island; Hradčany, Petřín, and even Letna, were aglow with garlands of electric lamps, distant bands began competing with each other, illuminated biplanes circled above Stvanice, while the immense V16 soared up from Vyšehrad all bedecked with lanterns" (009-021). Yet the ceremony's splendor masks the disquieting reality: the Burgomaster of Greater Prague resigns his office immediately afterward and "has gone into the country to recuperate" (009-029), while diplomats and officials experience overwhelming religious ecstasy.

The journalist Cyril Keval, covering the event for the *People's Journal*, documents both the civic pomp and the absurdity — including a rumor that the visible power station is merely a decoy, and that "a huge system of underground passages, storehouses, powder-magazines" conceals the real Karburator network beneath Prague's streets (009-008).

### The First Religious Outbreak

Prague becomes the first urban center to experience the Absolute's mass psychological effects. The Minister for War "saw the light" at his villa in Dejvice and "assembled the garrison of Prague, talked to them about eternal peace, and exhorted the troops to become martyrs" (005-036). The outbreak spreads through Prague's neighborhoods with alarming speed — Bondy reports a "fine case of ecstasy" near the White Mountain church, an "epidemic of religion" at the Petřín wireless station where operators broadcast "a sort of new gospel to the world at large," and cases in Vokovice and Košiře (005-034).

G. H. Bondy, walking through "the streets of Prague," reflects on the unraveling social order: "it was simply impossible to bring God to earth without a confounded lot coming of it" (010-003). The Industrial Bank suffers a religious strike within two days of installing a Karburator; officials declare "the bank's property to be a sacred trust for the poor" (010-003). The Absolute, Bondy realizes, "wants work. It clings furiously to life. Once it created the earth; now it has flung itself into manufacture" (010-006).

### Wartime Devastation

In the novel's apocalyptic conclusion — the so-called Greatest War fought with Karburator-powered weapons — Prague suffers catastrophic devastation. The city's population shrinks to "an insignificant little group"; the Vyšehrad Chapter dies out; and the last inhabitants "destroyed all the bridges, even the Charles Bridge, to save the right bank of the Vltava" from bears that had multiplied on Hradčany (025-008).

The image of Prague's iconic Charles Bridge — a symbol of the city's centuries of civilization — being demolished as a defensive measure against wild animals encapsulates Capek's dark vision: technology, having promised to illuminate and empower the city, ultimately reduces it to a state more primitive than the medieval.

### The Press and Public Discourse

Prague's newspapers become central to the narrative of the Absolute crisis. The *People's Journal*, with its Prague office, dispatches Cyril Keval to cover the Stvanice ceremony (009-002), and later becomes a hub of frantic reporting as the religious epidemic spreads through the city's squares — with Father Novaček performing miracles in Tyl Square and the Free Thought Society holding services in Havliček Square (022-002). Dr. Blahous, Lecturer in Comparative Religion at the University of Prague, prepares scholarly articles for the *Prague Times* attempting to analyze the phenomenon (012-002).

## Thematic Resonance

Prague embodies the tension between technological modernity and spiritual awakening that defines Capek's satire. The city's transformation — from industrial capital to site of mass religious ecstasy to war-ravaged ruin — mirrors the novel's central argument: that humanity, when given divine power through technology, cannot resist turning it toward destruction. The trajectory is written into the city's geography: from Břevnov's humble laboratory to Stvanice's triumphal power station to the shattered Charles Bridge, Prague's landmarks trace an arc from discovery through hubris to ruin.

Capek's choice of Prague as the epicenter carries particular resonance for his Czech readership. The novel's satire of bureaucracy, ecclesiastical politics, and nationalist fervor is grounded in the specific institutions and neighborhoods of the capital. The juxtaposition of the ancient (Hradčany castle, the baroque churches, the medieval Charles Bridge) with the hypermodern (atomic locomotives, wireless stations, Karburator-powered factories) creates a temporal vertigo that mirrors the spiritual vertigo of the Absolute itself. Prague's fate is the fate of civilization itself, condensed into one city's arc from enlightenment to ruin (025-008).
