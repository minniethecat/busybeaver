## Overview

The M.E.C. (Metallo-Electrical Company) is the industrial colossus that drives the central conflict of Karel Capek's *The Absolute at Large*. When [[g-h-bondy|G. H. Bondy]], Chairman of the [[board-of-directors|Board of Directors]], acquires the rights to [[rudy-marek|Marek]]'s [[the-karburator|Karburator]], his company transforms from a traditional heavy-industry concern into the world's most disruptive technology firm. The M.E.C. builds a massive factory complex at Vysočany, raises its share capital to a billion and a half, and begins churning out thousands of atomic motors — each of which releases [[the-absolute|the Absolute]] as a byproduct. Through the M.E.C., Capek satirizes the industrial-capitalist mindset that prioritizes production and profit over consequences, making the company both the novel's engine of technological progress and its vehicle of divine catastrophe.

## Structure

### The Board of Directors

The M.E.C. is governed by a [[board-of-directors|Board of Directors]], chaired by [[g-h-bondy|G. H. Bondy]]. Key board members include:

- **[[g-h-bondy|G. H. Bondy]]** -- Chairman and the novel's protagonist, an industrial strategist who balances commercial ambition against growing awareness of the Karburator's dangers.
- **[[dr-hubka|Dr. Hubka]]** -- leader of the opposition, initially the board's fiercest skeptic, questioning why the Company's own factories run on coal rather than Karburator power.
- **[[rosenthal|Rosenthal]]** -- an older board member forever seeking compromise ("m--m no, our Chairman will explain"), later driven to Talmudic mysticism by the Absolute.
- **[[mr-machat|Machat]]** -- a property magnate whose block of buildings receives a Karburator for central heating, making him the board's first visible casualty of "infection" when he gives away all his houses to the poor.
- **Miller, Homola, Kolator** -- board members who, once exposed, propose voluntary poverty and religious communalism.

### Share Capital and Finance

The M.E.C. is a publicly traded industrial powerhouse. Before the Karburator deal, the company "had ten factories and 34,000 employees" and was "the leading producer of iron" with world-famous grates and an unrivaled boiler business (Ch. I). After Bondy acquires [[rudy-marek|Marek]]'s invention:

> [(005-007)] "And we've increased our share capital. A billion and a half."

Within weeks, M.E.C. shares skyrocket:

> [(007-001)] "M.E.C. shares were quoted at 1,470 yesterday. The day before yesterday they were 750."

After mass-production begins in earnest, the share price reaches unprecedented heights:

> [(007-017)] "The Stock Exchange has simply gone mad. We stand at 8,000 today in Berlin."

The shares become known colloquially as "Absolute Stock":

> [(017-029)] "Further, that the M.E.C. shares, the so-called Absolute Stock, may go up still higher."

### Organizational Hierarchy

Beneath the Board, the M.E.C. operates through a General Manager who reports production figures directly to Bondy. The engineering division, headed by Krolmus, refines [[rudy-marek|Marek]]'s prototype into mass-producible designs. By the peak of production, the company has established "general agencies in forty-eight different capitals" and absorbed competitors through acquisition — the Stinnes interests and the American Ford works both amalgamate with the M.E.C.

## Role in the Novel

The M.E.C. is the corporate antagonist-as-protagonist. [[g-h-bondy|Bondy]]'s decision to commercialize the Karburator through the M.E.C.'s industrial infrastructure is the novel's inciting action. The company's factories at Vysočany become ground zero for [[the-absolute|the Absolute]]'s dissemination — each [[the-karburator|Karburator]] shipped to a bank, factory, hotel, or government building carries a payload of divine presence.

The M.E.C. embodies Capek's satire of industrial capitalism: an organization that can produce nearly-free, limitless energy, yet chooses to install coal boilers in its own factories while selling Karburators to everyone else, hoping to "infect the whole industrial and financial world with God, and preserve only our own country as an island of civilization" (Ch. X). The company's [[the-board-meeting|board meetings]] descend from orderly capitalist proceedings into ecstatic religious gatherings, mirroring the wider societal collapse.

## Key Scenes

### The Founding Decision (Chapter IV)

After experiencing the Absolute's effects in [[rudy-marek|Marek]]'s cellar at [[brevnov|Břevnov]], Bondy makes the fateful choice to commercialize:

> [(004-031)] "I'm not afraid," said Bondy, rising, "to manufacture Karburators wholesale. Karburators for trains and ships. Karburators for central heating, for houses, offices, factories, and schools. In ten years' time all the heating in the world will be done by Karburators."

Marek's final warning goes unheeded:

> [(004-031a)] "Bondy," Marek whispered, horror-stricken. "I warn you for the last time. It means letting God loose upon this world!"

### The Board Meeting (Chapter VI)

The board meeting of February 20th is the novel's definitive corporate farce. Bondy announces the commencement of mass production:

> [(006-001)] Bondy addressing the meeting of the Board of Directors of the M.E.C. (the Metallo-Electrical Company) held on February 20th -- "I have to inform you that one building of our new group of factories at Vysočany has been completed and began production yesterday. In a very few days the standardized production of Karburators will be in full swing, beginning with eighteen finished machines per day."

[[dr-hubka|Dr. Hubka]] challenges the Chairman on a glaring contradiction:

> [(006-005)] "Why hasn't our Chairman sufficient confidence in these Karburators of his to install them in our own establishment? Gentlemen, it's a shockingly bad advertisement for our Karburators if their manufacturer himself will not or cannot use them."

Bondy refuses to explain and instead offers the ultimate capitalist bribe:

> [(006-033)] "As far as the Karburator is concerned, gentlemen, I guarantee you a dividend of one hundred and fifty percent."

[[mr-machat|Machat]], now fully in the grip of the Absolute, proposes the board's dissolution:

> [(006-026)] "Gentlemen, let us give the factory to the poor! I move that we change the M.E.C. into a religious community of 'The Humble of Heart.'"

[[dr-hubka|Dr. Hubka]], ever the opportunist, closes the meeting with a motion that seals the board's fate:

> [(006-033a)] "I move, gentlemen, that every member of the Board of Directors shall receive one Karburator for testing purposes, so to speak."

### The Aftermath (Chapter VII)

Weeks later, the board has succumbed entirely:

> [(007-010)] "Yes, I think the Board of Directors must have caught it from Machat. You were not present at the last meeting, Chief. It was terrible; they talked religion until morning. Hubka moved that we hand over our establishments to the workers."

### Bondy's Strategic Calculus (Chapter X)

Walking the streets of [[prague-in-the-novel|Prague]], Bondy lays bare the M.E.C.'s real strategy -- weaponized industrial sabotage dressed as commerce:

> [(010-003)] "We'll flood the Germans and the French with them; then we'll bombard England with the Absolute. England is conservative, and won't have anything to do with our Karburators. Well, we'll drop them on her from airships like big bombs. We'll infect the whole industrial and financial world with God, and preserve only our own country as an island of civilization and honest labour free from God. It is a patriotic duty, so to speak, and besides, we have our own factories to consider."

### Mass-Production Unleashed (Chapter XIII)

The chronicler summarizes the M.E.C.'s industrial empire at its terrifying zenith:

> [(013-004)] "You must remember that the Stinnes interests with all their ramifications had amalgamated with the M.E.C., and that the American Ford works had flung themselves into mass-production which hurled thirty thousand finished Karburators out upon the world every day."

### The Ceremonial Karburator (Chapter VII)

The company's prestige reaches its apex with the Prague central Karburator at [[stvanice|Stvanice]]:

> [(007-035)] "It is to be ceremoniously set in motion at six o'clock the day after tomorrow, in the presence of the President, the Burgomaster, the City Council, and the representatives of the M.E.C."

### A Gift to the Holy See (Chapter XIX)

Even the Vatican receives an M.E.C. Karburator:

> [(019-017)] "Seven thousand priests with lighted candles escorted the Holy Father to St. Peter's, where the largest twelve-ton Karburator, a gift from the M.E.C. to the Holy See, had been erected behind the high altar."

## Significance

The M.E.C. is Capek's vehicle for satirizing the industrial-capitalist order of the early 20th century. It is a company that profits from God Himself -- the "Absolute Stock" -- while its chairman schemes to protect his domestic market from the same divine force he exports abroad. [[the-board-meeting|The board meeting]] in Chapter VI is a miniature of the entire novel's arc: rational business deliberation dissolves into religious ecstasy, and the profit motive proves unable to contain the forces it has unleashed.

The company's trajectory -- from iron and boilers to atomic motors to global religious chaos -- maps the novel's thesis: that technological "progress" driven solely by commercial logic is a form of catastrophe in slow motion. [[g-h-bondy|Bondy]]'s M.E.C. does not invent the Absolute; it merely industrializes it, packages it in copper cylinders, and ships it worldwide with a guarantee of "a dividend of one hundred and fifty percent." The irony is complete when Bondy himself, the arch-capitalist, finds himself muttering Christ's words in the garden of Gethsemane: "God! oh God! if it be possible, remove this cup..."

The M.E.C. stands alongside [[g-h-bondy|G. H. Bondy]] and [[rudy-marek|Rudolf Marek]] as one of the three pillars of the novel's central action -- the inventor, the industrialist, and the corporation that binds them together.
