## Definition

Pantheism — the doctrine that God or the Absolute pervades all matter and manifests equally in "men, as in stones, in the grass, the water — everywhere" (003-001) — is the philosophical key that unlocks Capek's central satirical conceit in *The Absolute at Large*. In the novel's theological vocabulary, pantheism posits that the divine substance is not transcendent (existing above and apart from the world) but immanent: imprisoned within every atom, awaiting liberation. When [[rudy-marek|R. Marek]]'s [[the-karburator|Karburator]] achieves complete combustion of matter, it releases not only atomic energy but the "chemically pure" (004-001) [[the-absolute|Absolute]] that matter had confined — a byproduct that Marek himself, a confirmed atheist, finds "absolutely intolerable" (003-001) because it forces him to confront the experimental verification of a hypothesis he had always dismissed.

## In the Novel

### Introduction: Marek's Pantheistic Hypothesis (Chapter III)

Pantheism enters the novel in Chapter III — titled simply "Pantheism" — when Marek, struggling to explain the Karburator's religious side-effects to the bewildered industrialist [[g-h-bondy|G. H. Bondy]], reaches not for atomic physics but for the metaphysical tradition:

> (003-001) "Do you know what Pantheism is? It's the belief that God, or the Absolute, if you prefer it, is manifest in everything that exists. In men, as in stones, in the grass, the water — everywhere. And do you know what Spinoza teaches? That matter is only the outward manifestation, only one phase of the divine substance, the other phase of which is spirit. And do you know what Fechner teaches?"

Marek's invocation of Spinoza (1632-1677) and Gustav Fechner (1801-1887) is strategically eccentric. Spinoza's *Deus sive Natura* identified God with the totality of existence — a position that got him excommunicated from Amsterdam's Jewish community and condemned by both Catholic and Protestant authorities. Fechner, a German physicist-philosopher, argued that the entire universe is animate and conscious. Marek then adds Leibniz's monadology to complete his ad hoc theological syllabus:

> (003-001) "Fechner teaches that everything, everything that is, is penetrated with the divine, that God fills with His being the whole of the matter in the world. And do you know Leibniz? Leibniz teaches that physical matter is composed of psychical atoms, monads, whose nature is divine."

With these three pillars of Western pantheism in place, Marek unveils the Karburator's true mechanism:

> (003-001) "Let us assume, for the sake of argument, that God is contained in all forms of physical matter, that He is, as it were, imprisoned in it. And when you smash this matter up completely, He flies out of it as though from a box. He is suddenly set free. He is released from matter as illuminating gas is from coal. You have only to burn one single atom up completely, and immediately the whole cellar is filled with the Absolute. It's simply appalling how quickly it spreads."

The Karburator thus transforms from an atomic motor into a device for de-incarcerating God — producing at one end mechanical power and at the other "pure and unconfined Absolute, God in a chemically pure form" (004-001). Marek, an atheist who "believed in matter and in progress and in nothing else" (003-001), is horrified by his own discovery:

> (003-001) "From the scientific point of view, Bondy, it is absolutely intolerable. It's a case of Him or science. I don't assert that God does not exist; I only assert that He ought not to exist, or at least ought not to let Himself be seen."

### Development: The Theological Confrontation (Chapter V)

The novel's most explicit institutional confrontation with pantheism occurs through [[bishop-linda|Bishop Linda]], whom Bondy has engaged as an "expert" to inspect the wonder-working Absolute. After spending three hours in the infected cellar — experiencing "illumination, miraculous powers, ecstasy, everything" (005-001) — Linda delivers the Church's verdict:

> (005-001) "From the Catholic standpoint God cannot be brought into the matter, that the Church absolutely rejects and forbids the pantheistic hypothesis as heresy. In short, that this isn't any legal, duly recognized God, supported by the authority of the Church, and that, as a priest, he must declare it false, perverse, and heretical."

The distinction between a "legal, duly recognized God" and the Absolute released by the Karburator is the novel's sharpest satire of institutional religion. The Church does not deny the Absolute's existence — what it denies is the Absolute's credentials. God, like a corporation, requires a charter; without ecclesiastical authorization, even the divine is merely a "heretical hypothesis."

Linda elaborates the Church's objection during a meeting at Bondy's office:

> (005-001) "Your Absolute opposes the laws of the Church. It contradicts the doctrine of the holy sacraments. It does not regard the traditions of the Church. It seriously violates the doctrine of the Trinity. It pays no attention to the apostolic succession. It does not even submit to the rites of exorcism."

Yet the Bishop is also a practical man. Recognizing that suppression is impossible, he proposes institutional capture:

> (005-001) "We cannot permit the rise of some new God or possibly a new religion."

Linda's solution is characteristically bureaucratic: the Church will place the Absolute "provisionally under its patronage," proclaim the Břevnov cellar a miraculous shrine and place of pilgrimage, and insist only that the Absolute be produced "only weak, almost innocuous, very much diluted" — "rather as at Lourdes" (005-001).

### Resolution: Canonization and the Fragmentation of Truth

The logic of institutional capture culminates in Chapter XIX ("The Process of Canonization"), where "the identity of the Absolute with the Third Person of the Trinity was definitely established on the basis of the Scriptures and the writings of the Fathers of the Church" (019-001). The ceremony concludes with the ringing of bells across the Catholic world and the proclamation "Habemus Deum" — We have a God (019-001).

But this resolution is immediately destabilized. The Patriarch of Constantinople declares the Absolute identical with the First Person of the Trinity (019-001). The Free Thought Society in Basle proclaims the Absolute as "the God of the Freethinkers" (019-001). The German Monist Association lays the foundation stone of "the future Cathedral of the Atomic God" (019-001). Pantheism, initially a single philosophical framework, fragments into competing theological claims — each group measuring off "a certain amount of Him and then thinking it is the entire God" (027-001), as Bondy later explains to Captain Trouble on a coral island in the Pacific.

## Interpretations

Pantheism functions in the novel as more than a plot device — it is the structural inversion of Capek's satire. The novel begins with pantheism as explanation (Marek's lecture to Bondy in Chapter III) and ends with pantheism as diagnostic symptom (Dr. Blahous's article in Chapter XII, and the global war of competing Absolutes that follows). The same idea, applied to the same phenomena, migrates from physics lecture to papal brief to psychiatric chart — each institutional frame producing a different "truth" about the same divine substance.

This migration mirrors the fate of the Karburator itself: a machine that starts as a solution to the coal crisis becomes a military weapon, a miracle factory, and finally a historical curiosity. Capek's point is not that pantheism is true or false, but that truth — like matter in Marek's formulation — is the prison from which no single institution can permanently liberate it.

The novel traces pantheism's career through five institutional frames:

| Frame | Authority | Pantheism Is... | Chapter |
|-------|-----------|----------------|---------|
| Scientific | R. Marek, engineer | Experimental confirmation of a metaphysical hypothesis | III — Pantheism |
| Ecclesiastical | Bishop Linda | Heresy, "false, perverse, and heretical" — negotiable if placed under Church patronage | V — Bishop Linda |
| Academic | Dr. Blahous, university lecturer | "Atavistic occurrence," clinical pathology, "animistic pantheism" | XII — Doctor Blahous |
| Bureaucratic | Monsignor Cullatti, Papal Secretary | Canonizable — the Absolute identified as the Third Person of the Trinity | XIX — The Process of Canonization |
| Pragmatic | G. H. Bondy | An inconvenient byproduct — best managed through institutional capture and territorial containment | V, XXVII |

Capek's deeper satirical insight is that pantheism is simultaneously the most rational and the most destabilizing theological position. If God is truly everywhere, then every person, every nation, every institution can legitimately claim to possess a fragment of divine truth — and the natural human response to that fragment is not humility but militancy. Bondy's diagnosis on the coral island is the novel's final word on pantheistic logic: "In order to convince himself that God is wholly his, he has to go and kill all the others" (027-001).
