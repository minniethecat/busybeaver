# Sin

**Sin** in Čapek's *The Absolute at Large* undergoes a radical transformation under the influence of the Absolute. Traditional moral categories dissolve as the Karburator's emanations induce ecstatic religious states that bypass conventional notions of guilt and transgression.

## Kuzenda's Sermon on the Dredge

The dredge operator Kuzenda, an unlikely preacher transformed by the Absolute, addresses the theme of sin directly in his waterfront sermon:

> "You, gamekeeper, drink far too much; you drive the poor from the forest, and curse and swear even when there is no need. Do it no more. And you, Mr. Hudec, are a better-class thief. You know very well what I mean. And you're shockingly bad-tempered. Faith will reform and redeem you."

## The Dissolution of Moral Categories

The Absolute's influence paradoxically both heightens awareness of sin and dissolves its traditional meaning. Kuzenda's congregation experiences moral transformation not through doctrine but through direct contact with the divine presence:

> "The greatest miracle is the influence He has on our feelings. It is so intensely beautiful that it fairly makes one shiver. Sometimes you feel as if you could die of love and happiness, as if you were one with the water below, with all the animals, with the very earth and stones, or as if gigantic arms were holding you embraced."

## Čapek's Satire of Religious Morality

Through the Absolute's effects, Čapek satirizes the institutional Church's monopoly on defining sin. The Absolute induces genuine moral reformation in individuals like the drunken gamekeeper, yet the Church authorities are more concerned with doctrinal compliance than with actual human transformation:

> "You are mistaken. Quite mistaken. Science without dogma is only a heap of doubts. What is worse, your Absolute opposes the laws of the Church. It contradicts the doctrine of the holy sacraments. It does not regard the traditions of the Church. It seriously violates the doctrine of the Trinity."
