# Religious Cults

**Religious cults** in Čapek's *The Absolute at Large* proliferate as the Absolute released from atomic combustion induces religious fervour across society, spawning diverse sects that range from primitive fetish-worship to organized denominational movements.

## Proliferation of Sects

Dr. Blahous, a scholar of comparative religion, catalogues the various cultic expressions that emerge. His analysis reveals the atavistic character of the new religious movements:

> "With the aid of newspaper reports and evidence given verbally, he gave a description of Kuzendism, in which he found traces of fetish-worship and even totemism (the dredge being made a sort of Totem God of Stechovice). In the case of the Binderians, he worked out their relationship to the Dancing Dervishes and ancient orgiastic cults."

## Kuzenda's Worship Community

On a dredge on the Vltava, the worker Kuzenda establishes an improvised religious community centred on the Karburator-powered vessel itself, which he treats as a sacred object:

> "Brethren and sisters, on this dredge, where signs of grace are shown to us, we are gathered together for worship."

Kuzenda preaches an egalitarian, anti-intellectual faith, declaring that God "cannot bear politics and money, intellect, pride, and self-conceit" and is "a thorough democrat".

## Institutional Responses

The established churches and secular organizations compete to absorb or claim the Absolute. The Catholic Church initiated an expedited canonization process, while the Monist Association asserted "that the Absolute is that Unity which the Monists have always proclaimed to be God". A workers' resolution "was passed that the Absolute was the exclusive property of the proletariat".
