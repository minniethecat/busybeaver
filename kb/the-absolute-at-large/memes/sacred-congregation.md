## Overview

The Sacred Congregation is the Vatican administrative body that processes the Absolute's admission into the Catholic Church in Chapter XIX of *The Absolute at Large*. Its appearance marks the novel's most concentrated satire of ecclesiastical bureaucracy: the Congregation does not ask whether the Absolute *is* God, but merely processes the petitions of the faithful and issues the canonical ruling — treating the divine with the procedural gravity of a planning application.

## Role in the Novel

### The Admission of the Absolute

The Sacred Congregation first appears when Father Jost, a journalist-priest, announces the Church's decision to his editorial staff:

> "Now, friend Jost, now shall Saul become Paul. You'll write a nice little leading article in which you will make it known that the Sacred Congregation, taking cognizance of the petitions of the faithful, has admitted the Absolute into the bosom of the Church. Mr. Novotny, here is the Apostolic Letter to that effect; have it set up in large bold type on the front page of the paper." (018-060)

The language is telling: the Sacred Congregation "takes cognizance of the petitions of the faithful" — the same formula it would use for a beatification cause or a liturgical reform. The Absolute is processed through existing ecclesiastical channels as though it were a candidate for sainthood rather than what it claims to be. The Apostolic Letter reduces the ontological shock of encountering God to a front-page typesetting decision.

### The Canonical Deliberation

The Congregation's deliberation extends to the question of baptism:

> It was carried out by virtue of the Papal Brief alone, and the College of Cardinals, being faced with the accomplished fact, merely deliberated whether the sacrament of baptism should be conferred upon the Absolute. It was decided to dispense with this. There was certainly well-known ecclesiastical precedent for the baptism of a God (vide John the Baptist); but even in such a case the candidate for baptism must be present in person. (019-002)

The College of Cardinals' debate over whether to baptize the Absolute is a masterpiece of theological absurdity played straight. The Congregation consults precedent — John the Baptist's baptism of Christ — and concludes that since the Absolute cannot be "present in person," the sacrament must be dispensed with. The reasoning is formally impeccable and ontologically preposterous: the Church treats God as a legal person who has failed to appear for his own hearing.

### The Alternative Sacrament

Faced with the baptismal impasse, the Sacred Congregation innovates:

> The Sacred Congregation therefore recommended that at the next Pontifical Mass the Holy Father should pray for the new member of the Church, and this was duly carried out in very solemn form. It was also made part of the body of Church doctrine that in addition to the sacrament of baptism and baptism by blood the Church also recognized baptism through works of piety, meritoriousness, and virtue. (019-002)

The Congregation solves a metaphysical problem with a bureaucratic workaround: if the Absolute cannot be baptized, create a new category of baptism. The invention of "baptism through works of piety" is simultaneously a genuine theological innovation and a cynical procedural dodge — exactly the kind of solution that an institution staffed by lawyers and administrators would devise when faced with an unprecedented case.

## Thematic Significance

The Sacred Congregation's handling of the Absolute is the novel's most precise satire of institutional religion's encounter with the genuinely sacred. The Congregation does not deny the Absolute; it processes it. Every step — the petition, the cognizance, the Apostolic Letter, the baptism debate, the doctrinal innovation — follows canon law. And yet the cumulative effect is not reverence but domestication: the Absolute is admitted to the Church in the same way a new saint is added to the calendar, through committee work and paperwork. Čapek's point is not that the Church is corrupt, but that institutional procedure is structurally incapable of responding to transcendence except by reducing it to a case.
