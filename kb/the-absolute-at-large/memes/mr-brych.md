## Overview

Mr. Brych is the stoker aboard Jan Binder Kuzenda's dredge M.E.28 on the [[vltava|Vltava River]], and one of the earliest lay participants in the Absolute-inspired religious gatherings that spread from the dredge to the surrounding countryside. Originally "a drunkard" (010-028) before experiencing the grace of the Absolute, Brych becomes the welcoming face of the dredge fellowship — greeting visitors, organizing impromptu services, and gently correcting newcomers who misunderstand the nature of the gatherings. He reappears in the novel's epilogue (Chapter XXX) as the proprietor of a locksmith's business, reunited with old friends in a quiet tavern, representing the ordinary life to which the world returns after the Absolute dissipates.

> [(010-028)] "Let me tell you, Mr. Hudec, that I was a drunkard and Kuzenda was in politics, and the grace of God came upon us, and our brethren and sisters here come to us in the evenings to pray for the same gift of the spirit."

## Role and Significance

### The Greeter of the Dredge

Brych's primary role in Chapter VIII is as greeter and master of ceremonies. From the dredge's deck above [[stechovice|Stechovice]], he identifies approaching visitors, shouts instructions to their boats, and organizes the seating arrangements with a brisk, practical hospitality:

> [(010-010)] "Now then, youngsters," Brych shouted to the boat. "To the left! Left! Give us your hand, lass. There we are. Up she comes!"

As the evening progresses, more visitors arrive — the baker, the postman, the gamekeeper, the young sweethearts from Stechovice, and finally Mr. Hudec, a curious newcomer. Brych handles them all with the same combination of warmth and efficiency:

> [(010-019)] "Go and fetch them over, you below," said Mr. Brych, "that all may hear the word of God. Brothers and sister, please sit down. Brother Kuzenda will bring you some coffee, and then we can start. Welcome, young people. Come right up."

### The Practical Theologian

Brych is not the visionary of the dredge — that role belongs to [[jan-binder-kuzenda|Kuzenda]], who levitates and delivers the sermon — but he is its practical theologian. When Mr. Hudec, the newcomer, refers to the gathering as a "seance," Brych gently corrects him:

> [(010-028)] "Our service," Brych mildly corrected him. "We are all brothers, here, you know."

This single-word correction carries theological weight. "Seance" implies summoning spirits; "service" implies worship. Brych is establishing the community's identity as a legitimate religious congregation, not an occult gathering. His correction also demonstrates the sincerity of his conversion. He is not merely participating in the Absolute's effects — he understands the grammar of the new faith.

### The Drunkard's Testimony

Brych's conversion story is brief but structurally significant. He was "a drunkard" (010-028), Kuzenda "was in politics" (010-028), and "the grace of God came upon us" (010-028). The parallelism establishes the dredge fellowship as a community of the redeemed — ordinary working men transformed by divine intervention. Brych's former alcoholism and Kuzenda's former political activism represent two forms of worldly attachment that the Absolute dissolves.

Brych is also the keeper of the miracles. When the baker claims Kuzenda "laid his hands on me" to cure his asthma, Brych corrects the record:

> [(010-029)] "Wait a bit, baker," Brych corrected him. "Kuzenda didn't lay his hands on you. He hadn't any notion he was going to work a miracle. He simply went like this with his hand, and then you said that you could breathe easily. That's the way it was."

This insistence on accuracy — the miracle wasn't intentional, it just happened — is characteristic of Brych's practical faith. He does not embellish. He testifies precisely.

### The First Blow Struck

Brych's faith is tested in Chapter XI, "The First Blow Struck," when [[jan-binder|Jan Binder]]'s merry-go-round arrives near the dredge and begins competing for converts. What follows is the novel's first religious war in miniature — a brawl between the dredge fellowship and Binder's followers. 

> [] "God Almighty!" shouted Brych, the stoker, forcing his way through the crowd to the front. "Just let him try!"

In the ensuing melee, Brych's front teeth are knocked out by a youth from Binder's camp wielding the gamekeeper's stolen gun. (011-023) The stoker who once preached brotherhood is now a casualty of sectarian violence — Capek's small-scale preview of the Greatest War to come.

### The Clarity of the Gift

One of Brych's most striking statements comes during Kuzenda's levitation. When Kuzenda asks, "You can see into people's souls too, can't you, Brych?" (008-037), Brych responds with a startlingly specific revelation:

> [(008-037)] "I can," said Mr. Brych. "The postman here is thinking this minute how fine it would be if you could help his little daughter. She's got scrofula, hasn't she, postman? Mr. Kuzenda will help her right enough if you bring her here."

This moment — the stoker reading a stranger's most private hopes — confirms that Brych, too, has received the Absolute's gift of clairvoyance. He is not merely Kuzenda's assistant; he shares in the dredge's spiritual power.

### The Epilogue: Locksmith and Lay Philosopher

In Chapter XXX, "The End of Everything," Brych has become "the proprietor of a locksmith's business" (030-001), reunited with old friends in a tavern run by Jan Binder. He reads the *People's Journal*, discusses the appointment of Dr. Blahous to Assistant Professor, and engages in a gentle theological debate over sauerkraut — which he uses as a metaphor for religious tolerance: "It's just the same with sauerkraut as it is with religion. One man can't understand how another can believe anything different." (030-037)

> [(030-037)] "Well, you know," said Mr. Brych meditatively, "it's just the same with sauerkraut as it is with religion. One man can't understand how another can believe anything different."

His final philosophical statement encapsulates the novel's moral:

> [(030-043)] "A man may certainly think that another religion is a bad one, but he oughtn't to think that the man who follows it is a low, vile, and treacherous fellow. And the same applies to politics and everything."

## Relationships

- **[[jan-binder-kuzenda|Jan Binder Kuzenda]]** — The dredge skipper and prophet. Brych is his practical counterpart: Kuzenda levitates and preaches; Brych greets and organizes. Their partnership mirrors the division between visionary and administrator that appears in many religious movements.
- **[[mr-hudec|Mr. Hudec]]** — The skeptical newcomer whom Brych corrects and welcomes. Hudec's conversion — from calling the gathering a "seance" to kneeling in prayer — is the novel's model of how the Absolute transforms the skeptical observer.
- **[[jan-binder|Jan Binder]]** — The merry-go-round proprietor whose competing ministry leads to the brawl in Chapter XI. In the epilogue, Binder is the innkeeper serving liver sausages — the former rivals now friends.
- **[[father-jost|Canon Jost]]** — The Catholic priest who joins the post-war tavern gathering, debating sauerkraut and religion with Brych in the epilogue scene. (030-025)
- **[[mr-rejzek|Mr. Rejzek]]** — The journalist who joins the epilogue gathering. (030-041)
- **[[dr-blahous|Dr. Blahous]]** — The youthful savant (aged 55) who wrote about "The Apostle of Kuzenda's Sect" — a phrase that referred to Brych. (030-047)
- **[[the-greatest-war|The Greatest War]]** — The global conflict (1944-1953) whose horrors Brych survived. His post-war reflections on tolerance are the novel's answer to the war's violence.

## Thematic Significance

Mr. Brych represents the working-class dimension of the Absolute movement. Unlike [[g-h-bondy|G.H. Bondy]], who interacts with the Absolute at the level of industrial capitalism, or [[emperor-bobinet|Bobinet]], who channels it into military conquest, Brych embodies the grassroots religious awakening. He is a stoker — a man who tends fire — and his transformation mirrors the broader pattern: the Absolute does not erase a person's character but redirects it toward religious expression.

Brych's trajectory — from drunkard to lay preacher to brawl casualty to locksmith-philosopher — traces the novel's arc from revelation through conflict to resolution. His practical faith, his insistence on accuracy, and his final wisdom about tolerance make him one of Capek's most likable characters. If Kuzenda is the dredge's saint, Brych is its mensch.
