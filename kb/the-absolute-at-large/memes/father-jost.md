**Father Jost** is a Catholic priest and editorial writer for *The People's Friend*, the largest Catholic newspaper, in Karel Čapek's *The Absolute at Large*. A minor but memorably comic figure, Jost embodies the Church's intellectual machinery at its most mechanical — a writer who deploys stock polemical phrases ("knavish imposture," "satanic machinations," "maniacal ravings") without ever pausing to consider whether the divine phenomenon he denounces might actually be God. He appears in two key scenes: his humiliating confrontation with [[bishop-linda|Bishop Linda]] in Chapter XVIII, and his reappearance years later as Canon Jost at [[jan-binder-kuzenda|Jan Binder]]'s supper table in Chapter XXX.

## Overview

Father Jost is Čapek's satirical portrait of institutional religious journalism at its most formulaic. Writing in the night editor's room alongside Kostal, he assembles his editorials from a mental catalogue of prefabricated phrases drawn from old files of *The People's Friend*. When the Church reverses its position on the Absolute — from condemnation to co-optation — Jost is humiliated, then redirected, producing precisely the same rhetorical product in service of the opposite conclusion. Years later, as Canon Jost, he reappears as a mellower figure, debating sauerkraut and delivering one of the novel's most reflective lines about religious belief.

## Role & Significance

### The Formulaic Journalist

Jost's writing process, revealed in the opening of Chapter XVIII, is the novel's most precise satire of mechanical intellectual production:

> "There's just a word I can't get. Have we already had 'satanic machinations'?"
> "The day before yesterday."
> "Aha. And has 'treacherous onslaught' been used too?"
> "Yes, we've had that."
> "'Knavish imposture'?"
> "We ran that today."
> "'Impious fabrication'?"
> "At least six times," said Kostal.
> "That's a pity," sighed Father Jost. "I think we've been a bit too lavish with our ideas." [(018-001)--(018-004)]

He asks the printer Novotny for suggestions and is offered "criminal narrow-mindedness" and "perverse malignity" — phrases Novotny has also retrieved from old newspaper files. Jost's writing is not thought but retrieval: the automated recombination of orthodox outrage. The content of the outrage is irrelevant; only the form matters.

### The Humiliation

Jost's confrontation with Bishop Linda is the novel's most concentrated satire of ecclesiastical authority. The Bishop storms in:

> "Have you got the leader?" repeated Bishop Linda, coming hurriedly into the room. "Who was it that wrote this morning's leader? Heaven forgive me, what a pretty mess you've made with it. What idiot wrote it?"
> "I ... I did," stammered Father Jost, retreating; "Bishop ... Your Lordship ... I thought ..."
> "You've no right to think," roared Bishop Linda, his eyeglasses flashing at him eerily. "Here, take the thing"; and crumpling up that morning's issue of *The People's Friend* in his hand, he flung it at Jost's feet. [(018-010)--(018-012)]

Linda forces Jost to read his own article aloud, then dictates a replacement:

> "Stop," said the Bishop with a sudden amiability. "Take down the following leading article: 'It has been scientifically demonstrated ... that I, Father Jost, am a jackass, a dolt and an idiot.' ... Have you got that?"
> "Yes," whispered Jost, utterly bewildered. "Please go on, my ... my lord." [(018-015)--(018-016)]

The humiliation is total. But what follows is more revealing: Linda explains that the Church's position has reversed. Every group — Monists, Masons, Lutherans, the Seventh International, Freethinkers, Theosophists — has claimed the Absolute as its own. The Church cannot afford to be the one institution denouncing what everyone else embraces:

> "Jost," cried the Bishop, rising to his feet, "our churches are empty, our flock is running after the Absolute. Jost, you blockhead, if we wish to bring our flock back to us, we must secure the Absolute. ... the Absolute must work for us; He must be ours, i.e. He must be ours alone." [(018-025)]

Jost's response — "*Capisco*," whispered Father Jost [(018-026)] — is the only possible response for a functionary: he understands that the content of his writing is irrelevant; only the institutional line matters.

### The Reversal

The chapter's most savage joke is that Jost's new article begins with exactly the same phrase as the old one:

> "We might say something after this style, my lord: The criminal narrow-mindedness and the perverse malignity of certain bodies ..."
> "Splendid!" [(018-030)]

The Bishop approves, then extends the draft. The rhetorical machinery is identical; only the target has changed. Jost is not allowed to think — he is allowed only to produce text in whatever direction the Church requires.

### Canon Jost: The Supper-Table Philosopher

In Chapter XXX, years after the Absolute crisis has passed, Jost reappears as Canon Jost, a mellower figure arriving at Jan Binder's tavern for liver sausages and sauerkraut. The scene is domestic, warm, and gently comic:

> "Praised be the Lord!" came a voice from the doorway, and Canon Jost stepped into the room, his cheeks ruddy with the cold, and hung up his hat and fur coat. [(030-005)]

The conversation turns to sauerkraut — specifically, the difference between the strained, greased sauerkraut Jost favors and the runny Moravian variety that Mr. Brych describes. When Brych draws an analogy — "it's just the same with sauerkraut as it is with religion. One man can't understand how another can believe anything different" — Jost protests:

> "Oh, enough of that!" protested Father Jost. "Why, I'd sooner believe in Muhammad than eat sauerkraut made any other way. After all, reason teaches one that sauerkraut ought only to be greased." [(030-010)]

But his position softens. He delivers one of the novel's most reflective lines:

> "And that's what so many people have hated and killed each other for," Father Jost declared. "You know, the greater the things are in which a man believes, the more fiercely he despises those who do not believe in them. And yet the greatest of all beliefs would be belief in one's fellow-men." [(030-020)]

And then, in a small but significant gesture of openness:

> "Mr. Binder," said Father Jost thoughtfully, "I wonder if you would make me some of that Moravian sauerkraut tomorrow. I'd like to try it." [(030-025)]

The man who could only write what the Church told him to write now opens himself to another way of making sauerkraut. It is a small conversion, but a genuine one.

## Appearances

### Chapter XVIII — In the Night Editor's Room

Jost's primary appearance. He sits with Kostal, the night editor, assembling the next day's leader from a repertoire of well-worn polemical phrases. His exchange with Novotny the printer about available rhetorical formulas reveals the factory-like production of religious journalism. When Bishop Linda arrives and demolishes him, Jost's collapse from confident polemicist to stammering subordinate is swift and total. The Bishop then redirects him: Jost must announce the Absolute's admission to the Church and welcome Bondy's baptism [(018-001)]--[(018-035)].

### Chapter XXX — The End of Everything

Now Canon Jost, he arrives at Jan Binder's tavern for a supper of liver sausages with Brych and Rejzek. The conversation moves from sauerkraut preparation to religious belief, and Jost's remark about "belief in one's fellow-men" serves as a quiet coda to the novel's satirical treatment of organized religion [(030-001)]--[(030-025)].

## Relationships

### Bishop Linda

Jost's ecclesiastical superior and tormentor. Linda transforms Jost's editorial line — from attacking the Absolute as "knavish imposture" to celebrating its admission into the Church — in the space of a single scene. The relationship is purely hierarchical: Linda commands, Jost complies.

### Kostal

The night editor of *The People's Friend*, silent partner in Jost's editorial production. When Linda rebukes Kostal for allowing Jost's article into print, Kostal's silent acceptance mirrors Jost's own subordination.

### Novotny

The printer who supplies Jost with rhetorical phrases from old newspaper files and is later tasked with typesetting the Apostolic Letter on the front page. Novotny, like Jost and Kostal, is rebuked by Bishop Linda for "thinking."

### Jan Binder and Brych

Jost's supper companions in the novel's closing chapter. Their conversation about sauerkraut and religion transforms Jost from the humiliated functionary of Chapter XVIII into a reflective, open-minded Canon willing to try new things.
