## Overview

Moravia is the eastern counterpart to Bohemia in the Czech lands. In *The Absolute at Large*, Moravia appears only in the novel's apocalyptic final chapters, where it is described as a bastion of Catholic resistance during the tumultuous period after the Greatest War, when foreign powers sweep across Central Europe.

## Location

Moravia occupies the eastern third of the Czech Republic, bordered by Bohemia to the west, Austrian Lower Austria to the south, Slovakia to the east, and Polish Silesia to the north. Its historical capital is Brno, which in the novel serves as the site of a Technical Institute involved in early Karburator testing.

## Significance in the Novel

### Catholic Resistance

In the novel's post-war scenario, Moravia's Catholic population becomes a source of stubborn resistance:

> The stubborn Catholics of Bohemia and Moravia moved out of the country, or became the victims of Chinese dragonades and confiscations, by which the number of national martyrs was increased to a remarkable extent.

This brief passage, set during the Chinese occupation of the former Austria-Hungary, positions Moravia alongside Bohemia as a region whose religious identity proves more durable than political borders — a darkly ironic conclusion given the novel's earlier satire of religious enthusiasm unleashed by the Karburator.

### Regional Identity

Moravia's appearance in the text, though minimal, reinforces Čapek's grounding of the novel's cosmic themes in the specific geography of the Czech lands. The pairing of "Bohemia and Moravia" throughout the novel's final chapters echoes the historical unity of the Czech crown lands while also suggesting the distinct regional character that persists even amid global catastrophe.

## Thematic Resonance

Moravia's role in the novel, though limited to a single passage, serves an important structural function. By naming both Bohemia and Moravia in the apocalyptic epilogue, Čapek extends the geographic scope of his cautionary tale to encompass the entire Czech homeland. The "stubborn Catholics" who become martyrs under foreign rule represent a tragic irony: the religious fervor that earlier in the novel was a source of chaos and violence here becomes a marker of cultural resilience and identity worth dying for.
