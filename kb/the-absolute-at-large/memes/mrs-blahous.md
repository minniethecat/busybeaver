## Overview

Mrs. Blahous is a minor character in Karel Čapek's novel *The Absolute at Large*, an elderly country woman living in the hamlet of Seven Cottages. Together with her husband, Grandfather Blahous, and their neighbour Mrs. Prouzova, she represents the rural civilian perspective on the cataclysmic events of the Greatest War — a conflict that unfolds in distant places while daily life in the countryside continues largely unchanged (028-001).

## Role & Significance

Mrs. Blahous serves as part of a comic chorus in the novel's late chapters. While the world is consumed by religious wars sparked by the Absolute, she and her neighbours in Seven Cottages carry on with peeling potatoes, gossiping, and remarking on the rising cost of candles. Her simple, pragmatic observations provide a stark contrast to the ideological frenzy sweeping the globe, embodying Čapek's ironic commentary on the gulf between world-historical events and the mundane concerns of ordinary people (028-001).

## Appearances

Mrs. Blahous appears exclusively in Chapter XXVIII ("At Seven Cottages"), where she is introduced peeling potatoes while the world beyond is crumbling:

> And while the world shook with the clash of armies, while the boundaries of States writhed to and fro like earthworms, and the whole earth was crumbling into a field of ruins, old Mrs. Blahous was peeling her potatoes in Seven Cottages, Grandfather Blahous was sitting on the doorstep smoking beech-leaves, and their neighbour, Mrs. Prouzova, was leaning on the fence, repeating meditatively, "Yes, yes." (028-001)

Throughout the chapter, she participates in the neighbours' conversation, which drifts from the war to the price of goods to the dampness of the weather. Her most characteristic interventions are domestic observations: "We could do with some rain. The potatoes are far too small. Like nuts" (028-004).

She also reflects on changing times and rising prices, lamenting that "fifteen hundred for a candle" has become the norm (028-003). Her worldview is grounded entirely in the local and the practical, untouched by the theological and political convulsions reshaping civilization.

## Relationships

- **[[grandfather-blahous|Grandfather Blahous]]** — Her husband, with whom she shares a quiet domestic life in Seven Cottages. She echoes his sentiments and supports his grumbling about the state of the world.
- **[[mrs-prouzova|Mrs. Prouzova]]** — Her neighbour, with whom she shares gossip and passes the time. Their relationship typifies the close-knit rural community.
- **[[dr-blahous|Doctor Blahous]]** — By surname, presumably related (possibly her son is "the Professor" referred to by Grandfather Blahous), though the exact familial connection is not explicitly stated in the text (030-001).
