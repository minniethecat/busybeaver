## Overview

Slaný is a historic Central Bohemian town that appears in *The Absolute at Large* as one of the earliest Karburator deployment sites. The M.E.C. chairman's report to the Board lists it among the successful test installations: "The five-kilo pattern, No. 6, is running a mill at Slaný".

## Location

Slaný lies approximately 25 kilometers northwest of Prague in Central Bohemia, on the main road toward Louny and the Ore Mountains. The town's name derives from a salt spring (*slaný* meaning "salty" in Czech), referencing its medieval origins. In the novel's industrial geography, it represents the smaller provincial towns that became part of the M.E.C.'s early deployment network.

## Significance in the Novel

### Industrial Testing

Slaný's Karburator — the five-kilo pattern — is the smallest model listed in the chairman's report, deployed to power a single mill rather than an entire city's lighting system (like Prague's fifty-kilo model) or a dredge (like the Vltava installations). This modest application represents the Karburator's promise of decentralized, small-scale clean energy: a single atomic boiler powering a provincial flour mill.

### Implied Fate

Though the novel never returns to Slaný explicitly, the trajectory of every Karburator-equipped location implies that the mill, its workers, and the surrounding community would have experienced the Absolute's spiritual effects. The five-kilo pattern, like all Karburators, released not just energy but the divine essence contained in matter.

## Thematic Resonance

Slaný represents the capillary reach of the Karburator revolution into ordinary provincial life. While Prague, Hradec Králové, and the great industrial centers receive the largest installations, it is the humble mill at Slaný — and hundreds of similar small deployments — that distribute the Absolute throughout the fabric of everyday existence. The five-kilo Karburator at Slaný is, in microcosm, the same force that eventually reshapes the world: small enough to fit in a mill, powerful enough to release God.
