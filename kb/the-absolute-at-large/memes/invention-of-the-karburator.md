## Summary

The Invention of the Karburator is the initiating event of Karel Capek's *[[the-absolute|The Absolute]] at Large*, spanning Chapters I through III. After twenty years of solitary labor, engineer [[rudy-marek|R. Marek]] perfects a machine that achieves complete atomic combustion — burning matter so thoroughly that nothing remains but pure energy and an unanticipated byproduct: the divine [[the-absolute|Absolute]], [[the-absolute|God]] released from atomic confinement. When his old schoolfellow [[g-h-bondy|G. H. Bondy]], the Chairman of [[m-e-c|M.E.C.]], discovers [[rudy-marek|Marek]]'s cryptic newspaper advertisement and visits the [[brevnov|Břevnov]] factory, the demonstration in the cellar transforms the industrialist into a believer — and the [[the-karburator|Karburator]] from a scientific marvel into a commercial product destined to flood the world.

## Context

### The Coal Crisis of 1943

The novel opens on New Year's Day, 1943, against the backdrop of a crippling energy crisis. [[g-h-bondy|Bondy]]'s newspaper reveals mines being exhausted, the Ostrava basin suspending work, and industrial production threatened by fuel shortages:

> [(001-003)] Bondy: "The Coal Crisis! ... Mines getting worked out; the Ostrava basin suspending work for years. Heavens above, it's a sheer disaster! We'll have to import Upper Silesian coal. Just work out what that will add to the cost of our manufactures, and then talk about competition."

This economic context is crucial: Marek's invention arrives precisely when industrial civilization most desperately needs new energy sources, making [[g-h-bondy|Bondy]]'s commercial embrace of the [[the-karburator|Karburator]] not merely greedy but historically inevitable.

### The Advertisement

The catalytic moment occurs when an irritating incomplete word — the syllable "tion" — compels [[g-h-bondy|Bondy]] to unfold his newspaper one more time, revealing Marek's modest classified ad:

> [(001-009)] Invention. Highly remunerative, suitable for any factory, for immediate sale, personal reasons. Apply R. Marek, Engineer, Břevnov, 1651.

[[g-h-bondy|Bondy]]'s memory stirs: "[[rudy-marek|R. Marek]], Engineer. Half a minute: mightn't that be that red-haired Marek — let's see, what was his name? Rudolph, [[rudy-marek|Rudy Marek]], my old chum Rudy of the Technical School?" [(001-012)]. The twenty-year gap since their school days gives the reunion its charge of nostalgia and curiosity.

### Marek's Twenty-Year Labor

Marek reveals he has been working on atomic combustion for two decades, building four prototypes before achieving success:

> [(002-005)] Marek: "For twenty years I've been working on it, and now — now, I'll sell it to the first man who comes along! My magnificent dream! The greatest invention of all the ages! Seriously, Bondy, I tell you, it's something really amazing."

The fourth model succeeded: "The fourth model was the first that really worked. It was only about so big, but it ran quite nicely" [(003-019)]. The full-scale [[the-karburator|Karburator]] erected in the cellar has been running continuously for six weeks, consuming only half a kilogramme of coal [(002-016)].

## Key Participants

### R. Marek — The Inventor

Marek is depicted as a man of scientific genius cursed with a sensitive conscience. His twenty-year quest has succeeded beyond imagination — and the result terrifies him. He presents the [[the-karburator|Karburator]] to [[g-h-bondy|Bondy]] with the desperation of a man trying to escape his own creation:

> [(002-020)] Marek: "I shovelled half a kilogramme of coal into the machine, then I shut it up and had it officially sealed in the presence of witnesses, so that no one could put any more coal in. Go and have a look at it for yourself — go on — go now!"

His warning — "don't stay down there long" — and the gas-mask he wears to rescue [[g-h-bondy|Bondy]] reveal that he already knows what his invention produces. He has witnessed his porter's conversion, experienced levitation and prophecy himself, and recognized the terrifying truth:

> [(003-004)] Marek: "I had moments of astounding illumination. I saw, for instance, quite clearly, things that would happen in the future. I predicted even your visit here. And once I tore my nail off on a lathe. I looked at the damaged finger, and all at once a new nail grew on it."

### G. H. Bondy — The Industrialist

[[g-h-bondy|Bondy]] arrives expecting a penniless inventor in a garret; he finds a functioning factory and a former classmate who is "neither so young as he used to be nor so unkempt as that inventor" [(001-029)]. His arc across the three chapters moves from skeptical amusement to dazed belief to commercial calculation.

His personality is established through small, sharp details: the romantic fantasy of rescuing a fallen friend, the irritation at finding Marek prosperous, the instinct to classify the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] as "poisonous gases" [(003-018)], and ultimately the businessman's reflex:

> [(003-029)] Bondy: "I'll give you three percent of the gross profits. The first year it will only be a few millions, perhaps."

## The Demonstration — Chapter II

The central scene of the invention occurs when [[g-h-bondy|Bondy]] descends alone into Marek's cellar. The physical description emphasizes the [[the-karburator|Karburator]]'s imposing solidity and the cellar's monastic cleanliness:

> [(002-047)] And there in the middle of the arched concrete cellar, clean as a monastery cell, lay a gigantic copper cylinder resting on cement supports. It was closed on all sides except at the top, where there was a grating bedecked with seals.

[[the-karburator|The Karburator]] operates in silence — "a smooth and regular motion the cylinder thrust forth a piston which slowly rotated a heavy flywheel. That was all" [(002-047)]. Only the ventilator rattles. Then the effect begins:

> [(002-047)] G. H. Bondy fell on his knees, lost in a bewildering, shining ecstasy. He felt as if he must shout and sing, he seemed to hear about him the rustle of unceasing and innumerable wings.

Marek, wearing a diver's helmet, drags him to safety: "Only just in time" [(002-048)]. The scene establishes the [[the-karburator|Karburator]]'s dual nature — a triumph of engineering that doubles as a religious experience — and [[g-h-bondy|Bondy]]'s permanent transformation from skeptic to something more complicated.

## The Explanation — Chapter III

Chapter III contains the novel's theological-scientific core. Marek, pacing [[g-h-bondy|Bondy]]'s room, traces the argument from Spinoza through Fechner to Leibniz:

> [(003-001)] Marek: "Fechner teaches that everything, everything that is, is penetrated with the divine, that God fills with His being the whole of the matter in the world. And do you know Leibniz? Leibniz teaches that physical matter is composed of psychical atoms, monads, whose nature is divine."

The explanation culminates in Marek's operational definition of his invention:

> [(003-003)] Marek: "As a result of its complete disintegration of matter, my Perfect Karburator manufactures a byproduct: pure and unconfined Absolute, God in a chemically pure form. At one end, so to speak, it emits mechanical power, and at the other, the divine principle. Just as when you split water up into hydrogen and oxygen, only on an immensely larger scale."

The equation is elegant and terrible: complete combustion of matter = unlimited energy + pure [[the-absolute|God]]. Marek's own attempts to contain the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] have all failed:

> [(003-021)] Marek: "I've tried every possible isolating material that might prevent the Absolute from getting out of the cellar. Ashes, sand, metal walls, nothing can keep it back. I've even tried covering the cellar with the works of Professor Krejči, Spencer, Haeckel, and all the Positivists you can think of: would you believe it, the Absolute goes calmly through even that stuff!"

The comedy of the Positivist texts failing as insulation — [[the-absolute|God]] penetrating Spencer and Haeckel as easily as sand and metal — is one of Capek's finest satirical touches.

## The Decision — Commercialization

The climax of the invention sequence is [[g-h-bondy|Bondy]]'s decision to mass-produce the [[the-karburator|Karburator]] despite Marek's warnings:

> [(003-024)] Marek: "Bondy, my Karburator is a terrific thing. It will overturn the whole world, mechanically and socially. It will cheapen production to an unbelievable extent. It will do away with poverty and hunger. It will some day save our planet from freezing up. But, on the other hand, it hurls God as a byproduct into the world."

[[g-h-bondy|Bondy]], characteristically, responds with a business proposal:

> [(003-026)] Bondy: "Perhaps you could come to some agreement with Him. A proper formal contract, in something like this style: 'We guarantee to produce You discreetly and continuously to an extent to be fixed by mutual agreement; in return for which You pledge yourself to refrain from any divine manifestations within such and such a radius from the place of origin.'"

Marek's final warning — "It means letting [[the-absolute|God]] loose upon this world!" [(003-030)] — goes unheeded. [[g-h-bondy|Bondy]]'s last line of the chapter: "Then He will be personally indebted to me to that extent. And I hope that He won't show me any ill-feeling" [(003-030)], seals the novel's trajectory from scientific breakthrough to global catastrophe.

## Aftermath

The immediate aftermath is documented in Chapter V — "[[bishop-linda|Bishop Linda]]." Within two weeks, [[brevnov|Mixa Street]] is closed by police after religious processions converge on the [[brevnov|Břevnov]] cellar [(005-018)]. Seven people are killed when police attempt to disperse worshippers. The [[m-e-c|M.E.C.]] begins constructing a massive factory at Vysočany with seven thousand bricklayers [(005-008)]. The Church, through [[bishop-linda|Bishop Linda]], attempts to negotiate jurisdiction over the miraculous site at 1651 [[brevnov|Břevnov]] [(005-050)].

The Minister for War "sees the light" in his Dejvice villa and assembles the [[prague-in-the-novel|Prague]] garrison to preach eternal peace — forcing his resignation [(005-036)]. The Petřín wireless station becomes an "epidemic of religion" as operators broadcast spontaneous gospels to the world [(005-034)].

Within months, as [[g-h-bondy|Bondy]] later catalogues, "several thousand [[the-karburator|Karburator]]s" are operating globally [(010-003)], each continuously emitting the [[the-absolute|Absolute]]. The Ford works in America hurl "thirty thousand finished [[the-karburator|Karburator]]s out upon the world every day" [(013-004)].

## Narrative Significance

The Invention of the Karburator serves multiple narrative functions:

1. **The Inciting Event**: It establishes the novel's central premise — unlimited energy carries an unlimited spiritual cost — and sets every subsequent event in motion.

2. **Character Establishment**: The three-chapter sequence defines [[g-h-bondy|Bondy]] and Marek as complementary opposites: the capitalist who sees only profit, the scientist who sees only danger. Their dynamic — Marek warning, [[g-h-bondy|Bondy]] ignoring — recurs throughout the novel.

3. **Theological Exposition**: Marek's explanation of pantheism, delivered in the language of engineering ("Just as when you split water up into hydrogen and oxygen, only on an immensely larger scale" [(003-003)]), establishes the novel's satirical method: treating metaphysical questions as technical problems.

4. **Tonal Signature**: The juxtaposition of [[g-h-bondy|Bondy]]'s commercial instincts ("three percent of the gross profits") with Marek's apocalyptic warnings ("letting [[the-absolute|God]] loose upon this world") establishes the novel's distinctive tone — a satire that is simultaneously comic and terrifying.

5. **Foreshadowing**: Marek's list of failed containment measures (sand, metal, Positivist philosophy) prefigures the novel's ultimate argument: that the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] — like any absolute conviction — cannot be contained once released into human society.
