# Dr. Wurm

Dr. Wurm is the Imperial German Chancellor during the period of the Absolute crisis, participating in high-level international diplomatic negotiations alongside representatives of other major powers.

## The International Conference

Dr. Wurm appears in Chapter XX as part of a diplomatic assembly that includes General Buchtin of Russia, Prince Trivelino of Italy, and the Japanese Ambassador. The gathering represents the great powers' attempt to manage the escalating global crisis caused by the Absolute's spread:

> The Russian General, Buchtin; the Imperial German Chancellor, Dr. Wurm; the Italian Minister, Prince Trivelino; and the Japanese Ambassador assembled to discuss the crisis. (020-015)

## Diplomatic Demeanour

Wurm is depicted with the controlled, bureaucratic manner characteristic of a career diplomat. His physical description emphasizes his professional detachment:

> Dr. Wurm turned over his papers, General Buchtin walked up and down the room. (020-030)

The image of Wurm calmly reviewing documents while the Russian General paces restlessly establishes a contrast between German methodical precision and Russian emotional intensity — a national stereotype that Čapek deploys satirically.

Wurm also exhibits diplomatic caution in his speech:

> "Those are mere local differences," replied Dr. Wurm. "Don't attach any importance to them, gentlemen. Germany is prepared to accept a reasonable compromise." 

This statement reveals Wurm's strategy: to downplay the severity of the crisis in order to secure a negotiated outcome favorable to German interests. His use of "local differences" to describe what are clearly global-scale religious upheavals is a deliberate rhetorical minimization.

## The Smile

Čapek endows Wurm with a distinctive facial expression that recurs throughout the diplomatic scenes:

> Dr. Wurm smiled as though his mouth were full of water. (020-035)

This peculiar simile — the "mouth full of water" smile — suggests a man who holds his words back, who says less than he knows. It is the smile of diplomatic calculation, of one who understands the stakes but refuses to show his hand. The image also carries a faintly absurdist quality, in keeping with the novel's satirical tone.

## Significance

Dr. Wurm represents Imperial Germany's response to the Absolute crisis: cautious, calculating, and geopolitical. His presence in the diplomatic conference places the Absolute's religious effects within the framework of international power politics. The fact that a German Chancellor is negotiating over what began as a Czech industrial accident underscores the scale of Čapek's satirical vision — a factory safety issue has become a matter of world diplomacy.

The name "Wurm" (German for "worm") may carry satirical connotations, suggesting something that gnaws from within — much as the Absolute gnaws at the foundations of established political and religious institutions.
