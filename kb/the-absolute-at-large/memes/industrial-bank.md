## Overview

The Industrial Bank is a Prague financial institution that features in *The Absolute at Large* as an early and particularly telling victim of the Karburator's spiritual effects. When an atomic boiler is installed in the bank building, the employees — rather than continuing their normal financial operations — declare the bank's property a sacred trust for the poor, effectively shutting down the institution through a "religious strike."

## Role in the Novel

### The Religious Strike

G. H. Bondy, walking through the streets of Prague and reflecting on the unfolding chaos, notes the incident with grim recognition:

> A religious strike broke out at the Industrial Bank today. We installed a Karburator there, and within two days the officials declared the bank's property to be a sacred trust for the poor. That couldn't have happened when Preis was manager.

The detail is precisely satirical: a bank — the most secular and profit-driven of institutions — becomes, under the Absolute's influence, an organ of radical charity. The officials' declaration that the bank's property is a "sacred trust for the poor" inverts the fundamental logic of banking, where property is held as collateral for profit, not as a sacred obligation to the destitute.

### Bondy's Reflection

The incident crystallizes Bondy's growing unease. He immediately follows it with the broader recognition: "Marek was right, Bishop Linda even more so. It was simply impossible to bring God to earth without a confounded lot coming of it". The Industrial Bank's transformation is the concrete evidence that supports this abstract conclusion — proof that the Absolute disrupts not only individual psyches but entire institutions, including those at the heart of the capitalist order.

### Nostalgia for the Old Order

Bondy's remark that "that couldn't have happened when Preis was manager" reveals his nostalgia for a pre-Absolute world where institutions could be trusted to behave according to rational self-interest. Preis (the previous manager) represents the old guard — competent, secular, profit-minded — whose departure marks the end of an era. The new manager, transformed by the Absolute, cannot be relied upon to protect shareholder value.

## Thematic Significance

The Industrial Bank episode is one of the novel's sharpest satirical jabs at capitalism. The premise — that a machine designed for industrial efficiency would, through its divine byproduct, turn a bank into a charity — suggests that the gap between Christian ethics and capitalist practice is so wide that divine intervention is required to bridge it. The bank officials, once exposed to the Absolute, do not become more efficient; they become more moral, which from the perspective of the economic system is a form of dysfunction — a "strike." The episode also demonstrates the Karburator's class-crossing reach: it began with Marek the engineer, affected the Minister for War in his villa, and now converts bank clerks in their counting houses.
