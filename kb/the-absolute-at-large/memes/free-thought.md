## Overview

The Free Thought Society is a secularist organization active in Prague during the Absolute crisis. The Society holds public services in Havliček Square, competing for congregants with the Catholic miracle-worker Father Novaček in nearby Tyl Square. The Free Thought Society embodies the rationalist, anti-clerical tradition in Czech public life — but its appearance in the novel raises the question of whether even secularism can remain immune to the Absolute's influence.

## Role in the Novel

### Competing Congregations

The Prague office of the *People's Journal* buzzes with reports of competing religious events:

> "The Free Thought Society is holding a service in Havliček Square today. Father Novaček is performing miracles in Tyl Square. There's sure to be a row between them, you'll see."

The juxtaposition is sharply satirical: a "Free Thought Society" — an organization whose identity is built on the rejection of religious authority — now holds what are effectively religious "services." The language of rationalism has been co-opted by the very forces it was designed to oppose. The Absolute makes no distinction between a cathedral and a free-thought lecture hall; both are equally susceptible to its emanations.

### The Unavoidable Transformation

The Free Thought Society's presence in the novel suggests that the Absolute's reach extends even to those most committed to resisting it. If even the organized secularists are holding "services," the boundary between religion and irreligion has dissolved. The Absolute does not require belief; it requires only proximity to a Karburator.

## Thematic Significance

The Free Thought Society represents one of the novel's most pointed ironies: that a movement defined by its opposition to religion finds itself, under the Absolute's influence, behaving indistinguishably from a church. Čapek's satire cuts both ways — the religious revival is absurd, but so is the secularist pretense that rationality can simply opt out of the divine. The Free Thought Society holds "services" because, once the Absolute is released, there is no secular space left to occupy.
