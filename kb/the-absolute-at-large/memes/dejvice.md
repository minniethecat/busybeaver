## Overview

Dejvice is a residential district of Prague, historically associated with the city's political and military elite. In *The Absolute at Large*, the Minister for War's villa in Dejvice becomes an early flashpoint of the Absolute's spiritual epidemic, demonstrating that the Karburator's effects respect no boundary between the industrial and the political, the working-class Břevnov cellar and the patrician villa garden.

## Location

Dejvice (Prague 6) lies northwest of Prague's historic center, on the left bank of the Vltava, near the Prague Castle complex (Hradčany). In the interwar period of the novel's setting, it was — and remains — one of Prague's most desirable residential quarters, housing government ministers, diplomats, and senior military officers.

## Significance in the Novel

### The Minister's Awakening

G. H. Bondy reveals to Marek the alarming news in hushed tones:

> "The Minister for War. He 'saw the light' all of a sudden in his villa at Dejvice. The following morning he assembled the garrison of Prague, talked to them about eternal peace, and exhorted the troops to become martyrs. Of course he had to resign at once."

The incident is devastating precisely because it occurs in Dejvice — not in the industrial suburbs where Karburators are manufactured and tested, but in the heart of the political establishment. The Minister's villa, presumably equipped with some early Karburator heating or lighting system, becomes a conduit for the Absolute's influence.

### The Spreading Threat

Marek's horrified response — "In Dejvice already! It's terrible, Bondy, the way it's spreading" — captures the geographic dimension of the crisis. If the Absolute has reached the Minister's villa in Dejvice, it has breached the boundary between the industrial working class (Břevnov) and the governing elite. No district, no class, no institution is safe.

The official cover-up — "The papers stated that his health had suddenly broken down" — establishes the pattern of institutional denial that will characterize the government's response to the Absolute crisis throughout the novel.

## Thematic Resonance

Dejvice serves a precise satirical function: it is the place where the Absolute's threat to the established order becomes undeniable. The Minister for War, whose entire professional identity is built on the rational management of violence, is transformed overnight into a pacifist preaching martyrdom to soldiers. That this happens not in a church or at a revival meeting but in a Dejvice villa — the seat of secular, military, bureaucratic power — is Čapek's pointed commentary on the fragility of institutional authority when confronted with genuine spiritual experience. The "health" cover story is the secular state's only available response: redefine the divine as disease.
