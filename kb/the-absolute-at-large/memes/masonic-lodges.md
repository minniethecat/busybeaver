## Overview

The Masonic Lodges in Capek's *The Absolute at Large* serve as a satirical lens on institutional religion's entanglement with the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] phenomenon unleashed by the [[the-karburator|Karburator]]. The Free French Masonic Lodge "[[hammer-and-star|The Hammer and Star]]," led by Worshipful Master [[g-h-bondy|G. H. Bondy]], becomes a microcosm of the broader societal confusion: the brethren debate whether the Absolute's manifestations are demonic or divine, while the Grand Lodge pragmatically orders its Masters to organize religious circles across every conceivable doctrine — from monism to Fletcherism — as a kind of spiritual market research (017-031).

## Structure

### The Hammer and Star Lodge

The principal Masonic body depicted in the novel is the Free French Masonic Lodge "The Hammer and Star," which meets in a black-draped chamber in Prague (017-008):

> The Brothers of the Free French Masonic Lodge, "The Hammer and Star," took their seats, never taking their eyes off the Worshipful Master, G. H. Bondy, who had called them together at such an unusual time. The lodge was as quiet as a church between the black-draped walls with the maxims of the craft woven in the hangings. (017-008)

The lodge's officers include the Worshipful Master (Bondy), the Judex Formidabilis (a brother named Judex who also belongs to several religious circles (017-023)), the Senior Warden, and the Junior Warden. The meeting follows formal Masonic procedure — the gavel, the call to labour, the structured debate — even as the agenda descends into theological chaos.

### The Grand Lodge

Above the local lodges stands the Grand Lodge, which responds to the Absolute crisis with a characteristically bureaucratic solution: a decree ordering all Masters to join religious circles and organize them "in the Masonic sense" (017-031). The Grand Lodge recommends examining doctrines including monism, abstinence, Fletcherism, and vegetarianism, with each circle instructed in a different faith to determine "which is the best for the welfare of mankind and for the glory of the Most High" (017-031).

## Role in the Novel

### The Accusation

The Lodges first appear in the novel's landscape through accusation. Dr. Blahous, surveying the newspapers, reports to Bondy:

> "Here, for instance, you have the Roman Catholic publications expressing the suspicion that 'the confusions of these times of religious excitement are being deliberately organized with Satanic subtlety by the Freemasons.'" (016-043)

The Clerical party systematically blames the Masonic Lodges for deliberately unleashing demoniacal powers through the [[the-karburator|Karburator]] phenomenon. This accusation — that a secret society has engineered a global spiritual crisis — places the Lodges at the centre of the novel's conspiracy-theory subplot. The Clericalist papers assert that the Masonic Lodges "have brought about deliberately this unfettering of demoniacal powers" (017-011).

### The Lodge Meeting (Chapter XVII)

The novel's most sustained depiction of Masonic life occurs in Chapter XVII, when Bondy convenes an emergency meeting. He presents the Clerical accusations to the brethren and asks what they should do "in the present time of calamity for the benefit of mankind and for the honour of the Most High" (017-011).

The debate that follows fractures along theological lines. The Junior Warden, in a speech thick with verbal tics ("so to speak"), declares the miracles, illuminations, and fits of love to be "in the highest degree regrettable" and moves to dissociate the Lodge from all such occurrences (017-013). Judex Formidabilis objects that the occurrences are "not so regrettable as our Brother Junior Warden thinks" (017-015), and the Lodge briefly dissolves into procedural chaos — motions for a smaller committee of three, five, or twelve members (017-017)(017-018)(017-019) — before the Worshipful Master restores order.

Judex Formidabilis, who belongs to religious circles that have "received divine grace in especial power," argues the Absolute's manifestations are "praiseworthy, uplifting, and moral" and moves that the Lodge declare neutrality (017-027). The Senior Warden, a skeptic who "doesn't believe in any of this stuff," opposes the motion — not on doctrinal grounds but because he finds the whole business undignified, moving instead that they "secretly let it be known that we have the very best information about the whole business" (017-028). Bondy, caught between his role as impartial Worshipful Master and his personal interest as M.E.C. chairman, steers the debate toward practical matters: the Industrial Federation has elected the Absolute as honorary President, M.E.C. shares are rising, and a donor has contributed one thousand shares to the Lodge's Benevolent Fund (017-029). The Junior Warden then attempts to withdraw his earlier remarks: "I beg to withdraw, so to speak, the regrettable occurrences" (017-030).

### The Grand Lodge Decree

The meeting concludes with Bondy reading the Grand Lodge's decree, which functions as the novel's most concentrated satire of institutional religion's response to the numinous (017-031):

> "The Grand Lodge recommends the Masters to join religious circles and organize them in the Masonic sense for the instruction of apprentices in the craft. The new Temples are to be conducted in an enlightened and anticlerical spirit. It is desirable to examine the various doctrines — monism, abstinence, Fletcherism, vegetarianism, and so forth. Each circle is to be instructed in a different faith in order to test out practically which is the best for the welfare of mankind and for the glory of the Most High." (017-031)

The decree treats religious truth as a product to be A/B tested — "each circle instructed in a different faith" — capturing Capek's satire of pragmatism applied to the sacred. These activities are made "obligatory on all Masters by decree of the Grand Lodge" (017-031).

### Public Endorsement

By Chapter XVIII, the Masonic Lodges have publicly aligned with the Absolute:

> "There was also the announcement that the Masonic Lodges commend the Absolute to the support of their members. Did you read that?" (018-046)

This endorsement places the Lodges alongside the Catholic Church, the Free Thought Society, the Salvation Army, and the Theosophical Centre as institutions competing to claim or channel the Absolute phenomenon. The Bishop's exasperation at the Masonic endorsement — coupled with his own strategic pivot to co-opt the Absolute — makes the Lodges a catalyst in the novel's broader satire of institutional religious opportunism.

## Thematic Significance

The Masonic Lodges exemplify the novel's central satirical thesis: that institutions, faced with the genuinely transcendent, can only respond with bureaucracy. The Grand Lodge does not ask whether the Absolute is true — it asks which doctrine "tests out practically" best (017-031). The Lodge meeting does not seek wisdom — it follows Robert's Rules of Order, with motions, amendments, and committee sizes (017-017)(017-018)(017-019). Capek's Masons are not villains; they are modern men whose institutional habits have atrophied their capacity for genuine spiritual response. The "enlightened and anticlerical spirit" the Grand Lodge mandates is itself a dogma, no more adequate to the Absolute than the superstitions it opposes (017-031).

The Lodge meeting in Chapter XVII serves as a structural miniature of the entire novel: a contained space where competing metaphysical claims are reduced to procedural theater. The Junior Warden buries the miraculous under layers of "so to speak" (017-013); the Senior Warden, a pure instrumentalist, cares only about public relations (017-028); Judex Formidabilis, the sincere believer, is outmaneuvered by parliamentary procedure. Bondy himself embodies the novel's portrait of the modern executive — aware that something genuinely transcendent is occurring, yet incapable of engaging with it on any terms other than share prices and industrial partnerships (017-029).
