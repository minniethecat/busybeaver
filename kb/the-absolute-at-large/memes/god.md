**God** in Capek's *The Absolute at Large* is radically redefined from a theological abstraction into a physical reality — a chemically pure substance released by the complete combustion of matter in [[rudy-marek|Marek]]'s [[the-karburator|Karburator]]. As Marek explains to [[g-h-bondy|Bondy]], the Karburator "manufactures a byproduct: pure and unconfined Absolute, God in a chemically pure form" (004-004). The novel's central conceit transforms the question of divine existence from philosophy to engineering, and in doing so launches a satirical exploration of what happens when the divine becomes an industrial commodity.

## Definition

God in the novel is not a transcendent being but an immanent substance — the Absolute that pervades all matter. Marek's pantheistic hypothesis, drawing on Spinoza and Fechner, posits that "all matter is really Matter plus Absolute" (003-041). When the Karburator achieves total atomic combustion, it destroys only the material half, leaving behind "the chemically unanalysable, immaterial residue" — pure God (003-041). This God is "not subject to the laws of matter" and manifests through miracles, prophecy, levitation, and healing (003-041), (004-010).

## In the Novel

### The Atheist's Discovery

The irony at the heart of the novel is that God is discovered not by a mystic or a theologian, but by a hard-nosed atheist engineer. Rudolf Marek, a man who "believed in matter and in progress and in nothing else," stumbles upon the divine entirely by accident while pursuing a more efficient method of combustion:

> (003-031) "I don't believe in anything," said Marek in a hard voice. "I don't want to believe. I have always been an atheist. I believed in matter and in progress and in nothing else. I'm a scientific man, Bondy; and science cannot admit the existence of God."

Yet Marek's own invention forces him to confront the very thing his scientific worldview cannot accommodate. He frames the conflict in stark terms: "But from the scientific point of view, Bondy, it is absolutely intolerable. It's a case of Him or science. I don't assert that God does not exist; I only assert that He ought not to exist, or at least *ought* not to let Himself be seen" (003-033). The novel thus opens with science and religion not as abstract debating positions but as concrete antagonists locked in a single man's psyche.

### The Pantheistic Hypothesis

Marek explains to Bondy the philosophical foundation of his discovery — the pantheistic belief that God permeates all matter:

> (003-035) "Do you know what Pantheism is? It's the belief that God, or the Absolute, if you prefer it, is manifest in everything that exists. In men, as in stones, in the grass, the water — everywhere."

Building on Spinoza and Fechner, Marek proposes that if God is omnipresent in all matter, then the complete destruction of matter must release God as an indestructible residue:

> (003-041) "What is left behind is pure God. A chemical nullity which acts with monstrous energy. Being immaterial, it is not subject to the laws of matter."

Marek elaborates that "all matter is really Matter plus Absolute" — what the Karburator destroys is only the material half, leaving behind "the chemically unanalysable, immaterial residue, which shows no spectrum lines, neither atomic weight nor chemical affinity, no obedience to Boyle's law, none, none whatever, of the properties of matter" (003-041). This is the novel's metaphysical coup: God is not above nature but *inside* it, and modern industrial technology is the key that unlocks the cage.

### Miraculous Manifestations

The release of the Absolute produces immediate, tangible supernatural effects. Marek himself becomes an unwilling miracle-worker as the Karburator operates in his cellar:

> (004-010) "I had moments of astounding illumination. I saw, for instance, quite clearly, things that would happen in the future. I predicted even your visit here. And once I tore my nail off on a lathe. I looked at the damaged finger, and all at once a new nail grew on it. Very likely I'd formed the wish, but all the same it's queer and… terrible. Another time — just think of it — I rose right up into the air. It's called levitation, you know."

The effects are not limited to Marek. His porter and his wife — "thoroughly hardheaded" people — are transformed by proximity to the Absolute:

> (004-014) "He got religion. He was inspired. He gave religious addresses and performed miracles. His wife uttered prophecies. My porter had been a thoroughly hardheaded chap, a monist and a freethinker, and an unusually steady fellow. Well, just fancy, from no visible cause whatever, he started healing people by laying on of hands."

The spread of the Absolute follows physical laws — "in the air it spreads as swiftly as light" — but its effects are anything but physical. Marek finds himself unable to contain it: "I've tried every possible isolating material that might prevent the Absolute from getting out of the cellar. Ashes, sand, metal walls, nothing can keep it back. I've even tried covering the cellar with the works of Professor Krejci, Spencer, Haeckel, and all the Positivists you can think of: would you believe it, the Absolute goes calmly through even that stuff!" (004-016)

### God as Industrial Byproduct

The Karburator's terrifying implication is that God becomes a manufacturing waste product:

> (004-004) "As a result of its complete disintegration of matter, my Perfect Karburator manufactures a byproduct: pure and unconfined Absolute, God in a chemically pure form."

Marek warns Bondy of the social consequences: "It hurls God as a byproduct into the world. I implore you, Bondy, don't underrate what it means. We aren't used to reckoning with God as a *reality*. We don't know what His presence may bring about — say, socially, morally, and so on. Why, man, this thing affects the whole of human civilization!" (004-018)

Bondy's response embodies the capitalist mindset that Capek satirizes throughout: "After all, I haven't anything against God. Only He oughtn't to interfere with business" (004-023). He even proposes negotiating a formal contract with the Absolute, stipulating production quotas and non-interference clauses:

> (004-025) "Perhaps you could come to some agreement with Him. A proper formal contract, in something like this style: 'We guarantee to produce You discreetly and continuously to an extent to be fixed by mutual agreement; in return for which You pledge yourself to refrain from any divine manifestations within such and such a radius from the place of origin.'"

Bondy goes so far as to purchase a baroque church on the White Mountain for three hundred thousand, offering it to the Absolute as "more dignified quarters" than a factory cellar — an attempt to manage the divine through real estate that encapsulates the novel's satirical vision of religion commodified.

### The Church's Response

The Catholic Church, represented by Bishop Linda, rejects this mechanically-produced deity as heretical:

> (005-028) "From the Catholic standpoint God cannot be brought into the matter, that the Church absolutely rejects and forbids the pantheistic hypothesis as heresy. In short, that this isn't any legal, duly recognized God."

The bishop warns: "Do not imagine that the Church brings God into the world. The Church merely confines Him and controls Him. And you two unbelievers are loosing Him upon the earth like a flood" (005-064).

The ecclesiastical response reveals a deep institutional paradox: the Church, whose entire purpose is to mediate between humanity and the divine, refuses to recognize a God that manifests without its authorization. Bishop Linda's position is not theological but jurisdictional — the Absolute is "false, perverse, and heretical" not because it is not God, but because it is not *the Church's* God. Capek's satire cuts both ways: industrial capitalism tries to commodify God, while organized religion tries to trademark Him.

### The Absolute's Proliferation and Consequences

God, once released, proves uncontrollable. Bondy observes the mounting chaos from his commercial empire:

> (010-003) Bondy: "There are several thousand Karburators at work in the world already, every one of them pouring forth the Absolute day and night."

The economic miracle produced by divinely-powered factories paradoxically destroys the economy — goods are produced so abundantly and given away so freely that markets collapse. As Bondy reflects on the impossibility of ordinary life under the Absolute:

> (010-028) "Then listen to me, Ellen," said Bondy. "I can tell you everything, for you'd read it in me in any case. I could never marry you, Ellen … neither business nor society is possible without thoughts that are not disclosed."

The culmination of God's release is the [[the-greatest-war|Greatest War]] — a global religious conflict in which nations fight not for territory but for theological supremacy. The novel's final chapters trace the self-consuming trajectory of unleashed divinity, ending with the exhaustion of the Absolute and the shattered remnants of human civilization.

## Interpretations

Capek's God-as-byproduct conceit operates on multiple satirical levels:

1. **The Collision of Science and Religion**: By making God a physical substance accessible to scientific verification, Capek exposes the mutual incompatibility of the two worldviews. Marek's anguish — "a case of Him or science" (003-033) — is not resolved but rendered irrelevant by the onward march of events.

2. **The Commodification of the Divine**: God becomes a product to be manufactured, distributed, and marketed. Bondy's negotiation — "I'll give you three percent of the gross profits" (004-031) — treats the creator of the universe as a licensor of intellectual property.

3. **Institutional Capture**: Both capitalism (through M.E.C.) and the Church attempt to control God. The Church's trajectory from rejection to canonization — culminating in "Habemus Deum" (019-017) — demonstrates organized religion's genius for absorbing even the most inconvenient revelations.

4. **The Democratic Divine**: Kuzenda's God is "a thorough democrat" who "cannot bear politics and money, intellect, pride, and self-conceit" (008-039). The Absolute democratizes religious experience — anyone can be a prophet, a saint, a miracle-worker — but this democratization leads to sectarian fragmentation and violence.

5. **Human Incapacity for the Divine**: The novel's darkest satirical insight is that humanity cannot tolerate an active, present God. Bishop Linda's prophecy that "not a single Karburator will be running" by year's end (005-060) proves correct: "mankind, whether believers or unbelievers, cannot do with a real and active God" (005-062).
