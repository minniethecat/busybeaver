# Types of Believers

Karel Capek's *The Absolute at Large* is not a novel about God so much as a novel about what people **do** with God once they have Him. The Absolute -- released from matter by Marek's Karburator as an industrial byproduct -- acts as a universal solvent that dissolves social convention and reveals what each person truly is. Every character who encounters the Absolute responds according to a preexisting disposition, and the resulting taxonomy of religious types constitutes one of Capek's sharpest satirical achievements. There is no single "religious experience" in the novel; there are six distinct patterns, each shaped by class, temperament, and institutional position. The point is not that the Absolute is different for different people -- it is remorselessly the same divine essence -- but that **human beings are incapable of encountering the divine without filtering it through their own limitations**. In presenting believers as types, Capek exposes the sociological machinery beneath religious enthusiasm and asks whether any human response to God can escape the gravitational pull of self-interest.

## The Reluctant Prophet: [[rudy-marek|Rudolf Marek]]

Marek is the novel's tragic ironist: the atheist who invents a God-machine. A scientific materialist who "believed in matter and in progress and in nothing else" [(003-008)], Marek has spent twenty years perfecting the complete combustion of matter, only to discover that his Karburator releases what he most despises -- pure, active divinity. His response is not belief but **horror**.

Unlike every other character in the novel, Marek experiences the Absolute and runs in the opposite direction. He witnesses levitation, clairvoyance, miraculous healing -- "I had moments of astounding illumination. I saw, for instance, quite clearly, things that would happen in the future. I predicted even your visit here" [(003-011)] -- and is appalled. He tries to clog the Karburator with sand, to line the cellar with positivist philosophy, to offer it a baroque church as a decoy. He is a prophet who refuses his calling, a Moses who, upon seeing the burning bush, attempts to extinguish it with a fire blanket.

What distinguishes Marek as a type is that he represents **the scientist who cannot reconcile empirical evidence with metaphysical commitment**. He sees the Absolute operating, acknowledges its reality, and yet cannot abandon his atheism because atheism is not, for him, a proposition about facts -- it is an identity. "I don't believe in anything. I don't want to believe" [(003-008)]. The italics on "want" are Capek's, and they are crucial: Marek's unbelief is volitional, not intellectual. His tragedy is that he is the only character in the novel who fully understands what the Absolute is and the only character who refuses to accept what it means.

> [(003-007)] Marek burst out, shaking his fists threateningly: "Look here, Bondy, that's why I must sell that Karburator! I simply can't stand it -- I can't stand it -- *I can't stand it*. I never dreamed my Karburator would do anything like *this* ... this ... terrifying mischief!"

Marek's fate is to hand his invention to Bondy and retreat. He warns Bondy -- "It means letting God loose upon this world!" [(003-016)] -- and then, his warning ignored, becomes complicit in the catastrophe. The reluctant prophet who abdicates responsibility is, in Capek's taxonomy, as dangerous as the fanatic.

## The Exploitative Capitalist: [[g-h-bondy|G. H. Bondy]]

Bondy is the novel's supreme pragmatist, a man for whom the Absolute is not a theological problem but a **business opportunity**. When he first encounters the Absolute in Marek's cellar, he falls to his knees in ecstasy -- a moment of genuine religious transport. But Bondy recovers with remarkable speed. Within minutes he is already thinking about share prices.

His genius is to recognize that the Absolute, whatever its metaphysical status, can be monetized. "So long as the Karburator really works," he tells Marek, the question of whether it produces God is secondary [(003-014)]. Bondy scales production to twelve hundred Karburators in six months, then ten thousand in a year. When the Absolute begins causing disruptions -- religious strikes at banks, miraculous overproduction at textile factories, the conversion of his own board members -- Bondy's response is not to stop production but to redirect it: "From today onward we will not deliver a single Karburator in Czechoslovakia ... We'll flood the Germans and the French with them; then we'll bombard England with the Absolute" [(010-001)].

Bondy represents the type of believer for whom **religious conviction is entirely instrumental**. He never doubts the Absolute's reality; he simply does not care. His famous line to Marek -- "From the business point of view, it's a matter of indifference. If He wants to exist, in Heaven's name, let Him. We aren't mutually exclusive" [(003-008)] -- is the novel's definitive statement of capitalist theology. Bondy is not an atheist; he is something more unsettling: a theist for whom God's existence has no implications.

> [(005-004)] Bondy: "I'll set aside three millions myself for purposes of research into protective measures against God. Better say two millions to start with."

The sardonic self-correction -- from three million to two -- captures Bondy's character with devastating economy. Even in planning defensive measures against the Almighty, the capitalist instinct for cost-cutting prevails.

Bondy's fate is ambiguous. He retains control of the M.E.C. through the crisis, but the novel implies that the destruction unleashed by the Absolute -- global religious warfare, economic collapse -- flows directly from his decision to mass-produce divinity. The exploitative capitalist profits from the Absolute, but the profit is measured in a currency he ultimately cannot spend.

## The Institutional Bureaucrat: [[bishop-linda|Bishop Linda]]

Bishop Linda is, in many ways, the novel's most sophisticated religious type. He is a professional man of God who, when confronted with an actual, verifiable divine manifestation, responds not with awe but with **administrative concern**. The Absolute is real, yes -- Linda does not deny the miracles -- but is it *authorized*? Does it comply with canon law? "It contradicts the doctrine of the holy sacraments. It does not regard the traditions of the Church. It seriously violates the doctrine of the Trinity. It does not even submit to the rites of exorcism" [(005-007)].

Linda's visit to Bondy's office is the novel's most concentrated satire of institutional religion. He proposes that the Church take over the Břevnov cellar and operate it as a pilgrimage shrine, but under strict conditions: the Absolute must be "only weak, almost innocuous, very much diluted ... rather as at Lourdes" [(005-009)]. The proposal is breathtaking: the Church does not want God; it wants a manageable, branded, franchised God who respects ecclesiastical hierarchy and generates tourist revenue. When Marek protests, Linda delivers the novel's most prophetic warning:

> [(005-011)] Bishop Linda: "Do not imagine that the Church brings God into the world. The Church merely confines Him and controls Him. And you two unbelievers are loosing Him upon the earth like a flood. The ship of Peter will survive even this deluge; like the Ark of Noah, it will ride out this inundation of the Absolute -- but your modern society, *that* will pay the price!"

This is a remarkable moment, because Linda is simultaneously the novel's most cynical and most perceptive character. He understands something Marek and Bondy do not: that civilization depends on keeping God at a distance. Organized religion, in Capek's rendering, is not about facilitating divine encounter but about **containing** it -- about substituting ritual for revelation, hierarchy for ecstasy, dogma for mystery. Linda is the institutional bureaucrat who knows exactly what his institution is for, and the knowledge makes him, paradoxically, the novel's closest approximation to a voice of reason.

Linda's fate is survival. He reappears in the epilogue as Bishop of Hradec Kralove, still dispensing "wise counsels" through wave after wave of regime change []. The institutional bureaucrat endures because institutions, unlike prophets or capitalists, are built to absorb disruption.

## The Ecstatic Populist: Jan Binder

Jan Binder, the proprietor of a merry-go-round, installs a Karburator to power his attraction and discovers that his machine does more than rotate painted horses -- it makes the earth itself spin while the merry-go-round becomes "a lovely island of quiet and repose" []. Binder's religious response pattern is **charismatic populism**: he has no theology, no institutional affiliation, no intellectual framework whatsoever. What he has is an irresistible personal magnetism, a striped jersey, and a talent for improvising sermons.

Binder's ministry is purely experiential. People come to his merry-go-round, they are overcome by "the dizzy whirling of the earth," they are "brought to safety" and "strengthened for their future life by the apt exhortations of Jan Binder" [(011-008)]. His address to the crowd captures the essence of his type: "Well, good people, here we have landed out of the whirl and confusion of the world. Here we have peace amid the storm. Here we are with God, as safe as in our beds" [(011-007)].

What distinguishes Binder from Kuzenda (to whom he is often compared) is that Binder's religion is fundamentally **spectacular**. It is religion as carnival, complete with an orchestrion, painted swans, and "free rides today." The external trappings -- the "broad-striped jersey," the deer with its "wildly staring eyes," the Three Graces painted on the orchestrion -- are not incidental to his ministry; they are its substance. Binder represents the type of believer for whom religious experience is inseparable from aesthetic and emotional stimulation.

> [(011-005)] Jan Binder stood there with his lips pursed and his hands in his pockets. He gazed upon his merry-go-round as though in a dream, seemingly entranced by something new and lovely.

His conflict with Kuzenda -- a sectarian brawl in which Kuzenda's baker breaks Binder's collarbone and Binder's followers smash the deer's antlers from the merry-go-round -- is Capek's satire of denominational schism reduced to its absurd essence: two groups of people who have both encountered the same Absolute, fighting over whose sacred object (dredge or merry-go-round) is the true vessel of divinity.

Binder's fate is perhaps the novel's gentlest arc. He reappears in the epilogue as a prosperous innkeeper, making liver sausages and quoting Kuzenda's wisdom: "Everyone believes in his own superior God, but he doesn't believe in another man, or credit him with believing in something good" [(030-008)]. The ecstatic populist, having lost his God, becomes a purveyor of earthly comforts -- and a better philosopher than he ever was a prophet.

## The Ascetic Mystic: [[jan-binder-kuzenda|Kuzenda]]

Kuzenda, the skipper of dredge M.E.28 on the Vltava, represents the **contemplative mystical type** -- the believer for whom the Absolute is not a source of power, profit, or spectacle, but of inward transformation. A former political activist, Kuzenda undergoes the novel's most complete metamorphosis: from party organizer to levitating saint. His ministry on the dredge has no program, no ambition, no institutional structure. It consists of coffee, healing, and gently delivered moral truths.

Kuzenda's defining characteristic is what might be called **involuntary holiness**. He does not seek to levitate; he is visibly embarrassed by it. He emerges from below deck carrying a tray of coffee cups, floating helplessly, "wearing the forced, embarrassed smile of one who is being shoved from behind and is trying to pretend that nothing is happening" [(010-013)]. Miracles happen through him, not because of him -- he heals the baker's asthma with an absent-minded gesture he cannot even remember making.

> [(010-024)] Kuzenda: "The greatest miracle is the influence He has on our feelings. It is so intensely beautiful that it fairly makes one shiver. Sometimes you feel as if you could die of love and happiness, as if you were one with the water below, with all the animals, with the very earth and stones."

This is the language of mystical union, and it is distinct from every other character's response to the Absolute. Where Marek intellectualizes, Bondy commercializes, Linda bureaucratizes, and Binder sensationalizes, Kuzenda simply **surrenders**. His theology is homespun -- "God is not so infinite as the Catholics assert. He is about six hundred metres in diameter, and even then is weak towards the edges" [(010-024)] -- but his experience is authentic. Kuzenda's mysticism is the democratized, working-class version of a tradition that usually belongs to saints and monastics.

Kuzenda's fate is obscurity. By the epilogue, his movement has dissipated. Brych reports that Kuzenda is "not very grand" but remains "one of the best men breathing" [(030-002)]. The ascetic mystic does not change the world, but the world does not change him either. His final recorded thought, relayed by Binder, captures the novel's deepest moral insight: "People should first of all believe in other people, and the rest would soon follow" [(030-008)]. In a novel about what happens when God becomes available to everyone, the conclusion reached by its purest mystic is that God is less important than other human beings.

## The Rationalist Observer: [[dr-blahous|Dr. Blahous]]

Dr. Blahous, fifty-five years old and still a mere Lecturer in Comparative Religion at the University of Prague, represents the **academic rationalist** -- the believer who insists on understanding religious experience through the lens of scholarly categories that render it safely remote. His article "Religious Phenomena of Recent Times" begins with Cicero and proceeds through 346 pages of comparative taxonomy, classifying Kuzenda's movement as "fetish-worship and even totemism," Binder's as "related to the Dancing Dervishes and ancient orgiastic cults," and the entire religious wave as "barbaric atavism" attributable to "degenerate hysterical subjects" and "collective psychical epidemic among the superstitious and mentally inferior masses" [(012-007)-(012-010)].

Blahous is the type of believer who maintains distance through classification. Every genuine religious phenomenon is immediately translated into the language of comparative anthropology, stripped of its disruptive power by being positioned as just another instance of a universal pattern. The irony -- and it is one of Capek's finest -- is that Blahous is writing this rationalist dismissal at the very moment he himself is succumbing to the Absolute.

> [(012-012)] Dr. Blahous stood there in the window, stiff and shaking. "There is something here all about me," he suddenly perceived with a sweet and overwhelming emotion. "Dear God, I am not alone after all! Someone's arm is around me, someone is beside me; oh, if he would only stay!"

Blahous finishes his 346-page rationalist treatise, leans out his window at half-past four in the morning, looks at the stars for the first time in thirty years, and is converted. He then returns to his desk and writes a conclusion that flatly contradicts everything he has just argued: "Nevertheless I say unto you: Give yourself up with your whole beings to the Absolute; believe in God, in whatsoever form He may declare Himself to you" [(012-015)]. The editor publishes the contradictory whole with a cautious note. Dr. Regner, the rival Lecturer in Philosophy, dismisses Blahous with the novel's cruelest line: "How on earth can a man have the nerve to pose as an expert on religion when he actually believes in God?" [(012-015)].

Blahous's fate is professional success. He gets his Assistant Professorship. But the Blahous of the final chapters -- represented by his elderly father, Grandfather Blahous of Seven Cottages, who declares that "the old one was good enough for us country people ... He didn't show himself to anybody, but we had peace instead" [(028-003)] -- suggests that the rationalist observer and the simple believer exist on a continuum, and that the academic's elaborate system-building is just as provincial, in its way, as the peasant's folk theology.

## Capek's Religious Satire: Why Believers Become Types

Capek's decision to present believers as types rather than as unique individuals is itself the novel's central satirical argument. By making the Absolute a standardized industrial product -- every Karburator releases exactly the same divine essence -- Capek creates a controlled experiment. The variable is not the God; the variable is the human being who encounters the God. And the result of the experiment is that **human nature cannot transcend itself, even in the presence of the transcendent**.

Each type in Capek's taxonomy is defined by a preexisting social role: the scientist, the capitalist, the bureaucrat, the entertainer, the activist, the academic. The Absolute does not transform these roles; it intensifies them. Marek becomes more scientifically scrupulous. Bondy becomes more commercially calculating. Linda becomes more institutionally shrewd. Binder becomes more theatrically populist. Kuzenda becomes more inwardly contemplative. Blahous becomes more academically productive -- 346 pages in a single night.

This is a deeply pessimistic vision of religion. The implication is that no one -- not the atheist, not the priest, not the mystic, not the scholar -- is capable of encountering God on God's own terms. Everyone domesticates the divine into a shape that fits their existing life. The tragedy of the Absolute is not that it is false but that it is true, and truth, once released, immediately becomes the property of liars, salesmen, bureaucrats, and fools.

And yet Capek is not entirely without hope. The epilogue, set many years after the Absolute has dissipated, brings together in a single tavern the survivors of the religious explosion: Binder the innkeeper, Brych the locksmith, Father Jost the canon, Rejzek the journalist. They eat liver sausages, discuss the proper preparation of sauerkraut, and arrive, through this mundane communion, at a wisdom that eluded them during the religious frenzy: "The world will be an evil place as long as people don't believe in other people" [(030-008)]. The six types of believers, having exhausted their respective approaches to God, discover that the only thing worth believing in is each other.

| Ecstatic Populist | Jan Binder | Channels the Absolute into carnivalesque spectacle; religion as entertainment and crowd-pleasing rhetoric | Loses his merry-go-round to sectarian violence; becomes a prosperous innkeeper; evolves into an accidental philosopher |
