# Omnipotence

**Omnipotence** in Čapek's *The Absolute at Large* is presented as a real but dangerously unorganized attribute of the Absolute — it manifests through chaotic overproduction, currency collapse, and random miracles rather than systematic divine providence.

## Economic Manifestation

The Absolute's omnipotence finds quantitative expression in industrial hyperproduction. The narrative frames the ensuing economic catastrophe as a direct consequence of divine limitlessness:

> "Whether you offered a halfpenny or half a million for a child's lollypop, it was all the same from the business point of view: you wouldn't get the lollypop, anyhow, for they had all disappeared. Figures had lost all significance. This collapse of the numerical system is, in any case, the natural consequence of the infinitude and omnipotence of God."

## Marek's Critique

Engineer Marek, who tracks the Absolute's activities methodically, identifies its lack of organization as a critical weakness:

> "I tell you that there isn't a scrap of consistency about them. They're all merely the improvisations of omnipotence. He performs tremendous tricks, but at random, disconnectedly, confusedly. His activity isn't organized a scrap."

Marek even drafts a mock arrest warrant listing the Absolute's "distinguishing marks: omnipotence" alongside its "crimes" — alienation of private property, illegal practice of medicine, and interference with officials.

## National Temperaments

The British diplomat Sir W. O'Patterney observes that the Absolute's omnipotence expresses differently across nations:

> "In my country He strikes one as much more of a Conservative. He behaves like an omnipotent clergyman. Holds meetings, processions, sermons in the streets, and such things."
