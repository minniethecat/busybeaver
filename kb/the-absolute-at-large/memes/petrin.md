## Overview

Petřín is a prominent hill in Prague's Malá Strana district, known for its observation tower and parkland. In *The Absolute at Large*, the hill becomes the site of an early and particularly visible outbreak of the Absolute's influence: the wireless telegraph operators stationed on Petřín begin spontaneously broadcasting religious messages, transforming a communications facility into an unwitting pulpit.

## Location

Petřín rises to 327 meters on the left bank of the Vltava in central Prague, between the Castle district (Hradčany) and the Smíchov quarter. Its wireless station, established in the early twentieth century, made it a strategic node in Czechoslovakia's international communications network — and thus a vector for the Absolute's global propagation.

## Significance in the Novel

### The Wireless Station Epidemic

G. H. Bondy describes the situation to Marek with alarm:

> "There was even one case right out in Vokovice and two in Košiře, while at the Petřín wireless station there's practically an epidemic of religion. All the wireless operators on duty up there are sending out ecstatic messages of their own accord, a sort of new gospel to the world at large: God coming down again to the earth to ransom it, and so forth."

The Petřín incident is particularly dangerous because of its technological dimension. Unlike the isolated religious experiences at the Minister's villa or the Břevnov cellar, the wireless station broadcasts the Absolute's message globally. The operators, transformed from technicians into prophets, use the most advanced communications technology of their era to evangelize — a fusion of divine inspiration and electromagnetic transmission that no government can contain.

### Public Scandal

Bondy notes the political fallout: "Now the progressive papers are going for the Post Office, and the fur's fairly flying. They're screaming about Clericalism showing its horns". The public interprets the Petřín incident through existing political categories — clericalism versus secularism — completely missing the Karburator as the true cause. This misattribution allows the Absolute to spread further while the public debate circles around the wrong question.

## Thematic Resonance

Petřín embodies the novel's central irony about technology: the same infrastructure that should serve rational communication — the wireless, the pinnacle of modern engineering — becomes instead a vehicle for unreason and ecstasy. The operators who were trained to transmit news and diplomatic cables find themselves transmitting gospels. Čapek's choice of Petřín — a hill associated with Prague's panoramic views, its Eiffel-like observation tower a symbol of rational perspective — as the site of irrational contagion is a deliberate inversion. From Petřín's heights, one should see clearly; instead, the Absolute imposes a different kind of vision.
