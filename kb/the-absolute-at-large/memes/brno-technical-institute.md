## Overview

The Brno Technical Institute is the engineering school where G. H. Bondy and Rudy Marek first met as students — a shared past that Bondy recalls in Chapter I when he notices Marek's advertisement. The Institute later serves as one of the Karburator's first independent testing sites, and its faculty become public commentators on the Absolute phenomenon. Throughout the novel, the Institute embodies the technological rationalism that the Karburator explosion both depends upon and ultimately overwhelms.

## Role in the Novel

### Bondy and Marek's Alma Mater

The Institute first appears through Bondy's memory in the novel's opening pages. Reading a newspaper advertisement for an invention, Bondy suddenly recognizes the name:

> G. H. Bondy sat up with a jerk. "R. Marek, Engineer; R. Marek, Engineer. Half a minute: mightn't that be that red-haired Marek — let's see, what was his name? Rudolph, Rudy Marek, my old chum Rudy of the Technical School?" (001-011)

This recollection establishes the Institute as the seedbed of the novel's central relationship — the bond between the inventor and the industrialist that will set the entire Absolute crisis in motion. The Technical School years are described as a time of "extravagant, romantic days" when the two friends dreamed of "turning the world upside down" — an ambition the Karburator accidentally fulfills.

### Karburator Testing Site

In Chapter VI, the Chairman of the M.E.C. Board presents the engineering test report on ten prototype Karburators:

> "Karburator No. 3 is in the testing laboratory of the Brno Technical Institute. No. 4 was damaged in transport. No. 5 is supplying the city of Hradec Králové with light." (006-015)

The Institute's testing laboratory is one of five independently verified deployment sites. That the Brno engineers confirm the Karburator's performance — "The Karburator is no swindle" — gives the Board the technical confidence to proceed with mass production, even as the directors themselves report strange spiritual side effects.

### The Opening Ceremony

When the Karburator Central Power Station is formally opened in Chapter IX, the Director of the Technical Institute attends among the dignitaries:

> "And the new Minister for War. You see, I told you so. The Director of the Technical Institute. The Chairman of the M.E.C. The Chief Rabbi." (009-010)

The Director's presence among ambassadors, ministers, and religious leaders at the ceremony illustrates the Institute's status as a pillar of the establishment — a scientific institution whose authority is invoked alongside that of church and state.

### Scientific Endorsement

In Chapter XIX, as public intellectuals respond to the Absolute phenomenon, Professor Lupen of the Brno Technical Institute issues a characteristically terse scientific endorsement:

> Professor Lupen of the Brno Technical Institute wrote: "From the standpoint of effective performance, the Absolute is a force deserving of the highest respect." (019-008)

Lupen's one-sentence judgment — evaluating the Absolute purely on "effective performance" — captures the Institute's engineering worldview in miniature. Where theologians debate doctrine and politicians negotiate treaties, the engineer measures output. The sentence is both a compliment and Čapek's quiet satire of a mindset that can encounter the divine and see only a heat rate.

## Thematic Significance

The Brno Technical Institute represents the novel's ambivalent portrait of scientific rationalism. On one hand, its engineers provide the honest, empirical verification that the Karburator is real — the antidote to superstition and fraud. On the other hand, their frame of reference is so narrowly technical that they cannot perceive the Absolute as anything but a performance metric. Professor Lupen's one-line endorsement is simultaneously the most honest and the most inadequate response to the Absolute in the novel: honest because it refuses to pretend to theological expertise; inadequate because "effective performance" is precisely what makes the Absolute so dangerous. The Institute's tragedy is not that it is wrong about the Karburator — it is right — but that being right about the machine gives it no wisdom about what the machine unleashes.
