## Overview

The Battle at Sandberk is a microcosm of the [[the-absolute|Absolute]]'s fragmenting effect on institutions. When the [[prague-in-the-novel|Prague]] garrison splits along doctrinal lines — the Vršovice barracks adopting the "Three Degrees of Salvation" dogma while the Černin barracks refuse — the result is an absurd civil war within a single city's military forces, foreshadowing the global religious conflicts to come.

## Context

By the time of Chapter XXII, the [[the-absolute|Absolute]]'s influence has thoroughly penetrated Czech society. Political parties have fragmented into religious sects; synagogues erupt in theological brawls; even the Communists have split into mystic and rationalist wings. The military, supposedly the last bastion of secular discipline, proves no exception.

## The Ultimatum

The crisis begins when the Vršovice barracks issues an ultimatum to the Černin barracks, demanding recognition of the "Vršovice dogma of the Three Degrees of Salvation." When the Černin garrison refuses this theological demand, Vršovice declares war. The soldiers barricade themselves in with machine-guns at the windows.

> [(022-008)] "The Vršovice barracks have sent an ultimatum to the Černin barracks calling on them to recognize the Vršovice dogma of the Three Degrees of Salvation. If they don't accept the doctrine, they are to report for battle at Sandberk."

## The Siege

The military response is immediate but absurd: the Dejvice artillerymen go to the Černin barracks "to disband" them — an ambiguous phrase that could mean dispersal or conversion. The Vršovice garrison is besieged by the Seventh Dragoons, the Castle Guard, and four light batteries. They are given six hours to surrender before the firing begins.

The choice of Sandberk as the designated battlefield — a location presumably on the outskirts of [[prague-in-the-novel|Prague]] — suggests the combatants still maintain some vestige of military protocol even as they prepare to kill each other over theological abstractions. The scene is reported by Mr. [[cyril-keval|Keval]] with a mixture of journalistic detachment and dark amusement:

> [(022-007)] "Rejzek, it's a real pleasure to be alive in these days."

## Narrative Significance

The Battle at Sandberk demonstrates the [[the-absolute|Absolute]]'s most dangerous property: its ability to turn any human institution against itself. An army exists to maintain order, but when every soldier is divinely certain of his own salvation doctrine, the chain of command dissolves into holy war. The battle is simultaneously comic (soldiers fighting over "Three Degrees of Salvation") and terrifying (machine-guns, barricades, a six-hour ultimatum). This tonal duality — the absurd and the lethal occupying the same frame — is Čapek's signature satirical technique throughout the novel.

## Aftermath

The Sandberk confrontation is subsumed into the larger pattern of fragmentation that precedes the Greatest War. It is reported by the historian Usinski alongside similar local battles at Gorochovky, Les Batignolles, and Nieuport — each a miniature holy war that, in aggregate, destroys the capacity for organized resistance to the [[the-absolute|Absolute]]'s spread.
