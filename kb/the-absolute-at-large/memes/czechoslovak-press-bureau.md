## Overview

The Czechoslovak Press Bureau functions as the state's narrative-management apparatus in *The Absolute at Large*. It is invoked by name in three chapters, each time in connection with the gap between official accounts and actual events. Cyril Keval, the journalist, cynically predicts its role before the Karburator ceremony even begins: "Ah well, the Czechoslovak Press Bureau will supply the speeches, and one can soon work up the trimmings" (009-013). The Bureau represents the institutional reflex to paper over inexplicable occurrences with official language — a reflex that Čapek treats with grim amusement.

## Structure

The Press Bureau is an organ of the Czechoslovak state. Though its internal hierarchy is never detailed in the novel, its function is clear: it collects and distributes official statements to newspapers, serving as the primary channel through which the government communicates with the public and the international press. In Chapter XXII, old Rejzek at the *People's Journal* is seen "turning over the papers from the Czechoslovak Press Bureau" (022-004), suggesting a steady flow of official bulletins that the newspaper staff processes as routine — even as the world outside descends into Absolute-induced chaos.

## Role in the Novel

### The Karburator Opening Cover-Up (Chapter IX)

The Press Bureau's most significant appearance occurs in the aftermath of the disastrous Karburator Central Power Station opening ceremony. Cyril Keval, watching the dignitaries arrive, forecasts the Bureau's role before anything has gone wrong: the speeches will be "supplied" by the Bureau, and "one can soon work up the trimmings — deep impressiveness of the occasion, magnificent progress, enthusiastic reception for the President" (009-013). Keval's cynicism is immediately vindicated: within hours, the ceremony has produced mass religious ecstasy, property destruction, and the death of the doyen of the Diplomatic Corps.

The Bureau's response is a masterpiece of bureaucratic denial:

> Contrary to the reports issued by a local publication which have obtained some currency abroad, we are able to state on the very best authority that no improper incidents of any kind took place on the occasion of the formal opening of the Karburator Central Power Station. In connection with this, the Burgomaster of Greater Prague has resigned his office and has gone into the country to recuperate. Mr. Billington, doyen of the Diplomatic Corps, is, contrary to published reports, well and active. (009-029)

The statement is Čapek at his most surgical: a dead diplomat is declared alive, a resigned mayor is described as "recuperating," and the Absolute's tangible effects are attributed to "insufficient ventilation, combined with excessive strain upon the nerves" (009-029). The Bureau does not merely lie — it constructs an alternative reality with the grammar of officialdom.

### Background Presence (Chapter XXII)

By Chapter XXII, the Press Bureau has become part of the background noise of a world unraveling. At the *People's Journal*, Keval observes the apocalyptic street scene while old Rejzek mechanically sorts Bureau dispatches (022-004). The Bureau continues to produce its bulletins even as the international order collapses around it — an image of institutional inertia persisting past the point of relevance.

## Thematic Significance

The Czechoslovak Press Bureau embodies one of the novel's central satirical targets: the inadequacy of bureaucratic language in the face of the genuinely inexplicable. The Absolute — a divine presence released by a technological accident — cannot be acknowledged in the vocabulary of official communiqués. The Bureau therefore does what bureaucracies do: it rewrites the event into language it can handle. That the result is transparently false is, for Čapek, the point: institutions do not report reality; they produce a version of reality that the institution can survive.

The Bureau also highlights the novel's recurring tension between private experience and public narrative. Keval, who witnessed the Absolute's effects firsthand, knows the communiqué is false — yet the official version is what enters the historical record. The Press Bureau is thus not merely a satirical target but a genuine epistemological problem: who gets to say what happened, and in what language?
