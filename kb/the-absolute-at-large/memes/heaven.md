# Heaven

**Heaven** in Čapek's *The Absolute at Large* operates on two registers: as a colloquial exclamation peppering the dialogue of early chapters, and as a substantive theological concept that the Absolute brings down to earth, transforming ordinary spaces into paradisal communities.

## From Exclamation to Reality

The novel opens with heaven as mere rhetoric — a word of exasperation in the mouths of industrialists facing economic crisis:

> "The Coal Crisis! Mines getting worked out; the Ostrava basin suspending work for years. Heavens above, it's a sheer disaster!" (014-014)

This casual usage gives way to the literal creation of heaven on earth as the Karburator's emanations spread.

## Paradise in the Tenements

Machat describes how the Absolute transforms a working-class apartment building into a paradise:

> "It's so beautifully warm in every one of the houses! Just as though they were being warmed with eternal love! Do you know, whoever enters there becomes at one stroke a changed man. It is like Paradise there. We are all living as if we were in Heaven. Oh, come and join us!"

## The Kingdom of Heaven on Earth

The chronicler's analysis frames the Absolute's religious wave as an attempt to establish the Kingdom of Heaven through evolutionary religious forms:

> "In our godless world the Kingdom of Heaven cannot be established without great sacrifices and confusion of doctrine. Nevertheless I say unto you: Give yourself up with your whole beings to the Absolute; believe in God, in whatsoever form He may declare Himself to you. Behold even now He cometh to set up on our earth, and perchance on other planets of our system also, the everlasting Empire of God, the Czardom of the Absolute."

## Čapek's Ironic Paradise

The novel satirizes the notion that heaven can be manufactured. Bondy's horror at the prospect of living in such a paradise underscores the central irony:

> "God forbid! No, no, Heaven defend me from that! I will not go!"
