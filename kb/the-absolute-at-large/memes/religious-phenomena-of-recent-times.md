## Overview

"Religious Phenomena of Recent Times" is the scholarly article that [[dr-blahous|Dr. Blahous]], Lecturer in Comparative Religion at the University of [[prague|Prague]], composes in Chapter XII of *[[the-absolute|The Absolute]] at Large*. The article begins as a dry exercise in the "exact science of religion" — cataloguing the new sects spawned by the [[the-karburator|Karburator]] as instances of barbaric atavism — but undergoes a dramatic metamorphosis when the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] itself invades [[dr-blahous|Blahous]]'s study mid-composition, transforming his conclusions into an ecstatic proclamation of the coming Kingdom of [[the-absolute|God]].

## Composition

[[dr-blahous|Dr. Blahous]], "only fifty-five years of age," sets down his title and begins with a characteristically academic opening: "The controversy over the definition of the idea of 'religion' has lasted ever since the days of Cicero" [(012-002)]. He proceeds through an exhaustive preamble — 115 pages to arrive at his own definition of religion, which "differed from his predecessor's by precisely one word" [(012-002)] — before turning to the contemporary religious phenomena.

The article's initial argument is rigorously reductionist. [[dr-blahous|Blahous]] classifies the new religious movements as survivals of primitive religion: Kuzendism as fetish-worship, the Binderians as related to Dancing Dervishes, Machat's community as fakirism and asceticism. He invokes "mental contagion and mass-suggestion," the Flagellants, the Crusades, Millenarianism, and "running amok" among the Malays, concluding that the entire religious wave is "nothing but examples of barbaric atavism, and a hotchpotch of the most rudimentary forms of worship still subconsciously active in the human imagination" [(012-003)].

## The Conversion

The composition breaks at page 346 when the [[the-absolute|Absolute]], radiating from a nearby [[the-karburator|Karburator]], enters [[dr-blahous|Blahous]]'s study. The old scholar, who had been "so terribly lonely all the time," experiences a moment of inexplicable sweetness: "Someone's arm is around me, someone is beside me" [(012-010)]. When he returns to his desk, his argument has inverted:

> [(012-012)] On the other hand, however, it is impossible to doubt that God cannot now reveal Himself otherwise than in primitive forms of worship. With the decay of faith in modern times our connection with the old religious life has been broken. To bring us back to Him, God must begin again from the beginning and do as He did with the savages in olden times: at first He is an idol, a fetish; the idol of a group, a clan or a tribe; He animates all nature and works through a witchdoctor.

The article that began as a dismissal of primitive religion ends as its apologia — and a prophetic warning of "an era of religious struggles which will surpass the Crusades in their fury and obstinacy and the last World Wars in their scope" [(012-012)].

## Publication and Reception

The article appears in print, though truncated: "The editor published part of his discussion of the new sects and the whole of his conclusion, with a cautious note to the effect that this paper by the youthful savant was certainly characteristic of our times" [(012-014)]. It causes no immediate stir — "overshadowed by other events" — though Dr. Regner, a rival lecturer, denounces [[dr-blahous|Blahous]] for the heresy of actually believing in [[the-absolute|God]] [(012-014)]. Years later, at the novel's close, the article is recalled as the work of "that [[dr-blahous|Blahous]] who once wrote an article about [[jan-binder-kuzenda|Mr. Kuzenda]]" — a modest epitaph for a paper that inadvertently documented the first stirrings of the [[the-absolute|Absolute]]'s global conquest [(030-006)].
