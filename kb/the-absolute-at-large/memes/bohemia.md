## Overview

Bohemia is the historical kingdom that forms the geographic and cultural core of the novel's setting. Every significant location in *The Absolute at Large* — Prague, Břevnov, Štěchovice, Hradec Králové, the Vltava valley — lies within its borders. Bohemia thus serves as the microcosm in which Čapek stages his satire of technology, religion, and human folly.

## Location

Bohemia occupies the western two-thirds of the Czech Republic, bordered by Moravia to the east, Austria to the south, Germany to the west, and Poland to the north. Its geography — the Vltava and Elbe river systems, the Central Bohemian uplands, and the ring of border mountains — provides the varied landscape through which the novel's characters move.

## Significance in the Novel

### The Setting of Industrial Transformation

The M.E.C.'s Karburator revolution begins and is most intensively felt in Bohemia. The company's headquarters, factories, and testing facilities — from the laboratory in Břevnov to the Central Power Station at Stvanice to the provincial deployments in Hradec Králové and Slaný — map the geography of Bohemian industrialization. The Chairman's report lists deployments across Bohemian towns: the Sázava, the Upper Vltava, Brno, all within or bordering the region.

### Spiritual Movements

Bohemia's religious history — particularly the Hussite tradition and the Bohemian Brethren — echoes through the novel's spiritual upheavals. Dr. Hubka's joining of "the Bohemian Brethren" is one of many references to the region's distinct religious identity. The grassroots spiritual movements that emerge (Kuzenda's dredge community, Binder's merry-go-round, the various miraculous outbreaks) all take place on Bohemian soil, suggesting a landscape particularly receptive to — or vulnerable to — divine manifestations.

### The Greatest War

In the novel's apocalyptic conclusion, Bohemia becomes a battleground. Hradec Králové, "mindful of its historical continuity," survives successive invasions — Prussians, Bobinets, Turks, and Chinese — sustained by its proud consciousness of having "the greatest battle in Eastern Bohemia" in its history. The Bohemian landscape bears the scars of the global conflict fought with Karburator weapons.

## Thematic Resonance

Bohemia's role in the novel is simultaneously specific and universal. Čapek's choice to set the story in his homeland — naming real towns, rivers, and districts — grounds the fantastical premise in recognizable geography. Yet Bohemia also functions as a stand-in for any modern nation confronted with forces beyond its comprehension or control. The progression from technological optimism to spiritual chaos to military devastation unfolds on Bohemian soil, but the novel's warning is addressed to all of civilization.
