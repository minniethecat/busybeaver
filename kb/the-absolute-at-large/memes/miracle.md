# Miracle

**Miracles** in Čapek's *The Absolute at Large* are natural byproducts of atomic combustion — when matter is completely disintegrated by the Karburator, the divine Absolute trapped within is released, producing phenomena that are "contrary to nature and downright miraculous".

## Mechanism

Engineer Marek explains that all matter contains the Absolute in a state of confinement. When the Karburator destroys matter completely, it leaves behind "pure God" — an immaterial residue that manifests with "monstrous energy":

> "Being immaterial, it is not subject to the laws of matter. Thence it already follows that its manifestations are contrary to nature and downright miraculous."

Marek draws a parallel between his mechanical process and traditional miracle-workers: "Christ and the miracle-workers, fakirs, mediums, and prophets have achieved it by means of their psychic power. My Karburator does it by a purely mechanical process. It acts, you might say, as a factory for the Absolute".

## Marek's Personal Experiences

Marek himself experienced miracle-working after building the Karburator. He prophesied events, regrew a torn nail instantly, and even levitated:

> "I had moments of astounding illumination. I saw, for instance, quite clearly, things that would happen in the future. I predicted even your visit here. And once I tore my nail off on a lathe. I looked at the damaged finger, and all at once a new nail grew on it. Very likely I'd formed the wish, but all the same it's queer and terrible. Another time — just think of it — I rose right up into the air. It's called levitation, you know."

His porter also began performing faith healings after exposure to the device: "He got religion. He was inspired. He gave religious addresses and performed miracles. His wife uttered prophecies." Marek noted that the porter had been "a thoroughly hardheaded chap, a monist and a freethinker" — the transformation was dramatic.

Marek also had visions of "gigantic, swampy primeval forests, overgrown with mosses and inhabited by weird monsters," which he speculated might be due to the Karburator burning Upper Silesian coal of the oldest geological formation: "Possibly the God of the Carboniferous Age is in it".

## The Church's Intervention

The Catholic Church moved to co-opt Marek's miracles. A Bishop visited Břevnov and proposed placing the Absolute under the Church's patronage, declaring the premises a "miraculous shrine and place of pilgrimage" — with conditions. The Absolute's production should be limited to "the smallest possible quantity" producing only "weak, almost innocuous, very much diluted Absolute, whose manifestations would be less uncontrollable and more irregular, rather as at Lourdes".

The Bishop further stipulated that the Karburator should only burn coal from Male Svantovice, home to a shrine of the Virgin, so that the Břevnov site might become "a centre for the worship of Our Lady". This cynical negotiation — the Church attempting to regulate divine manifestations for institutional benefit — is among Čapek's sharpest satirical passages.

## The Kuzenda Dredge Miracles

The most fully realized miracle community forms around Kuzenda's dredge on the Vltava. When a baker with chronic asthma visited, Kuzenda "simply went like this with his hand, and then you said that you could breathe easily" — a spontaneous, unselfconscious healing.

Kuzenda described the coffee multiplication miracle:

> "One Sunday recently, there were several hundred people here, even sitting on both banks of the river, and behold, our coffee multiplied itself so that there was enough for everybody — and what splendid coffee it was!"

But he insisted that physical wonders were secondary: "The greatest miracle is the influence He has on our feelings. It is so intensely beautiful that it fairly makes one shiver. Sometimes you feel as if you could die of love and happiness".

## The Official Narrative

The establishment struggled to manage the miracle epidemic. A government press statement attempted to normalize the phenomenon: "Every citizen has the right to fall to the earth and worship God, and the performance of miracles is not in conflict with any official position whatever in a democratic State". The statement blamed incidents on "insufficient ventilation, combined with excessive strain upon the nerves."

## Scientific Rationalization

Dr. Blahous, a scholar of comparative religion, attempted to categorize the miracles as "barbaric atavism" — "old and well-known devices of trickery and suggestion," recommending police attention and psychoanalysis. He attributed the phenomena to "pathological cases in degenerate hysterical subjects" and "a collective psychical epidemic among the superstitious and mentally inferior masses". His analysis, which reduces the genuinely supernatural to psychological pathology, represents the scientific establishment's desperate attempt to explain away what it cannot comprehend.

## Wartime Manifestations

During the Greatest War, miracles became battlefield phenomena, with invisible forces intervening in combat — seizing aircraft, deflecting projectiles, and creating cyclones around Karburator sites.
