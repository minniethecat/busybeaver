## Summary

The deification of the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] was the culminating religious ceremony held in [[the-catholic-church|Rome]] on the twelfth of December, at which the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] was formally declared a [[the-absolute|God]] by the Catholic Church. Following an expedited canonization process overseen by [[cardinal-varesi|Cardinal Varesi]] as Procurator Dei and Monsignor Cullatti as Advocatus Diaboli, the ceremony at St. Peter's Basilica drew seven thousand priests and resulted in the deaths of twelve hundred spectators crushed in the crowd. The Pope, in the presence of a twelve-ton [[the-karburator|Karburator]] gifted by the [[m-e-c|M.E.C.]], intoned "*In nomine Dei Deus*" and the Church proclaimed to the world "*Habemus Deum*" — "We have a [[the-absolute|God]]."

## Context

The deification ceremony was the endpoint of a rapid ecclesiastical process that began with the admission of the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] into the Church by Papal Brief. The College of Cardinals dispensed with the usual requirement of baptism, establishing instead the new doctrine of "baptism through works of piety, meritoriousness, and virtue" (019-002). Shortly thereafter, the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] was summarily beatified under the rule *super cultu immemorabili*, and an expedited canonization process commenced.

> You will readily understand that the admission of the Absolute to the bosom of the Church afforded, under the given circumstances, a great surprise. It was carried out by virtue of the Papal Brief alone, and the College of Cardinals, being faced with the accomplished fact, merely deliberated whether the sacrament of baptism should be conferred upon the Absolute. (019-001)

The process included a critical theological innovation: the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] was to be declared not merely a Saint, but a [[the-absolute|God]]. A special Deification Commission was appointed from among the Church's most distinguished scholars and pastors. [[cardinal-varesi|Cardinal Varesi]], Archbishop of Venice, was named Procurator Dei, while Monsignor Cullatti served as Advocatus Diaboli — the traditional "Devil's Advocate" role inverted to argue for deification.

> Almost simultaneously the summary beatification of the Absolute was enacted under the rule super cultu immemorabili in recognition of the virtuous life of the Absolute, now declared Blessed, and a regular but expedited process of canonization was arranged for. There was, however, one highly important innovation: the Absolute was to be declared, not a Saint, but a God. (019-003)

[[cardinal-varesi|Cardinal Varesi]]'s presentation of evidence was exhaustive. He submitted seventeen thousand documented testimonies of miracles, each bearing expert reports from medical faculties, professors of natural sciences, technicians, and economists, alongside eyewitness signatures and legal authentications (019-004). He stated that these represented "but an insignificant fraction" of the miracles performed, estimated to have already exceeded thirty million.

> Cardinal Varesi presented seventeen thousand testimonies to miracles performed, signed by nearly all the cardinals, patriarchs, primates, metropolitans, princes of the Church, archbishops, principals of Orders, and abbots. (019-004)

Supporting the deification were scientific luminaries whose opinions were published in the Acts of [[the-catholic-church|the Holy See]]. Professor Gardien of the Sorbonne acknowledged that the miraculous cures — including "death following on complete separation of the head from the trunk" and "strangulation in a subject hanged two days before" — could only be explained by "the interference of higher powers" (019-005). Professor Meadow of Glasgow cited the [[the-absolute|Absolute]]'s psychic operations as corroboration of his critique of psychophysical parallelism (019-006). Professor Lupen of Brno declared the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] "a force deserving of the highest respect" (019-007), and chemist Willibald of Tubingen found the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] in "admirable conformity with Einstein's theory of Relativity" (019-008).

## Aftermath

The deification ceremony had immediate and far-reaching consequences:

**Theological schism**: Before the ceremony took place, the Patriarch of Constantinople declared the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] to be the First Person of the Trinity (the Creator), contradicting the Roman identification with the Third Person. This heretical position was adopted by Old Catholics, Abyssinian Christians, Evangelicals of the Helvetian Confession, Nonconformists, and several American sects (019-010).

**Competing claims**: The Basle Congress of the Free Thought Society proclaimed the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] as the [[the-absolute|God]] of the Freethinkers, attacking all denominations for trying to monopolize the new divinity (019-011). Meanwhile, the German Monist Association laid the foundation stone for a Cathedral of the Atomic [[the-absolute|God]] in Leipzig (019-012). Among Jews, a secret doctrine identified the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] as Baal (019-010). In the Belgian Congo and French Senegambia, indigenous peoples slaughtered missionaries and worshipped atomic motors as the idol Ato or Alolto (019-013).

**The ceremony itself**: Twelve hundred of the faithful and spectators were crushed to death during the five-hour ceremony. The Pope intoned "*In nomine Dei Deus*" ("In the name of [[the-absolute|God]], [[the-absolute|God]]"), and at that moment all the bells of Catholic churches worldwide rang out as the Church proclaimed: "*Habemus Deum*" — "We have a [[the-absolute|God]]" (019-014).

> Seven thousand priests with lighted candles escorted the Holy Father to St. Peter's, where the largest twelve-ton Karburator, a gift from the M.E.C. to the Holy See, had been erected behind the high altar. (019-014)

## Narrative Significance

The deification ceremony represents the theological climax of the [[the-absolute|Absolute]]'s integration into organized religion and serves as a devastating satire of institutionalized faith. Čapek portrays the Church's machinery of canonization — complete with seventeen thousand documented miracles, scientific expert opinions, and intricate theological debate — as simultaneously absurd and inevitable. The fact that the [[the-absolute|Absolute]], an industrial byproduct, is declared [[the-absolute|God]] with full ecclesiastical pomp exposes the blurring of religious devotion and commercial interest (the [[m-e-c|M.E.C.]]'s twelve-ton [[the-karburator|Karburator]] sits behind the high altar as both sacred object and corporate product placement).

The competing theological claims to the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] — as First or Third Person of the Trinity, as Baal, as the Atomic [[the-absolute|God]], as the Freethinkers' [[the-absolute|God]] — dramatize the novel's central insight: that humanity inevitably projects its own doctrinal frameworks onto the ineffable. The deification ceremony's tragic cost (twelve hundred crushed to death) underscores Čapek's warning that organized religion, like nationalism and industrial capitalism, can become a destructive force when catalyzed by the [[the-absolute|Absolute]]'s intoxicating presence.
