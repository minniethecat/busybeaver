## Overview

*The People's Friend* (*Lidový přítel*) is described as "the largest Catholic or popular newspaper" in the world of *[[the-absolute|The Absolute]] at Large* [(018-002)]. It occupies a strategic position in the novel's satire of religious journalism: its editorial office is the stage for one of the book's most extended comic set-pieces, a scene that anatomizes the machinery of ecclesiastical press manipulation at the height of the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] crisis.

The newspaper appears in Chapter XVIII ("In the Night Editor's Room"), where its small editorial staff — [[father-jost|Father Jost]] as leader-writer, Kostal as night editor, and Novotny the printer — scramble to produce polemical copy denouncing the new religious movements, only to have their efforts overturned by the sudden arrival of [[bishop-linda|Bishop Linda]].

## Editorial Staff and Operations

The paper operates with a conspicuously modest staff:

- **[[father-jost|Father Jost]]** serves as leader-writer, composing polemics against the "knavish imposture" of rival sects while whistling between his teeth [(018-002)]. His method consists of recycling a limited stock of rhetorical formulas — "satanic machinations," "treacherous onslaught," "perverse malignity" — that he and the printer have been rotating for days [(018-005-018)].

- **Kostal** functions as night editor, a pipe-smoker of legendary pungency [(018-002)].

- **Novotny** is the printer, who not only sets type but supplies [[father-jost|Father Jost]] with fresh invective drawn "from the old files of *The People's Friend*" [(018-020)]. His suggestion of "criminal narrow-mindedness or perverse malignity" is received by [[father-jost|Jost]] with a "sigh of relief" [(018-018-019)].

The editorial process is systematically hollow: the leader-writer has exhausted his vocabulary, the printer feeds him back the paper's own archive of clichés, and no one — least of all [[father-jost|Jost]] — seems to have any substantive position to advance. The machinery produces polemic without content.

## Bishop Linda's Intervention

The newspaper's nominal independence collapses with the entrance of [[bishop-linda|Bishop Linda]], who demands the evening's leader and excoriates [[father-jost|Jost]] for the morning's issue. The Bishop's red-faced fury reveals the actual editorial hierarchy: "You've no right to think," he roars at [[father-jost|Jost]]. "Why didn't you telephone? Why didn't you ask what you were to write?" [(018-026)]

His declaration — "Nobody has to do anything but what I want" [(018-030)] — strips away any pretense of journalistic autonomy. *The People's Friend* is an instrument of episcopal will, and the leader-writer's sole function is to execute, not to think. The scene is Čapek's most concentrated satire of how institutional religion commands the press: not through argument but through raw authority, reducing the newspaper to a mouthpiece for the hierarchy.

## Key Passages

> [(018-002)] The largest Catholic or popular newspaper, *The People's Friend*, had not a very large editorial staff, and so at 9:30 p.m. there were only two men in the room — Kostal, the night editor (Heaven knows why night editors' pipes have such an amazing stink), and Father Jost, who sat writing the leader for the next issue and whistling between his teeth.

> [(018-020)] From the old files of *The People's Friend*. But that leader, your Reverence.

> [(018-026)] You've no right to think. Here, take the thing; and crumpling up that morning's issue of *The People's Friend* in his hand, he flung it at Jost's feet. I thought! Look at him, he thinks! Why didn't you telephone? Why didn't you ask what you were to write?

## Significance

*The People's Friend* functions as a microcosm of Čapek's satire on institutional communication. The newspaper — the Catholic Church's public voice — operates on a closed loop of recycled rhetoric, its content determined not by events or principles but by episcopal fiat. The scene anticipates the novel's broader theme: that the media, like religion and politics, is vulnerable to capture by the [[the-absolute|Absolute]]'s totalizing force. That the paper's name invokes "friendship" only underscores the irony — there is no friend here, only the chain of command.
