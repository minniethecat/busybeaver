## Summary

The Munich Popular Assembly was a mass political gathering held at 7 p.m. during the Bavarian religious uprisings of the Greatest War. Amid "the wildest enthusiasm," the Assembly passed a resolution for the secession of the Southern States from the German Empire. The Munich Government immediately wired Berlin that it was taking over the administration. In response, the Imperial Chancellor ordered ten thousand bayonets to Bavaria, and the troop trains were blown up at the Bavarian frontier — an act of sabotage that escalated the crisis into a full-scale Holy War.

## Context

The Munich Popular Assembly was one link in a chain of escalating religious-political violence that characterized the early phase of the Greatest War. The immediate trigger was a sectarian conflict in Augsburg, where Catholic workers at the Scholler lead-pencil factory beat a Protestant foreman over a dispute "relating to the worship of the Virgin" (025-011). The situation rapidly spiralled:

> Scholler, the owner of the factory, was killed, and two directors were stabbed. The clergy were compelled by force to carry the monstrance at the head of the procession. The Archbishop, Dr. Lenz, who came out to pacify the demonstrators, was thrown into the River Lech. (025-011)

The Social-Democratic leaders attempting to speak were forced into the synagogue, which was then blown up with dynamite at 3 p.m. While the looting of Jewish and Protestant shops continued, the Augsburg City Council "carried unanimously the Immaculate Conception of the Virgin Mary, and issued an impassioned appeal to the Catholic nations of the world to take up the sword in defence of the Holy Catholic Faith" (025-011).

This was the atmosphere in which the Munich Popular Assembly convened.

## Proceedings

The Munich Popular Assembly was not a deliberative body but a ratification of mob sentiment. The news from Augsburg — the murdered factory owner, the Archbishop thrown into the Lech, the synagogue dynamited, the City Council's appeal for a Catholic holy war — had primed the Bavarian population for radical action. "Upon the receipt of this news various manifestos were issued in other Bavarian cities" (025-011), and the Munich Assembly was the largest and most consequential.

The resolution for secession passed "amid the wildest enthusiasm" — a phrase that encapsulates the Absolutized crowd's susceptibility to collective fervour. There was no debate, no consideration of consequences, only the emotional release of declaring independence from Berlin.

## Aftermath

The chain of escalation that followed the Munich Assembly was rapid and catastrophic:

1. **Immediate**: The Munich Government wired Berlin that it was taking over administration — effectively a declaration of secession (025-011).

2. **Within hours**: Imperial Chancellor [[dr-wurm|Dr. Wurm]] called on the Minister of War, who ordered ten thousand bayonets to Bavaria from Saxony and the Rhineland garrisons (025-011).

3. **1 a.m.**: The troop trains were blown up at the Bavarian frontier — "machine-guns were turned upon the wounded" (025-011).

4. **3 a.m.**: The Munich Government, in alliance with the Alpine provinces, "decided to proclaim a Holy War on the Lutherans" (025-011).

The Munich Assembly thus transformed a local sectarian dispute in Augsburg into a war between Catholic Bavaria and the Protestant German Empire, with the [[the-absolute|Absolute]]'s influence ensuring that every escalation was met with an even more extreme response.

## Narrative Significance

The Munich Popular Assembly illustrates Čapek's theory of how political violence escalates under the [[the-absolute|Absolute]]'s influence. The progression — factory dispute → mob violence → municipal resolution → mass assembly → secession → military mobilization → sabotage → holy war — is compressed into a single paragraph of breathless, paratactic prose. Each step follows with accelerating momentum, as if the events are driven not by human agency but by the chemical reaction of the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] on collective psychology.

The Assembly itself is notably devoid of any named individual. Unlike [[emperor-bobinet|Bobinet]]'s coup or [[general-hampl|Hampl]]'s declaration — both driven by specific personalities — the Munich Assembly is an anonymous mass event. This anonymity underscores the novel's argument that the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] does not require charismatic leaders to produce catastrophe; it can work directly through the crowd. The "wildest enthusiasm" that carried the secession resolution is the same force that filled the Augsburg mob with murderous zeal and would soon fill Europe with the Greatest War.

The episode also functions as a structural pivot in the novel's timeline. It marks the transition from the religious-renewal phase of the [[the-absolute|Absolute]]'s influence (characterized by miracles, conversions, and theological ferment) to the military-political phase (characterized by war, nationalism, and state collapse). The Munich Assembly's secession resolution is the moment when spiritual fervour becomes territorial ambition.
