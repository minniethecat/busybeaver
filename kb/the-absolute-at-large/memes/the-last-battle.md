## Summary

The Last Battle, recounted in Chapter XXIX of *[[the-absolute|The Absolute at Large]]*, is the novel's satirical conclusion to [[the-greatest-war|the Greatest War]] — the global religious conflict that consumed civilization after the [[the-absolute|Absolute]]'s release from Marek's [[invention-of-the-karburator|Karburator]]. Rather than a heroic climax, Čapek delivers an anti-climax of exquisite absurdity: no historian can agree on which engagement was the last, the only reliable evidence comes from a body of over two hundred prophetic documents, and the war's final act consists of thirteen exhausted soldiers — the sole survivors of a conflict that consumed 198,000,000 men (025-003) — deciding, almost wordlessly, to stop fighting and go home (029-027).

## Context

By the autumn of 1953, the Greatest War has burned itself out. The war that began on February 12, 1944 had consumed 198,000,000 combatants — all but thirteen of whom perished (025-003). The world's armies have dissolved into nothing. The chronicler describes a landscape of ruins where "self-appointed generals" march from town to town commanding five men each — the war has become a hollow parody of military organization (029-002).

> (029-002) In the autumn of 1953 the Greatest War was drawing to a close. There were no armies left. The armies of occupation, cut off for the most part from their homes, were dwindling away and gradually vanishing like water in the sand. Self-appointed generals marched from town to town, or rather from heap to heap of ruins, at the head of five men, one a drummer, one a thief, one a schoolboy, one a man with a gramophone, and one of whom nobody knew anything.

> (029-003) Amid this universal and indescribable collapse the Greatest War drew to its close. The end came so unexpectedly that no one nowadays can tell just where the last so-called decisive battle was fought.

The chronicler catalogues the competing claims of military historians with deadpan mockery: Dührich, Assbridge, and Moroni favour the Battle of Lintz — a tavern brawl over the waitress Hilda at the Rose Inn, involving sixty soldiers from eleven hostile camps, won by an Italian named Giuseppe who promptly lost Hilda the next day to a Czech named Vaclav Hruska (029-003). Usinski favours a battle at Gorochovky, Leblond a skirmish at Les Batignolles, and Van Goo a fight near Nieuport (029-004). But the chronicler dismisses these as local patriotism rather than history — none of these engagements was, in any meaningful sense, the last battle of the world war (029-004).

## Key Participants

The thirteen soldiers are deliberately unnamed and uncharacterized — they are every soldier and no soldier, the irreducible human remainder of an apocalyptic conflict. Their anonymity is central to the chapter's meaning: Čapek strips away nationality, rank, ideology, and even the cause for which they fought. What remains is pure, universal humanity.

**The Thirteen Soldiers**

The thirteen represent the last survivors of all the warring armies. They have no commanders, no flags, and no stated nationality. Their dialogue — a cascade of ordinary human longings — constitutes their sole characterization:

- **The first soldier**: The initiator. History will never learn his name. He speaks the line that ends the war: "Oh, damn it, boys, let's chuck it!" (029-014)
- **The second soldier**: The first to agree, laying aside his weapon with relief (029-015)
- **The third soldier**: Hungry — asks for bacon "with a certain gentleness" (029-016)
- **The fourth soldier**: Craves a smoke (029-017)
- **The fifth soldier**: Pragmatic — "We're not going to have any more of it." (029-018)
- **The sixth soldier**: Willing to trade a cigarette for bread (029-019)
- **The seventh soldier**: The emotional heart — "We're going home, boys... think of it... home" (029-020)
- **The eighth soldier**: The wry realist — "Is your old woman expecting you?" (029-021)
- **The ninth soldier**: The exhausted one — hasn't slept in a proper bed in six years (029-022)
- **The tenth soldier**: The disgusted veteran — "What a mug's game it was, lads!" (029-023)
- **The eleventh soldier**: Conclusive — "but we've done with it now" (029-024)
- **The twelfth soldier**: Echoing — "We've done with it. We're not such fools. Let's go home, mates!" (029-025)
- **The thirteenth soldier**: The final voice, turning over to lie on his other side — "Oh, but I'm glad it's all over" (029-026)

Their exchange has no military content whatsoever. No one mentions the Absolute, the Karburator, G.H. Bondy, or any of the causes that propelled the war. The war collapses into a handful of modest appetites — food, tobacco, sleep, home. The thirteen are less characters in a narrative than a demonstration of what humanity looks like when ideology evaporates.

## The Prophecies

Unable to establish the historical facts, the chronicler turns to an alternative source: a corpus of over two hundred prophetic documents from the century preceding the war (029-004). In a brilliant satirical move, Čapek elevates prophecy above military history as the more reliable witness — cranks and mystics, it turns out, have more to tell us about the war's end than generals and historians.

| Prophet/Source | Year | Key Detail |
|---------------|------|-----------|
| Swabian prophecy | 1845 | "Thirteen nations would meet in the field *under a birch-tree*" (029-005) |
| Wali Schön (Turkish prophetess) | 1893 | "Thirteen emperors would make war upon each other... *under a birch-tree*" (029-006) |
| Negress of Massachusetts | 1909 | A vision of horned monsters with "thirteen" total horns fighting *under a tree* (029-007) |
| Very Rev. Dr. Arnold | 1920 | "The last battle of that war would be the last battle of the century" (029-008) |
| The Vision of Jonathan (Stockholm) | 1920 | "The last battle will last nine-and-ninety hours... all the victors will be able to find room in the shade of one *birch-tree*" (029-009) |
| German popular prophecy | 1923 | Battle on the Birkenfeld (Birch field) (029-010) |

The recurring motifs — the number thirteen and the birch tree — become the only verifiable facts about the war's end. Who took part, who won, and even where it happened remain unknown (029-011).

> (029-011) More than two hundred similar prophetic documents of the period between 1845 and 1944 have been preserved. In forty-eight of these the number "thirteen" occurs; in seventy of them the "birch-tree" appears; in fifteen merely the "tree." It may therefore be concluded that the last battle took place in the neighbourhood of a birch-tree.

The chronicler then performs a mock-scholarly narrowing of the search by cataloguing European place-names containing the word "birch" — one hundred and seven Czech place-names alone (Brezany, Brezovice, Brezolupy), plus German variants (Birkenberg, Birkenfeld, Birkenhaid, Birkenhammer, Birkicht, Birkental), English (Birkenhead, Birchington, Birchanger), and French (Boulainvilliers, Boulay) — concluding that the site is narrowed down to "a few thousand" localities (029-012). The joke lies in the collision of prophetic vision with pedantic philology: even armed with supernatural foreknowledge, we cannot do better than "somewhere in Europe, probably near a birch tree."

### Satirical Function of the Prophecies

The prophecy section operates on multiple satirical levels. First, it inverts the hierarchy of evidence: the supernatural and the irrational (prophecy) prove more reliable than the rational and the empirical (military history). Second, it exposes the vacuity of historical scholarship — the best historians can offer is a catalogue of inconclusive tavern brawls, while the ravings of mystics yield genuine patterns. Third, the prophecies themselves undercut any sense of the war's grandeur: they converge not on a heroic narrative of victory and defeat, but on a single, almost banal image — a birch tree and the number thirteen. The war that was supposed to reshape civilization leaves no trace more meaningful than this.

## The Final Scene

The chapter's closing passage is among the most memorable in the novel. Abandoning the mock-academic tone, the chronicler imagines the moment the war actually ends — not with a treaty or a victory, but with exhaustion, a hot October afternoon, and the simple desire for rest (029-013).

> (029-013) But perhaps after all — the fancy is alluring — there did stand near the scene of the last act of the world-tragedy a slender silvery birch. Perhaps a lark sang above the battlefield and a white butterfly fluttered over the heads of the combatants. And look, by this time there is hardly anyone left to kill! It is a hot October day, and one hero after another steps aside, turns his back upon the battlefield, eases himself, and lies down longing for peace in the shadow of the birch-tree. At last the whole thirteen of them are lying there, all the survivors of the Last Battle.

The details are carefully chosen for their anti-heroic quality. The soldier "eases himself" before lying down — Čapek inserts a bodily function into the grandiose frame of the "world-tragedy." The lark and the white butterfly, staples of pastoral poetry, intrude upon the battlefield. The thirteen sleep with their heads on each other's boots and backs, undisturbed by breathing (029-013). The war does not end in fire or triumph; it ends in a nap.

When the thirteen awake, they look at each other with suspicion and reach for their weapons (029-014). Then one — "history will never learn his name" — speaks:

> (029-014) "Oh, damn it, boys, let's chuck it!"

What follows is a cascade of ordinary longings. The second man lays aside his weapon with relief (029-015). The third asks for bacon (029-016). The fourth wants a smoke (029-017). The seventh cries, "We're going home, boys... think of it... home" (029-020). The ninth sighs, "My [[the-absolute|God]], it's six years since I slept in a proper bed" (029-022). The tenth declares, "What a mug's game it was, lads!" (029-023). And the thirteenth, turning over to lie on his other side, concludes, "Oh, but I'm glad it's all over" (029-026).

> (029-027) And such, one can well imagine, was the end of the Greatest War.

## Aftermath

The Last Battle has no military aftermath — by definition, it is the moment the war stops. But its narrative function is essential: it closes the arc of the Greatest War that began with [[the-battle-of-hradec-kralove|the Battle of Hradec Králové]] and provides the satirical thesis of the entire novel in miniature.

The war that began with cosmic ambition and religious fervour — with the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] transforming every combatant into a divine instrument, with [[emperor-bobinet|Emperor Bobinet]]'s messianic conquests, with [[general-hampl|General Hampl]]'s declaration of perpetual crusade — ends with thirteen tired men wanting bacon, cigarettes, and their own beds. The Absolute's promise of universal transcendence, of every soul becoming God, is answered by the most humble and human of desires (029-022) (029-023).

The chapter's structural position is significant: it is the penultimate chapter of the novel (XXIX of XXX). After the war's dissolution, only "The End of Everything" (Chapter XXX) remains — a brief coda in which the narrator, sitting in a quiet inn, reflects that he can hear nothing but the ticking of the clock and the scratching of his own pen. The thirteen soldiers lying under the birch tree are, in effect, the last living men in the world.

## Narrative Significance

The Last Battle chapter is Čapek's most concentrated statement on the nature of war and history. By displacing military narrative with prophecy (029-004) and then prophecy with an imagined scene of exhausted men (029-013), Čapek dismantles every convention of war literature. There is no strategy, no heroism, no meaning — only fatigue, hunger, and the desire for home.

The chapter's structural joke is that historians cannot tell us how the war ended, but prophets — dismissed as cranks and mystics — can (029-004). And what the prophets reveal is not divine purpose but the irreducible humanity of the combatants. The thirteen are all that remains when ideology is stripped away, and what they want has nothing to do with nations, gods, or causes.

The chapter also serves as a counterweight to the mock-grandeur of [[the-battle-of-hradec-kralove|the Battle of Hradec Králové]]. Where the earlier chapter compresses a world war into the frame of a single Bohemian town, the Last Battle dissolves the war entirely into the shade of a single tree and the desires of thirteen men (029-013). Together, the two chapters form Čapek's definitive anti-war statement — not a plea for peace, but a demonstration that war, when examined without the lens of patriotic narrative, is inherently absurd.

The closing line — "And such, one can well imagine, was the end of the Greatest War" (029-027) — is the novel's crowning irony. The qualifying phrase "one can well imagine" acknowledges that the scene is not history but imagination, and yet it is truer than any historical account could be. In Čapek's world, the imaginative truth of a birch tree and thirteen tired men carries more weight than the documentary truth of dates, place-names, and casualty figures. The war's end cannot be verified, but it can be imagined — and imagination, in the end, is the only reliable witness.
