In Karel Capek's satirical science fiction novel *The Absolute at Large* (1922), **the Absolute** is the divine essence that pervades all matter — God imprisoned in every atom. When the [[the-karburator|Karburator]], invented by engineer [[rudy-marek|R. Marek]], achieves complete atomic combustion, it releases the Absolute as an industrial byproduct. As Marek explains: "all matter contains the Absolute in some state of confinement … when you utterly destroy a piece of matter … you're left with an indestructible residue — free and active Absolute" (003-041). The resulting cascade of religious ecstasy, mass conversion, and global conflict forms the novel's satirical engine.

Capek's conceit literalizes the theological doctrine of divine omnipresence into a technological problem: God becomes a factory output, a pollutant, and ultimately a weapon. The novel traces the Absolute's trajectory from laboratory curiosity to world-historical catastrophe, satirizing along the way the institutions of religion, capitalism, science, and nationalism.

## Definition

The Absolute is defined in the novel as a pantheistic substance — the divine essence that is present in all matter and released when matter is completely destroyed. Marek's scientific-theological hypothesis builds on Spinoza and Fechner:

> (003-041) "We're assuming that all matter contains the Absolute in some state of confinement. We can call it a latent imprisoned force, or simply say that as God is omnipresent He is therefore present in all matter and in every particle of matter. And now suppose you utterly destroy a piece of matter, apparently leaving not the slightest residue. Then, since all matter is really Matter plus Absolute, what you have destroyed is *only* the matter, and you're left with an indestructible residue — free and active Absolute."

The Absolute is characterized as "the chemically unanalysable, immaterial residue, which shows no spectrum lines, neither atomic weight nor chemical affinity, no obedience to Boyle's law, none, none whatever, of the properties of matter" (003-041). Being immaterial, "it is not subject to the laws of matter" and its manifestations are therefore "contrary to nature and downright miraculous" (003-041).

## In the Novel

### Discovery

The Absolute enters the novel as an unintended consequence. R. Marek's Perfect Karburator, designed to solve the energy crisis through total atomic combustion, produces an unexpected byproduct:

> (004-004) Marek: "As a result of its complete disintegration of matter, my Perfect Karburator manufactures a byproduct: pure and unconfined Absolute, God in a chemically pure form. At one end, so to speak, it emits mechanical power, and at the other, the divine principle."

G. H. Bondy, visiting Marek's laboratory, experiences the Absolute's effects firsthand when he descends into the cellar:

> (002-047)–(002-048) Bondy went down the cellar steps … And there in the middle of the arched concrete cellar, clean as a monastery cell, lay a gigantic copper cylinder resting on cement supports. … G. H. Bondy fell on his knees, lost in a bewildering, shining ecstasy. He felt as if he must shout and sing, he seemed to hear about him the rustle of unceasing and innumerable wings.

This ecstatic kneeling — the hard-headed industrialist brought to his knees by a machine's exhaust — establishes the novel's fundamental irony: the Absolute does not discriminate between believer and skeptic.

### Proliferation

Once Bondy commercializes the Karburator, the Absolute spreads through industrial society like a contaminant. The cellar in Brevnov becomes so saturated that anyone entering experiences illumination:

> (004-012) Marek: "In a single day the cellar was chock-full of the Absolute, ready to burst with it; and it began to spread all over the house. The pure Absolute penetrates all matter, you know, but it takes a little longer with solid substances."

The Absolute proves impossible to contain. Marek's desperate experiments with isolating materials fail utterly:

> (004-016) Marek: "I've tried every possible isolating material that might prevent the Absolute from getting out of the cellar. Ashes, sand, metal walls, nothing can keep it back. I've even tried covering the cellar with the works of Professor Krejci, Spencer, Haeckel, and all the Positivists you can think of: would you believe it, the Absolute goes calmly through even that stuff!"

### Crisis

As Karburators multiply worldwide, the Absolute's effects escalate from individual ecstasy to social disruption. Bondy observes the mounting chaos:

> (010-003) Bondy: "There are several thousand Karburators at work in the world already, every one of them pouring forth the Absolute day and night. And this Absolute is fiendishly clever, too. It has an insane desire to exert itself, no matter how."

The Absolute begins taking over industrial operations:

> (010-005) Bondy: "The Absolute wants work. It clings furiously to life. Once it created the earth; now it has flung itself into manufacture. … It keeps the bank's books all on its own, does the accounts, carries on the correspondence. It gives orders to the Board of Directors in writing. It sends its clients fervent epistles about showing love by works."

Bondy's cynical response — "We'll flood the Germans and the French with them; then we'll bombard England with the Absolute" (010-006) — transforms the theological byproduct into a weapon of economic warfare, satirizing the fusion of nationalist ambition with industrial capability.

### Resolution

The Absolute's proliferation culminates in the so-called [[the-greatest-war|Greatest War]] (Chapter XXV), a global religious conflict fought with divine conviction rather than conventional weapons. The novel's resolution (Chapter XXX, "The End of Everything") sees the Absolute consume itself in its own excess, leaving humanity to reckon with the devastation.

## Interpretations

Capek deploys the Absolute as a multi-layered satirical device:

1. **The Commodification of Religion**: Marek's invention makes God a manufactured product. Bondy's business logic — "I'll give you three percent of the gross profits" (004-031) — reduces the divine to a commercial licensing agreement. The Church, through Bishop Linda, attempts to negotiate a franchise arrangement: "a miraculous shrine and place of pilgrimage — with certain conditions" (005-050).

2. **Religious Absolutism**: Once the Absolute is loosed, each person experiences it through their own doctrinal lens, leading to sectarian fragmentation. The novel's religious communities — Kuzendists, Binderians, Machat's Humble of Heart — parody denominational schism. As Bishop Linda warns: "The Church merely confines Him and controls Him. And you two unbelievers are loosing Him upon the earth like a flood" (005-064).

3. **The Limits of Scientific Rationalism**: Capek satirizes the positivistic confidence that science can explain everything. Dr. Blahous's academic article attempts to reduce the Absolute to "barbaric atavism" and "primitive forms of worship" (012-007), only to be converted mid-composition, writing: "Give yourself up with your whole beings to the Absolute" (012-013).

4. **Human Nature**: The novel asks whether humanity can bear unmediated access to the divine. The Absolute reveals people's latent religious impulses, but these impulses prove incompatible with modern civilization. As Bondy tells Ellen: "neither business nor society is possible without thoughts that are not disclosed" (010-028) — the Absolute's mind-reading property makes social life impossible.

|-----------------|-----------|---------|
| Industrial Capitalism | God as factory byproduct | M.E.C. mass-produces Karburators |
| Organized Religion | Church negotiates franchise rights | Bishop Linda's "conditions" |
| Academic Scientism | Positivist framework collapses | Dr. Blahous's conversion |
| Nationalism | God as economic weapon | Bondy's export strategy |
| Human Vanity | Absolute reveals hidden thoughts | Ellen's clairvoyance |
