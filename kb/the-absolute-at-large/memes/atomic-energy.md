# Atomic Energy

**Atomic energy** is the revolutionary scientific principle at the heart of Čapek's novel — the complete utilization of energy contained within atoms through total combustion of matter, a technology that promises unlimited power but releases an unintended divine byproduct.

## The Principle

Engineer Marek describes his invention to G.H. Bondy as "a bigger revolution in technical methods than Watt's invention of the steam-engine" — it provides for the "complete utilization of atomic energy". Modern science, Marek explains, teaches that all matter is composed of vast numbers of energy units; an atom is a collection of electrons — the tiniest particles of electricity.

The key insight is that conventional combustion is wasteful and incomplete. In combustion, heat is produced but the matter itself is merely transformed, not fully consumed. Marek's Karburator achieves atomic disintegration at a level that uses all the energy contained within matter:

> "If we used up the whole of the atomic energy, we should use up the whole of the atoms. In short, *the matter would vanish altogether*."

This process — whether called utilization of atomic energy, complete combustion, or disintegration of matter — all describes the same phenomenon.

## The Electron Theory of Matter

Marek grounds his invention in contemporary (1922-era) physics. Matter, he explains, is "astonishingly 'thin'" — the spaces within an atom are enormous. If one were to compress all matter on Earth to eliminate these spaces, "the whole of our globe might be compressed into a fair-sized wheelbarrow". This porousness is what makes total atomic disintegration theoretically possible: the Karburator exploits the gaps in matter, releasing the energy that binds electrons together.

## The Divine Byproduct

The novel's central twist — and satire — is that atomic combustion produces more than energy. Marek's theoretical model assumes that all matter contains the Absolute (God) in a state of confinement. When matter is completely destroyed, the material component vanishes but an "indestructible residue" remains — "free and active Absolute," a "chemical nullity which acts with monstrous energy".

> "All this proceeds from the assumption that God is present in all matter. Can you imagine, for the sake of argument, that He is really so present?"

This hypothesis transforms atomic energy from a purely technological question into a theological one, and Čapek uses the premise to satirize both scientific hubris and religious institutionalism.

## Industrial Application

Marek's vision of atomic energy's industrial potential is sweeping. With merely "half a hundredweight of coal" in the Karburator, one could sail around the world, light all of Prague, or power a huge factory. Ultimately, even coal would become unnecessary — any pebble or handful of dirt contains more energy than an enormous boiler.

This utopian vision of unlimited, essentially free energy is the bait that hooks Bondy and the industrial establishment. The M.E.C. (Marek Engineering Corporation) proceeds to commercialize the Karburator at scale, triggering the novel's cascade of supernatural consequences.

## Čapek's Foresight

Written in 1922, Čapek's "atomic energy" predates the discovery of nuclear fission by over a decade. His description of atoms as collections of "the tiniest particles of electricity" — electrons — and the concept of extracting energy from atomic disintegration was remarkably prescient. The name "Karburator" deliberately evokes "carburetor," grounding the speculative technology in the familiar language of combustion engines, while the satirical leap — that burning matter releases God — transforms scientific speculation into theological farce.
