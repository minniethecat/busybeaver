## Role in the novel

Mr. Kei represents China at the Supreme Council of the Great Powers, convened in great haste by the British Premier Sir W. O'Patterney on the remote island of St. Kilda [1564-PN]. He arrives on a warship alongside the other plenipotentiaries — the American Horatio Bumm, the French Premier Dudieu, the Russian General Buchtin, the German Chancellor Dr. Wurm, the Italian Prince Trivelino, and the Japanese Baron Yanato. The conference's stated purpose is to address the Absolute crisis that has thrown Europe into chaos, but it quickly reveals itself as an exercise in imperial posturing.

## Arrival at St. Kilda

The chronicler records Kei's arrival among the roster of great powers [1564-PN]:

> "There followed him, each on a warship, the Chinese plenipotentiary, Mr. Kei; the French Premier, Dudieu; the Imperial Russian General, Buchtin; the Imperial German Chancellor, Dr. Wurm; the Italian Minister, Prince Trivelino; and the Japanese Ambassador, Baron Yanato."

The setting is deliberately absurd — sixteen British torpedo-boats patrol to prevent reporters from landing, and a Danish whaling schooner is torpedoed for attempting to breach the cordon. The secrecy is so extreme that the only account of the conference comes from a *New York Herald* reporter disguised as a waiter.

## Physical description and manner

Čapek's description of Kei is filtered through period Orientalism [1574-PN]:

> "Mr. Kei, looking like a very yellow and very withered old lady, fingered some kind of Buddhist rosary. He was a mandarin in his own Flowery Land."

Throughout the luncheon discussions of sport and actresses, Kei remains silent. While Baron Yanato refuses all liquid refreshment and smiles his perpetual smile, and General Buchtin paces the room, Kei simply fingers his rosary with downcast eyes. His stillness contrasts with the nervous energy of the European diplomats, marking him as the conference's most opaque — and therefore most dangerous — participant.

## "Which one?"

Kei's single intervention is among the novel's most famous lines. After Sir W. O'Patterney delivers a lengthy peroration on the need to recognize a single supreme authority — presumably the Christian God — Kei speaks for the first and only time [1614-PN]:

> "'Which one?' suddenly asked the Chinese plenipotentiary, Mr. Kei, lifting at last his wrinkled eyelids."

When Patterney answers "Buddha," Kei's response is devastating in its minimalism [1622-PN]:

> "'Buddha,' Mr. Kei said, and again dropped his lids, becoming the very counterpart of a dried-up mummy."

The question — "Which one?" — cuts through the European diplomats' rhetoric with surgical precision. While they debate which Absolute to recognize (which nation's god, which denomination's, which political faction's), Kei exposes the absurdity of claiming exclusive access to a universal divine presence. Buddha is simply one name among many — and if that satisfies Patterney, Kei has no further interest in the proceedings.

## The aftermath

The conference collapses immediately after Kei's intervention. The diplomats leave the council chamber in states ranging from purple-faced rage (General Buchtin) to ghostly pallor (Prince Trivelino). Kei is the last to exit [1626-PN]:

> "The last to leave was Mr. Kei, with downcast eyes, an exceedingly long black rosary sliding through his fingers."

His exit image — silent, serene, rosary still moving — implies that Kei has won without having to argue. The European powers came to partition the Absolute; Kei demonstrated, in two words, that the project was philosophically incoherent.

## Satirical function

Kei embodies Čapek's strategy of using marginal perspectives to expose the pretensions of the powerful. The European powers treat the Absolute as property to be claimed, like a colonial territory; Kei treats their claims as a category error. His single question does more damage to institutional religion's territorial claims than any of the novel's explicit satire. At the same time, the Orientalist description of Kei — the "withered old lady," the "Buddhist rosary," the "Flowery Land" — satirizes European perceptions of Asia as much as it does the diplomats themselves.
