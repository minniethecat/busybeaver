## Overview

St. Kilda is a remote archipelago in the North Atlantic, the westernmost islands of the Outer Hebrides, whose main island (Hirta) was historically one of the most isolated permanently inhabited places in the British Isles. In *The Absolute at Large*, St. Kilda earns its own chapter title (Chapter XX), marking it as a significant node in the global geography of the Absolute's influence — proof that even the most distant human settlement cannot escape the Karburator's reach.

## Location

St. Kilda lies approximately 64 kilometers west-northwest of North Uist in the Outer Hebrides, making it the most remote part of the British Isles. In the 1920s, when Čapek's novel was published, St. Kilda still supported a small Gaelic-speaking population of fewer than 100 people (the last permanent residents would be evacuated in 1930).

## Significance in the Novel

### A Named Limit

That St. Kilda merits a chapter title alongside major locations like "The Board Meeting," "The Process of Canonization," and "The Greatest War" is itself significant. Čapek is mapping the Absolute's reach to the very edges of the known world. If the divine force can touch "the dredge M.E.28 above Štěchovice" on the one hand, and "St. Kilda" in the stormy North Atlantic on the other, then there is literally nowhere it cannot reach.

### Isolation as Theme

St. Kilda's isolation — its defining characteristic — resonates with the novel's broader meditation on escape and responsibility. Just as Bondy retreats to his coral island in the Pacific, the naming of St. Kilda suggests that the Absolute has reached every possible refuge. The island that for centuries was the most remote habitation in Europe, the last place before the open Atlantic, cannot serve as sanctuary from what Marek's invention has unleashed.

## Thematic Resonance

St. Kilda serves as the novel's northern boundary, complementing the Pacific coral island in the south. Together, these two remote islands — one in the stormy North Atlantic, one in the tropical Pacific — bracket the globe and demonstrate the Absolute's ubiquity. The choice of St. Kilda is particularly evocative: an island whose population was already declining in the 1920s, whose way of life was being extinguished by the forces of modernity even without divine intervention. The Karburator's arrival would be simply one more wave — cosmic rather than cultural — washing over a community that had already weathered millennia of North Atlantic storms.
