## Overview

The Prague City Council is the municipal government of the Czechoslovak capital. In *The Absolute at Large*, the Council appears as one of the civic bodies drawn into — and transformed by — the Karburator revolution. Its representatives attend the Stvanice ceremony, and its Burgomaster plays a prominent role in the public drama of the Absolute's emergence.

## Role in the Novel

### The Stvanice Ceremony

The City Council is intimately involved in the ceremonial opening of the Central Karburator Power Station at Stvanice. The event is a civic production as much as a corporate one:

> It is to be ceremoniously set in motion at six o'clock the day after tomorrow, in the presence of the President, the Burgomaster, the City Council, and the representatives of the M.E.C.

The Burgomaster of Greater Prague delivers a speech at the ceremony — a speech that drags on historically while the crowd waits for illumination. Reporter Cyril Keval, peering through the Power Station window, observes that "the speaker was the Burgomaster of Greater Prague, all red in the face", still talking as darkness falls.

### The Burgomaster's Resignation

After the ceremony — during which the Absolute's effects break through into the assembled dignitaries — the Burgomaster resigns his office. The international press delicately reports that "the Burgomaster of Greater Prague has resigned his office and has gone into the country to recuperate". The euphemism ("to recuperate") masks the reality: the city's highest elected official has been transformed by direct exposure to the divine, and municipal governance cannot accommodate that transformation.

### Civic Authority Dissolved

The City Council's trajectory — from ceremonial participant to institutional casualty — mirrors the broader dissolution of secular authority under the Absolute's pressure. The Burgomaster's resignation is not an isolated incident but part of a pattern: the Minister for War resigns, the doyen of the Diplomatic Corps falls to his knees, and the institutions of the secular state prove incapable of containing or co-opting the spiritual force that has been unleashed.

## Thematic Significance

The City Council represents municipal governance — the most local, practical, and apparently secular level of political authority — and its inability to function in the presence of the Absolute demonstrates that the Karburator's threat to the established order is total. If even a city council cannot continue its work when God is physically present in the machinery, then no level of government, however modest, is insulated from the consequences of Marek's invention.
