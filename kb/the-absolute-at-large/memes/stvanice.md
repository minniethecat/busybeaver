## Overview

[[stvanice|Stvanice Island]] (Czech: Stvanice) is the location of the Central Karburator Electric Power Station for Greater Prague -- "a fifty-kilo monster, a magnificent machine" (007-042) designed to light the entire city of Prague with power generated by Rudolf Marek's atomic invention. Its ceremonial inauguration, attended by the President of Czechoslovakia, the Burgomaster of Greater Prague, and [[g-h-bondy|M.E.C. Chairman G. H. Bondy]], represents both the apex of technological optimism in Capek's satire and the point of no return: the moment when the [[the-karburator|Karburator]]'s hidden spiritual dimension -- its release of the [[the-absolute|Absolute]], God as an industrial byproduct -- catastrophically breaks into public view.

The Stvanice ceremony is arguably the novel's most vividly rendered set-piece, occupying the entirety of Chapter IX ("The Ceremony") and narrated through the cynical yet increasingly bewildered perspective of district reporter [[cyril-keval|Cyril Keval]]. Unlike earlier, isolated miraculous incidents at [[brevnov|Brevnov]] or the Minister's villa in [[dejvice|Dejvice]], the Power Station opening concentrates hundreds of officials, foreign dignitaries, and citizens in sustained proximity to a massive fifty-kilo Karburator. The spiritual outbreak that results -- documented by Keval and subsequently spun into official denial by the [[czechoslovak-press-bureau|Czechoslovak Press Bureau]] -- marks the irreversible transformation of the Absolute from an industrial secret into a global phenomenon.

## Location

Stvanice is a [[vltava|Vltava]] island in the Karlin district of [[prague|Prague]], situated between Holesovice and the New Town. In the novel, the Central Power Station is a deliberately unassuming structure. Cyril Keval, arriving in evening dress to cover the ceremony, finds it described with Capek's characteristic deflation of technological grandeur:

> Cyril Keval, district reporter on the staff of the Prague *People's Journal*, hurried into evening-dress for the occasion and dashed off to Stvanice just after six o'clock in the evening, to write up the ceremonial opening of the new Central Karburator Electric Power Station for Greater Prague. He shouldered his way through the curious crowds that overflowed the whole Petrov quarter, penetrated the three ranks of police, and reached a small concrete structure decorated with flags. (009-004)

The station's humble exterior -- "no bigger than a public convenience" (009-004) -- is a deliberate authorial choice, setting up the central irony: cosmic forces are about to be unleashed from a building that could be mistaken for a public lavatory. The tension between the monumental occasion (heads of state, ambassadors, military bands) and the shabby venue is sustained throughout the chapter as a running joke.

The larger Stvanice district, meanwhile, becomes the ground over which Keval anxiously paces during the seemingly interminable speeches: "He then ran five times round the whole Stvanice district, came back to the Central Power Station, and again sprang up to the little window" (009-019).

## Significance in the Novel

### The Announcement

The Stvanice Karburator is first mentioned in Chapter VII, where Bondy's factory manager informs him of its completion. The passage establishes the scale of the machine and the political weight of the planned ceremony:

> The central Karburator at Stvanice, which is to light the whole of Prague, is finished. It is a fifty-kilo monster, a magnificent machine. It is to be ceremoniously set in motion at six o'clock the day after tomorrow, in the presence of the President, the Burgomaster, the City Council, and the representatives of the M.E.C. (007-042)

Bondy, already terrified by the Absolute's effects, reacts with horror -- "God! oh God! if it be possible, remove this cup" (007-046) -- but the industrial momentum is unstoppable. The planned festivities are elaborate: "military bands are going to play salutes and fanfares in the streets, the Male Voice Choirs and the other Choral Societies will sing, there'll be fireworks and a salute of a hundred and one guns, the Castle will be illuminated" (007-044). The ceremony is framed from the start as both a technological triumph and a civic-religious liturgy, though no one yet grasps how literal the religious dimension will become.

### The Ceremonial Opening

The inauguration of the Stvanice Power Station is the novel's dramatic turning point. The scene is narrated through the eyes of Keval, who finds himself locked outside the tiny concrete building while the dignitaries process inside. Reduced to peering through a small window, he witnesses an increasingly absurd spectacle: the Burgomaster's speech stretches on interminably ("sheer physical exhaustion brought the Burgomaster to an end" (009-021)), the foreign ambassadors "were pretending to be giving their whole souls to the address, of which they understood not a single word" (009-015), and Old Billington, the [[diplomatic-corps|doyen of the Diplomatic Corps]], achieves the ultimate diplomatic feat:

> Old Billington, the doyen of the Diplomatic Corps, had passed away on his feet, preserving even in death the expression of an attentive listener. (009-021)

The apex of the civic liturgy arrives when the President -- the Chief of State -- finally presses the lever:

> The Chief of State drew himself erect and pressed the lever. Then, in an instant, the whole of Prague shone out as a vast expanse of light, the crowds cheered, the bells in all the steeples began to swing, and from the Marianske fort there sounded the first boom of the cannon. (009-022)

The citywide illumination is followed by a spectacular display: "Flaming rockets shot up from Strelecky Island; Hradcany, Petrin, and even Letna, were aglow with garlands of electric lamps, distant bands began competing with each other, illuminated biplanes circled above Stvanice, while the immense V16 soared up from Vysehrad all bedecked with lanterns" (009-023). The festivity is total, a technological sublime rendered in the language of civic celebration.

### The Spiritual Rupture

But the sublime collapses into the grotesque almost immediately. When Keval peers back through the window to witness the dignitaries' reaction to the Karburator's activation, the scene inside has transformed:

> Trembling all over, Keval supported himself against the trunk of a tree. From the interior of the concrete building came sounds as of savages chanting a hymn. (009-029)

The transformation is instantaneous and complete. The assembled representatives of state, church, diplomacy, and commerce -- the entire apparatus of secular authority -- are reduced to primal religious ecstasy. Bondy, fleeing the scene, crashes into Keval and flees with the desperate command: "For Christ's sake, let go. And get out of here, as quick as you can" (009-025). The technological temple has become a Pentecostal revival, and the distinction between power plant and church has collapsed.

### The Official Cover-up

The aftermath is managed through the standard tools of state bureaucracy. The [[czechoslovak-press-bureau|Czechoslovak Press Bureau]] issues a statement that is a masterpiece of euphemism and denial:

> Contrary to the reports issued by a local publication which have obtained some currency abroad, we are able to state on the very best authority that no improper incidents of any kind took place on the occasion of the formal opening of the Karburator Central Power Station. In connection with this, the Burgomaster of Greater Prague has resigned his office and has gone into the country to recuperate. Mr. Billington, doyen of the Diplomatic Corps, is, contrary to published reports, well and active. The fact is that all present declare that nothing in their experience has ever made so powerful an impression upon them. Every citizen has the right to fall to the earth and worship God, and the performance of miracles is not in conflict with any official position whatever in a democratic State. It is in any case decidedly improper to connect the Chief of State with regrettable incidents occasioned only by insufficient ventilation, combined with excessive strain upon the nerves. (009-029)

The statement is a marvel of doublespeak. The Burgomaster's resignation and flight "to the country to recuperate" is admitted while its cause is denied; Billington's apparent death and revival are acknowledged only to be contradicted; the President's direct involvement in triggering the catastrophe is blamed on "insufficient ventilation." Capek's satire here targets the institutional reflex to absorb the inexplicable into bureaucratic language, to paper over the miraculous with the vocabulary of health and safety.

### Aftermath and Ripple Effects

In Chapter X, Bondy wanders "through the streets of Prague" reflecting on the Stvanice disaster as the opening move in what has become an uncontrollable chain reaction. The [[industrial-bank|Industrial Bank]] has suffered a religious strike; textile factories in Upice run their looms autonomously day and night, weaving "kilometres of towels, calico, cretonne, and everything imaginable" from whatever raw material is thrown into them (010-006). The Stvanice ceremony was not an isolated incident but the first large-scale demonstration that the Absolute, once released into industrial civilization, would not be contained.

## Thematic Resonance

Stvanice is the novel's richest symbol of the collision between technological hubris and spiritual reality. The Power Station -- "no bigger than a public convenience" -- is Capek's anti-cathedral: a monument not to faith but to engineering, yet the force it releases is indistinguishable from the divine. Every element of the ceremony is a secular liturgy: the crimson carpet, the national anthem, the military bands and choral societies, the hundred-and-one-gun salute, even the President's hand upon the lever as a technocratic high priest's gesture of consecration. When the Absolute breaks through, it mocks all these pretensions simultaneously: the Burgomaster resigns, the diplomat dies and revives, the President's lever becomes a conduit for the miraculous, and the distinction between a power plant and a temple collapses into irrelevance.

Capek's choice of island geography is thematically freighted. Stvanice is physically separated from the city it powers, accessible only by bridge -- a spatial metaphor for the distance between industrial modernity and the human communities it promises to serve. The island becomes a liminal zone where the boundary between the technological and the sacred dissolves. Keval's frantic circling of the district, running "five times round the whole Stvanice" while the speeches drag on, mirrors the novel's larger pattern: humanity circles its own creations, unable to enter or escape, waiting for forces it has set in motion but cannot control.

The Press Bureau's statement is perhaps the darkest element of the chapter's satire. Its conflation of divine manifestation with "insufficient ventilation" and "excessive strain upon the nerves" is not merely bureaucratic comedy -- it is an indictment of modernity's incapacity to recognize the sacred on any terms but its own instrumental ones. A power station can be a temple only if everyone agrees to call it something else. The Stvanice ceremony is the moment that agreement becomes impossible.
