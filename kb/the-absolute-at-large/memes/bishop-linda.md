**Bishop Linda** is the Suffragan Bishop who attempts to negotiate ecclesiastical control over the divine emanations from [[rudy-marek|Rudolf Marek]]'s [[the-karburator|Karburator]] cellar in Karel Čapek's *The Absolute at Large*. A small, jolly cleric with gold spectacles [(005-005)], Bishop Linda represents the institutional Church's pragmatic attempt to monopolize the Absolute -- an effort Čapek deploys to satirize organized religion's territorial instincts. He is one of the novel's most vivid secondary characters, appearing in three distinct phases: the negotiation with Bondy, the disciplining of Father Jost, and the crisis at Hradec Králové.

## Overview

Bishop Linda is a Suffragan Bishop of the Roman Catholic Church who serves as the Church's primary institutional actor in the novel's central conflict over the [[the-absolute|Absolute]]. First appearing in Chapter V, he arrives at [[g-h-bondy|G. H. Bondy]]'s office to propose that the Church take over management of the Břevnov cellar where the [[the-karburator|Karburator]] is producing the Absolute. A small man with gold spectacles and "a comical mouth puckered up in clerical fashion in pleasant childish folds" [(005-005)], Bishop Linda is simultaneously jovial and imperious, a church politician whose pragmatism masks a genuine theological insight: that mankind cannot handle a real and active God.

## Role & Significance

### The Church Politician

Bishop Linda embodies the novel's critique of institutional religion's response to genuine divine manifestation. Where [[rudy-marek|Marek]] sees the Absolute as a terrifying physical force and [[g-h-bondy|Bondy]] sees it as a commercial product, Bishop Linda sees it as an ecclesiastical asset to be managed, branded, and controlled. His intervention dramatizes the core tension at the heart of Čapek's satire: even God Himself, once released into the world, becomes subject to institutional capture.

> He began briskly, without beating about the bush. "I've come to you on a very delicate errand. Very delicate indeed," he repeated with relish. "We have been discussing your ... ahem ... your affair in the Consistory. His Eminence, the Archbishop, wishes to settle this regrettable incident with as little publicity as possible." [(005-005)]

The Bishop's proposal -- that the Church should take over operation of the Břevnov cellar and mediate the Absolute's distribution -- reveals the novel's deeper satire of religious bureaucracy. The Church, in Linda's telling, is the only institution competent to handle divinity. His argument is not theological but organizational: the Church has centuries of experience managing sacred manifestations, and the Absolute needs "proper handling."

### The Institutional Realist

Linda's theological sophistication sets him apart from other characters who encounter the Absolute. When Bondy took him to the Břevnov cellar as "an expert, to inspect the wonder-working Absolute," Linda spent three full hours there and "underwent them all: illumination, miraculous powers, ecstasy, everything" [(005-004)]. Yet unlike the workers and citizens who succumb to religious mania, Linda remained unmoved:

> "He simply doesn't. He said that the Church does not explain, but merely prescribes or prohibits. In short, he definitely refused to compromise the Church with any new and untried God." [(005-004)]

This is the key to Linda's character: he is a professional administrator of the sacred. A lifetime of handling God has immunized him against God's direct presence. His resistance to the Absolute is not atheism but institutional prudence -- the Church cannot afford to endorse a deity it did not create.

### The Prophet of Destruction

In his confrontation with Marek and Bondy, Linda delivers what proves to be the novel's most accurate prophecy:

> "And I tell you," said the Bishop quietly and sweetly, "that at the end of that year not a single Karburator will be running." [(005-010)]

When Bondy protests that thousands of Karburators will be manufactured, Linda responds with remarkable acuity:

> "Because mankind, whether believers or unbelievers, cannot do with a real and active God. We simply cannot, gentlemen. It is out of the question." [(005-011)]

> "Gentlemen, in the name of Heaven, do not imagine that the Church brings God into the world. The Church merely confines Him and controls Him. And you two unbelievers are loosing Him upon the earth like a flood. The ship of Peter will survive even this deluge; like the Ark of Noah, it will ride out this inundation of the Absolute -- but your modern society," cried the Bishop with a mighty voice, "that will pay the price!" [(005-012)]

This speech is the novel's thematic centerpiece. Linda articulates Čapek's central satirical insight: institutional religion exists not to unleash the divine but to contain it.

### The Authoritarian Disciplinarian

In Chapter XVIII, Linda's character takes a darker turn. When he discovers that [[father-jost|Father Jost]] has published a leader denouncing the Absolute as "knavish imposture," he storms into the *People's Friend* editorial room:

> "You've no right to think," roared Bishop Linda, his eyeglasses flashing at him eerily. "Here, take the thing"; and crumpling up that morning's issue of *The People's Friend* in his hand, he flung it at Jost's feet. "I thought! Look at him, he thinks! Why didn't you telephone? Why didn't you ask what you were to write?" [(018-010)]

His dictation of Jost's humiliating retraction -- "It has been scientifically demonstrated ... that I, Father Jost, am a jackass, a dolt and an idiot" [(018-015)] -- and his subsequent strategic reversal, ordering Jost to write a glowing endorsement of the Absolute, demonstrate his political ruthlessness. The Church must embrace, not oppose, the divine manifestation once it becomes clear that the Absolute's popularity cannot be suppressed.

> "Jost," cried the Bishop, rising to his feet, "our churches are empty, our flock is running after the Absolute. Jost, you blockhead, if we wish to bring our flock back to us, we must secure the Absolute. We will set up Atomic Karburators in all our churches ... but that, my little priest, is above your head. Bear in mind this one thing: the Absolute must work for us; He must be ours, i.e. He must be ours alone." [(018-025)]

### The Stabilizing Councilor

In Chapter XXVI, during the chaos of the [[the-greatest-war|Greatest War]], Bishop Linda appears in a gentler role. When [[general-hampl|General Hampl]] threatens Hradec Králové with his comic-opera coup, Burgomaster Skocdopole "called on Bishop Linda, who, however, laughed at his fears" [(026-010)]. Later, Hampl's mayoral administration is "blessed with comparative peace, thanks to the wise counsels of Bishop Linda and the Worshipful City Fathers" [(026-026)].

This final portrait of Linda -- the shrewd churchman who, having weathered the Absolute's storm, settles into the role of secular civic advisor -- completes his arc from ecclesiastical politician to institutional survivor.

## Appearances

### Chapter V -- The Negotiation

Bishop Linda's first and most substantial appearance. He arrives at Bondy's office to propose Church management of the Břevnov cellar. Bondy introduces Marek as "the owner of the ill-omened cellar at Břevnov"; the Bishop "rubbed his hands with delight while the wrathful engineer spluttered out something about being 'delighted to have the honour,' with a dogged expression that said clearly, 'Confound you for a canting humbug!'" [(005-005)]. Linda proposes three conditions: limiting Absolute production, using only coal from Male Svantovice (site of a Marian shrine), and granting the Church a monopoly. When Bondy and Marek refuse, Linda delivers his prophecy of destruction [(005-001)]--[(005-012)].

### Chapter XVIII -- In the Night Editor's Room

Linda storms into [[father-jost|Father Jost]]'s newspaper office, demolishes Jost for publishing an anti-Absolute editorial, dictates a humiliating retraction, and then reverses course entirely -- ordering Jost to announce the Absolute's admission into the Church. This scene shows Linda at his most authoritarian and most politically agile [(018-010)]--[(018-025)].

### Chapter XXVI -- The Battle of Hradec Králové

Linda serves as advisor to Burgomaster Skocdopole during [[general-hampl|General Hampl]]'s coup attempt, laughing at the supposed threat and later providing "wise counsels" to Hampl's administration [(026-010)]--[(026-026)].

## Relationships

### G. H. Bondy

Linda and [[g-h-bondy|Bondy]] are the novel's two great institutional operators -- one ecclesiastical, one industrial. Their negotiation in Chapter V is a duel of pragmatists, each recognizing the other as a fellow administrator of the uncontrollable. Bondy respects Linda's intelligence, later reflecting that "Bishop Linda [was] even more [right than Marek]" [(010-001)].

### Rudolf Marek

Mutual contempt defines this relationship. Marek sees Linda as a "canting humbug"; Linda sees Marek's cellar as a resource to be claimed for the Church. Their clash embodies the novel's tension between scientific-materialist and religious-institutional worldviews.

### Father Jost

Linda as ecclesiastical disciplinarian. His brutal humiliation of [[father-jost|Jost]] in Chapter XVIII reveals the authoritarian structure of Church authority. Yet the reversal -- forcing Jost to endorse what he had just condemned -- also shows Linda's willingness to subordinate theological consistency to institutional survival.

### The Absolute

Linda treats the Absolute as a resource to be franchised, not a divine reality to be worshipped. His inability to be moved by the Absolute's presence -- despite experiencing its full effects in the Břevnov cellar -- is his defining characteristic. He is a professional handler of God, and professionals do not get religion on the job.

### General Hampl

Though they never directly interact on the page, Linda's "wise counsels" help stabilize [[general-hampl|Hampl]]'s administration after the comic-opera coup. Linda's laughter at the supposed threat -- and his subsequent willingness to work with the usurper -- demonstrates his characteristic pragmatism.
