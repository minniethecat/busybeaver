## Definition

In the theological context of the novel, **salvation** shifts from a doctrinal promise about the afterlife to a tangible psychological state induced by the Absolute's presence. The Karburator produces not just free energy but a byproduct — the Absolute — which grants anyone in its vicinity an intense, transformative experience that the text describes in the language of personal salvation.

## A new kind of conversion

The chronicler of the Absolute's effects observes individual transformations with ethnographic curiosity [1487-PN]:

> "It was a remarkable spectacle to see into their hearts, to watch their personal faith and personal salvation come into being, to linger amid the new marvels of human sanctity."

The phrase "come into being" is significant — salvation is not received from an external authority but generated from within by exposure to the Absolute. This is the novel's most radical theological claim, made satirically: what the Church offers through centuries of doctrine and ritual, the Karburator's exhaust produces as a side effect.

## Salvation without the Church

The salvation motif exposes the central tension of the novel: if the Absolute can save souls directly through industrial byproduct, what purpose does the institutional Church serve? The Church's response — absorbing the Absolute through canonization, deification, and Bondy's baptism — is an attempt to reclaim a monopoly on salvation that the Karburator has broken.

This tension between mediated (ecclesiastical) and unmediated (experiential) salvation drives the novel's religious satire. The Church cannot deny the salvific effects of the Absolute — they are publicly observable — so it must instead assert that only the Church can properly administer and authenticate salvation, even when the salvation in question needs no administration at all.

## Irony

The ultimate irony is that Bondy, the capitalist who commercialized the Absolute, finds himself receiving the sacrament of baptism — a ritual of personal salvation — as part of the Church's PR campaign. The man who turned God into an industrial product is now being publicly saved, not because he repents, but because the Church needs a photo opportunity.
