The **People's Journal** (*Lidove noviny* in Czech editions) is the fictional [[prague-in-the-novel|Prague]] newspaper that appears throughout Karel Capek's *[[the-absolute|The Absolute at Large]]*, functioning as both a narrative device and a satirical vehicle. From the novel's opening scene — where [[g-h-bondy|G. H. Bondy]] reads the paper and discovers [[rudy-marek|Rudy Marek]]'s fateful advertisement (001-002) — to the war dispatches that close the story, the *People's Journal* serves as the reader's window onto the global catastrophe unleashed by the [[the-karburator|Karburator]]. Its newsroom in Chapter XXII becomes the novel's central observation post for watching civilization disintegrate under the pressure of absolute faith.

## Overview

The *People's Journal* is the primary secular newspaper depicted in the novel. It has "grown long ago to five times its ancient size" (001-002), reflecting the commercial expansion of the press in Capek's era. The paper functions at multiple narrative levels: as the medium through which crucial plot information reaches characters, as a satirical mirror of journalistic practice under conditions of universal enchantment, and as a vehicle for Capek's insider critique of the press — Capek himself was a working journalist who wrote for the real *Lidove noviny*.

## Nature and Purpose

The *People's Journal* is a commercial daily newspaper headquartered in Prague. Its operations include:

- **Finance and Commerce section**: Bondy's preferred reading, which "had grown long ago to five times its ancient size" (001-002), satirizing the business-centric orientation of the modern press.
- **District reporting**: [[cyril-keval|Cyril Keval]] serves as the paper's district reporter, dispatched to cover events like the Stvanice power station ceremony (009-002).
- **Press Bureau integration**: The paper relies on the "Czechoslovak Press Bureau" for official speeches and wire dispatches (009-013).
- **War correspondence**: By Chapter XXIII, the paper has transitioned to war reporting, receiving telephonic bulletins from the front (023-001).
- **Classified advertisements**: The medium through which Marek's fateful "Invention" ad reaches Bondy (001-009), setting the entire plot in motion.

The paper's commercial nature is emphasized from the opening paragraph — Bondy skips war news and cabinet crises in favor of the Finance section, treating the newspaper as a consumer product to be navigated selectively.

## Role in the Narrative

The *People's Journal* appears at three crucial junctures in the novel, each marking a stage in the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] crisis:

1. **Chapters I and IX — The Lens of Commerce**: The novel begins with [[g-h-bondy|Bondy]] browsing the "Finance and Commerce section" of the *People's Journal*, which "had grown long ago to five times its ancient size" (001-002). The paper is the medium through which Marek's innocuous classified advertisement reaches [[g-h-bondy|Bondy]] — the first domino in a chain that topples civilization. In Chapter IX, the paper dispatches its district reporter, [[cyril-keval|Cyril Keval]], to cover the ceremonial opening of the Central [[the-karburator|Karburator]] Power Station at [[stvanice|Stvanice]] — the event that publicly inaugurates the Atomic Age (009-002).

2. **Chapter XXII — The Newsroom as Chaos**: The longest sustained portrait of the *People's Journal* appears in "The Old Patriot," where "everyone was working at top speed" amidst the religious frenzy sweeping [[prague-in-the-novel|Prague]] (022-002). [[cyril-keval|Cyril Keval]], the paper's irreverent reporter, catalogs the day's disasters — armed clashes between theological factions, professors buried under the *Collected Works of Jan Vrba*, the Sparta and Slavia football clubs preparing to do battle for Zeus and Svantovit — while the editor-in-chief storms in to complain about a missed financial story. The newsroom becomes a darkly comic mirror of the collapsing world outside.

3. **Chapter XXIII — The War Correspondent**: When the religious conflict in Augsburg escalates into a Bavarian holy war against Prussia, the *People's Journal* receives the bulletins and dispatches [[cyril-keval|Keval]] to the front (023-001), (023-007). The paper's transition from local reporter to war correspondent tracks the crisis from domestic absurdity to international catastrophe.

## The Night Editor's Room (Chapter XVIII)

Though this chapter focuses on the rival Catholic newspaper *[[the-peoples-friend|The People's Friend]]*, it provides the novel's most sustained portrait of newspaper production under the [[the-absolute|Absolute]]'s influence. Kostal, the night editor, presides over a room where [[father-jost|Father Jost]] writes leaders denouncing the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] as a "knavish imposture" — until [[bishop-linda|Bishop Linda]] storms in, reverses editorial policy entirely, and orders [[father-jost|Jost]] to announce the [[the-absolute|Absolute]]'s admission to the bosom of the Church (018-002)–(018-058). The scene anatomizes the relationship between journalism and institutional power when reality itself becomes negotiable.

### Block Quotations

The novel's opening establishes the paper's commercial character:

> (001-002) On New Year's Day, 1943, G. H. Bondy, head of the great Metallo-Electrical Company, was sitting as usual reading his paper. He skipped the news from the theatre of war rather disrespectfully, avoided the Cabinet crisis, then crowded on sail (for the *People's Journal*, which had grown long ago to five times its ancient size, now afforded enough canvas for an ocean voyage) for the Finance and Commerce section.

Cyril Keval's dispatch to Stvanice and his journalistic cynicism:

> (009-002) Cyril Keval, district reporter on the staff of the Prague *People's Journal*, hurried into evening-dress for the occasion and dashed off to Stvanice just after six o'clock in the evening, to write up the ceremonial opening of the new Central Karburator Electric Power Station for Greater Prague.

> (009-013) "I'll never get in now," he said to himself. "Cvancara is right," he went on ruminating. "They always do something silly. Fancy putting up that little hut for an imposing ceremony like this. Ah well, the Czechoslovak Press Bureau will supply the speeches, and one can soon work up the trimmings — deep impressiveness of the occasion, magnificent progress, enthusiastic reception for the President —"

The newsroom in crisis:

> (022-002) In the Prague office of the *People's Journal* everyone was working at top speed. The telephone operator was yelling furiously into the telephone and quarrelling with the young lady at the Exchange. Scissors clicked and typewriters clattered, and Mr. Cyril Keval sat on the table and dangled his legs.

Keval's journalistic despair:

> (022-026) "I simply can't think up anything natural. When I think it over, everything is a miracle really."

The persistence of commercial journalism amid apocalypse:

> (022-030) "In the Finance and Commerce Section. An American combine has bought up the Pacific Islands and is subletting them. A tiny coral atoll costs fifty thousand dollars a year. Big demand even from the Continent of Europe. Shares have gone up to two thousand seven hundred already. G. H. Bondy interested to the extent of one hundred and twenty millions. And we haven't got a word about it," said the editor-in-chief angrily, and slammed the door behind him.

The "old patriot" letter — a voice from a world that no longer exists:

> (022-031) "Rejzek," cried Keval, "here's an interesting letter: 'Dear Sir — Forgive an old patriot, who can remember the evil times of oppression and the dark days of serfdom, if he raises a plaintive voice and begs you to use your skilful pen to make known to the Czech people the grief and sore anxiety we old patriots feel ... And therefore, I solemnly adjure you at this twelfth hour, when great and terrible dangers confront us on every hand, to urge our people to band themselves together in a union of the whole nation for the defence of our country.'"

War arrives at the newsroom:

> (023-003) By 11 p.m. the following telephonic communications had been received at the office of the *People's Journal*: — Czech Press Bureau. From Munich, 12th inst. — According to the Wolff Telegraph Agency, the demonstrations in Augsburg yesterday led to bloodshed. Seventy Protestants were killed.

## Thematic Analysis

The *People's Journal* serves multiple satirical functions in Capek's novel:

**Journalism as Commodity**: The paper appears first as a commercial product — [[g-h-bondy|Bondy]] skips "the news from the theatre of war" to reach the "Finance and Commerce section" (001-002). Before the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] arrives, journalism is already a creature of capital, grown to "five times its ancient size" on advertising revenue. The novel's premise — classified ad leads to god in a cellar — literalizes the absurdist collapse of the boundary between commerce and revelation.

**The Newsroom as Index of Collapse**: In Chapter XXII, the *People's Journal* newsroom becomes the narrative's seismograph. [[cyril-keval|Keval]]'s stream of chatter — "the Vrsovice barracks have sent an ultimatum to the Cernin barracks calling on them to recognize the Vrsovice dogma of the Three Degrees of Salvation" (022-013) — registers the disintegration of civil society into armed theological factions. The newsroom can only report what the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] has made of the world, and what it has made defies the conventions of naturalistic journalism.

**The Impossibility of Natural News**: [[cyril-keval|Keval]]'s confession — "I simply can't think up anything natural. When I think it over, everything is a miracle really" (022-026) — is the novel's most compressed satirical statement about journalism under conditions of universal enchantment. In a world where the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] renders everything miraculous, the reporter's task of finding the "natural" angle becomes impossible. The paper continues to function — editors slam doors over missed business stories, letters pour in from "old patriots" — but its categories have collapsed.

**The Rival Press**: The subplot of [[bishop-linda|Bishop Linda]]'s takeover of *[[the-peoples-friend|The People's Friend]]* editorial line (Chapter XVIII) extends the satire to the Catholic press, where the editorial position on the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] pivots from denunciation to embrace on the Bishop's command. Both newspapers — capitalist and Catholic — are shown to be instruments of institutional power, their truth claims contingent on the interests they serve.

**Capek's Own Experience**: Capek himself was a working journalist — he wrote for *Lidove noviny*, the real Czech newspaper whose name ("The People's Paper") the fictional "People's Journal" echoes. The novel's knowing depiction of editorial rhythms, press bureau dispatches, and the gap between events and their reportage draws on this experience, lending the satire an insider's precision beneath its comic exaggeration.

## Significance in the Narrative

The *People's Journal* is more than a setting or device — it is the novel's implicit theory of media. Capek suggests that journalism, like religion and capitalism, is an institution that processes reality through its own categories, and those categories prove catastrophically inadequate to the Absolute. The paper's staff do not cause the disaster, but neither can they comprehend or resist it. They report, they complain, they adapt — and the world burns.

The novel's final image of the paper — receiving telephonic bulletins of mass religious slaughter in Augsburg — completes the arc: the *People's Journal* has moved from classified advertisements to war dispatches, from commerce to apocalypse, without ever questioning the institutional logic that carried it there.
