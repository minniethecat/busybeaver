# Worship

**Worship** in *The Absolute at Large* becomes an involuntary human response to the Absolute's presence. Throughout the novel, exposure to the divine emanations from Karburators triggers spontaneous religious gatherings, rituals, and forms of devotion that span the spectrum from primitive fetishism to organized liturgy.

## The Dredge Congregation

One of the novel's most vivid depictions of Absolute-induced worship occurs on Kuzenda's dredge on the Vltava River, where a motley congregation gathers:

> "Brethren and sisters, on this dredge, where signs of grace are shown to us, we are gathered together for worship. We need not send away the unbelievers and mockers as the spiritualists do." 

Kuzenda's open-air service transforms ordinary working people — a drunken gamekeeper, a thieving businessman — into a community of faith through the Absolute's direct influence.

## Primitive Forms and Academic Analysis

Dr. Blahous, a scholar of religion, analyzes the Absolute-induced worship through an anthropological lens, finding it deeply regressive:

> "In both cases he demonstrated the atavistic occurrence of primitive forms of worship, the tendency to animistic pantheism and shamanism, a religious communism reminiscent of the Anabaptists, and a general surrender of reasoning power in favour of the grossest impulses of superstition, witchcraft, occultism, mysticism, and necromancy." (012-006)

Yet Blahous himself is eventually converted, recognizing that God must begin again from primitive forms in a world that has lost authentic faith:

> "God cannot now reveal Himself otherwise than in primitive forms of worship. With the decay of faith in modern times our connection with the old religious life has been broken." (012-014)

## Mass Spectacle at the Power Station

The opening of the Karburator Central Power Station demonstrates worship at an institutional scale, where diplomatic and political elites fall involuntarily into religious devotion:

> "Every citizen has the right to fall to the earth and worship God, and the performance of miracles is not in conflict with any official position whatever in a democratic State." 

## Čapek's Commentary

Through the worship phenomenon, Čapek satirizes both organized religion and secular modernity. The Absolute reduces sophisticated modern Europeans to what scholars dismiss as "primitive" religiosity — yet this very regression produces genuine moral transformation. The worship is ridiculous and sublime in equal measure.
