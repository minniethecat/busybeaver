## Role in the novel

Mr. Novotny is the printer at the *People's Journal*, the diocesan newspaper that Bishop Linda commandeers to orchestrate the Catholic Church's absorption of the Absolute. He appears in Chapters XIX and XX as a silent but functionally essential figure — the technician who physically produces the propaganda while the clergy debate its content. His appearances bookend the Church's campaign: first carrying wet proofs for Father Jost's polemics, then receiving the Apostolic Letter for typesetting.

## The printer's entrance

Novotny's first appearance is purely functional [1382-PN]:

> "At that moment Novotny, the printer, came in with the wet proofs."

Father Jost, interrupted mid-composition, asks whether they've already used "satanic machinations" — the kind of rhetorical stock-in-trade that Novotny, as printer, has no interest in evaluating. His role is mechanical, but Čapek uses this to satiric effect: Novotny is the only person in the room who produces anything physical, and his product is propaganda.

## Dialogue with Father Jost

Novotny is given brief but revealing dialogue. When Jost asks his opinion of the day's leader article, Novotny's response cuts through the clerical self-regard [1402-PN]:

> "'Strong stuff,' said the printer. 'But we ought to be getting some fresh copy in about the miraculous cures.'"

The line is devastating in its matter-of-factness. "Strong stuff" — the printer's professional assessment of theological polemic. "Fresh copy about the miraculous cures" — miracles treated as a content category requiring periodic updating, like sports results or weather reports. Novotny, speaking from the production floor, has absorbed the logic of journalism so completely that the supernatural has become just another beat.

## The phrase factory

When Father Jost exhausts his rhetorical resources, he turns to Novotny for help [1410-PN]:

> "'Mr. Novotny, can't you think of any suitable phrase?'"

Novotny's suggestion — "The sacred wrath of the Church" — satisfies Jost so completely that he exclaims [1414-PN]:

> "'That would do splendidly,' said Father Jost, with a sigh of relief. 'Where do you get all these bright ideas from, Novotny?'"

The exchange completes the satirical inversion: the priest, trained in theology and rhetoric, depends on the printer for his most effective propaganda phrases. Novotny, who has no theological training, understands what reads well in bold type — which, in the newspaper business, is the only kind of truth that matters.

## The Bishop's typesetter

When Bishop Linda arrives to impose his authority and redirect the paper's editorial line, Novotny is caught in the crossfire. Linda, furious at an unauthorized editorial, roars his name [1428-PN]:

> "'I thought! Look at him, he thinks! Why didn't you telephone? Why didn't you ask what you were to write? And you, Kostal, how could you put it in the paper? You thought, too, did you? Novotny!'"

Novotny's name, shouted at the end of Linda's tirade, becomes a general-purpose exclamation — the printer, as the final link in the production chain, is held responsible for everyone's intellectual independence.

But Linda also knows Novotny's value. After dictating the new editorial line — the Absolute's admission into the Church, Bondy's baptism, the Apostolic Letter — Linda turns to his most reliable instrument [1496-PN]:

> "'Mr. Novotny, here is the Apostolic Letter to that effect; have it set up in large bold type on the front page of the paper.'"

The instruction transforms a theological document into a newspaper headline. The Apostolic Letter, properly a formal ecclesiastical communication, becomes front-page "large bold type" — the typographic equivalent of a declaration of war.

## The propaganda pipeline

Novotny completes the chain from Bishop (strategy) to Jost (writing) to Novotny (production) to the reading public. His presence makes visible the material infrastructure of institutional religion's campaign: the ink, the type, the presses, the delivery routes. The Absolute may be divine, but its message reaches the faithful through the same mechanical processes that print the racing results. In a novel about the industrialization of the sacred — the Karburator as God-factory — Novotny is the production-floor supervisor.
