# Soul

The **soul** in *The Absolute at Large* is the point of contact between the human individual and the Absolute's emanations. It is through the soul that the Karburator's divine byproduct exerts its most profound effects — conversion, illumination, ecstasy, and moral reformation.

## The Absolute's Psychological Onset

The narrator describes the universal reach of the Absolute's effect on the human soul, emphasizing its inevitability:

> "After a few months of its activity there was practically not a single person on earth who had not experienced, if only for a moment, that religious shock by which the Absolute made known its presence to his soul." (011-012)

This universal penetration of the soul by the Absolute is presented not as a matter of belief or choice but as a physical inevitability — a kind of spiritual radiation to which no one is immune.

## Bondy's Confession

G. H. Bondy, the hard-headed industrialist, experiences the soul's vulnerability to the Absolute when he first enters Marek's cellar:

> "Look here, Rudy, it's all very extraordinary — but I believe you, so to speak. On my soul, I do believe you. You know, when I stood in front of that Karburator of yours, I felt that I was in the presence of something overpoweringly great, something a man could not withstand." (003-021)

Bondy's oath — "On my soul" — is simultaneously a casual figure of speech and a literal description of what is happening to him.

## The Innermost Soul

The Absolute's mode of attack is to bypass reason entirely and strike directly at the soul:

> "He attacks you in your innermost soul, and you're done for. When He can't convince you by reason, He sends miraculous enlightenment upon you. You know what He did with Saul." 

This description, attributed to Bondy, frames the Absolute's action as a kind of spiritual ambush — irresistible precisely because it operates below the level of rational deliberation.

## The Russian Soul

In the novel's geopolitical satire, national character becomes intertwined with spiritual susceptibility. General Buchtin claims the Absolute for Russia through the concept of the national soul:

> "He's a Russian, a genuine Russian, a Slav. With the great Russian soul, your Excellency. He's on the side of us muzhiks." 

Čapek here mocks the nationalist appropriation of spiritual phenomena — each nation claims the Absolute as its own.
