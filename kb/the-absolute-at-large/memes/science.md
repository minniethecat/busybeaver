# Science

**Science** in Čapek's *The Absolute at Large* occupies an ironic position: it is the means by which the Absolute is released into the world, yet it proves powerless to comprehend or contain what it has unleashed. The novel presents the scientific worldview as a fragile epistemological framework that collapses upon contact with genuine transcendence.

## Science as the Accidental Midwife of the Divine

The Karburator — the device that releases the Absolute — is the product of rigorous scientific inquiry. Marek explains to Bondy the physical principle behind his invention:

> "Modern science teaches that all matter — that is to say, its atoms — is composed of a vast number of units of energy. An atom is in reality a collection of electrons, i.e. of the tiniest particles of electricity."

The irony is profound: the most advanced scientific theory of matter, when applied industrially, produces not clean energy but God. Science, pursuing purely material knowledge, stumbles into the metaphysical.

## "Him or Science"

Marek, as a committed atheist and scientist, recognizes the incompatibility between his materialist worldview and what he has discovered. He frames the conflict in stark terms:

> "From the scientific point of view, Bondy, it is absolutely intolerable. It's a case of Him or science. I don't assert that God does not exist; I only assert that He ought not to exist, or at least ought not to manifest Himself."

This is one of the novel's sharpest satirical moments: the scientist does not deny God's existence on empirical grounds but objects to it on principle. Science is exposed not as a method for discovering truth but as a commitment to a God-free universe.
