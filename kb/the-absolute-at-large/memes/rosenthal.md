# Rosenthal

Rosenthal is a business associate within [[g-h-bondy|G. H. Bondy]]'s commercial circle who becomes one of the early casualties of [[the-absolute|the Absolute]]'s influence on the business world, reportedly driven to madness by religious awakening.

## The Report of Madness

Rosenthal's most dramatic narrative moment comes through a report delivered to Bondy:

> A manager leaned closer and said in a low voice, "Chief, Rosenthal has gone crazy." "Rosenthal!" exclaimed G. H. Bondy. (005-040)

Bondy's shocked reaction — the exclamation "Rosenthal!" — conveys that this is not an insignificant figure. Rosenthal is someone Bondy knows personally, whose breakdown signals that the Absolute's effects have reached the inner circles of industry.

## A Man of Business

What we know of Rosenthal before his breakdown comes through his business persona. He is portrayed as a typical commercial figure — accustomed to rational calculation, profit-seeking, and the orderly conduct of business affairs. His transformation from businessman to apparent madman represents one of the novel's sharpest satirical cuts: if the Absolute can drive a hard-headed businessman to religious [[ecstasy|ecstasy]], it can affect anyone.

## The Boardroom Breakdown

Rosenthal's madness manifests within the business context itself. He attempts to calm a colleague who has been seized by religious fervor:

> "Come, come," Mr. Rosenthal tried to calm him down. "We know you for a kind and upright man, Mr. Machat — yes, yes, it can all be explained." (005-055)

The irony is that Rosenthal himself, shortly after this attempt at rational mediation, succumbs to the same force. His effort to "explain" the unexplainable — to contain religious experience within business discourse — fails, and he joins the ranks of the Absolute's converts.

Rosenthal is also described as "anxious for peace":

> "M—m no," growled old Rosenthal, anxious for peace. "Our Chairman will explain. Yes, yes, it can all be explained." (005-050)

This insistence that "it can all be explained" marks Rosenthal as a man who believes in the power of rational business procedures — meetings, chairmen, explanations — to manage any crisis. The Absolute's triumph over this worldview is one of the novel's central satirical assertions.

## Significance

Rosenthal represents the vulnerability of the business establishment to forces it cannot commodity or negotiate with. His fate demonstrates that no amount of commercial success protects against the Absolute's transformative power. The fact that Bondy reacts to Rosenthal's madness with personal shock rather than business calculation suggests that even the most calculating capitalist recognizes a human dimension that the Absolute threatens.
