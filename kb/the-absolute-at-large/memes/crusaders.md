## Overview

The Crusaders are the Catholic military faction in the global religious war that consumes Central Europe in the novel's final chapters. Fighting against the Protestant Union, the Crusaders wage a war of extermination using Karburator-powered weapons — tanks, aircraft, and fifty-four-centimeter projectiles — that transforms the spiritual conflict unleashed by the Absolute into industrial-scale slaughter.

## Role in the Novel

### The Central European Front

The novel's report on the war situation describes the Crusaders' campaign:

> The Crusaders had made themselves masters of Holland, which they had flooded by breaking the dykes and letting in the sea, as well as of Hanover and Holstein as far as Lübeck, whence they were making inroads on Denmark.

The military language is precise and horrifying: flooded Holland, occupied Hanover, advancing on Denmark. The Crusaders' tactics are as ruthless as any medieval holy war, but their weapons are atomic. The name "Crusaders" — evoking the medieval campaigns to reclaim the Holy Land — is deliberately anachronistic, suggesting that the Absolute has revived not just religious feeling but its most violent historical forms.

### No Quarter

The war's brutality is absolute: "No quarter was given in the fighting. Cities were razed to the ground, the men killed, and women up to the age of fifty violated". The Crusaders, fighting in the name of the Catholic Absolute, are indistinguishable in their cruelty from their Protestant enemies. The divine force that began as a byproduct of peaceful industrial production ends as the justification for total war.

## Thematic Significance

The Crusaders embody the novel's darkest thesis: that religion, when wedded to modern technology, produces not enlightenment but atrocity. The name "Crusader" collapses the distance between medieval holy war and modern industrial warfare, suggesting that the Absolute has stripped away centuries of civilizational progress to reveal an unchanged human capacity for sacred violence. The Crusaders do not fight with swords and lances but with Karburator-powered dreadnoughts and fifty-four-centimeter shells — the same technology that once "lit the whole city of Prague" now "razed cities to the ground."
