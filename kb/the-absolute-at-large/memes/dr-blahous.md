**Dr. Blahous** is a Lecturer in Comparative Religion at the University of Prague who appears in Chapter XII of Karel Čapek's *The Absolute at Large*. "That youthful savant Doctor Blahous, Ph.D., only fifty-five years of age" [(012-005)], Blahous represents the academic establishment's response to the Absolute phenomenon — a response that begins as careerist calculation and ends in mystical transformation. His chapter is one of the novel's most concentrated satires of intellectual life: a scholar who sets out to explain away religious experience as "barbaric atavism" [(012-007)] and ends up writing that "God must begin again from the beginning" [].

## Overview

Dr. Blahous is Čapek's portrait of the academic as self-interested careerist who accidentally stumbles into genuine religious experience. At the start of Chapter XII, he sits down to write "a very topical little article" on the religious epidemic that will later engulf the world in the [[the-greatest-war|Greatest War]], confident it will secure him an Assistant Professorship and make "old Regner burst with fury" [(012-011)]. He writes 346 pages of scholarly dismissal — classifying Kuzendism as fetish-worship, the Binderians as Dancing Dervishes, the whole phenomenon as "barbaric atavism" — before leaning out his window at 4:30 a.m. and experiencing the Absolute's touch. The rest of his article, written in a state of rapture, contradicts everything he wrote before.

## Role & Significance

### The Careerist Scholar

Blahous's initial motivation is nakedly instrumental. His internal monologue as he begins his article reveals a man for whom the religious crisis is an opportunity, not a mystery:

> "I'll send this article to the *Prague Times*," he said to himself, "and just you wait, my revered colleagues, and see what a stir it will make! It's lucky for me that this religious epidemic has broken out just now! It will make it a very topical little article. The papers will say, 'That youthful savant, Dr. Blahous, has just published a penetrating study,' etc. Then I'll be given the Assistant Professorship, and old Regner will burst with fury." [(012-011)]

The irony is layered. Blahous is fifty-five — called "youthful" by the narrator with obvious sarcasm — and still chasing the promotion he should have had decades ago. His article runs to 346 pages, not because the subject demands it, but because academic ambition inflates everything it touches. He underlines the word "modern" in his own manuscript, a tic of scholarly vanity [(012-015)].

### The Scientific Dismissal

Blahous's analysis of the religious movements is a catalogue of reductionist explanation. He finds traces of "fetish-worship and even totemism" in Kuzendism (the dredge being "a sort of Totem God of Stechovice"), links the Binderians to "Dancing Dervishes and ancient orgiastic cults," connects the Power Station phenomena to "fire-worship of the Parsees," and identifies Machat's community with "fakirs and ascetics" [(012-006)].

> "It is not for us to decide," Dr. Blahous went on writing, "to what extent this is due to quackery and imposture on the part of individuals bent on exploiting human credulity; a scientific inquiry would doubtless show that the alleged 'miracles' of the thaumaturgists of today are only old and well-known devices of trickery and suggestion. ... The exact science of religion confines itself to establishing the fact that all these religious phenomena are at bottom nothing but examples of barbaric atavism, and a hotchpotch of the most rudimentary forms of worship still subconsciously active in the human imagination." [(012-007)]

He recommends the new sects to "the attention of our police authorities and psychoanalysts" — a sentence that perfectly captures the alliance between academic authority and state power that Čapek satirizes throughout the novel.

### The Window at 4:30 a.m.

The chapter's turning point is Blahous's experience at the window. Having finished his 346-page dismissal, he leans out into the night:

> The Lecturer raised his eyes to the sky. It was already paling a little, but it still shone in its infinite sublimity, sown with stars. "How long it is since I looked at the sky!" came suddenly into the scholar's mind. "Good heavens, it is more than thirty years!" [(012-009)]

And then:

> And then he felt a delicious coolness about his brow, as though someone had taken his head in cool and spotless hands. "I'm so lonely," the old man sighed, "so terribly lonely all the time! Yes, stroke my hair a little. Alas, it is thirty long years since anyone's hand was laid upon my brow!" []

> Dr. Blahous stood there in the window, stiff and shaking. "There is something here all about me," he suddenly perceived with a sweet and overwhelming emotion. "Dear God, I am not alone after all! Someone's arm is around me, someone is beside me; oh, if he would only stay!" [(012-011)]

This is one of the most genuinely moving passages in the novel. Stripped of irony, Čapek presents an old man who has spent thirty years studying religion without once looking at the sky — and who, in a moment of pure openness, finds what he has been analyzing from a distance. The scholarly apparatus collapses; what remains is a human being, alone and touched by grace.

### The Second Half of the Article

After this experience, Blahous returns to his desk and writes the conclusion that contradicts everything that came before:

> "On the other hand, however, it is impossible to doubt," he wrote rapidly, heedless of all that he had already written, "that God cannot now reveal Himself otherwise than in primitive forms of worship. With the decay of faith in modern times our connection with the old religious life has been broken. To bring us back to Him, God must begin again from the beginning and do as He did with the savages in olden times ... This evolution of religion is being repeated before our eyes, beginning with its prehistoric forms and working upwards to the loftier types. ... Nevertheless I say unto you: Give yourself up with your whole beings to the Absolute; believe in God, in whatsoever form He may declare Himself to you. Behold even now He cometh to set up on our earth, and perchance on other planets of our system also, the everlasting Empire of God, the Czardom of the Absolute. Ere it is too late, I say yet again unto you: Humble yourselves before Him!" []

The transformation is complete: the scholar who dismissed religious experience as atavism now preaches it in prophetic tones.

### The Irony of Publication

The chapter's final irony is its cruellest. Blahous's article appears, but "not in its entirety" — the editor publishes his dismissive analysis of the sects and his prophetic conclusion, with "a cautious note to the effect that this paper by the youthful savant was certainly characteristic of our times" [(012-014)]. The result is that Blahous repudiates himself in print.

The final judgment comes from his rival:

> Only that youthful savant, Dr. Regner, Lecturer in Philosophy, read it with immense interest, and afterwards proclaimed in various places: "Blahous is impossible. Utterly impossible. How on earth can a man have the nerve to pose as an expert on religion when he actually believes in God?" [(012-015)]

## Appearances

### Chapter XII — Doctor Blahous

The entirety of Blahous's major appearance. He writes his article, experiences the Absolute at the window, and rewrites his conclusion [(012-001)--(012-015)].

### Chapter XIII — The Chronicler's Apology

A brief farewell from the narrator: "Of you, too, Dr. Blahous, I must take my leave. I should like to spend many a year with you and describe your whole career ... Give my regards to your landlady at least" [(013-005)]. This is Čapek's wink to the reader — he could write more about Blahous, but must move on.

### Chapter XXX — The End of Everything

In the novel's closing coda, Mr. Brych reads aloud that Blahous has finally been appointed Assistant Professor — the promotion he schemed for at the chapter's opening. The irony is gentle: Blahous's career has advanced, but the world of the Absolute has vanished.

## Relationships

### Dr. Regner

Blahous's academic rival, a Lecturer in Philosophy. Blahous imagines Regner "bursting with fury" at his success. Regner's final judgment — that Blahous "actually believes in God" — is the novel's punchline about academic culture: belief in God is a disqualification for the academic study of religion.

### Grandfather Blahous and Mrs. Blahous

Dr. Blahous's parents, simple country folk living in the village of Seven Cottages. In Chapter XXVIII, they sit out the Greatest War peeling potatoes and smoking beech-leaves, completely oblivious to their son's intellectual world. Grandfather Blahous dismisses the entire war as a conspiracy "fixed up with the Pope and the Jews" and declares that "the old [God] was good enough for us country people" [(028-020)]. The contrast between the son's elaborate scholarly apparatus and the father's rustic skepticism is one of Čapek's gentler jokes.

### Jan Binder and Kuzenda

Blahous's article mentions both [[jan-binder-kuzenda|Kuzenda]] and Binder's religious communities, classifying them among the "barbaric atavisms." In the novel's epilogue, Brych recalls being called "the Apostle of Kuzenda's Sect" in Blahous's article [(030-005)].
