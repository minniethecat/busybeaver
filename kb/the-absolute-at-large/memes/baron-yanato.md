**Baron Yanato** is the Japanese Ambassador who attends the secret Conference of the Supreme Council of the Great Powers on the remote island of St. Kilda in Karel Čapek's *The Absolute at Large*. Distinguished by his impenetrable smile and polite refusal of all liquid refreshment, Baron Yanato delivers one of the novel's most economically devastating satirical lines: the Absolute "is a great Japanese" [(020-020)]. Through Yanato, Čapek exposes how nationalist co-optation of religious truth transforms theological debate into diplomatic leverage — and how the most dangerous claims are delivered with the most exquisite courtesy.

## Overview

Baron Yanato appears in a single chapter — Chapter XX ("St. Kilda") — but his presence dominates the Conference scene. While the other diplomats argue, bluster, and grow red-faced, Yanato maintains his composed smile, speaking only four times. Each utterance is a variation on the same theme: the Absolute belongs to Japan, and Japan would be happy to share its faith with the world. His calm amid the diplomatic chaos underscores the novel's suggestion that all claims to possess the Absolute are equally absurd, equally self-serving, and equally dangerous.

## Role & Significance

### The Smile as Weapon

Yanato's defining characteristic is his smile. He refuses all liquid refreshment and "listened silently and smiled as though his mouth were full of water" [(020-015)], while [[horatio-bumm|Horatio Bumm]] plays billiards alone and [[dr-wurm|Dr. Wurm]] turns over his papers. This impenetrability is strategic: while the other diplomats expose themselves through argument and emotion, Yanato reveals nothing. The smile is a diplomatic tool, a screen behind which nationalist ambition operates undisturbed.

### The Claim

His first intervention transforms the entire discussion:

> Baron Yanato then said smilingly: "In my country He is quite at home. A very, very nice God. He has adapted Himself very well. Indeed, a very great Japanese." [(020-020)]

Each word is chosen for maximum effect. "Quite at home" — the Absolute is comfortable in Japan, naturalized. "Has adapted Himself very well" — the Absolute, unlike in the quarrelsome West, has found harmony with Japanese culture. "A very great Japanese" — not merely a God who happens to be in Japan, but a Japanese God, a national deity. The claim is total and exclusive, delivered with the mildness of a compliment.

### The Missionary Threat

Yanato's subsequent interventions escalate from claim to demand:

> Baron Yanato smiled still more politely. "But we do not wish for neutrality. He is a great Japanese. The whole world can adopt the Japanese faith. We, too, would like to send out missionaries for once, and teach religion." [(020-027)]

The phrase "for once" carries the weight of history: Japan, long the recipient of Western missionary activity, would now export its own faith. When [[sir-w-opatterney|Sir W. O'Patterney]] protests about Anglo-Japanese relations, Yanato responds:

> "England can adopt the Japanese faith," smiled Baron Yanato, "and our relations will be even better." [(020-025)]

This is diplomatic extortion wrapped in courtesy:

> "England can adopt the Japanese faith," smiled Baron Yanato, "and our relations will be even better." [(020-025)]

The threat — that good relations depend on religious submission — is the same logic behind every imperial venture in the novel, but stated with a candor the Western diplomats cannot match.

### The Final Exit

When the Conference collapses — General Buchtin rushing out purple-faced, Dr. Wurm agitated, Sir W. O'Patterney red-faced with his hat on — Yanato remains composed:

> Prince Trivelino walked away, looking very pale, Baron Yanato at his heels with his perpetual smile. [(020-035)]

The "perpetual smile" is the chapter's closing image. The Conference has failed; no agreement has been reached; the St. Kilda agreement will later be "repudiated all round." But Yanato's smile — the mask of nationalist diplomacy — survives unchanged.

### Later References

The "St. Kilda agreement" is later reported as "being repudiated all round" [(018-040)], confirming the Conference's complete failure. Japan's subsequent role in the Greatest War — the Japanese fleet sailing against America, landing troops in California and British Columbia — demonstrates the militarization of the nationalist claims first articulated at St. Kilda.

## Relationships

### Prince Trivelino

The Italian Minister with whom Yanato walks during the intermission and at whose heels he departs. Their pairing suggests a Mediterranean–Far Eastern axis within the Conference, though no alliance is explicitly stated.

### General Buchtin

Yanato's primary ideological rival at the Conference. Buchtin's blustering Russian Orthodox absolutism — "If there's to be a religion, then it's to be our Orthodox religion" — is the antithesis of Yanato's smiling Japanese nationalist claim. Buchtin's purple-faced rage and Yanato's unflappable calm make them a perfect comic pair.

### Sir W. O'Patterney

The Conference host, caught between Yanato's polite demands and his own Liberal preference for avoiding religious entanglements. Yanato's suggestion that England could "adopt the Japanese faith" is aimed directly at O'Patterney's authority.

### Dr. Wurm

The German Chancellor represents a different form of nationalist appropriation — the "State Absolute" — but Yanato's approach is more elegant. Where Wurm argues, Yanato simply smiles and claims.

### The Absolute

Yanato treats the Absolute not as theological reality but as national asset. His claim that the Absolute "has adapted Himself very well" to Japan is the novel's purest expression of nationalist co-optation: the divine becomes whatever the nation needs it to be.

## Narrative Significance

The St. Kilda Conference is Čapek's most concentrated satire of international diplomacy, and Baron Yanato is its perfect instrument. Each diplomat claims the Absolute for his own nation — the Russian General insists He is Orthodox, the German Chancellor calls Him a "State Absolute," the American Ambassador describes a sports-loving, Prohibition-defying God — but Yanato's formulation is the most elegantly brutal. He does not argue theology; he simply claims the Absolute as Japanese, with a smile that never wavers.

Čapek's point is that religious imperialism and political imperialism are indistinguishable once the divine becomes a national asset. Yanato's proposal to send "missionaries for once" — to reverse the historical flow of Western proselytism — exposes the power dynamics underlying all missionary activity. The chapter's failure to produce any agreement demonstrates the novel's thesis: when every nation claims exclusive possession of the Absolute, the result is not religious unity but total war.
