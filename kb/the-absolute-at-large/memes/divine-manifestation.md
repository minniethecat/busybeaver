# Divine Manifestation

**Divine manifestation** is the central metaphysical concept of Čapek's novel — the idea that God (the Absolute) is physically present in all matter and can be released through complete atomic combustion.

## Philosophical Foundation

Marek explains the concept through Western philosophy: Spinoza taught that matter is "only the outward manifestation, only one phase of the divine substance, the other phase of which is spirit"; Fechner taught that everything "is penetrated with the divine, that God fills with His being the whole of the matter in the world"; Leibniz proposed that "physical matter is composed of psychical atoms, monads, whose nature is divine".

## Mechanical Release

The Karburator achieves what saints and mystics accomplished through spiritual discipline — the separation of the divine from the material:

> "Christ and the miracle-workers, fakirs, mediums, and prophets have achieved it by means of their psychic power. My Karburator does it by a purely mechanical process. It acts, you might say, as a factory for the Absolute."

## Contractual Negotiation

Bondy pragmatically suggests negotiating a formal contract with the Absolute: "We guarantee to produce You discreetly and continuously ... in return for which You pledge yourself to refrain from any divine manifestations within such and such a radius from the place of origin" — a satirical proposal to commodify divinity.
