**Rudolf Marek** is the inventor of the [[the-karburator|Karburator]] and, alongside [[g-h-bondy|G. H. Bondy]], one of the two central protagonists of Karel Čapek's *The Absolute at Large*. A former classmate of Bondy's at the Technical School, Marek is portrayed as a man of genuine scientific genius, doomed by a sensitive conscience that recoils from the consequences of his own creation. Having spent twenty years perfecting the complete combustion of matter, he discovers too late that his machine not only annihilates matter but releases its divine content — the Absolute — as an uncontrollable industrial byproduct (002-005).

## Role and Significance

Marek embodies the novel's central tragic irony: the greatest technical achievement in human history turns out to be the unwitting manufacture of God. Čapek draws him as neither hero nor villain but as a Faustian figure — the scientist who achieves what he sought, only to be horrified by what he has done.

> (002-005) Marek: "For twenty years I've been working on it, and now — now, I'll sell it to the first man who comes along! My magnificent dream! The greatest invention of all the ages!"

His dual identity as both "engineer" and "prophet" — he begins performing miracles and having visions as the Absolute saturates his Břevnov factory — satirizes the twentieth-century scientist's transition from rationalism to unwitting priesthood:

> (003-004) Marek: "I had moments of astounding illumination. I saw, for instance, quite clearly, things that would happen in the future. I predicted even your visit here. And once I tore my nail off on a lathe. I looked at the damaged finger, and all at once a new nail grew on it."

Unlike Bondy, who views the Karburator through the lens of capital, Marek is driven by pure scientific ambition — and pure terror at its results. He desperately warns Bondy:

> (003-001) Marek: "Bondy, I warn you for the last time. It means letting God loose upon this world!"

## Character Arc

Marek's trajectory across the novel can be understood in five distinct phases:

### Phase 1: The Obscure Inventor (Chapter I)

When the novel opens, Marek is an anonymous figure — known only through a cryptic newspaper advertisement: "Highly remunerative, suitable for any factory, for immediate sale, personal reasons" (001-009). Bondy, reminiscing about his old classmate, imagines him as a fallen genius in a garret, and the novel's central dramatic irony is seeded by Bondy's romantic fantasy of the impoverished inventor. The reality — Marek as proprietor of a tidy electric lamp factory in Břevnov — punctures Bondy's expectations and establishes Marek's complexity: he is neither martyr nor failed genius, but something harder to categorize.

Marek's prophetic foreknowledge already manifests when Bondy arrives: "Well, so you've come at last, Bondy! I've been expecting you!" (001-029). He has known Bondy would come — the first sign that his proximity to the Karburator has granted him supernatural perception.

### Phase 2: The Desperate Salesman (Chapter II)

From the moment Bondy sits down, Marek speaks like a man possessed. He interrupts, ignores pleasantries, and oscillates between frantic enthusiasm and bitter despair (002-001)–(002-005). He drags Bondy to the cellar to witness the Karburator, then — wearing a protective helmet — hauls him out when Bondy falls into ecstatic transport (002-020). His emotional volatility throughout this chapter reveals a man who has already been spiritually shattered by his own creation: he cannot bear to be near it, yet cannot bring himself to destroy it.

### Phase 3: The Reluctant Prophet (Chapters III–IV)

In the novel's most thematically dense chapters, Marek transitions from inventor to theologian, delivering a lucid explanation of pantheistic metaphysics as an engineering problem: "all matter contains the Absolute in some state of confinement ... when you utterly destroy a piece of matter ... you're left with an indestructible residue — free and active Absolute" (003-001). His confession of the miracles he has performed — levitation, nail regeneration, clairvoyance (003-004)–(003-010) — represents the scientist's nightmare: becoming the very kind of irrational mystic he once dismissed.

> (003-003) Marek: "I don't believe in anything. I don't want to believe. I have always been an atheist. I believed in matter and in progress and in nothing else. I'm a scientific man, Bondy; and science cannot admit the existence of God."

Marek's atheism is his tragedy. He is a man who spent his life disbelieving in God, only to manufacture Him in his cellar. His description of the porter — "a thoroughly hardheaded chap, a monist and a freethinker" — becoming a faith-healer (004-003) mirrors his own trajectory. The Karburator converts atheists into saints whether they want it or not, and Marek's frantic attempts to contain the Absolute — sand, metal walls, even "the works of Professor Krejči, Spencer, Haeckel, and all the Positivists" (004-003) — are the novel's sharpest satire of positivism's impotence before the divine.

### Phase 4: The Converted Industrialist (Chapter V)

By the time Marek sits in Bondy's office a fortnight after New Year's, his position has shifted from horrified inventor to ambivalent participant in the Karburator's commercialization. He has not returned to Břevnov for two weeks — "I daren't go there" (005-001) — yet when Bishop Linda arrives to negotiate the Church's franchise over the cellar, Marek erupts into sudden enthusiasm for mass production:

> (005-010) Marek: "Would you mind leaving the laws of Nature to us? After all, we don't interfere with your dogmas!"

> (005-012) Marek: "And I tell you, the Karburators *shall* be made. I'm in favour of them myself now. I mean to have them precisely because you don't want them. In spite of you, my Lord Bishop, in spite of all superstition, in spite of all Rome! And I mean to be the first to cry — Success to the Perfect Karburator!"

This dramatic reversal — from the man who begged Bondy not to commercialize the Karburator to the man who champions it against ecclesiastical opposition — reveals Marek's fundamental character: he is a scientist first, and ecclesiastical authority offends him more deeply than divine catastrophe. His anti-clericalism overrides his conscience.

### Phase 5: The Weary Observer (Chapters XVI, XXI)

Marek's final active role occurs at the Hut in Bear Valley, high in the Giant Mountains, where he has retreated from the world the Karburator is destroying. Here, in the novel's most reflective passage, he appears curled on a veranda, studying newspapers and tracking the Absolute's global progress with the detached interest of a scientist observing a natural phenomenon:

> (016-005) "How pure the air is here! Here, Heaven be praised, the Absolute is still latent, it still lies under a spell, hidden in everything, in these mountains and forests, in the sweet grass and the blue sky."

When Bondy arrives — broken, yellowed, having barely fought off his own religious conversion — Marek listens without sentiment and delivers his final diagnosis: the Absolute is not a master strategist but a cosmic improviser. "He doesn't know Himself what He wants and how to get it ... So far He's only a force of Nature. Politically, He's a fearful ignoramus. In the matter of economics He's a simple savage" (016-008). Marek has become the world's only expert on the divine — an atheist theologian, analyzing God's incompetence with clinical precision:

> (016-009) Marek: "He has flashes of genius, but He is unsystematic. I'm surprised that you haven't got the better of Him, Bondy, a wideawake fellow like you."

Marek's final words to Bondy — "Come closer, Bondy, and prick up your ears" (016-009) — suggest that his study of the Absolute is ongoing, his curiosity undimmed even as civilization collapses. He has moved from horror through complicity to philosophical detachment: the inventor watching his invention reshape the world from a safe height.

A brief coda occurs in Chapter XXI, when a telegraph messenger braves a blizzard to deliver Bondy's telegram — "Your predictions confirmed" — to Marek at the Hut (021-010). Marek, alone in the mountains, has become a distant oracle, consulted by the very industrialist who once dismissed his warnings.

**Key scenes**:

- **Chapter I — The Advertisement**: Marek places an anonymous newspaper advertisement (001-009) that draws Bondy to Břevnov after a twenty-year estrangement. Their reunion establishes the novel's central dynamic: the inventor who can no longer bear what he has made, and the industrialist who sees only profit (001-029).

- **Chapter II — The Karburator**: Marek demonstrates the Karburator to Bondy, dragging him into the cellar where Bondy experiences his first ecstatic episode (002-020). The scene unfolds as a reversal: the inventor showing his creation to the capitalist, who falls to his knees in religious transport.

- **Chapter III — Pantheism**: Marek delivers the novel's core theological-scientific exposition: "all matter contains the Absolute in some state of confinement ... when you utterly destroy a piece of matter ... you're left with an indestructible residue — free and active Absolute" (003-001). His confession of performing miracles and prophecies — levitation, nail regeneration, mind-reading (003-004)–(003-010) — establishes the comedy of the scientist turned involuntary saint.

- **Chapter V — Bishop Linda**: Marek confronts the Church's attempt to negotiate a "franchise" over the Břevnov cellar. His outburst — "Would you mind leaving the laws of Nature to us? After all, we don't interfere with your dogmas!" (005-010) — dramatizes the collision between scientific and ecclesiastical authority.

- **Chapter XVI — In the Mountains**: Marek's final extended appearance, at the Hut in Bear Valley (016-005)–(016-009). Bondy arrives broken after fighting off his own religious conversion, and Marek analyzes the Absolute's global strategy with the detachment of a scientist studying a failed experiment. His diagnosis — that the Absolute is an improviser, not a planner — is the novel's last word on the divine.

- **Chapter XXI — The Telegram**: A messenger braves a blizzard from Spindelmühl to deliver Bondy's one-line telegram to Marek: "Your predictions confirmed" (021-010). The scene serves as a brief but resonant epilogue to the Marek-Bondy relationship.

### The Marek-Bondy Dynamic

The relationship between Marek and Bondy is the novel's dramatic engine. Their first exchange in twenty years — "I've been expecting you!" / "And so you want to buy my invention" (001-029)–(002-001) — establishes a pattern that persists throughout: Marek anticipates, Bondy reacts; Marek warns, Bondy calculates. They are a study in complementary blindness: Bondy cannot see the spiritual catastrophe brewing, and Marek cannot see how to stop the commercial machine he has set in motion.

Their final exchange at the Hut in Bear Valley (016-005)–(016-009) inverts their initial positions. Bondy, who once dismissed Marek's warnings as hysteria, now arrives terrified by the Absolute's strategic cunning. Marek, who once begged Bondy to take his warnings seriously, now dismisses the Absolute as a cosmic amateur. Each has arrived at the truth the other began with.

## Interpretations

1. **The Faustian Scientist**: Marek is Čapek's portrait of the twentieth-century technologist — brilliant, driven, and morally unequipped for the consequences of his work. His "twenty years" of solitary labor (002-005) mirrors Faust's bargain, but the devil he summons is God. Unlike Faust, Marek receives no offer of redemption; he simply retreats to the mountains to study the catastrophe.

2. **The Unwilling Prophet**: Marek's transformation from atheist ("I have always been an atheist. I believed in matter and in progress and in nothing else" (003-003)) to miracle-worker satirizes the scientist's trajectory toward the very mysticism he sought to dispel. He becomes, against his will, the first saint of the Atomic Age.

3. **The Conscience of Progress**: While Bondy represents capitalism's blind appetite, Marek represents the scientific conscience — the voice that asks "should we?" rather than "can we?" His warning — "It means letting God loose upon this world!" (003-001) — is the novel's moral center, made all the more poignant by his eventual complicity in the very catastrophe he predicted.

4. **The Atheist Theologian**: Marek's final role as detached analyst of the divine (016-007)–(016-009) represents Čapek's most subtle irony: the only person in the world who truly understands God is the man who refuses to believe in Him. Marek studies the Absolute as he would study any natural phenomenon — recording its behavior, cataloguing its failures, and ultimately concluding that it is not a planner but a force, not a strategist but a cosmic amateur.
