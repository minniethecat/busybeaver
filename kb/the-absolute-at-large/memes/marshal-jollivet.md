## Overview

Marshal Jollivet is a minor character in Karel Čapek's *The Absolute at Large*, a French military officer who serves on the General Staff of Emperor Bobinet. He appears in two brief satirical anecdotes in Chapter XXIV that parody official hagiography and the cult of personality surrounding military leaders (024-001).

## Role & Significance

Marshal Jollivet functions as a straight-man foil in Čapek's satire of wartime propaganda. The two anecdotes in which he appears — "Bobinet and his Mother" and "Bobinet and Love of Country" — are presented in the style of official communiqués or hagiographic vignettes. Jollivet's role in these stories is to serve as the deferential subordinate whose reactions (tears, righteous indignation) validate the supposed virtues of the Emperor. He is the audience within the text, modelling the emotional response that the propaganda expects from its readers (024-001).

## Appearances

Marshal Jollivet appears in Chapter XXIV ("The Napoleon of the Mountain Brigade"), in two anecdotal vignettes that satirize the cult of Emperor Bobinet.

In the first anecdote, "Bobinet and his Mother," Jollivet is moved to tears by Bobinet's filial devotion:

> One day Bobinet was holding council at Versailles with his General Staff. As the day was hot, he had taken his place by an open window. Suddenly he noticed an aged woman in the park, warming herself in the sun. Bobinet at once interrupted Marshal Jollivet with a cry of "Look, gentlemen... my mother!" All present, even the most hardened generals, were moved to tears by this demonstration of filial affection. (024-001)

In the second, "Bobinet and Love of Country," Jollivet attempts to punish a soldier for splashing mud on the Emperor:

> Marshal Jollivet wished to punish the commander of the unfortunate battery by reducing him in rank on the spot. But Bobinet restrained him, saying, "Let him alone, Marshal. After all, this is the mud of France!" (024-002)

## Relationships

- **[[emperor-bobinet|Emperor Bobinet]]** — Jollivet's supreme commander, whom he serves with unquestioning devotion. Jollivet's instinct to harshly punish any slight against the Emperor is checked only by Bobinet's own magnanimity.
