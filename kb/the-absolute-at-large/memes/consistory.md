## Overview

The Consistory is the ecclesiastical council that governs the Catholic Church in Prague. In *The Absolute at Large*, the Consistory — through its agent Bishop Linda — attempts to negotiate a formal relationship with the Absolute, proposing to place it "under its patronage" and to declare Marek's Břevnov cellar a shrine. The Consistory's intervention represents the institutional Church's attempt to absorb and regulate the spontaneous spiritual phenomena unleashed by the Karburator.

## Role in the Novel

### The Negotiation

Bishop Linda, representing the Consistory, presents a detailed proposal to Bondy:

> The venerable Consistory decided that in the interests of the Church it would place the Absolute provisionally under its patronage. It would attempt to bring it into harmony with Catholic doctrine. It would proclaim the premises in Břevnov known as No. 1651 a miraculous shrine and place of pilgrimage.

The Consistory's approach is characteristically bureaucratic: the Absolute is to be "brought into harmony with Catholic doctrine," as if divine reality could be edited to conform to institutional theology. The proposal to establish a shrine at the Břevnov cellar — the industrial workshop where Marek invented the Karburator — is a bid to claim the site of the Absolute's first manifestation as Church property.

### The Male Svantovice Connection

The Consistory further specifies that the Karburator "is to be manufactured only from coal obtained at Male Svantovice," noting that "there is a miraculous shrine of the Virgin in that district, so that with the aid of this particular coal we might establish at No. 1651 Břevnov a centre for the worship of Our Lady". This detail — the attempt to link the Karburator's fuel supply to an existing Marian shrine — reveals the Consistory's strategy: absorption of the new divine phenomenon into the existing apparatus of Catholic devotion.

### The One Exception

When the Břevnov Karburator is ultimately shut down along with all others, "the one at Břevnov... remains the property of the Holy Church, and will be under her management". The Consistory succeeds, in the end, in claiming at least one Karburator as Church property — a victory of institutional persistence over industrial logic.

## Thematic Significance

The Consistory represents the institutional Church's response to genuine divine manifestation — not awe or repentance, but negotiation and institutional capture. The proposal to "bring it into harmony with Catholic doctrine" is Čapek's satire of ecclesiastical bureaucracy: an institution so confident in its own categories that it assumes the Absolute can be edited to fit them. The Consistory treats God not as a mystery to be honored but as a resource to be managed — which makes it, in its own way, no different from the M.E.C. board that treats the Absolute as an industrial byproduct.
