# Holiness

**Holiness** in Čapek's *The Absolute at Large* is a state of spiritual transformation experienced by those who come into contact with the Absolute through the Karburator. Unlike traditional religious notions of sanctity requiring institutional mediation, this holiness is democratized — available to anyone, regardless of their moral history or ecclesiastical standing.

## Direct Experience of Holiness

The most striking early manifestation of holiness occurs during a board meeting at the Břevnov monastery, where the industrialist Machat experiences the Absolute's transformative power firsthand:

> "If you only knew how wonderful it feels to be in a state of holiness! Gentlemen, take my sincere advice. Give away all that you possess! Become poor and holy! Deliver yourselves from Mammon, and glorify the One God!"

Machat's outburst is particularly significant because he is a businessman — a figure one would least expect to advocate poverty and holiness. The Absolute erases social roles and professional identities, replacing them with spiritual fervor.

## Holiness Without the Church

The novel repeatedly emphasizes that the holiness produced by the Absolute operates outside traditional ecclesiastical structures. Bishop Linda acknowledges this tension when he confronts Bondy about the Karburator, declaring that the Absolute "contradicts the doctrine of the holy sacraments" and "does not regard the traditions of the Church". The institutional Church recognizes holiness but claims a monopoly on its dispensation — a monopoly the Absolute shatters.
