## Role in the novel

Mr. Cvancara of the *Venkov* appears in Chapter II as one of the journalists invited to witness Marek's Karburator demonstration [823-PN]:

> "Old Cvancara of the *Venkov* was walking pensively up and down in front of the insignificant affair, no bigger than a public convenience."

He reappears in Chapter IX at the ceremonial opening of the Central Karburator Electric Power Station at Stvanice, where he serves as a foil to the younger journalist Cyril Keval of the *People's Journal* [823-PN]. Across both appearances, Cvancara embodies the voice of institutional scepticism — a career newsman who has witnessed too many claimed breakthroughs to be easily impressed.

## Scepticism and unease

Cvancara's defining trait is his refusal to accept the Karburator at face value. When the younger Keval enthuses about the machine's capacity to light all of Prague, Cvancara responds with the weary authority of experience [829-PN]:

> "Mr. Cvancara shook his head sceptically. 'We'll see, my young friend, we'll see. Nothing nowadays can surprise any of us of the old guard.'"

His scepticism is not dogmatic but experiential — he has seen too many claimed breakthroughs to be easily impressed. However, the Karburator's effects soon overcome even his professional caution. He whispers privately [829-PN]:

> "'Well, just look round and see if you can find something to eat; perhaps there's some sausage or something left.'"

The deflation from cosmic significance to hunger is classic Čapek — even journalists confronting the most important invention in history still need lunch.

## The veteran journalist at Stvanice

At the Stvanice ceremony, Cvancara's role shifts from participant to observer. While Keval scrambles for a view through the window, Cvancara patrols the perimeter "looking somewhat like a meditative heron" [823-PN]. His practical concern is emblematic of the old-guard press mentality [829-PN]:

> "'They haven't even got a reserve Karburator handy. Suppose this one broke down, or even, say, went up in the air, what then — do you see what I mean?'"

When Keval spins an elaborate fabrication about underground military Karburator fortresses, Cvancara punctures it with a single line of institutional memory [833-PN]:

> "'They've been doing it these forty years,' said Mr. Cvancara gloomily."

His deflation of Keval's fantasy with forty years of observed municipal incompetence is one of the novel's most economical character moments — the old journalist who has seen every scheme, every grand promise, and every disappointing outcome.

## The ceremony's climax

By the time the ceremony reaches its fateful conclusion, Cvancara has faded into the background. Keval, peering through the window during the climactic moment of the Absolute's eruption, sees only "the head of Mr. Cvancara could be seen nodding drowsily" at the back of the room [855-PN]. Whether Cvancara's drowsiness was genuine fatigue or an early symptom of the Absolute's psychological effects is left ambiguous — fitting for a character whose defining quality is the refusal to commit to any single interpretation of events.

## Narrative function

Cvancara represents the institutional press confronting the inexplicable. His initial scepticism mirrors society's broader resistance to the Absolute's effects, while his eventual submission to mundane bodily needs underscores the novel's thesis that transcendent experience cannot fully displace ordinary human appetites. As the doyen of the press corps, he also serves as a structural device — his presence at both the Marek demonstration and the Stvanice ceremony links these two key episodes in the Absolute's public emergence.
