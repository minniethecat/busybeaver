## Overview

The Monist Association appears in *The Absolute at Large* as one of many religious and philosophical organizations that rush to claim the Absolute for their own doctrinal frameworks. Unlike the established churches, which view the Absolute as competition or heresy, the Monists see it as vindication: the Absolute *is* the Unity that Monism has always identified with God. Their swift public assertion of this claim — and their subsequent literal construction of a temple to the "Atomic God" in Leipzig — embodies Čapek's satire of how quickly every ideology co-opts the inexplicable into its existing categories.

## Structure

The novel distinguishes between a local (presumably Czechoslovak) Monist Association and the German Monist Association, suggesting a federated or national-chapter structure. The local Association issues a communication that is reported in the press alongside statements from other religious bodies (018-044). The German Monist Association, operating from Leipzig, appears to be the more organizationally ambitious branch, undertaking the construction of the Cathedral of the Atomic God (019-014).

The broader intellectual context of Monism in the novel is established early: Cyril Keval's porter was "a monist and a freethinker" before his sudden conversion to miracle-working religiosity under the Absolute's influence (004-014). The Masonic Grand Lodge, in its strategic review of available doctrines, lists "monism" alongside abstinence, Fletcherism, and vegetarianism as belief systems worth examining for potential exploitation (017-031).

## Role in the Novel

### The Monist Claim (Chapter XVIII)

In Chapter XVIII, amid a flurry of religious activity triggered by the Absolute, the Monist Association issues a public communication asserting doctrinal ownership:

> "This morning, my reverend friend, there appeared first of all a communication from the Monist Association, asserting that the Absolute is that Unity which the Monists have always proclaimed to be God, and that therefore the cult of the Absolute is in complete correspondence with the doctrine of Monism." (018-044)

The claim is characteristically Monist: the Absolute is not a *new* revelation but confirmation of what Monism has always taught. In a novel where every institution scrambles to appropriate the Absolute, the Monists' argument is perhaps the most intellectually coherent — the Absolute *is* a single, all-pervading divine substance, exactly what Monism posits. The irony, as Čapek intends, is that being "right" about the Absolute's nature does not prevent the Monists from behaving exactly like every other religious faction: territorial, self-aggrandizing, and oblivious to the absurdity of building a cathedral to metaphysical unity.

### The Cathedral of the Atomic God (Chapter XIX)

The German Monist Association's most concrete action is the ceremonial laying of the foundation-stone for the future Cathedral of the Atomic God in Leipzig:

> At about the same time the German Monist Association laid in Leipzig, with great pomp and ceremony, the foundation-stone of the future Cathedral of the Atomic God. There was some disturbance, in which sixteen persons were injured and Lüttgen, the famous physicist, had his spectacles smashed. (019-014)

The passage is quintessential Čapek: a philosophical movement dedicated to rational unity celebrates its triumph with a religious ceremony indistinguishable from that of any traditional church, and the event immediately descends into physical violence. The injury of Lüttgen, a "famous physicist" who loses his spectacles, is a particularly deft touch — science itself is literally blinded by the enthusiasm of its would-be adherents.

### Background Threads

The Monist presence in the novel predates the Absolute crisis. Early in the story, Keval reports that his porter — "a thoroughly hardheaded chap, a monist and a freethinker" — was among the first to manifest Absolute-induced religious behavior, "healing people by laying on of hands" (004-014). Later, the Masonic Grand Lodge debates whether to co-opt Monism as one of several doctrines to be "tested out practically" for its utility to the craft (017-031). These details establish Monism as part of the era's intellectual furniture — a respectable secular worldview that, like every other worldview in the novel, proves helpless before the Absolute.

## Thematic Significance

The Monist Association serves as Čapek's vehicle for satirizing the boundary between science and religion. Monism, as a nineteenth-century philosophical movement (associated with Ernst Haeckel and others), claimed to replace supernatural religion with a scientific understanding of reality as a unified substance — yet in the novel, the Monists immediately revert to the same institutional behaviors as the churches they supposedly transcend: issuing dogmatic statements, constructing temples, and fighting over territory. The "Atomic God" is a self-contradicting concept that captures the novel's central absurdity: a technological accident has produced a theological crisis, and the people best positioned to understand the technology are least equipped to handle its consequences.

The smashing of Lüttgen's spectacles — the physicist blinded at his own movement's ceremony — is a miniature allegory of the entire novel: reason is the first casualty when reason's conclusions are elevated to the status of revelation.
