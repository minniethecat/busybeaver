## Who

I. Sawitt was an American journalist and foreign correspondent for the *New York Herald*.
He is notable as the only reporter who successfully infiltrated the top-secret diplomatic
conference on St. Kilda, providing the sole surviving account of that historic assembly.

## Role in the Novel

Sawitt appears in Chapter XX, during the international conference convened on the remote
island of St. Kilda to address the Absolute crisis. While other journalists perished —
including Joe Hashek of the *Chicago Tribune*, who died with twelve crewmen when their
ship sank — Sawitt took the extraordinary step of disguising himself as a waiter:

> Nevertheless, the representative of the *New York Herald*, Mr. I. Sawitt, spent the
> whole time on St. Kilda disguised as a waiter, and we are indebted to his pen for
> the scanty accounts of that memorable assembly which survived even the subsequent
> historic catastrophes. [[XX-007]]

Sawitt correctly deduced that the remote location was chosen to eliminate the
Absolute's influence on the diplomatic proceedings:

> Mr. I. Sawitt was of the opinion that this Conference on high politics was being
> held in this lonely spot in order to eliminate any direct influence of the Absolute
> on its decisions. In any other place the Absolute might well make its way into this
> gathering of serious statesmen in the guise of inspiration, enlightenment, or religious
> mania. [[XX-008]]

During the conference, Sawitt observed the diplomats at close quarters during luncheon:

> Not until about five o'clock (Greenwich time) did the illustrious diplomats sit down
> to luncheon, and it was here that I. Sawitt had the first opportunity of hearing with
> his own ears the representatives of the high contracting parties. After the meal they
> discussed sport and actresses. [[XX-012]]

## Significance

Sawitt's report, published in the *Herald*, represents the only journalistic record
of the St. Kilda Conference to survive the "subsequent historic catastrophes."
No official communiqué was issued beyond a vague statement about spheres of interest:

> This concludes the report which I. Sawitt published in the *Herald*. No official
> communication concerning this Conference was given out, except the one already
> mentioned relating to the spheres of interest, and if any decision was taken it
> was apparently of no great value. [[XX-039]]

Sawitt serves as a metafictional device — the lone journalistic witness whose
account survives where official records are lost, underscoring the novel's satire
of diplomatic secrecy and the fragility of historical documentation.
