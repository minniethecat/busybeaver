# Monsignor Cullatti

Monsignor Cullatti is a high-ranking Vatican official who appears in Chapter XXIII ("The Augsburg Imbroglio") during the Catholic Church's institutional response to the Absolute. As Papal Secretary, he serves as a key functionary in the Church's attempts to canonize — and ultimately deify — the Absolute.

## Role in the Deification Process

Three days before the publication of the Papal Brief addressing the Absolute phenomenon, Monsignor Cullatti received G. H. Bondy for a twenty-four-hour conference [1517-PPP]. The length of this meeting suggests the seriousness with which the Vatican treated Bondy's testimony, given his role in commercializing the Karburator and his first-hand experience with the Absolute's effects.

Following the summary beatification of the Absolute under the rule *super cultu immemorabili*, a Deification Commission was appointed from among the Church's most eminent scholars and pastors. Cardinal Varesi of Venice was named Procurator Dei (Advocate for God), while Monsignor Cullatti was assigned the counterbalancing role of Advocatus Diaboli — the Devil's Advocate [1519-PPP]. His duty was to argue against the deification, testing the evidence and ensuring that the process met canonical standards.

The scale of Cullatti's task was enormous: Cardinal Varesi presented seventeen thousand testimonies of miracles, representing only "an insignificant fraction" of the thirty million miracles the Absolute was estimated to have performed [1521-PPP].

## Significance

Monsignor Cullatti is a minor character whose function is largely institutional. He represents the Vatican's bureaucratic machinery engaging — with apparent seriousness — with the supernatural disruption caused by the Absolute. The irony lies in the Church applying its ancient canonical procedures (beatification, Advocatus Diaboli) to what is, in effect, an industrial by-product of atomic combustion.
