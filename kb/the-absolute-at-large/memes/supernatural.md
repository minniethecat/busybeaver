# Supernatural Phenomena

**Supernatural phenomena** in *The Absolute at Large* encompass the range of physically impossible events that occur when the Absolute is liberated from matter — from personal levitations to battlefield-scale divine interventions, and from miraculous healing to the multiplication of coffee.

## Marek's Scientific Explanation

Engineer Marek provides the novel's theoretical foundation for the supernatural. All matter contains the Absolute in a state of confinement; when the Karburator utterly destroys matter, it leaves behind "pure God" — a "chemically unanalysable, immaterial residue, which shows no spectrum lines, neither atomic weight nor chemical affinity, no obedience to Boyle's law, none, none whatever, of the properties of matter." Being immaterial, this residue is not subject to the laws of matter, and thus "its manifestations are contrary to nature and downright miraculous".

## Personal Manifestations

Marek himself experienced supernatural phenomena after building the Karburator. He described "moments of astounding illumination" during which he saw clearly things that would happen in the future, including Bondy's visit. His physical body was also affected:

> "And once I tore my nail off on a lathe. I looked at the damaged finger, and all at once a new nail grew on it. Very likely I'd formed the wish, but all the same it's queer and terrible. Another time — just think of it — I rose right up into the air. It's called levitation, you know. I never believed in any rubbish of that kind, so you can imagine the shock it gave me."

Marek's porter experienced similar effects: "He got religion. He was inspired. He gave religious addresses and performed miracles. His wife uttered prophecies".

## Factory Manifestations

When the Karburator was installed in factories, workers experienced "levitations, prophecies, miracle-working, visions, supernatural cures, sanctification, love for their neighbours, and other conditions equally unnatural, not to say miraculous". These were side effects of proximity to the liberated Absolute — the closer to the Karburator, the more intense the manifestations.

## The Kuzenda Dredge Community

Supernatural phenomena were most vividly depicted in the religious community that formed around Kuzenda's dredge on the Vltava River. Kuzenda described the manifestations:

> "One Sunday recently, there were several hundred people here, even sitting on both banks of the river, and behold, our coffee multiplied itself so that there was enough for everybody — and what splendid coffee it was!"

Beyond physical miracles, Kuzenda emphasized the psychological and spiritual effects: "The greatest miracle is the influence He has on our feelings. It is so intensely beautiful that it fairly makes one shiver. Sometimes you feel as if you could die of love and happiness, as if you were one with the water below, with all the animals, with the very earth and stones". The supernatural experience extended to a kind of universal understanding:

> "Everything around you is sounding and singing, you understand the speech of voiceless things, the water and the wind, you see deep into everything, how one thing is linked with another and with you; at one stroke you grasp everything better than if you had read it in print."

Kuzenda described the Absolute's presence in spatial terms: "God is not so infinite as the Catholics assert. He is about six hundred metres in diameter, and even then is weak towards the edges. He is at His strongest on the dredge. Here He performs miracles, but on the bank He only does inspirations and conversions, and in Stechovice, with a favourable wind, you only notice a kind of holy fragrance".

## Battlefield Scale

During the Greatest War, supernatural phenomena escalated to military proportions. Supernatural powers fought on both sides: "An invisible hand seized hostile aircraft and dashed them to the ground," fifty-four centimetre projectiles were intercepted mid-flight and hurled back, and the destruction of Karburators produced frightful explosions that "scooped out a crater over two hundred metres deep".

## Čapek's Satire

The cascade of supernatural phenomena serves Čapek's satirical purpose: when divine presence becomes an industrial byproduct, the miraculous becomes routine. The Absolute's manifestations — from nail regrowth to battlefield-scale interventions — are treated with the same casual, bureaucratic reporting as factory output statistics, underscoring the novel's central absurdity.
