## Summary

The extraordinary meeting of the Free French Masonic Lodge "The Hammer and Star" was convened by Worshipful Master [[g-h-bondy|G. H. Bondy]] to address the Clerical party's accusations that Freemasonry's secret activities had deliberately unleashed "demoniacal powers" — the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] — upon the world. The meeting degenerated into procedural paralysis as the brethren debated whether the [[the-absolute|Absolute]]'s manifestations were "regrettable" or "praiseworthy," with each speaker adjusting his position based on personal experience and financial interest. [[g-h-bondy|Bondy]] ultimately steered the Lodge toward accommodation with the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] by noting that the Industrial Federation had elected the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] as honorary President and that [[m-e-c|M.E.C.]] shares were rising.

## Context

By the time of the Lodge meeting, the [[the-absolute|Absolute]]'s effects had spread throughout society. Religious fervour, miraculous occurrences, and economic upheaval were commonplace. The Clerical press had begun attacking Freemasonry, asserting "that the Masonic Lodges have brought about deliberately this unfettering of demoniacal powers" (017-004). As both an industrialist who profited from the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] (through the [[m-e-c|M.E.C.]]) and the Worshipful Master of a major Masonic Lodge, [[g-h-bondy|Bondy]] found himself at the intersection of commerce, religion, and secret society politics.

> It concerns the systematic attacks upon our Order recently begun by the Clerical party. They state that our secret activities of the last hundred years are connected with certain extraordinary and regrettable occurrences in the industrial and spiritual field. (017-004)

[[g-h-bondy|Bondy]] called the meeting at an unusual time — a violation of Masonic protocol that signalled the gravity of the situation. The Lodge was "as quiet as a church between the black-draped walls with the maxims of the craft woven in the hangings" (017-003).

## Proceedings

The debate unfolded in four distinct phases, each representing a different strategy for the Lodge's relationship to the [[the-absolute|Absolute]]:

**Phase 1 — Denial (Junior Warden)**: The Junior Warden proposed that the Lodge declare all the "miracles, illuminations, fits of love for one's neighbour, and other disturbances" to be "in the highest degree regrettable" occurrences with which the Order should "decline all connection" (017-005). His speech, laden with the verbal tic "so to speak" (used twelve times in a single paragraph), rendered his position simultaneously pompous and vacuous.

> We must with all the discretion we owe to our Order decline all connection, so to speak, with these regrettable facts which, so to speak, do not agree with the traditional and progressive principles of our Great Order. (017-005)

**Phase 2 — Personal Testimony (Judex Formidabilis)**: Judex Formidabilis countered that the occurrences were "praiseworthy, uplifting, and moral" (017-009). He revealed that he himself attended religious circles where "divine grace" was received "in especial power" and admitted to having "been privileged to perform a few minor miracles" (017-008). He moved that the Lodge declare neutrality.

**Phase 3 — Cynical Accommodation (Senior Warden)**: The Senior Warden admitted he "certainly don't believe in any of this stuff" but proposed the Lodge "secretly let it be known that we have the very best information about the whole business, and that we approve of things going on as they are" (017-010). This position acknowledged the political value of appearing aligned with the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] while privately disbelieving.

**Phase 4 — Financial Pragmatism ([[g-h-bondy|Bondy]])**: [[g-h-bondy|Bondy]] ended the debate by introducing material facts: the Industrial Federation had elected the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] as honorary President, [[m-e-c|M.E.C.]] shares were rising, and an anonymous donor (himself) had contributed one thousand shares to the Lodge's Benevolent Fund (017-011). The Junior Warden immediately withdrew his objection, and [[g-h-bondy|Bondy]] relayed the Grand Lodge's directive: Masters were to "join religious circles and organize them in the Masonic sense," testing different doctrines — "monism, abstinence, Fletcherism, vegetarianism, and so forth" — to determine which best served "the welfare of mankind and for the glory of the Most High" (017-012).

## Aftermath

The meeting concluded with a complete reversal of the Lodge's position: from considering the [[the-absolute|Absolute]]'s manifestations as a threat to be disavowed, to actively participating in and organizing religious movements. The Grand Lodge's directive transformed the Masons from a secret society into a kind of religious research-and-development organization, testing faiths like consumer products.

The meeting also demonstrated the power of financial interest to override principle. The Junior Warden's swift withdrawal after [[g-h-bondy|Bondy]] mentioned the rising [[m-e-c|M.E.C.]] shares and the donated stock revealed that the Lodge's moral deliberations were ultimately subordinated to the material interests of its members.

## Narrative Significance

The Masonic Lodge meeting is one of Čapek's most concentrated satires of institutional decision-making. The entire debate is structured as a parody of parliamentary procedure, where rhetorical form completely overwhelms substantive content — the Junior Warden's twelve "so to speak"s, the procedural motions for committees of three, five, or twelve members, and the relentless focus on framing rather than fact.

The chapter exposes the underlying mechanism by which institutions absorb and co-opt radical phenomena. The Masons begin by viewing the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] as a threat to their Order; they end by organizing religious study circles to harness it. The trajectory from denial to accommodation to exploitation mirrors the broader societal response to the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] throughout the novel. [[g-h-bondy|Bondy]]'s role as both Worshipful Master and [[m-e-c|M.E.C.]] chairman embodies the fusion of spiritual and commercial interest that Čapek identifies as characteristic of modernity — the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] is simultaneously a theological problem and a stock-price catalyst.
