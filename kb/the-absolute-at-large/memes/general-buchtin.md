# General Buchtin

General Buchtin is an Imperial Russian General who represents Russian interests at the international diplomatic conference convened to address the escalating Absolute crisis.

## The International Conference

Buchtin appears in Chapter XX as part of the assembly of great-power representatives:

> The Imperial Russian General, Buchtin; the Imperial German Chancellor, Dr. Wurm; the Italian Minister, Prince Trivelino; and the Japanese Ambassador assembled to address the global crisis. (020-015)

The conference brings together the major powers of the era, each with its own agenda, and Buchtin serves as the voice of Imperial Russian military power.

## The Restless General

Buchtin is characterised physically by his inability to remain still:

> Dr. Wurm turned over his papers, General Buchtin walked up and down the room with Prince Trivelino. (020-030)

Where the German Chancellor reviews documents methodically, Buchtin paces — a contrast that reflects national stereotypes: German bureaucratic precision versus Russian emotional restlessness. His pacing suggests impatience, a military man forced into a diplomatic setting where action is constrained.

## Defending Russian Identity

Buchtin's most notable line comes when the Russian identity of a fellow delegate is questioned:

> "What do you mean, Japanese?" croaked out General Buchtin. "What are you talking about, batushko? He's a Russian, a genuine Russian, a Slav..." 

The exclamation "batushko" (батюшка, a Russian term of address) immediately marks Buchtin's national identity. His vehement insistence on the delegate's Russianness — "a genuine Russian, a Slav" — reveals both national pride and the anxieties of a multi-ethnic empire. The verb "croaked" adds a note of physical decay, suggesting an aging military establishment out of step with the times.

## Significance

General Buchtin represents Imperial Russia at a moment of crisis — the Absolute phenomenon creates conditions that expose the fragility of all established powers. His military bearing and national pride stand as markers of a world order that the Absolute threatens to dissolve. The fact that he appears alongside representatives of Germany, Italy, and Japan places the novel's events firmly within the great-power politics that would lead to world war — a prescient satirical touch.
