The **Process of Canonization**, recounted in Chapter XIX of *The Absolute at Large*, is Karel Čapek's satirical anatomy of how institutions absorb and domesticate the divine. After the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] is admitted to the Catholic Church by Papal Brief, the Church proceeds — with astonishing bureaucratic efficiency — to beatify, canonize, and ultimately deify it. The process is a masterpiece of institutional comedy: seventeen thousand miracle testimonies are filed, the world's leading scientists submit self-serving expert opinions, and the identity of the Absolute with the Third Person of the Trinity is "definitely established on the basis of the Scriptures." The chapter climaxes with the deification ceremony in St. Peter's, where seven thousand priests escort the Holy Father to a twelve-ton [[the-karburator|Karburator]] installed behind the high altar, and twelve hundred faithful are crushed to death as the Pope declares: "*Habemus Deum*."

## Summary

Chapter XIX documents the Catholic Church's formal absorption of the Absolute into its institutional structure. The process proceeds in stages: admission by Papal Brief, summary beatification, an expedited canonization process that is elevated to deification (declaring the Absolute not a saint but a God), theological debate over the Absolute's identity with the Persons of the Trinity, and finally a massive ceremony in St. Peter's concluding with the proclamation "*Habemus Deum*" — "We have a God." The chapter is Čapek's most concentrated satire of institutional religion's relationship with power, demonstrating how the Church succeeds where the industrialists failed in domesticating the uncontrollable divine.

## Context

The canonization process is the culmination of the Church's strategic pivot, first signaled by [[bishop-linda|Bishop Linda]] in Chapter XVIII when he ordered [[father-jost|Father Jost]] to reverse the Church's editorial line. Linda's logic — "if we wish to bring our flock back to us, we must secure the Absolute" [(018-025)] — is implemented at the highest levels. The Papal Brief admitting the Absolute is rushed through without consulting the College of Cardinals, presenting them with a *fait accompli*:

> You will readily understand that the admission of the Absolute to the bosom of the Church afforded, under the given circumstances, a great surprise. It was carried out by virtue of the Papal Brief alone, and the College of Cardinals, being faced with the accomplished fact, merely deliberated whether the sacrament of baptism should be conferred upon the Absolute. [(019-001)]

Behind the scenes, [[g-h-bondy|G. H. Bondy]] plays a crucial role: three days before the Brief's publication, the Pope grants him a lengthy audience, after which Bondy confers with Monsignor Cullatti, the Papal Secretary, for twenty-four hours [(019-001)]—[(019-003)]. The novel implies that Bondy's behind-the-scenes negotiations with the Church, hinted at since Chapter V, have reached their institutional conclusion.

## Key Participants

### Cardinal Varesi

[[cardinal-varesi|Cardinal Varesi]], Archbishop of Venice, is appointed Procurator Dei — the advocate for the Absolute's deification:

> Cardinal Varesi presented seventeen thousand testimonies to miracles performed, signed by nearly all the cardinals, patriarchs, primates, metropolitans, princes of the Church, archbishops, principals of Orders, and abbots. To each testimonial were appended expert reports by medical authorities and members of faculties, opinions from professors of natural sciences, technicians, and economists, as well as the signatures of eyewitnesses and legal authentications. [(019-004)]

These seventeen thousand testimonies "represented but an insignificant fraction of the miracles actually performed by the Absolute, their number having already at a conservative estimate exceeded thirty millions" [(019-004)].

### Monsignor Cullatti

[[monsignor-cullatti|Monsignor Cullatti]], the Papal Secretary, serves as Advocatus Diaboli — the Devil's Advocate. He is also Bondy's primary interlocutor in the twenty-four-hour conference that precedes the Papal Brief [(019-001)].

### The Scientific Experts

The chapter's richest satirical passages are the expert opinions from the world's leading scientists:

> Professor Gardien, Rector of the medical faculty in Paris, for example, after exhaustive researches, wrote as follows: "Seeing that innumerable cases presented to us for examination were from a medical standpoint completely hopeless and scientifically incurable (paralysis, cancer of the throat, blindness after surgical removal of both eyes, lameness following on amputation of both the lower extremities, death following on complete separation of the head from the trunk, strangulation in a subject hanged two days before, etc.), the medical faculty of the Sorbonne is of the opinion that the so-called miraculous cures in such cases can only be ascribed either to complete ignorance of anatomical and pathological conditions, clinical inexperience, and utter incompetence in medical practice, or — a possibility we do not wish to exclude — to the interference of higher powers not limited by the laws of nature or any knowledge thereof." [(019-005)]

Professor Meadow of Glasgow seizes the opportunity to attack his rival's theory of psychophysical parallelism:

> "Since in these activities there is manifest what is obviously a thinking being, capable of association, memory, and even of logical judgment, a being which performs these psychic operations without the medium of a brain and nervous system, it affords a striking corroboration of my crushing criticism of the psychophysical parallelism advocated by Professor Meyer." [(019-006)]

Professor Lupen of Brno writes simply that "from the standpoint of effective performance, the Absolute is a force deserving of the highest respect" [(019-007)], and Willibald of Tübingen notes that the Absolute "is in admirable conformity with Einstein's theory of Relativity" [(019-008)].

Each expert endorses the Absolute not on theological grounds but in service of personal academic feuds or disciplinary vanity. The scientists, like the priests, use the Absolute for their own purposes.

### The Rival Claimants

As deification approaches, theological competition erupts:

- The **Patriarch of Constantinople** declares the Absolute the First Person of the Trinity (the Creator), not the Third — a "heretical teaching" embraced by Old Catholics, Abyssinian Christians, and several American sects [(019-009)].
- **Jews** develop a secret doctrine identifying the Absolute as Baal [(019-009)].
- The **Free Thought Society** in Basle proclaims the Absolute "the God of the Freethinkers" [(019-011)].
- The **German Monist Association** lays the foundation stone of the "Cathedral of the Atomic God" in Leipzig, during which ceremony sixteen people are injured and the physicist Lüttgen has his spectacles smashed [(019-012)].

### The Pope

The Pope intones "*In nomine Dei Deus*" — "In the name of God, God" — at the ceremony's climax [(019-017)].

## Aftermath

The deification does not resolve the religious crisis; it intensifies it. The competing theological claims erupt into the sectarian violence that fuels the [[the-greatest-war|Greatest War]]. The St. Kilda Conference (Chapter XX), held after the deification, demonstrates that the world's nations cannot agree on whose version of the Absolute is legitimate. The deification in Rome, meant to consolidate the Church's authority, instead precipitates the fragmentation of religious authority worldwide.

The ceremony's body count — twelve hundred crushed to death — prefigures the vastly larger casualties of the war that follows. The carnival of competing claims (Monists, Masons, Lutherans, Communists, Freethinkers, Theosophists, all claiming the Absolute as their own) demonstrates that institutional capture of the divine inevitably produces institutional conflict.

## The Ceremony

The chapter's closing image is both majestic and grotesque:

> Seven thousand priests with lighted candles escorted the Holy Father to St. Peter's, where the largest twelve-ton Karburator, a gift from the M.E.C. to the Holy See, had been erected behind the high altar. The ceremony lasted five hours, and twelve hundred of the faithful and the spectators were crushed to death. At the stroke of noon the Pope intoned the "*In nomine Dei Deus*," and at the same moment the bells of all the Catholic churches in the world were set ringing as all the bishops and priests turned from the altars and announced to the world of believers: "*Habemus Deum.*" [(019-017)]

The twelve-ton Karburator — the very machine that unleashed the Absolute, manufactured by Bondy's [[m-e-c|M.E.C.]] — installed behind the high altar of Christendom's greatest church, physically embodies the novel's thesis that technology and religion are not opposites but collaborators in humanity's perpetual effort to control what it cannot understand. "*Habemus Deum*" — "We have a God" — echoes the papal election formula "*Habemus Papam*," reducing God to an elected official, a product of institutional process.

## Narrative Significance

The Process of Canonization chapter is Čapek's most concentrated satire of institutional religion's relationship with power. Every element — the legalistic debates over baptism, the seventeen thousand testimonies, the self-serving scientific endorsements, the competing theological claims — demonstrates how the Absolute is transformed from an uncontrollable divine presence into a manageable institutional asset. The Church succeeds where the industrialists failed: it domesticates God.

Yet the victory is pyrrhic. The deification triggers theological competition that spirals into the Greatest War. The chapter's structural irony is that the Church's greatest triumph — declaring God officially present — coincides with God's effective disappearance as a force of unity. Once every institution claims exclusive possession of the Absolute, the Absolute ceases to be a presence and becomes a pretext.
