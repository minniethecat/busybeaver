# Dr. Hubka

Dr. Hubka is a Czechoslovak parliamentary figure who serves as the leader of the political opposition. He appears during G. H. Bondy's presentation of the Carborundum project, offering skeptical interrogation that reveals the economic and political dimensions of the Absolute's discovery.

## The Parliamentary Interrogation

In Chapter III, Dr. Hubka questions Bondy about the proposed Carborundum factory plans. When Bondy mentions the need for "new quarters for our workmen," Hubka immediately challenges the industrial scale of the project:

> "Twelve boiler furnaces?" Dr. Hubka, the leader of the opposition, asked at a venture. (003-015)

His question, posed "at a venture" — that is, speculatively — suggests that he is probing Bondy's plans, trying to gauge the true magnitude of the industrial operation.

When Bondy confirms the scope, Hubka presses further:

> "That's strange," Dr. Hubka declared. (003-016)

This brief response carries significant weight: Hubka senses that something unusual is afoot. A factory requiring twelve boiler furnaces is far beyond normal industrial scale, and Hubka's political instincts tell him that Bondy is not being fully transparent.

## Political Role

As opposition leader, Dr. Hubka represents the institutional checks on Bondy's industrial power. His presence in the narrative establishes that the Carborundum project — and by extension, the release of the Absolute — was subject to at least nominal democratic oversight, even if that oversight proved ineffective.

> "And why the fifteen kilometres of railway?" Hubka continued, pressing Bondy on the logistical scale of the project. (003-018)

Hubka's detailed questioning about railway construction reveals his political experience: he understands that infrastructure projects of this scale represent enormous public expenditure and potential corruption.

## The Ironic Smile

Dr. Hubka's characteristic gesture is the ironic smile:

> Dr. Hubka smiled ironically. (003-020)

This smile recurs throughout the parliamentary scene, suggesting that Hubka perceives the absurdity of the situation — or at least the gap between Bondy's public explanations and the true nature of the Carborundum project. It marks him as one of the few characters who maintain skeptical distance from the Absolute phenomenon.

## Significance

Dr. Hubka represents the political establishment's response to the Carborundum/Atomic enterprise: suspicion, questioning, but ultimately ineffectual opposition. His parliamentary interrogation of Bondy highlights the tension between democratic oversight and industrial capitalism, a recurring theme in Čapek's work. The fact that Hubka's questions fail to prevent the Absolute's release underscores the novel's satirical treatment of political institutions.
