## Summary

Hampl's Declaration of War was a mock-military insurrection in Hradec Králové during the chaotic final phase of the Greatest War. [[general-hampl|Hampl]], a former tailor and deserter from [[emperor-bobinet|Bobinet]]'s army, executed a "coup *à la [[emperor-bobinet|Bobinet]]*" — marching through the city with drums and a red flag, issuing an ultimatum demanding the keys to the city gates, and writing out a formal declaration of war from a field called the "Little Mill." Overnight, thousands of curious citizens drifted to his camp for the spectacle, swelling his forces. When he marched on the city at dawn, he found it completely empty — the Burgomaster and citizens had simply left. [[general-hampl|Hampl]] declared victory and sent his "army" home.

## Context

[[the-greatest-war|The Greatest War]] had left Hradec Králové battered by successive occupations — Prussians, [[emperor-bobinet|Bobinet]]s, Turks, and Chinese had all passed through. The city's single [[the-karburator|Karburator]] in the brewery had been destroyed by the Prussians in their "Lutheran fury" (028-002). Under Burgomaster Skocdopole and [[bishop-linda|Bishop Linda]], the city had enjoyed comparative peace during the anarchy following the Mandarin Empire's collapse.

> Hradec Králové is too close to the railway, and so it very soon fell into the hands of the Prussians, who in their Lutheran fury destroyed the Karburator in the brewery. Nevertheless, mindful of its historical continuity, Hradec maintained an agreeable religious temperature. (028-002)

[[general-hampl|Hampl]] entered this fragile equilibrium as a disruptive force. A native of Hradec who had wandered the world — serving in the Foreign Legion in Algeria, marching with [[emperor-bobinet|Bobinet]]'s troops to India, deserting near Baghdad, and slipping through the lines of multiple armies — he returned carrying the virus of [[emperor-bobinet|Bobinet]]ism. "Stitching away at clothes didn't suit him any longer," so he founded the Social-Revolutionary Party and began agitating against the Burgomaster (028-003).

## The Coup

The insurrection unfolded in four stages, each more absurd than the last:

**Stage 1 — Agitation**: [[general-hampl|Hampl]] summoned a Popular Assembly in the Little Square, standing on the fountain and demanding that "Skocdopole, that scoundrel, reactionary, and lackey of the Bishop, should resign" (028-004). Skocdopole responded with posters declaring that as lawfully elected Burgomaster, he took orders from no one, "least of all from an interloper and a deserter" (028-005).

**Stage 2 — The March**: [[general-hampl|Hampl]] emerged from his house "waving a red flag, with two boys behind him beating drums with all their might." He paraded around the Great Square, paused before the Bishop's palace, and marched to the Little Mill field by the Orlice river. There "he stuck his standard into the ground and, seated on a drum, wrote out his declaration of war" (028-005).

**Stage 3 — The Ultimatum**: [[general-hampl|Hampl]]'s proclamation, issued "in the name of His Highness the [[emperor-bobinet|Emperor Bobinet]]," demanded the keys to the city gates by sunset, threatening to "attack the city at dawn with artillery, cavalry and bayonets." The ultimatum added: "Parlementaires will be shot. The Emperor does not parley" (028-006). The declaration was signed "[[general-hampl|General Hampl]]."

When Skocdopole convened an emergency City Council to surrender the keys, "it was then ascertained that there were no such keys in existence" — they had been carried off by the Swedes centuries earlier (028-007).

**Stage 4 — The "Battle"**: Overnight, a crowd gathered at the Little Mill — drawn by curiosity, the bonfires, and the picturesque spectacle. At dawn, [[general-hampl|Hampl]] led "several thousand men, all in civilian clothes, but for the most part armed" toward the city to the tune of "The Girls along the Highway" played by Mr. Červený's brass band (028-010). They found the city completely empty: "The Little Square was empty. The Great Square was empty. The whole city was empty" (028-011). The Burgomaster, citizens, and Bishop had simply left.

[[general-hampl|Hampl]] entered the City Hall, sat in the Burgomaster's Chair, and found sheets of paper on the green cloth, each bearing the words: "In the name of His Majesty the [[emperor-bobinet|Emperor Bobinet]]" (028-012) — the city's final, silent surrender. He declared victory from the window and sent his army home.

## Aftermath

[[general-hampl|Hampl]]'s "Hampelmen" dispersed, and the city returned to an uneasy peace. The coup had cost no lives and changed nothing substantive — the Burgomaster eventually returned, and [[general-hampl|Hampl]]'s revolutionary government proved as ephemeral as his military campaign. [[general-hampl|Hampl]]'s followers, known as "Hampelmen," became a local curiosity.

The episode demonstrated that [[emperor-bobinet|Bobinet]]ism was a replicable template. [[general-hampl|Hampl]]'s copycat coup followed the same script — drums, flags, ultimatums, self-promotion to "General" — but scaled down to municipal dimensions and stripped of all violence.

## Narrative Significance

Hampl's Declaration of War is Čapek's most concentrated satire of military-political ambition. The entire coup is a quotation of [[emperor-bobinet|Bobinet]]'s — [[general-hampl|Hampl]] explicitly performs it "*à la [[emperor-bobinet|Bobinet]]*" — but the miniature scale (two boys with drums standing in for an army, a declaration written on a drum, keys that don't exist) exposes the theatrical emptiness of the strongman template.

The chapter's structure mirrors the novel's broader argument about power and imitation. Just as the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] replicates itself through [[the-karburator|Karburator]]s, [[emperor-bobinet|Bobinet]]ism replicates itself through former soldiers like [[general-hampl|Hampl]]. The coup template, like the [[the-absolute|Absolute]], is a contagion — it spreads not because it is effective but because it is easy to perform. The citizens' response — drifting to the camp out of curiosity, then quietly abandoning the city — suggests that "the people" are less revolutionary than merely spectators to a drama they do not take seriously.

The final image — [[general-hampl|Hampl]] alone in the Burgomaster's Chair, reading the city's ironic surrender written in his own formula — encapsulates the novel's verdict on political ambition: the conqueror conquers nothing but an empty room, and the declaration he finds is one he himself inspired.
