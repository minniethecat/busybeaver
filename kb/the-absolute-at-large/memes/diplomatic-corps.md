## Overview

The Diplomatic Corps appears at two critical junctures in *The Absolute at Large*: first at the Karburator Central Power Station opening ceremony in Chapter IX, where foreign ambassadors endure interminable speeches under the Absolute's influence; and second at the Supreme Council conference on St. Kilda in Chapter XX, where the great powers attempt — and fail — to formulate a coordinated response to the Absolute. Čapek uses the diplomats to satirize the impotence of international institutions when faced with a phenomenon that transcends political calculation.

## Structure

The Diplomatic Corps in the novel includes representatives of the major powers of the early 1920s: the French Ambassador and Premier (Dudieu), the English Premier (Sir W. O'Patterney), the American Ambassador (Horatio Bumm), the Italian Minister (Prince Trivelino), the Imperial Russian General (Buchtin), the Imperial German Chancellor (Dr. Wurm), the Japanese Ambassador (Baron Yanato), and the Chinese plenipotentiary (Mr. Kei). The doyen of the Corps is Mr. Billington, who first appears at the Karburator opening and later — contrary to false press reports — proves to be "well and active" .

## Role in the Novel

### The Karburator Opening (Chapter IX)

The diplomatic corps assembles in force for the ceremonial opening of the Karburator Central Power Station. Cyril Keval, watching from a window, catalogs the arriving dignitaries:

> "The French Ambassador, the Minister of Public Works. I say, my friend, we'd better see about getting inside. The Archbishop, the Italian Ambassador, the President of the Senate, the Chief of the Sokol organization; you'll find that there's somebody they've left out." (009-011)

What follows is a masterclass in diplomatic endurance. The Burgomaster of Prague delivers an address of such stupefying length that it literally becomes lethal:

> Old Billington, the doyen of the Diplomatic Corps, had passed away on his feet, preserving even in death the expression of an attentive listener. (009-019)

The scene is Čapek at his most mordant: a diplomat who dies standing up, face frozen in polite attention, while a local politician talks himself purple. The Absolute, which has been released in quantity by the Karburator, sanctifies the proceedings so thoroughly that Billington's death is reported as a spiritual experience — and later denied entirely by official channels:

> Mr. Billington, doyen of the Diplomatic Corps, is, contrary to published reports, well and active. The fact is that all present declare that nothing in their experience has ever made so powerful an impression upon them. 

### The Supreme Council Conference (Chapter XX)

The diplomatic corps reconvenes in Chapter XX under far more urgent circumstances. As the Absolute crisis escalates into global conflict, Sir W. O'Patterney summons the great powers to the remote island of St. Kilda for a secret conference. The assembly is a roll call of the era's diplomatic archetypes:

> On the morning of the fourth day there arrived on H.M.S. *Edwin* the English Premier, the Right Hon. Sir W. O'Patterney, half an hour later came the American Ambassador, Mr. Horatio Bumm; and there followed him, each on a warship, the Chinese plenipotentiary, Mr. Kei; the French Premier, Dudieu; the Imperial Russian General, Buchtin; the Imperial German Chancellor, Dr. Wurm; the Italian Minister, Prince Trivelino; and the Japanese Ambassador, Baron Yanato. (020-004)

The conference's descent into chaos is swift. After luncheon conversation about salmon-fishing and billiards, the diplomats turn to the question of the Absolute. M. Dudieu declares the French position with characteristic clarity:

> "We cannot remain indifferent to Him. We must either recognize Him or deny Him. We Frenchmen are in favour of the latter course!" (020-010)

The attempt to vote on the existence of God — and France's preference for denial — precipitates the breakdown of international order. By evening, General Buchtin storms out "purple in the face," Dr. Wurm clutches his papers in agitation, Sir W. O'Patterney wears his hat indoors, and the conference collapses without resolution (020-035). The absolute failure of diplomacy in the face of the Absolute is complete.

## Thematic Significance

The Diplomatic Corps embodies the novel's critique of institutional rationality. Diplomats are trained to negotiate between conflicting human interests through compromise, ambiguity, and careful inaction — precisely the tools least suited to confronting a phenomenon that demands absolute recognition or absolute denial. The Supreme Council cannot compromise with the Absolute because the Absolute, by definition, admits no compromise. Čapek's satire is double-edged: the diplomats are absurd, but their absurdity reflects a genuine philosophical impasse. How does a political order built on negotiation respond to a truth claim that is by nature non-negotiable?
