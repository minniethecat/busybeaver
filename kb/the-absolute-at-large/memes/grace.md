# Grace

**Grace** in Čapek's *The Absolute at Large* is the unmerited divine favor bestowed through the Absolute, which transforms individuals irrespective of their prior moral condition. In the novel, grace functions as a democratizing force that bypasses ecclesiastical sacraments and falls upon ordinary people in the most mundane circumstances.

## Grace Without Prerequisites

The most vivid illustration of grace occurs among the working-class figures at the dredging works on the Vltava River. Brych, a reformed drunkard, explains his transformation to the journalist Hudec:

> "Let me tell you, Mr. Hudec, that I was a drunkard and Kuzenda was in politics, and the grace of God came upon us, and only now can we see properly what we were before. If only grace would descend upon the whole world!"

That grace should fall upon a drunkard and a political operative — rather than upon monks or saints — encapsulates Čapek's satire. The Absolute's grace is entirely indifferent to human hierarchies of worthiness.

## The Infinite Variety of Grace

The novel's chronicler marvels at the diversity of grace-induced phenomena across all social classes and temperaments, describing "what diverse, endlessly varying, strange and surprising instances and phenomena of heavenly grace" one could encounter. Grace in this world is not singular but plural — each recipient experiences it according to their own disposition, from the penitent to the miracle-worker.
