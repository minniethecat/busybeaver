## Overview

Cyril Keval is a district reporter for the Prague *People's Journal* who serves as one of the novel's primary journalistic witnesses to the unfolding Absolute crisis. Introduced in Chapter IX as he hurries "into evening-dress for the occasion" (011-005) to cover the ceremonial opening of the Central Karburator Power Station at [[stvanice|Stvanice]], Keval provides the reader with an outsider's perspective on the increasingly strange effects of [[the-absolute|the Absolute]]. His professional detachment, combined with his very human vanity and competitiveness, makes him one of Capek's most effective point-of-view characters.

> [(011-005)] Cyril Keval, district reporter on the staff of the Prague *People's Journal*, hurried into evening-dress for the occasion and dashed off to Stvanice just after six o'clock in the evening, to write up the ceremonial opening of the new Central Karburator Electric Power Station for Greater Prague.

## Role and Significance

### The Ceremony Reporter

Keval's primary function is as an eyewitness to the Stvanice ceremony (Chapter IX), which marks the Absolute's first mass public emergence. He shoulders his way through curious crowds, penetrates "the three ranks of police," and reaches "a small concrete structure decorated with flags" (011-005). His interaction with Old Cvancara of the *Venkov*, the doyen of the press corps, establishes the generational contrast: the veteran journalist who has "never yet seen a function where there wasn't some silly incident or other" (011-005) versus the eager young reporter trying to make his name.

> [(011-005)] "Well, my friend," he said quietly to the young journalist, "it's safe to bet on something happening today. I've never yet seen a function where there wasn't some silly incident or other. And I've been at it now, young fellow, for forty years."

### The Improvised Lie

A revealing moment of character: when Cvancara points out there is no reserve Karburator — "Suppose this one broke down, or even, say, went up in the air, what then" (011-005) — Keval is annoyed at not having thought of this himself. So he invents an elaborate fiction about underground Karburator fortresses:

> [(011-005)] Keval was annoyed at not having thought of this himself, so he dissented. "That's out of the question, sir," he began. "I have reliable information. This power station here is only for show. The real Central Station is somewhere else; it's ... it's ... right down underground, I mustn't say where. Haven't you noticed, sir, that they are continually repairing the streets in Prague?"

This moment — a journalist fabricating a story out of professional pique — is a typically Capekian irony. Keval's "reliable information" is pure invention, yet the lie reveals something true about the city's infrastructure: "They've been doing it these forty years," Cvancara confirms gloomily.

### The Witness at the Window

Keval's position throughout the ceremony is emblematic of journalism itself: he is outside looking in. Unable to get inside the Power Station, he repeatedly jumps up to a small window "about two metres from the ground" (011-005) to observe the proceedings:

> [(011-005)] Then Keval noticed a little window in the concrete wall of the Central Station about two metres from the ground. He looked around, then leapt up like a flash, caught hold of the grating, and drew his clever head up to the window.

From this vantage point he observes the Burgomaster speaking himself purple, the Chief of State standing with his hand on the lever, the Ambassador Billington who "had passed away on his feet, preserving even in death the expression of an attentive listener" (011-005). When the President finally presses the lever and Prague blazes with light, Keval is still hanging from his grating:

> [(011-005)] Then, in an instant, the whole of Prague shone out as a vast expanse of light, the crowds cheered, the bells in all the steeples began to swing, and from the Marianske fort there sounded the first boom of the cannon.

### The Terrifying Aftermath

What Keval sees next terrifies him. He utters "a hoarse cry," rolls his eyes, and drops from the window. As he falls, he collides with [[g-h-bondy|G.H. Bondy]], who is fleeing the Power Station "as pale as death" (011-005). Bondy strikes Keval and disappears among the trees. Keval, trembling, hears "sounds as of savages chanting a hymn" (011-005) from within the building. This is the moment the Absolute breaks through — and Keval, the professional observer, is reduced to stunned silence.

> [(011-005)] "What has happened, sir?" Keval stammered. "What are they doing in there?" "Let go of me," Bondy panted. "For Christ's sake, let go. And get out of here, as quick as you can."

### Later Appearances

Keval reappears in Chapter XVIII, "In the Night Editor's Room," and at the Czechoslovak Press Bureau in Chapter XXI, "The Telegram," where he processes international reports about the Absolute's global effects alongside [[mr-rejzek|Mr. Rejzek]] (023-001). The beard conversation — a running joke in which Keval and his colleagues debate whether the Absolute is causing men's beards to grow alarmingly — shows Keval still trying to maintain journalistic objectivity while the world becomes increasingly surreal.

## Thematic Significance

Cyril Keval represents the press's role in mediating between extraordinary events and public understanding — and the impossibility of that mediation. As a reporter, he is the character closest to the reader's position: an outsider trying to make sense of the inexplicable. His professional toolkit — the notebook, the evening dress, the ready lie, the window-ledge vantage point — proves wholly inadequate to the event he witnesses.

The window scene is particularly rich. Keval can only observe; he cannot participate. His position is literally precarious — hanging from a grating — and the view it affords is partial. When the Absolute breaks through, his response is not journalistic documentation but inarticulate terror. Capek's point is clear: journalism, for all its pretensions to objectivity, cannot capture the reality of the divine. It can only record the externals — the Burgomaster's purple face, the Ambassador's attentive death, the President's hand on the lever — while the essential event occurs invisibly, inside.

The invented lie about underground fortresses is also telling. Keval makes up a story to save face, and the story — of hidden infrastructures, military secrets, a city built on concealed power — turns out to be metaphorically true. The Absolute *is* the hidden infrastructure beneath the city's surface, and Keval's fiction is truer than his reporting.
