## Overview

Prague is the geographic and symbolic heart of Capek's *The Absolute at Large*. The novel's events unfold across the city's districts — from the industrial periphery of [[brevnov|Břevnov]] and Vysočany to the historic heights of Hradčany and [[petrin|Petřín]] — mapping [[the-absolute|the Absolute]]'s spread from a single cellar to a metropolis-wide crisis. Prague is not merely backdrop but a character in the novel: a modern industrial city whose institutions prove utterly incapable of containing the divine byproduct they have manufactured.

> [(001-002)] G. H. Bondy, head of the great Metallo-Electrical Company, was sitting as usual reading his paper.

The novel opens in [[g-h-bondy|Bondy]]'s Prague office and traces an expanding radius of contamination — Břevnov, [[dejvice|Dejvice]], Vysočany, [[stvanice|Stvanice]], the [[vltava|Vltava]] — until the entire city falls under the Absolute's influence.

## Key Locations

### Břevnov — The Origin Point

The western district where [[rudy-marek|R. Marek]]'s factory at 1651 Mixa Street houses the original [[the-karburator|Karburator]] in its cellar. It is the ground zero of the Absolute's release. See the dedicated page on [[brevnov|Břevnov]] for a full treatment.

### Vysočany — The Factory District

The [[m-e-c|M.E.C.]]'s new industrial complex, where Bondy constructs a massive factory for Karburator production:

> [(006-002)] Bondy addressing the meeting of the Board of Directors of the M.E.C. ... "I have to inform you that one building of our new group of factories at Vysočany has been completed and began production yesterday."

The Vysočany works employ seven thousand bricklayers and represent the industrial scaling of Marek's invention from a single cellar machine to global mass production [(005-008)]. Bondy increases the [[m-e-c|M.E.C.]]'s share capital to "a billion and a half" [(005-010)] to finance the expansion.

### Stvanice — The Central Power Station

[[stvanice|Stvanice]] Island in the Vltava becomes the site of the Central Karburator Electric Power Station, designed to light all of Greater Prague:

> [(007-043)] "The central Karburator at Stvanice, which is to light the whole of Prague, is finished. It is a fifty-kilo monster, a magnificent machine."

The ceremonial opening of the [[stvanice|Stvanice]] station (Chapter IX) is one of the novel's most vivid set pieces. The entire city's illuminations are wired to a single switch, to be thrown by the President of the Republic himself:

> [(009-004)] Keval: "Fancy this little building lighting the whole city of Prague and driving the trams and trains for sixty kilometres round, besides supplying power for thousands of factories..."

When the switch is thrown, "the whole of Prague shone out as a vast expanse of light, the crowds cheered, the bells in all the steeples began to swing, and from the Marianske fort there sounded the first boom of the cannon" [(009-020)]. But the Absolute overwhelms the gathering, and Bondy flees:

> [(009-022)] It was G. H. Bondy; he was as pale as death.

The Burgomaster of Greater Prague resigns his office and retreats to the country to recuperate [(009-029)].

### The Vltava River

The [[vltava|Vltava]] flows through the novel as both literal geography and symbolic artery of the Absolute's spread. The [[m-e-c|M.E.C.]]'s dredge M.E.28, powered by Karburator No. 2, operates on the Upper Vltava near [[stechovice|Štěchovice]] [(006-015)] [(008-001)], becoming the site of [[jan-binder-kuzenda|Kuzenda]]'s religious community. Binder's merry-go-round traces the river's course from Zlichov to Chuchle, Zbraslav, and finally Štěchovice [(011-011)].

> [(008-002)] The Paternoster shovel had long since ceased heaving up the cold sand from the bed of the Vltava River.

> [(008-036)] "Look at the little fish," whispered the young girl, pointing down to the smooth surface of the Vltava. "Look, Joe, the carp have come to listen, too."

By the novel's apocalyptic climax, the Vltava serves as a defensive moat: "the last inhabitants of Prague destroyed all the bridges, even the Charles Bridge, to save the right bank of the Vltava" from bears multiplying on Hradčany [(025-008)].

### Petřín — The Wireless Station

The [[petrin|Petřín]] hill, with its observation tower and wireless station, becomes one of the earliest sites of mass religious contagion:

> [(005-034)] Bondy: "while at the Petřín wireless station there's practically an epidemic of religion. All the wireless operators on duty up there are sending out ecstatic messages of their own accord, a sort of new gospel to the world at large: God coming down again to the earth to ransom it, and so forth."

The wireless station — a symbol of modern communications technology — is ironically the first vector for broadcasting the Absolute's message. Capek's satire here is pointed: the same infrastructure that enables global capitalism also enables global religious mania.

### Hradčany, Letna, Vyšehrad, Karlín — The Illuminated City

During the [[the-ceremony-at-stvanice|Stvanice ceremony]], the Karburator-powered illuminations transform Prague's landmarks into a unified spectacle:

> [(009-021)] Flaming rockets shot up from Střelecky Island; Hradčany, Petřín, and even Letna, were aglow with garlands of electric lamps, distant bands began competing with each other, illuminated biplanes circled above Stvanice, while the immense V16 soared up from Vyšehrad all bedecked with lanterns ... answered by the monitors from near Karlín.

The geography is precise: Hradčany (the castle district, seat of power), Petřín (the observation hill), Letna (the park plateau), Vyšehrad (the ancient fortress), and Karlín (the industrial quarter) — the entire city, from its most ancient to its most modern quarters, united under electric light produced by the same machine that releases God.

### Dejvice — The First Affected Suburb

The residential district of [[dejvice|Dejvice]], northwest of the center, is the site of the novel's most politically consequential early infection:

> [(005-036)] Bondy: "The Minister for War. He 'saw the light' all of a sudden in his villa at Dejvice. The following morning he assembled the garrison of Prague, talked to them about eternal peace, and exhorted the troops to become martyrs."

The Minister's forced resignation — officially attributed to "health" — marks the moment when the Absolute's effects cross from the religious to the political domain [(005-037)].

### The Industrial Bank

The [[industrial-bank|Industrial Bank]] (modeled on the real Zivnostenska Banka) becomes a focal point of the Absolute's disruption of financial capitalism:

> [(010-002)] Bondy: "A religious strike broke out at the Industrial Bank today. We installed a Karburator there, and within two days the officials declared the bank's property to be a sacred trust for the poor."

> [(010-003)] Bondy: "The Industrial Bank shares are mere waste paper: it would take a kilo of them to buy a bit of cheese."

The transformation of a bank — the temple of capital — into a charitable religious institution represents Capek's most direct satire of the collision between business and the Absolute. As Bondy later catalogues, the Karburator's effects spread through "Mr. Machat's buildings, at the Zivno Industrial Bank, in the textile works at Upice, aboard Kuzenda's dredge, and on Binder's merry-go-round" [(013-003)].

### The White Mountain Church

Bondy purchases a baroque church on the White Mountain (Bila Hora, west of Břevnov) in an attempt to lure the Absolute out of Marek's cellar:

> [(005-034)] Bondy: "It's the nearest one to Břevnov. It cost me three hundred thousand, man. Both in writing and by word of mouth I offered it to the Absolute down in the cellar to induce it to move over there. It's quite a pretty baroque church."

The church remains strangely unaffected — "in the church itself nothing miraculous happened" — while adjacent buildings experience ecstatic manifestations. The detail satirizes [[the-catholic-church|the Church]]'s inability to contain or channel the very God it claims to represent.

### The University of Prague and Prague Times

The academic and journalistic establishments of Prague attempt to process the Absolute phenomenon through their institutional frameworks. [[dr-blahous|Dr. Blahous]], Lecturer in Comparative Religion at the University of Prague [(012-002)], publishes articles in the *Prague Times* [(012-003)] that reduce religious epidemics to "barbaric atavism" and "primitive forms of worship" — before himself succumbing to conversion mid-composition.

### St. Vitus Cathedral

In the novel's later chapters, St. Vitus Cathedral at Hradčany becomes the site of ecumenical chaos:

> [(025-005)] In the course of those years services were held, or Mass celebrated, in the Church of St. Vitus by a pastor, a solicitor, an Imam, an Archimandrite and a bonze, none of them enjoying any permanent success.

The cathedral, symbol of Czech national and Catholic identity, becomes a revolving door for competing faiths — each claiming to represent the Absolute, none achieving dominance.

## Prague as Modern City

Capek's Prague is distinctly the capital of a modern industrial state — the First Czechoslovak Republic — with its factories, banks, newspapers, universities, and wireless stations. The city's modernity is essential to the novel's satire: this is not a medieval city susceptible to religious mania but a rational, technological metropolis whose very sophistication makes it vulnerable to the Absolute.

The paradox is encapsulated in [[g-h-bondy|Bondy]]'s response to the crisis: "At any rate we'll gain time to invent some sort of protective mask against the Absolute. Damn it, I'll set aside three millions myself for purposes of research into protective measures against God" [(010-008)]. The same technological mindset that produced the Karburator now seeks to produce a counter-technology — a gas mask for the soul.

## Thematic Significance

1. **The City as Laboratory**: Prague functions as the experimental space where the consequences of [[rudy-marek|Marek]]'s invention are first observed and measured. Its neighborhoods serve as concentric rings of infection, allowing Capek to trace cause and effect with geographic precision.

2. **Modernity's Vulnerability**: The institutions that define modern Prague — the [[industrial-bank|Industrial Bank]], the wireless station, the university, the factory — are precisely the institutions most thoroughly disrupted by the Absolute. Capek suggests that industrial modernity is not the antithesis of religious mania but its unwitting incubator.

3. **Sacred Geography**: The novel remaps Prague's sacred sites. St. Vitus, the White Mountain church, and the proposed shrine at [[brevnov|Břevnov]] compete for divine presence — but the Absolute ignores all of them, preferring factory cellars, dredges, and merry-go-rounds. Capek democratizes the sacred, relocating it from cathedrals to industrial infrastructure.

4. **The Defended City**: Prague's final transformation — bridges destroyed, bears on Hradčany, the city under siege from its own unleashed divine — literalizes the novel's argument: when absolute conviction is released into the urban fabric, the city consumes itself.
