## Overview

Štěchovice is a riverside town on the Vltava, south of Prague, that becomes central to one of the novel's most significant spiritual movements. The M.E.28 dredge moored above the town serves as headquarters for Kuzenda's religious community — a spontaneous congregation drawn by the Absolute emanating from the dredge's Karburator.

## Location

Štěchovice lies in the Vltava River valley, upstream from Prague and downstream from the Svatojánské proudy (St. John's Rapids). The novel situates it as a rural counterpoint to urban Prague — accessible enough for weekend pilgrims yet remote enough for an autonomous spiritual community to flourish outside institutional control.

## Significance in the Novel

### The Sacred Dredge

The dredge M.E.28, powered by a Karburator, becomes an "iron church" — a site where the Absolute performs miracles and inspires religious ecstasy. Kuzenda describes its presence: "The dredge M.E.28 stood motionless in the evening twilight above Stechovice", and the divine force radiating from it transforms the local population:

> God is not so infinite as the Catholics assert. He is about six hundred metres in diameter, and even then is weak towards the edges. He is at His strongest on the dredge. Here He performs miracles, but on the bank He only does inspirations and conversions, and in Stechovice, with a favourable wind, you only notice a kind of holy fragrance.

### Miraculous Events

The dredge community witnesses multiplying coffee, miraculous healings, and mass religious experience. A "young girl from Stechovice" testifies: "the baker had a ring of light around his head, and then Mr. Kuzenda charmed away my consumption". The accessible, democratic character of the Absolute attracts both "sweethearts from Stechovice" and pilgrims from across Bohemia.

### The Binder–Kuzenda Conflict

Štěchovice becomes the stage for a darkly comic religious war when Jan Binder's merry-go-round arrives. Binder's machine, also Karburator-powered but emanating a different spiritual frequency, sets up in town, culminating in a violent clash between the Kuzendists and Binderians:

> Binder's merry-go-round forsook Zlichov and went roaming along the bank of the Vltava up to Chuchle and Zbraslav, and so reached Stechovice. It had been working in Stechovice for four days with tremendous power, when an incident of a somewhat mysterious character took place.

The ensuing brawl — involving a baker, a gamekeeper, a postman, and a girl from Štěchovice who bites Binder's arm — leaves Kuzenda unconscious and Binder with a broken collarbone, satirizing sectarian violence.

## Thematic Resonance

Štěchovice represents the rural, populist dimension of the Absolute's influence. Unlike Prague's industrial and political Karburator deployment, the dredge community at Štěchovice illustrates how divine power reaches even the most humble settings, transforming ordinary laborers and villagers into mystics and zealots. The Binder–Kuzenda conflict functions as a miniature of the global religious wars to come, demonstrating that even a shared divine source (the Karburator) cannot prevent sectarian violence — a darkly ironic commentary on organized religion.
