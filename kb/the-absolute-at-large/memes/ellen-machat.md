# Ellen Machat

Ellen Machat is the daughter of Mr. Machat, a wealthy landlord and early investor in the M.E.C. (Metallo-Electrical Company). Following her father's exposure to the Absolute through a Karburator heating installation, Ellen undergoes a profound spiritual transformation, developing the supernatural ability to read the thoughts of others. She becomes a central figure in the novel's exploration of sanctity, transparency, and the impossibility of ordinary human relationships in the presence of absolute knowledge.

## Transformation and Gifts

Ellen is first mentioned during the M.E.C. board meeting in Chapter VI, when her father reports that she is tending the sick among the poor families now occupying his buildings [562-PPP]. G. H. Bondy is struck by the image: "Ellen, the fair-haired Ellen, with her seventy millions, tending the sick!" [564-PPP]. Bondy had half-expected Ellen to become his wife, and the news of her transformation disturbs him [564-PPP].

When Bondy later visits Ellen in Chapter X ("Saint Ellen"), he discovers the full extent of her change. She has acquired the ability to read thoughts — a gift she describes as common to all who know God: "Everyone who knows God has this same gift. Every one of your thoughts is born in my mind as soon as in yours" [936-PPP]. She reads Bondy's "bestial desires" without flinching [916-PPP] and tells him plainly, "It has cured me, with the help of God, of the love of riches" [940-PPP].

## Relationship with Bondy

Bondy's visit to Ellen is one of the novel's most emotionally complex scenes. Despite his love for her, Bondy realizes that marriage to a woman who can read his thoughts is impossible: "Neither business nor society is possible without thoughts that are not disclosed. And marriage, above all things, is impossible without thoughts that are not disclosed" [946-PPP].

Bondy's final advice to Ellen is both tender and devastating: "And even if you find the holiest of men, don't marry him as long as you can read his thoughts. A little illusion is the only bond between mortals that never breaks. Saint Ellen, you must not marry" [946-PPP].

## Significance

Ellen Machat represents the ultimate personal cost of the Absolute's influence. Unlike the industrialists who exploit it or the fanatics who worship it, Ellen is genuinely transformed into a better person — yet this very transformation makes ordinary human happiness impossible for her. Her "sainthood" is not a blessing but a kind of exile from normal life.
