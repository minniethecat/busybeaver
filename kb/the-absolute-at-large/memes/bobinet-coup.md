## Summary

Bobinet's Coup was the military campaign launched by Lieutenant [[emperor-bobinet|Toni Bobinet]] against the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] in the French Alps. Stationed on Les Aiguilles at over two thousand metres — where the air was free of the [[the-absolute|Absolute]]'s influence — [[emperor-bobinet|Bobinet]] read a newspaper editorial calling for a strong leader to steer France out of chaos. He deposed his captain, seized the military post at Sallanches, declared war on the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] "with Napoleonic brevity," and began systematically destroying [[the-karburator|Karburator]]s across southeastern France. Within weeks his force swelled from a single battery to forty thousand men, and he was proclaimed First Consul and Marshal of France. His campaign ultimately led to him crowning himself Emperor of the French and launching a global war of conquest.

## Context

By the time [[emperor-bobinet|Bobinet]] launched his coup, Europe was in chaos. [[the-absolute|The Absolute]], released by [[the-karburator|Karburator]]s, had driven populations into religious frenzy and political fragmentation. The Chapter of Accidents (Ch XXV) recorded daily declarations of war, massacres, and collapsing governments. In the French Alps, Lieutenant [[emperor-bobinet|Toni Bobinet]] — a twenty-two-year-old mountain artillery officer — found himself isolated on the Needles (Les Aiguilles) at an altitude where the [[the-absolute|Absolute]]'s influence could not reach.

> Just picture to yourself this little Lieutenant Bobinet sitting on Les Aiguilles with Europe falling into ruin all about him — a battery of mountain guns in front of him, and below him a miniature world which could easily be shot to pieces from where he sat. (026-003)

The isolation was crucial. At over two thousand metres, "the air is pure and free from the [[the-absolute|Absolute]], so that one can think clearly and freely" (026-003). An old newspaper editorial by M. Babillard calling for "the strong hand of a helmsman who will steer the good ship France out of the raging storm toward new power and glory" crystallized [[emperor-bobinet|Bobinet]]'s ambition.

## The Campaign

The coup unfolded in rapid stages over the spring and summer:

**Phase 1 — The Declaration (Les Aiguilles)**: [[emperor-bobinet|Bobinet]] wrote his mother a confused letter assuring her "she would soon be hearing of her Toni" (026-003). The next morning he assembled his battery, deposed his captain, took the military post at Sallanches, and "declared war on the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] with Napoleonic brevity" (026-003).

**Phase 2 — The Sweep (Savoie)**: [[emperor-bobinet|Bobinet]] destroyed the [[the-karburator|Karburator]] in the bakery at Thônes, occupied Bonneville railway station, and seized command at Annecy with three thousand men. Within a week he had destroyed over two hundred [[the-karburator|Karburator]]s and led fifteen thousand men against Grenoble (026-003).

**Phase 3 — The Capture**: On the road to Chambéry, [[emperor-bobinet|Bobinet]] captured the Minister for War, who had been sent to discipline him. Instead, the Minister was so impressed that he promoted [[emperor-bobinet|Bobinet]] to General on the following day (026-003).

**Phase 4 — Consolidation**: By April 1st, Lyons was "completely cleansed of every trace of the [[the-absolute|Absolute]]" (026-003). [[emperor-bobinet|Bobinet]]'s forces, known as "les [[emperor-bobinet|Bobinet]]s," were welcomed as liberators by sceptical Frenchmen who had remained rationalists even in [[the-absolute|Absolute]]-saturated communities (026-004).

**Phase 5 — The Empire**: In July, Parliament proclaimed [[emperor-bobinet|Bobinet]] First Consul with the title of Marshal. France was consolidated under State atheism — "any sort of religious demonstration was punishable by court-martial with death" (026-006). On August 14th, [[emperor-bobinet|Bobinet]] proclaimed himself Emperor of the French (026-007).

## Aftermath

[[emperor-bobinet|Bobinet]]'s rule established State atheism in France and launched a period of global warfare. His plan for world conquest by New Year's Eve 1944 was detailed in a fourteen-point strategy that included conquering all of Europe, Asia, Africa, and the Americas, and establishing "the greatest cultural and economic centre of the world in Greenland" (026-009). His victorious regiments swept across India into China, while the Yellow invasion simultaneously crossed Siberia into Europe, creating a chaotic intercontinental war.

[[emperor-bobinet|Bobinet]] himself eventually marched to the Irawaddy-Salwin-Mekong river territory seeking an Amazon Empire of women. He never returned (026-013).

The [[emperor-bobinet|Bobinet]]ist model of coup-making proved infectious. [[general-hampl|Hampl]], a former soldier who had marched with [[emperor-bobinet|Bobinet]]'s troops before deserting near Baghdad, returned to Hradec Králové and executed his own "coup à la [[emperor-bobinet|Bobinet]]" — waving a red flag, beating drums, and issuing ultimatums to the city Burgomaster (028-004).

## Narrative Significance

[[emperor-bobinet|Bobinet]]'s coup is Čapek's satire of Napoleonic ambition and the cult of the strongman leader. The entire campaign is triggered not by ideology or grievance but by boredom and a two-week-old newspaper — a reductio ad absurdum of the Great Man theory of history. [[emperor-bobinet|Bobinet]]'s trajectory from lieutenant to Emperor mirrors and mocks Napoleon's rise, compressing it into a few months and attributing it to the simple physiological fact that at high altitude one is beyond the [[the-absolute|Absolute]]'s reach.

The coup also serves as the novel's mechanism for exploring what happens when humanity fights back against the [[the-absolute|Absolute]]. [[emperor-bobinet|Bobinet]]'s solution — destroy all [[the-karburator|Karburator]]s and institute State atheism — works as a military strategy but merely replaces religious fanaticism with nationalist fanaticism. The "[[emperor-bobinet|Bobinet]]ist" model spreads, and [[general-hampl|Hampl]]'s imitation in Hradec Králové demonstrates that the coup template is as contagious as the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] itself. Čapek's warning is clear: removing the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] does not remove humanity's capacity for destructive zealotry; it simply redirects it.
