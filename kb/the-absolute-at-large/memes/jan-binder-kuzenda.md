**Jan Binder Kuzenda** (or simply **Kuzenda**) is the skipper of dredge M.E.28 on the Vltava River near Stechovice, and one of the most vividly drawn religious figures in Karel Capek's *The Absolute at Large*. A former political activist transformed by exposure to the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] emanating from a [[the-karburator|Karburator]]-powered dredge, Kuzenda becomes an unwilling prophet — levitating, healing the sick, reading souls, and hosting impromptu worship services on the deck of his "iron church." His story parodies the democratization of religious authority: when God becomes an industrial byproduct, a politically inclined dredge skipper can become a miracle-worker.

## Overview

Kuzenda is the central figure of Chapter VIII ("The Dredge") and reappears in later chapters as his movement grows and eventually fragments. He represents one of the novel's most fully realized satirical portraits — the accidental saint whose working-class background and political instincts shape a distinctively democratic theology. Together with his stoker [[mr-brych|Brych]], a reformed drunkard, Kuzenda forms the nucleus of a new religious community that attracts hundreds of pilgrims to the Vltava riverbank.

## Role and Significance

Kuzenda serves several satirical functions in Capek's novel:

1. **The Democratization of Religion**: When God is a factory byproduct, anyone can become a saint. Kuzenda's working-class background and political past make him an unlikely but authentic prophet. His theology reflects his politics — God is "a thorough democrat" who "cannot bear politics and money, intellect, pride, and self-conceit" (008-039).

2. **The Persistence of Sectarianism**: Despite (or because of) the Absolute's universal presence, religious communities fragment. Kuzendism and Binderianism parody denominational schism, culminating in a physical brawl at a fairground.

3. **The Banality of the Miraculous**: Kuzenda's miracles — floating with a coffee tray, healing asthma, multiplying coffee — are presented matter-of-factly. The supernatural becomes quotidian, and the quotidian becomes supernatural.

4. **The Chronicler's Sympathy**: The narrator's affectionate invocation — "I took an unusual liking to Mr. Kuzenda and Mr. Brych … as anyone is, as all of you are, as is every living human being" (013-008) — suggests that even within the satire, Capek finds genuine warmth in these accidental saints.

## Biography and Transformation

### Before the Absolute

Prior to the events of the novel, Kuzenda was "in politics" (008-021), a detail that Capek uses to ironic effect. The former political activist — accustomed to speeches, organizing, and ideological conviction — finds his rhetorical skills repurposed for religious exhortation. His co-worker [[mr-brych|Brych]] notes: "I was a drunkard and Kuzenda was in politics, and the grace of God came upon us" (008-021). The pairing suggests the Absolute's universal reach: the drunkard and the politician, both unlikely candidates for sainthood, become the nucleus of a new religious movement.

### Conversion

Kuzenda's conversion occurs when the dredge is fitted with a Karburator that runs without fuel. The machine's autonomy — "the dredge floats by itself wherever it ought to go … it starts itself and stops itself" — convinces Kuzenda that he is witnessing the miraculous:

> (008-039) Kuzenda: "When we saw all this, we began to think it over … until it all became clear to us. This is a sacred dredge, it is an iron church, and we are only here as its priests."

His theology is refreshingly literal and empirical: "God is not so infinite as the Catholics assert. He is about six hundred metres in diameter, and even then is weak towards the edges" (008-039). The "democratic" God of the dredge reflects Kuzenda's own political past — a divine being who shares the skipper's egalitarian instincts and his distrust of institutional authority:

> (008-039) Kuzenda: "I know that He cannot bear politics and money, intellect, pride, and self-conceit. I know He dearly loves both men and beasts, that He is very glad when you come here, and that good deeds are pleasing to Him. He is a thorough democrat, brethren."

## Miracles

Kuzenda's miracles include levitation, healing, clairvoyance, and the multiplication of coffee. His most spectacular manifestation occurs during an evening service:

> (008-027) Mr. Kuzenda was visible now from the waist up, carrying in both hands a large tin tray on which stood cups and tins of preserves … About eighteen inches above the opening he stopped and began groping with his feet. There he hung unsupported in the air.

## Theology

Kuzenda develops a distinctive theology shaped by his working-class background and the Absolute's peculiar manifestation on the dredge. Key tenets include:

- **God is finite and local**: "about six hundred metres in diameter, and even then is weak towards the edges" (008-039) — a stark contrast to the infinite, omnipresent deity of orthodox theology.
- **God is democratic**: He "cannot bear politics and money, intellect, pride, and self-conceit" (008-039).
- **God loves universal good**: He "dearly loves both men and beasts" and finds "good deeds pleasing to Him" (008-039).
- **The dredge is a church**: The industrial machine becomes sacred space — "an iron church" with Kuzenda and Brych as its priests (008-039).

## The Schism and Fight

Kuzenda's movement, "Kuzendism," eventually comes into conflict with a rival sect — the "Binderians" — led by a man named Binder. The theological rivalry culminates in a physical brawl at a fairground, described in Chapter XI:

> (011-023) Kuzenda was struck by a pole and knocked unconscious. … Binder had a broken collarbone, Kuzenda lay there unconscious, Brych was spitting out teeth and blood, and the girl from Stechovice was sobbing hysterically.

Capek's depiction of the fight — sectarian combat with carousel antlers and deer statues as weapons — satirizes religious schism by literalizing it as street violence. The Binderian-Kuzendist conflict serves as a microcosm of the global religious wars that consume the novel's later chapters.

## In the Scholarly Record

Dr. Blahous, the academic scholar of religion, categorizes Kuzenda's movement in his published study with characteristic condescension:

> (012-006) He gave a description of Kuzendism, in which he found traces of fetish-worship and even totemism (the dredge being made a sort of Totem God of Stechovice).

The narrator — "the chronicler of the Absolute" — offers a more sympathetic view: "I took an unusual liking to Mr. Kuzenda and Mr. Brych … as anyone is, as all of you are, as is every living human being" (013-008).

## Later Years

By the novel's end, Kuzenda's movement has dissipated. Brych, now a locksmith, reflects nostalgically: "Those were the days!" (030-021). Kuzenda's trajectory — from politics to prophecy to obscurity — mirrors the novel's larger arc: the Absolute's eruption into the world is intense but transient. The democratic God of the Vltava, like all the Absolute's manifestations, exhausts itself in its own excess.

## Appearances

- **Chapter VIII — The Dredge** (008-001)–(008-045): Kuzenda's primary chapter, introducing his conversion, theology, miracles, and the community forming around the dredge.
- **Chapter XI — The First Blow Struck** (011-023): The fairground brawl between Kuzendists and Binderians.
- **Chapter XIII — The Chronicler's Apology** (013-008): The narrator's affectionate assessment of Kuzenda.
- **Chapter XII — Doctor Blahous** (012-006): Dr. Blahous's academic analysis of Kuzendism.
- **Chapter XXX — The End of Everything** (030-021): Brych's retrospective mention of Kuzenda.

## Relationships

- **[[mr-brych|Brych]]** — Kuzenda's stoker and fellow prophet. Former drunkard whose conversion parallels Kuzenda's. Together they form the leadership of the dredge community. Their partnership represents the novel's most sympathetic portrayal of the Absolute's effects.

- **Binder** — Leader of the rival Binderian sect. The theological rivalry between Kuzendists and Binderians parodies denominational schism, culminating in the fairground brawl.

- **[[dr-blahous|Dr. Blahous]]** — Academic scholar who studies and categorizes (and dismisses) Kuzenda's movement as "fetish-worship" and "totemism" (012-006).

- **[[the-absolute|The Absolute]]** — The divine force that transforms Kuzenda. His relationship to the Absolute is distinctively practical and local — he measures it in metres and describes its preferences like a ward politician describing a constituent.

## Significance

Kuzenda represents several of Capek's satirical targets while also embodying the novel's most humane impulses. His working-class authenticity, his practical miracles (coffee, not cosmic revelation), and his "democratic" God all serve the novel's critique of institutional religion while offering an alternative vision of what unmediated religious experience might look like. That this vision collapses into sectarian violence is, of course, Capek's point — but the warmth of the portraiture suggests that the failure belongs to human institutions, not to the human beings caught up in them.

The narrator's farewell — embedded in the chapter "The Chronicler's Apology" — gives Kuzenda a dignity that transcends the satire: he is not merely a target but a testament to the sincerity that even Capek's irony cannot fully dissolve.
