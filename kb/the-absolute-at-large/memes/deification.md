## Definition

In Chapter XIX, the Church faces a procedural novelty: how to classify the Absolute within its existing hierarchy of blessedness. The solution — **deification** rather than mere canonization — represents the novel's sharpest satire of institutional religion's capacity to invent categories when existing ones prove insufficient.

## The Deification Commission

The key passage appears immediately after the summary beatification of the Absolute [1518-PN]:

> "There was, however, one highly important innovation: the Absolute was to be declared, not a Saint, but a God. A Deification Commission was immediately appointed from among the best of the Church's scholars and pastors."

Three elements combine to produce the satirical effect. First, the word "innovation" — applied to theology as if it were a product feature. Second, the bureaucratic reflex of forming a "Commission" in response to a metaphysical question. Third, the phrase "from among the best" — suggesting that even cosmic uniqueness can be processed through a committee staffed by qualified personnel.

## Procurator Dei and Advocatus Diaboli

The Commission's composition mirrors the Church's existing canonization machinery [1518-PN]:

> "Varesi, the Cardinal Archbishop of Venice, was appointed Procurator Dei, while Monsignor Cullatti was to act as Advocatus Diaboli."

These are adaptations of the traditional roles in canonization: *Promotor Fidei* (Promoter of the Faith, colloquially "Devil's Advocate") and *Postulator*. Čapek elevates the titles — *Procurator Dei* (Procurator of God) and *Advocatus Diaboli* (Advocate of the Devil) — to emphasize the absurdity of having human officials argue the case for or against a being that demonstrably performs miracles.

## Theological precedent and improvisation

The deification episode parallels the baptism debate: in both cases, the Church encounters a reality that doesn't fit its categories and responds by stretching those categories rather than questioning the categorical framework itself. The Deification Commission is a mirror image of the Karburator — both are human mechanisms for processing the divine into administrable form.
