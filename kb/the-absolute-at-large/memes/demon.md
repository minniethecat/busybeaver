# Demon

**Demonic** and Satanic imagery in *The Absolute at Large* serves as the primary rhetorical weapon through which established institutions — particularly the Catholic Church — attempt to discredit the Absolute. When the Karburator's emanations prove resistant to ecclesiastical control, they are reframed as demonic rather than divine.

## Marek's Warning

Even the atheist engineer Marek, in his desperate attempt to dissuade Bondy from commercializing the Karburator, invokes the diabolical:

> "You will, Bondy — even if it spews forth demons. It doesn't matter to you, Bondy, so long as you can get your millions out of it." (003-023)

Marek's choice of words is revealing: the Karburator's divine byproduct is indistinguishable from the demonic in its disruptive effects on human society.

## Clerical Demonization

The Catholic press and hierarchy systematically reframe the Absolute's manifestations as Satanic rather than sacred:

> "The Clericalist papers assert that the Masonic Lodges have brought about deliberately this unfettering of demoniacal powers." (017-011)

This rhetorical strategy reaches its apotheosis in the Church's pastoral letter, which denounces the Absolute as "the golden calf whose name is the Devil or the Absolute" (018-021), explicitly equating the divine byproduct with Satanic idolatry.

## Industrial Demons

The Absolute's capacity to possess and autonomously operate machinery lends it a demonic quality in the eyes of factory owners. When textile looms run uncontrollably for three weeks without human operators, the factory wires: "In the devil's name, take the finished goods off us, send us raw material, stop the machines!" (010-004)

## Čapek's Satire

Through the demon/divine ambiguity, Čapek satirizes how institutions determine what counts as legitimate religion. The Absolute is classified as divine or demonic based entirely on institutional convenience rather than its intrinsic nature. The distinction between God and Devil becomes a matter of branding.
