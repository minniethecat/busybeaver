## Overview

The Ceremony at Stvanice, depicted in Chapter IX of *[[the-absolute|The Absolute]] at Large*, is Čapek's satire of official pomp and public relations. The ceremonial opening of the Central [[the-karburator|Karburator]] Electric Power Station on Stvanice Island — a concrete building "no bigger than a public convenience" — brings together the President, the diplomatic corps, and the city's dignitaries. As the Burgomaster's speech drags on interminably, the [[the-absolute|Absolute]]'s presence overwhelms the assembled guests, reducing the ceremony to chaos. The chapter is narrated through the eyes of [[cyril-keval|Cyril Keval]], a young reporter from the *People's Journal*, who watches the proceedings through a small window in the concrete wall.

> [(009-001)] Cyril Keval, district reporter on the staff of the Prague *People's Journal*, hurried into evening-dress for the occasion and dashed off to Stvanice just after six o'clock in the evening, to write up the ceremonial opening of the new Central Karburator Electric Power Station for Greater Prague.

## The Setting

The Central Power Station is an absurdly modest structure for such a momentous occasion. The veteran journalist Cvancara of the *Venkov* — "somewhat like a meditative heron" — provides the cynical commentary of a forty-year press veteran:

> [(009-003)] "Well, my friend," he said quietly to the young journalist, "it's safe to bet on something happening today. I've never yet seen a function where there wasn't some silly incident or other."

[[cyril-keval|Keval]], playing the eager young reporter, invents an elaborate cover story on the spot: the visible station is merely "for show," while the real power station lies in a secret underground network of "sixteen [[the-karburator|Karburator]] fortresses" around [[prague-in-the-novel|Prague]] — explaining the city's perpetual street repairs as military construction.

## The Ceremony

The guest list assembles the entire machinery of state: the Burgomaster of [[prague-in-the-novel|Greater Prague]], the Minister for War, the Chairman of the [[m-e-c|M.E.C.]], the Chief Rabbi, the French Ambassador, the Archbishop, the Italian Ambassador, the President of the Senate, and the Chief of the Sokol organization. The President arrives along a crimson carpet to the strains of the national anthem.

> [(009-010)] "I'll never get in now," Keval said to himself. "Cvancara is right. They always do something silly. Fancy putting up that little hut for an imposing ceremony like this."

[[cyril-keval|Keval]], locked out of the building, discovers a small window in the concrete wall about two metres from the ground. He leaps up, catches the grating, and becomes the ceremony's unofficial witness. What he sees is bureaucratic time stretched to the breaking point:

- The Burgomaster speaks interminably, his face progressing from red to "purple bordering on blackness"
- The President stands with his hand on the lever throughout
- The Minister of Public Works "cutting his sentences down unmercifully to shorten his address"
- Old Billington, doyen of the Diplomatic Corps, "had passed away on his feet, preserving even in death the expression of an attentive listener"
- [[g-h-bondy|G. H. Bondy]] says "a few words" and concludes

> [(009-016)] The Chief of State drew himself erect and pressed the lever. Then, in an instant, the whole of Prague shone out as a vast expanse of light, the crowds cheered, the bells in all the steeples began to swing.

## The Absolute Strikes

The ceremony's aftermath is the chapter's satirical climax. As [[cyril-keval|Keval]] peers through the window at the interior after the lever is pressed, he sees something that makes him utter "a hoarse cry," roll his eyes, and drop heavily to the earth. [[g-h-bondy|Bondy]], fleeing the building "as pale as death," encounters [[cyril-keval|Keval]]:

> [(009-019)] "Let go of me," Bondy panted. "For Christ's sake, let go. And get out of here, as quick as you can."
>
> "But what has happened to them in there?"
>
> "Let me go," shouted Bondy; and knocking Keval back with his fist, he disappeared among the trees.

From inside the concrete building come "sounds as of savages chanting a hymn." [[the-absolute|The Absolute]] has overwhelmed the assembled dignitaries — the same force that turned Marek's porter into a miracle-worker and the [[brevnov|Břevnov]] cellar into a shrine has now claimed the highest officials of the state.

## The Official Denial

The chapter ends with a masterpiece of bureaucratic evasion — the Czechoslovak Press Bureau's official statement:

> [(009-023)] Contrary to the reports issued by a local publication which have obtained some currency abroad, we are able to state on the very best authority that no improper incidents of any kind took place on the occasion of the formal opening of the Karburator Central Power Station.

The statement concedes that the Burgomaster has "resigned his office and has gone into the country to recuperate," insists that Mr. Billington is "well and active" (despite having died during the speeches), and concludes with a declaration of principle: "Every citizen has the right to fall to the earth and worship [[the-absolute|God]], and the performance of miracles is not in conflict with any official position whatever in a democratic State." The final diagnosis — "insufficient ventilation, combined with excessive strain upon the nerves" — is a perfect bureaucratic euphemism for the presence of the divine.

## Narrative Significance

The chapter operates on two satirical levels simultaneously. On the surface, it mocks the pomposity of official ceremonies — the endless speeches, the unspoken competition for precedence, the absurdity of putting the entire apparatus of state inside a concrete shed. But beneath this is a more profound satire: the absolute inadequacy of official language to describe genuine transcendence. The Press Bureau's statement, with its careful qualifications and its reduction of divine ecstasy to "insufficient ventilation," is Čapek's portrait of modernity's fundamental incapacity to acknowledge what it cannot control.

The chapter also serves as a turning point in the novel's plot. Before Stvanice, the [[the-absolute|Absolute]]'s influence was largely confined to individuals and small groups. After Stvanice, with the Central Power Station radiating the [[the-absolute|Absolute]] across [[prague-in-the-novel|Prague]], the phenomenon becomes unstoppable — setting the stage for the mass conversions, sectarian conflicts, and eventually the [[the-greatest-war|Greatest War]] that will consume the world.
