# Mr. Hudec

Mr. Hudec is a local resident who visits Jan Binder Kuzenda's dredge on the Vltava River, initially out of curiosity about the reports of miraculous occurrences, and is drawn into the emerging Absolute-inspired religious community.

## Arrival at the Dredge

Mr. Hudec's first appearance is in Chapter VIII, when he arrives at the dredge after hearing about the strange events taking place there. He identifies himself modestly from the riverbank:

> "It is I am," came a voice from the side of the dredge. "My name's Hudec, and I'd very much like to hear you." (008-022)

Brych, the stoker, welcomes him warmly, and Hudec is helped aboard:

> "You are very welcome, Mr. Hudec," the stoker shouted down. "Come up, will you?—there's a ladder here. Half a minute and I'll give you a hand, Mr. Hudec, seeing you've never been here before." (008-023)

## A Skeptical Visitor

Unlike the regular attendees who have already embraced the Absolute's influence, Mr. Hudec arrives as an outsider — curious but not yet converted. When asked about his intentions, he admits he came merely to observe:

> "Why should I?" returned Mr. Hudec. "I just wanted to see your — to be present at your — séance." (008-033)

Mr. Brych gently corrects his choice of words:

> "Our service," Brych mildly corrected him. "We are all brothers, here, you know." (008-034)

This exchange reveals both Hudec's initial skepticism — his use of the word "séance" suggests he views the gathering as something occult or unusual — and the community's deliberate reframing of their activities in religious terms.

## Role in the Narrative

Mr. Hudec serves a narrative function as the newcomer through whose eyes the reader encounters the dredge community. His skepticism and curiosity mirror what a reasonable person of the time might feel upon hearing reports of miraculous industrial byproducts. His presence allows Brych and Kuzenda to explain their activities, providing exposition about the nature of the Absolute's effects.

## The Baker's Companion

Mr. Hudec arrives in the company of other local residents — the postman and the gamekeeper — indicating that the dredge gatherings have become a topic of widespread local discussion. The fact that multiple people travel together to attend suggests that the phenomenon has sparked organized community interest beyond individual curiosity.

## Significance

As a minor character, Mr. Hudec represents the average citizen's response to the Absolute phenomenon: initial skepticism, curiosity, and the gradual pull of the miraculous. His appearance in Chapter VIII helps establish the grassroots, community-level spread of the Absolute's influence — the first stage of what will later become a worldwide religious movement.
