## Role in the novel

Mr. Machat appears first in Chapter II as a tenant seeking central heating for his New Town residential block [532-PN]. His visit to Marek's workshop serves as a narrative device to demonstrate the Karburator's psychological effects on an ordinary citizen — Machat arrives with a business complaint and leaves in a state of uncharacteristic benevolence. He reappears in Chapter VI at the M.E.C. board meeting, where his transformation has deepened into full religious ecstasy, culminating in the founding of a charitable community that persists throughout the novel.

## The heating complaint

Machat's grievance is practical and mundane [532-PN]:

> "Mr. Machat, the proprietor of that block, is with us today. Would you mind, Mr. Machat?"

When prompted, the elderly gentleman initially seems confused [532-PN]:

> "The elderly gentleman of that name awoke as from a dream."

His complaint — that Marek's newly installed central heating system doesn't work — would normally provoke defensiveness. Instead, under the Karburator's influence, both parties experience a transformation.

## The Absolute effect

The scene's comedy derives from Machat's sudden shift from aggrieved customer to benign patron. When Marek points out that the heating was installed only the previous year [550-PN]:

> "'I did?' said Machat in tones of surprise. 'Oh, yes, you're right, so I did. But you know, that doesn't matter. People must make progress.'"

This is not the response of a property owner who came to complain about an expensive non-functional installation. Machat has been affected by the Absolute emanating from Marek's Karburator — the same phenomenon that will later sweep through society at scale. His individual transformation prefigures the mass conversions and ecstasies of the novel's later chapters.

## The board meeting and religious transformation

By Chapter VI, Machat's conversion has progressed far beyond goodwill. At the M.E.C. board meeting, he reveals that he has given away his entire property [550-PN]:

> "'But, you see, I have given those houses away now. I gave them all away.'"

When Bondy presses him on the recipients, Machat's response is simple and absolute [554-PN]:

> "Machat flushed slightly. 'Well, to poor people. I've let poor families occupy them.'"

Bondy, ever the businessman, interrogates him, but Machat can only stammer that he "couldn't help it" — the impulse to holiness overwhelmed his commercial instincts [558-PN]:

> "'I… I couldn't help it,' Machat stammered. 'It took me like that. Our lives should be holy, I mean.'"

## Ellen Machat and the lost engagement

A crucial subtext of the board meeting is Machat's daughter Ellen. Bondy's private reaction reveals that Ellen — "the fair-haired Ellen, with her seventy millions" — had half-consented to marry him [564-PN]. Instead, she is now tending the sick in her father's charitable community:

> "G. H. Bondy dropped his eyes. Machat's daughter Ellen, the fair-haired Ellen, with her seventy millions, tending the sick! Ellen, who was ready to be, who ought to be, who had half consented to be, Mrs. Bondy!"

This unspoken personal loss adds another layer to Bondy's growing unease about the Absolute — it has not only disrupted his business but cost him a fiancée.

## The religious community

Machat's transformation culminates in a vision of radical Christian communism. At the board meeting, he proclaims [576-PN]:

> "'If you only know how wonderful it feels to be in a state of holiness! Gentlemen, take my sincere advice. Give away all that you possess! Become poor and holy! Deliver yourselves from Mammon, and glorify the one God!'"

He proposes converting the M.E.C. factory into a religious community [580-PN]:

> "'Gentlemen, let us give the factory to the poor! I move that we change the M.E.C. into a religious community of "The Humble of Heart." Let us be the seed from which the tree of God shall spring. The Kingdom of God on earth!'"

Bondy quickly shuts down the discussion, dismissing Machat as "slightly indisposed" [594-PN]. Later, the narrator confirms that Machat's community indeed took root — Bondy recognizes it as "Old Machat's religious community" [926-PN], and the learned Dr. Vurm later classifies it among the fakir-like ascetic movements of the period [1030-PN].

## Character type and narrative function

Machat serves multiple narrative purposes. As a minor character, he demonstrates that the Absolute's effect is indiscriminate — affecting practical businessmen with real grievances as readily as religious seekers. His complete divestment of property parodies the radical economic implications of genuine religious conversion in a capitalist society. His connection to Ellen also personalizes the cost of the Absolute for Bondy, transforming an abstract business problem into a private wound.
