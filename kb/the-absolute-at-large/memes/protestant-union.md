# Protestant Union

The Protestant Union (formally the Union of Protestant States) was a military-political coalition of Protestant nations formed at Stettin in response to the Catholic crusade triggered by the Absolute phenomenon. It was one of the two major belligerent blocs in the catastrophic religious wars that consume Europe in the novel's later chapters.

## Formation

The Union was proclaimed at Stettin as the conflict between Catholics and Protestants escalated across Central Europe [1823-PPP]. The German Emperor, Kaspar I, assumed personal command of the Union's forces, declaring (as his predecessor had) that he "knew neither Catholics nor Protestants, but only Germans" [1804-PPP] — though this ecumenical sentiment proved futile against the sectarian fury unleashed by the Absolute.

## Military Campaigns

The Protestant Union's forces achieved significant early successes. By April, Protestant armies had pushed the Crusaders out of Berlin, gained a firm hold on Saxony, and occupied the neutral territory of Czechoslovakia — with the city of Prague coming under the command of a Swedish Major-General named Wrangel, possibly a descendant of the Thirty Years' War commander [1833-PPP].

By early April, Protestant armies were advancing into Austria and Bavaria through Czechoslovakia, while the Catholic forces simultaneously overran Denmark and Pomerania [1837-PPP]. The fighting was characterized by total destruction: cities razed, men killed, and women violated up to the age of fifty — but "the first things destroyed in every case were the enemy Karburators" [1833-PPP].

## Significance

The Protestant Union represents one half of the novel's culminating satire: a war in which both sides claim divine sanction, wield supernatural powers (attributed to their respective Karburators), and commit atrocities that render the theological distinctions between them meaningless. The Union is not so much a religious institution as a symptom of the Absolute's most destructive effect — the weaponization of absolute conviction.
