# Total Combustion

**Total combustion** is the foundational physical principle of the Karburator in Čapek's *The Absolute at Large* — the complete disintegration of matter into its constituent electrons, which simultaneously releases colossal energy and the imprisoned divine Absolute.

## Marek's Explanation

Engineer Marek describes total combustion to G. H. Bondy as the Karburator's core mechanism. Unlike ordinary burning that leaves ash, the Karburator annihilates matter entirely:

> "My Karburator actually does burn up matter, causes its utter combustion, so that not even a grain of dust remains. Or rather, it breaks it up, crushes it, splits it up into electrons, consumes it, grinds it — in short, uses it up completely."

## Energy Yield

The energy released through total combustion is practically infinite from even trivial amounts of fuel. Marek emphasizes the staggering efficiency:

> "With half a hundredweight of coal in the Karburator you can sail right round the world in a steamship, you can light the whole city of Prague, you can supply power for the whole of a huge factory, or anything you like. A bit of coal the size of a nut will do the heating and the cooking for a whole family."

He further predicts that any material — "the first pebble or handful of dirt we pick up in front of the house" — could serve as fuel once total combustion is mastered.

## The Absolute Byproduct

The process of total combustion does more than release energy — it liberates the divine Absolute that Marek believes is imprisoned in all matter. This byproduct is the Karburator's "one terrible defect," as Bondy's porter experienced religious conversion after exposure.
