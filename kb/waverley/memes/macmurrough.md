**MacMurrough** (also styled **MacMurrough nan Fonn**, "of the songs") is the hereditary *bhaird* of [[clan-ivor]], an aged Celtic poet whose declamations in Gaelic form the musical and emotional centrepiece of the feast at [[glennaquoich]]. His presence in the novel embodies the ancient Highland tradition of bardic poetry — a tradition that [[fergus-mac-ivor]] simultaneously honours and cynically manipulates. MacMurrough's art is both genuinely stirring and, in the changed world of the eighteenth century, an anachronism: Fergus himself observes that among "three things that are useless to a modern Highlander" are "a sword which he must not draw" and "a bard to sing of deeds which he dare not imitate" (022-004).

Scott uses MacMurrough to explore the relationship between oral poetry, clan loyalty, and political manipulation in [[highland-culture]], presenting the bard as a figure of genuine artistic power who is nevertheless instrumentalised by his chieftain for political ends.

## Biography

MacMurrough is an aged man by the time of the 1745 rising. His personal history includes a tragedy that links him to the novel's larger web of Highland feuds: his son was shot by [[baron-bradwardine]] "in a fray near Tully-Veolan, about seven years before" (020-014). This fact, revealed by Fergus in an aside to Waverley, explains the initial hostility of the aged Ballenkeiroch towards anyone connected with Bradwardine — and, by extension, the delicate clan politics that Fergus must navigate at the feast.

As the family bhaird of [[clan-ivor]], MacMurrough occupies an honoured but precarious position. He holds a "bard's croft" — a parcel of land — and receives donatives from the clan members whom he celebrates in verse. When he composed a song praising [[flora-mac-ivor]]'s beauty, he received "in donatives from the individuals of the clan, more seed-barley than would have sowed his Highland Parnassus, the bard's croft, as it was called, ten times over" (021-007).

MacMurrough's relationship with [[fergus-mac-ivor]] is one of mutual dependence: the bard needs the chieftain's patronage, and the chieftain needs the bard's songs to stir clan sentiment. Yet Fergus is perfectly aware of the instrumental nature of this arrangement. He tells his sister, "When the hand of the chief ceases to bestow, the breath of the bard is frozen in the utterance" (022-004), a Highland proverb that lays bare the economic logic beneath the romantic surface.

## Character and Traits

Physically, MacMurrough is "an aged man" (020-015). His performance style is described in detail: he begins "with low and rapid utterance," his eyes "fixed on the ground," but as his declamation progresses, "his ardor seemed to increase," his eyes cast around "as if beseeching, and anon as if commanding, attention," and his tones rise "into wild and impassioned notes, accompanied with appropriate gestures" (020-015). This is not the polite recitation of a drawing-room poet but a full-bodied, ecstatic performance in the ancient oral tradition.

The effect of his verse on his audience is electric. MacMurrough "seemed to recite many proper names, to lament the dead, to apostrophise the absent, to exhort, and entreat, and animate those who were present." Waverley discerns his own name in the torrent of Gaelic, confirmed when "the eyes of the company" turn towards him simultaneously. The audience's response is visceral: "Their wild and sun-burnt countenances assumed a fiercer and more animated expression; all bent forward towards the reciter, many sprung up and waved their arms in ecstasy, and some laid their hands on their swords" (020-015).

MacMurrough receives Fergus's gift of a silver claret cup "with profound gratitude": he drinks the wine, kisses the cup, and shrouds it "with reverence in the plaid which was folded on his bosom" (020-016). He then improvises an extemporaneous poem of thanks — a performance that, while "received with applause," does not produce "the effect of his first poem" (020-016). The distinction between the bard's inspired declamation and his dutiful praise of the chief suggests the compromised position of the poet in a patronage system.

Offstage, MacMurrough has a taste for whisky: Fergus remarks that "he has just drunk a pint of usquebaugh to correct, he said, the coldness of the claret" (023-002). [[flora-mac-ivor]] jokingly attributes her brother's theatricality to having "partaken of the inspiration of MacMurrough's cup rather than of mine" (023-006).

## Role in the Narrative

MacMurrough functions in the novel as the living embodiment of the ancient Highland bardic tradition. His performance at the Glennaquoich feast (Chapter XX) is the most extended evocation of Celtic oral poetry in the novel, and Scott uses it to convey both the power and the fragility of that tradition. The audience's ecstatic response demonstrates the bard's capacity to mobilise clan sentiment for martial purposes — a capacity that [[fergus-mac-ivor]] exploits deliberately.

At the same time, MacMurrough's art is already in decline. Fergus's cynical proverb about the frozen breath of the bard, his joke about the three useless things of a modern Highlander, and even the contrast between MacMurrough's first, inspired song and his second, merely dutiful one — all suggest that the bardic tradition cannot survive the passing of the clan system it serves. MacMurrough is, in this sense, a figure of elegy as much as celebration.

His collaboration with [[flora-mac-ivor]], who translates his Gaelic songs into English verse, also positions him within the novel's broader meditation on translation and cultural transmission. Fergus teases Flora that she is "in all the bard's councils, and acquainted with his songs long before he rehearses them in the hall" (022-002), suggesting that the bard's art is already being mediated for a wider, English-speaking audience — a process that mirrors Scott's own role as a translator of Highland culture for his readership.
