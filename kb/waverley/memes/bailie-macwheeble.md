# Bailie Macwheeble

## Biography / Character Overview

Bailie Macwheeble is the factor, or estate manager, of Tully-Veolan, and one of Scott's most successful comic minor characters. His name — Macwheeble — suggests something wheedling, negotiating, and, in the Bailie's case, eating. He is shrewd, practical, and utterly devoted to the Bradwardine family, though his devotion expresses itself less in heroic gesture than in the meticulous management of their financial affairs.

Macwheeble represents the commercial Scotland that was emerging alongside — and would eventually supplant — the feudal world of Baron Bradwardine. His skills are legal and financial rather than martial; his weapons are documents and accounts, not broadswords. The Bailie's relationship with the Baron is one of affectionate complementarity: the Baron provides the learning and the lineage; Macwheeble provides the practical competence that keeps the estate solvent.

## Role in the Narrative

Macwheeble serves as comic relief and as a reminder that the feudal world Scott depicts was sustained by economic infrastructure — estates, rents, accounts, and the men who managed them. His presence also grounds the novel's political themes: Macwheeble's Jacobitism is a matter of loyalty to his patron rather than ideological conviction, illustrating the personal, clientelist nature of political allegiance in the old Scotland.

## Appearances

Macwheeble appears intermittently from ch08 through the novel's conclusion, particularly in the Tully-Veolan chapters.
