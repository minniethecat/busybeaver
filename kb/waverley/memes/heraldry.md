# Heraldry

**Heraldry** — the science of armorial bearings, genealogical blazonry, and family escutcheons — runs through _Waverley_ as a thematic thread connecting family pride, political allegiance, and personal honour. From the opening chapters at Waverley-Honour to the comic pedantry of Baron Bradwardine, heraldic display functions as a visual language of identity that the novel both celebrates and gently satirizes.

## The Waverley Arms

The novel's opening chapters establish heraldry as the Waverley family's central preoccupation. Sir Everard Waverley's study of genealogy and armorial bearings defines his character:

> …the bearings, to use the language of heraldry, remain the same, though the tincture may be not only different, but opposed in the most irreconcilable manner. [001-004]

Scott deploys heraldic terminology — "bearings," "tincture" — to describe the divergence of political principles between the Tory Sir Everard and his Whig brother Richard. The heraldic metaphor is exact: the brothers share the same "bearings" (family identity) but wear opposing "tinctures" (political colours). This opening conceit establishes heraldry as the novel's master metaphor for the relationship between inherited identity and individual choice.

## Sir Everard's Genealogical World

Sir Everard has spent his life in genealogical study, constructing a heraldic tree that substitutes for living descendants:

> …he unrolled the tree of his genealogy, which, emblazoned with many an emblematic mark of honour and heroic achievement… [002-009]

The "emblazoned" genealogy — decorated with armorial symbols — represents Sir Everard's investment in a heraldic past rather than a living present. Scott describes how Sir Everard "associated a shield emblazoned with three ermines with the idea of personal purity" [002-020], showing how deeply heraldic symbols have been internalized as moral categories.

The irony is that Sir Everard's genealogical obsession, while providing Edward with a rich imaginative education, also leaves the boy unprepared for practical life. Heraldry, in this sense, is both a repository of meaning and a retreat from reality.

## The Baron of Bradwardine

The novel's most elaborate heraldic comedy involves Cosmo Comyne Bradwardine, whose pedantic obsession with feudal and heraldic minutiae provides some of the novel's most memorable passages. The Baron's famous disquisition on the bootjack — arguing that this humble object deserves heraldic recognition — is a set-piece of learned absurdity:

> …the Baron's bootjack may not stand as fair in heraldry as the water-buckets, wagons, cartwheels… [050-019]

The Baron argues that an object's heraldic worthiness depends not on its dignity but on the service it renders. This comic logic — seriously presented — reveals Scott's ambivalent attitude toward heraldry: it is both a genuine system of honour and an inherently ridiculous one.

Earlier, Edward Waverley finds the Baron's heraldic discourse exhausting: "he sometimes cursed in his heart the jargon of heraldry, its griffins, its moldwarps, its wyverns" [004-006]. The "jargon of heraldry" — its specialized vocabulary and fantastical beasts — represents a closed world of meaning that the modern young man finds impenetrable.

## Heraldry and Honour

For all its comic treatment, heraldry in _Waverley_ is not merely an object of satire. It represents a genuine code of honour rooted in ancestral obligation. The Baron's concern that any marriage arrangement should "not blemish my own coat of arms" [053-018] reflects a sincere belief that the escutcheon is a moral ledger — that stains on the shield correspond to stains on the character.

This connection between heraldic display and personal honour is reinforced throughout the novel. The "boast of heraldry" [014-006] — the pride taken in armorial ancestry — is presented as both admirable and limiting, a force that binds characters to their forebears while potentially blinding them to present realities.
