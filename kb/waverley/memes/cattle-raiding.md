# Highland Cattle-Raiding

## Definition / Overview

Cattle-raiding (*creagh* in Gaelic) was a deeply embedded feature of Highland society, functioning simultaneously as an economic enterprise, a form of social signalling, and an instrument of clan politics. Far from being mere banditry, the practice was governed by an elaborate customary code: certain times of year were considered the "lifting season," certain clans specialised in the trade, and the concept of *tascal* (a reward for information about stolen cattle) provided a quasi-legal framework for recovery. A successful raid demonstrated a young warrior's courage and competence; a chief who protected his clan's herds — or who enriched them through raids on rivals — proved his fitness to lead.

## Historical Background

Cattle were the primary form of movable wealth in the pre-modern Highlands, and their theft was, in effect, the Highlands' version of asset appropriation. The practice intensified after the Disarming Acts, which deprived clans of their military capacity for open warfare, channelling aggression into raids that were technically criminal but culturally licit. Lowland and English observers regarded cattle-raiding as simple theft; Highlanders saw it as a legitimate extension of clan rivalry, akin to the border reiving that had been suppressed in the Lowlands a century earlier.

## Role in Waverley

Scott presents cattle-raiding with ethnographic precision, particularly in the chapters concerning Donald Bean Lean. This "worthy" Highland cateran operates with the tacit protection of Fergus MacIvor and embodies the moral ambiguity of the practice: he is courteous to Waverley, loyal to his chief, and yet "systematic" in his depredations. The Baron of Bradwardine's dispute with Fergus over stolen cattle — and his indignation at the suggestion of "composition" (monetary settlement) — illustrates the gulf between Highland and Lowland attitudes: for the Baron, to accept payment for stolen cattle is ignominious, a stain on his honour.

## Related Characters

**Donald Bean Lean**: The novel's principal cattle-raider, a "gentleman" of the trade whose depredations extend to kidnapping as well as theft. **Fergus MacIvor**: The chieftain who benefits from the raiding economy while maintaining deniability — his authority provides the protective umbrella under which Donald Bean Lean operates. **Baron Bradwardine**: The Lowland victim of Highland depredation, whose outrage at cattle theft is framed in terms of legal and personal honour.
