**Colonel Gardiner** is the lieutenant-colonel commanding the regiment of dragoons into which [[edward-waverley]] is commissioned at the outset of the novel. A figure of striking personal history and unimpeachable integrity, Gardiner functions as a moral benchmark in the narrative: his death at the [[waverleys-trial]] in 1745 is one of the novel's most harrowing set-pieces and a moment that haunts Waverley's conscience for the remainder of the story.

Gardiner is based on the historical Colonel James Gardiner (1688–1745), who was killed at the Battle of Prestonpans while commanding the 13th Dragoons. Scott follows the broad outlines of the historical record — Gardiner's reputed youthful dissipation, his dramatic religious conversion, and his gallant death — while weaving the character into Waverley's fictional story.

## Biography

Colonel Gardiner first appears as "himself a study for a romantic, and at the same time an inquisitive youth." The narrator describes him as "tall, handsome, and active, though somewhat advanced in life" (007-003). His early years had been marked by what is euphemistically called gaiety, but his later life was defined by a dramatic religious conversion:

> It was whispered that a supernatural communication, of a nature obvious even to the exterior senses, had produced this wonderful change; and though some mentioned the proselyte as an enthusiast, none hinted at his being a hypocrite. (007-003)

This "singular and mystical circumstance" gives Gardiner "a peculiar and solemn interest" in Waverley's eyes (007-003), and the colonel's character shapes the moral tone of the regiment, which is more "sedate and orderly than a military mess always exhibits."

Gardiner's relationship with Waverley becomes strained when the young officer overstays his leave of absence in Scotland. Gardiner writes a first letter of "kind and gentle remonstrance" (051-010), warning of danger from "both foreign invasion and insurrection among the disaffected at home" and requesting Waverley's return to headquarters. When this and a second letter go unanswered (intercepted by the machinations of [[donald-bean-lean]]), Gardiner issues a peremptory third summons, the formal tone of which Waverley resents deeply (025-009).

Waverley's indignant response — a cold letter of resignation — is later revealed to be unjust. The intercepted letters, discovered in Waverley's portmanteau after the battle, show that Gardiner "must have had every reason to conclude they had come safely to hand" and that "less could not follow, on their being neglected, than that third and final summons" (051-012). Waverley is forced to make the *amende honorable* "to the memory of the brave and excellent writer."

Gardiner's conduct during the mutiny within Waverley's troop further vindicates his character. He rebukes the condemned mutineers, and his explanations convince Sergeant Houghton that the real instigator was the pedlar Ruthven, not Waverley. Houghton declares that "it was dishonourable and against Old England, the squire could know nought about it" (051-021). This conviction produces "that revolution in Colonel Gardiner's opinion which he expressed to Talbot" (051-022). [[colonel-talbot]] later reports that Gardiner, "from late circumstances, from a reexamination of the persons engaged in the mutiny, and from his original good opinion of your character, he was much softened towards you" (049-031).

Gardiner's death at Prestonpans is one of the most vividly realised battle deaths in the novel. Deserted by his own soldiers, he rides alone through the field to take command of "a small body of infantry, who, with their backs arranged against the wall of his own park (for his house was close by the field of battle), continued a desperate and unavailing resistance" (047-016). Already covered in wounds, he is "brought from his horse by the blow of a scythe" and receives "more wounds than would have let out twenty lives." In his final moments, he fixes Waverley with "an upbraiding, yet sorrowful, look" — a gaze that "did not strike him so deeply at that crisis of hurry and confusion as when it recurred to his imagination at the distance of some time" (047-016).

## Character and Traits

Gardiner is defined by the conjunction of martial valour and deep personal piety. [[fergus-mac-ivor]], while declining to carry Waverley's challenge to him, acknowledges that "Gardiner is a precise Huguenot, and has adopted certain ideas about the sinfulness of such rencontres, from which it would be impossible to make him depart, especially as his courage is beyond all suspicion" (025-019). This combination — a man who will not duel but whose bravery no one doubts — makes Gardiner a distinctive figure in a novel otherwise saturated with [[chivalry]] and the code of honour.

His religious conversion, "from doubt, if not infidelity, to a serious and even enthusiastic turn of mind" (007-003), is treated by Scott with respectful circumspection. The hint of the supernatural in his conversion story — "a supernatural communication, of a nature obvious even to the exterior senses" — is left deliberately ambiguous, but it colours the moral authority Gardiner carries in the narrative.

As a commanding officer, Gardiner is patient and forbearing. His first letter to Waverley is a "kind and gentle remonstrance" (051-010); his later severity is forced by circumstances beyond his control. Even after Waverley's apparent defection, Gardiner is capable of revising his judgment when evidence emerges, as his conversation with [[colonel-talbot]] demonstrates.

## Role in the Narrative

Colonel Gardiner serves as the novel's most fully realised representative of Hanoverian military virtue. In a narrative that often sympathetically portrays the Jacobite cause, Gardiner provides a counterweight: a man whose courage, piety, and integrity command respect irrespective of political allegiance. His death is a rebuke to the romance of rebellion, and his dying look at Waverley becomes a persistent moral goad that pushes the hero towards his eventual reconciliation with established authority.

Gardiner's posthumous influence on Waverley is profound. In the lonely winter on the shores of Ullswater, "a still more anxious recollection haunted his slumbers — it was the dying look and gesture of Colonel Gardiner" (060-020). This image, recurring alongside [[colonel-talbot]]'s rational arguments, helps complete Waverley's maturation from romantic adventurer to sober adult.

The historical Gardiner's house at Bankton, with its park wall against which the infantry made their last stand, is woven into the fictional geography with the precision of a military historian. Scott uses the topography of the battle to heighten the pathos of the colonel's death: he dies defending his own home.
