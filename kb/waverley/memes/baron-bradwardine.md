# Baron Bradwardine

## Biography / Character Overview

Baron Cosmo Comyne Bradwardine of Tully-Veolan is one of Scott's great comic creations — a Lowland Scottish laird whose mind is so saturated with feudal law and antiquarian learning that he inhabits a mental world several centuries removed from the 18th century in which he lives. The Baron is a gentleman of ancient lineage, moderate fortune, and immoderate erudition. He fought in the Rising of 1715 and remains a committed Jacobite, though his politics are less a matter of calculation (like Fergus MacIvor's) than of hereditary reflex: the Bradwardines have always been for the Stuarts, therefore the Baron is for the Stuarts.

The Baron's defining characteristic is his pedantry — specifically, his exhaustive and exhausting knowledge of feudal law. He can discourse at length on the precise legal distinction between *sasine* and *infeftment*, on the proper forms of *wassailing*, on the feudal services due from a vassal to his superior. This learning is simultaneously impressive and ridiculous, and Scott handles it with affectionate irony. The Baron is not mocked but celebrated — his pedantry is the expression of a complete, coherent, and now-vanishing world-view.

Beneath the pedantry, the Baron is a man of genuine decency and courage. He fights bravely at Prestonpans, protects Waverley when the young man is in danger, and bears the collapse of the Jacobite cause with dignity. His eventual restoration to Tully-Veolan — the estate having been purchased back by Waverley and Colonel Talbot — provides the novel with its satisfying comic resolution.

## Family and Relationships

The Baron's most important relationship is with his daughter Rose, whom he loves deeply but whose romantic life he manages with endearing incompetence. His dealings with Fergus MacIvor are characterised by a tension between political alliance and personal irritation — Fergus's cattle-raiding and his ambition both grate on the Baron's sense of propriety. His relationship with Waverley is paternal: he sees in the young Englishman a worthy recipient of his learning and eventually a suitable husband for his daughter.

## Role in the Narrative

The Baron serves several functions: he is the novel's principal source of comedy, its living repository of Scottish feudal tradition, and — along with Fergus — one of the two poles of Jacobite character between which Waverley navigates. Where Fergus represents the dangerous glamour of Jacobitism, the Baron represents its homely, decent, and slightly absurd domestic face.

## Appearances

The Baron appears throughout the novel:
- ch08–ch14: Tully-Veolan, introduction to Waverley
- ch30–ch48: Military campaign, Prestonpans
- ch60–ch72: Post-Rising, restoration to Tully-Veolan
