# Dirk

The **dirk** (_biodag_ in Gaelic) is the long Highland dagger worn at the belt, serving as both weapon and status symbol in _Waverley_. Paired with the [[claymore|broadsword]], the dirk completes the Highland warrior's armament. Scott mentions dirks alongside claymores and targets as the three essential elements of Highland martial equipment.

## Weapon and Ornament

Fergus MacIvor's dirk exemplifies the weapon's dual role as fighting blade and status display:

> …he had no weapon save a dirk, very richly mounted with silver. [018-058]

The silver mounting transforms the dirk from a mere weapon into an object of material display — a marker of chiefly rank. Fergus's dirk is "richly mounted" while his page carries the claymore, suggesting that the dirk's ornamental quality makes it suitable for a chief to carry personally even on casual excursions.

## In Flora's Lyric

Flora MacIvor's verses catalogue the dirk among the Highland warrior's essential gear:

> They call to the dirk, the claymore, and the targe,
> To the march and the muster, the foray and charge. [022-029]

The dirk appears first in the poetic enumeration — the closest weapon to the body, worn at the belt rather than carried in the hand. This physical proximity reflects the dirk's role as the personal, intimate weapon, as opposed to the claymore's public, martial function.

## Martial Context

Dirks could be wielded alongside broadswords in the heat of battle. The Highland charge — a tactic involving volley fire followed by a downhill rush with sword and dirk — relied on the dirk as the close-quarters follow-up weapon after the initial claymore stroke. Scott's description of Highland warriors "laying about them with their claymores" [036-022] implies the dirk's complementary role in the melee.
