# Education of a Scottish Gentleman

## Definition / Overview

The education of a Scottish gentleman in the 18th century followed a distinctive pattern, shaped by Scotland's legal and religious institutions, its comparatively democratic university system, and the widespread custom of completing one's formation through travel on the European continent. Unlike the English gentleman, whose education might be limited to a public school and possibly Oxford or Cambridge, the Scottish gentleman typically received a broad classical grounding, often followed by legal study at Edinburgh or Glasgow, and not infrequently rounded off by a Continental tour that exposed him to French, Italian, and German culture.

## Historical Background

Scotland's educational system in the 18th century was, by the standards of the time, remarkably accessible. The parish school system established by the Kirk after the Reformation ensured basic literacy across social classes, while the universities — particularly Edinburgh, Glasgow, Aberdeen, and St Andrews — provided advanced instruction at relatively modest cost. Legal education was a common path for young men of good family who did not expect to inherit the estate: the Faculty of Advocates in Edinburgh was a finishing school for the Scottish elite. Many supplemented this formal education with travel, especially to France and the Low Countries, acquiring languages, polish, and — in the case of those with Jacobite sympathies — political connections.

## Role in Waverley

Edward Waverley's education is the subject of sustained authorial attention in the early chapters, and Scott uses it to establish both his hero's character and his structural position between two cultures. Waverley's desultory reading in the great library at Waverley-Honour, under the eccentric tutelage of Mr. Pembroke (a nonjuring clergyman), produces a mind "abandoned to the orgies of fancy" — furnished with romantic ideas about honour, chivalry, and the past, but utterly unprepared for the practical demands of military or political life.

This education is at once Waverley's distinction and his vulnerability. His extensive but undirected reading renders him susceptible to the romantic appeal of the Highlands and the Jacobite cause; his lack of formal discipline makes him an easy target for manipulators like Donald Bean Lean. Scott's treatment of Waverley's education is gently ironic — the narrator notes that "the principal object of his studies was to acquire a general knowledge of books" — but also sympathetic: Waverley's cultivated sensibility is the basis of his genuine moral discrimination.

## Related Characters

**Edward Waverley**: The novel's protagonist, whose education exemplifies both the virtues and the dangers of the Scottish model — broad learning, romantic imagination, insufficient practical discipline. **Mr. Pembroke**: Waverley's tutor, a nonjuring clergyman whose Jacobite principles and pedantic scholarship shape his pupil's intellectual formation. **Baron Bradwardine**: A product of the same educational tradition, whose legal learning is matched only by his impracticality.
