# Mr. Pembroke

## Biography / Character Overview

Mr. Pembroke is the nonjuring clergyman employed as tutor to the young Edward Waverley at Waverley-Honour. A man of immense, pedantic, and entirely impractical learning, Pembroke is the author of "two treasonable sermons" (032-018) and the compiler of a manuscript that, if published, would — in his own estimate — revolutionise the understanding of British constitutional history. It never is published, of course: Pembroke's learning, like his Jacobite principles, belongs to a world that has already passed.

Pembroke is one of Scott's comic pedants, akin to Baron Bradwardine but without the Baron's redeeming warmth and active engagement with the world. His influence on Waverley is profound but indirect: Pembroke's romantic Jacobitism, his immersion in the literature of chivalry and lost causes, and his inability to distinguish between the world of books and the world of action are all qualities that his pupil inherits — to nearly fatal effect.

## Role in the Narrative

Pembroke appears only in the novel's early chapters, but his influence pervades the entire book. Waverley's "wavering" — his susceptibility to romantic glamour, his difficulty in distinguishing fantasy from political reality — is Pembroke's legacy. Major Melville's catalogue of the evidence against Waverley includes "his tutor a nonjuror and the author of two treasonable sermons" (032-018), linking Waverley's Jacobite entanglement directly to his education.

## Appearances

Pembroke appears in ch01–ch05 (Waverley's education) and is referenced throughout.
