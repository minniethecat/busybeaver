## Overview

The Jacobite army of 1745 was a remarkable military force: raised from the Highland clans in a matter of weeks, it defeated the government army at [[battle-of-prestonpans-1745|Prestonpans]], marched into England as far as Derby, and held together through a long retreat in worsening winter conditions before its final defeat at Culloden. Its organisation was clan-based — each chief commanded his own men — and its tactical doctrine centred on the Highland charge, a shock tactic that had proved devastating at Prestonpans but was fatally vulnerable to disciplined musket fire on open ground.

Scott's treatment of the Jacobite army in *Waverley* is characteristic in its dual focus: he depicts its glamour — the clan regiments on the march, the Prince's charismatic presence, the emotional power of the Jacobite cause — while also exposing its structural weaknesses. The factional disputes among the chiefs, the logistical chaos of a hastily assembled insurgent force, and the growing tension between Highland and Lowland contingents all receive careful attention.

## Clan-Based Organisation

The army's organising principle was the clan. Each Highland chief who declared for the Prince brought his own men, commanded them personally, and expected a degree of autonomy in their deployment. This system produced extraordinary cohesion at the regimental level — clansmen fought alongside kinsmen under chiefs they had known since birth — but created persistent difficulties at the higher command level.

[[fergus-macivor|Fergus MacIvor]]'s role in the army illustrates both the strengths and the tensions of clan-based organisation. His control over [[clan-ivor|Clan Ivor]] is absolute; his men follow him with fierce loyalty. But his relationship with other chiefs and with the Prince's council of war is fraught with rivalry and mutual suspicion. The army's command structure is less a hierarchy than a coalition, held together by shared loyalty to the Prince and by the absence of any alternative.

## The Prestonpans Campaign

The army's first and greatest victory came at [[battle-of-prestonpans-1745|Prestonpans]] in September 1745, where a government army under Sir John Cope was routed in a matter of minutes. The Highland charge, delivered at dawn through a morning mist, proved unstoppable. The victory transformed the Rising from a romantic adventure into a genuine threat to the Hanoverian regime.

Prestonpans was the Jacobite army at its best: clan cohesion, tactical surprise, and the ferocity of the Highland charge overwhelming a demoralised enemy. But the victory also concealed weaknesses that would prove fatal. The army had no siege train and could not take Edinburgh Castle; its logistical system was ad hoc; and the very speed of the victory encouraged unrealistic expectations about what the army could achieve.

## The March into England and Retreat from Derby

The army's march into England, reaching as far south as Derby, was its high-water mark — and the point at which its limitations became undeniable. The hoped-for English Jacobite rising had not materialised; French support had not arrived; and the government was concentrating forces that outnumbered the Jacobites. The decision to retreat from Derby, bitterly contested at the time and debated by historians ever since, was the moment when the army's strategic calculus shifted from victory to survival.

Scott's treatment of the retreat is notably sympathetic. The army's discipline on the long march back through worsening winter weather, its successful rearguard actions, and its ability to hold together despite disappointment and fatigue all testify to the resilience of clan-based organisation — even as that same organisation was proving inadequate to the strategic demands of the campaign.

## Culloden and Dissolution

The army's destruction at [[battle-of-culloden|Culloden]] in April 1746 was the inevitable terminus of its trajectory. On the open moor, the Highland charge broke against disciplined government volley fire. The clan regiments that had triumphed at Prestonpans were shattered in a single morning. The aftermath — the brutal government repression of the Highlands, the systematic dismantling of the clan system — marked the end of an era in Scottish history.

Clan Ivor's fate mirrors that of the army as a whole. Fergus's capture, trial, and execution represent the destruction not merely of one chieftain but of the entire social order his clan embodied. The army that marched south with such hopes in 1745 was, by the spring of 1746, a memory — and a warning.

## Role in Waverley

The Jacobite army is the novel's central military institution. Scott shows it from the perspective of an embedded observer — Edward Waverley serves in its ranks, witnesses both its triumphs and its dysfunctions, and ultimately escapes its destruction. Through Edward's eyes, the reader sees the army not as a monolithic force but as a complex human institution: brave, proud, faction-ridden, and doomed.
