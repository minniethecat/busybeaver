# Waverley's Arrest and Trial

## Overview

Waverley's arrest and examination at Cairnvreckan is the novel's central legal scene and the moment at which the consequences of his Jacobite entanglement become inescapable. Major Melville, a fair-minded Hanoverian magistrate, interrogates Waverley in the presence of the minister Mr. Morton, laying out the evidence: Waverley's family background of hereditary Jacobitism, his nonjuring tutor, his visits to known Jacobite households, his presence among the insurgents.

## Role in Waverley

The interrogation scene is a masterclass in Scott's handling of legal procedure as dramatic structure. Melville's systematic catalogue of the evidence against Waverley creates genuine suspense: the reader, like Waverley, sees how damning the circumstantial case is. Yet Melville's fairness — his willingness to listen, to weigh evidence, to treat his prisoner as an individual — embodies the rule of law that distinguishes the Hanoverian state from the arbitrary justice of the old regime.
