## Overview

The feast at [[glennaquoich|Glennaquoich]] is Edward Waverley's initiation into the full theatricality of Highland culture. Set in the great hall of Fergus MacIvor's stronghold, the feast combines lavish hospitality with an elaborate performance of clan identity, culminating in the bard MacMurrough nan Fonn's war-song — a performance so powerful that it electrifies the assembled clansmen and thoroughly captivates the visiting English officer.

Scott stages the feast with the eye of an ethnographer and the irony of a novelist who understands that the most "authentic" cultural performances are often the most carefully calculated. Every element of the evening — the seating hierarchy, the quality of drink, the bard's song, Fergus's own bearing — is designed to produce specific political effects. The feast is at once a generous act of hospitality, a display of chiefly magnificence, and a recruitment tool for the Jacobite cause.

## MacMurrough's War-Song: The Bard as Propagandist

The feast's dramatic climax is the performance of the clan bard, MacMurrough nan Fonn. His war-song, delivered in Gaelic and accompanied by the harp, is an incitement to battle cast in the form of art:

MacMurrough's performance demonstrates the fusion of aesthetic and political functions that characterises Highland culture as Scott depicts it. The bard is not merely an entertainer but a crucial instrument of chiefly power — his song transforms the feast from social occasion into political rally. The clansmen's emotional response to the song, rising from attention to enthusiasm to warlike fervour, is itself a demonstration of the bard's power to mobilise sentiment in the service of the chief's political goals.

## The Hierarchy of Hospitality

The feast's material arrangements encode the clan's social structure with diagrammatic precision. "Excellent claret and champagne were liberally distributed among the Chief's immediate neighbours; whisky, plain or diluted, and strong beer refreshed those who sat near the lower end" (020-006). The quality of drink maps directly onto social rank, with proximity to the chief determining not only seating but the very substance of refreshment.

This graduated hospitality is characteristic of the clan system's fusion of generosity and hierarchy. The chief provides for all, but the quality of provision marks and reinforces social distinction. The feast thus performs a double function: it binds the clan together through shared consumption while simultaneously materialising the hierarchies that structure clan society.

## Fergus as Impresario

Throughout the feast, Fergus MacIvor acts as impresario — directing the evening's performances, managing the emotional temperature of the hall, and ensuring that every element contributes to the desired political effect. His own bearing is itself a performance: the Highland chief as noble savage, combining martial dignity with patriarchal warmth.

Scott's treatment of Fergus's direction is characteristically ironic. The feast is magnificent, but its magnificence is in the service of a doomed cause. The very skill with which Fergus orchestrates the evening reveals the gap between performance and reality that will eventually prove fatal. The feast presents Highland culture at its most seductive — precisely because Fergus understands that seduction, not argument, is the most effective form of Jacobite recruitment.

## Role in the Narrative

The feast serves as Edward's most complete immersion in Highland culture before the hunting and the subsequent commitment to the Jacobite cause. The sensory richness of the experience — the food, the drink, the music, the visual spectacle — overwhelms Edward's critical faculties, making him receptive to the political message that the feast's aesthetic surface carries.

The event also introduces the character of Flora MacIvor, whose presence at the feast and whose later performance at the waterfall provide the emotional charge that Fergus's political direction requires. Flora is at once the feast's most beautiful ornament and its most effective political instrument — a duality that Scott exposes without quite resolving.
