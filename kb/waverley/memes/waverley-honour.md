# Waverley-Honour

## Description / Overview

Waverley-Honour is the English estate of the Waverley family, presided over by Sir Everard Waverley, Edward's Tory and Jacobite uncle. It is a place of comfortable retirement, extensive libraries, and fading political principles — a world in which the Jacobite cause has become a matter of sentiment and memory rather than action. It is at Waverley-Honour that Edward receives his eccentric education under Mr. Pembroke and acquires the romantic sensibility that will later make him susceptible to the glamour of the Highlands.

## Significance in Waverley

Waverley-Honour represents the English starting-point of the novel's cultural geography: it is the world Waverley leaves, the political inheritance he carries with him into Scotland, and — through Colonel Talbot's debt to Sir Everard — the connection that ultimately saves him. The estate's name, with its pun on "wavering," signals the novel's central theme.
