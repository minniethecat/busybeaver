**Mr. Rubrick** is the domestic chaplain to [[baron-bradwardine]] at [[tully-veolan]], a scholarly Episcopal clergyman whose conversation "harmonised very well with that of the Baron and his guest" (013-009). Though a relatively minor figure in the novel, Rubrick plays a small but meaningful role in the closing chapters: his kinship with the Laird of Duchran provides [[rose-bradwardine]] with a place of refuge during the dangerous aftermath of the rising, and he ultimately has "the satisfaction to unite their hands" when [[edward-waverley]] and Rose are married (070-012).

Rubrick's presence at Tully-Veolan helps complete the picture of the Baron's household as a small but fully articulated microcosm of traditional Scottish society — laird, chaplain, bailie, fool — and his role expands quietly in the novel's resolution, where his family connections prove instrumental to the happy ending.

## Biography

Mr. Rubrick is chaplain at [[tully-veolan]], a position that places him within the intimate domestic circle of [[baron-bradwardine]]. As a clergyman of the Scottish Episcopal Church — the church to which Jacobite families tended to adhere — Rubrick's religious affiliation aligns him with the Bradwardine household's political sympathies, though he is not himself portrayed as a political actor.

He is first mentioned when [[bailie-macwheeble]] "used sometimes gently to rally Mr. Rubrick, upbraiding him with the nicety of his scruples" (011-005). The Bailie, by contrast, had "kept pretty fair with all the different turns of state in his time" — the contrast between the chaplain's scrupulous consistency and the lawyer's flexible pragmatism is established early.

Rubrick appears as a dinner companion on Waverley's visit to Tully-Veolan, where "his information and discourse, as a clergyman and a scholar, harmonised very well with that of the Baron and his guest" (013-009). After dinner he joins the Baron and Waverley in ascending the "very steep, narrow, and winding stair" to Rose's apartment, following at a more leisurely pace (013-010). During the subsequent visit to the old tower, Rubrick contributes "some curious particulars" of the local superstition of Saint Swithin's Chair, which Rose then illustrates with a ballad (013-015).

During the crisis of the 1745 rising, Rubrick flees Tully-Veolan for safety. [[rose-bradwardine]] writes to Waverley: "Mr. Rubrick is fled to his cousin's at the Duchran, to be out of danger from the soldiers and the Whigs" (028-005). The Duchran, the estate of his kinsman, becomes Rose's own place of refuge while her father is in the field and then in hiding.

It is Rubrick who, after the rising's defeat, undertakes the charge of [[baron-bradwardine]]. [[colonel-talbot]] reports that [[prince-charles-edward-stuart]] complained of several gentlemen who "have fairly wrung from me, by their downright importunity, a present protection and the promise of a future pardon for that stubborn old rebel whom they call Baron of Bradwardine," among them "Rubrick of Duchran" (066-014). Talbot adds that "Rubrick has undertaken to keep him at his own house till things are settled in the country" (066-014). This intervention — securing a royal pardon through sheer persistence — is Rubrick's most consequential act in the novel.

In the closing chapters, Rubrick officiates at the marriage of [[edward-waverley]] and [[rose-bradwardine]]: "The Reverend Mr. Rubrick, kinsman to the proprietor of the hospitable mansion where it was solemnised, and chaplain to the Baron of Bradwardine, had the satisfaction to unite their hands" (070-012). His family coach forms part of the "nuptial party" that travels in "great style" (071-001). At the restored Tully-Veolan, he is present at the celebratory dinner, where the Baron "requested Mr. Rubrick, who was at home, would return thanks for the distinguished mercies it had been his lot to experience" (071-041).

## Character and Traits

Mr. Rubrick is defined primarily by three qualities: his scholarly disposition, his scrupulous principles, and his quiet loyalty to the Bradwardine family. The "nicety of his scruples" (011-005) — a trait that the worldly [[bailie-macwheeble]] finds faintly ridiculous — marks him as a man of conscience in an age of political trimming. Unlike the Bailie, who has survived by adapting to each change of regime, Rubrick maintains his principles even when they put him at risk.

As a scholar, he is comfortable in the learned atmosphere of [[baron-bradwardine]]'s table, where his conversation "harmonised" with the Baron's pedantry and Waverley's curiosity (013-009). He is knowledgeable about local antiquities and folklore, contributing "curious particulars" of the Saint Swithin's Chair superstition (013-015).

His practical loyalty to the Bradwardine family is demonstrated not in grand gestures but in quiet, effective action: providing refuge at the Duchran, securing the Baron's pardon through "downright importunity," and officiating at Rose's wedding. The narrator notes that Rubrick has "the satisfaction" of performing the marriage ceremony — a phrase that suggests his personal investment in the family's happiness.

## Role in the Narrative

Mr. Rubrick serves several modest but cumulatively significant functions. First, as the Baron's chaplain, he helps establish the texture of life at Tully-Veolan — a household complete with its spiritual as well as its secular officers. His presence alongside the Bailie and [[davie-gellatley]] sketches a miniature social order around the Baron.

Second, his family connection to the Duchran provides the narrative mechanism by which Rose finds refuge and the Baron eventually secures his pardon. Without Rubrick's kinship, the novel's resolution — the reunion of father and daughter, the marriage, the restoration — would lack its material infrastructure.

Third, as the clergyman who marries Waverley and Rose, Rubrick presides over the novel's central domestic resolution. His presence at the wedding and at the final dinner at the restored Tully-Veolan places him among the small company of loyal retainers who survive the catastrophe intact and participate in the restoration of order.
