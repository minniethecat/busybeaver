# Claymore

The **claymore** (_claidheamh mòr_, Gaelic for "great sword") is the two-handed Highland broadsword that serves as the principal weapon and status symbol of the Highland warrior in _Waverley_. Scott uses the claymore as a metonym for Highland military power — "five hundred claymores" means five hundred fighting men — and as an emblem of the clan society's martial values. The weapon appears in contexts ranging from ceremonial display to mortal combat, always carrying with it the weight of Highland honour.

## Weapon of the Clansman

The claymore is inseparable from the Highland warrior's identity. Evan Dhu Maccombich, Fergus's chief lieutenant, is introduced with his weapon prominently displayed:

> …a broadsword dangled by his side, a target hung upon his shoulder… [016-004]

The sword "dangled" suggests casual familiarity — it is worn as naturally as clothing. The pairing of broadsword and **target** (the Highland round shield, see [[target-shield]]) forms the classic armament of the Highland soldier, used in the novel's display of military exercises:

> Next, they paired off for the broadsword exercise; and, having manifested their skill in this noble art… [019-013]

## Fergus MacIvor's Claymore

The clan chief Fergus MacIvor wears his claymore as a mark of rank. His page carries the weapon for him during peaceful excursions:

> His page, as we have said, carried his claymore; and the fowling-piece, which he held in his hand, seemed only designed for sport. [018-058]

The distinction is telling: the chief carries a hunting gun ("fowling-piece") while his servant bears the claymore — yet the sword's presence is constant. The claymore is too sacred an object of chiefly authority to be left behind, even on a casual walk.

## "Five Hundred Claymores"

The claymore serves as a unit of military accounting — a synecdoche in which the weapon stands for the warrior who wields it. Fergus boasts:

> …I have seldom taken the field under five hundred claymores. [019-016]

"Five hundred claymores" means five hundred fighting men. This linguistic usage reveals the depth of identification between the Highland warrior and his weapon: to count swords is to count men. The claymore is not an accessory but a constitutive element of Highland martial identity.

## Martial Honour

The claymore is bound up with Highland conceptions of honour. The narrator speculates about Fergus's motivations:

> …at the moment he should unsheathe his claymore, it might be difficult to say whether it was for the sake of his Prince or of his own ambition. [021-004]

To "unsheathe the claymore" is to commit to war — an irreversible act that carries both personal and political consequence. The weapon's drawing marks the threshold between peace and violence, calculation and action.

## Flora's Lyric

In Flora MacIvor's poetic verses, the claymore appears alongside the dirk and targe as part of the Highland warrior's complete armament:

> They call to the dirk, the claymore, and the targe,
> To the march and the muster, the foray and charge. [022-029]

The lyric enumeration — dirk, claymore, targe — catalogs the material culture of Highland warfare in poetic form. In another verse, Flora sings of the "bloodless claymore" reddened with rust in peacetime [022-026], linking the weapon's condition to the state of Highland honour: a clean blade signifies readiness; a rusted one, decay.

## In Battle

When battle comes, the claymore's effectiveness is demonstrated with brutal economy:

> …and began to lay about them with their claymores. [036-022]

The phrase "lay about them" conveys the claymore's sweeping, two-handed fighting style — a weapon of broad, devastating strokes rather than precise thrusts. Scott's matter-of-fact description avoids glorification while conveying the weapon's lethal efficiency.
