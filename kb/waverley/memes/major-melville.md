# Major Melville

## Biography / Character Overview

Major Melville of Cairnvreckan is a Lowland Scottish gentleman, a Presbyterian, a Hanoverian magistrate, and an officer of the militia — in short, the political antithesis of the Jacobite world that dominates the novel. Yet Scott portrays him not as a villain but as a fair-minded public servant doing his duty in difficult circumstances. His interrogation of Waverley — the novel's most sustained legal scene — is a model of procedural fairness: Melville is suspicious of Waverley, as the evidence warrants, but he listens, weighs the facts, and ultimately treats his prisoner with a decency that transcends political allegiance.

## Role in the Narrative

Major Melville serves as the novel's representative of Hanoverian legal authority, and his presence provides a counterweight to the romanticised portrait of Jacobite society. His fairness to Waverley — and his willingness to see the young man as an individual rather than a political category — embodies the rule of law that the Hanoverian settlement, for all its faults, had established.

## Quotations
