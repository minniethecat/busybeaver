**Mrs. Flockhart** is the widowed landlady of the Edinburgh lodgings occupied by [[fergus-mac-ivor]] during the Jacobite occupation of the capital in the 1745 rising. A warm-hearted, plain-spoken Edinburgh woman of the middling sort, she provides comic relief and emotional grounding in the novel's martial sequences, her domestic perspective offering an implicit counterpoint to the high romance of Jacobite chivalry. She appears principally in two widely separated moments — the eve of the Jacobite army's march from Edinburgh and Waverley's melancholy return to the city after the rising has collapsed — and her fidelity to Fergus MacIvor's memory in the later scene is among the novel's most affecting tributes to the chieftain.

Her speech is rendered in a broad Scots vernacular that distinguishes her from the novel's English and Gaelic speakers, and her "soft heart" (042-027) repeatedly undermines her attempts at worldly pragmatism.

## Biography

When first encountered, Mrs. Flockhart is already established as the Edinburgh landlady of Vich Ian Vohr. Her introduction comes through a lively supper-table scene in which she banteringly converses with [[evan-dhu-maccombich]] about the coming battle against Sir John Cope. When she asks, "But will ye fight wi' Sir John Cope the morn, Ensign Maccombich?" the Gael replies with confident bravado (042-010-011). Her response mixes maternal concern with patriotic curiosity, establishing her as a sympathetic but detached observer of the Jacobite cause.

During this supper, [[fergus-mac-ivor]] entrusts her with his purse, declaring her his banker and executor: "these will serve my occasions; do you take the rest. Be my banker if I live, and my executor if I die" (042-026). Mrs. Flockhart's reaction reveals her character: "the soft heart of Mrs. Flockhart was melted within her at the Chieftain's speech; she set up a lamentable blubbering, and positively refused to touch the bequest" (042-027). [[fergus-mac-ivor]] is obliged to take back the money, and the scene dramatises the chasm between her sentimental attachment to the chieftain and her reluctance to profit from his danger.

Mrs. Flockhart, "smiling in her weeds like the sun through a mist, took the head of the table" (042-018) — the simile encapsulating her widowed state, her natural cheerfulness, and her pleasure at dining in company "so much above her usual associates." The narrator remarks drily that "she cared not how long the rebellion lasted that brought her into company so much above her usual associates" (042-018).

After the Jacobite defeat, Waverley encounters Mrs. Flockhart again while in Edinburgh under his assumed identity of Francis Stanley. She recognises him at once in the street (063-003). She brings him to her lodgings, where Fergus's bonnet still hangs on its peg. As Waverley's eyes fall on it, she explains that she will not let anyone take it down, dusting it daily herself, and sometimes "I just think I hear him cry to Callum to bring him his bonnet, as he used to do when he was ganging out" (063-006). When she asks "Oh, d'ye ken, sir, when he is to suffer?" the question carries the full weight of her helpless loyalty to a man she knows is condemned (063-006).

## Character and Traits

Mrs. Flockhart is defined by the tension between her warm, sentimental heart and her station as a practical Edinburgh tradeswoman. She is "soft" to the point of tears when Fergus speaks of his possible death (042-027), yet she is also sharp-witted in conversation, sparring spiritedly with [[evan-dhu-maccombich]] with proverbial Scots sayings. Her remark that Waverley's colonel is "no naething sae weel-far'd as your colonel, ensign" (042-006) reveals both her eye for a handsome soldier and her plain-spoken candour.

Her speech is consistently rendered in broad Scots — "Hout! hout!" (042-009), "unco kippage" (053-008), "garring the very stane-and-lime wa's dingle" (044-002) — marking her as a representative of Edinburgh's vernacular culture, distinct from the novel's English and Highland registers.

She is also gently satirised for her Presbyterian sensibility: [[fergus-mac-ivor]] jokes that he hopes to bring her "into the bosom of the Catholic church, or at least halfway, and that is to your Episcopal meetinghouse," parodying her forceful psalm-singing voice (042-039). Yet the satire is affectionate; her morning admonitions to her servants, heard through the walls, are part of the texture of domestic life in the Edinburgh wynds.

Her fidelity to Fergus's memory — dusting his bonnet, refusing to let it be removed, weeping at the thought of his execution — transforms her from a comic figure into a genuinely moving portrait of loyalty untainted by political calculation. The neighbours call her "a Jacobite," but she insists, "I am sure it's no for that — but he was as kindhearted a gentleman as ever lived, and as weel-fa'rd too" (063-006).

## Role in the Narrative

Mrs. Flockhart serves several narrative functions. First, she provides a domestic, civilian counterpoint to the exclusively male military world of the 1745 campaign. Her Edinburgh lodging is a space of food, warmth, and feminine solicitude that contrasts with the masculine arenas of battle and council. The supper scene (Chapter XLII) is one of the novel's richest evocations of conviviality, and it is Mrs. Flockhart who presides over it.

Second, her Scots vernacular and her status as an Edinburgh tradeswoman anchor the novel's action in a specific social world. She represents the urban middling sort — neither Jacobite nor Hanoverian by conviction, but loyal to individuals rather than causes. Her remark that she "cared not how long the rebellion lasted" if it brought her such company is both comically self-interested and revealing of the detachment with which many ordinary Scots regarded the dynastic struggle.

Third, her reappearance in Chapter LXIII is one of the novel's most poignant moments. The bonnet on the peg, silently maintained through the long months since the army marched, is a more powerful memorial to [[fergus-mac-ivor]] than any speech or eulogy. Her question — "when is he to suffer?" — underscores the human cost of the rebellion in a register entirely different from the political or juridical.
