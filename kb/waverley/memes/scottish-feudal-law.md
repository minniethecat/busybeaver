# Scottish Feudal Law

## Definition / Overview

Scottish feudal law was a distinctive body of legal doctrine governing land ownership, inheritance, and the obligations between superior and vassal. Unlike its English counterpart, which had been substantially modified by statute after the Restoration, Scottish feudal law retained many features of its medieval origins well into the 18th century. It was a system of interlocking rights and duties: the Crown was the ultimate superior of all land; beneath the Crown, a hierarchy of tenants-in-chief, vassals, and sub-vassals held their estates on conditions of military service, financial payment (*feu-duty*), or personal attendance. The system was renowned for its complexity — a complexity that Scott, himself trained in the law, mines for both erudition and comedy.

## Historical Background

Scotland's feudal system was introduced in the 12th century under David I and evolved along a path distinct from England's. Key differences included the persistence of *wardship* (the superior's right to manage the estate of an underage heir) and *recognition* (the payment due to a superior upon inheritance) into the modern period. The Treaty of Union in 1707 preserved Scotland's separate legal system, ensuring that feudal law remained a living body of practice — though increasingly anachronistic — throughout the 18th century. The heritable jurisdictions abolished in 1747 were themselves a feudal survival, and their abolition was one of the first major breaches in the old legal order.

## Role in Waverley

Baron Bradwardine of Tully-Veolan is the novel's living embodiment of Scottish feudal law. His surname — derived from *Bradwardine* — and his pedantic insistence on feudal terminology mark him as a man for whom legal antiquarianism is identity, not merely interest. The Baron's recitations of feudal doctrine — his disquisitions on *wassailing*, his precise enumeration of the feudal services due from his tenants, his obsessive concern with the precise legal distinction between *sasine* and *infeftment* — are simultaneously erudite and ridiculous, a characteristic Scottian mixture of learning and comedy.

The feudal framework also structures the novel's political geography. The Baron's relationship to his superior, to his own vassals, and to the Crown mirrors the larger conflict between Jacobite and Hanoverian, between old loyalty and new authority. When the Baron joins the Jacobite cause, he does so not from political calculation but from inherited loyalty — a feudal reflex rather than a strategic decision.

## Related Characters

**Baron Bradwardine**: The novel's principal vehicle for the exploration of feudal law. His pedantry, his essential decency, and his political naivety are all expressions of the feudal world-view he inhabits. **Bailie Macwheeble**: The Baron's legal agent, a more pragmatic and commercially-minded practitioner of the law whose presence highlights the gap between feudal ideal and economic reality.
