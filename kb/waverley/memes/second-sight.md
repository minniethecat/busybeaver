# Second Sight

## Definition / Overview

Second Sight (Gaelic: *An Da Shealladh*, "the two sights") was the reputed faculty of prophetic vision attributed to certain individuals in the Scottish Highlands. Those possessed of this gift were said to perceive future events — most commonly deaths, often in the form of a spectral wraith or funeral procession — with a vividness indistinguishable from ordinary sight. Unlike the learned astrology or divination of the Renaissance, Second Sight was a folk tradition, rooted in the Gaelic world's intimate relationship with the supernatural and the omnipresence of death in a society shaped by clan warfare and precarious subsistence.

## Historical Background

Belief in Second Sight was widely documented by travellers and ethnographers in the 17th and 18th centuries. Martin Martin's *A Description of the Western Islands of Scotland* (1703) devoted a chapter to the phenomenon, and Samuel Johnson, on his tour of the Highlands with Boswell in 1773, recorded local testimony about the practice with a mixture of scepticism and anthropological curiosity. The belief persisted into Scott's lifetime and forms part of the romantic apparatus of the Highland world he depicts.

## Role in Waverley

Second Sight appears in *Waverley* through the figure of the Bodach Glas, the grey spectre whose appearance presages death within the house of MacIvor. Fergus MacIvor takes the omen seriously, and his fatalism about it contributes to the novel's tragic undercurrent. Scott handles the supernatural with characteristic ambiguity: the Bodach Glas is never definitively confirmed as a genuine apparition, but its psychological effect on Fergus — and its narrative function as harbinger of the chief's doom — is treated with full seriousness. This is typical of Scott's approach to the preternatural: he shows it operating on character and plot while leaving rationalist explanations available.
