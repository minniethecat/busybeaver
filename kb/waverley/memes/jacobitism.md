# Jacobitism

## Definition / Overview

Jacobitism was the political movement that sought to restore the exiled House of Stuart to the thrones of England, Scotland, and Ireland after the Glorious Revolution of 1688 deposed James II (James VII of Scotland). The name derives from *Jacobus*, the Latin form of James. Adherents, known as Jacobites, regarded the Stuart line as the legitimate royal dynasty and the Hanoverian succession as a usurpation. The movement drew its strongest support from the Scottish Highlands, Ireland, and among English Tories and Roman Catholics, and was the driving force behind a series of uprisings throughout the 18th century, culminating in the Jacobite Rising of 1745 (032-018).

In the context of Sir Walter Scott's *Waverley*, Jacobitism is not merely a historical footnote but the central political framework around which the novel's plot turns. Scott sets his story "Sixty Years Since" — looking back from the early 19th century to the events of 1745 — precisely to capture a world in which Jacobite loyalties, though fading, still commanded fierce personal allegiance and could upend the life of a young English gentleman overnight (005-015).

## Historical Background

The Jacobite cause originated with the deposition of James II in 1688 and his flight to France. His supporters maintained that the divine right of kings rendered his removal illegitimate, regardless of his Catholicism. After James's death in 1701, the claim passed to his son James Francis Edward Stuart (the "Old Pretender" or "Chevalier de St. George"), and subsequently to his grandson Charles Edward Stuart ("Bonnie Prince Charlie" or "the Young Chevalier"), who led the rebellion of 1745 — known as "the Forty-Five" (021-004).

The movement drew on a complex coalition: Highland clan chieftains whose personal authority over their followers could deliver armed men to the cause, Episcopalians and Catholics disenfranchised by the Hanoverian settlement, Tories who regarded the Revolution Settlement as constitutionally illegitimate, and those disillusioned by the Union of 1707 between England and Scotland. Yet the strength of Jacobitism was always undermined by internal divisions and the asymmetrical nature of the coalition — Highland clansmen, Lowland gentry, English Tories, and French allies each had different objectives and levels of commitment (040-016).

## Role in Waverley

Jacobitism pervades *Waverley* at every level of the narrative. It is the cause that separates Edward Waverley from his Hanoverian regiment, draws him into the orbit of the Jacobite chieftain Fergus MacIvor, and ultimately places him in mortal peril as a suspected traitor to the Crown. Scott uses Jacobitism as a lens to examine the conflict between romantic loyalty and political reality — the novel's subtitle, *'Tis Sixty Years Since*, announces this retrospective, elegiac perspective (032-018).

The movement is embodied in different registers by different characters. For Fergus MacIvor (Vich Ian Vohr), Jacobitism is both a matter of clan honour and a vehicle for personal ambition — he sees the restoration as a path to his own advancement at the Stuart court. For Baron Bradwardine of Tully-Veolan, by contrast, Jacobitism is a matter of inherited principle, as "natural" and unquestionable as his feudal legal erudition. The Baron's family had been "hereditary Jacobites," and his politics were "greatly strengthened by habits of solitary authority" (010-005). This contrast between Fergus's strategic calculation and the Baron's principled antiquarianism shows Scott's nuanced understanding of the movement's diverse social basis.

Scott does not, however, romanticise the cause uncritically. The novel reveals the human costs of Jacobite allegiance: forced recruitment through the *creaghs* or forays that "forced many of the inhabitants to embrace the same cause from necessity, if not from inclination" (019-007), the brutal aftermath for those who chose the losing side, and the manipulation of naive young men like Waverley himself. The Chevalier appears in the novel as a figure of genuine charisma — his "usual favour" to Waverley and his courtly manners are recorded respectfully — but Scott also shows the factionalism and dissipation of his court-in-exile (050-012).

## Related Characters

Key characters whose fates are entangled with Jacobitism include:

- **Fergus MacIvor** (Vich Ian Vohr): A Highland chieftain and committed Jacobite who leads his clan into the 1745 rising. His motives combine dynastic loyalty with personal ambition
- **Baron Bradwardine**: The learned, eccentric Lowland laird of Tully-Veolan, a hereditary Jacobite whose politics are inseparable from his antiquarian identity
- **Edward Waverley**: The novel's protagonist, an English Whig by birth whose romantic imagination and personal attachments draw him unwittingly into Jacobite circles
- **Prince Charles Edward Stuart** (the Young Chevalier): The Stuart claimant, portrayed by Scott as a figure of personal charm and courtly grace, leading an army of six thousand into England

## Related Events

- **The Jacobite Rising of 1745**: The central historical event of the novel, in which Charles Edward Stuart landed in Scotland, raised his standard at Glenfinnan, marched into England as far as Derby, and was ultimately defeated at Culloden. The novel follows this arc from the gathering of the clans through the march into England to the retreat and dispersal
- **The Battle of Prestonpans** (1745): The first major engagement of the rising, in which the Jacobite army decisively defeated government forces under Sir John Cope

## Quotations
