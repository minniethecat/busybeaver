## Overview

The great hunting at [[glennaquoich|Glennaquoich]] is one of the novel's most spectacular set-pieces, a display of Highland martial culture disguised as traditional sport. Fergus MacIvor assembles "about three hundred of his clan, well armed and accoutred" (024-005) in the hills above his stronghold, with additional warriors from allied clans swelling the numbers further. What presents itself as a hunting expedition is, in reality, a military muster — a demonstration of chiefly power and a preparation for the coming Jacobite Rising.

The hunting provides Edward Waverley with his most immersive experience of clan society. For the first time, he witnesses the full scale of Highland military organisation: the tartans, the bagpipes, the patriarchal command structure, and the fierce loyalty that binds clansmen to their chief.

## The Muster as Political Theatre

Scott's genius in the hunting scene lies in his recognition that the muster is as much performance as preparation. Fergus stages the event to impress not only his own clansmen but also — and perhaps primarily — the visiting Edward Waverley. The hunting is a recruitment tool, designed to seduce the young English officer with the romance of Highland martial culture.

> The whole scene was new to Waverley, and the effect was heightened by the picturesque dresses of the Highlanders, their arms glancing in the sun, and the bold and active character of their figures and movements. (024-006)

The hunting's status as "disguised" muster is an open secret. Everyone present understands that the gathering has a purpose beyond sport, yet the fiction of the hunt is carefully maintained. This doubleness — surface entertainment masking political conspiracy — mirrors the larger structure of the novel's Jacobite plot, in which every social occasion is also a political event.

## Clan Organisation on Display

The hunting reveals the clan system's organisational logic in microcosm. The disposition of the hunters — by clan affiliation, rank, and proximity to the chief — reproduces in the field the same hierarchy visible in Glennaquoich's great hall. Junior clansmen serve as beaters; senior warriors hunt alongside the chief; allied chieftains receive ceremonial precedence.

The weaponry is particularly significant. While ostensibly hunting deer, the clansmen carry the same arms they would bear in battle. Scott makes clear that the transition from hunting party to war party requires no change in equipment or organisation — the clan is always already a military unit, and the distinction between sport and warfare is one of intent rather than form.

## Role in the Narrative

The great hunting serves as a narrative hinge between Edward's introduction to Highland society and his commitment to the Jacobite cause. Having witnessed the clan's military capacity at the hunting, Edward can no longer pretend that his stay at Glennaquoich is mere tourism. The hunting makes the coming Rising tangible, transforming Jacobitism from a romantic abstraction into a military reality.

The event also deepens Edward's relationship with Fergus. The chief's skill in directing the hunt — his tactical intelligence, his command presence, his ability to coordinate hundreds of men across difficult terrain — impresses Edward in ways that Fergus's political rhetoric alone could not. The hunting reveals what the feast could only suggest: that Fergus is not merely a performer of chieftainship but a genuine military leader.
