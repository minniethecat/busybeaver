# Pibroch

**Pibroch** (_pìobaireachd_ in Gaelic, meaning "pipership") is the classical music of the Highland bagpipe — a structured form of theme and variations played on the _pìob mhòr_ (great Highland bagpipe). In _Waverley_, the pibroch functions as the sonic signature of clan identity, a military signal, and an emotional register that marks key moments in the narrative. Scott uses the sound of pibroch and bagpipe to announce the gathering of clans, the approach of battle, and the presence of Highland power.

## The Sound of War

The pibroch's primary function in the novel is martial. It summons clansmen to battle and announces the gathering of Highland forces. As the clans assemble before the Jacobite army marches out, Scott describes:

> …each at the pibroch of their native clan, and each headed by their own Chieftain… [024-013]

Each clan has its own pibroch — a distinctive musical signature that serves the same identificatory function as the tartan pattern. The chief piper holds an honored position, second only to the chief himself:

> …the "proud step of the chief piper" of the "chlain MacIvor" was perambulating before the door… [044-002]

On the morning of battle, the pibroch is "distinctly heard" [044-002] — an auditory herald of the coming conflict. The sound fills the air alongside the visual spectacle of tartan and arms:

> …the sky itself, rang with the clang of the bagpipers, summoning forth, each with his appropriate pibroch, his chieftain and clan. [044-011]

## Ceremonial Music

Beyond the battlefield, pibroch accompanies ceremony and hospitality. At the Glennaquoich feast, three bagpipers provide continuous music:

> The bagpipers, three in number, screamed, during the whole time of dinner, a tremendous war-like medley… [020-006]

The verb "screamed" captures the piercing quality of Highland pipes in an enclosed hall — an overwhelming sensory experience that, like the Gaelic language, places the English visitor outside the acoustic culture. The pibroch's distinctive timbre — "the shrill clangour of the bagpipes" [044-018] — is repeatedly characterized by its penetrating, martial quality.

## Hunting and Leisure

The pibroch is not exclusively martial. Evan Dhu Maccombich whistles "part of a pibroch as he reloaded his piece" [016-017] during a hunting expedition, treating it as casual, ingrained music rather than formal performance. This moment reveals how deeply the pibroch is embedded in daily Highland life — it accompanies hunting and work, not only ceremony and war.

## Flora's Song

The novel's most direct poetic treatment of pibroch appears in Flora MacIvor's verses for Waverley:

> …not for the chase is the call;
> 'Tis the pibroch's shrill summons — but not to the hall. [022-029]

Flora's song contrasts the pibroch's peaceful uses (hunting, feasting) with its martial summons. The "shrill summons" of the pibroch calls the warrior away from domestic comfort — "not to the hall" but to the battlefield. This lyric crystallizes the pibroch's dual nature as both social music and death-call.

## Historical Significance

By Scott's time, the pibroch tradition was already in decline, preserved largely through the efforts of the Highland Society of London and individual collectors. Scott's representation participates in the Romantic recovery of Highland musical culture — presenting pibroch as the authentic voice of a vanishing world, simultaneously celebrated and elegized.
