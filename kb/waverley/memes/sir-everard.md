## Biography / Character Overview

Sir Everard Waverley is the elder brother of Richard Waverley and the uncle of Edward Waverley, the novel's protagonist. He inherited the family estate of Waverley-Honour and with it "the whole train of Tory or High-Church predilections and prejudices which had distinguished the house of Waverley since the Great Civil War" (002-003). This political heritage placed him in direct opposition to his younger brother Richard, who embraced Whig politics and sought advancement through government connections.

Scott describes Sir Everard as a man of "shy, proud, sensitive, and indolent" temper (002-018), whose character was shaped decisively by an early romantic disappointment. The memory of this unsuccessful amour served as "a beacon against exposing himself to similar mortification, pain, and fruitless exertion for the time to come" (002-018). Consequently, Sir Everard remained a bachelor into his seventy-second year, leaving his nephew Edward as the presumptive heir to his title and estate (002-004).

Despite his political differences with Richard, Sir Everard's essential nature was that of "the mildest of human beings" (002-008). His resentment against his brother proved short-lived, and when Richard's fortunes declined, Sir Everard did not pursue active measures against him, instead maintaining a cool but civil distance (002-019). This combination of political principle and personal gentleness defines Sir Everard's character throughout the novel.

## Family and Relationships

Sir Everard's most consequential relationship was with his younger brother Richard Waverley. The two brothers embodied the political divide of their era: Sir Everard the Tory baronet rooted in tradition and hereditary loyalty, Richard the ambitious Whig placeman who rose through ministerial patronage. Sir Everard learned of his brother's political advancement "from the public Newsletter" and tracked Richard's career with a mixture of dismay and distant observation (002-006).

His relationship with his nephew Edward was perhaps the most emotionally significant of Sir Everard's later life. Having no children of his own, Sir Everard found in Edward "something on which his hereditary pride might found the anticipated pleasure of a continuation of his lineage" (002-021). He took great interest in Edward's upbringing and education, though his political convictions prevented him from supporting Edward's entry into the Hanoverian military.

## The Lady Emily Episode

The defining event of Sir Everard's personal history was his courtship of Lady Emily at Blandeville Castle. Among "six young ladies" his taste "determined him in favour of Lady Emily, the youngest" (002-013). Sir Everard perceived "something uncommon in the restrained emotions which the young lady testified at the advances he hazarded" (002-014), and soon discovered that Lady Emily's affections were already engaged elsewhere.

In a gesture Scott explicitly compares to a romance hero, Sir Everard "withdrew his claim to the hand of Lady Emily" and "had even, before leaving Blandeville Castle, the address to extort from her father a consent to her union with the object of her choice" (002-016). This act of self-sacrifice, performed "with a grace and delicacy worthy the hero of a romance" (002-016), cemented Sir Everard's character as a man of genuine honour, but also set him on a path of permanent bachelorhood.

The psychological consequences were lasting. Sir Everard "manifested great emotion on receiving this intelligence" of Lady Emily's prior attachment (002-015), and the experience contributed to the "shy, proud, sensitive, and indolent" disposition that thereafter characterised him (002-018).

## Role in the Narrative

Sir Everard appears primarily in the early chapters of the novel, serving as both a moral touchstone and a structural device. His political convictions and family loyalty create the framework within which Edward Waverley's divided allegiances are prefigured. The tension between Sir Everard's Tory traditionalism and Richard's Whig ambition is mirrored in Edward's later struggle between Hanoverian duty and Jacobite sympathy.

> The Baronet, although the mildest of human beings, was not without sensitive points in his character: his brother's conduct had wounded him deeply. (002-008)

When Edward departs for military service, Scott marks the moment with a tender leave-taking:

> It was a melancholy day at Waverley-Honour when the young officer parted with Sir Everard, the affectionate old uncle to whose title and estate he was presumptive heir. (002-002)

Sir Everard also represents the passing of an older order. His Tory principles, his attachment to hereditary lineage, and his life at Waverley-Honour all belong to a world that the novel's events will fundamentally challenge. In this sense, Sir Everard functions as a symbol of the pre-1745 English establishment — honourable, tradition-bound, and increasingly distant from the forces reshaping Britain.
