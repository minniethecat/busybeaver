# Lady Emily

**Lady Emily Waverley** is Sir Everard Waverley's younger sister, remembered in the novel as the object of her brother's deepest affection and the unwitting architect of Edward Waverley's Highland destiny. Though she dies before the novel's main action, her marriage to a Highland gentleman creates the family connection through which Edward enters the Jacobite world.

## Sir Everard's Devotion

Lady Emily is introduced through Sir Everard's memory of her. The Baronet's "peculiar habits of life and the tone of his conversation" are shaped by a profound loss — his separation from the sister he loved:

> Sir Everard's reception in this family was, as it may be easily conceived, sufficiently favourable; but of the six young ladies, his taste unfortunately selected Lady Emily… [002-013]

Scott presents Sir Everard's attachment to his sister as the defining emotional event of his life. The phrase "his taste unfortunately selected" hints at the complication: Lady Emily's affections lie elsewhere.

## The Highland Marriage

Lady Emily's independent choice — marrying against her family's wishes — is the novel's hidden pivot. She marries a Highland gentleman, creating a blood tie between the Waverley family and the clan world of the Highlands. This connection, invisible through most of the novel's early chapters, provides the channel through which Edward Waverley is later drawn into Fergus MacIvor's circle and the Jacobite cause.

Sir Everard's response to his sister's choice reveals his character:

> Honour and generosity were hereditary attributes of the house of Waverley. With a grace and delicacy worthy the hero of a romance, Sir Everard withdrew… [002-016]

The Baronet neither condemns nor pursues — he withdraws with "grace and delicacy," preserving honour while accepting loss. This withdrawal shapes the emotional climate of Waverley-Honour, where Sir Everard lives in genealogical memory rather than present attachment.

## Legacy

Lady Emily's marriage produces the family connection that brings Edward Waverley to the Highlands. Her choice — romantic, independent, crossing the Lowland-Highland boundary — prefigures Edward's own journey from Hanoverian England to Jacobite Scotland. She is thus the novel's unseen ancestor: her romantic rebellion enables her nephew's adventure.
