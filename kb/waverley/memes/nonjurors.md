# Nonjurors

## Definition / Overview

Nonjurors were clergy of the established churches who refused to take the oaths of allegiance to William III and Mary II after the Glorious Revolution of 1688, and subsequently to the Hanoverian succession. Their position was both religious and political: they held that the oath of allegiance sworn to James II (and his heirs) was irrevocable, and that the Revolution Settlement was therefore a breach of divine and natural law. The nonjuring movement produced its own separate ecclesiastical hierarchy, which persisted in Scotland (within the Scottish Episcopal Church) and England into the late 18th century — precisely the period of *Waverley*.

## Historical Background

The nonjuring schism began in 1689, when Archbishop Sancroft of Canterbury and eight other bishops refused the oath. Deprived of their sees and benefices, the nonjurors maintained a shadow church with its own bishops, clergy, and congregations. In Scotland, the situation was more complex: the established Presbyterian Church of Scotland was firmly Hanoverian, but the Episcopal Church — to which many Jacobite gentry belonged — harboured a strong nonjuring wing. Nonjurors were not simply religious dissidents but the spiritual wing of the Jacobite movement: their refusal to abjure the Stuarts made them, in effect, a fifth column.

## Role in Waverley

The nonjuring tradition enters *Waverley* through the figure of Mr. Pembroke, Edward Waverley's tutor. Pembroke is a nonjuring clergyman whose political principles — loyalty to the Stuarts, refusal of the Hanoverian oaths — shape his pupil's education. Major Melville, cataloguing the evidence against Waverley, notes with prosecutorial precision: "his tutor a nonjuror and the author of two treasonable sermons" (032-018). The nonjuror is, in Melville's calculus, one link in a chain of Jacobite association.

Scott's treatment of Pembroke is characteristic: the clergyman is portrayed as pedantic, impractical, and politically naive — his "treasonable sermons" are the product of antiquarian zeal rather than active sedition. Yet Scott does not dismiss the nonjuring position entirely. The moral seriousness of refusing an oath on grounds of conscience, however misplaced, commands a certain respect.
