# Jacobite Rising of 1745

## Overview

The Jacobite Rising of 1745, commonly known as "the Forty-Five," was the final major attempt by the House of Stuart to regain the British throne. Prince Charles Edward Stuart ("Bonnie Prince Charlie") landed in Scotland in July 1745 with a handful of supporters, raised the Highland clans, defeated government forces at Prestonpans, marched into England as far as Derby, and held court at Holyroodhouse in Edinburgh before being forced to retreat and ultimately defeated at Culloden in April 1746.

## Role in Waverley

The Forty-Five provides the entire historical framework of the novel. Scott sets his story "Sixty Years Since" — a retrospective from the early 19th century — precisely to capture the Rising as a moment when the old Scotland and the new, Jacobite loyalty and Hanoverian order, collided. Every major character's fate is shaped by the Rising: Waverley's entanglement, Fergus's ambition and death, the Baron's exile and restoration.

## Key Phases

1. **Landing and Gathering** (July–August 1745): Charles Edward lands at Eriskay, raises his standard at Glenfinnan
2. **Edinburgh and Prestonpans** (September 1745): Occupation of Edinburgh, victory at Prestonpans
3. **March into England** (November–December 1745): Advance to Derby, council of war, retreat
4. **Retreat and Culloden** (January–April 1746): Retreat into Scotland, final defeat at Culloden

## Significance

The Rising was the last time a Scottish army invaded England and the last pitched battle on British soil. Its defeat led to the systematic destruction of Highland culture: the abolition of heritable jurisdictions, the proscription of Highland dress, and the transformation of clan chiefs into commercial landlords.
