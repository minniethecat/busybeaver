## The Ossian Poems and Their Context

The Ossianic poems — *Fingal* (1762), *Temora* (1763), and the earlier *Fragments of Ancient Poetry* (1760) — were among the most influential and controversial literary productions of the eighteenth century. Presented by James Macpherson as translations of ancient Gaelic epics composed by the blind bard Ossian in the third century, the poems sparked a fierce debate over their authenticity that continued well into Scott's lifetime.

For Scott, Ossian represented a complex cultural phenomenon: simultaneously a genuine expression of Highland oral tradition, a literary forgery that distorted that tradition, and a powerful imaginative stimulus that had reshaped European literature. The novel's single explicit reference to Ossian — grouped with "Homer and Ariosto" as "poets whom no man has ever read, and whom everyone is contented to admire" — captures this ambivalence with characteristic irony.

## Ossian in Waverley's Cultural Landscape

The Ossian controversy forms part of the novel's broader treatment of Highland culture and its representation. The poems had made the Scottish Highlands fashionable across Europe, creating the very stereotype of the "noble savage" Highlander that Scott simultaneously exploits and critiques in his portrayal of Fergus MacIvor and his clan.

The MacIvor domain at Glennaquoich stages Highland authenticity as a form of theatre, and this performance owes much to the Ossianic construction of Highland identity. Fergus's bard, the poetry recited at the feast, and the general atmosphere of antique chivalry all draw on the imaginative world that Macpherson's poems had popularised.

## Authenticity and the Problem of Cultural Transmission

Scott's position on the Ossian controversy was characteristically nuanced. While he acknowledged the strong evidence that Macpherson had substantially fabricated the poems, he also recognised that they contained genuine elements of Gaelic oral tradition. His deeper interest, however, was not in the question of factual authenticity but in the cultural dynamics that the controversy revealed.

> The whole tribe of Annot Lyle and the M'Leans in the Western Highlands are equally enthusiastic champions of Ossian.

The Ossian controversy thus functions in the novel as a case study in how cultures construct and contest their own pasts — a theme that resonates with the novel's treatment of Jacobitism as a political movement built on a partly imaginary vision of the past.

## Ossian and the Romantic Sensibility

The Ossianic poems were a crucial influence on European Romanticism, shaping the sensibilities of Goethe, Napoleon, and countless others. Scott's own literary imagination was profoundly affected by Ossian, even as his scholarly instincts led him to scepticism about the poems' authenticity.

This tension between imaginative engagement and critical distance is one that Scott reproduces in his protagonist. Edward Waverley is drawn to the Highland world by the same romantic impulses that made Ossian a European sensation — and like the Ossian controversy itself, Edward's Highland adventure exposes the gap between romantic representation and historical reality.
