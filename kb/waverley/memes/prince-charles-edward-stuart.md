# Prince Charles Edward Stuart

# Prince Charles Edward Stuart

## Biography / Character Overview

Charles Edward Louis John Casimir Sylvester Severino Maria Stuart (1720–1788), known to his supporters as the Young Chevalier and to popular memory as Bonnie Prince Charlie, was the grandson of James II (James VII of Scotland) and the Stuart claimant to the British throne. In 1745, at the age of twenty-four, he landed in Scotland with a handful of supporters and, against all expectations, raised the Highland clans, defeated the government forces at Prestonpans, marched into England as far as Derby, and held court at Holyroodhouse in Edinburgh. The adventure ended at Culloden in April 1746, after which Charles fled into the Highlands and, after five months as a fugitive, escaped to France. He never saw Scotland again.

In *Waverley*, the Chevalier appears not as a protagonist but as a figure of almost legendary glamour glimpsed by the novel's characters. Scott presents him as personally charming, courtly in manner, and genuinely brave — but also surrounded by faction, intrigue, and the fatal overconfidence of a cause that had come closer to success than its objective chances warranted.

## Role in the Narrative

The Chevalier appears directly in several key scenes: he receives Waverley at his court in Edinburgh, grants Colonel Talbot a pass, and — in one of the novel's most striking moments — bids farewell to the Baron of Bradwardine with an enigmatic valediction that the Baron treasures for the rest of his life. Scott handles the Chevalier with respect but also with distance: the Prince's charisma is acknowledged, but his cause is shown to be doomed by the very qualities — romantic glamour, factional intrigue, strategic overreach — that make it so compelling.

## Character Analysis

Scott's portrait of the Chevalier is notably generous for a writer of Tory sympathies. The Prince is neither caricatured as a Papist bogeyman (the Hanoverian propaganda line) nor sentimentalised as a flawless hero (the Jacobite one). Instead, Scott shows him as a human being — charismatic but fallible, brave but strategically outmatched — whose personal qualities were insufficient to overcome the structural weaknesses of his cause.
