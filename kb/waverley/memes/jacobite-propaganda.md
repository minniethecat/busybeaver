# Jacobite Propaganda and Song

## Definition / Overview

Jacobite propaganda was the diffuse but effective system of cultural persuasion through which the Stuart cause maintained its hold on the loyalties of Highland clans and, to a lesser extent, Lowland gentry and English Tories. Unlike modern political propaganda, it operated not through mass media but through intimate, embedded cultural forms: ballads and drinking songs performed at clan feasts, coded toasts offered at private dinners, the display of Jacobite symbols (the white cockade, the rose), and the circulation of manuscripts and printed broadsheets praising the "King over the Water." This cultural infrastructure was as important to the Jacobite movement as its military organisation.

## Historical Background

After the failure of the 1715 Rising and the effective suppression of open Jacobite political activity, the movement's survival depended on cultural transmission. Jacobite songs — many of them in Gaelic, some in Scots or English — circulated among clans and in the diaspora communities of France and Ireland. The genre included laments for the departed Stuarts, satires on the Hanoverian regime, and rallying cries like "Charlie Is My Darling" and "The Skye Boat Song." Women played a crucial role in this cultural transmission: Jacobite hostesses presided over salons where the cause was toasted and its songs performed.

## Role in Waverley

Scott depicts Jacobite cultural politics with ethnographic precision. At Fergus MacIvor's feast, the bard MacMurrough nan Fonn performs a war-song that works the assembled clansmen into a frenzy — "The tissues of their passions were so much excited that they appeared to Scott's narrator like an audience at a tragic drama" (020-016). The song is at once entertainment, historical commemoration, and recruitment tool. Fergus understands this perfectly: he controls the performance, directing the bard's cup and timing the emotional climax to maximise its political effect.

The novel also shows Jacobite culture in more intimate registers. Flora MacIvor's performance of a Highland air at the waterfall is a private, aestheticised version of the same cultural politics — beautiful, seductive, and politically charged. Throughout the novel, characters communicate their Jacobite sympathies through coded references: a toast "to the King" that pointedly avoids specifying *which* king, a snatch of song that identifies the singer's allegiance.

## Related Characters

**Flora MacIvor**: The novel's most articulate exponent of Jacobite cultural politics, whose musical performances embody the cause's fusion of aesthetic beauty and political commitment. **MacMurrough nan Fonn**: The bard of Clan Ivor, whose war-songs are weapons of political mobilisation. **Fergus MacIvor**: The strategist who deploys Jacobite propaganda as one instrument among many in his campaign for the Stuart restoration.
