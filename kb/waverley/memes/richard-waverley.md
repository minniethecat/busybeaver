## Biography / Character Overview

Richard Waverley is the younger brother of Sir Everard Waverley and the father of the novel's protagonist, Edward Waverley. Where Sir Everard embodied Tory traditionalism, Richard embraced the Whig cause with pragmatic ambition. Scott introduces him as a man whose political choices placed him at odds with his elder brother: "Sir Everard had inherited from his sires the whole train of Tory or High-Church predilections and prejudices... Richard, on the contrary, was destined for the law" (002-003). This fraternal division between Tory and Whig provides the novel's foundational political tension.

Richard's career advanced through the machinery of Whig patronage. He "met with a share of ministerial favour more than proportioned to his talents or his political importance" (002-006), rising to become a commissioner and eventually a figure of sufficient standing that he could anticipate being "distinguished as Sir Richard Waverley of Waverley-Honour" (002-004). His political success was built not on brilliance but on a methodical approach: "a grave mysterious demeanour, an attention to the etiquette of business rather more than to its essence, a facility in making long dull speeches" (025-004).

Unlike his brother, Richard had little interest in the day-to-day upbringing of his son Edward. He "was too much interested in his own plans of wealth and ambition to notice more respecting Edward than that he was of a very quiet disposition" (003-006). His relationship to his son was instrumental rather than affectionate, seeing in Edward primarily a means of securing the Waverley succession.

## Political Career

Richard Waverley's rise through Whig politics illustrates the mechanisms of eighteenth-century political patronage. His career began when his speaking abilities were noted "as a means of bringing over more converts" (002-006). He was returned "for the ministerial borough of Barterfaith" and subsequently "had taken a distinguished part in the debate upon the Excise Bill" (002-006), each advancement being communicated to Sir Everard through the public newsletters.

His ministerial connections deepened over time, and by the period of the novel's main action he was involved in political machinations at the highest level. The ministry, "after sounding Mr. Richard Waverley, were so satisfied with his sentiments and abilities as to propose that, in case of a certain revolution in the ministry, he should take an ostensible place in the new order of things" (025-005). These hints of impending greatness formed the substance of his letters to Edward, promising "greatness and influence which he was speedily to attain" (025-002).

Richard's political character is captured with ironic precision by Scott:

> This honest gentleman, by a grave mysterious demeanour, an attention to the etiquette of business rather more than to its essence, a facility in making long dull speeches, consisting of truisms and commonplaces, had raised himself to the degree of a kind of minister. (025-004)

## Family and Relationships

Richard's relationship with his elder brother Sir Everard was defined by political opposition and eventual estrangement. Sir Everard, a committed Tory, regarded Richard's Whig apostasy as a betrayal of family tradition. The severance was formal and dramatic: Sir Everard made "an excursion of the Baronet in his coach-and-six, with four attendants in rich liveries, to make a visit" to a political rival, an act widely interpreted as a deliberate snub to his brother (002-012).

Reconciliation came only indirectly, through the figure of Edward. When Sir Everard was "appeased by the exertions of the young candidate for his favour" and extended a conciliatory message to Richard, it "opened to Richard Waverley a door of reconciliation with his elder brother" (002-020). Even after this rapprochement, Richard remained primarily focused on his political ambitions rather than family life.

With his son Edward, Richard maintained a relationship of strategic distance. He was content to leave Edward's upbringing to Sir Everard and the tutor Mr. Pembroke, intervening only when political calculation demanded it — as when he arranged Edward's military commission in the Hanoverian army, "though with great dread of shocking his brother's prejudices" (005-007).

## Role in the Narrative

Richard Waverley functions as a representative of Whig political culture and a structural counterweight to Sir Everard's Tory traditionalism. His absence from the novel's central action is itself significant: he remains in London, pursuing political advancement while Edward navigates the dangerous terrain of the Jacobite rising.

Richard's political involvement has direct consequences for the plot. His ministerial connections make Edward's commission possible, but also place Edward in a compromised position when the government suspects Jacobite sympathies. Richard's letters to Edward, filled with "certain mysterious hints of greatness and influence" (025-002), serve to distract the young officer from the gravity of his situation.

In the novel's political allegory, Richard represents the new Britain — ambitious, pragmatic, and increasingly governed by party interest rather than hereditary principle. His career anticipates the Hanoverian settlement's triumph over Jacobite romanticism, just as his personal success in securing the Waverley succession (through Edward) represents the Whig absorption of the Tory landed interest.
