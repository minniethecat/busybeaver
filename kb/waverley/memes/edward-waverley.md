# Edward Waverley

## Biography / Character Overview

Edward Waverley is the only son of Richard Waverley, a Whig politician who has made his peace with the Hanoverian regime, and the nephew of Sir Everard Waverley, a Tory and Jacobite who remains at Waverley-Honour nursing old principles. This divided family inheritance — Whig father, Jacobite uncle — is the structural condition of Edward's character: he is a man raised between two political worlds, belonging wholly to neither. Educated desultorily by the nonjuring clergyman Mr. Pembroke, Waverley acquires a romantic sensibility fed by extensive but undisciplined reading in chivalric romance and poetry — "abandoned to the orgies of fancy," as Scott puts it.

Commissioned into a regiment of dragoons through his father's political influence, Waverley proceeds to Scotland, where a leave of absence takes him to Tully-Veolan, the estate of the Jacobite Baron Bradwardine. There, and subsequently at the Highland fastness of Glennaquoich, Waverley is drawn into Jacobite society — not through political conviction but through the seductions of hospitality, romance, and the glamour of a world utterly unlike his own. His eventual decision to join the Jacobite army is less a political choice than a drift — a series of accidents, misunderstandings, and personal attachments that carry him into treason almost without his noticing.

Waverley's trajectory through the novel is an education in disillusionment. The romance of the Jacobite cause fades as he witnesses its brutalities and internal contradictions; the heroic Fergus MacIvor proves to be a political manipulator; and Waverley's own life is saved only by the intervention of Colonel Talbot, a Hanoverian officer whose honour is more substantial than Jacobite glamour. By the novel's end, Waverley has been pardoned, has married Rose Bradwardine, and has settled into the quiet life of a country gentleman — his romantic education complete, his "wavering" resolved into stability.

## Family and Relationships

Waverley's family is the novel's political microcosm. His father Richard, a time-serving Whig, represents Hanoverian pragmatism; his uncle Sir Everard, the Jacobite Tory, represents the claims of hereditary loyalty. Edward inherits his uncle's sensibility without his convictions. His romantic attachments — to Flora MacIvor (idealised, unattainable) and to Rose Bradwardine (gentle, constant, ultimately chosen) — map onto his political journey: Flora embodies the glamour of the Jacobite cause, Rose the quieter virtues of domestic stability. His friendship with Colonel Talbot, forged in the shared adversity of civil war, represents the honour that transcends political allegiance.

## Role in the Narrative

Waverley is both the novel's central consciousness and its structural device: his journey from England to the Highlands, from Hanoverian to Jacobite and back, provides the armature on which Scott hangs his ethnographic portrait of 1745 Scotland. As a "passive hero" — a type Scott would reuse in subsequent novels — Waverley observes, reacts, and is acted upon, allowing the more dynamic characters (Fergus, Flora, the Baron) to drive the action while his consciousness registers its meaning. His "wavering" is not weakness but the structural condition of a man caught between two worlds.

## Historical Context

Unlike Fergus MacIvor (a fictional creation symbolising the Highland chieftain) or Prince Charles Edward (a historical figure), Waverley occupies a middle ground: he is fictional but typical — the young English gentleman of Jacobite sympathies, of whom there were many in 1745. His eventual pardon reflects the Hanoverian government's policy of leniency toward minor participants in the Rising.

## Appearances

As protagonist, Waverley appears in nearly every chapter of the novel. Key chapters include:
- ch01–ch05: Education and commissioning
- ch07–ch14: Arrival in Scotland, Tully-Veolan
- ch15–ch29: Highland journey, Glennaquoich, political entanglement
- ch30–ch48: Jacobite army, Prestonpans, military service
- ch49–ch72: Retreat, capture, pardon, marriage, resolution
