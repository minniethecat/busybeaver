# Davie Gellatley

## Biography / Character Overview

Davie Gellatley is the "innocent" — a simple-minded but loyal servant at Tully-Veolan. He belongs to a literary tradition of Shakespearean fools whose apparent simplicity conceals moments of unexpected wisdom, and Scott handles him with characteristic sympathy. Davie's relationship to the Bradwardine family is one of unquestioning devotion, and his presence at Tully-Veolan is part of the estate's feudal character — the lord's obligation to protect extends to the most vulnerable members of his household.

## Role in the Narrative

Davie serves as a touchstone of loyalty and a measure of the Bradwardine family's essential decency. His presence also allows Scott to explore the theme of innocence in a world of political calculation: Davie cannot comprehend the machinations of Fergus MacIvor or the politics of the Jacobite cause, but he understands — more clearly than many of the novel's sophisticated characters — the simple imperatives of love and loyalty.

## Appearances

Davie appears in the Tully-Veolan chapters (ch08–ch14) and the novel's resolution.

## Quotations
