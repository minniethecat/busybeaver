# Tartan

**Tartan** — the chequered woollen fabric woven in distinctive clan patterns — is the most visually striking element of Highland identity in _Waverley_. More than mere clothing, it functions as a system of heraldic identification, a symbol of martial pride, and a political marker in the Jacobite uprising of 1745. Sir Walter Scott devotes sustained descriptive attention to tartan throughout the novel, using it to distinguish characters by clan affiliation and to dramatize Edward Waverley's gradual absorption into Highland culture.

## Clan Identification

In the novel's Highland society, tartan patterns serve as visual clan signatures. Fergus MacIvor enforces this system with precision:

> At the bottom of the descent…Waverley could discover that his attendants were not of the clan of Ivor, for Fergus was particularly strict in requiring from his followers that they should wear the tartan striped in the mode peculiar to their race; a mark of distinction anciently general through the Highlands, and still maintained by those Chiefs who were proud of their lineage or jealous of their separate and exclusive authority. [037-005]

The phrase "striped in the mode peculiar to their race" indicates that each clan's tartan pattern — its _sett_, or arrangement of colours and stripes — functioned as wearable heraldry. Scott notes this as both an ancient custom and a living practice among proud chiefs in 1745.

## The "Cath Dath" — Battle Tartan

When Fergus prepares Edward Waverley for presentation to Prince Charles Edward Stuart, he summons his hereditary tailor with a specific Gaelic term:

> "Shemus, Mr. Waverley is to wear the _cath dath_ (battle colour, or tartan); his trews must be ready in four hours. You know the measure of a well-made man — two double nails to the small of the leg —" [041-007]

The Gaelic _cath dath_ (battle colour) reveals tartan's martial origin. Scott glosses it directly, identifying tartan as the war-dress of the Highland clansman. The speed demanded — "four hours" — and the formulaic measurement ("two double nails") convey both the urgency of the moment and the standardized craft knowledge of the hereditary tailor.

## The MacIvor Tartan

Fergus outfits Waverley in his own clan's tartan, symbolically adopting him into the House of Ivor:

> "Get a plaid of MacIvor tartan and sash," continued the Chieftain, "and a blue bonnet of the Prince's pattern, at Mr. Mouat's in the Crames. My short green coat, with silver lace and silver buttons, will fit him exactly, and I have never worn it. Tell Ensign Maccombich to pick out a handsome target from among mine." [041-009]

The comprehensive outfitting — plaid, sash, bonnet, coat, target (shield), dirk, purse, shoes — transforms Waverley into, as Fergus declares, "a complete son of Ivor." The tartan is the unifying element that binds these disparate items into a coherent Highland identity.

## Making and Wearing

Scott provides a vivid scene of tartan tailoring under duress. James of the Needle, the hereditary tailor of Vich Ian Vohr, works at tremendous speed while Callum Beg stands guard:

> Shemus's needle flew through the tartan like lightning; and as the artist kept chanting some dreadful skirmish of Fin Macoul, he accomplished at least three stitches to the death of every hero. [042-002]

The tailor's work-song — a Gaelic heroic ballad — merges the act of making with the martial culture the tartan represents. When Waverley dons the finished garment, Scott describes the transformation:

> His person promised firmness and agility, to which the ample folds of the tartan added an air of dignity. [042-003]

The tartan does not merely clothe but reshapes the wearer's bearing, lending "dignity" to Waverley's frame. The next morning, Waverley requires assistance to wear it correctly:

> Waverley sprung up, and, with Callum's assistance and instructions, adjusted his tartans in proper costume. [044-004]

This detail — that a Lowland gentleman cannot don Highland dress without a Highlander's help — underscores tartan's status as specialized cultural knowledge.

## Trews and Plaid

Scott distinguishes between two forms of tartan garment. The **trews** are close-fitting tartan trousers worn by chiefs and gentlemen:

> He wore the trews, or close trousers, made of tartan, chequed scarlet and white; in other particulars his dress strictly resembled Evan's. [018-058]

The **plaid** (or belted plaid, _féileadh mór_) is the voluminous wrapped garment of common clansmen, described when Waverley encounters a Highlander:

> …having for his sole garment a tartan frock, the skirts of which descended to the knee… [024-008]

The contrast between Fergus's tailored trews and the common Highlander's simple frock marks the social gradations within clan society — distinctions legible through tartan alone.

## Political Significance

Tartan carries political weight in the novel. Its proscription after the 1745 rising (the Disarming Act and [[proscription-of-highland-dress|Dress Act]]) would render the very fabric seditious. Within the novel's 1745 setting, tartan already signals Jacobite allegiance: the "Sidier Dhu" (Black Soldiers) of the Highland regiments are identified by their tartan [018-044], and the "old women, in tartan screens and red cloaks" who stream from Edinburgh before the battle [029-011] visually mark the city's Jacobite sympathies through their dress.
