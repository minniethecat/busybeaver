# Highland Dress

## Definition / Overview

Highland dress in the 18th century was a complete system of male costume comprising the *feileadh mor* (great kilt or belted plaid), the shorter *feileadh beag* (small kilt or philibeg), tartan hose, and the blue bonnet. The costume was simultaneously practical — the plaid served as clothing by day and blanket by night — and highly symbolic. Tartan patterns, though not yet rigidly codified into the "clan tartans" of Victorian invention, were associated with particular districts and families. The act of donning Highland dress was, by 1745, an implicitly political gesture: it marked the wearer as a participant in Highland culture and, by extension, as potentially sympathetic to the Jacobite cause.

## Historical Background

The Dress Act of 1746, passed in the aftermath of Culloden, made the wearing of "the Highland Garb" illegal in Scotland (with exceptions for the military). This extraordinary piece of sumptuary legislation aimed to destroy Highland culture by banning its most visible symbol. The Act remained in force until 1782, by which time the association of Highland dress with Jacobite rebellion had faded into romantic nostalgia — a process to which Scott's own writings, including *Waverley*, contributed significantly.

## Role in Waverley

Highland dress functions in *Waverley* as a marker of cultural crossing. When Waverley first arrives in the Highlands, he is an observer of the costume; by the time he joins the Jacobite army, he is wearing it. His adoption of the "garb of old Gaul" — the tartan and bonnet — is an outward sign of his inner transformation from Hanoverian officer to Jacobite partisan. The clansmen of Glennaquoich welcome him in this dress with "a loud shout," recognising a symbolic incorporation (045-002).

Scott is attentive to the social semiotics of dress: different qualities of tartan, the distinction between the great kilt and the philibeg, the military versus civilian styles, all convey information about status, allegiance, and intention.

## Related Characters

**Edward Waverley**: His adoption of Highland dress marks the climax of his romantic identification with the Jacobite cause — and his furthest point of remove from his identity as a British officer. **Fergus MacIvor**: The chieftain in full Highland regalia, whose dress is an expression of his authority and his political identity. **Evan Dhu Maccombich**: A clansman for whom Highland dress is simply daily wear — a reminder that what is symbolic for Waverley is ordinary for the Gael.

## Quotations
