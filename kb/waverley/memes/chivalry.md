## Definition and Literary Context

Chivalry in *Waverley* operates on multiple levels: as a historical institution nostalgically invoked by characters, as a literary convention that shapes the novel's narrative structures, and as a moral framework against which the characters' conduct is measured. Scott, deeply read in medieval romance and himself an editor of *Sir Tristrem*, deploys chivalric language with the precision of a scholar and the irony of a novelist who understands the distance between romance ideals and eighteenth-century realities.

The novel opens by invoking chivalry directly. Scott's narrator reflects on the naming conventions of fiction, noting that "the chivalrous epithets of Howard, Mordaunt, Mortimer, or Stanley" (001-002) would have promised readers a certain kind of romantic hero — precisely the kind, the narrator implies, that *Waverley* will not provide. This self-conscious engagement with chivalric convention establishes the novel's complex relationship with the romance tradition: it both invokes and undermines chivalric expectations.

## Sir Everard as Chivalric Archetype

Sir Everard Waverley is the novel's most sustained portrait of chivalric virtue translated into eighteenth-century domestic life. His conduct in the Lady Emily episode is explicitly framed in chivalric terms:

> With a grace and delicacy worthy the hero of a romance, Sir Everard withdrew his claim to the hand of Lady Emily. (002-016)

The comparison to "the hero of a romance" is double-edged. On one level, it praises Sir Everard's self-sacrifice as genuinely noble — an act of renunciation that any medieval knight would recognise as honourable. On another level, the framing as "romance" behaviour distances it from ordinary life, suggesting that such pure chivalry is an anachronism, a survival from an earlier age ill-suited to the political realities of Hanoverian Britain.

Sir Everard's examination of the family tree, with its "many an emblematic mark of honour and heroic achievement" (002-009), further anchors chivalry in the genealogical imagination. The Waverley coat of arms — three ermines passant with the motto *sans tache* — is itself a chivalric artifact, a material link to the knightly past that Sir Everard cherishes and Richard Waverley, the pragmatic Whig, has abandoned.

## Chivalry and Edward's Romantic Imagination

Edward Waverley's sensibility has been formed by chivalric reading. His tutor Pembroke's curriculum of "romances, and such like lighter studies" shapes Edward's expectations of the world, making him susceptible to a romanticised view of the Jacobite cause. Edward initially perceives Fergus MacIvor as a Highland chieftain straight out of romance — a perception Fergus actively cultivates. Only gradually does Edward recognise that Fergus's chivalric performance masks a calculating political ambition that has nothing chivalrous about it. The novel thus uses chivalry as a diagnostic tool, revealing how romantic ideals can both ennoble perception and enable self-deception.

## Chivalry in the Scottish Highlands

Scott's treatment of Highland culture is deeply informed by chivalric categories. The clan system appears as a kind of living chivalry — a social order organised around loyalty, honour, and martial virtue that has survived into the eighteenth century. Fergus MacIvor's banquet at Glennaquoich is staged as a chivalric entertainment, complete with bardic poetry, ceremonial drinking, and displays of clan loyalty.

Yet Scott consistently ironises this chivalric surface. The "chivalrous" Highlander is also a cattle-raider; the "romantic" clan chief is also a political conspirator. The novel suggests that chivalry, when removed from the literary page and embedded in actual social relations, becomes something far more ambiguous than romance conventions allow. The Highland chapters transform chivalry from a literary ideal into a sociological puzzle — how does a code of honour function when it is not merely read about but lived?

## Chivalry and the Novel's Moral Vision

The relationship between chivalry and honour in *Waverley* is recursive. If honour is the broader moral concept, chivalry is one of its most important historical instantiations. The novel traces the transformation of chivalric honour into modern gentlemanly conduct — a process of domestication that preserves the form of chivalry while altering its content.

This transformation is not uniformly celebrated. Scott registers a genuine nostalgia for the chivalric past, a sense that something valuable has been lost in the translation from knightly honour to modern propriety. But the novel also insists on the necessity of this loss. Chivalry, like Jacobitism, belongs to a world that history has superseded; to cling to it in its pure form is to court disaster, as Fergus's fate demonstrates.
