# The Fountain

## Definition

In Herman Melville's *Moby-Dick*, "The Fountain" (Chapter 85) examines the whale's spout — the visible exhalation that rises from the blowhole when a whale surfaces. Far from being a mere physiological detail, the spout becomes a philosophical locus: it is the only visible sign of the whale's inner life, yet its true nature (water? vapor? mist?) remains fundamentally unknowable. Melville transforms this whaling observation into a meditation on the limits of empirical knowledge and the irreducible mystery of living beings.

## Manifestations in the Novel

The fountain appears throughout the novel as both a practical navigational sign and a symbol of epistemological limits:

- **As a navigational marker**: Whalemen scan the horizon for spouts, which are often visible before the whale itself. The spout is the whale's signature in the void of the ocean.
- **As a physiological enigma**: Is the spout water expelled from the mouth, or condensed vapor from the lungs? Melville catalogs competing theories and refuses to settle the question — the spout remains a "wondrous phenomenon."
- **As a symbol of the unknowable**: The fountain mirrors the novel's central epistemological theme: like the whale hidden beneath the surface, the true nature of things eludes human scrutiny. "And so the bird of heaven, with archangelic shrieks, and his imperial beak thrust upwards, and his whole captive form folded in the flag of Ahab, went down with his ship."

## Key Passages

> "That for six thousand years — and no one knows how many millions of ages before — the great whales should have been spouting all over the sea, and sprinkling and mistifying the gardens of the deep, as with so many sprinkling or mistifying pots; and that for six thousand years, and no one knows how many millions of ages before, human beings should have been watching these fountains, and asking themselves — what is it?"
> (085-001)

> "And so, through all the thick mists of the dim doubts in my mind, divine intuitions now and then shoot, enkindling my fog with a heavenly ray. And for this I thank God; for all have doubts; many deny; but doubts or denials, few along with them, have intuitions."
> (085-009)

> "Go with your pitcher to this fountain and fill it, and bring it away; and then, after a comfortable mathematical meditation on the wonderful power of the waters, tell me, can you fill a single tumbler with the waters of the great Mississippi?"
> (085-010)

> "It is plain, then, that all these ceaseless and innumerable fountains of the whale must, at all events, be just so many vapory jets, exhaled from the lungs of the whale."
> (085-012)
