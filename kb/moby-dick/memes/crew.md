# Crew

## Definition

The crew in Moby-Dick refers both to the ship's company of the Pequod—the thirty-odd men who work the vessel—and the individual boat crews that lower for whales. Melville presents the crew as a multi-ethnic microcosm of humanity, a collective body whose will is bent to Ahab's purpose through the ritual theater of the quarter-deck, and a structural element that organizes the novel's social and dramatic architecture.

## The Pequod's Company

The Pequod's crew is assembled before the ship sails, and the process is described with deliberate mystery: Captain Ahab remains unseen, while Captains Peleg and Bildad manage the outfitting. Ishmael notes that the ship must "receive all her crew on board, ere the captain makes his appearance" （016-076）. When the crew finally boards, they "came on board in twos and threes" （021-038） at sunrise, and the voyage begins.

Melville emphasizes the crew's ethnic diversity. The harpooneers—Queequeg (South Seas), Tashtego (Gay Head Indian), and Daggoo (African)—form a "savage" vanguard, while the common sailors include "hardy peasants" from various nations （027-009）. Ishmael describes the crew as "chiefly made up of mongrel renegades, and castaways, and cannibals" （041-024）, a description that simultaneously acknowledges the crew's marginal status and Melville's democratic vision of the ship as a floating republic.

## Ahab and the Crew

The quarter-deck scene (Chapter 36) is the novel's pivotal dramatization of the relationship between captain and crew. Ahab's rhetoric—"The crew, man, the crew! Are they not one and all with Ahab?" （036-040）—transforms the crew from a collection of individuals into a single instrument of his will. He "darting his eyes among the crew" （036-010）, gauging their response, then ceremonially binds them through the shared drinking of grog: "The crew alone now drink. Round with it, round!" （036-045）.

Ishmael later reflects on his own complicity: "I, Ishmael, was one of that crew; my shouts had gone up with the rest" （041-001）. This admission establishes the crew's collective guilt—the entire ship's company is implicated in Ahab's blasphemous quest, not by coercion but by shared fervor.

## Boat Crews

The whale hunt is conducted by individual boat crews, each under the command of a mate. The principal boat crews are:

- **Starbuck's crew**: led by the first mate, whose prudence contrasts with Ahab's frenzy
- **Stubb's crew**: described at length in Chapter 48, where Stubb's "exordium to his crew" （048-013） mixes humor and exhortation
- **Flask's crew**: the third mate's boat, characterized by Flask's impetuousness
- **Fedallah's crew**: the mysterious "tiger-yellow crew" （048-037） concealed until the first lowering, consisting of Fedallah's phantom sailors

Each boat crew operates as a micro-community with its own character and relationship to its commander. Stubb "soothingly sighed" to his crew （048-012）, while Flask is "incessantly but whisperingly urging his crew" （048-016）. These variations create a taxonomy of leadership styles that contrasts with Ahab's singular, overwhelming command.

## The Crew as Collective Body

Throughout the novel, Melville treats the crew as a unified organism, subject to collective emotions. When Ahab announces the true purpose of the voyage, "the hearts of this my savage crew" （046-003） are moved as one. The crew's unity is both a source of dramatic power and a troubling political allegory—the ease with which a charismatic leader can transform independent men into instruments of a single, destructive will.
