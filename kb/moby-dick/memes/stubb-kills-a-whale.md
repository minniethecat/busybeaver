# Stubb Kills a Whale

## Overview

Stubb Kills a Whale (Chapter 61) is the first fully dramatized whale killing in *Moby-Dick*. After the dreamlike calm of the Indian Ocean lulls the entire crew into a trancelike sleep, a sperm whale suddenly surfaces close to the Pequod. The boats are lowered, and Stubb — the second mate, pipe eternally in mouth — leads the chase, harpoons the whale, and kills it with his lance.

The chapter is notable not only as action but as character revelation. Stubb's unflappable demeanor — puffing his pipe throughout the chase and kill — defines him as the novel's most easygoing officer, a man who treats mortal danger and death with the same casual indifference he brings to everything. The kill also provides Melville's first extended meditation on whaling violence, rendered in prose that moves from comic exuberance to something approaching awe at the magnitude of death.

## Chronology

### The Calm Before

The day is "exceedingly still and sultry" (061-003). The Indian Ocean offers a "vacant sea," and the entire crew — lookouts, helmsman, even Ishmael at the foremast-head — succumbs to sleep. Ishmael describes a state of dissociation: "at last my soul went out of my body; though my body still continued to sway as a pendulum will" (061-004).

### The Whale Surfaces

Ishmael is jolted awake: "close under our lee, not forty fathoms off, a gigantic Sperm Whale lay rolling in the water like the capsized hull of a frigate" (061-006). The whale, "of an Ethiopian hue, glistening in the sun's rays like a mirror," spouts tranquilly, "like a portly burgher smoking his pipe of a warm afternoon" (061-006). The simile is ironic: "that pipe, poor whale, was thy last" (061-006).

### The Chase

Ahab orders the boats cleared. The whale, alarmed, swims away with "steady tranquillity" (061-008). The boats glide silently in pursuit. Stubb, producing his match and igniting his pipe, counts on "the honor of the capture" (061-009).

> "Start her, start her, my men! Don't hurry yourselves; take plenty of time—but start her; start her like thunder-claps, that's all," cried Stubb, spluttering out the smoke as he spoke. (061-012)

The harpooneers — [[tashtego|Tashtego]], [[daggoo|Daggoo]], [[queequeg|Queequeg]] — answer with war cries. Tashtego "screamed the Gay-Header in reply, raising some old war-whoop to the skies" (061-013). Daggoo yells "Kee-hee! Kee-hee!" like "a pacing tiger in his cage" (061-014). Queequeg howls "Ka-la! Koo-loo!" as if "smacking his lips over a mouthful of Grenadier's steak" (061-015).

### The Kill

Tashtego harpoons the whale. The line runs, blistering through Stubb's hands. The boat is towed at terrifying speed — "Whole Atlantics and Pacifics seemed passed as they shot on their way" (061-018). When the whale slows, Stubb ranges alongside and "darted dart after dart into the flying fish" (061-019).

The sea turns to blood: "The red tide now poured from all sides of the monster like brooks down a hill. His tormented body rolled not in brine but in blood" (061-020). Stubb delivers the final thrust:

> When reaching far over the bow, Stubb slowly churned his long sharp lance into the fish, and kept it there, carefully churning and churning, as if cautiously seeking to feel after some gold watch that the whale might have swallowed, and which he was fearful of breaking ere he could hook it out. But that gold watch he sought was the innermost life of the fish. (061-021)

The whale enters its "flurry" — its death agony — thrashing in "impenetrable, mad, boiling spray" (061-021). At last, "gush after gush of clotted red gore, as if it had been the purple lees of red wine, shot into the frighted air" (061-022). Its heart has burst.

### The Aftermath

Daggoo announces the death: "He's dead, Mr. Stubb" (061-023). Stubb withdraws his pipe: "Yes; both pipes smoked out!" — scattering the dead ashes over the water and "for a moment, stood thoughtfully eyeing the vast corpse he had made" (061-024).

## Narrative Significance

This chapter is the novel's first extended treatment of whaling violence, and it establishes a pattern Melville will repeat: intimate, almost clinical description of the kill, followed by a moment of reflective stillness.

Stubb's character is defined through action here. His pipe — lit before the chase, puffed throughout, knocked out over the dead whale — is the perfect emblem of his philosophy. He faces death with the same casualness with which he faces everything. The joke "both pipes smoked out" conflates his tobacco and the whale's life-spout, reducing the enormity of killing to a punchline. This is Stubb's way: he lives in a universe he refuses to take seriously.

But the chapter's closing image complicates the comedy. Stubb stands "thoughtfully eyeing the vast corpse he had made" (061-024). Even the joker pauses before death. The whale is not merely a commodity but a "vast corpse" — something made, something killed, something deserving of a moment's silence.

## Aftermath / Consequences

This is the first whale killed on the Pequod's voyage. It establishes Stubb as a capable killer and introduces the rhythm of chase-kill-butcher that will structure much of the novel's middle section. The whale's body will be processed in subsequent chapters — stripped of blubber, boiled for oil — in the industrial sequence that transforms living creature into commodity.
