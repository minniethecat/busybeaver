# Darkness

## Definition

In *Moby-Dick*, darkness operates as more than the mere absence of light — it is the novel's primary register for the unknown, the pre-rational, and the elemental. Ishmael reflects that "darkness [is] indeed the proper element of our essences, though light be more congenial to our clayey part" [011-003], suggesting that human consciousness itself is grounded in darkness. The novel deploys darkness systematically: in the physical darkness of the Spouter-Inn and the night streets of New Bedford; in the racialized "darkness" of Queequeg and the "blackness of darkness" preached by Father Mapple [002-006]; and in the metaphysical darkness that the white whale embodies — a whiteness that is "the visible absence of color" and thus "the concrete of all colors" [042-008], making it the most terrifying form of darkness.

## Manifestations in the Novel

Darkness saturates the novel's opening chapters: Ishmael arrives in New Bedford on "a very dark and dismal night" [002-003] and stumbles into a "negro church" where the preacher's text concerns "the blackness of darkness, and the weeping and wailing and teeth-gnashing there" [002-006]. Kapitän Bildad calls Queequeg a "son of darkness" [016-066], inscribing racial and theological darkness onto the "savage" harpooneer. The try-works scene (Ch. 96) is the novel's most compressed meditation on darkness: the "Tartarean shapes of the pagan harpooneers" stoke fires that cast "snaky flames" and "sullen heaps" of smoke [096-003], and Ishmael warns against looking too long into the fire lest the "natural sun" of reason be extinguished and darkness become the only reality. Most radically, the chapter "The Whiteness of the Whale" (Ch. 42) inverts the darkness-light binary: whiteness, traditionally the color of purity and divinity, becomes the novel's most potent symbol of terror precisely because it contains and conceals darkness — the "heartless voids and immensities of the universe" [042-012].

## Scholarly Context

Darkness in *Moby-Dick* has been read through multiple lenses: as Melville's critique of Emersonian optimism (the "transparent eyeball" vs. the opaque dark); as an engagement with Calvinist doctrines of depravity; and as a proto-modernist acknowledgment of the limits of reason. The novel's darkness anticipates Conrad's "heart of darkness" and resonates with the Romantic tradition of the sublime — darkness as that which exceeds human comprehension and terrifies precisely through its ungraspability.
