# Surmises

## Definition

"Surmises" is the title and subject of Chapter 46, one of the novel's most psychologically acute chapters, in which Melville lays bare the rational infrastructure beneath Ahab's seemingly irrational obsession. The chapter reveals that Ahab's monomania is not simple madness but a calibrated instrument: the captain understands with "superlative sense and shrewdness" (046-002) the psychology of every man under his command, and he manipulates that psychology with the precision of a master strategist. Far from being blinded by his obsession, Ahab sees more clearly than anyone aboard — including the prudent Starbuck — the exact balance of forces that keeps the Pequod sailing toward the White Whale.

The chapter's title is significant: "Surmises" are neither certainties nor delusions, but calculated hypotheses about human behavior. The chapter is not narrated action but analytical reflection — Melville pauses the story to explain how Ahab's mind works, transforming what might have been a simple revenge plot into a study of leadership, manipulation, and the uses of rationality in the service of irrational ends.

## Manifestations in the Novel

### The Three-Part Strategic Calculation

Chapter 46 unpacks Ahab's strategy in three distinct layers, each addressing a different threat to his command.

**First, the commercial cover.** Ahab recognizes that he cannot abandon the ordinary business of whaling: "he was by nature and long habituation far too wedded to a fiery whaleman's ways, altogether to abandon the collateral prosecution of the voyage" (046-001). This is not sentiment but strategy. The crew signed on for a commercial voyage; they expect to fill oil casks. Ahab must "still in a good degree continue true to the natural, nominal purpose of the Pequod's voyage; observe all customary usages" (046-005). The whales he kills for oil are also, in his mind, a form of practice — "the more monsters he slew by so much the more he multiplied the chances that each subsequently encountered whale would prove to be the hated one he hunted" (046-001).

**Second, the management of Starbuck.** Ahab knows the chief mate's soul: "however magnetic his ascendency in some respects was over Starbuck, yet that ascendency did not cover the complete spiritual man" (046-002). Starbuck "in his soul, abhorred his captain's quest, and could he, would joyfully disintegrate himself from it, or even frustrate it" (046-002). Ahab's solution is to keep Starbuck busy with "ordinary, prudential, circumstantial influences" — the daily business of whaling that gives the first mate's conscience nothing specific to rebel against. The strategy is one of temporal dispersion: "the full terror of the voyage must be kept withdrawn into the obscure background (for few men's courage is proof against protracted meditation unrelieved by action)" (046-002). Keep the men occupied, and their courage will hold; let them brood, and mutiny becomes inevitable.

**Third, the appeal to cash.** Ahab's most cynical insight is his understanding of economic motivation. "The permanent constitutional condition of the manufactured man, thought Ahab, is sordidness" (046-003). However much the crew may be swept up in the romance of the hunt, "they must also have food for their more common, daily appetites" (046-003). Ahab explicitly compares his men to the Crusaders, who "were not content to traverse two thousand miles of land to fight for their holy sepulchre, without committing burglaries, picking pockets, and gaining other pious perquisites by the way" (046-003). Remove the prospect of cash — the lays they signed on for — and "this same quiescent cash all at once mutinying in them, this same cash would soon cashier Ahab" (046-003). The triple pun on "cash" / "quiescent" / "cashier" is one of Melville's most compressed statements about the relationship between money and power.

### The Charge of Usurpation

The chapter's deepest revelation concerns Ahab's legal vulnerability. By revealing the Pequod's "prime but private purpose" (046-004) on the quarter-deck, Ahab has "indirectly laid himself open to the unanswerable charge of usurpation" (046-004). The crew, "with perfect impunity, both moral and legal, could refuse all further obedience to him, and even violently wrest from him the command" (046-004). Ahab knows this. His entire strategy — the commercial cover, the management of Starbuck, the appeal to greed — is a defense against the lawful removal of his command. This transforms Ahab from a mere obsessive into something more complex: a man who knows the law is against him and has chosen to win by outsmarting it rather than defying it openly.

## Ahab's Rationality and Its Limits

Chapter 46 is crucial to any understanding of Ahab because it demonstrates that his madness is not delusional but strategic. Ahab sees reality — the crew's mixed motives, Starbuck's suppressed rebellion, the owners' legal rights — with perfect clarity. His monomania does not distort his perception; it gives that perception its direction. Every rational calculation in "Surmises" serves a fundamentally irrational end: the destruction of a whale that cannot be found, cannot be killed by ordinary means, and whose death will not restore Ahab's leg or his soul.

This union of supreme rationality and supreme irrationality is Ahab's defining paradox, and Chapter 46 is its fullest exposition. The chapter also explains why Starbuck, for all his moral clarity, cannot stop Ahab: Ahab has already anticipated every move Starbuck might make and has designed the voyage's routines to neutralize them in advance. The first mate is out-thought before he can act.

## Scholarly Context

"Surmises" has been a touchstone chapter for critics interested in Melville's theory of power. The chapter's Hobbesian vision of human nature — "the permanent constitutional condition of the manufactured man is sordidness" — has been compared to the political philosophy of *Leviathan*, while Ahab's manipulative intelligence has been read as Melville's commentary on leadership in democratic societies: the charismatic leader who rules not by force but by managing the desires and fears of those who could, at any moment, lawfully depose him.

The chapter's title has also drawn attention. "Surmises" names a mode of thought — conjectural, probabilistic, strategic — that stands in contrast to the prophetic and oracular modes that dominate the novel's rhetoric elsewhere. Ahab in this chapter is not the inspired madman of the quarter-deck but the calculating CEO of a perilous enterprise, performing cost-benefit analyses on human motivation. Melville's decision to devote an entire chapter to this analytical register suggests the importance he placed on establishing Ahab as more than a Gothic villain: a figure whose evil is inseparable from his intelligence.
