# The Dart

## Definition

The dart is the critical moment in the whale hunt when the harpooneer throws his iron into the whale. In Chapter 62 ("The Dart"), Ishmael describes the physical demands of this act and mounts a reformist argument against the "invariable usage of the fishery" that requires the harpooneer to exhaust himself rowing before making the throw. The dart becomes a meditation on efficiency, tradition, and the human cost of whaling's institutional inertia.

## Manifestations in the Novel

Chapter 62 follows directly from Ishmael's grisly account of Stubb's whale kill in Chapter 61. Ishmael uses the transition to critique the standard division of labor in the whale-boat. According to custom, "the whale-boat pushes off from the ship, with the headsman or whale-killer as temporary steersman, and the harpooneer or whale-fastener pulling the foremost oar" (062-002). The harpooneer must row strenuously throughout the chase, and only at the final moment — when he hears the cry "Stand up, and give it to him!" — must he "drop and secure his oar, turn round on his centre half way, seize his harpoon from the crotch, and with what little strength may remain... essay to pitch it somehow into the whale" (062-002).

Ishmael's argument is blunt and practical: the system is self-defeating. "No wonder, taking the whole fleet of whalemen in a body, that out of fifty fair chances for a dart, not five are successful; no wonder that so many hapless harpooneers are madly cursed and disrated; no wonder that some of them actually burst their blood-vessels in the boat" (062-002). The exhaustion of the harpooneer, not the speed of the whale, is the primary cause of failed hunts.

Ishmael proposes a reform: "The headsman should stay in the bows from first to last; he should both dart the harpoon and the lance, and no rowing whatever should be expected of him" (062-004). He appeals to "long experience in various whalemen of more than one nation" to support his case, insisting the slight loss of chase speed would be more than compensated by the harpooneer's preserved strength. The chapter closes with a maxim that extends beyond whaling: "To insure the greatest efficiency in the dart, the harpooneers of this world must start to their feet from out of idleness, and not from out of toil" (062-005).

## Scholarly Context

The Dart chapter exemplifies Melville's technique of using technical whaling discourse as a vehicle for broader philosophical arguments. Ishmael's critique of institutional inertia — the "invariable usage" that persists despite obvious inefficiency — has been read as a democratic argument against unexamined tradition. The closing maxim about starting "from out of idleness, and not from out of toil" has been compared to Ahab's monomaniacal industry and Queequeg's calm competence, suggesting that effective action requires repose, not frenzy.
