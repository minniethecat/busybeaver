# Oil

## Definition

Oil in *Moby-Dick* is both the literal substance for which whales are hunted and the novel's most versatile symbol of transformation. Whale oil fueled the lamps of the nineteenth-century world, and Melville insists on this material fact throughout: the "incredible quantity of oil" extracted from a single whale (P02-016), New Bedford's identity as "a land of oil" (006-005), the try-works that render whale blubber into marketable fluid. But oil is also the novel's figure for the metaphysical — the "oily calmness" that spreads across troubled water (009-035), the oil that cannot be poured upon waters when "God has brewed them into a gale" (009-040). Melville moves constantly between these registers: oil is money, oil is light, oil is calm, oil is the substance that holds the world's contradictions in suspension.

## Manifestations in the Novel

**The extracts: oil as global commodity (Etymology and Extracts)**. Before the novel proper begins, Melville's extracts establish whale oil as a global economic substance. The extracts quote Fuller: "a sea of oil swimming in them" (P02-043), and Uno Von Troil on the "vast quantity of oil" (P02-053) a single whale yields. The cumulative effect is to make oil the novel's economic ground bass — the reason the whaling industry exists at all, and therefore the reason the Pequod's voyage exists at all. Before there is a story, there is oil.

**New Bedford: land of oil (Chapter 6)**. Ishmael's description of New Bedford as "a land of oil" (006-005) is one of his most economical sentences. The phrase captures the town's prosperity and its strangeness: a "land" of the substance extracted at sea, a terrestrial economy built on a marine resource. Ishmael's famous description of the try-pots on the street corners — "they have reservoirs of oil in every house" (006-007) — makes oil the literal atmosphere of the town, the substance its citizens breathe and wear.

**Oily calmness (Chapter 9)**. Father Mapple's sermon produces an image of oil as metaphysical agent: "when instantly an oily calmness floats out from the east, and the sea is still" (009-035). Oil here is not a commodity but a principle of pacification — the substance that quiets the storm. But Melville immediately complicates this: "Woe to him who seeks to pour oil upon the waters when God has brewed them into a gale" (009-040). Oil can calm natural storms but not divine ones; there is a limit to what oil can do, and that limit is God.

**The try-works: oil as hellfire (Chapter 96)**. The try-works chapter is the novel's most intense meditation on oil as transformation. Whale blubber is rendered into oil in "the left wing of the day of judgment" (096-005), and the process turns the Pequod into a mobile factory — and a floating hell. The oil that emerges is both product and byproduct: the commercial goal that justifies the voyage and the hellish residue that condemns it. Ishmael's famous reversal at the tiller — mistaking the try-works' fire for the sun — makes oil the substance of perceptual crisis: when everything is lit by burning oil, nothing can be seen clearly.

**Ambergris: oil's fragrant double (Chapter 92)**. Melville distinguishes between different kinds of whale product: ambergris, the precious substance found in sick whales, is "the universal problem to all mankind" (092-002). Unlike the oil rendered from healthy blubber, ambergris is a product of disease — valuable precisely because the whale was unwell. The contrast between oil (industrial, bulk, rendered) and ambergris (rare, fragrant, found) suggests that value in Melville's world is always paradoxical.

**Stowing down: oil as completion (Chapter 98)**. The final stage of whale processing is "stowing down" — decanting the rendered oil into casks and cleaning the ship. Ishmael describes the process in terms that make it almost sacramental: the oil is the voyage's achieved substance, and stowing it down below decks is an act of closure. But the oil below decks is also a bomb waiting to go off — a store of flammable wealth that makes the ship, in its final catastrophe, a funeral pyre as much as a vessel.

## Key Passages

- (P02-016) — "What an incredible quantity of oil will be extracted out of one whale": the extract that establishes oil as economic marvel.
- (P02-043) — "A sea of oil swimming in them": Fuller's image of whales as mobile oil reservoirs.
- (001-007) — Ishmael's opening commitment to "all honorable respectable toils, trials, and tribulations": "toils" and "trials" both pun on oil-rendering labor.
- (006-005) — "It is a land of oil": New Bedford's identity compressed into six words.
- (009-035) — "An oily calmness floats out from the east": oil as metaphysical pacification.
- (009-040) — "Woe to him who seeks to pour oil upon the waters when God has brewed them into a gale": the limit of oil's power.
- (014-002) — Nantucketers using "a spile to stop a leak in an oil cask": oil as the substance of everyday life.
- (096-005) — "The left wing of the day of judgment; it is an argument for the pit": the try-works as hell, oil as its fuel.

## Scholarly Context

Oil in *Moby-Dick* has been central to materialist and eco-critical readings of the novel. Historians of the whaling industry note that whale oil was the petroleum of the nineteenth century — the primary illuminant and industrial lubricant — and that Melville's detailed attention to its extraction is not ornament but documentary precision. Eco-critical scholars read the try-works chapter as an early instance of petro-criticism: Melville's recognition that industrial energy extraction is a form of violence that transforms both the extracted substance and the extractors. The "oily calmness" passage has been read in theological terms as Melville's revision of the biblical "balm in Gilead" — the oil that cannot heal what God has wounded.
