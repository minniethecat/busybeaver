# Despair

## Definition

In *Moby-Dick*, despair is not merely a character's passing mood but the novel's fundamental emotional tonality — the ground bass beneath every ambition, every oath, and every philosophical speculation. It appears first in the marble tablets of the Whaleman's Chapel, where Ishmael contemplates the "immovable inscriptions" that cover no ashes: "What despair in those immovable inscriptions!" [007-007]. Despair in Melville's hands is the natural human response to a universe that offers no guarantees, no mercy, and no intelligible purpose. It is at once a theological condition — the soul's abandonment — and a practical one: men in open boats, "almost despairing of reaching any hospitable shore" [045-013], face the literal possibility of extinction. Ahab's despair, however, is of a different order: a "fatalistic despair" [118-004] that has hardened into defiance, the despair of a man who has stared into the void and decided to strike through its mask.

## Manifestations in the Novel

Despair manifests across the full arc of the narrative, from Ishmael's early confession — "Oh, I was plunging to despair" [009-012] — to the Mate's final recognition at the novel's close, when Starbuck's face is "bleached to a corpse's hue with despair" [132-018]. In the Whaleman's Chapel (Ch.7), despair is etched in stone, the communal grief of a seafaring community. In Father Mapple's sermon (Ch.9), Jonah's despair in the whale's belly becomes the archetype of the soul cut off from God. Ahab's despair — "the mad secret of his unabated rage" — is the engine of the plot, a despair that "seemed working in him" [064-003] like a slow poison. The Town-Ho's story (Ch.54) offers a collective despair: Steelkilt's mutiny is born from the "bowels of despair" [054-054], the desperation of men pushed beyond endurance. In the chapter "The Candles" (Ch.119), Ahab speaks of "the speechless, placeless power; but to the last gasp of my earthquake life will dispute its unconditional, unintegral mastery in me" — despair turned to rebellion. And in Pip, the cabin boy driven mad by abandonment at sea, despair takes its most poetic form: the community of the lost.

## Scholarly Context

Critics have long recognized despair as the novel's presiding emotion, linking it to Melville's reading of Shakespeare's tragedies — particularly *King Lear* and *Macbeth* — and to the philosophical pessimism of Schopenhauer. Ahab's despair has been read as the American Calvinist inheritance: a predestined soul raging against its election. More recent scholarship connects the novel's despair to the trauma of the whale hunt itself — the industrial-scale killing that produced the oil lighting nineteenth-century America — suggesting that the despair in *Moby-Dick* is not abstract but grounded in the material violence of the whaling industry.
