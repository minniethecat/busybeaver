# Samuel Enderby

## Overview

The Samuel Enderby is an English whaler out of London, captained by [[captain-boomer|Captain Boomer]]. Her encounter with the [[pequod|Pequod]] is the most philosophical of all the gams.

"Samuel Enderby is the name of my ship," Captain Boomer tells Ahab. She was named after the late Samuel Enderby, a London merchant and the "original of the famous whaling house of Enderby and Sons" (101-001).

The Enderby is "a jolly ship; of good fare and plenty" (101-005). Her crew, well-fed and good-humored, offers a living contrast to the Pequod's grim intensity.

## The Ship and Her Crew

The Samuel Enderby is fitted out for comfort. Melville describes her as having "a generous heart" — a ship where men eat well and drink well, where the captain's table has "roast fowl" rather than hard tack.

Captain Boomer is a practical Englishman who has lost his right arm to [[moby-dick|Moby Dick]]. Unlike Ahab, Boomer regards his injury with good-humored resignation. He does not curse the whale. He is simply glad to be alive.

Dr. Bunger, the Enderby's surgeon, attends to Boomer's wound with rum and wit. The camaraderie between captain and surgeon is a mirror image of Ahab's isolation.

## Encounter with the Pequod

Ahab, hearing that the Enderby has news of the White Whale, rows eagerly to her deck. He finds Boomer convalescing, his stump wrapped in a splint.

Boomer tells his story: the whale struck his boat, took his arm, and disappeared. "Aye, aye, I know that well enough," Ahab presses, wanting news of the whale's whereabouts. But Boomer has no interest in pursuing Moby Dick further. "He's welcome to the arm he has," the English captain says (100-022).

Ahab, disgusted by Boomer's lack of vengeful fire, "quitted the Samuel Enderby of London" abruptly (106-001). He does not even stay for the meal offered to him.

## Key Passages

- (100-022) — "Samuel Enderby is the name of my ship" — Captain Boomer introduces his vessel
- (101-001) — The ship's namesake: the late Samuel Enderby, merchant and whaling pioneer
- (101-005) — "The Samuel Enderby was a jolly ship; of good fare and plenty"
- (101-006) — Melville reflects on why the Enderby and other English whalers have better provisions
- (106-001) — Ahab quits the Enderby, unsatisfied with Boomer's lack of vengeance

## Fate

The Samuel Enderby returns safely to London. Captain Boomer lives with one arm and no obsession — a counter-example to Ahab's self-destructive quest.

Melville uses the Enderby to show that encountering Moby Dick does not have to end in doom. One can lose a limb and retain one's soul.

## Symbolic Significance

The Samuel Enderby represents the sane response to disaster: acceptance. Boomer is what Ahab could have been — and what he refuses to be.

The contrast between the two captains is stark. Boomer's cheerfulness and practicality highlight Ahab's monomania. The Enderby is "jolly" not because nothing bad has happened to her crew, but because they have chosen to survive rather than to destroy.

Her Englishness is also significant. Melville uses the English whaling tradition — older, more established than the American — as a foil for the Pequod's reckless intensity.
