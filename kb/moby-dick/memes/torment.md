# Torment

## Definition

Torment in *Moby-Dick* is not incidental suffering but the novel's baseline condition of conscious existence. From Ishmael's opening confession — "I am tormented with an everlasting itch for things remote" (001-014) — to the dying whale's "tormented flaming life" (118-007), Melville constructs torment as the universal register of mortal being. It afflicts men and whales alike, operates in both body and spirit, and serves a double function: torment is the price of consciousness, yet also the spur that drives characters toward the metaphysical questions the novel refuses to answer. Ahab is tormented by his wound; Ishmael by his restlessness; the whale by the harpoon; the sea itself by "guilty beings" who "transform themselves into the same" (051-009). Torment is everywhere, and everywhere it is the same — the creaturely response to limits it cannot accept.

## Manifestations in the Novel

**Ishmael's existential itch (Chapter 1)**. The novel's first statement about torment is also its most philosophical. Ishmael is "tormented with an everlasting itch for things remote" (001-014), a restlessness that precedes any specific grievance. This is torment as metaphysical disposition: not pain caused by something, but a constitutional inability to be satisfied with what is present. The torment drives him to sea — but as the novel unfolds, the sea proves to be torment's native element, not its cure.

**Ahab's waking torment (Chapters 41, 44, 123)**. Ahab's torment is the novel's most concentrated. "All that most maddens and torments; all that stirs up the lees of things; all truth with malice in it" (041-019) — this catalog of torment's sources spans the cosmic to the personal. His sleeplessness is a form of torment: "what trances of torments does that man endure who is consumed with one unachieved revengeful desire" (044-023). Even in sleep, torment finds him: when Starbuck's voice penetrates his cabin, it is described as "startling from out the old man's tormented sleep" (123-011). For Ahab, torment is continuous — it has no off switch, no respite, no harbor.

**The whale's tormented body (Chapters 61, 81, 118)**. Melville extends torment downward through the great chain of being to include the whale itself. The dying whale's body rolls "not in brine but blood" (061-020), its spout issues as "a continual tormented jet" (081-025), and the pain of the harpoon "often torments the Leviathan into soon rising" (081-029) — torment as physiological causation. In the try-works chapter, Ishmael sees "the tormented flaming life" of the whale rendered into oil (118-007). The whale suffers; its suffering is real; and the novel refuses to treat it as less significant than human torment.

**The tormented sea and cosmic torment (Chapters 51, 52, 86)**. At its widest aperture, torment becomes a property of the cosmos itself. The Pequod sails "launched into this tormented sea, where guilty beings transform themselves" (051-009) — torment as medium, not just experience. The chase of Moby Dick is "tormented chase of that demon phantom" (052-008). Even in simile, the whale's agony invokes "Satan thrusting forth his tormented colossal claw from the flame Baltic of Hell" (086-012). Torment scales from the itch in Ishmael's soul to the architecture of the infernal.

**The tormented surgeon (Chapter 91)**. A minor but vivid instance: the surgeon aboard the *Rose-Bud* is described as "the tormented surgeon, who, after in vain trying to ascertain the cause" (091-028) of the whale's ambergris odor. Here torment is comic — the pompous professional undone by what he cannot explain — but the comedy draws on the same structure: consciousness tormented by its own limits.

**Jonah's bed and the tormented deep (Chapter 9)**. Father Mapple's sermon furnishes torment with biblical precedent. Jonah lies in his berth, "his tormented eyes roll round the place" (009-030), and he looks "downward again towards the tormented deep" (009-032). The deep is tormented — not placid, not indifferent, but actively afflicted. This is torment as theological condition: the sinner's state before repentance, and the medium through which God works.

## Key Passages

- (001-014) — "I am tormented with an everlasting itch for things remote": Ishmael's self-diagnosis, torment as constitutional restlessness
- (009-030) — "his tormented eyes roll round the place": Jonah in the whale's belly, torment as theological encounter
- (009-032) — "downward again towards the tormented deep": the deep itself tormented, cosmic torment beyond the human
- (017-025) — "when it is a positive torment to him": the body's protest against spiritual discipline, torment as creaturely limit
- (041-019) — "All that most maddens and torments; all that stirs up the lees of things": Ahab's comprehensive catalog of torment's sources
- (044-023) — "what trances of torments does that man endure who is consumed with one unachieved revengeful desire": torment as monomania's psychological cost
- (044-024) — "the tormented spirit that glared out of bodily eyes": inward torment made outwardly visible
- (051-009) — "ourselves launched into this tormented sea, where guilty beings transform themselves": torment as the cosmic medium
- (052-008) — "in tormented chase of that demon phantom": the hunt as torment externalized into action
- (061-020) — "His tormented body rolled not in brine but blood": the whale's death-agony, torment as physiological fact
- (081-025) — "a continual tormented jet; while his one poor fin beat his side": the wounded whale's visible suffering
- (086-012) — "Satan thrusting forth his tormented colossal claw from the flame Baltic of Hell": torment in the novel's infernal register
- (091-028) — "This was the tormented surgeon": comic torment, but the same structure — consciousness thwarted by limits
- (118-007) — "full of its tormented flaming life": the whale rendered into fire and oil, torment as transformation
- (123-011) — "startling from out the old man's tormented sleep": Ahab's rest invaded by the torment that rules his waking hours
- (130-001) — "where his tormenting wound had been inflicted; the accurate latitude and longitude": the wound as the fixed point around which all torment orbits

## Scholarly Context

Melville's treatment of torment has been read through multiple critical lenses. Psychoanalytic critics see Ahab's torment as the return of the repressed — the whale as the object onto which his psychic pain is projected. Theological readings connect torment to the Calvinist doctrine of total depravity and the problem of theodicy: if God is sovereign, why does he permit — or orchestrate — such torment? The whale's torment has drawn attention from animal studies scholars who note Melville's unusual willingness to grant the animal full subjectivity in its suffering. Most broadly, critics identify torment as Melville's revision of the Romantic sublime: where Wordsworth and Coleridge found transcendence in nature's vastness, Melville finds torment — a nature that inflicts pain without explanation.
