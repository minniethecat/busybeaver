# Try-Works

## Definition

The try-works is the brick furnace installed amidships on a whaleship, used to render strips of whale blubber into oil. In *Moby-Dick*, the Pequod's try-works far exceeds its practical function to become one of the novel's most potent symbols. Melville describes it as the feature by which the Pequod is "outwardly distinguished" (096-001): a fiery industrial apparatus that transforms the ship, by night, into a vision of hell. The try-works operates at the literal and symbolic center of the novel, converting the whale's body into commercial value while simultaneously serving as "the material counterpart of her monomaniac commander's soul" (096-007).

## Manifestations in the Novel

The try-works first appears in practical terms during the description of cutting-in a whale (094-002), but its symbolic power is fully unleashed in Chapter 96 ("The Try-Works"), one of the most concentrated passages of imagery in the novel. Ishmael describes his first night watch at the helm while the try-works blaze behind him: the darkness, the fire, the boiling oil, and the "red heat" of the furnace combine to create a disorienting, infernal atmosphere.

Melville carefully constructs the scene as a descent into moral and perceptual confusion. The "savage harpooneers" tending the fires, the "smoke from the try-works besoot[ing] all the bulwarks" (098-004), and the "equatorial try-works" under "the equatorial sun" (098-007) collapse the distinction between day and night, civilized and savage, human and demonic. The try-works scene explicitly invokes the imagery of hell: "the ship, freighted with savages, and laden with fire, and burning a corpse, and plunging into that blackness of darkness, seemed the material counterpart of her monomaniac commander's soul" (096-007).

Ishmael's role as helmsman during the try-works scene is crucial. His back is turned to the fire, facing the darkness ahead, which Melville uses as a parable about the dangers of turning one's back on moral illumination. The try-works thus operates on multiple levels: as realistic whaling technology, as a symbol of Ahab's inner hell, and as a philosophical warning about the seduction of darkness.

Stubb's soliloquy by the try-works in Chapter 99 (099-009) — in which he contemplates the doubloon nailed to the mast — brings the symbolic resonance full circle. The try-works' fire illuminates the gold coin, just as the hellish energy of Ahab's obsession illuminates (and distorts) the meaning of the entire voyage.

## Scholarly Context

The try-works chapter is widely recognized as one of the most richly symbolic passages in American literature. Critics have read it as Melville's response to industrialization (the ship as factory), as a Dantean descent narrative (the *Inferno* is explicitly referenced in the chapter's epigraph), and as a political allegory of American expansion driven by hellish energy. The try-works' conversion of whale flesh into oil mirrors the transformation of nature into capital, while the moral warning Ishmael issues — "look not too long in the face of the fire" — has been read as Melville's caution against the seductions of Ahab's monomania, both for the individual and for the nation.

The try-works also participates in Melville's broader pattern of shipboard spaces as psychological landscapes. Just as the mast-head represents the dangers of abstraction (Chapter 35), the forecastle represents community, and the cabin represents hierarchy, the try-works represents the infernal furnace at the heart of the Pequod's — and Ahab's — purpose.
