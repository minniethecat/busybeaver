# Conscience

## Definition

Conscience in *Moby-Dick* is the internal court before which every character must stand — and from which none can fully escape. Melville presents conscience as a faculty distinct from external law, social convention, or religious doctrine: it is the voice that speaks from within, often unwelcome, and its demands cannot be negotiated. Father Mapple's Jonah, writhing in the hold of his own guilt, cries out "so my conscience hangs in me!" (009-030), and the sermon makes clear that conscience is the mechanism by which divine justice is internalized. Yet Melville is too honest to let conscience remain purely theological: Stubb dismissively calls it "a kind of Tic-Dolly-row" (029-010), reducing moral self-judgment to a nervous tic. The novel's deepest claim about conscience is cosmic: "the great mundane soul were in anguish and remorse for the long sin of the whale" (051-008), as if the ocean itself were a conscience, its tides the rhythm of moral reckoning.

## Manifestations in the Novel

**Jonah's guilty conscience (Chapter 9)**. Father Mapple's sermon is the novel's most sustained analysis of conscience. Jonah, fleeing God's command to preach to Nineveh, finds that "his conscience hangs in me!" and "conscience yet pricking him, as the plungings of the Roman race-horse" (009-031). The image is violent and physical: conscience is a goad, a spur, something that pierces. Mapple describes Jonah's soul as "all awry" (009-030) — conscience is not a calm moral compass but a destabilizing force that throws the self off balance. The sailor-preacher's own body, climbing the rope ladder to his pulpit "as if ascending the rigging of a ship" (009-001), enacts the physical strain of carrying conscience's weight.

**Ishmael and Queequeg's peaceful conscience (Chapter 10)**. After the terrors of the sermon, Ishmael and Queequeg retire to bed "at peace with our own consciences and all the world" (010-009). This is the novel's counter-image: conscience at rest, the possibility of moral tranquility achieved through human fellowship. The scene is deliberately placed after Mapple's thunderous sermon to suggest that conscience's demands, however severe, can be temporarily satisfied — not through theological obedience but through mutual trust between two formerly estranged souls.

**Bildad's puritanical conscience (Chapter 16)**. The Quaker owners Peleg and Bildad represent conscience institutionalized. Bildad declares "I would afore now had a conscience to lug about that would be heavy enough to founder the largest ship" (016-064), and Peleg mocks him: "thy conscience may be drawing ten inches of water" (016-065). The nautical metaphors turn conscience into cargo — a weight to be carried, a leak to be managed. Bildad's conscience is performative piety, a display of moral rigor that coexists comfortably with the profit motive. In his parsimonious negotiation over Ishmael's lay, conscience and commerce are revealed as not antagonists but partners.

**Stubb's reduction of conscience (Chapter 29)**. Stubb, the second mate, offers the novel's most deflationary account of conscience: "what some folks ashore call a conscience; it's a kind of Tic-Dolly-row" (029-010). By likening conscience to an involuntary muscular spasm, Stubb denies it any moral content — it is mere physiology, a twitch to be ignored. His dismissal is strategic: Stubb has just been humiliated by Ahab, and to acknowledge conscience would be to acknowledge that he should have protested Ahab's monomania. The "Tic-Dolly-row" is a defense mechanism, a refusal to let moral self-judgment interfere with survival.

**The ocean's conscience (Chapter 51)**. In "The Spirit-Spout," Melville extends conscience beyond the human: the sea itself seems endowed with moral sentience, "as if its vast tides were a conscience; and the great mundane soul were in anguish and remorse" (051-008). This is the novel's most audacious move — conscience is not a private faculty but a cosmic property. The whale, pursued across all oceans, becomes the object of a planetary guilt. The Pequod sails on a sea of conscience, unable to escape the moral weight of its mission.

**Ahab against his owners' conscience (Chapter 109)**. When Starbuck informs Ahab that the leaking oil casks must be attended to, Ahab responds: "the owners were my conscience" (109-012). He means that only the owners' profit motive — not his own moral sense — could constrain him. Conscience is externalized, delegated to an absent authority that cannot enforce its claims. Ahab's confession here is devastating: he has severed the connection between action and self-judgment, and in doing so has freed himself to pursue destruction without limit.

## Key Passages

- (009-030) — Jonah cries "so my conscience hangs in me!" (009-030): conscience as physical burden, the soul made crooked by guilt.
- (009-031) — "Conscience yet pricking him, as the plungings of the Roman race-horse": conscience as goad, painful and inescapable.
- (010-009) — Ishmael and Queequeg "at peace with our own consciences and all the world": conscience at rest through human fellowship, the sermon's counter-image.
- (016-064) — Bildad's conscience "heavy enough to founder the largest ship": conscience as performative cargo, piety displayed.
- (016-065) — Peleg to Bildad: "thy conscience may be drawing ten inches of water": conscience as leak, the gap between professed and actual.
- (029-010) — Stubb: conscience is "a kind of Tic-Dolly-row": the reduction of moral sense to involuntary twitch.
- (049-008) — "Like a quiet ghost with a clean conscience sitting inside the bars of a snug family vault": Ishmael's ironic image of death as cozy, conscience cleansed.
- (051-008) — "As if its vast tides were a conscience; and the great mundane soul were in anguish": conscience cosmicized, the sea as moral register.
- (109-012) — Ahab: "the owners were my conscience": conscience externalized and abandoned, the final severance.

## Scholarly Context

Melville's treatment of conscience reflects his deep engagement with Calvinist theology and its secular afterlives. The Jonah sermon draws on a tradition stretching from Augustine to Jonathan Edwards, in which conscience is the "spark" of divine presence in the fallen soul. Stubb's dismissal of conscience as "Tic-Dolly-row" has been read as Melville's anticipation of Nietzschean and Freudian critiques of conscience as internalized social control. More recently, affect theorists have focused on the physical vocabulary Melville uses for conscience — hanging, pricking, plunging — arguing that he treats moral experience as irreducibly embodied. The image of the ocean as conscience in Chapter 51 has drawn eco-critical attention as an early literary instance of planetary ethics, in which the non-human world is not merely a stage for human moral drama but an active participant.
