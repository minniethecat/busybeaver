# Home

## Definition

Home in *Moby-Dick* is less a place than an absence, a gravitational center defined by its distance from the narrative's events. The novel's characters are almost all away from home — whalemen on a three-year voyage, exiles from domesticity, wanderers whose relation to home is one of departure, loss, or impossibility. Ishmael's opening confession establishes homelessness as the novel's psychic baseline: he goes to sea not because he has a home to leave but because the shore has become uninhabitable. The Pequod sails "from home" (028-006), and the phrase carries the weight of all that is left behind and all that will not be returned to. Queequeg, the harpooneer from Kokovoko, is the novel's most vivid figure of permanent exile: "though now some time from home" (012-005), he will never return. Home is what is lost, what is remembered, and what the novel's metaphysical architecture cannot accommodate — for in Melville's cosmos, "homeward" is not a direction the Pequod ever truly sails.

## Manifestations in the Novel

**The Spouter-Inn as threshold (Chapters 3, 10)**. The Spouter-Inn is the novel's first space of provisional domesticity — a temporary home that is not a home. Ishmael mistakes Queequeg for an intruder and demands "the harpooneer's not coming home at all that night" (003-053) — his first use of "home" is a negation, an expectation of absence. But Queequeg does return, and the Inn becomes, for one night, a kind of home: "make himself at home" (012-004), Ishmael says of Queequeg's ease, though the savage is "now some time from home" (012-005). The doubling of "home" in these two adjacent sentences — one an invitation, the other an elegy — captures the novel's dual treatment of the concept: home is what we make for each other, and home is what we have lost.

**Nantucket as home-port (Chapters 14, 16)**. Nantucket is described as a place to which men return: "There is his home; there lies his business" (014-005). The island's identity is inseparable from the idea of return — the whale-man's home is not where he lives but where his business is, where his family waits, where the oil is landed. But Melville is scrupulous about the brevity of these returns: "the shore intervals at home so exceedingly brief" (016-076) that whalemen scarcely know their own houses. Home is a punctuation mark between voyages, not a life. Captain Bildad's home is the Try Pots Inn, where his sister "Mrs. Hussey being entirely competent to managing the inn" (015-006) in Bildad's absence: home, for the whaling capitalist, is an investment managed by proxy.

**Queequeg's lost homeland (Chapter 12)**. Queequeg tells Ishmael he is "now some time from home" (012-005), and the phrase is an understatement — he is a prince in exile, a harpooneer on foreign ships, severed from Kokovoko by an ocean he cannot cross. His coffin, built when he seems to be dying, is the novel's most concentrated image of permanent homelessness: the tomb is the only home Queequeg will build, and it becomes Ishmael's life-raft — a home that floats, a home that saves, but a home built for a dead man.

**Homeward winds (Chapter 23)**. The chapter "The Lee Shore" presents the novel's most compressed meditation on home and its impossibility. Ishmael speaks of the "winds that fain would blow her homeward" (023-002) — winds that offer safe passage to shore. But the lee shore is death, the place where ships are wrecked by the very winds that promise safety. To sail homeward is to sail toward destruction; the only safety is in the open sea, away from home. Melville's paradox is devastating: home is what draws us, and home is what kills us. The true sailor learns to resist the homeward impulse, to stay at sea, to accept wandering as the only viable condition.

**Ahab's departed home (Chapter 28)**. Ahab's first appearance is marked by absence: "when the ship had sailed from home, nothing but the dead wintry bleakness of the sea had been seen in him" (028-006). Home is what Ahab has left, and leaving it has been a kind of death: "the dead wintry bleakness" replaces whatever warmth home once provided. Ahab's leg was lost at sea, not at home, but the loss has retroactively hollowed out home — he returns to Nantucket not to recover but to plan the next voyage, and the next, until Nantucket becomes merely the place where the ship is provisioned.

**Home as oceanic grave (P02)**. In the Extracts section, Melville includes a fragment: "In his ocean home will be" (P02-155). The ocean as home — this is the novel's final inversion. The whale's home; the drowned sailor's home; Ishmael's home, for he is saved on the ocean, not from it. Home is not the shore but the sea that receives the dead, and the coffin that floats is the truest house the novel offers.

## Key Passages

- (001-001) — "whenever it is a damp, drizzly November in my soul": the novel opens not at home but in the psychic condition that makes home impossible
- (003-053) — "the harpooneer's not coming home at all that night": home introduced as negation, as expected absence
- (010-004) — "some twenty thousand miles from home, by the way of Cape Horn": the quantitative measure of exile
- (012-004) — "make himself at home. But this fine young savage — this": home as hospitality, the gift strangers give each other
- (012-005) — "now some time from home": Queequeg's permanent exile, the understatement of irreversible departure
- (014-005) — "There is his home; there lies his business": Nantucket as home-port, the place of economic return
- (016-045) — "upon arriving home, were mostly all carried ashore": homecoming as collapse, the whalemen unable to walk on land
- (016-076) — "the shore intervals at home so exceedingly brief": home reduced to a punctuation between voyages
- (023-002) — "winds that fain would blow her homeward; seeks all the lashed sea's": the lee shore's fatal invitation
- (028-006) — "when the ship had sailed from home, nothing but the dead wintry bleakness": Ahab's post-departure vacancy
- (P02-155) — "In his ocean home will be": the sea as the only home the novel finally acknowledges

## Scholarly Context

Home in *Moby-Dick* has been analyzed through several critical frames. Feminist readings emphasize the novel's systematic exclusion of domesticity: there are almost no women in *Moby-Dick*, and "home" is the feminized space the male whalemen flee — and to which they cannot return. Queer-theoretical approaches read Ishmael and Queequeg's "marriage" at the Spouter-Inn as the creation of an alternative domesticity, a home made between men that substitutes for the heterosexual home the novel refuses. Postcolonial criticism attends to Queequeg's permanent exile from Kokovoko, reading his homelessness as the novel's figure for indigenous displacement — the harpooneer who can never go home because colonialism has rendered "home" a memory rather than a place. Ecocritical approaches read the "ocean home" of the whale as Melville's intuition of a non-human dwelling, a mode of inhabiting the world that has no need of shore.
