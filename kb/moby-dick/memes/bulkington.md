# Bulkington

## Overview

Bulkington is one of the most enigmatic figures in *Moby-Dick*—a character who appears only twice, yet receives from Ishmael one of the novel's most impassioned philosophical eulogies. First encountered at the Spouter-Inn in [[new-bedford|New Bedford]], he stands "somewhat aloof" from his rowdy shipmates, a "tall, newlanded mariner" whose physical presence immediately commands Ishmael's attention (003-022, 023-001). When the [[the-pequod|Pequod]] departs on a "shivering winter's night," Ishmael is astonished to find Bulkington at the helm—a man who had just completed a four-year voyage, yet could not bear a single night ashore before shipping out again (023-002). The entire [[chapter-023|Ch. 23: The Lee Shore]] is devoted to him, though Melville famously calls it a "six-inch chapter" and a "stoneless grave" (023-002). In that compressed space, Bulkington becomes the novel's symbol of a soul that can find no rest on land—a "demigod" whose apotheosis lies not in safety but in the "howling infinite" of the open sea (023-004).

## Role in the Narrative

Bulkington's narrative function is brief but symbolically immense. He appears in only two chapters—the [[chapter-003|Spouter-Inn]] and [[chapter-023|The Lee Shore]]—and vanishes from the story thereafter. His fleeting presence serves as a philosophical keystone: Bulkington embodies the existential predicament that Ishmael names as the "mortally intolerable truth"—that all deep thinking is the soul's struggle to maintain its "open independence" against the "treacherous, slavish shore" of ease and conformity (023-003). Structurally, Bulkington frames the Pequod's departure. The "Lee Shore" passage transforms him from a minor character into a figure for the human condition: the soul that cannot come to land, for "in landlessness alone resides highest truth" (023-004).

## Key Appearances

- [[chapter-003|Ch. 3: The Spouter-Inn]] (003-022) — Ishmael first notices Bulkington, "full six feet in height, with noble shoulders, and a chest like a coffer-dam." His shipmates cry "Bulkington! where's Bulkington?" as he slips away.
- [[chapter-023|Ch. 23: The Lee Shore]] (023-001–023-004) — Ishmael sees him at the helm with "sympathetic awe and fearfulness," and the chapter unfolds into the novel's meditation on the lee shore.

## Character Traits

Bulkington is defined by his **physical stature**: six feet tall, face "deeply brown and burnt" by years at sea (003-022). His **reclusive disposition** is equally striking—he "held somewhat aloof" from his shipmates, disappearing without a word when the drinking peaked (003-022). His defining trait is a **visceral antipathy to land**: "the land seemed scorching to his feet" (023-002). This is not wanderlust but existential—Bulkington belongs to the "landlessness" where "highest truth" resides (023-004). He represents **apotheosis through peril**, transfigured from a minor character into a "demigod" whose destruction is transcendence (023-004).

## Relationships

**[[ishmael]]**: Bulkington exists almost entirely through Ishmael's gaze. "This man interested me at once" (003-022), and the "Lee Shore" chapter is Ishmael's eulogy, delivered with "sympathetic awe and fearfulness" (023-002).

**[[captain-ahab|Ahab]]**: The two never interact, but they are structural opposites. Ahab's obsession drives him toward the White Whale; Bulkington's compulsion drives him away from land. Both perish at sea, but where Ahab's death is monomania's culmination, Bulkington's unseen fate is presented as apotheosis (023-004).

**The [[the-pequod|Pequod]] crew**: Bulkington helms the ship on departure night and vanishes after Chapter 23, the novel's most spectral crew member.

## Notable Quotes

> "He stood full six feet in height, with noble shoulders, and a chest like a coffer-dam. I have seldom seen such brawn in a man. His face was deeply brown and burnt, making his white teeth dazzling by the contrast; while in the deep shadows of his eyes floated some reminiscences that did not seem to give him much joy." (003-022)

> "The land seemed scorching to his feet. Wonderfullest things are ever the unmentionable; deep memories yield no epitaphs; this six-inch chapter is the stoneless grave of Bulkington." (023-002)

> "Know ye now, Bulkington? Glimpses do ye seem to see of that mortally intolerable truth; that all deep, earnest thinking is but the intrepid effort of the soul to keep the open independence of her sea; while the wildest winds of heaven and earth conspire to cast her on the treacherous, slavish shore?" (023-003)

> "But as in landlessness alone resides highest truth, shoreless, indefinite as God—so, better is it to perish in that howling infinite, than be ingloriously dashed upon the lee, even if that were safety! Take heart, take heart, O Bulkington! Bear thee grimly, demigod! Up from the spray of thy ocean-perishing—straight up, leaps thy apotheosis!" (023-004)
