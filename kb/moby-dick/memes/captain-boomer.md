# Captain Boomer

## Overview

Captain Boomer is the commander of the London whaleship *Samuel Enderby*, encountered by the [[pequod|Pequod]] in Chapter 100, "Leg and Arm." He has lost his right arm to [[moby-dick|Moby Dick]] and wears an ivory prosthetic — a direct parallel to [[ahab|Captain Ahab's]] ivory leg. But where Ahab is consumed by vengeance, Boomer is good-humored, philosophical, and sensible.

He is "a darkly-tanned, burly, good-natured, fine-looking man, of sixty or thereabouts, dressed in a spacious roundabout" (100-002). His empty sleeve "streamed behind him like the broidered arm of a hussar's surcoat" — a detail that transforms his disfigurement into something almost martial and gallant.

Boomer's function in the novel is to show that a man can lose a limb to the White Whale and yet remain whole. He is the counter-example Ahab refuses to see.

## Role in the Narrative

Boomer appears in a single chapter but his presence crystallizes the novel's central thematic opposition: obsession versus acceptance.

The meeting between the two one-legged (and one-armed) captains is rich with dark comedy. Ahab hails the Samuel Enderby with his usual cry — "Hast seen the White Whale?" — and Boomer responds by holding up his ivory arm:

> "See you this?" (100-003)

Ahab, recognizing a fellow victim, has himself hoisted aboard via the blubber-hook. The two captains "shake bones together" — ivory arm crossing ivory leg "like two sword-fish blades" (100-006). Ahab exclaims: "an arm and a leg!—an arm that never can shrink, d'ye see; and a leg that never can run."

Boomer then recounts his encounter with Moby Dick. Unlike Ahab's metaphysical rage, Boomer's story is matter-of-fact, even comic. He attacked the whale, the whale attacked back, he lost his arm. End of story.

When asked whether he will hunt Moby Dick again, Boomer's response is definitive:

> "He's welcome to the arm he has, since I can't help it, and didn't know him then; but not to another one. No more White Whales for me; I've lowered for him once, and that has satisfied me."

This is the answer Ahab cannot give. Boomer has been wounded and has integrated the wound into a continuing life. Ahab has been wounded and has made the wound his entire existence.

## Character Traits

### Good-Humored Sensibility

Boomer's defining trait is his cheerful pragmatism. He jokes with his surgeon [[bunger|Dr. Bunger]], tolerates Ahab's intensity with polite bemusement, and regards his missing arm as a closed chapter. His good nature is not shallowness — it is a considered philosophical position.

### The Foil to Ahab

Every detail of Boomer's characterization contrasts with Ahab. Ahab is American, Boomer English. Ahab is gaunt and prophetic, Boomer burly and genial. Ahab's ship is named for a destroyed tribe, Boomer's for a prosperous merchant. Ahab demands vengeance; Boomer is "satisfied" with one encounter.

### The Survivor

Boomer has done what Ahab cannot: he has survived Moby Dick and let go. His ivory arm is a prosthetic he commissioned himself, "against all rule" according to Bunger, with a club-hammer at the end "to knock some one's brains out with, I suppose" (100-021). The joke is on Bunger — the mallet is a practical tool, not a weapon of vengeance.

## Relationships

- **[[ahab|Captain Ahab]]**: The novel's central contrast. Two captains, two prosthetic ivory limbs, two responses to disfigurement. Ahab sees in Boomer a reflection he refuses to acknowledge.
- **[[bunger|Dr. Bunger]]**: Boomer's ship's surgeon, with whom he shares a comedic double-act. Boomer teases Bunger mercilessly; Bunger, late of the clergy, plays the straight man.
- **[[moby-dick|Moby Dick]]**: The White Whale took Boomer's arm. Boomer's response — "he's welcome to the arm he has" — is the novel's most concise refutation of Ahab's monomania.
