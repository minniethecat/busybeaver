# The Shark Massacre

## Overview

On the night after [[stubbs-supper]], the dead sperm whale remains moored alongside the Pequod. In the Pacific, such a carcass attracts "incalculable hosts of sharks" (066-002) so voracious that "were he left so for six hours, little more than the skeleton would be visible by morning." Stubb, having concluded his whale-steak banquet, sets the anchor-watch. [[queequeg]] and a forecastle seaman take the deck, lower three lanterns over the side, and commence "an incessant murdering of the sharks" (066-003) with long-handled whaling-spades. The scene unfolds as a grotesque spectacle of reciprocal predation — the sharks devour the whale, the men stab the sharks, and the sharks, in their frenzy, devour each other and even themselves.

## Chronology

- (066-001) — The captured sperm whale lies alongside the Pequod through the night; customarily, cutting-in waits until daylight, with anchor-watches kept through the dark hours
- (066-002) — In the Pacific, especially near the Line, sharks gather in "incalculable hosts" around the moored carcass; Melville compares the sea to "one huge cheese, and those sharks the maggots in it"
- (066-003) — After Stubb's supper, Queequeg and a forecastle seaman take the anchor-watch; they lower three lanterns and begin stabbing sharks with "keen steel deep into their skulls, seemingly their only vital part"
- (066-003, cont.) — The sharks, in foamy confusion, reveal their "incredible ferocity": they snap at each other's entrails and even "bent round, and bit their own; till those entrails seemed swallowed over and over again by the same mouth"
- (066-003, cont.) — A "generic or Pantheistic vitality" seems to animate the sharks even after death: one almost takes Queequeg's hand off when he tries to shut its dead jaw
- (066-004) — A technical footnote describes the whaling-spade: made of the finest steel, about the size of a man's spread hand, kept razor-sharp, mounted on a twenty-to-thirty-foot pole
- (066-005) — Queequeg's verdict: "Queequeg no care what god made him shark... wedder Fejee god or Nantucket god; but de god wat made shark must be one dam Ingin."

## Narrative Significance

The Shark Massacre extends the [[stubbs-supper]] chapter's meditation on eating and being eaten into a darker, more visceral register. Where Stubb's supper was comic, the shark massacre is grotesque — a spectacle of "incessant murdering" (066-003) that collapses the distinctions between predator and prey, life and death, individual and mass.

> "A sort of generic or Pantheistic vitality seemed to lurk in their very joints and bones, after what might be called the individual life had departed." (066-003)

Melville's phrase "Pantheistic vitality" is key: the sharks possess a life-force that transcends individual death, a collective animation that persists even in dismembered parts. This image resonates with the novel's broader treatment of the whale as a creature of cosmic, almost divine, scale — and with Ahab's monomaniacal conviction that the White Whale is more than a whale, that behind its "dumb blankness" lurks an inscrutable malice.

Queequeg's closing line — "de god wat made shark must be one dam Ingin" (066-005) — is both comic relief and serious theology. It echoes Fleece's sermon in the previous chapter ("all angel is not'ing more dan de shark well goberned") while going further: the shark's nature implicates its Creator. If the shark is pure voracity, what does that say about the god who designed it?

## Aftermath / Consequences

The shark massacre, though a single night's watch, establishes the novel's vision of the sea as a realm of ceaseless, reciprocal predation. The image of sharks devouring themselves recurs in later passages, and the "Pantheistic vitality" that animates them becomes part of Melville's broader meditation on the life-force that connects all creatures — whale, shark, and man — in a single chain of consumption.
