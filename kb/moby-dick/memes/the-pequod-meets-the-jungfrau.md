# The Pequod Meets the Jungfrau

## Overview

The Pequod's encounter with the German whaling ship Jungfrau (the Virgin), captained by Derick De Deer of Bremen, is the novel's first extended whale-killing episode after the gam chapters begin in earnest. The German captain comes aboard begging for lamp oil — his ship is utterly dry — and Ahab shows no interest in him beyond the question of the White Whale. What follows is a grim race between the Pequod's boats and the German boats for a sick, old, blind sperm whale. The Pequod wins, and the killing that ensues is one of the most pitiable in the novel: the whale is elderly, half-blind, missing a fin, and dies slowly in a fountain of blood. The chapter ends with the Jungfrau chasing an uncapturable Fin-Back, a final image of futility.

## Chronology

### The Gam and the Beggar's Request

The two ships meet on the whale-grounds. Captain Derick De Deer approaches in his boat, impatiently standing in the bows rather than the stern, waving a lamp-feeder — he has come begging for oil. The Pequod's mates mock him: "it's a lamp-feeder and an oil-can. He's out of oil, and has come a-begging" (081-006). Stubb jokes that he's come to make coffee: "he's coming off to make us our coffee, is the Yarman" (081-005).

Ahab accosts Derick abruptly, seeking only news of the White Whale, but "in his broken lingo, the German soon evinced his complete ignorance of the White Whale; immediately turning the conversation to his lamp-feeder and oil can" (081-008). His necessities supplied, Derick departs.

### The Race for the Sick Whale

Almost immediately after Derick leaves, whales are raised from both ships' mast-heads. Derick, "so eager for the chase," slews his boat around without even stowing his oil-can and lamp-feeder aboard (081-009). The German boats have a considerable start on the Pequod's, having been closer to the pod.

Eight whales are fleeing at speed. But trailing far behind them is "a huge, humped old bull," afflicted with a yellowish incrustation — "seemed afflicted with the jaundice, or some other infirmity" (081-011). His spout is "short, slow, and laborious; coming forth with a choking sort of gush" (081-011). Stubb jokes about giving him paregoric for stomach-ache: "Lord, think of having half an acre of stomach-ache!" (081-012).

The whale reveals the cause of his erratic wake: "the unnatural stump of his starboard fin" (081-013). Whether lost in battle or born without it is unknowable.

All boats — German and Pequod's — converge on this one whale. He is the largest, nearest, and the only catchable one. Derick, having the start, seems confident he will win, and "occasionally with a deriding gesture shook his lamp-feeder at the other boats" (081-016).

Starbuck, Stubb, and Flask urge their crews on with mounting fury. Stubb offers "a hogshead of brandy to the best man" (081-018). Flask dances up and down crying: "There goes three thousand dollars, men! The bank of England! — Oh, do, do, do!" (081-019). Derick in desperation pitches his lamp-feeder and oil-can at the advancing Pequod boats (081-020).

### The Pequod's Victory

Just as Derick seems certain to win, "a righteous judgment descended upon him in a crab which caught the blade of his midship oarsman" (081-024). The German boat nearly capsizes, and the Pequod's three boats surge past.

Derick hazards "a most unusually long dart" as a last chance (081-026). But Queequeg, Tashtego, and Daggoo simultaneously spring to their feet and hurl their harpoons over the German harpooneer's head — "their three Nantucket irons entered the whale" (081-027). The three Pequod boats bump the German boat aside with such force that Derick and his harpooneer are spilled into the sea. Stubb, sailing past them, calls out: "Don't be afraid, my butter-boxes... ye'll be picked up presently — all right — I saw some sharks astern" (081-028).

### The Death of the Whale

The whale sounds deep, the lines gouging grooves in the loggerheads, the boats nearly pulled under. Melville pauses to reflect on the immense pressure the whale endures at depth — "the weight of fifty atmospheres" (081-029) — and the paradox of "three such thin threads" suspending "the great Leviathan" (081-030). He quotes Job: "the arrow cannot make him flee; darts are counted as stubble; he laugheth at the shaking of a spear!" — then adds, grimly: "that unfulfilments should follow the prophets" (081-030).

When the whale surfaces, his motions "plainly denoted his extreme exhaustion" (081-035). The lances dart into him, and because of the whale's unique non-valvular blood-vessel structure, "a deadly drain is at once begun upon his whole arterial system" (081-035). The whale rolls in his blood.

His eyes are revealed: "from the points which the whale's eyes had once occupied, now protruded blind bulbs, horribly pitiable to see" (081-036). Melville delivers one of the novel's most bitter sentences:

> For all his old age, and his one arm, and his blind eyes, he must die the death and be murdered, in order to light the gay bridals and other merry-makings of men, and also to illuminate the solemn churches that preach unconditional inoffensiveness by all to all. (081-036)

Flask strikes a discoloured protuberance on the whale's flank, and an "ulcerous jet shot from this cruel wound" (081-039). The whale thrashes, capsizes Flask's boat, and dies:

> It was most piteous, that last expiring spout. As when by unseen hands the water is gradually drawn off from some mighty fountain, and with half-stifled melancholy gurglings the spray-column lowers and lowers to the ground — so the last long dying spout of the whale. (081-039)

### The Carcase and Its Secrets

The whale's body begins to sink. Starbuck orders lines secured to buoy it. When the crew begins cutting in, they find "the entire length of a corroded harpoon" embedded in his flesh (081-041), and more remarkably, "a lance-head of stone" — "Who had darted that stone lance? It might have been darted by some Nor' West Indian long before America was discovered" (081-041).

The carcase grows heavier and begins to drag the Pequod sideways. In a dramatic scene, the ship tilts perilously: "to cross to the other side of the deck was like walking up the steep gabled roof of a house" (081-042). Queequeg seizes a hatchet and slashes at the fluke-chains, and with "a terrific snap, every fastening went adrift; the ship righted, the carcase sank" (081-044).

### The Jungfrau's Futile Chase

As the body sinks, the Pequod's mast-heads announce that the Jungfrau is lowering boats again — this time chasing a mere spout. But it is a Fin-Back, "belonging to the species of uncapturable whales, because of its incredible power of swimming" (081-047). The chapter's closing line is one of Melville's most famous aphorisms: "Oh! many are the Fin-Backs, and many are the Dericks, my friend" (081-048).

## Narrative Significance

Chapter 81 is the novel's most unflinching depiction of whaling's violence, and Melville uses it to indict not only the whalemen but the entire civilization that demands whale oil. The description of the whale's suffering — blind, old, crippled, bleeding out in torment — is deliberately unbearable. The bitter irony of "illuminat[ing] the solemn churches that preach unconditional inoffensiveness by all to all" (081-036) is among the most overt moral condemnations in *Moby-Dick*.

The chapter also establishes the competitive, predatory dynamic between whaling ships — even supposed allies — and the Pequod's ruthless efficiency. Where the Jungfrau is hapless, comical, and ultimately futile, the Pequod is deadly. Yet both ships participate in the same brutal economy.

## Aftermath / Consequences

The whale's body is lost to the sea before its full treasures can be taken — a recurring frustration in the novel. The discovery of the ancient stone lance-head inside the whale opens a deep temporal perspective: whaling extends into an unrecoverable past, and the whale's body is an archive of encounters across centuries. The Jungfrau, chasing the uncapturable Fin-Back, disappears to leeward, an emblem of wasted effort.

## Critical Analysis

The chapter's closing aphorism — "Oh! many are the Fin-Backs, and many are the Dericks, my friend" — works on multiple levels. On the surface, it laments the many whalemen who chase uncapturable quarry. Symbolically, it suggests that most human pursuits are as futile as chasing Fin-Backs. The Derick (the hapless pursuer) and the Fin-Back (the unreachable object) are archetypes Melville treats with weary, almost affectionate contempt.

The race sequence is structured as a dark parody of epic competition. The Pequod's three mates — Starbuck, Stubb, Flask — are presented as a comedic trio, each with his own rhetorical style (Starbuck's intensity, Stubb's mockery, Flask's greed), yet the cumulative effect is not comedy but horror. The whale's death is prolonged and described with clinical precision, forcing the reader to confront the cost of every barrel of oil.
