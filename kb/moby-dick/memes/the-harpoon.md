# The Harpoon

## Definition

The harpoon is the defining instrument of the whaling enterprise — the point of contact between human intention and the whale's living body. In *Moby-Dick*, Melville invests this utilitarian tool with extraordinary symbolic weight, treating it not merely as a weapon but as a medium of existential connection. The harpoon is "harpooned to be sure; but bethink you, how you will manage to keep in tow in the meanwhile" (P02-150): the act of striking is only the beginning of a relationship that binds hunter and hunted together in a shared fate. Throughout the novel, the harpoon functions as a material sign of human penetration into the natural world — and, reciprocally, of nature's power to draw the penetrator into its own destruction.

## Manifestations in the Novel

The harpoon enters the narrative in the Spouter-Inn (Chapter 3), where Ishmael encounters "rusty old whaling lances and harpoons all broken and deformed" (003-004) among the relics decorating the entryway. These broken weapons serve as a memento mori — evidence of past hunts, past failures, and the physical toll that whaling exacts on its instruments and its practitioners alike. The harpoon is already freighted with mortality before Ishmael ever steps aboard a whaleship.

The novel's most symbolically charged harpoon scene occurs in "The Forge" (Chapter 113), where Perth the blacksmith hammers Ahab's special harpoon, tempering it not in water but in the blood of the three harpooneers — Queequeg, Tashtego, and Daggoo. Ahab intones a "baptism" over the weapon: "Ego non baptizo te in nomine patris, sed in nomine diaboli!" This harpoon, forged specifically for Moby Dick, concentrates the novel's themes of vengeance, ritual, and perverted sacrament into a single object. It is at once a tool, a fetish, and a covenant with darkness.

The harpooneer as a figure receives sustained attention throughout the novel. Queequeg, the first harpooneer Ishmael meets, is introduced through his instrument: the landlord of the Spouter-Inn assures Ishmael that his bedfellow "had just arrived from the south seas, where he had bought up a lot of 'balmed New Zealand heads' and had 'a great chest, full of harpoons'" (003-007). The harpoon here functions as a marker of identity — to be a harpooneer is to occupy a specific and respected position in the whaling world's hierarchy. Queequeg's harpoon, like the man himself, is both practical and invested with the aura of distant, exotic origins.

In the climactic chase, the harpoon becomes the literal instrument of the *Pequod*'s destruction. Ahab's final strike pierces Moby Dick's flank but the whale dives, and "the harpoon was darted; the stricken whale flew forward; with igniting velocity the line ran through the groove" — Ahab is caught in the line and dragged under. The harpoon that was meant to kill becomes the means of Ahab's own death, fulfilling the symbolic logic that has been building since the Spouter-Inn: the weapon that penetrates the whale also binds the hunter to his prey in a fatal reciprocity.

## Key Passages

> "The Whale is harpooned to be sure; but bethink you, how you will manage to keep in tow in the meanwhile."
> (P02-150)

> "Among the former, were several enormous hams, and rusty old whaling lances and harpoons all broken and deformed. Some were thickly set with glittering teeth resembling ivory saws; others were tufted with knots of human hair; and one sickle-shaped sweep of a lance was set round with barbs of ivory."
> (003-004)

> "It would depend upon who the harpooneer might be, and that if he (the landlord) really had no other place for me, and the harpooneer was not decidedly objectionable, why rather than wander further about a strange town on so bitter a night, I would put up with the half of any decent man's blanket."
> (003-008)

> "The devil fetch that harpooneer, thought I, but stop, could it be—?"
> (003-029)
