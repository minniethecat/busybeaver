# The Sermon

## Definition

The Sermon is Father Mapple's dramatic homily on the Book of Jonah, delivered from the ship-shaped pulpit of the Whaleman's Chapel in Chapter 9. It is the novel's central theological set-piece, using Jonah's flight from God, his descent into the whale's belly, and his subsequent deliverance as a parable for the whaleman's—and every human's—relationship with divine will. The sermon establishes the moral and spiritual framework within which the Pequod's voyage will unfold.

## Manifestations in the Novel

The sermon takes place in the Whaleman's Chapel in New Bedford, where Ishmael attends service before shipping aboard the Pequod. Father Mapple climbs into the pulpit by means of a rope ladder, which he then draws up after himself—a gesture that isolates the preacher in his maritime "crow's nest" and symbolizes the spiritual solitude of the prophetic calling.

Father Mapple frames Jonah's story as "a two-stranded lesson": one strand addressing "us all as sinful men," the other addressing himself "as a pilot of the living God." He traces Jonah's trajectory through sin, flight, the storm at sea, being swallowed by the whale, repentance in the fish's belly, and final deliverance. The sermon culminates in an exhortation to "preach the Truth to the face of Falsehood" and to reject the easy path of self-justification.

Structurally, the sermon transforms the whaleman's world—storms, ships, the great fish—into a cosmic stage where the drama of sin and redemption plays out. The congregation of whalemen hears their own profession rendered as sacred history.

## Key Passages

> "Shipmates, this book, containing only four chapters—four yarns—is one of the smallest strands in the mighty cable of the Scriptures. Yet what depths of the soul does Jonah's deep sealine sound! what a pregnant lesson to us is this prophet! What a noble thing is that canticle in the fish's belly! How billow-like and boisterously grand!" (009-025)

Father Mapple opens his sermon by elevating the brief Book of Jonah to an almost oceanic profundity. The maritime imagery—"deep sealine," "billow-like"—translates scriptural exegesis into the language of the whaleman's experience.

> "As sinful men, it is a lesson to us all, because it is a story of the sin, hard-heartedness, suddenly awakened fears, the swift punishment, repentance, prayers, and finally the deliverance and joy of Jonah." (009-026)

This passage lays out the sermon's narrative arc: sin, fear, punishment, repentance, deliverance. The pattern mirrors the larger structure of *Moby-Dick* itself, particularly Ahab's trajectory—though notably without the repentance and deliverance that Jonah achieves.

> "Woe to him who seeks to pour oil upon the waters when God has brewed them into a gale! Woe to him who seeks to please rather than to appal!" (009-028)

Father Mapple rejects the notion of a comfortable, consoling religion. The true preacher's duty is to confront sin directly, not to soothe. This rhetorical stance aligns with Melville's own rejection of sentimental Christianity.

> "And now behold Jonah taken up as an anchor and dropped into the sea... He is standing now in the whale's belly, and is crying unto the Lord his God out of his affliction." (009-035)

The sermon's emotional climax pivots on Jonah's descent and his turn toward prayer. The whale—elsewhere in the novel a symbol of inscrutable nature or cosmic indifference—here becomes the very instrument of divine discipline and eventual salvation.

## Scholarly Context

The Sermon has drawn extensive critical attention. Many scholars read Father Mapple as Melville's mouthpiece for a Calvinist-inflected vision of human helplessness before an inscrutable God—a theological position that Ahab will later invert into defiance. Others emphasize the democratic dimension: a former whaleman turned preacher addresses a congregation of sailors, dissolving the boundary between sacred and profane labor. The sermon's centrality to the novel's architecture is underscored by its position immediately before Ishmael and Queequeg sign aboard the Pequod, making it the last word of moral instruction before the voyage into Ahab's world begins.
