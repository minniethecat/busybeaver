# Stubb Confronts Ahab

## Overview

Some days after the Pequod leaves the ice behind and enters the "bright Quito spring" of the tropical Pacific, the second mate [[stubb|Stubb]] makes a well-meaning suggestion: that [[ahab|Captain Ahab]] might muffle the noise of his ivory leg on the deck at night, so the crew can sleep. Ahab's response — "Down, dog, and kennel!" (029-004) — triggers one of the novel's most revealing minor confrontations: a brief, lopsided exchange that lays bare Ahab's inner torment and showcases Stubb's irrepressible, comic-philosophical temperament.

## Chronology

### The Tropics and the Tormented Captain

The chapter opens with a lush description of the tropical sea:

> "The warmly cool, clear, ringing, perfumed, overflowing, redundant days, were as crystal goblets of Persian sherbet, heaped up — flaked up, with rose-water snow." (029-001)

Yet this sensuous beauty only intensifies Ahab's inward suffering: "all the witcheries of that unwaning weather did not merely lend new spells and potencies to the outward world. Inward they turned upon the soul" (029-001).

Ahab has taken to pacing the deck at night. The narratorial commentary establishes his state before the confrontation even begins:

> "Old age is always wakeful; as if, the longer linked with life, the less man has to do with aught that looks like death." (029-002)

Ahab now spends more time on deck than in his cabin: "It feels like going down into one's tomb — he would mutter to himself — for an old captain like me to be descending this narrow scuttle, to go to my grave-dug berth" (029-002).

### The Suggestion

When the watches are set and the crew below are sleeping, Ahab habitually emerges. Usually, some "considering touch of humanity" (029-003) keeps him from patrolling the quarter-deck, "because to his wearied mates, seeking repose within six inches of his ivory heel, such would have been the reverberating crack and din of that bony step" (029-003).

But on this night, Ahab's mood overrides his consideration:

> "But once, the mood was on him too deep for common regardings; and as with heavy, lumber-like pace he was measuring the ship from taffrail to mainmast, Stubb, the old second mate, came up from below, with a certain unassured, deprecating humorousness, hinted that if Captain Ahab was pleased to walk the planks, then, no one could say nay; but there might be some way of muffling the noise; hinting something indistinctly and hesitatingly about a globe of tow, and the insertion into it, of the ivory heel." (029-003)

Melville's narrative aside — "Ah! Stubb, thou didst not know Ahab then" (029-003) — marks the moment as a threshold: Stubb is about to learn something.

### The Explosion

Ahab's response is instantaneous and violent:

> "'Am I a cannon-ball, Stubb,' said Ahab, 'that thou wouldst wad me that fashion? But go thy ways; I had forgot. Below to thy nightly grave; where such as ye sleep between shrouds, to use ye to the filling one at last. — Down, dog, and kennel!'" (029-004)

The progression is characteristically Ahabsian: mockery ("cannon-ball"), dark suggestion of mortality ("shrouds," "filling one at last"), and finally animal debasement ("dog," "kennel").

Stubb, stunned, protests: "I am not used to be spoken to that way, sir; I do but less than half like it, sir" (029-005). Ahab grits between his teeth: "Avast!" (029-006) and moves away "as if to avoid some passionate temptation" (029-006) — a gesture that suggests Ahab is struggling to restrain himself from worse.

When Stubb persists — "I will not tamely be called a dog, sir" (029-007) — Ahab escalates:

> "Then be called ten times a donkey, and a mule, and an ass, and begone, or I'll clear the world of thee!" (029-008)

Ahab advances on Stubb "with such overbearing terrors in his aspect, that Stubb involuntarily retreated" (029-009). The confrontation ends not with resolution but with Stubb's physical retreat — the instinctive response of a man who has glimpsed something unmanageable.

### Stubb's Soliloquy

Descending the cabin-scuttle, Stubb delivers a long internal monologue that transforms the confrontation from tragedy to comedy:

> "I was never served so before without giving a hard blow for it. … It's very queer. Stop, Stubb; somehow, now, I don't well know whether to go back and strike him, or — what's that? — down here on my knees and pray for him? Yes, that was the thought coming up in me; but it would be the first time I ever did pray." (029-010)

Stubb speculates on Ahab's condition: his sleeplessness, his rumpled hammock, his constant pacing, the hot pillow "as though a baked brick had been on it" (029-010). He diagnoses Ahab with "what some folks ashore call a conscience; it's a kind of Tic-Dolly-row they say — worse nor a toothache" (029-010).

His philosophical conclusion is pure Stubb: "Damn me, it's worth a fellow's while to be born into the world, if only to fall right asleep. And now that I think of it, that's about the first thing babies do, and that's a sort of queer, too. Damn me, but all things are queer, come to think of 'em. But that's against my principles. Think not, is my eleventh commandment; and sleep when you can, is my twelfth" (029-010).

He ends by talking himself out of his anger: "He might as well have kicked me, and done with it. Maybe he did kick me, and I didn't observe it, I was so taken all aback with his brow, somehow. … By the Lord, I must have been dreaming, though" (029-010).

## Narrative Significance

This brief encounter — the entire action occupies perhaps two minutes on deck — is one of the novel's most efficient character studies. It reveals Ahab's inner state through indirection: his fury at Stubb has almost nothing to do with Stubb. The "globe of tow" suggestion is a minor practical matter; Ahab's reaction is volcanic because it brushes against the raw nerve of his suffering. He is, as he will later confess to Starbuck, "all aleak" (109-010).

The chapter also establishes Stubb as a distinctive moral type. Unlike [[starbuck|Starbuck]], who confronts Ahab with rational argument and conscience (Chapters 109, 123), Stubb processes the confrontation through comedy and self-persuasion. His internal monologue — "Think not, is my eleventh commandment; and sleep when you can, is my twelfth" — is a survival strategy. Stubb survives Ahab not by resisting him but by refusing to dwell on what he has seen.

The structure of the chapter — violence followed by comic deflation — is a miniature of Melville's broader method. Ahab's "Down, dog, and kennel!" is genuinely terrifying; Stubb's retreat into philosophical whimsy is genuinely funny. The two tones coexist without canceling each other, and the chapter's final effect is neither pure tragedy nor pure comedy but something uniquely Melvillean: tragicomedy as epistemology, a way of seeing that holds terror and absurdity in a single frame.

## Aftermath / Consequences

Stubb returns to his hammock, determined to "see how this plaguey juggling thinks over by daylight" (029-010). The confrontation has no immediate practical consequence — Ahab continues his night pacing, Stubb continues his role as second mate — but it establishes a pattern. Stubb's strategy of comic deflection becomes his characteristic mode of dealing with Ahab throughout the voyage, most notably in Chapter 36 ("[[the-quarter-deck|The Quarter-Deck]]") when Ahab's blasphemous oath falls on Stubb's ears and the mate offers only a jest.

The chapter also deepens the portrait of Ahab's sleeplessness — a motif that recurs throughout the novel. In Chapter 37, Ahab will sit alone in his cabin, "the insanity of sleep" denied to him; in Chapter 44, Melville describes Ahab's "deadly wakefulness." The night pacing introduced here is the outward symptom of an inward condition that has no cure.
