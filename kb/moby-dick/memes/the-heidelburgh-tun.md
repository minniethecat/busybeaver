# The Heidelburgh Tun

## Definition

The Heidelburgh Tun is Ishmael's name for the sperm whale's "Case" — the vast upper chamber of the whale's head that contains the purest spermaceti. In Chapter 77, he compares it to the famous great wine cask of Heidelburgh Castle: "as that famous great tierce is mystically carved in front, so the whale's vast plaited forehead forms innumerable strange devices for the emblematical adornment of his wondrous tun" (077-004). The metaphor elevates whale anatomy to architecture, treating the sperm whale's head as a crafted vessel holding "by far the most precious of all his oily vintages."

## Manifestations in the Novel

Chapter 77 ("The Heidelburgh Tun") belongs to the sequence of cetological chapters (74–77) that anatomize the sperm whale's head after it has been hoisted alongside the Pequod. Having treated the sperm whale's head as a "solid oblong" (Ch. 74) and contrasted it with the right whale's head (Ch. 75), Ishmael now turns to the internal structure.

He divides the head into two "quoins" — a term he admits "is not a Euclidean term" but "belongs to the pure nautical mathematics" (077-003). The lower portion, called the "junk," is "one immense honeycomb of oil, formed by the crossing and recrossing, into ten thousand infiltrated cells, of tough elastic white fibres" (077-004). The upper portion — the Case — is the prized reservoir. "The tun of the whale contains by far the most precious of all his oily vintages; namely, the highly-prized spermaceti, in its absolutely pure, limpid, and odoriferous state. Nor is this precious substance found unalloyed in any other part of the creature" (077-004).

Ishmael describes the spermaceti's remarkable properties: "Though in life it remains perfectly fluid, yet, upon exposure to the air, after death, it soon begins to concrete; sending forth beautiful crystalline shoots, as when the first thin delicate ice is just forming in water" (077-004). A large whale's case yields "about five hundred gallons of sperm," though "considerable of it is spilled, leaks, and dribbles away." The inner membrane of the case is compared to "the silken pearl-coloured membrane, like the lining of a fine pelisse" (077-005). For an eighty-foot whale, Ishmael calculates the tun's depth at "more than twenty-six feet... when it is lengthwise hoisted up and down against a ship's side" (077-006). The chapter closes ominously, alluding to the "marvellous and — in this particular instance — almost fatal operation whereby the Sperm Whale's great Heidelburgh Tun is tapped" (077-008), foreshadowing Tashtego's near-death in the following chapter.

## Scholarly Context

The Heidelburgh Tun chapter is a prime example of Melville's "cetological sublime" — the elevation of whale anatomy to the scale of architecture and myth. The comparison to the Heidelburgh Tun, a famous landmark of European culture, places the whale within a global framework of monuments and craftsmanship. Critics have noted that the tun metaphor also carries Eucharistic overtones: the pure spermaceti as a kind of sacred oil, tapped from a living vessel. The chapter's position between the phrenological examinations (Ch. 74–75) and the near-fatal baling operation (Ch. 78) gives it a structural role as the point where scientific description gives way to dramatic action.
