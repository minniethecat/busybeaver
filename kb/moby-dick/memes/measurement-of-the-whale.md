# Measurement of the Whale

## Definition

"Measurement of the Whale" refers to Ishmael's account in Chapter 102 ("A Bower in the Arsacides") of measuring the skeleton of a great sperm whale washed ashore on the island of Tranque, one of the Arsacides. The chapter serves as the culmination of the novel's cetological sequence — the moment when Ishmael attempts to capture the whale not through description, taxonomy, or depiction, but through raw numerical measurement. The result is both comic and philosophically serious: the numbers Ishmael tattoos on his arm provide a kind of knowledge, but the chapter's real subject is the inadequacy of measurement as a way of knowing a living creature. The whale skeleton, transformed into a temple by the islanders, becomes a figure for the relationship between the measurable and the meaningful.

## Manifestations in the Novel

### The Arsacides Temple

The chapter's extraordinary setting is established through Ishmael's recollection of his "late royal friend Tranquo, king of Tranque, one of the Arsacides" (102-004). Tranquo, "being gifted with a devout love for all matters of barbaric vertu," has collected the skeleton of a great sperm whale that washed ashore after "an unusually long raging gale" and transported it to a "grand temple of lordly palms" in a glen (102-006). The skeleton has been transformed: "The ribs were hung with trophies; the vertebræ were carved with Arsacidean annals, in strange hieroglyphics; in the skull, the priests kept up an unextinguished aromatic flame, so that the mystic head again sent forth its vapory spout" (102-007). The whale's lower jaw "vibrated over all the devotees, like the hair-hung sword that so affrighted Damocles" (102-007).

### The Weaver-God Digression

Before reaching the measurements, Ishmael pauses for one of the novel's most famous philosophical digressions. Contemplating the living jungle that weaves itself around the dead skeleton, he delivers a rhapsody on nature as a cosmic loom:

> "Through the lacings of the leaves, the great sun seemed a flying shuttle weaving the unwearied verdure. Oh, busy weaver! unseen weaver!—pause!—one word!—whither flows the fabric? what palace may it deck? wherefore all these ceaseless toilings?" (102-008)

The weaver-god, deafened by his own weaving, cannot hear mortal voices — "and by that humming, we, too, who look on the loom are deafened; and only when we escape it shall we hear the thousand voices that speak through it" (102-008). This image of a universe that hums too loudly for its inhabitants to understand it frames the measurement attempt that follows: Ishmael's numbers are produced within a context that renders all merely quantitative knowledge absurd.

### The Act of Measurement

The priests object when Ishmael begins to measure their god: "'How now!' they shouted; 'Dar'st thou measure this our god! That's for us'" (102-011). A "fierce contest" breaks out among them "concerning feet and inches; they cracked each other's sconces with their yard-sticks" — allowing Ishmael to "quickly conclude my own admeasurements" (102-011) in the chaos. The scene satirizes the pretensions of empirical science: Ishmael's measurements are obtained not through disciplined observation but through opportunistic theft while the official guardians of knowledge are fighting among themselves about units.

The measurements themselves are recorded not in a notebook but tattooed on Ishmael's right arm — "as in my wild wanderings at that period, there was no other secure way of preserving such valuable statistics" (102-014). He confesses he "did not trouble myself with the odd inches; nor, indeed, should inches at all enter into a congenial admeasurement of the whale" (102-014). The dismissal of "inches" as unworthy of a "congenial" measurement is the chapter's epistemological punchline: the kind of knowledge that matters about the whale cannot be captured in units.

### The Temple as Double Symbol

The Arsacides temple fuses two opposing images: the whale as object of empirical inquiry (a skeleton to be measured) and the whale as object of worship (a god whose measurements are sacred mystery). The priests who fight over yardsticks while tending an eternal flame are the keepers of both modes, and Ishmael — the wandering sailor who measures and worships in equal measure — moves between these two ways of knowing without resolving them.

## Scholarly Context

"A Bower in the Arsacides" has been recognized as one of the novel's most concentrated allegorical episodes. The weaver-god passage has been read as Melville's response to the problem of evil (theodicy) — the deaf weaver who cannot hear mortal cries reimagines the deity as indifferent rather than malevolent. The measurement scene has been analyzed as a satire of the nineteenth-century scientific ambition to quantify everything, from phrenology to natural history, and as a defense of literary against scientific knowledge. The tattooed measurements — numbers inscribed on a living body rather than a dead page — literalize the novel's insistence on embodied, experiential knowledge over abstract system. Critics have also noted the chapter's engagement with colonial themes: Tranquo's "barbaric vertu" collection, the competing measurement systems of priests and sailor, and the implicit comparison between the whale skeleton and colonized bodies all gesture toward the violence that underwrites any claim to "own" knowledge of the other.
