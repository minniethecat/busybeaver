# Inscrutable

## Definition

"Inscrutable" is one of Melville's most deliberate and recurrent adjectives, appearing at crucial moments throughout *Moby-Dick* to mark the boundary where human understanding fails. The word names not a temporary ignorance but a structural condition: the world is not merely unknown but *designed* to resist scrutiny. When Ishmael speaks of "the inscrutable tides of God" (035-014), when Ahab's hatred is called an "inscrutable malice" (036-040), when the Parsee Fedallah's glance is "inscrutable" (130-003), and when Ahab confronts "what nameless, inscrutable, unearthly thing" (132-017) the whale might be, Melville uses the same word to connect divine mystery, human obsession, and cetological enigma. The inscrutable is the novel's name for what cannot be named — the residue that remains after all inquiry is exhausted.

## Manifestations in the Novel

**The inscrutable tides of God (Chapter 35)**. At the mast-head, Ishmael reflects that the sea engenders "the ever-moving and still-yawning hypaethral dome," and that by gazing into its depths, "by the sea, from the inscrutable tides of God" (035-014), the soul is drawn toward the "Descartian vortices." The phrase "inscrutable tides of God" is characteristically ambiguous: are the tides themselves inscrutable, or are they God's inscrutable instrument? The grammar permits both readings, and Melville exploits the ambiguity. The sea's motion is both natural (tides) and supernatural (God's), and the adjective "inscrutable" marks the point where these two causalities merge.

**Ahab's inscrutable malice (Chapter 36)**. When Ahab reveals his purpose on the quarter-deck, Ishmael describes his hatred of the whale as possessing "an inscrutable malice sinewing it" (036-040). The word "sinewing" is crucial — malice is not an emotion but a physical structure, a musculature that gives Ahab's hatred its power and its shape. "Inscrutable" here names what cannot be explained about Ahab: why this particular whale, why this intensity of hatred, why this willingness to sacrifice everything. The malice is inscrutable because it exceeds any rational account of cause and effect.

**Inscrutable Ahab (Chapter 48)**. During the first lowering, when Ahab appears unexpectedly in his whaleboat, the narrator calls him "inscrutable Ahab" (048-037) — the adjective now attached directly to the man rather than to his hatred or his motives. Ahab has become the inscrutable: his presence is itself an enigma. The crew cannot read him, and the narrator's withholding of Ahab's interiority makes the captain as opaque to the reader as he is to his men.

**The inscrutable sea-ravens (Chapter 51)**. As the Pequod pursues the spirit-spout, "the inscrutable sea-ravens" (051-008) fly in the ship's wake. These birds — "the inscrutable sea-ravens" — are omens without meaning, signs that signify only their own opacity. Melville's adjective transforms ordinary seabirds into hieroglyphs: they are present, they are watching, but what they mean cannot be decoded.

**The inscrutable Parsee (Chapter 130)**. Fedallah's glance "so awed the crew's" that even Ahab's power seemed checked by it — "the inscrutable Parsee's glance awed his" (130-003). Fedallah is the novel's most persistently inscrutable character, a figure from another cosmology who seems to know what Ahab cannot. His inscrutability is not a failure of characterization but its principle: Fedallah exists to be unreadable, a living instance of the inscrutable in human form.

**What nameless, inscrutable, unearthly thing (Chapter 132)**. In the Symphony chapter, Ahab asks the novel's great metaphysical question: "What is it, what nameless, inscrutable, unearthly thing is it; what cozening, hidden lord and master, and cruel, remorseless emperor commands me?" (132-017). The string of adjectives — "nameless, inscrutable, unearthly" — escalates from the unknown to the unknowable. The thing that commands Ahab cannot even be named, let alone understood. "Inscrutable" is the middle term: it is not that the thing has no nature, but that its nature cannot be read. Ahab's tragedy is that he cannot accept the inscrutable as inscrutable — he must personify it, pursue it, and try to kill it.

## Key Passages

- (035-014) — "By the sea, from the inscrutable tides of God": the sea as medium of divine inscrutability, the mast-head as philosophical station.
- (036-040) — "Outrageous strength, with an inscrutable malice sinewing it": Ahab's hatred as physically structured enigma.
- (048-037) — "What it was that inscrutable Ahab said to that tiger-yellow crew": Ahab himself becomes the inscrutable, his speech withheld.
- (051-008) — "The inscrutable sea-ravens": omens without meanings, signs that signify only opacity.
- (087-028) — "These inscrutable creatures at the centre freely and fearlessly": the whales themselves are inscrutable, sovereign in their opacity.
- (130-003) — "The inscrutable Parsee's glance awed his": Fedallah as the human embodiment of the inscrutable.
- (132-017) — "What nameless, inscrutable, unearthly thing is it": Ahab's culminating metaphysical question, the inscrutable as cosmic antagonist.

## Scholarly Context

Melville's use of "inscrutable" has been central to epistemological readings of the novel. The word appears far more frequently in *Moby-Dick* than in any of Melville's other works, suggesting it was a deliberate philosophical keyword. Scholars have linked it to Melville's reading of Calvinist theology (the *Deus absconditus*, the hidden God whose will is inscrutable), to Romantic theories of the sublime (the inscrutable as the sublime's cognitive correlate), and to Melville's critique of Enlightenment confidence in reason. The "inscrutable" is what remains after Enlightenment: not ignorance that can be cured by more knowledge, but a structural opacity that knowledge itself reveals.
