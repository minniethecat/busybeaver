# Fear

## Definition

Fear is the novel's most pervasive emotional tone. From the Gothic anxieties of the opening chapters to the existential terror of the final chase, Moby-Dick maps fear across a spectrum: physical fear of bodily harm, social fear of ostracism, religious fear of divine judgment, and the ineffable "pale dread" that whiteness itself provokes (042-007). Ishmael declares early that "Ignorance is the parent of fear" (003-056), grounding fear not in external danger but in the limits of human knowledge. The whale — unseen, unmastered — becomes the object of all these fears at once.

## Manifestations in the Novel

**Gothic fear at the Spouter-Inn (Chapters 3–4)**. Ishmael's entrance into the Spouter-Inn is steeped in Gothic terror. The strange painting, the dark passages, and the prospect of sharing a bed with an unknown harpooneer provoke "the most awful fears" (004-003). When Queequeg returns and Ishmael watches him undress — revealing tattoos, a tomahawk, and a shaven head — Ishmael lies "frozen with the most awful fears, not daring to drag away my hand" (004-003). Yet when fear is removed, the same sensations that were terrifying become comic: "take away the awful fear, and my sensations at feeling the supernatural hand in mine were very similar" to ordinary human contact (004-004).

**Ignorance and fear (Chapter 3)**. Ishmael formulates the novel's most direct theory of fear: "Ignorance is the parent of fear" (003-056). Being "completely nonplussed and confounded about the stranger" — Queequeg — Ishmael is consumed by terror born of not knowing. This epistemological dimension of fear runs throughout: what we cannot know, we fear.

**The fear of God (Chapter 9)**. In Father Mapple's sermon, fear takes a theological form. Jonah's "suddenly awakened fears" drive him to repentance (009-026), and his shipmates "look on him with a quick fear that was strange to them" (009-036). The fear of the Lord is presented as the beginning of wisdom — a fear that saves rather than destroys.

**Fear and whiteness (Chapter 42)**. The novel's deepest exploration of fear comes in "The Whiteness of the Whale." Ishmael argues that whiteness produces "spiritual wonderment and pale dread" (042-007) more powerfully than any known danger. The albatross, the White Steed, and the polar bear all evoke terror through their whiteness alone. Moby Dick is the ultimate object of this fear — not merely dangerous but existentially unsettling.

**Jonah's flight from fear (Chapter 9)**. Jonah's attempt to flee to Tarshish is driven by fear of God's command. Mapple's Jonah trembles before the sailors, "summoning all his boldness to his face, only looks so much the more a coward" — fear compounds itself, Jonah fearing both God and man.

## Key Passages

- (003-056) — "Ignorance is the parent of fear" — Ishmael's epistemological theory of terror.
- (004-003) — Ishmael lies "frozen with the most awful fears, not daring to drag away my hand" from Queequeg's "supernatural hand."
- (004-004) — "Take away the awful fear, and my sensations at feeling the supernatural hand in mine were very similar" — fear as the transformer of experience.
- (009-026) — Jonah's "suddenly awakened fears, the swift punishment, repentance, prayers, and finally the deliverance and joy" — fear as the beginning of redemption.
- (014-004) — The whale is "more to be dreaded than his most fearless and malicious assaults" — fear beyond physical danger.
- (042-007) — "those clouds of spiritual wonderment and pale dread, in which that white phantom sails in all imaginations" — fear and whiteness.

## Scholarly Context

Melville's treatment of fear draws on the Burkean sublime — the "delightful horror" that Edmund Burke theorized as the strongest emotion the soul is capable of feeling. Ishmael's analysis of ignorance as fear's parent also anticipates Freudian and existentialist accounts of anxiety as rooted in the unknown. The novel's Gothic apparatus in the early chapters — the Spouter-Inn, the coffin-shaped bar, the "heathenish" Queequeg — has been read as Melville's deliberate deployment and subsequent dismantling of Gothic conventions: what begins as sensational horror deepens into philosophical dread over the course of the voyage.
