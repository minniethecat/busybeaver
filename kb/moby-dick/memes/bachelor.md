# Bachelor

## Overview

The Bachelor is a [[nantucket|Nantucket]] whaleship the [[pequod|Pequod]] encounters in the Pacific. She has "just wedged in her last cask of oil" (115-002) — her hold is full, her voyage is successful, and she is bound for home.

Her crew is celebrating. They are "gay" and festive, in sharp contrast to the grim intensity aboard the Pequod. The Bachelor represents everything Ahab has sacrificed to his quest.

## The Ship and Her Crew

The Bachelor has had "the most surprising success" (115-004). Her casks are full. Her men have done their work and will be paid.

When the Pequod meets her, the Bachelor's crew is in high spirits. They have strung flags. The captain lifts a glass and invites Ahab aboard for a drink: "Come aboard, come aboard!" (115-008).

The Bachelor's men are men of ordinary ambition: they want money, home, and safety. They have what the Pequod's crew cannot have — a happy ending.

## Encounter with the Pequod

The encounter is brief and one-sided. Ahab calls out his standard question: "Hast seen the White Whale?" The Bachelor's captain, more interested in his celebration than in sea-lore, gives no useful answer.

Ahab does not board the Bachelor. He cannot bear its cheerfulness. The Pequod's crew watches the Bachelor recede: "the Bachelor's men never heeded the Pequod's desolate signals" (115-014).

The day after this encounter, whales are sighted and killed — the Pequod's work continues, as joyless as ever (116-001).

## Key Passages

- (115-002) — "It was a Nantucket ship, the Bachelor, which had just wedged in her last cask of oil"
- (115-004) — "The Bachelor had met with the most surprising success"
- (115-008) — "Come aboard!" cried the gay Bachelor's commander, lifting a glass
- (115-014) — "Glances towards the receding Bachelor; but the Bachelor's men never heeded"
- (116-001) — "The day after encountering the gay Bachelor, whales were seen"

## Fate

The Bachelor returns safely to Nantucket. Her crew is paid. Her casks are sold.

She is the novel's image of what whaling is supposed to be: dangerous work, honestly done, with a happy return. That this image exists in the novel at all makes the Pequod's fate the more terrible by comparison.

## Symbolic Significance

The Bachelor serves as the Pequod's narrative foil. One ship is full and heading home; the other is empty-handed and heading deeper into the Pacific. One crew celebrates; the other trembles.

The Bachelor's name is also meaningful. A bachelor is an unmarried man — free, unattached, independent. The ship embodies a freedom the Pequod's crew has surrendered to Ahab's obsession.

The encounter with the Bachelor is followed immediately by the killing of whales and the death of the Pequod's first mate in the whale hunt. Joy is always adjacent to death in Melville's Pacific.
