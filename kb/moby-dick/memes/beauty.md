# Beauty

## Definition

In *Moby-Dick*, beauty is never merely decorative. It is a property of the natural world that simultaneously attracts and annihilates, a quality that refines terror into something aesthetically overwhelming. Melville's narrator insists that "whiteness refiningly enhances beauty" [042-003], locating beauty not in the absence of danger but in its transfiguration. The whale's body is described as the site where "the lines of beauty are more exquisitely defined than in any other living thing" [086-002], yet this same body is a killing machine. The ocean offers "tranquil beauty and brilliancy" [114-002] that masks its fathomless violence. Melville's concept of beauty is thus thoroughly Romantic: beauty is not the opposite of the terrible but its most seductive guise. To behold the beautiful in *Moby-Dick* is to be drawn toward what may destroy you.

## Manifestations in the Novel

Beauty appears in the novel primarily through three channels: the whale, the sea, and the aesthetic qualities of whiteness. The whale's form — its "sculptural" body, its "mild and gentle" breaching motion, its "pyramidical silence" — is described with the language of fine art, as though the sperm whale were a mobile sculpture gallery. Yet Melville repeatedly insists that "the devilish brilliance and beauty of many of its most remorseless tribes" [058-011] makes beauty ethically ambiguous: the shark is beautiful, the storm is beautiful, the whale's death-flurry is beautiful. The sea's beauty — the "tranquil beauty and brilliancy of the ocean's skin" [114-002] — is the novel's primary instance of surface beauty concealing depth. And whiteness itself, the novel's central chromatic meditation, "refiningly enhances beauty, as if imparting some special virtue of its own" [042-003], linking aesthetic value to the very quality that makes the White Whale so terrifying. Even in the novel's human register, beauty appears: the Town-Ho's story speaks of "a simple old soul — Rad, and a beauty too" [054-014], reminding us that human beauty participates in the same aesthetic economy.

## Key Passages

- [042-003] — "Whiteness refiningly enhances beauty, as if imparting some special virtue of its own" — the aesthetic claim at the heart of the novel's chromatic theology
- [054-014] — Radney described as "a simple old soul — Rad, and a beauty too" — human beauty amid the brutal world of whaling
- [054-086] — "The appalling beauty of the vast milky mass" — the whale's beauty as inseparable from terror, the "milky" whiteness that appalls even as it attracts
- [058-011] — "The devilish brilliance and beauty of many of its most remorseless tribes" — the moral ambiguity of natural beauty, the shark as beautiful predator
- [086-002] — "The lines of beauty are more exquisitely defined than in any other living thing" — the whale's body as aesthetic ideal, sculptural perfection
- [086-005] — "Real strength never impairs beauty or harmony, but it often bestows it" — beauty as the visible sign of power, form following force
- [114-002] — "The tranquil beauty and brilliancy of the ocean's skin" — the sea's surface beauty, the deceptive calm that conceals "the heartless immensity" beneath

## Scholarly Context

Melville's treatment of beauty participates in the Romantic revaluation of the aesthetic, particularly Burke's distinction between the beautiful and the sublime. For Burke, beauty is associated with smallness, smoothness, and delicacy — qualities that Melville both deploys and subverts by applying them to the whale, a creature of massive scale. The novel's insistence on the "appalling beauty" of destructive nature also anticipates the twentieth-century concept of the "terrible beauty" — Yeats's phrase for the Easter Rising, itself a borrowing from the Romantic tradition. More recent eco-critical readings have seen in Melville's whale-beauty an early instance of what would become the environmental sublime: the recognition that nature's beauty is inseparable from its indifference to human concerns.
