# Cutting-In

## Definition

Cutting-in is the first stage of processing a captured whale — the methodical stripping of the blubber from the carcass while it lies alongside the ship. Melville presents cutting-in as a wholesale industrial operation that transforms the ship into a floating slaughterhouse: "the ivory Pequod was turned into what seemed a shamble; every sailor a butcher. You would have thought we were offering up ten thousand red oxen to the sea gods" (067-001). The process employs a system of enormous block-and-tackle rigged to the masthead, a great blubber hook weighing some one hundred pounds, and long cutting spades wielded by the mates. The whale is rolled over and over in the water by the strain of the windlass while the blubber is stripped off in a continuous spiral — "as the blubber envelopes the whale precisely as the rind does an orange, so is it stripped off from the body precisely as an orange is sometimes stripped by spiralizing it" (067-002).

## Manifestations in the Novel

Cutting-in appears as both a practical necessity of the whaling trade and a scene of concentrated symbolic power. When the Pequod takes a whale, the cutting-in process consumes the entire ship's company: mates wield spades on the cutting stages suspended over the whale's body, harpooneers descend onto the carcass to insert the blubber hook, the crew heaves at the windlass in a "wild chorus," and nimble hands coil the long blanket-pieces in the blubber-room below decks. The entire ship strains and careens under the load: "every bolt in her starts like the nail-heads of an old house in frosty weather; she trembles, quivers, and nods her frighted mast-heads to the sky" (067-002).

The process is inseparable from the danger that surrounds it. Sharks, attracted by the blood seeping from the carcass, swarm "like bees in a beehive" (072-006), and the harpooneer working on the whale's submerged back — tethered by the monkey-rope — risks being crushed between the whale and the ship or attacked by the ravenous predators. The cutting-in is thus not merely industrial but sacrificial: it enacts the novel's recurring drama of human labor poised between mastery and vulnerability, production and destruction.

The chapter "Cutting In" (Ch. 67) opens with the striking temporal marker "It was a Saturday night, and such a Sabbath as followed!" — casting the butchery that follows as a profanation of sacred time, an irony that deepens when the "ex officio professors of Sabbath breaking" are identified as "all whalemen." The cutting-in thus participates in the novel's larger pattern of sacralizing whale-killing even as it demystifies the labor involved.

> "The prodigious blood-dripping mass sways to and fro as if let down from the sky, and every one present must take good heed to dodge it when it swings, else it may box his ears and pitch him headlong overboard."

> (072-001)
