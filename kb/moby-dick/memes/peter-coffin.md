# Peter Coffin

## Overview

Peter Coffin is the landlord of the Spouter Inn in New Bedford, where [[ishmael|Ishmael]] lodges on his first night ashore before his whaling voyage. He is a minor character who appears directly only in Chapters 2 and 3, but his name — with its suggestion of death and the sea — sets the novel's mordant tone.

Ishmael first encounters Coffin's name on the inn's swinging sign:

> "...and these words underneath — 'The Spouter Inn:—Peter Coffin.'" (002-007)

The conjunction of "Spouter" (whale spout) and "Coffin" is not lost on Ishmael: "Coffin?—Spouter?—Rather ominous in that particular connexion, thought I" (002-007). Yet he notes pragmatically that "it is a common name in Nantucket" — the Coffins and Maceys being among the island's old whaling families.

## Role in the Narrative

Peter Coffin's primary function is nominal — his name is a piece of Melville's dark comedy, joining the gallows-like Try Pots sign and the tombstone tablets in the Whaleman's Chapel as emblems of mortality that cluster around Ishmael's journey to the sea.

In Chapter 2, Ishmael sees the sign and enters. In Chapter 3, he meets the landlord directly:

> "Landlord, for God's sake, Peter Coffin!" shouted I. "Landlord! Watch! Coffin! Angels! save me!" (003-064)

This cry — Ishmael's response to the unsettling painting in the Spouter Inn's entry — is simultaneously comic and prophetic. Calling for "Coffin" to save him from the "angels" evokes the novel's deepest preoccupations: death, salvation, and the sea.

Coffin himself is a practical man. He explains the inn's overcrowding, arranges for Ishmael to share a bed with [[queequeg|Queequeg]], and — significantly — recommends his "cousin Hosea Hussey of the Try Pots" in Nantucket, thus setting Ishmael on his path.

## Character Traits

### The Ominous Name

Coffin's name is the most conspicuous example of Melville's onomastic play in the early chapters. It joins the "Coffin" warehouse mentioned in Chapter 7's chapel scene and anticipates Queequeg's coffin-canoe in the novel's final pages. The innkeeper himself is ordinary; his name is not.

### The Practical Host

Coffin is a businessman running a cheap whalemen's inn. He manages overcrowding by doubling up strangers, defuses Ishmael's alarm at sharing a bed with a "cannibal," and offers practical advice about onward travel to Nantucket. He is the first in a series of threshold figures — innkeepers, prophets, captains — who guide Ishmael toward the Pequod.

## Relationships

- **[[ishmael|Ishmael]]**: The narrator and guest. Coffin treats him with the brisk efficiency of a man accustomed to transients.
- **[[queequeg|Queequeg]]**: Already lodging at the Spouter Inn when Ishmael arrives. Coffin's matter-of-fact attitude toward his "cannibal" guest is notable for its lack of prejudice.
- **[[mrs-hussey|Mrs. Hussey]]**: Connected through Coffin's cousin Hosea Hussey, her husband. Coffin's recommendation sends Ishmael to the Try Pots.
- **[[father-mapple|Father Mapple]]**: Coffin presides over the secular threshold (the inn); Father Mapple over the sacred one (the chapel). Both prepare Ishmael for what lies ahead.
