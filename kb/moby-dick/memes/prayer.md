# Prayer

## Definition

In *Moby-Dick*, prayer appears as both a formal religious act and an existential gesture — a reaching toward the divine that the novel interrogates at every turn. Father Mapple's sermon on Jonah (Ch. 9) provides the novel's most extended treatment of prayer: Jonah, from the belly of the fish, "offered a prayer so deeply devout that he seemed kneeling and praying at the bottom of the sea" [009-003]. Mapple draws the lesson that prayer must come from authentic desperation, not from comfort: "observe his prayer, and learn a weighty lesson" [009-035]. Against this Christian model, the novel sets Queequeg's pagan devotions — his "evening prayers" before his little black idol Yojo [010-008] — and Ahab's blasphemous invocations, which invert prayer into defiance. The novel's closing words are themselves a form of prayer: Ahab's final cry — "is this the end of all my bursting prayers? all my life-long fidelities?" [135-052] — goes unanswered.

## Manifestations in the Novel

Prayer structures key moments throughout the narrative. Father Mapple's sermon establishes the paradigm: true prayer emerges from the depths, from shipwreck and despair, modeled on Jonah's "swift punishment, repentance, prayers, and finally the deliverance" [009-026]. Queequeg's ritual fasting and prayer with his idol Yojo [016-003] offers an alternative religious economy — pagan but sincere, challenging Christian assumptions about the proper form of devotion. Peleg sends Ishmael off with the admonition "Don't forget your prayers, either" [022-023], a casual piety that contrasts with the desperate prayers soon to come. The novel's most haunting prayer-image comes from the whale-pod: the mother whales, "saying their prayers, and warning each young Leviathan" [088-006], naturalizing prayer as an instinct of protective love. But the novel's final word on prayer is Ahab's — unanswered, "bursting" with fury and futility [135-052].

## Scholarly Context

Prayer in *Moby-Dick* has been interpreted as Melville's extended meditation on the efficacy of religious language. The novel systematically juxtaposes Christian and pagan forms of prayer, refusing to privilege either while suggesting that both are ultimately gestures into the void. Critics have noted the influence of Calvinist doctrines of predestination on the novel's theology of prayer: if fate is fixed, what can prayer accomplish? Ahab's unanswered prayers dramatize this theological problem, making the Pequod's catastrophe not merely a physical but a spiritual shipwreck.
