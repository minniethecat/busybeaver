# The Sperm Whale's Head

## Definition

"The Sperm Whale's Head — Contrasted View" (Chapter 74) is Melville's comparative anatomical study of the two great leviathans hanging on either side of the Pequod: the sperm whale's head on one side, the right whale's on the other. What begins as a voyage of practical cetology — examining eyes, ears, jaw, and mouth — becomes a philosophical investigation into perception, consciousness, and the dignity of form. The sperm whale's head emerges as the novel's figure for the Platonian spirit: speculative, noble, symmetrical.

## Manifestations in the Novel

The chapter opens with a pedagogical invitation: "Here, now, are two great whales, laying their heads together; let us join them, and lay together our own" (074-001). Ishmael positions the Pequod's deck as the world's best classroom for practical cetology.

The comparison immediately privileges the sperm whale. "There is a certain mathematical symmetry in the Sperm Whale's which the Right Whale's sadly lacks. There is more character in the Sperm Whale's head. As you behold it, you involuntarily yield the immense superiority to him, in point of pervading dignity" (074-003). The sperm whale's pepper-and-salt coloring signals "advanced age and large experience."

The examination proceeds through four anatomical features. The eyes are tiny — "a lashless eye, which you would fancy to be a young colt's eye" — and placed far back on the sides of the head, a position that creates a profound philosophical problem (074-004). Because the whale's eyes are separated by "many cubic feet of solid head," each eye sees a completely distinct picture. "The whale, therefore, must see one distinct picture on this side, and another distinct picture on that side; while all between must be profound darkness and nothingness to him" (074-006). Melville speculates that this divided vision explains the whale's "extraordinary vacillations of movement" and "helpless perplexity of volition" (074-008).

The ears are even smaller — a hole "you can hardly insert a quill" into (074-009). From this observation, Melville draws a moral aphorism: "Why then do you try to 'enlarge' your mind? Subtilize it" (074-010).

The mouth, viewed from within, is "a really beautiful and chaste-looking mouth! from floor to ceiling, lined, or rather papered with a glistening white membrane, glossy as bridal satins" (074-011). The lower jaw is a "portentous" structure — "the long narrow lid of an immense snuff-box" whose teeth form "a terrific portcullis" (074-012).

The chapter concludes in Chapter 75 with a final comparative judgment: the sperm whale is "a Platonian, who might have taken up Spinoza in his latter years," while the right whale is "a Stoic" (075-011).

## Scholarly Context

The comparative head chapters (74–75) have been recognized as a key instance of Melville's method of "cetological philosophy" — the practice of using whale anatomy as a scaffold for metaphysical inquiry. The sperm whale's divided vision has been analyzed as Melville's figure for the limits of human epistemology: we too see only partial pictures and mistake them for the whole. The final philosophical pairing — Platonian vs. Stoic — has been read as Melville's typological classification of two forms of human response to death: speculative transcendence (the sperm whale) versus practical resolution (the right whale). The entire comparative exercise has been seen as Melville's critique of the limits of empirical classification itself, anticipating themes developed in the later cetological chapters.
