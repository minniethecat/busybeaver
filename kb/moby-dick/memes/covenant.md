# Covenant

## Definition

Covenant in *Moby-Dick* appears at two registers: the biblical and the cetological. In the Cetology chapter, Melville quotes the Book of Job's challenge — "Will he (the leviathan) make a covenant with thee? Behold the hope of him is vain!" (032-010) — marking the whale as a creature beyond human contract, a being with whom no agreement can be struck. The leviathan's refusal of covenant is what makes the whale sublime: it stands outside the network of mutual obligation that binds human society. In the Grand Armada chapter, however, the word returns in a naturalistic register: sperm whales are described as having "sworn solemn league and covenant for mutual assistance and protection" (087-007). The same word Melville uses for the unattainable divine pact is also the word for the whales' own social organization — as if the whales, among themselves, achieve the covenant that humans cannot achieve with them.

## Manifestations in the Novel

**Job's leviathan and the human failure of covenant (Chapter 32)**. In the Cetology chapter, Melville confronts the impossibility of his own task — to classify and describe the whale — by quoting Job: "Will he (the leviathan) make a covenant with thee? Behold the hope of him is vain!" (032-010). The rhetorical question is self-directed: Melville, the would-be cetologist, is attempting precisely to "hook the nose of this leviathan," and the biblical warning is that the whale will not cooperate. Covenant implies mutual obligation and reciprocal knowledge; the whale offers neither. The entire Cetology chapter, with its elaborate but ultimately inadequate classification system, is an extended demonstration of the failure of covenant — the whale resists capture by human categories as surely as it resists capture by harpoon.

**The whales' solemn league and covenant (Chapter 87)**. In the Grand Armada chapter, the word reappears in a strikingly different key: the sperm whales, now hunted across all oceans, "had sworn solemn league and covenant for mutual assistance and protection" (087-007). The language is political — "league and covenant" echoes the Solemn League and Covenant of 1643, the Scottish-English alliance — and it attributes to whales a deliberative sociality. The whales, Melville suggests, have formed among themselves exactly the kind of binding agreement that Job insists the leviathan will never make with humans. The covenant exists; it is simply not with us.

## Key Passages

- (032-010) — "Will he (the leviathan) make a covenant with thee? Behold the hope of him is vain!": Melville's self-admonishment as cetologist, the whale's refusal of human contract
- (087-007) — "sworn solemn league and covenant for mutual assistance and protection": sperm whales' social pact, the covenant that excludes humanity
