# Queequeg in His Coffin

## Overview

Queequeg in His Coffin is one of the most symbolically freighted episodes in *Moby-Dick*. In Chapter 110, the harpooneer [[queequeg|Queequeg]] falls gravely ill with a fever contracted in the hold. Believing himself near death, he orders a canoe-coffin built after the custom of his native Kokovoko. He lies in it, tests its fit, and then — through an act of sheer will — decides not to die.

The coffin, which Queequeg repurposes as a sea-chest and carves with the tattoos that cover his body, becomes one of the novel's key symbolic objects. In Chapters 126–127, the Pequod's life-buoy is lost and the coffin is converted to replace it — the same coffin that, in the Epilogue, buoys Ishmael to survival as the Pequod sinks. The episode weaves together the novel's deepest themes: death and resurrection, fate and free will, the body as text, and the cannibal as Christ-figure.

## Chronology

### The Illness

Queequeg contracts a fever while working in the Pequod's hold, "stripped to his woollen drawers, the tattooed savage was crawling about amid that dampness and slime, like a green spotted lizard at the bottom of a well" (110-004). He wastes away rapidly. Ishmael watches his friend approach death:

> How he wasted and wasted away in those few long-lingering days, till there seemed but little left of him but his frame and tattooing. But as all else in him thinned, and his cheek-bones grew sharper, his eyes, nevertheless, seemed growing fuller and fuller. (110-004)

### The Coffin Request

Queequeg asks for a canoe-coffin, recalling the Nantucket custom of burying dead whalemen in dark-wood canoes — "for it was not unlike the custom of his own race, who, after embalming a dead warrior, stretched him out in his canoe, and so left him to be floated away to the starry archipelagoes" (110-005). He shudders at the thought of being "tossed like something vile to the death-devouring sharks" in a hammock burial (110-005).

### Building the Coffin

The ship's carpenter is commanded to build the coffin from "heathenish, coffin-coloured old lumber" cut from the "aboriginal groves of the Lackaday islands" (110-006). He takes Queequeg's measure "with great accuracy, regularly chalking Queequeg's person as he shifted the rule" (110-006).

When complete, the carpenter "lightly shouldered the coffin" — a darkly comic image — and carried it forward (110-009). The crew tries to drive it away, but Queequeg demands it be brought to him (110-010).

### Lying in the Coffin

Queequeg has his harpoon iron, a paddle, biscuits, fresh water, and a bag of earth placed in the coffin. He is lifted in. He calls for his little god, Yojo, crosses his arms over his chest, and asks for the lid to be closed. "Rarmai" (it will do; it is easy), he murmurs (110-011).

Pip hovers nearby, delivering a strange elegy: "Poor rover! will ye never have done with all this weary roving? where go ye now?" and asks Queequeg to seek out Pip in the Antilles if the currents carry him there (110-013).

### The Recovery

Having prepared for death, Queequeg suddenly rallies. He explains that he recalled "a little duty ashore, which he was leaving undone; and therefore had changed his mind about dying" (110-017). Ishmael reflects on "Queequeg's conceit, that if a man made up his mind to live, mere sickness could not kill him: nothing but a whale, or a gale, or some violent, ungovernable, unintelligent destroyer of that sort" (110-017).

> "there is this noteworthy difference between savage and civilized; that while a sick, civilized man may be six months convalescing, generally speaking, a sick savage is almost half-well again in a day." (110-018)

### The Coffin Transformed

Queequeg repurposes his coffin as a sea-chest, spending "many spare hours" carving the lid with "all manner of grotesque figures and drawings" — copying in his "rude way" the tattoos on his body, which themselves contain "a complete theory of the heavens and the earth, and a mystical treatise on the art of attaining truth" (110-019).

In Chapter 126, the Pequod's life-buoy is lost. Starbuck suggests the coffin as replacement: "A life-buoy of a coffin!" (126-009). In Chapter 127, the carpenter converts it. And in the Epilogue, it is this coffin-life-buoy that rises from the vortex and saves Ishmael — "the coffin life-buoy shot lengthwise from the sea, fell over, and floated by my side" (Epi-001).

## Narrative Significance

The episode is the novel's most explicit meditation on death and the will to live. Queequeg's "conceit" — that sickness cannot kill a man who has decided to live — inverts the novel's prevailing fatalism. Where Ahab is driven by forces he cannot control, Queequeg exercises sovereignty over his own death.

The coffin-to-life-buoy transformation is one of Melville's most elegant structural symmetries. What Queequeg builds for death becomes Ishmael's means of life. The "savage" who was introduced in Chapter 3 as a terrifying cannibal becomes, by the novel's end, the unwitting instrument of the narrator's salvation. The coffin, carved with Queequeg's tattoos — a "mystical treatise on the art of attaining truth" that nobody can read — floats Ishmael to safety as the sole surviving text of the Pequod's story.

## Critical Analysis

The episode resonates with multiple symbolic registers. Queequeg in his coffin is a Christ-figure — dead and risen — but a deeply heterodox one, surrounded by pagan ritual objects (Yojo, the harpoon, tattoos). His resurrection is not divine intervention but an act of will, which aligns the episode with Melville's recurring interest in the relationship between mind, body, and fate.

The coffin's conversion into a life-buoy in Chapter 126 literalizes the novel's central paradox: the thing meant for death becomes the means of life. This pattern echoes throughout *Moby-Dick* — the whale line that kills also holds fast, the doubloon that inspires also mocks, the white whale that destroys also gives meaning.
