# The Squid

## Overview

The Squid (Chapter 59) is one of the most eerie and atmospheric episodes in *Moby-Dick*. On a "transparent blue morning" of preternatural stillness, [[daggoo|Daggoo]] sights a "great white mass" rising from the sea and cries out "The White Whale, the White Whale!" (059-003). The boats are lowered in pursuit — but what they find is not Moby Dick. It is a gigantic squid, "a vast pulpy mass, furlongs in length and breadth, of a glancing cream-colour," with "innumerable long arms radiating from its centre, and curling and twisting like a nest of anacondas" (059-006).

The crew, expecting the White Whale, encounters instead something far more alien — "no perceptible face or front did it have; no conceivable token of either sensation or instinct" (059-006). Starbuck's reaction is immediate and visceral: "Almost rather had I seen Moby Dick and fought him, than to have seen thee, thou white ghost!" (059-007). Ahab says nothing and silently returns to the ship.

The episode is a study in misrecognition and dread. The squid is the sperm whale's primary food — a creature of the abyss that whalemen almost never see. Its appearance is an omen, though of what, nobody can say with certainty.

## Chronology

### The Sighting

The Pequod sails through "meadows of brit" in serene weather. Daggoo, at the main-mast-head, spots a "strange spectre" in the distance: "a great white mass lazily rose, and rising higher and higher, and disentangling itself from the azure, at last gleamed before our prow like a snow-slide, new slid from the hills" (059-003). He cries out: "The White Whale, the White Whale!" (059-003).

### The Chase

Ahab, "bare-headed in the sultry sun," stands on the bowsprit and orders the boats lowered (059-004–005). Four boats pull toward the object, expecting the legendary White Whale.

### The Revelation

The white mass sinks and rises again. But this time, what appears is not a whale:

> "A vast pulpy mass, furlongs in length and breadth, of a glancing cream-colour, lay floating on the water, innumerable long arms radiating from its centre, and curling and twisting like a nest of anacondas, as if blindly to clutch at any hapless object within reach. No perceptible face or front did it have; no conceivable token of either sensation or instinct; but undulated there on the billows, an unearthly, formless, chance-like apparition of life." (059-006)

### The Aftermath

Starbuck's response is horror: "Almost rather had I seen Moby Dick and fought him, than to have seen thee, thou white ghost!" (059-007). Flask asks what it was; Starbuck answers: "The great live squid, which, they say, few whale-ships ever beheld, and returned to their ports to tell of it" (059-009). Ahab says nothing, turns his boat, and silently returns to the Pequod (059-010).

The chapter closes with a cetological note: the squid is believed to be the sperm whale's only food (059-011–012). The creature they have just seen is what Moby Dick eats. The omen is double-edged: the squid means whales may be near — but the white squid also mirrors the white whale, as if the prey foreshadows the predator.

## Narrative Significance

The Squid chapter is the novel's purest expression of the uncanny — the familiar made strange. The crew expects Moby Dick, the known terror. They encounter something far more alien: a creature without face or form, "an unearthly, formless, chance-like apparition of life" (059-006). The squid is not an antagonist; it is an absence where an antagonist should be.

The episode also establishes a crucial symbolic chain: white mass → Moby Dick → squid → formless life → the abyss. The white whale, when finally encountered, will carry this chain of associations with him. He is not merely a whale; he is the formlessness beneath the whale-shape, the abyss wearing a mask.

Starbuck's instinctive horror — preferring the known monster to the unknown — is the novel's epistemology in miniature. Moby Dick, for all his terror, is nameable. The squid is not. And what cannot be named cannot be hunted.

Ahab's silence is the chapter's most telling detail. The man who will speak at length to lightning, to the doubloon, to the whale itself — says nothing before the squid. Perhaps he recognizes in its formlessness a mirror of his own obsession: the white whale he pursues is, at bottom, as nameless and faceless as the creature before him.

## Aftermath / Consequences

The squid sighting contributes to the growing atmosphere of supernatural dread that surrounds Moby Dick. The whale's food is the abyss; the whale himself is the abyss made flesh. The episode also prepares the reader for the whale's first real appearance: when Moby Dick finally breaches, he will carry the squid's formlessness within his whiteness.
