# Schools and Schoolmasters

## The Social Whale

Chapter 88 of *Moby-Dick* — "Schools and Schoolmasters" — extends Ishmael's cetological system from anatomy into sociology. Where earlier chapters classified whales by their physical form (the Folio-Octavo-Duodecimo system of Chapter 32), this chapter classifies them by their social arrangements. The result is one of Melville's most sustained satires on human society, in which the social life of sperm whales becomes a mirror — sometimes comic, sometimes unsettling — of human customs around gender, power, and aging.

The chapter builds on the spectacle of the Grand Armada (Ch.87), the immense aggregation of whales whose "vast body" Ishmael has just described. But whereas the Grand Armada is a temporary and exceptional gathering, the "schools" that Ishmael now describes are the whale's permanent social units. "Now, though such great bodies are at times encountered, yet, as must have been seen, even at the present day, small detached bands are occasionally observed, embracing from twenty to fifty individuals each. Such bands are known as *schools*" (088-002).

## The Harem School

The primary social unit of sperm whales, as Melville describes it, is the harem — a polygynous arrangement in which a single mature male governs a band of females and their young. "In cavalier attendance upon the school of females, you invariably see a male of full grown magnitude, but not old; who, upon any alarm, evinces his gallantry by falling in the rear and covering the flight of his ladies" (088-003).

The language Melville uses to describe this arrangement is deliberately anachronistic and culturally freighted. The male whale is called "a luxurious Ottoman, swimming about over the watery world, surroundingly accompanied by all the solaces and endearments of the harem" (088-003). The Orientalist framing — "Ottoman," "harem," later "Bashaw" and "Grand Turk" — projects nineteenth-century Western fantasies of Eastern despotism onto the whale, simultaneously mocking human pretensions and using human categories to make cetacean behavior legible.

The physical disparity between the sexes is striking: "while he is always of the largest leviathanic proportions, the ladies, even at full growth, are not more than one-third of the bulk of an average-sized male. They are comparatively delicate, indeed; I dare say, not to exceed half a dozen yards round the waist" (088-003). The mock-gallant aside about the females' waistlines is pure Ishmael — a joke that winks at the reader while making a genuine natural-historical point about sexual dimorphism in sperm whales.

The harem lord is fiercely possessive. "Should any unwarrantably pert young Leviathan coming that way, presume to draw confidentially close to one of the ladies, with what prodigious fury the Bashaw assails him, and chases him away!" (088-005). But his possessiveness is ultimately futile: "do what the Bashaw will, he cannot keep the most notorious Lothario out of his bed; for, alas! all fish bed in common" (088-005). The joke, with its mock-tragic "alas!," undercuts the harem lord's authority at the very moment of describing it.

## The Life Cycle of the Schoolmaster

The schoolmaster whale's biography, as Ishmael sketches it, is a parable of male desire and its eventual exhaustion. The mature bull, "being a great traveller, he leaves his anonymous babies all over the world; every baby an exotic" (088-006). This cosmopolitan paternity — fathering offspring he never knows — is presented with ambivalence: part admiration for the whale's world-spanning virility, part recognition of its irresponsibility.

But the harem-phase of the whale's life is not permanent. "In good time, nevertheless, as the ardour of youth declines; as years and dumps increase; as reflection lends her solemn pauses; in short, as a general lassitude overtakes the sated Turk; then a love of ease and virtue supplants the love for maidens" (088-006). The aged bull "forswears, disbands the harem, and grown to an exemplary, sulky old soul, goes about all alone among the meridians and parallels saying his prayers, and warning each young Leviathan from his amorous errors" (088-006).

This transformation — from Ottoman despot to "exemplary, sulky old soul" — is one of Melville's most psychologically acute passages. The schoolmaster who once presided over a harem ends his life as a solitary moralist, "saying his prayers" and warning the young against the very life he lived. It is a pattern recognizable in human biography: the rake turned moralist, the sinner turned preacher, the man of appetite turned man of renunciation. Ishmael's dry comment — "it is therefore not in strict character, however admirably satirical, that after going to school himself, he should then go abroad inculcating not what he learned there, but the folly of it" (088-007) — catches the irony perfectly.

> The same secludedness and isolation to which the schoolmaster whale betakes himself in his advancing years, is true of all aged Sperm Whales. Almost universally, a lone whale — as a solitary Leviathan is called — proves an ancient one. Like venerable moss-bearded Daniel Boone, he will have no one near him but Nature herself; and her he takes to wife in the wilderness of waters.
> (088-008)

## The Bachelor Schools

In deliberate contrast to the harem schools, Melville describes the "schools composing none but young and vigorous males" (088-009), known to whalemen as forty-barrel-bulls. These bachelor bands are the rowdy counterparts to the domestic harems: "Like a mob of young collegians, they are full of fight, fun, and wickedness, tumbling round the world at such a reckless, rollicking rate, that no prudent underwriter would insure them any more than he would a riotous lad at Yale or Harvard" (088-010). The comparison to college students — specifically those at Yale and Harvard, the elite American institutions — maps cetacean behavior onto human social class, making the young bull whales recognizably analogous to privileged young men sowing their wild oats before settling down.

The bachelor schools are the most dangerous whales to hunt. "For while those female whales are characteristically timid, the young males, or forty-barrel-bulls, as they call them, are by far the most pugnacious of all Leviathans, and proverbially the most dangerous to encounter" (088-009). This pugnacity is both a practical hazard for whalemen and a narrative resource for Melville, who uses the bachelor bulls' aggressiveness to vary the emotional register of the whaling chapters.

A crucial difference between the male and female schools concerns loyalty under attack. "Say you strike a Forty-barrel-bull — poor devil! all his comrades quit him. But strike a member of the harem school, and her companions swim around her with every token of concern, sometimes lingering so near her and so long, as themselves to fall a prey" (088-011). The female whales' solidarity — their willingness to risk their own lives for a wounded companion — stands in pointed contrast to the male whales' abandonment of their stricken comrades. This is the chapter's final, quietly devastating observation: the harem, for all its satirical treatment as an Oriental despotism, is also a community of mutual care, while the bachelor band, for all its "fun and wickedness," dissolves instantly under threat.
