# Death

## Overview

Death is everywhere in *Moby-Dick*. It is the whaleman's constant companion, the subtext of every lowering, the end that awaits all aboard the [[pequod|Pequod]].

Ishmael opens the novel by confessing to suicidal thoughts. He goes to sea as "a substitute for pistol and ball" (001-001).

The novel never lets the reader forget that whaling is a lethal trade. Men are crushed, drowned, dragged under, and lost at sea with terrifying regularity.

> "Yes, there is death in this business of whaling — a speechlessly quick chaotic bundling of a man into Eternity." (007-010)

## Death in the Whaling Life

The whaling ship is a floating community constantly exposed to death.

Men fall from boats. Whales crush them. Lines snap and whip. Sharks circle. The sea offers no second chances.

Chapter 7, "The Chapel," is a sustained meditation on this theme. Ishmael visits the Whaleman's Chapel in New Bedford and reads the marble tablets memorializing men "lost overboard" or "killed by a whale."

> "But Faith, like a jackal, feeds among the tombs, and even from these dead doubts she gathers her most vital hope." (007-011)

Death in the whale fishery is not exceptional — it is statistical. Melville catalogs disasters as an actuary might.

## Ahab and Death

[[ahab|Ahab]]'s relationship to death is the novel's dark heart.

His lost leg is a death-in-life. The [[the-white-whale|White Whale]] has not killed him, but it has maimed him beyond repair.

Ahab embraces death as a cost worth paying. He binds the crew to his purpose with a rhetoric that treats death as a trifle:

> "Death to Moby Dick! God hunt us all, if we do not hunt Moby Dick to his death!" (036-051)

Yet Ahab's defiance is doubled by his knowledge of mortality. His speeches are full of death-longing. He is as much in love with death as he is with vengeance.

## Fedallah's Prophecy

[[fedallah|Fedallah]]'s prophecy foretells Ahab's death in riddles:

1. Ahab will see two hearses — one "not made by mortal hands" (the White Whale), one made of "American wood" (the Pequod).
2. Only hemp can kill him — the rope that drags him under.
3. Fedallah will die first — and Ahab will see him again.

Every element of the prophecy is fulfilled in the final chase. Death in *Moby-Dick* is not merely a fact — it is a plotted destiny.

## The White Whale as Death

[[the-white-whale|Moby Dick]] is the novel's primary symbol of death.

He is not death in a simple allegorical sense. He is death as unknowable force — "the ungraspable phantom of life" (001-004).

Ishmael devotes an entire chapter to the whale's whiteness, connecting it to "the king of terrors" — the pallor of the dead.

> "It cannot well be doubted, that the one visible quality in the aspect of the dead which most appals the gazer, is the marble pallor lingering there." (042-006)

The whale's whiteness is the whiteness of the corpse, the shroud, the skeleton.

## The Wreck of the Pequod

The novel's climax is a death scene on the grandest scale.

Moby Dick rams the Pequod. The ship sinks. Concentric circles seize the lone boat. All but one perish.

The sky-hawk, nailed to the mast by Tashtego, goes down with the ship — a final image of crucifixion and apotheosis.

> "Now small fowls flew screaming over the yet yawning gulf; a sullen white surf beat against its steep sides; then all collapsed, and the great shroud of the sea rolled on as it rolled five thousand years ago." (135-065)

Only [[ishmael|Ishmael]] survives — "and I only am escaped alone to tell thee" (Epilogue-001).

## Critical Readings

Death in *Moby-Dick* has been read through multiple lenses:

- **Freudian**: The death drive (Thanatos) as Ahab's true motivation — his hunt for the whale is a hunt for death.
- **Religious**: The novel as a theodicy — how can a good God permit death on this scale?
- **Ecological**: Whaling as industrialized death — the systematic killing of whales mirrors the crew's fate.

The novel's greatness lies partly in its refusal to resolve these readings. Death remains an "ungraspable phantom."
