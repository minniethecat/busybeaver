# Ambergris

## Definition

Ambergris is a waxy, highly fragrant substance formed in the digestive system of sperm whales, long prized in perfumery and luxury goods. In *Moby-Dick*, Melville devotes significant attention to ambergris as both a commercial product of the whaling industry and a philosophical symbol. "Now this ambergris is a very curious substance," Ishmael observes, "and so important as an article of commerce, that in 1791 a certain Nantucket-born Captain Coffin was examined at the bar of the English House of Commons on that subject" (092-001). The substance's mysterious origin — long unknown to science — and its paradoxical nature (precious fragrance emerging from the "inglorious bowels of a sick whale," 092-002) make it a perfect vehicle for Melville's meditation on the relationship between corruption and value, the visible and the hidden, the material and the spiritual.

## Manifestations in the Novel

Ambergris enters the narrative most prominently in Chapters 91 and 92, in what Ishmael calls "the ambergris affair" (093-004). The Pequod encounters a French whaler, the *Bouton de Rose* (Rose-Bud), which has two whales lashed alongside — whales that the French crew, inexperienced in whaling, believe to be worthless "blasted" carcasses. Stubb, with characteristic cunning, recognizes that one of the whales likely contains ambergris and tricks the French captain into abandoning the whales. His scheme succeeds: the whale yields "a matter like ripe mozzarella" that Stubb triumphantly declares to be ambergris, "worth a gold guinea an ounce to any druggist" (091-050).

The episode is a dark comedy of commerce and deception. Stubb's exploitation of the French captain's ignorance mirrors the broader patterns of whaling economics — value is hidden, knowledge is power, and profit often depends on seeing what others miss. The "ambergris affair" also connects to the novel's running exploration of what constitutes true worth: the Frenchman saw only rotting carcasses, but Stubb saw gold.

In Chapter 92, Ishmael turns from narrative to scientific and philosophical reflection. He provides a detailed natural history of ambergris — its distinction from amber, its commercial uses (perfumery, cooking, even carried to Mecca "for the same purpose that frankincense is carried to St. Peter's in Rome," 092-001), and its mysterious etiology. The chapter climaxes in a rhetorical question: "Now that the incorruption of this most fragrant ambergris should be found in the heart of such decay; is this nothing?" (092-004). Ishmael invokes St. Paul on "corruption and incorruption" (092-004) to elevate the ambergris from whaling trivia to theological symbol — a figure for the possibility of redemption, of preciousness emerging from the most unlikely source.

## Scholarly Context

The ambergris chapters have attracted critical attention as examples of Melville's encyclopedic ambition and his ability to move from empirical natural history to metaphysical speculation. The commercial plot (Stubb's trickery) and the scientific digression (Chapter 92) together enact one of the novel's central patterns: material commerce opens onto spiritual inquiry. The ambergris that comes from "the inglorious bowels of a sick whale" becomes, in Ishmael's hands, a figure for the entire whaling enterprise — a dirty, bloody business that somehow yields things of transcendent value.

Critics have also noted the ambergris episode's engagement with nineteenth-century commodity culture. The list of ambergris's uses — "perfumery, pastiles, precious candles, hair-powders, and pomatum" (092-001) — maps the global luxury trade that whaling sustained. The ambergris that Stubb steals from the French and that ends up flavoring claret or scenting a lady's dressing room is a concrete link between the Pequod's bloody decks and the drawing rooms of Europe and America.
