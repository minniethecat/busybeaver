# Resurrection

## Definition

Resurrection in *Moby-Dick* operates across a tonal spectrum unique to Melville's novel: from the comic and bodily to the theological and tragic. The motif first appears in the intimate comedy of "The Counterpane" (Ch. 4), where Ishmael, having survived the night pressed against Queequeg's tattooed arm, declares that no one could "hope for a resurrection" (004-003) after such a sixteen-hour immersion in bed. From this domestic parody, the resurrection motif expands to encompass the crew's rallying cry, the theological question of Lazarus, the carpenter's grim humor about death, and finally Ishmael's own literal survival as the novel's resurrected witness — the one who "only am escaped alone to tell thee."

## Manifestations in the Novel

The first and most unexpected appearance of resurrection is comic. In "The Counterpane," Ishmael wakes from his marriage-like bed-sharing with Queequeg and feels he has undergone a kind of death and rebirth in sleep: sixteen hours in bed, beyond any "hope for a resurrection" (004-003). The language is deliberately inflated, treating a good night's sleep as a mock-Resurrection, yet this comic moment establishes the motif that will darken as the novel progresses.

In "Midnight, Forecastle" (Ch. 40), the crew's chant takes on a revolutionary, almost blasphemous energy: "Tell 'em it's the resurrection; they must kiss their last, and come to judgment" (040-020). Here resurrection becomes a sailors' battle cry, fusing the Christian Last Judgment with the whalemen's imminent mortal combat. The crew appropriates resurrection not as passive hope but as active threat — the rising up of the marginalized against their fate.

The theological dimension is most explicit in the carpenter's scene (Ch. 108). As the carpenter works on Queequeg's coffin-lifebuoy, he jokes darkly: "let's finish it before the resurrection fellow comes a-calling with his horn" (108-036). The "resurrection fellow" is a grimly comic personification of the Last Judgment, and the carpenter's haste to finish the coffin before that trumpet sounds transforms resurrection into a deadline — both sacred and absurd.

The novel's most emotionally charged resurrection reference comes in "The Pequod Meets the Rachel" (Ch. 131). Captain Gardiner of the Rachel, searching for his lost son, pleads with Ahab with "uplifted hands — 'may the resurrection and the life'" (131-008). Gardiner's biblical language (John 11:25) invokes resurrection not as doctrine but as desperate hope — the only thing left to a father who has lost his child to the sea. Ahab's refusal to help makes this the novel's darkest anti-resurrection moment.

Ishmael's own survival — buoyed by Queequeg's coffin, picked up by the Rachel — constitutes the novel's literal resurrection. He is the Lazarus figure who "lived after his resurrection" (049-008), a "supplementary clean gain" of the tragedy. The epilogue's famous opening — "And I only am escaped alone to tell thee" (Job 1:15) — reframes the entire narrative as resurrection testimony.

## Key Passages

- (004-003) — "before I could hope for a resurrection. Sixteen hours in bed!": comic-bodily resurrection in the Spouter-Inn
- (007-007) — "draw upon all Faith, and refuse resurrections to the beings who have placelessly perished": the theological problem of the unburied dead at sea
- (040-020) — "Tell 'em it's the resurrection; they must kiss their last, and come to judgment": the crew's revolutionary battle cry
- (049-008) — "that Lazarus lived after his resurrection; a supplementary clean gain": the theological framing of survival
- (108-036) — "let's finish it before the resurrection fellow comes a-calling with his horn": the carpenter's grim humor
- (131-008) — "with uplifted hands — 'may the resurrection and the life'": Captain Gardiner's desperate plea
