# The Tail

## "I Celebrate a Tail"

Chapter 86 of *Moby-Dick* opens with a declaration of purpose that is at once mock-heroic and genuinely earnest: "Other poets have warbled the praises of the soft eye of the antelope, and the lovely plumage of the bird that never alights; less celestial, I celebrate a tail" (086-001). Ishmael positions himself as a poet of the unpoetic, a celebrant of the grossly physical rather than the conventionally beautiful. But the chapter that follows demonstrates that the tail is anything but a merely anatomical subject — it is, for Melville, an occasion for meditating on the relationship between power and grace, between the knowable surface of things and the unfathomable depths beneath.

The tail is the whale's most visible and symbolically richest organ. It is the part that whalemen see most often — rising from the water before a deep dive, striking boats in combat, propelling the creature through the sea with "infantileness of ease" that "undulates through a Titanism of power" (086-005). To "celebrate a tail" is thus to celebrate the whale as it most dramatically presents itself to human perception.

## Anatomy and Structure

Ishmael's description of the tail's anatomy is characteristically precise. "Reckoning the largest sized Sperm Whale's tail to begin at that point of the trunk where it tapers to about the girth of a man, it comprises upon its upper surface alone, an area of at least fifty square feet" (086-002). At its fullest expansion, "the tail will considerably exceed twenty feet across" (086-002), making it one of the largest single organic structures in the animal kingdom.

The tail's internal structure reveals a tripartite organization that Ishmael compares, in one of his characteristic analogical leaps, to Roman masonry. "The entire member seems a dense webbed bed of welded sinews; but cut into it, and you find that three distinct strata compose it: — upper, middle, and lower" (086-003). The middle layer, with its crosswise fibers sandwiched between horizontal outer layers, "will furnish a curious parallel to the thin course of tiles always alternating with the stone in those wonderful relics of the antique, and which undoubtedly contribute so much to the great strength of the masonry" (086-003). This architectural analogy elevates the whale's anatomy to the status of a built structure — nature's engineering, as refined as anything human civilization has produced.

The tail is not a self-contained organ but the convergence point of the whale's entire musculature. "The whole bulk of the leviathan is knit over with a warp and woof of muscular fibres and filaments, which passing on either side the loins and running down into the flukes, insensibly blend with them, and largely contribute to their might; so that in the tail the confluent measureless force of the whole whale seems concentrated to a point" (086-004). The image of "confluent measureless force" — all the whale's power flowing together and focused at a single point — makes the tail not merely a body part but the physical expression of the whale's total being. "Could annihilation occur to matter," Ishmael adds with genuine awe, "this were the thing to do it" (086-004).

## The Five Great Motions

The taxonomic heart of the chapter is Ishmael's enumeration of the tail's five distinct motions, each with its own purpose and character:

**First: Propulsion.** "Being horizontal in its position, the Leviathan's tail acts in a different manner from the tails of all other sea creatures. It never wriggles. In man or fish, wriggling is a sign of inferiority" (086-008). The whale's propulsion is dignified, not serpentine — a scroll-wise coiling and springing that produces "that singular darting, leaping motion" (086-008) unique to the sperm whale.

**Second: The Mace.** In combat with humans, the whale "chiefly and contemptuously uses his tail" (086-009). The blow is delivered by recoil — the whale curves its flukes away and the strike comes from the return. In open air, "the stroke is then simply irresistible. No ribs of man or boat can withstand it" (086-009). The adverb "contemptuously" is crucial: the whale does not consider man worthy of its head, reserving that weapon for other whales.

**Third: Sweeping — the Sense of Touch.** Ishmael proposes that "in the whale the sense of touch is concentrated in the tail; for in this respect there is a delicacy in it only equalled by the daintiness of the elephant's trunk" (086-010). This sweeping motion, performed "in maidenly gentleness" with "a certain soft slowness" (086-010), is the whale's most sensitive interaction with the world. The comparison to the elephant's trunk — a recurring motif in the chapter — establishes a kinship between the two largest land and sea creatures, each possessing an organ of extraordinary delicacy housed within overwhelming power.

**Fourth: Lobtailing — Play.** "Stealing unawares upon the whale in the fancied security of the middle of solitary seas, you find him unbent from the vast corpulence of his dignity, and kitten-like, he plays on the ocean as if it were a hearth" (086-011). The image of the "kitten-like" whale, its tail smiting the surface in play, producing a "thunderous concussion" that "resounds for miles" (086-011), fuses the domestic and the sublime. The whale at play is still the whale of terrifying power, but that power is momentarily bent to joy rather than destruction.

**Fifth: Peaking Flukes — the Grandest Sight.** "Excepting the sublime *breach* — somewhere else to be described — this peaking of the whale's flukes is perhaps the grandest sight to be seen in all animated nature" (086-012). As the whale prepares to dive, its flukes rise "with at least thirty feet of his body" into the air, vibrating for a moment before shooting downward. Ishmael's description of this motion becomes explicitly visionary: "Out of the bottomless profundities the gigantic tail seems spasmodically snatching at the highest heaven. So in dreams, have I seen majestic Satan thrusting forth his tormented colossal claw from the flame Baltic of Hell" (086-012). But he immediately qualifies this infernal image with a celestial alternative — "if in the Dantean, the devils will occur to you; if in that of Isaiah, the archangels" (086-012) — insisting that what you see in the whale's tail reflects your own spiritual condition.

> Standing at the mast-head of my ship during a sunrise that crimsoned sky and sea, I once saw a large herd of whales in the east, all heading towards the sun, and for a moment vibrating in concert with peaked flukes. As it seemed to me at the time, such a grand embodiment of adoration of the gods was never beheld, even in Persia, the home of the fire worshippers.
> (086-012)

## Strength, Beauty, and the Unknowable

Midway through the chapter, Melville articulates a philosophical principle that governs the entire book: "Real strength never impairs beauty or harmony, but it often bestows it; and in everything imposingly beautiful, strength has much to do with the magic" (086-005). This is the aesthetic creed of *Moby-Dick* itself — a book that finds beauty not in delicacy or refinement but in magnitude, force, and the terror of the sublime. The tail is the perfect embodiment of this principle: an organ of annihilation that is also, in its crescentic borders and graceful flexions, a thing of "exceeding grace" in which "no fairy's arm can transcend it" (086-006).

Yet for all his exhaustive description, Ishmael ends the chapter with an admission of defeat: "Dissect him how I may, then, I but go skin deep; I know him not, and never will. But if I know not even the tail of this whale, how understand his head? much more, how comprehend his face, when face he has none?" (086-015). The tail, the most visible and accessible part of the whale, remains finally impenetrable. "Thou shalt see my back parts, my tail, he seems to say, but my face shall not be seen" (086-015). The biblical echo — God's words to Moses in Exodus — transforms the whale from a zoological specimen into a theophany, a revelation that conceals even as it discloses.

This is the deepest point of Ishmael's celebration: the tail is magnificent precisely because it finally resists understanding. Its "mystic gestures" (086-015), which some hunters have compared to "Free-Mason signs and symbols" (086-015), suggest a form of communication and intelligence that human categories cannot capture. To celebrate the tail is to celebrate the limits of human knowledge — to find, in the failure of comprehension, an occasion for wonder rather than frustration.
