# A Squeeze of the Hand

## Definition

"A Squeeze of the Hand" is the title and central image of Chapter 94 of *Moby-Dick*, in which Ishmael describes the process of squeezing congealed lumps of spermaceti — the waxy substance harvested from the head of the sperm whale — back into liquid form. As Ishmael and his shipmates sit around a tub, their hands immersed in the softening matter, the repetitive, tactile labor produces an altered state of consciousness. The boundary between work and sensuality dissolves; the squeezing becomes a vehicle for an experience of collective intimacy so intense that Ishmael confesses to squeezing his fellow sailors' hands by accident — or not quite by accident — "in an affectionate manner" (094-006). The scene is among the most arresting in the novel and has become one of its most discussed passages in modern criticism.

## Manifestations in the Novel

Chapter 94 opens with a detailed description of the whale's "case" — the reservoir of pure spermaceti in the sperm whale's head — and the process of bailing it out and reducing it. Melville transitions from this technical account into the squeezing episode with characteristic fluidity, moving from cetological description to metaphysical meditation in the space of a paragraph.

The squeezing itself is presented as a collective, rhythmic labor. "It was our business to squeeze these lumps back into fluid" (094-003), Ishmael explains, and the word "our" is essential: this is not solitary work but a communal undertaking, performed in a circle. As the morning wears on, the spermaceti — "a sweet and unctuous mass" — begins to have its effects. The substance itself seems to transmit a quality of peace: "a strange sort of insanity came over me; and I found myself unwittingly squeezing my co-laborers' hands in our tub" (094-005).

What follows is a passage of extraordinary intimacy. Ishmael describes looking into the eyes of his fellow sailors "as feelingly as if they were my brothers" and declares: "I am ready to squeeze case eternally. In thoughts of the visions of the night, I saw long rows of angels in paradise, each with his hands in a jar of spermaceti" (094-006). The scene marries the bodily and the visionary, the industrial and the paradisal, in a way that is uniquely Melvillean.

The chapter's title — "A Squeeze of the Hand" — is a pun that never quite resolves into a joke. The hand-squeezing is both the literal labor of processing whale oil and an image of human connection that the labor unexpectedly enables. Melville does not press the point; he lets the double meaning hover, allowing the reader to decide how much to read into Ishmael's confession of unwitting — or half-witting — affection.

## Key Passages

> "It was our business to squeeze these lumps back into fluid. A sweet and unctuous mass! Full of such celestial qualities, that the mere handling of it, sent a magnetic virtue through the palms of the hands, and so into the very soul."
> (094-003)

> "Squeeze! squeeze! squeeze! all the morning long; I squeezed that sperm till I myself almost melted into it; I squeezed that sperm till a strange sort of insanity came over me; and I found myself unwittingly squeezing my co-laborers' hands in our tub, and looking up into their eyes as feelingly as if they were my brothers."
> (094-005)

> "Would that I could keep squeezing that sperm for ever! For now, while all my other senses were entranced, I seemed to hear a voice crying out to me, that on no account must I ever let go of that thing called a hand. In thoughts of the visions of the night, I saw long rows of angels in paradise, each with his hands in a jar of spermaceti."
> (094-006)
