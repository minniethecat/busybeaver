# Queequeg

## Overview

Queequeg is the tattooed Polynesian harpooneer who becomes [[ishmael|Ishmael]]'s closest friend.

He first appears at the Spouter-Inn in [[nantucket|Nantucket]], where he and Ishmael share a bed. Their bond forms one of the novel's deepest relationships.

A native of the fictional island of Kokovoko, Queequeg is the son of a High Chief. He left his homeland to learn about Christendom.

Despite his "cannibal" appearance, Queequeg emerges as one of the noblest characters in the novel. His courage, dignity, and spiritual depth stand as a rebuke to Western assumptions.

## Role in the Narrative

Queequeg enters the story in Chapters 3–4, where Ishmael's initial terror of him melts into friendship.

Their bond is sealed in Chapter 10, where they share a pipe and Queequeg declares them "married" — a "hearts' honeymoon" of cross-cultural brotherhood.

Aboard the [[pequod|Pequod]], Queequeg serves as [[starbuck|Starbuck]]'s harpooneer. He is fearless in the whale-boats.

His most dramatic act comes in Chapter 78, when he dives into the sea to rescue Tashtego from a sinking whale's head. He delivers Tashtego by C-section from within the head.

Later, Queequeg falls deathly ill and orders a coffin made. When he recovers, the coffin becomes the Pequod's life-buoy. It is this coffin that ultimately saves Ishmael from the wreck.

## Character Traits

### Nobility and Dignity

Queequeg may be a "savage" by Nantucket standards. Yet he consistently behaves with more dignity than most Christians in the novel.

He refuses to use his superior strength unfairly. He is honest, calm, and utterly without pretense.

Ishmael observes: "I'll try a pagan friend, thought I, since Christian kindness has proved but hollow courtesy" (010-008).

### Spiritual Depth

Queequeg worships a small black idol named Yojo. His faith is private and sincere.

When he fasts and sits motionless with Yojo on his head, Ishmael respects the ritual rather than mocking it.

His spiritual life is integrated rather than performative.

### Self-Ownership

Queequeg's tattoos mark his body with the "complete theory of the heavens and the earth."

He is the only character who seems free of the metaphysical torment that grips [[ahab|Ahab]]. He lives fully in his body and his actions.

## Relationships

- **[[ishmael|Ishmael]]** (Bosom Friend): The novel's central friendship. Ishmael's embrace of Queequeg is the moral heart of the early chapters.

- **[[ahab|Ahab]]** (Captain): They rarely speak directly. Queequeg's coffin is the symbolic negation of Ahab's death-quest.

- **[[starbuck|Starbuck]]** (First Mate): Queequeg serves as Starbuck's harpooneer. A professional relationship built on mutual competence.

- **Tashtego** (Fellow Harpooneer): Queequeg risks his life to save Tashtego from a sinking whale's head.

- **Yojo** (His Idol): His small black god. Queequeg consults Yojo before major decisions.

## Critical Interpretation

Queequeg is Melville's most direct challenge to nineteenth-century racial ideology.

Scholars have read him as a noble-savage figure, a critique of missionary imperialism, and an embodiment of Melville's democratic vision. His tattooed body, which carries a "complete cosmology," literalizes the idea that non-Western cultures possess complete world-systems.

Critics like Geoffrey Sanborn have argued that Queequeg represents a fantasy of cross-racial intimacy. Others see him as an anti-type to Ahab: where Ahab seeks mastery over nature, Queequeg accepts his place within it.
