# Sharks

## Definition

In *Moby-Dick*, sharks are the ubiquitous scavengers that shadow whaling vessels and swarm around captured whale carcasses. They appear throughout the novel as emblems of nature's predatory indifference — organisms whose entire existence is organized around consumption and who reduce the grandeur of the whale to mere meat. Melville's sharks embody a vision of the natural world stripped of sentimental piety: they are "the invariable outriders of all slave ships crossing the Atlantic, systematically trotting alongside" (041-007), and in them the novel finds one of its most potent symbols for the rapacious, amoral character of the sea itself.

## Manifestations in the Novel

Sharks appear in *Moby-Dick* as a persistent background presence that erupts into the foreground at moments of death and dismemberment. Ishmael's first mention of them in the main narrative sets the tone: "the crunching teeth of sharks" attend the whale's corpse (029-003). When Ahab first reveals his purpose on the quarter-deck, he invokes the shark as an image of his own monomania: "Whelped somewhere by the sharkish sea. The white whale is their demigorgon" (038-004).

The novel's most extended treatment of sharks comes in Chapter 66, "The Shark Massacre." After Stubb has killed a sperm whale and the carcass is moored alongside the Pequod overnight, "incalculable hosts of sharks gather round the moored carcase" (066-002). Melville's description of the ensuing slaughter is one of the novel's most visceral passages. The crew attacks the sharks with whaling-spades, but the creatures are so single-minded in their feeding that they continue devouring the whale even as they themselves are being hacked apart: "they viciously snapped, not only at each other's disembowelments, but like flexible bows, bent round, and bit their own" (066-004).

This scene is not merely gory spectacle. It elaborates a vision of cosmic predation. The sharks are themselves prey — "till they are left living on with half a heart and half a liver" (066-004) — yet they never stop eating. Queequeg's theological response serves as the chapter's dark punchline: "de god wat made shark must be one dam Ingin" (066-005). The shark, in Queequeg's folk theology, is evidence that the creator of such a creature cannot be wholly benevolent.

Earlier, in Chapter 42 ("The Whiteness of the Whale"), Ishmael associates the white shark with the novel's central meditation on color and terror: "the white shark of the tropics; what but their smooth, flaky whiteness makes them the transcendent horrors they are?" (042-006). The shark's whiteness links it to the White Whale itself — a connection that makes the sharks' feeding on whale carcasses resonate with the novel's broader patterns of self-consumption and recursive violence.

## Key Passages

> "But sometimes, especially upon the Line in the Pacific, this plan will not answer at all; because such incalculable hosts of sharks gather round the moored carcase, that were he left so for six hours, say, on a stretch, little more than the skeleton would be visible by morning."
> (066-002)

> "They viciously snapped, not only at each other's disembowelments, but like flexible bows, bent round, and bit their own; till those entrails seemed swallowed over and over again by the same mouth, to be oppositely voided by the gaping wound."
> (066-004)

> "'Queequeg no care what god made him shark,' said the savage, agonizingly lifting his hand up and down; 'wedder Fejee god or Nantucket god; but de god wat made shark must be one dam Ingin.'"
> (066-005)

> "As for the white shark, the white gliding ghostliness of repose in that creature, when beheld in his ordinary moods, strangely tallies with the same quality in the Polar bear: both are the transcendent horrors of the deep and the frozen wastes."
> (042-006)
