# Portent

## Definition

A portent in Moby-Dick is an outward sign or inward foreboding that signals approaching calamity. Melville distinguishes between external portents — the "portentous" whale, the squid, the corposants, the sea-ravens — and internal "presentiments" felt by the characters. The two are linked by Ishmael's conviction that "outward portents and inward presentiments were his" (026-001), each feeding and confirming the other. The novel accumulates portents the way a tragedy accumulates omens: each new sign deepens the atmosphere of dread without decisively proving what it foretells. This ambiguity is the point — portents in Moby-Dick are never merely supernatural machinery; they are the mind's way of reading its own fear onto the world.

## Manifestations in the Novel

**The portentous whale (Chapter 1)**. From the novel's opening, the whale is framed as inherently portentous: "Such a portentous and mysterious monster roused all my curiosity" (001-014). Ishmael's word choice establishes that the whale's significance exceeds its physical bulk — it is a "portentous" creature, meaning both enormous and ominous.

**Outward portents and inward presentiments (Chapter 26)**. Ishmael's philosophical reflection on the "dignity" of the common sailor contains the novel's most explicit statement on portents: "Outward portents and inward presentiments were his" (026-001). The common sailor lives in a world thick with signs, his superstitiousness a form of attention to what the rational mind dismisses. This passage explains why the crew is so susceptible to Ahab's manipulation — they already read the world as a text of omens.

**Rumors and portents concerning Moby Dick (Chapter 41)**. As the Pequod enters the whale's territory, the atmosphere thickens with "rumors and portents concerning him" (041-008). The whale becomes a floating portent, every sighting or report adding to the legend. Melville notes that the crew is "overawed" by these portents, their fear making them more pliable to Ahab's will.

**The portentousness of whiteness (Chapter 42)**. In the chapter on "The Whiteness of the Whale," Melville argues that whiteness is "more strange and far more portentous" (042-030) than any other color. The albino's absence of color becomes a sign of absence itself — of meaning, of mercy, of God. Whiteness is the ultimate portent because it signifies without specifying: it is "the intensifying agent in things the most appalling to mankind."

**The squid as "thing of portents" (Chapter 59)**. The great white squid is the novel's most concentrated portent. Even Starbuck, the rationalist, is shaken: "the vast white headless phantom floats further and further from the ship, and every ripple seemed a portent" (059-011). Ishmael describes it as having "gone far to invest it with portentousness" — the squid becomes a sign precisely because it is so rarely seen, so alien, so inexplicable.

**Queequeg's interpretation (Chapter 61)**. Where Starbuck sees a portent of doom, Queequeg reads differently: "to Queequeg it was quite a different object" (061-001). This moment establishes that portents are interpretive, not inherent — the same sign can mean different things to different observers, depending on their cosmology and temperament.

**Evil portents before the final chase (Chapters 123–126)**. In the days before the three-day chase, portents multiply: "evil portents" (123-005), "shudderings and evil portents" (124-016), and the sea-ravens that peck at Ahab's hat (126-007). These final omens form a crescendo that even the most skeptical reader cannot dismiss — the novel's portent-logic reaches its fulfillment in the Pequod's sinking.

## Key Passages

- (001-014) — "Such a portentous and mysterious monster roused all my curiosity" — the whale as portent from the novel's opening.
- (026-001) — "Outward portents and inward presentiments were his" — the common sailor's world of signs.
- (041-008) — The crew "overawed by the rumors and portents concerning him" — Moby Dick as collective portent.
- (042-030) — Whiteness is "more strange and far more portentous" — the semiotics of absence.
- (059-011) — The squid: "so rarely is it beheld, that though one and all of the crew declared it to be a thing of portents" — the concentrated omen.
- (061-001) — "To Queequeg it was quite a different object" — the relativity of portent-reading.
- (123-005) — "The evil portents preceding it" — the final pre-chase omens begin.
- (124-016) — "Shudderings and evil portents" — the typhoon as portent-storm.
- (126-007) — The sea-ravens pecking Ahab's hat as "a portent" — the last omen before the chase.

## Scholarly Context

The portent in Moby-Dick has been analyzed primarily through two frameworks. The first is genre criticism: the novel borrows the machinery of classical and Shakespearean tragedy, where omens, ghosts, and supernatural signs foreshadow the catastrophe. The second is epistemological: portents in Melville are always mediated by interpretation — they signify, but what they signify is contested. This aligns the novel with a broader 19th-century anxiety about signs and their reliability, from Puritan typology to Emersonian Nature-as-symbol. Post-structuralist readers have emphasized the "portentousness" of whiteness as the novel's master trope of indeterminacy: a sign that points to nothing, or to everything, which amounts to the same thing.
