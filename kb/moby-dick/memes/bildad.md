# Captain Bildad

## Overview

Captain Bildad is a retired whaling captain and co-owner of the [[the-pequod|Pequod]], who, along with his old shipmate [[captain-peleg|Captain Peleg]], manages the fitting out and crewing of the vessel before its fateful voyage. A stern Nantucket Quaker who spent thirty years studying Scripture, Bildad embodies the novel's satirical treatment of religious hypocrisy—he quotes the Sermon on the Mount while offering [[ishmael]] a miserly 777th lay, and piously refuses to bear arms against men while having "illimitably invaded the Atlantic and Pacific" and spilled "tuns upon tuns of leviathan gore" (016-044).

## Role in the Narrative

Bildad serves as one of the two gatekeepers Ishmael must pass through to join the Pequod's crew. First encountered seated "bolt-upright" on the transom in the ship's cabin, absorbed in his ponderous Bible (016-046), he functions together with Peleg as the ship's land-based authority before [[captain-ahab|Ahab]] takes command at sea. His primary narrative role is comic and satirical: Melville uses him to expose the contradiction between Quaker pacifism and the bloody violence of the whaling industry. When [[queequeg]] seeks to sign on as harpooneer, Bildad demands proof of Christian conversion and presses a tract into the pagan's hands (018-022).

## Key Appearances

- [[chapter-016|Ch. 16: The Ship]] — Ishmael meets Bildad in the Pequod's cabin; the old Quaker offers him the infamous 777th lay (016-053, 016-061).
- [[chapter-018|Ch. 18: His Mark]] — Bildad interrogates Queequeg about his Christian credentials, labels him a "son of darkness," and narrowly dodges a thrown harpoon (018-004, 018-022).
- [[chapter-020|Ch. 20: All Astir]] — Bildad handles all provisioning; his sister Charity bustles aboard with supplies (020-001, 020-005).
- [[chapter-022|Ch. 22: Merry Christmas]] — Bildad serves as pilot, sings psalmody, and delivers an emotional, penny-pinching farewell address (022-023).

## Character Traits

Bildad is defined by the gulf between his ostentatious piety and his ruthless commercial instincts. Ishmael describes him as "an incorrigible old hunks" and "a bitter, hard task-master" whose crew in earlier days were "mostly all carried ashore to the hospital, sore exhausted and worn out" (016-045). Yet he never used to swear, extracting "an inordinate quantity of cruel, unmitigated hard work" through sheer intimidating presence—his "drab-coloured eye intently looking at you, made you feel completely nervous" (016-045). Physically lean and spare, he is "the exact embodiment of his utilitarian character" (016-045). Even his posture expresses parsimony—sitting "bolt-upright" and never leaning "to save his coat tails" (016-046). Melville's diagnosis: Bildad "had long since come to the sage and sensible conclusion that a man's religion is one thing, and this practical world quite another. This world pays dividends" (016-044).

## Relationships

**[[captain-peleg|Captain Peleg]]**: Bildad's old shipmate and co-owner. They are a comic double act: Peleg is profane and explosive while Bildad is sanctimonious and maddeningly calm. They bicker constantly yet their bond is deep; Bildad "convulsively grasped stout Peleg by the hand" at the Pequod's departure (022-020).

**[[ishmael]]**: Bildad sees Ishmael as labor to be acquired at minimum cost, sizing him up with a quiet "He'll do" (016-053).

**[[queequeg]]**: Bildad treats the tattooed harpooneer as a heathen conversion project, calling him "son of darkness" and "young Hittite" (018-011).

**Charity**: Bildad's sister, "a lean old lady of a most determined and indefatigable spirit" (020-005), who helps provision the Pequod.

## Notable Quotes

> "He'll do," said Bildad, eyeing me, and then went on spelling away at his book in a mumbling tone quite audible. (016-053)

> "Captain Peleg, thou hast a generous heart; but thou must consider the duty thou owest to the other owners of this ship—widows and orphans, many of them—and that if we too abundantly reward the labors of this young man, we may be taking the bread from those widows and those orphans. The seven hundred and seventy-seventh lay, Captain Peleg." (016-063)

> "Captain Peleg," said Bildad steadily, "thy conscience may be drawing ten inches of water, or ten fathoms, I can't tell; but as thou art still an impenitent man, Captain Peleg, I greatly fear lest thy conscience be but a leaky one; and will in the end sink thee foundering down to the fiery pit, Captain Peleg." (016-065)

> "Son of darkness, I must do my duty by thee; I am part owner of this ship, and feel concerned for the souls of all its crew; if thou still clingest to thy Pagan ways, which I sadly fear, I beseech thee, remain not for aye a Belial bondsman. Spurn the idol Bell, and the hideous dragon; steer clear of the fiery pit!" (018-022)

> "God bless ye, and have ye in His holy keeping, men. I hope ye'll have fine weather now, so that Captain Ahab may soon be moving among ye—a pleasant sun is all he needs. Be careful in the hunt, ye mates. Don't stave the boats needlessly, ye harpooneers; good white cedar plank is raised full three per cent. within the year. Don't forget your prayers, either. Good-bye, good-bye!" (022-023)
