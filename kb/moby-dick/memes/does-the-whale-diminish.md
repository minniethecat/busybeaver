# Does the Whale's Magnitude Diminish?

## Definition

"Does the Whale's Magnitude Diminish? Will He Perish?" is Chapter 105 of *Moby-Dick*, in which Ishmael addresses a question of urgent concern to the nineteenth-century whaling industry: whether the sperm whale population is declining under the pressure of industrial-scale hunting. Melville's treatment of this question is remarkably sophisticated, anticipating modern environmental discourse by weighing evidence from fossil records, classical authorities, contemporary statistics, and comparative ecology. His conclusion — that the whale is "immortal in his species, however perishable in his individuality" (105-015) — is both a scientific argument and a metaphysical assertion, affirming the whale's capacity to endure human predation while acknowledging the fragility of individual creatures.

## Manifestations in the Novel

The chapter opens by framing the whale in cosmic time: "Inasmuch, then, as this Leviathan comes floundering down upon us from the head-waters of the Eternities, it may be fitly inquired, whether, in the long course of his generations, he has not degenerated from the original bulk of his sires" (105-001). The question of diminution is layered: first physical (has the whale shrunk?), then demographic (are whales declining in number?), and finally metaphysical (can the whale survive the remorseless expansion of human industry?).

Melville deploys paleontological evidence to answer the first question. Fossil whales from the Tertiary system are *smaller* than modern specimens — "the whales of the present day superior in magnitude to those whose fossil remains are found in the Tertiary system" (105-002). The Alabama fossil whale "was less than seventy feet in length in the skeleton. Whereas, we have already seen, that the tape-measure gives seventy-two feet for the skeleton of a large sized modern whale" (105-003). Far from diminishing, the whale appears to have grown larger over geological time.

Against this empirical evidence, Melville sets the extravagant claims of classical and early modern authorities: "Pliny tells us of whales that embraced acres of living bulk, and Aldrovandus of others which measured eight hundred feet in length — Rope Walks and Thames Tunnels of Whales!" (105-005). Ishmael dismisses these with a whaleman's practical skepticism: "But will any whaleman believe these stories? No. The whale of to-day is as big as his ancestors in Pliny's time" (105-006). The argument is reinforced by a comparison to Egyptian mummies — if men and cattle have not shrunk since antiquity, why should whales alone have degenerated?

The chapter's most forward-looking argument addresses the extinction question directly. Comparing whales to the American buffalo — "which, not forty years ago, overspread by tens of thousands the prairies of Illinois and Missouri" (105-008) but were now nearly exterminated — Melville acknowledges the surface plausibility of the extinction thesis. But he counters with a crucial ecological insight: the whale's habitat is vastly larger, and the efficiency of whaling is vastly lower, than terrestrial hunting. "Forty men in one ship hunting the Sperm Whales for forty-eight months think they have done extremely well, and thank God, if at last they carry home the oil of forty fish" (105-009). By contrast, the same number of buffalo hunters would have slain "not forty, but forty thousand and more" (105-009). The whale's oceanic refuge — "a pasture to expatiate in, which is precisely twice as large as all Asia, both Americas, Europe and Africa, New Holland, and all the Isles of the sea combined" (105-013) — guarantees its survival.

The chapter also offers a prescient analysis of whale behavioral adaptation. Whales "influenced by some views to safety, now swim the seas in immense caravans" (105-010), aggregating into larger, more widely separated groups. The right whales have retreated to "their Polar citadels... and in a charmed circle of everlasting December, bid defiance to all pursuit from man" (105-011). Melville here anticipates modern observations of animal behavioral responses to hunting pressure.

The conclusion fuses science and metaphysics. The whale's longevity means "at any one period of time, several distinct adult generations must be contemporary" (105-014), making the species resilient against individual mortality. The final paragraph soars into myth: the whale "swam the seas before the continents broke water; he once swam over the site of the Tuileries, and Windsor Castle, and the Kremlin. In Noah's flood he despised Noah's Ark; and if ever the world is to be again flooded... then the eternal whale will still survive, and rearing upon the topmost crest of the equatorial flood, spout his frothed defiance to the skies" (105-015).

## Key Passages

> "Inasmuch, then, as this Leviathan comes floundering down upon us from the head-waters of the Eternities, it may be fitly inquired, whether, in the long course of his generations, he has not degenerated from the original bulk of his sires."
> (105-001)

> "Comparing the humped herds of whales with the humped herds of buffalo, which, not forty years ago, overspread by tens of thousands the prairies of Illinois and Missouri... in such a comparison an irresistible argument would seem furnished, to show that the hunted whale cannot now escape speedy extinction."
> (105-008)

> "Forty men in one ship hunting the Sperm Whales for forty-eight months think they have done extremely well, and thank God, if at last they carry home the oil of forty fish. Whereas... the same number of moccasined men, for the same number of months... would have slain not forty, but forty thousand and more buffaloes."
> (105-009)

> "Wherefore, for all these things, we account the whale immortal in his species, however perishable in his individuality. He swam the seas before the continents broke water; he once swam over the site of the Tuileries, and Windsor Castle, and the Kremlin."
> (105-015)

> "In Noah's flood he despised Noah's Ark; and if ever the world is to be again flooded, like the Netherlands, to kill off its rats, then the eternal whale will still survive, and rearing upon the topmost crest of the equatorial flood, spout his frothed defiance to the skies."
> (105-015)
