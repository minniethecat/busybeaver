# Orphan

## Definition

The orphan in *Moby-Dick* is not just a character without parents; it is the novel's governing figure for the human condition. Ishmael begins his narrative by mentioning "the tears of orphans" (002-011) and ends it by becoming "another orphan" (136-002). Between these poles, Melville deploys orphanhood as a theological, economic, and existential category. The ship-owners are "widows and orphans" (016-063) whose investment in whaling ties their bereavement to the global economy. Souls are "like those orphans whose unwedded mothers die in bearing them" (114-006) — the image of an orphan who never had parents, whose orphanhood is original rather than accidental. To be an orphan, in Melville's cosmology, is to be born into a world where no parent acknowledges you — and the question the novel asks is whether such a world is livable.

## Manifestations in the Novel

**The tears of orphans (Chapter 2)**. In the Spouter-Inn, Ishmael sees a painting "so thoroughly besmoked" that it is nearly illegible, and the landlord explains it depicts "the prospect of the whale" that "drank the tepid tears of orphans" (002-011). The phrase appears as a joke — a landlord's hyperbole to sell a painting — but it plants the orphan as a figure from the novel's first pages. The whale drinks orphan's tears: the predator consumes the most vulnerable, and the image establishes the whale as an agent of orphan-making before we even know what a whale is.

**Widows and orphans (Chapter 16)**. When Ishmael signs onto the Pequod, Captain Peleg explains that the ship's owners are "widows and orphans, many of them" (016-063), and that their investment makes the voyage a matter of moral obligation. The economic logic is perverse: whaling, which makes widows and orphans by killing sailors, is funded by widows and orphans who depend on its profits. The orphan is both the victim of whaling and its beneficiary, and this double position makes the orphan the novel's figure for the economic contradictions of the industry.

**Souls as orphans (Chapter 114)**. In the Gilder chapter, Ishmael delivers one of his most compressed metaphysical images: "Our souls are like those orphans whose unwedded mothers die in bearing them" (114-006). The simile is devastating: the soul is an orphan, its mother (Nature? God?) dies in the act of giving birth, and the orphan is left with no parent and no inheritance. This is orphanhood as original condition — the soul was always already orphaned, because whatever gave it birth did not survive the giving. The unwedded mother makes the orphan's illegitimacy constitutional: the soul has no legitimate parent, no recognized origin, no claim on the world it is born into.

**Another orphan (Epilogue)**. The novel's last word on its narrator is that Ishmael survives as "another orphan" (136-002). The word "another" is crucial — Ishmael joins a company of orphans that includes Ahab (whose mother died when he was a child), Pip (abandoned at sea), and every soul aboard the Pequod. To be "another orphan" is to take one's place in the novel's community of the bereft. But it is also to survive: Ishmael is the orphan who lives to tell the tale. The novel ends not with Ahab's death but with Ishmael's orphanhood — the condition of being left behind to speak.

## Key Passages

- (002-011) — "The tepid tears of orphans": the whale as orphan-maker, the novel's first mention of the orphan theme.
- (016-063) — "Widows and orphans, many of them": the ship's owners, the economic logic of bereavement.
- (112-005) — "A delicious grief, and her orphans a truly venerable, legendary beauty": the blacksmith's backstory, orphanhood as the origin of art.
- (114-006) — "Our souls are like those orphans whose unwedded mothers die in bearing them": the soul as original orphan, orphanhood as metaphysical condition.
- (136-002) — "Only found another orphan": Ishmael's survival, the novel's last word on its narrator.

## Scholarly Context

The orphan in *Moby-Dick* has been read through biographical, psychoanalytic, and political frames. Biographically, Melville's own father died when Herman was twelve, leaving the family in financial ruin — the orphan condition was personal. Psychoanalytic readings see the novel's proliferation of orphans as a working-through of Melville's paternal loss: Ahab, Ishmael, and Pip are all versions of the fatherless son. Political readings link the orphan to Melville's critique of American individualism: the orphan is the American self stripped of inheritance, tradition, and community — the self-made man's dark double, the self-unmade. The "orphans whose unwedded mothers die" passage has been read as Melville's most radical theological claim: that the soul has no divine parent, no transcendent origin, no cosmic legitimacy.
