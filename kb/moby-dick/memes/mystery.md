# Mystery

## Definition

Mystery in *Moby-Dick* is not a puzzle to be solved but the permanent structure of the world. From the minor mysteries of the Spouter-Inn — the landlord's "otherwise unaccountable mystery" (003-046), the "mystery" of Queequeg's behavior that Ishmael attempts "to explain" (004-003) — to the cosmic mystery of "what sweet mystery about this sea" (111-002), Melville constructs a universe in which the unknown is not a temporary deficiency of knowledge but its final condition. The novel's greatest mysteries — the whale, the spout, Fedallah's origin, Ahab's compulsion — are never resolved. They deepen with investigation. "When the mystery is probed, it turns out always" to be something else, something more (110-014). Melville's mystery is not an invitation to solve but a discipline: the reader, like the sailor, must learn to live with what cannot be known.

## Manifestations in the Novel

**Comic mysteries of the Spouter-Inn (Chapters 3–4)**. The novel's first mysteries are domestic and humorous. The landlord's behavior is "the otherwise unaccountable mystery" (003-046); Queequeg's strangeness prompts Ishmael's "attempts to explain the mystery" (004-003); and the chowder's excellence is a "mystery delightfully explained" (015-012) — solved by the revelation of the recipe. These early mysteries establish a pattern: mystery presents itself, explanation is attempted, and sometimes succeeds. But as the novel darkens, the mysteries resist explanation, and the pattern breaks.

**Fedallah: the muffled mystery (Chapter 50)**. Fedallah is introduced as a mystery that will never be solved. "Fedallah remained a muffled mystery to the last. Whence he came is a problem still" (050-008). Unlike the Spouter-Inn mysteries, Fedallah's origin, identity, and allegiance are permanently opaque. He is mystery incarnate: the Parsee whose prophecies come true but whose own nature is never disclosed. His mystery is not a gap in the narrative but a constitutive feature — Fedallah *is* the unknown, and the unknown is what drives the plot.

**The mystery of the spout (Chapter 85)**. In "The Fountain," Ishmael confesses the limits of cetological knowledge: "owing to the mystery of the spout — whether it be water or vapor — the wisest thing the investigator can do is to let the spout alone" (085-005). The spout is the whale's most visible feature, yet its composition cannot be settled. Mystery here is not esoteric but empirical — the thing right before our eyes that nevertheless resists comprehension. Melville's point is epistemological: mystery is not what is hidden but what is most conspicuous.

**Ahab's unspoken reason (Chapter 106)**. "Always had it remained a mystery to some, why it was, that for a long time Ahab kept his purpose secret" (106-004). The mystery of Ahab's motivation — why he waited to reveal his quest, why he chose this voyage, what private logic governed his obsession — is one the novel documents but never fully explains. This is psychological mystery, the opacity of another mind, which for Melville is as unfathomable as the ocean.

**Queequeg's coffin and the probed mystery (Chapter 110)**. When Queequeg's illness leads him to have a coffin built, the narrative remarks: "and that when the mystery is probed, it turns out always that it was something else, something more" (110-014). The coffin that was to contain Queequeg's body becomes his sea-chest, then his life-preserver, then Ishmael's salvation. Mystery here is transformative: the thing you think you understand becomes something else, and that something else becomes the key to survival. Probing mystery does not destroy it — it reveals that there is always more mystery beneath.

**The sweet mystery of the Pacific (Chapter 111)**. The novel's most lyrical treatment of mystery: "one knows not what sweet mystery about this sea, whose gently awful stirrings seem to speak of some hidden soul beneath" (111-002). The Pacific is not mysterious because it conceals something; it is mysterious because it *is* — because its "gently awful stirrings" suggest without revealing, promise without delivering. This is mystery as aesthetic experience, the beautiful opacity of the natural world.

## Key Passages

- (003-046) — "the otherwise unaccountable mystery": the landlord's behavior as the novel's first mystery, domestic and ultimately soluble
- (004-003) — "attempts to explain the mystery": Ishmael's early confidence that mystery can be reasoned through
- (015-012) — "the mystery was delightfully explained": the chowder, mystery solved — the pattern that will not hold
- (050-008) — "Fedallah remained a muffled mystery to the last. Whence he came is a problem still": the permanent mystery, Fedallah as the unknown made flesh
- (085-005) — "owing to the mystery of the spout — whether it be water or vapor": empirical mystery, the visible thing that resists comprehension
- (106-004) — "always had it remained a mystery to some, why it was, that for a long time Ahab kept his purpose secret": psychological mystery, the opacity of another mind
- (110-014) — "when the mystery is probed, it turns out always that it was something else, something more": the transformative mystery, probing reveals more mystery, not less
- (111-002) — "one knows not what sweet mystery about this sea, whose gently awful stirrings seem to speak of some hidden soul beneath": the Pacific as mystery's aesthetic form, beautiful and inexhaustible

## Scholarly Context

Melville's concept of mystery has been central to philosophical readings of the novel. Stanley Cavell reads *Moby-Dick* as an exercise in skepticism — the acknowledgment that the world exceeds our knowledge of it, and that this excess is not a failure but a feature of reality. Sharon Cameron identifies Melville's mystery with the category of the impersonal: that which cannot be brought into the domain of the personal, the psychological, the narratable. Theological critics connect Melville's mystery to negative theology — the tradition that approaches God through what cannot be said about God — and find in the novel's refusal to resolve its central mysteries a form of reverence. Ecocritical readings treat the mystery of the whale and the sea as Melville's resistance to the Enlightenment project of making nature transparent to human reason.
