# The Monkey-Rope

## Definition

In whaling terminology, the monkey-rope is a line attached to a canvas belt around the harpooneer's waist, with the other end held by his bowsman on deck, serving as a safety tether while the harpooneer works on the submerged back of a whale carcass during cutting-in. Ishmael introduces it with technical precision: "from the ship's steep side, did I hold Queequeg down there in the sea, by what is technically called in the fishery a monkey-rope, attached to a strong strip of canvas belted round his waist" (072-002). Yet this practical piece of whaling equipment immediately becomes the novel's most concentrated metaphor for the human condition: the rope is fastened at both ends, so that "for better or for worse, we two, for the time, were wedded; and should poor Queequeg sink to rise no more, then both usage and honor demanded, that instead of cutting the cord, it should drag me down in his wake" (072-003).

## Manifestations in the Novel

The monkey-rope appears exclusively in Chapter 72, "The Monkey-Rope," where it governs the relationship between Ishmael and his "particular friend" Queequeg during the cutting-in of a whale. Queequeg, as harpooneer, descends onto the carcass to insert the blubber hook, standing "some ten feet below the level of the deck" on the whale's back as it revolves "like a tread-mill beneath him" (072-001). Ishmael, as his bowsman, holds the monkey-rope from the ship's side, jerking Queequeg clear when he threatens to be crushed between the whale and the hull.

From this concrete situation, Ishmael elaborates a philosophy of radical interdependence. The monkey-rope is "fast at both ends; fast to Queequeg's broad canvas belt, and fast to my narrow leather one" (072-003). Ishmael perceives that "my own individuality was now merged in a joint stock company of two; that my free will had received a mortal wound; and that another's mistake or misfortune might plunge innocent me into unmerited disaster and death" (072-004). This leads him to the chapter's central insight: "this situation of mine was the precise situation of every mortal that breathes; only, in most cases, he, one way or other, has this Siamese connexion with a plurality of other mortals" (072-004). The examples are mundane and devastating: if your banker breaks, you snap; if your apothecary sends poison by mistake, you die.

The chapter's footnote notes that the reciprocal tying of monkey and holder was "introduced by no less a man than Stubb, in order to afford the imperilled harpooneer the strongest possible guarantee for the faithfulness and vigilance of his monkey-rope holder" (072-005). This detail inverts the expected hierarchy: the second mate, not the captain, is the inventor, and his innovation binds the master to the servant, the civilized man to the "savage."

## Key Passages

> "It was a humorously perilous business for both of us. For, before we proceed further, it must be said that the monkey-rope was fast at both ends; fast to Queequeg's broad canvas belt, and fast to my narrow leather one. So that for better or for worse, we two, for the time, were wedded; and should poor Queequeg sink to rise no more, then both usage and honor demanded, that instead of cutting the cord, it should drag me down in his wake."
> (072-003)

> "I saw that this situation of mine was the precise situation of every mortal that breathes; only, in most cases, he, one way or other, has this Siamese connexion with a plurality of other mortals. If your banker breaks, you snap; if your apothecary by mistake sends you poison in your pills, you die."
> (072-004)

> "But handle Queequeg's monkey-rope heedfully as I would, sometimes he jerked it so, that I came very near sliding overboard. Nor could I possibly forget that, do what I would, I only had the management of one end of it."
> (072-004)

> "Are you not the precious image of each and all of us men in this whaling world? That unsounded ocean you gasp in, is Life; those sharks, your foes; those spades, your friends; and what between sharks and spades you are in a sad pickle and peril, poor lad."
> (072-009)
