# The Doubloon

## Definition

The doubloon is a Spanish gold coin — "of purest, virgin gold, raked somewhere out of the sea by some old Peruvian" — that Ahab nails to the Pequod's mainmast as a reward for the first man to sight the White Whale (099-003). It becomes the centerpiece of Chapter 99, where Ishmael describes each major character pausing before it and reading into its symbols their own worldview, making the coin a Rorschach test of the Pequod's crew.

## Manifestations in the Novel

The doubloon appears in Chapter 99 ("The Doubloon"), one of the novel's most structurally elegant chapters. Ahab first nails it to the mast, declaring "Whosoever of ye raises me a white-headed whale with a wrinkled brow and a crooked jaw... he shall have this gold ounce, my boys!" (099-002). The coin is "of purest, virgin gold" and richly figured with "three Andes summits; from one a flame; a tower on another; on the third a crowing cock; while arched over all was a segment of the partitioned zodiac" (099-003).

Each member of the crew who passes the coin reads it differently. Ahab sees himself — the volcano, the tower, the cock that crowed. Starbuck sees a dark valley of death and the Trinity. Stubb, in a comic interlude, sees nothing but a round coin and makes up a jig about it. Flask sees "just a doubloon" and a matter of "sixteen dollars." The Manxman sees an omen. Queequeg compares the figures to the tattoos on his thigh. Pip, the cabin boy turned prophet, delivers a cryptic sermon in which he calls the doubloon "the navel of the ship" (099-015).

## Key Passages

> "Now this doubloon was of purest, virgin gold, raked somewhere out of the sea by some old Peruvian. Doubloons, they say, are getting scarcer and scarcer every day."
> (099-003)

> "There's another rendering now; but I think the sight of him has made me think of them. The doubloon is the ship's navel, and men are fastened to it by a harpoon-line."
> (099-015)

## Scholarly Context

The doubloon chapter is widely read as a microcosm of the novel's epistemology: the same physical object yields completely different interpretations depending on who looks at it. Critics note that each reading reveals more about the reader than the coin — Ahab's monomania, Starbuck's piety, Stubb's materialism — making the doubloon a mirror held up to the crew's souls. The gold coin nailed to the mast also echoes the crucifixion, with the mast as a cross and the quest for Moby Dick as a dark pilgrimage.
