# Ivory Leg

## Definition

Ahab's ivory leg is the prosthetic limb fashioned from "the polished bone of the sperm whale's jaw" (028-004) that replaces the leg torn away by Moby Dick. More than a mere prosthesis, the ivory leg is the visible sign of Ahab's wound — the physical mark that transforms him from a whaling captain into a figure of tragic obsession. It appears throughout the novel as an object of dread, a dream-image, a practical vulnerability, and ultimately a symbol of the incomplete, prosthetic nature of Ahab's quest for vengeance.

## Manifestations in the Novel

The ivory leg is introduced dramatically in Chapter 28, when Ishmael first sees Ahab on deck: "He looked like a man cut away from the stake, when the fire has overrunningly wasted all the limbs without consuming them... His whole high, broad form, seemed made of solid bronze, and shaped in an unalterable mould, like Cellini's cast Perseus" (028-003). The leg itself is described matter-of-factly: "a slim rod of ivory, sustaining itself in a punctilious manner in the jaws of a sea-peacock" — a small socket bored into the deck planks. Ishmael notes that the ivory leg "had at sea been fashioned from the polished bone of the sperm whale's jaw" (028-004).

Before Ahab ever appears on deck, the ivory leg haunts the crew's imagination. In Chapter 31, Stubb recounts a dream: "You know the old man's ivory leg, well I dreamed he kicked me with it" (031-002). The dream — in which Stubb is kicked by Ahab's leg and then kicked again by the same leg after it detaches — captures the leg's status as an uncanny, semi-autonomous object.

The leg's material vulnerability is exposed in Chapter 106 ("Ahab's Leg"). After Ahab's violent departure from the Samuel Enderby, "his ivory leg had received a half-splintering shock" (106-001). The incident reveals a hidden history: "it had not been very long prior to the Pequod's sailing from Nantucket, that he had been found one night lying prone upon the ground, and insensible; by some unknown, and seemingly inexplicable, unimaginable casualty, his ivory limb having been so violently displaced, that it had stake-wise smitten, and all but pierced his groin" (106-002). This earlier accident, Melville suggests, was the secret cause of Ahab's reclusiveness before the voyage — a secret that "invested itself with terrors, not entirely underived from the land of spirits and of wails" (106-004).

Ahab's response to the damaged leg is characteristically extreme: "he called the carpenter" and "bade him without delay set about making a new leg," ordering that "the stoutest, clearest-grained stuff might be secured" from the ship's accumulated jaw-ivory, and commanding the blacksmith to "proceed at once to the forging of whatever iron contrivances might be needed" (106-006). The leg's fragility — and Ahab's ferocious insistence on its immediate repair — reveals the dependence that underlies the captain's terrifying autonomy.

The leg also appears as a marker of Ahab's identity during the encounters with other ships. When the Samuel Enderby's captain meets Ahab, Ishmael notes "his ivory leg plainly revealed to the stranger" (100-002). In Chapter 100, Ahab "putting out his ivory leg, and crossing the ivory arm" (100-008) creates a tableau of prosthetic symmetry that underscores his hybrid nature — part man, part whalebone artifact.

## Scholarly Context

Ahab's ivory leg has been extensively analyzed as a figure of what disability scholars term "narrative prosthesis" — the use of physical difference as a marker of character and meaning. The leg is at once a practical object (fashioned from whalebone by the ship's carpenter), a psychological symbol (the wound that drives the quest), and a theological sign (Ahab's incomplete humanity, his partial replacement by the very creature he hunts). Feminist critics have noted the phallic connotations of the rigid ivory rod and its insertion into the deck's socket-hole; posthumanist readings see the leg as an early figure of the cyborg — the human body supplemented and transformed by animal material. The episode of the leg's splintering in Chapter 106 also foregrounds questions of pain and mortality that Ahab's rhetoric of defiance typically suppresses: the great captain can be brought low by a mechanical failure in his prosthetic.
