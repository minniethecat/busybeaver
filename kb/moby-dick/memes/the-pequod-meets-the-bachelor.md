# The Pequod Meets the Bachelor

## Overview

The encounter with the Bachelor is the briefest but most symbolically concentrated of the Pequod's gams. The Bachelor is a Nantucket ship — Ahab's own home port — that has filled every cask, barrel, and pot with sperm oil and is celebrating its homeward voyage. The contrast between the Bachelor's carnival joy and the Pequod's grim, obsessive atmosphere is stark and total. Ahab refuses the captain's repeated invitations to come aboard and celebrate, instead pressing his monomaniacal question: "Hast seen the White Whale?" When the captain good-humoredly replies that he doesn't believe in the White Whale at all, the ships part — one going cheerily before the wind, the other fighting against it.

## Chronology

### The Jolly Ship

The Bachelor appears "some few weeks after Ahab's harpoon had been welded" (115-001). She is a Nantucket ship that "had just wedged in her last cask of oil, and bolted down her bursting hatches; and now, in glad holiday apparel, was joyously, though somewhat vain-gloriously, sailing round among the widely-separated ships on the ground, previous to pointing her prow for home" (115-002).

The ship is festooned with celebration: "The three men at her mast-head wore long streamers of narrow red bunting at their hats; from the stern, a whale-boat was suspended, bottom down; and hanging captive from the bowsprit was seen the long lower jaw of the last whale they had slain. Signals, ensigns, and jacks of all colours were flying from her rigging, on every side" (115-003).

The Bachelor has filled everything imaginable with sperm oil:

> Not only had barrels of beef and bread been given away to make room for the far more valuable sperm, but additional supplemental casks had been bartered for, from the ships she had met; and these were stowed along the deck, and in the captain's and officers' state-rooms. Even the cabin table itself had been knocked into kindling-wood. (115-004)

The cook has filled his boiler, the steward his coffee-pot, the harpooneers their iron sockets — "indeed everything was filled with sperm, except the captain's pantaloons pockets, and those he reserved to thrust his hands into, in self-complacent testimony of his own entire satisfaction" (115-004).

### The Carnival

As the Bachelor bears down on the Pequod, "the barbarian sound of enormous drums came from her forecastle" (115-005). The crew is dancing around the emptied try-pots, which have been covered with parchment-like whale stomach skin to make drums. On the quarter-deck, "the mates and harpooneers were dancing with the olive-hued girls who had eloped with them from the Polynesian Isles" (115-005). Three Long Island negroes with "glittering fiddle-bows of whale ivory" preside over the dancing (115-005). Others are demolishing the try-works brickwork: "You would have almost thought they were pulling down the cursed Bastille, such wild cries they raised" (115-005).

The captain "stood erect on the ship's elevated quarter-deck, so that the whole rejoicing drama was full before him, and seemed merely contrived for his own individual diversion" (115-006).

### The Contrast

The two captains embody the difference between their ships:

> And Ahab, he too was standing on his quarter-deck, shaggy and black, with a stubborn gloom; and as the two ships crossed each other's wakes — one all jubilations for things passed, the other all forebodings as to things to come — their two captains in themselves impersonated the whole striking contrast of the scene. (115-007)

The Bachelor's captain calls out: "Come aboard, come aboard!" lifting a glass and bottle (115-008). Ahab grits his reply: "Hast seen the White Whale?" (115-009). The captain answers cheerfully: "No; only heard of him; but don't believe in him at all. Come aboard!" (115-010).

Ahab mutters to himself: "How wondrous familiar is a fool!" Then aloud: "Thou art a full ship and homeward bound, thou sayst; well, then, call me an empty ship, and outward-bound. So go thy ways, and I will mine. Set all sail, and keep her to the wind!" (115-013).

### The Parting

The two ships separate in opposite directions: "while the one ship went cheerily before the breeze, the other stubbornly fought against it; and so the two vessels parted" (115-014). The Pequod's crew watch the Bachelor recede "with grave, lingering glances"; the Bachelor's crew never notice, absorbed in their revelry.

Ahab takes from his pocket "a small vial of sand" — Nantucket soundings — and looks from the receding ship to the vial, "seemed thereby bringing two remote associations together" (115-014). The sand from home and the departing homeward-bound ship: Ahab is moving away from everything the Bachelor represents.

## Narrative Significance

The Bachelor episode is the novel's purest expression of what Ahab has sacrificed. The Bachelor represents everything Ahab cannot have: completion, satisfaction, home, joy, community, the end of labor. The ship is Nantucket — Ahab's own home — making the rejection doubly poignant.

Melville's language in this chapter deliberately contrasts the "jubilations" of the Bachelor with the "forebodings" of the Pequod, and locates the difference in the two captains' souls. The Bachelor's captain is not named — he is Everyman, the ordinary, happy, successful whaleman. Ahab is the exception, the one who cannot accept completion.

The vial of Nantucket sand that Ahab produces at the chapter's end is the novel's most economical symbol of his condition. He carries home with him physically, as a sample in a vial, but cannot — will not — return to it. Home is a substance he can hold in his hand but cannot enter.

## Aftermath / Consequences

The ships part, and "the Pequod was now fairly embarked in this business of whaling" (116-001). The Bachelor's departure marks the point of no return — every subsequent gam brings darker omens. Four whales are killed the next day, but one sinks, a loss that foreshadows the novel's final catastrophe.
