# Jeroboam

## Overview

The Jeroboam is a [[nantucket|Nantucket]] whaleship the [[pequod|Pequod]] encounters in the Pacific. She is dominated by a strange figure: [[gabriel|Gabriel]], a Shaker prophet who has seized spiritual authority over her crew.

Unlike the other gams, which turn on practical matters of whaling and navigation, the Jeroboam encounter is saturated with religious mania and apocalyptic prophecy. It is one of Melville's darkest portraits of belief run wild at sea.

## The Ship and Her Crew

The Jeroboam is captained by [[captain-mayhew|Captain Mayhew]], but the real power aboard is Gabriel. A former Shaker from Nantucket, Gabriel has declared himself "the archangel" and holds the crew in thrall.

Gabriel had prophesied the death of the Jeroboam's chief mate, [[macey|Harry Macey]], when Macey proposed to hunt a whale Gabriel declared sacred. When Macey was killed by that very whale, Gabriel's authority became unassailable. "It seemed that the Jeroboam had not long left home, when" (071-017) the prophecy was fulfilled, and the crew — already superstitious — fell entirely under Gabriel's dominion.

The crew now obeys Gabriel absolutely. They believe he commands the whale. They do not challenge him. The captain is captain in name only.

## Encounter with the Pequod

When the Pequod and Jeroboam meet, Gabriel is rowed out in a boat. He sees Ahab on the Pequod's deck and issues a warning: he has had a vision of Ahab's death by the White Whale.

Ahab, characteristically, scorns the prophecy. The meeting is poisoned by Gabriel's presence. He shouts from his boat, a wild-eyed prophet on the Pacific, denouncing Ahab's quest.

"Pulling an oar in the Jeroboam's boat, was a man of a singular appearance" (071-006) — this is Gabriel, whose presence transforms an ordinary gam into a supernatural confrontation.

The Pequod takes no mail from the Jeroboam — an ominous breach of whaling custom. The two ships part with bad blood.

## Fate

The Jeroboam sails on, still under Gabriel's spell. Her fate is unknown, but the implication is dark: a ship governed by a mad prophet is a ship without reason.

Melville does not tell us whether the Jeroboam returns to Nantucket. In the novel's symbolic economy, the Jeroboam is less a ship than a warning: the Pequod's own captain is a prophet of destruction, and the Jeroboam shows what happens when a prophet goes unchecked.

## Symbolic Significance

The Jeroboam episode is Melville's parable of theocracy at sea. Gabriel is Ahab's dark double: both men claim supernatural authority, both bend a crew to their will, and both prophesy death. The difference is that Gabriel is transparently mad, while Ahab's madness wears the mask of command.

The Jeroboam's biblical name — Jeroboam was a king of Israel who led his people into idolatry — reinforces the theme. The ship is an idolatrous kingdom, worshipping Gabriel instead of God.

The encounter also establishes the novel's pattern: prophecy always comes true, and the one who scorns it always dies.
