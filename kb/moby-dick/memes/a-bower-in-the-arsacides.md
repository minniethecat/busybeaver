# A Bower in the Arsacides

## Definition

"A Bower in the Arsacides" is Ishmael's account of encountering a sperm whale skeleton that has been transformed into a sacred temple on the island of Tranque, in the Arsacides. The chapter serves as the novel's primary source for the detailed skeletal measurements of the sperm whale, but its deeper significance lies in Melville's meditation on the transformation of death into worship, nature into architecture, and bone into bower. The whale skeleton — stripped of flesh, bleached by the sun, and overgrown with tropical vines — becomes a site where life and death interpenetrate: "Life folded Death; Death trellised Life; the grim god wived with youthful Life, and begat him curly-headed glories" (102-009). The chapter extends Melville's loom metaphor into a vision of the natural world as a weaving in which the dead whale becomes both the weaver and the woven.

## Manifestations in the Novel

Ishmael prefaces the Arsacides account with a defensive justification of his anatomical authority: "How is it, that you, a mere oarsman in the fishery, pretend to know aught about the subterranean parts of the whale?" (102-002). He answers by citing two sources of knowledge: his dissection of a "small cub Sperm Whale" (102-003) hoisted onto the deck of a previous ship, and — far more significantly — his visit to the whale-skeleton temple on Tranque, where his "late royal friend Tranquo, king of Tranque, one of the Arsacides" (102-004) maintained a collection of "barbaric vertu" (102-005).

The whale skeleton had been "found dead and stranded, with his head against a cocoa-nut tree, whose plumage-like, tufted droopings seemed his verdant jet" (102-006) after a great gale. Its bones were dried in the sun, transported up a glen, and installed beneath a "grand temple of lordly palms" (102-006). The description of the resulting structure is one of the novel's most baroque passages:

> "The ribs were hung with trophies; the vertebrae were carved with Arsacidean annals, in strange hieroglyphics; in the skull, the priests kept up an unextinguished aromatic flame, so that the mystic head again sent forth its vapory spout; while, suspended from a bough, the terrific lower jaw vibrated over all the devotees, like the hair-hung sword that so affrighted Damocles." (102-007)

The transformation is complete: dead whale becomes living temple, bone becomes altar, the spout-hole becomes a censer. The priests who tend the flame in the skull have made the whale's anatomy into a site of worship — yet Ishmael, with his measuring-rod, approaches it as a scientist: "Dar'st thou measure this our god!" the priests cry, and a "fierce contest rose among them, concerning feet and inches; they cracked each other's sconces with their yard-sticks" (102-011).

The chapter's philosophical center is the loom meditation that follows Ishmael's measurements. The tropical wood surrounding the skeleton is described as a living loom: "The wood was green as mosses of the Icy Glen; the trees stood high and haughty, feeling their living sap; the industrious earth beneath was as a weaver's loom, with a gorgeous carpet on it, whereof the ground-vine tendrils formed the warp and woof, and the living flowers the figures" (102-008). The skeleton, lying amid this verdant weaving, is both "a gigantic idler" and "the cunning weaver; himself all woven over with the vines" (102-009). The passage culminates in one of Melville's most explicit statements of his metaphysical vision: "The weaver-god, he weaves; and by that weaving is he deafened, that he hears no mortal voice; and by that humming, we, too, who look on the loom are deafened; and only when we escape it shall we hear the thousand voices that speak through it" (102-008).
