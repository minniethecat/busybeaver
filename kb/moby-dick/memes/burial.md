# Burial

## Definition

Burial in *Moby-Dick* is the ritual that should close the relation between the living and the dead — and that in Melville's cosmos never quite does. The novel's primary burial form is burial at sea, which lacks the fixity of interment in earth: the sea receives the body but does not hold it still. Ishmael's early declaration — "my death and burial were locked up in my chest" (049-008) — establishes burial as something a living person can carry with them, not wait for. The novel's two most charged burial objects — Queequeg's coffin and its transformation into Ishmael's life-buoy — make burial's ambivalence material: the coffin is both death's container and life's vehicle. In *Moby-Dick*, burial is never only an ending.

## Manifestations in the Novel

**Burial locked in the chest (Chapter 49)**. After the first encounter with a whale in a storm, Ishmael makes a will and reflects: "I survived myself; my death and burial were locked up in my chest" (049-008). The phrase is extraordinary — burial as portable property, something a living man carries. Ishmael has already undergone his own death and burial in imagination, and this pre-burial is what enables him to face actual danger. The chest here is both literal (a sailor's sea-chest) and metaphorical (the ribcage, the heart where death-awareness lives).

**Decent burial as privilege (Chapter 100)**. When the *Samuel Enderby*'s captain describes losing his arm to Moby Dick, he notes a grim asymmetry: "the privilege of giving decent burial to the other, why in that case" (100-035) — one whaleboat crew buried, the other swallowed. Decent burial is a privilege, not a right. In the whale-fishery, the line between burial and consumption is thin; the sea does not guarantee ceremony.

**The hushed burial of the typhoon's calm (Chapter 116)**. After the typhoon, Melville describes "the hushed burial of its after calm" (116-005). Burial here becomes a metaphor for weather — the storm's violence is interred in stillness, but the stillness carries the memory of violence within it, like a grave holding a body. The language of burial permeates even the novel's nature descriptions, suggesting that burial is less an event than a condition of the novel's world.

**Queequeg's coffin and the sad burial (Chapter 131)**. The Pequod's encounter with the *Delight* produces the novel's most direct burial scene. The *Delight* is burying a sailor killed by Moby Dick, and her crew cries: "ye fly our sad burial; ye but turn us your taffrail" (131-012). The *Delight*'s burial is declined — the Pequod cannot pause for another ship's grief. This refusal of burial ritual is the novel's darkest communal moment: the shared ceremony that makes death bearable is rejected because Ahab's quest admits no delay, no acknowledgment of anyone else's dead.

**The coffin-lifebuoy (Chapters 110, 135)**. Queequeg's coffin, built when he believed himself dying, becomes the novel's central burial symbol. When he recovers, the coffin is converted to a sea-chest — burial container repurposed for life. In the novel's final moments, the coffin bursts from the sinking Pequod and becomes Ishmael's life-buoy: "the coffin life-buoy shot lengthwise from the sea" (Ch.135). Burial and salvation are fused. The thing built to hold a dead body becomes the thing that keeps the sole survivor alive. Melville's point is not consoling — it is that burial and survival are made of the same material, that the line between death and life is thinner than we think.

## Scholarly Context

Burial in *Moby-Dick* has been analyzed through anthropological, psychoanalytic, and material-culture frameworks. The "burial at sea" motif draws on centuries of maritime ritual, where the absence of fixed graves produces distinctive forms of memorialization (the Whaleman's Chapel's cenotaphs are burial's architectural supplement). Psychoanalytic readings see Ishmael's "burial locked in my chest" as a figure for the internalization of the lost object — mourning as incorporation. The coffin-lifebuoy has attracted particular attention from material-culture scholars: it is Melville's most condensed image of how objects can reverse their meaning, death-technology becoming life-technology. More broadly, the novel's refusal to let burial achieve closure aligns it with trauma theory's critique of the possibility of "working through."
