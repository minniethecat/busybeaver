# The Nut

## Definition

"The Nut" is the slang term invoked by Stubb upon first sighting Gabriel, the self-proclaimed archangel aboard the Nantucket whaleship *Jeroboam*, whose story occupies Chapter 71 of *Moby-Dick*. "That's he! that's he!—the long-togged scaramouch the Town-Ho's company told us of!" (071-007), Stubb exclaims, recognizing the figure from an earlier gam. Gabriel embodies one of the novel's recurring types: the religious fanatic whose madness grants him uncanny authority over others. His presence on the *Jeroboam* — and his dire prophecies concerning the White Whale — serves as a dark mirror to Ahab's own charismatic obsession and prophetic self-conception.

## Manifestations in the Novel

### Gabriel's Rise to Power

Gabriel's history is recounted as backstory within the chapter. Originally a member of the "crazy society of Neskyeuna Shakers," he had "several times descended from heaven by the way of a trap-door, announcing the speedy opening of the seventh vial, which he carried in his vest-pocket" (071-008). The vial, instead of containing gunpowder, "was supposed to be charged with laudanum." Seized by "a strange, apostolic whim," Gabriel left the Shaker community for Nantucket and signed aboard the *Jeroboam* as a green-hand — "with that cunning peculiar to craziness, he assumed a steady, common-sense exterior" (071-008).

Once the ship was out of sight of land, "his insanity broke out in a freshet" (071-008). He announced himself as "the archangel Gabriel" and "commanded the captain to jump overboard." He published a manifesto declaring himself "the deliverer of the isles of the sea and vicar-general of all Oceanica." The crew, confronted with "the unflinching earnestness with which he declared these things" and "all the preternatural terrors of real delirium" (071-008), fell under his sway. When the captain attempted to rid himself of Gabriel, the crew mutinied: "if Gabriel was sent from the ship, not a man of them would remain" (071-008). The archangel had won.

Melville pauses to reflect: "Nor is the history of fanatics half so striking in respect to the measureless self-deception of the fanatic himself, as his measureless power of deceiving and bedevilling so many others" (071-008).

### The Gam with the Pequod

When the *Jeroboam* meets the *Pequod*, Gabriel is in the captain's boat. An epidemic has broken out aboard the *Jeroboam*, and Captain Mayhew refuses direct contact — but Gabriel uses the occasion to deliver warnings. Each time Ahab asks about the White Whale, Gabriel interrupts with prophetic cries: "Think, think of thy whale-boat, stoven and sunk! Beware of the horrible tail!" (071-014).

The *Jeroboam*'s history with Moby Dick forms the chapter's tragic core. Macey, the chief mate, had insisted on hunting the White Whale despite Gabriel's denunciations. After harpooning the whale, Macey stood in the boat's bow "venting his wild exclamations upon the whale" when "a broad white shadow rose from the sea; by its quick, fanning motion, temporarily taking the breath out of the bodies of the oarsmen" (071-017). Macey was "smitten bodily into the air, and making a long arc in his descent, fell into the sea at the distance of about fifty yards. Not a chip of the boat was harmed, nor a hair of any oarsman's head; but the mate for ever sank" (071-017).

Gabriel's subsequent cry — "The vial! the vial!" — is described as having "called off the terror-stricken crew from the further hunting of the whale" (071-019). Melville notes with characteristic skepticism that "his credulous disciples believed that he had specifically fore-announced it, instead of only making a general prophecy, which any one might have done, and so have chanced to hit one of many marks in the wide margin allowed" (071-019).

### The Letter

The chapter closes with one of the novel's most chilling moments. Ahab, having a letter for a *Jeroboam* officer, discovers it is addressed to the dead Macey: "Mr. Harry Macey, Ship Jeroboam;—why it's Macey, and he's dead!" (071-025). When Ahab attempts to pass the letter to Mayhew, Gabriel snatches it, impales it on a boat-knife, and flings it back at Ahab's feet with the words: "thou art soon going that way" (071-027–028). The dead man's letter, sent back by a mad prophet, becomes an omen of Ahab's own fate — a prophecy that will prove devastatingly accurate.

## Scholarly Context

Gabriel has been read as a dark parody of prophetic authority: his Shaker origins connect him to a tradition of radical American religious enthusiasm, while his self-appointment as "archangel" echoes Ahab's own self-deification. The *Jeroboam* episode has been interpreted as a miniature of the novel's main plot — a captain whose ship is effectively commandeered by a monomaniac whose authority derives not from rank but from charismatic terror. Gabriel's accurate "prophecy" of Macey's death raises the same epistemological question that haunts the novel: if a madman correctly predicts a death, is he a prophet or merely, as Melville suggests, someone who "chanced to hit one of many marks in the wide margin allowed"? The dead-letter scene — a wife's letter reaching her husband's killer before news of his death — is one of the novel's most compressed and devastating images of fate's cruelty.
