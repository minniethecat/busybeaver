# Blood

## Definition

In *Moby-Dick*, blood functions as the irreducible physical sign of life and death aboard the whaling vessel. The whale's blood is described in startling physiological detail: "Ten or fifteen gallons of blood are thrown out of the heart at every pulsation" [P02-084], with an "impetus and velocity" [P02-085] that makes the creature's circulatory system a hydraulic marvel. But beyond anatomy, blood in the novel operates as a symbolic substance — the medium of oaths, the token of noble lineage, and the visible stain of violence. Ishmael speaks of men who have "excellent blood in his veins — royal stuff" [012-002], while the blood of the whale mingles with seawater to create the "bloody possession" [P02-147] that marks every whaling voyage as a scene of killing.

## Manifestations in the Novel

Blood flows through the novel at every register. In the prefatory material, the whale's massive cardiac output establishes the physiological scale of the creature. Among the crew, blood signifies ancestry: the "unvitiated blood of those proud warrior hunters" [027-008] that runs in the veins of the Pequod's harpooneers, marking them as descendants of martial races. Blood also carries the weight of moral judgment — Starbuck is described as a "sworn foe to human bloodshed" [016-044], yet his Quaker heritage is complicated by the bloodiness of the whaling profession. The most striking deployment of blood comes in the chase sequences, where the whale's blood turns the sea crimson, and in Ahab's oath-ritual, where the harpooneers drink from their own veins to seal their compact. Melville's narrator defends whalemen against the charge that they have "no good blood in their veins" [024-014], pointing to the paradox that the bloodiest of trades produces the noblest of men.

## Scholarly Context

Blood in *Moby-Dick* has been analyzed through the lens of sacrificial economy — the whale's blood as the price of illumination (whale oil), and Ahab's blood-covenant as a perversion of religious ritual. Critics have also noted the racial dimension: the "good blood" of the "savage" harpooneers challenges nineteenth-century hierarchies of lineage, while the blood of the white whale complicates any simple equation of whiteness with purity. The novel's sanguinary imagery participates in a broader Romantic fascination with blood as the site where the material body and the transcendent soul meet.
