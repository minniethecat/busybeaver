# Hump-backed Whale

## Classification

The Hump-backed Whale occupies Book I (Folio), Chapter IV of Ishmael's cetological system — one of the six whales of the first magnitude. It belongs to the baleen-bearing branch of the Folios, alongside the right whale and fin-back, distinct from the toothed sperm whale (032-020).

Modern taxonomy identifies it as *Megaptera novaeangliae*, a rorqual of the family Balaenopteridae. Its common name derives from the pronounced hump before its small dorsal fin, and its scientific name means "big-winged New Englander" — a reference to its extraordinarily long pectoral fins.

## In Cetology (Chapter 32)

Ishmael's description of the Hump-backed Whale (032-027) is brief but characteristically rich. He opens by noting its range — "often seen on the northern American coast" — and that it has been "frequently captured there, and towed into harbor."

The physical description centers on the namesake hump. The whale carries "a great pack on him like a peddler," Ishmael says, and adds: "or you might call him the Elephant and Castle whale." This latter name evokes the famous London coaching inn — a whimsical comparison that captures the whale's bulky, almost architectural profile.

He immediately qualifies the common name:

> At any rate, the popular name for him does not sufficiently distinguish him, since the sperm whale also has a hump though a smaller one. (032-027)

This taxonomic imprecision is a recurring theme in Chapter 32. Ishmael returns to it a few paragraphs earlier (032-025), observing that "the sperm whale and the humpbacked whale, each has a hump; but there the similitude ceases" — a meditation on the failure of single-feature classification that runs through the entire Cetology chapter.

The hump-backed whale's oil "is not very valuable," and it has baleen. But where the [[fin-back-whale|Fin-Back]] is solitary and misanthropic, the Hump-backed is defined by joy:

> He is the most gamesome and light-hearted of all the whales, making more gay foam and white water generally than any other of them. (032-027)

This single sentence sketches an entire temperament. Among Melville's cetological portraits — the sperm whale's majesty, the fin-back's lonely defiance, the killer's savagery — the hump-backed whale alone is characterized by play.

## Appearances in the Novel

The hump-backed whale appears almost exclusively in Chapter 32. Unlike the [[fin-back-whale|Fin-Back]], which surfaces in later chapters, or the [[narwhale|Narwhale]], which flickers through several, the Hump-backed's presence in the novel is largely confined to its cetological portrait.

However, the "hump" as a feature recurs throughout *Moby-Dick* — most significantly in connection with [[the-white-whale|Moby Dick]] himself, whose "high, pyramidical white hump" (041-014) is one of his most distinctive marks. The hump is, in a sense, shared anatomy: what distinguishes Moby Dick from other sperm whales connects him, however obliquely, to the gamesome hump-backed whale of Chapter 32.

## Whaling Context

Humpback whales were hunted extensively in the nineteenth century, particularly in coastal waters where they could be caught and towed to shore-based stations. Their relatively slow speed and tendency to feed near the surface made them vulnerable. Unlike the uncatchable [[fin-back-whale|Fin-Back]], the humpback was considered a manageable quarry.

Their oil, as Ishmael notes, was not premium — it ranked below spermaceti and even below right whale oil. But humpbacks yielded baleen, which commanded its own market. By the late nineteenth century, shore whaling had severely depleted North Atlantic humpback populations.

Modern humpbacks are known for their complex songs, a behavior Melville could not have observed from a whale-ship. Twentieth-century science — particularly the discovery of humpback song by Roger Payne in the 1960s — gave the species a new cultural significance, transforming it from a commercial resource into an icon of marine conservation.
