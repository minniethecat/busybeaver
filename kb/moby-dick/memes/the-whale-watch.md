# The Whale Watch

## Definition

The Whale Watch is the midnight vigil that Ahab and his Parsee harpooneer Fedallah keep together in Chapter 117, stationed beside a slain whale through the dark hours of night. Set in Ahab's whaleboat — the only one that could not return to the Pequod before nightfall — the watch becomes a ritual space of eerie intimacy between the monomaniac captain and his shadowy familiar. Under the "troubled flickering glare" of a lantern thrust into the dead whale's spout-hole, the two figures "seemed the last men in a flooded world" (117-004), and their whispered exchange about hearses, hemp, and immortality constitutes one of the novel's most concentrated passages of prophetic and supernatural charge.

The Whale Watch is distinct from the general theme of mast-head standing explored in Chapter 35 ("The Mast-Head"), which treats the philosophical dangers of transcendental reverie at the mast-head. Here, the watch is not about losing oneself in abstraction but about the uncanny proximity of two men bound by a shared fate — Ahab, who has pledged his soul to the destruction of the White Whale, and Fedallah, the "pilot" whose prophecies shadow the captain's every step toward destruction.

## Manifestations in the Novel

The scene opens with practical whaling business: four whales have been killed, and three brought alongside the Pequod before nightfall; "the windward one could not be reached till morning; and the boat that had killed it lay by its side all night; and that boat was Ahab's" (117-001). A waif-pole — the marker of a claimed whale — is thrust upright into the dead whale's spout-hole, and a lantern hung from it casts "a troubled flickering glare upon the black, glossy back, and far out upon the midnight waves" (117-002). This lantern-lit whale becomes a floating bier, a stage for the drama that follows.

The crew sleeps; only Fedallah remains awake, "crouching in the bow, sat watching the sharks, that spectrally played round the whale, and tapped the light cedar planks with their tails" (117-003). A sound "like the moaning in squadrons over Asphaltites of unforgiven ghosts of Gomorrah, ran shuddering through the air" (117-003) — the passage deliberately evokes the Dead Sea and the destroyed cities, framing the watch in eschatological terms.

Ahab wakes and, seeing the Parsee, speaks: "I have dreamed it again" (117-004). The dream is of hearses — the very hearses that Fedallah, in Chapter 117, reaffirms as conditions of Ahab's death: "ere thou couldst die on this voyage, two hearses must verily be seen by thee on the sea; the first not made by mortal hands; and the visible wood of the last one must be grown in America" (117-007). The prophecy, first delivered in Chapter 36 ("The Quarter-Deck"), here receives its most explicit and detailed restatement.

The exchange that follows is the heart of the scene. When Ahab asks "And who are hearsed that die on the sea?" (117-006), and Fedallah declares "Hemp only can kill thee" (117-013), Ahab erupts in derisive laughter: "The gallows, ye mean.—I am immortal then, on land and on sea!" (117-014). This declaration of immortality — a classic tragic boast — seals Ahab's hubris at the moment he believes himself safest. The reader, knowing the novel's outcome, recognizes that Fedallah's "hemp" refers not to the gallows but to the whale-line, and that Ahab's immortality is precisely what makes his fate so terrible: he will live to see the fulfillment of every prophecy.

The scene closes with the grey dawn and the slumbering crew arising — "Both were silent again, as one man" (117-015). The watch dissolves into ordinary morning, but the exchange lingers as one of the novel's structural pillars.

## Symbolic Dimensions

### The Lantern and the Dead Whale

The lantern thrust into the dead whale's spout-hole transforms the carcass into an altar. The "troubled flickering glare" illuminates the whale's "black, glossy back" while the midnight waves "gently chafed the whale's broad flank, like soft surf upon a beach" (117-002). The whale — the object of Ahab's obsession in its living form, here dead — becomes the silent witness to the captain's declaration of immortality. The juxtaposition is pointed: Ahab boasts of his own indestructibility while floating beside a creature he has destroyed.

### The Sharks as Specters

The sharks that "spectrally played round the whale" (117-003) prefigure the novel's closing image of sharks swarming around the sinking Pequod. They are death's attendants, circling even as Ahab declares himself beyond death's reach. Fedallah alone watches them, his vigil a recognition of what Ahab's boasting denies.

### Hemp and the Gallows

Fedallah's prophecy that "Hemp only can kill thee" (117-013) is the scene's most loaded line. Ahab immediately misreads it as gallows-rope and concludes he is immortal — but "hemp" in a whaling context is the whale-line, the rope attached to the harpoon. Ahab's death in the final chapter comes precisely by hemp: he is "shot out of the boat" by the whale-line around his neck (135-043). The prophecy is fulfilled not despite Ahab's interpretation but through the gap between what Ahab hears and what Fedallah means. This gap is the structural principle of all prophecy in the novel.

## Scholarly Context

Critics have recognized the Whale Watch as a crucial node in the novel's system of prophecies and foreshadowing. The scene's ritual quality — two men alone on a dark sea beside a dead leviathan, exchanging cryptic prophecies while sharks circle — has been compared to the consultations of oracles in classical epic. Fedallah's role as "pilot" (117-011) aligns him with Charon figures in the epic tradition, guides to the underworld who are also its prisoners.

The lantern-lit whale has also been read as an inversion of the "Candles" scene (Chapter 119), where the corpusants illuminate the Pequod's mastheads and Ahab addresses the "clear spirit of clear fire." In both scenes, unnatural illumination transforms the ship into sacred space; but where the corpusants scene is public and theatrical, the Whale Watch is private and intimate, Ahab's last quiet moment before the final chase.

The scene's structural position — Chapter 117, in the novel's closing movement — gives it the weight of a final testament before the three-day chase begins (Chapters 133–135). Every element of Ahab's fate is recapitulated here: the hearses, the hemp, the Parsee who "shall still go before thee thy pilot" (117-011), and the dawn that breaks on the last day Ahab will have before sighting Moby Dick.
