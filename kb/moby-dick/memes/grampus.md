# Grampus

## Classification

The Grampus is the foundational species of Ishmael's Octavo division. When he introduces the three great "Books" of his cetological system — Folio, Octavo, Duodecimo — he presents them through their types:

> As the type of the FOLIO I present the Sperm Whale; of the OCTAVO, the Grampus; of the DUODECIMO, the Porpoise. (032-019)

This is a significant choice. The [[sperm-whale|sperm whale]] defines the largest class; the porpoise the smallest. The Grampus — a dolphin by modern taxonomy — occupies the conceptual center, the "moderate octavo size" that bridges the extremes.

Modern taxonomy places Risso's dolphin (*Grampus griseus*) in the family Delphinidae (oceanic dolphins). It is the only species in its genus. Melville was ahead of his time in recognizing it as a cetacean — he notes that while it is "not popularly classed among whales," it possesses "all the grand distinctive features of the leviathan" (032-033).

## In Cetology (Chapter 32)

Ishmael's portrait of the Grampus (032-033) begins with its most famous attribute — its breath:

> Though this fish, whose loud sonorous breathing, or rather blowing, has furnished a proverb to landsmen, is so well known a denizen of the deep, yet is he not popularly classed among whales. (032-033)

The "proverb" is not named, but the implication is clear: the grampus's blow is loud enough to be heard — and spoken of — far beyond the whaling world. Melville adds that "possessing all the grand distinctive features of the leviathan, most naturalists have recognised him for one."

The physical description is compact but precise:

> He is of moderate octavo size, varying from fifteen to twenty-five feet in length, and of corresponding dimensions round the waist. He swims in herds; he is never regularly hunted, though his oil is considerable in quantity, and pretty good for light. (032-033)

Grampus oil, unlike the prized spermaceti of the [[sperm-whale|sperm whale]], is merely "pretty good for light" — serviceable but unexceptional. The species is "never regularly hunted," occupying a commercial no-man's-land between the valuable Folios and the negligible Duodecimoes.

The passage closes with an omen. Some fishermen regard the grampus's approach as "premonitory of the advance of the great sperm whale" (032-033) — the smaller cetacean announcing the larger, as heralds precede a king. This folk belief invests the grampus with a narrative function: it is not merely a whale but a sign.

## Appearances in the Novel

The grampus appears almost exclusively in Chapter 32. Its single additional mention comes in the description of the Killer whale (032-037), where the Killer is said to be "about the bigness of a grampus" — the grampus serving as a unit of cetological measurement, the standard against which other Octavo whales are sized.

This scarcity of appearances is itself significant. The grampus is the type of the Octavo — the conceptual center of Melville's system — yet it barely features in the narrative. Ishmael's cetology is built on creatures he has seen but does not hunt, whales that exist in the system without being pursued.

## Whaling Context

Risso's dolphin was not a target of American whaling. Its oil, while usable, was not valuable enough to justify the chase. Grampus were occasionally taken opportunistically, but no fishery depended on them.

The species' modern identity as Risso's dolphin came later. Georges Cuvier described it in 1812, naming it after Antoine Risso, a naturalist who studied Mediterranean cetaceans. Melville, writing decades after Cuvier, appears unaware of or uninterested in this taxonomic history. For him, the grampus is defined by its breath and its place in a self-invented system.

Today *Grampus griseus* is known for its distinctive scarring — older individuals are often nearly white, their bodies crisscrossed with the marks of squid beaks and social interactions. This is a detail Melville could not have observed from a whale-ship's deck.
