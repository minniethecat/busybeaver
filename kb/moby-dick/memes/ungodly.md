# Ungodly

## Definition

"Ungodly" in *Moby-Dick* is not a simple moral condemnation. When Peleg describes Ahab as "a grand, ungodly, god-like man" (016-079), the word sits between "grand" and "god-like," triangulating a quality that is neither divine nor demonic but something larger than both. The "ungodly" is what exceeds the merely human without attaining the sacred — a magnitude that is terrifying precisely because it refuses the categories that would contain it. Melville uses the word only three times in the novel, always of Ahab, and each usage tightens the paradox: to be ungodly is to possess qualities that would be divine if they were not directed against divinity itself. Ahab's ungodliness is not atheism (he believes in the powers he fights) but antitheism: a force equal and opposite to the divine, a counter-god.

## Manifestations in the Novel

**A grand, ungodly, god-like man (Chapter 16)**. Peleg's description of Ahab to Ishmael is the novel's first characterization of its absent captain and remains its most concentrated: "He's a grand, ungodly, god-like man, Captain Ahab; doesn't speak much; but when he does speak, then you may well listen" (016-079). The three adjectives form a progression that is also a contradiction: "grand" (scale), "ungodly" (refusal of piety), "god-like" (divine resemblance). Peleg's syntax makes "ungodly" the pivot: the word that both separates "grand" from "god-like" and makes their combination possible. Ahab's grandeur is the condition of his godlikeness, but his ungodliness is what makes that godlikeness terrible rather than holy.

**Ungodly guise (Chapter 18)**. When Captain Bildad reproaches Peleg for blasphemy, Peleg retorts: "Canst thou prate in this ungodly guise. Thou beliest thine own heart, Bildad" (018-025). The phrase "ungodly guise" captures the performative dimension of ungodliness: it is a mode of speech, a posture, a way of inhabiting language that refuses the pieties others demand. Peleg's ungodly guise is comic where Ahab's will be tragic, but the word links them: both men speak in a register that theological propriety cannot accommodate.

**The ungodly old man (Chapter 41)**. After Ahab has nailed the doubloon and sworn the crew to his quest, Ishmael reflects: "Here, then, was this grey-headed, ungodly old man, chasing with curses a Job's whale round the world" (041-024). The phrase "ungodly old man" is in apposition to "Job's whale" — the biblical reference makes the ungodliness explicit. Ahab is not Job (the patient sufferer) but Job's antagonist: the one who curses rather than submits, who chases rather than waits, who demands an answer from the whirlwind rather than accepting its rebuke. "Ungodly" here is the opposite of Job-like: it is the refusal to let God be God.

## Key Passages

- (016-079) — "A grand, ungodly, god-like man, Captain Ahab": Peleg's triadic description, the novel's definitive characterization of Ahab.
- (018-025) — "Canst thou prate in this ungodly guise": Peleg to Bildad, ungodliness as speech-act, as mode of address.
- (041-024) — "This grey-headed, ungodly old man, chasing with curses a Job's whale": Ahab as anti-Job, the ungodly as refusal of submission.

## Scholarly Context

Melville's coinage of "ungodly" as Ahab's epithet has been read in the context of the novel's deep engagement with Calvinist theology and its crises. The "ungodly" occupies a position that Calvinism reserved for the reprobate — those predestined to damnation — but Melville transforms the category from a divine decree into a human achievement. Ahab is not ungodly because God has abandoned him; he is ungodly because he has abandoned the posture of submission. This reversal has been read as Melville's humanist challenge to Calvinist anthropology: if the ungodly man can be "grand" and "god-like," then the categories of election and reprobation are inadequate to human reality. More recent readings link the "ungodly" to Melville's critique of Emersonian self-reliance: Ahab is what happens when self-reliance becomes absolute, when the self refuses any limit — including the divine.
