# Narwhale

## Classification

The Narwhale occupies Book II (Octavo), Chapter III of Ishmael's cetological system — the middle tier, reserved for whales of "middling magnitude." It shares the Octavo class with the [[grampus|Grampus]], [[black-fish|Black Fish]], Thrasher, and Killer (032-031).

Modern taxonomy places the narwhal in the family Monodontidae, alongside the beluga whale. Its scientific name, *Monodon monoceros*, means "one-tooth, one-horn." It is one of only two living species in its family.

## In Cetology (Chapter 32)

Ishmael's portrait of the Narwhale (032-035) opens with a characteristic quibble over nomenclature. The name "Narwhale" — which he glosses as "Nostril whale" — he attributes to "his peculiar horn being originally mistaken for a peaked nose." The creature, he tells us, is some sixteen feet in length, while its horn averages five feet, "though some exceed ten, and even attain to fifteen feet."

He is precise about its anatomy:

> Strictly speaking, this horn is but a lengthened tusk, growing out from the jaw in a line a little depressed from the horizontal. But it is only found on the sinister side, which has an ill effect, giving its owner something analogous to the aspect of a clumsy left-handed man. (032-035)

On the question of the tusk's purpose, Ishmael is playfully agnostic. Sailors claim the Narwhale uses it "for a rake in turning over the bottom of the sea for food." Charley Coffin says it is an ice-piercer. Ishmael's own opinion: "it would certainly be very convenient to him for a folder in reading pamphlets."

The passage then opens into historical lore. The tusk, he reports, was "in ancient days regarded as the great antidote against poison" and commanded immense prices. It was distilled into "volatile salts for fainting ladies." The set-piece is the story of Sir Martin Frobisher, who, returning from his Arctic voyage, presented Queen Elizabeth with "a prodigious long horn of the Narwhale, which for a long period after hung in the castle at Windsor" (032-035).

Ishmael's physical description of the living animal is brief but vivid: "The Narwhale has a very picturesque, leopard-like look, being of a milk-white ground colour, dotted with round and oblong spots of black" (032-036). Its oil is "very superior, clear and fine," but there is little of it, and the species is seldom hunted. It is mostly found in the circumpolar seas.

## Appearances in the Novel

Outside Chapter 32, the Narwhale surfaces in significant moments.

In Chapter 30, Ishmael describes Ahab's ivory leg, fashioned from a sperm whale's jawbone. The association with narwhale tusks is implicit but resonant: "of the tusks of the narwhale. How could one look at Ahab then, seated on that tripod of bones, without bethinking him of the royalty it symbolized?" (030-002). The narwhale's horn becomes a figure for Ahab's own prosthetic — both are singular, one-sided projections of will.

The Mast-Head chapter (35) offers a lighter touch. Among the stray thoughts that drift through a watch-stander's mind, Ishmael imagines whalemen "popping off the stray narwhales, or vagrant sea unicorns infesting those waters" (035-006).

In the pictorial chapters (55–56), the Narwhale reappears as an example of representational failure and success. Chapter 55 dismisses "an alleged 'whale' and a 'narwhale'" in contemporary illustrations (055-008). But Chapter 56 offers qualified praise: a certain book contains "four delicate miniatures of narwhales and porpoises" (056-007), treating us to at least a glimpse of the real creature.

## Whaling Context

The narwhale was never a significant commercial target. Its oil, though fine, is sparse — nothing like the bounty of a sperm whale's case. Arctic whalers encountered narwhales incidentally while pursuing bowhead whales, and the tusks were brought back as curiosities rather than commodities.

The tusk trade, however, had a long and peculiar history. Throughout the Middle Ages and Renaissance, narwhal tusks were sold as unicorn horns, believed to neutralize poison and cure disease. European courts and cabinets of curiosity prized them above gold. The Danish throne — made partly of narwhal tusk — testifies to their prestige. Melville, with his encyclopedic reach, captures the tail end of this tradition in his Frobisher anecdote.
