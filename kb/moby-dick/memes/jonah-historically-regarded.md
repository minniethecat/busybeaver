# Jonah Historically Regarded

## Definition

The story of the prophet Jonah—swallowed by a "great fish" and vomited forth three days later—appears twice in *Moby-Dick*: first in Father Mapple's celebrated sermon in Chapter 9, where it serves as a parable of sin, repentance, and prophetic duty; and second in the brief but devastating Chapter 83 ("Jonah Historically Regarded"), where Melville dismantles both the skeptics' objections and the believers' defenses with equal comic vigor. Taken together, the two treatments form a double-faced meditation on the nature of biblical truth: Chapter 9 presents Jonah as existential drama, a model of repentance and the preacher's solemn burden, while Chapter 83 reframes the same story as a problem of cetological plausibility—could a whale actually swallow a man?—and in the process makes fools of both "foolish pride of reason" and credulous miracle-mongering alike.

## Manifestations in the Novel

### Father Mapple's Sermon (Chapter 9)

The novel's first sustained engagement with Jonah comes through Father Mapple, the former whaleman turned preacher, who delivers his sermon from a pulpit shaped like a ship's bow in the Whaleman's Chapel at New Bedford. Mapple preaches on "the last verse of the first chapter of Jonah—'And God had prepared a great fish to swallow up Jonah'" (009-025), calling the four-chapter book "one of the smallest strands in the mighty cable of the Scriptures" yet asking: "what depths of the soul does Jonah's deep sealine sound! what a pregnant lesson to us is this prophet!" (009-026).

Mapple's sermon unfolds as a "two-stranded lesson": a lesson to all as sinful men, and a lesson to himself "as a pilot of the living God" (009-026). The first strand follows Jonah's sin—not any specific transgression, but "wilful disobedience of the command of God" (009-026)—and his attempt to flee "world-wide from God" by taking ship at Joppa for Tarshish. Mapple's Jonah is an existential fugitive: "skulking from his God; prowling among the shipping like a vile burglar hastening to cross the seas" (009-027). The sermon's dramatic power lies in Mapple's performance—"when describing Jonah's sea-storm, seemed tossed by a storm himself. His deep chest heaved as with a ground-swell" (009-036)—turning the biblical text into visceral theater.

The second strand, aimed at himself, recasts Jonah as the reluctant prophet who fled from the duty of "sounding those unwelcome truths in the ears of a wicked Nineveh" (009-039). Mapple's closing peroration—"Woe to him who seeks to please rather than to appal! Woe to him whose good name is more to him than goodness!" (009-040)—transforms Jonah's story into a preacher's self-indictment and, by extension, a meditation on the cost of truth-telling that resonates with the novel's larger concerns.

> "Delight is to him—a far, far upward, and inward delight—who against the proud gods and commodores of this earth, ever stands forth his own inexorable self." (009-041)

### Sag-Harbor's Skepticism (Chapter 83)

Chapter 83, one of the novel's shortest, subjects the Jonah story to mock-historical analysis framed through the objections of "one old Sag-Harbor whaleman" (083-002). Melville catalogs four skeptical arguments, each more absurd than the last:

1. **The Two-Spouted Whale**: Sag-Harbor's illustrated Bible shows "Jonah's whale with two spouts in his head" (083-002)—an anatomical feature belonging only to the Right Whale, whose "swallow is so very small" that "a penny roll would choke him." The rebuttal: Bishop Jebb suggests Jonah was merely "temporarily lodged in some part of his mouth," which "would accommodate a couple of whist-tables, and comfortably seat all the players."

2. **The Gastric Juice Problem**: Sag-Harbor worries about Jonah's body and "the whale's gastric juices" (083-003). The answer: a German exegetist proposes Jonah took refuge in a *dead* whale—"even as the French soldiers in the Russian campaign turned their dead horses into tents, and crawled into them."

3. **The Figurehead Theory**: Continental commentators divine that Jonah escaped to "some vessel with a whale for a figure-head" (083-003), possibly called "The Whale"—so nothing supernatural occurred at all.

4. **The Geography Problem**: The Mediterranean-to-Nineveh distance is too great for a whale to cover in three days. The rebuttal: "He might have carried him round by the way of the Cape of Good Hope" (083-004)—a suggestion that would "wrest the honor of the discovery of that great headland from Bartholomew Diaz, its reputed discoverer, and so make modern history a liar."

The chapter's cruel punchline is that all these "foolish arguments" merely "evinced his foolish pride of reason" (083-005)—and that a Portuguese Catholic priest actually *embraced* the Cape of Good Hope route as "a signal magnification of the general miracle." Skepticism and credulity, Melville implies, are equally ridiculous when each refuses to see its own absurdity. The chapter ends with the image of a Turkish mosque built in honor of Jonah, containing "a miraculous lamp that burnt without any oil" (083-005)—a detail that suspends the reader between belief and skepticism.

## Key Passages

> "What depths of the soul does Jonah's deep sealine sound! what a pregnant lesson to us is this prophet! What a noble thing is that canticle in the fish's belly! How billow-like and boisterously grand! We feel the floods surging over us; we sound with him to the kelpy bottom of the waters; sea-weed and all the slime of the sea is about us!" (009-026)

> "In this world, shipmates, sin that pays its way can travel freely, and without a passport; whereas Virtue, if a pauper, is stopped at all frontiers." (009-029)

> "He feels that his dreadful punishment is just. He leaves all his deliverance to God, contenting himself with this, that spite of all his pains and pangs, he will still look towards His holy temple. And here, shipmates, is true and faithful repentance; not clamorous for pardon, but grateful for punishment." (009-035)

> "Woe to him who seeks to please rather than to appal! Woe to him whose good name is more to him than goodness! Woe to him who, in this world, courts not dishonor! Woe to him who would not be true, even though to be false were salvation!" (009-040)

> "But all these foolish arguments of old Sag-Harbor only evinced his foolish pride of reason—a thing still more reprehensible in him, seeing that he had but little learning except what he had picked up from the sun and the sea." (083-005)

## Scholarly Context

The two Jonah chapters exemplify Melville's method of treating biblical narrative simultaneously as spiritual Truth and as material for comic-cetological inquiry. Father Mapple's sermon has been read as Melville's homage to the oral sermon tradition of New England whaling ports—a performance within a performance that models the novel's own rhetorical mode. Critics have noted that Mapple's theology, with its emphasis on "disobeying ourselves" as the hard kernel of obedience to God, anticipates Ahab's own defiant relationship with the divine: both men insist on the sovereign self even in submission. Chapter 83, by contrast, has been called a miniature exercise in Higher Criticism avant la lettre: by subjecting the Jonah story to the same evidentiary standards whalemen apply to whales, Melville exposes the absurdity of literalist hermeneutics while also mocking rationalist debunking. The two treatments, taken together, refuse to resolve into a single "position"—a characteristic Melvillean strategy that leaves the reader, like Ishmael, suspended between faith and doubt.
