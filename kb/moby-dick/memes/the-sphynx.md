# The Sphynx

## Definition

The Sphynx is Melville's figure for the severed head of a sperm whale, suspended alongside the Pequod after decapitation. In the uncanny calm of noon, Ahab addresses the head as an oracular Sphinx, demanding that it reveal the secrets of the deep it has witnessed. The episode dramatizes the central epistemological crisis of the novel: nature is saturated with meaning yet refuses to speak—the Sphynx has seen everything and will say nothing.

## Manifestations in the Novel

The episode occurs in Chapter 70, after the Pequod has killed and processed a sperm whale. The head, "nearly one third of his entire bulk," cannot be hoisted aboard and is instead suspended against the ship's side, "blood-dripping," compared to "the giant Holofernes's [head] from the girdle of Judith." During the noon silence, Ahab emerges alone from his cabin, takes Stubb's long spade, and leans over the side to address the head.

"It was a black and hooded head," Melville writes, "and hanging there in the midst of so intense a calm, it seemed the Sphynx's in the desert." The simile is decisive: like the Egyptian Sphinx, the whale's head is vast, ancient, and impenetrable. Ahab's address—"Speak, thou vast and venerable head... tell us the secret thing that is in thee"—conflates the whale with all the mysteries of the deep. The head has "moved amid this world's foundations," witnessed "unrecorded names and navies," seen "the locked lovers when leaping from their flaming ship." It has access to a totality of experience that humans can only imagine.

The Sphynx's silence is the point. Unlike Oedipus, who answered the Sphinx's riddle and was rewarded, Ahab receives no reply. The whale head is not withholding; it is ontologically incapable of speech. Nature does not refuse to answer—it cannot, because answer implies language, and language is human. Ahab's frustration at this silence drives his metaphysical rage: he demands meaning from a universe that offers none.

## Key Passages

> "It was a black and hooded head; and hanging there in the midst of so intense a calm, it seemed the Sphynx's in the desert." (070-005)

The central simile that gives the chapter its name. The "intense calm" is crucial—silence, stillness, and the absence of wind create the conditions for Ahab's metaphysical confrontation.

> "Speak, thou vast and venerable head... tell us the secret thing that is in thee. Of all divers, thou hast dived the deepest. That head upon which the upper sun now gleams, has moved amid this world's foundations." (070-006)

Ahab's apostrophe to the head. Note the paradox: he calls it "venerable" (worthy of reverence) yet demands that it serve him by speaking. The head's depth of experience—"thou hast dived the deepest"—makes its silence all the more maddening.

> "Thou saw'st the locked lovers when leaping from their flaming ship; heart to heart they sank beneath the exulting wave; true to each other, when heaven seemed false to them." (070-007)

Ahab catalogs what the head has witnessed—lovers drowning, murdered mates, pirates—a litany of oceanic death. The head is a repository of trauma, and its refusal to bear witness indicts the cosmos.

> "O head! thou hast seen enough to split the planets and make an infidel of Abraham, and not one syllable is thine!" (070-007)

The climactic exclamation. The head's accumulated vision would be enough to destroy faith itself—"make an infidel of Abraham"—yet it produces no testimony. This is the novel's bleakest epistemological statement: experience generates no wisdom, only silence.

## Scholarly Context

The Sphynx chapter has been extensively analyzed in Melville criticism, often in relation to Romantic and post-Kantian epistemology. The episode enacts what critics identify as the novel's central philosophical problem: the gap between empirical observation and meaning. The whale head—visible, tangible, immense—is the ultimate object of knowledge that nevertheless yields no knowledge. Some readers connect the episode to Melville's reading of Shakespeare, particularly *Hamlet* (Yorick's skull) and *Macbeth*; others see it as a response to Emersonian transparency, insisting instead on nature's opacity.
