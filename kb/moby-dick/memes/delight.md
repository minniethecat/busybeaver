# Delight

## Overview

The Delight is the last whaleship the [[pequod|Pequod]] meets before the final three-day chase of [[moby-dick|Moby Dick]]. Her name is a bitter irony: she is "most miserably misnamed the Delight" (131-001).

When the Pequod encounters her, the Delight has just lost a whaleboat and its entire crew to the White Whale. She is a floating warning — a preview of what awaits the Pequod — but Ahab refuses to read the omen.

## The Ship and Her Tragedy

The Delight carries the evidence of her disaster. A shattered life-buoy hangs from her stern — smashed by Moby Dick in the same encounter that took the whaleboat.

"Who is it? Who has lost a whaleboat?" Ahab calls out. The answer comes back: the White Whale has taken a boat and all its men. The Delight's crew is burying their dead at sea as the Pequod approaches.

The Delight's captain is unnamed. His grief speaks for him: he has lost men to the same whale Ahab pursues, and his only counsel is silence.

## Encounter with the Pequod

The encounter is brief and dreadful. As the Delight draws near, the Pequod's crew can see the ship's life-buoy, broken and dangling — "the strange life-buoy hanging at the Pequod's stern came into violent play" (131-011).

The Delight's crew call out a warning, but their voices are swallowed by the wind. Ahab, standing on his quarter-deck, does not heed them.

A body — a sailor being buried at sea — splashes into the water between the two ships. It is one of the Delight's dead. The Pequod sails past without stopping.

## Key Passages

- (131-001) — "Most miserably misnamed the Delight, was descried" — Melville's grim irony
- (131-011) — "The dejected Delight, the strange life-buoy hanging at her stern" — the emblem of destruction
- (007-010) — The word "delight" in the novel's lexicon: ambivalent temptation to embark
- (009-041) — "Delight is to him — a far, far upward, and inward delight" — the word's deeper meaning in Melville

## Fate

The Delight survives the encounter with Moby Dick, though with heavy losses. She sails on, diminished.

Her role in the novel is to be the final human warning. She is the last voice counseling prudence before the Pequod plunges into its fatal chase.

## Symbolic Significance

The Delight is the novel's most concentrated example of Melville's ironic naming. Nothing about this ship is delightful. Her crew is dead or grieving. Her life-buoy — the symbol of survival — is broken.

The shattered life-buoy hangs in the novel's symbolic economy as a direct prefiguration of the Pequod's fate. The Pequod's own life-buoy will later prove useless; only [[queequeg|Queequeg]]'s coffin will serve as a buoy, saving [[ishmael|Ishmael]].

The Delight episode also marks the point of no return. After this gam, there are no more warnings. The chase begins.
