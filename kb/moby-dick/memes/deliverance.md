# Deliverance

## Definition

Deliverance in *Moby-Dick* is a word Melville uses with deliberate theological weight and deliberate physical specificity, forcing the two registers into productive collision. The biblical Jonah prays for deliverance from the whale's belly, and Father Mapple's sermon lingers on "the deliverance and joy of Jonah" (009-026) as the paradigm of salvation. But in Chapter 78, Tashtego falls into a whale's head and is rescued by Queequeg in an act Melville describes as "the deliverance, or rather, delivery of Tashtego" (078-012). The revision — "or rather, delivery" — is crucial: Melville insists we hear the obstetrical meaning, the birth meaning, the physical meaning. Deliverance is not an abstract spiritual transaction; it is a body pulled from a cavity, a birth canal reversed, a life physically re-grasped. The novel's deepest claim is that all deliverance, even the theological, is ultimately physical — a hand reaching into darkness and pulling something living out.

## Manifestations in the Novel

**Jonah's deliverance (Chapter 9)**. Father Mapple's sermon on Jonah is the novel's most concentrated meditation on deliverance, and its structure is instructive: Jonah runs from God, is swallowed by the whale, repents, and is delivered. But Father Mapple's emphasis is not on God's mercy but on Jonah's predicament — the "tormented deep" (009-032), the "belly of hell" (009-039) — and on the physicality of the whale. Jonah's deliverance is preceded by confession: "he feels that his dreadful punishment is just" (009-035). The moral is that deliverance requires acknowledgment of guilt, and the acknowledgment must be as physical as the punishment. Jonah does not think his way out of the whale; he prays, and prayer for Father Mapple is a bodily act — "woe to him who seeks to please rather than to appal" (009-001).

**The prayer for direct deliverance refused (Chapter 9)**. Father Mapple's most severe moment comes when he declares that Jonah "did not weep and wail for direct deliverance" (009-035). The refusal of easy rescue is the sermon's ethical core: Jonah does not ask God to simply extract him from the whale; he acknowledges his culpability first. The phrase "direct deliverance" implies that there is an indirect deliverance, a mediated deliverance — perhaps the deliverance that comes through story, through confession, through the telling of one's own engulfment. Ishmael's narrative is itself a form of indirect deliverance: he tells the story because he survived, and his survival is the story's enabling condition.

**Tashtego's delivery (Chapter 78)**. The novel's most vivid and strange deliverance scene occurs when Tashtego, the Gay-Head harpooneer, falls into the cavity of a whale's head and is trapped there as the head sinks. Queequeg, acting as "obstetrician" (the word is Melville's), reaches into the whale's head and pulls Tashtego out by the leg: "the deliverance, or rather, delivery of Tashtego" (078-012). The pun on "deliverance/delivery" is not a joke; it is Melville's most compressed theological statement. To be delivered is to be born — born again, but not in the Christian sense. Tashtego emerges from a whale's head as from a womb, covered in spermaceti, "with something of a sort of a Feegee look" (078-013). Deliverance is messy, bodily, undignified. It is a midwife's work, not a priest's. Queequeg is the novel's true deliverer — not God, not Ahab, but the tattooed harpooneer whose arm reaches into death and pulls out life.

**Deliverance deferred: the Pequod's crew (Chapter 135)**. The novel pointedly denies deliverance to the Pequod's crew. Ishmael alone is delivered — by the coffin-lifebuoy, by the *Rachel*, by sheer contingency. The crew's collective death is the novel's counter-testament to Jonah: they do not repent, and they are not delivered. Ahab's monomania makes repentance impossible; he does not acknowledge guilt but project it onto the whale. Father Mapple's condition — "he did not weep and wail for direct deliverance" — is inverted: Ahab weeps and wails, but not for deliverance; he weeps and wails for vengeance. The crew are not delivered because their captain has foreclosed the possibility, and the novel's cosmos offers no deliverance to those who will not ask for it.

**Deliverance and joy (Chapter 9)**. Father Mapple's phrase "the deliverance and joy of Jonah" (009-026) links the two concepts inseparably. Deliverance produces joy; joy is the sign that deliverance has occurred. But the novel is suspicious of joy — Ishmael's opening confession locates joy in the distant past, in the "deep joy in his eyes" (009-041) that belongs to another man and another time. Deliverance in *Moby-Dick* is always partial, always shadowed by the knowledge that others were not delivered. Ishmael's joy at surviving is inseparable from his grief at being the only survivor. The "and" in "deliverance and joy" is fragile; deliverance can occur without joy, and joy can occur without deliverance — but the novel holds them together as a promise it cannot quite fulfill.

## Key Passages

- (009-026) — "prayers, and finally the deliverance and joy of Jonah": deliverance as the sermon's terminal point, the goal toward which Jonah's ordeal tends
- (009-035) — "did not weep and wail for direct deliverance": the refusal of easy rescue, the ethical condition of deliverance
- (009-041) — "a deep joy in his eyes": the sign of genuine deliverance in another, observed but not shared
- (078-012) — "the deliverance, or rather, delivery of Tashtego": the novel's great pun, deliverance collapsed into birth
- (078-013) — "with something of a sort of a Feegee look": the delivered man is changed, marked by his passage through the whale
- (Ch.135) — Ishmael's deliverance by coffin-lifebuoy: the physical residue of death becomes the instrument of the novel's only successful rescue

## Scholarly Context

Deliverance in *Moby-Dick* has been read through several interpretive frames. Theological approaches emphasize Melville's engagement with the Jonah narrative as a type of Christ's resurrection — deliverance from the whale as figura of deliverance from death. But Melville's insertion of the Tashtego "delivery" scene deliberately undercuts typological reading: if Jonah prefigures Christ, Tashtego's undignified extraction from a whale's head prefigures nothing. Gender-critical readings focus on the obstetrical imagery of Chapter 78, reading Queequeg as a male midwife whose "delivery" of Tashtego queers the boundaries of gender, profession, and species. Postcolonial approaches note that Tashtego is Native American, and his "deliverance" by Queequeg (a Pacific Islander) enacts a cross-indigenous solidarity that bypasses the white power structure of the ship — deliverance here is not from above but from alongside, not from God but from a fellow harpooneer.
