# The Pulpit

## Definition

The pulpit in the Whaleman's Chapel at New Bedford is no ordinary preaching platform. It is a ship-shaped structure, accessed by a rope ladder that Father Mapple climbs and then draws up after himself — an act that transforms the chapel into a vessel and the sermon into a voyage.

> What could be more full of meaning? — for the pulpit is ever this earth's foremost part; all the rest comes in its rear; the pulpit leads the world. (008-007)

Chapter 8, "The Pulpit," is devoted almost entirely to this architectural marvel, making it one of the novel's most concentrated exercises in symbolic description.

## Manifestations in the Novel

### The Rope Ladder

Father Mapple's pulpit is "a very lofty one" — so high that a regular staircase would consume too much space. Instead, a side ladder of "cloth-covered rope" provides access. Once the preacher ascends, he "slowly turning over the ladder to one side, with a most authoritative gesture" (008-002) draws it up behind him.

This gesture is rich with maritime symbolism: it is the action of a captain drawing up the ship's boarding ladder, severing connection with the shore. Father Mapple, a former whaleman himself, has literally and figuratively gone to sea. Ishmael reflects:

> I pondered some time without fully comprehending the reason for this. Father Mapple enjoyed such a wide reputation for sincerity and sanctity, that I could not suspect him of courting notoriety by any mere tricks of the stage. (008-004)

### The Ship-Shaped Structure

The pulpit's form is explicitly nautical:

> Its panelled front was in the likeness of a ship's bluff bows, and the Holy Bible rested on a projecting piece of scroll work, fashioned after a ship's fiddle-headed beak. (008-006)

The Bible — the "prow" of the pulpit-ship — cuts through the congregation like a vessel through waves. The identification of preaching with seafaring is complete.

### The Cenotaphs

On either side of the pulpit, marble tablets memorialize whalemen lost at sea:

> Between the marble cenotaphs on either hand of the pulpit, the wall which formed its back was adorned with a large painting representing a gallant ship beating against a terrible storm off a lee coast. (008-005)

These cenotaphs frame every sermon with the fact of death. The preacher speaks from within an architecture of mourning, addressing a congregation of men who may soon join the memorialized dead.

### Father Mapple's Ascent

The chapter opens with Father Mapple's entrance — a man of "venerable robustness" whose physical presence authenticates the pulpit's nautical symbolism:

> I had not been seated very long ere a man of a certain venerable robustness entered; immediately as the storm-pelted door flew open, he quietly approached the pulpit. (008-001)

He has earned the right to preach from a ship.

### The Sermon to Come

The pulpit chapter prepares for Chapter 9, "The Sermon," in which Father Mapple preaches on Jonah and the Whale. The architecture serves the theology: just as Jonah was swallowed and delivered, the congregation sits within a symbolic whale-ship, awaiting the word that will give their perilous lives meaning.

## Key Passages

- (008-001) — Father Mapple's entrance: a man shaped by the sea who "quietly approached the pulpit."
- (008-002) — The rope ladder and the drawing-up: "like a ship's boarding ladder."
- (008-004) — Ishmael ponders the symbolism: "its isolation from all worldly contact."
- (008-005) — The cenotaphs on either side and the storm painting.
- (008-006) — The pulpit's panelled front "in the likeness of a ship's bluff bows," the Bible as its figurehead.
- (008-007) — The chapter's culminating declaration: "the pulpit is ever this earth's foremost part."

## Scholarly Context

The Pulpit chapter exemplifies Melville's method of symbolic reading — taking a physical object and unfolding its meanings layer by layer. Critics have noted the typological dimension (the pulpit as a type of the Church sailing through the world's storms), the biographical resonance (Melville's ambivalent relationship to Calvinist preaching), and the architectural significance (the ship-church as a uniquely New England fusion of maritime and religious culture).
