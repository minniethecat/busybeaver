# Father Mapple's Sermon

## Overview

In Chapters 7 through 9, [[ishmael|Ishmael]] visits the Whaleman's Chapel in New Bedford on the eve of his whaling voyage. There he encounters the famous [[father-mapple|Father Mapple]], a former harpooneer turned chaplain, who delivers a thundering sermon on the Book of [[jonah|Jonah]]. The sermon — one of the novel's most frequently anthologized passages — uses Jonah's flight from God, his swallowing by the whale, and his eventual deliverance as a framework for Melville's meditation on sin, repentance, obedience, and the prophetic calling.

The three-chapter sequence functions as a narrative prologue in miniature. Father Mapple's sermon foreshadows the Pequod's voyage on multiple levels: the "great fish" that swallows Jonah prefigures [[the-white-whale|Moby Dick]]; Jonah's wilful disobedience mirrors [[ahab|Ahab]]'s defiance; and the sermon's closing exhortation — "woe to him who seeks to please rather than to appal!" (009-040) — becomes a template for Ahab's own rhetorical intensity on the quarter-deck. The chapel itself, with its memorial tablets to dead whalemen and its pulpit shaped like a ship's prow, establishes whaling as an enterprise conducted in the constant presence of death.

## Chronology

### The Chapel (Chapter 7)

[[ishmael|Ishmael]] enters the Whaleman's Chapel on a stormy Sunday morning and finds "a small scattered congregation of sailors, and sailors' wives and widows" (007-002). The atmosphere is funereal: "each silent worshipper seemed purposely sitting apart from the other, as if each silent grief were insular and incommunicable" (007-002). On the walls hang marble tablets memorializing whalemen lost at sea:

> "SACRED TO THE MEMORY OF JOHN TALBOT, Who, at the age of eighteen, was lost overboard, Near the Isle of Desolation, off Patagonia, November 1st, 1836." (007-003)

Another tablet records a boat's crew of six men "towed out of sight by a Whale" (007-004). These cenotaphs — graves without bodies — establish the "speechlessly quick chaotic bundling of a man into Eternity" (007-010) that defines whaling life. Yet Ishmael ends the chapter on a note of defiance: "methinks my body is but the lees of my better being. In fact take my body who will, take it I say, it is not me" (007-010).

### The Pulpit (Chapter 8)

[[father-mapple|Father Mapple]] enters, his "tarpaulin hat ran down with melting sleet, and his great pilot cloth jacket seemed almost to drag him to the floor with the weight of the water it had absorbed" (008-001). A former sailor and harpooneer, he embodies the fusion of maritime and spiritual authority that defines the novel's worldview.

The pulpit itself is a ship-shaped structure, accessed by a rope side-ladder "like those used in mounting a ship from a boat at sea" (008-002). Once ascended, the chaplain pulls the ladder up after himself, making the pulpit "a self-containing stronghold — a lofty Ehrenbreitstein, with a perennial well of water within the walls" (008-004). This act of isolation symbolizes the preacher's "spiritual withdrawal for the time, from all outward worldly ties and connexions" (008-004).

The chapter's closing lines announce its central metaphor:

> "For the pulpit is ever this earth's foremost part; all the rest comes in its rear; the pulpit leads the world. … Yes, the world's a ship on its passage out, and not a voyage complete; and the pulpit is its prow." (008-007)

### The Sermon (Chapter 9)

Father Mapple takes as his text "the last verse of the first chapter of Jonah — 'And God had prepared a great fish to swallow up Jonah'" (009-025). The sermon is a bravura performance, retelling the Jonah story with intense dramatic embellishment while extracting from it a "two-stranded lesson":

**The first strand — a lesson to all sinners.** Jonah's sin is "wilful disobedience of the command of God" (009-026). He flees to Tarshish, but "God is everywhere; Tarshish he never reached" (009-039). The storm, the casting of lots, and the swallowing by the whale are not punishments but vehicles of repentance:

> "Sin not; but if you do, take heed to repent of it like Jonah." (009-035)

Melville's Father Mapple emphasizes that "Jonah does not weep and wail for direct deliverance. He feels that his dreadful punishment is just. … And here, shipmates, is true and faithful repentance; not clamorous for pardon, but grateful for punishment" (009-035).

**The second strand — a lesson to the pilot-prophet.** Jonah was "bidden by the Lord to sound those unwelcome truths in the ears of a wicked Nineveh" but "fled from his mission, and sought to escape his duty and his God" (009-039). This is the burden of the preacher himself — and, by extension, of any who would speak truth to power:

> "Woe to him who seeks to please rather than to appal! Woe to him whose good name is more to him than goodness! Woe to him who, in this world, courts not dishonor! Woe to him who would not be true, even though to be false were salvation!" (009-040)

### The Closing Exultation

The sermon ends not in terror but in ecstasy. Father Mapple lifts his face and cries out:

> "Delight is to him — a far, far upward, and inward delight — who against the proud gods and commodores of this earth, ever stands forth his own inexorable self." (009-041)

This passage is one of the novel's definitive statements of Melvillean heroism: the solitary self standing against heaven and earth, acknowledging "no law or lord, but the Lord his God, and is only a patriot to heaven" (009-041). The sermon's rhetorical arc — from terror through repentance to exultant self-assertion — prefigures the emotional trajectory of Ahab's own speeches.

## Narrative Significance

The three-chapter Whaleman's Chapel sequence is one of the most densely layered passages in the novel. It operates on at least four levels simultaneously:

**As structural foreshadowing.** The Jonah story — flight, storm, swallowing by the whale, repentance in the belly, deliverance — maps almost precisely onto the novel's larger plot. Ahab is a Jonah who refuses repentance; the Pequod is a Tarshish-ship carrying contraband; the White Whale is "the great fish" that swallows but does not release.

**As theological framework.** Father Mapple's sermon articulates a distinctive theology: a God who commands rather than persuades ("he oftener commands us than endeavors to persuade" [009-026]), who punishes not to destroy but to deliver, and whose ultimate requirement is that His prophets "Preach the Truth to the face of Falsehood!" (009-039). This theology, with its emphasis on defiance of worldly authority and absolute fidelity to a higher calling, directly informs Ahab's self-conception.

**As character introduction.** Though the sermon is ostensibly about Jonah, it is equally about Father Mapple himself — "a greater sinner than ye" (009-039) who has himself fled from God and been delivered. His physical presence during the sermon — "his deep chest heaved as with a ground-swell; his tossed arms seemed the warring elements at work" (009-036) — makes him a living emblem of the spiritual storms he describes.

**As rhetorical model.** The sermon's structure — from narrative reenactment (Ch.9, 009-025 through 009-035) through personal confession (009-039) to ecstatic exhortation (009-041) — establishes the rhetorical template that Ahab will deploy throughout the novel, most notably in "The Quarter-Deck" (Ch.36).

## Aftermath / Consequences

The sermon is Ishmael's last spiritual preparation before shipping aboard the Pequod. It is alluded to once more in the novel when Ishmael describes Queequeg's story of his first encounter with whaling as a kind of sermon: "Deacon Deuteronomy — why Father Mapple himself couldn't beat it" (018-013). This playful callback positions Queequeg's "pagan" narrative as a counterpart to Mapple's Christian one — two versions of the same story of conversion to the whale.

The chapel's marble tablets return implicitly in the novel's final pages, when the Pequod sinks and "the great shroud of the sea rolled on as it rolled five thousand years ago" (135-029). The cenotaphs in the chapel are the novel's first intimation that the sea buries without memorial.
