# The Lamp

## "The Lamp" — A Neglected Chapter

Chapter 97 of *Moby-Dick* — "The Lamp" — is one of the novel's shortest chapters, a mere two paragraphs sandwiched between "The Try-Works" (Ch.96) and "Stowing Down and Clearing Up" (Ch.98). Its placement is not incidental: coming immediately after the infernal spectacle of the try-works at midnight and immediately before the businesslike accounting of stowing the processed oil, "The Lamp" offers a brief, luminous meditation on the closed economy of whale-oil light. The chapter is almost never discussed in critical literature, yet it performs an essential symbolic function: it traces the product of whaling labor — oil — back into the conditions of that labor's performance, revealing the circularity by which the whale illuminates its own destruction.

Ishmael opens the chapter with a direct statement of this circularity: "Had you descended from the Pequod's try-works to the Pequod's forecastle, where the off-duty watch were sleeping, for one single moment you would have almost thought you were standing in some illuminated shrine of canonized kings and counsellors" (097-001). The forecastle, lit by whale-oil lamps, becomes a shrine — but a shrine consecrated not to kings but to the whale itself, whose rendered fat provides the illumination.

## The Closed Economy of Light

The lamp in *Moby-Dick* participates in what might be called a closed symbolic economy. Whalemen kill whales, render their blubber into oil, and burn that oil to light their own labor — reading charts, sharpening lances, standing watch. The whale illuminates the hunt for more whales. This circularity is not merely practical but metaphysical: the whale's substance becomes the medium through which the whaleman perceives his world, so that the hunted creature quite literally conditions the hunter's reality.

Melville makes this circularity explicit in the chapter's second paragraph: "But what thinks Lazarus? Can he warm his blue hands by holding them up to the grand northern lights? Would not Lazarus rather be in Sumatra than here? Would he not far rather lay him down lengthwise along the line of the Equator?" (097-002). The reference to Lazarus — the biblical figure raised from the dead — introduces a resurrection motif into the economy of light. The whale dies, is rendered into oil, and in burning that oil the whaleman experiences a kind of illumination that is also a reminder of death. The whale's light is the light of a corpse.

## The Lamp's Many Forms

Throughout the novel, lamps appear in varied configurations, each carrying a distinct symbolic charge:

**The Spouter-Inn lamp.** Ishmael's first encounter with whale-oil light occurs before he ever sets foot on a whaleship. At the Spouter-Inn, a "dull red lamp swinging there" (015-003) casts its light over the collection of whaling relics, the obscure painting, and the strange landlord. The inn's lamp is colored — "dull red" — and its light is insufficient, failing to make the world fully legible. It anticipates the partial illuminations that whaling will provide throughout the novel: always enough light to see by, never enough to understand.

**The binnacle lamp.** Ahab smokes his pipe at the binnacle lamp — "Lighting the pipe at the binnacle lamp and planting the stool on the weather side of the deck" (030-001). The binnacle lamp is the ship's navigational light, the flame by which the compass is read. Ahab's use of it for the private ritual of smoking — before renouncing the pipe and casting it into the sea — makes the binnacle lamp a threshold symbol: it marks the boundary between ordinary comfort (the pipe) and obsessive navigation (the compass). When Ahab later destroys the quadrant and navigates by dead reckoning, the binnacle lamp's loss of authority is the unstated premise.

**The try-works lamp.** The most intense lamp in the novel is not a lamp at all but the try-works itself — the furnace on deck where whale blubber is rendered into oil (see [[try-works]]). Ishmael describes the try-works at night as a scene of infernal illumination: the flames leap, the boiling oil casts a "horrible oiliness" over everything, and the Pequod becomes a "red hell." The try-works is the lamp at its most primal — the fire that produces the oil that will later burn quietly in forecastle and cabin. Chapter 97's "The Lamp" is the necessary complement to Chapter 96's "The Try-Works": the peaceful aftermath of the infernal process.

**The forecastle lamp.** "But the only true lamp," Melville writes in Ch.97, "the lamp that burnt the brightest and the purest, was the lamp that burnt in the forecastle." The forecastle lamp illuminates the sleeping sailors — the "off-duty watch" — and its light, Ishmael claims, transforms them into "canonized kings and counsellors." The lamp democratizes: in its light, the common sailor becomes royalty. But this democratization is purchased at the cost of whale life — the lamp burns whale oil, and the whalemen who sleep beneath it are the agents of that death.

## Lamp-Feeder: The Insult and the Reality

In one of the novel's most revealing insults, the German whaleman of the *Jungfrau* is called a "lamp-feeder" — "Impossible! — a lamp-feeder!" (081-004) cries the German captain, and Flask retorts: "it's a lamp-feeder and an oil-can. He's out of oil!" (081-006). The insult reduces the whaleman to the product of his labor: he is not a hunter but merely the means by which lamps are fed. But in the symbolic economy Melville has constructed, Flask's insult is also an unwitting revelation: every whaleman *is* a lamp-feeder, whether he knows it or not. The entire enterprise of whaling, from the harpoon to the try-works to the cabin lamp, is a mechanism for converting whale life into human light.

This conversion is not innocent. "A lamp-feeder and an oil-can" — Flask's words collapse the distinction between the whale's body (oil-can) and the whaleman's function (lamp-feeder). The whaleman becomes indistinguishable from the whale he processes, just as Ahab becomes, in his own soliloquy, indistinguishable from the dying whale whose "final jets were the strongest and fullest of trouble" (030-003).

## Scholarly Context

The lamp has received far less critical attention than the try-works or the doubloon, yet it is arguably Melville's most economical symbol for the relationship between labor, illumination, and death. Howard P. Vincent, in *The Trying-Out of Moby-Dick* (1949), notes the influence of Whaling Museum artifacts on Melville's lamp imagery; more recently, critics working in ecocritical and energy-humanities frameworks have begun to read the lamp as a figure for extractive economy — the whale as fossil fuel avant la lettre, its body converted into illumination that blinds as much as it reveals.

The lamp also participates in the novel's broader meditation on whiteness. The flame of the whale-oil lamp burns white, and Melville's insistence on the "purest" light of the forecastle lamp echoes the whiteness that Ishmael elsewhere describes as the most terrifying color in nature (see [[whiteness-of-the-whale]]). The lamp purifies — and purification, in *Moby-Dick*, is never innocent.
