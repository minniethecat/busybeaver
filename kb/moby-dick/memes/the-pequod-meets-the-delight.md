# The Pequod Meets the Delight

## Overview

The Pequod Meets the Delight (Chapter 131) is the last of the nine gams in *Moby-Dick*, and the bleakest. The whaler *Delight* — "most miserably misnamed" — has just encountered [[the-white-whale|Moby Dick]] and lost five men. As the Pequod approaches, the Delight's crew is sewing a corpse into a hammock for burial at sea. The ship's shears display "the shattered, white ribs, and some few splintered planks, of what had once been a whale-boat" — a visible wreck that answers Ahab's question before it is asked (131-002).

This gam is different from all the others. There is no extended conversation, no storytelling, no negotiation. Ahab asks: "Hast seen the White Whale?" The captain points to the wreck. Ahab asks: "Hast killed him?" The captain answers: "The harpoon is not yet forged that ever will do that" (131-004–006).

Ahab, brandishing his own harpoon, declares it tempered for the whale's death. But even as he speaks, the Delight's crew heaves the corpse into the sea. Ahab orders the Pequod to sail on before the splash reaches them — but "the suddenly started Pequod was not quick enough to escape the sound of the splash that the corpse soon made as it struck the sea" (131-010).

The Delight's final cry is the last human voice Ahab will hear from outside the Pequod: "In vain, oh, ye strangers, ye fly our sad burial; ye but turn us your taffrail to show us your coffin!" (131-012) — pointing to the life-buoy coffin hanging at the Pequod's stern. It is the novel's most concentrated foreshadowing. The next four chapters are the chase.

## Chronology

### The Wreck

The Delight approaches. Her shears display a destroyed whaleboat — "but you now saw through this wreck, as plainly as you see through the peeled, half-unhinged, and bleaching skeleton of a horse" (131-002).

### The Exchange

Ahab's first question is, as always: "Hast seen the White Whale?" The Delight's captain, "hollow-cheeked," points wordlessly to the wreck (131-003–004). Ahab's second question — "Hast killed him?" — receives an answer that is also a prophecy: "The harpoon is not yet forged that ever will do that" (131-005–006).

Ahab seizes Perth's iron and holds it aloft: "here in this hand I hold his death! Tempered in blood, and tempered by lightning are these barbs; and I swear to temper them triply in that hot place behind the fin, where the White Whale most feels his accursed life!" (131-007).

The Delight's captain points to the corpse: "I bury but one of five stout men, who were alive only yesterday; but were dead ere night. Only *that* one I bury; the rest were buried before they died; you sail upon their tomb" (131-008).

### The Burial

The corpse is committed to the sea. Ahab orders the Pequod forward, trying to outrun the splash. He fails: the sound reaches them, and "some of the flying bubbles might have sprinkled her hull with their ghostly baptism" (131-010).

### The Coffin

As the ships diverge, a voice from the Delight cries out, pointing at Queequeg's coffin-life-buoy hanging at the Pequod's stern: "In vain, oh, ye strangers, ye fly our sad burial; ye but turn us your taffrail to show us your coffin!" (131-012). It is the novel's most explicit foreshadowing: the Pequod already carries its own death with it.

## Narrative Significance

The Delight gam is the structural negative of the earlier gams. The Bachelor (Chapter 115) was all joy and celebration; the Delight is all grief. The Rachel (Chapter 128) was a plea for help; the Delight is a warning that needs no words. Where the earlier gams offered Ahab choices — help the Rachel, celebrate with the Bachelor, heed the Jeroboam's Gabriel — the Delight offers none. It is simply what happens when you meet Moby Dick.

The chapter's brevity is its power. At roughly one page in most editions, it is the shortest gam, and the most efficient. Every detail is a portent: the shattered boat, the sewing of the shroud, the splash of the corpse, the coffin at the Pequod's stern. Melville strips the gam form to its essence: a question, a warning, a death, and a coffin.

## Aftermath / Consequences

The Delight is the last ship the Pequod meets. After this gam, there are no more outside voices, no more warnings, no more choices. The next four chapters (132–135) are the chase. The Delight's prophecy — "The harpoon is not yet forged that ever will do that" — proves true. Ahab harpoons Moby Dick but does not kill him. The whale kills Ahab and destroys the Pequod.
