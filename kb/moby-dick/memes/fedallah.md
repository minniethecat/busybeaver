# Fedallah

## Overview

Fedallah is the mysterious Parsee (Zoroastrian) harpooneer whom [[Ahab]] secretly smuggles aboard the Pequod before its departure. He leads Ahab's private whale-boat crew — a "tiger-yellow" company of five "phantoms" — and remains a "muffled mystery" (050-008) to the rest of the ship for much of the voyage. Fedallah is Ahab's shadow-self, his "evil shadow" as [[Stubb]] calls him, and the dark embodiment of the captain's monomania. His prophecies — that Ahab will die only after seeing a hearse not made by mortal hands and a hemp rope, and that Fedallah himself will die first and appear again — structure the novel's supernatural climax.

## Role in the Narrative

Fedallah appears only after the Pequod has been at sea for some time, emerging from the hold with his crew in [[Ch. 48: The First Lowering]](048-002). His presence unsettles the crew: "that hair-turbaned Fedallah remained a muffled mystery to the last" (050-008). He is the first to sight the Spirit Spout in [[Ch. 51: The Spirit Spout]](051-002), reinforcing his association with the uncanny and prophetic.

In [[Ch. 73: Stubb and Flask Kill a Right Whale]](073-010–073-042), Stubb and Flask debate Fedallah's nature at length — is he the devil in disguise? How old is he? Is he Ahab's kidnapper or servant? This comic interlude masks genuine unease: Fedallah represents something genuinely alien aboard the Pequod.

Fedallah's most important function is as the vehicle of the **prophecies** that drive Ahab's final days. His death on the first day of [[the-chase]] — entangled in whale-line and lashed to [[Moby Dick]]'s back, a "hearse not made by mortal hands" — fulfills the first part of his prophecy. His reappearance, dead and staring, lashed to the whale on the third day, fulfills the second, confirming to Ahab that his own death is imminent.

## Character Traits

Fedallah embodies **orientalist mystery** as nineteenth-century American literature understood it. He is a Parsee — a Zoroastrian fire-worshipper — which to Melville's Christian crew marks him as both exotic and demonic. His "hair-turbaned" head, his silence, his agelessness (Stubb estimates him impossibly old at "a hundred years" [073-025]), and his apparent foresight all associate him with the supernatural.

He is Ahab's **shadow** — a physical manifestation of the captain's hidden will. Where Ahab is all speech and fury, Fedallah is silence and stillness. He never addresses the crew directly; his words are reserved for Ahab alone (100-040–100-041). This muteness makes him more terrifying: he is the unspoken, the repressed, the thing Ahab will not acknowledge.

His **prophetic function** is crucial. The prophecies he delivers — presumably to Ahab, though we never hear them directly — give Ahab a false sense of invulnerability. Ahab interprets "hearse not made by mortal hands" and "hemp rope" as impossible conditions, but Fedallah's own death supplies the hearse (his corpse lashed to the whale), and a harpoon line supplies the hemp. The prophecy is fulfilled not despite but through Fedallah.

## Relationships

- **[[Ahab]]**: Fedallah is Ahab's shadow, his secret sharer, the dark half of his soul. Ahab brought him aboard in secret, and in the end Fedallah's death prefigures and guarantees Ahab's own. Their bond is the most intimate and the most sinister in the novel.
- **[[Stubb]]**: Stubb is simultaneously terrified of and fascinated by Fedallah. His comic interrogation in Chapter 73 ("I take that Fedallah to be the devil in disguise") masks real fear.
- **[[Flask]]**: Flask shares Stubb's suspicions — "Do you suppose Fedallah wants to kidnap Captain Ahab?" (073-032).
- **Fedallah's crew**: The five "tiger-yellow" phantoms who serve as his boat crew are unnamed except for their ethnic otherness. They share Fedallah's silence and mystery.
- **[[Moby Dick]]**: Fedallah's fatal entanglement with the White Whale confirms the whale as an agent of fate, converting Fedallah's own body into the prophesied hearse.
