# Spout

## Definition

The spout—also called the blowhole, fountain, or "spout-hole"—is the whale's defining anatomical feature in Herman Melville's Moby-Dick. Melville establishes it as the very criterion of whalehood: "a whale is a spouting fish with a horizontal tail" （032-014）. Far more than a mere physiological trait, the spout becomes a site of philosophical inquiry, a symbol of the whale's inscrutable interiority, and a navigational marker that drives the plot.

## Cetological Treatment

Melville devotes Chapter 85, "The Fountain," to a sustained examination of the whale's spout. He surveys competing theories of its nature—whether it is water expelled or vapor exhaled—and advances his own hypothesis:

- **The Mist Theory**: Melville's narrator proposes "that the spout is nothing but mist" （085-011）, a vapor produced when the whale's warm breath meets the cold ocean air.
- **Anatomical Description**: The spouting canal of the Sperm Whale "for the first few feet is horizontal" before ascending to the top of the head （085-007）. Melville notes that the Right Whale has "two external spout-holes" while the Sperm Whale has "only one" （075-009）.
- **Breathing Mechanism**: The whale "only breathes through his spout-hole" （085-005）, and the canal is connected internally to its respiratory system.

The spout's taxonomy extends to smaller cetaceans: Melville notes that a porpoise "spouts" as well, though "his spout is so small" it is often overlooked （032-042）.

## Symbolic Significance

### The Midnight Spout

In Chapter 51, "The Spirit-Spout," Melville introduces the mysterious midnight spout—a solitary jet observed at night across vast ocean distances. "This midnight-spout had almost grown a forgotten thing" （051-004） until Ishmael speculates that across all "latitudes and longitudes, that unnearable spout was cast by one self-same whale" （051-005）, anticipating the revelation of Moby Dick's singular pursuit.

### Ahab's Spout

During the quarter-deck ceremony, Daggoo compares Ahab to the whale: "And has he a curious spout, too" （036-029）, and Queequeg describes Ahab's imagined death: "till he spouts black blood and rolls fin out" （036-033）. The spout here becomes a mirror between hunter and hunted—Ahab's monomania projected onto the whale's most visible sign of life.

### Spouting Blood

The transition from vapor to blood marks the whale's wounding and death. When mortally struck, the whale "now spouts thick blood" （041-013）, transforming the mist fountain into a sign of agony: "the whale now spouting thick blood, with swift fury" （081-039）. This inversion of the spout—from life-giving breath to mortal hemorrhage—recurs throughout the novel's whaling scenes.

## Navigational Function

Whalemen depend on spouts to locate their quarry. "Tall spouts were seen to leeward" （073-003） signals the presence of whales, and the spout's height, angle, and frequency help experienced mariners identify species at a distance. The spout serves as the whale's visible signature on the horizon—the first and sometimes only sign of its presence before the chase begins.
