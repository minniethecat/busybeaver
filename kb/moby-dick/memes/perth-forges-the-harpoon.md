# Perth Forges the Harpoon

## Overview

Chapter 113, "The Forge," stages the creation of Ahab's harpoon for the White Whale. Ahab brings a leathern pouch containing the gathered nail-stubbs of racing horses to Perth, the ship's begrimed blacksmith. The forging is at once a practical act of metalworking and a ritual of demonic consecration: Ahab hammers his own iron, the Parsee Fedallah bows over the fire as if invoking a curse, and the finished barbs are tempered not in water but in the blood of the three pagan harpooneers — Tashtego, Queequeg, and Daggoo.

## Chronology

### Perth at the Forge

Perth, "with matted beard, and swathed in a bristling shark-skin apron," stands between forge and anvil at mid-day, hammering a pike-head, when Ahab approaches carrying "a small rusty-looking leathern bag" (113-001). Sparks from Perth's hammer fly close to Ahab, who observes: "Are these thy Mother Carey's chickens, Perth? they are always flying in thy wake; birds of good omen, too, but not to all; — look here, they burn; but thou — thou liv'st among them without a scorch" (113-002).

Perth's reply carries the weight of his backstory (told in the preceding chapter, "The Blacksmith"): "Because I am scorched all over, Captain Ahab. I am past scorching; not easily can'st thou scorch a scar" (113-003).

### The Unsmoothable Seam

Ahab, who "in no Paradise myself, I am impatient of all misery in others that is not mad," challenges Perth: "Thou should'st go mad, blacksmith; say, why dost thou not go mad?" (113-004). Then he asks whether Perth can smooth any seam and dent in metal. Perth answers yes — "all seams and dents but one" (113-009). Ahab seizes the opening:

> "Look ye here, then," cried Ahab, passionately advancing, and leaning with both hands on Perth's shoulders; "look ye here — here — can ye smoothe out a seam like this, blacksmith," sweeping one hand across his ribbed brow; "if thou could'st, blacksmith, glad enough would I lay my head upon thy anvil, and feel thy heaviest hammer between my eyes." (113-010)

The seam is unsmoothable: "it has worked down into the bone of my skull — that is all wrinkles!" (113-012).

### The Nail-Stubbs

Abandoning pikes and gaffs, Ahab jingles the leathern bag "as if it were full of gold coins" and declares: "I, too, want a harpoon made; one that a thousand yoke of fiends could not part, Perth; something that will stick in a whale like his own fin-bone" (113-012). He flings the pouch upon the anvil:

> "Look ye, blacksmith, these are the gathered nail-stubbs of the steel shoes of racing horses." (113-012)

Perth recognizes the material: "Why, Captain Ahab, thou hast here, then, the best and stubbornest stuff we blacksmiths ever work" (113-013). Ahab agrees: "these stubbs will weld together like glue from the melted bones of murderers. Quick! forge me the harpoon" (113-014).

### The Forging

Twelve rods are forged and tested; Ahab himself spirals each around an iron bolt, rejecting one with a flaw: "Work that over again, Perth" (113-015). When the time comes to weld the twelve into one, Ahab stays Perth's hand: "he would weld his own iron" (113-016). As he hammers "with regular, gasping hems," the Parsee Fedallah passes silently, "bowing over his head towards the fire," seeming to invoke "some curse or some blessing" (113-016). Stubb, watching from the forecastle, mutters: "That Parsee smells fire like a fusee; and smells of it himself, like a hot musket's powder-pan" (113-017).

The final heat applied, Perth plunges the hissing shank into water. Scalding steam shoots into Ahab's face. "Would'st thou brand me, Perth?" Ahab winces; "have I been but forging my own branding-iron, then?" (113-019). Perth confesses his fear: "Is not this harpoon for the White Whale?" "For the white fiend!" Ahab answers (113-020, 113-021).

### The Baptism

For the barbs, Ahab produces his own razors — "the best of steel; here, and make the barbs sharp as the needle-sleet of the Icy Sea" (113-021). Perth eyes the razors with reluctance but obeys. When the time comes for tempering, Perth calls for the water-cask. Ahab refuses:

> "No, no — no water for that; I want it of the true death-temper. Ahoy, there! Tashtego, Queequeg, Daggoo! What say ye, pagans! Will ye give me as much blood as will cover this barb?" (113-025)

Three dark nods. Three punctures are made in "the heathen flesh." As the malignant iron devours the blood, Ahab howls his inverted baptism:

> "Ego non baptizo te in nomine patris, sed in nomine diaboli!" (113-026)

### The Harpoon Complete

Ahab selects a hickory pole, fits it to the socket, stretches the tow-line until it "hummed like a harp-string" (113-027), and declares it good. "Pole, iron, and rope — like the Three Fates — remained inseparable." Ahab stalks away, his ivory leg and the hickory pole "both hollowly ringing along every plank" (113-028). The chapter closes on Pip's "wretched laugh, thy idle but unresting eye; all thy strange mummeries not unmeaningly blended with the black tragedy of the melancholy ship" (113-028).

## Narrative Significance

The Forge is the novel's dark Eucharist. Every element of the harpoon's creation is charged with ritual significance: the nail-stubbs of racing horses (speed and violence compressed into metal), Ahab hammering his own iron (the captain as demiurge), Fedallah's silent benediction over the fire, and the culminating baptism in pagan blood with the Latin liturgy inverted. The harpoon becomes the physical object that binds Ahab's will to the White Whale — "like the Three Fates, inseparable."

The chapter also deepens Perth, whose backstory in Chapter 112 (lost family, lost fortune, found solace in the forge) makes him Ahab's unexpected mirror. Both are men "scorched all over" by life — but where Ahab rages, Perth endures. Ahab cannot tolerate this: "I am impatient of all misery in others that is not mad."

## Aftermath / Consequences

The harpoon will appear again at critical moments: Fedallah prophesies that "hemp only can kill thee," and in the final chase, Perth's harpoon remains "firmly lashed" in its crotch until Ahab snatches it (131-007, 133-023). The baptism in blood prefigures the novel's final sacrifice — the Pequod and all its crew save one.
