# The Affidavit

## Definition

The Affidavit is Melville's extended narrative maneuver in Chapter 45, in which he pauses the story to defend the factual veracity of the whale encounters he recounts. Drawing on his own experience as a whaleman and on documented historical cases, Ishmael—or Melville speaking through him—deploys the form of a legal affidavit to preempt reader skepticism about the more extraordinary events of the novel. The chapter functions as both a rhetorical strategy and a meditation on the nature of truth in narrative.

## Manifestations in the Novel

Chapter 45 opens with Ishmael declaring that "the foregoing chapter, in its earlier part, is as important a one as will be found in this volume" but that its "leading matter" requires further elaboration "to take away any incredulity which a profound ignorance of the entire subject may induce in some minds." This meta-narrative framing is unusual even in a novel prone to digression—here the narrator steps outside the story entirely to argue for its plausibility.

Melville structures the affidavit as a series of evidentiary categories. First, he cites personal experience: "I have personally known three instances where a whale, after receiving a harpoon, has effected a complete escape; and, after an interval (in one instance of three years), has been again struck by the same hand, and slain." Second, he appeals to communal knowledge within the whaling industry: "It is well known in the Sperm Whale Fishery... that there have been several memorable historical instances." Third, he invokes named authorities and documented cases, from the whale Timor Tom to the Essex disaster.

The rhetorical strategy is sophisticated: by adopting the language of legal testimony—sworn affidavits, corroborating witnesses, documented evidence—Melville simultaneously shores up his novel's credibility and subtly undermines the very epistemological certainty he claims to establish. The more insistently he protests his truthfulness, the more the reader is reminded that all narrative is constructed.

## Key Passages

> "I care not to perform this part of my task methodically; but shall be content to produce the desired impression by separate citations of items, practically or reliably known to me as a whaleman; and from these citations, I take it—the conclusion aimed at will naturally follow of itself." (045-003)

Ishmael adopts the pose of an amateur legal witness, eschewing systematic argument in favor of cumulative testimony. The phrase "the conclusion aimed at will naturally follow" is itself a rhetorical trick: the appearance of letting facts speak for themselves.

> "I have personally known three instances where a whale, after receiving a harpoon, has effected a complete escape; and, after an interval (in one instance of three years), has been again struck by the same hand, and slain; when the two irons, both marked by the same private cypher, have been taken from the body." (045-004)

This is the affidavit's core testimonial move: first-person witness, specific details (the three-year interval, the marked irons), and the implication of irrefutable physical evidence. The doubled harpoon functions as material proof that narrative cannot easily dismiss.

> "For God's sake, be economical with your lamps and candles! not a gallon you burn, but at least one drop of man's blood was spilled for it." (045-023)

Melville pivots from testimony to moral exhortation, linking the whale oil that lights civilization to the violence and mortality of its acquisition. This is the affidavit's deeper purpose: to make readers feel the human cost behind the commodity.

## Scholarly Context

Critics have read "The Affidavit" as Melville's anticipation of modern metafiction—a text that calls attention to its own constructedness even as it asserts its truth. The chapter's legal framing has been linked to nineteenth-century debates about the reliability of eyewitness testimony and the status of experiential knowledge versus book learning. Some scholars see the affidavit as Melville's response to early reviews that dismissed his sea stories as improbable, making it a document not only of literary strategy but of authorial defensiveness.
