# The Jaw

## Definition

The jaw of the whale — particularly the sperm whale's massive lower mandible — serves in Moby-Dick as a recurring motif that bridges anatomical cetology and existential symbolism. Melville invests the jaw with multiple meanings: it is at once a tool for extracting ivory, a weapon that destroys whale-boats, an architectural element that frames the Spouter-Inn, and a metaphor for death itself. Across the novel, "the jaws of death" become more than cliché — they materialize in the physical jaws of the living whale.

## Manifestations in the Novel

The jaw first appears in architectural form at the Spouter-Inn in New Bedford, where Ishmael enters beneath "a swinging sign" and finds the entryway framed by a whale's jawbone. In Ch.3, the inn itself is described with a jawbone arch, establishing the whale's jaw as a threshold between the landsman's world and the whaleman's.

In cetological context, Chapter 74 ("The Sperm Whale's Head — Contrasted View") provides the definitive anatomical portrait. The sperm whale's lower jaw is "portentous," resembling "the long narrow lid of an immense snuff-box, with the hinge at one end, instead of one side" (074-012). When raised, its rows of teeth become "a terrific portcullis" that impales unwary whalemen. Melville notes how the jaw, when unhinged by "a practised artist," yields ivory teeth that fishermen fashion into "canes, umbrella-stocks, and handles to riding-whips" (074-013).

The jaw returns as mortal threat in the novel's climax. During the three-day chase, Moby Dick's jaw becomes the instrument of Ahab's destruction. In Chapter 133, the whale's jaw splinters Ahab's whale-boat: "the palm-white of the inside of the jaw was within six inches of Ahab's head" (133-025). In the final chapter, Ahab's dying cry names the jaw directly: "ye fools, the jaw! the jaw! Is this the end of all my bursting prayers?" (135-052).

The jaw also appears in Stubb's comic scene, where he "converted the jaws of death into an easy chair" (027-001) by leisurely eating a whale-steak cut from the newly killed whale — a moment where the literal and figurative jaws converge in dark humor.

## Scholarly Context

The jaw in Moby-Dick has been read through multiple critical lenses. The Spouter-Inn's jawbone arch has been interpreted as Melville's deliberate echo of the biblical Samson, who slew a thousand men with the jawbone of an ass — a motif of mythic violence transposed onto the industrial whaling world. The recurring "jaws of death" imagery has been analyzed as Melville's re-materialization of dead metaphor: by making death's jaws literal (the whale's), Melville restores the terror to a worn phrase. The jaw's dual role — as both commodity (ivory teeth) and destroyer (the portcullis that kills) — has been read as Melville's critique of industrial capitalism's blindness to the destructive force it harnesses.
