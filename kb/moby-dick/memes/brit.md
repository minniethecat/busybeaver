# Brit

## Definition

Brit is the nineteenth-century whaleman's term for the minute, yellow organic substance — likely a combination of plankton, krill, and microscopic crustaceans — that drifts in vast meadows on the ocean surface and serves as the primary food of the Right Whale. Melville introduces it in Chapter 58, titled simply "Brit," as both a biological fact of the whaling ecosystem and a point of departure for one of the novel's most philosophically ambitious meditations on the nature of the sea. The chapter moves from natural observation — "vast meadows of brit, the minute, yellow substance, upon which the Right Whale largely feeds" (058-001) — to cosmic speculation about the sea's essential hostility to all life, including its own offspring.

## Manifestations in the Novel

Brit first appears in Chapter 45, "The Affidavit," where Ishmael notes parenthetically that whales "enjoy great individual celebrity" in certain feeding grounds, specifically mentioning "that peculiar substance called brit" as the aliment of the Right Whale (045-025). The Pequod encounters brit in earnest while steering northeast from the Crozetts: "For leagues and leagues it undulated round us, so that we seemed to be sailing through boundless fields of ripe and golden wheat" (058-001).

The feeding Right Whales provide the chapter's central image: "with open jaws sluggishly swam through the brit, which, adhering to the fringing fibres of that wondrous Venetian blind in their mouths, was in that manner separated from the water that escaped at the lip" (058-002). Melville compares them to "morning mowers, who side by side slowly and seethingly advance their scythes through the long wet grass of marshy meads" (058-003). The pastoral simile is deliberate and ironic — it domesticates the whale's feeding only to sharpen the turn that follows.

For Chapter 58 does not remain a naturalist's observation. The brit meadows become the occasion for Ishmael's declaration of the sea's fundamental malevolence: "But not only is the sea such a foe to man who is an alien to it, but it is also a fiend to its own off-spring; worse than the Persian host who murdered his own guests; sparing not the creatures which itself has spawned" (058-010). The brit, which sustains the Right Whale, is itself consumed in an endless chain of predation — and this, Ishmael concludes, is the sea's "universal cannibalism" (058-012). The peaceful image of whales grazing in yellow meadows thus conceals a vision of nature as a system of total, recursive consumption, in which every creature feeds and is fed upon.

## Key Passages

> "Steering north-eastward from the Crozetts, we fell in with vast meadows of brit, the minute, yellow substance, upon which the Right Whale largely feeds. For leagues and leagues it undulated round us, so that we seemed to be sailing through boundless fields of ripe and golden wheat."
> (058-001)

> "As morning mowers, who side by side slowly and seethingly advance their scythes through the long wet grass of marshy meads; even so these monsters swam, making a strange, grassy, cutting sound; and leaving behind them endless swaths of blue on the yellow sea."
> (058-003)

> "But not only is the sea such a foe to man who is an alien to it, but it is also a fiend to its own off-spring; worse than the Persian host who murdered his own guests; sparing not the creatures which itself has spawned."
> (058-010)

> "Consider all this; and then turn to this green, gentle, and most docile earth; consider them both, the sea and the land; and do you not find a strange analogy to something in yourself?"
> (058-012)
