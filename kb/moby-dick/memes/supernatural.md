# The Supernatural

## Definition

The supernatural in *Moby-Dick* names the persistent intrusion of forces, beings, and events that exceed natural explanation. Melville renders the supernatural not as simple Gothic machinery but as an epistemological problem: how do we distinguish between natural phenomena that *feel* supernatural and genuinely preternatural causation? The "supernatural hand" Ishmael feels in his hotel room [003-023] may be Queequeg's physical hand, yet the sensation of the uncanny is real. Fedallah's prophecies fulfill themselves with an accuracy that defies chance. The whiteness of the whale activates what Melville calls "the supernaturalism of this hue" [042-005], a terror rooted in something beyond the rational. Throughout the novel, the supernatural troubles the boundary between belief and knowledge, superstition and truth.

## Manifestations in the Novel

The novel opens with supernatural overtones: Ishmael, seeking a bed at the Spouter-Inn, feels "a supernatural hand seemed placed in mine" [003-023] — a moment that turns out to have a natural explanation (Queequeg's arrival) yet retains its uncanny charge. Queequeg himself carries a "supernatural gravity" [004-003]. The most sustained engagement comes in "The Whiteness of the Whale" (Ch. 42), where Melville catalogs the "supernaturalism" of whiteness across cultures — from the "White Steed of the Prairies" to the "shrouded phantom of the whitened waters" [042-011]. Fedallah, Ahab's Parsee harpooneer, is the novel's most explicitly supernatural figure: "a muffled mystery" [050-007] whose prophecies shape Ahab's final chase. The corposants (St. Elmo's Fire) in Ch. 119 transform the Pequod into a supernatural tableau, the "pale, phosphorescent lights" turning the ship into a "phantom" [119-005]. The Spirit-Spout (Ch. 51) haunts the night watch, and the ghostly Rachel searches the seas for her lost children.

## Scholarly Context

The supernatural in Melville has been read as his negotiation with the decline of Calvinist certainty and the rise of scientific naturalism in nineteenth-century America. The novel's supernatural elements refuse both literal belief and easy dismissal, occupying an ambiguous middle ground that critics have aligned with the "fantastic" in Todorov's sense — the hesitation between natural and supernatural explanation. Fedallah has been analyzed as an Orientalist figure through whom Melville channels anxieties about Eastern "magic" and fatalism, while the ghostly whiteness of the whale connects the supernatural to racial discourse.
