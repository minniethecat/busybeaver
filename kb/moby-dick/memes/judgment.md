# Judgment

## Definition

Judgment in *Moby-Dick* operates at the intersection of theology, law, and existential discrimination. The word carries three distinct but interpenetrating meanings in Melville's usage: **divine judgment**, the eschatological reckoning that Father Mapple and the whalemen invoke against a backdrop of Calvinist certainty; **human judgment**, the captain's authority over his crew and the practical discernment required at sea; and **discriminatory judgment**, the cognitive capacity to distinguish — between one whale and another, between sanity and madness, between salvation and damnation. Ishmael begins the novel by invoking "freewill and discriminating judgment" (001-013) as the mark of human consciousness, yet the novel relentlessly tests whether such discrimination is possible in a universe Melville describes as "uncertain, unsettled, mysterious." The "trump of judgment" (051-002) that sounds when a whale is sighted collapses eschatology into cetology: the final judgment becomes a matter of whaleboats.

## Manifestations in the Novel

**Freewill and discriminating judgment (Chapter 1)**. Ishmael opens the novel with the phrase "freewill and discriminating judgment" (001-013), establishing judgment as a capacity of the reasoning mind. The context is his decision to go to sea rather than commit suicide or violence — the Cato-like dilemma of choosing life. Judgment here is the faculty that distinguishes one act from another and enables choice. This opening invocation sets a standard the novel will systematically undermine: Ishmael's discriminating judgment leads him onto the Pequod, into Ahab's orbit, and toward a catastrophe that no amount of discrimination could have foreseen or prevented.

**The trump of judgment (Chapter 51)**. When the spectral whale is sighted at night, the cry "There she blows!" produces an effect Melville compares to the eschaton itself: "Had the trump of judgment blown, they could not have quivered more" (051-002). The simile conflates two entirely unlike events — a whale sighting and the Last Judgment — with unsettling ease. The whale, not God, announces the end; the harpooneers, not angels, spring into action. Melville's irony is that the crew responds to a whale with the terror appropriate to the apocalypse, as if they already sense that this particular whale will indeed bring their world to an end.

**The so-called judgments of God (Chapter 54)**. In the Town-Ho's story, Ishmael describes the sinking of the ship as "one of those so called judgments of God which at times are said to overtake some men" (054-003). The phrase "so called" is crucial — it brackets the entire category of divine judgment as a human attribution, a name we give to events whose causation we cannot trace. The Town-Ho sinks because Steelkilt and Radney have a quarrel and the ship springs a leak; to call this divine judgment is to impose a theological narrative on mechanical causation. Melville leaves the question open: is there a God who judges, or do we simply call our disasters "judgments" because the alternative — that they are meaningless — is unbearable?

**Judgment in the forecastle (Chapters 40, 65)**. The midnight forecastle scene (Chapter 40) is a polyphonic judgment scene, with sailors of different nations offering competing verdicts on the voyage, on each other, and on the whale. The Spanish sailor's refrain — "Thy kiss their last, and come to judgment. That's the way — that's it; thy throat ain't spoiled with eating Amsterdam butter" (040-020) — mingles eschatology with insult, the Last Judgment with the judgment of appetite. Later, Ishmael delivers a mock-sermon on cannibalism: "at the day of judgment, than for thee, civilized and enlightened gourmand" (065-006), reversing the expected moral calculus. The cannibal Queequeg, Ishmael argues, will fare better at judgment than the gluttonous Christian.

**Righteous judgment and the whale (Chapter 81)**. When the Pequod encounters the Virgin, a decrepit whale, Ishmael reflects that "not a righteous judgment descended upon him in a crab" (081-024) — the whale's blindness from barnacle-like parasites. The phrase "righteous judgment" imports theological weight: is the whale's suffering deserved? Is Nature's cruelty a form of judgment, or is the category of judgment inapplicable to the non-human world? The whale cannot sin, yet it suffers; this is the problem of natural evil compressed into a crustacean.

**The try-works and the day of judgment (Chapter 96)**. In the try-works chapter, Ishmael calls the fires "the left wing of the day of judgment" (096-005). The industrial rendering of whale into oil becomes a preview of the apocalypse: "it is an argument for the pit" (096-005). Judgment here is not a distant eschatological event but a present industrial process — the Pequod is already in hell, already in the day of judgment, and the flames that light the sea are the same flames that would consume the damned.

**Ahab's judgment (Chapter 133)**. On the first day of the chase, Ahab "aroused the sleepers with such judgment claps that they seemed to exhale from the scuttle" (133-004). The captain's voice has become the voice of judgment itself — not divine but commanding, not eschatological but immediate. Ahab has assumed the role of judge; his judgment on the whale is the only judgment the crew will hear. But Ahab's judgment is precisely what the novel puts on trial: who gave this man the authority to condemn the whale? What right has the creature to judge the creator?

## Key Passages

- (001-013) — "freewill and discriminating judgment": the novel's opening philosophical axiom, judgment as human capacity
- (016-002) — "the excellence of Yojo's judgment and surprising forecast of things to come": Queequeg's idol as font of judgment, displacing human reason with pagan oracle
- (040-020) — "Thy kiss their last, and come to judgment": the Spanish sailor's conflation of eschatology and appetite
- (051-002) — "Had the trump of judgment blown, they could not have quivered more": whale-sighting as eschatological event
- (054-003) — "one of those so called judgments of God": the bracketing of divine judgment as human attribution
- (065-006) — "at the day of judgment, than for thee, civilized and enlightened gourmand": the reversal of moral judgment
- (081-024) — "not a righteous judgment descended upon him in a crab": the problem of natural suffering and divine justice
- (096-005) — "the left wing of the day of judgment; it is an argument for the pit": industrial fire as eschatological preview
- (133-004) — "aroused the sleepers with such judgment claps": Ahab's voice as judgment

## Scholarly Context

The theme of judgment in *Moby-Dick* has been analyzed through several frames. Theological readings emphasize Melville's engagement with Calvinist predestination and the problem of theodicy — how can judgment be just in a world where the innocent suffer? Legal-historical readings contextualize the captain's absolute judgment within 19th-century maritime law, which granted whaling captains near-unilateral authority. Narratological approaches focus on Ishmael's opening claim to "discriminating judgment" as a fiction the narrative voice must maintain even as the story demonstrates its impossibility. The "so called judgments of God" passage has been read as Melville's most direct engagement with the problem of divine justice — and his most skeptical, refusing either to affirm or deny that events constitute judgments.
