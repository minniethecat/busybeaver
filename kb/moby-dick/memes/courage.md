# Courage

## Definition

Melville devotes an entire chapter — "Knights and Squires" (Chapter 26) — to defining courage, establishing a careful taxonomy that distinguishes varieties of bravery as precisely as Cetology distinguishes species of whale. Courage in *Moby-Dick* is not a single virtue but a spectrum: at one end stands Starbuck's "reliable and useful courage" (026-001), grounded in practical experience and domestic attachment; at the other, Ahab's "undaunted" recklessness, which Melville calls "a thing simply useful to itself" (026-004). Between them lie the harpooneers' physical daring, the crew's endurance against "protracted mental terrors" (046-002), and — perhaps most audaciously — the whale's own capacity to "stagger courage" (042-004) by its sheer whiteness. The novel ultimately asks whether courage, divorced from purpose and community, ceases to be a virtue at all.

## Manifestations in the Novel

**Starbuck's reasoned courage (Chapter 26)**. Melville opens his taxonomy by declaring that "the most reliable and useful courage was that which arises from the fair estimation of the encountered peril" (026-001). Starbuck embodies this — his courage is "not a sentiment; but a thing simply useful to him" (026-003), a tool calibrated by risk assessment rather than impulse. Crucially, Starbuck's courage is tethered to home: he thinks of "his wife and child" (026-005) hundreds of miles away. This domestic anchor gives his bravery its moral weight, distinguishing it from Ahab's untethered audacity.

**Ahab's undaunted will (Chapters 36, 41, 99)**. If Starbuck's courage is a tool, Ahab's is a fire. On the quarter-deck he reveals a will that no peril can check, and Ishmael later reflects that Ahab's "courageous, the undaunted, and victorious" pursuit (099-007) masks a "fatal pride" beneath. In Chapter 41, Ahab is described as "intelligent and courageous enough in offering battle" (041-006) to the White Whale, yet his courage has curdled into monomania — bravery severed from reason. The [[the-doubloon|doubloon]] nailed to the mast becomes the symbolic wager that his courage stakes against the universe.

**The harpooneers' physical courage (Chapters 61, 72, 78)**. The non-white harpooneers display courage as physical skill. In the first lowering, the harpooneer "still encouraged his men to the onset" (061-015) despite the chaos of the whale boats. Queequeg's rescue of Tashtego from the sinking whale-head is described as requiring both "courage and great skill in obstetrics" (078-012) — here courage is inseparable from competence. Ishmael frames his own role tending Queequeg during the perilous scramble as "cheerful duty," calling out "Courage! there is good cheer in store" (072-010) — suggesting that fellowship can sustain courage where isolation cannot.

**Courage against the white-shrouded bear (Chapter 42)**. In the chapter on whiteness, Melville argues that no physical threat "can so stagger courage as the white-shrouded bear or the White Whale" (042-004). This is courage confronted not by danger but by *meaninglessness* — the blank whiteness that refuses to signify. It is the novel's most radical claim about courage: the hardest thing to face is not death but the void behind appearances.

**The quiet courage of endurance (Chapters 46, 93)**. Not all courage is dramatic. Ishmael notes that "few men's courage is proof against protracted mental terrors" (046-002) — the slow grind of waiting, the "handful of shavings" that survives a shipwreck. [[pip|Pip]], abandoned at sea, is exhorted "to cherish his courageousness to the utmost" (093-005), yet his mind breaks. His fate is a caution: courage has limits, and the ocean does not respect them.

## Key Passages

- (026-001) — "The most reliable and useful courage was that which arises from the fair estimation of the encountered peril" — Melville's thesis on courage.
- (026-003) — In Starbuck, "courage was not a sentiment; but a thing simply useful to him" — courage as practical tool.
- (026-004) — Ahab's courage contrasted: "a thing simply useful to itself" — bravery without external purpose.
- (041-006) — Ahab "intelligent and courageous enough in offering battle to the White Whale" — courage corrupted into obsession.
- (042-004) — Nothing "can so stagger courage as the white-shrouded bear or the White Whale" — courage against meaninglessness.
- (046-002) — "Few men's courage is proof against protracted mental terrors" — the slow test of endurance.
- (061-015) — The harpooneer "still encouraged his men to the onset" — physical courage in action.
- (072-010) — "Courage! there is good cheer in store" — Ishmael sustaining Queequeg, courage as fellowship.
- (078-012) — Queequeg's rescue of Tashtego "through the courage and great skill in obstetrics" — courage fused with competence.
- (093-005) — Pip exhorted "to cherish his courageousness to the utmost" — the limits of courage, and Pip's tragic failure.
- (099-007) — Ahab as "the courageous, the undaunted, and victorious" — courage as self-consuming fire.

## Scholarly Context

Melville's treatment of courage has attracted significant critical attention. The taxonomy in Chapter 26 is often read as Melville's response to Emersonian self-reliance: Starbuck's courage is social and situated, while Ahab's is the dark twin of transcendental individualism. C. L. R. James's Marxist reading emphasizes the class dimension — the officers' courage is calculated and contractual, the crew's is physical and expended. More recently, disability scholars have noted how Ahab's physical injury complicates the traditional association of courage with bodily wholeness, while trauma theorists read Pip's fate as Melville's acknowledgment that courage has neurological limits that no will can override.
