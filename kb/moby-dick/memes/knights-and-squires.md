# Knights and Squires

## Two Chapters, Two Meanings

Chapters 26 and 27 of *Moby-Dick* share the title "Knights and Squires," but they are radically different in content and register. Chapter 26 introduces Starbuck, the chief mate, in a long, dense portrait that morphs, in its final paragraphs, into one of the novel's most passionate declarations of democratic faith. Chapter 27 introduces the second and third mates (Stubb and Flask) and the three harpooneers (Queequeg, Tashtego, Daggoo), culminating in the vision of the Pequod's crew as "an Anacharsis Clootz deputation from all the isles of the sea" — a federation of "Isolatoes," each adrift on his own private continent, momentarily joined under one keel and one captain.

The chivalric title is both apt and ironic. In the medieval model, a knight was accompanied by his squire — a younger man who carried his weapons, tended his horse, and learned the arts of war. On the Pequod, each mate (Starbuck, Stubb, Flask) commands a whaleboat and is partnered with a harpooneer (Queequeg, Tashtego, Daggoo) who "in certain conjunctures provides him with a fresh lance" (027-006). The structure is indeed chivalric — but the knights are Quaker whalemen from Nantucket and Cape Cod, and the squires are a Polynesian cannibal, a Gay Head Indian, and an African "Ahasuerus." Melville's point is precisely that chivalry, far from being the exclusive property of medieval Europe, flourishes wherever men organize themselves around courage, loyalty, and shared peril.

## Starbuck and the Democratic God

Chapter 26's portrait of Starbuck is the longest sustained character introduction in the novel. He is "a long, earnest man," "hard as twice-baked biscuit," whose "pure tight skin was an excellent fit" — as though his body were a garment precisely tailored to his soul (026-001). He is brave but not reckless: his courage is "a thing simply useful to him, and always at hand upon all mortally practical occasions" (026-003). He is superstitious, but his superstition "seems rather to spring, somehow, from intelligence than from ignorance" (026-001). And he carries the buried knowledge of his father's and brother's deaths at sea — a grief that has not broken him but has made him careful, perhaps too careful, for the demands Ahab will later place on him.

But the portrait of Starbuck is only the occasion for the chapter's true subject, which erupts in paragraph 026-005 in one of the most extraordinary passages Melville ever wrote:

> Men may seem detestable as joint stock-companies and nations; knaves, fools, and murderers there may be; men may have mean and meagre faces; but man, in the ideal, is so noble and so sparkling, such a grand and glowing creature, that over any ignominious blemish in him all his fellows should run to throw their costliest robes.
> (026-005)

This is not a description of Starbuck but a credo — Melville's declaration of faith in a "democratic dignity" that "radiates without end from God; Himself! The great God absolute! The centre and circumference of all democracy!" (026-005). The rhetoric escalates into a prayer: Melville calls on the "just Spirit of Equality" to "bear me out" in his project of ascribing "high qualities, though dark" to "meanest mariners, and renegades and castaways" (026-006). This is the novel's artistic manifesto: to find tragic grandeur not in kings and courts but in the forecastle of a whaling ship.

The prayer's examples — Bunyan the "swart convict," Cervantes with his "stumped and paupered arm," Andrew Jackson "picked up from the pebbles" — establish a lineage of democratic heroism in which the Pequod's crew are the latest inheritors. Melville is claiming for his whalemen what epic tradition reserved for warriors and kings: the right to be taken seriously, to be treated as subjects worthy of tragic representation.

## The Squires: Queequeg, Tashtego, Daggoo

Chapter 27 catalogs the three harpooneers with a combination of ethnographic detail and mythic amplification. Queequeg, already known from the New Bedford chapters, is Starbuck's squire — the "Knight" of the chief mate and the "squire" who already occupies more narrative space than any other crew member except Ishmael and Ahab. Tashtego, "an unmixed Indian from Gay Head," is Stubb's squire — his "long, lean, sable hair" and "black rounding eyes" mark him as "an inheritor of the unvitiated blood of those proud warrior hunters" who once hunted moose in the New England forests (027-008). Daggoo, "a gigantic, coal-black negro-savage, with a lion-like tread — an Ahasuerus to behold," is Flask's squire — a man of "six feet five in his socks" beside whom "a white man standing before him seemed a white flag come to beg truce of a fortress" (027-009).

The three harpooneers represent three continents (Polynesia, North America, Africa) and three races, yet they are united by their function: each is the "squire" who throws the harpoon that fastens to the whale, while the "knight" (the mate) delivers the killing lance. Melville's chivalric metaphor thus racializes the hierarchy — the white officers command, the non-white harpooneers execute the most dangerous physical act — but it also ennobles it. The harpooneers are not servants but partners, their skill and courage the indispensable condition of the hunt.

## Isolatoes Federated Along One Keel

The chapter's final paragraph is among the novel's most celebrated passages. Having established that "not one in two of the many thousand men before the mast employed in the American whale fishery are Americans born," Melville introduces the concept of the "Isolato" — a term he coins to describe men who live on "a separate continent of his own," who do not acknowledge "the common continent of men" (027-009). The Pequod's crew, drawn from "all the isles of the sea, and all the ends of the earth," are all Isolatoes — yet they are "federated along one keel":

> An Anacharsis Clootz deputation from all the isles of the sea, and all the ends of the earth, accompanying Old Ahab in the Pequod to lay the world's grievances before that bar from which not very many of them ever come back.
> (027-009)

The reference to Anacharsis Clootz — the Prussian-born revolutionary who led a "deputation of the human race" before the French National Assembly in 1790 — transforms the Pequod's voyage into a political allegory. The ship is a floating republic, a microcosm of democracy, bearing the grievances of the world's peoples to the ultimate tribunal — death. The parenthetical mention of Pip, "Poor Alabama boy!" who "went before," casts a shadow forward: the federation of Isolatoes is also a company of the doomed.

The concept of the Isolato is one of Melville's most resonant inventions. It names a condition — radical individuality, the inability to feel at home in the common world — and simultaneously offers a counter to it: the ship, the shared labor, the "one keel" that temporarily binds these separate continents together. The Pequod, in this vision, is democracy in action — not a political philosophy but a physical arrangement of bodies from every corner of the earth, working together toward a common end, even if that end is catastrophe.

## Key Passages

- (026-001) — Starbuck's portrait: "his pure tight skin was an excellent fit," "like a patent chronometer, his interior vitality was warranted to do well in all climates"
- (026-005) — The democratic credo: "man, in the ideal, is so noble and so sparkling… that over any ignominious blemish in him all his fellows should run to throw their costliest robes"
- (026-005) — The democratic God: "that democratic dignity which, on all hands, radiates without end from God; Himself! The great God absolute! The centre and circumference of all democracy!"
- (026-006) — The artistic manifesto: "if I shall touch that workman's arm with some ethereal light… then against all mortal critics bear me out in it, thou just Spirit of Equality"
- (027-006) — The chivalric model: "each mate or headsman, like a Gothic Knight of old, is always accompanied by his boat-steerer or harpooneer"
- (027-009) — The Isolatoes: "each Isolato living on a separate continent of his own. Yet now, federated along one keel, what a set these Isolatoes were!"
- (027-009) — The Anacharsis Clootz deputation: "accompanying Old Ahab in the Pequod to lay the world's grievances before that bar from which not very many of them ever come back"

## Scholarly Context

"Knights and Squires" has been central to discussions of Melville's democratic aesthetics. The critic F.O. Matthiessen, in *American Renaissance* (1941), identified Chapter 26's invocation of the "democratic God" as the novel's clearest statement of Melville's artistic program: to write an epic of common labor rather than of aristocratic heroism. More recent criticism has complicated this reading by attending to the racial hierarchies the chivalric metaphor encodes — the white officers as "knights," the non-white harpooneers as "squires" — and asking whether Melville's democratic vision is genuinely egalitarian or merely assimilative.

The concept of the "Isolato" has attracted particular attention from existentialist and psychoanalytic critics. The Isolato is not merely a lonely man but a man who has broken with the "common continent" of shared meaning — a figure for the modern condition of alienation. The Pequod's brief federation of these Isolatoes has been read as Melville's image of community under conditions of modernity: fragile, temporary, built on shared labor rather than shared identity, and always shadowed by the knowledge of its own dissolution.
