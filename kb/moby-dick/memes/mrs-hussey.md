# Mrs. Hussey

## Overview

Mrs. Hussey is the proprietress of the Try Pots, a whalemen's inn in Nantucket where [[ishmael|Ishmael]] and [[queequeg|Queequeg]] lodge before sailing on the [[pequod|Pequod]]. She is a freckled woman with yellow hair and a yellow gown, brisk of speech and absolute in her domestic authority.

Her name is inseparable from her establishment's signature offering: chowder. Her refrain — "Clam or Cod?" (015-006) — is among the novel's most memorable comic moments. Yet beneath the comedy, Mrs. Hussey embodies the practical, unsentimental world of Nantucket whaling culture that Ishmael must pass through before his voyage begins.

## Role in the Narrative

Mrs. Hussey appears in two early chapters and provides both comic relief and a grounded portrait of Nantucket domestic life. She is a gatekeeper figure: her inn, the Try Pots, is the last shore-bound shelter Ishmael and Queequeg experience before launching into the whaling world.

In Chapter 15, she welcomes them with the novel's most famous culinary question:

> "Clam or Cod?" (015-006)

When Ishmael tries to order both, she hears only "clam" and bustles off. Through persistence he secures both chowders, discovering each is a "surpassingly excellent" creation (015-009). The scene closes with a vivid sketch of the Try Pots as "fishiest of all fishy places" — a world where Mrs. Hussey wears a necklace of codfish vertebra and her husband Hosea binds his account books in shark-skin (015-012).

In Chapter 17, Mrs. Hussey returns during [[queequeg|Queequeg's]] Ramadan crisis. When Ishmael cannot rouse Queequeg from his locked room, the chambermaid summons the landlady:

> Mrs. Hussey soon appeared, with a mustard-pot in one hand and a vinegar-cruet in the other (017-005)

Her response to the crisis is characteristically practical: she fears Queequeg has killed himself, not out of sentiment but because it will "be the ruin of my house" — another suicide like "unfort'nate Stiggs" would damage the Try Pots' reputation (017-006). She tries to prevent Ishmael from breaking down her door ("I won't have my premises spoiled") but ultimately provides the key.

## Character Traits

### The Absolute Landlady

Mrs. Hussey rules the Try Pots with unquestioned authority. She scolds men in purple shirts, gives crisp directions ("Get along with ye, or I'll be combing ye!" (015-004)), and enforces house rules without apology. When she demands Queequeg's harpoon, she invokes precedent — the cautionary tale of "young Stiggs" who returned from a four-year voyage with only three barrels of oil and was found dead with his harpoon in his side (015-014). Her rule is absolute but not arbitrary.

### The Practical Businesswoman

Hosea Hussey being "from home," Mrs. Hussey is "entirely competent to attend to all his affairs" (015-006). She runs the inn, manages the kitchen, and protects her property with fierce pragmatism. Her concern during the Ramadan crisis — that another suicide will damage the Try Pots' business — reveals a mind attuned to the economic realities of innkeeping in a whaling port.

### Comic Timing

Mrs. Hussey's comedy lies in her literal-mindedness. When Ishmael says "cod," she delivers cod chowder. His experiment proves her system works — the Try Pots runs on efficient, no-nonsense hospitality. The detail of the codfish vertebra necklace, reported without irony, captures Melville's gift for comic portraiture through material detail.

## Relationships

- **[[ishmael|Ishmael]]**: Treated with brisk professionalism. She feeds him, beds him, and expects no trouble. Their exchanges are cordial but transactional.
- **[[queequeg|Queequeg]]**: She learns his name and addresses him directly. Her confiscation of his harpoon — a weapon central to his identity — is done without sentiment but with clear reasoning. She shows no racial prejudice, only a landlady's universal suspicion of dangerous objects.
- **Hosea Hussey**: Her absent husband, the nominal proprietor. His absence means she runs the establishment. The household details — his shark-skin account books, his brindled cow that feeds on fish remnants — are reported by Ishmael as extensions of the Try Pots' fish-saturated world.
- **[[peter-coffin|Peter Coffin]]**: The landlord of the Spouter Inn in New Bedford, who recommends his "cousin Hosea Hussey" and the Try Pots to Ishmael.

## Critical Interpretation

Mrs. Hussey is a minor figure but a significant one. She represents the shore world that whalemen leave behind — ordered, domestic, female, and bound by practical economics rather than cosmic obsessions. Her "Clam or Cod?" is the novel's most succinct counterpoint to Ahab's metaphysical questions: there are two choices, they are simple, and you must pick one.

The Try Pots itself — with its gallows-like sign, its fish-bone pavements, its insistent maritime materiality — is a threshold space. Mrs. Hussey presides over this threshold. She is the last figure of settled domestic authority Ishmael encounters before the Pequod sails, and her brisk, unsentimental competence makes the whaling world that follows feel all the more unmoored.
