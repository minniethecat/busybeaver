# The Owners

## Overview

The Owners are the financial and institutional backbone of Nantucket whaling. In *Moby-Dick*, they are personified by Captains Peleg and Bildad, the principal owners of the [[pequod|Pequod]].

Ship ownership in the whaling industry was typically divided among multiple investors. A single whaleship might have a dozen part-owners, each holding a fraction of the vessel and sharing in its profits.

Peleg and Bildad represent the transition from active whalemen to shore-based capitalists. Both are retired captains who now manage ships from land, hiring captains and crews to do the dangerous work they once did themselves.

> "He was a Quaker, he was Bildad, and a ship-owner, and a man that had seen many strange things in his time." (016-023)

## Structure / Hierarchy

The ownership structure of the Pequod reflects the broader economics of Nantucket whaling:

- **Principal Owners** — Captains Peleg and Bildad: Hold the largest shares. Make the hiring decisions. Negotiate the lays (crew shares).
- **Part-Owners** — Ahab and others: Smaller stakes. Ahab holds a "small share" in the Pequod, aligning his interests — in theory — with the owners.
- **Agents** — Shore-based managers: Handle outfitting, provisioning, and cargo sales. Not named in the novel but implicit in the system.
- **Captain** — [[ahab|Ahab]]: Though part-owner, his role as captain places him in a subordinate position to the owners who hired him — a tension the novel exploits.

The owners' authority is absolute on shore but nearly nonexistent at sea. Once the Pequod sails, Ahab is answerable to no one. This structural gap — the captain's autonomy from ownership — is what enables Ahab's monomaniacal quest.

## Key Members

**Captain Peleg** — A "bluff, gruff" old whaleman. He conducts Ishmael's interview and negotiates the lay (share). Peleg is pragmatic, earthy, and deeply knowledgeable about the whaling life. He warns Ishmael about Ahab: "He's a grand, ungodly, god-like man" (016-045).

**Captain Bildad** — The pious Quaker. Bildad carries a Bible and quotes Scripture while driving the hardest possible bargain. His piety and parsimony are a single trait: he serves God by maximizing profit.

> "Bildad was a man of strong religious feeling, and the way in which he combined his piety with his parsimony was instructive." (016-025)

Together, Peleg and Bildad form a comic double act — the gruff pragmatist and the pious skinflint — but they also represent the economic order that whaling serves.

## Function in the Narrative

The Owners appear in only three early chapters (16, 20, 22), but their role is essential.

They establish the **economic frame** of the novel. Whaling is not romance; it is business. Men risk their lives so that investors in Nantucket can turn a profit. Ishmael's lay of 1/300th is a precise economic fact.

They introduce **Ahab by proxy**. Ahab himself does not appear until Chapter 28. For twelve chapters, the reader knows him only through Peleg's description: "a queer man," "grand, ungodly, god-like."

They represent the **world left behind**. The Pequod sails from Nantucket, and the owners remain on shore. The distance between ownership and labor, between capital and the dangerous work it funds, is one of the novel's quiet themes.

## The Quaker Paradox

Peleg and Bildad are Quakers — members of a pacifist religious sect — who make their living from a violent trade.

> "For some of these same Quakers are the most sanguinary of all sailors and whale-hunters. They are fighting Quakers; they are Quakers with a vengeance." (016-042)

This paradox is not merely comic. It goes to the heart of the novel's treatment of American identity: a nation that professes peace while practicing violence, that invokes Providence while pursuing profit.

## Economic Context

The Nantucket whaling industry at its peak (c. 1820–1850) was a complex financial system:

- Ships were typically owned by syndicates of 8–12 investors
- A whaleship cost $20,000–$30,000 to build and outfit
- A successful voyage could return 200–300% profit
- Crew were paid in shares (lays), not wages — aligning incentives but also shifting risk from owners to laborers
- Insurance was expensive and limited; many owners spread risk across multiple vessels

The Pequod's voyage, diverted from profit to vengeance, represents the collapse of this rational economic order into Ahab's irrational obsession.
