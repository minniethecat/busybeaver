# Queequeg Signs the Articles

## Overview

The signing of the Pequod's articles by [[queequeg|Queequeg]] is a pivotal moment of cultural negotiation that secures the harpooneer's place aboard the whaling ship. Walking down the Nantucket wharf with [[ishmael|Ishmael]], Queequeg is halted by Captain [[peleg|Peleg]]'s declaration that "he let no cannibals on board that craft, unless they previously produced their papers" (018-001).

What follows is a compact drama in three acts: Ishmael's sermon-like defense of Queequeg's spiritual standing, Queequeg's harpoon demonstration that silences material doubt, and the ritual signing — Queequeg copying his tattoo onto the page as his legal mark. Captain [[bildad|Bildad]] concludes with a tract warning of damnation, while Peleg awards the ninetieth lay.

The scene encapsulates the novel's core tension between savage and civilized, pagan and Christian, all reconciled — uneasily — by the pragmatism of the whaling industry.

## Chronology

- (018-001) — Ishmael and Queequeg walk down the wharf toward the Pequod. Captain Peleg shouts from his wigwam that he "let no cannibals on board that craft, unless they previously produced their papers."
- (018-002) — Ishmael climbs aboard to challenge Peleg, leaving Queequeg on the wharf. Captain Bildad emerges from behind Peleg, demanding to know if Queequeg is "in communion with any Christian church."
- (018-003) — Ishmael declares Queequeg "a member of the first Congregational Church" — a rhetorical dodge invoking the universal church of all mankind. Peleg grumbles that Queequeg "hasn't been baptized right either, or it would have washed some of that devil's blue off his face."
- (018-004) — Bildad presses: "Is this Philistine a regular member of Deacon Deuteronomy's meeting?" Ishmael expands his metaphor into a sermon on the "great and everlasting First Congregation of this whole worshipping world."
- (018-005) — Peleg, won over, cries, "Young man, you'd better ship for a missionary, instead of a fore-mast hand; I never heard a better sermon." He invites Queequeg aboard, noting "what a harpoon he's got there!"
- (018-006) — Queequeg silently demonstrates: he leaps into a whale-boat, aims at a tar spot on the water, and darts his harpoon "clean across the ship's decks, and struck the glistening tar spot out of sight" (018-006). His quiet verdict: "spos-ee him whale-e eye; why, dad whale dead."
- (018-007) — Peleg, aghast and impressed, orders Bildad to fetch the ship's papers: "We must have Hedgehog there, I mean Quohog, in one of our boats. Look ye, Quohog, we'll give ye the ninetieth lay."
- (018-008) — In the cabin, Queequeg is enrolled. Peleg, unaware Queequeg can sign, asks "dost thou sign thy name or make thy mark?" Queequeg copies "an exact counterpart of a queer round figure which was tattooed upon his arm" onto the paper.
- (018-009) — The entry stands as: **Quohog. his X mark.** — Peleg's mistaken rendering of Queequeg's name is now legally inscribed.
- (018-010) — Bildad solemnly presents Queequeg with a tract titled "The Latter Day Coming; or No Time to Lose," urging him to "spurn the idol Bell, and the hideous dragon; turn from the wrath to come."
- (018-011) — Peleg intervenes: "Pious harpooneers never make good voyagers — it takes the shark out of 'em." He recounts the cautionary tale of Nat Swaine, whose conversion ruined him as a whaleman.
- (019-006) — Departing the ship, Ishmael and Queequeg are accosted by [[elijah|Elijah]], who asks ominously: "Anything down there about your souls?"

## Key Participants

- **[[queequeg|Queequeg]]** — the harpooneer whose tattoo, harpoon skill, and quiet dignity overcome the captains' objections
- **[[ishmael|Ishmael]]** — the narrator, whose rhetorical improvisation transforms a practical obstacle into a moment of universalist theology
- **[[peleg|Captain Peleg]]** — co-owner of the Pequod, pragmatist; won over by the demonstration and eager to secure a skilled harpooneer
- **[[bildad|Captain Bildad]]** — co-owner, Quaker zealot; unable to resist a final religious tract, but the lay and the industry override his scruples

## Narrative Significance

The signing scene dramatizes the transactional logic of the whaling industry. Queequeg's cannibal body and tattooed skin — markers of absolute alterity — are recoded as assets once his harpoon proves his economic value. The "X mark" that concludes the chapter is a signature that is also a wound: Queequeg's identity is literally inscribed onto the legal instrument of his three-year bondage.

Ishmael's "First Congregational Church" sermon is one of the novel's key statements of religious cosmopolitanism, foreshadowing the multi-ethnic crew assembled in Chapter 40 ("Midnight, Forecastle") and the novel's sustained argument that the ship is a floating world.

The scene also introduces Bildad and Peleg as a comic double-act — the pious Quaker and the pragmatic sailor — whose bickering establishes the Pequod's divided character before Ahab ever appears.

## Aftermath / Consequences

Queequeg's status aboard the Pequod is formally established. Peleg's persistent misnaming ("Quohog") becomes an ironic running joke — a man whose mark proves his literacy is nevertheless recorded under the wrong name. The following day (Ch. 20), word is given that chests must be aboard, and the Pequod's preparations accelerate. The signing also brings Queequeg and Ishmael to the attention of [[elijah|Elijah]], who intercepts them on the wharf with cryptic warnings about [[ahab|Captain Ahab]].
