# Cannibalism

## Definition

Cannibalism in *Moby-Dick* operates on three interconnected levels: the literal cannibalism of Queequeg's South Seas origins, the metaphorical cannibalism of human social and economic relations, and the cosmic cannibalism of the natural world — "the universal cannibalism of the sea; all whose creatures prey upon each other, carrying on eternal war since the world began" (058-011). Melville uses the motif not to sensationalize but to dismantle: the civilized Christian who recoils from the "cannibal" harpooneer discovers, over the course of the novel, that cannibalism is not an exotic aberration but the fundamental condition of existence.

The motif traces Ishmael's moral education. He arrives at the Spouter-Inn terrified of sharing a bed with a "cannibal and a tomahawk" (004-004). He leaves it understanding that the cannibal's heart is more Christian than Christendom's. This inversion — the savage as moral exemplar, civilization as hypocritical predation — is one of Melville's most radical gestures.

## Manifestations in the Novel

### Queequeg: The Cannibal Who Teaches Humanity

The novel's treatment of cannibalism centers on Queequeg, a South Seas harpooneer from the fictional island of Kokovoko. Ishmael's first encounter with him is filtered entirely through the language of cannibal panic: "what monstrous cannibal and savage could ever have gone on a whaling voyage?" (003-004). The landlord's reassurances only heighten the horror: Queequeg has been "selling the heads of dead idolators" (003-046) — a literal traffic in human remains that confirms every stereotype Ishmael carries.

When Ishmael finally sees Queequeg, the description is a catalogue of savagery: "a wild cannibal, tomahawk between his teeth" (003-061), his face "a dark, purplish, yellow color" covered in tattoos, his manner "horrid flourishing of the tomahawk" (003-065). Ishmael's terror climaxes in the exclamation: "that infernal harpooneer was a cannibal!" (003-067).

Yet the revelation is immediate. The next morning, Queequeg's arm is thrown over Ishmael "in the most loving and affectionate manner." The cannibal, it turns out, has better manners than the Christian. "You had almost thought I had been his wife," Ishmael marvels. The word "cannibal" does not disappear from Queequeg's description — in New Bedford, "actual cannibals stand chatting at street corners" (006-002) — but it is neutralized by familiarity. By Chapter 10, Ishmael and Queequeg are "married" in a "bosom friendship" that makes the epithet irrelevant.

### The Universal Cannibalism of the Sea

In Chapter 58, "The Brit," Melville expands the cannibalism motif to cosmic scale:

> "Consider, once more, the universal cannibalism of the sea; all whose creatures prey upon each other, carrying on eternal war since the world began." (058-011)

This is cannibalism without moral valence. The whale eats the brit; the whaleman kills the whale; the shark eats the whaleman's corpse. The sea is a devouring force, and every creature in it is both predator and prey. Melville presents this not as evil but as the condition of life itself.

### Cannibals at the Table: The Metaphorical Extension

Melville extends cannibalism into metaphor throughout the novel. In "The Cabin Table" (Ch. 34), the officers' dining hierarchy is described as "the white waiter who waits upon cannibals" (034-009) — the "cannibals" here being the ship's officers, whose social predation is a refined version of the physical kind. In "Stubb's Supper" (Ch. 64), the sharks feeding on a whale carcass are described as "cannibally carving each other's live meat" (064-009).

The most explicit statement comes in Chapter 65, "The Whale as a Dish," where Melville directly addresses the reader: "Cannibals? who is not a cannibal?" (065-006). The question is rhetorical. All consumption, Melville implies, is a form of cannibalism — all life feeds on other life. The distinction between the savage who eats his enemy and the civilized man who eats his roast beef is one of custom, not essence.

## The Inversion of the Civilized/Savage Binary

The cannibalism motif serves Melville's larger project of interrogating the boundary between civilization and savagery. Queequeg, the literal cannibal, is the novel's most morally upright character — "George Washington cannibalistically developed" (010-003). He is loyal, generous, brave, and without guile. The civilized Christians he encounters — the Nantucket owners who cheat their crew, Ahab who sacrifices his men to a private vengeance — are the true predators.

This inversion reaches its most compressed form in Queequeg's rescue of the bumpkin in Chapter 13. The white sailor who had mocked Queequeg on shore falls overboard; Queequeg dives in and saves him. The cannibal saves the Christian. "We cannibals must help these Christians" (013-015), Queequeg says — a sentence that reverses every assumption the reader might bring to the word "cannibal."

## Scholarly Context

The cannibalism motif in *Moby-Dick* has been read through postcolonial, anthropological, and ecocritical lenses. Postcolonial critics have focused on Melville's use of the cannibal trope to critique Western imperialism: Queequeg is the colonized subject whose humanity the novel insists upon against the grain of 19th-century racial science. Anthropological readings connect Melville's treatment of cannibalism to the broader 19th-century fascination with "the primitive" — the novel both participates in and subverts this discourse.

Ecocritical approaches have emphasized the "universal cannibalism of the sea" passage as an early articulation of ecological interconnectedness. Melville's vision of a world in which "all whose creatures prey upon each other" anticipates the Darwinian understanding of nature as a web of consumption, but without the moral optimism of Victorian natural theology. The sea's cannibalism is eternal, amoral, and inescapable.
