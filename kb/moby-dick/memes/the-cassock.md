# The Cassock

## Definition

The Cassock is Melville's comic designation for the sperm whale's penis—an enormous, jet-black cone of flesh that, when flayed, dried, and worn as a protective garment by the mincer, becomes a parodic priestly vestment. Chapter 95 is one of the novel's most audaciously humorous set-pieces, using the whale's phallus to lampoon clerical authority while simultaneously marveling at the strangeness of the whale's body.

## Manifestations in the Novel

The episode occurs during the post-mortem processing of a sperm whale aboard the Pequod. After describing the whale's head (Chapter 70 through 75) and other anatomical wonders, Melville arrives at what he calls "a very strange, enigmatical object"—"that unaccountable cone,—longer than a Kentuckian is tall, nigh a foot in diameter at the base, and jet-black as Yojo, the ebony idol of Queequeg."

Melville's description oscillates between anatomical precision and mock-religious reverence. The organ is called "the grandissimus, as the mariners call it," and the process of flaying it is described in elaborate detail: the mincer "cylindrically removes its dark pelt, as an African hunter the pelt of a boa," then turns it inside out "like a pantaloon leg," stretches it, dries it in the rigging, and finally "lengthwise slips himself bodily into it."

The mincer, now "invested in the full canonicals of his calling," stands before the reader as a figure of clerical parody. His "office" of mincing blubber at a "curious wooden horse" with a tub beneath it becomes a mock-ecclesiastical ritual, complete with a "conspicuous pulpit" and "bible leaves"—the latter being the mates' cry for thin-cut blubber slices. Melville's footnote drives the joke home: "*Bible leaves! Bible leaves!* This is the invariable cry from the mates to the mincer."

The humor is not merely bawdy but structurally sophisticated. Melville takes the most sacred garment of Christian clergy—the cassock—and reveals its "true" form in the genital anatomy of a whale, simultaneously mocking religious pretension and celebrating the carnivalesque body. The chapter participates in a long tradition of Rabelaisian humor while advancing the novel's larger project of dismantling the boundary between sacred and profane.

## Key Passages

> "Not the wondrous cistern in the whale's huge head; not the prodigy of his unhinged lower jaw; not the miracle of his symmetrical tail; none of these would so surprise you, as half a glimpse of that unaccountable cone." (095-001)

Melville builds comic suspense by comparing the whale's penis to its other anatomical marvels and declaring it the most astonishing of all—a mock-heroic escalation.

> "And an idol, indeed, it is; or, rather, in old times, its likeness was. Such an idol as that found in the secret groves of Queen Maachah in Judea; and for worshipping which, King Asa, her son, did depose her, and destroyed the idol, and burnt it for an abomination at the brook Kedron." (095-001)

The biblical reference (1 Kings 15) elevates the phallus into the realm of idolatry, with the comic implication that phallic worship has ancient and distinguished precedent.

> "Arrayed in decent black; occupying a conspicuous pulpit; intent on bible leaves; what a candidate for an archbishopric, what a lad for a Pope were this mincer!" (095-004)

The paragraph's crescendo: the mincer, dressed in a whale-penis cassock, cutting blubber at his "pulpit," is proposed as a candidate for the highest ecclesiastical offices. The satire is unmistakable—religious authority is reduced to costume, posture, and performance.

## Scholarly Context

Critical readings of "The Cassock" have emphasized its place in Melville's broader pattern of religious skepticism and bodily humor. The chapter has been read through Bakhtinian carnival theory, with the whale's body serving as a site where hierarchies are inverted and the sacred is brought low. Feminist critics have noted the gendered dimensions of the humor, while queer theory approaches have found in the chapter a destabilization of normative masculinity. More recently, eco-critical readings have emphasized Melville's attention to the material reality of the whale's body as a counterweight to symbolic appropriation.
