# The Quadrant

## Definition

The quadrant — more precisely a sextant or reflecting quadrant — is the navigational instrument Ahab uses to take daily observations of the sun to determine the Pequod's latitude. Melville describes it as an "astrological-looking instrument" furnished with "coloured glasses, through which to take sight of that solar fire" (118-002), a device that enables precise celestial navigation on the open ocean. In Ahab's hands, however, the quadrant becomes the object of one of the novel's most dramatic gestures of rebellion: confronted with its inability to tell him "where some other thing besides me is this moment living" — specifically, where Moby Dick might be — Ahab denounces it as a "foolish toy" and "vain toy" and dashes it to the deck, splitting and trampling it (118-003).

## Manifestations in the Novel

The quadrant episode occurs in Chapter 118, "The Quadrant," as the Pequod approaches the equatorial Line. The day is fiercely bright, the Japanese sun "the blazing focus of the glassy ocean's immeasurable burning-glass" (118-002). Ahab takes his observation with meticulous precision, calculates his latitude, and then falls into a meditation that modulates from technical navigation into metaphysical rebellion. Addressing the sun directly — "Thou sea-mark! thou high and mighty Pilot!" — Ahab demands to know not where he is but where Moby Dick is, exposing the fundamental limitation of the instrument: it can locate a position in space but cannot reveal the position of a quarry or foretell a destiny.

Ahab's smashing of the quadrant is a calculated act of rejection, not a fit of temper. He enumerates the alternatives: "the level ship's compass, and the level dead-reckoning, by log and by line; *these* shall conduct me, and show me my place on the sea" (118-003). The word "level" — repeated five times in the chapter's climax — contrasts earthly, horizontal navigation against the vertical, celestial guidance the quadrant represents. Ahab chooses the horizontal: a course plotted by compass, log, and dead reckoning, instruments of human calculation rather than heavenly reference.

The consequences unfold across subsequent chapters. In "The Needle" (Ch. 124), lightning from the typhoon has reversed the Pequod's compasses, making them point east when the ship steers west — a catastrophe that Ahab dismisses with a "rigid laugh." In "The Log and Line" (Ch. 125), Ahab "remembered how his quadrant was no more, and recalled his frantic oath about the level log and line" (125-001), ordering the log heaved with a decayed line. Starbuck, in his anguished soliloquy in "The Musket" (Ch. 123), cites the smashed quadrant as one of the proofs of Ahab's madness: "Has he not dashed his heavenly quadrant? and in these same perilous seas, gropes he not his way by mere dead reckoning of the error-abounding log?" (123-008).

## Key Passages

> "Well that Ahab's quadrant was furnished with coloured glasses, through which to take sight of that solar fire. So, swinging his seated form to the roll of the ship, and with his astrological-looking instrument placed to his eye, he remained in that posture for some moments to catch the precise instant when the sun should gain its precise meridian."
> (118-002)

> "Thou sea-mark! thou high and mighty Pilot! thou tellest me truly where I *am* — but canst thou cast the least hint where I *shall* be? Or canst thou tell where some other thing besides me is this moment living? Where is Moby Dick?"
> (118-002)

> "Curse thee, thou quadrant! No longer will I guide my earthly way by thee; the level ship's compass, and the level dead-reckoning, by log and by line; *these* shall conduct me, and show me my place on the sea. Aye, thus I trample on thee, thou paltry thing that feebly pointest on high; thus I split and destroy thee!"
> (118-003)

> "Has he not dashed his heavenly quadrant? and in these same perilous seas, gropes he not his way by mere dead reckoning of the error-abounding log?"
> (123-008)
