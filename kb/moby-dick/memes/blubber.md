# Blubber

## Definition

In *Moby-Dick*, blubber is the thick, fibrous layer of fat that encases the whale's body beneath its transparent outer skin. Melville devotes an entire chapter (Ch. 68, "The Blanket") to anatomizing blubber, describing it as "something of the consistence of firm, close-grained beef, but tougher, more elastic and compact, and ranges from eight or ten to twelve and fifteen inches in thickness" [068-002]. Functionally, blubber serves as the whale's insulation against the frigid depths of the ocean, its energy reserve, and — crucially for the whaling industry — the raw material from which whale oil is rendered. Ishmael provocatively frames the question of whether blubber constitutes the whale's skin, noting that "you cannot raise any other dense enveloping layer from the whale's body but that same blubber; and the outermost enveloping layer of any animal, if reasonably dense, what can that be but the skin?" [068-003].

## Manifestations in the Novel

Blubber permeates the novel at every level — as anatomical substance, industrial commodity, and metaphorical register. Ishmael early on declares "no more of this blubbering now, we are going a-whaling" [002-012], using the word as a pun that equates sentimental weeping with whale fat. Throughout the narrative, blubber anchors the gritty material reality of whaling: the "great blubber hook, weighing some one hundred pounds" [067-002] that hoists the stripped blanket of fat aboard; the "blubber-room" deep in the ship where harpooneers retreat to squeeze coagulated lumps of spermaceti into oil (Ch. 94); the mincing of "horse-pieces of blubber for the pots" [095-003] in the try-works. Melville contrasts the epic scale of the whale's integument — "a mere part of whose mere integument yields such a lake of liquid" [068-004] — against the domestic economy of whale oil, lamp by lamp. Blubber even enters the novel's lexicon of scorn: landsmen dismiss whalers with "gamesome stuff about 'spouters' and 'blubber-boilers'" [053-006], blind to the grandeur of the pursuit.

## Scholarly Context

Blubber in *Moby-Dick* has been read as a figure for the material basis of American industrial capitalism — the raw substance that drives an entire global economy of light and lubrication. Critics have also noted its epistemological dimension: Ishmael's obsessive anatomy of blubber in Chapter 68 ("The Blanket") mirrors the novel's broader project of attempting to know the whale through its material body, a project that repeatedly confronts the limits of empirical knowledge. The pun on "blubbering" further ties the whale's body to human affect, linking the physical substance of the whale to the emotional register of the novel.
