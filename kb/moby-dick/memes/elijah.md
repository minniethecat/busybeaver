# Elijah

## Overview

Elijah is a mysterious, ragged prophet who appears on the New Bedford docks in Chapters 19 and 21 of *Moby-Dick*. He accosts [[ishmael|Ishmael]] and [[queequeg|Queequeg]] after they sign the [[pequod|Pequod's]] articles, delivering cryptic warnings about [[ahab|Captain Ahab]] and the voyage to come.

Ishmael initially dismisses him as "a humbug, trying to be a bugbear" (019-034). But Elijah's prophecies — about Ahab's hidden history, the shadowy figures boarding the ship, and the fate of those who sail — linger in Ishmael's mind long after the prophet himself vanishes from the narrative.

His name directly invokes the Biblical prophet Elijah, and like his Old Testament namesake, he speaks of things unseen and fates unavoidable. Yet Melville renders him shabby, scarred, and socially marginal — a prophet in rags, half-mad and wholly unsettling.

## Role in the Narrative

Elijah serves as the novel's first explicit voice of doom. Before Ishmael ever lays eyes on Ahab, Elijah plants seeds of unease that color every subsequent scene on the Pequod.

He first appears immediately after Ishmael and Queequeg sign the ship's papers:

> "Shipmates, have ye shipped in that ship?" (019-033)

His interrogation is oblique but relentless. He asks about their souls. He references Ahab's mysterious Cape Horn fit, the "deadly skrimmage with the Spaniard," and the prophecy that foretold Ahab losing his leg — none of which Ishmael knows. When Ishmael asks his name, the answer lands with biblical weight:

> "Elijah." (019-034)

In Chapter 21, Elijah reappears at dawn on sailing day. He insinuates himself between Ishmael and Queequeg, asks whether they have seen "four or five men" boarding the ship — an allusion to [[fedallah|Fedallah]] and his stowaway crew — and then vanishes with the cryptic parting:

> "Shan't see ye again very soon, I guess; unless it's before the Grand Jury." (021-017)

His function in the novel is that of a threshold guardian: one who warns at the gate, but whose warnings go unheeded.

## Character Traits

### The Prophet in Rags

Elijah's physical appearance is described in meticulous, unflattering detail: shabbily apparelled in faded jacket and patched trousers, his face deeply scarred by smallpox — "like the complicated ribbed bed of a torrent, when the rushing waters have been dried up" (019-033). He is physically repulsive yet magnetically compelling.

### Cryptic and Half-Revealing

Elijah never states his warnings plainly. Instead he speaks in fragments, ellipses, and foreboding allusions:

- "Anything down there about your souls?" (019-033)
- "Old Thunder" — Ahab's nickname among old sailors
- Ahab's Cape Horn fit, the silver calabash, the prophecy of the leg
- "What's signed, is signed; and what's to be, will be" (019-034)

His method is to unsettle, not to inform. He gives enough to disturb, never enough to prevent.

### The Biblical Echo

The name Elijah is not casual. In the Old Testament, Elijah was the prophet who confronted Ahab, the wicked king of Israel. In Melville's novel, Elijah confronts the sailors bound for Captain Ahab's ship. The inversion is deliberate: the biblical Elijah warned a king; Melville's Elijah warns ordinary men about a king-like captain.

Ishmael himself makes no explicit biblical connection — the irony is left for the reader.

## Relationships

- **[[ishmael|Ishmael]]**: Elijah's primary interlocutor. He treats Ishmael with a mixture of pity and cryptic playfulness. His final words — "you are just the man for him — the likes of ye" (019-034) — suggest he recognizes in Ishmael a worthy witness.
- **[[queequeg|Queequeg]]**: Elijah regards Queequeg with inscrutable glances but addresses him little. Queequeg's response is visceral — "go 'way!" (021-003) — suggesting instinctive recognition of something uncanny.
- **[[ahab|Captain Ahab]]**: Elijah never meets Ahab in the novel, but speaks of him with intimate knowledge — his nickname "Old Thunder," his secret history, his inexorable fate. Elijah functions as Ahab's shadow-herald.
- **[[fedallah|Fedallah]]**: Elijah's question about "four or five men" boarding the ship foreshadows Fedallah and his stowaway crew, though the connection is only made explicit in Ishmael's retrospective narration (048-020).

## Critical Interpretation

Elijah is one of Melville's most economical creations: he appears in only two chapters yet casts a shadow across the entire novel. Critics have read him as:

- A **folk-prophet figure** in the American Jeremiad tradition — warning of doom while knowing the warning will be ignored
- A **dramatic device** that builds Ahab's mythos before the captain ever appears on stage
- A **doubling of Ishmael** — both are interpreters of signs, but Elijah sees doom where Ishmael sees adventure

His ragged, marginal status — scarred, shabbily dressed, wandering the docks — embodies the Romantic trope of truth residing with society's outcasts.
