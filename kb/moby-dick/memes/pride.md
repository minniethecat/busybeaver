# Pride

## Definition

Pride in *Moby-Dick* operates on a scale from ordinary dignity to catastrophic arrogance. At its most innocent, it is the "lonely pride" (029-001) of Nantucket wives nursing the memory of absent husbands — domestic, nostalgic, forgivable. At its most destructive, it is Ahab's "fatal pride" (124-021), the spiritual arrogance that refuses all limits and drives a ship and its crew into the abyss. Between these poles, Melville catalogs intermediate forms: the "general pride of manhood" (054-012) that fuels shipboard rivalries, the "foolish pride of reason" (083-005) that mistakes human intellect for mastery over nature, and the whale's own "undashed pride of hull and spars" (055-012) — an animal dignity that exposes the absurdity of human grandiosity. The novel's tragic arc is, in large measure, the arc of pride's movement from dignity to destruction.

## Manifestations in the Novel

**The wives' lonely pride (Chapter 29)**. In a brief, tender image, Ishmael conjures the Nantucket wives who sit "nursing at home in lonely pride, the memory of their absent conquerors" (029-001). This is pride as emotional survival — the dignity of those left behind, sustaining themselves on memory. It is the only form of pride in the novel that Melville treats without irony.

**Ahab's fatal pride (Chapters 124, 135)**. The novel's catastrophe pivots on Ahab's refusal to bend. In "The Needle" (Chapter 124), after the storm has reversed the compass, the crew watches Ahab "in all his fatal pride" (124-021) as he recreates the ship's guidance by his own hand, defying the natural order. This is the summit of Ahab's self-deification — the captain claiming the powers of a god. In the final chase, Ahab sees himself "cut off from the last fond pride of meanest shipwrecked captains" (135-058) — even the dignity of a captain going down with his ship is denied him as he is dragged under by his own harpoon line. Pride demands total engagement; total engagement ensures total annihilation.

**The pride of manhood (Chapter 54)**. The Town-Ho story includes a revealing aside on masculine vanity: Steelkilt "was instantly his superior in general pride of manhood" (054-012) in comparison with Radney. This is the pride of physical prowess and social dominance, the competitive self-regard that structures the hierarchy of the forecastle. Unlike Ahab's cosmic pride, this is recognizable, almost banal — the vanity that drives fistfights and grudges rather than shipwrecks.

**The whale's undashed pride (Chapter 55)**. In one of the novel's most provocative gestures, Melville attributes pride to the whale itself: the sperm whale reveals "itself in all its undashed pride of hull and spars" (055-012). The adjective "undashed" is crucial — unlike human pride, which is always vulnerable to humiliation, the whale's pride is invulnerable, self-contained. The whale does not need recognition. By giving the animal the dignity that humans so anxiously pursue, Melville inverts the normal hierarchy: perhaps the whale has more right to pride than its hunters.

**The foolish pride of reason (Chapter 83)**. Jonah's story becomes a parable of intellectual arrogance: the biblical Jonah "evinced his foolish pride of reason" (083-005) by questioning the divine command. This is the pride of the Enlightenment — the assumption that human reason can sit in judgment on mysteries it cannot comprehend. The novel's many digressions on cetology, on the futility of systematic knowledge, all circle back to this insight: the pride of reason is the most seductive and the most self-defeating form of pride.

**Surgical pride (Chapter 70)**. Even the minor trades of the whaling industry are touched by pride: "whale surgeons very much pride themselves" (070-001) on their anatomical knowledge. This is professional vanity, the self-satisfaction of expertise — harmless in isolation, but part of the novel's broader argument that all human claims to mastery are provisional and precarious.

- (029-001) — Nantucket wives "nursing at home in lonely pride, the memory of their absent conquerors" — pride as domestic dignity.
- (054-012) — Steelkilt "was instantly his superior in general pride of manhood" — competitive masculine pride.
- (055-012) — The whale reveals "itself in all its undashed pride of hull and spars" — animal dignity beyond human reach.
- (070-001) — "Whale surgeons very much pride themselves" on anatomy — professional vanity.
- (083-005) — Jonah "evinced his foolish pride of reason" — intellectual arrogance as a form of sin.
- (124-021) — The crew "then saw Ahab in all his fatal pride" — the summit of self-deification.
- (135-058) — Ahab "cut off from the last fond pride of meanest shipwrecked captains" — pride's final cost.

## Scholarly Context

Ahab's pride has been the central subject of tragic readings of the novel since its critical rediscovery in the 1920s. F. O. Matthiessen placed Ahab in the tradition of Shakespearean tragic heroes whose *hubris* ensures their destruction. More recent postcolonial and ecocritical readings have complicated this framework: Ahab's pride is not merely personal failing but a concentrated expression of Western industrial humanity's domination drive. The whale's "undashed pride" (055-012) has been read as Melville's anticipation of what would now be called animal dignity — a non-human moral standing that exposes the violence of human claims to supremacy. The "foolish pride of reason" (083-005), meanwhile, aligns the novel with a counter-Enlightenment tradition that runs from Pascal through Kierkegaard.
