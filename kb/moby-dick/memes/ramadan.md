# Ramadan

## Definition

Ramadan in *Moby-Dick* refers not to the Islamic holy month but to Queequeg's personal day of "Fasting and Humiliation" — a prolonged, silent vigil observed by the harpooneer in the shared room at the Spouter-Inn. Ishmael, unfamiliar with the ritual, applies the term "Ramadan" loosely, having previously heard it described as "some sort of Lent or Ramadan, or day of fasting, humiliation, and prayer" (016-003).

The episode unfolds entirely in Chapter 17, "The Ramadan," and forms one of the novel's most sustained comic-philosophical meditations on religious practice and cross-cultural misunderstanding.

## Manifestations in the Novel

### The Vigil

Queequeg's Ramadan begins with Ishmael's departure to arrange their passage on a whaling ship. When Ishmael returns toward evening, he finds the door bolted from within. Queequeg has been sitting motionless all day, holding Yojo — his small black idol — on his head:

> As Queequeg's Ramadan, or Fasting and Humiliation, was to continue all day, I did not choose to disturb him till towards night-fall; for I cherish the greatest respect towards everybody's religious obligations, never mind how comical. (017-001)

The humor of the scene is layered: Ishmael, the self-described Presbyterian Christian, attempts to respect a practice he finds absurd while simultaneously worrying that Queequeg may have suffered a stroke.

### Mrs. Hussey's Intervention

[[mrs-hussey|Mrs. Hussey]], the landlady of the Spouter-Inn, arrives with mustard-pot and vinegar-cruet, suspecting apoplexy. Her practical concern contrasts with Ishmael's philosophical musings, grounding the episode in the domestic comedy of the inn.

### The Night Vigil

Ishmael spends a sleepless night in the same room with the fasting Queequeg, whose motionless presence becomes uncanny:

> Think of it; sleeping all night in the same room with a wide awake pagan on his hams in this dreary, unaccountable Ramadan! (017-023)

When dawn breaks, Queequeg rises stiffly, presses his forehead to Ishmael's, and announces his Ramadan is over. He promptly eats "a prodigiously hearty breakfast of chowders of all sorts" (017-030), restoring the comic equilibrium.

### Ishmael's Sermon

Before Queequeg breaks his fast, Ishmael delivers an earnest lecture on comparative religion, attempting to convince Queequeg that "all these Lents, Ramadans, and prolonged ham-squattings in cold, cheerless rooms were stark nonsense; bad for the health; useless for the soul" (017-026). The passage is rich with dramatic irony — Ishmael's Presbyterian confidence is undercut by his own admission that "we are all somehow dreadfully cracked about the head, and sadly need mending" (017-002).

## Key Passages

- (016-003) — Ishmael first describes Queequeg's practice as "some sort of Lent or Ramadan," establishing the borrowed terminology.
- (017-001) — The chapter's opening: Ishmael's declaration of respect for all religious obligations, "never mind how comical."
- (017-002) — "We are all somehow dreadfully cracked about the head" — the universalizing turn that transforms comedy into philosophy.
- (017-023) — Ishmael's sleepless night in the "dreary, unaccountable Ramadan."
- (017-026) — The anti-fasting sermon, with its burlesque argument that "hell is an idea first born on an undigested apple-dumpling."
- (017-030) — Queequeg's hearty breakfast, restoring bodily normalcy after spiritual discipline.

## Scholarly Context

The Ramadan chapter has been read as Melville's intervention in nineteenth-century debates about religious pluralism. Ishmael's initial posture of Christian superiority gives way to a recognition that Queequeg's "absurd" ritual is no more irrational than Presbyterian orthodoxy. The episode prefigures the novel's larger argument: that all systems of belief are, in some sense, arbitrary human constructs before the inscrutable vastness of the whale and the sea.
