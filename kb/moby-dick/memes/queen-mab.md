# Queen Mab

## Definition

"Queen Mab" is the title of Chapter 31 of *Moby-Dick*, named after the fairy queen of English folklore who was said to bring dreams to sleeping mortals (most famously in Mercutio's speech in *Romeo and Juliet*). In Melville's chapter, the second mate [[stubb|Stubb]] recounts to [[flask|Flask]] a bizarre dream he had after being kicked by [[ahab|Ahab]]'s ivory leg the previous night. The dream, in which a "badger-haired old merman" transforms the insult of the kick into a perverse honor, becomes one of the novel's sharpest explorations of power, submission, and the psychological alchemy by which the weak rationalize their subordination.

## Manifestations in the Novel

The chapter takes place the morning after Ahab's first appearance on the quarter-deck (Chapter 29), where Stubb had dared to suggest that Ahab moderate the noise of his nightly pacing. Ahab's response was a violent kick with his ivory leg and a torrent of abuse. Chapter 31 opens with Stubb telling Flask about the dream that followed.

**The Dream Narrative.** In Stubb's dream, he tries to kick Ahab back, but his own leg comes off. Ahab transforms into a pyramid, and Stubb — "like a blazing fool" — keeps kicking at it ineffectually. An old merman with a hump intervenes, revealing that his own stern is "stuck full of marlinspikes" — a deterrent to any would-be kicker.

**The Merman's Argument.** The merman's ingenious argument is that Ahab's kick was actually an *honor*: "Didn't he kick with right good will? it wasn't a common pitch pine leg he kicked with, was it? No, you were kicked by a great man, and with a beautiful ivory leg, Stubb. It's an honor; I consider it an honor." He compares it to English lords who consider it "great glory to be slapped by a queen, and made garter-knights of."

**The "Wise" Resolution.** Stubb wakes convinced that the dream has made him "a wise man." His new wisdom consists of a policy of total avoidance: "the best thing you can do, Flask, is to let the old man alone; never speak to him, whatever he says."

## Key Passages

> "Such a queer dream, King-Post, I never had. You know the old man's ivory leg, well I dreamed he kicked me with it; and when I tried to kick back, upon my soul, my little man, I kicked my leg right off! And then, presto! Ahab seemed a pyramid, and I, like a blazing fool, kept kicking at it." (031-005)

> "'Well then,' says he, 'wise Stubb, what have you to complain of? Didn't he kick with right good will? it wasn't a common pitch pine leg he kicked with, was it? No, you were kicked by a great man, and with a beautiful ivory leg, Stubb. It's an honor; I consider it an honor.'" (031-005)

> "I don't know; it seems a sort of foolish to me, tho.'" — Flask's response to Stubb's dream (031-007)

## Thematic Analysis

1. **The Psychology of Submission.** The dream dramatizes what modern psychology would call cognitive dissonance reduction or "identification with the aggressor." Unable to retaliate against Ahab, Stubb's psyche manufactures a justification that transforms humiliation into honor.

2. **The Pyramid Symbol.** Ahab-as-pyramid suggests the monumental, immovable, inhuman quality of absolute authority. A pyramid cannot be kicked, reasoned with, or overthrown — it simply *is*, indifferent to the "blazing fool" battering at its base.

3. **Class and Power.** The merman's reference to English lords and garter-knights embeds the dream in a critique of feudal hierarchy. The "honor" of being kicked by a great man is revealed as the psychological glue that holds unjust social structures together.

4. **Stubb's "Wisdom."** Stubb's conclusion — to avoid Ahab entirely — is both shrewd and cowardly. His "wisdom" is the wisdom of the survivor, not the resister. Melville leaves it ambiguous whether this constitutes genuine prudence or moral failure.

5. **Flask as Foil.** Flask's uncomprehending response ("it seems a sort of foolish to me") highlights the gulf between Stubb's psychological complexity and Flask's literal-minded simplicity. Flask cannot understand the dream because he lacks the imaginative capacity to process his own subordination.

## The Queen Mab Allusion

The title alludes to Mercutio's famous "Queen Mab" speech in *Romeo and Juliet* (I.iv), which describes a fairy who gallops through lovers' brains and "begats" dreams. Mercutio's speech, like Stubb's dream, uses fantastical imagery to deliver sharp social satire. Melville's borrowing of the title signals that the chapter will operate in this same mode — a dream that is at once absurd and devastatingly revealing.
