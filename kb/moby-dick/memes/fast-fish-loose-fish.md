# Fast-Fish and Loose-Fish

## Definition

The doctrines of Fast-Fish and Loose-Fish are the two foundational principles of whaling law introduced in Chapter 89. Melville states them with mock-legal brevity: "I. A Fast-Fish belongs to the party fast to it. II. A Loose-Fish is fair game for anybody who can soonest catch it" (089-004–005). A whale is technically "fast" when connected to an occupied ship or boat "by any medium at all controllable by the occupant or occupants — a mast, an oar, a nine-inch cable, a telegraph wire, or a strand of cobweb, it is all the same" (089-008). A Loose-Fish, by contrast, belongs to whoever can seize it first. Melville uses these seemingly narrow fishery regulations as the basis for a sweeping critique of property law, colonialism, and the nature of possession itself.

## Manifestations in the Novel

Melville introduces the doctrines midway through the novel, immediately after the extended cetological chapters, as if to pivot from natural history to political philosophy. The laws are presented as the whalemen's own invention — "the American fishermen have been their own legislators and lawyers in this matter" (089-003) — a system so concise it surpasses Justinian's Pandects, yet so ambiguous that "the admirable brevity of it necessitates a vast volume of commentaries to expound it" (089-006).

The chapter proceeds through a series of escalating examples. The legal commentary begins with a whaling dispute: an English court case in which the witty barrister Erskine compared a contested whale to an abandoned wife, arguing that a lady who had been "harpooned" and then abandoned became a "loose-fish," and therefore "when a subsequent gentleman re-harpooned her, the lady then became that subsequent gentleman's property" (089-014). The judge ruled for the defendants on the whale but for the plaintiffs on the boat — a decision Melville treats as emblematic of legal reasoning's absurdities.

From this comic courtroom scene, Melville accelerates into a ferocious catalogue. Fast-Fish becomes the metaphor for all unjust possession: "What are the sinews and souls of Russian serfs and Republican slaves but Fast-Fish, whereof possession is the whole of the law?" (089-015). The widow's last mite, the undetected villain's mansion, the Archbishop's income squeezed from starving laborers — all are Fast-Fish, all held by the mere fact of possession.

Loose-Fish becomes the doctrine of conquest: "What was America in 1492 but a Loose-Fish, in which Columbus struck the Spanish standard by way of waifing it for his royal master and mistress? What was Poland to the Czar? What Greece to the Turk? What India to England? What at last will Mexico be to the United States? All Loose-Fish" (089-017).

The chapter culminates in a vertiginous expansion: "What are the Rights of Man and the Liberties of the World but Loose-Fish? What all men's minds and opinions but Loose-Fish? ... What is the great globe itself but a Loose-Fish? And what are you, reader, but a Loose-Fish and a Fast-Fish, too?" (089-017). The reader herself is caught in the double bind — simultaneously possessed and up for grabs.

The companion Chapter 90 ("Heads or Tails") extends the satire to monarchy, recounting the legal requirement that a captured whale's head belongs to the King and its tail to the Queen — a literal embodiment of how political power carves up nature itself.

## Scholarly Context

The Fast-Fish and Loose-Fish chapter has been recognized as one of Melville's most concentrated political satires. Critics have read it as an anticipation of the legal realist critique that property is not a natural right but a social construction backed by force — "possession is half of the law," Melville writes, but "often possession is the whole of the law." The chapter's sweep from whaling dispute to global conquest maps directly onto nineteenth-century American expansion: "What at last will Mexico be to the United States?" is a pointed reference to the Mexican-American War (1846–48), contemporary with the novel's composition. The legal case of the whale and the wife has been analyzed as Melville's dark parody of the doctrine of coverture, under which married women were legal possessions of their husbands. The collapsing of whaling law, property law, marriage law, and imperial conquest into a single satiric framework makes Chapters 89–90 one of the novel's most radical and compressed philosophical performances.
