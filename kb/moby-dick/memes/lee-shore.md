# The Lee Shore

## The Chapter as "Stoneless Grave"

Chapter 23 of *Moby-Dick*, "The Lee Shore," is one of the shortest chapters in the novel — barely three paragraphs, which Melville himself calls a "six-inch chapter" — yet it occupies a critical structural position. Coming immediately after the crew has been assembled and the Pequod has finally put to sea, the chapter pauses the narrative to deliver what is in effect a funeral oration for Bulkington, a mariner glimpsed in the New Bedford chapters who has now shipped aboard the Pequod but will never be heard from again in the novel.

Melville signals the chapter's elegiac function directly: "Wonderfullest things are ever the unmentionable; deep memories yield no epitaphs; this six-inch chapter is the stoneless grave of Bulkington" (023-002). The chapter itself is a monument — not of stone but of prose, dedicated to a character who exists in the novel almost entirely as a symbol. Bulkington is the "land-hating sailor," the man for whom "the land seemed scorching to his feet" (023-002), and his disappearance from the narrative after this chapter makes him a ghost-figure, a personification of the principle the chapter articulates.

## The Lee Shore as Metaphysical Peril

The central figure of the chapter is drawn from nautical vocabulary: a lee shore is the coastline onto which the wind is blowing. For a sailing ship, a lee shore is the deadliest of situations — the wind drives the vessel inexorably toward the land, where rocks or surf will destroy it. The only salvation is to fight against the wind, to "crowd all sail off shore" and seek the open sea:

> The port would fain give succor; the port is pitiful; in the port is safety, comfort, hearthstone, supper, warm blankets, friends, all that's kind to our mortalities. But in that gale, the port, the land, is that ship's direst jeopardy; she must fly all hospitality; one touch of land, though it but graze the keel, would make her shudder through and through.
> (023-002)

The paradox is devastating in its simplicity: the very things that seem safest — the port, the land, the hearth — are, in the right conditions, the deadliest threat. The ship must resist its own instinct to seek shelter. Melville transforms a technical sailing problem into a metaphysical one: the soul, like the ship in a gale, is blown by "winds of heaven and earth" toward the "treacherous, slavish shore" of conventional safety, and its salvation lies in resisting that pull, in seeking "the open independence of her sea" (023-003).

## Landlessness as Highest Truth

From the metaphor of the lee shore, Melville extracts a radical philosophical claim:

> As in landlessness alone resides highest truth, shoreless, indefinite as God — so, better is it to perish in that howling infinite, than be ingloriously dashed upon the lee, even if that were safety!
> (023-004)

This is among the most extreme statements in the novel. Melville does not argue that truth is difficult to find on land, or that it is obscured by social convention; he argues that truth *cannot* reside on land at all. Truth is "shoreless, indefinite as God" — it has no fixed location, no boundary, no safe harbor. To seek truth is to accept landlessness as a permanent condition.

The chapter ennobles Bulkington precisely because he embodies this condition. Unlike Ishmael, who goes to sea to cure the hypos and will eventually return to tell the tale, Bulkington is constitutively unable to stay ashore: "just landed from a four years' dangerous voyage, could so unrestingly push off again for still another tempestuous term" (023-002). He is the sailor as existential hero — not because he conquers the sea but because he refuses the comfort of land.

The final lines of the chapter — "Take heart, take heart, O Bulkington! Bear thee grimly, demigod! Up from the spray of thy ocean-perishing — straight up, leaps thy apotheosis!" (023-004) — transform drowning into transcendence. To perish in the "howling infinite" is not defeat but apotheosis.

## Bulkington and the Novel's Economy of Characters

Bulkington's strange status in the novel — introduced with fanfare, then immediately disappeared — has puzzled readers since the novel's publication. He appears briefly in Chapter 3 at the Spouter-Inn, is mentioned again when the Pequod departs in Chapter 23, and is never heard from again. The critical consensus is that Bulkington is not a character in the conventional sense but a personified counter-principle to Ahab.

Where Ahab is driven by a single, obsessive purpose — to find and kill the White Whale — Bulkington is driven by no purpose at all except the refusal of land. Ahab's quest has a destination (the whale) and an end (death or victory); Bulkington's "quest" is simply to remain at sea, to remain in the condition of landlessness. He is the novel's purest image of what Ishmael, in Chapter 1, called "the ungraspable phantom of life" — a man who seeks nothing except the seeking itself.

The chapter also functions as a structural hinge. Coming between the shore-bound chapters of New Bedford and Nantucket and the open-sea chapters that follow, "The Lee Shore" marks the point at which the novel definitively leaves the land behind. After this chapter, the Pequod is at sea, and it will remain at sea until it sinks.

## The Lee Shore in the Novel's Imagery

Though the explicit metaphor of the lee shore is confined to Chapter 23, its logic pervades the novel. Ahab's pursuit of the White Whale is itself a flight from the lee shore — from the safety of home, from the conventional life of a whaling captain who fills his hold with oil and returns to port. Ahab, like Bulkington, chooses the "howling infinite" over the "inglorious" safety of the land. The difference is that Bulkington's choice is presented as noble and Ahab's as tragic — yet the novel's sympathies, in both cases, lie with the one who refuses the shore.

The Pequod itself enacts the lee-shore drama in its final moments: driven by the whale, drawn toward destruction, the ship is the vessel that cannot make port. The novel's last image — "the great shroud of the sea rolled on as it rolled five thousand years ago" (135-052) — is the ultimate image of landlessness: the sea that knows no shore, that will outlast all ships and all sailors.

## Scholarly Context

"The Lee Shore" has been central to existentialist readings of *Moby-Dick*. Critics have compared Bulkington to Kierkegaard's "knight of faith" — a figure who commits absolutely to an absurd condition without hope of worldly resolution. The chapter's rhetoric of "landlessness" and its celebration of self-destruction as "apotheosis" anticipate Nietzsche's concept of the *amor fati* — the affirmation of one's fate, however destructive.

Other critics have emphasized the chapter's place in Melville's political imagination. The "treacherous, slavish shore" (023-003) has been read as Melville's critique of American materialism and the safety of middle-class life. The choice between the lee shore and the open sea is also a choice between social conformity and individual authenticity. In this reading, Bulkington is Melville's Thoreauvian hero — the man who "marches to the beat of a different drummer" by refusing to march at all, by refusing to set foot on land.

The chapter's brevity has itself been the subject of critical commentary. At roughly 500 words, "The Lee Shore" is the novel's shortest chapter, and its compression has been read as a formal enactment of its content: the chapter is as "shoreless" as the truth it describes, refusing to provide the comfort of elaboration or the safety of extended argument.
