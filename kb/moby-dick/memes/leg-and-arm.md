# Leg and Arm

## Definition

Chapter 100, "Leg and Arm," stages one of the novel's most consequential encounters: Ahab's meeting with Captain Boomer of the English whaler *Samuel Enderby*, who has lost an arm to Moby Dick — just as Ahab has lost a leg. The chapter title names their shared condition: two captains, two missing limbs, one white whale. Yet the responses could not be more divergent. Where Ahab has organized his entire existence around revenge, Boomer has chosen to let the whale alone: "he's best let alone; don't you think so, Captain?" (100-036). The chapter functions as a controlled experiment in the psychology of trauma, posing the question of whether Ahab's monomania is an inevitable response to dismemberment or a choice for which he bears responsibility.

## Manifestations in the Novel

The encounter begins with Ahab's characteristic hail — "Ship, ahoy! Hast seen the White Whale?" (100-001) — and Boomer's wordless reply: he holds up "a white arm of sperm whale bone, terminating in a wooden head like a mallet" (100-004). This gesture establishes the chapter's method of dramatic parallelism. Both men carry their wounds visibly; both have replaced lost flesh with whalebone prosthetics. The difference lies not in what happened to them but in the meaning they have made of it.

Ahab's desperate eagerness to board the *Samuel Enderby* is thwarted by his own disability — without the *Pequod*'s "ingenious and very handy mechanical contrivance" (100-006), he is "abjectly reduced to a clumsy landsman again" (100-006). The scene is at once comic and painful: Ahab, the titanic captain, must be hoisted aboard in a blubber-hook "like sitting in the fluke of an anchor" (100-008). The meeting of the two captains is marked by a gesture of grim wit — Ahab crosses his ivory leg with Boomer's ivory arm "like two sword-fish blades" and cries, "let us shake bones together!—an arm and a leg!" (100-008).

Boomer's account of his encounter with Moby Dick is narrated with British understatement and self-deprecating humor, mediated by his ship's surgeon, Dr. Bunger, whose deadpan medical commentary provides a comic counterpoint. The whale, Boomer explains, took his arm when a harpoon line tangled in its teeth; the resulting wound was "as ugly gaping wound as surgeon ever saw" (100-027), and the arm was amputated. But unlike Ahab, Boomer has drawn a pragmatic conclusion: "No more White Whales for me; I've lowered for him once, and that has satisfied me" (100-036).

The chapter's diagnostic power lies in Dr. Bunger's examination of Ahab. Circling him "like a dog, strangely snuffing," Bunger declares: "this man's blood — bring the thermometer! — it's at the boiling point! — his pulse makes these planks beat!" (100-038). The medical gaze converts Ahab's spiritual condition into somatic symptom, diagnosing monomania as fever. Ahab's violent rejection — "Avast!" roared Ahab, dashing him against the bulwarks" (100-039) — confirms the diagnosis even as it silences the diagnostician. The chapter ends with Ahab's hasty departure, his "face set like a flint" (100-042), having heard but refused the lesson Boomer's example offers.

## Key Passages

> "So cried Ahab, once more hailing a ship showing English colours, bearing down under the stern. Trumpet to mouth, the old man was standing in his hoisted quarter-boat, his ivory leg plainly revealed to the stranger captain, who was carelessly reclining in his own boat's bow."
> (100-002)

> "Soon he was carefully swung inside the high bulwarks, and gently landed upon the capstan head. With his ivory arm frankly thrust forth in welcome, the other captain advanced, and Ahab, putting out his ivory leg, and crossing the ivory arm (like two sword-fish blades) cried out in his walrus way, 'Aye, aye, hearty! let us shake bones together!—an arm and a leg!—an arm that never can shrink, d'ye see; and a leg that never can run.'"
> (100-008)

> "'No, thank ye, Bunger,' said the English Captain, 'he's welcome to the arm he has, since I can't help it, and didn't know him then; but not to another one. No more White Whales for me; I've lowered for him once, and that has satisfied me. There would be great glory in killing him, I know that; and there is a ship-load of precious sperm in him, but, hark ye, he's best let alone; don't you think so, Captain?'—glancing at the ivory leg."
> (100-036)

> "'He is. But he will still be hunted, for all that. What is best let alone, that accursed thing is not always what least allures. He's all a magnet! How long since thou saw'st him last? Which way heading?'"
> (100-037)

> "'Bless my soul, and curse the foul fiend's,' cried Bunger, stoopingly walking round Ahab, and like a dog, strangely snuffing; 'this man's blood—bring the thermometer!—it's at the boiling point!—his pulse makes these planks beat!—sir!'"
> (100-038)
