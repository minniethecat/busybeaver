# Father Mapple

## Overview

Father Mapple is the chaplain of the Whalemen's Chapel in New Bedford, where [[Ishmael]] attends service before sailing on the [[Pequod]]. Introduced in [[Ch. 8: The Pulpit]](008-001), he is "the famous Father Mapple, so called by the whalemen, among whom he was a very great favourite." A former sailor and harpooneer in his youth who later dedicated his life to the ministry, Father Mapple embodies the fusion of maritime life and Puritan Christianity that pervades the novel. His sermon on Jonah and the Whale in [[Ch. 9: The Sermon]] is one of the most theologically charged passages in *Moby-Dick*, establishing the novel's central themes of sin, disobedience, repentance, and the terrifying majesty of God.

## Role in the Narrative

Father Mapple appears only in Chapters 8 and 9, but his sermon sets the spiritual and philosophical framework for everything that follows. His chapel itself — with its ship-like pulpit, its cenotaphs to dead whalemen, and its painting of a storm-tossed ship — is a space where the sacred and the maritime are inseparable.

The sermon on Jonah serves as a prefiguration of [[Ahab]]'s tragedy. Father Mapple presents Jonah not as a coward who fled God's command but as a sinner whose repentance required the ultimate trial: being swallowed into the "belly of hell" before he could deliver God's word. The sermon's climactic exhortation — "Woe to him who seeks to pour oil upon the waters when God has brewed them into a gale!" (009-034) — is an implicit warning against the kind of defiance Ahab will embody, and a call to submission before divine will.

Father Mapple's physical isolation in the pulpit — pulling up the ladder after himself so that he is "impregnable in his little Quebec" (008-003) — symbolizes the spiritual withdrawal of the preacher from worldly ties, a gesture that Ishmael interprets as signifying "his spiritual withdrawal for the time, from all outward worldly ties and connexions" (008-004).

## Key Appearances

- [[Ch. 7: The Chapel]](007-001) — Ishmael enters the Whalemen's Chapel and sees the marble tablets memorializing dead whalemen; the setting is prepared for Father Mapple's entrance
- [[Ch. 8: The Pulpit]](008-001–008-007) — Father Mapple enters the chapel, mounts his ship-like pulpit, and draws up the ladder behind him; description of the chapel's maritime architecture and its symbolism
- [[Ch. 9: The Sermon]](009-001–009-034) — Father Mapple delivers his sermon on Jonah and the Whale, from Jonah's flight from God to his deliverance from the whale, concluding with an exhortation to righteous obedience

## Character Traits

**Maritime piety** is Father Mapple's signature. His faith is not abstract or bookish but forged in the lived experience of a sailor. "He had been a sailor and a harpooneer in his youth, but for many years past had dedicated his life to the ministry" (008-001). The chapel itself reflects this fusion — the pulpit is shaped like a ship's prow, the ladder is rigged with man-ropes, and his entrance is performed "with a truly sailor-like but still reverential dexterity" (008-002).

His **physical robustness** is emphasized: he is in "the hardy winter of a healthy old age; that sort of old age which seems merging into a second flowering youth" (008-001). He arrives in the storm without umbrella or carriage, his tarpaulin hat "ran down with melting sleet." He is not a delicate clergyman but a hardened man of the sea.

His **oratorical power** dominates Chapter 9. His sermon on Jonah moves from "prolonged solemn tones, like the continual tolling of a bell in a ship that is foundering at sea in a fog" to "a pealing exultation and joy" (009-004). He embodies the Puritan tradition of the sermon as dramatic performance — a spiritual theater that transforms the chapel into a ship at sea.

## The Sermon on Jonah

Father Mapple's sermon in Chapter 9 is a close reading of the Book of Jonah, structured around Jonah's sin (disobedience to God's command to preach in Nineveh), his punishment (the storm and the whale), his repentance (prayer from the whale's belly), and his deliverance. Father Mapple's key theological move is to emphasize that Jonah's repentance was not a bargaining with God but a genuine submission: "He doth not say, 'Deliver me out of my peril if thou wilt deliver me.' He saith, 'I have cried unto thee — and yet will I cry unto thee.' Not conditional, but absolute" (009-026).

The sermon's conclusion — "Woe to him who seeks to pour oil upon the waters when God has brewed them into a gale! Woe to him who seeks to please rather than to appal!" (009-034) — is a call to confront divine truth without evasion. It resonates throughout the novel as an implicit critique of Ahab's refusal to repent, and as a statement of the novel's own aesthetic: *Moby-Dick* does not seek to "pour oil upon the waters" but to appal.

## Notable Quotes

> "It was the famous Father Mapple, so called by the whalemen, among whom he was a very great favourite. He had been a sailor and a harpooneer in his youth, but for many years past had dedicated his life to the ministry." (008-001)

> "Father Mapple was in the hardy winter of a healthy old age; that sort of old age which seems merging into a second flowering youth." (008-001)

> "What could be more full of meaning? — for the pulpit is ever this earth's foremost part; all the rest comes in its rear; the pulpit leads the world. From thence it is the storm of God's quick wrath is first descried, and the bow must bear the earliest brunt." (008-007)

> "Yes, the world's a ship on its passage out, and not a voyage complete; and the pulpit is its prow." (008-007)

> "He doth not say, 'Deliver me out of my peril if thou wilt deliver me.' He saith, 'I have cried unto thee — and yet will I cry unto thee.' Not conditional, but absolute." (009-026)

> "Woe to him who seeks to pour oil upon the waters when God has brewed them into a gale! Woe to him who seeks to please rather than to appal!" (009-034)

> "Woe to him who, in this world, courts not dishonour! Woe to him who would not be true, even though to be false were salvation! Yea, woe to him who, as the great Pilot Paul has it, while preaching to others is himself a castaway!" (009-034)
