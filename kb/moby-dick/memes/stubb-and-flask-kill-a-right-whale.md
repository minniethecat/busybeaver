# Stubb and Flask Kill a Right Whale

## Overview

While the Pequod still carries the sperm whale's head hoisted on her starboard side, patches of yellow brit signal the presence of right whales — an "inferior" species the crew normally disdains to hunt (073-002). Yet Ahab orders a right whale captured, and Stubb and Flask are dispatched. The hunt is driven not by commercial value but by [[fedallah]]'s cryptic prophecy: a ship that carries a sperm whale's head on one side and a right whale's head on the other "can never afterwards capsize" (073-008). The kill proceeds amid swarming sharks, and the chapter unfolds as a mix of violent action and darkly comic dialogue between Stubb and Flask — the latter probing Stubb's suspicions about Fedallah's demonic nature.

## Chronology

- (073-001–002) — The Pequod drifts into right whale grounds; though the crew disdains these "inferior creatures," and though the ship is not commissioned for them, Ahab orders a right whale captured
- (073-003) — Stubb and Flask's boats pursue; the whale is harpooned and tows the boats in a complete circuit around the Pequod in a near-catastrophic close pass that threatens to dash the boats against the hull
- (073-004) — Sharks that had been feeding on the sperm whale's carcass rush to the fresh blood: "thirstily drinking at every new gash, as the eager Israelites did at the new bursting fountains that poured from the smitten rock"
- (073-005) — The whale's spout grows thick; with a "frightful roll and vomit," it turns on its back, dead
- (073-007–008) — Stubb: "I wonder what the old man wants with this lump of foul lard." Flask reveals Fedallah's prophecy: a ship with a sperm whale's head on starboard and a right whale's on larboard "can never afterwards capsize"
- (073-010–011) — Flask confesses his unease about Fedallah: "I don't half like that chap, Stubb. Did you ever notice how that tusk of his is a sort of carved into a snake's head?" Stubb: "I take that Fedallah to be the devil in disguise"
- (073-012–013) — The two mates trade observations: Fedallah sleeps in his boots, coils himself in rigging — Stubb speculates it's "because of his cursed tail; he coils it down, do ye see, in the eye of the rigging"
- (073-017) — Stubb's theory: Ahab is "hard bent after that White Whale, and the devil there is trying to come round him, and get him to swap away his silver watch, or his soul, or something of that sort, and then he'll surrender Moby Dick"
- (073-019–037) — A long comic exchange: Stubb tells a story about the devil kidnapping "John," debates Fedallah's immortality, and boasts he'd wrench off the Parsee's tail if provoked — all while the two boats tow the right whale toward the ship
- (073-039) — Flask's prediction proves true: "yes, you'll soon see this right whale's head hoisted up opposite that parmacetti's"
- (073-040) — With both heads hoisted — sperm whale on starboard, right whale on larboard — the Pequod regains her even keel; Melville's gloss: "So, when on one side you hoist in Locke's head, you go over that way; but now, on the other side, hoist in Kant's and you come back again"
- (073-042) — Fedallah calmly eyes the right whale's head's wrinkles, glancing from them to the lines in his own hand; Ahab stands so that "the Parsee occupied his shadow"

## Narrative Significance

The killing of the right whale serves as the novel's most explicit dramatization of the Ahab-Fedallah bond as seen by the crew. Stubb and Flask's dialogue during the tow back to the Pequod is the longest sustained conversation between the two mates in the novel, and its subject — whether Fedallah is the devil — gives comic form to the serious theological question that underlies the entire Ahab plot: has the captain sold his soul?

> "I take that Fedallah to be the devil in disguise." (073-011)

Stubb's conviction, delivered between bites of whale meat and boasts about wrenching off tails, is both absurd and perceptive. The comic register — Stubb threatening to "give him such a pair of black eyes" (073-031) — contrasts with the genuine unease both mates feel. Flask's observation about Fedallah's tusk carved into a snake's head (073-010) suggests something genuinely uncanny about the Parsee, something the comic banter can't fully dispel.

Melville's philosophical aside at (073-040) — comparing the two whale heads to Locke and Kant — is one of the novel's most compressed epistemological jokes. The sperm whale (Locke: empiricism, the knowable) and the right whale (Kant: the noumenal, the unknowable) counterbalance each other, and the ship "regains her even keel" — but "in very poor plight." The joke is that neither philosopher, alone or together, can keep the ship of human understanding truly steady.

## Aftermath / Consequences

The two heads — sperm whale on starboard, right whale on larboard — remain hoisted through the subsequent chapters, a visual symbol of the dualistic tensions that pervade the novel: empiricism and idealism, knowledge and mystery, the known whale and the White Whale that cannot be known. Fedallah's silent contemplation of the right whale's wrinkles (073-042) reinforces his role as Ahab's shadow — the occult knowledge that parallels but never quite becomes the captain's own.
