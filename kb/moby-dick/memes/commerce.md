# Commerce

## Definition

Commerce in *Moby-Dick* is both foreground and background: the literal economic infrastructure of the whaling industry and the symbolic medium through which Melville explores the tension between material gain and metaphysical quest. The novel opens with commerce — "commerce surrounds it with her surf" (001-002) — and the economics of the whale fishery thread through every subsequent chapter, from the lay system that structures the crew's labor to the global trade networks that whale oil illuminates. Yet Melville consistently frames commerce as something that whaling *transcends* even as it depends upon it: the hunt for the whale is a business, but it is also a philosophy. To read commerce through *Moby-Dick* is to see how Melville uses the most material of subjects to pose the most immaterial of questions.

## Manifestations in the Novel

**Commerce as setting: the insular city (Chapter 1)**. The novel's first paragraph positions commerce as the medium in which New York — "your insular city of the Manhattoes" — floats: "belted round by wharves as Indian isles by coral reefs — commerce surrounds it with her surf" (001-002). The metaphor is ecological: commerce is not an activity the city performs but an element the city inhabits, as natural and enveloping as the sea itself. Ishmael's own journey begins as a commercial transaction — he signs aboard a whaleship for wages — yet the novel immediately signals that this commercial starting point will lead somewhere commerce cannot explain.

**Whaling as engine of empire (Chapter 24)**. In "The Advocate," Ishmael defends whaling's dignity by arguing for its world-historical economic significance. Before the whale fishery "rounded Cape Horn, no commerce but colonial, scarcely any intercourse but colonial, was carried on between Europe and the long line of the opulent Spanish provinces on the Pacific coast" (024-009). It was the whaleman, Ishmael argues, who "first broke through the jealous policy of the Spanish crown" and ultimately "eventuated the liberation of Peru, Chili, and Bolivia from the yoke of Old Spain, and the establishment of the eternal democracy in those parts" (024-009). Commerce here is not mere trade but a liberating force — the whale ship as the vanguard of global capitalism and political revolution.

**Cetology as commodity taxonomy (Chapter 32)**. Melville's elaborate classification of whales is also a commercial ranking. The sperm whale is "by far the most valuable in commerce; he being the only creature from which that valuable substance, spermaceti, is obtained" (032-021). The right whale, by contrast, yields only "whale oil, an inferior article in commerce" (032-022). Melville's Cetology chapter, with its mock-scholarly apparatus of Folios and Octavos, satirizes the reduction of living creatures to commercial grades — even as his own narrative participates in the fascination with the whale's material value.

**Ambergris: the paradox of precious waste (Chapter 92)**. Ambergris, Ishmael explains, "is a very curious substance, and so important as an article of commerce, that in 1791 a certain Nantucket-born Captain Coffin was examined at the bar of the English House of Commons on that subject" (092-001). The substance is found in the bowels of diseased whales — commerce's most valuable product is extracted from an animal's pathology. Stubb's discovery of ambergris inside a decaying whale body becomes one of the novel's most mordant economic allegories: value emerges from corruption, perfume from rot. "Who would think, then," Ishmael muses, "that such fine ladies and gentlemen should regale themselves with an essence found in the inglorious bowels of a sick whale!" (092-006).

**The Dutch commerce treatise (Chapter 101)**. In a characteristic comic aside, Ishmael recounts discovering "an ancient Dutch volume" whose title "Dan Coopman" he initially mistakes for the memoirs of "some Amsterdam cooper in the fishery" (101-008). The volume turns out instead to be a treatise on "the commerce of Holland" (101-008) — a joke that links commerce and whaling through the pun on "cooper"/"coopman," suggesting that the material culture of the fishery is inseparable from the commercial networks it sustains.

**The lay system: commerce as social contract**. The Pequod's crew sails not for wages but for a "lay" — a fractional share of the voyage's profits. This system, detailed in the ship's articles, makes every man a speculator in whale oil. Ishmael's lay is the three hundredth — "the three hundredth lay, I say, would be about the fair thing" (016-008) — and this economic arrangement structures the power relations aboard ship as surely as Ahab's will. Commerce on the Pequod is not external to the drama but constitutive of it: the men hunt the whale because they are paid to, and Ahab can command them because he controls the terms of payment.

## Key Passages

- (001-002) — "Commerce surrounds it with her surf" — the novel opens in the medium of commerce, as natural as the sea.
- (024-009) — "No commerce but colonial, scarcely any intercourse but colonial" until whaling opened the Pacific — commerce as empire's advance guard.
- (032-021) — The sperm whale "by far the most valuable in commerce" — Cetology as commodity taxonomy.
- (032-022) — Right whale oil "an inferior article in commerce" — the hierarchy of whale products.
- (092-001) — Ambergris "so important as an article of commerce" that Captains testified before Parliament — commerce's reach into the whale's intestines.
- (101-008) — The Dutch volume "treated of the commerce of Holland" — the pun that links commerce and the fishery.

## Scholarly Context

Melville's treatment of commerce has been central to historicist and Marxist readings of the novel. C. L. R. James emphasized the collective labor of the crew as a proto-revolutionary force; the lay system, for James, encoded both the exploitation and the solidarity of maritime workers. More recent eco-critical readers have explored how the novel's commercial framing prefigures modern extractive capitalism — the whale as a resource to be rendered into oil, quantified, and sold. Yet Melville resists any simple anti-commercial moralizing: the *Pequod*'s voyage is funded by commerce, and Ishmael's narrative is, in one sense, a balance sheet. The novel's refusal to separate its spiritual quest from its economic substrate is one of its most modern features.
