# Sag Harbor

## Overview

Sag Harbor is a historic whaling port on the eastern end of Long Island, New York. In *Moby-Dick*, it is woven into the backstory of Flask, the Pequod's third mate, and serves as one of the constellation of whaling ports — alongside Nantucket and New Bedford — that supplied the global whaling fleet with ships and men.

## In the Novel

Sag Harbor appears in three distinct contexts. First, it is the scene of Flask's origin story: "A Sag Harbor ship visited his father's bay, and having a grandfather who sailed from that port, Flask first shipped as a foremast hand" (012-003). The town shapes Flask's character — he is described as a blunt, no-nonsense Vineyarder who "arrived at last in old Sag Harbor" before joining the whale fishery (012-004).

Second, Sag Harbor features in Ishmael's account of the whale-line in Chapter 60: a sailor recalls seeing a magnificent whale-line display in Sag Harbor, "the owners of his ship, it seems, had lent him one" (013-003).

Third, Chapter 57 lists Sag Harbor alongside Nantucket and New Bedford as one of the ports where whalemen carve "skrimshander articles" from whale teeth and bone — evidence of a shared material culture among the whaling ports (057-002).

## Geographical Context

Sag Harbor is located on Gardiners Bay at the eastern end of Long Island, about 100 miles east of New York City. In the early 19th century it was a major whaling port, with a fleet that rivaled New Bedford's in the 1840s. The first whaling voyage from Sag Harbor sailed in 1785. By the time of *Moby-Dick*'s publication in 1851, the port's whaling industry was in decline, though its legacy is preserved in the Sag Harbor Whaling Museum and the town's historic architecture.

## Symbolic Significance

Sag Harbor represents the eastern Long Island extension of the New England whaling world — less mythologized than Nantucket, less opulent than New Bedford, but equally part of the maritime network that made the Pequod's voyage possible. Its association with Flask, the most prosaic of the mates, reinforces the town's role as a place of unromantic, workmanlike seamanship.
