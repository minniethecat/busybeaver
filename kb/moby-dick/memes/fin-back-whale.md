# Fin-Back Whale

## Classification

The Fin-Back is classified by Ishmael in Chapter 32 ("Cetology") as one of the six **Folio** whales — the largest category in his bibliographical system. It occupies Book I, Chapter III.

Modern taxonomy identifies it as *Balaenoptera physalus*, the second-largest whale species after the blue whale. It belongs to the rorqual family (Balaenopteridae), characterized by ventral pleats and a streamlined body.

Melville's Folio grouping places it alongside the sperm whale, right whale, hump-backed whale, razor back, and sulphur bottom — all whales of the first magnitude.

## In Cetology (Chapter 32)

Ishmael devotes a single concentrated paragraph to the Fin-Back (032-024), but it is one of his most poetically charged species portraits. He notes the whale's many aliases:

> Under this head I reckon a monster which, by the various names of Fin-Back, Tall-Spout, and Long-John, has been seen almost in every sea and is commonly the whale whose distant jet is so often descried by passengers crossing the Atlantic, in the New York packet-tracks. (032-024)

In size the Fin-Back resembles the right whale, but is "of a less portly girth, and a lighter colour, approaching to olive." Its lips present "a cable-like aspect, formed by the intertwisting, slanting folds of large wrinkles."

The defining feature is the dorsal fin:

> His grand distinguishing feature, the fin, from which he derives his name, is often a conspicuous object. This fin is some three or four feet long, growing vertically from the hinder part of the back, of an angular shape, and with a very sharp pointed end. (032-024)

Melville transforms this anatomical detail into one of the novel's most haunting images. When the fin projects above calm water marked with spherical ripples, it resembles "a dial, with its style and wavy hour-lines graved on it." The comparison deepens: "On that Ahaz-dial the shadow often goes back" — evoking the biblical sign given to King Ahaz, in which the shadow on the sundial moved backward as a portent.

This is not mere ornament. The Fin-Back's character emerges from its isolation:

> The Fin-Back is not gregarious. He seems a whale-hater, as some men are man-haters. Very shy; always going solitary; unexpectedly rising to the surface in the remotest and most sullen waters; his straight and single lofty jet rising like a tall misanthropic spear upon a barren plain; gifted with such wondrous power and velocity in swimming, as to defy all present pursuit from man; this leviathan seems the banished and unconquerable Cain of his race, bearing for his mark that style upon his back. (032-024)

## Appearances in the Novel

Beyond Chapter 32, the Fin-Back appears in two notable passages.

In Chapter 81, the [[pequod|Pequod]] encounters the *Jungfrau* (Virgin), a German whaler desperate for oil. The Fin-Back's spout is sighted, but the whale is dismissed — its speed makes it uncatchable, and its baleen (not spermaceti) marks it as commercially inferior:

> The spout in sight was that of a Fin-Back, belonging to the species of uncapturable whales. (081-047)

Ishmael's aside drives the point home: "Oh! many are the Fin-Backs, and many are the Dericks, my friend" (081-048) — the Fin-Back's elusiveness serving as wry commentary on human futility.

In Chapter 102, while exploring a whale-skeleton-temple in the Arsacides, Ishmael mentions "fine specimens of fin-backs" (102-012) among the whales whose bones are preserved there — a brief but telling inclusion that places the Fin-Back among the known leviathans of the whaling world.

## Whaling Context

The Fin-Back was not a primary target of nineteenth-century American whalers. Its speed — capable of outrunning any whaleboat — made it effectively uncatchable with hand-thrown harpoons. Its baleen (whalebone) was commercially valuable, but it lacked the spermaceti that made the [[sperm-whale|sperm whale]] the industry's true prize.

The Fin-Back's practical invulnerability shaped its symbolic role in Melville's cetology: it is the whale that cannot be caught, the one that defies human dominion. In a novel about the obsessive pursuit of a whale, the Fin-Back represents the opposite — the quarry that always escapes.

Modern whaling, with explosive harpoons and factory ships, would not be so easily outrun. Fin whales were hunted extensively in the twentieth century, their populations reduced to a fraction of pre-industrial levels. Today *Balaenoptera physalus* is classified as vulnerable.
