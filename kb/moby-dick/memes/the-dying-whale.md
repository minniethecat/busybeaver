# The Dying Whale

## Definition

In Chapter 116, "The Dying Whale," Melville describes the "strange spectacle observable in all sperm whales dying — the turning sunwards of the head, and so expiring" (116-003). This biological observation becomes the occasion for one of Ahab's most philosophically dense soliloquies, in which the whale's sunward death-orientation is read alternately as an act of worship, a gesture of faith, and a lesson in cosmic indifference. The chapter is a compact distillation of the novel's central concerns: the relationship between nature and meaning, the possibility of faith in a mute universe, and the interpretive obsession that drives Ahab's quest.

## Manifestations in the Novel

The chapter opens in a mood of deceptive serenity. After a successful day of hunting in which four whales are slain — one by Ahab himself — the Pequod floats in a "lovely sunset sea and sky" where "sun and whale both stilly died together" (116-002). The beauty of the scene, freighted with "sweetness and such plaintiveness" and "inwreathing orisons," establishes a liturgical atmosphere that gives way to Ahab's darker meditation.

Ahab, watching the dying whale from his boat, is struck by the creature's final orientation toward the sun. His response unfolds as a series of rhetorical addresses — to the whale, to the sun, to the "dark Hindoo half of nature," to the sea itself — each complicating the last. He acknowledges the whale's sun-worship as genuine devotion: "He too worships fire; most faithful, broad, baronial vassal of the sun!" (116-004). Yet he immediately undercuts this by observing that "no sooner dead, than death whirls round the corpse, and it heads some other way" (116-004) — the ritual is beautiful but meaningless, a biological reflex rather than a genuine act of faith.

This doubleness is the chapter's philosophical core. The whale's death teaches Ahab something about the nature of faith itself: "here, too, life dies sunwards full of faith; but see! no sooner dead, than death whirls round the corpse" (116-004). The structure of belief — turning toward light, toward meaning — is real and compelling in the moment of dying, but death itself is indifferent to orientation. Ahab's final turn to the sea as "foster-brother" (116-007) represents a shift from solar to oceanic faith, from the vertical aspiration of the dying whale to the horizontal embrace of "eternal tossings."

## Key Passages

> "It was far down the afternoon; and when all the spearings of the crimson fight were done: and floating in the lovely sunset sea and sky, sun and whale both stilly died together; then, such a sweetness and such plaintiveness, such inwreathing orisons curled up in that rosy air, that it almost seemed as if far over from the deep green convent valleys of the Manilla isles, the Spanish land-breeze, wantonly turned sailor, had gone to sea, freighted with these vesper hymns."
> (116-002)

> "For that strange spectacle observable in all sperm whales dying—the turning sunwards of the head, and so expiring—that strange spectacle, beheld of such a placid evening, somehow to Ahab conveyed a wondrousness unknown before."
> (116-003)

> "'He turns and turns him to it,—how slowly, but how steadfastly, his homage-rendering and invoking brow, with his last dying motions. He too worships fire; most faithful, broad, baronial vassal of the sun!—Oh that these too-favouring eyes should see these too-favouring sights. Look! here, far water-locked; beyond all hum of human weal or woe; in these most candid and impartial seas; where to traditions no rocks furnish tablets... here, too, life dies sunwards full of faith; but see! no sooner dead, than death whirls round the corpse, and it heads some other way.'"
> (116-004)

> "'Nor has this thy whale sunwards turned his dying head, and then gone round again, without a lesson to me.'"
> (116-005)

> "'Then hail, for ever hail, O sea, in whose eternal tossings the wild fowl finds his only rest. Born of earth, yet suckled by the sea; though hill and valley mothered me, ye billows are my foster-brothers!'"
> (116-007)
