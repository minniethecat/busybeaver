# Albatross

## Definition

In Moby-Dick, the albatross serves as a key emblem of whiteness and the sublime — a "white phantom" that "sails in all imaginations," evoking both "spiritual wonderment and pale dread" (042-007). Melville invokes the bird in Chapter 42, "The Whiteness of the Whale," to illustrate how whiteness itself possesses an ineffable power to unsettle the human soul. Ishmael recounts a personal encounter with an albatross in the Antarctic seas, describing it as a "regal, feathery thing of unspotted whiteness" whose inexpressible eyes seemed to hold "secrets which took hold of God" (042-008). The bird also recurs as a simile in the novel's storm and chase imagery, its wings a figure for both destruction and motion.

## Manifestations in the Novel

The albatross appears in three distinct registers within the text:

**As a symbol of whiteness (Chapter 42)**. In "The Whiteness of the Whale," Ishmael invokes the albatross alongside the White Steed as creatures whose whiteness intensifies their symbolic power. He asserts that "in the wondrous bodily whiteness of the bird chiefly lurks the secret of the spell" (042-009), contrasting the Antarctic albatross with the common grey albatrosses that fail to evoke the same emotion.

**As a lived encounter (Chapter 42)**. Ishmael interpolates a long footnote recounting his first albatross sighting during a prolonged gale near the Antarctic seas. The bird appeared on deck — "a regal, feathery thing of unspotted whiteness, and with a hooked, Roman bill sublime" — and Ishmael describes bowing before it "as Abraham before the angels" (042-008). A sailor calls it a "goney," a seaman's name Ishmael only later learns refers to the albatross. He insists the experience was not derived from Coleridge's *Rime of the Ancient Mariner*, which he had not yet read, but from Nature itself.

**As a simile for storm and chase (Chapters 123, 133)**. During the typhoon in Chapter 123, the shredded sails drift away "like the feathers of an albatross, which sometimes are cast to the winds when that storm-tossed bird is on the wing" (123-003). In the final chase of Chapter 133, the Pequod hoists all canvas, its stun-sails outstretched "like the double-jointed wings of an albatross" as the ship bears down in Moby Dick's wake (133-040).

## Key Passages

- (042-007) — "Bethink thee of the albatross, whence come those clouds of spiritual wonderment and pale dread, in which that white phantom sails in all imaginations? Not Coleridge first threw that spell; but God's great, unflattering laureate, Nature."
- (042-008) — Ishmael's vivid recollection of the first albatross he ever saw, describing its "regal, feathery thing of unspotted whiteness" and his awed response: "as Abraham before the angels, I bowed myself."
- (042-009) — "I assert, then, that in the wondrous bodily whiteness of the bird chiefly lurks the secret of the spell" — the grey albatross lacks the same power.
- (123-003) — Shredded sails cast adrift "like the feathers of an albatross, which sometimes are cast to the winds when that storm-tossed bird is on the wing."
- (133-040) — The Pequod under full sail, stun-sails outstretched "like the double-jointed wings of an albatross," bearing down in the leeward wake of Moby Dick.

## Scholarly Context

Melville's use of the albatross explicitly engages with Coleridge's *The Rime of the Ancient Mariner* (1798), in which the mariner's killing of an albatross brings a curse upon the ship. Ishmael pointedly denies that Coleridge's poem influenced his own mystical experience with the bird, insisting that "by no possibility could Coleridge's wild Rhyme have had aught to do with those mystical impressions which were mine" (042-008). Critics have noted that Melville simultaneously invokes and disavows the Coleridgean tradition — the albatross in Moby-Dick belongs not to a specific superstition but to the broader argument of Chapter 42: that whiteness itself, independent of any cultural association, produces "spiritual wonderment and pale dread." The bird thus functions as a case study in the chapter's central thesis about the "colorless, all-color of atheism."
