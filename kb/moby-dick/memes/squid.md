# The Great Live Squid

## Definition

The great live squid (*Architeuthis*), referred to in *Moby-Dick* as "the great live squid," is a rarely-seen deep-ocean creature that serves as the primary food of the sperm whale. Melville presents the squid as a creature of almost mythical status among whalemen: "few whale-ships ever beheld, and returned to their ports to tell of it" (059-009). Its rarity and alien appearance make it a natural symbol for the hidden, terrifying, and sublime aspects of the ocean that lie beyond ordinary human experience. The squid represents the unseen foundations of the visible world — the vast, monstrous life that exists in the lightless depths and only occasionally surfaces.

## Manifestations in the Novel

The squid's most significant appearance occurs in Chapter 59 ("Squid"), when the Pequod's crew spots "a vast pulpy mass" floating on the surface of the sea. Ishmael describes it with characteristic precision: "a vast pulpy mass, furlongs in length and breadth, of a glancing cream-color, lay floating on the water, innumerable long arms radiating from its centre, and curling and twisting like a nest of anacondas" (059-009). The crew's reaction reveals the creature's symbolic weight: Starbuck, usually stoic and pragmatic, is visibly shaken, declaring that "it was a sight full of quick wonder and awe" and that the sperm whale's pursuit of such prey into the uttermost depths "makes a whaleman's life so dangerous and toilsome" (059-010).

The squid functions as an omen within the narrative. Its appearance disturbs the crew's sense of the known world, hinting at the existence of forces and creatures that dwarf human comprehension. Just as the whale itself eludes complete understanding, the squid — the whale's prey — occupies an even more remote category of the unknown. It is "the food of the sperm whale" (045-025), the foundation of the food chain that sustains the object of Ahab's obsession, and thus a reminder that even the mighty whale is embedded in a larger, darker ecology.

In Chapter 92 ("Ambergris"), the squid reappears in a different register. The bony plates found in ambergris are identified as "pieces of small squid bones embalmed in that manner" (092-003), connecting the squid to the novel's meditation on value and transformation — from the living terror of the deep to the precious, fragrant substance of ambergris.

## Scholarly Context

The giant squid in *Moby-Dick* has attracted critical attention as an example of Melville's engagement with the natural history discourse of his time. Nineteenth-century science was only beginning to confirm the existence of giant cephalopods, which had long been dismissed as sailor's legend. Melville's inclusion of the squid positions the novel at the intersection of empirical observation and mythological imagination — the squid is both a real creature (now confirmed by modern marine biology) and a symbol of the unknowable.

Critics have also connected the squid to Melville's broader aesthetics of the sublime. Like the whiteness of the whale, the squid's "glancing cream-color" mass represents a form of visual and cognitive overload — something too vast and too strange for the human mind to fully assimilate. The squid's association with the sperm whale's deep-diving feeding habits reinforces one of the novel's central themes: that the visible world rests upon invisible, often monstrous, foundations.
