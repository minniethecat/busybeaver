# Stubb's Supper

## Overview

After killing a sperm whale, the Pequod's crew tows the carcass alongside the ship as darkness falls (064-001–002). While Captain Ahab retreats morosely to his cabin, the second mate [[stubb]] — flushed with triumph — demands a whale steak before sleeping. At midnight, by the light of two sperm-oil lanterns, Stubb dines at the capstan-head as if it were a sideboard (064-008). Below him, thousands of sharks devour the dead whale's flesh, their tails slapping against the hull inches from the sleeping crew's hearts. The scene unfolds as a darkly comic banquet, culminating in Fleece the old black cook's famous sermon to the sharks — a mock-evangelical address that ranks among the novel's most quoted comic passages.

## Chronology

- (064-001–002) — The crew tows the killed whale to the Pequod; darkness falls, and Ahab issues orders for securing the carcass for the night before vanishing to his cabin
- (064-005–006) — Stubb, the second mate, flushed with conquest, demands: "A steak, a steak, ere I sleep! You, Daggoo! overboard you go, and cut me one from his small!"
- (064-008) — At midnight the steak is cut and cooked; Stubb dines at the capstan-head lit by two lanterns of sperm oil, while sharks feast on the whale carcass below
- (064-011) — Stubb summons Fleece the cook: "Cook, cook!—where's that old Fleece?"
- (064-013) — Stubb complains the steak is overdone, then orders Fleece to preach to the sharks to keep quiet
- (064-014–015) — Fleece leans over the bulwarks and addresses the sharks: "Fellow-critters: I'se ordered here to say dat you must stop dat dam noise dare"
- (064-016–025) — Stubb repeatedly interrupts Fleece's sermon to correct his swearing; Fleece delivers his famous doctrine
- (064-024) — **Fleece's central doctrine**: "Your woraciousness, fellow-critters, I don't blame ye so much for; dat is natur, and can't be helped; but to gobern dat wicked natur, dat is de pint. You is sharks, sartin; but if you gobern de shark in you, why den you be angel; for all angel is not'ing more dan de shark well goberned."
- (064-026) — Fleece declares the sermon futile: "No use goin' on; de dam willains will keep a scougin' and slappin' each oder...dare bellies is bottomless"
- (064-029) — Fleece's benediction: "Cussed fellow-critters! Kick up de damndest row as ever you can; fill your dam' bellies 'till dey bust—and den die."
- (064-030–058) — Stubb interrogates Fleece about his age, birthplace, and cooking philosophy; the dialogue shifts from comic to gently philosophical as Stubb delivers his cooking instructions
- (064-059) — Fleece's parting mutter: "Wish, by gor! whale eat him, 'stead of him eat whale. I'm bressed if he ain't more of shark dan Massa Shark hisself"

## Key Participants

| Participant | Role |
|-------------|------|
| **[[stubb]]** | Second mate of the Pequod; the banqueter, whose comic appetite and casual authority drive the scene |
| **Fleece** | The old black cook (about 90 years old); delivers the reluctant shark sermon, whose dialect and weary wisdom provide the chapter's philosophical core |
| **[[daggoo]]** | Harpooneer ordered to cut Stubb's steak from the whale |
| **Sharks** | The "congregation" — thousands swarm the dead whale, their voracity serving as mirror and metaphor for human appetite |

## Narrative Significance

Stubb's Supper is the novel's most sustained comic interlude and a key example of Melville's technique of embedding philosophical meditation within farcical action. The chapter operates on multiple levels:

> "Your woraciousness, fellow-critters, I don't blame ye so much for; dat is natur, and can't be helped; but to gobern dat wicked natur, dat is de pint. You is sharks, sartin; but if you gobern de shark in you, why den you be angel; for all angel is not'ing more dan de shark well goberned." (064-024)

Fleece's sermon transforms the sharks from mere predators into a moral allegory. The "shark in you" becomes Melville's shorthand for the ungoverned appetites — hunger, greed, violence — that define both the human and the natural world. The comic structure (a cook preaching to unreachable sharks) undercuts any simple moralizing: Fleece himself recognizes the futility of his sermon, ending with a curse rather than a blessing.

The chapter also dramatizes the novel's pervasive theme of eating and being eaten. Stubb consumes the whale; sharks consume the whale; the whale itself is being systematically dismantled. Stubb's cannibalistic relish for whale flesh mirrors the sharks' feeding, collapsing the distinction between civilized dining and animal predation. Fleece's final mutter — that Stubb is "more of shark dan Massa Shark hisself" (064-059) — seals the inversion.

## Aftermath / Consequences

The scene leads directly into [[the-shark-massacre]] (Chapter 66), where Queequeg and a forecastle seaman slaughter sharks from the cutting stages. Fleece's sermon, though comic, resonates throughout the novel's treatment of appetite, predation, and the thin line between civilization and savagery. The image of the sharks swarming the dead whale becomes a recurring motif in the novel's meditation on death and consumption at sea.
