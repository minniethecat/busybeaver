# Pity

## Definition

Pity in *Moby-Dick* is the emotion that registers another's suffering — and the novel is both rich in pity and finally suspicious of it. The word appears in voices ranging from the casual ("What a pity they didn't stop up the chink," 002-009) to the anguished ("God pity his poor mother!" 017-010) to the cosmic. Melville tests pity against the scale of whaling: when a whale is dying, "pity there was none" (081-036), and the sharks that follow Ahab's boat are described as "unpitying" (135-039). The novel asks whether pity is adequate to the suffering it witnesses — whether human compassion is large enough for the ocean's indifference. In distinguishing pity from [[mercy|mercy]] (which involves action) and from [[love|love]] (which involves attachment), Melville maps a subtle emotional taxonomy in which pity is the acknowledgment of suffering that may or may not lead to response.

## Manifestations in the Novel

**The casual pity of strangers (Chapter 2)**. Ishmael's first night in New Bedford begins with an almost comic instance of pity: entering the Spouter-Inn, he overhears someone say, "What a pity they didn't stop up the chink" (002-009), referring to a draft in the room. The remark is trivial, but Melville plants it deliberately — pity is the coin of social exchange, a small acknowledgment of shared discomfort that costs nothing. The novel will move from this domestic "pity" to its vast, tragic variant.

**The community's pity for loss (Chapters 5, 17)**. Gathered around the Try Pots inn table, the sailors observe that "the more's the pity" (005-002) — an idiomatic shrug that masks the real dangers of the whaling life. In Chapter 17, when Ishmael imagines Queequeg's mother, he breaks into the narrative with sudden tenderness: "God pity his poor mother! — it will be the same with the other" (017-010). The exclamation is parenthetical but piercing — pity for the mothers who will never see their sons again, the domestic cost of the maritime adventure.

**Pity from the quarter-deck (Chapter 19)**. As Ishmael and Queequeg prepare to sail, a stranger's blessing is followed by the reflection that sailors face dangers "as any other men, God pity 'em! Morning to ye, shipmates" (019-027). The interjection "God pity 'em" is both prayer and farewell — a recognition that pity is what the living owe the soon-to-be-dead.

**Ahab's unpitying will (Chapter 27)**. In the description of Ahab's character during the chase, Ishmael notes that "he handled his unpitying lance coolly and off-handedly" (027-001). Pity is precisely what Ahab lacks — not only for the whale but for his crew, his ship, and himself. The "unpitying lance" is an extension of Ahab's will: the refusal of pity is the refusal of any check on monomania.

**The pity in hatred (Chapter 38)**. Starbuck's soliloquy reveals the complexity of pity: "to hate with touch of pity! For in his eyes I read some lurid woe" (038-002). The first mate hates Ahab for what he is doing, but he pities him too — Ahab is a sufferer as well as an agent of suffering. This "touch of pity" is what ultimately disarms Starbuck, preventing him from killing Ahab when he has the chance. Pity, Melville suggests, can be a form of moral paralysis.

**The pitiable whale, the absent pity (Chapters 75, 81)**. Melville twice confronts the reader with the whale's suffering. In Chapter 75, he laments: "A great pity, now, that this unfortunate whale should be so harpooned" (075-004). In Chapter 81, the dying whale is "horribly pitiable to see. But pity there was none" (081-036). The sentence break is devastating — the whale is objectively pitiable, yet the whalemen feel nothing. The absence of pity is the price of the whaling industry, and Melville's prose makes the reader feel the gap between the creature's suffering and the human capacity to register it.

**The unpitying sharks (Chapter 135)**. In the final chase, "the unpitying sharks accompanied him; and so pertinaciously stuck to the boat" (135-039). The sharks are nature's pitylessness made visible — they follow the whale for the same reason the Pequod does, to consume it. In the novel's final pages, pity has vanished entirely, replaced by the pure mechanism of predation.

## Key Passages

- (002-009) — "What a pity they didn't stop up the chink": pity as casual social exchange, the domestic scale of compassion.
- (005-002) — "The more's the pity": sailors' idiomatic acknowledgment of mortal risk, pity as accepted cost.
- (017-010) — "God pity his poor mother! — it will be the same with the other": sudden tenderness for the mothers left behind, the hidden cost of adventure.
- (019-027) — "God pity 'em! Morning to ye, shipmates": pity as farewell, the prayer of the living for the dying.
- (027-001) — Ahab "handled his unpitying lance coolly and off-handedly": the refusal of pity as the engine of monomania.
- (038-002) — Starbuck: "to hate with touch of pity! For in his eyes I read some lurid woe": pity as moral entanglement, the compassion that disarms.
- (075-004) — "A great pity, now, that this unfortunate whale should be so harpooned": pity for the whale, a rare direct appeal.
- (081-036) — "Horribly pitiable to see. But pity there was none": the gap between objective pitifulness and felt pity, the whaleman's necessary numbness.
- (135-039) — "The unpitying sharks accompanied him": nature's pitylessness, the final register of the novel's compassion.

## Scholarly Context

Melville's treatment of pity engages with a long philosophical tradition — from Aristotle's *Rhetoric*, which defines pity as pain at undeserved misfortune, to Rousseau's celebration of pity as the foundation of natural morality, to Nietzsche's critique of pity as the weapon of the weak. Starbuck's "touch of pity" for Ahab has been read as Melville's diagnosis of liberal paralysis: the capacity to pity one's oppressor, however humane, may prevent resistance. The whale's pitifulness and the whalemen's absence of pity has drawn attention from animal studies scholars, who read Melville as exposing the psychological mechanisms by which industrial violence is made emotionally sustainable. The arc from the small pity of the Spouter-Inn to the cosmic unpitying of the final chase has been read as the novel's moral trajectory — pity is real, Melville concedes, but it may not be enough.
