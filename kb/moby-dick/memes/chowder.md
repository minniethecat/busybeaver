# Chowder

## Definition

In *Moby-Dick*, chowder is the celebrated dish of the Try Pots inn in Nantucket, where Ishmael and Queequeg lodge before embarking on the *Pequod*. More than mere food, Melville's chowder serves as an emblem of the material abundance and rough hospitality of the whaling port — a world in which the ocean's bounty is transformed into communal sustenance. The Try Pots' chowder is so central to the inn's identity that its signboard depicts "a tall straight jet of misty white smoke" rising from a boiling pot, and its very name derives from the "huge try-pots" repurposed from whaling vessels into cooking vessels (015-001).

## Manifestations in the Novel

The chowder episode occupies Chapter 15, arriving after the harrowing shared bed scene of "The Counterpane" and before the formal signing of the *Pequod*'s articles in Chapter 16. Its placement is significant: chowder functions as a moment of domestic warmth and physical nourishment between two episodes of psychological and contractual intensity. Ishmael and Queequeg, having survived their first awkward night together, now share a meal that cements their bond in the language of bodily comfort.

Melville lavishes descriptive attention on the chowder itself, building suspense through the mystery of its preparation: "when that smoking chowder came in, the mystery was delightfully explained" (015-012). The chowder is not merely soup but a substantial, all-encompassing meal: "Chowder for breakfast, and chowder for dinner, and chowder for supper" (015-014). The inn's relentless repetition of the dish — "clam or cod to-morrow for breakfast, men?" (015-015) — suggests both the monotony and the sufficiency of a life organized around a single abundant resource.

Melville is aware of the comic potential of the dish and playfully deflects the proverbial insult of "chowder-headed people" (015-013), transforming a regional slur into a term of rough affection. The following morning, Ishmael and Queequeg enjoy "a prodigiously hearty breakfast of chowders of all sorts" (017-030) before heading to the docks, the meal providing the physical foundation for the adventure to come.

## Key Passages

> "He was called Coffin, and he was famous for his chowders. In short, he plainly hinted that we could not possibly do better than to try pot-luck at the Try Pots."
> (015-001)

> "But when that smoking chowder came in, the mystery was delightfully explained. Oh, sweet friends! hearken to me. It was made of small juicy clams, scarcely bigger than hazel nuts, mixed with pounded ship biscuit, and salted pork cut up into little flakes; the whole enriched with butter, and plentifully seasoned with pepper and salt."
> (015-012)

> "Chowder for breakfast, and chowder for dinner, and chowder for supper, till you began to look for fish-bones coming through your clothes."
> (015-014)

> "The landlord of the Spouter-Inn had recommended us to this place, for the quality of his chowder; and we sat down to a prodigiously hearty breakfast of chowders of all sorts, so that the landlord quite chuckled with satisfaction."
> (017-030)
