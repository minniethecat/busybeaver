# The Chart

## Definition

The Chart in *Moby-Dick* is both a literal navigational tool and a symbol of Ahab's methodical monomania. In Chapter 44, "The Chart," Melville reveals the rational machinery behind Ahab's apparent madness: the captain pores over "a large wrinkled roll of yellowish sea charts" (044-001), correlating old log-books, tides, currents, and the known migratory patterns of sperm whales to calculate where and when Moby Dick will appear. The Chart represents the convergence of empirical knowledge and obsessive purpose — Ahab uses the tools of science and seamanship not to find whales for profit but to hunt one specific whale for vengeance. In doing so, Melville explores how rationality, when yoked to an irrational end, becomes itself a form of madness.

## Manifestations in the Novel

The chapter opens with a hypothetical invitation: "Had you followed Captain Ahab down into his cabin after the squall that took place on the night succeeding that wild ratification of his purpose with his crew..." (044-001). The reader is ushered into Ahab's private sanctuary, where the captain spreads his charts on the "screwed-down table" and "with slow but steady pencil trace additional courses over spaces that before were blank" (044-001). The scene is one of intense, solitary concentration — Ahab as scholar of vengeance, compiling data from "piles of old log-books beside him, wherein were set down the seasons and places in which, on various former voyages of various ships, sperm whales had been captured or seen" (044-001).

The chapter's most striking image is the double inscription of lines — on the charts and on Ahab's face: "the heavy pewter lamp suspended in chains over his head, continually rocked with the motion of the ship, and for ever threw shifting gleams and shadows of lines upon his wrinkled brow, till it almost seemed that while he himself was marking out lines and courses on the wrinkled charts, some invisible pencil was also tracing lines and courses upon the deeply marked chart of his forehead" (044-002). The Chart and the man become mirror images: Ahab is himself mapped, inscribed, and determined by the very obsession he charts.

Ahab's strategy centers on the concept of "the Season-on-the-Line" (044-021) — the equatorial Pacific at a specific time of year where Moby Dick has been periodically sighted. Melville details the emerging science of whale migration, noting that "many hunters believe that, could he be closely observed and studied throughout the world... the migrations of the sperm whale would be found to correspond in invariability to those of the herring-shoals or the flights of swallows" (044-005). Ahab's method exploits this regularity: he knows that "at particular seasons within that breadth and along that path, migrating whales may with great confidence be looked for" (044-019). The Pequod's premature sailing from Nantucket is itself strategic — Ahab intends to spend the intervening year in "a miscellaneous hunt" (044-022), wandering the world's oceans as a contingency plan while the calendar ticks toward the Season-on-the-Line.

The chapter also reveals the psychological cost of Ahab's charting. Melville describes how "when forced from his hammock by exhausting and intolerably vivid dreams of the night... a wild cry would be heard through the ship; and with glaring eyes Ahab would burst from his state room, as though escaping from a bed that was on fire" (044-024). The Chart is not merely a practical instrument — it is the external manifestation of an internal torment. Ahab "sleeps with clenched hands; and wakes with his own bloody nails in his palms" (044-023). The chapter's final, devastating diagnosis: "God help thee, old man, thy thoughts have created a creature in thee; and he whose intense thinking thus makes him a Prometheus; a vulture feeds upon that heart for ever; that vulture the very creature he creates" (044-024).

## Key Passages

> "Had you followed Captain Ahab down into his cabin... you would have seen him go to a locker in the transom, and bringing out a large wrinkled roll of yellowish sea charts, spread them before him on his screwed-down table."
> (044-001)

> "While thus employed, the heavy pewter lamp... for ever threw shifting gleams and shadows of lines upon his wrinkled brow, till it almost seemed that while he himself was marking out lines and courses on the wrinkled charts, some invisible pencil was also tracing lines and courses upon the deeply marked chart of his forehead."
> (044-002)

> "For with the charts of all four oceans before him, Ahab was threading a maze of currents and eddies, with a view to the more certain accomplishment of that monomaniac thought of his soul."
> (044-003)

> "That particular set time and place were conjoined in the one technical phrase — the Season-on-the-Line. For there and then, for several consecutive years, Moby Dick had been periodically descried."
> (044-021)

> "God help thee, old man, thy thoughts have created a creature in thee; and he whose intense thinking thus makes him a Prometheus; a vulture feeds upon that heart for ever; that vulture the very creature he creates."
> (044-024)
