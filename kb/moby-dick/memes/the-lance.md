# The Lance

## The Instrument of Death

The lance in *Moby-Dick* is the whaleman's killing tool, distinct in form and function from the harpoon. Where the harpoon is thrown to fasten to the whale and secure it to the whaleboat by the line, the lance is thrust into the whale's body to reach its vital organs — the "life" — and dispatch it. The distinction is one that every green hand must learn: the harpoon catches, the lance kills. Ishmael, in Chapter 6, records the anxiety this distinction produces in the novice: "The axe! the axe! — cut the line! — the harpoon's out! — snatch the whale-lance!" (006-003). In the panic of the hunt, the lance is the final resort, the weapon of last proximity.

Chapter 84 of *Moby-Dick*, titled "The Lance," is the novel's extended meditation on this instrument. Like many of Melville's cetological chapters, it combines technical exposition with metaphysical reflection, treating the lance not merely as a tool but as a figure for human agency at the threshold of death.

## The Lance vs. the Harpoon

Melville is careful to distinguish the two principal weapons of the whale fishery. The harpoon is barbed, designed to hold fast; it is thrown from a distance and carries the whale-line, which connects the whaleboat to the sounding whale. The lance is unbarbed, designed to penetrate deeply and be withdrawn; it is thrust, not thrown, and the whaleman must bring it to bear at close quarters — close enough to see the whale's eye, to feel the heat of its body, to be within range of its flukes.

This proximity is the lance's defining condition. "The harpooneer must be a man of nerve," Melville writes, but the lancer must be something more: a man who can hold his position alongside a dying leviathan, thrust a steel blade into its body, and withdraw it without being dragged under. The chapter opens with a comparison between the harpoon and the lance, establishing the lance as the more demanding — and more decisive — of the two.

## Pitchpoling: The Lance-Throw

One of the most technically fascinating passages in "The Lance" describes the maneuver called pitchpoling — throwing the lance from a distance when the whale cannot be approached directly. "Small sword, or broad sword, in all its exercises boasts nothing like it," Ishmael declares (084-004). In pitchpoling, the lancer throws the weapon not point-first but with a specific rotation so that it arcs over the water and descends point-down into the whale's back. It is, Ishmael claims, a uniquely whaling skill — "seldom done" — and one that demands extraordinary coordination and judgment of distance.

The description of pitchpoling serves a dual purpose. On the technical level, it documents a specialized whaling practice that Melville's contemporary readers would not have known. On the symbolic level, it establishes the lance as a weapon that, even when thrown, must be aimed with an almost surgical precision. The lancer's skill is not brute force but controlled accuracy — the ability to place a killing point exactly where it will reach the whale's life.

Melville describes the procedure in exact detail: "Then holding the lance full before his waistband's middle, he levels it at the whale" (084-006). The phrase "waistband's middle" locates the act in the lancer's own body — the weapon is centered, aligned with the self, as if the lancer and the lance have become a single instrument. This fusion of body and tool is characteristic of Melville's treatment of whaling implements: the tool extends the body, and in extending it, transforms the body's capacities and risks.

## The Lance and Death

The lance brings death. This is its function, and Melville does not sentimentalize it. The chapter describes the lancer's work in clinical terms: once the whale has been "darted" (harpooned) and has "sounded" (dove) and has returned to the surface exhausted, the whaleboat approaches and the mate — the ranking officer in the boat — takes up the lance. "The mate," Ishmael writes, "now raised the lance, and after a brief shindy or two, darted it into the whale" (084-011).

But the clinical tone is deceptive. The act of lancing is the novel's most direct encounter with the fact of killing. The harpoon may catch the whale, the line may hold it, the whaleboat may pursue it — all these involve risk and skill and courage — but only the lance requires the deliberate, proximate, physical act of driving steel into living flesh. In the novel's moral universe, this makes the lance uniquely charged. Ahab's own "fiery lance" (016-079) is the instrument not only of his trade but of his war against the cosmic order — "fixed his fiery lance in mightier, stranger foes than whales" (016-079). The lancer's weapon becomes, in Ahab's hands, a metaphysical instrument: a tool for striking at the unreachable powers behind the whale, behind the visible world.

## Scholarly Context

The lance has not been the subject of any book-length study, but it appears frequently in material-culture approaches to Melville. Whaling historians note that the lance described by Melville — a long, slender, double-edged blade — was specific to the American sperm-whale fishery and differed from the shorter, heavier lances used in European right-whaling. Melville's insistence on the distinctiveness of the American lance is part of his larger argument for the superiority of American whalemen — and, by extension, American craft and industry — over their European counterparts.

The lance also invites comparison with other penetrating instruments in Melville's symbolic repertoire: the "lancet" of the surgeon, the "lance" of the knight, the "spear" of the biblical warrior. By using the word "lance" rather than "spear," Melville evokes chivalric combat — the knight's lance — while simultaneously insisting on the practical, industrial character of the whaleman's work. The whaleman is both knight and mechanic, his "lance" both romantic weapon and industrial tool.
