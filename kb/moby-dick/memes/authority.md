# Authority

## Definition

Authority in *Moby-Dick* is never simply given — it is claimed, contested, restricted, and ultimately exceeded. The novel maps authority across three domains: **religious authority**, the preacher's power over his congregation exemplified by Father Mapple's "mild voice of unassuming authority" (009-001); **maritime authority**, the captain's legal jurisdiction over his crew, codified by custom and restricted to "the navigation and general management of the vessel" (033-002); and **charismatic authority**, the personal magnetism by which Ahab draws the crew into his private vendetta, transforming a commercial voyage into a metaphysical quest. Melville's crucial insight is that authority, once established in one domain, bleeds into others — Ahab's maritime command becomes theological, Father Mapple's religious authority becomes dramatic, and the whale's physical power becomes a kind of sovereign dominion over the sea.

## Manifestations in the Novel

**Father Mapple's unassuming authority (Chapter 9)**. The novel's first image of authority is the pulpit, and its first authority figure is Father Mapple, who conducts the service "in a mild voice of unassuming authority" (009-001). The paradox is in the adjective "unassuming" — authority that does not claim itself, that rules by presence rather than proclamation. Father Mapple climbs into his pulpit by a rope ladder, then pulls the ladder up after himself — a gesture of withdrawal and self-isolation that prefigures Ahab's retreat into the cabin. But where Ahab's isolation is tyrannical, Father Mapple's is sacerdotal: he withdraws to mediate between his congregation and the God whose word he delivers. The sermon on Jonah is the novel's first sustained exercise of authority through language — the power to compel belief through story.

**The captain's restricted authority (Chapters 16, 33)**. Melville is precise about the legal limits of maritime authority. Ishmael notes that "in those days, the captain's authority was restricted to the navigation and general management of the vessel" (033-002), while the "specksynder" or chief harpooneer held jurisdiction over the whaleboats. This separation of powers is historical — "those days" refers to the earlier Dutch whaling system — and Ishmael presents it as a lost restraint. In the present of the Pequod, Ahab has absorbed both authorities, commanding ship and chase alike. The chapter "The Specksynder" (33) is a meditation on the degeneration of authority from distributed to concentrated, from checked to absolute.

**Authority and the whale (Chapter 32)**. Cetology itself becomes a site of authority claims. Ishmael notes that Scoresby, "the best existing authority" on the whale, "knew nothing and says nothing" of the Sperm Whale's true nature (032-007). The irony is double: the best human authority is incompetent, and the true authority — the whale itself — cannot be consulted. Throughout the novel, the whale's physical bulk conveys a kind of natural authority that exceeds all human claims; the whale does not argue, it simply is, and its being constitutes a verdict.

**Ahab's usurpation of authority (Chapters 36, 50)**. The quarter-deck scene (Chapter 36) is the novel's most concentrated drama of authority transfer. Ahab does not ask the crew to hunt the White Whale — he compels them, using the rituals of maritime command (the doubloon, the grog, the crossed lances) to convert a commercial whaling voyage into a personal revenge quest. Starbuck's objection — "I came here to hunt whales, not my commander's vengeance" (036-009) — is legally correct but politically futile; Ahab's charismatic authority has already bypassed the formal chain of command. In Chapter 50, Ahab's secret boat and crew represent authority's final privatization: "it might have been even authority over him; all this none knew" (050-008). Ahab has created a parallel command structure invisible to the ship's owners and, in some sense, to the reader.

**Authority on good authority (Chapter 45)**. Ishmael's narrative authority is itself thematized throughout the novel. When he reports a fact "on good authority" (045-024), he invokes the chain of testimony that underwrites all whaling knowledge — a chain that is always secondhand, always riddled with hearsay. The affidavit chapter (45) is an extended meditation on the epistemology of authority: what does it mean to believe something "on authority"? Ishmael's answer is pragmatic — we must believe, because the alternative is paralysis — but the question remains open.

**Does the whale's authority diminish? (Chapter 105)**. Ishmael asks whether the sperm whale population has declined, and answers no: "I have heard, on whalemen's authority, that Sperm Whales have been" sighted in increasing numbers (105-003). But the chapter's deeper question is about the authority of Nature itself. If the whale diminishes, human authority over the sea increases; if the whale persists, its authority — the authority of the non-human, the unmastered — remains intact. Melville's answer is characteristically ambiguous: the whale survives, but so does the hunt.

## Key Passages

- (009-001) — "in a mild voice of unassuming authority ordered the scattered people to condense": the novel's first image of legitimate authority, religious and gentle
- (016-005) — "some one having authority, in order to propose myself as a candidate for the voyage": Ishmael seeking legitimate authority to join the Pequod
- (016-006) — "who, by his aspect seemed to have authority": the visual semiotics of authority — Captain Peleg's appearance conveys command before he speaks
- (032-007) — "the best existing authority. But Scoresby knew nothing and says nothing": the failure of human authority before the whale
- (033-002) — "the captain's authority was restricted to the navigation and general management of the vessel": the legal architecture of maritime command
- (036-009) — "I came here to hunt whales, not my commander's vengeance": Starbuck's futile appeal to the proper scope of authority
- (045-024) — "I am told, on good authority, that on the Barbary coast": the epistemology of hearsay and the structure of testimonial knowledge
- (050-008) — "it might have been even authority over him; all this none knew": Ahab's secret boat as authority's final privatization
- (105-003) — "I have heard, on whalemen's authority, that Sperm Whales have been": the persistence of the whale as the persistence of non-human authority

## Scholarly Context

Authority in *Moby-Dick* has been extensively analyzed through political-theological frames. Carl Schmitt's concept of the "state of exception" — the sovereign who decides the exception — maps precisely onto Ahab's transformation of a commercial voyage into an exceptional quest. Hannah Arendt's distinction between authority, power, and violence illuminates the quarter-deck scene: Ahab's authority is initially legitimate (he is the captain) but becomes violence when it exceeds its legal scope. More recent postcolonial readings emphasize that the Pequod's multi-ethnic crew is held together not by shared purpose but by Ahab's coercive authority, making the ship a microcosm of imperial command. Father Mapple's "unassuming authority" has been read as Melville's model of legitimate spiritual leadership — authority that does not grasp, that withdraws to make room for the divine — in implicit contrast to Ahab's grasping, consuming command.
