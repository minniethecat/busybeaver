# Killer Whale

## Classification

The Killer Whale occupies Book II (Octavo), Chapter IV of Ishmael's cetological system — the final slot before the Thrasher closes the Octavo class (032-031). Modern taxonomy classifies the killer whale (*Orcinus orca*) as the largest member of the oceanic dolphin family (Delphinidae), not a whale proper. It is an apex predator found in all oceans.

## In Cetology (Chapter 32)

Ishmael's portrait of the Killer is brief but charged (032-037). He confesses that "of this whale little is precisely known to the Nantucketer, and nothing at all to the professed naturalist." From what he has seen at a distance, he judges the Killer to be "about the bigness of a grampus."

The dominant note is savagery:

> He is very savage — a sort of Feegee fish. He sometimes takes the great Folio whales by the lip, and hangs there like a leech, till the mighty brute is worried to death. (032-037)

The Killer is never hunted, and Ishmael admits ignorance of what sort of oil it yields.

The passage closes with a philosophical flourish on the name itself. "Exception might be taken to the name bestowed upon this whale, on the ground of its indistinctness. For we are all killers, on land and on sea; Bonapartes and Sharks included" (032-037). In this aside, Ishmael widens the lens from cetology to moral anthropology — the Killer's name is a mirror held up to all creation.

## Appearances in the Novel

The Killer Whale is confined almost entirely to Chapter 32. It makes no appearance in the Pequod's voyage, though its reputation as a Folio-whale harasser shadows the novel's central drama: Ahab, in his pursuit of the white whale, might be seen as a killer who "takes the great Folio whale by the lip."

A distant echo appears in Chapter 62 ("The Dart"), where Ishmael describes the harpooneer as "the headsman or whale-killer as temporary steersman" (062-002) — a functional echo of the name, stripped of savagery.

## Whaling Context

The 19th-century American whaleman had little practical acquaintance with killer whales. They were not hunted — their oil was unknown, their ferocity respected. Whalemen observed them attacking baleen whales and learned a grudging respect. The "Feegee fish" epithet reflects the mid-19th-century association of Fiji with cannibalism, a sensationalized trope in American maritime culture.

## Symbolic Meaning

The Killer functions in Melville's taxonomy as the limit case of aggression. Where the Sperm Whale is a noble adversary and the Right Whale a placid philosopher, the Killer is pure predation. Its attack method — hanging on the lip "like a leech" — inverts the epic violence of the whale hunt into something intimate and parasitic. The chapter's closing aphorism — "we are all killers" — is a characteristic Melville move, dissolving zoological distinction into universal moral condition.
