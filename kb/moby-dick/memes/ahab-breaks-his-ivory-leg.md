# Ahab Breaks His Ivory Leg

## Overview

In Chapter 106, [[ahab|Ahab]]'s ivory leg — the prosthetic fashioned from a sperm whale's jawbone that replaced the limb taken by [[the-white-whale|Moby Dick]] — is splintered during his precipitous departure from the [[samuel-enderby|Samuel Enderby]].

The injury is physically minor but metaphysically profound. Ahab interprets it as proof of a dark principle: that "all miserable events do naturally beget their like" (106-003). The chapter is a meditation on the heritability of suffering, the inseparability of grief and grandeur, and the theological problem of a universe in which sorrow propagates more reliably than joy.

In practical terms, Ahab orders the [[carpenter]] to fashion a new leg overnight. The blacksmith is summoned to forge the iron fittings. The episode sets up the extraordinary sequence of Chapters 107–108 — the philosophical portrait of the carpenter and the surreal dialogue between Ahab and the man he calls "manmaker" and "Prometheus."

## Chronology

### The Injury

The splintering occurs as Ahab leaves the [[samuel-enderby|Samuel Enderby]]:

> "He had lighted with such energy upon a thwart of his boat that his ivory leg had received a half-splintering shock. And when after gaining his own deck, and his own pivot-hole there, he so vehemently wheeled round with an urgent command to the steersman … then, the already shaken ivory received such an additional twist and wrench, that though it still remained entire, and to all appearances lusty, yet Ahab did not deem it entirely trustworthy." (106-001)

The leg is not visibly broken — "to all appearances lusty" — but Ahab knows it has been compromised. The language emphasizes the paradox of a living man depending on a dead thing: "that dead bone upon which he partly stood" (106-002).

### The Earlier Casualty

Melville reveals that this is not the first time Ahab's ivory leg has caused him grievous injury. Before the Pequod sailed from Nantucket, Ahab was found "one night lying prone upon the ground, and insensible":

> "His ivory limb having been so violently displaced, that it had stake-wise smitten, and all but pierced his groin; nor was it without extreme difficulty that the agonizing wound was entirely cured." (106-002)

This earlier wound explains Ahab's reclusiveness before the voyage — his "Grand-Lama-like exclusiveness" (106-005) that so puzzled Captain Peleg. The "direful mishap was at the bottom of his temporary recluseness" (106-005).

### The Meditation on Grief

The injury becomes the occasion for one of Ahab's most concentrated philosophical monologues:

> "He too plainly seemed to see, that as the most poisonous reptile of the marsh perpetuates his kind as inevitably as the sweetest songster of the grove; so, equally with every felicity, all miserable events do naturally beget their like." (106-003)

Ahab pushes the thought further. Grief outbreeds joy:

> "The ancestry and posterity of Grief go further than the ancestry and posterity of Joy. … Some guilty mortal miseries shall still fertilely beget to themselves an eternally progressive progeny of griefs beyond the grave." (106-003)

Yet in this darkness Ahab finds a kind of majesty. Heart-woes carry "a mystic significance, and, in some men, an archangelic grandeur" (106-003). To trace the genealogy of mortal miseries "carries us at last among the sourceless primogenitures of the gods" (106-003). The conclusion is almost Gnostic: "the gods themselves are not for ever glad. The ineffaceable, sad birth-mark in the brow of man, is but the stamp of sorrow in the signers" (106-003).

### The Summons

Ahab breaks off his meditation and turns to practical action:

> "But be all this as it may; let the unseen, ambiguous synod in the air, or the vindictive princes and potentates of fire, have to do or not with earthly Ahab, yet, in this present matter of his leg, he took plain practical procedures;—he called the carpenter." (106-006)

The abrupt shift from cosmic speculation to practical command is characteristic of Ahab: he can stand in the abyss of metaphysical despair and, in the next breath, issue orders.

The carpenter is instructed to make a new leg from the finest jaw-ivory accumulated on the voyage. The ship's forge is hoisted from the hold; the blacksmith is commanded to forge "whatever iron contrivances might be needed" (106-006). All must be done that night.

## Narrative Significance

Chapter 106 is a hinge. It connects the preceding gam with the Samuel Enderby — where Ahab encountered Captain Boomer, who also lost a limb to Moby Dick and counselled acceptance — to the two-chapter meditation on craftsmanship, materiality, and the Promethean impulse that follows.

The chapter's philosophical center is Ahab's genealogy of grief. The idea that suffering propagates more reliably than joy is one of the novel's darkest propositions. It provides a metaphysical framework for Ahab's entire quest: he is not merely avenging a personal wound but tracing grief to its "sourceless primogenitures among the gods." His war against the White Whale is a war against the structure of a universe in which misery outbreeds happiness.

The chapter also deepens our understanding of Ahab's body. His ivory leg is never merely a prosthetic. It is a "dead bone," a constant reminder of the original wound, a thing both part of him and foreign to him. When it splinters, the boundary between living flesh and dead matter blurs. The leg that was fashioned from a whale's jaw now threatens to pierce Ahab's own groin — as if the whale, even in death, continues to wound him.

The summons of the carpenter introduces one of the novel's strangest and most under-appreciated characters. The carpenter — "no duplicate" (107-001), a "pure manipulator" whose brain "must have early oozed along into the muscles of his fingers" (107-005) — is Ahab's opposite: unreflective, impersonal, indifferent to meaning. Their dialogue in Chapter 108, where Ahab orders "a complete man after a desirable pattern" — "fifty feet high in his socks … no heart at all, brass forehead" (108-012) — is a surreal comedy that masks profound questions about creation, intention, and the relationship between maker and made.

## Aftermath / Consequences

The carpenter completes the new leg overnight. In Chapter 108 ("Ahab and the Carpenter"), Ahab inspects the work while the carpenter sneezes from bone dust. The scene is at once comic and ominous: Ahab, standing on one leg, philosophizes about Prometheus while the carpenter, "a stript abstract" with "a certain impersonal stolidity" (107-005), files away at whalebone.

The new leg appears again in Chapter 109, where Ahab braces it "against the screwed leg of his table" (109-003) while calculating the Pequod's position. It breaks definitively in the novel's final pages: during the third day of the Chase, "His ivory leg had been snapped off, leaving but one short sharp splinter" (134-025). Ahab is carried to the mast-head, from which he will fall to his death.

The chapter's philosophical argument — that grief begets grief — finds its ultimate confirmation in the novel's conclusion. The Pequod sinks. All die but Ishmael. The genealogy of sorrow extends beyond the grave.
