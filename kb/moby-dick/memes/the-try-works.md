# The Try-Works

## Overview

The Try-Works (Chapter 96) is one of the most philosophically intense episodes in *Moby-Dick*. As the Pequod's crew boils whale blubber through the night — the try-works roaring with flames fed by the whale's own rendered fat — [[ishmael|Ishmael]] stands at the helm. Drowsy from the heat and smoke, he briefly falls asleep and wakes facing the wrong direction, nearly capsizing the ship. The near-disaster becomes the occasion for the novel's most concentrated meditation on the dangers of looking too long into the fire — a warning against the seductive power of obsession, and a metaphor for Ahab's monomania.

The chapter moves from technical description of the try-works, to a hellish vision of the ship as a "red hell," to Ishmael's personal crisis, to a philosophical sermon addressed directly to the reader. It is the novel in miniature: cetological fact, narrative action, and metaphysical reflection fused into a single, searing vision.

## Chronology

### The Try-Works Ignited

The try-works — "planted between the foremast and mainmast, the most roomy part of the deck" (096-002) — are first started on this voyage at about nine o'clock at night, with Stubb overseeing (096-004). The furnace is fed initially with wood shavings, then with the whale's own "crisp, shrivelled blubber, now called scraps or fritters" (096-005). Melville notes the self-consuming nature of the process:

> "Like a plethoric burning martyr, or a self-consuming misanthrope, once ignited, the whale supplies his own fuel and burns by his own body." (096-005)

### The Red Hell

By midnight, the works are fully operational. The scene becomes infernal:

> "The burning ship drove on, as if remorselessly commissioned to some vengeful deed. ... the rushing Pequod, freighted with savages, and laden with fire, and burning a corpse, and plunging into that blackness of darkness, seemed the material counterpart of her monomaniac commander's soul." (096-006–007)

The pagan harpooneers — "Tartarean shapes" — work the furnaces with "huge pronged poles," their "tawny features, now all begrimed with smoke and sweat" illuminated by the capricious flames (096-007).

### Ishmael's Crisis

Ishmael, at the helm through the long night, succumbs to drowsiness. Starting from a "brief standing sleep," he realizes something is "fatally wrong" (096-009). He has turned around in his sleep and is now facing the stern, his back to the compass:

> "A stark, bewildered feeling, as of death, came over me. Convulsively my hands grasped the tiller, but with the crazy conceit that the tiller was, somehow, in some enchanted way, inverted." (096-009)

He faces back just in time to "prevent the vessel from flying up into the wind, and very probably capsizing her" (096-009).

### The Sermon

The chapter's closing paragraphs are a direct address to the reader, delivered in Melville's most prophetic voice:

> "Look not too long in the face of the fire, O man! Never dream with thy hand on the helm! Turn not thy back to the compass; accept the first hint of the hitching tiller; believe not the artificial fire, when its redness makes all things look ghastly." (096-010)

The meditation deepens into the novel's darkest philosophical statement:

> "that mortal man who hath more of joy than sorrow in him, that mortal man cannot be true — not true, or undeveloped. ... The truest of all men was the Man of Sorrows, and the truest of all books is Solomon's, and Ecclesiastes is the fine hammered steel of woe. 'All is vanity.' ALL." (096-011)

Yet the chapter closes on a qualified note of hope: "There is a Catskill eagle in some souls that can alike dive down into the blackest gorges, and soar out of them again and become invisible in the sunny spaces" (096-012).

## Narrative Significance

The Try-Works chapter is a warning encoded in narrative form. Ishmael's near-capsize — turning his back on the compass while hypnotized by the fire — is a direct metaphor for Ahab's condition. Ahab has looked "too long in the face of the fire" of his obsession; he has turned his back on every moral compass (Starbuck's protests, the Rachel's plea, the Bachelor's joy). The try-works, consuming the whale's own body as fuel, images Ahab's self-consuming vengeance.

The chapter also contains Melville's most explicit statement of his dark metaphysics: the "Man of Sorrows" passage aligns truth with suffering, joy with delusion. This is the philosophical ground bass of the entire novel. Yet the final image of the Catskill eagle — capable of diving into the darkest gorges and soaring out again — offers a counter-movement, a possibility of transcendence that Ahab never achieves but Ishmael, the survivor, embodies.

## Aftermath / Consequences

Ishmael's experience at the helm becomes a cautionary tale that haunts the remaining chapters. His warning — "Look not too long in the face of the fire" — is precisely what Ahab cannot do. The try-works scene, with its fusion of industrial process and spiritual crisis, establishes the mode of the novel's final third: every action is also a symbol, every event a metaphysical test.
