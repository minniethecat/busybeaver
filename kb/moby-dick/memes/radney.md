# Radney

## Overview

Radney is the chief mate of the [[Town-Ho]], a Nantucket whaler whose ill-fated voyage becomes the subject of [[Ishmael]]'s extended narrative-within-the-narrative in [[Ch. 54: The Town-Ho's Story]]. A Vineyarder — a man from Martha's Vineyard — Radney is introduced as "the brutal overbearing" mate whose personal malice toward the Lakeman [[Steelkilt]] triggers a chain of events culminating in mutiny and, ultimately, Radney's own spectacular death in the jaws of [[Moby Dick]] (054-007). Melville presents him as the archetype of the petty tyrant: a man of inferior character in a position of authority whose "unconquerable dislike and bitterness" toward a subordinate of superior natural dignity drives the tragedy of the Town-Ho.

## Role in the Narrative

Radney's role in the novel is that of the antagonist within the Town-Ho subplot, a cautionary tale embedded in the larger tragedy of the [[Pequod]]. His persecution of Steelkilt provides a miniature study of power abused — the mate who cannot tolerate a common seaman who is demonstrably his "superior in general pride of manhood" (054-012).

The conflict begins when the Town-Ho develops a leak, and the crew are worked to exhaustion at the pumps. In this moment of collective strain, Radney singles out Steelkilt for humiliation: ordering him to perform the degrading task of sweeping the deck — work normally assigned to ship's boys. When Steelkilt refuses, Radney escalates, advancing on the seated Lakeman with a cooper's club hammer. After repeated provocations — shaking the hammer within inches of Steelkilt's face and "repeating a string of insufferable maledictions" — Radney touches the hammer to Steelkilt's cheek. In that instant Steelkilt's forbearance breaks: "the next instant the lower jaw of the mate was stove in his head; he fell on the hatch spouting blood like a whale" (054-023).

Radney survives the blow but does not learn from it. Even after mutiny convulses the ship, he insists on resuming his watch, against the captain's counsel. His fate is sealed not by Steelkilt — who plans a cold, calculated revenge — but by the white whale himself. During a later whale hunt, when the Town-Ho lowers for Moby Dick, Radney is tossed from his boat onto the whale's back, then into the sea, where "the whale rushed round in a sudden maelstrom; seized the swimmer between his jaws; and rearing high up with him, plunged headlong again, and went down" (054-049). The manner of his death — seized by the very whale he was hunting — carries the weight of cosmic retribution.

## Character Traits

**Brutal authority** defines Radney. He is not merely a disciplinarian but a man who uses his position to degrade those he envies. When Steelkilt refuses to sweep, Radney escalates immediately to physical intimidation, brandishing a hammer "within a few inches of his face" and "repeating a string of insufferable maledictions" (054-023).

His **cowardice masked as courage** is suggested by Melville's careful narration. Though Radney insists on returning to his watch after his injury — seemingly brave — the text hints he "dozed" on duty, sitting on the bulwarks in an "unseamanlike" posture (054-048). Steelkilt, watching, calculates his moment. The bravado is a performance.

**Physical repulsiveness** serves as Melville's shorthand for moral ugliness. The contrast between Radney ("ugly as a mule") and Steelkilt ("a tall and noble animal with a head like a Roman") is not incidental — it encodes the moral hierarchy of the story in physical terms.

## Relationships

- **[[Steelkilt]]**: The Lakeman who becomes Radney's victim and nemesis. Radney's persecution of Steelkilt is motivated by the deeper resentment of a man who "finds one of them to be very significantly his superior in general pride of manhood" and therefore "against that man he conceives an unconquerable dislike and bitterness" (054-012). Their antagonism forms the central dramatic arc of the Town-Ho story.
- **The Captain of the Town-Ho**: Radney's superior, who counsels against Radney resuming his watch after the mutiny — counsel Radney ignores.
- **[[Moby Dick]]**: The white whale becomes the unintentional instrument of Radney's destruction. When the whale seizes Radney, the moment echoes Steelkilt's earlier assertion that Radney had been "branded for the slaughter by the gods" (054-023).
