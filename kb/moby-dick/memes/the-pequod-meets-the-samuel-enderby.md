# The Pequod Meets the Samuel Enderby

## Overview

The gam with the Samuel Enderby is the most important ship encounter in *Moby-Dick*. Captain Boomer of the English whaler has, like Ahab, lost a limb to Moby Dick — his arm, torn off at the shoulder. But whereas Ahab has devoted his existence to revenge, Boomer has accepted his loss and wants nothing more to do with the White Whale. The chapter is structured around Boomer's narrative of his encounter and the dark comedy of his ship's surgeon, Dr. Bunger. Ahab's reaction — frantic, dismissive, almost terrified of Boomer's equanimity — reveals his monomania in its sharpest relief.

## Chronology

### The Meeting

Ahab hails the English ship with his usual question: "Hast seen the White Whale?" (100-001). Captain Boomer, "a darkly-tanned, burly, good-natured, fine-looking man, of sixty or thereabouts," is reclining in his boat's bow, "one empty arm of this jacket streamed behind him like the broidered arm of a hussar's surcoat" (100-002). In reply to Ahab's question, Boomer wordlessly holds up "a white arm of sperm whale bone, terminating in a wooden head like a mallet" (100-004).

### Ahab's Boarding

Ahab, in his excitement, orders his boat lowered immediately — but then faces "a curious difficulty." Since losing his leg, he has never boarded any vessel but the Pequod, which is fitted with a mechanical contrivance for lowering him. The stranger ship has no such device. Ahab finds himself "abjectly reduced to a clumsy landsman again; hopelessly eyeing the uncertain changeful height he could hardly hope to attain" (100-006).

Boomer, observing the difficulty, orders the cutting-tackle lowered — a massive blubber-hook. Ahab "slid his solitary thigh into the curve of the hook (it was like sitting in the fluke of an anchor, or the crotch of an apple tree)" and is hoisted aboard (100-008). The two captains greet each other with their ivory limbs extended: "let us shake bones together! — an arm and a leg! — an arm that never can shrink, d'ye see; and a leg that never can run" (100-008).

### Captain Boomer's Story

Boomer tells his tale in his own words, with interjections from Dr. Bunger and interruptions from the increasingly agitated Ahab. Boomer was ignorant of the White Whale when he first encountered him on the Line. His boat had fastened to a regular whale when "up breaches from the bottom of the sea a bouncing great whale, with a milky-white head and hump, all crows' feet and wrinkles" (100-013). Ahab, recognizing the description, exults: "It was he, it was he!" (100-014).

The White Whale got tangled in Boomer's whale-line, and when the boat pulled, they "bounce we came plump on to his hump!" (100-019). Boomer resolved to capture this magnificent whale. But Moby Dick's tail "looming straight up out of it, perpendicular in the air, like a marble steeple" came crashing down, cutting the boat in two (100-019). As Boomer clung to his harpoon-pole stuck in the whale, a combing sea dashed him off, and the barb of a second iron "bore me down to Hell's flames" — then miraculously "ript its way along the flesh — clear along the whole length of my arm — came out nigh my wrist, and up I floated" (100-019).

### Dr. Bunger's Commentary

Dr. Bunger, the ship's surgeon, provides a darkly comic medical perspective. The wound was "as ugly gaping wound as surgeon ever saw; more than two feet and several inches long. I measured it with the lead line" (100-027). He notes, with mock gravity, that "the digestive organs of the whale are so inscrutably constructed by Divine Providence, that it is quite impossible for him to completely digest even a man's arm" (100-035). The whale, he suggests, never means to swallow a limb — "he only thinks to terrify by feints" (100-035).

The banter between Boomer and Bunger — Boomer calling Bunger "you dog" and "you solemn rogue" — establishes a tone of easy, irreverent camaraderie utterly foreign to the Pequod.

### The Contrast

When Ahab asks whether Boomer has crossed Moby Dick's wake again, Boomer replies that he has — twice — but "didn't want to try to: ain't one limb enough? What should I do without this other arm? And I'm thinking Moby Dick doesn't bite so much as he swallows" (100-034). The final judgment: "he's best let alone; don't you think so, Captain?" — glancing at Ahab's ivory leg (100-036).

Ahab's response is grim: "He will still be hunted, for all that. What is best let alone, that accursed thing is not always what least allures. He's all a magnet!" (100-037).

Dr. Bunger, observing Ahab's agitation, attempts to take his pulse, noting "this man's blood — bring the thermometer! — it's at the boiling point!" (100-038). Ahab roars "Avast!" and dashes him against the bulwarks.

### The Departure

Ahab demands the whale's heading and, upon hearing it was east, immediately orders his boat. Boomer asks Fedallah: "Is your Captain crazy?" (100-040). Fedallah puts a finger to his lip and slides over the bulwarks without answering. Ahab departs "with back to the stranger ship, and face set like a flint to his own" (100-042).

## Narrative Significance

The Samuel Enderby chapter is the novel's most explicit argument against Ahab's monomania. Boomer has suffered the same physical loss as Ahab, but his psychological response is the diametric opposite: acceptance, humor, and a pragmatic "he's best let alone." The chapter asks whether Boomer is wiser than Ahab, or simply less great — and Melville refuses to answer definitively.

The episode also humanizes the wider whaling world. Boomer and Bunger are fully realized characters with individual voices, not mere foils. Their easy humor and mutual affection make the Pequod's atmosphere of dread and compulsion seem all the more abnormal.

## Aftermath / Consequences

Ahab leaves the Samuel Enderby more determined than ever. Boomer's refusal to pursue Moby Dick does not deter him — it isolates him further. The chapter's implicit question — is Ahab's pursuit heroic or insane? — will be answered only in the novel's final chapters.
