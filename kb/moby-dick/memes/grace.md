# Grace

## Definition

Grace in *Moby-Dick* operates in two registers — the physical and the theological — and Melville's genius is to refuse to separate them. The whale's motion is described as "exceeding grace" (086-006), the whaling line settles with "graceful repose" (060-010), and the Pequod's masts are "graceful" (118-005) even as she sails toward destruction. But grace is also a theological term, the unearned divine favor that Father Mapple's God may or may not extend, and that Queequeg — a "savage" — demonstrates more naturally than the Christians around him. Ishmael's claim that "those people have their grace as well as we" (013-004) is the novel's quiet theological revolution: grace is not the property of any creed but a quality of being that transcends cultural and religious boundaries.

## Manifestations in the Novel

**Queequeg's democratic grace (Chapter 13)**. After Queequeg rescues the green countryman who had mocked him, Ishmael reflects: "those people have their grace as well as we — though Queequeg does not perhaps share our more delicate sensibilities" (013-004). The scene is a parable: Queequeg's grace is demonstrated in action — diving into freezing water to save a stranger — while the white sailors gape from the deck. Melville's syntax ("as well as we") is deceptively simple, quietly erasing the hierarchy of Christian grace over pagan. Queequeg's grace is not doctrinal but behavioral, and the novel suggests this is the more authentic form.

**The tragic graces of the whale (Chapter 26)**. In "Knights and Squires," Melville describes how the whaleman's craft weaves "round them tragic graces; if even the most mournful, perchance the most abased" (026-006). The phrase "tragic graces" is a compressed theology of the whaling life: grace is not incompatible with suffering but is actually woven *from* it. The dignity Melville grants to common sailors — "thou great democratic God" (026-007) — is a grace conferred by labor and danger, not by birth or election.

**The graceful repose of the line (Chapter 60)**. The whaling line, that "magical, sometimes horrible whale-line" (060-001), is described at a moment of calm: "the graceful repose of the line, as it silently serpentines about the oarsmen" (060-010). The line is deadly — it can snag a man and drag him under — but in its stillness it is beautiful. Melville's attention to the aesthetics of the whaleman's tools is itself an act of grace: conferring beauty on objects of danger.

**The whale's exceeding grace (Chapter 86)**. In "The Tail," Melville lavishes his most sustained praise on the whale's physical grace. The tail's motion is "invariably marked by exceeding grace. Therein no fairy's arm can transcend it" (086-006). The whale's body becomes the novel's supreme example of grace in the physical sense — a beauty so perfect it seems to belong to another order of being. Yet Melville immediately notes that the whale's grace is "crippled" by human attack (086-005): the whaling industry's violence is registered as an aesthetic loss, a degradation of natural elegance.

**The Pequod's graceful doom (Chapter 118)**. As Ahab sails toward the final confrontation, Melville describes the ship: "her three firm-seated graceful masts erectly poised upon her long, ribbed hull" (118-005). The Pequod's grace is architectural — the harmony of mast and hull — but the reader knows the ship is sailing into destruction. Here grace attains its full tragic register: beauty and doom are not opposites but complements, each intensifying the other.

**Europa and the bull — grace as seduction (Chapter 133)**. In the chase, Ahab's boat is described with a mythological simile: "like ravished Europa clinging to his graceful horns" (133-017). The image is unnerving: Europa was abducted by Zeus in bull form, and the "graceful horns" she clings to belong to her captor. Grace here is seductive and treacherous — Ahab is ravished by the whale's beauty even as it destroys him.

## Key Passages

- (013-004) — "Those people have their grace as well as we": Queequeg's grace demonstrated in rescue, the novel's democratic theology.
- (026-006) — "Weave round them tragic graces; if even the most mournful, perchance the most abased": grace conferred by labor and suffering.
- (060-010) — "The graceful repose of the line, as it silently serpentines about the oarsmen": the aesthetics of whaleman's tools, beauty in danger.
- (086-005) — The harpoon "cripple[s] the graceful flexion of its motions": violence as aesthetic loss, grace degraded.
- (086-006) — The whale's tail "invariably marked by exceeding grace. Therein no fairy's arm can transcend it": the whale as paragon of physical grace.
- (118-005) — The Pequod's "firm-seated graceful masts erectly poised": architectural grace on the edge of destruction.
- (133-017) — "Ravished Europa clinging to his graceful horns": grace as seduction and doom, the lethal beauty of the whale.

## Scholarly Context

The dual meaning of grace in *Moby-Dick* — physical and theological — has been explored by scholars interested in Melville's fusion of the aesthetic and the religious. The democratic grace Melville attributes to Queequeg has been read as a response to Calvinist election: grace is not predestined but demonstrated, available to anyone who acts with courage and compassion. Queequeg's grace in Chapter 13 is often contrasted with Bildad's performative piety in Chapter 16 — Melville's suggestion that grace is visible in deeds, not professions. Eco-critical scholars have focused on the whale's physical grace, reading Melville's descriptions of cetacean motion as an early instance of what would later be called "animal aesthetics" — the recognition that non-human bodies possess their own formal perfection independent of human judgment.
