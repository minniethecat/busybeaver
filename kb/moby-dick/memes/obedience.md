# Obedience

## Definition

Obedience in Moby-Dick operates as a double-edged moral force. On one hand, it is the foundation of maritime hierarchy: the captain commands and the crew obeys, a structure Melville presents as necessary for survival at sea. On the other, the novel interrogates what happens when that obedience is demanded in service of a monomaniacal purpose — when "flat obedience to thy own flat commands" (123-008) becomes the only law. The book of Jonah, expounded in Father Mapple's sermon, provides the theological archetype: Jonah's "wilful disobedience of the command of God" (009-026) sets in motion a chain of punishment, repentance, and deliverance that mirrors the Pequod's own trajectory under Ahab.

## Manifestations in the Novel

**Jonah and divine obedience (Chapter 9)**. Father Mapple's sermon on Jonah establishes the moral framework for the novel's treatment of obedience. Jonah's "sin of disobedience" — his attempt to flee "world-wide from God" — is presented as the archetypal human rebellion. Mapple's lesson is that "if we obey God, we must disobey ourselves; and it is in this disobeying ourselves, wherein the hardness of obeying God consists" (009-027). This paradox — that true obedience requires self-denial — echoes throughout the novel.

**Ahab's absolute command (Chapter 33, 46)**. Ahab demands "implicit, instantaneous obedience" from his crew (033-005), a requirement that at first appears consistent with normal maritime discipline. But as the voyage progresses, Ahab becomes conscious that his crew "could refuse all further obedience to him, and even violently wrest from him the command" (046-004), revealing that his authority rests not on law but on the force of his personality.

**Starbuck's conflicted submission (Chapter 48, 123)**. Starbuck embodies the tension between duty and conscience. In the typhoon scene, he contemplates killing Ahab to save the crew, recognizing that "flat obedience to thy own flat commands, this is all thou breathest" (123-008). Yet he cannot bring himself to act — his obedience is to the office of captain, even when the captain is mad.

**Steelkilt's rebellion (Chapter 54)**. The Town-Ho's story provides a counter-example: Steelkilt leads a mutiny against the mate Radney. The crew, "in obedience to Steelkilt" (054-047), retreats into the hold rather than submit to unjust authority. This episode demonstrates that obedience in the novel is not absolute — it can be withheld when authority becomes tyranny.

**Gabriel's cult of command (Chapter 71)**. The archangel Gabriel commands a different kind of obedience on the Jeroboam: the crew "fawned before him; in obedience to his instructions, sometimes rendering him personal homage, as to a god" (071-008). Gabriel represents obedience perverted into fanaticism, a dark mirror of Ahab's own hold over the Pequod's crew.

## Key Passages

- (009-026) — "the sin of this son of Amittai was in his wilful disobedience of the command of God" — Father Mapple on Jonah's original sin.
- (009-027) — "if we obey God, we must disobey ourselves; and it is in this disobeying ourselves, wherein the hardness of obeying God consists" — the paradox of true obedience.
- (033-005) — Ahab's authority rests on "implicit, instantaneous obedience; though he required no man to remove the shoes from his feet."
- (046-004) — The crew "could refuse all further obedience to him, and even violently wrest from him the command" — Ahab's awareness of his precarious hold.
- (054-047) — The Town-Ho's crew acts "in obedience to Steelkilt" during the mutiny, obeying a fellow sailor rather than the lawful mate.
- (123-008) — Starbuck's bitter recognition: "Flat obedience to thy own flat commands, this is all thou breathest."

## Scholarly Context

Critics have long noted that Melville structures the Pequod as a microcosm of political society, with obedience as the binding force. The novel draws on Hobbesian themes: without a sovereign power to command obedience, the ship would descend into chaos. Yet Melville complicates this by showing how obedience can become complicity in evil. Ahab's demand for total submission echoes the critique of absolutism found in Melville's reading of Shakespeare's Lear and Macbeth. The Jonah frame further situates obedience within a Calvinist framework — disobedience to God brings wrath, yet God's command itself may be inscrutable.
