# Leviathan

## Definition

In *Moby-Dick*, the term "Leviathan" operates on multiple registers — biblical, political-philosophical, and natural-historical — all of which Melville deliberately layers to invest the sperm whale with a weight of cultural significance that no merely zoological description could supply. The word appears most densely in the "Extracts" section that prefaces the novel, where Melville assembles a cento of whale-lore from Genesis to the Elizabethans, establishing the Leviathan as a creature that "maketh a path to shine after him" and that God "hast made to play therein" (P02-006). By framing the whale within this scriptural and literary genealogy, Melville signals that his subject is not simply a marine mammal but an archetype — a creature that has haunted the human imagination since antiquity.

The political dimension is equally important. Hobbes's *Leviathan* (1651), explicitly quoted in the Extracts — "By art is created that great Leviathan, called a Commonwealth or State" (P02-033) — equates the whale with sovereign power, an analogy Melville exploits throughout the novel in his depiction of Ahab's absolute command aboard the *Pequod*. The whale is both the object of the hunt and the figure through which the novel thinks about authority, compulsion, and the limits of human will.

## Manifestations in the Novel

Melville's most sustained engagement with the Leviathan tradition occurs in Chapter 24 ("The Advocate"), where Ishmael mounts a rhetorical defense of whaling's dignity by invoking its ancient and honorable lineage. "Who wrote the first account of our Leviathan?" Ishmael asks. "Who but mighty Job!" (024-013). This appeal to biblical precedent is strategic: by connecting the Nantucket whaleman to the author of the Book of Job, Ishmael elevates a commercial enterprise into a sacred vocation. The whaleman is not a butcher but a chronicler of the divine creation, continuing a tradition of witness that begins with scripture itself.

The identification of Ahab with the Leviathan-hunter — and, increasingly, with the Leviathan's destructive power — runs throughout the middle chapters. In "The Pipe" (Chapter 30), Ishmael calls Ahab "a great lord of Leviathans" (030-002), a phrase that collapses the distinction between the hunter and the hunted. Ahab's monomania transforms him into something as vast and implacable as the creature he pursues. This mirroring reaches its apotheosis in the three-day chase, where the *Pequod* and Moby Dick become locked in a fatal embrace that recalls the eschatological combat of Isaiah.

Chapter 32 ("Cetology") offers a different register: the taxonomic. Here Ishmael attempts to classify the whale according to a mock-scientific system of Folios, Octavos, and Duodecimos, explicitly positioning his work alongside "the same seas with the Leviathan" (032-011). The contrast between the mythic register of the Extracts and the pseudo-systematic register of Cetology is deliberate — Melville shows that the whale exceeds all systems of knowledge, whether scriptural or scientific, remaining always partly beyond human comprehension.

## Key Passages

> "Leviathan maketh a path to shine after him; one would think the deep to be hoary."
> (P02-004)

> "By art is created that great Leviathan, called a Commonwealth or State — which is but an artificial man; though of greater stature and strength than the natural, for whose protection and defence it was intended."
> (P02-033)

> "Who wrote the first account of our Leviathan? Who but mighty Job! And who composed the first narrative of a whaling voyage? Who but the mighty Job?"
> (024-013)

> "And now the time of tide has come; the ship casts off her cables; and from her white belly, she darts forth the ponderous Leviathan."
> (032-011)
