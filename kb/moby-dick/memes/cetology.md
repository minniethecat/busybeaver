# Cetology

## Definition

Cetology is the branch of zoology that studies whales (from Latin *cetus*, whale). In *Moby-Dick*, Melville devotes the entirety of Chapter 32 ("Cetology") to a mock-scholarly classification of whales, dividing them by size into Folio, Octavo, and Duodecimo whales — a system borrowed from book formats. The chapter is simultaneously a parody of nineteenth-century scientific treatises and a serious engagement with the limits of human knowledge in the face of nature's vastness.

## Manifestations in the Novel

Chapter 32 opens with Ishmael declaring his ambition: "I promise nothing complete; because any human thing supposed to be complete, must for that very reason infallibly be faulty." He proposes to "take hold of the whales bodily, in their entire liberal volume, and boldly sort them." His system — based on book sizes — classifies the Sperm Whale, Right Whale, Fin-Back, Humpback, Razorback, and Sulphur-Bottom as Folio whales, the Grampus and Killer Whale as Octavo, and the Porpoise family as Duodecimo.

The classification is deliberately absurd — book sizes have nothing to do with biological taxonomy — yet it gestures toward a genuine taxonomic problem: whales were poorly understood by nineteenth-century science, and Ishmael's frustrated attempts at systematization mirror the novel's larger theme of the inadequacy of human categories before nature's irreducible complexity.

The Extracts section at the novel's beginning also engages with cetology, compiling quotations about whales from sources ranging from the Bible to Shakespeare to scientific treatises, "for veritable gospel cetology. Far from it."

## Scholarly Context

The Cetology chapter has been read as Melville's satire of the encyclopedic ambitions of nineteenth-century science (particularly the natural history of Cuvier and Agassiz), as a structural parody of the novel's own encyclopedic form, and as a meditation on the gap between representation and reality. The whale, like the novel itself, resists classification — "the classification of the constituents of a chaos, nothing less is here essayed" (032-005). The chapter's playful pedantry is of a piece with the novel's larger epistemological skepticism.
