# Cape of Good Hope

## Overview

The Cape of Good Hope is the rocky headland on the Atlantic coast of South Africa, long a crucial waypoint on maritime routes between Europe and the East. In *Moby-Dick*, Melville invokes the Cape both as a practical navigation reference and as a wry biblical metaphor — comparing Jonah's flight to Nineveh via the Cape to Ahab's monomaniacal pursuit of the White Whale.

## In the Novel

The Cape appears primarily in Chapter 53 ("The Gam") and Chapter 83 ("Jonah Historically Regarded"). In the former, Melville describes "the Cape of Good Hope, and all the watery region round about" as the principal meeting-ground for whaling ships (054-002). In the latter, Father Mapple's sermon on Jonah is re-examined with dry humor: "Jonah's going to Nineveh via the Cape of Good Hope was advanced as a signal magnification of the prophet's journey" (083-005).

The Cape also features in Captain Ahab's navigation: rounding the Cape of Good Hope is the shortest route from the Atlantic to the Indian Ocean whaling grounds, though the Pequod takes the Cape Horn route to the Pacific instead. In Chapter 119, Ahab mockingly quotes the received wisdom: "round the Cape of Good Hope is the shortest way to Nantucket" (119-023) — a sardonic reference to his determination to chase Moby Dick around the world.

## Geographical Context

The Cape of Good Hope was first rounded by Bartolomeu Dias in 1488. Before the Suez Canal (1869), it was the primary sea route between Europe and Asia. For American whalers operating in the Indian Ocean, the Cape was a familiar landmark. Its treacherous waters earned it the nickname "Cape of Storms" — a name Melville echoes in the opening of Chapter 51: "Cape of Good Hope, do they call ye? Rather Cape Tormentoto, as called of yore" (051-009).

## Symbolic Significance

The Cape of Good Hope — whose name is an optimistic counter to its fearsome reality — embodies the novel's persistent irony about human attempts to name and domesticate nature. Melville's comparison of Jonah's journey to the Cape route also links Ahab to the reluctant prophet: both men flee their appointed paths, and both are pursued by a divine (or natural) force that will not be evaded.
