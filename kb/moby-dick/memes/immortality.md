# Immortality

## Definition

In Moby-Dick, immortality operates as a charged and paradoxical concept. It is attributed to Moby Dick by superstitious whalemen who declare the White Whale "not only ubiquitous, but immortal (for immortality is but ubiquity in time)" (041-013) — a belief that he cannot be killed, that "though groves of spears should be planted in his flanks, he would still swim away unharmed." Yet the novel offers a counter-image of immortality in Chapter 127, when Ahab beholds Queequeg's coffin and muses that "in some spiritual sense the coffin is, after all, but an immortality-preserver" (127-023). The term thus oscillates between delusion and revelation, between the whale's mythic invincibility and the coffin's redemptive transformation.

## Manifestations in the Novel

**The White Whale's immortality (Chapter 41)**. In "Moby Dick," Ishmael reports the superstitions that have grown around the whale. Whalemen, "forced into familiarity" with the creature's apparent invulnerability after repeated assaults, declare him immortal. The narrator glosses the term with a striking definition: "for immortality is but ubiquity in time" (041-013). The whale's deathlessness is spatialized — he is everywhere and therefore cannot die. Any apparent bloodshed is dismissed as "a ghastly deception," for his "unsullied jet" will be seen again "in unensanguined billows hundreds of leagues away" (041-013).

**The coffin as immortality-preserver (Chapter 127)**. In "The Deck," Ahab watches the Carpenter caulk Queequeg's coffin and falls into a meditation. The coffin — "the very dreaded symbol of grim death" — has become "by a mere hap, the expressive sign of the help and hope of most endangered life" (127-023). Ahab asks: "Can it be that in some spiritual sense the coffin is, after all, but an immortality-preserver!" (127-023). This moment reworks the novel's central paradox: the instrument of death becomes the means of life, prefiguring how Queequeg's coffin will later save Ishmael from the Pequod's wreck.

## Key Passages

- (041-013) — "declaring Moby Dick not only ubiquitous, but immortal (for immortality is but ubiquity in time); that though groves of spears should be planted in his flanks, he would still swim away unharmed; or if indeed he should ever be made to spout thick blood, such a sight would be but a ghastly deception."
- (127-023) — "Here now's the very dreaded symbol of grim death, by a mere hap, made the expressive sign of the help and hope of most endangered life. A life-buoy of a coffin! Does it go further? Can it be that in some spiritual sense the coffin is, after all, but an immortality-preserver!"

## Scholarly Context

The two passages that explicitly name immortality — Chapter 41's whale legend and Chapter 127's coffin meditation — form a diptych on the novel's treatment of death and transcendence. The first is collective, folkloric, and arguably deluded: the whalemen's belief in the whale's immortality is superstition born of trauma. The second is solitary, philosophical, and visionary: Ahab's meditation on the coffin glimpses a genuine paradox of life emerging from death. Critics have linked Ahab's "immortality-preserver" line to the novel's broader engagement with resurrection imagery — Queequeg's coffin as an ark, Ishmael as a latter-day Job — and to Melville's reading of Ecclesiastes and the Book of Job, where the question of what survives death is central.
