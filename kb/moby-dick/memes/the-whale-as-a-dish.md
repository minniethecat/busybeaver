# The Whale as a Dish

## Definition

"The Whale as a Dish" is Ishmael's extended meditation in Chapter 65 on the practice of eating whale meat — "that mortal man should feed upon the creature that feeds his lamp, and, like Stubb, eat him by his own light" (065-001). The chapter uses the question of whale cuisine as a satirical lens through which to examine human hypocrisy about food, the moral contradictions of carnivorous civilization, and the self-consuming logic of the whaling industry.

## Manifestations in the Novel

The concept is concentrated in Chapter 65 ("The Whale as a Dish"), which follows Stubb's whale steak supper in Chapter 64. Ishmael opens by acknowledging the apparent outlandishness of eating whale — "this seems so outlandish a thing that one must needs go a little into the history and philosophy of it" (065-001) — and proceeds through a series of historical, cultural, and philosophical arguments.

He notes that three centuries earlier, "the tongue of the Right Whale was esteemed a great delicacy in France" and that porpoises — "a species of whale" — were considered fine eating, "made into balls about the size of billiard balls, and being well seasoned and spiced might be taken for turtle-balls or veal balls" (065-002). The old monks of Dunfermline held a "great porpoise grant from the crown." Among whalemen, the whale would be "considered a noble dish, were there not so much of him; but when you come to sit down before a meat-pie nearly one hundred feet long, it takes away your appetite" (065-003).

Ishmael catalogs the culinary uses of whale parts: the sperm whale's brains are "accounted a fine dish" — "the casket of the skull is broken into with an axe, and the two plump, whitish lobes being withdrawn... they are then mixed with flour, and cooked into a most delectable mess" (065-005). The spermaceti itself is "bland and creamy... like the transparent, half-jellied, white meat of a cocoanut" (065-004), and whalemen "dip their ship-biscuit into the huge oil-pots and let them fry there awhile. Many a good supper have I thus made."

The chapter's rhetorical climax comes when Ishmael turns the charge of barbarism back on the reader: "Cannibals? who is not a cannibal? I tell you it will be more tolerable for the Fejee that salted down a lean missionary in his cellar... than for thee, civilized and enlightened gourmand, who nailest geese to the ground and feastest on their bloated livers in thy pate-de-foie-gras" (065-006). He notes that the gourmand's knife-handle is made of ox bone and his toothpick of goose feather — "with what quill did the Secretary of the Society for the Suppression of Cruelty to Ganders formally indite his circulars?" (065-007).

## Key Passages

> "But what further depreciates the whale as a civilized dish, is his exceeding richness. He is the great prize ox of the sea, too fat to be delicately good."
> (065-004)

> "Cannibals? who is not a cannibal? I tell you it will be more tolerable for the Fejee that salted down a lean missionary in his cellar against a coming famine... than for thee, civilized and enlightened gourmand, who nailest geese to the ground and feastest on their bloated livers in thy pate-de-foie-gras."
> (065-006)

## Scholarly Context

The "Whale as a Dish" chapter is read by critics as an example of Melville's dark comedy and his strategy of using whaling minutiae to stage broader moral arguments. The chapter's movement from historical anecdote to philosophical diatribe mirrors the structure of the sermon form, with Ishmael as a secular preacher. The self-consuming logic — eating the whale by the light of its own oil — functions as a figure for the novel's broader theme of self-destruction, from Ahab's quest to the Pequod's fate.
