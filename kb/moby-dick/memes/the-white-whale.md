# The White Whale

## Overview

Moby Dick, the White Whale, is the central symbol of Melville's novel.

He is a [[sperm-whale|sperm whale]] of extraordinary size, ferocity, and whiteness. Whalemen across the oceans know his name and fear him.

To [[ahab|Captain Ahab]], Moby Dick is not merely the animal that took his leg. He is the visible face of all that is unknowable, hostile, and inscrutable in the universe.

The whale first appears in rumor. Then in memory. He does not appear in the flesh until the novel's final chapters.

## Physical Description

Moby Dick is an albino sperm whale. His whiteness is his most striking feature.

He has a "snow-white wrinkled forehead, and a high, pyramidical white hump" (041-014).

His jaw is deformed. His spout is distinctive. He carries a "labyrinth of snags" on his body: harpoons from previous encounters.

When he dives, his tail rises like a mountain of snow.

## The White Whale as Symbol

What Moby Dick represents is the novel's central question. Melville offers no single answer.

To Ahab, the whale is a "pasteboard mask" behind which an evil, reasoning power hides. He must "strike through the mask" (036-040).

To [[starbuck|Starbuck]], the whale is a "dumb brute." To hunt him for vengeance is blasphemy.

To [[ishmael|Ishmael]], the whale's whiteness is the "visible absence of color" — the terror of the void.

## The Whale's History

Moby Dick is known across the whaling fleet. His reputation precedes him.

He has destroyed boats from the [[jeroboam|Jeroboam]], the [[samuel-enderby|Samuel Enderby]], the [[rachel|Rachel]], and the [[delight|Delight]].

Captain Boomer of the Samuel Enderby lost an arm to him. Unlike Ahab, Boomer wants nothing further from the whale.

The whale seems ubiquitous. He is sighted in all oceans. Some whalemen believe he is immortal.

## The Three-Day Chase

Moby Dick is first sighted by Ahab himself on the first day of the chase (Ch. 133).

On the first day, he smashes Ahab's whale-boat. On the second, he destroys it again and kills [[fedallah|Fedallah]].

On the third day (Ch. 135), Ahab harpoons him. The whale turns and rams the [[pequod|Pequod]].

The ship sinks. Ahab, tangled in his own line, is dragged under. Moby Dick survives.

## Critical Interpretation

The whale has been read as God, as Nature, as the Freudian unconscious, and as the Lacanian Real.

D.H. Lawrence saw Moby Dick as "the deepest blood-being of the white race." C.L.R. James read him as the revolutionary masses.

The whale's whiteness, analyzed at length in Chapter 42 ("[[whiteness-of-the-whale|The Whiteness of the Whale]]"), remains the novel's most elusive meditation.
