# Ignorance

## Definition

Ignorance in Moby-Dick is not a simple lack to be remedied but a complex human condition with moral and existential dimensions. Ishmael's claim that "Ignorance is the parent of fear" — a phrase that echoes through the novel — establishes ignorance as the psychological root of the terrors that drive the narrative. Yet ignorance is also presented as protective: "courage from ignorance" (026-001) allows men to face dangers they would flee if they fully understood them. And at its deepest level, the novel confronts a more profound ignorance — the epistemological limits that even the most systematic knowledge (cetology, philosophy, theology) cannot overcome. Melville thus presents a hierarchy of ignorance, from the merely uninformed to the constitutively unknowable.

## Manifestations in the Novel

**Ignorance as the parent of fear (Chapter 3)**. At the Spouter-Inn, Ishmael's encounter with Queequeg dramatizes how ignorance breeds terror. Not knowing who or what the tattooed harpooneer is, Ishmael's imagination runs wild: "ignorance is the parent of fear" (003-056, as later cited by the narrative tradition). The unknown terrifies because the mind fills absence with projected horrors. When Ishmael later faces the whale, his fear is partly a fear of what he does not — cannot — know about the creature he hunts.

**Courage from ignorance (Chapter 26)**. In his meditation on the common sailor's dignity, Ishmael notes that courage sometimes derives "from ignorance" (026-001) rather than intelligence. The sailor who does not fully grasp the danger of his situation can act with a fearlessness that knowledge would extinguish. This is both a tribute to the sailor's bravery and a melancholic observation: their heroism depends on not understanding what they are doing.

**The profound ignorance of cetology (Chapter 32)**. In "Cetology," Ishmael confronts the limits of human knowledge directly. Despite centuries of whaling, the scientific understanding of whales remains mired in "profound ignorance" (032-007). The classification systems of the learned are shown to be as arbitrary and incomplete as any sailor's folklore. This chapter establishes the novel's epistemological skepticism: even systematic inquiry produces only partial, provisional knowledge — a form of organized ignorance.

**Ignorance and superstitiousness (Chapter 41)**. The crew's fear of Moby Dick is intensified by ignorance: "as a body unexempt from that ignorance and superstitiousness hereditary to all sailors" (041-004). Superstition flourishes where knowledge fails — the sailors' portent-reading is the mind's attempt to impose meaning on what it does not understand.

**The incredulity born of ignorance (Chapter 45)**. Ishmael acknowledges that his narrative may be met with "incredulity which a profound ignorance of the entire subject may induce" (045-001). The landsman's ignorance of whaling makes him skeptical of truths that sailors know as commonplaces. Here ignorance becomes a barrier to understanding the novel itself — the reader must overcome their own ignorance to receive the story.

**Ignorance of the White Whale (Chapter 81)**. The German captain of the Jungfrau "soon evinced his complete ignorance of the White Whale" (081-008), demonstrating the gap between those who have encountered Moby Dick and those who know him only by rumor. Ignorance here marks the boundary between initiates and outsiders — the Pequod's crew know what others cannot imagine.

**Ancient ignorance (Chapter 82)**. Even the ancients, for all their wisdom, operated within "the great ignorance of those times" (082-005) regarding the whale. This historical dimension universalizes ignorance — every age believes it knows, and every age is partly wrong.

**Ignorance in fever (Chapter 110)**. In Queequeg's fever, "men, all ignorance, have talked in ancient tongues" (110-014). The delirium of illness strips away acquired knowledge, revealing a deeper ignorance beneath. Yet this state is also presented as oracular — ignorance and prophetic speech converge in the fever dream.

## Key Passages

- (026-001) — Courage "from ignorance" rather than intelligence — the protective function of not-knowing.
- (032-007) — "The profound ignorance which, till some seventy years back, invested the then fabulous or utterly unknown Sperm Whale" — the history of cetological ignorance.
- (041-004) — The crew as "a body unexempt from that ignorance and superstitiousness hereditary to all sailors" — ignorance and superstition.
- (045-001) — "Incredulity which a profound ignorance of the entire subject may induce" — the reader's ignorance as barrier.
- (081-008) — The German captain's "complete ignorance of the White Whale" — insider vs. outsider knowledge.
- (082-005) — "The great ignorance of those times" — historical ignorance, the limits of every era's knowledge.
- (110-014) — "Men, all ignorance, have talked in ancient tongues" — ignorance and prophetic speech in fever.

## Scholarly Context

Ignorance in Moby-Dick has been discussed in relation to Melville's engagement with epistemology and skepticism. The novel's cetological chapters — long derided as digressive filler — are now read as a sustained meditation on the limits of systematic knowledge. Ishmael's failed taxonomy in "Cetology" anticipates 20th-century critiques of Enlightenment confidence in classification and mastery. The "Ignorance is the parent of fear" formulation has been linked to Melville's reading of Hobbes (fear of the unknown as the basis of religion and political obedience) and to the Calvinist doctrine of human epistemological limitation — the finite mind cannot comprehend the infinite God, just as the whaleman cannot fully know the whale.
