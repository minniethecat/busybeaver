# Pictures of Whales

## Definition

"Of the Monstrous Pictures of Whales" (Chapter 55) and "Of the Less Erroneous Pictures of Whales, and the True Pictures of Whaling Scenes" (Chapter 56) form a linked pair of chapters in which Ishmael surveys the entire history of human attempts to depict the whale — and finds every single one wanting. The survey ranges from "the oldest Hindoo, Egyptian, and Grecian sculptures" (055-002) through Renaissance paintings, scientific textbooks, popular prints, and shop signs, concluding with the radical claim that "the great Leviathan is that one creature in the world which must remain unpainted to the last" (055-014). What begins as a comic catalogue of error becomes a philosophical argument about the limits of representation itself.

## Manifestations in the Novel

### The Catalogue of Errors (Chapter 55)

Ishmael's survey proceeds through a relentless sequence of failed depictions, each more absurd than the last:

**Ancient and Mythological Sources.** The earliest portrait — the Matse Avatar in the cavern-pagoda of Elephanta — shows Vishnu incarnated as leviathan, but "that small section of him is all wrong. It looks more like the tapering tail of an anaconda, than the broad palms of the true whale's majestic flukes" (055-003). Guido's painting of Perseus rescuing Andromeda from the sea-monster fares no better, nor does Hogarth's version: "The huge corpulence of that Hogarthian monster undulates on the surface, scarcely drawing one inch of water" (055-004).

**The Bookbinder's Whale.** Ishmael pauses over an image familiar to every reader: "the book-binder's whale winding like a vine-stalk round the stock of a descending anchor — as stamped and gilded on the backs and title-pages of many books both old and new" (055-004). This image, though "a very picturesque but purely fabulous creature," was "introduced by an old Italian publisher somewhere about the 15th century, during the Revival of Learning" (055-004). The bookbinder's whale is the visual equivalent of received opinion — an image copied so long that its origins are forgotten and its falsity unchallenged.

**Scientific Failures.** The critique sharpens when Ishmael turns to putatively accurate depictions. Captain Colnett's "picture of a Physeter or Spermaceti whale, drawn by scale from one killed on the coast of Mexico" has an eye "which applied, according to the accompanying scale, to a full grown sperm whale, would make the eye of that whale a bow-window some five feet long. Ah, my gallant captain, why did ye not give us Jonah looking out of that eye!" (055-007). Goldsmith's "Animated Nature" presents "an alleged 'whale' and a 'narwhale'" — "this unsightly whale looks much like an amputated sow" (055-008). The capstone: Frederick Cuvier's 1836 picture of the Sperm Whale, which "is not a Sperm Whale, but a squash" (055-010).

**The Unpaintable Whale.** The chapter's philosophical conclusion is that the living whale cannot be depicted because it cannot be seen whole: "The living whale, in his full majesty and significance, is only to be seen at sea in unfathomable waters; and afloat the vast bulk of him is out of sight, like a launched line-of-battle ship" (055-012). Even the skeleton deceives: "the mere skeleton of the whale bears the same relation to the fully invested and padded animal as the insect does to the chrysalis that so roundingly envelopes it" (055-013). The conclusion is as devastating as it is comic: "any way you may look at it, you must needs conclude that the great Leviathan is that one creature in the world which must remain unpainted to the last" (055-014).

### Toward True Pictures (Chapter 56)

Chapter 56 softens the satire. Having established that no picture of the whale *itself* is accurate, Ishmael concedes that "true pictures of whaling scenes" — scenes of the *chase*, not the creature — can be found. He praises a French engraving after Garnier and two large French prints after H. Durand, commending them for capturing the action of whaling if not the form of the whale. The shift from the whale-as-object to whaling-as-action prefigures the novel's own method: since the whale itself escapes representation, the only truthful approach is to represent the *encounter* with the whale.

> "For it is one of the more curious things about this Leviathan, that his skeleton gives very little idea of his general shape." (055-013)

> "'However recklessly the whale may sometimes serve us,' said humorous Stubb one day, 'he can never be truly said to handle us without mittens.'" (055-013)

## Scholarly Context

The "Pictures of Whales" chapters have been read as Melville's contribution to the ancient debate between word and image — a comic demonstration that language (particularly Melville's own riotous, encyclopedic prose) can capture what visual representation cannot. The chapters also serve a structural function: positioned within the novel's extended cetological sequence, they justify Ishmael's verbal method by discrediting all visual alternatives. Critics have noted the democratic dimension of Ishmael's critique — the sign-painter and the scientist are equally wrong, the bookbinder and the count equally deluded — which levels hierarchies of knowledge and places the experienced whaleman above the credentialed expert. The "unpainted to the last" thesis has been read as Melville's sly assertion of the novel's own unique truth-status: only *Moby-Dick* itself, in its monstrous hybridity of narrative and encyclopedia, can get the whale right.
