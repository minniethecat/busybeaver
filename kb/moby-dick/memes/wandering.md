# Wandering

## Definition

Wandering in *Moby-Dick* is not merely a geographical condition but the novel's primary existential metaphor. Ishmael goes to sea because life on shore has become unbearable — "whenever I find myself growing grim about the mouth; whenever it is a damp, drizzly November in my soul" (001-001) — and his wandering is simultaneously flight from despair and pursuit of something he cannot name. The Pequod is a "world-wandering whale ship" (087-005), and its voyage recapitulates the human condition Melville diagnoses: we are wanderers on an "uncertain, unsettled, mysterious" planet, with no fixed home and no certain destination. The novel's deepest claim about wandering comes in Chapter 93: "wandering from all mortal reason, man comes at last to that celestial thought" (093-013). To wander is to leave the known, and to leave the known is the precondition of any genuine discovery — though whether the discovery is heaven or madness remains deliberately undecidable.

## Manifestations in the Novel

**Ishmael's wandering cure (Chapter 1)**. The novel opens with a confession of wandering as remedy. Ishmael's "damp, drizzly November in my soul" drives him to sea as "a substitute for pistol and ball" (001-001). Wandering here is not a choice but a compulsion — the alternative is suicide or violence. The Cato-like gesture of philosophically choosing the sea over self-destruction frames the entire narrative: to wander is to choose life, even if the life chosen is one of radical uncertainty. Ishmael's wandering is also epistemological: "the great flood-gates of the wonder-world swung open" (001-013), and wandering becomes the condition of wonder, the state in which the world reveals itself as strange.

**The world-wandering whale ships (Chapter 13)**. Ishmael observes the whale-ships at Nantucket lying "side by side the world-wandering whale ships lay silent and safe" (013-005). The phrase "world-wandering" is nearly a kenning — it names the ships by their ontological condition rather than their function. These are not merely ships that happen to travel; their identity is their wandering. A whaler without a fixed route, without a known destination, is the material form of the wandering consciousness Ishmael embodies.

**Wandering in the heart of unknown regions (Chapter 45)**. Ishmael describes whaling as "wandering in the heart of unknown regions" (045-003), emphasizing that the whalemen's wandering is not tourism but penetration into the unmapped. The "heart" of the unknown — the metaphor is anatomical and intimate. To wander is to enter the interior of the world's mystery, to go where no chart extends. This is the heroism of whaling for Ishmael, and also its terror: the unknown regions may not be survivable.

**The world-wandering whale-ship carries no cargo (Chapter 87)**. In the Grand Armada chapter, Ishmael notes that "the world-wandering whale-ship carries no cargo but herself and her crew" (087-005). Unlike merchant vessels that exchange goods, the whaler wanders to extract — and the extraction (whale oil) is stowed, not traded en route. The ship's wandering is self-contained, self-referential; it carries only what it needs to sustain its own wandering. This is a figure for the novel itself: *Moby-Dick* carries no moral cargo, delivers no lesson; it is pure wandering in prose, self-sufficient and self-consuming.

**Wild wanderings of youth (Chapter 102)**. In a retrospective passage, Ishmael mentions "my wild wanderings at that period" (102-014) when he was tattooed, suggesting that his wandering was not only geographical but also bodily — the skin becomes a record of travel, the body inscribed with the marks of distance. The tattoo, like the narrative, is a trace left by wandering, a text generated by motion.

**Wandering from mortal reason (Chapter 93)**. The novel's most radical statement on wandering comes in "The Castaway": "wandering from all mortal reason, man comes at last to that celestial thought" (093-013). The context is Pip's madness — the cabin-boy who, abandoned in the sea, loses his reason and gains what Ishmael calls "heaven's sense." To wander from reason is to risk madness, but it is also the only path to "celestial thought." The paradox is Melville's profoundest: the truth may require leaving the shore of reason, but the ocean of unreason may drown the wanderer. Pip wanders too far and cannot return; Ishmael wanders and barely survives. The difference between them is the difference between the novel's survivor and its casualty.

## Key Passages

- (001-001) — "whenever it is a damp, drizzly November in my soul": the founding impulse of the novel, the soul's weather that drives wandering
- (001-013) — "the great flood-gates of the wonder-world swung open": wandering as the opening of wonder
- (013-005) — "side by side the world-wandering whale ships lay silent and safe": the whale-ship defined by its wandering condition
- (045-003) — "wandering in the heart of unknown regions": whaling as penetration into the unmapped interior
- (087-005) — "the world-wandering whale-ship carries no cargo but herself and her crew": the self-contained wandering that mirrors the novel's narrative structure
- (093-013) — "wandering from all mortal reason, man comes at last to that celestial thought": the novel's ultimate claim about the epistemology of wandering
- (102-014) — "my wild wanderings at that period, there was no": the body as record of wandering, the tattooed skin as textual trace

## Scholarly Context

Wandering in *Moby-Dick* has been read through several critical lenses. Romanticist approaches emphasize Melville's debt to the Wordsworthian wanderer and the Byronic exile, figures for whom wandering is spiritual discipline and social refusal. Deconstructionist readings (particularly Hillis Miller's) treat the novel's narrative structure as itself a form of wandering — digressive, non-teleological, forever deferring the whale it pursues. Postcolonial criticism reads Ishmael's wandering in relation to the Pequod's multi-ethnic crew: the world-wandering ship is also a floating empire, and the "wild wanderings" of youth invoke the colonial privilege of mobility. The Pip passage has generated particular attention: Pip's wandering from reason into "celestial thought" has been compared to the Romantic tradition of holy madness (Blake's *The Marriage of Heaven and Hell*) and to the shamanic journey — the ecstatic departure from ordinary consciousness that returns with vision, or does not return at all.
