# Aunt Charity

## Overview

Aunt Charity is Captain Bildad's sister, a minor but warmly drawn character who appears in the Pequod's departure chapters. Though she never goes to sea, her presence is felt throughout the early voyage through the provisions she brings aboard — ginger, gingerbread, and other small comforts. Melville names her with characteristic irony: she "did not probably deserve her name" (020-005), yet her actions throughout the departure sequence demonstrate a practical kindness that is among the few uncomplicated virtues in the novel. Dough-Boy, the Pequod's steward, later recalls her as the source of the ship's ginger (072-015).

## Role in the Narrative

Aunt Charity serves as a representative of **Nantucket domestic life** at the threshold of the Pequod's departure. She appears briefly in Chapters 20 and 22, bustling about the ship's preparations, bringing gifts and provisions. Her presence anchors the domestic world that the crew is about to leave behind — the world of gingerbread, clean bedding, and spare Testaments.

Her narrative function is to humanize the Pequod's Quaker owners. Where Bildad is comically miserly and Peleg is gruffly practical, Aunt Charity introduces a note of genuine care: the small comforts she provides become tokens of a world beyond whaling's violence. Dough-Boy's later recollection of "Aunt Charity's gift" (072-020) shows that her gestures of kindness persist in the crew's memory.

## Key Appearances

- [[Ch. 20: All Astir]](020-005) — Aunt Charity is introduced as Bildad's sister who "did not probably deserve her name, which was Charity — Aunt Charity, as everybody called her"
- [[Ch. 22: Merry Christmas]](022-001) — "After the ever-thoughtful Charity had come off in a whale-boat" with final provisions, including a spare Bible for the crew
- [[Ch. 22]](022-009) — Charity brings a "small basket" of gingerbread and other comforts as the Pequod gets underway
- [[Ch. 72: The Monkey-Rope]](072-015) — Dough-Boy recalls Aunt Charity fondly: "it was Aunt Charity that brought the ginger on board"
- [[Ch. 72]](072-020) — Queequeg and Stubb discuss the ginger's origin: "the second was Aunt Charity's gift, and that was freely given to the steward"

## Character Traits

Aunt Charity is defined by her **practical kindness**, a quality Melville treats with gentle irony. Her name is a pun — she is "Charity" who is perhaps not especially charitable in the theological sense, but who demonstrates care through action rather than sentiment. Her gifts are modest (ginger, tea, a spare Bible) but they represent a form of love that the novel's grander passions largely exclude.

She embodies **Nantucket piety** of the domestic variety — the Quaker ethic of simplicity and service, expressed not in theology but in provisioning. Her spare Bible, brought for any sailor who lacked one, reflects a practical approach to religion that mirrors the Quaker owners' practical approach to whaling profits.

Unlike almost every other character associated with the Pequod, Aunt Charity is associated exclusively with **departure and provision**, not with the voyage or its catastrophe. She belongs to the shore, not the sea, and her gifts are the last touch of home.

## Relationships

- **[[bildad|Captain Bildad]]**: Charity's brother, the comically pious and miserly co-owner of the Pequod. Their shared Quaker background contrasts with their very different expressions of it — Bildad's stinginess versus Charity's generosity.
- **[[peleg|Captain Peleg]]**: The other co-owner of the Pequod, for whom Charity also provides.
- **[[dough-boy|Dough-Boy]]**: The Pequod's steward, who remembers Aunt Charity as the source of the ship's ginger — one of the few fond memories aboard the vessel.
- **[[stubb|Stubb]]**: Discusses the ginger's origin with Queequeg in Chapter 72, providing a comic moment of culinary genealogy.
