# Blasphemy

## Definition

Blasphemy in *Moby-Dick* is not a narrow religious offense but a cosmic category. When Starbuck calls Ahab's pursuit "blasphemous" (036-039) and later "impiety and blasphemy" (134-042), he is naming something larger than profane speech: the sin of treating the natural order as an enemy to be struck at, of refusing to accept that some forces — the whale, the sea, the divine — are beyond human challenge. Ahab's blasphemy is not speech but *action*: he would "strike the sun if it insulted me" (036-040). The novel uses blasphemy to frame its central moral question: at what point does human defiance cross from heroism into sacrilege, from tragic quest into cosmic crime?

## Manifestations in the Novel

**The quarter-deck: blasphemy declared (Chapter 36)**. The novel's first and most concentrated treatment of blasphemy occurs during Ahab's quarter-deck speech. When Starbuck protests that vengeance against "a dumb brute" acting from "blindest instinct" is madness, he adds: "To be enraged with a dumb thing, Captain Ahab, seems blasphemous" (036-039). Starbuck uses "blasphemous" precisely: to treat an animal as morally accountable is to misattribute agency, and to misattribute agency to nature is, in a Puritan framework, to usurp God's prerogative. Ahab's response is the novel's most famous declaration of cosmic defiance:

> "Talk not to me of blasphemy, man; I'd strike the sun if it insulted me. For could the sun do that, then could I do the other; since there is ever a sort of fair play herein, jealousy presiding over all creations." (036-040)

Ahab does not deny the charge of blasphemy; he *embraces* it. His "fair play" theology reimagines the cosmos as a contest between equals — he and the sun, he and the whale, he and whatever power stands behind the "pasteboard mask." Blasphemy, in Ahab's reframing, is the only honest response to a universe that wounds without explanation.

**The second day: blasphemy confirmed (Chapter 134)**. On the second day of the chase, after the whale has stove another boat and severed Ahab's ivory leg, Starbuck makes his final moral plea: "Great God! but for one single instant show thyself. Never, never wilt thou capture him, old man — In Jesus' name no more of this, that's worse than devil's madness." And then the climactic accusation: "Shall we be towed by him to the infernal world? Oh, oh, — Impiety and blasphemy to hunt him more!" (134-042).

By this point, the charge has deepened. What was in Chapter 36 a philosophical error — treating a brute as a moral agent — has become, by Chapter 134, a full cosmic transgression. The stakes are no longer Ahab's soul alone but the entire crew's: "shall we be dragged by him to the bottom of the sea?" Starbuck's "we" includes himself — he is complicit, and his cry of "blasphemy" is as much confession as accusation. The novel refuses to resolve whether Starbuck is right: Ahab does not reply to this charge, and the whale's third-day victory can be read as either divine judgment or meaningless accident.

**Blasphemy and the whale's divinity**. Throughout the novel, Melville plays with the idea that the whale is itself a kind of god, and that hunting it is therefore inherently sacrilegious. The whale is described in biblical language — "Leviathan" — and Ishmael's Cetology chapters borrow the taxonomic seriousness of Genesis. To kill a whale, in this framing, is always already a kind of blasphemy. The difference between the ordinary whaleman's killing and Ahab's is one of *intent*: the whaleman kills for oil, a commercial transaction with nature; Ahab kills for meaning, a metaphysical assault on the order of things.

**Starbuck's blasphemy threshold**. Starbuck is not simply the voice of piety. In Chapter 123, he contemplates murdering Ahab in his sleep — an act that would itself be a form of blasphemy against the chain of command that, in the novel's world, carries divine sanction. That Starbuck cannot pull the trigger suggests that blasphemy is not a boundary he can cross — but that he can *contemplate* crossing it suggests that Ahab's blasphemy has infected the entire ship. Blasphemy in *Moby-Dick* is contagious.

## Scholarly Context

The question of blasphemy in *Moby-Dick* has been central to theological readings of the novel. Lawrence Buell has argued that Melville's blasphemy is a specifically post-Puritan phenomenon — a rebellion that only makes sense within the framework it rejects. Others, following William Spanos, have read Ahab's blasphemy as an existentialist gesture: in a universe without transcendent meaning, "blasphemy" is an empty category, and Ahab's real sin is not against God but against the crew whose lives he wagers. The novel's refusal to adjudicate between these readings — is Ahab blasphemous or heroic, damned or free? — is the source of its enduring moral power.
