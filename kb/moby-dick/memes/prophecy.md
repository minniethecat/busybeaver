# Prophecy

## Definition

Prophecy in *Moby-Dick* operates on multiple levels: as folk superstition among sailors, as a narrative device that foreshadows the novel's catastrophic ending, and as a philosophical problem about whether the future is fixed or can be altered by human action. The novel presents prophecies from several sources — Elijah's cryptic warnings, Gabriel the Shaker's predictions, Fedallah's detailed prophecy of Ahab's death — and uses them to probe the relationship between foreknowledge and free will. Ahab consistently interprets prophecies in ways that flatter his own agency, a pattern of self-deception that Melville presents as both tragic and psychologically acute.

## Manifestations in the Novel

The novel layers prophecies from its earliest chapters. In Chapter 19 ("The Prophet"), a ragged stranger named Elijah accosts Ishmael and Queequeg on the Nantucket dock, asking ominously whether they have "signed the articles" and hinting at dark events aboard the Pequod: "nothing about that thing that happened to him off Cape Horn, long ago, when he lay like dead for three days and nights" (019-023). Elijah's warning establishes a pattern: prophecies in this novel are always retrospective, their meaning clear only after the event.

In Chapter 37 ("Sunset"), Ahab soliloquizes about a prophecy of his own dismemberment: "The prophecy was that I should be dismembered; and — Aye! I lost this leg. I now prophesy that I will dismember my dismemberer" (037-005). Ahab's response to prophecy is characteristic: he accepts the part that validates his power and inverts the rest into a declaration of his own will.

Gabriel the Shaker, aboard the *Jeroboam* in Chapter 71, is the novel's most explicit prophet-figure. After the mate Macey is killed by a whale, Gabriel claims he "had specifically fore-announced it, instead of only making a general prophecy, which any one might have done" (071-019). His warning to Ahab — "Think, think of the blasphemer — dead, and down there! — beware of the blasphemer's end!" — is ignored.

Fedallah's prophecy, delivered in Chapter 117 ("The Whale Watch"), is the novel's most elaborate. He tells Ahab that he will die only after two conditions are met: "ere thou couldst die on this voyage, two hearses must verily be seen by thee on the sea; the first not made by mortal hands; and the visible wood of the last one must be grown in America" (117-007). Ahab interprets this as a guarantee of his immortality — how could a hearse not be made by mortal hands? The tragedy unfolds in Chapter 135 when Ahab realizes the first hearse is Moby Dick himself (not made by mortal hands), carrying Fedallah's corpse tangled in the lines, and the second hearse is the Pequod, built of American wood.

The novel's most pathetic prophecy comes from Pip, who after his near-drowning becomes "a living and ever accompanying prophecy of whatever shattered sequel might prove her own" (093-001). Pip's madness embodies the novel's idea that prophecy is not about predicting the future but about already living inside it.

## Scholarly Context

The prophecies in *Moby-Dick* participate in the classical epic tradition — the Delphic oracle's riddling predictions, the witches' prophecies in *Macbeth* — but with a distinctively American, democratic twist. Melville's prophets are not priests or oracles but a dockside lunatic (Elijah), a fanatical Shaker (Gabriel), a Parsi harpooneer (Fedallah), and a cabin boy driven mad by terror (Pip). The truth-value of prophecy is democratized: anyone can see the future if they are broken enough by the present. Ahab's fatal misreading of Fedallah's prophecy — interpreting a guarantee of death as a promise of immortality — has been analyzed as Melville's critique of the American habit of hearing only what one wishes in providential signs.
