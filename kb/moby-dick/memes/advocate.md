# The Advocate

## A Forensic Form

Chapter 24 of *Moby-Dick*, "The Advocate," is unique in the novel's architecture. Alone among the 135 chapters, it takes the form of a formal rhetorical defense — a legal brief for the profession of whaling. Ishmael adopts the voice of counsel, addressing an imaginary jury of "ye landsmen" and methodically dismantling their prejudices against whalemen. The chapter's argumentative structure — assertion, objection, rebuttal — mirrors the classical *disputatio*, the scholastic form of debate that had been absorbed into the sermon and the political pamphlet, Melville's two great non-fiction influences.

The chapter's placement, between the introduction of the mates and harpooneers in "Knights and Squires" (Ch.26-27) and the departure of the Pequod, is strategic. Before the narrative can proceed into the open sea, Melville pauses to establish not just the facts of whaling — its tools, its economics, its dangers — but its dignity. "The Advocate" is the novel's credentialing chapter: it insists that whaling is worthy of the epic treatment Melville is about to give it.

## The Charge and the Rebuttal

Ishmael opens with the charge against whaling plainly stated: "this business of whaling has somehow come to be regarded among landsmen as a rather unpoetical and disreputable pursuit" (024-001). The word "unpoetical" is key — the landsman's objection is not moral but aesthetic. Whaling lacks the glamour of soldiering, the gentility of the liberal professions, the romance of exploration. It is, at best, "a butchering sort of business" (024-003).

Ishmael's rebuttal unfolds across five fronts:

**The soldier comparison.** If whalemen are butchers, so are soldiers — "butchers of the bloodiest badge have been all Martial Commanders whom the world invariably delights to honor" (024-003). And the whale-ship's dangers far exceed the battlefield's: "what are the comprehensible terrors of man compared with the interlinked terrors and wonders of God!" (024-003). The whale is not a human enemy but a force of nature, sublime and incomprehensible.

**Economic magnitude.** Ishmael rattles off statistics — seven hundred vessels, eighteen thousand men, four million dollars consumed yearly, seven million imported — to establish that whaling is not a marginal trade but a major industry, one that nations have subsidized and kings have patronized (024-006). The appeal is to the Enlightenment standard of utility: if magnitude of economic effect is a measure of importance, whaling is undeniably important.

**Geopolitical impact.** In the chapter's most audacious claims, Ishmael credits whaling with opening the Pacific to global commerce, liberating Peru, Chile, and Bolivia from Spanish rule, discovering Australia, and clearing the way for the missionaries who would Christianize Polynesia (024-008–024-010). The whaleman emerges as an agent of world history — the "pioneer in ferreting out the remotest and least known parts of the earth" (024-008), greater than Cook or Vancouver because he did it "in their succourless empty-handedness," without marines or muskets (024-008).

**Cultural pedigree.** To the objection that "the whale has no famous author, and whaling no famous chronicler," Ishmael counters with a catalogue of dignitaries: Job wrote of Leviathan, Alfred the Great recorded the whaling voyage of Ohthere, Edmund Burke eulogized whaling in Parliament (024-013). Benjamin Franklin was descended from a Nantucket whaling family — the Folgers — so that "this day darting the barbed iron from one side of the world to the other" are "all kith and kin to noble Benjamin" (024-015). Whaling, far from lacking pedigree, has the oldest and noblest of all — biblical, royal, philosophical.

**Cosmic dignity.** The chapter's rhetorical climax answers the objection that "there is no real dignity in whaling" with a gesture toward the heavens: "Cetus is a constellation in the South!" (024-022). The whale is literally inscribed in the stars. And the chapter's final sentence — "a whale-ship was my Yale College and my Harvard" (024-023) — reclaims whaling as an education superior to the institutions that credential the "liberal professions" the landsman reveres.

## The Advocate's Irony

The brilliance of "The Advocate" lies in its tonal ambiguity. Ishmael's defense of whaling is so hyperbolic, so relentlessly escalating in its claims, that the reader is never quite sure whether to take it at face value. When Ishmael asserts that whalemen liberated South America and discovered Australia, the claims are historically arguable at best. When he calls on the constellation Cetus as proof of whaling's dignity, the rhetoric tips into the comic.

Yet the chapter is not merely ironic. Melville's own experience as a whaleman gave him genuine respect for the profession, and the statistics he deploys — the size of the fleet, the value of the harvest — were accurate for the 1840s. The chapter's double register — earnest defense and self-aware hyperbole — is characteristic of Melville's mature voice. He means what he says, but he also means the reader to see that he is saying it with a certain performative excess, a lawyer's zeal that is itself part of the performance.

The chapter's final sentence — "a whale-ship was my Yale College and my Harvard" — resolves the ambiguity in the direction of sincerity. Melville dropped out of school at twelve; the whaling ship *was* his university, the place where he read Shakespeare and Milton and the Bible and thought through the questions that would produce *Moby-Dick*. The Advocate's defense of whaling is also Melville's defense of his own unusual education, his own claim to have earned, through work and danger and wide reading at sea, the right to write an epic.

## Scholarly Context

"The Advocate" has been read as Melville's most direct engagement with the rhetoric of American exceptionalism and Manifest Destiny. Written at the height of the Mexican-American War (1846-1848) and published in 1851, the chapter's claims for whaling as an agent of global liberation and democracy mirror and parody the contemporaneous claims made for American expansion. The whaleman "liberating" South America and "discovering" Australia is the American frontiersman in maritime form — the agent of progress who opens closed societies to commerce and Christianity, whether they wish to be opened or not.

More recent ecocritical readings have focused on the chapter's tension between its defense of whaling and the novel's evident awe of the whale. Ishmael the advocate argues that whales exist to be killed and turned into oil; Ishmael the narrator will later describe the whale in terms of sublimity, majesty, and divine mystery. The contradiction is not resolved but sustained — whaling is both a noble profession and a catastrophic violence, and the novel declines to choose between these perspectives.

The chapter's formal qualities — its adoption of a forensic voice, its Socratic dialogue with imaginary interlocutors — have been compared to the classical tradition of the *apologia*, from Plato's *Apology* to Sidney's *Defence of Poesy*. Melville, like Sidney, is defending a disreputable art form by claiming for it the highest possible dignities.
