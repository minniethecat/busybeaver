# Daggoo

## Overview

Daggoo is the third harpooneer of the [[Pequod]], introduced in [[Ch. 27: Knights and Squires]](027-009) as "a gigantic, coal-black negro-savage" from Africa. He serves in [[Flask]]'s whale-boat, where his immense size makes him a commanding physical presence — at one point Flask literally stands on Daggoo's shoulders in the boat (048-029–048-030). Unlike [[Queequeg]] or [[Tashtego]], Daggoo has relatively few spoken lines, but his actions mark him as a brave, observant, and physically formidable sailor. He is the one who first sights the giant squid in [[Ch. 59: Squid]](059-002), sparking one of the novel's eeriest passages.

## Role in the Narrative

Daggoo represents the African continent in Melville's global crew. Where Queequeg is Polynesian and Tashtego is Native American (Gay Head Indian), Daggoo completes the trio of "pagan" harpooneers who embody the novel's democratic vision of human diversity. His "coal-black" skin and "gigantic" stature (027-009) are repeatedly emphasized — Melville uses his physicality to create scenes of spectacular visual impact, as when Daggoo looms up "to thrice his real stature" in the storm-lit night (119-034).

Daggoo's most dramatic moment comes in [[Ch. 78: Cistern and Buckets]](078-003–078-010), when [[Queequeg]] falls into the sinking whale's head. Daggoo alone has the physical strength and presence of mind to act, balancing on the cutting-tackles to rescue his fellow harpooneer. His cry — "both! both!—it is both!" (078-009) when he sees Queequeg emerge with Tashtego — is one of the novel's few moments of pure triumphant joy.

## Key Appearances

- [[Ch. 27: Knights and Squires]](027-009) — Introduction: "a gigantic, coal-black negro-savage," third harpooneer, assigned to Flask's boat
- [[Ch. 34: The Cabin-Table]](034-008–034-009) — Daggoo's humor: he seizes a steward, holds him "harpoon-wise," and sits crosswise on the floor "for a bench"
- [[Ch. 36: The Quarter-Deck]](036-025–036-031) — Daggoo speaks: "a curious spout, too, very bushy, even for a parmacetty" — one of his rare verbal contributions, describing Moby Dick's spout
- [[Ch. 40: Midnight, Forecastle]](040-052) — The Spanish Sailor says "No" to something; "Daggoo showing his teeth" — a silent, threatening presence
- [[Ch. 48: The First Lowering]](048-026–048-030) — Daggoo's physical grandeur on display: Flask stands on Daggoo's shoulders in the whale-boat, "sustaining himself by one of the projecting spars" — a bizarre, almost comical image of hierarchy
- [[Ch. 59: Squid]](059-002–059-004) — Daggoo, from the main-mast-head, sights "a strange spectre" — the giant white squid that Ishmael interprets as a portent
- [[Ch. 61: Stubb Kills a Whale]](061-014–061-023) — Daggoo yells "Kee-hee! Kee-hee!" in the excitement of the chase; later pronounces the whale "dead"
- [[Ch. 78: Cistern and Buckets]](078-003–078-010) — His finest hour: Daggoo cries "Man overboard!" when Queequeg falls into the whale's head, balances on the tackles to fish him out, and shouts "both! both!—it is both!" as Queequeg surfaces with Tashtego
- [[Ch. 81: The Pequod Meets the Virgin]](081-027) — The three "tigers" — Queequeg, Tashtego, Daggoo — spring to their feet at the sight of a whale
- [[Ch. 119: The Candles]](119-034) — In the corposant-lit storm, Daggoo "loomed up to thrice his real stature" — an almost supernatural visual
- [[Ch. 130: The Hat]](130-009) — Ahab's glance lingers "long upon Daggoo, Queequeg, Tashtego" — the three harpooneers whom he respects but will not save
- [[Ch. 135: The Chase — Third Day]](135-033) — Daggoo and Queequeg are found "stopping the strained planks" of the sinking ship — working to the last moment

## Character Traits

Daggoo's most prominent trait is his **immense physical presence**. At "gigantic" stature (027-009), he is the largest man aboard the Pequod, and Melville repeatedly uses him as a visual anchor — a mountain of black flesh against which the novel's action unfolds. His physicality is often deployed for comic or spectacular effect, as when Flask stands on his shoulders or when he looms to thrice his height in the storm.

He is defined by **action rather than speech**. Unlike [[Stubb]]'s ceaseless chatter or [[Ishmael]]'s philosophical commentary, Daggoo speaks only a handful of times in the entire novel. His contributions are physical: sighting the squid, yelling in the chase, rescuing Queequeg, rousing the crew for the final chase. He is the novel's embodiment of competence and physical courage without introspection.

His **African identity** places him at the farthest remove from Nantucket whaling culture. Unlike Queequeg, who is a prince's son, or Tashtego, who is a Gay Head Indian familiar to New Englanders, Daggoo is from a continent Melville's readers knew only through imagination and stereotype. Yet Melville gives him moments of nobility — particularly in the rescue of Queequeg — that undercut the racial language of "savage."

## Relationships

- **[[Flask]]**: Daggoo is Flask's harpooneer, and their relationship is the novel's strangest physical pairing — the short third mate literally standing on the gigantic African's shoulders (048-029). Flask's boat is the most reckless of the three, and Daggoo's strength enables that recklessness.
- **[[Queequeg]]** and **[[Tashtego]]**: The three harpooneers form a brotherhood of "pagans." Daggoo and Tashtego work together at the cutting-in (072-008), and Daggoo's rescue of Queequeg in Chapter 78 is one of the novel's great moments of solidarity.
- **[[Ahab]]**: Ahab's "long" glance at Daggoo in Chapter 130 suggests a recognition of the harpooneer's worth. In the final chapters, Ahab calls on "Tashtego, Queequeg, Daggoo" (113-025) as his pagan warriors.
- **[[Stubb]]**: Daggoo and Stubb interact during the whale-killing in Chapter 61: Daggoo pronounces the whale dead, and Stubb confirms. A simple exchange that reveals the mates' dependence on the harpooneers' judgment.
