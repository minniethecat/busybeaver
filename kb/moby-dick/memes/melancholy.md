# Melancholy

## Definition

Melancholy in *Moby-Dick* is not a passing mood but the novel's fundamental emotional atmosphere — the "damp, drizzly November in my soul" that Ishmael confesses in the opening sentence. Unlike despair, which is a crisis of meaning, or solitude, which is a condition of isolation, melancholy is a sustained attunement to loss that neither breaks nor lifts. It is the emotional register in which the novel's events unfold, the affective ground bass beneath every narrative surface. Melville's treatment of melancholy draws on the long tradition of melancholic temperament from Aristotle through Burton's *Anatomy of Melancholy*, but transforms it into a distinctively modern sensibility: the sadness of a world from which transcendent meaning has receded.

## Manifestations in the Novel

Ishmael's opening confession — "a damp, drizzly November in my soul" — establishes melancholy as the novel's point of departure. This is not clinical depression but a philosophical mood: the "hypos" that drive him to sea are the soul's protest against the meaninglessness of shore life. The sea voyage is thus framed as a therapeutic response to melancholy, though the novel will complicate any simple cure.

The Pequod itself is introduced under the sign of melancholy. Ishmael describes it as "a most melancholy" craft, adding immediately: "All noble things are touched with that" (016-004). This single line contains the novel's melancholic philosophy: nobility and sadness are inseparable. The ship's weather-beaten grandeur — its "venerable" appearance, its bones of whale and timber from "old age" — makes it melancholy in the precise sense that its greatness is inseparable from its decrepitude.

The mast-head chapter (Ch. 35) identifies melancholy as an occupational hazard of the whaling life. The mast-head is "an asylum for many romantic, melancholy, and absent-minded young men" (035-009) who stare at the water and lose themselves in "the problem of the universe revolving in them." Melville's irony is sharp: what the ship-owners think is vigilance is actually philosophical reverie, and the melancholy of the mast-head watch is a kind of thinking — perhaps the only kind the novel permits.

Melancholy suffuses the novel's later episodes with accumulating weight. The dying whale's spout is described with "half-stifled melancholy gurglings" (081-039), transferring human mood to the animal's death throes. The blacksmith Perth's life story (Ch. 113) is framed by "the black tragedy of the melancholy ship" (113-028). And in the novel's penultimate chapter, the Pequod's encounter with the Rachel is rendered "the more melancholy" (128-013) by the knowledge that the Rachel's lost children include the captain's own son — a grief that Ahab refuses to share.

Through the Pequod, Melville suggests that melancholy adheres to the ship not as an accident but as an essence — "the black tragedy of the melancholy ship, and mocked it" (113-028). The ship that carries Ahab's quest also carries the accumulated sadness of its crew, its victims, and its doomed purpose.

## Key Passages

- (016-004) — "a most melancholy! All noble things are touched with that": the novel's thesis on the nobility of sadness
- (017-026) — "fanatic religionists cherish such melancholy notions about their hereafter": melancholy as religious disposition
- (028-002) — Ahab observed with "a certain indefinable sort of leaping and melancholy rapidity": melancholy in motion, the captain's embodied sadness
- (035-009) — "an asylum for many romantic, melancholy, and absent-minded young men": the mast-head as melancholy's institutional home
- (081-039) — "with half-stifled melancholy gurglings the spray-column lowers": melancholy extended to the dying whale
- (113-028) — "with the black tragedy of the melancholy ship, and mocked it!": the ship as melancholic entity
- (126-012) — Starbuck speaking "after a melancholy pause": melancholy as the interval between action and resignation
- (128-013) — "the more melancholy, was the circumstance, that not only was one of the Captain's sons among the missing": melancholy compounding through kinship
