# Dr. Bunger

## Overview

Dr. Bunger is the ship's surgeon aboard the English whaler Samuel Enderby, introduced in Chapter 100 ("Leg and Arm"). Melville portrays him as "imperturbable" and "godly-looking" (100-025), a man whose professional calm and dry humor provide a comic counterpoint to the chapter's central encounter between Ahab and Captain Boomer. Bunger is the attending physician who amputated Boomer's arm after the captain's own encounter with Moby Dick, and his pragmatic medical advice — that whaling with a whalebone prosthetic is preferable to chasing the white whale — stands as one of the novel's few voices of sensible restraint.

## Role in the Narrative

Bunger appears solely in Chapter 100, during the Pequod's gam with the Samuel Enderby. His dramatic function is to provide exposition about Captain Boomer's injury while simultaneously deflating the grandiosity of Ahab's quest with medical common sense. When Ahab eagerly questions Boomer about Moby Dick, Bunger is the sober presence who reminds both captains of the physical cost of obsession: "He'll get well if you let him rest" (100-019).

Melville uses Bunger's professional detachment to create a tonal shift — from Ahab's feverish intensity to the English crew's matter-of-fact acceptance of whale-inflicted injuries as an occupational hazard. Bunger embodies the empirical, non-tragic response to Moby Dick: the whale is dangerous, yes, but the rational response is medical treatment, not metaphysical vengeance.

## Character Traits

Bunger's defining characteristic is his **professional imperturbability**. In a chapter dominated by Ahab's volcanic intensity and Boomer's jovial resignation, Bunger maintains the detached composure of the clinical observer. He is "godly-looking" — a phrase that suggests both physical dignity and a certain ironic sanctimony — and his speech is marked by measured, qualified medical pronouncements.

His **dry wit** surfaces in his interactions with Boomer, whom he treats with the affectionate exasperation of a doctor whose patient refuses to follow orders. When Boomer jokes about his injury, Bunger responds with medical precision that inadvertently highlights the absurdity of the situation.

Crucially, Bunger represents **rational empiricism** in the face of Ahab's metaphysical fury. His medical advice — rest, recovery, avoidance of further trauma — is the voice of sanity that Ahab cannot hear.

## Relationships

- **[[captain-boomer|Captain Boomer]]**: Bunger's captain and patient. Their relationship is one of mutual respect laced with comic friction — Boomer teases Bunger as "you dog" and demands he "laugh out," while Bunger treats Boomer with the resigned patience of a long-suffering physician.
- **[[ahab|Captain Ahab]]**: Bunger's interaction with Ahab is brief but charged. Bunger bows slightly, delivers his medical account, and then watches Ahab with evident concern — recognizing, perhaps, a patient far beyond medical help.
- **[[Samuel Enderby|The Samuel Enderby]]**: Bunger serves as the ship's surgeon, part of the English whaler's crew that represents a more pragmatic, less tragic approach to whaling than the Pequod's.
