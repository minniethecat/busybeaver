# Pitchpoling

## The Finest Manoeuvre

In the repertoire of whaling techniques, pitchpoling occupies a special place — it is, Ishmael declares, the apex of the whaleman's art. "Of all the wondrous devices and dexterities, the sleights of hand and countless subtleties, to which the veteran whaleman is so often forced, none exceed that fine manœuvre with the lance called pitchpoling. Small sword, or broad sword, in all its exercises boasts nothing like it" (084-004). The comparison to swordsmanship is revealing: Ishmael frames pitchpoling not as mere labor but as a martial art, a form of disciplined violence that demands the same grace, precision, and split-second timing as fencing.

Pitchpoling is a technique of last resort, deployed only when the whale cannot be approached closely. "It is only indispensable with an inveterate running whale; its grand fact and feature is the wonderful distance to which the long lance is accurately darted from a violently rocking, jerking boat, under extreme headway" (084-004). The conditions are the worst imaginable for accurate throwing — a pitching boat, extreme speed, a fleeing target forty feet ahead — and yet the pitchpoler must deliver the lance to a precise spot on the whale's body, the "life spot" whose penetration will prove fatal.

## The Instrument: Lance and Warp

The pitchpoling lance differs significantly from the harpoon. "Steel and wood included, the entire spear is some ten or twelve feet in length; the staff is much slighter than that of the harpoon, and also of a lighter material — pine" (084-004). Where the harpoon is heavy, designed to embed itself deeply and hold fast by its barbs, the lance is light, designed to be thrown accurately over distance and then retrieved. "It is furnished with a small rope called a warp, of considerable length, by which it can be hauled back to the hand after darting" (084-004).

This distinction between harpoon and lance is crucial to the technique's tactical logic. "Though the harpoon may be pitchpoled in the same way with the lance, yet it is seldom done; and when done, is still less frequently successful, on account of the greater weight and inferior length of the harpoon as compared with the lance, which in effect become serious drawbacks. As a general thing, therefore, you must first get fast to a whale, before any pitchpoling comes into play" (084-005). The sequence is: harpoon first (to fasten the boat to the whale), then lance (to kill). Pitchpoling is the killing stroke delivered from a position of already-established connection, but at a distance that makes close-quarters lancing impossible.

The lance's lightness and the warp's retrieval mechanism mean that pitchpoling can be repeated — unlike the harpoon, which once thrown cannot easily be recovered while the whale runs. "Again and again to such gamesome talk, the dexterous dart is repeated, the spear returning to its master like a greyhound held in skilful leash" (084-008). The simile of the greyhound — a hunting dog bred for speed and trained to return to its master — captures the ideal relationship between the pitchpoler and his weapon: the lance is not a disposable projectile but an extension of the thrower's will, obedient and retrievable.

## Stubb's Performance

Stubb, the Pequod's second mate, is the novel's exemplar of pitchpoling. Ishmael introduces him as "a man who from his humorous, deliberate coolness and equanimity in the direst emergencies, was specially qualified to excel in pitchpoling" (084-006). The qualities that make Stubb a great pitchpoler are temperamental as much as physical: the "deliberate coolness" that keeps his hand steady, the "equanimity" that prevents panic, and the "humorous" disposition that transforms deadly work into something approaching play.

The description of Stubb's technique is among the most vividly cinematic passages in *Moby-Dick*:

> Look at him; he stands upright in the tossed bow of the flying boat; wrapt in fleecy foam, the towing whale is forty feet ahead. Handling the long lance lightly, glancing twice or thrice along its length to see if it be exactly straight, Stubb whistlingly gathers up the coil of the warp in one hand, so as to secure its free end in his grasp, leaving the rest unobstructed. Then holding the lance full before his waistband's middle, he levels it at the whale; when, covering him with it, he steadily depresses the butt-end in his hand, thereby elevating the point till the weapon stands fairly balanced upon his palm, fifteen feet in the air.
> (084-006)

Every detail of this description emphasizes control: the light handling, the careful inspection of the lance's straightness, the deliberate gathering of the warp, the steady depression of the butt-end. Stubb "minds you somewhat of a juggler, balancing a long staff on his chin" (084-006) — the comparison to a juggler, like the earlier comparison to a swordsman, frames pitchpoling as performance as well as labor.

The climactic moment is rendered with a single, magnificent sentence: "Next moment with a rapid, nameless impulse, in a superb lofty arch the bright steel spans the foaming distance, and quivers in the life spot of the whale" (084-006). The "rapid, nameless impulse" — too swift and too instinctive to be analyzed — releases the lance into a "superb lofty arch" that bridges the distance between boat and whale. The verb "spans" is architectural, as if the lance were building a bridge; "quivers" renders the moment of impact tactile and immediate.

The result is registered through Stubb's characteristic humor rather than through grim description: "'That drove the spigot out of him!' cried Stubb. 'Tis July's immortal Fourth; all fountains must run wine today!'" (084-007). Stubb transforms the whale's death-spout of blood into a festive fountain, a Fourth of July celebration. This is not callousness but a whaleman's coping mechanism — the conversion of killing into comedy, of blood into wine.

## Pitchpoling in the Economy of Whaling

Pitchpoling is not merely a spectacular technique; it is an economic necessity. A whale that cannot be lanced cannot be killed, and a whale that cannot be killed cannot be rendered into oil. The technique exists because whales do not conveniently stay still to be killed — they flee, and the fleeing whale is a commonplace of the fishery. "Nevertheless, the boats pursued, and Stubb's was foremost. By great exertion, Tashtego at last succeeded in planting one iron; but the stricken whale, without at all sounding, still continued his horizontal flight, with added fleetness" (084-003). The harpoon has been planted — the boat is fast — but the whale's continued flight makes it "imperative to lance the flying whale, or be content to lose him. But to haul the boat up to his flank was impossible, he swam so fast and furious. What then remained?" (084-003). The answer — pitchpoling — is the technique that bridges the gap between fastening and killing, the innovation without which many whales would escape wounded but alive.

The chapter also registers, in passing, the superstitious dimension of whaling practice. Queequeg's ritual anointing of the boat's bottom — "crawling under its bottom, where it hung over the side, and rubbing in the unctuousness as though diligently seeking to insure a crop of hair from the craft's bald keel" (084-001) — is presented as a "presentiment" that proves warranted by the event. The successful pitchpoling that follows is thus framed, however lightly, within a context of magical preparation, the boat made "slippery" not merely for speed but for luck.
