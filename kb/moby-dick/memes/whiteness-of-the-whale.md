# Whiteness of the Whale

## Definition

The "Whiteness of the Whale" is a central thematic concept in Herman Melville's Moby-Dick, explored most extensively in Chapter 42. Through Ishmael's philosophical meditation, Melville examines whiteness as a paradoxical symbol that simultaneously evokes purity and terror, divinity and annihilation, making it the novel's most profound exploration of color symbolism and the sublime.

## Manifestations in the Novel

Whiteness appears throughout Moby-Dick in multiple forms that each carry distinct symbolic weight:

- **Moby Dick himself**: The white sperm whale is the novel's central symbol, embodying an inscrutable, elemental force that defies human comprehension. His whiteness, as Ishmael argues, is the very quality that makes him terrifying rather than merely dangerous.
- **The Albino**: Ishmael notes that albino individuals—whether human or animal—evoke a peculiar unease, citing the "white man" as a source of awe and horror in many cultures.
- **The White Squall**: Melville connects whiteness to natural phenomena that inspire dread through their pallor.
- **The Polar Bear**: The white bear of the Arctic is described as possessing a spectral quality that enhances its fearsomeness.

## Key Passages

- （042-001） Though in many natural objects, whiteness refiningly enhances beauty, as if imparting some special virtue of its own—as in marbles, japonicas, and pearls... there yet lurks an elusive something in the innermost idea of this hue, which strikes more of panic to the soul than that redness which affrights in blood.
- （042-015） It was the whiteness of the whale that above all things appalled me.
- （042-020） Is it that by its indefiniteness it shadows forth the heartless voids and immensities of the universe, and thus stabs us from behind with the thought of annihilation?
- （042-025） And of all these things the Albino whale was the symbol. Wonder ye then at the fiery hunt?

## Scholarly Context

Critical interpretations of Melville's whiteness chapter have ranged from psychoanalytic readings (whiteness as the blank screen onto which Ahab projects his monomania) to post-colonial critiques (whiteness as racial anxiety) to theological interpretations (the "colorless all-color of atheism" as Melville's confrontation with a Godless universe). The chapter is widely regarded as one of the most philosophically ambitious passages in nineteenth-century American literature.
