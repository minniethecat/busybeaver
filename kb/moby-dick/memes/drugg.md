# Drugg

## Definition

A **drugg** (plural: *druggs*) is a specialized harpoon design associated with the Nantucket Indians, in which two thick squares of wood are fitted to the iron shank, acting as drag-anchors or drogues. When a whale is struck with a drugged-harpoon, the wooden blocks create resistance in the water, slowing the whale's escape and tiring it more quickly. Melville documents this Indigenous technology in Chapter 87 ("The Grand Armada") as part of his exhaustive cetological catalogue.

## Manifestations in the Novel

### The Nantucket Indian Design

In Chapter 87, Melville provides the novel's most detailed description of the drugg. The harpoon is distinguished by "two thick squares of wood of equal size" affixed to the iron (087-019). This design makes the harpoon "stout" and heavy — "next to impossible to dart" under normal conditions (087-020). The drugg represents a specialized tool that trades ease of throwing for holding power once embedded in the whale.

### The Grand Armada Context

The drugg appears in the climactic whaling scene of "The Grand Armada," where dozens of whales are being pursued simultaneously by multiple boats. The drugged whales become the focal point of a complex operation: while some boats continue "drugging the whales on the frontier of the armada" (087-029), others are dispatched to "pick up what drugged whales might be dropped astern" (087-034). Melville notes the sobering outcome: "Of all the drugged whales only one was captured" (087-035) — a reminder that even specialized technology cannot guarantee success against the whale.

### The Drugged Whale in Ch.91

In Chapter 91 ("The Pequod Meets the Rose-Bud"), the term reappears when Stubb mockingly refers to the Rose-Bud's crew as having "drugged whales we tickled the other day" (091-003). The drugged whale becomes both a literal marker of prior conflict and a symbol of unfinished business between whaling ships.

## Scholarly Context

The drugg represents an important example of Indigenous technological contribution to the American whaling industry. Nantucket's Wampanoag and other Native American whalemen brought specialized knowledge and tools — including the drugg — that were adopted by the broader whaling fleet. Melville's documentation of the drugg in Chapter 87 is among the most detailed 19th-century written accounts of this technology, which is otherwise sparsely recorded in whaling literature.
