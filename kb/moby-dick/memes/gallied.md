# Gallied

## Definition

"Gallied" is a whaling term that appears in the Grand Armada chapter of *Moby-Dick* to describe a whale that has become frightened and panicked, breaking from orderly formation into erratic, unapproachable movement. When whalemen "perceive it in the whale, they say he is gallied" (087-015). The term belongs to the novel's extensive lexicon of specialized whaling vocabulary — words that Melville not only records but makes into occasions for philosophical reflection. A gallied whale is a creature at the limit of its composure: the hunt has succeeded in terrifying it, but that very terror makes the hunt harder to complete. The gallied state is thus a paradox of the chase: success in frightening the prey makes the prey harder to kill.

## Manifestations in the Novel

**The rout of the Grand Armada (Chapter 87)**. The term appears at a pivotal moment in the Grand Armada chapter, when the ordered columns of sperm whales suddenly dissolve into chaos. The whales exhibit "that strange perplexity of inert irresolution, which, when the fishermen perceive it in the whale, they say he is gallied" (087-015). Their "compact martial columns" break into "one measureless rout," and the whales "seemed going mad with consternation." Melville's description moves from precise cetological observation to grand analogy — the gallied whales are compared to "King Porus' elephants in the Indian battle with Alexander" — and then to an even darker comparison: the panic of whales is no more foolish than "the madness of men." The gallied whale becomes a mirror for human panic, animal fear reflecting human terror.

**The drugg and the gallied harvest (Chapter 87)**. The practical consequence of a gallied state is described immediately after: "It is chiefly among gallied whales that this drugg is used" (087-019). The drugg — a wooden drag attached to a harpoon line — is a technology designed specifically for the gallied condition. When too many whales are panicked at once to be killed immediately, the drugg allows whalemen to "wing them, so that they can be afterwards killed at your leisure." The gallied whale is not freed by its panic; instead, its very disorder creates the conditions for a more efficient, industrial slaughter. The term, in Melville's treatment, carries the full weight of the hunt's violence: to be gallied is to be terrified and to be more efficiently killable.

## Key Passages

- (087-015) — "when the fishermen perceive it in the whale, they say he is gallied": the whaling definition, panic as the breakdown of cetacean order
- (087-019) — "chiefly among gallied whales that this drugg is used": the technology of exploiting panic, the drugg as the instrument of the gallied harvest
