# The Pequod Sets Sail

## Overview

The Pequod's departure from Nantucket occupies Chapter 22, "Merry Christmas." Captain Peleg and Captain Bildad, the Quaker co-owners of the vessel, personally pilot the ship out of harbor. Ahab remains unseen below deck, his absence explained as both convalescence and the custom of merchant captains. The chapter moves from the bustling, comic chaos of getting under weigh — Peleg's profane commands and kicks, Bildad's mournful psalmody — to the final, sober parting at sea, when the pilots depart and the Pequod "blindly plunged like fate into the lone Atlantic" (022-025).

## Chronology

### Getting Under Weigh

After the riggers are dismissed and the ever-thoughtful Charity has delivered a night-cap for Stubb and a spare Bible for the steward, Peleg and Bildad emerge from the cabin to direct the departure. Ahab is said to be in the cabin, "not yet completely recovered" (022-004). Peleg takes command with a "high hand on the quarter-deck" (022-004), issuing a rapid-fire sequence of orders: call all hands, strike the tent, man the capstan (022-002, 022-007, 022-008).

(022-002) — Peleg to Starbuck: "Now, Mr. Starbuck, are you sure everything is right? Captain Ahab is all ready—just spoke to him—nothing more to be got from shore, eh?"

### Peleg and Bildad as Pilots

The two captains divide their labor. Bildad, as a licensed pilot, stations himself at the bow, "actively engaged in looking over the bows for the approaching anchor, and at intervals singing what seemed a dismal stave of psalmody" (022-009). Meanwhile Peleg "ripped and swore astern in the most frightful manner" (022-010), punctuating his commands with kicks — Ishmael receives his "first kick" from Peleg at this moment (022-010).

(022-010) — Ishmael's account: "I almost thought he would sink the ship before the anchor could be got up."

### The Christmas Night

The anchor is raised around noon, and the short Christmas day soon yields to night. The Pequod sails into wintry seas:

> The long rows of teeth on the bulwarks glistened in the moonlight; and like the white ivory tusks of some huge elephant, vast curving icicles depended from the bows. (022-012)

Bildad, heading the first watch, sings his steady hymn: "Sweet fields beyond the swelling flood / Stand dressed in living green" (022-014–017). Ishmael, despite "wet feet and wetter jacket," finds the words full of hope: "there was yet, it then seemed to me, many a pleasant haven in store" (022-018).

### The Pilots Depart

When the Pequod gains sufficient offing, the pilot boat comes alongside. Bildad, deeply invested both financially and emotionally in the ship, lingers painfully — "paced the deck with anxious strides; ran down into the cabin to speak another farewell word there; again came on deck, and looked to windward" (022-020). Peleg takes the parting more philosophically, though "there was a tear twinkling in his eye" (022-021). His final speech calls for a reunion "this day three years" over a hot supper in old Nantucket (022-022).

(022-023) — Bildad's last words: "God bless ye, and have ye in His holy keeping, men ... Don't forget your prayers, either. Mr. Starbuck, mind that cooper don't waste the spare staves."

### Into the Atlantic

The ship and pilot boat diverge:

> Ship and boat diverged; the cold, damp night breeze blew between; a screaming gull flew overhead; the two hulls wildly rolled; we gave three heavy-hearted cheers, and blindly plunged like fate into the lone Atlantic. (022-025)

## Narrative Significance

The Pequod's departure establishes the ship's governing paradox. Two captains — one profane and vigorous (Peleg), one pious and anxious (Bildad) — stand in for the absent Ahab, who is spoken of but never seen. The chapter constructs a membrane of normality and comic bustle around a vessel whose true captain and true purpose are hidden below deck. The violent contrast between Peleg's kicks and Bildad's hymns makes the ship's social order vivid, while the final image — "blindly plunged like fate into the lone Atlantic" — forecloses any hope of a safe return.

The chapter also introduces the novel's persistent motif of invisible forces. Just as Ahab remains unseen, the ship's destiny is already sealed, unknown to the cheerful hands at the windlass.

## Aftermath / Consequences

The Pequod is now at sea, and Ahab will not appear on deck until Chapter 28 — six chapters later. During that interval the ship and its crew are established in his absence, so that when he does emerge, his impact is all the more shocking. The chapter's closing image of the lone Atlantic becomes the permanent backdrop of the novel.
