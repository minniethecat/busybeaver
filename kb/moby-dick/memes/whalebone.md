# Whalebone

## Baleen: The Whale's Filter

In the strict cetological sense, what the whaling industry called "whalebone" is not bone at all but baleen — a keratinous substance (chemically similar to human fingernails and hair) that grows in plates from the upper jaw of baleen whales, or mysticetes. These plates, fringed on the inner edge, form a sieve through which the whale strains seawater, trapping the tiny crustaceans and plankton on which it feeds. Melville describes the Right Whale, the species most valued for its baleen, as yielding "the article commonly known as whalebone or baleen; and the oil specially known as 'whale oil,' an inferior article in commerce" (032-022).

The distinction between baleen whales (mysticetes) and toothed whales (odontocetes) — which includes the Sperm Whale — is one of the fundamental divisions in cetology, and Melville wrestles with its taxonomic implications at length in Chapter 32, "Cetology." The presence or absence of baleen seems like an obvious basis for classification, yet Melville argues that "it is in vain to attempt a clear classification of the Leviathan, founded upon either his baleen, or hump, or fin, or teeth" because these features are "indiscriminately dispersed among all sorts of whales" in "irregular combinations" (032-025). The Humpback has baleen; the Fin-Back has baleen; the Right Whale has baleen — yet they differ in every other particular. "On this rock every one of the whale-naturalists has split" (032-025).

The paradox Melville identifies is genuine: baleen seems like the perfect taxonomic marker, yet it groups together whales that are otherwise profoundly different. The essay on cetology is, among other things, a meditation on the difficulty of classification itself — the way nature resists the categories the human mind insists on imposing.

## The Commerce of Whalebone

Before the development of spring steel and plastics in the late nineteenth century, baleen was a material of enormous commercial importance. Its combination of flexibility, strength, and light weight made it ideal for applications requiring a stiff but springy material: umbrella ribs, corset stays, carriage springs, fishing rods, and the hoops of hoop skirts. The "whalebone" of commerce was processed from the long baleen plates of right whales and bowhead whales, which could reach lengths of ten feet or more.

Melville, characteristically, does not dwell on the mundane commercial details. His interest is in the transformation of the whale's body into usable material — the process by which a living leviathan becomes "article" and "oil." The whale's body is a factory of commodities: spermaceti from the head, oil from the blubber, ambergris from the intestines, and baleen from the mouth. Each part has its market, its price, its place in the global economy that whaling sustains. The whalebone trade connected the Arctic whaling grounds to the fashion houses of Paris and London, the whale's mouth to the corseted waist of a European lady.

The chapter's title, "Cetology," with its scholarly pretensions, masks a brutally commercial reality: the classification of whales is also a price list, a guide to which species are worth killing and which are not. The Right Whale is "the most venerable of the leviathans" not because of any intrinsic dignity but because it yields the most valuable baleen (032-022). The Fin-Back "is not very valuable" despite having baleen because his baleen is inferior and his oil scant (032-027). Cetology, in Melville's hands, is a science conducted at the point of a harpoon.

## The Carpenter's Medium

In the later chapters of the novel, whalebone appears in a different register — not as a commodity but as a craft material, worked by the Pequod's carpenter into objects of use and beauty. The carpenter is introduced in Chapter 107 as a man of "a certain impersonal stolidity," "a stript abstract; an unfractioned integral" who works equally with wood and bone, indifferent to the difference between living tissue and dead material:

> Out of clean shaved rods of right-whale bone, and cross-beams of sperm whale ivory, the carpenter makes a pagoda-looking cage for it.
> (107-004)

The passage is remarkable for its compression: in a single sentence, the carpenter transforms the byproducts of two whale species into an object of exotic beauty, a "pagoda-looking cage" for a stray bird. The whale's body becomes architecture, becomes ornament, becomes cage. The carpenter's indifference to the source of his materials — "Teeth he accounted bits of ivory; heads he deemed but top-blocks; men themselves he lightly held for capstans" (107-005) — is both a feature of his character and a commentary on the whaling industry's reduction of life to raw material.

## Ahab's Bone Leg

The most symbolically charged instance of whalebone in the novel is Ahab's prosthetic leg, carved from the jaw-bone of a sperm whale. The leg first appears indirectly — Ahab's "ivory limb" (028-004), the "bone" on which he stands (028-006) — and receives its fullest treatment in Chapter 108, "Ahab and the Carpenter," where the carpenter is fashioning a new leg to replace the one Ahab damaged in a previous encounter.

The scene is darkly comic. The carpenter, working the bone, sneezes from the bone dust: "This is what an old fellow gets now for working in dead lumber. Saw a live tree, and you don't get this dust; amputate a live bone, and you don't get it" (108-002). The distinction between live bone and dead bone, between the tree and the lumber, collapses in the carpenter's workshop, where everything is material and nothing is sacred. Ahab's leg — the thing that supports him, that allows him to stand and walk and pursue his vengeance — is itself a piece of the enemy, a fragment of whale-body repurposed as prosthesis.

Ahab himself reflects on this with characteristic bitterness: "Here I am, proud as Greek god, and yet standing debtor to this blockhead for a bone to stand on!" (108-034). The whale took his leg; the whale, in the form of a jaw-bone carved into a prosthesis, gives him a new one. His ability to pursue the White Whale depends on the body of another whale — a grim irony that Ahab acknowledges in his meditation on "mortal inter-indebtedness," the universal condition of owing and being owed that he finds intolerable. He would "be free as air," owing nothing to anyone — but his own leg is borrowed bone.

## Scholarly Context

Whalebone has been the subject of considerable attention in material-culture approaches to *Moby-Dick*. Critics have traced the global commodity chains that connected the Arctic whaling grounds to the fashion industries of Europe, reading the novel's treatment of whalebone as an early instance of what would now be called supply-chain consciousness. The whale's body, in this reading, is not merely a symbol but a node in a global economic network, and Melville's exhaustive cataloguing of its commercial products is a form of economic realism.

The prosthetic leg has attracted psychoanalytic and disability-studies readings. Ahab's bone leg is a "phantom limb" in both the literal and the Lacanian sense — a substitute that marks an absence, a presence that testifies to a loss. The leg is also a figure for writing itself: Melville, like the carpenter, works in "dead lumber," transforming the body of the whale into an object — the novel — that will outlast it.
