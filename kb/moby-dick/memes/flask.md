# Flask

## Overview

Flask is the third mate of the [[Pequod]], a native of Tisbury in Martha's Vineyard. Introduced in [[Ch. 27: Knights and Squires]](027-004) as "a short, stout, ruddy young fellow, very pugnacious concerning whales," Flask is defined by his complete lack of reverence for the creatures he hunts. To him, the leviathan is no majestic mystery but "a species of magnified mouse, or at least water-rat" — an attitude that makes him, in Melville's framing, "ignorant" yet "unconsciously fearless." He is nicknamed "King-Post" aboard the Pequod for his stout, square build (027-004). Together with [[Starbuck]] and [[Stubb]], he commands one of the Pequod's three whale-boats, and his harpooneer is [[Daggoo]], the gigantic African.

## Role in the Narrative

Flask represents the unreflective, workmanlike dimension of whaling — the practical, lower-deck perspective that reduces the sublime to the mundane. Unlike [[Starbuck]], who wrestles with moral questions, or [[Stubb]], whose humor contains a latent philosophy, Flask simply kills whales without a second thought. Melville's portrait in Chapter 27 is pointed: "So utterly lost was he to all sense of reverence for the many marvels of their majestic bulk and mystic ways."

In the shipboard hierarchy, Flask occupies the lowest rung among the mates. [[Ch. 34: The Cabin-Table]](034-006) dramatizes this with dark comedy: Flask is "the youngest son, and little boy of this weary family party," the last to eat and the first to rise, perpetually hungry. Melville calls him "a butterless man" — a phrase that encapsulates his deprivation within the rigid etiquette of the officers' mess.

At [[Ch. 36: The Quarter-Deck]], Flask stands alongside Stubb and Starbuck as [[Ahab]] reveals his hunt for [[Moby Dick]]. Ahab's magnetic will overwhelms him as it does the others; the "three mates quailed before his strong, sustained, and mystic aspect" (036-047). He perishes with the Pequod in [[Ch. 135: The Chase — Third Day]].

## Character Traits

Flask's defining trait is **pugnacious irreverence** toward whales. He "somehow seemed to think that the great leviathans had personally and hereditarily affronted him; and therefore it was a sort of point of honor with him, to destroy them whenever encountered" (027-004). This stance is the polar opposite of [[Ahab]]'s: where Ahab invests Moby Dick with cosmic meaning, Flask strips all whales of any meaning whatsoever.

His **physical smallness** is emphasized throughout. He is "Little Flask," "one of the wrought ones; made to clinch tight and last long" (027-004). His nickname "King-Post" — a short, square timber that braces Arctic whalers against icy seas — captures his stout, unglamorous durability.

His **perpetual hunger** at the cabin table becomes a running joke that shades into pathos. As the lowest-ranking officer, he is the last served and the first dismissed: "ever since he had arisen to the dignity of an officer, from that moment he had never known what it was to be otherwise than hungry, more or less" (034-006). The hunger is both literal and symbolic — a mark of his position in the ship's social order.

## Relationships

- **[[Ahab]]**: Flask is wholly subordinate to Ahab's will; unlike Starbuck, he never questions or challenges the captain. He is the most pliable of the three mates.
- **[[Starbuck]]**: Flask's superior in the mate hierarchy; the two are contrasted — Starbuck's moral gravity versus Flask's unthinking pugnacity.
- **[[Stubb]]**: Flask's immediate superior in the mate hierarchy. The two are frequently paired; Stubb is "but a peg higher than Flask" (034-006), and Flask suffers if Stubb finishes his meal quickly.
- **[[Daggoo]]**: Flask's harpooneer, an "imperial negro" of gigantic stature (027-009). The visual contrast between the diminutive Flask and the towering Daggoo is one of Melville's most memorable images — Flask looks "like a chess-man beside him."

## Notable Quotes

(027-004)
