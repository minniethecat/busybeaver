# Starbuck and the Musket

## Overview

The Musket chapter is the moral center of *Moby-Dick*'s second half. In the aftermath of the typhoon, the wind suddenly shifts fair — a "good" omen that Starbuck must report to Ahab. Passing through the cabin, he sees the loaded muskets in their rack. What follows is one of the most psychologically precise depictions of temptation in American literature. Starbuck wrestles with the possibility of killing Ahab to save the entire crew from the doom he believes Ahab is steering them toward. He levels the musket at the cabin door, hears Ahab's tormented sleep-talking, and ultimately returns the weapon to its rack — unable to commit murder even to prevent catastrophe.

## Chronology

### The Fair Wind

The typhoon has abated. Starbuck and Stubb have cleared the wreckage of sails and set new ones. Starbuck goes below to report the change in wind to Ahab, as required by the captain's standing order — "to report immediately, and at any one of the twenty-four hours, any decided change in the affairs of the deck" (123-006).

### The Muskets

Before knocking, Starbuck pauses. The cabin lamp "was burning fitfully, and casting fitful shadows upon the old man's bolted door." The loaded muskets in the rack are "shiningly revealed, as they stood upright against the forward bulkhead" (123-007).

Melville narrates the birth of the temptation with extraordinary precision: "Starbuck was an honest, upright man; but out of Starbuck's heart, at that instant when he saw the muskets, there strangely evolved an evil thought; but so blent with its neutral or good accompaniments that for the instant he hardly knew it for itself" (123-007).

### The Soliloquy

Starbuck's interior monologue — one of the longest in the novel — is a masterwork of moral reasoning under pressure. He picks up the very musket Ahab once pointed at him:

> "He would have shot me once," he murmured, "yes, there's the very musket that he pointed at me;—that one with the studded stock; let me touch it — lift it. Strange, that I, who have handled so many deadly lances, strange, that I should shake so now." (123-008)

He inventories Ahab's transgressions: dashing the quadrant, navigating by dead reckoning in perilous seas, refusing lightning-rods during the typhoon. The logic is relentless:

> "But shall this crazed old man be tamely suffered to drag a whole ship's company down to doom with him? — Yes, it would make him the wilful murderer of thirty men and more, if this ship come to any deadly harm; and come to deadly harm, my soul swears this ship will, if Ahab have his way. If, then, he were this instant — put aside, that crime would not be his." (123-008)

He considers — and rejects — alternatives: imprisoning Ahab ("only a fool would try it"), reasoning with him ("not reasoning; not remonstrance; not entreaty wilt thou hearken to"), taking him home by force. The geography of morality closes in: "The land is hundreds of leagues away, and locked Japan the nearest. I stand alone here upon an open sea, with two oceans and a whole continent between me and law" (123-008).

### The Crisis

Starbuck levels the musket against the cabin door:

> "On this level, Ahab's hammock swings within; his head this way. A touch, and Starbuck may survive to hug his wife and child again. — Oh Mary! — But if I wake thee not to death, old man, who can tell to what unsounded deeps Starbuck's body this day week may sink, with all the crew! Great God, where art Thou?" (123-009)

At the last moment, Starbuck cannot do it. Instead, he speaks his original errand: "The wind has gone down and shifted, sir; the fore and main topsails are reefed and set; she heads her course" (123-009).

### Ahab's Dream-Voice

From within the cabin comes Ahab's sleeping voice: "Oh Moby Dick, I clutch thy heart at last!" (123-010). The words seem a direct response to Starbuck's crisis — as if Ahab's monomania has been speaking in his dreams all along, and Starbuck's voice has "caused the long dumb dream to speak" (123-011).

The musket "shook like a drunkard's arm against the panel; Starbuck seemed wrestling with an angel; but turning from the door, he placed the death-tube in its rack, and left the place" (123-012).

### The Cover

Starbuck returns to the deck and tells Stubb to go wake Ahab instead: "He's too sound asleep, Mr. Stubb; go thou down, and wake him, and tell him. I must see to the deck here. Thou know'st what to say" (123-013). The deflection is his final retreat from the temptation — he cannot face Ahab after what he almost did.

## Narrative Significance

The Musket chapter is the novel's most sustained meditation on the ethics of preventive violence. Starbuck's reasoning is impeccable — Ahab *is* leading the crew to destruction, and killing him would save thirty lives — but Melville refuses to let the logic resolve into action. The chapter dramatizes the gap between rational moral calculation and the actual capacity to kill.

The chapter also completes the Starbuck-Ahab relationship. In "The Quarter-Deck" (Ch.36), Starbuck objected to Ahab's quest on principle; here, he comes closer than anyone to stopping it by force. His failure is not cowardice — it is the failure of a good man to do an evil thing for a good reason.

## Aftermath / Consequences

Starbuck's decision to spare Ahab seals the crew's fate. From this point forward, no one will challenge Ahab's command. The moral authority Starbuck might have claimed is forfeit — he has looked into the abyss of what he might do and recoiled. The Pequod sails on toward its destruction with its last potential check removed.

## Critical Analysis

The chapter's biblical echoes are unmistakable. Starbuck "wrestling with an angel" recalls Jacob at Peniel (Genesis 32). The loaded musket is the forbidden fruit of the knowledge of good and evil — the thing that, once touched, changes everything. Starbuck's question "Great God, where art Thou?" echoes Christ on the cross.

But Melville inverts the biblical pattern. Jacob's wrestling *wins* him a blessing. Starbuck's wrestling *loses* him the chance to act. The angel he wrestles is not God but his own conscience, and the blessing he receives is not a new name but the knowledge of his own limits.

The chapter's psychological realism is remarkable. The "evil thought" that "strangely evolved" from Starbuck's heart is not presented as demonic possession but as the natural product of a rational man facing an impossible situation. Melville traces the thought's progress from perception (the muskets) to reasoning (the inventory of Ahab's crimes) to resolution (the leveled weapon) to collapse (the return to the rack) with the precision of a case study.
