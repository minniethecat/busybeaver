# Sulphur Bottom Whale

## Classification

The Sulphur Bottom closes Ishmael's Folio register — Book I, Chapter VI, the last and most mysterious of the great whales (032-020). It follows the [[sperm-whale|Sperm Whale]], [[right-whale|Right Whale]], [[fin-back-whale|Fin-Back]], [[hump-backed-whale|Hump-backed]], and [[razor-back-whale|Razor Back]]. Modern taxonomy identifies it as the Blue Whale (*Balaenoptera musculus*), the largest animal ever known to have existed on Earth.

## In Cetology (Chapter 32)

Ishmael's portrait (032-029) is the briefest of the Folio entries, yet it carries a distinct tone of rueful admiration. The Sulphur Bottom is:

> Another retiring gentleman, with a brimstone belly, doubtless got by scraping along the Tartarian tiles in some of his profounder divings. He is seldom seen; at least I have never seen him except in the remoter southern seas, and then always at too great a distance to study his countenance. (032-029)

The "Tartarian tiles" image is characteristic Ishmael — a geological metaphor for a biological fact, the whale's belly colored by its passage through the deep. The animal is never chased; "he would run away with rope-walks of line." And yet: "Prodigies are told of him."

The chapter ends with a valediction: "Adieu, Sulphur Bottom! I can say nothing more that is true of ye, nor can the oldest Nantucketer" (032-029). It is a farewell and an admission — the Sulphur Bottom resists cetology, resists capture, resists even description.

## Appearances in the Novel

Like the Razor Back, the Sulphur Bottom inhabits Chapter 32 alone. It never enters the Pequod's narrative, never appears on the horizon, never tests a harpoon. It is a creature of pure taxonomy — catalogued and released. This textual absence is, paradoxically, the most accurate thing Melville could say about the Blue Whale: in the 19th century, it was indeed almost never encountered by whalemen.

## Key Passages

- (032-020) — Listed as the sixth and final Folio whale
- (032-029) — Full description: retiring gentleman, brimstone belly, Tartarian tiles, seldom seen, never chased, "prodigies are told of him," farewell

## Whaling Context

The Blue Whale was effectively immune to 19th-century whaling. Its speed — capable of outrunning any whaleboat — and its tendency to sink when killed made it commercially irrelevant. Only with the advent of explosive harpoons and factory ships in the 20th century did the Blue Whale become a target, leading to its near-extinction. Melville's "Adieu" was, in this light, a blessing the 20th century revoked.

The "brimstone belly" that Ishmael attributes to the sea floor may refer to the yellowish sheen of diatoms that sometimes coats blue whales' undersides — a phenomenon known to modern marine biologists.

## Symbolic Meaning

In Melville's taxonomy, the Sulphur Bottom is the whale of the impenetrable distance. It occupies the last slot of the Folio — the great whales, the Leviathans — but occupies it by near-absence. The "brimstone belly" colors it with infernal associations, yet the tone is fond, not fearsome. The Sulphur Bottom, more than any other Folio whale, represents the limit of human knowledge: Ishmael can name it, classify it, and wave goodbye to it, but he cannot possess it.
