# Shroud

## Definition

No word in *Moby-Dick* carries a more concentrated double meaning than *shroud*. In maritime usage, shrouds are the standing rigging ropes that run from the masthead to the ship's sides — what sailors climb, what they grasp for support, the web of cordage that holds the mast upright. In funerary usage, a shroud is the cloth that wraps the dead for burial. Melville activates both meanings simultaneously and relentlessly: every grip on a shroud is a grip on one's own winding-sheet, and every shroud-as-rope is a reminder that at sea, the distance between working and dying is the width of a handhold. The white whale itself is described in shroud imagery — "the monumental white shroud that wraps all the prospect around" (042-031) — making the entire visible world a death-garment. When the *Pequod* sinks, "the great shroud of the sea rolled on as it rolled five thousand years ago" (135-062), collapsing the novel's final image into the word that has been accumulating meaning since the opening chapters.

## Manifestations in the Novel

**Jonah's lookout and the sinner's shroud (Chapter 9)**. Father Mapple's sermon introduces shroud in its maritime sense, but bathed in theological implication. Jonah, "stumbling to the deck, grasps a shroud, to look out upon the sea" (009-032) — the shroud here is a rope, but the "tormented deep" he gazes into will become his burial shroud in the whale's belly. Mapple's sermon makes the shroud-as-rope a threshold: the sinner clings to it, looking out at the abyss that will receive him unless he repents.

**"Sleep between shrouds" — the crew's initiation (Chapter 29)**. When Ahab first emerges on deck, Stubb's casual remark carries the novel's characteristic dark humor: "there such as ye sleep between shrouds, to use ye to the filling one at last" (029-004). The crew sleeps among the rigging shrouds; death will fill the winding-sheet shroud. The shroud here is a training device — the living shroud acclimates the sailor to the dead one. Melville's compression of meanings in a single sentence is almost Shakespearean.

**Ahab gripping the shroud (Chapters 36, 51)**. Ahab's posture on deck is repeatedly described through his grip on the shrouds. On the quarter-deck: "with one hand grasping a shroud, he ordered Starbuck to send everybody aft" (036-007). In the spirit-spout chapter: "with one hand firmly grasping a shroud, Ahab for hours and hours would stand" (051-010) gazing at the sea. The shroud-as-rope becomes part of Ahab's iconography — the captain who cannot let go, who is in fact already gripping his own death. Grasping the shroud: the posture of command and the posture of the dying man fused into one.

**The white shroud of the whale (Chapter 42)**. "The Whiteness of the Whale" gives shroud its most terrifying expansion. Whiteness "is at once the most meaning symbol of spiritual things, nay, the very veil of the Christian's Deity; and yet... the intensifying agent in things the most appalling to mankind" (042-015). And then: "the expressive hue of the shroud in which we wrap them" — the dead are wrapped in white. The white whale is "the shrouded phantom of the whitened waters" (042-023); the polar bear is "the white-shrouded bear" (042-004); and the sea itself becomes "the monumental white shroud that wraps all the prospect around" (042-031). In this chapter, shroud loses its specificity and becomes a universal category: whiteness is the shroud of existence itself.

**The silk-worm and the self-spun shroud (Chapter 127)**. Ahab's late meditation to the carpenter produces one of the novel's most compressed images: "Dost thou spin thy own shroud out of thyself? Look at thy bosom! A leak, and not a cavity in thy bones to stop it!" (127-020). The shroud here is simultaneously Ahab's body (leaking, doomed), his fate (self-woven), and his death-garment (already in production). The image of spinning one's own shroud collapses agency and fatality into a single figure: Ahab is the worm spinning the silk of his own destruction.

**The great shroud of the sea (Chapter 135)**. The novel's final sentence: "all collapsed, and the great shroud of the sea rolled on as it rolled five thousand years ago" (135-062). The Pequod sinks; Ishmael alone survives; and the shroud of the sea — death's garment, the winding-sheet of the entire voyage — closes over everything. The sea was always a shroud; the novel has been teaching us to see it that way. Now it is all that remains.

**Starbuck at the shrouds (Chapter 119)**. During the corposant scene, "Holding by a shroud, Starbuck was standing on the quarter-deck" (119-003), watching Ahab's defiance of the lightning. The shroud here is physical support, but the context — Ahab's blasphemous address to the "clear spirit of clear fire" — charges it with mortal implication. Starbuck holds the shroud while watching Ahab refuse to hold anything.

## Scholarly Context

The shroud-as-double-meaning has been a touchstone for critics interested in Melville's linguistic compression. The pun on "shroud" (rope/winding-sheet) exemplifies what Geoffrey Hartman called Melville's "hermeneutic of the pun" — the way single words carry incompatible meanings whose collision generates meaning. The whiteness chapter's shroud imagery has been central to readings of race in the novel (Toni Morrison's influential argument that whiteness in *Moby-Dick* is "the shroud of meaning itself"). Ecocritical readers have noted the shroud's function in collapsing the distance between human industry (rigging) and natural fatality (the sea that swallows the ship). The self-spun shroud of Chapter 127 has been read as Melville's figure for the novel's own narrative procedure: a story that weaves its own ending from its beginning, a shroud spun from the first page.
