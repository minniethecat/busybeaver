# Corpusants

## Definition

Corpusants (also spelled "corposants," from the Portuguese *corpo santo*, "holy body") are the maritime name for St. Elmo's fire — an electrical weather phenomenon in which luminous plasma appears at the tips of ships' masts and yardarms during thunderstorms. In *Moby-Dick*, the corpusants appear during the typhoon in Chapter 119 ("The Candles"), transforming the Pequod into a scene of supernatural illumination and providing the occasion for Ahab's most direct and defiant confrontation with the divine. The pale flames that "silently burn" on the three mastheads become both an omen and a theatrical stage, against which Ahab delivers his great speech to the "clear spirit of clear fire."

## Manifestations in the Novel

The corpusants manifest at the climax of the typhoon sequence in Chapter 119. The Pequod has been struck by a typhoon in the Japanese seas, stripped of her canvas and left vulnerable to the storm. Starbuck, seeing the lightning as a warning against Ahab's quest, urges the crew to drop the lightning rods overboard. Ahab refuses: "let's have fair play here, though we be the weaker side" (119-029).

It is then that the corpusants appear. Starbuck cries out: "The corpusants! the corpusants!" (119-030). Melville's description is one of the novel's most visually stunning passages: "All the yard-arms were tipped with a pallid fire; and touched at each tri-pointed lightning-rod-end with three tapering white flames, each of the three tall masts was silently burning in that sulphurous air, like three gigantic wax tapers before an altar" (119-031).

The crew's reaction reveals the corpusants' supernatural charge. Stubb, who moments before had been singing irreverent storm-shanties, now shifts his tone entirely: "The corpusants have mercy on us all!" (119-032). Even the hardened whalemen are struck silent: "While this pallidness was burning aloft, few words were heard from the enchanted crew" (119-034). The corpusants transform the ship into a sacred — or sacrilegious — space, with Daggoo looming "like the black cloud from which the thunder had come" and Queequeg's tattoos burning "like Satanic blue flames on his body" (119-034).

The corpusants' most significant function is as the occasion for Ahab's address to the fire. Standing before the "lofty tri-pointed trinity of flames" (119-041), Ahab delivers his great declaration of defiance: "Oh! thou clear spirit of clear fire, whom on these seas I as Persian once did worship... I now know that thy right worship is defiance" (119-042). The speech reaches its climax as lightning flashes and the flames leap higher, with Ahab refusing to avert his eyes: "Thou canst blind; but I can then grope. Thou canst consume; but I can then be ashes" (119-044). His final act — extinguishing the flame on his own harpoon with a blast of his breath (119-048) — is a gesture of Promethean defiance that seals the crew's submission to his will.

## Scholarly Context

The corpusants chapter has been extensively analyzed as the theological and dramatic center of *Moby-Dick*. Critics have noted the Zoroastrian imagery — Ahab's references to "Persian" fire-worship, the Parsee Fedallah kneeling at his feet — and the chapter's structural position as a dark inversion of religious ritual. The three masthead flames form a "trinity" that Ahab both worships and defies, positioning him as a kind of anti-Moses on a new Sinai.

The chapter's title, "The Candles," reinforces the domestic-sacred inversion: the whaling ship, whose entire purpose is to produce oil for lamps and candles, is itself lit by uncanny flame. Ahab's extinguishing of the harpoon-flame at the chapter's end has been read as a symbolic act of self-destruction — the captain literally "blows out" the light that guides him, sealing the Pequod's fate in darkness. The corpusants thus embody the novel's central paradox: the sacred manifests only to be defied, and illumination leads not to salvation but to destruction.
