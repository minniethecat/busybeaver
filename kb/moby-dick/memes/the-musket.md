# The Musket

## Definition

The musket is the instrument of Starbuck's moral crisis in Chapter 123 ("The Musket"). Finding Ahab asleep during a typhoon, the first mate sees the loaded muskets in their rack and is seized by the thought that killing Ahab would save the entire crew from his monomaniacal quest. The chapter stages the novel's most intense ethical dilemma: is killing one man to save thirty an act of murder or salvation? Starbuck's internal debate — which ends with him returning the musket to its rack — defines the moral limit that separates him from Ahab.

## Manifestations in the Novel

The scene unfolds in the aftermath of the typhoon (Chapters 119–122). The storm has damaged the ship, shattered the compass needles, and intensified the crew's dread. Starbuck goes below to report a favorable wind to Ahab and pauses before the captain's door. "The cabin lamp — taking long swings this way and that — was burning fitfully, and casting fitful shadows upon the old man's bolted door... The loaded muskets in the rack were shiningly revealed, as they stood upright against the forward bulkhead" (123-007). Melville marks this as the precise instant of temptation: "out of Starbuck's heart, at that instant when he saw the muskets, there strangely evolved an evil thought; but so blent with its neutral or good accompaniments that for the instant he hardly knew it for itself."

What follows is an extraordinary soliloquy in which Starbuck handles the very musket Ahab had once pointed at him. He reasons through every angle: Ahab "would fain kill all his crew," has "dashed his heavenly quadrant," has sworn "he would have no lightning-rods" (123-008). The conclusion seems unavoidable: "shall this crazed old man be tamely suffered to drag a whole ship's company down to doom with him? — Yes, it would make him the wilful murderer of thirty men and more." And yet: "If, then, he were this instant — put aside, that crime would not be his."

But Starbuck cannot act. He considers imprisoning Ahab — impossible, "only a fool would try it." He considers the law — "The land is hundreds of leagues away, and locked Japan the nearest. I stand alone here upon an open sea, with two oceans and a whole continent between me and law" (123-008). At the chapter's climax, "slowly, and stealthily, and half sideways looking, he placed the loaded musket's end against the door" (123-008), aiming through the panel at Ahab's sleeping head. In that suspended moment, Ahab cries out in his sleep — "Stern all! Oh Moby Dick, I clutch thy heart at last!" (123-010) — and Starbuck withdraws. "The yet levelled musket shook like a drunkard's arm against the panel; Starbuck seemed wrestling with an angel; but turning from the door, he placed the death-tube in its rack, and left the place" (123-012).

## Scholarly Context

The Musket chapter is one of the most analyzed scenes in Moby-Dick for its ethical structure. Critics note that Starbuck's reasoning follows the logic of utilitarian ethics — kill one to save many — but his inability to act affirms a deontological prohibition against murder, even for ostensibly good ends. The scene inverts the Abraham-and-Isaac paradigm: where Abraham was willing to sacrifice his son at God's command, Starbuck refuses to sacrifice his captain despite every rational argument. The loaded musket, like the doubloon, becomes a test object that reveals the moral character of the one who handles it.
