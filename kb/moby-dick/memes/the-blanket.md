# The Blanket

## Definition

In the vocabulary of nineteenth-century whalemen, "the blanket" (or "blanket-piece") refers to the long outer strip of blubber peeled from a sperm whale's body during the cutting-in process — analogous, both in shape and in function, to the blankets that cover a human sleeper. Chapter 68 of *Moby-Dick*, titled "The Blanket," investigates this layer of blubber as both a biological structure and a philosophical figure. Melville's Ishmael argues, against received opinion, that the blubber *is* the whale's skin — a skin of remarkable thickness and insulating power, whose function is not merely thermal but existential: it protects the whale's warm interior from the "shock" of the cold, hostile ocean.

## Manifestations in the Novel

The chapter opens with Ishmael's characteristic blend of pedantry and profundity: "I have given no small attention to that not unvexed subject, the skin of the whale. I have had controversies about it with experienced whalemen afloat, and learned naturalists ashore" (068-001). His thesis is heterodox: "Assuming the blubber to be the skin of the whale; then, when this skin, as in the case of a very large Sperm Whale, will yield the bulk of one hundred barrels of oil; and, when it is considered that, in quantity, or rather weight, that oil, in its expressed state, is only three fourths, and not the entire substance of the blubber — it therefore follows that a Sperm Whale of the largest magnitude, between the eighty-fifth and the ninetieth parallel of latitude, will yield some fifteen or twenty barrels of sperm oil" (068-004). The calculation of yield is rendered in the same breath as anatomical speculation — whaling economics and natural philosophy are inseparable in Melville's vision.

But the chapter's real argument is metaphorical. Ishmael draws an explicit parallel between the whale's blanket of blubber and the human need for insulation: "It does seem to me, that herein we see the rare virtue of a strong individual vitality, and the rare virtue of thick walls, and the rare virtue of interior spaciousness. Oh, man! admire and model thyself after the whale!" (068-007). The whale, wrapped in its foot-thick layer of oily protection, becomes a model for human self-preservation: "do thou, too, remain warm among ice. Do thou, too, live in this world without being of it" (068-007).

The blanket has a practical dimension as well. In Chapter 67, "Cutting In," Melville describes how "the long upper strip, called a blanket-piece, swings clear, and is all ready for lowering" (067-003). The blanket-pieces are hoisted aboard and stored in the blubber-room, where they await rendering in the try-works. The term thus spans the novel's material and symbolic registers: blanket as whale anatomy, as whaling commodity, and as existential metaphor.

## Key Passages

> "I have given no small attention to that not unvexed subject, the skin of the whale. I have had controversies about it with experienced whalemen afloat, and learned naturalists ashore. My original opinion still remains, that the skin of the whale is the blubber."
> (068-001)

> "It is stript from him in long pieces, called blanket-pieces. Like most sea-terms, this one is very happy and significant. For the whale is indeed wrapt up in his blubber as in a real blanket or counterpane."
> (068-006)

> "It does seem to me, that herein we see the rare virtue of a strong individual vitality, and the rare virtue of thick walls, and the rare virtue of interior spaciousness. Oh, man! admire and model thyself after the whale! Do thou, too, remain warm among ice. Do thou, too, live in this world without being of it."
> (068-007)

> "In life, the visible surface of the Sperm Whale is not the least among the many marvels he presents. Almost invariably it is all over obliquely crossed and re-crossed with numberless straight marks in thick array, something like those in the finest Italian line engravings."
> (068-005)
