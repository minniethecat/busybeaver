# The Right Whale's Head

## Definition

"The Right Whale's Head — Contrasted View" (Chapter 75) completes Melville's comparative anatomy lesson begun in Chapter 74. Where the sperm whale's head represents Platonic nobility and speculative transcendence, the right whale's head embodies Stoic practicality and "enormous practical resolution in facing death" (075-011). The chapter inventories the right whale's distinctive features — baleen, the enormous lower lip, the tongue, the double spout-holes — and in doing so, constructs a philosophical typology of two ways of being in the world.

## Manifestations in the Novel

The chapter opens with a direct invitation: "Crossing the deck, let us now have a good long look at the Right Whale's head" (075-001). Ishmael walks us from one side of the Pequod to the other, from the sperm whale's head to the right whale's.

The right whale's head is immediately characterized by its inelegance. Melville compares it to "a gigantic galliot-toed shoe" and notes that "two hundred years ago an old Dutch voyager likened its shape to that of a shoemaker's last" (075-002). Yet as one draws closer, the head reveals different aspects from different angles. From above, the two F-shaped spoutholes make the head resemble "an enormous bass-viol" (075-003). The barnacled encrustation on top — called the "crown" or "bonnet" by whalemen — suggests a diademed king of the sea.

The most striking feature is the enormous lower lip: "a sulk and pout, by carpenter's measurement, about twenty feet long and five feet deep; a sulk and pout that will yield you some 500 gallons of oil and more" (075-003). This pout gives the right whale's head its expression of Stoic resolution.

Entering the mouth, Ishmael encounters the baleen — "those wondrous, half vertical, scimetar-shaped slats of whalebone, say three hundred on a side" (075-004). These "Venetian blinds" are the right whale's filtering apparatus, through which it strains the sea for brit. The baleen plates bear marks by which "some whalemen calculate the creature's age, as the age of an oak by its circular rings" (075-004).

The tongue is "very fat and tender" — a "rug of the softest Turkey" — and Melville estimates it at "a six-barreler" of oil (075-008). The interior of the mouth, with its colonnades of baleen, suggests "the great Haarlem organ" with "its thousand pipes" (075-008).

The chapter concludes with Melville's summation of the differences: the right whale has no sperm well, no ivory teeth, no long slender lower jaw; instead, it has baleen blinds, the huge lower lip, and a tongue. These anatomical differences are then transposed into philosophical types: the right whale is "a Stoic," the sperm whale "a Platonian" (075-011).

## Scholarly Context

The right whale's head has been read as Melville's figure for the material, practical, and unillusioned approach to existence — the Stoic counterpart to the sperm whale's Platonism. Critics have noted that Melville gives the right whale the chapter's final, most arresting lines about "enormous practical resolution in facing death," suggesting a quiet admiration for the Stoic position even as the novel's sympathies seem aligned with the sperm whale's speculative reach. The baleen — the filtering apparatus — has been read as a figure for hermeneutic practice itself: the right whale strains meaning from the sea of experience, retaining what nourishes and expelling the rest. The "crown" or "bonnet" has been interpreted as Melville's ironic commentary on sovereignty and kingship in a democratic age.
