# Instinct

## Definition

Instinct in *Moby-Dick* is not merely animal impulse. Melville constructs a ladder of instinct that runs from the blind, brute reflex of a wounded whale to what he calls — in one of the novel's most daring metaphysical formulations — the "secret intelligence from the Deity" (044-019) that guides sperm whales on their migrations. Between these poles, instinct operates in the novel as a bridge concept: it links the animal to the human, the unconscious to the conscious, the demonic to the divine. Ishmael follows instinct through the streets of New Bedford (002-004); Ahab is accused of acting from "blindest instinct" (036-039); the whale is said to possess "the instinct of the knowledge of the demonism in the world" (042-027). To read instinct through the novel is to trace Melville's epistemology of the body — his conviction that the deepest knowledge is not reasoned but felt, not learned but inherited, and that what we call reason may be only instinct's after-the-fact justification.

## Manifestations in the Novel

**Instinct as guide: Ishmael's entry (Chapter 2)**. On his first night in New Bedford, having rejected the expensive inns, Ishmael "now by instinct followed the streets that took me waterward, for there, doubtless, were the cheapest, if not the cheeriest inns" (002-004). Instinct here is practical wisdom — a bodily knowledge that bypasses calculation. It is the first of many moments in which Melville suggests that the body knows where it needs to go before the mind does.

**Blind instinct vs. reason: Ahab and Starbuck (Chapter 36)**. When Starbuck protests Ahab's monomaniacal pursuit, he frames the quarrel as one of instinct against reason: "Vengeance on a dumb brute! that simply smote thee from blindest instinct! Madness! To be enraged with a dumb thing, Captain Ahab, seems blasphemous" (036-039). Starbuck's taxonomy is precise: the whale's act was instinct — mindless, without malice — and to treat it as intentional is itself a form of madness. Ahab's response, "Talk not to me of blasphemy, man; I'd strike the sun if it insulted me" (036-040), rejects the very distinction Starbuck insists upon: for Ahab, instinct, intention, and cosmic insult are inseparable.

**The demonic instinct (Chapter 42)**. In his meditation on whiteness, Melville makes instinct a vehicle for the uncanny: "here thou beholdest even in a dumb brute, the instinct of the knowledge of the demonism in the world" (042-027). The white horse or white whale terrifies not because it attacks but because it *knows* — through instinct — something about the void behind appearances. Instinct becomes a mode of perception that outstrips conscious understanding: the animal senses what the philosopher can only theorize.

**Secret intelligence from the Deity (Chapter 44)**. Melville's most vertiginous reframing of instinct comes in his description of sperm whale migration. The whales move "guided by some infallible instinct — say, rather, secret intelligence from the Deity — mostly swim in *veins*" with a precision that "no ship ever sailed her course, by any chart, with one tithe of such marvellous precision" (044-019). The dash between "instinct" and "secret intelligence" is the pivot of Melville's revision: instinct is not below reason but above it, a direct channel to divine navigation that human charts cannot replicate.

**Instinctive solidarity (Chapters 51, 54, 81)**. Instinct in the novel also operates as a social force. When Fedallah spots the moonlit spirit-spout, "almost every soul on board instinctively desired a lowering" (051-002) — the crew is united by a pre-rational pull toward the whale. In the Town-Ho story, the "instinctive love of neatness in seamen; some of whom would not willingly drown without first washing their faces" (054-018) elevates a mundane custom to a near-ritual. And when the three harpooneers — Queequeg, Tashtego, Daggoo — see their chance at a whale, they "instinctively sprang to their feet, and standing in a diagonal row, simultaneously pointed their barbs" (081-027). Their coordination requires no planning; instinct synchronizes them as if they were a single organism.

**Whale instinct (Chapters 58, 87)**. Melville repeatedly asks whether the whale's vast bulk can house consciousness. Watching a pod from the mast-head, he wonders how "such bulky masses of overgrowth can possibly be instinct, in all parts, with the same sort of life that lives in a dog or a horse" (058-005). Yet when the whales navigate the ocean veins with "wonderful instinct" (087-014), the question recedes — the whale's behavior demands a category beyond either blank mechanism or human-like consciousness. The great squid, by contrast, shows "neither sensation nor instinct" (059-006), a pure blank that makes the whale's instinct look warm by comparison.

**Instinct as character: the carpenter (Chapter 107)**. The Pequod's carpenter, for all his skill, is described as working "not so much by reason or by instinct, or simply because he had been so long a carpenter" (107-005). Ishmael cannot decide which faculty drives him — and concludes it may not matter. The carpenter's stolid indifference to meaning, his reduction of "teeth" to "ivory" and "heads" to "top-blocks," represents instinct drained of both the demonic and the divine: a mere habit, a living without reflection. He is the novel's anti-Ahab: where Ahab's instinct has become cosmic rage, the carpenter's has become pure function.

## Scholarly Context

Melville's treatment of instinct engages a debate central to nineteenth-century thought. Darwin's *Origin of Species* (1859) would recast instinct as evolutionary inheritance, while Emerson's essays celebrated intuition as the organ of transcendental insight. Melville, writing in 1851, anticipates both conversations while fitting neatly into neither. His instinct is too cosmic for Darwin — the whale's "secret intelligence from the Deity" is not natural selection — and too dark for Emerson — instinct in *Moby-Dick* leads as often to demonism and destruction as to transcendence. More recent cognitive-evolutionary critics have read Melville's instinct psychology alongside theories of embodied cognition, while psychoanalytic readers have linked Ahab's "blindest instinct" to the death drive. The novel's instinct philosophy resists systematization: it is a force Melville maps rather than defines, a spectrum rather than a concept.
