# The Castaway

## Definition

The Castaway is Chapter 93 of *Moby-Dick*, in which [[pip|Pip]], the Pequod's black cabin boy, is abandoned in the open ocean after jumping from a whale boat in terror. Left to drift alone under the "awful lonesomeness" of the empty sea, Pip is driven mad by the experience. When rescued, he is no longer the fearful boy but a prophetic figure — "the ship's eccentric," whose madness permits him to speak truths others cannot.

> By the merest chance the ship itself at last rescued him; but from that hour the little negro went about the deck an idiot; such, at least, they said he was. (093-011)

## Manifestations in the Novel

### Pip Before the Fall

Pip is introduced as the Pequod's cabin boy — cheerful, musical (he plays the tambourine), and notably gentle. Ishmael describes him as "the most insignificant of the Pequod's crew" (093-001), a judgment the chapter will overturn. Pip's very insignificance makes his transformation more devastating: what happens to him could happen to anyone in the fishery.

### The First Jump

Pip's first lowering with Stubb goes poorly. Frightened by the whale, Pip jumps from the boat and becomes entangled in the whale line:

> Tashtego stood in the bows. He was full of the fire of the hunt. He hated Pip for a poltroon. Snatching the boat-knife from its sheath, he cut the line — and so the whale was lost and Pip was saved. (093-007)

Stubb warns Pip never to jump again: "a whale would sell for thirty times what you would, Pip, in Alabama" (093-008). The threat is economic but carries the weight of racial violence — Pip's body priced in the language of the slave market.

### The Second Jump

Pip jumps again. This time, by a terrible arithmetic of whaling logic, Stubb makes the choice to leave him:

> But we are all in the hands of the Gods; and Pip jumped again. It was under very similar circumstances to the first performance; but this time he did not so much incite Stubb's wrath as his indifference. (093-010)

Stubb's reasoning is brutally practical: to turn the boat for Pip would mean losing the whale. The whale is worth more.

### The Madness of the Sea

What drives Pip mad is not drowning but solitude:

> The sea had jeeringly kept his finite body up, but drowned the infinite of his soul. Not drowned entirely, though. Rather carried down alive to wondrous depths, where strange shapes of the unwarped primal world glided to and fro before his passive eyes. (093-011)

This passage is among Melville's most startling: Pip's soul descends into the cosmic while his body floats. He sees "God's foot upon the treadle of the loom" — the machinery of creation itself.

### The Transformed Pip

After his rescue, Pip becomes the ship's holy fool. His madness grants him a peculiar intimacy with [[ahab|Ahab]], who recognizes in the boy's condition a reflection of his own brokenness. In a later chapter, Ahab takes Pip into his cabin: "There is that in thee, poor lad, which I feel too curing to my malady" (125-008). The captain and the cabin boy — the novel's highest and lowest — become mirror images of each other's castaway souls.

## Key Passages

- (093-001) — Pip introduced as "the most insignificant of the Pequod's crew."
- (093-005) — The first lowering: Pip's nervousness exposed.
- (093-007) — Tashtego cuts the line: whale lost, Pip saved, Stubb's warning delivered.
- (093-008) — "A whale would sell for thirty times what you would, Pip, in Alabama."
- (093-010) — The second jump: Stubb's indifference seals Pip's fate.
- (093-011) — The sea drowns Pip's soul but keeps his body — the novel's most compressed account of madness.
- (093-012) — Stubb's self-justification and the ship's belated rescue.
- (093-014) — The narrator's verdict: "blame not Stubb too hardly. The thing is common in that fishery."

## Scholarly Context

The Castaway is one of the most analyzed chapters in *Moby-Dick*. Key critical approaches include racial readings (Pip as the novel's most explicit engagement with antebellum American slavery, his body priced by Stubb in the language of the slave market), prophetic readings (Pip as Shakespearean fool — the madman who sees truth, his descent granting access to cosmic vision), and doubling readings (Pip mirrors Ahab — both are "castaways" from normal human community, both have been broken by the whale fishery).
