# Revenge

## Definition

Revenge in *Moby-Dick* operates on at least two levels. On the surface, it is Ahab's motive for hunting the White Whale — the "monomaniac revenge" (041-024) that drives the plot. But Melville complicates revenge by giving it a double: in Chapter 54, "The Town-Ho's Story," Steelkilt pursues his own "proper and private revenge" (054-069) against the mate Radney, paralleling Ahab's cosmic grievance with a shipboard drama of personal vengeance.

The novel thus distinguishes between private revenge (Steelkilt's personal grudge), monomaniac revenge (Ahab's all-consuming obsession), and supernatural revenge (the attribution of cosmic agency to the whale). Through this layering, Melville asks whether revenge is a human action or a cosmic principle — and whether the distinction matters.

## Manifestations in the Novel

### The Quarter-Deck: Revenge as Covenant (Ch. 36)

Revenge enters the novel explicitly in Chapter 36, "The Quarter-Deck," when Ahab nails the doubloon to the mainmast and reveals the true purpose of the voyage. The crew, caught up in Ahab's theatrical intensity, swears the oath to hunt the White Whale.

Ishmael's retrospective account in Chapter 41, "Moby Dick," acknowledges his own complicity: "I, Ishmael, was one of that crew; my shouts had gone up with the rest; my oath had been welded with theirs; and stronger I shouted, and more did I hammer and clinch my oath, because of the dread in my soul. A wild, mystical, sympathetical feeling was in me; Ahab's quenchless feud seemed mine" (041-001).

The psychological mechanism is precise: fear intensifies allegiance. Ishmael shouts louder because he is afraid, and his shouting binds him more tightly to the revenge he fears.

### The Theology of Revenge (Ch. 41)

Chapter 41 develops revenge into something approaching a theology. Ahab's madness is popularly ascribed to his wound, and "so too, all the added moodiness which always afterwards, to the very day of sailing in the Pequod on the present voyage, sat brooding on his brow" (041-023).

Melville then frames Ahab's revenge in explicitly cosmic terms: "Here, then, was this grey-headed, ungodly old man, chasing with curses a Job's whale round the world, at the head of a crew, too, chiefly made up of mongrel renegades, and castaways, and cannibals" (041-024).

Ahab's revenge is not simply retaliation for a lost leg. It is a war against the principle of injury itself — against a universe that permits a dumb beast to maim a man. The whale becomes, in Ahab's theology, the mask behind which all cosmic malevolence hides.

### The Rational Infrastructure of Revenge (Ch. 44)

Chapter 44, "The Chart," reveals that Ahab's revenge is not only passionate but methodical. In the solitude of his cabin, he maps the whale's probable course with "a maze of currents and eddies, with a view to the more certain accomplishment of that monomaniac thought of his soul" (044-003).

Revenge in Ahab's hands becomes a science. The charts, the log-books of previous encounters, the known feeding grounds and migration routes — all are pressed into the service of a single, obsessive purpose. This is what distinguishes Ahab's revenge from mere rage: it has a rational infrastructure.

The chapter also raises the question that haunts the entire enterprise: can a single whale be reliably found in the "broad boundless ocean" (044-023)? Ahab's revenge depends on a near-impossible act of location — the meeting of one man and one whale in the vastness of the sea.

### The Chase Narrative: Revenge as Natural Fact (Ch. 45)

In Chapter 45, "The Affidavit," Melville backs Ahab's revenge with documentary evidence. He cites Owen Chace's narrative of the *Essex*: "Every fact seemed to warrant me in concluding that it was anything but chance which directed his operations; he made two several attacks upon the ship" (045-012).

The affidavit transforms revenge from a psychological motive into a natural fact. If whales can exhibit "the horrid aspect and revenge of the whale" (045-013) — if revenge can be attributed to the animal itself — then Ahab's pursuit is not madness but reciprocity. He is answering the whale's own revenge with his own.

This move destabilizes the boundary between hunter and hunted. Both Ahab and the whale are agents of revenge. The hunt is not a pursuit but a mutual vendetta.

### The Town-Ho: Private Revenge (Ch. 54)

Chapter 54, "The Town-Ho's Story," provides the counter-example. Steelkilt, the Lakeman, nurses a grievance against Radney, the chief mate who struck him: "he kept his own counsel (at least till all was over) concerning his own proper and private revenge upon the man who had stung him in the ventricles of his heart" (054-069).

Steelkilt's revenge is "proper and private" — it is personal, not cosmic. He plans to kill Radney with his own hands. But the novel denies him this satisfaction. Radney is killed by Moby Dick instead: "a fool saved the would-be murderer from the bloody deed he had planned. Yet complete revenge he had, and without being the avenger" (054-079).

The twist is crucial. Steelkilt gets his revenge, but it is executed by the White Whale — by the same cosmic force that Ahab is hunting. Melville suggests that revenge, even when it succeeds, escapes human agency. It is always already appropriated by larger forces.

## Revenge as Narrative Structure

Revenge is not only a theme in *Moby-Dick* but the principle of its narrative. The entire plot is organized as a revenge quest — the pursuit of a specific object (the White Whale) by a specific agent (Ahab) for a specific injury (the lost leg).

Yet Melville complicates every element of this formula. The injury is ambiguous — does a whale "injure" a man, or does it merely act according to its nature? The agent is divided — Ahab's crew are bound to his revenge by oath and gold, not by their own grievances. The object is elusive — the White Whale cannot reliably be found, and even when found, cannot reliably be killed.

The result is a revenge narrative that constantly questions its own premises. The closer Ahab gets to his revenge, the more the novel asks whether revenge is a coherent concept at all.

Steelkilt's story in "The Town-Ho" provides the necessary counterpoint. The private revenge succeeds, but the avenger is displaced — "complete revenge he had, and without being the avenger." The novel's most explicit revenge plot resolves without the revenge being executed by the person who desired it. This suggests that revenge, in Melville's world, is always already cosmic — always already taken out of human hands.

## Scholarly Context

Revenge in *Moby-Dick* has been read as an allegory of American imperial ambition (Ahab's pursuit of the whale as America's pursuit of manifest destiny), as a psychological study of trauma and repetition compulsion, and as a theological inquiry into the problem of cosmic justice.

The Senecan revenge tradition provides one important context. Ahab's elaborate rhetoric, his theatrical staging of the oath, and his self-conscious performance of the revenger's role all draw on the conventions of revenge tragedy. Melville transposes the genre from the court to the sea, replacing the poisoned cup with the grog-filled harpoon socket.

The Town-Ho counterplot has received less attention but is essential to Melville's design. Steelkilt's story shows that Melville could have written a straightforward revenge narrative if he had wanted to. By placing that straightforward narrative as a story-within-the-story, and by having its resolution executed by the very whale Ahab hunts, Melville frames private revenge as insufficient — even when it works, it is not enough.
