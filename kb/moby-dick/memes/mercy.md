# Mercy

## Definition

In *Moby-Dick*, mercy is the attribute of God that the novel's characters most desperately invoke and that the novel's universe most conspicuously withholds. The word appears first and most powerfully in Father Mapple's sermon on Jonah: "His all the mercy and the power" [009-024], a declaration that frames mercy as the defining attribute of the divine. Yet the world of the novel systematically refuses to confirm this theology. The sea "spares not the split wrecks of ships. No mercy, no power but its own controlling" [058-010]. Nature is governed not by mercy but by an indifferent force that the novel repeatedly calls "the universal cannibalism of the sea." Mercy, in Melville's cosmos, may be prayed for — the crew cries "The corpusants have mercy on us all!" [119-032] during the typhoon — but it is never guaranteed. The novel thus stages a tension between the Christian theology of mercy (articulated most fully in the Jonah sermon) and the experiential reality of a universe in which mercy appears to be a human projection onto a void.

## Manifestations in the Novel

Mercy's most sustained treatment comes in Father Mapple's sermon (Ch.9), where Jonah's cry from the whale's belly becomes the paradigm of the sinner's plea: "not yet supplicating God for mercy, since he but too well knew the darkness of his deserts" [009-034]. The sermon insists that mercy is available only when the sinner fully acknowledges his guilt — a condition that Ahab, the novel's central sinner, pointedly refuses. Queequeg's "Ramadan" prompts Ishmael to observe that "Heaven have mercy on us all — Presbyterians and Pagans alike" [017-002], extending mercy's reach beyond sectarian boundaries. In "Midnight, Forecastle" (Ch.40), Pip's cry — "somewhere in yon darkness, have mercy on this small black boy down here!" [040-062] — is the novel's most direct plea, a child's appeal to an unseen power. During the typhoon in "The Candles" (Ch.119), the crew repeatedly cries "The corpusants have mercy on us all!" [119-032, 119-036, 119-038], transforming the electrical phenomenon into a quasi-divine agency capable of mercy. But Ahab, characteristically, refuses the posture of supplication, grasping the lightning-rods and addressing the fire directly. The novel's final verdict, delivered through the sinking of the Pequod, suggests that mercy — like the "all-destroying but unconquering whale" — is a quality that the universe does not possess.

## Scholarly Context

Mercy in *Moby-Dick* has been read through the lens of Melville's Calvinist inheritance and his rebellion against it. The Calvinist doctrine of predestination makes mercy — the suspension of deserved punishment — logically problematic: if all is foreordained, mercy is merely the unfolding of a predetermined script. Father Mapple's sermon, with its insistence on Jonah's agency in repentance, represents an Arminian counter-current within the novel's otherwise Calvinist drift. Critics have also noted the racial dimension of mercy's distribution: Pip, the Black cabin boy, cries for mercy and receives only madness; Ahab, the white captain, refuses to ask for mercy and is granted a hero's death. The novel's final withholding of mercy — the Pequod's sinking, Ishmael's solitary survival — has been interpreted as Melville's rejection of theodicy, the refusal to justify the ways of God to man.
