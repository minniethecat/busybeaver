# Sperm Whale

## Overview

The sperm whale is the central animal of *Moby-Dick*. It is the species to which [[the-white-whale|Moby Dick]] belongs.

Melville devotes multiple chapters to its anatomy, behavior, and cultural significance. His cetology forms a substantial portion of the novel.

The sperm whale was the most valuable quarry in nineteenth-century whaling. Its head contains spermaceti, a waxy oil prized for candles and lubricants.

Ishmael calls it "the most formidable of all whales to encounter" (032-021).

## Physical Characteristics

The sperm whale has a massive, block-shaped head that comprises one-third of its body length.

Within this head is the spermaceti organ, a reservoir of fine oil. Melville describes it in encyclopedic detail across Chapters 74–77.

Its jaw is long and narrow, lined with teeth that can reach eight inches. Unlike baleen whales, the sperm whale is a toothed predator.

It fights back. Unlike the [[right-whale|right whale]], which is relatively docile, the sperm whale is known to attack whale-boats.

## Cetology in Moby-Dick

Melville classifies the whale according to size in Chapter 32 ("Cetology").

The sperm whale is the largest of the "Folio" whales. Ishmael elevates it above all other whales: the sperm whale is "the most majestic in aspect" (032-021).

Chapters 74–77 offer a systematic anatomy. The head, the case, the junk, the spout-hole — each gets its own chapter.

These chapters are not digressions. They root the novel's symbolism in physical fact.

## The Whale as Industry

The sperm whale fueled the American economy. Spermaceti oil lit the lamps of the world.

Harvesting it was dangerous, brutal work. The try-works, where blubber was rendered, turned the [[pequod|Pequod]]'s deck into a fiery hell.

Melville captures the paradox: beauty and violence, industry and slaughter, knowledge and destruction.

## Sperm Whale vs. Right Whale

The sperm whale and the [[right-whale|right whale]] are the two great divisions of Melville's cetology.

The right whale yields baleen (whalebone). The sperm whale yields oil and spermaceti.

Ahab's pursuit is specifically of the sperm whale — "the white whale" is an albino sperm whale, not a right whale.

Melville uses the contrast between the two species to explore different modes of perception and value.

## Cultural Significance

The sperm whale appears in the folklore of many whaling cultures.

Melville draws on natural histories by Beale, Bennett, and Scoresby. He corrects errors, amplifies wonders, and transforms zoology into philosophy.

The whale becomes a vehicle for exploring the limits of human knowledge. "I know him not, and never will," Ishmael admits.
