# Superstition

## Definition

In *Moby-Dick*, superstition is not a peripheral quirk of uneducated sailors but a structural principle of the whaling world. From the earliest chapters, Ishmael establishes supernatural belief as a condition of life at sea: the whaleman, "shut up in the hold of a ship," is by his isolation "strongly inclined to superstition" (026-001). Melville traces how the maritime environment — its vastness, its opacity, its mortal dangers — generates belief systems that blur the line between rational observation and supernatural conviction. The novel neither endorses nor dismisses these beliefs; instead, it treats superstition as a form of knowledge native to the whaling condition, one that the pragmatic ship-master, the philosophical narrator, and the obsessed captain must each negotiate in their own way.

## Manifestations in the Novel

Superstition permeates every level of the novel's world. Among the common sailors, it takes the form of folk-beliefs transmitted through generations of whalemen. The sperm whalemen "in general" are described as subscribing to "whatever superstitions" their predecessors held, including the belief that "a whale taken by one ship, and afterwards killed by another" rightfully belongs to the second vessel (059-011). This is not mere irrationality but a practical doctrine disguised in supernatural clothing — a way of rationalizing the lawless economy of the open ocean.

At a higher register, Melville links superstition to political and religious authority. The officers of whaling ships recognize that "when extreme political superstitions invest them," men may be made to obey commands against their own reason (033-006). The quarter-deck itself — the ship's command center — is described in terms that evoke the superstitious awe accorded to sovereign power. This insight connects maritime hierarchy to the broader dynamics of belief that sustain all forms of authority.

The novel's most charged deployment of superstition centers on the White Whale itself. As Ahab's obsession deepens, the crew's perception of Moby Dick shifts from natural creature to supernatural agent: some sailors "would go still further in their superstitions; declaring Moby Dick not only ubiquitous, but immortal" (041-013). The whale becomes a screen onto which the crew projects their collective anxieties, a process Melville diagnoses with clinical precision: "Nor even in our superstitions do we fail to throw the same snowy mantle round our phantoms" (042-015). This is the white whale as psychological phenomenon — not merely a whale, but the shape that mortal terror assumes when it cannot be named.

Ishmael himself is not immune. He traces the "immemorial superstition of their race" in the conduct of the *Pequod*'s crew, whose diverse nationalities share a common substrate of magical thinking (051-005). Even Queequeg, whose pragmatic cannibal religion initially seems refreshingly free of Western theological anxiety, participates in this shared credulity. And there is an irony in the fact that the sperm whale's own bodily substance — spermaceti — is credited by "the old Paracelsan superstition" with medicinal virtues (094-004). The whale is not only the object of superstition but also its material medium.

## Key Passages

> "A whaleman in the general, shut up in the hold of a ship, with his pipe in his mouth, is strongly inclined to superstition; but to that sort of superstition, which by its very nature, debars the mind from the regions of the supernatural."
> (026-001)

> "For be a man's intellectual superiority what it will, it can never assume the practical, available supremacy over other men, without the aid of some sort of external arts and entrenchments, always, in themselves, more or less paltry and base."
> (033-006)

> "Some of them would go still further in their superstitions; declaring Moby Dick not only ubiquitous, but immortal (for immortality is but ubiquity in time)."
> (041-013)

> "Nor even in our superstitions do we fail to throw the same snowy mantle round our phantoms; all ghosts rising in a milk-white fog — Yea, while these terrors seize us, let us add, that even the king of terrors, when personified by the evangelist, rides on his pallid horse."
> (042-015)
