# The Whale-Line

## Definition

The whale-line is the rope — "only two-thirds of an inch in diameter" — that connects the harpoon to the whale boat. When a whale is struck, the line runs out at terrifying speed, capable of snaring a man and dragging him to his death. Melville devotes the entirety of Chapter 60 ("The Line") to this piece of whaling equipment, transforming it from a mundane tool into a symbol of the invisible connections that bind all humans to one another and to mortality.

## Manifestations in the Novel

Melville introduces the whale-line with characteristic mix of technical precision and metaphysical reach: "With reference to the whaling scene shortly to be described, as well as for the better understanding of all similar scenes elsewhere presented, I must here give a little account of the magical, sometimes horrible whale-line" (060-001). He describes its manufacture from hemp (sometimes manila), its exact diameter, its careful coiling in tubs, and the deadly danger it poses: a man caught in the running line is pulled overboard and drowned before anyone can react.

The line's metaphoric power emerges when Melville compares it to the bonds connecting all people: "All men live enveloped in whale-lines. All are born with halters round their necks; but it is only when caught in the swift, sudden turn of death, that mortals realize the silent, subtle, ever-present perils of life" (060-005). The whale-line becomes a figure for fate — invisible, coiled, ready to spring.

The whale-line also features in the novel's most famous image of interdependence: the monkey-rope tying Ishmael to Queequeg in Chapter 72, and the line connecting Ahab to the whale in the final chase.

## Scholarly Context

Chapter 60 is one of Melville's most concentrated exercises in turning technical whaling knowledge into metaphysical allegory. The whale-line has been read as a figure for the interconnectedness of all life, for original sin (the "halter" at birth), for fate, and — in the context of the monkey-rope chapter — for the ethical bonds between humans. The line that kills is also the line that connects; the harpoon-line that fastens men to the whale also fastens them to each other.
