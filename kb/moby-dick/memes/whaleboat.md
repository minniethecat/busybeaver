# Whaleboat

## Definition

The whaleboat is the small, fast, oar-propelled craft deployed from a whaleship for the actual pursuit and killing of whales. In *Moby-Dick*, the whaleboat is distinguished from other ships' boats by its specialized design: it has "no seat astern, no sofa of that sort whatever, and no tiller at all" [055-011], being steered instead by a long oar. Unlike a captain's cushioned gig, the whaleboat is a stripped-down instrument of mortal purpose — built for speed, maneuverability, and the deadly work of closing with a whale. Each whaleship carries multiple whaleboats (the Pequod carries at least four), each commanded by a mate and crewed by a harpooneer and oarsmen.

## Manifestations in the Novel

The whaleboat is the theater of the novel's central action. It is from a whaleboat's bow that Ahab delivers his oath: "Drink, ye harpooneers! drink and swear, ye men that man the deathful whaleboat's bow — Death to Moby Dick!" [036-051]. The whaleboat mediates between the ship (the Pequod as floating society) and the whale (the object of the hunt), serving as the liminal space where human will confronts natural force. Stubb presides over his whaleboat "as if the most deadly encounter were but a dinner, and his crew all invited guests" [050-006]. The whaleboat's vulnerability is absolute: "bits of wreck, oars, whaleboats, canoes, blown-off Japanese junks" [050-007] litter the ocean, testifying to the frequency with which whaleboats are destroyed in the chase. In "The Grand Armada" (Ch. 87), whaleboats venture into the heart of a whale pod, where "all whaleboats carry certain curious contrivances" [087-019] for the perilous work of the fishery.

## Scholarly Context

The whaleboat in Melville's novel has been understood as a figure for democratic community — a small, interdependent crew whose survival depends on coordinated action and mutual trust. The whaleboat also represents the technological extension of human will: a crafted instrument that allows humans to engage with creatures of sublime scale. Critics have noted how the destruction of Ahab's whaleboat in the final chase (Ch. 135) prefigures the Pequod's own annihilation, the smaller vessel's fate telescoping into the larger.
