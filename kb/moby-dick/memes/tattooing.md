# Tattooing

## Definition

In *Moby-Dick*, tattooing is not merely bodily decoration but a system of inscription — a form of writing that precedes and exceeds alphabetic literacy. Queequeg's tattooed body serves as the novel's most elaborate instance of this phenomenon. Melville presents Queequeg's tattoos as simultaneously aesthetic, cosmological, and funerary: they are "a complete theory of the heavens and the earth, and a mystical treatise on the art of attaining truth" (110-019), inscribed directly onto living flesh by a prophet of his island home. The tattoos are a text that Queequeg himself cannot read — a sacred writing whose full meaning is inaccessible even to its bearer — and this epistemological gap between inscription and comprehension becomes one of the novel's most powerful figures for the limits of human knowledge.

## Manifestations in the Novel

Queequeg's tattoos are introduced in the early chapters as a marker of alterity and danger. Ishmael, sharing a bed with the stranger at the Spouter-Inn, fixates on the "dark complexioned" harpooneer's body, which he imagines to be covered in "the traces of some strange creature" (010-003). The initial horror — Ishmael's conviction that Queequeg is a cannibal, a "cursed Jonah" (003-005) — gradually transforms into fascination and then intimacy. This arc from fear to love is mediated through the tattoos themselves: as Ishmael comes to know Queequeg, the markings shift from signs of savagery to objects of wonder.

Melville stresses that Queequeg's tattoos are not purely personal but carry a communal, even cosmic, significance. In "The Counterpane" (Chapter 4), Ishmael wakes to find Queequeg's arm "tattooed all over with an interminable Cretan labyrinth of a figure" (004-001) thrown over him. The image of the labyrinth — a classical figure for unknowability and entrapment — links bodily marking to the paradoxes of interpretation. Later, in "A Bosom Friend" (Chapter 10), the narrator observes that "through all his unearthly tattooings, I thought I saw the traces of a simple honest heart" (010-003), suggesting that the tattoos are simultaneously a concealment and a revelation of character.

The tattoos reach their fullest symbolic elaboration in "Queequeg in His Coffin" (Chapter 110), where the dying harpooneer orders a coffin made and, as his final act, has "the twisted tattooing on his body" copied onto the coffin lid (110-019). This act of transcription transforms the tattoos from a living, perishable text into a permanent record — a mirror of Melville's own project of memorializing the whaling world in prose. Queequeg's recovery from his illness does not undo the significance of this gesture; the coffin becomes the *Pequod*'s life-buoy, and the tattoos, having been copied, survive the body that bore them.

The tattooing of Ishmael himself, briefly mentioned in "The Bower in the Arsacides" (Chapter 102), provides a late counterpoint. Ishmael has the measurements of a whale skeleton "tattooed" on his right arm (102-014), reducing a sublime creature to a set of numerical notations. The contrast with Queequeg's cosmological tattoos is pointed: Ishmael's scientific inscription marks the limits of empirical knowledge, while Queequeg's sacred inscription points toward truths that exceed rational comprehension.

## Key Passages

> "Through all his unearthly tattooings, I thought I saw the traces of a simple honest heart; and in his large, deep eyes, fiery black and bold, there seemed tokens of a spirit that would not be overcome."
> (010-003)

> "This tattooing had been the work of a departed prophet and seer of his island, who, by those hieroglyphic marks, had written out on his body a complete theory of the heavens and the earth, and a mystical treatise on the art of attaining truth; so that Queequeg in his own proper person was a riddle to unfold."
> (110-019)

> "With a wild whimsiness, he now used his coffin for a sea-chest; and emptying into it his canvas bag of clothes, set them in order there. Many spare hours he spent, in carving the lid with all manner of grotesque figures and drawings; and it seemed that hereby he was striving, in his rude way, to copy parts of the twisted tattooing on his body."
> (110-019)

> "In the New England States, it is well known, that many tattooed savages sailing in Nantucket ships are at times found to be more than commonly valuable as harpooneers."
> (018-005)
