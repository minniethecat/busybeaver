# The Symphony

## Definition

The Symphony is Melville's name for Chapter 132, the penultimate chapter before the three-day chase of Moby Dick. On a "clear steel-blue day" of extraordinary beauty, the sky and sea merge into a single "all-pervading azure," and Ahab—moved by the harmony—experiences a fleeting moment of tenderness and self-knowledge. His conversation with Starbuck reveals his full tragic awareness: he knows his quest is madness, yet he cannot turn back. The chapter is the novel's emotional apex, the last pause before catastrophe.

## Manifestations in the Novel

The Symphony unfolds in two movements. The first is a nature description of unusual lyricism: the air is "transparently pure and soft, with a woman's look," the sea "robust and man-like," and the two "seemed one; it was only the sex, as it were, that distinguished them." The sun appears "like a royal czar and king," giving "this gentle air to this bold and rolling sea; even as bride to groom." This cosmic eroticism establishes a world of perfect, generative harmony—the opposite of the destructive monomania that drives Ahab.

Into this harmony steps Ahab. He leans over the side, watching his shadow "sank and sank to his gaze, the more and the more that he strove to pierce the profundity." Then, unexpectedly, the beauty works on him: "the lovely aromas in that enchanted air did at last seem to dispel, for a moment, the cankerous thing in his soul." And "from beneath his slouched hat Ahab dropped a tear into the sea; nor did all the Pacific contain such wealth as that one wee drop."

The second movement is Ahab's confession to Starbuck. In an extraordinary monologue, he catalogues his forty years of whaling—"forty years of privation, and peril, and storm-time!"—and acknowledges what he has lost: home, wife, child, the simple sweetness of land. "I widowed that poor girl when I married her, Starbuck," he says. He knows his pursuit is madness: "what a forty years' fool—fool—old fool, has old Ahab been!" Yet when Starbuck pleads with him to turn for home, Ahab cannot. "What is it, what nameless, inscrutable, unearthly thing is it... that against all natural lovings and longings, I so keep pushing, and crowding, and jamming myself on all the time?" The answer—Fate, predestination, or simply the irreversible momentum of obsession—is left suspended.

Starbuck, "blanched to a corpse's hue with despair," steals away. The chapter ends with Ahab seeing Fedallah's reflected eyes in the water, a reminder that darker forces are watching.

## Key Passages

> "It was a clear steel-blue day. The firmaments of air and sea were hardly separable in that all-pervading azure; only, the pensive air was transparently pure and soft, with a woman's look, and the robust and man-like sea heaved with long, strong, lingering swells, as Samson's chest in his sleep." (132-001)

The opening sentence establishes the chapter's mood of eroticized nature. The gendered imagery—feminine air, masculine sea—is atypical for Melville and signals the extraordinary register of this moment.

> "From beneath his slouched hat Ahab dropped a tear into the sea; nor did all the Pacific contain such wealth as that one wee drop." (132-006)

Perhaps the most famous sentence in the chapter. The tear is both infinitely precious (it contains Ahab's suppressed humanity) and infinitely small (it disappears instantly into the vast Pacific). The tension between these two valences is the tragedy in miniature.

> "Oh, Starbuck! is it not hard, that with this weary load I bear, one poor leg should have been snatched from under me?" (132-008)

Ahab speaks here not as defiant captain but as wounded man. The "weary load" is his accumulated grief; the missing leg is the physical token of that grief, but not its source.

> "What is it, what nameless, inscrutable, unearthly thing is it; what cozening, hidden lord and master, and cruel, remorseless emperor commands me; that against all natural lovings and longings, I so keep pushing, and crowding, and jamming myself on all the time?" (132-013)

This is Ahab's most lucid statement of his own unfreedom. He recognizes his obsession as an external force—"some invisible power"—yet this recognition does not liberate him. The question mark is genuine; the chapter provides no answer.

## Scholarly Context

"The Symphony" is universally recognized as one of the great chapters of *Moby-Dick* and a high point of tragic art in American literature. Critics have emphasized its Shakespearean dimensions—Ahab's soliloquy echoes Lear and Macbeth in its fusion of self-knowledge and helplessness. The chapter has also been central to theological readings of the novel: Ahab's inability to turn back, even when he wants to, has been interpreted as Melville's Calvinist critique of predestination. Eco-critical approaches have focused on the chapter's nature description as an expression of what Melville scholar Lawrence Buell calls "environmental imagination"—a world that exists independently of human drama, beautiful and indifferent.
