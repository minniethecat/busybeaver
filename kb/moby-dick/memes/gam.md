# The Gam

## Definition

A *gam* is the meeting of two whaleships at sea, a custom unique to the whaling industry. Melville defines it with mock-scholarly precision in Chapter 53: "You might wear out your index-finger running up and down the columns of dictionaries, and never find the word. Dr. Johnson never attained to that erudition; Noah Webster's ark does not hold it." The gam involves the two captains visiting each other's vessels, exchanging news, and — crucially for the novel — sharing intelligence about Moby Dick.

## Manifestations in the Novel

Chapter 53 ("The Gam") provides the formal definition and social anthropology of the practice. Melville contrasts the whaleman's hospitable gam with the cold formality of naval vessels: the whaler "has a 'Gam,' a thing so utterly unknown to all other ships that they never heard of the name even." The gam is a democratic institution — the captain stands in the whale-boat (there being no seat), "all alive to the importance of sustaining his dignity by maintaining his legs."

The Pequod's nine gams with other ships structure the second half of the novel, each encounter revealing something about Ahab's quest:
- **The *Goney* (Albatross)**: A haunted, silent gam — the *Goney*'s captain drops his speaking-trumpet into the sea.
- **The *Town-Ho***: The gam-within-a-gam, told as a story by Ishmael.
- **The *Jeroboam***: A gam interrupted by plague and prophecy, introducing Gabriel.
- **The *Jungfrau* (Virgin)**: A gam marked by competitive whale-chasing and German bathos.
- **The *Samuel Enderby***: The crucial gam where Ahab meets another captain who has lost a limb to Moby Dick.
- **The *Rachel***: A gam of tragic appeal — Captain Gardiner begs Ahab to help search for his lost son.
- **The *Delight***: The final gam, where a shattered whaleboat and a corpse foretell the Pequod's fate.

Each gam is a chance for Ahab to turn back, and each time he refuses.

## Scholarly Context

The gams function as a kind of morality-play structure, each ship offering an alternative fate for the Pequod. Critics note that the gam is Melville's innovation on the epic convention of the hero's encounters on a journey — a democratic, American version of Odysseus's island stops. The gam also embodies the tension between the isolation of Ahab's monomania and the community offered by the whaling fraternity.
