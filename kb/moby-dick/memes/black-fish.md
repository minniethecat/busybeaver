# Black Fish

## Classification

The Black Fish occupies Book II (Octavo), Chapter II of Ishmael's cetological system — the second entry in his middle tier of "whales of middling magnitude" (032-031). Modern taxonomy identifies it as the long-finned pilot whale, *Globicephala melas*, a large oceanic dolphin of the family Delphinidae.

The name "Black Fish" is, as Ishmael immediately acknowledges, a poor one. Blackness is "the rule among almost all whales" (032-034), making the designation almost comically uninformative. His solution is characteristic: rename it.

## In Cetology (Chapter 32)

Ishmael's portrait of the Black Fish (032-034) is brief, vivid, and punctuated by one of his most memorable coinages. He opens by noting his practice of using "the popular fishermen's names," then immediately breaks with it:

> I do so now, touching the Black Fish, so-called, because blackness is the rule among almost all whales. So, call him the Hyena Whale, if you please. (032-034)

The name sticks. The reason is in the face:

> His voracity is well known, and from the circumstance that the inner angles of his lips are curved upwards, he carries an everlasting Mephistophelean grin on his face. (032-034)

The Mephistophelean grin — a reference to the devilish tempter of Faust legend — is one of Melville's most concentrated images. A single anatomical detail (the upturned lip corner) becomes a moral portrait. The Hyena Whale does not merely eat; it grins while eating, and the grin is infernal.

The physical description that follows is compact: "This whale averages some sixteen or eighteen feet in length. He is found in almost all latitudes." The detail that lingers is the dorsal fin:

> He has a peculiar way of showing his dorsal hooked fin in swimming, which looks something like a Roman nose. (032-034)

The commercial note is pragmatic. Though not a primary quarry, the Black Fish serves a purpose:

> When not more profitably employed, the sperm whale hunters sometimes capture the Hyena whale, to keep up the supply of cheap oil for domestic employment—as some frugal housekeepers, in the absence of company, and quite alone by themselves, burn unsavory tallow instead of odorous wax. (032-034)

The comparison to frugal housekeepers burning tallow captures the Black Fish's economic status precisely: a substitute, a fallback, the cheap oil you use when no one is watching. Yet even this modest whale yields "upwards of thirty gallons of oil" — no small quantity from a sixteen-foot animal.

## Appearances in the Novel

The only other mention of the black fish in the narrative comes in Chapter 115, where the [[pequod|Pequod]] encounters the *Bachelor*, a festively decorated Nantucket ship homeward bound. Among the practical details of whaling equipment, Ishmael notes "the poke or stomach skin of the black fish" (115-005) — used, presumably, for some shipboard purpose. The reference is fleeting, but it places the black fish among the functional objects of the whale-ship world, as much tool as creature.

## Whaling Context

Pilot whales were taken opportunistically by American whalers, particularly when sperm whales were scarce. Their oil — "unsavory tallow" compared to spermaceti's "odorous wax" — was used for household lighting, lubrication, and soap-making. The meat was sometimes consumed by the crew.

The species' tendency to travel in large, tight-knit pods made it vulnerable to drive hunting — a practice that continues in the Faroe Islands today (*grindadráp*). Melville, writing from the perspective of the open-ocean whaler, does not describe this coastal hunting method, but his note that the black fish is "found in almost all latitudes" (032-034) aligns with the pilot whale's wide distribution.

Modern research has revealed the complexity of pilot whale social structures — matrilineal pods, communal care of young, lifelong bonds. The "Mephistophelean grin" that Melville saw as a mark of voracity is, in a different light, simply an anatomical feature of a highly social cetacean.
