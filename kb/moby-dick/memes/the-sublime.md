# The Sublime

## Definition

The sublime, in eighteenth- and nineteenth-century aesthetics (Edmund Burke, Immanuel Kant), is the experience of awe mingled with terror aroused by objects of vast scale, power, or obscurity — the ocean, a mountain, a storm, the infinite. In *Moby-Dick*, Melville deploys the sublime as both a subject (the whale, the sea) and a narrative mode, pushing language toward the limits of representation in the face of what exceeds human comprehension.

## Manifestations in the Novel

The sublime pervades the novel from its opening pages. Ishmael's first sight of Queequeg, the tattooed cannibal sharing his bed, strikes him as "something almost sublime in it" (010-004). Ahab's monomania is described in sublime terms: his "broad brow... seemed made of solid bronze" and his "dilated nostrils snuffed in the sublime life of the worlds" (034-009).

The sea itself is the novel's primary locus of the sublime — vast, indifferent, beautiful, and deadly. In Chapter 58, Melville explicitly invokes the sublime in describing "the appalling ocean" that "surrounds the verdant land," comparing the soul to an island besieged by "all the horrors of the half known life." The whiteness of the whale, elaborated in Chapter 42, is perhaps Melville's most original contribution to sublime aesthetics: it is the absence of color, the "colorless, all-color of atheism from which we shrink," that produces the most profound terror.

The "sublime uneventfulness" of the tropic whaling life (035-004) represents a domesticated sublime — the daily encounter with vastness that becomes routine yet never fully loses its power. And in "The Candles" (Chapter 119), the corposants that light the Pequod's masts transform the ship into a scene of supernatural sublimity.

> (058-012)

> (042-008)

## Scholarly Context

Melville's engagement with the sublime is a major topic in *Moby-Dick* criticism. Scholars trace the influence of Burke's *Philosophical Enquiry into the Origin of Our Ideas of the Sublime and Beautiful* (1757) and Thomas Carlyle's transcendentalism. Melville extends the Burkean sublime — traditionally associated with nature — to the human: Ahab is a sublime figure, the whale is sublime, and the act of writing about them becomes a sublime performance. The "whiteness of the whale" chapter is often read as Melville's revision of the sublime for an American, democratic age — where terror comes not from kings or gods but from the indifferent material world itself.
