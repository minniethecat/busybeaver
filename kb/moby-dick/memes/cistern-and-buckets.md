# Cistern and Buckets

## Definition

"Cistern and Buckets" names both Chapter 78 of Moby-Dick and the whaling process it describes: the extraction of spermaceti from the whale's head-case, known to whalemen as the "case" or the "Heidelburgh Tun." The head of the sperm whale contains a vast reservoir of the finest oil — the spermaceti — which must be baled out bucket by bucket. Melville uses this technical procedure as the stage for one of the novel's most dramatic episodes: the harpooneer Tashtego's fall into the cistern and his underwater rescue by Queequeg.

## Manifestations in the Novel

The process begins with Tashtego mounting the whale's head, which is suspended alongside the Pequod. "Nimble as a cat, Tashtego mounts aloft; and without altering his erect posture, runs straight out upon the overhanging mainyard-arm" (078-001). With a whip-tackle and a stout iron-bound bucket, he begins the rhythmic labor of bailing the spermaceti from the deep cistern within the head.

The work proceeds methodically until disaster strikes. As "the eightieth or ninetieth bucket came suckingly up," Tashtego loses his grip and plunges headfirst into the great tun of spermaceti, disappearing "with a horrible oily gurgling" (078-002). The scene instantly transforms from industrial labor to life-or-death drama.

Daggoo attempts a rescue with the bucket, but the situation worsens when one of the enormous hooks suspending the head tears loose, causing the entire mass to crash into the sea with Tashtego trapped inside. At the critical moment, Queequeg dives overboard with a boarding-sword. He swims down to the sinking head, slashes a hole in its bottom, and — in Melville's unforgettable image — performs an underwater delivery, hauling Tashtego out "by the head" (078-011).

The chapter closes with Melville's extraordinary conceit: "Midwifery should be taught in the same course with fencing and boxing, riding and rowing" (078-012). The rescue is reframed as an obstetrical act — Queequeg delivers Tashtego from the whale's head-womb in a "running delivery." Melville then offers a final meditation: had Tashtego perished, it would have been "a very precious perishing; smothered in the very whitest and daintiest of fragrant spermaceti; coffined, hearsed, and tombed in the secret inner chamber and sanctum sanctorum of the whale" (078-015).

## Scholarly Context

The "Cistern and Buckets" episode has attracted sustained critical attention for its fusion of the homosocial, the homoerotic, and the obstetric. Queequeg's "deliverance" of Tashtego — with its language of "obstetrics" and "delivery" — has been read as Melville's queering of the all-male world of the whale-ship, wherein a tattooed cannibal enacts a form of male midwifery in the open sea. The closing meditation on Tashtego's hypothetical "precious perishing" — smothered in the "whitest and daintiest" spermaceti — has been analyzed as Melville's characteristic move from physical fact to metaphysical speculation, where the material substance of whaling (oil) becomes the medium for contemplating death, sweetness, and the seductions of annihilation.
