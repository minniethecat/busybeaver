# Stowing Down and Clearing Up

## Definition

"Stowing Down and Clearing Up" (Chapter 98) describes the final phase of processing a captured sperm whale: the decanting of the rendered oil into six-barrel casks and the meticulous cleansing of the ship afterward. Melville frames this industrial procedure as at once a triumph of human industry and a bitter emblem of life's endless, repetitive labor — a cycle that begins again the moment it ends.

## Manifestations in the Novel

The chapter is structured as a diptych — first the mess, then the cleansing:

**The Stowing Down.** While the oil is still warm ("like hot punch"), it is poured into enormous casks on the heaving deck. The ship becomes a chaos of industry: the casks "perilously scoot across the slippery deck, like so many land slides" while every sailor hammers at the hoops. Once filled, the casks are lowered through the hatchways "to their final rest in the sea" — returning the whale's substance to the depths from which it came, "sliding along beneath the surface as before; but, alas! never more to rise and blow."

**The Clearing Up.** The transformation is miraculous. A day or two after the bloody chaos of the cutting-in and trying-out, the Pequod is spotless. Sperm oil, Melville notes, "possesses a singularly cleansing virtue." The decks are scrubbed white, the rigging is brushed clean of soot, every implement is put away, and the crew themselves emerge "fresh and all aglow, as bridegrooms new-leaped from out the daintiest Holland."

**The Cycle Resumes.** Melville's coda is devastating: just as the crew admire their spotless ship, the cry of "There she blows!" rings out, and the whole "man-killing" process must begin again. "Oh! my friends, but this is man-killing! Yet this is life."

## Key Passages

> "While still warm, the oil, like hot punch, is received into the six-barrel casks; and while, perhaps, the ship is pitching and rolling this way and that in the midnight sea, the enormous casks are slewed round and headed over, end for end, and sometimes perilously scoot across the slippery deck, like so many land slides." (098-005)

> "Now, with elated step, they pace the planks in twos and threes, and humorously discourse of parlors, sofas, carpets, and fine cambrics; propose to mat the deck; think of having hanging to the top; object not to taking tea by moonlight on the piazza of the forecastle." (098-013)

> "Oh! my friends, but this is man-killing! Yet this is life. For hardly have we mortals by long toilings extracted from this world's vast bulk its small but valuable sperm; and then, with weary patience, cleansed ourselves from its defilements, and learned to live here in clean tabernacles of the soul; hardly is this done, when — *There she blows!* — the ghost is spouted up, and away we sail to fight some other world, and go through young life's old routine again." (098-015)

> "Oh! the metempsychosis! Oh! Pythagoras, that in bright Greece, two thousand years ago, did die, so good, so wise, so mild; I sailed with thee along the Peruvian coast last voyage — and, foolish as I am, taught thee, a green simple boy, how to splice a rope!" (098-017)

## Thematic Analysis

1. **The Laboring Body.** The chapter is one of Melville's most sustained descriptions of physical labor. The sailors' swollen wrists, the "man-killing" ninety-six-hour shifts, the equatorial sun combined with the try-works fires — all convey the brutal physical cost of whaling.

2. **Cleansing and Civilization.** The "clearing up" represents a small triumph of civilization over chaos — the human capacity to impose order, cleanliness, and even elegance on a world of blood and grease. The sailors' joking about "parlors, sofas, carpets" reveals their hunger for the domestic sphere they have left behind.

3. **The Eternal Return.** The chapter's deepest philosophical move is its transformation of the whaling cycle into a universal metaphor. "There she blows!" — the cry that restarts the whole process — becomes the call of life itself, the signal that "we sail to fight some other world, and go through young life's old routine again." The labor is never done; the cleansing is never final.

4. **Metempsychosis.** Melville's invocation of Pythagoras and the doctrine of the transmigration of souls provides the chapter's metaphysical climax. The whale's substance passes into the ship, into the sailors, into the market — a materialist version of the soul's journey. Ishmael's whimsical claim to have sailed with Pythagoras "along the Peruvian coast" collapses the distance between ancient philosophy and the grimy reality of the whaling deck.

5. **The Ghost as Metaphor.** The "ghost" that is spouted up when the next whale is sighted is not supernatural — it is the ghost of labor itself, the eternal return of the task that is never finally completed. "There she blows!" is the summons that haunts all human striving toward rest.
