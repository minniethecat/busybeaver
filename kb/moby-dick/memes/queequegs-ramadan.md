# Queequeg's Ramadan

## Overview

Chapter 17, "The Ramadan," is one of the novel's most tonally complex early chapters — a comic domestic episode that modulates into a serious meditation on religious pluralism, cultural misunderstanding, and the limits of rational argument against deeply held belief.

[[queequeg|Queequeg]] has declared that the day is his "Ramadan, or Fasting and Humiliation," and [[ishmael|Ishmael]], respecting "everybody's religious obligations, never mind how comical" (017-001), leaves him alone until evening. What follows is a farcical sequence in which Ishmael, [[mrs-hussey|Mrs. Hussey]], and the chambermaid panic over Queequeg's apparent catatonia, break down the door, and find him "squatting on his hams, and holding Yojo on top of his head" (017-014) — having sat motionless for upwards of ten hours.

The chapter's comedy is undercut by a serious argument. Ishmael's attempt to persuade Queequeg that "all these Lents, Ramadans, and prolonged ham-squattings in cold, cheerless rooms were stark nonsense" (017-026) fails utterly. Queequeg listens with "a sort of condescending concern and compassion, as though he thought it a great pity that such a sensible young man should be so hopelessly lost to evangelical pagan piety" (017-029). The chapter ends with Queequeg eating "a prodigiously hearty breakfast of chowders of all sorts, so that the landlady should not make much profit by reason of his Ramadan" (017-030) — a gesture at once practical, generous, and wholly indifferent to Ishmael's theological reasoning.

## Chronology

### The Decision to Leave Queequeg Alone

Ishmael learns that Queequeg intends to fast all day. He resolves not to interfere:

> "I cherish the greatest respect towards everybody's religious obligations, never mind how comical, and could not find it in my heart to undervalue even a congregation of ants worshipping a toad-stool." (017-001)

The tone is characteristically Ishmaelean: tolerant, amused, and faintly superior — but the tolerance is genuine. He extends it to "those other creatures in certain parts of our earth, who with a degree of footmanism quite unprecedented in other planets, bow down before the torso of a deceased landed proprietor merely on account of the inordinate possessions yet owned and rented in his name" (017-001), a satirical glance at ancestor-worship and, by implication, at all forms of reverence for inherited wealth.

### The Alarm

By evening, Queequeg's door is locked and silent. Ishmael peeps through the keyhole, sees "part of the foot-board of the bed and a line of the wall, but nothing more" (017-003), and panics. The chambermaid is summoned, then Mrs. Hussey:

> "Mrs. Hussey soon appeared, with a mustard-pot in one hand and a vinegar-cruet in the other, having just broken away from the occupation of attending to the castors, and scolding her little black boy meantime." (017-005)

The detail of the mustard-pot and vinegar-cruet — domestic props wielded as weapons of alarm — anchors the chapter's comedy in the mundane. Mrs. Hussey, finding Queequeg's harpoon missing, immediately concludes suicide: "It's unfort'nate Stiggs done over again — there goes another counterpane" (017-010).

### The Forced Entry

Ishmael charges the door:

> "With a prodigious noise the door flew open, and the knob slamming against the wall, sent the plaster to the ceiling; and there, good heavens! there sat Queequeg, altogether cool and self-collected; right in the middle of the room; squatting on his hams, and holding Yojo on top of his head." (017-014)

Queequeg "looked neither one way nor the other way, but sat like a carved image with scarce a sign of active life" (017-014). The image is deliberately sculptural and hieratic — Queequeg has become an idol himself, mirroring the little black figurine he balances on his crown.

### The Long Night

Ishmael goes to supper, listens to sailors' stories until nearly eleven, and returns to find Queequeg "just where I had left him; he had not stirred an inch" (017-021). He throws his bearskin jacket over the motionless figure and tries to sleep:

> "Think of it; sleeping all night in the same room with a wide awake pagan on his hams in this dreary, unaccountable Ramadan!" (017-023)

At daybreak, Queequeg rises "with stiff and grating joints, but with a cheerful look," presses his forehead against Ishmael's, and announces that his Ramadan is over (017-024).

### The Failed Argument

Ishmael delivers a lecture on comparative religion, arguing that "fasting makes the body cave in; hence the spirit caves in; and all thoughts born of a fast must necessarily be half-starved" (017-026). The argument's climax is one of the novel's most quoted lines:

> "Hell is an idea first born on an undigested apple-dumpling; and since then perpetuated through the hereditary dyspepsias nurtured by Ramadans." (017-026)

The theory is ingenious, reductionist, and completely ineffective. Queequeg's only response, when asked if he has ever suffered dyspepsia, is to recall a feast after a great battle "wherein fifty of the enemy had been killed by about two o'clock in the afternoon, and all cooked and eaten that very evening" (017-027) — a reply that effortlessly demolishes Ishmael's framework by introducing a scale of "religious practice" far beyond the question of fasting.

### The Resolution

Queequeg emerges from his fast with an enormous breakfast:

> "Queequeg, taking a prodigiously hearty breakfast of chowders of all sorts, so that the landlady should not make much profit by reason of his Ramadan, we sallied out to board the Pequod, sauntering along, and picking our teeth with halibut bones." (017-030)

The detail that Queequeg eats heartily "so that the landlady should not make much profit by reason of his Ramadan" is a lovely touch — his fast is a private religious act, not an economic imposition on his hostess.

## Narrative Significance

"The Ramadan" is the novel's most sustained treatment of religious pluralism. Ishmael's opening declaration — "Heaven have mercy on us all — Presbyterians and Pagans alike — for we are all somehow dreadfully cracked about the head, and sadly need mending" (017-002) — is both tolerant and condescending, and the chapter systematically undermines the condescension while preserving the tolerance.

The chapter enacts a reversal of cultural authority. Ishmael, the educated white Christian, marshals arguments from "the rise and progress of the primitive religions" (017-026) to convince Queequeg of the irrationality of his fast. Queequeg, the "savage," listens with "condescending concern and compassion" (017-029) and remains entirely unmoved. The comedy arises from Ishmael's inability to recognize that his own rationalist Christianity is, from Queequeg's perspective, just another set of "half-crazy conceits."

The chapter also deepens the Ishmael-Queequeg relationship. Ishmael's genuine anxiety during the locked-door sequence, his impulsive rush to break down the door, and his tenderness in covering the motionless Queequeg with his bearskin jacket all demonstrate an attachment that has moved beyond the "sleeping-partner" arrangement of Chapters 3–4 into something like friendship.

## Aftermath / Consequences

Queequeg's Ramadan has no direct plot consequences, but its thematic resonance unfolds across the novel. The chapter establishes Queequeg's religious practice as a counterpoint to the Christianity of Father Mapple's sermon (Ch.7–9) and the quasi-religious intensity of Ahab's quest. Queequeg's Ramadan is a private, embodied discipline — silent, immobile, indifferent to observation — in contrast to Ahab's public, theatrical, world-transforming rage.

The chapter also introduces the motif of the locked door and the mysterious interior. Ishmael's peeping through the keyhole and seeing only "a crooked and sinister" partial view (017-003) prefigures the novel's larger epistemological concern with what cannot be seen or known — the "ungraspable phantom of life" (001-014).
