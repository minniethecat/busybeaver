# The Funeral

## Definition

"The Funeral" is Melville's name for the grim spectacle of a stripped whale carcass drifting away from the whaling ship after its blubber and head have been taken. Chapter 69 of *Moby-Dick* transforms this routine whaling aftermath into a meditation on death, nature's rapacity, and humanity's superstitious response to the unknown. The chapter is one of the shortest in the novel — a single sustained paragraph of prose poetry — yet it packs some of the book's sharpest philosophical barbs.

## Manifestations in the Novel

The chapter opens with the command "Haul in the chains! Let the carcase go astern!" and proceeds to describe what happens when the Pequod releases a butchered whale:

**The Corpse Adrift.** The "peeled white body of the beheaded whale flashes like a marble sepulchre" — still colossal, still intact in bulk, but now a "vast white headless phantom" drifting further and further away. Around it swarm sharks and sea-fowl in "murderous din," feasting on the remains.

**The Mocking Funeral.** Melville frames this as a grotesque parody of a human burial: "There's a most doleful and most mocking funeral! The sea-vultures all in pious mourning, the air-sharks all punctiliously in black or speckled." The creatures that would never have helped the living whale now piously attend its corpse.

**The Ghost That Frightens Ships.** Melville's bitterest irony comes in the final movement: passing ships, seeing the white mass floating in the distance and mistaking it for breakers, mark the spot on their charts as dangerous shoals. For years afterward, ships avoid the place — "leaping over it as silly sheep leap over a vacuum" — in what Melville calls "the story of your obstinate survival of old beliefs never bottomed on the earth."

## Key Passages

> "The peeled white body of the beheaded whale flashes like a marble sepulchre; though changed in hue, it has not perceptibly lost anything in bulk. It is still colossal. Slowly it floats more and more away, the water round it torn and splashed by the insatiate sharks, and the air above vexed with rapacious flights of screaming fowls, whose beaks are like so many insulting poniards in the whale." (069-005)

> "There's a most doleful and most mocking funeral! The sea-vultures all in pious mourning, the air-sharks all punctiliously in black or speckled. In life but few of them would have helped the whale, I ween, if peradventure he had needed it; but upon the banquet of his funeral they most piously do pounce. Oh, horrible vultureism of earth! from which not the mightiest whale is free." (069-007)

> "Thus, while in life the great whale's body may have been a real terror to his foes, in his death his ghost becomes a powerless panic to a world." (069-011)

## Thematic Analysis

1. **Nature's Indifference.** The funeral scene strips away any sentimental view of nature. The sharks and sea-birds are not evil — they are simply hungry. Yet Melville's rhetoric ("horrible vultureism of earth") insists that this indifference itself constitutes a kind of cosmic horror.

2. **The Persistence of Error.** The ships that mistake the floating carcass for shoals embody Melville's critique of unexamined tradition: "There's your law of precedents; there's your utility of traditions; there's the story of your obstinate survival of old beliefs never bottomed on the earth." This is Enlightenment skepticism delivered with savage satirical force.

3. **The Whale as Christ Figure.** The "marble sepulchre" and the language of sacrifice and desecration invite a Christological reading: the whale, like Christ, is killed, stripped, and becomes a source of both terror and salvation. But Melville inverts the resurrection — the whale's "ghost" survives only as a navigation hazard.

4. **Ghosts and Belief.** The chapter's final lines — "Are you a believer in ghosts, my friend? There are other ghosts than the Cock-Lane one, and far deeper men than Doctor Johnson who believe in them" — pivot from satire to something more unsettling, implicating the reader in the very superstition the chapter has mocked.
