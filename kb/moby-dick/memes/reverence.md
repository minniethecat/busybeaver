# Reverence

## Definition

Reverence in *Moby-Dick* is the spiritual attitude that Melville sets against Ahab's blasphemous defiance — a deep, natural piety toward the whale, the sea, and the mysteries they embody. Unlike the doctrinal faith of Father Mapple's Calvinism or the passive wonder of the mast-head dreamer, reverence is an active moral orientation: it acknowledges limits, bows before what exceeds human comprehension, and refuses to weaponize the self against the cosmos. Starbuck is its primary vessel; Ahab's tragedy is its absence. When Ahab cries "to neither love nor reverence wilt thou be kind" (119-042), he names exactly what he has lost — and what the novel, in its deepest structure, laments.

## Manifestations in the Novel

**Starbuck's natural reverence (Chapter 26)**. Ishmael introduces Starbuck as "endued with a deep natural reverence" (026-001) — the phrase is carefully chosen. "Natural" distinguishes Starbuck's reverence from institutional religion: it arises from temperament and experience rather than doctrine. His "wild watery loneliness of his life" (026-001) has cultivated in him a humility before the sea's power that Ahab, for all his greater experience, has systematically destroyed in himself. Starbuck's reverence makes him the novel's moral compass — the one who recognizes that the white whale should not be hunted, that the "clear spirit of clear fire" should not be defied.

**Ahab's loss of reverence (Chapters 28, 36, 41)**. Ahab's first appearance on deck is a study in the absence of reverence. He is described in terms of his wound — the ivory leg, the scar that "came upon him, deadly" — and everything about him bespeaks a man who has stopped bowing to anything. The oath scene on the quarter-deck (Ch. 36) is an anti-liturgy: instead of reverence for the whale as a creature of God or nature, Ahab demands that his crew swear vengeance. "The ancient Ophites of the east reverenced in their statue devil" (041-019) — Melville names reverence but only to show how Ahab has inverted it into devil-worship. Ahab does not lack reverence; he has transferred it from the sacred to the obsessive, from God to the white whale as anti-god.

**Ishmael's ethnographic reverence (Chapter 27)**. When Ishmael observes Queequeg, Tashtego, and Daggoo, he notes how each man "was lost to all sense of reverence for the many marvels of their majestic bulk and mystic ways" (027-004) when absorbed in the work of whaling. This is not a moral failure but a phenomenological observation — reverence requires distance, and intimacy with the whale's body can erode it. The harpooneers' loss of reverence is the occupational hazard of whaling, a preview of the larger spiritual danger the Pequod faces.

**The whale as object of trembling reverence (Chapter 42)**. In the great meditation on the whiteness of the whale, Ishmael describes how "he was the object of trembling reverence and awe" (042-011). The whale inspires reverence not despite but because of his terror. This is the paradox at reverence's core: the proper response to what threatens to destroy you may be awe, not hatred. Ahab's failure is not that he fears the whale but that he cannot let his fear ripen into reverence.

**The typhoon as reverence's test (Chapter 119)**. The climactic confrontation between reverence and defiance occurs during the storm in "The Candles." The corposants transform the ship into an altar of fire, and Ahab addresses the "clear spirit of clear fire" with magnificent irreverence: "to neither love nor reverence wilt thou be kind; and e'en for hate thou canst but kill; and all are killed" (119-042). Ahab's speech is a theodicy in reverse — an accusation against the cosmos for its indifference. Starbuck's objection — "let us go below; let us not tempt this dangerous fire" — is reverence's voice, small but persistent, always urging retreat from the blasphemous edge.

**Perth's unreverenced grief (Chapter 112)**. The blacksmith Perth, who lost his family and took to sea, is described as having "his every woe unreverenced; his grey head a scorn to flinty hearts" (112-006). The image inverts reverence: here it is the man's suffering that goes unreverenced, that receives no acknowledgment. Melville suggests that reverence is owed not only to the divine but to human suffering itself — that the failure to revere a man's grief is itself a form of impiety.

## Key Passages

- (016-048) — Peleg's "present irreverence" in the face of prophecy: the casual irreverence of the practical man
- (026-001) — Starbuck "endued with a deep natural reverence": the novel's moral center defined
- (027-004) — The harpooneers "lost to all sense of reverence": the occupational erosion of awe
- (041-019) — Ahab and "the ancient Ophites of the east reverenced in their statue devil": reverence inverted into devil-worship
- (042-011) — Moby Dick as "the object of trembling reverence and awe": terror and reverence as inseparable
- (112-006) — Perth's "every woe unreverenced": the failure to revere human suffering as impiety
- (119-042) — "to neither love nor reverence wilt thou be kind": Ahab's accusation against the cosmos, naming what he has lost

## Scholarly Context

Melville's treatment of reverence engages with the Romantic tradition of natural piety — Wordsworth's "natural piety" and Coleridge's "primary imagination" as a repetition in the finite mind of the eternal act of creation. But Melville darkens this tradition: reverence in *Moby-Dick* is not the serene contemplation of benevolent nature but the trembling acknowledgment of a cosmos that may be indifferent or hostile. Critics have linked Starbuck's "deep natural reverence" to the Quaker tradition of silent worship and inner light, and Ahab's irreverence to the Promethean defiance that runs from Aeschylus through Milton's Satan to Shelley's liberated Prometheus — a lineage Melville simultaneously invokes and interrogates.
