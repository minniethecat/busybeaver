# The Candles

## Definition

"The Candles" is the title of Chapter 119 of *Moby-Dick*, one of the novel's most dramatic and philosophically charged episodes. During a typhoon in the Japanese seas, the Pequod's three masts are lit by [[the-corpusants|corpusants]] (St. Elmo's fire) — electrical discharges that turn each yard-arm into what appears to be a giant wax taper burning with "pallid fire." Seizing the theatrical moment, [[ahab|Ahab]] delivers an extraordinary address to the "clear spirit of clear fire," articulating his philosophy of tragic defiance: worship through rebellion, acknowledgment of a higher power combined with absolute refusal to submit to it.

## Manifestations in the Novel

The chapter falls into four movements:

**The Storm.** A typhoon strikes the Pequod directly, tearing away her canvas. Lightning and thunder split the darkness. Starbuck notes with alarm that the gale comes from the eastward — the very course Ahab intends to run in pursuit of [[the-white-whale|Moby Dick]] — and sees in this a divine warning: "The gale that now hammers at us to stave us, we can turn it into a fair wind that will drive us towards home."

**The Corpusants Appear.** As the storm intensifies, the "corpusants" ignite at the yard-arms: "all the yard-arms were tipped with a pallid fire; and touched at each tri-pointed lightning-rod-end with three tapering white flames, each of the three tall masts was silently burning in that sulphurous air, like three gigantic wax tapers before an altar." The crew is transfixed with awe.

**Ahab's Address.** In the chapter's centerpiece, Ahab grasps the lightning-chain links and delivers a speech to the fire itself — a declaration of metaphysical war. He acknowledges the fire's power ("I own thy speechless, placeless power") but insists on his own irreducible selfhood: "In the midst of the personified impersonal, a personality stands here." He cannot defeat the cosmic power, but he can refuse to kneel to it.

**The Harpoon Flame.** As Ahab concludes, his harpoon — forged by [[perth|Perth]] the blacksmith — bursts into flame at its barb. Starbuck interprets this as a divine sign: "God, God is against thee, old man; forbear!" But Ahab extinguishes the flame with a single breath — "thus I blow out the last fear!" — and threatens to transfix any sailor who attempts to turn the ship homeward.

## Key Passages

> "All the yard-arms were tipped with a pallid fire; and touched at each tri-pointed lightning-rod-end with three tapering white flames, each of the three tall masts was silently burning in that sulphurous air, like three gigantic wax tapers before an altar." (119-055)

> "Oh! thou clear spirit of clear fire, whom on these seas I as Persian once did worship, till in the sacramental act so burned by thee, that to this hour I bear the scar; I now know thee, thou clear spirit, and I now know that thy right worship is defiance." (119-077)

> "In the midst of the personified impersonal, a personality stands here. Though but a point at best; whencesoe'er I came; wheresoe'er I go; yet while I earthly live, the queenly personality lives in me, and feels her royal rights." (119-077)

> "All your oaths to hunt the White Whale are as binding as mine; and heart, soul, and body, lungs and life, old Ahab is bound. And that ye may know to what tune this heart beats; look ye here; thus I blow out the last fear!" (119-089)

## Thematic Analysis

1. **Defiant Worship.** Ahab's theology is paradoxical: he acknowledges a higher power but worships it through defiance rather than submission. This is not atheism but antitheism — a theism of rebellion that grants the divine its existence while denying it moral authority.

2. **The Unconquerable Self.** "In the midst of the personified impersonal, a personality stands here." This is perhaps the novel's most concentrated statement of Ahab's (and Melville's) philosophy: against the vast, impersonal forces of nature and fate, the individual self asserts its "queenly" sovereignty — not to win, but to refuse to be erased.

3. **Fire as Double Symbol.** Fire in this chapter is both destructive and illuminating, divine and demonic. Ahab claims kinship with it ("of thy fire thou madest me") while also defying it. The fire becomes a metaphor for consciousness itself — the Promethean spark that both empowers and torments.

4. **Starbuck's Counter-Voice.** Starbuck's plea — "let me square the yards, while we may, old man, and make a fair wind of it homewards" — represents the voice of prudence, domesticity, and survival. Ahab's extinguishing of the harpoon flame is his final, theatrical rejection of this alternative.

5. **The Crew's Submission.** The chapter dramatizes Ahab's absolute command: when the crew attempts to mutiny, a single gesture from Ahab ("dashing the rattling lightning links to the deck, and snatching the burning harpoon") reduces them to "a terror of dismay." His personal charisma overcomes even their instinct for self-preservation.

## Scholarly Context

"The Candles" is widely regarded as one of the novel's central set-pieces, often compared to the storm scene in *King Lear* for its fusion of natural fury and metaphysical rebellion. The chapter's Zoroastrian imagery (Ahab as "Persian" fire-worshipper) draws on Melville's reading in comparative religion, particularly the Persian fire-cult described in contemporary sources. Critics have also noted the chapter's structural position — coming near the novel's climax — as marking the point at which Ahab's quest becomes explicitly, rather than implicitly, a war against heaven.
