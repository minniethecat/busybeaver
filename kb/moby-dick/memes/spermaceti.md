# Spermaceti

## Definition

Spermaceti is the waxy, crystalline oil found in the head-case (or "case") of the sperm whale — the substance that gives the species its name and that was, in the nineteenth-century whaling economy, the most highly prized of all whale products. Melville describes it with a mixture of scientific precision and rhapsodic wonder: "the spermaceti itself, how bland and creamy that is; like the spring fineness of translucent, semi-transparent ice" (065-004). In its raw state, spermaceti is a liquid that, upon exposure to air, cools and crystallizes into a "white, waxy, and highly fragrant" solid (077-004). It was burned in the finest candles, used in cosmetics, and valued above all other whale oils for the clarity and steadiness of its flame.

## Manifestations in the Novel

Spermaceti appears throughout *Moby-Dick* as both an economic fact of whaling life and a substance charged with metaphorical significance. Ishmael's first reference establishes it as the ultimate object of the whaling quest: "that valuable substance, spermaceti, is obtained" (032-021). When the Pequod's crew processes a captured whale, the spermaceti is extracted with ritual care, "hoisted out," strained, and stored in the ship's magazine.

The novel's most sustained encounter with spermaceti comes not during the violent extraction of the case but in the Chapter 94 reverie, "A Squeeze of the Hand." Here Ishmael, seated on deck with his hands plunged into a tub of cooling spermaceti, experiences a transcendence that is at once physical and spiritual: "Squeeze! squeeze! squeeze! all the morning long; I squeezed that sperm till I myself almost melted into it; I squeezed that sperm till a strange sort of insanity came over me; and I found myself unwittingly squeezing my co-laborers' hands in it" (094-005). The passage modulates from manual labor into erotic communion into cosmic philosophy: "I have perceived that in all cases man must eventually lower, or at least shift, his conceit of attainable felicity" (094-006).

The dual nature of spermaceti — as material commodity and mystical substance — is central to the novel's vision. The same substance that burns in "spermaceti candles" (119-036), illuminating the darkness, is also the product of butchery, extracted from a "coffined, hearsed, and tombed" whale head (078-015). Stubb's "spermaceti supper at the capstan-head" (064-008) is a feast of whale-meat that juxtaposes gustatory pleasure with the visceral reality of dismemberment. Spermaceti thus mediates between the novel's material and symbolic registers: it is simultaneously the reason men risk their lives at sea and the occasion for Ishmael's most ecstatic vision of human solidarity.

## Key Passages

> "But the spermaceti itself, how bland and creamy that is; like the spring fineness of translucent, semi-transparent ice, though a little held to the heat."
> (065-004)

> "Squeeze! squeeze! squeeze! all the morning long; I squeezed that sperm till I myself almost melted into it; I squeezed that sperm till a strange sort of insanity came over me; and I found myself unwittingly squeezing my co-laborers' hands in it, mistaking their hands for the gentle globules."
> (094-005)

> "Would that I could keep squeezing that sperm for ever! For now, since by many prolonged, repeated experiences, I have perceived that in all cases man must eventually lower, or at least shift, his conceit of attainable felicity; not placing it anywhere in the intellect or the fancy; but in the wife, the heart, the bed, the table, the saddle, the fire-side, the country."
> (094-006)

> "In good time, the flames caught it, and we had, in Stubb's own inexorable words, 'a full dish of that young cub's best; fit for a king!'"
> (064-008)
