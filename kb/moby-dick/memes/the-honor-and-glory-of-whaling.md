# The Honor and Glory of Whaling

## Definition

In Chapter 82, titled "The Honor and Glory of Whaling," Ishmael delivers an exuberant defense of his profession by constructing a mythological genealogy of the whaleman. Tracing whaling's lineage through five legendary figures — Perseus, St. George, Hercules, Jonah, and Vishnoo — he argues that whaling is not a sordid commercial enterprise but a calling of ancient nobility: "I am transported with the reflection that I myself belong, though but subordinately, to so emblazoned a fraternity" (082-002). The chapter is simultaneously a parody of antiquarian scholarship and a serious claim about the dignity of manual labor and the mythic resonance of the whaling life.

## Manifestations in the Novel

The chapter opens with a methodological disclaimer that is itself a stylistic credo: "There are some enterprises in which a careful disorderliness is the true method" (082-001). This principle governs the chapter's associative, digressive argument, which moves through its five mythological exhibits with a logic that is more rhetorical than historical.

The first whaleman, Ishmael contends, was Perseus, who rescued Andromeda from a sea monster that was unmistakably a whale: "Those were the knightly days of our profession, when we only bore arms to succor the distressed, and not to fill men's lamp-feeders" (082-003). The transition from chivalric rescue to commercial lamp-oil marks a fall from grace that the chapter seeks to reverse through the sheer force of its rhetoric.

St. George's dragon, Ishmael argues, was actually a whale — "for in many old chronicles whales and dragons are strangely jumbled together, and often stand for each other" (082-004). This enables the claim that "one of our own noble stamp, even a whaleman, is the tutelary guardian of England" (082-005). The argument is deliberately outrageous, yet Melville's prose invests it with a conviction that unsettles the boundary between jest and earnestness.

Hercules presents a more ambiguous case — swallowed by a whale and then disgorged, he "may be deemed a sort of involuntary whaleman" (082-006). Jonah follows through parallel reasoning, linked to Hercules by shared narrative structure. The chapter's climax comes with Vishnoo, who "became incarnate in a whale" (082-008) to retrieve the sacred Vedas from the ocean floor, thus sanctifying the whale as divine vessel. The concluding roll-call — "Perseus, St. George, Hercules, Jonah, and Vishnoo! there's a member-roll for you! What club but the whaleman's can head off like that?" (082-009) — is at once comic boasting and a genuine theological claim about the whaleman's place in the cosmic order.

## Key Passages

> "The more I dive into this matter of whaling, and push my researches up to the very spring-head of it so much the more am I impressed with its great honorableness and antiquity; and especially when I find so many great demi-gods and heroes, prophets of all sorts, who one way or other have shed distinction upon it, I am transported with the reflection that I myself belong, though but subordinately, to so emblazoned a fraternity."
> (082-002)

> "The gallant Perseus, a son of Jupiter, was the first whaleman; and to the eternal honor of our calling be it said, that the first whale attacked by our brotherhood was not killed with any sordid intent. Those were the knightly days of our profession, when we only bore arms to succor the distressed, and not to fill men's lamp-feeders."
> (082-003)

> "Any man may kill a snake, but only a Perseus, a St. George, a Coffin, have the heart in them to march boldly up to a whale."
> (082-004)

> "That wondrous oriental story is now to be rehearsed from the Shaster, which gives us the dread Vishnoo, one of the three persons in the godhead of the Hindoos; gives us this divine Vishnoo himself for our Lord;—Vishnoo, who, by the first of his ten earthly incarnations, has for ever set apart and sanctified the whale."
> (082-008)

> "Perseus, St. George, Hercules, Jonah, and Vishnoo! there's a member-roll for you! What club but the whaleman's can head off like that?"
> (082-009)
