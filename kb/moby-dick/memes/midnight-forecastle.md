# Midnight, Forecastle

## Overview

Chapter 40 of Moby-Dick is unique in form: it is presented entirely as a play script, with stage directions and dialogue attributed to sailors of various nationalities. On the Pequod's forecastle at midnight, harpooneers and sailors gather to sing, dance, and carouse. The revelry — driven by the tambourine of [[pip]], the black cabin boy — unfolds as a multicultural chorus, with voices from Nantucket, the Netherlands, France, Iceland, Malta, Sicily, China, Tahiti, Portugal, Denmark, England, Spain, and the Azores. Beneath the surface gaiety, however, dark currents run: a near-fight between [[daggoo]] and a Spanish sailor over race, the Old Manx sailor's meditation on dancing over graves, and the rising storm that mirrors the crew's inner turbulence.

## Chronology

- (040-002–017) — The scene opens with the foresail rising to reveal the watch singing a chorus: "Farewell and adieu to you, Spanish ladies!" The collective song sets a jaunty, defiant tone
- (040-018–019) — The Mate's voice from the quarter-deck calls eight bells; the Second Nantucket Sailor rouses the watch: "Eight bells there below! Tumble up!"
- (040-020) — **Dutch Sailor**: Praises the "grand snoozing" and the deadening effect of the "old Mogul's wine" — a note of alcoholic oblivion
- (040-021–023) — **French Sailor** and Pip drive the dancing: "Jig it, men, I say; merry's the word!" Pip, sulky and sleepy, can't find his tambourine
- (040-024–027) — **Iceland Sailor**, **Maltese Sailor**, **Sicilian Sailor**, and **Long-Island Sailor** each chime in — complaints about the springy deck, demands for dancing partners, homely pragmatism
- (040-028–032) — **Azore Sailor** ascends with Pip's tambourine; the dancing intensifies. **China Sailor** urges Pip: "Rattle thy teeth, then, and pound away; make a pagoda of thyself." **French Sailor**: "Merry-mad! Hold up thy hoop, Pip, till I jump through it!"
- (040-033) — **Tashtego**, quietly smoking apart: "That's a white man; he calls that fun: humph! I save my sweat." — a note of racial distance and dignity
- (040-034) — **Old Manx Sailor**: "I wonder whether those jolly lads bethink them of what they are dancing over. I'll dance over your grave, I will... O Christ! to think of the green navies and the green-skulled crews!" — the chapter's thematic pivot
- (040-036) — The sky darkens, the wind rises — nature intrudes on the revelry
- (040-037) — **Lascar Sailor**: "By Brahma! boys, it'll be douse sail soon. The sky-born, high-tide Ganges turned to wind!"
- (040-038–040) — **Maltese**, **Sicilian**, and **Tahitan Sailors** recline, dreaming of women and dancing girls — erotic nostalgia as counterpoint to the rising storm
- (040-041–043) — **Portuguese**, **Danish**, and **Fourth Nantucket Sailors** respond to the weather: "How the sea rolls swashing 'gainst the side!"; the Danish sailor praises the mate's steadiness; the Nantucketer recalls Ahab's order to "kill a squall"
- (040-044–045) — **English Sailor**: "Blood! but that old man's a grand old cove! We are the lads to hunt him up his whale!" — **All**: "Aye! aye!" — the crew unites around Ahab's quest
- (040-046) — **Old Manx Sailor**: "How the three pines shake!... This is the sort of weather when brave hearts snap ashore, and keeled hulls split at sea. Our captain has his birthmark; look yonder, boys, there's another in the sky — lurid-like, ye see, all else pitch black."
- (040-047–049) — **Daggoo**: "What of that? Who's afraid of black's afraid of me! I'm quarried out of it!" The **Spanish Sailor** (aside): "He wants to bully, ah!... thy race is the undeniable dark side of mankind — devilish dark at that." Daggoo grimly: "None."
- (040-051–054) — Lightning flashes; the Spanish Sailor calls it "Daggoo showing his teeth." Daggoo springs: "Swallow thine, mannikin! White skin, white liver!" The Spaniard meets him: "Knife thee heartily! big frame, small spirit!" — the revelry explodes into racial violence
- (040-055–058) — **All** cry "A row! a row! a row!" as the chapter cuts off mid-confrontation; Tashtego's final line: "A row a'low, and a row aloft — Gods and men — both brawlers! Humph!"

## Narrative Significance

Midnight, Forecastle is the novel's most formally audacious chapter — Melville's only experiment with pure dramatic form. The play-script structure achieves several effects simultaneously: it democratizes the narrative voice, giving equal stage time to a dozen nationalities; it dramatizes the Pequod's microcosmic diversity; and it creates a choral effect in which individual voices merge into collective rhythms.

The chapter operates as a compressed allegory of the novel's larger tensions. The revelry — singing, dancing, drinking — represents the crew's attempt to manufacture gaiety against the encroaching darkness of the voyage. The Old Manx Sailor's grave-imagery punctures this surface: the men are dancing "over" the drowned dead, and their own graves may lie beneath the same planks. The rising storm mirrors the inner turbulence that the singing is meant to suppress.

The Daggoo-Spaniard confrontation crystallizes the racial tensions that simmer throughout the novel. Daggoo's "Who's afraid of black's afraid of me!" is both proud self-assertion and an implicit claim on the novel's central symbol — the white whale as the "white" that the "black" men aboard are enlisted to destroy. Tashtego's final "Humph!" — his characteristic dismissal of white men's quarrels — closes the chapter on a note of weary distance from the entire affair.

## Aftermath / Consequences

The chapter ends without resolution — the "row" is left hanging, as if Melville deliberately cuts away from the violence to preserve the symbolic charge of the confrontation. The multicultural chorus of the forecastle will reappear throughout the novel in moments of collective action (the whale hunts, the gams), but never again in such concentrated, theatrical form. The chapter's dark undertow — dancing over graves, racial violence barely contained, the storm rising — prefigures the Pequod's final catastrophe.
