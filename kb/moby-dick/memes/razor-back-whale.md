# Razor Back Whale

## Classification

The Razor Back occupies Book I (Folio), Chapter V in Ishmael's cetological system — the penultimate of the six great whales (032-020). It is sandwiched between the [[hump-backed-whale|Hump-backed]] and the [[sulphur-bottom-whale|Sulphur Bottom]]. Modern scholars dispute its identity: some identify it with the Fin Whale (*Balaenoptera physalus*), while others note that Melville's description fits no known species precisely.

## In Cetology (Chapter 32)

Ishmael's account of the Razor Back (032-028) is the shortest cetological entry in the entire Folio — three sentences that are as much about what cannot be said as what can:

> Of this whale little is known but his name. I have seen him at a distance off Cape Horn. Of a retiring nature, he eludes both hunters and philosophers. Though no coward, he has never yet shown any part of him but his back, which rises in a long sharp ridge. Let him go. I know little more of him, nor does anybody else.

The passage is remarkable for its epistemological honesty. Ishmael refuses to fabricate knowledge where he has none. The razor back — "a long sharp ridge" — is the only visible sign of the whale's existence. The phrase "eludes both hunters and philosophers" places scientific and commercial pursuit on the same plane: both are forms of capture that this whale evades.

The closing "Let him go" is a release that is also a benediction. In a book about the compulsion to pursue, Ishmael here grants a whale the right to remain unknown.

## Appearances in the Novel

The Razor Back appears nowhere else in Moby-Dick. It exists only as a chapter heading and three sentences. This minimal presence is, in Melville's design, the point: some whales resist representation. The Razor Back is defined by what it withholds — its face, its habits, its oil, its meaning.

## Key Passages

- (032-020) — Listed as the fifth Folio whale, between Hump-backed and Sulphur Bottom
- (032-028) — Full description: "little is known but his name," seen off Cape Horn, "eludes both hunters and philosophers," "a long sharp ridge," "Let him go"

## Whaling Context

If the Razor Back is the Fin Whale, it was largely immune to 19th-century American whaling — too fast to be caught by open boats, too lean to be worth the risk. Fin whales became commercial targets only with mechanized whaling in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. Melville's "retiring nature" was, in commercial terms, a lucky escape.

## Symbolic Meaning

The Razor Back is Melville's whale of the impenetrable. It presents only a knife-edge to the world — "a long sharp ridge" that cuts the water and disappears. It is not aggressive like the [[killer-whale|Killer]] or noble like the [[sperm-whale|Sperm Whale]]; it is simply absent. In the taxonomy of Moby-Dick, where every whale carries symbolic weight, the Razor Back represents the outer limit of what cetology can know — and, more importantly, Ishmael's willingness to acknowledge that limit.

"Let him go" is one of the most humane sentences in Moby-Dick. It stands as a counterweight to Ahab's monomania: the willingness to release what cannot be known, the opposite of the chase.
