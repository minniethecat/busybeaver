# Hell

## Definition

Hell in *Moby-Dick* is never merely a place of posthumous punishment. Melville distributes it across three registers: as **theological threat**, the Calvinist hellfire wielded by Father Mapple and Bildad; as **psychological interior**, the self-consuming inferno Ahab discovers "in himself" (044-024); and as **material spectacle**, the try-works whose "red hell" (096-007) renders damnation visible on deck. The novel's most famous line — "from hell's heart I stab at thee" (135-058) — completes the distribution: hell is the heart from which Ahab acts, the condition he inhabits, and the force he addresses. In Melville's cosmos, hell is not elsewhere; it is woven into the fabric of mortal consciousness and into the fire that renders whale into oil.

## Manifestations in the Novel

**The opening maw (Chapter 9)**. Father Mapple's sermon on Jonah furnishes the novel's first vivid hell imagery. Jonah looks down from the whale's belly and "saw the opening maw of hell" (009-009), then prays from "out of the belly of hell" (009-039). The whale's interior is hell — a living, breathing damnation — and the sermon's geography is explicitly infernal: hell below, the "tormented deep" all around, and deliverance only through submission.

**Bildad's warning and hell as idea (Chapters 16–17)**. When Ahab first appears, Bildad admonishes Peleg with the Puritan diagnosis: "that creature that he's bound to hell" (016-066). But Ishmael immediately complicates the notion: "hell is an idea first born on an undigested apple-dumpling" (017-026) — dyspepsia as the origin of theology. This deflation is characteristic Melvillean irony: hell may be real, but Ishmael reminds us that ideas have bodily origins, that metaphysical terror may be nothing more than indigestion reified.

**Ahab's internal hell (Chapter 44)**. The most dangerous hell in the novel is inside Ahab. Alone over his charts, "when this hell in himself yawned beneath him" (044-024), Ahab contemplates the whale. The phrase "in himself" is crucial — hell is not imposed from outside but generated from within, the product of monomania's self-consuming logic. Ahab's sleeplessness, his isolation, his refusal of all human comfort: these constitute a hell he builds nightly and inhabits willingly.

**The try-works as inferno (Chapter 96)**. The Pequod at night, rendering whale blubber into oil, becomes a floating hell. The try-works "steadfastly shot her red hell further and further into the blackness of the sea and the night" (096-007). Ishmael, at the helm, is hypnotized by the flames until he nearly capsizes the ship — the helmsman literally steered toward hell by the spectacle of fire. The pagan harpooneers, tending the works, appear as devils tending damned souls. This is hell rendered material, industrial, and terrifyingly beautiful.

**Perth's theology of soot (Chapter 108)**. The blacksmith Perth, scarred by a life of loss, delivers the novel's most pragmatic hell theology: "so hell's probable" (108-012) — because soot clings to everything, and soot belongs to fire, and fire belongs to hell. His companions joke that "three places" stand "in one hell" (108-036) — the harpooneers tending the forge. Perth's hell is the working-class hell of the smithy, the material inference from observed fact: where there is fire and soot, hell cannot be far.

**Hell's despair and progeny of griefs (Chapter 106)**. Ahab's leg, replaced by the whalebone prosthetic, becomes a figure for "the joy-childlessness of all hell's despair" (106-003). Hell here is defined not by fire but by sterility — the inability to produce joy or to participate in any generative cycle. The "eternally progressive progeny of griefs" (106-003) extend hell forward into an infinite future, just as Ahab's monomania extends the hunt into a metaphysical project with no natural terminus.

**The climax: from hell's heart (Chapter 135)**. On the third day of the chase, Ahab's final words to the whale fuse all three registers: "from hell's heart I stab at thee; for hate's sake I spit my last breath at thee" (135-058). Hell is now the source of action (from its heart), the mode of address (spitting hate), and the destination (the Pequod, "like Satan, would not sink to hell till she had dragged a living part of heaven along with her" [135-061]). Ahab becomes hell's agent, or hell becomes Ahab's — the distinction collapses.

## Key Passages

- (009-009) — "I saw the opening maw of hell": Jonah in the whale, the novel's primal infernal image
- (009-039) — "'out of the belly of hell' — when the whale grounded upon the ocean's utmost bones": Jonah's prayer, hell's geography made tangible
- (016-066) — "that creature that he's bound to hell. Flukes and flames! Bildad, say that again to me": Bildad's Calvinist hell applied to Ahab
- (017-026) — "hell is an idea first born on an undigested apple-dumpling": Ishmael's deflationary counter-theology
- (044-024) — "when this hell in himself yawned beneath him, a wild cry would be heard": Ahab's internal hell as psychological fact
- (096-007) — "steadfastly shot her red hell further and further into the blackness of the sea and the night": the try-works as material inferno
- (096-011) — "would rather talk of operas than hell; calls Cowper, Young, Pascal, Rousseau": Stubb's comic indifference to damnation
- (106-003) — "the joy-childlessness of all hell's despair": hell defined by negation of joy, not presence of fire
- (108-012) — "so hell's probable. How the soot flies": Perth's empiricist infernal theology
- (108-030) — "to feel the fiery pains of hell for ever, and without a body?": the problem of bodiless damnation
- (135-058) — "from hell's heart I stab at thee; for hate's sake I spit my last breath at thee": Ahab's consummatory speech, hell as origin and mode
- (135-061) — "like Satan, would not sink to hell till she had dragged a living part of heaven along with her": the Pequod's infernal apotheosis

## Scholarly Context

Melville's deployment of hell draws on three traditions. The Calvinist doctrine of predestination — that some souls are bound for hell from birth — is voiced by Bildad and undercut by Ishmael's digestive counter-theology. The Puritan sermon tradition, exemplified by Father Mapple, uses hell as rhetorical architecture: the "opening maw" is both Jonah's predicament and the congregation's warning. Romanticism, particularly Blake's "The Marriage of Heaven and Hell," provides a third register: Melville's hell is not simply evil's territory but a site of creative-destructive energy, as in the try-works chapter where the flames are both terrifying and hypnotically beautiful. Critical consensus reads the try-works episode as Melville's most concentrated Dantean passage, the Pequod becoming an *Inferno* on water, with Ishmael as a pilgrim who nearly loses his way.
