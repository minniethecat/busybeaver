# Spirit

## Definition

The spirit in Moby-Dick operates across multiple registers: as the active, outward-reaching faculty of the self (distinguished from the soul's interiority); as the supernatural agency that pervades the ocean world; and as the literal spirits—liquor—that sustain the whalemen's courage. Melville exploits the word's rich polysemy to weave together the novel's psychological, metaphysical, and material dimensions.

## Spirit and Soul

Melville consistently distinguishes spirit from soul, though the terms overlap. Where the soul is the seat of interior life and moral identity, the spirit is the active, outward-facing faculty—the force that moves a person to action. Ishmael notes that Queequeg "signifies his spiritual withdrawal for the time" （008-004） during his Ramadan fast, suggesting spirit as the capacity for engagement with the world.

The body-spirit relationship is materialist: when "the body cave in; hence the spirit caves in; and all thoughts born of the spirit" （017-026）, Melville argues for a physiological basis of spiritual states. Yet he also entertains the spirit's independence: "their spirits penetrate through the thick haze of the future" （035-003）, as Ishmael says of the whalemen on the mast-head.

## Ahab's Spirit

Ahab possesses what Melville calls "a spirit that would dare a thousand devils" （010-003）, a formulation that captures both his heroic grandeur and demonic intensity. This spirit is characterized by "an infinity of firmest fortitude, a determinate, unsurrenderable wilfulness" （035-013）.

Yet Ahab's spiritual condition is also one of torment. In Chapter 119, "The Candles," Ahab addresses the corposants—electrical fire on the mastheads—as "thou clear spirit of clear fire" （119-042）. This apostrophe reveals Ahab's paradoxical relationship to spirit: he worships the very force he defies, acknowledging a spiritual power even as he refuses submission to it. His famous declaration—"thy right worship is defiance"—encapsulates the contradiction.

## Spiritual Terrors

Ishmael identifies "the more spiritual terrors" （026-004） as a distinct category of fear, more profound than physical danger. This concept is developed most extensively in Chapter 42, "The Whiteness of the Whale," where Ishmael explores how whiteness evokes "spiritual wonderment and pale dread" （042-007）. The whale's whiteness is identified as "the most meaning symbol of spiritual things, nay, the very veil of the Christian's Deity" （042-030）.

These spiritual terrors are distinguished from mere superstition. They arise not from ignorance but from the confrontation with realities that exceed rational comprehension—the sublime, the infinite, the void. Melville suggests that the whaling voyage, by stripping away the comforts of land and society, exposes the soul to spiritual realities that shore-dwellers can ignore.

## The Spirit-Spout

Chapter 51, "The Spirit-Spout," personifies the mysterious midnight spout as a supernatural entity. The chapter title's play on "spirit" and "spout" links the whale's breath to ghostly presence: "as if some winged spirit had lighted in the rigging" （051-002）. The spirit-spout haunts the Pequod's voyage, a spectral promise of the white whale's proximity.

## Material Spirits

Melville grounds the lofty discourse on spirit with a literal meaning: the alcohol distributed to the crew. "The first contained strong spirits, and was handed to Queequeg" （072-020） during the whale hunt. This material use of "spirits" creates a deliberate irony—the spiritual and the spirituous are linguistically identical, and Melville suggests that much of what passes for courage and inspiration at sea is chemically induced.
