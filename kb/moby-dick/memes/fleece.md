# Fleece

## Overview

Fleece, universally called "old Fleece," is the elderly Black cook of the [[the-pequod|Pequod]] who appears in [[chapter-064|Ch. 64: Stubb's Supper]]. He is roused from his hammock at a late hour by [[stubb]], the second mate, who demands that the cook preach a sermon to the [[sharks]] swarming around the whale carcass lashed to the ship's side (064-011). The resulting scene—a decrepit old man leaning over the bulwarks with tongs and lantern, solemnly exhorting a "fishy mob" to moderate their greedy feeding (064-014)—is one of the novel's richest passages of comic relief, layering minstrel conventions over a meditation on predation and appetite.

Fleece speaks in a heavy dialect marked by dropped consonants and inverted diction—"dat," "dere," "den," "t'all" (064-053)—and his physical decrepitude is rendered in vivid detail: his "knee-pans" are in poor condition, he shuffles and limps, and uses his cook's tongs as a makeshift cane (064-012). Though he appears in only a single chapter, Fleece has drawn sustained critical attention for how Melville fuses racial caricature with genuine pathos.

## Role in the Narrative

Fleece serves a double function. On the surface, he provides comic relief—a dialect-speaking "old darky" cook ordered about by Stubb for entertainment (064-051–053). Beneath the comedy, his scene recapitulates one of the novel's central preoccupations: the universality of predation. The sharks devour the whale's blubber; Stubb devours his whale steak; and Fleece's "sermon"—which Stubb approvingly calls "Christianity" (064-025)—highlights the hypocrisy of condemning one form of consumption while practicing another. Fleece appears only in Chapter 64.

## Key Appearances

- [[chapter-064|Ch. 64: Stubb's Supper]] (064-011–064-057) — Fleece is summoned by Stubb, ordered to preach to the sharks, delivers his sermon and benediction, and is briefly dismissed before being recalled.

## Character Traits

Fleece is **physically decrepit**: old and infirm, with bad knees ("something the matter with his knee-pans") that cause him to shuffle and limp (064-012). He uses his tongs as a walking aid and has to stoop to bring his "best ear" into play. He is **obedient and resigned**: despite being "not in any very high glee" at being roused, he obeys Stubb without protest, his demeanor "sullen" (064-014). His speech is rendered in heavy eye-dialect, aligning him with period minstrel conventions. Yet he is also **performatively solemn**: when preaching to the sharks, Fleece adopts an exaggerated clerical manner—flourishing his tongs shrilly—and his muttered asides suggest an inner life the surface comedy does not suppress.

## Relationships

**[[stubb]]**: The second mate defines Fleece's every appearance, summoning him, teasing him about his knees, and dismissing him only to recall him seconds later (064-011, 064-051–053, 064-057). The hierarchy is absolute, but Fleece's sullen compliance preserves a residue of self-respect.

**The [[sharks]]**: Fleece's unwilling "congregation" (064-014)—he addresses them as moral agents, scolding them for their "gobbling" greed. The sharks ignore him completely.

**The [[the-pequod|Pequod]] Crew**: Fleece occupies a low position as cook. The narrative notes "they called him" old Fleece, implying casual condescension (064-012).

## Notable Quotes

> "Cook, cook!—where's that old Fleece?" he cried at length, widening his legs still further, as if to form a more secure base for his supper; and, at the same time darting his fork into the dish, as if stabbing with his lance; "cook, you cook!—sail this way, cook!" (064-011)

> The old black, not in any very high glee at having been previously roused from his warm hammock at a most unseasonable hour, came shambling along from his galley, for, like many old blacks, there was something the matter with his knee-pans, which he did not keep well scoured like his other pans; this old Fleece, as they called him, came shuffling and limping along, assisting his step with his tongs. (064-012)

> Sullenly taking the offered lantern, old Fleece limped across the deck to the bulwarks; and then, with one hand dropping his light low over the sea, so as to get a good view of his congregation, with the other hand he solemnly flourished his tongs, and leaning far over the side in a mumbling voice began addressing the sharks. (064-014)

> "Well done, old Fleece!" cried Stubb, "that's Christianity; go on." (064-025)

> "All dention," said Fleece, again stooping over upon his tongs in the desired position. (064-031)

> "Up dere," said Fleece, holding his tongs straight over his head, and keeping it there very solemnly. (064-051)
