# Wrath

## Definition

Wrath in *Moby-Dick* is passion with direction — the outward, aggressive face of suffering that distinguishes it from the inward-turning of grief. The novel deploys wrath across a theological spectrum: at one pole, the "storm of God's quick wrath" (008-007) that Father Mapple invokes as divine judgment; at the other, the "whale's direful wrath" (041-018) that is purely creaturely, the animal's response to human aggression. Between them stands Ahab, whose "awakened wrath" (016-068) against the whale is the novel's central engine of action. Melville's innovation is to refuse any simple moral taxonomy of wrath — it can be divine or demonic, justified or mad, creative or destructive — and to insist that wrath, whatever its object, is the one passion the novel's cosmos cannot ignore.

## Manifestations in the Novel

**God's quick wrath (Chapter 8)**. Father Mapple's sermon establishes wrath's theological register. He describes the storm sent after Jonah as "the storm of God's quick wrath" (008-007), where "quick" means both swift and alive — divine wrath is not a settled disposition but an active, pursuing force. The sailors "flee from the wrath to come" (018-022), a phrase repeated from Matthew's gospel, making wrath eschatological: the direction of flight is always from wrath, and the question is whether flight is possible at all.

**Peleg's awakened wrath (Chapter 16)**. The first human wrath in the novel is comic. When Peleg learns of Ahab's condition, Ishmael expects him to "vanish from before the awakened wrath of Peleg" (016-068). But Peleg's wrath is bluster — Quaker bombast that dissipates as quickly as it rises. This early comic wrath establishes a pattern: human wrath in *Moby-Dick* is often performative, a social gesture rather than a genuine passion. Ahab's wrath will later prove the exception.

**The mast-head and God's wrath (Chapter 35)**. Ishmael's meditation on the mast-head watch takes a sudden theological turn: "in the dread gale of God's wrath; therefore, we cannot give the rest" (035-003). Even the contemplative Ishmael cannot escape wrath — it is the medium through which divine power manifests. The mast-head, which should be a site of reverie, becomes another place where wrath threatens.

**The whale's direful wrath (Chapter 41)**. The novel's most concentrated wrath passage describes Moby Dick himself. The whale's whiteness is "the curds of the whale's direful wrath" curdling "into the serene, exasperating sunlight" (041-018). Wrath here is a property of the whale, almost a substance — something that can curdle and spread. The whale is not merely an object of Ahab's wrath but a subject with wrath of its own. The two wraths meet, and the novel is their collision.

**Stubb's whale: wrath relaxed (Chapter 61)**. The death of Stubb's first whale provides wrath's physiological resolution: "the waning whale relaxed in his wrath" (061-021). The phrase "relaxed in his wrath" is almost oxymoronic — wrath as tension, as a holding-in of force, that in death is finally released. The whale's wrath is bodily, not moral; it is the physical response to the harpoon's pain, and its relaxation is the relaxation of life itself.

## Key Passages

- (008-007) — "the storm of God's quick wrath is first descried, and the bow must bear the earliest brunt": wrath as divine meteorological force
- (016-068) — "vanish from before the awakened wrath of Peleg": comic human wrath, performative and passing
- (018-022) — "turn from the wrath to come; mind thine eye": Elijah's prophetic warning, wrath as eschatological direction
- (035-003) — "in the dread gale of God's wrath; therefore, we cannot give the rest": even contemplation is suspended by divine wrath
- (041-018) — "the curds of the whale's direful wrath into the serene, exasperating sunlight": wrath as substance, curdling into the visible world
- (061-021) — "the waning whale relaxed in his wrath": the physiological arc of wrath, from tension to release

## Scholarly Context

Melville's treatment of wrath engages multiple intellectual traditions. The Calvinist doctrine of divine wrath — God's righteous anger against sin — is present but complicated by the novel's suggestion that divine wrath may be indistinguishable from natural violence. Classical epic provides another register: Ahab's wrath echoes Achilles' *menis* in the *Iliad*, the wrath that sets a poem in motion and drives it to its end. Melville's innovation is to distribute wrath across the ontological spectrum — God has wrath, whales have wrath, men have wrath — and to refuse any hierarchy among them. The whale's "direful wrath" is as real and as consequential as Ahab's, and Melville treats it with equal seriousness.
