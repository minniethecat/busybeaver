# Ishmael Signs onto the Pequod

## Overview

In Chapter 16, [[ishmael|Ishmael]] — acting on the mysterious counsel of [[queequeg|Queequeg]]'s idol Yojo — sets out alone to find a whaling ship. After inspecting three vessels at anchor in Nantucket, he selects the [[pequod|Pequod]], a "rare old craft" (016-004) adorned with whalebone and ivory. What follows is one of the novel's great comic set-pieces: Ishmael's negotiation with the ship's two Quaker co-owners, the blustering [[peleg|Captain Peleg]] and the scripture-mumbling [[bildad|Captain Bildad]], over the terms of his lay — the share of the voyage's profits he will receive.

The chapter introduces the Pequod as both a physical object and an economic enterprise, establishes the Quaker-whaleman paradox that runs through the novel, and builds anticipation for [[ahab|Captain Ahab]] — the unseen commander described by Peleg as "a grand, ungodly, god-like man" (016-079).

## Chronology

### Yojo's Decree

The chapter opens with Queequeg's startling announcement: his little black god Yojo has decreed that Ishmael alone must choose their ship. Yojo "had already pitched upon a vessel, which, if left to myself, I, Ishmael, should infallibly light upon, for all the world as though it had turned out by chance" (016-001). Ishmael is skeptical but acquiesces:

> "I did not like that plan at all. I had not a little relied upon Queequeg's sagacity to point out the whaler best fitted to carry us and our fortunes securely." (016-003)

### Choosing the Pequod

Ishmael surveys three ships offering three-year voyages: the *Devil-dam*, the *Tit-bit*, and the *Pequod*. He chooses the Pequod — named for "a celebrated tribe of Massachusetts Indians; now extinct as the ancient Medes" (016-003).

Melville's description of the ship is one of the novel's most elaborate set-pieces:

> "She was a ship of the old school, rather small if anything; with an old-fashioned claw-footed look about her. Long seasoned and weather-stained in the typhoons and calms of all four oceans, her old hull's complexion was darkened like a French grenadier's, who has alike fought in Egypt and Siberia." (016-004)

The Pequod's bulwarks are "garnished like one continuous jaw, with the long sharp teeth of the sperm whale" (016-004). Her tiller is carved from "the long narrow lower jaw of her hereditary foe" (016-004). The ship is "a thing of trophies. A cannibal of a craft, tricking herself forth in the chased bones of her enemies" (016-004).

### Meeting Captain Peleg

Ishmael finds Captain Peleg seated in a wigwam-like structure on the quarter-deck, made from "the long, huge slabs of limber black bone taken from the middle and highest part of the jaws of the right-whale" (016-005). Peleg is "brown and brawny, like most old seamen" with a Quaker cut to his clothing and "a fine and almost microscopic net-work of the minutest wrinkles interlacing round his eyes" (016-007).

When Ishmael asks if he is the captain, Peleg deflects: "Supposing it be the captain of the Pequod, what dost thou want of him?" (016-009). The interview that follows is a blend of intimidation and comedy. Peleg tests Ishmael's motives:

> "Dost know nothing at all about whaling, I dare say — eh?" (016-013)

When Ishmael mentions his merchant service, Peleg erupts: "Merchant service be damned. Talk not that lingo to me. Dost see that leg? — I'll take that leg away from thy stern, if ever thou talkest of the marchant service to me again" (016-015). He accuses Ishmael of possibly being a pirate or murderer: "Hast not been a pirate, hast thou? — Didst not rob thy last Captain, didst thou? — Dost not think of murdering the officers when thou gettest to sea?" (016-015).

### The First Hint of Ahab

When Ishmael says he wants to see what whaling is, Peleg asks: "Want to see what whaling is, eh? Have ye clapped eye on Captain Ahab?" (016-019). Ishmael has not. Peleg reveals that Captain Ahab "is the Captain of this ship" (016-021) and offers a warning:

> "Clap eye on Captain Ahab, young man, and thou wilt find that he has only one leg." (016-023)

When Ishmael asks if a whale took the leg, Peleg's response is visceral: "Lost by a whale! Young man, come nearer to me: it was devoured, chewed up, crunched by the monstrousest parmacetty that ever chipped a boat! — ah, ah!" (016-025).

### The Test of Seeing the World

Peleg challenges Ishmael to look over the bow and report what he sees. Ishmael sees only "nothing but water; considerable horizon though, and there's a squall coming up" (016-037). Peleg's retort is philosophical: "Can't ye see the world where you stand?" (016-038). Ishmael, though "a little staggered," remains determined: "go a-whaling I must, and I would" (016-039).

### Negotiating the Lay

Peleg leads Ishmael below deck into the cabin, where Captain Bildad sits on the transom reading the Scriptures. Bildad is described as an "incorrigible old hunks" (016-045) — a hard-hearted Quaker who, though "a sworn foe to human bloodshed, yet had he in his straight-bodied coat, spilled tuns upon tuns of leviathan gore" (016-044).

The negotiation over Ishmael's lay — his share of the profits — is a masterpiece of comic dialogue. Ishmael, a green hand, expects at least the 275th lay. Bildad suggests the 777th — "the seven hundred and seventy-seventh wouldn't be too much, would it? — 'where moth and rust do corrupt, but lay —'" (016-058), weaving scripture into his stinginess.

Peleg, outraged, counters: "Why, blast your eyes, Bildad, thou dost not want to swindle this young man! he must have more than that" (016-060). He offers the 300th lay. Bildad protests on behalf of "the other owners of this ship — widows and orphans, many of them" (016-063), but Peleg prevails.

The negotiation nearly comes to blows when Bildad suggests Peleg's "conscience be but a leaky one; and will in the end sink thee foundering down to the fiery pit" (016-065). Peleg threatens to "swallow a live goat with all his hair and horns on" (016-066) and rushes at Bildad, who "with a marvellous oblique, sliding celerity" (016-067) eludes him. The storm passes as quickly as it arose: "the squall's gone off to leeward, I think" (016-068).

### Signing and the Unseen Captain

Ishmael signs for the 300th lay and mentions Queequeg as another hand. Bildad perks up only to ask: "What lay does he want?" (016-071). After signing, Ishmael realizes he has committed himself to a captain he has never seen. Peleg's description of Ahab closes the chapter:

> "He's a grand, ungodly, god-like man, Captain Ahab; doesn't speak much; but, when he does speak, then you may well listen. Mark ye, be forewarned; Ahab's above the common; Ahab's been in colleges, as well as 'mong the cannibals; been used to deeper wonders than the waves; fixed his fiery lance in mightier, stranger foes than whales." (016-079)

## Narrative Significance

Chapter 16 is the novel's true embarkation. While earlier chapters establish Ishmael's sensibility (Chapter 1), his friendship with Queequeg (Chapters 3–12), and the symbolic world of Nantucket (Chapters 13–15), it is here that Ishmael commits himself irrevocably to the voyage. The chapter is economic, comic, and ominous by turns.

The negotiation over the lay is more than comic relief. It introduces the whaling industry's economic structure — "in the whaling business they paid no wages; but all hands, including the captain, received certain shares of the profits called lays" (016-054) — and establishes that Ishmael's stake in the voyage is, literally, a gamble. He signs for a fraction of an unknown sum from an enterprise whose captain he has never met.

The Quaker-whaleman paradox — Bildad reading "Lay not up for yourselves treasures upon earth" while haggling over the 777th lay — is one of Melville's sharpest satires of American capitalism. These "fighting Quakers" are "Quakers with a vengeance" (016-042), men whose professed piety coexists with ruthless commercial instinct.

Peleg's description of Ahab — "a grand, ungodly, god-like man" — is the novel's first thumbnail portrait of its absent center. The triple epithet captures Ahab's essential paradox: he is grand (magnificent), ungodly (defiant), and god-like (possessed of powers beyond the human). The chapter ends not with the ship or the crew but with Ahab's name, establishing him as the gravitational center toward which all else is drawn.

## Aftermath / Consequences

Ishmael is now a member of the Pequod's crew for the three hundredth lay. The following morning, he brings Queequeg to sign the articles — Queequeg who, having "killed more whales than I can count" (016-073), impresses Peleg and Bildad by demonstrating his harpooning skill with a nail-studded harpoon. The two are now irrevocably bound to the Pequod, to Ahab, and to the fate that awaits them in the Pacific.

The chapter also plants the seeds of the novel's long middle section. Ishmael's ignorance of whaling becomes the pretext for the cetological chapters; his economic stake — "better than nothing; and if we had a lucky voyage, might pretty nearly pay for the clothing I would wear out on it" (016-054) — grounds the narrative in material reality; and Ahab's deferred appearance builds the dramatic tension that will explode in Chapter 28, when the captain finally walks the quarter-deck.
