# The Hat

## Definition

The incident of "The Hat" occurs in Chapter 130 of *Moby-Dick*, when Ahab, having taken his post at the masthead to personally watch for the White Whale, is robbed of his hat by a black sea-hawk. The bird snatches the hat from his head and drops it into the sea — a moment Melville explicitly compares to the classical omen of the eagle that removed and replaced Tarquin's cap, signaling his future kingship of Rome. Where the Roman omen promised elevation, Ahab's inverted omen promises only descent: the hat is taken, never returned, and swallowed by the sea.

## Manifestations in the Novel

The hat episode occurs at a moment of gathering tension. Chapter 130, titled simply "The Hat," opens by establishing the narrowing scope of Ahab's world: "all humor, forced or natural, vanished. Stubb no more strove to raise a smile; Starbuck no more strove to check one" (130-002). The Pequod has entered the final ocean-fold where Ahab believes Moby Dick must be found, and the captain has become increasingly distrustful of his crew's watchfulness, resolving to take the first sighting himself.

Ahab rigs a basket of bowlines and is hoisted aloft to the main-masthead, where he sits scanning the horizon. Within ten minutes, a "red-billed savage sea-hawk" flies close by and, in a passage Melville narrates with almost comic abruptness, steals the hat: "'Your hat, your hat, sir!' suddenly cried the Sicilian seaman... But already the sable wing was before the old man's eyes; the long hooked bill at his head: with a scream, the black hawk darted away with his prize" (130-013–014).

What makes this moment portentous rather than merely farcical is Melville's immediate invocation of classical precedent: "An eagle flew thrice round Tarquin's head, removing his cap to replace it, and thereupon Tanaquil, his wife, declared that Tarquin would be king of Rome. But only by the replacing of the cap was that omen accounted good" (130-015). Ahab's hat is not replaced. The sea "jammed" it between iron and wood, and it is never recovered — a dark inversion of the auspicious Roman sign.

The omen is a double signal. It is simultaneously a piece of physical bad luck (a bird steals a hat, the sea swallows it) and a narrative device that confirms what the reader already suspects: Ahab will not return. Melville frames this in mythic terms but anchors it in the mundane detail of a masthead watch gone wrong — characteristic of the novel's ability to find cosmic significance in the accidents of whaling life.

## Key Passages

> "'Your hat, your hat, sir!' suddenly cried the Sicilian seaman, who being posted at the mizen-mast-head, stood directly behind Ahab, though somewhat lower than his level, and with a deep gulf of air dividing them."
> (130-013)

> "But already the sable wing was before the old man's eyes; the long hooked bill at his head: with a scream, the black hawk darted away with his prize."
> (130-014)

> "An eagle flew thrice round Tarquin's head, removing his cap to replace it, and thereupon Tanaquil, his wife, declared that Tarquin would be king of Rome. But only by the replacing of the cap was that omen accounted good. Ahab's hat was never restored."
> (130-015)

> "In this foreshadowing interval too, all humor, forced or natural, vanished. Stubb no more strove to raise a smile; Starbuck no more strove to check one. Alike, joy and sorrow, hope and fear, seemed ground to finest dust, and powdered, for the time, in the clamped mortar of Ahab's iron soul."
> (130-002)
