# The Carpenter

## Definition

The carpenter of the Pequod is more than a minor character — he is a philosophical figure through whom Melville examines the nature of work, personality, and the relationship between human consciousness and the material world. Introduced in Chapter 107 and featured in Chapter 108 ("Ahab and the Carpenter"), he is described as "no duplicate" (107-001) in a world of interchangeable men, yet his distinctiveness lies paradoxically in his very impersonality. He is a "pure manipulator" whose brain "must have early oozed along into the muscles of his fingers" (107-005). He is the maker of both Ahab's prosthetic ivory leg and Queequeg's coffin — two of the novel's most symbolically freighted objects.

## Manifestations in the Novel

Chapter 107 opens with a general philosophical prologue on human individuality before introducing the carpenter specifically: "Seat thyself sultanically among the moons of Saturn, and take high abstracted man alone; and he seems a wonder, a grandeur, and a woe. But from the same point, take mankind in mass, and for the most part, they seem a mob of unnecessary duplicates" (107-001). The carpenter, though "most humble," is declared exempt from this mass duplication.

Melville inventories the carpenter's skills exhaustively. He repairs stove boats and sprung spars, reforms oars, inserts bull's-eyes in the deck, and new tree-nails in side planks. But beyond his official duties, "he was moreover unhesitatingly expert in all manner of conflicting aptitudes, both useful and capricious" (107-002). He makes a pagoda-shaped cage for a stray bird out of whalebone and sperm whale ivory. He concocts a soothing lotion for a sprained wrist. He paints Stubb's requested vermillion stars on oar-blades. He even serves as the ship's dentist, drilling sailors' ears for shark-bone earrings and extracting teeth with his vice.

Yet Melville insists that this versatility does not signal "uncommon vivacity of intelligence." The carpenter is remarkable "for a certain impersonal stolidity as it were; impersonal, I say; for it so shaded off into the surrounding infinite of things, that it seemed one with the general stolidity discernible in the whole visible world" (107-005). He is compared to a multi-tool pocket knife — "containing, not only blades of various sizes, but also screw-drivers, cork-screws, tweezers, awls, pens, rulers, nail-filers, countersinkers" (107-005). His superiors use him as they would any tool.

In Chapter 108, Ahab visits the carpenter at his vice-bench to check the progress of his new ivory leg. Their dialogue is a dark comedy of mutual incomprehension. Ahab speaks in metaphysical rhapsodies about Prometheus and the creation of a new, improved man; the carpenter responds with practical literalism and sneezes from bone-dust. Yet it is this same carpenter who will later build Queequeg's coffin — which becomes the life-buoy that saves Ishmael.

## Scholarly Context

The carpenter has been read as Melville's figure for the artist or craftsman whose work transcends personality. His "impersonal stolidity" has been compared to Keats's negative capability and to Emersonian notions of transparent agency. Critics have also noted the carpenter's role in the novel's meditation on prosthetics and bodily wholeness: he makes Ahab's ivory leg, literally replacing the limb the white whale took. The carpenter's transformation of Queequeg's coffin into Ishmael's life-buoy — an object of death becoming an instrument of survival — has been read as Melville's figure for the artist's power of transfiguration through craft.
