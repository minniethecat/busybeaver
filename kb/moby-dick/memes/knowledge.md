# Knowledge

## Definition

Knowledge in *Moby-Dick* is a problem, not a possession. Ishmael opens the Cetology chapter by quoting a "late consumptive usher" who lamented "the impenetrable veil covering our knowledge of the cetacea" (032-005), and the entire novel might be read as an extended demonstration of that impenetrability. Melville distinguishes between kinds of knowing — book-knowledge, experiential knowledge, intuitive knowledge, and the knowledge that comes only through suffering — and consistently privileges the embodied and the partial over the systematic and the totalizing. "Of real knowledge there be little," Ishmael admits, "yet of books there are a plenty" (032-006). The whale, finally, is the figure of what exceeds knowledge: it can be pursued, studied, dissected, and even killed, but it cannot be fully grasped. To know the whale would be to possess it, and Melville's deepest insight is that some things exist to resist knowledge.

## Manifestations in the Novel

**The cetological veil (Chapter 32)**. The Cetology chapter is the novel's most elaborate performance of the limits of knowledge. Ishmael attempts a systematic classification of whales — Folio, Octavo, Duodecimo — but the system keeps breaking down. He confesses: "the impenetrable veil covering our knowledge of the cetacea" (032-005) is the starting condition. The chapter piles detail upon detail not to achieve knowledge but to demonstrate that total knowledge is impossible. "Of real knowledge there be little, yet of books there are a plenty" (032-006) — a sentence that might stand as the novel's epistemological motto. Book-knowledge is a substitute for, not a form of, real knowledge.

**Experiential knowledge versus hearsay (Chapter 16)**. When Peleg boasts of his "certain knowledge" of thirty years at sea (016-047), Ishmael immediately undercuts the claim by noting the gap between Peleg's confident assertions and the reality of the whale. Melville repeatedly contrasts the "certain knowledge" of experienced whalemen with the deeper uncertainty the novel itself cultivates. Bildad's biblical knowledge and Peleg's practical knowledge are both partial; neither captures the whale.

**Knowledge held down (Chapter 38)**. Starbuck, in his soliloquy, describes himself as a man "with soul beat down and held to knowledge, — as wild, untutored things are forced to feed" (038-004). Knowledge here is not liberation but constraint — something the soul is "held to" against its will. Starbuck *knows* Ahab is leading them to destruction, and this knowledge is a burden, not a power. The image of the untutored creature "forced to feed" suggests that knowledge is a kind of forced consumption, something the soul must digest whether it wants to or not.

**Restricted knowledge of the White Whale (Chapter 41)**. Ishmael admits that his knowledge of Moby Dick is limited: his "sole knowledge of the leviathan is restricted to the armchair" (041-006). The admission is a joke — Ishmael has been at sea — but it reveals a serious point: even direct experience yields only partial knowledge. Ahab's quest is driven by the conviction that there *is* a hidden knowledge behind the whale's whiteness, a secret agent responsible for his suffering. The novel suggests this is Ahab's great error: the whale is not withholding knowledge; it simply exceeds the category.

**Knowledge and demonism (Chapter 42)**. In the whiteness chapter, Ishmael speculates that the whale's color might produce "the instinct of the knowledge of the demonism in the world" (042-027). This is knowledge not as rational understanding but as intuitive recognition of evil — an "instinct" rather than a deduction. Melville's phrase "instinct of the knowledge" captures the paradox: we can *feel* what we cannot *prove*. The whale's whiteness triggers this instinctive knowledge of cosmic malevolence, but the knowledge remains at the level of instinct — it cannot be articulated or verified.

**The Towns-Ho story: knowledge by strange transmission (Chapter 54)**. The story of the Towns-Ho reaches the Pequod through a chain of tellings so improbable that Ishmael marvels at "those who came to the full knowledge of it, and by such a strange and irresistible convulsion of their own" (054-003). Here knowledge is not sought but received — it arrives through "strange convulsion," a bodily transmission that bypasses rational verification. The Towns-Ho story itself, with its themes of mutiny and divine intervention, is a kind of knowledge that cannot be verified but must be *believed*.

**Exact knowledge of bones (Chapter 102)**. Ishmael's visit to the whale skeleton in the Arsacides leads him to claim "my exact knowledge of the bones of the leviathan" (102-004). The phrase is characteristically ironic — "exact knowledge" of dead bones is not knowledge of the living whale. The skeleton is knowable precisely because it is dead; the living whale evades the categories that successfully describe its remains.

**Muffled knowledge (Chapter 106)**. When Ahab's whalebone leg shatters, he orders the carpenter "to muffle up the knowledge of this thing from others" (106-004). Knowledge, here, is a secret to be concealed — Ahab's vulnerability must not be known by the crew. The scene inverts the novel's usual epistemology: knowledge is not something humans fail to attain but something they actively suppress.

**Knowledge that comes too late (Chapter 128)**. The Rachel, having lost a whaleboat with the captain's son aboard, encounters the Pequod, and Ishmael reflects that "their first knowledge of a whaleman's career shall be its final catastrophe" (128-013). The sentence is about the son's fate, but it also serves as a definition of tragic knowledge: understanding that arrives only after the event, too late to change anything. This is knowledge as elegy — the form of knowing that the novel itself performs.

## Key Passages

- (009-041) — God "acknowledges no law or lord, but the Lord": divine knowledge as absolute, the unknowable God who commands Jonah.
- (016-047) — "Thirty years, to my certain knowledge": Peleg's confident knowing, immediately undercut by the whale's resistance to certainty.
- (032-005) — "The impenetrable veil covering our knowledge of the cetacea": the novel's epistemological motto, knowledge as always obstructed.
- (032-006) — "Of real knowledge there be little, yet of books there are a plenty": the gap between book-learning and genuine understanding.
- (038-004) — "With soul beat down and held to knowledge": knowledge as burden, the soul constrained by what it knows.
- (041-006) — Ishmael's "sole knowledge of the leviathan is restricted to the armchair": the irony of partial knowledge even from direct experience.
- (042-027) — "The instinct of the knowledge of the demonism in the world": intuitive knowledge, felt rather than proven.
- (054-003) — The Towns-Ho story known "by such a strange and irresistible convulsion": knowledge as transmission, arriving through bodily convulsion.
- (102-004) — "My exact knowledge of the bones of the leviathan": the irony of knowing only what is dead.
- (106-004) — Ahab orders "to muffle up the knowledge of this thing from others": knowledge actively suppressed, vulnerability concealed.
- (128-013) — "Their first knowledge of a whaleman's career shall be its final catastrophe": tragic knowledge, understanding that arrives too late.

## Scholarly Context

Melville's epistemology has been a major concern of Melville studies since at least the mid-twentieth century. The Cetology chapter's elaborate failure to classify whales has been read as Melville's parody of Enlightenment taxonomy — a demonstration that living creatures resist the categories by which science tries to fix them. More recently, post-structuralist and deconstructive readings have focused on knowledge in *Moby-Dick* as an instance of *différance*: meaning is always deferred, never arriving. Eco-critical scholars have argued that the whale's resistance to knowledge is not a failure of epistemology but a form of animal agency — the whale *refuses* to be known, and this refusal is its power. The distinction between book-knowledge and experiential knowledge has been linked to Melville's own autodidacticism and his skepticism toward formal education — Ishmael's knowing, like Melville's, is assembled from fragments, quotations, and direct encounters.
