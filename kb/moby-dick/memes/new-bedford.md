# New Bedford

## Overview

New Bedford, Massachusetts, is the great whaling port where Ishmael arrives at the beginning of *Moby-Dick*, having traveled from Manhattan. By the 1850s it had surpassed Nantucket as the busiest whaling harbor in the world, and Melville devotes several chapters to its streets, inns, chapel, and people. The town functions as the novel's gateway between the land world and the sea world — a place where "actual cannibals stand chatting at street corners" and where whaling wealth has produced "patrician-like houses; parks and gardens more opulent, than in New Bedford" (006-005).

## In the Novel

New Bedford appears primarily in the early chapters (Chapters 2–13), as Ishmael waits for the next packet to Nantucket. Arriving on a "dark and dismal night, bitingly cold and cheerless" in December, he wanders the streets looking for affordable lodging (002-003). He visits the Spouter-Inn, where he meets Queequeg, and the Whaleman's Chapel, where Father Mapple delivers his famous sermon on Jonah.

The town is described in vivid detail in Chapter 6, where Ishmael takes a daylight stroll and marvels at the mix of wealth and wildness. Melville contrasts New Bedford's opulent mansions and blooming women with the "cannibals" and "savages outright" on its street corners — the multiracial crew of the global whaling industry already visible on shore.

In Chapter 13, Ishmael and Queequeg depart New Bedford aboard the schooner for Nantucket, watching as "New Bedford rose in terraces of streets, their ice-covered trees all glittering in the clear, cold air" (013-005).

## Geographical Context

New Bedford lies on the Acushnet River in southeastern Massachusetts, about 50 miles south of Boston. In the mid-19th century it was the wealthiest city per capita in North America, its prosperity built entirely on whaling. Melville himself sailed from New Bedford aboard the whaler *Acushnet* in 1841 — the voyage that would provide the raw experience for *Moby-Dick*. The town's Whaleman's Chapel (Seamen's Bethel) still stands today.

## Symbolic Significance

New Bedford represents the ambiguous threshold between land and sea, civilization and wilderness. It is simultaneously the "dearest place to live in, in all New England" and a town where "actual cannibals stand chatting at street corners." This duality mirrors the novel's larger tension between the comfortable land-world and the perilous but sublime ocean. The Tyre-and-Carthage comparison (002-002) frames New Bedford as the commercial successor to Nantucket's original whaling spirit — prosperous but derivative, rich but already in decline.
