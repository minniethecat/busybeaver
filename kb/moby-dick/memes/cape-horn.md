# Cape Horn

## Overview

Cape Horn is the southernmost headland of the Tierra del Fuego archipelago in Chile, where the Atlantic and Pacific Oceans meet in notoriously violent seas. In *Moby-Dick*, Cape Horn is the great maritime gate that every whaling voyage must pass — a place of physical extremity, danger, and transformation. Ishmael declares his intention from the very first page: "I stuffed a shirt or two into my old carpet-bag, tucked it under my arm, and started for Cape Horn and the Pacific" (002-001).

## In the Novel

Cape Horn serves as the boundary between the known world and the whaling grounds. It is referenced across the novel as a benchmark of maritime hardship. Captain Peleg challenges Ishmael and Queequeg with the question: "Do ye wish to go round Cape Horn to see any more of it, eh? Can't ye see the world where you stand?" (016-038). For the whalemen, rounding the Horn is a rite of passage — a "three years' voyage round Cape Horn was only a jolly joke that lasted a long time" (027-004).

Ahab's personal mythology is tied to the Cape. His lost leg, his "Cape Horn fit," and his silver calabash are all associated with the passage (019-034). After rounding the Horn, Starbuck reflects that Ahab "seemed left behind him with the Cape Horn swells, and he came forth from his cabin with a changed aspect" (041-020).

## Geographical Context

Cape Horn lies at 55°59' S, 67°16' W, on Isla Hornos in the Hermite Islands group. Before the Panama Canal opened in 1914, it was the primary sailing route between the Atlantic and Pacific Oceans. The Drake Passage between Cape Horn and Antarctica is one of the stormiest bodies of water on Earth. In Melville's era, a whaling voyage from New England to the Pacific whaling grounds required rounding the Horn — a passage that could take weeks of beating against westerly gales.

## Symbolic Significance

Cape Horn represents the threshold between worlds — the civilized Atlantic and the wild Pacific, the known and the unknown. Passing it transforms the whalemen: Ahab's character intensifies, the voyage becomes more perilous, and the Pequod enters the domain where Moby Dick awaits. The Cape's notorious storms also make it a symbol of nature's indifference to human endeavor — a preview of the greater indifference the crew will face in the open Pacific.
