# The Jeroboam's Story

## Overview

The Jeroboam's Story (Chapter 71) is the Pequod's second gam — a meeting with the Nantucket whaler *Jeroboam*, which is carrying a malignant epidemic and a mad prophet. The prophet, Gabriel, is a former Shaker who has convinced most of the crew that he is the archangel Gabriel, with power over the plague and the fate of the ship.

When [[ahab|Ahab]] asks the customary question — "Hast seen the White Whale?" — Gabriel answers for the captain. He has not only seen Moby Dick; he has prophesied against hunting him. One of the Jeroboam's mates, who defied Gabriel's warning, was killed by the White Whale. Gabriel now extends the same warning to Ahab: "Think, think of the blasphemer — dead, and down there! — beware of the blasphemer's end!"

Ahab ignores him. But Gabriel's curse — delivered across the water as the ships separate — is one of the most chilling moments in the novel: the mad prophet holds up a vial and shakes it toward the Pequod, as if loosing the plague.

## Chronology

### The Gam

The Pequod sights the Jeroboam, a Nantucket whaler. Because of an epidemic aboard, Captain Mayhew refuses to board the Pequod; the conversation is conducted between the two vessels, with the Jeroboam's boat keeping a cautious distance (071-004–005).

### Gabriel

In the Jeroboam's boat is a figure of "singular appearance" — small, freckled, with "redundant yellow hair" and a "deep, settled, fanatic delirium" in his eyes (071-006). Stubb immediately recognizes him from a story told by the Town-Ho's crew: this is Gabriel, the Shaker prophet who has seized spiritual control of the Jeroboam.

Gabriel's history is extraordinary. Originally from the "crazy society of Neskyeuna Shakers," he claimed to have "several times descended from heaven by the way of a trap-door" (071-008). He signed onto the Jeroboam as a green hand, but once at sea revealed himself as the archangel Gabriel, "commanded the captain to jump overboard," and "published his manifesto, whereby he set himself forth as the deliverer of the isles of the sea and vicar-general of all Oceanica" (071-008). The crew, terrified and awed, now obeys him.

### Moby Dick's Victim

Ahab asks the question: has the Jeroboam seen the White Whale? Gabriel answers. The Jeroboam encountered Moby Dick, and Gabriel warned against lowering for him. The chief mate defied Gabriel. Moby Dick killed him.

The mate's death has become Gabriel's sermon: the White Whale is the Shaker God incarnate, and those who hunt him are blasphemers. Gabriel extends this warning to Ahab:

> "Think, think of the blasphemer — dead, and down there! — beware of the blasphemer's end!"

### The Separation

Ahab, characteristically, is undeterred. As the ships separate, Gabriel holds up a vial — "the seventh vial" of Revelation — and shakes it toward the Pequod, calling down divine judgment on Ahab's quest. The image fuses plague, prophecy, and the White Whale into a single terrifying gesture.

## Narrative Significance

The Jeroboam gam expands the novel's mythology of Moby Dick. The whale is not merely Ahab's obsession; he has a history that precedes the Pequod. Gabriel's warning — that Moby Dick is an agent of divine judgment — is one of several interpretations the novel offers, and it is neither confirmed nor denied. The whale kills Gabriel's skeptic just as it kills Ahab. Is Gabriel a true prophet or a madman? Melville refuses to decide.

The chapter also deepens the novel's interest in the psychology of belief. Gabriel is clearly insane, yet his power over the crew is absolute. The captain, nominally in command, cannot act against him. The Jeroboam is a ship ruled by a madman's theology — and the Pequod, with Ahab at the helm, is not so different.

## Aftermath / Consequences

Gabriel's curse hangs over the remaining chapters. The "seventh vial" he shakes toward the Pequod is never explicitly fulfilled, but the novel's final catastrophe — the sinking of the Pequod, the death of all but Ishmael — can be read as the vial's pouring out. Whether Gabriel is a prophet or a lunatic, his warning proves accurate: those who hunt the White Whale die.
