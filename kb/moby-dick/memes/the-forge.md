# The Forge

## Definition

In Chapters 112–113 of *Moby-Dick*, the Pequod's forge — operated by Perth, the ship's blacksmith — becomes a site of symbolic intensity. The forge is where iron is heated and hammered into harpoons, lances, and the specially barbed iron intended for the White Whale. More than a practical workspace, Melville invests the forge with Promethean and demonic overtones: it is the place where Ahab's abstract obsession is literally forged into deadly instruments. Fire, iron, sweat, and prophetic intensity converge in this below-decks space.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **Perth's backstory**: The blacksmith's personal history of ruin — he lost his family and home to drink — makes him a figure of penance and quiet craftsmanship, a foil to Ahab's grandiose vengeance.
- **The tempering of the harpoon**: Ahab demands that Perth forge a special barbed iron from the steel of razors, and in an almost ritualistic scene, the harpoon is tempered not in water but in the blood of the three pagan harpooneers (Queequeg, Tashtego, and Daggoo).
- **Promethean symbolism**: Ahab explicitly invokes Prometheus, casting the forge as a site of titanic rebellion against divine order. "The blacksmith was commanded to proceed at once to the forging of the harpoon."
- **The forge as hell-mouth**: The glowing coals, the ringing hammer, and the enclosed space below decks transform the forge into an infernal workshop where Ahab's dark purpose takes material form.
