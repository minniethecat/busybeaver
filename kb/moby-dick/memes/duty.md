# Duty

## Definition

Duty in Moby-Dick is the moral and professional adhesive that holds the whaling world together. It operates on multiple registers: the religious duty that Jonah flees, the contractual duty that binds sailors to ship-owners, the professional duty of each man at his station, and the paradoxical "cheerful duty" of attending to a friend. Melville presents duty not as simple obligation but as a force that can be exploited (Ahab uses the crew's sense of duty to pursue his private revenge), evaded (Jonah's flight), or transformed into pleasure (the "sweet and unctuous duty" of squeezing spermaceti). Throughout the novel, duty and profit, duty and conscience, duty and self-preservation are held in constant tension.

## Manifestations in the Novel

**Jonah's flight from divine duty (Chapter 9)**. Father Mapple's sermon establishes duty's theological dimension. Jonah "sought to escape his duty and his God by taking ship at Joppa" (009-039), a sin of omission that brings calamity. Mapple warns that the world "charms from Gospel duty" (009-040), substituting worldly concerns for divine obligation. This frames the Pequod's voyage: every sailor on board has, in some sense, signed away a higher duty for a lower one.

**The contractual duty of the voyage (Chapter 16)**. When Ishmael signs the Pequod's articles, Peleg reminds him: "thou must consider the duty thou owest to the other owners of this ship" (016-063). Duty here is transactional — a legal bond enforced by the ship's papers. Bildad's Quaker piety adds a sanctimonious layer, but the underlying structure is economic: duty as the price of one's "lay."

**The harpooneer's office (Chapter 35, 72)**. The mast-head standers have a "duty" (035-007) to keep watch, though they are prone to reverie rather than vigilance. More concretely, Queequeg's role as harpooneer creates a specific duty: "it was his duty, as harpooneer, to descend upon the monster's back" (072-001). Ishmael's corresponding "cheerful duty" is to attend Queequeg during the perilous task: "it was my cheerful duty to attend upon him while taking that hard-scrabble scramble" (072-002). Here duty becomes a form of friendship — obligation willingly embraced.

**Duty and profit hand in hand (Chapter 48)**. During the first lowering, the crew's motivation is crystallized: "This at least is duty; duty and profit hand in hand!" (048-018). Melville's irony is sharp — the men risk their lives, and the mate justifies it by merging moral obligation with economic incentive. The line captures the novel's persistent fusion (and confusion) of moral and commercial imperatives.

**Steelkit's rebellion and the limits of duty (Chapter 54)**. The Town-Ho story dramatizes when duty ceases to be binding. Radney demands the crew's duty but abuses his authority. When the crisis comes, "the probability was that the deck happen to forget his duty in that respect" (054-010) — duty dissolves under the pressure of injustice. After Radney's death, the crew is ordered "instantly to return to their duty" (054-037), restoring the hierarchical order. Steelkilt, however, remains in the hold — his duty is to his own conscience, not to the mate who wronged him.

**The "sweet and unctuous duty" (Chapter 94)**. In one of the novel's most sensual passages, duty becomes pleasure. Squeezing the spermaceti globules back into liquid is described as a "sweet and unctuous duty" (094-003). This transformation of labor into delight — "No wonder that in old times this sperm was such a favorite cosmetic" — offers a rare vision of duty without coercion, where obligation and joy coincide.

## Key Passages

- (009-039) — Jonah "sought to escape his duty and his God by taking ship at Joppa" — the archetypal flight from duty.
- (016-063) — Peleg to Ishmael: "thou must consider the duty thou owest to the other owners of this ship" — contractual duty.
- (018-022) — Bildad: "Son of darkness, I must do my duty by thee; I am part owner of this ship" — duty as moral self-justification.
- (048-018) — "This at least is duty; duty and profit hand in hand!" — duty and economic incentive fused.
- (054-010) — "if the deck happen to forget his duty in that respect" — the fragility of enforced duty under injustice.
- (054-086) — "when fast to a fish, it was his duty to sit next him" — the harpooneer's specific professional duty.
- (072-001) — Queequeg's "duty, as harpooneer, to descend upon the monster's back" — the perilous office.
- (072-002) — Ishmael's "cheerful duty to attend upon him" — duty transformed into friendship.
- (094-003) — "A sweet and unctuous duty!" — labor as pleasure, the apotheosis of duty in the novel.
- (069-002) — "The tackles have now done their duty" — duty extended even to inanimate objects on the whaling ship.

## Scholarly Context

Duty in Moby-Dick has been read through multiple critical lenses. Political theorists note the Hobbesian structure: the ship requires a sovereign to whom duty is owed, without which the vessel descends into the war of all against all. Labor historians emphasize the economic basis of shipboard duty — the "lay" system that made each sailor a shareholder in the voyage, converting duty into self-interest. Religious readings trace the Calvinist language of "Gospel duty" through Father Mapple's sermon and into the novel's moral architecture. More recently, affect theorists have drawn attention to the "sweet and unctuous duty" of Chapter 94 as a vision of non-alienated labor, where duty and desire converge in physical pleasure.
