# The Chase

## Overview

The Chase is the catastrophic three-day pursuit of [[the-white-whale|Moby Dick]] that forms the climax of Herman Melville's *Moby-Dick*.

Spanning Chapters 133, 134, and 135, it is one of the most sustained action sequences in nineteenth-century literature — a crescendo of violence that moves from pursuit through battle to annihilation.

[[ahab|Captain Ahab]] leads his boats against the White Whale three times. On the first day, the whale smashes Ahab's boat. On the second, the whale destroys the boats again and [[fedallah|Fedallah]] is killed. On the third, Moby Dick rams the [[pequod|Pequod]] itself, sinking the ship and dragging Ahab to his death.

Only [[ishmael|Ishmael]] survives, buoyed by [[queequeg|Queequeg]]'s coffin.

## Chronology

### The First Day (Chapter 133)

Ahab himself sights Moby Dick. "There she blows! — there she blows! A hump like a snow-hill! It is Moby Dick!" (133-001).

The boats are lowered. Ahab takes the lead. Moby Dick dives and resurfaces beneath Ahab's boat, biting it in two.

The whale swims away, the shattered boat and crew left bobbing. Ahab is rescued by the other boats. The Pequod picks everyone up.

That night, Ahab paces the deck. [[starbuck|Starbuck]] does not sleep.

### The Second Day (Chapter 134)

At dawn, Moby Dick is sighted again. Three boats are lowered.

The whale charges. Boats are shattered. The harpooneers strike but cannot hold.

Fedallah is caught in the line and dragged under — killed by the whale he came to hunt. The first part of his prophecy is fulfilled: he dies before Ahab.

Ahab's whale-bone leg is splintered. He is carried back to the [[pequod|Pequod]]. The crew is battered, exhausted, terrified.

Starbuck begs Ahab to stop. "Moby Dick seeks thee not. It is thou, thou, that madly seekest him!" (134-020).

Ahab refuses.

### The Third Day (Chapter 135)

Ahab lowers again, despite all warnings.

> "For when three days flow together in one continuous intense pursuit, be sure the first is the morning, the second the noon, and the third the evening and the end of it." (135-028)

The boats seek the whale. Moby Dick surfaces. He turns on the boats. The crew sees the corpse of Fedallah, lashed to the whale by tangled lines — the second part of the prophecy fulfilled.

Moby Dick attacks the Pequod. The ship goes down.

Ahab hurls his last harpoon. The line catches around his neck. He is dragged under, "voicelessly as Turkish mutes bowstring their victim" (135-059).

The Pequod sinks. The whirlpool pulls everything under. Only Ishmael remains.

## The Prophecy Fulfilled

Fedallah's prophecy governs the chase's structure:

| Prophecy | Fulfillment |
|----------|------------|
| Ahab will see two hearses | The White Whale (hearse not made by mortal hands); the Pequod (hearse of American wood) |
| Only hemp can kill Ahab | The harpoon line around his neck |
| Fedallah dies first; Ahab will see him again | Fedallah's corpse tied to Moby Dick on the third day |

The prophecy is not a prediction but a narrative architecture — each element locks into place with the inexorability of fate.

## Narrative Significance

The Chase is the narrative destination toward which the entire novel has been moving.

Every earlier chapter — the cetological digressions, the gam encounters, the philosophical meditations — finds its resolution here. The novel's symbolic machinery converts to plot machinery.

The chase transforms *Moby-Dick* from a whaling novel into an apocalyptic vision. Melville draws on the Book of Revelation, Paradise Lost, and classical tragedy to give the three days mythic weight.

Ahab's death is simultaneously a defeat (the whale wins) and a consummation (Ahab achieves the encounter he sought).

## Aftermath

The Pequod is gone. Of her crew, only Ishmael survives, clinging to Queequeg's coffin until the [[rachel|Rachel]] — still searching for her lost children — picks him up.

The "great shroud of the sea" rolls on. Nothing marks the spot where the Pequod sank.

Ishmael lives to tell the tale — "and I only am escaped alone to tell thee" (Epilogue-001) — but he is forever changed, the sole survivor of a catastrophe that consumed everyone else.

## Critical Analysis

The Chase has been read as:

- **American tragedy**: The destruction of the Pequod as the fate of American exceptionalism — the ship named after an exterminated tribe, destroyed by the force it sought to dominate.
- **Cosmic futility**: Ahab's quest as emblematic of human striving against an indifferent universe.
- **Ecological revenge**: The whale striking back — nature's retaliation against industrial predation.

The chase's power lies in its refusal to settle on a single meaning. It is simultaneously triumph, tragedy, justice, and catastrophe.
