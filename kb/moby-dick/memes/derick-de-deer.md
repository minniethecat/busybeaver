# Derick De Deer

## Overview

Derick De Deer is the German captain of the Bremen whaler Jungfrau, encountered by the [[pequod|Pequod]] in [[Ch. 81: The Pequod Meets the Jungfrau]]. Short on lamp oil and desperate, he hails the Pequod for a supply, only to sprint away the moment a whale is sighted — his boat giving chase before his oil-casks are even secured. The episode is one of the novel's most sustained comic set-pieces, and Derick serves as a satirical portrait of German inefficiency in the whaling industry (081-001–081-048).

## Role in the Narrative

Derick appears solely in Chapter 81, but his role as a comic foil to Ahab and the Pequod is carefully constructed. When the Jungfrau meets the Pequod, Derick — "master, of Bremen" — boards and delivers "a lantern-slide show of his various necessities" (081-007). His ship is clean out of oil, a humiliating condition for any whaler, and the scene lays bare the absurd economics of the industry: one ship begging fuel from another mid-ocean.

The comedy turns to slapstick when a whale is sighted. Derick abandons all pretense and rushes back to his boat, lowering away in frantic haste. The Pequod's boats join the chase, and the encounter becomes a race — Derick's boat initially leads, but the Pequod's men, driven by Ahab's ferocity, overtake and kill the whale. The episode ends with "both Derick and his baffled harpooneer" pitched into the sea by the whale's death-throes (081-027), while the Pequod claims the prize.

- [[Ch. 81: The Pequod Meets the Jungfrau]](081-001–081-048) — The full encounter: Derick's plea for oil; the whale sighting; the race between boats; Derick's initial lead and ultimate humiliation; his boat crew "sprawling about in the sea"; and Melville's parting reflection on "the many Dericks" who pursue the whale

## Character Traits

**Comic ineptitude** defines Derick as a whaleman. Despite his "indubitably conducted" grampus-like determination (081-007), he loses a race that he had won at the start — a parable, Melville suggests, of German whaling in general. His ship's very name, Jungfrau ("Virgin"), underscores his inexperience; he is a virgin among whalers, outmatched by the Pequod's hardened crew.

His **desperation** is both comic and poignant. The Jungfrau has no oil — the fundamental currency of the whaling industry — and Derick must beg from a rival. When opportunity appears in the form of a whale, his frantic haste reveals a man who sees the whale not as a noble adversary but as a desperately needed source of fuel. His needs are practical, not metaphysical: he wants oil, not vengeance.

Melville uses Derick as a vehicle for **national satire**. The chapter's closing reflection — "many are the Fin-Backs, and many are the Dericks, my friend" (081-048) — generalizes the German captain into a type. Just as the Fin-Back whale is fast but not worth catching (too thin, too swift), Derick and his nation are quick but insubstantial — flash without substance.

## Relationships

- **[[ahab|Ahab]]**: Derick is Ahab's comic opposite. Where Ahab pursues the White Whale with cosmic fury, Derick chases any whale for lamp oil; where Ahab inspires terror, Derick inspires laughter.
- **[[starbuck|Starbuck]]**: As the Pequod's chief mate, Starbuck oversees the practical aspects of whaling — the very skills that Derick conspicuously lacks.
