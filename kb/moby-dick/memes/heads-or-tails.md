# Heads or Tails

## Definition

"Heads or Tails" is the title of Chapter 90 of *Moby-Dick*, a companion piece to the preceding chapter on "Fast-Fish and Loose-Fish." Melville takes as his text a maxim from the thirteenth-century English jurist Bracton: *"De balena vero sufficit, si rex habeat caput, et regina caudam"* — "Of the whale, it suffices if the King have the head and the Queen the tail" (090-001–002). From this curious legal relic, Melville constructs a satire of monarchy, property law, and the logic by which hereditary power carves up the natural world. The chapter extends the previous chapter's argument — that all property is ultimately maintained by force calling itself law — into the specific domain of royal prerogative.

## Manifestations in the Novel

The chapter opens by treating the Bracton maxim with the straight-faced gravity of legal commentary: "A division which, in the whale, is much like halving an apple; there is no intermediate remainder" (090-002). Melville notes that the law "under a modified form, is to this day in force in England," and promises to treat it in a separate chapter "on the same courteous principle that prompts the English railways to be at the expense of a separate car, specially reserved for the accommodation of royalty" (090-002).

### The Duke of Wellington and the Cinque Ports Whale

The chapter's centerpiece is a comic anecdote: some "honest mariners of Dover, or Sandwich, or some one of the Cinque Ports" have killed and beached a fine whale, promising themselves "a good £150 from the precious oil and bone" (090-004). But "up steps a very learned and most Christian and charitable gentleman, with a copy of Blackstone under his arm; and laying it upon the whale's head, he says — 'Hands off! this fish, my masters, is a Fast-Fish. I seize it as the Lord Warden's'" (090-004).

What follows is a devastating sketch of legalized theft. The mariners' protests — "We have been at great trouble, and peril, and some expense" (090-009), "I thought to relieve my old bed-ridden mother by part of my share of this whale" (090-013) — are met each time with the same flat reply: "It is his" (090-008, 010, 012, 014, 016). The whale is seized and sold, and his Grace the Duke of Wellington receives the money. When "an honest clergyman" respectfully petitions for reconsideration, the Duke replies that he "had already done so, and received the money, and would be obliged to the reverend gentleman if for the future he (the reverend gentleman) would decline meddling with other people's business" (090-017).

### The Logic of Royal Anatomy

Having recounted the anecdote, Melville turns to the legal reasoning behind the head-and-tail division. Plowdon's justification — that the whale belongs to the King and Queen "because of its superior excellence" (090-018) — is presented as self-evidently absurd. William Prynne's explanation — "Ye tail is ye Queen's, that ye Queen's wardrobe may be supplied with ye whalebone" (090-020) — collapses under Melville's deadpan correction: "this same bone is not in the tail; it is in the head, which is a sad mistake for a sagacious lawyer like Prynne. But is the Queen a mermaid, to be presented with a tail? An allegorical meaning may lurk here" (090-020).

The chapter closes by extending the logic to the sturgeon, the other "royal fish," and suggesting by comic analogy that "the sturgeon must be divided in the same way as the whale, the King receiving the highly dense and elastic head peculiar to that fish, which, symbolically regarded, may possibly be humorously grounded upon some presumed congeniality" (090-021). The comparison of the King to a sturgeon's head is the chapter's final, silently devastating joke.

## Scholarly Context

"Heads or Tails" has been read as one of Melville's most direct political satires, aimed squarely at the English monarchy and the principle of hereditary entitlement. The chapter's structure — anecdote followed by increasingly absurd legal commentary — mirrors the method of "Fast-Fish and Loose-Fish," but narrows the target from property-in-general to royal prerogative specifically. Critics have noted the chapter's place in the novel's extended critique of all systems that convert force into right: just as Ahab's claim on the White Whale converts obsession into a binding oath, so the Duke's claim on the Cinque Ports whale converts a Blackstone quotation into theft. The chapter's final image — the King's head compared to a sturgeon's — is typical of Melville's method of advancing devastating argument through deadpan humor.
