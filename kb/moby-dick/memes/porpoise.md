# Porpoise

## Classification

The Porpoise serves as the type of Ishmael's DUODECIMO class — the smallest whales, and the final tier of his cetological system. "Beyond the DUODECIMO, this system does not proceed, inasmuch as the Porpoise is the smallest of the whales" (032-044). Ishmael distinguishes three varieties:

- **Huzza Porpoise** (Book III, Chapter 1) — the common porpoise, found globally
- **Algerine Porpoise** (Book III, Chapter 2) — a pirate, savage, Pacific only
- **Mealy-mouthed Porpoise** (Book III, Chapter 3) — also called Right-Whale Porpoise, the largest type

Modern taxonomy assigns porpoises to the family Phocoenidae, distinct from dolphins (Delphinidae). They are among the smallest cetaceans, typically under 2.5 meters.

## In Cetology (Chapter 32)

### Huzza Porpoise

The longest and most affectionate porpoise portrait (032-042):

> This is the common porpoise found almost all over the globe. The name is of my own bestowal; for there are more than one sort of porpoises, and something must be done to distinguish them.

Ishmael names them for their irrepressible cheerfulness: "he always swims in hilarious shoals, which upon the broad sea keep tossing themselves to heaven like caps in a Fourth-of-July crowd." They are "the lads that always live before the wind" and "accounted a lucky omen." The emotional test follows: "If you yourself can withstand three cheers at beholding these vivacious fish, then heaven help ye; the spirit of godly gamesomeness is not in ye."

The practical whaleman's eye never closes: a Huzza Porpoise yields "one good gallon of good oil," and the "fine and delicate fluid extracted from his jaws is exceedingly valuable" — prized by jewellers and watchmakers. Its meat is "good eating" (032-042).

### Algerine Porpoise

A single paragraph (032-043) paints the Algerine as "a pirate. Very savage." Found only in the Pacific, it is somewhat larger than the Huzza. "Provoke him, and he will buckle to a shark." Ishmael has lowered for it many times but "never yet saw him captured."

### Mealy-mouthed Porpoise

The largest porpoise (032-044), also called Right-Whale Porpoise for its association with that Folio whale. Ishmael describes it with unusual tenderness:

> He has no fins on his back (most other porpoises have), he has a lovely tail, and sentimental Indian eyes of a hazel hue. But his mealy-mouth spoils all.

The description of its coloration — "black above and white below," with a distinct boundary line marking the "bright waist" — is precise, but the final judgment is comic: "A most mean and mealy aspect!" (032-044).

## Appearances in the Novel

Beyond Chapter 32, porpoises surface repeatedly in the texture of the voyage:

- **Chapter 6** — In New Bedford, porpoises appear as a unit of local commerce: young men "dowering their nieces with a few porpoises a-piece" (006-007). It is the first hint that porpoises have economic value.
- **Chapter 46** — Ishmael insists that the dutiful officer "not omit reporting even a porpoise" (046-006), emphasizing the vigilance required at sea.
- **Chapter 55** — In the chapter on "monstrous pictures," porpoises and narwhales are dismissed as misrepresentations (055-008).
- **Chapter 56** — But the next chapter offers "four delicate miniatures of narwhales and porpoises" (056-007), one of Melville's rare compliments to cetological art.
- **Chapter 61** — During a whale chase, the sea "affords fewer glimpses of porpoises, dolphins, flying-fish" (061-003), their presence marking normalcy under disruption.
- **Chapter 65** — The most extended treatment outside Cetology. Ishmael discourses on porpoise as food: historically served with "an admirable sauce" at Henry VIII's court (065-002), the meat "made into balls about the size of billiard balls" that "might be taken for turtle-balls or veal balls." The old monks of Dunfermline "were very fond of them. They had a great porpoise grant from the crown."

## Whaling Context

Porpoises were never a primary target of American whaling, but they were caught opportunistically for food and oil. The "porpoise grant" Ishmael mentions has historical basis — medieval Scottish monasteries held rights to porpoise carcasses washed ashore. In the 19th century, porpoise jaw oil was indeed valued for fine lubrication, used by watchmakers and instrument makers. Porpoise meat was eaten by sailors and in some coastal communities.

## Symbolic Meaning

Among Melville's whales, the porpoise is unique in carrying an unambiguously positive charge. Where the [[sperm-whale|Sperm Whale]] is sublime terror and the [[right-whale|Right Whale]] is stoic philosophy, the Huzza Porpoise is joy. It represents what the Pequod's voyage lacks: lightheartedness, community, and what Ishmael calls "the spirit of godly gamesomeness."

The porpoise as "a Sperm whale in miniature" (032-042) is a fractal image — the great and the small reflecting each other, the whale compressed into a creature no larger than a man. This is Melville's vision at its most integrated: the cosmos in a grain of sand, the Leviathan in a porpoise.
