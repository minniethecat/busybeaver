## Overview

Sir Walter Scott (1771–1832) was a Scottish historical novelist, poet, and historian whose works include the Waverley novels, *Ivanhoe*, and *Rob Roy*. Scott was among the most widely read authors of the nineteenth century, and his historical romances shaped the Victorian imagination of the medieval past. In *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*, Scott appears as an object of affection for Captain, a minor character at the [[university-college-dublin|university]].

## In the Novel

In Chapter V, during a conversation among students at the [[university-college-dublin|university]], Captain's literary devotion is singled out for remark. The observation is blunt and definitive: "The captain has only one love: sir Walter Scott" [005-565]. When pressed, Captain himself confirms this passion with surprising warmth: "I love old Scott," he says, "I think he writes something lovely" [005-567]. His admiration is absolute — he declares that "There is no writer can touch sir Walter Scott" [005-567].

The episode is brief but telling. Captain's devotion to Scott is treated with gentle irony by the narrative voice, yet the sincerity of his affection is not mocked. Scott represents a form of literary pleasure that is uncomplicated by the aesthetic scruples that preoccupy [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] — a direct, unironic love of storytelling and historical romance.

## Critical Analysis

### Scott as Counterpoint to Stephen's Modernism

The Captain's enthusiasm for Scott offers a striking contrast to [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s own literary preoccupations. Where Stephen is absorbed in [[aristotle-poetics|Aristotle]], [[saint-thomas-aquinas|Aquinas]], and the Elizabethan lyricists, the Captain finds his fulfilment in the expansive narrative world of the Waverley novels. This contrast is not merely one of taste but of intellectual posture. Stephen reads to theorize; the Captain reads to love.

The brief episode with the Captain belongs to the broader pattern in Chapter V of juxtaposing Stephen's developing modernist sensibility against more traditional forms of literary engagement. The Captain's love of Scott is, in its way, no less authentic than Stephen's love of [[byron-poems|Byron]] — it is simply less self-conscious, less analytical, less driven by the need to convert aesthetic preference into philosophical principle.

### Scott's Place in Irish Literary Culture

Scott's historical novels — particularly those dealing with Scottish resistance to English domination — had a complex resonance in nineteenth-century Ireland. The Waverley novels provided a template for Irish national novelists seeking to represent their own history of conquest and resistance, and Scott's influence is detectable in the work of Irish writers throughout the century.
