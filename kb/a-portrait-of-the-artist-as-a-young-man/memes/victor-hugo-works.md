## Overview

Victor Hugo (1802–1885) was the towering figure of French Romanticism, known for novels such as *Les Misérables* (1862) and *Notre-Dame de Paris* (1831) as well as voluminous poetry and drama. In *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*, Hugo appears as a test case in a conversation about literary judgment — a writer whose acknowledged greatness is subject to the reservations of Catholic literary opinion.

## In the Novel

Hugo enters the novel during a remembered conversation between [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] and a priest, probably during his time at [[belvedere-college|Belvedere]] or [[clongowes-wood-college|Clongowes]]. Stephen, reading Macaulay's estimate of the poet James Clarence Mangan, had asked the priest "if Victor Hugo were not the greatest French writer" [004-032]. The priest's response was carefully qualified: he acknowledged that "even Victor Hugo, great as he certainly was, had not refrained from some Sacrilegious and profane passages" [004-033].

The exchange reveals a tension that runs through Stephen's entire education: the conflict between the Catholic aesthetic of moral propriety and the Romantic artist's claim to represent experience fully, including its darker and more sacrilegious dimensions. The priest's concession — Hugo is great *despite* his profanity — implicitly endorses a moral criterion for literary judgment that Stephen is already beginning to question.

## Significance

Hugo matters in Stephen's development because the conversation about him crystallises a problem central to [[aesthetic-theory|Stephen's aesthetic theory]]: must art be morally edifying to be great? The priest says yes, with qualifications; Stephen, moving toward an aesthetic of detached perception (*stasis* rather than *kinesis*), is already beginning to separate artistic greatness from moral content. Hugo — "great as he certainly was" — becomes a limit case for the Catholic literary conscience, and therefore a marker of the distance Stephen must travel to reach his own artistic independence.
