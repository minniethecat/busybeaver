## Overview

The Christmas dinner argument is one of the most dramatic scenes in Chapter I of *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*. During the family's Christmas dinner, a heated argument erupts between [[dante-riordan|Dante]] ([[dante-riordan|Mrs. Riordan]]), a devout Catholic who supports [[the-catholic-church|the Church]]'s condemnation of [[charles-parnell|Parnell]], and [[mr-casey|Mr. Casey]] and [[simon-dedalus|Simon Dedalus]], who defend [[charles-parnell|Parnell]] as a betrayed Irish patriot. The young [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] watches in confusion and distress as the adult world of politics and religion violently collides at the family table.

## In the Novel

The scene is set at the [[stephen-dedalus|Dedalus]] family Christmas dinner, with [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s father Simon, [[uncle-charles|Uncle Charles]], [[dante-riordan|Dante]], and [[mr-casey|Mr. Casey]] all present. The argument begins when the conversation turns to [[charles-parnell|Charles Stewart Parnell]], the Irish nationalist leader whose political career was destroyed by the Catholic Church after the revelation of his affair with Katherine O'Shea [001-015].

[[dante-riordan|Dante]], a strict Catholic, defends [[the-catholic-church|the Church]]'s position: "It is religion, [[dante-riordan|Dante]] said. They are doing their duty to God" [001-288]. She insists that "the bishops and [[priesthood-and-religion|priest]]s of Ireland have spoken, and they must be obeyed" [001-305].

[[mr-casey|Mr. Casey]], a passionate [[charles-parnell|Parnell]]ite, fires back with equal fervor. He accuses the [[priesthood-and-religion|priest]]s of meddling in politics, warning that "the people may leave their church alone" [001-306]. He tells a story of being spat upon by an old woman for his political views, a story that ends with him declaring that he spat his tobacco juice into her eye [001-364].

The argument escalates rapidly. [[simon-dedalus|Simon Dedalus]] joins [[mr-casey|Mr. Casey]] in condemning the [[priesthood-and-religion|priest]]s, crying out "Poor [[charles-parnell|Parnell]]! My dead king!" [001-422]. [[dante-riordan|Dante]], in turn, denounces [[charles-parnell|Parnell]] as an adulterer. The dinner ends in chaos, with [[dante-riordan|Dante]] storming from the table and [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] watching the adults he loves tear each other apart over questions he cannot yet fully understand [001-387].

## Significance

This scene crystallizes the central conflict that will shape [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s entire development: the tension between Irish nationalism and the Catholic Church, between individual conscience and institutional authority. The argument introduces the young [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] to the reality that the adult world is divided by irreconcilable allegiances.

The Christmas dinner argument also serves as [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s first encounter with the figure of [[charles-parnell|Parnell]], who will become a recurring symbol of the betrayed hero throughout the novel. The scene's placement at the family dinner table — the supposed center of domestic harmony — underscores how deeply politics and religion penetrate Irish life.
