## Overview

Fleming is a minor but sympathetically drawn classmate of [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen Dedalus]] at [[clongowes-wood-college|Clongowes Wood College]] in Chapter I of *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*. Unlike the bullies [[wells|Wells]] or [[nasty-roche|Nasty Roche]], Fleming appears in the narrative as a fundamentally decent boy — he checks on [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] when he is ill, colours the world creatively in his geography book, and eventually proposes a rebellion against unjust authority. He represents the ordinary kindness that punctuates [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s otherwise harsh introduction to institutional life.

## In the Novel

Fleming first appears in the infirmary scene, where he shows genuine concern for the feverish [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]. "Fleming was very decent to ask him. He wanted to cry" [001-097], the narrative notes, capturing how even small kindnesses loom large in [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s vulnerable state. In a moment of quiet intimacy, "Fleming said: —Sick in your breadbasket... because your face looks white. It will go away" [001-095].

Fleming's creative side emerges when [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] recalls that "Fleming had a box of crayons and one night during free study he had coloured the earth green and the clouds maroon" [001-115]. This seemingly minor detail resonates deeply with [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s developing aesthetic sensibility — the colours echo the political symbolism of [[dante-riordan|Dante]]'s brushes, the green for [[charles-parnell|Parnell]] and the maroon for Michael Davitt, though "he had not told Fleming to colour them those colours. Fleming had done it himself" [001-115].

Fleming also appears as a provocateur in the classroom. "Fleming one night for a cod had written on the opposite page" [001-123] of [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s geography book — a schoolboy jest that places him within the ordinary mischief of [[clongowes-wood-college|Clongowes]] life. Later, when the prefect of studies' severity becomes intolerable, "Fleming said: —Let us get up a rebellion" [001-385], propo[[sin-and-confession|sin]]g collective action against the disciplinary regime. Though the rebellion — if it materialises — is not depicted, Fleming's willingness to voice dissent marks him as posses[[sin-and-confession|sin]]g a nascent courage that [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] himself will develop more fully.

## Relationships

Fleming is a fellow student at [[clongowes-wood-college|Clongowes]], sharing a classroom and dormitory with [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]. His relationship to [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] is defined by small gestures of solidarity rather than intimacy — he is decent without being close, kind without being a confidant. He forms part of the ordinary student body that surrounds [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]], distinct from the bullies ([[wells|Wells]], [[nasty-roche|Nasty Roche]]) and the allies ([[athy|Athy]], who shares [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s sense of being an outsider).

## Significance

Fleming's narrative function is to provide contrast: against the cruelty of [[wells|Wells]], the strangeness of [[athy|Athy]], and the institutional violence of [[father-dolan|Father Dolan]], Fleming stands as evidence that decency exists even within the harsh world of [[clongowes-wood-college|Clongowes]]. His colouring of the globe — green earth, maroon clouds — is one of the novel's subtle image-clusters, linking the schoolboy's crayons to the political passions that divide adult Ireland. That Fleming does this "himself," without instruction, suggests an intuitive aesthetic sense that [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]], the future artist, recognizes and records.
