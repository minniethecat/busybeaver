## Overview

Donovan is a student at [[university-college-dublin|University College Dublin]] who appears in [[ch05|Chapter V]] of *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*. He is distinguished from [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s other university acquaintances by his knowledge of continental European literature and his urbane, self-possessed manner. In conversation with [[lynch]] and Stephen, Donovan invokes [[goethe-works|Goethe]] and [[lessing-works|Lessing]] as authorities on the classical and romantic schools of art.

## Life

Donovan appears in a single extended scene in Chapter V, during a discussion among university students about art, nationality, and literature. The conversation turns to the classical and romantic schools, and Donovan intervenes with a display of European learning:

> —Goethe and Lessing, said Donovan, have written a lot on that subject, the classical school and the romantic school and all that. The Laocoon interested me very much when I read it. Of course it is idealistic, German, ultra-profound. [005-412]

The remark is characteristic: Donovan presents himself as a cosmopolitan intellectual, comfortable citing German aesthetic theory, yet the tag "ultra-profound" betrays a slight detachment — he is performing worldliness rather than simply inhabiting it.

Earlier in the same scene, [[lynch]] teases Donovan about his romantic interests: "With women, Donovan?" [005-406] — to which Donovan responds with a theatrical gesture, laying his hand on his chest. The gesture hints at a certain self-consciousness, a quality of performance that aligns Donovan with the broader theme of posturing and identity in the university episodes.

Before leaving, Donovan speaks of his family obligations with a studied benevolence: "I have a strong suspicion, amounting almost to a conviction, that my sister intended to make pancakes today for the dinner of the Donovan family" [005-414]. The sentence — at once elaborate and trivial — captures Donovan's mode perfectly: the mock-pompous vocabulary ("amounting almost to a conviction"), the soft domesticity of the subject matter, the self-ironising tone of a young man who knows he is being theatrical and does it anyway.

Stephen's own view of Donovan is suggested by a sardonic aside to Cranly: "If you think I have an excrementitious intelligence run after Donovan and ask him to listen to you" [005-420]. The adjective "excrementitious" — one of Stephen's more colourful coinages — dismisses Donovan's intellectual posturing as something to be excreted, expelled, cast off. Yet the very vigour of the dismissal suggests that Donovan represents something Stephen recognises and wishes to distance himself from: the university intellectual who knows European literature but lacks the creative fire Stephen believes himself to possess.

## Significance

Donovan functions as a foil for Stephen in the university chapters. Where Stephen is intense, self-lacerating, and agonised about his artistic vocation, Donovan is urbane, self-amused, and comfortable in his intellectual skin. He knows Goethe and Lessing; he can talk about the *Laocoon*; he has read the European canon — but for Stephen, this knowledge is shallow, mere intellectual furniture. Donovan is what Stephen fears becoming: a cultivated talker rather than a genuine creator.

At the same time, Joyce does not mock Donovan outright. There is genuine charm in his elocution, and his invocation of Goethe and Lessing introduces names that matter to Joyce himself. The portrait is nuanced: Donovan is slightly ridiculous, slightly enviable — exactly the kind of minor character through whom Joyce registers the social texture of Edwardian Dublin's university life.
