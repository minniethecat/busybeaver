## Introduction

**Ben Jonson** (1572–1637) was an English playwright and poet, a contemporary of Shakespeare and one of the most influential figures of the English Renaissance. In *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*, Stephen Dedalus repeats a song by Ben Jonson during his morning walk across Dublin, the lyric forming part of his carefully curated aesthetic consciousness. The song appears twice in the narrative: first as part of Stephen's foreknown aesthetic itinerary during his walk in Chapter V, and later as an echo when he sings an Elizabethan song to Emma Clery in a moment of nostalgia and performance.

## In the Novel

Stephen's morning walk across Dublin begins from his family home and traces a path through the city's streets and canals. During this walk, Stephen's mind moves through a premeditated sequence of aesthetic references — Newman's prose, Ben Jonson's song, and the dainty songs of the Elizabethans — each summoned at a specific geographical waypoint. As the narrative describes:

> He foreknew that as he passed the sloblands of Fairview he would think of the cloistral silverveined prose of Newman; that as he walked along the North Strand Road, glancing idly at the windows of the provision shops, he would recall the dark humour of Guido Cavalcanti and smile; that as he went by Baird's stonecutting works in Talbot Place the spirit of Ibsen would blow through him like a keen wind; and that passing a grimy marine dealer's shop beyond the Liffey he would repeat the song by Ben Jonson which begins: [^1]

The song line itself follows immediately:

> I was not wearier where I lay. [^2]

The line is from Jonson's "Hymn to Diana" from *Cynthia's Revels* (1600), though the poem's more famous opening is "Queen and huntress, chaste and fair." Stephen's choice to recall a later, more subdued line — one of weariness and repose — reflects his own emotional state during the walk, which begins amid "an ache of loathing and bitterness" before his soul is "loosed of her miseries" by the grey morning light and the fragrance of wet leaves and bark [^3].

The aesthetic placement of the song within Stephen's itinerary is significant. The walk moves from Newman (cloistral, silverveined, religious prose) through Cavalcanti (dark humour, medieval Italian) and Ibsen (keen wind, modern Scandinavian drama) to Jonson (Elizabethan lyric grace). This sequence traces a history of literary influence that leads toward Stephen's own emerging artistic identity.

Later in the same chapter, the song returns indirectly when Stephen, during a charged conversation with the English Dean of Studies, feels the sting of linguistic displacement:

> He felt with a smart of dejection that the man to whom he was speaking was a countryman of Ben Jonson. He thought: —The language in which we are speaking is his before it is mine. How different are the words *home*, *Christ*, *ale*, *master*, on his lips and on mine! I cannot speak or write these words without unrest of spirit. His language, so familiar and so foreign, will always be for me an acquired speech. I have not made or accepted its words. My voice holds them at bay. My soul frets in the shadow of his language. [^4]

This passage transforms Ben Jonson from a purely aesthetic reference into a figure of linguistic and colonial tension. The Dean — courteous, vigilant, a "countryman of Ben Jonson" — embodies the English literary tradition that Stephen, as an Irish artist writing in English, must reckon with. Jonson's song, earlier a pleasing item on Stephen's aesthetic checklist, now reverberates as part of an inherited language that is at once intimate and alien.

The motif completes its arc near the chapter's end, when Stephen sits at a piano and sings to Emma Clery:

> a dainty song of the Elizabethans, a sad and sweet loth to depart, the victory chant of Agincourt, the happy march of *Greensleeves*. [^5]

The Elizabethan song here is no longer named as Jonson's, but the echo is deliberate — "dainty songs of the Elizabethans" is the very phrase used earlier to describe Stephen's turn toward Jonson and his contemporaries when wearied of philosophical abstraction [^6].

## The Song

The referenced work is Ben Jonson's "Hymn to Diana" from the play *Cynthia's Revels* (1600). The poem is a lyric address to the goddess Diana (also called Cynthia), celebrating her chastity and beauty:

> Queen and huntress, chaste and fair,
> Now the sun is laid to sleep,
> Seated in thy silver chair,
> State in wonted manner keep:
>     Hesperus entreats thy light,
>     Goddess excellently bright.

The line Stephen quotes — "I was not wearier where I lay" — is not from the opening stanza but from a later passage in the hymn. By selecting this quieter, more introspective line, Joyce shapes the allusion to fit Stephen's mood of exhaustion and aesthetic retreat on the morning walk.

## Thematic Significance

Ben Jonson's song operates on three interconnected thematic levels in the novel.

**Aesthetic program.** The song is part of Stephen's self-conscious effort to curate his aesthetic experience. The morning walk is not spontaneous reverie but a carefully planned itinerary in which each geographical landmark triggers a predetermined literary association. Jonson represents the Elizabethan lyric tradition — "dainty," formally polished, a retreat from the rigors of scholastic philosophy into pure pleasure. When Stephen's mind grows "wearied of its search for the essence of beauty amid the spectral words of Aristotle or Aquinas," it turns "for its pleasure to the dainty songs of the Elizabethans" [^6].

**Language and colonialism.** The second reference to Jonson — through the Dean of Studies — transforms the song into a site of linguistic anxiety. Stephen's realization that English is "his before it is mine" stages the central dilemma of the Irish modernist writer: how to create art in a language that is both native and colonial. The Dean, a "countryman of Ben Jonson," owns the language by birthright; Stephen, by contrast, must acquire it, and his "soul frets in the shadow" of that language. Jonson's song thus crystallizes Stephen's relationship to the English tradition — one of apprenticeship, appropriation, and eventual independence.

**Performance and intimacy.** Stephen's later performance of an Elizabethan song for Emma completes the motif's emotional arc. The songs are "quaint old songs" that offer temporary respite — "while he sang and she listened, or feigned to listen, his heart was at rest" — but the peace is fleeting; when the song ends, "the voices in the room" return, and with them Stephen's ironic self-consciousness [^7]. The Elizabethan lyric, for all its grace, cannot permanently resolve the tensions of Stephen's inner life.
