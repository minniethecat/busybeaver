## Overview

The Congregation of Christian Brothers (often called the Irish Christian Brothers) is a Catholic religious institute founded in 1802 by Edmund Ignatius Rice in Waterford, Ireland. The order was dedicated to providing free education to poor Catholic boys at a time when the Penal Laws restricted Catholic schooling. In "A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man," the Christian Brothers appear as the foil to the more prestigious Jesuit institutions — a mark of the social ambition that drives Simon Dedalus to keep his son in Jesuit schools despite the family's declining finances.

## In the Novel

The Christian Brothers are mentioned only briefly but tellingly in a conversation between Simon Dedalus and Stephen's mother about Stephen's education. When Mrs Dedalus suggests that Stephen might attend the Christian Brothers school, Simon explodes in contempt: "Christian brothers be damned!... Is it with Paddy Stink and Micky Mud? No, let him stick to the jesuits in God's name since he began with them" [001-1467].

This outburst reveals the social dimension of Mr Dedalus's commitment to Jesuit education. The Christian Brothers, who taught the children of the poor and lower-middle class, carry a stigma of social inferiority that the status-conscious Simon Dedalus cannot abide. His insistence on the Jesuits — "they'll be of service to him in after years. Those are the fellows that can get you a position" [001-1467] — underscores the practical, worldly calculation behind what might otherwise appear as religious preference.

The reference to the Christian Brothers thus serves as an index of the Dedalus family's precarious social position. Simon Dedalus, whose own fortunes are declining, clings to the symbolic capital of a Jesuit education for his son as a way of distinguishing the family from the Catholic masses and preserving a connection to the professional classes.

## Significance

The Christian Brothers function in the novel as a class marker, delineating the boundary between the aspiring Catholic middle class and the laboring poor. The casual contempt with which Simon dismisses the order reveals the class anxieties of late-19th-century Catholic Ireland, where educational institutions mapped directly onto social hierarchies. For Stephen, his father's insistence on Jesuit education — whatever his private skepticism about religion — ensures he receives the rigorous classical training that will shape his intellectual formation as an artist.
