## Overview

Horace (Quintus Horatius Flaccus, 65–8 BC) was the leading Roman lyric poet of the Augustan age, known for his *Odes*, *Satires*, and *Epistles*. His poetry combined formal elegance with philosophical reflection, and his *Ars Poetica* became a standard text in the classical curriculum. In *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*, Horace appears as a tactile memory — a text whose physical presence ("the pages of his timeworn Horace") matters as much as its content.

## In the Novel

Horace enters the novel in Chapter V, when [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] reflects on his classical education at [[belvedere-college|Belvedere]]. The paragraph in which Horace appears is a dense meditation on the relationship between language and sensation. [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] remembers being taught Roman history through "the trite words *in tanto discrimine*" and being introduced to Roman social life through the phrase *implere ollam denariorum*, which "the rector had rendered sonorously as the filling of a pot with denaries" [005-048].

Then comes the crucial sentence: "The pages of his timeworn Horace never felt cold to the touch even when his own fingers were cold" [005-048]. The observation is striking in its physicality. Horace's poetry is not simply read; it is *felt*. The "timeworn" quality of the pages — worn smooth by generations of student hands — makes the book itself into a sensory object. The Latin text becomes a bridge between the classical past and [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s immediate physical experience.

This passage forms part of Chapter V's sustained meditation on language as an aesthetic medium. Just as the word *ivory* (in its Latin, French, Italian forms) shone in [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s brain "clearer and brighter than any ivory," the tactile sensation of Horace's pages suggests that the physical vehicle of language — paper, ink, the worn surface of a book — participates in the aesthetic experience it carries.

## Significance

Horace matters in [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s development not because of any specific doctrine or influence, but because of the *mode* of engagement he represents. [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s relationship to Horace is sensory and intimate — the cold fingers, the timeworn pages, the sonorous voice of the rector translating *implere ollam denariorum*. This is consistent with [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s aesthetic theory, particularly his emphasis on the *integritas* (wholeness) of the aesthetic object — the work apprehended first as a *thing*, a unified sensible presence, before it is analyzed.
