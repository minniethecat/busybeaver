## Overview

The Dominican Order (Order of Preachers, O.P.) is a mendicant Catholic religious order founded by St. Dominic de Guzmán in 1216. The order's charism emphasizes preaching, study, and the defense of Catholic doctrine. Historically, the Dominicans were entrusted with the Inquisition and made major contributions to scholastic theology, most notably through the work of St. [[thomas-aquinas|Thomas Aquinas]], whose aesthetic writings profoundly shape Stephen Dedalus's artistic philosophy. In *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*, the Dominican Order appears during a pivotal private conversation in Chapter IV in which the director of Belvedere College subtly tests Stephen's possible vocation to the priesthood.

## In the Novel

### The Director's Conversation

The Dominican Order is invoked not during the retreat sermons (as one might expect) but in a quieter, more consequential scene: the director's private conversation with Stephen in the college parlour. The director begins obliquely, speaking of matters that seem casual but are in fact carefully chosen to lead toward a serious question:

> The director had begun to speak of the Dominican and Franciscan orders and of the friendship between saint Thomas and saint Bonaventure. The Capuchin dress, he thought, was rather too.... [^1]

The broken sentence about the Capuchin habit — left trailing — is a deliberate conversational gambit. The director proceeds to discuss the practical inconveniences of the Capuchin dress, recounting an anecdote about seeing Capuchins cycling in Belgium "with this thing up about their knees," which they call *les jupes* [^2]. This light, almost gossipy tone is a carefully calibrated prelude — the director is establishing camaraderie before moving to the real purpose of the interview.

### The Vocation Invitation

After the digression on religious dress, the director's voice changes. The casual preamble gives way to the question he has been leading toward:

> —Have you ever felt that you had a vocation? [^3]

He clarifies: "I mean, have you ever felt within yourself, in your soul, a desire to join the order?" [^4]. The "order" here is, strictly speaking, the Society of Jesus (Jesuits) — the order that runs Belvedere College. But the director's opening invocation of the Dominican-Franciscan friendship serves a specific rhetorical function: it situates the Jesuit vocation within the broader communion of the religious orders, connecting Stephen's possible call to a centuries-old tradition of Catholic religious life.

The director's appeal is framed in the language of power and privilege:

> To receive that call, Stephen, said the priest, is the greatest honour that the Almighty God can bestow upon a man. No king or emperor on this earth has the power of the priest of God. No angel or archangel in heaven, no saint, not even the Blessed Virgin herself, has the power of a priest of God: the power of the keys, the power to bind and to loose from sin [^5].

Stephen is momentarily drawn to this vision. He had often imagined himself "a young and silentmannered priest, entering a confessional swiftly, ascending the altarsteps, incensing, genuflecting, accomplishing the vague acts of the priesthood which pleased him by reason of their semblance of reality and of their distance from it" [^6]. But the attraction is to the *aesthetics* of priesthood — the gestures, the incensing, the semblance — not to its substance. Stephen ultimately refuses the vocation, choosing the "slow and dark and cold" path of the artist over the certainties of religious life.

## The Aquinas Connection

The director's pairing of "saint Thomas and saint Bonaventure" is rich with irony when read against Stephen's subsequent development. St. Thomas Aquinas — the Dominican theologian par excellence — becomes, for Stephen, not a model of religious obedience but a source of aesthetic theory. Stephen's mature aesthetic philosophy, articulated in Chapter V, draws heavily on Aquinas's definitions of beauty: *integritas* (wholeness), *consonantia* (harmony), and *claritas* (radiance). The Dominican whose friendship with a Franciscan saint the director invokes as an example of holy religious community becomes, for Stephen, the intellectual bridge from scholastic theology to modernist aesthetics.

The trajectory is telling: the director offers Stephen the Dominican tradition as a path toward priesthood; Stephen instead appropriates Aquinas's thought as a foundation for art. The "order" Stephen ultimately joins is not the Society of Jesus but the priesthood of the artist.

## Significance

The Dominican reference in *Portrait* illustrates how Joyce embeds the institutional culture of Catholicism into Stephen's intellectual formation while simultaneously charting Stephen's movement away from that culture. The director's strategic mention of the Dominicans — as part of the furniture of religious life, one order among many — is absorbed by Stephen into a very different project: the construction of an aesthetic theory that will justify his flight from the nets of religion, nation, and family.

The scene also dramatizes a characteristic Joycean technique: the serious is broached through the trivial. The director's question about Capuchin dress — "It must be troublesome, I imagine" — is the conversational needle through which the thread of vocation is drawn. The Dominican-Franciscan friendship, like the Capuchin habit, is deployed as small talk that masks large intentions.
