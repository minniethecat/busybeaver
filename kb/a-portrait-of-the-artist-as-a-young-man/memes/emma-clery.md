## Overview

Emma Clery (referred to simply as "Emma") is a young woman who functions as the primary object of [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen Dedalus]]'s adolescent romantic imagination in *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*. Though she appears only briefly in Chapter III — her name mentioned just three times — her image pervades [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s consciousness during the pivotal scenes of the retreat, embodying the tension between physical desire and spiritual aspiration that defines his adolescent crisis.

## In the Novel

Emma first enters [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s consciousness after the [[the-retreat|retreat sermons]], as he walks homeward through the square. The sound of a girl's laughter strikes his "burning ear" and triggers a cascade of shame and self-recrimination. "The image of Emma appeared before him, and under her eyes the flood of shame rushed forth anew from his heart" [003-052]. [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] is tormented by the thought of what Emma would think if she knew "to what his mind had subjected her or how his brutelike lust had torn and trampled upon her innocence" [003-052]. This passage reveals the complex psychology of adolescent desire: Emma is simultaneously the object of [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s sexual fantasies and the imagined judge of those same fantasies.

In the vision that follows, [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] imagines himself standing "near Emma in a wide land," humbly kis[[sin-and-confession|sin]]g the elbow of her sleeve in a gesture of courtly reverence [003-053]. This fantasy of purified love — a chivalric gesture of submission and adoration — represents [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s attempt to reconcile his physical desires with his spiritual aspirations. The image is completed by a divinely sanctioned union: "Take hands, [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] and Emma. It is a beautiful evening now in heaven" [003-055]. This imagined reconciliation, spoken by a maternal voice that blends the earthly and the divine, offers [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] a vision of love that is both innocent and transcendent.

## Significance

Emma Clery occupies a crucial position in the novel's treatment of desire, art, and transcendence. She is less a character than an image — a screen onto which [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] projects his conflicting impulses. In Chapter III, she embodies the crisis of the retreat: [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s attempt to move from the sordidness of his sexual experiences to the purity of religious devotion. Emma represents the possibility of a middle ground — a love that is neither brutish nor disembodied, but human and tender.

Joyce's treatment of Emma anticipates the more fully realised figure of the wading girl in Chapter IV, who represents the aesthetic resolution of the conflicts that Emma crystallises. The progression from Emma ([[sin-and-confession|guilt]]-ridden idealisation) to the wading girl (aesthetic transcendence) traces [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s development from adolescent confusion to artistic vocation. Emma thus functions as a necessary stage in [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s Bildung — the first intimation that desire might be transformed rather than merely suppressed.
