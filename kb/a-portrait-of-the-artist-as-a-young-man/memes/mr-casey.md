## Overview

Mr. Casey is one of the most memorable minor characters in *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*. A friend of [[simon-dedalus|Simon Dedalus]] and a frequent guest at the family's [[christmas-dinner-argument|Christmas dinner]], he is a man of strong passions and stronger language — a Fenian nationalist whose loyalty to [[charles-parnell|Charles Stewart Parnell]] borders on the religious. His confrontation with [[dante-riordan|Dante]] at the [[christmas-dinner-argument|Christmas dinner]] table is the dramatic climax of Chapter I and introduces the young [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] to the violent collision of politics and religion in Irish life.

## In the Novel

Mr. Casey appears at the [[stephen-dedalus|Dedalus]] family [[christmas-dinner-argument|Christmas dinner]], sitting in an easychair "in the shadow of the window" alongside [[dante-riordan|Dante]] [001-235]. At first, the atmosphere is convivial — he "took the glass, drank, and placed it near him on the table" [001-243] — but when the conversation turns to [[charles-parnell|Parnell]] and the [[priesthood-and-religion|priest]]s who destroyed him, Mr. Casey's anger erupts.

His indictment of [[the-catholic-church|the Church]] is direct and personal. He warns that if the [[priesthood-and-religion|priest]]s do not "leave politics alone, the people may leave their church alone" [001-306], deploying the threat of mass apostasy as a political weapon. His contempt for [[priesthood-and-religion|clerical authority]] is visceral: "Princes of the church," he says "with slow scorn" [001-331], turning [[the-catholic-church|the Church]]'s own honorific into an insult.

Mr. Casey's most famous moment comes when he tells the story of an old woman who spat at him for his political views. The story builds to a climax of defiant vulgarity: "I let her bawl away. It was a cold day and to keep up my heart I had a few balls of malt in my pocket. Then and there I took out my quid and spat it into her eye" [001-364]. The story crystallizes Mr. Casey's character — crude, passionate, unrepentant — and leaves the dinner table in stunned silence.

His final outburst — "Poor [[charles-parnell|Parnell]]! My dead king!" [001-422] — is both a political lament and a personal cry of grief. For Mr. Casey, [[charles-parnell|Parnell]]'s fall is not an abstract political event but a wound that still bleeds.

## Relationships

Mr. Casey is [[simon-dedalus|Simon Dedalus]]'s close friend and political ally. Together they represent the nationalist, anti-clerical side of Irish opinion, opposed to [[dante-riordan|Dante]]'s Catholic orthodoxy. Their bond is forged in shared political passion, and their joint assault on [[dante-riordan|Dante]] at the dinner table reveals the depth of their commitment to [[charles-parnell|Parnell]]'s legacy.

## Significance

Mr. Casey embodies the tradition of Irish revolutionary nationalism that runs from Wolfe Tone through [[charles-parnell|Parnell]] to the Easter Ri[[sin-and-confession|sin]]g. His story of the spitting woman is one of the novel's most powerful set-pieces — a miniature of the larger conflict between individual conscience and communal authority. More than any other adult in Chapter I, Mr. Casey teaches [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] that passion and conviction can take the form of defiant, almost theatrical individuality.
