## Overview

Simon Moonan is a distinctive figure among [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen Dedalus]]'s classmates at [[clongowes-wood-college|Clongowes Wood College]] in Chapter I of *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*. He occupies an ambiguous position in the school's social world — charming enough to get away with tea[[sin-and-confession|sin]]g the prefect, well-dressed enough to attract [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s admiration, yet implicated in the "smugging" scandal that marks him as one of the boys whose behaviour has crossed a line.

## In the Novel

Moonan first appears in the narrative as a boy with a particular relationship to the prefect. "Simon Moonan used to tie the prefect's false sleeves behind his back and the prefect used to let on to be angry" [001-082]. This detail — the playful transgression, the prefect's performative anger — suggests a boy who has learned to navigate institutional authority through charm. The word used to describe him — "suck" — is registered by [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] as "a queer word" [001-082], a sound whose ugliness he connects to the gurgle of dirty water going down a drain.

[[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s admiration for Moonan is evident in his recollection: "Simon Moonan had nice clothes and one night he had shown him a ball of creamy sweets that the fellows of the football fifteen had rolled down to him along the carpet in the middle of the refectory" [001-369]. The image is cinematic — the ball of sweets rolling along the carpet, the older boys' recognition of Moonan as someone worth favouring — and [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s wonder at it reveals his own position as an outsider to such exchanges.

Moonan's fall comes through [[athy|Athy]]'s revelation in the infirmary: "They were caught with Simon Moonan and [[boyle|Tusker Boyle]] in the square one night" [001-367]. Later, "[[athy|Athy]] pointed across the playground to where Simon Moonan was walking by himself kicking a stone before him" [001-361], the solitary figure a visual marker of disgrace. The narrative never specifies what "smugging" means — the word remains opaque, its meaning accessible only to those initiated into the school's sexual knowledge — but Moonan's isolation in the playground makes the consequences clear.

## Relationships

Moonan is linked to Boyle as a fellow participant in the "smugging" scandal. His relationship with the prefect — tea[[sin-and-confession|sin]]g but tolerated — suggests a boy who understands how to manage authority figures. For [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]], Moonan is an object of fascinated observation: his nice clothes, his creamy sweets, his ease with older boys, his sudden disgrace — all of it registers as a lesson in the precariousness of social standing.

## Significance

Simon Moonan's trajectory in Chapter I — from charming favourite to isolated transgressor — mirrors in miniature the novel's larger concern with [[sin-and-confession|sin]], fall, and social judgement. The word "suck," applied to Moonan by another boy, reverberates through [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s consciousness as one of those words whose sound carries its meaning: ugly, wet, vaguely obscene. And the "smugging" scandal, in which Moonan is a central figure, represents [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s first confused encounter with the idea that there are acts so shameful they cannot be named — only whispered about, their nature inferred from the isolation of those who have committed them.
