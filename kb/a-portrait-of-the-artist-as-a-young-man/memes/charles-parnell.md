## Overview

Charles Stewart Parnell, known as the "Uncrowned King of Ireland," was the leader of the Irish Home Rule movement in the 1880s. Though he died before the novel's action, his political martyrdom — brought about by the exposure of his affair with the married Katharine O'Shea and the subsequent condemnation by the Irish Catholic hierarchy — provides the emotional and ideological backdrop of [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen Dedalus]]'s childhood. The bitter [[christmas-dinner-argument|Christmas dinner]] argument between [[dante-riordan|Dante Riordan]] and [[simon-dedalus|Simon Dedalus]] over Parnell's betrayal by [[the-catholic-church|the Church]] [001-305]–[001-422] is one of the novel's most powerful scenes and a formative moment in [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s growing awareness of the fractures running through Irish society.

## Role in the Novel

### The Green Velvet Back

Parnell enters the novel through the material world of [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s childhood home. "There was a picture of the earth on the first page of his geography : a big ball in the middle of clouds. Farther on the sun was a red lamp. Farther still another sun was just going out as he counted them. All the brass knobs were bright but the green velvet back was for Parnell and the brush with the maroon velvet back was for Michael Davitt" [001-015]. The child [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] does not understand the political symbolism that assigns velvet colors to heroes, but the objects — and the passionate loyalties they encode — surround him from his earliest consciousness [001-091]–[001-109].

### The Christmas Dinner Argument

The [[christmas-dinner-argument|Christmas dinner]] at the [[stephen-dedalus|Dedalus]] household is the novel's great set-piece of political division. [[dante-riordan|Dante Riordan]], a devout Catholic who has turned against Parnell after the bishops' condemnation, sits opposite [[simon-dedalus|Simon Dedalus]] and [[mr-casey|Mr Casey]], who remain defiant Parnellites. The argument escalates from apparently casual recollections to a furious confrontation. "The bishops and [[priesthood-and-religion|priest]]s of Ireland have spoken," declares [[dante-riordan|Dante]], "and they must be obeyed" [001-305]. Simon and [[mr-casey|Mr Casey]] counter that the [[priesthood-and-religion|priest]]s betrayed Ireland's greatest leader: "the [[priesthood-and-religion|priest]]s' pawns broke Parnell's heart and hounded him into his grave" [001-338]. [[dante-riordan|Dante]]'s final cry — "Devil out of hell! We won! We crushed him to death!" [001-415] — ends the meal in tears and collapse. The child [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]], watching from his seat, sees adult authority fracture along irreconcilable lines.

### Parnell as Political Ghost

Parnell's ghost haunts the rest of the novel. [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] recalls the name in lists of early memories: "[[dante-riordan|Dante]], Parnell, Clane, [[clongowes-wood-college|Clongowes]]" [002-251]. At age ten he tries to write a poem about Parnell on the back of his father's bills [002-051]. And when, as a university student, [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] reflects on Irish nationalism, the name of Parnell recurs as a benchmark of betrayed promise: "The Ireland of Tone and of Parnell seemed to have receded in space" [005-073], replaced by a diminished Ireland of cultural nationalism. In the climactic argument with [[davin|Davin]], [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] invokes Parnell as part of a longer tradition: "You sold him to the enemy or failed him in need or reviled him and left him for another" [005-319].

## Significance

Parnell functions in the novel as a template for [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s own understanding of the artist's relationship to his people. Parnell was a leader betrayed by the very nation he sought to serve — brought down, [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] comes to believe, not merely by the [[priesthood-and-religion|priest]]s but by the Irish people's incapacity to tolerate greatness that refuses conventional morality. The parallel with [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s own artistic vocation is deliberate: [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] imagines himself as a kind of aesthetic Parnell, preparing to be rejected by Ireland even as he dedicates his art to forging its "uncreated conscience" [005-909].

Critically, the Parnell drama also encodes Joyce's own political worldview. Joyce deeply admired Parnell and saw his destruction by a coalition of Church, press, and public opinion as the paradigmatic Irish tragedy — the nation that destroys its own best. This theme recurs in Joyce's later work, including the "Cyclops" episode of *Ulysses* and the recurrent Parnell references in *Finnegans Wake*. In *Portrait*, the child [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s exposure to the Parnell controversy is the first crack in the unified Catholic-nationalist ideology of late-Victorian Ireland, opening a space in which the artist's dissenting consciousness can grow.
