## Overview

Aristotle's *Poetics* (c. 335 BC) is the earliest surviving work of dramatic theory and the foundational text of Western aesthetics. In its surviving portion, it analyzes tragedy, epic poetry, and the elements of literary art — including *mimesis* (imitation), *catharsis* (purgation of pity and fear), and the structural components of plot, character, and diction. In *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*, Aristotle's philosophical works are among the "spectral words" that [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen Dedalus]] broods upon, and his aesthetic theory constitutes both a source and a foil for [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s own developing ideas.

## In the Novel

### Stephen's Reading

Aristotle enters the novel in Chapter V as a sustained presence in [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s intellectual life. The narrative describes how [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s "mind when wearied of its search for the essence of beauty amid the spectral words of Aristotle or Aquinas turned often for its pleasure to the dainty songs of the Elizabethans" [005-035]. The word "spectral" is carefully chosen: for [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]], the philosophers are ghostly presences, neither fully alive nor fully dead, haunting his consciousness.

What his peers at [[university-college-dublin|the university]] believe to be profound philosophical study is in fact "only a garner of slender sentences from Aristotle's poetics and psychology and a Synopsis Philosophiae Scholasticae ad mentem divi Thomae" [005-036]. The "slender sentences" — carefully selected, individually pondered, torn from their original context — are the building blocks of [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s own thought. Joyce's choice of "garner" (a storehouse of grain) and "slender" conveys both the modesty of [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s actual reading and the fertility of his method: from these few seeds, an entire aesthetic theory grows.

### Pity and Terror: Stephen's Revision

In his conversation with [[lynch|Lynch]], [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] makes a bold claim: "Aristotle has not defined pity and terror. I have" [005-337]. This is not mere adolescent arrogance — or rather, it is adolescent arrogance that also happens to be philosophically productive. [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s definitions — pity as the emotion that arrests the mind in the presence of "whatsoever is grave and constant in human sufferings" and unites it with "the sufferer," terror as the emotion that arrests the mind in the presence of "whatsoever is grave and constant in human sufferings" and unites it with "the secret cause" — are genuinely original reworkings of Aristotelian concepts [005-337]–[005-340].

### Working by the Light of One or Two Ideas

Later, [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] reflects on his method with characteristic self-awareness: "For my purpose I can work on at present by the light of one or two ideas of Aristotle and Aquinas" [005-099]. The phrasing is deliberate. He does not claim to be a systematic Aristotelian or Thomist; he admits he is borrowing selectively, instrumentally — taking only what serves his purpose, and reserving the right to transform or discard the rest. This is the method of the artist as bricoleur, assembling a personal philosophy from whatever the tradition offers.

## Significance

### From Student to Successor

Aristotle's *Poetics* is essential to [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s intellectual development because it gives him a vocabulary for thinking about art that is neither theological nor merely impressionistic. Where the retreat sermons of Chapter III frame experience in terms of sin and damnation, and where the romanticism of [[byron-poems|Byron]] frames it in terms of passion and rebellion, Aristotle offers [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] a *method* — a way of analyzing art that is systematic, rational, and grounded in the work itself rather than in moral judgement.

Yet [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] does not simply adopt Aristotle's system. He critiques and extends it, most notably when he claims to define pity and terror where Aristotle did not — a claim that is both contestable (Aristotle *did* discuss both concepts) and revealing ([[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] needs to position himself as surpassing his predecessors). This pattern — absorbing a predecessor's framework and then going beyond it — is the engine of [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s growth throughout the novel and, indeed, of Joyce's own relationship to the literary tradition.

### The Scholastic Method

The pairing of Aristotle with [[saint-thomas-aquinas|Aquinas]] throughout Chapter V is significant. Thomas Aquinas had synthesized Aristotelian philosophy with Christian theology in the thirteenth century; [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s adaptation of both thinkers for a secular aesthetic theory enacts, in miniature, the intellectual history of the West — classical philosophy absorbed by medieval scholasticism, then liberated from theology by the modern artist. The "slender sentences" [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] gleans from the *Synopsis Philosophiae Scholasticae* are fragments of this vast tradition, which he reassembles according to his own design.
