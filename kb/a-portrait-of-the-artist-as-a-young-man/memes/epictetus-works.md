## Overview

Epictetus (c. 55–135 AD) was a Greek Stoic philosopher born into slavery who taught that the path to freedom lies in accepting what is beyond one's control and mastering one's own responses. His teachings were recorded by his pupil Arrian in the *Discourses* and the *Enchiridion* (Handbook), which became one of the most influential texts of ancient philosophy. In *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*, Epictetus is invoked in a conversation about patience and mockery — a specifically Stoic virtue applied to the bruising social world of [[university-college-dublin|University College Dublin]].

## In the Novel

Epictetus appears twice in Chapter V, both times in the context of the university. The dean of studies, attempting to light a fire during a conversation with Stephen, observes: "Epictetus also had a lamp" [005-102]. The remark is casual, almost teasing — the dean is comparing his own minor domestic frustration to the legendary poverty of the Stoic philosopher, who is said to have owned nothing but a lamp. The comparison is self-deprecating but also reveals the dean's classical learning, a shared intellectual currency between him and Stephen.

Stephen returns to Epictetus later in the chapter, in a moment of private reflection about the social mockery he endures from his fellow students. He counsels himself: "Patience. Remember Epictetus. It is probably in his character that he should be mocked" [005-171]. The Stoic lesson — that mockery says more about the mocker than the mocked — becomes a strategy for survival. Stephen uses Epictetus's example to place his own suffering in a philosophical frame, transforming personal hurt into a universal pattern.

## Significance

Epictetus matters in Stephen's development because he offers a model of intellectual dignity under social pressure. Stephen, isolated among classmates who mock his seriousness, finds in Epictetus the archetype of the philosopher who is misunderstood by his contemporaries. The Stoic doctrine of *apatheia* — freedom from emotional disturbance — resonates with Stephen's own [[aesthetic-theory|aesthetic theory]] of *stasis*: the artist, like the Stoic sage, contemplates the world without being swept away by it. Epictetus also represents an alternative to the Catholic framework of sin and confession that dominates earlier chapters — a secular ethics of self-mastery.
