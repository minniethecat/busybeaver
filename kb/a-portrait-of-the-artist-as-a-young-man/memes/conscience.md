## Overview

Conscience in Joyce's novel is the internal theater where Stephen's moral struggles are staged. The retreat sermons of Chapter III transform conscience from a quiet guide into a terrifying accuser, making it the primary instrument of Stephen's religious fear. As the novel progresses, conscience evolves from a Catholic formation mechanism into a personal aesthetic criterion — Stephen replaces the priest's judgment with the artist's self-judgment.

## In the Novel

### The Retreat and the Awakening of Conscience

The retreat in Chapter III is explicitly designed to activate conscience. Father Arnall explains that a retreat signifies withdrawal from worldly cares in order to examine "the state of our conscience" [003-040]. Stephen, already burdened by guilt over his sexual experiences, is the retreat's ideal subject — his conscience primed for the assault that follows.

### The Worm of Conscience

The hell sermons transform conscience into a physical torment. Drawing on patristic imagery, the preacher describes "the pain of conscience" as a worm that gnaws eternally [003-107]. The conscience's sting is "threefold": the memory of past pleasures, a "late and fruitless sorrow for sins committed," and the accusation that "you had time and opportunity to repent and would not" [003-107]. Pope Innocent III's image of "the worm of the triple sting" [003-107] and the description of conscience as "the viper which gnaws the very heart's core" [003-108] fuse psychological anguish with visceral bodily horror.

### Self-Examination

After the sermons, Stephen kneels in his room "to be alone with his soul, to examine his conscience, to meet his sins face to face, to recall their times and manners and circumstances, to weep over them" [003-124]. This moment of solitary self-examination represents conscience at its most intense — internalized religious discipline operating without external enforcement.

## Analysis

Joyce traces conscience through three distinct phases in Stephen's development. In childhood, conscience is externally imposed — rules from parents, prefects, and priests. During the retreat, conscience is internalized with terrifying intensity as the "worm" that speaks from within. By Chapter V, Stephen has redefined conscience on his own terms, replacing religious scruple with aesthetic integrity. The trajectory mirrors the novel's larger pattern: the transformation of religious structures into artistic ones.
