## Overview

The *Pange Lingua Gloriosi* ("Sing, My Tongue, of the Glorious Body") is a Latin hymn composed by [[saint-thomas-aquinas|Thomas Aquinas]] (1225–1274) for the Feast of Corpus Christi. It is one of the most celebrated hymns of the Catholic liturgy, renowned for its theological depth and intricate versification. The last two stanzas, beginning *Tantum Ergo Sacramentum*, are sung during Benediction of the Blessed Sacrament. In *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*, the *Pange Lingua* appears in Chapter V as [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] discusses hymns and aesthetic judgement with [[lynch|Lynch]].

## In the Novel

The *Pange Lingua* enters the novel in Chapter V, during [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s extended conversation with [[lynch|Lynch]] about aesthetics. After articulating his theory of beauty — drawing on [[aristotle-poetics|Aristotle]] and [[saint-thomas-aquinas|Aquinas]] — [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] turns to Aquinas's poetic achievement: "He was a poet himself. He wrote a hymn for Maundy Thursday. It begins with the words *Pange lingua gloriosi*. They say it is the highest glory of the hymnal. It is an intricate and soothing hymn. I like it" [005-390].

The praise is genuine but qualified. Immediately after commending the *Pange Lingua*, [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] adds: "but there is no hymn that can be put beside that mournful and majestic processional song, the *Vexilla Regis* of Venantius Fortunatus" [005-390]. The *Pange Lingua* is "intricate and soothing" — terms of analysis and comfort — while the *Vexilla Regis* is "mournful and majestic" — terms of emotional depth and grandeur. The distinction reveals [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s mature aesthetic sensibility: he values art that moves the soul over art that merely delights the intellect.

## Significance

The *Pange Lingua* serves as both an example of Aquinas's artistic achievement and a foil for [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s independent critical judgement. [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] acknowledges the conventional opinion — "they say it is the highest glory of the hymnal" — but does not accept it uncritically. His preference for the older, more austere *Vexilla Regis* demonstrates that his aesthetic judgement operates independently of received authority, even when that authority is the liturgical tradition he was raised in.

This independence of taste is continuous with [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s earlier defence of [[byron-poems|Byron]] against Tennyson in Chapter II. In both cases, [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] asserts the primacy of his own aesthetic response against the consensus of his community. The difference is that by Chapter V, he can articulate his judgement in philosophical terms.
