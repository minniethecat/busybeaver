## Overview

Eileen Vance is a quietly luminous presence in the childhood sections of *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*, [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen Dedalus]]'s earliest remembered object of romantic feeling. The Vances live at number seven, and young [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] hides under the table declaring that "when they were grown up he was going to marry Eileen" [001-040]. Her significance extends far beyond this childhood infatuation: through Eileen, Joyce introduces the entwined themes of religious difference, aesthetic perception, and the transformation of sensory experience into artistic vision.

## In the Novel

Eileen first appears in [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s earliest memories. "The Vances lived in number seven. They had a different father and mother. They were Eileen's father and mother" [001-040]. The child's logic — that having different parents makes the Vances somehow a different species of family — captures the dawning awareness of social difference that is Eileen's narrative function.

The sensory memory of Eileen crystallizes around her hands: "Eileen had long white hands. One evening when playing tig she had put her hands over his eyes: long and white and thin and cold and soft. That was ivory: a cold white thing. That was the meaning of *Tower of Ivory*" [001-296]. [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s associative leap — from the sensation of Eileen's hands to the image from the Litany of the Blessed Virgin — is a miniature of the artistic process itself: sensory experience transformed into symbol, the particular made universal.

This association recurs throughout the novel. "Eileen had long thin cool white hands too because she was a girl. They were like ivory; only soft. That was the meaning of *Tower of Ivory* but protestants could not understand it and made fun of it" [001-374]. The addition of religious difference — Eileen is Protestant, and Protestants cannot understand the litany — introduces the sectarian dimension that complicates [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s feeling. [[dante-riordan|Dante]] forbids [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] to play with Eileen "because Eileen was a protestant and when she was young she knew children that used to play with protestants and the protestants used to make fun of the litany of the Blessed Virgin" [001-295].

Another memory adds a painterly quality: "She had put her hand into his pocket where his hand was and he had felt how cool and thin and soft her hand was. She had said that pockets were funny things to have: and then all of a sudden she had broken away and had run laughing down the sloping curve of the path. Her fair hair had streamed out behind her like gold in the sun" [001-374]. The image — the girl running, her hair streaming gold — is one of the novel's most purely beautiful passages, a moment of uncomplicated joy preserved in memory.

In Chapter IV, during [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s encounter with the wading girl on the strand, the memory of Eileen resurfaces: "he remembered the day when he and Eileen had stood looking into the hotel grounds, watching the waiters running up a trail of bunting on the flagstaff and the fox terrier scampering to and fro on the sunny lawn, and how, all of a sudden, she had broken out into a peal of laughter and had run down the sloping curve of the path" [004-068]. This echo — Eileen's laughter, Eileen's running — links [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s first romantic feeling to his aesthetic awakening at the novel's climax.

## In the Eyes of Others

- **[[stephen-dedalus|Stephen Dedalus]]'s view**: Stephen sees Eileen through a lens of aesthetic adoration, transforming her physical features into sacred symbols. Her hands become the "Tower of Ivory" from the Litany of the Blessed Virgin, her hair becomes "gold in the sun," and her Protestantism becomes part of her mystery — something that sets her apart yet makes her more alluring. His declaration that "when they were grown up he was going to marry Eileen" [001-040] reveals the depth of his childhood attachment and his instinct to formalize feeling into commitment.

- **[[dante-riordan|Dante]]'s view**: As the household's fierce guardian of Catholic orthodoxy, Dante sees Eileen primarily as a religious threat. She forbids Stephen to play with Eileen "because Eileen was a protestant" [001-295], recalling stories of Protestant children who mocked the litany. Dante's prohibition introduces Stephen to the adult world of sectarian boundaries, where affection must yield to religious loyalty — a lesson that will resonate throughout his later struggles with Irish Catholicism.

- **The Vance household**: The Vances are perceived by young Stephen as fundamentally "different" — "They had a different father and mother" [001-040]. In the child's taxonomy this marks them as a separate category of being, an early intimation of the social and religious partitions that structure Irish society.

- **Eileen's own voice**: Though Eileen speaks only indirectly through Stephen's memories — her remark that "pockets were funny things to have," her sudden laughter, her running down the path — her actions convey a spontaneous, joyful vitality. She is not a passive object of Stephen's gaze but a lively presence who initiates contact (putting her hands over his eyes, putting her hand in his pocket) and whose laughter and movement resist being fixed in any single interpretation.

## Significance

Eileen Vance is far more than a childhood crush. She is the novel's first embodiment of the connection between sensory beauty and spiritual meaning — her hands become ivory, her hair becomes gold, and the litany of the Virgin becomes a living reality rather than a rote prayer. At the same time, she introduces the theme of religious division that runs through the novel: the Protestant girl whose beauty transcends sectarian categories even as those categories forbid the friendship. In the arc of [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s development, Eileen represents the first step in a sequence that leads from childhood infatuation through adolescent lust to the aesthetic transfiguration of desire into art. She is prelude to the wading girl, who is herself prelude to the artist's vocation.

## Narrative Technique

- **Sensory-to-symbol transformation**: Joyce traces the full arc of artistic perception through Eileen — the cold, thin, soft sensation of her hands becomes "ivory," which becomes "Tower of Ivory," which becomes a meditation on the relationship between physical experience and spiritual meaning. This technique of layering sensation into symbol is the novel's fundamental aesthetic method and Eileen is its first demonstration.

- **Memory as recursive structure**: The memory of Eileen is not told once but revisited. Her hands appear first in direct recollection, then again with the added dimension of religious difference, then again in the context of the pocket and the running — each return adding a new layer. This recursive, accretive structure mirrors the way memory actually works, gathering associations over time.

- **Litany as literary device**: Joyce embeds the Litany of the Blessed Virgin — "Tower of Ivory," "House of Gold" — into Stephen's thoughts about Eileen, creating a palimpsest where Catholic devotion and romantic feeling are superimposed. The litany gives Stephen a vocabulary for beauty that he does not yet possess in secular terms, while the Protestant mockery of the litany introduces the fracture between aesthetic appreciation and religious identity.

- **Proleptic echo**: The memory of Eileen running down the path, her hair streaming gold, prefigures the wading girl on the strand. Joyce plants this childhood image so that when Stephen sees the girl in Chapter IV — "her thighs, fuller and softhued as ivory, were bared almost to the hips" [004-129] — the reader (and Stephen himself) recognizes the pattern: the first stirring of beauty in childhood returns, transfigured, at the moment of artistic vocation. Eileen is the seed; the wading girl is the flower.

- **Free indirect discourse**: The child's voice — "That was ivory: a cold white thing" — is rendered in free indirect style, neither fully Stephen's childish diction nor the mature narrator's analytical voice. This technique allows Joyce to present the child's associative thinking with immediacy while maintaining the retrospective awareness that this is the artist remembering his own formation.

## Relationships

Eileen is [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s neighbor in Bray and his first object of romantic attachment. Her family is Protestant, which creates tension with the devoutly Catholic [[dante-riordan|Dante]], who forbids the association. The Vance family occupies number seven, next to or near the [[stephen-dedalus|Dedalus]] household, and Eileen's parents are noted as being "different" — an observation that reflects [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s emerging consciousness of social and religious categories.
