## Overview

The Society of Jesus, commonly known as the Jesuits, is a Catholic religious order of priests and brothers founded by Saint Ignatius Loyola in 1540. The order's motto, *Ad Majorem Dei Gloriam* (A.M.D.G., "For the Greater Glory of God"), appears throughout Stephen Dedalus's school life. In *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*, the Jesuits are the educators responsible for Stephen's entire formal schooling, first at Clongowes Wood College and later at Belvedere College, and one of their priests, the director, attempts to recruit Stephen into the order.

## In the Novel

The Jesuit presence pervades Stephen's experience of education and religious authority. Young Stephen at Clongowes wonders about the hierarchical structure of the order: if a priest committed a sin, "he would go to confession to the minister. And if the minister did it he would go to the rector: and the rector to the provincial: and the provincial to the general of the jesuits" [001-519]. He recalls his father saying "they were all clever men. They could all have become high-up people in the world if they had not become jesuits" [001-590].

During the retreat sermons at Belvedere, Stephen passes portraits of Jesuit saints in the dark corridor: "saint Ignatius Loyola holding an open book and pointing to the words *Ad Majorem Dei Gloriam* in it, saint Francis Xavier pointing to his chest, Lorenzo Ricci with his berretta on his head" [003-221]. The physical iconography of the order imprints itself on Stephen's imagination, the portraits serving as silent witnesses to the spiritual exercises that will transform his adolescent conscience.

Mr Dedalus values a Jesuit education pragmatically, declaring: "let him stick to the jesuits in God's name since he began with them. They'll be of service to him in after years. Those are the fellows that can get you a position" [002-467]. Later he adds: "O, a jesuit for your life, for diplomacy!" [002-501].

At the Whitsuntide play, Stephen encounters "a plump freshfaced jesuit" painting boys' faces in the vestry, and a "young jesuit" who rocks rhythmically "from the tips of his toes to his heels," his "spotless decency of his soutane" matching "his spotless shoes" [002-741]. A saying of his father's comes to mind: "you could always tell a jesuit by the style of his clothes" [002-743].

The most consequential Jesuit encounter occurs in Chapter IV, when the director of Belvedere summons Stephen and, after casual conversation about "the colleges of the order abroad, the transference of masters" [004-019], asks whether Stephen has "ever felt within yourself, in your soul, a desire to join the order?" [004-021]. Stephen contemplates the "secret knowledge and secret power" of priesthood, imagining himself "a priest for ever according to the order of Melchisedec" [004-081]. But passing the "jesuit house in Gardiner Street," he realizes "his destiny was to be elusive of social or religious orders" [004-054], rejecting the vocation and choosing instead the life of an artist.

Stephen's considered judgment of the Jesuits is measured. Despite having heard of "the craft of jesuits," his own experience contradicts the stereotype: "His masters, even when they had not attracted him, had seemed to him always intelligent and serious priests, athletic and high-spirited prefects" [004-047]. In all his years at Clongowes and Belvedere, "he had never heard from any of his masters a flippant word" [004-047]. This fair-minded assessment, coming from a young man who has decisively rejected the Jesuit vocation, testifies to the order's genuine intellectual and moral formation even upon those who leave its orbit.

## Significance

The Jesuit order serves as the institutional embodiment of the religious, intellectual, and social order that Stephen must ultimately reject to become an artist. The order represents a genuine temptation — the offer of "secret knowledge and secret power" — and Stephen's refusal of the director's invitation marks the decisive turn from religious vocation to artistic calling. The meticulous intelligence Joyce attributes to the Jesuits also mirrors the scholastic precision of Stephen's own mental habits, suggesting that even in rebellion Stephen remains shaped by his Jesuit education. The order's pedagogical method, with its emphasis on rigorous disputation and the examination of conscience, provides the very mental tools Stephen will later use to construct his aesthetic theory.
