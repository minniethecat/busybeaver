## Overview

Ovid's *Metamorphoses* (completed AD 8) is a Latin narrative poem in fifteen books, chronicling the history of the world from creation to the deification of Julius Caesar through a series of mythological transformations. It is one of the most influential works of classical literature, shaping the Western artistic imagination from the Middle Ages through the Renaissance and beyond. In *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*, the *Metamorphoses* appears as a remembered text from [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s school days, one of the first examples he learned in Latin.

## In the Novel

The *Metamorphoses* surfaces in Chapter V as [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] reflects on his linguistic education. Remembering the Latin examples he had learnt as a schoolboy, he recalls: "India mittit ebur; and he recalled the shrewd northern face of the rector who had taught him to construe the Metamorphoses of Ovid in a courtly English, made whimsical by the mention of porkers and pots of comfiture" [005-046]. The rector's rendering of classical Latin into courtly English — a translation that mingles the elevated and the domestic — becomes for [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] a memory of how language mediates between worlds, and how the physical texture of words ("ivory, ivoire, avorio, ebur") carries its own aesthetic charge.

[[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s recollection of the *Metamorphoses* forms part of a larger meditation in Chapter V on the relationship between language, sensation, and beauty. The Latin word *ebur* (ivory) "shone in his brain, clearer and brighter than any ivory sawn from the mottled tusks of elephants" [005-046] — the word itself becomes an aesthetic object, independent of the thing it signifies.

## Significance

The *Metamorphoses* represents for [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] the point at which language instruction becomes aesthetic experience. He does not simply remember construing a Latin text; he remembers the *pleasure* of the act — the rector's "courtly English," the whimsical domestic details that brought the classical world into the schoolroom. This is consistent with [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s developing aesthetic theory, in which the *quidditas* or "whatness" of a thing — including a word — is the source of its beauty.
