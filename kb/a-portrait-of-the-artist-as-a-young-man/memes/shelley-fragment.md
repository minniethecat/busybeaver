## Introduction

**Shelley's Fragment** refers to an untitled lyrical fragment by Percy Bysshe Shelley (1792–1822) beginning "Art thou pale for weariness / Of climbing heaven and gazing on the earth." The poem addresses the moon and was among the Romantic verses that deeply influenced Stephen Dedalus's aesthetic sensibility in *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*. Shelley was a central figure of English Romanticism, known for his radical politics, his atheism, and his lyrical mastery — qualities that made him a compelling figure for the young Stephen.

## In the Novel

Stephen repeats "the lines of Shelley's fragment" to himself during a moment of emotional intensity, finding in its "alternation of sad human ineffectualness with vast inhuman cycles of activity" a reflection of his own adolescent melancholy [^1]. Later, he recalls "the words of Shelley's fragment upon the moon wandering companionless, pale for weariness" when hearing music, the verse merging with his sensory experience [^2]. Stephen also reflects on a "mysterious instant Shelley likened beautifully to a fading coal" — a reference to Shelley's *Defence of Poetry* [^3].

## The "Fading Coal" and Aesthetic Theory

Shelley's metaphor of inspiration as a "fading coal" — from *A Defence of Poetry* (1821) — resonates throughout Stephen's developing aesthetic philosophy. Shelley writes that "the mind in creation is as a fading coal, which some invisible influence, like an inconstant wind, awakens to transitory brightness." This conception of artistic creation as a fleeting, uncontrollable illumination parallels the Thomistic *claritas* that Stephen later adopts as the third quality of beauty. Stephen's encounter with Shelley thus provides a Romantic vocabulary for experiences that he will later re-articulate in the scholastic language of [[thomas-aquinas|Aquinas]].

## The Romantic Artist as Model

Shelley's figure of the poet as "unacknowledged legislator of the world" — set apart from society, sensitive to invisible influences, compelled to create — becomes a template for Stephen's self-conception. The Shelleyan artist is solitary, visionary, and defiant, qualities Stephen increasingly claims for himself. Yet Stephen's mature aesthetic, articulated in Chapter V, ultimately moves beyond Romantic subjectivity toward a more classical, Thomistic framework. Shelley represents a necessary phase in Stephen's development rather than its destination.

## Thematic Significance

Shelley's poetry provides Stephen with a vocabulary for articulating his own aesthetic experiences. The Romantic conception of the artist as a sensitive, isolated figure becomes a template for Stephen's developing self-conception. The moon imagery of the fragment — a solitary body "wandering companionless" — mirrors Stephen's own sense of isolation, while Shelley's "fading coal" provides an image for the ephemeral nature of inspiration that Stephen will wrestle with throughout his artistic maturation.
