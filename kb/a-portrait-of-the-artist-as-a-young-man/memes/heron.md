## Overview

Vincent Heron is one of [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s classmates at [[belvedere-college|Belvedere College]] and a memorable antagonist of Chapter II. With "a bird's face as well as a bird's name," Heron is the leader of a small gang — along with [[boland|Boland]] and [[nash|Nash]] — that torments [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] over his literary opinions. His casual cruelty and social confidence make him a representative of the ordinary world's hostility to artistic sensibility, and his demand that [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] recant his admiration for [[byron-poems|Byron]] is one of the novel's sharpest depictions of the pressure to conform.

## In the Novel

Heron first appears when [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] is walking with his father and encounters a group of [[belvedere-college|Belvedere]] students. "Before they reached them he had recognised Heron by his voice" [002-091]. Heron immediately adopts a mocking tone, "salaaming" before beginning "to poke fun at him" [002-093]. His manner is both aggressive and performative — he performs his superiority for the benefit of his friends.

The central confrontation between Heron and [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] turns on the question of literary taste. When the conversation turns to who is the greatest poet, [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] answers "[[byron-poems|Byron]], of course" [002-156]. Heron, reflecting the respectable Catholic opinion of the era, is outraged: "[[byron-poems|Byron]] the greatest poet! He's only a poet for uneducated people" [002-159]. He invokes morality against art: "In any case [[byron-poems|Byron]] was a heretic and immoral too" [002-166].

When [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] refuses to recant, Heron resorts to physical violence, striking [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] with a cane and demanding: "Admit that [[byron-poems|Byron]] was no good" [002-180]. Under duress, [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] capitulates. But the concession is hollow — his loyalty to [[byron-poems|Byron]] remains intact, and the episode becomes a formative experience in the young artist's understanding that the world will not welcome his difference.

## Relationships

Heron is the leader of the trio that includes [[boland|Boland]] and [[nash|Nash]]. His relationship with [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] is adversarial but not personal — Heron bullies [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] because [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] is different, not because of any particular grievance. This makes Heron a more frightening figure than a personal enemy would be: he represents the impersonal force of social conformity.

## Significance

Heron embodies the social world's resistance to artistic individuality. His demand that [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] abandon [[byron-poems|Byron]] for Tennyson is not merely a question of taste — it is an assertion that the artist must conform to respectable opinion. [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s forced recantation is a miniature of the larger drama of the novel: the artist's struggle to preserve his truth against the world's demand for conformity.
