## Overview

Mr Gleeson is a prefect at [[clongowes-wood-college|Clongowes Wood College]] in Chapter I of *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*. Though he never appears directly in a scene, his presence looms large in the schoolboy imagination as the figure who administers corporal punishment, becoming a symbol of institutional authority and the fear it generates.

## In the Novel

Mr Gleeson is first mentioned in the context of the group punishment following the "fecking" incident at [[clongowes-wood-college|Clongowes]]. A boy reports that "Mr Gleeson and the minister" were on the stairs, and that the offending students had been summoned [001-429]. This report circulates among the boys, feeding the atmosphere of fear and uncertainty that surrounds the exercise of authority at the school.

The figure of Mr Gleeson becomes central to the schoolboy lore of punishment. When students discuss the fate of the boys who broke their glasses, the threat of being "flogged by Mr Gleeson" is invoked as the ultimate sanction [001-493][001-585]. One boy acts out the ritual of punishment, rolling up his sleeves "to show how Mr Gleeson would roll up his sleeves" before administering a beating [001-506]. This performative re-enactment reveals how the prefect has become more myth than man in the schoolboy imagination — a figure whose gestures and rituals are scrutinised, parodied, and feared.

[[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] himself, in later reflection, considers what Mr Gleeson and another prefect "would have become if they had got the position they had applied for" [001-519] — a moment of imaginative empathy that hints at [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s growing ability to see authority figures as human beings with their own disappointments and limitations.

## Relationships

- **[[stephen-dedalus]]** — [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] participates in the collective schoolboy fear of Mr Gleeson but also shows glimmers of empathy toward him.
- **[[father-dolan]]** — Another prefect of studies whose more direct and violent exercise of authority contrasts with Mr Gleeson's role as a more distant, mythologised figure of punishment.

## Significance

Mr Gleeson illustrates Joyce's technique of characterisation through absence and rumour. Though never directly present in a scene, he is one of the most vividly imagined authority figures in Chapter I, constructed entirely through schoolboy gossip and re-enactment. This technique serves the novel's thematic concern with how institutions are experienced from below — not through official pronouncements but through the collective imagination of those subject to them.

The contrast between Mr Gleeson (imagined punisher, distant threat) and [[father-dolan|Father Dolan]] (actual punisher, immediate violence) structures the treatment of authority in Chapter I. Mr Gleeson represents the idea of punishment; [[father-dolan|Father Dolan]] represents its reality. [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s later reflection on what Mr Gleeson "would have become" marks an important developmental moment: the recognition that authority figures have their own histories, ambitions, and failures — a recognition that is part of [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s broader movement from seeing the world in absolutes to understanding it in complexities.
