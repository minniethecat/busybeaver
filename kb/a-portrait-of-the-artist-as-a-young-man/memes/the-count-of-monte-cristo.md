## Introduction

**The Count of Monte Cristo** (*Le Comte de Monte-Cristo*) is an 1844 adventure novel by Alexandre Dumas (père). In *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*, the young Stephen Dedalus reads a "ragged translation" of this novel during his adolescence in Blackrock, and its themes of betrayal, imprisonment, and revenge resonate with Stephen's developing sense of alienation and romantic self-conception. The novel's imaginative world — the island cave of treasure, the sunny port of Marseilles, the figure of the wronged and avenging hero — provides the raw material for Stephen's earliest attempts at self-fashioning.

## In the Novel

### The Ragged Translation

Stephen encounters *The Count of Monte Cristo* during a pivotal period of his boyhood, after the family's move from the comforts of Clongowes to the more modest circumstances of Blackrock. With his evenings free, Stephen retreats into a private imaginative world:

> His evenings were his own; and he pored over a ragged translation of *The Count of Monte Cristo*. The figure of that dark avenger stood forth in his mind for whatever he had heard or divined in childhood of the strange and terrible. At night he built up on the parlour table an image of the wonderful island cave out of transfers and paper flowers and coloured tissue paper and strips of the silver and golden paper in which chocolate is wrapped. [^1]

The "ragged translation" is significant — it is a second-hand, physically worn, linguistically mediated text, much like the English language itself is for Stephen. The "dark avenger" Edmond Dantès becomes the first in a series of literary models through which Stephen tries to understand his own emerging selfhood.

### The Romantic Fantasy

The novel's influence extends far beyond reading. Stephen constructs an elaborate fantasy life that blurs the boundary between Dumas's fiction and his own Dublin reality:

> Outside Blackrock, on the road that led to the mountains, stood a small whitewashed house in the garden of which grew many rosebushes: and in this house, he told himself, another Mercedes lived. Both on the outward and on the homeward journey he measured distance by this landmark: and in his imagination he lived through a long train of adventures, marvellous as those in the book itself, towards the close of which there appeared an image of himself, grown older and sadder, standing in a moonlit garden. [^2]

Stephen does not merely read Dumas's novel — he inhabits it, transposing its romantic geography onto the familiar landscape of the Dublin mountains. The whitewashed house with its rosebushes becomes the Count's island retreat; an unknown woman within becomes Mercedes, the novel's heroine. Stephen himself becomes the protagonist of his own adventure, "grown older and sadder" — a boy rehearsing the melancholy of a man he has not yet become.

The fantasy takes on an increasingly erotic charge as Stephen's adolescence progresses:

> He returned to Mercedes and, as he brooded upon her image, a strange unrest crept into his blood. Sometimes a fever gathered within him and led him to rove alone in the evening along the quiet avenue. He did not want to play. He wanted to meet in the real world the unsubstantial image which his soul so constantly beheld. [^3]

The "unsubstantial image" — Mercedes, the romantic ideal — prefigures the series of idealized feminine figures that will haunt Stephen throughout the novel: Eileen, the bird-girl, Emma Clery. Each is a variation on the same impossible desire: to encounter in the real world an image generated by art.

### Performance and Play

Stephen's identification with Dantès shapes his social behavior. He founds a "gang of adventurers" with his friend Aubrey Mills and, consciously modeling himself on literary and historical figures, adopts a style of dress and command:

> Stephen, who had read of Napoleon's plain style of dress, chose to remain unadorned and thereby heightened for himself the pleasure of taking counsel with his lieutenant before giving orders. [^4]

This moment — a boy refusing costume ornaments because Napoleon would not wear them — captures the seriousness with which Stephen treats his literary identifications. He is not playing at being a hero; he is *rehearsing* a role he believes himself destined to play. The gang's forays into gardens and castle battles are not mere childhood games but early experiments in self-creation.

Stephen even quotes the novel directly. Confronted with muscatel grapes, he adopts the Count's worldly sophistication:

> —Madam, I never eat muscatel grapes. [^5]

The line, absurd in its pretension when spoken by a Dublin schoolboy, is nevertheless a genuine attempt to inhabit a more refined, more powerful self — the self Stephen imagines he might become if he can escape the nets that constrain him.

## The Edmond Dantès Archetype

The protagonist of Dumas's novel, Edmond Dantès, follows a trajectory of unjust imprisonment, secret education under the Abbé Faria, escape from the Château d'If, discovery of hidden treasure on the island of Monte Cristo, and calculated revenge against those who betrayed him. This narrative pattern offers Stephen an imaginative template for his own predicament: like Dantès in his cell, Stephen feels imprisoned by the nets of family, church, and nation that he will later declare his intention to fly past. The Count's transformation — from naive young sailor to sophisticated, multilingual, fabulously wealthy agent of justice — mirrors Stephen's own aspiration to transform himself from a passive recipient of Dublin life into an active creator of art.

The treasure cave that Stephen builds on the parlour table — "out of transfers and paper flowers and coloured tissue paper" — is a child's model of artistic creation: the transformation of humble materials into an imagined world. The "tinsel" that eventually wearies him anticipates his later rejection of Romantic excess in favor of aesthetic austerity.

## Thematic Significance

*The Count of Monte Cristo* occupies a specific developmental stage in Stephen's imaginative life. It belongs to the Romantic adventure tradition — alongside [[walter-scott-works|Walter Scott]] and [[lord-byron|Byron]] — that shaped Stephen's early reading, before his taste matured toward [[aristotle-poetics|Aristotle]], [[thomas-aquinas|Aquinas]], and the more austere aesthetic principles of his later years [^6].

But the novel's influence persists even as Stephen outgrows its tinsel. The "dark avenger" archetype — the isolated, wronged figure who remakes himself through knowledge and will — remains a submerged pattern in Stephen's self-conception. When Stephen later declares that he will "fly by those nets" of nationality, language, and religion, he is still, in a transformed key, the boy who imagined himself the Count of Monte Cristo, standing alone in a moonlit garden, awaiting a destiny of his own making.

The episode also illuminates the social texture of Stephen's reading life. The "ragged translation" is not a pristine literary text but a material object marked by use, passed through hands, possibly the only version of Dumas available to a boy in provincial Dublin. Stephen's literary culture is, from the beginning, second-hand and mediated — a condition that echoes his relationship to the English language itself, which he will later describe as "so familiar and so foreign," an "acquired speech" in the shadow of which his soul "frets" [^7].
