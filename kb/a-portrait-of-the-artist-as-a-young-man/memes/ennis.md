## Overview

Ennis is a student at [[belvedere-college|Belvedere College]] who appears in Chapters III and IV of *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*. He is primarily remembered for two vivid details: his humiliation in the mathematics classroom when unable to define a surd, and his scarlet belt with a snaky clasp, which [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] later sees as a sign of adolescent awkwardness.

## In the Novel

Ennis first appears in the mathematics lesson during the [[the-retreat|retreat sermons]] of Chapter III. When [[father-arnall|Father Arnall]] asks him to define a surd, Ennis fails dramatically, prompting the teacher's sardonic remark: "Well now, Ennis, I declare you have a head and so has my stick! Do you mean to say that you are not able to tell me what a surd is?" [003-010]. Ennis's public humiliation in the classroom is one of the many small cruelties of Jesuit education that Joyce depicts, and it contributes to the atmosphere of fear and inadequacy that pervades the retreat chapters.

After the lesson, Ennis goes out to the yard and returns, saying something to the class [003-017] — a minor detail that shows the ordinary rhythms of school life continuing around moments of individual drama.

Ennis reappears in Chapter IV, where [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] encounters him on the street after his period of intense religious devotion. Seeing his former classmates — Shuley without his collar, Ennis without his scarlet belt with the snaky clasp, and Connolly without his Norfolk coat — [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] is struck by how "characterless" they look [004-095]. The detail of the scarlet belt is particularly evocative: it captures both the adolescent desire for distinctive adornment and [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s newly critical, detached perspective on his peers. The boys' "pitiable nakedness" and their "secret dread" are seen by [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] from a distance that marks his growing alienation from the community of his schoolfellows.

## Significance

Ennis serves two important functions in the novel's structure. In Chapter III, his classroom humiliation contributes to the atmosphere of judgment and inadequacy that the [[the-retreat|retreat sermons]] amplify — the fear of being found wanting, of being publicly exposed as ignorant or [[sin-and-confession|sin]]ful, permeates both the academic and spiritual dimensions of [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s life. In Chapter IV, Ennis's reappearance — specifically the detail of his scarlet belt — becomes one of the sensory triggers through which [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] perceives the gulf that has opened between himself and his former companions. What was once familiar now appears "characterless," and Ennis's snaky belt clasp becomes a symbol of the adolescent world that [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] is preparing to leave behind.
