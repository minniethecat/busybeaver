## Overview

Mick Lacy is a figure from the youth of [[simon-dedalus|Simon Dedalus]], recalled during the family's visit to [[cork|Cork]] in Chapter II. He is remembered not for any direct appearance in the novel's present action but as a voice — a singer whose performance of a sentimental song embodies the nostalgic, self-indulgent world of Simon's reminiscences.

## In the Novel

### A Voice from the Past

The Cork episode begins with Stephen waking to his father singing "The Mountain Dew," a sentimental come-all-you about a young man's parting from his sweetheart. The song's melody — "strange sad happy air" — clears the night's ill humour from Stephen's mind, and he tells his father he likes it [^1]. Simon, twirling his moustache, responds with a lament for the singer who could truly do the song justice:

> —It's a pretty old air, said Mr Dedalus, twirling the points of his moustache. Ah, but you should have heard Mick Lacy sing it! Poor Mick Lacy! He had little turns for it, grace notes that he used to put in that I haven't got. That was the boy who could sing a come-all-you, if you like. [^2]

The exclamation "poor Mick Lacy" — twice repeated — marks Lacy as dead by the time of the novel's action. He is another ghost from Simon's past, summoned in a hotel room for an audience of one: his silent, increasingly alienated son.

### Among the Living and the Dead

Later in the same episode, as Simon walks Stephen past the Groceries — a Cork public house of his youth — he recites a fuller roll-call of his old companions:

> —Ay, bedad! And there's the Groceries sure enough! cried Mr Dedalus. You often heard me speak of the Groceries, didn't you, Stephen. Many's the time we went down there when our names had been marked, a crowd of us, Harry Peard and little Jack Mountain and Bob Dyas and Maurice Moriarty, the Frenchman, and Tom O'Grady and Mick Lacy that I told you of this morning and Joey Corbet and poor little goodhearted Johnny Keevers of the Tantiles. [^3]

Lacy is embedded in a catalogue of nine names — a litany of the dead and dispersed. For [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]], who "walked on at his father's side, listening to stories he had heard before, hearing again the names of the scattered and dead revellers who had been the companions of his father's youth," these names provoke not nostalgia but a "faint sickness" that "sighed in his heart" [^4].

### Stephen's Alienation

The Cork episode is structured around a growing chasm between Simon's reminiscences and Stephen's internal experience. While Simon seeks to pass on a legacy — "I treat you as your grandfather treated me when I was a young chap. We were more like brothers than father and son" [^5] — Stephen can only retreat inward, sickened by the past that his father keeps resurrecting:

> Nothing moved him or spoke to him from the real world unless he heard in it an echo of the infuriated cries within him. He could respond to no earthly or human appeal, dumb and insensible to the call of summer and gladness and companionship, wearied and dejected by his father's voice. [^6]

Mick Lacy, in this context, is not simply a colorful anecdote from Simon's youth. He is one of many names that Simon deploys to construct a world of masculine camaraderie — "fine decent fellows," "all gentlemen," "bloody good honest Irishmen" — that Stephen feels himself fundamentally excluded from. Simon's boast that "everyone of us could do something" — one could sing, one could act, one could row — is a taxonomy of belonging that Stephen, with his "equivocal position" and his "monstrous reveries," cannot inhabit [^7].

## Significance

Mick Lacy is one of the novel's many spectral presences — characters who never appear in the present action but whose names are invoked in memory and story. He belongs to the novel's pattern of songs, singers, and voices: the sailor's hornpipe of Stephen's infancy, the hymn *[[pange-lingua|Pange Lingua]]*, the sentimental ballad "Lily Dale" that moves Stephen to tears, and "The Mountain Dew" that Mick Lacy sang so beautifully that Simon Dedalus still remembers it decades later.

But Lacy also serves a structural function in the novel's treatment of fathers and sons. Simon's reminiscences are an attempted transmission — a father trying to give his son a usable past. Stephen's inability to receive this gift — his "faint sickness" at the names of the dead, his retreat into "the infuriated cries within him" — marks the failure of that transmission. The voices of Simon's world (Mick Lacy's silver thread among them) cannot reach Stephen, who must find his own voice, in his own language, on his own terms.

These voices — some sacred, some profane, some remembered — form the auditory texture of Stephen's world, the background music against which he learns to find his own voice. Mick Lacy never sings in the novel; we hear only the echo of his song through Simon's memory. But that echo, fading and irrecoverable, is precisely the point.
