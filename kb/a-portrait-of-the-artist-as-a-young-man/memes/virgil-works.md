## Overview

Virgil (70–19 BC) was the greatest poet of Augustan Rome, author of the *Eclogues*, the *Georgics*, and above all the *Aeneid*, the national epic of Rome. His poetry was central to the classical curriculum of Catholic education in Ireland, and Latin verse was a daily fact of Stephen's life at [[clongowes-wood-college|Clongowes]] and [[belvedere-college|Belvedere]]. In *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*, Virgil appears not as a name to be cited but as a voice felt in the body — language that "gives utterance" to a mood beyond paraphrase.

## In the Novel

Virgil enters the novel in Chapter IV, during a passage of intense lyrical concentration. Stephen, walking through the streets of Dublin, is remembering the sound of lines from Virgil that he associates with Cardinal [[cardinal-newman-prose|Newman]]: "He remembered that Newman had heard this note also in the broken lines of Virgil, 'giving utterance, like the voice of Nature herself, to that pain and weariness yet hope of better things...'" [004-067].

The phrase "broken lines" is significant. Virgil's hexameters are not broken in any formal sense, but Stephen — following Newman — hears them as *broken*, as if the Latin verse carries within its flawless surface a fracture, an incompleteness that speaks to the human condition. The "note" that Newman and Stephen both hear is elegiac: a sound of longing, of exile (Virgil's Aeneas is a perpetual exile), of the gap between the world as it is and the world as the heart desires.

This moment is all the more powerful because Stephen does not name a specific passage. What matters is not a particular line but the quality of Virgil's voice — a quality that survives translation, and even survives the decay of the language itself into fragments of memory.

## Significance

Virgil occupies a unique place in Stephen's aesthetic education because he is heard rather than read. Unlike [[horace-works|Horace]], whose pages Stephen *touches*, Virgil is a sound — the "broken lines" that ring in the memory. This auditory dimension is essential to Stephen's emerging [[aesthetic-theory|aesthetic theory]], which privileges the *sensible* qualities of the work of art (its rhythm, its sonic texture) over its discursive content. Virgil, the poet of exile, also prefigures Stephen's own sense of displacement — his feeling of being neither fully Irish nor fully at home in the cosmopolitan literary world he aspires to join.
