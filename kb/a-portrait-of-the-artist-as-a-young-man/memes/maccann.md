## Overview

MacCann is a student activist at [[university-college-dublin|University College Dublin]] who appears in Chapter V as the bearer of a petition for universal peace. He is energetic, well-meaning, and completely unable to understand [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s refusal to participate in collective political action. Their confrontation is one of the novel's clearest statements of [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s artistic credo: the artist serves no cause but his own.

## In the Novel

MacCann enters briskly, "marching" toward [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] and [[cranly|Cranly]]: "Here you are! said MacCann cheerily" [005-204]. He is immediately identified by his political enthusiasm — "MacCann is in tiptop form. Ready to shed the last drop" [005-194], [[cranly|Cranly]] says. MacCann presents his petition, and the conversation quickly becomes a philosophical confrontation.

MacCann challenges [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] directly: "I thought you were an idealist" [005-213]. His assumption is that anyone with ideals must support organized political action. [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s refusal confounds him. When [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] speaks of the artist's necessary detachment, MacCann cuts through with characteristic bluntness: "Metaphors! Come to facts" [005-238]. For MacCann, the only facts are political ones — signatures on a petition, actions in the world.

The confrontation ends in mutual incomprehension. MacCann cannot understand why [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] will not sign, and [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] cannot explain to MacCann why the artist's refusal is itself a form of action. MacCann's final "Good! You are a reactionary!" [005-236] is both a political judgment and an accurate description of [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s position — if "reactionary" is understood as a refusal to be swept up in the progressive currents of one's time.

## Relationships

MacCann is a fellow student at [[university-college-dublin|the university]], respected by [[cranly|Cranly]] but fundamentally opposed to [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s worldview. His relationship with [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] is defined by the petition — an attempt at recruitment that becomes a philosophical debate. Unlike [[cranly|Cranly]] or [[lynch|Lynch]], MacCann does not seek to understand [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]; he seeks to enlist him.

## Significance

MacCann represents the world of political action — the Ireland of Tone and [[charles-parnell|Parnell]], of petitions and movements — that [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] is leaving behind. His inability to comprehend [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s refusal mirrors the broader Irish incomprehension of Joyce's own artistic trajectory. MacCann is not a villain — he is [[sin-and-confession|sin]]cere in his beliefs — but his [[sin-and-confession|sin]]cerity is precisely what makes him dangerous to the artist: he offers a cause worth serving, and [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] must refuse it.
