## Overview

Victor Hugo was a French poet, novelist, and dramatist of the Romantic movement, author of *Les Misérables* (1862) and *Notre-Dame de Paris* (1831). In the novel, Hugo appears in a remembered conversation between [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] and a Christian Brother — a moment that crystallises Stephen's developing sense of the conflict between artistic greatness and institutional conformity. Hugo's career as an artist who defied political authority (he spent nearly twenty years in exile opposing Napoleon III) makes him an especially apt figure for Stephen's emerging conviction that the true artist must stand apart from institutions.

## In the Novel

### The Conversation with the Priest

Stephen, walking along the Bull Wall in Chapter IV, recalls a conversation he once had with a Christian Brother about literature:

> "He had then asked the priest if Victor Hugo were not the greatest French writer." [004-032]

The priest's reply is cautious, hedging between recognition of Hugo's achievement and disapproval of his moral character:

> "The priest had answered that Victor Hugo had never written half so well when he had turned against the church as he had written when he was a Catholic." [004-033]

This response — evaluating a writer's work by his orthodoxy rather than his art — strikes Stephen as intellectually dishonest. The priest's judgment is a miniature of the conflict Stephen increasingly feels: the claims of art against the claims of the [[the-catholic-church|Church]].

### Stephen's Silent Judgment

Stephen does not argue with the priest. He absorbs the answer, files it away, and continues to read what he wishes. But the remembered conversation marks a turning point: Stephen is beginning to see that the institutional world around him will always subordinate aesthetic truth to doctrinal conformity. Hugo's name becomes, in Stephen's private lexicon, a signifier of the art that the Church cannot fully contain or evaluate.

> "He had then asked the priest if Victor Hugo were not the greatest French writer... The priest, who considered that even Victor Hugo, great as he certainly was, had written less admirably after his break with the Church, had little doubt that the French master's later work showed a decline." [004-032, 004-033, abridged]

## Hugo the Romantic Artist

Hugo's career trajectory — from Catholic royalist in his youth to republican freethinker in his maturity — mirrors the arc that Stephen himself will trace, albeit in compressed form. Hugo's exile on Guernsey (1855–1870), during which he produced some of his greatest work including *Les Misérables*, embodies the Romantic ideal of the artist who must separate himself from his native country in order to truly create. Stephen's own declaration at the novel's end — that he will "fly by those nets" of nationality, language, and religion — echoes the Hugo who defied Napoleon III and chose artistic integrity over political safety.

## Significance

Victor Hugo serves as a touchstone for Stephen's emerging aesthetic independence. The conversation with the priest encapsulates the fundamental tension of the novel: the artist's soul against the nets of religion, language, and nationality that Stephen declares he will fly by. Hugo — the French Romantic who defied Napoleon III and went into exile — represents the artist's vocation as Stephen increasingly understands it: a calling that may require one to break with the institutions of one's youth.
