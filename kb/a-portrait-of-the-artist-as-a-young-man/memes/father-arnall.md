## Overview

Father Arnall is a Jesuit [[priesthood-and-religion|priest]] who appears in two crucial chapters of *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*. In Chapter I, he is [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s Latin teacher at [[clongowes-wood-college|Clongowes Wood College]], a figure of stern but not unkind authority. In Chapter III, he reappears at [[belvedere-college|Belvedere College]] to deliver the [[the-retreat|retreat sermons]] on death, judgment, heaven, and hell — the most psychologically intense passages in the novel. Through Father Arnall, Joyce channels the full rhetorical power of Jesuit education and its capacity to terrify as well as to instruct.

## In the Novel

In Chapter I, Father Arnall teaches [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s class at [[clongowes-wood-college|Clongowes]]. [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] initially respects him: "Father Arnall knew more than [[dante-riordan|Dante]] because he was a [[priesthood-and-religion|priest]]" [001-053]. In the classroom, Father Arnall is demanding but fair, writing "a hard sum on the board" [001-063] and growing stern when students cannot answer: "You should be ashamed of yourself, you, the leader of the class!" [001-515]. When [[father-dolan|Father Dolan]] unjustly punishes [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]], Father Arnall's quiet presence in the scene underscores the ambiguity of [[priesthood-and-religion|clerical authority]].

Father Arnall's most powerful appearance comes in Chapter III, where he delivers the [[the-retreat|annual retreat]] sermons at [[belvedere-college|Belvedere]]. His descriptions of hell draw on centuries of Catholic rhetoric: the "terrible conflagration of the last day" [003-073] that "has purged the world," the "doctrines of the church" [003-023] presented with terrifying vividness. His words penetrate [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s [[sin-and-confession|guilt]]y conscience with surgical precision, reducing the boy to a state of existential terror that drives him to [[sin-and-confession|confession]].

The contrast between the classroom teacher of Chapter I and the hell-fire preacher of Chapter III is one of the novel's most effective psychological transitions. The same [[priesthood-and-religion|priest]] who once taught [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] Latin now teaches him the geography of [[hell|damnation]].

## Significance

Father Arnall personifies the dual nature of Jesuit education — intellectually rigorous and psychologically powerful. He is neither a villain nor a hero; he is the institutional voice of [[the-catholic-church|the Church]], performing his duty with the same competence whether teaching Latin or describing eternal torment. His sermons represent the most complete expression of the Catholic worldview [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] must absorb before he can reject it.

Father Arnall also serves a structural function in the novel: his reappearance links [[clongowes-wood-college|Clongowes]] and [[belvedere-college|Belvedere]], the classroom and the chapel, showing that the same institutional authority follows [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] throughout his youth. The [[priesthood-and-religion|priest]]'s transition from teacher to preacher mirrors [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s own transition from intellectual curiosity to spiritual crisis.
