## Overview

Cranly is the most significant of [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen Dedalus]]'s university companions in *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*. Dark-eyed, long-lipped, and emotionally intense, Cranly serves as [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s secular confessor — the friend to whom [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] unburdens himself of his rejection of the Catholic Church and his decision to leave Ireland. Their extended conversation in Chapter V is the novel's emotional climax, a dialogue that tests [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s artistic absolutism against the claims of human love.

## In the Novel

Cranly first appears in Chapter V as a figure of quiet intensity. When [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] thinks of him, he finds that "when he thought of Cranly he could never raise before his mind the entire body but the head only" [005-039] — an image of Cranly as a disembodied intelligence, a mind rather than a person. Throughout the novel's final chapter, Cranly watches [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] with "dark eyes" [005-176], his "pale face" [005-183] and "long thinlipped mouth" [005-178] constituting a physical presence that is at once intimate and unnerving.

The central scene between [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] and Cranly occupies the latter part of Chapter V. As they walk through [[dublin|Dublin]] together, [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] reveals that he has refused to make his Easter duty — the Catholic obligation of [[sin-and-confession|confession]] and communion — and has quarreled with his mother over it. Cranly listens, questions, and finally challenges [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] with a question that cuts to the heart of the matter: "Whatever else is unsure in this stinking dunghill of a world a mother's love is not" [005-498]. The line is famous not only for its earthy poetry but for its moral force: Cranly names the human cost of [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s absolutism.

Cranly's final question — "Are you a believer in the word as it is?" [005-500] — is perhaps the most searching in the novel. It asks not whether [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] believes in God but whether he believes in love, in loyalty, in the bonds that hold human beings together. [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s inability to give a straightforward answer marks the limit of his artistic vision.

## Relationships

Cranly is [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s most intimate friend, but the intimacy is asymmetrical. [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] confides in Cranly; Cranly questions and challenges. Their relationship has the quality of a [[sin-and-confession|confession]] without a [[priesthood-and-religion|priest]], a friendship that takes on the weight of spiritual direction. Cranly is also protective of [[temple|Temple]], the eccentric student, revealing a tenderness beneath his austere exterior.

## Significance

Cranly represents the human world [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] must leave behind. Unlike [[maccann|MacCann]] or [[davin|Davin]], who represent specific ideologies, Cranly represents love itself — the claim of human connection against the artist's necessary isolation. [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s inability to answer Cranly's final question is Joyce's acknowledgment that the artistic vocation comes at a real human cost.
