## Overview

Thomas Aquinas (1225–1274) was a Dominican friar and scholastic philosopher whose works, particularly the *Summa Theologica*, exercised a profound influence on Catholic theology and education. In *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*, [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen Dedalus]] engages extensively with Aquinas's ideas, u[[sin-and-confession|sin]]g them as the intellectual foundation for the aesthetic theory he develops in Chapter V. For [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]], Aquinas represents the most sophisticated expression of the Catholic intellectual tradition he is simultaneously absorbing and transcending.

## In the Novel

[[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s engagement with Aquinas is concentrated in Chapter V, where he expounds his aesthetic theory to [[lynch|Lynch]]. He invokes Aquinas repeatedly to define and justify his ideas about beauty and art: "Aquinas, answered [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]], says *pulcra sunt quae visa placent*" — that is beautiful the apprehension of which pleases [005-086]. He uses Aquinas's definition of beauty as the starting point for his own theory, acknowledging that "Bonum est in quod tendit appetitus" — the good is that towards which an appetite tends [005-088].

[[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s relationship with Aquinas is both reverent and critical. He applies "Aquinas. So far as this side of esthetic philosophy extends, Aquinas will carry me all along the line" [005-388], but also acknowledges the medieval thinker's limitations. When [[lynch|Lynch]] protests that "after all Aquinas, in spite of his intellect, was exactly a wise man", [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] deflects with humour [005-389].

The key Aquinas-derived concepts in [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s theory are the three requirements for beauty: *integritas* (wholeness), *consonantia* (harmony), and *claritas* (radiance). [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] quotes Aquinas: "Ad pulcritudinem tria requiruntur: integritas, consonantia, claritas" [005-419]. From these scholastic categories, [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] builds his own modernist aesthetic, translating Aquinas's Latin terms into a theory of artistic apprehension.

## Significance

Aquinas's presence in the novel serves multiple purposes. He represents the intellectual sophistication of the Catholic tradition that formed [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]], demonstrating that [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s rebellion is not against ignorance but against a system whose intellectual heights he fully appreciates. At the same time, [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s selective use of Aquinas — taking what serves his artistic purposes and discarding the rest — mirrors his broader selective relationship with tradition.

The figure of Aquinas also embodies the novel's mediation between scholastic rationalism and modernist [[aesthetic-theory|aesthetics]]. [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s project of building an aesthetic theory from Aquinas's theological categories is a microcosm of Joyce's own artistic method: transforming the materials of his Catholic education into the substance of his art.
