## Overview

*Claritas* (Latin for "clarity" or "radiance") is the third term in [[saint-thomas-aquinas|Thomas Aquinas]]'s triad of requirements for beauty — *integritas* (wholeness), *consonantia* (harmony), and *claritas* — which [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen Dedalus]] adapts as the foundation of his aesthetic theory in Chapter V of *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*. Of the three terms, *claritas* is the most mysterious and the most contested: [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] himself acknowledges its vagueness before offering his own interpretation, in which *claritas* becomes synonymous with the artistic revelation of a thing's essential "whatness."

## In the Novel

[[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] introduces the triad during his extended aesthetic dialogue with [[lynch|Lynch]], quoting Aquinas directly: "*Ad pulcritudinem tria requiruntur integritas, consonantia, claritas*" — three things are required for beauty: wholeness, harmony, and radiance [005-419]. He explains *integritas* as the mind's separation of an object from the rest of the visible universe, and *consonantia* as the apprehension of the object's internal rhythmic structure [005-424]. But when he arrives at the third term, [[lynch|Lynch]] interrupts him with a challenge: "Tell me now what is *claritas* and you win the cigar" [005-427].

[[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] concedes the difficulty: "The connotation of the word, Stephen said, is rather vague. Aquinas uses a term which seems to be inexact. It puzzled me myself long ago" [005-428]. He rejects interpretations that reduce *claritas* to a merely intellectual quality — "the artistic discovery and representation of the divine purpose in anything or a force of generalization which would make the aesthetic image a universal one" [005-428] — and instead grounds it in a more concrete experience: the moment when "the clear radiance of the aesthetic image is apprehended luminously by the mind" [005-429].

His final definition links *claritas* to the Scholastic concept of *quidditas*, the "whatness" of a thing: "the instant wherein that supreme quality of beauty, the clear radiance of the aesthetic image, is apprehended luminously by the mind which has been arrested by its wholeness and fascinated by its harmony is the luminous silent stasis of aesthetic pleasure" [005-429]. In this formulation, *claritas* is not something the artist adds to the work but something the work reveals when it has achieved *integritas* and *consonantia* — the moment when form becomes radiant with meaning.

## Significance

*Claritas* occupies a pivotal position in [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s aesthetic because it marks the threshold between technique and revelation. Where *integritas* and *consonantia* can be achieved through craft — the artist separates the image from the world, then arranges its parts in rhythm — *claritas* seems to happen *to* the artist and the audience rather than being produced *by* them. It is the quality that transforms a well-made thing into a work of art.

This quasi-mystical dimension of *claritas* connects [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s aesthetic theory to the religious vocabulary he has ostensibly abandoned. The "luminous silent stasis of aesthetic pleasure" echoes the language of mystical contemplation, and the idea that the supreme quality of beauty is apprehended rather than manufactured recalls the theology of grace. [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] has transposed the structure of religious experience into the key of art: the artist becomes a secular priest, and the aesthetic image becomes a secular sacrament, radiant with its own immanent meaning.

The concept also illuminates Joyce's own artistic practice. The famous "epiphanies" that Joyce wrote and collected — moments of sudden spiritual manifestation — are essentially experiences of *claritas*, instants in which the "whatness" of a thing shines forth. *Portrait* itself can be read as an extended demonstration of *claritas* in action: the novel's prose rhythm, its symbolic patterning, and its structural integrity are all in service of those moments of luminous apprehension that constitute the novel's highest effects.
