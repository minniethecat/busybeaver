## Overview

Saint Francis Xavier was a sixteenth-century Navarrese missionary and one of the original companions of Saint Ignatius of Loyola in founding the [[jesuit-order|Society of Jesus]]. In the novel, he is the patron saint of both [[clongowes-wood-college|Clongowes Wood College]] and [[belvedere-college|Belvedere College]], and his feast day provides the occasion for the annual retreat that dominates the spiritual crisis of Chapter III.

## In the Novel

### The College Patron

The portrait of Saint Francis Xavier hangs in the study hall at Clongowes, part of the visual landscape of Stephen's childhood. [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] sees it alongside other images of Jesuit devotion:

> "There was a picture of the earth on the first page of his geography: a big ball in the middle of clouds... and in the study hall the picture of saint Francis Xavier pointing to his chest, Lorenzo Ricci with his biretta, the three patrons of holy youth — saint Ignatius Loyola holding an open book, saint Francis Xavier pointing to his chest, saint Stanislaus Kostka with the infant Jesus." [001-590, abridged]

This catalogue of Jesuit iconography — Xavier pointing to his chest where, according to legend, he carried a picture of the Virgin — establishes the visual and devotional atmosphere in which Stephen's childhood unfolds.

### The Retreat

The annual retreat is held in honour of Saint Francis Xavier's feast day:

> "The retreat will begin on Wednesday afternoon in honour of saint Francis Xavier whose feast day is Saturday. The retreat will go on from Wednesday to Friday. On Friday confession will be heard all the afternoon after beads." [003-028]

The retreat sermons preached by [[father-arnall|Father Arnall]] use Xavier's life as a model of zeal and sanctity:

> "He would tell them the story of the life of saint Francis Xavier, I suppose, the patron of your college. A great saint, saint Francis Xavier! A great fisher of souls!" [003-032, 003-034]

The phrase "fisher of souls" transforms Xavier's missionary work into a metaphor for the spiritual conquest that the retreat urges upon the boys — the conquest of their own sinful natures.

### Xavier's Intercession

Father Arnall concludes the retreat with a prayer invoking Xavier's merits:

> "Year after year for much longer than any of you, my dear little boys, can remember... the young men of this college have made this annual retreat... in honour of the merits of His zealous servant Francis Xavier, that such a soul may be led to the true knowledge of God." [003-039, 003-042, abridged]

The irony is deep: the retreat meant to bring Stephen closer to God instead precipitates his most intense crisis of faith. Xavier, the "great fisher of souls," fails to net Stephen's.

## Significance

Saint Francis Xavier represents the institutional weight of Jesuit education in the novel. His feast day frames the retreat that marks the peak of Stephen's religious experience — and the beginning of its dissolution. Like the other Jesuit saints depicted at Clongowes, Xavier is both a spiritual exemplar and a reminder of the system of authority, discipline, and guilt that Stephen will ultimately reject.
