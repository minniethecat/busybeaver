## Overview

Temple is one of the most peculiar minor characters in *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*. A student at [[university-college-dublin|University College Dublin]], he is physically small, socially inept, and perpetually intruding on the conversations of others with inappropriate enthusiasm. He functions primarily as comic relief in the intellectually dense atmosphere of Chapter V, but his presence also illuminates the social dynamics of [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s university circle — particularly the protective role [[cranly|Cranly]] plays toward him.

## In the Novel

Temple's most extended appearance comes in Chapter V, during the political discussions that precede [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s walk with [[cranly|Cranly]]. When [[maccann|MacCann]] is collecting signatures for his petition, Temple interrupts with the declaration: "I believe in universal brotherhood" [005-221]. His pronouncement, delivered with "glancing about him out of his dark oval eyes," is met with derision — [[cranly|Cranly]] "took him by the neck and shook him" and told him to "go home" [005-224].

Temple's persistence in the face of rejection is both comic and slightly painful. He "bent eagerly across [[cranly|Cranly]]" [005-262] to address [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]], attempting to insert himself into conversations where he is not wanted. His physical awkwardness — "Temple wriggled in his grip, laughing" [005-272] — marks him as a perpetual outsider, tolerated but never fully accepted.

[[cranly|Cranly]]'s treatment of Temple reveals an unexpected tenderness. When [[cranly|Cranly]] tells him "Temple, I declare to the living God if you say another word I'll fling you out of the window" [005-278], the threat is clearly affectionate, and Temple's knowing grin suggests he understands the dynamic. The relationship between [[cranly|Cranly]] and Temple — protector and protected, older brother and younger fool — humanizes [[cranly|Cranly]] and reveals the social complexity beneath the intellectual surface of [[university-college-dublin|the university]] scenes.

## Relationships

Temple is attached to [[cranly|Cranly]]'s circle but exists on its periphery. His relationship with [[cranly|Cranly]] is the most developed in the novel — [[cranly|Cranly]] treats him with a mixture of exasperation and protectiveness that suggests genuine affection. [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] observes Temple with the detachment of the artist, noting his peculiarities without cruelty.

## Significance

Temple serves several functions in the novel. He provides comic relief in Chapter V's dense intellectual exchanges. He reveals [[cranly|Cranly]]'s capacity for kindness beneath his austere exterior. And he embodies the social reality that [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s circle, for all its intellectual pretension, includes the awkward and the marginal — a reminder that university life is not only about ideas but about the strange community of young men thrown together by circumstance.
