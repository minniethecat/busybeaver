## Overview

The *Vexilla Regis* ("The Banners of the King") is a Latin hymn composed by Venantius Fortunatus (c. 530–609 AD) for the procession of a relic of the True Cross to a monastery in Poitiers. It is one of the great liturgical hymns of the Western Church, sung during Passiontide (the two weeks before Easter), and is renowned for its mournful majesty and its fusion of military and devotional imagery. In *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*, the *Vexilla Regis* appears in Chapter V as a touchstone in [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s conversation about aesthetics with [[lynch|Lynch]].

## In the Novel

The *Vexilla Regis* appears at the culmination of [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s aesthetic conversation with [[lynch|Lynch]] in Chapter V. After expounding his theory of beauty — drawing on [[saint-thomas-aquinas|Aquinas]] and [[aristotle-poetics|Aristotle]] — [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] reflects on Aquinas's own poetic gift: "He was a poet himself. He wrote a hymn for Maundy Thursday. It begins with the words *Pange lingua gloriosi*. They say it is the highest glory of the hymnal. It is an intricate and soothing hymn. I like it; but there is no hymn that can be put beside that mournful and majestic processional song, the *Vexilla Regis* of Venantius Fortunatus" [005-390].

This passage is remarkable for several reasons. First, it reveals that [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s aesthetic judgement extends to the liturgical art of the Church he is otherwise abandoning. He can assess hymns with the same critical vocabulary — "mournful and majestic" — that he applies to secular poetry. Second, it shows [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] preferring the older, more austere hymn (the *Vexilla Regis*, from the sixth century) to the more famous one (Aquinas's *Pange Lingua*, from the thirteenth century). This preference for the ancient and severe over the intricate and soothing is characteristic of [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s developing aesthetic.

## Significance

The *Vexilla Regis* serves a double function in the novel. On one level, it demonstrates that [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s rejection of the priesthood does not entail a rejection of all Catholic art; he retains the capacity to be moved by liturgical beauty even as he refuses the faith that produced it. On another level, his preference for the *Vexilla Regis* over Aquinas's hymn reinforces his aesthetic independence. Even when agreeing with Catholic tradition, he asserts his own judgement — he will not accept the received ranking of hymns any more than he accepts the received ranking of poets ([[byron-poems|Byron]] over Tennyson).
