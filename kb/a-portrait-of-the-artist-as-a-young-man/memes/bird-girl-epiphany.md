## Overview

The bird-girl epiphany is the culminating scene of Chapter IV and one of the most celebrated passages in *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*. Walking alone on Dollymount Strand after definitively rejecting the Jesuit vocation, Stephen Dedalus sees a young woman wading in the sea. Her image — described with the intensity of a religious vision but celebrating mortal, physical beauty — triggers Stephen's aesthetic awakening. This moment marks his definitive turn from the service of the altar to the religion of art.

## In the Novel

### Prelude: The Unrest of Vocation

The epiphany is the answer to a question posed earlier in the chapter. The director of Belvedere College has asked Stephen: "Have you ever felt that you had a vocation?" (004-038). Stephen is momentarily drawn to the vision of priestly power — "the power of the keys, the power to bind and to loose from sin" — but his deeper instincts rebel (004-045). Passing the Jesuit house on Gardiner Street, he realizes his destiny "was to be elusive of social or religious orders" (004-054). He will never swing the thurible before the tabernacle. He is destined instead "to learn his own wisdom apart from others or to learn the wisdom of others himself wandering among the snares of the world" (004-054).

This rejection does not bring peace. Stephen feels himself "about to fall" — not yet fallen, but certain that he will fall (004-055). He crosses the Tolka, passes the shrine of the Virgin "fowl-wise on a pole," and smiles at the "disorder, the misrule and confusion of his father's house" (004-056). His soul is suspended between the certainties of religious life and the uncharted territory of the world.

### The Vision on the Strand

The epiphany unfolds on Dollymount Strand, where Stephen wanders after his decision. The passage begins not with a vision but with the sensory world — seaweed, clouds, water — rendered in a prose of dawning receptivity:

> There was a long rivulet in the strand and, as he waded slowly up its course, he wondered at the endless drift of seaweed. Emerald and black and russet and olive, it moved beneath the current, swaying and turning. The water of the rivulet was dark with endless drift and mirrored the highdrifting clouds. The clouds were drifting above him silently and silently the seatangle was drifting below him and the grey warm air was still and a new wild life was singing in his veins. (004-112)

The prose itself transforms, becoming lyrical, incantatory. Stephen's isolation — "He was alone. He was unheeded, happy and near to the wild heart of life" — is not the loneliness of alienation but the solitude of artistic readiness (004-114).

Then the girl appears:

> A girl stood before him in midstream, alone and still, gazing out to sea. She seemed like one whom magic had changed into the likeness of a strange and beautiful seabird. Her long slender bare legs were delicate as a crane's and pure save where an emerald trail of seaweed had fashioned itself as a sign upon the flesh. Her thighs, fuller and softhued as ivory, were bared almost to the hips, where the white fringes of her drawers were like feathering of soft white down. Her slateblue skirts were kilted boldly about her waist and dovetailed behind her. Her bosom was as a bird's, soft and slight, slight and soft as the breast of some darkplumaged dove. But her long fair hair was girlish: and girlish, and touched with the wonder of mortal beauty, her face. (004-115)

Joyce's language is deliberately sacramental. The "emerald trail of seaweed" that "fashioned itself as a sign upon the flesh" is a secular stigmata. The dove imagery echoes the iconography of the Annunciation. The repetition — "soft and slight, slight and soft" — and the chiasmus — "girlish: and girlish" — create a liturgical rhythm. This is a vision, but its object is not the Virgin Mary; it is a living girl, "touched with the wonder of mortal beauty."

### The Gaze and the Call

The encounter is silent — a transaction of looks, not words:

> She was alone and still, gazing out to sea; and when she felt his presence and the worship of his eyes her eyes turned to him in quiet sufferance of his gaze, without shame or wantonness. Long, long she suffered his gaze and then quietly withdrew her eyes from his and bent them towards the stream, gently stirring the water with her foot hither and thither. (004-116)

The girl's "quiet sufferance" is crucial. She does not invite; she permits. She is not a temptress — the vocabulary of sin that haunted Stephen in Chapters II and III is entirely absent. She meets his look with a composure that transforms his desire from lust into worship.

Stephen's response is ecstatic and profane:

> —Heavenly God! cried Stephen's soul, in an outburst of profane joy. (004-117)

The exclamation is at once prayer and blasphemy — the language of religion deployed in service of a vision of mortal beauty. Stephen turns away and strides across the strand:

> Her image had passed into his soul for ever and no word had broken the holy silence of his ecstasy. Her eyes had called him and his soul had leaped at the call. To live, to err, to fall, to triumph, to recreate life out of life! A wild angel had appeared to him, the angel of mortal youth and beauty, an envoy from the fair courts of life, to throw open before him in an instant of ecstasy the gates of all the ways of error and glory. (004-119)

The "wild angel" replaces the angel of the Annunciation. The "call" to religious life — which the director urged upon Stephen and which Stephen's soul "was not there to hear" — is answered here, on a beach, by a girl wading in the water. Stephen's vocation is now clear: not the priesthood but art, not the service of God but the "recreation of life out of life."

## The Aftermath: Resurrection and Deliverance

The epiphany does not fade; it erupts into a sustained crescendo of affirmation. Stephen's companions, bathing nearby, call him by his Greek name: "Stephaneforos!" (004-104) — Stephen, the crown-bearer, the one destined for wreaths of victory rather than the thurible of the priest. The name rings across the water like an acclamation at a coronation.

In the passage that follows the vision, Stephen's soul undergoes a symbolic death and resurrection. His former fears become "cerements shaken from the body of death — the fear he had walked in night and day, the incertitude that had ringed him round, the shame that had abased him within and without — cerements, the linens of the grave" (004-107). The imagery deliberately echoes Christ's resurrection: the graveclothes are stripped away, the body rises. "His soul had arisen from the grave of boyhood, spurning her graveclothes" (004-108).

Then comes the triple affirmation that is Stephen's artistic *fiat*:

> Yes! Yes! Yes! He would create proudly out of the freedom and power of his soul, as the great artificer whose name he bore, a living thing, new and soaring and beautiful, impalpable, imperishable. (004-108)

The "great artificer whose name he bore" is Daedalus, the mythic craftsman who fashioned wings and a labyrinth. Stephen claims his namesake's creative power at the very moment he claims his freedom. The alliteration of "new and soaring and beautiful, impalpable, imperishable" pushes the prose past argument into incantation — language no longer describing a transformation but enacting one.

## Significance

The bird-girl epiphany is the structural and thematic pivot of *Portrait*. It closes the arc that began with Stephen's childhood at Clongowes and his adolescent fall into sin and religious scrupulosity. The erotic drive that led him to the prostitutes of Chapter II and the confessional of Chapter III is here transmuted — not repressed but sublimated into an aesthetic vision. The girl on the strand is both a real person (sensual, physical, with "bared" thighs and "softhued" flesh) and a symbol (birdlike, dove-like, angelic). Joyce refuses to separate the two.

### The Appropriation of Religious Language

Joyce's prose in this passage systematically appropriates Catholic vocabulary for an artistic creed. The encounter is a "vision"; the girl's seaweed mark is "a sign upon the flesh"; Stephen's response is an "ecstasy" in "holy silence"; the girl is a "wild angel" and "an envoy from the fair courts of life." The triple "Yes! Yes! Yes!" — the artist's echo of Mary's "Be it done unto me" — is a secular *fiat* (004-108). Stephen Dedalus, namesake of the great artificer Daedalus, accepts his vocation.

This is the Joycean epiphany in its purest form: not a divine revelation but a sudden apprehension of significance in the ordinary, the "whatness" of a thing throwing the soul into a state of creative readiness. Stephen's aesthetic theory, which he will elaborate in Chapter V using the scholastic vocabulary of [[thomas-aquinas|Aquinas]], is born here — not in the library but on the strand, not from argument but from vision.

### The Call and the Answer

The structural genius of the bird-girl episode lies in its relationship to the director's earlier question. "Have you ever felt that you had a vocation?" the director asked, and Stephen could not answer. On the strand, the girl's eyes "had called him and his soul had leaped at the call." The religious vocation is replaced by an artistic one, the priestly call by a wordless summons from mortal beauty. "This was the call of life to his soul," Joyce writes, "not the dull gross voice of the world of duties and despair, not the inhuman voice that had called him to the pale service of the altar" (004-105).

Stephen's "Yes! Yes! Yes!" is his answer — not to the director but to life itself. It is the definitive affirmation that will carry him through Chapter V and into his artistic exile.
