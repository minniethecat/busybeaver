## Overview

The retreat is a three-day religious exercise held annually at [[belvedere-college|Belvedere College]] before the feast of St. Francis Xavier. During this retreat, [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen Dedalus]], already tormented by [[sin-and-confession|guilt]] over his sexual [[sin-and-confession|sin]]s, hears a series of terrifying sermons on death, judgment, hell, and the four last things. The retreat marks a critical turning point in his spiritual development, leading directly to his [[sin-and-confession|confession]] and a period of intense religious devotion.

## In the Novel

The retreat is announced on a Wednesday at [[belvedere-college|Belvedere College]]: "The retreat will begin on Wednesday afternoon in honour of saint Francis Xavier" [003-028]. The rector explains that all boys are expected to attend and participate fully in the spiritual exercises [003-041], urging them "to make this retreat in honour of saint Francis with all the fervour of [their] young hearts" [003-042].

The retreat sermons, delivered by [[father-arnall|Father Arnall]], focus on the four last things: death, judgment, heaven, and hell. [[father-arnall|Father Arnall]]'s descriptions of hell are particularly vivid and terrifying. He paints a picture of "the general judgement" and "the last day" that fills [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] with existential dread [003-047]. [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s mind is consumed by the images: "the wind of the last day blew through his mind; his [[sin-and-confession|sin]]s, the jewel-eyed harlots of his imagination, fled before the hurricane" [003-051].

The sermons drive [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] to a state of acute spiritual terror. He feels that "the sun of the last day was going down and that all souls were being gathered for the judgement" [003-101]. This terror culminates in his decision to confess his [[sin-and-confession|sin]]s, which he ultimately does not at the [[chapel|college chapel]] but at a small church on Church Street, where he makes a full [[sin-and-confession|confession]] to an old Capuchin [[priesthood-and-religion|priest]] [003-149].

## Significance

The retreat serves as the catalyst for [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s spiritual transformation in Chapter III. It represents one of the most intense periods of religious experience in the novel and demonstrates Joyce's masterful depiction of Catholic [[sin-and-confession|guilt]] and the psychology of religious terror. The retreat sermons are among the most famous passages in the novel, drawing on Joyce's own experience of Jesuit retreats and the hell-fire rhetoric common in nineteenth-century Irish Catholicism.

The retreat also establishes a pattern that recurs throughout the novel: [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s oscillation between extremes — [[sin-and-confession|sin]] and repentance, sensuality and asceticism, faith and apostasy. His post-retreat piety is as intense as his pre-retreat dissolution, foreshadowing his eventual rejection of [[the-catholic-church|the Church]] altogether.
