## Overview

Grafton Street is one of Dublin's principal thoroughfares, running from St. Stephen's Green to College Green. In *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*, it appears twice in [[ch05|Chapter V]], both times in connection with Stephen Dedalus's experience of poverty and his fraught relationship with [[emma-clery|Emma Clery]].

## In the Novel

Grafton Street first appears as Stephen leaves a flower-selling girl who has reminded him of Emma. Walking away from the encounter, he feels the weight of his shabbiness:

> Grafton Street, along which he walked, prolonged that moment of discouraged poverty. [005-071]

The street — synonymous with commerce, fashion, and middle-class prosperity — amplifies Stephen's sense of exclusion. He recalls having attended the laying of a memorial slab to Wolfe Tone at the head of the street, and the memory of that "tawdry tribute" sharpens his bitterness about Irish nationalism and his family's diminished circumstances.

Grafton Street reappears later in Stephen's diary entry for April 15. He records a chance encounter with Emma:

> Met her today point blank in Grafton Street. The crowd brought us together. We both stopped. [005-903]

The meeting is awkward. Emma asks why he never comes to see her; Stephen, defensive, talks "rapidly of myself and my plans," then makes an involuntary "gesture of a revolutionary nature" that draws stares from passers-by. She shakes hands and leaves, saying she hopes he will do what he said. The street here becomes a stage for Stephen's self-conscious theatricality — his performance of the artist-rebel, which he immediately dissects in his own diary with bitter irony, invoking "the spiritual-heroic refrigerating apparatus, invented and patented in all countries by Dante Alighieri."

## Significance

The two Grafton Street passages form a diptych of Stephen's social alienation. In the first, the street externalises his poverty; in the second, it witnesses his self-dramatising awkwardness with the woman he has idealised from a distance throughout the novel. Grafton Street — a place of public display and middle-class consumption — is precisely the setting where Stephen's marginality is most acutely felt.
