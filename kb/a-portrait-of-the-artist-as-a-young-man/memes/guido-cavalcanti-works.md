## Overview

Guido Cavalcanti (c. 1255–1300) was a Florentine poet and philosopher, a friend of [[dante-alighieri-works|Dante Alighieri]], and one of the leading figures of the *dolce stil novo* (sweet new style) in medieval Italian poetry. His philosophical canzone *Donna me prega* is among the most intellectually ambitious lyric poems of the Middle Ages. In *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*, Cavalcanti appears as part of the cosmopolitan literary landscape through which [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] moves on his morning walk.

## In the Novel

In the celebrated passage of Chapter V that traces Stephen's mental itinerary across the city, his walk becomes a procession of literary affinities. As he passes along the North Strand Road, "glancing idly at the windows of the provision shops, he would recall the dark humour of Guido Cavalcanti and smile" (005-033).

The detail is exquisite in its precision. Cavalcanti's poetry is characterized by a philosophical darkness — a preoccupation with the destructive power of love that sets him apart from the sweeter, more idealized manner of his contemporaries. That Stephen should smile at the recollection of this "dark humour" reveals the sensibility of a young man who finds companionship not in the living but in the dead — in poets whose vision of experience answers to his own.

### Cavalcanti's Place in the Morning Litany

Cavalcanti occupies a specific position in the geographical and intellectual cartography of Stephen's walk. The passage maps four literary figures onto four Dublin locations: Newman at Fairview, Cavalcanti on the North Strand Road, Ibsen at Baird's stonecutting works in Talbot Place, and Ben Jonson beyond the Liffey (005-033). Each writer is tethered to a specific spot — a mental landmark in Stephen's private city — and each represents a distinct register of artistic sensibility.

Cavalcanti's position between Newman's "cloistral silverveined prose" and Ibsen's "wayward boyish beauty" is architecturally deliberate. Newman represents the Catholic intellectual inheritance; Ibsen represents the modern, secular, northern European aesthetic. Cavalcanti — medieval, Italian, philosophically dark — stands at the hinge between them: a Catholic poet whose subject is not divine love but the wounding, disordering power of human passion. His "dark humour" provides the counterweight to Newman's disciplined radiance and Ibsen's boyish freedom.

The passage's syntax reinforces this geography of the mind. Stephen "foreknew" what he would think — the walk is not a journey of discovery but of recollection, a ritual in which the self confirms its own composition by moving through a city that has been mentally mapped onto a library.

## Critical Analysis

### Cavalcanti and Stephen's Medievalism

Stephen's affection for Cavalcanti belongs to his broader engagement with medieval philosophy and poetry. Throughout Chapter V, he moves between [[aristotle-poetics|Aristotle]], [[saint-thomas-aquinas|Aquinas]], and the poets of the Italian Middle Ages, assembling from these materials an aesthetic theory that is at once scholastic in its rigour and personal in its application.

Cavalcanti's appeal for Stephen is likely twofold. First, Cavalcanti was a philosopher-poet — a figure who, like Stephen himself, refused to separate intellectual inquiry from artistic practice. Second, Cavalcanti's "dark humour" — his unflinching representation of love as a force that wounds and disorders the soul — resonates with Stephen's own experience of desire as something that is simultaneously exalting and humiliating.

### Dante and Cavalcanti

The reference to Cavalcanti acquires additional resonance through its proximity to the novel's reference to [[dante-alighieri-works|Dante]]. Cavalcanti was Dante's older friend and poetic mentor — "the first of my friends," Dante called him in the *Vita Nuova*. Yet Dante placed Cavalcanti's father among the heretics in the *Inferno*, and Guido himself is conspicuously absent from Dante's *Paradiso*. The relationship between the two poets — intimate, competitive, shadowed by theological disagreement — is one of the richest dramas of medieval literary history.

Stephen's smile at the thought of Cavalcanti's dark humour is thus charged with an awareness of the tensions within the tradition he claims as his own. To admire Cavalcanti is to take a position within the Dante-Cavalcanti dialectic — to prefer, as Stephen implicitly does, the poet of darkness and doubt to the poet of cosmic certainty.

### The Meaning of "Dark Humour"

Joyce's phrase "the dark humour of Guido Cavalcanti" compresses a complex literary judgment into three words. Cavalcanti's "darkness" is not merely tonal but philosophical. His poetry, particularly *Donna me prega*, theorizes love as an accident that befalls the soul from without — a force that darkens reason rather than illuminating it. This stands in stark contrast to the Dantean model, in which love — even earthly love — leads upward toward the divine. Cavalcanti's love is a pathology, a wound, an obscuring of the intellect.

That Stephen should "smile" at this darkness is revealing. It is not the smile of superiority but of recognition. The young man who has been tormented by desire, who has fled from prostitutes and confessionals, who has sublimated erotic energy into aesthetic vision through the bird-girl epiphany, finds in Cavalcanti a poet who understands the darkness without needing to redeem it.
