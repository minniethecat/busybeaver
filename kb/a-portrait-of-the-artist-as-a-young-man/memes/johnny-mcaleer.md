## Overview

Johnny McAleer is a minor character in *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*, appearing as one of [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen Dedalus]]'s schoolmates at [[clongowes-wood-college|Clongowes Wood College]]. He is mentioned in Chapters I and II, part of the constellation of boys whose names populate the background of Stephen's early education.

## Life

McAleer is one of the Clongowes boys whose names surface in the texture of school life. Like [[athy]], [[fleming]], and other minor schoolmates, he is not developed as an individual character but functions as part of the social fabric of the college — one of the many names that define the geography of Stephen's childhood world.

In the schoolboy society of Clongowes, names carry weight. As Stephen learns the unwritten rules of the playground and the study hall, the names of his peers — who is clever, who is strong, who can be trusted — form a map of social relations that the young boy must learn to navigate. McAleer belongs to this background chorus of schoolboy voices.

## Significance

Like many of the minor Clongowes characters, McAleer contributes to Joyce's technique of rendering childhood consciousness through the accumulation of names, impressions, and fragmentary encounters. The novel does not pause to introduce these figures in conventional fashion; instead, their names drift through the narrative as they would through a child's awareness — sometimes prominent, sometimes barely registered, always part of the dense social texture of boarding-school life.

This technique — the refusal to distinguish between "major" and "minor" characters through formal introduction — is one of Joyce's innovations in the novel. Every name in Stephen's world is treated with the same fidelity to perception, creating a fictional universe where significance is not predetermined by narrative convention but emerges from the protagonist's own attention.
