## Overview

The Irish Parliamentary Party, also known as the Home Rule Party, was the dominant Irish nationalist political organization in the late 19th century, led by Charles Stewart Parnell from 1880 until his death in 1891. The party sought Irish self-government ("Home Rule") within the United Kingdom through constitutional means. After Parnell was named co-respondent in the O'Shea divorce case in 1890, the party split into pro-Parnell and anti-Parnell factions, a rupture that reverberates throughout *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man* as a defining trauma of Stephen's childhood.

## In the Novel

The Parnell split dominates the Christmas dinner scene in Chapter I, where Stephen's family and guests erupt into furious argument. The political symbolism is embedded even in domestic objects: the young Stephen knows that "the green velvet back was for Parnell" and "the brush with the maroon velvet back was for Michael Davitt" [001-015], Dante's brushes marking the fault line of Irish politics within the home. When Dante tears "the green velvet back off the brush that was for Parnell" with her scissors [001-109], the act of domestic vandalism prefigures the political violence of the dinner-table argument.

The Christmas dinner itself explodes when Simon Dedalus defends Parnell passionately against the attacks of Mrs Riordan ("Dante"), who represents the Catholic anti-Parnell faction. Simon declares Parnell "a great man" and laments that "the priests' pawns broke Parnell's heart and hounded him into his grave" [001-338]. Dante condemns Parnell as "a public sinner" whom the bishops were right to denounce. Mr Casey adds his own bitter memories: "Poor Parnell! My dead king!" [001-422], invoking the quasi-religious devotion Parnell inspired in his followers.

The argument traumatizes young Stephen, who watches "the face of his father... grown older and more tired" as the adult world of politics and religion crashes into his childhood. Stephen himself aligns instinctively with his father: "He was for Ireland and Parnell and so was his father" [001-387]. The scene establishes the central tension between Irish nationalism and Catholic authority that Stephen will navigate throughout the novel.

In Chapter II, Stephen attempts to memorialize Parnell by writing a poem: "He saw himself sitting at his table in Bray the morning after the discussion at the Christmas dinner table, trying to write a poem about Parnell on the back of one of his father's second moiety notices" [002-051]. The Irish party leader thus becomes an early subject for Stephen's literary ambition, linking political commitment to artistic expression. Later, as Stephen's memory of his childhood fades, "he recalled only names. Dante, Parnell, Clane, Clongowes" [002-251] — the name of Parnell persists among the elemental fragments of his early life.

References to Irish politics surface throughout the Dedalus family's social world. Mike Flynn, the running trainer whom Stephen visits with his father and Uncle Charles, talks "athletics and politics" [001-357], a pairing typical of Irish nationalist circles where physical culture and national revival were intertwined.

## Significance

The Irish Parliamentary Party and the Parnell schism serve as Stephen's introduction to the irreconcilable demands of Irish public life: nationalism versus religion, personal loyalty versus institutional authority. The Christmas dinner argument is the primal scene of Stephen's political education, demonstrating that even the intimate sphere of family cannot escape the divisions of Irish politics. Parnell becomes for Stephen a figure of the betrayed hero — an artist or leader destroyed by the very people he sought to serve — a pattern that shapes Stephen's own self-conception as an artist who must reject the claims of nation and church alike.
