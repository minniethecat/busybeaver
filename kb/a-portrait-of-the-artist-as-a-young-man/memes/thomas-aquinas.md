## Overview

Thomas Aquinas appears in Joyce's novel not as a historical figure but as an intellectual resource — the medieval scholastic whose aesthetic categories provide Stephen Dedalus with the conceptual language for his emerging theory of art. Stephen's engagement with Aquinas is selective and creative: he borrows the terms _integritas_, _consonantia_, and _claritas_ while adapting them to a modernist artistic framework the theologian himself would not have recognized. The distinction between this concept page and the [[saint-thomas-aquinas]] work page is that here the focus is on Aquinas's ideas as they function within the novel's aesthetic argument.

## In the Novel

### Aquinas as Aesthetic Authority

In Chapter V, Stephen turns to Aquinas repeatedly as he develops his aesthetic theory. He cites Aquinas's definition of beauty: "_pulcra sunt quae visa placent_" — those things are beautiful the apprehension of which pleases (005-086). Acknowledging that Aquinas "says _Bonum est in quod tendit appetitus_" (005-088), Stephen distinguishes the aesthetic from the merely desirable.

### The Three Requisites of Beauty

The cornerstone of Stephen's borrowed aesthetic is Aquinas's triadic formula: "_Ad pulcritudinem tria requiruntur: integritas, consonantia, claritas_" (005-419) — three things are required for beauty: wholeness, harmony, and radiance. Stephen translates these into his own experiential terms, describing the progression from the apprehension of an object as "one integral thing" through the perception of its "rhythm" to the moment of epiphanic radiance.

### Aquinas as Colloquial Presence

Aquinas's name circulates casually among Stephen's university companions. Lynch challenges Stephen's reliance on the scholastic: "Is that the best you and Aquinas can do?" (005-376), while Stephen jokes that "Perhaps Aquinas would understand me better than you" (005-390). The banter domesticates the medieval thinker, making him a living participant in modern intellectual conversation.

### Stephen's Independence from Aquinas

Though Stephen repeatedly invokes Aquinas as an authority, his relationship to the scholastic is instrumental rather than devotional. He tells Cranly that "for my purpose I can work on at present by the light of one or two ideas of Aristotle and Aquinas" (005-099) — the phrasing reveals his method: Aquinas provides "light," not doctrine. Stephen borrows what he needs and is untroubled by what he leaves behind. When MacAlister dismisses his theory as "applied Aquinas" — a phrase Stephen himself adopts with satisfaction (005-388) — the epithet captures the hybrid nature of Stephen's project: scholastic categories applied to modernist ends.

Stephen's intellectual independence emerges most clearly in his handling of _claritas_, the third and most elusive of Aquinas's requisites. Stephen admits that "Aquinas uses a term which seems to be inexact" (005-428) and proceeds to offer his own speculative interpretation: "I thought he might mean that claritas is the artistic discovery and representation of the divine purpose in anything or a force of generalization which would make the esthetic image a universal one, make it outshine its proper conditions" (005-428). The hesitancy of the language — "I thought he might mean" — is the mark of a mind that has moved from discipleship to independent inquiry. Stephen no longer needs Aquinas to be correct; he needs him to be useful.

## Analysis

Stephen's use of Aquinas is emblematic of Joyce's broader method: inherited intellectual frameworks are repurposed rather than discarded. Stephen acknowledges that "all my esthetic theory applied Aquinas" (005-388), yet Lynch's rejoinder — "Aquinas, in spite of his intellect, was exactly a good round friar" (005-389) — reminds the reader of the historical distance between the scholastic context and Stephen's modernist appropriation. The tension between fidelity and creative misreading is itself the point: the artist does not inherit a tradition passively but forges it anew.

The movement from Aquinas's _claritas_ to Stephen's theory of epiphany is the novel's most significant act of intellectual transformation. Where Aquinas located radiance in the divine order shining through the created object, Stephen relocates it in the artist's act of perception and creation — a secular transposition that mirrors Joyce's own artistic practice.
