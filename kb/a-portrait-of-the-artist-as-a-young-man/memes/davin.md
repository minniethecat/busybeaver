## Overview

Davin is one of the most sympathetically drawn characters in Chapter V of *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*. A peasant student from the Irish countryside who attends [[university-college-dublin|University College Dublin]], Davin embodies the tradition of Gaelic nationalism — athletic, [[sin-and-confession|sin]]cere, and deeply rooted in the land. [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] respects Davin more than any of his other university companions, but their friendship also dramatizes the gulf between [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s cosmopolitan artistic ambitions and the rural Irish identity Davin represents.

## In the Novel

[[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s thoughts turn to Davin early in Chapter V: "he thought of his friend Davin, the peasant student" [005-050]. Davin is introduced as a foil to [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s other companions — where [[cranly|Cranly]] is intellectually intense and [[lynch|Lynch]] is skeptical, Davin is straightforward and earnest. [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] recalls sitting "in Davin's rooms in Grantham Street," listening to stories "of prowess of his uncle Mat Davin, the athlete" [005-053]. These stories, rooted in physical courage and family pride, belong to a world [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] can observe but not inhabit.

Davin's most significant moment comes when he tells [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] the story of a young woman who invited him to stay the night at her cottage after a hurling match. The story reveals Davin's simple decency — he refused the woman's advance out of respect for her — but also his unreflective patriotism. "In a way he felt that he was in the company of a Fenian," [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] thinks of him. [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s final verdict on Davin's story — "The last words of Davin's story sang in his memory" [005-061] — captures the mixture of admiration and distance that defines their friendship.

Later, Davin reappears watching a hurling match, "his voice crying out excitedly" [005-274]. When [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] approaches him about leaving [[university-college-dublin|the university]], Davin's response is characteristically direct: "You're a terrible man, Stevie" [005-297]. It is not an intellectual critique but a statement of bewildered affection from a friend who cannot follow where [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] is going.

## Relationships

Davin is [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s friend but also his opposite — the rooted Irishman to [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s rootless artist. In [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s aesthetic vocabulary, Davin represents the "kinetic" emotions of patriotism and communal loyalty that the artist must refine out of existence. Yet [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s affection for Davin is genuine, and their friendship is one of the few relationships in the novel that survives [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s intellectual detachment.

## Significance

Davin functions as a symbol of the Ireland [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] cannot embrace. He is not a caricature — Joyce renders him with genuine warmth — but his limitations are the limitations of the nationalist tradition itself: [[sin-and-confession|sin]]cere but uncritical, physically courageous but intellectually passive. [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s inability to be what Davin represents is at the heart of his decision to leave Ireland.
