## Overview

Michael Davitt was an Irish republican, agrarian agitator, and founder of the Irish National Land League. In *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*, he appears not as a character but as a symbolic presence — encoded in the maroon velvet back of [[dante-riordan|Dante]]'s brush, paired with [[charles-parnell|Parnell]]'s green. This domestic detail, introduced in the novel's opening pages, condenses the factional landscape of late nineteenth-century Irish nationalism into a child's visual memory.

Davitt's presence in the novel, though limited to this single indirect reference, is far from incidental. He represents a strand of Irish nationalism — agrarian, radical, Fenian-adjacent — that existed in productive tension with Parnell's constitutional parliamentarianism. The two brushes in [[dante-riordan|Dante]]'s press are not merely decorative; they encode a political taxonomy that the novel's opening chapters will elaborate into the full-scale drama of the [[christmas-dinner-argument|Christmas dinner argument]]. Davitt is the maroon to Parnell's green — a subordinate but essential colour in the palette of Irish political identity.

## In the Novel

### Dante's Brushes

The single explicit reference to Davitt occurs in the novel's first chapter, during the domestic scene before [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] is sent to [[clongowes-wood-college|Clongowes]]:

> "Dante had two brushes in her press. The brush with the maroon velvet back was for Michael Davitt and the brush with the green velvet back was for Parnell. Dante gave him a cachou every time he brought her a piece of tissue paper." [001-015]

The two brushes encode the major division in Irish nationalist politics at the time of [[charles-parnell|Parnell]]'s fall. Davitt, a former Fenian who had served time in Dartmoor Prison, was associated with the more radical, land-reform strain of nationalism — distinct from Parnell's constitutional parliamentary strategy. [[dante-riordan|Dante]]'s possession of brushes honouring both figures suggests a Catholicism that could accommodate multiple strands of nationalist sentiment before the Parnell split fractured that unity.

The passage's syntax is instructive: Davitt's brush is named first, but Parnell's green is the climactic colour. The sequence — maroon, then green — encodes a political hierarchy even within the domestic symbolism of Dante's press. Davitt is acknowledged, but Parnell is the chief. The detail of the cachou — a breath-sweetening lozenge — given in exchange for tissue paper adds a further layer: political symbols (the brushes) and domestic rituals (the cachou) are woven together in the child's experience, indistinguishable.

### The Christmas Dinner Context

Although Davitt is not mentioned by name at the [[christmas-dinner-argument|Christmas dinner argument]], his political tradition — the alliance between agrarian reform and Catholic nationalism — forms the subtext of the quarrel. [[dante-riordan|Dante]]'s defence of the bishops against [[charles-parnell|Parnell]], and [[mr-casey|Mr Casey]]'s invocation of the Fenian dead, map onto the very divisions that Davitt's career had navigated: the tension between revolutionary nationalism and constitutional reform, between the Church and the Land League.

The Land League, which Davitt founded and Parnell led, had been the great alliance of Irish nationalism in the 1880s — uniting Fenian radicals, agrarian tenants, and constitutional politicians behind the demand for land reform. By the time of the novel's Christmas dinner scene (set in the early 1890s, after Parnell's fall), that alliance had shattered. [[dante-riordan|Dante]]'s brushes, with their equal accommodation of Davitt and Parnell, represent a moment before the fracture — a unity that the Christmas dinner will dramatise as irretrievably lost.

## Historical Context

Michael Davitt was born in County Mayo in 1846, during the Great Famine. His family was evicted and emigrated to Lancashire when he was a child. At age eleven, he lost his right arm in a factory accident — a biographical detail that would become part of his mythos, the one-armed agitator who spoke for the landless. He joined the Irish Republican Brotherhood (Fenians) and was arrested in 1870, serving seven years of a fifteen-year sentence. After his release, he founded the Irish National Land League in 1879 with [[charles-parnell|Parnell]] as president, campaigning for the "Three Fs" — fair rent, fixity of tenure, and free sale of the tenant's interest. He was elected to Parliament in 1895 and died in 1906.

Davitt's political trajectory is emblematic of the evolution of Irish nationalism in the late nineteenth century: from Fenian insurrectionism to mass agrarian agitation to parliamentary politics. His partnership with Parnell — the revolutionary providing the grassroots energy, the parliamentarian providing the political strategy — was the engine of the Land War that transformed Irish rural society. When that partnership fractured in the wake of Parnell's adultery scandal, Irish nationalism lost the coalition that had made it formidable. Dante's two brushes, side by side in her press, memorialise a political unity that history had already undone.

## Significance in the Novel

Davitt's function in *Portrait* is synecdochic: he stands for an entire tradition of Irish radicalism that the novel evokes but does not directly dramatise. The maroon brush is a fragment — like the "hamilton-rowan|thrown hat" on the ha-ha, like the "cork" park in whose name [[simon-dedalus|Simon Dedalus]]'s nostalgia bathes — through which a child's consciousness registers the presence of a history too large to comprehend.

What distinguishes Davitt from the other historical allusions in the novel is his embeddedness in the domestic sphere. He is not a figure encountered in a book or a legend attached to a castle; he is a brush in [[dante-riordan|Dante]]'s press, an object handled daily, part of the furniture of home. Irish nationalism, for the young [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]], is not an ideology but a texture — a maroon velvet back, a green velvet back, a cachou exchanged for tissue paper. The political is suffused into the domestic, and the domestic carries the political as its secret grammar.

This treatment of political symbolism as domestic object is characteristic of Joyce's method throughout *Portrait*. The novel's politics are never declaimed from a platform; they are glimpsed through a child's half-comprehending gaze, embedded in the material culture of the Dedalus household. Davitt's brush is a masterclass in this technique: a single image that opens onto the entire landscape of Irish nationalist politics, without ever leaving the child's eye-level view.
