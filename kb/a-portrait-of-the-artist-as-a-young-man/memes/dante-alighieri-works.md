## Overview

Dante Alighieri (c. 1265–1321) was a Florentine poet and the author of the *Divine Comedy* (*Divina Commedia*), one of the foundational works of Western literature. His *Inferno*, *Purgatorio*, and *Paradiso* established Italian as a literary language and created a cosmic vision of the afterlife that profoundly influenced European culture. In *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*, Dante appears at two levels: explicitly, in a characteristically ironic passage of [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen Dedalus]]'s interior monologue; and structurally, as the model of the artist whose name — Alighieri — contains both the wings of flight and the craft of making that [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s mythical namesake Daedalus embodies.

## In the Novel

### The Spiritual-Heroic Refrigerator

In Chapter V, during a passage of interior monologue that follows [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s aesthetic theorizing, Dante is invoked in a deliberately incongruous metaphor. [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s mind moves with comic swiftness through a series of mechanical images, arriving at the phrase: "the spiritual-heroic refrigerating apparatus, invented and patented in all countries by Dante Alighieri" [005-903].

The reference operates at multiple levels. At the surface, it is irreverent and playful — the poet of the *Divine Comedy*, the supreme artist of the spiritual realm, is recast as the inventor of a domestic appliance. The juxtaposition of "spiritual-heroic" with "refrigerating apparatus" is characteristic of the wit that runs through [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s consciousness. At a deeper level, however, the image of a "refrigerating apparatus" for spiritual-heroic matters is not entirely absurd: Dante's *Inferno* does, in a sense, refrigerate — it preserves in ice the souls of the treacherous, frozen in the lowest circle of hell. The comic image contains a kernel of genuine literary insight.

### Dante's Brushes in Chapter I

Dante the poet is also present, indirectly, through [[dante-riordan|Dante Riordan]], the governess whose name invokes the Florentine. [[dante-riordan|Dante]]'s two brushes — maroon for Michael Davitt, green for [[charles-parnell|Parnell]] [001-015] — encode the political divisions of late nineteenth-century Ireland within the domestic sphere. The governess's name is a Joycean pun: Dante the poet of cosmic justice becomes Dante the enforcer of domestic and political orthodoxy. The sublime and the mundane, the eternal and the local, are pressed together in a single syllable.

## Critical Analysis

### Dante and the Comic Sublime

[[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s ironic treatment of Dante exemplifies a broader pattern in his mental life — and in Joyce's own method. The young artist who will one day write *Ulysses* already shows a tendency to bring the sublime down to earth through comic incongruity. The "spiritual-heroic refrigerating apparatus" is a miniature instance of the technique that Joyce himself described as "jocoserious" — the fusion of jest and earnestness, the conviction that the highest things can only be approached through the mode of comedy.

Yet the reference also signals [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s genuine engagement with Dante. The *Divine Comedy* provides one of the most ambitious models in Western literature for the artist as cosmographer, as the maker of total worlds — a model that haunts [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s own aesthetic ambitions. His comic deflation of Dante is, paradoxically, a form of homage: one can only parody what one takes seriously enough to have absorbed.

### Daedalus and Dante

The novel's most profound engagement with Dante is structural rather than explicit. [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s surname, Dedalus, invokes the mythical craftsman Daedalus, who built wings to escape the labyrinth of Crete. Dante Alighieri's name contains a different kind of flight: the poet who descended into hell and ascended to paradise. Both figures — the Greek artificer and the Italian poet — are types of the artist whose medium is transformation. When [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] prays at the novel's end, "Old father, old artificer, stand me now and ever in good stead" [005-904], he addresses Daedalus directly, but Dante Alighieri — the poet-as-artificer — stands in the shadow of the invocation.

### Dante in Joyce's Work

Dante Alighieri was a pervasive presence in James Joyce's imagination. Joyce read Dante in Italian, and the *Divine Comedy* — particularly the *Inferno* — echoes throughout his work. The hellish sermons of Chapter III, in which [[father-arnall|Father Arnall]] enumerates the torments of the damned [003-080]–[003-100], are Joycean variations on Dantean themes: the physical specificity of suffering, the graded hierarchy of sin, the conversion of theology into sensory experience. In *Ulysses*, the structural parallels with the *Inferno* would become even more elaborate, with each episode corresponding to a different circle of experience. [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s jesting reference to Dante as the inventor of a "spiritual-heroic refrigerating apparatus" is an early, winking acknowledgement of a literary debt that would deepen throughout Joyce's career.
