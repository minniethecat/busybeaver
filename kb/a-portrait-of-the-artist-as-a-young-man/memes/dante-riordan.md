## Overview

Dante Riordan (Mrs. Riordan) is the governess of the [[stephen-dedalus|Dedalus]] household and one of the most vivid secondary characters in Chapter I of *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*. Known to the children simply as "Dante," she appears early in the novel as a figure of warmth and domestic routine, owning "two brushes in her press" — one with a green velvet back for [[charles-parnell|Parnell]] and one with a maroon velvet back for Michael Davitt [001-015]. Her dramatic transformation from family caretaker to fierce theological combatant during the [[christmas-dinner-argument|Christmas dinner]] scene is one of the novel's most memorable character arcs.

## In the Novel

Dante first appears as a benevolent presence in the [[stephen-dedalus|Dedalus]] household. Young [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] sits "at the fire with Dante waiting for Brigid to bring in the tea" [001-053], and she teaches him about geography and religion. [[uncle-charles|Uncle Charles]] and [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s father agree "that Dante was a clever woman and a wellread woman" [001-053], and her intellectual influence on [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] is evident from his early fascination with language and names.

Her allegiances are signaled early through her two brushes — one green for [[charles-parnell|Parnell]], one maroon for Davitt [001-091] — which sit side by side in seeming harmony. But this symbolic coexistence shatters when [[charles-parnell|Parnell]]'s political fall divides Irish society. "Dante had ripped the green velvet back off the brush that was for [[charles-parnell|Parnell]] one day with her scissors" [001-109], a violent gesture that prefigures the [[christmas-dinner-argument|Christmas dinner]] explosion.

During the [[christmas-dinner-argument|Christmas dinner]] argument, Dante emerges as the most uncompromi[[sin-and-confession|sin]]g voice for [[the-catholic-church|the Church]]. When [[mr-casey|Mr. Casey]] and [[simon-dedalus|Simon Dedalus]] attack the [[priesthood-and-religion|priest]]s who destroyed [[charles-parnell|Parnell]], Dante responds with absolute conviction: "It is religion, Dante said. They are doing their duty to God" [001-288]. She insists that "the bishops and [[priesthood-and-religion|priest]]s of Ireland have spoken, and they must be obeyed" [001-305]. Her parting shot — denouncing [[charles-parnell|Parnell]] as an adulterer and declaring [[the-catholic-church|the Church]]'s moral authority supreme — leaves the dinner table in ruins.

## Relationships

Dante's relationships are defined by her religious convictions. She is affectionate toward [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] but also represents the Catholicism he must eventually reject. Her conflict with [[mr-casey|Mr. Casey]] and [[simon-dedalus|Simon Dedalus]] is not personal but ideological — a clash between nationalist loyalty and ecclesiastical obedience that defines Irish political life. Her [[uncle-charles|Uncle Charles]] serves as a mild-mannered counterpoint, attempting to defuse the tensions she inflames.

## Significance

Dante Riordan embodies the institutional power of the Catholic Church at its most intimate — in the family home, at the dinner table, in the education of children. She represents the force of religious orthodoxy that [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] must internalize, struggle against, and ultimately transcend. Her ripping of the green velvet from [[charles-parnell|Parnell]]'s brush is a miniature of the novel's central conflict: the destruction of the artist-hero by the forces of conformity, and the artist's eventual refusal to serve any master but his own conscience.
