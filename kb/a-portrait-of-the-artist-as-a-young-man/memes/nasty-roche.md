## Overview

Nasty Roche is one of the first named antagonists [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen Dedalus]] encounters at [[clongowes-wood-college|Clongowes Wood College]] in Chapter I of *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*. His nickname — "Nasty" — immediately marks him as a figure of menace in the schoolboy taxonomy that [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] is struggling to learn. Unlike [[wells|Wells]], whose cruelty is physical, Nasty Roche's aggression is social and verbal: he asks probing questions designed to expose [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s naivety, and his confidence — grounded in his father's status as a magistrate — contrasts sharply with [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s uncertainty.

## In the Novel

Roche first appears in a rapid-fire sequence of interactions that establish his character. [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s internal monologue delivers the judgment immediately: "[[rody-kickham|Rody Kickham]] was a decent fellow but Nasty Roche was a stink" [001-058]. The binary — decent fellow versus stink — is the elementary moral vocabulary of [[clongowes-wood-college|Clongowes]], and Roche is firmly assigned to the latter category.

Roche's defining trait is his probing curiosity about [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s background. "Nasty Roche had big hands. He called the Friday pudding dog-in-the-blanket. And one day he had asked: —What is your father?" [001-058]. The question is not innocent — at [[clongowes-wood-college|Clongowes]], where social distinctions are keenly felt, asking about a boy's father is an inquiry into his class position. When [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] cannot give a satisfactory answer, "Nasty Roche had asked: —What is he?" [001-059], pres[[sin-and-confession|sin]]g the point until [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s inadequacy is exposed.

Later, Roche and another boy named Saurin are observed drinking "cocoa that their people sent them in tins" because "they could not drink the tea; that it was hogwash. Their fathers were magistrates, the fellows said" [001-091]. This detail — the cocoa sent from home, the magistrate fathers, the disdain for the school's weak tea — places Roche among the privileged boys whose families can afford to supplement the institutional provisions.

Roche's malice is not gratuitous but social: his questions are instruments of classification, designed to determine where [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] fits in the hierarchy and to expose any deficiency. For [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]], who is only beginning to understand that the world is organized around such distinctions, Roche represents the frightening competence of those who already know the rules.

## Relationships

Nasty Roche moves in the same social circle as Saurin, another boy whose father is a magistrate. His relationship to [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] is one of casual domination — he has the confidence to ask questions and the social standing to make the answers matter. He is distinct from [[wells|Wells]] in his mode of aggression: where [[wells|Wells]] uses physical force, Roche uses social interrogation.

## Significance

In the taxonomy of [[clongowes-wood-college|Clongowes]] that [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] must master, Nasty Roche occupies the category of "stink" — not the worst kind of boy, but not to be trusted. He embodies a particular kind of social confidence that [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] lacks: the assurance that comes from knowing who you are, who your father is, and where you stand. His questions about [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s father anticipate the novel's broader concern with paternity and authority — what does it mean to be someone's son, and what happens when you cannot give a satisfactory account of that relationship? Roche, with his magistrate father and his tinned cocoa, already knows the answers; [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]], with his increa[[sin-and-confession|sin]]gly precarious family, is only beginning to sense the questions.
