## Overview

Alfred, Lord Tennyson (1809–1892) served as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1850 until his death. His major works include *In Memoriam A.H.H.*, *The Charge of the Light Brigade*, and the Arthurian cycle *Idylls of the King*. In *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*, Tennyson represents the received literary opinion against which [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] rebels.

## In the Novel

During an exchange among [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] and his classmates at [[belvedere-college|Belvedere]], the question of who is the greatest poet arises. When [[boland|Boland]] asks who the best poet is, [[heron|Heron]] answers without hesitation: "Lord Tennyson, of course" [002-150]. [[nash|Nash]] confirms that Tennyson's poetry is a household possession: "We have all his poetry at home in a book" [002-151].

[[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] challenges this consensus with characteristic defiance, dismissing Tennyson as a mere technician of verse: "Tennyson a poet! Why, he's only a rhymester!" [002-153]. His classmates are shocked, insisting that "Everyone knows that Tennyson is the greatest poet" [002-154].

## Critical Analysis

### Tennyson versus Byron: Convention versus Rebellion

The Tennyson debate is the counterpoint to [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s preference for [[byron-poems|Byron]]. Where Tennyson represents Victorian respectability — the Poet Laureate, the voice of establishment England — Byron represents Romantic transgression. The boys who champion Tennyson are also those who condemn Byron as "a heretic and immoral too" [002-166]. The choice between the two poets is thus a choice between two models of artistic identity: the laureate who serves his society, or the exile who defies it.

[[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s dismissal of Tennyson as "only a rhymester" [002-153] is less a considered literary judgement than a gesture of self-definition. By rejecting Tennyson, he rejects the path of the socially integrated artist — the poet who enjoys official recognition and public approval. His preference for [[byron-poems|Byron]] signals his affinity for the artist as outsider, as critic of convention, as heretic.

### Tennyson in Catholic Ireland

The boys' easy invocation of Tennyson at [[belvedere-college|Belvedere]] reflects the poet's status as a cultural authority even in Catholic Ireland, where the Laureate of Protestant England might have been regarded with suspicion. That Tennyson is treated as an unquestioned benchmark of poetic greatness — "Everyone knows that Tennyson is the greatest poet" — indicates the extent to which English literary culture permeated Irish Catholic education in the late nineteenth century.
