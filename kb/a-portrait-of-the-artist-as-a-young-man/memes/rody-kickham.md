## Overview

Rody Kickham appears in the opening pages of Chapter I of *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man* as the antithesis of the young [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen Dedalus]] — athletic where [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] is weak, socially confident where [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] is uncertain, a "decent fellow" in the taxonomy of [[clongowes-wood-college|Clongowes]] where [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] struggles merely to avoid being a "stink." He is the boy [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] cannot be, the embodiment of a world of physical prowess and uncomplicated belonging from which the future artist is already exiled.

## In the Novel

Kickham is introduced in the novel's first extended scene, on the playing fields of [[clongowes-wood-college|Clongowes]]: "Rody Kickham was not like that: he would be captain of the third line all the fellows said" [001-056]. The passive construction — "all the fellows said" — captures how reputation functions at [[clongowes-wood-college|Clongowes]]: not through individual assessment but through collective consensus, a process [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] observes from outside.

[[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s internal taxonomy follows immediately: "Rody Kickham was a decent fellow but [[nasty-roche|Nasty Roche]] was a stink. Rody Kickham had greaves in his number and a hamper in the refectory" [001-058]. The details are material and social — greaves (shin guards) mark Kickham as a serious footballer, a hamper in the refectory marks him as a boy whose family sends provisions from home. These are the signs of belonging that [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]], with his weak eyes and his uncertain social position, cannot match.

Kickham makes one more brief appearance during the football scene: "Rody Kickham held the ball by its greasy lace. A fellow asked him to give it one last: but he walked on without even answering the fellow" [001-081]. The image — Kickham holding the ball, walking past without acknowledgment — is a small assertion of status, the privilege of the athlete to ignore the supplicant.

The full name "Roderick Kickham" appears near the end of the [[clongowes-wood-college|Clongowes]] section [001-530], in a list of boys, the formal version of the name suggesting the adult world toward which these schoolboys are tending.

## Relationships

Kickham is a fellow student at [[clongowes-wood-college|Clongowes]], respected for his athletic ability and socially secure. He is contrasted explicitly with [[nasty-roche|Nasty Roche]] — "Rody Kickham was a decent fellow but [[nasty-roche|Nasty Roche]] was a stink" [001-058] — in [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s developing moral taxonomy. His relationship to [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] is distant: [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] observes Kickham from the margins, aware of the gulf between them. Kickham exists in the centre of [[clongowes-wood-college|Clongowes]] life, where [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] exists on its periphery.

## Significance

Rody Kickham's narrative function is almost entirely contrastive: he is what [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] is not. His "greaves in his number" and his hamper, his captaincy of the third line, his ability to hold the ball and walk past — these details establish a world of physical ease and social confidence from which [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] is excluded. But [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s exclusion is not merely social; it is temperamental and, ultimately, artistic. Kickham belongs to the world of action, of footballs and greaves and hampers; [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] belongs — or will come to belong — to the world of reflection, of words and sensations and the transformation of experience into art. Kickham is not an antagonist; he is simply evidence that [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s path lies elsewhere.
