## Overview

Uncle Charles is a minor but sympathetically drawn character in the early chapters of *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*. An elderly relative who lives with the [[stephen-dedalus|Dedalus]] family, he appears primarily in Chapter I as a benign, mildly ineffectual presence — a man who claps at [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s dancing, enjoys his [[christmas-dinner-argument|Christmas dinner]], and attempts unsuccessfully to defuse the political arguments that erupt around him.

## In the Novel

Uncle Charles's first appearance establishes his gentle nature. When young [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] dances for the family, "Uncle Charles and [[dante-riordan|Dante]] clapped" [001-014], and [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] notes that "Uncle Charles was older than [[dante-riordan|Dante]]" — a simple observation that places the old man in the generational landscape of the household.

During the [[christmas-dinner-argument|Christmas dinner]] argument, Uncle Charles tries repeatedly to calm the escalating conflict. He attempts to mediate when [[dante-riordan|Dante]] and [[mr-casey|Mr. Casey]] clash: "Quite right, ma'am, said uncle Charles. Now Simon, that's quite enough" [001-295]. But his mild interventions are powerless against the force of ideological conviction. When [[simon-dedalus|Simon Dedalus]]'s fury mounts, Uncle Charles appeals to the presence of the child: "Simon! Simon! said uncle Charles. The boy" [001-315], reminding his son that [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] is watching the adult world tear itself apart.

Uncle Charles speaks little but is present at the dinner table throughout the scene, eating his turkey — Mr. [[stephen-dedalus|Dedalus]] "served uncle Charles and [[mr-casey|Mr Casey]] to large pieces of turkey" [001-317] — and serving as a silent witness to the family's implosion. His presence humanizes the domestic setting and underscores the tragedy of the argument: even the gentlest family member cannot restore peace when politics and religion collide.

## Relationships

Uncle Charles is the elder statesman of the [[stephen-dedalus|Dedalus]] household, related by blood to [[simon-dedalus|Simon Dedalus]] and by cohabitation to the entire family. His relationship with [[dante-riordan|Dante]], his generational peer, is cordial but overshadowed by her fierce religiosity. He seems to be on no side in the political arguments — "both his father and uncle Charles said that [[dante-riordan|Dante]] was a clever woman" [001-053] — and represents a pre-ideological domesticity that the modern political world has rendered impossible.

## Significance

Uncle Charles exemplifies what Joyce's critics have called the "Uncle Charles Principle" — the narrative technique by which the prose style adapts to the consciousness of the character being described. Though a minor figure, his presence in the novel reminds the reader of the ordinary domestic world that political and religious passions disrupt. His helplessness at the [[christmas-dinner-argument|Christmas dinner]] table foreshadows [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s own position: a witness to conflicts he cannot control, learning that the adult world offers no refuge from ideology.
