## Overview

Maurice Dedalus, the younger brother of [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen Dedalus]], appears only fleetingly in *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man* — yet his presence is quietly significant. He is the sibling who shares Stephen's domestic world, the companion repeatedly invoked by [[simon-dedalus|Simon Dedalus]] as a foil to Stephen's intelligence, and a silent witness to the family dynamics that shape the novel's protagonist. Joyce draws Maurice with the lightest of touches, but his presence rounds out the portrait of the Dedalus household as a place of crowded, sometimes comic domesticity.

## In the Novel

### The Breakfast Scene

Maurice appears most vividly at the family breakfast table in Chapter II. When [[simon-dedalus|Mr Dedalus]] discusses the possibility of sending Stephen to a Jesuit college, [[mrs-dedalus|Mrs Dedalus]] remarks: "O, I'm sure he'll work very hard now, especially when he has Maurice with him" [002-067]. Mr Dedalus, with characteristic jocular cruelty, responds: "O, Holy Paul, I forgot about Maurice. Here, Maurice! Come here, you thick-headed ruffian! Do you know I'm going to send you to a college where they'll teach you to spell c.a.t. cat" [002-068]. Maurice's response is wordless: he simply "grinned at his father and then at his brother" [002-069]. The grin — at once good-natured and opaque — is virtually Maurice's sole contribution to the novel's dialogue, and it suffices to establish him as a presence: amiable, uncomplaining, palpably less intense than his elder brother.

### Sibling Contrast

The breakfast scene establishes a clear contrast between the Dedalus sons. Stephen "mumbled his bread without answering his father's gaze" [002-070], retreating into a sullen interiority, while Maurice grins and absorbs his father's teasing without apparent hurt. The contrast illuminates Stephen's particularity — his sensitivity, his inability to take the rough-and-tumble of family life with Maurice's equanimity. Maurice, the "thick-headed ruffian," is in some sense the normal son, the one who fits the family, against whom Stephen's estrangement can be measured.

### A Name Among Names

Maurice's name also appears indirectly when Simon Dedalus, in his Cork reminiscences, lists the companions of his youth: "Maurice Moriarty, the Frenchman" [002-242]. The echo of the name "Maurice" across generations — from Simon's dead friend to his living son — suggests the web of naming and memory that binds the Dedalus men to their past. Maurice Dedalus carries a name freighted with his father's history, even as he remains a background figure in his brother's story.

## Significance

In a novel so intensely focused on the consciousness of a single character, Maurice Dedalus serves as a reminder of the lives that Stephen's narrative excludes — the siblings, the "thick-headed ruffians," the ordinary children who grin and bear it. He is a structural counterweight to Stephen's exceptionalism, a figure of normalcy against which Stephen's difference becomes visible.
