## Overview

Mary Dedalus is one of the most quietly significant characters in *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*. As [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s mother, she represents the world of family, faith, and domestic affection that her son must ultimately leave behind. She speaks fewer lines than almost any other major adult in the novel, yet her presence haunts [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s consciousness — particularly in Chapter V, when his refusal to make his Easter duty causes her to weep, and [[cranly|Cranly]] confronts him with the moral claim of a mother's love.

## In the Novel

Mrs. [[stephen-dedalus|Dedalus]] appears first at the [[christmas-dinner-argument|Christmas dinner]], where she attempts to keep peace among the warring adults. She "laid down her knife and fork" [001-293] when [[dante-riordan|Dante]]'s vehemence threatens to destroy the meal, and appeals to [[dante-riordan|Dante]] "to let the matter drop now" [001-301]. When [[mr-casey|Mr. Casey]] and Simon escalate, she cries out "[[mr-casey|Mr Casey]]! Simon! Let it end now" [001-308]. Her interventions are futile — she cannot control her husband or his guests — but they reveal her as the emotional center of the household, the one who cares more for peace than for ideological victory.

Throughout the novel, Mrs. [[stephen-dedalus|Dedalus]] is associated with the practical management of family life. She "was eating little" at the [[christmas-dinner-argument|Christmas dinner]] [001-317], attending to others rather than herself. She arranges [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s transfer from [[clongowes-wood-college|Clongowes]] to [[belvedere-college|Belvedere]] [002-059] as the family's finances decline. But her most significant moment comes offstage, in Chapter V, when [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] reveals that his refusal of Easter communion has made his mother cry.

[[cranly|Cranly]] relays the mother's anguish to [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]: "Your mother has a queer opinion of you. She says you have a wicked heart" [005-498]. The accusation is devastating because it is true — not in the moral sense his mother intends, but in the sense that the artist must cultivate a certain hardness of heart. Mrs. [[stephen-dedalus|Dedalus]]'s tears and [[cranly|Cranly]]'s challenge — "Whatever else is unsure in this stinking dunghill of a world a mother's love is not" — represent the novel's most powerful statement of the human cost of [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s artistic vocation.

## Relationships

Mary Dedalus is married to [[simon-dedalus|Simon Dedalus]] and is the mother of [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] and his siblings. Her relationship with Simon is marked by his financial irresponsibility and her quiet endurance. Her relationship with [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] is the novel's most emotionally charged maternal bond — a love that [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] must wound to become himself.

## Significance

Mrs. [[stephen-dedalus|Dedalus]] represents the claims of ordinary human love against the artist's extraordinary demands. She is not an antagonist — she is the most sympathetic figure in the novel — but her love is precisely what [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] must refuse. Joyce's treatment of Mary Dedalus is characteristically unsentimental; he makes her a fully human figure whose suffering is real, even as her son's rejection of her world is presented as necessary.
