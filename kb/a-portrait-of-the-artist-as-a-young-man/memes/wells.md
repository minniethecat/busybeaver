## Overview

Wells is [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen Dedalus]]'s first named antagonist in *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*, a fellow student at [[clongowes-wood-college|Clongowes Wood College]] whose casual cruelty — shoving [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] into the "square ditch" — becomes one of the novel's defining early memories. Wells embodies the arbitrary meanness of institutional life, where the strong prey on the weak and where [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s difference makes him a natural target.

## In the Novel

Wells's defining action occurs offstage but is recalled repeatedly throughout Chapter I: "That was mean of Wells to shoulder him into the square ditch because he would not swop his little snuffbox for Wells's seasoned hacking chestnut, the conqueror of forty" [001-085]. The specificity of the detail — the "seasoned hacking chestnut" that had defeated forty others, the "little snuffbox" [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] refused to trade — grounds the bullying in the material culture of schoolboy life, where possessions and trades carry immense social weight.

The sensory memory of the ditch water haunts [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]: "How cold and slimy the water had been! A fellow had once seen a big rat jump into the scum" [001-085]. The cold, the slime, the imagined rat — these sensations merge in [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s consciousness with his sense of Wells as a source of physical threat and humiliation.

Wells reappears later in a moment of mockery. When [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] cannot answer a question about kis[[sin-and-confession|sin]]g his mother, "Wells said: —Tell us, [[stephen-dedalus|Dedalus]], do you kiss your mother before you go to bed?" [001-100]. When [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] answers honestly — "I do" — and the other boys laugh, "Wells said: —O, I say, here's a fellow says he kisses his mother every night before he goes to bed" [001-103]. The mockery exposes [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s naivety and his failure to grasp the unspoken rules of schoolboy discourse, where sentiment is weakness.

[[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s fear of Wells surfaces even in illness. In the infirmary, "Wells's face was there. He looked at it and saw that Wells was afraid" [001-163] — but the face dissolves into the prefect's, suggesting that [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s fear of Wells merges with his broader anxiety about authority. Later, [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] imagines his own death as a vindication: "And Wells would be sorry then for what he had done" [001-178], a fantasy of posthumous moral triumph that reveals how deeply Wells's cruelty has lodged in [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]]'s imagination.

Wells also appears as a knowing commentator on school sexual politics: "A fat lot you know about it, Thunder! Wells said. I know why they scut" [001-353], suggesting a worldly knowledge that places him among the more sexually aware older boys.

## In the Eyes of Others

- **[[stephen-dedalus|Stephen Dedalus]]'s view**: For Stephen, Wells is the template of the antagonist — a figure of physical threat who transforms Stephen's sincerity into a joke and his body into a site of cold and fear. Stephen's recollection "That was mean of Wells" [001-085] carries the weight of a child's moral judgment, and his fantasy that "Wells would be sorry then for what he had done" [001-178] reveals both the depth of the wound and Stephen's powerlessness to address it in the real world.

- **The other boys' view**: Wells operates with the tacit approval of the schoolboy hierarchy. No one intervenes when he shoulders Stephen into the ditch; the boys laugh when he mocks Stephen's affection for his mother. He is not a loner-bully but a participant in the collective enforcement of social norms — the boy who polices the boundary between "decent fellows" and outsiders.

- **Wells's own self-perception**: Wells seems unaware of himself as a bully in any moral sense. His question about kissing mother is presented as banter, his shoving of Stephen as a casual enforcement of the rules of trade. He inhabits the world of schoolboy values with unreflective confidence — a world where physical dominance and social mockery are not cruelty but simply the way things are.

- **The narrator's implicit judgment**: Joyce's narrative voice never condemns Wells directly, but the juxtaposition of Wells's casual cruelty with Stephen's intense inner suffering creates an implicit indictment. The boy who shoves another into "cold and slimy" water and then forgets about it stands in stark contrast to the boy who cannot forget — and it is the latter, Joyce suggests, who will become the artist.

## Narrative Technique

- **Offstage violence**: Wells's defining action — the shoving into the ditch — is never narrated directly. It exists only in Stephen's retrospective thought. This technique gives the event the quality of traumatic memory: it happened before the narrative began, yet it echoes through every subsequent scene, coloring Stephen's perception of Clongowes as a place of latent threat.

- **Metonymic detail**: The "seasoned hacking chestnut, the conqueror of forty" [001-085] functions as a metonym for the entire schoolboy value system — combat, conquest, possession — that Stephen cannot or will not participate in. Wells's chestnut and Stephen's snuffbox are small objects that carry enormous symbolic weight, standing in for two incompatible ways of being a boy.

- **Sensory residue**: Joyce anchors the memory of Wells in physical sensation — "cold and slimy," the imagined rat "jump into the scum" [001-085]. This sensory language belongs to the pre-verbal world of childhood fear, and its recurrence in Stephen's thoughts demonstrates how trauma lodges in the body before it can be articulated in words.

- **Dissolving identity**: In the infirmary fever-dream, "Wells's face" dissolves into the prefect's [001-163], merging personal antagonist with institutional authority. This technique — the blurring of individual and system — recurs throughout the novel and underlies Stephen's growing recognition that his real adversary is not any single boy or master but the entire structure of expectations and punishments that Clongowes (and later Ireland itself) represents.

## Relationships

Wells is a bully to [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] but operates within the normal hierarchies of [[clongowes-wood-college|Clongowes]]. He is not a ringleader like [[heron|Heron]] in Chapter II but rather an ordinary schoolboy whose cruelty is casual rather than systematic. His relationship to [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] is defined by the power imbalance of physical strength and social confidence — Wells belongs to the world of "decent fellows" and "stinks" that [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] struggles to navigate.

## Significance

Wells represents the banality of schoolboy cruelty. He is not a villain in any grand sense — he is simply a boy who, given the opportunity to torment someone weaker, takes it. For [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]], Wells becomes the template for the antagonist: the person who exposes his vulnerability, who transforms his [[sin-and-confession|sin]]cerity into a joke, who makes his body feel weak and cold and afraid. The memory of the square ditch, with its cold slimy water and its imagined rat, recurs as a metaphor for the hostile world into which [[stephen-dedalus|Stephen]] has been thrust — a world where art, sensitivity, and [[sin-and-confession|sin]]cerity find no protection.
