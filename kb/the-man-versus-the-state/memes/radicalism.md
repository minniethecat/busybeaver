## Definition

**Radicalism**, in the context of nineteenth-century British politics, was the political movement advocating thoroughgoing reform of established institutions — the extension of the franchise, the abolition of aristocratic privilege, and the restructuring of government along democratic lines. Herbert Spencer's treatment of radicalism in *Social Statics* was distinctive in its refusal to treat radicals and conservatives as representing fundamentally different moral tendencies. Instead, Spencer argued that both parties were "units of the same society, acted upon by the same spirit of the age, moulded after the same type of character." Radicalism, no less than the aristocracy it denounced, was in Spencer's analysis a product of the same social conditions and subject to the same moral limitations — a diagnosis that set him apart from both the radical and conservative camps.

> While indignant Radicalism denounces "the vile aristocrats," these in their turn enlarge with horror on the brutality of the mob. Neither party sees its own sins. Neither party recognizes in the other, itself in a different dress. Neither party can believe that it would do all the other does if placed in like circumstances.

## Key Arguments

**The moral symmetry of class antagonism**. Spencer's central claim about radicalism was that radicals and aristocrats were moral equivalents — each condemned in the other what they failed to recognise in themselves. The radical's denunciation of aristocratic oppression and the aristocrat's denunciation of mob brutality were, Spencer argued, "the inflammatory symptoms of a uniformly-diffused immorality." This was not a claim that radicalism was wrong in its political objectives but that the moral self-righteousness with which radicals attacked their opponents was misplaced. The mechanical law that "action and reaction are equal and opposite" had, Spencer claimed, "its moral analogue": "the deed of one man to another tends ultimately to produce a like effect on both, be the deed good or bad."

**Social determinism of political character**. Spencer grounded his analysis of radicalism in a broader theory of social determinism. "Whoso is placed among the savage will in process of time grow savage too; let his companions be treacherous and he will become treacherous in self-defence; surround him with the kind-hearted and he will soften; amid the refined he will acquire polish." The political character of both radicals and aristocrats was, in this view, the product of their social environment, not of any inherent moral superiority or inferiority. The implication was that changing political institutions — the radical's programme — could not by itself produce moral improvement, since the moral character of the people would remain shaped by the same social conditions that had produced the existing corruption.

**The limits of institutional reform**. Spencer's social determinism placed a fundamental limitation on the radical programme. Political institutions, he argued, were expressions of the moral character of a people, not its causes. "No division into castes, no differences of wealth, can prevent men from assimilating" to the moral level of their society. Radicalism, in focusing on institutional change, risked mistaking the symptom for the disease: the oppressive character of aristocratic government reflected the character of a society still governed by the ethic of status and the psychology of power-worship; replacing aristocrats with democrats would not, by itself, change that character. True reform required what Spencer called the "adaptation of character" to the conditions of social life — a process far slower and more fundamental than any legislative programme.

**Historical evidence of class symmetry**. Spencer supported his argument with historical examples. The cruelties of the old Roman rulers "were fully paralleled by those over which the populace gloated in their arenas." During the servile wars of the middle ages, "barons tortured rebels and rebels tortured barons, with equally diabolical ferocity." The Galician massacres of the 1840s "covered with infamy both the people who committed them and the government which paid for them at per head." Each example demonstrated the same pattern: those who suffered oppression were, when positions were reversed, no less oppressive than their former masters.

## Historical Context

Spencer wrote *Social Statics* at a moment of intense radical agitation in Britain. Chartism, though its peak had passed by 1851, had mobilised millions around demands for universal male suffrage and parliamentary reform. The radical press, from the *Northern Star* to the *Poor Man's Guardian*, regularly denounced the "vile aristocrats" in language of the kind Spencer quoted. His refusal to endorse radical denunciations of aristocracy placed him at odds with the main current of working-class political movements. Spencer was not a conservative — he opposed aristocratic privilege as strongly as any radical — but his evolutionary perspective led him to see both sides of the class struggle as manifestations of the same underlying social condition, and his reform programme focused on the gradual adaptation of character rather than the immediate overthrow of institutions.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

- **[[toryism]]**: The political ideology of the aristocratic interest, which Spencer treated as the mirror image of radicalism — each defined by its opposition to the other, each sharing the same underlying moral character.
- **[[liberalism]]**: The political philosophy from which Spencer sought to distinguish his own position — "true Liberalism," in Spencer's terms, was neither radical nor conservative but grounded in the law of equal freedom.
- **[[democracy]]**: The institutional objective of radical reform — which Spencer regarded as an advance over aristocracy but not a guarantee of liberty, since democratic majorities could be as oppressive as aristocratic minorities.
- **[[militant-type]]**: The social form characterised by compulsory cooperation and hierarchical command — the soil in which both radical and aristocratic class antagonism flourished.
- **[[industrial-type]]**: The social form toward which Spencer believed society should evolve — characterised by voluntary cooperation that would, over time, dissolve the class antagonisms on which both radicalism and toryism depended.
