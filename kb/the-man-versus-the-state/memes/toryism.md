## Definition

**Toryism**, as analysed by Herbert Spencer, denotes the political doctrine that the state possesses an inherent, positive authority to direct and regulate social life, and that the proper remedy for social ills is an extension of governmental power. Spencer's distinctive contribution was to argue that this doctrine was not confined to the Conservative Party but had been adopted — in a new and more dangerous form — by the Liberal advocates of legislative reform. In *The Man Versus the State* (1884), Spencer identified a "New Toryism" that used the language of progress and humanitarian reform to pursue the same expansion of state power that the old Toryism had justified by tradition and hierarchy.

This analysis was central to Spencer's broader diagnosis of the political trajectory of Victorian Britain: the gradual abandonment of classical liberal principles in favour of an interventionist state — not despite democratization, but through it.

## Key Arguments

Spencer's analysis of Toryism unfolds across several dimensions.

**First, the essence of Tory doctrine is the legitimacy of governmental authority as such**. Whether justified by divine right, prescriptive tradition, or democratic mandate, the core Tory claim is that the state possesses a positive mandate to direct society. The content of policy is secondary; what defines Toryism is the premise that government is the appropriate agent of social improvement.

**Second, the "New Toryism" is more dangerous than the old**. Old Toryism, with its doctrines of passive obedience and divine right, was at least intellectually honest about its authoritarian character. It could be recognized, debated, and opposed. New Toryism, by contrast, operates through ostensibly liberal institutions — elected parliaments, reform legislation, humanitarian rhetoric — and is therefore harder to identify and resist. It "did but give definite shape" to a logic already latent in the Benthamite identification of rights with legislative enactment.

> The Tories have proverbially been the advocates of authority, but the Liberals have now taken the same ground.

**Third, Toryism is a trans-party phenomenon**. Spencer's most provocative claim was that the legislative expansion of the nineteenth century — factory acts, public health acts, compulsory education, municipal housing — was not a departure from Tory principles but their realization under new management. When Liberals advocated extending state control over sanitation, education, and commerce, they were, in Spencer's analysis, acting as Tories in all but name. The Tory principle was "the authority of the State over the individual," and the Liberal principle had historically been "the liberty of the individual against the State" — a distinction that was being erased by the convergence of both parties on the interventionist model.

**Fourth, Toryism versus industrialism**. Spencer contrasted the Tory political type with the industrial type of social organization. Toryism belonged to the "militant" stage of social evolution — hierarchical, coercive, status-based — while the industrial type was voluntary, cooperative, and contract-based. The resurgence of legislative interventionism in an ostensibly industrial society represented an atavism, a reversion to the militant pattern under industrial conditions.

## Historical Context

The term "Tory" originated in the seventeenth century as a label for those who supported the royal prerogative against Parliament. By Spencer's time, the Tory Party (increasingly called the Conservative Party under Peel and Disraeli) had shifted from the defence of Stuart absolutism to a more flexible alliance of landed interests, Anglican establishment, and paternalist reform — exemplified by Disraeli's "One Nation" conservatism.

Spencer wrote *The Man Versus the State* in the context of the Liberal Party's internal crisis over Irish Home Rule and the broader question of whether liberalism meant individual liberty or collective improvement through state action. The "New Toryism" was Spencer's name for what later generations would call "new liberalism" or "social liberalism" — the transformation of the Liberal Party from a party of limited government into a party of welfare-state expansion.
