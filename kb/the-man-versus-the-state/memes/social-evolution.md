## Definition

Social evolution, in Herbert Spencer's theoretical system, is the process by which human societies develop from simple, homogeneous aggregates into complex, heterogeneous structures through the twin mechanisms of differentiation (the separation of functions) and integration (the coordination of differentiated parts). Spencer articulated this theory before and independently of Darwin's biological evolution, though he later incorporated natural selection into his framework. For Spencer, social evolution is not merely analogous to biological evolution but is a manifestation of the same universal law of progress that operates across all domains of nature.

Spencer's evolutionary theory provides the foundation for his entire social and political philosophy. His critique of state intervention, his defense of individual liberty, his analysis of institutional development, and his predictions about the future of industrial society all rest on the evolutionary premise that social forms develop through gradual, spontaneous processes that legislation can disrupt but cannot replace.

## Key Arguments

### The Law of Differentiation and Integration

Spencer posits a universal developmental law: all systems evolve from incoherent homogeneity to coherent heterogeneity. In the social domain, this means that primitive societies, in which every individual performs essentially the same functions, give way to advanced societies characterized by elaborate divisions of labor, specialized institutions, and complex interdependencies. Spencer illustrates this with a sustained biological analogy:

> "The earliest social organisms consist almost wholly of repetitions of one element. Every man is a warrior, hunter, fisherman, builder, agriculturist, toolmaker. Each portion of the community performs the same duties with every other portion; much as each slice of the polyp's body is alike stomach, muscle, skin, and lungs." (Social Statics, "General Considerations")

The progress from this primitive uniformity to modern complexity is neither accidental nor reversible. It is driven by the same necessity that produces the differentiation of organs in biological organisms — the increasing efficiency that comes from specialization.

### Militant vs. Industrial Types

Spencer's most influential application of evolutionary theory to politics is his distinction between two social types. The militant type, adapted to chronic warfare, is characterized by compulsory cooperation, rigid hierarchy, and the subordination of individual welfare to collective defense. The industrial type, adapted to peaceful exchange, is characterized by voluntary cooperation, contract-based relationships, and the protection of individual rights.

Social evolution, in Spencer's account, consists in the progressive displacement of militant structures by industrial ones — a process that is neither linear nor guaranteed. Regression is always possible, and Spencer saw the legislative expansion of his own day as precisely such a regression — the re-emergence of compulsory cooperation under industrial guise.

### Evolution and Ethics

Spencer integrates his evolutionary theory with his ethical system. The ultimate standard of conduct, he argues, is adaptation to the conditions of social life. The "ideal man" is the man fully adapted to the requirements of the industrial type — capable of voluntary cooperation, respectful of the equal freedom of others, and independent of coercive direction:

> "The ultimate man will be one whose private requirements coincide with public ones. He will be that manner of man who, in spontaneously fulfilling his own nature, incidentally performs the functions of a social unit." (Social Statics, "General Considerations")

This evolutionary ethics provides the ultimate justification for Spencer's principle of equal freedom: only under conditions of liberty can the adaptive process operate, progressively eliminating conduct unsuited to social life and promoting conduct conducive to it.

## Historical Context

Spencer's theory of social evolution predates Darwin's *On the Origin of Species* (1859). Spencer had published *Social Statics* in 1851 and had outlined his evolutionary philosophy in essays throughout the 1850s. He coined the phrase "survival of the fittest" in 1864, which Darwin later adopted. Spencer's evolutionary thought was influenced by Lamarckian ideas about the inheritance of acquired characteristics and by the embryological theory of Karl Ernst von Baer, which described development as a progression from homogeneity to heterogeneity.

Spencer's social evolutionism was enormously influential in the late nineteenth century, shaping the emerging disciplines of sociology and anthropology. It was later criticized for its teleological character, its assumption of unilinear progress, and its use to justify colonial hierarchies. Within Spencer's own system, however, social evolution is not a guarantee of progress but a description of the conditions under which progress can occur — conditions that unwise legislation can undermine.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

Social evolution is the master concept of Spencer's theoretical system, underlying all his more specific doctrines. It provides the framework for his distinction between **[[militancy]]** and **[[industrialism]]**, the two poles of social organization. It grounds his critique of **[[expediency]]** — empiricist legislation ignores the evolutionary laws that govern social development. It illuminates the concept of the **[[social-organism]]**, the analogy between biological and social organization. It explains why **[[poor-law]]** relief is not merely unjust but counterproductive — it interferes with the adaptive mechanism by which conduct is brought into harmony with conditions. The **[[law-of-equal-freedom]]** is, in evolutionary terms, the condition under which the adaptive process operates most efficiently.
