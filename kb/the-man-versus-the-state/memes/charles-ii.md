## Biography

Charles II (1630–1685) was King of England, Scotland, and Ireland from the Restoration of the monarchy in 1660 until his death. The eldest surviving son of Charles I, who was executed in 1649, Charles spent the Interregnum years in exile on the Continent before being invited to return by the Convention Parliament following the collapse of the Protectorate.

His reign — the Restoration period — was marked by a reaction against Puritan austerity and by the re-establishment of the Anglican Church, the theatre, and other institutions suppressed under Cromwell. Politically, Charles II sought to strengthen royal prerogative, clashing repeatedly with Parliament over religious toleration, foreign policy, and the succession. His secret Treaty of Dover (1670) with Louis XIV of France, by which he promised to declare himself a Catholic in return for French subsidies, exemplified both his absolutist leanings and his reliance on extra-parliamentary means.

The latter part of his reign saw the emergence of the Whig party in opposition to his efforts to exclude his Catholic brother James from the succession, a conflict that would define English political alignments for generations.

## Spencer's References

Herbert Spencer cited Charles II in three separate passages across *Social Statics* and *The Man versus The State*, each illustrating a different dimension of his case against arbitrary governmental power.

### Sumptuary Legislation and the Regulation of Private Life

In his extended satirical catalogue of state interventions in *Social Statics*, Spencer invoked Charles II among a series of monarchs who presumed to dictate how their subjects should dress:

> "Speaking of taste naturally reminds us of dress, in which sundry improvements might be enforced; for instance — the abolition of hats: we should have good precedents either in Edward IV., who fined those wearing 'any gown or mantell' not according to specification, and who limited the superfluity of peoples' boot-toes, or in Charles II., who prescribed the material for his subjects' grave-clothes."

Spencer placed this reference within a *reductio ad absurdum* argument against the logic of state intervention: if the State may properly regulate work, education, health, and amusements, consistency demands that it also regulate dress, diet, and even burial garments — a conclusion Spencer considered manifestly absurd. Charles II's sumptuary prescription served as a historical datum proving that rulers, once granted the principle of intervention, will extend it into the most intimate domains of private life.

### Colonial Authorization and the Arrogation of Power

In the chapter on "Government Colonization" in *Social Statics*, Spencer cited Charles II's charter to the Connecticut colony as an instance of royal presumption dressed as legitimate authority:

> "Nor did Charles II. show less coolness, when he gave to Winthrop, Mason, and others, power to 'kill, slay, and destroy, by all fitting ways, enterprises, and means whatsoever, all and every such person or persons as shall at any time hereafter attempt or enterprise the destruction, invasion, detriment, or annoyance of the inhabitants,' of the proposed plantation of Connecticut."

Spencer grouped this grant alongside Pope Alexander VI's partitioning of the New World and Queen Elizabeth's charter to Sir Humphrey Gilbert, arguing that all colonizing expeditions "have borne a repulsive likeness to the doings of buccaneers." For Spencer, the moral problem was structural: no government, however constituted, possesses the authority to grant powers of life and death over distant peoples, because such authority presupposes a warrant no earthly ruler can claim.

### Whig Resistance and the Birth of English Liberalism

In *The Man versus The State*, Spencer identified opposition to Charles II as the formative moment of the Whig tradition:

> "Whiggism began with resistance to Charles II. and his cabal, in their efforts to re-establish unchecked monarchical power. The Whigs 'regarded the monarchy as a civil institution, established by the nation for the benefit of all its members'; while with the Tories 'the monarch was the delegate of heaven.'"

Spencer used this historical framing to advance a broader thesis: that the Whig/Tory distinction originated as a structural opposition between voluntary co-operation (the industrial type) and compulsory co-operation (the militant type). The resistance to Charles II's absolutist project represented, in Spencer's evolutionary schema, the first clear political expression of industrial-type social organization — the principle that rulers are answerable to the ruled and that governmental authority is conditional, not absolute.

## Historical Significance

Charles II occupies a pivotal position in the history of English liberty. His reign saw both the assertion of royal prerogative at its most ambitious since the Civil War and the emergence of organised parliamentary opposition that would, within a generation, permanently subordinate the Crown to Parliament in the Glorious Revolution of 1688.

Spencer's three references to Charles II map onto three enduring themes of liberal constitutional thought: (1) the principle that the State has no legitimate business regulating the private choices of individuals, even in matters as trivial as clothing; (2) the principle that no ruler possesses inherent authority to dispose of the lives and territories of unrepresented peoples; and (3) the principle, foundational to Whig constitutionalism, that monarchy is a human institution answerable to the nation, not a divine mandate immune from resistance.

The sumptuary laws context is particularly revealing of Spencer's method: he treated regulations that his contemporaries might dismiss as quaint historical curiosities as evidence of a persistent logic — once you concede that the State may act for a subject's own good, the principle admits of no logical stopping point short of total regulation.
