## Definition

"The Coming Slavery" is the title of the second chapter of Herbert Spencer's *The Man Versus the State* (1884), and serves as one of his most influential and controversial arguments. The phrase names Spencer's central thesis that the gradual accumulation of state interventions — each individually plausible and well-intentioned — collectively produces a system of compulsory cooperation that differs from chattel slavery in form but not in essential character. Spencer argues that the legislative expansion of the late nineteenth century, driven by a "new Toryism" that appropriated liberal rhetoric, was steadily transforming free citizens into dependents of the state.

The concept is grounded in Spencer's distinction between two fundamental modes of social organization: the *militant type*, characterized by compulsory cooperation and status-based hierarchy, and the *industrial type*, characterized by voluntary cooperation and contract-based relationships. The coming slavery, in Spencer's analysis, represents a regression toward the militant type under the guise of progressive reform.

## Key Arguments

### Compulsory vs. Voluntary Cooperation

Spencer frames his argument through a fundamental dichotomy in forms of social organization:

> "If, instead of using the word 'co-operation' in a limited sense, we use it in its widest sense, as signifying the combined activities of citizens under whatever system of regulation; then these two are definable as the system of compulsory co-operation and the system of voluntary co-operation. The typical structure of the one we see in an army formed of conscripts, in which the units in their several grades have to fulfil commands under pain of death, and receive food and clothing and pay, arbitrarily apportioned; while the typical structure of the other we see in a body of producers or distributors, who severally agree to specified payments in return for specified services, and may at will, after due notice, leave the organization if they do not like it." (The Man Versus the State, "The New Toryism")

Every new legislative mandate, Spencer contends, shifts the balance from voluntary to compulsory cooperation. A law requiring school attendance, a law regulating working hours, a law providing public housing — each may seem reasonable in isolation, but the cumulative trajectory is toward a society in which every aspect of life is prescribed by state authority.

### The New Toryism

Spencer coins the term "New Toryism" to describe the political transformation he observes in his own time. Traditional Toryism had been honest in its defense of hierarchy and state authority; the "New Tories" were those who called themselves Liberals while pursuing policies that expanded state power far beyond what any old Tory would have contemplated.

The mechanism is self-reinforcing: each intervention creates expectations and dependencies that generate demands for further interventions. The state that provides poor relief is soon called upon to provide education; the state that educates is then called upon to regulate employment; the state that regulates employment is then expected to provide housing, healthcare, and pensions. Spencer saw this spiral unfolding in the legislation of his own day and projected its logical terminus.

### The Evasion of Natural Penalties

A core element of Spencer's argument is that state intervention severs the connection between conduct and its natural consequences — what he calls "the evasion of natural penalties." Just as the Poor Law protects the improvident from the consequences of improvidence, so each expansion of state protection shields individuals from the discipline imposed by reality:

> "To separate pain from ill-doing is to fight against the constitution of things, and will be followed by far more pain. Saving men from the natural penalties of dissolute living, eventually necessitates the infliction of artificial penalties in solitary cells, on tread-wheels, and by the lash." (The Man Versus the State, "The Coming Slavery")

The paradox is that humanitarian intervention generates increasingly inhumane forms of control. The soft hand of the welfare officer is backed by the hard fist of the police power; the two expand together.

## Historical Context

*The Man Versus the State* was published in 1884, at the height of the Victorian debate over the role of government. The Reform Acts of 1867 and 1884 had dramatically expanded the franchise, creating a political market for legislative promises of social improvement. Meanwhile, the rise of the German welfare state under Bismarck provided a visible model of state-administered social insurance. In England, the Factory Acts, the Public Health Acts, the Education Act of 1870, and the expanding scope of municipal government all testified to the growth of state activity.

Spencer wrote as a defender of classical liberal principles against what he saw as their betrayal by the Liberal Party itself. His critique was directed as much at his fellow liberals — who had abandoned the principle of limited government in pursuit of electoral advantage — as at traditional conservatives. The essay generated intense controversy and established Spencer as the leading philosophical opponent of the emerging welfare state.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

The Coming Slavery extends Spencer's critique of **[[poor-law]]** from a specific policy to a general principle of political dynamics. It operationalizes the **[[law-of-equal-freedom]]** by showing how each statutory restriction diminishes the sphere of voluntary action. The argument presupposes Spencer's theory of **[[social-evolution]]**, particularly the distinction between militant and industrial social types. It also engages critically with **[[utilitarianism]]**, whose "greatest happiness" formula Spencer saw as providing rhetorical cover for unlimited state expansion. The argument's logical structure mirrors that of **[[expediency]]** — both diagnose the failure of empirical, case-by-case policymaking unaided by general principles.
