## Biography

George William Lyttelton (1817–1876), 4th Baron Lyttelton, was a British peer, Liberal politician, and educational reformer. Educated at Trinity College, Cambridge, he succeeded to the barony in 1837. He served as Under-Secretary of State for War and the Colonies from 1846.

Lyttelton was a founding member of the Canterbury Association, which organized the Anglican settlement of Christchurch, New Zealand. He was active in educational and ecclesiastical affairs, serving as Chief Commissioner of Endowed Schools and as a church commissioner.

His most striking appearance in political history is through a memorial addressed to William Gladstone, which Herbert Spencer seized upon as a confession of the state's incompetence in managing public works.

## Key Ideas

### The Gladstone Memorial and Public Works Failure

In a memorial adopted under Lord Lyttelton's chairmanship and addressed to Gladstone, the assembled peers, MPs, ratepayers, and inhabitants of London documented the comprehensive failure of state-managed public works:

> "...there is still a lamentable and deplorable state of our whole arrangements with regard to public works — vacillation, uncertainty, costliness, extravagance, meanness, and all the conflicting vices that could be enumerated, are united in our present system"

For Spencer, this memorial was a generalizable indictment of the state's capacity to manage practical affairs. The six vices — vacillation, uncertainty, costliness, extravagance, meanness, and their mutual conflict — described the inherent characteristics of governmental administration divorced from market discipline.

### Confessions of Statesmen as Evidence

Spencer built his case against state expansion on "confessions made by statesmen and by State departments," not on abstract theory. The Lyttelton memorial served as a powerful example because it came from within the political class:

> "I refer rather to confessions made by statesmen and by State departments. Here, for example, in a memorial addressed to Mr. Gladstone, and adopted by a highly-influential meeting held under the chairmanship of the late Lord Lyttelton..."

This rhetorical strategy — using the admissions of political insiders against the system they served — was central to Spencer's method in *The Man Versus the State*. The memorial demonstrated that governmental incompetence was not a partisan allegation but an acknowledged fact among administrators.

## Major Works

Lord Lyttelton published several works on educational and ecclesiastical topics. His most politically consequential act, however, was the memorial to Gladstone on public works administration — a document that Spencer preserved as evidence of the systemic incompetence of state management.

## Influence / Legacy

Though Lyttelton himself remains an obscure figure, his memorial to Gladstone achieved a kind of immortality through Spencer's use of it. The six-fold indictment of state public works — "vacillation, uncertainty, costliness, extravagance, meanness, and all the conflicting vices" — became a standing challenge to advocates of expanded governmental administration.

The question Spencer implicitly posed through Lyttelton's confession remains relevant: if the state cannot competently manage roads and sewers, why entrust it with the far more complex task of managing the conditions of social life?
