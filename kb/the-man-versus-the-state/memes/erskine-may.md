## Biography

Thomas Erskine May (1815–1886), later Sir Erskine May, was an English constitutional theorist and long-serving clerk of the House of Commons. He entered parliamentary service in 1831 as an assistant librarian and rose to become Clerk of the House in 1871 — the highest official in the Commons administration.

May's most enduring contribution is *A Treatise upon the Law, Privileges, Proceedings and Usage of Parliament* (1844), known universally as "Erskine May: Parliamentary Practice." It remains the authoritative guide to parliamentary procedure to this day. His *Constitutional History of England* (1861–1863) was a landmark study of British constitutional development.

Herbert Spencer cited May's *Constitutional History* in *The Man Versus the State* to document the human suffering caused by class-based legislation — specifically, the Corn Laws that protected the agricultural rents of the landed class at the expense of the hungry poor.

## Key Ideas

### Parliament as the Instrument of Class Legislation

Spencer invoked Erskine May's authority to demonstrate that parliamentary legislation has historically served dominant economic interests rather than universal welfare. May's *Constitutional History* provided the documentary evidence that Spencer needed:

> "A list coming down in our own country to those under which there were long maintained slavery and the slave-trade, torturing nearly 40,000 negroes annually by close packing during a tropical voyage, and killing a large percentage of them, and ending with the corn-laws, by which, says Sir Erskine May, 'to ensure high rents, it had been decreed that multitudes should hunger.'"

The Corn Laws (1815–1846) imposed tariffs on imported grain, artificially raising bread prices to protect the incomes of the landowning class. May's judgment reduced the parliamentary process to its starkest ethical terms: legislation as legalized harm.

### Continuity of Militant Ethics in Civil Government

Spencer placed May's evidence within a broader argument about the persistence of military ethics in civilian governance. In war, the commander requisitions lives and property without consent. In peacetime, Spencer argued, the legislature inherits this sovereign power and treats citizens' property as equally disposable:

> "Inevitably the established code of conduct in the dealings of Governments with citizens, must be allied to their code of conduct in their dealings with one another."

The Corn Laws were not an aberration but a manifestation of the deep structure of state power — the same logic of plunder that governed international relations operating domestically through class legislation.

### The Limits of Constitutional History

May's work represented the Whig interpretation of constitutional history: the story of liberty gradually expanding through parliamentary reform. Spencer's use of May was thus ironic — he deployed the foremost constitutional historian of the age to undermine the presumption that Parliament is a reliable guardian of liberty.

If the same institution that maintained the slave trade and the Corn Laws could not be trusted with unchecked power, then constitutional safeguards — not parliamentary goodwill — were the true foundation of freedom.

## Major Works

- *A Treatise upon the Law, Privileges, Proceedings and Usage of Parliament* (1844) — The definitive manual of parliamentary procedure, regularly updated and still authoritative.
- *Constitutional History of England since the Accession of George III* (1861–1863, 2 vols.) — A comprehensive survey of British constitutional development, cited by Spencer at volume ii, page 617.

## Influence / Legacy

Erskine May's *Parliamentary Practice* remains the procedural bible of the House of Commons and of Westminster-model parliaments worldwide. His *Constitutional History*, while reflecting the Whig historiographical tradition, provided generations of political thinkers with the factual foundation for arguments about the relationship between legislation and liberty.

Spencer's citation of May in *The Man Versus the State* ensured that the constitutional historian's work entered the canon of classical liberal argumentation. The phrase "to ensure high rents, it had been decreed that multitudes should hunger" became a touchstone for critiques of class legislation.
