## Biography

Herbert Spencer (27 April 1820 – 8 December 1903) was an English philosopher, biologist, sociologist, and prominent classical liberal political theorist of the Victorian era. Born in Derby, England, Spencer was largely self-educated, having rejected a Cambridge education offered by his uncle. He worked first as a railway engineer before turning to journalism and eventually to full-time philosophical writing.

Spencer's magnum opus was the *Synthetic Philosophy*, a multi-volume work spanning biology, psychology, sociology, and ethics, unified by the principle of evolution. His political philosophy was most forcefully expressed in *Social Statics* (1851) and *The Man Versus the State* (1884). In these works, Spencer argued for the maximum freedom of the individual against the encroaching power of the state, developing what he called the "law of equal freedom."

Spencer was among the most widely read and influential intellectuals of the nineteenth century. His ideas on evolution — which he developed independently of and prior to Darwin's *On the Origin of Species* — shaped the intellectual climate of his age. He coined the phrase "survival of the fittest," which Darwin later adopted. By the end of his life, Spencer's reputation had begun to decline, but his influence on classical liberalism, libertarianism, and sociology remains substantial.

## Key Ideas

**The Law of Equal Freedom.** Spencer's foundational ethical principle states that "every man has freedom to do all that he wills, provided he infringes not the equal freedom of any other man." This principle, derived from the conditions necessary for social existence, serves as the first principle from which all specific rights — to property, to life, to free speech — are deduced.

**The Moral Sense.** Responding to Benthamite utilitarianism, Spencer argued that humans possess an innate moral sense — an instinctive perception of right and wrong — analogous to the geometric sense that perceives spatial relations. This moral sense is not infallible but provides the intuitive basis from which reason can develop scientific morality.

**Evolution and Adaptation.** Spencer extended the evolutionary principle beyond biology to society, psychology, and ethics. He held that human nature is progressively adapting from its original predatory state to the requirements of social life. Evil and suffering result from "non-adaptation of constitution to conditions," and progress consists in the gradual diminution of this mismatch.

**Anti-Statism.** In *The Man Versus the State*, Spencer warned against the "New Toryism" — the tendency of nominally Liberal governments to expand coercive regulation. True liberalism, he argued, had historically meant *decreasing* compulsory co-operation and *increasing* voluntary co-operation. The growing legislative interference in economic and social life represented a reversion to the coercive regime characteristic of militarism.

**Social Organism.** Spencer developed an extended analogy between society and a biological organism. Both evolve from simple, homogeneous structures toward complex, heterogeneous ones characterized by increasing division of labour and mutual dependence. However, unlike the biological organism where the cells exist for the whole, in society the whole exists for the benefit of its individual members.

## Major Works

- **Social Statics** (1851) — Spencer's first major political work, establishing the law of equal freedom and applying it to property rights, women's rights, children's rights, and the limits of state authority.
- **The Principles of Psychology** (1855) — An early application of evolutionary principles to mental phenomena.
- **First Principles** (1862) — The foundational volume of the *Synthetic Philosophy*, articulating the law of evolution as the universal principle of change.
- **The Principles of Biology** (1864–1867) — Extended evolutionary theory to biological phenomena and coined "survival of the fittest."
- **The Principles of Sociology** (1876–1896) — Applied evolutionary analysis to social institutions including ceremonial, political, ecclesiastical, and industrial systems.
- **The Principles of Ethics** (1879–1893) — Culmination of the *Synthetic Philosophy*; Part IV (*Justice*) provided the mature formulation of Spencer's political ethics.
- **The Man Versus the State** (1884) — A fierce critique of the expansion of state power under the guise of liberalism, warning against "the coming slavery."
- **An Autobiography** (1904) — Posthumously published, detailing Spencer's intellectual development and personal life.

## Influence / Legacy

Spencer's influence was immense during his lifetime. In the United States, his ideas were championed by industrialists and laissez-faire advocates. His evolutionary sociology influenced William Graham Sumner and the early development of American sociology. His individualism and anti-statism prefigured much of twentieth-century libertarian thought, including figures such as Friedrich Hayek and Robert Nozick.

Spencer's reputation declined in the early twentieth century as "social Darwinism" fell into disrepute and as the welfare state expanded. However, his critique of government overreach and his defense of individual liberty have experienced periodic revivals, particularly among classical liberal and libertarian thinkers. His organic theory of society, despite its limitations, anticipated later developments in systems theory and complexity science.

## Relationship to Other Figures

- [[jeremy-bentham]] — Spencer's primary philosophical opponent; he critiqued Benthamite utilitarianism for its lack of a fixed moral principle and its implicit reliance on the very "moral sense" it denied.
- [[herbert-spencer]] and [[john-locke]] — Spencer built on Lockean natural rights theory, but grounded rights in evolutionary adaptation rather than in a hypothetical state of nature.
- [[herbert-spencer]] and [[charles-darwin]] — Though often conflated, Spencer's evolutionary theory predated Darwin's and was more Lamarckian in character; Darwin adopted Spencer's phrase "survival of the fittest."
