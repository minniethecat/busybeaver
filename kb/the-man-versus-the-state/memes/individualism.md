## Definition

Individualism, in Herbert Spencer's political philosophy, is the principle that the individual person — not the state, the community, or the species — is the fundamental unit of moral and political analysis. Every claim made on behalf of the collective must ultimately be justified in terms of its effects on the individuals who compose it. As Spencer writes, "the welfare of society considered as an aggregate of individuals" is the only defensible standard of political evaluation.

Spencer's individualism is not atomistic in the sense of denying social bonds. On the contrary, he devotes considerable attention to the social nature of human beings and the organic character of society. But he insists that society has no interests apart from the interests of the individuals who constitute it, and that collective action that sacrifices some individuals for the supposed benefit of others is illegitimate.

## Key Arguments

### The Individual as the Locus of Value

Spencer grounds his individualism in two converging arguments. The first is ethical: happiness, the ultimate criterion of value, is experienced by individuals, not by abstractions. "Society" does not feel pleasure or pain; only the individual members of society do. Therefore, any political evaluation that appeals to "the good of society" without tracing that good back to the welfare of specific individuals is either confused or dishonest.

The second argument is biological: the human organism is an individuated unit whose faculties require free exercise for their development and whose deterioration cannot be compensated by improvements elsewhere. Unlike the cells of a body, which genuinely exist for the sake of the organism, individual humans have their own centers of consciousness and their own claims to consideration.

### Individualism Versus Socialism

Spencer frames individualism in explicit opposition to socialism. He notes that "a league for the defense of liberty and property, largely consisting of Conservatives, has taken for its motto 'Individualism versus Socialism.'" This opposition, Spencer argues, is fundamental and irreconcilable. Socialism proposes that the state direct economic life and redistribute resources; individualism demands that each person retain the fruits of their labor and make their own economic decisions.

Spencer's specific contribution is to argue that the choice between individualism and socialism is not merely a matter of economic efficiency but of moral principle. Socialism, by substituting state direction for individual choice, destroys the conditions under which human character develops. Even if socialism were more efficient — a claim Spencer vigorously disputes — it would still be objectionable on the grounds that it reduces individuals to instruments of collective purposes.

### Individuality and Evolution

Spencer connects individualism to his broader evolutionary framework through the concept of "individuation" — the process by which organisms become more distinct, more autonomous, and more internally integrated over the course of evolution. "Individuation: life and morals, and dependence; social and individual; and evolution" — this progression, Spencer argues, is the master trend of both biological and social development.

In the social domain, individuation means the increasing differentiation of persons from one another: the development of distinct talents, preferences, and life-projects. A progressive society is one that maximizes the space for individuation; a regressive one suppresses individual differences in favor of uniformity imposed from above.

### The Limits of State Action

Spencer's individualism yields a clear criterion for evaluating state action: does it expand or contract the sphere within which individuals may exercise their own judgment? By this standard, Spencer argues, most modern legislation fails. Compulsory education, state-sponsored sanitation, factory regulation, poor relief — each of these, whatever its welfare rationale, displaces individual decision-making with state command, and therefore diminishes the scope of individuation.

Crucially, Spencer does not argue that the state should do nothing. He recognizes a legitimate sphere of state action — the protection of individual rights through the administration of justice and the enforcement of contracts. But this sphere is strictly bounded by the equal-freedom principle, and every extension beyond it must be scrutinized for its effects on individual liberty.

## Historical Context

Spencer's individualism developed in the context of nineteenth-century debates about the scope of state action. The rise of factory legislation, public health regulation, and compulsory education in Victorian Britain prompted a vigorous intellectual counter-movement — often called the "Individualist" school — of which Spencer was the most prominent representative. Figures such as Auberon Herbert, Thomas Mackay, and the Liberty and Property Defense League drew on Spencer's arguments to oppose the expansion of state power.

At the same time, Spencer was writing against the backdrop of the emerging socialist movement, which proposed a far more extensive role for the state in economic life. Spencer's insistence on "Individualism versus Socialism" was not merely rhetorical; it reflected his conviction that these two principles represented fundamentally incompatible visions of social organization — the one grounded in voluntary cooperation, the other in state compulsion.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

- [[liberalism]] — True liberalism, for Spencer, is the political expression of individualism: the protection of each person's sphere of free action against state encroachment.
- [[law-of-equal-freedom]] — The legal-institutional mechanism by which individual spheres of action are delineated and protected. Individualism supplies the moral foundation; equal freedom supplies the operational rule.
- [[socialism]] — The polar opposite of individualism in Spencer's framework. Socialism sacrifices the individual to the collective; individualism insists on the priority of each person's claims.
- [[rights]] — Natural rights are the juridical form of individualism: they specify what each individual is entitled to, independent of any collective decision.
