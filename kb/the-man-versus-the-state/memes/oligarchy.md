## Definition

**Oligarchy** is a political system in which governing authority is held by a small group of individuals, distinguished from monarchy (rule by one) and democracy (rule by the many). Herbert Spencer treated oligarchy as a recurring structural form in the evolution of political institutions. In *Social Statics*, he depicted Spartan society as one in which the Helots "were not more coerced by their Spartan masters than these in turn by their oligarchy" — a system of layered domination in which each stratum was subordinated to the one above. In *The Man Versus the State*, Spencer traced the Roman Republic to "a small oligarchy within a larger oligarchy: the members of each being unchecked autocrats," arguing that such a society was "by its intrinsic nature, more nearly allied to an ordinary despotism than to a society of citizens politically equal."

> The Roman Government was that of a small oligarchy within a larger oligarchy: the members of each being unchecked autocrats. A society in which the relatively few men who had political power, and were in a qualified sense free, were so many petty despots, holding not only slaves and dependents but even children in a bondage no less absolute than that in which they held their cattle, was, by its intrinsic nature, more nearly allied to an ordinary despotism than to a society of citizens politically equal.

## Key Arguments

**Oligarchy as institutionalised power-worship**. Spencer situated oligarchy within his analysis of the "sentiment of power-worship" — the psychological disposition to submit to authority that he regarded as characteristic of the militant type of society. Oligarchy extended this sentiment beyond the single figure of the despot to a broader class, but without fundamentally altering the relationship between ruler and ruled. Each member of the oligarchic class was, within his own sphere, a petty despot, "holding not only slaves and dependents but even children in a bondage no less absolute than that in which they held their cattle." The multiplication of despots did not dilute despotism's essential character — it merely distributed it.

**The chain of domination**. Spencer observed that oligarchic systems typically exhibited a hierarchical chain of domination: each stratum exercised unchecked authority over those below while submitting to those above. The feudal system exemplified this structure — "the people were bondsmen to the nobles, the nobles were vassals to their kings, their kings to the pope." In Russia at the time of Spencer's writing, "the aristocracy are dictated to by their emperor much as they themselves dictate to their serfs." Oligarchy thus perpetuated the psychology of command and submission throughout the social hierarchy, with each level reproducing the pattern of domination it experienced from above.

**Oligarchy and the ethic of status**. Like aristocracy — a closely related concept — oligarchy was, for Spencer, a social system governed by the ethic of status rather than the ethic of contract. An individual's rights, obligations, and life prospects were determined by membership in a class rather than by voluntary agreement; the law of equal freedom was violated not merely in particular cases but systemically, as the entire structure of society was organised around the principle of unequal standing. The transition from oligarchy to democracy represented a partial movement from status toward contract, but Spencer warned that even democratic institutions could reproduce oligarchic patterns if not constrained by the principle of equal freedom.

**Oligarchy and the classification of political forms**. Spencer's treatment of oligarchy intersected with his broader critique of how political measures are classified. In *The Man Versus the State*, he warned against "mistaken classings of political measures which have misled" liberalism — "classings, as we shall see, by conspicuous external traits instead of by internal natures." The external form of a government — whether it called itself a monarchy, an aristocracy, an oligarchy, or a democracy — was less important, in Spencer's analysis, than its internal nature: the extent to which it respected or violated the law of equal freedom. A democratic government that overstepped the proper sphere of government could be, in its internal nature, as oligarchic as any aristocratic regime.

**Oligarchy in modern disguise**. Spencer suggested that oligarchic structures could persist under formally democratic institutions. The "new Toryism" of legislative paternalism — in which a class of politicians and administrators exercised increasingly detailed control over citizens' lives — reproduced, in Spencer's view, the essential structure of oligarchic rule: a minority making decisions for a majority, with the law of equal freedom providing no effective constraint. The external form had changed from hereditary aristocracy to elected officialdom, but the internal nature — the unchecked authority of the few over the many — remained substantially the same.

## Historical Context

Spencer wrote against the backdrop of Britain's extended transition from aristocratic to democratic governance. The Reform Acts had progressively widened the franchise, but the House of Lords retained significant legislative power, and the civil service and officer corps remained dominated by the aristocratic class. Spencer's analysis of oligarchy was thus directed at both the residual power of the traditional aristocracy and the emerging power of a new administrative class. His warning that oligarchy could survive the formal democratisation of political institutions anticipated later critiques of bureaucratic rule and the "iron law of oligarchy" later formulated by Robert Michels.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

- **[[aristocracy]]**: A closely related concept — Spencer often used the terms in proximity, with aristocracy emphasising hereditary class rule and oligarchy emphasising the structural concentration of power in a small group irrespective of its basis.
- **[[despotism]]**: The extreme form of concentrated rule from which oligarchy represented a partial diffusion — yet Spencer argued that oligarchy was "more nearly allied to an ordinary despotism than to a society of citizens politically equal."
- **[[monarchy]]**: Rule by one, which Spencer treated as one terminus of the political-evolution spectrum, with oligarchy representing rule by the few as an intermediate stage.
- **[[democracy]]**: The form of government that oligarchy was historically succeeded by — though Spencer warned that democratic institutions could reproduce oligarchic relations if not constrained by the law of equal freedom.
- **[[toryism]]**: The ideology associated with the defence of aristocratic and oligarchic interests — Spencer's critique of the "new Toryism" of legislative paternalism extended the analysis of oligarchy to modern bureaucratic government.
- **[[militant-type]]**: The social form in which oligarchic concentration of power was the natural and functional political structure — oligarchy was the typical government of the militant type.
