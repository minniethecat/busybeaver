## Biography

Sir David Brewster (1781–1868) was a Scottish physicist, mathematician, and science administrator, best known for his contributions to optics and the invention of the kaleidoscope. He served as Principal of the University of Edinburgh and was a prominent figure in the British Association for the Advancement of Science, delivering a notable address at its Edinburgh meeting in 1850.

Brewster was a vigorous advocate for the public funding and institutional recognition of scientific research. His vision of science as a publicly supported enterprise drew the critical attention of Herbert Spencer, who saw in Brewster's proposals a dangerous precedent for the expansion of State authority over intellectual life.

## Key Ideas

### State-Funded "Priesthood of Science"

Brewster's most distinctive proposal — and the one that drew Spencer's sustained criticism — was his call for "men ordained by the State to the undivided functions of science," forming what he termed "an intellectual priesthood" whose mandate would be "to develop the glorious truths which time and space embosom." Spencer, in *Social Statics* (Chapter XXI, "The Limit of State-Duty"), invoked Brewster's scheme as the *reductio ad absurdum* of the principle that the State should superintend all aspects of social life. Spencer asked: if the State is to establish an intellectual priesthood alongside the religious one, what prevents it from also establishing a "priesthood of physic" or a system of government funerals?

### The Endowment of Research

In *The Man Versus the State* ("The New Toryism"), Spencer returned to Brewster's ideas decades later, warning that the growing enthusiasm for the "endowment of research" — then already funded at £4,000 annually through the Royal Society — would inevitably lead to "that paid 'priesthood of science' long ago advocated by Sir David Brewster." Spencer treated this as a case study in how seemingly modest state interventions in science expand into comprehensive systems of official control.

## Relationship to Spencer

Spencer cited Brewster not as an intellectual adversary to be refuted, but as a well-meaning reformer whose proposals illustrated the slippery slope of state intervention. Brewster's advocacy for publicly funded science was, in Spencer's analysis, one more instance of the widespread assumption that government should take responsibility for domains properly belonging to voluntary association and individual initiative.

## Major Works

- *Treatise on Optics* (1831) — Comprehensive survey of optical science
- *Letters on Natural Magic* (1832) — Popular science work addressed to Walter Scott
- *Address to the British Association at Edinburgh* (1850) — The speech in which Brewster advocated for state-ordained scientific priests
