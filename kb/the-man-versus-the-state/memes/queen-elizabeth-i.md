## Biography

Elizabeth I (1533–1603) was Queen of England and Ireland from 1558 until her death, the last monarch of the Tudor dynasty. Her forty-five-year reign, known as the Elizabethan era, was a period of English cultural flourishing — the age of Shakespeare, Spenser, and Bacon — and of England's emergence as a major maritime and colonial power.

Elizabeth's religious settlement of 1559 established a moderate Protestant Church of England, navigating between Catholic and Puritan extremes. Her foreign policy, marked by the defeat of the Spanish Armada in 1588, secured England's independence from Catholic Europe and laid the groundwork for the expansion of English naval power.

In the history of political thought, Elizabeth appears in Herbert Spencer's work not as a central figure but as an illustration of a broader pattern: the casual presumption by European monarchs of authority over lands and peoples of which they had no direct knowledge. Spencer cited Elizabeth's colonial charter to Sir Humphrey Gilbert alongside Pope Alexander VI's division of the New World between Spain and Portugal as examples of the "coolness" with which rulers arrogated to themselves rights they did not possess.

## Key Ideas

### The Presumption of Colonial Authority

In *The Man Versus the State*, Spencer used Elizabeth's grant to Gilbert as evidence of a recurring pattern in the history of state power: rulers claiming authority over matters far beyond their legitimate jurisdiction. Elizabeth's patent to Gilbert empowered him to "discover and take possession of remote and heathen countries" and to "exercise rights, royalties, and jurisdiction, in such countries and seas adjoining." For Spencer, this illustrated how state power, once granted the premise of unlimited authority, extends itself without regard to the actual consent or interests of those affected.

### Monarchy and the State Principle

Spencer's reference to Elizabeth was part of a broader argument about the continuity between monarchical and democratic forms of state power. The colonial presumption of Tudor monarchs, Spencer suggested, was not fundamentally different in kind from the regulatory presumption of Victorian parliaments: both assumed that a central authority could legitimately dispose of resources, rights, and lives far removed from its immediate knowledge or accountability.

### The Limits of Royal Prerogative

Elizabeth's reign was also a period of intense debate about the limits of royal authority — debates that would culminate in the English Civil War under her Stuart successors. While Spencer did not engage deeply with Elizabethan constitutional history, his citation of Elizabeth's colonial actions participates in a longer liberal tradition of questioning the scope of prerogative power, whether exercised by monarchs or by majorities.

## Major Works

Elizabeth I was not a systematic political thinker, but her speeches, letters, and official pronouncements have been studied for their political rhetoric:

- The Tilbury Speech (1588) — Elizabeth's address to troops assembled to resist the Spanish Armada
- The Golden Speech (1601) — Her final address to Parliament, reflecting on her reign
- Letters Patent to Sir Humphrey Gilbert (1578) — The colonial grant that Spencer cited as evidence of arbitrary state power

## Influence / Legacy

Elizabeth's legacy in political thought is dual. For mainstream historiography, she is celebrated as Gloriana — the queen who presided over England's golden age and established the foundations of British naval and imperial power. For critics of state power like Spencer, she exemplified the monarchical principle that would later be inherited and extended by democratic legislatures: the claim of centralized authority over distant lands and peoples.

Spencer's use of Elizabeth as a negative example — alongside Pope Alexander VI and Charles II — reflects the radical liberal tradition's suspicion not only of modern bureaucracy but of the deeper historical roots of state absolutism in monarchical prerogative.
