## Biography

Adam Smith (1723–1790) was a Scottish economist and moral philosopher, widely regarded as the founder of modern economics. Born in Kirkcaldy, Fife, he studied at the University of Glasgow under Francis Hutcheson and later at Balliol College, Oxford. He returned to Glasgow as professor of logic (1751) and then of moral philosophy (1752), where he delivered the lectures that would become his two great works.

Smith's first major publication, *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* (1759), established his reputation across Europe. In this work, Smith developed a theory of moral psychology grounded in the operation of sympathy — the capacity to imaginatively enter into the feelings of others and to judge conduct from the standpoint of an impartial spectator. The book went through six editions in Smith's lifetime.

*An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations* (1776) transformed economic thought by arguing that national prosperity depends not on the accumulation of gold but on the productivity of labour, the division of labour, and the freedom of exchange. Smith's critique of mercantilism — the doctrine that "money is wealth" — provided the intellectual foundation for free trade. Herbert Spencer drew upon both of Smith's major works: the theory of sympathy informs Spencer's account of the evolution of the moral sense, while Smith's political economy shapes Spencer's arguments against state intervention in economic life.

## Key Ideas

### The Theory of Moral Sentiments and Sympathy

Smith's moral philosophy is built upon the operation of sympathy. He argued that we judge the propriety of conduct by considering whether an impartial spectator would sympathize with the motives and feelings of the agent. When we witness suffering, we "derive sorrow from the sorrow of others" and are moved to relieve it; when we witness injustice, we feel indignation on behalf of the victim.

Spencer adopted Smith's theory as the foundation for his own evolutionary account of morality. In *Social Statics*, Spencer writes that "the way to a solution of this difficulty has been opened by Adam Smith in his Theory of Moral Sentiments." Smith's insight that moral judgment involves imaginatively placing oneself in the position of the impartial spectator is, in Spencer's hands, transformed into a naturalistic explanation: the moral sense is not a supernatural gift but an evolved instinct, rooted in the capacity for sympathy that natural selection has progressively cultivated in social animals.

Spencer extended Smith's theory beyond what Smith himself had recognized. If sympathy is the foundation of morality, Spencer argued, then it has one "most important application" that Smith overlooked: it provides the ultimate justification for the law of equal freedom. Our sympathetic aversion to the suffering caused by aggression and coercion is the psychological basis of the perception of individual rights.

### The Critique of Mercantilism

Smith's *Wealth of Nations* is, in large part, a sustained critique of the mercantilist system — the network of tariffs, monopolies, navigation acts, and colonial restrictions by which European states sought to accumulate precious metals. Smith demonstrated that "money is wealth" was a fundamental error: real national wealth consists in the annual produce of land and labour, not in the stock of gold and silver.

Spencer repeatedly invokes Smith's critique of mercantilism in his own attacks on protectionist and interventionist legislation. In *The Man Versus the State*, Spencer draws an explicit parallel: just as Smith exposed the fallacy that governments can create prosperity by restricting trade, so Spencer exposes the fallacy that governments can remedy social ills by multiplying regulations. "Money is wealth" was the dogma of legislators before Adam Smith; "Acts of Parliament can cure all evils" is, Spencer implies, the equivalent dogma of the modern era.

### The Division of Labour

Smith's analysis of the division of labour — illustrated by his famous example of the pin factory — showed how the specialization of tasks dramatically increases productivity. This principle became central to Spencer's evolutionary sociology. In *Principles of Sociology*, Spencer argued that social evolution proceeds from the simple to the complex through the progressive differentiation of functions — a generalization of Smith's economic insight to the entire social organism.

## Major Works

- **The Theory of Moral Sentiments** (1759) — Smith's moral philosophy, developing the theory of sympathy and the impartial spectator. Spencer adopted this theory as the naturalistic foundation of his own ethical system.
- **An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations** (1776) — The founding text of classical economics, arguing for free trade, the division of labour, and limited government. Spencer drew on Smith's critique of mercantilism in his own critique of state intervention.
- **Lectures on Jurisprudence** (published posthumously) — Smith's lectures at Glasgow, covering the theory of law and government.

## Relationship to Other Figures

- [[herbert-spencer]] — Spencer adopted Smith's theory of sympathy from *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* as the foundation of his evolutionary ethics. He also drew on Smith's political economy in his arguments for laissez-faire.
- [[david-ricardo]] — Ricardo extended Smith's economic analysis, particularly the theory of rent and comparative advantage. Spencer was influenced by the classical political economy tradition that Smith founded.
- [[thomas-malthus]] — Malthus's principle of population provided one of the key mechanisms for both Darwin and Spencer's evolutionary theories.
- [[edmund-burke]] — Burke, Smith's contemporary, shared his scepticism of state intervention and his respect for the spontaneous order of society.
