## Background

The Reform Act of 1832 emerged from decades of mounting pressure for parliamentary reform in Britain. The existing electoral system was grossly unrepresentative: "rotten boroughs" with handfuls of voters returned MPs while rapidly growing industrial cities like Manchester and Birmingham had no direct representation. The Industrial Revolution had transformed the demographic landscape, but the political structure remained frozen in a pre-industrial configuration.

The French Revolution of 1789 and the Napoleonic Wars had initially suppressed reform agitation, as the British establishment feared any concession would unleash revolutionary forces. However, the post-war period saw renewed calls for change, fueled by economic distress, the Peterloo Massacre of 1819, and the growing political consciousness of the middle classes. The Whig Party, led by Earl Grey, championed reform as both a matter of justice and a safeguard against more radical demands.

## Description

The Representation of the People Act 1832 (2 & 3 Will. IV, c. 45) was introduced by Lord John Russell and steered through Parliament by Prime Minister Earl Grey. After two rejections by the House of Lords, the government resigned and was reinstated following the refusal of the Duke of Wellington to form an alternative administration. Facing the threat of King William IV creating sufficient Whig peers to force the bill through, the Lords ultimately passed the measure on 7 June 1832.

The Act made three principal changes. First, it disenfranchised 56 "rotten boroughs" with populations under 2,000 and reduced the representation of 31 others. Second, it created 67 new constituencies, granting representation to the growing industrial towns and cities. Third, it standardized and expanded the county franchise to include tenants-at-will paying an annual rent of £50, while the borough franchise was extended to male householders occupying property worth £10 per year. The total electorate increased from approximately 435,000 to 652,000 — roughly one in five adult males.

## Significance / Impact

The Reform Act marked a decisive shift in British constitutional development. It established the principle that parliamentary representation should reflect population rather than property or historical privilege, and demonstrated that the constitution could be reformed peacefully through legislation rather than revolution.

Spencer situates the Reform Act within the broader arc of Whig achievement in reducing coercive rule. In *The Man Versus the State*, he writes that the Reform Bill narrowed "the political serfdom of the unrepresented" and that "alike generally and locally, the many were less under the coercion of the few." For Spencer, the Reform Act exemplified the Whig principle of replacing compulsory co-operation with voluntary co-operation — the defining axis of political evolution from militancy toward industrialism.

However, Spencer also recognized that the Reform Act was an early, incomplete step. The franchise remained property-based and excluded the working classes, setting the stage for Chartism and the subsequent Reform Acts of 1867 and 1884. The Act's limitations illustrated Spencer's broader thesis that political freedom expands only as fast as the moral sense of the population develops.

> "Under growing Whig influence there came repeal of the laws forbidding combinations among artisans as well as of those which interfered with their freedom of travelling... The political serfdom of the unrepresented was narrowed in area, both by the Reform Bill and the Municipal Reform Bill; so that alike generally and locally, the many were less under the coercion of the few." — *The Man Versus the State*, Chapter: The New Toryism

> "Of Social Statics... Reform Bills, Chartism, &c., and consider how through all of them there runs a kindred spirit, and how this spirit is manifesting itself with constantly-increasing intensity and universality, we shall see that these facts imply some moral change; and explicable as they are by the growth of this compound faculty responding to the law of equal freedom, it is reasonable to consider them as showing the mode in which such faculty seeks to place social arrangements in harmony with that law." — *Social Statics*, Chapter: The Right to Ignore the State
