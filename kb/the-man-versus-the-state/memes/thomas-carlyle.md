## Biography

Thomas Carlyle (1795–1881) was a Scottish historian, social critic, and essayist, one of the most commanding literary figures of the Victorian era. Born in Ecclefechan, Dumfriesshire, to a strict Calvinist family, Carlyle studied at the University of Edinburgh with the intention of entering the ministry, but abandoned this path after a protracted spiritual crisis. His early work consisted of translations from German, notably Goethe's *Wilhelm Meister*, and essays for the *Edinburgh Review* introducing German Romantic thought to British readers.

Carlyle rose to national prominence with *The French Revolution: A History* (1837), a work of extraordinary narrative power that treated history as a drama of great personalities rather than impersonal forces. His doctrine of hero-worship, elaborated in *On Heroes, Hero-Worship, and the Heroic in History* (1841), held that "the history of what man has accomplished in this world is at bottom the History of the Great Men who have worked here." This "great man" theory of history became Carlyle's most enduring — and most contested — intellectual legacy.

Carlyle's later writings grew increasingly authoritarian. In *Latter-Day Pamphlets* (1850) and *Shooting Niagara: And After?* (1867), he denounced parliamentary democracy, advocated strong-armed government, and proposed coercive solutions to social problems. It is this Carlyle — the Carlyle of the drilled regiments and the worship of force — that Herbert Spencer engages most directly in *The Man Versus the State* and *Social Statics*, treating him as an exemplar of the authoritarian impulse that Spencer's liberalism was designed to resist.

## Key Ideas

### The Great Man Theory of History

Carlyle's most famous doctrine holds that history is driven by the actions of exceptional individuals — heroes, prophets, kings, and poets — rather than by economic forces, social structures, or the gradual evolution of institutions. "The history of the world," Carlyle wrote, "is but the biography of great men." Spencer directly challenged this doctrine, asking his readers to "ponder on the doings of all the great men by whom, according to the Carlylean theory, society is framed," and to ask whether those great men had not themselves been shaped by the social conditions they supposedly directed. Spencer's evolutionary sociology inverts the Carlylean picture: great men are products of their age, not its producers.

### Coercive Solutions to Social Problems

Carlyle's response to the social distress of the 1840s — particularly the Irish Famine — was characteristically authoritarian. Spencer cites Carlyle as an example of the tendency to reach for force when faced with suffering: "shall we adopt the analogous suggestion of Mr. Carlyle, who would remedy the distresses of Ireland by organizing its people into drilled regiments of diggers?" For Spencer, Carlyle's proposal to organize the Irish poor into labour battalions under military discipline represented the purest form of the coercive impulse — the assumption that social problems can be solved by the forcible organization of human beings. Spencer contrasted this approach with the voluntary cooperation characteristic of industrial society.

### The Shell and the Kernel: Carlyle's Calvinism

In one of his most penetrating observations, Spencer identifies Carlyle as a case study in the persistence of theological habits of mind after the abandonment of theological belief. In his student days, Carlyle "giving up, as he thought, the creed of his fathers, rejected its shell only, keeping the contents; and was proved by his conceptions of the world, and man, and conduct, to be still among the sternest of Scotch Calvinists." Carlyle's worldview — his belief in a providential order, his reverence for authority, his grim moral seriousness, his conviction that suffering is a necessary discipline — remained Calvinist in substance even after he had discarded the Calvinist creed. Spencer uses this observation to argue that the form of one's beliefs may change while the underlying mental disposition — the "shell" discarded, the "contents" retained — persists unchanged.

### The Critique of Democracy and the Worship of Force

Carlyle's later political writings became increasingly contemptuous of democratic institutions. He mocked parliamentary government as "the national palaver," dismissed the extension of the franchise, and called for a return to strong, even despotic, leadership. Spencer found in Carlyle's later writings the clearest expression of the militant type of social organization that industrial society was supposedly overcoming. Far from being a reactionary eccentric, Carlyle was, in Spencer's view, the symptom of a deeper failure of liberal thought — the failure to articulate a principled, rather than merely expedient, defence of individual liberty.

## Major Works

- **The French Revolution: A History** (1837) — Carlyle's breakthrough work, a dramatic narrative history that established his literary reputation and exemplified his "great man" approach to historical causation.
- **On Heroes, Hero-Worship, and the Heroic in History** (1841) — The canonical statement of Carlyle's great man theory, presenting six types of hero: divinity, prophet, poet, priest, man of letters, and king.
- **Past and Present** (1843) — A critique of industrial England, contrasting the spiritual discipline of a medieval monastery with the "cash nexus" of modern society.
- **Latter-Day Pamphlets** (1850) — A series of polemical essays on contemporary issues, notable for their increasingly authoritarian tone and contempt for parliamentary democracy.
- **History of Friedrich II of Prussia, Called Frederick the Great** (1858–1865) — Carlyle's massive biography of Frederick the Great, the ultimate expression of his hero-worship and his admiration for strong, even ruthless, leadership.

## Relationship to Other Figures

- [[herbert-spencer]] — Spencer cites Carlyle's proposal to organize the Irish into "drilled regiments of diggers" as an example of coercive social thinking, and diagnoses Carlyle's residual Calvinism as evidence that abandoning a creed's form does not entail abandoning its substance.
- [[john-stuart-mill]] — Mill was an early admirer and friend of Carlyle; Mill's *On Liberty* (1859) can be read, in part, as a response to the authoritarian Carlylean challenge that Mill had once entertained.
- [[edmund-burke]] — Like Carlyle, Burke emphasized the organic growth of institutions and the dangers of rationalist reform, but Burke's conservatism was constitutional and parliamentary, not authoritarian and force-worshipping.
- [[thomas-hobbes]] — Hobbes, like Carlyle, reached for sovereign power as the solution to social disorder, though Hobbes's argument was rational-contractarian while Carlyle's was mystical-heroic. Spencer's critique of both figures follows a common pattern.
