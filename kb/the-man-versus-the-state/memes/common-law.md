## Definition

The **common law** is the body of legal rules and principles developed through judicial decisions and customary practice rather than through legislative statutes. For Herbert Spencer, the common law was of profound theoretical importance: it demonstrated that binding norms of conduct — including the recognition of individual rights — exist prior to, and independently of, the state. In *The Man Versus the State* (1884), Spencer appealed to the common law tradition to refute the Benthamite claim that rights are creations of legislation, arguing instead that the common law "is mainly an embodiment of 'the customs of the realm'" and that it "did but give definite shape to that which it found existing."

This argument was central to Spencer's broader case against the expansion of state power. If rights are natural and pre-political, merely recognized rather than created by law, then legislation that overrides or extinguishes those rights is not an exercise of sovereign authority but an act of usurpation.

> Bentham and his followers seem to have forgotten that our own common law is mainly an embodiment of "the customs of the realm." It did but give definite shape to that which it found existing.

## Key Arguments

Spencer's appeal to the common law serves several argumentative functions in his critique of legislative expansion.

First, **the priority of custom over legislation**. Spencer marshaled extensive anthropological evidence — from the Snake Indians' recognition of private property in horses to the Arafuras' respect for property rights "in the fullest sense of the word" without any political authority — to establish that norms of justice, property, and contract are not legislative inventions but spontaneous products of social life. The common law tradition, in this view, institutionalizes the recognition that just rules are discovered, not decreed.

Second, **common law as an evolving embodiment of natural justice**. Spencer cited the legal historian Sir Frederick Pollock, who noted that "so early as the fifteenth century, we find a common-law judge declaring that, as in a case unprovided for by known rules the civilians and canonists devise a new rule according to 'the law of nature which is the ground of all laws,' the Courts of Westminster can and will do the like." The common law thus has an immanent rationality: it evolves through the resolution of particular disputes, guided by a conception of natural justice, rather than through the imposition of a priori legislative schemes.

Third, **Equity as recognition of pre-legal rights**. Spencer pointed to the system of Equity — developed to remedy the rigidities of the common law — as further evidence that the law recognizes rights existing apart from legal warrant: "our system of Equity, introduced and developed as it was to make up for the shortcomings of Common-law, or rectify its inequities, proceeded throughout on a recognition of men's claims considered as existing apart from legal warrant." When Parliament gave married women the right to their own earnings, it did not create the right, but finally recognized it: "The reformed law did not create the right, but recognition of the right created the reformed law."

Fourth, **common law as a bulwark against arbitrary power**. The common law tradition, with its emphasis on precedent, procedural regularity, and the accumulated wisdom of generations, stands in contrast to the legislative will that Spencer saw sweeping through late Victorian Britain. Where legislation is an expression of current majority will — which may be uninformed, capricious, or tyrannical — the common law embodies the slow growth of rules tested by experience.

## Historical Context

The common law tradition that Spencer invoked was the product of centuries of English legal development, from the consolidation of royal courts under Henry II in the twelfth century to the systematizing work of Sir Edward Coke in the seventeenth. Coke's assertion in *Dr. Bonham's Case* (1610) that "when an Act of Parliament is against common right and reason, or repugnant, or impossible to be performed, the common law will control it, and adjudge such Act to be void" represented the high-water mark of common law supremacy — a doctrine that was later superseded by the principle of parliamentary sovereignty.

Spencer's invocation of common law must be understood against the background of Benthamite legal positivism, which had become the dominant jurisprudence of nineteenth-century British reformers. Bentham famously dismissed natural rights as "nonsense upon stilts" and argued that all rights are the creations of the sovereign legislator. Spencer saw this positivism as the intellectual foundation of the legislative expansion he opposed, and he sought to counter it by reviving the older common-law understanding of rights as grounded in custom and nature rather than in sovereign command.

## Critiques and Counterarguments

Spencer's appeal to common law has been subject to several criticisms. First, the common law has historically recognized extensive state authority, including the royal prerogative, and cannot be straightforwardly equated with limited government or individual rights.

Second, the evolutionary character of common law — its gradual adaptation to changing circumstances — is not fundamentally different from the evolutionary character of legislation, which also develops through precedent, amendment, and judicial interpretation. Spencer's sharp opposition between "organic" common law and "artificial" legislation may overstate the difference.

Third, the claim that common law merely "recognizes" pre-existing rights begs the question of which customs are elevated to the status of law. The common law has recognized slavery, coverture (the legal subordination of married women), and various forms of hierarchical privilege — all of which Spencer would reject as violations of natural rights.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

- **[[natural-rights]]**: The pre-political rights that Spencer argues the common law recognizes rather than creates — the philosophical foundation of his legal theory.
- **[[positive-law]]**: The legislatively-enacted law that Spencer contrasts with the evolutionary, precedent-based common law, associating the former with Benthamite positivism and the expansion of state power.
- **[[law-of-nature]]**: The broader jurisprudential concept that common-law judges historically invoked as the ground of their decisions when positive law was silent.
- **[[expediency]]**: The political logic that Spencer saw as driving legislative expansion at the expense of common-law principles grounded in enduring conceptions of justice.
- **[[individualism]]**: The social philosophy consistent with the common-law tradition, emphasizing the priority of individual rights over collective purposes.
