## Definition

The **divine right of kings** is the political doctrine that a monarch's authority derives directly from divine will, rendering the sovereign beyond earthly accountability. Herbert Spencer, in *Social Statics* (1851) and *The Man Versus the State* (1884), treats this doctrine not merely as a historical curiosity but as the paradigmatic instance of what he calls "political superstition" — the unwarranted attribution of unlimited authority to governing power.

Spencer argues that the divine right of kings was logically consistent given its theological premises: if the king is God's deputy, then unlimited obedience follows necessarily. The problem, for Spencer, is that modern democracies have abandoned the theological premise while retaining the conclusion of unlimited governmental authority. "The oil of anointing," he writes, "seems unawares to have dripped from the head of the one on to the heads of the many."

## Key Arguments

Spencer's analysis of divine right proceeds through three stages. First, he traces the **logical structure** of the doctrine. If one accepts — as did Sir Robert Filmer in *Patriarcha* (1680) — that "men are not naturally free," then rights can only be granted by a divine sovereign. The inference to divine right "is an easy step." Spencer observes that this reasoning contains its own refutation: since no earthly ruler can demonstrate genuine divine appointment, "divine right of kings means the divine right of any one who can get uppermost."

> He who asserts that the law of equal freedom is not true, that is, he who asserts that men have not equal rights, has two alternatives. He may either say that men have no rights at all, or that they have unequal rights... Foremost of those who deny rights altogether, stands that same Sir Robert Filmer already named, with his dogma that "men are not naturally free." Starting thus, he readily finds his way to the conclusion that the only proper form of government is an absolute monarchy.

Second, Spencer identifies the **historical correlation** between divine right belief and social violence. In ages of chronic warfare, societies needed rapid, unquestioning obedience to centralized command. The divine right doctrine provided the ideological warrant for this functional requirement. As societies became more peaceful and industrial, the doctrine weakened — but Spencer argues it did not disappear; it merely **migrated**.

Third, Spencer traces the **migration of divine right** from monarch to parliament. The modern form of the superstition is "the divine right of parliaments" — or, equivalently, "the divine right of majorities." Democratic legislatures, no less than absolute monarchs, claim unlimited authority without supernatural warrant.

> The great political superstition of the past was the divine right of kings. The great political superstition of the present is the divine right of parliaments. The oil of anointing seems unawares to have dripped from the head of the one on to the heads of the many, and given sacredness to them also and to their decrees.

Spencer argues that this modern version is actually *less* consistent than the old one. The old doctrine had a clear premise (divine appointment) leading to a clear conclusion (unlimited obedience). The new doctrine retains the conclusion while abandoning the premise — "the deputy-godhood of which nobody asserts."

## Historical Context

The divine right of kings reached its zenith in seventeenth-century Europe, defended by theorists such as Filmer, Bossuet (who taught that kings "are gods, and share in a manner the Divine independence"), and Hobbes. It was challenged by Locke's *Two Treatises of Government* (1689) and the Whig tradition of limited government. Spencer writes from the aftermath of this debate, but his distinctive contribution is to argue that the Whig victory was incomplete: the divine right was transferred, not abolished.

Spencer's critique of parliamentary absolutism was shaped by the Victorian expansion of legislation. Between 1830 and 1880, the British Parliament passed an unprecedented volume of regulatory statutes — factory acts, public health acts, education acts — which Spencer saw as the reassertion of unlimited governmental authority under democratic auspices.
