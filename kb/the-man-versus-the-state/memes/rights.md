## Definition

Rights, in Herbert Spencer's political philosophy, are not grants from government or products of social convention. They are **necessary conditions of human flourishing** deduced from the law of equal freedom. Each individual has a right to exercise his faculties to the fullest extent compatible with the like exercise of faculties by all others.

Spencer's theory of rights is distinctive in its rejection of both utilitarian and contractarian foundations. Against Bentham, he denies that rights are creations of law — "nonsense upon stilts," as Bentham famously called natural rights. Against Hobbes, he denies that rights originate in a hypothetical social contract. For Spencer, rights are grounded in the constitution of human nature and the logical preconditions of social life.

## Derivation from Equal Freedom

The law of equal freedom — "every man has freedom to do all that he wills, provided he infringes not the equal freedom of any other man" — is the parent principle from which all specific rights descend. Rights are not a list of arbitrary claims but the particular applications of this general law to distinct domains of human action.

The right to **life** follows from the fact that all other freedoms presuppose existence. The right to **liberty** follows from the requirement that faculties be exercised without impediment from others. The right to **property** follows from the fact that action upon material objects — taking, transforming, consuming — is inseparable from the exercise of faculties.

Each right, then, is a logical corollary rather than an independent postulate.

## The Instinct of Rights

Spencer identifies a psychological foundation for rights in what he terms the "instinct of rights" — a natural impulse in each person to resist encroachment on his sphere of action. This instinct is "entirely selfish" in its primary operation, impelling its possessor to maintain only his own rights.

The extension to respecting the rights of others requires what Spencer calls "sympathetic excitement" of the instinct. Through sympathy — the capacity to feel what others feel — the instinct of rights is generalized from self-regarding to other-regarding. We perceive injury to another's rights as a form of injury to rights in general, and we react with indignation.

This explains why narratives of political oppression excite moral outrage in people far removed from the events. The instinct of rights, awakened sympathetically, generates a sense of injustice even when one's own rights are not directly threatened.

## Historical Affirmations

Spencer documents the near-universal recognition of equal rights across political traditions, arguing that this very universality confirms its grounding in human nature. The Levellers during the English Civil War affirmed that "all men are equal by nature, and the use of the same faculties, should also be equal one amongst another without subordination or subjection."

The American Declaration of Independence declared "all men have equal rights to life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness" as a self-evident truth. Hooker and Blackstone articulated similar principles in the English legal tradition. Spencer interprets this cross-cultural convergence not as coincidence but as evidence that the moral sense, however imperfectly, intuits the law of equal freedom.

## Rights vs. Expediency

Spencer's theory of rights stands in direct opposition to the utilitarian reduction of rights to useful fictions. For Bentham, rights are created by law for the purpose of maximizing utility; they have no independent moral standing. Spencer regards this as a "plexus of absurdities" — a circular argument in which the sovereign people create a government that creates rights and confers them back on the people who created the government.

Spencer's alternative is that rights are logically prior to government. Government does not *create* rights; it exists to *protect* pre-existing rights. The state's sole legitimate function is the administration of justice — the enforcement of rights against violation. Any exercise of state power beyond this sphere is not the protection but the violation of rights.
