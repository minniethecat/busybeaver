## Definition

**Democracy**, in Herbert Spencer's political vocabulary, refers to a system of government in which political authority derives from the consent of the governed and decisions are made through representative institutions accountable to the population. Spencer occupied a distinctive position in Victorian political thought: he was neither an uncritical enthusiast for democracy nor an aristocratic opponent of it. He regarded democratic institutions as an essential safeguard against the abuse of state power — a "check upon the governing agency" — while simultaneously warning that democracy carried its own characteristic dangers, particularly the tendency of electoral majorities to use the state apparatus for their own economic advantage at the expense of minorities.

> The assumption, by a delegated assembly, of the power to lengthen its own existence from three years to seven, is an infraction of the representative principle.

Spencer's treatment of democracy must be understood within his broader theory of the state as a necessarily limited institution whose proper function is the protection of rights, not the pursuit of collective happiness through majoritarian legislation.

## Key Arguments

**Democracy as a check, not a source of authority**. Spencer's most distinctive contribution to democratic theory was his insistence that representative institutions derive their legitimacy not from the fact that they express the popular will, but from the fact that they constrain the state. The franchise is valuable because it prevents government from becoming an instrument of class oppression — not because "the voice of the people is the voice of God." Democratic majorities possess no inherent moral authority to override individual rights.

> The function of a representative body is not to express the will of the people — for the people have no single will — but to prevent the governing agency from encroaching upon individual liberty.

**The problem of majority tyranny**. Writing before John Stuart Mill's *On Liberty* (1859) popularized the phrase "tyranny of the majority," Spencer had already identified the danger that democratic institutions could be captured by electoral coalitions seeking to use state power for their own benefit. The Poor Laws, trade protections, and various forms of class legislation demonstrated, in his view, how majorities could employ the apparatus of the state to transfer resources from minorities to themselves — all while claiming the legitimacy of democratic mandate.

**The competence problem**. Spencer expressed deep scepticism about the capacity of the average voter to assess complex questions of legislation. This was not merely elitism but a structural argument: the division of labour that characterized modern industrial society meant that no individual could possibly master more than a small fraction of the knowledge required for intelligent governance. The voter who could competently judge a candidate's fitness on one question was necessarily ignorant on a hundred others.

> The enfranchised labourer, however intelligent in his own craft, is not thereby qualified to judge of the measures which a government should adopt for the regulation of commerce, the administration of justice, or the management of colonial affairs.

**The connection to character**. In keeping with his broader philosophy, Spencer insisted that the quality of democratic institutions depended on the moral character of the population. Where the people were corrupt, democratic forms would produce corrupt outcomes. Where individuals lacked the habits of self-restraint and respect for the rights of others, no constitutional mechanism could prevent the abuse of power.

**The liberal critique of democratic expansion**. Spencer documented with alarm the tendency of nineteenth-century British governments — both Whig and Tory — to expand the regulatory reach of the state while claiming democratic legitimacy. The Reform Bills, which extended the franchise, were accompanied by an explosion of legislation that restricted individual freedom in ever more domains. Spencer regarded this combination — broader democracy plus broader state power — as a dangerous trajectory toward what he called "the coming slavery."

## Historical Context

Spencer wrote during the era of the great Reform Bills (1832, 1867) and witnessed the progressive extension of the franchise in Britain. His political formation occurred in the aftermath of the French Revolution, when the question of popular sovereignty was the central controversy of European politics. The Chartist movement, the debates over universal manhood suffrage, and the expanding administrative state all formed the backdrop against which Spencer developed his distinctive synthesis of democratic scepticism and liberal individualism.

Spencer's position placed him at odds with both the conservative defenders of aristocratic privilege and the radical champions of unlimited popular sovereignty. Like the Benthamite Radicals, he favoured the extension of the franchise as a check on class legislation; unlike them, he rejected the utilitarian justification that democracy would maximize aggregate happiness.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

- **[[representative-government]]**: The institutional form through which democratic principles are implemented — which Spencer distinguished from direct democracy and subjected to his own critique of delegation and accountability.
- **[[rights]]**: The sphere of individual liberty that democratic majorities have no legitimate authority to invade.
- **[[majority-rule]]**: The decision-making principle characteristic of democracy, which Spencer regarded as a practical necessity but not a source of moral legitimacy.
- **[[tyranny]]**: The danger Spencer warned against — the use of democratic forms to legitimize the oppression of minorities by majorities.
- **[[despotism]]**: The pole opposite to democracy, in which a single ruler exercises arbitrary power — though Spencer warned that democratic despotism was equally possible and equally dangerous.
