## Definition

Property is the right of exclusive possession, use, and disposal of goods — both material (land, chattels) and intellectual (ideas, inventions). In Herbert Spencer's political philosophy, property rights are not creations of the state but direct deductions from the law of equal freedom. Spencer treats property as a fundamental right that the state exists to protect, not to create.

## Spencer's Derivation of Property Rights

Spencer rejects the Lockean labour-mixing theory of property. The Lockean argument assumes what it must prove: that one has a right to mix one's labour with resources that, by hypothesis, belong to all mankind in common.

The real question is whether labour gives a right greater than "the pre-existing rights of all other men put together." Instead, Spencer derives property from the law of equal freedom. Each person is free to claim all gratifications obtainable within the limits set by the equal freedoms of others.

As Spencer states: "When we assert the entire liberty of each, bounded only by the like liberties of all, we assert that each is free to claim for himself all those gratifications ... attainable by him within those limits." Those who obtain more through greater strength or ingenuity — without trespassing on equal freedoms — have an exclusive moral right to those acquisitions.

## Critique of Socialism

Spencer subjects socialism to systematic criticism. Equal division of produce irrespective of contribution violates equal freedom by giving some a claim over the fruits of others' labour.

Proportional division based on contribution, while "abstractedly just," is impracticable without market mechanisms to determine relative contributions.

Spencer anticipates the logical endpoint: if all property is robbery, a person has no right even to the food they consume — and therefore no property in their own body. He concludes that until "Communism can be carried to that extent, it will be best to stand by the old doctrine."

## Property in Ideas

Spencer extends property rights to intellectual creations. The produce of the brain is "equally valid" with the produce of the hands. Treating patents as mere "privileges" rather than natural rights reflects "a thick-skinned perception of justice."

The inventor's mental toil, costly experiments, and patient perseverance represent "accumulated labour" no different from the capitalist's savings. Spencer acknowledges one qualification: simultaneous independent discovery means intellectual property cannot be perpetual — though determining the precise term is difficult.

## The Benthamite Fallacy

Spencer rejects Bentham's doctrine that government creates rights and confers them on individuals. If the sovereign people create a government that creates rights, then the people create an agent to create rights and confer them back on themselves — "a marvellous piece of political legerdemain."

Matthew Arnold's claim that "property is the creation of law" is met with Spencer's retort: the truly shadowy metaphysical phantom is "this which supposes a thing to be obtained by creating an agent, which creates the thing, and then confers the thing on its own creator." Pre-existing rights create the need for government, not the reverse.
