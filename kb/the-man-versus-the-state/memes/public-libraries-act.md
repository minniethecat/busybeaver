## Background

The Public Libraries Act was introduced by William Ewart MP and passed by Parliament in 1850. It empowered municipal boroughs with populations over 10,000 to levy a halfpenny rate to fund the establishment and maintenance of free public libraries. The act was permissive rather than compulsory — it could only be adopted in a borough that approved it by a two-thirds majority of ratepayers in a public vote. Despite this democratic safeguard, the act represented a significant departure from the traditional principle that cultural and educational provision was a matter of private initiative and voluntary association.

The libraries movement drew on utilitarian arguments that accessible reading would improve public morality and social conditions. It also reflected the post-1832 Reform Act political landscape, in which the newly enfranchised middle classes increasingly looked to local government as an instrument of social improvement. By the 1860s and 1870s, the principle of rate-supported libraries had gained wide acceptance, and further legislation extended its scope.

## Description

The 1850 Act permitted town councils to adopt the legislation by local referendum, after which they could levy a rate of up to one halfpenny in the pound to fund library buildings, staff, and collections. The act was amended and extended by subsequent legislation, most notably in 1855 (extending it to parishes and raising the rate limit to one penny) and in 1866 (removing the population limit).

Spencer encountered the Public Libraries Act in the context of his comprehensive survey of expanding British legislation in "The New Toryism." He catalogued it among dozens of acts passed under Palmerston, Russell, and Gladstone that progressively extended the scope of government regulation. He described it with the same ironic tone he used for licensing acts and cattle-shed regulations:

> "Under the Ministry of Lord John Russell, in 1866, have to be named... a Public Libraries Act, giving local powers by which a majority can tax a minority for their books."

By the 1880s, the act had become a cultural touchstone for the normalization of state provision. Spencer observed the social pressure to conform:

> "A town which does not adopt the Free Libraries Act is sneered at as being alarmed by a measure so moderately communistic."

## Significance / Impact

Spencer's treatment of the Public Libraries Act is significant because it illustrates his method of treating seemingly innocuous pieces of legislation as symptoms of a deeper structural transformation. The act was not, in itself, the most oppressive piece of legislation he catalogued — it was permissive, local, and subject to democratic approval. Yet it was precisely this apparent mildness that made it, in Spencer's analysis, the more dangerous: it accustomed the public to the proposition that the state could legitimately tax one group to provide cultural goods to another.

The act exemplified what Spencer called "that increasing need for administrative compulsions and restraints, which results from the unforeseen evils and shortcomings of preceding compulsions and restraints." Each new extension of state provision — public libraries, board schools, free museums, baths and washhouses — normalized the principle that government should meet social needs, creating constituencies that would demand further extensions.

Spencer's most fundamental objection to the act appears in his argument that such legislation violated the "vital principle alike of individual life and of social life":

> "A government which takes fractions of the incomes of multitudinous people, for the purpose of... making public libraries and public museums, &c., takes for granted that, not only proximately but ultimately, increased general happiness will result from transgressing the essential requirement to general happiness — the requirement that each shall enjoy all those means to happiness which his actions, carried on without aggression, bring him."
