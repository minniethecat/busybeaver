## Definition

**Communism**, in Herbert Spencer's usage, referred to the doctrine that the community as a whole should retain ownership of the land and the means of production, cultivating them by "joint-stock agency" and sharing the produce among all members. In *Social Statics* (1851), Spencer treated communism and socialism as largely synonymous, designating both as schemes in which "the public, instead of letting out the land to individual members of their body, shall retain it in their own hands; cultivate it by joint-stock agency; and share the produce." Spencer's critique was distinctive in grounding itself not on pragmatic or economic grounds alone, but on the moral principle of equal freedom — the foundational axiom of his entire political philosophy.

> Socialism. The doctrine that all men have equal rights to the use of the Earth, seems at first sight, to countenance a species of social organization, at variance with that from which the right of property has just been deduced; an organization, namely, in which the public, instead of letting out the land to individual members of their body, shall retain it in their own hands; cultivate it by joint-stock agency; and share the produce: in fact, what is usually termed Socialism or Communism.

Spencer acknowledged that the communistic scheme appeared "plausible" at first sight. However, he argued that of the two forms in which it could be presented — equal distribution irrespective of labour contributed, or distribution proportional to labour — "the one is ethically imperfect, and the other, although correct in theory, is impracticable." This double-edged critique, rooted in the law of equal freedom, formed the core of Spencer's rejection of communist social organisation.

## Key Arguments

**The ethical failure of equal shares**. Spencer's primary objection was that awarding every man an equal portion of the earth's produce, "irrespective of the amount or quality of the labour he has contributed," commits a breach of equity. The law of equal freedom requires not that all shall have "like shares of the things which minister to the gratification of the faculties, but that all shall have like freedoms to pursue those things — shall have like scope." Spencer drew a sharp distinction between equality of opportunity (which the law of equal freedom guarantees) and equality of outcome (which communism demands), arguing that the latter violates the former. To give each person an opportunity to acquire objects is one thing; to give the objects themselves, regardless of effort, is "quite a different thing."

**The property-in-self paradox**. Spencer deployed a *reductio ad absurdum* against communist property theory. If, as some communists argued, no person can acquire a right to land or its products through labour, then the same logic applies to the food a person consumes. Following the argument to its conclusion, Spencer wrote: "as the whole of his bones, muscles, skin, &c., have been thus built up from nutriment not belonging to him, a man has no property in his own flesh and blood — has no more claim to his own limbs than he has to the limbs of another." This was a deliberately provocative extension, designed to expose what Spencer saw as the absurdity of denying property rights rooted in individual labour and consumption.

> Did we exist after the same fashion as those compound polyps, in which a number of individuals are based upon a living trunk common to them all, such a theory would be rational enough. But until Communism can be carried to that extent, it will be best to stand by the old doctrine.

**The slavery inherent in state-controlled production**. In *The Man Versus the State* (1884), Spencer returned to the communism question through the lens of the Democratic Federation's proposals for "agricultural and industrial armies under State-control." He argued that such a system necessarily presupposes "grades of officers, by whom obedience would have to be insisted upon," transforming every worker into a subordinate in a command hierarchy — "each would stand toward the governing agency in the relation of slave to master." Even if the governing agency were democratically controlled, "each member of the community as an individual would be a slave to the community as a whole." Spencer traced this relation to the militant type of society, in which the citizen "belonged neither to himself nor to his family, but belonged to his city," and argued that "socialism unawares re-introduces into a state intended to be purely industrial" the ethics of war.

**The bureaucratic degeneration problem**. Spencer further argued that "the administration will presently become not of the intended kind, and that the slavery will not be mild." The socialist speculation, he wrote, "is vitiated by an assumption like that which vitiates the speculations of the 'practical' politician. It is assumed that officialism will work as it is intended to work, which it never does." The machinery of communism, like existing social machinery, would be operated by fallible human beings with their own interests — a point Spencer considered fatal to the benevolent aspirations of communist theory.

## Historical Context

Spencer wrote during the formative period of modern socialist thought. *Social Statics* (1851) appeared three years after the *Communist Manifesto* and during the brief efflorescence of Owenite and Fourierist communities in Britain and America. By the time of *The Man Versus the State* (1884), the context had shifted: the Democratic Federation (founded 1881) was agitating for land nationalisation, and Henry George's *Progress and Poverty* (1879) had rekindled debates over common land ownership. Spencer's persistent critique of communism must thus be read as a response to a movement that grew from a marginal intellectual tendency in 1851 to a significant political force by the 1880s. Significantly, Spencer distinguished his own radical individualism from both laissez-faire conservatism and socialist collectivism — he opposed communism not from attachment to existing property distributions, but from the principle of equal freedom, which he held to be prior to and more fundamental than any economic arrangement.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

- **[[law-of-equal-freedom]]**: The foundational moral principle from which Spencer derived his critique of communism — equal freedom requires equal scope for individual effort, not equal distribution of outcomes.
- **[[right-of-property]]**: The institution that communism proposes to abolish or radically transform — Spencer's defence of property was grounded in the same principle of equal freedom that he used to reject communism.
- **[[state-socialism]]**: The milder, more gradual form of collectivism that Spencer saw as leading, through a succession of interventions, toward the same condition that revolutionary communism proposed to reach directly.
- **[[the-coming-slavery]]**: Spencer's warning that socialist and communist policies, even when enacted with benevolent intent, lead to the subjugation of the individual to the state.
- **[[industrial-type]]**: The voluntary-cooperation social model that Spencer contrasted with the compulsory organisation inherent in communist schemes.
