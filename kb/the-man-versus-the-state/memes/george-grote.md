## Biography

George Grote (1794–1871) was an English historian, banker, and radical politician, best known for his monumental *History of Greece* (12 volumes, 1846–1856). A leading figure in the philosophical radical movement, Grote applied utilitarian principles to historical analysis and was a close associate of John Stuart Mill and Jeremy Bentham. His work on Greek democracy provided nineteenth-century liberals with a historical model of political freedom untainted by monarchical or aristocratic institutions.

## Key Ideas

### Hero-Worship and Political Freedom

Spencer drew on Grote's authority in *Social Statics* (Chapter XXV, "General Considerations") to establish a correlation between the absence of hero-worship and the presence of political liberty. Grote had observed that "in no city of historical Greece did there prevail either human sacrifices or deliberate mutilations, such as cutting off the nose, ears, hands, feet, &c., or castration, or selling of children into slavery, or polygamy, or the feeling of unlimited obedience towards one man" — practices which existed among the contemporary Carthaginians, Egyptians, Persians, and Thracians.

For Spencer, this observation supported his broader thesis that the strength of the "sentiment which leads men to prostrate themselves before any manifestation of power" varies inversely with the recognition of individual rights. Greek political culture, lacking the extreme forms of ruler-worship found in Asiatic despotisms, provided the historical conditions for the emergence of citizen equality.

### Greek Government

Spencer also referenced Grote's analysis of Greek governmental forms, using the contrast between Greek and Eastern political institutions to illustrate the relationship between national character and political structure.

## Relationship to Spencer

Spencer treated Grote as a reliable empirical authority whose historical research confirmed theoretical expectations about the relationship between political psychology and political institutions. Grote's *History of Greece* supplied Spencer with comparative evidence for the claim that liberal political cultures emerge where the sentiment of personal rights is strongest.
