## Biography

Matthew Arnold (1822–1888) was an English poet, literary critic, and cultural commentator, the son of the celebrated headmaster of Rugby School, Thomas Arnold. Educated at Winchester, Rugby, and Balliol College, Oxford, Arnold began his career as a school inspector — a position he held for thirty-five years while producing a steady stream of poetry and prose. His early poetry, including "Dover Beach" and "The Scholar-Gipsy," established him as one of the major Victorian poets, but it was as a critic of culture and society that Arnold exerted his widest influence.

In *Culture and Anarchy* (1869), Arnold articulated his vision of culture as "the best which has been thought and said," a corrective to the philistinism of the middle classes and the anarchic individualism of liberal politics. Arnold advocated an expanded role for the state in education, culture, and public life — a position that set him at odds with the laissez-faire liberalism of his age. He coined the term "sweetness and light" to describe the civilizing mission of culture, and he divided English society into three classes — the Barbarians (aristocracy), the Philistines (middle class), and the Populace (working class) — each of which required the discipline of culture to overcome its characteristic defects.

It is not Arnold the poet or Arnold the apostle of culture that Herbert Spencer engages, however, but Arnold the anti-theorist — the critic who denied the existence of natural rights and who insisted that property is merely "the creation of law." Spencer found in Arnold's writings a particularly clear expression of the utilitarian-positivist denial of abstract rights that Spencer's own philosophy was designed to refute.

## Key Ideas

### The Denial of Natural Rights

Spencer quotes Arnold in *The Man Versus the State* as a representative voice of the anti-rights school. In an article on copyright, Arnold declared: "An author has no natural right to a property in his production. But then neither has he a natural right to anything whatever which he may produce or acquire." This statement, for Spencer, was among the most candid admissions of the philosophical assumptions underlying the new interventionist liberalism. If no one has a natural right to anything they produce or acquire, then property rights have no moral foundation — and if property rights have no moral foundation, the state may take or regulate property as it sees fit.

Spencer responded by arguing that the consequence of Arnold's doctrine is logically a system of complete state control over all economic life. If "neither has he a natural right to anything whatever which he may produce or acquire," then the state becomes the sole legitimate proprietor of everything, distributing or withholding according to its pleasure. Arnold, Spencer implies, did not fully appreciate the totalitarian implications of his own premises.

### Property as the Creation of Law

Arnold argued, following the legal positivist tradition, that "property is the creation of law." There is no such thing as a right to property anterior to or independent of the legal system; property rights exist only insofar as the state chooses to recognize and enforce them. Spencer quotes Arnold contending "in the article above quoted, that 'property is the creation of law,' tells us to beware of the 'metaphysical...'" — the ellipsis, presumably, standing for "metaphysical rights" or "metaphysical abstractions."

For Spencer, this view was not merely mistaken but dangerously unprincipled. If property is merely a creation of the state, then the state has unlimited power to define, restrict, and abolish property rights as it sees fit — a doctrine that Spencer believed licensed precisely the kind of predatory legislation he spent his career opposing. Spencer's counter-argument, developed at length in *Social Statics* and *The Man Versus the State*, was that property rights derive from the law of equal freedom — the fundamental principle that each individual's liberty is bounded only by the equal liberty of others — and are therefore morally prior to the positive law.

### The Spencer-Arnold Dispute over Intellectual Property

The particular context of Arnold's remarks was copyright law — the question of whether authors have a natural property right in their intellectual productions. Arnold denied that they did; an author "has no natural right to a property in his production." Spencer did not directly answer Arnold on this specific point in the passage quoted, but his broader philosophy implies that intellectual labour, like physical labour, grounds a natural right to its products. The dispute over copyright was, for Spencer, merely a particularly clear instance of the larger dispute between the natural-rights and the legal-positivist conceptions of property — a dispute with implications far beyond the realm of literary property.

## Major Works

- **Poems** (1853, with subsequent editions) — Arnold's collected poetry, including "Dover Beach," "The Scholar-Gipsy," "Empedocles on Etna," and "Sohrab and Rustum," establishing his place among the major Victorian poets.
- **Essays in Criticism** (1865, 1888) — Two volumes of literary criticism, introducing the concept of "disinterestedness" in critical judgment and the touchstone method of literary evaluation.
- **Culture and Anarchy** (1869) — Arnold's major work of social criticism, arguing for the civilizing role of culture against the anarchy of individualism and the philistinism of the middle classes.
- **Literature and Dogma** (1873) — Arnold's attempt to salvage the ethical content of Christianity from the supernatural elements he considered untenable — a project Spencer would have recognized as "keeping the content while rejecting the shell."
- **Discourses in America** (1885) — Lectures delivered during Arnold's American tour, covering literature, politics, and education.

## Relationship to Other Figures

- [[herbert-spencer]] — Spencer quotes Arnold's denial of natural rights as evidence of the philosophical assumptions underlying interventionist liberalism. Arnold's insistence that property is "the creation of law" represents, for Spencer, the antithesis of his own natural-rights philosophy.
- [[jeremy-bentham]] — Arnold's legal positivism descends, however indirectly, from Bentham's rejection of natural rights as "nonsense upon stilts." Bentham's influence on English legal thought created the intellectual climate in which Arnold's views on property could gain currency.
- [[john-stuart-mill]] — Mill, like Arnold, was an apostle of culture — but Mill's liberalism remained anchored in the principle of individual liberty in a way that Arnold's, with its embrace of state authority, did not.
- [[edmund-burke]] — Burke, like Arnold, valued the organic growth of institutions and distrusted abstract rights — but Burke's target was revolutionary rationalism, not individual property, which Burke defended as a natural right.
