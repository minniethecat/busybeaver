## Biography

Thomas Hobbes (1588–1679) was an English philosopher, best known for his work on political philosophy. Born in Westport, Wiltshire, he studied at Oxford and later travelled extensively in Europe, meeting leading intellectuals including Galileo and Descartes. The tumult of the English Civil War (1642–1651) deeply shaped his thinking; Hobbes fled to Paris in 1640 and remained there for eleven years, during which he wrote his most famous work, *Leviathan* (1651).

Hobbes's political philosophy is grounded in a materialist conception of human nature: he viewed humans as self-interested beings driven by appetites and aversions, whose actions in the absence of political authority lead inevitably to conflict — the famous "war of every man against every man." His solution to this predicament is the social contract, by which individuals transfer their natural rights to a sovereign in exchange for security.

Though Hobbes argued in the interests of absolute monarchy, his method — deriving political legitimacy from individual consent rather than divine right — marks him as a foundational figure of modern political thought. Herbert Spencer, writing two centuries later, subjects Hobbes's premises to sustained critique in *The Man Versus the State* and *Social Statics*, questioning both the historical accuracy of his state of nature and his assumptions about human character.

## Key Ideas

### The State of Nature and the Social Contract

Hobbes's most influential argument begins with the postulate that, "during the time men live without a common power to keep them all in awe, they are in that condition which is called war … of every man against every man" (*Leviathan*, ch. 13). From this premise, he derives the necessity of an absolute sovereign to whom individuals transfer their natural rights by covenant.

Spencer challenges this postulate directly. He observes that "there are some small uncivilized societies in which, without any 'common power to keep them all in awe,' the members live together in amity" (Man Versus the State, ch. 4). The historical and anthropological evidence, Spencer argues, does not support the claim that life without a state is necessarily a condition of universal war.

### The Derivation of Sovereignty

For Hobbes, political authority arises from a covenant: "where no covenant hath preceded, there hath no right been transferred, and every man has a right to everything; and consequently, no action can be unjust. But when a covenant is made, then to break it is unjust" (*Leviathan*, ch. 14). The sovereign, not being party to the covenant, retains absolute power and cannot act unjustly toward subjects.

Spencer notes two significant implications of this derivation. First, "State-authority as thus derived, is a means to an end, and has no validity save as subserving that end: if the end is not subserved, the authority, by the hypothesis, disappears" (Man Versus the State, ch. 4). Second, the entire function of the state implied by Hobbes's reasoning is limited to protection against direct and indirect aggressions — what Spencer calls the "administration of justice." This reading of Hobbes paradoxically supports Spencer's own minimal-state position.

### The Problem of Human Character

Hobbes's theory rests on a particular view of human nature — that people will not keep their covenants without a coercive power threatening punishment. Spencer questions this assumption: "Were people's characters in Hobbes's day really so bad as to warrant his assumption that none would perform their covenants in the absence of a coercive power and threatened penalties?" He counters that "in our day 'the names of just and unjust can have place' quite apart from recognition of any coercive power," citing personal acquaintances who keep their word without threat of force (Man Versus the State, ch. 4).

This critique reflects Spencer's broader evolutionary optimism: as society evolves from the militant to the industrial type, coercive external control is progressively replaced by internalized moral sentiments. Hobbes's psychology, Spencer implies, reflects the militant stage of society rather than a permanent truth about human nature.

### Hobbes and the Binding of Posterity

Spencer further challenges Hobbes's assumption that a social contract can bind future generations. Even if one were to "accept, also, the implication that their descendants for ever are bound by the covenant which remote ancestors made for them," the historical evidence of such a primordial contract is entirely absent. Spencer concludes that "the hypothesis of a social contract, either under the shape assumed by Hobbes or under the shape assumed by Rousseau, is baseless. Nay more, it must be admitted that even had such a contract once been formed, it could not be binding on the posterity of those who formed it" (Man Versus the State, ch. 4).

## Major Works

- **Leviathan** (1651) — Hobbes's magnum opus, presenting the social contract theory and the case for absolute sovereignty. The foundational text of modern political philosophy, cited extensively by Spencer in his critique of unlimited state power.
- **De Cive** (1642) — An earlier systematic treatment of political philosophy, developing the concepts of the state of nature and natural law that would be more fully elaborated in *Leviathan*.
- **Elements of Law, Natural and Politic** (1640) — Hobbes's first major work of political theory, circulated in manuscript during the lead-up to the English Civil War.
- **De Corpore** (1655) — The first part of Hobbes's tripartite philosophical system, articulating his materialist metaphysics and theory of language.
- **De Homine** (1658) — Hobbes's treatment of human nature, completing his philosophical trilogy.

## Relationship to Other Figures

- [[jeremy-bentham]] — Bentham, though sharing Hobbes's utilitarian logic, "far from being an absolutist like Hobbes, wrote in the interests of popular rule" (Man Versus the State, ch. 4). Spencer contrasts Hobbes's absolutism with Bentham's democratic utilitarianism.
- [[john-austin]] — Austin, described by Spencer as Hobbes's "modern admirer," attempted "to derive the authority of law from the unlimited sovereignty of one man, or a number of men." Spencer notes that Austin "was originally in the army; and it has been truly remarked that his views on sovereignty are those of a soldier" (Man Versus the State, ch. 4).
- [[herbert-spencer]] — Spencer engages Hobbes as his central interlocutor on sovereignty, using Hobbes's own logic of contract to argue for limited government. Spencer's *Man Versus the State* devotes an entire chapter to critiquing Hobbes's derivation of absolute authority.
- [[john-locke]] — Locke adopted the social contract framework but rejected Hobbes's absolutist conclusions, arguing for limited government and the right of revolution — positions closer to Spencer's own conclusions.
- [[jean-jacques-rousseau]] — Rousseau's version of the social contract, with its general will and unlimited majority power, is criticized alongside Hobbes's by Spencer as equally baseless and dangerous.
