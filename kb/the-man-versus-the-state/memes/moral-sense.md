## Definition

The moral sense is the innate human capacity to perceive moral distinctions — to recognize some actions as right and others as wrong — without deliberative reasoning. The doctrine, most fully developed in British moral philosophy by the third Earl of Shaftesbury and Francis Hutcheson, holds that humans possess an internal moral faculty analogous to the physical senses: just as the eye perceives light and color, the moral sense perceives virtue and vice.

Herbert Spencer, in *Social Statics* (1851), devotes a full chapter to the moral sense doctrine. While critical of its unsystematic application by earlier philosophers, Spencer defends the core claim — that there exists a natural moral intuition — and integrates it into his broader system as one of three convergent routes to the law of equal freedom.

## Spencer's Defense of Moral Intuition

Spencer confronts the central objection to moral sense philosophy: if morality is innate, why do moral codes vary dramatically across cultures and eras? The patrician who believes in natural hierarchy, the cardinal who abhors keeping faith with infidels, the slaveholder who perceives slavery as morally legitimate — all claim the authority of their moral sense, yet reach contradictory conclusions.

Spencer's response is that the error lies not in the doctrine but in its application. The moral sense, like appetite, is a reliable guide in its proper domain but requires intellectual discipline to apply correctly. Just as appetite generally guides us to nutritious food but requires reason to distinguish wholesome from harmful choices, moral intuition generally points toward equal freedom but requires rational analysis to formulate precisely.

The failures of earlier moral sense philosophers — their inability to produce a consistent, scientific morality from intuition alone — are failures of method, not of foundation. They "did not start from a wrong principle, but rather missed the right way from that principle."

## Bentham's Inadvertent Appeal

Spencer deploys a rhetorical masterstroke against Bentham's critique of moral sense. Bentham, who ridiculed moral intuition as arbitrary and subjective — "my moral sense tells me it is" — turns out to rest his own greatest happiness principle on precisely the same foundation.

Spencer dramatizes this through an imagined dialogue. A plebeian asserts the greatest happiness principle. A patrician demands: "Who told you that you have as good a right to happiness as I have? Give me your authority." The plebeian, stripped of all appeals, is forced to confess: "he has no other authority but his own feeling — that he has simply an innate perception of the fact; or, in other words, that his moral sense tells him so."

The implication is clear: all ethical systems, however rationalistic their presentation, ultimately rest on moral intuitions that cannot be further justified. Spencer's candor about this fact — his willingness to acknowledge the foundational role of moral sense while insisting on its rational development — distinguishes his approach from both naive intuitionism and naive rationalism.

## The Sympathetic Mechanism

Spencer identifies sympathy as the psychological mechanism through which the moral sense operates. The instinct of rights, in its raw form, is purely self-regarding — each person is moved to defend only his own rights. But through sympathy, the instinct is "excited" on behalf of others.

Adam Smith, whom Spencer cites approvingly, observed that we "blush for the impudence and rudeness of another, though he himself appears to have no sense of the impropriety of his behaviour." Women start or shriek on seeing an accident occur to others. Unpracticed assistants at surgical operations faint. These sympathetic reactions are the raw material from which moral intuitions are built.

Spencer extends Smith's analysis to explain the qualms of conscience felt by those who have committed dishonest actions, and the satisfaction derived from paying another what is due. These are not learned responses but manifestations of a deeper neural architecture — what Spencer would later, in his evolutionary writings, describe as inherited moral dispositions shaped by the experience of the species.

## Three Convergent Routes

The moral sense is one of three independent routes Spencer identifies as converging on the law of equal freedom. The first route is rational deduction from the conditions of greatest happiness. The second is analysis of human nature as a "congeries of faculties." The third is the testimony of the moral sense.

That all three routes arrive at the same conclusion is, for Spencer, compelling evidence of the principle's truth. The convergence transforms moral intuition from a merely subjective feeling into an intersubjectively validated datum — a signal that, however imperfectly, points toward a real moral structure in human relationships.
