## Biography

Charles Robert Darwin (1809–1882) was an English naturalist whose theory of evolution by natural selection ranks among the most significant scientific achievements in history. Born in Shrewsbury, Darwin studied medicine at Edinburgh before moving to Cambridge, where his interest in natural history deepened. In 1831, he embarked on the five-year voyage of HMS *Beagle*, during which his observations of geology, fossils, and the geographical distribution of species planted the seeds of his evolutionary theory.

After returning to England in 1836, Darwin spent over two decades gathering evidence and refining his argument. The publication of *On the Origin of Species* in 1859 — prompted by the independent discovery of natural selection by Alfred Russel Wallace — provoked immediate controversy. Darwin's mechanism of natural selection, operating on random variation over immense timescales, provided a naturalistic explanation for the diversity and adaptedness of life that did not require divine intervention.

Herbert Spencer had independently developed an evolutionary theory before Darwin's publication. In *Social Statics* (1851) and *Principles of Psychology* (1855), Spencer argued for the progressive development of life, mind, and society through the operation of universal laws. Spencer's term "survival of the fittest" — coined in *Principles of Biology* (1864) — was subsequently adopted by Darwin in later editions of the *Origin* as a synonym for natural selection. Darwin acknowledged Spencer's contribution, though he once remarked that reading Spencer made him feel "like a worm" for not having constructed so grand a philosophical system.

## Key Ideas

### Natural Selection and "Survival of the Fittest"

Darwin's central insight was that the struggle for existence, combined with heritable variation, produces a natural process of selection analogous to the artificial selection practised by breeders. Organisms with variations that confer an advantage in the struggle for survival and reproduction are more likely to pass those variations to the next generation. Over many generations, this process of "natural selection," as Darwin called it, produces adaptation — the exquisite fit between organism and environment.

Spencer embraced and popularized Darwin's theory. In *Principles of Biology*, Spencer wrote that "the process of 'natural selection,' as Mr. Darwin called it, co-operating with a tendency to variation and to inheritance of variations, he has shown to be a chief cause" of organic evolution. Spencer's own term, "survival of the fittest," emphasized the outcome of the process — the preservation of those individuals and traits best adapted to the conditions of existence.

### The Evolution of Mind and Society

Darwin's *The Descent of Man* (1871) extended evolutionary theory to human beings, arguing that the mental and moral faculties of humans differ from those of other animals in degree, not kind. The moral sense, Darwin argued, arises from the social instincts — sympathy, loyalty, and the desire for approbation — which natural selection has cultivated in social animals.

Spencer had anticipated this conclusion two decades earlier in *Social Statics* (1851), arguing that the moral sense is not a supernatural endowment but an evolved faculty shaped by the conditions of social life. Spencer and Darwin agreed that ethics must be naturalized, but they diverged on the mechanisms. Darwin placed greater emphasis on group selection and the role of habit; Spencer emphasized the direct inheritance of acquired characteristics (Lamarckism) and the progressive adaptation of the individual to the social state. Spencer's Lamarckism, though rejected by modern biology, was an integral part of his broader evolutionary philosophy.

### Darwin, Spencer, and the Transformation of Victorian Thought

Together, Darwin and Spencer transformed the intellectual landscape of the nineteenth century. Darwin provided the mechanism — natural selection — that made evolution scientifically credible. Spencer provided the philosophical synthesis — the application of evolutionary principles to psychology, sociology, ethics, and politics — that made evolution a universal worldview. Though Spencer's reputation declined in the twentieth century while Darwin's grew, their mutual influence was profound. Spencer acknowledged Darwin as the discoverer of the chief mechanism of organic change; Darwin acknowledged Spencer as the great systematizer who had applied evolutionary thinking across the whole range of human knowledge.

## Major Works

- **On the Origin of Species** (1859) — Darwin's foundational work, presenting the theory of evolution by natural selection.
- **The Descent of Man, and Selection in Relation to Sex** (1871) — Darwin's application of evolutionary theory to human origins, including the evolution of the moral sense.
- **The Expression of the Emotions in Man and Animals** (1872) — An evolutionary account of emotional expression, influencing Spencer's own work on the psychology of the feelings.
- **The Voyage of the Beagle** (1839) — Darwin's journal of the *Beagle* expedition, recording the observations that sparked his evolutionary thinking.

## Relationship to Other Figures

- [[herbert-spencer]] — Spencer independently developed an evolutionary philosophy before the *Origin* (1859); his term "survival of the fittest" was adopted by Darwin. Though their mechanisms differed (Lamarckism vs. natural selection), they shared a commitment to naturalistic evolution.
- [[thomas-huxley]] — "Darwin's Bulldog," Huxley was the most vigorous public defender of Darwin's theory and a close friend. Spencer debated Huxley on evolutionary ethics.
- [[alfred-russel-wallace]] — Wallace independently discovered natural selection, prompting Darwin to publish the *Origin*. Wallace later diverged from Darwin on the evolution of the human mind.
- [[thomas-malthus]] — Malthus's *Essay on the Principle of Population* (1798) provided Darwin with the key insight: the struggle for existence is a natural consequence of the pressure of population on resources.
