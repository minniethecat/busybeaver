## Nature / Structure

The British Parliament is the supreme legislative authority in the United Kingdom, composed of the House of Commons (elected by a popular franchise), the House of Lords (historically hereditary, now largely appointed), and the Crown-in-Parliament. Its origins trace to the medieval royal councils and the development of representative government from the thirteenth century onward.

In Spencer's analysis, Parliament represents the institutionalization of the "expediency" principle — the belief that a body of men, duly elected and deliberating, can determine by inspection what measures will promote the public good. Spencer treats this as a modern, democratized form of the "divine right" theory: "It merely modifies 'divine right of kings' into divine right of majorities."

## Functions / Powers

Parliament possesses the power to make and unmake any law; no person or body can override or set aside its legislation (the doctrine of parliamentary sovereignty). It controls taxation and public expenditure, scrutinizes the executive through questions and committees, and provides the personnel of government from among its members.

Spencer devotes sustained attention to the gap between Parliament's professed function — protecting the rights of citizens — and its actual operation. He documents how "those who possess authority will, if permitted, use it for selfish purposes," citing historical evidence from inclosure laws, the Game Laws, the "Black Act," the manipulation of the land tax, and the perversion of public school funds. For Spencer, the self-interested behavior of legislators is not a contingent corruption but a necessary consequence of human nature: "Were we not selfish, legislative restraint would be unnecessary. Evidently, then, the very existence of a State-authority proves that irresponsible rulers will sacrifice the public good to their personal benefit."

## Historical Development

Parliament evolved through a long struggle between monarch and people. Landmarks include Magna Carta (1215), the Civil War and Glorious Revolution (1642–1689), the Bill of Rights (1689), and the successive Reform Acts of 1832, 1867, and 1884 that gradually extended the franchise.

Spencer viewed this history through the lens of his evolutionary framework. Representative government represents a transitional stage between the military-despotic regime of status and the fully industrial regime of voluntary contract. It is an improvement over absolutism, but it remains a form of coercion — "the system of compulsory co-operation" — and therefore an imperfect arrangement from the standpoint of the moral law.

In *The Man Versus the State*, Spencer traced how the originally anti-statist character of Whig/Liberal politics gradually inverted itself, as Liberals came to seek the public good *directly* through legislation rather than *indirectly* through the removal of restraints.

## Relationship to the State

The relationship between Parliament and the individual citizen is the core theme of Spencer's political thought. Parliament, as the institutional embodiment of state authority, stands in permanent tension with the liberty of the individual. Every Act of Parliament, however well-intentioned, represents a restriction on some person's freedom of action — a trespass against the law of equal freedom.

Spencer's central charge is that Parliament lacks any principled limit to its power. "These restraints which all would put to the will of the majority, are the restraints set up by that law" — the law of equal freedom. But because Parliament recognizes no such law, its authority is in principle unlimited. The only practical check — the electoral system — is itself inadequate, because "those who possess authority will, if permitted, use it for selfish purposes."

This analysis culminates in Spencer's warning that parliamentary democracy, absent a principled commitment to individual liberty, does not abolish despotism but merely redistributes it: "It is despotism democratized."
