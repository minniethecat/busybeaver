## Biography

John Elliott Cairnes (1823–1875) was an Irish political economist of the classical school. Educated at Trinity College Dublin, he was called to the Irish Bar before turning to political economy. He held the Whately Professorship of Political Economy at Trinity College Dublin and later the Chair of Political Economy at University College London.

Cairnes was a rigorous exponent of the deductive method in economics, following in the tradition of David Ricardo and John Stuart Mill. His major works addressed the logic of economic method, the economics of slavery, and the theory of value. He was regarded as one of the last major figures of the classical school before the marginalist revolution.

In the 1870s, Cairnes engaged in a notable intellectual exchange with Herbert Spencer, criticizing Spencer's application of evolutionary theory to social affairs — particularly Spencer's argument that social evolution proceeds through fixed laws independent of deliberate human effort.

## Key Ideas

### The Deductive Method in Political Economy

Cairnes's most significant methodological contribution was his defence of the deductive approach in economic reasoning. In *The Character and Logical Method of Political Economy* (1857), he argued that political economy proceeds not by induction from empirical data but by deduction from premises about human nature — chiefly, the desire for wealth and the aversion to labour. This placed him in opposition to the rising historical and statistical schools.

### Critique of Spencer's Social Evolution

Cairnes criticized Herbert Spencer on the grounds that Spencer's evolutionary doctrine seemed to render deliberate philanthropic effort superfluous. If social change is determined by the "natures of citizens" operating under given conditions, then what role remains for intentional moral and political action? Cairnes charged that "according to Mr. Spencer, the future of the human race may be safely trusted to the action of motives of a private and personal kind."

### The Role of Higher Motives

Cairnes insisted that "higher" motives — altruism, justice, political aspiration — play an indispensable role in social progress, and that any theory omitting them is descriptively inadequate and practically dangerous. This challenge forced Spencer to clarify in the Postscript to *The Man Versus the State* that his evolutionary framework "not only necessarily includes" altruistic motives "in the totality of motives, but repeatedly insists upon them as all-essential."

## Major Works

- *The Character and Logical Method of Political Economy* (1857) — a defence of deductive reasoning in economics
- *The Slave Power* (1862) — an influential analysis of the economics of slavery, used by the Union cause during the American Civil War
- *Some Leading Principles of Political Economy Newly Expounded* (1874) — his major theoretical work on value theory and cost of production

## Influence / Legacy

Cairnes's exchange with Spencer illuminates a central tension in nineteenth-century social thought: between deterministic theories of social evolution and the voluntaristic belief in the efficacy of deliberate reform. Although Spencer defended himself vigorously, Cairnes's critique anticipated later objections — from T. H. Green, L. T. Hobhouse, and others — that Spencerian individualism failed to account for the role of collective moral agency in shaping society.

Cairnes is also remembered as one of the last major classical economists. His work on method and value theory was soon overtaken by the marginalist revolution led by Jevons, Menger, and Walras, but his careful reasoning about the scope and limits of economic science remains instructive.
