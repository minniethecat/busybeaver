## Definition

Political superstition, in Herbert Spencer's usage, denotes the persistent and irrational belief that political authority, whatever its form, is subject to no inherent limits. The concept receives its fullest treatment in the essay "The Great Political Superstition" (*Man Versus the State*, 1884), where Spencer opens with his most quoted formulation:

> "The great political superstition of the past was the divine right of kings. The great political superstition of the present is the divine right of parliaments. The oil of anointing seems unawares to have dripped from the head of the one on to the heads of the many, and given sacredness to them also and to their decrees."

Spencer's argument is not merely that governments overreach in practice. It is that modern political thought has retained the *form* of unlimited authority while discarding its theological *foundation* — a position that is logically less coherent than the old divine-right doctrine it replaced.

## The Three Ages of Political Superstition

Spencer traces the superstition through three historical forms. Under monarchy, the king was held to be a god, a descendant of a god, or divinely appointed — and from these premises, unlimited obedience followed with perfect logical consistency. As Spencer notes, when theologians like Bossuet taught that kings "are gods, and share in a manner the Divine independence," the conclusion that no bounds could be set to governmental commands was "inevitable."

The second age transferred this unlimited authority to parliaments. But here Spencer identifies a fatal inconsistency: "Making no pretention to divine descent or divine appointment, a legislative body can show no supernatural justification for its claim to unlimited authority; and no natural justification has ever been attempted." The old belief was at least internally coherent; the modern one, Spencer argues, is held without warrant of any kind.

The third and most subtle form is the divine right of majorities — the assumption that a numerical majority possesses powers without bounds. Spencer calls this "a purely conditional right, valid only within specific limits." He demonstrates its absurdity through a series of *reductio ad absurdum* examples: could a majority justifiably enact the drowning of all children born in the next ten years? Could the more numerous of two races enslave the other? If not, then majority power is not absolute — there exists a higher law to which even the popular will must defer.

## Bentham and the Paradox of Created Rights

Spencer locates the intellectual root of modern political superstition in Benthamite utilitarianism. Bentham held that government fulfills its office "by creating rights which it confers upon individuals" — rights of personal security, protection for honour, and property. Simultaneously, Bentham's *Constitutional Code* fixed sovereignty in "the whole people."

Spencer exposes the resulting circularity with characteristic sharpness:

"The sovereign people jointly appoint representatives, and so create a government; the government thus created, creates rights; and then, having created rights, it confers them on the separate members of the sovereign people by which it was itself created. Here is a marvellous piece of political legerdemain!"

The paradox is irreconcilable. Either rights precede government — in which case government cannot be their creator — or they do not, in which case citizens possess rights only "by sufferance of parliament," a formula indistinguishable in its practical effect from the old absolutism Spencer opposes.

## The Legitimate Limit of Subordination

Spencer does not reject all subordination of minority to majority. The defense of each citizen against internal and external aggressors requires collective action, and for the maintenance of this "vital principle, alike of individual life and social life," subordination to the majority is legitimate — but only insofar as it trenches on individual freedom and property "no more than is requisite for the better protecting of his freedom and property."

Beyond this protective function, "such subordination is not legitimate." An aggression upon the individual greater than is required for his protection is a breach of the very principle it purports to uphold. The assumed divine right of parliaments and the implied divine right of majorities are, in Spencer's final judgment, "superstitions" — beliefs retained after their rational warrant has collapsed.

## Empirical vs. Rational Utilitarianism

Spencer distinguishes between what he calls "empirical utilitarianism" and "rational utilitarianism." The empirical variety, which he identifies with the Benthamite tradition, contemplates "only the effects of particular interferences on the actions of particular classes of men, and ignored the effects produced by a multiplicity of such interferences on the lives of men at large." It is this narrow, case-by-case reasoning that makes the political superstition possible — for if each individual state intervention is judged only by its immediate utility, the cumulative erosion of liberty remains invisible.

Rational utilitarianism, by contrast, recognizes that the general conditions of social life — security of person and property, freedom of contract and movement — must be maintained even when particular interventions promise local benefits. When these "vital conditions to life, individual and social, come to be so imperfectly fulfilled that the life decays," the cost of the political superstition becomes manifest.

## The Militant Residue

Spencer closes "The Great Political Superstition" with a historical explanation. The belief in unlimited governmental authority is a residue of the "militant type" of social organization — the regime of status and compulsory cooperation characteristic of warlike societies. So long as nations remain oriented toward external conflict, "the current political superstition must hold its ground." Only with the advance of the "industrial type" — characterized by voluntary cooperation and the regime of contract — can the doctrine of limited governmental functions gain widespread acceptance.

This framing ties political superstition to Spencer's broader theory of social evolution: the superstition is not merely an intellectual error but a structural feature of societies that have not yet fully transitioned from militant to industrial organization.
