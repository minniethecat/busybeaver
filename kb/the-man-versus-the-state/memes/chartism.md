## Background

Chartism was the first mass working-class political movement in British history. It emerged in the late 1830s from the convergence of several currents of discontent: the disappointment of working-class radicals with the limited scope of the Reform Act of 1832 (which had extended the franchise to the middle classes but excluded the working class), the economic hardship of the industrial districts, and the organisational experience accumulated through earlier campaigns such as the anti-Poor Law agitation and the Ten Hours movement.

The movement took its name from the "People's Charter," a six-point programme of parliamentary reform drafted in 1838 by the London Working Men's Association under the leadership of William Lovett. The Charter demanded: universal male suffrage, the secret ballot, the abolition of property qualifications for Members of Parliament, payment of MPs, equal electoral districts, and annual parliaments. These demands, radical in the context of the 1830s, were designed to democratise a political system still dominated by the landed aristocracy and to give the working class a voice in the government of the country.

Chartism drew its support primarily from the industrial working classes of the manufacturing districts — Lancashire, Yorkshire, the Midlands, and South Wales — as well as from distressed handloom weavers and other artisans threatened by industrialisation. At its peak in the early 1840s, the movement mobilised hundreds of thousands of supporters. Massive petitions were submitted to Parliament in 1839, 1842, and 1848, each bearing millions of signatures (the 1842 petition claimed over three million). Parliament rejected all three petitions, and the movement declined after 1848, though Chartist ideas and personnel persisted in later radical and labour movements.

## Description

Chartism was not a unified organisation but a broad coalition of local associations, newspapers (notably the *Northern Star*, edited by Feargus O'Connor), and competing leadership factions. The movement oscillated between "moral force" and "physical force" strategies: Lovett and his allies advocated peaceful agitation, education, and self-improvement; O'Connor and the more militant wing threatened armed insurrection if the Charter were not granted. The tension between these strategies was never fully resolved, and it contributed to the movement's ultimate failure to achieve its immediate objectives.

The three petition campaigns marked the movement's high points. The 1839 petition was rejected by Parliament by 235 votes to 46, and the subsequent Newport Rising — an armed attempt to seize the town of Newport in Monmouthshire — was suppressed, with its leaders transported to Australia. The 1842 petition, presented amid the economic depression of the "Hungry Forties," was rejected even more decisively. The 1848 petition, presented by O'Connor amid the revolutionary atmosphere sweeping Europe, was subjected to scrutiny by a parliamentary committee that found many of the signatures to be forgeries (including those of Queen Victoria and the Duke of Wellington), fatally discrediting the movement in the eyes of the political establishment.

Despite its failure to achieve the Charter in its own time, Chartism had a lasting impact. Five of the six points of the Charter (all except annual parliaments) were eventually enacted, and the movement created a political consciousness and organisational tradition that fed into the later trade union and labour movements. Historians have recognised Chartism as a crucial chapter in the democratisation of British politics.

## Significance / Impact

Spencer's treatment of Chartism is notable for what it reveals about his method of political analysis. He did not discuss the Charter's specific demands or the movement's organisational history. Instead, he placed Chartism within a broader current of moral evolution that he detected across a series of superficially disparate political movements.

> When we observe those agitations for popular freedom — Magna Charta, declarations of rights, liberty of the press, slave-emancipation, removal of religious disabilities, Reform Bills, Chartism, &c., and consider how through all of them there runs a kindred spirit, and how this spirit is manifesting itself with constantly-increasing intensity and universality, we shall see that these facts imply some moral change.
>
> — Herbert Spencer, *Social Statics* (1851), "The Constitution of the State"

For Spencer, Chartism was not primarily an economic or class phenomenon but a manifestation of the same "kindred spirit" — the compound faculty responding to the law of equal freedom — that had driven Magna Carta, the abolition of slavery, Catholic Emancipation, and the Reform Bills. Each of these movements, in his analysis, represented the gradual assertion of the individual's claim to equal liberty against the inherited structures of privilege and coercion. Chartism was thus continuous with, not opposed to, the liberal tradition of which Spencer considered himself a part.

Spencer was also acutely aware of how Chartism was perceived by its opponents. He noted that a "Country Parson" had written that "you must convert the Chartist spirit as you would reform the drunkard's spirit, by showing that it is a rebellion against the laws of God." Spencer cited this as evidence of the inability of those deficient in the "sense of rights" to recognise legitimate claims to freedom when those claims were advanced by the politically powerless. The same individuals who celebrated the Reform Bill of 1832 as a triumph of liberty condemned Chartism as sedition, failing to see that both drew from the same moral source.
