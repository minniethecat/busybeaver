## Background

The First Anglo-Afghan War arose from the strategic anxieties of British India in the 1830s. The Governor-General, Lord Auckland, feared that Russian influence was extending into Afghanistan, threatening the security of British India's north-western frontier. The ruling Afghan emir, Dost Mohammad Khan, had been courted by Russian agents, and Auckland determined to replace him with a more compliant ruler, Shah Shuja Durrani, who had been living in exile under British protection since 1809.

The expedition was justified through the language of imperial defence and commercial advantage. The East India Company, which effectively governed British India, framed the invasion as necessary to secure the frontier and protect British trade routes into Central Asia. The "Great Game" of Anglo-Russian rivalry gave the enterprise a geopolitical rationale that resonated with the imperial imagination of the British public and Parliament.

The war was launched in 1839 with the assembly of the "Army of the Indus," a force of some 20,000 British and Indian troops, accompanied by an enormous baggage train and camp followers. The initial advance into Afghanistan proceeded without major military resistance, and Shah Shuja was installed in Kabul. However, the occupation quickly deteriorated into a protracted insurgency that culminated in one of the most catastrophic reverses in British military history.

## Description

The initial phase of the war appeared successful: Kandahar and Ghazni were captured, and Shah Shuja was enthroned in Kabul in August 1839. However, the British presence was deeply resented by the Afghan population. The occupation was maintained at enormous expense, requiring subsidies to tribal chiefs and the upkeep of a large garrison far from its supply base. By 1841, a widespread revolt broke out in Kabul, led by Dost Mohammad's son, Akbar Khan. The British political agent, Sir William Macnaghten, was killed during negotiations, and the British garrison found itself besieged.

In January 1842, the Kabul garrison of approximately 4,500 troops (mostly Indian sepoys) and 12,000 camp followers attempted to retreat through the winter snows of the Khyber Pass toward Jalalabad. Over the course of a week, the column was systematically annihilated by Afghan tribesmen. A single survivor, Dr. William Brydon, reached Jalalabad. The disaster stunned the British public and became one of the defining imperial traumas of the nineteenth century.

A punitive expedition later in 1842 briefly reoccupied Kabul, burned the bazaar, and withdrew. Dost Mohammad was eventually restored to the throne, rendering the war futile in its stated objective. The financial cost to the East India Company was staggering; the total expenditure, which Spencer would later cite as £17,000,000, significantly contributed to the Company's mounting debt.

## Significance / Impact

Spencer's use of the Afghan War in *Social Statics* formed part of his systematic critique of colonial expansion. He argued that the war, together with the conquests of Scinde (costing £1,000,000 per annum) and the Punjaub, constituted a financial drain on the English people that was justified by the spurious claim of commercial benefit. The "everlasting excuse of more trade," Spencer wrote, was used to rationalise expenditure that yielded negligible or negative returns.

> Should you, under the fear that the East India Company's debt may some day be saddled upon the people of England, lament the outlay of £17,000,000 over the Afghan war, the sinking of £1,000,000 a year in Scinde, and the swallowing up of untold treasure in the subjugation of the Punjaub, there still comes the everlasting excuse of more trade.
>
> — Herbert Spencer, *Social Statics* (1851), "Government Colonization"

Spencer extended his analysis beyond the financial costs to the structural dynamics of empire. He argued that colonial aggression was self-perpetuating: each expansion generated new frontiers, new insecurities, and new demands for military expenditure. The mechanics of empire functioned like the monkey in the fable, unable to withdraw its hand from the jar because it refused to let go of what it had seized. The Afghan War, in this analysis, was not an aberration but a predictable consequence of the imperial logic of acquisitiveness.

Spencer also drew a pointed contrast between state-conducted colonisation and private settlement. He celebrated the example of William Penn's Pennsylvania, established without state military protection or coercion of native peoples, as a demonstration that peaceful private colonisation was both morally and economically superior. The Afghan War represented the opposite model: state aggression that was costly, destructive, and self-defeating.
