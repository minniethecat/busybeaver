## Definition

The **industrial type** (or *industrial type of society*) is one of the two fundamental forms of social organization in Herbert Spencer's evolutionary sociology, standing in opposition to the **militant type**. Spencer introduced this distinction in *The Man Versus the State* (1884), defining the two types as expressions of "two opposed types of social organization, broadly distinguishable as the militant and the industrial — types which are characterized, the one by the regime of status, almost universal in ancient days, and the other by the regime of contract, which has become general in modern days." The industrial type is thus distinguished by the **regime of contract** — a social order in which individuals freely negotiate their mutual obligations through voluntary exchange — whereas the militant type is characterized by the **regime of status**, in which individuals' positions and duties are assigned by hierarchical command.

The industrial type, for Spencer, is not simply a society that engages in commerce rather than war; it is a comprehensive organizational logic in which voluntary cooperation pervades political, economic, and social institutions. Spencer argues that the historical transition from militancy to industrialism is the central narrative of social evolution, and that the ascendancy of the industrial type represents a higher stage of civilization marked by individual liberty, legal equality, and the contraction of state power.

> Dating back to an earlier period than their names, the two political parties at first stood respectively for two opposed types of social organization, broadly distinguishable as the militant and the industrial — types which are characterized, the one by the regime of status, almost universal in ancient days, and the other by the regime of contract, which has become general in modern days, chiefly among the Western nations, and especially among ourselves and the Americans.

## Key Arguments

Spencer's account of the industrial type proceeds from the claim that peaceful productive activity generates a form of social cooperation fundamentally different from that required by war. A society engaged in industry — in which individuals produce and exchange goods and services — does not need the centralized command structure that a war-making society requires. Instead, it thrives when individuals are free to contract with one another, when property rights are secure, and when government is limited to the enforcement of contracts and the protection of individual rights.

First, **voluntary cooperation** is the defining feature of the industrial type. Spencer contrasts this with the compulsory cooperation of the militant type: "If, instead of using the word 'co-operation' in a limited sense, we use it in its widest sense, as signifying the combined activities of citizens under whatever system of regulation; then these two are definable as the system of compulsory co-operation and the system of voluntary co-operation." In the industrial type, individuals cooperate not because they are commanded to do so, but because they perceive mutual benefit in exchange.

Second, the industrial type is associated with **political decentralization and limited government**. Spencer argues that just as the militant type generates centralized authority and unquestioning obedience, the industrial type generates representative institutions, the protection of individual rights, and the progressive limitation of state power. Historically, he notes that resistance to coercive rule consistently arose from "town-populations, formed of workers and traders accustomed to co-operate under contract."

> During social evolution in England, the distinction between these two fundamentally-opposed forms of co-operation, made its appearance gradually; but long before the names Tory and Whig came into use, the parties were becoming traceable, and their connexions with militancy and industrialism respectively, were vaguely shown.

Third, the industrial type fosters a distinctive **psychology of individual autonomy**. Where the militant type cultivates faith in authority and submission to command, the industrial type cultivates skepticism toward government, a preference for individual initiative, and the habit of voluntary association. Spencer sees the historical shift from Toryism (rooted in militancy) to classical Liberalism (rooted in industrialism) as the political expression of this deeper social-psychological transformation.

## Historical Context

Spencer developed the militant-industrial typology in the context of nineteenth-century debates about the direction of social evolution. The distinction drew on a long tradition of contrast between ancient and modern societies — from Montesquieu's distinction between republics founded on virtue and despotisms founded on fear, to Benjamin Constant's contrast between the "liberty of the ancients" (collective political participation) and the "liberty of the moderns" (individual independence). Spencer's innovation was to ground the distinction not in political philosophy but in a sociological analysis of how different modes of subsistence — war-making versus peaceful industry — generate systematically different institutional structures.

Spencer's typology also responded to the rise of "New Liberalism" and socialism in late Victorian Britain. By the 1880s, the Liberal Party was increasingly advocating state intervention in economic and social life — factory regulation, public education, poor relief. Spencer saw this as a reversion to the militant type under a new guise, which he called "the New Toryism." The industrial-type concept was thus both an analytical tool and a polemical weapon: by showing that liberty and limited government were not contingent political preferences but structural requirements of an industrial society, Spencer sought to delegitimate the expansion of state power.

## Critiques and Counterarguments

The militant-industrial typology has been criticized on several grounds. First, critics argue that it is overly dichotomous: real societies exhibit mixtures of both types, and Spencer's own examples (such as England) showed elements of both. Spencer acknowledged this, treating the types as ideal-typical endpoints on a continuum, but his rhetoric often suggested a sharper opposition.

Second, the typology has been accused of historical determinism. Spencer's claim that the industrial type naturally supersedes the militant type as warfare declines has been challenged by the persistence of large military establishments and authoritarian governments in industrialized societies. Twentieth-century totalitarianism — which combined industrial technology with militant social organization — poses a particularly sharp challenge to Spencer's evolutionary optimism.

Third, the sharp association of industrialism with liberty has been questioned by later scholars who note that industrial capitalism has coexisted with various forms of authoritarianism, and that the regime of contract can itself become a form of coercion when bargaining power is radically unequal.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

- **[[militant-type]]**: The opposing type in Spencer's social typology, characterized by compulsory cooperation and the regime of status.
- **[[social-evolution]]**: The broader theoretical framework within which the militant-industrial typology is situated. Spencer saw the transition from militancy to industrialism as the central trajectory of social evolution.
- **[[laissez-faire]]**: The policy doctrine associated with industrial-type social organization, advocating minimal state interference in economic and social life.
