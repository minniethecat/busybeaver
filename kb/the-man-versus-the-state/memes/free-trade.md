## Definition

**Free trade**, in Herbert Spencer's usage, is the principle that commercial exchange between individuals (and, by extension, between nations) should proceed without state-imposed restrictions, tariffs, or regulatory interventions. Spencer treats free trade not merely as an economic policy preference but as a direct application of the **law of equal freedom** to the economic sphere: every individual has the right to exchange the products of their labor with whomever they choose, on whatever terms are mutually agreed.

Spencer's distinctive contribution to free trade discourse is his insistence that what is conventionally called "protection" is, in reality, **aggression**. A tariff that prevents B from buying from C in order to protect A's profits is, for Spencer, an act of coercion against B — a violation of the law of equal freedom no less than physical assault.

> For nothing can be more certain than that if, to maintain A's profit, B is forbidden to buy of C, or is fined to the extent of the duty if he buys of C, then B is aggressed upon that A may be "protected."

## Key Arguments

Spencer's case for free trade is built on both principled and empirical grounds. The **principled argument** derives from his first principle of ethics. The law of equal freedom entails that each individual may exercise their faculties as they see fit, provided they do not infringe the equal freedom of others. Commercial exchange — the voluntary transfer of goods and services — is a paradigmatic exercise of individual liberty. State interference with exchange is therefore a prima facie violation of rights.

The **empirical argument** draws on historical evidence. Spencer traces the parallel development of commercial liberty and general liberty in English history. Under Edward III, exporting staple goods was "a capital crime." Over subsequent centuries, as commercial restrictions were gradually lifted, general political liberty expanded correspondingly. The correlation, Spencer argues, is not coincidental: the habits of voluntary cooperation cultivated by free exchange generate the psychological dispositions — independence, respect for others' rights, resistance to arbitrary authority — that sustain political freedom.

> We shall find that from the days when exportation was a capital crime, down to our own free-trade era, there has been a constant ratio kept between the stringency of mercantile restraints and the stringency of other restraints, as there has also between the increase of commercial liberty and the increase of general liberty.

Spencer extends the free trade principle beyond goods to encompass services, including **medicine and education**. He rejects the argument that the state must regulate medical practice because "ignorant people cannot distinguish good treatment from bad." This paternalistic logic, he observes, is identical to the logic that justified every historical trade restriction and every established priesthood. The proper remedy for ignorance is the diffusion of knowledge through voluntary means, not state compulsion.

In his discussion of **currency and banking**, Spencer makes the case for free trade in money. He contrasts the heavily regulated English banking system — which had suffered repeated crises — with Scotland's "wholly uncontrolled system" of nearly two centuries, which had operated with "scarcely a single failure." The implication is that even in a domain as seemingly technical as money, voluntary arrangements outperform state management.

## Historical Context

Free trade was the defining political-economic issue of nineteenth-century Britain. The repeal of the Corn Laws in 1846, achieved by the Anti-Corn Law League led by Richard Cobden and John Bright, marked the triumph of free trade principles. Spencer wrote in the aftermath of this victory but perceived a dangerous reversal: the regulatory legislation of the 1860s–1880s (factory acts, public health acts, education acts) represented, in his view, a new form of protectionism directed at domestic conduct rather than foreign goods.

The Cobden Club's 1880 essay prize — which Spencer criticizes for describing "the truth of Free Trade is clouded over by the laissez-faire fallacy" — demonstrated to him that even professed free traders had abandoned the principle's philosophical foundations in favor of pragmatic state intervention.
