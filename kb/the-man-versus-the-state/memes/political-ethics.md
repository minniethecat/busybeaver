## Definition

**Political ethics** is the branch of moral philosophy concerned with the principles that ought to govern the conduct of rulers, legislators, and governmental institutions towards the governed. For Herbert Spencer, political ethics was not an autonomous domain with its own sui generis principles but an application of the same fundamental moral law — the law of equal freedom — that governs relations between individuals. In a pivotal passage in *The Man Versus the State*, Spencer argued that "political ethics, originally identical with the ethics of war, must long remain akin to them; and can diverge from them only as warlike activities and preparations become less." This thesis — that the morality which governments apply internally to their own citizens is historically continuous with the morality they apply externally to enemies — was one of Spencer's most provocative contributions to political thought.

> An obvious implication is that political ethics, originally identical with the ethics of war, must long remain akin to them; and can diverge from them only as warlike activities and preparations become less.

## Key Arguments

**The martial origin of political ethics**. Spencer traced the ethics of government to the necessities of military organisation. "To make an efficient fighting community, must the citizens be subordinate to their government. They must furnish recruits to the extent demanded, and yield up whatever property is required." War-making required unconditional command and unconditional obedience — the suppression of individual will for collective survival. This primitive political ethics, Spencer argued, persisted long after the conditions that produced it had receded. Even in peacetime, the habit of command learned in war coloured governmental dealings with citizens: the state continued to treat individuals as means to collective ends rather than as ends in themselves.

**The persistence of military ethics in domestic legislation**. Spencer documented the survival of military-style political ethics in the peacetime legislation of his own era. Conscription on the Continent meant that "the citizen is free only when his services as a soldier are not demanded; and during the rest of his life he is largely enslaved in supporting the military organization." Even in Britain, where conscription did not exist, Spencer argued that "a serious war would, by the necessitated conscription, suspend the liberties of large numbers and trench on the liberties of the rest, by taking from them through taxes whatever supplies were needed — that is, forcing them to labour so many days more for the State." The fiscal apparatus of the modern state — taxation — was, in Spencer's analysis, the domesticated form of the military power of requisition.

**The catalogue of legislative crimes**. Spencer invoked a long historical record of legislative wrongdoing — "beginning with that of King Khufu, the stones of whose vast tomb were laid in the bloody sweat of a hundred thousand slaves toiling through long years under the lash" — to demonstrate that governments, when unchecked by the principle of equal freedom, systematically sacrificed the welfare of individuals to the interests of rulers and dominant classes. Down to his own day, Spencer pointed to "laws made in the interests of dominant classes — a list coming down in our own country to those under which there were long maintained slavery and the slave-trade, torturing nearly 40,000 negroes annually by close packing during a tropical voyage, and killing a large percentage of them, and ending with the corn-laws, by which, to ensure high rents, it had been decreed that multitudes should hunger." This historical catalogue served a philosophical purpose: it demonstrated that political ethics, unconstrained by the law of equal freedom, had no intrinsic tendency toward justice.

> I am not under the title of this article about to treat of the trespasses and the revenges for trespasses, accounts of which mainly constitute history; nor to trace the internal inequities which have ever accompanied the external inequities.

**The survival of trial by battle**. Spencer offered a striking illustration of the persistence of military ethics in domestic institutions: the survival of trial by battle in English law. Although abolished as a formal procedure, trial by battle "survives under another form: counsel being the champions and purses the weapons." Civil litigation, in this analysis, was a sublimated form of private war — its adversarial structure, its determination by resources rather than truth, and its zero-sum logic all reflecting its descent from the military ethic. The gradual civilising of legal procedure represented, for Spencer, the slow divergence of political ethics from its martial origins.

**The normative alternative**. Against the inherited military political ethics, Spencer set the principle of equal freedom as the sole legitimate basis of governmental action. Political ethics properly understood was not a relaxation of the morality of war but its replacement — the substitution of voluntary cooperation for compulsory command. The industrial type of society, characterised by contract rather than status, by persuasion rather than coercion, and by free association rather than hierarchical subordination, embodied a fundamentally different political ethics: one in which the state's sole function was the protection of individual rights.

## Historical Context

Spencer wrote amid the nineteenth-century transformation of the British state from a night-watchman model to an interventionist, regulatory apparatus. The Factory Acts, the Public Health Acts, and the expansion of state education all represented assertions of governmental authority that their proponents defended in the language of political ethics — as necessary protections of the weak against the strong. Spencer's insistence on the martial origins of political ethics was a genealogical argument designed to delegitimise this new interventionism: by revealing its descent from the command-and-submission logic of war, he sought to show that the "beneficent" legislation of his own day rested on the same ethical foundation as the tyrannies of the past.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

- **[[law-of-equal-freedom]]**: The fundamental moral principle that Spencer held should replace the inherited military political ethics as the basis of governmental conduct.
- **[[militant-type]]**: The form of social organisation in which political ethics is identical with the ethics of war — compulsory cooperation, hierarchical command, and the subordination of the individual to the collective.
- **[[industrial-type]]**: The social form toward which political ethics must evolve — characterised by voluntary cooperation, contract, and the protection of individual rights.
- **[[the-sins-of-legislators]]**: Spencer's catalogue of historical legislative wrongdoing, deployed to demonstrate the systematic failure of political ethics unconstrained by the principle of equal freedom.
- **[[despotism]]**: The purest expression of military political ethics in domestic governance, in which the ruler's will is law and the subject possesses no rights against the state.
