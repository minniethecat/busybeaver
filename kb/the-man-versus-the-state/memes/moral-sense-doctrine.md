## Definition

The **moral sense doctrine** is the theory that human beings possess an innate psychological faculty — the "moral sense" — which disposes them toward ethically upright conduct. Herbert Spencer defends this doctrine in the opening chapters of *Social Statics* (1851) as part of his broader naturalistic ethics. He positions the moral sense as one among a class of "prompters" or desires that guide human behavior toward ends beneficial to the individual and the species.

Spencer's central analogy is between the moral sense and physical instincts. Just as the desires for food, sleep, and warmth are "irresistible, and quite independent of foreseen advantages," so too, he argues, there exist "kindred instrumentalities prompting the conduct called moral." The moral sense is not a supernatural endowment but an evolved psychological adaptation — a product of the same natural processes that produce all other human faculties.

> Answering to each of the actions which it is requisite for us to perform, we find in ourselves some prompter called a desire; and the more essential the action, the more powerful is the impulse to its performance, and the more intense the gratification derived therefrom.

## Key Arguments

Spencer's defense of the moral sense proceeds through several interconnected arguments. First, he deploys an **argument from analogy**. All other domains of vital activity — nutrition, reproduction, social bonding — are governed by instinctual prompters rather than rational calculation. It would be anomalous, Spencer contends, if moral conduct alone were left to conscious deliberation. "May we not then reasonably expect to find kindred instrumentalities prompting the conduct called moral?"

Second, Spencer addresses the **objection from moral diversity**. Critics of the moral sense doctrine point to the wide variation in moral beliefs across cultures — the Thug who regards assassination as a religious act, the Egyptian who takes pride in successful lying. Spencer responds that this variation is precisely what an evolutionary theory predicts: the moral sense, like any adaptive faculty, develops gradually and imperfectly, shaped by the social conditions in which a population lives. Uniform moral intuitions would be evidence *against* a naturalistic account.

> In bar of this conclusion it is urged, that did there exist such an agency, men would exhibit a more manifest obedience to its supposed dictates than they do. There would be a greater uniformity of opinion as to the rightness or wrongness of actions.

Third, Spencer connects the moral sense to his **law of adaptation**. Just as organisms physically adapt to their environments over generations, human moral intuitions adapt to the requirements of social life. The sense of justice, the impulse toward truth-telling, and the aversion to unnecessary harm are not arbitrary cultural conventions but accumulated adaptations to the conditions of cooperative existence. The moral sense is thus both innate (as a faculty) and malleable (in its specific content).

Spencer distinguishes his position from both pure rationalism (which treats morality as a matter of deductive reasoning) and pure empiricism (which treats morality as entirely learned). The moral sense provides the *motive force* for ethical behavior; reason provides the *content* by clarifying the implications of the law of equal freedom.

## Historical Context

The moral sense doctrine has roots in eighteenth-century British philosophy, particularly the work of Shaftesbury, Hutcheson, and Hume, who argued for an innate "moral sense" against Hobbesian egoism and Lockean empiricism. Spencer's contribution was to recast the doctrine in evolutionary terms, drawing on Lamarckian biology to explain how moral intuitions could be both innate and historically contingent.

Spencer's chief polemical target in *Social Statics* was Benthamite utilitarianism, which he regarded as philosophically incoherent. Bentham and his followers treated moral judgment as a calculation of pleasures and pains, denying any innate moral faculty. Spencer argued that utilitarianism could not explain *why* people feel moral approval or disapproval — it could only provide a post-hoc rationalization.
