## Definition

**Laissez-faire** (from the French "let do" or "let alone") is the doctrine that government should refrain from interfering in economic and social life beyond the enforcement of contracts and the protection of individuals against force and fraud. For Herbert Spencer, laissez-faire was not merely a policy preference but a structural imperative of industrial society: just as the militant type of social organization required centralized command and compulsory cooperation, the industrial type required the free play of voluntary contract and individual initiative. Any departure from laissez-faire, Spencer argued, represented a reversion toward the militant type and its regime of status.

Spencer lamented that by the 1880s, laissez-faire had come to be spoken of "as an exploded doctrine," and that "people are no longer frightened at the thought of socialism." In *The Man Versus the State* (1884), he set out to vindicate the principle by showing that the alternative — expanding state interference — led logically to the erosion of liberty and the re-establishment of a command society.

> They speak of laissez-faire as an exploded doctrine. "People are no longer frightened at the thought of socialism," is the statement which meets us one day.

## Key Arguments

Spencer's defense of laissez-faire rests on a sociological foundation rather than on narrow economic efficiency.

First, **laissez-faire as the political expression of industrialism**. Spencer argued that the historical transition from militancy to industrialism had a political correlate: the shift from Tory paternalism (which treated the subject as a dependent of the ruler) to classical Liberalism (which treated the citizen as a self-owning individual free to contract). The key legislative achievements of Liberalism — the Habeas Corpus Act, the independence of the judiciary, the Bill of Rights — all had "the same intrinsic nature": they "diminished compulsory co-operation throughout social life and increased voluntary co-operation." Laissez-faire was the policy principle that generalized this logic from constitutional law to economic and social life.

Second, **the structural alignment thesis**. Spencer's distinctive contribution was to argue that laissez-faire was not an ideological preference but a functional requirement. An industrial society organized around voluntary exchange could not thrive under the same institutional logic as a military society organized around command. The complexity and interdependence of industrial production — with its elaborate division of labor and intricate networks of contract — could not be effectively directed from above. Spencer pointed to the failure of state-directed enterprises (such as the Glasgow Improvement Trust's model housing) as evidence that government could not replicate the efficiency of voluntary market coordination.

Third, **the ethical argument from equal liberty**. Spencer's "law of equal freedom" — that "every man has freedom to do all that he wills, provided he infringes not the equal freedom of any other man" — implies that the proper sphere of government is limited to enforcing this equal liberty. Any interference beyond this boundary violates the rights of individuals by compelling them to act (or forbidding them from acting) in ways that do not infringe the equal freedom of others.

Fourth, **the psychological consequences of laissez-faire's abandonment**. Spencer warned that the rejection of laissez-faire produced a population that looked to the state rather than to its own efforts for the amelioration of its condition. This erosion of self-reliance was, for Spencer, a form of moral regression that would ultimately make voluntary social cooperation impossible.

## Historical Context

The phrase "laissez-faire" is traditionally attributed to the French merchant Legendre, who reportedly told Jean-Baptiste Colbert (Louis XIV's finance minister) in the 1680s: "Laissez-nous faire" — "Let us alone." The doctrine was systematized by the Physiocrats in eighteenth-century France and reached its classic formulation in Adam Smith's *The Wealth of Nations* (1776). In Britain, the movement to repeal the Corn Laws (1846), led by Richard Cobden and John Bright, represented the political high-water mark of laissez-faire.

By the time Spencer wrote *The Man Versus the State* in 1884, the tide had turned. The Factory Acts, public health legislation, compulsory education, and municipal enterprise had all expanded the role of the state far beyond its classical liberal boundaries. Spencer saw the Liberal Party's embrace of this interventionism as a betrayal of its own principles — a "New Toryism" that differed from the old only in being motivated by altruism rather than class interest.

## Critiques and Counterarguments

Laissez-faire has faced sustained criticism from multiple directions. First, the historical record suggests that the period of "laissez-faire" in nineteenth-century Britain was far less doctrinaire than Spencer's rhetoric implied: the state was actively involved in enclosure, colonization, public health, and the enforcement of poor laws throughout the century.

Second, critics argue that the "equal liberty" principle provides no clear criterion for distinguishing legitimate from illegitimate state action. Public goods, externalities, information asymmetries, and bargaining-power inequalities all complicate the simple model of voluntary exchange that laissez-faire presupposes.

Third, the democratic objection: if an enfranchised population consistently votes for state intervention, on what grounds can laissez-faire theorists claim that such intervention is illegitimate? Spencer's answer — that majorities can be as tyrannical as kings — was consistent with his philosophy but placed him in opposition to the democratic currents of his age.
