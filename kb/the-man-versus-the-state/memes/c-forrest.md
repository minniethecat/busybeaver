## Biography

C. Forrest (fl. 1840s–1880s) was a builder and contractor based at Museum Works, 17 Victoria Park Square, Bethnal Green, in London's East End. With forty years of experience in the building trade, he held multiple local offices — church warden, member of the vestry, and member of the board of guardians — giving him extensive knowledge of local public affairs in addition to his practical expertise in construction.

Herbert Spencer personally consulted Forrest when preparing his chapter "The Sins of Legislators" for *The Man Versus the State*. Forrest authorized Spencer to publish his name and his testimony, lending concrete, first-hand evidence to Spencer's argument that legislation consistently produced consequences opposite to those intended.

Forrest represents a type of figure that Spencer valued highly: the practical man of business whose direct experience of legislative effects provided a corrective to the abstract theories of parliamentary reformers.

## Key Ideas

### Building Regulations as a Tax on the Poor

Forrest's expert testimony documented how building regulations, timber duties, and the window tax combined to inflate the cost of working-class housing. Spencer reproduced Forrest's evidence alongside that of "Architect" and C. Gatliff to construct a multi-source indictment of legislative interference in the housing market:

> "Mr. Forrest, who authorizes me to give his name, verifies the foregoing statements, with the exception of one which he strengthens. He says that 'Architect' understates the evil entailed by the definition of 'a fourth-rate house'; since the dimensions are much less than those he gives."

Forrest's testimony carried particular weight because he was not a theorist but a practitioner — someone whose livelihood depended on building houses that poor people could afford, and who had watched legislation systematically undermine that possibility.

### The Ground-Rent Inflation

Forrest documented how ground-rents in Bethnal Green had risen over sixty years from £1 to £8 10s. for a fourth-rate house — an increase of 750 percent. This inflation, combined with other legislative burdens, had obliged him to abandon plans for industrial dwellings he had intended to build:

> "Besides illustrating the bad effects of great increase in ground-rents (in sixty years from £1 to £8 10s. for a fourth-rate house) which, joined with other causes, had obliged him to abandon plans for industrial dwellings he had intended to build..."

The ground-rent case illustrated Spencer's broader argument: legislation creates a chain of unintended consequences that compounds over time, making the poor poorer and reducing the supply of affordable housing — the very opposite of legislative intent.

### Prohibitory Local Imposts

Forrest identified an additional burden on small-house construction: local authorities charged the cost of pavement, roadway, and sewerage according to the length of frontage, which "bears a far larger ratio to the value of a small house than to the value of a large one." He termed these charges "prohibitory imposts." This regressive cost structure meant that the poorest housing carried the heaviest relative burden of local taxation — a perverse outcome that Spencer cited as typical of legislative intervention.

## Major Works

Forrest left no published works. His testimony survives solely through Herbert Spencer's *The Man Versus the State*, where his forty years of practical experience in the building trade serve as factual foundation for Spencer's critique of housing regulation.

## Influence / Legacy

C. Forrest exemplifies the role of the expert informant in Victorian social investigation. Like the factory inspectors and poor-law commissioners whose reports filled parliamentary blue books, Forrest provided the raw data from which political arguments were constructed. His case is particularly noteworthy because Spencer, rather than relying on official reports, went directly to a working builder — demonstrating a commitment to empirical verification that distinguished *The Man Versus the State* from more abstract political treatises.
