## Biography

Richard Cobden (1804–1865) was an English manufacturer, Radical politician, and one of the most influential advocates of free trade in British history. Born in Dunford, Sussex, the son of a farmer, Cobden was sent to a dame school and later worked as a clerk before establishing himself as a calico printer in Manchester. His commercial experience in the industrial north gave him an intimate understanding of the burdens imposed by the Corn Laws — the protectionist tariffs on grain that raised the price of bread for working families while enriching landowners.

Cobden entered Parliament as MP for Stockport in 1841, but his national reputation was already established through his leadership of the Anti-Corn Law League, founded in 1838 alongside John Bright. The League was the most powerful extra-parliamentary political movement of the era — a disciplined campaign of public meetings, pamphlets, petitions, and parliamentary pressure that culminated in the repeal of the Corn Laws by Sir Robert Peel in 1846. Peel himself acknowledged Cobden as the man whose arguments had prevailed.

After the triumph of free trade, Cobden devoted his energies to the cause of international peace, negotiating the Cobden-Chevalier commercial treaty between Britain and France in 1860. He opposed the Crimean War, argued for non-intervention in foreign affairs, and believed that free commerce between nations would make war economically irrational. Herbert Spencer invokes Cobden's name in a characteristic passage about the logical limits of state regulation: if Parliament is to regulate the hours and conditions of some workers, why should it not regulate all? Spencer writes:

> "Why not, as Mr. Cobden asks, consider the cases of the glass-blowers, the nightmen, the iron-founders, the Sheffield knife-grinders, and indeed all other classes, including the hardworked M.P.'s themselves?"

This *reductio ad absurdum* perfectly captures the Cobdenite argument against selective state intervention: once the principle of regulation is conceded, its application has no principled boundary.

## Key Ideas

### Free Trade as a Moral Principle

Cobden's free trade advocacy was not merely an economic calculation but a moral conviction. He held that freedom of exchange is a natural right of individuals — that the state has no legitimate authority to tax one man's bread in order to enrich another's rent. Free trade, in Cobden's view, would cheapen food, stimulate industry, and bind nations together in peaceful commerce. Spencer shared this conviction and viewed the repeal of the Corn Laws as one of the great victories of liberal principle over class legislation. The Anti-Corn Law League demonstrated that organised public opinion, not parliamentary intrigue, could achieve fundamental reform.

### Opposition to Factory Legislation

Cobden consistently opposed legislation that sought to regulate the hours or conditions of adult workers. He regarded such interference as a violation of the liberty of contract — adults, he argued, are capable of judging their own interests and should be free to sell their labour on whatever terms they choose. Spencer quotes Cobden's characteristically sharp question about the logical endpoint of factory legislation: if Parliament claims the right to regulate the hours of factory operatives, why should it not also regulate glass-blowers, miners, knife-grinders, nightmen — indeed "all other classes, including the hardworked M.P.'s themselves"? This question, for Spencer, exposed the fundamental arbitrariness of selective intervention.

### The Cobden Club and the Betrayal of Laissez-Faire

The Cobden Club, founded in 1866 to perpetuate Cobden's principles, became in Spencer's lifetime a symbol of the intellectual confusion of the new liberalism. Spencer notes with evident dismay that "in the essay to which the Cobden Club awarded its prize in 1880, there occurs the assertion that 'the truth of Free Trade is clouded over by the laissez-faire fallacy.'" For Spencer, this was a profound irony: an institution created to honour Cobden's legacy was now publishing essays that disparaged the very principle — laissez-faire — upon which free trade logically depends. The Cobden Club had, in Spencer's view, forgotten that free trade in commerce cannot be consistently maintained while repudiating free trade in principle.

### Peace Through Commerce

Cobden believed that free trade was the surest path to international peace. Tariffs and trade barriers bred suspicion, rivalry, and ultimately war; free commerce created mutual interest and mutual understanding among nations. He devoted the last two decades of his life to this cause, opposing British militarism and imperialism and arguing for arbitration as an alternative to war. Spencer — who held that industrial societies are inherently more peaceful than militant ones — saw in Cobden's career a practical demonstration of the pacifying effects of commercial civilization, and his argument that free trade in goods leads naturally to free exchange of ideas and mutual respect between peoples.

## Major Works

- **England, Ireland, and America** (1835) — Cobden's first political pamphlet, arguing for free trade, non-intervention in foreign affairs, and a conciliatory approach to Ireland.
- **Russia** (1836) — A sequel pamphlet warning against the anti-Russian panic of the time and arguing for peaceful international relations.
- **1793 and 1853, in Three Letters** (1853) — Pamphlet urging Britain to avoid involvement in the Crimean War, comparing the situation to the wars of the French Revolution.
- **Speeches on Questions of Public Policy** (1870, posthumous) — Collected parliamentary and platform speeches edited by John Bright and J. E. Thorold Rogers, documenting Cobden's public career.
