## Definition

Positive law, in the jurisprudential sense examined by Herbert Spencer in *Man Versus the State* (1884), is law whose validity depends not on its conformity to principles of justice but solely on its enactment by a recognized sovereign authority. The term is associated principally with John Austin, the nineteenth-century English legal philosopher whose *Province of Jurisprudence Determined* (1832) provided the canonical statement of legal positivism in the Anglophone tradition.

For Austin, the sovereign — whether a single ruler, an aristocracy, or a democratic majority — possesses unlimited authority over the community. Law is simply the command of that sovereign, backed by the threat of sanction. The justice or injustice of the command is irrelevant to its status as law.

## Spencer's Critique of Austin

Spencer mounts a fundamental challenge to Austin's positivism. He observes that Austin, a former army officer, "assimilates civil authority to military authority; taking for granted that the one, as the other, is above question in respect of both origin and range." The model of law as command assumes an unquestionable hierarchy, but it provides no account of why the hierarchy itself is justified.

Austin's argument, Spencer contends, merely relocates the problem without solving it. To derive the validity of positive law from unlimited sovereignty, and then to assert unlimited sovereignty as a given, is to "move a step further back and there left unsolved" the essential question. Spencer's formulation of the challenge is direct: "We will dispense with your process of deriving positive law from unlimited sovereignty: the sequence is obvious enough. But first prove your unlimited sovereignty."

## The Missing Warrant

Spencer's objection is that Austin can offer no warrant for the unlimited sovereignty on which his entire system depends. In the absence of "admitted divine descent or appointment," neither a single ruler nor a ruling body can demonstrate why its commands ought to be obeyed without question. Austin's system is, in effect, a secularized divine right of kings — the form of theological justification has been removed, but the substance of unlimited authority remains.

The practical consequence, Spencer warns, is that positive-law theory provides a ready-made justification for any government action, however intrusive. If law is simply whatever the sovereign commands, and if the sovereign is simply whoever holds effective power, then there is no principled limit to what the state may do. The theory is an intellectual enabler of legislative overreach.

## Austin's Concession

Spencer notes an important tension in Austin's own position. Despite his insistence on the unlimited sovereignty of the law-making body, Austin is compelled, "in the last resort, to admit a moral limit to its action over the community." Austin allows that "a government may be hindered by positive morality from abridging their political liberty," even though legally it could do so. For Spencer, this concession is fatal: it acknowledges the very distinction between legal validity and moral legitimacy that Austin's theory was designed to collapse.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

- [[law-of-nature]] — Spencer's counter-concept to positive law; where positive law derives from command, the law of nature derives from moral recognition.
- [[divine-right]] — Positive law is, in Spencer's analysis, the secularized form of divine-right doctrine; both rest on an unargued premise of unlimited sovereign authority.
- [[sovereignty]] — Austin's conception of unlimited sovereignty is the foundation of positive law and the primary target of Spencer's critique.
- [[rights]] — In positive-law theory, rights are created by the sovereign and conferred on subjects; in Spencer's theory, rights are natural, pre-political, and recognized, not created, by law.
