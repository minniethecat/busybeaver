## Definition

Divine right, in Herbert Spencer's usage, denotes the claim that political authority flows from a supernatural source, rendering the commands of the ruler or ruling body beyond rational challenge. In *Social Statics* (1851) and *Man Versus the State* (1884), Spencer traces a historical transformation of this doctrine: from the "divine right of kings" — the assertion that the monarch is God's delegate on Earth — to the "divine right of parliaments" and ultimately the "divine right of majorities."

Spencer does not merely reject divine-right claims as factually false. He argues that the doctrine is a *superstition* — a belief retained after its original warrant has collapsed. The oil of anointing, as he puts it, has "unawares dripped from the head of the one on to the heads of the many, and given sacredness to them also and to their decrees."

## The Divine Right of Kings

In *Social Statics*, Spencer traces the logic of the divine right of kings to Sir Robert Filmer's dogma that "men are not naturally free." If men have no natural rights, then rights can only be conferred by a special divine grant — and the recipient of that grant is the monarch. From this premise, divine right follows as "an easy step."

Spencer observes that the doctrine, in practice, conferred legitimacy on whoever could seize power: "since, according to its assertors, no man can be supposed to occupy the position of supreme ruler in opposition to the will of the deity, it follows that whoever attains to that position, whether by fair means or by foul, be he legitimate or be he usurper, has divine authority on his side." The divine right of kings was thus, in its real operation, the divine right of any one who could get uppermost.

## The Great Political Superstition

The centerpiece of Spencer's analysis appears in the essay "The Great Political Superstition" (*Man Versus the State*, 1884). He opens with a memorable formulation:

> "The great political superstition of the past was the divine right of kings. The great political superstition of the present is the divine right of parliaments."

Spencer grants that the older belief was at least internally consistent. If one accepts that the king is a god, a descendant of a god, or divinely appointed, then unlimited obedience to his will follows logically. But for the modern belief in parliamentary supremacy, "such a warrant does not exist." Legislatures make no pretense to divine descent or divine appointment; they can show "no supernatural justification for [their] claim to unlimited authority; and no natural justification has ever been attempted."

The result is a doctrine more incoherent than its predecessor: unlimited authority is claimed without the theological premise that once justified it.

## The Divine Right of Majorities

Spencer extends the argument to democratic majoritarianism. The expediency-philosophy, he writes, "merely modifies 'divine right of kings' into 'divine right of majorities.' It is despotism democratized." The subordination of the minority to the majority is placed beyond question, not by appeal to God, but by appeal to utility — yet the practical result is the same: the individual possesses rights "only by sufferance of parliament."

This is the intermediate stage in Spencer's historical schema. Between the old regime, under which the citizen was the private property of his ruler and had no rights at all, and the final regime, under which individual rights will be "entire and inviolable," comes the present phase: the citizen is "allowed to possess rights, but only by sufferance of parliament."

## Bentham and the Paradox of Popular Sovereignty

Spencer identifies a deep logical problem in the utilitarian version of divine right. Bentham, who wrote in the interests of popular rule and fixed sovereignty in "the whole people," simultaneously held that government fulfills its office "by creating rights which it confers upon individuals." Spencer exposes the circularity:

"The sovereign people jointly appoint representatives, and so create a government; the government thus created, creates rights; and then, having created rights, it confers them on the separate members of the sovereign people by which it was itself created. Here is a marvellous piece of political legerdemain!"

The paradox is that a people said to possess original sovereignty are then told that their rights are gifts of the government they themselves constituted. Either rights precede government, or they do not; the utilitarian attempt to have it both ways collapses into incoherence.

## The Only Legitimate Subordination

Spencer does not deny that subordination of minority to majority has a legitimate domain. The defense of each citizen against internal and external invaders requires collective action, and for the maintenance of this "vital principle, alike of individual life and social life," subordination to the majority is justified — but only insofar as it trenches on individual freedom and property no more than is requisite for protecting that same freedom and property.

Beyond this protective function, "such subordination is not legitimate." An aggression upon the individual greater than is required for his protection is a breach of the very principle it purports to uphold. The assumed divine right of parliaments and the implied divine right of majorities are, in Spencer's final judgment, "superstitions."
