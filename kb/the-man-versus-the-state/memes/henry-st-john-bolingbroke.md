## Biography

Henry St John, 1st Viscount Bolingbroke (1678–1751) was an English politician, diplomat, and political writer. A leading Tory minister under Queen Anne, he fled to France after the Hanoverian succession and was attainted for treason, later returning to England. His political writings, particularly *A Dissertation upon Parties* (1733–1734) and *The Idea of a Patriot King* (1738), exercised a lasting influence on eighteenth- and nineteenth-century constitutional thought.

## Key Ideas

### The Whig–Tory Distinction

Bolingbroke's most significant contribution to Spencer's argument was his analysis of the ideological contrast between Whig and Tory principles. In *The Man Versus the State* ("The New Toryism"), Spencer quoted Bolingbroke at length from *A Dissertation upon Parties*:

> "The power and majesty of the people, and original contract, the authority and independency of Parliaments, liberty, resistance, exclusion, abdication, deposition; these were ideas associated, at that time, to the idea of a Whig, and supposed by every Whig to be incommunicable, and inconsistent with the idea of a Tory. Divine, hereditary, indefeasible right, lineal succession, passive obedience, prerogative, non-resistance, slavery, nay, and sometimes popery too, were associated in many minds to the idea of a Tory, and deemed incommunicable and inconsistent, in the same manner, with the idea of a Whig."

Spencer deployed this passage to identify the essential character of the two parties: "in the one party there was a desire to resist and decrease the coercive power of the ruler over the subject, and in the other party to maintain or increase his coercive power." This distinction transcended "all other political distinctions," providing Spencer with the conceptual framework for his central thesis — that the Liberal Party of his own day had abandoned its Whig heritage and embraced a "New Toryism" of expanded state compulsion.

### From Historical Analysis to Contemporary Critique

Bolingbroke's framework allowed Spencer to argue that the true dividing line in politics was not between parties conventionally understood, but between the principles of voluntary and compulsory co-operation. Spencer cited the Habeas Corpus Act, the independence of the judiciary, and the Bill of Rights as historical enactments of Whig principles that weakened compulsory co-operation, contrasting them with the factory acts, compulsory education, and state regulation of his own day.

## Relationship to Spencer

Spencer treated Bolingbroke as an authoritative diagnostician of the fundamental political divide. Bolingbroke's analysis, written some fifty years before Spencer's own, served as the historical anchor for *The Man Versus the State*'s most provocative claim: that contemporary Liberals had become the new Tories.

## Major Works

- *A Dissertation upon Parties* (1733–1734) — Analysis of the Whig–Tory party division
- *The Idea of a Patriot King* (1738) — Constitutional treatise on monarchical authority
- *Letters on the Study and Use of History* (1752) — Reflections on historical method
