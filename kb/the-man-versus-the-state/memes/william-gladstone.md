## Biography

William Ewart Gladstone (1809–1898) was a British statesman and Liberal politician who served as Prime Minister four times between 1868 and 1894 — a record unmatched in British political history. Born in Liverpool to a wealthy merchant family, Gladstone was educated at Eton and Christ Church, Oxford, where he achieved a double first in classics and mathematics. He entered Parliament in 1832 as a Tory, but his views evolved over a long career toward Liberalism, free trade, and — controversially — Irish Home Rule.

Gladstone's first ministry (1868–1874) was one of the most legislatively active in British history, passing reforms in education, the army, the civil service, and the Irish church. Herbert Spencer, writing in *The Man Versus the State* (1884), surveyed Gladstone's legislative record as a case study in the accelerating expansion of state power. Spencer listed the measures introduced under Gladstone's government — state telegraphy, the Irish Land Act, the Education Act, expanded public works — as evidence that the Liberal Party, despite its name, had abandoned the principle of limited government in favour of a "new Toryism" of state intervention.

Spencer's critique of Gladstone was not personal but structural. Gladstone was, for Spencer, the personification of a transformation in British political culture: the belief that Acts of Parliament could remedy every social ill — a belief that Spencer saw as the modern equivalent of the mercantilist dogma that "money is wealth."

## Key Ideas

### Gladstone's Legislative Program and the Expanding State

Spencer documented Gladstone's legislative record as evidence of a fundamental shift in the character of British government. Under Gladstone's first ministry (1868–1874), the state acquired new powers over telegraphy (1869), land relations in Ireland (1870), elementary education (1870), and public health administration. Each of these measures, Spencer argued, transferred functions from private initiative to state control, diminishing the sphere of individual responsibility.

Spencer noted the irony that these expansions of state power were enacted by a Liberal government. "Passing now to the legislation under the first Ministry of Mr. Gladstone," Spencer wrote, "we have, in 1869, the establishment of State-telegraphy, with the accompanying interdict on telegraphing through any other agency." The Liberal Party, once the party of individual liberty and limited government, had become the vehicle for a "new Toryism" — the use of state power to achieve social ends by compulsion rather than voluntary cooperation.

### The Memorial to Gladstone and the Demand for Further Intervention

Spencer cited a memorial addressed to Gladstone by a highly influential meeting of peers, members of Parliament, and other public figures, chaired by Lord Lyttelton. The memorial called for further legislative action on social issues, illustrating for Spencer the self-reinforcing dynamic of state expansion: each new intervention generates demands for yet more intervention, as people come to believe that every problem is susceptible to a legislative solution.

The memorial, Spencer observed, exemplified the "political superstition" — the quasi-religious faith in the omnipotence of legislation — that he had identified as the defining ideology of the age. Gladstone's willingness to receive and act upon such memorials demonstrated, in Spencer's view, how thoroughly the assumptions of unlimited state authority had penetrated even the party of liberty.

### Liberalism Transformed

Gladstone's career embodied the transformation of nineteenth-century Liberalism from a doctrine of individual freedom and limited government into a doctrine of reform through state action. Spencer, who considered himself the true heir of classical liberalism, saw Gladstone's "new Liberalism" as a betrayal of liberal principles. The test of a true liberal, Spencer argued, was not the ends he pursued — education, public health, temperance — but the means he employed. Liberty consists in the absence of coercion; a policy that pursues desirable ends through coercion remains illiberal regardless of its intentions.

## Major Works

- Gladstone's extensive published writings include works on theology, classical literature, and politics, but his primary "work" was his legislative program — the body of statutes enacted under his ministries that Spencer analyzed in *The Man Versus the State*.
- Spencer's documentation of Gladstone's legislation appears in *The Man Versus the State* (1884), ch. 2 ("The Coming Slavery"), where Gladstone's measures are presented as evidence of the new Toryism.

## Relationship to Other Figures

- [[herbert-spencer]] — Spencer subjected Gladstone's legislative program to sustained critique in *The Man Versus the State*, arguing that the expansion of state power under Gladstone betrayed classical liberal principles.
- [[lord-palmerston]] — Palmerston, Gladstone's predecessor and rival, represented an earlier phase of Liberal politics. Spencer contrasts Palmerston's relatively restrained approach with Gladstone's interventionism.
- [[richard-cobden]] — Cobden, the leader of the Anti-Corn Law League, represented the classical liberalism of free trade and non-intervention that Spencer admired. Gladstone was influenced by Cobden but, in Spencer's view, departed from his principles.
- [[john-bright]] — Bright, Cobden's ally and a leading Radical, shared Spencer's suspicion of state expansion and opposed much of Gladstone's interventionist program.
