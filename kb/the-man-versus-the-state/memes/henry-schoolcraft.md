## Biography

Henry Rowe Schoolcraft (1793–1864) was an American geographer, geologist, and ethnologist, best known for his discovery of the source of the Mississippi River in 1832 and for his extensive studies of Native American cultures. Born in Guilderland, New York, Schoolcraft studied at Union College and Middlebury College before embarking on a career that combined scientific exploration with ethnographic documentation.

In 1820, Schoolcraft joined the Cass Expedition to explore the Upper Mississippi and the Great Lakes region. In 1832, he led a second expedition that successfully identified Lake Itasca in present-day Minnesota as the true source of the Mississippi. His account of these expeditions, *Narrative of an Expedition Through the Upper Mississippi to Itasca Lake* (1834), became a foundational text of American geographical exploration.

Schoolcraft later served as Indian agent at Sault Ste. Marie, Michigan (1822–1841), where he married Jane Johnston, the daughter of an Irish fur trader and an Ojibwe woman. Jane's knowledge of Ojibwe language and oral tradition deeply influenced Schoolcraft's later ethnographic work. His multi-volume *Historical and Statistical Information Respecting the History, Condition and Prospects of the Indian Tribes of the United States* (1851–1857) remains a significant, if imperfect, record of nineteenth-century Native American life.

## Key Ideas

### Property Rights Before Government

Herbert Spencer cited Schoolcraft's observations of the Ojibwe (whom Spencer called "Chippewayans") in *The Man Versus the State* to support a central argument of his political philosophy: that property rights exist prior to and independently of formal government. Schoolcraft noted that although the Ojibwe had "no regular government, as every man is lord in his own family," they nonetheless recognized private property as one of the key principles "which conduce to their general benefit."

For Spencer, this was crucial empirical evidence against the Benthamite view that property is "the creation of law." If a stateless people could recognize and enforce property rights through custom alone, then property was not a mere legal artifact but a natural institution arising from the requirements of cooperative social life.

### Customary Law and Social Evolution

Schoolcraft's detailed descriptions of Ojibwe social organization contributed to nineteenth-century evolutionary anthropology, which sought to understand the development of human institutions from "primitive" to "civilized" forms. Spencer used this anthropological data to argue that law does not create social order but rather formalizes customs that have already evolved organically through generations of social interaction. The common law itself, Spencer noted, was "mainly an embodiment of the customs of the realm."

### The Limits of Governmental Authority

Schoolcraft's observation that Ojibwe society functioned effectively without centralized state authority provided Spencer with a living counterexample to the assumption that government is necessary for social order. The Ojibwe managed to coordinate collective life, recognize property, and maintain social peace through custom and mutual limitation — precisely the model of spontaneous order that Spencer advocated against the expanding administrative state of Victorian Britain.

## Major Works

- *Narrative of an Expedition Through the Upper Mississippi to Itasca Lake* (1834) — Account of the expedition that identified the source of the Mississippi River
- *Algic Researches* (1839) — Collection of Native American legends and oral traditions
- *Historical and Statistical Information Respecting the History, Condition and Prospects of the Indian Tribes of the United States* (1851–1857) — Multi-volume ethnographic compendium commissioned by the U.S. Congress

## Influence / Legacy

Schoolcraft's legacy is complex. His ethnographic work preserved significant cultural knowledge about Ojibwe and other Native American peoples, and his recordings of Ojibwe oral traditions later inspired Henry Wadsworth Longfellow's *The Song of Hiawatha* (1855). However, his work was shaped by the colonial assumptions of his era, and his role as Indian agent placed him within the apparatus of U.S. expansion.

In the history of political thought, Schoolcraft's influence runs through Spencer's use of his anthropological evidence. Spencer's argument that property rights predate government — and that government's proper role is merely to recognize and enforce rights that have evolved spontaneously — drew on Schoolcraft's observations as empirical support. This argument became a cornerstone of classical liberal political philosophy in the English-speaking world.
