## Definition

Industrialism, in Herbert Spencer's sociology and political theory, denotes the type of social organization characterized by "voluntary cooperation" — the coordination of social activity through contract and exchange rather than command — and by the "regime of contract," in which rights and obligations are determined by mutual agreement rather than by birth or decree. It stands in opposition to militancy, the type based on "compulsory cooperation" and the "regime of status."

Spencer introduces the distinction in *Man Versus the State* (1884), where he traces the Tory and Whig parties to their roots in these two opposed forms of social organization. Toryism, he argues, originated in the militant type characteristic of rural, military-dominated districts; Whiggism and later Liberalism originated in the industrial type characteristic of towns "formed of workers and traders accustomed to co-operate under contract."

## Voluntary Cooperation

The defining institutional feature of industrialism is that cooperation is entered into by consent. Spencer contrasts the army — "formed of conscripts, in which the units in their several grades have to fulfil commands under pain of death, and receive food and clothing and pay, arbitrarily apportioned" — with "a body of producers or distributors, who severally agree to specified payments in return for specified services, and may at will, after due notice, leave the organization if they do not like it."

This distinction is not merely economic. It is constitutional: it concerns the fundamental relationship between the individual and the collective. Under industrialism, the individual retains the power of exit; his participation in any cooperative enterprise is conditional on its continuing to serve his interests. This structure of voluntary association, Spencer argues, is the social basis of individual liberty.

## Regime of Contract

Where the militant type assigns social position by status — fixed by birth, rank, or decree — the industrial type makes position a function of contract. Individuals enter into obligations by consent, exchange goods and services on negotiated terms, and possess rights that are not grants of the sovereign but conditions of social cooperation itself.

Spencer observes that this regime "has become general in modern days, chiefly among the Western nations, and especially among ourselves and the Americans." The historical shift from status to contract — a thesis later made famous by Sir Henry Maine — is, for Spencer, the master process of social evolution.

## Industrialism and Liberalism

Spencer's immediate political purpose in developing the distinction was to diagnose a betrayal. The Liberal party, which had historically advanced the industrial type by resisting the expansion of state power, had — in Spencer's analysis — begun to reverse course. Under the influence of Benthamite expediency, Liberals were enacting precisely the kind of compulsory interventions that characterized the militant type.

"The New Toryism," Spencer argued, was Liberalism that had forgotten its constitutional logic. The expansion of state regulation, the growth of bureaucracy, and the multiplication of compulsory interventions were reversions to militant patterns of organization — justified now not by divine right but by utility, but producing the same structural result: the subordination of individual will to central command.

## The Evolutionary Framework

Spencer embeds the contrast within a broader theory of social evolution. The militant type is adapted to chronic warfare: it requires swift, unified action under centralized command. The industrial type emerges as warfare recedes and peaceful exchange becomes the dominant mode of social interaction. Under these conditions, rigid hierarchies become maladaptive; flexibility, innovation, and individual initiative — fostered by voluntary cooperation — yield superior outcomes.

This transition is not completed, and Spencer is clear-eyed about the obstacles. "So long as the religion of enmity predominates over the religion of amity," he writes, militant habits of thought persist. The belief in unlimited governmental authority is a residue of the militant type, retained after the conditions that made it functional have passed.

## English Industrial Superiority

Spencer deploys an empirical argument for industrialism's superiority. English engineers constructed the first gas-works in Paris after a French company failed; English capital opened continental mineral fields; English engineers built bridges across Europe. This was not due to innate superiority, Spencer argues, but to "difference of discipline." Having been "left in a greater degree than others to manage their own affairs, the English people have become self-helping, and have acquired great practical ability." The "comparative helplessness of the paternally-governed nations of Europe" was a direct consequence of state superintendence — exactly the policy that England's New Tories were now eagerly adopting.
