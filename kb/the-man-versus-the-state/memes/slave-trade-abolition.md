## Background

The transatlantic slave trade was conducted by European powers from the sixteenth through the nineteenth centuries, transporting millions of Africans to the Americas under brutal conditions. Britain was the dominant slave-trading nation in the eighteenth century, with ports such as Liverpool and Bristol prospering from the traffic.

The abolitionist movement in Britain gathered force in the late eighteenth century, led by figures such as William Wilberforce, Thomas Clarkson, and Granville Sharpe. Their campaign combined religious conviction, humanitarian sentiment, and Enlightenment principles of natural rights. The movement achieved its first major legislative victory with the Slave Trade Act of 1807, which prohibited British ships from engaging in the trade. The Slavery Abolition Act of 1833 subsequently emancipated enslaved people throughout most of the British Empire.

## Description

Herbert Spencer provides a striking analysis of the British naval suppression campaign in his chapter "Unguided Expediency." The Royal Navy deployed a squadron of forty ships to the West African coast at an annual cost of £700,000 (equivalent to roughly £70 million in modern currency), with the explicit purpose of interdicting slave ships and ending the trade.

Spencer documents the perverse effects of this apparently humanitarian intervention. The naval blockade:
- Incentivized the construction of faster, smaller slaving vessels with decks as little as "one foot six inches apart"
- Caused suffocation, disease, and a mortality rate of 35% among the enslaved
- Led desperate captains, when pursued, to throw entire cargoes of 500 people into the sea
- Provoked African chiefs on the blockaded coast to execute 200 captives and display their heads on poles "in sight of the squadron"

As Spencer notes, the abolitionists "little thought that such a measure instead of preventing would only aggravate the horrors, without sensibly mitigating the extent of the traffic." The humanitarian intentions of the legislation were not in question; the outcome was nonetheless catastrophic for those it was meant to help.

## Significance / Impact

The abolition of the slave trade represents one of the first mass humanitarian movements in modern history and a landmark in the development of international human rights norms. It demonstrated that organized public opinion could reshape national policy in the face of powerful economic interests.

However, Spencer deployed the case as a philosophical argument rather than a historical narrative. His purpose was to illustrate a general principle: that legislative action, even when motivated by the best intentions and pursuing an obviously worthy goal, produces unintended consequences that often worsen the very evils it seeks to remedy. The empirical, case-by-case approach of "expediency" — judging each measure by its stated aim — cannot predict the complex chain of social reactions that any intervention sets in motion.

This analysis is central to Spencer's broader critique of utilitarianism. If even the abolition of the slave trade — arguably the most morally unambiguous cause of the age — could produce such perverse outcomes through legislative action, then the Benthamite faith in government as a neutral instrument for maximizing happiness was fundamentally misplaced.
