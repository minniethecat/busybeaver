## Biography

Granville Sharp (1735–1813) was an English abolitionist, classical scholar, and philanthropist. He was one of the founding figures of the movement to abolish the slave trade in the British Empire. Born in Durham, the son of a clergyman, he initially trained as a linen draper before joining the Ordnance Office.

Sharp's involvement in the anti-slavery cause began in 1765 when he encountered Jonathan Strong, an enslaved African who had been beaten and abandoned by his master. Sharp took up Strong's legal case, teaching himself enough law to argue that slavery could not exist under English common law.

The landmark case *Somerset v Stewart* (1772), in which Lord Mansfield ruled that a slave could not be forcibly removed from England, was substantially influenced by Sharp's legal activism. He was also a founder of the Society for Effecting the Abolition of the Slave Trade (1787).

Herbert Spencer cited Sharp as one of the historical witnesses to the universality of the intuition of equal human liberty.

## Key Ideas

### The Intuition of Equal Liberty

Spencer, arguing that the "law of equal freedom" is a fundamental moral truth intuitively perceived by all people, cited Sharp among many historical figures who asserted natural human equality:

> "The doctrine that 'all men are naturally equal'... has not only been asserted by philanthropists like Granville Sharpe, but, as Sir Robert Filmer tells us, 'Heyward, Blackwood, Barclay, and others that have bravely vindicated the rights of kings, with one consent admitted the natural liberty and equality of mankind'"

For Spencer, the fact that even defenders of absolute monarchy conceded natural equality was powerful evidence that the principle was a universal moral intuition rather than a partisan doctrine. Sharp represented its practical application in the abolitionist struggle.

### Law as the Expression of Moral Intuition

Sharp's legal strategy — arguing that slavery was incompatible with English common law — embodied the Spencerian principle that positive law should conform to fundamental moral law, not override it. Sharp did not seek a new statute; he argued that existing law, properly understood, already prohibited slavery.

> "This first and all-essential law, declaratory of the liberty of each limited only by the like liberties of all, is that fundamental truth of which the moral sense gives an intuition, and which the intellect has to develop into a scientific morality."

Spencer's framing placed Sharp's activism within a philosophical framework: Sharp was acting on a moral intuition that the intellect must develop into systematic principle. This method — appealing to deeper principles against surface legislation — paralleled Spencer's own strategy against unlimited parliamentary sovereignty.

## Major Works

- *A Representation of the Injustice and Dangerous Tendency of Tolerating Slavery* (1769) — Sharp's first major anti-slavery tract.

- *The Law of Retribution* (1776) — A theological and legal argument against slavery grounded in natural law.

- *Remarks on the Uses of the Definitive Article in the Greek Text of the New Testament* (1798) — "Sharp's Rule" of Greek grammar, influencing New Testament translation.

## Influence / Legacy

Granville Sharp's most visible legacy is the abolition of the slave trade (1807) and of slavery itself (1833) in the British Empire. His legal arguments established the principle that English soil was free soil.

Spencer's citation of Sharp in *Social Statics* placed the abolitionist in a lineage of thinkers — from Edward I's writs to the American Declaration of Independence — who recognized the equal liberty of all persons. Sharp's activism and Spencer's law of equal freedom were thus situated within a single moral tradition.
