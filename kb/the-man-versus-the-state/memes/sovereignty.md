## Definition

Sovereignty is the concept of supreme political authority — the notion that some person, body, or institution possesses ultimate and unlimited power within a given territory. In classical political theory, it designates the highest source of law and coercive authority, from which all positive law is said to derive its validity.

In the context of Herbert Spencer's political philosophy, sovereignty is not an unquestioned premise but the central object of critique. Spencer devotes the culminating chapter of *Man Versus the State* (1884) to what he terms "The Great Political Superstition" — the belief that a majority, merely by virtue of being a majority, possesses unlimited authority over the minority.

## Spencer's Critique: From Hobbes to Austin

Spencer traces sovereignty through Thomas Hobbes, who derived it from a hypothesized social contract made to escape the "war of every man against every man." Hobbes argued that without a "coercive power to compel men equally to the performance of their covenants, by the terror of some punishment," the names of just and unjust could have no place.

Spencer identifies two significant implications in Hobbes. First, state authority is a means to an end (enforcement of justice) and has no validity outside that purpose. Second, the end is solely justice enforcement and protection from external enemies — not the open-ended regulation of all conduct.

Spencer then turns to John Austin, who sought to derive positive law from unlimited sovereignty. Spencer notes that Austin, a former soldier, "assimilates civil authority to military authority" — taking both as above question in origin and range. But Spencer identifies the fatal gap: Austin never answers "Whence the sovereignty? What is the warrant for this unqualified supremacy?"

## The Divine Right of Majorities

Spencer identifies modern sovereignty as the "divine right of parliaments," ultimately meaning "the divine right of majorities." This is the "current theory which all accept without proof as a self-evident truth."

Spencer challenges it through the analogy of an incorporated company. Members are bound to submit to the majority only "in all matters concerning the fulfilment of the objects for which they are incorporated; but in no others."

Applied to the nation, majority rule is valid only within the sphere for which citizens would give unanimous consent — defence of person, property, and territory. Beyond that sphere, coercion of the minority by the majority is "gross tyranny."

## The Moral Warrant for Majority Rule

Spencer argues that neither the social contract hypothesis nor superior physical force justifies unlimited sovereignty. The only defensible warrant lies in what citizens would now agree to with "practical unanimity."

For defence against external invasion and internal aggression, agreement would be nearly universal. But for religion, dress, beverages, or other private concerns, large portions of the population would refuse majority control.

The question resolves into "What are the relative claims of the aggregate and of its units?" Spencer answers through his doctrine of natural rights grounded in the law of equal freedom.

## Critique of Bentham's Theory of Rights

Spencer subjects Bentham's doctrine to devastating criticism. If the sovereign people create a government that creates rights, then the people create an agent to create rights and confer them back on themselves — "a marvellous piece of political legerdemain."

Each citizen, in his "public capacity" as sovereign, owns a millionth share of all rights. In his "private capacity," he has no rights until the government confers them. Spencer calls this circular reasoning "a plexus of absurdities." For Spencer, pre-existing rights create the need for government, not the reverse.
