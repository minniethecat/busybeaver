## Biography

Henry Mayers Hyndman (1842–1921) was an English political activist and writer, best known as the founder of Britain's first socialist political party, the Social Democratic Federation (SDF), established in 1881. Born into a wealthy family and educated at Trinity College, Cambridge, Hyndman worked as a journalist before turning to politics. Originally a Conservative, he was radicalized by his reading of Karl Marx and by the visible poverty of Victorian London.

Hyndman played a pivotal role in introducing Marxist ideas to British political life. His 1881 book *England for All* paraphrased Marx's *Capital* without attribution, provoking a rift with Marx and Engels. Nevertheless, the SDF became an important vehicle for early British socialism, counting among its members figures such as William Morris, Eleanor Marx, and John Burns.

In 1884, Hyndman published *Socialism and Slavery*, a direct reply to Herbert Spencer's essay "The Coming Slavery" (later incorporated into *The Man Versus the State*). This exchange epitomized the late-Victorian confrontation between laissez-faire liberalism and emergent socialism.

## Key Ideas

### Socialism as Liberation, Not Slavery

Hyndman contested Spencer's central metaphor — that the expansion of state functions constituted a "coming slavery." For Hyndman, Spencer's characterization inverted the real relationship: the existing economic order, with its wage-dependence and poverty, was the true slavery. Collective ownership and democratic control of the means of production were, in his view, the path to genuine freedom.

### The Misattribution of Spencer's Position

Spencer noted in his reply to Hyndman that "as usual with antagonists, they ascribe to me opinions which I do not hold." Hyndman assumed that Spencer's opposition to socialism entailed approval of existing arrangements. Spencer clarified: "Disapproval of socialism does not, as Mr. Hyndman assumes, necessitate approval of existing arrangements. Many things he reprobates I reprobate quite as much; but I dissent from his remedy."

### The Abolition of Individual Rights

Spencer identified Hyndman as a leading voice pushing the doctrine of community sovereignty over property to its "logical issue." In *The Sins of Legislators*, Spencer writes: "Mr. George and his friends, Mr. Hyndman and his supporters, are pushing the theory to its logical issue. They have been instructed by examples, yearly increasing in number, that the individual has no rights but what the community may equitably over-ride; and they are now saying... abolish individual rights altogether."

## Major Works

- *England for All* (1881) — an exposition of socialist principles drawing heavily on Marx
- *Socialism and Slavery* (1884) — a reply to Herbert Spencer's "The Coming Slavery"
- *The Historical Basis of Socialism in England* (1883) — a survey of English radical and socialist traditions
- *The Economics of Socialism* (1896) — a popular exposition of socialist economic theory

## Influence / Legacy

Hyndman's exchange with Spencer captures the intellectual watershed of the 1880s — the moment when socialism emerged as a serious political force in Britain and directly challenged the individualist premises of classical liberalism. Spencer treated Hyndman as a representative figure of the "new Toryism" — the tendency, whether from the right or left, to expand state authority over individual life.

Although Hyndman's SDF never achieved electoral success, it laid organizational groundwork for the later Labour Party. Hyndman's insistence on the class character of the state and the necessity of collective ownership influenced a generation of British socialists, even as his autocratic style and doctrinal rigidity alienated many allies.
