## Background

The Contagious Diseases Acts were enacted in response to concern about the prevalence of venereal disease among British military personnel. The Crimean War (1853–1856) and subsequent military deployments had drawn attention to rates of infection that, according to army medical reports, incapacitated significant numbers of soldiers. A parliamentary committee recommended the adoption of the system already in place in certain continental European states, whereby prostitutes in military districts were subjected to compulsory medical examination and, if found infected, detention in "lock hospitals" until cured.

The Acts applied initially to eleven garrison and port towns in England and Ireland, including Portsmouth, Plymouth, Chatham, Woolwich, and Cork. The legislation targeted women only — no provision required the examination or treatment of soldiers or the male clients of prostitutes.

## Description

The first Contagious Diseases Act (1864) empowered plainclothes police officers to identify women "common prostitutes" and require them to submit to medical examination. Women who refused could be brought before a magistrate and, upon the sworn testimony of a single police officer, ordered to undergo examination. If found to be infected, they could be detained in a certified hospital for up to three months. The Acts of 1866 and 1869 extended the maximum detention period, expanded the geographical coverage, and introduced a system of periodic compulsory examination for registered women — in effect, a system of licensed and regulated prostitution on the continental model.

The Acts created an extraordinary inversion of ordinary legal protections. The presumption of innocence was reversed; the requirement of probable cause was eliminated; the right to refuse medical examination was abolished; and all of this applied not to the criminal law generally but specifically to one class of persons — poor women in the vicinity of military installations.

## Significance / Impact

The Contagious Diseases Acts provoked one of the most significant civil liberties campaigns of the Victorian era. Led by Josephine Butler and the Ladies' National Association for the Repeal of the Contagious Diseases Acts, the repeal movement mobilized thousands of women to demand the restoration of legal protections. The campaign was remarkable for its willingness to discuss venereal disease and prostitution publicly — subjects considered indecent for respectable women to mention. The Acts were suspended in 1883 and formally repealed in 1886.

Spencer cited the Contagious Diseases Acts in *The Man Versus The State* as a prime instance of "New Toryism" — legislation that, under progressive and sanitary pretexts, extended state coercion beyond its traditional boundaries. He noted with particular alarm that the Acts gave police "powers which, in respect of certain classes of women, abolished sundry of those safeguards to individual liberty" that had been built up over centuries. For Spencer, the logic of sanitary compulsion was inexorable: if the state could forcibly examine women to prevent venereal disease, it could compel vaccination, dictate diet, regulate housing — there was no principled stopping point.

> "Of compulsory legislation dating from 1864, may be named an extension of the Factories Act to various additional trades... Also there were passed a Chimney-Sweepers Act, an Act for further regulating the sale of beer in Ireland, an Act for compulsory testing of cables and anchors, an Act extending the Public Works Act of 1863, and the Contagious Diseases Act: which last gave the police, in specified places, powers which, in respect of certain classes of women, abolished sundry of those safeguards to individual liberty which we had thought established for all." — *The Man Versus The State*, Chapter: The New Toryism
