## Biography

Edmund Burke (1729–1797) was an Anglo-Irish statesman, political theorist, and orator, widely regarded as the father of modern conservatism. Born in Dublin and educated at Trinity College, Burke moved to London in 1750 to study law at the Middle Temple, though he soon abandoned the legal profession for a literary and political career. His early work, *A Philosophical Enquiry into the Origin of Our Ideas of the Sublime and Beautiful* (1757), established his reputation as an aesthetic theorist and introduced a sustained treatment of sympathy as a fundamental human capacity.

Burke entered Parliament in 1765 as MP for Wendover, serving as secretary to the Marquess of Rockingham, leader of the Whig opposition. Over a parliamentary career spanning nearly three decades, Burke involved himself in the great controversies of the age: he defended the American colonists against parliamentary taxation, prosecuted Warren Hastings for misrule in India, championed Catholic emancipation in Ireland, and — most famously — denounced the French Revolution and the abstract rationalism that he believed had inspired it.

Burke's *Reflections on the Revolution in France* (1790) is the canonical text of conservative political philosophy. Against the revolutionaries' appeal to abstract rights, Burke appealed to prescription, inheritance, and the slow accumulation of social wisdom across generations. Society, he argued, is a partnership "between those who are living, those who are dead, and those who are to be born" — a conception profoundly opposed to the revolutionary notion that each generation may remake society according to rational principles. Herbert Spencer references Burke's treatment of sympathy in the context of his own account of the moral sentiments, drawing upon the same eighteenth-century tradition of moral psychology that produced Adam Smith's *Theory of Moral Sentiments*.

## Key Ideas

### Sympathy and the Moral Sentiments

Burke's early aesthetic treatise, the *Philosophical Enquiry*, contains an important discussion of sympathy as the psychological mechanism by which we enter into the feelings of others. For Burke, sympathy is the source of our concern for the welfare of strangers and the foundation of social cohesion. "It is by this principle," Burke wrote, "that we are moved to pity, to compassion, to lenity, and to tenderness." Herbert Spencer references Burke on this point in *Social Statics*, where Spencer develops Adam Smith's theory of sympathy into a full-fledged evolutionary account of the moral sense. Burke, Smith, and Spencer form a continuous tradition of British moral psychology in which sympathy — the capacity to imaginatively stand in another's position — is recognized as the psychological foundation of moral judgment and social obligation.

### The Organic Conception of Society

Burke's most influential contribution to political thought is his conception of society as an organic, intergenerational partnership rather than a contractual agreement among contemporaries. "Society is indeed a contract," Burke wrote in the *Reflections*, but it is not a contract of the sort that can be dissolved at pleasure: "It is a partnership in all science; a partnership in all art; a partnership in every virtue, and in all perfection." Because the ends of this partnership "cannot be obtained in many generations, it becomes a partnership not only between those who are living, but between those who are living, those who are dead, and those who are to be born."

Spencer shared Burke's organic conception of society — his conviction that society is not an artifact of human design but a growth, governed by laws of development that deliberate legislation cannot override. In *Social Statics* and *The Man Versus the State*, Spencer repeatedly invokes the slow, unconscious evolution of social institutions against the legislative presumption that Acts of Parliament can remake society overnight. Burke and Spencer differ, however, on the political implications of organicism: where Burke found in it a justification for traditional authority and established hierarchies, Spencer found in it a justification for limiting the state — for allowing the natural evolution of society to proceed without the constant interference of meddlesome legislators.

### The Critique of Abstract Rights

Burke's attack on the French Revolution is, at its core, an attack on the doctrine of abstract rights. The revolutionaries, Burke charged, had torn down the inherited institutions of France in pursuit of metaphysical abstractions — the "rights of man" — that bore no relation to the concrete conditions of social life. "The pretended rights of these theorists are all extremes," Burke wrote; "and in proportion as they are metaphysically true, they are morally and politically false." Real rights, Burke contended, arise from prescription, custom, and the accumulated experience of a people — not from abstract deduction.

This critique of abstract rights places Burke in apparent opposition to the natural-rights tradition that Spencer belonged to — the tradition of Locke, the Levellers, and the American revolutionaries. Spencer, however, did not regard his own conception of natural rights as "abstract" in the sense Burke condemned. The law of equal freedom, for Spencer, was not a metaphysical postulate but an empirical generalization from the conditions of social life — a principle discovered by observing the actual conditions under which cooperation and mutual adaptation become possible. Spencer's natural rights, unlike the French revolutionaries' "rights of man," were grounded in evolution, not in rationalist deduction.

### Prescription and Gradual Reform

Burke's political method was one of reform, not revolution. He distinguished carefully between "change" — which might be necessary and prudent — and "innovation" — which was reckless and destructive. "A state without the means of some change," he wrote, "is without the means of its conservation." Burke supported the reform of specific abuses — he prosecuted Hastings for corruption, he sought relief for Irish Catholics — but he insisted that reform must be gradual, piecemeal, and respectful of inherited institutions. Spencer's own gradualist evolutionism carries echoes of this Burkean prudence, though Spencer's theory of social evolution gave the preference for gradualism a scientific rather than a traditionalist foundation.

## Major Works

- **A Philosophical Enquiry into the Origin of Our Ideas of the Sublime and Beautiful** (1757) — Burke's early aesthetic treatise, containing his influential treatment of sympathy as the foundation of moral and aesthetic response.
- **Reflections on the Revolution in France** (1790) — The foundational text of modern conservatism, arguing against the abstract rationalism of the French revolutionaries and in favour of prescription, inheritance, and organic social development.
- **A Letter to a Noble Lord** (1796) — Burke's eloquent defence of his political career and his pension, containing a powerful restatement of his principles in the face of personal attack.
- **Speeches on American Taxation, Conciliation with America, and on Fox's East India Bill** — Burke's major parliamentary speeches, demonstrating the practical application of his principles to the great political questions of his time.

## Relationship to Other Figures

- [[herbert-spencer]] — Spencer references Burke's treatment of sympathy in *Social Statics*, situating Burke within the tradition of British moral psychology that Spencer sought to naturalize through evolutionary theory. Spencer and Burke shared an organic conception of society but drew opposed political conclusions from it.
- [[adam-smith]] — Burke and Smith were contemporaries and acquaintances. Smith's theory of sympathy in *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* parallels Burke's treatment in the *Philosophical Enquiry*, and both belong to the Scottish and Irish Enlightenment tradition of moral psychology from which Spencer drew.
- [[john-locke]] — Locke's natural-rights philosophy, particularly the right of property, influenced Burke's own defence of property — though Burke grounded property in prescription rather than in Lockean labour-mixing.
- [[thomas-hobbes]] — Burke rejected Hobbes's contractarian absolutism as thoroughly as he rejected revolutionary rationalism. Burke's constitution was historical, not contractual; his liberty was inherited, not deduced.
- [[william-gladstone]] — Gladstone, though a Liberal, acknowledged Burke as a formative influence on his political thought, particularly in matters of empire, Ireland, and the limits of rationalist reform.
