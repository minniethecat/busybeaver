## Biography

Pierre-Joseph Proudhon (1809–1865) was a French philosopher, politician, and economist, commonly considered the first self-declared anarchist. Born in Besançon to a working-class family, he was largely self-educated and worked as a printer before turning to political writing. His 1840 work *What Is Property?* established his reputation with its famous thesis that "property is theft."

Proudhon was active during the French Revolution of 1848, serving in the Constituent Assembly, and was imprisoned for three years for his writings against Louis-Napoléon Bonaparte. Unlike many of his socialist contemporaries, he rejected state-centred solutions and argued for a decentralised federation of worker associations — a position that later influenced both the syndicalist and anarchist traditions.

Herbert Spencer encountered Proudhon's work through the broader European debate on property and socialism. In *Social Statics* (1851), Spencer engaged directly with Proudhon's assertion that "all property is robbery," using it as the starting point for his own extended defence of property rights grounded in the law of equal freedom.

## Key Ideas

### Property as Theft

Proudhon's most famous thesis — "property is robbery" — challenged the moral legitimacy of exclusive ownership. He argued that property rights enable their holders to extract unearned income from the labour of others, and that the legal apparatus of the state exists primarily to protect this arrangement.

Spencer took this challenge seriously but rejected its conclusion. In *Social Statics*, he argued that Proudhon's principle, if followed to its logical end, would deny a person ownership even of their own body — since the body is built from consumed food whose ownership could equally be contested. Spencer wrote that in a world without property rights, "a man has no property in his own flesh and blood — has no more claim to his own limbs than he has to the limbs of another."

### Mutualism versus State Socialism

Proudhon favoured a system of mutual banking and voluntary exchange among worker-owned enterprises, distinct from the state-socialist schemes of contemporaries such as Louis Blanc. He rejected the centralised state control of production, arguing instead for contractual relationships among freely associating producers.

Spencer shared Proudhon's hostility to state-administered production but differed on the remedy. While Proudhon sought to replace capitalist property with mutualist associations, Spencer saw private property — honestly acquired through labour — as a necessary corollary of the law of equal freedom. The division, Spencer argued, between merits-based inequality and state-enforced equality was fundamental.

### Justice and Liberty

Proudhon's later work, particularly *De la Justice dans la Révolution et dans l'Église* (1858), attempted to ground justice in human dignity rather than in theological or state authority. He saw liberty as the supreme social value and argued that justice could only be realised through the abolition of all forms of external authority — including the authority of property itself.

Spencer, while arriving at different conclusions about property, shared with Proudhon a conviction that justice must be derived from a first principle rather than from convention or utility. For Spencer, that principle was the law of equal freedom; for Proudhon, it was the sovereignty of individual conscience.

## Major Works

- *Qu'est-ce que la propriété?* (*What Is Property?*, 1840) — His breakthrough work, containing the declaration that "property is theft"
- *Système des contradictions économiques* (*The System of Economic Contradictions*, 1846) — A critique of both capitalism and utopian socialism, which prompted Marx's *The Poverty of Philosophy* in reply
- *De la Justice dans la Révolution et dans l'Église* (1858) — An attempt to establish a secular theory of justice
- *Du Principe fédératif* (1863) — His mature political work advocating federalism against both centralised state authority and atomised individualism

## Influence / Legacy

Proudhon's influence extended in several directions. His anarchist ideas shaped the French syndicalist movement and later influenced thinkers such as Bakunin and Kropotkin. His mutualist economics anticipated aspects of the cooperative and credit-union movements. And his critique of property — though rejected by classical liberals like Spencer — forced defenders of private ownership to articulate more rigorous philosophical justifications.

For Spencer, Proudhon served as a foil: the most articulate exponent of the view that property lacks moral foundation. Spencer's extensive engagement with this claim in *Social Statics* makes Proudhon an important figure in the intellectual architecture of nineteenth-century liberal thought, even where Spencer rejected his conclusions.
