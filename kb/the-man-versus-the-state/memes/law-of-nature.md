## Definition

In Herbert Spencer's *Man Versus the State* (1884), the "law of nature" is the principle that certain norms of justice have an existence independent of positive enactment — they are the ground from which human laws derive their legitimacy, not the product of legislative will. Spencer draws on a tradition reaching back through the English common-law judges, the civilians, and the canonists, all of whom recognized a higher law against which positive enactments could be measured.

Spencer's invocation of the law of nature is not a metaphysical claim about a transcendent moral order, but a jurisprudential claim about the relationship between justice and legislation: the rights that law ought to protect are *prior* to law, and the function of legislation is to recognize and specify them, not to create them.

## Common Law and Natural Justice

Spencer finds support for this view in the historical practice of English common-law judges. He cites Professor Frederick Pollock, who observes that "so early as the fifteenth century, we find a common-law judge declaring that, as in a case unprovided for by known rules the civilians and canonists devise a new rule according to 'the law of nature which is the ground of all laws,' the Courts of Westminster can and will do the like."

The system of Equity, Spencer notes, was developed precisely to supplement and correct the common law where its rigid rules produced injustice — and it proceeded throughout "on a recognition of men's claims considered as existing apart from legal warrant." Equity did not invent new rights; it recognized rights that the common law had failed to protect.

## The Victorian Example: Married Women's Property

Spencer offers a contemporary illustration: the Married Women's Property Act, which for the first time gave married women a right of property in their own earnings. He argues that this reform did not *create* the right; rather, "the reformed law did not create the right, but recognition of the right created the reformed law." The legislation was occasioned by a growing consciousness that "the natural connexion between labour expended and benefit enjoyed, is one which should be maintained in all cases."

This is Spencer's model of legal progress: legislation does not *confer* rights but *acknowledges* rights that have become apparent through moral evolution. The direction of causation runs from moral recognition to legal codification, not the reverse.

## The Contrast with Positive Law

Spencer's treatment of the law of nature stands in direct opposition to the legal positivism of John Austin, who derived the authority of law from the "unlimited sovereignty" of the lawmaker — whether a monarch, an aristocracy, or a democratic majority. For Austin, a law is valid because it is the command of the sovereign, regardless of its content. For Spencer, a law is valid only insofar as it conforms to the principles of justice that the law of nature embodies.

Austin, Spencer remarks, "assimilates civil authority to military authority; taking for granted that the one, as the other, is above question in respect of both origin and range." By deriving positive law from unlimited sovereignty, Austin merely "moved a step further back and there left unsolved" the fundamental question: "Whence the sovereignty? What is the assignable warrant for this unqualified supremacy?"

## The Biological Analogy

Spencer reinforces the law of nature with a biological metaphor drawn from his broader evolutionary philosophy. The command "if any would not work neither should he eat" is, he writes, "simply a Christian enunciation of that universal law of Nature under which life has reached its present height — the law that a creature not energetic enough to maintain itself must die." The natural order of consequences — that effort and reward, misconduct and suffering, are linked — ought to be respected by human law, not severed by misguided benevolence.

To separate pain from ill-doing, Spencer warns, "is to fight against the constitution of things, and will be followed by far more pain." Artificial interventions that break the link between conduct and consequence produce worse outcomes than the suffering they aim to relieve.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

- [[positive-law]] — The law of nature is Spencer's counter-concept to Austin's legal positivism; where positive law derives from sovereign command, natural law derives from moral recognition.
- [[rights]] — Natural rights are the substantive content of the law of nature; the law of nature is the formal principle under which rights are recognized.
- [[law-of-equal-freedom]] — Spencer's first principle — that each person's freedom is limited only by the equal freedom of every other — is his specification of the law of nature's primary requirement.
