## Definition

**Feudalism** is a social and political system based on hierarchical relationships of land tenure, personal allegiance, and military obligation. For Herbert Spencer, feudalism was not merely a medieval historical phenomenon but a stage in the general evolution of societies — the transitional form between primitive military organization and modern industrial society. In *Social Statics* (1851), Spencer used feudalism as both a historical reference point and a critical diagnostic category: the habits of deference, status-consciousness, and coercive regulation that characterized feudal society persisted into the nineteenth century, manifesting as what Spencer called "sentimental feudalism" — the disposition to arrange society hierarchically, with the state as a surrogate lord exercising protective and directive authority over dependent subjects.

> It is either a sentimental feudalism; or it is a state under which the people shall behave "lowly and reverently to all their betters" and "do their duty in that state of life unto which it shall please God to call them."

## Key Arguments

Spencer's treatment of feudalism ranged across historical analysis, psychological diagnosis, and political critique.

**First, feudalism as an evolutionary stage**. Within Spencer's classification of social types, feudalism occupies the intermediate zone between the purely militant type — where all social organization is subordinated to war — and the industrial type — where voluntary cooperation replaces coercion. Feudal society retains the military hierarchy (lords, vassals, knights) but stabilizes it through territorial settlement and hereditary obligation. The feudal bond is personal rather than institutional; authority derives from status rather than contract.

**Second, the persistence of feudal mental habits**. Spencer's most distinctive contribution to the analysis of feudalism was his insistence that the *mental structures* of feudal society outlive its formal institutions. The disposition to defer to superiors, to accept status-based inequality as natural, to look to authority for guidance and protection — these "survivals" of feudalism, Spencer argued, persisted in nineteenth-century British society long after the legal apparatus of feudalism had been dismantled. "Those intolerable restraints once borne by the manufacturing classes of France were contemporary with intense despotism at court, and a still lingering feudalism in the provinces."

**Third, sentimental feudalism as a political pathology**. Spencer coined the phrase "sentimental feudalism" to describe a specific political disposition: the yearning for a hierarchical, ordered society in which each person knows their place and receives protection from above. This disposition, Spencer argued, underlay much of the support for state intervention and paternalistic legislation. The desire for the state to act as a protective lord — regulating commerce, providing education, supervising health — was, in his analysis, a feudal atavism disguised in modern language. The Tory paternalism of the nineteenth century was "sentimental feudalism" dressed in parliamentary robes.

**Fourth, feudalism and commercial regulation**. Spencer drew a direct connection between feudal social structure and commercial restriction. The "tyranny in commercial laws" that characterized protectionist economic policy was, he argued, "in the same category" as domestic tyranny and the despotic treatment of children — all expressions of the same coercive, status-based mode of social organization that feudalism had institutionalized.

## Historical Context

Spencer wrote in a period when the formal structures of feudalism had been largely dismantled in Britain — the last vestiges of feudal land tenure were abolished in the mid-nineteenth century — but when the social and psychological legacy of feudalism remained visible. The Victorian cult of deference, the hierarchical structure of the established church, the persistence of aristocratic privilege in Parliament, and the paternalistic ethos of much social legislation all testified to the continued influence of feudal modes of thought.

Spencer's analysis was influenced by the comparative-historical method of Henry Maine, whose *Ancient Law* (1861) traced the movement from status to contract as the keynote of legal evolution. Spencer generalized this insight into a comprehensive theory of social evolution in which feudalism represented one phase of the larger movement from compulsory cooperation (the militant type) to voluntary cooperation (the industrial type).

## Relationship to Other Concepts

- **[[militant-type]]**: The social type of which feudalism is a specific historical expression — characterized by coercive regulation, hierarchical subordination, and the subordination of individual welfare to collective military strength.
- **[[industrial-type]]**: The social type toward which evolution tends — characterized by voluntary cooperation and the progressive dissolution of feudal hierarchies.
- **[[toryism]]**: The political ideology that, in Spencer's analysis, represents the survival of feudal dispositions in modern parliamentary form.
- **[[despotism]]**: The extreme form of coercive rule — feudalism's hierarchical authority carried to its logical conclusion.
