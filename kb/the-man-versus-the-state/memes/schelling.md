## Biography

Friedrich Wilhelm Joseph Schelling (1775–1854) was a German philosopher of the idealist tradition, a contemporary and sometime rival of Hegel. His work passed through several phases — Naturphilosophie, transcendental idealism, and later the philosophy of mythology and revelation. Schelling's early conception of nature as a dynamic, self-organizing process exercised a significant influence on nineteenth-century scientific thought.

## Key Ideas

### The Tendency to Individuation

Schelling's most important contribution to Spencer's thought was the concept of a "tendency to individuation" — the idea that living things manifest an inherent drive toward greater individuality and complexity. In *Social Statics* (Chapter XXV, "General Considerations"), Spencer acknowledged that he was initially unaware that Coleridge had borrowed this idea from Schelling. It was only later, while writing *The Classification of the Sciences* in 1864, that Spencer recognized Schelling as the original source.

Spencer noted that "the conception of an individual is a metaphysical one, and the tendency to individuation cannot be represented in physical terms." He nevertheless incorporated the insight into his own evolutionary framework, observing that "the integration of matter and concomitant dissipation of motion, is a process by which there is formed an aggregate — a distinct object — an individual something." Spencer thus translated Schelling's metaphysical concept into the physical language of his evolutionary philosophy.

### From Metaphysics to Physics

Spencer's treatment of Schelling illustrates his broader project of translating idealist philosophy into evolutionary science. The tendency to individuation, re-described as the integration of matter, became a universal law applying to "all evolving things, inorganic as well as organic" — not merely a trait of living beings.

## Relationship to Spencer

Spencer regarded Schelling with respect, crediting him as a philosophical forerunner whose core insight — once stripped of its metaphysical character — aligned with the law of evolution. The acknowledgment appears prominently in a long footnote to *Social Statics*, indicating Spencer's concern to trace the intellectual genealogy of his ideas.

## Major Works

- *System of Transcendental Idealism* (1800) — Foundational text of German idealism
- *Ideas for a Philosophy of Nature* (1797) — Core Naturphilosophie text
- *Philosophical Inquiries into the Nature of Human Freedom* (1809)
