## Definition

Natural rights are entitlements that individuals possess by virtue of their nature as human beings, independently of any grant, permission, or recognition by government. In classical liberal thought, these include rights to life, liberty, and property — rights that the state exists to protect, not to create.

The doctrine of natural rights stands in opposition to legal positivism, which holds that rights are exclusively creations of law. For the positivist, a right without legal recognition is no right at all. For the natural-rights theorist, legal recognition merely *acknowledges* rights that exist antecedently — in human nature, in divine will, or in the conditions of social existence.

[[herbert-spencer]] occupies a distinctive position in this debate. He affirms the existence of natural rights — indeed, he makes them the foundation of his entire political philosophy — but he derives them from evolutionary adaptation rather than from theology or hypothetical states of nature. This places him between [[john-locke]], who grounded natural rights in divine creation, and [[jeremy-bentham]], who dismissed them as "nonsense upon stilts."

## Spencer's Evolutionary Derivation

Spencer's derivation of natural rights begins not with a hypothetical "state of nature" but with the observable conditions of social life. All human beings have "like faculties" and "like claims" to exercise those faculties. In a world of finite space and resources, the exercise of faculties by different persons inevitably clashes. The only principle for resolving these clashes that treats all persons as moral equals is the [[law-of-equal-freedom]]: each may exercise his faculties to the fullest extent compatible with the like exercise by every other.

From this master principle, Spencer deduces specific natural rights:

- **The right to life**: the right to physical integrity, forbidding assault and murder.
- **The right to personal liberty**: the right to move, speak, and act without physical coercion.
- **The right to property**: the right to exclusive possession of the products of one's own labor and the gifts or bequests of others.
- **The right to contract**: the right to bind oneself by voluntary agreement and to enforce such agreements.
- **The right to free exchange**: the right to trade one's property or services on mutually agreeable terms.
- **The right to free belief and worship**: the right to hold and express religious opinions without penalty.

Each of these rights, Spencer argues, is a necessary implication of the equal-freedom principle. To deny any one of them is to deny that all persons have equal claims to exercise their faculties.

## Spencer vs. Bentham on Natural Rights

The most famous attack on natural rights came from Jeremy Bentham, who declared:

> "Natural rights is simple nonsense: natural and imprescriptible rights, rhetorical nonsense — nonsense upon stilts."

Bentham's objection was twofold. First, he argued that rights are necessarily creatures of law — there is no "natural" right any more than there is a "natural" contract or a "natural" statute. Second, he argued that appeals to natural rights are politically dangerous, providing a rhetorical cover for whatever the speaker happens to prefer, without the discipline of legal reasoning or empirical evidence.

Spencer's response was subtle. He agreed with Bentham that earlier formulations of natural-rights theory — particularly those resting on theological premises — were vulnerable to positivist critique. But he argued that Bentham's own principle of utility implicitly presupposed the very natural rights it purported to reject. The "greatest happiness of the greatest number" only makes sense if each person's happiness counts equally — which is itself a natural-rights claim: the equal right of all to have their happiness counted.

Spencer further demonstrated that Bentham's "expediency" philosophy provided no principled basis for distinguishing good legislation from bad. Without a fixed standard — such as the law of equal freedom — legislation becomes whatever a majority happens to prefer. This reduces "the greatest happiness principle" to "the divine right of majorities."

## Natural Rights and the State

For Spencer, natural rights are not merely moral claims but the constitutive limits of legitimate government. The state's sole legitimate function is the protection of natural rights — defending individuals against internal aggression (crime) and external aggression (invasion), and enforcing voluntary contracts.

Every expansion of state activity beyond this protective role represents a violation of natural rights. When the state provides education, it compels some individuals to pay for the education of others, violating their property rights. When the state establishes a church, it compels dissenters to support a religion they reject, violating their rights of conscience. When the state regulates sanitation, it overrides individual judgment about personal health, violating the right of personal liberty.

> "The State is simply a joint-stock company for the mutual insurance of men against each other and against foreign enemies."

This minimalist conception placed Spencer in opposition not only to Benthamite reformers — who sought to use the state for utilitarian ends — but also to the broader Victorian consensus that government should actively promote "the public good."

## Relationship to Evolutionary Ethics

Spencer's most original contribution to natural-rights theory is its grounding in evolutionary biology. Rights are not divine decrees or metaphysical abstractions but the conditions under which the highest form of social life becomes possible.

Societies that respect natural rights allow individuals to adapt to their circumstances through the free exercise of their faculties. This adaptation drives moral and intellectual progress. Societies that violate natural rights — through slavery, caste, sumptuary laws, or paternalistic legislation — impede this adaptive process. They preserve abnormal faculties at the expense of normal ones, degrading the species.

This evolutionary framework provides Spencer with an answer to the most persistent objection against natural rights: the problem of justification. Why should we respect natural rights? Because doing so promotes human flourishing — not in the utilitarian sense of maximizing pleasure, but in the evolutionary sense of developing the full range of human faculties under conditions of equal freedom.

## Critiques

**The individualism objection.** Critics argue that Spencer's list of natural rights is excessively individualistic, ignoring the social and collective dimensions of human flourishing. Rights to education, healthcare, or a minimum standard of living find no place in Spencer's scheme.

**The evolutionary fallacy.** The derivation of "ought" from "is" — moral norms from evolutionary facts — has been challenged as a category error. Even if equal freedom promotes adaptation, it does not follow that equal freedom is morally obligatory.

**The enforcement problem.** Spencer acknowledges that natural rights are not self-enforcing. The state is needed to protect them. But the state, once created, tends to expand beyond its protective function — a dynamic Spencer documented at length but could not solve theoretically.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

- [[justice]]: Justice is the principle that enforces the equal-freedom constraints from which natural rights are deduced.
- [[liberty]]: Liberty is the positive freedom; natural rights are the specific entitlements that make equal liberty concrete.
- [[property]]: The right of property is the first deduction from equal freedom — the right to exclusive use of one's own faculties and their products.
- [[sovereignty]]: Spencer critiques unlimited sovereignty precisely because it permits the violation of natural rights by majority vote.
- [[greatest-happiness]]: The utilitarian alternative; Spencer argued that happiness is better pursued indirectly through the protection of natural rights.
