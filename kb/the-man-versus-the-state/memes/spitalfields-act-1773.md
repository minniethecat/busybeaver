## Background

In the eighteenth century, Spitalfields in East London was the centre of English silk-weaving, a skilled trade employing several thousand artisans. The weavers faced intense competitive pressure from imported silks and fluctuating market demand, leading to depression of wages and widespread hardship. In this climate of distress, the weavers agitated for parliamentary protection from market forces that they believed were driving them into poverty.

The appeal to fix wages by statute was not unprecedented. English legislation had a long tradition of regulating wages, dating back to the Statute of Labourers under Edward III (1351), which compelled labourers to work for fixed wages after the Black Death had created labour shortages. Throughout the medieval and early modern periods, justices of the peace had statutory authority to set wages in various trades. By the mid-eighteenth century, however, the prevailing economic doctrine had shifted towards free-market principles, making the Spitalfields Act a notable late instance of direct wage-fixing.

The immediate impetus for the Act was the weavers' conviction that their declining earnings resulted not from market forces but from the greed of master-manufacturers who, in the weavers' view, exploited their position to drive wages below a subsistence level. The weavers lobbied Parliament intensively, presenting their case as a matter of justice and public welfare. Their arguments resonated with legislators who were receptive to interventionist measures aimed at mitigating visible distress.

## Description

The Act of 1773 (13 Geo. III) established a mechanism by which magistrates were empowered to fix and enforce minimum wage rates for silk-weavers in the Spitalfields district. The core provision stipulated that wages could not fall below rates determined by the local justices of the peace, who were to set rates periodically based on their assessment of what constituted a fair and reasonable remuneration. Master-manufacturers who paid below the fixed rates were subject to penalties.

The Act represented a direct intervention in the labour market. By prohibiting weavers from accepting work at rates below the statutory minimum and forbidding employers from offering such rates, the legislation effectively suspended the operation of supply and demand in the Spitalfields silk trade. The assumption underlying the measure was that the market, left to itself, produced unjust outcomes, and that magisterial authority could correct this by imposing a "fair" price for labour.

Subsequent Spitalfields Acts extended and modified the original framework. Most notably, the legislation also restricted the geographical location of silk factories, prohibiting manufacturers from establishing their works more than ten miles from the Royal Exchange in the City of London. This provision was intended to prevent employers from evading the wage regulations by relocating beyond the magistrates' jurisdiction.

## Significance / Impact

The consequences of the Spitalfields Acts proved disastrous for the very community they were intended to protect. By fixing wages above the level that market conditions could sustain, the legislation rendered Spitalfields silk production increasingly uncompetitive. Foreign and provincial competitors, not bound by the same wage constraints, could produce silk goods more cheaply. Trade migrated away from Spitalfields: by 1793, some 4,000 looms had been brought to a standstill as a result of the trade going elsewhere.

> No doubt the temptation which led them to obtain the Act of 1773, fixing a minimum of wages, was a strong one; and the anticipations of greater comfort to be secured by its enforcement must have seemed reasonable enough to all. Unfortunately, however, the weavers did not consider the consequences of being interdicted from working at reduced rates; and little expected that before 1793, some 4000 looms would be brought to a stand in consequence of the trade going elsewhere.
>
> — Herbert Spencer, *Social Statics* (1851), "Unguided Expediency"

The Acts remained in force until 1824, during which period they "long galvanized in Spitalfields a decaying industry, and fostered there a miserable population." Rather than preserving a thriving community of well-paid artisans, the legislation trapped weavers in a declining trade, deterring them from seeking alternative employment or adapting their skills to new market conditions. When Parliament finally repealed the Acts, the Spitalfields silk industry had already been irretrievably damaged.

Spencer placed the Spitalfields Acts at the centre of his argument against legislative interference with economic laws. He cited them alongside the Statute of Labourers and usury laws as examples of a persistent pattern: legislators, acting on philanthropic motives but ignorant of the complex dynamics of social and economic life, repeatedly produce consequences opposite to their intentions. The Spitalfields case illustrated what Spencer regarded as a general law — that interventions in the natural operation of supply and demand invariably create new evils more severe than those they were designed to cure.

> Of like spirit and followed by allied mischiefs have been the many endeavours to fix wages, which began with the Statute of Labourers under Edward III., and ceased only sixty years ago; when, having long galvanized in Spitalfields a decaying industry, and fostered there a miserable population, Lords and Commons finally gave up fixing silk-weavers' earnings by the decisions of magistrates.
>
> — Herbert Spencer, *The Man Versus The State* (1884), "The Sins of Legislators"

The Spitalfields episode also served Spencer's broader polemical purpose in *The Man Versus The State*. Writing in the 1880s, he observed that while the lesson of Spitalfields had been learned with respect to wage-fixing, the same interventionist impulse was being applied in new fields — factory legislation, public health, housing regulation — where he predicted analogous unintended consequences would follow.
