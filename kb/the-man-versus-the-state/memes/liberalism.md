## Definition

Liberalism, in Herbert Spencer's political philosophy, is the doctrine that the proper sphere of government is limited to the protection of individual rights, and that all legislation beyond this sphere constitutes an illegitimate expansion of state coercion. In *The Man Versus the State* (1884), Spencer defines true liberalism by its historical character: "in past times Liberalism habitually stood for individual freedom versus State-coercion."

Spencer's analysis is both historical and conceptual. Historically, liberalism emerged as the force that dismantled feudal and ecclesiastical restraints — liberating trade, abolishing religious tests, extending the franchise, and securing personal liberties. Conceptually, liberalism corresponds to what Spencer calls the "industrial" type of social organization, characterized by voluntary cooperation, as opposed to the "militant" type based on hierarchy and compulsion.

## Key Arguments

### Liberalism as the Removal of Restraints

Spencer argues that every genuine liberal measure in British history took the form of removing some existing restraint on individual action. The repeal of the Corn Laws liberated trade; Catholic Emancipation removed religious disabilities; the Reform Acts extended political participation. The common character of these measures was negative — they *removed* barriers rather than *imposing* new obligations.

This pattern, Spencer contends, is not accidental. It follows from the nature of industrial society, which requires maximum individual freedom to coordinate complex voluntary exchanges. Liberalism thus aligned with the deepest evolutionary tendencies of modern civilization.

### The Transformation into New Toryism

Spencer's central thesis in *The Man Versus the State* is that liberalism, from the mid-nineteenth century onward, abandoned its defining principle. "Most of those who now pass as Liberals, are Tories of a new type." The "new Tories" continued to use the language of reform, but the character of their legislation inverted: instead of removing restraints, they multiplied them.

Factory Acts, public health regulations, compulsory education, state-funded sanitation — each of these, Spencer argues, extended state authority into domains previously left to individual choice and voluntary association. Liberals had come to value the *ends* of earlier reforms (popular welfare) without recognizing that those ends had been achieved by the *means* of removing restraints, not by imposing new ones.

### The Confusion of Ends and Means

Spencer identifies a fatal semantic shift at the root of this transformation. Liberal reforms were historically associated with popular benefits — cheaper bread, political representation, religious liberty. Over time, "the gaining of a popular good, being the external conspicuous trait common to Liberal measures in earlier days," came to be seen as the *essence* of liberalism itself. The means (dismantling state power) were forgotten; the end (popular welfare) was redefined as the criterion of liberal legislation.

This confusion, Spencer warns, is self-reinforcing. Once the state is seen as the proper instrument of every popular good, there is no logical stopping point. Each new intervention creates constituencies that demand further interventions, and the sphere of individual freedom contracts accordingly.

### Militant vs. Industrial Social Types

Underlying Spencer's analysis of liberalism is his broader sociological distinction between two types of social organization. "Dating back to an earlier period than their names, the two political parties at first stood respectively for two opposed types of social organization, broadly distinguishable as the militant and the industrial."

The militant type, corresponding to Toryism, organizes society through hierarchy, command, and compulsory cooperation — the organizational logic of warfare. The industrial type, corresponding to true liberalism, organizes society through contract, voluntary exchange, and spontaneous cooperation. Spencer's critique of the new liberalism is that it has adopted the organizational logic of militancy (state compulsion) while retaining the liberal name.

## Historical Context

Spencer wrote *The Man Versus the State* in 1884, at a moment when British politics was being reshaped by the expansion of the franchise (Reform Acts of 1867 and 1884) and the rise of interventionist legislation. The Factory Acts, the Public Health Acts, and the Education Act of 1870 had each extended the regulatory reach of the state into domains previously governed by private arrangement.

Spencer saw in these developments not progress but regression — a return to the paternalistic, status-based social order that liberalism had spent centuries dismantling. His analysis drew on the evolutionary sociology he had developed in *Social Statics* (1851) and *The Study of Sociology* (1873), which framed the movement from militant to industrial society as the master trend of human history.
