## Background

The Declaration of Independence was adopted by the Continental Congress on July 4, 1776, in the midst of the American Revolutionary War. Drafted primarily by Thomas Jefferson, with revisions by John Adams and Benjamin Franklin, it articulated the philosophical and legal justifications for the Thirteen Colonies' separation from the British Crown. The declaration drew heavily on the natural rights philosophy of John Locke and the English radical Whig tradition, grounding its claims in the assertion that "all men are created equal" and endowed with "unalienable Rights" including "Life, Liberty and the pursuit of Happiness."

By the time Spencer wrote in the mid-19th century, the Declaration was already recognized as a foundational text of modern liberal democracy. Spencer's interest, however, was not in the Declaration as a historical artifact but in the two distinct arguments it could be made to serve: the philosophical claim of human equality and the practical indictment of state-administered colonial governance.

## Description

The Declaration consists of three main sections: a preamble asserting the philosophical basis of the right of revolution (the famous "self-evident" truths); an indictment of King George III listing twenty-seven specific grievances; and a concluding declaration of sovereignty. It was signed by fifty-six delegates and disseminated throughout the colonies and abroad as both a legal instrument and a work of political persuasion.

Spencer quoted the Declaration at two distinct junctures in *Social Statics*, using it for different purposes. On page 47, he invoked the Declaration's philosophical preamble as evidence of the universal human tendency to recognize natural equality of rights:

> "Again, we find the declaration of American independence affirming that 'all men have equal rights to life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness.'"

On page 194, Spencer turned to the Declaration's grievances section, quoting at length the colonists' complaints against George III as a contemporaneous account of the evils of state-managed colonization:

> "In the Declaration of American Independence we have a candid statement of experience on this point. Speaking of the king — the personification of the parent State — the settlers say: — 'He has obstructed the administration of justice, by refusing his assent to laws for establishing judiciary powers. He has erected a multitude of new offices, and sent hither swarms of officers to harass our people, and eat out their substance. He has kept among us in times of peace standing armies, without the consent of our legislatures.'"

## Significance / Impact

Spencer's dual use of the Declaration reveals its layered significance in his political philosophy. First, the Declaration served as authoritative testimony that the belief in natural rights — the "equality of human rights" — was not a philosopher's abstraction but a conviction embedded in the foundational acts of modern nations. Spencer cited the Declaration alongside Locke, Blackstone, Hooker, and Granville Sharpe as part of a continuous tradition of asserting the primacy of individual rights against arbitrary government.

Second, the Declaration's catalogue of grievances provided Spencer with a ready-made indictment of state-managed colonization. The complaints — obstruction of justice, multiplication of offices, quartering of troops, taxation without consent, cutting off trade — were, in Spencer's reading, not peculiar to 18th-century British rule but the generic consequences of governing colonies through a centralized imperial bureaucracy. He connected these grievances to the contemporary discontent in Canada, South Africa, the West Indies, and Australia, arguing that "though tyrannies so atrocious as these do not commonly disgrace colonial legislation in the present day," the underlying pattern persisted.

Spencer placed the Declaration's assertion of human equality in deliberate tension with Benthamite utilitarianism, which he saw as logically committed to denying the existence of natural rights altogether. He argued that Bentham's "greatest happiness" principle, when examined, "explodes into the astounding assertion, that all men have equal rights to happiness — an assertion far more sweeping and revolutionary than any of those which are assailed with so much scorn."
