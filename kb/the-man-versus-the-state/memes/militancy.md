## Definition

Militancy, in Herbert Spencer's sociology and political theory, denotes one of two fundamental types of social organization. The militant type is characterized by "compulsory cooperation" — the coordination of social activity through command and obedience, modeled on military hierarchy — and by the "regime of status," in which an individual's rights, duties, and social position are determined by birth and rank rather than by voluntary agreement. It stands in opposition to the "industrial type," characterized by "voluntary cooperation" and the "regime of contract."

Spencer introduces the distinction most explicitly in *Man Versus the State* (1884), where he argues that the original political parties of England — Tory and Liberal — "stood respectively for two opposed types of social organization, broadly distinguishable as the militant and the industrial."

## Compulsory vs. Voluntary Cooperation

The defining institutional difference between the two types lies in the mode of cooperation each employs. In the militant type, cooperation is compulsory: "The typical structure of the one we see in an army formed of conscripts, in which the units in their several grades have to fulfil commands under pain of death, and receive food and pay in proportions determined by their ranks."

In the industrial type, cooperation is voluntary: individuals coordinate their activities through contract and exchange, each pursuing his own ends while benefiting others through the division of labor. The distinction is not merely economic but constitutional — it concerns the fundamental relationship between the individual and the collective, between rights and authority, between liberty and command.

## Regime of Status vs. Regime of Contract

The militant type's regime of status fixes each person's position by inheritance or decree. Social hierarchy is rigid, mobility is limited, and the individual's obligations to the collective — military service, tribute, obedience — take priority over personal autonomy. Spencer saw this as "almost universal in ancient days."

The industrial type's regime of contract makes social position a function of voluntary agreement. Individuals enter into obligations by consent, exchange goods and services on negotiated terms, and possess rights that are not grants of the sovereign but conditions of social cooperation itself. This type, Spencer observed, "has become general in modern days, chiefly among the Western nations, and especially among ourselves and the Americans."

## The New Toryism

Spencer's immediate political purpose in developing the militant-industrial distinction was to diagnose what he called "The New Toryism" — the tendency of nineteenth-century British Liberals to adopt policies characteristic of the militant type while retaining the Liberal name. The expansion of state regulation, the growth of bureaucracy, and the multiplication of compulsory interventions were, in Spencer's analysis, reversions to militant patterns of organization under the guise of progressive reform.

"Most of those who now pass as Liberals, are Tories of a new type," Spencer wrote. The Liberal party, which had historically stood for the reduction of state power and the expansion of individual liberty, had become the vehicle for a new form of compulsory cooperation — justified not by divine right but by expediency, but producing the same structural result: the subordination of individual will to central command.

## The Evolutionary Framework

Spencer embeds the militant-industrial distinction within a broader theory of social evolution. Societies, like organisms, evolve from simpler to more complex forms. The militant type is adapted to an environment of chronic warfare: it requires swift, unified action under centralized command, and it subordinates individual interests to collective survival.

The industrial type emerges as warfare recedes and peaceful exchange becomes the dominant mode of social interaction. Under these conditions, the rigid hierarchies of militant organization become maladaptive; the flexibility, innovation, and individual initiative fostered by voluntary cooperation yield superior outcomes.

Crucially, Spencer does not present this as a completed transition. "So long as the religion of enmity predominates over the religion of amity, the current political superstition must hold its ground." The militant type persists not merely in institutions but in habits of thought — in the unquestioned assumption that centralized authority is the natural form of social order.

## Militancy and Political Superstition

The connection between militancy and the political superstition is direct. The belief in unlimited governmental authority "is a residue of the militant type of social organization." Under conditions of chronic warfare, such authority was functionally necessary. But as societies transition toward peaceful industrial cooperation, the same unlimited authority becomes both unnecessary and destructive — a superstition, in Spencer's precise sense: a belief retained after the conditions that justified it have disappeared.

This is why Spencer closes "The Great Political Superstition" with the observation that the superstition's decline depends on the advance of industrial organization and the corresponding retreat of militant institutions and attitudes. Political theory and social structure are inseparable; you cannot have limited government in a society still organized for war.
