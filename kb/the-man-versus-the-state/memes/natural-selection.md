## Definition

Natural selection, the evolutionary mechanism identified by Charles Darwin and independently formulated by Herbert Spencer (who coined the phrase "survival of the fittest"), denotes the process by which organisms better adapted to their environment survive and reproduce at higher rates than those less adapted. Spencer extended this biological principle into social and political theory, arguing that the discipline imposed by natural selection on human populations — the elimination of maladaptive traits and the propagation of adaptive ones — is a beneficent force that state intervention systematically and destructively obstructs.

## Spencer and Darwin

Spencer's engagement with natural selection predates Darwin's *Origin of Species* (1859). Spencer had published his own evolutionary theory in "*The Development Hypothesis*" (1852) and had used the phrase "survival of the fittest" in *Principles of Biology* (1864) — a term Darwin himself later adopted as a synonym for natural selection. Spencer acknowledged Darwin's priority in establishing the mechanism's biological importance, writing in *Man Versus the State* that "the process of 'natural selection,' as Mr. Darwin called it, co-operating with a tendency to variation and to inheritance of variations, he has shown to be a chief cause... of that evolution through which all living things, beginning with the lowest and diverging and re-diverging as they evolved, have reached their present degrees of organization and adaptation."

But Spencer drew different practical conclusions. Where Darwin was cautious about social application, Spencer insisted that the same mechanism operates in human society — and that deliberate interference with it produces perverse results.

## The Discipline of Experience

Spencer's social application of natural selection centers on the concept of adaptation. Human beings, like all organisms, are shaped by their conditions of existence. Nature secures progress "partly by weeding out those of lowest development, and partly by subjecting those who remain to the never-ceasing discipline of experience."

State intervention, however well-intentioned, suspends this discipline. "If to be ignorant were as safe as to be wise, no one would become wise." Every measure that shields individuals from the consequences of their actions — from medical licensing that protects the gullible from quacks, to poor laws that blunt the penalty of idleness — diminishes the pressure toward adaptive behavior.

## Survival of the Unfittest

Spencer's most provocative application of natural selection appears in his critique of late-Victorian social legislation. "Now that this truth is recognized by most cultivated people — now that the beneficent working of the survival of the fittest has been so impressed on them... now more than ever before in the history of the world, are they doing all they can to further survival of the unfittest!"

Spencer was not advocating cruelty. His argument was consequentialist: measures that preserve the maladapted from the consequences of their maladaptation increase the proportion of the maladapted in future generations. Sympathy that "prompts to a breach of equity" by suspending the relationship between constitution and conditions "favours the multiplication of those worst fitted for existence, and, by consequence, hinders the multiplication of those best fitted for existence — leaving, as it does, less room for them."

## The Sanitary Supervision Argument

Spencer's most detailed application of natural selection to policy appears in his chapter on "Sanitary Supervision" in *Social Statics*. The argument that the state should protect citizens from quacks and unsanitary conditions, he contends, ignores the adaptive function of suffering the consequences of poor judgment. The person who patronizes an incompetent physician and suffers accordingly learns to discriminate; others who observe the case learn vicariously. State intervention short-circuits this process.

"A sad population of imbeciles would our schemers fill the world with, could their plans last. A sorry kind of human constitution would they make for us — a constitution continually going wrong, and needing to be set right again — a constitution ever tending to self-destruction. Why the whole effort of Nature, is to get rid of such — to clear the world of them, and make room for better."

## The Margaret Case

Spencer illustrates the principle with a striking empirical example. In a county on the Upper Hudson River in New York, remarkable for its ratio of crime and poverty to population, investigators traced the problem to a single "gutter-child" known as "Margaret" — "the prolific mother of a prolific race" whose descendants, generation after generation, filled the prisons and poorhouses. The case demonstrated, for Spencer, the cumulative social cost of artificially preserving those whom natural selection would eliminate: a single intervention at the start could have prevented generations of misery.

## The Limits of the Analogy

Spencer's application of natural selection to society has limits that he himself partly recognized. He did not advocate the abolition of all charity or the complete withdrawal of state protection. His argument was that each intervention should be judged by its aggregate and long-run consequences, not merely by its immediate humanitarian appeal. The law of equal freedom — the principle that each individual's liberty is limited only by the equal liberty of others — represents, for Spencer, the social expression of natural selection's wisdom: it allows the "discipline of experience" to operate within bounds that prevent the strong from preying on the weak, while still ensuring that maladaptive behavior carries its natural costs.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

- [[survival-of-the-fittest]] — Spencer's own term for natural selection; in his social theory, the two are closely related but distinct in application.
- [[sanitary-supervision]] — Spencer's primary case study in the social application of natural selection principles.
- [[law-of-equal-freedom]] — The principle that sets the bounds within which natural selection may legitimately operate in society.
- [[expediency]] — The utilitarian method that, by evaluating each case in isolation, systematically ignores the selective consequences of state action.
- [[militancy]] — Militant societies suspend natural selection through compulsory cooperation; industrial societies permit it through voluntary cooperation.
- [[industrialism]] — The social type most compatible with natural selection, because it minimizes the artificial suspension of adaptive discipline.
