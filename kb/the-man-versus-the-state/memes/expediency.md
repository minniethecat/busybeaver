## Definition

Expediency, in Herbert Spencer's political philosophy, denotes the method of evaluating state actions case-by-case according to their expected contribution to "the greatest happiness," without reference to any fixed limiting principle. It is the practical expression of utilitarian ethics in governance, and Spencer devotes the opening chapters of *Social Statics* (1851) to exposing what he considers its fatal defects. The expediency-philosophy, he argues, is not merely inadequate but actively harmful: it provides intellectual cover for unlimited state expansion while delivering results inferior to those produced by adherence to a first principle.

## The Indefiniteness Critique

Spencer's first objection is that the central term of the expediency system — "greatest happiness" — has no stable meaning. "In all ages — amongst every people — by each class — do we find different notions of it entertained." The gipsy's conception of happiness differs from the Swiss peasant's; the Hebrew heaven differs from the Norse; the Australian aboriginal hopes after death to "jump up a white fellow, and have plenty of sixpences." A principle whose key term admits of infinite variation cannot serve as a guide to action.

But Spencer pushes deeper. Even if all mankind agreed on what constitutes happiness, the expediency system would still fail, because "there yet remains the unwarranted assumption that it is possible to determine empirically by what methods it may be achieved." Human foresight is radically insufficient to the complexity of social causation. Spencer illustrates this with legislative case studies: the Bavarian marriage restriction intended to check population growth instead produced a city where "half the births are illegitimate"; the British armed squadron on the African coast meant to suppress the slave trade produced unintended consequences that frustrated the original purpose.

The statute-book itself, Spencer observes, is "but a record of such unhappy guesses," and "nearly every parliamentary proceeding is a tacit confession of incompetence" — nearly every bill introduced is entitled "An Act to amend an Act."

## Despotism Democratized

Spencer's most politically charged critique identifies the expediency-philosophy as a form of absolutism in democratic dress. In the chapter "Political Rights," he writes:

"There have been books written to prove that the monarch's will should be the subject's absolute law; and if instead of monarch we read legislature, we have the expediency-theory. It merely modifies 'divine right of kings' into divine right of majorities. It is despotism democratized."

The structural similarity is the key point. Under monarchy, the subject possessed rights only at the sovereign's pleasure; under the expediency system, the citizen "is allowed to possess rights, but only by sufferance of parliament." The form of the ruling body has changed but the logic of unlimited authority persists. Between the old regime of total subjection and Spencer's envisioned future of "entire and inviolable" rights, expediency represents an intermediate and unstable stage — a halfway house between slavery and freedom.

## The Steward Analogy

Spencer develops a memorable analogy to illustrate the presumption of expediency-based government. A steward appointed to manage an estate who proceeded to argue that the owner had "virtually resigned" the property into his possession — that the owner now lived on it only by the steward's sufferance — and that all future emoluments would be dispensed at the steward's "good pleasure" — such a steward would be recognized as a usurper. Yet this, Spencer contends, is precisely "an appropriate travesty upon the behaviour of governments to nations."

The analogy crystallizes Spencer's core claim: government is properly an agent of the governed, not their proprietor. The expediency-philosophy reverses this relationship by treating the rights of citizens as grants from the state rather than as the prior conditions that the state exists to protect.

## The Definitional Challenge

Spencer challenges the expediency-philosopher to produce something the philosophy conspicuously lacks: a definition. "To say that government ought to do that which is 'expedient,' or to do that which will tend to produce the 'greatest happiness'... is to say just nothing; for there are countless disagreements respecting the natures of these desiderata."

Between the extremes of total non-interference and total paternalism "there lie innumerable stopping places; and he who would have the State do more than protect, is required to say where he means to draw the line, and to give us reasons why it must be just there and nowhere else." Spencer contends that this challenge has never been met. The expediency tradition has no criterion for distinguishing legitimate from illegitimate state action; it is, in practice, a license for unlimited regulation.

## Empirical vs. Rational Utilitarianism

Spencer does not reject utilitarian reasoning entirely. He distinguishes between "empirical utilitarianism" — the Benthamite practice of judging each intervention by its immediate and visible effects — and "rational utilitarianism," which takes account of "the effects produced by a multiplicity of such interferences on the lives of men at large." The former examines only the proximate consequences of particular policies; the latter recognizes that the general conditions of social life — security of person and property, freedom of contract — must be maintained even when specific interventions promise local benefits.

The error of empirical utilitarianism, Spencer argues, is the same error that preceded the French Revolution: "so excessive a regulation of men's actions in all their details, and such an enormous drafting away of the products of their actions to maintain the regulating organization, that life was fast becoming impracticable." Expediency, pursued case by case without limiting principle, produces a cumulative burden that its practitioners never see because they never look at the aggregate.

## The Self-Defeating Nature of State Happiness-Provision

Spencer argues that the expediency-philosophy embodies a deeper confusion: "the belief that government ought not only to guarantee men the unmolested pursuit of happiness, but should provide the happiness for them." This project is self-defeating because human beings consist of faculties that produce gratification *through their own exercise*. To supersede a faculty by state action is to leave it unexercised, "and therefore to diminish his happiness."

"To do anything for him by some artificial agency, is to supersede certain of his powers — is to leave them unexercised, and therefore to diminish his happiness." The very attempt to provide happiness through state machinery destroys the mechanism — active, self-directed faculty-exercise — by which happiness is produced.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

- [[political-superstition]] — Expediency is the methodological engine of the political superstition; it provides the case-by-case justification for unlimited state authority.
- [[law-of-equal-freedom]] — Spencer's first principle is the alternative to expediency: a fixed limiting rule against which all proposed state actions can be tested.
- [[utilitarianism]] — Expediency is Spencer's term for utilitarian method as applied to legislation; the two concepts are closely related but expediency emphasizes the practical failure of utilitarian guidance.
- [[rights]] — Under expediency, rights are contingent grants of the state; under Spencer's natural-rights framework, they are pre-political conditions of social life.
- [[divine-right]] — Spencer traces a direct lineage: divine right of kings → divine right of parliaments → expediency (divine right of majorities).
- [[greatest-happiness]] — The Benthamite formula that expediency purports to pursue but, Spencer argues, cannot coherently define.
