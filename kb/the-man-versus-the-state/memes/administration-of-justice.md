## Definition

The **administration of justice** is the state function of adjudicating disputes between individuals, enforcing legally recognized rights, and providing a forum for the peaceful resolution of conflicts. For Herbert Spencer, the administration of justice constituted the very core of legitimate state activity — "the vital necessity of civilized life" — yet he devoted extensive analysis to the gap between this ideal and the corrupt, costly, and inaccessible reality of the English judicial system in his time.

> While, on the one hand, they admit that administration of justice is the vital necessity of civilized life, they maintain, on the other, that justice may be administered too well!

Spencer defined the proper scope of the state's judicial function in rigorously limited terms: the state exists to protect individuals against aggression, and the administration of justice is the mechanism through which that protection is delivered. It is not a general instrument for social improvement, redistribution, or moral regulation, but a specific and bounded duty — to secure each person's rights against infringement by others.

## Key Arguments

Spencer set out his analysis of the administration of justice across several interconnected arguments, each building on his broader theory of limited government.

**The foundational character of judicial administration**. For Spencer, the administration of justice stands alongside national defence as one of the two essential and legitimate functions of the state. Addressing wrongs between citizens and punishing violations of rights is, in his framework, the reason governments exist. The state's role is not to confer benefits but to prevent harms: "it is the duty of the state to protect, not to aggrandise."

> Administration of justice was originally impracticable, Utopian, and has become more and more practicable only as men have become less savage. The old system of settling disputes by personal contest, and the new system of settling them by State-arbitration, have coexisted throughout all ages.

**The corruption of the existing system**. A substantial portion of Spencer's writing on the subject is devoted to cataloguing the failures of the English legal apparatus. He denounced the costliness of litigation, the venality of legal officers, the capriciousness of judicial decisions, and the fact that the system effectively excluded the poor from seeking redress. The gap between the theoretical purpose of judicial institutions and their actual operation was, in his view, a scandal of the first order.

> The dangers of law are proverbial. The names of its officers are used as synonyms for trickery and greediness. The decisions of its courts are typical of chance.

**The cost argument and its refutation**. Spencer addressed and rejected the claim that making justice cheap would produce an intolerable flood of litigation. The argument — "were law cheap, all men would avail themselves of it" — he regarded as either absurd or disingenuous. If one litigation is worse than one injustice, then the logical conclusion would be to have no administration of justice at all. The real issue, Spencer contended, was not that justice was too accessible but that the existing system's expense systematically excluded those most in need of protection.

> If, for the sake of escaping this absurdity, it be assumed that, as things now are, all great wrongs are rectified — that the costliness of law prevents insignificant ones only from being brought into court — then denial is given to the obvious fact that, by the poverty they inflict, many of the greatest wrongs incapacitate their victims from obtaining redress.

**The evolutionary perspective**. Spencer situated the administration of justice within his broader theory of social evolution. The transition from private vengeance to state arbitration was not a sudden invention but a gradual development paralleling the moral improvement of society. The persistence of archaic forms — trial by battle, which nominally continued as late as 1819 in England — demonstrated how slowly judicial institutions shed their primitive character. The "partially-surviving code of ethics arising in, and proper to, a state of war, still vitiates governmental action."

**The connection to individual character**. A recurring theme in Spencer's analysis was the interdependence of judicial institutions and the moral quality of the population. A corrupt people could not sustain an honest judiciary; a violent society could not support a peaceful system of arbitration. The administration of justice could improve only as fast as the people themselves improved — a point with profound implications for political reform, since it meant that institutional change alone, without moral progress, was insufficient.

> It is impossible for a judicial system to become efficient faster than men become good. It is only after public morality has gained a certain ascendency, that the civil power gets strong enough to perform its simplest functions.

## Historical Context

Spencer's treatment of the administration of justice drew on a long tradition of English legal criticism. The Court of Chancery's notorious delays and expenses, the abuses of the ecclesiastical courts, and the general inaccessibility of legal remedies to the poor were widely acknowledged problems in Victorian Britain. Jeremy Bentham had devoted decades to proposals for legal reform, and the County Courts Act of 1846 had only recently established more accessible local tribunals.

What distinguished Spencer's contribution was his refusal to treat judicial reform as a merely technical or administrative question. For him, the administration of justice was inseparable from the broader theory of state duty — and the state's persistent failure to deliver justice efficiently was not an argument for entrusting it with additional functions, but a warning against any expansion of its authority.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

- **[[rights]]**: The administration of justice is the practical mechanism through which rights — particularly the right to personal security and property — are enforced.
- **[[state-authority]]**: The legitimate scope and limits of state power, of which the administration of justice is the core function.
- **[[negative-beneficence]]**: Spencer's concept of the duty to refrain from harming others, which the administration of justice exists to enforce.
