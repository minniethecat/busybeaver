## Nature / Structure

The Church of England is the established Christian church in England, with the British monarch serving as its Supreme Governor. Its governance is hierarchical: the Archbishops of Canterbury and York, diocesan bishops, and parish clergy form a structure integrated into the state through the presence of bishops in the House of Lords and through parliamentary control over certain ecclesiastical legislation.

Spencer addresses the Established Church in his chapter on "Religious Establishments" in *Social Statics*. He sees the state-church relationship as a case study in the broader problem of state action: the use of coercive power to support religious institutions that should, by right, be matters of purely voluntary association.

## Functions / Powers

The Church of England historically exercised extensive powers: it operated the parish-based system of poor relief (before 1834), controlled marriage law, maintained the ecclesiastical courts (with jurisdiction over probate and matrimonial causes until 1857), and held a monopoly over university education at Oxford and Cambridge through religious tests (repealed 1854–1871).

Spencer's critique focuses not on any particular abuse but on the principle of establishment itself. The state, by compelling citizens to support a particular religious institution through taxation (church rates), violates the law of equal freedom: it compels those who do not share the established faith to subsidize its propagation. This is a trespass against liberty no different in kind from any other exercise of coercive power.

In Spencer's analysis, the Dissenters' long struggle against church rates was an exemplary instance of the broader liberal project: the removal of state coercion from a sphere of life properly governed by voluntary choice.

## Historical Development

The Church of England was established through the English Reformation in the sixteenth century, separating from Rome under Henry VIII. Its character as a comprehensive national church — broad enough to encompass considerable theological diversity — was shaped by the Elizabethan Settlement (1559) and the Restoration (1660).

The nineteenth century witnessed a progressive erosion of the Church's established privileges. The repeal of the Test and Corporation Acts (1828), Catholic Emancipation (1829), the admission of Dissenters to Oxford and Cambridge (1854–1871), the abolition of compulsory church rates (1868), and the disestablishment of the Church of Ireland (1869) collectively represented what Spencer saw as the gradual triumph of the voluntary principle over the coercive one.

Spencer viewed this trajectory as evidence for his evolutionary theory of social progress. "In our day... Dissenters were allowed to believe as they pleased without suffering certain civil penalties... Catholics enabled to profess their religion without losing part of their freedom." Each step represented a diminution of "compulsory co-operation" and an expansion of "voluntary co-operation" — the defining characteristic of genuine liberalism.

## Relationship to the State

For Spencer, the Church of England exemplifies the broader category of "religious establishments" — state-supported institutions that violate the principle of equal freedom by compelling citizens to support beliefs they may not share. The proper relationship between religion and the state, in Spencer's framework, is one of complete separation: religion is a matter of individual conscience and voluntary association, while the state's sole legitimate function is the protection of individual rights.

Spencer extends this argument beyond religion to all forms of state-enforced belief or practice. The logic that condemns an established church equally condemns state education, state-sponsored morality, and any other attempt to use coercive power to shape the beliefs or character of citizens. As he argued in *The Man Versus the State*, the growth of state intervention in education, health, and welfare merely extends the principle of establishment into new domains.
