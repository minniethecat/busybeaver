## Biography

Samuel Taylor Coleridge (1772–1834) was an English poet, literary critic, and philosopher, best known as a founding figure of the Romantic movement alongside his collaborator William Wordsworth. Born in Ottery St Mary, Devon, the youngest son of a clergyman, Coleridge was educated at Christ's Hospital and Jesus College, Cambridge, though he left without taking a degree.

His early poetic achievements — *The Rime of the Ancient Mariner*, *Kubla Khan*, and *Christabel* — established him as one of the most original poetic voices in English literature. But from the 1810s onward, Coleridge turned increasingly to philosophical and theological writing. His *Biographia Literaria* (1817) introduced German idealist philosophy — particularly the thought of Kant, Schelling, and the Schlegel brothers — to an English audience.

Herbert Spencer drew on Coleridge's work in two contexts: his remark that "a knave is a fool with a circumbendibus," which Spencer used to argue that intellect alone cannot produce moral conduct, and his theory of life as the "tendency to individuation," which Spencer developed into a broader evolutionary framework connecting biological and moral progress.

## Key Ideas

### Intellect and Moral Character

Coleridge's aphorism — "a knave is a fool with a circumbendibus" — encapsulated a view that Spencer found congenial: that intellect is instrumental rather than directive in human conduct. A clever person may simply find more roundabout ways to satisfy the same base desires.

Spencer quoted Coleridge directly in his chapter on national education: "If, as Coleridge says, 'a knave is a fool with a circumbendibus,' then, by instructing the knave, you do but make the circumbendibus a wider one." For Spencer, this supported the argument that education alone — without the formation of character through the natural consequences of actions — cannot produce moral improvement.

### The Tendency to Individuation

Coleridge's most philosophically ambitious contribution was his definition of life. As Spencer quoted: "By life, I everywhere mean the true idea of life, or that most general form under which life manifests itself to us, which includes all other forms. This I have stated to be the tendency to individuation; and the degrees or intensities of life to consist in the progressive realizations of this tendency."

Spencer used this definition as a launching point for his own evolutionary ethics, arguing that "only by entire fulfilment of the moral law can life become complete" and that the tendency toward individuation — each person realising their distinct nature — is the direction of all progress.

Spencer later noted, in a revision of the text, that Coleridge was "indebted to Schelling for this idea" and that the principle of individuation could be reinterpreted in physical terms as the process of evolutionary integration — matter becoming more organised and differentiated over time.

### German Idealism and English Thought

Coleridge served as a crucial conduit through which German philosophical ideas entered British intellectual life. His introduction of Kantian epistemology, Schelling's nature-philosophy, and the historical criticism of the Higher Biblical Critics shaped a generation of English thinkers, including Thomas Carlyle and, indirectly, Herbert Spencer — even though Spencer's naturalism ultimately pointed in a very different direction.

## Major Works

- *The Rime of the Ancient Mariner* (1798) — His most celebrated poem, published in *Lyrical Ballads* with Wordsworth
- *Biographia Literaria* (1817) — A philosophical autobiography introducing German idealism to English readers
- *Aids to Reflection* (1825) — Religious and philosophical aphorisms, influential in the Broad Church movement
- *On the Constitution of the Church and State* (1830) — His final major prose work on the cultural role of a national church

## Influence / Legacy

Coleridge's influence on Herbert Spencer is most clearly visible in Spencer's adoption and transformation of the idea of individuation. Where Coleridge treated individuation as a metaphysical tendency inherent in life itself, Spencer reinterpreted it as a physical process: the integration of matter and dissipation of motion that characterises all evolution, organic and inorganic.

Coleridge's warning about the limits of intellect — that teaching a knave only widens his "circumbendibus" — also resonated with Spencer's broader argument that moral progress depends on the adaptation of character to social conditions rather than on the inculcation of correct opinions. This placed Coleridge, somewhat unexpectedly, in the genealogy of Spencer's evolutionary ethics.
