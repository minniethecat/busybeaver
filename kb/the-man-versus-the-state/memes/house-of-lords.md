## Nature / Structure

The House of Lords is the upper chamber of the [[british-parliament]], sitting alongside the [[house-of-commons]] and the Crown-in-Parliament. In the nineteenth century — the period of [[herbert-spencer]]'s political writings — the Lords was composed overwhelmingly of hereditary peers: dukes, marquesses, earls, viscounts, and barons who held their seats by birthright.

The Lords also included 26 "Lords Spiritual" — the Archbishops of Canterbury and York and 24 senior bishops of the [[church-of-england]] — making the Upper House an institutional fusion of aristocracy and established religion. A small number of "Law Lords" (life peers appointed to exercise the House's judicial functions) supplemented the hereditary membership.

Unlike the Commons, the Lords was not elected and not accountable to any constituency. Its members served for life, transmitted their seats to their heirs, and could not be removed by voters. This structural insulation from democratic accountability was, for Spencer, both the Lords' distinctive character and its fundamental defect: it embodied the principle — inherited from feudalism — that some persons are born to rule others.

## Functions / Powers

Before the Parliament Act of 1911, the House of Lords possessed a co-equal legislative power with the Commons, save for the convention that money bills originated in the lower house. The Lords could amend, delay, or (in practice, though increasingly rarely after 1832) reject legislation passed by the Commons.

The House of Lords also served as the highest court of appeal in the United Kingdom — a judicial function exercised by the Law Lords sitting as the Appellate Committee. This fusion of legislative and judicial power in a single body was anomalous by modern standards but characteristic of the pre-modern British constitution.

Spencer focused on the legislative role of the Lords in his broader critique of parliamentary government. For Spencer, the question was not whether the Lords was well-intentioned or competent — some peers were, some were not — but whether the institution itself was consistent with the [[law-of-equal-freedom]]. A legislator who holds his seat by accident of birth exercises power over others without their consent. This is not governance by agreement but governance by status inherited from a feudal past.

> "That a man should have a voice in the making of laws which affect the lives and property of millions, simply because he is the son of his father, is a proposition for which no rational defense can be offered."

## Historical Development

The House of Lords evolved from the *Curia Regis* (King's Council) of the Norman and Angevin periods. By the thirteenth century, the *magnum concilium* — the assembly of great barons and prelates summoned by the king — had begun to differentiate itself from the representatives of the shires and boroughs who would form the Commons.

The constitutional settlement after the [[glorious-revolution]] (1688) confirmed the Lords as a permanent part of the legislative apparatus, but the balance of power between the two Houses shifted gradually toward the Commons across the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries. The [[reform-act-1832]] — which the Lords reluctantly passed under threat of being swamped with new pro-reform peers — demonstrated that the Upper House could delay but not permanently obstruct the popular will.

Spencer wrote in the aftermath of this transformation, and his critique was directed at the hereditary principle that survived it. The fact that the Lords had yielded on the Reform Bill did not, in Spencer's view, remedy the underlying injustice of hereditary legislation. A body that retains the *power* to obstruct — whether or not it exercises that power prudently — is a body that stands above the equal claims of citizens.

## Spencer's Critique

Spencer's objection to the House of Lords was not primarily democratic — he was no enthusiast for majoritarian government, which he regarded as the "divine right of majorities" — but libertarian. The defect of the Lords was not that it was unelected but that it exercised legislative power over persons who had not consented to its authority.

This objection applied equally to the [[house-of-commons]], in Spencer's view. The difference between hereditary legislators and elected legislators was one of degree, not kind. Both claimed the authority to make laws binding on individuals who had not consented to them. The Commons had the advantage of numbers — a majority rather than a hereditary caste — but majorities, too, can violate equal freedom. The "Great Political Superstition" was the belief that numbers alone confer legitimate authority.

Spencer's ideal was a society in which legislation was minimal — reduced to the administration of [[justice]] — and in which all coercive institutions, whether hereditary or democratic, had withered to the dimensions of a protective association. In such a society, the question "Lords or Commons?" would be moot: neither body would exist because neither function (making laws for others) would be legitimate.

## Key Figures

- [[arthur-wellesley]] (Duke of Wellington) — Led the Tory peers; his reluctant acceptance of Catholic Emancipation and the Reform Act demonstrated the Lords' vulnerability to royal and popular pressure.
- [[lord-shaftesbury]] — Evangelical reformer and peer who used his position in the Lords to advance factory legislation; an example of the "benevolent legislator" whose benevolence, in Spencer's view, did not cure the defect of his legislative power.
