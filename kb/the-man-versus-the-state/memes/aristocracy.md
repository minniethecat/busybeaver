## Definition

**Aristocracy** is a form of government in which a minority class — typically defined by birth, wealth, or military status — holds political power and exercises authority over the majority. Herbert Spencer understood aristocracy not as a static constitutional form but as one point on a continuum of political evolution. In *Social Statics*, he described "aristocracy and democracy" as marking "the stages in the progress towards complete individuality" — the historical process by which the state's claims over the individual diminish and personal independence expands. Aristocracy represented, in Spencer's evolutionary schema, an advance beyond pure despotism but a condition still far from the ideal of voluntary association that characterised the fully industrial type of society.

> Aristocracy and democracy are not, as they have been called, separate and conflicting principles; but they and their various mixtures with each other and with monarchy, mark the stages in this progress towards complete individuality.

## Key Arguments

**Aristocracy as a stage of political evolution**. Spencer situated aristocracy within his broader theory of social evolution. In the movement "from the one extreme, in which the State is everything and the individual nothing, to the other extreme, in which the individual is everything and the State nothing," society "must pass through many modified structures." Aristocracy, along with its mixtures with monarchy and democracy, represented an intermediate stage — one in which the concentration of power had been partially diluted from the single despot to a broader class, but in which the principle of individual rights remained unrecognised. The passage from despotism through aristocracy to democracy measured, for Spencer, the progressive liberation of the individual from collective control.

**The psychology of power-worship and class domination**. Spencer identified aristocracy with a particular psychological disposition — the "sentiment of power-worship." In a passage linking aristocracy with despotism and tyranny, Spencer observed that "readiness to cringe is accompanied by an equal readiness to tyrannize." The Spartan Helots were "not more coerced by their Spartan masters than these in turn by their oligarchy." The servile Hindu, he noted, "indemnifies themselves for their passiveness to their superiors by their tyranny, cruelty, and violence to those in their power." The feudal system exemplified this hierarchical chain of domination: "the people were bondsmen to the nobles, the nobles were vassals to their kings, their kings to the pope." Aristocracy thus perpetuated the psychology of command and submission that Spencer regarded as characteristic of the militant type of society.

> During the feudal ages, while the people were bondsmen to the nobles, the nobles were vassals to their kings, their kings to the pope.

**Aristocracy and the ethics of status**. Spencer's analysis of aristocracy drew on his distinction between the "ethic of status" and the "ethic of contract." In an aristocratic society, each person's rights and obligations are determined by their inherited position within a hierarchy — the ethic of status. The alternative, which Spencer's industrial type embodied, is the ethic of contract: relations between individuals are freely negotiated, premised on equal standing before the law, and governed by mutual consent rather than inherited subordination. Aristocracy was, in this framework, a social system that systematically denied the law of equal freedom by substituting birth-determined inequality for the natural inequalities of effort and ability that a free society permits.

**Austin's legal positivism and aristocratic sovereignty**. In *The Man Versus the State*, Spencer critiqued John Austin's theory of sovereignty — which derived law from the commands of an unlimited sovereign — as an intellectual vestige of aristocratic thinking. Austin, Spencer noted, "assimilates civil authority to military authority; taking for granted that the one, as the other, is above question in respect of both origin and range." The sovereign Austin described — whether a monarch, "an aristocracy, or that larger body of men who have votes in a democracy" — was defined without any moral warrant. Spencer's point was that Austin's positivism supplied a jurisprudential justification for aristocratic rule no less than for monarchical or democratic rule: in each case, the sovereign's commands were treated as law without reference to their conformity with the law of equal freedom.

## Historical Context

Nineteenth-century Britain was itself a society in transition from aristocratic to democratic governance. The Reform Acts of 1832, 1867, and 1884 progressively expanded the franchise, diluting the political power of the landed aristocracy. Spencer published *Social Statics* in 1851, between the first two Reform Acts, and *The Man Versus the State* in 1884, the year of the third Reform Act. His treatment of aristocracy was thus not a detached historical analysis but an intervention in an ongoing political transformation. He warned that the demise of formal aristocratic institutions did not guarantee the triumph of individual liberty — the "new Toryism" of legislative paternalism threatened, in his view, to reconstruct aristocratic control under democratic forms.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

- **[[oligarchy]]**: A closely related concept — Spencer sometimes used the terms interchangeably, though oligarchy tended to emphasise rule by the few without the hereditary or class-based connotations of aristocracy.
- **[[monarchy]]**: The form of one-person rule from which aristocracy represented an advance in the diffusion of power. Spencer treated monarchy, aristocracy, and democracy as points on a single continuum of political evolution.
- **[[despotism]]**: The extreme form of concentrated rule against which Spencer measured political progress — aristocracy was an advance beyond despotism, but shared its fundamental structure of command and submission.
- **[[toryism]]**: The political ideology associated with aristocratic interests in Spencer's Britain, which he contrasted with the "true Liberalism" that sought to minimise state power rather than redirect it to new ends.
- **[[democracy]]**: The next stage in Spencer's evolutionary sequence beyond aristocracy — though he warned that democracy without the constraint of equal freedom could become a new form of class rule.
