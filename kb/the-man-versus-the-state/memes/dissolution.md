## Definition

**Dissolution** is the process of decomposition through which an integrated structure — whether organic, psychological, or social — breaks down into its simpler constituent elements. For Herbert Spencer, dissolution stood as the necessary complement to evolution: if evolution is the integration of matter and the dissipation of motion, producing increasing heterogeneity and structural complexity, dissolution is the absorption of motion and the disintegration of matter, producing decreasing heterogeneity and a return toward homogeneity. In the social domain, dissolution manifests as the breakdown of cooperative structures, the loss of specialized functions, and the reversion of a complex society toward a simpler, less differentiated condition.

> The progressive realizations of this tendency.

## Key Arguments

Spencer's treatment of dissolution operates at multiple levels, from the metaphysical to the empirically social.

**First, dissolution as a universal process**. Spencer posited dissolution as the universal inverse of evolution, operating in all domains. Just as the biological organism, at death, undergoes dissolution — its differentiated tissues breaking down, its integrated functions ceasing — so too do social aggregates undergo dissolution when the forces that hold them together (military cohesion, economic interdependence, shared belief systems) weaken or disappear. The Roman Empire, in Spencer's implicit historical schema, exemplified social dissolution on the largest scale: the progressive disintegration of a highly differentiated imperial structure into the simpler, more homogeneous societies of the early medieval period.

**Second, equilibrium and the rhythm of change**. Spencer's framework posited that all systems oscillate between evolution and dissolution around a point of moving equilibrium. Societies do not evolve indefinitely in one direction; rather, they experience alternating phases of integration and disintegration, complexity and simplification. This rhythm is driven by the balance between internal cohesive forces (shared norms, functional interdependence, coercive regulation) and external disintegrative forces (military defeat, economic disruption, loss of legitimating beliefs).

**Third, the role of belief in social cohesion**. Spencer recognized that shared belief systems — religious, political, moral — function as the "moral forces" that bind societies together. "Nor indeed, considering the matter closely, does it appear that society could ever hold together were not opinion in some way or other enforced." When these binding beliefs lose their hold — when the "equilibrium of moral forces" shifts — the conditions for dissolution are established. This insight anticipated later sociological analyses of anomie and social disintegration.

**Fourth, dissolution and the militant-industrial transition**. Spencer applied the dissolution concept to contemporary politics. The militant type of society — held together by coercive regulation and external threat — is vulnerable to dissolution when the conditions sustaining militancy are removed. The transition from the militant to the industrial type is not, in Spencer's view, a smooth progression but a precarious process in which the dissolution of old coercive structures may, if not accompanied by the growth of voluntary cooperation, produce social instability rather than liberation.

## Historical Context

Spencer developed the evolution-dissolution framework most fully in *First Principles* (1862), where it formed part of his grand synthetic philosophy. The concept drew on physical analogies — the dissolution of compounds, the entropy-like dissipation described in thermodynamics — and on biological observations of decay and death. In the social domain, Spencer was influenced by the historical spectacle of imperial decline (Rome, Spain, the Ottoman Empire) and by contemporary anxieties about social cohesion in an age of rapid industrialization, urbanization, and religious doubt.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

- **[[social-evolution]]**: The integrative process of which dissolution is the inverse — evolution builds complexity; dissolution reduces it.
- **[[aggregation]]**: The initial stage of the evolutionary process — the coming together of separate units — which dissolution reverses.
- **[[adaptation]]**: The mechanism that counteracts dissolution by continuously adjusting social structures to their conditions — maladaptation invites dissolution.
- **[[militant-type]]**: The social type most dependent on coercive integration and therefore most vulnerable to dissolution when coercive forces weaken.
