## Definition

Representative government, in Herbert Spencer's political thought, is the system in which the people, or a portion of them, elect delegates to exercise legislative and executive powers on their behalf. Spencer treats representative government not as the final form of political organization but as an intermediate stage in the evolution from coercive to voluntary social coordination.

Writing across four decades — from *Social Statics* (1851) through the essay "Representative Government" (1857) to *Man Versus the State* (1884) — Spencer's assessment of representative institutions grows progressively darker. The system that once appeared as the mechanism for securing individual liberty comes, in his later work, to be seen as the vehicle for its erosion.

## The Transitional Character

In *Social Statics*, Spencer presents representative government as a substantial advance over monarchy and aristocracy, but not as a permanent resting point. Just as "representative government, and is still with many nations," was once impracticable because the people lacked the moral development to sustain it, so too may future institutional arrangements — more fully consistent with the law of equal freedom — be impracticable now but attainable through further moral evolution.

The key insight is that representative government does not abolish the problem of unlimited authority; it merely transfers that authority from the one to the many. The structure of command remains; only the identity of the commander changes. Spencer's question is whether the substitution of a majority for a monarch genuinely solves the problem of political power or merely disguises it.

## The Failure of Legislation

Spencer's deepening pessimism about representative government is fueled by empirical observation of legislative outcomes. In "The Sins of Legislators" (*Man Versus the State*), he documents a pattern of repeated failure: "scarcely one statute connected with the administration of public relief has produced the effect designed by the legislature, and … the majority of them have created new evils, and aggravated those which they were intended to prevent."

He cites the Shipwreck Committee of 1836 and the cascade of merchant-shipping legislation that followed: "scarcely a session has passed without some Act being passed," the statutes became "a scandal and a reproach," and yet "the loss of life and of ships has been greater since 1876 than it ever was before" — all while the cost of administration quadrupled. The pattern is one of interventions that fail, generating pressure for further interventions that also fail, in a self-perpetuating cycle of legislative expansion.

## The New Toryism

Spencer's most distinctive contribution to the analysis of representative government is his diagnosis of "The New Toryism." The Liberal party, historically the defender of individual liberty against state power, has become the primary engine of coercive legislation. Liberals now "vie with one another in multiplying interferences," producing an alliance of old Tories and new Liberals in expanding the state's reach.

The mechanism Spencer identifies is electoral. Politicians compete for votes by promising benefits to constituencies, and each benefit requires a new exercise of state power. The logic of representative government, under conditions of mass franchise, drives ever-greater intervention regardless of the nominal ideology of the party in power. "The desire for votes," he observes, generates a bidding war in which liberty is the currency spent.

## Spencer's Proposed Safeguard

Spencer does not simply criticize; he proposes a structural remedy. The legitimate function of representative government, he argues, is strictly limited to the protection of individual rights — defense against external enemies and internal criminals. Beyond this protective minimum, majorities have no legitimate authority over minorities. The subordination of minority to majority is justified "only such a trenching on the freedom and property of each, as is requisite for the better protecting of his freedom and property." Any further trenching is a breach of the very principle representative government purports to serve.

This is not an argument against representation as such, but against the unlimited scope of representative authority. A properly constituted representative body would be limited by the law of equal freedom — it could act only where individual rights required collective defense, and its actions would be subject to the same moral constraints as the actions of the individuals who compose it.
