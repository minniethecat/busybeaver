# The Church

The church near Hollyoake Square is the site of the novel's central pivotal event: the secret marriage of Basil and Margaret Sherwin. It appears in the narrative at two crucial junctures — first as the setting of the clandestine wedding ceremony in Chapter 12, and later as the site of Margaret's grave, where Robert Mannion delivers his terrible threat to Basil in Chapter 27. The church embodies the morbid, oppressive atmosphere of enforced secrecy and the inexorable pull of consequences that define the novel's tragic arc.

## Description

Collins renders the church in unremittingly gloomy terms. On the day of the wedding, the weather conspires with the architecture to produce an atmosphere of foreboding:

> "All the gloom and dampness of the day seemed to be collected in this room — a dark, cold, melancholy place, with one window which opened on a burial-ground steaming in the wet. The rain pattered monotonously on the pavement outside." (012-040)

This is the vestry, where the wedding party waits for the officiating clergyman. The proximity of the burial-ground to the wedding preparations is no coincidence — it establishes the thematic link between the marriage and death that will govern the rest of the novel.

The interior of the church itself is no more inviting. When the clergyman arrives and the party enters, they find:

> "the church, with its desolate array of empty pews, and its chill, heavy, weekday atmosphere" (012-041)

The emptiness of the pews underscores the secrecy of the ceremony — no congregation, no witnesses, no celebration. The "chill, heavy, weekday atmosphere" (012-041) suggests a space unused to joy, a place of rote function rather than spiritual warmth.

Outside, the church portico provides the setting for Basil's moment of stunned reflection after the ceremony. Standing alone under it, he tries "to realise to myself that I was married" (012-042), while the rain continues to fall and the fog thickens around him.

The churchyard, adjacent to the building, becomes the second focal point. It is a space of solitude and reckoning: "I stood alone in the churchyard by the grave of Margaret Sherwin" (027-001). After the burial party departs, "the workers and the idlers in the churchyard had alike departed" (027-005), leaving Basil alone with his grief — and with the approaching figure of Mannion.

## Narrative Role

### The Secret Wedding

The church scenes in Chapter 12 constitute the formal, legal, and emotional crux of the novel. The wedding party assembles at the church in miserable weather: "As we proceeded to it, the rain fell heavily, and the mist of the morning was thickening to a fog" (012-040). They wait in the vestry with "the white surplices which hung before me in a half-opened cupboard" and "the long-shaped books, bound in brown leather, which were on the table" (012-040).

When the clergyman arrives, the ceremony proceeds at the altar (012-041). Basil's mental state during the service is one of profound dislocation:

> "As we ranged ourselves round the altar, a confusion overspread all my faculties. My sense of the place I was in, and even of the ceremony in which I took part, grew more and more vague and doubtful every minute." (012-041)

He is haunted by "an impression that my father had discovered my secret, and was watching me from some hidden place in the church" (012-041). This paranoid fantasy — of the father's gaze penetrating the clandestine ceremony — crystallises the novel's central anxiety about secrecy and exposure.

After the ceremony, the party returns to the vestry. In a passage of grim efficiency, the legal formalities are discharged: "The fees were paid; we wrote our names in the books and on the certificate; the clergyman quietly wished me happiness; the clerk solemnly imitated him; the pew-opener smiled and curtseyed" (012-042). Mr. Sherwin, characteristically, "made congratulatory speeches, kissed his daughter, shook hands with me, frowned a private rebuke at his wife for shedding tears, and, finally, led the way with Margaret out of the vestry" (012-042). The condition imposed by Mr. Sherwin — "the leaving my wife at the church-door" (012-045) — means that the newlyweds are immediately separated.

The day culminates not in joy but in Basil's terrifying wedding night. Alone at home, he suffers a complete nervous collapse: "I was possessed by a gloom and horror, caused by no thought, and producing no thought: the thinking faculty seemed paralysed within me, altogether" (012-054). The chapter closes with his grim summation: "And this was my wedding-night! This was how the day ended which had begun by my marriage with Margaret Sherwin!" (012-055).

### The Churchyard and Mannion's Threat

The church returns to the narrative in Chapter 27, when Basil stands at Margaret's grave (027-001). The churchyard, empty of all other mourners, becomes the stage for Mannion's reappearance. Disfigured and vengeful, Mannion approaches across the graves:

> "Under this earth that we stand on...lies buried with the buried dead, the last influence which might one day have gained you respite and mercy at my hands." (027-012)

Standing with his foot on Margaret's grave, Mannion delivers the threat that will dominate the remainder of the novel: "I will make your whole life to come, one long expiation of this deformity...and of that death" (027-014). He promises, "Go where you will, this face of mine shall never be turned away from you" (027-014). The churchyard, place of consecrated ground and eternal rest, is desecrated by this declaration of unending persecution.

The "horror of the fiend-face" (027-015) remains with Basil long after he leaves: "Never for a moment, while I lingered alone in the churchyard; never, when I quitted it, and walked through the crowded streets" (027-015).

### Echoes and Reflections

The church recurs in moments of bitter reflection. On her deathbed, Margaret cries out in feverish delirium: "let's go back to the church, and get unmarried again" (026-093) — a grotesque inversion of the wedding vows that underscores the tragedy of the marriage.

The "old grey stone church which stands solitary on the hilltop" overlooking Basil's Cornish cottage (028-008) serves as a haunting echo of the London church. Its churchyard, "surrounded by the lonesome moor" (028-008), mirrors the earlier churchyard of Margaret's grave. Basil sits "on the coffin stone at the entrance of the churchyard, or wandering round the sturdy granite tower" (028-008), as if drawn back to the symbolic space that sealed his fate.

## Symbolism

The church in *Basil* is a profoundly ironic space — a place of holy ritual that presides over unholy outcomes. The marriage ceremony, traditionally a celebration of love and community, is here emptied of all warmth: the "desolate array of empty pews" (012-041) signifies the absence of family, friends, and social sanction that will doom the union from its inception.

The **proximity of the burial-ground** to the vestry window (012-040) operates as heavy foreshadowing. Collins places the marriage and the grave in immediate visual relation to one another, collapsing the distance between the wedding day and the day of Margaret's burial. The "burial-ground steaming in the wet" (012-040) is the novel's emblem of futurity — what awaits beyond the ceremony.

The **churchyard** functions as a threshold between life and death, guilt and punishment. It is here that Basil confronts both his grief (at Margaret's grave) and his nemesis (in Mannion). That Mannion chooses consecrated ground for his most blasphemous threat — to make Basil's life a "long expiation" (027-014) — underscores the novel's preoccupation with the failure of sacred institutions to protect against evil.

The **Cornwall church** (028-008) offers a structural parallel. The "old grey stone church" on the moor repeats the image of the solitary church, now transposed to a wilder landscape. Where the London church witnessed the sealing of Basil's secret, the Cornwall church overlooks the consequences of its exposure. The "coffin stone at the entrance of the churchyard" (028-008) where Basil now sits suggests that his entire life has become a vigil over death — a permanent residence in the shadow of the church.

## Real-World Basis

Collins does not name a specific London church. Its location "near at hand" (012-040) to Hollyoake Square and North Villa places it in the suburban districts of mid-nineteenth-century London. The description — a church with a portico (012-042), a vestry with a window opening onto a burial-ground (012-040), and a churchyard with scattered tombs — is consistent with numerous parish churches of the Regency and early Victorian period in what were then the developing suburbs of northwest London. The generic quality of the church may be intentional: it represents not a specific identifiable building but the institutional apparatus of marriage and burial that the novel's events simultaneously invoke and subvert.
