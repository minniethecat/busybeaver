# First Meeting of Basil and Margaret

The novel's inciting event occurs on a London omnibus, where Basil — a young aristocrat — first sees Margaret Sherwin, the daughter of a linen-draper. This chance encounter, made possible by the democratic mixing of the modern city, sets in motion the entire plot of passion, secrecy, and catastrophe.

## The Encounter

Basil is struck by Margaret's beauty in the anonymous public space of the omnibus, where "persons of all classes and all temperaments are so easily and so strangely intermingled" (007-004). He follows her to her home in the suburban district of North Villa, initiating the pursuit that will lead to the secret marriage.

## Significance

The omnibus encounter is significant for what it says about the modern city as a space of social possibility and danger. In the class-segregated world of Victorian England, the omnibus is one of the few places where an aristocrat and a linen-draper's daughter might encounter one another as strangers. Collins uses this urban chance to launch a plot that will systematically destroy the social assumptions that the encounter momentarily suspends.
