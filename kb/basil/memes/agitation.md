## Definition

Agitation in _Basil_ is the body's testimony to psychological disturbance. Collins uses the term to describe a spectrum of states, from the mild nervousness of social embarrassment to the violent physical trembling that accompanies moral crisis. Agitation is the most legible sign of inner turmoil — it cannot be concealed, and its presence in a character signals that something is wrong beneath the surface. In a novel structured around secrecy and deception, agitation functions as an involuntary confession: the body speaking truths that the mind would prefer to hide.

## Manifestation

The language of agitation appears at crucial moments throughout the novel, consistently marking points of psychological pressure. Basil's father departs for London "with ill-concealed agitation and apprehension" (004-008), his composure cracking under the strain of family crisis. Margaret Sherwin is described as flushing "not merely in moments of agitation, but even when she is walking" (005-001), her body perpetually on the verge of betraying her.

Basil's own agitation reaches its peak during his clandestine visits to North Villa. "At first, my agitation was so violent that I was perfectly unable to speak" (009-041), he recalls, describing a state in which emotional intensity overwhelms the capacity for speech — the body short-circuiting the mind's ability to maintain social performance. After the confrontation with Mannion, Basil describes "the restlessness, the appearance of agitation" (020-010) that possesses him, a state in which he cannot be still, cannot sleep, cannot escape his own body's testimony to his distress.

> "The restlessness, the appearance of agitation" (020-010)

This phrase captures the dual nature of agitation in the novel: it is both an internal experience (restlessness) and a social sign (appearance of agitation). Characters read one another's agitation, drawing conclusions about guilt, fear, or concealment. The transparency of agitation makes it dangerous in a world where so much depends on maintaining appearances.

The novel's most violent agitation is experienced by Basil's father during the confrontation scene: "overwhelmed by the violence of her agitation, my father had sunk into a chair" (022-092). Here agitation is contagious — Clara's distress triggers her father's collapse — and so extreme that it disables the body entirely.

Collins also connects agitation to physical illness. The surgeon notes that Margaret's condition was "greatly increased by mental agitation, or bodily weakness" (026-041), establishing a causal link between psychological distress and physical deterioration. The narrative itself is described as written "under the incessant strain and agitation of writing" (028-067), suggesting that the act of confession is itself agitating — that telling the story renews the distress it describes.

## Characters

**Basil** experiences agitation as a chronic condition throughout the novel. His agitation is the register of his guilt: every time he deceives his father, conceals his marriage, or confronts the consequences of his actions, his body betrays him with "violent" agitation that he cannot control.

**Margaret Sherwin** is described as constitutionally agitated — her tendency to flush and tremble is presented as a feature of her character, perhaps a sign of the nervous sensitivity that also makes her susceptible to manipulation and moral weakness.

**Basil's Father** experiences agitation as a rupture in his aristocratic composure. His "ill-concealed agitation" when departing for London signals that paternal authority, however formidable, is not immune to the body's testimony.

**Mr. Sherwin** attributes his and his wife's "confusion to the nervous agitation which more or less affected him" (015-004), using the language of nervous disorder to excuse socially awkward behaviour — an example of how agitation could be deployed strategically as an explanation for conduct that might otherwise invite suspicion.

## Significance

Agitation in _Basil_ participates in the Victorian medical discourse of nervous disorders, which held that the stresses of modern life — urbanization, social competition, moral conflict — produced pathological states of nervous excitation. Collins draws on this discourse to give his characters' psychological suffering a somatic dimension, making inner torment visible on the body.

The novel's treatment of agitation also serves its sensational aims. Agitation is inherently dramatic — it is the visible sign of invisible conflict, the body's contribution to the novel's atmosphere of tension and suspense. By making his characters physically agitated, Collins keeps the reader in a state of sympathetic nervous excitement, aligning the experience of reading with the experiences being described.

## Historical Context

The Victorian period saw the emergence of "nervousness" as a distinct medical and cultural category. Physicians like Thomas Trotter and later George Miller Beard identified nervous disorders as characteristic maladies of modern civilization. The symptoms Collins describes — flushing, trembling, inability to speak, restless movement — correspond closely to the period's clinical descriptions of nervous agitation, lending his fiction a quasi-medical authority.
