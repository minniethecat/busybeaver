## Description

The hotel is rendered in a single, devastating phrase: "a neglected, deserted, dreary" place (019-016). Unlike the comfortable domestic interiors that dominate the novel's earlier scenes, the hotel is defined by absences — no welcome, no comfort, no domestic warmth. It is a place of transit and concealment, chosen not for its amenities but for its obscurity.

The hotel's situation is defined by its relationship to the tavern opposite (019-033), from which Basil conducts his surveillance. The distance between the two buildings — a street's width — becomes the measure of everything Basil has lost.

## Narrative Role

### The Discovery

Basil tracks Margaret to the hotel after her flight from North Villa. "I stopped at a tavern, situated exactly opposite to the hotel" (019-033), he records, establishing the pattern of observation that will dominate the hours that follow. From the tavern window, he watches the hotel door with desperate attention.

### The Vigil

The vigil outside the hotel is one of the novel's most concentrated passages of psychological intensity. Basil fixes his "aching eyes vigilantly on the hotel" (019-036), every movement at the door a fresh torment. When "the door of the hotel in the street open once more" (019-041), the event is charged with the full weight of Basil's dread — each opening threatens revelation, confirmation, or fresh catastrophe.

### Margaret's Final Hours

In the novel's closing movement, the hotel becomes the site of Margaret's last days. Basil leaves her "at the hotel" (025-009), and the phrase "every corner of my existence is at the hotel, half-frantic because I won't" (025-012) captures the centripetal force the place exerts over him — his entire being drawn toward that single, squalid point in London.

## Associated Characters

**[[basil]]** experiences the hotel as the theater of his helplessness. Unable to enter, unwilling to leave, he is suspended in the street between the tavern and the hotel door, a position that literalizes his social and emotional paralysis in the novel's final act.

**[[margaret-sherwin]]** is concealed within the hotel, her presence known only through the movements at the door and Basil's desperate inferences. The hotel reduces her from the vivid figure of the garden-gate to a rumor of occupancy, a presence behind a locked door.

**[[robert-mannion]]** is the agent of Margaret's concealment. A "gentleman came into this hotel a little while ago" (019-018), and that gentleman is Mannion, his control over the hotel's threshold mirroring his control over Margaret herself.

## Symbolism

The hotel functions as the anti-North Villa: where the Sherwin house was insistently domestic, the hotel is anonymous; where North Villa displayed its threadbare pretensions, the hotel hides its secrets behind a blank façade. The trajectory from the garden at North Villa to the street outside this hotel traces the full arc of Basil's catastrophe — from the garden-gate with its sunlit promise to the locked hotel door with its cargo of disgrace.
