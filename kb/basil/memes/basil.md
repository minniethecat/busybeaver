# Basil

Basil is the protagonist and first-person narrator of Wilkie Collins's 1852 novel _Basil_. A younger son of an ancient aristocratic family, his impulsive secret marriage to [[margaret-sherwin]] — a linen-draper's daughter — sets in motion a chain of events that leads to disgrace, betrayal, and near-fatal tragedy. The novel is structured as his retrospective confession, written after the catastrophe has unfolded, in which the older, penitent Basil judges the errors of his younger self. Basil's story is, in his own framing words, a tale "innocent in its beginning, guilty in its progress, fatal in its results" (001-004).

## Role

Basil serves a dual function in the novel: he is both the protagonist whose actions drive the plot and the narrator whose retrospective voice shapes the reader's understanding of those actions. As protagonist, he embodies the conflict between [[passion]] and duty, romantic love and family [[honour]]. As narrator, he is a guilt-ridden moral commentator on his younger self, framing the entire story within the opening pages as a cautionary confession.

> "I would fain relate the story of an error, innocent in its beginning, guilty in its progress, fatal in its results" (001-004)

This retrospective moral framing establishes the split consciousness that defines the novel's narrative voice: the older Basil judges the younger Basil, creating a constant tension between the immediacy of the events narrated and the moral perspective from which they are recounted. The narrative is "fatal" from its first articulation (009-043), and Basil's retrospective voice continuously reminds the reader that the story has already ended in catastrophe — that he writes as a survivor seeking to understand, and perhaps to warn.

As the younger son of an aristocratic family, Basil occupies a structurally liminal position. He is wealthy enough to live without a profession — he could "practise my profession or not, just as I chose" (002-008) — but he is not the heir to the family estate. This in-between status is mirrored in his fatal attraction to Margaret Sherwin, who occupies a similarly liminal class position. Basil's role as narrator-protagonist makes him an early and significant example of Collins's first-person narrators, a technique the author would develop further in _The Woman in White_ (1859) and _The Moonstone_ (1868).

## Biography

### Family and Early Life

Basil is the younger son of a family whose lineage, on his father's side, "dates back beyond the Conquest" (002-001). His father is a man of "peculiar pride" (003-003) — a pride that is personal and moral rather than merely conventional or aristocratic. Basil describes his father as having treated his children with formal courtesy rather than warmth:

> "I mean by this, that he was a father to us, but never a companion" (003-008)

This emotional reserve, combined with the father's intense investment in family honour, creates the psychological conditions for Basil's catastrophic secrecy. The father's code of conduct is absolute: "one fatal crime which could never be forgiven" (003-007) was any breach of family honour.

Basil's brother Ralph serves as a cautionary precursor. Ralph's "awkward love adventure" (004-008) with a tenant's daughter prefigures Basil's own transgression across class lines. Ralph ultimately left England after a "serious disagreement between father and son" (004-016). Basil's sister [[clara]] — "four years younger than I am" (005-001) — is the moral anchor of the family, a figure of unwavering devotion who would prove essential to Basil's eventual recovery.

### The Meeting and Courtship

Basil's life is irrevocably changed when he encounters Margaret Sherwin on an omnibus. He had "often before ridden in omnibuses to amuse myself by observing the passengers" (007-004), but on this day his observation turns to obsession. Struck by Margaret's beauty — her "voluptuous languor of black eyes" (007-017) — Basil follows her to the suburban district of North Villa, where he discovers that her father is a linen-draper. The social chasm is immediate and recognized: "Love! love for a shopkeeper's daughter!" (007-067), Basil cries, already understanding the fatality of his attraction but powerless to resist it.

> "Still, the image of the dark beauty of the morning was the one reigning image of my thoughts" (007-045)

Basil engineers an introduction to Mr. Sherwin and requests permission to court his daughter (010-031). The courtship is conducted in the suburban setting of North Villa, away from the social world of Basil's family. Basil's passion for Margaret is all-consuming; she inspires in him "the passion, which is the absorbing passion of humanity" (008-005), a love that he had never known before and that obliterates all other considerations.

### The Secret Marriage

The marriage to Margaret is contracted in secrecy, without Basil's father's knowledge. Basil's motive for secrecy is terror of his father's judgment: he knows that "the consequences of a degrading marriage contracted by his son (degrading in a social point of view)" (008-007) would be catastrophic. This secret isolates Basil from his family and places him in a position of profound vulnerability. He begins living a double life, appearing at his father's house while hiding a wife in the suburban obscurity of North Villa. Basil himself describes the deception as a "fatality" (012-007), acknowledging that "the more evil I was doing, the more incapable I seemed of speaking freely."

The secret marriage also places Margaret in an impossible social position. She is kept "out of the society of her own class" (016-030) without being accepted into Basil's, condemned to a social limbo that breeds isolation and resentment. The unnaturalness of the arrangement is compounded by the presence in the Sherwin household of [[robert-mannion]], Mr. Sherwin's confidential clerk, whose role in Basil's tragedy would prove decisive.

### Catastrophe

The discovery of Margaret's infidelity with Mannion at a "neglected, deserted, dreary" hotel (019-016) is the novel's climactic scene. Basil, tracking his wife, hears sounds from within the hotel and finds Margaret with Mannion. The revelation is devastating and transforming — Basil sees that Margaret's "guilt and terror had made her hideous to look upon already" (019-044). Margaret dies shortly afterward, and Basil himself suffers a complete psychological collapse.

### Confession and Recovery

The novel's moral climax comes when Basil makes his confession to his father. The scene is rendered with extraordinary emotional intensity:

> "to weep out on his bosom my confession that I was no more worthy to be called his son" (021-047)

The father's response is complex — he shows neither anger nor immediate forgiveness, but a profound silence that Basil finds more terrible than any rebuke. The absence of "a single word of rebuke, of passion, or of sorrow, after such a confession" (022-050) strikes Basil as the most powerful judgment imaginable. Yet the father does not abandon him; instead, he ensures that Basil is cared for and begins the slow work of family reconciliation.

Clara's role in Basil's recovery is essential. During his illness, she whispers to him with the tenderness of a mother: "Basil!—let me call you as your mother would call you if she was alive" (023-050). Her earlier plea — "you have not done what you promised me; you have not been easy in your mind" (022-091) — speaks to the intuitive understanding that has characterized her relationship with Basil throughout. When Basil finally recovers, Clara's words acknowledge the transformative power of survival: "God has given you back to us, Basil, to make us happy in you" (020-022).

### The Pursuit and the Letters

The novel's final sections trace Basil's pursuit of Mannion, chronicled in the Journal (028), and his eventual recovery, documented in the concluding Letters (029-031). In the final letter, Basil reflects from the "churchyard by the grave of Margaret Sherwin" (027-001), where he has achieved a measure of peace. His closing note is one of qualified redemption: "Live the free life which Margaret Sherwin has restored to you by her death" (027-014), Mannion tells him — a dark irony that Basil seems to accept even as he rejects Mannion's nihilism.

## Relationships

- **[[margaret-sherwin]]**: Basil's secret wife. His passion for her — the "absorbing passion" (008-005) of his life — drives the plot; her betrayal destroys him. The relationship is defined by class asymmetry, unequal emotional investment, and the fatal gap between Basil's idealized image of Margaret and the real woman.

- **[[basil-father]]**: The relationship that most defines Basil's moral world. His father's "peculiar pride" (003-003) and the code of family honour are the standards against which Basil measures his own fall. His terror of his father's judgment is the engine of his secrecy; their eventual reconciliation is the novel's emotional climax.

- **[[clara]]**: Basil's younger sister, "four years younger than I am" (005-001). Her intuitive understanding of Basil's character and her unwavering devotion make her the novel's moral anchor. She is, in Basil's retrospective judgment, the purest figure in his world: the one who loved him most and whom he most wronged through his secrecy.

- **Ralph**: Basil's older brother, whose youthful transgressions — a love affair with a tenant's daughter (004-008), a duel with an opera dancer (004-010) — prefigure Basil's own catastrophic error. Ralph's fate — exile from England — stands as a warning that Basil fails to heed.

- **[[robert-mannion]]**: Basil's antagonist. Introduced as Mr. Sherwin's confidential clerk — "forty, or a year or two more" (010-069) — Mannion becomes Basil's confidant, his betrayer, and ultimately his relentless pursuer. Basil is "positively awestruck before Mr. Mannion" (014-013) from their first meeting, sensing something dangerous and unknowable.

- **Mr. Sherwin**: Margaret's father, a linen-draper who treats his daughter's marriage as a commercial opportunity. His class aspirations and his complicity in the secrecy of the marriage make him an ambivalent figure — at once Basil's ally and one of the architects of his ruin.

## Significance

Basil is a landmark figure in the development of Collins's fictional method and in the broader history of the Victorian novel. As a first-person narrator who is also the protagonist of a story of guilt and confession, Basil anticipates Walter Hartright in _The Woman in White_ (1859) and Franklin Blake in _The Moonstone_ (1868). The split between Basil-as-protagonist and Basil-as-narrator — between the young man driven by passion and the older man driven by remorse — creates the distinctive psychological depth of the novel. This narrative architecture, in which the telling is itself an act of moral reckoning, makes _Basil_ a significant precursor to the sensation novel, the genre that Collins would come to define.

As a character, Basil represents the Victorian gentleman caught between the demands of passion and the codes of [[social-class]] — a figure who appears throughout Collins's fiction. His tragedy is that he internalizes his father's code of honour even as he violates it; his secrecy is a perverse form of filial piety, a desperate attempt to preserve the family name even as he dishonours it. The novel's critique of this dynamic — the way in which rigid codes of honour produce the very transgressions they are designed to prevent — is one of its most modern features.

Basil's relationship with [[love]] is similarly complex. His passion for Margaret is intense but fundamentally abstract: he falls in love with an image, a "dark beauty" (007-045) onto which he projects his own desires. The novel's realism lies in its unsparing depiction of what happens when that image collides with the actual woman. The [[secret-marriage-ceremony]] is thus not merely a plot device but a figure for the gap between romantic idealization and social reality — a gap that Collins would continue to explore throughout his career.

The [[confession]] structure of the novel places Basil in a tradition of first-person moral testimony that reaches back to Defoe and forward to the psychological novel of the twentieth century. Unlike the unproblematically reformed narrators of many Victorian novels, Basil remains ambivalent: his confession is genuine, but his self-knowledge is incomplete. The novel ends not with resolution but with a kind of exhausted peace, achieved at the grave of the woman he loved and destroyed.

## Quotations

> "I would fain relate the story of an error, innocent in its beginning, guilty in its progress, fatal in its results; and I would fain hope that my plain and true record will show that this error was not committed altogether without excuse." (001-004)

> "I had known nothing of the passion, which is the absorbing passion of humanity. No woman had ever encouraged me to love her. I had been brought up to think of marriage as an affair of rank and convenience; of love, as a species of courtly rapture" (008-005)

> "to weep out on his bosom my confession that I was no more worthy to be called his son" (021-047)

> "Still, the image of the dark beauty of the morning was the one reigning image of my thoughts." (007-045)

## Appearances

Basil appears in or narrates every chapter of the novel. Key appearances:

- **Part I, Chapters I–XII** (001–012): Family background; the omnibus meeting with Margaret; courtship at North Villa; the secret marriage
- **Part II, Chapters I–VII** (013–019): The marriage under strain; introduction of Mannion; growing isolation; discovery of Margaret's infidelity and her death
- **Part III, Chapters I–VIII** (020–027): Basil's collapse and illness; confession to his father; Clara's care; recovery; Mannion's confession
- **Journal** (028): Basil's pursuit of Mannion across England
- **Letters in Conclusion** (029–031): Recovery, reflection, and Basil's final reckoning
