# Mr. Sherwin

Mr. Sherwin is Margaret's father and one of the novel's most subtly drawn characters. A linen-draper of the lower-middle class, his social ambition for his daughter drives him to accept — and actively engineer — a secret marriage that he believes will elevate the family's status. In doing so, he becomes complicit in the very tragedy that destroys his daughter.

## Role

Mr. Sherwin functions as the novel's representative of commercial-class social ambition. Unlike Basil's father, whose pride is personal and aristocratic, Sherwin's calculations are commercial: he treats his daughter's marriage as a transaction, weighing its advantages and risks with the same logic he would apply to a business deal. His complicity in the secret marriage — he agrees to keep it hidden because "a private marriage gives us a chance for the future" (010-060) — makes him, in a moral sense, as responsible for the catastrophe as Basil himself.

At the same time, Collins avoids making Sherwin a simple villain. His ambition for Margaret is genuine, if misguided; his desire to see her rise above her class is comprehensible, if destructive. Sherwin is a product of the same class system that traps Basil and Margaret — a man whose social aspirations are both a response to and a reinforcement of the hierarchy that governs Victorian life.

## Biography

Mr. Sherwin is introduced indirectly, through Basil's investigation of the household at North Villa. Basil learns that "the master of the house was 'Sherwin'" (007-031) and that he "kept a large linen-draper's shop" (007-033). This commercial identification is significant: Sherwin is defined by his trade, and his social position is inseparable from his economic activity.

Sherwin's response to Basil's interest in Margaret reveals his character. Rather than protecting his daughter from an inappropriate suitor, he sees an opportunity for social advancement. He negotiates the terms of the secret marriage with a businessman's attention to detail, extracting Basil's commitment while preserving his own position. His agreement that "the strictest secrecy" (010-015) must be maintained is a strategic calculation, not a concession to necessity.

When the marriage collapses, Sherwin's role becomes even more compromised. He is revealed to have known — or to have chosen not to know — about Mannion's relationship with Margaret. His ambition has blinded him to the dangers that surround his daughter. In the novel's moral economy, Sherwin is culpable not for malice but for a failure of care — he treated his daughter as an asset rather than a person, and the asset was destroyed.

## Relationships

- **Margaret Sherwin**: His daughter. His love for her is real but fatally compromised by his ambition. He treats her marriage as a social investment and pays the price when the investment fails.
- **Basil**: His daughter's husband and his social superior. Sherwin's dealings with Basil are a study in the psychology of class deference — he is at once deferential and calculating, respectful and manipulative.
- **Mannion**: His confidential clerk. The relationship between employer and employee is complicated by Mannion's hidden power — the clerk who knows his master's secrets is, in some ways, the master's master.

## Significance

Mr. Sherwin represents Collins's critique of the commercial values that were transforming Victorian society. Unlike the aristocratic Basil family, whose code of honour is rigid but genuine, Sherwin's morality is transactional — he treats relationships as investments and social position as currency. His tragedy is that he applies commercial logic to human relationships and discovers, too late, that the logic does not hold. Sherwin is not evil; he is simply a man who has allowed ambition to replace care, and who must live with the consequences.
