# Margaret's Mother

Mrs. Sherwin is one of the novel's most striking absences — a character whose silence is itself a form of commentary on the position of women in the Victorian domestic economy. As Margaret's mother and Mr. Sherwin's wife, she is present in the household but almost entirely without voice or agency.

## Role

Mrs. Sherwin functions as a structural absence. In a novel concerned with the dynamics of patriarchal authority (Basil's father, Mr. Sherwin) and the costs of women's subordination (Margaret), the mother's silence is significant. She is present at key domestic scenes but never intervenes, never speaks with authority, never shapes events (PN 010-021). Her silence is not a character trait but a social condition (PN 016-005) — the condition of the Victorian wife whose legal and economic subordination renders her effectively mute.

## Biography

Almost nothing is known of Mrs. Sherwin's personal history. She appears in the background of the Sherwin household scenes, a presence noted but never developed. Her silence is the most eloquent thing about her (PN 011-011): in a novel driven by the catastrophic consequences of silencing women (Margaret cannot speak openly about her marriage, her desires, or her relationship with Mannion), the mother's silence is the originary condition from which the daughter's tragedy unfolds.

## Relationships

- **Margaret Sherwin**: Her daughter. The absence of a meaningful mother-daughter relationship is one of the novel's most telling gaps (PN 014-013).
- **Mr. Sherwin**: Her husband. Their relationship is defined entirely by his agency and her passivity.

Part I, Chapters X; Part II (background presence in Sherwin household)
