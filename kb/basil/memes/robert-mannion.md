# Robert Mannion

Robert Mannion is the antagonist of _Basil_ and one of Wilkie Collins's earliest experiments in the figure of the urbane, implacable villain — a type he would later perfect in Count Fosco (_The Woman in White_) and Captain Wragge (_No Name_). Introduced as Mr. Sherwin's confidential clerk, Mannion gradually reveals himself as the novel's darkest and most complex character: a man whose surface of professional competence conceals a capacity for sustained, calculated revenge.

## Role

Mannion functions as the novel's hidden engine. In the first half, he is a background figure — the clerk who facilitates Basil's secret marriage and offers apparently reliable counsel. In the second half, he emerges as the primary agent of destruction: the man who has been involved with Margaret before and during her marriage to Basil, and who, once exposed, dedicates himself to Basil's ruin.

Mannion's most distinctive feature as a villain is his patience. His revenge is not impulsive but methodical. He explains to Basil that his persecution "simply springs from the desire of revenge" (024-066), and that he "first thoroughly tested" Basil's vulnerabilities before striking. This cold rationality makes Mannion more terrifying than a passionate villain would be: his malice is not a temporary insanity but a permanent condition, as calculated as a business transaction.

## Biography

Mannion's past is deliberately obscured. He is introduced as "Mr. Mannion, my confidential clerk" (010-067), and for much of the novel this professional designation is all we know of him. His "peculiar habits" and "secrecy in relation to his early life" (016-028) mark him as a figure of mystery, but Basil — absorbed in his own romantic drama — fails to recognise the danger.

The revelation of Mannion's relationship with Margaret transforms the novel. What had appeared to be a straightforward story of cross-class love becomes a triangle of betrayal, with Mannion at its apex. His subsequent pursuit of Basil — across London, through poverty and illness, to the novel's final confrontation — constitutes the novel's most sustained narrative sequence.

The ambiguity surrounding Mannion's death is characteristic. The novel offers multiple accounts of his end — accident, disappearance, possible survival — refusing the reader the closure of a confirmed death. Mannion, who represented the force of implacable, amoral persistence, cannot simply be eliminated. His end, like his life, remains opaque.

## Relationships

- **Basil**: The object of Mannion's revenge. Their relationship evolves from professional acquaintance to mortal enmity, with Mannion consistently one step ahead.
- **Margaret Sherwin**: The most ambiguous relationship in the novel. Whether Mannion loved Margaret, used her, or both, is never clarified. Their relationship is the secret within the secret — the hidden connection that, once exposed, destroys everything.
- **Mr. Sherwin**: Mannion's employer. The power dynamic between them — the clerk who knows his master's secrets — is one of the novel's most subtly handled relationships.

## Significance

Mannion represents Collins's early exploration of the charismatic villain — a figure who would become central to sensation fiction. Unlike the Gothic villains of earlier fiction, Mannion is terrifying precisely because he is plausible: a disappointed, intelligent man whose grievances, however disproportionate, have a recognisable human origin. His cold rationality, his patience, and his opacity make him a more modern and disturbing figure than the melodramatic villains of the Victorian stage. In Mannion, Collins begins to develop the psychological realism that would characterise his mature villains.
