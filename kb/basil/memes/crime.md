## Definition

In *Basil*, crime operates on two distinct but interlocking levels: the legal violation (forgery, adultery, bigamy) and the moral transgression (deception, concealment, the violation of social trust). Collins's novel traces the trajectory from the latter to the former — from Basil's initial moral crime of marrying in secret beneath his social station, through Mannion's criminal scheme of forgery and revenge, to the culminating discovery that crimes of passion, once set in motion, inevitably breed crimes of calculation. The novel's preface explicitly frames this arc, acknowledging that "scenes of misery and crime must of necessity, while human nature is what it is, be possessed of a certain gloomy interest" (P02-010), establishing crime as both narrative instrument and moral subject.

## Manifestation

The novel's treatment of crime follows a clear escalation pattern. Basil's initial transgression — the secret marriage to Margaret Sherwin — is not illegal but is experienced as criminal in its violation of class norms and filial duty. His father's world defines social deviation itself as crime: "the one fatal crime which could never be forgotten" (003-007). Basil internalizes this judgment, casting his own conduct in criminal vocabulary:

> "I felt as if I had been detected in a crime" (007-056)

The criminal self-perception precedes any legal violation; Basil feels like a criminal before he has broken any law, because the novel's moral economy treats secrecy itself as proto-criminal.

The decisive turn from moral to legal crime occurs with Mannion's revelation. His forgery and the circumstances surrounding it represent crime as cold calculation. Mannion's confessional letter — the novel's most sustained meditation on criminal psychology — traces the mental processes by which a man persuades himself to act:

> "He had taken refuge from misfortune in a crime. He believed — as all men who commit crime believe — that he had the best chance of success" (024-015–024-016)

This passage accomplishes two things simultaneously: it narrates the specific criminal act (forgery) and it offers a general theory of criminal psychology. The parenthetical generalization — "as all men who commit crime believe" — elevates Mannion's case into a universal proposition about the self-justifying nature of the criminal mind.

Margaret Sherwin's complicity in the concealment of her affair with Mannion adds a further dimension. Mannion reflects on "the motives of her crime" (024-002), which were, he claims, "still hidden" even from him. This opacity — the ungraspable interiority of another person's criminal motivations — becomes a source of horror for Basil, who can never fully know what Margaret knew or chose. Mannion's letter also contains a disturbing reflection on the relationship between crime and imagination:

> "Throughout this letter I have spoken of the perpetration of impossible crimes" (024-079)

The phrase "impossible crimes" captures the paradox at the heart of the novel's criminal economy: crimes that should be unthinkable become, under the pressure of passion and resentment, not only thinkable but actual.

In the novel's final movement, the accumulated weight of crime threatens to produce further crime. Basil's father warns that "you can never silence but by a crime" the voice of conscience (027-014), acknowledging that the logic of concealment tends toward escalation. The novel thus presents crime not as an isolated act but as a chain reaction — one transgression generating the conditions for the next. The confession that structures the narrative is itself an attempt to break this chain: to convert crime from a proliferating secret into a completed story.

## Characters

**Basil** is the novel's moral-criminal protagonist, whose initial social transgression sets the entire criminal chain in motion. He never commits a legal crime, but his consciousness is saturated with criminal self-perception (007-056), and the novel insistently tests the boundary between moral and legal culpability through his narration.

**Robert Mannion** is the novel's actual criminal — a forger, manipulator, and would-be murderer. His confession letter constitutes one of Victorian fiction's most detailed portraits of the criminal mind, tracing the rationalization that transforms grievance into methodical transgression (024-015–024-016).

**Margaret Sherwin** occupies an ambiguous position as both victim and accomplice. Her concealment of her relationship with Mannion is presented as criminal by the novel's moral standard, yet "the motives of her crime were still hidden" (024-002), and her terror and eventual death mark her as more sinned-against than sinning.

**Mr. Sherwin** enables the initial crime through his mercenary cooperation with Basil's secret suit, embodying the complicity of those who profit from transgression without directly committing it.

## Significance

Crime in *Basil* functions as the novel's central structural principle: the secret marriage, the hidden affair, the forgery, and the climactic confrontation together form a chain of transgression that drives the plot from initiation to catastrophe. Collins's treatment of crime is notable for its psychological realism — he is less interested in the legal fact of crime than in the mental states that precede, accompany, and follow criminal acts. The novel's first-person narration intensifies this focus by filtering every criminal event through Basil's retrospective consciousness, making the reader complicit in the very process of concealment and revelation that constitutes the crime plot.

The novel also registers a mid-Victorian anxiety about the porous boundary between respectability and criminality. Basil, the son of a gentleman, discovers that the distance between a secret marriage and Mannion's forgery is shorter than his class position would suggest. Crime, in Collins's hands, is not the province of a criminal class but a latent possibility within ordinary social life.

## Historical Context

*Basil* was published in 1852, at a moment when the Victorian novel was deeply engaged with questions of crime and punishment. The "Newgate novel" controversy of the 1830s–40s (sparked by works like Bulwer-Lytton's *Paul Clifford* and Ainsworth's *Jack Sheppard*) had established the criminal as a viable — if morally contested — subject for fiction. Collins departed from this tradition by relocating crime from the urban underworld to the domestic sphere, anticipating the domestic crime plots of his later sensation novels. The novel's treatment of forgery as Mannion's specific crime also reflects contemporary legal realities: forgery remained a capital offense in England until 1837, and its association with gentlemanly criminality (as opposed to violent street crime) made it particularly suited to Collins's exploration of class and transgression.
