## Definition

Mystery in _Basil_ operates on three levels simultaneously. As a plot device, it generates suspense: Who is Robert Mannion? What does he want? What is Margaret hiding? As a social condition, it reflects the opacity of urban life, where strangers harbour unknowable pasts and neighbours conceal devastating secrets. As a philosophical problem, it gestures toward the limits of human knowledge — the ultimate unknowability of other minds, the "impenetrable mystery" (013-011) that even the most intimate relationships cannot fully dispel.

## Manifestation

Robert Mannion is the novel's living mystery. From his first appearance, he is described in terms that resist comprehension: his face "did not even look vacant — a mystery for your eyes and your mind to solve" (014-010). Mr. Sherwin confirms the assessment: "Mannion, Sherwin, is a complete walking mystery that I want to solve" (014-024). And yet the mystery resists solution.

> "A mystery for your eyes and your mind to solve" (014-010)

This description of Mannion's face captures the dual nature of mystery in the novel: it is at once a visual puzzle (for your eyes) and an intellectual one (for your mind). The face that should reveal character instead conceals it, transforming the act of looking into an act of interpretation that is doomed to fail. Basil's "idea of trying to penetrate the mystery connected with him" (014-035) becomes an obsession precisely because it cannot be satisfied, and Mannion becomes "a more exasperating mystery to me than ever" (015-013) as the plot progresses.

The mystery of Mannion is matched by the mystery of his motivations. When he finally reveals his history in Chapter 24, he is "about to disclose the mystery which evidently hung over his origin" (015-036), yet the disclosure, far from dispelling mystery, deepens it — the revelation of Mannion's past explains his actions without making them comprehensible in any ordinary moral sense.

Margaret Sherwin is a different kind of mystery. Her "impenetrable mystery" (013-011) is that of a character whose inner life is never fully revealed to the reader. Unlike Basil, who narrates his own thoughts at length, or Mannion, who delivers a comprehensive confession, Margaret remains opaque — her motives, her feelings, her complicity in her father's schemes, all matters of conjecture. "The mystery connected with it" (024-001), Basil writes of Margaret's letter, "not disclose the mystery in which the motives of her conduct throughout had been involved" (024-002). The double mystery — of the letter and of the life — resists narrative resolution.

The novel also presents mystery as a social phenomenon. "The mystery of the accident in Westwood" (021-014) that injured Mannion is the kind of urban mystery that newspapers report and neighbours speculate about — a mystery generated by the anonymity of modern city life. Basil's own "worn, melancholy face" is "a mystery" to the "eyes" of his new acquaintances in Cornwall (028-006), who cannot reconcile his youthful years with his aged appearance.

## Characters

**Robert Mannion** is the novel's primary mystery and its most sophisticated creation. His opacity is not simply a function of narrative withholding — it is a character trait, the expression of a personality that has been shaped by secrecy and that now deploys secrecy as a weapon.

**Margaret Sherwin** remains mysterious even after her death. The narrative never fully explains her motivations, and this incompleteness is arguably the novel's most realistic feature — in life as in fiction, Collins suggests, some mysteries never resolve.

**Basil** is both the investigator of mysteries and, increasingly, a mystery to himself. His own motivations — why he married Margaret, why he submitted to the Sherwins' conditions, why he could not see what was happening — become increasingly opaque to him as his narrative unfolds.

**Mr. Sherwin** is, for Basil, a mystery of a different order — the mystery of vulgarity, of a man whose values and assumptions are so different from his own that they might as well belong to another species. The mystery of class difference is one of the novel's most persistent concerns.

## Significance

Mystery in _Basil_ is the narrative technology that transforms social critique into sensation fiction. The secrets that characters keep — about their pasts, their marriages, their desires, their plans — are the engines that drive the plot forward. But Collins also uses mystery to explore deeper questions about knowledge and intimacy: How much can one person ever know about another? What are the limits of confession as a mode of truth-telling? If even the most exhaustive narrative leaves mysteries unresolved, what does that say about the possibility of genuine understanding between human beings?

These questions give _Basil_ a philosophical dimension that elevates it above mere thriller. The novel is a mystery story, but it is also a story about mystery — about the ways in which the unknown and the unknowable shape human experience.

## Historical Context

_Basil_ was published during the great age of the Victorian mystery narrative, from the sensation novels of the 1860s to the detective fiction that would emerge later in the century. Collins himself would go on to write _The Moonstone_ (1868), often cited as the first English detective novel. _Basil_ represents an earlier stage in this development, when mystery was still more atmospheric than procedural — less about solving puzzles than about inhabiting an atmosphere of uncertainty and dread. The novel's use of mystery as both narrative device and thematic concern reflects a culture increasingly aware of the limits of knowledge in a complex, urbanized, and socially mobile world.
