# Honour

The Victorian concept of honour — encompassing family reputation, personal integrity, and social standing — serves as the moral framework against which the characters of _Basil_ measure their actions and judge one another.

## Definition

In _Basil_, honour operates on two levels: the public, familial honour embodied by Basil's father, and the private, personal honour that Basil struggles to maintain while concealing a secret marriage beneath his class. The novel interrogates whether honour is a matter of outward reputation or inward integrity, a tension that drives the plot toward its catastrophic climax.

## Manifestation

Basil's father is introduced as a man defined by an unconventional but absolute code of honour. Unlike the "conventional pride, which the popular notions attach to rank and wealth," his father's honour is described as a deeply personal standard (003-001). The narrator emphasises that "if he had any pride, it was impossible to detect it" in ordinary matters, yet his sense of family honour was absolute (003-002).

This family code of honour becomes the obstacle that prevents Basil from revealing his marriage to Margaret Sherwin. Basil acknowledges that his father is "incapable of voluntarily dishonouring his family, even in his most violent moments" (004-008), yet this very certainty paralyses Basil. He cannot risk the disclosure that would, in his father's eyes, constitute a permanent stain on the family name.

The irony of Basil's position is that he repeatedly appeals to honour in his dealings with the Sherwins. He assures Mr. Sherwin of his "honourable feeling" and "honourable purpose" (010-033, 009-050), even as he engages in a course of deception toward his own family. Mannion himself notes this contradiction, mockingly referring to Basil's "delicate sense of honour" (010-041) while manipulating him toward destruction.

When Basil's secret is finally revealed, his father's response shows that honour, in his conception, is not about public scandal but about the breach of trust within the family. The novel thus presents honour as a double-edged code: it motivates virtuous conduct but can also become a prison of silence and concealment.

## Characters

- **Basil**: The protagonist torn between filial honour and romantic passion. His inability to reconcile these competing obligations drives the plot.
- **Basil's Father**: Embodies an austere, personal code of honour rooted in family integrity rather than social convention.
- **Mr. Sherwin**: Invokes the language of honour strategically, using it to extract Basil's commitment while having no genuine investment in the code.
- **Mannion**: Exploits Basil's sense of honour as a weapon, recognising that Basil's commitment to honourable conduct makes him predictable and vulnerable.

## Significance

Honour in _Basil_ reflects Collins's broader critique of Victorian social codes. By showing how a rigid code of honour can produce exactly the dishonour it seeks to prevent — secrecy, deception, and ultimately tragedy — Collins exposes the contradictions within the Victorian moral framework. The novel suggests that honour, when divorced from honesty and compassion, becomes a destructive rather than a protective force.
