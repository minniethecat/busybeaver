## Description

The garden at North Villa is described with deliberate meanness. It is "a strip of garden—London garden—a close-shut dust-hole" (008-003), hemmed in by "unfinished shops, unfinished gardens" (007-022) of Hollyoake Square's incomplete development.

Far from the pastoral ideal that the word "garden" conventionally evokes, the North Villa garden is a space of constraint: narrow enough to be crossed in a few paces, visible from the windows of the house, and bounded by a gate that becomes one of the novel's most charged thresholds.

## Narrative Role

### The First Glimpse

Basil's initial sighting of Margaret occurs at the garden-gate. Hiding "behind a pillar of the garden-gate" (007-051), he watches her pass, and the image of her dark dress and the "afternoon sunshine" becomes the germ of his obsession.

### The Courting Stage

Basil's visits to North Villa during his courtship repeatedly feature the garden as setting and symbol. He enters through the garden-gate (009-003), waits at it (009-005), and departs through it, with Mr. Sherwin standing at the gate "to watch my departure" (010-079). The gate is a site of surveillance and transaction.

### Margaret's Performance

The garden serves as the setting for Margaret's moments of calculated display. She closes the garden gate "softly" (009-035) after signaling to Basil, passes out "slowly through the garden gate" (009-041), and walks down the square with a deliberation Basil reads as modesty but which retrospect reveals as performance.

### The Fever Dream Return

During Basil's brain-fever, the garden recurs as a hallucinated space. He is "again in the garden at North Villa accidentally" (020-009), the scene replaying with the heightened intensity of delirium, confirming the garden's psychological imprint.

## Associated Characters

**[[basil]]** uses the garden as his point of access to the Sherwin household, and the garden-gate as his watching-post. His relationship to the garden shifts from eager anticipation to haunted memory.

**[[margaret-sherwin]]** is first seen at the garden-gate. Her movements through the garden are described with an attention to gesture that the novel rarely accords her elsewhere.

**[[mr-sherwin]]** polices the garden-gate, standing at it to watch Basil's departures and thereby asserting control over access to his daughter.

**[[clara]]** watches Basil from the dining-room window (009-038), her gaze crossing the garden in a direction that reverses the novel's primary line of sight.

## Symbolism

The garden at North Villa serves as a figure for the relationship it contains: constrained, overlooked, and sterile. It is not the fertile garden of pastoral convention but a "dust-hole" (008-003) where things grow poorly.

The gate functions as a threshold that is crossed but never properly opened: Basil passes through repeatedly but is never truly admitted to the household. His relationship with Margaret remains, like the garden, something cultivated hastily on barren ground.
