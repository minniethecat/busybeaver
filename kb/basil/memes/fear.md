# Fear

Fear is the emotional engine of concealment in _Basil_. If secrecy is the plot mechanism, fear is the psychological force that drives it — the fear of a father's judgment, the fear of social disgrace, the fear of losing what one has risked everything to gain. Collins shows fear not as a momentary emotion but as a chronic condition that shapes character and drives decisions over the entire course of the narrative.

## Definition

In _Basil_, fear operates primarily as fear of consequence — not fear of danger in the abstract, but fear of what will happen when the truth is known. It is fear that keeps Basil silent; fear that drives Mr. Sherwin's calculations; fear that makes Margaret complicit in Mannion's schemes. Collins presents fear as the natural companion of secrecy: once a secret is kept, fear of its exposure becomes the dominant emotional reality.

## Manifestation

Basil's fear of his father is the novel's founding psychological fact. He describes his father in terms that emphasise not tyranny but unapproachability — a man whose very integrity makes him terrifying to confess to. It is not that Basil's father would be cruel, but that his disappointment would be absolute. This fear of moral judgment is, in some ways, more paralysing than fear of punishment would be: there is no appeal against a father's profound disappointment.

Fear also operates socially. Mr. Sherwin's agreement to the secret marriage is driven by fear — fear that the opportunity for social advancement will be lost, fear that Basil will lose interest in Margaret, fear that his own social position is too precarious to risk demanding a public marriage. His fear makes him a collaborator in the very secrecy that will destroy his daughter.

Margaret's fear is the most opaque but perhaps the most consequential. What does she fear? Exposure? Abandonment? Mannion himself? The novel leaves her inner life deliberately obscure, but fear — of Basil's discovery, of Mannion's power, of the consequences of her own choices — is the most plausible reading of her increasingly desperate behaviour.

## Characters

- **Basil**: Fear of his father's judgment is the primary obstacle to honesty, driving the entire plot of concealment.
- **Mr. Sherwin**: Fear of lost social opportunity drives his strategic management of the secret marriage.
- **Margaret Sherwin**: Her fear — of exposure, of Mannion, of the collapse of her social position — is the novel's great unspoken emotional reality.

## Significance

Fear in _Basil_ is not the Gothic fear of supernatural forces but the realistic fear of social and moral consequences. This is characteristic of sensation fiction's relocation of terror from the supernatural to the social realm. Collins shows that the fear of disgrace, of losing one's place in the social order, of being judged by those one loves — these are as powerful and as destructive as any supernatural dread.
