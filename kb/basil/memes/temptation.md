## Definition

In the moral universe of _Basil_, temptation is not an abstract theological category but an acutely physical and psychological force that threatens to overwhelm reason and resolve. Collins treats temptation as a test of character that operates on multiple registers: the sensual lure of forbidden desire, the social pull of worldly advancement, and — most darkly — the primal impulse toward violence. Unlike the static notion of sin as transgression, temptation in the novel unfolds as a dramatic process, one that the narrator retrospectively anatomises with the precision of a confession.

## Manifestation

Temptation appears at three critical junctures in the novel, each escalating in severity and consequence.

### The Sensual Temptation

Basil's first and defining encounter with temptation occurs upon seeing Margaret Sherwin in an omnibus. From the moment of that chance encounter, he is seized by an urge that he recognises as dangerous yet finds himself powerless to resist. He frames the question to himself in stark terms: "was I still strong enough to resist the temptation which accident had thrown in my way?" (008-006). The metaphor is telling — temptation is an external force, a thing "thrown" into his path by accident rather than something he has sought.

Basil contemplates extreme measures: he asks himself whether he should "leave London, leave England, fly from the temptation, no matter where, or at what sacrifice" (008-011). The language of flight positions temptation as an adversary from which one must physically escape. Yet he does not flee. Instead, he rationalises pursuit of Margaret, and this failure to resist sets the entire tragic plot in motion.

### The Worldly Temptation

Through Mannion's bitter disclosure, the reader learns that Margaret herself was not immune to temptation — but of a different order. Mannion reveals that "in spite of the temptations of youth, wealth, and birth which your proposals held out to her, she accepted them at first [...] with a secret terror and misgiving, produced by the remembrance of me" (024-064). Margaret's temptation is social ambition: the prospect of marrying above her station, of securing "your father's money, and carriages, and fine houses" (024-063). Her yielding to this temptation, despite her lingering feelings for Mannion, makes her complicit in the novel's central deception.

### The Murderous Temptation

The most harrowing instance of temptation occurs in the final confrontation on the Cornish cliffs. Standing beside Mannion, with "the incessant crashing of the sea on the outer rocks" and his brain "turned to fire" and heart "to ice," Basil experiences "a horrible temptation to rid myself forever of the wretch before me, by hurling him over the precipice at my feet" (028-056). This is temptation at its most primal — the impulse to murder, born of rage and despair. Unlike the earlier temptations of desire and ambition, this one threatens not merely social standing or happiness but the soul itself.

### Temptation Resisted

The novel also records moments of successful resistance. After Margaret's death, Basil receives her letters: "I resisted the temptation to dash them away" (027-039). This small act of self-mastery, set against the larger pattern of yielding, suggests the possibility of moral growth through suffering. It is significant that Basil's moment of resistance comes after his greatest losses — temptation is most potent, it seems, when one still has something to risk.

## Characters

**[[basil]]** is the character most thoroughly defined by his struggles with temptation. His yielding to the temptation of Margaret, his near-yielding to the temptation to kill Mannion, and his eventual, hard-won resistance when reading Margaret's letters trace a moral arc from weakness toward self-mastery.

**[[margaret-sherwin]]** faces the temptation of social elevation. Her acceptance of Basil's proposal, despite her secret connection to Mannion, reveals her as a character capable of calculating self-interest even in matters of the heart.

**[[ralph]]**, Basil's brother, provides an early model of succumbing to temptation. His "reformatory attachment to a woman older than himself" (004-017) — an affair that scandalises the family — prefigures Basil's own transgressions and establishes the theme of moral weakness as a family inheritance.

**[[robert-mannion]]** functions less as a tempted figure than as temptation personified. He is the serpent in the garden: the one who reveals Margaret's duplicity, who cultivates Basil's torment, and whose very presence on the cliff edge provokes the murderous impulse.

## Significance

Temptation serves as the novel's central moral dynamic, the hinge upon which character and plot both turn. Collins uses it to construct a distinctly Victorian moral psychology in which the individual is perpetually tested by forces both internal and external. The novel's first-person narration heightens this effect: because we experience temptation entirely through Basil's retrospective consciousness, we inhabit his struggle rather than merely observing it.

Structurally, temptation escalates across the novel's three parts: from romantic desire (Part I), through the social pressures of deception (Part II), to the ultimate temptation of violence and murder (Part III). This escalation pattern is characteristic of sensation fiction's tendency to push moral situations to their extreme, forcing both character and reader to confront the limits of ethical restraint.

The theological dimension is muted but present. Mr. Sherwin's insistence on a secret marriage, Basil's father's "strict principles of honour which made no allowance for temptations and weaknesses" (024-017), and the repeated language of sin and confession all situate temptation within a recognisably Christian moral framework, even as Collins's interest lies more in psychological process than in doctrinal resolution.
