# The Landlady

The landlady is a minor but significant figure in the novel's later sections. During Basil's period of poverty and pursuit — when he has been reduced to "penny-a lining in third-class newspapers" (024-028) — she provides shelter and basic human kindness. Her presence offers a glimpse of a world outside the novel's claustrophobic dynamics of family, class, and revenge.

## Role

The landlady represents the ordinary decency that exists outside the novel's central drama. She is not involved in the plots of passion, secrecy, and revenge that destroy the main characters; she is simply a person who provides shelter to someone in need. Her practical kindness — asking few questions, accepting Basil on his own terms — represents a form of human relationship that is notably absent from the novel's main plot.

## Biography

The landlady appears in the sections of the novel that describe Basil's life after the catastrophe — his period of poverty, his pursuit by Mannion, and his eventual illness. She provides the room in which Basil lives and, eventually, the sickroom in which he recovers from his fever. Her presence is practical rather than dramatic; she is a function (shelter) more than a character.

## Relationships

- **Basil**: Her tenant. Their relationship is defined by practical kindness rather than emotional involvement — a form of human connection that the novel implicitly values.

## Appearances

Journal section; Letters in Conclusion (Basil's illness and recovery)
