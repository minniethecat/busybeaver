## Definition

Anguish in *Basil* is the emotional keynote of the novel's confessional mode. Unlike the more abstract "suffering" or the more theatrical "despair," anguish names a specific quality of pain — acute, inward, and resistant to expression — that Collins associates with the experience of concealed guilt. Basil's narration is saturated with the language of anguish: his heart "strained and writhed in anguish" (019-026), his memories are "anguish of remembrance" (021-007), and the death of Margaret provokes "the last earthly anguish of the tortured spirit" (026-065). Anguish is what guilt feels like from the inside, and Collins's innovation in *Basil* is to make this interior experience the primary subject of the novel.

## Manifestation

Basil introduces anguish early, connecting it to the moral contagion of his secrecy. Describing the effect of his hidden marriage on his relationship with his sister Clara, he writes:

> "I felt guilty of infecting with my anguish the pure heart that loved me" (005-012)

The metaphor of infection is striking: anguish is not a private emotion but a communicable disease, spreading from the guilty to the innocent. This early passage establishes the pattern that will govern the novel's emotional economy — anguish originates in transgression and propagates outward, contaminating everyone it touches.

The novel's most intense concentration of anguish occurs in the chapters surrounding Margaret's death. Basil describes his psychological state in language that fuses physical and emotional pain:

> "My heart strained and writhed in anguish; the life within me raged and struggled" (019-026)

The verbs — "strained," "writhed," "raged," "struggled" — are all verbs of bodily extremity. Collins refuses to separate mental anguish from physical sensation; the suffering mind is a suffering body, and the first-person narration registers anguish as a somatic event.

When Basil reconstructs the chronology of Margaret's death, he marks the moment with a parenthetical cry:

> "The day (oh anguish of remembrance!) on which I wrote to my father" (021-007)

The exclamation — enclosed in parentheses as if Basil cannot bear to let it fully into the sentence — performs the very anguish it names. The narrative act itself becomes an occasion of renewed suffering: remembering is anguish, and writing is remembering, so writing is anguish. This recursive structure — anguish about anguish — is characteristic of the novel's confessional mode.

In the novel's final movement, anguish acquires a redemptive dimension. Basil describes his dying father's last moments:

> "His eyes were fixed in anguish and in love unutterable on the dying woman" (022-098)

The pairing — "anguish and in love unutterable" — yokes suffering to affection, suggesting that the deepest love expresses itself as anguish. This is the novel's most developed emotional insight: that the capacity for anguish is inseparable from the capacity for love, and that both are heightened, not diminished, by the experience of moral catastrophe.

The novel's concluding pages return to anguish as the definitive emotional residue of Basil's story. In the final retrospective passage, he describes Margaret's deathbed:

> "Instantly, an expression of anguish and shame flew over the features" (026-121)

Anguish and shame, paired in this final image, are the twin legacies of the secret marriage plot. They are inseparable — shame produces anguish, anguish registers shame — and together they constitute the emotional inheritance that Basil carries into his Cornish exile.

## Characters

**Basil** is the novel's primary vessel of anguish. His first-person narration is, in effect, a sustained exercise in the articulation of anguish — the novel exists because Basil needs to confess what he has suffered. His anguish is both genuine (he has suffered) and performative (the confession is also a bid for sympathy), and Collins's achievement is to hold both possibilities in suspension.

**Margaret Sherwin** experiences anguish that the novel can only report at second hand. Her deathbed cries — "Innocent Margaret! Oh, me!" — are filtered through Basil's narration, and her interior experience remains inaccessible. The asymmetry is structural: Basil's anguish is the novel's subject; Margaret's anguish is its object.

**Ralph Basil** (Basil's father) is the character who most visibly embodies the transition from composure to anguish. His discovery of the secret marriage shatters the stoic authority he has maintained throughout the novel, and his deathbed scene is the novel's most powerful image of anguish transformed into love.

**Robert Mannion** notably does *not* experience anguish. His confession letter is remarkable for its emotional detachment — he describes his suffering without performing it. Mannion's immunity to anguish is what makes him terrifying; he is the only character who can inflict anguish without feeling it.

## Significance

Anguish in *Basil* serves a double function. At the level of character, it is the consequence of moral transgression — the price Basil pays for his secrecy. At the level of narration, it is the rhetorical mode through which the reader is invited to sympathize with a narrator who has done terrible things. The novel's emotional logic is: if Basil suffers enough, the reader will forgive him. Whether the equation works is a question Collins leaves for each reader to answer.

The concept also illuminates the novel's relationship to its Gothic inheritance. The Gothic novel externalized terror in castles, ghosts, and villains; the sensation novel, as Collins practices it, internalizes terror as anguish. The haunted castle becomes the haunted mind, and the novel's most frightening passages are not those that describe physical danger but those that register the "last earthly anguish of the tortured spirit" (026-065).

## Historical Context

The mid-Victorian period saw a growing interest in the psychology of emotion, influenced by the rise of phrenology, early psychiatry, and the literature of religious confession. Collins's treatment of anguish draws on the tradition of spiritual autobiography (Augustine, Bunyan) while secularizing it: Basil's confession is made not to God but to the reader, and his anguish is not the prelude to redemption but its substitute. This secularization of anguish — suffering as its own justification, rather than a stage on the path to salvation — is characteristic of the sensation novel's broader challenge to Victorian moral certainties.
