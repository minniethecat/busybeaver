# Death

Death is the terminal point toward which the plot of _Basil_ moves — not as a single climactic event but as a series of fatalities that mark the stations of the novel's moral descent. From Margaret's death in disgrace to Mannion's ambiguous end, death is the consequence that secrecy, passion, and revenge ultimately deliver.

## Definition

In _Basil_, death functions both as a plot event and as a thematic principle. As plot, deaths mark the novel's major turning points. As theme, death represents the ultimate cost of the moral failures the novel chronicles — the price that must be paid for passion unchecked by duty, secrecy unrelieved by confession, and revenge un tempered by mercy.

## Manifestation

Margaret's death is the novel's central fatality — the event that transforms Basil's moral failure into an irreparable catastrophe. Her death is described in the language of physical and moral decay: "guilt and terror had made her hideous" (019-044). Death does not redeem Margaret or purify her memory; it simply ends her suffering and removes her from the narrative. Collins's refusal to sentimentalise Margaret's death is one of the novel's most striking features.

Mannion's death is more ambiguous. The novel offers multiple accounts of his end — an accident, a disappearance, a possible survival — leaving the reader uncertain whether he is dead at all. This ambiguity is thematically appropriate: Mannion, who represented the force of relentless, amoral persistence, cannot simply be killed off. His death must be as uncertain as his life was opaque.

Against these dark deaths, the novel sets the possibility of survival. Basil does not die — he endures. Clara does not die — she sustains. The novel's closing pages suggest that survival, not death, is the final word, but survival marked and permanently altered by the deaths that have preceded it.

## Characters

- **Margaret Sherwin**: Her death is the novel's central fatality — the event that makes Basil's guilt irreparable and sets Mannion's revenge in final motion.
- **Mannion**: His death (or disappearance) closes the revenge narrative but leaves questions unresolved — appropriate for a figure defined by opacity.
- **Basil**: Survives the deaths of those around him, but his survival is a form of living death — a life defined by what has been lost.
- **Clara**: Her survival represents the possibility of life continuing beyond catastrophe — the novel's sole unambiguous affirmation.

## Significance

Death in _Basil_ reflects the sensation novel's willingness to push beyond the conventional moral economy in which the guilty die and the innocent live. Collins distributes death without regard to desert: Margaret dies, but so might Mannion; Basil lives, but his survival is a punishment as much as a reprieve. This moral complexity — death as consequence rather than justice — is characteristic of Collins's mature fiction.
