## Event

On the fourth day after Margaret Sherwin's death, Basil stands alone in the churchyard by her grave. He has watched her dying moments and now performs the last duty: "it was left for me to bestow on her remains the last human charity which the living can extend to the dead" (PN 027-001-002). The funeral is a solitary affair — Mr. Sherwin has refused to attend, having "never forgave the action by which she thwarted him in his mercenary designs" (PN 027-003). Ralph has returned to London and offered assistance, but Mr. Bernard has taken on the practical responsibilities (PN 027-004).

Basil remains at the graveside after all others have departed: "There was no reason why I should not follow them; and yet I remained, with my eyes fixed upon the freshly-turned earth at my feet, thinking of the dead" (PN 027-005). It is in this moment of solitary grief that Mannion appears, "clothed in a long cloak drawn loosely around his neck, and wearing a shade over his eyes, which hid the whole upper part of his face" (PN 027-006). He approaches slowly, walking with the help of a stick — a figure of physical ruin and moral menace.

The graveside encounter culminates in Mannion's dramatic self-revelation: "'Do you know me again?' he said. 'Do you know me for Robert Mannion?' As he pronounced his name, he raised the shade and looked at me" (PN 027-007). The funeral thus becomes not only a moment of mourning but a site of confrontation — the dead woman's grave serving as the stage for the living antagonist's return.

## Significance

Margaret's funeral is a turning point in the novel's final movement. It marks the end of the Margaret plotline and the beginning of the final pursuit of Mannion. The graveside encounter is charged with Gothic intensity: Mannion, physically ruined, rising like a spectre at the graveside to reveal himself. The event underscores the novel's preoccupation with the consequences of transgression — Margaret's death and burial are shadowed by the man who engineered her ruin. Basil's solitary vigil at the grave also represents his final act of care for Margaret, despite all that has passed between them.
