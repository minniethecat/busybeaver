## Event

During the dark days of Basil's recovery from brain fever, Clara keeps vigil at his bedside. The event is defined by Basil's gradual return to consciousness and the first stirring of his will to live. "Darkly and wearily the days of my recovery went on," Basil records. "After that first outburst of sorrow on the evening when I recognised my sister, and murmured her name as she sat by my side, there sank over all my faculties a dull, heavy trance of mental pain" (PN 021-001).

Clara's presence is constant and wordless. Basil notes "my father's considerate forbearance, Clara's sorrowful devotion" (PN 021-002), but even their care cannot penetrate the "poisonous" remembrances that gnaw at him (PN 021-002). His bodily strength revives feebly, but his "mental energies never showed a sign of recovering" (PN 021-002).

The turning point comes one evening when Basil is alone — his father has taken Clara for a walk. In the solitude, a question forms in his mind that restores his sense of purpose: "I instinctively started to my feet with something of the vigour of my former health... feeling... that my life had purposes and duties, trials and achievements, which were yet to be fulfilled" (PN 021-006). He rushes to the library to consult a newspaper, his "burning anxiety and impatience" driving him to action for the first time since his illness (PN 021-007). The moment is decisive: "The destiny of my future life might be decided by the discovery I was now about to make!" (PN 021-008).

## Significance

Clara's vigil represents the redemptive power of familial love in the novel. While Basil's father offers "considerate forbearance," it is Clara's quiet, steadfast presence that anchors Basil to life. Her role as silent guardian — present at the moment of recognition and sustaining him through the darkness — establishes her as the novel's moral centre. The event also marks Basil's transition from passive suffering to active seeking, the first sign of the narrative agency he will need for the climactic events to follow.
