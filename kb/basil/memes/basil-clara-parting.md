## Summary

After a week's visit to his family's country hall — a visit he extended only for Clara's sake — Basil prepares to return to London and to Margaret. His father's coldness has not thawed, and the house that was once home feels alien to him. Clara, perceiving his lowered spirits, gives their farewell a carefully cheerful character: "she refrained from making any allusion to the shortness of my stay; and merely said that we should soon meet again in London" (017-026). The parting crystallises both the strength of Clara's understanding and the burden of Basil's continued secrecy.

## Context

Basil has returned to the family hall in the aftermath of his failed attempt to confess his secret marriage to his father. His father, detecting evasion rather than candour, has refused to hear anything short of full confession. Basil, paralysed between the desire to unburden himself and the fear of consequences, has said nothing. The week at the hall has been painful — his father's coldness, the winter gloom, and above all his longing to be back with Margaret have made home feel "home no longer, except in name" (017-025).

## The Event

### The Failed Confession

Before the parting, Basil makes "my first and last attempt at venturing towards the revelation of my secret to my father, by hints and half-admissions" (017-022). His father detects the evasion instantly: "You speak hesitatingly — your eyes do not meet mine fairly and boldly" (017-020). He delivers an ultimatum — "Choose at once, between saying nothing, and saying all" (017-020) — and Basil, incapable of either, says nothing. The father leaves the room, and the opportunity passes forever.

### The Parting from Clara

Clara's farewell is remarkable for what it does not say. She "refrained from making any allusion to the shortness of my stay" (017-026), instead giving their parting "as happy and hopeful a character as possible" (017-026). Basil recognises this as an act of deliberate kindness: "She evidently saw that my visit had weighed a little on my spirits" (017-026). The mutual understanding between brother and sister — "We now thoroughly understood each other; and that was some consolation on leaving her" (017-026) — is one of the few unambiguously positive relationships in the novel.

## Significance

This minor scene accomplishes several things. It demonstrates Clara's perceptiveness and emotional intelligence — she sees Basil's unhappiness without needing to interrogate its cause. It underscores the cost of Basil's secrecy: the warmth of family has become inaccessible to him because he cannot be honest. And it establishes the pattern of Clara as the one person who understands Basil without judgment, a role that will become crucial after the catastrophe.

## Characters Involved

**[[basil]]** is trapped in a contradiction of his own making: he wants his father's understanding but cannot offer the honesty that understanding would require. His parting from Clara is both a comfort and a reproach — her kindness exposes what his secrecy has cost him.

**[[clara]]** demonstrates the emotional intelligence that makes her the novel's moral centre. She does not demand explanations; she offers support in the form most Basil can accept: a cheerful farewell that pretends, for his sake, that nothing is wrong.

**[[basil-father]]** maintains his cold distance to the end of Basil's visit, parting from his son "exactly as we had met" (017-026). His refusal to accept half-truths is morally correct but humanly inflexible — he will not meet Basil halfway.
