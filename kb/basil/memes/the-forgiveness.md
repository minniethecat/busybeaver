## Summary

Margaret Sherwin dies in a London lodging-house, attended only by Basil and a doctor. At the moment of her death, Basil finds that he has already forgiven her — not through an act of will but as a fact of feeling that precedes conscious thought. The forgiveness scene is quiet, private, and morally complex: Basil's calm is not indifference but the exhaustion of passion, a state in which forgiveness is the only emotion left.

## Context

Margaret's death follows the catastrophic discovery of her betrayal with Mannion, the collapse of the secret marriage, and her flight from North Villa. She has been living in concealment, her health destroyed by the consequences of her transgression. Basil, summoned to her deathbed, arrives to find a woman utterly broken — "guilt and terror had made her hideous" (019-044) in the discovery, and now she lies dying, her body as ruined as her reputation.

## The Event

### The Deathbed

The doctor attending Margaret conducts his examination with professional calm. He places his hand on her heart, then "softly drew up the bed-linen, till it hid her face" (026-132). His words — "she is asleep; asleep, never to wake again. She is dead" (026-133) — mark the end of Margaret's brief, tragic trajectory from the beautiful object of Basil's infatuation to a corpse in a rented room.

### The Forgiveness

Basil's response to Margaret's death is striking in its quietness: "I turned aside my head in silence, for my thoughts, at that moment, were not the thoughts which can be spoken by man to man" (026-134). When the doctor praises his composure — "I am glad to see that you can behave so calmly under so hard a trial" (026-135) — Basil's internal reply constitutes the scene's moral centre: "at that moment it was fit that I should be calm; for I could remember that I had forgiven her" (026-137).

## Significance

The forgiveness of Margaret is one of the novel's most theologically and psychologically rich moments. Basil's forgiveness is not the product of deliberation or virtue but of emotional exhaustion — he has passed beyond anger into a state where forgiveness is simply what remains. The scene also completes the novel's treatment of Margaret: she is not merely the instrument of Basil's punishment but a human being whose suffering Basil can finally acknowledge without the distorting lens of either infatuation or outrage.

## Characters Involved

**[[basil]]** achieves, in the moment of Margaret's death, a moral clarity that has eluded him throughout the novel. His forgiveness is quiet, untheatrical, and all the more powerful for being involuntary — not a decision but a discovery of what he already feels.

**[[margaret-sherwin]]** dies offstage from the novel's main action, her death the logical terminus of a life shaped by her father's social ambition and her own weakness. The forgiveness Basil extends to her is the only grace her tragedy receives.
