# London

London is more than the setting of _Basil_ — it is a condition of the novel's plot and a metaphor for the social forces that drive it. The anonymity of the crowd, the mixing of classes in public spaces, and the possibility of concealment that the modern city offers are all essential to the story Collins tells.

## Urban Anonymity

Collins's London is a space where "persons of all classes and all temperaments are so easily and so strangely intermingled" (007-004). This intermingling — the omnibus where Basil first sees Margaret, the streets where strangers pass without recognition — is what makes the secret marriage possible. In a small town or rural estate, everyone would know everyone; in London, a man can keep a wife hidden for months.

## Class Geography

The novel maps London along class lines: the aristocratic quarters of Basil's family, the suburban commercial district of the Sherwins, and the poorer districts where Basil lives during his period of poverty ("penny-a lining in third-class newspapers," 024-028). This geographical segregation is the spatial expression of the class barriers that drive the plot.

## Significance

Collins's London is a characteristically modern city — a place of freedom and danger, anonymity and exposure. It represents both the possibility of escaping social constraints and the impossibility of doing so without cost.
