## Event

On the morning of October 22nd, Basil leaves his cottage at the fishing hamlet of Treen, walking through thick mist towards Penzance. Disoriented by the fog and the crashing of the sea, he loses his way and finds himself on a treacherous promontory — a place of "narrow ledges and treacherous precipices" (PN 028-046). Here, at the edge of the Cornish cliffs, with the Atlantic waves thundering against the granite below, the final confrontation with Mannion takes place. Basil records that "The events of this one day have changed the whole future of my life" (PN 028-037).

Basil departs at dawn, meeting only silent neighbours who "drew aside to let me advance, without saying a word" (PN 028-038). The storm has abated but the sea retains its "terror and grandeur," with "huge Atlantic waves still hurled themselves, foaming and furious, against the massive granite of the Cornish cliffs" (PN 028-039). Basil walks with the sound of the sea on his right, navigating by ear through the mist (PN 028-041), until the sound shifts strangely to both sides and he realises he has wandered onto the deadly promontory (PN 028-042-043).

The promontory contains a "black, yawning hole" — a blowhole that "slanted nearly straight downwards, like a tunnel, to unknown and unfathomable depths" (PN 028-045). This geological feature becomes central to the confrontation's violent resolution.

## Significance

The Treen confrontation is the novel's climactic event, bringing Basil and Mannion face to face on the edge of the abyss — both literally and metaphorically. The setting on the storm-lashed Cornish coast, amid mist and precipice, externalises the moral and psychological extremity of their encounter. The blowhole, "yawning" into "unknown and unfathomable depths," symbolises the moral void at the heart of Mannion's character and the destruction that has consumed both men's lives.
