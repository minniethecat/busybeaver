## Definition

Suspense in *Basil* is simultaneously a narrative method and a thematic preoccupation. As method, it is the mechanism by which Collins controls the reader's access to information — delaying revelations, protracting crises, and holding the reader in a state of anticipatory anxiety that mirrors the protagonist's own. As theme, suspense names the psychological condition of waiting under uncertainty, a state that the novel's characters inhabit for extended periods and that Collins presents as a distinctive form of suffering. The preface articulates this dual function: Collins aims "to excite the suspense" of the reader while also "occupying his deeper feelings" (P02-007), making suspense not merely a technique of entertainment but a vehicle for psychological exploration.

## Manifestation

Collins deploys suspense most intensively around the novel's central crises. The two-day interval between Basil's proposal to Margaret and her father's response becomes a sustained study in temporal distortion through anxiety:

> "In the suspense and impatience of that long interval of expectation, all my thoughts were centred on Margaret" (009-039)

Time itself is warped by the state of waiting; the "long interval" is objectively brief but subjectively interminable. Basil attempts to manage the suspense by removing himself from its immediate context: "It was necessary to pass my two days' ordeal of suspense away from home — far enough away to make it impossible for me to return before the appointed time" (011-003). The spatial displacement — going away to wait — externalizes the psychological displacement of suspense, in which the sufferer is always somewhere other than where resolution will occur.

The night of Margaret's death marks the novel's supreme suspense sequence. Basil waits at a hotel while the surgeon attends Margaret, and every minute stretches into agony:

> "A sharp agony of suspense tried me fiercely" (019-035)

Collins protracts the scene through a series of sensory details — the ticking of a watch, the creak of a stair — that externalize the internal state of dread. Earlier, Basil describes himself as being "under the influence of an absorbing suspense" while waiting alone (022-003), and notes how his "present agitation and suspense" might affect his judgment (022-015). The accumulation of these references across a single chapter demonstrates Collins's technique of building suspense through repetition and variation rather than through a single dramatic peak.

The motif recurs throughout the novel's confessional passages. Mannion reflects on "the suspense under which she might be suffering" (024-090), revealing that even the villain is aware of suspense as a form of torment. In the final Cornish chapters, Basil confesses the cumulative toll:

> "I should have sunk under the torment of suspense and suspicion in which my life was now passed" (028-012)

The phrase "under the constant suspense in which I have been living" (028-067) appears in the same section, confirming that suspense has become not an intermittent state but the permanent condition of Basil's existence after the catastrophe. The pervasiveness of suspense across all phases of the novel — courtship, crisis, aftermath — suggests that it is the default emotional condition of a life built on concealment.

## Characters

**Basil** is the novel's primary subject of suspense — his first-person narration places the reader inside a consciousness perpetually awaiting some dreaded outcome. Every phase of his story is structured around an interval of uncertain waiting: the courtship (009-039), the night of crisis (019-035), the pursuit of Mannion, the Cornish exile (028-012).

**Margaret Sherwin** is both the object and the fellow-sufferer of suspense. Basil keeps her waiting for the revelation of his identity (018-007); later, she waits in terror for the exposure of her own secret. Margaret's suspense is largely inaccessible to the reader, filtered through Basil's incomplete understanding, which itself becomes a second-order suspense — the reader is suspended between what Basil knows and what Margaret may be concealing.

## Significance

Suspense is the formal principle that distinguishes the sensation novel from other Victorian fictional modes, and *Basil* — Collins's first major work in the genre — shows the technique in its emergent form. Unlike the elaborate multi-plot mysteries of *The Woman in White* or *The Moonstone*, *Basil* derives its suspense from a single, tightly focused situation: the consequences of one secret marriage. This concentration gives the novel's suspense an existential dimension absent from more mechanically plotted sensation fiction. Basil's waiting is not merely a plot device but a representation of the human condition under secrecy — the perpetual deferral of a reckoning that, when it finally arrives, is worse than the waiting itself.

The novel also reflects on the ethics of suspense. Collins's preface acknowledges a "sacrifice, in certain places, of dramatic effect" for the sake of "a good moral use" (P02-010), suggesting that suspense must be balanced against moral seriousness. This tension — between the narrative imperative to delay and the moral imperative to resolve — is never fully reconciled, and it is precisely this irresolution that gives *Basil* its distinctive emotional texture.

## Historical Context

Collins's theory and practice of suspense developed in the context of Victorian serial publication, where the interval between installments naturally prolonged reader uncertainty. Although *Basil* was published in three volumes rather than in monthly parts, its structure — with the climactic revelation placed at the end of the second volume — adapts serial suspense techniques to the triple-decker format. Collins's mature sensation novels would perfect this art of installment-based suspense, but *Basil* already demonstrates his grasp of the principle that "the reader's interest" depends on "exciting his suspense" through strategic delay (P02-007). The novel's self-consciousness about its own suspense mechanisms also reflects the influence of Collins's close friendship with Charles Dickens, whose *Bleak House*, published the same year, would push serial suspense to new heights of structural complexity.
