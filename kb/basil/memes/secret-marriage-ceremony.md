# The Secret Marriage

The secret marriage between Basil and Margaret Sherwin is the central event from which all subsequent action follows. Contracted without the knowledge of Basil's father, across class lines, and with the active complicity of Margaret's father, the marriage is a social impossibility made temporarily real through secrecy — a condition that cannot be sustained.

## The Agreement

Basil proposes the secret marriage to Mr. Sherwin as a strategic necessity: "a public, acknowledged marriage was, under existing circumstances, out of the question" (009-070). Mr. Sherwin agrees on the condition of "the strictest secrecy" (010-015), calculating that "a private marriage gives us a chance for the future" (010-060). The marriage is thus founded on a contradiction: it is a public institution (marriage) conducted in private (secrecy), a social contract that defies the social order.

## Consequences

The secrecy of the marriage isolates Margaret from Basil's world and Basil from his family. It creates the conditions for Mannion's manipulation and Margaret's betrayal. What was intended as a temporary measure — secrecy until Basil could "divulge my marriage to my father, with the best chance of" success (010-053) — becomes a permanent condition from which there is no escape.
