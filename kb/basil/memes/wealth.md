## Definition

In *Basil*, wealth operates as both a structural condition and a moral problematic. The novel distinguishes between two forms of wealth: the inherited, landed fortune of the Basil family, which confers status, leisure, and moral authority, and the newly acquired commercial wealth of Stephen Sherwin, which is treated with suspicion and resentment. Collins uses wealth not merely as a background fact but as an active force in the narrative — it enables Basil's secret marriage by funding North Villa, it shapes the power dynamics between father and son, and it becomes the currency (literal and metaphorical) of concealment, bribery, and ruin. The novel interrogates whether wealth protects or corrupts character, ultimately suggesting that it does both.

## Manifestation

Wealth is introduced early in the novel as a defining feature of the Basil lineage. The narrator establishes his father's fortune in the opening pages:

> "I knew that my father's wealth, exclusive of his landed property, was enormous" (002-008)

This wealth is inseparable from the father's moral stature — it is old money, land-based, and therefore legitimate. Ralph Basil's world is one in which "wealth, power, and position" (022-073) form an indivisible triad, each term reinforcing the others.

The novel's central class conflict turns on the distinction between old and new money. Mr. Sherwin, as "a merchant of enormous wealth, who had recently been raised" (003-004), represents the ascendant commercial class whose fortune lacks the social legitimacy of inherited land. Basil's narrative voice betrays an inherited disdain for "the weakness of rank-worship and wealth-worship in men of Mr. Sherwin's kind" (009-071), yet he is himself a beneficiary of precisely the same "rank-worship" he criticizes.

In Mannion's confession, wealth becomes a direct instrument of temptation and corruption. Mannion's father, a "weak, kind, careless" man, was ruined by proximity to wealth: "he was wrong. The wealthy patron held strict principle" (024-017). The confession also reveals Mannion's resentment of Basil's inherited advantages:

> "Your proposal offered to throw me into temptation of the temptations of youth, wealth, and birth" (024-064)

Here Mannion explicitly names wealth as a temptation — one of the three pillars, alongside youth and birth, that constitute Basil's unearned privilege. Mannion's own father had been ruined by "great position, and great wealth" (024-014), and the son's revenge plot is, in part, a response to the injuries that wealth inequality inflicts.

The theme surfaces again in the negotiation with Sherwin over Margaret's reputation. When Basil's father learns of the secret marriage, the conflict is framed in explicitly financial terms: "he interested, or fancied he interested, one wealthy neighbour after another" (024-014). The family's wealth becomes both shield and weapon — it can buy silence, as Sherwin coldly acknowledges:

> "They know our family is a wealthy family; and they will make the bargain accordingly" (025-035)

Basil's father also invokes wealth as a bulwark against scandal, declaring that he will "give you every advantage of my recommendation and a good introduction to his wealthy manufacturing correspondents" (024-051). The offer of a new life abroad, funded by paternal wealth, is the family's preferred solution to the crisis — a solution that only wealth makes possible, but which also reveals wealth's function as an instrument of erasure, buying silence and exile rather than confronting the moral truth.

## Characters

**Ralph Basil** (Basil's father) embodies the moral authority of inherited wealth. His fortune, "exclusive of his landed property" (002-008), is the foundation of the family's social position. He wields wealth as a paternal instrument, offering Basil financial rescue in the form of continental exile, and his values are inseparable from his class position — duty, honour, and reputation are the virtues of a man whose wealth has never been in question.

**Stephen Sherwin** represents the morally ambiguous figure of the newly wealthy merchant. His "enormous wealth" (003-004) has purchased a suburban villa and a veneer of gentility, but Collins treats his money as tainted — the product of trade rather than land, and therefore suspect. Sherwin's willingness to leverage his daughter's marriage for social advancement and his later attempt to extract payment for silence both reflect a commercialized view of human relationships that the novel condemns.

**Basil** is the conflicted inheritor of wealth, simultaneously dependent on his father's fortune and resentful of the constraints it imposes. His secret marriage is an act of rebellion against the class expectations that wealth enforces, yet he cannot escape them — when crisis comes, he falls back on paternal wealth for protection.

**Robert Mannion** is the novel's most penetrating critic of wealth. His confession reveals a man whose life has been shaped by wealth's uneven distribution — his father ruined by a wealthy patron, himself forced into service and dependence. His revenge against Basil is, in part, class revenge: the impoverished secretary destroying the wealthy heir.

## Significance

Wealth in *Basil* serves as both enabler and corrupter. It enables Basil's family to contemplate — and ultimately execute — a strategy of concealment and exile that preserves their social standing at the cost of moral honesty. At the same time, the novel consistently associates the pursuit and defense of wealth with moral compromise. The complicity of every character in the system of "rank-worship and wealth-worship" (009-071) is Collins's indictment of a society in which money determines not only status but truth — what can be said, what must be hidden, and who must pay.

The novel's treatment of wealth also reflects a transitional moment in Victorian class relations. The tension between the Basils (landed gentry) and the Sherwins (commercial bourgeoisie) captures the anxiety of an era in which inherited status was being challenged by new forms of wealth. Collins sides, ultimately, with the old order — Ralph Basil's wealth is treated as legitimate, Sherwin's as vulgar — but the novel is honest enough to show that both forms of wealth are complicit in the machinery of concealment and ruin that destroys Margaret and nearly destroys Basil.

## Historical Context

The 1840s and 1850s saw the rapid expansion of the British commercial middle class, whose wealth increasingly rivaled that of the landed aristocracy. The 1846 repeal of the Corn Laws marked a symbolic victory for commercial over landed interests, and the social anxieties generated by this shift pervade mid-Victorian fiction. Collins's depiction of Sherwin — the wealthy merchant whose fortune cannot purchase gentility — reflects a broader cultural debate about whether wealth alone could confer social legitimacy. The novel's answer is equivocal: Sherwin is vulgar and morally compromised, but the Basils' "legitimate" wealth proves equally capable of sustaining injustice. This ambivalence places *Basil* within a tradition of Victorian fiction — from *Vanity Fair* (1848) to *Great Expectations* (1861) — that interrogated the moral meaning of money.
