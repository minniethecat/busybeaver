## Description

The sea enters the novel on its first page, the "sea-breeze" (001-006) blowing fresh across the beach where fishermen's boats are drawn up. It is introduced as a benign presence, part of the pastoral frame that the fishing hamlet provides.

But the sea of the later chapters is a different force entirely. From the cliff path, Basil looks down toward a sea whose "majesty" has "lost none of its terror" (028-039). When "wind and sea arise together" (028-010), the coast becomes a place of elemental violence. The sea is "never silent in this frightful" channel (028-045), its perpetual noise a reminder of forces beyond human control.

## Narrative Role

### The Frame

The sea provides the novel's acoustic and visual frame. The "sound of the sea always at the same distance" (028-041) accompanies Basil's writing, a constant presence that marks the remoteness of his retreat. When "the noise of the sea was altering completely" (028-042), the change signals a shift in Basil's perception, the landscape itself registering his psychological disorientation.

### Margaret's Cry

In her final illness, Margaret cries out: "Water! water! drown me in the sea; drown me deep, away from the" (026-103). The sea here is not the cleansing element of Romantic convention but the wished-for agent of annihilation — a place deep enough to hide disgrace. That the sea is invoked at this moment of maximum suffering links the Cornish frame to the London tragedy.

### The Prospect

Basil pauses to take in "the radiant prospect of earth, and sea, and sky" (027-008), a brief moment of aesthetic relief that the novel's darkness quickly swallows. The sea participates in this prospect as the largest element, the horizon toward which everything tends.

## Associated Characters

**[[basil]]** measures his psychological states against the sea: its calm mirrors his reflective moments, its violence his inner turmoil. Keeping "the sound of the sea always at the same distance" (028-041) becomes a discipline of self-preservation.

**[[margaret-sherwin]]** invokes the sea in her dying delirium (026-103), her cry linking the novel's two geographic poles — the London deathbed and the Cornish coast — in a single, desperate image of immersion and disappearance.

**[[robert-mannion]]** meets his end on the cliffs above the sea, his fall carrying him from the granite heights toward the water below. The sea receives what the cliff rejects.

## Symbolism

The sea in *Basil* is double: it is both the cleansing, purifying element that Romantic tradition promises, and the annihilating deep that Margaret's cry invokes. The novel refuses to resolve this doubleness. The sea that provides Basil with his "radiant prospect" (027-008) is the same sea into which Margaret wishes to be drowned, and the same sea whose perpetual noise accompanies the violent struggle on the cliff. Nature in Collins's hands is morally indifferent — beautiful and terrible, frame and abyss.
