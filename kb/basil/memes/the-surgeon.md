# The Surgeon

The surgeon appears in the novel's late sections as the medical professional who attends Basil during his fever. His presence introduces a note of clinical rationality into a narrative otherwise governed by passion, secrecy, and revenge.

## Role

The surgeon represents the external, rational perspective that the novel's main characters lack. His diagnosis — that Basil's fever may be connected to a brain disorder, asking "Either there has been madness in his family, or his brain has" (026-033) suffered some injury — introduces the Victorian discourse of medical psychology into the narrative. His presence reminds the reader that the psychological states the novel explores — passion, guilt, revenge — have physiological dimensions that Victorian medicine was beginning to theorise.

## Biography

The surgeon appears only in the medical crisis that concludes Basil's narrative. He treats Basil's fever, offers a clinical opinion on its possible causes, and then departs from the narrative. His significance is not biographical but functional: he represents the world of rational, professional observation that exists outside the novel's central drama.

## Relationships

- **Basil**: His patient. The surgeon's clinical gaze offers an alternative perspective on Basil's suffering — not moral judgment but medical diagnosis.
