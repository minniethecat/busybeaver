# Marriage

Marriage is the central institution around which the plot of _Basil_ revolves. The secret marriage between Basil and Margaret Sherwin — contracted across class lines and concealed from family — drives every major event in the novel and serves as the lens through which Collins examines Victorian social codes.

## Definition

In _Basil_, marriage is presented not as a romantic resolution but as a site of conflict, deception, and social negotiation. The novel subverts the conventional marriage plot of Victorian fiction by making the marriage itself the problem rather than the solution. A clandestine marriage, entered into without family sanction and across class boundaries, becomes the engine of tragedy rather than the guarantee of happiness.

## Manifestation

The marriage plot begins with Basil's impulsive attraction to Margaret, which he himself describes as a passion that overrides all other considerations: "I had known nothing of the passion, which is the absorbing passion of a man's life" (008-005). His love leads directly to the proposal, but the marriage cannot be public. As Basil explains to Mr. Sherwin, "a public, acknowledged marriage" is impossible because of his father's certain opposition (009-070).

The marriage is thus contracted in secret, "until I can divulge my marriage to my father, with the best chance of" favourable reception (010-053). Mr. Sherwin consents to this arrangement, calculating that "a private marriage gives us a chance for the future" (010-060). The secrecy of the marriage isolates Margaret from Basil's social world and Basil from his family, creating the conditions for Mannion's manipulation.

Collins foregrounds the legal and social dimensions of marriage throughout. Basil's father is said to view "a degrading marriage contracted by his son" as the ultimate family catastrophe (008-007). The word "degrading" is significant: it is not the marriage itself but its cross-class character that makes it unacceptable. The novel thus exposes the class logic embedded in Victorian marriage customs.

When the marriage collapses — through Margaret's infidelity with Mannion — the novel enters its darkest phase. The marriage that was supposed to unite becomes the vehicle of destruction. In the final Letters in Conclusion, Clara's fate offers a contrasting model of love and loyalty, suggesting that genuine affection need not take the institutional form that proved so catastrophic for Basil.

## Characters

- **Basil**: The husband whose secret marriage isolates him from all sources of support. His romantic idealism about marriage is systematically destroyed.
- **Margaret Sherwin**: The wife whose position in a cross-class marriage leaves her alienated from both her own class and her husband's.
- **Basil's Father**: Represents the patriarchal authority over marriage; his anticipated opposition is the reason for the secrecy.
- **Mr. Sherwin**: The father who treats his daughter's marriage as a social transaction, calculating its advantages with commercial logic.
- **Clara**: Basil's sister, whose devotion offers an implicit critique of the institutional marriage that has destroyed her brother.

## Significance

Collins's treatment of marriage in _Basil_ is notably dark for its period. By making a marriage — not a courtship, not an adultery — the source of catastrophe, the novel challenges the Victorian assumption that marriage is inherently stabilising and redemptive. The secret, cross-class marriage becomes a laboratory in which the contradictions of Victorian social and gender ideology are exposed.
