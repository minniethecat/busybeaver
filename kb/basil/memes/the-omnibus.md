# The Omnibus

The London omnibus is the setting for the novel's originary moment — Basil's first sight of Margaret Sherwin. As a mobile public space where "persons of all classes and all temperaments are so easily and so strangely intermingled" (007-004), the omnibus is the perfect vehicle for the chance encounter that sets the plot in motion.

## Significance

The omnibus represents the democratic randomness of urban life. On an omnibus, a young aristocrat may sit beside a linen-draper's daughter — something that would never happen in the class-segregated spaces of drawing rooms and country estates. The encounter is pure chance, yet its consequences are fatal. Collins uses the omnibus to suggest that the modern city creates possibilities — for connection, for transgression, for catastrophe — that the traditional social order cannot contain.
