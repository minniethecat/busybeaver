## Description

The drawing-room at North Villa is the family's stage for its performance of middle-class respectability. Basil is "shown into the drawing-room" (011-008) on his formal visits, and the room's furnishings — "brand-new" (010-078) — announce the Sherwins' aspirations and their insecurity in equal measure. Everything in the room is new because nothing is inherited, and the absence of family history is itself a statement about the Sherwins' social position.

The room is contrasted explicitly with the drawing-room at Basil's family home, where his father entertains with the unconscious ease of established rank: "What a contrast was this room to the drawing-room at North Villa!" (015-010). The comparison is social as much as aesthetic.

## Narrative Role

### The Formal Visits

Basil's courtship of Margaret is conducted almost entirely in the drawing-room. Mr. and Mrs. Sherwin are always present as chaperones, and Margaret's "manner grew more" constrained (012-049) under their watch. The room becomes a theater of propriety in which every participant plays an assigned role.

### Mannion's Appearances

It is in the drawing-room that Basil first encounters Mannion as a regular presence in the Sherwin household. Mannion "joined us in the drawing-room at North Villa" (018-011) on several occasions, his silent watchfulness adding an undercurrent of menace to the domestic tableau. The drawing-room, ostensibly the site of polite sociability, becomes the space in which the novel's central conspiracy takes shape.

### Mrs. Sherwin's Disappearance

The drawing-room also marks Mrs. Sherwin's gradual erasure from the narrative. Basil notes that on "entering the drawing-room, I missed Mrs. Sherwin" (014-002), her absence going unremarked by the family. The room that was designed to display the family's wholeness instead reveals its fractures.

### Basil's Haunted Return

After the catastrophe, Basil's fevered memory returns him to the drawing-room: "I stood again in the drawing-room; I saw him enter" (020-007). The room, like the garden, becomes a site of traumatic recurrence.

## Symbolism

The drawing-room represents the Sherwin family's aspirational facade. Its "brand-new" furnishings signify not comfortable wealth but anxious acquisition; its formal rituals of visiting and chaperonage mask the underlying transaction — a marriage being brokered between a gentleman's son and a linen-draper's daughter.

The contrast with Basil's family home is explicit and pointed. Where Basil's father's drawing-room embodies inherited status, the Sherwins' room embodies status purchased but never quite owned.
