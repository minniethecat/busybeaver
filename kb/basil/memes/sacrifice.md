## Definition

Sacrifice in *Basil* is an asymmetrical moral structure: it is almost always performed by those with more power for the sake of those with less, and it is almost always unrewarded. The novel presents sacrifice as the highest moral gesture available to its characters — the one act that can partially redeem a compromised moral universe — while simultaneously exposing its cost and questioning its rationality. Collins's treatment of sacrifice is distinctive in its refusal to sentimentalize the act: sacrifice in *Basil* is genuinely painful, often futile, and not infrequently the product of the same self-deception that drives the novel's darker deeds.

## Manifestation

The language of sacrifice pervades Basil's narration of his relationship with Margaret. From the earliest stages of the courtship, he frames his conduct in sacrificial terms:

> "All the self-sacrifice to which I had cheerfully consented" (011-065)

This sacrificial self-presentation is deeply ambiguous — it is at once genuinely felt and strategically deployed, a way of ennobling a course of action that Basil knows to be morally compromised. Earlier, he had wondered whether he should "take refuge in my own resources, no matter where, or at what sacrifice" (008-011), presenting sacrifice as an acceptable price for following desire. His promise to Margaret is similarly framed: "no matter with what sacrifice, what hazard, or what delay" (011-055), he will marry her. The accumulation of sacrificial language reveals a pattern: Basil uses the rhetoric of sacrifice to dignify decisions that are, in fact, driven by passion rather than principle.

The ambiguity intensifies in the aftermath of the marriage's collapse:

> "I sacrificed my own happiness and my own prospects" (025-034)

The retrospective framing makes it unclear whether this was noble self-denial or catastrophic misjudgment. His father's ultimatum — "Any other sacrifice, even to taking the girl back with you" (025-035) — reduces human relationships to units of exchange, exposing the transactional logic that underlies even the most elevated sacrificial rhetoric.

Clara, Basil's sister, offers the novel's least ambiguous example of genuine sacrifice:

> "She was so gentle, so kind, so pure in heart; so ready to forget her own sorrows in her anxiety to soothe mine; and yet so noble in self-sacrifice, so firm in her integrity" (009-054)

Clara's sacrifice is the moral counterweight to Basil's self-regarding version — she gives without expecting return, and her sacrifice is directed outward (toward another's welfare) rather than inward (toward the aggrandizement of her own moral self-image). Her willingness to forfeit her own comfort to care for her disgraced brother demonstrates sacrifice as the one genuine moral currency in the novel's otherwise corrupted economy of virtue.

Margaret, by contrast, is described as having been "sacrificed" — the passive voice is telling — to Mannion's schemes: "the wickedness to which I had been sacrificed" (021-016). This use of "sacrifice" in the passive voice transforms the concept from a noble act into something closer to victimization. Margaret is sacrificed by others; she does not sacrifice herself. The tragic irony is that her own husband describes her situation — "Margaret, for whose sake I have sacrificed and suffered so much" (018-004) — as if she were the beneficiary rather than the victim of his sacrifices.

## Characters

**Basil** is the novel's central sacrificial figure, but his sacrifice is morally ambivalent — simultaneously generous and self-serving, authentic and performative. His narrative is, in part, an attempt to recuperate his sacrifices as meaningful even after they have been rendered futile by events.

**Clara** represents sacrifice in its purest form: uncomplaining, effective, and directed entirely toward the good of another. Her sacrifice is the moral standard against which Basil's is implicitly measured — and found wanting.

**Margaret Sherwin** is the novel's principal victim of sacrifice — sacrificed by her father's social ambition, by Basil's class anxiety, and by Mannion's vengefulness. Her trajectory from object of desire to sacrificial victim traces the dark underside of the Victorian marriage plot.

## Significance

Sacrifice is the moral concept through which *Basil* most directly engages the Victorian ideology of separate spheres. The sacrifices demanded of women (Margaret's sexual purity, Clara's self-denying care) are structurally different from those demanded of men (Basil's social position, his inheritance). Collins does not explicitly critique this asymmetry, but the novel's accumulation of sacrificial failures — every major sacrifice in the novel either fails to achieve its object or exacts a disproportionate cost — constitutes an implicit critique of a moral economy that demands renunciation while offering little in return.

The novel's preface acknowledges a "sacrifice, in certain places, of dramatic effect" for "a good moral use" (P02-010), suggesting that sacrifice operates even at the level of narrative construction. Collins's willingness to subordinate sensation to moral seriousness is itself presented as a sacrifice — one that distinguishes *Basil* from less morally serious sensation fiction and aligns it with the Victorian novel's larger project of ethical instruction through entertainment.

## Historical Context

The Victorian cult of self-sacrifice — exemplified in works like Coventry Patmore's *The Angel in the House* (1854) and John Ruskin's *Sesame and Lilies* (1865) — elevated feminine self-denial to the status of a spiritual ideal. Collins's treatment of sacrifice in *Basil* both participates in and subtly undermines this ideal. Clara conforms perfectly to the Angel-in-the-House model, yet her virtue, however genuine, is powerless to prevent the novel's catastrophe. The novel thus registers, without fully endorsing, the suspicion that the Victorian idealization of sacrifice served to naturalize and thereby perpetuate structures of inequality — making women's subordination appear as women's virtue.
