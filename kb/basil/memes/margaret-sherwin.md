# Margaret Sherwin

Margaret Sherwin is the object of Basil's passion and the central female figure in the novel's tragedy. A linen-draper's daughter whose "extreme beauty" (010-022) captivates the aristocratic Basil, Margaret occupies an ambiguous social space — above the working class, below the gentry — that makes her both desirable as a romantic object and unacceptable as a wife within the codes of Victorian [[social-class]]. Her secret marriage to [[basil]], her isolation from both her own class and his, and her eventual betrayal with [[robert-mannion]] drive the novel's tragic arc. Yet Margaret is also the novel's most opaque figure: her inner life is deliberately obscured, seen only through Basil's adoring and then disillusioned gaze, making her at once a victim of the social system that entraps her and an agent in her own destruction.

## Role

Margaret functions as the catalyst of the plot and the site of the novel's deepest moral and narrative ambiguities. As the woman Basil secretly marries, she is the reason for his estrangement from his family. As the woman who betrays him with Mannion, she is the agent of his destruction. Yet Margaret's own perspective is never directly accessible to the reader. She is always seen through Basil's narration: first as an idealized object of romantic desire, then as an increasingly opaque and troubling wife, then as a fallen woman whose "guilt and terror had made her hideous to look upon already" (019-044), and finally — in the retrospective accounts of Mannion (024) and her own mother (023) — as a figure of contested moral responsibility.

This narrative opacity is the key to her significance. Collins refuses to make Margaret a simple villain or a simple victim. She is complicit in the secret marriage and in her relationship with Mannion, yet she is also the product of a social system that gives her few choices and a father who treats her marriage as a commercial transaction. The novel's unwillingness to resolve Margaret into a single moral category is one of its most modern features, and it anticipates Collins's later, more complex female characters such as Marian Halcombe in _The Woman in White_.

## Biography

### Background

Margaret is the daughter of Mr. Sherwin, a linen-draper who keeps a shop in a suburban district of London. The family lives at North Villa, an unfinished house in an unfinished square — a setting whose half-built incompleteness (007-022) mirrors Margaret's own liminal social position. Her father's trade is discovered by Basil through the servant at North Villa, who reveals that the family is named "Sherwin" and that Mr. Sherwin "kept a large linen-draper's shop" (007-033). The servant also volunteers that Mr. Sherwin "has one daughter — Miss Margaret — who is reckoned a very fine young lady" (007-040).

Margaret's mother, Mrs. Sherwin, is a shadowy, silent, and oppressed figure — a woman broken by her marriage to a domineering husband. Her eventual denunciation of her own daughter — "Margaret Sherwin is guilty; she is no more worthy to be called your wife" (023-043) — is one of the novel's most shocking moments, a repudiation that speaks to the moral corruption the Sherwin household has bred.

### The Courtship

Basil first encounters Margaret on an omnibus. The moment is rendered through Basil's retrospective, idealizing gaze: Margaret possesses a "voluptuous languor of black eyes" (007-017), and the image of her becomes "the one reigning image of my thoughts" (007-045), impossible to dislodge. The description emphasizes the physical, almost hypnotic quality of Margaret's beauty — a beauty that Basil will later recognize as fundamentally misleading.

Basil engineers an introduction through Margaret's father. Mr. Sherwin's eager acceptance of Basil's suit is motivated by social ambition; the prospect of marrying his daughter to an aristocrat's son is an extraordinary elevation. Basil describes his intentions formally: "the object of my visit is to ask your permission to pay my addresses to Miss Sherwin" (010-031). Mr. Sherwin's commercial logic treats the marriage as a transaction — the same logic he applies to his linen-draper's business.

Margaret's response to Basil's attentions is a complex mixture of coquetry, reserve, and what may be genuine feeling. At their first private interview, Basil attempts intimacy by calling her "Margaret" rather than "Miss Sherwin," asking: "I do not think of you as Miss Sherwin, but as Margaret — are you offended?" (009-059). Margaret's reaction is unreadable — to Basil, and to the reader. Collins consistently refuses access to her consciousness, leaving her motivations opaque. Basil himself acknowledges this opacity when he reflects that he "can give no good reason" for much of what passed between them at their "fatal interview" (009-043).

### The Secret Marriage

The marriage isolates Margaret catastrophically. The secrecy demanded by Basil's fear of his father — the fear of "a degrading marriage contracted by his son (degrading in a social point of view)" (008-007) — means that Margaret cannot be acknowledged as Basil's wife in society. She is kept "out of the society of her own class" (016-030) by the need to maintain the fiction that she is still "Miss Sherwin," while unable to enter Basil's aristocratic world. The ambiguity of her position is registered in the social arrangements: anyone meeting her would "imagine her to be Miss Sherwin still" (015-037).

This social limbo breeds isolation and resentment. Margaret's position within the Sherwin household deteriorates, and her relationship with Mannion — her father's confidential clerk — develops in the shadows of her unconsummated marriage. Basil himself describes their arrangement with the telling phrase "my maiden wife" (018-010), a formulation that captures the unnatural stasis of their relationship.

### The Betrayal

The question of when and why Margaret's relationship with Mannion began is one that the novel deliberately refuses to settle. Mannion's retrospective confession to Basil in chapter 24 provides the most detailed account, but it is filtered through Mannion's manipulative perspective. He claims that Margaret was "just changing from childhood to girlhood" (024-040) when he first knew her, and that he "marked the promise of future beauty in her face and figure" (024-040). As "Margaret grew older, her beauty both of face and form approached perfection" (024-045), and Mannion — by his own account — began a calculated campaign to win her.

Mannion's account is devastating in its coldness: "What I wanted was youth and beauty; she was young and beautiful" (024-048). He claims to have been in control of Margaret's "strong passions" (024-048) from the beginning, and asserts that "Margaret Sherwin was not a pure-minded girl" (024-065) — a judgment that Basil, blinded by love, could never see. Whether this account is truthful, self-serving, or a mixture of both, the novel never definitively tells us. What is clear is that Margaret, isolated and vulnerable, became entangled with Mannion, and that this entanglement proved fatal.

The intensity of Margaret's emotional life is sometimes visible despite the narrative's refusal of interior access. When provoked, she could fly into "the fury of passion which possessed her" (016-022), a rage that suggests far more turbulent depths than Basil's idealized image of her allows. Her "large black eyes" in such moments are transformed into terrifying expressions of uncontrolled emotion.

### Death

Margaret's death is the novel's most brutal and unsentimental passage. When Basil discovers her with Mannion at a neglected hotel (019-016), Margaret's transformation is complete and horrifying:

> "her eyes glared like an idiot's; guilt and terror had made her hideous to look upon already" (019-044)

Collins refuses the conventional Victorian deathbed scene in which the fallen woman repents and is forgiven. Margaret simply dies — her inner life as opaque in death as it was in life. There is no redemption, no sentimental reconciliation, no moralizing closure. The brutality of this refusal is one of the novel's most powerful artistic choices.

Margaret is buried in a churchyard that Basil visits in the novel's concluding pages (027-001). Mannion's final taunt to Basil — "Live the free life which Margaret Sherwin has restored to you by her death" (027-014) — captures the terrible irony of her fate: she is the sacrifice on which Basil's eventual recovery is built.

## Relationships

- **[[basil]]**: Her husband. His passion for her — the "absorbing passion" (008-005) of his life — is overwhelming but fundamentally unequal. Basil falls in love with an image, a "dark beauty" (007-045), rather than with the actual woman. His famous declaration that his "love for Margaret Sherwin was worthy to be offered to the purest and perfectest woman that ever God created" (008-012) reveals the extent to which he has idealized her — and the disastrous consequences that must follow when reality intrudes.

- **Mr. Sherwin**: Her father. A linen-draper who sees his daughter's marriage as a social and commercial opportunity. He manages Margaret's courtship with the same practical calculation he applies to his shop. His ambition for her is inseparable from his ambition for himself, and his complicity in the secret marriage arrangement makes him one of the architects of her destruction.

- **[[robert-mannion]]**: Her father's confidential clerk and, ultimately, her lover. The exact nature of their relationship — love affair, seduction, coercion, or some combination of all three — is the novel's central ambiguity. Mannion's retrospective account claims to have shaped Margaret's character from girlhood, cultivating her passions while remaining in control of them (024-048, 024-060). Yet Mannion is an unreliable narrator, and the extent to which Margaret was his victim rather than his willing partner remains unresolved.

- **Mrs. Sherwin**: Her mother, a silent and oppressed presence throughout the novel. Her eventual denunciation of Margaret — pronouncing her own daughter "guilty" and "no more worthy to be called your wife" (023-043) — is one of the novel's most startling moments. It reveals both the corruption of the Sherwin household and the depth of Margaret's isolation: even her own mother condemns her.

- **[[clara]]**: Basil's sister — a relationship defined by its absence. Margaret never meets Clara, and the contrast between the two women — Clara the ideal of pure devotion, Margaret the fallen wife — structures the novel's moral universe in ways that constrain Margaret's narrative possibilities.

## Significance

Margaret Sherwin is one of Collins's most provocative and interesting female characters precisely because of her narrative opacity. Unlike the morally legible heroines of conventional Victorian fiction — the virtuous Angel in the House, or the penitent fallen woman — Margaret is irreducibly ambiguous. She is at once a victim of the class system, of her father's ambition, of Basil's idealization, and of Mannion's predation; and she is also an agent of betrayal, capable of deception and passion that the narrative deliberately refuses to explain or excuse.

Her treatment by the narrative — seen always through Basil's eyes, never given her own voice — reflects the broader Victorian problem of representing female interiority. The novel's first-person confessional structure necessarily subordinates Margaret's consciousness to Basil's, and yet this subordination is itself part of the novel's meaning. Margaret's silence is a structural feature of a narrative that cannot accommodate female subjectivity except as an object of male perception and judgment.

Modern feminist criticism has found in Margaret a figure of considerable interest. Her opacity — the way the novel systematically refuses to let her explain herself — can be read not as a failure of characterization but as a deliberate formal choice that exposes the limits of the male confessional narrative. Margaret is what the narrative cannot contain: a woman whose motivations remain outside the frame of Basil's moral understanding. Mannion's description of her as "not a pure-minded girl" (024-065) is itself a form of narrative control — an attempt to fix her in a morally legible category that the novel as a whole refuses to endorse.

Margaret also embodies the novel's critique of class. Her liminal social position — "out of the society of her own class" (016-030), unable to enter Basil's — makes her the victim of a system in which marriage across class lines is both imaginable (within the logic of romantic love) and catastrophic (within the logic of social honour). The secret marriage, with all its destructive consequences, is a figure for the impossible position of someone who tries to cross class boundaries in a society that treats such crossings as "degrading" (008-007).
