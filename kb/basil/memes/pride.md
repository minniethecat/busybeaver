# Pride

Pride is the psychological engine of _Basil_. It prevents confession, fuels concealment, and shapes the self-destructive choices that lead to the novel's catastrophe. Collins presents pride as a fundamentally ambivalent force — it can be noble, even admirable, yet it is also the primary obstacle to honesty, reconciliation, and survival.

## Definition

In _Basil_, pride operates across a spectrum from virtuous self-respect to destructive arrogance. The novel distinguishes between the father's "peculiar pride" — a quiet, personal standard of family honour — and Basil's own pride, which manifests as an inability to admit error or seek help. Pride in its various forms is the force that keeps characters silent when they should speak, and isolated when they most need connection.

## Manifestation

Basil's father is introduced through the lens of pride. "His was not that conventional pride, which the popular notions attach to rank and wealth" (003-001). Instead, his pride is described as so deeply personal that "if he had any pride, it was impossible to detect it" in ordinary social interactions (003-002). This characterisation is crucial: it establishes that the father's pride is not a vulgar social vanity but a genuine moral standard. Yet this very quality makes him unapproachable, and it is the fear of wounding this pride — of "dishonouring his family" — that prevents Basil from confiding in him.

Basil's own pride is more self-aware but no less destructive. He acknowledges that "it may be pride — miserable, useless pride — which has made me hesitate" to confess his love openly (009-043). This moment of self-knowledge is significant: Basil recognises pride as an obstacle, yet he cannot overcome it. His pride is "concerned in being able to" present his marriage as a fait accompli rather than a request for permission (013-007).

The most devastating expression of pride in the novel is its power to prevent reconciliation. Basil reflects that after the revelation of his marriage, "my pride was concerned in being able to say that I was independent" — that he could survive without his family's support (013-007). This pride-driven isolation leaves him vulnerable to Mannion's manipulation and Margaret's betrayal.

Even in the novel's darkest moments, pride persists. Basil's father's reaction to the disaster is described as a struggle between "parental affection and his family pride" (022-048). The resolution of this struggle — when "the great shock" of events overcomes the father's pride — represents one of the novel's few moments of genuine emotional breakthrough. Collins suggests that pride can be overcome, but only at catastrophic cost.

## Characters

- **Basil's Father**: Embodies a noble but rigid pride that makes him unapproachable. His pride is a moral code, not a social performance, which makes it both more admirable and more impossible to challenge.
- **Basil**: His pride manifests as an inability to seek help or admit failure. He is aware of his pride, critiques it, yet remains in its grip.
- **Mr. Sherwin**: Displays a different kind of pride — the insecure pride of the socially ambitious, quick to take offence and eager to assert status.
- **Mannion**: Appears to lack pride entirely, which makes him uniquely dangerous. His freedom from pride is part of what makes him inscrutable and threatening.

## Significance

Collins's treatment of pride in _Basil_ complicates the simple moral schema in which pride is a sin to be punished. The novel acknowledges that pride can be a form of integrity, even as it demonstrates that pride, unchecked by honesty and compassion, leads to destruction. This ambivalence is characteristic of Collins's mature fiction, which resists the moral clarity of the conventional Victorian novel.
