# The Courtship of Margaret Sherwin

The courtship of Margaret Sherwin is the extended event sequence that transforms Basil's chance omnibus encounter into a clandestine marriage. Spanning the latter half of Part I, the courtship is structured around a series of transgressions — of class boundaries, of family obligations, of the norms of honourable conduct — each of which deepens the secrecy that will eventually destroy the relationship it was meant to protect.

## Summary

After first seeing Margaret Sherwin on an omnibus and following her to her home at North Villa, Basil embarks on a systematic courtship conducted entirely in secret from his aristocratic family. He engineers an introduction through the Sherwins' servant, negotiates a clandestine engagement with Margaret's father — a linen-draper who sees the match as a social opportunity — and agrees to a year-long probationary period during which he must neither see Margaret nor communicate with her. The courtship is thus marked from its inception by secrecy, class anxiety, and the commodification of marriage: a romance conducted as a commercial negotiation, whose terms are dictated not by the lovers but by the father of the bride.

## Context

The courtship follows immediately from the first meeting on the omnibus (chapter 7), in which Basil is struck by Margaret's beauty and follows her to the suburban square where the Sherwin family lives. The social gulf between them is made explicit from the outset: Basil is the younger son of an ancient aristocratic family, while Margaret is the daughter of a linen-draper — "a linen-draper's shop — a linen-draper's daughter!" (007-035). Basil's question to himself — "Was I in love? — in love with a girl whom I had accidentally met in an omnibus?" (007-024) — establishes the tension between passion and prudence that will define the entire courtship.

Basil is aware from the beginning of the transgressive nature of his desire. He knows that "the consequences of a degrading marriage contracted by his son (degrading in regard to rank) would be terrible: fatal to his" (008-007) father's expectations and his family's honour. Yet his passion overrides these considerations: "I had known nothing of the passion, which is the absorbing passion of humanity" (008-005), he writes, and this passion, once awakened, cannot be resisted.

## The Courtship

### The Pursuit (Chapters 7–9)

Basil's courtship begins as a pursuit. After the omnibus encounter, he learns the family name — "Margaret! — I had heard her name, too. Margaret! —" (007-041) — through inquiries made of a servant. He returns to North Villa repeatedly, walking the streets of the unfinished suburban square, watching for a glimpse of Margaret. His state of mind is one of desperate fixation: "I felt that my heart's life or death was set on the hazard of the next day" (008-004).

The pursuit culminates in the garden at North Villa, where Basil engineers his first direct conversation with Margaret. He bribes the family's servant to arrange the meeting, and when "Margaret turned suddenly and looked at me" (009-042), he delivers what he later describes as "my first interview with Margaret Sherwin" (009-043). The encounter is charged with the language of class difference. Margaret's principal objection to Basil's advances is the social gulf between them: "Difference in rank — that was the only objection" (009-054). Basil attempts to overcome this objection by imagining an equality that does not exist: "if we were both living in the same rank, Margaret —" (009-057).

### The Negotiation (Chapter 10)

The courtship moves from the garden to the parlour when Basil approaches Mr. Sherwin to request his daughter's hand. This negotiation scene is one of the novel's most socially acute passages. Mr. Sherwin, the linen-draper, receives Basil in a room decorated with the "cheap editions of the classical works of ancient and modern" (015-010) — a display of cultural aspiration that Collins renders with satirical precision.

Basil's proposal is framed from the outset as a commercial transaction: "the object of my visit is connected with your daughter, with Miss Margaret Sherwin —" (010-017). The critical condition is secrecy: "to keep both the courtship and the marriage secret" (010-051). Mr. Sherwin, scenting social advantage, agrees to the arrangement but imposes terms that transform the romance into a contract. The marriage will be secret, the engagement will last a year, and during that year Basil will not see or communicate with Margaret.

### The Year of Probation (Chapters 11–12)

The year-long engagement is the courtship's defining structure — and its most psychologically destructive feature. Mr. Sherwin proposes the delay: "until another year is out — say a year from this time" (011-035). Basil, however reluctantly, accepts: "I will wait the year" (011-037). The terms are severe: "for the space of one year never to attempt to see her" (011-052). This enforced absence transforms the courtship from a romance into a trial of endurance.

The courtship's daily texture is established through Basil's evening visits to North Villa. Despite Mr. Sherwin's efforts "to make my visits to North Villa as few as possible" (013-004), Basil becomes a regular presence in the household. The evenings take on a rhythm of domestic intimacy, as described in Basil's retrospective narration:

> "Evening after evening passed away — one more happily than another" (013-009)

Yet the retrospective framing darkens this idyll. The same narrator who recalls those happy evenings immediately recoils from the memory:

> "When I look back to those evenings at North Villa, I shudder as I look" (013-013)

This double vision — the happiness of the past experienced as horror in retrospect — is characteristic of Collins's narrative technique and central to the novel's tragic irony. The courtship that felt like the beginning of life was, in fact, the beginning of destruction.

Throughout the year of probation, the class dynamics that structured the courtship from the beginning intensify. Margaret is placed in an impossible position: she is married but not acknowledged, removed from her own social world without being admitted to Basil's. As Basil acknowledges, she is "out of the society of her own class, until my year's probation was over" (016-030). The secrecy that was supposed to protect her instead isolates her, creating the conditions for the tragedy that will follow.

The courtship culminates in the secret marriage ceremony at the end of the year — "the period of the year at which on all former occasions" (012-009) the family had left London — and the wedding night that Basil describes as the darkest moment of his life: "wedded to a linen-draper's daughter! And what a marriage! What a condition weighed on it! What a probation was now to follow it!" (012-043). The courtship that began in the heat of passion ends in the cold reality of a clandestine union, and "this was my wedding-night! This was how the day" (012-055) that was to have been the culmination of his hopes became instead the inauguration of his suffering.

## Key Passages

> "I had known nothing of the passion, which is the absorbing passion of a man's life — I had known nothing of it, until now." (008-005)

Basil's declaration of his total absorption in love for Margaret establishes the emotional logic that will govern the entire courtship. The passion is "absorbing" — it consumes all other considerations, including prudence, family duty, and social judgment. This is both the courtship's romantic intensity and its fatal flaw.

> "When I look back to those evenings at North Villa, I shudder as I look. At this moment, I see the room again — the dark, badly-proportioned room, with its round table, and its eternal lamp, and its heavy, hot-smelling velvet curtains." (013-013)

The retrospective shudder is the signature gesture of Basil's narration. The physical details of North Villa — the dark room, the eternal lamp, the heavy curtains — are invested with the accumulated weight of subsequent tragedy. The courtship's domestic setting becomes, in retrospect, the scene of a crime.

## Consequences

The courtship establishes the conditions that produce the novel's catastrophe. Its defining features — secrecy, class transgression, commercial negotiation, enforced delay — each contribute to the tragedy:

- **Secrecy** isolates Basil from his family and Margaret from her social world, creating a vacuum that Mannion will eventually fill.
- **Class transgression** ensures that the marriage can never be publicly acknowledged, condemning it to permanent illegitimacy.
- **Commercial negotiation** reduces the marriage to a transaction, undermining any genuine emotional foundation it might have had.
- **Enforced delay** places Margaret in a year-long limbo during which her relationship with Mannion — always present in the North Villa household — has time to develop.

The courtship that Basil experiences as romantic passion is, in structural terms, a trap. Every element that seems to promise happiness — the secret engagement, the year of patient waiting, the eventual marriage — is in fact a mechanism of destruction. The novel's tragic irony is that Basil collaborates enthusiastically in his own undoing, mistaking the instruments of his destruction for the architecture of his happiness.

## Characters Involved

- **[[basil|Basil]]**: The suitor, whose passion for Margaret overrides every consideration of prudence, honour, and family duty. His courtship is marked by a combination of romantic ardour and social anxiety that reflects the contradictions of his position.

- **[[margaret-sherwin|Margaret Sherwin]]**: The object of Basil's courtship. Her role in the courtship is largely passive — she is pursued, negotiated over, and eventually married — but her moments of agency (her insistence on the difference in rank, her acceptance of Basil's terms) shape the course of events.

- **[[mr-sherwin|Mr. Sherwin]]**: Margaret's father, who manages the courtship as a commercial transaction. His calculation of social advantage, his imposition of the year-long delay, and his insistence on secrecy make him the architect of the courtship's destructive structure.

- **[[clara|Clara]]**: Basil's sister, who is excluded from knowledge of the courtship. Her relationship with Basil is strained by his unexplained absences and his emotional distance, establishing the pattern of family estrangement that the secrecy of the courtship produces.

## Narrative Technique

The courtship is narrated in the first person by Basil, writing retrospectively after the catastrophe has unfolded. This narrative structure creates a double perspective that is fundamental to the novel's effect. The younger Basil experiences the courtship as romantic passion; the older Basil narrates it as tragic error. The tension between these two perspectives — between the immediacy of desire and the retrospect of guilt — produces the distinctive texture of Collins's prose.

This retrospective narration also enables Collins to deploy dramatic irony systematically. Every moment of courtship happiness is shadowed by the narrator's knowledge of what will follow. When Basil describes "Evening after evening passed away — one more happily than another" (013-009), the reader who has absorbed the older Basil's tone knows that this happiness is a prelude to destruction. The courtship is narrated as a memory that the narrator cannot bear to remember — a structure that transforms the conventional romance plot into an instrument of psychological horror.

The narrative also foregrounds the physical and material details of the courtship's setting — the "badly-proportioned room," the "eternal lamp," the "heavy, hot-smelling velvet curtains" (013-013) — with a specificity that anticipates the realist novel's attention to environment as a determinant of character and fate. North Villa is not merely the backdrop to the courtship; it is an active agent in the tragedy, its oppressive domesticity mirroring the social constraints that the courtship both defies and reinforces.

## Critical Discussion

The courtship in *Basil* has been read by critics as a case study in the commodification of marriage in Victorian England. The negotiation between Basil and Mr. Sherwin — in which a daughter is traded for social advantage — literalises the marriage market metaphor that pervades nineteenth-century fiction. Where Jane Austen treats the marriage market with ironic detachment, Collins treats it with something closer to horror: the commercial logic that Mr. Sherwin applies to his daughter's marriage is shown to be destructive not merely of romantic illusion but of human life itself.

Class critics have also noted the significance of the courtship's suburban setting. North Villa, in its unfinished square of half-built houses, represents the social limbo of the lower-middle class — neither the respectable working class nor the gentry, but an aspirational zone in which the traditional markers of social identity are unstable. The courtship that unfolds in this setting is correspondingly unstable: a relationship that cannot be assimilated to any existing social category, and that must therefore be conducted in secrecy until it collapses under the weight of its own contradictions.

Recent scholarship has drawn attention to the gendered dynamics of the courtship. Margaret's near-total silence during the negotiation scenes — in which her future is decided by two men, neither of whom consults her — reflects the legal and social realities of Victorian marriage. The courtship that Basil experiences as romantic passion is, for Margaret, a transfer of ownership from father to husband. Collins's decision to narrate the courtship entirely from Basil's perspective — excluding Margaret's interiority — can be read either as a replication of Victorian patriarchal structures or as a deliberate exposure of them. The novel's refusal to give Margaret a voice of her own is, on this reading, not a failure of sympathy but a structural critique: the courtship is narrated as Basil experiences it precisely because that experience — in which Margaret is an object, not a subject — is the problem the novel anatomises.
