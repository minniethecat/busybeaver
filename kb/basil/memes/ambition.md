## Definition

Ambition in _Basil_ is presented as a force that cuts across class lines, manifesting differently but destructively in every stratum of society. For the aristocratic Basil family, ambition is the engine of political and social prestige — the father's "ambition to make a name in politics" (002-007), the eldest son raised to "inspire him with ambition; to get him to go into parliament" (004-006). For the tradesman Sherwin family, it is the desperate drive to ascend socially through strategic marriage. For Mannion, it is the "insane ambition" (024-043) of revenge. Collins traces how ambition, when untethered from moral constraint, leads inexorably to ruin.

## Manifestation

The novel opens with an anatomy of the Basil family's ambitions. Basil's father had dedicated his life to politics, and his eldest son Ralph was shaped to pursue public life. Basil's own ambitions are literary — he aspires to write a great work — but his secret marriage to Margaret Sherwin derails these plans. Reflecting on his fall, Basil laments "how much ambition, how much hope, how many of my dearest plans" (008-001) were destroyed when he first saw Margaret.

> "When I toiled in the service of ambition! Now, when I had given myself up to love" (009-049)

This opposition between ambition and love structures the novel's early chapters. Basil presents his infatuation with Margaret as a noble surrender of worldly ambition, but Collins ironizes this self-presentation: Basil's "love" is itself a form of ambition — the ambition to possess beauty, to defy his father, to live out a romantic fantasy of class-transcending passion.

The novel's most devastating portrait of ambition is Mr. Sherwin. He is "not wanting in ambition to ascend to the highest degree in the social scale" (016-030), and he pursues this ambition through his daughter's marriage to Basil. Mannion's confession reveals the full extent of Sherwin's ambition: it was Sherwin's "insane ambition on the subject of his daughter" (024-043) that made him susceptible to Mannion's manipulations. Mannion further describes Sherwin's daughter as driven by "her vulgar ambition, her reckless vanity" (024-065). This ambition is vulgar and transparent, yet Collins grants it a certain pathos — it is born of genuine paternal desire for a daughter's elevation.

Mannion's own ambition is of a different order. Where Sherwin seeks social ascent, Mannion seeks destruction. His confession traces the origin of his revenge: "from that fact sprang the ambition which all my influence, dating from that time" (024-068) was directed toward restoring — through vengeance rather than honest labour. The destruction of his father's reputation becomes the engine of Mannion's life, a dark mirror of the social ambition that drives the Sherwins.

## Characters

**Basil's Father** embodies the legitimate ambition of the aristocracy — ambition that operates within institutional channels: Parliament, property, family name. His "lofty ambition to be mentor and mistress" (004-017) of his family reflects the paternalistic order of aristocratic society. Yet the novel suggests that even legitimate ambition is corrosive — the father's absorption in political and dynastic concerns leaves him emotionally distant from his children.

**Basil** moves from worldly ambition to romantic obsession and finally, in the novel's closing letters, to a chastened form of ambition. He reflects on "that earliest and best ambition which I had abandoned for love" (025-059), and concludes with a vision of "that bravest ambition which is vigorous enough to overleap the obstructions of custom and prejudice" (031-020) — ambition redefined as moral courage rather than social striving. The novel's final pages offer ambition a qualified redemption: the hero resolves to "avenge myself with the heroes of ambition, and fight my way upward from the wreck of my early hopes" (031-016).

**Mr. Sherwin** is the novel's embodiment of middle-class ambition. His linen-draper's shop is both his reality and his shame; his daughter's marriage to an aristocrat is his escape route. Collins treats Sherwin with more complexity than the stereotype would suggest — his ambition is "insane" in its recklessness, but it is also recognizably human.

**Margaret Sherwin** internalizes her father's ambitions. Mannion notes her desire to be seen as "a new customer added to her father's shop" (024-069) — the fantasy of class transcendence made literal, a woman who wishes to be a stranger to her own origins. This detail captures the psychological cost of internalized social ambition.

**Robert Mannion** transforms ambition into pure destructiveness. His father's execution for forgery destroyed the family's social position, and Mannion's life becomes a project of vengeance that mimics the structure of legitimate ambition — it requires patience, planning, and deferred gratification — while serving no purpose beyond destruction.

## Significance

Collins's treatment of ambition in _Basil_ reflects the anxieties of a society in which traditional class hierarchies were being destabilized by industrialization, urbanization, and the rise of a wealthy middle class. The Sherwins' ambition is vulgar because it seeks to bypass the slow accumulation of cultural capital that legitimized aristocratic status — a shortcut that the novel punishes with devastating thoroughness.

Yet by making Basil's own ambitions equally destructive — his literary dreams abandoned, his family destroyed — Collins suggests that ambition itself, regardless of its class inflection, is a force that corrodes moral judgment. The novel's resolution, in which Basil commits himself to a reformed ambition of moral striving, offers an uneasy compromise: ambition cannot be eliminated from human nature, but it can be redirected from social climbing to self-improvement.

## Historical Context

_Basil_ was written during a period of intense social mobility in Britain. The Reform Act of 1832 had expanded the franchise, the Industrial Revolution had created new fortunes, and the rigid class structure of Regency England was yielding to a more fluid — and more anxious — social order. The figure of the ambitious tradesman, seeking to marry his daughter into the gentry, was a recognizable social type and a frequent target of Victorian fiction. Collins's innovation is to treat this figure not as comic but as tragic, showing how the structures of class aspiration deform everyone caught within them — the climbers and the climbed-upon alike.
