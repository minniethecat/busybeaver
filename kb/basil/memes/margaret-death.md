# Death of Margaret Sherwin

The death of Margaret Sherwin is the novel's darkest passage and the event that completes the destruction of Basil's romantic illusions. Following the discovery of her betrayal with Mannion, the death of her mother, and her flight to a London hospital, Margaret contracts typhus fever and dies in a scene of extraordinary narrative intensity — a deathbed stripped of sentimentality, redemption, or peace.

## Summary

Margaret Sherwin dies of typhus fever in a London hospital, having fled there after her mother's death. Her deathbed is attended by Basil, by the surgeon who treats her, and — grotesquely — by Mannion himself, who watches her final moments with a cold detachment that renders him "an incarnate blasphemy in the presence of the dying" (026-097). Margaret dies in delirium, calling Basil's name repeatedly in her fever-dreams ("Basil! Basil! Basil!" — 026-079), yet unable to recognise him when he stands before her. Her last words are fragmented, measured in "slow, measured cadence" (026-106), and she dies without the conventional Victorian deathbed redemption. The surgeon's pronouncement — "never to wake again. She is dead" (026-133) — is delivered with a clinical finality that underscores Collins's refusal to sentimentalise the moment.

## Context

Margaret's death occurs at the end of a catastrophic chain of events. Basil has discovered her with Mannion, a discovery that transforms her appearance: "her eyes glared like an idiot's; guilt and terror had made her hideous" (019-044). Basil collapses into a brain fever from which he barely recovers. He confesses his secret marriage to his father, and in the confrontation with Mr. Sherwin that follows, the full scope of the tragedy becomes apparent.

The immediate catalyst for Margaret's death is the death of her own mother, Mrs. Sherwin. Margaret is held responsible — "of having been the cause of her mother's death; and called down the" (026-002) maledictions of her father. In the aftermath, Margaret flees to a London hospital where she had previously volunteered, seeking both refuge and perhaps punishment. The surgeon attending her reports that "she was infected with typhus at the hospital" (026-054), having contracted "one of the worst attacks of typhus I ever remember to have seen" (026-053).

## The Event

### The Preceding Illness

Before the deathbed scene, Basil learns through his brother Ralph that Margaret has left her father's house. Ralph reports having tracked her to a London hospital. The urgency is established through the news that "the fatal illness of Mrs. Sherwin" had occurred and that Margaret "might be dying — dead for aught I knew — when he reached the hospital" (025-038).

### The Hospital Deathbed

The deathbed scene occupies the latter portion of chapter 26 and constitutes one of the most sustained passages of grim naturalism in Victorian fiction. The surgeon describes Margaret's condition with clinical precision: she is suffering from typhus, and "the delirium, an invariable result of typhus, is particularly marked in her case" (026-055). The physical details are unsparing: "the smouldering fever in her cheeks; the glare of the" (026-083) eyes, the "hideous clutching of the outstretched" (026-083) hands.

Mannion's presence at the deathbed is the scene's most disturbing element. He is described as "leering and scowling by the bedside, an incarnate blasphemy in the presence of the dying" (026-097). His cold observation of Margaret's final moments — "listening for her last words; watching his opportunity" (026-119) — converts the deathbed from a sacred space into a theatre of psychological warfare.

### Margaret's Final Words

Margaret's delirium is punctuated by moments of terrible lucidity. She calls Basil's name repeatedly — "calling my name incessantly in her fever-dream: 'Basil! Basil! Basil!'" (026-079) — yet when Basil approaches and speaks to her, she does not recognise him. Her fragmented consciousness produces utterances that hover between accusation and prayer: "pray that I may be struck dead in it, and Robert too" (026-105). The surgeon notes that her disclosures, "unconsciously disclosed on the deathbed, are secrets sacred" (026-076) — a judgment that acknowledges the moral gravity of what the dying woman reveals while refusing to exploit it for narrative sentiment.

> "She uttered the last words in slow, measured cadence — the" (026-106)

Margaret's final words resist the consolations of conventional Victorian deathbed literature. There is no repentance, no reconciliation, no peace. The surgeon's pronouncement — "never to wake again. She is dead" (026-133) — is the novel's definitive closure on Margaret's story, delivered without ornament or consolation.

### The Aftermath

The morning after Margaret's death, Basil describes standing alone: "from the morning when she had died, I stood alone in the churchyard" (027-001). The funeral imagery returns in Margaret's own fevered words, which transform the wedding ceremony into its dark inversion: "we are going to the funeral of our wedding" (026-093). The secret marriage that began the tragedy is symbolically buried with Margaret's corpse.

## Key Passages

> "She was calling my name incessantly in her fever-dream: 'Basil! Basil! Basil!'" (026-079)

> "We are going to the funeral of our wedding. Come, Basil! let's go back to the church — the church where we were married, you know — and then, we shall see the end of it all." (026-093)

These two passages exemplify the distinctive character of Margaret's death. The first captures the central agony of her delirium: even in fever, her consciousness is fixed on Basil, whose name she repeats obsessively even as she fails to recognise him. The second transforms the novel's central event (the secret marriage) into a funeral rite, collapsing the categories of wedding and burial into a single image of terminal consequence.

## Consequences

Margaret's death completes the novel's tragic arc. It extinguishes the possibility of reconciliation between Basil and his wife, transforming his passion into an object of pure grief. The death also liberates Mannion from any remaining restraint: his campaign against Basil intensifies after Margaret's death, as she is no longer an obstacle to his revenge.

For Basil, the death is both an end and a beginning. His marriage — the secret structure that governed his life — is dissolved. But the guilt he carries is deepened rather than relieved. Margaret dies without forgiving him, without being forgiven, without the narrative closure that Victorian convention demanded. Collins's refusal to provide a redemptive deathbed scene is one of the novel's most radical gestures, placing *Basil* in a tradition of unconsoled realism that anticipates the darker strains of sensation fiction and, eventually, the naturalist novel.

## Characters Involved

- **[[basil|Basil]]**: Margaret's secret husband, who attends her deathbed. His presence at the scene is both an act of devotion and a source of agony, as Margaret's delirium both calls for him and fails to recognise him.

- **[[margaret-sherwin|Margaret Sherwin]]**: The dying woman. Her death is the culmination of a trajectory from beauty and desirability through betrayal to guilt, illness, and finally an unconsoled end.

- **[[robert-mannion|Robert Mannion]]**: Present at the deathbed as a grotesque spectator. His cold observation of Margaret's final moments transforms the scene into an occasion of psychological horror.

- **[[margaret-mother|Mrs. Sherwin]]**: Though already deceased, her death is the psychological catalyst for Margaret's illness. Margaret's guilt over her mother's death — "Once more, her mother's last words of earthly lament" (026-063) — haunts her delirium.

- **[[ralph|Ralph]]**: Basil's brother, who brings the news of Margaret's presence at the hospital and facilitates Basil's visit to her deathbed.

- **[[the-surgeon|The Surgeon]]**: The medical attendant who treats Margaret, provides the clinical account of her typhus, and delivers the pronouncement of death. His presence grounds the scene in medical realism.

## Narrative Technique

Collins's narration of Margaret's death is notable for its rejection of the conventions of Victorian deathbed literature. In the typical Victorian deathbed scene — familiar from Dickens, Gaskell, and countless lesser novelists — the dying character achieves a moment of moral clarity, repents, reconciles with loved ones, and dies in peace. Collins systematically refuses every element of this convention.

Margaret does not repent. She does not reconcile with Basil (she cannot recognise him). She does not die in peace (her death is agitated, fevered, haunted). And she is not given the privacy of a domestic deathbed; instead, she dies in a public hospital, watched by her husband's enemy. The deathbed, traditionally a space of spiritual meaning, becomes instead a site of psychological horror and clinical observation.

The first-person narration intensifies the effect. Everything is filtered through Basil's consciousness — his grief, his guilt, his sense of Mannion's malevolent presence. The reader is denied access to Margaret's interiority even at the moment of death. As throughout the novel, Margaret remains opaque: seen, described, interpreted, but never truly known.

## Critical Discussion

Margaret Sherwin's death has attracted considerable critical attention as one of the earliest examples in English fiction of what might be called the "unconsoled death" — a death that refuses the moral and emotional resolutions that nineteenth-century readers had been trained to expect. Tamar Heller, in *Dead Secrets: Wilkie Collins and the Female Gothic* (1992), reads Margaret's death as part of Collins's broader critique of Victorian ideologies of feminine virtue and redemption. By denying Margaret a redemptive death, Heller argues, Collins exposes the violence that conventional narrative forms inflict on female characters who transgress sexual norms.

More recently, scholars of the medical humanities have examined the typhus deathbed scene as a document in the history of clinical observation. The surgeon's detailed description of Margaret's symptoms — "the delirium, an invariable result of typhus" (026-055) — reflects the emerging medical discourse of the mid-nineteenth century, in which the physician's authority increasingly competed with the priest's at the deathbed. Collins, who trained briefly as a medical student, renders this competition with unusual precision: the surgeon's clinical language coexists uneasily with the moral vocabulary of guilt, sin, and forgiveness that the scene simultaneously invokes and refuses.

The death scene also functions as a dark counterpart to the novel's wedding scene. Margaret's fevered reference to "the funeral of our wedding" (026-093) explicitly links the two ceremonies, suggesting that the secret marriage was always already a form of death — the death of Basil's relationship with his family, the death of Margaret's social position, and ultimately the death of romantic illusion itself.
