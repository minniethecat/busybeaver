# Passion

Passion is the catalyst of _Basil_. It is the force that drives Basil to cross class boundaries, contract a secret marriage, and set in motion the chain of events that leads to catastrophe. Collins presents passion not as a romantic ideal but as a dangerous, potentially degrading force that overwhelms reason and moral judgment.

## Definition

In _Basil_, passion is the experience of being overtaken by emotion to the point where rational judgment and social obligation lose their hold. Basil describes it as "the absorbing passion of a man's life" (008-005) — not one emotion among many but the single force that reorganises his entire existence around its object. The novel traces the trajectory of passion from its exhilarating beginning through its "deteriorating" effects to its catastrophic end.

## Manifestation

Basil's passion for Margaret is presented from the start as something that overwhelms rather than enriches him. He admits that "I had known nothing of the passion" before encountering Margaret (008-005), and this inexperience makes him vulnerable. His passion is not tempered by judgment or experience; it is a force that possesses him entirely.

Collins is careful to show that passion has social consequences. Basil acknowledges that his passion has a "deteriorating" effect "on the exercise of every honourable energy of my mind" (008-008). Passion does not merely add emotion to Basil's life — it degrades his character, making him capable of deceptions and compromises that his pre-passionate self would have rejected.

Margaret herself becomes aware of "the passion with which she had inspired me" (008-010), and this knowledge gives her a power over Basil that she — or Mannion through her — exploits. Passion thus creates not only emotional vulnerability but practical vulnerability: the passionate person can be manipulated.

The novel's treatment of passion culminates in the question of whether it can coexist with genuine love. The contrast between Basil's passion for Margaret (destructive, degrading, ultimately fatal) and Clara's steady devotion to her brother suggests that Collins distinguishes between passion as a destabilising force and love as a sustaining one.

## Characters

- **Basil**: The passionate protagonist whose surrender to romantic obsession sets the entire plot in motion. His passion is both his driving force and his fatal flaw.
- **Margaret Sherwin**: The object of Basil's passion, whose own emotional life remains opaque. The asymmetry of their passion is part of the tragedy.
- **Mr. Sherwin**: Exploits Basil's passion strategically, recognising that a man in love is a man who can be managed.

## Significance

Collins's treatment of passion in _Basil_ participates in the Victorian debate about the place of strong emotion in moral life. By showing passion as a force that degrades rather than elevates, the novel challenges the Romantic idealisation of passionate love. At the same time, Collins does not condemn passion outright — he presents it as a natural and powerful human experience whose danger lies not in its existence but in the social conditions (class barriers, family secrecy) that force it into destructive channels.
