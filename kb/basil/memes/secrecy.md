# Secrecy

Secrecy is the engine of the plot in _Basil_. From the clandestine courtship to the hidden marriage and Mannion's concealed past, the novel is structured around knowledge that is withheld from those who most need to know it.

## Definition

In _Basil_, secrecy operates as both a narrative device and a thematic concern. As a narrative mechanism, it generates suspense and drives the plot toward its catastrophic revelations. As a theme, it explores the psychological and moral consequences of concealment — how secrets deform relationships, isolate individuals, and create the conditions for tragedy.

## Manifestation

The novel's central secret is Basil's marriage to Margaret Sherwin. From the beginning, the relationship is conducted in shadow: the suburban North Villa is chosen precisely because there is "no look of shadow, shelter, secrecy, or retirement in any one nook or corner of it" — an ironic description, given the clandestine purpose it serves (010-002). The courtship is conducted with "the strictest secrecy of course" (010-015), and the marriage itself is kept hidden from Basil's family.

Basil himself reflects on "the concealment and secrecy, under the oppression of which I suffered" (012-025), acknowledging that the burden of secrecy falls most heavily on the secret-keeper. The secrecy that began as a practical necessity — to buy time before revealing the marriage to his father — becomes a permanent condition that isolates Basil from his family and poisons his relationship with Margaret.

Secrecy radiates outward from the central concealment. Mannion's "peculiar habits" and "secrecy in relation to his early life" make him an opaque and threatening figure (016-028). The Sherwin household itself is characterised by "the same secrecy which distinguished everything" (018-011). Secrecy is not merely a plot device but the atmosphere the characters inhabit.

The novel's epistolary conclusion — the Letters in Conclusion — represents the breaking of secrecy, as characters finally speak openly about what has been hidden. Yet the damage done by prolonged concealment is irreversible.

## Characters

- **Basil**: The primary secret-keeper, whose concealment of his marriage creates the conditions for tragedy. His secrecy is motivated by fear of his father's reaction but becomes a self-perpetuating state.
- **Margaret Sherwin**: Both an object of Basil's secrecy and, through her relationship with Mannion, a keeper of her own devastating secrets.
- **Mannion**: The novel's most enigmatic figure, whose hidden past and true motives are revealed only gradually. His secrecy is active and weaponised.
- **Mr. Sherwin**: Complicit in the secret marriage, motivated by social ambition for his daughter.

## Significance

Secrecy in _Basil_ serves Collins's broader critique of Victorian social hypocrisy. The novel suggests that the rigid codes of class and honour that make honesty impossible are themselves responsible for the culture of secrecy they condemn. Basil's tragedy is not that he keeps a secret, but that the social order gives him no honourable way to be honest.
