# Revenge

Revenge is the engine of the novel's second half. Once Mannion's secret relationship with Margaret is exposed and the marriage collapses, the narrative shifts from a story of concealed love to one of relentless pursuit, driven by Mannion's "desire of revenge" (024-066) against the man who has wronged him.

## Definition

In _Basil_, revenge is not presented as a momentary impulse but as a systematically pursued project. Mannion's revenge is the novel's darkest psychological portrait — a cold, calculated campaign of destruction that reveals the capacity for sustained malevolence that can exist behind a controlled exterior. Collins uses revenge to explore the question of what happens when intelligence and patience are placed in the service of pure malice.

## Manifestation

Mannion's revenge is introduced gradually. Early hints of his capacity for sustained resentment accumulate until, in the novel's final act, he reveals the full scope of his project. He explains that his persecution of Basil "simply springs from the desire of revenge" (024-066), and describes how he "first thoroughly tested" Basil's vulnerabilities before striking. This is not impulsive vengeance but strategic destruction.

The most chilling aspect of Mannion's revenge is its apparent rationality. When a confederate asks "will revenge put a halfpenny into your pocket?" (025-077), the question highlights the economic irrationality of revenge — it costs more than it yields. Mannion's reply — that revenge is "the only occupation" left to him (024-078) — reveals that revenge has become his identity. He is defined entirely by his hatred of Basil.

Collins frames this revenge within the language of madness. Mannion's persistence is described as "the very madness of malignity and revenge" (024-078), suggesting that sustained revenge-seeking is itself a form of psychological derangement. The novel thus presents revenge not as justice or even as comprehensible passion, but as a species of insanity — a self-consuming obsession that destroys the avenger as surely as the target.

## Characters

- **Mannion**: The novel's agent of revenge, whose entire existence in the second half of the novel is organised around the destruction of Basil. His revenge is cold, patient, and total.
- **Basil**: The target of Mannion's revenge, whose initial wrong (exposing Mannion's relationship with Margaret) sets the revenge in motion. Basil is both victim and, in Mannion's eyes, the original aggressor.
- **Margaret Sherwin**: Her death removes her from Mannion's revenge calculus, but her betrayal of Basil is the event that triggers the revenge narrative.

## Significance

Revenge in _Basil_ serves as a dark counterpoint to the novel's treatment of honour and justice. Where honour demands measured response and justice requires impartiality, revenge is pure, asymmetrical destruction. Collins's portrayal of Mannion as a figure of almost supernatural persistence and malignity influenced later Victorian treatments of the revenge theme, including Collins's own Count Fosco in _The Woman in White_.
