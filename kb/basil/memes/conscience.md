## Definition

In *Basil*, conscience is the internal tribunal before which characters — above all Basil himself — stand perpetually accused. Collins presents conscience not as a rational faculty of moral deliberation but as an involuntary, affective force: it "sears," "accuses," and "brands." This conception draws on the Victorian inheritance of Evangelical inwardness, in which conscience is the voice of God within the soul, but Collins strips it of its redemptive dimension. Conscience in the novel punishes but does not purify; it is a mechanism of torment, not a path to salvation.

## Manifestation

Basil's conscience is active from the earliest stages of his secret marriage. Even as he resolves to proceed with the clandestine courtship, he recognizes that he cannot "feel secure, in my own conscience, that I should not return the friendship offered to me" (008-011). The phrase "in my own conscience" locates the source of moral knowledge within the self, yet the knowledge conscience provides is consistently unwelcome: it tells Basil what he does not wish to hear.

The tension between conscience and desire structures Basil's entire experience of the courtship. He confesses to himself a fundamental dishonesty:

> "My conscience was giving the lie all the while to what I was saying" (010-056)

This formulation dramatizes the split between the desiring self and the judging self — a split that the first-person narrative form makes structurally visible. After the marriage, this internal division intensifies:

> "Every syllable of it seemed to sear my conscience; to brand Hypocrite on my heart" (012-015)

The physical metaphors — searing, branding — present conscience as a form of violence done to the self by the self. The capitalization of "Hypocrite" transforms the moral judgment into something almost tangible, a mark that cannot be erased.

Mannion's confession letter offers a counterpoint. He addresses Basil with knowing cynicism, "suspecting how his uneasy conscience was accusing him" (023-056), and offers strategic advice: "silence any uneasy upbraidings of conscience, should you feel them, by saying that her crime deserved to be visited on her" (024-006). Mannion understands conscience as a weakness to be managed rather than a guide to be followed, representing it as something that can be strategically suppressed. This instrumentalization of conscience marks Mannion as the novel's true moral void.

After the catastrophe, conscience becomes fused with memory: "My conscience taught me only too easily and too quickly how to look back" (017-002). The retrospect that constitutes the novel itself — Basil's written confession — is thus a product of conscience, which compels the telling of what cannot be untold. The guilty conscience also distorts perception: "Perhaps my guilty conscience deceived me, but I thought he looked at me with a glance of suspicion" (021-043), revealing conscience as not merely a judge but an active transformer of experience.

## Characters

**Basil** is the character most fully governed — and most fully tortured — by conscience. His entire narrative is an act of conscience-driven confession, and his retrospective self-judgment is harsher than any external verdict could be. His conscience is active from the earliest deception (008-011) through the catastrophe to the final act of writing.

**Basil's Father** represents an externalized form of conscience. His rigid moral code, his intolerance of deviation, and his eventual forgiveness all function as a social correlative of the internal tribunal that already judges Basil from within. His warning that only "a crime" can silence conscience (027-014) acknowledges conscience as the ultimate deterrent.

**Robert Mannion** is defined by his apparent freedom from conscience. His ability to rationalize, plan, and execute his revenge without evident moral compunction makes him the novel's most disturbing figure — he demonstrates what a human being becomes when the internal tribunal is silenced.

## Significance

Conscience in *Basil* performs a crucial structural function: it converts external plot events into internal psychological drama. The novel's sensational elements (secret marriage, adultery, forgery, attempted murder) are filtered through a narrator whose conscience transforms them from melodramatic incidents into sustained examinations of guilt and responsibility. This interiorization of plot through conscience is one of the features that distinguishes *Basil* from simpler sensation fiction and aligns it with the psychological novel.

The treatment of conscience also illuminates the novel's ambivalent relationship to Victorian morality. On one hand, conscience is presented as inescapable — it always "accuses," and its judgments are always correct. On the other hand, conscience in the novel produces only suffering, never reform. Basil's conscience torments him but does not make him better; it is a punitive rather than a rehabilitative faculty. This bleak view of conscience — as an instrument of torture rather than a guide to virtue — is one of the novel's most quietly subversive features.

## Historical Context

The Victorian period saw intense contestation over the nature and authority of conscience. Evangelical theology emphasized conscience as the direct voice of God, while secularizing currents (from Utilitarianism to evolutionary psychology) sought to naturalize it as a product of social conditioning. John Stuart Mill's *On Liberty* (1859) would argue for the sovereignty of individual conscience against social coercion, while Cardinal Newman's *Apologia Pro Vita Sua* (1864) would trace the workings of conscience as the engine of religious conversion. Collins, writing *Basil* in 1852, participates in this wider discourse by presenting conscience as a real and powerful psychological force while remaining agnostic about its transcendent source — his conscience is immanent, secular, and punishingly effective.
