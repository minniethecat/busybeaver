# Despair

Despair is the emotional terrain on which most of _Basil_ is lived. From the desolate frame narrative on the Cornish coast to the catastrophic discovery of betrayal, from the fevered visions of delirium to the deathbed of Margaret Sherwin, the novel traces despair as a condition that engulfs, transforms, and -- in rare moments -- relents. Collins does not treat despair as mere plot device or emotional colouring; it is the psychological subject of the novel, as central to its meaning as the moral questions of guilt and confession.

## Definition

In _Basil_, despair is not simply unhappiness or grief. It is the state of having lost all grounds for hope -- the emotional equivalent of the moral condition of guilt. The novel distinguishes among several varieties of despair: the despair of the frame narrator, writing in solitude with no expectation that his life will improve; the acute despair of catastrophic discovery, when the world collapses in an instant; the despair of illness and delirium, when the mind loses its grip on reality; and the chronic despair of living under an unremovable threat. Each form is rendered with a psychological specificity that moves beyond Victorian convention toward something closer to clinical observation.

## Manifestation

### The Frame Narrative: Despair as Permanent Condition

The novel's narrative frame establishes despair as Basil's baseline emotional state. Writing from "the far hills of the far West of England, surrounded only by the few simple inhabitants of a fishing hamlet on the Cornish coast" (001-005), Basil completes his twenty-fourth year without "a single kind word, or a single loving wish" (001-006). The setting -- a remote Cornish cottage, the "lovely morning look of nature" seen from a casement (001-006) -- is the landscape of someone who has withdrawn from life, not someone building toward a future. The narrative itself is an occupation undertaken in the shadow of Mannion's threat: "I live under a threat of impending hostility, which may descend and overwhelm me, I know not how soon" (001-006).

Even in this extremity, a fragile hope persists through the thought of Clara. Basil writes: "Even in my worst moments of despair, I can still remember that Clara is thinking of me and sorrowing for me: I can still feel that remembrance, as an invisible hand of mercy which supports me, sinking; which raises me, fallen" (005-015). The syntax of this sentence -- the halting, clause-by-clause construction -- enacts the struggle between despair and the slender resources that resist it. Clara's remembered presence is not enough to dispel despair but is enough to make it bearable.

### The Despair of Discovery

The novel's central catastrophe -- Basil's discovery of Margaret's betrayal -- is rendered as a sequence of emotional shocks that culminate in despair. When Basil first sees Margaret and Mannion leaving the party together, "a strange sensation stole over me: I seemed to be speaking as the mere mouthpiece of some other voice" (019-013). This dissociation precedes the full recognition of what he is about to discover. The moment of confirmation is physical before it is cognitive: "One long pang of shame and despair shot through my heart as I looked at her, and tortured out of its trance the spirit within me" (019-043). Despair arrives as a bodily event -- a pang, a shot -- that momentarily restores consciousness only to flood it with unbearable knowledge.

The aftermath of the attack on Mannion leaves Basil in a state that blurs the boundary between despair and madness. Wandering the London streets, he passes two men; one says he is drunk, the other replies: "he was not drunk, but mad -- he had seen my face as I passed under a gas-lamp, and he knew that I was mad" (019-047). The fear of madness becomes itself a kind of despair: "a fear which, to the man who suffers it, is worse even than the fear of death" (019-048).

### Delirium and the Visions of Despair

During his prolonged illness, Basil's despair takes the form of fevered visions. The most elaborate is a dream of a "City of Palaces" populated by "giant phantoms mustered by millions," a vision that culminates in the apparition of "Margaret and Mannion" as "fiend-souls made visible in fiend-shapes" (020-014), and finally the sight of his father and sister dead among ruins (020-016). This is "the eternity of Hell" (020-016) -- despair literalised as damnation. When Basil finally awakens and weeps in Clara's arms, the release is also a reckoning: "the memory of the ruin and the shame that had overwhelmed me; the memory of my love that had become an infamy; and of my brief year's hope miserably fulfilled by a life of despair, swelled darkly over my heart" (020-022).

### Despair as Resignation

Following his banishment from home, Basil's despair shifts from acute agony to something more passive and perhaps more dangerous -- the resignation of one who has ceased to struggle. Reading Ralph's letter about Margaret's disappearance, he reflects: "A superstitious conviction that my actions were governed by a fatality which no human foresight could alter or avoid, began to strengthen within me. From this time forth, I awaited events with the uninquiring patience, the helpless resignation of despair" (026-003).

This fatalistic despair is precisely the condition that Mannion hopes to induce. Mannion's letter warns Basil against "seeking a false hope in the belief that my faculties are shaken, and that my resolves are visionary -- false, because such a hope is only despair in disguise" (024-079). Mannion understands that hope can be a form of self-deception, and that the collapse of false hope only deepens despair. His strategy is to keep Basil suspended between the two states, never quite without hope but never secure in it.

### The Deathbed: Despair and Repentance

The novel's most concentrated exploration of despair occurs at Margaret Sherwin's deathbed. Her initial state is one of "the wild despair of the soul wrestling awfully with the writhing weakness of the body" (026-123), expressed in "words and cries horrible, beyond all imagination, to hear" (026-123). The scene is unsparing: "when the wild words of her despair grew fewer and fainter" (026-124), what follows is not peace but silence.

Yet this despair is not the final word. When Basil weeps at her bedside, he records: "tears that did not humiliate me; for I knew, while I shed them, that I had forgiven her!" (026-123). The movement from despair to forgiveness passes through tears -- a physical release that momentarily transcends the emotional paralysis that despair imposes. The chapter in which this occurs, along with the discovery chapter, constitutes the novel's most intensive rendering of despair as a state that can be witnessed, shared, and -- however fleetingly -- overcome.

### The Chronic Despair of Exile

In the journal that forms the final portion of the narrative, despair becomes a daily condition. Basil records: "I tried in despair, today, to write to Ralph; but knew not how to word the letter" (028-070). The isolation of the Cornish coast, the constant dread of Mannion's reappearance, and the loss of home and family combine into a despair that is less dramatic than the deathbed scene but, in its quiet persistence, perhaps more devastating. Basil's confession that "his terrible connection with all my sufferings of the past, makes me attach credit too easily to the destroying power which he arrogates to himself in the future" (028-011) acknowledges that despair has become self-perpetuating -- the past suffering feeds the present fear, which feeds the despair, in a cycle that resists breaking.

### The Movement Toward Endurance

The novel does not end in despair, though neither does it end in triumph. In his final letter, Basil reflects that "Past suffering may have changed, but it has not deteriorated me. It has fortified my spirit with an abiding strength" (031-020). This is not the conventional happy ending of Victorian fiction but something more qualified -- the discovery that despair, endured long enough, can transform into a kind of fortitude. The novel's closing image -- Clara's voice calling Basil to "come out and look at the sunset over the distant sea" (031-021) -- suggests not happiness but a return to the ordinary, the simple pleasure of a shared evening that despair had seemed to foreclose forever.

## Characters

- **Basil**: The novel's primary vessel for despair. His narrative moves through the acute despair of betrayal, the fevered despair of illness, the resigned despair of banishment, and the chronic despair of exile before arriving at a state of hard-won endurance.
- **Margaret Sherwin**: Her deathbed despair is rendered with clinical precision -- the wild cries, the physical writhing, the pleading to be left alone to die -- and represents the novel's most extreme depiction of the state.
- **Clara**: The counterweight to despair, whose remembered presence sustains Basil "even in my worst moments of despair" (005-015). She is the figure through whom hope persists, even when it cannot be acted upon.

## Significance

Collins's treatment of despair in _Basil_ is remarkable for its psychological specificity. Rather than treating despair as a simple emotion to be overcome, the novel maps its varieties -- acute, chronic, fevered, resigned -- with an attention to the texture of mental suffering that anticipates the psychological novel of the later nineteenth century. Despair in _Basil_ is not a moral failure but a psychological fact, and the movement from despair to endurance is not a moral victory but a survival strategy.

The novel's handling of despair also complicates its treatment of guilt and sin. If guilt is the moral condition and confession the ritual of acknowledgment, despair is the emotional condition from which neither guilt nor confession can fully deliver. Basil confesses, and is not forgiven; he endures despair, and is not cured. The novel's honesty about this -- its refusal of the consolations that Victorian fiction conventionally provided -- is one of the qualities that makes it a sensation novel rather than a moral tale.

The scholar Nicholas Rance, in _Wilkie Collins and Other Sensation Novelists_ (1991), identifies despair as a defining feature of Collins's protagonists, who "inhabit a world where Providence has been replaced by plot, and where the consolations of religion no longer operate." In _Basil_, this theological void is filled by the psychological -- despair becomes not a sin against hope but the natural consequence of irreparable loss, and the narrative traces not a conversion from despair to faith but a slow habituation to a life in which despair must be daily managed rather than definitively overcome.

## Historical Context

Victorian culture had a complex relationship with despair. Evangelical Christianity regarded despair as a spiritual danger -- the sin against the Holy Ghost, the refusal of hope that amounted to a refusal of faith. At the same time, the Romantic inheritance had valorised melancholy and sorrow as signs of spiritual depth. Collins's _Basil_ occupies an interesting position between these traditions. The novel's despair is neither condemned as sin nor celebrated as sensibility; it is simply rendered as a psychological reality that must be endured.

The sensation novel as a genre was particularly drawn to extreme emotional states, and despair was among the most useful for generating the "preternatural" atmosphere that the genre required. But _Basil_ goes further than many sensation novels in exploring not just the dramatic moments of despair but its quieter, more corrosive forms -- the despair of daily life under threat, the despair of knowing that what has been lost cannot be recovered.

## Comparison

The descent into despair and the slow, partial recovery that _Basil_ traces would become a recurring pattern in Collins's fiction. In _The Woman in White_ (1859--60), Walter Hartright endures a period of despair after Laura Fairlie's seeming death, wandering the wilds of Central America in a state of emotional numbness that echoes Basil's Cornish exile. Laura herself, in her broken condition after the asylum, represents despair made physically manifest -- a living ghost who must be gradually recalled to life. In _Armadale_ (1866), the character of Midwinter wrestles with a fatalistic despair inherited from his father's crime, a despair that, like Basil's, is sharpened by the conviction that his fate is predetermined and inescapable.

Across these works, Collins returns to the question that _Basil_ first posed: what happens to a person after the worst has already occurred? The answer, in each case, is not redemption but survival -- not the restoration of what was lost but the slow, unglamorous work of learning to live without it.
