# Clara

Clara is Basil's younger sister and the moral centre of the novel. In a narrative dominated by secrecy, passion, and betrayal, Clara represents the redemptive power of familial love — a love that is steady, selfless, and unconditional. Collins himself marks her importance in the most personal terms, pausing the narrative to acknowledge that "wherever I have but casually mentioned my sister, the pen has trembled and stood still" (004-019).

## Character Portrait

Clara is introduced through a sustained character sketch that is unique in the novel for its warmth and sincerity. "My sister Clara is four years younger than I am" (005-001), Basil begins, before launching into a description that blends affection with moral admiration:

> "The greatest charms that my sister has on the surface, come from beneath it." (005-002)

The distinction is crucial to Collins's characterisation. Clara's virtue is not a matter of surface propriety but of deep-seated goodness — "the strong and deep feelings of my sister's nature lay far below the surface — for a woman, too far below it" (005-012). She possesses what Basil describes as "the influence thus possessed, and unconsciously possessed, by my sister over everyone with whom she came in contact" (005-003), a natural moral authority that operates without consciousness of its own power.

Clara's portrait is implicitly contrasted with the fashionable women of Basil's London acquaintance: "To women such as these, my sister Clara presented as complete a contrast as could well be conceived" (005-005). Where they are artificial, she is natural; where they perform virtue, she embodies it.

## Role in the Narrative

Clara functions as the novel's moral exemplar, but she is not a didactic figure. Collins presents her goodness not as a lesson but as a fact — she simply is what Basil is not: honest, constant, and immune to the passions that destroy him. Her role in the narrative is to demonstrate that love need not be destructive, that devotion need not be obsessive, and that family loyalty can survive even the most catastrophic breaches of trust.

The contrast between Clara and Margaret is implicit but significant. Where Margaret is opaque, seen only through Basil's desiring and then disillusioned gaze, Clara is transparent — her love for Basil is direct, uncomplicated, and fully legible. This contrast is not merely personal but structural: Clara represents the domestic ideal that Basil abandons for the risky world of cross-class romance, and to which he returns — damaged but not destroyed — in the novel's final pages.

## Key Scenes

### The Confrontation Scene

Clara's most dramatic moment occurs during the confrontation between Basil and his father after the catastrophe. Entering the room "slowly and noiselessly" (022-075), she interrupts a scene of violent paternal fury:

> "The silence and suddenness of her entrance at such a moment; the look of terror which changed to unnatural vacancy the wonted gentle expression of her eyes..." (022-076)

She defies her father's command to leave Basil, and when her father commands "Clara, I command you, leave him!" (022-095), she refuses. The scene culminates in a tableau of reconciliation: "Clara was in my father's arms, her head lay on his shoulder, her face was as still in its heavenly calmness as if the world..." (022-097). In this moment, Clara's love bridges the rupture between father and son — she becomes the instrument of the family's survival.

### The Delirium Vigil

During Basil's brain fever, Clara nurses him through "your delirium at its worst" (022-036). She hears the words he speaks in his fevered state — the involuntary confession of his secret life — and emerges from the vigil not with recrimination but with deepened devotion. Her role as nurse and protector makes her privy to Basil's most abject moments, yet her love remains unchanged.

## Relationships

- **[[basil]]**: Her brother and the primary object of her devotion. Her love for him is the novel's moral constant — unchanged by his disgrace, undiminished by his failures. Even in his darkest moments, Basil can write: "Even in my worst moments of despair, I can still remember that Clara is thinking of me and sorrowing for me" (005-015).
- **[[basil-father]]**: As his daughter, Clara shares in the family's moral code, but she embodies it differently — with warmth rather than rigidity, with love rather than pride. Her defiance of his command to leave Basil is one of the novel's most powerful assertions of moral independence.
- **[[ralph]]**: Her other brother, whose "awkward love adventure" with a tenant's daughter may have given Clara early insight into the dangers of cross-class romance.

## Significance

Clara represents Collins's engagement with the Victorian ideal of domestic womanhood — but with an important twist. Unlike the passive, decorative heroines of much Victorian fiction, Clara is an active moral force. Her love is not merely felt but enacted: she sustains Basil through his disgrace, she defies her father to protect her brother, and her letters provide the novel's final, steadying perspective.

In a novel that is otherwise deeply pessimistic about human relationships — the marriage to Margaret is a catastrophe, the bond with Mannion is destructive, the relationship with Mr. Sherwin is transactional — Clara is the single point of unqualified affirmation. She is what Basil returns to, what he holds onto, and ultimately what makes his survival meaningful. Collins knew what he was doing when he paused the narrative to say: "wherever I have but casually mentioned my sister, the pen has trembled and stood still" (004-019).

## Appearances

- **Part I, Chapters IV–V** (PN 004-007 to 005-015): Introduction and character portrait — the novel's most sustained passage of affection
- **Part II**: Background presence in the family, the standard against which Margaret is implicitly measured
- **Part III, Chapters XXII–XXIII** (PN 022-075 to 022-097): The confrontation scene — Clara's defiance of her father, the tableau of reconciliation
- **Letters in Conclusion**: Direct voice through her letters; emotional resolution of the novel
