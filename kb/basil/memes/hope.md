# Hope

Hope is the emotional counterpoint to fear in _Basil_. If fear drives concealment, hope drives action — the hope that the secret marriage can be sustained, that the father will eventually accept it, that love can overcome class barriers. The novel's tragedy is, in part, the systematic destruction of hope: each hope Basil entertains is, in turn, revealed as delusion.

## Definition

In _Basil_, hope is an ambiguous force. It is what enables Basil to act — to pursue Margaret, to contract the secret marriage, to maintain the deception — but it is also what blinds him to reality. Collins presents hope not as a virtue but as a psychological mechanism that, when disconnected from reality, becomes a form of self-deception. The novel traces the arc from hopeful expectation through disillusionment to despair.

## Manifestation

Basil's hopes structure the novel's emotional trajectory. He hopes that his father will eventually accept Margaret; he hopes that the secret marriage can be sustained until the right moment for disclosure; he hopes that Mannion is a reliable ally rather than a hidden enemy. Each of these hopes is systematically destroyed, and the destruction of hope is presented as more devastating than the destruction of any particular plan.

The novel's most bitter meditation on hope comes late, when Basil reflects that "it was madness to put a moment's trust" in the hope of reconciliation or recovery (022-002). Hope here is not merely disappointed but pathologised — it becomes a form of madness, a refusal to accept reality. This is the novel's darkest psychological insight: that hope, when it outlasts all reasonable grounds, is not a virtue but a symptom.

Yet the novel does not end in absolute despair. Clara represents a hope that survives catastrophe — not hope for restoration (Basil can never undo what he has done) but hope for continuance. The Letters in Conclusion suggest that life goes on, that affection endures, that survival itself is a form of hope. This qualified, chastened hope — hope without illusion — is the emotional ground on which the novel finally rests.

## Characters

- **Basil**: His hopes drive the plot forward and, when destroyed, mark the stations of his psychological descent. His final state is not hope but endurance.
- **Clara**: Represents a hope that is compatible with reality — hope not for the restoration of what was lost but for the continuation of what remains.
- **Margaret Sherwin**: Her hopes — for social elevation, for Basil's constancy, for escape from her father's household — are among the novel's most poignant, and their destruction among its most bitter.

## Significance

The treatment of hope in _Basil_ reflects Collins's mature pessimism about the possibility of happy endings. Unlike the conventional Victorian novel, in which hope is rewarded and virtue triumphs, _Basil_ offers hope only in its most chastened form — as the bare possibility of survival. This pessimism is characteristic of sensation fiction's challenge to the moral optimism of mainstream Victorian culture.
