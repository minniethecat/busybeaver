# Social Class

The rigid class hierarchy of Victorian England forms the social backdrop against which the tragedy of _Basil_ unfolds. The gulf between Basil's aristocratic family and the Sherwins' commercial milieu is the fundamental obstacle that drives the plot toward secrecy and catastrophe.

## Definition

In _Basil_, social class is not merely a background condition but an active force that shapes character choices and forecloses possibilities. Class determines whom one may marry, how one is perceived, and what future one may expect. The novel exposes the cruelty of a system in which love across class lines is treated as a degradation, and in which social ambition is punished as presumption.

## Manifestation

The class divide is established early, through the contrast between Basil's father — a man of ancient family and "peculiar pride" — and the Sherwins, a linen-draper's family in the suburban reaches of London. Basil's father embodies the aristocratic code: his pride is "not that conventional pride, which the popular notions attach to rank and wealth" (003-001), yet it is no less absolute for being personal rather than conventional.

Basil himself is acutely aware of the class dimension of his marriage. He reflects that Margaret is "out of the society of her own class" (016-030), isolated by the very marriage that was supposed to elevate her. The Sherwin household, meanwhile, is described as a place that "kept most young men of her class away from the house" (024-049), suggesting that Margaret's social position is already precarious even before Basil enters her life.

The class tension reaches its sharpest expression in the figure of Mr. Sherwin, whose social ambition for his daughter drives him to accept — and even engineer — a secret marriage that he believes will raise the family's status. His calculations reveal a keen awareness of the class system's mechanics, even as his schemes demonstrate its cruelty.

Collins portrays the London streets as a space where "persons of all classes and all temperaments" mingle (007-004), yet this apparent democracy of the crowd only highlights the rigidity of the social barriers that govern private life. The public spaces of the city offer an illusion of class fluidity that the novel's plot systematically destroys.

## Characters

- **Basil**: An aristocrat who crosses class lines for love but cannot overcome the social consequences. His class position is both a privilege and a prison.
- **Margaret Sherwin**: A linen-draper's daughter whose marriage to Basil places her in a social limbo — elevated above her own class but never accepted into his.
- **Basil's Father**: Represents the aristocratic ideal of family honour, which is inseparable from class position.
- **Mr. Sherwin**: Embodies lower-middle-class social ambition; his manipulation of the marriage is driven by a desire to ascend the class ladder.

## Significance

Collins's treatment of class in _Basil_ is characteristic of sensation fiction's willingness to expose the hypocrisies of Victorian society. By making class difference the engine of tragedy rather than a picturesque obstacle to be overcome, the novel offers a darker, more realistic vision than the marriage-plot conventions it superficially resembles.
