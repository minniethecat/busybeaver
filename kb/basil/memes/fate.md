## Definition

Fate in _Basil_ is not the majestic destiny of classical tragedy but something smaller, meaner, and more modern: the inexorable working-out of consequences from apparently trivial beginnings. Basil himself names it "a paltry fate, whose germ was the accident of an idle day" (008-001), collapsing the distance between cosmic destiny and random chance. This fusion of the trivial and the catastrophic is central to Collins's method: sensation fiction derives its power from the vertiginous sense that a life can be undone by a single unlucky encounter.

## Manifestation

### Basil's Fatal Accident

Basil's first extended meditation on fate comes as he sits with his neglected manuscript, the embodiment of all his intellectual ambitions. He reflects that his "stirring ideas," his "store of knowledge patiently heaped up," his "visions of better sights than this world can show" have all been "withered up by the hot breath of the senses—doomed by a paltry fate, whose germ was the accident of an idle day" (008-001). The key word is "germ": fate begins as something microscopic, a seed planted without intention, which then grows into full catastrophe. This is not the fate of Oedipus, ordained by prophecy; it is the fate of modernity, born of contingency.

### Mannion and Inherited Fate

If Basil's fate originates in accident, Mannion's originates in inheritance. His father was hanged for forgery, his mother died in a madhouse, and Mannion describes himself as starting "in life with the hangman's mark on me" (024-023). Wherever he turns, "the gallows still rose as the same immovable obstacle between me and fortune, between me and station, between me and my fellowmen" (024-026). Mannion's fate is social determinism in its cruelest form: the sins of the father visited upon the son, not through divine decree but through the implacable machinery of reputation.

### Mannion Left to His Fate

The phrase "left to his fate" occurs at a pivotal moment when Mannion, exposed as the seducer of Basil's wife, walks out of a coaching inn to face a hostile crowd. "Immediately afterwards an inner door in the house banged violently; and I knew that Mannion had been left to his fate" (019-037). Here fate takes the concrete form of social retribution: Mannion is not struck by lightning but by the rough justice of a mob. The banging door is a brilliantly mundane detail that anchors the moment in physical reality even as the language invokes destiny.

### The Master-Thought of Fate

After his illness, the question of Mannion's fate becomes Basil's "one master-thought" — "the thought that gave back its vigilance to my intellect, and its manhood to my heart" (021-004). Fate here becomes a motivator rather than a burden: the need to know what became of his enemy restores Basil to purpose. This inversion — fate as goad rather than doom — is characteristic of Collins's psychological sophistication.

## Characters

**[[basil]]** is the novel's primary vehicle for the theme of fate. His retrospective narration is shaped by the awareness that a single "accident of an idle day" set in motion everything that followed. He is both the victim of fate and its analyst.

**[[robert-mannion]]** embodies inherited fate. His entire life is shaped by the consequences of his father's crime — a fate he did not choose but cannot escape. The contrast between Basil's "accidental" fate and Mannion's "inherited" fate is one of the novel's most suggestive structural parallels.

## Significance

Collins's treatment of fate marks his departure from the providential assumptions of much Victorian fiction. In _Basil_, fate operates without moral design: it does not punish the wicked and reward the virtuous, but distributes suffering with apparent indifference. This vision aligns the novel with the darker strands of sensation fiction, which traded the consolations of providence for the frisson of contingency.

The language of fate also serves a narrative function. By casting his story in terms of doom and destiny, Basil — consciously or not — mitigates his own responsibility. If his ruin was fated, it was also to some degree inevitable; the rhetoric of fate becomes a form of self-exculpation, subtly shaping the reader's sympathy.
