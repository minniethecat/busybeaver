# Cornwall

Cornwall is both the physical setting of the novel's frame narrative and the site of its most dramatic confrontation. Basil flees to this remote western coast after the death of Margaret Sherwin, seeking seclusion in which to write his confession. It is here that Robert Mannion tracks him down, turns the local fishing community against him, and ultimately meets his own death in a storm-lashed chasm on the Cornish cliffs.

## Description

Cornwall appears in the novel as a wild, elemental landscape of granite cliffs, Atlantic surf, and isolated fishing hamlets. The novel opens with Basil describing his surroundings from the casement of his cottage window: the fishermen spreading their nets, children playing around the boats on the beach, and

> "the sea-breeze blows fresh and pure towards the shore — all objects are brilliant to look on, all sounds are pleasant to hear" (001-006)

The fishing hamlet where Basil writes is situated "on the southern shore of Cornwall, not more than a few miles distant from the Land's End" (028-005). His cottage is "built of rough granite, rudely thatched, and has but two rooms" (028-005). From the churchyard on the hilltop, the view encompasses the full grandeur of the coast:

> "Mighty piles of granite soar above the fishermen's cottages on each side; the little strip of white beach which the cliffs shut in, glows pure in the sunlight; the inland stream that trickles down the bed of the rocks, sparkles, at places, like a rivulet of silver-fire" (028-009)

The landscape is dominated by the "old grey stone church which stands solitary on the hilltop, surrounded by the lonesome moor" (028-008). The granite promontories that "project into the sea, as natural breakwaters, on the southern shore of Cornwall" (028-043) define the coastline. Among these headlands lies a particularly dangerous chasm — "a black, yawning hole that slanted nearly straight downwards, like a tunnel, to unknown and unfathomable depths below" — where "even at calm times the sea was never silent" and on stormy days the "wild waves boiled and thundered in their imprisonment, till they seemed to convulse the solid cliff about them, like an earthquake" (028-045).

The Cornish weather shapes the entire atmosphere of the later chapters. Mist and storm are persistent presences: the "thick white mist, now hanging, still and dripping, down to the ground; now rolling in shapes like vast smoke-wreaths" (028-039) obscures the coast. When storms rage, "the crash of the breakers on the reef howls upon me with a sound of judgment; and the voice of the wind, growling and battling behind me in the hollows of the cave, is, ever and ever, the same thunder-voice of doom and warning in my ear" (028-010).

## Narrative Role

Cornwall serves three crucial narrative functions in the novel: as the site of composition, as the setting of exile, and as the arena of the final confrontation.

### The Frame Narrative

The novel opens and closes in Cornwall. In the very first paragraphs, Basil introduces himself as writing "hidden amid the far hills of the far West of England, surrounded only by the few simple inhabitants of a fishing hamlet on the Cornish coast" (001-005). He lives under "a threat of impending hostility" from "an enemy, determined and deadly, patient alike to wait days or years for his opportunity" (001-005). This establishes the sense of peril that colours the entire narrative.

### Exile and Writing

After Margaret Sherwin's death and Mannion's appearance at her grave (027-001 through 027-015), Basil's brother Ralph convinces him to leave London for Clara's sake (027-018 through 027-028). Influenced by childhood memories — "My nurse had been a Cornish woman; my first fancies and first feelings of curiosity had been excited by her Cornish stories" (027-035) — Basil chooses Cornwall as his destination. He resolves upon this and "left London for Cornwall, without making any attempt to conceal my departure" (027-043).

In the fishing hamlet, Basil experiences what he calls "the completest seclusion" (028-005). He begins his Journal from "my retirement on the Cornish coast" (028-004), recording events day by day. The fishermen, who "still preserve almost all the superstitions, even to the grossest, which were held dear by their humble ancestors, centuries back" (028-006), initially regard him with deep suspicion, recalling "old Cornish legends of solitary, secret people who had lived, years and years ago, in certain parts of the county — coming, none knew whence; existing, none knew by what means; dying and disappearing, none knew when" (028-006). Over time, however, Basil earns their trust through small kindnesses to their children, and receives modest gifts — including "two or three gulls' eggs placed in a basket before my door" (028-007).

### The Confrontation

Cornwall proves not to be the refuge Basil hoped. Mannion follows him and orchestrates the systematic destruction of his relationship with the community: "Mannion had followed me to Cornwall: his threats were executed to the very letter!" (028-032). Using the fishermen's superstition as a weapon, Mannion turns them against Basil, forcing him to flee. On the clifftop, in a scene of extraordinary dramatic intensity, Mannion confronts Basil:

> "Take care of your life. It is not yours to throw away — it is mine!" (028-049)

Standing "close by me in that wild solitude" (028-056), Mannion declares his intention to hunt Basil forever: "The horrible joy of hunting you through the world, leaps in my blood like fire!" (028-055). The confrontation pushes Basil to the brink of murder — "A horrible temptation to rid myself forever of the wretch before me, by hurling him over the precipice at my feet, seized on me" (028-056) — but he resists. Moments later, Mannion's own violence undoes him: making a threatening gesture on the slippery granite, he loses his footing and plunges into the abyss. Basil witnesses the horrifying end:

> "I saw two livid and bloody hands tossed up against the black walls of the hole, as he dropped into it" (028-060)

### Aftermath

After Mannion's apparent death, the narrative records Basil's subsequent departure from Cornwall and later return. The Letter from a miner's wife at "Bartallock, in Cornwall" (029-001) provides a secondary perspective on the region. Basil later reflects, "After my removal from Cornwall (shall I ever forget the first..." (031-006), and returns to "see the good miner and his wife" (031-011) after the novel's central events have concluded.

## Associated Characters

- **[[basil]]** — Cornwall is Basil's place of exile, writing, and near-destruction. It represents both his punishment and his means of redemption through confession.
- **[[robert-mannion]]** — Mannion follows Basil to Cornwall, weaponises local superstition against him, and meets his death on the Cornish cliffs. Cornwall is the stage for Mannion's final act of persecution.
- **The Cornish fishermen** — The unnamed but vividly drawn community of superstitious fisherfolk who first welcome, then reject Basil. They serve as instruments of Mannion's scheme and exemplify the novel's concern with collective suspicion.
- **The miner of Bartallock and his wife** — Kindly figures who shelter Basil after his ordeal; the miner's wife writes the letter that opens the "Letters & Appendices" section (029-001).

## Symbolism

Cornwall operates symbolically on multiple levels. It is, first and foremost, a landscape of **exile and purification** — a place "far from the home from which I am exiled" (028-003), where Basil confronts his guilt through the act of writing. The wildness of the coast mirrors the turbulence of his inner life: the calm, sunlit sea (028-009) corresponds to moments of contrition and hope, while the storm-lashed cliffs (028-010, 028-039) externalise his terror and despair.

The **granite promontories and the chasm** function as a moral topography. The "great granite promontories which project into the sea" (028-043) stand as immutable witnesses to human transgression — ancient, indifferent, sublime. The yawning hole into which Mannion falls (028-045, 028-060) suggests the abyss of total moral destruction that both Basil and Mannion skirt throughout the novel. That Mannion — not Basil — plunges into it is crucial: Basil resists the temptation to murder, and cosmic justice (or accident) intervenes instead.

Cornwall also embodies the **threshold between civilisation and wilderness**. Basil moves from the fashionable squares of London to a cottage of "rough granite" with "no furniture but my bed, my table, and my chair" (028-005). This stripping-away of social identity is both punishment and liberation — he can no longer be Basil the gentleman, but he can become the anonymous writer whose "plain and true record" (001-004) may do good.

Finally, the **superstition of the Cornish community** represents how fear and ignorance can be weaponised. Mannion's exploitation of the fishermen's "old Cornish legends of solitary, secret people" (028-006) demonstrates the novel's dark view of collective credulity — a theme that resonates with Mannion's earlier manipulation of Mr. Sherwin and his ultimate destruction of the Sherwin household.

## Real-World Basis

Collins places the fishing hamlet "on the southern shore of Cornwall, not more than a few miles distant from the Land's End" (028-005). The journey toward Penzance (028-040) and the mention of the village of Treen (028-037) anchor the narrative in identifiable geography. The granite cliffs, the promontories functioning as natural breakwaters (028-043), and the treacherous chasms along this stretch of coast correspond to the real topography of the Land's End peninsula. The "Bartallock" of the miner's letter (029-001) appears to be a fictional or alternate name for a settlement in the region.
