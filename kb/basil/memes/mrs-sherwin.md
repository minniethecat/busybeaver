## Role

Mrs. Sherwin is a supporting character in _Basil_, occupying the position of wife to Mr. Sherwin and mother to Margaret. Her role in the narrative is defined almost entirely by her subordination to her husband. She is a peripheral figure at North Villa, barely noticed by visitors and silenced by her husband's domineering presence.

## Biography

Mrs. Sherwin is introduced as part of the household at North Villa: "The family only consisted of Mr. and Mrs. Sherwin, and the young lady, their daughter" (PN 007-031). She is first properly encountered when Basil visits the Sherwin home for tea. Basil records his first impression with telling brevity: "Poor Mrs. Sherwin! I had hardly noticed her on the former occasion" (PN 011-011). She attempts polite conversation about the weather, speaking "almost inaudibly" (PN 011-012), but is repeatedly interrupted and spoken over by her husband, who treats her with undisguised impatience. When Basil tries to draw her into conversation, Mr. Sherwin interposes, dismissing her contribution (PN 011-019). Her presence at North Villa is spectral — she exists but is barely permitted to be seen or heard.

## Relationships

- **mr-sherwin**: Her husband, who dominates and silences her. His impatience with her is openly displayed before guests. He refers to her as "My wife; Mrs. Sherwin" with visible impatience (PN 010-021), and she starts nervously at the sound of his bell (PN 011-014).
- **margaret-sherwin**: Her daughter. The nature of their relationship is not extensively depicted, though Mrs. Sherwin's presence in the household suggests a maternal role, however diminished by her husband's authority.

## Significance

Mrs. Sherwin serves as a foil to her husband and an index of the household's dysfunctional dynamics. Her near-invisibility and cowed silence expose Mr. Sherwin's tyranny and the oppressive atmosphere of North Villa. She represents the domestic cost of Sherwin's social climbing — a wife reduced to a shadow, her identity absorbed into her husband's commercial ambitions. Her presence also foreshadows the vulnerability of Margaret within the same patriarchal household structure.
