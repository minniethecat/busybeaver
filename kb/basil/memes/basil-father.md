# Basil's Father

Basil's father is the moral anchor of the novel's aristocratic world — a man whose "peculiar pride" (003-001) is at once admirable and forbidding. His character establishes the standard of honour against which Basil's actions are measured, and his eventual capacity for forgiveness provides the novel's emotional resolution.

## Role

Basil's father embodies the aristocratic code of honour that governs the novel's moral universe. Unlike the conventional pride "which the popular notions attach to rank and wealth" (003-001), his pride is deeply personal — a private standard of integrity that makes him unapproachable to his own son. He is not a tyrant but a man whose very goodness is intimidating.

## Biography

The father is introduced through an extended character portrait that emphasises his moral seriousness. He performs "his duties towards his family honourably, delicately, and kindly" (003-007), and his "chivalrous sense of honour" (003-012) is visible "in every glance." This portrait establishes him as a man of genuine virtue — but also as a man before whom confession is terrifying.

The revelation of Basil's secret marriage is the father's greatest test. His response — not rage but sorrow — surprises Basil and the reader. The struggle between "parental affection and his family pride" (022-048) ends with the triumph of affection, but only after immense suffering. The father's eventual forgiveness is the novel's most powerful affirmation of the possibility of reconciliation.

## Relationships

- **Basil**: His younger son. The fear of disappointing this father is the primary obstacle to Basil's honesty.
- **Clara**: His daughter, whose warmth complements his austerity.
- **Ralph**: His elder son, whose simpler character provides contrast.

## Significance

Basil's father represents Collins's exploration of patriarchal authority — not as tyranny but as a moral standard so high that it becomes paralysing. His character complicates the novel's critique of Victorian social codes by showing that honour can be genuine and yet destructive.

Part I, Chapters III–IV (portrait); Part III (confession scene); Letters (reconciliation)
