## Description

The Cornish coast is the novel's most dramatically rendered landscape. It is "away from the regular line of coast, out on one of the great granite" headlands (028-043) that Basil's final confrontation with Mannion occurs. The coast is described through its absences as much as its presences: no shelter, no witness, no civilization — only rock, sea, and sky.

The "fishing hamlet on the Cornish coast" (001-005) where Basil writes his narrative provides a frame for the entire novel. It is from this place of retirement that he looks back on his London life, the physical distance from the city mirroring the temporal distance from his catastrophe.

## Narrative Role

### Basil's Retreat

After the devastation of his marriage, Basil retreats to the Cornish coast. The "retirement on the Cornish coast" (028-004) provides the solitude in which he can reconstruct his shattered identity. The landscape's extremity — its wind, its granite, its distance from London — matches the extremity of his psychological state.

### The Confrontation

The novel's climactic scene takes place on the cliffs. Basil and Mannion meet "in that wild solitude" (028-056), on the edge of the precipice, and their physical struggle culminates in Mannion's fall. The coast is not merely the setting for this confrontation but its enabling condition: only in such a place, far from witnesses and social norms, could the novel's central conflict be resolved in violence.

### The Walking Figure

After the confrontation, as Basil makes his "weary way along the coast" (028-062), the landscape becomes a mirror for his exhausted state. "There was always the same pa" — the sentence breaking off as consciousness itself threatens to dissolve under the weight of what has happened.

## Associated Characters

**[[basil]]** finds in the Cornish coast both refuge and arena. The landscape that shelters him also forces him toward his final reckoning with Mannion.

**[[robert-mannion]]** follows Basil to the coast, his pursuit transforming the refuge into a trap. His fall from the cliffs literalizes the novel's moral architecture: the villain cast down from the heights.

## Symbolism

The Cornish coast functions as the novel's symbolic opposite to London. Where London is social, constrained, and artificial, the coast is solitary, elemental, and terrifyingly real. The movement from city to coast traces the novel's larger trajectory from civilization to its discontents, from the drawing-room to the abyss.
