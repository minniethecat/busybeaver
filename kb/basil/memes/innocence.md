## Definition

In *Basil*, innocence is never a stable possession — it is a claim made by characters who have already lost it, a judgment pronounced by those who lack the knowledge to assess it, and a weapon wielded by those who have abandoned any pretense of it. The concept circulates most intensely around Margaret Sherwin, whose sexual innocence — or guilt — becomes the novel's central epistemological and moral question. Collins stages this question not as a mystery to be solved but as a discursive battlefield, where the language of innocence is deployed by different characters for incompatible purposes: Basil's father asserts it to protect his son; Sherwin commodifies it to extract payment; Margaret herself embodies its destruction as physical and psychological collapse.

## Manifestation

The novel's most concentrated treatment of innocence occurs in the aftermath of Margaret's death, when Basil's father confronts his son with the revelation of her alleged affair. The scene is constructed around competing claims of innocence. Basil, narrating, describes his own earlier state as one of naive confidence:

> "I thought of our youth, our situation, and our innocence" (021-020)

The retrospective irony is devastating — Basil now knows what the reader is about to learn, and the word "innocence" becomes bitterly ironic when spoken by a narrator who has since understood how thoroughly it was a fiction.

Mr. Sherwin's letter to Ralph Basil is the novel's most cynical treatment of the concept:

> "You keep your opinion of her innocence. We have silence to buy, and we must pay accordingly" (025-075)

Here innocence is not a moral fact but a negotiating position. Sherwin does not believe in his daughter's innocence; he believes in its market value. The chilling calculus — "we have silence to buy" — reduces the most intimate moral question to a commercial transaction.

Basil's father, by contrast, deploys the rhetoric of innocence as paternal protection. He tells his son that he is "as convinced of the spotless innocence of my child as I do" (021-022), and urges Basil to adopt the same conviction — not because the facts warrant it, but because the family's survival depends on it. The phrase "spotless innocence" echoes the language of sexual purity while operating as a deliberate act of willed ignorance.

The tragic irony culminates in Margaret's deathbed scene. Basil, still clinging to his belief, cries out:

> "Innocent Margaret! Oh, me! what innocence!" (026-111)

The exclamation is ambiguous — it could be a cry of conviction or the agonized recognition of its opposite. Collins leaves the ambiguity unresolved, refusing the reader the comfort of certainty. Margaret's innocence or guilt matters less than the fact that the question itself has destroyed her.

Mannion's confession later confirms that Margaret's silence was coerced: "the instinct of terror bound her tongue to the last" (024-071). If she was innocent, she was never permitted to prove it; if she was guilty, her guilt was produced by the very forces that condemned her. Either way, the concept of "innocence" as the novel deploys it is always already compromised — a word that signifies the absence of what it names.

## Characters

**Margaret Sherwin** is the novel's central figure of contested innocence. Her body, her speech, and her silence are all read as evidence either for or against her purity, and the novel traces the destruction of a woman whom the discourse of innocence cannot accommodate — she is neither wholly innocent (she kept secrets) nor wholly guilty (she was manipulated and terrified).

**Basil** begins the novel in a state of moral and sexual innocence that the narrative systematically dismantles. His "innocence" in the early chapters is indistinguishable from naivety — a trust in his own judgment that proves catastrophic. By the novel's end, he has lost both his innocence and his belief in the concept itself.

**Ralph Basil** (Basil's father) wields the language of innocence as a paternal strategy. His insistence on his son's "innocence" is a refusal to acknowledge facts that would shatter the family's social standing. His innocence-talk is a form of protective denial.

**Stephen Sherwin** treats innocence as a commodity. His letter to Ralph Basil is a masterclass in moral cynicism: he does not trouble himself with the truth of his daughter's conduct, only with the price of suppressing the question.

## Significance

The treatment of innocence in *Basil* reflects a broader Victorian anxiety about the knowability of female sexuality. The mid-nineteenth-century cult of domestic purity invested enormous ideological energy in the figure of the sexually innocent woman, yet this figure was constitutively unstable — innocence could never be proven, only asserted, and its assertion always invited doubt. Collins exposes this instability by making Margaret's innocence the object of competing, incompatible claims, none of which can be verified.

The concept also functions as a critique of the Victorian family's investment in appearances. The Basil family's determination to maintain the *appearance* of innocence — in Basil, in Margaret, in the marriage itself — requires the suppression of truth on a scale that is itself a form of moral corruption. Innocence, in the novel's grim logic, cannot survive the effort to protect it.

## Historical Context

The Victorian "double standard" — which held women to a standard of sexual purity from which men were largely exempt — permeates the novel's treatment of innocence. The 1857 Matrimonial Causes Act had recently made divorce more accessible, but the stigma attached to female sexual transgression remained absolute. Collins, who lived unconventionally (maintaining two households with two women), was personally invested in challenging Victorian sexual hypocrisy. The treatment of Margaret Sherwin — whose "innocence" or lack thereof is ultimately less important than the machinery of judgment that destroys her — anticipates the more explicit critique of sexual double standards that Collins would develop in *The Woman in White* (1859) and *No Name* (1862).
