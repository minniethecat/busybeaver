## Event

After Margaret's death, Basil receives a letter from Mannion containing explicit threats. Alone in his lodgings, Basil opens and re-reads the letter, gripped by the menace of its contents: "I opened the letter again, and reread the threats against me at its conclusion. One by one, the questions now arose in my mind: how can I resist, or how escape the vengeance of this evil spirit? how shun the dread deformity of that face, which is to appear before me in secret? how silence that fiend's tongue, or make harmless the poison which it will pour drop by drop into my life?" (PN 025-040).

The letter plunges Basil into a state of paralysed terror. He imagines Mannion's "dread deformity" appearing before him unseen, the "poison" of his words seeping "drop by drop" into his existence. The letter represents Mannion's mastery of psychological torment — he does not need to be physically present to destroy Basil's peace.

The tension of the moment is broken by a knock at the door. Basil's immediate fear is that Mannion himself has come: "But would he come thus? openly, fairly, in the broad daylight, through the populous street?" (PN 025-042). But the step on the stairs is not Mannion's — it is Clara's: "A light, quick step ascended the stairs — my heart bounded; I started to my feet. It was the same step which I used to listen for, and love to hear, in my illness. I ran to the door, and opened it. My instinct had not deceived me! it was my sister!" (PN 025-043).

## Significance

The Mannion letter crystallises the antagonist's method: psychological warfare conducted through language. Mannion's threat is not physical violence but the promise of endless, invasive persecution — "poison" administered "drop by drop." The letter also dramatises the novel's contrast between evil and good through its structure: Mannion's written menace is immediately followed by Clara's physical presence, her "light, quick step" on the stairs serving as an antidote to the letter's poison. The event marks the moment when Basil's passive suffering begins to transform into the active pursuit that drives the novel's final movement.
