## Description

Treen is a small fishing village on the Cornish coast. It is a remote, working-class community whose inhabitants include the Penhale family — William, a miner who turns to fishing when mine work declines, and his wife Mary. The village is described through the Penhales' correspondence, which conveys a world of hard manual labour, family devotion, and simple rural life. William writes: "I thought to myself after you had gone, 'Hadn't I better try what I can do in the fishing at Treen?' And I went there; and, thank God, have got on well by it" (PN 029-004).

## Narrative Role

Treen is the setting for some of the most dramatic events of the novel's conclusion. It is where Mannion, gravely ill, is taken in and cared for by the Penhale family. The journal entry dated "Village of Treen, October 22nd" records that "The events of this one day have changed the whole future of my life" (PN 028-037). The Penhales' letters (PN 029-031) provide an external perspective on the crisis, describing the "poor young gentleman who is so bad at Treen" (PN 030-003) and the aftermath of the final confrontation. Treen thus functions as the stage for the novel's climactic resolution, far removed from the London settings of the earlier chapters.

## Associated Characters

- **mannion**: Mannion is the "poor young gentleman" convalescing at Treen after being struck down. His presence there draws Basil and others to the village for the final confrontation.
- **basil**: Basil travels to Treen following Mannion; the village becomes the site of the novel's climactic events.
- **william-penhale**: A Cornish miner and fisherman who lives at Treen; he and his wife Mary care for the injured and write the letters that document the novel's conclusion.
- **mary-penhale**: William's wife, whose letters provide the narrative of events at Treen.

## Symbolism

Treen represents a world entirely removed from the London society of the novel's opening. The remote Cornish fishing village, with its honest labourers and simple domestic virtues, stands in stark contrast to the corruption, secrecy, and class anxiety of North Villa and Holyoak Square. The Penhales' humble goodness provides a moral counterweight to Mannion's calculated villainy and Sherwin's social climbing. The shift of setting from London to Treen mirrors the novel's movement from social complication toward final resolution.
