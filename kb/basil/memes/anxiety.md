## Definition

Anxiety in _Basil_ is the emotional atmosphere in which the novel's events unfold. Unlike fear, which has a specific object, anxiety in Collins's rendering is often diffuse — a generalized dread that attaches itself to whatever circumstance presents itself. The secret marriage generates a perpetual state of anxiety that colours every social interaction, every family dinner, every private thought. Anxiety is the price of secrecy, and Collins traces with clinical precision how the cost accumulates over time.

## Manifestation

Basil's anxiety is established early as the governing emotion of his clandestine life. In his conversations with Mr. Sherwin, he experiences "the embarrassment and anxiety which I then felt" (010-011), a compound state in which social discomfort and moral dread are indistinguishable. Sherwin, for his part, displays "a moment's cessation, in his anxiety to hear what I had to say" (010-016) — anxiety as eager anticipation, the emotional signature of a man whose social ambitions depend on another's decisions.

The asymmetry of anxiety between the Sherwin parents is revealing. Mr. Sherwin's "anxiety was to make my visits to North Villa" (013-004) appear natural and unremarkable, while Mrs. Sherwin's anxiety manifests differently — as the "mean anxiety on Sherwin's part to clench his bargain" (011-055). Anxiety, in the Sherwin household, is inseparable from social aspiration: the fear of being discovered, the fear of losing the aristocratic connection, the fear of the whole scheme collapsing.

> "In my burning anxiety and impatience I could hardly breathe" (021-007)

This image of anxiety as a physical constriction — a burning that obstructs breath — captures the intensity with which Collins renders psychological states. Basil's anxiety is not a mild worry but a physiological emergency, a state in which the body's basic functions are disrupted by the mind's distress.

The novel also explores anxiety as a response to narrative uncertainty. The doctor's letter reports that "we wait with much anxiety to gain some further insight" (021-012) into Basil's condition, positioning anxiety as the natural response to incomplete knowledge — the same position the reader occupies throughout the novel. Mannion, in his confession, describes his "elaborate anxiety to sham entire ignorance" (024-023) of Basil's marriage, revealing anxiety as something that can be performed as well as felt.

Margaret's "strange expression of sadness and anxiety in her face" (021-038) is one of the novel's most economical images of her inner state — a single look that conveys the compound misery of a woman caught between her father's ambitions, her husband's suspicions, and her lover's schemes.

## Characters

**Basil** is the novel's most anxious character — necessarily so, since he has the most to conceal. His anxiety is the constant companion of his secrecy, growing in intensity as the plot moves toward its catastrophic revelations.

**Mr. Sherwin** experiences anxiety as the emotional cost of social climbing. His "burning anxiety" about the marriage negotiations, his "elaborate anxiety" to maintain appearances — these are the telltale signs of a man whose position is fundamentally insecure.

**Margaret Sherwin** is more opaque than the male characters, but her anxiety is legible on her face and in her manner. Her "strange expression of sadness and anxiety" suggests an inner life that the novel never fully reveals — one of its most suggestive ambiguities.

**Basil's Father** experiences anxiety as a rupture in his paternal authority. His anxiety about his son's behaviour, about the family's reputation, about the social consequences of disgrace, reveals that even the most powerful characters in the novel's world are vulnerable to the fear of what might happen next.

## Significance

Anxiety in _Basil_ is both a plot mechanism and a psychological condition. As a plot mechanism, it generates suspense: the reader shares Basil's anxiety about whether his secret will be discovered, whether Margaret will be faithful, whether Mannion will strike. As a psychological condition, it is the novel's most sustained portrait of the subjective experience of guilt — what it feels like, moment by moment, to live with a secret that could destroy you.

Collins's attention to the physical symptoms of anxiety — burning sensations, constricted breathing, facial expressions — aligns his fiction with the period's medical literature on nervous disorders while also serving his sensational aesthetic. Anxiety is the emotion that sensation fiction is designed to produce in its readers, and _Basil_ embeds that emotion in its very subject matter.

## Historical Context

The mid-nineteenth century saw anxiety emerge as a distinct medical and cultural category, distinct from the older language of "melancholy" and "nervousness." The rapid pace of social change, the instability of class boundaries, and the pressures of urban life all contributed to what contemporaries recognized as a new kind of psychological distress. _Basil_ registers these cultural anxieties in its portrait of a protagonist whose suffering is at once deeply personal and recognizably modern.
