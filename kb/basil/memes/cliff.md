## Description

The cliff is the novel's most extreme landscape, a place of "massive granite" (028-039) and "perilous surface" (028-056) that seems to belong less to England than to some older, more violent geology. It rises from "a strip of white beach" (028-009) to a height from which a fall is fatal.

Weather magnifies the cliff's menace. Overhead, "the sky was hidden" (028-039) by clouds that seem to "convulse the solid cliff about them, like an earthquake" (028-045). The cliff is not inert scenery but an active participant, its instability matching the psychological volatility of the men who meet upon it.

## Narrative Role

### The Ascent

Basil ascends "the cliff path which led to the moor" (028-038) after leaving the fishing hamlet, the climb itself an abandonment of the human community below. Each step upward carries him further from safety and closer to the confrontation the novel has been building toward since its London chapters.

### The Confrontation

The cliff is the arena for the novel's climactic scene. Basil is standing "close to the edge of the cliff, when a hand was laid suddenly" (028-048) upon him — Mannion's hand, the livid hand that has haunted the novel. Their struggle takes place on the very edge, the precipice transforming a fistfight into a mortal contest.

### The Fall

Mannion's fall from the cliff resolves the novel's central conflict through the agency of the landscape itself. The cliff edge that Basil and Mannion have fought upon becomes the instrument of judgment: "the edge of the cliff" (028-048) is not merely the setting for violence but its mechanism.

## Associated Characters

**[[basil]]** meets his double on the cliff. The isolation of the place strips away every social mediation, leaving only the raw physical confrontation with the man who has destroyed his life.

**[[robert-mannion]]** follows Basil to the cliff, his pursuit ending in the fatal fall. The cliff's "perilous surface" (028-056) claims him as the novel's architecture of moral justice demands: the villain cast down from the heights.

## Symbolism

The cliff is the symbolic terminus of the novel's geographic argument. Basil has moved from London drawing-rooms through suburban gardens to this elemental edge of England, and at each stage the settings have shed another layer of social convention. On the cliff there is nothing left but granite, wind, and the two men whose conflict can no longer be mediated by anything but violence. That the landscape itself seems to participate in the judgment — the storm, the unstable rock, the fatal drop — is the novel's most Gothic assertion: nature itself enforces the moral order that society has failed to uphold.
