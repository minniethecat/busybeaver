## Description

The village inn is a rustic establishment near the Basil family's country home. Basil describes it in affectionate detail: "I see the same sanded parlour, decorated with the same old sporting prints, furnished with the same battered, deep-coloured mahogany table, and polished elm tree chairs, that I remember in our own village inn" (PN 006-008). The inn embodies a timeless, unchanging rural character, with its traditional furnishings and local ale.

## Narrative Role

The village inn appears in Basil's nostalgic recollections of his country life. It serves as a point of contrast with his London existence: the inn represents an older, simpler England of rural traditions, stable domesticity, and innocent pleasures—the world Basil associates with Clara and his childhood. The inn also features in the courtship period, as Clara finds "bits of character and humour" among the people encountered there (PN 006-008).

## Associated Characters

- **basil**: Basil associates the village inn with his country childhood and his idealised vision of rural English life.
- **clara**: Clara accompanies Basil on excursions and finds amusement in the characters at the inn.

## Symbolism

The village inn symbolises rural simplicity and the lost innocence of Basil's youth. It stands in opposition to the moral complexity and urban corruption represented by London and North Villa. The inn's unchanging nature—the same sanded parlour, the same sporting prints—represents a stability and moral clarity that Basil loses as he becomes entangled in the events of the novel.
