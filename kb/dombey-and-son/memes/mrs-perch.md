# Mrs. Perch

## Overview

Mrs. Perch is the wife of [[Mr. Perch|mr-perch]], the messenger at [[Dombey's|[[dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]]-and-son-firm]] counting-house. She resides at [[Ball's Pond|balls-pond]] with a steadily expanding [[family|family-and-kinship]] — seven [[children|childhood]] at one count, later "nursing twins" (PN 125-059) — and serves as her husband's audience for office gossip and a choric presence at the servants' gatherings around the Dombey wedding and bankruptcy. Dickens treats her with amused sympathy, making her perpetual "interesting situation" a running joke while never denying her humanity.

## Biography

Mrs. Perch first enters the novel by proxy: her husband asks [[Walter Gay|walter-gay]] to send "a jar of preserved Ginger, cheap, for Mrs Perch's own eating, in the course of her recovery from her next confinement" (PN 038-016). Her condition is a constant — when she faces Mr. Dombey on the stairs on his wedding day, the narrator notes she is "in an interesting situation" with the parenthesis "(but that she always is)" (PN 090-012), and [[Captain Cuttle|captain-cuttle]]'s iron hook is deemed a domestic hazard "destructive to that lady's hopes" (PN 049-047).

She attends the Dombey-[[Edith|edith-dombey]] wedding as a spectator with the other servants, becoming "joyous and beaming" at the celebratory breakfast — "lifted so far above the cares of life, that if she were asked just now to direct a wayfarer to Ball's Pond, where her own cares lodge, she would have some difficulty in recalling the way" (PN 090-028). Her domestic "cares" are her many children, and in a rare moment of candour she tells Cook she fears Perch "is not so much attached to his home, as he used to be, when they were only nine in family" (PN 090-048).

Following Edith's elopement, Mrs. Perch enjoys one of the novel's most sustained passages of servant speech: she frets that Mr. Dombey's "confidence in woman is shaken" and that he "half expects on coming home at night to find her gone off with some Viscount" (PN 160-046). The narrator notes the absurdity of this statement "on a par with the baby" but allows it weight nonetheless.

In the bankruptcy aftermath, she takes a leading role in the servants' council, arguing with "several pious axioms, original and selected" (PN 163-063) that delicacy demands they all leave the ruined house. Her moral performance carries the day.

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Perch is Dickens's comic chorister of the lower-middle-[[class|class-and-society]] domestic world. Her defining trait — perpetual pregnancy — is treated with a blend of farce and genuine warmth unusual even for Dickens: the joke is never cruel, and her voice in the servants' councils carries real weight. She functions as a barometer of [[the Dombey household|the-dombey-household]]'s fortunes: at the wedding she is giddy with reflected glamour; at the bankruptcy she is the voice of moral propriety. Between these poles, her anxieties about her husband's wandering eye and her endless childbearing sketch a life of quiet struggle beneath the comedy.

## Relationships

Mrs. Perch is defined by her [[marriage|marriage]] to [[mr-perch|Mr. Perch]], whose office life at Dombey's provides the news and gossip she receives at Ball's Pond. In the servants' hierarchy she is a follower, described as possessing "the happy social faculty of always wondering when other people wonder, without being at all particular what she wonders at" (PN 099-059). Her closest associate among the servants is Cook, whose speeches move her to tears and declarations that "Cook is an angel" (PN 163-025).
