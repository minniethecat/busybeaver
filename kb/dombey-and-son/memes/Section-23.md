[023-001]“What a pretty fellow he is when he’s asleep!” said [[Berry|miss-berry]], stopping to

look at [[Paul|paul-dombey-jr]] in bed, one night when she took up [[Mrs Wickam|mrs-wickam]]’s supper.

[023-002]“Ah!” sighed Mrs Wickam. “He need be.”

[023-003]“Why, he’s not ugly when he’s awake,” observed Berry.

[023-004]“No, Ma’am. Oh, no. No more was my Uncle’s Betsey Jane,” said Mrs

[023-005]Wickam.

[023-006]Berry looked as if she would like to trace the connexion of ideas

between [[paul-[[dombey|paul-dombey]]|Paul Dombey]] and Mrs Wickam’s Uncle’s Betsey Jane.

[023-007]“My Uncle’s wife,” Mrs Wickam went on to say, “died just like his Mama.

[023-008]My Uncle’s child took on just as Master Paul do.”

[023-009]“Took on! You don’t think he grieves for his Mama, sure?” argued Berry,

sitting down on the side of the bed. “He can’t remember anything about

her, you know, Mrs Wickam. It’s not possible.”

[023-010]“No, Ma’am,” said Mrs Wickam “No more did my Uncle’s child. But my

[023-011]Uncle’s child said very strange things sometimes, and looked very

strange, and went on very strange, and was very strange altogether. My

[023-012]Uncle’s child made people’s blood run cold, some times, she did!”

[023-013]“How?” asked Berry.

[023-014]“I wouldn’t have sat up all night alone with Betsey Jane!” said Mrs

[023-015]Wickam, “not if you’d have put Wickam into [[money-and-[[commerce|money-and-commerce]]|business]] next morning for

himself. I couldn’t have done it, Miss Berry.

[023-016]Miss Berry naturally asked why not? But Mrs Wickam, agreeably to the

usage of some ladies in her condition, pursued her own branch of the

subject, without any compunction.

[023-017]“Betsey Jane,” said Mrs Wickam, “was as sweet a child as I could wish

to see. I couldn’t wish to see a sweeter. Everything that a child could

have in the way of illnesses, Betsey Jane had come through. The cramps

was as common to her,” said Mrs Wickam, “as biles is to yourself, Miss

[023-018]Berry.” Miss Berry involuntarily wrinkled her nose.

[023-019]“But Betsey Jane,” said Mrs Wickam, lowering her voice, and looking

round the room, and towards Paul in bed, “had been minded, in her

cradle, by her departed mother. I couldn’t say how, nor I couldn’t say

when, nor I couldn’t say whether the dear child knew it or not, but

[023-020]Betsey Jane had been watched by her mother, Miss Berry!” and Mrs

[023-021]Wickam, with a very white face, and with watery eyes, and with a

tremulous voice, again looked fearfully round the room, and towards

[023-022]Paul in bed.

[023-023]“Nonsense!” cried Miss Berry—somewhat resentful of the idea.

[023-024]“You may say nonsense! I ain’t offended, Miss. I hope you may be able

to think in your own conscience that it is nonsense; you’ll find your

spirits all the better for it in this—you’ll excuse my being so free—in

this burying-ground of a place; which is wearing of me down. Master

[023-025]Paul’s a little restless in his sleep. Pat his back, if you please.”

[023-026]“Of course you think,” said Berry, gently doing what she was asked,

[023-027]“that he has been nursed by his mother, too?”

[023-028]“Betsey Jane,” returned Mrs Wickam in her most solemn tones, “was put

upon as that child has been put upon, and changed as that child has

changed. I have seen her sit, often and often, think, think, thinking,

like him. I have seen her look, often and often, old, old, old, like

him. I have heard her, many a [[time|time-and-change]], talk just like him. I consider that

child and Betsey Jane on the same footing entirely, Miss Berry.”

[023-029]“Is your Uncle’s child alive?” asked Berry.

[023-030]“Yes, Miss, she is alive,” returned Mrs Wickam with an air of triumph,

for it was evident. Miss Berry expected the reverse; “and is married to

a silver-chaser. Oh yes, Miss, SHE is alive,” said Mrs Wickam, laying

strong stress on her nominative case.

[023-031]It being clear that somebody was dead, [[Mrs Pipchin|mrs-pipchin]]’s niece inquired who

it was.

[023-032]“I wouldn’t wish to make you uneasy,” returned Mrs Wickam, pursuing her

supper. “Don’t ask me.”

[023-033]This was the surest way of being asked again. Miss Berry repeated her

question, therefore; and after some resistance, and reluctance, Mrs

[023-034]Wickam laid down her knife, and again glancing round the room and at

[023-035]Paul in bed, replied:

[023-036]“She took fancies to people; whimsical fancies, some of them; others,

affections that one might expect to see—only stronger than common. They

all died.”

[023-037]This was so very unexpected and awful to Mrs Pipchin’s niece, that she

sat upright on the hard edge of the bedstead, breathing short, and

surveying her informant with looks of undisguised alarm.

[023-038]Mrs Wickam shook her left fore-finger stealthily towards the bed where

[023-039][[florence-dombey|Florence]] lay; then turned it upside down, and made several emphatic

points at the floor; immediately below which was the parlour in which

[023-040]Mrs Pipchin habitually consumed the toast.

[023-041]“Remember my words, Miss Berry,” said Mrs Wickam, “and be thankful that

[023-042]Master Paul is not too fond of you. I am, that he’s not too fond of me,

I assure you; though there isn’t much to live for—you’ll excuse my

being so free—in this jail of a house!”

[023-043]Miss Berry’s emotion might have led to her patting Paul too hard on the

back, or might have produced a cessation of that soothing monotony, but

he turned in his bed just now, and, presently awaking, sat up in it

with his hair hot and wet from the effects of some childish dream, and

asked for [[Florence|florence-dombey]].

[023-044]She was out of her own bed at the first sound of his voice; and bending

over his pillow immediately, sang him to sleep again. Mrs Wickam

shaking her head, and letting fall several tears, pointed out the

little group to Berry, and turned her eyes up to the ceiling.

[023-045]“He’s asleep now, my dear,” said Mrs Wickam after a pause, “you’d

better go to bed again. Don’t you feel cold?”

[023-046]“No, nurse,” said Florence, laughing. “Not at all.”

[023-047]“Ah!” sighed Mrs Wickam, and she shook her head again, expressing to

the watchful Berry, “we shall be cold enough, some of us, by and by!”

[023-048]Berry took the frugal supper-tray, with which Mrs Wickam had by this

time done, and bade her good-night.

[023-049]“Good-night, Miss!” returned Wickam softly. “Good-night! Your aunt is

an old lady, Miss Berry, and it’s what you must have looked for,

often.”

[023-050]This consolatory farewell, Mrs Wickam accompanied with a look of

heartfelt anguish; and being left alone with the two [[childhood|children]] again,

and becoming conscious that the wind was blowing mournfully, she

indulged in melancholy—that cheapest and most accessible of

luxuries—until she was overpowered by slumber.

[023-051]Although the niece of Mrs Pipchin did not expect to find that exemplary

dragon prostrate on the hearth-rug when she went downstairs, she was

relieved to find her unusually fractious and severe, and with every

present appearance of intending to live a long time to be a comfort to

all who knew her. Nor had she any symptoms of declining, in the course

of the ensuing week, when the constitutional viands still continued to

disappear in regular succession, notwithstanding that Paul studied her

as attentively as ever, and occupied his usual seat between the black

skirts and the fender, with unwavering constancy.

[023-052]But as Paul himself was no stronger at the expiration of that time than

he had been on his first arrival, though he looked much healthier in

the face, a little carriage was got for him, in which he could lie at

his ease, with an alphabet and other elementary works of reference, and

be wheeled down to [[the sea|the-sea-and-waves]]-side. Consistent in his odd tastes, the

child set aside a ruddy-faced lad who was proposed as the drawer of

this carriage, and selected, instead, his grandfather—a weazen, old,

crab-faced man, in a suit of battered oilskin, who had got tough and

stringy from long pickling in salt water, and who smelt like a weedy

sea-beach when the tide is out.

[023-053]With this notable attendant to pull him along, and Florence always

walking by his side, and the despondent Wickam bringing up the rear, he

went down to the margin of the ocean every day; and there he would sit

or lie in his carriage for hours together: never so distressed as by

the company of [[children|childhood]]—Florence alone excepted, always.

[023-054]“Go away, if you please,” he would say to any child who came to bear

him company. “Thank you, but I don’t want you.”

[023-055]Some small voice, near his ear, would ask him how he was, perhaps.

[023-056]“I am very well, I thank you,” he would answer. “But you had better go

and play, if you please.”

[023-057]Then he would turn his head, and watch the child away, and say to

[023-058]Florence, “We don’t want any others, do we? Kiss me, Floy.”

[023-059]He had even a dislike, at such times, to the company of Wickam, and was

well pleased when she strolled away, as she generally did, to pick up

shells and acquaintances. His favourite spot was quite a lonely one,

far away from most loungers; and with Florence sitting by his side at

work, or reading to him, or talking to him, and the wind blowing on his

face, and the water coming up among the wheels of his bed, he wanted

nothing more.

[023-060]“Floy,” he said one day, “where’s India, where that boy’s friends

live?”

[023-061]“Oh, it’s a long, long distance off,” said Florence, raising her eyes

from her work.

[023-062]“Weeks off?” asked Paul.

[023-063]“Yes dear. Many weeks’ journey, night and day.”

[023-064]“If you were in India, Floy,” said Paul, after being silent for a

minute, “I should—what is it that Mama did? I forget.”

[023-065]“Loved me!” answered Florence.

[023-066]“No, no. Don’t I [[love|love]] you now, Floy? What is it?—Died. If you were in

[023-067]India, I should die, Floy.”

[023-068]She hurriedly put her work aside, and laid her head down on his pillow,

caressing him. And so would she, she said, if he were there. He would

be better soon.

[023-069]“Oh! I am a great deal better now!” he answered. “I don’t mean that. I

mean that I should die of being so sorry and so lonely, Floy!”

[023-070]Another time, in the same place, he fell asleep, and slept quietly for

a long time. Awaking suddenly, he listened, started up, and sat

listening.

[023-071]Florence asked him what he thought he heard.

[023-072]“I want to know what it says,” he answered, looking steadily in her

face. “The sea” Floy, what is it that it keeps on saying?”

[023-073]She told him that it was only the noise of the rolling waves.

[023-074]“Yes, yes,” he said. “But I know that they are always saying something.

[023-075]Always the same thing. What place is over there?” He rose up, looking

eagerly at the horizon.

[023-076]She told him that there was another country opposite, but he said he

didn’t mean that: he meant further away—farther away!

[023-077]Very often afterwards, in the midst of their talk, he would break off,

to try to understand what it was that the waves were always saying; and

would rise up in his couch to look towards that invisible region, far

away.

[023-078]CHAPTER IX.

[023-079]In which [[the Wooden Midshipman|the-wooden-midshipman]] gets into Trouble

[023-080]That spice of romance and love of the marvellous, of which there was a

pretty strong infusion in the nature of young [[walter-gay|[[Walter|walter-gay]] Gay]], and which the

guardianship of his Uncle, old [[solomon-gills|Solomon Gills]], had not very much

weakened by the waters of stern practical [[experience|innocence-and-experience]], was the occasion

of his attaching an uncommon and delightful interest to the adventure

of Florence with [[Good Mrs Brown|mrs-brown]]. He pampered and cherished it in his

[[memory|memory-and-the-past]], especially that part of it with which he had been associated:

until it became the spoiled child of his fancy, and took its own way,

and did what it liked with it.

[023-081]The recollection of those incidents, and his own share in them, may

have been made the more captivating, perhaps, by the weekly dreamings

of [[old Sol|solomon-gills]] and [[captain-cuttle|Captain Cuttle]] on Sundays. Hardly a Sunday passed,

without mysterious references being made by one or other of those

worthy chums to Richard Whittington; and the latter gentleman had even

gone so far as to purchase a ballad of considerable antiquity, that had

long fluttered among many others, chiefly expressive of maritime

sentiments, on a dead wall in the Commercial Road: which poetical

performance set forth the courtship and nuptials of a promising young

coal-whipper with a certain “lovely Peg,” the accomplished daughter of

the master and part-owner of a Newcastle collier. In this stirring

legend, [[Captain Cuttle|captain-cuttle]] descried a profound metaphysical bearing on the

case of Walter and Florence; and it excited him so much, that on very

festive occasions, as birthdays and a few other non-Dominical holidays,

he would roar through the whole song in the little back parlour; making

an amazing shake on the word Pe-e-eg, with which every verse concluded,

in compliment to the heroine of the piece.
