# Mr Sownds

Mr Sownds is the beadle at the church where two significant marriages in *[[Dombey and Son|[[dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]]-and-son-firm]]* take place. He appears alongside [[Mrs Miff|mrs-miff]] the pew-opener, and the pair form a comic duo of minor church officials who observe the great events of the wealthy from their humble positions.

## Description

Mr Sownds is described as "fleshy, and has a bald head"[159-010], in contrast to the wheezy and vinegary Mrs Miff. He carries a cocked hat and performs his beadle's duties with professional solemnity. Dickens portrays him as somewhat more genial and liberal than his colleague, a difference attributed to his office rather than his nature: "Mr Sownds the beadle is more liberal than Mrs Miff—but then he is not a pew-opener"[159-004].

## Role in the Novel

### Dombey's Second Marriage

Mr Sownds is present at the church on the morning of Mr Dombey's wedding to [[Edith|edith-dombey]]. He arrives early to prepare: "Mr Sownds the Beadle has long risen from the steps, and is in waiting with his cocked hat in his hand"[088-054]. Throughout the ceremony, he "glides into the vestry"[089-004] and assists in arranging the wedding party.

At the conclusion of the [[marriage|marriage]] service, "Mrs Miff, and Mr Sownds the Beadle, sit upon the steps to rest"[089-047], their duties discharged. The beadle's presence adds a note of official solemnity to the proceedings, while his humble station provides an ironic counterpoint to the grandeur of Mr Dombey's wedding.

### Florence's Wedding

Mr Sownds reappears at the church near the end of the novel for the wedding of [[Florence Dombey|florence-dombey]] and [[Walter Gay|walter-gay]]. "Mr Sownds the beadle, and Mrs Miff the pew-opener, are early at their posts"[158-071]. At the second wedding, he is "sitting on the steps"[159-005] as Mrs Miff dusts the church, the pair once again serving as the institutional witnesses to a marriage that represents the novel's movement toward happiness and reconciliation.

## Character

Mr Sownds holds orthodox views and takes his official position seriously. When Mrs Miff comments on the nature of marriage, "Mr Sownds the Beadle thinks it is a true saying"[089-030], lending his beadle's authority to her observations. His physical description — fleshy and bald — and his profession as beadle make him a recognizable Dickensian type: the minor parish official whose [[dignity|pride]] is slightly absurd but whose presence is indispensable to the formal proceedings of the church.
