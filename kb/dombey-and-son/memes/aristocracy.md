# Aristocracy

Dickens's portrait of the aristocracy in *[[Dombey and Son|[[dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]]-and-son-firm]]* is characteristically sharp: the titled and the well-born are, almost without exception, hollow — living proof that rank guarantees neither wisdom nor virtue. From the faded grandeur of [[Mrs. Skewton|mrs-skewton]] to the amiable ineffectuality of [[Cousin Feenix|cousin-feenix]], these figures embody a [[class|class-and-society]] in decline. ## About This List

The novel's aristocratic characters function as a satirical counterpoint to the commercial [[pride|pride]] of the Dombeys. Where the merchant believes he can buy importance, the aristocrat believes he was born to it — and Dickens exposes the emptiness of both claims through their encounters and entanglements.
