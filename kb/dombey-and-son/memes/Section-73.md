[073-001]“No thank’ee, Ma’am,” returned the Captain.

[073-002]“Have a roast fowl,” said [[Mrs MacStinger|mrs-macstinger]], “with a bit of weal stuffing

and some egg sauce. Come, Cap’en Cuttle! Give yourself a little treat!”

[073-003]“No thank’ee, Ma’am,” returned the Captain very humbly.

[073-004]“I’m sure you’re out of sorts, and want to be stimulated,” said Mrs

[073-005]MacStinger. “Why not have, for once in a way, a bottle of sherry wine?”

[073-006]“Well, Ma’am,” rejoined the Captain, “if you’d be so good as take a

glass or two, I think I would try that. Would you do me the favour,

[073-007]Ma’am,” said the Captain, torn to pieces by his conscience, “to accept

a quarter’s rent ahead?”

[073-008]“And why so, Cap’en Cuttle?” retorted Mrs MacStinger—sharply, as the

[073-009]Captain thought.

[073-010]The Captain was frightened to dead “If you would Ma’am,” he said with

submission, “it would oblige me. I can’t keep my [[money-and-[[commerce|money-and-commerce]]|money]] very well. It

pays itself out. I should take it kind if you’d comply.”

[073-011]“Well, Cap’en Cuttle,” said the unconscious MacStinger, rubbing her

hands, “you can do as you please. It’s not for me, with my [[family|family-and-kinship]], to

refuse, no more than it is to ask.”

[073-012]“And would you, Ma’am,” said the Captain, taking down the tin canister

in which he kept his cash, from the top shelf of the cupboard, “be so

good as offer eighteen-pence a-piece to the little family all round? If

you could make it convenient, Ma’am, to pass the word presently for

them [[childhood|children]] to come for’ard, in a body, I should be glad to see ’em.”

[073-013]These innocent MacStingers were so many daggers to the Captain’s

breast, when they appeared in a swarm, and tore at him with the

confiding trustfulness he so little deserved. The eye of Alexander

[073-014]MacStinger, who had been his favourite, was insupportable to the

[073-015]Captain; the voice of Juliana MacStinger, who was the picture of her

mother, made a coward of him.

[073-016][[captain-cuttle|Captain Cuttle]] kept up appearances, nevertheless, tolerably well, and

for an hour or two was very hardly used and roughly handled by the

young MacStingers: who in their childish frolics, did a little damage

also to the glazed hat, by sitting in it, two at a [[time|time-and-change]], as in a nest,

and drumming on the inside of the crown with their shoes. At length the

[073-017]Captain sorrowfully dismissed them: taking leave of these cherubs with

the poignant remorse and [[grief|grief-and-loss]] of a man who was going to execution.

[073-018]In the silence of night, the Captain packed up his heavier property in

a chest, which he locked, intending to leave it there, in all

probability for ever, but on the forlorn chance of one day finding a

man sufficiently bold and desperate to come and ask for it. Of his

lighter necessaries, the Captain made a bundle; and disposed his plate

about his person, ready for flight. At the hour of midnight, when Brig

[073-019]Place was buried in slumber, and Mrs MacStinger was lulled in sweet

oblivion, with her infants around her, the guilty Captain, stealing

down on tiptoe, in the dark, opened the door, closed it softly after

him, and took to his heels.

[073-020]Pursued by the image of Mrs MacStinger springing out of bed, and,

regardless of costume, following and bringing him back; pursued also by

a consciousness of his enormous crime; [[Captain Cuttle|captain-cuttle]] held on at a

great pace, and allowed no grass to grow under his feet, between Brig

[073-021]Place and the Instrument-maker’s door. It opened when he knocked—for

[073-022]Rob was on the watch—and when it was bolted and locked behind him,

[073-023]Captain Cuttle felt comparatively safe.

[073-024]“Whew!” cried the Captain, looking round him. “It’s a breather!”

[073-025]“Nothing the matter, is there, Captain?” cried the gaping Rob.

[073-026]“No, no!” said Captain Cuttle, after changing colour, and listening to

a passing footstep in the street. “But mind ye, my lad; if any lady,

except either of them two as you see t’other day, ever comes and asks

for Cap’en Cuttle, be sure to report no person of that name known, nor

never heard of here; observe them orders, will you?”

[073-027]“I’ll take care, Captain,” returned Rob.

[073-028]“You might say—if you liked,” hesitated the Captain, “that you’d read

in the paper that a Cap’en of that name was gone to Australia,

emigrating, along with a whole ship’s complement of people as had all

swore never to come back no more.”

[073-029]Rob nodded his understanding of these instructions; and Captain Cuttle

promising to make a man of him, if he obeyed orders, dismissed him,

yawning, to his bed under the counter, and went aloft to the chamber of

[073-030][[solomon-gills|Solomon Gills]].

[073-031]What the Captain suffered next day, whenever a bonnet passed, or how

often he darted out of the shop to elude imaginary MacStingers, and

sought safety in the attic, cannot be told. But to avoid the fatigues

attendant on this means of self-preservation, the Captain curtained the

glass door of communication between the shop and parlour, on the

inside; fitted a key to it from the bunch that had been sent to him;

and cut a small hole of espial in the wall. The advantage of this

fortification is obvious. On a bonnet appearing, the Captain instantly

slipped into his garrison, locked himself up, and took a secret

observation of the enemy. Finding it a false alarm, the Captain

instantly slipped out again. And the bonnets in the street were so very

numerous, and alarms were so inseparable from their appearance, that

the Captain was almost incessantly slipping in and out all day long.

[073-032]Captain Cuttle found time, however, in the midst of this fatiguing

service to inspect the stock; in connexion with which he had the

general idea (very laborious to Rob) that too much friction could not

be bestowed upon it, and that it could not be made too bright. He also

ticketed a few attractive-looking articles at a venture, at prices

ranging from ten shillings to fifty pounds, and exposed them in the

window to the great astonishment of the public.

[073-033]After effecting these improvements, Captain Cuttle, surrounded by the

instruments, began to feel scientific: and looked up at the stars at

night, through the skylight, when he was smoking his pipe in the little

back parlour before going to bed, as if he had established a kind of

property in them. As a tradesman in the City, too, he began to have an

interest in the Lord Mayor, and the Sheriffs, and in Public Companies;

and felt bound to read the quotations of the Funds every day, though he

was unable to make out, on any principle of navigation, what the

figures meant, and could have very well dispensed with the fractions.

[073-034][[florence-[[dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]]|Florence]], the Captain waited on, with his strange news of [[Uncle Sol|solomon-gills]],

immediately after taking possession of the Midshipman; but she was away

from home. So the Captain sat himself down in his altered station of

life, with no company but [[Rob the Grinder|rob-the-grinder]]; and losing count of time, as

men do when great changes come upon them, thought musingly of [[walter-gay|Walter]],

and of Solomon Gills, and even of Mrs MacStinger herself, as among the

things that had been.

[073-035]CHAPTER XXVI.

[073-036]Shadows of the Past and Future

[073-037]Your most obedient, Sir,” said the Major. “Damme, Sir, a friend of my

friend [[paul-dombey|Dombey]]’s is a friend of mine, and I’m glad to see you!”

[073-038]“I am infinitely obliged, [[james-[[carker|james-carker]]|Carker]],” explained Mr Dombey, “to Major

[073-039]Bagstock, for his company and conversation. [[major-bagstock|Major Bagstock]] has rendered

me great service, Carker.”

[073-040]Mr Carker the Manager, hat in hand, just arrived at [[leamington|Leamington]], and

just introduced to the Major, showed the Major his whole double range

of teeth, and trusted he might take the liberty of thanking him with

all his heart for having effected so great an Improvement in Mr

[073-041]Dombey’s looks and spirits.

[073-042]“By Gad, Sir,” said the Major, in reply, “there are no thanks due to

me, for it’s a give and take affair. A great creature like our friend

[073-043]Dombey, Sir,” said the Major, lowering his voice, but not lowering it

so much as to render it inaudible to that gentleman, “cannot help

improving and exalting his friends. He strengthens and invigorates a

man, Sir, does Dombey, in his moral nature.”

[073-044]Mr Carker snapped at the expression. In his moral nature. Exactly. The

very words he had been on the point of suggesting.

[073-045]“But when my friend Dombey, Sir,” added the Major, “talks to you of

[073-046][[Major Bagstock|major-bagstock]], I must crave leave to set him and you right. He means

plain Joe, Sir—Joey B.—Josh. Bagstock—Joseph—rough and tough Old J.,

[073-047]Sir. At your service.”

[073-048]Mr Carker’s excessively friendly inclinations towards the Major, and Mr

[073-049]Carker’s admiration of his roughness, toughness, and plainness, gleamed

out of every tooth in Mr Carker’s head.

[073-050]“And now, Sir,” said the Major, “you and Dombey have the devil’s own

amount of business to talk over.”

[073-051]“By no means, Major,” observed Mr Dombey.

[073-052]“Dombey,” said the Major, defiantly, “I know better; a man of your

mark—the Colossus of commerce—is not to be interrupted. Your moments

are precious. We shall meet at dinner-time. In the interval, old Joseph

will be scarce. The dinner-hour is a sharp seven, Mr Carker.”

[073-053]With that, the Major, greatly swollen as to his face, withdrew; but

immediately putting in his head at the door again, said:

[073-054]“I beg your pardon. Dombey, have you any message to ’em?”

[073-055]Mr Dombey in some embarrassment, and not without a glance at the

courteous keeper of his business confidence, entrusted the Major with

his compliments.

[073-056]“By the Lord, Sir,” said the Major, “you must make it something warmer

than that, or old Joe will be far from welcome.”

[073-057]“Regards then, if you will, Major,” returned Mr Dombey.

[073-058]“Damme, Sir,” said the Major, shaking his shoulders and his great

cheeks jocularly: “make it something warmer than that.”

[073-059]“What you please, then, Major,” observed Mr Dombey.

[073-060]“Our friend is sly, Sir, sly, Sir, de-vilish sly,” said the Major,

staring round the door at Carker. “So is Bagstock.” But stopping in the

midst of a chuckle, and drawing himself up to his full height, the

[073-061]Major solemnly exclaimed, as he struck himself on the chest, “Dombey! I

[[jealousy|envy]] your feelings. God bless you!” and withdrew.

[073-062]“You must have found the gentleman a great resource,” said Carker,

following him with his teeth.

[073-063]“Very great indeed,” said Mr Dombey.

[073-064]“He has friends here, no doubt,” pursued Carker. “I perceive, from what

he has said, that you go into society here. Do you know,” smiling

horribly, “I am so very glad that you go into society!”

[073-065]Mr Dombey acknowledged this display of interest on the part of his

second in command, by twirling his watch-chain, and slightly moving his

head.

[073-066]“You were formed for society,” said Carker. “Of all the men I know, you

are the best adapted, by nature and by position, for society. Do you

know I have been frequently amazed that you should have held it at

arm’s length so long!”

[073-067]“I have had my reasons, Carker. I have been alone, and indifferent to

it. But you have great social qualifications yourself, and are the more

likely to have been surprised.”

[073-068]“Oh! I!” returned the other, with ready self-disparagement. “It’s quite

another matter in the case of a man like me. I don’t come into

comparison with you.”

[073-069]Mr Dombey put his hand to his neckcloth, settled his chin in it,

coughed, and stood looking at his faithful friend and servant for a few

moments in silence.

[073-070]“I shall have the pleasure, Carker,” said Mr Dombey at length: making

as if he swallowed something a little too large for his throat: “to

present you to my—to the Major’s friends. Highly agreeable people.”

[073-071]“Ladies among them, I presume?” insinuated the smooth Manager.

[073-072]“They are all—that is to say, they are both—ladies,” replied Mr Dombey.

[073-073]“Only two?” smiled Carker.

[073-074]“They are only two. I have confined my visits to their residence, and

have made no other acquaintance here.”

[073-075]“Sisters, perhaps?” quoth Carker.
