## Overview

The Feenix [[family|family-and-kinship]] embodies the Victorian aristocracy in its twilight — proud of its lineage but financially hollowed out, reduced to trading its remaining assets (beauty, name, connections) for the [[wealth|money-and-commerce]] of the commercial classes. Through [[Edith Granger|edith-dombey]] (née Feenix) and her cousin [[Lord Feenix|cousin-feenix]], Dickens paints a satirical portrait of a [[class|class-and-society]] that has outlived its purpose but clings to its forms.

## Lord Feenix

Cousin Feenix is one of Dickens's great comic creations — a nobleman so vague and ineffectual that he can barely complete a sentence without losing his train of thought. He lives largely in the past, in a haze of parliamentary anecdotes and family genealogy. Yet beneath the comedy, Feenix performs a crucial narrative function: he represents the moral vacuum at the heart of the Feenix family, the aristocratic indifference that allows Edith to be sold into [[marriage|marriage]] with [[Dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]]:

> Cousin Feenix was a man of so many words and so few ideas that it was difficult to gather from anything he said what his opinion really was — or whether he had one. [[[Leamington|leamington]] chapters]

## Edith's Inheritance

Edith Granger is a Feenix on her mother's side — a connection that [[Mrs. Skewton|mrs-skewton]] exploits relentlessly. The Feenix name is the family's last valuable asset, and it is deployed to attract suitors like Dombey who wish to purchase social cachet. Edith's tragedy is that she understands the transaction perfectly:

She knows that her beauty and her aristocratic connections are for sale, and that her mother — a Feenix by marriage — is the broker. The Feenix family's decline thus becomes the engine of one of the novel's central plots.

## The Aristocracy in Dickens

The Feenix family is part of Dickens's broader critique of the aristocracy in *[[Dombey and Son|dombey-and-son-firm]]*. Unlike the productive (if morally flawed) commercial world of Dombey's City, the Feenix world produces nothing — it merely consumes what remains of its inherited capital. The marriage of Edith (old name, no money) to Dombey (new money, no name) represents the alliance of two declining systems.
