# Doctor Blimber

## Overview

Doctor Blimber is the proprietor and headmaster of [[dr-blimbers-school|the Blimber establishment]], a [[Brighton|brighton]] academy where young gentlemen are subjected to a relentless regime of classical learning. With his characteristic gravity and [[love|love]] of the classics, he embodies Dickens's critique of an [[education|education]] system that prizes rote knowledge over genuine understanding.

## Character and Establishment

Doctor Blimber's academy is, in Dickens's description, "a great hot-house, in which there was a forcing apparatus incessantly at work" (030-010). The metaphor suggests an industrial approach to learning in which boys are treated as crops to be forced into premature ripeness. The atmosphere is one of relentless intellectual pressure, with every boy expected to absorb an impossible quantity of Latin, Greek, and other scholarly matter.

The Doctor's assistant, [[mr-feeder|[[Mr. Feeder|mr-feeder]], B.A.]], is described as "a kind of human barrel-organ" (030-023) who mechanically dispenses knowledge without genuine understanding — an extension of the Doctor's own pedagogical philosophy.

## Role in the Novel

When [[paul-dombey-jr|[[Paul Dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]] Junior]] is brought to the academy for an interview, Doctor Blimber's response is characteristically impersonal. Asked whether he is at home, the reply comes: '"Doctor Blimber is at home, I believe?"' (030-034). When the Doctor finally meets Paul, his reaction is brief and portentous: '"Ha!" said Doctor Blimber. "Shall we make a man of him?"' (031-007).

The question embodies the Doctor's educational philosophy: boys are raw material to be processed into men through the application of classical learning. For the delicate Paul, this regime proves too much. The Doctor's daughter [[cornelia-blimber|[[Cornelia|cornelia-blimber]] Blimber]], his wife [[mrs-blimber|Mrs. Blimber]], and Mr. Feeder all participate in the scholarly apparatus that eventually contributes to Paul's decline.
