# Time and Change

## Overview

*[[Dombey and Son|[[dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]]-and-son-firm]]* spans over a decade of narrative time, and the passage of time — its speed, its violence, its inexorability — is one of the novel's most insistent themes. Dickens sets the old world of sail and stagecoach against the new world of steam and railway, using temporal acceleration as both a fact of Victorian life and a metaphor for moral reckoning. [[The waves|the-sea-and-waves]] that Paul Jr. hears carry the novel's deepest meditation: time as an eternal rhythm indifferent to human will, yet somehow containing the possibility of meaning.

## Manifestation

[[The railway|the-railway]] is the novel's most powerful symbol of temporal change. It represents the compression of time — journeys that once took days now take hours — but also the violence of progress. [[Carker|james-carker]]'s [[death|death-and-mortality]] by train (152-010) is the most literal expression: time itself, in the form of the railway engine, destroys the novel's most calculating character. The railway also transforms [[Staggs's Gardens|staggs-gardens]] from a rural village to an urban slum (057-012), marking time's destructive passage on the physical landscape.

[[Solomon Gills's shop|solomon-gills-shop]], [[the Wooden Midshipman|the-wooden-midshipman]], is the counter-symbol: a place where time stands still, where nautical instruments sit unsold, where the old man waits years for his nephew's return (001-004). The tension between sail and steam — between Gills's world and the railway's world — is Dickens's way of registering the cost of progress.

> "The old [[Sol Gills|solomon-gills]], so long put away, came out of his retreat" (167-018)

Dombey's arc is structured around time's humbling. He begins believing himself master of time — he can plan his son's future, arrange his daughter's [[marriage|marriage]], control his firm's destiny. Time dismantles each of these pretensions systematically. His son dies young, his daughter marries against his will, his firm collapses. The novel's final image of Dombey — an old man in a chair, "silver-haired" (170-034), content with his grandchildren — registers time's ultimate gift: not mastery but acceptance.

The sea provides the novel's alternative temporality. Paul Jr. hears in the waves "the eternal something" (016-002) — a time that is not linear but cyclical, not measured by clocks but by tides. This oceanic time is associated with [[childhood|childhood]], [[memory|memory-and-the-past]], and death: the temporal modes that the railway cannot reach.

## Dickens's Treatment

Dickens registers time's passage with extraordinary sensitivity in this novel. He is not simply nostalgic for the pre-railway world (Solomon Gills's shop is, after all, failing commercially), nor is he a simple celebrant of progress (the railway kills Carker). His treatment is elegiac: change is real, [[loss|grief-and-loss]] is permanent, and yet time also makes possible the novel's redemptions — [[Florence|florence-dombey]]'s maturing [[love|love]], [[Walter|walter-gay]]'s return, Dombey's humbled second chance.
