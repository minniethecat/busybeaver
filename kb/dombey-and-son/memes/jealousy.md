# Jealousy

## Overview

Jealousy is one of the most psychologically acute themes in *[[Dombey and Son|dombey-and-son-firm]]*, explored primarily through [[Mr. Dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]]'s resentment of the love others receive — particularly the [[affection|love]] between [[Florence|florence-dombey]] and Paul Jr., and later between Florence and [[Edith|edith-dombey]]. Dickens portrays jealousy as a hidden passion that festers beneath Dombey's cold exterior, driving him to acts of cruelty he cannot himself explain.

## Manifestation

Dombey's jealousy appears early, interwoven with his [[pride|pride]]. He "viewed with so much bitterness" (004-034) the nurse [[Polly Richards|polly-richards]]'s affectionate bond with baby Paul — a jealousy born from his own emotional incapacity. He cannot love, and so he resents anyone who can. Dickens notes that there was "as sharp a jealousy of any second check or cross" (011-015) in Dombey's dealings with others, a possessiveness that extended even to his [[children|childhood]]'s affections.

The jealousy intensifies around Florence. Dombey is "disquieted by no jealousy of the many conferences enjoyed between Florence and Paul" (036-006) only in the sense that he refuses to acknowledge the emotion. When Florence becomes the object of Edith's affection and [[Walter|walter-gay]]'s love, Dombey's resentment hardens into cruelty. His "bitterness and jealousy" (038-030) toward his own daughter is one of the novel's most devastating psychological portraits.

The theme reaches its fullest expression in Dombey's rage at Edith. The narrator asks explicitly: "Did a mad jealousy and withered pride, poison sweet remembrances?" (053-008). Dickens answers through the plot: Dombey's jealousy of Edith's independence, her past, and her refusal to be owned drives him to employ [[Carker|james-carker]] as his spy, setting in motion the novel's tragic climax.

## Dickens's Treatment

Dickens's treatment of jealousy shows his deepening psychological sophistication as a novelist. Unlike the more theatrical jealousies of earlier Victorian fiction, Dombey's jealousy is cold, repressed, and all the more destructive for being denied. He cannot name the emotion, and so it rules him unchecked.

The novel presents jealousy as a symptom of a deeper disorder: the inability to give or receive love freely. Dombey's jealousy is ultimately a form of possessiveness — he treats people as property and is enraged when their affections go elsewhere. Only through his final humiliation and the unconditional love of Florence can this jealousy be dissolved. Dickens's insight — that jealousy is the jealous person's prison, not the beloved's — is one of the novel's most modern psychological observations.
