## Overview

[[Walter Gay|walter-gay]]'s departure for Barbados is a pivotal event in the early chapters of *[[Dombey and Son|dombey-and-son-firm]]*. [[Mr. Dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]], resenting Walter's kindness to [[Florence|florence-dombey]] and the boy's association with his son Paul, arranges to have him posted to a junior position in the firm's Barbados office — effectively an exile. The departure separates the novel's young lovers before their relationship can fully develop and sets the stage for Walter's presumed [[death|death-and-mortality]] and dramatic return.

## Dombey's Motive

Walter Gay works for Dombey and Son as a junior clerk in the counting-house, a position obtained through his uncle [[Solomon Gills|solomon-gills]]. When Dombey learns that Walter befriended Florence during her abduction ordeal and that Paul Jr. had grown fond of him, his [[jealousy|jealousy]] and [[class|class-and-society]]-consciousness are aroused. Walter — warm, open-hearted, and without fortune — represents everything Dombey distrusts.

Dombey summons Walter and delivers the news with characteristic coldness:

> "In the West Indies. At Barbados. I am going to send you," said Mr. Dombey. "You will be expected to fill a position of trust and confidence there." [015-005]

The promotion is, in reality, a removal — a way to put distance between Walter and Dombey's [[family|family-and-kinship]].

## The Parting

The news devastates Solomon Gills, the uncle who raised Walter as his own son. At [[the Wooden Midshipman|the-wooden-midshipman]], the little nautical instrument shop that serves as their home, the old man struggles to conceal his [[grief|grief-and-loss]]:

> "My dear boy," said Uncle Sol, "I have been thinking that I shall be very lonely without you." [016-008]

[[Captain Cuttle|captain-cuttle]], the loyal family friend, provides what comfort he can — offering his watch and his characteristic blend of nautical philosophy and simple kindness. The scene is one of Dickens's most affecting depictions of male friendship and familial [[love|love]].

Florence, still a child, learns of Walter's departure with a sorrow she does not fully understand. Their farewell — brief and constrained by propriety — plants the seed of the love that will eventually bring them together.

## At Sea and Beyond

Walter departs for Barbados. News eventually reaches England that his ship has been lost, and he is presumed dead. The grief that follows — particularly for Solomon Gills, who sets out on his own desperate search — deepens the novel's themes of [[separation|alienation-in-dombey-and-son]] and loss. Walter's eventual return, years later, is one of the novel's most satisfying reversals.

## Significance

Walter's departure is Dombey's sin of commission — unlike his sins of [[neglect|parental-neglect]] toward Florence, this is an active act of cruelty designed to preserve his sense of social order. It also serves the novel's plot mechanics: the separation-and-return pattern that Dickens perfected creates narrative suspense that spans hundreds of pages.
