# Paul's Christening

The christening of [[paul-dombey-jr|[[Paul Dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]] Jr.]] is one of the earliest and most memorable set-pieces in Charles Dickens's *[[Dombey and Son|dombey-and-son-firm]]*. Occupying much of chapters five and six, the event brings together the novel's principal characters for a ceremony that should celebrate new life and community but instead exposes the emotional sterility at the heart of the Dombey world.

## The Preparations

The christening is stage-managed by [[mrs-[[chick|mr-chick]]|Mrs. Chick]], Dombey's sister, who treats the event as an occasion for social display rather than spiritual significance. Her efforts are described with characteristic Dickensian irony:

> She has exerted herself so wonderfully in the preparations for his christening on the next day but one. [^1]

The exaggeration — "so wonderfully" — carries a satirical edge. [[Mrs. Chick|mrs-chick]]'s exertions are directed entirely toward the social spectacle of the occasion, not toward the child at its centre. The christening becomes, in her hands, an extension of the Dombey obsession with appearances and [[status|class-and-society]].

The question of godparents consumes an inordinate share of the preparations. Mrs. Chick is adamant that the godfathers must be persons of consequence:

> "Godfathers, of course," continued Mrs Chick, "are important in point of position; and you, my [[love|love]], are called upon to make a suitable choice." [^1]

The emphasis on "position" rather than character or spiritual fitness tells us everything about the world Dickens is anatomising. The christening is, from its inception, an exercise in social performance rather than religious meaning. The child at its centre — the soul whose spiritual life the ceremony is meant to inaugurate — is an afterthought.

## The Ceremony

The christening itself is rendered as a scene of acute discomfort, the solemnity of the religious ritual undermined by the awkwardness of the assembled party. Dickens emphasises the funereal atmosphere that hangs over what should be a joyful occasion:

> The chief difference between the christening party and a party in a [[mourning|grief-and-loss]] coach was that the former was more ghastly. [^2]

This juxtaposition of christening and funeral is thematically significant. Throughout the novel, events that should affirm life — births, marriages, ceremonies — are shadowed by [[death-and-[[mortality|death-and-mortality]]|death]]. Paul Jr.'s christening prefigures his early death, just as the cold formality of the occasion prefigures the emotional death that Dombey's [[pride|pride]] will inflict on his [[family|family-and-kinship]].

> The chill of Paul's christening had struck home, perhaps to some purpose. [^2]

Dickens's narrator is unusually direct here: the ceremony has consequences. The "chill" that settles over the christening is not merely atmospheric — it is a moral temperature, and it will shape the child's brief life.

## Florence's Exclusion

[[florence-dombey|[[Florence|florence-dombey]] Dombey]] is pointedly excluded from the christening, her father deeming her presence unnecessary to an event centred on his son and heir. The exclusion crystallises the novel's central wound — Dombey's [[parental-[[neglect|parental-neglect]]|neglect of his daughter]] — at a moment that should unite rather than divide the family:

> Her flushed face, and her new christening attire was very much disheveled. [^2]

Florence, watching from the margins, becomes a silent witness to her own erasure. The phrase "her new christening attire" is painfully ironic — she has dressed for an occasion she is not permitted to attend. The detail is small but devastating: even a child's dress becomes, in Dombey's world, an instrument of exclusion and humiliation.

## Social Satire

The christening operates as one of Dickens's great set-pieces of social satire. The assembled guests — including [[miss-tox|Miss Tox]], [[major-bagstock|Major Bagstock]], and a cast of minor dignitaries — perform their roles with a rigidity that exposes the emptiness of the social forms they observe. Dombey himself stands at the centre of the scene, remote from the human warmth of the occasion:

> Mr. Dombey was on one side, and [[Miss Tox|miss-tox]] (who stood confessed as the author of the "miserable night") on the other. [^2]

The christening feast that follows — the eating and drinking that should be an occasion of communal joy — is instead a grim exercise in form without feeling. The guests go through the motions of celebration with the same mechanical precision that characterises every other ritual of Dombey's world. No one, Dickens implies, is actually celebrating anything — least of all the child.

## Significance

As an early-novel event, Paul's christening establishes the emotional and thematic temperature of the world Dickens is anatomising. It introduces the pattern — repeated throughout the novel — in which rituals of connection become occasions of [[estrangement|alienation-in-dombey-and-son]]. The ceremony that should welcome Paul Jr. into a community of love instead confirms his place in a world governed by coldness and form.

The christening also functions as a structural premonition. The "chill" that Dickens describes will deepen as the novel progresses, culminating in the death of Paul Jr. and the long winter of Dombey's spiritual desolation. The christening, in this sense, is not merely a satirical set-piece but a tonal keynote — the novel's first sustained demonstration of how the Dombey world corrupts even the most sacred of human rituals.
