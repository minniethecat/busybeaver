# Mrs MacStinger

Mrs MacStinger is [[Captain Cuttle|captain-cuttle]]'s landlady in *[[Dombey and Son|[[dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]]-and-son-firm]]*, a woman of formidable temper and ferocious housekeeping habits. She is one of the novel's memorable comic characters, embodying the domestic tyranny that the soft-hearted Captain Cuttle is powerless to resist.

## Description

Mrs MacStinger is characterized by her fierce temper and her [[devotion|love]] to aggressive cleanliness. She is a woman and a mother, and Dickens presents her as a force of nature in the domestic sphere: "The feelings of Mrs MacStinger, as a woman and a mother, were outraged"[067-007] by any slight to her authority.

She presides over a household of "little MacStingers"[025-038], her [[children|childhood]], whose "moments of recreation" she vigilantly controls. Her door is a barrier that Captain Cuttle dreads to cross, and she is known to lie in wait for him: "Mrs MacStinger might be lying in wait below"[025-052].

## Role in the Novel

Mrs MacStinger first appears when [[Walter Gay|walter-gay]] visits Captain Cuttle at his lodgings. Walter must navigate the peril of her presence: "when Walter got downstairs, Mrs MacStinger glided out of the little back parlour"[025-054], intercepting him with her menacing politeness.

She treats the Captain's attempts at independence with contempt. When told that someone might "knock my door down," she responds dismissively: "said Mrs MacStinger, contemptuously, 'can get over it'"[025-039]. Her authority is absolute within her domain.

The Captain's fear of Mrs MacStinger is a recurring comic motif. He dreads her cleaning days: "on these occasions, Mrs MacStinger resorted to a great distance"[043-029]. His eventual escape from her household involves a tactical withdrawal: he plans "to get under weigh while Mrs MacStinger was still slumbering"[055-020], fleeing like a ship escaping a hostile port.

When the Captain later returns to visit, he encounters "one of Mrs [[MacStinger's|macstinger-boarding]] great cleaning days"[067-004], and the full force of her domestic fury is once again unleashed upon him. Her door remains a barrier of terror, "that worthy but redoubtable female"[067-006] standing guard over her scrubbed and polished fortress.

## Character

Mrs MacStinger belongs to the Dickensian tradition of formidable landladies — [[women|gender-and-womens-roles]] whose domestic power makes them figures of comic dread. Her cleaning days are campaigns, her door is a battlement, and her maternal feelings are weapons. The contrast between the fierce little landlady and the gentle, timid Captain Cuttle generates much of the comedy of the early chapters.
