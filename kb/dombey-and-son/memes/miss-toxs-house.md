# Miss Tox's House

## Overview

[[Miss Tox|miss-tox]]'s dwelling-place in [[Princess's Place|princesss-place]] receives its own chapter title in *[[Dombey and Son|[[dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]]-and-son-firm]]* — "A Bird's-eye Glimpse of Miss Tox's Dwelling-place: also of the State of Miss Tox's Affections" (Chapter VII) — making it one of the few private residences afforded extended descriptive treatment in the novel. The house is a shabby-genteel retreat in the West End of London, where Miss Tox lives on the fringes of fashion, sustained by the conviction that proximity to greatness confers [[status|class-and-society]].

## Description

Miss Tox inhabited "a dark little house that had been squeezed, at some remote period of English History, into a fashionable neighbourhood at the west end of the town, where it stood in the shade like a poor relation of the great street round the corner, coldly looked down upon by mighty mansions." [^1] The house sits in Princess's Place — "not exactly in a court, and it was not exactly in a yard; but it was in the dullest of No-Thoroughfares, rendered anxious and haggard by distant double knocks." [^1]

The atmosphere of the neighbourhood is one of faded gentility. Grass grows between the chinks in the stone pavement. Nearby stand Princess's Chapel, with its tinkling bell, and the Princess's Arms public house, much resorted to by footmen. A sedan chair sits permanently within the railing of the Arms, immemorial in its stillness. [^1]

Miss Tox's bedroom commands "a vista of Mews, where hostlers, at whatever sort of work engaged, were continually accompanying themselves with effervescent noises." [^1] The interior is furnished in the style of a bygone generation: a plate-warmer, an obsolete harpsichord, and the miniature portrait of a deceased relative with "a powdered head and a pigtail" — the owner of the "fishy eye in the locket" who bequeathed the house to her. [^1]

A prevailing mustiness pervades the house, and in warm weather Miss Tox may be seen "apparently writing in sundry chinks and crevices of the wainscot with the wrong end of a pen dipped in spirits of turpentine." [^1] The entry and staircase are perhaps the smallest and most inconvenient in England — but Miss Tox unfailingly says, "What a situation!" [^1]

## Role in the Novel

Princess's Place is the setting for Miss Tox's private life — her social aspirations, her friendship (and possible romantic interest) with [[Major Bagstock|major-bagstock]], and her quiet [[devotion|love]] to the Dombey [[family|family-and-kinship]] from a respectful distance. The house is the physical expression of Miss Tox's character: genteel, squeezed, overlooked by grandeur, yet sustained by the conviction that it is "well connected." [^1]

The retired butler and housekeeper who live in the other private house in Princess's Place let furnished apartments to a single gentleman — "a wooden-featured, blue-faced Major, with his eyes starting out of his head" — none other than Major Bagstock. [^1] Through the medium of the Major's "dark servant," Miss Tox and the Major exchange newspapers, pamphlets, and "such Platonic dalliance." [^1]

## Associated Characters

- [[miss-tox|Miss Tox]] — owner and inhabitant
- [[major-bagstock|Major Bagstock]] — neighbour in Princess's Place

## Themes

Miss Tox's house is a minor masterpiece of Dickensian place-portraiture. It is simultaneously comic and pathetic — a house defined by what it is not (grand, sunlit, convenient) and sustained by what it is near (the great street round the corner). In its careful rendering of genteel poverty, it prefigures the boarding-house world of *Martin Chuzzlewit* and the shabby-genteel interiors of *Bleak House*.
