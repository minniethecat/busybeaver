# Solomon Gills

## Overview

Solomon Gills — "Uncle Sol" to [[Walter Gay|walter-gay]] — is the proprietor of [[the Wooden Midshipman|the-wooden-midshipman]], a nautical instrument shop that serves as one of the novel's most important symbolic locations. Against [[Dombey's|[[dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]]-and-son-firm]] world of cold [[commerce|money-and-commerce]] and proud [[ambition|ambition]], Gills represents a different kind of trade: modest, honest, and rooted in personal bonds rather than profit. His long vigil for Walter's return from sea — extending across years and sections of the novel — is one of Dickens's most affecting portraits of patient hope.

## Biography

Solomon Gills first appears in Section 4 at the Wooden Midshipman, his shop of nautical instruments — "a little timber midshipman in a blue coat and cocked hat" standing outside as its sign (004-033). The shop is failing commercially, its instruments gathering dust in an age of steam that has little use for the tools of sail. Gills himself is an anachronism: a man of the old maritime world whose knowledge and values are being rendered obsolete by [[the railway|the-railway]] age.

> "The old Sol Gills, so long put away, came out of his retreat" (167-018)

Gills is Walter Gay's uncle and guardian, and his [[love|love]] for his nephew is the emotional center of his life. When Walter is sent abroad by Mr. Dombey, Gills sets out on his own quest — traveling in search of news, unable to bear the waiting in one place. He entrusts the Wooden Midshipman to [[Captain Cuttle|captain-cuttle]], leaving behind a letter that reveals his desperate hope: that Walter will return, and that the shop — the symbol of their shared life — will be preserved.

His eventual return in the novel's final sections is a moment of profound resolution. The "old Sol Gills" who emerges from his long retreat is aged but undiminished in spirit. His reunion with Walter, his meeting with [[Florence|florence-dombey]], and his quiet contentment in the novel's closing pages affirm the values he has always represented: love over commerce, patience over ambition, hope over calculation.

## Others' View of Gills

- **[[Walter Gay]]'s perspective**: To Walter, Uncle Sol is everything — father, mentor, and protector. Walter's devotion to Gills is expressed not in grand declarations but in small acts of care and concern. When Walter prospers in the novel's second half, his first thought is for the uncle who raised him.
- **[[Captain Cuttle]]'s perspective**: Cuttle's friendship with Gills is one of the novel's purest bonds — two old sailors whose trust in each other is absolute. Cuttle guards the Wooden Midshipman in Gills's absence with the devotion of a brother-in-arms, and his joy at Gills's return is among the novel's most moving moments.
- **The narrator's perspective**: Dickens presents Gills with uncomplicated affection. There is no irony in the portrait of the old instrument-maker — only warmth, respect, and a gentle melancholy for a world that is passing away.

## Authorial Technique

Dickens uses the Wooden Midshipman shop as an extended metaphor for Gills himself. Both are "[[old-fashioned|memory-and-the-past]]," both belong to the age of sail rather than steam, and both survive through patience and loyalty rather than commercial success. The shop's nautical instruments — compasses, sextants, chronometers — are tools for navigation, and Gills's function in the novel is navigational too: he provides the moral bearings by which Walter, Cuttle, and ultimately Florence orient themselves.

The name "the Wooden Midshipman" carries symbolic weight. A midshipman is a naval officer in training — young, aspiring, not yet arrived. The shop's name suggests a world of hope and becoming, and it is fitting that it becomes the haven for the novel's young lovers. Gills, the old man who tends this monument to [[youth|childhood]] and possibility, is the novel's guardian of hope.

## Relationships

- **[[Walter Gay]]** — His nephew and adopted son; their bond is the novel's model of familial love untainted by commerce or [[pride|pride]]
- **[[Captain Cuttle]]** — His oldest friend; their friendship, built on years of shared maritime [[experience|innocence-and-experience]], represents a masculine bond of pure loyalty that the novel sets against Dombey's transactional relationships
- **[[Florence Dombey]]** — She finds refuge at the Wooden Midshipman, and Gills welcomes her as [[family|family-and-kinship]]; their connection bridges the novel's two moral worlds

## Significance

Solomon Gills is Dickens's answer to Dombey — the good merchant against the proud one. Where Dombey's commerce is cold, competitive, and ultimately self-destructive, Gills's trade is modest, personal, and enduring. The Wooden Midshipman may be failing as a business, but it succeeds as something more important: a home, a sanctuary, a place where love and loyalty are the only currency that matters. In a novel that chronicles the collapse of a great commercial house, the survival of the little shop — and the old man who tends it — is Dickens's quiet statement about what truly lasts.
