# Dombey's Second Marriage

## Overview

The second marriage of [[paul-dombey|Paul Dombey Sr.]] to [[edith-dombey|Edith Granger]], orchestrated by [[mrs-skewton|Mrs. Skewton]] and [[major-bagstock|Major Bagstock]] at [[leamington|Leamington Spa]], is one of the pivotal events in *Dombey and Son*. It represents the convergence of [[pride]], [[money-and-commerce|money]], and [[class-and-society|social ambition]] in a union that is doomed from its inception. Neither party enters the marriage with affection: Dombey seeks a beautiful, statuesque ornament for [[dombey-house|Dombey House]] and a fitting mother-figure to preside over his table; Edith, a proud widow embittered by a lifetime of being marketed by her mother, enters the arrangement with cold resignation and concealed contempt for the transaction. The wedding itself, spanning Sections 86 through 88, is rendered by Dickens with devastating irony — a spectacle of mercenary alliance dressed in bridal finery. The marriage's swift collapse — culminating in [[ediths-rebellion|Edith's rebellion]] and [[dombeys-bankruptcy|Dombey's bankruptcy]] — constitutes the novel's central crisis and moral reckoning.

## Context

### The Courtship

The courtship unfolds in [[leamington|Leamington]] and [[brighton|Brighton]], where [[Major Bagstock|major-bagstock]], the blustering matchmaker, introduces [[Dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]] to [[Mrs. Skewton|mrs-skewton]] and her daughter [[Edith|edith-dombey]]. Mrs. Skewton — a painted, simpering widow who addresses her grown daughter in infantile diminutives — has been marketing Edith's beauty for decades. The Major describes Edith to Dombey in purely commercial terms:

> "He died, Sir, in the second year of his [[marriage|marriage]]." The Major ran the representative of the deceased Granger through and through the body with the walking-stick he carried.

-- Charles Dickens, *[[Dombey and Son|dombey-and-son-firm]]*, PN 059-062

Dombey, for his part, sees in Edith a woman whose "haughtiness and coldness" mirror his own. Their [[pride|pride]] recognizes itself in the other, and Dombey flatters himself that Edith's formidable will defers to his:

> It flattered him to think how these deferred to him, in Edith's case, and seemed to have no will apart from his. It flattered him to picture to himself, this proud and stately woman doing the honours of his house, and chilling his guests after his own manner. The dignity of Dombey and Son would be heightened and maintained, indeed, in such hands.

-- Charles Dickens, *Dombey and Son*, PN 086-031

### The Wedding Preparations

The week before the wedding, chronicled in Section 86, is a blur of commercial activity — milliners, jewellers, lawyers, florists, pastry-cooks — in which Edith takes no interest. She looks at nothing, cares for nothing, and allows her mother to manage every detail. The tension between mother and daughter is palpable, with Edith's contempt barely concealed beneath a surface of icy compliance.

### The Wedding Day

The wedding day (Sections 87-88) is rendered as a grotesque carnival. [[The Dombey household|the-dombey-household]] stirs before dawn; the pastry-cook labors in the "funereal room"; the bell-ringers, marrow-bone players, and a brass band all converge on the event. [[Mr. Toots|toots]] dresses "as if he were at least the Bridegroom" to watch from a secret corner of the gallery. [[Cousin Feenix|cousin-feenix]], Edith's befuddled aristocratic relative, nearly disrupts the ceremony with an inappropriate joke. Dickens's satire is merciless:

> The men who play the bells have got scent of the marriage; and the marrow-bones and cleavers too; and a brass band too. The first, are practising in a back settlement near Battlebridge; the second, put themselves in communication, through their chief, with Mr [[Towlinson|mr-towlinson]], to whom they offer terms to be bought off; and the third, in the person of an artful trombone, lurks and dodges round the corner, waiting for some traitor tradesman to reveal the place and hour of breakfast, for a bribe.

-- Charles Dickens, *Dombey and Son*, PN 088-004

### The Marriage Itself

After a Paris honeymoon, the Dombey marriage rapidly deteriorates. At home in Dombey House, the couple's mutual coldness curdles into open hostility. Dombey attempts to assert his will over Edith; she, having bartered her freedom for wealth and position, refuses to be subjugated. The battle of pride reaches its crisis at a dinner party where Dombey, through his manager [[james-carker|James Carker]], instructs Edith that she must be "more deferential." This humiliation triggers [[ediths-rebellion|Edith's rebellion]] — she flees Dombey House with Carker, not for love but to destroy both men through scandal. The collapse of the marriage precipitates [[dombeys-bankruptcy|Dombey's bankruptcy]] and the dissolution of [[dombey-and-son-firm|Dombey and Son]].

## Key Characters

- [[paul-dombey|Paul Dombey Sr.]] — The bridegroom: proud, cold, emotionally incapable of love; seeks a wife as he would acquire a piece of property
- [[edith-dombey|Edith Dombey (nee Granger)]] — The bride: proud, embittered, beautiful; enters the marriage knowing it is a transaction and intending to exact its price
- [[mrs-skewton|Mrs. Skewton]] — The mother: a painted relic of the Regency, who has spent decades marketing her daughter's beauty; her infantile affection masks mercenary calculation
- [[major-bagstock|Major Bagstock]] — The matchmaker: a blustering, self-important retired officer who brings the parties together for his own social advantage
- [[florence-dombey|Florence Dombey]] — The innocent witness: included in the wedding party as a pawn in Mrs. Skewton's manipulations, her presence troubles Dombey's conscience
- [[james-carker|James Carker]] — The catalyst of destruction: Dombey's manager, whose role as intermediary in the marital power struggle precipitates the catastrophe
- Cousin Feenix — Edith's aristocratic relation: well-meaning but befuddled, his comic interventions at the wedding underscore the absurdity of the proceedings

## Significance

The second marriage is the structural hinge of *Dombey and Son*. It gathers the novel's major themes — [[pride]], [[commerce|money-and-commerce]], [[gender|gender-and-womens-roles]], [[class-and-society|class]], and the failure of human connection — into a single catastrophic event. Dickens uses the marriage to dramatize his critique of Victorian social arrangements: when a union is founded on money rather than affection, it becomes a prison for both parties. Dombey and Edith are mirror images — each too proud to bend, each viewing the other as an instrument of their own will. Their mutual destruction is inevitable, and from its ruins the novel's redemptive arc — Florence's love, Dombey's humbling, and the eventual [[dombeys-reconciliation|reconciliation]] — must be built.

The marriage also serves as Dickens's most sustained examination of the Victorian "marriage market." Mrs. Skewton, with her grotesque cosmetics and infantile endearments, embodies the corruption of maternal love by commercial instinct. Edith's tragedy is that she has internalized the market's logic: she knows her worth and despises those who would buy her, yet has no language for love or tenderness. Her rebellion, when it comes, is the novel's most powerful assertion of female agency — destructive, self-immolating, but unmistakably her own.
