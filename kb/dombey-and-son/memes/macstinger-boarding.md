## Overview

Mrs. MacStinger's boarding house is one of Dickens's great comic institutions — a supposedly respectable establishment where Captain [[Edward Cuttle|captain-cuttle]] lodges under a regime of domestic terror. Mrs. MacStinger, the landlady, rules her house with an iron will, and the Captain — for all his nautical bluster — lives in perpetual fear of her displeasure.

## The Formidable Mrs. MacStinger

Mrs. MacStinger is one of Dickens's most memorable minor characters. A widow of fierce propriety and fiercer temper, she runs her boarding house as a strict moral institution. Her cleanliness is aggressive, her expectations of her lodgers impossibly high, and her wrath — when provoked — is biblical in its intensity:

> Mrs. MacStinger was a woman of a lofty and independent spirit, who considered that the Captain, in presuming to lodge under her roof, had entered into an implied contract to be respectable, punctual in his payments, sober in his habits, and — above all — quiet. [Docklands chapters]

Captain Cuttle, despite his imposing physical presence and his repertoire of nautical maxims, is reduced to a state of anxious compliance. He tiptoes through [[the house|[[dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]]-and-son-firm]], dreads the sound of Mrs. MacStinger's footsteps on the stairs, and regards her with a mixture of terror and reluctant admiration.

## Captain Cuttle's Domestic Life

The boarding house provides the setting for some of the novel's most purely comic scenes. Captain Cuttle's attempts to entertain guests without disturbing the landlady, his elaborate precautions against detection, and his eventual expulsion from the establishment are rendered with Dickens's characteristic blend of farce and pathos.

The Captain's relationship with Mrs. MacStinger also serves as a comic counterpoint to the novel's more serious domestic arrangements. Where Dombey's household is cold and formal, MacStinger's is hot with passion — a different kind of tyranny, but one that is, in its way, more human.

## Significance

Mrs. MacStinger's boarding house is part of Dickens's comprehensive portrait of domestic life in Victorian England. It represents the lower-middle-[[class|class-and-society]] lodging house — a world of narrow stairs, thin walls, and fierce respectability — that stood between the poverty of the streets and the comfort of the middle-class home. For Captain Cuttle, the sailor ashore, it is the closest thing to a home he has.
