# Cousin Feenix

Cousin Feenix is a member of the aristocratic Feenix [[family|family-and-kinship]] in *[[Dombey and Son|[[dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]]-and-son-firm]]*, the cousin of [[Edith Dombey|edith-dombey]] (née Granger). He is a good-natured but vague and "dim" nobleman who gives Edith away at her wedding to Mr Dombey.

## Description

Cousin Feenix is presented as a faded aristocrat, a member of the "Feenix brood"[085-020] whose family seat is occupied by another relative. He has been "over from abroad"[088-013] for the wedding, suggesting a life of genteel wandering. Dickens describes him as dim and indistinct: "very dim, indeed,"[088-014] he appears at the wedding, a figure whose noble name is more substantial than his presence.

His rooms reflect a faded grandeur: a room that "had gradually settled, in the course of years, into a dark brown colour"[086-033], long occupied by a member of [[the Feenix family|feenix-family]].

## Role in the Novel

Cousin Feenix first enters the story as a point of reference for Edith's aristocratic connections. Mrs Skewton, Edith's mother, "is sister to the late Lord Feenix, and aunt to the present Lord"[059-059], establishing the family's noble lineage.

When Mr Dombey seeks to marry Edith, Cousin Feenix is summoned to give the bride away. He arrives from abroad for the occasion. At the wedding ceremony, "Cousin Feenix does that"[089-020] — performs the [[duty|duty-and-obligation]] of giving away the bride — having come from [[Baden-Baden|baden-baden]] for the purpose.

His good nature is evident in his remarks at the wedding breakfast. "Confound it," Cousin Feenix says, "when we do get a rich City fellow into the family, let me tell you"[089-021], expressing with candour the aristocratic family's mercenary interest in the match. He is described as a "good-natured creature"[089-021], and his frankness about the financial motivation behind the [[marriage|marriage]] provides a moment of uncomfortable honesty amid the formal proceedings.

Cousin Feenix signs the register last: "All the party sign; Cousin Feenix last; who puts his noble name"[089-031] to the document, his aristocratic signature lending its weight to the union.

## Character

Cousin Feenix represents the decayed aristocracy that Dickens often portrays — good-natured, honourable in a vague way, but insubstantial and financially dependent on alliances with the commercial classes. His "dimness" is both physical and metaphorical: he is a fading remnant of a [[class|class-and-society]] whose power is being supplanted by men like Mr Dombey.

His frank admission that the family values the match for its [[wealth|money-and-commerce]] — "a rich City fellow" — strips away the pretense of romance from the marriage, revealing the transaction at its heart. Yet he is not malicious; he is simply too vague and good-natured to maintain the fiction.
