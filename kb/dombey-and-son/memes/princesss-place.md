# Princess's Place

Princess's Place is a London street that serves as the location of [[mrs-pipchins|[[Mrs. Pipchin|mrs-pipchin]]'s Establishment]], the boarding house where the young [[paul-dombey-jr|[[Paul Dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]] Jr.]] is sent for the benefit of his health — and where, later, [[florence-dombey|[[Florence|florence-dombey]] Dombey]] lodges during a period of [[neglect|parental-neglect]] and exile from her father's house.

## Location and Character

Princess's Place is one of those London locations — like [[staggs-gardens|Staggs's Gardens]] or [[brig-place|Brig Place]] — that Dickens invests with a distinct atmospheric character. It is not a grand address; it belongs to the middling, respectable London of boarding houses and retired professionals, a world away from the opulent Dombey mansion. [[Mrs. Pipchin's establishment|mrs-pipchins]] here is described in terms that emphasise its austerity: the rooms are dark, the furnishings are hard, and the general atmosphere is one of rigid, joyless discipline. The name "Princess's Place" is itself an ironic touch, given the decidedly un-regal character of the Pipchin regime.

## Paul Dombey Jr.'s Residence

Paul Dombey Jr. is sent to Mrs. Pipchin's at Princess's Place during his early [[childhood|childhood]], as part of [[paul-dombey|Mr. Dombey]]'s attempt to strengthen the boy's constitution — and, more significantly, to remove him from the softening influence of Florence. During his [[time|time-and-change]] at Princess's Place, Paul forms his characteristic habits of dreamy observation and precocious melancholy. The Pipchin establishment, with its dark rooms and its formidable mistress, becomes one of the settings for Paul's gradual physical decline — the "[[old-fashioned|memory-and-the-past]]" child wasting away in an environment as hard and unyielding as the worldview of the father who placed him there.

> Mrs. Pipchin's establishment in Princess's Place was not a cheerful house. The rooms were dark, the staircases were dark, and the very atmosphere was dark. — The oppressive atmosphere of the place mirrors the emotional deprivation it represents.

## Florence's Exile

Later in the novel, after [[paul-dombey-jr|Paul's]] [[death|death-and-mortality]] and during the period of Mr. [[Dombey's|dombey-and-son-firm]] increasing [[estrangement|alienation-in-dombey-and-son]] from his daughter, Florence is sent to lodge at Princess's Place — an effective banishment from her father's house. Here she passes a period of isolation, her only comfort coming from her dog [[diogenes|Diogenes]] and the occasional visits of [[susan-nipper|Susan Nipper]]. The return to Princess's Place, the site of her brother's decline, is a cruel symmetry: Florence, like Paul before her, is consigned to this dour establishment because [[the Dombey household|the-dombey-household]] cannot accommodate the inconvenient demands of [[love|love]].

## Significance

Princess's Place functions in the novel as an architectural counterpoint to [[the Dombey mansion|dombey-house]]. Where the Dombey house is cold because it is grand — its marble halls and formal chambers repelling warmth — Princess's Place is cold because it is pinched, mean, and disciplinary. Both environments are inimical to childhood and to love, but they represent opposite ends of the social spectrum: the coldness of [[wealth|money-and-commerce]] and the coldness of its absence. In this sense, Princess's Place is one of several locations in the novel that embody Dickens's critique of a society that organises space — and human life — according to commercial rather than emotional principles.
