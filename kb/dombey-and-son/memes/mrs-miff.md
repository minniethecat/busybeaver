# Mrs Miff

Mrs Miff is the pew-opener at the church where significant marriages in *[[Dombey and Son|[[dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]]-and-son-firm]]* take place. She is a minor but vividly drawn character, appearing first at Mr Dombey's wedding to [[Edith|edith-dombey]] and later at the [[marriage|marriage]] of [[Florence Dombey|florence-dombey]] and [[Walter Gay|walter-gay]].

## Description

Mrs Miff is introduced as "the wheezy little pew-opener" with "a vinegary face" and "a mortified bonnet"[087-042]. Her physical description suggests a woman worn by her occupation and years of service to the church. She moves with a "wheezing little way" that Dickens uses to characterize her throughout both marriage scenes.

## Role in the Novel

### Dombey's Second Marriage

Mrs Miff is busy on the morning of Mr Dombey's wedding to Edith, making preparations at the church. She interacts with [[Mr Sownds the Beadle|mr-sownds]], and the two church officials observe the ceremony together. When Mr Dombey arrives, "Mrs Miff curtseys and proposes chairs"[088-054], performing her duties with professional deference.

During the ceremony, Mrs Miff positions herself to observe the proceedings. At the conclusion of the service, "Mrs Miff, and Mr Sownds the Beadle, sit upon the steps to rest"[089-047], having completed their duties for the wealthy couple.

### Florence's Wedding

Mrs Miff reappears near the end of the novel at the church for Florence's wedding to Walter Gay. "Mr Sownds the beadle, and Mrs Miff the pew-opener, are early at their posts"[158-071], once again serving as the church officials for a marriage ceremony. Her presence at both weddings provides a subtle continuity, linking the unhappy first marriage of Mr Dombey to the joyful union of his daughter.

## Character

Mrs Miff's personality is conveyed through her speech and manner. She is described as less liberal than the beadle, "but then he is not a pew-opener"[159-004], suggesting the parsimony that her modest occupation imposes. Her "mortified bonnet" and vinegary expression paint the picture of a woman whose life has been spent in the service of weddings and funerals, observing the great events of others from the margins.
