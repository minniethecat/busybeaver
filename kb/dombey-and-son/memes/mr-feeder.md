# Mr. Feeder

## Overview

Mr. Feeder, B.A., is [[Doctor Blimber|dr-blimber]]'s assistant master at the [[Brighton|brighton]] academy where [[Paul Dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]] Junior and [[Mr. Toots|toots]] are educated. With his habit of yawning on "such a great scale" (PN 032-040) and his cheerful pedantry, he serves as comic relief in the schoolroom scenes. By the novel's close, he emerges as a loyal friend and eventually marries [[Cornelia Blimber|cornelia-blimber]].

## Biography

Mr. Feeder first appears in the classroom at Doctor Blimber's establishment, "sitting at another little desk" (PN 032-032) and assisting in the relentless cramming of young minds. His defining characteristic is his yawning, performed with such vigour that Paul is "quite alarmed" (PN 032-040). He assists the Doctor in the daily ritual of classical interrogation: "It is remarkable, Mr Feeder, that the Romans—" (PN 033-009).

His relationship with [[toots|Mr. Toots]] evolves from master-pupil to genuine friendship. Years after Toots leaves the school, Mr. Feeder remains a fixture at Doctor Blimber's, and when Toots returns in the novel's final chapters, their bond is rekindled. At Toots's wedding to [[Susan Nipper|susan-nipper]], Mr. Feeder serves in the wedding party, "scarcely less" happy than the principal guests (PN 62869).

In the novel's denouement, Mr. Feeder marries [[cornelia-blimber|Cornelia Blimber]], suggesting that the dusty academic world of [[the Blimber establishment|the-blimber-establishment]] continues, softened by human [[affection|love]].

## Relationships

Mr. Feeder's most important connection is with [[toots|Mr. Toots]], whose simple-hearted friendship he values. As Doctor Blimber's assistant, he occupies a middle ground between the tyrannical curriculum of [[dr-blimber|Doctor Blimber]] and the bewildered pupils. His [[marriage|marriage]] to [[cornelia-blimber|Cornelia Blimber]] ties him permanently to the Blimber establishment, a comic resolution that Dickens handles with affection rather than satire.
