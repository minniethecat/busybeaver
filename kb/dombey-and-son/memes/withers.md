# Withers

Withers is the page and manservant employed by [[mrs-skewton|Mrs. Skewton]] — the ageing, painted coquette known to her circle as "Cleopatra" — during the middle portion of *[[Dombey and Son|[[dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]]-and-son-firm]]*. A minor figure in the novel's cast, Withers nonetheless provides a window into the servant [[class|class-and-society]] that attends upon Dickens's wealthy characters, and his presence in key scenes illuminates the performative nature of [[Mrs. Skewton|mrs-skewton]]'s social world.

## Position and Duties

As Mrs. Skewton's page, Withers occupies a distinct niche in the household hierarchy. He is not a senior servant — not a butler or housekeeper — but a page, a role that combines elements of footman, messenger, and personal attendant. In the Skewton establishment, his duties include announcing visitors, escorting his mistress, and attending upon her during social calls. His presence at [[royal-hotel-[[leamington|leamington]]|Leamington]] during Dombey's courtship of [[edith-dombey|[[Edith|edith-dombey]] Granger]] places him at the periphery of one of the novel's crucial narrative sequences.

## The Servant's Perspective

Dickens frequently uses servants as witnesses to the follies and vices of their employers, and Withers fits this pattern. He is present during Mrs. Skewton's elaborate toilette — the rituals of paint and artifice by which the ageing Cleopatra attempts to preserve the [[illusion|appearance-vs-reality]] of [[youth|childhood]] — and his silent observation carries an implicit commentary. When Mrs. Skewton, propped up by Withers and her maid, makes her halting [[progress|time-and-change]] through social engagements, the page's physical support becomes a metaphor for the artificiality of the social performance he serves.

> Withers, the page, was a living witness to the Cleopatra's arts. — The servant as silent observer of his mistress's theatrical self-presentation.

## Key Appearances

Withers appears most prominently in the Leamington chapters (Chapters 21–28), where Mrs. Skewton and [[Major Bagstock|major-bagstock]] engineer the match between [[paul-dombey|Mr. Dombey]] and Edith. He is present at the [[Royal Hotel|royal-hotel-leamington]] and during the social calls that surround the courtship. His appearances taper off after Mrs. Skewton's decline and [[death|death-and-mortality]], though he is visible in the London chapters that follow, still attending upon what remains of the Skewton establishment.

## Significance

Though Withers speaks few lines and occupies no significant plot function, his presence — like that of [[mr-[[towlinson|mr-towlinson]]|Mr. Towlinson]] in [[the Dombey household|the-dombey-household]] or the footmen who populate the novel's social scenes — contributes to the rich texture of domestic service that Dickens weaves through the narrative. The novel's vision of Victorian society is as much about those who wait at table and answer doors as it is about those who sit at the head of the table and give orders. In this economy of observation and service, Withers is one of the many eyes through which the reader glimpses the inner workings of [[wealth|money-and-commerce]] and its discontents.
