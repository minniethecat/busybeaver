# Sir Barnet Skettles

## Overview

[[Sir Barnet|sir-barnet]] Skettles is a baronet, Member of Parliament, and resident of a "pretty villa at Fulham, on the banks of the Thames" (PN 069-025). His defining trait is a relentless sociability summed up by the narrator:

> Sir Barnet's object in life was constantly to extend the range of his acquaintance. Like a heavy body dropped into water — not to disparage so worthy a gentleman by the comparison — it was in the nature of things that Sir Barnet must spread an ever widening circle about him, until there was nothing left to spread. [PN 069-027]

He appears first at [[Doctor Blimber|dr-blimber]]'s school party, later hosts [[Florence [[Dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]]|florence-dombey]] at Fulham, and ends the novel with a diplomatic appointment earned by his "popular manners" (PN 174-010).

## Biography

Sir Barnet Skettles makes his first appearance at the Blimber party with his wife [[Lady Skettles|lady-skettles]] and their son Master Skettles, a prospective new boy. On [[learning|education]] that Paul Dombey Junior is the son of the great Mr. Dombey, Sir Barnet immediately interposes: he has "had the honour of meeting Paul's father at a public dinner" and sends his "best compliments" (PN 041-048). He tells Lady Skettles in an aside: "City—very rich—most respectable—Doctor mentioned it" (PN 041-049), revealing the social calculus that governs his attentions.

His most revealing scene comes in conversation with [[Mr. Baps|mr-baps]] the dancing-master, whom Sir Barnet mistakes for a political economist. Sir Barnet "had much to say upon the question, and said it" (PN 042-014) of raw materials and gold. When Doctor Blimber reveals that Baps is merely the dancing-master, Sir Barnet's admiration turns instantly to fury: he "flew into a perfect rage, and glowered at Mr Baps over on the other side of the room" and damned him for "his most con-sum-mate and con-foun-ded impudence" (PN 042-029). The episode lays bare Sir Barnet's snobbery — a man who talks like a gentleman of affairs is an imposture if he is only a dancing-master.

Dickens provides Sir Barnet's definitive portrait in the Fulham chapters:

> He expressed his personal consequence chiefly through an antique gold snuffbox, and a ponderous silk pocket-kerchief, which he had an imposing manner of drawing out of his pocket like a banner and displaying at full length in the air, as an ensign of greatness. [PN 069-026]

His social [[ambition|ambition]] is described through an extended simile comparing him to a heavy body dropped into water that must "spread an ever widening circle about him" (PN 069-027), and his "voyage of discovery through the social system" is likened to a sound wave travelling "for ever through the interminable fields of space" (PN 069-027). His particular pleasure is "making people acquainted with people" (PN 069-028) — he will claim to know anyone a guest wishes to meet, even a complete stranger, and immediately engineer an introduction.

Sir Barnet hosts Florence at Fulham after Paul's [[death|death-and-mortality]], treating her with genuine kindness and repeatedly sending his compliments to her father. At her departure he calls her "the light of our house" (PN 081-010). The novel's close rewards his social gifts:

> His popular manners had obtained him a diplomatic appointment, the honours of which were discharged by himself and Lady Skettles, to the satisfaction even of their own countrymen and countrywomen: which was considered almost miraculous. [PN 174-010]

## Character Analysis

Sir Barnet Skettles is one of Dickens's comic social types — the compulsive networker whose identity dissolves into his acquaintance list. His "object in life" (PN 069-027) is entirely external: he measures himself by whom he knows, not what he is. The narrator's mock-heroic similes (the heavy body, the sound wave) expose the emptiness of this project while maintaining an amused [[affection|love]] for its subject.

His treatment of Mr. Baps is the key to his character. Sir Barnet's fury at being deceived by a dancing-master reveals the [[class|class-and-society]] anxiety beneath his geniality: talk that sounds like "gentleman's talk" must come from a gentleman, or the whole social order is threatened. Yet Dickens does not make him a villain — his kindness to Florence is genuine, and his diplomatic appointment at the novel's close is presented as a fitting reward rather than a satire.

## Relationships

Sir Barnet is inseparable from his wife [[lady-skettles|Lady Skettles]], with whom he forms "very good people" (PN 069-025) and a "worthy couple" (PN 071-039). His son Master Skettles (later called Barnet Junior) attends Doctor Blimber's school. His attentions to [[florence-dombey|Florence Dombey]] are a mixture of genuine kindness and social calculation — she represents a connection to the wealthy Mr. Dombey. His comic foil is [[mr-baps|Mr. Baps]], the dancing-master whose pretensions to gentlemanly conversation expose Sir Barnet's own intellectual pretensions.
