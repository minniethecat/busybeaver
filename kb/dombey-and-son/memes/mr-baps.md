# Mr. Baps

## Overview

Mr. Baps is the dancing-master at [[Doctor Blimber|dr-blimber]]'s [[Brighton|brighton]] academy — or, in the Doctor's characteristically inflated phrase, "our Professor of dancing" (PN 042-027). He appears solely at the evening party before the midsummer holidays, where his grave demeanour and habit of asking a single rote question about political economy expose the rigid [[class|class-and-society]] prejudices of Blimber's titled guests.

> He was a very grave gentleman, with a slow and measured manner of speaking; and before he had stood five minutes in the room, he began to talk to Toots (who had been silently comparing themselves with the company that was about them) of what you were to do with your raw materials when they came into your ports in return for your drain of gold. [PN 041-022–023]

## Biography

Mr. Baps arrives at the Blimber party "accompanied by Mrs Baps, to whom [[Mrs Blimber|mrs-blimber]] was extremely kind and condescending" (PN 041-021), the adverb marking the Bapses' subordinate social station. He poses his signature economic question identically to both [[Mr. Toots|toots]] — who memorably suggests "Cook 'em" (PN 041-024) — and later to [[Sir Barnet Skettles|sir-barnet-skettles]].

When [[Sir Barnet|sir-barnet]], impressed by the economic-sounding talk, asks Doctor Blimber whether Baps has "ever been in the Board of [[Trade|money-and-commerce]]" (PN 042-015), the Doctor reveals that Baps is merely the dancing-master. The revelation transforms the scene:

> This quite altered Sir Barnet Skettles's opinion of Mr Baps. It might have done so more, but that Sir Barnet had had so much to say upon the question, and had said it; which was a great relief to him. He flew into a perfect rage, and glowered at Mr Baps over on the other side of the room. He even prevailed upon himself to intimate to a few gentlemen near him, what his most con-sum-mate and con-foun-ded impudence was. [PN 042-029]

The comedy turns on class snobbery — Sir Barnet was prepared to respect Baps as an expert on trade, but a dancing-master who presumes to discuss political economy is an outrage.

Mrs. Baps spends the evening largely neglected, "pretending to look over the music-book of the gentleman who played the harp" (PN 042-018). At the close of the party, she takes leave of [[Paul|paul-dombey-jr]] "quite as heartily as anybody in the room" (PN 042-040), prompting the narrator's sympathetic "poor Mrs Baps!"

## Character Analysis

Mr. Baps is one of Dickens's minor comic grotesques — a character defined by a single verbal tic (the raw-materials question) that he deploys without variation, as if it were a calling card. His "grave" and "measured" manner suggests a man who has learned gentlemanly deportment by rote, just as his pupils learn dance steps. He is harmless, yet his presence at the Blimber party acts as a reagent: Sir Barnet's fury at being "deceived" by a dancing-master reveals the brittleness of the class system that structures the school's social world. The Doctor's euphemism "Professor of dancing" is itself a form of the same pretension that Baps is punished for — a minor character who illuminates a major theme.

## Relationships

Mr. Baps is employed by [[dr-blimber|Doctor Blimber]], who vouches that he is "a very worthy sort of man" (PN 042-027). His comic function is amplified by his encounters with [[toots|Mr. Toots]] — whose "Cook 'em" response is pure Toots — and with [[sir-barnet-skettles|Sir Barnet Skettles]], whose snobbery provides the scene's satirical point. His wife Mrs. Baps shares his subordinate social position, treated kindly but condescendingly by Mrs. Blimber.
