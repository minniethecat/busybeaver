# Mr. Perch

Mr. Perch is the messenger at [[Dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]]'s counting-house, husband of [[mrs-perch|Mrs. Perch]], and resident of [[Ball's Pond|balls-pond]]. A comic figure who carries office news home to his perpetually expanding [[family|family-and-kinship]], he appears throughout the novel as a choric observer of [[the firm|dombey-and-son-firm]]'s rise and fall.

## Description

Perch occupies a marginal but structurally significant place in the Dombey establishment. His station is a "little bracket, like a timepiece"[035-048], positioned at the threshold between the outer office and the inner sanctum of power. From this perch — his name is suggestive — he observes the comings and goings of the great commercial house, and his reports to his wife at Ball's Pond form a kind of domestic news-service.

He is defined by his relationship to information. As messenger, he is neither a decision-maker nor a clerk; he is a conduit, and his importance lies in what he overhears and transmits. When Dombey's fortunes decline, Perch is among the first to register the [[change|time-and-change]]: "As to Perch, the messenger, he is in a fair way of being ruined for life"[125-048], his identity so bound up with the firm's [[dignity|pride]] that its collapse threatens his own sense of self.

## Role in the Novel

Perch's first significant appearance establishes his function as a comic intermediary. When [[Mr. Carker|james-carker]] wishes to communicate with Dombey, it is Perch who conveys the message: "when Mr Perch the messenger knocked softly at the door"[125-048], his deference encoding the rigid hierarchy of the counting-house.

His domestic life at Ball's Pond provides a running comic subplot. His wife, [[Mrs. Perch|mrs-perch]], is perpetually pregnant, and Perch's function as a bringer of news from the great world of [[commerce|money-and-commerce]] to the domestic sphere is the subject of gentle satire. The journey "from Balls Pond"[146-040] to the city and back again traces the novel's larger movement between public and private spheres.

During the celebrations surrounding [[Dombey's second [[marriage|marriage]]|dombeys-second-marriage]], Perch participates in the servants' festivities with characteristic self-importance. Yet his ultimate fate illustrates the novel's theme of dependency: when the firm collapses, Perch's identity collapses with it, for he has no existence independent of the institution he serves.

## Character

Perch belongs to Dickens's gallery of minor functionaries whose comic self-importance masks a genuine pathos. He is proud of his proximity to power, yet powerless; he traffics in information, yet understands little. His name — suggesting both his physical station and the precariousness of his position — is characteristic Dickensian shorthand for a life lived on the margin of great events.
