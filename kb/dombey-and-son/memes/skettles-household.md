## Overview

The Skettles household represents the world of comfortable, fashionable English society that [[Florence [[Dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]]|florence-dombey]] enters after fleeing her father's house. [[Sir Barnet Skettles|sir-barnet-skettles]], a genial but ineffectual baronet, and his wife [[Lady Skettles|lady-skettles]] provide Florence with her first [[experience|innocence-and-experience]] of a [[family|family-and-kinship]] that operates on [[affection|love]] rather than cold commercial calculation. The household serves as a foil to the Dombey establishment — warm where [[Dombey's house|dombey-house]] is cold, casual where [[Dombey's|dombey-and-son-firm]] is formal.

## Sir Barnet and Lady Skettles

[[Sir Barnet|sir-barnet]] Skettles is a minor baronet who has done nothing in particular to earn his title but wears it amiably. He is characterized chiefly by his good nature and his tendency to agree with everyone. Lady Skettles is more socially astute — she recognizes Florence's quality immediately and treats her with genuine kindness:

> Lady Skettles was a lady of very cheerful and agreeable manners, who had known Florence as a child, and who took the greatest interest in her. [Fulham chapters]

Their household in Fulham, a then-fashionable suburb on the Thames, is a gathering place for polite society — a world of dinners, carriage rides, and genteel conversation far removed from the counting-houses of the City.

## Florence's Refuge

After Florence's dramatic flight from her father's house, the Skettles household becomes her first place of safety. Here she is treated not as a burden or an inconvenience but as a welcome guest. The contrast with Dombey's house is deliberate and pointed:

The Skettles's kindness to Florence demonstrates Dickens's belief that family is defined by affection rather than blood or [[money|money-and-commerce]]. The baronet's household — aristocrats by title but bourgeois in their domestic warmth — represents a middle way between the cold formality of Dombey's world and the desperate poverty of the novel's lower depths.

## Role in the Novel

The Skettles household appears primarily in the middle sections of the novel, providing a narrative bridge between Florence's departure from her father's house and her eventual reunion with [[Walter Gay|walter-gay]]. It is one of several alternative households — along with [[the [[Toodle|mr-toodle]] family|the-toodle-family]], [[the Gills-Cuttle circle|the-gills-cuttle-circle]], and the Dombey establishment itself — that Dickens uses to explore different models of domestic life.
