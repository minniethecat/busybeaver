# Pride

## Overview

Pride is the central moral theme of *Dombey and Son*, embodied primarily through the character of [[paul-dombey|Paul Dombey]] Sr., whose obsession with the family firm, social status, and having a male heir drives the novel's tragedy. Dickens portrays pride as a blinding force that isolates the proud from genuine human connection, standing in stark opposition to the humble love represented by [[florence-dombey|Florence Dombey]]. The theme intertwines with [[money-and-commerce|Money and Commerce]] and [[class-and-society|Class and Society]], as Dombey's pride is inseparable from his identity as a wealthy Victorian merchant who equates financial power with personal worth.

## Manifestation

[[Mr. Dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]]'s pride manifests from the novel's opening chapters. His emotional coldness toward his daughter [[Florence|florence-dombey]] stems from his proud fixation on his son Paul Jr. as the heir to [[Dombey and Son|dombey-and-son-firm]]. In the early chapters, Dickens establishes pride as the governing principle of Dombey's inner life:

> Unable to exclude these things from his [[remembrance|memory-and-the-past]], or to keep his mind free from such imperfect shapes of the meaning with which they were fraught, as were able to make themselves visible to him through the mist of his pride, his previous feeling of indifference towards little Florence changed into an uneasiness of an extraordinary kind.
>
> — PN 007-050, Chapter 7

This metaphor of pride as a "mist" is central to Dickens's treatment of the theme. Pride does not simply make Dombey arrogant — it fundamentally distorts his perception, preventing him from seeing the [[love|love]] that Florence offers and the humanity of those he considers beneath him. The narrative voice consistently undercuts Dombey's proud self-image with irony, showing the reader what the character cannot see.

The theme reaches its devastating climax after [[Dombey's second [[marriage|marriage]]|dombeys-second-marriage]] collapses. When he confronts Florence after [[Edith|edith-dombey]]'s departure, his pride has curdled into something darker:

> Did he see before him the successful rival of his son, in health and life? Did he look upon his own successful rival in that son's affection? Did a mad [[jealousy|jealousy]] and withered pride, poison sweet remembrances that should have endeared and made her precious to him?
>
> — PN 053-008, Chapter 53

The novel demonstrates that pride, unlike genuine dignity, must be broken before [[redemption|redemption-and-transformation]] is possible. Dombey's [[financial ruin|dombeys-bankruptcy]] in [[dombeys-bankruptcy|Dombey's Bankruptcy]] becomes the instrument of his moral salvation — only when stripped of [[wealth|money-and-commerce]] and [[status|class-and-society]] can he finally see clearly.

## Pride as Social Criticism

Dickens uses pride as a lens for social criticism of the Victorian mercantile class. Dombey's pride is inseparable from his identity as a businessman — his belief that wealth and the firm's reputation confer inherent superiority. The novel argues that such pride is both morally corrosive and, ultimately, self-defeating: Dombey loses his son, his wife, his business, and nearly his daughter before he can be humbled and redeemed.

The Dickensian treatment of pride extends beyond Dombey himself. The novel presents a society structured by gradations of pride: the cold formalism of [[the Dombey household|the-dombey-household]], the self-satisfaction of [[Mrs. Chick|mrs-chick]], the wounded aristocratic pride of Edith Granger, and the social climbing of characters like [[Major Bagstock|major-bagstock]]. In each case, pride isolates — it builds walls where love requires bridges.

## Dombey's Pride Arc

1. **Establishment (Chapters 1–5)** — Pride presented as Dombey's defining trait. The death of his wife matters less than the "sore humiliation" of needing a wet-nurse for his heir. "And yet in his pride and jealousy, he viewed with so much bitterness the thought of being dependent... on a hired serving-woman" (PN 004-034).

2. **Deepening (Chapters 7–18)** — The "mist of his pride" prevents Dombey from recognizing Florence's love. His pride attaches to young Paul as the vessel of dynastic [[ambition|ambition]], blinding him to the boy's fragility.

3. **Crisis of [[Grief|grief-and-loss]] (Chapters 18–20)** — Paul Jr.'s death wounds Dombey's pride but does not humble him. He retreats deeper into cold formality, transferring his frustrated ambition onto the abstract idea of the firm.

4. **The Second Marriage (Chapters 21–35)** — Dombey's pride leads him to treat [[edith-dombey|Edith]] as an acquisition — a beautiful, high-born ornament to his status. His inability to see her as a person with her own pride guarantees the marriage's destruction.

5. **Catastrophe (Chapters 36–54)** — Edith's [[ediths-rebellion|rebellion]] and Carker's [[betrayal|betrayal-and-deception]] strike at the heart of Dombey's pride. The "mad jealousy and withered pride" (PN 053-008) drive him to strike Florence, severing his last human connection.

6. **Humiliation and Redemption (Chapters 55–62)** — [[dombeys-bankruptcy|Financial ruin]] completes the destruction of Dombey's pride. Only in abject humility does he become capable of receiving Florence's forgiveness and love. [[dombeys-reconciliation|The reconciliation]] is the novel's moral climax.

## Florence as the Antidote to Pride

Florence Dombey provides the counter-example: she endures her father's coldness with quiet dignity rather than pride, ultimately becoming the instrument of his redemption. Where Dombey's pride builds walls, Florence's love persists in seeking entry. Her constancy — maintaining devotion to a father who cannot love her — is the novel's answer to the question of what can overcome pride: not argument, not confrontation, but patient, suffering love.

## Dickens's Narrative Technique

Dickens employs free indirect discourse to expose Dombey's pride from within while simultaneously undercutting it with ironic narrative commentary. The reader is positioned to see both Dombey's self-perception and its absurdity. This technique invites self-reflection: the reader is encouraged to recognize pride in themselves and their society. The novel's moral ambition is not merely to depict pride but to inoculate against it.
