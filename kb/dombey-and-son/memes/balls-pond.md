# Ball's Pond

## Overview

Ball's Pond is the suburban London home of Mr [[Perch|mr-perch]], the messenger of [[Dombey and Son|[[dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]]-and-son-firm]], and his wife. Though mentioned only briefly, the location serves as a minor but telling counterpoint to the grandeur of the Dombey establishment — a place where the firm's fortunes are felt not as ledgers and bills but as gossip, anxiety, and the threat of unemployment.

## Description

The hamlet of Ball's Pond (now part of Islington in North London) appears in *Dombey and Son* purely through the domestic life of the Perch [[family|family-and-kinship]]. Mr Perch commutes from Ball's Pond to the City each day, and his home life there is woven into the fabric of the novel's domestic-comic subplot.

On the morning of Mr Dombey's wedding to [[Edith|edith-dombey]], "from Balls Pond, Mr Perch brings [[Mrs Perch|mrs-perch]] to spend the day with Mr Dombey's servants, and accompany them, surreptitiously, to see the wedding." [^1] Mrs Perch is one of the many subsidiary figures drawn into the orbit of the great event.

## Role in the Novel

Ball's Pond appears at moments of domestic anxiety and institutional crisis. When Mr Perch is overworked, he seeks consolation "in the society of Mrs Perch at Balls Pond; and Mrs Perch frets a good deal, for she fears his confidence in woman is shaken now, and that he half expects on coming home at night to find her gone off with some Viscount." [^2]

After the [[collapse of Dombey and Son|dombeys-bankruptcy]], Mr Perch becomes a minor celebrity among his neighbours, relating to "gaping listeners" at Ball's Pond "the hours of acute uneasiness he and Mrs Perch had suffered out at Balls Pond, when they first suspected 'things was going wrong.'" [^3] The location thus becomes the stage for the firm's tragedy as refracted through the perspective of its humblest employee — a domestic echo of the great commercial catastrophe.
