[031-001]The little friend being something too small to be seen at all from

where the Doctor sat, over the books on his table, the Doctor made

several futile attempts to get a view of him round the legs; which Mr

[031-002][[paul-[[dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]]|Dombey]] perceiving, relieved the Doctor from his embarrassment by taking

[031-003]Paul up in his arms, and sitting him on another little table, over

against the Doctor, in the middle of the room.

[031-004]“Ha!” said the Doctor, leaning back in his chair with his hand in his

breast. “Now I see my little friend. How do you do, my little friend?”

[031-005]The clock in the hall wouldn’t subscribe to this alteration in the form

of words, but continued to repeat how, is, my, lit, tle, friend? how,

is, my, lit, tle, friend?”

[031-006]“Very well, I thank you, Sir,” returned Paul, answering the clock quite

as much as the Doctor.

[031-007]“Ha!” said [[Doctor Blimber|dr-blimber]]. “Shall we make a man of him?”

[031-008]“Do you hear, Paul?” added Mr Dombey; Paul being silent.

[031-009]“Shall we make a man of him?” repeated the Doctor.

[031-010]“I had rather be a child,” replied Paul.

[031-011]“Indeed!” said the Doctor. “Why?”

[031-012]The child sat on the table looking at him, with a curious expression of

suppressed emotion in his face, and beating one hand proudly on his

knee as if he had the rising tears beneath it, and crushed them. But

his other hand strayed a little way the while, a little farther—farther

from him yet—until it lighted on the neck of [[florence-dombey|Florence]]. “This is why,”

it seemed to say, and then the steady look was broken up and gone; the

working lip was loosened; and the tears came streaming forth.

[031-013]“[[Mrs Pipchin|mrs-pipchin]],” said his father, in a querulous manner, “I am really

very sorry to see this.”

[031-014]“Come away from him, do, Miss Dombey,” quoth the matron.

[031-015]“Never mind,” said the Doctor, blandly nodding his head, to keep Mrs

[031-016]Pipchin back. “Never mind; we shall substitute new cares and new

impressions, Mr Dombey, very shortly. You would still wish my little

friend to acquire—”

[031-017]“Everything, if you please, Doctor,” returned Mr Dombey, firmly.

[031-018]“Yes,” said the Doctor, who, with his half-shut eyes, and his usual

smile, seemed to survey Paul with the sort of interest that might

attach to some choice little animal he was going to stuff. “Yes,

exactly. Ha! We shall impart a great variety of information to our

little friend, and bring him quickly forward, I daresay. I daresay.

[031-019]Quite a virgin soil, I believe you said, Mr Dombey?”

[031-020]“Except some ordinary preparation at home, and from this lady,” replied

[031-021]Mr Dombey, introducing Mrs Pipchin, who instantly communicated a

rigidity to her whole muscular system, and snorted defiance beforehand,

in case the Doctor should disparage her; “except so far, Paul has, as

yet, applied himself to no studies at all.”

[031-022]Doctor Blimber inclined his head, in gentle tolerance of such

insignificant poaching as Mrs Pipchin’s, and said he was glad to hear

it. It was much more satisfactory, he observed, rubbing his hands, to

begin at the foundation. And again he leered at Paul, as if he would

have liked to tackle him with the Greek alphabet, on the spot.

[031-023]“That circumstance, indeed, Doctor Blimber,” pursued Mr Dombey,

glancing at his little son, “and the interview I have already had the

pleasure of holding with you, renders any further explanation, and

consequently, any further intrusion on your valuable [[time|time-and-change]], so

unnecessary, that—”

[031-024]“Now, Miss Dombey!” said the acid Pipchin.

[031-025]“Permit me,” said the Doctor, “one moment. Allow me to present Mrs

[031-026]Blimber and my daughter; who will be associated with the domestic life

of our young Pilgrim to Parnassus [[Mrs Blimber|mrs-blimber]],” for the lady, who had

perhaps been in waiting, opportunely entered, followed by her daughter,

that fair Sexton in spectacles, “Mr Dombey. My daughter [[Cornelia|cornelia-blimber]], Mr

[031-027]Dombey. Mr Dombey, my [[love|love]],” pursued the Doctor, turning to his wife,

[031-028]“is so confiding as to—do you see our little friend?”

[031-029]Mrs Blimber, in an excess of politeness, of which Mr Dombey was the

object, apparently did not, for she was backing against the little

friend, and very much endangering his position on the table. But, on

this hint, she turned to admire his classical and intellectual

lineaments, and turning again to Mr Dombey, said, with a sigh, that she

envied his dear son.

[031-030]“Like a bee, Sir,” said Mrs Blimber, with uplifted eyes, “about to

plunge into a garden of the choicest flowers, and sip the sweets for

the first time Virgil, Horace, Ovid, Terence, Plautus, Cicero. What a

world of honey have we here. It may appear remarkable, Mr Dombey, in

one who is a wife—the wife of such a husband—”

[031-031]“Hush, hush,” said Doctor Blimber. “Fie for shame.”

[031-032]“Mr Dombey will forgive the partiality of a wife,” said Mrs Blimber,

with an engaging smile.

[031-033]Mr Dombey answered “Not at all:” applying those words, it is to be

presumed, to the partiality, and not to the [[forgiveness|forgiveness]].

[031-034]“And it may seem remarkable in one who is a mother also,” resumed Mrs

[031-035]Blimber.

[031-036]“And such a mother,” observed Mr Dombey, bowing with some confused idea

of being complimentary to Cornelia.

[031-037]“But really,” pursued Mrs Blimber, “I think if I could have known

[031-038]Cicero, and been his friend, and talked with him in his retirement at

[031-039]Tusculum (beau-ti-ful Tusculum!), I could have died contented.”

[031-040]A learned enthusiasm is so very contagious, that Mr Dombey half

believed this was exactly his case; and even Mrs Pipchin, who was not,

as we have seen, of an accommodating disposition generally, gave

utterance to a little sound between a groan and a sigh, as if she would

have said that nobody but Cicero could have proved a lasting

consolation under that failure of the Peruvian Mines, but that he

indeed would have been a very Davy-lamp of refuge.

[031-041]Cornelia looked at Mr Dombey through her spectacles, as if she would

have liked to crack a few quotations with him from the authority in

question. But this design, if she entertained it, was frustrated by a

knock at the room-door.

[031-042]“Who is that?” said the Doctor. “Oh! Come in, Toots; come in. Mr

[031-043]Dombey, Sir.” Toots bowed. “Quite a coincidence!” said Doctor Blimber.

[031-044]“Here we have the beginning and the end. Alpha and Omega. Our head boy,

[031-045]Mr Dombey.”

[031-046]The Doctor might have called him their head and shoulders boy, for he

was at least that much taller than any of the rest. He blushed very

much at finding himself among strangers, and chuckled aloud.

[031-047]“An addition to our little Portico, Toots,” said the Doctor; “Mr

[031-048]Dombey’s son.”

[031-049]Young Toots blushed again; and finding, from a solemn silence which

prevailed, that he was expected to say something, said to Paul, “How

are you?” in a voice so deep, and a manner so sheepish, that if a lamb

had roared it couldn’t have been more surprising.

[031-050]“Ask [[Mr Feeder|mr-feeder]], if you please, Toots,” said the Doctor, “to prepare a

few introductory volumes for Mr Dombey’s son, and to allot him a

convenient seat for study. My dear, I believe Mr Dombey has not seen

the dormitories.”

[031-051]“If Mr Dombey will walk upstairs,” said Mrs Blimber, “I shall be more

than proud to show him the dominions of the drowsy god.”

[031-052]With that, Mrs Blimber, who was a lady of great suavity, and a wiry

figure, and who wore a cap composed of sky-blue materials, proceeded

upstairs with Mr Dombey and Cornelia; Mrs Pipchin following, and

looking out sharp for her enemy the footman.

[031-053]While they were gone, Paul sat upon the table, holding [[Florence|florence-dombey]] by the

hand, and glancing timidly from the Doctor round and round the room,

while the Doctor, leaning back in his chair, with his hand in his

breast as usual, held a book from him at arm’s length, and read. There

was something very awful in this manner of reading. It was such a

determined, unimpassioned, inflexible, cold-blooded way of going to

work. It left the Doctor’s countenance exposed to view; and when the

[031-054]Doctor smiled suspiciously at his author, or knit his brows, or shook

his head and made wry faces at him, as much as to say, “Don’t tell me,

[031-055]Sir; I know better,” it was terrific.

[031-056]Toots, too, had no [[money-and-[[commerce|money-and-commerce]]|business]] to be outside the door, ostentatiously

examining the wheels in his watch, and counting his half-crowns. But

that didn’t last long; for Doctor Blimber, happening to change the

position of his tight plump legs, as if he were going to get up, Toots

swiftly vanished, and appeared no more.

[031-057]Mr Dombey and his conductress were soon heard coming downstairs again,

talking all the way; and presently they re-entered the Doctor’s study.

[031-058]“I hope, Mr Dombey,” said the Doctor, laying down his book, “that the

arrangements meet your approval.”

[031-059]“They are excellent, Sir,” said Mr Dombey.

[031-060]“Very fair, indeed,” said Mrs Pipchin, in a low voice; never disposed

to give too much encouragement.

[031-061]“Mrs Pipchin,” said Mr Dombey, wheeling round, “will, with your

permission, Doctor and Mrs Blimber, visit Paul now and then.”

[031-062]“Whenever Mrs Pipchin pleases,” observed the Doctor.

[031-063]“Always happy to see her,” said Mrs Blimber.

[031-064]“I think,” said Mr Dombey, “I have given all the trouble I need, and

may take my leave. Paul, my child,” he went close to him, as he sat

upon the table. “Good-bye.”

[031-065]“Good-bye, Papa.”

[031-066]The limp and careless little hand that Mr Dombey took in his, was

singularly out of keeping with the wistful face. But he had no part in

its sorrowful expression. It was not addressed to him. No, no. To

[031-067]Florence—all to Florence.

[031-068]If Mr Dombey in his insolence of wealth, had ever made an enemy, hard

to appease and cruelly vindictive in his hate, even such an enemy might

have received the pang that wrung his proud heart then, as compensation

for his injury.

[031-069]He bent down, over his boy, and kissed him. If his sight were dimmed as

he did so, by something that for a moment blurred the little face, and

made it indistinct to him, his mental vision may have been, for that

short time, the clearer perhaps.

[031-070]“I shall see you soon, Paul. You are free on Saturdays and Sundays, you

know.”

[031-071]“Yes, Papa,” returned Paul: looking at his sister. “On Saturdays and

[031-072]Sundays.”

[031-073]“And you’ll try and learn a great deal here, and be a clever man,” said

[031-074]Mr Dombey; “won’t you?”

[031-075]“I’ll try,” returned the child, wearily.

[031-076]“And you’ll soon be grown up now!” said Mr Dombey.

[031-077]“Oh! very soon!” replied the child. Once more the old, old look passed

rapidly across his features like a strange light. It fell on Mrs

[031-078]Pipchin, and extinguished itself in her black dress. That excellent

ogress stepped forward to take leave and to bear off Florence, which

she had long been thirsting to do. The move on her part roused Mr

[031-079]Dombey, whose eyes were fixed on Paul. After patting him on the head,

and pressing his small hand again, he took leave of Doctor Blimber, Mrs

[031-080]Blimber, and Miss Blimber, with his usual polite frigidity, and walked

out of the study.

[031-081]Despite his entreaty that they would not think of stirring, Doctor

[031-082]Blimber, Mrs Blimber, and Miss Blimber all pressed forward to attend

him to the hall; and thus Mrs Pipchin got into a state of entanglement

with Miss Blimber and the Doctor, and was crowded out of the study

before she could clutch Florence. To which happy accident Paul stood

afterwards indebted for the dear [[remembrance|memory-and-the-past]], that Florence ran back to

throw her arms round his neck, and that hers was the last face in the

doorway: turned towards him with a smile of encouragement, the brighter

for the tears through which it beamed.

[031-083]It made his childish bosom heave and swell when it was gone; and sent

the globes, the books, blind Homer and Minerva, swimming round the

room. But they stopped, all of a sudden; and then he heard the loud

clock in the hall still gravely inquiring “how, is, my, lit, tle,

friend? how, is, my, lit, tle, friend?” as it had done before.
