## Overview

The wedding of [[Paul Dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]] to [[Edith Granger|edith-dombey]] marks the novel's midpoint and represents the culmination of [[Dombey's|dombey-and-son-firm]] second attempt at securing a wife befitting his commercial and social station. Where his first [[marriage|marriage]] produced the long-awaited male heir, this second marriage is driven by [[pride|pride]], appearance, and the machinations of Edith's calculating mother, [[Mrs. Skewton|mrs-skewton]]. The union, transactional from the start, becomes the novel's central portrait of a failed marriage — a gilded prison from which Edith ultimately rebels.

## Context

Following the [[death|death-and-mortality]] of his first wife [[Fanny|fanny-dombey]] and the devastating [[loss|grief-and-loss]] of his son Paul Jr., Dombey seeks a new wife. His motivations are not [[love|love]] or companionship but the desire for a woman of beauty and accomplishment who will adorn his household and enhance his social standing. [[Major Bagstock|major-bagstock]], a retired military officer, facilitates the introduction between Dombey and the widowed Edith Granger during a sojourn at [[Leamington|leamington]].

Edith, a proud and beautiful woman, has been groomed from [[childhood|childhood]] by her mother, the Honourable Mrs. Skewton, to marry for [[wealth|money-and-commerce]] and position. She enters the arrangement with clear-eyed cynicism, fully aware that she is being sold. Her consent is won not by affection but by exhaustion — she has been trained for nothing else.

## The Event

The wedding takes place without warmth or celebration. Dickens describes the ceremony with an irony that exposes the emptiness beneath the social forms. The bride is beautiful, the groom is wealthy, the guests are fashionable — and yet the entire affair is hollow. [[Cousin Feenix|cousin-feenix]] gives the bride away, and the couple departs for their honeymoon, but the reader already understands that this is a marriage of two people who neither love nor respect each other.

Dickens's narration emphasizes the commercial nature of the transaction. Dombey has purchased a magnificent wife; Edith has been sold by her mother. The wedding, like the marriage that follows, is a business arrangement dressed in social ceremony. The contrast with the novel's earlier scenes of domestic feeling — however compromised — is stark.

## Consequences

The marriage quickly deteriorates. Dombey expects obedience and gratitude; Edith refuses to be treated as property. Their power struggle culminates in Edith's open defiance at a dinner party, Dombey's humiliation, and ultimately Edith's flight with [[James Carker|james-carker]] — not out of love for Carker, but as a deliberate act of revenge against her husband.

The collapse of this marriage precipitates the novel's final crisis: Dombey's emotional breakdown, [[Florence's flight|florences-flight]] from home, Carker's death beneath a train, and the eventual humbling and [[redemption|redemption-and-transformation]] of Dombey himself. What begins as a fashionable wedding becomes the engine of the novel's most devastating events, exposing the rot at the heart of Dombey's world. (030-002)
