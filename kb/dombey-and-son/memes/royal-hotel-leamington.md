# Royal Hotel, Leamington

## Overview

The Royal Hotel at [[Leamington|leamington]] Spa is the fashionable resort where Mr [[Dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]] takes up residence during the pivotal summer of his courtship of [[Edith Granger|edith-dombey]]. Under the orchestration of Major Joseph Bagstock — who has maneuvered himself into the role of [[Dombey's|dombey-and-son-firm]] companion and social chaperon — the hotel becomes the stage for one of the novel's most artificial and ultimately tragic romantic sequences.

## Description

The Royal Hotel is presented as a place of material comfort and social pretension. "Rooms and dinner had been ordered" [^1] for Mr Dombey's arrival, and the Major — "staying at the Royal Hotel, with his friend Dombey" [^2] — makes himself thoroughly at home, eating and drinking to the point where "he so oppressed his organs of speech by eating and drinking, that when he" attempted to converse, his voice failed him.

The hotel's name itself becomes a target of the Major's characteristic bluster. Old Joe proposes "an alteration in the name of the Royal Hotel, and that it should be called the Three Jolly Bachelors, in honour of ourselves and [[Carker|james-carker]]" [^3] — a joke that reveals the all-male, performative, and faintly desperate social world that Dombey inhabits at Leamington.

## Role in the Novel

The Royal Hotel is the setting for Dombey's second great error in personal judgment. Having lost his first wife and alienated his daughter, he comes to Leamington seeking not [[love|love]] but a suitable ornament for his position — and the hotel, with its ordered rooms and staged dinners, is the perfect environment for a transaction dressed as romance. It is here that Mrs Skewton (Edith's mother) and [[Major Bagstock|major-bagstock]] negotiate the terms of the match while Dombey and Edith perform the outward forms of courtship.

The hotel thus represents the novel's critique of [[marriage|marriage]] as [[commerce|money-and-commerce]]: even the most intimate human relationships are negotiated in public spaces, over hotel dinners, by third parties with competing agendas.
