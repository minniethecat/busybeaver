# Mrs. Wickam

## Overview

Mrs. Wickam is a waiter's widow who replaces Polly [[Toodle|mr-toodle]] as [[Paul Dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]]'s attendant at [[Brighton|brighton]]. With her perpetual sighs, significant shakes of the head, and morbid tales of her Uncle's Betsey Jane, she brings a lugubrious presence to the sickroom. She is one of Dickens's comic-grotesque servant portraits — well-meaning in her way, but constitutionally incapable of cheerfulness. The narrator introduces her with the ominous observation that she "often said she never see a dear" child so unfortunate (PN 019-047).

## Biography

Mrs. Wickam enters the narrative after Polly Toodle (Richards) is dismissed from her post as Paul's nurse. From the outset, she casts a pall over Paul's sickroom. Her most memorable contributions are the stories of her Uncle's Betsey Jane, a child who died young and whom Mrs. Wickam relentlessly compares to Paul:

> "Betsey Jane," said Mrs. Wickam, lowering her voice, and looking round, "was as sweet a child as I could wish to see. I never saw a child, not excepting your own brother, that I took to so much. I may say I never saw a child I took to so much at all. And when my Uncle's wife died — which was a good ten year ago, but my Uncle couldn't bear it — when my Uncle's wife died, Betsey Jane was as near losing her senses as ever a child did." [PN 023-017]

The implication — that Paul is destined for the same fate — hangs unspoken in the air. When [[Florence|florence-dombey]] protests that Paul is nothing like Betsey Jane, Mrs. Wickam darkly replies, "My Uncle's wife died just like his Mama" (PN 023-007), drawing a web of morbid parallels that circle around the sick child.

Mrs. Wickam's exchanges with the servant Berry provide moments of grim comedy. She unburdens her mind to Berry (PN 022-061), who proves a sceptical audience. "Nonsense!" cried [[Miss Berry|miss-berry]] (PN 023-023), but Mrs. Wickam's foreboding is irrepressible — her lugubriousness is not a performance but a settled disposition.

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Wickam belongs to a distinct Dickensian type: the servant who inflicts psychological damage under the guise of care. Her Betsey Jane stories are not deliberately cruel — she means to comfort — but they saturate Paul's sickroom with the language of [[death|death-and-mortality]]. She is the anti-Polly Toodle: where Polly offered warmth, vitality, and the teeming life of the Toodle household, Mrs. Wickam offers sighs, shakes of the head, and a dead child held up as a mirror. The substitution is cruel dramatic irony — Paul needed life and got Mrs. Wickam.

## Relationships

Mrs. Wickam's primary charge is [[paul-dombey-jr|Paul Dombey Junior]], whose declining health she tends with a mixture of genuine care and morbid fascination. Her relationship with [[florence-dombey|Florence Dombey]] is more guarded — she views [[Florence's [[devotion|love]]|the-dutiful-daughter]] to Paul with a sort of professional [[jealousy|jealousy]]. Her confidante is [[miss-berry|Miss Berry]], a fellow servant who provides reluctant audience to Mrs. Wickam's gloomy pronouncements.
