## Overview

The Midshipman is a public house situated near [[Solomon Gills's shop|solomon-gills-shop]] — [[the Wooden Midshipman|the-wooden-midshipman]] — in a maritime quarter of London. It serves as a gathering place for sailors, merchants, and waterfront characters, contributing to the nautical atmosphere that surrounds the world of [[Captain Cuttle|captain-cuttle]] and [[Walter Gay|walter-gay]]. Like the Wooden Midshipman itself, the pub evokes a world of ships, voyages, and maritime camaraderie that stands in contrast to the cold commercialism of [[Dombey's|[[dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]]-and-son-firm]] counting-house.

## Description

Dickens presents the Midshipman as a typical sailors' tavern of the period: low-ceilinged, smoky, and filled with the paraphernalia of [[the sea|the-sea-and-waves]]. The atmosphere is convivial and rough — a place where men of the sea [[trade|money-and-commerce]] stories, drink grog, and discuss voyages. The pub's proximity to the Wooden Midshipman makes it a natural extension of [[Solomon Gills|solomon-gills]]'s world, frequented by the same nautical clientele who visit the instrument-maker's shop.

## Role in the Novel

The Midshipman pub functions primarily as atmospheric background rather than a site of major dramatic action. It reinforces the novel's maritime setting and provides context for characters like Captain Cuttle, whose identity is inseparable from the world of ships and sailors. The pub represents the rough, honest, working world of the sea — a realm governed by loyalty and practical wisdom rather than the social pretensions that dominate Dombey's circle.

## Associated Characters

[[captain-cuttle|Captain Cuttle]] is the character most naturally associated with the Midshipman, as the pub lies within his familiar maritime territory. [[solomon-gills|Solomon Gills]], as proprietor of the nearby Wooden Midshipman, is another nearby presence. [[walter-gay|Walter Gay]], before his departure for sea, inhabits this same neighborhood, growing up in the orbit of his uncle's shop and the nautical world that surrounds it. The pub's unnamed sailors and regulars form the anonymous backdrop of this maritime corner of Dickens's London.
