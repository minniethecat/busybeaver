# Resistance, Endurance, and Complicity: Women's Agency in *Dombey and Son*

## Thesis

*[[Dombey and Son|[[dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]]-and-son-firm]]* offers one of Dickens's richest explorations of women's responses to patriarchal power. Rather than presenting a single model of female virtue, the novel constructs a typology of agency: [[Edith Dombey|edith-dombey]]'s furious resistance, [[Florence Dombey|florence-dombey]]'s quiet endurance, [[Alice Marwood|alice-marwood]]'s bitter revenge, and [[Mrs. Skewton|mrs-skewton]]'s strategic complicity. Each woman negotiates the constraints of her position differently, and each pays a different price. Together, they reveal a Dickens who understood that women under [[patriarchy|gender-and-womens-roles]] were not a monolithic category but a spectrum of survival strategies — some of which the novel condemns, some it celebrates, and some it mourns.

## Argument

### 1. Edith Dombey: Agency as Resistance

Edith is the novel's most articulate critic of the patriarchal bargain. Married to Dombey for her beauty and breeding — "he had bought a beautiful possession" (070-014) — she refuses the performance of submission that her role demands. Her rebellion is not impulsive but deliberate: she weaponizes her own reputation, destroying Dombey's [[pride|pride]] by fleeing with [[Carker|james-carker]], knowing that the scandal will wound her husband more deeply than any argument could.

Yet Edith's agency is tragically constrained. She can resist only by destroying herself — her flight with Carker is an act of self-immolation as much as revenge. She ends the novel in exile, her dignity intact but her life hollowed out. Dickens grants her the novel's most eloquent critique of patriarchy — but he cannot, within the Victorian moral framework, grant her a happy ending.

> "I am what you have made me" (070-014)

### 2. Florence Dombey: Agency as Endurance

Florence represents the survival strategy that Dickens most fully endorses: patience, [[love|love]], and endurance. Neglected by her father, she does not rebel but persists — loving him despite his coldness, caring for Paul Jr., building a network of relationships ([[Walter|walter-gay]], [[Captain Cuttle|captain-cuttle]], [[Susan Nipper|susan-nipper]]) that sustain her through her father's emotional abandonment.

Florence's agency is invisible by design. She does not confront; she absorbs. Her flight from [[Dombey's house|dombey-house]] — after he strikes her — is her only overt act of resistance, and it is framed as self-preservation rather than rebellion. Dickens rewards her with the novel's happiest ending: [[marriage|marriage]] to Walter, [[children|childhood]], and the eventual reconciliation with her humbled father. In the novel's moral economy, endurance wins.

### 3. Alice Marwood: Agency as Revenge

Alice Marwood is the novel's darkest portrait of female agency. Seduced and abandoned by James Carker, transported as a convict for a crime connected to her exploitation, she returns to England consumed by bitterness. Her agency is entirely negative — she cannot build, only destroy; cannot love, only accuse.

Yet Alice is also the novel's most powerful witness against the system that destroyed her. Her encounter with [[Harriet Carker|harriet-carker]] — the sister of the man who wronged her — becomes one of the novel's most morally complex scenes. Harriet's compassion does not erase Alice's suffering, but it offers the novel's only glimpse of what justice might look like: not punishment (Carker's [[death|death-and-mortality]] is [[the railway|the-railway]]'s work, not Alice's) but recognition.

### 4. Mrs. Skewton: Agency as Complicity

Mrs. Skewton represents the corruption of agency into complicity. She has internalized the patriarchal system so thoroughly that she becomes its enforcer — training her daughter Edith to be a beautiful surface, a commodity, a wife for sale. Her rouge, her wig, her artificial postures are the physical manifestations of a self that has been entirely hollowed out by decades of playing the game.

Dickens's treatment of Mrs. Skewton is merciless. Her death — stripped of her cosmetics, reduced to a paralyzed body that can only gesture — is the novel's judgment on a life spent serving a system that ultimately consumes its servants. She is what Edith might become if Edith's resistance were to fail: a woman who has made peace with her own commodification.

## Conclusion

The four women — Edith, Florence, Alice, and Mrs. Skewton — map the spectrum of female response to patriarchal power in *Dombey and Son*. Dickens does not treat them equally: Florence's endurance is rewarded, Edith's resistance is dignified but exiled, Alice's revenge is mourned, and Mrs. Skewton's complicity is condemned. The novel thus endorses a particular kind of female agency — quiet, patient, loving — while acknowledging, with varying degrees of sympathy, the other strategies that women adopt when love is denied them. The result is a surprisingly nuanced portrait of women's agency in a world that offers them very little.
