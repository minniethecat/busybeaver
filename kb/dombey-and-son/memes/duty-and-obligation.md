# Duty and Obligation

The language of duty pervades Charles Dickens's *[[Dombey and Son|[[dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]]-and-son-firm]]*, appearing in the speech and thoughts of characters across the social spectrum. The novel subjects the concept of duty to sustained scrutiny, distinguishing between genuine moral duty — the obligation to [[love|love]], care, and protect — and the hollow performance of social obligation that sustains the novel's cold, transactional world.

## Duty as Social Performance

From the novel's opening chapters, duty is invoked as a justification for behaviour that is in fact selfish or cruel. When Dombey's sister [[mrs-[[chick|mr-chick]]|Mrs. Chick]] meddles in his affairs, she cloaks her interference in the language of obligation:

> "Jemima will do your duty by you." [005-053]

The phrase is characteristic of [[Mrs. Chick|mrs-chick]]'s rhetorical style — duty becomes a weapon to wield against others, a mask for the exercise of social power. The novel consistently exposes this instrumentalisation of duty, revealing it as a form of emotional fraud.

## The Severe Duty of Position

Mr. Dombey himself understands his life in terms of position and its attendant obligations. His coldness toward [[florence-dombey|Florence]] is rationalised as the necessary posture of a man of his station:

> He was patient to discharge the severe duty of his position. [012-029]

The adjective "severe" is telling — duty, in Dombey's world, is not about warmth or connection but about the grim performance of a role. His failure as a father is inseparable from his conception of fatherhood as a set of duties to be discharged rather than a relationship to be lived.

## Genuine Duty

Against these hollow invocations of duty, the novel sets examples of genuine moral obligation. When [[mr-[[morfin|mr-morfin]]|Mr. Morfin]], the assistant manager at [[dombey-and-son-firm|Dombey and Son]], acts to protect those harmed by the firm's collapse, he acts from a sense of duty that requires no performance or social recognition. Similarly, [[harriet-[[carker|james-carker]]|[[Harriet|harriet-carker]] Carker]]'s devotion to her brother — however misplaced — springs from a genuine sense of familial obligation rather than social display:

> "You have done your duty." [013-041]

The novel suggests that true duty is quiet, self-effacing, and oriented toward the welfare of others. False duty, by contrast, is loud, self-regarding, and oriented toward the maintenance of social appearances.

## Duty and Gender

The novel's treatment of duty is inflected by [[class-and-society|gender expectations]]. Female characters — particularly [[mrs-chick|Mrs. Chick]], [[miss-tox|Miss Tox]], and [[mrs-pipchin|Mrs. Pipchin]] — are frequent purveyors of the rhetoric of duty, using it to police the boundaries of acceptable feminine behaviour. Yet the novel's heroines — Florence and Harriet — transcend this language entirely, acting from love rather than obligation. The implication is that genuine moral action lies beyond the vocabulary of duty, in a realm of spontaneous affection that the language of social obligation cannot capture.
