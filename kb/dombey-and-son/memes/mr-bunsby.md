# Mr Bunsby

Captain Jack Bunsby is a shipmaster in *[[Dombey and Son|[[dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]]-and-son-firm]]*, a friend of [[Captain Cuttle|captain-cuttle]] who is consulted for his oracular wisdom. His manner is solemn and inscrutable, and his pronouncements — though often impenetrable — are treated by Captain Cuttle with the utmost reverence.

## Description

Bunsby is introduced as the commander of a vessel. When Captain Cuttle brings [[Florence|florence-dombey]] to consult him, Bunsby is found aboard his ship: "Bunsby was to the last degree soft-hearted"[068-003], though his outward appearance — "his hands in his pockets"[068-011] — suggests stolid indifference. Dickens describes him as a man of few words, and those words delivered with an air of profound mystery that Captain Cuttle interprets as the deepest sagacity.

Captain Cuttle whispers to Florence "that Bunsby had never in his life expressed surprise"[068-012], a quality that the Captain regards as proof of Bunsby's extraordinary wisdom.

## Role in the Novel

Bunsby first appears by name when Captain Cuttle seeks his advice. The Captain describes him with characteristic admiration: "His name's Bunsby," said the Captain. "But Lord, it might be anything for the matter of that, with such a mind as his!"[044-046]

Later, when Florence is in distress, Captain Cuttle brings her to consult "the oracular Bunsby"[067-049] for guidance. They visit Bunsby aboard his vessel, the *Cautious Clara*. The consultation scene is one of the novel's comic set pieces: Bunsby delivers opaque pronouncements that Captain Cuttle receives as profound revelations.

When the Captain hails him — "Bunsby, my lad, how fares it?"[068-013] — Bunsby responds with characteristic gravity. His eventual pronouncement, beginning abruptly with "Shipmet"[068-022], is treated by the Captain as a decisive verdict, though its meaning remains obscure to everyone else present.

## Character

Bunsby represents the Dickensian type of the comic sage — a man whose reputation for wisdom rests on an impenetrable manner rather than any actual profundity of thought. He "certainly had not the least appearance of being troubled"[068-015] by anything, a quality that Captain Cuttle mistakes for deep philosophical calm.

The contrast between Bunsby's inscrutable silence and Captain Cuttle's enthusiastic interpretation of it creates the comedy of their scenes together. Bunsby says little, but what he does say is received as oracular truth by his devoted friend.
