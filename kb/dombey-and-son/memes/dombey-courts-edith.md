## Overview

[[Dombey's|[[dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]]-and-son-firm]] courtship of [[Edith Granger|edith-dombey]] at the fashionable spa town of [[Leamington|leamington]] is one of the novel's most incisive portrayals of Victorian [[marriage|marriage]] as commercial transaction. Orchestrated by [[Major Bagstock|major-bagstock]] and Edith's scheming mother [[Mrs. Skewton|mrs-skewton]], the courtship is a negotiation in which beauty and social [[status|class-and-society]] are exchanged for [[wealth|money-and-commerce]] and position — with catastrophic consequences for all involved.

## The Setting: Leamington

Dombey travels to Leamington at Major Bagstock's urging, ostensibly for his health after Paul Jr.'s [[death|death-and-mortality]]. The spa town — with its pump rooms, promenades, and fashionable lodgings — provides the perfect stage for the social theater of courtship:

> Mr. Dombey was at Leamington, in lodgings that were fashionable enough and dear enough, but which, in the Major's opinion, were not half good enough for him. [020-025]

Major Bagstock, ever the sycophant, plays matchmaker with transparent self-interest, hoping to ingratiate himself further with the wealthy Dombey.

## Edith Granger

Edith Granger is beautiful, proud, and impoverished — a widow trapped by her mother's [[ambition|ambition]] and society's limited options for [[women|gender-and-womens-roles]]. She makes no pretense about the nature of the proposed marriage. In one of the novel's most striking moments of honesty, she tells her mother:

> "You know that I have been bought and sold. You know that the only terms on which I can live are terms of bargain and sale." [026-015]

Mrs. Skewton, for her part, sees Edith purely as a commodity. The Major's crude enthusiasm — "Shall we marry him to Edith Granger, Ma'am?" — punctuates the proceedings with grotesque comedy.

## The Proposal

Dombey's proposal is characteristically formal and transactional. He does not speak of [[love|love]] but of position, property, and [[obligation|duty-and-obligation]]. His letter to Edith is delivered through intermediaries — a gesture that underscores the impersonality of the arrangement:

> "Here is Mr. Dombey's letter, Edith." [030-012]

Edith accepts — not from affection but from a kind of exhausted fatalism. She knows that in Victorian society, a woman of her class has few alternatives. The engagement is announced, and preparations for the wedding begin.

## Significance

The courtship at Leamington encapsulates the novel's critique of marriage in a commercial society. Dombey seeks a beautiful ornament for his home and a stepmother for his [[children|childhood]]; Edith seeks financial security; neither seeks love. The transaction sows the seeds for the mutual humiliation and rebellion that will define the novel's second half.
