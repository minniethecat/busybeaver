# Ambition

## Overview

Ambition in *[[Dombey and Son|dombey-and-son-firm]]* is a double-edged force: it drives the novel's mercantile world forward but also corrodes the souls of those it possesses. Dickens explores ambition through multiple characters — [[Mr. Dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]]'s dynastic ambition for his firm and son, [[Carker|james-carker]]'s ruthless career ambition, [[Edith|edith-dombey]]'s thwarted social ambition, and even [[Mrs. [[Chick|mr-chick]]|mrs-chick]]'s petty social climbing. The novel argues that ambition, when untempered by [[love|love]] or moral principle, becomes a destructive passion.

## Manifestation

Mr. Dombey's ambition is established in the novel's opening pages — a "glorious and stirring ambition in the breast of the least ambitious of his [[children|childhood]]" (001-016) that centers entirely on the continuation of the firm. His "scheme of parental interest and ambition" (011-015) treats his son Paul as an extension of his [[business|money-and-commerce]] plan rather than as a child. Dickens describes this as an "icy current" that chills everything it touches.

The novel contrasts Dombey's dynastic ambition with Carker's predatory variety. Carker has "always pampered the vanity and ambition of his employer" (148-029) while secretly pursuing his own advancement. His ambition is the more dangerous for being hidden — a "sharp [[jealousy|jealousy]] of any second check or cross" (011-015) that ultimately leads to his dramatic downfall.

For secondary characters, ambition takes smaller but equally telling forms. Mrs. Chick schemes to maintain her social position; [[Major Bagstock|major-bagstock]] inflates his military credentials; and even the "superior classes" (005-046) are shown perpetually jockeying for [[status|class-and-society]]. Dickens demonstrates that ambition is not confined to the powerful — it infects every rung of the social ladder.

## Dickens's Treatment

Dickens's treatment of ambition reflects his broader critique of Victorian society. The novel distinguishes between legitimate aspiration ([[Walter Gay|walter-gay]]'s honest desire to make his way) and corrupt ambition (Dombey's and Carker's). The moral test is whether ambition serves others or only the self.

The novel's conclusion makes the point explicit: Dombey's ambition destroys his business, his second [[marriage|marriage]], and nearly his daughter before he is humbled. Only when ambition is replaced by love — Dombey's final reconciliation with [[Florence|florence-dombey]] — is [[redemption|redemption-and-transformation]] possible. Carker, who never repents, is destroyed by the very ambition that elevated him. Dickens's moral is clear: ambition without conscience leads to ruin.
