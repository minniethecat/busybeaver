# Mr. Towlinson

Mr. Towlinson is a servant in [[the [[Dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]] household|the-dombey-household]]. In the wake of [[Dombey's bankruptcy|dombeys-bankruptcy]], he emerges as an orator among the domestics, delivering a celebrated speech on servant solidarity and announcing his intention to open a greengrocery — a scene that transforms the collapse of a great house into a moment of comic [[dignity|pride]] for its humblest members.

## Description

Towlinson is introduced early in the novel as a fixture of the Dombey establishment. [[Mrs. [[Chick|mr-chick]]|mrs-chick]], with characteristic condescension, vouches for his reliability: "Towlinson, I believe he has no prejudice in our favour"[021-024], the remark revealing as much about the [[family|family-and-kinship]]'s attitude toward its servants as about Towlinson himself.

He is characterised by a formal, almost parliamentary manner of speech. Even in small exchanges — his courteous "Pretty well, Towlinson, I thank you"[021-025] response to [[Miss Tox|miss-tox]]'s inquiry — he maintains a dignity that contrasts with the moral chaos of his employers. Dickens describes him with mock-heroic formality: "Mr Towlinson, whom to know is to esteem, and to esteem is to wish well"[090-026], elevating the domestic servant to the [[status|class-and-society]] of a public figure.

## Role in the Novel

Towlinson's most significant appearance comes after [[Dombey's|dombey-and-son-firm]] bankruptcy, when the household is being dismantled and the servants face dispersal. In this crisis, Towlinson rises to the occasion with an act of comic heroism: he delivers a speech to the assembled domestics.

The speech is a masterpiece of mock-oratory. "Mr Towlinson then says that, in the first place, he would beg to propose"[143-008] — the formal preamble establishing his parliamentary pretensions. He announces his plan to open a greengrocery, transforming personal necessity into a public declaration of independence. The speech reduces [[Mrs. [[Perch|mr-perch]]|mrs-perch]] to tears, and Towlinson emerges from the wreck of the Dombey establishment as a figure of unexpected pathos and dignity.

The scene is characteristic Dickens: the great house falls, but the servants — who have watched its follies from below stairs — respond not with despair but with a kind of democratic resilience. Towlinson's greengrocery represents the possibility of self-reinvention that the novel withholds from its proud protagonists.

## Character

Towlinson belongs to the great Dickensian tradition of servants who are morally superior to their masters. His formal manner is not a parody of gentility but a genuine assertion of self-worth. When he proposes his greengrocery, he is not merely changing trades; he is declaring independence from a system of dependency that the novel has spent hundreds of pages anatomising. His speech, with its comic formality and real emotion, is one of the novel's small democratic triumphs.
