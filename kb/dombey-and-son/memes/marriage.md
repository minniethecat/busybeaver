# Marriage

## Overview

Marriage in *[[Dombey and Son|dombey-and-son-firm]]* is treated not as a romantic ideal but as a social transaction — an extension of the commercial logic that governs [[Mr. Dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]]'s life. Through two marriages (Dombey's first to [[Fanny|fanny-dombey]] and his second to [[Edith|edith-dombey]]) and several contrasting unions, Dickens anatomizes the institution of marriage in Victorian society, exposing how [[money|money-and-commerce]], [[pride|pride]], and social [[ambition|ambition]] poison what should be a bond of [[love|love]].

## Manifestation

The novel presents marriage as a spectrum from mercenary arrangement to genuine partnership. Dombey's first marriage to Fanny produced two [[children|childhood]] but no emotional connection; after her [[death|death-and-mortality]], he is consumed not by [[grief|grief-and-loss]] but by "visions of the youth, [[education|education]], and destination of his baby son" (004-034). [[The second marriage|dombeys-second-marriage]] to Edith Granger is presented from the outset as "a connexion by marriage of my brother's wife's mother" (084-018) — a thicket of social connections rather than a meeting of hearts.

The wedding preparations in Section 86–88 are rendered with Dickens's characteristic irony: "marriage company and marriage altars" (086-035), the beadle "expressly to attend the marriage" (088-012), and [[Cousin Feenix|cousin-feenix]]'s inappropriate suggestion to "make a double marriage of it" (088-023). The spectacle overwhelms the substance.

Dickens contrasts these hollow unions with alternative models: [[Solomon Gills|solomon-gills]]'s quiet devotion, the possibility of genuine love between [[Florence|florence-dombey]] and [[Walter|walter-gay]], and even the comic practicality of the [[Toodle|mr-toodle]] household. [[Polly Richards|polly-richards]]'s "marriage certificate" (004-033) is a bureaucratic detail in Dombey's file, but her genuine maternal love stands in quiet opposition to Dombey's cold formality.

## Dickens's Treatment

Dickens uses marriage to critique Victorian social arrangements. The Dombey–Edith marriage is a masterpiece of psychological realism: two proud people bound by social contract but unable to connect. Edith's famous line — "My education was completed long ago. I am too old now" (080-002) — speaks to the way the marriage market has shaped and hardened her.

The novel argues that marriage, when reduced to a transaction, becomes a prison for both parties. Only when based on mutual affection — as in Florence and Walter's eventual union — can it fulfill its proper role as a source of human happiness. This is one of the novel's most Dickensian arguments: that love, not money or status, is the proper foundation for [[family|family-and-kinship]] life.
