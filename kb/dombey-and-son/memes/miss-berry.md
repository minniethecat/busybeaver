# Miss Berry

Miss Berry is a servant at [[the Brighton establishment|brighton]] where the ailing [[paul-dombey-jr|[[Paul Dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]] Junior]] is sent for his health. Her primary narrative function is as the reluctant audience for [[mrs-[[wickam|mrs-wickam]]|Mrs. Wickam]]'s morbid pronouncements, her sceptical responses providing a note of comic relief amid the gathering gloom of Paul's sickroom.

## Description

Miss Berry is introduced alongside Mrs. Wickam as one of the attendants watching over young Paul at Brighton. She is characterised by an instinctive warmth toward the child — stopping to admire him as he sleeps: "What a pretty fellow he is when he's asleep!"[023-001] — but also by a resistance to Mrs. Wickam's relentless pessimism.

Her role is defined almost entirely through her dialogue with Wickam. Where Wickam spins elaborate theories of doom and divine judgment, Berry supplies the voice of ordinary common sense, though her objections are invariably overborne by Wickam's dark eloquence.

## Role in the Novel

Miss Berry appears in the Brighton episodes of the novel, which form part of the long [[death|death-and-mortality]]-watch over Paul Dombey. Her exchanges with Mrs. Wickam establish an ironic counterpoint to the central tragedy: two servants philosophising at the bedside of a dying child, one predicting calamity with professional satisfaction, the other protesting weakly.

The key exchange occurs when Wickam invokes the death of her own child as evidence of providential design. Berry attempts to resist the logic: "Berry looked as if she would like to trace the connexion of ideas"[023-006] between Wickam's domestic tragedy and Paul's illness. But Wickam's rhetorical momentum is unstoppable, and Berry can only repeat helplessly that she "hopes" Wickam will continue her visits — "This was the surest way of being asked again."[023-033]

Berry's final contribution to the Brighton scenes is one of physical [[tenderness|love]]: "Miss Berry's emotion might have led to her patting Paul too hard on the head"[023-043], a gesture that suggests genuine feeling beneath her comic function.

## Character

Miss Berry belongs to the [[class|class-and-society]] of Dickensian minor servants whose principal narrative purpose is to provide an audience for more voluble characters. She is the straight woman to Mrs. Wickam's tragic comedienne, and her baffled responses supply the humour that leavens the pathos of Paul's decline. In this respect she functions as the reader's surrogate, bewildered by Wickam's logic but unable to articulate a satisfactory counter-argument.
