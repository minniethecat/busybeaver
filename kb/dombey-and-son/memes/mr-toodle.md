# Mr. Toodle

## Overview

Mr. Toodle is a railway engine driver and the husband of Polly Toodle (Richards), who serves as [[Paul Dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]]'s wet-nurse. A stout, honest working man, Toodle appears at key moments in the novel as a representative of the respectable labouring [[class|class-and-society]]. His warm [[family|family-and-kinship]] life and practical wisdom provide a sharp contrast to the cold formality of [[the Dombey household|the-dombey-household]].

## Biography

Toodle first appears when his wife Polly is engaged as a wet-nurse for the infant Paul Dombey. The interview at the Dombey house reveals his character: when asked what he thinks of his wife taking the position, he answers simply, "[Polly heerd it]" (005-002), deferring to her judgment with a humility that the haughty Mr. Dombey finds baffling.

> "I heerd it," said Toodle, "but I don't know as I understood it rightly Sir, account of being no scholar, and the words being—ask your pardon—rayther high. But Polly heerd it. It's all right."
>
> "As you appear to leave everything to her," said Mr Dombey, frustrated in his intention of impressing his views still more distinctly on the husband, as the stronger character, "I suppose it is of no use my saying anything to you."
>
> "Not a bit," said Toodle. "Polly heerd it. She's awake, Sir."
>
> — [005-002]

A stoker on [[the railway|the-railway]], Toodle embodies the new industrial England. Dickens draws a vivid physical contrast between the working man and his employer that encapsulates the class divide at the heart of the novel:

> Thus arrested on the threshold as he was following his wife out of the room, Toodle returned and confronted Mr Dombey alone. He was a strong, loose, round-shouldered, shuffling, shaggy fellow, on whom his clothes sat negligently: with a good deal of hair and whisker, deepened in its natural tint, perhaps by smoke and coal-dust: hard knotty hands: and a square forehead, as coarse in grain as the bark of an oak. A thorough contrast in all respects, to Mr Dombey, who was one of those close-shaved close-cut moneyed gentlemen who are glossy and crisp like new bank-notes, and who seem to be artificially braced and tightened as by the stimulating action of golden showerbaths.
>
> — [004-051]

When offered [[money|money-and-commerce]] and reminded that he has "[[children|childhood]], and more coming," Toodle replies with straightforward [[dignity|pride]]: "Thereabouts, I suppose, Sir" [005-005]. In the novel's closing chapters, Toodle reappears in a transformed role. By then, Mr. [[Dombey's|dombey-and-son-firm]] downfall has softened his pride, and Toodle arrives at the empty Dombey house to help. The once-disdained working man is now treated as an equal — a quiet symbol of the novel's moral arc.

## Relationships

Toodle's central relationship is with his wife [[polly-toodle|Polly Toodle]], whose employment as Paul's wet-nurse draws [[the Toodle family|the-toodle-family]] into the Dombey orbit. His interactions with [[paul-dombey|Mr. Dombey]] chart the novel's shifting class dynamics: where Dombey once viewed Toodle as barely worth addressing, the final scenes show a grudging mutual respect. Through his large, affectionate family — the "Toodles" — he represents the domestic warmth that the Dombey household lacks.
