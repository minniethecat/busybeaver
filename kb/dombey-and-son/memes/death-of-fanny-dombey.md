## Overview

The novel opens with the [[death|death-and-mortality]] of [[Fanny Dombey|fanny-dombey]], [[Mr. Dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]]'s first wife, who dies shortly after giving birth to their son Paul. The event — handled with Dickens's characteristic blend of pathos and social satire — immediately establishes the novel's central themes: the coldness of the commercial heart, the vulnerability of [[children|childhood]], and the transactional nature of [[Dombey's|dombey-and-son-firm]] relationships.

## The Deathbed Scene

Fanny Dombey lies dying as her husband contemplates the future of his firm. The infant Paul and six-year-old [[Florence|florence-dombey]] are brought to her bedside. [[Mrs. [[Chick|mr-chick]]|mrs-chick]], Dombey's sister, attempts to console with her characteristic blend of sentimentality and moralizing:

> "Now, really, Fanny my dear," said the sister-in-law, altering her position, "this is a world of effort you know, Fanny, and we must never yield, when so much depends upon us." [001-018]

The dying woman's last gesture is to embrace her children — Florence especially — in a moment of mute [[tenderness|love]] that contrasts sharply with the bustling, self-regarding concern of the adults around her:

> "Fanny, my dear," said Mrs. Chick, with assumed lightness, "here's Mr. Dombey come to see you, and here's your little boy — the baby, Fanny, you know; you have hardly seen him yet, I think." [001-015]

## Dombey's Response

Mr. Dombey's reaction to his wife's death reveals his character with devastating economy. His primary concern is not [[grief|grief-and-loss]] but the inconvenience to the firm and the loss of a woman who might have produced more heirs. When informed of her condition, his response is measured in commercial terms:

> "But what is this about Fanny, herself?" said Mr. Dombey. "How is Fanny?" [001-002]

His coldness toward the infant Paul — who has now become the sole focus of his dynastic ambitions — and his [[neglect|parental-neglect]] of Florence are both set in motion by Fanny's death. The motherless children become the emotional center of the novel.

## Significance

The death of Fanny Dombey is more than a plot device; it is the primal wound from which the novel's emotional dynamics flow. Florence loses her only source of unconditional love; Paul becomes an object rather than a child; and Dombey's emotional incapacity is laid bare. Dickens uses the scene to critique a society in which even death is measured by its impact on [[business|money-and-commerce]].
