# Psychological Themes

Dickens is not always credited as a psychological novelist, yet *[[Dombey and Son|dombey-and-son-firm]]* contains some of the most penetrating studies of inner life in Victorian fiction. From [[Paul Dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]]'s frozen [[pride|pride]] to [[Florence|florence-dombey]]'s quiet resilience, from [[Edith|edith-dombey]]'s bitter self-knowledge to [[Carker|james-carker]]'s calculating [[duplicity|betrayal-and-deception]], the novel maps the interior landscapes that [[drive|ambition]] outward action. This page aggregates all entries tagged "psychological."

## About This List

The entries collected here trace the psychological currents that run beneath the novel's social surface: alienation, [[grief|grief-and-loss]], [[jealousy|jealousy]], betrayal, and the hard-won possibility of [[redemption|redemption-and-transformation]]. Together they reveal a novelist intensely curious about what happens inside his characters — and convinced that the inner life is where the most important dramas unfold.
