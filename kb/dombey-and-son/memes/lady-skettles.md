# Lady Skettles

## Overview

Lady Skettles is the wife of [[Sir Barnet Skettles|sir-barnet-skettles]] and the mother of Master Skettles. Described as one of "very good people" (069-025), she surveys the world through an opera glass and dispenses kindness with fashionable grace. Her maternal solicitude for the ailing [[Paul Dombey|[[paul|paul-dombey-jr]]-dombey]] Junior and her warm hospitality towards [[Florence Dombey|florence-dombey]] at Fulham mark her as a rare figure of uncomplicated benevolence in the novel's social landscape.

## Biography

Lady Skettles first appears at [[Doctor Blimber|dr-blimber]]'s evening party, where she "took a panoramic survey" of the Doctor's study "through her glass" and pronounced it "Very good" (041-042). Her use of the opera glass establishes her as a fashionable woman, but her manner belies the affectation:

> Lady Skettles surveyed the Doctor and his wife "through her glass" and pronounced it "Very good." Turning to the frail Paul, she asked gently, "And what is your name, my pale child?"
>
> — [041-042]

Throughout the party she shows consistent kindness to Paul, sitting with him on the sofa as he reclines, propped among the pillows, too ill to join the dancing. She tells him "that he seemed very fond of music" (042-036) and eagerly requests to hear Florence sing. When the party ends, Dickens records a moment of genuine [[tenderness|love]]:

> She kissed Paul, patted his hair upon his brow, and held him in her arms — a maternal farewell to a child she has only just met.
>
> — [042-040]

When Florence is introduced, Lady Skettles exclaims softly: "What eyes! What hair! What a lovely face!" (042-004). Her kindness is recorded as genuine [[grief|grief-and-loss]] after Paul's [[death|death-and-mortality]]: [[Mrs. Pipchin|mrs-pipchin]] reports that "Lady Skettles loved him like a—" breaking off to reach for her handkerchief (066-004). She writes "exceedingly kind notes" and repeatedly invites Florence to Fulham, offering the motherless girl a refuge from her father's cold house.

At Fulham she is an "excellent mother" (069-029), solicitous that young Barnet should be attentive to Florence. Her social grace extends to forgiving lapses: when [[Mr. Feeder|mr-feeder]]'s street songs might "give offence to Lady Skettles," she is "so very kind as to beg [[Mrs Blimber|mrs-blimber]] not to mention it" and receives the explanation "with the greatest courtesy and politeness" (042-034). At the novel's close, she shares in her husband's diplomatic appointment, discharging its honours admirably.

## Relationships

Lady Skettles is the domestic partner of [[sir-barnet-skettles|[[Sir Barnet|sir-barnet]] Skettles]], whose relentless sociability she supports with grace. Her warmth towards [[paul-dombey-jr|Paul Dombey Junior]] is striking — she is one of the few adults at Blimber's who perceives the child's fragility. Her hospitality to [[florence-dombey|Florence Dombey]] at Fulham provides one of the novel's few depictions of a functional, welcoming household.
