# George's First Kiss of Rita's Hand

At the conclusion of Monsieur George's first visit to the Pavilion in Marseilles, Doña Rita calls him back from the doorway and, without speaking, presses the back of her hand against his lips — a startling gesture that inaugurates the charged physical and emotional dynamic between them.

## Summary

After an afternoon of conversation in which Rita has revealed aspects of her past, her relationship with Henry Allègre, and her involvement with the Carlist cause, Mills and George prepare to depart. Mills bids Rita a cordial goodbye — "Goodbye, old Enchantress" — and she responds with "Goodbye, poor Magician" ([205-206]). Rita gives George a formal "Bon voyage and a happy return" as he bows distantly. But as George reaches the door, Rita calls him back with a pretense of having forgotten something. When George returns to the middle of the room, she extends her bare arm and presses the back of her hand against his lips. George is too astonished to respond: "I was too startled to seize it with rapture. It detached itself from my lips and fell slowly by her side" ([206]). The gesture transforms their relationship irrevocably: "We had made it up and there was nothing to say" ([206]).

## Chronology

### Lead-up

George's first visit to the Pavilion occurs shortly after the Carnival meeting (the-carnival-meeting). Mills and Blunt have arranged the visit so that Rita can assess George as a potential participant in the Carlist gun-running operation. During the visit, Rita has been alternately confiding and distant, discussing Allègre, the Carlist cause, her sense of unreality, and her fear. Mills has served as a mediating presence. As the visit concludes, Mills's farewell to Rita as "old Enchantress" establishes the mythic register in which Rita is perceived by the men around her. George, by contrast, receives only a formal farewell — until Rita calls him back.

### The Event

The moment is remarkable for its wordlessness. Rita calls "Oh, a moment... I forgot..." — a transparent pretense, as she has forgotten nothing ([205]). George returns to find her waiting "in the middle of the room with lowered head, with a mute gleam in her deep blue eyes" ([205]). The gesture itself — "she extended to me without a word her bare white arm and suddenly pressed the back of her hand against my lips" — is simultaneously intimate and remote ([205-206]). By offering the back of her hand rather than the palm, Rita retains a formal distance even as she initiates physical contact. George's failure to respond — "too startled to seize it with rapture" — underscores his youth and inexperience. The hand "detached itself from my lips and fell slowly by her side" — a withdrawal that leaves the moment suspended, unresolved, and permanently significant.

### Aftermath

George hurries from the room. Rita turns to the window. The gesture inaugurates the central dynamic of their relationship: a physical and emotional intimacy that both characters perpetually resist consummating. George reflects that "We had made it up and there was nothing to say" — the kiss resolves an unspoken tension while creating a new, deeper bond of complicity ([206]). The event marks the transition from Part II (the Pavilion visit) to Part III (the gun-running voyages). From this point forward, George is no longer merely a potential recruit but is bound to Rita by a personal tie that complicates his professional role in the conspiracy.

## Narrative Function

This scene is the novel's pivotal turning point in the George-Rita relationship. Conrad stages the moment with extraordinary economy: no words are exchanged, yet everything changes. The gesture's ambiguity — is it affection, enchantment, or a test? — echoes throughout the novel. Rita's simultaneous boldness (initiating the contact) and reserve (offering the back of the hand, immediately withdrawing) encapsulates her contradictory nature. George's startled passivity establishes him as someone to whom things happen rather than someone who acts — a quality that will define his role in the narrative. The scene also structurally bridges the novel's two halves: the establishment of the conspiracy (Parts I–II) and its execution (Parts III–V).
