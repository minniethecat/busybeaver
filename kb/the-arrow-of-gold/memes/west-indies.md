# West Indies

The West Indies (the Caribbean) threads through *The Arrow of Gold* as a place the narrator has left behind but cannot quite detach from: it defines his past, shapes his present credibility, and beckons as an escape he ultimately declines.

## Location

- **Geography**: The Caribbean archipelago, between North and South America
- **Coordinates in the Novel**: Referenced exclusively through George's personal history — his voyages, his business interests, his lingering ties
- **Real-World Counterpart**: Real — the West Indies is a literal geographical region; Conrad himself had Caribbean maritime experience

## Role in the Narrative

The West Indies establishes George's maritime credentials from the novel's opening. He has "just returned from my second West Indies voyage," and his "eyes were still full of tropical splendour" (PN=001-004). This experience distinguishes him from the salon-bound Legitimists and marks him as a man of action, not merely a spectator.

The region also serves as a bond between George and Mills, who were "very close associates in the West Indies from where we had returned together" — Mills "had a notion that I could be depended on" (PN=007-011). This shared history of Caribbean voyaging underpins Mills's trust in recruiting George for the Carlist conspiracy.

Later, the West Indies becomes a test of George's commitment. Rita, learning from Mills that George has "been to the West Indies" and has "some interests there" (PN=014-072), probes whether his attachment to her outweighs those ties. In the novel's later passages, George admits he "had not the strength of mind to leave for the West Indies, or elsewhere" (PN=018-001), acknowledging that his pursuit of Rita and the Royalist cause keeps him in Europe against his practical judgment.

## Symbolic Dimension

The West Indies represents an alternate life — one of freedom, simplicity, and escape from the tangled emotional and political webs of Europe. George's "eyes full of tropical splendour" (PN=001-004) evoke a world of colour and vitality that contrasts with the dim cafés and shadowy conspiracies of Marseilles. Yet tellingly, the West Indies adventures left him "untouched" — "they were other men's adventures, not mine" (PN=001-004). The distant islands become a measure of George's incomplete engagement: he is not fully in the Romantic world of Rita's circle, nor can he return to the elemental life of the sea.

> "I had just returned from my second West Indies voyage. My eyes were still full of tropical splendour, my memory of my experiences, lawful and lawless, which had their charm and their thrill; for they had startled me a little and had amused me considerably. But they had left me untouched." ((PN=001-004))
