# Street of the Consuls

The Marseilles street that houses Doña Rita's town residence — a threshold between the public world of political conspiracy and the private world of romantic obsession.

## Location and Character

The Street of the Consuls is a narrow, quiet thoroughfare in old Marseilles, its "traffic mostly pedestrian and far between" (PN=012-014). Unlike the glittering Cannebière, it is a street of discretion — appropriate for a house that serves simultaneously as a Legitimist safe-house, a rendezvous point for Carlist officers, and the residence of the novel's most enigmatic woman.

## In the Novel

### The House

Rita's house in the Street of the Consuls features a black-and-white marble hall with a defective gas burner held aloft by a silver statuette (PN=003-075, PN=018-006). This hall — dimly lit, echoing, traversed by conspirators and visitors at all hours — becomes one of the novel's most atmospheric spaces. Monsieur George first enters it "on the heels of Captain Blunt" and later lives there as Rita's tenant and protector (PN=003-075).

### The Mistral

The street is exposed to the mistral, the fierce Provençal wind that "rushing down the street of the Consuls made me shiver to the very marrow" (PN=003-075). The wind becomes a recurring motif — cold, cleansing, indifferent to human intrigues — that marks arrivals and departures at the house.

### Therese's Domain

Under Therese's management, the Street of the Consuls house acquires an almost conventual atmosphere. She receives visitors — including the Marquis de Villarel's secretary — with "piously ghoulish" suspicion, defends her territorial claim ("the house was her property"), and brings her peasant religiosity to bear on its "godless" history (PN=018-006, PN=012-020).

### A Conspiratorial Address

The house's address is known to the Carlist network. Mills and Blunt visit; Dominic is brought there by George; a stranger in a top hat lets himself in with a latchkey late at night (PN=010-031). The street's quietness makes every arrival audible, every departure significant.
