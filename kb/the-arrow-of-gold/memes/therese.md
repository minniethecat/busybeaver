# Therese

Doña Rita's older sister—"six, ten years older than my sister" (008-027)—who serves as a moral counterweight to the novel's world of passion and political intrigue, embodying the claims of conventional piety against the freedoms Rita has claimed.

## Identity

- **Full Name**: Therese (surname unknown)
- **Nationality / Origin**: Basque, from the same rural background as Rita
- **Affiliation**: Conventional Catholic morality; disapproves of Rita's life
- **First Appearance**: First glimpsed in the household (002-101)

## Role in the Narrative

Therese functions as the voice of moral judgment within the novel's otherwise permissive world. When Rita shows her bruises from an altercation, she tells Therese, who becomes a confidante of sorts (008-025). The narrator's first encounter with "Therese of the whispering lips" (002-101) establishes her as a figure of hushed disapproval. Her presence in Rita's household introduces a constant tension between the sisters' divergent life choices.

Therese is "six, ten years older than my sister" (008-027), an age gap that positions her as a maternal or guardian figure rather than a peer. Her moral authority derives from this seniority and from her adherence to traditional values that Rita has rejected.

## Characterization

Conrad presents Therese through physical detail that suggests her moral severity: "Therese of the whispering lips and downward eyes" evokes a figure of conventual restraint. Her whispers suggest a world of judgment delivered in undertones, never openly stated but always present.

## Critical Interpretation

Therese represents the world Rita left behind—the world of Basque peasant piety and conventional morality. Her presence in the novel serves to remind the reader that the freedom Rita enjoys has costs, measured in the disapproval of those who represent the claims of tradition and family.
