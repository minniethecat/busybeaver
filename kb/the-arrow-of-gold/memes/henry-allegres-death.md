# Henry Allègre's Death

The death of Henry Allègre — the art collector and protector who had shaped Doña Rita's entire adult life — occurs before the novel's main action, yet its reverberations define her circumstances throughout. Rita's firsthand account of his final moments, delivered to Mills and Monsieur George in the garden of her Paris hotel, is one of the most intimate and psychologically revealing passages in the novel.

## Summary

On an early morning, at four o'clock, Henry Allègre dies in his bed. Rita, summoned from sleep, runs barefoot into his room. He recognizes her and whispers two final utterances: "You are flawless," then, after a pause for thought, "Such is my character. I am like that." When Rita asks if she should lift him higher on his pillows — she is strong enough; she has done it before — he raises his hand just enough to signify that he does not wish to be touched. It is his last gesture. Rita, who has never witnessed death, experiences a powerful impulse to flee: "I nearly ran out of the house just as I was, in my nightgown." Allègre's will, written on a half sheet of paper bearing his personal device of an Assyrian bull, leaves his entire fortune and art collection to Rita — transforming her overnight into "the heiress of Mr. Allègre," a public figure stripped of the protective obscurity he had maintained around her.

## Chronology

### Lead-up

Henry Allègre had been Rita's guardian for approximately eight years, since discovering her as a young goatherd in the hills of northern Spain. He provided her with an education, surrounded her with art and intellectual company at his Pavilion in Passy, and — by Rita's own account — filled "the whole field of vision for me" ([006-010]). He exercised an almost total control over her exposure to the world: "no nun could have had a more protected life" ([006-010]). According to Mr. Blunt's account, Allègre's death occurred less than three months after his last morning ride with Rita in the Bois de Boulogne — the final occasion on which "she surveyed the world of men and women from the saddle" ([003-015]).

Allègre's will was notably informal: "written on a half sheet of paper, with his device of an Assyrian bull at the head" ([003-014]). He had no family, no parentage in the social sense — Blunt calls him "a man absolutely without parentage. Without a single relation in the world. Just a freak" ([003-013–003-014]). The absence of any natural heir meant his entire fortune, including the significant art collection, passed uncontested to Rita.

### The Event

Rita's firsthand account of the death is the novel's definitive record:

> "The morning he died they came to call me at four o'clock. I ran into his room barefooted. He recognized me and whispered, 'You are flawless.' I was very frightened. He seemed to think, and then said very plainly, 'Such is my character. I am like that.' These were the last words he spoke." ([006-012])

She continues:

> "I hardly noticed them then. I was thinking that he was lying in a very uncomfortable position and I asked him if I should lift him up a little higher on the pillows. You know I am very strong. I could have done it. I had done it before. He raised his hand off the blanket just enough to make a sign that he didn't want to be touched. It was the last gesture he made." ([006-012])

Her immediate reaction was visceral and disorientating:

> "I hung over him and then — and then I nearly ran out of the house just as I was, in my nightgown. I think if I had been dressed I would have run out of the garden, into the street — run away altogether. I had never seen death. I may say I had never heard of it. I wanted to run from it." ([006-012])

She murmurs the phrase "*Fuir la mort*" — to flee from death ([006-012]). The experience was a violent disruption of her sheltered existence:

> "When the end came it was like falling out of a balcony into the street. It was as sudden as that." ([006-016])

Yet she adds a crucial qualification: "I didn't break anything — not even my heart" ([006-016]).

Allègre's final words — "You are flawless. Such is my character. I am like that" — become for Rita both a benediction and a burden. Later she reflects on them more fully:

> "I shall never forget his last words. He saw me standing beside his bed, defenceless, symbolic and forlorn, and all he found to say was, 'Well, I am like that.'" ([006-032])

### Aftermath

The immediate aftermath was chaos. According to Rita, the first person to insert himself was the journalist-politician with the "white hair" — a man who "knows how to worm his way in anywhere":

> "In the first days he was useful and somehow he made it look as if Heaven itself had sent him. In my distress I thought I could never sufficiently repay... Well, I have been paying ever since." ([006-022])

People descended upon the house: "all sorts of people that Henry Allègre used to know — or had refused to know. I had a sensation of plotting and intriguing around me, all the time" ([006-022]). Soon Don Rafael de Villarel, a Carlist grandee, arrived on behalf of the Pretender to offer condolences — the first step in Rita's recruitment into the Carlist cause.

Approximately a year and a half later, Rita returned to Paris, and the press anointed her: "The heiress of Mr. Allègre has taken up her residence again amongst the treasures of art in that Pavilion so well known to the élite" ([003-022]). The label stuck. Blunt observes that from this point, "Doors fly open before the heiress of Mr. Allègre. She has inherited the old friends, the old connections" ([003-034]).

It was at this juncture that Rita adopted the name "Madame de Lastaola," explaining to George: "one must have a name. That's what Henry Allègre's man of business told me. He was quite impatient with me about it. But my name, amigo, Henry Allègre had taken from me like all the rest of what I had been once. All that is buried with him in his grave" ([008-009]).

Monsieur George, reflecting on Allègre's death from a narrative distance, interprets it with a harder edge:

> "It is strange how the death of Henry Allègre, which certainly the poor man had not planned, acquired in my view the character of a heartless desertion. It gave one a glimpse of amazing egoism in a sentiment to which one could hardly give a name, a mysterious appropriation of one human being by another as if in defiance of unexpressed things and for an unheard-of satisfaction of an inconceivable pride. If he had hated her he could not have flung that enormous fortune more brutally at her head. And his unrepentant death seemed to lift for a moment the curtain on something lofty and sinister like an Olympian's caprice." ([008-008])

## Narrative Function

Allègre's death is the novel's founding event — the catastrophe that sets every subsequent circumstance in motion. Without it, Rita would remain the protected inhabitant of the Pavilion; with it, she is thrust into the exposed position of a wealthy, unattached woman in a world of predatory men. The death functions on multiple levels:

**As psychological rupture.** Rita's account is dominated by the language of dislocation: she is "falling out of a balcony," wanting to "run away altogether." The death is her expulsion from the garden — the privileged, curated existence Allègre built — into the street of ordinary human complication.

**As identity crisis.** Allègre has taken her original name, her original self, "like all the rest of what I had been once," and buried it with him. She must construct a new identity — "Madame de Lastaola" — from fragments of memory (a Basque hillside where she once grazed goats).

**As moral test.** George's verdict — "heartless desertion," "Olympian's caprice" — suggests that even Allègre's final act of beneficence (leaving Rita everything) was really an act of egoism: the collector making a posthumous gift of his most prized possession. Whether this is fair or merely George's jealousy speaking is left ambiguous.

**As structural device.** The death is a backstory event narrated retrospectively, allowing Conrad to build Rita's character through the gap between the naive girl who "had never seen death" and the worldly woman who recounts the story — between the barefoot girl in the nightgown and "Madame de Lastaola," the heiress who can open any door in Paris.
