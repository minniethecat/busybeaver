# South Carolina

South Carolina appears in *The Arrow of Gold* not as a setting — no scene takes place there — but as a biographical anchor for Captain Blunt, explaining at once his courtly manners, his ambiguous Americanness, and the sense of displacement that defines him.

## Location

- **Geography**: Southeastern United States, on the Atlantic coast
- **Coordinates in the Novel**: Referenced only as the site of Blunt's family plantation — a world left behind
- **Real-World Counterpart**: Real — Conrad specifies "pre-abolition days," fixing the reference historically

## Role in the Narrative

Captain Blunt's South Carolina origin is introduced early as a gloss on his character. Mills, observing George's curiosity, explains simply: "The Captain is from South Carolina" (PN=001-052). This single fact carries considerable weight in the novel's social calculus — it places Blunt outside the European class system that the Legitimists navigate so carefully, making him at once exotic and unplaceable.

The fuller context of Blunt's Southern past emerges in a striking, almost throwaway observation: Rita reflects that when she was seventeen, Blunt's sister (her future sister-in-law through a marriage that never materialized) "wore snowy muslin dresses on the family plantation in South Carolina, in pre-abolition days" (PN=013-010). The phrase "pre-abolition days" does heavy historical work — it locates the Blunt family in the antebellum slave-holding South, implying wealth built on plantation agriculture, and hints at a world that no longer exists by the time of the novel's events (circa 1870s).

## Symbolic Dimension

South Carolina serves as a marker of Blunt's otherness. He is "eminently elegant" and "very Parisian" in appearance, yet Mills's quiet revelation of his American origin undercuts this surface. Blunt is a man without a clear national home: too polished to be the rough American, too Southern to be fully European, too displaced to return to a world that (in "pre-abolition days") has been swept away by history. This rootlessness mirrors the broader condition of the Legitimist cause itself — a movement fighting for a past that no longer has a place in the present.

> "Mills, sitting silent with his air of watchful intelligence, seemed to read my thoughts, waved his pipe slightly and explained: 'The Captain is from South Carolina.'" ((PN=001-052))
