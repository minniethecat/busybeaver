# Passy

Passy is a neighbourhood in the 16th arrondissement of Paris, on the Right Bank of the Seine. In *The Arrow of Gold*, it is the location of Henry Allègre's "mysterious Pavilion" — the house hidden away in this quiet quarter where Doña Rita held court over a circle of remarkable personalities, and where the novel's central conspiracy took its earliest shape.

## Location

- **Geography**: 16th arrondissement, Paris, on the Right Bank of the Seine, between the Bois de Boulogne and the Trocadéro
- **Coordinates in the Novel**: Referenced at the Café de la Cannebière, Marseilles, in Mills and Blunt's conversation about Allègre
- **Real-World Counterpart**: Real — Passy was annexed to Paris in 1860 and was, in the late nineteenth century, a fashionable residential quarter

## Role in the Narrative

Passy enters the novel through a cryptic reference during the Carnival-night conversation in Marseilles. As Mills and Blunt sketch the portrait of Henry Allègre for Monsieur George's benefit, they mention his multiple residences:

> "This house is full of costly objects. So are all his other houses, so is his place in Paris — that mysterious Pavilion hidden away in Passy somewhere." ((PN=002-012))

The Pavilion in Passy is the original setting of Doña Rita's transformation from an orange-merchant's niece into the enigmatic "Doña Rita" of Parisian legend. It was here that Allègre entertained "the privileged personalities great in art, in letters, in politics, or simply in the world," and where Rita sat on the big sofa "like an object of art from some unknown period." After Allègre's death, Rita inherited the Pavilion, and it became a node in the Legitimist network — a place where Carlist agents, Spanish grandees, and secret emissaries could meet under the cover of social life.

## Symbolic Dimension

Passy represents the paradox of Rita's position: a place of seclusion that is also a centre of worldly power. The neighbourhood's quiet, residential character — "hidden away" — mirrors Rita's own ambiguous status: visible only to the elect, mysterious to everyone else. The Pavilion in Passy is the spatial correlative of the "sacred core" that the narrator identifies at the heart of every mystery: a place one must be admitted to, never merely stumble upon.

> "…so is his place in Paris — that mysterious Pavilion hidden away in Passy somewhere." ((PN=002-012))
