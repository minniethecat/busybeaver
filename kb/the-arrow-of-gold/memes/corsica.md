# Corsica

A Mediterranean island off the coast of France and Italy, Corsica appears in *The Arrow of Gold* in two distinct time-frames: as the destination of Henry Allègre's early elopement with Rita, and later as the location where Dominic finds the sailing vessel essential to the Carlist gun-running plot.

## Location

- **Geography**: Mediterranean island, west of Italy and southeast of mainland France
- **Coordinates in the Novel**: Off the southern coast of France, accessible by mail-boat from Marseilles
- **Real-World Counterpart**: Real — Corsica is a French territorial collectivity; Conrad's use is literal, not fictionalized

## Role in the Narrative

Corsica functions as a romantic origin-point and a practical resource. In Blunt's account of Rita's early history with Allègre, Corsica is where the painter first carried her off — a "yearly pilgrimage" that marks the beginning of their life together (PN=002-068). Allègre built a house there, one of many properties he constructed "all over the place," and after his death Rita inherited it along with everything else he owned (PN=002-097). Blunt cynically observes that "that Corsican palace is the portion that will stick the longest to Doña Rita," since no one would want to buy such a remote and extravagant property.

Later, when the Carlist conspiracy requires a fast smuggling vessel, Dominic knows "of one suitable but she was in Corsica" and offers to "start for Bastia by mail-boat in the morning" to secure her (PN=007-007). This transforms Corsica from a repository of sentimental memory into an operational asset for the conspiracy.

## Symbolic Dimension

Corsica embodies the duality of Rita's inheritance from Allègre: material wealth that is also a burden. The house is simultaneously a "palace" and a white elephant — magnificent but unsellable, like the art collection and the Prado villa. The island's dual role — pilgrimage site in the past, logistical asset in the present — mirrors the novel's broader tension between romantic memory and political action.

> "They were on their way to Corsica. A yearly pilgrimage. Sentimental perhaps. It was to Corsica that he carried her off — I mean first of all." ((PN=002-068))
