# Dominic's Balancelle Voyages

The series of clandestine Mediterranean voyages in the balancelle *Tremolino*, including the Corsica expedition to purchase the vessel and the smuggling runs supplying Carlist forces on the Spanish coast.

## Summary

Dominic's balancelle voyages form the operational backbone of the Carlist gun-running enterprise. After George and Dominic agree on the plan at Madame Léonore's café on the Marseilles quay, Dominic departs immediately for Bastia by mail-boat to purchase a suitable vessel [860]. He returns with a balancelle—a traditional Mediterranean lateen-rigged craft prized for its speed, shallow draft, and ability to work close to windward—that will become the *Tremolino*. The voyages that follow are the novel's purest expression of seamanship and clandestine action: night passages across the Gulf of Lions, moonless approaches to the Spanish coast, rendezvous with mule-trains of Carlist partisans waiting on the beach, and the constant, shared vigilance of men who know that "in that sort of enterprise there is no room for mistakes" [918]. Each voyage deepens the partnership between George and Dominic, whose grim joviality and absolute competence provide the human ballast for George's more romantic engagement with the Carlist cause.

## Key Voyages

### The Corsica Expedition

The enterprise begins at Madame Léonore's café, where George and Dominic hold "an earnest and endless confabulation" over a marble table, heads close together [860]. Dominic, a former smuggler who regards upper-class political enthusiasms with genial scorn, cannot resist the opportunity to exercise his talents. They agree the vessel must be a balancelle "and something altogether out of the common" [860]. Dominic sails for Bastia the next morning. His return with the *Tremolino* marks the true commencement of operations.

### The First Trip

The inaugural voyage is described as "in the nature of a trial trip" [918], carrying all the anxiety of an untested enterprise. George's gnawing concern—whether every man involved will be "intelligent enough, faithful enough, bold enough"—is shared by Dominic. The success of this first run establishes the pattern for the voyages that follow: the quiet departure from Marseilles, the passage across the Gulf of Lions, the landfall on the Spanish coast, and the rendezvous with Carlist irregulars who "from the very moment they start on a trip risk a bullet in the head at any moment, but who have a perfect conviction that I will never fail them" [2402].

### Subsequent Runs

The rhythm of the voyages becomes George's life. He returns from each trip to the Pavilion on the Street of the Consuls, to the unchanging welcome of Therese, to stolen hours with Doña Rita. Dominic's "grim joviality" subsides during the final twenty-four hours of each homeward run, and he customarily abandons George without a word at the quay, returning to Madame Léonore. The voyages vary in their particulars—different cargoes, different landing points on the Spanish coast, different hazards of weather and patrol—but share the essential character of all smuggling work: stealth, competence, and the peculiar intimacy of men who depend on each other for their lives.

### The Final Catastrophe

The last voyage ends in disaster. The *Tremolino*, "broken and gone like the only toy of a lonely child" [2630], is lost. The circumstances are not a fair fight at sea but carry "the memory of a suicide"—a deliberate destruction rather than a defeat. The catastrophe destroys not only the vessel but Dominic's "moral entity," leaving him a diminished figure whose "diminished head" [2738] George cannot even locate afterward. George himself survives to wash ashore at Marseilles, entering the city by rail after "many adventures, one more disagreeable than another" [2630], exactly twelve months after the Carnival evening that began his involvement in the Carlist world.

## Narrative Function

The balancelle voyages represent the novel's most sustained engagement with the sea—Conrad's great subject—and perform several narrative functions: (1) they ground the Carlist political intrigue in the physical realities of wind, wave, and clandestine navigation, preventing the novel from becoming purely a salon drama; (2) the partnership of George and Dominic offers the novel's most uncomplicatedly positive relationship, based on shared competence and mutual trust rather than the desire, jealousy, and ambiguity that characterize all the other relationships; (3) the *Tremolino*'s destruction enacts a classic Conradian pattern—the loss of a ship as the loss of a world—and prepares George for the inward drama of the novel's final movement; (4) Madame Léonore and her café provide a counter-setting to the Pavilion, a world of working sailors and honest affection that stands outside the corruptions of politics and wealth.
