# The Gun-Running Operation

The Carlist arms-smuggling enterprise across the Mediterranean, organized by Monsieur George with the seaman Dominic using the balancelle *Tremolino*, supplying munitions to the Legitimist forces in Spain.

## Summary

The gun-running operation is the central enterprise of the novel's middle movement and the practical expression of George's commitment to the Carlist cause—and to Doña Rita. Recruited by Mills and Blunt precisely because of his maritime experience and connections among the sailors of the Old Port, George organizes a supply line to run arms and ammunition to the Carlist detachments fighting in southern Spain. He partners with Dominic, a Corsican seaman of absolute fidelity and recklessness, whose balancelle *Tremolino* becomes the operation's workhorse. The enterprise combines political commitment, seamanship, and the peculiar romance of contraband: night passages across the Gulf of Lions, clandestine landfalls on the Spanish coast, the constant threat of Republican warships. The operation defines George's role in the Carlist world—he is not a salon politician but the man who gets things done at sea. Its eventual catastrophic collapse, described as "a stunning thunderclap" [2630], leaves George shipwrecked, destitute, and spiritually shattered, washing him back to Marseilles on the last evening of Carnival, exactly one year after the meeting that began it all.

## Chronology

### Lead-up

The Carlist cause urgently requires a maritime supply line for arms and ammunition. Mills, having learned of George's eccentric maritime background—his pretensions to seamanship, his contacts in the Old Port, his "ill-defined and vaguely illegal enterprise in the Gulf of Mexico" [076]—identifies him as "the very person" the legitimists need. At the Carnival Meeting, Mills tells the cautionary tale of his own arms-running attempt: a small steamer chartered from a wealthy relative, chased ashore by the Republican ironclad *Numancia*, ending in shipwreck below Bayonne and Mills swimming to shore "clad simply in a money belt and a pair of trousers" as shells fell around him [160]. The lesson is clear: the next attempt must be smarter, smaller, and entrusted to a real seaman.

### The Operation

George takes command. He engages Dominic, a Corsican sailor of formidable competence—"the embodiment of fidelity, resource, and courage" [1076]—and his balancelle *Tremolino*. The balancelle, a traditional Mediterranean lateen-rigged vessel, is ideal for the work: fast, shallow-draft, unobtrusive among the coastal traders. The operation involves multiple voyages: running cargoes from French ports to the Spanish coast, evading the Republican naval patrols, navigating the treacherous Gulf of Lions in variable weather. Dominic proves unshakeable; his first meeting with Doña Rita establishes a bond of mutual recognition—"her seduction, his recklessness, were both simple, masterful and, in a sense, worthy of each other" [1076].

George's connection to Rita deepens through the work. She is the operation's patron and enabler—her connections, her fortune inherited from Henry Allègre, her access to "any army over the frontier" [524] make the logistics possible. George, for his part, brings the seamanship and the nerve. Rita suggests he take up quarters in her house on the Street of the Consuls, the Pavilion, cementing his dual identity as Carlist operative and inhabitant of Rita's intimate world.

### The Collapse

The operation ends in catastrophe. The *Tremolino*, "broken and gone like the only toy of a lonely child" [2630], is lost at sea. The circumstances are not a fair fight but "the memory of a suicide"—a deliberate scuttling or sacrifice that destroys not only the vessel but "all that there was in me of independent life" [2630]. Dominic's "moral entity" is destroyed by what he regards as "a most tragic ending of our common enterprise." George survives, but barely, finding himself "weary, heartsore, my brain still dazed," entering Marseilles by rail after "many adventures, one more disagreeable than another" [2630].

### Aftermath

The collapse of the gun-running operation marks the end of George's active Carlist service. He returns to Marseilles penniless, unshaven, and unwashed, on the last evening of Carnival—exactly twelve months after the meeting that recruited him into the affair. The symmetry is devastating: "It was exactly twelve months since that other carnival evening when I had felt a little weary and a little lonely but at peace with all mankind" [2680]. The operation's failure strips George of everything but his connection to Rita, which now deepens into the novel's final movement of passion and recovery. The practical Carlist work—the smuggling, the voyages, the partnership with Dominic—gives way to the inward drama of love, jealousy, and the duel that awaits.

| Mills | The original architect: his earlier failed attempt with the *Numancia* provided the cautionary lesson that shaped George's approach |

## Narrative Function

The gun-running operation serves as the novel's action spine and accomplishes several structural tasks: (1) it provides the practical substance of George's involvement with the Carlist cause—he is not merely Rita's lover but a man of action risking his life at sea; (2) it introduces Dominic, the novel's most uncomplicatedly admirable figure, whose loyalty and competence offer a moral counterweight to the ambiguities of Blunt, Mills, and the Carlist politicians; (3) the *Tremolino*'s destruction enacts the novel's governing pattern of loss and initiation—George must lose everything (ship, purpose, independence) before he can fully understand what Rita means to him; (4) the operation's collapse on the anniversary of the Carnival Meeting creates the novel's most powerful structural irony, framing the entire adventure as a cycle of entry and exit, birth and death, sealed by the symmetry of Carnival.
