# Rose

Doña Rita's devoted maid, whose quiet competence and unwavering loyalty anchor the domestic world that surrounds the novel's central intrigue.

## Identity

- **Full Name**: Rose (surname unknown)
- **Nationality / Origin**: Likely French
- **Affiliation**: Doña Rita's household
- **First Appearance**: Summoned by Rita to attend to Monsieur George (007-056)

## Role in the Narrative

Rose appears at key domestic moments, summoned by Rita to attend to household needs: "rang for Rose, and told her to bring me my... " (007-056). Her presence establishes the domestic texture of Rita's world—the house in Marseilles with its servants, its routines, its quiet spaces that contrast with the dangerous political operations conducted within its walls.

Rose's loyalty extends beyond mere service. She travels to meet Rita at the railway station (008-029) and is described as someone who "was going to stop with her for..." (008-030), suggesting a personal devotion that goes beyond the employer-servant relationship. Rita herself acknowledges that Rose "would have done for me that..." (008-028), implying a depth of care that the narrative only partially reveals.

## Characterization

Conrad presents Rose through her actions rather than through description—she is defined by what she does for Rita rather than by any independent characterization. This functional presentation reflects her social position as a servant, but also serves the novel's focus on the central figures; Rose exists to enable Rita's world.

## Critical Interpretation

As a servant figure, Rose belongs to a tradition in Conrad's fiction of secondary characters whose loyalty illuminates the moral qualities of the protagonists they serve. Her quiet competence provides a baseline of normalcy against which the novel's more dramatic events are measured.
