# The Balcony Scene

The "balcony scene" is not a single dramatic incident but a recurring motif in the novel: Monsieur George's solitary presence in the street of the Consuls at night, below the windows of the house where Doña Rita dwells, suspended between his life as a gun-runner and his consuming devotion to a woman he can neither possess nor abandon.

## Summary

George's room in Rita's house on the street of the Consuls places him in a state of perpetual proximity and distance. The windows "gave out on the street of the Consuls which as usual was silent" ([256-257]). From this vantage, George watches, waits, and reflects — never quite inside Rita's life, never quite outside it. The street itself becomes a liminal space: by day, the thoroughfare of diplomats and consulates; by night, an empty corridor where George returns in disguise, avoiding recognition, slipping into the house that is both home and not-home. The "balcony" of the title is metaphorical — Rita herself provides the image when she describes her protected life with Allègre as "looking at mankind from a fourth-floor window" and her sudden exposure to the world as "falling out of a balcony into the street" ([227-228]).

## Chronology

### Lead-up

After George takes up residence in Rita's house in the street of the Consuls — a move she suggested to facilitate his work for the Carlist cause — he inhabits a strange in-between existence ([206]). The house is a "known outpost of Legitimacy" yet also Rita's property, managed by her sister Therese. George's room on the first floor overlooks the silent street. His gun-running missions take him away for days at a time; his returns are furtive, often at night.

### The Vigil

The pattern of George's nighttime returns establishes the vigil motif. He comes back from the Café Léonore dressed as a common sailor to avoid recognition, slipping through dark streets to the Consuls quarter ([260-261]). He observes the house, the street, the rare passersby — once witnessing three strangers (two girls and a man in a top hat) enter the house ahead of him, a sight that fills him with inexplicable unease ([261]). Therese, encountered in the darkness with a candle, remarks on the danger of the street: "This is a very lonely street. What could prevent you to kill me now and then walk out again free as air?" ([262]).

In the morning light, George stands at his window "not knowing what I was looking at across the road — the Desert of Sahara or a wall of bricks" ([264]). His vigil is inward as much as outward: the street below becomes a blank screen for his obsession. Blunt visits him in this room, looks out the same window, and remarks with heavy irony: "Isn't this street ridiculous?" ([265]).

### Metaphorical Resonance

Rita's own language provides the deepest key to the scene's significance. Recounting her life with Allègre, she says: "My life might have been described as looking at mankind from a fourth-floor window for years. When the end came it was like falling out of a balcony into the street" ([227-228]). The image of the balcony — a platform suspended between interior and exterior, private and public — captures the condition of both main characters: Rita fell from hers when Allègre died; George remains on his, unable either to enter fully into Rita's life or to walk away.

### Aftermath

The vigil motif persists through the novel's climactic events. On the second Carnival night, exactly one year after the fateful meeting, George walks the streets in a state of "philosophic indifference," bereaved of the world and composing a letter of farewell to Rita in his head ([267-268]). His encounter with Ortega on that night — finding the wretched journalist wandering the streets — will lead directly to the violent crisis at the Pavilion. The street, which has been the site of George's passive longing, becomes the scene of action and danger.

## Narrative Function

The balcony/street motif is Conrad's formal device for representing George's psychological state. George is a man of action — a sailor, a gun-runner — yet his most significant moments occur in stillness, waiting, watching. The street of the Consuls, with its diplomatic flags and nocturnal silence, externalizes his condition: he is a participant in a political cause to which he is personally indifferent, driven by a passion he cannot act upon. The motif also echoes the novel's broader concern with thresholds and boundaries — between sea and land, action and contemplation, possession and renunciation. Rita's balcony metaphor ("falling out of a balcony into the street") becomes the novel's governing image of the transition from protected innocence to exposed experience.
