# Blunt's Duel with George

The pistol duel between Captain Blunt and Monsieur George in a walled garden near Marseilles, fought over Doña Rita, in which both men are gravely wounded.

## Summary

After George's return to Marseilles following the collapse of the gun-running enterprise, a friend warns him that Captain Blunt has been spreading calumny in Parisian society: that Doña Rita has become "the prey of a young adventurer who was exploiting her shamelessly" [3342]. George, honour-bound to defend Rita's reputation, challenges Blunt. The duel is fought in a walled garden outside Marseilles—correct in every conventional particular yet charged with an earnestness "that could be felt in the very air" [3352]. George fires first on the word and hits Blunt in the upper arm, disabling his pistol hand. But Blunt, with "nothing equivocal about his determination," deliberately transfers the weapon to his left hand, takes careful aim, and shoots George through the left side of the breast [3356]. Both men survive, though George hovers near death for many days. The duel is the novel's climactic act of male violence and the last appearance of Captain Blunt in the narrative.

## Chronology

### Lead-up

The rivalry between George and Captain Blunt has simmered since the Carnival Meeting. Blunt, an American legitimist and Parisian man-about-town, had been among the first to recognize Doña Rita's extraordinary qualities when she was Henry Allègre's protégée. His feelings for her—a mixture of genuine passion, class condescension, and hatred of her inherited wealth—created what Rita called "a false situation" from which he retreated "like a wounded man" [2266]. Blunt's mother, the formidable Mrs. Blunt, had her own designs on Rita's fortune and position.

After the collapse of the *Tremolino* enterprise and George's return to Marseilles, a friend reports that Blunt has been slandering George in Paris, calling him an adventurer exploiting Rita. George, whose love for Rita has been sealed by shared danger and sacrifice, sees no alternative to a challenge.

### The Event

The duel takes place in a walled garden within easy driving distance of Marseilles. Four seconds and two surgeons attend—conventionally correct arrangements that barely contain the intensity beneath. Before the signal, George breaks protocol: "Disregarding the severe rules of conduct in such cases Monsieur George approached his adversary and addressed him directly" [3352], making a final appeal to Blunt's reason. Blunt responds only with a small bow—"perfectly ruthless" [3356].

On the word, George fires first. Whether by luck or skill, his shot strikes Blunt in the upper arm, rendering his pistol hand useless. But Blunt does not drop his weapon. With "the greatest deliberation he reached with his left hand for his pistol and taking careful aim shot Monsieur George through the left side of his breast" [3356]. The seconds and surgeons rush forward.

### Aftermath

George is carried toward Marseilles at a walking pace. On the road, a brougham approaches from the opposite direction; a thickly veiled woman looks out, assesses the scene, and orders: "Follow my carriage" [3356]. It is Doña Rita. George drifts through days of fever and hallucination, seeing Rita and Rose in confused visions. Mills, summoned by Rita's telegram, arrives from his retreat as fast as "boats and trains could carry him South" [3376].

George survives. His convalescence is long, and when he finally regains full consciousness, he sends a message to Rita through Mills: "Tell her that now at last—surer than Mr. Blunt's bullet—the arrow has found its mark. There will be no more dreaming" [3404]. The duel marks Blunt's permanent exit from the story and George's passage through a near-death ordeal that transforms his understanding of love.

## Narrative Function

The duel is the novel's climactic scene of male violence and serves multiple structural purposes: (1) it brings the Blunt-George rivalry—established in the very first Carnival meeting—to its terminal point, removing Blunt from the narrative; (2) it literalizes the "arrow of gold" metaphor: George's declaration that Rita's arrow has struck "surer than Mr. Blunt's bullet" equates love with a wound more profound than any physical injury; (3) the duel's formal correctness—seconds, surgeons, a walled garden—contrasts with the raw intimacy of the hatred behind it, underscoring the novel's theme of passion barely contained by social forms; (4) George's near-death and convalescence provide the structural hinge between the novel's action (arms smuggling, Carnival, attack) and its final movement into the "season of roses" and the consummation of his relationship with Rita.
