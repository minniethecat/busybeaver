# The Carnival Meeting

Monsieur George's fateful encounter with Mills and Captain Blunt at a café on the Cannebière during the last evening of Carnival, leading to his recruitment into the Carlist conspiracy.

## Summary

On the last evening of Carnival in Marseilles, a young Monsieur George—feeling weary, idle, and a little lonely—strolls into a brightly-lit café on the Cannebière. There he encounters two men who will alter the course of his life: the burly, well-connected Mills and the insomniac Captain Blunt, an American expatriate and legitimist. The three strangers fall into conversation, later moving to a more select establishment, the Salon des Palmiers. Mills recounts his recent adventure running arms to the Carlist forces—a shipment undone by the Republican ironclad *Numancia*. The evening culminates at Blunt's studio apartment in the Street of the Consuls, a house that will become the Pavilion of Doña Rita. Unknown to George, Mills and Blunt had been deliberately searching for him that very evening, having already decided he should be "drawn into the affair" [078]. The meeting is thus not chance but design, and it inaugurates George's entry into the secret world of Carlist conspiracy, arms smuggling, and his fateful connection to Doña Rita.

## Chronology

### Lead-up

The narrative situates this evening as the closing of Carnival, "the last evening of that part of my life in which I did not know that woman" [136]. George, at this point a young man of adventure but no fixed direction, is in Marseilles with no particular purpose. Mills and Blunt, already operatives in the Carlist cause, have independently decided that George possesses the right qualities for their enterprise—Mills describes him as "a promising person, but, from another point of view, not dangerous" [078].

### The Event

George enters the café alone. The Cannebière outside is filled with the "bedlam" of Carnival: masked companies sweeping the streets, gusts of the cold mistral swaying the gas lights [096]. Inside, the café is "festal and overlighted, but cheerful" [094]. A waiter approaches for orders; it is then George learns his first fact about Captain Blunt—that he suffers from insomnia.

Their conversation is briefly interrupted by Prax, a sculptor friend of George's, who bursts in wearing a Faust costume complete with a property dagger. Prax addresses George as "Young Ulysses" and invites him to "an infernal supper" at the Maison Dorée [138]. George demurs, and Prax exits theatrically.

The three men talk; George, flattered by the company of these intriguing strangers, proposes they dine not at the riotous Maison Dorée but at the Salon des Palmiers—a legitimist establishment where George boasts of having a reserved corner table [198]. There, Mills tells the story of his recent shipwreck: a small steamer chartered to carry arms to the Carlist army, chased ashore on the French coast below Bayonne by the Republican ironclad *Numancia*. Mills swam to shore "clad simply in a money belt and a pair of trousers" while shells fell around him [160].

### Aftermath

After supper, Blunt leads the group to his rooms at a house on the Street of the Consuls. The house—with its black-and-white marble hall, its garden, and the studio lean-to where Blunt hosts them—strikes George with its shabby grandeur [220–222]. It is here that George will later encounter Doña Rita, whose presence transforms the house into "the Pavilion." The meeting closes the chapter of George's life before Rita and opens the chapter in which Carlist conspiracy, Mediterranean adventure, and an overwhelming passion will consume him.

## Narrative Function

The Carnival Meeting serves as the novel's initiating incident. Structurally, it performs four functions: (1) it introduces the two principal male figures—Mills and Blunt—who mediate between George and the Carlist world; (2) Mills's story of the *Numancia* shipwreck establishes the stakes and dangers of the Carlist enterprise; (3) the Carnival setting—with its masks, bedlam, and mistral—creates an atmosphere of disorientation and transformation that mirrors George's passage from one life to another; (4) the introduction of Blunt plants the seed of the romantic rivalry that will culminate in the novel's climactic duel. George's later reflection—"those two, Mills and Blunt, had been actually looking everywhere for our man" [078]—retrospectively frames the meeting as a calculated entrapment, not serendipity.
