# The Arrow of Gold

The title object of Conrad's novel — never explicitly defined within the narrative, yet functioning as a symbolic center around which themes of value, desire, and mystery coalesce.

## Role in the Narrative

The Arrow of Gold appears most directly in the novel's closing pages, where it is mentioned as the title of the work itself (026-002, 026-016). Its meaning is deliberately left open: it may suggest the golden arrow of Cupid (romantic love), the arrow of a compass pointing toward an elusive truth, or the arrow of a weathervane registering the shifting winds of political fortune. Conrad's characteristic indirection invites the reader to hold multiple interpretations simultaneously.

The object is never physically described or handled within the story, making it a purely symbolic presence — a title that names the work's central mystery rather than any single item within its world.
