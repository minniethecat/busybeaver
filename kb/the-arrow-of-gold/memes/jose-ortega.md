# José Ortega

A minor operative in the Carlist network whose dullness and utility together embody the unglamorous reality behind the novel's romantic adventure.

## Identity

- **Full Name**: José Ortega
- **Nationality / Origin**: Spanish
- **Affiliation**: Carlist cause
- **First Appearance**: Introduced as a potentially useful contact (019-022)

## Role in the Narrative

Ortega appears late in the novel as a figure in the Carlist network—described simply as "very useful. His name is José Ortega" (019-022). The narrator's subsequent encounter with him is memorably anticlimactic: "Señor Ortega was not stimulating. He was p..." (020-005). This deflation captures something essential about the novel's treatment of political conspiracy: it is, in practice, often boring.

The narrator's physical description of Ortega—"trod on my heels" (020-004), paying "no attention to us" (020-004)—paints a picture of awkwardness and social ineptitude that contrasts sharply with the romantic glamour the novel elsewhere cultivates.

## Characterization

Conrad uses Ortega to puncture the romantic atmosphere the novel has built. After chapters of intrigue, passion, and danger, Ortega is simply dull—a reminder that political conspiracy is made up of ordinary people as much as charismatic adventurers.

## Critical Interpretation

Ortega exemplifies Conrad's willingness to include the mundane within the romantic. His brief appearance serves as a reality check, reminding the reader that the Carlist cause depended on unglamorous operatives as well as magnetic figures like Doña Rita. This inclusion of the ordinary within the extraordinary is characteristic of Conrad's realist instincts.
