# Dominique

The practical man of action in the Carlist smuggling operation—a sailor whose competence and loyalty provide the operational backbone for the novel's central intrigue.

## Identity

- **Full Name**: Dominique (full name unknown)
- **Nationality / Origin**: Mediterranean; described as "that Mediterranean sailor" (007-007)
- **Affiliation**: Carlist gun-running operation
- **First Appearance**: Mentioned in the Author's Note as a figure from Conrad's earlier work (P03-006)

## Role in the Narrative

Dominique is the operational counterpart to the novel's more glamorous figures. While Doña Rita provides the connections and Monsieur Mills provides the introduction, Dominique provides the practical maritime expertise needed to actually run guns to the Carlist forces. The narrator goes "in search of Dominic" (007-007) when the operation requires practical execution.

Conrad explicitly connects Dominique to his earlier fiction, noting "my Dominic of The Mirror of the Sea" (P03-006). This intertextual reference positions Dominique within Conrad's broader fictional universe and suggests the character's enduring significance for the author. The narrator describes "the romantic qualifications of Dominic for the task" (007-009), emphasizing that even the practical operative carries a romantic aura.

## Characterization

Dominique represents the ideal of masculine competence in Conrad's fiction—the skilled seaman whose value lies in what he can do rather than in what he says. His Mediterranean identity and maritime expertise connect him to Conrad's own experience as a sailor and to his earlier fictional creations.

## Critical Interpretation

The explicit link to *The Mirror of the Sea* (1906) makes Dominique one of the few characters in *The Arrow of Gold* whom Conrad openly identifies as a recurring figure in his imaginative world. This self-referential gesture suggests that Dominique represents something essential in Conrad's vision of masculine competence and loyalty.
