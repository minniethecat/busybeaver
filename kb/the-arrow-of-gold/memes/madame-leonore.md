# Madame Léonore

The café proprietress whose establishment on the Marseilles quay becomes the unofficial headquarters of the Carlist gun-running operation, and the devoted companion of Dominic, the operation's maritime commander.

## Identity

- **Full Name**: Known only as Madame Léonore
- **Nationality / Origin**: Italian, a Roman by birth — described by Doña Rita as "une belle Romaine" (PN=007-010). She recalls growing up in a village and being haunted by a "young cavalier" she glimpsed at sixteen
- **Affiliation**: Neutral politically; her loyalty is entirely personal, centred on Dominic
- **First Appearance**: Late at night at her café when Monsieur George seeks out Dominic to recruit him for the Carlist enterprise (PN=007-007)

## Role in the Narrative

Madame Léonore's café functions as the maritime nerve-centre of the novel's action. It is where Dominic and Monsieur George plan their smuggling voyages, where they return exhausted after dangerous expeditions, and where the practical realities of the gun-running conspiracy intersect with everyday Marseilles life (PN=007-007, PN=010-001).

Her relationship with Dominic provides the novel's warmest depiction of mature, settled love — a counterpoint to the turbulent, unattainable passion that Doña Rita inspires. She treats Dominic with a blend of maternal tenderness, romantic possessiveness, and amused tolerance for his "frolics" with younger men (PN=007-007).

She is also a perceptive judge of character. Her pilgrimage to the Prado to observe Doña Rita — the "rival" Dominic has spoken of — yields one of the novel's most penetrating assessments: "She is for no man! She would be vanishing out of their hands like water that cannot be held" (PN=010-027). This judgment, delivered by a woman who makes no claim to refinement, proves more accurate than any other character's analysis of Rita.

During the novel's crisis, she provides refuge to Dominic as he fights off attackers outside her café, and her establishment remains the fixed point around which the conspirators' maritime operations revolve (PN=016-075–078, PN=025-006).

## Characterization

Conrad draws Madame Léonore with vivid, sensual detail. She is "a woman of thirty-five with an open Roman face and intelligent black eyes," moving with "mature nonchalant grace" in "a black silk skirt, with gold earrings, with her raven hair elaborately dressed" (PN=007-007, PN=010-002). Her voice is "rich," her manner poised, her intelligence sharp.

Her speech blends earthy wisdom with unexpected poetry. She can declare of Rita, in the same breath, that the younger woman is both "a head like a gem" and a creature "for no man" (PN=010-018, PN=010-027). Her closing gesture in her first major scene — opening her empty hand to illustrate the futility of holding Rita — encapsulates her role as the novel's voice of clear-eyed feminine pragmatism.

> "You men never grow up," she says, touching the grey hair above Dominic's temple — the novel's gentlest moment of domestic intimacy (PN=007-007).

## Critical Interpretation

Madame Léonore represents a Conradian type: the mature woman whose wisdom is earned through experience rather than refinement, and whose clear-eyed pragmatism exposes the illusions that drive the novel's aristocratic and political actors. She is one of the few characters in *The Arrow of Gold* who neither participates in nor is seduced by the glamour of the Carlist enterprise; she sees it as a "frolic of boys" (PN=007-007).

Her Roman origins — emphasised by Doña Rita's epithet "une belle Romaine" — associate her with a classical, almost monumental femininity, in contrast to Rita's elusive, half-supernatural allure. Where Rita is "water that cannot be held," Léonore is earth: grounded, present, and capable of holding onto her man through decades of dangerous work.
