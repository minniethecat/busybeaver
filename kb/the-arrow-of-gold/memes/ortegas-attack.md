# Ortega's Attack

José Ortega's violent intrusion at the Pavilion on the last night of Carnival, where he threatens Doña Rita with a dagger and is subdued by Monsieur George in the fencing-room.

## Summary

On the last night of Carnival—exactly one year after George's fateful meeting with Mills and Blunt—José Ortega arrives in Marseilles as a courier bound for Carlist headquarters at Tolosa. George, tasked with meeting him at the railway station, immediately recognizes Ortega's instability: a pale, shivering man with black shark-fin whiskers, red lips, and suspicious black eyes [2682]. Over supper at the Maison Dorée, Ortega's hostility sharpens into menace. On the table between them, his fingers grope among the knife handles; his eyes fix on George "as though his eyes had been stilettos" [2684]. George realizes with sudden clarity: "Must I then kill that brute?" [2742]. Arriving at the Pavilion on the Street of the Consuls, Ortega breaks into the house, produces a dagger, and attempts to force his way to Doña Rita's room. George intercepts him in the fencing-room—the studio space with its foils and masks—and in a desperate physical struggle disarms the intruder. The attack exposes the vulnerability of the Pavilion household and marks Ortega as the figure both Doña Rita and Rose had long feared.

## Chronology

### Lead-up

José Ortega is a childhood acquaintance of Doña Rita from the Basque hills—the boy who once stoned her among the goats, forced her to swear marriage, and whose obsessive hatred she later described as terrifying [1116–1124]. As an adult, Ortega has attached himself to Carlist circles, but his fixation on Rita has curdled into something dangerous. The Commissary General, unaware of Ortega's history, dispatches him as a courier to Tolosa and asks George to receive him at the Marseilles station [2670]. Rita herself has warned George of Ortega's violence: "His Southern blood makes him very violent sometimes" [1946]. Rose, too, carries a deep dread of him.

### The Event

George meets Ortega at the railway station on the last night of Carnival. Ortega is shivering, hungry, and radiating underhand hostility. George takes him to the Maison Dorée for supper, hoping to keep him away from the house where Rita resides [2684]. In the restaurant, Ortega's behaviour deteriorates: he accuses George of being "the chief of the great arms smuggling organization" and fixes him with a murderous stare. His fingers creep among the knife handles beside his plate—though the rounded restaurant knives prevent an immediate killing.

Walking through the Carnival streets to the Street of the Consuls, George contemplates the necessity of killing Ortega to protect Rita. "Between him and Doña Rita I couldn't hesitate" [2742]. At the house, Ortega grows increasingly agitated. He produces a dagger—a real weapon, unlike the restaurant knives—and lunges toward the interior rooms where Rita is. George meets him in the fencing-room (the studio with its practice foils, masks, and iron stove). In the struggle that follows, George disarms Ortega using the physical skills of his seafaring life. The commotion brings Therese and eventually Rose, while Rita remains sequestered in her upstairs room.

### Aftermath

Ortega is subdued but not killed. George, despite his earlier resolve, refrains from the ultimate act. The attack confirms the danger Ortega poses and the depth of his obsession. In the immediate aftermath, arrangements are made to remove Ortega from Marseilles—though his shadow hangs over the household. The attack deepens the bond between George and Rita: he has proven himself her protector in the most literal sense. The incident also foreshadows the novel's later violence—the duel with Blunt—and underscores the theme of physical peril woven through the romantic narrative.

## Narrative Function

Ortega's Attack is the novel's most concentrated scene of physical violence and the crisis point of Part Three. Its narrative functions are multiple: (1) it dramatizes the real danger underlying the Carlist intrigue—this is not merely political play-acting but a world where men carry daggers and jealousy turns murderous; (2) it completes George's transformation from recruited operative to Rita's sworn protector, giving physical proof of his devotion; (3) it introduces Ortega as a foil to both Blunt (the romantic rival who respects the forms of honour) and George (the man of action who acts without forms); (4) the Carnival setting—the same bedlam that inaugurated George's entry into this world exactly twelve months earlier—creates an ironic symmetry, marking the passage from innocence to the knowledge of what this life truly costs.
