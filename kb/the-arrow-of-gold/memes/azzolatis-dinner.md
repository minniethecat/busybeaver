# Azzolati's Dinner

Doña Rita's dinner with the international banker and financier Azzolati, arranged to gain access to a diplomatic contact, collapses into confrontation when Azzolati's mask of sentimentality slips to reveal a predatory contempt for the "penniless, beggarly lot of the dear, honest, meritorious poor" — prompting Rita to expel him from her presence.

## Summary

Needing to convey information to a diplomatic personage who has refused all direct contact, Doña Rita seizes upon Azzolati — a wealthy international banker and former acquaintance from the Allègre Pavilion days — as an intermediary. She invites him to a private dinner at her Paris hotel, making an elaborate toilette to impress him. Azzolati, for his part, arrives dressed "as if for a reception at court," displaying his decorations and an orange ribbon of some Catholic order ([225-226]). The dinner begins with Azzolati in an abjectly submissive mood, ready to do anything for Rita provided she promises never to shut her door against him. As the evening progresses and wine flows, Azzolati's tone shifts from sentimental groaning about his overstrained life to a sudden, brutal confession: his deepest satisfaction is "to feel all that penniless, beggarly lot of the dear, honest, meritorious poor wriggling and slobbering under one's boots" ([227]). Rita, shocked, orders him out. Azzolati retreats with crocodile tears, claiming she has misunderstood his "honesty."

## Chronology

### Lead-up

By this point in the narrative, Doña Rita has abandoned the Pavilion in Passy, finding it haunted by memories of Henry Allègre: "When I have to go there I begin to feel after an hour or so that it is haunted" ([224-225]). She now stays in Paris hotels. She needs to reach a diplomatic personage — someone who "mistrusts his own susceptibility" and has refused all direct contact ([225]). Azzolati, who has been bombarding Rita with messages reminding her that he is "an old friend," presents a convenient channel: he has been invited to a shooting party at Rambouillet where the diplomatic contact will also be present. Rita sends him a note inviting him to dinner.

### The Event

The dinner takes place in Rita's hotel suite. Rita dresses elaborately for the occasion — "It was a most solemn affair" — and Azzolati arrives in full formal regalia, a "brochette of all sorts of decorations on the lapel of his *frac*" with a broad orange ribbon across his shirtfront ([225]). Azzolati's initial manner is abjectly deferential: "He was ready to do anything in the world for me providing that I would promise him that he would never find my door shut against him as long as he lived" ([226]).

Rita, already irritated by a day of "irritating interviews," finds his submissive attitude unnerving. The conversation turns to Azzolati's complaints about his stressful life. Growing bored, Rita remarks that she is surprised a man of such wealth continues to strive for more. At this, Azzolati — "he must have been sipping a good deal of wine" — reveals his true nature: "No, you can't imagine what a satisfaction it is to feel all that penniless, beggarly lot of the dear, honest, meritorious poor wriggling and slobbering under one's boots" ([227]).

Rita's reaction is immediate and physical: "I felt my bare arms go cold like ice." She rings for Rose, has her fur cloak brought, and then delivers her verdict: "Take yourself off instantly. Go trample on the poor if you like but never dare speak to me again" ([227]). Azzolati makes a show of wounded dignity, claiming "I have only tried to be honest with you, Rita," and retreats, pretending to dash a tear from his eye ([227]).

### Aftermath

Rita recounts the dinner to Mills and Monsieur George, using it as an example of the ugly truths she constantly uncovers: "It's like taking the lids off boxes and seeing ugly toads staring at you. In every one. Every one" ([227]). She speculates that Azzolati "imagines himself a noble beast of prey" ([228]). The incident reinforces Rita's disgust with the world of power and money, and her sense that even her putative allies are contemptible. The practical consequence is that she loses her channel to the Rambouillet contact, further isolating her from the political networks she needs.

## Narrative Function

The Azzolati dinner scene functions as a moral test that Rita passes decisively. In a novel full of compromised characters — Blunt with his mercenary motives, the Carlist leaders with their incompetence, the journalists and courtiers with their intrigues — Rita's expulsion of Azzolati demonstrates an uncompromising moral instinct. She may be uncertain about her own identity and purpose, but she knows evil when she hears it. The scene also illuminates the transactional nature of Rita's world: everyone, from bankers to aristocrats, wants something from her, and "friendship" is merely a guise for self-interest. Her observation that revealing confidences are "the bitterest kind of insult" crystallizes a central theme: in a world stripped of illusions, intimacy becomes a form of violation.
