# Captain Blunt

The American adventurer whose polished charm masks a desperate passion—a rival to Monsieur George whose presence darkens the novel's romantic comedy into something more dangerous.

## Identity

- **Full Name**: Captain Blunt (first name not given in narrative)
- **Nationality / Origin**: American, from South Carolina; a Confederate veteran
- **Affiliation**: Former Confederate officer; now operates in European legitimist and Carlist circles
- **First Appearance**: Among the gathering at the legitimist salon where the narrator meets Mills (001-005)

## Role in the Narrative

Blunt functions as both foil and rival to Monsieur George. Where George is young, inexperienced, and emotionally transparent, Blunt is polished, worldly, and opaque. He is described as "spotlessly honourable. A delightful, aristocratic figure" (002-060), but this surface perfection conceals darker currents. His "dispassionate" manner of speaking (002-046) suggests emotional control that may be less composure than suppression.

Blunt's rivalry with George for Doña Rita's attention provides the novel's primary romantic tension. Unlike George, whose passion is presented as genuine if naive, Blunt's polished exterior suggests a man who has learned to perform emotion rather than feel it. His Confederate past—fighting for a lost cause—establishes him as someone who has already lived through one romantic political disaster and emerged, on the surface at least, intact.

## Characterization

Conrad presents Blunt through the narrator's wary eyes. The "delightful, aristocratic figure" is a performance, and the narrator's growing awareness of what lies beneath—desperation, perhaps violence—creates the novel's most sustained sense of interpersonal danger. Blunt is what George might become if his romantic illusions curdled into something harder.

## Critical Interpretation

Blunt belongs to Conrad's gallery of charming but dangerous men—figures like Gentleman Brown in *Lord Jim* whose surface polish conceals moral vacancy. His American identity and Confederate past position him as an outsider in European society, someone whose charm is the weapon of a man who belongs nowhere and must therefore perform belonging everywhere.
