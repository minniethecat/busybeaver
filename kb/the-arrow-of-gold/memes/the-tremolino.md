# The Tremolino

The Tremolino is the fast Mediterranean sailing craft — a balancelle — purchased by Dominic in Corsica for the Carlist arms-smuggling operation. Though never named in the novel's main narrative (where it is referred to only as "the little vessel" or "the little craft"), Conrad identifies it in the Author's Note as the same balancelle Tremolino that appears in *The Mirror of the Sea*. The vessel serves as the physical instrument of George's initiation into the "life of passion" and its deliberate wrecking marks the violent end of his Carlist adventure.

## Description

The Tremolino is a balancelle — a type of Mediterranean lateen-rigged sailing vessel known for speed and manoeuvrability, traditionally used for coastal trade and, in the hands of men like Dominic, for smuggling. Dominic insists on a vessel "altogether out of the common" ([007-007]). The purchase is arranged swiftly: Dominic knows of a suitable balancelle in Corsica and departs for Bastia by mail-boat the following morning.

The vessel is small — repeatedly called "the little craft" or "the little vessel" — with a quarterdeck intimate enough that George can later imagine Rita seated there cross-legged ([011-061]). After the purchase, shipwrights work on her deck under Dominic's supervision while George spends a day on board observing ([018-004]).

Conrad provides the vessel's name and lineage in the Author's Note:

> "I never tried to conceal the origins of the subject matter of this book... some reviewers indulged themselves with a sense of triumph in discovering in it my Dominic of *The Mirror of the Sea* under his own name (a truly wonderful discovery) and in recognizing the balancelle *Tremolino* in the unnamed little craft in which Mr. George plied his fantastic trade and sought to allay the pain of his incurable wound." ([P03-006])

The vessel has no armament and relies entirely on speed and seamanship to evade the Republican naval forces and customs patrols that hunt it.

## Provenance

- **Origin**: Corsica. Dominic knows of the vessel's availability through his Mediterranean maritime connections and travels to Bastia to secure the purchase.
- **Owner(s)**: Monsieur George (as financier and principal of the Carlist gun-running enterprise) and Dominic (as captain and sailing master). The vessel is acquired specifically for the Carlist cause.
- **Key Appearances**:
  - Purchase arranged at Madame Léonore's café in Marseilles ([007-007])
  - Shipwright refitting under Dominic's supervision ([018-004])
  - Night watches at sea, the vessel's light "dancing away at sea in the windy distance" ([009-031])
  - The deliberate wrecking, described as a "suicide" and a killing ([019-002], [021-021])

## Symbolic Significance

The Tremolino is the vehicle of George's initiation. Conrad states this directly in the Author's Note:

> "What the story of the *Tremolino* in its anecdotic character has in common with the story of *The Arrow of Gold* is the quality of initiation (through an ordeal which required some resolution to face) into the life of passion." ([P03-006])

The vessel's fate mirrors George's psychological trajectory. Its wrecking is not an accident but a deliberate act — "we had to wreck the little vessel. It was awful. I feel like a murderer. But she had to be killed" ([021-021]) — and George's language anthropomorphizes it: "It was a faithful little vessel" ([021-025]). The destruction is experienced as a kind of execution, and for George it precipitates a symbolic death of self:

> "The little vessel, broken and gone like the only toy of a lonely child, the sea itself, which had swallowed it, throwing me on shore after a shipwreck that instead of a fair fight left in me the memory of a suicide." ([019-002])

The simile "like the only toy of a lonely child" connects the vessel to George's emotional isolation and to the end of innocence. The shipwreck is a "suicide" not a battle — an extinguishing of the self that had been defined by the Carlist enterprise and his devotion to Rita.

George also weaves the vessel into his reveries of Rita, imagining her aboard in sailor's dress, the confined quarterdeck accommodating "her cross-legged attitudes" while "the blue sea would balance gently her characteristic immobility" ([011-061]). In this fantasy the vessel becomes a private theatre for the one passion that matters to him — a contained world where Rita might share his adventure.

## Narrative Function

The Tremolino serves as the enabling instrument of the novel's central action — the gun-running that supplies the Carlist cause and provides the pretext for George's continued proximity to Rita's world. Without the vessel, there is no smuggling operation; without the smuggling operation, George has no role in the conspiracy.

Structurally, the vessel's acquisition, operation, and destruction trace the arc of George's involvement. The purchase at Madame Léonore's café marks his commitment to the cause ([007-007]). The months of operations — "I threw myself into my unlawful trade with a sort of desperation, dogged and hopeless" — represent his immersion in a double life. The wrecking ([019-002], [021-021]) is the catastrophic rupture that returns him, destitute and disoriented, to Marseilles during Carnival — the novel's darkest moment of dislocation.

The vessel also serves a thematic function as the link between Conrad's maritime world and the novel's psychological terrain. By naming it in the Author's Note as the same Tremolino from *The Mirror of the Sea*, Conrad establishes continuity between the outwardly focused sea tales and this inward-turning narrative of erotic obsession. The little vessel is the last physical artefact of the adventure-story tradition within a novel that otherwise abandons action for introspection.
