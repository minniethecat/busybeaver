# Old Town (Marseilles)

The Old Town of Marseilles is the historic quarter surrounding the Vieux Port (Old Harbour). In *The Arrow of Gold*, it is the social world that defines Monsieur George's pre-conspiracy life: a zone of working-class maritime culture where the young adventurer formed friendships with "pilots, coasters, sailors, workers of all sorts." It is from this network that Dominic — the "desperate smuggler" turned Carlist mariner — would eventually be drawn.

## Location

- **Geography**: The historic quarter of Marseilles around the Vieux Port, characterized by narrow streets, quayside cafés, and a dense population of maritime workers
- **Coordinates in the Novel**: Referenced as George's social milieu before his recruitment by Mills and Blunt
- **Real-World Counterpart**: Real — the Quartier du Panier and the quays of the Vieux Port were, in the 1870s, a rough, working-class maritime district

## Role in the Narrative

The Old Town enters the novel as a piece of background intelligence gathered by Mills during his investigation of the young man who would become Monsieur George. Mills's portrait of George's double life is delivered in a single, precisely observed paragraph:

> "What Mills had learned represented him as a young gentleman who had arrived furnished with proper credentials and who apparently was doing his best to waste his life in an eccentric fashion, with a bohemian set (one poet, at least, emerged out of it later) on one side, and on the other making friends with the people of the Old Town, pilots, coasters, sailors, workers of all sorts. He pretended rather absurdly to be a seaman himself and was already credited with an ill-defined and vaguely illegal enterprise in the Gulf of Mexico." ((PN=P04-007))

This passage is crucial: it establishes George as a man who moves between two worlds — the bohemian-artistic (the poet, the salon) and the maritime-working-class (the Old Town, the sailors). It is precisely this duality that makes him "the very person" for the Carlist scheme. The Old Town is not merely a place but a credential: proof that George possesses the practical maritime contacts — Dominic foremost among them — without which the arms-smuggling operation would remain a drawing-room fantasy.

## Symbolic Dimension

The Old Town represents the unglamorous, practical side of the romantic adventure. The Carlist conspiracy presents itself in the language of chivalry and Legitimacy, but its execution depends on the skills and loyalties of working sailors — men like Dominic, whose world is the Old Town quays and Madame Léonore's café. George's ability to move comfortably among these men is what distinguishes him from the salon Legitimists and makes him operationally valuable. The Old Town is the novel's reminder that even the most idealistic political intrigues must touch ground somewhere — and that ground is not the Pavilion in Passy but the rough stones of the Vieux Port.

> "…making friends with the people of the Old Town, pilots, coasters, sailors, workers of all sorts." ((PN=P04-007))
