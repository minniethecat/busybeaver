# Romance

The double register of the novel—both a story of romantic entanglement and a self-conscious literary romance in the tradition of chivalric adventure, framed by Conrad as something beautiful but already departing from the modern world.

## Definition

"Romance" in *The Arrow of Gold* operates at two levels. At the surface, it names the genre of the tale: "the obvious romance for the use of royalists" ((001-006)) describes how the Carlist intrigue presents itself as ready-made narrative material. At a deeper level, it names a mode of experience—the investment of political action, personal relationships, and self-conception with the heightened significance of romantic feeling. Conrad's narrator, looking back, frames the entire narrative within the elegy of "the departing romance" ((P04-004)).

The novel's subtitle, "A Story Between Two Notes," itself suggests a romantic interlude—something contained, framed, set apart from ordinary life. Doña Rita explicitly invokes the concept: "Yes—there is nothing like romance while one is young. So I will" ((013-013)), linking romance to youth and suggesting its temporal boundedness.

## Context in the Novel

The novel's central relationship—between the young protagonist Monsieur George and the enigmatic Doña Rita—is conducted in the language of romance, but Conrad consistently troubles the boundary between genuine feeling and self-dramatization. The Carlist cause itself is described as having "the obvious romance for the use of royalists" ((001-006)), implying that political commitment may be as much an aesthetic and emotional choice as an ideological one.

The retrospective narrator's voice complicates the romance further: by framing the entire story as belonging to a departed world, Conrad suggests that romance itself is a form of historical consciousness—a way of investing the past with the heightened significance that the present cannot sustain. The "departing romance" is not merely the end of a love affair but the end of a historical era in which such romantic adventures were still possible.

### Key Passages

> "It was the obvious romance for the use of royalists but it arrested my attention." ((001-006))

> "Yes—there is nothing like romance while one is young. So I will." ((013-013))

> "The usual grave moral disapproval tinged by a shamefaced regret for the departing romance." ((P04-004))

## Critical Interpretation

Conrad's use of romance in this late novel has often been read as a return to the mode of his earlier work—particularly the Malay novels—but with a crucial difference in tone. Where the early romances are direct, *The Arrow of Gold* is reflexive: it knows it is a romance and comments on its own romantic character. The phrase "the departing romance" has been read by critics as Conrad's acknowledgment that the romance mode was itself historical, belonging to a literary tradition that modernism was in the process of displacing.

The novel's subtitle—"A Story Between Two Notes"—reinforces this self-consciousness. A "story" rather than a "history" or a "tale," it explicitly claims a lesser, more contingent generic status, as though apologizing for its own romantic character even as it indulges it.
