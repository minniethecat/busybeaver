# Maritime Alps

The Maritime Alps are the southernmost section of the Alpine mountain range, straddling the border between southeastern France and northwestern Italy. In *The Arrow of Gold*, they provide the setting for George and Doña Rita's retreat — a period of "deep and joyous tenderness" that the narrator deliberately suppresses from the main narrative, preserving only its bare geographic and emotional outline.

## Location

- **Geography**: Southern Alps, on the French-Italian border, extending from the Col de Tende to the Mediterranean coast near Menton
- **Coordinates in the Novel**: A "small house built of dry stones and embowered with roses" in the region of the Maritime Alps
- **Real-World Counterpart**: Real — the Maritime Alps (Alpes Maritimes) are a well-defined geographical region; the area around Menton and the Roya Valley was popular with English travellers in Conrad's time

## Role in the Narrative

The Maritime Alps appear at a pivotal structural moment: the transition between the novel's central drama and its aftermath. After the crises of the Carnival season — the duel, Ortega's attack, Rita's flight — George and Rita withdraw to a remote stone house in the mountains:

> "During their retreat in the region of the Maritime Alps, in a small house built of dry stones and embowered with roses, they appear all through to be less like released lovers than as companions who had found out each other's fitness in a specially intense way." ((PN=025-002))

The narrator confesses to having "suppressed" the pages describing this period, partly out of discretion ("a sustained commentary on love is not fit for every eye") and partly because he judges the material lacking in dramatic interest. The sole companion of the two hermits in their "rose-embowered hut of stones" was Rose, Doña Rita's devoted servant, whose "apprehensive, helpless desolation" the narrator imagines without being able to report.

## Symbolic Dimension

The Maritime Alps retreat is the novel's most explicit instance of narrative suppression — a gap that is also a meaning. The stone house embowered with roses evokes pastoral and Edenic imagery, but the narrator's refusal to narrate the idyll transforms it into a "sacred core" that must be protected from the reader's gaze. The contrast between the solidity of the dry-stone construction and the fragility of the roses that cover it mirrors the paradox of George and Rita's relationship: a "precarious bliss" built on an unyielding foundation of shared experience. The Maritime Alps are the geographical correlative of the novel's governing aesthetic principle — that some things are more powerful when withheld than when told.

> "During their retreat in the region of the Maritime Alps, in a small house built of dry stones and embowered with roses, they appear all through to be less like released lovers than as companions who had found out each other's fitness in a specially intense way." ((PN=025-002))
