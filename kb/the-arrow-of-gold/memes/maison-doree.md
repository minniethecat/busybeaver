# Maison Dorée

The gilded restaurant on the Cannebière whose "grand salon" and private rooms host both carnival revelry and the novel's most harrowing confrontation.

## In the Novel

### The Infernal Supper

The Maison Dorée first appears as the venue for Prax's "truly infernal supper" — a carnival-night gathering "organized across the road at the Maison Dorée﻿—upstairs" from the café where George meets Mills and Blunt for the first time (PN=001-024). Prax's Faust costume and theatrical manner set the tone for an evening that marks the boundary between George's bohemian life and his recruitment into the Carlist conspiracy.

### Ortega's Confrontation

The Maison Dorée returns in the novel's climactic section as the setting for José Ortega's final, desperate attempt to reach Doña Rita through George. George finds Ortega waiting outside: "The usual row of fiacres outside the Maison Dorée was not there" (PN=020-026) — a detail that heightens the atmosphere of isolation and menace. Their encounter in the restaurant's "grand salon" becomes a protracted psychological duel, with Ortega drinking heavily while George maintains a precarious composure (PN=023-004).

### Symbolic Space

The Maison Dorée — literally "the Gilded House" — functions as the novel's temple of surface appearances. Its opulence is both real and hollow; its private rooms conceal desperation as readily as they host celebration. The contrast between the carnival-night revelry of the opening chapter and Ortega's "torture" in the same venue later in the novel maps George's transformation from carefree bohemian to burdened conspirator.
