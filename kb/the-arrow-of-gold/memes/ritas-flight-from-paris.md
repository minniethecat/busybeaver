# Rita's Flight from Paris

Doña Rita's departure from Paris occurs at the climax of the personal crisis in Part IV, when the accumulated tensions of the Carlist conspiracy, romantic entanglements, and Ortega's menace converge, compelling Rita to abandon the Pavilion and George to choose duty over love.

## Summary

After the confrontations with Captain Blunt (who has declared his love and proposed marriage) and the growing threat from José Ortega, Doña Rita resolves to leave Paris. George, torn between his devotion to Rita and his commitment to the gun-running operation, arrives at the Pavilion on the evening of his scheduled sailing. In an emotionally charged scene in the Pompeiian room, Rita urges George to leave — not for the Carlist cause, but because she believes he must. Their encounter culminates in George kissing Rita's throat, after which she releases him. Dominic arrives to collect George for the night's sailing. Rose, Rita's maid, warns George of a "dangerous person" (Ortega) who is trying to see Rita alone and begs him to stay, but George departs "heroically," leaving Rose to exclaim bitterly that "Madame has no friends."

## Chronology

### Lead-up

The crisis has been building throughout Part IV. Captain Blunt has proposed to Rita and been rejected, leaving him "like a wounded man retreating after a fight" ([272-273]). Rita has confided in George about Blunt's proposal, her near-acceptance of it, and her refusal: "I have made up my mind not to lose my head" ([274-275]). Meanwhile, Ortega — the journalist and former associate of Henry Allègre — has been stalking Rita, and Rose has been struggling to keep him away from her mistress ([275-276]). George, who has been absent from his ship for two days, has forgotten his sailing orders entirely, consumed by the emotional intensity of the situation ([275-277]).

### The Event

George and Rita's final encounter takes place in the Pompeiian room at dusk. Rita urges George to confirm that he is leaving: "But it is true that you will go. You will surely. Not because of those people but because of me. You will go away because you feel you must" ([275-276]). As she embraces him, pressing his head to her breast and repeating her insistence that he leave, George kisses her throat. Rita reacts with stunned surprise, "as though she had never even heard of such a thing as a kiss in her life," and her arms fall away ([276-277]).

Dominic, who has been searching for George all afternoon, arrives at the Pavilion. He confirms that Madame Léonore has been uneasy about George since seeing "that American cavalier" (Blunt), and that nothing has changed with the sailing orders ([277-278]). Rose intercepts George in the hall, warning him that a dangerous person — Ortega — is "everlastingly trying to see Madame alone" and that she "daren't even speak to Madame about it" ([278]). Rose begs George to stay, but he refuses: "Not this time" ([278]).

### Aftermath

George departs into a rain squall, instructing Rose to tell Rita that "I am gone — heroically" ([279]). Rose screams after him: "No! Madame has no friends. Not one!" ([279]). Rita returns to Paris, leaving the Pavilion behind. George sails on his gun-running mission, and the separation marks the beginning of the final phase of their relationship — one in which Rita's isolation and vulnerability to Ortega become central to the novel's climactic events.

## Narrative Function

This scene marks the turning point from the romantic entanglement of Part IV to the darker, more perilous world of Part V. George's departure — and his refusal to stay and protect Rita — sets in motion the events that will culminate in Ortega's attack at the Pavilion. The kiss on Rita's throat, and her startled reaction, crystallizes the central dynamic of their relationship: an intense physical and emotional intimacy that both characters resist consummating. Rita's insistence that George leave "because of me" reveals her awareness that their relationship cannot coexist with the world of political intrigue and violence. Rose's despairing cry — "Madame has no friends" — foreshadows Rita's ultimate isolation and disappearance at the novel's end.
