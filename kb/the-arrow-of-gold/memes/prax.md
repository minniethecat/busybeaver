# Prax

The sculptor at the centre of Monsieur George's bohemian circle in Marseilles, whose theatrical flair and artistic sensibility provide a counterpoint to the novel's world of political conspiracy.

## Identity

- **Full Name**: Known only by the nickname "Prax" — "a sculptor whom we called Prax for short" (PN=001-018)
- **Nationality / Origin**: Presumably French; a fixture of the Marseilles bohemian scene
- **Affiliation**: Leader of the artistic-bohemian circle centred on the Cannebière
- **First Appearance**: Introduced as the ringleader of George's half-dozen idle companions — "the bohemian circle, not very wide" (PN=001-018)

## Role in the Narrative

Prax presides over the world Monsieur George inhabits before his recruitment into the Carlist conspiracy. He is the one who dubs George "Young Ulysses" — a nickname George accepts with pleasure — and his theatrical entrances, such as striding into a café "in a sort of medieval costume very much like what Faust wears in the third act," establish the tone of playful, self-conscious artistry that defines George's pre-conspiracy life (PN=001-018, PN=001-024).

His function is structural: he represents the aesthetic, bohemian world that George leaves behind when he enters the sphere of Doña Rita and political intrigue. His Faust costume — complete with "plumed velvet toque" and "property dagger" — is both comic and symbolic: the artist playing at damnation while the real dangers of the Carlist enterprise await (PN=001-024).

A single detail — the "strange head painted by El Greco" that Prax has hung on his wall and "used to rail at; yet not without a certain respect" — suggests genuine artistic discernment beneath the theatricality (PN=012-019). This El Greco painting, associated with spiritual intensity and elongated forms, later serves as George's point of reference for Therese's "extraordinary clayey aspect."

## Characterization

Conrad sketches Prax with economical, vivid strokes. His operatic entrance — Faust costume, plumed hat sweeping the floor, hand on dagger hilt — is pure theatricality, yet Mills and Blunt, the novel's most discerning observers, receive it without mockery. Prax's El Greco painting, mentioned only in passing, serves as a kind of artistic signature: the taste for spiritual distortion that distinguishes the genuine artist from the mere poseur.

## Critical Interpretation

Prax belongs to the novel's gallery of minor figures who define the world George inhabits before — and after — his transformative encounter with Doña Rita. As the namer ("Young Ulysses"), he participates in the novel's Homeric subtext: Ulysses the wanderer, the "first historical seaman" as Mills notes, is an identity George carries from bohemian Marseilles into the Carlist Mediterranean and beyond. That this epic epithet originates with a costumed sculptor in a gilded café captures the novel's persistent tension between the grand and the ironic.
