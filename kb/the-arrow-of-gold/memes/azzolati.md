# Azzolati

A minor but memorable figure in the novel's romantic geometry—a man whose heartbroken devotion to Doña Rita is treated by the narrative with a mixture of comedy and sympathy.

## Identity

- **Full Name**: Azzolati (first name unknown)
- **Nationality / Origin**: Presumably Italian
- **Affiliation**: Doña Rita's circle of admirers
- **First Appearance**: Referenced in conversation about Rita's effect on men (005-017)

## Role in the Narrative

Azzolati exists in the novel primarily as an anecdote—a cautionary tale about the effects of Doña Rita's charm. The question "What have you done to that poor Azzolati? Did you really tell him to go..." (005-017) establishes him as someone whose romantic hopes have been decisively crushed. Later references to "the heartbroken Azzolati" (007-038) confirm his status as a figure of romantic disappointment.

Rita herself addresses the matter: "No, I assure you Azzolati had done nothing to me. I knew..." (007-039), suggesting that his heartbreak was not the result of any wrongdoing but simply the natural consequence of loving someone who cannot return the feeling. The narrator later contemplates "the inspiration to make use of Azzolati" (007-051), implying that even heartbroken admirers have their uses in the world of political intrigue.

## Relationships

| Character | Relationship | Key Interactions |
|-----------|-------------|------------------|
| Doña Rita | Unrequited admirer | His heartbreak is a recurring topic in Rita's circle (005-017, 007-038) |

## Characterization

Azzolati is characterized entirely through other characters' speech—he never appears directly. This mediated presentation transforms him from a person into a story, a cautionary exemplar of what happens to those who love Doña Rita without being loved in return.
