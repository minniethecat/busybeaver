# Mills's Visit to Headquarters

Mills's journey to the Carlist headquarters at Tolosa serves as a critical intelligence-gathering mission: sent to assess the state of the Legitimist military campaign in the Pyrenees, Mills returns with a sobering report that reveals the fundamental disorganization behind the cause to which Doña Rita has devoted her resources.

## Summary

At some point during the events of Part III, Mills travels across the French frontier to the Carlist army's headquarters at Tolosa, in the Basque region of Spain. His mission is to liaise with the Legitimist command and evaluate the military situation for Doña Rita, who is financing and organizing the arms-smuggling operation from Marseilles. Upon his return, Mills reports to Rita in the company of Monsieur George (who narrates the account). Mills delivers a candid assessment: while the rank-and-file soldiers display admirable devotion and bravery, the leadership suffers from "a deplorable levity of outlook, a fatal lack of decision, an absence of any reasoned plan" ([232-233]). His report is framed as a personal duty to Rita — "I feel that you of all people, Doña Rita, ought to be told the truth" ([232]).

## Chronology

### Lead-up

The Carlist war — the Second Carlist War (1872–1876) in the novel's historical context — provides the political framework for the entire conspiracy. Doña Rita, as the heiress of Henry Allègre, has used her inherited fortune and social connections to support the Legitimist cause ([229-230]). She has organized the gun-running operation that supplies arms to Carlist detachments in the South, with Dominic and Monsieur George executing the maritime smuggling. Mills, a well-connected Englishman and "relation of Lord X" ([018-019]), serves as an intermediary and advisor. His visit to headquarters is prompted by the need for reliable intelligence on the war's progress.

### The Event

Mills travels across the frontier into Spain to visit the Carlist army. The narrative does not dramatize the journey itself; instead, Mills's findings are reported in conversation at Doña Rita's residence. During his visit, Mills observes both the admirable qualities of the Carlist soldiers and the fatal shortcomings of their commanders. He notes that Captain Blunt, who was also serving at the front, avoided him during the visit — "He is always with his regiment at the outposts" — and that Blunt had acquired a reputation for being "foolhardy" in combat ([232-233]).

The conversation between Mills and Rita reveals the political dynamics within the Carlist leadership. Mills alludes to shifting factions — "The fair and the dark are succeeding each other like leaves blown in the wind" — and Rita dismisses the court intrigue with weary familiarity: "I understand it all, I am as old as the world" ([232-233]). Rita pointedly asks after Blunt, and Mills's report of Blunt's reckless bravery prompts Rita to observe that "he needn't seek death as a refuge. There will be nothing in his life great enough for that" ([233]).

### Aftermath

Mills's report confirms Rita's growing disillusionment with the Carlist cause. The conversation turns to her sense of isolation and the hollowness of the political world she inhabits. Mills's frankness — rare among the sycophants and intriguers who surround Rita — reinforces their intellectual bond. The report also contextualizes the larger futility of the gun-running operation: the weapons are reaching brave men, but those men are being squandered by incompetent leadership.

## Narrative Function

Mills's visit to headquarters provides the novel's most direct assessment of the Carlist war as a political and military enterprise. The scene establishes that the cause to which Rita has devoted her fortune and George has risked his life is fundamentally mismanaged — a judgment that colors all subsequent events. Mills's honesty contrasts with the flattery and self-interest that surround Rita, marking him as one of the few characters who treats her as an equal intelligence rather than a resource to be exploited. Rita's weary response — "I am as old as the world" — underscores her isolation and foreshadows her eventual withdrawal from the entire enterprise.
