# Mademoiselle Therese

Doña Rita's older sister, a Basque peasant woman whose severe piety and rustic simplicity form a stark counterpoint to Rita's worldly existence. She serves as housekeeper at Rita's Marseilles property and appears in several domestic scenes with Monsieur George.

## Identity

- **Full Name**: Therese (surname not given; referred to as "Mademoiselle Therese")
- **Nationality / Origin**: Spanish Basque, from the hills near Tolosa
- **Age**: Six to ten years older than Doña Rita
- **Affiliation**: Doña Rita's household; the Catholic Church
- **First Appearance**: Described as "Therese of the whispering lips and downcast eyes slipping out to an early mass" (005-008)

## Role in the Narrative

Therese embodies the world Rita left behind. The narrator first glimpses her as a spectral figure: "Therese of the whispering lips and downcast eyes slipping out to an early mass from the house of iniquity into the early winter murk of the city of perdition" (005-008). She dresses "in a sort of medieval costume" with "a black handkerchief enfolding her head tightly, with the two pointed ends hanging down her back" — a "nun-like" appearance that marks her apart from every other character in the novel (005-008).

Rita describes their shared childhood in the Basque hills, where Therese already showed her severity: when young Rita complained about the boy José Ortega's harassment, Therese "was quite scandalized. She called me a sinful girl, a shameless creature" (009-036). This moral rigidity persists into adulthood — Therese "decided that I was a lump of wickedness, and now she believes that I will lose my soul altogether" (009-036).

When Therese comes to Marseilles at Rita's invitation, she arrives looking "like a pilgrim to a saint's shrine" in a homemade brown dress "like a nun's habit" carrying "all her belongings tied up in a handkerchief" (009-036). Her first question: "And does this big place really belong to our Rita?" She immediately makes herself at home and stays on as caretaker — eventually managing No. 10, the house where Monsieur George lodges during the novel's later chapters.

## Interactions with Monsieur George

George lives at No. 10 under Therese's domestic regime. She wakes him each morning with pious observations, "on some pious phrase asserting the spiritual comfort of early mass, or on angry lamentations about the unconscionable rapacity of the dealers in fish and vegetables" (029-004). Her moral commentary extends to criminal cases in the news — "That's what carnal sin (*pêché de chair*) leads to," she tells him after a murder case, adding: "And then the devil furnishes the occasion" (025-012).

George observes that "Therese did somehow resemble an achievement, wonderful enough in its way, in unglazed earthenware" (009-038), and notes the incongruity between the two sisters: "Contrary to what generally happens, it was when one saw those two women together that one lost all belief in the possibility of their relationship near or far."

## Characterisation

Therese is defined by three traits:
1. **Religious severity**: A daily churchgoer who views Rita's life as sinful and prays for her soul
2. **Peasant practicality**: She "likes money" (Rita observes) and is angered by having "to actually give money to people" when shopping
3. **Domestic competence**: "I believe she really knows how to make men comfortable," Rita remarks. "Upon my word I think she likes to look after men. They don't seem to be such great sinners as women are."
