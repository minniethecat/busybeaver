# Society of the Cincinnati

The Society of the Cincinnati is a hereditary fraternal organization founded in [[1783]] by officers of the [[Continental Army]] at the close of the [[American Revolutionary War]].

The society was organized at the [[Verplanck Homestead]] in [[Fishkill, New York]] — a location "made still further famous by the fact that under its roof was organized in 1783 the Society of the Cincinnati" (009-003). Named after [[Lucius Quinctius Cincinnatus]], the Roman farmer-soldier who returned to his plow after military service, the organization embodied the ideals of republican citizenship.

## Foundation

The society was established in May 1783, before the final peace treaty with Britain. [[George Washington]] served as its first president. Membership was restricted to officers who had served in the Continental Army and their eldest male descendants.

The founding principles emphasized: commemoration of the Revolutionary struggle, mutual assistance among members and their families, and preservation of the liberties for which they had fought.

## The Verplanck Homestead

The choice of the [[Verplanck Homestead]] as the society's birthplace connected the organization to one of the oldest Dutch-American families in the [[Hudson Valley]].

The homestead, built by the [[Verplanck family]], had been a center of colonial life and Revolutionary War activity in the [[Fishkill]] region. The society's formation there linked the new nation's military leadership with its colonial heritage.

## Significance

The Society of the Cincinnati represents an early expression of American institutional identity — an organization founded by citizen-soldiers, not aristocrats, yet deliberately modeled on classical republican virtues. Its hereditary structure was controversial in the early Republic, with critics seeing it as an attempt to create an American nobility.
