# Religious Architecture

**Religious architecture** was identified in the January 11, 1890 issue of *The American Architect and Building News* as a major architectural category alongside other building types. The journal's discussion of [[Civil Architecture in Antiquity]] (Section 3) traced the historical development of religious structures.

## In the Journal

Section 3 noted that ancient builders devoted themselves to:

> The rearing of religious and sepulchral monuments, and to the construction of public buildings. (003-001)

The journal also recognized the historical importance of religious architecture, noting "new departures, not only in religious and monastic architecture" (002-012) as a driver of architectural innovation in antiquity. These departures included "libraries and museums" alongside "houses for public amusements, as theatres, amphitheatres and circuses" (002-007).

## Significance

Religious architecture served as one of the primary vehicles for architectural innovation throughout history, driving advances in structural engineering, spatial organization, and decorative arts. The journal's recognition of this category within its architectural taxonomy reflects the continuing importance of ecclesiastical buildings in late 19th-century architectural discourse.
