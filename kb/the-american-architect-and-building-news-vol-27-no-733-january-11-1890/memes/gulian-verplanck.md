Gulian Verplanck was a Dutch colonial settler and original grantee of the [[Rombout-Verplanck Patent]] in the [[Province of New York]]. In 1683, he and [[Francis Rombout]] purchased from the [[Wappinger Indians]] a tract of eighty-five thousand acres along the Hudson River.

This land grant would sustain generations of the [[Verplanck Family]] and become the site of the historic [[Verplanck Homestead]] at [[Fishkill, New York]].

## Biography

Gulian Verplanck was a merchant and landholder of Dutch descent who settled in the [[Province of New York]] during the late seventeenth century. His partnership with [[Francis Rombout]] secured one of the largest private land patents in colonial New York history.

The transaction, conducted under license from [[Governor Thomas Dongan]], was formalized in 1685 by letters patent from [[King James II]] (009-001).

Verplanck did not live to see the Homestead established. He died before 1685, leaving minor children, and settlement on his portion of the land was thereby postponed for decades (009-002). His estate was later partitioned among heirs through divisions recorded in 1708, 1722, and 1740.

## The Land Grant of 1683

The [[Rombout-Verplanck Patent]] was an immense territory bounded by the Hudson River and extending eastward into the interior of [[Dutchess County]].

> The Verplanck homestead stands on the lands granted by the Wappinger Indians, in 1683, to Gulian Verplanck and Francis Rombout, under a license given by Governor Thomas Dongan Commander-in-Chief of the Province of New York, and confirmed, in 1685, by letters patent from King James the II. (008-011)

The purchase encompassed all land on the east side of the Hudson, from the Fresh Kill (called Matteawan by the natives) northward along the river for "fouer Houres goeing" into the woods, or easterly sixteen English miles.

The consideration paid to the [[Wappinger Indians]] included money and goods as recorded in the original patent deed at Albany.

## The Verplanck Homestead

The [[Verplanck Homestead]], a low Dutch farmhouse of stone, stands on a bluff overlooking the [[Hudson River]] about a mile and a half north of [[Fishkill Landing]]. Its exact construction date is uncertain, though the date commonly assigned is a little before 1740 (009-002). The house is one story and a half, with a gambrel roof, dormer windows, and plastered stone walls.

> Some years after peace was restored, the Verplanck family appear to have occupied the Homestead from time to time. Philip Verplanck, a grandson of Gulian the original grantee, was a native of the patent, but his public life was spent elsewhere. (009-006)

The Homestead gained prominence during the [[American Revolutionary War|Revolutionary War]] when [[Baron Steuben]] chose it as his headquarters, likely for its proximity to [[George Washington]]'s headquarters across the river. In 1783, under its roof, the [[Society of the Cincinnati]] was organized in a west room overlooking the river that is preserved to this day (009-003).

## Descendants and Legacy

Gulian Verplanck's descendants shaped the civic and political life of New York for generations:

| Descendant | Relationship | Notable Contributions |
|---|---|---|
| [[Philip Verplanck]] | Grandson | Engineer, surveyor; served Colonial Assembly 1734-1768; [[Verplanck's Point]] named for him |
| [[Daniel Crommelin Verplanck]] | Great-grandson | Served in Congress and on the bench; planted the Homestead's trees |
| [[Gulian C. Verplanck]] | Great-great-grandson | Born at Homestead in 1786; became a well-known New York statesman and author |

[[Daniel Crommelin Verplanck]] brought his large family to live permanently at the Homestead after an active career in New York. He planted the notable coffee-tree and other fine trees that shade the lawn. Around 1810, a wood-frame north end was added to the house, though the original [[Dutch Colonial Architecture|Dutch colonial]] character was partly lost in the enlargement (009-007).

## Significance

Gulian Verplanck's 1683 grant exemplifies the pattern of [[Dutch Colonial Architecture|Dutch colonial]] land acquisition in the Hudson Valley -- negotiated directly with [[Native Americans in New York|Native American]] tribes and confirmed by royal patent.

The [[Verplanck Homestead]] that arose on his land represents a rare case of continuous family occupation spanning over two centuries. It preserves a physical [[Dutch Colonial Architecture|architectural]] artifact and associations with figures from [[Baron Steuben]] to [[George Washington]], and institutions from the [[Society of the Cincinnati]] to the early [[New York Legislature]].
