# Cement and Concrete

## Definition

Cement is a hydraulic binding material that hardens when mixed with water. In 1890s construction practice, cement was used both as a mortar component for masonry and as the binding agent in concrete — a composite of cement, aggregate (sand, gravel, or crushed stone), and water. The journal documents cement as a key commodity in the building materials trade.

## Historical Context

By 1890, natural and Portland cements were well-established in American construction. The journal's business survey (Section 14) identifies cement alongside "lumber, stone" and "iron and steel" as one of the primary building materials for which "manufacturers are protecting themselves against unwarranted and unexpected advances in the cost of their raw material by making purchases for future requirements" (014-002). This forward-purchasing strategy reflected cement's central role in the construction economy.

The rapid industrialization of the late 19th century drove cement demand. "Greater activity in house-building, and greater activity in the building of shops and factories" (014-002) were cited as stimulants to trade in 1890. Cement was essential to foundations, walls, and the growing use of concrete in industrial flooring.

## Technical Description

### Cement in Masonry Construction

Cement served primarily as mortar in [[masonry-construction|brick and stone construction]]. The journal documents a massive "wall of stone and cement twenty feet thick" (009-010) at the Combined Locks Paper Company mill in Wisconsin — a structure that demonstrated cement's structural role in heavy industrial construction.

### Hydraulic Performance

The Wisconsin mill incident also revealed cement's limitations. An "upheaval of rock upon which the mills are located" cracked the twenty-foot stone-and-cement wall, creating "a saddle-back several hundred feet long and six inches high in the bed rock" (009-010). The failure was attributed to "hydraulic pressure between the seams of rock beneath" (009-010), illustrating the challenges of building on sites with groundwater pressure.

### Commercial Context

The journal's trade survey reports that "users of lumber, stone, cement and building material generally" (014-002) were purchasing three to six months ahead to guard against price increases. Southern pig-iron makers were similarly "debating with themselves whether they will accept orders for pig-iron to be delivered next summer or wait a few months" (014-002), reflecting the interconnectedness of the construction materials market.

## Significance

Cement and concrete represented the industrialization of building — a shift from craft-based masonry to manufactured, standardized materials. The journal's inclusion of cement in its business survey alongside iron, steel, and lumber indicates its recognized status as a fundamental building commodity. The forward-purchasing behavior documented in 1890 shows that cement had become sufficiently standardized and essential to warrant the same supply-chain management as other industrial raw materials.

## In the Journal

The January 11, 1890 issue references cement in three contexts: the decorative terra-cotta and ceramic work of the [[exhibition-palaces|Paris Exposition palaces]] (005-004), the structural failure of a cement-and-stone wall in Wisconsin (009-010), and the commercial outlook for building materials including cement (014-002).
