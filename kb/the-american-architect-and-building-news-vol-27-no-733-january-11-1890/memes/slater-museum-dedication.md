# Dedication of Slater Memorial Museum

The dedication of the [[Slater Memorial Museum]] in [[Norwich, Connecticut]], took place in November [[1888]], marking the opening of one of the finest collections of art reproductions in the [[United States]] (007-001).

The event celebrated the liberality of industrialist [[W.A. Slater]], who funded both the building — designed by [[Sturgis & Brigham]] of [[Boston]] — and the collection of 491 plaster casts, 215 photographs, and 6 electrotypes representing European art masterpieces.

## Background

The museum was conceived as an educational institution for a small American city. Norwich, an industrial center in eastern Connecticut, lacked the major art collections of [[Boston]] or [[New York]]. The [[slater-memorial-museum|Slater Memorial Museum]] aimed to bring reproductions of the world's great sculpture to an audience that could not travel to [[Europe]].

The total collection cost was $27,112.97, excluding the purpose-built [[museum-design-and-construction|museum building]] (007-002). This economy was achieved through strategic procurement of casts from leading European workshops.

## The Dedication

The dedicatory exercises included addresses celebrating the museum's educational mission and its potential to inspire similar institutions. The event brought together Norwich citizens, educators, and art advocates.

> If to the founder or founders of such an institution it be sufficient recompense for their liberality to see their gift used, appreciated and enjoyed by people of all classes, the brief experience of the Slater Memorial Museum answers the question with a strong affirmative.
> — (007-002)

## Significance

The [[slater-memorial-museum|Slater Museum]] dedication demonstrated that a small-city institution could establish a teaching collection of national significance. Its model — affordable casts rather than original artworks — was replicable and influenced later American museum development.

By January 1890, when the *American Architect* reviewed the museum's costs and management, it had become a case study in accessible art education ([[Section-7]]).

## In the Journal
