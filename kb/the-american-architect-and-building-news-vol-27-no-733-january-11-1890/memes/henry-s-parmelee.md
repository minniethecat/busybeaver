# Henry S. Parmelee

Henry S. Parmelee of New Haven, Connecticut, was the inventor of the sealed automatic sprinkler — the first practical, commercially manufactured automatic fire sprinkler system. His invention, introduced approximately twelve years before 1890 (around 1878), was "the first, and for many years the only automatic-sprinkler manufactured and sold, and actually performing service over accidental fires" (011-022).

## Invention

Parmelee's sealed automatic sprinkler used a heat-sensitive seal that would melt or break when exposed to fire temperatures, releasing pressurized water through a deflector onto the fire below. While numerous patents for sprinkler devices had been granted since the early nineteenth century, Parmelee's was the first to achieve practical and commercial success (011-022). Nearly 200,000 [[automatic-sprinklers|Parmelee sprinklers]] were installed before his design was supplanted by newer forms (011-023).

## Legacy

By 1890, approximately two million [[parmelee-sprinkler|automatic sprinklers]] were in service across American industrial buildings, all tracing their lineage to Parmelee's original sealed design. The journal credited him with "the distinction of being the father of the art of extinguishing fires by this means" (011-022). His invention, combined with slow-burning [[slow-burning-mill-construction|mill construction]] methods, fundamentally changed the economics of industrial fire protection — reducing average fire losses to one-nineteenth of previous levels.
