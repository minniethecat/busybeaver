# Garden Architecture

## Definition

Garden architecture — the design of cultivated outdoor spaces in relationship to buildings — appears in the 1890 journal across multiple historical and contemporary contexts. It encompasses the walled gardens of ancient Egyptian private dwellings, the ornamental gardens framing Exposition pavilions, and the tranquil garden settings of venerable European cathedral towns.

## Historical Context

### Ancient Egyptian Precedent

The journal's treatment of private dwellings in ancient Egypt provides the earliest historical reference to garden architecture:

> The dwellings of the wealthy lords were usually erected in the centre of a garden, or of a cultivated court, and were surrounded by a high wall. [004-007]

This arrangement — a dwelling centered within a walled garden — represents an early integration of architecture and landscape, where the garden functioned as private sanctuary within a defensive enclosure. The Egyptian model established a pattern of domestic architecture embedded within cultivated nature that persisted across civilizations.

### St. Machar's Cathedral and Its Ancient Gardens

The journal's description of Old Aberdeen provides a picturesque vision of houses standing "in ancient gardens" around the venerable cathedral of St. Machar:

> Its houses are venerable, standing generally in ancient gardens; and save that the beauty and tranquillity of the spot have led to the erection of a few pleasant modern villas, dotting it here and there, whoever treads the one echoing street of the Alton for the first time, feels that two centuries must have brought very little external change to the objects by which he is surrounded. [009-033]

This passage captures the 1890 aesthetic appreciation for gardens as settings that preserved historical character — ancient gardens lending tranquillity and timelessness to architectural ensembles.

## Exposition Gardens

### The Champ de Mars Upper Garden

At the 1889 Paris Exposition, the upper garden of the [[champ-de-mars|Champ de Mars]] formed a key organizing element between major architectural features:

> The City of Paris desired to have in the Champ de Mars a serious and useful exhibition, so it began by paying no sort of attention to the decorative and architectural side of its two pavilions, placed in the centre of the upper garden between the monumental fountain and the central dome. [004-014]

The upper garden served as connective tissue between the monumental fountain, the central dome, and the two City of Paris pavilions — a spatial sequence that structured the visitor's experience.

### Porticos and Garden Restaurants

The Palace of the Liberal Arts incorporated garden-level restaurants beneath its porticos:

> On the garden under the porticos are restaurants. [005-002]

This integration of dining facilities with garden spaces represents a functional dimension of garden architecture — the garden as an amenity within the exhibition complex.

## The De Soto Hotel Garden Context

The journal's description of the [[hotel-de-soto|Hotel de Soto]] in Savannah, Georgia, notes that the climate favored "freely open" building designs oriented to capture breezes — an approach that often placed hotels within garden settings to maximize [[building-ventilation|ventilation]] and amenity:

> The climate renders it desirable to have it freely open and exposed to the cool southeast winds which blow refreshingly up from the bay. [010-005]

## Significance

Garden architecture in the 1890 journal reflects a continuity of practice from antiquity to the modern era. The ancient Egyptian model of the walled garden dwelling established a pattern that resonated through architectural history. The Exposition gardens of 1889 demonstrated how landscape could organize large-scale architectural ensembles, while the "ancient gardens" of Old Aberdeen illustrated the role of cultivated grounds in preserving the historical character of architectural settings. Across all three contexts, the journal presents gardens not as mere decoration but as integral components of architectural composition.
