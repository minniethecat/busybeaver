# Magnesia Covering

A heat-insulation material composed of carbonate of magnesia, applied as sectional coverings to steam pipes, boilers, and other heated surfaces to dramatically reduce radiant [[heat-radiation|heat loss]] and improve fuel efficiency in industrial plants and naval vessels.

## Definition

Magnesia covering (also referred to as "carb. magnesia" or "magnesia sectional-coverings") is a non-organic thermal insulation material made from carbonate of magnesia. In the late 19th century, it emerged as a superior alternative to organic pipe coverings such as hair-felt, offering both better thermal performance and greatly reduced fire hazard (005-014). The material was manufactured in pre-formed sectional pieces that could be fitted around pipes of various diameters, then secured in place (006-002).

## Historical Context

The development and adoption of [[heat-radiation|magnesia covering]] occurred during a period of rapid industrialization in the United States, when steam power was the dominant energy source for factories, electric-light plants, and naval vessels. Before magnesia, organic materials like hair-felt were commonly used to insulate steam pipes, but these posed a serious fire risk — the organic material could slowly carbonize when in prolonged contact with hot pipes and eventually ignite (005-016). The recognition of this hazard, combined with the need for greater fuel efficiency, drove the search for superior non-combustible insulation materials (005-015).

By 1890, [[heat-radiation|magnesia covering]] had accumulated a substantial record of successful installations. Professor Cooley of the Michigan Engineering Society reported on its performance at the Thomson-Houston electric-light plant in Ann Arbor, Michigan, where it reduced moisture entrainment in steam from 31% to under 4% (006-002). The [[herreshoff-manufacturing-company|Herreshoff Manufacturing Company]] adopted magnesia as a lining for the shells of its coil boilers, where it withstood the intense heat of forced-draught firing (006-004).

## Technical Description

### Principles

Magnesia covering functions by exploiting the low thermal conductivity of carbonate of magnesia. Comparative calorimeter measurements showed that while 25 mm of Wm. Berkefield's fossil meal transmitted 7.7 heat-units, the same thickness of magnesia transmitted only 6.7 heat-units — a 13% improvement (006-001). By reducing the rate at which heat escapes from steam-containing vessels and pipes, the covering ensures that more thermal energy reaches its intended destination rather than being lost to the surrounding air.

### Application and Performance

The covering was typically manufactured as sectional pieces sized to fit standard pipe diameters. Installation involved fitting these sections around pipes and steam drums, then securing them. At the Thomson-Houston plant, covering approximately 60 feet of seven-inch pipe and two large steam-drums reduced entrained water from 31.01% to 3.61%, saving an estimated 125 pounds of coal per hour — roughly half a ton per day, or $450 per year at $3 per ton of coal (006-002). The cost of the covering installation was approximately $150, yielding a payback period of about four months (006-003).

### Fire Resistance

A dramatic demonstration of magnesia's [[fire-protection|fire-resistance]] occurred during a conflagration in Lynn, Massachusetts. A two-inch steam pipe in the machine shop of Messrs. Rollins & Glozier was covered with magnesia. The fire reached temperatures between 2,756°F (the melting point of cast iron) and 2,950°F (the melting point of wrought iron). The pipe fell 20 feet during the fire and some covering was broken by the impact, but the pipe itself was found to be uninjured and was subsequently reused in the repla[[cement-and-concrete|cement]] building (006-003).

### Naval Application

The U.S. Navy adopted [[heat-radiation|magnesia covering]] for its new warships. During the trial trip of the cruiser USS *Baltimore* in September 1890, all boiler and steam surfaces were covered with magnesia. The fire and engine rooms remained so comfortable that the first watch of firemen refused to be relieved after their two-hour shift and insisted on running the ship for the entire four-hour trial (006-005).

## Significance

Magnesia covering represented a significant advance in both industrial efficiency and safety. Economically, it offered a return on investment measured in months rather than years — the [[heat-radiation|magnesia covering]] on a two-inch pipe cost 27 cents and saved 91 cents in coal per year, paying back in under four months (006-001). From a safety perspective, it eliminated the fire hazard inherent in organic coverings, protecting both property and the workers who operated steam plants (005-015). The journal argued that architects, mechanical engineers, and other professional advisers had a moral obligation to recommend proven non-combustible insulation materials rather than perpetuating the use of hazardous organic alternatives (006-006).
