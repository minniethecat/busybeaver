# Civil Architecture

Civil architecture is the branch of architecture devoted to buildings serving [[civic]], [[residential]], and [[public]] functions — distinct from [[religious architecture|religious]] temples and [[sepulchral architecture|sepulchral]] monuments.

Originating with the [[ancient Greeks|Greeks]] and extended by the [[ancient Romans|Romans]], it represents the architectural expression of secular society and urban life (003-001). Its emergence marks a pivotal transition: from building exclusively for gods and kings to building for citizens.

## Definition

Civil architecture encompasses all non-religious, non-military building types intended for the benefit of the living population: dwellings, municipal buildings, markets, baths, theaters, and hospitals.

The term distinguishes these works from [[religious architecture]] (temples, churches) and [[sepulchral architecture]] (tombs, pyramids), which dominated the architectural output of most ancient civilizations before [[ancient Greece|Greece]] and [[ancient Rome|Rome]].

| Domain | Building Types |
|--------|---------------|
| Civic | forums, basilicas, municipal halls, public squares |
| Residential | houses, apartments (insulae), palaces |
| Infrastructure | aqueducts, bridges, dikes, streets |
| Social | markets, baths (thermae), theaters, stadia, gymnasia |
| Welfare | hospitals, asylums, charitable retreats |
| Commemorative | honorary monuments, triumphal columns, statues |

## Historical Development

### Before Greece

In the earliest traditions, man's first architectural efforts were directed toward religious monuments.

> As soon as man had contrived a shelter for himself, he turned his thoughts toward the divine being of his worship, and the first steps in art were taken in the monuments which he raised to his gods.
> — (002-010)

Ancient civilizations — [[ancient India|India]], [[ancient China|China]], [[ancient Egypt|Egypt]], [[Mesopotamia]], and [[ancient Persia|Persia]] — produced magnificent religious and palatial structures.

These served the divine or the sovereign, not the civic population. Civil architecture "came into existence only with an already advanced state of civilization, when cities were forming and peoples were organizing" (002-010).

### Greek and Roman Innovation

Civil architecture originated with the [[ancient Greeks|Greeks]], who developed building types serving the polis. After temples and palaces, the people grouped into cities and surrounded themselves with fortifications. Then practical needs emerged:

> aqueducts were constructed to supply water; bridges established communication between the opposite banks of streams; dikes confined the rivers; streets were laid out along which houses were built in orderly fashion, public squares were marked off where the products of industry could be exchanged, where justice was dispensed.
> — (002-010)

Mental and physical demands followed, giving rise to theatres, stadia, gymnasia, and thermae.

The [[ancient Romans|Romans]] extended civil architecture remarkably, creating [[forum|forums]], [[basilica|basilicas]], [[Roman baths|baths]], [[aqueduct|aqueducts]], and apartment blocks (insulae) that served all classes of urban society (003-001).

### Christianity's Influence

> With the advent of Christ came new ideas which caused new departures, not only in religious and monastic architecture, but in civil architecture, as well. Christianity, in proclaiming a new virtue, love, created retreats for the unfortunate, asylums for their reception and hospitals for their care.
> — (002-012)

These new types — [[hospital|hospitals]], [[asylum|asylums]], and charitable retreats — introduced an institutional architecture of care that became a permanent part of the Western building tradition.

## Ancient Civilizations

### India

The sacred books of the [[Hindus]] preserve plans for forty different kinds of cities, with streets crossing at right angles and the city center reserved for sacred uses (003-002). This tradition predates comparable Greek and Roman grid systems.

### China

[[Chinese architecture|Chinese civilization]] rivals India in antiquity. Documentary evidence establishes that the Chinese, centuries before the Christian era, built from designs still in use in the nineteenth century — a continuity spanning over two millennia (003-005).

### Persia

At [[Persepolis]], the [[Medes]] and [[Persians]] attained high civilization under [[Cyrus the Great|Cyrus]]. However, "their art is derived from the Babylonians and Assyrians, from whom they must have largely borrowed their customs" (003-017). No authentic records of their civil architecture survive.

### Egypt

In [[ancient Egypt|Egypt]], archaeological attention was long focused on temples and tombs at the expense of domestic dwellings. By 1890, study had begun to reveal the everyday built environment of ordinary Egyptians (004-001). See [[Section-4]] for a detailed treatment.

## Significance

The development of civil architecture marks a crucial threshold — the point where architecture began serving the broader population rather than exclusively gods and rulers.

> In this rapid review of the civil architecture of the ancient peoples of Asia and Egypt, we have discovered no traces of structures whose destination indicated any care for the development of the lower classes of society, no remains which implied their participation in any municipal life whatever, no edifice erected for the purpose of national education.
> — (004-011)

This absence underscored the uniquely democratic character of Greek and Roman civil architecture. The commemorative dimension also emerged: "public services rendered by certain illustrious men called for some enduring memorial," giving rise to honorary monuments, triumphal columns, and statues populating the civic landscape (002-010).
