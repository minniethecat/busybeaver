# Brick Construction

## Definition

Brick construction refers to the use of fired or unfired clay bricks as a primary building material. In 1890 architectural practice, brick was one of the most common construction materials, valued for its fire resistance, durability, and economy compared to stone.

## Historical Context

Brick has one of the longest continuous histories of any building material. The 1890 journal traces its use across civilizations, from ancient Egypt and China to contemporary American practice. The evolution from sun-dried unburned brick to kiln-fired brick represented a major technological advance in building durability.

In antiquity, as the journal documents, Egyptian "houses were built of unburned brick, made from a heavy clay, mixed with straw" (004-005). The Chinese "generally used brick and wood" as their primary building materials (003-016). The Romans extended brick construction across their empire, though many cultures "were not known to have built of brick before the Roman period" (003-025).

## Technical Description

Brick construction in the 1890s encompassed several varieties and techniques. Common brick was the standard building brick, typically red or brown. Face brick, selected for uniform color and texture, was used for visible exterior surfaces. Enameled brick, described in the journal as "a kind of brick covered with enamel on one side" (005-003), offered decorative possibilities.

The journal describes the [[slater-memorial-museum|Slater Memorial Museum]]'s construction as using "local brick for the lower portions" (010-006) of the building. At [[old-trinity-church|Old Trinity Church]], "the inside walls are finished in brick and stone" (010-008), demonstrating the common practice of combining brick structure with stone finish. Wellington red brick was specified "for the facings above plinth" (010-018) in another building described.

Brick was also central to industrial architecture. The journal notes "potteries and brick works, over forty glass furnaces" (013-023) in the Pittsburgh region, indicating the scale of brick manufacturing.

## Significance

Brick occupied a middle ground in the building material hierarchy: more durable and fire-resistant than timber, more economical than stone. Its standardization and widespread availability made it the default material for urban construction in the late 19th century. The journal's attention to brick across historical and contemporary contexts reflects its fundamental role in the built environment.

## In the Journal

The January 11, 1890 issue references brick construction in discussions of ancient Egyptian and Chinese architecture (Sections 3-4), contemporary museum and church buildings (Sections 5, 10), and industrial manufacturing (Section 13).
