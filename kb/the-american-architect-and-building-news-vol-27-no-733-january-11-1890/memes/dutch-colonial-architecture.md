# Dutch Colonial Architecture

**Dutch Colonial architecture** in New York was referenced in the January 11, 1890 issue of *The American Architect and Building News* through its discussion of the Verplanck family homestead and the architectural traditions of Dutch settlers along the Hudson River.

## Historical Context

The journal's Section 9, "Early Land Grants and the Verplanck Family," described the Verplanck Homestead:

> The Homestead, the present low Dutch farm-house was built. (009-002)

This structure represented the Dutch colonial building tradition — characterized by low profiles, gambrel roofs, and stone or brick construction — that shaped the architectural landscape of the Hudson Valley from the 17th through early 19th centuries.

## Architectural Characteristics

The Dutch colonial farmhouse was adapted to the New York climate and available materials. The journal noted architectural evolution over time:

> Apparently, in those days, so the Dutch type was lost in making this addition. (009-007)

This observation suggests that later additions and modifications sometimes departed from the original Dutch colonial character, reflecting changing architectural tastes and building practices.
