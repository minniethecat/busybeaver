# Chimney Design

The architectural design of chimney-pieces — the fireplace surrounds and mantels that formed focal points of late 19th-century domestic interiors, executed as custom woodwork to complement the interior finish of principal rooms.

## Definition

In 1890s architectural practice, "chimney-pieces" referred to the decorative surrounds of fireplaces — comprising the mantel shelf, overmantel, and flanking panels — which were designed as integral elements of a room's interior architecture. These were typically custom-designed to match the room's woodwork, employing the same timber species and decorative vocabulary as the wall paneling, cornices, and other joinery elements. [010-018]

## Materials and Execution

Chimney-pieces in the American domestic interior of 1890 were predominantly executed in native hardwoods. American walnut and pitch-pine were favored for their rich color, figure, and workability, and were used consistently across all interior woodwork in a given room to achieve a unified decorative scheme. The chimney-piece, as the visual focus of the room, received the highest level of craft attention within the joinery program. [010-018]

## In the Journal

> The principal chimney-pieces in the house have been made to special design, and are chiefly executed in American walnut and pitch-pine. The dining-room is panelled the full height up to a richly-modelled frieze. [010-018]
