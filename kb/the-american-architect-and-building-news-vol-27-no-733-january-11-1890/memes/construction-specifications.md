# Construction Specifications

In late-nineteenth-century American architectural practice, construction specifications (or "plans and specifications") were the written technical documents that accompanied architectural drawings, defining the materials, workmanship standards, dimensions, and contractual obligations governing a building project. Together with the general drawings, specifications formed the complete design package against which contractors bid and by which their work was judged.

## Definition

Construction specifications are the textual complement to architectural drawings. While plans show the geometric arrangement and dimensions of a building, specifications describe in words the quality of materials, methods of construction, standards of workmanship, and contractual responsibilities. In the 1890 journal, the phrase "plans and specifications" appears repeatedly as a compound term, reflecting the inseparable nature of the two documents in professional practice (012-013) (013-001).

## Professional Practice

### Competitive Bidding

Specifications served as the basis for [[contract-system|competitive bidding]]. Contractors would submit bids — "sub-bids" for portions of the work — based on the plans and specifications prepared by the architect. The specifications defined what each contractor was obligated to deliver, enabling fair comparison of bids and preventing disputes over scope (012-013). However, the system was vulnerable to abuse: contractors could use an architect's specifications to solicit bids from subcontractors without the architect's consent or compensation (013-001).

### Commission and Ownership

A significant professional issue concerned the ownership and reuse of specifications. The journal published a letter from architect F.C. Sauer, who had prepared plans and specifications for one client, only to discover that two other builders had used his specifications to construct a separate building without his permission or payment (013-001) (013-003). The editors affirmed that the architect was entitled to a full commission of five percent on the cost of the unauthorized building and could legally collect it (013-007). This case illustrated the principle that specifications, like drawings, were the intellectual property of the architect who prepared them.

### Supervision and Inspection

Specifications also defined the architect's supervisory role. Standard practice called for the architect to "look after and approve the plans and specifications and to give continual inspection of the erection" (012-031). This inspection responsibility ensured that the contractor's work conformed to the specifications and was executed "in a good, workmanlike and substantial manner, equal to the best class of work" (012-019).

## Fee Structure

The journal described a tiered fee structure based on the architect's scope of services. Full services — preparing general drawings and specifications, soliciting bids, and providing continual inspection — commanded the full five percent commission. When the architect provided only general drawings without specifications, a reduced fee of 1.5 percent applied. If specifications were prepared but the architect was not engaged for inspection, 3.5 percent was due (012-025). This graduated fee schedule reflected the relative value and liability associated with each component of professional service.

## Significance

Construction specifications represented the professionalization of American architectural practice in the late nineteenth century. By formalizing the technical requirements in written documents, architects established themselves as indispensable intermediaries between owners and builders, with legally enforceable rights to compensation. The specification system also served public safety by ensuring that building work met defined standards — a concern reflected in the journal's detailed coverage of fire protection, structural integrity, and proper [[building-ventilation|ventilation]].
