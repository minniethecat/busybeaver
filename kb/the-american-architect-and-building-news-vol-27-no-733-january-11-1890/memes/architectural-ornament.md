# Architectural Ornament

## Definition

Architectural ornament refers to decorative elements applied to or integrated with building structures. In 1890 architectural discourse, ornament was a central concern: its appropriate use, its relationship to structural expression, and its role in defining architectural character were actively debated.

## Historical Context

Ornament has been an integral part of architecture across all civilizations. The 1890 journal traces ornamental traditions from antiquity, noting that in Egyptian architecture "some of the gates were ornamented, others were plain" (003-022), indicating that the selective use of ornament—as opposed to universal decoration—was an ancient practice.

The late 19th century saw increasing tension between historical ornamental traditions and modernist impulses toward simplification. The journal captures this tension in its discussion of contemporary buildings, where ornament was both celebrated and questioned.

## Technical Description

Ornament in 1890 architecture took many forms: carved stone, molded brick, terra cotta decoration, ironwork, and painted surfaces. The journal describes an important innovation in brick construction where "a kind of brick covered with enamel on one side" (005-003) created permanent ornamental surfaces. This principle applied to "the brick itself as well as of its ornamentation" (005-003), integrating decoration with the structural material rather than applying it as a separate layer.

The design philosophy expressed in one church description reflects the era's prevailing attitude: "the use of elaborate materials as a vain one, ornament goes no farther" (010-002) than what the structure and materials naturally express. This suggests a disciplined approach where ornament was subordinate to material honesty and structural clarity.

## Significance

Architectural ornament in 1890 occupied the contested ground between tradition and modernity. The journal's treatment of ornament—both in its historical surveys and contemporary building descriptions—reflects an architectural profession grappling with industrial production, new materials, and changing tastes. The integration of ornament with construction, as in enameled brick, pointed toward modernist principles while remaining rooted in decorative tradition.
