# H.H. Richardson

Henry Hobson Richardson (1838–1886) was one of the most influential American architects of the 19th century, known for developing a distinctive Romanesque Revival style often called "Richardsonian Romanesque." In the journal, his career serves as a case study in how publication and publicity can elevate an architect's professional reputation (001-010).

## Biography

### Early Life

Richardson was born in 1838 in Louisiana. He studied at Harvard University and later at the École des Beaux-Arts in Paris, making him one of the few formally trained American architects of his era.

### Career

Richardson established his practice in New York before moving to Brookline, Massachusetts. His distinctive Romanesque Revival style, characterized by heavy stonework, rounded arches, and integrated sculptural elements, influenced a generation of American architects.

### Later Years / Legacy

Richardson died in 1886 at the age of 47, at the height of his career. Despite his untimely death, his influence on American architecture was profound and lasting. The journal's editors noted that his posthumous reputation was directly tied to the publication of his designs (001-010).

## In the Journal

Richardson is cited multiple times in Section 2 of the journal as the editors argue that architects benefit from having their work published. The article references his career trajectory and the initial resistance he faced:

> "The late Mr. Richardson, even to a comparatively late period in his professional career, was afflicted with a most unfortunate unwillingness to allow his designs to be published, and the consequence was that they were not published, for no one could get access to them unless he stole them, and nobody wanted to steal them at that time." (001-010)

The editors note that after Richardson permitted his work to be published, his reputation grew rapidly:

> "Richardson would never have attained a fraction of his reputation if he had not allowed his designs to be published." (001-010)

The journal uses Richardson as the ultimate benchmark of architectural fame, noting that "although we do not say that all the architects who send us their drawings will attain the fame of a Richardson, we do say that Richardson would never have attained a fraction of his reputation if he had not allowed his designs to be published" (001-010).

## Key Works / Contributions

- Trinity Church, Boston (1872–1877) — his most celebrated work
- Marshall Field Wholesale Store, Chicago (1885–1887)
- Allegheny County Courthouse, Pittsburgh (1884–1888)
- Sever Hall, Harvard University (1878–1880)
- Numerous public libraries, railroad stations, and residences across the northeastern United States
