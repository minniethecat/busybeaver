# Iron Construction

Iron construction refers to the use of cast iron and wrought iron structural elements — columns, beams, frames, and connections — in American buildings of the late nineteenth century. The journal discussed iron construction particularly in the context of fire protection, noting that "many details of American construction, such as the encased iron frame, are direct contributions to safety in case of fire" (001-010).

## Definition

Iron construction encompasses two primary forms: cast iron, used for columns and decorative elements, and wrought iron, used for beams and tension members where its greater ductility was advantageous. The "encased iron frame" — iron structural members surrounded by masonry or terra cotta fireproofing — was recognized as an American innovation that combined the strength of iron with the fire resistance of masonry (001-010).

## Fire Performance

Despite being non-combustible, iron had a critical vulnerability: at high temperatures it lost structural strength rapidly. The journal documented this through the Lynn, Massachusetts conflagration, where cast iron machine beds melted (at 2,756°F) while a steam pipe protected by [[heat-radiation|magnesia covering]] survived and was reused (006-003). Buildings "whose iron and masonry construction is called fireproof" still suffered severe losses because the iron frame, once heated, could collapse even if it did not burn (010-035). This led to the practice of encasing iron members in fire-resistant materials.

## Significance

Iron construction represented the transition from traditional masonry-and-timber building to modern structural engineering. By 1890, the encased iron frame had become a standard feature of American mill and commercial construction, contributing to the development of the [[slow-burning-mill-construction|slow-burning construction]] system that combined iron, heavy timber, and [[parmelee-sprinkler|automatic sprinklers]] into a comprehensive fire-safety strategy.
