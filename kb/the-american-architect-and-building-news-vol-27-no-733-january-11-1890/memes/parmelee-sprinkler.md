# Parmelee Automatic Sprinkler

The **[[henry-s-parmelee|Parmelee]] automatic sprinkler** was the dominant automatic fire-suppression device in late 19th-century America, invented by [[Henry S. Parmelee]] of New Haven, Connecticut. By 1890, approximately 200,000 [[automatic-sprinklers|Parmelee sprinklers]] had been installed, and their effectiveness was a major topic in *The American Architect and Building News* coverage of [[Slow-Burning Mill Construction]].

## Historical Context

The development of automatic sprinklers represented a revolution in [[Fire Protection]] for industrial buildings. Prior to their introduction, mill and factory fires relied on manual firefighting, which often arrived too late. The January 11, 1890 issue devoted extensive coverage to sprinkler systems in its discussion of slow-burning [[slow-burning-mill-construction|mill construction]] (Section 11).

> Automatic-sprinklers have proved to be a most valuable form of fire protection. (011-021)

The article noted that in fires where automatic sprinklers "formed a part of the apparatus for extinguishing the fire, then the total damage from the number of fires" was dramatically reduced compared to unprotected buildings (011-022).

## Technical Description

### The Parmelee Invention

Patents for automatic sprinklers had been issued "since the early part of the present century," but the decisive breakthrough came with "the invention of the [[automatic-sprinklers|sealed automatic-sprinkler]] by [[henry-s-parmelee|Henry S. Parmelee]] of New Haven" (011-025). This design was:

> For many years the only automatic-sprinkler manufactured and sold. (011-025)

The [[henry-s-parmelee|Parmelee]] sprinkler used a fusible link that melted at a predetermined temperature, releasing water through a perforated head. This simple but reliable mechanism made it the industry standard.

### Scale of Deployment

By January 1890, the scale of [[henry-s-parmelee|Parmelee]] sprinkler deployment was remarkable:

> Although nearly or quite 200,000 Parmelee automatic-sprinklers have been installed, their manufacture and sale still continue to be a very large business. (011-026)

### Reliability and Industry Acceptance

When first introduced, there was considerable skepticism:

> When automatic-sprinklers were first introduced there were many who doubted their efficacy. (011-027)

However, actual performance proved decisive. The journal noted that damage in sprinkler-equipped fires was "nominal in the case of well-constructed sprinklers" (011-028). An "association of underwriters" compiled statistics demonstrating their effectiveness, and insurance companies began offering reduced premiums for sprinkler-equipped buildings.

## Significance

The Parmelee sprinkler system was transformative for industrial architecture, enabling the [[Slow-Burning Mill Construction]] approach that replaced heavy timber with lighter construction — because the sprinkler system, not the structural mass, provided the [[fire-protection|fire safety]]. This fundamentally changed mill design economics and influenced factory architecture nationwide.

## In the Journal

The sprinkler discussion appears in **Section 11** (011-021 through 011-029), forming a key part of the slow-burning [[slow-burning-mill-construction|mill construction]] analysis. The journal traces the patent history, deployment scale, insurance industry response, and continuing challenges — including the problem of "the time necessarily involved in notifying the fire department of the existence of a fire" even with sprinklers active (011-030).
