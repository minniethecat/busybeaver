# Fire Hazard

Fire hazard, in the context of late-nineteenth-century architectural and engineering practice, refers to the risk of fire ignition, spread, and structural damage arising from building materials, construction methods, and [[mechanical-engineering|mechanical systems]] — particularly the use of combustible organic materials on heated surfaces such as steam pipes.

## Definition

Fire hazard encompasses both the probability of fire occurrence and the potential severity of resulting damage. The journal analyzed fire hazard through two lenses: the inherent combustibility of organic building materials (such as hair-felt pipe covering and timber construction) and the economic consequences measured through insurance losses. "The fire-loss is a most oppressive tax" (010-024), the journal observed, emphasizing that fire hazard was not merely a technical problem but an economic one.

## Sources of Fire Hazard

### Organic Coverings on Steam Pipes

A significant and under-appreciated fire hazard came from organic materials used to insulate steam pipes. Hair-felt and similar organic coverings, when in prolonged contact with hot pipes, would slowly carbonize and could eventually ignite spontaneously. The journal asked pointedly: "How many fires (cause unknown?) have really originated from the slow carbonizing of organic material on steam-pipes?" (005-016). The man who recommended such materials was described as having "no conception of the fire-hazard which his advice was to thrust upon the innocent inquirer" (005-015).

### Structural Combustibility

The liability to injury by fire was described as "a hazard inherent to all building construction" (010-022). Even buildings described as "fireproof" because of their iron and masonry construction remained vulnerable — iron, though non-combustible, lost structural strength at high temperatures, and interior finishes, furnishings, and stored goods provided ample fuel (010-035).

## Insurance and Economic Impact

Fire hazard was directly linked to insurance costs. Buildings with recognized fire hazards faced higher premiums, while those adopting fire-protection measures such as [[parmelee-sprinkler|automatic sprinklers]] could expect reduced rates — though ironically, when sprinklers were first introduced in England, apprehension about water damage actually caused insurance rates to increase (011-024). The underwriters' role in quantifying and pricing fire hazard helped drive adoption of safer construction practices.

## Mitigation

The journal advocated two complementary approaches to reducing fire hazard: eliminating combustible materials (replacing organic coverings with non-combustible magnesia) and installing automatic fire-suppression systems. "The subject of the fire-hazard of organic coverings has been pretty thoroughly investigated and can be pretty well-known, when there is any inclination to get out of ruts" (005-016).
