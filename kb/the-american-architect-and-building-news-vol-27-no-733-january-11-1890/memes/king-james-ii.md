# King James II

King of England, Scotland, and Ireland from 1685 to 1688. James II appears in the journal in connection with the colonial land grants that established the [[Verplanck Homestead]] in [[Fishkill, New York]] (008-011).

## Biography

### Early Life

James was born in 1633, the second surviving son of King Charles I of England. He was created Duke of York in 1644 and spent part of the English Civil War in exile.

### Career

As Duke of York, James oversaw the administration of England's American colonies. The Province of New York was named in his honor after its capture from the Dutch in 1664. He ascended to the throne in 1685 following the death of his brother, Charles II.

His reign was marked by religious conflict — as a Catholic monarch ruling a predominantly Protestant nation, his policies of religious toleration generated significant opposition.

### Later Years / Legacy

James was overthrown in the Glorious Revolution of 1688 and fled to France. He was succeeded by his Protestant daughter Mary II and her husband William III. James died in exile in 1701 at the Château de Saint-Germain-en-Laye.

## In the Journal

James II is mentioned in Section 8 of the journal in the context of the legal foundation of the [[Verplanck family]]'s land holdings in the Hudson Valley:

> "The Verplanck homestead stands on the lands granted by the Wappinger Indians, in 1683, to Gulian Verplanck and Francis Rombout, under a license given by Governor Thomas Dongan Commander-in-Chief of the Province of New York, and confirmed, in 1685, by letters patent from King-James the II." (008-011)

The 1685 letters patent confirmed the Rombout-Verplanck land grant, comprising approximately 85,000 acres in what is now Dutchess County, New York. This royal confirmation placed the authority of the English Crown behind the Indian deed and gubernatorial license, securing the legal title that would pass through generations of the [[Verplanck family]].

## Key Facts

- Reigned as King of England, Scotland, and Ireland: 1685–1688
- Overthrown in the Glorious Revolution of 1688 and succeeded by William III and Mary II
- As Duke of York (before 1685), the Province of New York was named in his honor
- Issued the letters patent confirming the Rombout-Verplanck land grant in 1685
