# Contract System in Construction

The **contract system** in late 19th-century American construction was the subject of extensive discussion in the January 11, 1890 issue of *The American Architect and Building News*, which devoted Section 13 to "[[competitive-bidding|Competitive Bidding]] and Construction Ethics" and referenced contractual matters across multiple sections.

## Historical Context

By 1890, the construction industry had developed formal bidding and contracting procedures. The journal examined these from multiple angles: the architect's role in preparing "plans and specifications for contractors to estimate on" (012-013), the allocation of risk between owner and contractor, and the ethical dimensions of competitive bidding.

## Contractual Framework

### Contractor Responsibilities

The journal outlined the standard contractual framework:

> The contractors are Mr. T. Lewman & Co. (010-006)

This naming of specific contractors in project descriptions reflected the industry practice of public accountability. The journal noted that when there was "deficiency or carelessness of the contractors, the cost of same is to be" borne by the contractor (012-015), establishing the principle of contractor liability for workmanship.

More seriously, "negligence or violations of contracts by the contractors" could result in additional claims (012-016), and the journal warned that certain practices constituted "a violation of the contract" (012-017).

### Insurance and Risk

The insurance dimension of contracts appeared in Section 2, which discussed "the contract of insurance" (002-003) in relation to fire protection requirements. The journal noted that insurance policies might not cover certain building types or construction methods, creating contractual obligations for builders to meet underwriting standards.

### Cost Estimation and Drawings

The journal addressed the professional obligation regarding architectural drawings:

> Damage will be made for drawings contracted for and not furnished. (012-018)

This established the deliverable-based nature of architectural services contracts.

## Ethical Concerns

Section 13 specifically addressed competitive bidding ethics. The journal was critical of practices where contractors submitted unrealistically low bids to win work, then recovered through change orders and claims. The system of competitive bidding, while intended to ensure fair pricing, created incentives for corner-cutting that the journal's editors viewed as detrimental to construction quality.

The issue also touched on the practice of importing "qualified plumbers under contract from London to do work on public buildings" (002-005), indicating the international dimension of construction labor contracting in an era before widespread unionization of American building trades.

## Significance

The January 1890 treatment of construction contracts reflects the professionalization of the building industry. The journal positioned itself as a forum for discussing the ethical and practical dimensions of contractual relationships — a role that would grow as construction law developed into a distinct legal specialty over the following decades.

## In the Journal

Contract-related content appears across multiple sections:
- **Section 2** (002-001, 002-003, 002-005): Insurance contracts and labor contracting
- **Section 10** (010-006): Named contractors in building projects
- **Section 12** (012-013—012-018): Mill construction contracting and specifications
- **Section 13** (013-001+): Competitive bidding ethics
