# Champ de Mars

The Champ de Mars, in [[Paris]], was the primary site of the [[1889 Exposition Universelle]], the world's fair that gave France the [[Eiffel Tower]].

The grounds hosted the [[exhibition-palaces|Palaces of Fine Arts and Liberal Arts]] — two enormous exhibition halls constructed of [[iron]], [[architectural terra-cotta|terra-cotta]], and ceramic work — along with the Industrial section and foreign national pavilions (005-002).

## The Exhibition Palaces

The two main palaces were of equal dimensions and similar aspect, each covering 21,000 square metres. A large central nave, 209 metres long by 53.5 metres wide, formed the core of each building, surrounded by galleries on two levels (005-002).

Each palace connected to the Industrial section via a vestibule 30 metres wide by 115 metres long. The Palace of Fine Arts contained the sculpture exhibition; the Palace of Liberal Arts housed musical instruments and the retrospective exhibition of labor (005-002).

## Terra-Cotta and Ceramic Innovation

The palaces were striking demonstrations of [[architectural-terra-cotta|architectural ceramics]]. Each dome — 55 metres high and 32 metres wide — was covered with over 50,000 enamelled tiles in more than 600 varieties, forming a mosaic rather than a conventional tile covering (005-003).

> These two palaces are striking examples of the richness which can be introduced in a moment by the artistic employment of terra-cotta and ceramic work.
> — (005-002)

At the base of each dome, 24 pottery vases three metres high were arranged on consoles, decorated in tones of blue and natural terra-cotta (005-003). Above each bay, a terra-cotta frieze two metres high bore children holding cartouches on a golden background (005-004).

## The Retrospective Exhibition

The Palace of Liberal Arts also housed the retrospective history of labor — a pioneering display of anthropological science and industrial history. Organized into five divisions (anthropology, liberal arts, arts and trades, transportation, and military arts), it aimed "to retrace with broad strokes the stages of human genius" (005-006).

## In the Journal

The January 11, 1890 issue devoted [[Section-5]] to the Palaces of the Liberal and Fine Arts, describing the Champ de Mars exhibition architecture in detail. The terra-cotta and ceramic innovations showcased there were cited as evidence of the material's decorative potential for modern buildings.
