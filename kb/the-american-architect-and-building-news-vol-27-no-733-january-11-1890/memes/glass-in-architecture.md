# Glass in Architecture

## Definition

Glass in architecture encompasses the use of glass as a building material for windows, roofing, skylights, and interior partitions. In 1890s architectural practice, glass served both functional and aesthetic roles: admitting daylight, providing transparency in public buildings, and creating visual distinction in façades.

## Historical Context

By 1890, glass manufacturing had reached industrial scale in the United States. The journal documents "over forty glass furnaces" (013-023) operating in the Pittsburgh region, fueled by the natural gas supply of Ohio and Indiana. Plate-glass had become the standard for urban building windows, with New York City leading in its adoption.

A contemporary trade debate surrounded the tariff on imported glass. As J.S. Moore wrote in the *New York Times*, quoted in the journal: "there is not a city in the world that can beat New York in handsome windows" (013-018). The duty on plate-glass varied by size, ranging from approximately 14% for small panes to over 130% for the largest sizes (013-021).

## Technical Description

### Glass Types and Applications

The 1890 journal distinguishes several categories of architectural glass. Plate-glass, used in the "houses of the rich" (013-018), was the premium option for storefronts and upscale residences. Common green window glass, also called sheet glass, served the broader market: "those houses are glazed by what is known as common green window glass" (013-019).

### Structural Uses

Glass found structural application as roofing material in public buildings. The [[hotel-de-soto|De Soto Hotel]] in Savannah featured "a 'rotunda' with glass roof at the rear of hall" (010-006), functioning as a lounging area. School designs of the period used "movable glass screens" (010-020) to divide classrooms from central halls while preserving light transmission.

### Water Quality Demonstration

An exhibition display at the 1889 Paris Exposition used "three glass reservoirs, containing water from the Seine, from the Ourcq and the Vanne" (004-015) to demonstrate water quality differences. This application shows glass's role in scientific and sanitary demonstration in addition to its architectural uses.

## Significance

Glass transformed the architectural experience of interior space. Large plate-glass windows and glass roofs allowed daylight to penetrate deep into buildings, while glass partitions enabled flexible interior arrangements without sacrificing illumination. The journal's extensive treatment of glass tariffs (Sections 13) reflects its economic importance: an estimated $1,563,000 in annual duties on common window glass alone (013-021).

## In the Journal

The January 11, 1890 issue references architectural glass in multiple contexts: as roofing material at the [[hotel-de-soto|De Soto Hotel]] (010-006), as movable screens in a school competition design (010-020), and in a detailed discussion of glass tariffs and their impact on consumers (013-018–013-023). The Exposition section notes glass water reservoirs as display objects (004-015).
