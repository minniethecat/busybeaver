# Francis Rombout

Francis Rombout was a seventeenth-century landholder in the Province of New York who, together with [[Gulian Verplanck]], received a substantial land grant from the Wappinger Indians in 1683.

## Biography

### Land Grant

The Verplanck homestead lands, located along the east side of the Hudson River, were granted by the Wappinger Indians in 1683 to [[Gulian Verplanck]] and Francis Rombout, under a license given by [[Governor Thomas Dongan]], Commander-in-Chief of the Province of New York. The purchase was confirmed in 1685 by letters patent from [[King James II]] of England.

> [008-011] "The Verplanck homestead stands on the lands granted by the Wappinger Indians, in 1683, to Gulian Verplanck and Francis Rombout, under a license given by Governor Thomas Dongan Commander-in-Chief of the Province of New York, and confirmed, in 1685, by letters patent from King James the II."

The grant comprised 85,000 acres and extended from the Fresh Kill (called Matteawan by the natives) northward along the Hudson River for five hundred rods beyond the Great Wappinger's Kill, and easterly into the woods for sixteen English miles.
