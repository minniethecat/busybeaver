# Lucius Quinctius Cincinnatus

Legendary Roman statesman and military leader of the 5th century BCE, celebrated in Roman tradition for his civic virtue — relinquishing absolute power to return to his farm after saving Rome from invasion. He was the namesake of the **[[Society of the Cincinnati]]**, the hereditary society of Revolutionary War officers organized at the [[Verplanck Homestead]] in 1783 (009-003).

## Biography

### Early Life

Cincinnatus lived in the 5th century BCE during the early Roman Republic. According to Roman tradition, he was a patrician who lived on a small farm outside Rome, embodying the Roman ideal of agrarian simplicity and civic duty.

### Career

According to Livy's history, in 458 BCE Cincinnatus was appointed dictator of Rome to rescue a Roman army besieged by the Aequi on Mount Algidus. He defeated the enemy in a single day, celebrated a triumph in Rome, and resigned his dictatorship after just sixteen days, returning immediately to his farm.

Roman tradition records a second dictatorship in 439 BCE, when Cincinnatus was called upon at the age of eighty to suppress the alleged conspiracy of Spurius Maelius.

### Later Years / Legacy

Cincinnatus became the archetype of the citizen-soldier who serves the republic in crisis and returns to civilian life without seeking permanent power. This ideal deeply resonated with the founders of the American Republic, who saw themselves as modern Cincinnatus figures.

## In the Journal

Cincinnatus is not directly mentioned in the journal text, but his legacy is invoked through the [[Society of the Cincinnati]], whose founding at the [[Verplanck Homestead]] is documented in two sections.

The journal notes the homestead's role as the birthplace of the society:

> "It is made still further famous by the fact that under its roof was organized in 1783 the Society of the Cincinnati." (009-003)

The society's founding principles echoed the Cincinnatus legend: military service to the republic followed by a return to peaceful civilian pursuits (008-011).

## Historical Significance

The Society of the Cincinnati was founded by officers of the Continental Army in May 1783, on the eve of the army's disbandment. They chose the name **Cincinnati** — the plural of Cincinnatus — to honor the Roman ideal of the citizen-soldier who returns to civilian life after serving the republic. [[George Washington]] served as the society's first President General.

The society's founding principles rejected the temptation of monarchical or dictatorial power in favor of republican virtue, directly echoing the Cincinnatus legend that had inspired the Revolutionary generation.
