# Competitive Bidding

The practice of inviting multiple contractors or architects to submit competing proposals for building work, a system that in 1890 was both standard industry practice and a frequent source of professional disputes over plans, commissions, and compensation. [013-001]

## Definition

Competitive bidding in 19th-century American architecture involved architects preparing plans and specifications that clients or competing builders could use to solicit sub-bids from contractors. The system created tension between the architect's right to compensation for intellectual work (plans and specifications) and the client's or competitor's ability to execute those plans without paying the originating architect. [013-001]

## Historical Context

The [[contract-system|competitive bidding]] system emerged alongside the professionalization of architecture in America. As architectural services became distinct from building trades, the question of who owned the design — and who should be paid for it — became contentious. The journal *The American Architect and Building News* served as a forum for adjudicating these disputes, publishing architects' grievances and offering editorial opinions that functioned as de facto professional standards. [013-003]

## Technical Description

### The Bidding Process

The standard [[contract-system|competitive bidding]] process involved an architect preparing plans and specifications for a client. Competing builders or architects would then solicit sub-bids from the same pool of subcontractors, using the original architect's documents. The lowest bidder typically won the contract — but the original architect might be excluded from the construction phase entirely. [013-001]

### The Sauer Case (1890)

A representative dispute published in the January 11, 1890 issue involved architect F.C. Sauer. After preparing plans and specifications for Client A, Sauer discovered that competing builders B and C were using his documents to solicit sub-bids for a related building, without his authorization or compensation. When confronted, B and C offered no reply to Sauer's demand for commission. [013-001]

The journal's editors ruled decisively in Sauer's favor:

> "We think that you are entitled to the full commission of five per cent on the cost of the 35-foot building, and believe that you can collect it." [013-003]

### Commission Structure

The standard architectural commission in 1890 was five percent of construction cost. The professional consensus, as articulated by the journal's editors, held that the architect was entitled to this commission whenever their plans and specifications were used — even if another builder executed the work. This established an important precedent for intellectual property rights in architectural practice. [013-003]

## Significance

The [[contract-system|competitive bidding]] debate highlighted fundamental tensions in architectural professionalism: the architect as artist versus the architect as service provider, the value of design documents as intellectual property, and the role of professional publications in establishing ethical norms. The journal's willingness to adjudicate disputes and publish editorial opinions helped establish standards of practice before formal licensing and professional codes existed.
