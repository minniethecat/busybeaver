# Dome Construction

## Definition

Dome construction refers to the design and building of curved roof structures that span large spaces without intermediate supports. In the 1890 architectural discourse, domes represented both technical achievement and architectural symbolism, appearing in contexts from ancient vaults to contemporary exhibition halls.

## Historical Context

Domes have been a feature of monumental architecture since antiquity. The journal traces their development from early civilizations, noting that "square rooms were covered with dome-shaped vaults" (003-020) in ancient architecture. The Egyptian "central dome" (004-014) appeared in palace and temple complexes, while the design of "the domes of the pavilions at the angles" (005-004) in French exhibition architecture demonstrated the form's continued relevance.

The 1889 Paris Exhibition, extensively discussed in the journal, featured some of the most ambitious dome construction of the era, showcasing French engineering prowess.

## Technical Description

The journal's most detailed dome reference describes the Palace of Liberal Arts at the 1889 Paris Exhibition: "Each is crowned with a dome fifty-five metres high" (005-002), a remarkable height for the period. The covering of these domes required special attention—"the covering of the two domes of the palaces has given a valuable opportunity" (005-003) for innovation in roofing materials.

The structural challenge of dome construction was substantial. The domes were integrated with other architectural elements, with "staircases opening under the dome upon each side of the rotunda" (005-007), creating a complex spatial composition that united structural and circulatory functions.

## Significance

Dome construction in the 1890 journal represents architectural ambition at its most visible. The fifty-five-metre domes of the Paris Exhibition palaces were engineering achievements that defined the skyline of the era's most important architectural event. The journal's detailed attention to their design reflects the profession's fascination with spanning great spaces and the symbolic weight carried by dome forms.

## In the Journal

The January 11, 1890 issue discusses domes in the context of ancient architecture (Sections 3-4) and the 1889 Paris Exhibition palaces (Section 5). The Exhibition domes are described with technical precision, reflecting the journal's dual commitment to historical scholarship and contemporary engineering.
