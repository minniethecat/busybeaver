# Exhibition Palaces

The Palaces of the Fine Arts and Liberal Arts were two monumental exhibition buildings of equal dimensions constructed for the 1889 Paris Exposition Universelle, each covering 21,000 square metres and featuring a central nave 209.31 metres long by 53.5 metres wide, surrounded by galleries and distinguished by innovative [[architectural-terra-cotta|ceramic decoration]] that integrated architectural fabric with ornamentation.

## Definition

Exhibition palaces were large-scale temporary or semi-permanent structures erected for international expositions and world's fairs in the nineteenth century. Distinguished from permanent civic architecture, they were designed to house displays of art, industry, and technology while themselves serving as architectural showpieces demonstrating the latest construction techniques and decorative arts. The twin palaces at the 1889 Paris Exposition represented the apex of this building type.

## The 1889 Paris Palaces

### Architecture and Scale

The two palaces — one devoted to the Fine Arts, the other to the Liberal Arts — were of identical dimensions and similar appearance, each covering 21,000 square metres (005-002). A large central nave measuring 209.31 metres in length by 53.5 metres in width formed the principal exhibition space, surrounded by galleries on the ground floor and first story. Pavilions at the angles of the Seine-facing palace featured domes covered in enamelled porcelain tiles.

### Ceramic Innovation

The palaces showcased French advances in [[architectural-terra-cotta|architectural ceramics]]. Ceramic work became integral to the building fabric through the Müller brick — a brick covered with enamel on one face, bearing the name of its inventor (005-003). The domes of the corner pavilions were covered with enamelled porcelain tiles, a new product invented by M. Parvillée, described as having "great decorative richness" (005-004). Above each bay, a terra-cotta frieze two metres in height repeated a motif of children holding cartouches and garlands (005-004).

The architects responsible — Bréult, Boulanger, and Mortreux — also contributed to the ceramic division and foreign pavilions throughout the exposition grounds (005-003).

### The Retrospective Exhibition

The Palace of the Liberal Arts housed one of the exposition's principal attractions: the retrospective history of labor and anthropologic science. Conceived by M. Jules Simon, president of the Superior Commission, the exhibition aimed "to retrace with broad strokes the history of labor, to show that work is not only a necessity but an honor" (005-005) (005-006). The central nave was wholly occupied by this exhibit, with grand woodwork porticos and galleries forming four divisions with interior courts, approached by monumental staircases opening under the central dome that sheltered a theater (005-007).

## Significance

The 1889 exhibition palaces demonstrated the integration of industrial ceramic production with monumental architecture, pointing toward new possibilities for decorative building materials. The retrospective exhibition within the Liberal Arts palace also represented an early example of the museological approach to industrial and labor history — treating tools, products, and craft traditions as worthy of systematic display alongside fine art.
