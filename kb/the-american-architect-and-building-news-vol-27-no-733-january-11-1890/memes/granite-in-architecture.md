# Granite in Architecture

## Definition

Granite is a coarse-grained igneous rock composed primarily of quartz, feldspar, and mica. In architecture, granite is valued for its extreme durability, compressive strength, and resistance to weathering. Its hardness — while an asset for longevity — makes it the most challenging of the common building stones to carve and decorate.

## Historical Context

Granite has been used in monumental construction across civilizations. The journal documents a Chinese bridge at Sueno chou Fou "built over the point of an arm of the sea" comprising "two hundred and fifty piles made of material of enormous bulk" (003-009). The roadway was "formed with single blocks of granite" and "guarded on each side by a balustrade" (003-009) — demonstrating the use of granite for both structural spans and architectural finishing.

In the 1890s, granite continued to be specified for buildings demanding permanence and dignity. Its use in [[religious-architecture|ecclesiastical architecture]] connected contemporary practice to ancient traditions.

## Technical Description

### Material Properties

Granite's defining characteristic is its obduracy. The journal describes it as "the stern nature of the primitive rock — obdurate alike to the chisel and to time" (010-002). This dual resistance shaped architectural expression: granite buildings could not rely on elaborate carving for their effect, instead drawing visual power from "bold design" and mass.

The journal notes that Egyptian granite statuary retained "the glittering lustre of the polished cheeks of Rameses" (010-002) after thousands of years, while also preserving "the cold, stony eyes of the sphynx, precisely as the chisel last touched them" (010-002). This incredible permanence made granite the material of choice for monuments meant to endure across millennia.

### Architectural Expression in Granite

The journal's description of [[old-trinity-church|Old Trinity Church]] in New York (Section 10) provides a detailed case study of granite's architectural consequences. Because the material "has entirely governed the character of the architecture," it "has precluded lightness and decoration" while providing "opportunities for a certain gloomy dignity" (010-002).

Specific architectural features shaped by granite included the great west window — "not mullioned, but divided by long massive stone shafts into seven arched compartments" (010-002) — and "the low-browed doorway beneath, with its heavy semicircular arch" (010-002). The upper tier of "storm windows" featured "the plain, unmoulded arch" (010-002) characteristic of earlier Norman architecture. Ornament was minimal: "one or two niches and other small details, have been decorated; but as if the artist had abandoned the task of chiselling his obdurate materials as a vain one, ornament goes no farther" (010-002).

The church's carved, gilded, and brightly-colored timber roof provided deliberate contrast: "the eye gratefully relieves itself from the gloom below, by wandering over its quaint devices and gaudy hues" (010-002).

## Significance

Granite occupies a unique position in the hierarchy of building stones. Its extreme durability made it the material of temples, monuments, and churches — buildings meant to convey permanence. But its resistance to carving also constrained architectural expression, forcing designers toward mass, proportion, and silhouette rather than surface ornament. The journal's extended discussion of granite in the context of [[old-trinity-church|Old Trinity Church]] illustrates how material choice fundamentally shapes architectural character.
