# Roof Construction

## Definition

Roof construction encompasses the structural design, materials, and methods used to create the uppermost covering of a building. In 1890 architectural practice, roof types ranged from flat roofs on urban buildings to pitched roofs with various covering materials, each suited to different climates, styles, and structural systems.

## Historical Context

Roof forms have always reflected climate, available materials, and cultural traditions. The journal's survey of ancient architecture reveals the diversity of roof construction across civilizations. Egyptian houses typically had "flat roofs with a framework of light materials" (004-004), while many had "a light roof supported by slender wooden columns" (004-004) over terraces.

In China, the distinctive curved roof form was noted as "in the form of a Chinese roof" (003-012) over certain temple structures. Egyptian buildings often had "flat roofs, and, as is the case of all flat roofs, they were protected by a light framework" (003-024).

## Technical Description

The 1890 journal describes several roof types and materials. Flat roofs were common in ancient and contemporary architecture, requiring careful waterproofing and drainage. Gambrel roofs—a two-sided roof with two slopes on each side—appear in the description of the Verplanck homestead: "The gambrel roof is shingled, descends low" (009-003).

Roof coverings varied by region and building type. The journal references "tiles for the roofs" (010-018) in one building description, with "the few stone dressings being of Ham Hill" stone used selectively. The structural weight of heavy roof systems was a significant engineering concern—the journal notes one building's design responded "to the extreme weight of the roof" (005-009), requiring "a series of strong iron bars" as reinfor[[cement-and-concrete|cement]].

Roofs also served as platforms for building services. The journal recounts an incident where a worker "rushed up on the roof of the building to cut the wires" (009-021) during an electrical emergency, and another where "a tinner was killed on the roof of a building on touching an electric wire" (009-023), illustrating roof access for maintenance.

## Significance

Roof construction in the 1890s journal reflects the breadth of architectural knowledge expected of practitioners: from ancient precedents to contemporary materials, from structural engineering to workplace safety. The roof was simultaneously the building's primary weather barrier and an expression of regional and stylistic identity.
