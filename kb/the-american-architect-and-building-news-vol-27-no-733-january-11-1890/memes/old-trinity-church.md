# Old Trinity Church

Old Trinity Church, New York, was a granite ecclesiastical edifice remarkable for the durability of its stone construction. The journal described it as bearing "unmistakable marks of age, but none of decay" — gray with centuries of weathering yet displaying none of the crumbling deterioration typical of less durable building materials (010-001).

## Architecture

The church was constructed of granite, the same material that "handed down to us, through thousands of years, the cold, stony eyes of the sphynx, precisely as the chisel last touched them" (010-002). The journal noted the unusual survival of only the nave — typically the chancel was the first part erected and sometimes the only surviving portion. The remains of the central and eastern portions told of the building's construction sequence and subsequent history (010-003).

## Material Durability

The journal's description of Old Trinity Church served as an object lesson in the importance of material selection in architecture. Granite, unlike softer stones, resisted centuries of weathering without losing the crispness of its carved ornament. This contrasted sharply with the fire-vulnerable iron-and-[[masonry-construction|masonry building]]s discussed elsewhere in the same issue, positioning Old Trinity as an exemplar of material permanence in an era increasingly concerned with building longevity and [[fire-protection|fire safety]].
