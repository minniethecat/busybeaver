# Architectural Drawing

## Definition

Architectural drawing encompasses the production of plans, perspectives, sketches, and detail drawings used to communicate design intent to clients, contractors, and the public. In the 1890 journal, architectural drawing appears in three distinct contexts: publication and media representation, professional contract documentation, and architectural education.

## Historical Context

By 1890, architectural drawing had become an essential tool of professional practice. The American Architect and Building News regularly relied on architects to supply drawings for publication, though the editors frequently lamented the difficulty of obtaining them from American practitioners.

## Drawing for Publication

### The Struggle to Represent American Architecture

The editors of the journal devoted considerable space to explaining the challenges of securing architectural drawings for publication. They contrasted the cooperation received from European architects with the reluctance of American architects:

> We know where we can get drawings, plans, photographs, descriptions and details of all the best current work in North and South Germany, Italy, France and England, and even in Russia, but to secure anything like a decent representation of modern American architecture has hitherto been, according to our experience, and for some mysterious reason, the most difficult of all. [001-008]

The editors detailed a typical encounter: an architect would appear cooperative during an editorial visit, only to never actually deliver the promised materials:

> The latter seems pleased to see him, shows him the drawings of the building in question, appears to be gratified at his praises, and readily agrees to allow the publication of the plan and perspective. The editor lays these drawings aside, and proposes to take them with him, but the architect politely insists that he cannot allow him to burden himself, and promises that he will send them immediately by express. [001-009]

### Editorial Frustration

After years of such experiences, the editors expressed a frank weariness with the situation:

> After a good many years of such struggles, we should be inclined to say that we would trouble ourselves no further, and that American architects who are capable of carrying out important work successfully, and do not want other people to know it, may please themselves in the matter. [001-010]

Yet they persisted, convinced that American architecture deserved international recognition:

> We are sure that the best of it is equal to the best anywhere in Europe, and we venture to say that there is nobody more anxious than we are to see it properly published and properly set forth before the rest of the world. [001-010]

### Drawing as Professional Exposure

The journal argued that publication of drawings served the architect's own interests:

> It is not to his advantage, nor yet to the advantage of architecture in this country, that his work is not published. The interest of every architect lies in the exposure of his design to the gaze of the cold world. [001-010]

## Drawing in Contract Practice

### Standard Contract Provisions

The journal published an agreement between architect and client that specified how drawings were to be treated in contractual relationships. Drawings formed the basis for payment schedules and defined the scope of professional services:

> Upon completion of the preliminary sketches, 1 per cent of estimated cost; upon completion of the general drawings and specifications, an additional 1-1/2 per cent of estimated cost; upon completion of details an additional 1 per cent of estimated cost; and upon completion of the work, the charge for supervision or inspection. [012-025]

### Drawings and Contractor Obligations

The contract specified that drawings were to be followed precisely by contractors:

> The final certificate being an adjustment of the contract and extras, and also an expression of judgment on our part that the work has been carried out according to the general drawings and specifications and contracts by the contractors. [012-021]

### Timing and Payment

Drawings were subject to deadlines, with payment due even if construction was delayed:

> In case contracts are not entered into for the work within six months after the drawings are ready for contractors to estimate, payment shall be made for the work done at the rates herein before specified. [012-027]

## Drawing in Architectural Education

The journal reported on the formation of the Boston Architectural Club, which included drawing instruction in its curriculum:

> It is intended, in addition to the usual monthly competitions, to make a special feature of regular class-work throughout the year, this will consist of courses in constructional work, free-hand drawing, water-color work, plumbing, architectural history, etc. [012-005]

## The Value of Competent Drawings

The journal argued that proper architectural drawings were essential for protecting property owners, who might otherwise think "an architect is superfluous":

> Owners frequently think that unless they want "fancy" drawings and fronts, an architect is superfluous. [012-032]

This passage reveals the 1890 perception that architectural drawings were sometimes seen as a luxury rather than a necessity — a view the journal's editors actively worked to counter.

## Significance

Architectural drawing in the 1890 journal appears not merely as a technical skill but as a professional imperative. The journal's extended discussion of the difficulty of obtaining drawings from American architects reveals tensions within the profession: between public exposure and professional privacy, between European models of architectural publishing and American reticence. The contract provisions published in the journal demonstrate that by 1890, standardized practices for compensating architects for drawing production had been established, with detailed schedules linking payment to specific drawing deliverables.
