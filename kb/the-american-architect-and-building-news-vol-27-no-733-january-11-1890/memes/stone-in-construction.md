# Stone in Construction

## Definition

Stone in construction refers to the use of natural stone—including freestone, limestone, granite, and hewn stone—as a primary or facing building material. In 1890 architectural practice, stone was valued for its durability, fire resistance, and aesthetic qualities, though its use varied significantly by region and building type.

## Historical Context

Stone has been a fundamental building material throughout architectural history. The 1890 journal reflects a period when stone was widely used for foundations, walls, and ornamental dressings. The choice between stone, brick, and timber often depended on local availability, cost, and the building's intended prestige.

In antiquity, as the journal documents, "the foundations are of hewn stone, the rest is of brick faced with smoothly-joined stone" (003-007). The Egyptians "abounded in stone that could be easily cut" (003-025), demonstrating how local geology shaped architectural traditions.

## Technical Description

Stone construction in the 1890s encompassed several techniques. Rubble masonry used rough, irregular stones for foundations and walls. Ashlar masonry employed precisely cut and fitted stone blocks for visible faces. Freestone—fine-grained stone that could be cut in any direction—was preferred for ornamental carving.

The journal reports that at [[old-trinity-church|Old Trinity Church]], New York, "the inside walls are finished in brick and stone" (010-008), while the church's structure featured "long massive stone shafts into seven arched compartments" (010-002). The fatal vulnerability of freestone in fire was noted: at Trinity, "they were built of freestone. Incrusted, as it were, in the fire" (010-003).

Stone also served in large-scale civil works. The journal describes "a great wall of stone and [[cement-and-concrete|cement]] twenty feet thick" (009-010), illustrating the material's application beyond building construction. The [[slater-memorial-museum|Slater Memorial Museum]] was "encompassed by a massive stone wall" (007-005).

## Significance

Stone represented the intersection of geology, craftsmanship, and architectural expression. Its durability made it the preferred material for monumental and institutional buildings. The 1890 journal's discussion of stone spans ancient construction techniques to contemporary American practice, reflecting its enduring importance.

The material was also a significant commercial commodity. The journal references "users of lumber, stone, [[cement-and-concrete|cement]] and building material" (014-002) in trade surveys, indicating stone's role in the broader construction economy.

## In the Journal

The January 11, 1890 issue references stone construction in contexts ranging from ancient Egyptian and Chinese architecture (Sections 3-4) to contemporary American churches and museums (Sections 7, 9, 10). The material appears in discussions of durability, fire resistance, and commercial supply.
