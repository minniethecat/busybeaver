# C.J.H. Woodbury

C.J.H. Woodbury was a [[Boston]]-based [[mechanical engineering|mechanical engineer]] active in the late nineteenth century, specializing in [[mill construction]] and [[fire protection]] engineering.

He contributed papers to the [[American Society of Mechanical Engineers]] and advised the *American Architect* on structural methods in industrial building (012-002). His expertise spanned wooden column design, auger boring techniques, and the prevention of structural weakness in [[slow-burning construction]].

## Professional Work

Woodbury's engineering practice focused on the structural integrity of [[mill construction|mill buildings]], particularly the design and boring of heavy timber columns.

> The method followed by the best mill-builders is to bore a hole along the axis one and three-fourth to two inches in diameter.
> — (013-011)

He described the evolution of this technique: the older method — boring from both ends — often failed when augers followed the wood grain, causing the holes to miss and creating structural weakness at the column sides (013-011).

The improved approach Woodbury advocated used a lathe with a hollow headstock and guide, boring from one end only to maintain a true central axis. This method defined the column's axis whether turned or left square.

## Significance

Woodbury represents the professionalizing class of mechanical engineers who bridged industrial practice and engineering science in late-nineteenth-century [[United States|America]].

| Area | Contribution |
|------|-------------|
| Mill construction | Boring methods for heavy timber columns |
| Fire protection | Structural practices for slow-burning buildings |
| Professional engineering | Papers before [[american-society-of-mechanical-engineers|ASME]] |
| Technical publishing | Advice to architectural journals |

His work on transverse vent holes — a pair of five-eighth-inch holes bored near each column end — reduced checking in the same manner used "time immemorial, for getting out hubs for wagon wheels" (013-011).
