# Steel in Construction

## Definition

Steel is an alloy of iron and carbon, distinguished from cast and wrought iron by its superior tensile strength, ductility, and uniformity. In 1890s construction, steel was increasingly displacing wrought iron for structural members, driven by improvements in the Bessemer and open-hearth manufacturing processes that made steel more economical and reliable.

## Historical Context

By 1890, the American steel industry was centered in Pittsburgh, where "the iron and steel mills of Pittsburgh" (013-023) formed the core of an industrial network powered by natural gas from the Ohio and Indiana fields. The industry was expanding rapidly: the journal's business survey notes "a very great increase in rolling-stock" and "a greater increase in locomotive capacity than has been made during any one year in our history" (014-002), all of which depended on steel production.

The steel market of 1890 showed signs of overheating. "The users of steel blooms and billets have bought so far ahead that manufacturers are now declining to make further contracts" (014-002). This forward-buying reflected both confidence in continued construction growth and caution about potential price increases.

## Technical Description

### Steel vs. Iron

The journal distinguishes between cast iron, wrought iron, and steel, noting their different thermal properties. Cast iron melts at 2,756°F, while wrought iron melts at 2,950°F (006-003). These differences mattered for fire-resistant construction, where the failure temperature of structural metal determined building safety.

### Industrial Context

Steel production in 1890 was closely tied to the natural gas industry. Professor Edward Orton of Ohio warned the American Geological Society that natural gas "is now being used as the basis of a varied line of manufactures, the annual products of which aggregate many millions of dollars, and it is driving, besides the iron and steel mills of Pittsburgh, potteries and brick works, over forty glass furnaces" (013-023). He predicted the gas supply would be "drained in less than nine years" (013-023), threatening the energy foundation of the steel industry.

### Commercial Role

The journal's trade survey identifies "users of iron ore and iron and steel" (014-002) alongside users of lumber, stone, and [[cement-and-concrete|cement]] as the primary consumers in the building materials market. These users were "protecting themselves against unwarranted and unexpected advances in the cost of their raw material by making purchases for future requirements, ranging from three to six months" (014-002).

## Significance

Steel represented the cutting edge of structural materials in 1890. Its growing availability and declining cost enabled taller buildings, longer bridge spans, and more efficient industrial structures. The journal's attention to steel supply chains — from ore mining to mill production to end-user purchasing — reflects steel's central role in the construction economy. The natural gas dependency documented in the journal also presaged the energy transition challenges that would face heavy industry.

## In the Journal

The January 11, 1890 issue references steel in its natural gas report (013-023), its comprehensive trade and business survey (014-002), and indirectly in its discussion of fire-resistant construction temperatures (006-003). The coverage emphasizes steel's manufacturing and commercial context rather than specific architectural applications.
