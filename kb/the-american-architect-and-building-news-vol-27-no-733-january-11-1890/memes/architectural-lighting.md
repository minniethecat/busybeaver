# Architectural Lighting

## Definition

Architectural lighting encompasses both natural daylighting strategies — the design of windows, skylights, and apertures — and artificial lighting systems, including gas and the newly emerging electric lighting. In 1890, architecture was at a transition point between traditional daylight-dependent design and the integration of electric illumination as a building service.

## Historical Context

Lighting has been a fundamental architectural concern since antiquity. The journal's survey of ancient dwellings notes that in Judea, "certain windows, doubtless those lighting the women's apartments, were provided with lattices similar to the _moucharabiehs_ of the Arab houses of the present day" (003-026). These lattice screens filtered light while maintaining privacy — an architectural solution that persisted across millennia and cultures.

By 1890, electric lighting had moved from novelty to practical building system. The Brush system, one of the first commercially successful arc-lighting technologies, was being installed in major buildings. The [[hotel-de-soto|De Soto Hotel]] in Savannah, completed in 1890, featured "the Brush system of electric lighting" (010-006) throughout its 300 rooms.

## Technical Description

### Natural Daylighting

Traditional architectural lighting relied on window pla[[cement-and-concrete|cement]], courtyard design, and building orientation. Ancient dwellings used "flat roofs surrounded by a parapet" (003-026) where people could sleep outdoors in warm seasons, supplementing interior daylight with open-air living. Glass windows, roof glazing, and the "movable glass screens" (010-020) of school designs extended daylight deeper into buildings.

### Electric Lighting Systems

The Brush electric lighting system installed at the [[hotel-de-soto|De Soto Hotel]] represented state-of-the-art building services in 1890. Developed by Charles F. Brush, the system used high-voltage direct-current arc lamps. Its installation alongside "elevator, heating and laundry work" (010-006) by the Whittier Machine Co. shows that electric lighting was already treated as one of several mechanical building systems requiring specialized contractors.

The hotel's integration of lighting with other services was comprehensive: "special arrangements have been made for introducing large volumes of warmed or cooled air into the halls and corridors" (010-006), while L. Haberstroh & Son "decorated the walls and ceilings, making a special feature of the dining-room" (010-006). This coordination of lighting, HVAC, and interior decoration exemplifies the increasingly complex building services coordination of the period.

### Glass and Light Transmission

The architectural use of glass — from "[[glass-in-architecture|plate-glass]]" windows to the De Soto's "rotunda with glass roof" (010-006) — was inseparable from lighting design. See [[Glass in Architecture]].

## Significance

Architectural lighting in 1890 marked the beginning of a transformation from purely passive, daylight-dependent design to integrated active systems. The Brush system at the [[hotel-de-soto|De Soto Hotel]], combined with the building's glass-roofed rotunda and verandas, represented a hybrid approach: electric light supplemented and extended daylight rather than replacing it. This transitional period shaped modern architectural practice, where lighting is treated as a distinct building service discipline.

## In the Journal
