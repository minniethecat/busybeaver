# Sanitary Entombment

A mortuary system proposed in 1890 for disposing of the dead in above-ground sepulchres within handsome suburban edifices, using controlled anhydrous (dry) [[ventilation-systems|air circulation]] to desiccate remains and destroy disease germs, thereby satisfying both the sanitary requirements of public health and the emotional demands of the bereaved.

## Definition

Sanitary entombment refers to the practice of placing the deceased in sealed, above-ground sepulchres — chambers within a mausoleum building — where the atmosphere is controlled to maintain anhydrous (moisture-free) conditions. The dry air circulates through each sepulchre, absorbing moisture and any morbific (disease-causing) matter, which is then conveyed to a separate furnace structure for thermal destruction before the cleansed air is released to the external atmosphere. The primary mechanism of preservation is desiccation — natural drying that halts decomposition without the mutilation or chemical substitution involved in embalming (008-003).

## Historical Context

In the late nineteenth century, growing urban populations and heightened awareness of public health led to increasing concern about traditional burial practices. Earth-burial in crowded urban cemeteries was recognized as a potential source of ground-water contamination and disease transmission. Two alternatives existed: cremation, which had gained some acceptance but failed to satisfy the emotional needs of the bereaved, and entombment, which had long been preferred by those who could afford it but was not yet proven sanitary (007-010).

The sanitary entombment movement argued that entombment had always been "the best that the living could do for their dead" (007-013). If entombment could be rendered sanitary through modern engineering and architectural design, it would become the ideal solution — satisfying both affection and public health.

## Technical Description

### Principles — Desiccation

The core scientific principle is desiccation: the removal of moisture from remains and the surrounding atmosphere. Wherever dry air envelops lifeless forms, decomposition is prevented. This principle was observed in nature — in deserts, on elevated plains, and on mountain slopes where dry winds passed over the dead — as well as in the morgue of the Hospice of St. Bernard, where bodies dried undecomposed in the thin, dry alpine air (008-004).

Unlike embalming, which the journal characterized as involving the mutilation of the body and substitution of foreign substances for human flesh, desiccation is a natural process that permits "a due return of material humanity to the elements that gave it birth" (008-003). Embalming was not a lost art — the journal noted it could be practiced as skillfully as in ancient Egypt — but desiccation was presented as a more dignified and natural alternative (008-002).

### Disease Germ Destruction

Experiments by Dr. Sternberg of the Hoagland Laboratory, corroborated by other expert investigators, established that desiccation efficiently destroys disease germs. This scientific finding was crucial to the sanitary entombment proposition, as it demonstrated that the drying process would not merely preserve remains but would actively neutralize the pathogens that made traditional burial dangerous (008-007).

### Proposed Facility Design

The proposed mausoleum facility would be an extensive and handsome edifice, potentially located even in thickly-populated urban areas. The unit of construction would be the individual sepulchre — a sealed chamber constructed so that anhydrous air could enter, absorb moisture and morbific matter, and exit to a separate furnace structure. The furnace would complete the sanitary process by thermally destroying all noxious material before releasing cleansed air to the atmosphere (008-009). Electrical appliances, lighting, and decorative elements were envisioned to make each sepulchre a dignified place of repose (008-009).

## Significance

Sanitary entombment represented an innovative intersection of architecture, engineering, and public health. The proposition addressed a genuine urban problem — the unsanitary conditions of crowded cemeteries — with a solution that respected both scientific knowledge and human sentiment. While the evidence from natural desiccation and Dr. Sternberg's experiments was described as "ample" (008-005), the journal acknowledged that further carefully-conducted experiments under the supervision of medical and engineering authorities were being pursued to "make assurance doubly sure" (008-006).

## In the Journal

> It remains to be considered whether entombment can be made sanitary. If it can be the problem is solved, for entombment has ever been the best that the living could do for their dead, and, with the added advantage of promoting, or ceasing to be prejudicial to, the public health entombment will be the choice of all whom cost or caprice does not deter.
> — (007-013)

> When, now, it is added that desiccation has been ascertained to be an efficient agent in the destruction of disease germs, as proved by the experiments of Dr. Sternberg, of the Hoagland Laboratory, and by the investigations of other experts, enough seems to have been said to establish the truth of the assertion that entombment can be made sanitary.
> — (008-007)
