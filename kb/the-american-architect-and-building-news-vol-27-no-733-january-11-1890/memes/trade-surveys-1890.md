# Trade Surveys of 1890

A regular feature of *The American Architect and Building News* providing economic intelligence and business outlook analysis for the architecture and construction industries. [014-001]

## Definition

Trade surveys were periodic economic reports published in the journal, surveying business conditions across manufacturing, construction, transportation, and finance. They served architects and builders as market intelligence, helping them anticipate demand for construction services, material costs, and regional development opportunities. [014-001]

## Historical Context

By January 1890, the American economy was entering a period of significant expansion. The trade survey for the first issue of the new year captured this optimism, cataloging the factors driving construction demand: railway expansion, industrial growth, and an active housing market. These surveys reflect the journal's role not just as a technical publication but as a business intelligence resource for the architectural profession.

## Technical Description

### Economic Indicators for 1890

The January 11, 1890 survey identified multiple growth factors for the construction industry:

- Railway construction projected at 50% more mileage than the previous year
- Significant increases in lake tonnage and inland waterway shipping capacity
- Record locomotive manufacturing capacity
- Active house-building and shop/factory construction
- Expansion of banking and building-and-loan associations, particularly in newer states
- Increased output from western gold and silver mines
- Opening of new coal beds supporting local industrialization [014-002]

### Market Intelligence Function

The trade surveys provided practical intelligence for architectural practice by:

- Tracking raw material costs (cotton, wool, metals) and manufacturer purchasing patterns
- Monitoring the balance between production output and "consumptive requirements"
- Reporting on price trends, described as advancing "by infinitesimal degrees"
- Noting the protective purchasing strategy of manufacturers buying 3–6 months ahead to hedge against material cost increases [014-002]

### Relationship to Architectural Practice

For architects, the trade surveys offered context for project planning. Active railway construction signaled opportunities for station and depot design. Factory and shop building activity indicated demand for industrial architects. The growth of building-and-loan associations pointed to expanding residential markets. The opening of coal beds and mines suggested new communities requiring civic and commercial buildings. [014-002]

## Significance

The trade surveys illustrate the dual nature of architectural journalism in 1890: technical and aesthetic discourse alongside practical business intelligence. For the modern reader, these surveys provide a granular snapshot of the economic forces shaping American building at the dawn of the 1890s, a decade that would see the rise of the skyscraper, the expansion of railroad architecture, and the professional consolidation of American architecture.

## In the Journal

> The salient features of the business situation this week afford every encouragement to the promoters of new schemes and the pioneers in industry. [014-001]

> Speaking conservatively and in the light of recent experience, it is safe to assume and assert that production will be evenly gauged to consumptive requirements. [014-002]
