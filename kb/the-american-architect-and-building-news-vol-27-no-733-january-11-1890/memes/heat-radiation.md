# Heat Radiation

**Heat radiation** through uncovered or inadequately insulated building service pipes was the subject of Section 6 of *The American Architect and Building News*, January 11, 1890. The article presented quantitative analysis of heat loss and the comparative effectiveness of insulating materials, particularly magnesia-based coverings.

## Historical Context

By 1890, central [[heating-and-ventilation-systems|heating systems]] with boiler-pipes, cylinders, and radiators had become common in American buildings. The efficiency loss from uninsulated distribution — heat radiating into basements and service spaces rather than occupied rooms — had become a recognized engineering problem warranting dedicated journal coverage.

## Technical Analysis

### The Magnesia Covering

Section 6 was titled "Heat Radiation Through [[magnesia-covering|Magnesia Covering]]" (006-001). The foundational premise was economic:

> Every unit of heat radiated by boiler-pipe, cylinder or heater is absolute loss. (006-011)

The article compared multiple insulating materials, calculating precise heat transmission rates:

> Through 25 m.m. of Wm. Berkefield's fossil meal was 7.7 heat-units, through 25 m.m. of carb. magnesia it was 6.7 heat-units. (006-019)

The comparative ratio "7.7: 6.7 = $5.50: $4.80" (006-019) translated the thermal performance directly into coal-cost savings, making the economic case for insulation in terms building owners could understand.

### Fire Resistance Testing

The magnesia covering demonstrated remarkable fire resistance. A test at Lynn, Massachusetts, documented that surfaces covered with magnesia withstood fire conditions where "the heat of the fire at this place has been curiously determined to have been over 2,000°F" (006-003). The material was:

> So effective was its heat-resisting and non-heat-conducting power. (006-021)

### Real-World Application

The journal reported a practical case where a client initially rejected magnesia covering due to cost, but after experiencing excessive heat loss:

> Returned and directed that the magnesia covering be applied to his work. (006-014)

This anecdote illustrated the growing market acceptance driven by demonstrated performance.

## Significance

The January 1890 treatment of heat radiation represents an early example of building performance quantification. The article moved beyond qualitative claims ("good insulation") to calorimeter measurements, heat-unit calculations, and coal-value equivalents — anticipating the modern building science approach.

## In the Journal

Section 6 (006-001 through 006-023) is entirely devoted to this topic, with subsidiary references in Section 5 (005-014 through 005-016) regarding organic coverings and fire hazard.
