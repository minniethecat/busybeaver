# Timber Construction

## Definition

Timber construction refers to the use of wood beams, posts, and framing as the primary structural elements of a building. In 1890, timber remained the dominant structural material for most construction types, though its fire vulnerability had become a central concern in architectural and engineering discourse.

## Historical Context

Timber has been used in construction since the earliest human settlements. In the 19th century, the abundance of North American forests made timber the default building material for residential and light commercial construction. Heavy timber framing, using large-section wood members, was the standard for mill and factory buildings.

By 1890, the fire risk associated with timber construction had prompted significant innovation. The "slow-burning [[slow-burning-mill-construction|mill construction]]" method, which used heavy timbers with minimal projections and careful joinery, was developed to mitigate fire spread while retaining timber's economic advantages.

## Technical Description

The journal's discussion of timber construction centers on fire protection engineering. "The number of projections of timbers, which are more easily ignited" (011-001), was identified as a key fire risk factor. In [[slow-burning-mill-construction|slow-burning construction]], careful attention was paid to "securely joining them to the timber beams" (011-004).

The structural limitations of timber were acknowledged. At critical connections, the load could be "greater than can be sustained by timber" (011-005), requiring alternative materials or design solutions. The emphasis was on minimizing exposed timber surfaces and protecting beam ends from fire exposure.

## Significance

Timber construction in 1890 represented both the industry's foundation and its greatest challenge. The material's low cost and workability made it irreplaceable for most building types, but its combustibility drove the development of fire protection engineering as a distinct discipline. The [[slow-burning-mill-construction|slow-burning construction]] method, detailed in the journal's Section 11, was a direct response to timber's limitations and represented a significant advance in building safety.

## In the Journal

The January 11, 1890 issue discusses timber primarily in the context of fire protection and slow-burning [[slow-burning-mill-construction|mill construction]] (Section 11). The material's fire vulnerability is contrasted with emerging protective technologies and construction methods.
