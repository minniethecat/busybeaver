# Plaster Work

Plaster work, in late-nineteenth-century American building practice, encompassed both the traditional trade of applying plaster finishes to walls and ceilings and the specialized craft of working with plaster casts — assembling, repairing, and mounting sculptural reproductions for museum display.

## Definition

Plaster work involves the application of gypsum or lime-based plaster to building surfaces for finish, fireproofing, and ornament. In the museum context documented by the journal, plaster work extended to the skilled assembly of imported casts — large sculptural reproductions shipped from European workshops — which required expert plaster-workers to piece together, repair transit damage, and mount on pedestals or wall surfaces (006-018).

## Museum Application: Slater Memorial Museum

The [[slater-memorial-museum|Slater Memorial Museum]] in Norwich, Connecticut, provided a detailed case study in the costs and labor of plaster work. The museum's collection of casts — 124 in the Greek and Roman section and 103 in the Renaissance section — included some of the largest casts then made, such as selections from the Pergamon reliefs and the Nike of Samothrace, imported from London, Paris, Berlin, Munich, Florence, Rome, Naples, and Athens (006-012). Most of the plaster work was done by two men, with a third added occasionally, at an aggregate cost of $1,626.75 (006-018).

The museum's total cost for casts, including packing and transportation but excluding setup, was $13,968.68, averaging slightly less than $62 per catalog number. The journal cautioned that this average was "a dangerous guide" due to enormous differences in cast sizes, prices, and shipping distances (006-017).

## Significance

The journal's detailed accounting of plaster work costs served a deliberate purpose: to demonstrate that establishing a museum of art reproductions was financially accessible to any American city or college. "Is there any city or college in the Union in which this sum [$27,112.97] could not be raised for a similar purpose?" (006-014).
