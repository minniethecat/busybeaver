# Formation of the Society of the Cincinnati

The formation of the [[Society of the Cincinnati]] took place in May [[1783]] at the [[Verplanck Homestead]] in [[Fishkill, New York]], as the [[American Revolutionary War]] drew to a close.

Officers of the [[Continental Army]], soon to be disbanded, gathered to establish a fraternal organization that would preserve the bonds formed during eight years of war and commemorate the republican ideals for which they had fought.

## The Venue

The [[Verplanck Homestead]], seat of one of the oldest Dutch-American families in the [[Hudson Valley]], provided the historic setting for the society's founding.

The homestead was "made still further famous by the fact that under its roof was organized in 1783 the [[society-of-the-cincinnati|Society of the Cincinnati]]" (009-003). The choice of this location reflected the intersection of colonial heritage and Revolutionary identity.

## The Founding

The society was named after [[Lucius Quinctius Cincinnatus]], the Roman farmer-soldier who led Rome's armies and then returned to his plow — the model of republican virtue the founders sought to embody.

[[George Washington]], commander-in-chief of the Continental Army, was elected the society's first president. Membership was hereditary, passing to the eldest male descendants of the original officer members.

## Significance

The formation of the Cincinnati represented a pivotal moment in American institutional history. It was the new nation's first major fraternal organization and embodied the tension between republican equality and the natural desire of military leaders for permanent recognition.

The hereditary clause proved controversial, with critics including [[Benjamin Franklin]] and [[Thomas Jefferson]] arguing it would create an American aristocracy. Despite this, the society survived and its chapters spread across the original thirteen states.
