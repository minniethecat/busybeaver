## William Berkefield

William Berkefield (also referred to as Wm. Berkefield) was an American inventor and manufacturer known for developing "Berkefield's fossil meal composition," a highly effective inorganic insulation material for steam-pipe covering used in the late 19th century.

### Fossil Meal Insulation

Berkefield's fossil meal composition was recognized as the most powerful inorganic non-heat conductor for pipe covering at the time investigations were conducted. A thin coating — as little as 32/100 of an inch — dramatically reduced heat radiation from steam pipes. (005-019)

The American Architect provided comparative data: from 100 feet of uncovered two-inch steam pipe carrying 60 pounds of pressure, annual radiation loss was approximately $95.87 in coal value. The same pipe covered with Berkefield's composition at 32/1000-inch thickness lost only $5.89 worth of coal — a saving of over 93%. (005-019)

Comparative testing showed Berkefield's fossil meal to be competitive with carbonate of magnesia (magnesia covering), the subject of contemporary discussion in the journal. Through 25 mm of Berkefield's fossil meal, radiation was measured at 7.7 heat-units; through the same thickness of carbonate of magnesia, 6.7 heat-units. (006-001)
