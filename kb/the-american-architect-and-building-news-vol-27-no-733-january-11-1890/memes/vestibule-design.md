# Vestibule Design

A grand entrance hall or connecting passageway that functions as a transitional space between exterior and interior, or between major building sections, scaled in monumental exposition architecture to accommodate the circulation of mass publics.

## Definition

In 19th-century architectural practice, the vestibule was an entrance hall of generous proportions that mediated between the street or garden and the principal interior spaces. In exposition architecture, vestibules took on a connective function, linking adjacent palace buildings while providing orientation and dispersal space for crowds. Their dimensions reflected the scale of the events they served — at the 1889 Paris Exposition, vestibules connecting the Fine Arts and Industrial sections measured thirty metres in width by 115 metres in length. [005-002]

## Programmatic Functions

The exposition vestibule served multiple programs simultaneously. The vestibule of the Fine Arts palace at the 1889 Exposition contained the exhibition of sculpture, capitalizing on the generous dimensions and controlled lighting conditions of the space. Its counterpart in the Industrial section housed a large display of musical instruments. In this way, the vestibule transcended its traditional role as mere anteroom and became a major exhibition gallery in its own right. [005-002]

## In the Journal

> Each of these palaces is connected with the Industrial section of the foreign countries by a large vestibule thirty metres wide by 115 in length, one of which, that of the Fine Arts, contains the exhibition of sculpture, and the other contains a large part of the musical instruments. [005-002]
