# Slow-Burning Mill Construction

A method of industrial mill building developed in 19th-century New England that combines low initial cost with high resistance to fire destruction through careful arrangement of heavy timber structural members and elimination of concealed spaces. [011-001]

## Definition

Slow-burning mill construction is a building method that masses structural timber to minimize ignition surfaces, isolates iron from fire exposure, and compartmentalizes building sections to contain fire spread. The fundamental goal is to house manufacturing at the "minimum annual cost" by balancing construction economy with [[fire-protection|fire safety]]. [011-001]

## Historical Context

This construction method emerged from the practical experience of New England textile manufacturers, who continually refined their building techniques through trial and error. As noted by [[c-j-h-woodbury|C.J.H. Woodbury]] in a paper before the [[american-society-of-mechanical-engineers|American Society of Mechanical Engineers]], the development was "the outgrowth of a continual profiting by experience, adopting some features and modifying others," representing "the result of persistent efforts to adapt means to ends in the endeavor to reduce the cost of manufacture." [012-001]

No single individual is credited with inventing the system; rather, it evolved through the "concurrent action of the large number of minds engaged on the same problem." [012-001]

## Technical Description

### Structural Principles

The fundamental principles of slow-burning construction are fourfold: eliminate concealed spaces within the structure, reduce timber projections that ignite more readily than flat surfaces, shield iron components from direct fire exposure, and maximize compartmentalization between buildings, rooms, and stories. [011-001]

### Floor Construction

Mill floors are the most critical element. They are laid on Southern pine beams measuring 12 × 14 inches, spaced 8 to 10 feet apart between centers. At columns, beams rest on cast-iron caps. Support between columns uses cast-iron pintles, preferably with a Greek cross section for secure joining to timber beams. A cast-iron plate at the pintle top supports the column above. [011-002]

The floor planks are typically spruce, 3 to 4 inches thick, grooved on both edges and joined with hardwood splines. Planks span two bays in length, breaking joints at least every 4 feet. A top floor of 1¼ to 1½ inch hardwood is laid above, with fire resistance increased by [[plaster-work|plastering]] between layers or covering the underside with plaster on wire lathing. [011-002]

### Columns and Fire Protection

Timber columns are preferred over iron unless loads exceed timber capacity. The massed timber approach means columns "possessing in a very high degree features which offer resistance to the fire" are "weakened by the temperature only to a slight extent as they are slowly burned away under the exposure to a very severe fire." [011-008]

### Fire-Walls and Fire-Doors

Fire-walls divide mills into compartments, but their effectiveness depends on proper fire-doors. The best form uses two thicknesses of matched boards at right angles, nailed together, covered with tin, and bolted to hangers. Doors run on inclined tracks with fusible solder catches, so "they will be closed by the heat of a fire, if not closed by hand." [011-009]

### One-Story Mills

Where land permits, the one-story mill offers superior immunity from fire, lower cost per square foot, and manufacturing advantages in supervision and stock movement. "The hazard of a building increases very rapidly with its height, and to some extent with its area." [011-010]

## Significance

Slow-burning mill construction represented a practical engineering response to the severe fire risks of 19th-century industrial manufacturing. By combining economy with safety, it enabled the expansion of textile and other manufacturing industries. The floor system alone saves "about ten inches in height of wall, stairs, belting, steam-pipes, and all vertical connections reaching from floor to floor," producing significant total cost savings. [011-008]
