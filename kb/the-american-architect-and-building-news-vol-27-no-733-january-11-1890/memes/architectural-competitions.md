# Architectural Competitions

The practice of soliciting competitive architectural designs, operating at multiple scales: international competitions for major public buildings, local competitions for schools and institutions, and regular sketch-club competitions for professional development. [001-008]

## Definition

Architectural competitions are structured contests in which architects submit designs in response to a brief, with winning entries selected by jury or client. In the 1890s, competitions served three distinct functions: procurement (selecting designs for public and institutional buildings), professional development (training through monthly sketch-club contests), and publication (competitive drawings featured regularly in architectural journals as illustrations of current practice). [001-008]

## Historical Context

By 1890, architectural competitions were an established feature of professional practice on both sides of the Atlantic. The Paris École des Beaux-Arts system of design competitions had deeply influenced American architectural education. At the same time, the rise of illustrated architectural journals created a new channel for disseminating competition drawings, exposing practitioners to the latest design approaches from Europe and across America. [001-008]

## Technical Description

### Competition Drawings as Journal Content

*The American Architect and Building News* regularly illustrated "every important competition" with drawings from the competitive entries themselves. These illustrations, described as "a rule," provided readers with visual access to current design thinking across building types. The practice of publishing competition drawings functioned as a form of continuing education for the profession. [001-008]

### The Newark Architectural Sketch Club

A representative example of competition culture appears in the December 1889 founding of the Newark Architectural Sketch Club. Beyond its "usual monthly competitions," the club emphasized regular class-work throughout the year, covering constructional work, free-hand drawing, water-color work, plumbing, and architectural history. All courses were directed by club-member specialists, making the club a self-contained professional development organization. Officers included President W. Frank Bowers, Vice-President J.C. Swinnerton, Secretary H.A. Hickok, and Treasurer W.C. Hudson. [012-005]

### Schools Competition

School design competitions were a notable category. A documented entry "prepared in competition for schools near London" used a central hall system separating boys' and girls' wings, with specialized facilities in connecting buildings. This demonstrates the competition format's role in advancing school building typology. [010-020]

### Exhibition Palace Design

The design of international exhibition palaces was frequently determined through competition. The 1889 Paris Exposition's Palaces of the Liberal and Fine Arts, for example, represented competitive design at the highest level, producing buildings that "at once imposed themselves upon the public in the most forcible manner" through "simplicity, purity, and harmony." [005-001]

## Significance

Architectural competitions functioned as a distributed system for design innovation, professional training, and public engagement with architecture. The journal's role in publishing competition drawings democratized access to design knowledge, allowing provincial practitioners to study the work of leading firms. The sketch-club competition model provided structured career development at a time when formal architectural education was still limited.

## In the Journal

> Every important competition is regularly illustrated from the competitive drawings themselves, which are, as a rule, the most interesting exhibits of the progress of architecture. [001-008]

> In addition to the usual monthly competitions, to make a special feature of regular class-work throughout the year, this will consist of courses in constructional work, free-hand drawing, water-color work, plumbing, architectural history, etc. [012-005]
