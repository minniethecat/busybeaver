# American Society of Mechanical Engineers

The American Society of Mechanical Engineers ([[ASME]]) is a professional society founded in [[1880]] to advance the practice of [[mechanical engineering]] in the [[United States]].

By [[1890]], it had become a leading forum for the presentation of technical papers on [[mill construction]], [[fire protection]], and industrial technology (012-002).

## Role

ASME provided a platform where practicing engineers could share research and practical experience. The society's proceedings covered a broad range of industrial topics relevant to late-nineteenth-century American manufacturing and construction.

Unlike older engineering institutions focused on civil works, ASME represented the newer mechanical engineering discipline — encompassing steam power, factory design, [[slow-burning-mill-construction|mill construction]], and industrial safety.

## Contributors

Among the engineers presenting before ASME was [[C.J.H. Woodbury]] of [[Boston]], who contributed papers on [[slow-burning-mill-construction|mill construction]] development and structural methods for heavy timber columns (012-002).

[[c-j-h-woodbury|Woodbury]]'s presentation, abstracted in the January 11, 1890 issue of the *American Architect*, addressed the evolution of slow-burning [[slow-burning-mill-construction|mill construction]] — a topic of great practical importance given the frequent catastrophic mill fires of the era.

## Significance

ASME exemplifies the professionalization of American engineering in the late nineteenth century. By creating a forum for systematic knowledge exchange, it helped transform engineering from craft tradition to applied science.
