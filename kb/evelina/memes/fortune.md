# Fortune

**Fortune** — encompassing wealth, inheritance, and financial standing — is a central force in *Evelina* (1778), driving both plot and character motivations. In the 18th-century social world Burney depicts, fortune determines marriageability, social rank, and the power dynamics between characters.

## Fortune as Social Determinant

Evelina Belmont's legal status as "heiress to two large fortunes" paradoxically leaves her with nothing, as her father Sir John Belmont has denied her legitimacy (004-007). Mr. Villars, her guardian, notes she is "entitled as she is to lawfully inherit his fortune and estate" yet cannot claim either (004-005). This contradiction — being an heiress in law but a nobody in practice — drives much of the novel's social tension.

The narrative explicitly frames fortune as destiny: "Fortune alone has hitherto been sparing of her gifts; and she, too, now opens the way" (027-008). Mr. Villars remarks that recovering Evelina's inheritance would secure "the fortune and rank of life to which she is lawfully entitled" (027-011).

## Marriage and Fortune

Fortune and marriage are inextricably linked throughout the novel. Sir John Belmont's original crime was burning his marriage certificate when "disappointed of the fortune he expected" from the Duvals (002-014). Later, Sir Clement Willoughby cynically weaponizes the language of fortune in pursuit of Evelina: "Fortune has now kindly favoured me with one," he declares while seizing her hand (021-154).

## Madame Duval and Economic Motives

Madame Duval's interventions are largely driven by fortune. She advocates a lawsuit to "prove the validity of his marriage with Miss Evelyn; the necessary consequence of which proof will be, securing his fortune and estate" (027-006). Her motives blend family loyalty with financial calculation.

## Villars's Philosophy

Mr. Villars represents a counterpoint to fortune-obsession. He wished Evelina "to possess a very moderate fortune" and sought "to contract her views to something within it" (004-003). His own plan was "to adopt her the heiress of my small fortune, and to bestow her upon some worthy man" (028-009) — valuing moral worth over wealth. Yet even he acknowledges the tension: "I was in a perpetual conflict, between the desire that she should have justice done her, and the apprehension that, while I improved her fortune, I should endanger her mind" (028-009).
