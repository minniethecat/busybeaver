# Pantheon

The Pantheon on Oxford Street was one of the grandest [[assembly-rooms|assembly]] rooms of Georgian [[london|London]]. In [[Fanny Burney]]'s *[[evelina|Evelina]]*, it is the final public entertainment the heroine attends before leaving the capital — and its unusual [[reputation|character]], part chapel and part [[assembly-rooms|ballroom]], provides a fitting symbolic threshold between her [[london|London]] adventures and the resolution that awaits at [[Bristol]].

## The Venue

Opened in 1772, the Pantheon was designed by James Wyatt as a winter alternative to [[Ranelagh]]. Its vast domed interior, inspired by Hagia Sophia in Istanbul, as[[fashion|ton]]ished visitors. Where [[ranelagh|Ranelagh]]'s rotunda encouraged promenading sociability, the Pantheon's architecture — with its towering dome and niches for statuary — struck a more solemn note.

## Evelina's Visit

[[evelina|Evelina]] attends the Pantheon on the last night of her [[london|London]] stay. Her description captures the building's effect on a first-time visitor:

> I was extremely struck with the beauty of the building, which greatly surpassed whatever I could have expected or imagined. Yet it has more the appearance of a chapel than of a place of diversion; and, though I was quite charmed with the magnificence of the room, I felt that I could not be as gay and thoughtless there as at Ranelagh.

— (024-001)

This quality of "awe and solemnity" makes the Pantheon an apt setting for [[evelina|Evelina]]'s departure from the gaieties of [[london|London]]: it is a place that elevates rather than merely entertains.

## Sir Clement and the Nobleman

Two encounters at the Pantheon carry weight in the novel. [[Sir Clement Willoughby]] interrogates [[evelina|Evelina]] and Maria [[captain-mirvan|Mirvan]] about their impressions of the venue — his [[reputation|character]]istic probing for information — and a forward nobleman makes advances that [[evelina|Evelina]] must repel. [[Rev. Villars]], learning of this latter incident, dismisses the nobleman as too openly licentious to be a genuine threat: "a man who appears so openly licentious... is one who, to a mind such as my [[evelina|Evelina]]'s, can never be seen but with the disgust which his [[breeding|manners]] ought to excite" (030-001).

## Significance

The Pantheon marks the end of [[evelina|Evelina]]'s [[london|London]] education. After this evening, the novel's geography shifts decisively toward [[Bristol]] and the resolution of [[evelina|Evelina]]'s identity. The building's ambiguous [[reputation|character]] — sacred architecture in the service of secular entertainment — mirrors [[evelina|Evelina]]'s own position: a virtuous young woman navigating spaces designed for display and seduction.
