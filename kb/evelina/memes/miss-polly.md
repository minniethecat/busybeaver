# Miss Polly

Miss Polly Branghton is the younger daughter of Mr. Branghton, the Holborn silversmith whose household becomes Evelina's reluctant London residence. Polly is distinguished from her elder sister Biddy by her romantic preoccupations: she considers Mr. Smith, the lodger on the first floor, her "lover" and is consumed by the desire to secure his affections. Through Polly, Burney explores the limited romantic imagination of a tradesman's daughter whose aspirations to gentility are channelled entirely through the pursuit of a suitable — or seemingly suitable — husband.

## Identity

- **Full Name**: Polly Branghton
- **Title/Rank**: Miss
- **Family**: Mr. Branghton (father), Miss Biddy Branghton (sister), Tom Branghton (brother)
- **Residence**: Snow Hill, Holborn, London

## Role in the Novel

Miss Polly appears during Evelina's stay with the Branghtons (Letters XL–LV). She is immediately characterised by her romantic rivalry with her sister. While Biddy has "taken it into her head to have a liking for Mr. Smith, as lodges on the first floor" (040-053), Polly considers Mr. Smith her own suitor. She dismisses her sister's chances with confidence born of romantic self-deception: "he'll never have her, for he's quite a fine gentleman" (040-053).

Polly's descriptions of Mr. Smith reveal both her infatuation and the social ambition that drives it. She boasts that his room is "a very pretty one, and only up one pair of stairs, and nicely furnished, and everything" (040-037), and that "he's quite like one of the quality, and dresses as fine, and goes to balls and dances, and everything, quite in taste; and besides, Miss, he keeps a foot-boy of his own too" (042-018). The insistent "and everything" that punctuates her speech is a class marker — the verbal equivalent of aspiring to describe a world she cannot quite name.

Her romantic hopes are threatened when Mr. Brown reports that Mr. Smith "said he'd never marry as long as he lived, for he'd no opinion of matrimony" (040-053). Burney withholds the resolution of this tension; by the time the novel concludes its London section, Polly's romantic fate remains uncertain, left as one of the loose ends that the novel's tidy comic resolution cannot — or chooses not to — resolve.

## Character Traits

Polly is defined by her naivety and her romanticism. Unlike her sister, who is sharp and quarrelsome, Polly is softer, more eager to please, and more transparent in her desires. Her competition with Biddy for Mr. Smith's attention is presented not as malice but as the inevitable consequence of two young women with limited prospects pursuing the same scarce resource: an eligible man.

She is also, like all the Branghtons, a figure of social comedy. Her language — the repeated "and everything," the breathless catalogues of Mr. Smith's possessions — marks her as belonging to a class that can imitate gentility but not inhabit it. Yet Burney's satire is gentler with Polly than with Biddy: where Biddy's sharpness invites mockery, Polly's hopefulness invites something closer to sympathy.
