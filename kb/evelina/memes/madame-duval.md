# Madame Duval

Madame Duval is Evelina's long-estranged French grandmother and a principal source of conflict in [[Fanny Burney]]'s *[[Evelina]]*. Formerly a tavern waiting-girl who married above her station, she embodies vulgarity, ill-temper, and the corrupting effects of unearned wealth. Her sudden reappearance in [[Evelina]]'s life threatens to uproot the young heroine from the safety of her guardian [[Rev. Villars]] and expose her to a world governed by selfish impulse rather than [[propriety]].

## Origins and Character

Madame Duval's origins are humble: she was "a waiting-girl at a tavern" when she captivated Mr. Evelyn, Evelina's grandfather, with her beauty. Their disastrous marriage drove Mr. Evelyn to abandon England for France, where "shame and repentance" followed him. After his death two years later, Madame Duval married Monsieur Duval and remained in Paris, becoming "at once uneducated and unprincipled; ungentle in temper, and unamiable in her manners."

> She is at once uneducated and unprincipled; ungentle in temper, and unamiable in her manners. I have long known that she has persuaded herself to harbour an aversion for me — Unhappy woman! I can only regard her as an object of pity!
>
> (001-001)

## Relationship with Lady Belmont

Madame Duval's treatment of her daughter, Lady Belmont, is the darkest chapter of her history. She attempted to force a marriage between Lady Belmont and one of Monsieur Duval's nephews. When Lady Belmont refused, Madame Duval "treated her with the grossest unkindness, and threatened her with poverty and ruin." Lady Belmont's subsequent secret marriage and early death left Madame Duval seized with "the agonies of grief and remorse"—though this repentance proved temporary.

## Arrival in England

Madame Duval's return to England, after years of silence, throws the Mirvan household into disarray. She arrives attended by the Frenchman Monsieur Du Bois and immediately clashes with [[Captain Mirvan]], whose open contempt for all things French ignites a running war of insults. Their conflict dominates the social gatherings that follow, with [[Sir Clement Willoughby]] skillfully manipulating both sides for his own amusement.

> Finding her efforts to render the evening agreeable were fruitless, Mrs. Mirvan proposed a party to [[Ranelagh]]. Madame Duval joyfully consented to it; and the Captain though he railed against the dissipation of the women, did not oppose it.
>
> (016-001)

## Social Comedy and Vulgarity

Madame Duval provides much of the novel's broad comedy through her malapropisms, vanity, and complete ignorance of English social codes. At [[Ranelagh]], she declares it "a shocking thing to see ladies come to so genteel a place with hats on" and frequently invokes the superior fashion of Paris—only to reveal her own provincialism. Her inability to govern her temper makes her an easy target for Captain Mirvan's cruel practical jokes, culminating in the notorious incident where she is deliberately thrown into a mud puddle.

## Significance

Madame Duval serves as a dark mirror to Evelina's anxieties about birth, legitimacy, and social belonging. She represents what Evelina might become without proper guidance: coarse, unprincipled, and isolated from respectable society. Her presence in the novel tests the limits of filial duty — [[Rev. Villars]] counsels Evelina to treat her grandmother with respect regardless of her conduct, affirming that "the failure of duty on her part, can by no means justify any neglect on yours."

> Conduct yourself towards her with all the respect and deference due to so near a relation, remembering always, that the failure of duty on her part, can by no means justify any neglect on yours.
>
> (015-001)
