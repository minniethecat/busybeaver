# Propriety

Propriety — the code of socially acceptable behaviour — is one of the most contested concepts in [[Fanny Burney]]'s *[[Evelina]]*. It functions simultaneously as a genuine moral safeguard, protecting young women like [[Evelina]] from ruin, and as an instrument of class policing, wielded by the fashionable to exclude the unfashionable. The novel's treatment of propriety is thus fundamentally ambivalent: it affirms the code's necessity while exposing its vulnerabilities to manipulation and hypocrisy.

## The Double Edge of Propriety

The novel demonstrates that propriety is an unreliable guide to character. [[Lord Orville]] observes every form of propriety, yet his behaviour springs from genuine respect rather than mere rule-following. [[Sir Clement Willoughby]] also obeys the forms — his address is always polite, his manners always correct — yet uses them to mask predatory intentions. The code of propriety, the novel suggests, is only as good as the [[conduct]] that animates it.

## Evelina's Struggle

For [[Evelina]], propriety is a burden as well as a shield. Her constant anxiety about doing the right thing — refusing the wrong partner at an [[assembly]], managing the competing claims of [[Madame Duval]] and [[Rev. Villars]], navigating the ambiguous attentions of [[Sir Clement Willoughby]] — reflects the extraordinary pressure placed on young women in a society where a single misstep could mean social death. Her experience reveals that propriety, while ostensibly gender-neutral, imposes far heavier demands on women than on men.

## The Ranelagh Test

At [[Ranelagh]], propriety breaks down under real-world pressure. When the coach is soaked and broken, when [[Sir Clement Willoughby]] physically seizes [[Evelina]] under cover of assistance, the polite code that should govern behaviour collapses into chaos. The scene reveals propriety's fragility: it functions only when everyone agrees to play by the same rules, and [[Captain Mirvan]] and [[Madame Duval]] have no such agreement.

## Propriety as Moral Education

[[Rev. Villars]], writing from [[Berry Hill]], treats propriety not as an end in itself but as a tool for moral formation. His letters teach [[Evelina]] that the forms of politeness are valuable precisely because they create the conditions in which genuine virtue — [[conduct]] — can develop and be recognized. This is the novel's most sophisticated argument: that propriety, properly understood, is not the enemy of authenticity but its necessary framework.

> Be careful, therefore, that no remissness of attention, no indifference of obliging, make known to her the independence I assure you of.
