# Delicacy

Delicacy is one of the most frequently invoked terms in [[Fanny Burney]]'s moral vocabulary. It denotes a quality of refined perception — the capacity to feel rightly, to be moved by beauty and goodness, and to recoil from what is coarse or cruel. In *Evelina*, it is the attribute that distinguishes the heroine from nearly everyone around her.

## A Quality of Mind

Delicacy is not merely politeness; it is a quality of mind and feeling. [[Rev. Villars]] praises Evelina's "delicacy of sentiment" as something innate — a natural moral compass that, properly cultivated, will guide her through a world of ambiguous appearances. When she is disgusted by [[Captain Mirvan]]'s cruelty or discomforted by [[Sir Clement Willoughby]]'s forwardness, it is her delicacy that sounds the alarm.

## Delicacy and Gender

The novel distinguishes between feminine and masculine delicacy. Evelina's delicacy makes her vulnerable — she is "too delicate" to repel unwanted advances forcefully, and her sensitivity to social awkwardness causes her genuine distress. [[Lord Orville]]'s delicacy, by contrast, is an active virtue: it makes him attentive to others' feelings, quick to protect, and slow to judge. His delicacy is power harnessed by morality; hers is perception that requires protection.

## The Fragility of Delicacy

Rev. Villars's warning that "nothing is so delicate as the reputation of a woman" (009-002) employs the word in both its senses — fragile and morally refined. The pun encapsulates the novel's central anxiety: that the very quality which makes Evelina admirable also makes her vulnerable to a world that does not share it.

## Delicacy and [[Conduct]]

Where [[conduct]] is the external practice of moral principles, delicacy is the internal sensitivity that makes right conduct possible. A person may follow the rules of propriety without delicacy — like [[Madame Duval]], whose adherence to fashion is mechanical — but such conduct is hollow. Delicacy is what gives moral rules their life.
