# Sir Clement Willoughby

Sir Clement Willoughby is the most artful antagonist in [[Fanny Burney]]'s *[[Evelina]]* — a baronet whose polished manners and quick wit conceal a predatory nature. Unlike the boorish [[Captain Mirvan]] or the vulgar [[Madame Duval]], Sir Clement operates through charm and manipulation, making him a far more dangerous figure in [[Evelina]]'s social world. He serves as the decisive foil to [[Lord Orville]]: where Orville's politeness springs from genuine respect, Sir Clement's is a weapon of calculated self-interest.

## Character and Methods

Sir Clement's defining trait is his ability to read and exploit social situations. He "very successfully studies all the humours" of those around him, adapting his manner to win favour. His pursuit of [[Evelina]] is relentless but never overtly violent — he uses flattery, manufactured emergencies, and social pressure rather than force. He is, as [[Rev. Villars]] observes, a man whose "conversation and boldness are extremely disgustful."

> He affects to enter the house with the freedom of an old acquaintance; and this very easiness, which, to me, is astonishing, is what most particularly recommends him to the Captain. Indeed, he seems very successfully to study all the humours of that gentleman.
>
> (017-001)

## The Ridotto Incident

Sir Clement's first significant appearance is at the [[ridotto]], where his forward behaviour toward [[Evelina]] establishes the pattern of his pursuit. When she finds herself unattended, he seizes the opportunity to press his attentions upon her, forcing her to borrow [[Lord Orville]]'s name as protection — an act of impropriety that mortifies her and deepens her embarrassment.

## Manipulator of Conflict

At the Mirvans', Sir Clement quickly identifies the fault line between [[Captain Mirvan]] and [[Madame Duval]] and exploits it masterfully. He "exerted all his powers of argument and of ridicule, to second and strengthen whatever was advanced by the Captain: for he had the sagacity to discover, that he could take no method so effectual for making the master of the house his friend, as to make Madame Duval his enemy."

> He managed the argument so skilfully, at once provoking Madame Duval, and delighting the Captain, that I could not forbear admiring his address, though I condemned his subtlety.
>
> (016-002)

## The Ranelagh Episode

At [[Ranelagh]], Sir Clement's predatory instincts surface unmistakably. When the Mirvan party's coach breaks down in the rain, he physically seizes [[Evelina]] — "I was lifted suddenly from it by Sir Clement Willoughby, who begged permission to assist me, though he did not wait to have it granted, but carried me in his arms back to Ranelagh." This moment of physical appropriation, disguised as gallantry, reveals the coercive impulse beneath his polished surface.

## Role as Foil

| Quality | [[Lord Orville]] | Sir Clement Willoughby |
|---------|-------------|------------------------|
| Politeness | Genuine respect | Calculated manipulation |
| Pursuit of Evelina | Restrained, honourable | Aggressive, self-serving |
| Social intelligence | Used to protect others | Used to exploit others |
| Treatment of inferiors | Courteous | Contemptuous when useful |
| Response to rejection | Graceful withdrawal | Redoubled scheming |

## Wit and Social Dexterity

Sir Clement's wit, unlike Captain Mirvan's brute sarcasm, is agile and adaptable. At the theatre, he deftly manages the social complexities of the box; at dinner, he knows precisely when to provoke [[Madame Duval]] and when to appear gallant. His intelligence makes him entertaining company even as his character renders him untrustworthy — a tension Burney exploits to critique the gap between polished manners and genuine [[conduct]].
