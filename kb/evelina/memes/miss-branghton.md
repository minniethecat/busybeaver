# Miss Branghton

Miss Biddy Branghton is the eldest daughter of the Branghton family, whose pride, ill-temper, and jealousy make her one of the most vividly drawn members of Evelina's London circle. Unlike her giddy younger sister Polly, Biddy possesses a sharpness that shades into malice, making her an active antagonist rather than a merely foolish one.

## Identity

- **Full Name**: Biddy Branghton (referred to in narrative as Miss Branghton)
- **Title/Rank**: Miss
- **Family**: daughter of Mr. Branghton; sister of Tom Branghton and Miss Polly Branghton; great-niece of Madame Duval
- **Residence**: Snow Hill, Holborn, London

## Role in the Novel

Miss Branghton is introduced in Evelina's first description of the Branghton family: she "is by no means ugly; but looks proud, ill-tempered, and conceited. She hates the city, though without knowing why; for it is easy to discover she has lived nowhere else" (017-013). This capsule judgment — proud without cause, contemptuous without knowledge — defines her character for the remainder of the novel.

Her jealousy of Evelina is immediate and visceral. At their first meeting, she examines Evelina's dress with open hostility, and throughout the London episodes she seizes every opportunity to diminish her cousin. When Evelina reveals she has never been to an opera, Miss Branghton exclaims: "Miss will think us very vulgar to live in London, and never have been to an opera; but it's no fault of mine, I assure you, Miss, for my papa says it's all nonsense" (017-032). The disclaimer — "it's no fault of mine" — simultaneously excuses herself and indicts her father, a characteristic manoeuvre.

At the theatre, Miss Branghton's behaviour reaches its nadir. She is "abrupt" (021-013), loud, and entirely without the self-consciousness that might restrain a better-bred young woman. She insists on sitting in the pit and declares that "my gown was not good enough for the pit" is a slight against her — a misunderstanding that reveals her class anxiety and her determination to take offence (021-021).

Her romantic situation is the source of much of her bitterness. Miss Polly reveals that Biddy "has taken it into her head to have a liking for Mr. Smith, as lodges on the first floor; but, Lord, he'll never have her, for he's quite a fine gentleman; and besides, Mr. Brown heard him say one day, that he'd never marry as long as he lived, for he'd no opinion of matrimony" (040-053). That her younger sister has a suitor while she does not is a running grievance, and her unrequited passion for the unattainable Mr. Smith fuels her ill-temper toward everyone around her.

## Character Traits

Miss Branghton's dominant trait is pride — but a pride entirely without foundation. She "hates the city, though without knowing why" (017-013), aspiring to a gentility she cannot define and would not recognise if she achieved it. Her pride manifests as touchiness: she is perpetually on the lookout for slights, perpetually ready to take offence where none is intended.

Her ill-temper is the natural consequence of frustrated pride. Denied the social elevation she craves, denied the suitor she desires, denied even the consolation of being her father's favourite (that role belongs to Tom), she lashes out at the nearest targets — usually Evelina or her sister Polly.

Her ignorance is comprehensive but not innocent. Unlike Polly, whose foolishness has a certain sweetness, Biddy's ignorance is weaponised — she uses it to wound. Her comment at the theatre, "I'm sure I thought people went to the play to see it" (021-043), is both genuinely ignorant and strategically deployed to mock Evelina's more refined expectations.

## Key Relationships

### With Evelina

Miss Branghton resents Evelina from their first meeting. Evelina's gentility — her dress, her manners, her education at Berry Hill — is a standing reproach to everything Biddy lacks. Her hostility takes the form of small slights and loud complaints, the weapons of the powerless. She is, in the novel's scheme, what Evelina might have become had she been raised in Snow Hill rather than Berry Hill — a cautionary tale about the importance of environment in forming character.

### With Miss Polly Branghton

The rivalry between the Branghton sisters is one of the novel's most entertaining subplots. Biddy envies Polly's suitor, Mr. Smith, and resents being the elder sister without a lover. Polly, for her part, is not above goading her sister: "I know it's all out of spite. You must know, Miss, it makes her as mad as can be that I should have a lover before her" (040-053). Their quarrels expose the petty dynamics of a family without education or refinement.

### With Tom Branghton

Biddy's relationship with her brother Tom is one of mutual irritation. He teases her about Mr. Smith, and she scolds him for his vulgarity. They are allies in nothing, united only by their shared descent into the London middling class.

### With Mr. Smith

Biddy's "liking" for Mr. Smith is the secret wound that explains much of her behaviour. She cannot have him — he prefers Polly, and in any case has declared against matrimony — and the frustration of this desire poisons her relations with everyone, especially her sister.
