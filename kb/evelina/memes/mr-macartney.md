# Mr. Macartney

Mr. Macartney is the central figure of the novel's principal subplot: a young Scotsman of gentle birth reduced to dire poverty, whose story intersects with Evelina's through acts of charity that prove unexpectedly consequential. His narrative — involving a pistol, a lost love, and a secret parentage — mirrors Evelina's own in its concern with identity and social belonging.

## Identity

- **Full Name**: Mr. Macartney (first name never given)
- **Title/Rank**: Mr., of gentle birth
- **Residence**: lodgings at the Branghtons' house, Snow Hill, London
- **Role**: impoverished gentleman; Evelina's beneficiary

## Role in the Novel

Macartney first appears as a spectral figure in the Branghton household: "the first object I saw was Mr. Macartney, with a book in his hand, seated in the same corner where I saw him last; but his looks were still more wretched than before, his face yet thinner, and his eyes sunk almost hollow into his head" (041-002). Evelina's instinctive compassion — she secretly gives him money — initiates a chain of revelations that culminates in the discovery that Macartney is, in fact, her half-brother, the son of Sir John Belmont by a different mother.

## Character Traits

Macartney is defined by his acute sensibility — a capacity for deep feeling that is both his virtue and his torment. His poverty shames him; his lost love devastates him; and his near-suicidal despair at being unable to repay his debts makes him, briefly, a figure of Gothic intensity. Yet he is fundamentally decent, and his eventual reunion with his beloved — the young lady he saw at the Bristol assembly — provides one of the novel's few uncomplicated happy endings.

## Key Relationships

### With Evelina
Evelina's charity to Macartney — giving him money when she has little to spare — is the most morally significant action she takes in the novel. It leads directly to the discovery of their shared parentage and the resolution of Evelina's identity.

### With Sir John Belmont
Macartney is revealed to be Sir John Belmont's son, making him Evelina's half-brother — a connection that transforms the novel's final act.
