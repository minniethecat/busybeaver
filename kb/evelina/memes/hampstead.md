# Hampstead

A village on the northern outskirts of 18th-century [[london|London]], known for its [[assembly-rooms|assembly]] rooms and public entertainments. [[evelina|Evelina]] attends a ball at the long room at Hampstead, and the location is referenced in discussions of [[london|London]]'s social venues.

## Overview

- **Type**: village / entertainment venue
- **Location**: North of [[london|London]]
- **Period**: 18th century

## Description in the Novel

The novel mentions two specific venues at Hampstead. A "[[assembly|Hampstead assembly]]" is first proposed by Mr. Smith, who calls "to offer me a ticket for the next [[assembly|Hampstead assembly]]" (043-003). Mr. Smith later "called to acquaint us that the [[assembly|Hampstead assembly]] was to be held that evening" (050-009). Young Brangh[[fashion|ton]] asks [[evelina|Evelina]] "if I had ever been to George's at Hampstead," a more plebeian venue that Mr. Smith dismisses contemptuously — "how came you to think the young lady would like to go to such a low place as George's?" (044-028, 044-031). The ball [[evelina|Evelina]] ultimately attends is held at "the long room at Hampstead" (050-049).

## Significance to the Plot

The [[assembly|Hampstead assembly]] represents a step down in social status from the events [[evelina|Evelina]] attended in the earlier [[london|London]] season, reflecting the Brangh[[fashion|ton]] circle's lower [[rank|social rank]]. The distinction between "George's at Hampstead" and the "long room" ball maps onto the [[social-class|class]] distinctions the novel carefully traces: Mr. Smith's eagerness to distance himself from Brangh[[fashion|ton]]'s plebeian taste mirrors [[madame-duval|Madame Duval]]'s earlier pretensions to [[paris|Paris]]ian elegance (044-031).

## Social Context

Hampstead in the novel represents the intermediate tier of [[london|London]] entertainment — neither the fashionable brilliance of [[ranelagh|Ranelagh]] and [[pantheon|the Pantheon]] nor the disreputable venues [[evelina|Evelina]] is sometimes exposed to. The quarrel over George's versus the "long room" ball encapsulates the novel's persistent interest in the gradations of [[rank|social rank]], in which even a village [[assembly-rooms|assembly]] could be parsed for its [[social-class|class]] implications.
