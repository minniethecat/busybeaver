# Monsieur Du Bois

Monsieur Du Bois is Madame Duval's French companion — a figure whose inability to speak English and perpetual proximity to Madame Duval make him the target of Captain Mirvan's Francophobic mockery and the victim of a series of comic misfortunes. Though a minor character, Du Bois serves an important structural function: his foreignness provides a foil that highlights English provincial hostility to French manners, and his constant attendance on Madame Duval fuels the novel's running joke about the ambiguity of their relationship.

## Identity

- **Full Name**: not given (referred to only as Monsieur Du Bois)
- **Title/Rank**: Monsieur
- **Family**: none mentioned
- **Residence**: London; lodges with the Branghtons in High Holborn

## Role in the Novel

Monsieur Du Bois first appears when Madame Duval arrives in London, introducing him as her French companion: "She was attended by a French gentleman, whom she introduced by the name of Monsieur Du Bois" (016-004). Captain Mirvan's immediate hostility — "Pray who asked you to bring that there spark with you?" (016-004) — establishes the dynamic that will define Du Bois's presence throughout the novel.

Du Bois speaks no English and understands it "so imperfectly, that he might possibly imagine he had received a compliment" when the Captain insults him (016-007). This linguistic barrier renders him perpetually vulnerable — unable to defend himself or even to recognise when he is being mocked. His sole recorded utterance in English is limited to the phrase "plaît-il, Monsieur?" (025-018), underscoring the asymmetry of his position: he can be attacked but not reply.

At Ranelagh, Du Bois's attempt at gallantry — carrying Madame Duval over a muddy patch — produces the novel's most memorable comic disaster: "his foot slipt … down we both came, together, all in the mud" (016-075). Both are soaked and filthy, their clothing ruined. The Captain's delight is unrestrained: "He really shouted with pleasure; and, shaking Monsieur Du Bois strenuously by the hand, wished him joy of having *touched English ground*" (016-076). Du Bois's response — "wet, shivering, and disconsolate, had crept to the fire" (016-074) — is an image of abject misery rendered comic by its context.

The Ranelagh disaster becomes a running joke that the Captain revives at every opportunity. At a later gathering, he mockingly quotes the incident, and Du Bois, realising he is being ridiculed, makes a formal defence in French "with great solemnity": he hoped the company would acknowledge "he did not come from a nation of brutes" and that his fall was "owing to a sudden but violent push" — an accusation directed at the Captain — "which, he was shocked to say, some malevolent person, with a design to his injury, must certainly have given him" (019-021).

The Captain's most direct assault occurs during a coach journey, when he "very roughly" pulls Du Bois from his seat beside the ladies: "Look'ee, Monseer, this here may be a French fashion for aught I know — but give and take is fair in all nations; and so now, d'ye see, I'll make bold to show you an English one" (025-009). Du Bois instantly puts his hand upon his sword (025-011), but Madame Duval intervenes in French: "Eh, laissez-le, mon ami, ne le corrigez pas; c'est une villaine bête qui n'en vaut pas la peine" (025-013). Du Bois eventually "very wisely gave up the point, and took leave" (025-022) — a prudent retreat that nonetheless underscores his powerlessness.

## Character Traits

Du Bois's defining characteristic is his politeness, which — rendered impotent by his language barrier — becomes almost pathos. He "made a profound bow" at his introduction (016-007), but the gesture is wasted on the hostile Captain. His attempts at gallantry consistently backfire: the mud incident transforms a chivalrous impulse into public humiliation. His French formality, expressed through bows, hand-kissing, and elaborate speeches, appears absurd in the English context — not because it is inherently ridiculous, but because it is received by an audience determined to find it so.

There is a genuine dignity in Du Bois's formal self-defence at the Mirvans', where he argues with "great solemnity" that a Frenchman could not "wilfully offend any lady" (019-021). The length and formality of the speech — rendered in English by Evelina, not in direct French dialogue — suggests Burney wanted readers to register Du Bois as a man of sensibility trapped in a comic frame.

### The Ambiguity of His Role

His relationship with Madame Duval is a source of speculation and innuendo. Captain Mirvan calls him her "beau" (033-024), and Evelina herself notes: "it is strange that they should be so constantly together, though I believe I should not have taken notice of it, but that Captain Mirvan is perpetually rallying me upon my *grandmama's beau*" (025-008). At Howard Grove, Evelina is shocked to find Du Bois in Madame Duval's bedchamber at breakfast — a moment that reveals her innocence of "foreign customs" (017-004) as much as any impropriety. The nature of their attachment remains deliberately ambiguous: companion, suitor, or simply fellow French expatriate clinging to familiar company in a hostile city.

## Key Relationships

### With Madame Duval

Du Bois is inseparable from Madame Duval, attending her at every social occasion. He lodges with the Branghtons at her arrangement and accompanies her on all her visits. When she catches a cold after Ranelagh, he too "is so hoarse, he can hardly speak" (017-006) — their ailments mirroring their social inseparability. When Madame Duval is distressed — after the Captain's pranks, after social slights — Du Bois is invariably present, though his language barrier limits his effectiveness as an ally. Their dynamic is one of mutual dependence: she provides his social entrée into English society, while he provides the gallant attendance that buttresses her pretensions to French sophistication.

### With Captain Mirvan

The Captain's treatment of Du Bois is unrelenting mockery disguised as heartiness. He shakes Du Bois's hand "strenuously," congratulates him on misfortunes, and uses him as a proxy in his war against all things French. The Captain calls him "Monseer" (025-009) — a deliberately mangled form of address that denies Du Bois even the dignity of his title. Du Bois's incomprehension of English provides the Captain with a safely unresisting target; when Du Bois does respond — reaching for his sword, delivering his solemn French defence — the Captain merely redoubles his mockery.

### With Evelina

Du Bois makes little direct impression on Evelina, who regards him with the same mixture of embarrassment and sympathy she extends to her grandmother's entourage. She is "surprised" to find him in Madame Duval's bedchamber (017-004) and notes with some discomfort the Captain's "perpetually rallying" about the relationship (025-008). Her treatment of Du Bois is consistently polite but distant — she neither joins in the mockery nor intervenes to stop it.

## Narrative Function

Like Madame Duval herself, Du Bois belongs to the novel's comic register. His function is to provide a safely ridiculous figure of French manners — polite but ineffectual, gallant but accident-prone — against which English bluntness (and brutality) can be measured. Yet his presence also complicates the novel's treatment of national character. The Captain's treatment of Du Bois is funny, but it is also uncomfortable: an innocent man, unable even to understand the insults directed at him, is tormented for the crime of being foreign. When Du Bois delivers his lengthy, dignified defence in French — a speech Evelina renders in careful English — Burney invites the reader to see him as something more than a comic butt. He is, in his way, a figure of pathos: a man whose only failing is to be French in a nation at war with France, and whose politeness is no defence against brute hostility.
