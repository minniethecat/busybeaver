# Mrs. Beaumont

The Honourable Mrs. Beaumont is the hostess of Clifton Hill, the fashionable Bristol residence where the novel's climactic social encounters unfold. As a relation of Lord Orville, she commands the respect of the fashionable world and presides over a circle that includes Lord Merton, Lady Louisa Larpent, Mr. Lovel, Lord Orville, and — through Mrs. Selwyn's contrivance — Evelina herself. More than a mere backdrop, Mrs. Beaumont embodies a particular type of aristocratic woman: gracious, neutral, and possessed of the quiet authority that comes from being entirely secure in her station.

## Identity

- **Full Name**: not given (referred to as the Honourable Mrs. Beaumont)
- **Title/Rank**: The Honourable Mrs. — indicating connection to the peerage
- **Family**: relation of Lord Orville
- **Residence**: Clifton Hill, near Bristol Hotwells — "beautifully situated" (063-005)

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Beaumont is first mentioned as the destination of Lord Orville's journey to Bristol: "They are to be at the Honourable Mrs. Beaumont's. She is a relation of my Lord's, and has a very fine house upon Clifton Hill" (062-064). This intelligence throws Evelina into emotional turmoil: "It will be impossible for me to avoid seeing him, as Mrs. Selwyn is very well acquainted with Mrs. Beaumont" (062-065). Mrs. Beaumont's house thus becomes, even before Evelina sets foot in it, the stage for the novel's romantic climax. Evelina notes with relief that she has "an escape in not being under the same roof with him, for Mrs. Beaumont invited us to her house immediately upon our arrival; but the inconvenience of being so distant from the pump-room made Mrs. Selwyn decline her civility" (062-065) — a small arrangement that preserves the delicate social distance on which the plot depends.

The house itself is described as "beautifully situated" on Clifton Hill (063-005). Evelina's first visit begins auspiciously: "I was happily relieved from my perturbation, when I saw Mrs. Beaumont was alone" (063-005) — a moment of respite before the fashionable world arrives. It is here that Evelina encounters Lady Louisa Larpent ("hobbling rather than walking into the room" — 063-007) and Lord Merton ("shuffling into the room with his boots on, and his whip in his hand" — 063-006) for the first time, attends the dinner at which the gentlemen debate the merits of a venison feast (064-051), witnesses the old women's race engineered by Mrs. Selwyn, and attends the ball at which her true identity as Miss Belmont is revealed.

Mrs. Beaumont herself remains largely in the background — a gracious presence who facilitates rather than dominates the action. Her most significant interventions are small but revealing. She asks Lady Louisa if she has met her brother (063-011), confirms Lord Orville's wish to see his sister (063-014), gently corrects Lord Merton's exaggerations about the time — "It is almost three" (063-019) — and attempts to calm Lady Louisa's theatrical distress: "Compose yourself, Lady Louisa, the gentlemen will think better of the scheme; they are neither of them in earnest" (064-037).

Her neutrality is significant: unlike Mrs. Selwyn, who actively champions Evelina, or Lady Louisa, who actively snubs her, Mrs. Beaumont treats Evelina with the civility due to any guest, no more and no less. She receives her visitors "very graciously" (064-006), though she "somewhat distressed me by the questions she asked concerning my family; — such as, Whether I was related to the Anvilles in the North?" (064-006). These enquiries, while uncomfortable for Evelina, are the natural due diligence of a woman whose drawing-room is a theatre of rank. After dinner, she "recommended the gentlemen to the care of Lord Orville, and then attended the ladies to the drawing room" (064-052) — a ritual gesture that asserts her authority over the domestic sphere while ceding the public one to the ranking gentleman.

The conversation in her drawing-room "till teatime, was extremely insipid; Mrs. Selwyn reserved herself for the gentlemen, Mrs. Beaumont was grave, and Lady Louisa languid" (064-053). The word "grave" is suggestive: Mrs. Beaumont does not perform animation as Lady Louisa does; her gravity is the natural expression of a woman who has nothing to prove. When Lord Orville applies to her "for her vote" on some social question (064-082), he acknowledges her position as arbiter within her own domain.

## Character Traits

Mrs. Beaumont embodies the ideal of the aristocratic hostess: gracious, unobtrusive, and in command of her domain. Her house is a model of fashionable hospitality, and her management of the diverse personalities gathered under her roof — the languid Lady Louisa, the boisterous Lord Merton, the prickly Mrs. Selwyn, the anxious Evelina — demonstrates social skill of the highest order. She greets her guests with ease, presides over the entertainments, and maintains the social order of her house without visible effort.

Her most distinctive quality is her neutrality. She is, in the novel's moral scheme, a benign figure. She has neither the affectation of Lady Louisa nor the brutality of Captain Mirvan. Her connection to Lord Orville suggests shared family values of honour and courtesy, though she lacks his warmth and moral engagement. She is a background figure, but the background she provides is essential to the novel's resolution: a neutral ground where social distinctions can be contested and ultimately resolved.

She possesses what might be called the authority of indifference — the confidence to intervene or not intervene as the situation requires, without anxiety about her own status. When Lord Merton acts boorishly, she corrects him without heat. When Lady Louisa performs invalidism, she suggests composure without confrontation. When Evelina's identity is questioned, she asks the necessary questions without pressing for answers that would embarrass.

## Key Relationships

### With Lord Orville

Mrs. Beaumont is Lord Orville's relation, and her house is his destination in Bristol. Their kinship aligns her with the novel's moral centre, and her hospitality provides the setting in which Orville can demonstrate his constancy to Evelina. When Orville arrives at Clifton Hill, he addresses himself "immediately to Mrs. Beaumont" to enquire after his sister, but upon seeing Evelina "he suddenly exclaimed, 'Miss Anville!'" (063-030) — the hostess momentarily eclipsed by the beloved. Mrs. Beaumont, characteristically, does not register the slight. She later observes to Mrs. Selwyn that "I am afraid this young lady is too weak for another long walk till she is again rested" (063-036), prompting Orville's offer to escort Evelina — a small but crucial intervention that advances the romantic plot.

### With Lady Louisa Larpent

Mrs. Beaumont hosts Lady Louisa with the same grace she extends to all her guests, but her gentle corrections — confirming that Orville wished to see his sister (063-014), attempting to calm her distress about the gentlemen's scheme (064-037) — suggest a quiet authority that even Lady Louisa's affectation cannot overwhelm. She manages Lady Louisa's theatrical invalidism without either indulging or confronting it, a tact that preserves the social surface while implicitly signalling that she is not deceived.

### With Lord Merton

Lord Merton's raffish manner is accommodated but not endorsed by Mrs. Beaumont. He enters "shuffling into the room with his boots on, and his whip in his hand; and having made something like a bow to Mrs. Beaumont, he turned towards me" (063-006). The phrase "something like a bow" captures both Merton's carelessness and Mrs. Beaumont's imperturbability — she accepts the gesture without comment. At dinner, he "stalking up to Mrs. Beaumont, to whom alone he bowed, he hoped he had not made her wait" (064-017). Mrs. Beaumont does not reprove his manners; she simply proceeds as hostess, her silence a more effective reproof than any words.

### With Mrs. Selwyn

Mrs. Selwyn is "very well acquainted with Mrs. Beaumont" (062-065), and their relationship is one of social equals. Mrs. Selwyn's repeated invitations to stay at Clifton Hill, and her equally repeated refusals — citing "the inconvenience of being so distant from the pump-room" (062-065) — suggest a friendship conducted on terms of mutual independence. Mrs. Selwyn "reserved herself for the gentlemen" (064-053) in Mrs. Beaumont's drawing-room, confident that her hostess requires no conversational assistance. Later, Mrs. Selwyn jokes about "this poor Mrs. Beaumont" being "so eager to have a discharge in full of her debt to me, that out of mere compassion, I am induced to listen to her" (064-115) — a remark whose teasing tone implies long familiarity.

### With Evelina

Mrs. Beaumont's treatment of Evelina is correct but not warm. She "received us very graciously" (064-006) but distresses Evelina with questions about her family connections. She neither champions her (like Mrs. Selwyn) nor persecutes her (like Lady Louisa). Her neutrality is, in its way, a form of acceptance — by treating Evelina as she treats all her guests, she implicitly acknowledges her right to be among them. Yet the limits of this acceptance are clear: Mrs. Beaumont's hospitality is conditional on Mrs. Selwyn's sponsorship, and her interest in Evelina is the interest of a hostess in the social classification of her guests.

## Narrative Function

Mrs. Beaumont serves a structural function in the novel: she provides the neutral ground on which the social conflicts of the final volume can be staged and resolved. Her house is not a battlefield but a theatre — a space in which characters can perform their natures without the house itself taking sides. This neutrality is itself a moral position. Where Lady Louisa's drawing-room would be a court of fashionable judgement, and Captain Mirvan's house a cockpit of pranks, Mrs. Beaumont's house is a space of civilised accommodation in which the novel's values — honour, constancy, genuine feeling — can finally triumph.
