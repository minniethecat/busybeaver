# Snow Hill

Snow Hill is a street in the [[holborn|Holborn]] area of [[london|London]], where Mr. Brangh[[fashion|ton]] the silversmith keeps his shop and where the Brangh[[fashion|ton]] family resides. It represents the commercial, middling [[london|London]] — a world of trade and industry far removed from the fashionable West End that [[evelina|Evelina]] enters through the [[captain-mirvan|Mirvan]]s and [[lord-orville|Lord Orville]].

## Overview

- **Type**: street / commercial district
- **Location**: near [[holborn|Holborn]], [[london|London]]
- **Period**: 18th century

## Description in the Novel

The Brangh[[fashion|ton]]s introduce Snow Hill to [[evelina|Evelina]] on their first meeting, as they explain their journey: "they came on foot from Snow Hill, where Mr. Brangh[[fashion|ton]] keeps a silversmith's shop" (017-015). The detail of their having walked — and complained of "a very dirty walk" — establishes both the distance and the social remove of Snow Hill from the fashionable quarters of the West End.

The Brangh[[fashion|ton]]s extend an invitation that firmly links their home to this location: "the first time I went towards Snow Hill, they should be very glad if I would call upon them" (017-034). The phrasing "towards Snow Hill" suggests that the street itself is a directional marker, a district rather than merely an address.

## Significance to the Plot

[[evelina|Evelina]]'s visits to Snow Hill are invariably occasions for social mortification. A hackney-coach conveys the party to the Brangh[[fashion|ton]]s' shop: "A hackney-coach was then called, and we proceeded to Snow Hill" (040-028). The mode of transport — a hired coach rather than a private carriage — is itself a social marker.

On another occasion, [[evelina|Evelina]] receives "an invitation to dine and spend the day at Mr. Brangh[[fashion|ton]]'s" and is conducted to Snow Hill by M. Du Bois (042-003). These visits expose [[evelina|Evelina]] to the vulgarity of her low-born relations, sharpening the [[social-class|class]] tensions that structure the novel. On one visit, a rain shower sends them hurrying into the shop, where [[evelina|Evelina]] unexpectedly encounters Mr. Macartney: "the first object I saw was Mr. Macartney, with a book in his hand, seated in the same corner" (048-004) — one of the novel's most charged moments, connecting the two parallel plots.

## Social Context

Snow Hill embodies the commercial [[london|London]] of tradespeople — the Brangh[[fashion|ton]]s are silversmiths, members of the respectable but unfashionable middling sort. [[fanny-burney|Burney]] contrasts this world sharply with the aristocratic circles of [[lord-orville|Lord Orville]] and the genteel household of the [[captain-mirvan|Mirvan]]s. The physical distance between [[queen-ann-street|Queen Ann Street]] and Snow Hill maps onto the social distance between [[evelina|Evelina]]'s guardians and her low-born grandmother's relations. That [[evelina|Evelina]] must traverse this distance — and endure the society she finds at its end — is one of the novel's persistent sources of comic humiliation and [[social-class|class]] commentary.
