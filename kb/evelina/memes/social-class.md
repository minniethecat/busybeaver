# Social Class

Social class is the invisible architecture of the world [[Fanny Burney]] depicts in *Evelina*. Every encounter, every judgment, every possibility of marriage is shaped by the hierarchy of rank — and [[Evelina]]'s ambiguous position within that hierarchy, as the unacknowledged daughter of a baronet raised in rural seclusion, drives the novel's plot and generates its moral drama.

## The Class Ladder

The novel presents a finely graded social order:

| Rank | Characters | Characteristics |
|------|-----------|-----------------|
| Aristocracy | [[Lord Orville]] | Effortless superiority, genuine delicacy |
| Landed gentry | Lady Howard, the Mirvans | Respectable but not fashionable |
| Baronetage | Sir John Belmont, [[Sir Clement Willoughby]] | Rank without necessarily virtue |
| Clergy | [[Rev. Villars]] | Moral authority without wealth |
| Trade / moneyed | The Branghtons | Wealth without breeding |
| Servants / working | Footmen, maids | Invisible but observant |

## Evelina's Ambiguous Position

Evelina's class position is the novel's central problem. Born the legitimate daughter of Sir John Belmont, she is entitled to the status of a baronet's daughter. But her father's refusal to acknowledge her, her mother's death, and her upbringing by a country clergyman leave her in social limbo — too well-bred for the Branghtons' company, too obscure for the Orvilles' world.

This ambiguity makes her vulnerable. [[Sir Clement Willoughby]] can pursue her with an insolence he would not dare with a woman of acknowledged station. The novel's resolution — public recognition by Belmont — is thus not merely sentimental but structural: it restores Evelina to her proper place in the class order.

## Class and Morality

Burney's treatment of class is reformist rather than revolutionary. She criticizes the abuse of rank — Sir Clement's predatory entitlement, the Branghtons' vulgar pretension — but does not question the hierarchy itself. [[Lord Orville]]'s genuine nobility of character validates the idea that rank and virtue should align. The novel's ideal is not a classless society but one in which rank is justified by the conduct of those who hold it.
