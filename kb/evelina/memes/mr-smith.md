# Mr. Smith

Mr. Smith is a lodger at the Branghtons' house in Snow Hill, Holborn, whose pretensions to gentility provide comic relief in the London section of the novel. He occupies the first-floor room, keeps a foot-boy, and is regarded by the Branghton sisters as a gentleman of fashion — though his actual status as a lodger in a silversmith's house tells a different story. Where Lord Orville represents genuine aristocratic ease and Sir Clement Willoughby the dangerous charm of the libertine, Mr. Smith embodies a third masculine type: the aspiring tradesman whose imitation of gentility is forever betrayed by his origins.

- **Full Name**: not given (referred to only as Mr. Smith)
- **Title/Rank**: Mr.
- **Family**: none mentioned
- **Residence**: first floor, Mr. Branghton's house, Snow Hill, Holborn, London

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Smith appears during Evelina's extended stay with the Branghtons in London (Letters XL–LV). He is the object of Miss Biddy Branghton's unrequited affection and the suitor of Miss Polly Branghton, creating a triangle of romantic rivalry between the two sisters. His social pretensions — the foot-boy, the finely furnished room, the fashionable dress — mirror the novel's broader satire of London's middling class aspiring to gentility.

Miss Polly describes Mr. Smith's room as "a very pretty one, and only up one pair of stairs, and nicely furnished, and everything" (040-037). She boasts to Evelina that "he's quite like one of the quality, and dresses as fine, and goes to balls and dances, and everything, quite in taste; and besides, Miss, he keeps a foot-boy of his own too" (042-018). The foot-boy serves as Mr. Smith's proxy, delivering messages about the availability of his room and summoning guests to tea (042-064, 042-066).

Mr. Smith's refusal to lend his room for dining — "one day it happened to be a little greased" (042-018) — but willingness to lend it for tea suggests a concern for appearances typical of his class. He initially declines to join the party, sending word that he was "engaged already to dinner" (044-014), maintaining the social distance of a gentleman.

When he does appear in person, Mr. Smith assumes the role of gallant host with studied self-importance. He is described "lolling indolently out of the other" window (042-067), and at Evelina's entrance he "most officiously handed me to a great chair at the upper end of the room, without taking any notice of Madame Duval, till I rose and offered her my own seat" (042-067). This moment is revealing: his exaggerated courtesy to Evelina is paired with a social slight to Madame Duval, suggesting that his politeness is performance rather than principle.

His most consequential statement is his declared opposition to marriage: Mr. Brown reported that Mr. Smith said "he'd never marry as long as he lived, for he'd no opinion of matrimony" (040-053). This creates dramatic tension, as Miss Polly's romantic hopes rest on a man who has publicly renounced the institution.

### The Hampstead Assembly Episode

Mr. Smith's most extended appearance comes when he calls on Evelina with a ticket for the Hampstead assembly. Approaching "with an air of triumph," he declares "Well, Ma'am, now I think you can't possibly keep to your denial" (043-010). Evelina, trapped between politeness and her distaste for his company, ultimately escapes by invoking the precedent of Mr. Lovel: "I had refused several persons in his absence" from dancing, and therefore could not accept him without offending them (050-059). The invocation of Lovel's name here is telling — Mr. Smith, like Lovel, is a man whose social advances Evelina must manage through the delicate machinery of refusal.

### The Vauxhall Party

At the planning of the evening's entertainment, Mr. Smith's behaviour reveals both his pretensions and his limitations. When Tom Branghton proposes a low venue, Mr. Smith repeats the name "contemptuously; how came you to think the young lady would like to go to such a low place as that!" (044-031). He proposes Vauxhall instead, positioning himself as the arbiter of taste. When the party cannot agree on a destination, Mr. Smith orchestrates a voting ritual in which each member names a preference and Evelina is asked to give "the casting vote" (045-038) — a parody of parliamentary procedure that exposes the group's pretensions to social sophistication.

His gallantry to Evelina is persistent and unwelcome. He attempts to take her hand as she leaves (045-048), makes laboured jokes about church — "I would go anywhere with you, Ma'am, unless, indeed, it were to church; — ha, ha, ha!" (045-028) — and generally positions himself as her favoured escort, oblivious to her discomfort. Evelina's private judgement is damning: comparing him to Sir Clement, she notes that Sir Clement's "language, though too flowery, is always that of a gentleman; and his address and manners are so very superior to those of the inhabitants of this house, that, to make any comparison between him and Mr. Smith, would be extremely unjust" (042-068).

## Character Traits

Mr. Smith embodies the London tradesman-aspirant who affects the manners of the gentry without possessing their substance. His "fine" dress, attendance at balls and dances, and employment of a foot-boy mark him as a man striving to live above his station. His fastidiousness about his room — refusing to lend it after it was "a little greased" — reveals a concern for property and appearance that is fundamentally bourgeois rather than aristocratic. The true gentleman, Burney implies, is careless of such things; the aspirant is defined by his anxiety about them.

His language oscillates between attempted refinement and unconscious vulgarity. He addresses Evelina with elaborate courtesy but makes jokes about matrimony that no gentleman would direct at an unmarried young woman. He is "always in such spirits!" (044-049) — a phrase that, in the context of Burney's social taxonomy, marks him as lacking the restraint that distinguishes true gentility.

## Key Relationships

### With Miss Polly Branghton

Miss Polly considers Mr. Smith her "lover" and takes pride in having a suitor before her elder sister. She frequently references his room, his possessions, and his habits as evidence of her connection to a gentleman (040-037, 042-018). Her jealousy when other women show interest in him is noted by the narrator.

### With Miss Biddy Branghton

Miss Biddy "has taken it into her head to have a liking for Mr. Smith, as lodges on the first floor" (040-053), creating a rivalry with her younger sister. Miss Polly dismisses her sister's chances: "he'll never have her, for he's quite a fine gentleman" (040-053). The competition between the sisters for Mr. Smith's attention is one of several tensions in the Branghton household, and it reflects a broader theme: the limited romantic prospects available to women of the trading class.

### With the Branghton Family

Mr. Branghton is Mr. Smith's landlord, and the family's access to his room for social occasions is a recurring negotiation. Young Mr. Branghton (Tom) functions as an intermediary, and the foot-boy serves as the formal channel of communication between Mr. Smith and the household (042-064). The relationship is transactional — rent for lodging — but Mr. Smith's pretensions to social superiority complicate it.

## Narrative Function

Mr. Smith serves as a satirical portrait of London's lower-middle-class aspirant culture. He is not evil — unlike Sir Clement, he poses no real threat — but he is absurd, and his absurdity lies in the gap between his self-conception and his actual circumstances. Burney uses him to demonstrate that gentility cannot be purchased with a foot-boy and a well-furnished room; it is a quality of mind and manner that Mr. Smith, for all his efforts, cannot acquire.
