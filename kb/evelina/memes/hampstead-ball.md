# Hampstead Ball

The [[hampstead|Hampstead]] ball — held at the long room in [[hampstead|Hampstead]] — represents a different order of social entertainment from Mrs. Stanley's select private ball. It is a [[assembly-rooms|public assembly]] where [[evelina|Evelina]], accompanied by her vulgar Brangh[[fashion|ton]] relations, experiences the discomfort of being seen in company that reflects poorly upon her.

## Overview

- **Type**: public ball / [[assembly-rooms|assembly]]
- **Location**: the long room at Hampstead, then a village north of [[london|London]]
- **Date**: Spring, during [[evelina|Evelina]]'s [[london|London]] stay

## The Event

The [[hampstead|Hampstead]] ball is arranged as a country outing that combines the pleasures of a rural excursion with those of a public dance. [[evelina|Evelina]]'s companions are the Brangh[[fashion|ton]]s, whose enthusiasm for the event is [[reputation|character]]istically coarse: Miss Brangh[[fashion|ton]] scoffs at [[evelina|Evelina]]'s reluctance — "yes, that's a likely matter truly, when people go to balls" (050-034) — implying that no one attends a ball without hoping to be admired.

The venue itself — "the long room at Hampstead" (050-049) — signals a less exclusive social register than the private [[london|London]] ball. Public long rooms were hired for [[assembly|assemblies]], concerts, and dances, and admission was generally open to anyone who could pay. The [[hampstead|Hampstead]] setting, while pleasant, lacks the cachet of a private [[london|London]] residence.

## Significance

The [[hampstead|Hampstead]] ball is significant primarily for what [[evelina|Evelina]] brings to it rather than what happens there. "In truth," she writes, "I had no power to attend to him; for all my thoughts were occupied in retracing the transactions of the two former balls, at which I had been present" (050-061). Her mind is elsewhere — at [[private-ball|the private ball]] where she first met [[lord-orville|Lord Orville]], at the [[ridotto]] where [[sir-clement-willoughby|Sir Clement Willoughby]]'s forwardness alarmed her. The [[hampstead|Hampstead]] ball thus becomes a reflective space, a pause in the narrative during which [[evelina|Evelina]] measures her own social progress.

Rev. [[rev-villars|Villars]]'s response confirms the moral value of [[evelina|Evelina]]'s conduct at [[hampstead|Hampstead]]: "I rejoice to find that my conduct at the [[hampstead|Hampstead]] ball has had so good an effect" (055-005). Unlike the earlier [[london|London]] balls, where her innocence led to embarrassing mistakes, at [[hampstead|Hampstead]] she has begun to exercise [[prudence|judgment]] — a sign of her developing social competence.

## Social Context

[[hampstead|Hampstead]] in the eighteenth century was a village on the northern edge of [[london|London]], known for its fresh air, its springs, and its long room — a venue for public entertainments that attracted a mixed company. The [[hampstead|Hampstead]] ball occupies a social position between the exclusivity of the private [[london|London]] ball and the dangerous publicity of the pleasure garden. It is respectable without being fashionable, pleasant without being distinguished — much like [[hampstead|Hampstead]] itself. For [[evelina|Evelina]], whose social standing is precarious, such a venue offers fewer perils but also fewer opportunities for distinction.
