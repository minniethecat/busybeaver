# White-Conduit House

White-Conduit House was an eighteenth-century tea-house and pleasure garden in Isling[[fashion|ton]], then a rural suburb of [[london|London]]. In *[[evelina|Evelina]]*, it is the destination chosen by the Brangh[[fashion|ton]] party after much disputation, representing the kind of vulgar public entertainment that causes [[evelina|Evelina]] acute social embarrassment.

## Overview

- **Type**: pleasure garden / tea-house
- **Location**: Isling[[fashion|ton]], then on the outskirts of [[london|London]]
- **Period**: 18th century

## Description in the Novel

White-Conduit House enters the novel as the contested choice in a scene of protracted comic indecision. The Brangh[[fashion|ton]] brother declares: "I'm for White-Conduit House; so let's go there" (045-022). His sister immediately objects — "White-Conduit House, indeed!" (045-023) — and a debate ensues in which various candidates are proposed and rejected: Saltero's Coffeehouse, Mother Red Cap's, and Bagnigge Wells are all canvassed before the party settles on the brother's preference (045-037).

"White-Conduit House was at last fixed upon," [[evelina|Evelina]] records, "and, notwithstanding my great dislike of such parties and such places, I was obliged to accompany them" (045-054). Her reluctance is palpable: she dislikes both the "parties" — the Brangh[[fashion|ton]] family gatherings — and the "places" — the unfashionable public venues they frequent.

## Significance to the Plot

The White-Conduit House episode is not a major plot event but a scene of [[reputation|character]] and social texture. It illustrates the Brangh[[fashion|ton]]s' taste for what [[fanny-burney|Burney]]'s contemporaries would have recognized as distinctly middling entertainments. The very process of choosing — a loud, democratic disputation in which every family member has a vote — contrasts sharply with the polite invitations that structure [[evelina|Evelina]]'s social life in higher circles.

[[evelina|Evelina]]'s discomfiture at being "obliged to accompany them" (045-054) underscores the central tension of her situation: bound by family ties to people whose [[breeding|manners]] and tastes she cannot share, she is perpetually exposed to the shame of association.

## Social Context

White-Conduit House belonged to a [[social-class|class]] of suburban [[london|London]] pleasure gardens that catered to tradespeople and the middling sort — a tier below the fashionable venues like [[ranelagh|Ranelagh]] and [[vauxhall|Vauxhall]]. The gardens offered tea, walks, and sometimes music, but lacked the aristocratic cachet of the West End pleasure gardens. [[fanny-burney|Burney]] uses the Brangh[[fashion|ton]]s' preference for such places to mark their social position: respectable enough to afford an outing, but not fashionable enough to choose — or even know — the right places to be seen. [[evelina|Evelina]]'s acute awareness of this distinction is one measure of her social education.
