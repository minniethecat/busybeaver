# Lord Merton

Lord Merton is the novel's most disturbing portrait of aristocratic dissipation. Engaged to Lord Orville's sister, Lady Louisa Larpent, he nevertheless pursues Evelina with aggressive gallantry and organises the grotesque old women's race that stands as the moral nadir of the novel's social world. He represents everything that Lord Orville is not: where Orville is restrained, Merton is riotous; where Orville protects, Merton preys.

## Identity

- **Title/Rank**: Lord
- **Family**: engaged to Lady Louisa Larpent (Lord Orville's sister)
- **Residence**: part of the fashionable set gathered at Bristol Hot Wells
- **Role**: fashionable libertine, secondary antagonist to Evelina

## Role in the Novel

Lord Merton appears in the Bristol/Clifton section of the novel as one of the fashionable set gathered at the Hot Wells. His engagement to Lady Louisa — a match Lord Orville cannot prevent but clearly deplores — gives him access to the Beaumont household and, through it, to Evelina. He is introduced through the social gatherings at Mrs. Beaumont's, where his "free" behaviour immediately marks him as a danger.

His most infamous act is the wager settled by a race between two octogenarian women. Evelina first learns of the scheme from Lord Orville himself:

> "He told me that, to his great satisfaction, the parties had been prevailed upon to lower the sum from one thousand to one hundred pounds; and that they had agreed it should be determined by a race between two old women, one of whom was to be chosen by each side, and both were to be proved more than eighty years of age, though, in other respects strong and healthy as possible."
>
> (050-009)

On the day of the race, Merton's character reveals itself without disguise. Drunk and triumphant, he "handed, with loud shouts, the old women to the race-ground, and encouraged them by liberal promises to exert themselves" (055-007). When the elderly contestants collide and fall, he is "too eager to have the affair deferred" (055-009). After his contestant wins — the other woman having fallen and injured herself — he celebrates his victory by harassing Evelina:

> "Lord Merton, entirely off his guard, giddy equally from wine and success, was very troublesome to me; and, regardless of the presence of Lady Louisa, which hitherto has restrained him even from common civility, he attached himself to me, during the walk, with a freedom of gallantry that put me extremely out of countenance. He paid me the most high-flown compliments; and frequently and forcibly seized my hand, though I repeatedly, and with undissembled anger, drew it back."
>
> (055-019)

The sequence is carefully constructed to link Merton's cruelty to the old women with his predatory behaviour toward Evelina. In both cases, those weaker than himself exist only for his amusement.

## Character Traits

Lord Merton embodies the moral vacancy that Burney saw at the heart of fashionable libertinism. His treatment of the old women reduces human beings to objects of sporting amusement: "the two gentlemen are as anxious, as if their joint lives depended upon it" (054-006). His pursuit of Evelina, undertaken "regardless of the presence of Lady Louisa" (055-019), demonstrates a contempt for women that extends from a poor octogenarian to his own fiancee.

Crucially, Merton is not a villain in the Gothic mode — he is not wicked in any supernatural or extraordinary sense. He is simply a man of rank and wealth who has never been taught that other people's suffering matters. His evil is banal, institutional, the product of a social system that treats rank as licence.

## Key Relationships

### With Lady Louisa Larpent
Merton's engagement to Lady Louisa is a match of convenience that he treats with open disregard. His flirtation with Evelina in Lady Louisa's presence is calculated to wound both women — demonstrating his power over his fiancee while testing his power over Evelina. Lady Louisa's "smiles were converted into looks of disdain" (055-019) when she witnesses his behaviour, but she, like everyone else in her circle, is powerless to hold him to account.

### With Lord Orville
Lord Orville's grave silence during the old women's race — "Lord Orville excepted, who looked very grave during the whole transaction" (055-007) — marks the moral chasm between the two noblemen. They share a class but not a code. Orville's distaste for Merton, and his inability to prevent his sister's marriage to such a man, is one of the novel's sharpest critiques of the arranged marriage system.

### With Mr. Coverley
Merton's friendship with Coverley is the camaraderie of shared vice. Their bet, their drinking, and their joint contempt for the women they exploit mark them as two of a kind — the dissolute aristocracy against which the novel's moral standards are defined.

## Narrative Function

Lord Merton serves as Lord Orville's dark double. Both are noblemen; both are wealthy; both move in the same fashionable circles. But where Orville uses his rank to protect, Merton uses his to exploit. The contrast between them is the novel's most explicit argument that virtue is not a function of class — that a man may be a lord and yet be contemptible, or a lord and yet be honourable. Merton's engagement to Orville's sister makes this contrast personal and painful, transforming an abstract moral argument into a family tragedy.
