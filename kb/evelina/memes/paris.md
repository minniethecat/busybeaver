# Paris

The French capital, frequently invoked in the novel as a symbol of fashion, refinement, and the cultural superiority [[madame-duval|Madame Duval]] attributes to all things French.

## Overview

- **Type**: city
- **Location**: France, continental Europe
- **Period**: 18th century

## Description in the Novel

Paris is not depicted directly in the novel, as none of the action takes place there. Instead, it functions as a rhetorical counterpoint to [[england|England]]: [[madame-duval|Madame Duval]] repeatedly contrasts the supposed elegance and politeness of Paris with what she regards as the coarse [[breeding|manners]] of the English. She insists that at [[ranelagh|Ranelagh]] "There is no such a thing to be seen in Paris" as ladies wearing hats, and declares that "in Paris no woman needn't be at such a trouble" to be noticed (016-018, 016-021). She further claims that "in Paris nobody never says nothing about religion, no more than about politics," presenting the city as a refuge from the social awkwardness she perceives in [[england|England]] (016-027).

## Significance to the Plot

Paris shapes the backstory and drives the plot at several critical junctures. [[evelina|Evelina]]'s mother, Caroline Evelyn, was sent to Paris after her mother married Monsieur [[madame-duval|Duval]], and there [[madame-duval|Madame Duval]] attempted to force a marriage between Miss Evelyn and one of [[madame-duval|Duval]]'s nephews (002-013). Later, Sir John Belmont's clandestine affair with Caroline took place in Paris, where he "effected his departure" after learning of her pregnancy (051-016). The city remains central to the novel's resolution: Lady Howard writes to Paris to contact Sir John Belmont (030-003), and [[madame-duval|Madame Duval]] repeatedly threatens to take [[evelina|Evelina]] there for a French education, insisting she "greatly wanted the polish of a French education" (017-009) and promising that after a few months in Paris [[evelina|Evelina]]'s "education and [[breeding|manners]] might receive their last polish" (026-008).

## Social Context

Paris in the novel functions as the emblem of French cultural capital — a place [[madame-duval|Madame Duval]] invokes to assert superiority over English [[breeding|manners]]. It is associated with fashion ([[madame-duval|Madame Duval]]'s cloak was purchased in Paris, 034-021), refinement, and the kind of social polish [[madame-duval|Madame Duval]] believes [[evelina|Evelina]] lacks. The novel treats this Francophilia with gentle satire, as [[madame-duval|Madame Duval]]'s Parisian pretensions are consistently undercut by her own vulgarity.
