# Compassion

**Compassion** — the capacity to feel for and with others — is the moral heart of *Evelina* (1778). In the tradition of the 18th-century sentimental novel, Burney makes compassion the definitive marker of virtue, distinguishing the truly good from the merely polite.

## Compassion as Moral Signature

Evelina's compassionate nature is established from the novel's opening. Mr. Villars describes her as possessing a heart "compassionately tender" (014-039), linking compassion to her essential goodness. This quality defines her responses throughout the novel — from her pity for Madame Duval's humiliations to her sympathy for the destitute Macartney.

## The Sentimental Framework

Burney writes within the tradition of sentimental fiction, where the capacity to feel — and to be moved by the suffering of others — is the highest moral faculty. When Evelina is "so much surprised and confounded at the blow, that, for some time, I suffered her to rave without making any answer; but her extreme agitation" (033-113) she responds with compassion even to those who wrong her.

## Compassion and Class

Compassion crosses class boundaries in the novel. Evelina's sympathy extends to servants, tradespeople, and the socially marginal. This egalitarian impulse challenges the rigid hierarchies of rank and fashion, suggesting that moral worth transcends social station.

## The Limits of Compassion

Yet Burney also explores the limits of compassion. Mr. Villars's concern that "while I improved her fortune, I should endanger her mind" (028-009) reflects anxiety that too much exposure to the world's cruelty might overwhelm a compassionate nature. The novel balances sympathy with prudence, feeling with judgment.
