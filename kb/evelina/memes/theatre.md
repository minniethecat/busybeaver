# Theatre

The theatre outings in *[[evelina|Evelina]]* serve as occasions for social performance that rival the drama onstage. A visit to [[drury-lane|Drury Lane Theatre]] to see *King Lear* becomes one of the novel's pivotal episodes, combining the pleasures of art with the perils of public exposure.

## Overview

- **Type**: theatrical performance
- **Venue**: [[drury-lane|Drury Lane Theatre]] (primary), plus references to other playhouses
- **Date**: Spring season, during [[evelina|Evelina]]'s [[london|London]] stay

## The Events

The theatre is introduced as part of the roster of [[london|London]] entertainments available to [[evelina|Evelina]]: "Two playhouses are open—the Opera-house—[[ranelagh|Ranelagh]]—and [[pantheon|the Pantheon]]" (008-008). The sheer abundance of diversions captures the overwhelming novelty of [[london|London]] for a provincial newcomer.

[[evelina|Evelina]]'s first theatrical experience is one of rapt absorption. After seeing *King Lear*, she reports: "We are just returned from the play, which was King Lear, and has made me very sad" (012-042). The [[sentiment|emotion]]al impact of tragedy — its capacity to move a sensitive viewer to genuine sorrow — marks [[evelina|Evelina]] as a young woman of [[sentiment|feeling]], in the eighteenth-century sense: someone whose [[sentiment|emotion]]al responsiveness is a sign of moral refinement.

Yet the theatre is also a site of social danger. At [[drury-lane|Drury Lane]], [[evelina|Evelina]] is exposed to unwanted attention from foppish admirers and must navigate the complex social geography of the boxes. The theatre combines the pleasures of spectatorship with the anxieties of being a spectacle oneself — a tension that runs throughout [[evelina|Evelina]]'s [[london|London]] experience.

## Significance

The theatrical episodes advance the novel's concern with performance and authenticity. Just as the actors onstage assume roles, so too must [[evelina|Evelina]] perform the part of the polite young lady — a role for which her provincial education has left her underprepared. The blurring of theatrical and social performance reaches its apex when the [[conduct|behaviour]] of [[evelina|Evelina]]'s companions in the box becomes a spectacle in its own right, drawing more attention than the play.

Mrs. [[captain-mirvan|Mirvan]]'s regular attendance at the theatre — "I intend to ask Mrs. [[captain-mirvan|Mirvan]] to go to the play every night while we stay in town" (010-011) — establishes the theatre as a respectable and indeed essential component of a [[london|London]] season, however fraught with social peril.

## Social Context

The eighteenth-century theatre was a microcosm of [[london|London]] society. The boxes were the domain of the fashionable, the pit housed critics and connoisseurs, and the gallery held servants and the lower orders. A young woman's presence in a box — and the company she kept there — was a matter of public scrutiny. [[fanny-burney|Burney]], herself a playwright as well as a novelist, understood the theatre as both a literal venue and a metaphor for the social world: a stage on which [[reputation|character]] is tested and [[honour|reputation]] displayed.
