# Evelina Anville

Evelina Anville is the eponymous protagonist of [[Fanny Burney]]'s 1778 novel *Evelina*. Raised in rural seclusion at Berry Hill under the guardianship of [[Rev. Villars]], she enters London society at the age of seventeen — a journey that tests her innocence against the complexities of fashionable life. Her story unfolds through letters, primarily written to her guardian, creating an intimate portrait of a young woman discovering both the world and herself.

## Identity

- **Full Name**: Evelina Anville (born Evelina Belmont)
- **Title/Rank**: Miss
- **Family**: Sir John Belmont (father), Lady Belmont (mother, deceased), Rev. Arthur Villars (guardian)
- **Residence**: Berry Hill, Dorsetshire

## Character

Evelina embodies the virtues Rev. Villars has cultivated in her: "gentleness and modesty are the peculiar attributes of your sex" (084-001). Yet she is no passive figure — she possesses a lively sensibility and a natural curiosity about the world. Her guardian warns her that "nothing is so delicate as the reputation of a woman; it is at once the most beautiful and most brittle of all human things" (009-002).

Throughout the novel, Evelina navigates a series of social trials: the forward advances of [[Sir Clement Willoughby]], the vulgarity of her grandmother [[Madame Duval]], the boorish pranks of [[Captain Mirvan]], and the ambiguous attentions of [[Lord Orville]]. Each encounter forces her to exercise judgment that her sheltered upbringing has not prepared her for.

## Key Relationships

### With Rev. Villars

Rev. Villars is the moral compass of Evelina's life. She addresses her letters to him with filial devotion, and his replies provide the ethical framework through which she interprets her experiences. He calls her "the child of my bosom — the comfort of my age — the sweet solace of all my infirmities" (048-001).

### With Lord Orville

Lord Orville represents the ideal of gentlemanly conduct against which Evelina measures all other men. From their first meeting at the [[assembly]], she is struck by his "amiable" nature. Her growing attachment to him — and her uncertainty about his intentions — forms the emotional core of the novel.

### With Madame Duval

Madame Duval, Evelina's French grandmother, embodies everything Rev. Villars warned against: "the violence and vulgarity of this woman, her total ignorance of propriety" (007-003). Their relationship forces Evelina to balance filial duty against moral discernment.

## Narrative Voice

Evelina's letters are remarkable for their immediacy and psychological acuity. She records her impressions with a freshness that makes the reader share her discoveries. When she writes "I looked about for some of *my* acquaintance, but in vain; for I saw not one person that I knew, which is very odd, for all the world seemed there" (010-001), the gentle self-mockery captures both her innocence and her growing self-awareness.
