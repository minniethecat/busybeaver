# Marybone Gardens Visit

The visit to Marybone (Marylebone) Gardens is one of the most harrowing episodes in [[evelina|Evelina]]'s [[london|London]] experience. What begins as a routine evening of entertainment — a concert and fireworks — becomes a perilous adventure when [[evelina|Evelina]] is separated from her party and exposed to the dangers of unsupervised [[london|London]] nightlife.

## Overview

- **Type**: pleasure garden entertainment (concert and fireworks)
- **Venue**: Marybone (Marylebone) Gardens, [[london|London]]
- **Entertainment**: violin concerto by Mr. Barthelemon; firework display of the story of Orpheus and Eurydice by M. Torre
- **Party**: [[madame-duval|Madame Duval]], the Brangh[[fashion|ton]]s, M. Du Bois, Mr. Smith, Mr. Brown, and [[evelina|Evelina]]

## The Event

The outing is settled upon after the [[vauxhall|Vauxhall]] adventures have lost their novelty: "Yesterday it was settled that we should spend the evening in Marybone Gardens, where M. Torre, a celebrated foreigner, was to exhibit some fireworks" (045-001). The party is [[reputation|character]]istically ill-assorted — [[madame-duval|Madame Duval]], the vulgar Brangh[[fashion|ton]] family, M. Du Bois, Mr. Smith, and Mr. Brown — and Mr. Brangh[[fashion|ton]]'s determination to have "all he could get for his money" (045-003) sets the [[fashion|ton]]e of petty economy that governs the evening.

The garden itself disappoints: "This Garden, as it is called, is neither striking for magnificence nor for beauty" (045-007). A violin concerto by the celebrated Mr. Barthelemon, "a player of exquisite fancy, [[sentiment|feeling]] and variety" (045-007), provides the only genuine pleasure.

The firework display is elaborate — "told, with wonderful ingenuity, the story of Orpheus and Eurydice" (045-009) — but the climactic explosion scatters the party: "at the moment of the fatal look which separated them forever, there was such an explosion of fire, and so horrible a noise, that we all, as of one accord, jumpt hastily from the form, and ran away some paces" (045-009). In the confusion, [[evelina|Evelina]] becomes separated from everyone she knows.

## Evelina's Distress

Separated in the crowd, [[evelina|Evelina]] is accosted by a "young officer" who seizes her hand and declares, "You are a sweet pretty creature, and I enlist you in my service" (045-011). Desperate, she runs to two women for protection — only to discover that they are themselves disreputable. They hold her fast and mock her distress with "a loud laugh" (045-013) at her "very simply" told story.

The ordeal reaches its peak when [[lord-orville|Lord Orville]] passes by and sees [[evelina|Evelina]] in the company of these women. The moment is devastating: "had I, indeed, been sunk to the guilty state which such companions might lead him to suspect, I could scarce have had [[sentiment|feeling]]s more cruelly depressing" (045-020). She escapes when Mr. Brown appears, but the two women force themselves upon the party — and [[lord-orville|Lord Orville]] encounters them again, his eyes filled with "surprise and concern" (045-027).

The episode concludes with a moment of grace: [[lord-orville|Lord Orville]] returns after [[evelina|Evelina]] frees herself from the women, his solicitude — for so reserved a man — suggesting genuine care.

## Significance

The Marybone Gardens episode dramatises the central anxiety of the novel: a young woman's vulnerability in public space. [[evelina|Evelina]]'s separation from her party is not her fault — it is caused by a general panic — but the consequences fall entirely upon her. The episode demonstrates that in the absence of a respectable escort, a woman in eighteenth-century [[london|London]] is prey to predatory men and exploitative women alike. [[lord-orville|Lord Orville]]'s concern, and his return to check on her, marks him as genuinely solicitous — a significant development in their relationship.
