# Assembly Rooms

**Assembly Rooms** were public venues for balls, concerts, and social gatherings in 18th-century [[england|England]], and they serve as crucial settings in *[[evelina|Evelina]]* (1778). It is at [[assembly|assemblies]] that [[evelina|Evelina]] first encounters [[fashion|fashionable society]], meets [[lord-orville|Lord Orville]], and navigates the complexities of public social life.

## Assemblies in the Novel

[[evelina|Evelina]]'s first assembly is a disorienting experience: "A confused idea now for the first time entered my head, of something I had heard of the rules of an assembly; but I was never at one before" (011-047). Her innocence of assembly [[politeness|etiquette]] — she refuses one partner and accepts another, committing a social blunder — drives much of the novel's early drama.

## Rules and Propriety

Assemblies were governed by strict codes of conduct. Mrs. [[captain-mirvan|Mirvan]] tells [[evelina|Evelina]] "it was highly improper for young women to dance with strangers at any public assembly" (013-007). Despite her mortification, [[evelina|Evelina]] resolves "that I will never again be tempted to go to an assembly" (012-027), though she is persuaded to attend another (013-005).

## Social Function

Assemblies served as marriage markets and social display arenas. For [[fanny-burney|Burney]], they are laboratories of [[reputation|character]] — where true breeding is distinguished from its counterfeit, and where the dangers posed by predatory [[gallantry|gallant]]ry are most visible.
