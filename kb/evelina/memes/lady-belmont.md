# Lady Belmont

Caroline Evelyn, later Lady Belmont, is the deceased mother of Evelina and the daughter of Mr. Evelyn. Though she dies before the novel's opening, her tragic story — an unacknowledged marriage to Sir John Belmont, her deathbed entrustment of the infant Evelina to the Rev. Villars, and Belmont's denial of the marriage — constitutes the novel's central backstory and the engine of its plot. Her posthumous presence haunts the narrative, shaping the motivations of nearly every major character.

## Identity

- **Full Name**: Caroline Evelyn, later Lady Belmont
- **Title/Rank**: Lady Belmont (by marriage to Sir John Belmont)
- **Family**: Daughter of Mr. Evelyn; wife of Sir John Belmont; mother of Evelina; former ward of Madame Duval
- **Residence**: Died at Berry Hill, the Villars estate; formerly raised in France under Madame Duval's care

## Role in the Novel

Lady Belmont's legacy governs the novel's entire plot. Her marriage to Sir John Belmont was conducted privately by Rev. Villars (028-008). Belmont, however, denied the marriage and destroyed the certificate, effectively branding Caroline as his mistress rather than his lawful wife. Lady Belmont, "firmly persuaded of her approaching dissolution, frequently and earnestly besought me, that if her infant was a female, I would not abandon her to the direction of a man so wholly" unworthy (028-008).

Rev. Villars gave his "word so solemnly given to Lady Belmont, that her child should never be owned but with her self" (028-022), meaning Evelina would never be acknowledged as Belmont's daughter unless Caroline's own legitimacy as his wife was simultaneously recognized. This solemn promise shapes the entire course of Evelina's upbringing under the name "Anville" rather than "Belmont."

Lady Howard, writing to Sir John Belmont, notes that Evelina "is the lovely resemblance of her lovely mother" (031-009). The public revelation of Sir John Belmont's acknowledged daughter threatens to "revive the remembrance of Miss Evelyn's story in all who have heard it — who the mother was, will be universally demanded" (073-007).

## Character Traits

Lady Belmont is consistently portrayed through others' testimony as virtuous, beautiful, and deeply wronged. She is described with an air of tragedy and innocence: Villars speaks of "the wrongs, not guilt, by which she suffered" (028-014), drawing a sharp distinction between her moral character and the circumstances imposed upon her by Belmont's faithlessness. Her daughter Evelina is said to be "the lovely resemblance of her lovely mother" (031-009), linking Caroline's beauty and goodness to the novel's heroine.

She is also depicted as a figure of maternal devotion: her dying thoughts were entirely occupied with securing her infant daughter's future and protecting her from a father she knew to be unworthy (028-008).

## Key Relationships

### With Sir John Belmont

Sir John Belmont privately married Caroline Evelyn but subsequently denied the marriage, destroying the marriage certificate (028-008). This act of denial is the original wrong from which all subsequent plot complications flow. Mrs. Selwyn later confronts Belmont directly: "Had you a thousand children, Sir John, this only one, of which Lady Belmont was the mother, ought to be most distinguished" (078-017). When Belmont asks in astonishment "does Caroline Evelyn still live!" (078-023), the narrative reveals both his guilt and his long suppression of the truth.

### With Rev. Arthur Villars

Villars was both the officiant of Caroline's marriage and the recipient of her dying trust. He recalls: "Lady Belmont, who was firmly persuaded of her approaching dissolution, frequently and earnestly besought me" to protect her infant daughter (028-008). His inviolable promise to her — that Evelina would never be acknowledged by Belmont without Caroline herself being acknowledged — becomes the moral foundation of Evelina's entire upbringing (028-022).

### With Madame Duval

Madame Duval was Caroline's guardian during her upbringing in France. After Caroline's death, Duval abandoned her, as Lady Howard notes: "the unfortunate Lady Belmont, at a time when a mother's protection was" most needed, was abandoned by Duval (001-007). This abandonment accounts for much of the animosity between Duval and the other guardians of Evelina.

### With Evelina

Lady Belmont's relationship with her daughter is entirely posthumous — mediated through letters, promises, and the physical resemblance Evelina bears to her mother. Lady Howard observes that Evelina "is the lovely resemblance of her lovely mother" (031-009). The entire plot of the novel may be read as the working-out of Lady Belmont's dying wishes and the vindication of her reputation.

## Significant Appearances

Lady Belmont does not appear in the novel's present action. Her presence is entirely textual — through letters, testimonies, and recollections:

- **Letter from Lady Belmont to Sir John Belmont** (Letter LXXIV): Her own voice appears in a letter, listed in the chapter heading: "Lady Belmont to Sir John Belmont" (074-001), though the novel does not reproduce the full text directly.
- **Rev. Villars's narrative** (Letter XXVIII): The most extended account of Lady Belmont's story, including her marriage, Belmont's denial, and her deathbed wishes (028-008, 028-014, 028-022).
- **Lady Howard's letters** (Letters I, XXXI): References to Lady Belmont and the circumstances of her death and Evelina's upbringing (001-004, 001-007, 031-009).
- **Mrs. Selwyn's confrontation of Sir John Belmont** (Letter LXXVIII): The dramatic scene in which Mrs. Selwyn invokes Lady Belmont's memory to shame Sir John Belmont into acknowledging his daughter (078-017, 078-023).

## Narrative Significance

Lady Belmont functions as a moral touchstone and a narrative origin-point. Her story — of seduction, betrayal, and maternal sacrifice — echoes the sentimental novel tradition of Richardson and provides the novel's deepest emotional stakes. The eventual acknowledgment of Caroline Evelyn as Belmont's lawful wife, and Evelina as his legitimate daughter, constitutes the novel's resolution and the vindication of Lady Belmont's posthumous reputation. Her absence from the present action paradoxically makes her the most consequential figure in the novel's moral universe.
