# Politeness

**Politeness** — the 18th-century code of refined manners and civil conduct — is both a social currency and a moral test in *Evelina* (1778). Burney distinguishes genuine politeness grounded in character from its performative counterfeit, using politeness as a lens through which to judge every character.

## Genuine vs. False Politeness

Mr. Villars articulates the novel's standard when he praises Evelina's education: "that politeness which is acquired by an acquaintance with high life, is in her so evidently the result of natural apprehension and good sense" (006-009). True politeness flows from inner qualities, not mere social training.

Lord Orville consistently demonstrates authentic politeness. After a dance leaves Evelina flurried, "he led me to a seat, saying that he would not suffer me to fatigue myself from politeness" (011-037). His civility is considerate rather than performative.

## Politeness as Relief and Weapon

Evelina experiences politeness as genuine relief in awkward situations: "This politeness relieved me" (013-112). Yet politeness can also serve as a weapon of social manipulation, as seen in Sir Clement Willoughby's overly elaborate gallantries and the calculated civilities of the fashionable world.

## The Gender of Politeness

Politeness operates asymmetrically across gender lines. Women are judged more harshly for breaches of etiquette, while men may deploy politeness as seduction. Evelina's constant anxiety about correct behaviour reflects the gendered burden of politeness in 18th-century society.
