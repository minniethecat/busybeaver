# Holborn

A commercial district in [[london|London]] where [[evelina|Evelina]] lodges with the Brangh[[fashion|ton]] family during her second stay in the capital. The address marks a significant step down in social standing from her earlier residence in [[queen-ann-street|Queen Ann Street]].

## Overview

- **Type**: street / district
- **Location**: [[london|London]]
- **Period**: 18th century

## Description in the Novel

Holborn is introduced when Mr. Brangh[[fashion|ton]] declares "our lodgings were in Holborn" (040-057). [[evelina|Evelina]]'s direction is given as "at Mr. Dawkin's, a hosier in High Holborn" (040-060) — lodging above a shop, a clear marker of the Brangh[[fashion|ton]]s' commercial rather than genteel status. Several of [[evelina|Evelina]]'s letters are dated from Holborn: June 9 (042-002), June 13 (044-002), June 17 (046-002), June 18 (047-002), June 27 (050-002), and July 1 (052-002, 053-002).

## Significance to the Plot

Holborn represents the nadir of [[evelina|Evelina]]'s social descent in [[london|London]]. The shift from [[queen-ann-street|Queen Ann Street]] to Holborn maps her removal from the [[captain-mirvan|Mirvan]]s' respectable [[breeding|gentility]] to the Brangh[[fashion|ton]]s' vulgar commercial world. It is in Holborn that [[evelina|Evelina]] suffers some of her most painful humiliations: young Brangh[[fashion|ton]] speculates [[lord-orville|Lord Orville]] "saw you and I a-walking up Holborn Hill" (055-016), and it is from Holborn that [[evelina|Evelina]] writes her most distressed letters to [[rev-villars|Mr. Villars]]. When the coach stopped "at an hosier's in High Holborn," [[sir-clement-willoughby|Sir Clement Willoughby]]'s eyes "were very busily employed in viewing the place" (046-163), and soon after, [[lord-orville|Lord Orville]] discovers her lodging there — a mortifying exposure [[evelina|Evelina]] feels "cannot fail of being unfavourable" to her [[honour|reputation]] (052-050).

## Social Context

The movement from [[queen-ann-street|Queen Ann Street]] to Holborn is one of the novel's most economically precise social markers. Holborn in the 18th century was a mixed commercial and residential district, increasingly associated with trade and the middling sort. For a young woman of [[evelina|Evelina]]'s precarious status — genteel by birth but unacknowledged by her father — lodging above a hosier's shop places her in a social limbo that threatens her [[honour|reputation]] and marriage prospects. The discovery of her Holborn residence by [[lord-orville|Lord Orville]] precipitates one of the novel's most acute scenes of social shame.
