# Sentiment

**Sentiment** — refined feeling and moral emotion — is central to the 18th-century sentimental tradition in which *Evelina* (1778) participates. Burney both embraces and complicates the cult of sensibility, using sentiment as a marker of virtue while exposing its vulnerabilities.

## Sentiment as Moral Faculty

In the sentimental framework, the capacity for refined feeling distinguishes the virtuous from the coarse. Evelina's sensibility — her quickness to feel, to sympathise, to be moved — is her defining virtue. Mr. Villars praises her natural sensibility while worrying about its consequences in a harsh world (004-004).

## Sentiment and the Novel Form

Burney writes in the tradition of Richardson and Sterne, where the novel serves as a vehicle for cultivating moral sentiment in readers. Evelina's letters perform this function: her emotional responses to events model the proper sentimental response for the reader.

## The Dangers of Sentiment

Yet Burney is no uncritical advocate of sensibility. Excessive sentiment can cloud judgment, as when Evelina's compassion leads her into imprudent situations. The novel critiques the potential for sentiment to become self-indulgent or to enable manipulation by designing characters like Sir Clement Willoughby.
