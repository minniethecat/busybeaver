# Honour

A central social and moral code governing personal conduct, reputation, and social standing in the world of *Evelina*. The concept encompasses both internal virtue (personal integrity) and external reputation (social esteem), with characters constantly navigating the tension between the two.

## Definition

In the 18th-century context of *Evelina*, honour operates on two levels: the gentleman's code of personal honour (tied to rank and conduct) and the woman's reputation (tied to chastity and social propriety). For a young woman like Evelina, honour is virtually synonymous with reputation — a fragile possession that, once lost, can never be fully restored.

> His disavowal of his marriage with Miss Evelyn means she shall never, at the expense of her mother's honour, receive a part of her right.
>
> (004-005)

## Manifestations in the Novel

### Through Evelina

Evelina's journey through London society is fundamentally a negotiation of honour. Her innocence and lack of worldly knowledge constantly place her reputation at risk, while her innate virtue guides her through social pitfalls.

> Addressing Lord Orville with great respect, he said, "I beg pardon, my Lord — if I was — as I fear might be the case — rather too severe in my censure of the lady who is honoured with your protection."
>
> (012-013)

### Through Lord Orville

Lord Orville embodies the ideal of gentlemanly honour — courteous, protective of women's reputations, and acting from principle rather than self-interest. His conduct provides the novel's moral standard against which other male characters are measured.

> Lord Orville, whom we saw in the stage-box, came to us; and he honoured us with his company all the evening.
>
> (020-003)

### Through Sir Clement Willoughby

Sir Clement represents the corruption of honour — a man who uses the language and forms of gallantry to mask predatory intentions. His pursuit of Evelina repeatedly places her reputation in jeopardy.

## Key Passages

> And while he continues to persevere in disavowing his marriage with Miss Evelyn, she shall never, at the expense of her mother's honour, receive a part of her right as the donation of his bounty.
>
> (004-005)

## Critical Perspectives

In Burney's novel, honour serves as both a genuine moral compass and a tool of social control. The novel critiques the double standard by which men's honour is measured by their actions while women's honour is measured by their reputation — a reputation that can be destroyed by the actions of others regardless of the woman's own conduct.
