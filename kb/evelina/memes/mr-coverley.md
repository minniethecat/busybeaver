# Mr. Coverley

Mr. Coverley (referred to familiarly as "Jack Coverley") is a minor nobleman who appears in the Bristol/Clifton section of the novel (Letters LXII–LXXXII) as a companion of Lord Merton. He serves as a satirical portrait of the dissipated, wit-obsessed man of fashion whose sole accomplishments are reckless driving and crude humour. His most distinguishing characteristic is his verbal tic — "Egad" — and his most memorable scene is the "old women's race," a degrading wager that Burney uses to expose the casual cruelty of fashionable male society.

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Coverley appears exclusively in the Bristol section, and his function is almost entirely satirical. He is introduced through Lord Merton's mention that "Coverley has been driving against me as if he was mad: he has got two of the finest spirited horses I ever saw" (064-023). His first entrance into the narrative proper is dramatic: he arrives late to dinner "with a thousand apologies in a breath for being so late, but said he had been retarded by a little accident, for that he had overturned his phaeton, and broke it all to pieces" (064-030). Lady Louisa screams at this intelligence and "declared she would never go into a phaeton again" (064-030).

The reckless-driving motif establishes Coverley as the embodiment of the fashionable gentleman's indifference to danger — both his own and others'. When Lord Merton dismisses the accident with "never mind Jack Coverley; for he does not know how to drive" (064-031), Coverley immediately escalates: "I'll drive against you for a thousand pounds" (064-032). The exchange demonstrates the competitive recklessness that defines his social circle, where gambling on dangerous stunts is routine amusement.

## The Old Women's Race

Coverley's most significant — and most damning — scene is the "old women's race" in Letter LXVIII. He and Lord Merton, "both so excessively gay and noisy, that I soon found they had been free in drinking to their success" (068-013), stage a footrace between two elderly women for their amusement. "They handed, with loud shouts, the old women to the race-ground, and encouraged them by liberal promises" (068-013). The race ends with both women falling, "heavy and helpless, they had not been able to save themselves, but fell with their whole weight upon the gravel" (068-015).

When his contestant falls and cannot continue, Coverley's response reveals the brutality beneath the wit: "Mr. Coverley was quite brutal: he swore at her with unmanly rage, and seemed scarce able to refrain even from striking her" (068-018). The adverb "unmanly" here is the novel's judgement — true manhood, Burney implies, does not swear at injured old women. Later, "Mr. Coverley endeavoured, in vain, to conceal his chagrin" (068-020) at losing — his concern is entirely for his wager, not for the woman he has injured.

## Character Traits

Coverley is defined by two qualities: his reckless pursuit of amusement and his verbal mannerisms. His characteristic "Egad" punctuates nearly every utterance — "Egad, my Lord, your Lordship has a most remarkable odd way of taking things" (064-102), "Egad, I don't see what right you have to the best old, and the best young woman too, in the same day" (068-026), "Egad, the baronet has a mind to tip us a touch of the heroics this morning!" (077-036).

His wit, such as it is, consists of crude wordplay and sexual innuendo. Mrs. Selwyn, who tolerates no foolishness, identifies him as an easy target: "Pray, Mr. Coverley, why do you think it necessary to tell me this so often?" (077-044). When he boasts that he "never could resist a smart play upon words" (068-029), the irony is that his wordplay is anything but smart.

In the final comic confrontation with Captain Mirvan (Letter LXXXII), Coverley joins in the general mockery of Mr. Lovel, urging him toward a duel: "Send him a challenge, and I'll be your second" (082-138), "Lovel, you must certainly pink him: you must not put up with such an affront" (082-168) — all while knowing perfectly well that Lovel is a coward. His eagerness to see another man fight a duel he himself would never risk is the final stroke in Burney's portrait of the fashionable gentleman: all bravado, no substance.
