# Conduct

Conduct is the governing moral concept of [[Fanny Burney]]'s *[[Evelina]]* — a term that bridges the gap between outward behaviour and inner character. In the eighteenth-century lexicon, "conduct" carried more weight than mere manners: it implied the alignment of action with principle, the visible expression of moral worth. Every significant character in the novel is measured against this standard, and the plot itself is structured as [[Evelina]]'s education in what true conduct requires.

## Conduct vs. Politeness

The novel's central moral distinction is between genuine conduct and polished performance. [[Lord Orville]] embodies the former — his courtesy springs from real respect for others regardless of rank. [[Sir Clement Willoughby]] embodies the latter — his gallantry is a tool of manipulation, his politeness a mask for predation. This distinction structures the novel's critique of fashionable society: a world that rewards the appearance of virtue while permitting its absence.

> Conduct yourself towards her with all the respect and deference due to so near a relation, remembering always, that the failure of duty on her part, can by no means justify any neglect on yours.

## Rev. Villars as Moral Tutor

[[Rev. Villars]] is the novel's principal voice of conduct literature — his letters from [[Berry Hill]] form a sustained tutorial in moral behaviour. He teaches [[Evelina]] that conduct is not merely about following rules but about cultivating the disposition from which right action flows naturally. His counsel on how to treat the unworthy [[Madame Duval]] — with scrupulous respect despite her failure to deserve it — is the novel's hardest test of this principle.

## Conduct Literature Context

Burney's focus on conduct places *Evelina* within a major eighteenth-century genre: the conduct book, which prescribed behaviour for young women entering society. But the novel transforms the genre by testing its precepts against the messy realities of social life. [[Evelina]] does not simply learn rules; she discovers that the rules can be weaponized by the unscrupulous ([[Sir Clement Willoughby]]) and that the genuinely virtuous ([[Lord Orville]]) may be misjudged by those who follow only the letter of the code.

## The Novel's Verdict

The plot of *Evelina* ultimately vindicates the conduct-book tradition while exposing its limitations. [[Evelina]]'s innate virtue — nurtured at [[Berry Hill]], tested in [[London]] — proves stronger than the forces arrayed against her. But her trials also reveal how easily genuine conduct can be misread in a society that judges by appearances. The novel thus argues for a conduct that is both internal (rooted in character) and legible (visible to the discerning observer), recognizing that in a fallen social world, virtue must be not only possessed but performed.
