# Prudence

**Prudence** — the virtue of caution, discretion, and sound judgment — is a recurring moral standard in *Evelina* (1778). Burney presents prudence as the necessary companion to innocence, protecting virtuous characters from the snares of a dangerous social world.

## Prudence as Protection

Mr. Villars exemplifies prudence in his guardianship of Evelina. His careful management of her upbringing — concealing her true name, limiting her exposure to fashionable society — reflects a prudential philosophy. He balances "the desire that she should have justice done her" against "the apprehension that, while I improved her fortune, I should endanger her mind" (028-009).

## Innocence vs. Prudence

A central tension in the novel is between Evelina's natural innocence and the prudence demanded by society. Her artlessness repeatedly places her in compromising situations — from the ridotto to Vauxhall — where a more prudent young woman might have avoided danger. Burney does not condemn this innocence but dramatises its costs.

## Prudence and Gender

Prudence operates with particular force upon female characters. While men may act imprudently with relative impunity, women's reputation depends on constant prudential self-surveillance. Evelina's education in prudence is, in effect, her education in surviving as a woman in 18th-century society.
