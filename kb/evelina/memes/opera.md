# The Opera

The Italian opera at the Haymarket [[theatre|Theatre]] was the pinnacle of fashionable entertainment in eighteenth-century [[London]]. In [[Fanny Burney]]'s *[[Evelina]]*, the opera serves a function distinct from the [[assembly]] or the pleasure garden: it is a space of spectatorship where looking and being looked at are equally important, and where the drama on stage competes with the drama in the boxes.

## The Theatre of Fashion

At the opera, the audience itself is the spectacle. [[Evelina]]'s party secures "places in the front row of a side-box" — a position of maximum visibility. The expense and prominence of opera attendance made it a marker of fashion, and the [[conduct|behaviour]] of [[reputation|character]]s in their boxes reveals as much about them as any private conversation would. [[Sir Clement Willoughby]], [[reputation|character]]istically, positions himself to attend the ladies to the theatre, using the social ritual as cover for his pursuit.

> Our places were in the front row of a side-box. Sir Clement Willoughby, who knew our intention, was at the door of the theatre, and handed us from the carriage.
>
> (020-001)

## A Stage for Rivalry

The opera boxes create an intimate space where romantic rivalries intensify. Here [[Sir Clement Willoughby]] and [[Lord Orville]] share the confined quarters of the box with [[Evelina]], their competing attentions made more charged by the proximity. The arrival of other gentlemen — including the comic Mr. Smith, with his malapropisms and pretensions — adds further layers of social performance to an already overdetermined scene.

| **Opera** | Watching (and being watched) | Vertical (tiers of boxes, [[social-class|rank]]s) |
| [[Assembly]] | Dancing | Horizontal (on the floor, paired) |
| [[Ranelagh]] | Promenading, tea | Fluid (circulating, mingling) |
| [[Ridotto]] | Masked dancing | Concealed (identity play) |

## Music and Distraction

[[fanny-burney|Burney]], herself the daughter of a famous music historian, uses the opera to explore the tension between aesthetic attention and social obligation. Characters rarely attend to the music; the opera is a place to talk, flirt, and display oneself. This instrumentalization of art — the reduction of musical performance to social backdrop — is part of the novel's broader critique of a society that values appearance over substance, form over genuine [[conduct]].

## The Opera in Evelina's Education

For [[Evelina]], the opera is another [[social-class|class]]room in the education of a young woman entering society. She must learn not only how to behave but how to *appear* to behave — how to manage the scrutiny that falls on a beautiful young woman in a prominent box. Her discomfort at this visibility, her longing for the quiet of [[Berry Hill]], measures the distance between her innate [[modesty]] and the exhibitionism that fashionable life demands.
