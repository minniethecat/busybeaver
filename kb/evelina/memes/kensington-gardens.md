# Kensington Gardens

**Kensington Gardens**, adjacent to Hyde Park in [[london|London]], was a fashionable site for promenading and social display in the 18th century. In *[[evelina|Evelina]]* (1778), the gardens represent the public spaces where social performance and chance encounters shape the narrative.

## The Gardens in Context

Kensington Gardens, originally the private grounds of Kensington Palace, were opened to the public on select days, becoming a prime location for fashionable display. The broad avenues and formal plantings provided a stage where [[social-class|rank]], fashion, and [[reputation|character]] could be observed and judged — a perfect setting for [[fanny-burney|Burney]]'s social satire.

## Promenading Culture

The 18th-century promenade was a ritual of visibility. Walking in Kensington Gardens allowed [[reputation|character]]s to see and be seen, to encounter acquaintances and strangers, and to perform their social identities in public. For a novelist like [[fanny-burney|Burney]], such settings generate the chance meetings and misunderstandings that propel plot.
