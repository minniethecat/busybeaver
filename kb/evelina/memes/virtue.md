# Virtue

Virtue is the moral standard against which all characters in [[Fanny Burney]]'s *Evelina* are measured. It is the quality [[Rev. Villars]] has spent seventeen years cultivating in his ward — and it is the quality that [[Evelina]]'s encounters with London society continually threaten and ultimately confirm.

## A Gendered Standard

In the novel's moral universe, virtue is gendered. For Evelina, virtue means chastity, modesty, obedience, and the preservation of reputation. For [[Lord Orville]], virtue means honour, restraint, generosity, and the protection of those weaker than himself. The asymmetry is deliberate: Burney exposes the double standard that makes female virtue infinitely more fragile than its male counterpart.

Rev. Villars's most famous counsel makes this explicit: "nothing is so delicate as the reputation of a woman; it is at once the most beautiful and most brittle of all human things" (009-002).

## Virtue Tested

The novel's plot is structured as a series of tests of Evelina's virtue:

| Test | Threat | Outcome |
|------|--------|---------|
| [[Sir Clement Willoughby]]'s advances | Seduction through charm | Evelina recoils, recognizing insincerity |
| [[Madame Duval]]'s influence | Vulgarity and worldliness | Evelina maintains filial duty without adopting her values |
| The [[Vauxhall]] Dark Walk | Physical danger | Lord Orville's intervention preserves her safety |
| Lord Orville's ambiguous courtship | Emotional vulnerability | She guards her heart until his honour is proven |

## Virtue Rewarded

Unlike the tragic seduction narratives that preceded it, *Evelina* rewards virtue with happiness. Evelina's moral steadfastness is not merely preserved but publicly recognized — she is restored to her father, her birth, and the man she loves. The novel thus makes a case that virtue, properly understood as active moral judgment rather than passive innocence, can successfully navigate even the most treacherous social waters.
