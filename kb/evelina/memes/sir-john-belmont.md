# Sir John Belmont

Sir John Belmont is a baronet whose predatory conduct toward the young Miss Evelyn forms the dark pre-history of *Evelina*. He secretly married Caroline Evelyn (later Lady Belmont), brought her to England, burned the marriage certificate when her family refused him the expected fortune, and denied the union had ever taken place — leaving his pregnant wife destitute and disgraced. His eventual acknowledgement of a false daughter complicates the novel's climactic recognition plot. Where Lord Orville represents genuine aristocratic virtue and Sir Clement Willoughby the polished libertine, Sir John Belmont embodies the cruel consequences of unchecked male power: his actions destroy Lady Belmont, threaten Evelina's legitimacy, and drive the novel's central mystery of identity.

## Identity

- **Full Name**: John Belmont
- **Title/Rank**: Sir (Baronet)
- **Family**: Evelina Belmont (biological daughter), Lady Belmont (née Caroline Evelyn, deceased wife), Mr. Macartney (son), Polly Green (false daughter)
- **Residence**: London

## Role in the Novel

Sir John Belmont functions as the absent antagonist whose past actions generate the novel's plot. He appears in person only in the final volume (Letters LXXVII–LXXX), yet his shadow falls across the entire narrative. The secrecy surrounding Evelina's birth is Mr. Villars's direct response to Belmont's villainy; every restriction placed upon Evelina — her secluded upbringing, her false name, her ignorance of her parentage — is a consequence of Belmont's conduct.

The backstory, recounted by Mr. Villars in Letters XXVII–XXVIII, establishes Belmont as a "very profligate young man" who "had but too successfully found means to insinuate himself into" Miss Evelyn's favour (002-014). The marriage was secret, conducted "without a witness" (002-014). When Madame Duval and her family refused the fortune Belmont expected, "he infamously burnt the certificate of their marriage, and denied that they had ever been united" (002-014). Lady Belmont died shortly after giving birth, having been abandoned by her husband and her mother.

Belmont's reputation is consistently described in the harshest terms throughout the novel. Lady Howard calls him "that abandoned man" (003-008). Mr. Villars refers to "the villany of Sir John Belmont" (028-004) and describes him as "her destroyer" (028-005), asking rhetorically: "Could I feel an affection the most paternal for this poor sufferer, and not abominate her destroyer?" (028-005). Mrs. Selwyn, in the climactic confrontation, compares his actions to those of "Nero or Caligula" (077-127), suggesting his crimes exceed even those of history's most notorious tyrants.

The novel's action is set in motion when Madame Duval, stirred by ambition rather than maternal guilt, proposes a lawsuit to force Belmont's acknowledgement. Mr. Villars counsels against it, noting that "Sir John Belmont would still have it in his power, and, if irritated, no doubt in his inclination, to cut off her granddaughter with a shilling" (028-018). The lawsuit is abandoned in favour of direct appeal: Lady Howard writes to Belmont (Letter XXXI), and he responds with a brief, dismissive reply signed simply "John Belmont" (035-010) — a signature that, in its lack of ceremony, conveys contempt.

## The Two Letters

Two letters addressed to Sir John Belmont illuminate the moral stakes of the plot.

### Lady Howard's Appeal (Letter XXXI)

Lady Howard writes, urging Belmont to acknowledge his daughter publicly:

> "Believe me, Sir, she merits your utmost attention and regard. You could not see and know her, and remain unmoved by those sensations of affection which belong to so near and tender a relationship. She is the lovely resemblance of her lovely mother;—pardon, Sir, the liberty I take in mentioning that unfortunate lady; but I think it behoves me, upon this occasion, to show the esteem I felt for her: allow me, therefore, to say, and be not offended at my freedom, that the memory of that excellent lady has but too long remained under the aspersions of calumny; surely it is time to vindicate her fame" (031-009).

### Lady Belmont's Final Letter (Letter LXXIV)

The most devastating document in the novel is Lady Belmont's deathbed letter, written in the hours before she died giving birth to Evelina. It reveals that Belmont had already remarried and fathered another child — the novel's subplot of Mr. Macartney:

> "In the firm hope that the moment of anguish which approaches will prove the period of my sufferings, once more I address myself to Sir John Belmont, in behalf of the child, who, if it survives its mother, will hereafter be the bearer of this letter" (074-003).

She appeals to his conscience with prophetic warning:

> "Listen, Oh Belmont, to their dictates! reprobate not your child, though you have reprobated its mother. The evils that are past, perhaps, when too late, you may wish to recall; the young creature you have persecuted, perhaps, when too late, you may regret that you have destroyed" (074-009).

And with remarkable compassion for her destroyer:

> "Oh, Belmont, all my resentment softens into pity at the thought! what will become of thee, good Heaven, when, with the eye of penitence, thou reviewest thy past conduct!" (074-009).

The letter is signed "Caroline Belmont" (074-019) — a name she claimed in life and death, though Belmont had denied it to her.

## The False Daughter: Polly Green

The novel's most bizarre complication is the discovery that the woman known as "Miss Belmont" at Clifton — the acknowledged daughter and heiress of Sir John Belmont — is not Evelina at all. A nurse named Dame Green, who had attended Lady Belmont in her last illness, conveyed her own child to Sir John Belmont, claiming the infant was Lady Belmont's (078-044). Belmont, "unwilling at that time to confirm the rumour of my being married" (077-127), sent mother and child to France. The girl was educated in a convent and only recently brought to England as "Miss Belmont."

Dame Green's motive was social ambition for her daughter: "All her hope was to have had her daughter married before it took place; for which reason she had so much promoted Mr. Macartney's addresses; for though such a match was inadequate to the pretensions of Miss Belmont, she well knew it was far superior to those her daughter could form after the discovery of her birth" (078-044).

## The Confrontation and Resolution

The climactic scene occurs when Mrs. Selwyn visits Belmont at his London house. She reports their interview in detail (077-127). Belmont initially dismisses the claim as "that ridiculous old story" (077-127), but Mrs. Selwyn's blistering condemnation — comparing his actions to "Nero or Caligula" — forces him to reveal the truth about the false daughter. The detail of Belmont ringing for his hairdresser in the middle of this confrontation is a masterly satirical touch: even when his most terrible crimes are laid bare, his first concern is his appearance.

Belmont's eventual response is partial but real. He acknowledges Evelina, provides her with a £30,000 settlement (079-024), and agrees to the double marriage scheme that gives both his true daughter and the unfortunate Polly Green a path to respectability. Lord Orville's insistence that Polly "should be considered, indeed, as my sister, and as the co-heiress of my father" (080-058) ensures that Belmont's belated justice extends even to the victim of his negligence.

## Character Traits

Sir John Belmont is defined by absence and silence. He appears in exactly one scene and speaks only a handful of lines. His power is exerted through his past actions, his social position, and the legal and social structures that permit a man of his rank to destroy a woman's reputation with impunity. He is not charming like Sir Clement, nor actively cruel in the manner of a Gothic villain; his evil is the cold, bureaucratic evil of denial — the burnt certificate, the refused acknowledgement, the convenient fiction of a substitute daughter.

His eventual repentance, such as it is, is brought about not by conscience but by exposure. Mrs. Selwyn's threat to "make his conduct as public as it was infamous" (077-127) is what compels his cooperation. The novel does not ask the reader to forgive Sir John Belmont; it asks only that his victims be granted the justice of recognition.
