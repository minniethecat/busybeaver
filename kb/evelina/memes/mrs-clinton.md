# Mrs. Clinton

Mrs. Clinton is the housekeeper at Berry Hill, Rev. Arthur Villars's estate, and Evelina's former nurse. A "most worthy woman" (004-010) of steadfast loyalty and good sense, she serves as Evelina's trusted attendant throughout the novel's journeys between Berry Hill, Howard Grove, London, and Bristol. Though a minor character, she plays a crucial role in the novel's resolution by proposing the theory that unravels the mystery of Sir John Belmont's long denial of Evelina's parentage.

## Identity

- **Full Name**: Mrs. Clinton (first name not given)
- **Title/Rank**: Mrs.
- **Role**: Housekeeper at Berry Hill; formerly Evelina's nurse
- **Residence**: Berry Hill, the estate of Rev. Arthur Villars

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Clinton serves as Evelina's travelling companion and guardian figure whenever Evelina moves between locations. Rev. Villars entrusts Evelina's first journey to Howard Grove to Mrs. Clinton: "Mrs. Clinton, a most worthy woman, formerly her nurse, and now my housekeeper, shall attend her thither next week" (004-010). She is repeatedly described with affection and trust: Evelina exclaims "O how happy shall I be, when the worthy Mrs. Clinton arrives!" (050-007), and Villars later writes "Mrs. Clinton can take sufficient care of you" (067-016).

Mrs. Clinton's most significant contribution occurs late in the novel when, upon learning of Evelina's situation with Sir John Belmont, "an idea occurred to her which seemed to clear up all the mystery of my having been so long disowned" (078-031). She recalls that the nurse who had claimed to bring Belmont's child to him — the supposed daughter he had acknowledged — had suddenly disappeared from the neighbourhood: "Mrs. Clinton remembers, that her quitting the place appeared, at the time, very extraordinary to the neighbours; but, as she was never heard of afterwards, she was by degrees quite forgotten" (078-032).

This recollection prompts Mrs. Selwyn to investigate, leading to the discovery that Belmont had been deceived by a substitute child — the true-born daughter having died, the nurse had passed off her own infant as Belmont's legitimate heir (078-033). Mrs. Selwyn, "attended by Mrs. Clinton" (078-035), interrogates the nurse and eventually extracts a confession, clearing the way for Evelina to be acknowledged as Belmont's true daughter.

## Character Traits

Mrs. Clinton is defined by her reliability, discretion, and quiet competence. Villars's description of her as "a most worthy woman" (004-010) is consistently borne out. She provides steady, unobtrusive support — accompanying Evelina on difficult journeys, caring for her during emotional distress ("So great was the depression of my spirits upon the road, that it was with great difficulty I could persuade the worthy Mrs. Clinton I was not ill" [057-008]), and remaining attentive to her charge's wellbeing throughout.

Her most notable trait is her long memory and observant nature: it is Mrs. Clinton's recollection of the neighbourhood gossip from many years earlier — the mysterious disappearance of the nurse — that provides the crucial clue which unravels Belmont's years-long error (078-031, 078-032). This combination of quiet loyalty and sharp observation makes her, though a minor character, indispensable to the plot's resolution.

## Key Relationships

### With Rev. Arthur Villars

Mrs. Clinton is Villars's trusted housekeeper and the executor of his domestic arrangements. Villars relies on her to escort Evelina safely between locations (004-010, 049-010, 055-003). The mutual trust between them is evident in Villars's repeated willingness to place Evelina in Mrs. Clinton's care.

### With Evelina

Mrs. Clinton served as Evelina's nurse in infancy and remains her devoted attendant throughout the novel. Evelina consistently refers to her as "the worthy Mrs. Clinton" (050-007, 057-008) and relies on her for emotional as well as practical support. Their relationship, though that of servant and mistress, is marked by genuine affection and mutual respect.

### With Mrs. Selwyn

In the final volume, Mrs. Clinton accompanies Mrs. Selwyn to Bristol and becomes her investigative partner. Mrs. Selwyn recognizes the value of Mrs. Clinton's recollection and acts upon it immediately: "Mrs. Selwyn determined immediately to discover the truth or mistake of this conjecture; therefore, the moment she had dined, she walked to the Hot Wells, attended by Mrs. Clinton" (078-035). Their collaboration leads directly to the novel's climactic revelation.

## Significant Appearances

- **Letter IV**: First mentioned as Evelina's escort to Howard Grove (004-010)
- **Letter XLIX–LVII**: Travels between Berry Hill and London to collect Evelina; delivers letters from Villars to Madame Duval (049-010, 050-007, 055-003, 056-003, 057-004, 057-006, 057-008)
- **Letter LXI**: Accompanying Evelina at Bristol (061-006)
- **Letter LXXVIII**: Her crucial recollection of the nurse's disappearance, leading to Mrs. Selwyn's investigation and the exposure of the substituted-child plot (078-031, 078-032, 078-033, 078-035, 078-038)

## Narrative Significance

Mrs. Clinton exemplifies the reliable servant figure of eighteenth-century fiction — loyal, discreet, and observant. Her narrative function is that of a facilitator: she enables Evelina's physical movement between locations and, more importantly, provides the key piece of memory that unlocks the novel's central mystery. Without Mrs. Clinton's long recollection of the neighbourhood gossip, the truth about Sir John Belmont's deceived acknowledgment might never have been discovered, and the novel's comic resolution — the restoration of Evelina to her rightful name and station — would have been impossible.
