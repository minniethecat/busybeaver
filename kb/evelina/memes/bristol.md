# Bristol Hotwells

[[hot-wells|Bristol Hotwells]] is the spa resort where the climactic events of [[Fanny Burney]]'s *[[evelina|Evelina]]* unfold. It is here, away from the fashionable whirl of [[london|London]], that [[Evelina]] is reunited with her father, Sir John Belmont, and where her romance with [[Lord Orville]] reaches its resolution.

## The Hotwells

The Hotwells were a fashionable spa in the eighteenth century, known for their medicinal waters. Situated on the Avon Gorge near Bristol, they attracted invalids and society figures seeking health and diversion — a natural setting for the novel's final act, where physical healing and social restoration run in parallel.

## Role in the Novel

Bristol represents a shift in the novel's geography and mood. [[london|London]] is the arena of [[evelina|Evelina]]'s exposure to society — its balls, theatres, and [[assembly|assemblies]] test her [[virtue]] at every turn. Bristol, by contrast, is where the tangled threads of her identity are unraveled:

- **Mrs. Selwyn** accompanies [[evelina|Evelina]] to Bristol, providing the sharp-[[fashion|ton]]gued chaperonage that protects her from further social predation
- **Sir John Belmont**, [[evelina|Evelina]]'s father, resides near Bristol — and it is here that the long-deferred recognition scene occurs
- **[[lord-orville|Lord Orville]]** follows [[evelina|Evelina]] to Bristol, confirming the sincerity of his attachment

## The Recognition Scene

The reunion with Sir John Belmont is the novel's [[sentiment|emotion]]al turning point. After seventeen years of separation — and Belmont's rejection of [[evelina|Evelina]] on first seeing her in [[london|London]] — father and daughter are finally reconciled. [[evelina|Evelina]]'s restoration to her true identity as [[evelina|Evelina Belmont]] resolves the ambiguity that has shadowed her since birth.
