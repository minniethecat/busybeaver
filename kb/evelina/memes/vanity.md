# Vanity

**Vanity** — excessive pride in one's appearance, accomplishments, or social standing — is a recurring object of Burney's satire in *Evelina* (1778). The novel anatomises vanity across classes and genders, exposing it as both comic folly and moral failing.

## The Vanity of Fashion

The fashionable world of London is presented as a theatre of vanity. Characters perform elaborately for one another's regard, their self-worth dependent on the opinions of others. Evelina's outsider perspective — her innocence of fashionable vanity — makes her an effective vehicle for satirising this social theatre.

## Male Vanity

Burney is particularly sharp on male vanity. Sir Clement Willoughby's elaborate self-regard, Captain Mirvan's crude boastfulness, and even the foppish minor characters all illustrate how vanity distorts male behaviour. These portraits suggest that vanity is not merely a feminine vice but a universal human failing.

## Vanity vs. Virtue

The novel consistently opposes vanity to genuine virtue. Characters who are most concerned with appearances — whether of wealth, rank, or fashion — are morally the most suspect. True worth, Burney suggests, is demonstrated through conduct rather than self-display.
