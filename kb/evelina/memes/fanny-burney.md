# Fanny Burney

Frances "Fanny" Burney (1752–1840) is the author of *Evelina, or the History of a Young Lady's Entrance into the World* — the epistolary novel that serves as the source corpus for this wiki. Published anonymously in 1778, *Evelina* was an immediate sensation, admired by figures from Samuel Johnson to Edmund Burke, and established Burney as one of the most significant novelists of the late eighteenth century.

## Identity

- **Full Name**: Frances Burney (later Madame d'Arblay)
- **Title/Rank**: Madame (after 1793 marriage to General Alexandre d'Arblay)
- **Family**: Dr. Charles Burney (father, noted music historian), Esther Sleepe Burney (mother, died 1762)
- **Residence**: London, England; Paris, France (after marriage)

## Literary Significance

Burney's *Evelina* transformed the novel of manners. By telling the story through [[Evelina]]'s own letters, Burney created an intimate, psychologically acute portrait of a young woman's social education that was unprecedented in English fiction. The novel's satire of fashionable society — embodied in figures like [[Madame Duval]], [[Captain Mirvan]], and [[Sir Clement Willoughby]] — established the template that Jane Austen would later refine.

## Evelina's Creation

*Evelina* was written in secret — Burney was only twenty-six, and the novel was published without her father's knowledge. Its success was immediate and transformative. The character of [[Evelina]] herself — innocent, observant, and morally serious — modeled a new kind of heroine: not the idealized paragon of sentimental fiction but a psychologically believable young woman whose virtues are tested by real social pressures.

The character of [[Rev. Villars]], Evelina's guardian, may reflect Burney's own relationship with her father, Dr. Charles Burney — a figure of moral and intellectual authority whose approval she both sought and feared.
