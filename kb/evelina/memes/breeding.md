# Breeding

**Breeding** — encompassing upbringing, manners, and social formation — is a key marker of quality in *Evelina* (1778). Burney distinguishes between breeding as innate refinement and breeding as mere social training, using the concept to critique class pretensions.

## Natural vs. Acquired Breeding

The novel consistently values natural refinement over acquired polish. Evelina, raised in rural seclusion, possesses an innate grace that outshines the studied manners of fashionable London. Her "natural apprehension and good sense" (006-009) produces true politeness, whereas characters who have merely acquired the forms of breeding lack its substance.

## Breeding and Class

Breeding serves as a class signifier but also as a vehicle for social critique. The Branghtons, with their vulgar manners despite material comfort, demonstrate that breeding is not simply a function of wealth. Conversely, Lord Orville's breeding reflects not just his rank but his character.

## The Branghton Counter-Example

The Branghton family provides the novel's most sustained portrait of deficient breeding. Their crude manners, mercenary values, and social clumsiness contrast sharply with Evelina's natural refinement, illustrating that breeding is a moral as much as a social quality.
