# England

The primary geographical setting of the novel. Most of the action takes place in and around [[london|London]], with excursions to [[bristol|Bristol]], [[bath|Bath]], and various country estates. England serves as a cultural and moral landscape against which [[reputation|character]]s from France — particularly [[madame-duval|Madame Duval]] — are measured.

## Overview

- **Type**: country
- **Location**: Britain
- **Period**: 18th century (Georgian era)

## Description in the Novel

England is most vividly rendered through [[evelina|Evelina]]'s experience of [[london|London]] society: its public gardens, [[assembly-rooms|assembly]] rooms, theatres, and the intricate codes of politeness that govern fashionable life. The novel contrasts rural England ([[berry-hill|Berry Hill]], [[howard-grove|Howard Grove]]) with the dangers and excitements of the capital. [[madame-duval|Madame Duval]], arriving from France, declares she "had been in England only two days" when [[evelina|Evelina]] first encounters her at the theatre (014-017), and she proceeds to denounce English [[breeding|manners]] at every opportunity — complaining that "in England" women are forced to work for social notice in ways unnecessary in [[paris|Paris]] (016-021).

## Significance to the Plot

England is the site of [[evelina|Evelina]]'s entrance into society and all the trials, humiliations, and triumphs that follow. Lady Howard's initial letters establish the expectation of a journey to England by [[madame-duval|Madame Duval]], whose "family occurrences have still detained her in France" (001-005). [[rev-villars|Mr. Villars]] reflects that he had hoped [[evelina|Evelina]] "would, ere now, have bid adieu to [[london|London]]" and returned to the safety of [[berry-hill|Berry Hill]] (015-003). The novel's moral geography positions rural England — [[berry-hill|Berry Hill]] in particular — as a place of [[virtue]] and safety, while [[london|London]] represents both opportunity and moral peril (007-006).

## Social Context

The novel participates in a broader 18th-century discourse contrasting English liberty and simplicity with French artifice. [[madame-duval|Madame Duval]]'s Francophilia — her constant invocations of [[paris|Paris]] as the standard of elegance — is presented as absurd and affected. England, by implication, is the domain of genuine [[sentiment|feeling]] and moral seriousness, even if its social forms can be perilous for an inexperienced young woman.
