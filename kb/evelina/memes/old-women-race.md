# Old Women's Race

The old women's race is a grimly memorable episode near the end of *[[evelina|Evelina]]*, in which [[lord-orville|Lord]] Mer[[fashion|ton]] and Mr. Coverley resolve a substantial wager by pitting two octogenarian women against each other in a footrace. The spectacle takes place in Mrs. Beaumont's garden at [[clifton|Clifton]] and serves as one of the novel's sharpest critiques of aristocratic dissipation.

## Overview

- **Type**: wager race (gaming entertainment)
- **Venue**: a gravel-walk in Mrs. Beaumont's garden, [[clifton|Clifton]]
- **Stakes**: reduced from £1,000 to £100
- **Contestants**: two women, each over eighty years of age

## The Event

The race originates in a bet between [[lord-orville|Lord]] Mer[[fashion|ton]] and Mr. Coverley, two fashionable gentlemen. [[evelina|Evelina]] learns that "the parties had been prevailed upon to lower the sum from one thousand to one hundred pounds; and that they had agreed it should be determined by a race between two old women, one of whom was to be chosen by each side, and both were to be proved more than eighty years of age, though, in other respects strong and healthy as possible" (050-009). [[lord-orville|Lord Orville]] regards the scheme with distaste; [[evelina|Evelina]], for her part, is "surprised at this extraordinary method of spending so much money" (050-009).

On the appointed Thursday, the company assembles in Mrs. Beaumont's garden: "The place marked out for the race, was a gravel-walk in Mrs. Beaumont's garden, and the length of the ground twenty yards. When we were summoned to the *course*, the two poor old women made their appearance" (055-007). [[evelina|Evelina]]'s reaction is immediate compassion: "Though they seemed very healthy for their time of life, they yet looked so weak, so infirm, so feeble, that I could feel no sensation but that of [[compassion|pity]] at the sight" (055-007). [[lord-orville|Lord Orville]] — alone among the spectators — "looked very grave during the whole transaction" (055-007).

The first attempt collapses into farce: "the poor creatures, feeble and frightened, ran against each other: and, neither of them able to support the shock, they both fell on the ground" (055-009). After wine and recovery, they try again, hobbling and tottering to the "inexpressible diversion of the company" (055-011). The race ends when one of the women slips and falls heavily. Mr. Coverley, furious at losing, "swore at her with unmanly rage, and seemed scarce able to refrain even from striking her" (055-015). [[lord-orville|Lord]] Mer[[fashion|ton]], triumphant, declares it "a hollow thing."

## Significance

The old women's race is the moral nadir of the novel's social world. Where earlier entertainments — the [[ridotto]], the opera, the [[assembly-rooms|assembly]] — exposed [[evelina|Evelina]] to the perils of vanity and impertinence, this episode reveals something darker: the capacity of the fashionable world to treat human suffering as sport. Two elderly, impoverished women are made to run on gravel for the amusement of drunken aristocrats; when one falls and cannot rise, her patron curses her rather than helping.

[[lord-orville|Lord Orville]]'s grave silence throughout marks him as the moral centre: his refusal to participate in the general laughter aligns him with [[evelina|Evelina]]'s instinctive [[compassion|pity]] and against the callousness of his own [[social-class|class]]. The race also exposes [[lord-orville|Lord]] Mer[[fashion|ton]]'s [[reputation|character]] without disguise — his "freedom of [[gallantry|gallant]]ry" toward [[evelina|Evelina]] immediately afterwards, "regardless of the presence of Lady Louisa" (055-019), confirms the connection between cruelty to the powerless and disrespect toward women.
