# Rev. Arthur Villars

The Reverend Arthur Villars is the moral anchor of [[Fanny Burney]]'s *Evelina*: the clergyman who raises the orphaned [[Evelina]] from infancy and whose letters provide the ethical commentary that frames her adventures. Though he never appears in London, his presence is felt on every page — every letter Evelina writes is addressed to him, and every decision she makes is measured against the principles he has instilled.

## Identity

- **Full Name**: Arthur Villars
- **Title/Rank**: Reverend (Rev.)
- **Family**: Mrs. Villars (wife, deceased)
- **Residence**: Berry Hill, Dorsetshire

## Role as Guardian

Rev. Villars took charge of Evelina when her mother, Lady Belmont, died in childbirth. He describes his bond with his ward in terms of paternal love: "She loved me as her father; nor was Mrs. Villars less valued by her; while to me she became so dear, that her loss was little less afflicting than that which I have since sustained of Mrs. Villars herself" (001-003).

His decision to allow Evelina to accompany the Mirvans to London is made with characteristic prudence. He warns Lady Howard: "I apprehend nothing more than too much raising her hopes and her views, which the natural vivacity of her disposition would render but too easy to effect" (003-001). His reluctance reflects not mistrust of Evelina but a deep understanding of the dangers facing a beautiful, penniless young woman in fashionable society.

## Philosophical Guidance

Villars's letters articulate the novel's moral philosophy. His most famous counsel warns: "nothing is so delicate as the reputation of a woman; it is at once the most beautiful and most brittle of all human things" (009-002). When he learns of Evelina's growing attachment to [[Lord Orville]], his response blends tenderness with severity: "Long, and with the deepest regret, have I perceived the ascendancy which Lord Orville has gained upon your mind" (078-001).

His metaphor of Imagination and Reason — "Imagination took the reins; and Reason, slow-paced, though surefooted, was unequal to the race of so eccentric and flighty a companion" (078-002) — captures the central tension of the novel between feeling and judgment.

## Narrative Function

Villars is the novel's epistolary anchor — every significant event is filtered through Evelina's reports to him, and his replies provide the moral interpretation. This structure allows Burney to maintain dramatic immediacy in Evelina's London scenes while ensuring the reader never loses sight of the ethical framework against which those scenes should be judged.

At the novel's conclusion, when Evelina is restored to her father, she reaffirms her bond with Villars: "You are the Parent my heart acknowledges, and to you do I vow eternal duty, gratitude, and affection" (084-002).
