# Modesty

Modesty is, along with gentleness, what [[Rev. Villars]] calls "the peculiar attributes of your sex" (084-001). In the moral universe of [[Fanny Burney]]'s *Evelina*, modesty is not merely a social grace — it is the visible sign of inner [[virtue]], the quality that distinguishes the true heroine from the coquette, the vulgarian, and the merely fashionable.

## A Public Virtue

Modesty in *Evelina* is fundamentally public. It is demonstrated through blushes, downcast eyes, reluctance to speak of oneself, and — most importantly — the refusal to put oneself forward in mixed company. Evelina's constant embarrassment, her "confusion," and her inability to accept compliments are not weaknesses in the novel's moral scheme; they are proofs of her virtue. A woman who is too comfortable with public attention has, by definition, lost her modesty.

## Modesty and Class

The novel complicates the relationship between modesty and social rank. [[Madame Duval]], though Evelina's grandmother, is immodest in her speech, dress, and conduct — her vulgarity is presented as a form of moral coarseness that corresponds to her low birth. Yet [[Lord Orville]], the highest-ranking character, is also the most genuinely modest: his delicacy and restraint are the natural expressions of true nobility. Burney thus distinguishes between the arrogance of purchased rank and the modesty that accompanies genuine worth.

## The Danger of False Modesty

Not all modesty in the novel is genuine. [[Sir Clement Willoughby]] uses a performance of deference to gain access and trust. His "modesty" is a tool of seduction — a reminder that in a society where virtue is judged by externals, appearances can be weaponized. Evelina's task is to learn to distinguish the performance from the reality.
