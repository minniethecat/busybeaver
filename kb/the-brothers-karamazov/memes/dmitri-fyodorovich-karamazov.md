# Dmitri Fyodorovich Karamazov (Mitya)

The eldest son of Fyodor Pavlovitch Karamazov by his first wife Adelaida Ivanovna, a sensual, impulsive, and fundamentally honorable man whose violent passions entangle him in a catastrophe that culminates in a false accusation of parricide.

## Identity and Appearance

Dmitri Fyodorovich Karamazov, called Mitya, is the firstborn son of Fyodor Pavlovitch. At the time of the novel's main events he is twenty-seven years old (006-001), eight years older than Alyosha.

Dmitri is "a young man of eight and twenty" -- with "a face that seemed surprisingly youthful" (014-001). He has a powerful physical presence: "Mitya put his powerful hand under his elbow to help him jump" (020-008), and is described as having "uncontrolled, spontaneous" energy (028-014). He is a former military officer, giving him a commanding bearing despite his disordered life.

His childhood was tragic. His mother Adelaida Ivanovna abandoned the family when Mitya was three (003-003), and his father "had, in fact, quite forgotten" him, leaving him to the care of the servant Grigory (004-001, 004-004). He inherited property left by his mother, but was consistently cheated of his proper share by his father (004-003, 004-004).

By the time of the novel, Dmitri has left military service and is engaged in a bitter financial and romantic rivalry with his father. He "was in open quarrel with his father and even planning to bring an action against him" concerning his inheritance (005-012).

## Character Traits

Dmitri is defined by passionate extremes. He is "the only one" among the "wrangling, quarrelsome party" at the monastery who commanded a kind of rough respect -- even his father admits "Dmitri was the only one who could respect" (007-016). Despite his violence of temperament, "Dmitri was much perturbed" by the disorder he caused (007-016).

His most revealing self-description is the famous confession to Alyosha: he speaks of the "broad Karamazov nature" that can simultaneously contain extremes -- "the ideal of the Madonna and the ideal of Sodom." He quotes Schiller's poetry with tears, revealing a sensitive, idealistic soul beneath the rough, debauched exterior. He is drawn to both the highest spiritual beauty and the depths of sensual degradation.

Dmitri is capable of extraordinary tenderness and self-sacrifice. When he first met Katerina Ivanovna, he gave her money to save her father from disgrace and expected nothing in return. This act of spontaneous generosity redeems him even as his later conduct causes suffering.

His impulsiveness is his fatal flaw. He often acts without reflection, then is consumed by remorse. He "cried suddenly" (014-012), "broke into uncontrolled, spontaneous" laughter (028-014), and reacts with the immediacy of a child. His father notes: "he may be honest, our Mitya (he is stupid, but honest)" (015-034).

## Story Arc

### Major Plot Involvement

Dmitri's entry into the novel proper is dramatically delayed. At the gathering in the elder's cell, everyone waits for him: "more than half-past twelve, but Dmitri, on whose account they had all assembled, was still absent" (013-001). When he finally arrives, "the guest so long expected, Dmitri Fyodorovitch, came in" and immediately confronts his father over money and Grushenka (013-037).

Dmitri is engaged to Katerina Ivanovna Verkhovtsev, a woman of high social standing and moral rectitude, but he has fallen desperately in love with Grushenka Svetlov, a kept woman whom his father also lusts after. The father-son rivalry over Grushenka is the central erotic conflict. Fyodor Pavlovitch screams: "He dared to call her!" -- and accuses Dmitri of abandoning his betrothed (014-042).

Desperate for money to run away with Grushenka, Dmitri borrows from Katerina Ivanovna and others. When all attempts fail and he believes Grushenka has gone to his father, he rushes to Fyodor Pavlovitch's house intending violence. He strikes the servant Grigory with a brass pestle (leaving him unconscious), but does not kill his father.

Fyodor Pavlovitch is murdered that night by Smerdyakov. All circumstantial evidence points to Dmitri: his threats, his desperation for money, his presence at the house, and the sudden appearance of money for the Mokroe orgy. He is arrested, tried, and wrongly convicted of parricide.

At Mokroe on the night of the murder, Dmitri experiences a strange spiritual awakening. In his dream of a burning village and a crying baby, he feels an overwhelming sense of compassion for all suffering humanity -- the beginnings of his moral transformation.

### Key Dialogues and Conflicts

Dmitri's confession to Alyosha ("The Confession of an Ardent Heart") is the most extended character revelation in the novel. He recites Schiller's "Ode to Joy," then graphically describes the Karamazov sensuality -- the simultaneous attraction to beauty and degradation. "Beauty is a terrible and awful thing!" he cries.

His confrontations with his father are savage. Fyodor Pavlovitch yells "Dmitri! Mitya!" and the two nearly come to blows over Grushenka and money (014-032, 014-038). The hereditary Karamazov sensuality makes them rivals for the same woman, literalizing the Oedipal conflict.

At his trial, Dmitri maintains his innocence of the murder while freely confessing his moral guilt -- his desire to kill his father, his debauchery, his violence. This paradox is central to his character: he is legally innocent but spiritually complicit.

## Thematic Significance

Dmitri embodies the "broad" Russian nature that Dostoevsky saw as both the nation's tragedy and its potential redemption. He is the sensual Karamazov -- driven by passion, capable of both great cruelty and great tenderness, never calculating but always immediate. His trajectory from the "insect" of sensuality toward the dawning of moral consciousness (the dream of the babe) represents Dostoevsky's belief that suffering can be a path to redemption.

Dmitri is the Christ-figure of the novel in an unexpected sense: he is the innocent who takes on the punishment that is metaphorically deserved by all. His false conviction for a crime he did not commit (though he wished it) makes him a scapegoat, while his acceptance of suffering in Siberia points toward resurrection.

He is the most fully embodied of the three brothers -- Alyosha tends toward the spiritual, Ivan toward the intellectual, but Dmitri is all flesh and blood, passion and action. In Dostoevsky's moral universe, this means he is both the most dangerous and the most redeemable.
