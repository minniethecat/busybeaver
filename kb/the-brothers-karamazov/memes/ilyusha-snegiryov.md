# Ilyusha Snegiryov

The son of the destitute retired Captain Snegiryov, Ilyusha is a proud, sensitive boy whose fierce defense of his father's honor sets in motion the novel's subplot of childhood suffering, reconciliation, and redemptive memory.

## Identity and Appearance

Ilyusha, also called Ilusha, is the young son of Nikolay Ilyitch Snegiryov, a retired captain fallen into poverty and disgrace (036-016, 036-013). He is a schoolboy of the preparatory class in the local school, and his family consists of his father, his mother (Madame Snegiryov), and his sister (081-036). He lives in a poor lodging at a considerable distance from the town center (079-130). The narrator introduces him as "the boy stabbed with a pen-knife" by Kolya Krassotkin (077-012).

## Character Traits

Ilyusha is defined by fierce pride and loyalty to his father. When his father is publicly humiliated and dragged by the beard by Dmitri Karamazov, Ilyusha is devastated. He had been defending his father against the taunts of his schoolmates, and his response to this family shame is both a moral sensitivity unusual in a child and a consuming anguish (077-012).

The captain describes how Ilyusha's spirit was stirred by the humiliation: "An ordinary boy, a weak son, would have submitted, and have felt ashamed of his father, but he threw himself on every one and defended his father" (037-013). He is "a gallant spirit" in his father's eyes (037-013). Their walks together, hand in hand, are tenderly recalled by the captain in one of the novel's most heart-breaking passages (037-014).

Ilyusha's illness is a crucial aspect of his portrayal. He is consumed by consumption (tuberculosis) and also by guilt -- believing he caused the death of the dog Zhutchka after throwing a piece of bread with a pin in it to the hungry animal (080-014). He confesses tearfully: "It's because I killed Zhutchka, father, that I am ill now. God is punishing me for it" (080-014). This religious-moral sensitivity reveals a deeply conscientious nature.

## Story Arc

### Major Plot Involvement

Ilyusha first enters the narrative indirectly: Alyosha encounters a group of schoolboys throwing stones, one of whom has bitten his finger. This is Ilyusha, acting out of rage at the world after his father's humiliation (033-014, 034-020).

Alyosha seeks out the Snegiryov family and encounters the captain, who recounts the devastating effect the public disgrace has had on Ilyusha (036-029, 037-003). The father describes their walk along the town wall, a beautiful and lonely spot, and the pathos of Ilyusha asking if there are any good people left on earth (037-015).

Ilyusha falls gravely ill. Alyosha visits the boy and brings him comfort, becoming a beloved figure to him. The schoolboys, initially his tormentors, are reconciled to him through Alyosha's mediation. Kolya Krassotkin, the leader of the boys and the one who had stabbed Ilyusha, eventually comes to visit the dying boy, bringing his dog Perezvon which they pretend is the resurrected Zhutchka (081-029, 081-031).

Ilyusha dies, and his funeral becomes the occasion for Alyosha's famous speech at the stone, in which he urges the assembled boys to cherish the memory of Ilyusha and of their shared love, declaring that "nothing is higher and stronger and more wholesome for life in the future than some good memory, especially one brought from childhood."

### Key Dialogues and Conflicts

The central conflict is Ilyusha's relationship with his schoolmates. The boys taunt him about his father, and Ilyusha, in his desperation, stabs Kolya Krassotkin with a pen-knife (033-014, 037-010). This act isolates him and deepens his misery. Alyosha's intervention -- bringing the boys together and facilitating reconciliation -- is one of the novel's most redemptive arcs.

The Zhutchka episode forms a powerful emotional core. Ilyusha's guilt over the dog, his frantic joy when Kolya brings Perezvon claiming it is Zhutchka, and his final moments of happiness before death constitute one of the novel's most affecting sequences (081-029, 081-031, 081-032).

## Thematic Significance

Ilyusha embodies the novel's central trope of innocent suffering -- the tears of children that Ivan cites in his rebellion against God. His illness, poverty, and the psychological torment he endures from his father's disgrace make him a living instance of the unjust suffering that challenges religious faith. Yet Dostoevsky does not leave Ilyusha's suffering merely as an indictment; through Alyosha's love and the boys' reconciliation, suffering becomes the ground of redemption and community.

Ilyusha's death and the speech at the stone transform private grief into a source of moral education and collective memory. The boys are told to remember Ilyusha and to let that memory become a bond of love that guides their lives. In this way, Ilyusha becomes the novel's Christ-like innocent whose suffering redeems others.
