# Immortality

Book I, Chapter IV. The Third Son, Alyosha — The narrator introduces Alyosha's character, explaining why this intelligent youth chose the monastery. This is the foundational biographical context: Alyosha's predisposition toward faith, which makes him the novel's central vessel for the immortality question.

## Definition in Dostoevsky's Framework

(007-002) Alyosha entered upon his religious path because it 'struck his imagination and presented itself to him as offering an ideal means of escape for his soul from darkness to light.' The narrator prefaces this by noting Alyosha was not stupid, merely a youth of his time who chose a drastic path — the question of immortality lurks beneath his vocational commitment to the monastery.

(012-036) Elder Zosima: 'Strive to love your neighbor actively and indefatigably. In as far as you advance in love you will grow surer of the reality of God and of the immortality of your soul. If you attain to perfect self-forgetfulness in the love of your neighbor, then you will believe without doubt.'

(014-011) Miusov diverts the argument by recalling that Ivan 'solemnly declared in argument that there was nothing in the whole world to make men love their neighbors. That there was no law of nature that man should love mankind.' This sets up the confrontation: Ivan denies the natural basis for neighbor-love, which Zosima will connect to immortality.
