# The Murder of Fyodor Karamazov

The murder of the debauched patriarch [[fyodor-pavlovitch-karamazov|Fyodor Pavlovitch]] is the central crime around which the novel's courtroom drama revolves. Although [[smerdyakov|Smerdyakov]] is the actual killer, circumstantial evidence, motive, and his own guilty conscience combine to convict [[dmitri-fyodorovich-karamazov|Dmitri Karamazov]]. The crime forces the novel's central question into criminal reality: who is really [[guilt|guilty]] when the physical murderer acted on ideas he believed came from [[ivan-fyodorovich-karamazov|Ivan]], the son with the strongest motive is factually innocent, and the murdered father was in a sense the author of his own destruction?

## Overview

On the night of the murder, Dmitri Karamazov, desperate for money and convinced that Grushenka has fled to his father, rushes to Fyodor Pavlovitch's house. In a frenzy, he peers through the window and sees his father — alive. He strikes the servant Grigory with a brass pestle, leaving him unconscious, but he does not enter the house or harm his father. Convinced that Grushenka is not there, he flees to Mokroe, where he finds her and spends the night in a drunken orgy funded by money he had kept concealed (063-015).

Meanwhile, Smerdyakov seizes the opportunity. He had previously arranged with Ivan to be absent, interpreting Ivan's philosophical nihilism and his statement that "everything is permitted" as a directive (092-005). Smerdyakov admits to Ivan in their third interview:

> "I did kill him. I did it then. I did it all. Why are you pretending? You knew, you knew perfectly well, that he would be murdered. You yourself knew it and told me to go away" (092-007)

Dmitri, intoxicated and guilt-ridden from the assault on Grigory, is discovered at Mokroe and arrested. His own behavior damns him: he had publicly threatened to kill his father, he was at the scene, he had motive (money and the rivalry over Grushenka), and he suddenly possesses money for the Mokroe orgy. The preliminary investigation (Chapters 066–074) builds an airtight circumstantial case.

The truth — that Smerdyakov committed the murder, that he saw it as the logical consequence of Ivan's teachings, and that Ivan bears moral responsibility — is known only to Ivan, who is driven to the brink of madness by the revelation. Dmitri is tried, convicted, and sentenced to twenty years of penal servitude in Siberia.

## Narrative Significance

The murder is the novel's structural pivot, transforming philosophical debates about guilt and responsibility into criminal reality. Dostoevsky constructs one of literature's most ingenious plots: the man with the strongest motive and the clearest opportunity is innocent of the actual killing, while the true murderer is the one no one suspects. This design forces the reader — and the jury — to confront the difference between legal guilt and moral truth.

More profoundly, the murder externalizes the novel's central philosophical question: who is really guilty? Smerdyakov committed the physical act, but he carried out what Ivan had intellectually willed. Dmitri did not kill his father, but his hatred and threats make him guilty in desire. Fyodor Pavlovitch, the sensualist who neglected and exploited his sons, is in a sense the author of his own destruction.

> "And what is the use of Christ's words, unless we set an example? The people are lost without the Word of God, for their soul is athirst for the Word and for all that is good" (001-005)

The novel suggests that all are responsible for all, just as Father Zossima taught. The crime is collective, and the judgment of any single individual as "the murderer" is a simplification that justice requires but truth cannot sustain. The murder thus becomes the crucible in which the novel's deepest ideas — shared guilt, the limits of rational judgment, and the inadequacy of legal categories to capture moral reality — are tested and confirmed.
