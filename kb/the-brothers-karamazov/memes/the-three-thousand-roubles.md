# The Three Thousand Roubles

The sum that circulates through the novel like a dark current, binding together [[dmitri-fyodorovich-karamazov|Dmitri]]'s debt to [[katerina-ivanovna|Katerina Ivanovna]], [[fyodor-pavlovitch-karamazov|Fyodor Pavlovitch]]'s attempt to buy [[grushenka|Grushenka]]'s favor, and the prosecution's theory of motive at [[dmirtis-trial|Dmitri's trial]]. The three thousand roubles are more than a sum of money — they are the material embodiment of the novel's concern with [[guilt]], [[sin|sensuality]], and the corrupting power of material desire.

## Physical Description

The three thousand roubles take multiple physical forms throughout the novel. Fyodor Pavlovitch prepares "a big envelope with three thousand roubles in it, tied up with ribbon and addressed to Grushenka" (044-051). This envelope, with its inscription "A present of three thousand roubles for my angel Grushenka, if she comes to me" (067-018), is the physical lure that the old man keeps hidden. At the Mokroe orgy, Dmitri produces "about two or three thousand roubles" in notes, his hands and face covered in blood (066-020). The physical form — notes in an envelope, sealed, inscribed — becomes central to the forensic question of whether the money was ever actually stolen, since the envelope is found empty and the murder is not a robbery.

## Narrative Function

The three thousand roubles serve as the novel's principal plot mechanism in three interconnected ways:

**As debt and dishonor**: Dmitri has spent three thousand roubles that belong to Katerina Ivanovna — money she entrusted to him and which he squandered on Grushenka at Mokroe. His desperate need to repay this sum before confronting his father is the engine of his actions in the days leading up to the murder: "I hoped to gain his object for three thousand roubles. Mitya had reached this conclusion" (057-004).

**As motive**: At the trial, the prosecution argues that Dmitri killed his father to obtain the three thousand roubles that Fyodor Pavlovitch had prepared for Grushenka. The money becomes the key piece of circumstantial evidence: "He was in great need of money — it was three thousand roubles precisely" (068-041). The fact that Dmitri suddenly has money to spend at Mokroe completes the prosecution's narrative of robbery-murder.

**As absence**: One of the trial's crucial revelations is that the three thousand roubles were never actually in the envelope on the night of the murder. Smerdyakov explains to Ivan that the envelope contained only strips of paper (092-007). The absence of the money — and the persistence with which everyone believes it was there — exposes the gap between material fact and the narratives people construct around money.

## Symbolic Significance

The three thousand roubles symbolize the entanglement of material desire with spiritual corruption. The sum recurs with an almost incantatory rhythm — "three thousand, three thousand roubles" — as if the number itself exerts a hypnotic power over the characters. Dostoevsky uses the money to literalize the novel's moral equation: every relationship in the Karamazov world is mediated, distorted, and ultimately corrupted by the flow of money.

The money also functions as a test of character. Dmitri's failure to repay Katerina Ivanovna is not merely a financial failure but a moral one — a breach of honor that he experiences as a burning shame. His eventual acceptance of suffering in prison, where he dreams of escape not to spend money but to "sing a hymn," marks his liberation from the three-thousand-rouble obsession.
