# The Prosecutor's Speech

Prosecutor Ippolit Kirillovitch's speech at Dmitri Karamazov's trial is the centerpiece of Book XII and one of the most famous courtroom speeches in literature. It is both a seemingly irresistible argument for Dmitri's guilt and a subtle demonstration of how rational analysis can construct a persuasive falsehood.

## Overview

The prosecutor's speech opens "Sketches of Character" (101-001) and extends across four chapters: "Sketches of Character," "An Historical Survey," "A Treatise on Smerdyakov," and "The Galloping Troika." Ippolit Kirillovitch approaches the case not as a mere murder investigation but as a moral and psychological drama, framing Dmitri's actions within what he calls the "Karamazov nature."

He constructs a portrait of Dmitri as a man of uncontrolled passions, shaped by a dissolute father and a society in moral decay. The "historical survey" (102-001) traces the Karamazov family history as a parable of modern Russian degeneration. The "treatise on Smerdyakov" (103-001) dismisses the possibility of the servant's guilt with psychological arguments about Smerdyakov's weak character and lack of motive -- a reasonable but fatally wrong assessment.

The speech's emotional climax comes in the "Galloping Troika" section (104-001), where the prosecutor transforms Dmitri into a symbol of Russia itself -- a wild, passionate force careering toward destruction. This rhetorical move is devastatingly effective with the jury, appealing to their deepest cultural anxieties. The speech receives thunderous applause, and the prosecutor is congratulated as if he has already won.

The irony is profound. As the reader knows, the prosecutor's construction is brilliant fiction. His psychological portrait of Dmitri is perceptive but irrelevant; his dismissal of Smerdyakov is logical but wrong; and his cultural parable about Russia, however stirring, bears no relation to the specific facts of Fyodor Pavlovitch's murder. The speech demonstrates precisely the danger Ivan identified in "The Grand Inquisitor": the capacity of reason and rhetoric to construct convincing falsehoods that serve institutional needs at the expense of truth.

## Key Participants

- **Ippolit Kirillovitch** -- the prosecutor, a man of genuine intellect and rhetorical power whose psychological approach to the case produces a "judicial error" (101-001).
- **Dmitri Karamazov** -- the defendant, transformed by the speech from a specific individual into a symbol of national pathology.
- **Fetyukovitch** -- the defense attorney, whose logical rebuttal of the prosecution's case is delivered after the emotional damage is done (105-001).

## Narrative Significance

The prosecutor's speech is Dostoevsky's most sustained critique of the modern rationalist approach to human behavior. Ippolit Kirillovitch represents the Enlightenment faith in psychological analysis, the belief that human actions can be explained, predicted, and judged through the systematic application of reason. His failure -- arriving at a persuasive but false conclusion -- demonstrates the limits of this approach.

The speech also mirrors the Grand Inquisitor's arguments in a crucial way. Both the Inquisitor and the Prosecutor claim to understand human nature better than humans understand themselves, and both offer a seductive narrative that flatters the audience's prejudices. Where the Inquisitor appeals to the masses' desire for "miracle, mystery, and authority," the Prosecutor appeals to the jury's desire for moral clarity and cultural reassurance. Both are wrong in ways that reveal the danger of interpretive power unchecked by humility.

Structurally, the prosecutor's speech is counterpoint to Ivan's "Rebellion." Where Ivan's rebellion was a private philosophical act, the prosecutor's speech is a public institutional act. Both involve the reduction of complex moral reality to a compelling narrative, and both exemplify the danger Dostoevsky saw in the rationalist dismissal of mystery, faith, and the irreducible particularity of human souls.
