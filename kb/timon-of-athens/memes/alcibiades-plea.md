# Alcibiades's Plea before the Senate

## Context

[[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]'s plea occurs in Act III, Scene V, the pivotal scene of the play's political subplot. By this point in the play, [[timon|Timon]]'s financial ruin is well underway: his [[debt|creditors]] have called in their debts (Act III, Scenes I–III), and his false [[friendship|friends]] — [[lucullus|Lucullus]], [[lucius|Lucius]], and [[sempronius|Sempronius]] — have each refused him assistance. The theme of institutional betrayal is already fully established when the action shifts from [[timon|Timon]]'s house to the [[senate-house|Senate-House]], where a different kind of betrayal is about to unfold.

The scene opens with the Senate already in session, having voted to condemn a soldier — a friend of [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] — to death for killing a man in a brawl (009-003). [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] enters to plead for the soldier's life, setting up a confrontation that mirrors [[timon|Timon]]'s own experience of institutional ingratitude. Where [[timon|Timon]] is betrayed by the private economy of gift and [[friendship]], [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] will be betrayed by the public machinery of [[law]] and state authority.

## Key Participants

- **[[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]**: The Athenian military captain who comes as a "humble suitor" (009-008) to plead for his soldier friend's life. His transformation from loyal petitioner to exiled avenger begins in this scene.
- **[[first-senator|First Senator]]**: The presiding voice of the Senate, who articulates the principle that "Nothing emboldens sin so much as mercy" (009-003) and delivers both the death sentence and the decree of banishment.
- **[[second-senator|Second Senator]]**: Supports the First Senator's hard line, dismissing [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]'s arguments as "vain" (009-013) and cataloguing the soldier's moral failings as a "sworn rioter" (009-017).

## Narrative Summary

[[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] enters the [[senate-house|Senate-House]] and begins his plea with an appeal to the senators' nobler instincts: "I am an humble suitor to your virtues; / For pity is the virtue of the law" (009-008). His argument is carefully constructed. He acknowledges his friend's guilt — the soldier "hath stepp'd into the law" — but frames the killing as a response to wounded [[honour|honour]] rather than cowardice or malice: "Nor did he soil the fact with cowardice — / An honour in him which buys out his fault" (009-008). The soldier acted "with a noble [[anger|fury]] and fair spirit" when his reputation was "touch'd to death" (009-008).

The First Senator's response (009-009) transforms the plea into a philosophical debate about [[law|justice]] and [[mercy]]. He accuses [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] of "labour'ding] / To bring manslaughter into form" and articulates a doctrine of passive endurance: "He's truly valiant that can wisely suffer / The worst that man can breathe" (009-009). This Stoic ideal — that true courage is bearing wrongs rather than avenging them — is the Senate's ideological justification for severity.

[[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] pivots to a military counterargument (009-012), asking why soldiers should face battle if suffering is the highest virtue: "Why do fond men expose themselves to battle, / And not endure all threats? ... Why then, women are more valiant / That stay at home" (009-012). He appeals directly to the senators' greatness: "As you are great, be pitifully good" (009-012). His most pragmatic argument follows: the soldier's military service "At Lacedaemon and Byzantium / Were a sufficient briber for his life" (009-014). The soldier has shed blood for [[athens|Athens]]; [[athens|Athens]] should repay that debt with [[mercy]].

The [[second-senator|Second Senator]] counters by attacking the soldier's character, describing him as "a sworn rioter" whose "days are foul and his drink dangerous" (009-017). The [[first-senator|First Senator]] delivers the definitive judgment: "He dies" (009-018). [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] makes one final appeal, offering to pawn his own "victories, all / My [[honour|honours]] to you" as security for the soldier's good behaviour (009-019). But the Senate is immovable: "We are for law: he dies; urge it no more, / On height of our displeasure" (009-020).

When [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] protests further — "Must it be so? it must not be" (009-021) — the confrontation escalates. He reminds the senators of his own service, and when his plea for "common grace" (009-025) is met with rebuke, the [[first-senator|First Senator]] pronounces the sentence that changes everything: "We banish thee for ever" (009-026).

[[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]'s response (009-027–009-029) transforms him from petitioner to antagonist. He turns the language of banishment back on the Senate: "Banish your dotage; banish [[debt|usury]], / That makes the senate ugly" (009-027). In his closing soliloquy, he names the Senate's real character — "the usuring senate" (009-029) — and resolves to strike at [[athens|Athens]] with his "discontented troops" (009-029). The scene that began with a plea for [[mercy]] ends with a vow of [[revenge|vengeance]].

## Significance

This scene is the structural hinge of the play's political subplot, transforming [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] from a loyal Athenian captain into the military antagonist who will ultimately conquer [[athens|the city]]. The confrontation parallels [[timon|Timon]]'s own experience of institutional ingratitude: both men have served [[athens|Athens]] — [[timon|Timon]] through [[bounty|generosity]], [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] through military sacrifice — and both are rejected by the institutions they served. The difference is in their responses. Where [[timon|Timon]] withdraws into misanthropic solitude, [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] mobilizes an army. The play thus offers two models of response to betrayal: philosophical negation and political action.

The scene is also the play's most concentrated examination of the [[mercy]]-versus-[[law|justice]] tension. The senators deploy the language of legal principle — "We are for law" (009-020), "Nothing emboldens sin so much as mercy" (009-003) — but their rigid legalism is exposed as self-serving. [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] names their hypocrisy directly: the senators who condemn a soldier's one rash act are themselves practitioners of [[debt|usury]], a form of predation that the play presents as systemic rather than episodic. The Senate's "law" protects the institution, not the citizens who serve it.

For the play's larger architecture, this scene establishes the conditions for the convergence of the two plots in Act IV, where [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] will meet [[timon|Timon]] in [[the-woods|the wilderness]] and receive the gold that funds his campaign. The plea before the Senate is the cause; the conquest of [[athens|Athens]] is the effect. The scene therefore functions as both a self-contained drama of institutional injustice and a necessary plot mechanism connecting the play's personal and political dimensions of [[ingratitude]].
