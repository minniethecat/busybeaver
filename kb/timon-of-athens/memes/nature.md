# Nature in Timon of Athens

## Overview

Nature in *[[timon-of-athens|Timon of Athens]]* functions as the play's most complex symbolic domain, operating simultaneously as physical landscape (the woods [[the-woods|outside Athens]] where [[timon|Timon]] retreats), philosophical concept (human nature and its [[disease|corruption]]), and moral standard (the natural order that [[athens|Athens]] has violated). The movement from [[athens|Athens]] to [[the-woods|the wilderness]] in Act IV is the play's structural hinge — [[timon|Timon]] leaves the built environment of feasts and [[debt|credit]] for a landscape of roots and caves, and in doing so exchanges one set of meanings for another. The wilderness does not offer redemption, as it might in a pastoral or romance; instead, it intensifies [[timon|Timon]]'s misanthropy by stripping away the social conventions that previously disguised it. Nature, in this play, is neither benevolent nor curative — it is simply indifferent, and that indifference is both a judgment on [[athens|Athens]] and a source of terror.

## Key Passages

> For since dishonour traffics with man's nature, He is but outside: these pencill'd figures are Even such as they give out. (001-076)

[[timon|Timon]]'s remark on [[painter|the Painter]]'s work introduces the play's central anxiety about human nature: that [[shame|dishonour]] "traffics" with it — negotiates, does business, corrupts. When nature is corrupted, a man becomes mere "outside," a painted surface signifying nothing. The word "traffics" is the key — it is commerce, the economic activity that defines [[timon|Timon]]'s [[athens|Athens]], that corrupts nature. The play will test whether nature can be recovered outside the commercial sphere, and [[the-woods|the wilderness]] scenes of Acts IV–V constitute that test.

> Why this spade? this place? This slave-like habit? and these looks of care? Thy flatterers yet wear silk, drink wine, lie soft; Hug their diseased perfumes, and have forgot That ever Timon was. Shame not these woods, By putting on the cunning of a carper. (013-063)

[[apemantus|Apemantus]]' confrontation with [[timon|Timon]] in [[the-woods|the wilderness]] crystallizes the play's nature/culture opposition. He contrasts [[timon|Timon]]'s "slave-like habit" and spade with the [[flattery|flatterers]]' silk and wine, but his central charge is that [[timon|Timon]] "shames" the woods by importing human "cunning" into them. Nature, for [[apemantus|Apemantus]], should be a place of honest simplicity; [[timon|Timon]]'s misanthropy is a form of artifice — he is performing poverty rather than accepting it. The woods become a theatre of authenticity where neither [[timon|Timon]] nor [[apemantus|Apemantus]] can convincingly claim the natural role.

> *To the gold.* O thou sweet king-killer, and dear divorce 'Twixt natural son and sire! thou bright defiler Of Hymen's purest bed! (013-128)

[[timon|Timon]]'s apostrophe to gold uses "natural" in its most painful sense — the bond between parent and child, which gold can "divorce." [[gold|Gold]] is figured as an anti-nature force, breaking the most fundamental natural bonds: family ("son and sire"), marriage ("Hymen's bed"), and even [[gods|divine]] chastity ("Dian's lap"). If nature is what connects, gold is what severs; if nature is what generates, gold is what destroys. The speech is nature's obituary in a world where everything natural has been commodified.

> Shame not these woods, By putting on the cunning of a carper. (013-063)

[[apemantus|Apemantus]]' plea that [[timon|Timon]] not "shame" the woods captures the play's nature ethic in a single line. The woods are imagined as possessing a [[honour|dignity]] that human behaviour can violate — they are not merely a backdrop for human action but a moral presence that judges it. This is the closest the play comes to the pastoral tradition, but it immediately undercuts the impulse: [[timon|Timon]] does shame the woods, and the woods do not care.

> for there is boundless theft In limited professions. Rascal thieves, Here's gold. (013-151)

[[timon|Timon]]'s address to the bandits, set in the woods, completes the play's inversion of nature and civilization. The "limited professions" of [[athens|Athens]] — senators, merchants, [[friendship|friends]] — are the true thieves, their theft "boundless" precisely because it is legal. The actual bandits, by contrast, are "rascal thieves" — petty and honest in their criminality. Nature, which should be the domain of honest simplicity, has become the refuge of those who see through civilization's pretenses, and [[timon|Timon]] funds their continued predation as a form of revenge against the greater thieves of [[athens|Athens]].

## Character Perspectives

**[[timon|Timon]]** undergoes the play's most radical transformation in relation to nature. In Acts I–II he is a creature of [[athens|the city]], his identity constituted entirely by social performance. In [[the-woods|the wilderness]], he attempts to become natural — digging for roots, wearing rags, living in a cave — but discovers that human nature cannot be shed like clothing. His misanthropy is the residue of his social self, the part of [[timon|Timon]] that cannot be naturalized no matter how far he retreats from [[athens|Athens]].

**[[apemantus|Apemantus]]** claims the role of nature's philosopher, the man who has always lived according to nature (eating roots, rejecting [[gold|wealth]]) and who therefore has standing to judge [[timon|Timon]]'s performative poverty. His accusation that [[timon|Timon]] "shames" the woods reveals his investment in nature as a moral category — an investment [[timon|Timon]] dismisses but the play does not wholly repudiate.

**[[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]** occupies the boundary between nature and civilization. His military camp [[the-woods|outside Athens]] is neither city nor wilderness, and his campaign against [[athens|Athens]] is motivated by a sense of natural [[law|justice]] that [[athens|the city]]'s laws have violated. His final promise to "make war breed peace" suggests that nature, properly channeled through martial discipline, may restore what civilization has corrupted.

## Critical Interpretations

The wilderness in *[[timon-of-athens|Timon of Athens]]* participates in a long tradition of Shakespearean green worlds — the Forest of Arden in *As You Like It*, the heath in *King Lear*, Prospero's island in *The Tempest*. Unlike these other spaces, [[timon|Timon]]'s woods offer no transformation, reconciliation, or renewal. The green world tradition, as Northrop Frye argued, typically moves from court to forest and back to a renewed court; [[timon|Timon]]'s play moves from city to wilderness and stops there, refusing the return that would constitute comic or redemptive form. This structural refusal makes *[[timon|Timon]]* Shakespeare's most radically anti-pastoral play.

The early modern period saw intense debate about the state of nature and the origins of civil society, debates that would culminate in Hobbes's *Leviathan* (1651) but were already active in Shakespeare's lifetime. [[timon|Timon]]'s trajectory from social benefactor to cave-dwelling misanthrope can be read as a thought experiment in what happens when the social contract is voided — when the bonds of [[debt|obligation]], gratitude, and [[gratitude|reciprocity]] that constitute civil society are revealed as fictions. The answer the play offers is profoundly pessimistic: without those fictions, human life is solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short — not because nature is Hobbesian, but because [[timon|Timon]] has been Hobbesianized by civilization's betrayal.
