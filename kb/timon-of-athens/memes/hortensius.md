## Role

Hortensius is a servant sent by one of [[timon|Timon]]'s [[debt|credit]]ors to demand [[gratitude|repayment]]. He appears in the scene outside [[timon|Timon]]'s house where multiple [[debt|credit]]ors' servants gather, each seeking to be first to present their master's bill. What distinguishes Hortensius from his fellows is his expressed reluctance: "It is against my heart," he says of the errand [004-492]. This single line of moral discomfort gives him a humanity that other [[debt|credit]]or-servants lack, making him a small but significant voice of conscience in the play's anatomy of ingratitude.

## Key Scenes

- **Act 4** — Hortensius meets fellow servants Titus, Varro's servants, and [[lucius|Lucius]]'s servant outside [[timon|Timon]]'s house. Their gathering forms a small comic-tragic ensemble: each servant is there to dun [[timon|Timon]], and each acknowledges the awkwardness of the situation while being unable to refuse their masters' orders. Hortensius's line "It is against my heart" [004-492] is the moral high point of the scene — a recognition that what they are doing is wrong, even as they do it.

## Relationships

- **Titus and [[caphis|Caphis]]**: Fellow servants with whom Hortensius shares the errand of debt collection. Together they represent the ordinary men who must execute the ingratitude of the powerful.
- **[[timon|Timon]]**: Hortensius's moral discomfort suggests an awareness of [[timon|Timon]]'s former [[bounty|generosity]] and the in[[law|justice]] of the [[debt|credit]]ors' demands. He is a [[debt|credit]]or's servant who recognizes the debt of gratitude — even as his master recognizes only the debt of [[gold|money]].
- **[[flavius|Flavius]]**: Like [[flavius|Flavius]], Hortensius occupies the position of the loyal servant, but his [[service|loyalty]] is divided between his master (who demands payment) and his conscience (which rebels against the task).

## Character Analysis

Hortensius's single moral protest — "It is against my heart" — is one of the play's small but telling moments. In a drama where ingratitude is systematic and nearly universal, a servant's quiet reluctance registers as a significant moral event. Hortensius cannot refuse his master's orders, but he can name the in[[law|justice]] of what he is compelled to do. His conscience, however, is powerless: he goes through with the errand despite his heart. This gap between moral recognition and moral action is central to the play's pessimistic vision — knowing what is right is not enough when social and economic structures compel wrongdoing.
