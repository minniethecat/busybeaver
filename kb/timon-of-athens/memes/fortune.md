# Fortune in Timon of Athens

## Overview

Fortune is the governing principle of *[[timon-of-athens|Timon of Athens]]*, operating simultaneously as a personified classical goddess and as an impersonal mechanism of social and economic reversal. The play's structure is built around Fortune's wheel: [[timon|Timon]] rises to the pinnacle of Athenian society through his [[gold|wealth]] and [[bounty|generosity]], then plummets to destitution when his [[debt|credit]]ors call, only to discover hidden gold in [[the-woods|the wilderness]] — a perverse second turn of Fortune's wheel that brings not restoration but misanthropic despair. Shakespeare draws on the medieval and Renaissance tradition of *De Casibus* t[[anger|rage]]dy, where Fortune's wheel illustrates the mutability of worldly success. Unlike in the romances, where Fortune's turns ultimately resolve into providential order, [[timon|Timon]]'s Fortune offers no redemption — she is a force of pure caprice that exposes rather than heals.

## Key Passages

> his large fortune Upon his good and gracious nature hanging Subdues and properties to his love and tendance All sorts of hearts; yea, from the glass-faced flatterer To Apemantus, that few things loves better Than to abhor himself: even he drops down The knee before him (001-034)

The [[poet|Poet]]'s opening description establishes [[wealth|fortune]] as a gravitational force — [[timon|Timon]]'s "large [[wealth|fortune]]" hangs upon his nature and "subdues" all hearts to his service. The image is deliberately ambiguous: does [[wealth|fortune]] hang upon [[timon|Timon]]'s nature like a garment, or like a weight? The verb "subdues" suggests conquest rather than attraction, and the universal obeisance, even from the cynical [[apemantus|Apemantus]], hints that this power is unnatural and unsustainable.

> Ceremony was but devised at first To set a gloss on faint deeds, hollow welcomes, Recanting goodness, sorry ere 'tis shown; But where there is true friendship, there needs none. Pray, sit; more welcome are ye to my fortunes Than my fortunes to me. (002-006)

[[timon|Timon]]'s [[feast|banquet]] speech reveals his understanding of the relationship between [[wealth|fortune]] and [[friendship|friends]]hip. Ceremony is the counterfeit currency of false [[friendship|friends]]hip, necessary only where genuine bonds are absent. The concluding line — "more welcome are ye to my [[wealth|fortune]]s / Than my [[wealth|fortune]]s to me" — is both generous and prophetic. [[timon|Timon]] literally welcomes his guests to his [[gold|wealth]] while acknowledging his own alienation from it, an alienation that will become complete when his [[wealth|fortune]]s vanish.

> No villainous bounty yet hath pass'd my heart; Unwisely, not ignobly, have I given. Why dost thou weep? Canst thou the conscience lack, To think I shall lack friends? Secure thy heart; If I would broach the vessels of my love, And try the argument of hearts by borrowing, Men and men's fortunes could I frankly use As I can bid thee speak. (004-092)

[[timon|Timon]]'s response to [[flavius|Flavius]]' warning encapsulates his tragic misunderstanding of [[wealth|fortune]]. He insists his giving has been "unwisely, not ignobly" — morally sound if financially reckless — and believes his [[friendship|friends]] will reciprocate when tested. The phrase "men and men's [[wealth|fortune]]s could I frankly use" reveals the fatal confusion: [[timon|Timon]] has mistaken his [[gold|wealth]] for love, his gifts for bonds. Fortune, in his worldview, is fungible with [[friendship|friends]]hip — a category error the play systematically punishes.

> you shall perceive how you Mistake my fortunes; I am wealthy in my friends. (004-094)

The compressed irony of these two lines is devastating. [[timon|Timon]] is "[[gold|wealth]]y in his [[friendship|friends]]" only in the sense that his [[friendship|friends]] have been [[gold|wealth]]-seeking — they are rich *from* him, not rich *for* him. The word "Mistake" carries its full weight: [[flavius|Flavius]] and the audience already perceive what [[timon|Timon]] cannot, that his [[wealth|fortune]] and his [[friendship|friends]] are the same thing, and losing one means losing both.

## Character Perspectives

**[[timon|Timon]]** embodies the play's central confusion between moral and material [[wealth|fortune]]. He treats his [[gold|wealth]] as an extension of his character — [[bounty|generosity]] is his identity, not merely his behaviour. When [[wealth|fortune]] abandons him, he experiences it not as economic mis[[wealth|fortune]] but as cosmic betrayal, a wound that transforms him from philanthropist to misanthrope. His discovery of gold in Act IV is Fortune's cruelest joke: she returns what he no longer values, proving that her gifts are arbitrary rather than merited.

**[[apemantus|Apemantus]]** is the play's anti-Fortune philosopher. His [[misanthropy|cynicism]] represents a total rejection of Fortune's logic — he neither seeks her gifts nor mourns their loss. His diet of roots and water is a deliberate repudiation of the feast economy [[timon|Timon]] presides over. Yet [[apemantus|Apemantus]] is not Fortune's master so much as her conscientious objector; his withdrawal from the game of [[gold|wealth]] and favour isolates him as thoroughly as [[timon|Timon]]'s exile.

**The false [[friendship|friends]]** ([[lucullus|Lucullus]], [[lucius|Lucius]], [[sempronius|Sempronius]]) are Fortune's perfect agents — they follow her movements with mechanical precision. When [[timon|Timon]]'s [[wealth|fortune]] is high, they flatter and receive gifts; when it falls, they invent excuses and withdraw. Their behaviour is so predictable that it becomes a structural principle of the play, a demonstration of how [[wealth|fortune]] rather than character determines social relations in Shakespeare's [[athens|Athens]].

**[[flavius|Flavius]]** the steward provides the play's only example of [[service|loyalty]] independent of [[wealth|fortune]]. While the lords abandon [[timon|Timon]], [[flavius|Flavius]] remains — weeping, sharing his own [[gold|money]], and eventually seeking [[timon|Timon]] in [[the-woods|the wilderness]]. His constancy demonstrates that genuine human bonds can survive Fortune's wheel, but the play treats this as an exception that proves the rule. [[flavius|Flavius]] is alone in his [[service|loyalty]], a single counterexample to the universal [[disease|corruption]] Fortune exposes.

## Critical Interpretations

The figure of Fortune in *[[timon-of-athens|Timon of Athens]]* has been read as Shakespeare's response to the economic transformations of early Jacobean England. The play was written in the years following James I's accession (1603), a period marked by rampant inflation, the sale of honours, and the rise of a [[debt|credit]] economy that made traditional aristocratic [[gold|wealth]] — based on land and gift-exchange — increasingly precarious. [[timon|Timon]]'s t[[anger|rage]]dy, in this reading, is the t[[anger|rage]]dy of a feudal lord who has not understood that [[wealth|fortune]] now operates through market mechanisms rather than personal virtue.

Medieval and Renaissance iconography of Fortune as a blindfolded woman turning a wheel was ubiquitous in Shakespeare's culture, appearing in everything from morality plays to royal pageantry. The play's debt to this tradition is explicit, but Shakespeare's innovation lies in making Fortune's wheel turn through specifically economic mechanisms — [[debt|credit]], debt, [[debt|interest]] — rather than through the arbitrary interventions of a personified goddess. Fortune in *[[timon|Timon]]* is not a supernatural force but a name for the emergent logic of capital.

Feminist critics have noted the gendered dimension of Fortune in the play. [[timon|Timon]]'s apostrophe to gold in Act IV addresses the metal in terms that blur the distinction between Fortune, gold, and the female body — all are figured as objects of desire that promise satisfaction and deliver [[disease|corruption]]. The prostitutes [[phrynia|Phrynia]] and [[timandra|Timandra]] literalize this identification: they are bodies exchanged for gold, embodiments of a Fortune that is simultaneously desired and despised.
