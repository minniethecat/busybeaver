## Context

The siege of [[athens|Athens]] by [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] is the climactic military event of Act 5, representing the final consequence of Athenian [[ingratitude]]. [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] was banished by the Senate in Act 3 for pleading on behalf of a soldier condemned to death (009-026). Since that banishment, he has raised an army and marched on [[athens|the city]]. In the wilderness, he encountered [[timon|Timon]], who gave him [[gold]] and urged him to show no [[mercy]] (013-039). Now, as the play draws to its conclusion, the walls of [[athens|Athens]] are before him and the Senators -- desperate after failing to persuade [[timon|Timon]] to return and lead the city's defence (014-078) -- must face the consequences of their own actions.

## Key Participants

- **[[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]** -- The banished Athenian general, now leading an army against the city that rejected him. His grievance is specific (the Senate refused to pardon his soldier), but his campaign channels the play's broader indictment of institutional [[ingratitude]].
- **[[first-senator|First Senator]]** and **[[second-senator|Second Senator]]** -- Representatives of the Athenian Senate who plead with [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] at the walls, attempting to negotiate a surrender that spares the innocent.
- **The Soldier** -- A messenger who arrives from [[the-woods|the woods]] bearing [[timon|Timon]]'s epitaph, taken from the grave with wax (016-003).
- **[[timon|Timon]]** -- Though already dead when the siege concludes, his epitaph and memory shape [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]'s final reflections. [[timon|Timon]]'s gold has, in part, funded the army that now besieges [[athens|Athens]] (013-040).

## Narrative Summary

By Act 5, Scene 2, [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]'s army has reached the walls of [[athens|Athens]]. The Senators are in disarray. A messenger reports that [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]'s files are as full as reported and that his "expedition promises / Present approach" (015-004). Compounding their desperation, another messenger reveals that [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] has sent letters to [[timon|Timon]] asking him to join the cause against [[athens|the city]] (015-006). But the Senators who visited [[timon|Timon]] return empty-handed: "No talk of Timon, nothing of him expect" (015-009). The enemy's drum is heard, and the city braces for assault.

In the final scene, trumpets sound as [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] enters with his army before the walls (017-002). He issues a decisive proclamation: the time has come when the Senators' power must yield. He denounces their governance as "licentious measure" -- self-serving rule that made their wills "the scope of [[law|justice]]" (017-005). The Senators appear on the walls, and from this position of weakness, they plead.

The Senators attempt multiple strategies. First, they claim they had tried to make amends before [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] had the power to threaten them (017-006). [[second-senator|Second Senator]] argues that not all Athenians deserve "the common stroke of war" (017-007). [[first-senator|First Senator]] reminds [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] that the walls themselves were not built by those who wronged him, and that "these great towers, trophies and schools should fall / For private faults in them" (017-008). Crucially, they reveal that those directly responsible for [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]'s banishment have already died of shame: "[[shame|Shame]] that they wanted cunning, in excess / Hath broke their hearts" (017-009).

[[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] agrees to spare all but those enemies of both [[timon|Timon]] and himself whom the Senators themselves shall identify. He throws down his glove as a token of [[honour]] and pledges that "not a man / Shall pass his quarter, or offend the stream / Of regular justice in your city's bounds" (017-014). The gates are opened.

A Soldier then arrives bearing [[timon|Timon]]'s epitaph, which he has taken from the grave in wax impression (017-018). [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] reads the epitaph aloud -- "Here lie I, Timon; who, alive, all living men did hate" (017-019) -- and delivers his reflection on [[timon|Timon]]'s life and death. He notes that though [[timon|Timon]] scorned human grief, "yet rich conceit / Taught thee to make vast Neptune weep for aye / On thy low grave, on faults forgiven" (017-019). The play ends with [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]'s promise to enter [[athens|the city]] with the olive branch alongside the sword: "Make war breed peace, make peace stint war, make each / Prescribe to other as each other's leech" (017-019).

## Significance

The siege of [[athens|Athens]] serves as the play's political resolution, providing an answer to the question posed by [[timon|Timon]]'s tragic trajectory: what happens when betrayal meets power? Where [[timon|Timon]] responds to [[ingratitude]] with curses, misanthropy, and self-destruction, [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] responds with military force and negotiated settlement. The contrast is structural. [[timon|Timon]]'s response is absolute -- all humanity is damned, no distinction is made between guilty and innocent. [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]'s response is proportionate -- those personally responsible will face justice, and the city's gates will open in peace. The play thus offers two models of [[revenge]]: one cosmic and terminal, the other political and incomplete. The fact that [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]'s solution is openly compromised -- his [[mercy]] is conditional, his justice is negotiated under threat of force -- is not a failure of the play but its argument. Political order, Shakespeare suggests, is always partial, always a matter of "make war breed peace" (017-019) rather than the complete vindication [[timon|Timon]] demands.

The episode also connects the personal and the political dimensions of the play's tragedy. The Senators approach [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] at the walls because they could not persuade [[timon|Timon]] to return and lead the defence. [[timon|Timon]]'s gold funded the army now besieging the city, and his epitaph provides the moral frame for [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]'s final reflections. [[timon|Timon]]'s death -- alone, in the wilderness, cursing humanity -- juxtaposes [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]'s entry into [[athens|the city]], surrounded by soldiers, promising to "use the olive with my sword" (017-019). The play thus closes on an image of integration where [[timon|Timon]]'s story ended in isolation. But the integration is earned through military force, not through the restoration of community or the vindication of [[friendship]]. The siege is resolution, but not redemption -- a political settlement layered over an unresolved moral wound.
