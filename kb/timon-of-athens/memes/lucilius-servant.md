## Role

Lucilius's Servant appears among the crowd of [[debt|credit]]ors' agents who gather at [[timon|Timon]]'s house to demand payment. His master is [[lucilius|Lucilius]] — the very man whose marriage [[timon|Timon]] enabled in Act 1 with a generous dowry. The Servant's presence thus carries a particular dramatic irony: he represents a debt owed by the beneficiary of [[timon|Timon]]'s most uncomplicated act of [[bounty|generosity]]. In the original text, the character is designated "Lucilius' Servant" (or "Lucullus' Servant" in some editions), distinguishing him from the [[lucilius|Lucilius]] of the marriage subplot. (008-002–008-070)

## Key Scenes

**The Gathering Outside Timon's House** (008-002–008-045): Lucilius's Servant enters alongside Varro's two servants and meets [[titus|Titus]], [[hortensius|Hortensius]], and other creditors' agents waiting for [[timon|Timon]] to emerge. He is the first to articulate the shared nature of their errand: "Ay, and I think / One business does command us all; for mine / Is money" [008-006]. When [[philotus|Sir Philotus]] arrives, Lucilius's Servant greets him by name — "And Sir Philotus too!" [008-009] — and engages in the scene's running commentary on [[timon|Timon]]'s decline. His observation that "a prodigal course / Is like the sun's; but not, like his, recoverable" and his fear that "'tis deepest winter in Lord Timon's purse" [008-017] show a sharp, cynical intelligence — he understands the situation better than most.

**The Confrontation with Flavius** (008-035–008-041): When [[flavius|Flavius]] passes through the crowd in a muffled cloak, it is Lucilius's Servant who spots him — "Ha! is not that his steward muffled so? / He goes away in a cloud: call him, call him" [008-035] — and urges the others to detain him. When [[flavius|Flavius]] rebukes them — "If 'twill not serve, 'tis not so base as you; / For you serve knaves" [008-042] — Varro's First Servant mutters in response, but it is Lucilius's Servant who initiated the confrontation.

**The Presentation of Bills** (008-054–008-065): When [[timon|Timon]] appears in a rage, it is Lucilius's Servant who prompts [[titus|Titus]] forward — "Put in now, Titus" [008-054] — and then presents his own bill: "Here's mine" [008-056]. He specifies the amount: "Five thousand crowns, my lord" [008-065]. His persistence — pushing forward when others hesitate — marks him as the most aggressive of the creditors' agents.

## Relationships

**[[lucilius|Lucilius]] (his master)**: The master-servant relationship here is charged with dramatic irony. [[lucilius|Lucilius]] was the first beneficiary of [[timon|Timon]]'s [[bounty|generosity]] that the audience witnesses — [[timon|Timon]] provided the dowry that enabled Lucilius's marriage. Now Lucilius sends a servant to demand five thousand crowns from the ruined [[timon|Timon]]. The Servant is the physical embodiment of Lucilius's [[ingratitude]] — the [[debt]] of gratitude transformed into a debt of [[gold|money]].

**[[timon|Timon]]**: The Servant has no personal relationship with [[timon|Timon]]; he is a pure instrument of economic demand. Yet his demand carries more moral weight than the others' precisely because his master owes everything to [[timon|Timon]]. Five thousand crowns is what Lucilius now demands from the man who made his life possible.

**[[titus|Titus]] and [[hortensius|Hortensius]]**: Fellow creditors' servants. Lucilius's Servant is the most articulate and aggressive of the group, driving the conversation forward and prompting others to act. His relationship with [[titus|Titus]] is particularly noteworthy — he urges Titus forward when [[timon|Timon]] appears, as if delegating the initial confrontation before presenting his own demand.

**[[flaminius|Flaminius]] and [[flavius|Flavius]]**: Lucilius's Servant interacts with [[flaminius|Flaminius]] — "Flaminius! Sir, a word: pray, is my lord ready to come forth?" [008-030] — and with [[flavius|Flavius]] when he spots the muffled steward. These interactions position him on the opposing side of the economic divide: the pursuers versus the pursued.

## Character Analysis

Lucilius's Servant is the most verbally distinctive of the creditors' agents. Where [[hortensius|Hortensius]] registers moral discomfort — "It is against my heart" [008-022] — and the Varro servants are nearly anonymous, Lucilius's Servant speaks with intelligence, cynicism, and a certain relish for the situation. His sun/prodigal metaphor [008-017] is genuinely witty; his prompt to [[titus|Titus]] to "Put in now" shows strategic thinking; his demand for five thousand crowns is made without hesitation.

This sharpness makes him, in a sense, the most culpable of the creditor-servants. He cannot plead ignorance or mere obedience — he understands the situation fully and acts with initiative. His master [[lucilius|Lucilius]] may be the one who sends him, but the Servant brings his own intelligence to the task of dunning a ruined man.

The existence of this character also raises an important textual and dramaturgical point: there are TWO distinct Lucilius figures in the play. One is the servant whom [[timon|Timon]] helps to marry in Act 1 (designated simply "Lucilius" in the text). The other is this servant — "Lucilius' Servant" — who appears in Act 3 as a creditor's agent. Shakespeare's practice of reusing names is well documented, and the double Lucilius is typical of the textual fluidity that characterises plays composed for performance rather than print. The distinction between the two figures is essential for understanding the play's treatment of [[ingratitude]]: the Lucilius of Act 1 is the beneficiary whose marriage represents [[timon|Timon]]'s [[bounty|generosity]] at its best; the Lucilius who sends a servant to demand repayment represents the corruption of that same relationship by the logic of [[debt|debt and credit]].

## Notable Quotations

Lucilius's Servant delivers the scene's most memorable image of [[timon|Timon]]'s fall, using a cosmic metaphor that captures both judgment and inevitability:

> You must consider that a prodigal course / Is like the sun's; but not, like his, recoverable. / I fear 'tis deepest winter in Lord Timon's purse
> — Lucilius's Servant, [008-017]

His practical ruthlessness emerges in his prompt to [[titus|Titus]]:

> Put in now, Titus.
> — Lucilius's Servant, [008-054]

And his demand is matter-of-fact:

> Five thousand crowns, my lord.
> — Lucilius's Servant, [008-065]
