# Alcibiades

## Role in the Play

Alcibiades is the play's secondary protagonist, an Athenian military captain whose banishment from [[athens|Athens]] mirrors [[timon|Timon]]'s exile and whose armed return provides the play's political resolution. Structurally, Alcibiades' plot functions as a counterpoint to [[timon|Timon]]'s: where [[timon|Timon]] responds to betrayal with misanthropic withdrawal, Alcibiades responds with military action. The historical Alcibiades (c. 450–404 BCE) was a controversial Athenian general and statesman, famous for switching [[friendship|allegiance]]s between [[athens|Athens]], Sparta, and Persia during the Peloponnesian War — a figure of brilliant opportunism whose historical complexity Shakespeare simplifies into a relatively straightforward agent of [[law|justice]]. Alcibiades' campaign against [[athens|Athens]], funded by [[timon|Timon]]'s gold, channels the play's accumulated anger into a project of political reconstruction that [[timon|Timon]]'s personal t[[anger|rage]]dy cannot achieve.

## Key Scenes

**Act I, Scene 2 — The Banquet** (002-023): Alcibiades appears at [[timon|Timon]]'s feast, where his martial perspective provides a counterpoint to the civilian flattery. His remark that he "could wish my best friend at such a feast" — referring to enemies bleeding on a battlefield rather than lords dining at a table — establishes his identification with military rather than social values.

**Act III — The Senate Scene**: Alcibiades petitions the [[senate-house|Athenian Senate]] to pardon a soldier who killed a man in a [[anger|rage]]. The Senate refuses, and when Alcibiades protests too vehemently, he is banished. This scene establishes the parallel with [[timon|Timon]]: both men are rejected by the institutions they have served, and both respond with armed [[revenge|vengeance]]. The crucial difference is that Alcibiades' grievance is specific (a soldier's life) while [[timon|Timon]]'s is structural (the failure of the gift economy).

**Act IV, Scene 3 — Accepting [[discovery-of-gold|Timon's Gold]]** (013-028, 013-040): Alcibiades, marching on [[athens|Athens]] with his army, encounters [[timon|Timon]] in [[the-woods|the wilderness]]. [[timon|Timon]] gives him gold and curses him to "be as a planetary [[disease|plague]]" (013-039). Alcibiades' response — "I'll take the gold thou givest me, Not all thy counsel" (013-040) — is the play's most lucid articulation of the distinction between material assistance and moral instruction. He accepts the former and politely declines the latter.

**Act V — The Resolution**: Alcibiades conquers [[athens|Athens]] and, in his final speech, promises to "make war breed peace" and restore order. This political resolution is deliberately incomplete — the play does not show us the restored [[athens|Athens]], only Alcibiades' promise — but it provides the only movement toward order in a play otherwise committed to destruction.

## Relationships

**[[timon|Timon]]**: The relationship between Alcibiades and [[timon|Timon]] is transactional but honest — the most functional relationship in the play. Alcibiades accepts [[timon|Timon]]'s gold without pretending to accept [[timon|Timon]]'s philosophy, and [[timon|Timon]] gives the gold without expecting gratitude. Their mutual recognition that the transaction is purely instrumental makes it paradoxically the play's most authentic exchange.

**[[apemantus|Apemantus]]**: Alcibiades and [[apemantus|Apemantus]] represent the two possible responses to [[athens|Athens]]' [[disease|corruption]]: political action (Alcibiades) and philosophical withdrawal ([[apemantus|Apemantus]]). The play's ending — Alcibiades in power, [[apemantus|Apemantus]] presumably still in [[the-woods|the wilderness]] — suggests that action, however compromised, is more viable than pure critique. But Alcibiades' final speech is notably modest, promising only to "make war breed peace," a formula that acknowledges the limits of political solutions.

## Character Analysis

Alcibiades is the most politically functional character in a play that is deeply skeptical of political solutions. His banishment from [[athens|Athens]] mirrors [[timon|Timon]]'s exile, but his response — to raise an army and conquer [[athens|the city]] — represents the path not taken by [[timon|Timon]]. Where [[timon|Timon]] curses and withdraws, Alcibiades acts; where [[timon|Timon]] seeks cosmic destruction, Alcibiades seeks political reform. The play's ending, in which Alcibiades promises to restore order, is notably incomplete — we do not see the restored [[athens|Athens]], and the promise of peace is hedged with the acknowledgment that it will be born from war. This incompleteness is structural: *[[timon-of-athens|Timon of Athens]]* cannot fully endorse a political resolution because its critique of human [[disease|corruption]] is too comprehensive. Alcibiades offers not a solution but a direction, a movement toward order that the play's tragic logic cannot fully authorize but also cannot entirely refuse.

The historical Alcibiades was a far more complex and morally ambiguous figure than Shakespeare's version. The real Alcibiades was an opportunist who betrayed [[athens|Athens]] to Sparta and Persia before returning to Athenian favour; Shakespeare suppresses this complexity to make Alcibiades a more straightforward agent of [[law|justice]]. The simplification is dramatically effective — it provides the play with a clear political alternative to [[timon|Timon]]'s misanthropy — but it also reveals the limits of the political in Shakespeare's tragic vision. Alcibiades can "make war breed peace," but the peace he makes is outside the play's frame, and the war that breeds it is funded by misanthropic gold.
