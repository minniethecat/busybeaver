## Overview

The [[law|senate]]-house is the institutional centre of [[athens|Athens]] as depicted in *[[timon-of-athens|Timon of Athens]]*. It appears in a single scene — Act 9 — yet its shadow falls across the entire play. The Senate's decision to condemn [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]'s soldier and banish [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] himself crystallizes the play's critique of law, power, and ingratitude. The [[law|senate]]-house is the counterpoint to [[timon|Timon]]'s house: where [[timons-house|Timon's hall]] represents [[bounty|generosity]] corrupted by ingratitude, the senate-house represents [[law|justice]] corrupted by self-[[debt|interest]].

## Setting Description

The stage direction simply reads "The same. The [[law|senate]]-house" [009-001], situating it within the same Athenian world as [[timon|Timon]]'s residence but marking it as a distinct space of authority. The Senate is composed of unnamed senators who speak collectively — "[[first-senator|First Senator]]," "[[second-senator|Second Senator]]" — suggesting an institution rather than individuals. Their collective voice is the voice of the state: impersonal, procedural, and merciless.

## Key Scenes

- **Act 9** — [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] enters the senate-house to plead for a fellow soldier condemned to death for killing a man in a brawl. His argument — that military service should earn mercy — is met with procedural rigidity: "We are for law: he dies; urge it no more" [009-020]. When [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] persists, the senators banish him. The scene exposes the gap between the language of [[law|justice]] ("[[mercy|pity]] is the virtue of the law" [009-008]) and the practice of power.

## Symbolic Significance

The [[law|senate]]-house represents institutional [[athens|Athens]] — the [[athens|Athens]] of law, order, and hierarchy that [[timon|Timon]]'s [[bounty|generosity]] briefly transcended and that [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]'s sword will ultimately conquer. Its single scene is enough to establish that the play's political world is as corrupt as its social world. The senators who speak of mercy while dealing in death are the public face of the same ingratitude that [[timon|Timon]]'s false [[friendship|friends]] practise in private. [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]'s banishment from the senate-house mirrors [[timon|Timon]]'s exile from his own house: both men are expelled from the spaces that defined them.
