[001-001] [[athens|Athens]]. A hall in **[[timon|Timon]]’s** house.

::: table pn=001-002

<!-- pn=001-002 -->|  | *Enter **[[poet|Poet]]**, **[[painter|Painter]]**, **[[jeweller|Jeweller]]**, **[[merchant|Merchant]]**, and others, at several doors.* |

<!-- pn=001-003 -->| Poet | Good day, sir. |

<!-- pn=001-004 -->| Painter | I am glad you’re well. |

<!-- pn=001-005 -->| Poet | I have not seen you long: how goes the world? |

<!-- pn=001-006 -->| Painter | It wears, sir, as it grows. |

<!-- pn=001-007 -->| Poet | Ay, that’s well known: But what particular rarity? what strange, Which manifold record not matches? See, Magic of bounty! all these spirits thy power Hath conjured to attend. I know the merchant. |

<!-- pn=001-008 -->| Painter | I know them both; th’ other’s a jeweller. |

<!-- pn=001-009 -->| Merchant | O, ’tis a worthy lord. |

<!-- pn=001-010 -->| Jeweller | Nay, that’s most fix’d. |

<!-- pn=001-011 -->| Merchant | A most incomparable man, breathed, as it were, To an untirable and continuate [[kindness|goodness]]: He passes. |

<!-- pn=001-012 -->| Jeweller | I have a jewel here﻿— |

<!-- pn=001-013 -->| Merchant | O, pray, let’s see’t: for the Lord Timon, sir? |

<!-- pn=001-014 -->| Jeweller | If he will touch the estimate: but, for that﻿— |

<!-- pn=001-015 -->| Poet | *Reciting to himself.* “When we for recompense have praised the vile, It stains the glory in that happy verse Which aptly sings the good.” |

<!-- pn=001-016 -->| Merchant | ’Tis a good form. *Looking at the jewel.* |

<!-- pn=001-017 -->| Jeweller | And rich: here is a water, look ye. |

<!-- pn=001-018 -->| Painter | You are rapt, sir, in some work, some dedication To the great lord. |

<!-- pn=001-019 -->| Poet | A thing slipp’d idly from me. Our poesy is as a gum, which oozes From whence ’tis nourish’d: the fire i’ the flint Shows not till it be struck; our gentle flame Provokes itself and like the current flies Each bound it chafes. What have you there? |

<!-- pn=001-020 -->| Painter | A picture, sir. When comes your book forth? |

<!-- pn=001-021 -->| Poet | Upon the heels of my presentment, sir. Let’s see your piece. |

<!-- pn=001-022 -->| Painter | ’Tis a good piece. |

<!-- pn=001-023 -->| Poet | So ’tis: this comes off well and excellent. |

<!-- pn=001-024 -->| Painter | Indifferent. |

<!-- pn=001-025 -->| Poet | Admirable: how this grace Speaks his own standing! what a mental power This eye shoots forth! how big imagination Moves in this lip! to the dumbness of the gesture One might interpret. |

<!-- pn=001-026 -->| Painter | It is a pretty mocking of the life. Here is a touch; is’t good? |

<!-- pn=001-027 -->| Poet | I will say of it, It tutors nature: artificial strife Lives in these touches, livelier than life. |

<!-- pn=001-028 -->|  | *Enter certain **Senators**, and pass over.* |

<!-- pn=001-029 -->| Painter | How this lord is follow’d! |

<!-- pn=001-030 -->| Poet | The senators of Athens: happy man! |

<!-- pn=001-031 -->| Painter | Look, more! |

<!-- pn=001-032 -->| Poet | You see this confluence, this great flood of visitors. I have, in this rough work, shaped out a man, Whom this beneath world doth embrace and hug With amplest [[feast|entertainment]]: my free drift Halts not particularly, but moves itself In a wide sea of wax: no levell’d malice Infects one comma in the course I hold; But flies an eagle flight, bold and forth on, Leaving no tract behind. |

<!-- pn=001-033 -->| Painter | How shall I understand you? |

<!-- pn=001-034 -->| Poet | I will unbolt to you. You see how all conditions, how all minds, As well of glib and slippery creatures as Of [[death|grave]] and austere quality, tender down Their services to Lord Timon: his large fortune Upon his good and gracious nature hanging Subdues and properties to his love and tendance All sorts of hearts; yea, from the glass-faced flatterer To [[apemantus|Apemantus]], that few things loves better Than to abhor himself: even he drops down The knee before him, and returns in peace Most rich in Timon’s nod. |

<!-- pn=001-035 -->| Painter | I saw them speak together. |

<!-- pn=001-036 -->| Poet | Sir, I have upon a high and pleasant hill Feign’d [[fortune|Fortune]] to be throned: the base o’ the mount Is rank’d with all deserts, all kind of natures, That labour on the bosom of this sphere To propagate their states: amongst them all, Whose eyes are on this sovereign lady fix’d, One do I personate of Lord Timon’s frame, Whom [[fortune|Fortune]] with her ivory hand wafts to her; Whose present grace to present slaves and servants Translates his rivals. |

<!-- pn=001-037 -->| Painter | ’Tis conceived to scope. This throne, this Fortune, and this hill, methinks, With one man beckon’d from the rest below, Bowing his head against the sleepy mount To climb his happiness, would be well express’d In our condition. |

<!-- pn=001-038 -->| Poet | Nay, sir, but hear me on. All those which were his fellows but of late, Some better than his value, on the moment Follow his strides, his lobbies fill with tendance, Rain sacrificial whisperings in his ear, Make sacred even his stirrup, and through him Drink the free air. |

<!-- pn=001-039 -->| Painter | Ay, marry, what of these? |

<!-- pn=001-040 -->| Poet | When Fortune in her shift and change of mood Spurns down her late beloved, all his dependants Which labour’d after him to the mountain’s top Even on their knees and hands, let him slip down, Not one accompanying his declining foot. |

<!-- pn=001-041 -->| Painter | ’Tis common: A thousand moral paintings I can show That shall demonstrate these quick blows of Fortune’s More pregnantly than words. Yet you do well To show Lord Timon that mean eyes have seen The foot above the head. |

<!-- pn=001-042 -->|  | *Trumpets sound. Enter **Lord Timon**, addressing himself courteously to every suitor; a **Messenger** from **[[ventidius|Ventidius]]** talking with him; **[[lucilius|Lucilius]]** and other **Servants** following.* |

<!-- pn=001-043 -->| Timon | Imprison’d is he, say you? |

<!-- pn=001-044 -->| Messenger | Ay, my good lord: five talents is his debt, His means most short, his creditors most strait: Your honourable letter he desires To those have shut him up; which failing, Periods his comfort. |

<!-- pn=001-045 -->| Timon | Noble Ventidius! Well; I am not of that feather to shake off My friend when he must need me. I do know him A gentleman that well deserves a help: Which he shall have: I’ll pay the debt, and free him. |

<!-- pn=001-046 -->| Messenger | Your lordship ever binds him. |

<!-- pn=001-047 -->| Timon | Commend me to him: I will send his ransom; And being enfranchised, bid him come to me. ’Tis not enough to help the feeble up, But to support him after. Fare you well. |

<!-- pn=001-048 -->| Messenger | All happiness to your honour! *Exit.* |

<!-- pn=001-049 -->|  | *Enter an **[[old-athenian|Old Athenian]]**.* |

<!-- pn=001-050 -->| Old Athenian | Lord Timon, hear me speak. |

<!-- pn=001-051 -->| Timon | Freely, good father. |

<!-- pn=001-052 -->| Old Athenian | Thou hast a servant named Lucilius. |

<!-- pn=001-053 -->| Timon | I have so: what of him? |

<!-- pn=001-054 -->| Old Athenian | Most noble Timon, call the man before thee. |

<!-- pn=001-055 -->| Timon | Attends he here, or no? Lucilius! |

<!-- pn=001-056 -->| Lucilius | Here, at your lordship’s service. |

<!-- pn=001-057 -->| Old Athenian | This fellow here, Lord Timon, this thy creature, By night frequents my house. I am a man That from my first have been inclined to thrift; And my estate deserves an heir more raised Than one which holds a trencher. |

<!-- pn=001-058 -->| Timon | Well; what further? |

<!-- pn=001-059 -->| Old Athenian | One only daughter have I, no kin else, On whom I may confer what I have got: The maid is fair, o’ the youngest for a bride, And I have bred her at my dearest cost In qualities of the best. This man of thine Attempts her love: I prithee, noble lord, Join with me to forbid him her resort; Myself have spoke in vain. |

<!-- pn=001-060 -->| Timon | The man is honest. |

<!-- pn=001-061 -->| Old Athenian | Therefore he will be, Timon: His honesty rewards him in itself; It must not bear my daughter. |

<!-- pn=001-062 -->| Timon | Does she love him? |

<!-- pn=001-063 -->| Old Athenian | She is young and apt: Our own precedent passions do instruct us What levity’s in youth. |

<!-- pn=001-064 -->| Timon | *To **Lucilius**.* Love you the maid? |

<!-- pn=001-065 -->| Lucilius | Ay, my good lord, and she accepts of it. |

<!-- pn=001-066 -->| Old Athenian | If in her marriage my consent be missing, I call the gods to witness, I will choose Mine heir from forth the beggars of the world, And dispossess her all. |

<!-- pn=001-067 -->| Timon | How shall she be endow’d, If she be mated with an equal husband? |

<!-- pn=001-068 -->| Old Athenian | Three talents on the present; in future, all. |

<!-- pn=001-069 -->| Timon | This gentleman of mine hath served me long: To build his fortune I will strain a little, For ’tis a bond in men. Give him thy daughter: What you bestow, in him I’ll counterpoise, And make him weigh with her. |

<!-- pn=001-070 -->| Old Athenian | Most noble lord, Pawn me to this your honour, she is his. |

<!-- pn=001-071 -->| Timon | My hand to thee; mine honour on my promise. |

<!-- pn=001-072 -->| Lucilius | Humbly I thank your lordship: never may The state or fortune fall into my keeping, Which is not owed to you! *Exeunt **Lucilius** and **Old Athenian**.* |

<!-- pn=001-073 -->| Poet | Vouchsafe my labour, and long live your lordship! |

<!-- pn=001-074 -->| Timon | I thank you; you shall hear from me anon: Go not away. What have you there, my friend? |

<!-- pn=001-075 -->| Painter | A piece of painting, which I do beseech Your lordship to accept. |

<!-- pn=001-076 -->| Timon | Painting is welcome. The painting is almost the natural man; For since [[shame|dishonour]] traffics with man’s nature, He is but outside: these pencill’d figures are Even such as they give out. I like your work; And you shall find I like it: wait attendance Till you hear further from me. |

<!-- pn=001-077 -->| Painter | The gods preserve ye! |

<!-- pn=001-078 -->| Timon | Well fare you, gentleman: give me your hand; We must needs dine together. Sir, your jewel Hath suffer’d under praise. |

<!-- pn=001-079 -->| Jeweller | What, my lord! dispraise? |

<!-- pn=001-080 -->| Timon | A more satiety of commendations. If I should pay you for’t as ’tis extoll’d, It would unclew me quite. |

<!-- pn=001-081 -->| Jeweller | My lord, ’tis rated As those which sell would give: but you well know, Things of like value differing in the owners Are prized by their masters: believe’t, dear lord, You mend the jewel by the wearing it. |

<!-- pn=001-082 -->| Timon | Well mock’d. |

<!-- pn=001-083 -->| Merchant | No, my good lord; he speaks the common tongue, Which all men speak with him. |

<!-- pn=001-084 -->| Timon | Look, who comes here: will you be chid? |

<!-- pn=001-085 -->|  | *Enter **Apemantus**.* |

<!-- pn=001-086 -->| Jeweller | We’ll bear, with your lordship. |

<!-- pn=001-087 -->| Merchant | He’ll spare none. |

<!-- pn=001-088 -->| Timon | Good morrow to thee, gentle Apemantus! |

<!-- pn=001-089 -->| Apemantus | Till I be gentle, stay thou for thy good morrow; When thou art Timon’s dog, and these knaves honest. |

<!-- pn=001-090 -->| Timon | Why dost thou call them knaves? thou know’st them not. |

<!-- pn=001-091 -->| Apemantus | Are they not Athenians? |

<!-- pn=001-092 -->| Timon | Yes. |

<!-- pn=001-093 -->| Apemantus | Then I repent not. |

<!-- pn=001-094 -->| Jeweller | You know me, Apemantus? |

<!-- pn=001-095 -->| Apemantus | Thou know’st I do: I call’d thee by thy name. |

<!-- pn=001-096 -->| Timon | Thou art proud, Apemantus. |

<!-- pn=001-097 -->| Apemantus | Of nothing so much as that I am not like Timon. |

<!-- pn=001-098 -->| Timon | Whither art going? |

<!-- pn=001-099 -->| Apemantus | To knock out an honest Athenian’s brains. |

<!-- pn=001-100 -->| Timon | That’s a deed thou’lt die for. |

<!-- pn=001-101 -->| Apemantus | Right, if doing nothing be death by the law. |

<!-- pn=001-102 -->| Timon | How likest thou this picture, Apemantus? |

<!-- pn=001-103 -->| Apemantus | The best, for the innocence. |

<!-- pn=001-104 -->| Timon | Wrought he not well that painted it? |

<!-- pn=001-105 -->| Apemantus | He wrought better that made the painter; and yet he’s but a filthy piece of work. |

<!-- pn=001-106 -->| Painter | You’re a dog. |

<!-- pn=001-107 -->| Apemantus | Thy mother’s of my generation: what’s she, if I be a dog? |

<!-- pn=001-108 -->| Timon | Wilt dine with me, Apemantus? |

<!-- pn=001-109 -->| Apemantus | No; I eat not lords. |

<!-- pn=001-110 -->| Timon | An thou shouldst, thou’ldst anger ladies. |

<!-- pn=001-111 -->| Apemantus | O, they eat lords; so they come by great bellies. |

<!-- pn=001-112 -->| Timon | That’s a lascivious apprehension. |

<!-- pn=001-113 -->| Apemantus | So thou apprehendest it: take it for thy labour. |

<!-- pn=001-114 -->| Timon | How dost thou like this jewel, Apemantus? |

<!-- pn=001-115 -->| Apemantus | Not so well as [[honesty|plain-dealing]], which will not cost a man a doit. |

<!-- pn=001-116 -->| Timon | What dost thou think ’tis [[honour|worth]]? |

<!-- pn=001-117 -->| Apemantus | Not worth my thinking. How now, poet! |

<!-- pn=001-118 -->| Poet | How now, philosopher! |

<!-- pn=001-119 -->| Apemantus | Thou liest. |

<!-- pn=001-120 -->| Poet | Art not one? |

<!-- pn=001-121 -->| Apemantus | Yes. |

<!-- pn=001-122 -->| Poet | Then I lie not. |

<!-- pn=001-123 -->| Apemantus | Art not a poet? |

<!-- pn=001-124 -->| Poet | Yes. |

<!-- pn=001-125 -->| Apemantus | Then thou liest: look in thy last work, where thou hast feigned him a worthy fellow. |

<!-- pn=001-126 -->| Poet | That’s not feigned; he is so. |

<!-- pn=001-127 -->| Apemantus | Yes, he is worthy of thee, and to pay thee for thy labour: he that loves to be flattered is worthy o’ the flatterer. Heavens, that I were a lord! |

<!-- pn=001-128 -->| Timon | What wouldst do then, Apemantus? |

<!-- pn=001-129 -->| Apemantus | E’en as Apemantus does now; hate a lord with my heart. |

<!-- pn=001-130 -->| Timon | What, thyself? |

<!-- pn=001-131 -->| Apemantus | Ay. |

<!-- pn=001-132 -->| Timon | Wherefore? |

<!-- pn=001-133 -->| Apemantus | That I had no angry wit to be a lord. Art not thou a merchant? |

<!-- pn=001-134 -->| Merchant | Ay, Apemantus. |

<!-- pn=001-135 -->| Apemantus | Traffic confound thee, if the gods will not! |

<!-- pn=001-136 -->| Merchant | If traffic do it, the gods do it. |

<!-- pn=001-137 -->| Apemantus | Traffic’s thy god; and thy god confound thee! |

<!-- pn=001-138 -->|  | *Trumpet sounds. Enter a **Messenger**.* |

<!-- pn=001-139 -->| Timon | What trumpet’s that? |

<!-- pn=001-140 -->| Messenger | ’Tis [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]], and some twenty horse, All of [[friendship|companionship]]. |

<!-- pn=001-141 -->| Timon | Pray, entertain them; give them guide to us. *Exeunt some Attendants.* You must needs dine with me: go not you hence Till I have thank’d you: when dinner’s done, Show me this piece. I am joyful of your sights. |

<!-- pn=001-142 -->|  | *Enter **Alcibiades**, with the rest.* |

<!-- pn=001-143 -->|  | Most welcome, sir! |

<!-- pn=001-144 -->| Apemantus | So, so, there! Aches contract and starve your supple joints! That there should be small love ’mongst these sweet knaves, And all this courtesy! The strain of man’s bred out Into baboon and monkey. |

<!-- pn=001-145 -->| Alcibiades | Sir, you have saved my longing, and I feed Most hungerly on your sight. |

<!-- pn=001-146 -->| Timon | Right welcome, sir! Ere we depart, we’ll share a bounteous time In different pleasures. Pray you, let us in. *Exeunt all except **Apemantus**.* |

<!-- pn=001-147 -->|  | *Enter two **Lords**.* |

<!-- pn=001-148 -->| [[first-lord|First Lord]] | What time o’ day is’t, Apemantus? |

<!-- pn=001-149 -->| Apemantus | Time to be honest. |

<!-- pn=001-150 -->| First Lord | That time serves still. |

<!-- pn=001-151 -->| Apemantus | The more accursed thou, that still omitt’st it. |

<!-- pn=001-152 -->| [[second-lord|Second Lord]] | Thou art going to Lord Timon’s feast? |

<!-- pn=001-153 -->| Apemantus | Ay, to see meat fill knaves and wine heat fools. |

<!-- pn=001-154 -->| Second Lord | Fare thee well, fare thee well. |

<!-- pn=001-155 -->| Apemantus | Thou art a fool to bid me farewell twice. |

<!-- pn=001-156 -->| Second Lord | Why, Apemantus? |

<!-- pn=001-157 -->| Apemantus | Shouldst have kept one to thyself, for I mean to give thee none. |

<!-- pn=001-158 -->| First Lord | Hang thyself! |

<!-- pn=001-159 -->| Apemantus | No, I will do nothing at thy bidding: make thy requests to thy friend. |

<!-- pn=001-160 -->| Second Lord | Away, unpeaceable dog, or I’ll spurn thee hence! |

<!-- pn=001-161 -->| Apemantus | I will fly, like a dog, the heels o’ the ass. *Exit.* |

<!-- pn=001-162 -->| First Lord | He’s opposite to humanity. Come, shall we in, And taste Lord Timon’s bounty? he outgoes The very heart of kindness. |

<!-- pn=001-163 -->| Second Lord | He pours it out; Plutus, the god of gold, Is but his steward: no meed, but he repays Sevenfold above itself; no gift to him, But breeds the giver a return exceeding All use of quittance. |

<!-- pn=001-164 -->| First Lord | The noblest mind he carries That ever govern’d man. |

<!-- pn=001-165 -->| Second Lord | Long may he live in fortunes! Shall we in? |

<!-- pn=001-166 -->| First Lord | I’ll keep you company. *Exeunt.* |
