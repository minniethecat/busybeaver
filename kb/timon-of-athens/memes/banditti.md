# Banditti

## Role in the Play

The Banditti are a group of outlaws and thieves who appear in Act 4, Scene 3, encountering [[timon|Timon]] in [[the-woods|the wilderness]] shortly after he has discovered [[gold]]. Unlike the other visitors who seek [[timon|Timon]] for selfish purposes -- the [[poet|Poet]] and [[painter|Painter]] who pretend [[friendship|friendship]], the senators who seek his military leadership -- the Banditti are refreshingly direct: they admit they are thieves and that they have come for [[gold]]. This honesty earns them a measure of respect from [[timon|Timon]], who declares, "Yet thanks I must you con / That you are thieves profess'd, that you work not / In holier shapes" (013-151). The Banditti serve a dual dramatic function: they embody the lawlessness that exists beyond [[athens|Athens]]' walls, and they become instruments of [[timon|Timon]]'s misanthropic revenge against the society that betrayed him.

## Key Scenes

**Act 4, Scene 3 — Hunting for Gold** (013-135): The three Banditti enter, having heard rumour that [[timon|Timon]] possesses treasure. Their dialogue reveals both their poverty and their pragmatism. The First Bandit speculates that "the mere want of gold, and the falling-from of his friends, drove him into this melancholy" (013-136), showing an awareness of [[timon|Timon]]'s circumstances. The Second Bandit confirms "It is noised he hath a mass of treasure" (013-137), and the Third Bandit proposes they "make the assay upon him" (013-138), calculating that if [[timon|Timon]] is indifferent he will freely share, and if covetous, they can simply take it.

**Act 4, Scene 3 — "Soldiers, not thieves"** (013-144): When the Banditti greet [[timon|Timon]] with "Save thee, Timon" (013-144), he immediately names them: "Now, thieves?" (013-145). They attempt to dignify themselves with "Soldiers, not thieves" (013-146), but [[timon|Timon]] cuts through the pretense: "Both too; and women's sons" (013-147). When they protest "We are not thieves, but men that much do want" (013-148), [[timon|Timon]] responds with a speech that rejects their claim of necessity by pointing to nature's abundance -- roots, springs, mast, and berries -- before paradoxically praising their honest profession of thievery.

**Act 4, Scene 3 — Timon's Gift and the "All is Theft" Speech** (013-151): [[timon|Timon]] gives the Banditti gold and delivers one of the play's most philosophically radical speeches, arguing that theft is not a deviation from nature but its fundamental principle: "The sun's a thief, and with his great attraction / Robs the vast sea: the moon's an arrant thief" (013-151). He urges them to "rob one another" and to "to Athens go, / Break open shops" (013-151), transforming the Banditti into unwilling agents of his anti-social vengeance.

**Act 4, Scene 3 — The Aftermath** (013-152): [[timon|Timon]]'s speech produces an unexpected effect. The Third Bandit admits that [[timon|Timon]] "Has almost charmed me from my profession, by persuading me to it" (013-152). The First Bandit recognizes the malice behind the advice: "'Tis in the malice of mankind that he thus advises us; not to have us thrive in our mystery" (013-153). The Second Bandit resolves to "give over my trade" (013-154), and the First Bandit closes the scene with the play's quietest declaration of moral independence: "there is no time so miserable but a man may be true" (013-155).

## Relationships

**[[timon|Timon]]**: The Banditti encounter [[timon|Timon]] at the peak of his misanthropy, and their interaction reveals [[timon|Timon]]'s willingness to weaponize anyone -- even outlaws -- against [[athens|Athens]]. Unlike the flatterers who abandoned [[timon|Timon]], the Banditti approach him with a kind of honesty that [[timon|Timon]] paradoxically respects, even as he uses them. Their reaction to his speech -- one is almost persuaded to continue thieving, another is dissuaded -- demonstrates [[timon|Timon]]'s continuing rhetorical power, even in his ruin.

**[[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]**: The Banditti appear in the same scene as [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]], and both receive [[timon|Timon]]'s gold with instructions to destroy [[athens|Athens]]. Where [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] represents organized rebellion with political grievances, the Banditti represent unstructured, apolitical lawlessness. Together, they show [[timon|Timon]]'s willingness to fund destruction at every level of society.

**[[athens|Athens]]**: The Banditti exist outside [[athens|Athens]]' social order, and their presence in the woods demonstrates that the city's corruption extends beyond its walls into the wilderness where law has no hold. [[timon|Timon]]'s instruction to "to Athens go, / Break open shops" (013-151) connects the Banditti directly to the city, suggesting that the line between civilization and outlawry is thinner than [[athens|Athens]]' citizens imagine.

## Character Analysis

The Banditti occupy a distinctive position in *Timon of Athens* as characters who are neither flatterers nor friends, senators nor servants, but outlaws whose honesty about their nature gives them a unique moral clarity. In a play where nearly every character performs some form of falsehood -- the flattering [[poet|Poet]], the dissembling senators, the self-serving [[steward|Steward]] -- the Banditti stand out precisely because they make no pretense about their intentions. They are thieves, and they admit it. This candour is what [[timon|Timon]] acknowledges when he thanks them for being "thieves profess'd, that you work not / In holier shapes" (013-151). His point is that the "boundless theft" of legitimate professions -- lawyers, physicians, merchants, priests -- is more dangerous than honest banditry because it wears the mask of respectability.

The Banditti also serve as a test case for [[timon|Timon]]'s universal misanthropy. If [[timon|Timon]] truly hates all mankind, what does he owe to men who are already outside the social contract? His decision to give them gold and urge them toward greater violence reveals that his [[misanthropy]] is not passive withdrawal but active hostility -- a desire to accelerate the destruction of the society that wronged him. Yet the Banditti's response complicates [[timon|Timon]]'s nihilistic vision. Rather than embracing his mandate to "rob one another" (013-151), the thieves are nearly persuaded to abandon their trade entirely. The Third Bandit is "charmed" from his profession by [[timon|Timon]]'s very argument for it; the Second Bandit resolves to reform; and the First Bandit's closing line -- "there is no time so miserable but a man may be true" (013-155) -- is the play's most concise affirmation that moral choice survives even in the worst circumstances. The Banditti, whom [[timon|Timon]] attempts to make instruments of his hatred, instead become witnesses to the possibility of [[honour|integrity]] that [[timon|Timon]] himself has forsaken.
