# The Poet and Painter's Visit to Timon

The Poet and Painter's visit to Timon in the woods is one of the final scenes of Act 4, occurring near the end of Timon's self-imposed exile in the wilderness. It is a bitterly ironic episode that mirrors the flattering artistic circle of Act 1, revealing that nothing fundamental has changed in Athenian society — flatterers still pursue gold, and artists still sell their praise to the highest bidder.

## Context and Motivation

The visit occurs after Timon has discovered buried gold while digging for roots ([[timon-finds-gold|Timon's Discovery of Gold]]). Word of his new wealth spreads rapidly through Athens, carried by servants and soldiers who encountered him. The Poet and the Painter, both of whom we met in Act 1 presenting works of praise to the wealthy Timon, hear these rumors and immediately resolve to track him down. Their motivation is transparent from the outset: they come not for love or loyalty, but for gold. The Painter acknowledges this bluntly, noting that "promising is the very air o' th' time" (2893-2894) — a cynical admission that empty promises are the currency of their age.

Shakespeare constructs this scene as a deliberate structural echo. In Act 1, the Poet describes an allegorical work about Fortune's hill, where men climb to favor and then tumble down — a work he was presenting to Timon himself. Now, in Act 4, the actual Poet and Painter chase the fallen man up a different hill, drawn by the rumor of his second rise. The symmetry is exact and cruel.

## The Encounter

Timon, now deeply misanthropic after his [[water-banquet|Water Banquet]] and [[timons-banishment|self-banishment from Athens]], sees the artists approaching from a distance. He immediately recognizes their intention: "What a god's gold, that he is worshipp'd in a baser temple than where swine feed?" (2910-2911). Even before they speak, he has sized them up as gold-worshippers more base than the animals he now prefers to human company.

When the Poet and Painter arrive, they adopt a posture of loyal friendship, pretending they have sought him out from genuine concern. The Painter claims they "have heard of your afflictions" and came to offer comfort. The Poet, ever the wordsmith, spins elaborate praise of Timon's past generosity. Timon's response is masterful in its layered irony: he feigns warmth and welcome, playing the part of the grateful recipient of their affection. He calls them "honest men" repeatedly — a word choice loaded with savage irony, since he knows, and the audience knows, that they are anything but honest.

## The Gift of Gold

Timon gives both the Poet and the Painter gold. On the surface, this appears to be a return to his old habit of lavish gift-giving. But the context transforms it into an act of bitter mockery. By giving them gold, Timon confirms their true motivation: they came for money, and they get money. He performs the role of the dupe, but the performance itself exposes them. He instructs them to take the gold and "be gone" — there is no pretense of lasting friendship, no invitation to stay. The gold is a dismissal fee, a payment that says: you have been seen, you have been weighed, and you are worth exactly this.

After they depart, Timon drops the mask entirely and unleashes a curse: "Hence, pack! There's gold; you came for gold, ye slaves" (2954). The word "slaves" is telling — Timon now sees the flatterers not as fellow citizens engaged in a social game, but as slaves to their own greed, unfree in the most fundamental sense. He calls after them with a litany of contempt: "you copper-nose knaves... you botchers of the law... you cozeners... you cutpurses" — lumping the artists together with all the parasitic types he sees infesting civilization.

## Thematic Significance

This scene reinforces one of the play's central arguments: that human nature, particularly in its grasping and hypocritical dimensions, is essentially unchanging. The Poet and Painter have learned nothing from Timon's downfall. They have not reflected on their own complicity in a system of false friendship and purchased praise. The moment new gold appears, they pursue it with the same eagerness they showed in Act 1.

Timon's performance of warmth marks a significant development in his character. In Act 1, he was the sincere recipient of flattery, genuinely believing in the friendship he was buying. Now, he has become a kind of actor himself, using the tools of flattery and feigned warmth as weapons rather than gifts. His transformation is complete: he has gone from the deceived to the deceiver, though his deception is transparent and designed to expose rather than exploit.

The scene also serves as one of the last encounters Timon has before his death. After the Poet and Painter leave, Timon's isolation deepens. The brief return of Athenian society to his world only confirms the righteousness of his withdrawal. The woods, with all their privation, remain preferable to a city populated by such men.

## The Mirror of Act 1

The structural parallel between this scene and Act 1's opening is one of Shakespeare's most economical devices in the play. The Poet and Painter in Act 1 come to Timon's house bearing art and praise, expecting patronage. In Act 4, they come to the woods expecting the same. But Timon is no longer a patron; he is a prophet of misanthropy. Where once he received them with genuine warmth, now he receives them with theatrical irony. The mirror reveals that the artists have traveled nowhere at all — they are frozen in the posture of supplicants, and their art, for all its pretensions, has always been a means to an end: gold.
