## Overview

Timon's Great Feast in Act 2 is the play's spectacle of [[bounty|generosity]] at its apex. In a [[feast|banquet]]ing-room at [[timon|Timon]]'s house, "a great [[feast|banquet]] served in" with "loud music," the Athenian lords gather to enjoy their patron's [[feast|hospitality]]. The feast includes a masque of Cupid, introduced by a presenter, and culminates in [[timon|Timon]]'s emotional speech about [[friendship|friends]]hip — a speech whose sincerity will be brutally tested in the acts that follow. The feast is the last moment of [[timon|Timon]]'s golden world before the [[debt|credit]]ors arrive.

## Context

The feast occurs immediately after the play has established [[timon|Timon]]'s boundless [[bounty|liberality]] (Act 1: freeing [[ventidius|Ventidius]] from debt, endowing [[lucilius|Lucilius]]) and introduced [[apemantus|Apemantus]]'s cynical counterpoint. The [[feast|banquet]] is the social manifestation of [[timon|Timon]]'s philosophy of giving: food, wine, music, and performance flow without limit, and the guests — the same lords who will later refuse him loans — bask in his largesse. The masque of Cupid allegorizes the feast's theme: love and bounty are sovereign, and [[timon|Timon]] is their earthly embodiment.

## Key Moments

- **The [[feast|banquet]] setting**: "A [[feast|banquet]]ing-room in [[timon|Timon]]'s house. Hautboys playing loud music. A great [[feast|banquet]] served in" [002-002]. The stage direction establishes the scale of [[timon|Timon]]'s [[feast|hospitality]] — no modest dinner but a full ceremonial feast with professional musicians.

- **[[apemantus|Apemantus]]'s dissent**: While the lords feast, [[apemantus|Apemantus]] eats roots and speaks bitter truths: "Rich men sin, and I eat root" [002-019]. His presence at the banquet is a structural irony — the cynic is included in the [[bounty|generosity]] he despises.

- **The masque of Cupid**: Cupid enters with "Ladies as Amazons" with lutes and dances, offering an allegorical [[feast|entertainment]] that frames the feast as a celebration of love and bounty. The masque is one of the play's few moments of pure spectacle.

- **[[timon|Timon]]'s speech on [[friendship|friends]]hip**: "I do protest, / Myself were else a villain, that this company / Should take all comforts" — [[timon|Timon]]'s emotional declaration of love for his [[friendship|friends]], a moment of genuine feeling that the subsequent acts will render devastatingly ironic.

## Dramatic Function

The feast establishes the height from which [[timon|Timon]] will fall. By staging [[bounty|generosity]] as spectacle, the play sets up the betrayal that follows as not merely financial but moral — the lords who eat [[timon|Timon]]'s food and praise his name will, in the very next acts, deny him the smallest loan. The feast also introduces the play's central dramatic pattern: [[timon|Timon]] gives, the world takes, and no [[gratitude|reciprocity]] binds them.
