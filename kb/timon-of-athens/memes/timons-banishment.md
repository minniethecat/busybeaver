# Timon's Banishment from Athens

Timon's self-banishment from Athens marks the structural turning point of the play. Occurring immediately after the [[water-banquet|Water Banquet]], it is the moment when Timon transforms from a disappointed philanthropist into a committed misanthrope. Unlike Alcibiades, who is forcibly banished by the Senate, Timon exiles himself — but the voluntariness of the act does not diminish its violence. It is a declaration of separation as absolute as any legal decree.

## The Decision to Leave

After the Water Banquet, in which Timon served his false friends lukewarm water instead of the promised feast and drove them away with stones and curses, he makes a swift decision to abandon Athens entirely. His servant Flavius reports that "he is gone" — Timon has already left the city walls before anyone can reason with him. The departure is abrupt and irreversible. Timon does not announce his intention, negotiate terms, or leave a path for reconciliation. He simply walks out.

This is significant because Timon is not being expelled by the state. Unlike Alcibiades, who faces a formal sentence ("We banish thee for ever"), Timon has broken no law. His creditors may hound him, but the city has passed no judgment. His banishment is self-imposed, which makes it more radical in its implications: Timon is rejecting Athens, not the other way around. The move reverses the expected direction of exile — it is the citizen who finds the city unworthy, not the city that finds the citizen intolerable.

## The Curse Speech

Timon's farewell to Athens is one of the most rhetorically concentrated passages in the play. Standing at the city gates, he delivers a systematic curse against everything that constitutes civilized life:

> "Piety, and fear,  
> Religion to the gods, peace, justice, truth,  
> Domestic awe, night-rest, and neighbourhood,  
> Instruction, manners, mysteries, and trades,  
> Degrees, observances, customs, and laws,  
> Decline to your confounding contraries" (2037-2042)

The list is exhaustive in its scope. It moves from the cosmic (piety, religion, fear of the gods) through the social (peace, justice, truth, neighbourhood) to the institutional (instruction, degrees, customs, laws). Nothing is spared. Timon is not merely angry at individuals; he wishes for the collapse of the entire structure of civilization. The phrase "confounding contraries" suggests a world in which every ordered thing reverts to its opposite — where the stable categories by which society functions dissolve into chaos.

This curse is often read alongside King Lear's storm speech as one of Shakespeare's most powerful invocations of cosmic disorder. But where Lear calls on the storm to punish ingratitude, Timon goes further: he calls for the annihilation of the conditions that make ingratitude possible. He wants the stage itself dismantled, not just the actors punished.

## "Timon Will to the Woods"

At the heart of the banishment speech is Timon's declaration: "Timon will to the woods" (2031). The use of the third person is striking. Timon speaks of himself as if from outside, as if the Timon who goes to the woods is already a different person from the Timon who lived in Athens. This grammatical distancing mirrors his psychological transformation: the social self is being shed, and a new, solitary self is being born in the wilderness.

The woods represent the antithesis of Athens. Where Athens is organized by laws, customs, degrees, and commerce, the woods are ungoverned, primitive, and indifferent to human hierarchy. Timon seeks out the "brother of the earth" — a phrase that invokes a pre-civilizational state, a kinship with beasts and soil rather than with men and institutions. His most famous line from this transition is "Destruction fang mankind!" (2032), a compact curse that personifies destruction as a beast sinking its fangs into the human species.

## Parallel with Alcibiades

The play structures Timon's and Alcibiades's banishments as parallel events, inviting comparison. Alcibiades is exiled by the Senate for arguing against the death sentence of a soldier who killed a man in a brawl. His banishment is a legal act, imposed from without, and he responds with a vow of revenge: he will raise an army and return to destroy Athens. His trajectory is outward and then back — exile with a return plotted.

Timon's banishment has no return. He does not vow revenge in the active, martial sense. He does not plan to raise an army. His revenge takes the form of a curse — a verbal, almost magical, invocation of destruction. And where Alcibiades's grievance is specific (the Senate's injustice in a particular case), Timon's grievance is total: Athenian society, and ultimately all human society, is rotten.

The two banishments together present a diptych of exile: one political and practical, the other philosophical and absolute. Alcibiades can be satisfied when the Senate is punished and the city is taken. Timon can never be satisfied, because the offense is existence itself.

## Structural Role

The self-banishment divides the play into two distinct halves. Everything before it takes place within the walls of Athens — in Timon's house, at the Senate, in spaces defined by social relationships. Everything after it takes place in the woods or on the shore — in spaces defined by the absence of society. The physical movement from city to wilderness maps onto a psychological movement from sociability to solitude, from trust to paranoia, from philanthropy to misanthropy.

This structural division is unusually sharp for Shakespeare. In many of his tragedies, the protagonist's relationship with the state evolves gradually across the five acts. Here, the break is clean and irreversible. Once Timon passes through the city gates, he never returns through them. The final act will bring Alcibiades back to Athens, but Timon will die in the wilderness, his grave marked only by an epitaph of his own composition — a final act of self-authorship that completes the logic of the self-banishment.

## Comparison with Coriolanus

The self-banishment invites comparison with [[coriolanus|Coriolanus]], who also turns his back on Rome. Coriolanus's exile is forced by the tribunes, and his response is to join the Volscians and march against his own city. His grievance, like Alcibiades's, is political and personal. Timon's grievance is existential. Coriolanus still believes in martial honor, in the virtue of the warrior, in the possibility of a society led by the aristocratic few. Timon believes in nothing. His self-banishment is therefore more absolute: Coriolanus wants a different Rome; Timon wants no city at all.

The banishment speech, with its systematic demolition of all social values, stands as one of Shakespeare's most uncompromising statements of anti-civilizational thought. It is a document of pure negation, and it sets the terms for all of Timon's subsequent encounters in the woods — encounters that will only confirm the rightness, in his eyes, of his departure.
