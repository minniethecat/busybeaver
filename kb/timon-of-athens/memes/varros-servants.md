## Role

Varro's two servants appear among the crowd of [[debt|credit]]ors' agents who gather at [[timon|Timon]]'s house to demand payment. They serve a master named Varro — a usurer or creditor who is never seen on stage — and their presence dramatises the transformation of [[timon|Timon]]'s social world from a network of [[bounty|gift]]-giving to an impersonal system of financial extraction. The doubling of the servants (two of them for a single master) emphasises the anonymous, fungible nature of the economic forces that overwhelm [[timon|Timon]]. (008-002–008-070)

## Key Scenes

**The Gathering Outside Timon's House** (008-002–008-070): Varro's two servants enter alongside the Servant of [[lucius|Lucius]], meeting [[titus|Titus]], [[hortensius|Hortensius]], and other creditors' agents who are waiting for [[timon|Timon]] to emerge. The First Servant greets the assembly with "Well met; good morrow, Titus and Hortensius" [008-003], establishing the collegial yet businesslike tone of the scene. When [[titus|Titus]] observes that all are there on the same errand — "So is theirs and ours" — the First Servant confirms the collective nature of their mission [008-007].

**The Presentation of Bills** (008-054–008-069): When [[timon|Timon]] finally appears in a rage, the creditors' servants press forward with their demands. Varro's First Servant announces "My lord—" and the Second echoes "My lord—" [008-067–008-068], but the text does not report their specific amounts; [[timon|Timon]] cuts them off before they can state their sums. The doubling of their entreaty — two voices for one debt — reinforces the sense of multiplication and pressure that characterises the scene of economic siege.

**The Aftermath** (008-070): After [[timon|Timon]]'s furious exit, [[hortensius|Hortensius]] delivers the scene's verdict: "these debts may well be called desperate ones, for a madman owes 'em." The Varro servants, though silent in this moment, are implicated in the collective judgment — they are the instruments of a system that [[hortensius|Hortensius]] himself finds "against my heart" [008-022].

## Relationships

**Varro (their master)**: Varro is never seen or heard. The servants are his proxies, his economic will made flesh. His name carries Roman associations — Varro was a Roman scholar and polymath — which in a play so concerned with classical [[athens|Athens]] may carry ironic weight: the name of learning attached to the most impersonal of financial transactions.

**[[timon|Timon]]**: The servants have no personal relationship with [[timon|Timon]]; they are purely functional figures. Their demands are not motivated by [[ingratitude|ingratitude]] (as the lords' refusals are) but by the impersonal logic of [[debt|credit]]. This makes them, in a sense, less culpable than the [[friendship|false friends]] — and also more terrifying, because they represent a system in which individual moral choice is irrelevant.

**[[titus|Titus]] and [[hortensius|Hortensius]]**: Fellow creditors' servants. Varro's First Servant greets them warmly — "Well met; good morrow, Titus and Hortensius" — and their exchange has the quality of colleagues meeting at work. Together they form a chorus of ordinary men doing an ordinary job that happens, in this instance, to be destroying a man's life.

**[[flavius|Flavius]]**: When [[flavius|Flavius]] passes through the crowd in a muffled cloak, Varro's Second Servant tries to detain him — "By your leave, sir—" [008-037] — only to receive a withering rebuke. This brief exchange positions Varro's servants on the side of the pursuers, not the pursued.

## Character Analysis

The two Varro's servants are less characters than functions, and their very anonymity is the point. In a play that anatomises the difference between personal [[ingratitude|betrayal]] (the lords who refuse [[timon|Timon]]) and impersonal economic force (the system of [[debt|credit]] that destroys him), Varro's servants embody the latter. They have no personality, no moral qualms (unlike [[hortensius|Hortensius]], whose "it is against my heart" [008-022] registers a conscience), and no individual identity — there are two of them, and they are interchangeable.

The name "Varro" itself deserves notice. Marcus Terentius Varro was a Roman scholar renowned for his learning, yet in this play the name attaches to a usurer who sends servants to dun a ruined man. The irony is characteristically Shakespearean: names of dignity and learning harbour the same venality as any other. The unseen Varro, like the unseen senators who send [[caphis|Caphis]], represents the hidden structure of economic power — the masters who profit from debt while their servants do the dirty work of collection.

The doubling of the servants is a small but significant dramaturgical choice. Shakespeare could have sent one servant; he sends two. The effect is multiplication — the sense that [[timon|Timon]]'s creditors are not a handful of individuals but a swarm. Each creditor has a servant (or two), and each servant has a bill. The arithmetic of [[debt]] overwhelms the algebra of [[friendship]].
