# Pride

## Pride as Tragic Flaw

If Timon of Athens has a hamartia — a tragic flaw in the Aristotelian sense — it is pride. Not the vaulting ambition of Macbeth or the jealousy of Othello, but a subtler and more pervasive pride that infects every phase of Timon's trajectory: the pride of the benefactor, the pride of the victim, and finally the pride of the misanthrope. The play traces a transformation not in the fact of Timon's pride but in its object. He moves from pride in giving to pride in hating, but the pride itself never diminishes.

## Pride in Generosity

Timon's generosity is real — he gives freely and asks nothing in return — but it is also performative. His banquets are displays of magnificence designed to attract admiration. When Flavius, his steward, warns him that his estate is being consumed, Timon refuses to listen. "You make me marvel wherefore ere this time you had not fully laid my state before me," he says (338-339), blaming Flavius for not warning him sooner — even though Flavius has been warning him for some time and Timon has simply refused to hear. This is the pride of the man who cannot admit he has made a mistake.

Timon's giving is also a form of dominance. By placing everyone in his debt, he occupies the superior position in every relationship. He is the patron, the benefactor, the sun around whom lesser bodies orbit. When the poet describes Timon as "the fountain" from which all good things flow (65), he is flattering Timon, but he is also describing the power dynamic: Timon is the source, everyone else is the recipient. This is a position of immense pride, disguised as generosity.

## Pride in Refusal

When Timon's fortune collapses, his pride takes a new form: the refusal to accept help. His loyal steward Flavius offers to share his own small savings. Timon rejects this offer absolutely. His former general Alcibiades offers companionship in his exile, but Timon drives him away. The painter and poet return in the woods, and Timon scorns them. Even the senators, who come to offer reconciliation, are cursed and rejected.

On one level, Timon's refusal is understandable: he has been burned and is determined not to be burned again. But on another level, his refusal is pride masquerading as wisdom. He cannot accept help because accepting help would mean admitting need, and admitting need would mean abandoning the position of moral superiority he has constructed. Timon would rather be alone and "right" than connected and vulnerable.

## Pride in Misanthropy

The final transformation of Timon's pride is his misanthropic absolutism. Having been let down by a specific group of Athenians, Timon condemns all humanity. "I am Misanthropos and hate mankind," he announces (838). This is pride of the most comprehensive kind: Timon sets himself apart from and above the entire human race.

Apemantus, who knows something about pride himself, diagnoses Timon precisely: "The middle of humanity thou never knewest, but the extremity of both ends" (857-858). Timon has oscillated from extreme generosity to extreme hatred without ever passing through the moderate middle. This is the structural signature of pride: it cannot do moderation. It must be all or nothing, love or hate, giving or taking.

## The Pride of Others

Pride is not Timon's monopoly. Apemantus displays pride in his cynicism, taking pleasure in being the one who sees through everything. His philosophy is accurate — the flatterers are indeed parasites — but his manner is arrogant and uncharitable. The senators display institutional pride when they banish Alcibiades rather than admit they might be wrong. Even Alcibiades, the play's closest approximation to a hero, is driven partly by wounded pride: he has been publicly humiliated by banishment, and his siege of Athens is as much about honor as justice.

## The Structural Role of Pride

Pride is the engine of the play's catastrophes. Without Timon's pride, he would listen to Flavius and moderate his spending. Without the senators' pride, they would pardon Alcibiades's friend and avoid civil war. Without Timon's post-ruin pride, he might accept help and find a way back. Pride closes off every exit, forecloses every possibility of repair. The play suggests that pride is not merely one vice among many but the root vice — the one that makes all other errors irreversible.
