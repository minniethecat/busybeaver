## Overview

The Senators' plea to [[timon|Timon]] is the final confrontation between Athenian authority and the man it destroyed. In Act 5, with [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] marching on the city, the [[first-senator|First]] and [[second-senator|Second Senator]] come to the woods to beg [[timon|Timon]] to return as captain-general and lead the defence of [[athens|Athens]]. It is a scene of complete role reversal: the men who presided over a city that abandoned [[timon|Timon]] now kneel — figuratively — before the outcast, asking him to rescue them. [[timon|Timon]] responds not with forgiveness but with the play's most sustained exercise in savage irony, offering the senators not a general but a tree from which to hang themselves.

## Context

The plea occurs late in Act 5, immediately after [[timon|Timon]] has driven away the [[poet|Poet]] and [[painter|Painter]] — two more false friends drawn to his cave by the rumour of his [[gold|gold]] (014-043 to 014-045). [[flavius|Flavius]], who has already visited [[timon|Timon]] in Act 4 and been recognised as "one honest man" (013-166), now returns with the two senators, warning them that their mission is futile: "It is in vain that you would speak with Timon; / For he is set so only to himself / That nothing but himself which looks like man / Is friendly with him" (014-047). The senators press on regardless, driven by the desperation of a city under siege. The scene's dramatic power derives from the fact that the same political body that sat silent while [[timon|Timon]]'s false [[friendship|friends]] abandoned him to [[debt|bankruptcy]] now finds itself without any other recourse.

## Key Participants

- **[[timon|Timon]]** — The misanthropic exile, living in a cave, whose transformation from generous lord to hater of mankind is now complete. He has gold again, but only to fund destruction.
- **[[first-senator|First Senator]]** — The primary spokesman for the Athenian delegation, offering increasingly desperate entreaties. His tone shifts from formal diplomatic greeting to bleak acknowledgement of failure.
- **[[second-senator|Second Senator]]** — The second voice of the supplicant state, who frames Athens's change of heart with remarkable candour, confessing "forgetfulness too general, gross" (014-058).
- **[[flavius|Flavius]]** — [[timon|Timon]]'s loyal steward, who leads the senators to the cave but insists their mission is hopeless, acting as a reluctant intermediary between the world and his master's absolute misanthropy.

## Narrative Summary

The scene opens with [[flavius|Flavius]] and the two senators entering the woods near [[timon|Timon]]'s cave. [[flavius|Flavius]] tells the senators plainly that their errand is doomed: [[timon|Timon]] "is set so only to himself / That nothing but himself which looks like man / Is friendly with him" (014-047). But the [[first-senator|First Senator]] insists: "It is our part and promise to the Athenians / To speak with Timon" (014-048). The [[second-senator|Second Senator]] adds a note of qualified hope — "time, with his fairer hand, / Offering the fortunes of his former days, / The former man may make him" (014-049) — but this hope will prove groundless.

When [[timon|Timon]] emerges, his first words are a curse: "Thou sun, that comfort'st, burn! Speak, and be hang'd: / For each true word, a blister! and each false / Be as cauterizing to the root o' the tongue" (014-052). This greeting sets the tone for everything that follows. The senators present their case with remarkable self-awareness, conceding the city's collective guilt. The [[second-senator|Second Senator]] offers the play's most candid political confession: "They confess / Toward thee forgetfulness too general, gross: / Which now the public body, which doth seldom / Play the recanter, feeling in itself / A lack of Timon's aid, hath sense withal / Of its own fail" (014-058). They offer [[timon|Timon]] not just restoration but elevation — "special dignities" and "absolute power" (014-060) — if he will take the captainship and drive back [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]].

[[timon|Timon]] toys with them, feigning to be moved: "You witch me in it; / Surprise me to the very brink of tears" (014-059). But the performance is venomous. When the senators press for an answer, [[timon|Timon]] delivers his response in terms of deliberate, theatrical cruelty. He tells them he does not care if [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] kills his countrymen or sacks [[athens|Athens]]: "let him know, and tell him Timon speaks it, / In pity of our aged and our youth, / I cannot choose but tell him, that I care not" (014-063). His contempt extends to every Athenian throat — "there's not a whittle in the unruly camp / But I do prize it at my love before / The reverend'st throat in Athens" (014-063).

When the senators' hopes briefly flutter — [[timon|Timon]] says "I love my country" (014-067) and offers to "some kindness do them" (014-072) — he springs his trap. The kindness he offers is his tree: "Tell Athens, in the sequence of degree / From high to low throughout, that whoso please / To stop affliction, let him take his haste, / Come hither, ere my tree hath felt the axe, / And hang himself" (014-074). This is the climax of the scene and one of the cruelest jokes in Shakespearean drama — the promise of succour transformed into an invitation to suicide.

[[timon|Timon]] closes with a final, permanent farewell: "Come not to me again: but say to Athens, / Timon hath made his everlasting mansion / Upon the beached verge of the salt flood" (014-076). He names the sea as his grave and his gravestone as the city's oracle. The senators admit defeat: "His discontents are unremoveably / Coupled to nature" (014-077) and "Our hope in him is dead" (014-078). They exit, leaving [[timon|Timon]] alone — and leaving [[athens|Athens]] to face [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] without its greatest soldier.

## Significance

The scene is the culmination of [[timon|Timon]]'s trajectory from philanthropist to misanthrope. Every earlier betrayal — the false [[friendship|friends]] who refused him loans, the Senate that offered no defence — is now answered with a refusal so absolute it transcends personal [[anger|vengeance]] and becomes philosophical. [[timon|Timon]] is not merely rejecting the senators; he is rejecting the very possibility of social repair. His offer of the tree is a parody of his former [[bounty|generosity]]: where once he gave [[gold|wealth]] and [[feast|feasts]], he now gives only the means of self-destruction.

Politically, the scene dramatises a complete inversion of power. The senators who once embodied Athenian [[law|authority]] are now supplicants; the outcast they permitted to be ruined now holds their fate in his hands. Their confession of "forgetfulness too general, gross" (014-058) is an admission that the [[law|polis]] itself is structurally ungrateful — a theme the play has developed since the first creditors appeared at [[timon|Timon]]'s door. The "public body," as the [[second-senator|Second Senator]] calls it, "doth seldom / Play the recanter" (014-058); that it does so now is a measure not of moral growth but of military desperation.

The scene also sets up the play's final movement. [[timon|Timon]]'s refusal to defend [[athens|Athens]] clears the way for [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]'s conquest, and his announcement of his own impending [[death|death]] ("My long sickness / Of health and living now begins to mend," 014-065) prepares the audience for the end. The senators' exit — "It requires swift foot" (014-079) — carries the urgency of a state in existential crisis, a crisis [[timon|Timon]] has chosen to watch rather than avert.
