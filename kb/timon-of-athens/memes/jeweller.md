# Jeweller

## Role in the Play

The Jeweller appears in Scene I as part of the crowd of petitioners, artists, and merchants who surround [[timon|Timon]] in his [[wealth|prosperity]]. He offers a jewel for [[timon|Timon]]'s appraisal, his presence illustrating the gift economy of flattery and [[hope|expectation]] that sustains [[timon|Timon]]'s court. When [[apemantus|Apemantus]] arrives, the Jeweller becomes one of the targets of [[apemantus|the philosopher]]'s contempt. (001-010)

## Key Scenes

**The Offering of the Jewel** (001-010–001-017): The Jeweller presents his jewel to [[timon|Timon]], describing its quality: "I have a jewel here" (001-012). He defers to [[timon|Timon]]'s judgment — "If he will touch the estimate" (001-014) — making the valuation of his work dependent on the patron's favour. The [[merchant|Merchant]] vouches for the jewel's richness: "here is a water, look ye" (001-017). The scene shows how [[timon|Timon]]'s [[gold|wealth]] attracts not only [[flattery|flatterers]] but honest artisans hoping for patronage.

**The Encounter with [[apemantus|Apemantus]]** (001-079–001-094): When [[apemantus|Apemantus]] dispa[[anger|rage]]s [[timon|Timon]]'s [[bounty|generosity]], the Jeweller protests: "What, my lord! dispraise?" (001-079). He insists on the objective value of his craft — "My lord, 'tis rated As those which sell would give" (001-081) — attempting to distinguish honest commerce from the flattery that surrounds it. [[apemantus|Apemantus]] dismisses him along with everyone else: "You know me, [[apemantus|Apemantus]]?" / "I know thee not" is the implicit response to the Jeweller's attempt at recognition (001-094).

## Relationships

**[[timon|Timon]]**: The patron to whom the Jeweller presents his jewel. The relationship is professional rather than personal, representing the legitimate commerce that the play distinguishes from the false [[friendship|friends]]hip of the lords.

**[[merchant|Merchant]]**: Fellow tradesman who vouches for the Jeweller's work. Together they represent the commercial class that exists at the periphery of [[timon|Timon]]'s aristocratic circle.

**[[poet|Poet]]** and **[[painter|Painter]]**: Fellow suitors in Scene I. The Jeweller represents the third art — craftsmanship — alongside [[poet|the Poet]]'s verse and [[painter|the Painter]]'s portraiture.

**[[apemantus|Apemantus]]**: The cynic philosopher who treats the Jeweller with the same contempt he shows everyone else.
