# Lucilius

## Role in the Play

Lucilius is one of [[timon|Timon]]'s servants, appearing primarily in Act 1, Scene 1 in a subplot that mirrors the play's main action in miniature. Lucilius wishes to marry the daughter of an [[old-athenian|Old Athenian]], but the father objects because Lucilius lacks the [[gold|wealth]] to be a suitable match. [[timon|Timon]] intervenes — providing the dowry that makes the marriage possible — in an act of [[bounty|generosity]] that establishes his character before the larger plot unfolds. Lucilius reappears in Act 6 (or Act 3) conversing with three Strangers about [[timon|Timon]]'s fall, serving as a witness to and commentator on the [[ingratitude]] of the Athenian elite. His [[service|loyalty]] to [[timon|Timon]] contrasts with the lords' betrayal, making him part of the play's alternative community of [[trust|faith]]ful servants. (001-052)

## Key Scenes

**The Marriage Subplot** (001-052): The [[old-athenian|Old Athenian]] complains to [[timon|Timon]] that Lucilius, "this thy creature," frequents his house by night and courts his daughter. [[timon|Timon]]'s response — summoning Lucilius and asking simply "Love you the maid?" — cuts through the father's objections to the essential question. When Lucilius answers "Ay, my good lord, and she accepts of him," [[timon|Timon]] immediately provides the financial solution: he will match the daughter's dowry, making Lucilius an acceptable suitor. This scene establishes the pattern of [[timon|Timon]]'s [[bounty|generosity]]: he uses [[gold|wealth]] to solve human problems, expecting only the moral bond that his gift creates. (001-064)

**The Conversation with the Strangers** (006-002): Lucilius meets three Strangers who ask about [[timon|Timon]]'s [[wealth|fortune]]s. His responses — "Who, the [[timon|Lord Timon]]? he is my very good friend, and an honourable gentleman" — demonstrate the [[service|loyalty]] that the lords lack. When the Strangers ask whether [[timon|Timon]]'s ruin is true, Lucilius's defensiveness — "Fie, no, do not believe it" — reveals both his [[service|loyalty]] and his powerlessness: he can defend [[timon|Timon]]'s [[honour|reputation]] in conversation but cannot prevent his fall. (006-003)

**The Messenger** (006-012): Lucilius encounters [[servilius|Servilius]], another of Timon's servants, who is delivering messages to [[timon|Timon]]'s [[debt|credit]]ors. Their exchange dramatises the servants' collective [[shame|embarrassment]] at their master's fall — they are the unintended casualties of [[timon|Timon]]'s t[[anger|rage]]dy, men whose livelihoods depend on a system that has collapsed. (006-014)

## Relationships

**[[timon|Timon]]**: Lucilius is the first beneficiary of [[timon|Timon]]'s [[bounty|generosity]] that the audience witnesses, and his gratitude — "Never may that state or [[wealth|fortune]] fall into my keeping, which is not owed to you!" — establishes the standard of reciprocal [[debt|obligation]] that the lords will later betray. [[timon|Timon]]'s gift to Lucilius is the play's most uncomplicatedly successful act of [[bounty|generosity]]. (001-072)

**The [[old-athenian|Old Athenian]]**: The father of Lucilius's beloved, whose objections are based entirely on [[gold|wealth]]. His complaint — "My estate deserves an heir more raised / Than one which holds a trencher" — articulates the play's central equation of human [[honour|worth]] with [[gold|money]], an equation that will destroy [[timon|Timon]] as thoroughly as it nearly destroyed Lucilius's marriage prospects. (001-053)

**[[flavius|Flavius]]**: Lucilius is part of [[timon|Timon]]'s household alongside [[flavius|Flavius]], [[flaminius|Flaminius]], and [[servilius|Servilius]]. Together, these servants form a counter-community of [[service|loyalty]] that stands in pointed contrast to the false [[friendship]] of the Athenian lords. (001-042)

## Character Analysis

Lucilius's subplot is a structural miniature of the play: a man's happiness depends on [[gold|wealth]] he does not have; a [[gold|wealth]]y benefactor provides it; the question is whether the beneficiary will reciprocate when the benefactor needs help. The difference — and it is a crucial difference — is that Lucilius is not tested. The play does not show us Lucilius refusing [[timon|Timon]], because Lucilius, as a servant, has nothing to refuse with. His [[service|loyalty]] is never put to the material test that the lords fail. This structural choice is itself significant: the play suggests that genuine [[service|loyalty]] may only be possible among those who have nothing to give but [[service|loyalty]] itself. The lords fail because they have [[gold]]; Lucilius is never asked to give what he does not have, and his devotion — expressed in words alone — can remain pure precisely because it is never required to become material. (001-072)


## Notable Quotations

Lucilius's reaction to the news of [[lucullus|Lucullus]]'s denial reveals his own [[trust|faith]] in [[timon|Timon]] — and his naive [[trust|confidence]] that he himself would behave differently:

> What a strange case was that! now, before the gods, I am ashamed on't. Denied that honourable man! there was very little honour showed in't. For my own part, I must needs confess, I have received some small kindnesses from him, as money, plate, jewels and such-like trifles, nothing comparing to his; yet, had he mistook him and sent to me, I should ne'er have denied his occasion so many talents.
>
> (006-009)

Yet when [[servilius|Servilius]] arrives with [[timon|Timon]]'s request, Lucilius replicates the false [[friendship|friends]]' logic precisely — lamenting his own poor timing rather than the moral failure of refusal:

> What a wicked beast was I to disfurnish myself against such a good time, when I might ha' shown myself honourable!
>
> (006-020)
