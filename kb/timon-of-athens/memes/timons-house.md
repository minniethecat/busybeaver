## Overview

[[timon|Timon]]'s house is the play's central interior space, the stage upon which his [[bounty|generosity]] is performed and his ruin enacted. Over the course of the first ten scenes, the house transforms from a hall of feasting — where "a great [[feast|banquet]] served in" with "loud music" [002-002] — to a place besieged by [[debt|credit]]ors' servants. This transformation mirrors [[timon|Timon]]'s own trajectory from patron to debtor, and the house itself becomes a symbol of the instability of [[gold|wealth]] and the fragility of social status in a world governed by [[debt|credit]].

## Setting Description

The house contains at least two distinct spaces referenced in the stage directions: a hall ("A hall in [[timon|Timon]]'s house" [001-001, 004-001, 008-001]) and a [[feast|banquet]]ing-room ("A [[feast|banquet]]ing-room in [[timon|Timon]]'s house" [002-001, 010-001]). The hall is the public-facing space where suitors, servants, and [[debt|credit]]ors gather; [[timons-feast|the banquet]]ing-room is the private space of [[timon|Timon]]'s legendary [[feast|hospitality]]. The distinction between the two spaces maps onto the play's movement from public [[bounty|generosity]] to private ruin.

## Key Scenes

- **Act 1** — The hall fills with "a confluence, a great flood of visitors" [001-032], including [[poet|the Poet]], [[painter|Painter]], [[jeweller|Jeweller]], and [[merchant|Merchant]], all bearing gifts or seeking patronage. [[timon|Timon]] moves among them with effortless grace, dispensing favours.

- **Act 2** — The [[feast|banquet]]ing-room hosts the great feast with [[timons-feast|the masque of Cupid]]. Music, food, and performance create the play's most spectacular display of [[timon|Timon]]'s [[bounty|liberality]].

- **Act 4** — The hall becomes a place of siege. Creditors' servants — [[caphis|Caphis]], Varro's Servants, Isidore's Servant — gather to demand payment. The [[fool|Fool]] and [[apemantus|Apemantus]] provide the scene's bitter commentary.

- **Act 10** — The water [[feast|banquet]]. [[timon|Timon]] stages his revenge in [[timons-feast|the banquet]]ing-room, serving warm water and stones to his false [[friendship|friends]]. The house, once filled with guests, is now a theatre of recrimination.

## Symbolic Significance

[[timon|Timon]]'s house operates as a microcosm of his world. Its open doors in the early acts represent his philosophy of boundless giving; its besieged state in the middle acts represents the consequences of that philosophy. When [[timon|Timon]] abandons the house for the woods, the movement is irreversible — the house of [[bounty|generosity]] has become the house of debt, and there is no going back.
