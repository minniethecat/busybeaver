# Revenge in Timon of Athens

## Overview

Revenge is the engine of *[[timon-of-athens|Timon of Athens]]*'s second half, the motive that transforms [[timon|Timon]] from passive sufferer into active agent of destruction. Unlike the revenge t[[anger|rage]]dies of Shakespeare's contemporaries — *The Spanish T[[anger|rage]]dy*, *Hamlet*, *The Revenger's T[[anger|rage]]dy* — *[[timon|Timon]]* does not focus on a specific wrong demanding specific retribution. [[timon|Timon]]'s revenge is structural rather than personal: he funds [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]' campaign against [[athens|Athens]] not because [[athens|Athens]] killed his father but because [[athens|Athens]] failed to honour his gifts. This is revenge as economic critique — [[timon|Timon]] uses gold, the medium of his former [[bounty|generosity]], to purchase destruction, converting the gift economy of Acts I–II into the revenge economy of Acts IV–V.

## Key Passages

> Put up thy gold: go on—here's gold—go on; Be as a planetary plague, when Jove Will o'er some high-viced city hang his poison (013-039)

[[timon|Timon]]'s gift of gold to [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] is the play's central act of revenge — not a sword but a subsidy, not a threat but an investment. The imperative "go on" is repeated like a drumbeat, and the simile of [[gods|Jove]]'s planetary [[disease|plague]] transforms [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] from a disaffected general into an instrument of [[gods|divine]] retribution. [[timon|Timon]]'s revenge is vicarious — he funds others to do what he cannot do himself — and this vicariousness is both its power (his gold reaches further than his arm) and its pathos (he cannot even enjoy his own revenge).

> Here's gold. Go, suck the subtle blood o' the grape, Till the high fever seethe your blood to froth (013-151)

[[timon|Timon]]'s gift to the bandits extends the revenge economy to the criminal underworld. The gold is given not to sustain life but to accelerate death — "suck the subtle blood" until fever boils the blood to froth. This is anti-[[bounty|generosity]], a parody of [[timon|Timon]]'s former bounty, in which every gift is a poison and every beneficiary a victim.

> I'll give you gold, Rid me these villains from your companies: Hang them or stab them, drown them in a draught (014-043)

The acceleration of [[timon|Timon]]'s revenge rhetoric — "Hang them or stab them, drown them" — reveals the murderous logic underlying his entire post-betrayal existence. The list of killing methods is offered with the casualness of a menu, each option equally acceptable so long as the result is death. Revenge has become so abstract that any death will do, any victim will serve.

## Character Perspectives

**[[timon|Timon]]** transforms revenge from motive into identity. He does not seek particular vengeance against particular men — he seeks the destruction of the human species, or as much of it as his gold can reach. His revenge is the dark twin of his former [[bounty|generosity]]: the same absolutism, the same refusal of moderation, the same need to give everything.

**[[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]** channels [[timon|Timon]]'s gold into a political project of conquest and reform. His revenge against [[athens|Athens]] (for the Senate's refusal to pardon his soldier) is specific and achievable where [[timon|Timon]]'s is cosmic and impossible. The play's ending — [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] promising to restore order — suggests that political revenge can be productive in a way that personal revenge cannot.

**[[apemantus|Apemantus]]** refuses revenge as he refuses everything else. His consistency — he hated [[athens|Athens]] before [[timon|Timon]]'s betrayal and will hate it after [[timon|Timon]]'s revenge — makes him immune to the revenge dynamic. He is the play's proof that [[misanthropy|cynicism]] is a form of protection against the wounds that make revenge seem necessary.

## Critical Interpretations

*[[timon-of-athens|Timon of Athens]]* occupies an unusual position in the revenge t[[anger|rage]]dy tradition. Unlike Kyd, Marston, or Webster, Shakespeare does not give [[timon|Timon]] a specific crime to avenge — the betrayal is collective, the wrong is structural, and the revenge is correspondingly abstract. This abstraction has been read as Shakespeare's critique of the revenge genre itself: revenge requires a coherent moral universe in which wrongs can be identified and righted, but [[timon|Timon]]'s [[athens|Athens]] has no such coherence. In a world where everyone is complicit and no one is uniquely guilty, revenge is merely another form of consumption.
