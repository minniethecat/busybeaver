# Soldiers

The **Soldiers** are the armed followers of [[Alcibiades]] who appear primarily in [[Act 5]] of *Timon of Athens*, forming the military force that besieges Athens and ultimately brings about the play's resolution. Though unnamed and largely undifferentiated as individuals, the Soldiers function as a collective character whose presence shapes the closing movement of the drama.

## Military Role and Dramatic Function

The Soldiers constitute [[Alcibiades]]'s army, the instrument through which he translates his personal grievance against [[Athens]] into a credible threat of destruction. After the [[senators]] banish Alcibiades for pleading on behalf of a soldier condemned to death, he raises a force and marches on the city. The Soldiers thus embody the military dimension of Alcibiades's alternative social order — a community bound by loyalty and shared purpose, standing in pointed contrast to the transactional "friendship" that has collapsed around [[Timon]].

As a dramatic device, the Soldiers represent raw power external to the social world of Athens. Their appearance at the walls signals that the city's internal decay — its ingratitude, its corruption of justice, its reduction of all relationships to financial calculation — has provoked a response from outside. The Soldiers are not merely instruments of revenge; they are the physical manifestation of consequences that language and diplomacy cannot resolve.

## The Soldier as Messenger: Timon's Epitaph

A crucial dramatic moment involving the Soldiers occurs when one of them delivers [[Timon]]'s epitaph to [[Alcibiades]] near the end of Act 5 ([[TN 5.4.70-73]]). This unnamed soldier serves as a bridge between Timon's misanthropic solitude in the wilderness and Alcibiades's political-military world. The epitaph, which Timon composed before his death, reads:

> "Here lies a wretched corse, of wretched soul bereft:
> Seek not my name: a plague consume you wicked caitiffs left!"

The soldier who carries this message becomes a kind of unwitting mediator between two spheres that have remained separate throughout the play — Timon's absolute withdrawal and Alcibiades's active engagement with Athens. That a soldier delivers this final word from Timon is thematically significant: soldiers are agents of action in a world where Timon's words have proven powerless.

## Instruments of Justice and Mercy

The Soldiers occupy an ambivalent moral position. They arrive as instruments of retribution — Alcibiades has vowed to destroy Athens for its ingratitude — yet they ultimately become instruments of measured justice rather than indiscriminate slaughter. When Alcibiades spares the mass of Athenian citizens and limits his vengeance to his declared enemies ([[TN 5.4.56-63]]), the Soldiers follow his command without question. Their discipline and obedience contrast sharply with the faithlessness of the Athenian lords.

This disciplined mercy is significant. The Soldiers could easily become a destructive mob, but under Alcibiades's leadership they demonstrate the very virtues — loyalty, restraint, proportionality — that the Athenian elite have abandoned. Shakespeare uses them to suggest that honorable conduct is found not among the powerful but among those who serve.

## Comparison with Other Armed Groups

The Soldiers invite comparison with the **Banditti** (thieves) who encounter [[Timon]] in the woods in Act 4. Both groups operate outside the law, and both seek something from the established order. The Banditti are motivated by poverty and desperation; the Soldiers by loyalty to their commander and a sense of shared grievance. Timon gives the Banditti gold to encourage their criminality — a gesture of nihilistic misanthropy. Alcibiades, by contrast, directs his Soldiers toward a specific and limited political goal.

This contrast illuminates the play's treatment of power. The Banditti represent chaotic, undirected force — violence without purpose, mirroring Timon's own descent into undifferentiated rage. The Soldiers represent disciplined, purposive force — violence in service of a recognizable justice. Where Timon's gold corrupts the Banditti further, Alcibiades's command channels the Soldiers toward resolution.

## Loyalty and the Critique of False Friendship

The Soldiers' loyalty to [[Alcibiades]] forms the final element in the play's sustained critique of false friendship. The Athenian lords — [[Lucullus]], [[Lucius]], [[Sempronius]] — have all betrayed [[Timon]], refusing him aid in his financial distress despite having accepted his lavish gifts for years. In the Soldiers, Shakespeare presents a counter-model: men who follow their leader not for personal enrichment but out of genuine allegiance. Alcibiades's relationship with his Soldiers is the play's only functional social bond.

This loyalty functions structurally as well. The play ends not with Timon's death in the wilderness but with Alcibiades entering Athens at the head of his army. The final image is one of order restored — not the old order of corrupt senators, but a new dispensation backed by military force and personal fidelity. The Soldiers make this restoration possible.

## Thematic Significance

The Soldiers serve several interrelated thematic functions. They embody the **consequences of ingratitude**, demonstrating that Athens's betrayal of both Timon and Alcibiades has material costs. They represent an **alternative social model** based on loyalty rather than transaction. They provide the **resolution mechanism** that the play's accumulated betrayals demand — someone must answer for Athens's failures, and the Soldiers are that answer.

Perhaps most importantly, the Soldiers close the distance between the play's two plots. Throughout Acts 3 and 4, Timon's story in the wilderness and Alcibiades's story of grievance and banishment run in parallel without direct connection. The Soldier who brings Timon's epitaph to Alcibiades fuses these narrative strands, ensuring that Timon's voice is heard — however bleakly — in the moment of political judgment.
