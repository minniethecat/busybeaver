## Role

The Senators appear in Act 5 as a collective representation of Athenian political authority. They first approach [[timon|Timon]] in the woods, offering him restoration and command if he will lead the city against [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]; when [[timon|Timon]] refuses them with savage mockery, they return to [[athens|Athens]] and negotiate with [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] at the city walls. Their trajectory — from confident governing body to desperate supplicants — mirrors the play's larger pattern of reversal. As an anonymous collective, they embody institutional rather than personal power. (014-046–014-079, 017-004–017-016)

## Key Scenes

**The Embassy to Timon** (014-046–014-079): Two Senators, accompanied by [[flavius|Flavius]], approach [[timon|Timon]]'s cave to plead for his return. [[flavius|Flavius]] warns them it is hopeless — "It is in vain that you would speak with Timon; / For he is set so only to himself / That nothing but himself which looks like man / Is friendly with him" [014-047] — but the Senators persist. The [[first-senator|First Senator]] frames their mission in terms of public duty: "It is our part and promise to the Athenians / To speak with Timon" [014-048]. The [[second-senator|Second Senator]] expresses the [[hope]] that time may have changed [[timon|Timon]]: "time, with his fairer hand, / Offering the fortunes of his former days, / The former man may make him" [014-049].

When [[timon|Timon]] emerges, the Senators offer him "special dignities" [014-057] and "the captainship" [014-060] — absolute military power — if he will defend [[athens|Athens]] against [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]. Their offer is striking: the same Senate that oversaw [[timon|Timon]]'s fall now offers him supreme authority. The [[second-senator|Second Senator]] admits "forgetfulness too general, gross" [014-058] and promises "heaps and sums of love and wealth" [014-058] in recompense. [[timon|Timon]]'s response is the play's most savage piece of comic cruelty: he offers them his tree — "tell Athens, in the sequence of degree / From high to low throughout, that whoso please / To stop affliction, let him take his haste, / Come hither, ere my tree hath felt the axe, / And hang himself" [014-074]. The Senators depart defeated, the [[first-senator|First Senator]] recognising that "His discontents are unremoveably / Coupled to nature" [014-077] and the [[second-senator|Second Senator]] concluding "Our hope in him is dead" [014-078].

**The Surrender to Alcibiades** (017-004–017-016): The Senators appear on the walls of [[athens|Athens]] as [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] approaches with his army. They sue for peace, offering to yield the city and to submit those who wronged [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] to justice — "By decimation, and a tithed death" [017-009]. The [[first-senator|First Senator]] argues that collective guilt should not fall on all: "crimes, like lands, / Are not inherited" [017-010]. [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] accepts their terms, throwing down his glove as a token of honourable entry [017-014], and the Senators descend to open the gates. In this scene, the nameless Senators — "Both" speaking with one voice — represent the city's formal capitulation. Their pragmatism is transparent but effective: they save [[athens|Athens]] by sacrificing their "private enemies."

## Relationships

**[[timon|Timon]]**: The Senators' relationship with [[timon|Timon]] is defined by reversal. They come to him in desperation, offering the power they once failed to protect him from losing. [[timon|Timon]]'s refusal — and his offer of the hanging-tree — is his definitive rejection of the political world that abandoned him.

**[[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]**: The Senators banished [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] (through the actions of the [[first-senator|First Senator]] and [[second-senator|Second Senator]] in Act 3), then come crawling back when his army appears at the gates. Their willingness to reverse their own decree reveals the pure pragmatism of institutional power — principle bends to survival.

**[[first-senator|First Senator]] and [[second-senator|Second Senator]]**: The individualised Senators of Acts 3 and 4 recede into the anonymous collective "Senators" of Act 5. The named Senators' earlier actions (banishing [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]], then sending servants to dun [[timon|Timon]]) are absorbed into the corporate identity of the Senate. The individual disappears into the institution.

**[[flavius|Flavius]]**: [[flavius|Flavius]] guides the Senators to [[timon|Timon]]'s cave, warning them of futility but assisting them nonetheless. His role as mediator between the political world and [[timon|Timon]]'s wilderness is his final act of [[service|service]].

## Character Analysis

The Senators are less dramatic characters than structural elements — they represent the political order that the play systematically dismantles. Their appearance in Act 5 completes a pattern: the lords who exploited [[timon|Timon]]'s [[bounty|generosity]] are exposed as false [[friendship|friends]]; the Senate that failed to protect him is exposed as purely instrumental; and the very concept of institutional [[law|justice]] is shown to be a mask for power.

The Senators' willingness to reverse their banishment of [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] — and to offer [[timon|Timon]] supreme authority — reveals the flexibility of political morality. They have no principles beyond self-preservation. When [[timon|Timon]] was [[wealth|rich]], they benefited from his [[bounty|generosity]]; when [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] threatens destruction, they offer surrender and sacrifice. The fact that they are nameless — "Senators" without individual identity — is essential to their meaning: they are the system, not the people in it.

The [[second-senator|Second Senator]]'s admission of "forgetfulness too general, gross" contains a flicker of self-awareness, but it is immediately followed by a bribe — "heaps and sums of love and wealth." The Senators understand their failure in economic terms and offer economic recompense. [[timon|Timon]]'s refusal is the play's judgment on this logic: some debts cannot be repaid because the currency of [[trust]] has been destroyed. The hanging-tree is [[timon|Timon]]'s final answer to a world that values everything in [[gold]] — it is, in its terrible way, the only gift he has left worth giving.

## Notable Quotations

The Senators' offer to [[timon|Timon]] distils their combination of admission and bribery:

> They confess / Toward thee forgetfulness too general, gross: / Which now the public body, which doth seldom / Play the recanter, feeling in itself / A lack of Timon's aid, hath sense withal / Of its own fail, restraining aid to Timon
> — Second Senator, [014-058]

[[timon|Timon]]'s reply is one of the cruellest and funniest moments in Shakespeare:

> I have a tree, which grows here in my close, / That mine own use invites me to cut down, / And shortly must I fell it: tell my friends, / Tell Athens, in the sequence of degree / From high to low throughout, that whoso please / To stop affliction, let him take his haste, / Come hither, ere my tree hath felt the axe, / And hang himself.
> — Timon, [014-074]
