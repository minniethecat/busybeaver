## Role

Timandra is one of two courtesans (with [[phrynia|Phrynia]]) in [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]'s retinue. She appears in a single scene — the encounter with [[timons-exile|Timon in the woods]] — yet her exchange with the misanthrope is charged with dramatic significance. [[timon|Timon]] singles her out by name: "Art thou Timandra?" [013-701], a rare moment of personal address in his [[nature|wilderness]] tirades. Where [[phrynia|Phrynia]] curses [[timon|Timon]], Timandra engages him with incredulous questions, making her the slightly more reflective of the two women. Her presence, along with [[phrynia|Phrynia]]'s, introduces the exploitation of women's bodies into the play's broader critique of Athenian [[disease|corruption]].

## Key Scenes

- **Act 13 (the woods)** — [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]], [[phrynia|Phrynia]], and Timandra encounter [[timon|Timon]] outside his cave. [[timon|Timon]] unleashes a torrent of curses upon the women: "Consumptions sow / In hollow bones of man; strike their sharp shins, / And mar men's spurring" [013]. He exhorts them to "[[disease|plague]] all," to use their sexuality as a weapon against Athenian men. When [[timon|Timon]] asks "Art thou Timandra?" she answers simply "Yes" [013-702], and when he tells her to "be a whore still" and destroy those who use her, she retorts "Hang thee, monster!" [013-704]. The exchange is brief but fierce — a collision between [[timon|Timon]]'s abstract misanthropy and the lived reality of women whose bodies are already commodified.

## Relationships

- **[[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]**: As a camp follower, Timandra accompanies [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]'s army. Her relationship to him is that of a courtesan to a general — transactional, mobile, outside the bounds of Athenian domesticity.
- **[[phrynia|Phrynia]]**: Timandra's companion, with whom she shares both profession and scene. Their pairing recalls other doubles in the play, and their joint presence suggests that even the military alternative to [[athens|Athens]] ([[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]'s campaign) is implicated in the commodification of bodies.
- **[[timon|Timon]]**: [[timon|Timon]] addresses Timandra by name, distinguishing her from the faceless mass of humanity he otherwise curses. Her response — calling him "monster" — refuses the authority of his misanthropic vision. She is one of the few characters who talks back to [[timon|Timon]] in [[the-woods|the wilderness]] and walks away.

## Notable Quotes

> "Is this the Athenian minion, whom the world Voiced so regardfully?"
> — Timandra, asking incredulously about Timon, [013-700]

> "Hang thee, monster!"
> — Timandra, to Timon, [013-704]

## Character Analysis

Timandra's incredulity — "Is this the Athenian minion?" — registers the distance between [[timon|Timon]]'s former magnificence and his present degradation. Her question is the voice of the world outside the woods, the world that once admired [[timon|Timon]] and now cannot recognize him. Her curse — "Hang thee, monster!" — is the frankest judgment passed on [[timon|Timon]] by any character in the play. Unlike [[apemantus|Apemantus]], who shares [[timon|Timon]]'s [[misanthropy|cynicism]], or [[flavius|Flavius]], who pities him, Timandra simply names what he has become: a monster. Her clarity, unclouded by philosophy or [[service|loyalty]], may be the play's most honest assessment of what ingratitude and bitterness have made of its protagonist.
