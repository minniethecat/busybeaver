# Lucullus

## Role in the Play

Lucullus is one of the three named false [[friendship|friends]] of [[timon|Timon]] who receive his [[bounty|generosity]] and later refuse to reciprocate when he is in need. Unlike the anonymous lords ([[first-lord|First Lord]] and [[second-lord|Second Lord]]), Lucullus has a name and a dedicated scene — Act 5 (or Act 3 in some editions), Scene 1 — that dramatises his refusal in detail. His scene is a miniature of the play's larger structure: a petitioner arrives seeking help, and the man in power, having previously benefited from the relationship, refuses with a combination of self-justification and contempt. Lucullus is not a villain in any dramatic sense; he is a rational economic actor who has internalised the logic of a system in which [[friendship]] is indistinguishable from investment. (005-001)

## Key Scenes

**The Refusal Scene** (005-001): Lucullus's sole substantial appearance is the scene in which [[flaminius|Flaminius]], [[lucilius|Timon's servant]], comes to borrow [[gold|money]]. The scene opens in Lucullus's own house, and his first words — "One of [[timon|Lord Timon]]'s men? A gift, I warrant" — reveal his immediate assumption that any approach from [[timon|Timon]] must bring him benefit. When [[flaminius|Flaminius]] explains his errand, Lucullus's transformation from welcoming host to out[[anger|rage]]d [[debt|credit]]or is instantaneous: "La, la, la, la! 'nothing doubting,' says he? Alas, good lord!" (005-007)

**The Bribe Attempt** (005-014): Lucullus attempts to bribe [[flaminius|Flaminius]] to lie to [[timon|Timon]] — "I have noted thee always wise. Here's to thee" — offering him coins to report that Lucullus was not at home. This moment reveals Lucullus's understanding of all relationships as transactions: even his refusal must be managed through payment. [[flaminius|Flaminius]]'s rejection of the bribe — throwing the [[gold|money]] back — is one of the play's few moments of moral clarity. (005-016)

**The Servants' Report** (006-006): Lucullus's refusal is reported by the Strangers in Act 6, where it becomes part of the accumulating evidence of universal betrayal. The Stranger's casual mention — "one of his men was with the Lord Lucullus to borrow so many talents, nay, urged extremely for't, and showed what necessity belonged to't, and yet was denied" — confirms that Lucullus's behaviour is not exceptional but typical. (006-006)

## Relationships

**[[timon|Timon]]**: Lucullus has "hunted with his honour" — accepted [[timon|Timon]]'s [[feast|hospitality]] and gifts — yet refuses to lend him [[gold|money]] when asked. His relationship to [[timon|Timon]] is defined entirely by self-[[debt|interest]]: when [[timon|Timon]] is rich, Lucullus is a friend; when [[timon|Timon]] is poor, Lucullus is unavailable. (004-097)

**[[lucius|Lucius]]** and **[[sempronius|Sempronius]]**: Lucullus is part of the trio of named false [[friendship|friends]], each of whom provides a distinct variation on the theme of refusal. Together they demonstrate that the betrayal of [[timon|Timon]] is not an individual moral failing but a structural feature of Athenian elite society. (008-078)

**[[flaminius|Flaminius]]**: [[lucilius|Timon's servant]], sent to borrow [[gold|money]] from Lucullus. [[flaminius|Flaminius]]'s righteous anger at Lucullus's attempted bribe — "May these add to the number that may scald thee!" — provides the moral judgment that the play's structure renders implicit. (005-018)

## Character Analysis

Lucullus's refusal scene is a study in the psychology of the false friend. His self-justification — "this is no time to lend [[gold|money]], especially upon bare [[friendship|friends]]hip without security" — is perfectly rational: [[timon|Timon]] has no collateral, and [[friendship|friends]]hip, in Lucullus's world, is not security. The scene's power lies in its demonstration that Lucullus is not uniquely wicked; he is simply a man who has understood the rules of his society better than [[timon|Timon]] has. When he says "I have observed thee always wise," he is attempting to recruit [[flaminius|Flaminius]] into the same understanding — an understanding in which every relationship, including [[flaminius|Flaminius]]'s [[service|loyalty]] to [[timon|Timon]], has a price. [[flaminius|Flaminius]]'s refusal of the bribe is the scene's moral triumph and its structural t[[anger|rage]]dy: the one honest man can do nothing to change the system. (005-011)


## Notable Quotations

Lucullus, upon seeing [[flaminius|Flaminius]] at his door, immediately assumes [[timon|Timon]] has sent a gift — revealing the transactional frame through which he views all relationships:

> One of Lord Timon's men? a gift, I warrant. Why, this hits right; I dreamt of a silver basin and ewer to-night. Flaminius, honest Flaminius; you are very respectively welcome, sir.
>
> (005-007)

His refusal speech demonstrates the self-justifying logic of the false friend:

> La, la, la, la! "nothing doubting," says he? Alas, good lord! a noble gentleman 'tis, if he would not keep so good a house. Many a time and often I ha' dined with him, and told him on't, and come again to supper to him, of purpose to have him spend less, and yet he would embrace no counsel, take no warning by my coming. Every man has his fault, and honesty is his: I ha' told him on't, but I could ne'er get him from't.
>
> (005-011)

The bribery speech crystallises Lucullus's worldview — all relationships are transactions, and even a refusal must be managed through payment:

> Draw nearer, honest Flaminius. Thy lord's a bountiful gentleman: but thou art wise; and thou knowest well enough, although thou comest to me, that this is no time to lend money, especially upon bare friendship, without security.
>
> (005-016)
