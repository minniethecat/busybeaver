# Timon's Great Feast

## Context

[[timon|Timon]]'s Great Feast in Act 1, Scene 2 is the first full spectacle of the protagonist's boundless [[bounty|generosity]]. The preceding scene (Act 1, Scene 1) has established [[timon|Timon]]'s reputation through the [[poet|Poet]]'s and [[painter|Painter]]'s descriptions and through his initial acts of largesse — freeing [[ventidius|Ventidius]] from [[debt|debtor]]'s prison (001-045) and endowing [[lucilius|Lucilius]]'s marriage with a sum to outmatch the [[old-athenian|Old Athenian]]'s demands. The feast translates this personal [[bounty|generosity]] into social spectacle: music, food, performance, and gifts flow without limit, and the Athenian lords — the same lords who will later refuse him the smallest loan — gather to enjoy his [[feast|hospitality]].

The feast takes place in a banqueting-room in [[timon|Timon]]'s house, with "hautboys playing loud music" and "a great [[feast|banquet]] served in" (002-002). The stage direction alone establishes the scale: this is not a modest dinner but a ceremonial feast with professional musicians, multiple courses, and a large company of attendants. [[flavius|Flavius]], [[timon|Timon]]'s steward, oversees the service, and his private asides throughout the scene provide the counter-narrative of financial ruin that cuts against the visible abundance.

## Key Participants

- **[[timon|Timon]]**: The host, at the height of his prosperity and social influence. His speech on [[friendship]] articulates the philosophy of unconditional giving that will destroy him.
- **[[apemantus|Apemantus]]**: The cynic philosopher, present as structural counterpoint. He refuses the food, eats roots, delivers a bitter anti-grace, and diagnoses the [[flattery]] that [[timon|Timon]] mistakes for [[friendship]].
- **[[flavius|Flavius]]**: [[timon|Timon]]'s loyal steward, whose private asides (002-054, 002-075) reveal that the feast is being funded by an empty coffer. His silent desperation is the scene's dramatic irony made audible.
- **[[ventidius|Ventidius]]**: The debtor whom [[timon|Timon]] freed in Act 1, Scene 1, who now attempts to repay the debt and is refused — the exchange crystallizes [[timon|Timon]]'s gift economy: "there's none can truly say he gives, if he receives" (002-004).
- **[[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]**: The Athenian captain, whose martial identity provides a contrast to the feast's civilian excess. [[timon|Timon]] gives him gifts specifically because as a soldier he is "seldom rich" (002-083).
- **Cupid and the Ladies**: Performers in the masque — Cupid as presenter, Ladies costumed as Amazons with lutes. They provide the feast's theatrical centerpiece and represent the arts that [[timon|Timon]] patronizes.
- **The Lords** ([[first-lord]], [[second-lord]], [[third-lord]]): The collective recipients of [[timon|Timon]]'s gifts, whose effusive praise masks the self-interest that later scenes will expose.

## Narrative Summary

The scene opens with the feast already in progress. [[ventidius|Ventidius]] immediately attempts to return the money that freed him from prison, but [[timon|Timon]] refuses repayment absolutely: "I gave it freely ever; and there's none can truly say he gives, if he receives" (002-004). This refusal to accept reciprocity is not incidental to [[timon|Timon]]'s character — it is the essence of his philosophy. Gifts must be pure, and purity requires that they flow in one direction only.

[[timon|Timon]] then delivers his speech on ceremony and [[friendship]], the scene's philosophical keynote. "Ceremony was but devised at first," he declares, "to set a gloss on faint deeds, hollow welcomes, recanting [[kindness|goodness]], sorry ere 'tis shown; but where there is true friendship, there needs none" (002-006). This speech — delivered while the lords stand "ceremoniously looking on Timon" (002-006) — is a rich dramatic irony: [[timon|Timon]] disavows ceremony in a speech that is itself a ceremonial performance, addressed to men whose [[friendship]] he has mistaken for something real.

[[apemantus|Apemantus]] enters "discontentedly, like himself" (002-002) and immediately establishes his function as the feast's critical conscience. When [[timon|Timon]] welcomes him, he refuses the welcome: "I come to have thee thrust me out of doors" (002-010). Given his own table, he delivers his anti-grace — an inverted prayer in which he asks the [[gods]] to keep him from trusting oaths, bonds, weeping women, sleeping dogs, jailers, and "my [[friendship|friends]], if I should need 'em" (002-019). "Rich men sin, and I eat root," he concludes, his meager food a deliberate antithesis to the feast's excess.

The masque of Cupid interrupts the banquet with spectacle. Cupid enters with "a mask of Ladies as Amazons, with lutes in their hands, dancing and playing" (002-043). The masque allegorizes the feast's governing themes: bounty, beauty, and the arts that [[timon|Timon]]'s [[gold|wealth]] sustains. [[apemantus|Apemantus]] dissects it immediately as "a sweep of vanity" (002-044) and delivers a piercing cultural critique: "Like madness is the glory of this life as this pomp shows to a little oil and root. We make ourselves fools, to disport ourselves; and spend our flatteries, to drink those men upon whose age we void it up again, with poisonous spite and envy" (002-044). The image of regurgitation — consuming now to vomit later — is the play's earliest articulation of the reciprocity-in-reverse that will define [[timon|Timon]]'s own trajectory from feast-giver to curse-hurler.

After the masque, the gift-giving intensifies. [[timon|Timon]] calls for the "little casket" of jewels, and [[flavius|Flavius]], retrieving it, speaks the scene's most devastating private line: "More jewels yet! There is no crossing him in's humour; else I should tell him — well, i' [[trust|faith]] I should, when all's spent, he'd be crossed then, an he could. 'Tis [[mercy|pity]] [[bounty]] had not eyes behind, that man might ne'er be wretched for his mind" (002-054). [[timon|Timon]] distributes the jewels to the [[first-lord|First Lord]], then gives a bay courser to the [[second-lord|Second Lord]] simply because he once praised it (002-079). To [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] he gives charity in recognition of the soldier's poverty: "all thy living is 'mongst the dead, and all the lands thou hast lie in a pitched field" (002-083).

The feast closes as it opened, with gifts and protestations of [[friendship]]. The lords depart with effusive thanks, and [[apemantus|Apemantus]], remaining behind, delivers his final warning: "Thou givest so long, Timon, I fear me thou wilt give away thyself in paper shortly: what need these feasts, pomps and vain-glories?" (002-093). [[timon|Timon]] dismisses him — "an you begin to rail on society once, I am sworn not to give regard to you" (002-094) — and [[apemantus|Apemantus]] prophesies the consequence: "Thou wilt not hear me now; thou shalt not then: I'll lock thy [[gods|heaven]] from thee. O, that men's ears should be to counsel deaf, but not to [[flattery]]" (002-095).

## Significance

The Great Feast is the structural and thematic foundation upon which the play's entire second half rests. It establishes the height from which [[timon|Timon]] will fall, and every subsequent event — the [[debt|credit]]ors' demands, the friends' refusals, the [[water-banquet]], the exile, the discovery of gold — derives its force from the contrast with this initial abundance. The feast is the play's thesis statement: that unbounded [[bounty|generosity]] performed before an audience of self-interested [[flattery|flatterers]] is not [[kindness|virtue]] but vulnerability.

The feast serves three dramatic functions simultaneously. First, it characterizes [[timon|Timon]]'s philosophy of [[friendship]] through action rather than exposition — his refusal of [[ventidius|Ventidius]]'s repayment, his speech on ceremony, and his compulsive escalation of gift-giving all establish a worldview in which giving is not a transaction but an identity. Second, it introduces [[flavius|Flavius]]'s counter-narrative, planting the knowledge of financial ruin that makes every subsequent gift an act of dramatic irony. Third, it establishes [[apemantus|Apemantus]] as the truth-teller whom [[timon|Timon]] cannot hear — the cynic who is always right about the present but powerless to change the future.

Structurally, this feast is the antithesis of the [[water-banquet]] in Act 3. Where the first feast serves food and jewels, the second serves warm water and stones. Where the first celebrates [[friendship]], the second destroys it. Where the first ends with [[timon|Timon]] surrounded by admirers, the second ends with him alone, cursing all humanity. The two banquets, taken together, trace [[timon|Timon]]'s complete arc from philanthropist to [[misanthropy|misanthrope]], and the emotional force of the second feast depends entirely on the audience having experienced the first.

The feast also introduces the play's central concern with the economics of social performance. Every character at the table is performing: the lords perform gratitude for gifts they will not reciprocate; [[timon|Timon]] performs [[friendship]] for men he does not know; [[apemantus|Apemantus]] performs cynicism to an audience that refuses to learn. Only [[flavius|Flavius]] does not perform, and his private asides — the only non-performative speech in the scene — are the only speech that speaks the truth. The feast, for all its music and abundance, is a theatre of mutual deception, and the play's tragedy is that [[timon|Timon]] is the last character on stage to understand that he is acting in a play whose other performers have already read the final act.
