# Sempronius

## Role in the Play

Sempronius is the third of [[timon|Timon]]'s named false [[friendship|friends]], and his refusal scene — coming after those of [[lucullus|Lucullus]] and [[lucius|Lucius]] — provides the play's most psychologically complex variation on the theme of betrayal. Where [[lucullus|Lucullus]] is openly contemptuous and [[lucius|Lucius]] is hypocritically regretful, Sempronius is self-righteously offended: he refuses [[timon|Timon]] not because he cannot pay but because he was not asked first. His logic — that [[timon|Timon]] has insulted him by approaching others before him — transforms the refusal into a matter of wounded [[honour|honour]], allowing Sempronius to feel virtuous while behaving selfishly. His scene completes the anatomy of false [[friendship]] that runs through the play's middle acts. (007-001)

## Key Scenes

**The Refusal Scene** (007-001): Sempronius's scene opens in his house, where he receives [[lucilius|Timon's servant]] with the question: "Must he needs trouble me in't — hum! — 'bove all others?" His first concern is not [[timon|Timon]]'s need but his own precedence — he is offended to be asked last. When informed that [[lucullus|Lucullus]] and [[lucius|Lucius]] have already refused, Sempronius seizes on this as justification: "How! have they denied him? Has [[ventidius|Ventidius]] and [[lucullus|Lucullus]] denied him? And does he send to me? Three? Hum!" (007-003)

**The Self-Righteous Refusal** (007-005): Sempronius's refusal is unique among the false [[friendship|friends]] in that it transforms selfishness into a principle. He argues that [[timon|Timon]] has shown him "[[shame|disgrace]]" by asking others first, and therefore "his [[shame|disgrace]]s are to be equally presented." This logic — that [[timon|Timon]]'s failure to honour Sempronius above all others justifies Sempronius's refusal to help — is a masterpiece of self-deception. It allows Sempronius to keep his [[gold]] while maintaining his [[honour|self-regard]]. (007-005)

**[[timon|Timon]]'s Final Invitation** (008-078): In Act 8 (or Act 3, Scene 6), [[timon|Timon]] instructs his servant to "bid all my [[friendship|friends]] again, [[lucius|Lucius]], [[lucullus|Lucullus]], and Sempronius: All, sirrah, all." This is the invitation to the [[water-banquet|water banquet]], [[timon|Timon]]'s performative revenge. Sempronius's inclusion in this list confirms his status as one of the archetypal false [[friendship|friends]]. (008-078)

## Relationships

**[[timon|Timon]]**: Sempronius has received [[timon|Timon]]'s [[feast|hospitality]] — "commend me to their loves" — yet his refusal is the most elaborately justified of the three. His wounded pride at not being asked first reveals the narcissism at the heart of the patronage system: even in refusal, the false friend makes himself the injured party. (004-097)

**[[lucullus|Lucullus]]** and **[[lucius|Lucius]]**: Sempronius's refusal is structurally dependent on those of [[lucullus|Lucullus]] and [[lucius|Lucius]] — he uses their prior refusals as his justification. This interdependence reveals that the false [[friendship|friends]] function as a system, not as isolated moral failures. Each man's refusal reinforces the others', and together they create a world in which genuine [[friendship]] is structurally impossible. (007-005)

**[[ventidius|Ventidius]]**: Sempronius mentions [[ventidius|Ventidius]] in his refusal speech — "Has [[ventidius|Ventidius]] and [[lucullus|Lucullus]] denied him?" — grouping him with the false [[friendship|friends]]. [[ventidius|Ventidius]]'s refusal to help the man who paid his debts makes him, in Sempronius's telling, the most culpable of all. (007-005)

## Character Analysis

Sempronius is the most psychologically sophisticated of the false [[friendship|friends]] because his vice is dressed as virtue. His refusal is not based on greed (like [[lucullus|Lucullus]]) or convenience (like [[lucius|Lucius]]) but on a perverted sense of [[honour|honour]]. He is offended — genuinely offended, in his own mind — that [[timon|Timon]] would ask others before him. This wounded pride allows him to refuse while feeling righteous, transforming his selfishness into a matter of principle. Shakespeare's insight here is devastating: the worst betrayals are not those motivated by greed or cowardice but those motivated by self-righteousness, because self-righteousness is the one motivation that cannot be corrected by evidence. Sempronius will always find a way to be the injured party, and his [[friendship]], like that of all the false [[friendship|friends]], was never anything more than a performance waiting for an excuse to end. (007-003)


## Notable Quotations

Sempronius's refusal is distinguished by wounded vanity — he is less offended by the request for [[gold|money]] than by the implication that he was not [[timon|Timon]]'s first choice:

> Must he needs trouble me in't—hum!—'bove all others? He might have tried Lord Lucius or Lucullus; And now Ventidius is wealthy too, Whom he redeem'd from prison: all these Owe their estates unto him.
>
> (007-003)

His self-righteous anger transforms refusal into a principled stand:

> How! have they denied him? Has Ventidius and Lucullus denied him? And does he send to me? Three? hum! It shows but little love or judgment in him: Must I be his last refuge? His friends, like physicians, Thrive, give him over: must I take the cure upon me? Has much disgraced me in't; I'm angry at him, That might have known my place.
>
> (007-005)
