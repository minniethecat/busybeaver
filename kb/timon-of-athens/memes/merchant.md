# Merchant

## Role in the Play

The Merchant appears in Scene I as part of the gathering of suitors around [[timon|Timon]]. He speaks the play's first words of praise for [[timon|Timon]] — "O, 'tis a [[honour|worth]]y lord" (001-009) — and his escalating encomium establishes the hyperbolic register of Athenian flattery. Later in the scene, he engages with [[apemantus|Apemantus]] in a cynical exchange about commerce and divinity. (001-011)

## Key Scenes

**Praising [[timon|Timon]]** (001-009–001-017): The Merchant's opening tribute to [[timon|Timon]] is characteristically expansive: "A most incomparable man, breathed, as it were, To an untirable and continuate [[kindness|goodness]]: He passes" (001-011). He then turns to practical matters, urging the [[jeweller|Jeweller]] to present his wares: "O, pray, let's see't: for the [[timon|Lord Timon]], sir?" (001-013). The seamless movement from hyperbolic praise to commercial transaction captures the symbiosis of flattery and self-[[debt|interest]] in [[timon|Timon]]'s court.

**The Philosophy of Commerce** (001-083–001-136): When [[apemantus|Apemantus]] mocks [[timon|Timon]]'s [[bounty|generosity]], the Merchant defends the patronage system with a commercial analogy: "He speaks the common tongue, Which all men speak with him" (001-083). [[apemantus|Apemantus]] retorts that "traffic" (commerce) is the god of such men. The Merchant's reluctant admission — "If traffic do it, the gods do it not" (001-136) — concedes [[apemantus|Apemantus]]'s point: the gift economy is commerce disguised as virtue.

## Relationships

**[[timon|Timon]]**: The patron whose "incomparable" [[kindness|goodness]] the Merchant praises. The Merchant's relationship to [[timon|Timon]] is commercial rather than personal — he values [[timon|Timon]] as a market for the [[jeweller|Jeweller]]'s wares.

**[[jeweller|Jeweller]]**: The Merchant vouches for the [[jeweller|Jeweller]]'s work and facilitates the commercial transaction. Their partnership represents the honest commerce that functions within [[timon|Timon]]'s patronage system.

**[[poet|Poet]]** and **[[painter|Painter]]**: Fellow suitors in Scene I. The Merchant's pragmatic commercialism contrasts with [[poet|the Poet]]'s artistic self-presentation and [[painter|the Painter]]'s craft pride.

**[[apemantus|Apemantus]]**: The cynic whose critique of "traffic" as false religion the Merchant half-concedes.


## Notable Quotations

The Merchant's opening praise of [[timon|Timon]] escalates from simple approval to hyperbolic encomium:

> O, 'tis a worthy lord.
>
> (001-009)

> A most incomparable man, breathed, as it were, To an untirable and continuate goodness: He passes.
>
> (001-011)

When [[apemantus|Apemantus]] challenges the commercial basis of the patronage system, the Merchant half-concedes the cynic's point — commerce and religion are not easily distinguished in [[timon|Timon]]'s [[athens|Athens]]:

> If traffic do it, the gods do it not.
>
> (001-136)
