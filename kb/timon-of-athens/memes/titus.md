## Role in the Play

Titus is a servant in the household of one of [[timon|Timon]]'s [[debt|credit]]ors, dispatched to collect on his master's outstanding debt of fifty talents. He appears in the scene outside [[timon|Timon]]'s house where multiple [[debt|credit]]ors' servants gather, each bearing a bill and waiting for their master's money. Unlike [[hortensius|Hortensius]], who expresses moral discomfort at the errand, Titus is pragmatic and direct: he observes the absurdity of the situation with detached irony but pursues the debt without hesitation. He serves as an everyman figure in the play's anatomy of ingratitude — an ordinary man who carries out his master's will without questioning the moral dimensions of the act, embodying the machinery of debt that grinds [[timon|Timon]] to ruin.

## Key Scenes

- **Act 4, Scene 4** — Titus enters with [[hortensius|Hortensius]] and other servants of [[timon|Timon]]'s [[debt|credit]]ors, waiting for [[timon|Timon]] to come forth [004-472]. Greeted by Varro's First Servant with "Well met; good morrow, Titus and Hortensius," he returns the greeting courteously: "The like to you, kind Varro" [004-474]. When the servants discuss their shared business, Titus acknowledges their common purpose with "So is theirs and ours" [004-477].

- **Act 4, Scene 4 (continued)** — Titus offers a wry observation on the paradoxical nature of their situation: "I'll show you how to observe a strange event. Your lord sends now for money," noting that the lord in question "wears jewels now of Timon's gift, / For which I wait for money" [004-489]. This remark reveals his sharp eye for irony — the [[debt|credit]]ors demand repayment while adorned with [[timon|Timon]]'s [[bounty|gifts]].

- **Act 4, Scene 4 (climax)** — When [[timon|Timon]] appears in a rage, Lucilius' Servant urges Titus forward with "Put in now, Titus" [004-524], and Titus presents his bill directly: "My lord, here is my bill" [004-525]. As the [[debt|credit]]ors swarm [[timon|Timon]], Titus specifies the amount he is owed: "Mine, fifty talents" [004-533]. His directness contrasts with [[timon|Timon]]'s increasingly desperate responses, underscoring the impersonal force of debt that overwhelms all sentiment.

## Relationships

- **[[hortensius|Hortensius]]**: Titus and [[hortensius|Hortensius]] are fellow servants of [[debt|credit]]ors who appear together throughout the scene. Where [[hortensius|Hortensius]] confesses the errand is "against my heart," Titus offers no such moral commentary: he is the practical counterpart to [[hortensius|Hortensius]]'s reluctant conscience, and together they represent the two faces of ordinary complicity — the one who feels wrong but obeys, and the one who simply obeys.

- **[[timon|Timon]]**: Titus's relationship to [[timon|Timon]] is purely transactional. He holds a bill for fifty talents and demands payment without sentiment. His ironic observation that the [[debt|credit]]ors wear [[timon|Timon]]'s own [[bounty|jewels]] while demanding money recognizes the injustice of the situation, yet he makes no move to mitigate it. He is, in effect, the human face of the impersonal debt obligation that destroys [[timon|Timon]].

- **[[caphis|Caphis]] and [[philotus|Philotus]]**: Titus shares the stage with these fellow [[debt|credit]]or-servants, forming with them a small chorus that dramatizes the collective nature of [[timon|Timon]]'s abandonment. While [[caphis|Caphis]] is summoned explicitly by name by his master and [[philotus|Philotus]] arrives late to the gathering, Titus is consistently present as an active participant in the dunning, stepping forward first when [[timon|Timon]] appears.

## Character Analysis

Titus occupies a distinctive position among the [[debt|credit]]ors' servants who descend upon [[timon|Timon]]'s house. He is neither reluctant like [[hortensius|Hortensius]] nor eager like Varro's servants; he is simply professional. His ironic observation about the [[debt|credit]]ors wearing [[timon|Timon]]'s [[bounty|jewels]] while demanding money demonstrates that he sees the absurdity and injustice of the situation clearly [004-489]. Yet this recognition does not translate into action: he presents his bill and demands his fifty talents with the same directness as any of his fellows. This gap between perception and behavior is characteristic of the play's pessimistic vision — in a world governed by debt and obligation, moral awareness offers no protection against complicity.

Titus's role is small but structurally significant. He is the first of the servants named in the stage direction at the scene's opening [004-472], and when [[timon|Timon]] finally appears, it is Titus who is urged forward — "Put in now, Titus" [004-524] — and Titus who presents the first bill. He thus serves as the spearhead of the [[debt|credit]]ors' assault, the point at which the abstract force of debt becomes a concrete demand. His specification of "Mine, fifty talents" [004-533] reduces the entire drama of [[timon|Timon]]'s [[generosity|generosity]], betrayal, and ruin to a single figure on a ledger. In this reduction lies the play's bleakest insight: that all the bonds of [[friendship]] and gratitude can be dissolved into a column of numbers.
