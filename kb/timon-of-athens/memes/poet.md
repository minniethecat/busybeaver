# Poet

## Role in the Play

The Poet appears primarily in Act 1, Scene 1, as part of a group of artists and merchants who gather at [[timon|Timon]]'s house seeking his patronage. Along with the [[painter|Painter]], [[jeweller|Jeweller]], and [[merchant|Merchant]], the Poet represents the class of Athenians who depend on [[timon|Timon]]'s [[bounty|generosity]] for their livelihood. His role is dramatically significant because he delivers the play's first extended philosophical statement — an allegorical poem about [[fortune|Fortune]] — that establishes the central theme of [[gold|wealth]]'s instability before [[timon|Timon]] even appears on stage. (001-002)

## Key Scenes

**Act 1, Scene 1 — The Gathering at [[timons-house|Timon's House]]** (001-002): The Poet enters with [[painter|the Painter]], [[jeweller|Jeweller]], and [[merchant|Merchant]] at several doors, all seeking audience with [[timon|Timon]]. His opening exchange with [[painter|the Painter]] establishes the competitive yet collegial relationship between artists dependent on the same patron. (001-003)

**The Allegory of [[fortune|Fortune]]** (001-036): The Poet recites his work-in-progress to [[painter|the Painter]] — an allegorical poem depicting [[fortune|Fortune]] enthroned on a hill, with all of humanity striving toward her. In his verse, one figure "of [[timon|Lord Timon]]'s frame" is raised by [[fortune|Fortune]]'s "ivory hand," only to be later cast down. The poem functions as dramatic irony: it accurately predicts [[timon|Timon]]'s trajectory while the Poet himself fails to grasp its implications for his own prospects. (001-038)

**The Encounter with [[apemantus|Apemantus]]** (001-117): The Poet exchanges barbs with [[apemantus|Apemantus]], who dismisses him as a flatterer. When [[apemantus|Apemantus]] asks "Art not one?" the Poet's defensive response reveals his self-awareness about the performative nature of patronage poetry. (001-118)

## Relationships

**[[timon|Timon]]**: The Poet's patron — or would-be patron. He has written a dedication to [[timon|Timon]] but has not yet presented it when the play opens. His relationship to [[timon|Timon]] is purely transactional: he trades praise for [[gold]], making him complicit in the [[flattery]] system that ultimately destroys [[timon|Timon]]. (001-015)

**[[painter|Painter]]**: The Poet's fellow artist and conversational partner. Their dialogue in Act 1, Scene 1 reveals the competitive dynamics of artistic patronage, as each vies to demonstrate the superior merit of his work. (001-003)

**[[apemantus|Apemantus]]**: The cynic philosopher immediately identifies the Poet as a flatterer, calling him a "poet" with contempt. Their exchange highlights the gap between the Poet's self-image as an artist and his function as a supplier of flattery. (001-117)

## Character Analysis

The Poet is a paradoxical figure: he speaks the play's deepest truths about [[fortune|Fortune]] and instability yet remains blind to his own entrapment within the patronage system. His allegory of [[fortune|Fortune]] is a work of genuine insight, but he treats it as a commodity — something to be "slipp'd idly" from him in hopes of [[gold|payment]]. The play suggests that art produced under patronage cannot function as genuine criticism, because the artist's material dependence on the patron precludes honest observation. The Poet's return in Act 5, where he and [[painter|the Painter]] seek out [[timon|Timon]] after hearing of his [[discovery-of-gold|gold]], confirms this reading: their art, like everyone else's [[service|loyalty]], follows the [[gold|money]]. (001-019)


## Notable Quotations

The Poet describes his own creative process through an extended metaphor of flint and fire — a passage that is both art theory and self-promotion:

> Our poesy is as a gum, which oozes From whence 'tis nourish'd: the fire i' the flint Shows not till it be struck; our gentle flame Provokes itself and like the current flies Each bound it chafes.
>
> (001-019)

The Poet's allegory of [[fortune|Fortune]], composed for [[timon|Timon]], prefigures the play's entire trajectory:

> I have upon a high and pleasant hill Feign'd Fortune to be throned: the base o' the mount Is rank'd with all deserts, all kind of natures, That labour on the bosom of this sphere To propagate their states: amongst them all, Whose eyes are on this sovereign lady fix'd, One do I personate of Lord Timon's frame, Whom Fortune with her ivory hand wafts to her; Whose present grace to present slaves and servants Translates his rivals.
>
> (001-036)

The moral of the allegory, revealing the Poet's [[misanthropy|cynicism]] beneath his flattery:

> When Fortune in her shift and change of mood Spurns down her late beloved, all his dependants Which labour'd after him to the mountain's top Even on their knees and hands, let him slip down, Not one accompanying his declining foot.
>
> (001-040)
