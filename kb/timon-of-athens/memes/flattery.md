# Flattery in Timon of Athens

## Overview

Flattery is the dominant speech act of *[[timon-of-athens|Timon of Athens]]*, the verbal mechanism through which the play's social economy operates. From the opening scene, where [[poet|the Poet]] and [[painter|Painter]] compete to praise [[timon|Timon]] in hopes of patronage, to the final act, where former flatterers seek [[timon|Timon]]'s newly rediscovered gold, flattery is the constant medium of Athenian social life. Shakespeare presents flattery not as an individual moral failing but as a systemic condition — a language that everyone speaks because everyone benefits from it, and whose grammar is shaped entirely by the distribution of [[gold|wealth]]. The play's critique is structural rather than psychological: flatterers are not uniquely wicked but are rational actors responding to incentives, and the t[[anger|rage]]dy is that a society built on flattery inevitably destroys the very virtues it pretends to celebrate.

## Key Passages

> yea, from the glass-faced flatterer To Apemantus, that few things loves better Than to abhor himself: even he drops down The knee before him (001-034)

The [[poet|Poet]]'s catalogue of [[timon|Timon]]'s admirers establishes flattery as a universal solvent that dissolves even the most resistant character. The epithet "glass-faced" is precise — a glass face is both reflective (the flatterer mirrors the patron's desires) and transparent (the flattery is visible to all who care to look). That even [[apemantus|Apemantus]], the professional misanthrope, "drops down the knee" before [[timon|Timon]]'s [[gold|wealth]] suggests that flattery is not a choice but a gravitational force, irresistible to all within its field.

> he that loves to be flattered is worthy o' the flatterer. (001-127)

[[apemantus|Apemantus]]' aphorism contains the play's moral mathematics of flattery in a single line. The flatterer and the flattered are not predator and victim but partners in a consensual exchange — the patron purchases praise with gifts, the flatterer purchases gifts with praise. Neither party is innocent, and neither can claim to have been deceived. [[timon|Timon]]'s t[[anger|rage]]dy, by this logic, is not that he was flattered but that he failed to understand the terms of the transaction.

> We make ourselves fools, to disport ourselves; And spend our flatteries, to drink those men Upon whose age we void it up again, With poisonous spite and envy. (002-044)

[[apemantus|Apemantus]]' [[feast|banquet]] commentary anatomizes the economics of flattery with brutal precision. Flattery is "spent" like [[gold|money]] — invested now for future returns — but the investment cycle is fundamentally digestive rather than productive. The flatterer consumes the patron's favour and voids it as poison. The bodily imagery (drink, void, spite) reduces the elegant rituals of [[timon|Timon]]'s [[feast|banquet]] to their physiological substrate: flattery is ingestion followed by excretion, and the excreted product is toxic.

> O, that men's ears should be To counsel deaf, but not to flattery. (002-095)

[[apemantus|Apemantus]]' exit line is the play's [[death|epitaph]] for [[timon|Timon]]'s [[athens|Athens]]. The antithesis between "counsel" (truth that wounds) and "flattery" (lies that please) defines the moral geography of the play. [[timon|Timon]]'s ears, like the ears of all powerful men in the play, are structurally open to flattery and structurally closed to truth. This is not a contingent failing but a feature of power itself: the powerful can always find someone to tell them what they want to hear, and the incentives to tell them what they need to hear are vanishingly small.

> Who, stuck and spangled with your flatteries, Washes it off, and sprinkles in your faces Your reeking villany. (010-047)

[[timon|Timon]]'s curse upon his false [[friendship|friends]] at the second [[feast|banquet]] — [[timons-feast|the banquet]] of lukewarm water — is the play's most theatrical unmasking of flattery. The image of flattery as a cosmetic appliqué ("stuck and spangled") that can be "washed off" reveals the underlying violence it conceals. By throwing water rather than dispensing gifts, [[timon|Timon]] performs a literal de-flattering, stripping his guests of their verbal makeup and exposing the "reeking villany" beneath.

## Character Perspectives

**[[timon|Timon]]** is flattery's perfect target: [[gold|wealth]]y, generous, and emotionally invested in the belief that the praise he receives is genuine. His catastrophic discovery that his [[friendship|friends]]' affection was flattery triggers a total psychological reversal — from believing all praise to believing none, from universal trust to universal suspicion. [[timon|Timon]]'s misanthropy is flattery's dialectical product, the mirror image of his former credulity.

**[[apemantus|Apemantus]]** is the play's anti-flattery conscience. He alone refuses to participate in the economy of praise, preferring abuse to insincerity. Yet his position is self-defeating: by refusing to flatter, he forfeits all influence over those who might benefit from his honesty. [[apemantus|Apemantus]] proves that truth without flattery is socially impotent — a diagnosis the play offers without pretending to solve.

**The [[poet|Poet]] and [[painter|Painter]]** represent flattery as a professional class. They are not hypocrites in any simple sense — they openly acknowledge that their art is produced for patronage and that patronage requires praise. Their return in Act V, seeking [[timon|Timon]]'s gold under the guise of [[friendship|friends]]hip, demonstrates that flattery is a habit that survives its own exposure. Even knowing that [[timon|Timon]] sees through them, they cannot stop performing the role their livelihood depends upon.

**The Senatorial Lords** illustrate flattery as a collective practice. Each lord who flatters [[timon|Timon]] at his [[feast|banquet]]s and refuses him in his need is playing a structurally identical role, differentiated only by the ingenuity of their excuses. Shakespeare's decision to give these lords nearly identical scenes of refusal emphasizes the systemic rather than individual nature of their behaviour — they are not uniquely wicked men but typical products of a flattery-based social order.

## Critical Interpretations

The Renaissance was deeply engaged with the problem of flattery, inheriting from classical sources a rich tradition of anti-flattery discourse. Plutarch's essay "How to Tell a Flatterer from a Friend" was widely read and directly informs the play's central diagnostic problem. Shakespeare's innovation is to shift the question from "how can one identify flatterers?" (Plutarch's concern) to "what kind of society makes flattery the rational choice for everyone?" This structural turn anticipates Enlightenment critiques of court culture — particularly Rousseau's and Montesquieu's analyses of how monarchy breeds flattery as a survival strategy.

The play's treatment of flattery also engages with early modern anxieties about the marketplace of print. The [[poet|Poet]] and [[painter|Painter]], as professional flatterers seeking patronage, mirror the position of early modern writers who depended on aristocratic or royal favour. Shakespeare, as a shareholder in the King's Men and a successful commercial playwright, occupied an unusually independent position in this economy, and the play's critique of patronage-based flattery may reflect his professional perspective as an artist who did not need to flatter to survive.

Contemporary social psychology has developed extensive empirical literature on the effects of flattery, confirming what Shakespeare dramatizes: flattery is highly effective even when the target knows it is insincere, power increases susceptibility to flattery, and exposure to flattery degrades decision-making quality. [[timon|Timon]]'s trajectory from beloved patron to ruined misanthrope is, in this light, not a moral fable but a behavioural prediction — a demonstration of what happens when unlimited [[gold|wealth]] meets unlimited praise in the absence of institutional constraints on either.
