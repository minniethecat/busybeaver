[014-001] The woods. Before **[[timon|Timon]]’s** cave.

::: table pn=014-002

<!-- pn=014-002 -->|  | *Enter **[[poet|Poet]]** and **[[painter|Painter]]**; **Timon** watching them from his cave.* |

<!-- pn=014-003 -->| Painter | As I took note of the place, it cannot be far where he abides. |

<!-- pn=014-004 -->| Poet | What’s to be thought of him? does the rumour hold for true, that he’s so full of gold? |

<!-- pn=014-005 -->| Painter | Certain: [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] reports it; [[phrynia|Phrynia]] and [[timandra|Timandra]] had gold of him: he likewise enriched poor straggling soldiers with great quantity: ’tis said he gave unto his steward a mighty sum. |

<!-- pn=014-006 -->| Poet | Then this breaking of his has been but a try for his [[friendship|friends]]. |

<!-- pn=014-007 -->| Painter | Nothing else: you shall see him a palm in [[athens|Athens]] again, and flourish with the highest. Therefore ’tis not amiss we tender our loves to him, in this supposed distress of his: it will show honestly in us; and is very likely to load our purposes with what they travail for, if it be a just true report that goes of his having. |

<!-- pn=014-008 -->| Poet | What have you now to present unto him? |

<!-- pn=014-009 -->| Painter | Nothing at this time but my visitation: only I will promise him an excellent piece. |

<!-- pn=014-010 -->| Poet | I must serve him so too, tell him of an intent that’s coming toward him. |

<!-- pn=014-011 -->| Painter | Good as the best. Promising is the very air o’ the time: it opens the eyes of [[hope|expectation]]: performance is ever the duller for his act; and, but in the plainer and simpler kind of people, the deed of saying is quite out of use. To promise is most courtly and fashionable: performance is a kind of will or testament which argues a great sickness in his judgment that makes it. ***Timon** comes from his cave, behind.* |

<!-- pn=014-012 -->| Timon | *Aside.* Excellent workman! thou canst not paint a man so bad as is thyself. |

<!-- pn=014-013 -->| Poet | I am thinking what I shall say I have provided for him: it must be a personating of himself; a satire against the softness of [[wealth|prosperity]], with a discovery of the infinite flatteries that follow youth and opulency. |

<!-- pn=014-014 -->| Timon | *Aside.* Must thou needs stand for a villain in thine own work? wilt thou whip thine own faults in other men? Do so, I have gold for thee. |

<!-- pn=014-015 -->| Poet | Nay, let’s seek him: Then do we sin against our own estate, When we may profit meet, and come too late. |

<!-- pn=014-016 -->| Painter | True; When the day serves, before black-corner’d night, Find what thou want’st by free and offer’d light. Come. |

<!-- pn=014-017 -->| Timon | *Aside.* I’ll meet you at the turn. What a god’s gold, That he is worshipp’d in a baser temple Than where swine feed! ’Tis thou that rigg’st the bark and plough’st the foam, Settlest admired reverence in a slave: To thee be worship! and thy saints for aye Be crown’d with plagues that thee alone obey! Fit I meet them. *Coming forward.* |

<!-- pn=014-018 -->| Poet | Hail, worthy Timon! |

<!-- pn=014-019 -->| Painter | Our late noble master! |

<!-- pn=014-020 -->| Timon | Have I once lived to see two honest men? |

<!-- pn=014-021 -->| Poet | Sir, Having often of your open bounty tasted, Hearing you were retired, your friends fall’n off, Whose thankless natures﻿—O abhorred spirits!﻿— Not all the whips of [[gods|heaven]] are large enough: What! to you, Whose star-like nobleness gave life and influence To their whole being! I am rapt and cannot cover The monstrous bulk of this ingratitude With any size of words. |

<!-- pn=014-022 -->| Timon | Let it go naked, men may see’t the better: You that are honest, by being what you are, Make them best seen and known. |

<!-- pn=014-023 -->| Painter | He and myself Have travail’d in the great shower of your gifts, And sweetly felt it. |

<!-- pn=014-024 -->| Timon | Ay, you are honest men. |

<!-- pn=014-025 -->| Painter | We are hither come to offer you our service. |

<!-- pn=014-026 -->| Timon | Most honest men! Why, how shall I requite you? Can you eat roots, and drink cold water? no. |

<!-- pn=014-027 -->| Both | What we can do, we’ll do, to do you service. |

<!-- pn=014-028 -->| Timon | Ye’re honest men: ye’ve heard that I have gold; I am sure you have: speak truth; ye’re honest men. |

<!-- pn=014-029 -->| Painter | So it is said, my noble lord; but therefore Came not my friend nor I. |

<!-- pn=014-030 -->| Timon | Good honest men! Thou draw’st a counterfeit Best in all Athens: thou’rt, indeed, the best; Thou counterfeit’st most lively. |

<!-- pn=014-031 -->| Painter | So, so, my lord. |

<!-- pn=014-032 -->| Timon | E’en so, sir, as I say. And, for thy fiction, Why, thy verse swells with stuff so fine and smooth That thou art even natural in thine art. But, for all this, my honest-natured friends, I must needs say you have a little fault: Marry, ’tis not monstrous in you, neither wish I You take much pains to mend. |

<!-- pn=014-033 -->| Both | Beseech your honour To make it known to us. |

<!-- pn=014-034 -->| Timon | You’ll take it ill. |

<!-- pn=014-035 -->| Both | Most thankfully, my lord. |

<!-- pn=014-036 -->| Timon | Will you, indeed? |

<!-- pn=014-037 -->| Both | Doubt it not, worthy lord. |

<!-- pn=014-038 -->| Timon | There’s never a one of you but trusts a knave, That mightily deceives you. |

<!-- pn=014-039 -->| Both | Do we, my lord? |

<!-- pn=014-040 -->| Timon | Ay, and you hear him cog, see him dissemble, Know his gross patchery, love him, feed him, Keep in your bosom: yet remain assured That he’s a made-up villain. |

<!-- pn=014-041 -->| Painter | I know none such, my lord. |

<!-- pn=014-042 -->| Poet | Nor I. |

<!-- pn=014-043 -->| Timon | Look you, I love you well; I’ll give you gold, Rid me these villains from your companies: Hang them or stab them, drown them in a draught, Confound them by some course, and come to me, I’ll give you gold enough. |

<!-- pn=014-044 -->| Both | Name them, my lord, let’s know them. |

<!-- pn=014-045 -->| Timon | You that way and you this, but two in company; Each man apart, all single and alone, Yet an arch-villain keeps him company. If where thou art two villains shall not be, Come not near him. If thou wouldst not reside But where one villain is, then him abandon. Hence, pack! there’s gold; you came for gold, ye slaves: *To **Painter**.* You have work’d for me; there’s payment for you: hence! *To **Poet**.* You are an alchemist; make gold of that. Out, rascal dogs! *Beats them out, and then retires to his cave.* |

<!-- pn=014-046 -->|  | *Enter **[[flavius|Flavius]]** and two **Senators**.* |

<!-- pn=014-047 -->| Flavius | It is in vain that you would speak with Timon; For he is set so only to himself That nothing but himself which looks like man Is friendly with him. |

<!-- pn=014-048 -->| [[first-senator|First Senator]] | Bring us to his cave: It is our part and promise to the Athenians To speak with Timon. |

<!-- pn=014-049 -->| [[second-senator|Second Senator]] | At all times alike Men are not still the same: ’twas time and griefs That framed him thus: time, with his fairer hand, Offering the fortunes of his former days, The former man may make him. Bring us to him, And chance it as it may. |

<!-- pn=014-050 -->| Flavius | Here is his cave. Peace and content be here! Lord Timon! Timon! Look out, and speak to friends: the Athenians, By two of their most reverend [[law|senate]], greet thee: Speak to them, noble Timon. |

<!-- pn=014-051 -->|  | ***Timon** comes from his cave.* |

<!-- pn=014-052 -->| Timon | Thou sun, that comfort’st, burn! Speak, and be hang’d: For each true word, a blister! and each false Be as cauterizing to the root o’ the tongue, Consuming it with speaking! |

<!-- pn=014-053 -->| First Senator | Worthy Timon﻿— |

<!-- pn=014-054 -->| Timon | Of none but such as you, and you of Timon. |

<!-- pn=014-055 -->| First Senator | The senators of Athens greet thee, Timon. |

<!-- pn=014-056 -->| Timon | I thank them; and would send them back the [[disease|plague]], Could I but catch it for them. |

<!-- pn=014-057 -->| First Senator | O, forget What we are sorry for ourselves in thee. The senators with one consent of love Entreat thee back to Athens; who have thought On special dignities, which vacant lie For thy best use and wearing. |

<!-- pn=014-058 -->| Second Senator | They confess Toward thee forgetfulness too general, gross: Which now the public body, which doth seldom Play the recanter, feeling in itself A lack of Timon’s aid, hath sense withal Of its own fail, restraining aid to Timon; And send forth us, to make their sorrow’d render, Together with a recompense more fruitful Than their offence can weigh down by the dram; Ay, even such heaps and sums of love and [[gold|wealth]] As shall to thee blot out what wrongs were theirs And write in thee the figures of their love, Ever to read them thine. |

<!-- pn=014-059 -->| Timon | You witch me in it; Surprise me to the very brink of tears: Lend me a fool’s heart and a woman’s eyes, And I’ll beweep these comforts, worthy senators. |

<!-- pn=014-060 -->| First Senator | Therefore, so please thee to return with us And of our Athens, thine and ours, to take The captainship, thou shalt be met with thanks, Allow’d with absolute power and thy good name Live with authority: so soon we shall drive back Of Alcibiades the approaches wild, Who, like a boar too savage, doth root up His country’s peace. |

<!-- pn=014-061 -->| Second Senator | And shakes his threatening sword Against the walls of Athens. |

<!-- pn=014-062 -->| First Senator | Therefore, Timon﻿— |

<!-- pn=014-063 -->| Timon | Well, sir, I will; therefore, I will, sir; thus: If Alcibiades kill my countrymen, Let Alcibiades know this of Timon, That Timon cares not. But if be sack fair Athens, And take our goodly aged men by the beards, Giving our holy virgins to the stain Of contumelious, beastly, mad-brain’d war, Then let him know, and tell him Timon speaks it, In [[mercy|pity]] of our aged and our youth, I cannot choose but tell him, that I care not, And let him take’t at worst; for their knives care not, While you have throats to answer: for myself, There’s not a whittle in the unruly camp But I do prize it at my love before The reverend’st throat in Athens. So I leave you To the protection of the prosperous gods, As thieves to keepers. |

<!-- pn=014-064 -->| Flavius | Stay not, all’s in vain. |

<!-- pn=014-065 -->| Timon | Why, I was writing of my [[death|epitaph]]; It will be seen to-morrow: my long sickness Of health and living now begins to mend, And nothing brings me all things. Go, live still; Be Alcibiades your plague, you his, And last so long enough! |

<!-- pn=014-066 -->| First Senator | We speak in vain. |

<!-- pn=014-067 -->| Timon | But yet I love my country, and am not One that rejoices in the common wreck, As common bruit doth put it. |

<!-- pn=014-068 -->| First Senator | That’s well spoke. |

<!-- pn=014-069 -->| Timon | Commend me to my loving countrymen﻿— |

<!-- pn=014-070 -->| First Senator | These words become your lips as they pass thorough them. |

<!-- pn=014-071 -->| Second Senator | And enter in our ears like great triumphers In their applauding gates. |

<!-- pn=014-072 -->| Timon | Commend me to them, And tell them that, to ease them of their griefs, Their fears of hostile strokes, their aches, losses, Their pangs of love, with other incident throes That nature’s fragile vessel doth sustain In life’s uncertain voyage, I will some kindness do them: I’ll teach them to prevent wild Alcibiades’ [[anger|wrath]]. |

<!-- pn=014-073 -->| First Senator | I like this well; he will return again. |

<!-- pn=014-074 -->| Timon | I have a tree, which grows here in my close, That mine own use invites me to cut down, And shortly must I fell it: tell my friends, Tell Athens, in the sequence of degree From high to low throughout, that whoso please To stop [[misery|affliction]], let him take his haste, Come hither, ere my tree hath felt the axe, And hang himself. I pray you, do my greeting. |

<!-- pn=014-075 -->| Flavius | Trouble him no further; thus you still shall find him. |

<!-- pn=014-076 -->| Timon | Come not to me again: but say to Athens, Timon hath made his everlasting mansion Upon the beached verge of the salt flood; Who once a day with his embossed froth The turbulent surge shall cover: thither come, And let my grave-stone be your oracle. Lips, let sour words go by and language end: What is amiss plague and infection mend! Graves only be men’s works and death their gain! Sun, hide thy beams! Timon hath done his reign. *Retires to his cave.* |

<!-- pn=014-077 -->| First Senator | His discontents are unremoveably Coupled to nature. |

<!-- pn=014-078 -->| Second Senator | Our hope in him is dead: let us return, And strain what other means is left unto us In our dear peril. |

<!-- pn=014-079 -->| First Senator | It requires swift foot. *Exeunt.* |
