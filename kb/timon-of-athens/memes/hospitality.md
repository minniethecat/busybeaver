# Hospitality

## The Social Economy of the Open House

Hospitality in *Timon of Athens* is not merely background or atmosphere — it is the central mechanism of the plot. Timon's open house, his endless feasts and gifts, constitutes the social world of the play's first half. Through hospitality, Timon creates and sustains his identity as a great man. Through hospitality, the flatterers and parasites gain access to his wealth. And through the perversion of hospitality — the water banquet — Timon delivers his most devastating act of revenge. The play is, among other things, a systematic study of what happens when the ancient virtue of hospitality is pushed to its limit and beyond.

## The Feast as Ritual

Timon's banquets are not simply meals. They are rituals of status and reciprocity. In Act 1, Scene 2, Timon presides over a lavish feast attended by senators, lords, and poets. The scene establishes the social hierarchy: Timon at the center, distributing food, drink, gifts, and favor. The guests perform gratitude — the poet reads his work, the painter shows his portrait, the lords make speeches of friendship. But the performance is hollow. The guests are there for what Timon gives, not for Timon himself.

Hospitality in the ancient world carried specific obligations. The Greek concept of *xenia* bound host and guest in a relationship of mutual obligation: the host provided food and shelter; the guest was expected to reciprocate when the host visited, or to refrain from harming the host's household. Timon's guests violate this compact comprehensively. They accept his hospitality, consume his wealth, and then refuse to help him when he is in need. The play thus depicts the collapse of an ancient ethical framework under the pressure of a more modern, transactional social order.

## The Parasite Economy

Timon's hospitality attracts a specific class of character: the parasite. The poet, the painter, the jeweler, the lords Lucius and Lucullus — all feed on Timon's generosity. Apemantus, the cynical philosopher, sees through the charade from the beginning. "He that loves to be flattered is worthy o' the flatterer," he tells Timon (90-91), diagnosing the symbiosis between host and parasite. Timon needs flatterers as much as they need his gold; his hospitality is not pure generosity but a system of mutual dependence.

The figure of the parasite has a long theatrical history, going back to Greek and Roman comedy. Shakespeare draws on this tradition — the flatterers who attach themselves to a wealthy patron, the feasts that draw them — but he darkens it considerably. In comedy, the parasite is usually exposed and expelled. In *Timon of Athens*, the parasites consume their host and walk away unscathed. The comedy curdles into tragedy.

## The Water Banquet: Hospitality Inverted

The play's most shocking scene is Timon's final feast (Act 3, Scene 6). He invites his false friends to another banquet. They arrive, expecting a reconciliation and more gifts. Timon unveils covered dishes that contain only warm water and stones. He hurls the water in their faces and drives them out with curses. "Uncover, dogs, and lap," he cries (792).

This is the perversion of hospitality as ritual. Timon uses the form of the banquet — the invitation, the covered dishes, the host's speech — to deliver not nourishment but humiliation. The water banquet inverts every norm of hospitality: the host does not welcome but attacks; the food does not sustain but offends; the guests do not give thanks but flee. It is hospitality weaponized, and it marks Timon's decisive break with civilization. After this scene, he retreats to the woods and never returns.

## Hospitality in Context

The play's treatment of hospitality reflects tensions in both ancient Athens and Shakespeare's England. In classical Athens, hospitality was a sacred duty — Zeus himself was the god of guests, *Zeus Xenios*. Violating the laws of hospitality invited divine punishment. In Jacobean England, hospitality was a marker of aristocratic virtue and social obligation, but it was also under pressure from new forms of wealth and individualism. Timon's fall can be read as a fable about the unsustainability of traditional hospitality in a world governed by credit, interest, and self-interest.
