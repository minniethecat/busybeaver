## Overview

The banishment of [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] is the play's central political crisis. When [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] petitions the Senate to pardon a soldier who has killed a man in a brawl, the senators refuse — and when [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] persists, they banish him from [[athens|Athens]]. The scene crystallizes the play's critique of law and [[law|justice]]: the same senators who practise [[debt|usury]] now condemn a soldier to death, cloaking cruelty in the language of legal necessity. [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]'s banishment transforms him from a loyal Athenian general into the leader of an army that will eventually conquer [[athens|the city]].

## Context

The banishment scene follows directly from the scenes of [[timon|Timon]]'s financial ruin. Where [[timon|Timon]]'s t[[anger|rage]]dy is economic and personal, [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]'s is political and martial. The two men are mirror figures: both have served [[athens|Athens]], both are betrayed by its institutions, and both respond with [[anger|rage]] — [[timon|Timon]] through misanthropy, [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] through military action. The Senate's treatment of [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] demonstrates that ingratitude is not merely a private vice (as with [[timon|Timon]]'s false [[friendship|friends]]) but a structural feature of Athenian governance.

## Key Moments

- **[[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]'s plea**: [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] argues that the soldier's military service should earn him mercy: "In vain! his service done / At Lacedaemon and Byzantium" (009-014). The plea is reasonable — a soldier's life for a soldier's offence — but the Senate is unmoved.

- **The Senate's logic**: "Nothing emboldens sin so much as mercy" (009-003) and "For [[mercy|pity]] is the virtue of the law, / And none but tyrants use it cruelly" (009-008). The senators deploy the language of legal humanism to justify judicial murder, a rhetorical strategy the play exposes as hollow.

- **The banishment sentence**: "We banish thee for ever" (009-026). The sentence is delivered not as [[law|justice]] but as power — the Senate silencing a voice that has challenged its authority.

- **[[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]'s defiance**: "Banish me! / Banish your dotage; banish [[debt|usury]], / That makes [[senate-house|the senate]] ugly" (009-027). [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] turns the sentence back upon the senators, naming their [[disease|corruption]] explicitly. His banishment is, in his framing, a liberation from a rotten state.

## Dramatic Function

The banishment establishes [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] as the play's political counterweight to [[timon|Timon]]. Where [[timon|Timon]] withdraws from society, [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] attacks it. His banishment also provides the play's second plot — the military campaign against [[athens|Athens]] — which will ultimately bring about the play's ambiguous resolution. The scene's legal language and its exposure of institutional hypocrisy connect the personal t[[anger|rage]]dy of [[timon|Timon]] to the political t[[anger|rage]]dy of [[athens|Athens]].
