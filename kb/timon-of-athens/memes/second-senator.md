# Second Senator

## Role in the Play

The Second Senator appears in two key institutional scenes: [[senate-house|the senate]]'s harsh judgment of [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]'s friend (Scene IX) and the senators' desperate embassy to [[timon|Timon]] after [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] threatens [[athens|Athens]] (Scene XIV–XV). In both, he embodies the impersonal, procedural logic of state power — a logic that proves as merciless in punishment as it is helpless in self-preservation. (009-004)

## Key Scenes

**The Senate Judgment** (009-004–009-022): The Second Senator supports the death sentence for [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]'s friend. His initial pronouncement — "Most true; the law shall bruise him" (009-004) — sets the tone of institutional implacability. When [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] argues for mercy, the Senator dismisses the plea: "You breathe in vain" (009-013). His final speech catalogues the condemned man's offences — "He has made too much plenty with 'em; He's a sworn rioter" (009-017) — reducing a complex human being to a legal case. The banishment of [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] that follows this scene is the proximate cause of [[athens|Athens]]'s military crisis.

**The Embassy to [[timon|Timon]]** (014-049–015-005): After [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] marches on [[athens|Athens]], the Second Senator joins the delegation that seeks [[timon|Timon]]'s help. His report — "They confess Toward thee forgetfulness" (014-058) — acknowledges [[athens|the city]]'s ingratitude. But his final assessment — "Our hope in him is dead" (015-005) — confirms that the senators' belated appeal to [[timon|Timon]] has failed. The scene dramatises the reversal of [[wealth|fortune]]: those who wielded institutional power without mercy must now beg for mercy from the man they wronged.

## Relationships

**[[first-senator|First Senator]]**: His senatorial colleague, with whom he forms a unified front of institutional authority. Their shared harshness in Scene IX and shared desperation in Scene XIV trace the arc of [[athens|Athens]]'s political collapse.

**[[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]**: The Athenian captain whose friend [[senate-house|the senate]] condemns. The Second Senator's refusal of mercy triggers [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]'s rebellion, which ultimately forces the senators into the humiliating position of supplicants.

**[[timon|Timon]]**: The misanthropic exile whose military genius the senators belatedly seek. The Second Senator's assessment that [[timon|Timon]] is beyond reach marks the final collapse of Athenian political hope.


## Notable Quotations

The Second Senator's opening judgment is swift and implacable — law must crush without mercy:

> Most true; the law shall bruise him.
>
> (009-004)

When [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] argues eloquently for his friend's life, the Senator dismisses the plea with three blunt words:

> You breathe in vain.
>
> (009-013)

His catalogue of the condemned man's offences reduces a human being to a legal case — a rhetoric of institutional implacability:

> He has made too much plenty with 'em; He's a sworn rioter: he has a sin that often Drowns him, and takes his valour prisoner.
>
> (009-017)
