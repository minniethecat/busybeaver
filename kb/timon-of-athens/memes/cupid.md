# Cupid

## Role in the Play

Cupid is a mythological personification who appears as the presenter and marshal of the allegorical masque at [[timons-feast|Timon's Great Feast]] in Act 1, Scene 2. He functions as a herald, announcing the arrival of Ladies dressed as Amazons who perform a dance with lutes representing the five senses paying tribute to [[timon|Timon]] as their patron. Cupid is not a character in the dramatic sense — he has no psychology, no arc, and no independent agency — but rather a personified abstraction drawn from the tradition of courtly masque. His presence elevates [[timon|Timon]]'s [[feast|banquet]] from a private dinner to a ceremonial spectacle, framing [[timon|Timon]]'s [[bounty|generosity]] as a cosmic principle that commands the allegiance of love itself and the senses that love governs. The servant describes Cupid as "a forerunner, my lord, which bears that office, to signify their pleasures" (002-037), defining his dramatic function: he exists to announce, to bridge the space between the mortal feast and the allegorical entertainment, and then to vanish.

## Key Scenes

**Act I, Scene 2 — Entrance as Forerunner** (002-039): Cupid enters after a servant announces a messenger "which bears that office, to signify their pleasures" (002-037). His entrance is itself a theatrical moment — the mythological god of love stepping into [[timons-house|Timon's house]] as if the mortal world were worthy of divine visitation.

**Act I, Scene 2 — The Five Senses Speech** (002-040): Cupid delivers his only substantial speech, an allegorical address in which he declares that the five best senses "Acknowledge thee their patron" and "come freely / To gratulate thy plenteous bosom" (002-040). He explains that four senses — ear, taste, touch, and smell — have been satisfied by Timon's [[feast|hospitality]], and only sight remains: "They only now come but to feast thine eyes" (002-040). The speech transforms [[timon|Timon]]'s material [[bounty|generosity]] into a philosophical principle: he is not merely a rich man giving [[gold|money]] away, but the patron of all sensory experience, a figure whose [[bounty|generosity]] is so complete that it satisfies every faculty of human perception.

**Act I, Scene 2 — The Masque of the Amazons** (002-043): Cupid reenters with "a mask of Ladies as Amazons, with lutes in their hands, dancing and playing" (002-043). He presides silently over the performance, a mythological master of ceremonies whose presence sanctifies the entertainment. The masque is one of the play's few moments of pure spectacle, and Cupid serves as its living emblem.

**Act I, Scene 2 — Exit with the Ladies** (002-050): After [[timon|Timon]] thanks the Ladies — "You have done our pleasures much grace, fair ladies" (002-046) — Cupid and the Ladies exit together with the line "Most thankfully, my lord" (002-050). Cupid's final word, "thankfully," echoes the play's central concern with [[gratitude]] and [[reciprocity]], though his gratitude, like everything about him, is allegorical rather than personal.

## Relationships

**[[timon|Timon]]**: Cupid's relationship to [[timon|Timon]] is entirely ceremonial. He hails [[timon|Timon]] as the senses' "patron" (002-040) and frames the feast as a tribute to [[timon|Timon]]'s [[bounty|generosity]]. Cupid's address flatters [[timon|Timon]] in mythological terms, contributing to the atmosphere of adoration that surrounds [[timon|Timon]] at the height of his fortune and makes his subsequent fall all the more vertiginous. Cupid represents the kind of ornamental [[flattery]] that [[apemantus|Apemantus]] will immediately denounce.

**[[apemantus|Apemantus]]**: Cupid has no direct interaction with [[apemantus|Apemantus]], but their juxtaposition is dramaturgically significant. Cupid embodies the world of [[flattery|flattery]], spectacle, and courtly performance — precisely the world that [[apemantus|Apemantus]] exists to critique. During Cupid's masque, [[apemantus|Apemantus]] delivers his most sustained attack on the feast's vanity: "Hoy-day, what a sweep of vanity comes this way! They dance! they are mad women. / Like madness is the glory of this life" (002-044). Cupid's mythological grace and [[apemantus|Apemantus]]'s cynical commentary represent the play's central opposition between the aesthetics of [[bounty|generosity]] and the philosophy of [[misanthropy|cynicism]].

## Notable Quotes

> Hail to thee, worthy Timon, and to all / That of his bounties taste! The five best senses / Acknowledge thee their patron; and come freely / To gratulate thy plenteous bosom (002-040)

> They only now come but to feast thine eyes. (002-040)

## Character Analysis

Cupid is the most purely allegorical figure in *Timon of Athens*, a character whose entire being is identical with his dramatic function. He appears, he announces, he presides, he exits — and in doing so he transforms [[timon|Timon]]'s private [[feast|banquet]] into a public spectacle with cosmological pretensions. The choice of Cupid as masque-presenter is thematically precise: love, in the play's symbolic economy, is the governing principle of [[timon|Timon]]'s world. [[timon|Timon]] gives not from calculation but from an overflowing of affection for his [[friendship|friends]], and Cupid's presence suggests — however ironically — that love itself acknowledges [[timon|Timon]]'s sovereignty. The speech about the five senses (002-040) is a miniature allegory of [[timon|Timon]]'s philosophy: the material world exists to be given, and the senses exist to receive what is given.

Yet Cupid's presence also carries a sharp dramatic irony. The god of love appears at the feast to celebrate [[friendship|friends]]hip and [[bounty|generosity]], but the [[friendship|friends]] who witness this spectacle will, in the very next acts, refuse [[timon|Timon]] the smallest loan when his fortune collapses. Cupid's speech about "feasting the eyes" (002-040) takes on retrospective bitterness: the lords at the feast do indeed feast only their eyes — on [[timon|Timon]]'s wealth, his food, his entertainment — without any corresponding [[gratitude|obligation]] of the heart. The masque, in retrospect, is less a celebration of [[timon|Timon]]'s [[bounty|generosity]] than a demonstration of its futility: all this [[feast|spectacle]], all this [[flattery|ceremony]], and in the end there is no [[gratitude|reciprocity]], no loyalty, no love.

Cupid's silence during the masque itself — he does not speak while the Ladies dance, only before and after — reinforces his function as a framing device rather than a character. He is the threshold figure between the mortal world of the feast and the mythological world of the allegory, and once that threshold is crossed, he has nothing more to say. His exit with the Ladies (002-050) is absolute: Cupid never reappears in the play, because after Act 1 the world of mythological [[flattery|celebration]] is no longer available to [[timon|Timon]]. The god of love departs, and what follows is a world without love, without [[bounty|generosity]], and without the consoling [[feast|beauty]] of spectacle — a world of [[debt|creditors]], [[betrayal|false friends]], and [[misanthropy|bitterness]] in which Cupid has no place.
