# First Lord

## Role in the Play

The First Lord is one of the unnamed Athenian aristocrats who populate [[timon|Timon]]'s social world. Appearing alongside the [[second-lord|Second Lord]], he participates in the feasts, receives [[timon|Timon]]'s gifts, and speaks the language of [[friendship]] while practising the reality of self-[[debt|interest]]. Like his counterpart, the First Lord is distinguished only by his numerical designation — a deliberate choice by Shakespeare that emphasises the systemic rather than individual nature of [[timon|Timon]]'s betrayal. Any lord would have done the same, because the system of patronage, [[bounty|generosity]], and [[gratitude|reciprocity]] makes genuine [[service|loyalty]] impossible. (001-148)

## Key Scenes

**Act 1, Scene 1 — First Words** (001-148): The First Lord's first line — "What time o' day is't, [[apemantus|Apemantus]]?" — is an attempt at polite conversation that [[apemantus|Apemantus]] immediately rebuffs. The exchange establishes the First Lord as a man of social convention, uncomfortable with [[apemantus|the philosopher]]'s refusal to observe the niceties of elite interaction. (001-150)

**Act 1, Scene 1 — Defence of [[timon|Timon]]** (001-162): When [[apemantus|Apemantus]] criticises humanity, the First Lord defends [[timon|Timon]] — "He's opposite to humanity" — and later praises "The noblest mind he carries / That ever governed man." This effusive praise, delivered in [[timon|Timon]]'s presence, demonstrates the hyperbolic [[flattery]] that [[timon|Timon]] mistakes for genuine affection. (001-164)

**Act 1, Scene 2 — The Banquet** (002-007): The First Lord attends the [[feast]] and responds to [[timon|Timon]]'s [[bounty|generosity]] with feigned modesty: "Might we but have that happiness, my lord, that you would once use our hearts…" His language performs the dance of reciprocal [[debt|obligation]] that the lords will later refuse to honour. (002-025)

**Act 3 — The Abandonment**: When [[timon|Timon]]'s [[wealth|fortune]] collapses and his servants seek loans, the First Lord is conspicuously absent. His earlier declarations of "virtuous bond" and "the best of happiness" are revealed as the currency of a social performance that has no value outside [[timons-feast|the banquet]] hall. (002-085)

## Relationships

**[[timon|Timon]]**: The First Lord is a recipient of [[timon|Timon]]'s [[bounty|generosity]] who later abandons him. His relationship to [[timon|Timon]] is defined entirely by [[gold|wealth]] — when [[timon|Timon]] has [[gold]], the First Lord is present; when [[timon|Timon]] is bankrupt, the First Lord disappears. (001-148)

**[[second-lord|Second Lord]]**: The First Lord's companion and mirror. Together they demonstrate the collective nature of Athenian [[flattery]] — no individual lord is uniquely guilty because all participate in the same system. (001-152)

**[[apemantus|Apemantus]]**: When [[apemantus|Apemantus]] refuses to observe social niceties, the First Lord's response — "Hang thyself!" — reveals the violence that lurks beneath the polished surface of aristocratic civility. The First Lord can tolerate adulation but not honesty. (001-158)

## Character Analysis

The First Lord, like the [[second-lord|Second Lord]], is a representative figure rather than a developed character. His anonymity — he is identified only by a number, not a name — is Shakespeare's way of suggesting that the problem is not particular individuals but the entire structure of Athenian elite society. The First Lord's language is the language of [[friendship]] and [[honour|honour]], but his actions are the actions of a rational economic actor. When [[timon|Timon]] is rich, [[friendship|friends]]hip costs nothing and yields [[gold]]; when [[timon|Timon]] is poor, [[friendship|friends]]hip costs gold and yields nothing. The First Lord's choice is rational — and this rationality is precisely the play's indictment of a world in which human bonds have been reduced to calculations of profit and loss. (001-162)


## Notable Quotations

The First Lord's praise of [[timon|Timon]] reveals the flattering hyperbole that sustains the gift economy:

> He's opposite to humanity. Come, shall we in, And taste Lord Timon's bounty? he outgoes The very heart of kindness.
>
> (001-162)

His final tribute encapsulates the idealised image of [[timon|Timon]] that the play will systematically dismantle:

> The noblest mind he carries That ever govern'd man.
>
> (001-164)
