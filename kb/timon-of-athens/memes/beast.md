# Beast Imagery in Timon of Athens

## Overview

No Shakespeare play deploys animal imagery with the density and systematic intent of *[[timon-of-athens|Timon of Athens]]*. From the opening scene's incidental references to hawks and greyhounds through [[timon|Timon]]'s [[nature|wilderness]] tirades that reduce all of [[athens|Athens]] to a "forest of beasts" (013-111), the play constructs a zoological lexicon of human degradation. The imagery follows a clear arc: in Acts I–II, animal references are largely ornamental — the hounds and horses of aristocratic display. In Acts III–V, after [[timon|Timon]]'s ruin, the bestiary darkens into a catalogue of predation: wolves, tigers, vultures, serpents. [[timon|Timon]]'s central philosophical move is not simply to call men beasts but to argue that beasts are morally superior to men — that the animal kingdom, governed by instinct and need, is more honest than the human world governed by greed and hypocrisy. The play's final scene literalizes this argument: [[timons-death|Timon's epitaph]] declares that "Some beast rear'd this" (016-003), and the question of whether any man survives [[honour|worth]]y of the name is left deliberately unresolved.

## Key Passages

> What a wicked beast was I to disfurnish myself against such a good time, when I might ha' shown myself honourable! (006-020)

[[lucilius|Lucilius]]'s self-reproach, delivered after he has spent his [[gold|money]] and cannot assist his patron [[timon|Timon]], introduces the play's characteristic equation of moral failure with bestiality. Calling himself a "wicked beast" is not mere rhetoric — it reflects the play's growing insistence that human behaviour, when stripped of its social pretenses, is indistinguishable from animal instinct. The irony is that [[lucilius|Lucilius]]'s honest shame distinguishes him from the unrepentant lords who will refuse [[timon|Timon]] without a trace of self-reproach.

> in that beastly fury He has been known to commit such outrages (009-017)

The Senators' description of [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]' friend uses "beastly [[anger|fury]]" to characterize the violence of the man they have condemned. The word "beastly" here functions as a rhetorical weapon — by labeling the condemned man's actions as bestial, the Senators position themselves as defenders of civilization against animal disorder. But the play's larger structure undermines this opposition: the Senators' own conduct (refusing mercy, prioritizing property over life) reveals that "beastly" behaviour is not confined to those they condemn.

> A beast, as thou art. The canker gnaw thy heart, For showing me again the eyes of man! (013-006)

[[timon|Timon]]'s first words in [[the-woods|the wilderness]] scene, spoken to [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]], are the play's definitive reduction of humanity to bestiality. The line performs a double move: first, it names [[timon|Timon]]'s interlocutor a beast; second, it identifies the "eyes of man" — the capacity for human connection, for being seen by another — as the canker that gnaws at [[timon|Timon]]'s heart. To be a beast is to have escaped the burden of human relationship; to be a man is to suffer, and to cause [[misery|suffering]], through the very capacity for recognition.

> Give it the beasts, to be rid of the men. (013-107)

[[apemantus|Apemantus]], offering [[timon|Timon]] food that [[timon|Timon]] refuses, draws the sharpest possible distinction between beast and man. The beasts deserve food because they are innocent of the vices [[timon|Timon]] attributes to humanity. To give food to men would be to sustain the very [[disease|corruption]] [[timon|Timon]] has fled. This line crystallizes the play's most radical argument: that the non-human world is morally cleaner than the human, and that [[timon|Timon]]'s misanthropy is a form of ecological protest against a species that has betrayed its own nature.

> the commonwealth of Athens is become a forest of beasts. (013-111)

[[apemantus|Apemantus]]'s diagnosis transforms [[athens|the city]] — civilization's defining form — into a "forest," nature's space of lawless predation. The word "common[[gold|wealth]]" carries heavy irony: [[athens|Athens]] is a common[[gold|wealth]] only in the sense that all its citizens share a common bestial nature. The image of a "forest of beasts" is the play's most compressed statement of its social vision — a polity in which every citizen is a predator and every relationship a food chain.

> Nor on the beasts themselves, the birds, and fishes; You must eat men. (013-151)

[[timon|Timon]]'s injunction to the bandits reverses the expected hierarchy of predation. Beasts, birds, and fishes — the traditional quarry of human hunters — are declared off-limits. The bandits "must eat men," must turn their predatory instincts against their own species, because humanity is the one creature whose consumption is morally permissible, even necessary. This is the logical endpoint of [[timon|Timon]]'s beast philosophy: cannibalism as moral imperative, the species devouring itself as the only fitting punishment for its crimes.

> Some beast rear'd this; there does not live a man. (016-003)

The Soldier's discovery of [[timons-death|Timon's grave]] in the final scene provides the play's last word on the human/beast distinction. The [[death|epitaph]] — "there does not live a man" — is both factual ([[timon|Timon]] is dead) and philosophical (no true man exists; all are beasts). The ambiguity of "rear'd" (reared, raised, constructed) leaves open whether a literal beast built the tomb or whether the human who built it was so degraded as to be bestial. The play ends where [[timon|Timon]]'s philosophy began: with the claim that humanity has forfeited its claim to distinction from the animals.

## Character Perspectives

**[[timon|Timon]]** undergoes the play's most complete transformation in his relationship to beast imagery. In Acts I–II he is the generous host who sets hounds upon his guests as gifts — animals as tokens of aristocratic display. In Acts IV–V he is the misanthropic exile who argues systematically for the moral superiority of beasts over men. His trajectory from animal-giver to animal-identifier represents the play's philosophical arc: the discovery that the giving of gifts was itself a bestial instinct, a pack behaviour that collapsed the moment the alpha could no longer provide.

**[[apemantus|Apemantus]]** shares [[timon|Timon]]'s bestial vocabulary but applies it differently. Where [[timon|Timon]]'s beast-talk is passionate and wounded, [[apemantus|Apemantus]]'s is cool and diagnostic. His observation that [[athens|Athens]] is "a forest of beasts" (013-111) is not a cry of pain but a clinical description — he has always known what [[timon|Timon]] must learn through catastrophe. [[apemantus|Apemantus]]'s [[misanthropy|cynicism]] represents the intellectual position that [[timon|Timon]] reaches only through emotional devastation.

**The Bandits** provide the test case for [[timon|Timon]]'s beast philosophy. When [[timon|Timon]] urges them to "eat men" and continue their crimes, they briefly consider whether his advice is sincere or malicious. Their conclusion — that "'Tis in the malice of mankind that he thus advises us" (013-153) — suggests that even professional criminals recognize a distinction between beastly behaviour and the calculated cruelty [[timon|Timon]] now advocates.

**[[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]** represents the beast imagery's political dimension. His "beastly [[anger|fury]]" (009-017) is the Senators' term for his righteous anger at in[[law|justice]]. The play asks whether a beastly response to a beastly society is not merely natural but necessary — whether [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]' military assault on [[athens|Athens]] is the only language a city of beasts can understand.

## Critical Interpretations

The beast imagery in *[[timon-of-athens|Timon of Athens]]* draws on a rich classical tradition, particularly [[apemantus|the Cynic]] philosophy associated with Diogenes, who was called "the Dog" (*kyon*) for his shameless rejection of social convention. [[apemantus|Apemantus]], the play's resident Cynic, explicitly links beastliness to philosophical truth-telling: to see men as beasts is to see them as they are, stripped of the social fictions that sustain civilized life. The play's beast imagery is thus not mere insult but a philosophical method — a systematic reduction of the human to the animal as a way of exposing the hollowness of social pretensions.

The play's ecology of predation — wolves, tigers, vultures — has been read as a proto-Hobbesian vision of the state of nature. [[timon|Timon]]'s [[athens|Athens]], after the collapse of his bounty, becomes a war of all against all, a "forest of beasts" in which every man preys upon every other. Shakespeare wrote the play around the same time as *King Lear* (1605–1606), which shares the beast imagery and the vision of a natural order in collapse, suggesting a period of concentrated meditation on the boundary between human and animal.

More recent ecocritical readings have argued that *[[timon-of-athens|Timon of Athens]]* is Shakespeare's most radical environmental text. [[timon|Timon]]'s retreat to [[the-woods|the wilderness]], his digging for roots, his discovery that the earth yields gold rather than sustenance — all these elements construct a vision of nature corrupted by human values. The beasts are innocent; it is human contact that poisons the natural world. [[timon|Timon]]'s suicide in his self-dug [[death|grave]] by the sea can be read as a return to elemental nature, a rejection of the human that is also a form of ecological self-sacrifice.
