# Second Lord

## Role in the Play

The Second Lord is one of the Athenian aristocrats who populate [[timon|Timon]]'s social world. Alongside the [[first-lord|First Lord]] and other unnamed lords, he participates in the culture of [[flattery]] and gift-exchange that defines the Athenian elite. When [[timon|Timon]] is [[gold|wealth]]y, the Second Lord speaks of him with hyperbolic admiration; when [[timon|Timon]] is ruined, he vanishes with the rest. The Second Lord has no distinguishing personality or individual arc — and this is precisely the point. His interchangeability with the other lords demonstrates that [[timon|Timon]]'s [[friendship|friends]] are not individuals who happen to be false but representatives of a social system in which falseness is structural. (001-152)

## Key Scenes

**Act 1, Scene 1 — First Appearance** (001-152): The Second Lord first speaks when he announces he is going to [[timon|Lord Timon]]'s, establishing him as a participant in the patronage economy. His exchange with [[apemantus|Apemantus]] reveals his discomfort with [[apemantus|the philosopher]]'s bluntness — when [[apemantus|Apemantus]] refuses to flatter, the Second Lord calls him an "unpeaceable dog." (001-160)

**Act 1, Scene 2 — The Banquet** (002-016): The Second Lord attends [[timon|Timon]]'s [[feast]], receiving gifts and offering praise. He calls for more wine and participates in the ritual of mutual admiration that defines [[timon|Timon]]'s [[feast|hospitality]]. When [[timon|Timon]] calls for his coffers, the Second Lord is among those who benefit from his [[bounty|generosity]]. (002-028)

**Act 2, Scene 2 — The Refusal** (002-069): When [[lucilius|Timon's servant]]s come seeking loans in Act 3 (or Act 2 in some editions), the Second Lord's refusal — delivered through a servant — confirms the pattern established by [[lucullus|Lucullus]], [[lucius|Lucius]], and [[sempronius|Sempronius]]: each friend has a carefully crafted excuse, and none will reciprocate the [[bounty|generosity]] they have received. (002-077)

## Relationships

**[[timon|Timon]]**: The Second Lord is one of [[timon|Timon]]'s false [[friendship|friends]] — he accepts [[timon|Timon]]'s gifts, praises him at [[timons-feast|the banquet]], and refuses him assistance when his [[wealth|fortune]] turns. He represents the generic Athenian nobleman whose [[service|loyalty]] is entirely contingent on [[timon|Timon]]'s [[gold|wealth]]. (001-152)

**[[first-lord|First Lord]]**: The Second Lord's counterpart and fellow flatterer. The two lords function as a pair, demonstrating that false [[friendship]] is not an individual moral failing but a collective social practice. (001-148)

**[[apemantus|Apemantus]]**: The Second Lord is a target of [[apemantus|Apemantus]]'s contempt. When [[apemantus|Apemantus]] rebuffs his overtures, the Second Lord reveals his true colours — calling [[apemantus|Apemantus]] an "unpeaceable dog" and threatening violence. His inability to tolerate honesty is characteristic of the flattery system he inhabits. (001-160)

## Character Analysis

The Second Lord is deliberately underdeveloped as a character. Shakespeare gives him no name, no distinguishing traits, no personal history — only a numerical designation and a set of representative actions. This anonymity is thematically significant: [[timon|Timon]]'s t[[anger|rage]]dy is not that he chose the wrong [[friendship|friends]] but that the very concept of [[friendship]] in a commercial society is corrupted. Any lord would have behaved as the Second Lord does, because the system of patronage and [[gratitude|reciprocity]] that defines Athenian elite society makes genuine [[service|loyalty]] structurally impossible. The Second Lord is not a villain; he is a function of a world in which all human bonds have been reduced to transactions. (001-163)


## Notable Quotations

The Second Lord's praise of [[timon|Timon]]'s [[bounty|generosity]] describes a gift economy of miraculous multiplication:

> He pours it out; Plutus, the god of gold, Is but his steward: no meed, but he repays Sevenfold above itself; no gift to him, But breeds the giver a return exceeding All use of quittance.
>
> (001-163)
