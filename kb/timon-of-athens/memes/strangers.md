# Three Strangers

The **Three Strangers** appear in [[Act 3]], Scene 2 of *Timon of Athens*, where they witness and comment upon [[Lucullus]]'s refusal to lend money to [[Timon]]. Though they occupy only a single scene, their dialogue constitutes one of the play's most important passages of moral commentary, functioning much like a Greek chorus to articulate the audience's ethical judgment on the events unfolding.

## Dramatic Context

The scene occurs after Timon has sent servants to three of his supposed friends — [[Lucullus]], [[Lucius]], and [[Sempronius]] — requesting financial assistance. The Strangers encounter [[Flavius]] and other servants outside Lucullus's house and learn that Lucullus has denied Timon's request. Unlike the lords themselves, who rationalize their refusals with self-serving logic, the Strangers respond with immediate and unambiguous moral clarity.

## The First Stranger: Moral Witness

The **First Stranger** speaks the most extended and significant lines in the scene. Upon hearing of Lucullus's refusal, he observes:

> "We know him for no less, though we are but strangers to him" ([[TN 3.2.61-62]])

This line establishes the strangers' paradoxical position: they have no personal relationship with Timon, yet they recognize his worth more authentically than the lords who claimed his friendship. Their status as "strangers" is crucial — precisely because they have no financial stake in Timon's fortunes, their judgment carries the weight of disinterested truth.

The First Stranger then delivers the scene's most famous speech:

> "Why, this is the world's soul; / And just of the same piece / Is every flatterer's spirit. Who can call him his friend / That dips in the same dish? For, in my knowing, / Timon has been this lord's father, / And kept his credit with his purse; / Supported his estate; nay, Timon's money / Has paid his men their wages" ([[TN 3.2.64-71]])

This passage articulates the play's thesis with extraordinary concision. The "world's soul" — the animating principle of society as it actually operates — is flattery and self-interest, not genuine friendship. The First Stranger identifies the core betrayal: Lucullus has accepted Timon's patronage as though it were friendship, then abandoned Timon as though there had been no relationship at all.

Most tellingly, the First Stranger declares:

> "I protest, I will not call him knave to my face, / But I had such a courage to do him good. / But give him a thousand pounds — what a thing it is! / Had I but the means, so much I love his heart, / I would supply his want" (paraphrase, [[TN 3.2.80-84]])

This hypothetical generosity — "So much I love his heart" — stands as the play's moral benchmark. A stranger, with no history of obligation or reciprocity, would do what Timon's "friends" refuse. The irony is devastating.

## The Second Stranger: Confirmation

The **Second Stranger** has fewer lines but plays a crucial confirmatory role. He is the one who first announces the refusal to the First Stranger:

> "I tell you, denied, my lord" ([[TN 3.2.57]])

By speaking these words, he establishes the factual basis for the moral reflection that follows. His function is to anchor the commentary in verifiable event — this is not rumor or speculation, but a concrete report of what has transpired. The dramatic economy of this distribution is characteristic of Shakespeare's late style: one stranger delivers the fact, another draws the moral, and the third closes with the aphorism.

## The Third Stranger: Moral Summation

The **Third Stranger** speaks only a single line, but it is one of the most quoted in the critical literature on *Timon of Athens*:

> "Religion groans at it" ([[TN 3.2.86]])

This line condenses the entire moral universe of the play into four words. "Religion" here carries its full Elizabethan weight — it encompasses piety, moral obligation, the bonds that hold society together, and the fear of divine judgment. That religion "groans" — a visceral, embodied response — suggests that the betrayal of Timon is not merely a personal wrong but a cosmic violation, something that the fabric of the moral universe itself registers with pain.

The Third Stranger's contribution, though minimal in word count, completes the moral arc of the scene: fact (Second Stranger), analysis (First Stranger), and judgment (Third Stranger).

## Choral Function

The Strangers function as a **modernized Greek chorus**. In classical drama, the chorus provided commentary, context, and moral judgment on the action. Shakespeare, whose theater lacked a formal chorus in most plays, embeds this function within named (or, in this case, deliberately unnamed) characters who observe the action from its margins.

The Strangers are particularly effective in this role because they have no narrative agenda. They are not scheming for advantage, not allied with any faction, not seeking Timon's favor. They are what their name implies — outsiders, passersby, men with no stake in the outcome. Their moral authority derives precisely from this lack of investment.

## Universality and Anonymity

That the three characters are called simply "Strangers" rather than being given names is thematically essential. They represent **any honest person** — the universal moral witness who recognizes right and wrong without needing a personal connection to the parties involved. Their anonymity makes their judgment representative rather than idiosyncratic. What any reasonable person would think about Athens's treatment of Timon is what the Strangers say.

This universality connects to the play's broader satire. The named characters — the lords, the senators, the flatterers — are all corrupted by their entanglement in Athenian social and economic systems. Only the unnamed, the marginal, the "strangers" can see clearly.

## The Closing Aphorism

The scene concludes with the First Stranger delivering what may fairly be called the play's thesis statement:

> "Policy sits above conscience" ([[TN 3.2.89]])

"Policy" here means expediency, calculation, political prudence — the logic of self-interest that governs the behavior of Lucullus and his fellow lords. "Conscience" means moral awareness, the capacity to distinguish right from wrong and to act accordingly. That policy "sits above" conscience means that in Athens — and, by implication, in the world the play depicts — practical calculation has displaced moral judgment as the governing principle of human relations.

This line echoes through the remainder of the play. Timon's subsequent misanthropy is a response to exactly this realization. Alcibiades's siege of Athens is a practical demonstration of the consequences when conscience is so thoroughly subordinated to policy.

## Thematic Relationships

The Three Strangers occupy a crucial structural position between the play's main character groups. They are neither lords nor servants, neither flatterers nor cynics. Their perspective aligns with that of [[Flavius]], the loyal steward who also perceives the injustice of Timon's treatment, but they speak with greater philosophical authority because their judgment is entirely disinterested.

Their presence also anticipates the perspective of [[Apemantus]], the philosopher-critic who has denounced Athenian society from the play's opening scenes. But where Apemantus is bitter, performative, and self-involved in his cynicism, the Strangers are measured, spontaneous, and genuinely pained by what they witness. They represent the play's moral center — the position from which the audience is meant to view the action.
