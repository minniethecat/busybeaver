## Overview

The visit of [[flavius|Flavius]] to [[timon|Timon]] in the woods is the single moment of [[grace|grace]] in a play otherwise unremittingly dark. Where every other character who comes to [[timon|Timon]]'s cave comes seeking [[gold|gold]] — the [[poet|Poet]] and [[painter|Painter]], [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]], the bandits, [[phrynia|Phrynia]] and [[timandra|Timandra]], the senators — [[flavius|Flavius]] comes seeking only to serve. He weeps at his master's fallen state, offers what little money he has saved, and asks nothing in return. [[timon|Timon]], whose misanthropy has become absolute, is momentarily shaken: he proclaims [[flavius|Flavius]] "one honest man" (013-166) before sending him away with gold and the impossible command to "hate all" (013-168). The scene is the exception that proves the rule of universal human [[ingratitude|betrayal]] — and, in that, it is also the most painful proof of the rule itself.

## Context

The visit occurs late in the long, crowded fourth scene of Act 4, after [[timon|Timon]] has already encountered [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] (and his courtesans), [[apemantus|Apemantus]], and the bandits. Each previous visitor has reinforced [[timon|Timon]]'s conviction that all humanity is predatory: even [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]], who claims [[friendship|friendship]], takes [[timon|Timon]]'s [[gold|gold]] and ignores his counsel (013-040). [[timon|Timon]] himself has dug in the earth for roots and found [[gold|gold]] instead — the very substance that defined his former life, now transformed into an instrument of destruction. His soliloquy on [[gold|gold]] as "thou common whore of mankind" (013-003) has already established the terms of his comprehensive [[hate|misanthropy]]. When [[flavius|Flavius]] enters at 013-156, the audience has seen every variety of human self-interest approach the cave; [[flavius|Flavius]] alone will prove the exception.

## Key Participants

- **[[flavius|Flavius]]** — [[timon|Timon]]'s steward, the devoted servant who tried to warn his master of his impending financial ruin throughout Acts 1 and 2. He has saved a small sum from his own resources and sought [[timon|Timon]] out through the wilderness, driven solely by love and [[service|duty]].
- **[[timon|Timon]]** — The ruined lord, now living as a root-eating hermit in the woods, whose radical misanthropy has hardened against every human face — until he sees [[flavius|Flavius]] weep.

## Narrative Summary

[[flavius|Flavius]]'s entrance is preceded by a soliloquy that establishes his motives with unambiguous clarity. Seeing [[timon|Timon]] from a distance, he cries: "O you gods! / Is yond despised and ruinous man my lord? / Full of decay and failing? O monument / And wonder of good deeds evilly bestow'd!" (013-157). He laments the perversity of a world where "friends / Who can bring noblest minds to basest ends" flourish, and resolves: "Grant I may ever love, and rather woo / Those that would mischief me than those that do!" (013-157). This declaration of disinterested love — love that expects no return — is unique in the play. When he finally addresses [[timon|Timon]], it is with the simple, heartbreaking "My dearest master!" (013-157).

[[timon|Timon]]'s first response is reflexive rejection: "Away! what art thou?" (013-158). When [[flavius|Flavius]] asks if he is forgotten, [[timon|Timon]] replies with his now-habitual absolute: "I have forgot all men; / Then, if thou grant'st thou'rt a man, I have forgot thee" (013-160). [[flavius|Flavius]] identifies himself as "an honest poor servant of yours" (013-161), but [[timon|Timon]] dismisses even this: "I never had honest man about me, I; / all I kept were knaves, to serve in meat to villains" (013-162).

The turning point comes when [[flavius|Flavius]] weeps. He protests: "The gods are witness, / Ne'er did poor steward wear a truer grief / For his undone lord than mine eyes for you" (013-163). [[timon|Timon]], seeing the tears, is brought up short: "What, dost thou weep? Come nearer. Then I love thee, / Because thou art a woman, and disclaim'st / Flinty mankind; whose eyes do never give / But thorough lust and laughter" (013-164). Even in this moment of softening, [[timon|Timon]]'s misanthropy frames his response — he can only accept [[flavius|Flavius]]'s [[honesty|honesty]] by categorising it as feminine, non-male, outside the category of "flinty mankind."

[[flavius|Flavius]] presses his offer: he begs [[timon|Timon]] "to accept my grief and whilst this poor wealth lasts / To entertain me as your steward still" (013-165). This is the key distinction: [[flavius|Flavius]] offers his own money — not [[timon|Timon]]'s — and asks to continue serving, not to receive. The offer breaks through [[timon|Timon]]'s defences. His response is the play's most-quoted passage:

> Forgive my general and exceptless rashness,
> You perpetual-sober gods! I do proclaim
> One honest man — mistake me not — but one;
> No more, I pray — and he's a steward. (013-166)

This is the play's moral turning point, and its tragedy. [[timon|Timon]] recognises [[honesty|virtue]] — but the recognition does not restore his [[faith|faith]] in humanity. Instead, [[flavius|Flavius]] becomes the exception that proves the rule: there is but one honest man, and his existence makes the dishonesty of everyone else the more damning. [[timon|Timon]] immediately doubts even this: "But tell me true — / For I must ever doubt, though ne'er so sure — / Is not thy kindness subtle, covetous, / If not a usuring kindness?" (013-166). The question is both cruel and heartbreaking — it shows how thoroughly [[timon|Timon]]'s experience of [[ingratitude|betrayal]] has poisoned his capacity for [[trust|trust]].

[[flavius|Flavius]]'s answer is the play's purest statement of selfless [[service|loyalty]]: "That which I show, heaven knows, is merely love, / Duty and zeal to your unmatched mind, / Care of your food and living" (013-167). He wishes only that [[timon|Timon]] had power and [[wealth|wealth]] so that he could requite him — by making [[timon|Timon]] rich, not himself. It is a perfect inversion of the transactional logic that governs every other relationship in the play.

[[timon|Timon]]'s response is as generous as it is poisoned. He gives [[flavius|Flavius]] gold — "the gods out of my misery / Have sent thee treasure" (013-168) — but attaches a condition that is the essence of his misanthropy: "thou shalt build from men; / Hate all, curse all, show charity to none, / But let the famish'd flesh slide from the bone, / Ere thou relieve the beggar" (013-168). He cannot give without also giving his philosophy of [[hate|hatred]]; even his recognition of virtue becomes a vector for his misanthropy. [[flavius|Flavius]] begs to stay and comfort him, but [[timon|Timon]] sends him away: "If thou hatest curses, / Stay not; fly, whilst thou art blest and free: / Ne'er see thou man, and let me ne'er see thee" (013-170).

## Significance

The scene performs a crucial dramatic function: it proves that [[timon|Timon]]'s misanthropy is not delusional but circumstantially earned, while simultaneously proving that it is not comprehensive either. There is genuine [[honesty|goodness]] in the world — [[flavius|Flavius]] embodies it — but it is too rare and too late to save [[timon|Timon]]. This is what makes the play tragic rather than merely cynical. If everyone really were a villain, [[timon|Timon]]'s hatred would be mere accuracy; the existence of [[flavius|Flavius]] shows that [[timon|Timon]]'s hatred, while intelligible, is not the whole truth.

The scene also illuminates the play's treatment of [[service|service]] as a moral category. [[flavius|Flavius]] is a steward — a servant — and his [[honesty|virtue]] is inseparable from his [[service|role]]. The play repeatedly contrasts servants ([[flavius|Flavius]], [[flaminius|Flaminius]], [[servilius|Servilius]]) with their masters, and finds the servants more honest. [[flavius|Flavius]] is the apotheosis of this pattern: the man paid to manage money is the one man who does not value [[gold|gold]] above [[friendship|love]].

Finally, the scene deepens the play's central irony. [[timon|Timon]] gives [[flavius|Flavius]] gold and tells him to "hate all" — but everything we know about [[flavius|Flavius]] tells us he will do no such thing. The command is impossible to obey for the very [[honesty|honesty]] that earned it. [[timon|Timon]]'s gift is thus doubly tragic: it rewards virtue with a poisoned philosophy that virtue cannot follow, and it severs the one human connection that might have offered [[timon|Timon]] a path back from the abyss.
