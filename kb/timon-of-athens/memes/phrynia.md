## Role

Phrynia is one of two prostitutes (with [[timandra|Timandra]]) who accompany [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] on his military campaign against [[athens|Athens]]. They encounter [[timons-exile|Timon in the woods]], where he greets them not with desire but with curses, exhorting them to spread disease among the men of [[athens|Athens]]. Phrynia's response — "Thy lips rot off!" [013-688] — is one of the play's rawest exchanges, a moment where misanthropy meets its match in a woman who refuses to be passive before [[timon|Timon]]'s [[anger|rage]]. Her presence, like [[timandra|Timandra]]'s, brings the play's critique of Athenian society into the realm of gender and sexual economics.

## Key Scenes

- **Act 13 (the woods)** — [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] enters with Phrynia and [[timandra|Timandra]]. [[timon|Timon]], emerging from his cave, initially spurns them all, then directs his particular venom at the two women. He exhorts them to "[[disease|plague]] all," to "bring down rose-cheeked youth to the tub-fast and the diet," and to spread venereal disease through the Athenian populace. Phrynia's retort — "Thy lips rot off!" — is a moment of resistance to [[timon|Timon]]'s rhetorical dominance. She refuses the role of silent vessel for his misanthropy.

## Relationships

- **[[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]**: Phrynia follows [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] as a camp follower, part of the military retinue that will eventually conquer [[athens|Athens]]. Her relationship to him is transactional — she is a courtesan, not a wife — yet her presence in his company signals the worldly, unsentimental character of [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]'s campaign.
- **[[timandra|Timandra]]**: Phrynia's fellow concubine, with whom she shares the scene. Together they form a pair that mirrors other doubles in the play ([[poet|the Poet]] and [[painter|Painter]], the pairs of false [[friendship|friends]]). Where [[timandra|Timandra]] engages [[timon|Timon]] with questions, Phrynia responds with curses — two modes of confronting the misanthrope.
- **[[timon|Timon]]**: Phrynia and [[timon|Timon]] exchange curses in a scene that strips away any pretence of civility. [[timon|Timon]] sees in the two women the embodiment of Athenian [[disease|corruption]]; Phrynia sees in [[timon|Timon]] a madman whose words are as venomous as any disease.

## Notable Quotes

> "Thy lips rot off!"

## Character Analysis

Phrynia and [[timandra|Timandra]] are among Shakespeare's most marginal named characters — two prostitutes in a play that has little room for women. Yet their scene with [[timon|Timon]] is significant precisely because it brings gender into the play's critique of Athenian society. [[timon|Timon]]'s curses upon the women — that they should spread disease, corrupt youth, and ruin families — reveal a strain of misogyny within his broader misanthropy. Phrynia's defiant response suggests that even the most marginalized figures in the play's world possess a capacity for resistance that [[timon|Timon]]'s philosophy cannot entirely suppress.
