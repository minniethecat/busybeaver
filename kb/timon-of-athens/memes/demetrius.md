# Demetrius

## Role in the Play

Demetrius is a servant in the household of [[timon|Timon]]. He appears by name in the Dramatis Personae of _Timon of Athens_ as one of Timon's servants, alongside [[flavius|Flavius]] (the steward), [[flaminius|Flaminius]], [[servilius|Servilius]], [[lucilius|Lucilius]], and others. Unlike those named servants who have speaking roles and identifiable dramatic functions, Demetrius has no surviving dialogue in the Folio text and no distinct scene entry attributed to him. His presence is vestigial — a name in the cast list that stands for one of the many household attendants who populate Timon's halls during the play's first half and who scatter upon his ruin (013-001–013-003).

The very existence of a named servant without lines is itself significant to the play's social architecture. Shakespeare's _Timon of Athens_ distinguishes itself from other tragedies by populating the margins with named household figures — servants who are not merely stage furniture but individuals with names, grievances, and perspectives that collectively constitute a chorus of the dependent class. Demetrius belongs to this nameless-named collective: a named entity in the cast list but a spectral presence on stage, one of "two or three [[Servants]]" (013-001) who enter with [[flavius|Flavius]] after Timon's bankruptcy, or one of the attendants who serve at the great banquet in Act I, Scene 2. His designation as a named character without individuation mirrors the play's broader concern with the relationship between personal identity and social station, between [[service]] and selfhood.

## Key Scenes

**The Household in High Fortune (Act I, Scene 2)**: During Timon's great banquet, the stage direction reads "a great banquet served in; [[flavius|Flavius]] and others attending" (004-109). The banquet is the play's fullest depiction of Timon's household functioning at its height of conspicuous [[bounty]]. Demetrius, as one of the attendants, would belong to this world of orderly service — the frame that sustains Timon's gift-giving and enables his performance of [[friendship]]. His presence, though unnamed in the scene, is the presence of the household itself: the invisible labour that makes generosity possible. (004-109)

**The Summons and the Crisis (Act III)**: As Timon's creditors converge upon his house, his servants — [[flavius|Flavius]], [[flaminius|Flaminius]], [[servilius|Servilius]] — are dispatched to various lords to request loans. Demetrius is not among the named emissaries as they survive in the text, but he is part of the household infrastructure that increasingly finds itself stranded between a master who refuses to acknowledge his [[debt]] and a world that has already withdrawn from him. The crisis is not merely Timon's; it is a household crisis, a crisis of all who depend upon him for their livelihood and social identity. (007-005–007-050)

**The Disbanding of the Household (Act IV, Scene 2)**: The stage direction describes "[[flavius|Flavius]], with two or three [[Servants]]" (013-001). These "two or three servants" represent the remnant of Timon's household staff, those who have not yet fled. Their exchange — "Are we undone? cast off? nothing remaining?" (013-004) — is the voice of dependency confronting its own dissolution. Demetrius may be among these unnamed figures; if so, his presence is the presence of those who served faithfully and now find that [[loyalty]] counts for nothing in a world where [[wealth]] is the only currency of social standing. The scene culminates in [[flavius|Flavius]] distributing his meagre savings: "The latest of my wealth I'll share amongst you. Wherever we shall meet, for Timon's sake, Let's yet be fellows" (013-018–013-020). This act of shared destitution is the household's final moment as a community before it atomizes into the [[misery]] of individual survival.

## Relationships

**[[timon|Timon]]**: The master-servant relationship between Timon and his named household attendants is a study in asymmetric dependence. Timon's servants depend upon him for their station; Timon depends upon them for the enactment of his [[bounty]]. Demetrius, as a named but voiceless figure, embodies the servant who exists only insofar as the master's world gives him a place — and who vanishes from the text when that world collapses. The [[trust]] that binds servant to master is a one-way bond in this play: the servants serve, but when the master falls, [[trust]] does not save any of them from destitution except those, like [[flavius|Flavius]], who choose to share their own poverty.

**[[flavius|Flavius]]**: The steward is the play's exemplary servant — the one figure whose [[service]] outlasts the conditions that gave it meaning. Demetrius and the other named servants exist in [[flavius|Flavius]]'s orbit; they populate the household he manages, and their fates are entangled with his. When [[flavius|Flavius]] shares his savings with the departing servants, he speaks on behalf of a fellowship that includes figures like Demetrius, figures who are named in the cast list but have no voice of their own.

**The Servant Collective**: The named servants of Timon's household — [[flavius|Flavius]], [[flaminius|Flaminius]], [[servilius|Servilius]], [[lucilius|Lucilius]], Demetrius, [[caphis|Caphis]], [[hortensius|Hortensius]], Philotus, Titus — constitute a collective presence that is unusual in Shakespearean tragedy. Their sheer number creates a social texture: Timon's house is not a private residence but a community, a microcosm of Athenian society. When that community disintegrates, the disintegration is not merely economic but social and moral. The named servants without lines — Demetrius among them — are the play's gesture toward the people whose lives are determined by forces they cannot control and whose stories the tragedy does not have space to tell.

## Notable Quotations

Demetrius has no surviving dialogue in the Folio text, but the words of [[flavius|Flavius]] to the household servants speak to his condition and the condition of all who served in Timon's house:

> Let me be recorded by the righteous gods, I am as poor as you.
>
> (013-004)

The anonymous servant speaks the collective fear that Demetrius shares:

> Such a house broke! So noble a master fall'n! All gone! and not one friend to take his fortune by the arm, And go along with him!
>
> (013-005–013-008)

[[flavius|Flavius]]'s parting words capture the fellowship that binds named and unnamed servants alike:

> Good fellows all, The latest of my wealth I'll share amongst you. Wherever we shall meet, for Timon's sake, Let's yet be fellows.
>
> (013-018–013-020)

## Character Analysis

Demetrius is a minor figure in every sense: a named servant with no dialogue, no distinct scene, no identifiable dramatic action. And yet his existence in the Dramatis Personae is a datum worth attending to. Shakespeare took the trouble to name him, even if he did not give him words. This act of naming-without-individuation is characteristic of _Timon of Athens_, a play that populates its margins with more named servants than any other Shakespearean tragedy and then systematically strips them of identity as the social world of the protagonist disintegrates (013-001–013-003).

The significance of named servants in this play has been read by critics as evidence of Shakespeare's interest in the social texture of dependency. The household is not a backdrop; it is a structure of relationships that Timon's [[bounty]] sustains and his ruin destroys. Figures like Demetrius, Philotus, Titus, and [[hortensius|Hortensius]] are the human infrastructure of aristocratic generosity — the people who carry the dishes, open the doors, and deliver the gifts that constitute Timon's performance of [[friendship]]. When the performance ends, they are the first casualties. Their disappearance from the play's second half mirrors the collapse of the social world that gave their service meaning.

The contrast with [[flavius|Flavius]] is instructive. Where [[flavius|Flavius]] is individuated by his [[loyalty]] and his capacity for moral action, Demetrius and the other unnamed-named servants are individuated only by their names — and those names survive as a ghostly trace of the community that Timon's fall destroyed. They are the casualties of tragedy who do not get a tragic death, only the slow erasure of being made irrelevant. In this sense, Demetrius is not merely a dramatic artefact but a representation of something the play understands deeply: that the collapse of a great man is never his alone, that the ripples of [[fortune]]'s wheel reach the ground floor, and that those who stand there — named but voiceless, present but peripheral — bear the weight of a tragedy not their own.
