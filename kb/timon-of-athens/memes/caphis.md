## Role

Caphis is a servant in the household of one of [[timon|Timon]]'s senators, dispatched to collect on his master's debts. He is among the first wave of [[debt|credit]]ors' servants who arrive at [[timon|Timon]]'s house, transforming the once-hospitable hall into a place of demands and recriminations. His role, though brief, embodies the reversal of [[timon|Timon]]'s [[wealth|fortune]]s: where once suitors came bearing gifts, now servants come bearing bills.

## Key Scenes

- **Act 4, Scene 3** — Caphis is summoned by his master with the call "Caphis, ho! Caphis, I say!" and instructed to demand payment from [[timon|Timon]]. He replies obediently: "Here, sir; what is your pleasure?" and "I go, sir." His brief dialogue captures the impersonal mechanism of debt collection — he is merely an instrument of his master's will.

## Relationships

- **His senator master**: Caphis serves without question, embodying the dutiful servant who executes orders regardless of their moral character. His obedience contrasts with [[hortensius|Hortensius]], who expresses reluctance at dunning [[timon|Timon]].
- **[[timon|Timon]]**: Though Caphis never interacts directly with [[timon|Timon]] in the surviving text, he is part of the apparatus of [[debt|credit]]ors that descends upon [[timon|Timon]]'s household, contributing to the atmosphere of siege that defines the middle acts.
- **[[hortensius|Hortensius]] and Titus**: Fellow servants of [[debt|credit]]ors whom Caphis meets at [[timon|Timon]]'s house. Together they form a chorus of ordinary men caught in the machinery of debt.

## Character Analysis

Caphis is less a character than a function — he represents the transformation of [[timon|Timon]]'s social world from a network of gift-giving to a system of debt collection. His name is called twice by his master with an urgency that mirrors the suddenness of [[timon|Timon]]'s reversal. Caphis himself has no personality beyond obedience; he is a blank instrument of economic necessity. This blankness is precisely the point: in a world governed by [[debt|credit]] and debt, individuals become mere carriers of financial [[debt|obligation]], their humanity subordinated to the ledger.
