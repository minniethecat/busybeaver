# The Water Banquet

## Context

The [[water-banquet|water banquet]] is [[timon|Timon]]'s final act in [[athens|Athens]] before his exile to [[the-woods|the wilderness]]. It comes after the catastrophic sequence of Act III in which [[timon|Timon]]'s financial collapse has become fully visible. His steward [[flavius|Flavius]] has been warning him for some time that his estate is consumed by [[debt]]; his [[debt|credit]]ors have sent servants to demand payment (Act III, Scene 4); and — most devastatingly — each of his false [[friendship|friends]] has been approached for assistance and has refused. [[lucullus|Lucullus]] offers a bribe to [[flaminius|Flaminius]] to say he wasn't home (Act III, Scene 1). [[lucius|Lucius]] pretends he wasn't asked first (Act III, Scene 2). [[sempronius|Sempronius]] claims offence that he wasn't approached before the others (Act III, Scene 3). The accumulation of these refusals is systematic: each friend has a different excuse but the result is the same. [[timon|Timon]] understands now that his [[gold|wealth]] purchased performance, not [[friendship]].

The water [[feast|banquet]] is [[timon|Timon]]'s response — a counter-performance that exposes the logic of the original [[feast|feasts]]. Where the first [[feast|banquet]] (Act I, Scene 2) was an expression of [[timon|Timon]]'s philosophy of gift, the water [[feast|banquet]] is the theatrical destruction of that philosophy. It is simultaneously the play's most comic and its most savage scene.

## Key Participants

- **[[timon|Timon]]**: The host, transformed from generous patron to theatrical avenger. His speech during [[timons-feast|the banquet]] is his formal renunciation of [[athens|Athens]] and humanity
- **The Athenian Lords** ([[lucius|Lucius]], [[lucullus|Lucullus]], [[sempronius|Sempronius]], and others): [[timon|Timon]]'s false [[friendship|friends]], assembled under the illusion that [[timon|Timon]]'s financial crisis was a test and that his invitation signals restored favour
- **[[flavius|Flavius]]**: [[flavius|Timon's steward]], who knows the truth of the situation and whose silent presence underscores the t[[anger|rage]]dy beneath the theatrical [[revenge]]

## Narrative Summary

[[timon|Timon]] sends invitations to his false [[friendship|friends]], announcing another [[feast]]. The lords, interpreting the invitation as evidence that [[timon|Timon]]'s financial troubles were feigned, eagerly attend — their greed overcoming whatever [[shame|embarrassment]] their recent refusals might have caused. The scene opens with the lords congratulating themselves on their perspicacity in "seeing through" [[timon|Timon]]'s supposed test (010-005). The irony is devastating: they believe [[timon|Timon]] was testing their [[friendship]]; in reality, he is about to test their greed one final time.

[[timon|Timon]] enters and delivers a prayer before the meal that is a [[curse]] disguised as a grace (010-044). He calls upon the [[gods]] to destroy the Athenian senators and the common people, and he declares of his guests: "as they are to me nothing, so in nothing bless them, and to nothing are they welcome." The prayer's form — it addresses the gods — is the last vestige of ceremony in [[timon|Timon]]'s world, and it is being weaponized.

The dishes are uncovered, revealing warm water and stones (010-044). [[timon|Timon]]'s accompanying speech is the play's definitive rejection of Athenian society: "You knot of mouth-[[friendship|friends]]! smoke and luke-warm water Is your perfection. This is [[timon|Timon]]'s last." He throws the water in the lords' faces, drives them from his house with curses, and ends with his formal renunciation: "Burn, house! sink, [[athens|Athens]]! henceforth hated be Of [[timon|Timon]] man and all humanity!" (010-047).

The scene ends with [[timon|Timon]] alone, having completed his transformation. The house that was the center of Athenian social life is now empty, and [[timon|Timon]] exits toward [[the-woods|the wilderness]], carrying nothing but his [[anger|rage]].

## Significance

The water [[feast|banquet]] is the hinge between the play's two halves — the point at which [[timon|Timon]]'s passive [[bounty|generosity]] becomes active [[revenge|vengeance]] and his [[trust|faith]] in human bonds becomes absolute [[misanthropy]]. It is the most self-consciously theatrical moment in a play deeply concerned with performance: [[timon|Timon]] stages a feast whose content reveals the emptiness that was always present in the form. The water and stones are not merely insults; they are a critique. What was served at [[timon|Timon]]'s feasts, the scene argues, was always empty ceremony — words of [[friendship]] with no substance behind them. The warm water makes the emptiness literal.

The scene is also Shakespeare's most concentrated statement on the economics of social performance. The lords attend because they believe there is something material to gain; [[timon|Timon]] hosts because he wants to expose the greed that underlies their attendance. Everyone is performing, but [[timon|Timon]]'s performance — unlike the lords' — is consciously theatrical. He has become the director of his own t[[anger|rage]]dy, staging his exit from Athenian society as a piece of theatre whose audience (the lords) are also its victims. The water [[feast|banquet]] does not change [[timon|Timon]]'s circumstances — he still loses his house, still goes into exile — but it gives him authorship over the manner of his loss, and that authorship is the only power left to him.

The water [[feast|banquet]] also establishes the structural pattern that the [[discovery-of-gold]] will later invert. At the water [[feast|banquet]], [[timon|Timon]] uses the form of the feast to attack its content (ceremony without substance). In [[the-woods|the wilderness]], he will use [[gold]] — the substance of [[bounty|generosity]] — to attack the form of human society itself. The progression from the water [[feast|banquet]] to the gold discovery traces [[timon|Timon]]'s journey from social critic to cosmic [[misanthropy|misanthrope]].


## Notable Quotations

The mock [[feast|banquet]] is [[timon|Timon]]'s revenge upon the false [[friendship|friends]]. He serves them covered dishes that, when revealed, contain only warm water and stones. His "grace" before the meal is a bitter parody:

> Live loathed and long, Most smiling, smooth, detested parasites, Courteous destroyers, affable wolves, meek bears, You fools of fortune, trencher-friends, time's flies, Cap and knee slaves, vapours, and minute-jacks! Of man and beast the infinite malady Crust you quite o'er!
>
> (010-029)

After the false [[friendship|friends]] depart in confusion, [[timon|Timon]]'s final word on the feast distils his newfound misanthropy:

> Burn, house! sink, Athens! henceforth hated be Of Timon man and all humanity!
>
> (010-062)
