## Overview

Law in *[[timon-of-athens|Timon of Athens]]* is an instrument of power rather than a guarantee of justice. The [[senate-house|Athenian Senate]], which serves as both legislative and judicial body, deploys the language of law to condemn [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]'s soldier friend to death and to threaten [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] himself with banishment. The play's treatment of law is consistently ironic: those who speak for "the law" are the same figures whose [[debt|usury]] and ingratitude the law ought to condemn. In [[the-woods|the wilderness]], [[timon|Timon]] inverts the legal order, exhorting nature and beasts to replace the corrupt laws of men.

## Key Passages

> "Right, if doing nothing be death by the law."
> — Apemantus, [001-101]

[[apemantus|Apemantus]]'s early quip reduces law to absurdity. If passivity is a capital crime, then the law is a mechanism for punishing the powerless — those who "do nothing" — while the active crimes of [[flattery|flatterers]] and usurers go unpunished.

> "Most true; the law shall bruise him."
> — Second Senator, [009-004]

The Senate's response to [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]'s plea is chilling in its brevity. "The law shall bruise him" treats legal violence as a matter of course, not justice. The verb "bruise" suggests that the law's purpose is not to adjudicate but to punish — and that the Senate sees itself as the law's instrument rather than its custodian.

> "For pity is the virtue of the law, And none but tyrants use it cruelly."
> — First Senator, [009-008]

This passage contains the play's deepest irony: the Senator who speaks of [[mercy|pity]] and mercy is the same man who has just condemned a soldier to death. The language of legal humanism is deployed to mask institutional cruelty. The Senators are, by their own definition, "tyrants."

> "We are for law: he dies; urge it no more, On pain of our displeasure."
> — First Senator, [009-020]

The Senate's final word on the soldier's case reduces legal process to an assertion of power. "We are for law" means "we are for death" — the identification of law with capital punishment strips the institution of any claim to impartiality or mercy.

> "Crack the lawyer's voice, That he may never more false title plead, Nor sound his quillets shrilly."
> — Timon, [013-047]

In his [[nature|wilderness]] curses, [[timon|Timon]] targets lawyers specifically. "Quillets" — legal quibbles, hair-splitting arguments — are, in [[timon|Timon]]'s view, the instruments by which the powerful maintain their grip. His curse on lawyers is a curse on law itself as practised in [[athens|Athens]].

## Character Perspectives

**The [[senate-house|Athenian Senate]]** embodies law as power. Their decisions — condemning the soldier, banishing [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] — are presented as legal acts, yet the play exposes them as exercises in political convenience. The Senators' [[debt|usury]] [009-027] reveals their hypocrisy most starkly: the men who sit in judgment are themselves practitioners of the economic exploitation that the law ought to regulate.

**[[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]** represents the challenge of justice to law. His plea for his soldier's life is a plea for mercy within the legal framework; when it fails, he turns to military force. [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]'s trajectory from petitioner to conqueror raises uncomfortable questions about whether justice can ever be achieved through legal channels when the law itself is corrupt.

**[[timon|Timon]]** moves beyond law to curse. In [[the-woods|the wilderness]], he rejects human law entirely, calling upon nature to replace it. His vision of a world without law — where "son and daughter" are confounded, where "maid" is made a "whore" [013] — is a kind of anti-legal utopia, the logical endpoint of his disillusionment with Athenian institutions.

## Critical Interpretations

The play's treatment of law reflects the Jacobean anxiety about the relationship between law and royal prerogative. The [[senate-house|Athenian Senate]], like James I's court, claims to speak for the law while exercising arbitrary power. The Senators' [[debt|usury]] — explicitly condemned in the play — aligns them with the [[gold|money]]-lenders and monopolists whose influence at court was a source of contemporary political tension.

[[timon|Timon]]'s [[nature|wilderness]] curses, with their systematic dismantling of every social institution, are an extended exercise in anti-legal rhetoric. By calling on nature to overthrow law, [[timon|Timon]] imagines a world outside the legal order — a world that, as the play's ending demonstrates, is indistinguishable from death. [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]'s final speech, promising to "make war breed peace" [017], gestures toward a restored legal order, but the play's bleakness leaves open the question of whether any law can be truly just.
