# The Page

**The Page** is a young servant who appears in [[Act 2]], Scene 2 of *Timon of Athens*, participating in a scene of comic banter with the [[Fool]] and [[Apemantus]]. Though his role is small, his exchanges illuminate the play's treatment of hierarchy, wit, and the relationship between masters and servants.

## Appearances and Function

The Page appears in a single sustained scene ([[TN 2.2.66-130]]), where he encounters the [[Fool]] on his way to attend to his masters' business. The scene unfolds as a series of verbal exchanges — first between the Page and the Fool, then involving Apemantus when he enters. The Page primarily serves as a foil and straight-man for the Fool's wordplay, though he is not without his own verbal resources.

As a dramatic figure, the Page represents the bottom rung of the Athenian household hierarchy — young, subordinate, tasked with carrying messages and performing minor errands. His presence fills out the social world of the play, reminding the audience that beneath the lords' banquets and Timon's largesse lies an extensive infrastructure of service.

## Dialogue with the Fool: Masters and Servants

The Page's exchange with the [[Fool]] is structured around the theme of service and its discontents. When the Fool asks the Page about his occupation, the Page's reply — "She is the better in her credit, and not in my purse" — initiates a running commentary on the economic realities of household labor ([[TN 2.2.68-69]]).

The Fool turns the conversation toward the state of "bawds" (pimps) in Athens, and the Page responds with a conventional expression of disdain. This allows the Fool to pivot to a more pointed observation:

> "I think no usurer but has a fool to his servant" ([[TN 2.2.97]])

This line — ostensibly a joke about fools serving usurers — carries a darker suggestion: that the entire Athenian economy, built on credit and debt, turns everyone into a fool or a servant or both. The Page, by his earnest responses, helps stage this satirical insight without articulating it himself.

## The Page and Apemantus: Wit and Power

When [[Apemantus]] enters and greets the Page, the interaction exposes the dynamics of power and wit. Apemantus asks:

> "Would thou hadst been born to have been hanged" ([[TN 2.2.89]])

The Page responds dutifully, asking if Apemantus would accompany him to his lords. Apemantus's reply — "Away, you green-sallet" — dismisses the Page with a term meaning an inexperienced youth ([[TN 2.2.91]]).

This brief exchange is revealing. Apemantus, the professional cynic who claims to despise wealth, status, and flattery, treats the Page — the least powerful figure in the scene — with casual contempt. The philosopher who berates lords for their moral failings has no kindness for a servant boy. This detail complicates Apemantus's position: his critique of Athenian society is genuine, but his manner toward the powerless suggests that his misanthropy, like Timon's later, has its own element of cruelty.

The Page, for his part, does not respond with wit or defiance. He accepts the dismissal and exits. Unlike the Fool, who has the professional privilege of speaking freely, the Page knows his place and keeps to it. His silence in the face of Apemantus's rudeness speaks to the constraints of his role.

## The Page as Role, Not Person

That the character is called simply "the Page" — a job title rather than a name — is thematically significant. In the world of the play, servants are defined by function rather than identity. [[Flavius]] is an exception: Timon's steward is named because his loyalty and interior life matter to the drama. The Page, by contrast, is interchangeable — any young male servant would fill the same dramatic niche.

This anonymity connects the Page to other unnamed figures in the play: the [[Three Strangers]] (identified by their function as moral witnesses), the [[Soldiers]] (defined by their military role), the various servants of creditors. Shakespeare populates the margins of *Timon of Athens* with figures who lack names but who collectively constitute the social infrastructure against which the named characters act out their dramas of wealth, ingratitude, and collapse.

## Youth and the Household Hierarchy

The Page's youth is his defining characteristic within the servant world. He is contrasted implicitly with:

- **[[Flavius]]**, the loyal steward — mature, trusted, capable of independent moral judgment
- **The creditors' servants** — older and more hardened, sent to demand payment rather than carry polite messages
- **The [[Fool]]** — also a dependant, but one with the professional license to mock and subvert

Within this spectrum, the Page represents the beginning of a servant's life — obedient, unformed, not yet disillusioned. He has not seen what Flavius sees: the systematic corruption of the Athenian elite and its consequences for those who serve them. His innocence, such as it is, makes him a suitable foil for the Fool's cynical wisdom.

## Comic Relief and Thematic Advancement

The scene involving the Page and the Fool provides comic relief after the tension of Timon's creditors confronting him and before the darker developments of Acts 3 and 4. However, the comedy is not merely diversion. The Fool's wordplay, facilitated by the Page's earnest responses, consistently circles back to the play's central concerns: the collapse of value, the emptiness of social obligation, and the economic underpinnings of all human relationships in Athens.

The Page thus participates in what might be called **thematic ventilation** — moments in which the play's serious ideas are aired in a lighter register, allowing the audience to absorb them through laughter rather than direct statement. This technique is a hallmark of Shakespeare's mature dramaturgy, and the Page's scene with the Fool is one of its clearest instances in *Timon*.

## Contrast with the Fool

The relationship between the Page and the [[Fool]] in this scene illuminates both characters. The Fool is professionally licensed to speak uncomfortable truths — he is the one character who can say what he thinks without social penalty. The Page, by contrast, has no such license. He is a literal servant, bound to deference and obedience.

Yet the Fool's wisdom depends on having someone to play against, and the Page serves this function admirably. His straight-man responses — earnest, slightly bewildered, conventional — provide the surface against which the Fool's wit can reflect. Without the Page, the Fool would be reduced to soliloquy; with him, the comedy becomes dialogue, and the thematic work is accomplished through interaction rather than declamation.

## Thematic Significance

The Page contributes to several of the play's major thematic currents:

**Service and Hierarchy.** The Page embodies the reality that Athenian society rests on a vast structure of service. Lords depend on servants, who in turn depend on lords, who depend on Timon, who depends on credit. The Page is the bottom of this chain — as far down as the play's vision extends within the city's households.

**Youth and Corruption.** As a young figure, the Page represents what might be — the possibility of a different kind of service, a different relationship between powerful and powerless. His presence invites the audience to consider whether Athens's corruption is innate or learned, whether the next generation of servants will simply replicate the patterns of the present.

**Speech and Silence.** The Page speaks when addressed and falls silent when dismissed. His verbal restraint contrasts with the extravagant speech of the lords and the bitter eloquence of Timon and Apemantus. In a play obsessed with language — its power, its emptiness, its capacity for betrayal — the Page's economy of words is itself a kind of commentary.
