# The Feast in Timon of Athens

## Overview

The feast is the master metaphor of *[[timon-of-athens|Timon of Athens]]*, the ritual form through which the play's social order is both celebrated and destroyed. Shakespeare structures the play around three banquets: the magnificent feast of Act I where [[timon|Timon]] distributes gifts with prodigal abundance; the second banquet of Act III where [[timon|Timon]] serves lukewarm water and stones in a theatrical reversal of hospitality; and the implicit anti-feast of [[the-woods|the wilderness]], where [[timon|Timon]] digs for roots and discovers gold — a banquet of the earth that mocks the social feasts of [[athens|Athens]]. The feast tradition carried rich cultural associations in Shakespeare's England — from the Christian Eucharist to the Roman convivium to the aristocratic banquet as display of [[gold|wealth]] and status. [[timon|Timon]]'s feasts draw on all these resonances, transforming the meal from a symbol of communion into a symbol of betrayal, and finally into a weapon of revenge.

## Key Passages

> Thou art going to Lord Timon's feast? (001-152)

The [[second-lord|Second Lord]]'s casual question establishes the feast as the gravitational center of Athenian social life. Attendance at [[timon|Timon]]'s feast is not optional — it is the mechanism through which status is conferred, alliances are maintained, and [[gold|wealth]] is redistributed. The question, asked in passing, reveals a world where social identity is constituted by proximity to [[timon|Timon]]'s table. That the same lords who eagerly attend the feast will later refuse [[timon|Timon]] a loan is the play's central dramatic irony.

> They only now come but to feast thine eyes. (002-040)

Cupid's speech, introducing the masque of the five senses, crystallizes the feast's transformation from physical consumption to symbolic display. The senses — ear, taste, touch, smell — have been satisfied by [[timon|Timon]]'s bounty; only the eyes remain to be "feasted" by the spectacle of the masque. This progressive dematerialization of the feast (from food to performance) mirrors [[timon|Timon]]'s progressive detachment from economic reality. He is feasting others' eyes while his own substance is being consumed.

> The place which I have feasted, does it now, Like all mankind, show me an iron heart? (008-053)

[[timon|Timon]]'s address to his own house — now locked against him — personifies the feast's architecture as a moral agent. The house that "feasted" has become an "iron heart," and its transformation mirrors that of the [[friendship|friends]] who dined within it. The house, unlike the [[friendship|friends]], is innocent — it is only stone and timber — but [[timon|Timon]]'s rhetoric conflates physical structure with moral character, suggesting that the very space of feasting has been corrupted by what occurred within it.

> Go, bid all my friends again, Lucius, Lucullus, and Sempronius: All, sirrah, all: I'll once more feast the rascals. (008-078)

[[timon|Timon]]'s decision to host a second banquet marks the turning point from passive [[misery|suffering]] to active revenge. The word "feast" has changed its meaning: no longer an act of [[bounty|generosity]], it becomes an act of punishment. The epithet "rascals" announces [[timon|Timon]]'s new perspective on his former [[friendship|friends]], and the imperative "I'll once more feast" carries the menace of a threat. The feast, which began as the medium of [[timon|Timon]]'s social identity, now becomes the instrument of his revenge.

## Character Perspectives

**[[timon|Timon]]** is defined by the feast — as host, as benefactor, and finally as avenger. His identity is constructed at [[timons-feast|the banquet]] table, where his [[bounty|generosity]] can be publicly performed and acknowledged. The first banquet establishes [[timon|Timon]] as the ideal host, giving without calculation; the second banquet reveals him as a host who has learned to weaponize hospitality. [[timon|Timon]]'s trajectory from feast-giver to feast-destroyer mirrors his trajectory from philanthropist to misanthrope — both are transformations of the same fundamental impulse, the need to give, redirected from love to [[hate|hatred]].

**[[apemantus|Apemantus]]** is the feast's uninvited critic, the guest who refuses to be a guest. He attends [[timon|Timon]]'s banquet only to denounce it, eating roots and drinking water while the lords consume [[timon|Timon]]'s bounty. His grace before the meal — a prayer for distrust of [[friendship|friends]] — functions as a counter-ritual within the ritual, exposing the feast's pretensions even as the feast proceeds around him. [[apemantus|Apemantus]] is the feast's bad conscience, and his presence ensures that the audience can never fully enjoy the spectacle of [[timon|Timon]]'s [[bounty|generosity]].

**The Masque of the Five Senses** (Cupid and the Amazons) represents the feast at its most purely theatrical. The masque transforms consumption into spectacle, the satisfaction of appetite into the entertainment of the eye. That this performance is paid for by [[timon|Timon]] and received by guests who will later abandon him makes it a miniature of the play's larger critique: the feast is always a theatre of mutual deception, where hosts perform [[bounty|generosity]] and guests perform gratitude, and neither performance survives the withdrawal of material support.

## Critical Interpretations

Anthropological approaches to the play have emphasized the feast's function as a ritual of social cohesion and its violation as a ritual of social dissolution. In Marcel Mauss's terms, [[timon|Timon]]'s first feast is a classic potlatch — a competitive display of [[bounty|generosity]] that creates [[debt|obligation]]s of reciprocation. When the guests fail to reciprocate, they violate not merely a personal bond but a fundamental social contract, and the second banquet — the feast of water and stones — is [[timon|Timon]]'s ceremonial announcement that the contract is void.

Theological readings connect the play's feast imagery to the Eucharist and its parody. [[timon|Timon]]'s first banquet, with its language of grace, blessing, and communion, echoes the language of the Prayer Book; his second banquet, with its water and curses, inverts the Eucharistic pattern into what amounts to a Black Mass of misanthropy. The "stones" [[timon|Timon]] serves in the second banquet may allude to Matthew 7:9 ("what man is there of you, whom if his son ask bread, will he give him a stone?"), transforming a biblical image of paternal care into an image of anti-paternal revenge.

The feast in *[[timon-of-athens|Timon of Athens]]* also participates in a broader Shakespearean pattern of disrupted banquets that includes *Macbeth* (Banquo's ghost at the feast), *The Tempest* (Prospero's disappearing banquet), and *Titus Andronicus* (the cannibal feast). In each case, the meal that should affirm community becomes the site of its destruction. [[timon|Timon]]'s contribution to this pattern is uniquely economic: rather than a ghost or a magic trick, it is debt — simple, structural, inescapable debt — that destroys the feast, making *[[timon|Timon]]* the most materialist of Shakespeare's banquet plays.
