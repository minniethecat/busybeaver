# First Senator

## Role in the Play

The First Senator is the most politically significant of the play's anonymous authority figures. He appears in two crucial scenes: first as the presiding voice in [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]' banishment trial (Act 9/3.5), where he embodies the inflexibility of [[law|Athenian law]]; and later as the leader of the senatorial delegation to [[timon|Timon]] (Act 14/5.1), where he attempts to persuade the misanthrope to return and defend [[athens|Athens]] against [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]' army. The First Senator's trajectory — from rigid enforcer of law to desperate supplicant — mirrors the play's larger arc from [[trust|confidence]] to desperation, and his willingness to reverse his position when [[athens|Athens]] is threatened reveals the pragmatism beneath the Senate's moral posturing. (009-003)

## Key Scenes

**The Banishment Trial** (009-003): The First Senator presides over the Senate's judgment of [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]], who has pleaded for mercy on behalf of a friend condemned to death. The Senator's responses — "You undergo too strict a paradox" and "You cannot make gross sins look clear" — articulate a legal absolutism that refuses to consider circumstance or proportion. When [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] protests, the Senator pronounces the sentence of banishment with chilling finality: "He dies. / We are for law: he dies; urge it no more." (009-009)

**The Confrontation with [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]** (009-026): When [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] threatens the Senate, the First Senator's response — "Do you dare our anger? 'Tis in few words, but spacious in effect" — reveals the arrogance of institutional power. He cannot imagine that an individual might successfully challenge the Senate's authority. This [[trust|confidence]] proves misplaced when [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] returns at the head of an army. (009-028)

**The Delegation to [[timon|Timon]]** (014-048): In a reversal of [[wealth|fortune]], the First Senator leads the Athenian delegation to [[the-woods|the wilderness]] to beg [[timon|Timon]] to return and defend [[athens|the city]]. His language shifts from the imperative to the supplicatory: "O, forget / What we are sorry for ourselves in thee." The Senator who once declared "We are for law" now pleads for mercy, revealing the gap between the Senate's principles and its survival instinct. (014-055)

**The Final Appeal** (014-062): The First Senator's final attempt to persuade [[timon|Timon]] — invoking the Senate's authority to "give thee dignities" and "repair thy [[wealth|fortune]]s" — fails utterly. [[timon|Timon]]'s refusal exposes the hollowness of political power: the Senate that banished [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] cannot protect [[athens|Athens]], and the Senate that offers [[timon|Timon]] dignities cannot command his respect. (014-057)

## Relationships

**[[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]**: The First Senator is [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]' antagonist in the banishment scene. His rigid legalism — "We are for law" — sets in motion the military conflict that will ultimately threaten [[athens|Athens]]' survival. [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]' return as conqueror is a direct refutation of the Senator's belief in institutional invulnerability. (009-003)

**[[timon|Timon]]**: The First Senator's relationship to [[timon|Timon]] is entirely political. He had no role in [[timon|Timon]]'s fall, but when [[athens|Athens]] needs a military defender, he seeks [[timon|Timon]]'s help with the same transactional logic that governed [[timon|Timon]]'s false [[friendship|friends]]. [[timon|Timon]]'s refusal is his revenge not just on the lords but on the entire Athenian political establishment that the Senator represents. (014-055)

**The Senate**: The First Senator speaks for the institution of the Senate rather than for himself. His use of the plural "we" throughout — "We are for law," "We are sorry" — reflects his identity as a functionary whose moral agency is subsumed into his institutional role. (009-020)

## Character Analysis

The First Senator embodies the paradox of institutional authority in *[[timon-of-athens|Timon of Athens]]*: the Senate is simultaneously the source of law and the agent of in[[law|justice]]. The Senator's insistence that [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]' friend must die because "We are for law" is legally correct — the man is guilty — but it exposes a [[law|legal system]] that has no room for mercy, proportion, or circumstance. When the same Senate later begs [[timon|Timon]] for mercy, the contradiction is complete: an institution that cannot show mercy cannot expect to receive it. The First Senator's arc — from inflexible judge to desperate supplicant — is the political parallel to [[timon|Timon]]'s personal trajectory, and both demonstrate the same truth: absolute principles, whether of law or of [[friendship]], collapse when confronted with the reality of human need. (009-011)


## Notable Quotations

The First Senator articulates [[senate-house|the senate]]'s harsh philosophy of [[law|justice]], opposing mercy in favour of exemplary punishment:

> My lord, you have my voice to it; the fault's Bloody; 'tis necessary he should die: Nothing emboldens sin so much as mercy.
>
> (009-003)

His response to [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]]' plea distils a Stoic critique of revenge into a single aphorism:

> You cannot make gross sins look clear: To revenge is no valour, but to bear.
>
> (009-011)

When [[alcibiades|Alcibiades]] argues for his friend's life by citing military service, the Senator dismisses the appeal with chilling directness:

> He dies.
>
> (009-018)
