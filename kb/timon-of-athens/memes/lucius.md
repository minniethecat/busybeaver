# Lucius

## Role in the Play

Lucius is one of the three named false [[friendship|friends]] of [[timon|Timon]], appearing alongside [[lucullus|Lucullus]] and [[sempronius|Sempronius]]. The Dramatis Personae lists him as "a flattering lord," identifying his dramatic function before he speaks a word. Like the other false [[friendship|friends]], Lucius participates in [[timon|Timon]]'s feasts, receives his gifts, and refuses to help when [[timon|Timon]]'s [[wealth|fortune]] fails. His refusal is distinguished by its particular hypocrisy: when approached by [[lucilius|Timon's servant]]s, he claims he would have helped had he been asked first — a counterfactual [[bounty|generosity]] that costs him nothing and reveals the bad [[trust|faith]] at the heart of Athenian elite society. (P01-002)

## Key Scenes

**The Banquet Attendance** (002-069): Lucius sends word to [[timon|Timon]]'s feast that he "hath presented [[timon|Timon]] with four milk-white horses, trapped in silver" — an ostentatious gift that publicly performs [[friendship]] while privately serving as an investment in [[timon|Timon]]'s continued favour. The gift's extravagance mirrors [[timon|Timon]]'s own excess and demonstrates how the patronage system escalates rather than authenticates [[bounty|generosity]]. (002-069)

**The Refusal** (006-011): When [[lucilius|Timon's servant]] approaches Lucius for a loan, Lucius's response reveals the logic of the false friend: he claims he is "sorry" and "had no notice," insisting that had [[timon|Timon]] asked him first, "I would have put my [[gold|wealth]] into donation." This counterfactual [[bounty|generosity]] is cost-free — he can claim virtue while practising selfishness. The Strangers who witness this exchange serve as a Greek chorus, confirming for the audience what Lucius will not admit. (006-011)

**The Meeting of Servants** (008-002): Lucius's servant meets with the servants of other [[debt|credit]]ors, and their conversation — "What, do we meet together?" — reveals the coordinated nature of the lords' refusal. The scene transforms individual moral failings into a collective social phenomenon: everyone's master has refused [[timon|Timon]], and everyone's servant is embarrassed by it. (008-005)

## Relationships

**[[timon|Timon]]**: Lucius has been the recipient of [[timon|Timon]]'s [[bounty|generosity]] — his gift of horses was reciprocated with lavish [[feast|hospitality]] — yet his [[service|loyalty]] evaporates with [[timon|Timon]]'s [[gold|wealth]]. His claim that he "would have put my [[gold|wealth]] into donation" is the most transparently hypocritical of all the refusals. (002-069)

**[[lucullus|Lucullus]]** and **[[sempronius|Sempronius]]**: Together with [[lucullus|Lucullus]] and [[sempronius|Sempronius]], Lucius forms the trio of named false [[friendship|friends]]. Each refusal is a variation on the same theme: [[lucullus|Lucullus]] is openly contemptuous, Lucius is hypocritically regretful, and [[sempronius|Sempronius]] is self-righteously offended. (008-078)

**[[flavius|Flavius]]**: The steward's aside — "Lord Lucius and [[lucullus|Lucullus]]? hum!" — when [[timon|Timon]] sends to these lords for help is the play's first signal that their [[friendship]] is counterfeit. [[flavius|Flavius]] knows what [[timon|Timon]] cannot see: the lords' affection is contingent on [[timon|Timon]]'s [[gold|wealth]]. (004-099)

## Character Analysis

Lucius is the most insidious of the false [[friendship|friends]] because his hypocrisy is dressed as regret. Unlike [[lucullus|Lucullus]], who is openly contemptuous of [[timon|Timon]]'s request, Lucius performs concern — he is "sorry," he "would have helped," he was simply not asked in time. This performance of virtue is more damaging than open refusal because it preserves the fiction of [[friendship]] while practising the reality of abandonment. Lucius embodies the play's critique of language itself: words like "friend," "gift," and "honour" have become currencies in a social economy that has emptied them of meaning. When Lucius says he would have given, he is not lying — he is participating in a linguistic system in which statements of [[bounty|generosity]] are themselves the only [[bounty|generosity]] required. (006-011)


## Notable Quotations

Lucius's refusal speech reveals the self-serving absurdity of the false friend — he laments his own bad timing in spending [[gold|money]] rather than the moral failure of refusing a friend in need:

> What a wicked beast was I to disfurnish myself against such a good time, when I might ha' shown myself honourable! how unluckily it happened, that I should purchase the day before for a little part, and undo a great deal of honoured! Servilius, now, before the gods, I am not able to do—the more beast, I say:—I was sending to use Lord Timon myself, these gentlemen can witness! but I would not, for the wealth of Athens, I had done't now. Commend me bountifully to his good lordship.
>
> (006-020)

The First Stranger's commentary on Lucius's refusal distils the play's moral lesson:

> Why, this is the world's soul; and just of the same piece Is every flatterer's spirit. Who can call him His friend that dips in the same dish? for, in My knowing, Timon has been this lord's father, And kept his credit with his purse, Supported his estate; nay, Timon's money Has paid his men their wages.
>
> (006-025)
