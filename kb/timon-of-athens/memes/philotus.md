## Role

Philotus is a servant of one of [[timon|Timon]]'s creditors who appears in Act 3, Scene 4 outside [[timon|Timon]]'s house, joining a growing assembly of fellow creditor-servants. He is notably addressed as "Sir Philotus" [008-009] by [[lucilius|Lucilius]]'s servant, an honorific that implies a degree of status not accorded to the other servants in the scene. Philotus arrives late to the gathering — the others are already in conversation when the stage direction announces "*Enter Philotus*" [008-008]. His role, though limited to a handful of lines, contributes to the cumulative portrait of [[timon|Timon]]'s besieged household: where once the hall was thronged with petitioners seeking favour, it is now crowded with collectors demanding [[debt|payment]].

## Key Scenes

- **Act 3, Scene 4** — Philotus enters to find the other creditors' servants assembled outside [[timon|Timon]]'s house. Welcomed as "Sir Philotus" by [[lucilius|Lucilius]]'s servant, he reveals he has been "Labouring for nine" [008-012] — working to collect debts since nine in the morning. He asks whether [[timon|Timon]] has been seen yet and, upon hearing he has not, remarks "I wonder on't; he was wont to shine at seven" [008-016], recalling [[timon|Timon]]'s former punctuality and public accessibility. Philotus later concurs with [[hortensius|Hortensius]]'s cynical assessment of [[timon|Timon]]'s fallen fortunes: "I am of your fear for that" [008-018]. At the climactic moment when [[timon|Timon]] bursts out in [[anger|rage]] and the servants present their bills one by one, Philotus delivers the final line: "All our bills" [008-059], a collective gesture encompassing every demand against the fallen lord.

## Relationships

- **[[timon|Timon]]**: Philotus's relationship to [[timon|Timon]] is purely transactional — he is the bearer of his master's financial demand. Yet his remark that [[timon|Timon]] "was wont to shine at seven" [008-016] carries a faint note of wonder at the change in the great man, suggesting that even a mere creditor's servant registers the magnitude of the fall.
- **[[titus|Titus]], [[hortensius|Hortensius]], and [[caphis|Caphis]]**: Fellow creditor-servants with whom Philotus forms a collective presence. Together they constitute a chorus of ordinary men executing the extraordinary ingratitude of their masters. Philotus's late entrance and the honorific "Sir" set him slightly apart, but his function is identical: to present "all our bills" [008-059].
- **[[lucilius|Lucilius]]'s Servant**: The servant who greets Philotus by name and with the respectful "Sir," engaging him in the scene's running commentary on [[timon|Timon]]'s decline. Their exchange serves to fill out the scene's atmosphere of weary waiting and cynical observation.

## Character Analysis

Philotus operates at the intersection of two of the play's central concerns: [[debt|debt]] and [[service]]. As a servant executing his master's financial orders, he is a cog in the machinery of credit that destroys [[timon|Timon]] — yet as "Sir Philotus" he carries a faint residue of individual dignity, a suggestion that even within a system of impersonal obligation, persons retain at least the outward marks of standing. His late arrival at the scene and the greeting he receives indicate that he is a known and respected figure among the creditor-servants, however modest his dramatic function.

The line "Labouring for nine" [008-012] is Philotus's most revealing. In three words it conjures an entire morning of door-knocking, bill-presenting, and deferred waiting — the hidden labour of the credit economy. Philotus has been at this work since nine o'clock, and it is now the hour when [[timon|Timon]] used to "shine at seven." The temporal gap between [[timon|Timon]]'s former punctual radiance and his present absence is the measure of his fall, and Philotus is the clock by which that fall is reckoned. His final line, "All our bills" [008-059], spoken as the other servants have each presented their individual demands, telescopes the particular into the universal: the debts are not one master's or another's but a general siege. Philotus thus functions as both participant in and emblem of the collective action against [[timon|Timon]] — a single servant whose name is remembered, but whose purpose is indistinguishable from the system he serves.
