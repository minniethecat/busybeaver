## Overview

Mrs. Austin, known universally as **Dodo** among her circle of friends, is an executive secretary of an important [[the-american-west|California]] commission and a core member of the group of professional women who gather around [[helen-davies|Helen Davies]] in [[san-francisco|San Francisco]]. Cynical yet devoted, divorced yet clear-eyed about relationships, she represents the generation of career women navigating the tensions between professional [[economic-ambition|ambition]] and personal fulfillment in early twentieth-century America.

## Role in the Narrative

Dodo appears primarily in the latter chapters of the novel, as part of the women's circle that meets at [[helen-davies|Helen]]'s cottage on Russian Hill. She is introduced during a beach excursion where the women's personalities and views are sketched in quick, vivid strokes.

> Anne, high-bred, imperious, with something of untamed freedom in every gesture—Anne was a teacher of economics! Beside her Willetta, demure, brown-eyed, brown-haired, knitting busily, had come from unknown labors in social service work. Across the aisle Sara and Mrs. Austin—they called her Dodo—were discussing samples of silk. And Sara was a miniature painter, Dodo executive secretary of an important California commission. (024-3133)

She engages in spirited arguments with [[anne-lester|Anne Lester]] over labor unions and political questions, her "calm, unruffled brow" and "serious lips" contrasting with Anne's passionate intensity (024-3139).

## Character and Views

### Professional Life

As executive secretary of an important California commission, Dodo occupied a position of genuine responsibility unusual for women of her era. Her professional identity was central to her character — she was not merely employed but held a role that required administrative judgment and political acumen. This professional standing informed her sharp, pragmatic outlook on the social questions the women's circle debated.

The commission's work touched on the pressing labor and economic issues of the day, giving Dodo firsthand exposure to the structural forces that shaped working-class life. This experience lent weight to her interventions in the circle's discussions: when Anne Lester spoke passionately about labor unions and strikes, Dodo could respond from a position of institutional knowledge rather than mere opinion. Their arguments — "Oh, you don't know what you're—" "If you'd read the reports of your own commission!" — reveal the tension between academic conviction and administrative experience (024-3139). The calm, unruffled composure with which Dodo made her points reflected years of navigating the male-dominated spaces of government bureaucracy.

### Cynicism and Experience

Dodo's cynicism is noted as a defining trait, one whose origin [[helen-davies|Helen]] comes to understand:

> She learned the tragedy that had shattered Willetta's home, and the reason for the cynicism that sometimes sharpened Dodo's tongue. (024-3157)

This cynicism is rooted in personal history — an "eight-years'-old divorce" that colors her perspective on [[marriage-and-courtship|marriage]] and relationships (024-3227). When the younger women discuss romantic ideals, Dodo speaks with the authority of hard-won experience.

### On Marriage and Money

Dodo articulates one of the novel's key social critiques — the economic dimension of modern [[marriage-and-courtship|marriage]]:

> "It's the money that's at the root of it. You don't want to marry a man you'll have to support — not that you'd mind doing it, but his self-respect would go all to pieces if you did. And yet you can't find a man who makes as much money as you do, who cares about music and poetry and things. I'm putting money in the bank and reading Masefield. I don't see why a man can't. But somehow I've never run across a man who does." (024-3215)

### On the Limitations of Career Women

Dodo is also the voice of sober self-assessment within the group, puncturing their self-congratulation with a broader view of American [[gender-roles|womanhood]]:

> "We're freaks. We don't represent the mass of women. We go around and around in our little circles and think we're modern women because we make a lot of noise. But we aren't. We're of no importance at all, with our charity boards and our social surveys and our offices. It's the girls who marry in their teens — millions of 'em, in millions of the little homes all over America — that really count." (024-3249)

## The War and Departure

As the narrative approaches its close and America moves toward entering World War I, Dodo makes a characteristically decisive move — relocating to Washington, D.C., to serve in the Department of Labor:

> "I've been thinking about it for some time. My assistants can keep things going here till I can arrange to put in someone else. I don't know whether this country's going into the war or not, but if it does, I want to be in the heart of things. I'd be no good in France, but I can do something in our own Department of Labor." (024-3279)

Her departure, along with Marian's journey to France and [[helen-davies|Helen]]'s own departure, marks the dissolution of the women's circle:

> "The old crowd's breaking up," they said. "Marian in France, and Dodo in Washington, and now Helen's going. Nothing's going to be the same any more." (024-3413)

## Relationships

Dodo is part of the tight-knit group of women that includes [[anne-lester|Anne Lester]], [[marian-marcy|Marian Marcy]], [[willetta|Willetta]], and [[sara|Sara]]. The circle gathered regularly at Helen's cottage on Russian Hill, where the crackling fire might blaze upon them all, gathered by chance, chattering like schoolgirls while Marian speared marshmallows with a hatpin (024-3159). Dodo's role in these gatherings was distinctive: where Anne brought passionate argument about labor and economics, and Sara brought artistic sensibility, Dodo brought the hard-won pragmatism of a woman who had already navigated a failed marriage and built a career.

She extends warmth to [[helen-davies|Helen]] when the newcomer joins their circle, in a moment of unguarded affection that cuts through the intellectual banter:

> "You know," said Dodo suddenly, putting a warm hand over Helen's. "I like you." (024-3149)

This gesture — simple, direct, unsentimental — captures Dodo's essential quality: beneath the cynicism is a capacity for genuine connection, and beneath the professional composure is a woman who has learned that friendship among women who understand each other's struggles is one of the few reliable consolations in a world that offers women few enough of them.
