# Whiskey Row

The main commercial street of Coalinga during the oil-boom era, defined by its boardinghouses and its stark contrast to the desert emptiness beyond — a place where Helen Davies confronted the limits of her options.

## Description

Whiskey Row was the functional center of Coalinga, a small oil-boom town in the San Joaquin Valley. It contained "a few stores, already supplied with clerks, a couple of boardinghouses on Whiskey Row, and scores of pretty little houses in which obviously no servants were employed" (019-010). The name itself — "Whiskey Row" — evokes the rough, masculine character of the oil fields, where the town's primary lodging establishments catered to transient workers rather than families.

Beyond Whiskey Row, the landscape changed abruptly: "When she had passed Whiskey Row the road ran straight before her, a black line of oiled sand drawn to a vanishing-point on the level desert" (020-007). The street marked the boundary between the fragile civilization of the oil town and the vast emptiness of the San Joaquin desert.

## Significance

Whiskey Row represents the economic precarity Helen faced during her most desperate period. Arriving in Coalinga with no money for a telegram and no marketable skills beyond telegraphy — a field already filled — she found no work waiting for her here. The boardinghouses on Whiskey Row offered shelter but no solution. The "help wanted" advertisements in the local paper were for stenographers and cooks on the oil-leases, positions for which she was unqualified (019-010).

The street serves as a narrative marker of Helen's movement between desperation and determination. She passed Whiskey Row twice: once in defeat, scanning its storefronts for opportunities that did not exist, and again in grim perseverance, driving "quickly past Whiskey Row" on her way back from an exhausting land-sale expedition, "too tired to be troubled or pleased by anything" (021-004). Whiskey Row was never a destination — it was always a place she passed through.
