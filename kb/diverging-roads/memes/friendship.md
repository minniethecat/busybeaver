# Friendship

Friendship in *Diverging Roads* offers an alternative model of human connection — one that stands outside the gravitational pull of romance and the obligations of family. For [[helen-davies|Helen Davies]], who spends much of the novel navigating the competing claims of love, ambition, and duty, friendship provides something rarer: a relationship "demanding nothing, unconstrained, full of understanding" [021-071]. In a novel structured around the choices women make about love and marriage, friendship is the road that does not require choosing.

The novel traces Helen's evolution from isolation to community, charting how each friendship she forms — tentative, often complicated — builds toward the warm circle she eventually finds in San Francisco. This arc is itself an argument: that friendship is not a consolation prize for those who fail at love, but a distinct form of human fulfillment with its own rewards and demands.

## The Arc from Loneliness to Community

Helen arrives in San Francisco profoundly alone. Her first winter is marked by a solitude so complete that "the evenings were the worst" [007-040] — hours spent in her rented room with no one to talk to. Her first attempts at connection are fragile and often disappointing. [[louise|Louise Latimer]], with her "butterfly heart" [017-055], offers a rush of friendliness — "they must go out to some of the cafés together; they must have tea at Techau's" [017-055] — but the friendship proves shallow, a whirl of dancing and flirtation that leaves Helen emptier than before.

This early disappointment is instructive. Friendship in *Diverging Roads* is not simply a matter of meeting people; it requires finding people who can meet *her* — who can see past her guardedness into the person beneath. "The friendship of women was a new thing to her" [028-022], the narrator observes, and Helen approaches it with the same learning curve she brings to telegraphy and business. She must learn who is worth trusting.

## The San Francisco Circle

The novel's richest portrait of friendship emerges in the women's circle Helen discovers in San Francisco. Gathering at a quiet restaurant luncheon table, these women — [[willetta|Willetta]], [[dodo|Dodo]], [[marian-marcy|Marian]], [[anne-lester|Anne]], [[sara|Sara]], and others — "dropped in when they pleased, sure of finding a friend and sympathetic talk" [021-076]. Their bond is remarkable precisely because it makes no demands. "Any other evening in the week might see Willetta running up the steps, knitting in hand, to spend an hour curled among the cushions on the hearth or to depart blithely if Helen were busy" [021-077].

The group is diverse in temperament and opinion yet harmonious: "One would say they quarreled at the luncheon table where they met daily, flinging pointed epigrams and sharp retorts at each other, growing excited over most incongruous subjects — the war, poems, biology, hairdressers — arguing, laughing, teasing each other all in a breath. But their good humor never failed, and affection for each other burned like an unflickering candle flame in all their gusts of controversy" [021-045].

Within this circle, Helen is allowed the luxury of being known gradually. "She learned the tragedy that had shattered Willetta's home, and the reason for the cynicism that sometimes sharpened Dodo's tongue" [021-076]. The reciprocal vulnerability of friendship — "If they wondered about her own life they asked no questions" [021-076] — is both respect and invitation. They do not pry, but they make space for whatever she wishes to share.

## Friendship vs. Courtship

The novel draws a deliberate contrast between the conventions of courtship and the informality of friendship. Where courtship requires performance — "men loved women for being beautiful, gay, unexacting, sweet-tempered always" [011-004] — friendship permits authenticity. When Dodo puts "a warm hand over Helen's" and says simply, "I like you" [021-072], the gesture carries none of the calculation that marks romantic pursuit.

Yet the novel is careful not to position friendship as a replacement for love. "Any other evening in the week" belongs to the women, but "Sunday found Helen tacitly left to Paul" [021-077]. Friendship and romance coexist, each offering something the other cannot. This balance — neither hierarchical nor competitive — is one of the novel's subtlest arguments about what a full life requires.

The tension between these two modes of connection is dramatized in a telling scene: "His unexpected coming upon the whole group broke ever so slightly the charm of their companionship. She had felt the same thing in entering her office when all the salesmen were there. Some intangible current of sympathy was cut, an alien element introduced. One thought before speaking, as if to a stranger who did not perfectly comprehend the language" [029-005]. A man's presence, however welcome, changes the register of intimacy among women. This is not Paul's fault — it is a structural truth the novel observes without accusation.

## Professional Friendship

The novel also explores friendship in the context of work, through Helen's complex relationship with her employer, [[mr-clark|Mr. Clark]]. "Three years of working with him had brought her an understanding of this nervous, quick-witted, harassed man. There was comradeship between them, a sympathy tempered by wariness on both sides" [025-010]. Their bond is not quite friendship in the conventional sense — "Neither would have lost the slightest business advantage for the other" [025-010] — but it carries its own form of mutual respect. "A business interview between them was like a friendly bout between fencers" [025-010].

This professional comradeship represents yet another model of connection: one built on shared work rather than shared leisure, on competence rather than intimacy. It is friendship stripped of sentiment but not of care — and it matters in the novel because it shows that meaningful human connection is not confined to the domestic sphere.

## Friendship Across Difference

Helen's capacity for friendship extends beyond her immediate circle. She cultivates "her curious friendship with a tiny Chinese mother who lived in the Wong 'family house'" [021-083] and befriends "the hordes of ragged, adorable Spanish children who ran to her for cakes when she climbed the crazy stairs that were the streets of Telegraph Hill" [021-083]. These cross-cultural connections deepen the novel's portrait of friendship as a mode of encounter with the world — an openness to difference that romantic love, with its narrowing intensity, cannot sustain.

The "curious friendship" with the Chinese mother is particularly significant: it crosses barriers of language, culture, and class that the novel's romantic relationships never do. Friendship, in this light, appears as the most democratic of human bonds — available to anyone willing to extend it, unconstrained by the social conventions that govern courtship and marriage.

## Friendship as Freedom

For Helen, who has spent years navigating the demands of men — [[paul|Paul's]] quiet expectations, [[bert|Bert's]] volatile charm, the business world's condescension — friendship with women represents a new kind of freedom. "She felt now the charm of this companionship, demanding nothing, unconstrained, full of understanding. It was freedom, relaxation, without loneliness" [021-071]. The simile that follows — "Like a plant kept too long in constricting soil and now transplanted to friendlier earth, she felt stirring within her innumerable impulses reaching out for nourishment" [021-071] — suggests that friendship is not merely pleasant but essential, a condition for growth that romantic love alone cannot provide.

This freedom includes the liberty to be neither performer nor supplicant. In her romantic relationships, Helen is constantly performing — being "beautiful, gay, unexacting, sweet-tempered always" [011-004] because that is what men expect. Among her women friends, she can be tired, uncertain, ambitious, or sad without fear of forfeiting their regard. The women's circle is, in this sense, the novel's answer to the question it poses throughout: where, in a world that judges women by their surfaces, can a woman simply *be*?
