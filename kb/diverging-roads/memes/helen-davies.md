# Helen Davies

Helen Davies is the [[protagonist]] of *Diverging Roads*, a young woman whose journey from [[community-and-belonging|small-town]] [[masonville|Masonville]] restlessness to hard-won independence traces the novel's central themes: the conflict between [[economic-ambition|ambition]] and [[domesticity]], the courage required to choose an unconventional path, and the cost of freedom. As a [[telegraphy|telegraph operator]] navigating [[gender-roles|gender expectations]] in early 20th-century [[the-american-west|California]], Helen embodies the novel's title — every choice she makes is a diverging road, each leading irrevocably away from the others.

## Role in the Story

Helen is the novel's moral and emotional center. From the opening scene, where she stands in the doorway of Richardson's store, she is defined by her refusal to accept the life that [[masonville|Masonville]] offers:

> Helen Davies, pausing in the doorway of Richardson's store on a warm spring afternoon, said to herself that she would be glad never to see Masonville again. The familiar sight of its one drowsy street, the rickety wooden awnings over the sidewalks, the boys pitching horseshoes in the shade of the blacksmith-shop, was almost insupportable. (001-001)

Her arc follows her through [[telegraphy|telegraph]]y school, professional work, [[marriage-and-courtship|courtship]] with [[paul]] and [[bert]], and the gradual realization that the life she wants may not fit any of the available templates. The central tension of her character — between [[economic-ambition|ambition]] and the desire to be loved — is crystallized early:

> She did not want to work. She wanted to be soft and pretty, tempting and teasing and sweet. She wanted to win the things she desired by tears and smiles and coaxing. But she did not know how. (001-006)

Her rejection of the domestic destiny mapped out for her is visceral:

> She did not want to stand there looking at it. She did not want to follow the old stale road home to the old farmhouse, which had not changed since she could remember. She felt that she should be doing something, she did not know what. (001-003)

## Biography

Helen grows up on a farm outside [[masonville|Masonville]], in a [[domesticity|household]] that has "not [[change-and-progress|change]]d since she could remember" (001-003). She graduates from the local school alongside [[paul|Paul]], a [[mining|mine]]r's son with whom she shares dreams of escape. She learns [[telegraphy|telegraph]]y, first as a way to be near [[paul|Paul]], then as a genuine professional identity.

A killing frost that threatens the family farm becomes Helen's call to action. Confronting her parents' weary resignation, she feels a transformative surge of purpose:

> "Well, anyway, I'm through school now, and I'll be doing something," Helen said. She had no clear idea what it would be, but suddenly she felt in her youth and happiness a strength that her discouraged father and mother did not have. For the first time they seemed to her old and worn, exhausted by an unequal struggle, and she felt that she could take them up in her arms and carry them triumphantly to comfort and peace. (003-012)

Her father's bitter realism grates against her youthful confidence:

> "You're right it ought to," her father replied bitterly. "A good crop never brings 'em." (003-010)

She moves to Sacramento to attend [[telegraphy]] school, where she endures isolation, inadequate conditions, and the disorienting strangeness of city life. Her perseverance is fueled by visions of a shared future with [[paul|Paul]]. Later she works at the St. Francis hotel and becomes a [[economic-ambition|success]]ful businesswoman, negotiating contracts that take her to [[san-francisco|San Francisco]] (018-013).

Her romantic life follows diverging paths: [[paul|Paul]]'s steady, railroad-bound future offers security; [[bert|Bert's]] speculative excitement promises risk and reward. Her choice between them is also a choice about what kind of woman she will be — and whether the very question can be answered within the frameworks available to her.

## Character Analysis

### Ambition and Gender

Helen's central struggle is against the constraints placed on women of her time. She wants professional achievement in a world that sees female labor as either temporary or shameful. Her fierce insistence is a recurring motif:

> "I don't see why!" she flashed. "A girl like me has to work if she's going to get anywhere. I bet I could do as well as a boy if I had a chance." (001-007)

The novel registers both the force and the fragility of this claim:

> Paul looked at her admiringly. He said, "I guess you could, all right. You're pretty smart for a girl." She glowed with pleasure. (001-007)

She never fully resolves the tension between her professional drive and her longing for the comforts of conventional [[domesticity]].

### The Mirror Scene

One of the novel's most revealing moments occurs when Helen, still a schoolgirl, examines her reflection after [[education-and-advancement|learning]] that [[paul|Paul]] has spoken of her with grudging admiration:

> The flutter at her heart had stopped then. No, she was not pretty. Her features were too large, her forehead too high. She despised the face that looked back at her. She longed for tiny, pretty features, large brown eyes, a low forehead with curling hair. The eyes in the mirror were gray and the hair was straight and brown. Not even a pretty, light brown. It was almost black. For the first time she had desperately wanted to be pretty. (001-009)

This moment establishes the central contradiction Helen will navigate throughout the novel: the desire to be valued for her competence and the simultaneous, deeply felt need to be desired. It is a conflict the narrative never fully resolves, granting her character a complexity unusual in early 20th-century fiction.

### Youth and Responsibility

The frost episode (Chapter III) marks Helen's transition from daughter to actor. Her recognition of her parents' exhaustion — and her decision to carry them "triumphantly to comfort and peace" (003-012) — reconfigures her [[economic-ambition|ambition]] as something not merely selfish but protective. This sense of responsibility becomes a through-line, complicating her later choices between independence and family obligation.

The scene of her departure for [[helens-first-job|telegraphy school]] reveals the cost of this ambition in familial terms:

> There was a flurry on the last morning. Between tears and excitement, Mabel was half hysterical, Tommy kept getting in the way, her mother unpacked the bag a dozen times to be sure that nothing was left out. They all drove to town, crowded into the two-seated light wagon, and there was another flurry at the station when the train came in. She hugged them all awkwardly, smiling with tears in her eyes. She felt for the first time how much she loved them. (003-045)

Her mother's parting words carry a shadow of warning as well as care:

> "There's something I got to say to you, Helen," she said, keeping her eyes on the waist she was ironing and flushing hotly. "Your father's still against this idea of your going away. He says first thing we know we'll have you back on our hands, in trouble." (003-043)

### The Professional Woman

Helen's transformation from telegraphy student to businesswoman is compressed but decisive. By the novel's later chapters, she negotiates land sales, manages professional relationships, and moves through the world with a competence that would have seemed impossible to the girl who stood in Richardson's doorway. The scene in the *Pacific Coast* magazine office captures her evolved self-conception:

> "But towns have characters, like people. Every town in California is full of stories, atmosphere, romance, color. Why, you couldn't write the character of one of them without interesting every reader of your magazine!" (020-019)
