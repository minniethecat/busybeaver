## Role

Mrs. Davies is the mother of [[helen-davies|Helen Davies]], the novel's protagonist. She lives in Masonville and represents the familial ties that Helen leaves behind when she departs for San Francisco and later San Jose [007-021]. Though she appears only at the margins of the narrative, Mrs. Davies serves an important structural function: she is the gravitational center of the home Helen has left, the embodiment of the conventional life against which Helen's independence is measured.

## Biography

Little is directly revealed about Mrs. Davies beyond her relationship to Helen. She and Mr. Davies raised Helen in Masonville, where Helen grew up alongside [[paul|Paul]] [001-002]. The Davies household represents a stable, small-town life of limited economic means but intact family bonds. When Helen leaves for San Francisco, she boards with [[mrs-campbell|Mrs. Campbell]], and Mrs. Davies remains at home in Masonville.

During difficult moments, Helen considers that she could return to her mother — "she could get more money from her mother and go home to stay" [007-021] — but the thought of retreating to dependency drives her to persevere. This moment is crucial for understanding Mrs. Davies's narrative function: she is not a character who acts but a condition to which Helen might return. The option of going home to mother is always present, and Helen's repeated refusal of that option is the measure of her commitment to independence.

At Christmas, Helen sends gifts home: "a length of black silk and a ten-dollar gold piece for her mother" [014-1426]. The detail is small but revealing. A length of black silk suggests a woman of modest tastes and practical needs; the gold piece represents Helen's pride in being able to contribute financially to the household she has left. The gifts she sends — socks, ties, a shaving cup for her father; silk and gold for her mother; hair ribbons for Mabel; a knife and pocketbook for Tommy — sketch the outlines of the family she has left behind, a family whose material needs are modest and whose emotional bonds are sustained across distance by small gestures.

## Narrative Function

### The Pull of Home

Mrs. Davies embodies what the novel calls "the pull of home" — the gravitational force that draws Helen back toward the familiar, the safe, the conventional. When Helen faces hardship in the city, the option of returning to her mother is both a comfort and a temptation:

> She could get more money from her mother and go home to stay. (007-021)

The novel makes clear that this is a real option, not an illusion. Helen *could* go home. Her mother would take her back. The fact that she does not — that she consistently chooses hardship and independence over the safety of home — is what defines her character. Mrs. Davies, by embodying the always-available alternative, makes Helen's choice meaningful.

### The Unseen Life

Mrs. Davies's near-invisibility in the narrative is itself significant. The novel is told almost entirely from Helen's perspective; what happens in Masonville after Helen leaves is glimpsed only through letters, gifts, and brief returns. Mrs. Davies exists in the novel's negative space — the life Helen is not living, the world she has left behind. This narrative choice reinforces the novel's thematic focus on departure and independence: the mother is not a character to be explored but a condition to be transcended.

### Mothers and Daughters

Mrs. Davies belongs to a small but significant group of mothers in the novel. [[mrs-campbell|Mrs. Campbell]], Helen's landlady, is a maternal figure who turns hostile. [[mrs-brown|Mrs. Brown]], who runs the restaurant where Helen rents a room, offers a different kind of maternal attention — practical, undemanding, grounded in the exchange of money for service. Mrs. Davies, by contrast, represents the biological bond that neither transactional nor hostile relationships can replace. She is the mother to whom Helen could always return — and whom Helen chooses, repeatedly, not to return to.

## Significance

Mrs. Davies is not, and is not meant to be, a fully developed character. She is a condition of the narrative: the home that exists, the mother who waits, the life Helen might have led. Her presence — faint, distant, always implied rather than dramatized — creates the gravitational field against which Helen's trajectory is measured. Every step away from Masonville is a step away from her mother; every refusal to return home is a refusal of the conventional life Mrs. Davies represents. She is the mother who, precisely by offering unconditional return, makes Helen's refusal of that return a genuine choice rather than a necessity.
