# Marriage and Courtship

Marriage is the gravitational center around which *Diverging Roads* orbits. For [[helen-davies|Helen Davies]], the question of whom to marry — and whether to marry at all — is inseparable from the question of what kind of life she will lead. [[paul|Paul]] offers security, shared history, and the steady road of [[economic-ambition|self-improvement]]; [[bert|Bert]] offers risk, excitement, and the volatile world of speculation. Each suitor represents not just a different man but a different future — a different self Helen might become. The novel's title crystallizes in the marital decision: every choice is a road taken and a road abandoned, and marriage is the choice that forecloses the most.

## In the World of the Novel

In early twentieth-century America, marriage was the expected destination for women of Helen's class. Courtship was a formalized social process — a young woman assessed a suitor's prospects, and her family assessed him alongside her. To remain unmarried was to occupy an ambiguous, often precarious social position. The novel is clear-eyed about the economics underlying the institution: marriage was, among other things, a property arrangement.

Helen discovers this dimension with genuine shock when, late in the novel, she researches the institution itself:

> She began to frequent the public library. In a locked room, to which the librarian gave her the key after an embarrassed scrutiny, she found on forbidden shelves a history of marriage, and curled among the cushions on her window-seat, she spent an afternoon absorbed in tracing that institution from the first faint appreciation of the property value of women into the labyrinth of custom and morality to which it led. (022-005)

This research — conducted not as academic exercise but as self-interrogation — marks a turning point. Helen, who has been married and widowed, who has loved two men and lost both in different ways, begins to see marriage not as fate but as a legal and social construct, one of the "contracts so blithely entered upon by men and women who would not so unquestioningly subscribe to any other legal agreement" (022-005).

## Generational Expectations

The pressure Helen feels is not merely personal; it is generational and structural. Her mother articulates the assumption with devastating clarity:

> "Do you think I'd steer you or Louise up against marriage?" (009-018)

The question is rhetorical. A mother's duty, in this world, is precisely to steer her daughters toward good matches. The verb — "steer" — reveals the underlying premise: young women do not choose their own course; they are guided, by mothers who know the terrain, toward the destination that awaits all women of their class. Helen's rebellion against this guidance is partial and painful. She does not reject marriage; she simply cannot accept it on the terms offered. Her ambivalence — wanting to be loved, wanting to be free — is the novel's most sustained emotional note.

## Helen's Suitors: Two Roads

### Paul: The Steady Road

Paul's vision of marriage is inseparable from his vision of a properly ordered life. Walking through the orchard with Helen during his visit from Ripley, the word arrives almost as a reflex:

> Much later they found a little nook under the willows on the levee bank and sat there with the river rippling at their feet, his arm around her, her head on his shoulder. They talked a little then. Paul told her again all about Ripley, but she did not mind. "When we're married—" said Paul, and the rest of the sentence did not matter. (006-030)

The sentence that "did not matter" is revealing. For Paul, marriage is the assumed destination; what follows "when we're married" needs no elaboration because the script is already written. He will work; she will keep house. This is the life his mother — a widow who kept "an immaculate house" — modeled, and it is the life he expects to replicate. His tragedy is not that this vision is wrong but that it is insufficient. Helen cannot want what he offers, though she tries, and the novel refuses to condemn either of them for this mismatch.

### Bert: The Volatile Road

Bert's courtship is antithetical to Paul's — impulsive where Paul is deliberate, theatrical where Paul is earnest. His proposal, delivered in a crowded room "beneath the glaring lights and crepe-paper decorations" (012-014), is a performance: "'I really do.' His eyes were on hers, and she saw his confidence change to certainty. 'You're game!' he said, and kissed her triumphantly" (012-014).

Where Paul's marriage promises routine, Bert's promises intensity. The novel is unsparing about what that intensity costs. Bert's speculative energy — the same energy that makes him exciting — makes him dangerous. His "gold pieces" tossed in the air (014-010) are the gesture of a man who treats money and emotion as the same volatile substance. Helen's marriage to Bert is an education in the gap between romantic promise and material reality.

## Marriage as Lived Experience

> She was not unfamiliar with the idea of marriage. At twenty-three, she had seen her friends marry — some well, some poorly — and had watched the transformation from girl to wife with an attention that was not quite envy and not quite relief. She had imagined herself married, but the image was as vague as the face of a child unborn; it did not come into focus. (010-003)

The novel also portrays marriage from the inside, in its daily textures. Married characters appear throughout: Helen's parents, exhausted by years of "unequal struggle" (003-012); the widowed Mrs. Brown, still shaped by a marriage that ended years before; the young couple at Rollo, whose shared telegraphy work offers a glimpse of partnership on different terms.

Paul's mother — widowed, competent, formidable — is perhaps the most telling portrait. When Helen encounters her on Ripley's main street, she is "bareheaded, carrying a parasol above her smooth, gray hair. Small as she was, there was something formidable in the lines of her stocky figure and in the crispness of her stiff white shirtwaist" (016-018). This is marriage's afterlife: a woman who has survived loss and built a life of self-sufficient dignity. Helen's visit to her immaculate house provokes an unexpected response:

> There was not a detail of the room that Helen would not have changed, but as she looked at it tears came unexpectedly into her eyes. Something was here that she wanted, something that she had always missed. (016-019)

That "something" is never named — it is the quality of settledness, of belonging, of a life built on the steady accumulation of small choices rather than the vertiginous swings of speculation. Helen weeps for what she cannot have, or cannot let herself have, and the novel holds her ambivalence steady, refusing either to endorse domesticity or to dismiss it.
