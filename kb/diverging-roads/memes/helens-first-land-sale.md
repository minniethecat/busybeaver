# Helen's First Land Sale

The first successful real estate sale of Helen Davies's career, completed alone in the Coalinga oil fields after a grueling expedition — the moment she proved to herself that she could survive and succeed on her own terms.

## Before the Sale

Helen had arrived in Coalinga with no money, no marketable skills beyond telegraphy, and no clear path forward. After days of searching for work and finding none, she had spent the last of her resources. The local paper carried advertisements for stenographers and cooks on the oil-leases — positions she was unqualified for. Desperate, she turned to the one asset she had: her knowledge of land sales, acquired during her brief time with Clark & Hayward.

She set out alone into the oil fields, "her difficult prospect" a reluctant buyer she had identified after days of canvassing. The expedition required three days of "toiling over dusty fields with a soil-augur, skilfully countering objections before they took form, nursing an engine that coughed on three cylinders" (026-031). She had no backup, no support, and no guarantee of success.

## The Sale

The sale was not a single dramatic moment but the culmination of three days of relentless effort. Helen had studied her prospect, anticipated his objections, and prepared counter-arguments. She had shown him competing tracts with "fair-seeming impartiality that damned them by indirection." When he finally signed, she had earned her commission through sheer force of will.

"Clutching the gold piece, she reminded herself that she must eat" (021-004). The gold piece — tangible, heavy, real — was more than payment. It was proof that she could do this work, that she had value in the marketplace, that she was not dependent on any man for her survival.

## Significance

The first land sale is a turning point in Helen's arc of self-sufficiency. Before this moment, she had always been dependent: on her mother, on Paul, on Bert, on employers who underpaid her. The gold piece represented her first earnings from a skill she owned — the art of persuasion, the discipline of preparation, the courage to ask for what she was worth.

This sale also marks the beginning of Helen's career as a land saleswoman, the profession that would eventually make her one of the most successful women in her field. From this one hard-won transaction, she built a territory, a reputation, and an identity.
