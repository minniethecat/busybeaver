# Clark & Hayward

Clark & Hayward is the San Francisco real-estate firm that serves as the institutional engine of *Diverging Roads*'s second half. It employs [[bert|Bert Kennedy]] as a salesman, funds his speculative campaigns in the oil fields, and later becomes the vehicle for [[helen-davies|Helen Davies's]] professional transformation — from telegraph operator to land saleswoman. The firm's "wide, clean-looking office, where salesmen lounged against the counters, their elbows resting on plate glass that covered surveyor's maps and photographs of alfalfa fields" (020-002) is the novel's most fully realized portrait of corporate California.

## Overview

Clark & Hayward is introduced through Bert, who announces his ambitions with characteristic grandiosity: "'I'm going down to see Clark & Hayward. I'm going to put through a deal with them that'll put us on velvet,' he declared" (012-018). The firm is the gatekeeper to the speculative economy — the entity that can fund campaigns, issue letters of credit, and transform a salesman's promises into material reality.

The firm's business model is land speculation: buying tracts, subdividing them, and selling farms to settlers drawn by promotional campaigns. On the morning war is declared, Helen enters the office and takes in its characteristic atmosphere:

> The headlines screamed the news at her. It was war. She missed one train to San José in order to talk to Mr. Clark. The news had made no change in the atmosphere of Clark & Hayward's wide, clean-looking office, where salesmen lounged against the counters, their elbows resting on plate glass that covered surveyor's maps and photographs of alfalfa fields. The talk, as she stopped to speak to one and another, was the usual news of sales made and lost, quarrels over commissions, personal gossip. (020-002)

It is, as Helen will later observe, a system of "parasites living off the land without giving anything in return" (019-014) — a critique the novel allows her to voice without fully endorsing or rejecting.

## Role in the Narrative

Bert's relationship with Clark & Hayward drives the novel's central crisis. The firm gives him "an unlimited letter of credit" and "free rein" for a "gigantic selling campaign in the oil fields" (016-018), trusting his "truly remarkable personality as a salesman." When Bert overextends, writes bad checks, and disappears, the firm becomes an antagonist — the entity that can press charges, whose losses must be covered.

Helen's intervention — selling MacAdams a farm in [[ripley|Ripley Farmland Acres]] to generate a check made out to Clark & Hayward — is the novel's most dramatic sequence of professional performance. She operates under the firm's name without its knowledge: "'Clark & Hayward, San Francisco. I'm representing them. H. D. Kennedy — I'm at the hotel'" (014-016). The fraud is audacious and effective; the firm, when it learns of the sale, is more impressed than angry.

Later, after Bert's disappearance, Clark & Hayward becomes Helen's legitimate employer. She sells land on commission, develops her own territory, and eventually confronts Mr. Clark himself about the ethics of the business:

> "Six-tenths of our business can be wiped out without doing any harm. A real-estate salesman hasn't any real reason for existing. We're just a barrier between the land and the people who want it." (019-014)

Mr. Clark's response — "It's a game. Just a game. That's all." (020-004) — articulates the firm's philosophy, and the novel's ambivalence about the economic system it represents.

### The Bert Kennedy Catastrophe

Mr. Clark, "a lean, shrewd-looking man about forty-five years old," gave "the impression of having kept his nerves at high tension for so many years that now he must strain them still tighter or relax altogether" (016-017). His interview with Helen after Bert's disappearance reveals the scope of what the firm had bet on Bert's talent:

> "That young man has a truly remarkable personality as a salesman. He had us completely up in the air." (016-018)

The campaign had begun with "astounding success." Bert "had brought huge crowds to hear speakers sent down from the city; had gathered the names of thousands of 'leads'; had imported fifty salesmen to canvass these names and bring in prospective buyers. Scores of these had been taken to the land and hundreds more were promised. Clark & Hayward contemplated hiring special trains for them" (016-018). But expenses ran "into disquieting amounts for the actual results produced" (016-018). The firm began "a quiet investigation" and "decided that he was spending too much of their money for personal expenses" (016-018). They withdrew the letter of credit and advised creditors in Bakersfield that the firm would no longer pay Mr. Kennedy's bills.

> Mr. Kennedy had been informed of this. He had taken one of the firm's automobiles and disappeared. Later his check had come in. Clark & Hayward could not make that good, in addition to their other losses. (016-018)

The total debt Bert owed — "roughly five thousand dollars" (016-018) — was devastating, including "the cost of the automobile" and Bert's "expensive" recreations (016-018).

### Helen as Clark & Hayward Representative

After covering Bert's bad check through the MacAdams sale, Helen negotiates her own position with Clark & Hayward:

> She proposed that she should continue working for the firm on twelve and a half percent, five percent to apply on the amount Bert owed them. Mr. Clark countered by offering her ten percent with the same arrangement. She was stubborn, and he yielded. (016-018)

She walks out with three hundred dollars in her purse, and her life begins again. "She saw that the sun was shining, and as she walked through the crowded, familiar streets, passing flower-stands gay with color, feeling the cool breeze on her face, and seeing white clouds sailing over Twin Peaks, she felt that the bright day was mocking her. She understood why most suicides occur on days of sunshine" (016-018).

Her subsequent work selling Ripley Farmland Acres in the oil fields transforms her. She became known "throughout the fields as the Real-Estate Lady" (015-001). The firm's thin envelopes arrived regularly: "announcements of sales, Farm 406 — J. D. Hutchinson; Farms 915-917 — H. D. Kennedy" (015-005).

### The Advertising Confrontation

Years later, as an established saleswoman, Helen critiques the firm's advertising philosophy directly to her business partner Hutchinson:

> Now Clark & Hayward's advertising was all "Whoop'er up! Come on!" stuff. It made a bid for suckers. (016-042)

She observes that "men who would fall for that advertising were not of the class that had bank accounts" (016-044), proposing instead "small type, reading matter, sensible, straightforward arguments" (016-042) — a philosophy that signals her intellectual independence from the firm that trained her. When Hutchinson takes her plan to Mr. Clark, the firm gives him "a small advance on commission, and the Santa Clara Valley territory" (016-047), demonstrating Clark & Hayward's pragmatic willingness to adapt when money is on the line.
