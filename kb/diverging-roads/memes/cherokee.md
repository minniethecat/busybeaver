# Cherokee

Cherokee is the town "over the hill" from Masonville — defined by its mine, haunted by the ghosts of the men it has killed. The plume of Number Five unrolling over "the crest of Cherokee Hill" (001-004) connects this mining town to the railroad that is slowly replacing the extractive economy it represents.

## Description

### The Physical Geography

Cherokee sits just over the hill from [[masonville|Masonville]], a proximity that makes it a constant presence in the novel's geography even though it rarely appears as a setting. The afternoon hack that rattles between the two towns, the "long purple curl of smoke unrolling over the crest of Cherokee Hill" (001-004), the whistle of Number Five — these sensory details embed Cherokee in the daily consciousness of the novel's characters without requiring them to visit.

### The Mine

The mine is Cherokee's defining feature and its tragedy:

> The tale of California's early days is an epic, an immortal song of daring, of hope, of the urge of youth to unknown trails, of struggle, and of heartbreak. (001-001)

By the time the novel opens, the great days have passed. "Placer mining no longer paid in Cherokee, and the camp moved on across the mountains. Cherokee Valley was left behind, a desolate little hollow among the hills" (001-004). The mine that made the town is also what killed it — and what killed Paul's father.

Cherokee appears in the novel primarily as a point of reference rather than a fully realized setting. It is where Paul's father died — "killed in a mine over at Cherokee" (001-009). The afternoon hack rattles between Cherokee and Masonville, linking the two towns in a shared regional economy. Cherokee is the past that Masonville is trying to outgrow:

> Those were the great days of Cherokee, days of feverish activity, of hard, fierce living, of marvelous event. (001-002)

## Significance

### The Gold Rush Legacy

Cherokee represents the originating moment of the novel's world — the gold rush that drew people to California and set in motion the chain of events that would shape the lives of characters who never saw the mines. Paul's father was killed in a Cherokee mine (001-002); his death made Paul a fatherless boy determined to escape the poverty that mining left behind:

> His father was dead, killed in a mine over at Cherokee. He had come down to the Masonville school, and they were in the same class, the class that would graduate that spring. He was studying hard, trying to get as much education as possible before he would have to go to work. (001-002)

> He was a stocky, dark-haired boy, with blue eyes. His father was dead, killed in a mine over at Cherokee. He had come down to the Masonville school, and they were in the same class, the class that would graduate that spring. (001-002)

For Helen, Cherokee Hill was the geographical marker of departure — "the shining lines of track beyond Cherokee Hill into a vague big world that baffled her thoughts" (003-038) — the threshold between the known world of Masonville and the unknown world beyond. The hill itself, with its "long purple curl of smoke unrolling over the crest" (001-005), was a daily reminder of the world that lay beyond the valley.

Cherokee gives the novel its economic backstory. The mine represents the original California gamble — the extraction of mineral wealth at the cost of human life. Paul's entire trajectory — away from mining, toward telegraphy and the railroad — is a movement away from Cherokee's world of physical danger and toward a more modern, less lethal form of risk.

## The Human Cost

Cherokee's mines extracted more than gold from the hills. They took lives — Paul's father among them — and left families to survive on memory and determination. The mine death shaped Paul's trajectory as decisively as any living influence:

> A handful of dogged miners remained, and a few Portuguese families living in little cabins, harvesting a bare subsistence from the unwilling soil. (001-004)

The image of the "desolate little hollow" denuded of its trees and scarred by shallow tunnels is the novel's most concise statement about the true cost of California's mineral wealth. The boom was spectacular, but the bust was permanent: the trees did not grow back, the soil did not recover, and the families that lost their breadwinners did not get them back. Cherokee's legacy in the novel is not just the gold that was taken from its hills but the lives it claimed and the futures it foreclosed.
