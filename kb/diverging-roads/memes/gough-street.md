# Gough Street

A modest residential street in San Francisco where Helen Davies found her first independent room — a shabby but liberating space under the eaves, with a discouraged palm in a tub and a rent of two dollars and a half a week.

## Description

Gough Street was lined with modest two-story houses, their porches bearing the marks of long wear. The house Helen found there was characteristic: "a shabby two-story house on Gough Street, with a discouraged palm in a tub on the front porch" (011-006). Her room was "a small, neat place under the eaves, furnished with an iron bed, a washstand, a chair, and a strip of rag carpet." The bathroom was on the lower floor, and the rent was two dollars and a half a week — a sum that defined the limits of her working-class budget.

The street itself was exposed to the city's characteristic wind and sun. When Helen finally left, she "let herself out into the windy sunshine" (011-006), the weather itself marking her departure from this temporary home.

## Significance

Gough Street represents Helen's first truly independent living situation. Unlike the boardinghouse where she had previously stayed under her mother's shadow, this room was her own — spartan, cramped, and situated under the eaves, but hers alone. The "discouraged palm in a tub on the front porch" serves as an apt image for Helen's own state at this point: transplanted, struggling, but still alive.

The room also represents a turning point. When Helen finally packs her bag and leaves Gough Street, "her feet wanted to dance when she went down the narrow stairs for the last time" (011-006). She was leaving behind not just a room but a phase of privation, moving toward a more promising chapter of her life.
