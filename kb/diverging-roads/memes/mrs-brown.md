# Mrs. Brown

[[helen-davies|Helen Davies]]'s first landlady in Sacramento, whose real name is [[kittie-brown|Kittie Brown]] — a young widow
running a boarding-house and restaurant whose hidden life complicates the moral world
of the novel.

## Role in the Story

Mrs. Brown is a minor character whose dual nature serves as a quiet moral revelation for [[helen-davies|Helen]].
On the surface, she is a hardworking landlady with a "mechanically gay voice," feverish energy,
and bright yellow hair. But beneath this industrious facade, she is known around town as a "bad
woman" — [[kittie-brown|Kittie Brown]] — a revelation that shocks [[helen-davies|Helen]] and forces her first confrontation with
the moral complexity of city life. Mrs. Brown's character illustrates the precarious position of
women on their own, and the thin line between [[social-class|respectability]] and survival. (003-051) (004-009)

## Biography

Mrs. Brown keeps a row of rooms above a restaurant she manages in Sacramento, renting them at
six dollars a month. She is described as a sallow little woman with very bright brown eyes and
yellow hair, who talks "continuously in a light, mechanically gay voice" while making quick
movements with her hands and whisking about in rustling silk petticoats, "driven, it seemed, by
an intensity of energy almost feverish." (003-051)

She is apparently a young widow — she often looks at her wide wedding-ring and turns it on her
finger "as if she were not quite accustomed to wearing it." [[helen-davies|Helen]] never sees Mr. Brown and
concludes he is not alive, romanticizing Mrs. Brown's brief [[marriage-and-courtship|marriage]]. But Mrs. Brown's thin
figure and bright yellow hair are those of a girl, while "only her eyes were old" — the hard,
weary look that [[helen-davies|Helen]] mistakes for grief. (004-010)

Mrs. Brown works hard, sweeping the hall and keeping the rooms in order, though not very well.
There is "something piteous about her, as there is about a little [[the-railroad|train]]ed animal on the stage,
set to do tasks for which it is not fitted." [[helen-davies|Helen]], feeling guilty about Mrs. Brown's workload,
secretly sweeps her own room at night. (004-012)

One night, [[helen-davies|Helen]] overhears Mrs. Brown entertaining two young men in her room, laughing and
drinking beer. They call her "Kittie" and banter with her to "be a sport." [[helen-davies|Helen]], not
understanding what is happening, feels hurt that "Mrs. Brown should give a party and not ask
her." (004-023)

The truth comes out through [[mrs-campbell|Mrs. Campbell]], who bluntly tells [[helen-davies|Helen]] that "this [[kittie-brown|Kittie Brown]], she
calls herself" is "a bad woman" and that "everybody in town knows what *she* is." The revelation
sends "a wave of blackness" through [[helen-davies|Helen]]'s mind and poisons her sense of her surroundings. It
is a moment of lost innocence — [[helen-davies|Helen]]'s first direct encounter with the sexual economy of the
city. (004-028)

## Notable Appearances

- **Chapter III (003-051, 003-055)**: First appearance — Mrs. Brown shows [[helen-davies|Helen]] the room and
  takes her first rent payment with the friendly warning: "if you get lonesome, just come and
  knock on my door."
- **Chapter IV (004-008–012)**: [[helen-davies|Helen]] observes Mrs. Brown's hard life — her work, her shyness
  with [[helen-davies|Helen]], her gaiety with male customers, her piteous attempts at housekeeping.
- **Chapter IV (004-023)**: [[helen-davies|Helen]] overhears the late-night party in Mrs. Brown's room and
  cries herself to sleep, feeling excluded.
- **Chapter IV (004-028)**: [[mrs-campbell|Mrs. Campbell]]'s revelation of [[kittie-brown|Kittie Brown]]'s real identity forces
  [[helen-davies|Helen]] to flee the boarding-house.
