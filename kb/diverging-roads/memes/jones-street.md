# Jones Street

A steep San Francisco residential street where Helen Davies kept a little house in the final years of her city life — the last home she left behind when she departed for Guatemala.

## Description

Jones Street was one of San Francisco's many hilly residential streets, its slopes and cobblestones defining the neighborhood's vertical geography. Helen lived in "the little house" on Jones Street, a modest residence that represented her achieved independence. On the morning of her final departure, she "clutching her bag, her purse, her gloves, slammed the door of the little house and ran down the stairs of Jones Street to the waiting cab" (027-034).

The cab carried her "bumping over the cobbles" with Sara beside her and "the bags, the hatbox, an armful of roses, the shawl-strapped steamer-rug, jostled in confusion about her" (027-034). Jones Street — with its stairs descending to the street level, its cobblestones rattling the taxi — was the physical point of departure from her San Francisco life.

## Significance

Jones Street is the last San Francisco address in Helen's story, and it appears only in the moment of leaving. Unlike the Gough Street rooming house — which marked her arrival and her poverty — the Jones Street house signaled her arrival at a stable, independent existence. The act of slamming its door and running down its stairs carries the weight of finality.

Looking through the taxi's plate-glass windows at the city passing by, Helen "felt nothing but a sense of unreality. Incredible that these would still be here when she was gone! Incredible that she was going, actually going!" (027-034). Jones Street, like every other San Francisco landmark in that final passage, was receding into memory.
