## Overview

Nell Allan is a woman in the [[san-francisco|San Francisco]] social circle of [[louise|Louise]], encountered by [[helen-davies|Helen Davies]] during her first night out in [[san-francisco|the city]]. Described as "resplendent," she is part of the group of café-goers who represent the glittering, morally ambiguous urban world that contrasts with [[masonville|Masonville]]'s [[community-and-belonging|small-town]] propriety.

## Appearance

Nell Allan appears in a single but vivid scene — the restaurant dinner that marks [[helen-davies|Helen]]'s introduction to [[san-francisco|San Francisco]] nightlife:

> Momma was there, her hat dripping with plumes, white furs flung negligently over her shoulders, her fingers a blaze of rings. There was another resplendent woman, named Nell Allan; a bald-headed fat man called Bob; a younger man, with a lean face and restless blue eyes, hailed by Louise as Duddy. [009-1154]

She dances with Bob during the evening's entertainments:

> Duddy and momma, Bob and Mrs. Allan, swept out into a whirl of white arms and dark coats, tilted faces and swaying bodies. [009-1162]

The use of "Mrs. Allan" here suggests she is married, though her husband is never mentioned — a detail consistent with the novel's casual treatment of the café crowd's domestic arrangements.

## Thematic Significance

Nell Allan is part of the loose constellation of minor figures who populate the margins of [[helen-davies|Helen]]'s story — glimpsed vividly but briefly, never developed into full portraits, yet essential to the texture of the social worlds [[helen-davies|Helen]] moves through. Together with [[duddy|Duddy]], Bob, and momma, she represents a particular stratum of [[san-francisco|San Francisco]] life: worldly, pleasure-seeking, unburdened by the moral seriousness of the women's circle or the commercial drive of the land-sales world.
