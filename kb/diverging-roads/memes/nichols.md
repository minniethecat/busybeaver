# Mr. Nichols

A real-estate agent for [[ripley-farmland-acres|Ripley Farmland Acres]] whose aggressive but tone-deaf salesmanship
teaches [[helen-davies|Helen]] [[gilbert-kennedy|Kennedy]] a hard lesson about what really matters in closing a deal.

## Role in the Story

Nichols is the local agent [[helen-davies|Helen]] enlists to help her sell farmland to MacAdams — the sale upon
which [[bert|Bert]] [[gilbert-kennedy|Kennedy]]'s freedom depends. Nichols nearly sabotages the deal through a combination
of excessive talking, obliviousness to the buyer's signals, and ingrained sexism toward [[helen-davies|Helen]].
His failure as a salesman serves as a foil that highlights [[helen-davies|Helen]]'s own growing instincts about
human nature and persuasion. When [[helen-davies|Helen]] finally wrests control of the sale from him with a
shocking outburst — "He's my prospect. And, by God! I'm going to sell him or lose him myself!"
— it marks one of her most decisive moments of self-assertion. (013-001) (013-005)

## Biography

Nichols works as a land agent for [[ripley-farmland-acres|Ripley Farmland Acres]], a seventy-thousand-acre tract of
[[the-american-west|California]] farmland. He is introduced when [[helen-davies|Helen]], desperate to close a sale that will save
[[bert|Bert]] from a bad-check warrant, [[telegraphy|telegraph]]s him to meet her and the buyer MacAdams at the [[ripley|Ripley]]
[[the-railroad|train]] station. (013-001)

His defining characteristic as a salesman is his inability to stop talking. He has a
"consciously frank eye, and a smooth manner," and hustles his clients energetically into a
dusty automobile covered with canvas advertisements. But he talks "too much and too
enthusiastically," filling every silence with "galvanic enthusiasm" and worn phrases.
Crucially, he is oblivious to MacAdams's silence — and to the fact that MacAdams "gazing
contemplatively at the skyline, said nothing." (013-001) (013-003)

Nichols also represents the casual sexism of the business world. He treats [[helen-davies|Helen]] as a
nuisance, apologizing to MacAdams for saying "damn" in her presence — an apology that "conveyed
resentment" — and generally dismisses her as irrelevant to "this affair between men." When
[[helen-davies|Helen]] tries to intervene, he shakes off her hand impatiently. (013-003) (013-005)

The turning point comes when [[helen-davies|Helen]] confronts Nichols directly: "He's my prospect. And, by God!
I'm going to sell him or lose him myself!" The outburst shocks Nichols into submission and
shocks [[helen-davies|Helen]] herself — "her words shocked her like a thunderclap, but the shock steadied her."
From that point, Nichols retreats to the background while [[helen-davies|Helen]] guides the sale to its
[[economic-ambition|success]]ful conclusion. (013-005)

After the sale closes and MacAdams counts out $1,273.90 in gold coins and banknotes, Nichols
figures the amount "madly" and announces it in "a shaking voice." He is exultant and squeezes
[[helen-davies|Helen]]'s arm — the sale is done, and even he recognizes that she was the one who closed it.
(013-010)
