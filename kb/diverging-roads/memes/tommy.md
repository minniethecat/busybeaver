# Tommy

[[helen-davies|Helen Davies]]'s younger brother, a farm boy whose presence in the novel — like [[mabel|Mabel]]'s — anchors the family life Helen progressively outgrows. Tommy never speaks a line of dialogue. He exists in the novel as a receiver of gifts, a witness to departures, and a reminder of the life Helen has chosen not to live.

## Role in the Story

Tommy appears only briefly, but his appearances mark the rhythms of family life on the Davies farm: the morning chaos of Helen's departure, the Christmas gifts sent from far away, the small farm crises — a dead horse, the need for a new one — that Helen's city salary helps resolve. He is one of the people for whom Helen's earnings matter, one of the reasons sending money home feels like both obligation and satisfaction. In a novel otherwise focused on the choices and struggles of its adult characters, Tommy represents the life that continues unchanged in Masonville, indifferent to the dramas unfolding elsewhere.

## Biography

Tommy is the youngest Davies sibling, old enough to get in the way during packing and young enough to chant derisively at the breakfast table while licking a mixing spoon [002-010]. On the morning Helen leaves for telegraph school, "Tommy kept getting in the way" amid the flurry of tears and excitement, and he rides with the family to the Masonville station to see her off [003-046].

At Christmas in San Francisco, Helen sends him "a knife and a pocketbook with a two-dollar bill in it" [009-087] — small tokens of the sister who has gone to the city and now earns her own money. The gifts are modest but significant: a knife, a tool of rural self-sufficiency; a pocketbook, an object for carrying money, suggesting the adult world of transactions Tommy is approaching; a two-dollar bill, the tangible evidence of Helen's earning power. Together they sketch the relationship: Helen, the older sister who has escaped the farm, sends money and useful objects to the younger brother who remains.

When the family's old horse Nell dies — "Tommy found her dead in the pasture when he went to get the cows" — it is Helen's salary that helps buy the replacement, a six-year-old "blind in one eye" that can be got cheap [009-094]. The dead horse and the one-eyed replacement are, in miniature, the story of farm life: loss and frugal substitution, crisis and practical response. Tommy is the one who discovers the crisis; Helen is the one whose earnings resolve it. Their complementary roles — observer and actor, receiver and provider — recapitulate the novel's larger structure of departure and return.

## The Silent Witness

Tommy's silence is narratively significant. Unlike [[mabel|Mabel]], who speaks and acts and occasionally quarrels with Helen, Tommy is purely a presence — someone who is there, who sees, who receives. His derisive chant at the breakfast table ("But — good gracious, Helen! You're only a little girl!") [002-010] is a child's mockery that inadvertently captures the family's incomprehension: Helen, a little girl, is about to do something no one in the family has done before. Tommy's chant, mocking and affectionate in equal measure, is the voice of Masonville itself — not hostile, but limited, unable to imagine the scope of Helen's ambition.

## The Brother Who Stays

Structurally, Tommy is the brother who stays, as [[paul|Paul]] is the lover who leaves. Both male figures in Helen's early life represent different relationships to the home she abandons: Tommy, bound to the farm by youth and circumstance; Paul, driven away by ambition and the death of his miner father. Tommy will presumably grow up to work the farm as his father does; Paul will take the train to Ripley and never really come back. Between them, they sketch the two trajectories available to Masonville's young men: stay and inherit, or leave and become someone else. Helen, by leaving, takes the male path — and the novel measures the cost of that choice in the family she sends Christmas gifts to but can never fully rejoin.

## Significance

Tommy matters in the novel precisely because he does not matter to its plot. He is a background figure, a member of the family chorus, a recipient of gifts. But his very marginality is significant: he represents the life Helen would have been embedded in had she stayed — the life of family obligations, farm crises, and small-town rhythms that she escaped by learning the telegraph key. His presence at the breakfast table and at the train station and in the pasture where the horse died are markers of a world that continues without Helen — a world she can support from a distance but can never fully re-enter. In the novel's emotional economy, Tommy is one of the people for whom Helen earns money, and one of the reasons earning money matters.
