# Social Class

Social class in *Diverging Roads* is an invisible architecture that determines where characters live, whom they may marry, and what futures they can imagine. Masonville's class structure — from the established families on "complacent streets" to the miners' widows and telegraph operators — maps the possibilities available to each character. The novel registers class not as a fixed hierarchy but as something "performed, earned, and sometimes lost" — a system of boundaries that characters navigate through work, marriage, and strategic self-presentation.

## Definition

In early twentieth-century small-town California, class was defined by a combination of family lineage, occupation, property ownership, and perceived respectability. The mining and railroad economies had disrupted older hierarchies, creating new wealth alongside persistent poverty. For women, class status was largely determined by the men they married — a reality the novel both documents and challenges through [[helen-davies|Helen]]'s insistence on earning her own living.

## In the Novel

Class boundaries are drawn through physical space. Mrs. Campbell's "two-story white house on a complacent street" with its "immaculate propriety" (005-046) stands in contrast to the farmhouse Helen longs to escape. Paul, the son of a dead miner, occupies a precarious position — educated enough to aspire, poor enough to be vulnerable:

> His father was dead, killed in a mine over at Cherokee. He had come down to the Masonville school, and they were in the same class, the class that would graduate that spring. He was studying hard, trying to get as much education as possible before he would have to go to work. He lived with his mother in a little house near the edge of town, on the road to the farm. (001-009)

Helen's class anxiety permeates her choices. She learns telegraphy partly because it offers respectability without requiring capital: a working woman occupies an ambiguous class position, neither dependent nor fully established. Her suitors represent different class trajectories — Paul's steady railroad career, Bert's speculative ventures.

The novel registers class through small details: the quality of clothing, the possession of a telephone, the ability to travel. When Helen walks through San Francisco's hotel lobbies as a businesswoman, she navigates spaces that would have excluded her as a Masonville farm girl. Class is not fixed; it is performed, earned, and sometimes lost.

### Class and Domestic Space

Class is inscribed in domestic architecture throughout the novel. The Davies farmhouse, mortgaged and vulnerable, sits at the edge of economic viability. When a killing frost hits, the family's future hinges on "getting young Mason to renew" the mortgage (003-041). Helen's mother works "silently, stepping heavily about the kitchen, now and then glancing through the window toward the barn" (003-041). The contrast with Mrs. Campbell's house — where Helen works as a domestic — is stark:

> Running back to the house, she met Mrs. Campbell returning from a sewing-circle meeting. Mrs. Campbell was neatly hatted and gloved, and the expression in her pale blue eyes behind the dotted veil suddenly made Helen realize how blow-away she looked, bareheaded, her loosened hair ruffled by the breeze, her blouse sagging under the arms. She stood awkwardly self-conscious while Mrs. Campbell unlocked the front door. (005-046)

### Class and Consumption

Bert's world in San Francisco reveals class through consumption. Helen reflects that "they paid nearly half that sum for rent, and that the very clothes she was wearing had cost more than his month's salary" (012-012) when she compares her life to Paul's. Bert's insistence on hotel living over a house with a kitchenette is a class performance:

> Bert had scouted her idea of getting an apartment with a kitchenette. He said he had not married a cook, and he hated women with burned complexions and red hands. He made her feel plebeian and common in preferring a home to a hotel. (011-020)

The hotel, the clothes, the restaurants: these are the markers of a speculative class that lives on credit and appearance.

### Class and the Professions

The novel maps class onto occupations with precision. Railroad work — Paul's territory — is steady, respectable, working-class. Telegraphy offers Helen a ladder out of the farm without requiring marriage: "Operators in great demand. Graduates earn $75 to $100 a month up" (003-035). Teaching — Anne's profession — is the genteel option for educated women. Real estate — Bert's and later Helen's field — is morally ambiguous, floating between legitimate business and confidence work.

The women Helen meets on the train to Sacramento in the novel's later chapters articulate this tension directly. Dodo, an executive secretary, declares:

> "We're freaks. We don't represent the mass of women. We go around and around in our little circles and think we're modern women because we make a lot of noise. But we aren't. We're of no importance at all, with our charity boards and our social surveys and our offices. It's the girls who marry in their teens — millions of 'em, in millions of the little homes all over America — that really count." (021-025)

This speech articulates the class anxieties that run through the novel: the professional woman's uncertain status, the distance between the "freaks" who work and the masses who marry.

### Class and Advertising

Helen's critique of Clark & Hayward's advertising methods is fundamentally a class analysis:

> Men who would fall for that advertising were not of the class that had bank accounts. (016-044)

She understands that the firm's "Whoop'er up! Come on!" advertising "made a bid for suckers" (016-042), while sensible farmers with money require "small type, reading matter, sensible, straightforward arguments" (016-042). The distinction between the classes that spend and the classes that save is at the heart of her approach to business.
