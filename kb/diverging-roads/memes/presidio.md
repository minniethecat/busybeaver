# Presidio

The U.S. Army military reservation at the northern tip of San Francisco, visible from the Golden Gate — a landmark that marked both a young man's first wonder at the city and a woman's final glimpse of home.

## Description

The Presidio occupied the strategic northern headlands of the San Francisco Peninsula, commanding views of the Golden Gate and the Pacific Ocean. From the deck of an outbound steamer, it appeared as part of the dramatic sequence of coastal landmarks: "the Ferry Tower, the high cliffs of Telegraph, the castle-like height of Russian Hill, the Presidio, Cliff House, the beach" (027-034). The military reservation's position at the very edge of the continent made it a natural endpoint in any catalog of the city's defining features — the last point of land before the open sea.

The Presidio was also one of the established sights on the city's tourist circuit. Paul, newly arrived in San Francisco, reported to Helen that "he had seen Chinatown, the Presidio, the beach, and Seal Rocks" — the standard itinerary of a sightseeing car (003-004).

## Significance

Like Chinatown and Cliff House, the Presidio functions as a narrative bookend, first seen through Paul's excited tourist eyes and last seen through Helen's grieving gaze as she departs forever. The Presidio's unchanging presence — a military installation, stable and permanent — contrasts with the flux of Helen's own life.

The sightseeing car episode captures the imbalance in Helen and Paul's early relationship: Paul was out in the world, seeing "splendid" things and visiting landmarks, while Helen remained trapped in Masonville, her world contracting around her. The Presidio and Chinatown, described in the same letter, represented a world Helen could only imagine. By the novel's end, she had not only seen these places but lived among them — and was leaving them behind.
