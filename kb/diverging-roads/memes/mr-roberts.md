# Mr. Roberts

The telegraph office manager in Sacramento who gives [[helen-davies|Helen Davies]] her first paying job — and whose behavior toward her becomes a defining early test of her resilience. He embodies the vulnerability of a working woman whose livelihood depends on the goodwill of a man who does not wish her well.

## Role in the Story

Mr. Roberts is both facilitator and predator. He appears initially as a "very friendly" manager who pats Helen's hand in what she naively interprets as a fatherly gesture (005-058). But his friendliness curdles into sexual aggression in one of the novel's most harrowing scenes: the encounter in the battery room where he accuses her of "playing" him, traps her in the close space, and she can only escape by holding her ground until his fury exhausts itself (007-010 through 007-017).

Structurally, Mr. Roberts marks Helen's passage from innocence into the harder knowledge of how power works between men and women in the workplace. After the encounter, she must continue to face him, to ask him for an advance, to secure the recommendation she needs for her next job — lessons in the fragility of a working woman's position and the toughness required to survive it.

## Biography

Mr. Roberts manages the main telegraph office in Sacramento where Helen works after completing her training under [[mr-weeks|Mr. Weeks]]. When Helen first approaches him seeking work, he is welcoming: "Mr. Roberts, the manager, was very friendly, talking to her for some time and patting her hand in a manner which she thought fatherly and found comforting" (005-058). He tells her to come back — he "might do something."

Once Helen is employed, his demeanor shifts. He walks past her without greeting, his "voice snapped like a whiplash" over delayed messages (007-002), and he withholds the raise he has promised. Helen, newly estranged from her boarding-house after [[mrs-campbell|Mrs. Campbell]]'s accusations, needs money urgently and thinks of asking Mr. Roberts for an advance — but he is in "a black mood" and "some obscure fury was working in him" (007-002).

The crisis comes when Mr. Roberts calls Helen down to the battery room under the guise of showing her the equipment, then turns on her: "How long're you going to think you're making a damn fool of me?" He catches her wrist, his body shaking, and accuses her of "playing" him, of "acting innocence" (007-010). Helen, terrified but composed, faces him through the assault and its aftermath. When his anger subsides, he simply leaves, and she steadies herself among the "uncaring battery jars" before climbing back to the daylight office (007-018).

The next morning, Helen returns to the office and faces him again. "Mr. Roberts' recommendation was necessary before she could get another telegraph job" — the practical reality that keeps her tethered to a man who has abused his power over her.

## The Battery Room Scene: A Close Reading

The battery room encounter is one of the novel's most tightly constructed set pieces, and it deserves close attention for what it reveals about Roberts's psychology and the dynamics of workplace predation.

The setting is significant: a basement room filled with "rows of glass jars" — the telegraph batteries that power the office above. It is a utilitarian, windowless space, a place of machinery rather than human interaction. Roberts summons Helen there under a professional pretext, exploiting the hierarchical relationship that requires her to follow his instructions. The glass jars become a haunting image — they "spin around her" when he grabs her wrist, and later, when he has left, she waits "among the uncaring battery jars, steadying herself" (007-018). The adjective "uncaring" is precise: the machinery of the workplace is indifferent to what happens to the workers within it.

Roberts's accusation — that Helen has been "playing" him, "acting innocence" — reveals the psychology of the predator who constructs a narrative in which he is the victim. His fury is driven by a sense of entitlement betrayed: he has given her a job, been "friendly," and she has failed to reciprocate in the way he expected. The scene does not depict calculated seduction; it depicts a man losing control, shaking with rage, because a young woman has not performed the gratitude he believes he is owed.

Helen's response is notable for what it is not: it is not flight, not screaming, not fainting. She holds her ground. She waits him out. She survives not through heroic resistance but through the sheer stubborn endurance that characterizes her throughout the novel. When he leaves, she collects herself and climbs back to the office — and goes on working.

## Aftermath and Continuation

The novel's treatment of the aftermath is as significant as the scene itself. There is no police report, no public accusation, no justice. Helen must continue to work in the same office, depend on the same man for her next recommendation, and disguise the encounter as if nothing happened. This is the reality the novel insists upon: for a working woman in Helen's position, there is no recourse. Survival means endurance, not justice. When she finally secures [[mr-bryant|Mr. Bryant]]'s telegram offering her a position in San Francisco, it arrives as rescue — but it is rescue she has earned through her own persistence in a hostile environment.

## Quotations

He caught her wrist, and she felt his whole body shaking. "You — you — damned little —" The rows of glass jars spun around her. She hardly understood the words he flung at her.
>
> (007-010)

> She waited a while among the uncaring battery jars, steadying herself, and wiping her face with her handkerchief. When she forced herself to climb up into the daylight again there was no one in the office but McCormick, who sat at the San Francisco wire, gazing into space, whistling "Life's a funny proposition after all," while the disregarded sounder clattered fretfully, calling him.
>
> (007-018)

The detail of McCormick whistling "Life's a funny proposition after all" while the sounder clatters unattended is one of the novel's most effective uses of ironic juxtaposition. The world continues its indifferent routine; the San Francisco wire calls for attention; a man whistles a popular tune; and a young woman climbs the stairs having just survived an assault. The machinery of work and the machinery of male indifference operate in parallel.

## Character Type

Roberts belongs to the novel's taxonomy of male obstruction figures. Unlike [[monroe|Monroe]], whose condescension is professional rather than sexual, Roberts's predation is explicitly physical. Unlike [[mr-bryant|Mr. Bryant]], whose impersonality represents safety, Roberts's "friendliness" is the mask for a threat. The novel distinguishes carefully among these male types, mapping the different forms that male power takes in the workplace: the mentor ([[mr-weeks|Mr. Weeks]]), the functionary (Bryant), the condescending rival (Monroe), and the predator (Roberts). Each requires a different strategy from Helen; Roberts's case, the most dangerous, requires endurance and escape.
