# Central Trust Company

A Los Angeles financial institution controlled by former Judge G. H. Kennedy — the bank at the center of an embezzlement scandal that cast a long shadow over the Kennedy family.

## Overview

The Central Trust Company of Los Angeles was controlled by "former Judge G. H. Kennedy" until "his indictment some years ago for mishandling its funds" (005-007). The scandal came to public attention when Gilbert Kennedy Jr. was arrested for cashing a bogus check on the Metropolitan National Bank, and newspaper coverage of the younger Kennedy's troubles reached back to recount his father's crimes.

The elder Kennedy, after his indictment, had lost both his position at the bank and his relationship with his son. By the time of his son's arrest, the former judge "denied knowledge of his son's whereabouts, saying that he had not been on good terms with his son for several years" (005-007).

## Role

The Central Trust Company appears in the novel primarily as a symbol of the corruption that runs through the Kennedy family. The father's embezzlement and the son's check fraud form a linked chain of financial malfeasance — a pattern of speculation and dishonesty that contrasts sharply with Helen Davies's hard-won integrity.

The bank also functions as a narrative device: Gilbert Kennedy Jr. (Helen's occasional suitor and later a figure of scandal) was shaped by his father's disgrace. The telegram Helen relays — "G. H. Kennedy, Central Trust Company, Los Angeles. Drawing on you for five hundred. Must have it. Absolutely sure thing this time" (005-007) — captures the Kennedy approach to money: urgent, unsecured, and always promising more than it delivers.
