## Overview

The relationship between Helen Davies and Paul forms the emotional core of the novel. Their bond evolves from childhood classmates in Masonville to young lovers, through years of separation, and into a complex adult connection shaped by Helen's marriage to Bert Kennedy and the pressures of war. More than a simple love story, their relationship embodies the novel's central metaphor — two lives traveling on separate roads that occasionally converge, raising the question of whether they can ever truly share the same path.

## Childhood and Courtship

Helen and Paul meet as schoolmates in Masonville, drawn together by shared ambition and restlessness with small-town life [001-P02]. Paul, with his quiet charm and practicality, balances Helen's restless energy. Their early courtship unfolds in pastoral scenes: Sunday buggy rides through golden hills, gathering wild flowers, and quiet moments under live-oaks where they dream of futures as telegraph operators together [002-P02].

Paul's work on the riverboat introduces Helen to a new world. When he shows her the dim tank filled with flopping fish scooped from the river, she finds her own thrill in his discovery, picturing "their cool lives in the green river, and the city supper-tables at which they would be eaten" [006-019]. Paul's patient explanations of things she cannot fully understand deepen her affection. On one excursion, struggling to straighten her hat and brush sand from her skirt, Helen stumbles aboard with Paul's hand steadying her — a small gesture that captures the protective ease between them [006-068].

## Separation

The relationship is first tested when Paul leaves Masonville to pursue work. Helen, too, departs for San Francisco. Their paths diverge — Helen marries Bert Kennedy, while Paul marries and starts a family [017-P01]. Yet the bond is never fully severed. Even in Willetta's parlor, far from Paul, Helen almost speaks his name aloud: "I'll be right here the rest of my life, too," she says, "and for a moment Paul's name was on her lips" [022-005]. When she forgets him momentarily in the excitement of her career, the thought strikes her: "How could she have forgotten Paul even for a moment?" [022-019].

## Reunion

When they meet again in San Jose during the war years, both are changed. Helen has become a successful real estate agent; Paul works at the ice-plant and has acquired business interests in the valley [017-P02]. Their reunion carries the weight of years and choices made. "His appearance in San José a few months earlier had been like the sight of a cool spring in a desert" — Helen realizes that despite everything, her feelings for Paul have endured [017-P02].

Their renewed connection unfolds through small domestic moments: the creaking of the wicker chair when Helen settles into it, the sharp crackle of Paul's match as he lights his after-dinner cigar [019-019]. Paul telephones with flimsy excuses — a message from his mother, a bit of news from Ripley Farmland Acres — "some negligible matter which she heard without listening, knowing that to both of them it was unimportant" [017-036]. What matters is the sound of his voice.

## Conflict and Tension

The relationship is not without strain. Paul is "aggrieved" when Helen seems to pull away [021-001], and Helen, "drooping wearily on one of the suitcases, contritely searched her mind for a reply" [021-002]. When Helen describes her plans for a San Francisco apartment with wood-paneled walls and a kitchenette where they would cook supper together, she is "eager to take Paul's imagination with her" — but she perceives that she is "alarming Paul" [021-028]. His practicality collides with her ambition.

The question of money complicates their bond. Helen insists that "her relation to Paul had nothing to do with money; in their love they were equal, and when Paul added the fruit of his work to the scale the balance would be uneven" [021-052]. Paul, however, feels the pressure to match her independence: "if things do go to pieces, as you said — can't you see I'll — just have to be in a position to do something?" [017-066].

## Significance

Helen and Paul's relationship illustrates the novel's central theme: the roads people take and the ones left behind. Their bond survives separation, other marriages, and the passage of time, but the question of whether they will ultimately reunite remains open, mirroring the uncertainty of life's diverging paths [024-P01]. In Paul's final, hesitant goodbye — "Oh, Helen — sweetheart —" then quickly departing without kissing her — the novel leaves their future suspended, unresolved [017-085].
