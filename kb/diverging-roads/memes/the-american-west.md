# The American West

California is more than the setting of *Diverging Roads* — it is the novel's condition of possibility. The American West, as both a real geography and a cultural mythology, provides the horizon against which characters measure their ambitions, their failures, and their capacity for self-reinvention.

## Definition

By the early twentieth century, the American West had passed from frontier to settled territory. The transcontinental railroad, completed in 1869, had collapsed the distance between California and the eastern cities. Towns like Masonville represented an intermediate stage — no longer raw frontier, not yet fully civilized, suspended between wilderness and respectability. The mythology of the West as a land of unlimited opportunity persisted even as its material basis — free land, open range, untapped resources — was closing.

## In the Novel

The novel opens with a portrait of Masonville as a town in stagnation, a place where "little has occurred for many years" and the men "lounge aimlessly about the street corners" (001-001). This is the West in its post-frontier lethargy — the adventure has moved elsewhere, leaving behind those who could not or would not follow.

Against this stasis, the railroad represents the West's continuing dynamism. The trains that pass through Masonville carry drummers, mail, and the promise of connection to larger worlds: San Francisco, the San Joaquin Valley, and beyond. Paul follows the tracks south to Ripley; Helen dreams of following him.

The California landscape — its deserts, valleys, orchards, and mountains — is rendered with vivid specificity throughout the novel. The train journey through the Central Valley captures the contrast between desolation and cultivation: "leafy orchards and vineyards and the cool green of alfalfa fields" give way to "a level plain covered with dry grass" where "desolation was written upon it" (014-003). The land itself tells the story of human effort and its limits.

The West also functions as a psychological landscape. Helen's inner turmoil is repeatedly externalized onto the physical world — the road that forks, the trail that climbs, the horizon that recedes. The Western setting makes the novel's central metaphor of diverging roads not just a figure of speech but a literal feature of the terrain.

The tale of California, as the novel's epigraph-like opening suggests, is "an epic, an immortal song of daring" (P02-001). But that epic is behind the characters; they live in its aftermath, navigating the ordinary consequences of extraordinary history.
