# Risk and Speculation

California was built on speculation — on the gamble that a patch of ground might contain gold, that a railroad would transform a desert into a city, that a deal made today would pay off tomorrow. *Diverging Roads* captures this speculative energy in its characters' economic choices and in the atmosphere of uncertainty that pervades their lives.

## Definition

Speculation in the early twentieth-century California context meant investing capital — or borrowed money — in ventures whose returns were uncertain: mining claims, land purchases, business startups. Unlike steady employment on the railroad, speculation offered the possibility of sudden wealth and the certainty of frequent ruin. It was an economic culture that rewarded boldness and punished caution in equal measure.

## In the Novel

Bert personifies the speculative impulse. His deals materialize and dissolve with dizzying speed; he ships suitcases express to Bakersfield on the Lark (012-008), always one step ahead of fortune. His charisma is inseparable from his risk-taking — he is exciting precisely because he is unreliable.

Helen is drawn to and repelled by speculation in turn. The telegraph office connects her to the world of market quotations, where "January wheat, February wheat, May corn, became a drama" (009-001). She learns to read the rhythms of commerce, but she also sees what speculation costs: the anxiety, the instability, the lives wrecked by a bad bet.

The novel's setting in the aftermath of the Gold Rush hangs over every economic decision. Mining, the original California gamble, killed Paul's father (001-009). The speculative economy that replaced it — land, railroads, telegraphs — offered new forms of risk and reward. The question the novel poses is whether any road leads to security, or whether all roads are, in the end, gambles.
