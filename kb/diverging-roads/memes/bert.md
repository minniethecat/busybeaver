# Bert

Bert Kennedy is the novel's embodiment of the speculative spirit — charming, mobile, unreliable, and exciting. Where [[paul|Paul]] represents the steady road of disciplined work, Bert represents the fork in the path: risk, unpredictability, and the possibility of a life unconstrained by [[community-and-belonging|small-town]] expectations. He is [[helen-davies|Helen]]'s husband and, for a time, the magnetic center of her world.

## Role in the Story

Bert enters [[helen-davies|Helen]]'s life as an alternative to [[paul|Paul]]. He moves through the world on his own terms — shipping suitcases express (012-008), chasing deals across [[the-american-west|California]], living by his wits rather than a salary. His relationship with [[helen-davies|Helen]] offers a vision of life built on excitement rather than security, on [[risk-and-speculation|speculation]] rather than the steady accumulation of a railroad career. He becomes her husband, and the two years of their marriage — spent in a San Francisco apartment hotel — are the novel's anatomy of a relationship built on unequal power, charm, and dependence.

## Biography

Little is revealed of Bert's origins — he arrives fully formed, as speculators do, without the backstory of family and town that defines [[helen-davies|Helen]] and [[paul|Paul]]. He operates in the spaces between established institutions: not the railroad but the deal, not the office but the hotel lobby, not the steady salary but the windfall and the loss. His mobility — "I'm going south tonight on the Lark" (012-008) — is both his freedom and his evasion of the commitments that bind other characters.

The two years of marriage reveal Bert's pattern: grand plans, magnetic enthusiasm, and inevitable collapse. After the "unexplained failure of his plan to go to Argentine" and a honeymoon that ended with "sneaking out of the hotel leaving an unpaid bill behind them" (012-004), his ambition turns to real estate. He persuades [[clark-and-hayward|Clark & Hayward]] to give him an unlimited letter of credit, launches the [[ripley-farmland-acres|Ripley Farmland Acres]] campaign, and briefly seems destined for triumph before overreaching into disaster.

### The Husband

Two years into their marriage, Helen's perception of Bert crystallizes around the imbalance of their relationship. She has learned hard lessons from her two years of marriage:

> She had learned that men loved women for being beautiful, gay, unexacting, sweet-tempered always, docile without being bores. She had learned that men were infuriated by three things; questions, babies, and a woman who was ill. She had learned that success in business depended upon "putting up a front" and that a woman's part was to help in that without asking why or for what end. She had learned that the deepest need of her own nature was to be able to look up to the man she loved, even though she must go down on her own knees in order to do it. She knew that she adored her husband blindly, passionately, and that she dared not open her eyes for fear she would cease to do so. (011-001)

The morning routine of their marriage reveals its dynamic. Helen tiptoes through the darkened apartment while Bert sleeps, waiting "at any moment" for him to wake and call to her, unable to dress "for he saw a reproach in that, and was annoyed" (011-002). When collectors ring, she freezes:

> Her heart beat thickly, and the color faded from her face and then poured back in a burning flush. The bell rang again more imperatively. The very sound of it proclaimed that it was rung by a collector. (011-005)

Bert's response to her domestic longings reveals his worldview. When she proposes buying a house, he retorts: "It isn't the money you save; it's the money you make that counts" (011-023). And with casual brutality: "as long as I'm giving you three meals a day and glad rags, it isn't up to you to worry. I'll look after the finances if you'll take care of your complexion. It's beginning to need it" (011-025).

### The Salesman

Bert's salesmanship is the core of his identity and the source of both his success and his destruction. Mr. Clark's description captures the contradiction: "That young man has a truly remarkable personality as a salesman. He had us completely up in the air" (016-018).

Watching him work a room of skeptical salesmen, Helen sees his gift:

> Bert, lounging against the heaped-up pillows on the bed, with a cigar in his hand and whisky and ice-water at his elbow, let them talk until it seemed that despondency could not be more blacker, then suddenly sitting up, he poured upon them a flood of tingling words. His eyes glowed, his face was vividly keen and alive, and his magnetic charm played upon them like a tangible force. (013-004)

But his enthusiasm could turn to recklessness. The men he fired up one day would be "morose when they returned to the machine, and Bert's enthusiasm was forced" (013-004). When his elaborate campaign collapsed, he "had taken one of the firm's automobiles and disappeared" (016-018).

### The Fugitive

The news of Bert's downfall arrives in a newspaper item:

> Bert Kennedy Sought on Bad Check Charge (013-015)

Helen's response captures the novel's ambivalence about him. She feels "a blow on a spot numbed by an anesthetic" (013-016), then resolves to save him. "Money. Bert needed money. It was the only thing that stood between him and unthinkable horrors of suffering and disgrace" (013-016). She takes over his abandoned sales territory, sells his remaining prospects, and covers his debts.

When she finally meets Mr. Clark, she learns the full scope of Bert's obligations — five thousand dollars, including "the cost of the automobile" and expenses for "wine" and "expensive" recreations (016-018). The firm had "withdrawn the letter of credit and advised creditors in Bakersfield that the firm would no longer pay Mr. Kennedy's bills" (016-018). Bert, informed of this, had simply vanished.

Even after his disappearance, Bert remains a presence through his absence. A letter in her hotel box brings back the old hope:

> A letter from Clark & Hayward was in her box at the hotel. She tore it open quickly. As always, she had a wild thought that it contained news of Bert. (016-025)

The novel never resolves his fate definitively; he becomes, like the speculative economy he represents, a force that shapes without remaining.
