# The Frontier

By the time of *Diverging Roads*, the American frontier had officially closed — the Census Bureau declared it so in 1890. Yet the frontier persists in the novel as a psychological presence: the wild land beyond the town limits, the unbroken horizon, the possibility of starting over that draws characters westward. The frontier is what California was, and its memory shapes what California is becoming.

## Definition

Historian Frederick Jackson Turner argued in 1893 that the frontier experience — the encounter with wilderness, the necessity of self-reliance, the constant reinvention of social forms — had produced the distinctive American character. By the novel's era, the frontier had retreated into memory, but its mythology remained potent: the West as a place where the past could be shed and a new self forged.

## In the Novel

The frontier appears in *Diverging Roads* as landscape and as metaphor. The "level plain covered with dry grass" where "desolation was written upon it" (014-005) is the frontier's aftermath — land that was promised, settled, and found wanting. The small unpainted houses standing "like periods at the end of sentences expressing the futility of human hope" mark the gap between frontier mythology and agricultural reality.

Helen's restlessness is itself a frontier impulse — the desire to move, to break with the given, to find or make a new life. The novel's diverging roads are frontier roads: unmapped, uncertain, chosen by the traveler rather than prescribed by tradition. When the novel opens with the assertion that "the tale of California's early days is an epic, an immortal song of daring" (P02-001), it announces the frontier as both subject and elegy.

The frontier's closing leaves its mark on the novel's men — the loungers on Masonville's street corners are, in a sense, frontiersmen without a frontier, their restlessness curdled into aimlessness (001-001).
