## Overview

Bakersfield is a city in California's southern San Joaquin Valley that serves as a key location in Bert Kennedy's itinerant land-sales career. The novel uses Bakersfield to sketch the geography of California's oil-boom economy — a landscape of sudden fortunes and sudden disasters, where land salesmen chase prospects across the desert from one "rotten territory" to the next.

## Bert's Sales Campaigns

### Departure and Collapse

Bert's connection to Bakersfield is established when he calls Helen to announce a sudden departure:

> "Helen? Bert. I'm going south tonight on the Lark. Pack my suitcases and ship 'em express to Bakersfield, will you?" [015-1908]

The Bakersfield campaign ends in failure — a pattern that recurs throughout Bert's career:

> She gathered from their bitter talk that they were land salesmen, that a campaign in Bakersfield had ended in some sudden disaster — "blown up," they said — and that they found a miserable pleasure in repeating that Coalinga was a "rotten territory." [015-1976]

### The Oil Leases

Bakersfield is situated in oil country, and Bert's work there involves driving prospects out to view leases:

> Apparently he had bought it in Bakersfield, for it had been some months since he had sold the gray one. In the afternoon they drove out to the oil leases, and she sat in the machine while the salesmen scattered to look for land-buyers. [015-1994]

The "large green automobile" — purchased in Bakersfield with the proceeds of a previous sale — is a typical detail of Bert's boom-and-bust cycle: the visible symbol of success that precedes the next collapse.

## Geographic Context

Bakersfield is part of the southern San Joaquin Valley oil and agricultural region, south of Coalinga and north of Los Angeles. In the novel's economic geography, it represents the frontier of speculative development — a place where men like Bert chase quick fortunes in land that may or may not yield oil.
