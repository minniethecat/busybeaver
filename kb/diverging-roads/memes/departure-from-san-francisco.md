# Departure from San Francisco

Helen Davies's final journey out of San Francisco — a steamer passage through the Golden Gate that carried her away from the city she had called home for over a decade, toward an uncertain future in Guatemala.

## Before the Departure

The departure had been long in coming. Bert was already in Guatemala, and Helen had spent her final weeks in San Francisco disentangling herself from her work, her house on Jones Street, and the network of relationships she had built. The decision to leave had been made — less a choice than an acceptance of forces larger than her own will.

On the morning of departure, Helen "clutching her bag, her purse, her gloves, slammed the door of the little house and ran down the stairs of Jones Street to the waiting cab" (027-034). Sara accompanied her, and the taxi carried them through the city's hilly streets, past Chinatown's markets and Grant Avenue's polished facades, toward the waterfront.

## The Departure

The steamer slipped past the landmarks of San Francisco in a slow dissolve: "When the last sight of the dear gray city was lost, when the Ferry Tower, the high cliffs of Telegraph, the castle-like height of Russian Hill, the Presidio, Cliff House, the beach, had sunk into grayness on the horizon, she went down to her stateroom" (027-034).

In her stateroom, Helen found gifts from those she was leaving behind — "long striped boxes that held flowers, baskets of fruit, square silver-corded packages that spoke of bonbons, others large and small. She had not known that so many people cared" (027-034). The surprise of being loved, of having mattered to people, arrived too late to change her course but not too late to register.

## Significance

The departure from San Francisco is the novel's emotional climax. Every San Francisco landmark that Helen watches disappear — Telegraph Hill, Russian Hill, the Presidio, Cliff House — has been woven through her story. The city that had once been the distant wonder of Paul's letters, the intimidating metropolis of her first telegraphy job, the landscape of her ambition and her heartbreak — all of it receded into the gray Pacific horizon.

The departure is both an ending and a beginning. Helen leaves behind not just a city but an identity: the telegraph operator, the land saleswoman, the magazine writer, the boardinghouse resident, the Jones Street homeowner. What she will become in Guatemala is unknown, to the reader and perhaps to Helen herself.
