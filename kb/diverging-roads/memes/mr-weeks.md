# Mr. Weeks

Mr. Weeks is the telegraphy instructor who trains [[helen-davies|Helen Davies]] in Sacramento, launching her professional career and providing the novel's foundational example of benevolent male authority — the mentorship against which later figures like [[mr-roberts|Mr. Roberts]] and [[mr-bryant|Mr. Bryant]] are measured. Yet Weeks is a more complex figure than the word "mentor" suggests: his personal failure — the alcoholism that leaves Helen stranded without a diploma or a job — is as formative to her journey as his instruction.

## First Meeting

Helen first encounters Mr. Weeks upon arriving in Sacramento with Mrs. Updike. She had responded to an advertisement in the Masonville weekly paper promising that graduates of Weeks's school would earn "seventy-five to a hundred dollars a month" (003-039), and her parents had mortgaged the farm to raise the fifty-dollar tuition. Helen and Mrs. Updike "worried at intervals, fearing lest by some mischance Mr. Weeks, the manager of the school, would fail to meet them at the Sacramento station. They wore bits of red yarn in their buttonholes so that he would recognize them" (003-047).

The school that Weeks shows them is modest: "two rooms, a small one, which was the office, and a larger one, bare and not very clean, lighted by two high windows looking out on an alley. In the large room were half a dozen tables, each with a telegraph-sounder and key upon it" (003-049). He explains that the school does no business on Saturday afternoons but will make an exception for Helen. He is brisk, reassuring, businesslike — everything a worried farm girl and her chaperone could want in a school manager.

## Instruction and Absence

Weeks's teaching is erratic. "Sometimes its sounder opened, and they knew that Mr. Weeks was going to send them something to copy. They moved to that table eagerly. There were days when the sounder did not click again, and after a while one of the boys would tiptoe to the office and report that Mr. Weeks was asleep" (004-003). Then "for several days Mr. Weeks would not come to the school at all. They sank into a kind of stupor, sitting in the close, warm room, while flies buzzed on the windowpane" (004-004).

Despite this negligence, Helen persists. "She kept at work doggedly, knowing how much depended upon her success. Always before her was the vision of the station where she would work with Paul, a little yellow station with housekeeping rooms upstairs" (004-004). The need is desperate: her money is dwindling, her family has borrowed to send her, and there is no path back to Masonville that does not mean failure.

## The Revelation

Near the end of her planned three months, Helen gathers her courage to ask Weeks about a job. "She watched his shaking hand fumbling with some papers on his desk, and felt pityingly that she should not bother him when he was sick. But desperation drove her on. She did not suspect the truth until he looked up at her with reddened eyes and answered incoherently. Then she saw that he was drunk" (004-017).

The discovery is devastating but clarifying. Helen realizes she "could hope for nothing from the telegraph school" (004-022). With only two dollars, a half-used meal-ticket, and a week's rent, she takes the initiative that defines her character: she walks into the Western Union office managed by Mr. Roberts and announces herself as "a graduate of Weeks' School of Telegraphy" (005-007) — a credential from an institution whose manager has just betrayed her.

## Significance

Mr. Weeks's importance to the novel operates on two levels. On the surface, he establishes the baseline of appropriate professional conduct: an older, experienced man who teaches a younger woman a marketable skill without exploiting his position. This baseline makes the subsequent violations — Roberts's predation, Monroe's condescension — legible as deviations from a norm.

On a deeper level, however, Weeks's failure is the novel's first lesson in self-reliance. The drunk instructor is the broken bridge: Helen cannot depend on his certificate, his connections, or his guarantee. She must present herself to the world on her own terms, armed only with the skill she has taught herself in his mostly empty classroom. Later, she tells Roberts "about the farm and the mortgage and the failure of the fruit crop, even, shamefaced, about Mr. Weeks' drinking" (005-093) — the story of Weeks's failure becomes part of her own narrative of perseverance.
