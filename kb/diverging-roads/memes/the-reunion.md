# Helen and Paul's Reunion

When Helen meets Paul at the San Francisco train station, the reunion is charged with the accumulated distance of their separation. She has been waiting "for some time, her pulses fluttering with excitement," but "her self-confidence deserted her when she saw the crowds pouring from the cars" (006-001). The boy she knew in Masonville has become a man — familiar and strange in equal measure.

## Summary

Helen shrinks "back into the waiting-room doorway" as she watches for Paul among the crowd (006-001). When they finally meet, the conversation navigates the terrain between who they were and who they have become. The reunion does not resolve their relationship; it clarifies its impossibility. The roads have diverged too far.

## Significance

The reunion is the novel's most direct dramatization of its central theme. Two people who once shared dreams — telegraphy, escape, a future together — now occupy different worlds. The station, where trains arrive and depart, is the perfect setting for a meeting that is also a parting.
