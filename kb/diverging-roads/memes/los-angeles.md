## Overview

Los Angeles appears in the novel primarily through the Kennedy family — former Judge G. H. Kennedy, controller of the Central Trust Company, and his son [[gilbert-kennedy|Gilbert Kennedy]]. The city serves as a distant backdrop of money, indictment, and family estrangement, casting a shadow over Gilbert's life in San Francisco and his relationship with [[helen-davies|Helen Davies]].

## The Kennedy Family

### Old Money and Scandal

The Kennedys are introduced through Louise's gossip as "one of those Los Angeles Kennedys" — a phrase freighted with notoriety:

> "Well, he's one of those Los Angeles Kennedys. You know, old man was indicted for something awhile ago. Loads of money." Louise, dabbing on cold-cream, spoke in jerks. "His brother was the one that ran off with Cissy Leroy, and his wife shot her up. Don't you remember? It was in all the papers." [009-1278]

The Kennedy family history — indictment, scandal, public notoriety — is concentrated in the name "Los Angeles," which functions almost as a metonym for a certain kind of California wealth: big, corrupt, and morally untethered.

### The Central Trust Company

Gilbert's first appearance in the narrative involves a telegram to his father at the Central Trust Company in Los Angeles:

> "G. H. Kennedy, Central Trust Company, Los Angeles. Drawing on you for five hundred. Must have it. Absolutely sure thing this time. Full explanations follow by letter. Gilbert." [009-1064]

The message reveals the financial dependency that defines Gilbert's relationship with his father — a pattern of urgent requests and failed promises.

### The Warrant

The Los Angeles connection reaches its culmination when Gilbert's criminal troubles become public:

> Charging Gilbert H. Kennedy, well-known along the city's joy zones, with cashing a bogus check for a hundred dollars on the Metropolitan National Bank, Judge C. K. Washburne yesterday issued a warrant for the arrest of the young man on a felony charge. The police search for Kennedy and his young wife, a former candy-store girl, has so far proved fruitless. Interviewed at his residence in Los Angeles last night, former Judge G. H. Kennedy, father of the missing man, controller of the Central Trust Company until his indictment some years ago for mishandling its funds, denied knowledge of his son's whereabouts. [016-2039]

The newspaper report collapses distance: Los Angeles is where the elder Kennedy denies his son, where the "former candy-store girl" (Helen) is named, and where the family's public unraveling is documented.
