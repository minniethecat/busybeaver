# Economic Ambition

The impulse to rise — to earn more, own more, and be more — drives the characters of *Diverging Roads* through choices that echo across decades. Economic ambition in the novel is both creative and destructive: it lifts Paul from a dead miner's son to a man with prospects, it carries Helen from the Masonville farmhouse to the San Francisco telegraph office and beyond, and it ensnares Bert in a cycle of speculation that nearly destroys them both. The novel treats money not as a backdrop but as a character in its own right — counted, borrowed, earned, and lost in sums that the narrative records with almost documentary precision.

## Definition

In the context of early twentieth-century America, economic ambition was inseparable from the mythology of the West — the belief that hard work and daring could transform anyone's circumstances. The closing of the frontier had shifted this energy from land to commerce, from homesteading to speculation, from physical courage to financial risk. *Diverging Roads* captures this transitional moment with unusual clarity: its characters are no longer pioneers but they are not yet salaried professionals. They inhabit a world where telegraphy is a high-tech career, where land speculation is a respectable gamble, and where a woman earning her own living is still remarkable enough to be the subject of a novel.

## In the Novel

### Paul: The Steady Climb

Paul embodies the classic arc of self-made ambition. Orphaned by a mining accident — his father killed in the very industry that shaped the region — he studies furiously, learns telegraphy, and climbs the railroad's professional ladder. His determination is captured early:

> "If a fellow really went at that and worked hard, there was no telling what it might lead to" (002-026) His ambition is clear-eyed, unromantic, and effective. He envisions a future at "seventy-five dollars a month" (003-028) — a specific sum, a real wage, the kind of money that can support a wife and a household.

> "seventy-five dollars a month" (003-028)

His is the ambition of deferred gratification: work now, enjoy later.

But Paul's ambition also carries a controlling impulse. He articulated a vision of domesticity that Helen would come to resist:

> "He did not want her to work with him at Ripley; she would have to wait until he made money enough to support her" (009-003) His vision of success is gendered in ways he cannot see: he climbs so that Helen can wait. The tension between his conception of prosperity and hers is one of the novel's central conflicts.

### Helen: Ambition Against the Grain

Helen's ambition is more complicated and, in the novel's telling, more heroic. She shares Paul's drive:

> "A girl like me has to work if she's going to get anywhere" (001-021) — but faces barriers he does not. The novel tracks her efforts to build a career in a world where women's work is undervalued and marriage is the expected destination.

Her economic journey is marked by specific sums that anchor the abstraction of ambition in material reality. Fifty dollars, borrowed against the family mortgage, "seemed a fortune on which to live for three months" (003-041) at telegraphy school. The school's advertisement promises graduates "seventy-five to a hundred dollars a month" (003-039) — sums that represent liberation. Mr. Bryant's telegram offers "night duty St. Francis hotel forty-five dollars a month" (007-046) — her first real wage, her first step into economic independence. She owes "her mother a hundred dollars — months of privation and heartbreaking work" (007-021) — a debt that measures the cost of her ambition in more than dollars.

Helen's sales career represents the fullest expression of her economic agency. She discovers a talent she did not know she possessed: "The skill in handling another's thoughts before they took form, learned in her life with Bert, enabled her to impress these pictures upon her hearer's mind so that they seemed his own, and grimy men in oil-soaked overalls, listening to her without combativeness because she was a woman and not to be taken seriously in business, felt that they must buy this land so temptingly described" (015-005). The passage is remarkable for its layered irony: Helen succeeds because men underestimate her, and she uses skills developed in a marriage to advance in a profession her husband could not sustain.

The culmination is her MacAdams commission: "Helen came out of the office with three hundred dollars in her purse" (014-064). The sum is larger than any she has earned before — a validation of her skill and a repudiation of the assumption that a woman cannot succeed in business. "She would make money; she would clear Bert's name, which was hers; she would buy a little house and make it beautiful" (016-003) — ambition transformed from escape to construction, from running from something to building something.

### Bert: The Speculator

Bert represents a different form of ambition — speculative, impatient, charismatic. He chases deals and schemes with an energy that is magnetic and self-destructive in equal measure. "There's millions of dollars a year pouring out of these wells," he declares; "We're going to get ours, boys, believe me!" (012-053). His is the ambition of the boom, the conviction that wealth is a matter of being in the right place at the right time rather than patient accumulation.

Bert's ambition is also the most conventionally masculine — expansive, risk-taking, performative — and the novel is clear-eyed about its costs. The money flows and vanishes around him: Helen must sell the farm he failed to sell, negotiate the commission he could not close, salvage the reputation he has damaged. His ambition creates the crisis that hers must resolve.

## The Diverging Roads of Ambition

The novel's title finds one of its meanings in the diverging economic paths its characters follow. Paul's steady climb leads to security but also to a kind of emotional constriction — "You didn't use to be like this a bit, Helen," he says, unable to recognize the woman ambition has made (010-049). Bert's speculative ventures lead to excitement and collapse. Helen's path — the hardest, the most improvised, the one that must navigate a world not designed for it — leads to something like self-possession.

Late in the novel, Helen walks through the San Francisco streets accompanied by a vision of her younger self:

> "the ghost of the girl who had come down from Masonville, dazzled, wide-eyed, so pitifully sure of herself, to learn to telegraph" (018-008) The economic ambition that sent that girl down from Masonville has been realized — but at a cost the girl could not have calculated, and in ways she could not have predicted. The novel's final judgment on ambition is neither celebration nor condemnation but something rarer: a clear-eyed accounting of what it costs to want more than the world intends to give you.
