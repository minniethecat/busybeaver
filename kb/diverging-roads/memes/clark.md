# Mr. Clark

The head of [[clark-and-hayward|Clark & Hayward]], a [[san-francisco|San Francisco]] real-estate firm, whose tense, shrewd
pragmatism defines the business world that [[helen-davies|Helen]] must navigate after her [[marriage-and-courtship|marriage]].

## Role in the Story

Mr. Clark is the institutional face of the real-estate world in which much of the novel's
middle section unfolds. As head of [[clark-and-hayward|Clark & Hayward]], he employs [[helen-davies|Helen]] [[gilbert-kennedy|Kennedy]] as a land
saleswoman and must deal with the aftermath of [[bert|Bert]] [[gilbert-kennedy|Kennedy]]'s fraudulent use of the firm's
letter of credit. He is neither villain nor benefactor — he is a businessman operating by the
cold logic of the market, occasionally capable of a "kindly expression" but never at the
expense of the firm's interests. His interactions with [[helen-davies|Helen]] illustrate the power dynamics
between capital and labor, men and women, in early twentieth-century commerce. (013-015)

## Biography

### Partnership at Clark & Hayward

Clark was the senior partner at Clark & Hayward, the San Francisco real estate firm that served as a nexus for the novel's land speculation plotlines. His partnership with Hayward represented a significant position in the city's business hierarchy, giving him authority over transactions that shaped the fortunes of characters like Helen and Bert.

### Relationship with Helen

Clark's professional relationship with Helen Davies evolved from employer-employee to a more complex dynamic as her talent for sales became apparent. He recognized her ability to close deals that his male agents could not, and his willingness to give her opportunities — despite the gender conventions of the era — made him an unusual figure in the novel's treatment of professional advancement.

Mr. Clark is described as "a lean, shrewd-looking man about forty-five years old" who "gave
the impression of having kept his nerves at high tension for so many years that now he must
s[[the-railroad|train]] them still tighter or relax altogether." He lives in dread of "losing his grip," which
he would consider "the ultimate calamity." [[helen-davies|Helen]] senses that "he would not be cruel unless
there were some purpose to be achieved by it," though there is "even a kindly expression in
his eyes at times." (013-015)

When [[bert|Bert]] [[gilbert-kennedy|Kennedy]] first approaches [[clark-and-hayward|Clark & Hayward]], Mr. Clark is won over by [[bert|Bert]]'s charisma:
"That young man has a truly remarkable personality as a salesman. He had us completely up in
the air." [[bert|Bert]] proposes a gigantic selling campaign in the oil fields, and [[clark-and-hayward|Clark & Hayward]]
give him an unlimited letter of credit and free rein. [[bert|Bert]] imports fifty salesmen, gathers
thousands of leads, and promises special [[the-railroad|train]]s for buyers — but expenses run far ahead of
actual sales, and disturbing rumors begin to surface. (013-015)

When the firm investigates and discovers [[bert|Bert]] is spending money on personal expenses —
including wine and other unmentionable "recreations" — Clark withdraws the letter of credit.
[[bert|Bert]] responds by taking a company automobile and disappearing. The firm is left with
substantial losses, and a warrant is issued for [[bert|Bert]] on a bad-check charge. (013-015)

[[helen-davies|Helen]] first meets Mr. Clark when she arrives at his [[san-francisco|San Francisco]] office with a check from
[[nichols|Nichols]] and a signed contract from the MacAdams sale. She negotiates for a sales commission
that will pay down [[bert|Bert]]'s five-thousand-dollar debt to the firm. Clark starts by offering her
seven-and-a-half percent but concedes to ten percent after she holds her ground —
demonstrating both his bargaining instinct and his recognition of her value. (013-015)

Later, Clark sends [[hutchinson|Hutchinson]] and Monroe to take over the oil-field territory [[helen-davies|Helen]] has
developed — a decision that reflects the firm's assumption that a "young saleswoman" cannot
handle a territory alone, regardless of her proven competence. (012-080)
