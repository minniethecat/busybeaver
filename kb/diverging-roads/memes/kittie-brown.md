## Overview

Kittie Brown is a woman in [[masonville|Masonville]] who runs the boarding house where [[helen-davies|Helen Davies]] first stays upon arriving in town. She is the subject of pointed moral disapproval from [[mrs-campbell|Mrs. Campbell]], making her an early marker of the social judgments and [[community-and-belonging|small-town]] moral codes that [[helen-davies|Helen]] encounters.

## Role in the Narrative

Kittie Brown appears indirectly, through the reactions of others to [[helen-davies|Helen]]'s association with her. When [[helen-davies|Helen]] arrives at the [[telegraphy|telegraph]] office in [[masonville|Masonville]] and mentions she is staying with Kittie Brown, the response is immediate and charged:

> "You're staying with Kittie Brown?" Again a glance passed over her head. Both of them looked at her with intensified interest, for which she saw no reason. "Yes," she replied. She felt keenly that it was an awkward moment, and bewilderment added to her confusion. [003-0472]

The nature of the scandal is later made explicit by [[mrs-campbell|Mrs. Campbell]]:

> "Well," — Mrs. Campbell drew a long breath and plunged — "I suppose you know the sort of person this Kittie Brown, she calls herself, is? I suppose you know she's a bad woman?" [003-0496]

## Thematic Significance

Kittie Brown serves as an early introduction to the social stratification and moral policing of [[masonville|Masonville]]. [[helen-davies|Helen]]'s innocent association with her — she simply needed a place to stay — immediately marks [[helen-davies|Helen]] as an outsider unaware of local codes. The episode foreshadows the broader theme of [[morality-and-honor|reputation]]al judgment and the constraints [[community-and-belonging|small-town]] society places on women.

[[mrs-campbell|Mrs. Campbell]]'s intervention, while protective in intent, also reveals the mechanisms by which women police other women's behavior and associations in the novel's social world.
