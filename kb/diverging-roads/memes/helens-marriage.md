# Helen's Marriage

Helen Davies and Paul were married impulsively at the Sacramento river landing, after a boat journey from San Francisco — a marriage born of passion and fear, consummated without ceremony or witnesses beyond the night.

## Before the Marriage

The marriage was neither planned nor prepared. Helen and Paul had spent the day together on a riverboat journey from Lancaster to Sacramento — a journey Helen had already imagined as a kind of honeymoon. Paul's mood had shifted from playful to urgent, and when they were alone on the boat, his intensity overwhelmed her hesitation.

"We can be married — we will be — just as soon as we get to Sacramento," Paul said, his kisses smothering her protests. "The very first thing in the morning! We'll manage somehow. I'll always love you just as much" (008-014). Helen was caught between her longing for him and a sense that events were moving too fast, beyond her control.

## The Marriage

The marriage itself passed without description in the novel — a gap that speaks to its impulsive, almost furtive character. After the boat docked at the Sacramento landing at twilight, Helen emerged from the stateroom "neither asleep nor awake, pushing her hair back from a face that seemed sodden and lifeless. A pale twilight filled the stateroom" (008-014). The wedding, evidently performed somewhere in Sacramento the following morning, was not a celebration but a transaction — a legal act that bound them together without the rhythms of courtship or community.

The narrative jumps from the boat's arrival to the aftermath: Helen and Paul were married, and the reality of what they had done began to settle over both of them. There had been no family to witness it, no church, no celebration — only the two of them, making an irreversible decision in the gray light of a Sacramento morning.

## Significance

The marriage marks a decisive pivot in Helen's story. It was the culmination of years of longing and separation, but it arrived not as a fulfillment but as a complication. The furtive circumstances of the wedding — the absence of family, the rushed timing, Paul's insistence overriding Helen's uncertainty — prefigure the difficulties that would follow.
