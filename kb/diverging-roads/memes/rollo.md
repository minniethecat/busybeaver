# Rollo

A small town on the railroad line in California, known in the novel only for its telegraph station — where a young married couple lived above the depot and worked the station between them, becoming the model of domestic partnership that haunted Helen's imagination.

## Description

Rollo existed as little more than a name on the railroad line, a stop on the route through California's rural interior. Its defining feature was the depot: a telegraph station where "a man and his wife handle the station between them. He works nights, and she works daytimes. They live over the depot, and if anything goes wrong she can call him" (003-005).

This arrangement — two people sharing work and life, their professional and domestic spheres perfectly merged — crystallized an ideal that Helen and Paul could only approach playfully. "They made a sort of play of it, laughing about it. They were only supposing, of course. They carefully refrained from voicing the thought that clamored behind everything they said" (003-005).

## Significance

Rollo represents the life Helen and Paul imagined but never achieved. The young couple at Rollo — a telegraph operator and his wife, sharing the work, living above the station — embodied a vision of marriage as true partnership. For Helen, separated from Paul by distance and circumstance, "the thought of that young couple at Rollo" became a recurring pang of envy (003-004).

The town's name recurs at critical moments of separation. When Paul had gone to San Francisco and Ripley while Helen remained trapped in Masonville, "she thought again of that young couple at Rollo, and pangs of envy were added to the misery in which she was living" (003-004). Rollo was never a destination in the novel — it was always a counterfactual, the road not taken.
