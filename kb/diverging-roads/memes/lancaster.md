# Lancaster

A small river town on the route between San Francisco and Sacramento, where passengers transferred between boats — the place where Helen and Paul shared their first taste of a life together over small sandwiches and milk in a white-painted dining room.

## Description

Lancaster was a transfer point on the riverboat line connecting San Francisco and Sacramento, a place of brief stops and hurried connections. When Helen and Paul traveled together, "they had to get off at Lancaster in order to catch the afternoon boat back to Sacramento" (007-004). The town itself was barely described — a levee, a few "lounging Japanese" dock workers with "expressionless, slant eyes," and a white-painted dining room on the boat.

The dining room, with its "smooth linen, the shining silver, and the imposing waiters," was a world of luxury that both confused and delighted Helen (007-004). The prices on the elaborate menu cards terrified her, but Paul — despite his own "worried calculation" — overruled her protests and led her in.

## Significance

Lancaster marks a pivotal moment in Helen and Paul's relationship. Waiting for the afternoon boat to Sacramento, Paul insisted they eat in the white-painted dining room — an act of courtship that crossed from the practical into the romantic. As Helen sat "happily at the white table, eating small delicious sandwiches and drinking milk, smiling across immaculate linen at Paul," she "played with the fancy that it was a honeymoon trip, and a thrill ran along her nerves" (007-004).

This moment of pretense — of trying on the idea of being married, of sharing a life — prefigures their actual impulsive marriage later that same journey. Lancaster was where they first rehearsed being a couple, however briefly, in the suspended time between boats.
