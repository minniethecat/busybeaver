# Paul

Paul is [[helen-davies|Helen Davies's]] first love and the character who most clearly embodies *Diverging Roads*'s ethic of steady, disciplined [[education-and-advancement|self-improvement]]. The son of a [[mining|mine]]r killed at [[cherokee|Cherokee]], he pursues [[education-and-advancement|education]] and [[telegraphy]] as a means of escaping the poverty that claimed his father. In the novel's central romantic geometry, Paul represents the road of security and patience — the path [[helen-davies|Helen]] might have taken, and the one she cannot quite bring herself to choose.

## Character Portrait

The novel introduces Paul with economical precision:

> He was a stocky, dark-haired boy, with blue eyes. His father was dead, killed in a mine over at Cherokee. He had come down to the Masonville school, and they were in the same class, the class that would graduate that spring. He was studying hard, trying to get as much education as possible before he would have to go to work. (001-008)

His lips are "full and very firm," and when he sets them tightly "he looked determined. There was something obstinate about the line of his chin and the slight frown between his heavy black brows" (001-011). This portrait — sturdy, resolute, physically grounded — establishes him as a figure of solidity against [[bert|Bert's]] mercurial energy.

## Role in the Story

Paul is [[helen-davies|Helen]]'s counterweight — the steady road she might have taken. He studies hard, sets up a [[telegraphy|telegraph]] set in the woodshed, and shares with [[helen-davies|Helen]] a vision of shared professional lives modeled on a young married couple they know. His philosophy is the [[social-class|class]]ic self-made creed:

> Now here was the idea of learning telegraphy. That looked pretty good. If a fellow really went at that and worked hard, there was no telling what it might lead to. You might get to be a train-dispatcher or even a railroad superintendent. There were lots of big men who didn't have any better start than he had. Look at Edison. (002-026)

His departure from [[masonville|Masonville]] for the [[ripley|Ripley station]] marks the novel's first divergence: he leaves, [[helen-davies|Helen]] stays, and the distance between them grows into something permanent. When they reunite at the [[san-francisco|San Francisco]] station, the "awkward silence between them" (006-008) measures the gap that time and separate experience have opened.

The telegraph set in the woodshed becomes the emblem of his ambition:

> "I'm going to learn to be an operator," he said. "I've got most of the alphabet already. Listen." He made the instrument click. "I'm going to practise receiving, listening to the wires in the depot. Morrison says I can after I get through work. Telegraph-operators make as much as seventy dollars a month, and some of them, on the fast wires, make a hundred. I guess the train-dispatcher makes more than that." (001-012)

## Biography

Paul's biography is the [[social-class|class]]ic self-made narrative. Orphaned by a [[mining]] accident, he pursues [[telegraphy|telegraph]]y as his ladder out of working-[[social-class|class]] obscurity. He believes that hard work and skill can overcome any obstacle. He rises through the railroad's professional hierarchy — from the [[masonville|Masonville]] depot to night operator at [[ripley|Ripley]] station in the San Joaquin valley, and eventually to more responsible positions on the fast wires.

His departure scene reveals the weight of the obligations that drive him:

> "I got to go," he said. "It's a good job. Fifty dollars a month. I got to support mother, you know. Her money's pretty nearly gone already, and she spent a lot putting me through school. I just got to go. I wish — I wish I didn't have to." (003-021)

But the novel suggests that his very discipline may also be a limitation. His vision of the future is unwaveringly conventional: a good job, a wife who keeps house, steady advancement. When [[helen-davies|Helen]] begins to want things that don't fit this template, Paul's inability to imagine a different kind of life becomes a barrier. His decency is genuine, but it is also narrow — he "hates to see a girl go to work" (001-010), a sentiment [[helen-davies|Helen]] experiences as both protective and suffocating.

> "Gee, I hate to see a girl go to work," said Paul. (001-010)

The reunion in San Francisco is painful in its awkwardness — two people who once shared everything now struggling to find common ground. They go somewhere to talk, and Paul's admission is simple but devastating:

> "It is rather hard to get started," he admitted. "You look different than I thought you would, somehow. But I guess we haven't changed much really." (006-003)

## Character Analysis

### The Self-Made Man

Paul represents a distinctly American ideal: the working-[[social-class|class]] boy who rises through discipline, skill, and perseverance. His [[telegraphy|telegraph]]y set in the woodshed, his relentless studying after long days of physical labor, his careful planning for the future — these are the habits of the self-made man in a country that promises that effort equals reward. His invocation of Edison is telling: the inventor as proof that "lots of big men" began with nothing.

The novel treats this ideal with respect but also with critical distance. Paul's self-making requires a certain narrowness of vision. He cannot imagine a future that doesn't look like the one he has planned, and this inability to improvise — to deviate from the script — ultimately costs him [[helen-davies|Helen]].

### The Road Not Taken

[[helen-davies|Helen]]'s relationship with Paul is the novel's central "road not taken." Had she chosen him, she would have chosen security, [[social-class|respectability]], a life of quiet domestic comfort. The novel never dismisses this life as worthless — Paul is never a villain or even a bad match. His tragedy is that he is good, but not sufficient. [[helen-davies|Helen]]'s reasons for not choosing him have less to do with his flaws than with her own restlessness, a quality the novel treats as neither virtue nor vice but simply as fact.

The bridge scene, where Paul and Helen part, captures the moment of their first divergence with heartbreaking clarity:

> "Oh, Helen! Oh, Helen!" She was crushed against the beating of his heart, his arms hurt her. She wanted them to hurt her. "You're so — you're so — sweet!" he stammered, and gropingly they found each other's lips. (003-022)

And then, the irrevocable resolution:

> "I got to go," he said. The finality of the words was like something solid beneath their feet once more. (003-023)
