# Bob Mason

Grandson of "Old Man" Mason — one of Masonville's founders — and head clerk at Robertson's store. He marries [[mabel|Mabel Davies]], linking the Davies farming family to the town's founding commercial dynasty. His life in Masonville represents the settled path of small-town respectability — the road Helen consciously chose not to take.

## Role in the Story

Bob Mason embodies the respectable small-town trajectory that [[helen-davies|Helen Davies]] rejects. While Helen pursues telegraphy in Sacramento, real estate in Ripley, and eventually journalism abroad, Bob Mason stays in Masonville, rises to head clerk at Robertson's store, and marries Helen's younger sister — living out the settled life of commerce, marriage, and local standing that the novel presents as one valid road, if not Helen's.

His presence in the story is mostly indirect — through Mabel's letters, through the mention of "young Mason" at the bank, through the lore of Mason's bar — but the Mason name carries weight. As the grandson of the man who built Masonville's dance-hall and ordered a billiard table sent from San Francisco during the boom years (P02-003), Bob inherits a legacy of frontier entrepreneurship now domesticated into store clerking and respectable marriage.

## Biography

The Mason family is woven into Masonville's origin story. When Cherokee was booming and miners came down with their sacks of gold-dust, "Mason, prospering, ordered a billiard-table sent up from San Francisco, built a dance-hall" (P02-003). Mason's bar became the town's social hub — the place where old-timers told stories of [[jim-thane|Jim Thane]] striking it rich on North Branch, of stagecoach holdups and miners' windfalls.

The founder's prosperity was brief but legendary: "Those were the great days of Cherokee, days of feverish activity, of hard, fierce living, of marvelous event. The tales came down to Masonville, where the stage stopped to change horses, and drivers, express-messengers, and prospectors gathered in Mason's bar" (P02-002).

A generation later, "young Mason" appears at the bank — the man [[helen-davies|Helen]]'s father approaches to renew a loan: "See if I can get young Mason to renew it" (002-043). This is Bob Mason, "Old Man" Mason's grandson, now a figure in the town's financial and commercial establishment. The loan renewal places Bob Mason at the center of the town's economic machinery, empowered to grant or deny the Davies family's request — a quiet inversion of the town's social hierarchy.

Bob Mason's most significant appearance is through Mabel's letter to Helen announcing her engagement. She is marrying "the most wonderful man in the world, Bob Mason, 'Old Man' Mason's grandson, who was head clerk of Robertson's store" (016-027). The announcement triggers one of Helen's most poignant reactions: the rose lunch cloth she had been embroidering — a symbol of domestic hope — became "something Helen could not keep. It was too keenly a symbol of all that she had missed, all that she wanted her little sister to have" (016-028). Helen sends it to Mabel "in a rose-lined white box, with a letter and a check" (016-028).

The marriage is a "splendid match" for a farmer's daughter — Mabel's letter is "awkwardly triumphant" about it, though she protests that "money isn't everything" (016-028). By the novel's end, Mabel and Bob have a baby, and Helen — passing through Masonville on her way to the Orient — sees Mabel "looking at her enviously over the baby's head" (024-001). The envy is mutual and circular: Mabel envies Helen's freedom, while Helen once envied Mabel's settled happiness.

## The Mason Legacy

The arc of the Mason family mirrors Masonville's trajectory: from frontier entrepreneurship to respectable commerce. "Old Man" Mason's bar was the center of a booming town; Bob Mason's clerk position at Robertson's store is the center of a sleepy one. The novel describes this decline: "The stirring times of enterprise and daring were gone forever. The epic had ended in bad verse. Masonville slipped quietly to sleep, like an old man sitting in the sun with his memories" (P02-006).

Bob Mason inherits not the fortune but the name — and in a town like Masonville, the name still opens doors. His marriage to Mabel Davies consolidates the Mason family's position as the town's leading commercial dynasty, even as the town itself fades from relevance.
