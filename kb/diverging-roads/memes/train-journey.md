# The Train Journey Through the Central Valley

Helen's train journey through California's Central Valley is the novel's most concentrated passage of introspection. The landscape scrolling past the window becomes a screen onto which her life is projected, and the metaphor of diverging roads receives its most explicit expression.

## Summary

The train runs "across a level plain covered with dry grass" where "desolation was written upon it, and small unpainted houses stood here and there like periods at the end of sentences expressing the futility of human hope" (014-005). Earlier, passing through Fresno, there was "leafy orchards and vineyards and the cool green of alfalfa fields" (014-003) — the contrast between cultivation and desolation maps Helen's own oscillation between hope and despair.

The journey crystallizes in a moment of pure metaphor: "Against the black square of the window her life arranged itself before her. How many times she had seen her life lying before her like a straight road, and how different it had been!" (016-002). The straight road she expected has become the diverging roads she must navigate.

## Significance

This journey is the novel's thematic center. The railroad, which throughout the novel has represented opportunity and escape, becomes here a vehicle for reckoning. Helen cannot outrun herself; the landscape keeps throwing her questions back at her.
