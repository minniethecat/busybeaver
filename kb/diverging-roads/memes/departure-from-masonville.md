# Departure from Masonville

Helen Davies' departure from [[masonville|Masonville]] is the pivotal event that transforms the novel from a story of small-town restlessness into a story of ambition, independence, and the cost of leaving home behind. It is not a single moment but a chain of decisions: the growing dissatisfaction, the determination to learn telegraphy, and the eventual journey down the railroad tracks beyond [[cherokee|Cherokee Hill]] into a world she can barely imagine.

The event also establishes the novel's structural pattern: someone leaves, someone is left behind, and neither person is ever quite the same. This pattern repeats with Paul's earlier departure, Helen's later departure from San Francisco, and Paul's departure for the river — each echoing and deepening the meaning of this first leave-taking.

## Background: A Town Asleep

By the time Helen comes of age, Masonville has already faded from its frontier prime. The town that was founded on mining wealth and the energy of Mason's bar has become a place where "the stirring times of enterprise and daring were gone forever. The epic had ended in bad verse. Masonville slipped quietly to sleep, like an old man sitting in the sun with his memories" (P02-006).

For a young person of ambition, this sleep is suffocating. The novel opens with Helen already alienated from her surroundings. Standing in the doorway of Richardson's store, "she said to herself that she would be glad never to see Masonville again. The familiar sight of its one drowsy street, the rickety wooden awnings over the sidewalks, the boys pitching horseshoes in the shade of the blacksmith-shop, was almost insupportable" (001-002). This is not a reasoned assessment but a visceral rejection — a young woman suffocating in a town where little has occurred for many years.

The novel paints this dissatisfaction in concrete terms: "Men sauntered past, going toward the station. The postmaster appeared in his shirtsleeves, pushing a wheelbarrow filled with mail sacks down the middle of the street" (001-004). Every detail conveys the same verdict: nothing happens here.

## The Decision to Leave

The decision crystallizes when [[paul|Paul]] leaves first. "He had gone down the shining lines of track beyond Cherokee Hill into a vague big world that baffled her thoughts" (003-038). Paul's departure transforms Helen's vague discontent into concrete action. His letters from San Francisco — describing "Chinatown, the Presidio, the beach, and Seal Rocks" — make the outside world real and desirable. But they also introduce fear: "she felt that she was losing touch with him, and when she contemplated two or three long years of waiting she felt that she would lose him entirely" (003-039). The terror of being left behind is more powerful than any positive ambition.

Two weeks after Paul leaves, Helen announces to her mother that she will become a telegraph operator (003-039). The notice in the Masonville weekly paper — "Operators in great demand. Graduates earn $75 to $100 a month up" — provides the practical justification, but the deeper motive is the fear of being the one who stays. She writes to the telegraphy school in Sacramento and receives a reply "assuring her that she could learn in three months, that railroad and telegraph companies were clamoring for operators, that the school guaranteed all its graduates good positions. The tuition was fifty dollars" (003-039).

## The Journey

Helen's actual departure scene is brief — she boards the train and watches Masonville recede: "Until the train rounded the curve south of town she gazed back at Masonville and the little yellow station where Paul had worked. Then she settled back against red velvet cushions to watch unfamiliar trees and hills flashing backward past the windows" (004-001). The narrative economy is telling: the novel is less interested in the drama of leaving than in its consequences.

Each subsequent step carries her further from the "old stale road home to the old farmhouse, which had not changed since she could remember" (001-003): the arrival in [[sacramento|Sacramento]], the telegraphy school, the first job, and eventually the move to [[san-francisco|San Francisco]]. The departure, once made, proves impossible to undo — not because Helen can never return to Masonville physically, but because she can never return to being the person who lived there.

## The Pattern of Return

The novel complicates the departure by staging a series of returns. Late in the story, Paul suggests Helen might go back to Masonville after Bert's departure. She is startled: "Why on earth should I go to Masonville?" (022-P01). When Paul points out that her mother is there, Helen's response reveals the irreversibility of her original leaving:

> "I'm fond of her and all that, you know I've been up to see her a good many times — but after all we've been apart a long time, and my life's been so different. She doesn't exactly know what to make of me. I honestly don't think either of us would be very happy if I were to go back there now." (022-P01)

The final return is the most poignant. Passing through Masonville on her way to the Orient, Helen sees Mabel "looking at her enviously over the baby's head" (024-001) — her sister, who stayed, envying Helen who left, who once envied Mabel's settled life. The novel refuses to resolve which road was the right one.

## Significance

Helen's departure from Masonville is more than a plot point; it is the thesis statement of the novel. Every subsequent event — her career, her marriages, her returns and re-departures — is a variation on this original leaving. The novel's title, *Diverging Roads*, finds its first and most consequential fork in the moment Helen chooses the railroad over the farmhouse, the unknown over the familiar.

The departure also establishes the novel's emotional grammar: looking back with a mix of nostalgia and relief, the peculiar loneliness of having outgrown where you came from, and the understanding — which Helen only fully grasps years later, standing on [[russian-hill|Russian Hill]] — that "you can't go home again — it isn't there to go back to" (022-P02).
