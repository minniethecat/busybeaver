# Sacramento

The capital city of California, where Helen Davies first left her rural home to study telegraphy and where she later returned as a successful land saleswoman — a place marking both her youthful vulnerability and her mature professional triumph.

## Description

In the early 1900s, Sacramento was "a big town" with a railway station where strangers were identified by bits of red yarn in their buttonholes (003-P48). By the 1910s, it had transformed:

> Sacramento had changed. It had been a big town; it was now a city, radiating interurban lines, thrusting tall buildings toward the sky, smudging that sky with the smoke of factories and canneries. Its streets were sluggishly moving floods of automobiles; its wharves were crowded with boats; across the wide, yellow river spans of new bridges were reaching toward each other. (018-P09)

The Sacramento River served as a major transportation artery; Helen and Paul traveled by boat between Sacramento and San Francisco, arriving at the "Sacramento landing" on the riverfront after their fateful overnight journey (006-P97). The city's hotel district accommodated traveling salespeople like Helen, who "came exhausted into the Sacramento hotel late the third night, with no thought in her mind but a bath and bed" (017-P88).

## Helen's Two Sacramentos

### The Girl Who Arrived

Helen's first Sacramento is a city of bewilderment. She arrives as a country girl, clutching a fifty-dollar tuition payment, identified by a bit of red yarn pinned to her buttonhole so that Mr. Weeks, the telegraphy school proprietor, could recognize her at the station (003-P48). The advertisement in the Masonville paper had promised: "Operators in great demand. Graduates earn $75 to $100 a month up" (003-P40). The reality fell far short.

The loneliness of those early months is acute:

> The thought of Paul was the one clear reality in Helen's life while she blundered through the bewilderments of the first months in Sacramento. It was the only thing that warmed her in the midst of the strangeness that surrounded her like a thin, cold fog. (004-P01)

She ate poorly, slept in a rented room, and endured the advances of a fellow student who "looked at Helen with eyes she did not like, and once asked her to go to a show with him" (004-P08). She refused. Paul would not have liked it. "At the end of three months in Sacramento these were the only people she knew, except Mrs. Brown" (004-P08).

### The Marriage Landing

It is at the Sacramento landing that Helen and Paul impulsively decide to marry. Returning by boat from a day trip, they find themselves alone on deck, and Paul's determination sweeps Helen along:

> "We can be married — we will be — just as soon as we get to Sacramento." His kisses smothered her. "The very first thing in the morning! We'll manage somehow. I'll always love you just as much. Helen, what's the matter? Look at me. Darling!" (006-P92)

The morning after brings no celebration: "The stopping of the boat struck her aching nerves like a blow. She sat up, neither asleep nor awake, pushing her hair back from a face that seemed sodden and lifeless. A pale twilight filled the stateroom. She smoothed her hair, straightened her crumpled dress as well as she could, and went out on the deck. The boat lay at the Sacramento landing" (006-P97).

Sacramento is where Helen becomes a wife — not in a church with flowers, but at a cold dawn on a riverboat landing, exhausted and uncertain. The city that first promised independence has delivered her into an impulsive marriage.

### The Woman Who Returned

Years later, Helen returns to Sacramento as a land saleswoman for Clark & Hayward, and the contrast is stark. She arrives with professional mastery rather than girlish uncertainty:

> All the statistics of the city's growth, of the great reclamation projects, of the rich farms spreading over the old grain lands, were at Helen's fingertips. A hundred times she had gone over them, drawn conclusions from them, pounded home-selling arguments with them, since she had added Sacramento valley lands to the San Joaquin properties she handled. But more eloquently her reviving memories showed her the gulf between the old days and the new. (018-P10)

Her return is an exercise in measuring the distance she has traveled — from the frightened student with a red yarn in her buttonhole to the competent businesswoman who knows every statistic of the city's growth. But even this triumph is shadowed. At the office, Hutchinson presses her about her plans, deploying the familiar arguments against her independence:

> "But what do you want to do it for? What's the idea? Aren't we getting along all right?" He was longing to ask if she were going to Bert, whose arrival and immediate departure had not been explained to him. The whole organization, she knew, was discussing it, and Hutchinson, on the very scene of their meeting, was in the unhappy position of being unable to give the interesting details. (018-P60)

## Significance

Sacramento represents the gateway between Helen's rural past and her urban future. It was here that she first encountered the wider world — the "bewilderments of the first months in Sacramento," surrounded by "strangeness that surrounded her like a thin, cold fog" (004-P01). The city was the site of her telegraphy training, her first independent living, her first encounters with urban social dangers, and ultimately her unconventional marriage to Paul.

Later, Sacramento became the center of Helen's most successful professional period. As a land saleswoman for the San Joaquin properties, she mastered "all the statistics of the city's growth, of the great reclamation projects, of the rich farms spreading over the old grain lands" (018-P10). Her return to Sacramento as a successful businesswoman — in stark contrast to her first arrival as a frightened student — measures the arc of her personal transformation.

Sacramento is also the city of the novel's most compressed historical perspective. The transformation from "a big town" to a city "smudging that sky with the smoke of factories and canneries" (018-P09) happens within Helen's own adult lifetime — her personal arc mirrors California's explosive growth. The girl who arrived with red yarn in her buttonhole and the woman who returned with sales statistics at her fingertips are separated by the same forces that turned a sleepy river town into a booming capital.
