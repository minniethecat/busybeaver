# Love and Courtship

Love, in *Diverging Roads*, is less an answer than a question: "What *is* love?" Helen asks late in the novel [021-128], and Sara's reply — "Why, love is just love" [021-129] — only deepens the mystery. The novel treats romantic love as a force that is simultaneously the most real thing in its characters' lives and the most difficult to reconcile with the practical demands of work, ambition, and selfhood. Where the related concept [[marriage-and-courtship]] examines the institution, "Love and Courtship" traces the experience — the yearning, the moments of grace, and the inevitable collision between feeling and the world.

Helen Davies loves three men over the course of the novel, and each relationship illuminates a different facet of romantic experience: Paul, the quiet constancy of first love; Bert, the intoxicating danger of passion without foundation; and the lingering question of whether she ever truly knew either of them. These are not sequential loves — they overlap and haunt each other, creating the novel's characteristic texture of emotional simultaneity.

## Early Courtship: Pastoral Idyll

The courtship of [[helen-davies|Helen]] and [[paul|Paul]] unfolds in a landscape saturated with promise. Sunday buggy rides carry them through golden hills where "the yellow road, winding on among the trees, disappearing, appearing again like a ribbon looped about the curves of the hills" [002-021] mirrors the uncertain trajectory of their feelings. These early scenes are marked by an almost sacramental quality. When Helen finally speaks the words Paul has been waiting for — "I love you" — the narrator reports that she said it "as though it were a sacrament" [003-033].

The pastoral setting is essential to the novel's vision of early love. Under the live-oaks, gathering wild flowers, Helen and Paul inhabit a world where feeling is unmediated by economic calculation. "There was gold in the green of the fields, gold in the poppies beside the road, gold in the ruddiness of young apricot twigs" [002-021] — the landscape itself becomes a metaphor for emotional abundance. Yet the novel is careful to show that this idyll cannot last. The same road that carries them through the hills will eventually carry them apart.

Even in these early scenes, the seeds of difficulty are visible. Paul's love is steady but inarticulate; Helen's is ardent but restless. "She said his name to herself, under her breath: 'Paul.' A quiver ran along her nerves at the sound of it" [002-055] — the intimacy of the whispered name suggests a love that is more private performance than shared understanding.

## The Vocabulary of Feeling

Throughout the novel, Helen struggles to find language adequate to her emotions. She "said his name to herself, under her breath: 'Paul.' A quiver ran along her nerves at the sound of it" [002-055]. The name, spoken in solitude, carries more weight than any declaration. Later, reunited with Paul in San Jose, the same pattern holds: "the creaking of the wicker chair when Helen settles into it, the sharp crackle of Paul's match as he lights his after-dinner cigar" [019-019] — small sensory details that carry the weight of unspoken feeling.

The novel suggests that love's truth lives in these moments rather than in grand declarations. When Paul telephones with flimsy excuses — "some negligible matter which she heard without listening, knowing that to both of them it was unimportant" [017-036] — what matters is not the pretext but the simple fact that he called. Love, in this world, speaks through indirection.

## Courtship as Performance

If Helen and Paul's early courtship represents love in its most unguarded form, the novel also shows courtship as social performance. [[louise|Louise]] and her circle treat romantic pursuit as a game with well-understood rules. Helen, observing them, learns that "men loved women for being beautiful, gay, unexacting, sweet-tempered always, docile without being bores" [011-004]. This is courtship as theater, where authentic feeling must be filtered through the conventions of femininity.

[[bert|Bert Kennedy's]] courtship of Helen operates in this register. His proposal, delivered "beneath the glaring lights and crepe-paper decorations" [012-014], is a performance — and Helen, caught up in it, consents. The novel does not condemn this as false; rather, it suggests that courtship always involves a degree of performance, and the danger lies not in performing but in forgetting that one is doing so.

The reckoning comes quickly. Helen's realization that she has married a man she barely knows — "She knew nothing of him, nothing that signified. She had fallen in love with a voice, a glance, a way of looking at her that made her feel beautiful and desired" [013-027] — is one of the novel's most devastating passages. Courtship, as performance, creates its own reality; but marriage, as institution, demands a different kind of knowing.

## Love in the Women's Circle

The novel also explores romantic love through the prism of the women's circle. [[willetta|Willetta]] carries "the tragedy that had shattered her home" [021-076]; [[dodo|Dodo's]] cynicism is explained by some private disappointment "that sometimes sharpened her tongue" [021-076]. These secondary stories suggest that Helen's experience with love — its mixture of hope and disillusion — is not exceptional but typical. The women's circle is, among other things, a community of people who have been wounded by love and have found in each other a different form of sustenance.

Helen, looking at these women, feels "with something of wistful envy" the affection that holds them together [028-022]. She hopes "they would like her, too" [028-022]. This envy is telling: love between women, she is beginning to understand, may offer something that romantic love has not.

## The Question Unanswered

By the novel's end, the question Sara raised — "what *is* love?" — remains unresolved. Helen, having traveled from Masonville to San Francisco and back, having loved two men and lost both in different ways, glimpses something like an answer in her final solitude: "Deliberately she thought of Paul, and already the deep hurt was gone" [024-022]. This is not the passionate declaration of the young woman who said "I love you" as a sacrament. It is something quieter — love transformed by time and choice into acceptance. Whether this transformation represents love's fulfillment or its dissolution is a question the novel leaves for the reader.
