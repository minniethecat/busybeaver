# Paul's Departure

When Paul leaves Masonville for his position as night operator at Ripley station in the San Joaquin valley, the novel's central relationship crosses its first irrecoverable threshold. His departure is both a professional triumph and a personal rupture — he is ascending, but he is ascending alone.

## Summary

Paul secures a position at Ripley, "a station in the San Joaquin valley," and tells Helen with "a shade of self-importance" in his voice (003-028). She is left behind, watching him follow "the shining lines of track beyond Cherokee Hill into a vague big world that baffled her thoughts" (003-038). The distance between them becomes literal — railroad miles, telegraph wires — before it becomes emotional.

## Significance

Paul's departure establishes the pattern that the novel's title announces: roads diverge. His path leads south, toward steady railroad employment and eventual respectability. Helen's path remains in Masonville — for now. The question that will occupy the rest of the novel is whether their roads will ever converge again.
