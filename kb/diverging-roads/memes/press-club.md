# Press Club

A San Francisco professional and social club for journalists, writers, and publishing professionals — part of the city's lively media culture and the scene of Helen Davies's farewell to her colleagues.

## Overview

The Press Club was the social hub of San Francisco's newspaper and magazine world, where journalists, editors, stenographers, and writers gathered after hours. Its dances and gatherings brought together the diverse workforce of the city's publishing industry — from the *Post*'s stenographers to established editors like A. C. Hayden of the *Pacific Coast*.

The club appears in the novel only at the moment of farewell: on the deck of the steamer, Helen recognizes "men with whom she had danced at the Press Club" among the crowd of well-wishers (027-034). Their presence at her departure testifies to the professional community she had built during her years as a writer.

## Role

The Press Club represents the professional network Helen established through her writing career. Unlike the Radical Club, which offered intellectual stimulation from outside the mainstream, the Press Club was a thoroughly professional institution — a place where working journalists and writers met as colleagues rather than as ideologues.

The fact that these colleagues showed up at the steamer to say goodbye, that they pressed gifts into her hands, that Clark himself ("Oh, Mr. Clark! How good of you to come") was among them — all this speaks to the genuine relationships Helen had forged in her professional life. She may have felt, in her final taxi ride through the city, that she was leaving everything behind, but the crowd at the gangplank told a different story.
