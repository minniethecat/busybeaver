# J. D. Hutchinson

A veteran real-estate salesman assigned by [[clark-and-hayward|Clark & Hayward]] to share [[helen-davies|Helen]] [[gilbert-kennedy|Kennedy]]'s oil-field
territory, whose grudging respect for her competence evolves into a genuine business
partnership.

## Role in the Story

Hutchinson enters the narrative as an antagonist — one of two experienced salesmen sent by
[[clark-and-hayward|Clark & Hayward]] to take over the oil-field territory that [[helen-davies|Helen]] has developed single-handedly.
His initial appearance embodies the institutional sexism of the business world: he and his
partner Monroe smile at [[helen-davies|Helen]]'s arguments and condescend to her as "a woman, and a charming
one." Over time, however, [[helen-davies|Helen]] wins his respect through her competence, and the two develop
a working partnership that represents one of the novel's few examples of cross-[[gender-roles|gender]]
professional collaboration. (012-082) (016-009)

## Biography

Hutchinson is a tall, angular man who moves in an "easygoing way as if his body had nothing
to do with the loose-fitting, gray clothes he wore." He has frank eyes with a humorous
expression, but deep lines from his nostrils to the corners of his mouth, and when he smiles
— which he does easily — two more deep lines appear in his cheeks. He is a career salesman
whose easy manner masks professional shrewdness. (012-082)

When [[clark-and-hayward|Clark & Hayward]] reassign the oil-field territory, Hutchinson and Monroe are the two men
sent to divide [[helen-davies|Helen]]'s commissions. Hutchinson explains the arrangement with patronizing
courtesy: the firm has given them the territory; they are experienced salesmen; naturally,
[[clark|Clark]] would not leave it in "the hands of a young saleswoman, however charming personally."
(012-089)

[[helen-davies|Helen]] fights back by giving them a list of worthless leads, collecting a written agreement
for two-and-a-half percent commission on any sales to those names. When Hutchinson discovers
the deception, he takes it with surprising grace: "You certainly put one over on us," he says
"without malice." From that point, their relationship shifts — "they regarded her more as an
equal than as a woman." (012-093) (016-004)

Later, when the oil fields begin to decline, [[helen-davies|Helen]] proposes an advertising-based approach to
land sales and invites Hutchinson to partner with her on a fifty-fifty basis. Though initially
dismissive — making an inappropriate joke about his wife — Hutchinson ultimately accepts when
the oil-field work dries up, "remembering that Mrs. [[gilbert-kennedy|Kennedy]] had funds for an advertising
campaign." (016-009)
