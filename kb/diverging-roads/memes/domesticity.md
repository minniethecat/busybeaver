# Domesticity

Domesticity — the world of the household, of cooking and cleaning and caring for others — is the default horizon for women in the Masonville of *Diverging Roads*. Helen Davies feels its gravitational pull throughout the novel: the farmhouse that has "not changed since she could remember" (001-003), the dishes that spark quarrels with her sister, the parlor where courtship is conducted under parental eyes. Domesticity represents both safety and suffocation, and the novel's treatment of it is more nuanced than simple rejection — it registers the genuine satisfactions of domestic life even as it insists on the right to choose otherwise.

## Historical Context

In early twentieth-century America, the ideology of "separate spheres" assigned women to the private realm of the home and men to the public realm of commerce and politics. The domestic sphere was valorized as a moral sanctuary — "the hearth" — but it was also a site of unpaid, invisible labor. For women without independent means, domesticity was an economic necessity as much as a cultural ideal. The novel is set in a period when this ideology was under pressure from women's increasing participation in the paid workforce, the suffrage movement, and the social transformations of the Progressive Era. Helen's trajectory from farmhouse to telegraph office to real estate sales tracks a historical shift as well as a personal one.

## Domesticity in the Novel

### The Farmhouse: Domesticity as Stagnation

Helen's relationship to domesticity begins with resistance. She recoils from the domestic imperative of the household routine:

> She did not want to stand there looking at it. She did not want to follow the old stale road home to the old farmhouse, which had not changed since she could remember. She felt that she should be doing something, she did not know what. (001-003)

The Davies farmhouse is presented as a closed loop — the same rooms, the same tasks, the same expectations, repeating without change. The domestic quarrel over the dishes crystallizes the petty bondage of household routine:

> "Oh, don't start whining!" Helen began. They always quarreled about the dishes. "I'd like to know who did every smitch of work yesterday, while you went chasing off." But looking down at Mabel's sullen little face, she felt a wave of compassion. (002-050)

The kitchen described at the novel's opening is a space of unremarked labor, where Helen's mother works in silence:

> Her mother worked silently, stepping heavily about the kitchen, now and then glancing through the window toward the barn. When her husband came clumping up the path and stopped at the back steps to scrape the mud from his boots, she went to the door and opened it, saying almost harshly, "Well?" (002-034)

### The Boarding House: Domesticity as Performance

Mrs. Campbell's boarding house in Sacramento represents domesticity transformed into social performance:

> Mrs. Campbell lived in splendor in a two-story white house on a complacent street. The smoothness of the well-kept lawns, the immaculate propriety of the swept cement walks, cried out against Helen's shabbiness. She had never been so aware of it. When she was seated in Mrs. Campbell's parlor, oppressed by the velvet carpet and the piano and the bead portieres, she tried to hide her feet beneath the chair and did not know what to do with her hands. (005-046)

The boarding house is domesticity commodified — Mrs. Campbell provides a simulacrum of home to paying guests, and her moral surveillance of Helen (the accusations, the eviction) reveals the coercive underside of the domestic ideal. A woman who fails to conform to domestic norms — who works late, who receives male visitors, who has ambitions beyond the parlor — loses the protection that domestic respectability is supposed to provide.

> "I might as well talk straight out to you," Mrs. Campbell said, as if it were a customary phrase. "I met Mrs. Morris, Mrs. Updike's sister, at the lodge convention in Oakland last week, and she told me about you, and I promised to look you up. Well, when I found out! I told Mr. Campbell I was coming straight down here to talk to you." (005-022)

### The Widow's House: Domesticity without a Husband

Mrs. Brown, the young widow, inhabits a third version of domesticity: the household without a husband. She haunts her house as a ghost, her wedding ring a reminder of a domestic life cut short:

> Helen speculated about Mr. Brown. She never saw him; she felt quite definitely that he was not alive. Yet Mrs. Brown often looked at her wide wedding-ring, turning it on her finger as if she were not quite accustomed to wearing it. A widow, and so young! Helen's heart ached at the thought of that brief romance. (004-010)

Her situation reveals a structural truth the novel never states explicitly: domesticity as an ideology depends on the presence of a husband. Without one, the domestic sphere becomes a space of absence rather than fulfillment — a house rather than a home. Mrs. Brown's domestic labor is rendered in terms that emphasize its pathos — she sweeps "with quick, feverish strokes," her "high heels clicked on the floor," and there is "something piteous about her, as there is about a little trained animal on the stage, set to do tasks for which it is not fitted" (004-009).

### Louise and Momma: Domesticity as Style

[[louise|Louise]] and her mother ("Momma") represent a fourth variant: domesticity as aesthetic performance, the apartment as stage set for a life of surfaces. Momma, "slim in a silk petticoat and a frilly dressing-sack," with a "pearly complexion" that is "liberally helped by powder" and hair "almost unnaturally golden" (010-001), represents a version of femininity that is all performance and no substance. Louise shares her mother's commitment to appearance but channels it toward marriage rather than maintenance. For both, domesticity is a matter of looking right rather than being good — a survival strategy in a world that judges women by their surfaces. The scene of Louise hanging over the banister, announcing a visitor in an excited stage-whisper, reinforces this theatrical quality of their domestic life (010-002).

### Helen's Yellow Kitchen: Domesticity Reconceived

The novel's most striking image of domesticity is Helen's fantasy of the yellow kitchen:

> "Some day, she thought, she would have a yellow kitchen. She had in mind the shade of yellow, a clear yellow, like sunshine. There would be cream walls and yellow woodwork, at the windows sheer white curtains, which would wash easily, and on the windowsill a black jar filled with nasturtiums." (018-001)

This is domesticity not as inherited fate but as creative project — a space designed by its inhabitant rather than imposed upon her. The yellow kitchen fantasy is significant because it shows that Helen's rebellion is against compulsory domesticity, not domesticity itself. She wants a home; she wants it on her own terms.

## For Men: Domesticity as Something to Leave

For the novel's men, domesticity is something they come home to — or leave. Paul's ambition drives him away from home and toward the railroad. The domestic sphere he leaves is meticulously kept by his mother:

> His mother was a little woman with brisk, bustling manner. She always stood at the door to see that they wiped their feet before they went in. The house was very neat. There was an ingrain carpet on the front-room floor, swept till every thread showed. The center-table had a crocheted tidy on it and a Bible and a polished seashell. (001-014)

Bert Kennedy moves through hotel rooms and apartments, never settling into household routine. The domestic sphere is, in the novel's terms, a woman's sphere — and the asymmetry of this arrangement is precisely what the novel critiques. Men can leave domesticity behind without losing their moral standing; women who do the same risk becoming [[mrs-campbell|Mrs. Campbell]]'s cautionary tales.

## The Novel's Argument

*Diverging Roads* does not reject domesticity; it rejects domesticity as destiny. The yellow kitchen is as real in Helen's imagination as the telegraph key is in her hand. The novel's argument is that women should be able to choose both — and that the choice should be theirs to make. In a world that offered women few choices, the novel insists on the right to want more than one thing: a career and a home, independence and connection, the telegraph key and the yellow kitchen.
