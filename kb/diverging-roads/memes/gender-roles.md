# Gender Roles

Gender is the most persistent constraint on the characters of *Diverging Roads*. For Helen Davies, being a woman means that ambition must be negotiated against the expectation of marriage, that work is a prelude rather than a career, and that independence carries the cost of social disapproval.

## Definition

In early twentieth-century America, the ideology of separate spheres assigned men to commerce and women to the home. A woman who worked outside the home — particularly in a skilled profession like telegraphy — was testing the boundaries of respectability. Masculinity was defined by earning power, self-control, and eventual headship of a household.

## In the Novel

Helen's central conflict is gendered: "A girl like me has to work if she's going to get anywhere," she protests, immediately adding the bitter recognition — "A girl has to marry somebody" (001-021, 001-037). The novel tracks her effort to reconcile these competing imperatives. Telegraphy offers a middle ground — a respectable female occupation that nevertheless marks her as unusual.

The men of the novel navigate a different set of expectations. Paul's masculinity is expressed through ambition and self-discipline; he must prove himself economically before he can claim a wife. Bert performs a more flamboyant masculinity — the risk-taker, the deal-maker, the man who lives by his wits rather than his salary.

Helen's mother embodies the older generation's gender norms, steering her daughters toward marriage as the only safe harbor. The widowed Mrs. Brown represents the vulnerability of women without husbands — her wedding ring, "turning it on her finger as if she were not quite accustomed to wearing it" (004-010), speaks to the fragility of the domestic security that marriage is supposed to guarantee.
