# Monroe

Monroe is a veteran salesman in the California oil fields who appears in the later chapters of *Diverging Roads* as both a professional rival and a vehicle for the novel's critique of the gendered workplace. Paired with his colleague [[hutchinson|Hutchinson]], Monroe embodies the paternalistic condescension that [[helen-davies|Helen Davies Kennedy]] must navigate as a woman in a male-dominated industry.

## Appearance and Manner

Monroe is described with careful physical detail that underscores his character: "older, shorter, and stout," with "a smooth suavity in the effect of his neat, dapper person, his heavy gold watch-chain, his eyeglasses" [016-010]. His habitual gesture — removing his glasses "as if from habit, wiping them with a silk handkerchief" — accentuates "his blandly paternal manner" [016-010]. The description is simultaneously precise and dismissive: "His eyes were set too close to the thin bridge of a nose that grew heavy at the tip, but his gray hair, the kindly patronage of his smile, and his soft, heavy voice were impressive" [016-010]. The "but" in this sentence is narratively significant — Monroe's physical shortcomings are compensated for by a performative charm that the novel regards with unmasked skepticism.

The details of the gold watch-chain, the eyeglasses, and the silk handkerchief serve as markers of a particular type: the middle-aged salesman whose prosperity is displayed through accessories, whose authority is performed through manner rather than earned through character.

## The Gendered Workplace

Monroe's function in the narrative is to personify the obstacles Helen faces as a woman in business. When the firm assigns Hutchinson and Monroe to share the oil fields territory with her, their response is not professional collaboration but gender-based condescension. "They asked her permission to smoke, but not her consent to their business arrangements. They smiled at her arguments. After all, she was of the sex that must be humored" [016-013].

Monroe's speech is marked by a characteristic verbal tic — the hesitating "ah" that punctuates his most dismissive remarks: "'My dear Mrs. Kennedy,' said Monroe, gallantly. 'Do let us be — ah — reasonable'" [016-013]. This mannerism, combined with phrases like "my dear Mrs. Kennedy," creates a veneer of gallantry that barely conceals his assumption of superiority. He will pick up her handkerchief when it falls; he will not take her business arguments seriously. The contrast between surface courtesy and substantive dismissal is the novel's precise critique of a particular form of sexism — the kind that smiles while it obstructs.

## Business Rivalry

Monroe's competence as a salesman is genuine: he is "able to handle the smart young tool-dressers; his bland paternal manner crushed them into a paralyzing sense of their youth and crudeness" [016-025]. His effectiveness as a salesman — precisely *because* of his paternal manner, not despite it — makes him a more complex and dangerous antagonist than simple chauvinism would suggest. The conflict between Helen and Monroe is about territory both literal and professional. When Monroe "got hold of a tool-dresser she had canvassed and hoped to sell," the resulting fight over commissions reveals that "she was still alone, but now she was among enemies" [016-025].

Helen's response to Monroe is strategic rather than confrontational. She feeds him and Hutchinson worthless leads, allowing them to waste their time while she makes genuine sales [016-020]. "She hoped viciously that Monroe would see that she had quite understood his words" [016-020] — the phrase captures her satisfaction in outmaneuvering a man who underestimated her. This is a recurring pattern in Helen's professional development: she learns to work around obstacles that cannot be directly confronted, converting disadvantage into strategy.

## Later Appearances

Monroe reappears briefly later in the novel when Helen encounters him unexpectedly. "'Mr. Monroe! You're up here too! How's it going?' She gave him a dust-grimed hand" [017-090]. The greeting, delivered with Helen's characteristic directness, suggests that their relationship has evolved from rivalry to something closer to professional recognition. Monroe, for his part, serves as an unwitting conduit of news — telling Helen that Paul has been asking about her [017-094] and inadvertently revealing that Paul has been trying to find her. In this final appearance, Monroe the antagonist becomes, almost accidentally, Monroe the messenger — carrying news that will reshape Helen's future. The irony is rich: the man who once dismissed her as someone to be "humored" now delivers the information that sets her on the path toward reunion.

## Character Type and Function

Monroe belongs to a recognizable type in the novel: the established man whose authority is not challenged but whose power depends on a system Helen must learn to navigate. He shares DNA with [[mr-roberts|Mr. Roberts]], though Roberts's predation is sexual where Monroe's is professional. Both men represent obstacles that cannot be overcome through direct confrontation; both require Helen to develop strategic rather than confrontational skills. The difference is instructive: Roberts is genuinely dangerous; Monroe is merely obstructive. The novel's moral calibration is precise — it distinguishes between a predator and a condescending colleague, treating the former as a trauma and the latter as a problem to be solved.

## Significance

Monroe is not a fully rounded character; he does not need to be. His narrative function is to embody a specific obstacle — the man who is polite enough to be disarming but dismissive enough to be dangerous to a woman's career. In a novel about a woman learning to navigate male-dominated professional spaces, Monroe represents the antagonist who cannot simply be defeated but must be outmaneuvered. His victory, such as it is, lies in forcing Helen to sharpen her skills; her victory is more concrete, and more satisfying. As a character, he illustrates the novel's argument that gender discrimination in the workplace often wears a smile and a gold watch-chain.
