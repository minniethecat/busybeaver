# Cliff House

A landmark restaurant and social venue perched on the cliffs above the Pacific Ocean at San Francisco's western edge — a place of both youthful escapades and a poignant farewell.

## Description

The Cliff House stood on the high cliffs at the northwestern edge of San Francisco, overlooking the Pacific Ocean and the beach. It was one of the city's most recognizable landmarks, visible from the water as ships departed the Golden Gate. Helen, leaving San Francisco by steamer for the last time, watched "the high cliffs of Telegraph, the castle-like height of Russian Hill, the Presidio, Cliff House, the beach" sink "into grayness on the horizon" (027-034). In this final image, the Cliff House appears as part of a sequence of defining San Francisco landmarks — distinct and iconic enough to merit its own place in the visual catalog of the departing city.

The venue was known as a destination for social gatherings and celebrations. When "Louise's American Beauty man was in town," a group planned "a party at the Cliff House," suggesting it was a place one went for special occasions — a destination restaurant removed from the everyday life of the city (014-015).

## Significance

The Cliff House serves two distinct narrative functions. In the earlier episode, it represents youthful, somewhat clandestine social activity — the boardinghouse residents planning a covert evening out, sneaking away from an unwanted suitor to enjoy the glamour of the famous seaside restaurant. In the closing scene, it appears in a very different register: as one of the landmarks Helen watches disappear from the deck of her departing steamer, a fixed point in the geography of a city she is leaving behind.

The contrast between these two appearances of the Cliff House — first as a destination for frivolous party-going, then as a solemn marker of departure — traces Helen's own transformation from a young woman navigating the boardinghouse social scene to a mature woman leaving behind an entire phase of her life.
