A wound, in the world of [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s memoir, is an ambivalent thing — at once a ticket [[england|home]] and a mark of sacrifice, a physical rupture that carries complex social and psychological meanings in the economy of [[trench-warfare|trench warfare]].

## In the Memoir

Wounds appear throughout the narrative in several distinct registers. There is the grim arithmetic of casualty reports: "two killed and ten wounded was the only result" [007-012], "one killed and four wounded" [014-007]. There is the visceral immediacy of the wounded in the field: "other wounded men were crawling back" [007-008], a man "wounded in one arm and patiently waiting" [007-009]. And there is the dark fantasy of the [[england|Blighty]] wound — an injury serious enough to earn a passage [[england|home]] but not so serious as to kill or maim permanently.

The memoir lingers on the figure of the medical officer "dressing the wounds of moaning men" [012-014], a scene of intimacy and helplessness that recurs in [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s war writing. Wounds are not merely clinical facts; they are spectacles — "the discovery of a dead or wounded enemy might have caused a reaction" [014-017], the narrator muses, as if surprise rather than compassion were the expected response. The tally continues relentlessly: "several wounded" [016-006], men "killed or wounded" [009-003], the language of injury becoming the memoir's punctuation.

## Sassoon's Perspective

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] is candid about the soldier's secret wish for a [[england|Blighty]] wound. "I began to wonder if I should ever get a [[england|Blighty]] wound" [009-002], he writes, acknowledging the perverse calculus by which a bullet or shrapnel becomes a prize. This honesty is central to the memoir's anti-war credibility — [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] refuses to romanticize the soldier's relationship to injury. He knows that "to blow one's brains out" is an alternative to "nothing more serious than a [[england|Blighty]] wound" [010-007], and the juxtaposition exposes the desperation beneath the gallows humor.

The narrator's attitude toward wounds evolves from fear to something more complex. He observes that "delicate discrimination about wounds was impossible" [009-003] at the front — the sheer volume of injury overwhelms moral or aesthetic judgment. The wound becomes a fact of existence, neither heroic nor shameful, simply inevitable.

## Historical Reality

In WWI, approximately 1.6 million British and Empire soldiers were wounded. The [[england|Blighty]] wound was a genuine phenomenon — soldiers hoped for injuries that would remove them from combat without permanent disability. Medical officers sometimes suspected self-inflicted wounds, though [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s memoir does not directly address this.

The memoir's treatment of wounds reflects the historical reality of industrial warfare: shrapnel, machine-gun fire, and shell fragments produced injuries on a scale never before seen. Casualty clearing stations and field hospitals struggled to cope with the volume. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s own experience — he was wounded in 1917 and sent to [[craiglockhart|Craiglockhart]] — exemplifies the wounded officer's trajectory through the medical system.
