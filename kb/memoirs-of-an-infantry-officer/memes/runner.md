# Runner

A soldier assigned to carry messages between company and battalion headquarters when telephone lines were cut — a role that required running through shellfire and across open ground, and carried a casualty rate among the highest in the infantry.

## Definition & Context

In the First World War, battlefield communication relied heavily on runners. Field telephones were vulnerable to artillery fire cutting the wires, and wireless was too primitive for tactical use. Runners were therefore essential: they memorised or carried verbal and written messages, navigating trenches and open ground under constant fire. The role demanded physical fitness, nerve, and detailed knowledge of the trench system. A company might be reduced to as few as six runners, and their loss could sever a unit's connection to command. [013-017] [013-029]

## The Fragile Telephone

The runner's indispensability was a direct consequence of the field telephone's vulnerability. Sassoon records sharing a dugout with "two signallers who were working a field telephone," the "tick-tack of the telephone" providing an audible thread of communication through the night [032-002]. But that thread was easily severed — a single shell could cut the wire, and in the chaos of an attack the lines were among the first things to go. The telephone required static positions and intact cables; the runner required only his legs and his nerve. In the gap between the telephone's promise of instant communication and its frequent failure, the runner existed — a human substitute for a severed wire, carrying words through fire that electricity could not traverse.

## In the Memoir

Runners appear at critical moments throughout Sassoon's account of the Somme offensive. During the attack, a few runners come "back and ammunition parties go forward," the exchange of men across no-man's-land rendered as a bare logistical transaction. [013-009] [013-021] The image of a single runner coming "back across the open" — exposed, alone, carrying information vital to the battle's management — recurs as a motif of the infantryman's vulnerability. [013-011] [013-023]

At battalion headquarters, a runner arrives "with a verbal message" concerning the postponed attack, the spoken word travelling through fire when the telephone could not. [014-014] In another sector, Sassoon meets "a runner with a verbal message from Battalion" describing the situation — a fragment of command authority embodied in a breathless soldier. [033-010]

The institutional value placed on runners is captured in the trench standing orders, which prescribe "a free passage way clear for runners" through communication trenches [034-010]. This regulation — the army's formal acknowledgement that runners must be able to move — is also an implicit admission of how frequently the telephone system failed, and how essential the unprotected messenger remained.

The personal cost of the role is made stark when Sassoon records that one of his own runners "was shot through the face" — a laconic notation that conveys the casual way in which such losses were absorbed. [024-004]

## Sassoon's Perspective

Sassoon depicts runners without sentimentality but with evident respect. They are the connective tissue of the battalion, the men who make command possible, and their exposure is treated as one of the war's quiet horrors. Unlike the dramatic figure of the attacking infantryman, the runner's heroism is functional and largely invisible — a man running alone through shellfire because a wire was cut.
