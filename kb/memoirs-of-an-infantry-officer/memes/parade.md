# Parade

The military parade — the formal assembly of troops for inspection, drill, or orders — which appears throughout Sassoon's memoir as a symbol of the army's obsession with outward order. In the memoir, parade ground discipline often stands in ironic contrast to the chaos and squalor of the front line.

## Definition & Context

In the British Army of WWI, parade was a daily ritual. Troops fell in for morning parade, kit inspection, weapons inspection, and ceremonial occasions. Parade-ground drill was the foundation of military discipline, instilling habits of obedience and uniformity. For the regular army and the Kitchener volunteers alike, parade was the visible manifestation of military order — polished buttons, aligned ranks, and punctilious obedience to the sergeant-major's commands.

## In the Memoir

Parade appears as early as Sassoon's first morning with the battalion: "I went down the hill to my first parade. If only they don't chivvy us too much!" [002-008]. Sergeant-Major Flook is told he must be "polished up and punctual on parade" [002-002]. The memoir notes the Army Service Corps on "a slap-up parade, obviously the climax of several days' rehearsing" [003-004] — a display of rear-echelon ceremonial that feels remote from combat.

Before the Somme offensive, there are "no parades today, and we were going into action" [015-002], marking the transition from routine to killing. A soldier is reminded "that he was going on parade without a tie" [023-009], a detail that underscores the absurd persistence of dress regulations near the front. In a training camp, "they were out on their afternoon parades — probably learning how to take the last half-hour before death" [042-009], Sassoon's bitter gloss on the futility of parade-ground drill in the age of machine guns.

## Sassoon's Perspective

Sassoon treats parade with subdued irony. The punctiliousness of parade-ground discipline — polished boots, aligned ranks, correct dress — is never directly mocked, but it is consistently juxtaposed against the realities of trench warfare. Parade represents the army as an institution maintaining its forms even as the substance (the lives of the men) is being consumed. His tone is not contemptuous but elegiac: parade is one more element of the pre-war world that the war has rendered hollow.

## Historical Reality

The British Army's emphasis on parade-ground discipline was both a practical tool for unit cohesion and a cultural inheritance from the Victorian military tradition. In training camps in England and at the base depots in France (like Étaples, where Sassoon spent time), parade and drill occupied a large portion of the soldier's day. At the front, parades were less frequent but still conducted when units were in reserve.
