The **Flintshire Fusiliers** is the fictionalized regimental name that Siegfried Sassoon gave to his own regiment — the Royal Welch Fusiliers — in the Sherston trilogy. The renaming was part of Sassoon's larger project of transforming autobiography into fiction: it granted him the novelist's freedom to compress, invent, and rearrange events while preserving the essential truth of his military experience. Throughout *Memoirs of an Infantry Officer*, the Flintshire Fusiliers are the institutional identity that shapes Sherston's war — the regiment he belongs to, the tradition he serves, the collective entity whose fate he shares.

## The Fictionalization

Sassoon's decision to rename the Royal Welch Fusiliers was consistent with his treatment of the memoir as "fiction" — a designation that allowed him to combine historical fidelity with imaginative freedom. The name "Flintshire Fusiliers" retains the Welsh county association of the original (Flintshire is a historic county in northeast Wales, adjacent to Denbighshire and facing the Irish Sea), while signaling to the knowing reader that the narrative operates in the borderland between memoir and novel. Sherston is Sassoon, but not quite; the Flintshire Fusiliers are the Royal Welch Fusiliers, but not quite.

## In the Memoir

### Regimental Identity

The Flintshire Fusiliers appear in the memoir as a source of pride and identity. When Sherston's company marches toward the Somme, the narrative registers the regiment's self-consciousness with characteristic understatement: "even a single company of Flintshire Fusiliers (with a ten minute interval between it and B and D Companies) was justified in feeling that the eyes of Europe were upon it" [003-005]. The parenthetical precision about intervals between companies — the staff officer's detail inserted into the novelist's sentence — captures the double register of military and literary consciousness that defines the memoir.

### Prestige and Responsibility

The regiment's reputation is a burden as well as a source of pride. During the wire-cutting expedition before the Somme, Sherston reflects on the expectations that attach to the regimental name: "And what would the Manchesters say about the Flintshire Fusiliers if the wire wasn't properly cut? So it seemed to me that our prestige as a Regular Battalion had been entrusted to my care on a front of several hundred yards" [014-004]. The responsibility of the regimental identity — the fear of letting down not just the battalion but the regiment's history — is compressed into the young officer's anxiety about a patch of uncut wire.

### The Regiment at Rest

The Flintshire Fusiliers appear at moments of respite as well as crisis. At Clitherland Camp, the regiment's training depot, the memoir observes how "for more than eighteen months it had been manufacturing Flintshire Fusiliers, many of whom it was now sending back to the Front for the second and third time" [042-005]. The industrial metaphor — "manufacturing" soldiers — captures the mechanized nature of the war's consumption of men. The depot produces Fusiliers; the front consumes them. The cycle is the regiment's reality.

## The Regiment and the Memoir

The Flintshire Fusiliers are never the subject of sustained description in the memoir — Sassoon assumes the reader understands what a regiment is — but they are the invisible architecture that supports every scene. The regiment provides the framework of belonging, the standards of conduct, the history and traditions that give meaning to individual actions. By renaming the Royal Welch Fusiliers, Sassoon acknowledged that he was making literature out of lived experience — but the literature would be truer to the experience than any literal transcription could be.
