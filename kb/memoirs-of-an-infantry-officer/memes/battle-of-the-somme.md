# Battle of the Somme

The **Battle of the Somme** (July–November 1916) is the pivotal military event of Sassoon's memoir — the offensive that transforms his war from a personal adventure into a confrontation with industrialised mass death. The memoir's central section (Part VIII, "Battle of the Somme") is named after it, and the battle's shadow extends across every subsequent page.

## Anticipation

The Somme offensive is first mentioned obliquely at the Army School, where Sassoon observes a lecture audience that "contained as it did a splendid sample of the Fourth Army which began the Somme Battle a couple of months afterwards" [002-009]. The peaceful spring landscape along the Somme river — "breezes ruffled the reedy pools and creeks along the Somme, and here and there a peaceful fisherman forgot that he was a soldier on active service" [002-004] — stands in dramatic contrast to what will follow.

## The July Offensive

The battle opens on 1 July 1916, the bloodiest day in British military history. Sassoon's battalion is held in reserve but witnesses the attack: through field glasses he watches the 21st Division "advancing about three-quarters of a mile away on the left" while "some Yorkshires a little way below on the left, watching the show and cheering as if at a football match" [013-014]. The journal-like entries of Part IV capture the battle's unfolding with clinical immediacy.

Sassoon is thrust into the fighting at Quadrangle Trench, where he engages in close combat with bombs and a rifle: "I hadn't expected the Battle of the Somme to be quite like this" [014-015]. Exhausted and exhilarated, he reflects on the peculiar morality of the battlefield: "My courage was of the cockfighting kind. Cockfighting is illegal in England, but in July 1916 the man who could boast that he'd killed a German in the Battle of the Somme would have been patted on the back by a bishop in a hospital ward" [014-015].

## The Aftermath

The battle grinds on through summer and autumn. Sassoon's Second Battalion returns from "two months in the Cléry sector of the Somme Front, where they had endured some of the severest weather of the War" [030-003]. The cost is staggering: frost, coke-fume poisoning, and relentless shelling reduce the battalion to a shadow of itself.

Walking the old battlefield, Sassoon encounters the debris of the July fighting. "We agreed that it felt queer to be walking along No Man's Land and inspecting the old German trenches" [016-004]. The landscape has been permanently altered — villages reduced to "skeleton" ruins, woods blasted to stumps, the ground churned into a waste of craters.

## Moral Turning Point

The Somme represents a threshold in Sassoon's moral development. The battle strips away any remaining romantic illusions about war. The exhausted Division he sees marching up towards Bazentin — "an exhausted Division" whose faces show "the patient expectant look of men who had been through such attacks before" [016-018] — becomes emblematic of the war's grinding inhumanity.

Later, comparing the Arras offensive to the Somme, he notes that the troops displayed "the same spirit which had animated the men who went over on the First of July" [031-026] — a remark whose irony cuts deep, given that the first day of the Somme produced nearly 60,000 British casualties.

## Significance

The Battle of the Somme is the memoir's central historical event not merely because Sassoon fought in it, but because it represents the moment when the war's rhetoric of glory and sacrifice becomes unsustainable. The contrast between the "peaceful war-pictures" of the Army School [002-009] and the "butchered hostility" of the dead in No Man's Land [014-005] is the distance between the war as imagined and the war as experienced.
