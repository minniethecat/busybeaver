Insomnia runs through [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s memoir as a condition so pervasive it barely needs to be named. Men sleep when they can — in brief intervals between bombardments, in dugouts whose walls shake with concussion, in hospital beds where the body finally surrenders to exhaustion. But true rest remains elusive: the memoir is a document of prolonged wakefulness, of consciousness sharpened rather than soothed by the conditions of war.

## Definition & Context

In civilian life, insomnia is a disorder — a deviation from the normal rhythm of sleep and waking. On the Western Front, sleeplessness was the norm. The artillery bombardment that "kept us wide awake in civilian life" became, in the trenches, merely the background against which soldiers learned to fall asleep — or tried to [014-008]. The body adapted to conditions it was never designed to endure, and the adaptation was itself a form of damage.

The medical literature of the period recognized insomnia as a symptom of shell shock, but Sassoon's memoir does not diagnose. It records sleeplessness as an atmospheric condition — the "haggard clarity of insomnia" that sharpens perception even as it erodes the self [046-006].

## In the Memoir

Sassoon's attention to sleep and its absence begins early. On his first night at the front, he records "the unsleeping strangeness of it" — the sense that the trench world is a place where normal rhythms have been suspended [005-007]. A comrade "was muttering in his sleep" — the unconscious speaking in a language the conscious mind cannot process [005-008].

The memoir's most direct statement about insomnia comes in a moment of almost clinical observation: "they must suffer terribly from insomnia with so many guns firing fifty or sixty shells a minute" [015-005]. The observation is attributed to no particular speaker — it floats in the narrative as a general truth, a fact of trench life so obvious it requires no attribution.

Later, in the memoir's final sections, insomnia takes on a different character — less a response to noise than a state of being: "the haggard clarity of insomnia" [046-006]. By this point, the sleeplessness is no longer caused by bombardment; it has become a condition of the self, a permanent alteration of consciousness that the war has wrought.

## Sassoon's Perspective

For Sassoon, insomnia is never merely a physical complaint. It is a mode of perception — a state in which the defenses that sleep provides are stripped away, leaving the mind exposed to reality without the buffer of unconsciousness. The memoir's characteristic clarity — its refusal to soften or sentimentalize — is itself a kind of literary insomnia: a determination to stay awake, to keep looking, to refuse the consolation of oblivion.
