# Sentry

The figure of the sentry recurs throughout Sassoon's memoir — a solitary watcher at the boundary between the known trench world and the hostile darkness of No Man's Land. The sentry embodies the war's distinctive combination of vigilance, boredom, and vulnerability, often appearing at moments of tension or transition in the narrative.

## Watching at the Raid

During the tense minutes of the trench raid, Sassoon waits on the parapet while the raiding parties have "vanished into the darkness on all fours" [007-007]. The two sentries at a nearby post have become "only spectators" — their usual function of watching for enemy activity suspended as the raid unfolds. One mutters to his companion, "They'll never keep that —﻿—﻿— inside the trench" [007-007], his speech a mixture of technical observation and helpless commentary. In the "ominous silence" that follows, punctuated by "an occasional flare, never near our craters" that "revealed the streaming rain" [007-007], the sentry's passive watchfulness mirrors the larger experience of trench warfare: waiting for something to happen, powerless to influence the outcome.

## The Sentry at the Crossroads

In a quieter moment, returning from an afternoon walk with Ormand, Sassoon passes "the sentry who stood by a glowing brazier at the crossroads" [030-010]. This sentry is a figure of routine rather than danger — part of the landscape of the rear area, like "the looming aerodrome, and the lines of lorries under the trees" [030-010]. The "glowing brazier" gives the image a domestic warmth that contrasts with the sentry's official function. He stands at a crossroads, a place of passage, as Sassoon and Ormand move between the brief respite of their walk and the "disgusting dinner in the smoky hut" [030-010] that awaits them.

## Inadequacy in the Wilderness

When Sassoon's company takes over a sector of the Hindenburg Trench, the sentries are a measure of his unit's exposed position. "Our Company was only about eighty strong and its sector was fully 600 yards. The distance between the sentry-posts made me aware of our inadequacy in that wilderness" [033-008]. The sentries must "clamber up a bank of loose earth before they could see over the top" [033-008], their precarious vantage point reflecting the broader vulnerability of the depleted battalion. Later, returning from a solo patrol in No Man's Land, Sassoon is "huskily challenged by an irresolute sentry" [033-010] — the sentry's hesitation a human detail in the grey half-light of dawn.

## The Bored Sentry

At Clitherland, arriving to face the consequences of his anti-war protest, Sassoon finds that "a bored sentry was the only witness of my arrival, and for him there was nothing remarkable in a second-lieutenant telling a taximan to dump his luggage down outside the officers' mess" [045-012]. The sentry's boredom is a measure of Sassoon's isolation — his moral crisis is invisible to the military machine, which continues its routines regardless. "It was I who was desolate and distracted," he reflects, while the Camp remained "exactly as it would have been if I'd returned as a dutiful young officer" [045-012]. The sentry, posted to observe, sees nothing — or rather, sees a second-lieutenant with luggage, unremarkable, one of thousands.
