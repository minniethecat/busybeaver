# Rats

Rats infested the trenches of the Western Front in vast numbers, feeding on the dead, spreading filth, and troubling the living. In Sassoon's memoir, the rat appears as a creature of the trench underworld — scurrying, omnivorous, indifferent to human suffering — and occasionally as a vehicle for the sardonic humour that sustained soldiers through intolerable conditions.

## Definition & Context

The trench environment of the Western Front created ideal conditions for rat proliferation. Abundant food waste, corpses buried in shallow graves or lying in No Man's Land, and the labyrinthine structure of trenches and dugouts provided both sustenance and shelter. Rats grew to extraordinary size, with contemporary accounts describing animals as large as cats. They contaminated food, spread disease, and gnawed at the dead. Efforts to control them — terrier dogs, organised rat-hunts, poisoning — had limited effect. The rat became an inescapable feature of trench life, as emblematic of the war's squalor as mud and shellfire.

## In the Memoir

Sassoon's references to rats are brief but resonant. During an evening at the front, as he contemplates the quiet air and a smouldering sunset, the spell is broken by movement:

> A rat scampered across the tin cans and burst sandbags, and trench atmosphere reasserted itself in a smell of chloride of lime. [009-006]

The rat is part of the sensory assault of trench existence — noise, movement, smell — that undercuts any attempt to aestheticise the war. In a single sentence, Sassoon captures the reassertion of squalid reality against the backdrop of a beautiful sunset.

The only direct speech reference to rats appears in a moment of recalled conversation with his friend David Cromlech. Sassoon reflects that what he ought to have said, in response to some provocation, was "Oh rats, David!" [023-009]. This colloquial usage — "rats" as an expression of dismissive exasperation — reveals how thoroughly the trench vocabulary had permeated soldiers' speech. The rat had become a metaphor for anything worthless, annoying, or absurd — which, in the context of the war, covered a great deal of experience.

Elsewhere, the rat appears obliquely in a passage describing the servants at a farmhouse billet whose animal vitality plays out against impending death:

> But there was harmony in our Company Mess, as if our certainty of a volcanic future had put an end to the occasional squabblings which occurred when we were on one another's nerves. A rank animal healthiness pervaded our existence during those days of busy living and inward foreboding. [010-010]

The rat does not appear directly in this passage, but its spirit — the persistence of animal life amid death — pervades the scene. The "rank animal healthiness" of the men mirrors the rats' own tenacious existence.

## Sassoon's Perspective

Sassoon's treatment of rats is characteristically understated. He does not dwell on their horror as some memoirists do, nor does he ignore them. The rat is acknowledged as part of the trench landscape, a detail that establishes verisimilitude without demanding thematic elaboration. His single descriptive mention — "scampered across the tin cans and burst sandbags" — is precise, visual, and sufficient. The colloquial "Oh rats" suggests a deeper integration: the rat was not merely an external nuisance but had entered the language, a measure of how thoroughly trench conditions had colonised the soldiers' consciousness.

## Historical Reality

Contemporary accounts confirm the severity of the rat problem on the Western Front. Robert Graves wrote of rats that "came up from the canal, fed on the plentiful corpses, and multiplied exceedingly." Soldiers organised rat-hunts with terriers and bayonets as a form of sport. Official efforts at control were largely ineffective, and the rat population only declined with the end of hostilities and the clearance of the battlefields. The trench rat endures in the cultural memory of WWI as a symbol of the war's degradation of the natural order.
