# Casualty

A **casualty**, in the military lexicon of the First World War, is a soldier rendered unavailable for duty by death, wounding, illness, or capture. The term's bureaucratic neutrality belies its human content. In Sassoon's *Memoirs of an Infantry Officer*, casualties are simultaneously the raw data of attritional warfare and the intimate devastations that hollow out a battalion's collective life.

## The Arithmetic of Attrition

The memoir is punctuated by casualty figures delivered with the flatness of official returns. "The Royal Irish had had sixty casualties; we had one killed and four wounded" [013-030]. Sassoon records these numbers without commentary, allowing their disproportion to speak: sixty for one battalion, five for another — the randomness of industrialised warfare compressed into a ledger entry.

"About forty casualties; only four killed" [013-030] — the word "only" does quiet violence to the four dead, who are simultaneously counted and dismissed by the logic of military arithmetic. A 10% fatality rate among casualties is, by Western Front standards, a "good" result. Sassoon's deadpan transcription of this logic is his most effective critique of the language that makes killing manageable.

## Casualties as Spectacle

The Somme offensive transforms casualties from statistics into a visible landscape of suffering. "At 2:50 no one to be seen in No Man's Land except the casualties (about halfway across)" [013-026]. The wounded become topographical features — bodies halfway across the fire-swept ground, their presence a reproach to the operation that put them there.

The raid narrative provides the memoir's most concentrated casualty vignettes. "Two killed and ten wounded was the only result of the raid" — the word "only" again, a dozen men reduced to a disappointing return on the operation's investment. Sassoon himself tends the wounded: "I went out, found Mansfield badly hit, and left him with two others who soon got him in. Other wounded men were crawling back."

Among the wounded is "a grey-haired lance-corporal, who had one of his feet almost blown off; I half carried him" — the detail of the grey hair and the half-severed foot make the casualty specific, a particular human body broken in a particular way. O'Brien, too, "moaned when I touched him; he seemed to have been hit in several places." The plural — "several places" — evokes the random multiplicity of shrapnel wounds, the body as a surface peppered with metal.

## Routine and the Ordinary Chance

Before the Somme, casualties were absorbed into the routine of trench warfare. "Tours of trenches had been routine, with an ordinary chance of casualties" [012-007]. The phrase "ordinary chance" captures the fatalism of front-line service — death and wounding are neither expected nor unexpected; they are simply the background condition of existence.

This changes with the great offensives, where the "ordinary chance" becomes a near-certainty. The memoir traces the psychological shift from the manageable losses of trench-holding to the catastrophic losses of the attack, where entire battalions can be destroyed in a morning. A raid that produced "casualties were numerous and no prisoners were brought back" is both a tactical failure and, for the men who did not return, the end of their war.

## The Invisible Casualties

Sassoon extends the concept of casualty beyond the physical. Officers who break under the strain — the "shell-shock" cases sent to Craiglockhart — are casualties too, though the Army often treats them as malingerers or cowards. The memoir's own narrator, sent to a psychiatric hospital after his protest against the war, is arguably a casualty of the moral and psychological pressures of prolonged exposure to the killing.

"Tours of trenches had been routine, with an ordinary chance of casualties" [012-007] — but what no statistical return can capture is the cumulative damage to the mind, the erosion of the capacity to believe in the war's purposes. The casualty lists name the dead and wounded; they say nothing of the men who carry the war home in their nerves. These invisible casualties are, in the end, the memoir's true subject.
