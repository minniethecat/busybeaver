# Horses in the War

The horses and mules that shared [[france|the Western Front]] with the soldiers — pulling wagons, carrying officers, enduring the same shellfire and weather — and that [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]], a fox-[[fox-hunting|hunting]] man before the war, observes throughout his memoir with an attention born of lifelong equestrian knowledge and an unspoken grief at what the war did to creatures that had no stake in it.

## Definition & Context

The British Army in World War I was, despite its mechanised image, fundamentally horse-drawn. Artillery pieces were pulled by teams of heavy draught horses. Supply wagons and limbers were drawn by lighter horses and mules. Officers rode horses for mobility and reconnaissance. The cavalry — still considered, in 1914, the decisive arm — waited behind the lines for the breakthrough that never came [003-004][006-003].

The scale of equine mobilisation was staggering. [[england|Britain]] alone sent over a million horses and mules to the war. They were shipped across the Channel in converted vessels, fed on imported fodder, treated in dedicated veterinary hospitals, and — when they died, as they did in their hundreds of thousands — rendered into fertiliser and glue. The war was an equine catastrophe as much as a human one.

## In the Memoir

Horses appear throughout [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s memoir with a frequency that reflects both their military ubiquity and the author's personal connection to them. His pre-war identity as a fox-[[fox-hunting|hunt]]er — a man who knew horses intimately and valued them for their beauty and spirit as well as their utility — informs every equine passage in the book.

The memoir's horses range from the grand to the humble. There are the "hundreds of light and heavy draught horses" drawn up along a road for inspection — an image of military order that recalls the agricultural shows of peacetime [003-004]. There are the cavalry [[fox-hunting|hunt]]ers that Colonel Hesmon keeps for fox-[[fox-hunting|hunting]] behind the lines — "In spite of the War, [[fox-hunting|hunting]] had been going on" — a detail that captures the surreal persistence of pre-war aristocratic culture [021-004]. And there are the "weary horses" of the transport section, "sitting stiffly" under their loads as the battalion moves up to the line — the unglamorous working animals who shared the soldiers' exhaustion [016-017].

The most disturbing equine passage in the memoir is the image of "dead horses" lying among the debris of battle — "rags and shreds of uniforms" intermingled with animal carcasses in a landscape where human and animal death are indistinguishable [014-004]. The image is all the more powerful for being understated; [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] does not dwell on it, but the reader who has absorbed his earlier horse-passages understands what this death means.

## Sassoon's Perspective

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s treatment of horses is never sentimental — his fox-[[fox-hunting|hunting]] background prevented that — but it is consistently attentive in a way that few war memoirs achieve. The horses are not props or background; they are fellow creatures whose suffering is registered alongside the soldiers'.

His fox-[[fox-hunting|hunting]] past gives him a particular authority on the subject. When he describes the "temperatures" of horses, or notes that "the horses were none too sound," or observes the "tails switching in the sunshine," he is writing from the inside of equestrian knowledge [018-004][015-005][006-003]. This knowledge sets his horse-passages apart from the generic animal-sympathy that characterises much war writing.

The memoir also registers, tacitly, the class dimensions of horses in the war. Officers rode; men walked. The transport section's drivers were working-class soldiers whose pre-war occupations — farm labourers, carters, grooms — had prepared them for the work they now did under fire. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s appreciation of [[dottrell|Dottrell]]'s competence as Transport Officer is, in part, an appreciation of a man who understood horses as working animals rather than as sporting assets.

## Historical Reality

Horse casualties on [[france|the Western Front]] were appalling. Approximately eight million horses and mules died in the war from all causes — shellfire, disease, exposure, exhaustion. The British Army's veterinary services treated over 2.5 million equine cases during the war, returning roughly 80% to duty — a recovery rate comparable to that of wounded soldiers.

The horse's military obsolescence was one of the war's lessons. By 1918, the tank, the lorry, and the aircraft had begun to displace the horse from its traditional military roles. The cavalry charge — the archetypal military image of the nineteenth century — was never to return as a serious tactical option.
