# Signaller

## Overview

The **signaller** was the soldier responsible for battlefield communications in the British infantry battalion of the First World War — operating field telephones, laying and repairing wire, and maintaining the fragile link between companies at the front and battalion headquarters in the rear. In Sassoon's memoir, signallers appear as figures of technical competence in a world of mud and chaos, their work invisible until it failed. The signaller's battlefield was the telephone wire, laid across shell-torn ground, a thread of copper that could be severed by a single shell and that had to be repaired under fire.

## The Signallers in a Reduced Company

Sassoon's most vivid image of the signaller's place in the battalion comes from a diary entry made during the Battle of the Somme: "C Company now reduced to six runners, two stretcher-bearers, Company-Sergeant-Major, signallers, and Barton's servant" [013-029]. The list is a roll-call of what remains when a company is stripped to its essentials. The signallers are irreducible — alongside the runners, the stretcher-bearers, and the C.S.M., they are among the last men standing. The company could lose its riflemen and still function; it could not lose its communicators.

The pairing of signallers and runners in this passage is significant. Signallers represented the modern, technological solution to battlefield communication — the field telephone. Runners represented the ancient, human solution — a man carrying a message on foot. In practice, both were needed, because the telephone wire was always being cut.

## The Dugout Telephone

Later, on the march toward Arras, Sassoon takes shelter in a small dugout: "After dark I found my way into a small dugout occupied by a Trench Mortar Sergeant-Major and two signallers who were working a field telephone" [032-002]. The signallers are at their post, maintaining the communications link while the Sergeant-Major "regaled us, in omniscient tones, with rumours about the desperate fighting at Wancourt and Heninel" [032-002]. The signallers work while the senior N.C.O. talks — they are the quiet professionals, their labor continuous and unglamorous.

Sassoon adds a sensory detail that anchors the scene: "I dozed through the night without ever being unaware of the coke fumes from the brazier and the tick-tack of the telephone" [032-002]. The "tick-tack" of the field telephone is the signaller's signature sound — the rhythm of Morse code and voice communication that ran through every dugout and headquarters. It is part of the soundscape of the war, as constant as the rumble of guns, but intimate rather than terrifying.

## The Signaller's Vulnerability

The signaller's work was among the most dangerous in the battalion. Telephone wires had to be laid across open ground and repaired wherever they were cut — which was everywhere and constantly. A signaller tracing a break in the line was exposed to shellfire, sniper fire, and the chaos of no-man's-land. Unlike the infantryman, who could take cover, the signaller had to follow the wire wherever it led, tracing the break with his hands in the dark, vulnerable and alone.

Sassoon does not describe this vulnerability directly — the memoir has no set-piece signaller casualty — but the reduced-company list tells the story by implication. The signallers are among the survivors of the company because their work kept the company alive.
