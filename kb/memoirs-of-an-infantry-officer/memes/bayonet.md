# Bayonet

A blade attached to the muzzle of a rifle, used for close-quarters fighting. In the trench warfare of the Western Front, the bayonet represented both practical necessity and symbolic martial aggression.

## Definition & Context

In the British Army of the First World War, bayonet-fighting was a core component of infantry training. It was taught as both a physical skill and a method of instilling aggressive spirit in soldiers. The bayonet was the weapon of the infantry assault — crossing no-man's-land and entering enemy trenches — though in practice, its use was far less common than rifle fire, machine guns, and artillery. [002-011] [002-012]

## In the Memoir

The bayonet appears in Sassoon's narrative from the earliest stages of his military experience. As a young officer in training, he practises bayonet-fighting, reflecting on the absurdity of the exercise: the straw-filled sacks that simulate human bodies are presented with "a homicidal expression" by his instructor. [003-001] This early encounter establishes the bayonet as a symbol of the institutional effort to transform civilians into killers.

In the trenches, the bayonet takes on a more practical and grim reality. During a raid, bayonet men crawl back from no-man's-land with news of wire conditions, their role a blend of scout and assault trooper. [007-007] On the march towards the Somme, Sassoon observes sunlight flashing on distant bayonets — the tiny figures moving across the landscape reduced to the glint of steel, a recurring image that merges pastoral beauty with the machinery of death. [013-009] [013-021]

The soundscape of the trenches also includes the bayonet: the "hollow clink" of blades against petrol-cans carried by wiring parties evokes the nightly labour behind the lines, where bayonets were as much a tool as a weapon. [016-017] In the detailed order-of-battle documentation, bayonet men are listed alongside carriers and bombers as a distinct battlefield role, their function formalised within the infantry platoon's structure. [034-004]

## The Major's Lecture: Bayonet Training as Ritual

One of the memoir's most vivid set-pieces is the bayonet lecture delivered by a Major at the Army School. The instructional pamphlet declares that the bayonet "is essentially an offensive weapon" and that in "a bayonet assault all ranks go forward to kill or be killed" [002-012]. But it is the Major's theatrical delivery that Sassoon preserves. The Major speaks "with homicidal eloquence" while a Sergeant "trained to such a pitch of frightfulness that at a moment's warning he could divest himself of all semblance of humanity" demonstrates the techniques [002-013]. When told to "put on the killing face," the Sergeant obeys, "combining it with an ultra-vindictive attitude" [002-013]. The language is telling: "homicidal eloquence" yokes violence to rhetorical skill; the Sergeant is a "tall sinewy machine" who has been "trained" rather than taught. The Major's final maxim — "There's only one good Boche, and that's a dead one!" — crystallizes the doctrine of total war into a slogan [002-013].

The lecture's aftermath is one of the memoir's most striking tonal shifts. Sassoon retreats to "a wood of hazels and beeches" where "the evening air smelt of wet mould and wet leaves" and the "church bell was tolling in the town" [002-014]. Yet the Major's words continue to "batter on my brain": "The bullet and the bayonet are brother and sister." "If you don't kill him, he'll kill you." "Three inches will do for him; when he coughs, go and look for another" [002-014]. The pastoral peace of the wood — its bells and wet leaves — is invaded by the Major's instructional violence. Sassoon structures the passage so that the bayonet lecture and the woodland retreat occupy contiguous paragraphs, refusing to let the reader rest in either register. The bayonet, in this arrangement, becomes not only a weapon but a test of consciousness: how does a thinking person hold these two realities — the hazel wood and the killing face — in a single mind?

## Sassoon's Perspective

Sassoon treats the bayonet with characteristic irony. He records his training instructor's theatrical pronouncement — "The bullet and the bayonet are brother and sister" — not with reverence but with a note of dark humour. [002-013] [002-014] The phrase captures the official doctrine that melds killing into a family romance, and Sassoon's presentation of it exposes the rhetorical machinery behind the physical weapon.

The bayonet becomes, in his hands, a metonym for the war's reduction of men to implements. The sight of advancing troops reduced to "the tips of their bayonets" giving a particular "impression" suggests the depersonalisation of industrialised warfare: soldiers become visible only through their weapons. [038-009] [038-010] This visual reduction — men to bayonets — parallels his broader critique of the war's erasure of individual humanity.
