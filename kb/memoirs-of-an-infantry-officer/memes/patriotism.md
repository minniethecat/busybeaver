# Patriotism

Patriotism — the public language of national loyalty and wartime duty — is one of the memoir's most fiercely contested concepts. Sassoon does not argue against patriotism in the abstract; instead, he shows how the word functions in practice: as a mechanism for suppressing truth, as a rhetorical resource for those safely distant from the front, and as a demand that soldiers accept their suffering in silence.

## The Press and Patriotic Consensus

Early in the home-front sequence, Sassoon establishes his stance toward patriotic journalism. Reading the *Unconservative Weekly* — his preferred dissenting paper — he notes that it allows him "to disagree with the bellicose patriotism of the Morning Post" [037-002]. The phrase "bellicose patriotism" is precise: it is not patriotism as such that Sassoon rejects, but its belligerent, warmongering form — the kind that demands more war, more sacrifice, while remaining insulated from the consequences. Yet Sassoon is also self-aware about his own position: the paper's arguments "passed out of my head as quickly as they entered it" [037-002]. Political conviction, even in dissent, has limits; what remains is the brute fact of front-line experience that journalism cannot contain.

## Patriotism as Class Performance

Sassoon's critique of patriotism targets its class dimensions. The "acquiescent patriotism" he opposes [042-002] is specifically the patriotism of those who benefit from the war while others fight — the newspaper editors, the civilian diners, the officers at the rear. At a lunch with the editor Markington, the word "patriotism" is exposed as a mechanism of control: "In wartime the word 'patriotism' means suppression of truth" [040-003]. Markington's formulation is a direct statement of the memoir's thesis — that the public language of patriotism serves not to inspire genuine loyalty but to enforce silence about the war's realities.

Sassoon's own relationship to patriotism is complicated by his class position. As an officer from the landed gentry, he is expected to embody patriotic sacrifice. His protest — the public declaration against the war's prolongation — is framed not as a rejection of patriotism but as a demand that patriotism mean something other than complicity in slaughter. The memoir never resolves this tension, and that irresolution is part of its honesty.

## "Patriotism Means Suppression of Truth"

The memoir's most concentrated treatment of patriotism comes in the lunch scene with Markington, the Liberal editor. Markington explains why his paper cannot print truthful accounts of infantry experience: "if he were to print veracious accounts of infantry experience his paper would be suppressed as prejudicial to recruiting. The censorship officials were always watching for a plausible excuse for banning it" [040-003]. Then comes the memoir's most quoted line: "In wartime the word 'patriotism' means suppression of truth" [040-003]. The sentence is Markington's, but it speaks for the memoir's deepest conviction. Patriotism, in the official sense, is a mechanism for managing public perception — for ensuring that the civilian population continues to support a war whose realities they are never allowed to see.

## The Purchasable Principles of the Patriotic

Markington extends his critique to the middle-aged men who "after a dinner at G.H.Q. and a motor drive in the direction of the trenches" return to England "able to talk and write in support of the War with complete confidence in themselves" [040-003]. "Their principles are purchasable," he concludes. "Once they've been invited to visit G.H.Q. they never look back. Their own self-importance is all that matters to them. And any lie is a good lie as long as it stimulates unreasoning hatred of the enemy" [040-003]. The critique is devastating in its precision: patriotism here is not a moral conviction but a social transaction — access and status in exchange for complicity in the official narrative.

## "Acquiescent Patriotism": Civilians and the War

Sassoon does not only target the press and politicians. The memoir's subtler critique is directed at what he calls "acquiescent patriotism" — the ordinary civilian population that sustains the war by not questioning it. In London, walking with Tyrrell to interview a Member of Parliament, Sassoon observes "evidences of civilian callousness" that sharpen his "quarrel with acquiescent patriotism" [042-002]. The phrase is carefully chosen: not aggressive patriotism, not jingoistic patriotism, but *acquiescent* — the patriotism of those who do not actively cheer for the war but who also do nothing to oppose it. They accept it as a natural condition, their quiet complicity more effective than any editorial in the *Morning Post*.

At Cambridge, Sassoon sees the university "looking contented enough in the afternoon sunshine, as though the Long Vacation were on" — but the Colleges "appeared to have forgotten their copious contributions to the Roll of Honour" [042-009]. The cadets are out on parade, "probably learning how to take compass-bearings, or pretending to shoot at an enemy who was supposedly advancing from a wood nine hundred yards away." The word "pretending" is lethal: these young men are being trained to die, and the training is a charade. The "contented" appearance of Cambridge — sunshine, empty streets, the rhythms of a peacetime university — conceals the machinery of war production that it has become.

This critique of civilian society extends to the home itself. At Butley, living with his Aunt Evelyn, Sassoon is "compelled to lead a double life" [043-001]. He cannot tell her about his impending protest — "I couldn't allow my protest to become a domestic controversy" — and so he sits at her dinner table "morose and monosyllabic," performing the ordinary nephew while inwardly preparing for a confrontation with the military authorities [043-002]. The domestic space becomes another arena of "acquiescent patriotism": not hostile, not unsympathetic, but structurally incapable of receiving the truth that Sassoon must tell.

Even the soldiers at the front are not exempt from Sassoon's critique of patriotic language. In a revealing detail from the Craiglockhart section, he quotes a visitor's report of an officer who had "apparently mistaken the War for a football match between England and Germany" — a young man who "laughed, his clear eyes gleaming with excitement" as he described killing Germans with a Mills bomb [038-009]. The football metaphor, deployed by a civilian visitor seeking to package the war for home consumption, reduces the moral complexity of killing to a sporting contest. Sassoon quotes this passage without explicit comment, but the absence of commentary is itself a judgment: the language speaks for itself, and it damns the discourse that produced it.

## Patriotism and Protest

The memoir's critique of patriotism culminates in Sherston's declaration — the public protest that Sassoon himself made in 1917. The declaration is an act of patriotism in the deeper sense: a refusal to let the nation continue a war that has become "a war of aggression and conquest." But the army's response — a medical board rather than a court-martial — reveals the limits of patriotic dissent. The state prefers to medicalize protest rather than engage with its arguments. Sassoon's narrator is left with the knowledge that speaking truth to power, however patriotically motivated, will be pathologized rather than heard.
