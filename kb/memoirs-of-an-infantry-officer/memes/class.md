# Class and Social Hierarchy

Social class permeates the world of *Memoirs of an Infantry Officer*, shaping relationships between officers and men, the internal dynamics of the officer corps, and the civilian-military divide. Sassoon presents class as an inescapable structure that the war both reinforces and, at moments, renders absurd.

## The Officer Class Ethos

The British officer corps of the Great War was drawn largely from the upper and upper-middle classes, and its members were expected to embody the ideal of the "gentleman." Sassoon portrays this ethos through Shirley, a young officer "educated at Winchester" whose first year at Oxford had been interrupted by the War. Described as "a delicate-featured and fastidious young man, an only child, and heir to a comfortable estate in Flintshire" [032-005], Shirley represents the traditional officer material. When confronted with the imminent battle, "true to the traditions of his class," he "simulated nonchalance" [032-005], maintaining the composure expected of his station even as fear showed in his eyes. Both Shirley and Rees, his socially mismatched fellow officer, "were killed before the autumn" [032-005] — class distinctions ultimately meaningless before the indiscriminate violence of the Western Front.

## Class and Social Incompatibility

The enforced proximity of trench life brought together men of different social backgrounds who would never have mixed in peacetime. Sassoon contrasts Shirley with Rees, "a garrulous and excitable little Welshman" who "made no pretensions to being 'a gentleman'" [032-005]. Their incompatibility is drawn in small, telling details: "Rees rather got on our nerves with his table manners, and Shirley deprecated the way he licked his thumb when dealing the cards" [032-005]. Yet Sassoon notes that "social incompatibilities were now merged in communal discomfort" [032-005] — the shared ordeal of the front line temporarily overrode the rigid class distinctions of Edwardian society.

## Civilian Class Divisions at Home

While soldiers endured the trenches, Sassoon observed stark class inequalities on the home front. The wealthy continued to dine in luxury: "the people in the Savoy were guzzling their way through elaborate dinners while the poorer classes stood for hours in queues outside the provision shops" [043-002]. During his period of leave and protest, Sassoon's Aunt Evelyn embodied the comfortable upper class, unable to comprehend his anti-war stance. Her suggestion that he "go to the seaside for a few days' golf" [043-002] exemplified the gulf between civilian privilege and the realities of the Western Front. In London, Sassoon found "evidences of civilian callousness and complacency were plentiful" [042-002], particularly among "the idle, empty-headed, and frivolous ingredients of Society" who were "having a tolerably good time" [042-002] while soldiers died.

## The Army School as Microcosm

Even within the military's own training structures, class reproduced itself. Sassoon's description of the Army School explicitly invokes the public school analogy: "It was not unlike the first day of a public school term, and my form-master (we were divided into classes of twenty-eight) was a youngish Major" [002-008]. The comparison casts the Army's hierarchical organisation as an extension of Britain's class-bound educational system, where officers were schooled in the same manner as the sons of privilege had always been.

## Class, War, and Narrative

Throughout the memoir, Sassoon occupies an ambivalent position regarding class. He is a product of the system he critiques — a fox-hunting gentleman turned infantry officer — and his social position gives him both the perspective and the platform to articulate his dissent. His friendship with Tyrrell, "a great man and to be thought of as in a class by himself" [042-002], connects him to a network of influential dissenters who could bring his protest before Parliament. Sassoon's class privilege thus becomes both the object of his critique and the instrument of his resistance.
