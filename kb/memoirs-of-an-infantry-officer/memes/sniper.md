# Sniper

The sniper embodied the personalised violence of trench warfare — an enemy who watched, waited, and killed with deliberate precision. In Sassoon's memoir, the sniper represents both the omnipresent threat of the front line and the crystallisation of combat motivation into a singular, lethal purpose.

## Definition & Context

Military sniping emerged as a specialised practice during the First World War, particularly after the static trench lines created opportunities for sustained observation and targeted shooting. German snipers, often equipped with telescopic sights, dominated No Man's Land during the early war years. The British responded by establishing sniper schools and deploying their own sharpshooters. The sniper's psychological effect — the sense of being watched and targeted — was often as significant as the physical casualties inflicted.

## In the Memoir

Sassoon first encounters the sniper threat during a patrol in the Fricourt sector. Mansfield, a fellow officer, has been detailed for a dangerous night reconnaissance; the bright moonlight that aids navigation also exposes patrols to enemy marksmen. "Mansfield had gloomily remarked that he'd something — well go on the razzle if he got through Thursday night with his procreative powers unimpaired" [005-007]. The sniper's presence turns every movement in No Man's Land into a calculated risk.

The memoir's most intense sniper episode occurs in Quadrangle Trench following the death of Lance-Corporal Kendle. Sassoon's reaction is immediate and visceral:

> After blank awareness that he was killed, all feelings tightened and contracted to a single intention — to settle that sniper on the other side of the valley. If I had stopped to think, I shouldn't have gone at all. As it was, I discarded my tin hat and equipment, slung a bag of bombs across my shoulder, abruptly informed Fernby that I was going along the line to the right, and went. [014-016]

The sniper becomes a personal antagonist, and Sassoon's pursuit is driven by grief transmuted into focused rage. This episode is one of the memoir's most candid portrayals of combat motivation stripped of patriotic rhetoric.

Later, in the safety of Nutwood Manor, Sassoon reflects on his actions with ironic distance. Speaking with his hostess, he describes having "carelessly offered myself as a target for a sniper" [038-013]. The word "carelessly" contains a complex self-assessment — neither heroic nor ashamed, but recognising the reckless indifference to survival that combat had instilled.

While convalescing, Sassoon's dreams return him to the front:

> Shapes of mutilated soldiers came crawling across the floor; the floor seemed to be littered with fragments of mangled flesh. Faces glared upward; hands clutched at nothing. Dummy figures used to deceive snipers; others were alive and looked at me with reproachful eyes. [036-012]

The sniper's world — deception, observation, lethal precision — haunts the sleeping mind as much as the waking trenches.

## Sassoon's Perspective

Sassoon's treatment of the sniper reveals his understanding of combat as fundamentally personal. The sniper is not an abstract enemy force but a specific human antagonist whose elimination becomes a comprehensible goal amid the incomprehensible scale of industrial warfare. His self-described "single intention" to kill the sniper is presented without moral commentary — it simply is what combat demanded. The retrospective framing ("carelessly offered myself") introduces critical distance without disowning the authentic intensity of the moment.

## Historical Reality

Sniping caused a significant proportion of trench casualties, particularly among officers and NCOs who exposed themselves to observe the enemy. The Germans initially held the advantage, with more telescopic sights and a developed doctrine of sniper employment. British sniper training was formalised from 1916 onward under the guidance of experts like Major Hesketh-Prichard. By 1918, sniping had evolved into a professionalised counter-sniper war, with observers, decoys, and dedicated sniper plates in trench parapets.
