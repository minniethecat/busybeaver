# Communication Trench

A secondary trench connecting front-line trenches with support and reserve lines, enabling movement of men, ammunition, rations, and wounded under cover from enemy fire.

## Military Function

Communication trenches were essential to trench warfare logistics. Unlike fire trenches (which faced the enemy), they ran at right angles to the front, creating protected corridors for supply and reinforcement. They were typically narrower and shallower than front-line trenches [007-004].

A well-developed trench system featured a network of communication trenches, often named after London streets or familiar landmarks. They served as the arteries of trench warfare — every ration party, wiring fatigue, ammunition resupply, and stretcher-bearer detail passed through them.

Their depth was a constant concern; a flooded or collapsed communication trench could isolate a forward position for hours [007-004].

Because communication trenches were less strongly held than fire trenches, they were particularly vulnerable to shellfire. A single well-placed shell could block a trench entirely, forcing men to move above ground — exposed to snipers and machine-gun fire.

The trench guide system evolved partly to navigate the labyrinth of communication trenches, which could be baffling to newcomers and relief parties arriving at night [007-004].

## Construction and Maintenance

Communication trenches were continuously dug, repaired, and deepened. Sassoon notes half-dug communication trenches near Bottom Wood, barely remembered terrain from a previous tour [014-020]. The constant labour of maintaining these trenches — shoring up collapses, pumping out water, deepening traverses — fell to the infantry and pioneer battalions, often at night under sporadic shellfire.

The distinction between communication trenches and the deeper Tube communications used by staff officers was a class marker in itself. "The Colonel and the Adjutant doing their bit in a deep dug-out or Tube, while the rest slogged up communication trenches" [007-004], Sassoon observes with dry irony.

## In the Memoir

Sassoon repeatedly moves through communication trenches during his service. His first trench tour involves "sploshing" up a muddy communication trench under shellfire [007-004]. Later, during the raid preparations, the communication trench is shelled while men pass through in "their leisurely way" [007-007]. After the raid, soldiers jump over communication trenches in retreat [007-014].

At Mametz, a communication trench holds badly mangled bodies [014-005], and a half-dug communication trench near Bottom Wood is noted as barely remembered terrain [014-020]. The twisting communication trench at the front carries wounded men night after night [031-034], and soldiers huddle against their sides under bombardment [033-006].

During his trench-raid narrative, Sassoon describes the communication trench as a place of tense waiting: men crouched, bayonets fixed, flinching at every shell-burst overhead while officers checked watches and exchanged last whispered instructions [007-007]. The trench itself becomes a character — a narrow, muddy corridor that simultaneously protects and traps.

## Sassoon's Perspective

The communication trench in Sassoon's account is not a safe space — it is shelled, muddy, and often lined with casualties. He describes it with a weary matter-of-factness, treating it as part of the unavoidable geography of the line rather than a place of shelter.

There is a class dimension to Sassoon's treatment of communication trenches. The contrast between staff officers who rarely leave deep dugouts and the men who trudge communication trenches carrying rations, ammunition, and stretchers is a recurring undercurrent.

The communication trench thus becomes a symbol of the frontline soldier's lot — the unglamorous, exhausting labour of simply moving through the war's landscape [007-004].
