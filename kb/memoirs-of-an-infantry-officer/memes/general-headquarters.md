# General Headquarters (G.H.Q.)

The supreme command echelon of the British Expeditionary Force in France — an institution that appears in the memoir only through its emanations: the gas expert who lectures, the communiqué that sanitizes, the staff cars that hurry past marching infantry.

## Identity & Structure

G.H.Q., located at Montreuil-sur-Mer for most of the war, was the brain of the B.E.F. — the staff organization through which the Commander-in-Chief directed the operations of the five field armies on the Western Front. In the memoir, G.H.Q. has no physical location and no named personnel. It is an abstraction made concrete only through its products: the tactical doctrines taught at the [[fourth-army-school|Fourth Army School]], the gas warfare that was "still in its infancy" [002-010], and the communiqués that transformed chaotic trench raids into "spirited bombing fights" [007-015].

## In the Memoir

G.H.Q. enters the memoir most memorably through its gas expert: "A gas expert from G.H.Q. would inform us that 'gas was still in its infancy'" [002-010]. The sentence is characteristic of [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s method — the institution is named, the expert speaks, and the narrator's parenthetical observation that "most of us were either dead or disabled before gas had had time to grow up" delivers the verdict without needing to argue for it.

The G.H.Q. communiqué receives similar treatment. After the [[trench-raid|trench raid]], the official report transforms twelve casualties into a "mention in the G.H.Q. communiqué" [007-015]. The gap between the communiqué's language and the crater's reality is, for the memoir, the war's essential dishonesty.

Later, the memoir records a glimpse of G.H.Q.'s social world: "After a dinner at G.H.Q. and a motor drive in the direction of" the front [040-003]. The dinner party and the motor drive are the war as experienced by those who direct it — comfortable, distant, insulated from consequence.

## War Record

G.H.Q.'s war record, as the memoir presents it, is inseparable from the battles it directed. Its strategic decisions — the Somme offensive, the Arras offensive, the Third Ypres campaign — are the context within which the memoir's local actions take place. The memoir does not evaluate G.H.Q.'s military competence directly; it evaluates it indirectly, through the gap between intention and result, between the operational order's optimism and the battalion's casualties.

## Sassoon's Perspective

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s treatment of G.H.Q. is a study in the rhetoric of understatement. He never says "G.H.Q. was incompetent." He shows a gas expert lecturing men who will be dead before the technology matures. He shows a communiqué that calls a failed raid a success. He shows a dinner party at headquarters while battalions march toward the line. The critique is present in the juxtaposition, not in the commentary.

## Historical Counterpart

G.H.Q. was the real headquarters of the B.E.F. under Sir Douglas Haig (who succeeded Sir John French in December 1915). Its Montreuil location, its staff culture, and its distance from the front were subjects of persistent criticism by front-line officers throughout the war. The memoir's treatment of G.H.Q. is consistent with this tradition of criticism while exceeding it in literary sophistication.
