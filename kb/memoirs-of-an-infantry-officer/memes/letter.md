# Letter

The written message that bridged the gap between the Western Front and England — in Sassoon's memoir, letters serve as the primary medium of emotional connection, a marker of mortality, and a recurring narrative device for revealing the private selves behind military roles.

## Definition & Context

Letter-writing was the central form of communication between soldiers and civilians during World War I. The British Army's postal service delivered millions of letters weekly, and for most soldiers the arrival of mail was the high point of the day — a tangible connection to the world they had left behind. Officers, who enjoyed greater privacy than other ranks, wrote in dugouts, billets, and estaminets, often under conditions that made the act itself a small defiance of the war's chaos.

The letter also carried a darker significance: the "farewell letter" written before battle, to be delivered only if the author did not survive. These letters became one of the war's most poignant literary forms — final words from the dead to the living.

## In the Memoir

Sassoon's memoir opens its account of the raid that earned him the [[military-cross|Military Cross]] with the admission that "I had just written a farewell letter to Aunt Evelyn" [007-001]. This letter, written in the expectation of death, frames the raid as a mortal undertaking — and Aunt Evelyn, as the letter's designated recipient, becomes the memoir's representative of the home front.

Letters from home sustain the narrative's emotional register. Aunt Evelyn's correspondence — including her membership ticket to the Army and Navy Stores, "posted back to her afterwards" — provides a thread of domestic continuity through the war's disruptions. Letters from fellow officers carry darker news: "This news came in a letter from Durley" reports the death of a friend, the written word becoming the bearer of loss [020-002].

The most extended letter sequence involves Dottrell, whose two letters — "written on consecutive days" — Sassoon receives after the man's death, creating one of the memoir's most painful ironies [020-008]. "The second letter, which I chanced to open," reads Sassoon — the casual verb "chanced" understating the devastating experience of reading words from someone already dead [020-011].

## Sassoon's Perspective

Letters in the memoir function as evidence of interiority. While military routine demands the suppression of private feeling, letters reveal the emotional reality beneath — the fear, the longing, the love that official language cannot accommodate. Sassoon treats his own letters with characteristic self-deprecation, but the letters of others — particularly the dead — are presented with a restraint that amplifies their emotional power.

## Historical Reality

The British Army Postal Service handled approximately 12 million letters per week during the war. Letters to and from the front were censored by junior officers, who read their comrades' correspondence for operational security. Sassoon himself, as a transport officer and later company officer, would have performed this duty — a fact the memoir never mentions, though it adds an additional layer to his treatment of the letter as a document suspended between private emotion and institutional surveillance.
