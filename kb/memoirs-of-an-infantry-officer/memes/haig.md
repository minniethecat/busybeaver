# Haig

## Overview

Field-Marshal Sir **Douglas Haig** (1861–1928) was the Commander-in-Chief of the British Expeditionary Force on the Western Front from December 1915 until the end of the First World War. In Sassoon's memoir, Haig appears only briefly — a distant figure glimpsed from a marching column, a name attached to official despatches — but his presence permeates the book as the ultimate authority behind the strategic decisions that send men to their deaths. Sassoon's treatment of Haig is indirect but devastating: the Field-Marshal is less a character than a symbol of the gulf between the High Command and the men who fought.

## The Sighting on the March

Haig's single physical appearance in the memoir occurs during the battalion's march toward the Somme battlefield: "My exiguous diary has preserved a few details of that nine mile march. Field-Marshal Haig passed us in his motor; and I saw a doctor in a long white coat standing in the church door at Morlancourt" [016-002]. The sentence is remarkable for its compression. Haig "passed us in his motor" — the personal pronoun "us" contrasting with the impersonal "his motor," the Commander-in-Chief insulated from the marching infantry by glass and horse-power. In the same sentence, Sassoon records seeing a doctor in a church doorway — as if the Field-Marshal and the doctor occupy the same category of observed but unattainable figures.

The diary-like brevity of the passage is itself a comment. Haig merits no more space than the "rich yellow evening light" or the "rattling limbers on the Citadel road" that follow. In Sassoon's moral economy, the Commander-in-Chief is less significant than the sound of the transport that feeds his men.

## Haig's Despatches

Haig's second appearance is still more ironic. In a convalescent hospital, Sassoon observes a wealthy father reading to his legless son: "Then the father, with his neatly-trimmed beard and elegant buff linen waistcoat, begins to read him Haig's latest despatch. 'There is strong evidence that the enemy forces engaged on the battlefront have been severely shaken by the repeated successes gained by ourselves and our Allies…' The level cultivated voice palavers on until the nurse approaches brightly with a spouted feeding-cup. 'Time for some more beef-tea!' Nourishment is administered under approving parental eyes" [019-003].

The scene is a masterpiece of satirical juxtaposition. Haig's despatches, with their language of "repeated successes" and "severely shaken" enemy forces, are read to a young man who has lost a leg in one of those "successes" — the Gommecourt attack, which Sassoon has already identified as "nothing but a massacre of good troops" [019-003]. The "level cultivated voice" of the father, the "beef-tea" administered by the nurse, and the approving parental gaze form a tableau of Home Front incomprehension, with Haig's official prose as its sacred text.

## The Symbol of Distance

Haig functions in the memoir as the personification of the gulf between the planning of battles and the experience of them. The father keeps "a war map with little flags on it" and moves "a little flag an inch forward after breakfast" when a wood is reported captured [019-003]. "For him the Wood was a small green patch on a piece of paper. For the Welsh Division it had been a bloody nightmare" [019-003]. Haig's despatches and the father's flag-map are manifestations of the same phenomenon — the reduction of human suffering to strategic abstraction.

Sassoon never attacks Haig directly; that would be too crude for his method. Instead, he allows the Field-Marshal to be seen from the marching column, from the hospital bed — from the perspective of the men whose lives were the currency of his strategy. The effect is more damning than any direct indictment could be.
