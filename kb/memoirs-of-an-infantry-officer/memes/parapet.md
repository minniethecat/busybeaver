# Parapet

The forward wall of a trench, built up from the excavated earth and reinforced with sandbags, through which soldiers observed no-man's-land and fired at the enemy. Looking over the parapet was an act of deliberate exposure — often fatal.

## Definition & Context

In the trench systems of the Western Front, the parapet was the side facing the enemy. It was typically built to a height of several feet above ground level, with loopholes for observation and rifle fire, and reinforced against shellfire. The parapet's condition — whether intact or blown in — was a constant preoccupation of trench garrisons, and sentries spent their watches peering through it into the darkness beyond. ## In the Memoir

Sassoon's references to the parapet mark some of his most intense front-line experiences. In a trench near Fricourt, a mortar bomb sits "squatly above us on the parapet," a domestic image of menace — as if the weapon had been left there like an object on a mantelpiece. [007-005] shell craters yawn "a few yards from our parapet," the proximity of destruction made material in the scarred earth. [007-006]

The parapet is the threshold between relative safety and mortal danger. Sassoon describes sitting on it during a lull, "waiting for something to happen" — the soldier's habitual posture of vigilance and resignation. [007-007] When a raid returns, men scramble "clumsily over the parapet," their blackened faces and white eyes the only visible features in the darkness. [007-008] The lowering of a wounded man over the parapet is described with clinical precision: a stretcher-bearer bends over him, the ritual of casualty evacuation enacted against the earthen wall. [007-011]

Further up the line, a company commander appears "above the parapet" with "almost aunt-like anxiety," checking on his men — an incongruously domestic concern in a landscape defined by the parapet's lethal geometry. [012-011] Beyond it, figures bob "up and down" with "sotto voce incitements," the world outside the trench reduced to glimpsed movement and muffled sound. [012-013]

In quieter moments, the parapet becomes a place of contemplation. Leaning his elbows on it, Sassoon looks at the ridge and sees a glint of water — the Ancre — in the morning light. [032-007] Sentries stare "over sodden parapets until the sky reddened," the endless wet vigil of the front-line soldier distilled to a single image. [031-034]

## Sassoon's Perspective

The parapet functions in Sassoon's narrative as a border between the human and the inhuman. Inside the trench, men talk, eat, sleep, and joke; outside, the landscape of no-man's-land is defined by craters, wire, and corpses. To look over the parapet is to cross that border, if only visually. Sassoon treats the act with neither heroism nor melodrama — it is simply what the war requires, and what the war takes.
