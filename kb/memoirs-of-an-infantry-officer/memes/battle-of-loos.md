# Battle of Loos (1915)

The major British offensive of autumn 1915, fought in the mining basin of northern [[france|France]] — the first large-scale engagement in which [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s battalion took part, and an event that recurs throughout the memoir as a grim benchmark against which subsequent battles are measured and found wanting.

## Background

The Battle of Loos was the British contribution to the Allied autumn offensive of 1915, conceived by Sir John French under pressure from Joffre to demonstrate British commitment to the coalition war. It was the first time the British employed poison gas on [[france|the Western Front]], and the first major test of Kitchener's New Army divisions in a large-scale offensive. The battle opened on 25 September 1915 and continued, in diminishing intensity, through October.

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s battalion was present at Loos, and the experience left an indelible mark on the memoir's understanding of military planning. When [[durley|Durley]] remarks that the staff "had allowed the infantry to educate themselves at Loos, regardless of expense," he is voicing the memoir's central critique: that tactical learning was purchased with soldiers' lives [012-005].

## The Event

The memoir does not provide a sustained narrative of Loos — the battle occurred before the memoir's main chronological span — but it is repeatedly invoked as a reference point. When the battalion sets out for the Somme in July 1916, the men feel "that perhaps this was only going to be a second edition of the Battle of Loos" [012-007], the anticlimax of that phrase capturing the soldier's wary fatalism. The comparison is not flattering: Loos had become, in battalion memory, the archetype of a battle that promised much and delivered nothing but casualties.

## In the Memoir

Loos appears in the memoir primarily as memory and comparison. The veterans who had been through it — [[durley|Durley]], the older N[[conscientious-objection|CO]]s, the battalion's collective memory — invoke it as a cautionary tale. [[durley|Durley]]'s remark about the staff "learning their job as they go along, like the rest of us" is the memoir's most compressed judgment on the relationship between planning and experience: the staff officers who plan battles are learning at the same rate as the men who die in them [012-005].

[[david-cromlech|Cromlech]]'s veteran status is partly defined by Loos: "It was something to have been in the [[the-somme-offensive|Battle of the Somme]]; but to have been at the Battle of Loos as well made him feel quite a big gun" [023-008]. The hierarchy of battle experience — Loos, Somme, Arras — is one the memoir records without endorsing, the grim arithmetic of survival conferring an authority that no decoration can match.

## Casualties & Aftermath

The memoir does not enumerate Loos casualties. Its aftermath is registered indirectly: in the battalion's institutional memory, in the fatalism of veterans, in the unspoken understanding that battles are not won but survived. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s brother Hamo was killed at Gallipoli in the same autumn — a loss that, when combined with the battalion's Loos experience, helped form the emotional landscape from which the memoir's anti-war feeling would eventually emerge.

## Sassoon's Commentary

The memoir's treatment of Loos is characteristically indirect. It never says "Loos was a disaster." Instead, it records the way men talk about Loos — the irony, the understatement, the bone-deep skepticism about military planning that the battle instilled. When Holman complains about a fellow officer's "hot air about the Battle of Loos, and his brainwaves about who really wrote the Bible" [016-010], the pairing of battle and Biblical authorship is not random: both are subjects on which men hold strong opinions unsupported by evidence.
