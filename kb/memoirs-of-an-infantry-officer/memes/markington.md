# Mr. Markington

The editor of the *Unconservative Weekly*, a Liberal newspaper whose political dissent and personal encouragement play a catalytic role in [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s decision to make a public protest against the War.

## In the Memoir

Sassoon first meets Markington at his London club after impulsively writing to suggest that the *Unconservative Weekly* publish an outspoken account of infantry experience. "My luncheon with Mr. Markington was the result of a letter impulsively written from Nutwood Manor" [040-003].

Markington is described as "a sallow spectacled man with earnest uncompromising eyes and a stretched sort of mouth which looked as if it had ceased to find human follies funny" [040-003].

Markington's political commentary profoundly affects Sassoon. He tells Sassoon that England had been asked by the new Russian Government to state its War Aims and publish secret treaties, but had refused [040-007].

He is "bitter against the military caste in all countries" [040-007] and informs Sassoon that "what we were fighting for was the Mesopotamian Oil Wells" [040-008] — a revelation that transforms Sassoon's understanding of the conflict.

## Catalyst for Protest

Sassoon later returns to Markington's office, declaring he has "made up my mind that I ought to do something about it" [040-009]. Markington advises caution but also provides practical help — directing Sassoon to [[thornton-tyrrell|Thornton Tyrrell]] for further counsel [040-009]. Tyrrell confirms his readiness to assist after reading "Markington's letter" [041-001].

Back at [[butley|Butley]], Sassoon reads "the last few numbers of Markington's weekly" as preparation for his protest [043-001], though [[aunt-evelyn|Aunt Evelyn]] disapproves of the paper.

## The Liberal Opposition

Markington belongs to a specific historical moment: the Liberal anti-war movement of 1917, when disillusionment with the conflict had begun to fracture the political consensus. His disclosure about "the Mesopotamian Oil Wells" [040-008] was not fringe conspiracy but reflected contemporary critiques — voiced by figures like E. D.

Morel and the Union of Democratic Control — that the war had become a contest for imperial resources rather than a defence of liberal values.

Sassoon's presentation of Markington is carefully balanced. The journalist is not a caricature of the pacifist intellectual; he is "bitter" but not dogmatic, capable of caution as well as conviction.

When Sassoon arrives at his office declaring he must "do something," Markington's response is measured — he directs Sassoon to [[thornton-tyrrell|Thornton Tyrrell]], a philosopher better equipped to handle the moral dimensions of the case [040-009].

This referral suggests a man who understands the limits of his own role: Markington can provide the political context, but the existential reckoning belongs elsewhere.

The detail that Sassoon reads "the last few numbers of Markington's weekly" [043-001] at Butley, and that [[aunt-evelyn|Aunt Evelyn]] disapproves, establishes the journal as a marker of political allegiance. To read the *Unconservative Weekly* is to position oneself outside the patriotic consensus — a small act of defiance that prefigures the larger one to come.

## Significance

Markington represents the civilian political opposition to the War that Sassoon encounters during his London leave. Unlike the military authorities who demand unquestioning obedience, Markington offers an alternative moral framework — one that treats the War as a political crime rather than a patriotic duty.

His influence is instrumental: without Markington's validation of Sassoon's doubts, the memoir's climactic "wilful defiance of military authority" might never have occurred.
