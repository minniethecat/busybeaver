# Bombardment

Bombardment — the sustained artillery barrage — is the defining soundscape of [[france|the Western Front]] and a central experience in [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s memoir, representing the industrial scale of destruction that characterized modern warfare.

## Definition & Context

In WWI, bombardment referred to the prolonged artillery shelling of enemy positions, lasting from hours to days. By 1916, British artillery doctrine had evolved to employ "creeping barrages" — a moving wall of shellfire behind which infantry advanced. The Somme offensive opened with a week-long bombardment intended to destroy German [[barbed-wire|wire]] and dugouts, though its effectiveness was often limited. For the infantry, bombardment meant hours of waiting under shellfire, unable to move or retaliate, with survival dependent on chance rather than skill [010-010].

## In the Memoir

Bombardment pervades the memoir as both sound and threat. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] describes it with a mixture of technical precision and sensory overwhelm: "on the horizon the bombardment bumped and thudded in a continuous blasting" [012-002]. The verb choice — "bumped and thudded" — captures how even massive artillery, at a distance, becomes a kind of background percussion, simultaneously terrifying and oddly routine.

The memoir distinguishes between routine and intensive bombardment. "The trench was getting its usual evening bombardment, and a man had been killed" [009-006] — "usual" is devastating here, normalizing death. During the preparation for the Somme offensive, "the intense bombardment had begun that morning," and the narrator observes it with the detachment of a spectator [010-012].

The aftermath of bombardment punctuates the narrative. On a reconnaissance, "the bombardment went on steadily, with periodical outbursts of heavy gunfire" [012-007], and the narrator notes the strange peace that follows: "peaceful after a violent early bombardment" [012-012]. The alternation between noise and silence becomes a rhythm of trench life — each silence carrying the question of whether the bombardment has ended or merely paused.

## Sassoon's Perspective

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s treatment of bombardment reflects his broader critique of the war's industrialization. Artillery kills impersonally, at a distance, without the human confrontation of infantry combat. The narrator observes the bombardment of enemy lines with a kind of abstract horror — "the bombardment begins on Saturday" [010-010] — the casual planning of mass destruction rendered in matter-of-fact dialogue.

The memoir never glorifies bombardment or treats it as a spectacle of power. Instead, it registers the psychological toll: the waiting, the helplessness, the way continuous shelling erodes the distinction between sanity and breakdown. Soldiers under bombardment are reduced to passive endurance — a state fundamentally at odds with the active, purposeful masculinity that military culture valorized.

## Historical Reality

Artillery was the single greatest cause of casualties in WWI, accounting for an estimated 60–75% of all deaths and wounds. The British Army fired over 1.7 million shells during the seven-day preliminary bombardment for the Somme — roughly 170 shells per second. Despite this, German deep dugouts often survived, and many shells were duds due to manufacturing defects.

The psychological impact of bombardment produced what was then called "shell shock" — a condition whose very name linked mental breakdown to artillery exposure, though its causes were far more complex. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s memoir, written over a decade after the war, reflects the growing understanding that bombardment was not merely a physical threat but a profound psychological assault.
