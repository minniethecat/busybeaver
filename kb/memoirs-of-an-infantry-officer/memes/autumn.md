# Autumn

The autumn months on the Western Front, which in Sassoon's memoir carry a particular emotional weight — a season of renewed offensives, deepening mud, lengthening casualty lists, and the fading of any hope that the war might end before winter.

## Definition & Context

Autumn on the Western Front typically brought a second wave of offensives after the summer campaigns. The "autumn attacks" of 1916 and 1917 — continuing the Somme and launching Passchendaele — were characterized by deteriorating weather, heavy rain, and ground that turned to deep mud. For the infantry, autumn was a season of grim endurance between the intensity of summer fighting and the frozen misery of winter.

## In the Memoir

Autumn appears in the memoir as a season of accumulated loss. Sassoon refers to "the autumn attacks" as "a sprawling muddle of attrition" [025-005] and notes how death piled "agony on agony, toward the autumn" [016-014]. The season carries sensory power: an "autumn-smelling air" with an owl hooting [020-007], "misty autumn mornings, which made me free of the war" [021-004], and the "smell of an apple orchard in autumn" [034-009]. These pastoral notes are undercut by the toll: "Rees was killed before the autumn" [032-005] and another comrade "was killed in the autumn up at Ypres" [034-018]. Sassoon's hope that "the War would be over by next autumn" [028-001] becomes a recurring, disappointed refrain.

## Sassoon's Perspective

Sassoon's treatment of autumn is characteristically dual: the beauty of misty mornings and orchard scents is inseparable from the knowledge of death. Autumn becomes the season when the memoir's elegiac quality is most pronounced. The falling leaves and shortening days mirror the attrition of the battalion's officers — each autumn brings a new crop of replacements and a new round of losses.

## Historical Reality

The autumn offensives of 1916 (Somme continued through November) and 1917 (Passchendaele/Third Ypres, July–November) were among the costliest campaigns of the war. Sassoon's portrayal of autumn as a season of grinding, inconclusive attrition accurately reflects the historical reality of Western Front warfare, where autumn rains turned battlefields into quagmires.
