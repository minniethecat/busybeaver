# Patrol

The hazardous practice of sending small parties of men into no-man's-land or the forward trench system, ostensibly to gather intelligence or harass the enemy, which [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] uses as a narrative device to compress the war's terror, absurdity, and moral complexity into concentrated night scenes.

## Definition & Context

Patrolling was a standard duty of infantry units on [[france|the Western Front]], intended to dominate the ground between the opposing trench lines, gather intelligence on enemy dispositions, and maintain "offensive spirit." Patrols varied from quiet reconnaissance — crawling out to check the state of the German [[barbed-wire|wire]] — to fighting patrols designed to engage the enemy and take prisoners [002-005][005-007].

For junior officers, patrol duty was one of the most psychologically demanding responsibilities. The officer leading a patrol was alone with his decisions in the darkness, responsible for the lives of men who would be killed or captured if anything went wrong. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s memoir registers this pressure with unusual candor.

## In the Memoir

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s most extended treatment of patrol duty comes in the context of the [[mametz|Mametz sector]], where Corporal O'Brien — a figure of professional competence — had "done two patrols last week" [005-007]. The patrol is presented as a test of nerve and skill, a routine whose dangers are so constant as to become almost invisible.

But the memoir's defining patrol scene is the trench raid in Chapter 7, which is essentially a fighting patrol writ large. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s description of waiting in the dark while his men crawl into enemy territory captures the patrol experience in miniature: the rain, the flares, the "ominous silence," and the sudden eruption of violence that reduces careful plans to chaos [007-007][007-009].

Later in the memoir, patrol appears in a more reflective register. In Chapter 33, [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] is ordered to patrol his front and reflects on the futility of the exercise — a recognition that patrolling has become a bureaucratic ritual, something officers do because they are expected to, regardless of its tactical value [033-010].

## Sassoon's Perspective

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] treats patrol duty with the same double vision he brings to all of [[trench-warfare|trench warfare]]. The patrol is genuinely dangerous — men do die on patrols — and the officer who leads one is performing a real duty. But the patrol is also a species of theater, an activity maintained because the staff expects "aggressive patrolling" whether or not it yields useful intelligence.

The patrol officer, in [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s telling, occupies an impossible position: responsible for the success of a mission whose value he may not believe in, and for the lives of men whose deaths would be recorded in the [[official-language|communiqué]] as a "successful patrol" regardless of its actual outcome.

## Historical Reality

Patrol reports were among the most common documents generated by infantry units on [[france|the Western Front]]. The British Army's emphasis on "dominating no-man's-land" meant that patrols were required even during quiet periods, and the casualty rate from patrol duty — while lower than that of major offensives — was cumulatively significant. The distinction between "patrol" and "raid" was often blurred in practice, with the larger raids being essentially patrols with a specific objective (capturing prisoners, destroying a machine-gun post).
