# Royal Flying Corps

The Royal Flying Corps was the air arm of the British Army during World War I — a new and glamorous service whose officers, in the memoir, represent an alternative to the mud-bound existence of the infantry, and whose appeal to Sassoon's circle reveals the desperation with which front-line officers sought escape from the trenches.

## Identity & Structure

The Royal Flying Corps (R.F.C.) was established in 1912 as the British Army's aerial warfare branch. By the period of the memoir (1916–1917), it had expanded rapidly from a small pre-war cadre into a large service operating reconnaissance, artillery-spotting, and fighter squadrons over the Western Front.

The R.F.C. occupied a distinct cultural position within the British Army: its officers were drawn from the same social class as the infantry but lived apart — literally, above — the trench war. Its mortality rate was appallingly high, but its mythology was of individual combat, clean machines, and escape from the collective anonymity of the infantry.

## In the Memoir

The Royal Flying Corps appears in the memoir not as an operational force but as an object of fantasy. [[mansfield|Mansfield]] — one of Sassoon's fellow officers — is described as "playing at 'being in the Royal Flying Corps'" [027-008]. The verb "playing" captures the memoir's double attitude: the R.F.C. is desirable, but the desire itself is childish, a schoolboy fantasy of glamour that the reality of aerial combat would quickly dispel.

The memoir records its narrator's own "forlorn attempt to enter the Flying Corps" [037-005], an effort that "succeeded in being rejected." The adjective "forlorn" carries the weight of the confession: Sassoon presents his application to the R.F.C. not as a considered career choice but as a symptom of desperation — anything, even the lethally dangerous air war, seemed preferable to another tour in the trenches.

## War Record

The Royal Flying Corps fought throughout World War I, playing increasingly critical roles in reconnaissance and air superiority. In April 1917 — the period covered by the later chapters of the memoir — the R.F.C. suffered catastrophic losses during "Bloody April," losing 245 aircraft and over 400 aircrew. The memoir makes no reference to these losses; its interest in the R.F.C. is psychological rather than operational.

## Sassoon's Perspective

Sassoon's treatment of the Royal Flying Corps is characteristically ironic. He neither romanticizes the air war nor dismisses its appeal. The R.F.C. serves the memoir as a measure of trench desperation: when men who have survived the Somme apply to transfer into a service whose life expectancy was measured in weeks, the calculus of front-line existence has become profoundly distorted.

The memoir's most telling R.F.C. detail is its smallest: Sassoon records his rejection without comment, as if failure even to escape the infantry was, by this point in the war, simply to be expected.

## Historical Counterpart

The Royal Flying Corps was a real service, which merged with the Royal Naval Air Service in April 1918 to form the Royal Air Force. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] uses the real name; the R.F.C. was too large and well-known an institution to require fictionalization.
