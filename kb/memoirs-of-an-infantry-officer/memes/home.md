# Home

Home functions in [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s memoir not as a simple geographical location but as a psychological polar opposite to the front — a remembered world of comfort, peace, and civilian identity that the trenches render both achingly desirable and increasingly inaccessible.

## Definition & Context

In the context of [[france|the Western Front]], "[[england|home]]" carried multiple overlapping meanings. It denoted [[england|England]] itself — the green [[landscape|countryside]], the domestic routines, the civilian life that continued in the soldier's absence. It also referred to "[[england|home]] service" — military postings within [[england|Britain]], which carried a stigma among frontline officers who regarded them as a form of shirking. Most potently, "[[england|home]]" represented the individual soldier's private world: a particular house, a family, the life that existed before August 1914 and might never be recovered. The gulf between the trenches and [[england|home]] was not merely geographical but existential; as the war ground on, [[england|home]] became for many soldiers a place that existed more vividly in memory than it could ever again in reality [003-006].

## In the Memoir

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] invokes [[england|home]] from the memoir's earliest pages, establishing it as the emotional baseline against which the war's depredations are measured. A wounded soldier's last words are reported matter-of-factly: "he said that he would never see his [[england|home]] in Wiltshire again" [003-006]. The line is devastating precisely because it is offered without comment — the reader understands that this man's private [[england|England]], his particular Wiltshire, is now permanently beyond reach.

The memoir tracks [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s own complicated relationship with [[england|home]] throughout the narrative. There is the childhood memory of "the grandfather clock in the hall at [[england|home]]" [015-002], one of the sensory anchors that the narrator carries into the trenches. There are the moments of leave — "driving [[england|home]] from cricket matches before the war" [016-013] recalled as a talisman against present misery. And there are the soldiers who have made themselves "quite at [[england|home]]" [014-015] in the trenches, a phrase that captures the terrible normalization of the front-line world.

Home becomes most charged when the possibility of reaching it appears. The narrator's wound — the "[[blighty-wound|Blighty one]]" that every soldier hoped for — is described in language of almost religious deliverance: "Good luck had 'wangled me [[england|home]]'" [018-009]. The colloquial verb captures the randomness of survival; no virtue or courage earns the journey [[england|home]], only the blind operation of chance. On the hospital train, "Peaceable stay-at-[[england|home]]s waved to the Red Cross Train" [018-011], the civilians separated from the soldiers by an unbridgeable gulf of experience.

## Sassoon's Perspective

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s treatment of [[england|home]] is marked by a growing ambivalence that mirrors his political radicalization. Early in the memoir, [[england|home]] is a straightforward source of comfort and longing. By its later sections, the narrator has begun to recognize that "[[england|home]]" is also the place that sustains the war — the civilians who "seemed to know that the long-expected war had begun" [010-001] without understanding what it meant, the "scrupulous people at [[england|home]] getting thin" [019-002] through voluntary rationing while soldiers died.

The narrator's own periods of "[[england|home]] service" [025-006] become increasingly intolerable — not because [[england|home]] has lost its comfort, but because the comfort itself feels like complicity. The memoir's great political achievement is to show how the trenches redefined [[england|home]], making it not a refuge from war but an extension of the machinery that produced it.

## Historical Reality

The division between the Home Front and [[france|the Western Front]] was one of the defining features of the British experience of the Great War. The British Expeditionary Force was a volunteer army for the first two years of the war; the men who served had chosen to leave [[england|home]], and the [[england|home]] they left behind was intensely conscious of their sacrifice. Yet the distance between the two worlds — measured not in miles but in mutual incomprehension — became a source of profound alienation for returning soldiers.

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s memoir captures this alienation before it had a name. The "stay-at-[[england|home]]s" [018-011] who wave at hospital trains are not villains; they are simply inhabitants of a world that the soldier can no longer fully enter. The memoir's treatment of [[england|home]] anticipates the postwar literature of disillusionment, but its tone is elegiac rather than accusatory — [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] mourns the loss of [[england|home]] as he mourns the loss of the men who will never see theirs again.
