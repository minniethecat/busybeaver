# Young Ormand

A junior officer of the [[second-battalion|Second Battalion, Flintshire Fusiliers]] — barely more than a boy when he arrived in [[france|France]], whose presence runs through the memoir as a thread of youthful resilience, and whose death in the final months of the war becomes one of the narrative's most quietly devastating losses.

## Role & Identity

Ormand was one of the subalterns who served alongside [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] in the [[second-battalion|Second Battalion]]'s front-line companies. He is consistently described as "young Ormand" — the epithet itself a marker of the memoir's protective, almost paternal regard. He had been at Sandhurst shortly before deployment, a product of the accelerated officer training that the war's insatiable demand for subalterns had made necessary [009-002].

His social world is that of the junior officers' mess — the camaraderie of young men thrown together by circumstance, sharing the peculiar intimacies of trench life. He appears in scenes of domestic routine (sitting round the table with Mansfield and the narrator) and in the tense intervals between actions, "pulling wry faces" and making "periodic remarks" about the duration of bombardments [005-004][023-003][023-004].

## In the Memoir

Ormand's first substantial appearance is as one of the original members of [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s company, present in the early chapters that establish the texture of battalion life before the Somme. He and Corporal O'Brien had "done well in a raid" early in their service, earning the respect that combat performance conferred [005-007]. His presence at Crawley Ridge during the preliminary bombardment for the Somme offensive is recorded with a visual precision that suggests the narrator's protective attention [006-008].

A significant structural role Ormand plays is as companion on the journey to the [[second-battalion|Second Battalion]] — he rides with [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] from the [[first-battalion|First Battalion]] posting, and his "cheerfulness diminished as we got nearer" is a quiet register of the fear that the memoir's understatement cannot entirely conceal [030-009][030-010]. This shared journey makes him, for the reader, a figure of identification: the young man who is afraid but does not complain.

His death, when it comes, is reported through indirection — through [[dottrell|Dottrell]]'s letters, through the narrator's reflex of deflection ("I tried to think of something else"), through the final image of him "dying miserably out in a shell-hole" [038-021][041-003]. The memoir never narrates the death directly; it arrives, as such news did for those away from the battalion, through the mediated form of letters and reports.

## Significance

Ormand personifies the memoir's central elegy: the destruction of youth. He is never given a full name, a backstory, or a distinctive psychology — and this is precisely the point. He stands for the thousands of young officers whose individuality the war erased before it had a chance to develop, remembered in fragments by those who survived.

The memoir's treatment of his death is also significant for what it reveals about [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s narrative strategy. Rather than a set-piece death scene — the kind that war memoirs of an earlier generation might have provided — Ormand's end is reported, reflected upon, and then absorbed into the narrator's accumulating burden of grief. The indirection is a form of honesty: grief in wartime was rarely afforded the space for full expression; it was processed alongside the continuing demands of survival.

## Historical Counterpart

Ormand is believed to be a composite or lightly fictionalised portrait of one or more junior officers who served with [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] in the [[second-battalion|2nd Battalion]], [[royal-welch-fusiliers|Royal Welch Fusiliers]]. The memoir's consistent use of the epithet "young" and its refusal to supply biographical detail suggest either a composite character drawing on multiple real individuals, or a deliberate narrative choice to make Ormand representative rather than particular. This is in keeping with [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s method in the [[sassoon-siegfried|Sherston]] trilogy, where characters often serve dual roles as individuals and as types.
