# Billet

A billet — the lodgings assigned to soldiers behind the lines, usually in French civilian homes, barns, or village buildings — recurs throughout Sassoon's memoir as a site of contrast: between the front line and the rear, between civilian domesticity and military life, between comfort and the anticipation of violence.

## The Transition from Trench to Village

Sassoon frequently marks the distance between front-line existence and billeting arrangements. Arriving in Amiens after a period in the trenches, he observes troops "going placidly to their billets" amid blossoming fruit trees [002-004]. The contrast is explicit: "I had been in close contact with trench warfare, and here was a demonstration of its contrast with cosy civilian comfort" [002-004]. A billet is never merely accommodation — it is a reminder of the world the war has displaced, a world of "green grass and a miller's wagon with four horses, and the spire of Amiens Cathedral rising above the congregated roofs of an undamaged city" [002-004].

## The Billet as Domestic Space

Individual billets are rendered with attention to their civilian character. Returning to familiar lodgings, Sassoon notes how "the horsechestnut in front of the house was in flower and there were a few peonies and pink roses in the neglected little garden at the back" [005-003]. The garden's neglect signals the civilian space's partial reclamation by military occupation — cared for enough to flower, but not tended. Inside, the billet becomes a space of uneasy rest: "Flook had spread my 'fleabag' on the tiled floor, and I had soon slipped into it and blown out my candle" [005-006]. The sleeping bag on a floor, the shared room with another officer — these details define billet life as provisional and stripped of comfort, even as the architecture is civilian.

## The Gap Between Officers and Men

Sassoon is acute about how the billet reasserts class distinctions that shared danger temporarily erases. In the line, "one builds up a sort of comradeship with the tough and willing ones," but "back in billets the gap widens and one can't do much to cheer them up" [009-003]. The officer returns to his billet — perhaps a house with a garden — while the men occupy barns or outbuildings. The spatial separation maps onto social hierarchy. This candid acknowledgment of the limits of cross-class comradeship is characteristic of Sassoon's unsentimental realism.

## The Billet on the Eve of Battle

Billets acquire a particular tension just before an attack. On a morning when the Battalion is about to move up to the front, Sassoon describes how "on the horizon the bombardment bumped and thudded in a continuous bubbling grumble" [012-002]. The billet, momentarily peaceful, becomes saturated with dread. He watches "the white dusty road, past a field where a flock of pigeons rose up and wheeled away" [012-002] — the peaceful tableau fractured by the percussion of distant guns. The billet is where men wait, where they exist in the gap between safety and the line, and where the ordinary textures of life — a dusty road, wheeling pigeons — become charged with the imminence of death.

## The Billet in Movement: La Chaussée and Corbie

The billet is not only a static rear-area refuge; it also punctuates the Battalion's movements across the landscape of the war. When Sassoon returns to his company before the Somme offensive, he finds them "billeted in some buildings round a friendly farmhouse" near Morlancourt [010-010]. The billet here becomes a theatre of social life: "a comedy of primitive courtship was being enacted in the kitchen" as the servants compete for the favours of a farmhouse woman, while outside "the cool night smelt of mown grass and leafy gardens" and "bullfrogs croaked continuously in the marshes along the river" [010-011]. Returning to his own billet across the road, Sassoon "almost fell over a goat which was tethered among some currant bushes in the garden" [010-011]. The goat, the currant bushes, the bullfrogs — these are the textures of a French village that persists underneath military occupation, and Sassoon records them with an affection that deepens the reader's awareness of what the war threatens.

Movement also means the degradation of billets. After the Australians have passed through La Chaussée, Sassoon reports that they left the billets "in a very bad state" — "Sanitation had been neglected, and the inhabitants were complaining furiously that their furniture had been used for firewood" [018-003]. The note is brief but pointed: billeting is a practice that wears on the civilian world, and different units leave different traces. Later, when Sassoon himself is evacuated from La Chaussée, his servant Flook retrieves the belongings he left behind, "treading in with clumsy embarrassment to deposit the packful of oddments by my bed, announcing in a hoarse undertone, 'Ah've brought the stoof'" [018-008]. The billet, in this valedictory moment, becomes the site of the relationship between officer and servant — a bond that is at once military hierarchy and genuine mutual care.

In Corbie, the billet takes on a more comic register. After a night of bad champagne, Sassoon finds himself navigating "the dark little shop, which was full of strong-smelling vegetables," before climbing to "an unventilated cupboard which reeked of onions" [031-022]. The greengrocer's shop, the onion reek, the steep stairs — these are the unheroic realities of a billet that no military history would record, but which Sassoon preserves with the precision of a diarist who knows that such details are the war's real fabric.

## Historical Reality

Billeting was a standard feature of the Western Front's rotational system. Units spent roughly a third of their time in the front line, a third in support, and a third in reserve or rest — the latter typically spent in billets in villages behind the lines. The French civilian population, particularly in the Somme region, lived in uneasy proximity to British troops throughout the war. Sassoon's record captures the ambivalent texture of this arrangement — gratitude for comfort, guilt at its availability, and the persistent knowledge that every billet was only a temporary reprieve.
