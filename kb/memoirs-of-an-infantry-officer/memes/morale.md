# Morale

Morale — the spirit that enables soldiers to endure, the fragile texture of collective resilience, the officer's responsibility to sustain what circumstances constantly erode — is a concept that Sassoon approaches obliquely. He rarely names morale directly, preferring to show it through small gestures, moments of cheerfulness in the face of despair, and the quiet effort required to maintain it.

## Sustaining Morale Under Fire

Sassoon records an instance of the officer's morale-sustaining role with characteristic understatement: "When I had posted the exhausted sentries, with as much cheeriness as I could muster, I went along to look for the Company on our left" [033-009]. The phrase "as much cheeriness as I could muster" captures the performance that morale requires — the officer must project confidence he does not feel, must appear untroubled when he is exhausted, must manufacture cheerfulness as a military duty. Morale here is not a spontaneous feeling but a deliberate act of will, sustained for the sake of the men who depend on it.

## Morale and the Officer's Burden

The memoir repeatedly shows officers managing not only their own fear but the collective emotional state of their units. When Sassoon describes "cheering as if at a football match" during the Somme [013-007], the simile suggests both the artificiality of morale — it must be performed, like spectatorship — and its necessity. Men facing machine-gun fire need to believe they are participating in something other than their own destruction. The officer's role includes sustaining this necessary fiction, even as he knows it to be a fiction.

## The Fragility of Morale

Sassoon is honest about morale's limits. The exhausted sentries, the men on working parties with "nothing to look forward to but going on patrols," the recurring image of soldiers pushed past the point where cheerfulness is sustainable — these accumulate across the memoir to suggest that morale is always provisional, always one more bombardment away from collapse. When Sherston himself "cracks up" — when his recklessness becomes indistinguishable from suicidal despair — the memoir registers the point at which morale, having been stretched too far, becomes its opposite.

## Morale in Retrospect

At Cambridge, looking back, Sassoon observes: "But here was Cambridge, looking contented enough in the afternoon sunshine, as though the Long Vacation were on" [042-010]. The university's unruffled contentment is a bitter contrast to the morale he sustained and saw shattered at the front. The civilian world's morale — its ability to continue as if nothing had changed — is revealed as a form of ignorance, not resilience. True morale, the memoir implies, is what soldiers maintained while knowing exactly what they faced; civilian morale is merely the absence of that knowledge.

## Morale and the Officer's Role

The maintenance of morale fell disproportionately on junior officers, who lived among their men and absorbed the daily strain of trench conditions. Sassoon's own position as a platoon and later company officer placed him at the intersection of command authority and personal intimacy with his soldiers. The memoir records small gestures — a cheery greeting from the Medical Officer [006-004], the shared routine of the Company Mess [005-002] — that cumulatively sustain morale without ever being named as such.

This is consistent with Sassoon's narrative method: he rarely tells the reader what to feel, preferring to show the conditions under which feeling becomes possible or impossible. Morale is not a theme he analyzes but a texture he conveys through the accumulation of detail — the men "keeping their spirits up as vocally as they could" [012-006] before an attack, the "spirit of mischievous destruction" [016-004] that possesses soldiers handling unused ammunition. These moments capture morale not as an abstract military category but as the lived quality of collective endurance.

## Morale and the Failure of Institutions

The memoir is also attentive to what undermines morale. The gap between Brigade's planning and the men's experience — "all that bosh which made Brigade think they were busy" [009-002] — erodes trust in the institutional structures that soldiers depend on. Sassoon's critique of the raid as a wasteful performance designed to impress higher command [006-002] is fundamentally a critique of how military bureaucracy damages the morale it purports to sustain.

The court-martial scene, in which "The court had recommended the prisoner to mercy" [030-010] only for the sentence to be confirmed, suggests another institutional failure: military justice, ostensibly designed to maintain discipline, instead reveals the indifference of the system to individual soldiers. Morale, in Sassoon's rendering, survives despite institutions, not because of them.
