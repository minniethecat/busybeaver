# Prisoner

The figure of the prisoner — the German soldier captured in a trench raid, or the possibility of becoming one — threads through Sassoon's memoir as a marker of shared humanity across the lines. Prisoners are rarely central to the action, but their presence, or conspicuous absence, reveals the memoir's quiet insistence that the enemy is ultimately a man like any other.

## The Goal of the Raid

In the memoir's extended account of the trench raid, prisoners are the stated objective. Joe Dottrell explains the tactical logic: "the idea had been revived early this year, when some Canadian toughs had pulled off a fine effort, and since then such entertainments had become popular with the Staff" [006-002]. But he adds a grim note: the Second Battalion's recent raid had "casualties numerous and no prisoners were brought back" [006-002]. The prisoner, in the Staff's calculus, is an object of intelligence value; to the men who must capture him, he is a deadly risk. The gap between these perspectives is one of Sassoon's recurring critiques of the war's command structure.

## The Raid's Design

The operational orders for the raid specify its objectives with bureaucratic clarity: "to enter the enemy loop on the edge of the crater; to enter Kiel Trench at two points; to examine the portions of trench thus isolated, capture prisoners, bomb dugouts, and kill Germans" [007-006]. The list is jarring in its matter-of-factness — "capture prisoners" sits between "examine the portions of trench" and "bomb dugouts, and kill Germans" as items on a checklist. The prisoner is a bureaucratic category before he is a human being. Sassoon's flat rendering of the orders allows the reader to register the dissonance without authorial commentary.

## Prisoners on the Somme

During the Battle of the Somme, Sassoon observes German soldiers surrendering: "I can see the 21st Division advancing about three-quarters of a mile away on the left and a few Germans coming to meet them, apparently surrendering" [013-015]. The word "apparently" captures the uncertainty of the battlefield encounter — surrendering soldiers might be genuine, or they might be a ruse. But the image of men walking toward their enemy with hands raised also restores the human dimension that combat erases. The German prisoner is no longer an abstraction but a man making the most dangerous decision of his life — to trust his enemy.

## The Unseen Prisoner

Notably, Sassoon's memoir contains almost no direct depiction of German prisoners being interrogated or processed. They appear at the margins — "a few Germans coming to meet them, apparently surrendering" [013-015] — and then vanish from the narrative. This absence itself is telling. The memoir is relentlessly focused on the experience of the British officer; the German soldier remains, even when captured, fundamentally unknowable. But Sassoon's refusal to demonize or caricature the enemy — his steady implication that the men on the other side of no-man's-land are as trapped, as frightened, as human as his own comrades — is one of the memoir's most significant moral accomplishments.

## Historical Reality

Trench raids were a regular feature of the Western Front from 1915 onward, and capturing prisoners for intelligence was among their primary objectives. Prisoners provided information about enemy unit identification, morale, and defensive preparations. The difficulty and danger of capturing prisoners — as Sassoon's account makes clear — meant that raids frequently resulted in heavy casualties with no prisoners taken. German treatment of British POWs was generally consistent with the Hague Conventions, though conditions in POW camps were harsh. Sassoon's memoir, while not directly addressing the POW experience, registers the prisoner as a figure who destabilizes the binary of enemy and comrade.


## The Prisoner as Spectacle

The memoir also registers the prisoner as a figure of public display. Sassoon records the "sight of a handcuffed German prisoner" being led through the rear areas [015-005] — a man who would "soon provide material for the photographers" [015-005]. The prisoner here is not a combatant but an exhibit, a trophy whose function is to reassure those behind the lines that the war is being won.

This spectacle is contrasted with the actual experience of capture. In the aftermath of the Somme offensive, "processions of prisoners were brought down" [016-012] — the impersonal plural "processions" reducing individual men to a category. Sassoon's attention to the single handcuffed figure, and the photographers who will exploit him, suggests an unease with the way prisoners are transformed from men into propaganda.

## The Court-Martial Prisoner

The memoir's other prisoner is the British soldier facing military justice. Sassoon witnesses a court-martial where "The court had recommended the prisoner to mercy" [030-010] — the formal language of military law, with its careful distinction between verdict and recommendation, its procedural decorum standing in stark contrast to the arbitrary violence of the front. The British prisoner, unlike the German, is subject to an elaborate legal apparatus, and Sassoon's brief account of the proceeding registers the strangeness of applying legal forms to the conditions of trench warfare.
