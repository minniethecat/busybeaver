# Military Telegram

The **military telegram** was the swiftest form of written communication on the Western Front, carrying orders, summonses, and news through the Army's telegraph network. In Sassoon's *Memoirs of an Infantry Officer*, the telegram is an instrument of remote authority — a few words on a slip of paper that can end uncertainty, deliver reprieve, or compel a soldier's return to the front.

## The Medium of Command

The telegram's power lies in its brevity. When Sassoon's leave is interrupted, "the conundrum was solved by a telegram, *Report how situated*. There was nothing for it but to obey the terse instructions" [044-003]. The phrase "terse instructions" captures the telegram's essence: it does not explain, justify, or persuade. It commands, and the recipient obeys.

A second telegram is even more compressed: "it contained only two words, *Report immediately*" and "was obviously a telegram which did not need to be read twice" [045-001].

Two words are sufficient to upend a life — to pull a man from the safety of England back to the trenches. The telegram is the technological embodiment of military hierarchy, a voice that cannot be questioned because it offers nothing to question.

## Telegrams of Life and Death

The memoir also records the telegram as a bearer of unexpected news. Sassoon learns that David Cromlech, believed killed, is alive: "I had seen his name in the newspaper list of killed, but soon afterwards someone telegraphed to tell me that he was in a London hospital and going on well." The telegram reverses death — a friend mourned for a fortnight is restored by a message through the wire.

This redemptive telegram stands in contrast to the summons-telegrams from Clitherland [044-003] [045-001]. The same technology that resurrects the dead also commands the living back to the place where they may die. The telegram is morally neutral; it is the content — and the sender — that determines whether it brings relief or dread.

## A New Variety of Suspense

Sassoon observes that the second Clitherland telegram "put me out of my misery" [045-001] — a wry formulation that acknowledges how even an unwelcome certainty is better than waiting. The first telegram demanded a report; the second demanded his person.

Between the two, a different quality of anxiety: not the paralysis of not knowing, but the dread of what is already decided and merely not yet experienced.

The telegram arrives, is read, and is obeyed. There is no reply, no negotiation, no appeal. In this, it mirrors the experience of the infantry officer himself — a recipient of orders he did not author, delivered through channels he cannot access, compelling actions he may not survive.

## The Telegram as Narrative Device

Beyond its military function, the telegram serves the memoir as a narrative hinge. Sassoon uses it to close one chapter and open the next — the arrival of the second Clitherland telegram ends the leave sequence and propels the narrator back toward the front [045-001].

The telegram's very concision — two words, "Report immediately" — mirrors the structure of a chapter ending: abrupt, definitive, leaving the reader with the same sinking sensation as the recipient.

In this sense, Sassoon transforms a military document into a literary device, letting the form of the communication carry the emotional weight that its content only implies.

The telegram also marks the boundary between worlds. Receiving it at home — in the civilian comfort of England — the narrator is instantly transferred, in psychic terms, back to the military reality. The slip of paper dissolves the distance between the home front and the firing line, and for a moment the reader experiences what the soldier knows: that the war is never more than a telegram away.
