# Corporal

## Overview

The **corporal** was the junior non-commissioned officer rank in the British infantry of the First World War — the lowest rung of the command ladder, positioned between the private soldier and the sergeant. In Sassoon's memoir, corporals and lance-corporals appear as men who lead from within the ranks rather than above them: they share the same dangers as the privates but carry the additional burden of responsibility. The rank is embodied most vividly in Corporal O'Brien, the Irish N.C.O. whose steady competence anchors the trench raid narrative, and Lance-Corporal Kendle, whose death is one of the memoir's most affecting losses.

## Corporal O'Brien

Corporal O'Brien appears first in the context of patrol work: "He and Ormand and Corporal O'Brien had done two patrols last week, but the bright moonlight had prevented them from properly inspecting the German wire" [005-007]. The phrasing places O'Brien alongside the officers — Ormand is a fellow subaltern — suggesting the trust placed in this particular N.C.O. to carry out dangerous nocturnal work.

On the night of the trench raid, O'Brien accompanies Sergeant Miles and Captain Mansfield on the final reconnaissance: "In the meantime Mansfield was making a final reconnaissance of the ground with Sergeant Miles and Corporal O'Brien" [007-002]. O'Brien is the third man in the command group, the junior N.C.O. who knows the ground as well as his seniors.

O'Brien also appears in a vignette during a later defensive action, described at a moment of chaos during the attack near Fontaine-lès-Croisilles. He is shown as the competent counterpoint to an inexperienced officer — "the corporal had a cooler head and he advised discretion" [034-016] — the junior N.C.O. whose battlefield experience outstrips his rank.

## The Cameronian Corporal

During the confused attack, a Cameronian corporal appears silently: "I saw that he was a Cameronian corporal; we did not speak" [034-015]. In the chaos of battle, the corporal becomes an unspoken ally: "After that I went bombing busily along, while the corporal (more artful and efficient than I was) dodged in and out of the saps — a precaution which I should have forgotten" [034-015]. The corporal knows his way around the trench system: "I had no idea where our objective was, but the corporal informed me that we had reached it, and he seemed to know his business" [034-015]. The scene is a study in the gap between officer status and N.C.O. competence — the corporal is the one who actually knows what to do.

## Lance-Corporal Kendle

Kendle is one of the few named casualties whose death Sassoon records with personal grief. He is introduced as "a nineteen year old lance-corporal from my platoon" [012-011], working alongside Worgan to repair the wire — an image of ordinary duty, "slicing away for all they were worth." The wire-cutting is futile, "the unimpressive effect of the snippings" visible as the light increases, and the work itself prefigures Kendle's fate.

Kendle dies not in a grand attack but in the random violence of a shell: "The circumstances being what they were, I had no justification for feeling either shocked or astonished by the sudden extinction of Lance-Corporal Kendle" [014-016]. The sentence is characteristic of Sassoon's emotional restraint — the feeling is there, but it is acknowledged only to be questioned. Sassoon cannot justify his shock because shock was the permanent condition of trench life, yet the loss registers.

Kendle returns to Sassoon's thoughts much later, in a hut behind the lines: "I thought of Dick Tiltwood, and how he used to come into this hut with such shining evidences of youth in his face; and of dark-haired little Fernby, who was just such another; and of Lance-Corporal Kendle, and all those others whose violent deaths had become a blurred memory" [025-003]. Kendle belongs to a litany of the dead — Tiltwood, Fernby, Kendle, "all those others" — the lance-corporal remembered alongside the young officers, his rank no barrier to grief.

## The Grey-Haired Lance-Corporal

A brief but vivid image appears after the trench raid: "Among them was a grey-haired lance-corporal, who had one of his feet almost blown off; I half carried him in and when he was sitting on the fire-step he said, 'Thank God Almighty for this; I've been waiting eighteen months for it and now I can go home'" [007-008]. The grey-haired lance-corporal is an older man who has survived eighteen months at the front, and his wound is a deliverance — a "Blighty one" that will send him home. His gratitude for mutilation is one of the memoir's most damning indictments of the war.
