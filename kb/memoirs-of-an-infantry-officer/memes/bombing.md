# Bombing

In WWI terminology, "bombing" referred to grenade-throwing in trench combat — a specialised and particularly hazardous infantry role. A "bombing party" was a small group of men armed with Mills bombs who led assaults on enemy trench positions.

## Military Context

Bombing was central to trench-raiding tactics. Unlike rifle fire, which required a visible target, bombs could be thrown over traverses and around corners into dugouts and trench bays. The bombing officer was a recognised specialist role, and proficiency in bombing was a mark of an aggressive junior officer. Bombing fights — close-range exchanges of grenades across trench blocks and craters — were among the most chaotic and deadly forms of trench combat [007-009].

The Mills bomb, introduced in 1915, was the standard British hand grenade. Its serrated cast-iron body fragmented on detonation, producing a lethal radius of about thirty yards in open ground — and considerably more in the confined space of a trench bay. A bombing party typically consisted of a thrower, a carrier with a bucket of bombs, and two bayonet men to protect the thrower while he armed and threw. The work required nerve, coordination, and a particular disregard for the fact that a fumbled bomb meant death for the entire party [007-009].

Bombing also played a key role in consolidating captured positions. After an assault took a section of enemy trench, bombing parties would work outward from the captured point, clearing dugouts and trench bays with grenades while other men built barricades of sandbags and wire to block German counter-attacks. Sassoon describes this grim, methodical work: clearing a trench was "a matter of bombing from bay to bay" [032-007].

## In the Memoir

Sassoon participates in and observes multiple bombing actions. During his first raid, "the bombing and rifle fire had slackened" as the action moved to the craters [007-009]. At the crater fight, he maintains a "spirited bombing fight" before withdrawing [007-015]. Covering parties have a "bombing fight in the Wood" during the assault on the German position [014-007].

Sassoon takes charge of a bombing post at Quadrangle Trench, leaving Fernby to "look after the bombing post" [014-018]. He notes that the Germans were "bombing" along the trench during the confusion of battle [014-015]. Three unsuccessful bombing attacks are made on a German-held Tower [032-007]. During a later action, men go "over the barrier" and start "bombing up the trench" [034-013], and Sassoon himself goes "bombing busily along" [034-015].

The bombing scenes in the memoir grow progressively more surreal. The early raid accounts are detailed and tactical. By the later bombing episodes, Sassoon's prose fragments into blurred impressions: "a confused jumble of the crater fight and bombing up trenches" [037-009]. This degradation of narrative clarity mirrors the psychological toll of repeated close-quarters combat.

## Sassoon's Perspective

Sassoon treats his bombing exploits with characteristic self-deprecation. He acknowledges his proficiency — "you were very good at bombing" [046-015] — but frames it within his larger ambivalence about the war. The bombing scenes are rendered as blurry, chaotic fragments: "a blurred picture of people bombing one another up and down ditches" [037-009].

The tension between his skill at bombing and his revulsion at the war is one of the memoir's central contradictions. Sassoon was by his own account an effective bombing officer — cool under fire, aggressive when needed — yet he came to see the whole enterprise as murderous futility. The bombing episodes thus carry a double register: the tactical satisfaction of a well-executed attack, and the retrospective horror of a man remembering what he did with grenades in a trench [046-015].
