## Thesis

Sassoon's memoir is, at one level, a war book. At another level, it is a sustained examination of the British [[class|class system]] as it operated within the Army of 1914–1918. The officer/enlisted-man division reproduces the class hierarchy of civilian society and renders it lethal: men die not only because of German shells but because of a social structure that determines who gives orders, who receives them, and whose deaths are recorded with what degree of particularity.

## Evidence

### The Officer as Gentleman

The memoir's officer characters are unmistakably gentlemen: products of public schools, shaped by "the traditions of his class" [032-005]. [[dottrell|Joe Dottrell]], with his cheerful incompetence and genuine courage, embodies the type: well-meaning, physically brave, intellectually unprepared for the war he is fighting. [[ormand|Ormand]] represents the career officer, rooted in regimental tradition, viewing the war through the lens of professional obligation [023-010]. [[kinjack|Colonel Kinjack]] commands with the assured authority of a man who has never questioned his right to command.

### The Enlisted Man as Anonymous Sufferer

By contrast, the memoir's enlisted men are rendered largely as a collective. They appear as "the men" — bathing in the Ancre, marching in "droves of victims," enduring conditions that the officers observe and occasionally share but from which they can, to some degree, retreat (to the Mess, to leave, to the comparative comfort of Battalion HQ). Sassoon's narrator is aware of this asymmetry and troubled by it, but the memoir's very narrative perspective — the first-person account of an officer — structurally reproduces the hierarchy it critiques.

### The Limits of Class Solidarity

The memoir complicates any simple class narrative. [[david-cromlech|David Cromlech]] — based on Robert Graves — represents a cross-class intellectual friendship that transcends military hierarchy. The narrator's relationships with individual soldiers (his [[servant|batman]], the men in his platoon) are rendered with genuine warmth and respect. But these individual connections cannot overcome the structural inequality that the Army's organization enforces. The narrator's [[protest|anti-war declaration]] is, in part, a recognition that the war's costs fall disproportionately on those with the least power to question it.

### Class and the Language of War

The class system is most visible in the memoir's attention to language. [[official-language|The official vocabulary]] of war — communiqués, training manuals, patriotic speeches — is the language of the officer class. The soldiers' own speech, by contrast, is largely absent from the narrative, a silence that is itself a form of representation: the memoir cannot give voice to those whom the class system has rendered voiceless.

## Conclusion

The memoir's class critique is never stated as an explicit argument — that would violate Sassoon's commitment to showing rather than telling. But it is present in every scene: in the Mess rituals that persist while men die in the trenches, in the leave system that allows officers to escape while their men cannot, in the very narrative perspective that centers an officer's consciousness. The class system is the war's invisible infrastructure, and Sassoon's memoir, by rendering it visible, performs its own quiet act of subversion.
