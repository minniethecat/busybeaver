# Hope

Hope in [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s memoir is a fragile and gradually depleting resource — the mental habit of looking forward that sustained soldiers through the war but became increasingly difficult to maintain against the accumulating evidence of loss and futility.

## Definition & Context

In the context of WWI, hope operated on multiple scales: the hope of surviving the next attack, the hope of a "[[england|Blighty]] wound" that would mean evacuation to [[england|England]], the hope that the war would end, and the broader hope — increasingly strained — that victory would justify the sacrifice. Military authorities actively cultivated hope through propaganda, censorship, and the ritual optimism of [[official-language|communiqué]]s. For the individual soldier, hope was a psychological necessity, the alternative being the despair that led to "swinging the lead" (feigning illness) or the more extreme collapse labeled as shell shock [009-003].

## In the Memoir

Hope in the memoir is most often expressed indirectly — through small, specific wishes rather than grand declarations. Soldiers "hoped for the best" when throwing bombs in a raid [005-008]; a wounded officer "hoped to God the Germans wouldn't" start shelling again [014-016]; the narrator "hurried up the hill in hope of seeing him again" [016-005], seeking a friend who may already be dead.

The memoir charts hope's gradual erosion. Early passages contain genuine forward-looking optimism — "he told me that he hoped to be a historian, and I listened" [003-006] — the future tense still available to young officers who have not yet seen heavy fighting. By the middle of the memoir, hope has narrowed to the merely physical: "sitting down and hoping it wouldn't rain" [014-022]. The grand hopes of 1914 — glory, adventure, a quick victory — have been reduced to wishing for dry weather.

The memoir records moments where hope is explicitly cancelled. Of a failed attack: "the companies on the left never had a hope. They got enfiladed" [024-004]. Here hope is not a feeling but a tactical calculation — and the calculation is negative. The war's mathematics override individual aspiration.

## Sassoon's Perspective

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s treatment of hope is characteristically ironized. He never writes "I hoped" with rhetorical passion; hope is always reported, observed, attributed to others or to a past self now viewed with knowing detachment. The gap between the hope expressed by characters and the outcome known to the reader (and to the retrospective narrator) generates the memoir's distinctive tone — not quite despair, but a weary recognition that hope was always a confidence trick the war played on the young.

The narrator's own hopes — for meaning, for justice, for some redemptive arc to the suffering — are rarely stated directly. They emerge instead through negation: in his anger at official language, his contempt for the "complacent patriotism" of the [[england|home]] front, his solidarity with the enlisted men who endure without illusion. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s hope, if it survives at all, is displaced into the act of writing itself — the memoir as testimony, the belief that accurate witness might prevent recurrence.

## Historical Reality

The psychological literature on WWI emphasizes the role of hope — and its loss — in combat motivation and breakdown. Soldiers who lost the ability to project a future beyond the war were at highest risk for what was then called "[[shell-shock|neurasthenia]]." Military psychiatrists like W.H.R. [[w-h-r-rivers|Rivers]] at [[craiglockhart|Craiglockhart]] treated officers suffering from the collapse of hope — not its absence, but its traumatic contradiction by experience.

The phenomenon of "trench optimism" — the soldier's irrational belief that he personally would survive despite the statistical evidence — was widely noted by observers and later analyzed as an adaptive psychological mechanism. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s memoir, with its retrospective clarity, both records and dismantles this mechanism.
