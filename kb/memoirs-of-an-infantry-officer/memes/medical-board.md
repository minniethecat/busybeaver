# Medical Board

The official medical assessment [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] underwent after his [[protest|anti-war protest]] and treatment at [[craiglockhart|Craiglockhart]] — a bureaucratic procedure that determined his fitness to return to duty and that he presents with characteristic irony as the war's ultimate institutional absurdity.

## Background

After [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s "Soldier's Declaration" was sent to his commanding officer in July 1917, he expected to be court-martialed. Instead, through the intervention of [[robert-graves|Robert Graves]] and the military psychiatrist W.H.R. [[w-h-r-rivers|Rivers]], he was sent to [[craiglockhart|Craiglockhart War Hospital]] in Edinburgh. The diagnosis was "shell shock" — a medical classification that simultaneously acknowledged the psychological damage of war and neutralized [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s political protest by reclassifying it as illness [045-001].

The Medical Board was the mechanism by which officers were assessed for fitness. A board of doctors would interview the patient, review his record, and issue one of several classifications: fit for general service, fit for [[england|home]] service only, or unfit. For [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]], the board represented the final stage of the process that had begun with the protest.

## The Event

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s Medical Board appears in the memoir's closing chapters as a scene of concentrated irony. The board members — military doctors with a bureaucratic rather than therapeutic function — must decide whether a decorated officer who has publicly declared the war unjust is "fit" to return to it.

The examination is described with deadpan precision: the questions, the physical examination, the deliberation. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] does not rail against the board; he simply records it, and the recording is devastating. The doctors are not villains — they are functionaries performing a ritual whose meaning has been hollowed out by the contradictions it is designed to manage.

## In the Memoir

The Medical Board occupies Chapters 45–46 — the memoir's final pages — where it serves as the narrative's closing statement on the institutional absorption of dissent. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] is passed fit for general service. The protest has been processed, classified, neutralized. He will return to the front.

The board's verdict is not a defeat — [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] never claims the protest should have ended his service — but it is an acknowledgment of limits. The individual conscience can speak, but the institution will classify what it hears into categories that serve the institution's purposes.

## Casualties & Aftermath

The "casualties" of the Medical Board are not physical but institutional. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s protest is buried not by suppression but by absorption — the most effective form of institutional defense. He returns to duty, serves in Palestine and [[france|France]], is wounded again. The war continues.

## Sassoon's Commentary

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s treatment of the Medical Board is the memoir at its most Swiftian — a scene of bureaucratic absurdity rendered in prose of perfect seriousness. The joke is on everyone: on [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] for thinking a declaration could end a war, on the doctors for pretending their examination is medical, on the army for devising a system that must pretend to cure what it has caused.
