# New Army

The volunteer army raised by Lord Kitchener beginning in August 1914 — the mass citizen-soldier formations that transformed the British Expeditionary Force from a small professional army into a national war machine, and whose character the memoir registers in its observations of the men who replaced the pre-war Regulars.

## Identity & Structure

The New Army — officially "Kitchener's Army" — was created in response to the destruction of the Regular Army's experienced manpower in the war's opening months. Unlike the pre-war Regular battalions, which drew from a narrow social base of professional soldiers, the New Army recruited from every class and occupation, creating units whose members had been civilians months before. By 1916, when the memoir opens in earnest, the New Army had become the British infantry's dominant component.

The memoir does not describe the New Army's structure in institutional terms. It registers the New Army instead through its human products: the "unseasoned New Army troops" who relieve [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s battalion at Mametz Wood [014-022], the "still undiminished New Army Battalion" that passes them on the road [012-008], the second-in-command who finds himself leading "a New Army Battalion" in England [028-001].

## In the Memoir

The New Army appears in the memoir primarily as a demographic fact rather than a named institution. The men who fill the ranks — the undersized Welshmen, the "jostling company of exclamatory" newcomers, the replacements who arrive after each battle — are New Army men, and [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s attitude toward them is protective in a way that his attitude toward the professional military establishment is not.

The memoir's most pointed New Army observation comes at Mametz Wood, when the battalion is relieved by troops who "numbered more than double our own strength" but who are "unseasoned": "as I watched them arriving at the first stage of their battle experience I had a sense of their victimization" [014-022]. The New Army men are victims not of the enemy but of a system that sends them into battle without the knowledge that experience confers.

## War Record

The New Army's war record is the war record of the British infantry on the Somme: it absorbed the casualties, sustained the offensive, and died in numbers that the Regular Army of 1914 could not have supplied. The memoir records this fact indirectly — in the constant arrival of replacements, in the diminishing proportion of pre-war Regulars, in the battalion's transformation from a professional unit into a composite of veterans and novices.

## Sassoon's Perspective

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] was himself, in military terms, a New Army officer — a temporary gentleman, commissioned from the ranks of civilian volunteers rather than from Sandhurst. His sympathy for the New Army soldier is, in part, a sympathy for his own position: a man who would not have been an officer in peacetime, serving in a war that the pre-war army's assumptions could not accommodate.

The memoir's class politics are inseparable from its treatment of the New Army. The Regular officers — men like [[kinjack|Kinjack]] — represent a military tradition that the war is destroying. The New Army represents the future, and the memoir is ambivalent about what that future holds.

## Historical Counterpart

Kitchener's New Army was a real historical formation, raised through the famous "Your Country Needs You" recruitment campaign of 1914–1915. By the Somme, the New Army had become the backbone of the British infantry, and its character — civilian-soldiers in uniform, with civilian habits and civilian expectations — had permanently altered the army's social composition. The memoir's observations on this transformation are among its most historically valuable passages.
