# Durley

A junior officer in [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s company and his billet-mate during the [[mametz|Mametz]] period — a figure whose quiet domestic presence anchors the memoir's most intimate passages of trench life and whose fate exemplifies the war's casual destruction of ordinary men.

## Role & Identity

Durley is a fellow officer, probably a subaltern, sharing a billet with [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] during the memoir's early chapters. He appears almost entirely in domestic contexts — in the mess, in the billet at night, in the shared rituals of meals and sleep that constitute trench life between action. His role is structural: Durley represents the ordinary, the everyday, the human background against which the war's violence occurs [005-004][005-005].

## In the Memoir

Durley's most vivid appearance comes in Chapter 5, where [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] describes the evening routine of the officer's billet: "Dusk had fallen when I returned from a stroll in the fields; the candles were lit, there was a smell of cooking, and the servants were clattering tin plates in the sizzling kitchen. Durley, Birdie Mansfield, and young [[ormand|Ormand]] were sitting round the table" [005-004].

Later that night: "Flook had spread my 'fleabag' on the tiled floor, and I had soon slipped into it and blown out my candle. Durley, on the other side of the room, was asleep in a few minutes" [005-005]. The domestic detail — the fleabag, the tiled floor, the candle, the sleeping roommate — is the memoir's quietest and most powerful realism.

Durley reappears in Chapter 18, visiting [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] in hospital, "joking with me about how well I was looking" [018-006]. The hospital visit reverses the roles: now Durley is the one returning to the front, [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] the one in safety. "They took my fortitude away with them" — the visit that should be comforting is instead devastating.

## Historical Counterpart

Durley appears to be a lightly fictionalized composite of several [[royal-welch-fusiliers|Royal Welch Fusiliers]] officers who served alongside [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]. The specificity of the billet scenes suggests real experience; the generic quality of the name suggests a representative rather than a portrait.

## Significance

Durley is the memoir's Everyman — the ordinary officer doing his duty without heroics, without protest, without the intellectual apparatus that [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] and [[david-cromlech|Cromlech]] bring to their moral arguments. He represents what the war destroys most casually: not the exceptional men but the ordinary ones, the ones who just wanted to survive and go [[england|home]].
