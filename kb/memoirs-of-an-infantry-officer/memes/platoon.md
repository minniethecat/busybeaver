# Platoon

## Overview

The **platoon** was the smallest infantry unit led by a commissioned officer — typically a subaltern (second lieutenant or lieutenant). For Sassoon, a young platoon commander, the platoon was the most intimate level of military leadership, where the officer's relationship with his men was tested daily [010-010].

## The Subaltern's Burden

Sassoon's experience as a platoon commander forms the emotional core of the memoir. Leading a platoon meant direct responsibility for the lives of approximately thirty to forty men in conditions of extreme danger. "I couldn't see myself leading my platoon with Mortleman's Patent Sound-Proof Ear Drums" [010-007], he writes, with the dark humor that characterizes much of his self-deprecation about his own courage.

The weight of command is captured in moments of reflection: "a few kind words could make a platoon commander consider them jolly fine fellows" [010-010]. The young officer's need for approval from both superiors and subordinates runs throughout the memoir.

## The Platoon Sergeant

The platoon sergeant was the backbone of the unit — an experienced NCO who bridged the gap between the young officer and the enlisted men. Sassoon's relationship with Sergeant Miles illustrates this dynamic. After a night raid, "Miles, my platoon sergeant, hadn't been quite his usual self since the raid" [009-003], hinting at the psychological toll that even seasoned soldiers bore.

## The Men

Sassoon's portrayal of his platoon members is one of the memoir's defining features. He names individuals: "a fair-haired young lance-corporal from my platoon" [012-011], "Worgan (one of the tough ones)" [012-011]. These vignettes of ordinary soldiers — their courage, humor, and suffering — form the moral argument against the war.

"The men of his platoon were lying down a little way off" [016-005] — a quiet image of exhausted soldiers that says more than any overt protest. Before going into action, Sassoon checks "my platoon all present and correct" [012-002], a routine administrative act freighted with the knowledge that after the coming battle, some would not be.
