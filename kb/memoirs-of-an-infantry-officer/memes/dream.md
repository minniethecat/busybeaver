Dreams in [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s memoir occupy the boundary between the war and the inner life — they are the soldier's refuge from the trenches and, paradoxically, the space where the war's trauma most intimately intrudes.

## In the Memoir

Dreaming in the memoir takes multiple forms. There are the "pleasant dreams" that the war denies: "it was difficult to imagine that the War didn't allow people many pleasant dreams" [005-008]. There are the daydreams of [[england|England]] that sustain the soldier through long watches — [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] allows himself "a little daydream, based on the leisurely ticking" of a clock [015-003], conjuring "an [[england|England]] where there was" peace and permanence [015-004]. These waking fantasies of [[england|home]] are "excellent material for a domestic daydream when I was at the front" [044-005], a mental escape route that runs parallel to the physical impossibility of leaving.

But dreams are also the site of trauma's return. At [[craiglockhart|Craiglockhart]], [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] is "worried by bad dreams" [036-012], the war invading his sleep even as his body heals. The memoir captures the ambiguity of the dream-state: "for a week, perhaps, I could dream that for me the War was over" [036-002] — the conditional "perhaps" betraying the fragility of the illusion. The "loss of last year's daydreams about [[england|England]]" [031-031] marks a point of disillusionment, the surrender of the consoling fictions that had made endurance possible.

## Sassoon's Perspective

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] treats dreaming with the same unsentimental clarity he brings to all his subjects. He knows that "it was no use dreaming about all that now" [010-008] — the dream is a luxury the realist cannot afford. Yet he keeps returning to it, because the dream is also the only place where an alternative to the war can be imagined. The memoir's closing note is wry: "I never dreamt it would be such a long job" [046-010] — the [[protest|anti-war protest]] has its own duration, its own exhausting reality.

The distinction between dream and reality is deliberately blurred. "No daydream" becomes a metaphor for "uncut [[barbed-wire|wire]]" [012-008], the material obstacle that will determine whether men live or die. The dream is not escape but preparation — a way of mentally rehearsing the return to life.

## Historical Reality

Dreams and nightmares were a recognized symptom of what WWI physicians called "shell shock." Soldiers reported recurring nightmares of combat, inability to sleep, and waking flashbacks — what would now be classified as PTSD symptoms. The memoir's account of "bad dreams" [036-012] at [[craiglockhart|Craiglockhart]] aligns with the clinical literature of the period, which documented the persistence of traumatic dreams long after physical wounds had healed.

The "daydream of [[england|England]]" that [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] describes was a widespread psychological phenomenon among soldiers — the idealized image of [[england|home]] as the polar opposite of the front. This mental geography, with its sharp division between the "real" war and the "dream" of peace, was central to the soldier's psychological survival.
