# Anger

Anger is the moral engine of [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s memoir — the force that transforms personal suffering into political witness, and that distinguishes the narrator's voice from the many other memoirs of the Great War that record horror without indictment.

## Definition & Context

In the context of [[france|the Western Front]], anger took forms that were distinct from the personal irritations of peacetime. Soldiers were angry at specific targets — the staff officers who sent them into futile attacks, the civilians who "seemed to know that the long-expected war had begun" without understanding what it meant, the politicians who spoke of sacrifice from safety. But underneath these specific grievances was a deeper, more existential anger: the recognition that the war had become a self-perpetuating machine, consuming lives for objectives that had long since lost any connection to strategic sense. For the infantry officer, anger was also a professional hazard — it threatened the composure that leadership required [031-006].

## In the Memoir

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s anger emerges gradually across the memoir's narrative. It is barely present in the early chapters, where the narrator is still an earnest young officer learning his trade. It builds through the middle sections as the casualties accumulate and the futility becomes harder to ignore. And it crystallizes in the later chapters, where the narrator's political consciousness transforms private resentment into something approaching a public position.

The memoir records anger directed at the logical absurdities of military life: the "danger" of incompetent commanders who "would have been less dangerous in his preexisting ignorance" [030-005], the casual disregard for soldiers' welfare, the arbitrary distinctions of rank and privilege. But the anger is never merely personal. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] distinguishes between the "sorrow" and the "anger" with which he views the war: "more in sorrow than in anger" [031-006] — the phrase is deliberately biblical, framing the narrator's response as a form of lamentation rather than mere complaint.

The memoir also records the anger that comes from encounters with the [[england|home]] front: "I wanted to stop strangers in the street and ask them whether they knew" [040-008]. The gap between the trenches and civilian life is experienced not as sadness but as a goad — the feeling that the people at [[england|home]] have no right to their ignorance, and that their comfort is purchased with others' suffering.

## Sassoon's Perspective

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s treatment of anger is unusual among Great War memoirs for its refusal to sublimate the emotion into stoic acceptance or patriotic consolation. The narrator does not forgive; he does not find meaning in sacrifice. Instead, he lets the anger stand as an accusation. This is what made [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s wartime protest — his famous "Soldier's Declaration" of 1917 — a logical extension of the memoir's moral argument rather than a sudden break.

The memoir's anger is also carefully differentiated. It is not directed at the German enemy, who appear rarely and without hatred. It is not directed at the ordinary soldiers, whose suffering the narrator shares. It is directed upward — at the generals, the politicians, the "scrupulous people at [[england|home]] getting thin" through voluntary rationing. The anger is political in its object even when personal in its expression.

## Historical Reality

The historical [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s anger led directly to action: his 1917 protest against the war's prolongation, which resulted in his being sent to [[craiglockhart|Craiglockhart War Hospital]] rather than court-martialed. The protest itself was an act of controlled anger — a refusal to continue participating in a war that had become, in [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s view, a war of conquest rather than defense.

The memoir transforms this biographical fact into a structural principle. The anger that drove the historical [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] to protest is the same anger that gives the memoir its distinctive voice — the refusal to accept, the insistence on naming what others preferred not to see. What might have been, in a lesser writer, merely bitterness becomes, in [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s hands, an instrument of moral clarity.
