# Fourth Army

The British field army formed in February 1916 under the command of [[general-rawlinson|General Sir Henry Rawlinson]] — the formation within which [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s battalion served during the [[somme-battle|Battle of the Somme]] and the institutional entity that, for the memoir, represents the remote, impersonal high command whose decisions determined the fate of battalions it never saw.

## Identity & Structure

The Fourth Army was one of five British field armies on the Western Front. Formed specifically for the Somme offensive, it comprised multiple corps, each of several divisions, with [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s battalion falling under its jurisdiction through the 7th Division. The army's headquarters was a sprawling administrative apparatus that the memoir treats with characteristic irony: [[dottrell|Dottrell]] notes that "the permanent staff at Fourth Army Headquarters now numbered anything" [006-001], the vagueness of the number itself a comment on headquarters bloat.

The army commander, [[general-rawlinson|General Rawlinson]], is a distant presence in the memoir — seen only at inspections, his visit to the troops "brief (and mercifully informal)" [015-001]. The distance between army-level command and battalion-level experience is one of the memoir's structural themes.

## In the Memoir

The Fourth Army appears in the memoir primarily through its institutional accessories: the [[fourth-army-school|Fourth Army School]] at Flixécourt, where [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] spent a month's refresher course; the "splendid sample of the Fourth Army" assembled in the schoolroom for lectures [002-009]; the staff officers who plan operations from châteaux while battalions execute them in mud.

The memoir's most concentrated portrait of Fourth Army culture comes from the Fourth Army School chapters, where instructors teach "open warfare" tactics to men who spend their nights in mine-craters, and where a gas expert from G.H.Q. informs the audience that "gas was still in its infancy" [002-010]. The gap between the army's institutional language and the infantry's lived experience is the memoir's most persistent subject.

## War Record

During the period covered by the memoir (spring 1916–summer 1917), the Fourth Army was responsible for the Somme sector, including the attacks on Mametz Wood, Bazentin Ridge, and Delville Wood. By early 1917, the memoir records a Fourth Army "ready to advance with what one might call a brilliant unanimity of self-confidence" [025-005] — a confidence that the subsequent chapters will systematically undermine.

The army's war record, as the memoir sees it, is one of plans that fail on contact with reality, of optimistic assumptions that get men killed, and of a command structure that learns "as they go along, like the rest of us" [012-005].

## Sassoon's Perspective

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s attitude toward the Fourth Army is not hostile so much as it is diagnostic. The army is not villainous — it is merely large, distant, and inevitably wrong about local conditions. The memoir's critique is structural rather than personal: the problem is not that Fourth Army commanders are bad men but that the distance between the château and the trench makes accurate knowledge impossible.

The memoir's most telling Fourth Army detail may be its smallest: [[dottrell|Dottrell]]'s observation that "the further you got from the front-line the further you got from the War." The Fourth Army is the institutional embodiment of that distance.

## Historical Counterpart

The Fourth Army is a real historical formation, commanded by General Sir Henry Rawlinson, and its role in the Somme offensive is as the memoir describes. Unlike the "Flintshire Fusiliers" (a fictionalized name for the [[royal-welch-fusiliers|Royal Welch Fusiliers]]), the Fourth Army appears under its real name, reflecting [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s selective approach to fictionalization: regiments and individuals get pseudonyms; the larger institutional architecture of the war does not.
