# Attack

The offensive military operation in World War I — a planned advance against enemy positions — which Sassoon's memoir examines from the training manuals of the Fourth Army School to the visceral terror of the Somme battlefield.

## Definition & Context

In the military doctrine of World War I, the attack was the decisive operation. Infantry units, supported by artillery preparation, would advance across no-man's-land to seize enemy trenches. The British Army's training emphasized the bayonet charge: "To attack with the bayonet effectively requires good direction, strength, and determination" — a formula Sassoon quotes directly from the manual, with evident irony [002-011][002-012].

The attack represented the moment of maximum danger and maximum meaning in the infantry officer's experience. It was the test that separated the "dud officer" from the effective leader, and the event around which all trench routine — the working parties, the sentry duty, the endless waiting — was organized.

## In the Memoir

Sassoon's first encounters with the attack are anticipatory rather than experiential. At the Fourth Army School, he learns the "theory" of offensive operations in a safe environment far behind the lines. His return to the front brings the reality: "next week's attack is what I have been waiting for" becomes both a statement of duty and a note of dread [010-011].

The memoir's most sustained treatment of an attack comes during the Somme offensive of July 1916. Sassoon records the preparations in precise detail — the postponement of "forty-eight hours" due to weather [012-006], the wire-cutting that revealed gaps "for the attacking battalion to pass through" [012-008], the operational orders that traveled down the chain of command.

His diary-like entries from the battle's opening day capture the attack in fragmented real time: "Manchesters attack at 2:30" [013-013], "Fricourt attack has failed" [013-016], "several hundred prisoners" [013-016]. The attack is rendered not as a coherent narrative but as a series of discrete observations — smoke, noise, casualties, rumors — that mirror the infantry officer's limited perspective.

## Sassoon's Perspective

Sassoon's treatment of the attack balances professional competence with moral critique. He describes his own preparations — the "wire-cutters with rubber-covered handles" bought at the Army and Navy Stores — with the practical concern of an experienced officer, yet his account of the Somme attack resists the language of glory or triumph.

His retrospective judgment is explicit: the attack exposes the gap between staff planning and infantry reality. Staff officers appear in "well-cut riding breeches" far behind the line [012-005] while the attacking battalions go forward into machine-gun fire. The attack, for Sassoon, becomes the event that most clearly reveals the war's structural injustice.

## Historical Reality

The attack on the Somme — July 1, 1916 — was the bloodiest day in British military history, with nearly 60,000 casualties. Sassoon's battalion, the Royal Welch Fusiliers, attacked near Fricourt as part of the wider offensive. The attack he describes was a subsidiary operation to the main assault, reflecting the piecemeal nature of the Somme's opening phase. The failure he records at Fricourt was one of many local setbacks that characterized the battle's first day.
