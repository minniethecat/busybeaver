# Mametz Wood

The large, dense woodland south of the Bapaume road on the Somme battlefield — the objective of the 7th Division's attack in mid-July 1916, and the site of one of the memoir's most concentrated portraits of combat chaos and command failure.

## Background

Mametz Wood lay on the right flank of the British advance during the opening phase of the [[somme-battle|Battle of the Somme]]. The wood, roughly a mile long and half a mile deep, was heavily fortified by German defenders who had occupied it since 1914. The 7th Division's battle plan, as [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] transcribed it, identified "Rose Trench, Orchard Alley, Apple Alley, and Willow Avenue" as objectives — names whose pastoral associations the memoir registers with deliberate irony: "my mind very properly insisted on their gentler associations" [012-003].

The 38th (Welsh) Division attacked the wood on 7 July 1916 and suffered catastrophic casualties. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s battalion entered the fight several days later, when the wood was supposed to have been cleared — a supposition that proved catastrophically wrong.

## The Event

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s account spans Chapters 14–16. On the night of 14 July, his battalion marched toward the wood, passing "the blurred orange sunset" and the ground that "sloped treeless to [[bazentin-ridge|Bazentin]] Ridge" [014-003]. The operation plan assumed the near side of the wood was already in British hands. It was not.

The assault collapsed into confusion almost immediately. When the battalion halted in a sunken road, "an uproar broke out at the edge of the wood, which demonstrated with machine-guns and bombs that the Staff had guessed wrong" [014-006]. The Scots units in the wood were being cut to pieces, and [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s battalion was ordered forward into a situation that no one understood.

The memoir records the aftermath with careful understatement. The survivors straggled back. The wood, when they finally entered it properly, was a charnel house: the artillery had "converted the green thickets of Mametz Wood to a desolation of skeleton trees" [014-022]. The Welsh Division, which had attacked it earlier, had been shattered.

## In the Memoir

Mametz Wood occupies Chapters 14–15 as the memoir's most extended treatment of large-scale combat confusion. Unlike the [[trench-raid]], which is narrated as a discrete action with a clear before-and-after, the Wood fighting is presented as something the participants never fully understood. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] moves through it in fragments: the sunken road, the quarry, the bombing fight, the glum officers in the strong-point, young Fernby's "contrast to the apathetic trio" [014-015].

The Wood also introduces the memoir's most pointed class observation about the infantry. When the battalion is relieved by "unseasoned New Army troops" — undersized Welshmen who outnumber them two to one — [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] notes: "I had a sense of their victimization" [014-022]. The green troops are walking into what the veterans have just survived, and no one has told them what to expect.

## Casualties & Aftermath

The battalion lost about 100 men at Mametz Wood, though only a dozen were killed [015-001]. The relatively light casualties — by Somme standards — do not diminish the memoir's sense of the action's futility. The men had been thrown into a situation about which the Staff was demonstrably wrong, fought for two days over ground that was supposed to have been cleared, and withdrew having achieved nothing that anyone could articulate.

## Sassoon's Commentary

The memoir's judgment on Mametz Wood is delivered not through direct statement but through juxtaposition. The battle plan's "topographical Arcadia" — Rose Trench, Orchard Alley — gives way to "a desolation of skeleton trees" [014-022]. The Staff's assumption that the wood was unoccupied is answered by machine-gun fire. And the Welshmen arriving at "the first stage of their battle experience" are presented not as reinforcements but as victims.
