# Nature

Nature in [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s memoir is never merely scenery. It is the moral and aesthetic standard against which the war is measured — the green world of pre-war [[england|England]] that the trenches have obliterated, the birdsong that persists despite bombardment, and the "reversal of the order of nature" [046-004] that the war represents.

## Definition & Context

For the generation that fought the Great War, nature carried a particular cultural weight. The Georgian poetry movement, of which [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] was a part, had made the English [[landscape|countryside]] the central subject of its verse — hedgerows and hills, streams and meadows, the particular light of a summer evening. The war shattered this pastoral tradition by making the landscape itself a victim: the Somme battlefield was not merely a place where men died but a place where the earth had been turned into a corpse. Trees were reduced to splintered stumps, meadows became crater-fields, and the birds that had populated Georgian poetry were largely silent [046-004].

## In the Memoir

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s treatment of nature operates through juxtaposition. The memoir repeatedly sets the remembered English landscape against the devastated French one, not as explicit argument but as structural counterpoint. The narrator recalls "driving [[england|home]] from cricket matches before the war" [016-013] — the memory is sensory, specific, the green world of an English summer preserved in the mind's eye.

The memoir's most powerful nature imagery is also its quietest. The "lark singing confidently overhead" on a "cloudless and hot" day [013-012] — a bird whose song is indifferent to the war raging beneath it. The "thrushes and blackbirds singing in the brown undergrowth" [031-031], their song coexisting with the noise of guns. Nature in these passages is not a refuge from the war — there is no refuge — but a reminder that a world exists outside the war's logic, a world the narrator remembers but can no longer fully inhabit.

The memoir's bitterest nature image is also one of its briefest: "like a reversal of the order of nature" [046-004]. The phrase appears in a description of an interview with a superior officer, but its resonance extends across the entire memoir. The war has reversed the natural order — the young die while the old survive, the earth is poisoned, and the pastoral world that [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s generation inherited has been rendered uninhabitable.

## Sassoon's Perspective

As both a poet of the English [[landscape|countryside]] and a soldier who witnessed its antithesis, [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] occupies a unique position. His nature writing is never sentimental — he knows that the "good-natured face" [002-002] of a comrade or the "good-natured easygoing Majors" [023-004] exist in a world where good nature offers no protection. The memoir's nature passages are elegiac but not nostalgic: they mourn what has been lost without pretending it can be recovered.

## Historical Reality

The environmental destruction of [[france|the Western Front]] was unprecedented in scale. An estimated 1.5 billion shells were fired during the war, each one scarring the landscape. The battlefields of the Somme and Ypres remain, over a century later, zones of contaminated earth where unexploded ordnance still surfaces. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s memoir captures the beginning of this environmental consciousness — the recognition that modern warfare destroys not only human bodies but the [[landscape|natural world]] itself.
