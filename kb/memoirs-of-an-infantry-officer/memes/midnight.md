# Midnight

The midnight hour as it recurs throughout Sassoon's memoir — a time of trench raids, battalion reliefs, and eerie stillness on the Western Front. Midnight marks the boundary between one day's routine and the next, and in the memoir it is almost always a moment of tension, movement, or quiet dread.

## Definition & Context

In trench warfare, much essential military activity took place at night: wiring parties, patrols, trench raids, ration deliveries, and battalion reliefs all occurred under cover of darkness. Midnight was both a practical necessity — darkness reduced the risk of enemy observation — and a psychological threshold. The night routine inverted normal civilian life, making midnight the hour of maximum alertness rather than rest.

## In the Memoir

Midnight appears at key moments throughout the narrative. During a trench raid, "it was now midnight. The five parties had vanished into the gloom" [007-007]. A raid on the German lines is timed: "it was forty-five minutes after midnight. Two companies were to attack" [014-010]. Battalion movements frequently occur at this hour: "it was midnight when I left him" at Bazentin Ridge [016-005] and "the Battalion was expected about midnight" [016-016]. Sassoon experiences the war's disorienting temporal shifts: "Summer time began at midnight, which meant one hour less sleep" [030-010]. A "midnight halt" on the march recalls "the hill still looming in silhouette" [033-004]. Arriving at a rest area "after midnight" forces him to wait for a bed [034-022].

## Sassoon's Perspective

Sassoon treats midnight with a poet's attention to atmosphere — darkness, silence, the looming shapes of hills and trenches. The hour strips away the daylight routines of military life and exposes the war's essential strangeness: men moving through devastated landscapes in darkness, waiting for flares to expose them. Midnight is when the memoir's dream-like quality is strongest, blending the real and the surreal.

## Historical Reality

Night operations were standard on the Western Front. Trench raids — small-scale night attacks to capture prisoners and gather intelligence — were a routine feature of trench warfare. Battalion reliefs (moving a fresh unit into the front line to replace one being withdrawn) were almost always conducted at night to avoid enemy artillery fire.
