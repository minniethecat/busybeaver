# Barbed Wire

The dense belts of steel entanglements that stretched across [[france|the Western Front]], protecting trench lines from infantry assault — and that appear in [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s memoir as both a practical obstacle and a potent visual symbol of the war's inhuman character.

## Definition & Context

Barbed wire was one of the defining technologies of [[trench-warfare|trench warfare]]. Stretched in belts up to 30 yards deep in front of the fire trench, it was designed to channel attacking infantry into killing zones covered by machine guns. Clearing wire — by artillery bombardment or by hand under cover of darkness — was a prerequisite for any assault, and failure to do so was one of the commonest causes of attack failure [003-007][005-007].

The wire was also a constant presence in the soldier's daily life. Wiring parties went out at night to repair or extend entanglements. [[patrol|Patrol]]s crawled through gaps cut in the enemy's wire. The sight of dead men hanging in the wire — a common image in war photography and memoir — became one of the war's most haunting visual symbols.

## In the Memoir

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s first mention of wire is professional rather than symbolic: soldiers at the [[fourth-army-school|Army School]] practice "putting up screw-pickets and barbed wire with rapidity and precision" [003-007]. The wire is equipment — a skill to be learned, a task to be performed.

But as the memoir moves to the front, wire acquires a darker resonance. Corporal O'Brien's [[patrol|patrol duty]] includes "looking at the German wire" — a reconnaissance task that means crawling close enough to see whether gaps have been cut [005-007]. Before the raid, "a gap had been cut in our wire, and it was believed that some of the enemy had been working in the craters" [007-005] — the wire is no longer equipment but territory, a contested boundary between the living and the dead.

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] also associates wire with the memoir's most basic physical reality: the difficulty of moving through the trench landscape. Men "jump concertina wire in the shell-holes along the edge of the craters" [009-002] — the wire is everywhere, a constant impediment that makes every movement an exertion.

## Sassoon's Perspective

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] never makes wire an explicit symbol — he is too concrete a writer for that. But the wire accumulates symbolic force through repetition: it is always there, the material fact that defines the battlefield's geography. The wire is the war's most honest object: it does not pretend to be anything other than what it is — a machine for tearing flesh, designed and manufactured for that purpose.

In [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s understated way, wire becomes an indictment: this — this tangled steel, these "dense belts of rust and barbs" — is what civilization has produced. The memoir's anti-war argument is made not through rhetoric but through the accumulation of such details.

## Historical Reality

Barbed wire was originally an American agricultural invention (patented 1874) adapted for military use during the Boer War and deployed on an industrial scale during World War I. British factories produced millions of miles of barbed wire during the war. The wire's effectiveness as a defensive obstacle was repeatedly demonstrated, and wire-cutting remained one of the most dangerous infantry tasks throughout the conflict.
