# General Rawlinson

Commander of the British Fourth Army, under whose authority the narrator's battalion fought on the Somme — a figure who embodies the distant, institutional character of high command in the memoir's critique of military authority.

## Role & Identity

General Sir Henry Rawlinson (1864–1925) commanded the British Fourth Army during the [[somme-battle|Battle of the Somme]] in 1916. His "Army Area" encompassed the sector where [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s battalion operated, and his tactical decisions — particularly the large-scale frontal assaults of July–November 1916 — resulted in casualties that transformed British attitudes toward the war.

In the memoir, Rawlinson appears only as a name: "the realm of Rawlinson was left behind us" as the battalion marches from one Army Area into another [031-024]. This single mention is deliberately slight — the general is not a character but a metonym for the system.

## In the Memoir

Rawlinson's one named appearance occurs during a march to Beauval, as the battalion passes from his command into [[first-battalion|Allenby]]'s: "We passed into another 'Army Area'; the realm of Rawlinson was left behind us and our self-sacrificing exertions were now to be directed by Allenby" [031-024]. The phrase "self-sacrificing exertions" is ironized by the context — wet snow, monotonous roads, and the absurd spectacle of the Corps Commander bellowing at Colonel Easby about uncovered rifle muzzles.

The passage's tone captures the memoir's attitude toward generalship: these men are remote, interchangeable, and their concerns — fifty-yard intervals between Companies, muzzle covers — are trivial next to the "self-sacrificing exertions" they direct. Rawlinson is not singled out for blame; he is simply the name attached to one section of the vast military apparatus that consumes men.

## Historical Counterpart

The historical Rawlinson commanded the Fourth Army through the Somme campaign. Military historians have debated his responsibility for the 1 July 1916 casualties — 57,000 British in a single day — with some arguing that his tactics were products of the Army's institutional limitations rather than personal failure. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] does not engage this debate; the memoir's critique is directed at the structure, not the man.

## Significance

Rawlinson's significance in the memoir is as a representative absence. The generals who direct the war are never confronted, never questioned, never even seen — they are names on maps, boundaries between "Army Areas." This structural remoteness is precisely the memoir's complaint: the men who order the attacks do not share their cost. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s [[protest|eventual protest]] is directed at this system of "callous complacency," of which Rawlinson is one anonymous face among many.
