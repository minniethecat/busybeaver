# Dressing Station

A forward medical facility located close behind the front line, forming the first stage in the casualty evacuation chain. In Sassoon's memoir, dressing stations appear as threshold spaces: neither the battlefield nor the base hospital, but sites where the wounded first encounter organised medical care and where the narrator himself experiences the vulnerability of the wounded body.

## Definition & Context

In the British Army's medical system, the Regimental Aid Post was the first point of treatment, followed by the Advanced Dressing Station and then the Main Dressing Station. These were rudimentary facilities — often dugouts, cellars, or sandbagged shelters — where wounds were cleaned, dressed, and assessed for evacuation priority. The dressing station was a place of triage, where the lightly wounded might be patched up and returned to duty, while the severely wounded were stabilised for transport to a Casualty Clearing Station or base hospital. [032-004]

## In the Memoir

The dressing station first enters the narrative indirectly, through a comrade's death. Near the front, Sassoon notes the presence of "the dressing-station where Dick Tiltwood had died," the location marked not by its medical function but by the loss it witnessed. [006-004] This early mention establishes the dressing station as a place where men go and do not always return.

When Sassoon himself is wounded — struck by a sniper's bullet — the dressing station becomes the site of his own passage through the medical system. Arriving there, "they took a scrap of shell out of my back," the laconic phrasing capturing the routinised nature of the procedure. [024-005] The wound is treated, documented, and he is sent on.

Later, suffering from an infected hand, Sassoon goes "out to the underground dressing-station to get my festering finger attended to," the adjective "underground" evoking both the physical reality of dugout medical posts and a deeper sense of descent — into the earth, into the body's vulnerability. [032-004] The routine of wound-dressing becomes part of the infantry officer's existence: a fellow officer "investigated my wound, put a field-dressing on it, and left me sitting there," the casual competence of the act set against the gravity of the situation. [034-016]

## Sassoon's Perspective

Sassoon's treatment of the dressing station is characteristically understated. He does not linger on the gore or the drama of the operating theatre; instead, he records the dressing station as one more functional space in the architecture of war. Yet the understatement itself conveys a moral point: the dressing station normalises wounding. It processes damaged bodies with the same bureaucratic efficiency that the supply chain processes rations. For Sassoon, the dressing station is a place where the war's violence is made administratively manageable — and this, perhaps, is its most unsettling aspect.
