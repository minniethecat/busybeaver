The **adjutant** in the British infantry battalion of the First World War was the commanding officer's administrative right hand — responsible for orders, returns, discipline, and the endless paperwork that kept the battalion functioning as a military unit. In Sassoon's memoir, the adjutant appears only in glimpses, but those glimpses reveal a figure defined by overwork, caught between the colonel's demands and the operational chaos of the front.

## In the Memoir

### The Adjutant at Battalion Headquarters

Sassoon's most extended depiction of an adjutant comes during the Somme offensive, when Sherston returns from patrol to find Colonel Kinjack in a fury:

> "Kinjack sat glowering in a surface dugout in a sandpit at the edge of Bottom Wood. I went in from the sunlight. The overworked Adjutant eyed me sadly from a corner of an ammunition-box table covered with a grey blanket, and the Colonel's face caused me to feel like a newly captured prisoner." [031-025]

The adjutant's "sad" eye and his corner of the ammunition-box table convey everything about his position: physically proximate to power but powerless to intervene, a witness to the colonel's rage rather than an actor in the scene. The detail of the grey blanket — the improvised office furnishing of trench headquarters — anchors the scene in the material reality of the front.

### The Adjutant as Messenger

Earlier, during the preparations for the July 1st attack, the adjutant appears as a bearer of information and a figure of strained optimism:

> "Adjutant has just been up here, excited, optimistic, and unshaven. He went across last night to ginger up A Company who did very well, thanks to the bombers." [031-024]

The adjutant's excitement and optimism, paired with his unshaven face, capture the exhaustion that underlay the performance of military cheerfulness. He is the conduit through which the colonel's will reaches the companies, and his unshavenness — a small breach of military standards — testifies to the conditions under which that work was performed.

### The Lost Battalion

The adjutant also appears at moments of operational confusion, as when a stray unit lost its way in the support trench:

> "They had lost their way, and I remember how the exhausted men propped themselves against the sides of the trench while their exasperated Adjutant and a confused civilian Colonel grumbled to Barton about the ambiguity of their operation orders." [031-024]

The adjutant's exasperation is directed at the "ambiguity of operation orders" — the staff work that, when done poorly, left men wandering in the dark under shellfire. The adjutant is both the victim and the agent of the military bureaucracy: the one who must make sense of ambiguous orders and the one who must answer for the consequences when they cannot be made sense of.

## The Adjutant's Role

The adjutant was, in the formal structure of the battalion, responsible for all correspondence, orders, and records. He managed the battalion office, supervised the clerks, drafted operation orders, and maintained the war diary. He was typically a captain, often a regular officer, and his competence — or lack of it — had direct consequences for the battalion's effectiveness. Sassoon's adjutants are neither heroes nor villains; they are men trying to do an impossible job under impossible conditions, their exhaustion and occasional exasperation the only visible signs of the burden they carry.
