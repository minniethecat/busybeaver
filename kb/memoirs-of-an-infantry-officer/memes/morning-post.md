# The Morning Post

The conservative daily newspaper that, in [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s memoir, stands as shorthand for the patriotic press — the newspapers that celebrated the war, demanded its vigorous prosecution, and viewed any criticism of military policy as evidence of defective loyalty or outright treachery.

## About the Work

The Morning Post was a real London daily newspaper, founded in 1772, that by the early twentieth century had established itself as the voice of conservative, imperial, and military opinion. Its readership included the officer class, the gentry, and the upper reaches of the civil service — precisely the social world that [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] inhabited and from which he was becoming estranged.

The newspaper was known for its unwavering support of the war effort, its hostility to [[conscientious-objection|conscientious objector]]s and peace advocates, and its close connections to the military establishment. When [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s protest became public in 1917, the Morning Post was among the newspapers that would have condemned it — a fact that the memoir registers indirectly through its treatment of the paper as emblematic of the forces arrayed against dissent [037-002].

The Morning Post survived until 1937, when it was absorbed by the Daily Telegraph. During the war years, it was one of the most influential conservative voices in British journalism, competing with The Times for the loyalty of the establishment readership.

## In the Memoir

The Morning Post appears in the memoir not as a frequent presence but as a pointed reference — a name invoked to conjure a whole world of patriotic assumption. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] notes that "my name caused him to consult The Times" and that the Morning Post represented "bellicose patriotism" — the newspaper as metonym for the political culture that sustained the war [018-009][037-002].

The journalistic opposition between the Morning Post and [[unconservative-weekly|the Unconservative]] Weekly is one of the memoir's most economical political statements. The Morning Post stands for uncritical, jingoistic support of the war; [[unconservative-weekly|the Unconservative]] Weekly for critical but ultimately accommodating liberalism. Between them, they map the narrow range of permissible opinion in wartime [[england|Britain]] — a range that [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s protest would exceed.

Markington's comment that "the Morning Post would be anathematizing him" — referring to [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] — captures the newspaper's function as the voice of patriotic orthodoxy, the institutional enforcer of acceptable opinion [040-005]. The verb "anathematize" — with its ecclesiastical overtones — suggests that the Morning Post functioned as a kind of secular church, excommunicating those who deviated from the national creed.

A small but telling domestic detail: [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s [[aunt-evelyn|Aunt Evelyn]] "read it automatically in her Morning Post" — the adverb capturing the unreflective consumption of establishment opinion by the very class whose sons were being killed in the war the newspaper supported [007-015].

## Thematic Significance

The Morning Post represents the discursive machinery of war — the newspapers, editorials, and public rhetoric that sustained civilian support for the conflict and policed the boundaries of acceptable criticism. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s treatment of it is more contemptuous than his treatment of [[unconservative-weekly|the Unconservative]] Weekly; where the liberal journal is at least intelligent in its limitations, the Morning Post simply embodies the prejudices of its readership.

But the memoir does something more interesting than simply attack the Morning Post. By noting that [[aunt-evelyn|Aunt Evelyn]] reads it "automatically," [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] suggests that the problem is not merely the newspaper's editorial line but the broader culture of unreflective patriotism that made such journalism possible. The Morning Post is not an external force imposing opinion on a passive public; it is the voice of that public's own assumptions, printed and circulated.

The contrast with [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s own writing — the memoir itself — is implicit but unmistakable. Where the Morning Post offers the simplified certainties of patriotic journalism, the memoir offers the complexity, ambivalence, and moral seriousness of literary prose. The war, [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] implies, cannot be truthfully represented in the language of newspaper editorials; it requires the resources of literature.
