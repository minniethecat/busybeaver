# Artillery

## Overview

**Artillery** was the dominant weapon of the First World War, and its presence pervades Sassoon's memoir. From the landscape of the rear areas — "supply-convoys, artillery horse-lines" [002-004] — to the crescendo of bombardment before an attack, artillery shaped every aspect of the infantry officer's experience.

## The Artillery Preparation

Before any major attack, the artillery delivered a preparatory bombardment designed to cut German wire, destroy positions, and demoralize defenders. Sassoon records the anxious question hanging over every infantry assault: "Had the artillery preparation been inadequate?" [012-006]. The answer, too often, was yes.

An anonymous humorist captured the grim irony: "the batteries had been silenced by our artillery" [012-003] — a claim whose falsehood was known to every man who heard the German guns still firing. The gap between artillery's promised effectiveness and its actual results was a recurring source of bitter humor.

## Friendly Fire and Betrayal

The most devastating passages concern artillery failing its own infantry. Sassoon describes men who "got badly cut up by the artillery support which ought to have made them feel fairly safe" [012-015]. The "ought to" is devastating — artillery was supposed to protect infantry, not destroy them.

Sassoon records "congestion of transport and artillery on the roads" [014-023] in the aftermath of battle, the material evidence of the vast industrial machinery of which artillery was the most visible component.

## The Soundscape of War

Artillery created the defining soundscape of the Western Front. Sassoon could "order the artillery to stop firing" [016-015] only in fantasy — in reality, the guns were an inescapable presence. Even on quiet days, "the wet weather was causing the artillery to spend an inactive Sunday" [033-012], the silence itself noteworthy.

The artillery general appears briefly in the memoir as a figure of detached professionalism: "An urbane Artillery General assured us that high explosive would be our best friend" — a statement whose irony Sassoon does not need to underline.
