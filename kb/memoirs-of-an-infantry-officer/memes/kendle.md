**Kendle** is a nineteen-year-old lance-corporal in C Company of the Second Battalion — a figure of irrepressible youthful energy whose presence illuminates some of the memoir's most intimate front-line scenes. "Many a company had its Kendle," Sassoon writes, "until the war broke his spirit." The sentence, with its shift from the particular to the universal and its devastating final clause, is a miniature of the memoir's method: the individual rendered with precision, the generalization earned through accumulated loss.

## In the Memoir

### The Wire-Cutting Expedition

Kendle first appears prominently during the night expedition to cut the German wire before the Somme offensive. Sherston's party, working in darkness under shellfire, dwindles as men are wounded or lose heart:

> "In the first greying of dawn only three of us were still at it. Kendle (a nineteen year old lance-corporal from my platoon) and Worgan (one of the tough characters of our company) were slicing away for all they were worth." [007-003]

When the work proves impossible in the growing light, Kendle responds with characteristic insouciance: "Kendle disappeared into the trench and sauntered back to me, puffing a surreptitious Woodbine. I was making a last onslaught on a clawing thicket... 'We can't do any more in this daylight,' said Kendle." [007-003] The surreptitious cigarette, the casual assessment of tactical reality — these details establish Kendle as a soldier who has mastered the art of survival without losing his humanity.

Sassoon pauses to fix the moment in memory: "That moment has impressed itself strongly on my memory; young Kendle was remarkable for his cheerfulness and courage, and his cheeky jokes. Many a company had its Kendle, until the war broke his spirit." [007-003] The elegiac turn — from the specific young man to the universal type, from present vitality to future devastation — is one of the memoir's most compressed emotional movements. The "cheeky jokes" are what the war will destroy; the memoir is their preservation.

### The Second Expedition

The next day, Sherston decides to return to the wire. "Kendle was unwilling to be left out of the adventure, but two of us would be less conspicuous than three, and my feeling for Kendle was somewhat protective" [007-006]. Sherston's protectiveness toward Kendle — the officer's paternal concern for the youngest man in his platoon — is one of the memoir's quiet affirmations of the bonds that the war forged between men of different ranks and backgrounds.

### The Attack on Quadrangle Trench

Kendle reappears during the chaotic night attack on Quadrangle Trench. In the darkness, "Everyone was anonymous in the dark, but 'It's me, Kendle, Sir,' from a looming figure beside me implied an intention to share my explorations" [013-023]. The volunteered identification — "It's me, Kendle, Sir" — is both practical (a man identifying himself in darkness) and personal (a relationship asserted in the midst of chaos). Together they navigate the dark wood, encounter a panicked officer, and eventually join the rest of the company.

Later, exhausted, the men rest on the fire-step: "The men were sitting on the rough-hewn fire-step, and soon we were all dozing. Barton's bulky figure nodded beside me, and Kendle fell fast asleep with his head against my shoulder" [013-025]. The image of Kendle sleeping against Sherston's shoulder is one of the memoir's most tender — a moment of physical intimacy between officer and enlisted man, made possible only by the extremity of shared exhaustion.

## Character and Significance

Kendle is the memoir's representative of a particular kind of war loss: not the death of innocence (that belongs to Durley, to Ormand, to the others whose deaths are recorded) but the gradual erosion of spirit. "Many a company had its Kendle, until the war broke his spirit" — the sentence does not record Kendle's death, only his breaking. What happened to him after the memoir's events is left unsaid, but the elegy is already complete in the space between his jaunty smile in the wire-cutting scene and the knowledge, shared by narrator and reader, that the war will not spare such men.
