# Kit

The military term for a soldier's issued equipment and personal effects — web-equipment, packs, weapons, groundsheet, and the small necessities of trench life.

## Definition & Context

In the British Expeditionary Force, "kit" encompassed the full range of equipment a soldier carried: webbing, ammunition pouches, water bottle, entrenching tool, groundsheet, and pack. Officers carried additional items including revolvers, binoculars, maps, and valises. The weight of full marching order — often sixty pounds or more — was a defining physical reality of infantry service, and soldiers frequently sought to reduce their loads to the essentials. [012-001] [016-002]

## In the Memoir

The memoir is punctuated by moments where men handle their equipment — assembling kits before a move, searching for lost items in the dark, discarding packs before an assault. In one scene, the sounds of men "putting their kits together" in a barn before a relief are described: a clatter of voices and metal that captures the perpetual cycle of packing and unpacking that structured a soldier's existence. [012-001]

The most vivid kit-related passage comes during the preparations for the Somme offensive. Two battalions called up from reserve are heard "getting into their equipment," the creaking and clanking of webbing and weapons filling the darkness. [014-004] Before the assault, Sassoon observes rows of packs "tidily arranged" — the abandoned equipment of men who would not return — and counts the "sets of equipment" left behind as a measure of the attack's cost. [014-015] [014-017] He himself discards his tin hat and equipment to move more freely, slinging only a bag of bombs across his shoulder. [014-016]

After being wounded, Sassoon's equipment is cut from his body by a stretcher-bearer who ties up his throat wound — the kit serving as both burden and, in its removal, a marker of the transition from combatant to casualty. [024-005] On the march, soldiers trudge under "the heavy weight of their equipment," a physical endurance test that shapes every movement. [030-005]

## The Wire-Cutters

One of the memoir's most sustained engagements with kit involves a pair of wire-cutters — an item Sassoon procured himself rather than relying on army issue. "Armed with Aunt Evelyn's membership ticket," he records with characteristic irony, "I had invaded the Army and Navy Stores and procured a superb salmon, two bottles of old brandy, an automatic pistol, and two pairs of wire-cutters with rubber-covered handles" [010-007]. The wire-cutters, he declares, were "my private contribution to the Great Offensive" [010-007]. The army-issue cutters were notoriously blunt — "I had often cursed the savage bluntness of our Company's wire-cutters" — and Sassoon's private purchase reflects an officer's privilege but also his practical foresight: "if we were going over the top we might want to cut our own wire first, to say nothing of the German wire" [010-007]. The wire-cutters become a recurring motif, their effectiveness noted after a trench raid: "young Kendle remarked that my new wire-cutters were a fair treat" [012-011].

## Sassoon's Perspective

Sassoon depicts kit with an eye for its material detail and its psychological weight. A fellow officer declares, "I've reduced my kit to a minimum this time. No revolver, only a toothbrush" — a sardonic distillation of what experience teaches a man to value. [025-002] The groundsheet, a ubiquitous piece of equipment, becomes in Sassoon's telling a symbol of helplessness: "You just lie there, chewing the groundsheet with your kit." [038-020] The phrase captures the infantryman's reduction to his equipment — a man who can only wait, teeth clenched on the fabric issued to keep him dry.
