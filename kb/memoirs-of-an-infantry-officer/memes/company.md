# Company & Company Mess

## Overview

The infantry **company** (approximately 80–100 men) was the fundamental tactical and social unit in Sassoon's war experience. As a company officer, Sassoon's daily life revolved around the Company Mess — the shared living quarters of the company's officers — which served as both a physical refuge and a social microcosm of the army's class structure [005-002].

## The Company as Military Unit

A company formed the building block of battalion operations. In trench warfare, companies rotated between front-line, support, and reserve positions. "For the first three days Barton's Company was in reserve at 71. North" [006-004], illustrating the rotation that structured life on the Western Front.

The company was led by a Company Commander (usually a Major or senior Captain), supported by subalterns (junior officers) and a Company Sergeant-Major who managed the enlisted ranks [009-001]. Companies were further divided into platoons, each led by a subaltern.

## The Company Mess as Social Institution

The Company Mess was the heart of officer life. Sassoon describes it with characteristic ambivalence: "There was harmony in our Company Mess, as if our certainty of impending action had brought us together in a quiet exchange of comradeship" [010-011]. The Mess was where officers ate, drank, wrote letters, and shared the peculiar intimacies of men living under the shadow of death.

Sassoon's return from leave was marked by returning to the Mess: "I felt almost glad to be back as I walked up to our old Company Mess with Flook carrying my valise on his back" [005-002]. The Mess represented a form of belonging, even within the absurdity of war.

## Casualties and the Company

The company bore the direct human cost of trench warfare. After a raid, Sassoon records: "I looked down at him and the tears were running down my face. He had been one of the best men in our Company" [007-011]. The intimacy of company life meant that every casualty was personal.

By 1916, a company could be reduced to a skeleton: "C Company now reduced to six runners" [007-012 note in index]. The attrition of company strength through death, wounds, and shell-shock was the lived reality of infantry command.
