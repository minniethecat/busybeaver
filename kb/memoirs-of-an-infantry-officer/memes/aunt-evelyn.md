# Aunt Evelyn

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s aunt, who appears in the memoir primarily through her letters and parcels — a figure of civilian solicitude whose monthly kippers and faithful reading of the [[official-language|communiqué]]s represent the dissonance between [[england|home]]-front understanding and trench reality.

## Role & Identity

Aunt Evelyn is [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s maternal aunt and his primary [[england|home]]-front connection during the memoir. She sends him kippers twice a month, reads the G.H.Q. [[official-language|communiqué]]s in the newspaper, and receives his farewell letter before the trench raid. She exists entirely as a presence at a distance — the civilian correspondent whose concern is genuine but whose understanding of the war is necessarily mediated by the official language she reads [003-005][007-015].

## In the Memoir

Aunt Evelyn enters the memoir indirectly, through the arrival of her parcels: "the box of kippers which Aunt Evelyn sent me twice a month" [003-005]. The kippers are a comic detail — incongruous civilian comfort arriving in a trench billet — but they are also a structural element: the monthly parcel marks time, connects the front to [[england|home]], reminds the soldier that there is a world where kippers are packed in boxes and mailed with love.

Her most significant appearance is also indirect: before the trench raid, [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] "had written a farewell letter to Aunt Evelyn. I did not read it through; I was afraid of its self-pitying tone" [007-001]. The letter is the memoir's most honest acknowledgment of what the [[england|home]] front means to the soldier: the person you write to when you think you might die.

Aunt Evelyn also reads the [[official-language|communiqué]]s: "Aunt Evelyn probably read it automatically as she munched her breakfast toast" [007-015]. The image is devastating in its ordinariness — the aunt who sends kippers is the same person who reads "maintained a spirited bombing fight" over breakfast, never knowing what those words actually describe.

## Historical Counterpart

Aunt Evelyn is likely based on one of [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s actual aunts; his mother's sisters were an important part of his civilian support network during the war. The name "Evelyn" may be a tribute to his friend Evelyn Waugh or may be a real family name.

## Significance

Aunt Evelyn represents the memoir's most sophisticated treatment of the civilian-military divide. She is not ignorant or indifferent — she sends the kippers, she reads the [[official-language|communiqué]]s, she cares. But the structure of the war makes genuine understanding impossible. She can only know what the [[official-language|communiqué]] tells her, and the [[official-language|communiqué]] is designed to hide what the memoir reveals.

## Others' View of Aunt Evelyn

Aunt Evelyn never speaks directly in the memoir, yet she is perceived through several distinct lenses:

- **[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s view**: The narrator's attitude toward his aunt is a complex mixture of affection, guilt, and protective distance. He accepts her kippers gratefully, writes his farewell letter knowing she will worry, and never attempts to tell her what the war is actually like. His affection is genuine; his silence is a form of care — he protects her from the knowledge that would destroy her world [003-005][007-001][007-015].
- **The implied author's view (1930)**: The mature Sassoon who writes the memoir in 1930 looks back at Aunt Evelyn with a tenderness that the younger self could not fully acknowledge. The image of her reading the communiqué over breakfast toast — "probably read it automatically" — contains no satire, only a quiet sadness at the unbridgeable distance between experience and its representation [007-015].
- **[[aunt-evelyn|Aunt Evelyn]]'s view of herself**: Inferred through her actions rather than stated: she is a woman who expresses love through parcels and membership tickets, who worries about her nephew's safety and career, who reads the war news because it is the closest she can come to understanding where he is. Her "lightning-conductor" — a device for warding off Zeppelin anxiety — is a small, poignant symbol of civilian attempts to manage fears they cannot fully grasp [010-003][010-004][015-003].
- **The reader's view**: The memoir positions the reader to see Aunt Evelyn with double vision: as the loving aunt she is, and as the embodiment of a civilian ignorance that the war makes inevitable rather than culpable. She is not satirised; she is mourned, as one mourns the innocence that the war destroyed in everyone [010-001].

## Authorial Technique

Sassoon's construction of Aunt Evelyn exemplifies his command of the indirect portrait:

- **Character by parcel**: Aunt Evelyn is the memoir's purest example of a character built entirely through objects — kippers, a membership ticket, a lightning-conductor, sweet peas and roses arranged in a bathroom. Each object is freighted with meaning: the kippers are love, the ticket is connection, the flowers are the peacetime world persisting. Sassoon never describes Aunt Evelyn directly; he describes what she sends, and the objects do the work of characterisation [003-005][010-004][015-003].
- **The breakfast toast juxtaposition**: The image of Aunt Evelyn reading the communiqué while munching toast is one of the memoir's most technically accomplished sentences. It juxtaposes the mundane (toast) with the appalling (the action the communiqué describes) and the pathos of a civilian trying to understand. The adverb "automatically" does the heaviest work: she reads without comprehension because the language of communiqués is designed to prevent comprehension [007-015].
- **Absence as structural principle**: Aunt Evelyn never appears in a direct scene — she exists only in letters received, parcels opened, and the narrator's reports of her actions at home. This technique mirrors the soldier's actual experience of family: present only through the postal service, loved from a distance that the memoir never attempts to close [010-002][015-003].
- **The farewell letter as emotional proxy**: The letter Sassoon writes before the raid — then refuses to reread "afraid of its self-pitying tone" — uses Aunt Evelyn as the figure who receives the narrator's fear without ever knowing it. She is the addressee of the letter, but the real communication is between Sassoon and the reader, who understands what the aunt cannot [007-001].
