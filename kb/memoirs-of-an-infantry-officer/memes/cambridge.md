**Cambridge** appears in Sassoon's memoir as a recurring symbol of the civilian intellectual life that the war has deferred, interrupted, or compromised. It is at once a place of lost possibility — the university career that will never happen — and a site of the war's encroachment on England, as the Cadet Battalion transforms its colleges into training grounds.

## In the Memoir

### The Undergraduate Who Never Was

Cambridge enters the memoir obliquely, through the figure of [[allgood|Allgood]], the gentle young officer with whom Sherston spends his convalescence. "It was easy to imagine him as an undergraduate at Cambridge," Sassoon writes, "travelling in Germany during the Long Vacation and taking a good degree. But his degree had been postponed indefinitely" [002-007]. Allgood is killed shortly afterwards, and Cambridge becomes — in the memoir's quiet logic of loss — not a place but an absence: the life that was owed to a generation and never delivered.

### Scholars at the Front

The motif recurs with [[bill-eaves|Bill Eaves]], "a Classical Scholar at Cambridge" who "had won medals there for writing Greek and Latin epigrams" [005-003]. Eaves, who gets a "nice bullet wound in the shoulder" and later returns to the front only to be killed leading a local attack, embodies the incongruity between the civilisation Cambridge represents and the barbarism it has been conscripted into serving. The classical education that should have produced scholarship produces instead — in the memoir's bitter irony — epitaphs.

### The Cadet Battalion

The most sustained Cambridge episode occurs late in the memoir, when Sherston applies for an instructorship with a Cadet Battalion stationed there — a ruse to buy time while he prepares his anti-war protest [040-001]. The college that houses the orderly room "had been a library (the books were still there)," and the Colonel "had been a History Don with a keen interest in the Territorials." The mingling of scholarship and militarism is rendered with quiet absurdity: Sherston, inwardly committed to denouncing the war, plays the part of "respectful young applicant for instructorship in the Arts of War" so convincingly that "the existence of my 'statement' became, for the moment, an improbability."

### King's Chapel and the Conscience

After the interview, Sherston sits in King's Chapel and tries "to recover my conviction of the nobility of my enterprise" [040-004]. It is there that he reminds himself that Cambridge had dismissed [[tyrrell|Tyrrell]] from his lectureship "because he disbelieved in the War" — and uses this institutional intolerance to stiffen his own resolve. "One can't possibly side with people like that," he tells himself. "All they care about is keeping up with the other colleges in the casualty lists." Cambridge, in this moment, becomes both sanctuary and antagonist: the place that shelters his conscience and the institution that would expel it.

### The Air Raid

Sherston's journey to Cambridge is itself interrupted by history: a German air raid on Liverpool Street Station kills a traveller on the platform and halts the noon express [040-001]. The juxtaposition — the pastoral calm of the Cambridge train and the sudden violence of bombs on English soil — is characteristic of the memoir's method: the war is never far away, even when one is travelling toward a university town in "sleepy green landscapes."
