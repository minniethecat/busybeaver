# Transport

## Overview

**Transport** in Sassoon's memoir refers to the battalion's horse-drawn logistics system — the limbers, wagons, and horses that carried rations, ammunition, and equipment between the rear areas and the front line. The Transport section was the battalion's lifeline, operating from rear-area camps a few miles behind the trenches. For Sassoon, service as Transport Officer was an interlude of relative safety, and the Transport Lines appear throughout the memoir as a threshold between the front and the world behind it.

## The Transport Lines

The Transport Lines were a fixed feature of the battalion's rear-area landscape. Sassoon's first view of them is detailed and static: "I stared at the Transport lines below us — a few dirty white bell-tents and the limbers and wagons and picketed horses" [006-003]. The scene is domestic and agricultural rather than military — horses switching their tails, men stooping to groom legs, bees humming in a neglected garden. The Transport Lines represent the world the war had not yet destroyed.

During the Battle of Bazentin Ridge, Sassoon was sent to the Transport Lines as one of eight officers kept in reserve: "Next day I moved to the Transport Lines, a couple of miles back, for I was one of eight officers kept in reserve. There I existed monotonously while the Battalion was engaged in the Battle of Bazentin Ridge. My boredom was combined with suspense, for after the first attack I might be sent for at any moment, so I could never wander far from the Transport Lines" [016-003]. The Transport Lines here are a place of waiting — safe but psychologically taxing, the officer suspended between the relief of being out of danger and the guilt of being out of the fight.

The Transport Lines were also a place of rumor and news. Returning to the tent after an excursion to the old front line, Sassoon "found everyone in a state of excitement" [016-006] — the ration-party had returned with information about the attack, including news of casualties.

## The Transport Officer

Sassoon himself served as Transport Officer early in his time in France, a role his Aunt Evelyn clung to as a comforting fiction: "Poor Aunt Evelyn was still comfortingly convinced that I was transport officer, though I had given up that job nearly three months ago" [007-001]. The Transport Officer's duties were logistical rather than combatant, and the post was seen as safer — hence Aunt Evelyn's preference.

When Sassoon fell ill after the Somme, he discovered a remnant of his transport duties: "I remembered that I possessed a thermometer, which had been handed over to me when I was Transport Officer. I had never taken the temperatures of any of the horses, but I now experimented shakily on myself" [018-004]. The thermometer, meant for horses, registered 105° — a darkly comic detail that underscores the improvisational character of rear-area medical care.

## Joe Dottrell and the Transport World

Joe Dottrell, the Transport Officer who succeeded Sassoon, is one of the memoir's most warmly drawn characters. He appears each evening "with the ration-limbers" [006-004], the human face of the supply system. Dottrell moves the Transport back during the battalion's relief at the Somme: "Late in the afternoon Dottrell moved the Transport back about three miles, to a hill above Dernancourt" [016-016].

Dottrell was popular among the men. A Transport man wanted to volunteer for a trench raid, but Dottrell talked him out of it: "One of the Transport men wanted to send his name in for this one; but I told him to think of his poor unfortunate wife, and we're pushing him off on a transport-course to learn cold-shoeing" [006-002]. The detail is characteristic — Dottrell protects his men with paternal pragmatism, finding bureaucratic pretexts to keep them safe.

## Bates the Quartermaster

Bates, the Quartermaster of the Second Battalion, is described as "a burlier prototype of Joe Dottrell, with fewer political prejudices" [031-005]. His devotion to his animals was legendary: "It was said that on one occasion, when the Transport was having a rough time, Bates had rigged up a tarpaulin shelter for his mare and slept out in the open himself" [031-005]. The anecdote typifies the culture of the Transport section — men who identified more with their animals and their practical duties than with the abstractions of military glory.

## Transport and the Battlefield

The Transport was the essential connective tissue of the battalion's operations. During the retreat from the Somme, "the first four miles took us four hours, owing to congestion of transport and artillery on the roads round Fricourt" [014-023]. After an attack, "all sorts of transport arriving at Cemetery Cross Roads" [013-031] marked the consolidation of gains — the logistical tail following the combat spearhead.

Even in the deep tunnel system before Arras, the Transport's reach extended underground: "Owing to the scarcity of water (which had to be brought up by the Transport who were eight miles back, at Blairville) washing wasn't possible" [033-011]. The Transport was always eight miles back, always arriving — the rhythm of supply that made trench warfare sustainable.

The mule's "short solo from the Transport Lines" [031-029] that Sassoon heard on Good Friday morning is a final, almost comic detail — the Transport as a place of animal sounds and ordinary life, audible even at the edge of battle.
