# Moonlight

## Overview

**Moonlight** appears throughout Sassoon's memoir as an atmospheric element that transforms the wartime landscape. It is simultaneously beautiful and treacherous — a source of aesthetic relief from the squalor of the trenches, and a tactical hazard that exposes soldiers to enemy observation. Moonlight bridges the memoir's twin registers of lyrical nature writing and military realism.

## Moonlight as Tactical Hazard

On night patrols, moonlight was the infantryman's enemy. Sassoon records that "the bright moonlight had prevented them from properly inspecting the German wire" [005-007], forcing patrols to abort their missions. The company commander's "language about moonlight and snipers was a masterpiece" [005-007] — a frustration familiar to every officer who had tried to move men across No Man's Land under a full moon.

## Moonlight and the Aesthetics of War

Despite its dangers, moonlight offered moments of strange beauty. In billets behind the lines, Sassoon "blew out the candle so that I could watch the moonlight which latticed the floor with shadows of the leaded windows" [020-007], adding the reflection: "The War's all right as long as one doesn't get killed or smashed up." The moonlight transforms a bare billet room into something worth looking at, a brief respite from war's ugliness.

"An hour before dawn the road was still an empty picture of moonlight" [016-017] — the word "picture" signaling Sassoon's pre-war aesthetic sensibility, the landscape framed as though for a painting. As the night gave way to morning, "moonlight and dawn began to mingle, and I could see the barley swaying indolently against the sky" [016-017].

## Moonlight and Comradeship

Moonlight appears at moments of unofficial fellowship. Leake and Sassoon "meandered along an empty street, accompanied by our tipsy shadows" [031-022] in the "white moonlight." The battalion had supper "out in the moonlight, sitting round a brazier with plates of ration stew on our knees" [031-028] — the moon presiding over a scene of rough domesticity at the edge of battle.

## The Ghastly Glimmer

Moonlight could also render the horrors of war more visible. After an attack, Sassoon remembered "some hazy moonlight" illuminating "the figures of men huddled against the sides of communication trenches; seeing them in some sort of ghastly glimmer — (was it, perhaps, the diffused whiteness of a sinking flare beyond the ridge?) I was doubtful whether they were asleep or dead, for the attitudes of many were like death, grotesque and distorted" [033-006]. The moonlight here is not beautiful but ghastly, refusing to distinguish the living from the dead.

## Moonlight in England

The memoir's frame narrative returns to moonlight in the English countryside. At Aunt Evelyn's house, "the white beehives glimmered in the moonlight like bones" [043-001] — the simile "like bones" carrying the war back into the peaceful English landscape, the moonlight connecting both worlds.
