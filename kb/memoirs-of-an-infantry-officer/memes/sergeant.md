# Sergeant

## Overview

The **sergeant** (and the senior rank of Company-Sergeant-Major) was the backbone of the British infantry battalion in Sassoon's memoir. While officers came and went — often with little experience — the sergeants and N.C.O.s embodied institutional knowledge, discipline, and the human reality of command at the platoon and company level.

## The Sergeant-Instructor

At the Army School, the sergeant-instructor represented a terrifying ideal of military professionalism. One sergeant was "a tall sinewy machine" who had been "trained to such a pitch of frightfulness that at a moment's warning he could divest himself of all semblance of humanity" [002-013]. Yet when Sassoon encountered the sergeant-instructor at bayonet training, he found him "polite and unformidable" [003-001] — the ferocity was a performance demanded by the system, not the man.

## Regular N.C.O.s and Recruit Training

At the infantry depot in England, the training of recruits was "left mainly to sergeant-instructors, most of whom were Regular N.C.O.s of the best pattern, hard-worked men who were on their legs from morning to night, and strict because they had to be strict" [023-004]. Sassoon noted the deteriorating quality of recruits — "what in earlier days had been drafts of volunteers were now droves of victims" [023-004] — yet the sergeants maintained standards regardless.

## The Platoon Sergeant

Miles, Sassoon's own platoon sergeant, appears as a figure of quiet endurance. After the trench raid, Sassoon observed that Miles "hadn't been quite his usual self since the raid; but he'd been in France nearly a year, which was longer than most men could stick such a life" [009-003]. Sassoon reflected that if Miles were still there months later, "some fresh young officer from England will be accusing him of being windy" [009-003] — a recognition of the injustice done to experienced N.C.O.s by inexperienced officers.

Sergeant Baldock, another platoon sergeant, is described as "an admirably impassive little man who never ceased to behave like a perfectly trained and confidential manservant" [034-015]. When Sassoon closed his eyes expecting death, it was Baldock who was beside him: "When I reopened them Sergeant Baldock was beside me, discreet and sympathetic, and to my surprise I discovered that I wasn't dead" [034-016].

## The Company-Sergeant-Major

The C.S.M. was the hardest-working man in the battalion. Sassoon states it explicitly: "it cannot be too often repeated that C.S.M.s were the hardest worked men in the infantry; everything depended on them, and if anyone deserved a K.C.B. it was a good C.S.M." [033-001]. On the march from Corbie, it was the C.S.M. who "was carrying three rifles as well as his own" [031-023] — the physical embodiment of the N.C.O.'s burden.

The C.S.M. also served as the human link between officers and men: delivering the rum ration [009-001], managing the chaos of battalion moves [012-001], and appearing as one of the irreducible elements of a company reduced to its essentials: "six runners, two stretcher-bearers, Company-Sergeant-Major, signallers, and Barton's servant" [013-029].

## The Quartermaster-Sergeant

In the rear areas, the Quartermaster-sergeant "ruled the ration-dump" [006-004], a small sovereign of supply, and Dottrell the transport officer arrived each evening "with the ration-limbers" [006-004]. The Q.M.S. represented the lifeline of food and matériel that kept the battalion functioning.

## Casualties Among Sergeants

Sassoon records the deaths of sergeants with terse finality. "C.S.M. Miles and Danby both killed" [020-013] appears in a diary-like list. "Poor Sergeant Blaxton was killed" [038-019] — the adjective "poor" carrying the weight of personal loss. A breathless and dishevelled sergeant appears at H.Q. with "an incoherent statement about their having been driven back" [034-014], the bearer of battlefield news that no officer wanted to hear.
