"Sacrifice" — the word that public rhetoric used to ennoble the dead — is one of the memoir's most quietly contested terms. Sassoon never denies that men sacrificed themselves; what he refuses is the narrative that makes that sacrifice meaningful. Throughout the narrative, the language of sacrifice is shown to be inadequate to the facts it is asked to contain, a consolatory fiction that the memoir dismantles by simply showing what sacrifice actually looked like.

## Flixécourt as Prelude to Sacrifice

### The Theatrical Frame

The memoir's most extended reflection on sacrifice comes in a passage that frames the Flixécourt period — the weeks at the Army School before the Somme — as a dramatic prelude. Sassoon writes of "life at the Army School, with its superb physical health" being "like a prelude to some really conclusive sacrifice of high-spirited youth" [025-005]. The theatrical metaphor — "Act I," "Act II" — gives the narrative a tragic structure, but the tragedy is one without catharsis. The sacrifice is "conclusive" not in the sense of achieving something, but in the sense of being final, terminal. The "high-spirited youth" are not redeemed by their deaths; they are simply gone.

### The Ritual Metaphor

Sassoon extends the metaphor with a self-consciousness that borders on the uncanny: "it had been almost as if my own germinant aliveness were interfused with some sacrificial rite which was to celebrate the harvest" [025-005]. The language of ritual sacrifice — the harvest, the rite — is deployed with an irony so fine that it is almost invisible. The word "almost" keeps the passage from tipping into full irony; Sassoon recognizes that the feeling was real, even as he now knows it to have been an illusion. The "sacrificial rite" was a way of making sense of experience that the experience itself would later invalidate.

## Sacrifice on the Somme

### The Arithmetic of Loss

The Battle of the Somme transforms sacrifice from a literary metaphor into a brute fact of arithmetic. "The Royal Irish had had sixty casualties; we had one killed and four wounded" [014-007] — the comparison between battalions renders suffering as a kind of competitive accounting, and the word "only" hovers unspoken over the tally. Sixty men are dead or wounded, and the memoir records it as a comparative statistic. This is what sacrifice becomes under the conditions of industrialized warfare: not an act of individual heroism but a number in a report, a line in a casualty return, a figure to be compared against other figures to determine which battalion has "done well."

### The Aid Post Buried

The letter from a comrade that Sassoon incorporates into the narrative makes the arithmetic human: "The aid-post, a bit of a dugout hastily made, was blown in. At the time it contained 5 wounded men, 5 stretcher-bearers, and the doctor. All were killed except the Doc." [020-010]. Five wounded men seeking safety, five stretcher-bearers tending them, all "sacrificed" in the most literal sense — given up, spent, consumed — by a shell that made no distinction between combatant and non-combatant, between the wounded and those who cared for them.

## The Brigadier's Sacrifice

The memoir's most brutal treatment of sacrifice comes in a passage about a Brigadier who ordered an impossible raid "which had been prevented by a providential thaw and caused numerous deaths in a subsequently sacrificed battalion" [034-006]. The word "sacrificed" here is not metaphorical. Men died — "numerous deaths" — because a commander with a "bee in his bonnet" wanted results. The battalion was "sacrificed" in the most literal sense: given up, spent, consumed for a purpose that even the narrator cannot dignify with a name.

This is sacrifice stripped of all its consoling associations: not an offering but a waste, not noble but bureaucratic. The Brigadier's "bee in his bonnet" — the phrase's dismissive colloquialism is itself an indictment — is all that stands between the men and their survival. The "providential thaw" that prevented the first attempt is characterized in language that might belong to a religious narrative, but the providence is ironic: God sends a thaw to save the battalion, and the Brigadier simply waits for better weather to destroy it.

## Sacrifice and the Home Front

### The Abstraction of Sacrifice

At Nutwood Manor and elsewhere on the home front, Sassoon encounters a society whose understanding of sacrifice is entirely abstract. The "pleasant-mannered neighbours who dropped in to tea" speak of the war with a "blandness" that is "incompatible with the notion of 'sacrifice'" [038-014]. For them, sacrifice is a word — a term in political discourse, a category in newspaper editorials, a sentiment to be expressed over tea. They have no referent for what the word describes: the grey-haired lance-corporal with his foot blown off, the stretcher-bearers buried in the collapsed aid post, the battalion "sacrificed" for a brigadier's obsession.

### Class and Sacrifice

The gap between home-front sacrifice and front-line sacrifice is one of the memoir's most persistent structural ironies. At Nutwood Manor, Sassoon encounters "the patrician privileges for whose preservation I had chucked bombs at Germans and carelessly offered myself as a target for a sniper" [038-019] — the language of sacrifice ("offered myself as a target") collides with the language of privilege, and the collision exposes the class structure that the rhetoric of sacrifice is designed to conceal.

## The End of Sacrifice

By the memoir's conclusion, sacrifice has lost whatever meaning it may once have held. Sherston's declaration — his public protest against the war — is a refusal to participate further in the sacrificial system. The medical board that diagnoses shell shock rather than court-martialing him is a different kind of sacrifice: the army spares the man to preserve the institution's reputation. Sassoon's narrator, convalescing in England, is left with the knowledge that the language of sacrifice will survive the war intact, ready to be deployed in the next conflict. The memoir's final, unspoken accusation is that this continuity — the persistence of a rhetoric that turns death into meaning — is itself a kind of crime.

The war's casualties are counted in "convoys of wounded and gassed being carried into medical stations" [044-008], a procession of suffering that continues long after the guns fall silent. Sacrifice, in the memoir's final accounting, is not something men do; it is something that is done to them — by generals with bees in their bonnets, by politicians who speak of honor, by a society that needs the dead to have died for something.

## Sassoon's Perspective

Sassoon's treatment of sacrifice is the memoir's most sustained act of moral seriousness. He never mocks the dead — to do so would violate his deepest loyalties. Instead, he refuses to let the dead be used. The memoir insists on the particularity of each loss against the generalizing language of sacrifice. What makes the critique so effective is its restraint: Sassoon does not argue against sacrifice, he simply shows what it means, and trusts the reader to recognize that the word has been emptied of meaning by the reality it describes.
