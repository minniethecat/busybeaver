# David Cromlech

A fellow officer in the [[first-battalion|1st Battalion]], [[royal-welch-fusiliers|Royal Welch Fusiliers]], based on the poet [[robert-graves|Robert Graves]] — [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s closest friend in the memoir, whose argumentative energy and moral certainty provide the counterpoint against which [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] develops his own evolving position on the war.

## Role & Identity

David Cromlech (a lightly fictionalized [[robert-graves|Robert Graves]]) is an officer in [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s battalion. He is "big and impulsive," intellectually restless, argumentative — the kind of man who cannot let a proposition pass without challenging it [016-005]. His nickname among the other officers — "that queer bird Cromlech" — captures his outsider status: too intellectual, too intense, too serious for the mess-room banter [016-007].

Cromlech is also, crucially, [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s moral interlocutor. When [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] develops his [[protest|anti-war protest]], it is Cromlech who argues against it — not from principle but from pragmatism, from concern for his friend's safety, from the conviction that protest is futile. Their arguments structure the memoir's later chapters as a kind of Socratic dialogue on the morality of war.

## In the Memoir

Cromlech appears first in Chapter 16, during the Somme period, where his larger-than-life personality immediately distinguishes him from the other officers. "David certainly was deplorably untidy" — the detail is trivial but diagnostic: Cromlech cares about ideas, not appearances [016-009].

His most significant appearances come in the memoir's final chapters (40–46), where he acts as [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s confidant and critic during the protest period. Cromlech's position is essentially conservative — he believes [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] is misguided, that the protest will achieve nothing, that the proper course is to return to duty. But his arguments are made with such energy and affection that they never read as mere opposition. Cromlech is the friend who disagrees with everything you believe and whom you cannot dismiss.

## Historical Counterpart

David Cromlech is a transparent fictionalization of [[robert-graves|Robert Graves]] (1895–1985), the poet and author of "Goodbye to All That." [[robert-graves|Graves]] and [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] served together in the [[royal-welch-fusiliers|Royal Welch Fusiliers]] and remained close friends through the war and its aftermath. [[robert-graves|Graves]]'s intervention was instrumental in [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s being sent to [[craiglockhart|Craiglockhart]] rather than court-martialed after the protest.

## Significance

Cromlech/[[robert-graves|Graves]] serves a crucial structural function in the memoir: he is the voice of loyal opposition, the friend who argues against protest not because he supports the war but because he fears what protest will do to the protester. His presence prevents the memoir from becoming a monologue — [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s position is tested and refined through argument with Cromlech.
