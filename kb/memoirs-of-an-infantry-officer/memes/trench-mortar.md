# Trench Mortar

The **trench mortar** — a short-barrelled artillery piece firing high-angle projectiles — was a ubiquitous feature of the Western Front, bridging the gap between hand-thrown bombs and long-range artillery. In Sassoon's *Memoirs of an Infantry Officer*, trench mortars appear as part of the landscape of trench life: their distinctive sound, their white smoke, and their capacity to make a position untenable.

## The Sound and the Smoke

Sassoon's first mention of trench mortars is visual and almost pastoral: "Very still evening; sun rather hazy. Looking across to Fricourt; trench mortars bursting in the cemetery; dull white smoke slowly floats away over grey-green grass with buttercups and saffron weeds." The conjunction of trench mortars and buttercups, of explosive smoke drifting over wildflowers, captures the memoir's central tension — the natural world and the machinery of war occupying the same frame.

The trench mortar's ordnance is distinctive: its projectile arcs high before descending nearly vertically into the trench. Unlike the horizontal trajectory of field guns or the random fall of howitzer shells, the mortar's path is visible, its approach audible, its arrival a matter of seconds.

## Tactical Role

Trench mortars served a specific tactical function: the destruction of barbed wire and the targeted bombardment of enemy trench positions at close range. The memoir records that "some sort of damage had been done to the German wire which had been strafed by trench mortars during the day" — the mortar's high-angle fire making it the ideal weapon for cutting the wire that flat-trajectory field guns could not reach.

At the Sunken Road before the Somme attack, the bombardment intensifies: "Trench-mortars are knocking hell out of Sunken Road trench and the ground where the Manchester will attack soon." The colloquial "knocking hell out of" captures the mortar's concentrated violence — not the sweeping destruction of the barrage but a focused, positional pounding.

## The Enemy's Mortars

The memoir is careful to record the German trench mortars as well — the "Boche trench-mortars" that had been "strafing us more than usual that evening." Sassoon's physical misery mirrors the bombardment: "I'd got sore feet and a trench-mouth and food tasted filthy." The trench mortar is not a distant abstraction; it is the specific cause of a specific night's misery, as personal as a toothache.

The mortar's projectile — the "canister" that "came over in the evening dusk with a little trail of sparks behind them" — is rendered with almost aesthetic attention. The trail of sparks, visible against the darkening sky, gives the mortar round a terrible beauty, a signature that distinguishes it from the invisible shell and the silent bullet.
