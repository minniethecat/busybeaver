Conscientious objection — the refusal to participate in military service on moral or religious grounds — was one of the most contentious social issues in [[england|Britain]] during the First World War. In [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s memoir, it appears as a foil to his own evolving position: he was not a conscientious objector in the legal sense, yet his 1917 protest against the war's prolongation was an act of conscience that risked the same penalties — court-martial and imprisonment — faced by COs.

## Definition & Context

The Military Service Act of 1916 introduced conscription in [[england|Britain]] and established tribunals to hear applications for exemption on grounds of conscience. Those whose claims were rejected, or who refused to participate in the tribunal process, faced imprisonment, harsh treatment, and widespread public condemnation as "shirkers" and cowards. Approximately 16,000 men registered as conscientious objectors during the war, of whom about 6,000 were court-martialed and imprisoned. The "absolutist" COs — those who refused any form of alternative service — received the harshest treatment, including solitary confinement and force-feeding during hunger strikes.

## In the Memoir

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s engagement with conscientious objection in the memoir is ambivalent and layered. He observes staff officers with "a conscientious objection to physical discomfort" [028-008] — an ironic use of the term that mocks those who invoke conscience only when their own comfort is threatened. When he encounters an actual conscientious objector — "an intellectual conscientious objector (lately released after two years' imprisonment)" — [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s description is notable for its even-handedness, recording the man's ideas without immediate dismissal [042-001].

The memoir's central statement on the subject comes not in a passage about COs but in [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s own declaration: "I am not a conscientious objector. I am a soldier who believes that the war is being deliberately prolonged by those who have the power to end it." [041-003] This careful distinction — soldier, not CO — reveals both [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s residual identification with the military and his strategic awareness of the political cost of the conscientious objector label.

## Sassoon's Perspective

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s position toward conscientious objectors is complex. He shared their moral critique of the war but rejected their absolutism and their disengagement from military life. His protest was designed to work within the military system — a soldier's protest, not a civilian's refusal. This distinction was politically shrewd: by framing his objection as that of a decorated fighting officer rather than a pacifist outsider, [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] made his critique far harder to dismiss. Yet the memoir's treatment of the intellectual CO at the Depot suggests a grudging respect for those who had taken the harder path of outright refusal. [042-001] [046-011]

## Historical Reality

The historical [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s protest — expressed in his letter "A Soldier's Declaration" of July 1917 — was resolved not through court-martial, as [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] had expected and perhaps hoped, but through the intervention of [[robert-graves|Robert Graves]], who persuaded the medical board to send [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] to [[craiglockhart|Craiglockhart War Hospital]] for treatment of [[shell-shock|neurasthenia]]. This outcome — being diagnosed with shell shock rather than tried for mutiny — was in its own way a comment on how the British establishment preferred to manage dissent: as sickness rather than sedition.
