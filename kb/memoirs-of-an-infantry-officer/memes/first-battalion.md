# First Battalion, Flintshire Fusiliers

The regular battalion of the [[royal-welch-fusiliers|Flintshire Fusiliers]] to which [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] was first posted on arrival in [[france|France]] — the unit in which he learned the trade of infantry officer, formed his earliest combat friendships, and against which all his subsequent military experience was implicitly measured.

## Identity & Structure

The First Battalion was, like the Second, a regular army battalion of the [[royal-welch-fusiliers|Flintshire Fusiliers]] — part of the pre-war professional army that was largely destroyed in the first two years of fighting. By the time [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] joined it in 1915, the battalion had already seen heavy service and many of its original officers and men had become casualties. The "old hands" who remained — men who had been in [[france|France]] since 1914 — exercised a tacit authority that new arrivals like [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] could only respect [031-003].

The battalion's culture was that of the regular army: professional, unsentimental, and deeply conservative in its social assumptions. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s account registers both his admiration for this culture — its competence, its stoicism — and his growing distance from its political and class assumptions. The First Battalion thus serves as the memoir's baseline for "normal" military identity, against which [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s subsequent evolution can be measured.

## In the Memoir

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s service with the First Battalion occupies the memoir's early chapters and establishes the experiential foundation for everything that follows. It is in the First Battalion that he first encounters [[trench-warfare|trench warfare]], first experiences bombardment, first sees men killed — the initiations that transform the newly arrived officer into a combat veteran [020-001].

The memoir's retrospective references to the First Battalion are consistently affectionate. "Old times with the First Battalion" becomes a refrain — a shorthand for the camaraderie of shared initiation, the period before the Somme's industrialised slaughter made such nostalgia problematic [023-003]. "Old Joe" [[dottrell|Dottrell]] (who later transfers to the [[second-battalion|Second Battalion]]) and other figures from the First Battalion period persist in the memoir's memory as touchstones of authenticity.

When [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] transfers to the [[second-battalion|Second Battalion]] — and later, when he is posted away from both battalions — the First Battalion continues to exert a gravitational pull. One officer "appeared to regard me as the man who did stunts with the First Battalion" — a reputation that follows [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] and marks him as belonging to that earlier, perhaps more innocent phase of the war [031-004].

## War Record

The First Battalion's war service, as reflected in the memoir and historical record, included the early [[trench-warfare|trench warfare]] of 1915, the Battle of [[battle-of-loos|Loos]] (September 1915), and the holding operations of 1916 before [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s transfer to the [[second-battalion|Second Battalion]]. The battalion would have continued to serve through the Somme, Arras, and the campaigns of 1918, but the memoir's coverage is limited to the period of [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s direct experience.

## Sassoon's Perspective

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s treatment of the First Battalion carries a particular emotional charge. Unlike the [[second-battalion|Second Battalion]] — where he served longer and perhaps more deeply — the First Battalion is associated with first experiences: the initial shock of the trenches, the discovery of his own capacity for courage, the formation of friendships under the peculiar pressure of shared danger.

The memoir's nostalgia for the First Battalion is acknowledged with characteristic irony. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] notes that the battalion's "old hands" had "already become legendary" — the process of myth-making already underway even as the war continued. The "good old First Battalion" is, in part, a construction of memory — but it is a construction that the memoir treats with respect, as one of the necessary fictions by which soldiers made their experience bearable.

## Historical Counterpart

The First Battalion, Flintshire Fusiliers represents the 1st Battalion, [[royal-welch-fusiliers|Royal Welch Fusiliers]]. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] served briefly with the 1/RWF before being transferred to the [[second-battalion|2nd Battalion]], and his portrait of the "First Battalion" as the unit of his initiation accords with the biographical record of his early service in [[france|France]].
