**Mules** appear in Sassoon's memoir as part of the vast logistical infrastructure that sustained the British Expeditionary Force — the pack animals that hauled ammunition, supplies, and equipment alongside the more prestigious horses of the cavalry and transport sections. They are mentioned only in passing, but their presence registers the animal labor without which the industrialized war could not have functioned.

## In the Memoir

### The Horsehood of England

Sassoon's most sustained reflection on military animals comes during an inspection parade at the Army School, where "hundreds of light and heavy draft horses were drawn up along a road for an inspection by the Commander-in-Chief." The narrator observes that "the horsehood of England was there with every buckle of its harness brightened" before adding, with characteristic precision: "There weren't many mules among them, for mules were mostly with the Artillery, and this was a slap-up Army Service Corps parade" [005-004]. The mule's absence from the parade — its relegation to the less glamorous work of the artillery — is a small index of the military hierarchy that extended even to animals. Horses paraded; mules worked.

### The Camp at the Front

Later in the memoir, mules appear in the context of the bustling rear-area camps that sustained the front: "There was a makeshift effect of men coming and going, loading and unloading limbers and wagons, carrying fodder, shouting at horses and mules, attending to fires, and causing a smell of cooking" [042-005]. The mules are part of the background noise of military life — present, essential, and entirely unremarked except in passing. The "smell of cooking" that mingles with the shouts at horses and mules creates a sensory picture of the rear area as a place of animal and human labor intertwined.

A third mention occurs during a rear-area scene at the edge of a wood, where "a mule contributed a short solo" [031-029] to the general noise of the camp. The detail is almost comic — the mule's bray cutting through the military din — but it serves Sassoon's characteristic purpose: registering the presence of creatures and people who would otherwise vanish from the historical record. The mule's "short solo" is one of the memoir's many small acts of preservation, a sound that survives because someone noticed it and wrote it down.

Sassoon also records mules in the transport lines at the training camp: "There weren't many mules among them, for mules were mostly with the Artillery" [003-004], an early observation that establishes the mule's working-class status within the animal hierarchy of the Army. Even at the training depot, before any shots have been fired, the class distinctions are already in place.

## The Mule in Military Service

Mules were preferred over horses for certain types of military transport because of their superior endurance, sure-footedness, and resistance to disease. They carried ammunition to the guns, hauled supply wagons over ground that horses could not navigate, and required less fodder and care than their more glamorous equine counterparts. A mule train could carry 200 pounds of shells over broken terrain that would lame a horse in an hour. In the rigid hierarchy of military animals — cavalry charger, officer's mount, draft horse, pack mule — the mule occupied the lowest rung, but its contribution to the war effort was indispensable. Sassoon's glancing references to mules are consistent with his larger project: registering the presence of the unglamorous, the essential, and the overlooked.
