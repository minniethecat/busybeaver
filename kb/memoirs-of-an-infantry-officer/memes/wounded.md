The wounded man is a recurring figure in [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s memoir — crawling back from failed raids, crouching in shell-holes, waiting patiently for stretcher-bearers who may not come. The wounded are everywhere: in the casualty lists read aloud in the mess, in the columns of "walking wounded" straggling along the roads from the Somme, in the hospital wards behind the lines where the boundary between combatant and invalid dissolves. Their presence shapes the memoir's emotional register as surely as the dead.

## Definition and Context

Being "wounded" in the First World War carried a particular set of meanings within the British Army. A wound was the only honorable exit from the front — the "Blighty wound" that was serious enough to warrant evacuation to England but not so serious as to maim or kill. As Sassoon observes, "delicate discrimination about being killed or wounded was, as I have indicated, still prevalent" — the distinction between different grades of injury was a matter of intense practical interest to every soldier [009-003].

The wounded existed in a liminal state between the living and the dead, the fighting and the invalid. Their presence on the battlefield complicated the clean categories of military accounting. A wounded man was no longer effective as a soldier but still required resources — stretcher-bearers to carry him, medical officers to treat him, transport to evacuate him. The wound was simultaneously a military statistic, a medical emergency, and a human catastrophe — and Sassoon's memoir registers all three dimensions without conflating them.

## The Grey-Haired Lance-Corporal

The memoir's most vivid image of the wounded comes in the aftermath of the trench raid, when Sassoon encounters "a grey-haired lance-corporal, who had one of his feet almost blown off" [007-008]. The narrator half-carries him in and sets him on the fire-step, where the man says simply "Thank God Almighty" — a response so unadorned that Sassoon records it without commentary. The grey-haired man, older than the typical private soldier, embodies the indiscriminate reach of wounds: no amount of experience or maturity offers protection. Nearby, "another man was crouching beside him, wounded in one arm and patiently waiting for help" [007-009]. The word "patiently" is characteristic — Sassoon's wounded men endure rather than protest, and their endurance is itself a quiet accusation.

## Casualty Arithmetic

Sassoon records wounds with the flat precision of a war diary. After the trench raid, "two killed and ten wounded was the only result" [007-012] — the casualty arithmetic rendered in a single sentence that moves from numbers to the word "only," whose irony is devastating. The raid has produced nothing except dead and wounded men. On the Somme, men lie "wounded and killed by sniping" in trenches "only a foot deep" [014-015], the shallowness of the trench and the depth of the suffering in equal measure. "The Royal Irish had had sixty casualties; we had one killed and four wounded" [014-007] — the comparison between battalions renders suffering as a kind of competitive accounting. The phrase "from a military point of view" that follows such tallies carries the memoir's characteristic irony: the men are statistics, but the statistics are also men.

## The Walking Wounded

A distinct category in the memoir's taxonomy of suffering is the "walking wounded" — men injured seriously enough to leave the line but not so seriously that they cannot move under their own power. Along the road from Fricourt to Méaulte, "small parties of 'walking wounded' straggled thankfully toward the Casualty Clearing Station" [016-012]. The word "thankfully" is doing complex work: the men are grateful to be alive, grateful to be leaving the line, but their gratitude is shadowed by the knowledge that worse wounds — or death — might have been their lot. The walking wounded occupy an intermediate zone in the memoir's moral geography, neither fully combatant nor fully victim, their visible injuries a reproach to the invisible system that produced them.

## The Wound as Transformation

A wound transforms the soldier not only physically but socially. When Sassoon himself is wounded, his "equipment is cut from his body by a stretcher-bearer who ties up his throat wound" — the kit that defined him as a combatant is removed, and with it the identity of the fighting man [024-005]. The wounded soldier enters a different world: the hospital, the convalescent home, the medical board. "Durley was badly wounded, in neck and chest, I think" [020-013] — the hesitation in "I think" registers how even serious wounds become, for those still at the front, half-remembered fragments of news. The wounded man disappears from the narrative, and his disappearance is itself part of the wound's meaning: to be wounded is to exit the story.

## Sassoon's Perspective

Sassoon's treatment of the wounded is consistent with his larger method: he refuses to sensationalize, and by refusing, makes the reality more terrible. There are no operatic death scenes in the memoir, no lingering descriptions of gore. Instead, there is the grey-haired man "patiently waiting" with a wounded arm, the men crawling back from craters, the flat numbers of the casualty lists. This restraint is itself a moral position. The wounded are not props in an anti-war argument; they are men whose suffering demands a response that rhetoric cannot provide. Sassoon's understatement is a form of respect — an acknowledgment that the reality of wounds exceeds any language available to describe them.

The memoir's ultimate treatment of wounds comes not in description but in structure: the narrator himself is wounded, and his wound removes him from the front, placing him in the liminal world of hospitals and convalescent homes where the protest that will define the memoir's final movement begins to take shape. The physical wound becomes, in the memoir's architecture, the condition of possibility for moral witness.
