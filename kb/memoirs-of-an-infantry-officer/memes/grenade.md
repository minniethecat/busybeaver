# Hand Grenade

The **hand grenade** — particularly the **Mills bomb** — was a defining weapon of trench warfare, essential for raiding, bombing parties, and close-quarter fighting in the confined spaces of the Western Front. Unlike the anonymous killing of the artillery shell, the grenade was intimate: carried in the pocket, primed with a pin, and thrown by hand at an enemy you could sometimes see.

## The Mills Bomb

The Mills bomb was the standard British fragmentation grenade. Sassoon encounters it at every stage of his service. At the Army Bombing School, officers attend "a lecture about the tactical employment of the Mills Bomb" [003-004] — the weapon reduced to classroom theory, complete with "explosions and smoke-clouds" for the students' edification.

During raid preparations, the men become walking arsenals: they "were brandishing knobkerries, stuffing Mills bombs into their pockets and hatchets into their belts" [007-003]. The detail is precise — bombs stuffed into pockets, not hung from webbing — evoking the improvisational, almost piratical quality of the raiding party. In the attack at the Sunken Road, men carry "two Mills bombs" as standard assault equipment [012-007], the grenade as routine as a water bottle.

## In Trench Raids

Grenades were the primary weapon of trench raiding. The raid at Kiel Trench turns on bombing: when O'Brien's party discovers they "can't get through the second belt of wire," the order is to "throw a bomb and retire" [007-007]. The grenade becomes the punctuation mark of a failed operation — thrown not to kill but to signal retreat.

The aftermath is visual chaos: "a bomb burst in the water of the left-hand crater, sending up a phosphorescent spume" [007-008], followed by "bombing and rifle fire" [007-009] as the raiders scramble back through the darkness. The G.H.Q. communiqué later sanitises the event into "a spirited bombing fight" [007-015] — a phrase that captures the gap between official language and the reality Sassoon experienced.

## Bombing Parties

Beyond raids, bombing parties were integral to trench fighting. At Quadrangle Trench, Sassoon is sent forward with bombers to establish a strong-post. He crawls along the sap carrying "a bag of bombs" while "the Germans were bombing" [014-015] from their positions.

The physical act of throwing a grenade is rendered with visceral precision: Sassoon pulls "the safety-pin out of a Mills bomb" with his right hand, then another with his left, before throwing "my two bombs" [014-016] into the German position. The two-handed bombing — a technique taught at the School — becomes, under fire, a desperate act of self-preservation.

## The Weapon of Close Combat

Sassoon's pre-raid meditation captures the grenade's ambiguous status as both weapon and burden. "Revolver shooting wasn't so bad, and as for bombs, you just chucked them and hoped for the best" [006-003]. The studied casualness — "chucked them" — belies the terror of handling an object designed to explode in your hand.

Walking through a rear area, Sassoon reflects on the randomness of trench death: "how silly it would be if I got laid out by a stray bullet, or a rifle-grenade, or one of those clumsy 'canisters' that came over in the evening dusk with a little trail of sparks behind them." The rifle-grenade, lobbed from a distant trench, is as impersonal as the artillery shell — yet it shares the grenade's identity as a hand-thrown weapon, an instrument of killing miniature enough to travel in a pocket.
