# Far from the Madding Crowd

Thomas Hardy's fourth published novel and his first major success — the story of Bathsheba Everdene, Gabriel Oak, and Sergeant Troy set in the fictional Wessex [[landscape|countryside]] — which [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] carried with him to [[france|the Western Front]] and which functions in the memoir as both a literal object (a book in a dugout) and a symbolic counterweight to the war's destruction of the English landscape.

## About the Work

*Far from the Madding Crowd* was published in 1874, appearing first as a serial in the *Cornhill Magazine* before its publication in two volumes. The novel marked Hardy's transition from the more melodramatic mode of his earlier fiction to the tragic realism that would characterise his major works. Its Wessex setting — the semi-fictionalised rural world of Dorset and its neighbouring counties — established the imaginative geography that Hardy would develop through *The Return of the Native*, *Tess of the d'Urbervilles*, and *Jude the Obscure*.

The novel's title, taken from Thomas Gray's "Elegy Written in a Country Churchyard," carries an irony that would not have been lost on [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] in the trenches. Gray's line — "Far from the madding crowd's ignoble strife" — imagines rural isolation as escape from urban conflict. For [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]], crouched in a dugout opposite [[fricourt|Fricourt]], Hardy's Wessex was doubly removed: a fictional rural [[england|England]] at two removes from the industrialised slaughter of the Somme.

Hardy's importance to [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] extended beyond this single novel. Hardy was the elder statesman of English letters whom [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] admired and visited, and his poetry — particularly the war poems of *Moments of Vision* (1917) — influenced [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s own war writings. The copy of *Far from the Madding Crowd* in [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s dugout was thus freighted with personal and literary significance.

## In the Memoir

The novel appears in a single, striking passage early in the memoir. Arriving at his first billet behind the lines, [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] describes unpacking his few possessions: "I could arrange my few books. Hardy's *Far from the Madding Crowd* was one of them. Also Lamb's *Essays*" [002-007]. The passage is brief but carefully weighted — the act of arranging books is a gesture toward civilisation in an environment that systematically dismantled it.

The novel's presence recurs indirectly in a later passage where [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]], on the march, reflects that "it was some comfort to be carrying it, for Thomas Hardy's [[england|England]] was between its covers" [012-007]. Here, the novel becomes explicitly what it had been implicitly: a portable fragment of the [[england|England]] for which the war was ostensibly being fought — an [[england|England]] of hedgerows, shepherds, and harvests rather than of trenches, bombardments, and casualties.

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s choice of Hardy is significant in ways that the memoir leaves unspoken. Hardy was the great elegist of a vanishing rural [[england|England]] — the chronicler of a world being destroyed by the very modernity of which [[france|the Western Front]] was the most extreme expression. To carry Hardy into the trenches was to carry a literary witness to what was being lost.

## Thematic Significance

*Far from the Madding Crowd* functions in the memoir as a symbol of the pastoral [[england|England]] that the war was destroying — not merely the physical landscape of [[france|France]], churned into mud and craters, but the imaginative landscape of English rural life that had sustained centuries of literature before 1914.

The book's appearance early in the memoir — before the Somme, before the worst of the casualties — marks a moment of relative innocence. The narrator still believes that literature can provide adequate shelter against experience. As the memoir darkens, such consolations recede; books are mentioned less frequently, and the prose becomes increasingly absorbed in the immediate realities of the front. The trajectory of the memoir is, in part, a trajectory away from Hardy and toward a more direct confrontation with what Hardy's Wessex could not accommodate.

But the novel's presence is not merely elegiac. It also represents a form of quiet resistance — the assertion of civilised values in an environment that denied them. To read Hardy in a dugout was to insist, against all evidence, that there was a world beyond the war and that literature remained a valid response to experience. This is, in miniature, the argument of [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s memoir itself.
