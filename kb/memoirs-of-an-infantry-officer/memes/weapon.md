# Weapon

## Overview

**Weapons** in Sassoon's memoir range from the bayonet — "essentially an offensive weapon" [002-011] — to the Mills bomb, the revolver, and the artillery that dominated the battlefield. Sassoon's treatment of weapons is ambivalent: he is a decorated combat officer who has killed with his revolver, yet the memoir's deeper argument is that the weapons of industrial warfare have rendered individual courage meaningless. The weapon is both tool and symbol — of the war's brutality, of the officer's responsibility, and of the technological transformation of combat.

## The Bayonet

The bayonet is the weapon Sassoon discusses most explicitly. The memoir opens with a scene of bayonet training: "It is essentially an offensive weapon. In a bayonet assault all ranks must be imbued with the desire to kill" [002-011]. The language is the official doctrine of the training manual, and Sassoon reproduces it at length — the six thrusts, the parries, the injunction to "transfix" the enemy. The effect is deliberately unsettling: the bayonet is presented as an instrument of industrialized murder dressed in the language of sport and discipline.

Sassoon never describes using a bayonet in combat. The weapon appears in training and in theory — the memoir's treatment of it is almost anthropological, observing the gap between the training-ground fantasy of cold steel and the reality of shellfire and machine-guns. The bayonet was the weapon of the old army; the war it found itself in had made it nearly obsolete.

## The Revolver

The revolver is the officer's personal weapon, and Sassoon carries one as a matter of course. It appears in moments of violent action: "another with his revolver, and marched the remainder back to our own lines" [038-009] — the revolver as an instrument of battlefield authority, the officer using it to control prisoners.

Sassoon had owned a revolver before the war: "the only explosive weapon I owned before the war was a starting pistol" [010-008]. The admission is characteristically self-deprecating — the sporting gentleman's pistol as precursor to the combat officer's sidearm. The revolver was a class marker as much as a weapon: officers carried revolvers, not rifles.

The revolver appears in a strange moment of weapon-as-prop at the Flintshire Festiniog Railway Company dinner before the war, where Sassoon's friend David Cromlech brandishes "quite a charming little revolver" [010-007] — the weapon as social accessory, a joke in civilian hands that would become deadly earnest in war.

## The Mills Bomb

The Mills bomb — the British Army's standard hand grenade — appears as a favored weapon of the trench raid. Sassoon records a Transport man laughing about it: "'Grand weapon, the Mills!' he laughed, his face shining with enthusiasm" [038-009]. The man had just killed two Germans with one. The juxtaposition of the cheerful exclamation with the act of killing is typical of Sassoon's method: the weapon is presented without editorializing, its horror left implicit.

Sassoon himself carried Mills bombs on trench raids. Before the raid on Kiel Trench, the men carried "two Mills bombs" [012-007] among their equipment. The grenade was the weapon of close combat in the confined spaces of trenches and dugouts — more practical than the rifle, more destructive than the revolver. It represented the shift from aimed fire to area destruction that defined twentieth-century warfare.

## Weapons as Burden

Weapons were also physical burdens. The infantryman carried rifle, bayonet, ammunition, and grenades — "the creaking of weapons and equipment" [007-005] was the constant soundtrack of a unit on the move. The weight of weapons added to the exhaustion of the march and the difficulty of movement in the trenches.

Sassoon records a maxim about weapons and morale: "Never deprive a man of his weapon in a post of danger" [024-004]. The weapon was a psychological necessity as much as a practical one — a man without his rifle felt naked, even if the rifle was useless against shellfire.

## The Artillery as Invisible Weapon

The memoir's most important weapon is one that Sassoon rarely treats as a "weapon" at all: artillery. Shells caused the majority of casualties on the Western Front, and the bombardment — its sound, its effects, its terror — is a constant presence in the memoir. Yet Sassoon almost never describes artillery pieces themselves as weapons. Instead, shellfire is an environmental condition: "the blink and growl of its bombardments" [033-006], the noise of "our guns" [006-007], the landscape "plastered with shell-holes" [014-006].

This treatment is itself a statement about the nature of industrial warfare. The artillery was too vast, too impersonal, to be discussed in the same terms as a revolver or a bayonet. The shell was a weapon without a wielder — delivered from miles away by gunners who never saw their targets. It rendered the individual soldier's weapons almost irrelevant: a man with a bayonet was helpless against a high-explosive shell.

## The Weapon and the Man

Sassoon's relationship with weapons is complicated by his identity as both a combat officer and a nascent pacifist. He describes the revolver as "quite a charming little weapon" [010-007] with irony — he knows that finding a weapon charming is obscene, and he wants the reader to know that he knows. The memoir's treatment of weapons is always double-voiced: the combat officer's professional appreciation coexists with the moralist's revulsion.

The bayonet training passage at the memoir's opening is the most sustained treatment of any weapon, and it serves as a kind of thesis statement. The language of "transfix," "withdraw," "slash" — the clinical vocabulary of killing taught as drill — is presented without comment. The reader is left to absorb the dissonance between the words and their meaning, between the parade-ground practice and the reality of driving steel into a human body. Sassoon never needs to say that this is monstrous; the manual's own words do the work.
