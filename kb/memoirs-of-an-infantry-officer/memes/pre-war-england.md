**Pre-war England** exists in Sassoon's memoir as a ghost — a world of fox-hunting, cricket, country houses, and pastoral tranquility that the war has rendered irretrievable. It is the England the narrator remembers, the England he fights for, and the England that the war itself has destroyed. The memoir's backward glances toward this lost world are among its most elegiac passages, and the contrast between the pre-war pastoral and the wartime inferno is one of the narrative's deepest structural principles.

## The Pastoral Memory

Sassoon's narrator carries pre-war England within him as a private talisman. When he slips a volume of Thomas Hardy into his haversack before going up to the front, it is "a comfort to be carrying it, for Thomas Hardy's England was between its covers" [003-004]. The England of Hardy's novels — rural, ancient, rooted in landscape and custom — is the England the war is destroying, and the book in the haversack is an act of preservation as much as consolation.

The memoir's pastoral memories are specific and sensory: "I remembered the Volunteers whose torchlight march-past had made such a glowing impression on my nursery window" [043-004] — the military spectacle seen through a child's eyes, innocent of the knowledge that will later hollow it out. The torchlight parade is pre-war England in miniature: patriotic, picturesque, and entirely unaware of what war actually means.

## Fox-Hunting and Cricket

Sassoon's pre-war England is an England of sport and leisure — the world of the country gentry that produced the officer class. Fox-hunting appears in the memoir as a metonym for everything the war is not: individual, gracious, governed by codes of conduct that, however class-bound, were at least intelligible. Cricket — Sassoon's other great pre-war passion — appears only in its absence: "It would have been nice to read the first-class cricket averages for a change, and their absence was an apt epitome of the life we were condemned to" [005-005]. The cricket averages, with their ordered columns of names and numbers, represent a world of comprehensible competition, of rules that are observed, of summers that follow one another in peaceful succession.

## What Was Lost

The memoir's backward glances toward pre-war England are not merely nostalgic; they are accusatory. By preserving the memory of what existed before the war, Sassoon measures the magnitude of what was lost — not only the dead, but the world they died to preserve, a world that the war itself rendered unrecoverable. The pastoral England of Hardy and cricket and fox-hunting is not merely a memory; it is a reproach to the present, a reminder that the civilization the war claimed to be defending was being destroyed by the very means employed to defend it.
