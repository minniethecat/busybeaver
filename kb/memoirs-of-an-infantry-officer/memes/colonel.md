# Colonel

In the British Army of the First World War, the **colonel** commanded an infantry battalion — roughly a thousand men — and occupied a position of near-absolute authority within that unit. In Sassoon's *Memoirs of an Infantry Officer*, the colonel appears not as an abstract rank but as a series of vividly specific men whose personalities, for better or worse, define the character of their battalions.

## Kinjack: The Colonel as Force of Nature

The most fully realised colonel in the memoir is Colonel Kinjack, commanding officer of the First Battalion. Sassoon's first encounter sets the tone: "Perhaps Colonel Kinjack had heard that I'd been looking for trouble" [002-001]. Kinjack is a professional soldier of the old school — brusque, competent, impatient with inefficiency, and possessed of a physical courage that makes him a genuinely commanding presence.

Sassoon describes blundering "into Kinjack's Headquarters at Maple Redoubt" [007-004] to report the raiders' presence. The colonel's response is immediate and absolute: "Certainly not," said the Colonel, "your job is to stop in our trench and count the men as they come back." He spoke with emphasis and "he was not a man whose orders could be questioned." Even the narrative voice, normally so assured, becomes self-deprecating in Kinjack's presence.

Yet Kinjack is no mere martinet. He is "never afraid of giving the brass-hats a bit of straight talk" — the colloquial "brass-hats" contrasting with the formal respect owed to senior officers, and revealing a colonel willing to shield his men from unrealistic Brigade demands. The raid that Sassoon volunteers for is one Kinjack "wasn't keen about" and had "talked pretty straight to the Brigade Major" about. The colonel's authority, it emerges, can also be exercised upward, as a buffer between his battalion and the costly enthusiasms of higher command.

## The Colonel's Conference

The colonel's authority is exercised most formally at the "Colonel's final conference of company commanders" [012-006] before major operations. These conferences translate the abstractions of Brigade orders into concrete tasks for individual companies and platoons.

Barton, one of Sassoon's company commanders, "came back from the Colonel's final conference" [012-006] bearing the detailed plans that will determine who lives and who dies. The colonel's decisions at these conferences — which company attacks where, in what strength, at what time — are exercises in applied mortality. The memoir's account of the preparations for the Somme offensive shows the colonel as the crucial hinge between higher command and the men who will actually go over the top.

## The Human Cost of Command

Sassoon does not flinch from the toll that command exacts on the man who holds it. "The overworked Adjutant eyed me sadly from a corner of an ammunition-box table covered with a grey blanket, and the Colonel's face caused me to feel like a boy who had stayed out late" [014-021]. The paternal register is deliberate — the colonel is a father-figure whose disappointment wounds, and whose approval is craved.

Yet the memoir also records the fate of colonels in battle with brutal concision: "Their Colonel went across and was killed" [013-027]. The rank confers no immunity from the killing. In the chaos of the Somme, colonels die alongside their men, often leading from the front in the traditions of the old Army that the war itself is destroying.

Kinjack's own face betrays the cost. After a harrowing night operation, Sassoon encounters a version of the colonel he has never seen: "His blonde face was haggard; the last few hours had been no fun for him either. This was a Kinjack I'd never met before, and it was the first time I had ever shared any human equality with him. He spoke kindly to me." The colonel who had once been an intimidating force of nature is, in the aftermath of shared danger, a tired man speaking gently to a junior officer. The moment of equality passes quickly, but it reveals the humanity beneath the rank.

## The Colonel's Judgement

The colonel's authority extends beyond the battlefield into judgements of character and competence. "If an officer crumpled up Kinjack sent him home as useless, with a confidential report. Several such officers were usually drifting about at the Depot." The phrase "crumpled up" captures the physical metaphor for psychological collapse, and Kinjack's response — swift administrative removal — is both pragmatic and pitiless. A battalion at the front cannot afford officers who break under the strain.

This harshness is balanced by loyalty. Sassoon returns from leave bearing "Kinjack's salmon" — a peace offering, a gift, an acknowledgment of the colonel's dominion made material in a carefully transported fish. The salmon "spelt safety at Battalion Headquarters," a ritual of appeasement in the small and intensely personal world of the battalion.

## The Colonel in Sassoon's Moral Universe

Sassoon's attitude toward colonels is characteristically ambivalent. He respects professional competence and physical courage; he despises the unthinking application of those qualities in the service of futile operations. "Brigade have got the idea of a raid hot and strong," another officer tells him, "and they've nothing to lose by it one way or the other, except a few of our men" [006-002]. The colonel, caught between Brigade's demands and his own knowledge of the cost, occupies an unenviable position — an agent of authority who must sometimes give orders he knows to be wasteful.

He "was heard to remark" [016-008] on the eccentricities of his officers — a vignette that humanises the colonel as a manager of personalities as much as a commander of troops. The battalion is, in the end, a small and intensely personal world, and the colonel sits at its uneasy centre: answerable to the Brigade above him, responsible for the men below, and possessed of no more immunity from the shells than the newest private.
