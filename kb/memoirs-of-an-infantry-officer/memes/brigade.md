# Brigade

## Overview

The **brigade** was the intermediate formation between battalion and division, typically comprising three to four infantry battalions. In Sassoon's memoir, the brigade appears primarily as a remote planning authority — the source of orders, raids, and the bureaucratic machinery that officers at the front resented [009-002].

## Brigade as Command Layer

Brigade Headquarters (H.Q.) was the planning center where trench-maps were studied and raids conceived. Sassoon describes "the big bugs back at Brigade and Divisional H.Q. studying trench-maps with corrugated brows" [012-010], capturing the distance between planners at the rear and the men who executed their orders.

The Brigade Major was a key liaison figure. Sassoon recalls how an officer "talked pretty straight to the Brigade Major; he was never afraid of speaking his mind" [006-003], suggesting the tension between front-line experience and staff decisions.

The brigade command structure reflected the British Army's effort to coordinate multiple battalions across a sector. A brigadier-general commanded the brigade, with a staff that included the Brigade Major (operations), a staff captain (administration), and signalling and intelligence officers. Sassoon rarely mentions these figures by name, treating "Brigade" as an impersonal, almost bureaucratic force — less a group of individuals than a system of demands and orders [009-002].

## The Raid Economy

Brigade was the primary driver of trench raids — small-scale night attacks that Sassoon came to see as wasteful and performative. "Brigade have got the idea of a raid held as a kind of trump card" [006-002], he writes, with evident frustration at raids designed to impress higher command rather than achieve meaningful objectives.

The administrative demands of Brigade created what Sassoon saw as make-work: "all that bosh which made Brigade think they were busy" [009-002]. This critique of the gap between front-line reality and rear-area planning is a central theme of the memoir.

Sassoon's critique of the raid economy has a specific structural target: the way Brigade used raids as metrics to demonstrate aggressiveness to Division, which in turn reported them to Corps and Army. Each raid was a reportable event, a line in a war diary that proved the sector was "active." The human cost — the dead and wounded bombers, the reprisal shelling — was borne entirely by the men at the front, not by the planners who ordered it [006-002].

## Brigade in Major Operations

When the battalion moved as part of larger offensives, brigade-level coordination became visible. Sassoon records the assembly of "the four units of our Brigade" on a hillside before going into action [014-001]. During the Somme offensive, he notes that "two Brigades of our Division were now going in" [013-007], registering the scale of the operation in brigade-level terms.

The brigade's role in a major attack involved coordinating the assault waves of multiple battalions, managing reserves, and relaying intelligence between Division and the front. When things went wrong — as they frequently did — the brigade was often invisible to the men in the forward trenches, its communication lines cut by shellfire, its orders arriving too late or not at all [013-007].

## Sassoon's View of the Brass

Sassoon's attitude toward Brigade reflects his broader critique of the British Army's command culture. The "brass hats" — a soldier's term for high-ranking officers identified by the gold braid on their caps — are a recurring object of his irony. Brigade is the nearest embodiment of this remote authority, close enough to issue direct orders yet far enough from the line to be insulated from their consequences. In Sassoon's rendering, Brigade is not malevolent but disconnected — a machine that produces raids and paperwork with no real grasp of the front-line experience it governs [012-010].
