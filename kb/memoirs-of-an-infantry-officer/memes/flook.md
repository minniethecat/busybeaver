**Flook** is George Sherston's servant (batman) in Sassoon's memoir — a figure of steadfast domestic loyalty whose presence anchors the narrative's rare moments of comfort and whose red-faced, grinning solidity provides a counterweight to the memoir's more harrowing passages. In a narrative dominated by officers, Flook is one of the few enlisted men granted a name and a recognizable personality.

## In the Memoir

### The Faithful Servant

Flook first appears in the memoir as the officer's servant — the soldier assigned to attend to Sherston's personal needs: polishing equipment, carrying luggage, bringing hot water for shaving. His role is domestic within the military hierarchy, and Sassoon renders him with affection and respect. "Flook and I were very good friends," Sherston notes, "and his vigilance for my personal comfort was such that I could more easily imagine him using his rifle in defence of my valise than against the Germans" [025-002]. The observation is characteristic of Sassoon's ironic sensibility — Flook's loyalty is to his officer personally, not to the abstract cause for which they are supposedly fighting.

### Departure for the Army School

When Sherston is sent to the Fourth Army School at Flixécourt, Flook accompanies him. Barton sees them off: "'Lucky Kangaroo — to be hopping away for a holiday!' he exclaimed, as I climbed into the elderly bus. My servant Flook hoisted up my bulging valise, wiped his red face with his sleeve, and followed me to the roof" [005-009]. Flook's physical presence — the red face, the heavy valise, the grin — is rendered with a novelist's attention to the telling detail.

At the Army School, Flook's competence transforms the spartan billet into something approaching comfort: "Flook entered with my Sam Brown belt and a jug of hot water. He remarked that we'd come to the right place, for once, and regretted that we weren't there for the duration" [010-001]. Flook's wry commentary — the soldier's knowing assessment of relative comfort — gives him a voice and a perspective that the memoir rarely grants to enlisted men.

### Return to the Battalion

When Sherston returns to the battalion after his course, it is Flook who carries his valise: "I felt almost glad to be back as I walked up to our old Company Mess with Flook carrying my valise on his back" [025-005]. The image of Flook burdened with Sherston's luggage is a small emblem of the class structure that organizes military life — the officer walks free while the servant carries the load. Sassoon records it without comment, but the detail is there for the reader who notices.

## Character and Significance

Flook's significance in the memoir exceeds his modest narrative role. He represents the human infrastructure of the officer's life — the men who polished boots, brought meals, and maintained the small dignities of existence that made the war's degradations endurable. His loyalty to Sherston is personal rather than institutional, a relationship between two men rather than between ranks. In a memoir that is otherwise dominated by the officer's perspective, Flook registers the presence of the enlisted soldier as an individual rather than a type.
