# Revolver

## Overview

The **revolver** was the standard sidearm of British infantry officers in the First World War, and it appears in Sassoon's memoir as both a practical tool and a symbol of the officer's role. Unlike the rifle, which belonged to the collective identity of the rank and file, the revolver was personal — an extension of individual authority, carried on the body, drawn in moments of crisis.

## The Officer's Sidearm

The revolver was part of the officer's daily equipment, fetched as routinely as one might pick up a hat. Sassoon records the matter-of-fact gesture: "On my way to the dugout, to fetch my revolver and attend the twilight ceremony of stand-to and rifle inspection" [009-006]. The revolver and the inspection ritual were paired — the weapon was not for immediate use but for readiness, a permanent state of preparedness that defined trench existence.

Before a battalion move, Barton emerged festooned with equipment including his "revolver" alongside "field-glasses, gas-mask, map-case, and other oddments" [012-002]. The revolver was just one item among many, its lethal purpose normalized by bureaucratic routine.

## The Weapon as Plaything

Sassoon treated his revolver with a mixture of practicality and indifference. Of his Colt revolver, he admitted: "I was weary of my Colt revolver, with which I knew I couldn't hit anything, although I had blazed it off a few times in the dark when I was pretending to be important in No Man's Land" [010-007]. The phrase "pretending to be important" is self-deprecating — the revolver, fired blindly into darkness, becomes almost a theatrical prop, a way of performing the role of officer rather than an effective weapon.

When offered a German automatic pistol as a replacement, he described it as "only a plaything" [010-007] — a diminutive that strips the weapon of its menace while acknowledging its appeal as an object.

## Revolver in Combat

In the accounts of others, the revolver was used to deadly effect. A fellow officer described killing Germans in a trench raid: "He killed two with a Mills bomb... wounded another with his revolver, and marched the remainder back to our own lines" [038-009]. The revolver here is efficient and decisive — the weapon of a man who knows what he is doing, in contrast to Sassoon's own self-deprecating accounts.

## Revolver Practice

At the Army School, revolver shooting was part of the curriculum. "Revolver shooting wasn't so bad," Sassoon noted, "and as for bombs, you just chucked them and hoped for the best" [005-008]. The laconic "wasn't so bad" suggests a skill that was neither especially demanding nor especially valued — a secondary competence for an officer whose primary weapons were leadership and endurance.

On the home front, an elderly civilian was "known to have practised revolver-shooting in his garden, addressing insults to individual tree trunks and thus ventilating his opinion of Germany as a whole" [021-004] — the revolver becoming a channel for patriotic aggression far from the front.
