# Psychology of War

Sassoon's memoir is as much an account of a mind under siege as a body in danger.
The psychological arc of *Memoirs of an Infantry Officer* — from youthful patriotism
through trauma and disillusionment to open protest — is one of the most fully rendered
interior journeys in war literature. The pages below gather entries concerned with
the mental and emotional experience of combat: the clinical (shell-shock, neurasthenia),
the affective (fear, anger, grief), and the political (conscience, protest, the morality
of killing).

## Notes

The `psychology` tag covers entries dealing with psychological states, mental health,
and the interior experience of war (shell-shock, fear, courage, memory, grief).
The `protest` tag identifies entries concerned with Sassoon's anti-war stance,
his "Soldier's Declaration," and the moral questioning that defines the memoir's
second half. Pages about W.H.R. Rivers and the Craiglockhart episode carry both tags.
