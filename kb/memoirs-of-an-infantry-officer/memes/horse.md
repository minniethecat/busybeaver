# Horse

## Overview

**Horses** in Sassoon's memoir are the ubiquitous animals of the First World War — draft horses pulling limbers and wagons, officers' chargers, Indian cavalry mounts, and the picketed animals of the Transport Lines. Sassoon's memoir is set at the moment when horse-drawn warfare was giving way to mechanization, and the horse appears throughout as both a practical fact of military logistics and a symbol of a vanishing world. The horse is treated with affection, pity, and sometimes dark comedy.

## Equine Abundance and Military Scale

The sheer number of horses in the war zone impressed Sassoon. At the infantry training school, "hundreds of light and heavy draft horses were drawn up along a road for an inspection by the Commander-in-Chief" [003-004]. The horses, "attached to their appropriate vehicles and shining in their summer coats, looked a picture of sleekness and strength" [003-004]. The display was a reminder that the army was, in its logistics, still a horse-drawn institution.

Even in the rear areas, the horse was the primary visual signature of military activity. "The War was abundantly visible in supply-convoys, artillery horse-lines, in the dirty white tents of a Red Cross camp" [002-004] — the horse-lines were as much a marker of the war's presence as the tents and the troops.

## Horses in the Transport Lines

The Transport Lines were, above all, a place of horses. Sassoon's first view is almost pastoral: "I stared at the Transport lines below us — a few dirty white bell-tents and the limbers and wagons and picketed horses. I could see the horses' tails switching and smell them" [006-003]. The horses are the life of the rear area, their physical presence a reminder of agricultural rather than industrial labor.

The Transport men's relationship with their animals was intimate and protective. Joe Dottrell, the Transport Officer, protected his men and horses with paternal pragmatism — sending a volunteer away on a transport course rather than letting him join a trench raid [006-002]. The horse-lines were a small society built around animal care, set apart from the combat infantry. Sassoon's own role as Transport Officer meant that horses were his professional concern — "I thought you were only looking after the horses" [010-002], his aunt protested when he mentioned his Military Cross recommendation.

## Horses in the Transport Economy

The Transport Lines at Morlancourt were a scene of constant equine activity: "a makeshift effect of men coming and going, loading and unloading limbers and wagons, carrying fodder, shouting at horses and mules, attending to fires, and causing a smell of cooking" [016-012]. The horses were at the center of the supply chain — the limbers bringing rations and ammunition forward, the general service wagons carrying fodder and equipment.

Horses dictated the pace of movement. When Sassoon and Barton went to Amiens on leave, "the horses none too sound" [015-005] limited their travel — they left the horses behind and motored the last seven miles. The phrase is a quiet acknowledgment that horse-transport was as much limitation as capability.

## The Indian Cavalry

Sassoon encounters the Indian cavalry in a vivid passage: "Soon after, I was strolling along by the river. I passed the horse-lines of the Indian cavalry; the horses were being watered — black-faced men in turbans were astride their backs as they rode them into the stream" [016-014]. The scene is one of the memoir's moments of aesthetic arrest — the Indian cavalrymen and their horses as a fragment of color and motion in the drab landscape of the Somme rear area.

The cavalry represented a form of warfare already obsolete on the Western Front, but their presence still carried romance. Sassoon's description is appreciative and slightly exoticizing — the turbaned men on their horses are a picture from another century. "The worn landscape looked parched and shabby; only the poppies made harsh spots of red, matching the head caps of the Indian cavalry who were camped near by" [016-012] — the visual echo between the red poppies and the red turbans is Sassoon's painterly eye at work.

## An Officer and His Horse

Sassoon's own relationship with horses was that of an officer expected to ride but never quite at ease in the saddle. His pre-war equestrian identity was that of the hunting gentleman: "I decided to write to old Colonel Hesmon" [020-003] to borrow a horse. The hunting scene on leave — "out with the Ringwell Hounds on Colonel Hesmon's horses" [021-004] — is a study in class irony, officers on leave pursuing foxes while the trenches waited. The phrase "alone with the creak of the saddle" [021-006] catches the sensory solitude of a rider in winter landscape.

In France, Sassoon rides with less relish. He acquires "a horse which looked scarcely up to his work" [031-003] — the officer's mount as an ambiguous privilege. When Colonel E. asks "Are you stuck to that bloody horse?" [031-024], the comedy of the dismounted officer underscores Sassoon's alienation from the equestrian culture of his class.

The horse was also a marker of rank and status. Staff officers wore "well-cut riding boots" [012-005] and were "evidently in a hurry to rejoin his horse" — the horse as a class signifier, the mounted officer set apart from the marching infantryman.

## The Horse and Pre-War Nostalgia

Horses were a thread connecting the war to Sassoon's pre-war life in rural England. The hunting field, the stables at home, the familiar objects of equestrian life — "In Dixon's little harness-room, saddles were getting mouldy and there were rust-spots on the bits and stirrup-irons" [020-003] — represent a world the war has put on hold. "A tin of Harvey's Hoof Ointment had obviously been there since 1914" [020-003]: the unopened tin is a miniature memento mori for the pre-war equestrian world.

The old mowing-machine pony that "grazed in shaggy solitude" [020-003] is a pastoral ghost — the horse reduced from working animal to companion of nettles and apple-trees, belonging to an England that Sassoon feared was disappearing as surely as the men at the front.

## Dead and Suffering Horses

The horse in battle appears most often as a corpse. After the Somme, Sassoon notes "some dead horses" [014-004] among the debris of the battlefield — the animal's body as part of the landscape of ruin, no different from the broken wagons and scattered equipment. The dead horse is a silent witness, its presence more pitiable than the military wreckage because the horse had no choice in being there.

The shriek of a frightened horse breaks the silence in one of the memoir's most arresting images: "I heard a horse neigh, shrill and scared and long" [016-017]. The sound is described without commentary — the horse's terror is presented as a fact, its meaning left for the reader to absorb. The neigh comes in "an interval of silence," a single animal cry against the background of war.

The exhausted horse appears in a grotesque image: "a shaggy farm horse was sitting in the corner of a field" [020-003] — an animal so spent it assumes a human posture. This was not a war horse but a farm horse in England, yet the image resonates across the memoir's equine representations: the horse reduced by labor, its dignity compromised.
