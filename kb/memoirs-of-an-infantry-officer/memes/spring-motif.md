# Spring (Motif)

The memoir's opening seasonal frame — "Spring arrived late in 1916" — which [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] develops into a sustained motif that contrasts the natural cycle of renewal with the war's systematic destruction of young life, establishing the elegiac register that governs the entire narrative.

## Definition & Context

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s memoir opens with a seasonal declaration: "I have said that Spring arrived late in 1916, and that up in the trenches opposite [[mametz|Mametz]] it seemed as though [[winter|Winter]] would last forever" [002-001]. The sentence establishes the memoir's temporal frame — Part I is structured around the arrival and passing of spring 1916 — and its governing irony: the [[landscape|natural world]] renews itself while the human world slaughters itself [002-001].

The spring motif draws on a literary tradition stretching from Chaucer to the Georgian poets, in which spring signifies rebirth, love, and hope. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s innovation is to preserve the beauty of the traditional spring while denying its symbolic promise — the fruit trees blossom, but the men still die [002-014][002-004].

## In the Memoir

The spring motif is most prominent in the memoir's opening chapters (Ch. 1–5), where [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] is still in training behind the lines. The late-arriving spring of 1916 becomes a kind of reprieve — "everyone seemed to be off duty; spring had arrived and the fruit trees were in blossom" [002-004]. The [[fourth-army-school|Army School]] at Flixécourt offers an almost bucolic interlude: clean streets, evening walks, "the perfect performance of a nightingale" [002-008].

But the spring is shadowed by what it postpones. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s return from the [[fourth-army-school|Army School]] is described as a return to the real: "I looked, and there was Morlancourt in the hollow. On the whole I considered myself lucky to be returning to a place where I knew my way about" [005-001]. Spring has passed; the fighting season has begun.

The motif reappears in reverse when [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]], wounded and in hospital, contemplates the English spring from the other side of the Channel. The "prophetic foreign spring" of 1916 has become the irretrievable past [002-014].

## Sassoon's Perspective

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] uses the spring motif with full awareness of its literary freight. The Georgian poet in him wants to celebrate the beauty of the [[landscape|natural world]]; the infantry officer in him knows that spring means a new offensive, not renewal. The tension between these two perspectives — the poet's eye and the soldier's knowledge — is one of the memoir's deepest formal resources.

The motif also serves a structural function: by framing the memoir's opening with a specific season, [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] gives the narrative the shape of elegy — a lament not just for the dead but for a world that was beautiful and is now gone.

## Historical Reality

The spring of 1916 was indeed a period of preparation for the [[the-somme-offensive|Somme Offensive]], which was originally scheduled for June but postponed to July due to bad weather and the French demands at Verdun. For the men in the trenches, spring meant the return of campaigning weather, and [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s account aligns with other memoirs that register the seasonal cycle as one of the war's few remaining connections to normal life.
