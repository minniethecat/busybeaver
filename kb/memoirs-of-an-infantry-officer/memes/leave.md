# Leave

The military furlough system that periodically removed soldiers from the trenches and deposited them, for a week or ten days, into the civilian world of wartime [[england|Britain]] — creating a psychological dislocation that [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] explores as one of the memoir's central tensions.

## Definition & Context

In the British Army of World War I, officers and men were entitled to periodic leave — typically 7–10 days every 3–6 months on active service. The leave journey itself was part of the ritual: a train from the railhead to a Channel port, a Channel crossing, then another train to London or [[england|home]]. The return journey reversed this, with the leave-train taking men "the wrong way" back toward the front [010-008][010-005].

Leave was understood officially as essential for morale, but [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s memoir reveals it as a source of psychological damage in its own right — the contrast between [[england|home]] comfort and trench deprivation made the return to the front almost unbearable [009-001].

## In the Memoir

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s first leave comes in Chapter 9–10, where the expectation of leave becomes a narrative countdown. "He was due for leave next week and his mind was already packed with plans" — the fantasy of [[england|home]] functions as both comfort and distraction from the immediate dangers of the trenches [006-006]. When [[barton|Barton]] returns from leave "unable to disguise his depression," the pattern is established: leave doesn't refresh, it wounds [009-001].

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s own leave in Chapter 10 is the memoir's most sustained treatment of the concept. Officers going on leave have been "cautioned to say nothing about the [[somme-battle|Somme Battle]]" — an official injunction to silence that mirrors the psychological impossibility of communicating trench experience to civilians [010-001]. He describes the two leave trains as a spectacle, the people-watching of soldiers in transit, and his own decision to overstay his leave by twenty-four hours [010-005][010-007].

The "wrong-way leave train" becomes a recurring motif: each return journey to the front is a small death, a surrender to inevitability. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] captures this with characteristic understatement: "I wasn't going to wreck my leave with facing facts" [010-003].

## Sassoon's Perspective

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s treatment of leave is characteristically Janus-faced: he describes the pleasures of [[england|home]] (the "first-class cricket averages," the civilized conversation) with genuine affection, but undercuts every [[england|home]]-front scene with the knowledge of what awaits on return [010-006].

The concept of leave becomes a vehicle for the memoir's central critique: the impossibility of communicating war experience to those who have not shared it. The injunction to "say nothing about the [[somme-battle|Somme Battle]]" is, in [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s telling, both an official policy and a deeper social truth — [[england|England]] didn't want to know, and soldiers learned not to tell [010-001].

The overstayed leave by twenty-four hours is a small, almost comic act of rebellion — but it prefigures the much larger protest to come. The officer who couldn't make himself go back on time is the same man who will later refuse to serve at all.

## Historical Reality

The British Army's leave system was one of the more regularized aspects of [[trench-warfare|trench warfare]], with dedicated leave trains, leave camps, and a rotation system that tried — however imperfectly — to give every man a periodic respite. The system's very regularity made it a reliable source of anxiety: men counted the days until their next leave, and the prospect of missing one was a genuine morale crisis.

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s account of overstaying leave was not uncommon — the army dealt with "absent without leave" (AWOL) cases regularly, and the penalties ranged from loss of pay to field punishment.
