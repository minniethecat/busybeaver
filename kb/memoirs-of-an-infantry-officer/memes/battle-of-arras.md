# Battle of Arras (1917)

The British offensive launched on 9 April 1917 — a subsidiary operation to the French Nivelle Offensive further south — in which [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]], having returned to the front with the [[second-battalion|Second Battalion]], was wounded in the shoulder while exposing himself to German fire, an act of conspicuous courage that would later complicate his anti-war stance.

## Background

By the [[spring-motif|spring]] of 1917, the Allied high commands had agreed on a coordinated offensive strategy for [[france|the Western Front]]. The French Army, under General Nivelle, would attack on the Aisne with overwhelming force. The British, at French request, would mount a preliminary offensive at Arras to draw German reserves away from the main attack. The Battle of Arras was thus, from its inception, a diversion — a fact that [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]], with characteristic irony, registers in his treatment of the operation [031-036].

The British First and Third Armies, under Allenby, assembled a concentration of artillery unprecedented in density — over 2,800 guns on a front of roughly twenty miles. The preliminary bombardment began on 4 April and continued for five days, pulverising the German forward positions and the city of Arras itself.

## The Event

The infantry assault went in at 5:30 a.m. on 9 April 1917 — Easter Monday. In the initial phase, the British achieved their most dramatic advances since the war began. The Canadian Corps captured Vimy Ridge, a position that had resisted two years of French attacks. The Third Army advanced up to three miles on the first day, capturing the German first and second trench systems.

But the offensive quickly bogged down. German reserves arrived, the weather turned — snow and sleet made movement and supply difficult — and the initial gains could not be exploited. The battle continued into May, degenerating into the familiar pattern of limited attacks at heavy cost. British casualties totalled approximately 158,000; German losses were roughly comparable.

## In the Memoir

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s treatment of the Battle of Arras in the memoir is characteristically compressed. The battle itself is announced with a single, almost documentary sentence — "The Battle of Arras began at 5:30 next morning" — and the fighting that follows is rendered not as tactical narrative but as sensory fragments: the noise of the barrage, the chaos of the advance, the sudden shock of the wound [031-036].

His wounding during the battle is described with the self-deprecating understatement that is his signature mode. He is hit while "showing off" — exposing himself unnecessarily to fire in an act of bravado that he later analyses as a form of combat intoxication. The wound in the shoulder removes him from the front, and it is during his subsequent convalescence in [[england|England]] that the memoir's political consciousness — culminating in the [[protest|anti-war protest]] of July 1917 — develops.

The battle's aftermath is rendered through the image of a dead soldier: "dying for his country in the Battle of Arras, and we who marched past him were alive" — a juxtaposition of the dead and the living that captures the arbitrary arithmetic of survival [032-003].

## Casualties & Aftermath

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s personal casualty — the shoulder wound — was, in the context of the battle, relatively light. The battalion's losses were heavier, and the memoir notes them with the same compressed grief that characterises its treatment of all casualties: names mentioned, faces recalled, and then the narrative moves on, because it must.

The Battle of Arras is also significant as the immediate context for [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s evolving attitude toward the war. It was during his convalescence from the Arras wound that he began to formulate the critique of the war's conduct and purpose that would lead, with the encouragement of Bertrand Russell and other pacifist intellectuals, to his public protest. The battle thus occupies a pivotal position: the last major action in which [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] participated as a combatant before his transformation into a protester.

## Sassoon's Commentary

The memoir's treatment of Arras is notably restrained. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] does not claim the battle as a turning point in his consciousness — that claim would have been too neat, too literary. Instead, he records the battle, the wound, and the aftermath as events in sequence, allowing the reader to trace the connection between physical trauma and political awakening without forcing the relationship.

The image of the soldier "dying for his country" and the marchers "who passed him" is the memoir's most explicit Arras commentary: a stark juxtaposition that refuses to moralise, yet whose very flatness constitutes a moral statement. The dead soldier and the living marchers share a road; the difference between them is luck, not merit.
