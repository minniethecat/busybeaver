# Colonel Kinjack

The battalion commander during [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s early months on [[france|the Western Front]] — a figure of institutional authority whose presence structures the memoir's depiction of military hierarchy and the gap between command decisions and frontline experience.

## Role & Identity

Kinjack is the commanding officer (Lieutenant-Colonel) of the [[first-battalion|1st Battalion]] during the period covered by Part I of the memoir. He appears primarily through indirect report — [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] hears about him, receives orders from him, and watches others interact with him — rather than through direct scenes. This narrative distance itself is significant: the battalion commander exists in a different world from the junior officers who execute his orders [002-001][005-004].

## In the Memoir

Kinjack enters the memoir through [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s anxiety: "Perhaps Colonel Kinjack had heard that I'd been looking for a cushy job at the [[fourth-army-school|Army School]]" [002-001]. The commanding officer is a figure of judgment — the one who decides who gets the "cushy" assignments and who goes on the dangerous ones.

[[barton|Barton]]'s relationship with Kinjack provides the memoir's most revealing glimpse of battalion-level dynamics. "Skiting to Kinjack about the [[raid|Raid]], I'll bet" — the junior officers' suspicion that their company commander is selling the raid to the colonel as a success, regardless of its actual cost [005-004]. The gap between what the colonel is told and what actually happened is the same gap the memoir exists to expose.

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] himself decides to approach Kinjack directly: "Anyhow, I meant to ask Kinjack to let me go on the [[raid|Raid]]" [005-008]. The request is granted — Kinjack is not a villain, merely a man embedded in a system. The memoir's fairness to authority figures is part of its moral complexity.

## Historical Counterpart

Kinjack likely represents one of the [[royal-welch-fusiliers|Royal Welch Fusiliers]]' actual commanding officers during [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s service, though the name is fictionalized. The [[sassoon-siegfried|Sherston]] trilogy retains the same figure under the same name.

## Significance

Kinjack embodies the institutional distance that the memoir critiques without condemning. He is not cruel or incompetent; he simply occupies a position from which the reality of trench experience is invisible. The memoir's argument is not that Kinjack is a bad officer but that the system of command itself is structurally unable to see what it is doing.

## Others' View of Kinjack

Kinjack is seen almost entirely through the eyes of his subordinates, each perspective revealing a different facet of command:

- **[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s view**: The narrator's attitude toward Kinjack oscillates between anxiety and grudging respect. He worries that Kinjack has heard about his "cushy job" ambitions; he dreads Kinjack's displeasure over the comedy episode; yet when he asks to go on the raid, Kinjack grants the request without hesitation — and Sassoon records the moment with something close to gratitude. "Kinjack had thanked me. It was splendid" [002-001][010-007][007-014].
- **[[durley|Durley]]'s view**: The cynical company officer who suspects that [[barton|Barton]] is "skiting to Kinjack about the Raid" — selling the operation as a success to maintain the colonel's favour. Durley's remark captures the institutional pressure to report upward what commanders want to hear, and the junior officers' awareness that this pressure distorts what reaches the colonel's ears [005-004].
- **[[barton|Barton]]'s view**: The company commander who serves as the crucial intermediary between Kinjack and the men. Barton reports to headquarters, receives orders, and returns to the company to execute them. His relationship with Kinjack appears professional and correct — but the memoir makes visible what the formal relationship hides: the gap between what happens in the line and what Kinjack is told about it [005-005][007-004].
- **The institutional view**: The Army sees Kinjack as a competent battalion commander — the officer who can be entrusted with a unit, who will carry out orders and maintain discipline. The memoir does not dispute this assessment; it simply notes that institutional competence and moral clarity are different things, and that the system rewards the former while remaining indifferent to the latter [009-003].

## Authorial Technique

Sassoon's construction of Kinjack illustrates his skill at the indirect portrait of authority:

- **Character by hearsay**: Kinjack is the memoir's purest example of a character built almost entirely through what others say about him. He appears directly in only a handful of scenes; his presence is felt through the anxiety he generates, the orders that flow from his headquarters, the judgments his subordinates anticipate and fear. The technique captures the actual experience of command in a hierarchical institution: the commanding officer is less a person than a force field [002-001][005-004].
- **The salmon episode**: The memoir's most extended treatment of Kinjack occurs in a comic register — the farcical episode in which Sassoon acquires a salmon for the colonel's table, navigating the absurdities of Army logistics with an anxiety disproportionate to the stakes. The episode is characteristic of Sassoon's method: a trivial incident reveals the structure of authority more vividly than direct critique could [010-007][010-008].
- **The institutional lens**: Kinjack is seen by the narrator not as a full human being but in his institutional role — the colonel who sends officers home as "useless" if they "crumple up," the commander who receives reports and issues orders. The limitation of perspective is itself a narrative statement: in the Army, human beings become functions, and the memoir records this reduction without approving it [009-003].
- **Fairness as critique**: Sassoon's treatment of Kinjack is scrupulously fair — no villainy, no incompetence, no malice. This fairness is the memoir's most devastating strategy. By showing that even a decent commanding officer cannot see what is happening to his men, the memoir indicts not the individual but the system that makes seeing impossible [005-008][007-014].
