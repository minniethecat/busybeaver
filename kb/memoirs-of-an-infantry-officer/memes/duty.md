# Duty

The concept that structured a British officer's professional and moral life — the obligation to serve, to obey, to lead, and, in the last resort, to die — which [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s memoir treats not as a fixed code but as a field of tension between institutional loyalty, personal morality, and the claims of conscience.

## Definition & Context

In the British Army of 1914–1918, "duty" was the master term of ethical discourse. It encompassed the officer's obligations to his men, his regiment, his sovereign, and his country. It justified sacrifice and disciplined dissent. A man who "did his duty" had met the standard; a man who failed in his duty had forfeited the respect of his peers [034-018][045-019].

But "duty" was also an essentially contested concept. Did duty require unquestioning obedience to orders, or did it permit — even demand — the exercise of moral judgment? Was an officer's primary duty to the men under his command or to the chain of command above him? Could it be one's duty to refuse to fight — to protest an unjust war — as [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] eventually concluded it was?

## In the Memoir

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s treatment of duty is developmental rather than static. In the memoir's early chapters, duty is largely unexamined — simply the framework within which officers function. Men described as having "an inexhaustible sense of duty" are praised without irony; the term carries its conventional weight [034-018].

But as the memoir progresses, duty becomes increasingly problematic. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] registers the gap between official conceptions of duty and the reality of the war. The medical board that finds him fit for duty exemplifies the institutional machinery that processes men back to the front without regard for their psychological state [037-011]. The nurse who considers it her "duty, as a patriotic Englishwoman" to denounce Belgian refugees reveals the way duty-language could serve prejudice and cruelty [020-004].

The memoir's climax — [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s [[protest|anti-war protest]] — is fundamentally an argument about duty. When he writes that "it was my duty to do my little bit to stop it," he is asserting a conception of duty that transcends military hierarchy: a duty to truth, to conscience, and to the men being sacrificed in an unjust cause [046-011]. The protest is not a rejection of duty but its radical reinterpretation.

The memoir also records the institutional response to this reinterpretation. The officer who tells [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] that "it was obviously his duty to remonstrate with me severely" represents the military's counter-claim: that duty consists in obedience and conformity, not in individual moral judgment [045-019]. The conflict between these two conceptions of duty is the memoir's deepest ethical drama.

## Sassoon's Perspective

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] never simply rejects the concept of duty. He was too much a product of his class and his training for that. Instead, he attempts to reclaim it — to argue that true duty is served by protest, not by acquiescence. This is a more complex position than simple anti-war sentiment, and it accounts for the memoir's continuing power: [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s argument with himself about duty is never entirely resolved.

His treatment of other officers' sense of duty is consistently respectful, even when he disagrees with the uses to which it is put. Men like [[dottrell|Dottrell]], who do their duty without questioning it, are portrayed with affection rather than condescension. The memoir does not claim that [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s own, more complex, relation to duty is morally superior — only that it is different, and that the difference cost him something.

## Historical Reality

The officer's code of duty in World War I was a compound of Victorian moralism, public-school ideology, and the professional ethos of the regular army. It was reinforced by an honour culture in which "letting down the regiment" or "failing one's men" were failures worse than death. The code was remarkably effective at producing brave and self-sacrificing officers; it was also remarkably effective at suppressing dissent.

Conscientious objection — the formal route by which soldiers could legally refuse to fight — was available only on religious grounds and was viewed by the military establishment as a form of cowardice. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s protest — a decorated officer publicly criticising the war's conduct — was a different and more threatening phenomenon, precisely because it undermined the assumption that only shirkers and cowards questioned the war.
