# Field Telephone

The **field telephone** was the primary means of battlefield communications on the Western Front, linking battalion headquarters, brigade, and the forward positions from which infantry officers received their orders. In Sassoon's memoir, the telephone appears repeatedly as the technological medium through which the chain of command exercises its distant authority — and as the fragile wire that, when cut, leaves men isolated in the chaos of battle.

## The Tick-Tack of Headquarters

The sound of the field telephone is one of the memoir's recurring auditory details, as distinctive to the dugout as the rumble of guns is to the open field. In a dugout near Mercatel, Sassoon dozes "without ever being unaware of the coke fumes from the brazier and the tick-tack of the telephone" [032-002]. The telephone is inseparable from the atmosphere of headquarters — the enclosed, anxious space where officers wait for news they cannot see for themselves.

During the attack at the Sunken Road, a "tick-tacking telephone-orderly in a corner received a message that the attack had started" [034-013]. The telephone orderly is a distinct military role, the human interface between the command structure and the men who execute its decisions. He is not a combatant but a relay point, his world reduced to the tick-tack of the instrument and the messages that pass through it.

## The Broken Line

The telephone's unreliability under fire is a recurring source of tension. Shellfire cuts the wire; the connection dies; headquarters falls silent. "Then the telephone-orderly announced that communication with Captain Macnair's headquarters had broken down; after that the suspense continued monotonously" [034-014].

The broken telephone line signifies the breakdown of command itself. Officers in HQs can no longer know what is happening at the front, and the men in the attack are cut off from reinforcement, from orders, from the larger plan that gave their advance its meaning. The suspense is "monotonous" — a word that captures the peculiar agony of waiting without information, the emptiness that replaces the busy tick-tack of connection.

## The Medium of Reproach

The telephone is also a medium of hierarchical discipline, carrying blame as efficiently as orders. After Sassoon's patrol stays out too long, Kinjack is "ticked off over the telephone by the Brigadier" [014-021]. The chain of reproach travels down the wire: from Brigadier to Colonel, from Colonel to the confused junior officer returning from the line.

The verb "ticked off" is a deliberate echo of the telephone's own tick-tack — the Brigadier's displeasure reduced to the same mechanical rhythm as the instrument that conveys it. The telephone anonymises reproach, allowing senior officers to deliver criticism without the inconvenience of presence. Sassoon, at the receiving end of the chain, experiences the reprimand as something already processed through multiple technological and bureaucratic layers.

## Telephonic Hierarchy

Sassoon extends the telephone metaphor to describe the entire command structure of the Western Front. "The Army Commander, I suppose, was in telephonic communication with the Commander-in-Chief, who with one eye on Marshal Foch, was hoping to satisfy his King and Country" [034-007].

The phrase "I suppose" is crucial — Sassoon, the infantry officer in the trench, can only imagine the phone calls passing above his head. The telephone is the medium of a command structure "biased against" the infantry, a system of communication that connects everyone except the men who actually fight. The technology that should unify the army instead diagrams its hierarchy: the closer you are to the telephone, the further you are from the bullet.
