# Dugout

The underground chambers excavated into trench walls and banks — ranging from crude scrapes to elaborately timbered rooms — that served as sleeping quarters, mess rooms, and command posts for the men of [[france|the Western Front]], and that [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] presents as the war's most claustrophobic domestic spaces.

## Definition & Context

Dugouts were essential to trench life. They provided shelter from shellfire and weather, privacy (however minimal), and a space for the rituals — meals, card games, letter-writing — that maintained morale. Officers' dugouts were typically more elaborate than those of the men, with timbered walls, candle lighting, and the paraphernalia of mess life: maps, whiskey bottles, gramophones [006-004][007-001].

The dugout was also a site of command: orders were written there, patrols planned, reports filed. It was, in microcosm, the army's domestic-administrative space — a desk underground.

## In the Memoir

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s dugouts appear throughout the memoir as settings for its most concentrated dialogue scenes. The "sackcloth smelling dugout at 71 North" is where the trench raid is planned and where officers gather before and after the action [007-001]. The "large dugout just along the road" is where the raiding party receives its final instructions and its issue of rum [007-002].

The dugout functions structurally as the memoir's indoor space — the alternative to the open-air violence of the trenches. In the dugout, men talk, plan, argue, grieve. It is the war's closest equivalent to a domestic interior, and [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] renders it with the attention to detail of a novelist describing a room: the candlelight, the maps on the wall, the smell of sacking and earth.

The dugout also carries a symbolic charge. It is a return to the earth, a form of burial before death. "The crouching imprisonment of [[trench-warfare|trench warfare]]" — [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s phrase for the dugout's psychological condition — captures the dual nature of the shelter: it protects and it traps [010-009][006-004].

## Sassoon's Perspective

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s dugouts are liminal spaces — neither battlefield nor [[england|home]], neither combat nor safety. They are where the memoir's most intimate moments occur (conversations with [[barton|Barton]], the planning of the raid, the aftermath of action) and where its deepest anxieties are nursed.

The dugout is also a class-marked space. Officer dugouts are described differently from the men's shelters — more elaborate, but also more psychologically fraught, because the officer in his dugout is alone with the burden of command. The memoir registers this solitude with characteristic understatement.

## Historical Reality

Dugout construction became increasingly sophisticated as the war progressed. By 1916, British dugouts were being built to specified designs with timber revetting, sandbag reinforcement, and minimum depth requirements to resist shell penetration. Deep dugouts — 20–30 feet below ground — were virtually shell-proof and were used as battalion and brigade headquarters.
