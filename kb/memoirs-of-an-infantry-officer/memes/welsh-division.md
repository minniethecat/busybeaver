# Welsh Division (38th)

The 38th (Welsh) Division — a New Army formation raised in Wales and committed to the attack on [[mametz-wood|Mametz Wood]] on 7 July 1916, where it suffered over 4,000 casualties in a single week of fighting. The memoir records the division's ordeal not as a tactical narrative but as an emblem of the war's capacity to consume the inexperienced.

## Identity & Structure

The Welsh Division was one of Kitchener's New Army divisions, recruited primarily from Welsh mining and industrial communities. It arrived in France in December 1915 and was committed to the Somme offensive in July 1916, its first major action. The division's composition — volunteers from a single nation, led by officers who were themselves learning their trade — made it representative of the New Army's strengths (local loyalty, high morale) and vulnerabilities (inexperience, vulnerability to avoidable mistakes).

The memoir does not describe the division's order of battle. It registers the division instead through its human remnants: the "unseasoned New Army troops" who relieve [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s battalion, the "undersized men" whose "jostling company" fills the trench [014-022].

## In the Memoir

The Welsh Division enters the memoir at Mametz Wood, where [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s battalion arrives to find that the Staff's assumption — that the wood was unoccupied — has been disproven by the Welshmen's casualties. "The Welsh Division had attacked Mametz Wood earlier in the day" [015-001], and the attack had failed, leaving the wood still full of Germans and the ground between the British line and the trees littered with Welsh dead.

The memoir's most direct engagement with the division's fate comes through aftermath. The Welshmen who relieve [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s battalion are not triumphant — they are shaken, diminished, "a panicky rabble" in [[kinjack|Kinjack]]'s unsympathetic assessment [014-022]. But the memoir's judgment is different: "I had a sense of their victimization" [014-022]. The division has been thrown into a fight for which no preparation could have been adequate.

Later, the division's ordeal becomes a known quantity: "For the Welsh Division it had been a bloody nightmare" [019-003]. The phrase is not [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s — it is reported speech, a staff officer's admission — but the memoir records it without comment, allowing the admission to stand as its own judgment.

## War Record

The 38th Division's attack on Mametz Wood was a military failure redeemed, if at all, only by the courage of the men who executed it. The division was withdrawn after a week, having suffered casualties that approached 50 percent of its fighting strength. It would recover and fight again, but its reputation — as a division that had been thrown away by incompetent staff work — was established at Mametz and never fully erased.

## Sassoon's Perspective

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s treatment of the Welsh Division is characteristic of his method as a whole: he does not tell the reader what to think about the division; he shows the division's survivors, records the Staff's errors, and allows the juxtaposition to do the work. His "sense of their victimization" is the memoir's most explicit statement of solidarity with the infantry — not with his own battalion, which was relatively fortunate, but with the men who took the worst of it and were blamed for the failure.

## Historical Counterpart

The 38th (Welsh) Division was a real New Army formation, raised in Wales under the patronage of David Lloyd George. Its attack on Mametz Wood on 7–12 July 1916 is a well-documented episode of the Somme battle, and its casualties — 4,000 in a week — were among the worst suffered by any division in that phase of the offensive. The memoir's account is consistent with the historical record while achieving a compression and emotional force that no official history could match.
