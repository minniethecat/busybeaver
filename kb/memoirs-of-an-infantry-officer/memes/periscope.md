The **periscope** was an optical device — essentially a tube with angled mirrors — that allowed soldiers in a front-line trench to observe no-man's-land without exposing their heads above the parapet. In Sassoon's memoir, the periscope appears at two significant moments, each illustrating a different relationship between observation, authority, and danger.

## In the Memoir

### The Shopping Expedition

The periscope first appears during Sherston's leave in London, when he visits the Army and Navy Stores to equip himself for the coming offensive. "The man in the Weapon Department at the Stores had been persuasive about a periscope (probably prismatic)," Sherston notes, "but I came to the conclusion that a periscope was a back number in my case. I shouldn't be in the trench long enough to need it" [007-001]. The dark joke — Sherston's assumption that he will either be killed or wounded before a periscope becomes useful — is characteristic of the memoir's gallows humour. The periscope, a device designed to preserve life through cautious observation, is dismissed by a man who has ceased to believe in his own survival.

### Kinjack's Inspection

The periscope's second appearance is more pointed. On the afternoon before the Somme offensive, [[kinjack|Kinjack]] — the Battalion Colonel — comes up to inspect the gaps cut in the British wire for the attacking troops to pass through. "With the assistance of his big periscope," Sassoon writes, "he soon discovered that the wire wasn't properly cut" [007-012]. The Colonel's periscope — larger than the standard trench model, suggestive of a superior instrument for a superior rank — becomes an emblem of command's remote gaze: the officer who observes danger from safety and issues orders to those who must expose themselves to it. The periscope enables Kinjack to see what is wrong; it does not enable him to fix it. That task falls to Sherston, who spends the night with his wire-cutters doing the physical work the Colonel's inspection demanded.

### Observation and Its Costs

The periscope's two appearances frame a tacit argument about observation and power in the memoir. For the infantry officer, observation means exposure — looking over the parapet, going out on patrol, cutting wire in no-man's-land. For the senior officer, observation is mediated by optics and distance: the periscope, the trench map, the Corps Intelligence Summary. The device that protects the observer also separates him from what he observes. Sherston's rejection of the periscope — "a back number in my case" — is, in this reading, a rejection of the attitude it represents: the war viewed from a safe distance.
