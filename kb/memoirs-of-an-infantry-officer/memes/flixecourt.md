# Flixécourt

The small town in Picardy, behind the Somme front, where the [[fourth-army-school|Fourth Army School]] was located — the memoir's emblematic rear-area location, and the setting for [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s month-long course in the military arts he would never apply.

## Geography & Context

Flixécourt is a town in the Somme département of northern [[france|France]], situated on the river Nièvre approximately 25 kilometers behind the front line of 1916 — close enough to hear the guns, far enough that the war could be studied rather than experienced. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] describes it as "a clean little town" [002-005], the adjective "clean" functioning as a distinction from the mud of the trenches and as an implicit critique of the institution it housed.

The Fourth Army School occupied requisitioned buildings in the town, with lecture rooms, a mess, and billets for the officers sent there for refresher training. The school's curriculum — open warfare tactics, gas drill, map-reading — was taught by instructors whose competence the memoir treats with characteristic irony.

## In the Memoir

Flixécourt provides the setting for the memoir's opening chapters. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] arrives there in the spring of 1916 for a month's course, and the town becomes the frame within which he introduces the memoir's cast of officers: the instructors, the students, the staff officers who lecture on subjects they have not experienced. The memoir opens at the school not because it is the chronological beginning of Sassoon's war but because it is the analytic beginning: the place where the gap between military doctrine and infantry experience first becomes visible.

The month at Flixécourt is remembered with a characteristic double feeling. On returning to the front, Sassoon finds that "my month at Flixécourt was already obliterated" [005-005] — the experience of the trenches so overwhelming that the school's ordered world cannot survive in memory. Yet later, he records that it was "natural that I should remember Flixécourt. Those four weeks had kept the War at arm's length" [025-005]. The distance that seemed like evasion at the time becomes, in retrospect, a last experience of ordinary life.

The Flixécourt chapters contain the memoir's most sustained satire of military education: the gas expert from [[general-headquarters|G.H.Q.]] who announces that "gas was still in its infancy" [002-010], the instructors who teach tactics appropriate to a war of movement while their students return nightly to a static trench system. The school is not villainous — it is merely irrelevant, and its irrelevance is the memoir's accusation.

## Military Significance

Flixécourt had no military significance as a battlefield. Its function was training, and its distance from the front was the condition of that function. The memoir's interest in Flixécourt is precisely its distance — the physical and psychological gap between the schoolroom and the trench, which becomes the memoir's governing metaphor for the gap between the war as planned and the war as lived.

## Atmosphere & Description

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s Flixécourt is a town of small pleasures and small absurdities. He recalls "the little room at Flixécourt where I had been able to sit and read" [005-001] — the private space of reading mattering more than the military instruction. The town's cleanliness, its intact buildings, its functioning shops: these ordinary features of civilian life are rendered extraordinary by contrast with the devastated landscape only twenty-five kilometers away.
