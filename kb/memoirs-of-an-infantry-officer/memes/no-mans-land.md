# No Man's Land

**No Man's Land** — the strip of ground between the British and German front lines — is the memoir's most charged geography, a space that is neither one side's territory nor the other's, belonging to the dead and to the men who venture into it after dark. In Sassoon's *Memoirs of an Infantry Officer*, No Man's Land is simultaneously a physical place of craters and wire and a psychological landscape of dread and fascination.

## The Strip of Ground

No Man's Land varied in width from a few dozen yards to several hundred, and its character depended on the sector. Sassoon describes "that strip of No Man's Land opposite Bois Français," a specific patch of ground with which he has developed an almost proprietary relationship. The mine-craters in this sector fascinate him, "though I'd often feared that they'd be the death of me." The craters are landmarks in a landscape otherwise defined by absence — no trees, no buildings, no human habitation, only the churned earth and the wire.

A road that once led to Fricourt now "wandered evilly across No Man's Land, and then gave itself up to the Germans." The verb "wandered" personifies the road with malign intent; "gave itself up" suggests surrender, collaboration, a landscape that has chosen sides. The road, once a conduit of civilian life, is now a feature of the battlefield, as dangerous as the shell-holes that pock it.

## The Work of Darkness

No Man's Land comes alive at night, when patrols and wiring-parties venture into it. The memoir records the aftermath of the raid when Sassoon returns to "tidy up the mess in No Man's Land" — the dead and wounded, the abandoned equipment, the evidence of the night's violence that must be retrieved before daylight exposes it.

"Curiously enough, I hadn't yet seen a German," Sassoon notes. "I had seen dim figures on my dark patrols; but no human faces." No Man's Land is a place where the enemy is present but invisible, known only by the wire he has laid, the flares he sends up, the bullets that cross the darkness. The absence of the human face makes the killing abstract — and, paradoxically, more terrifying.

## A Queer Daylight Magic

Despite its horror, No Man's Land exerts a peculiar pull on Sassoon's imagination. The Fricourt road "had for me a queer daylight magic, especially in summer." The word "magic" is unexpected — a registration of the aesthetic attraction that even the most desolate landscape can hold. The strip of ground that is the war's most concentrated space of death is also, in certain lights and certain moods, beautiful.

This ambivalence — terror and fascination, repulsion and attraction — is characteristic of Sassoon's treatment of the war's physical environment. No Man's Land is where men die; it is also, for the memoir's narrator, a place of intense imaginative engagement, a frontier that is simultaneously geographical and psychological.
