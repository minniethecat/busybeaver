**Censorship** in Sassoon's memoir is not merely a bureaucratic mechanism but a moral category — the institutional filter through which the reality of the trenches is prevented from reaching the civilian population. It is the force that keeps soldiers silent and the public ignorant, and it becomes, for Sherston, the immediate target of his awakening political consciousness.

## In the Memoir

### Markington's Diagnosis

The memoir's most concentrated treatment of censorship occurs during Sherston's luncheon with [[markington|Markington]], the editor of an anti-war paper. Sherston has written to Markington offering "to provide such details as I knew from personal experience" of the front, hoping to publish "something outspoken so as to let people at home know what the War was really like" [038-005]. Markington's response is blunt: if he were to print veracious accounts of infantry experience, his paper "would be suppressed as prejudicial to recruiting." The censorship officials, he explains, "were always watching for a plausible excuse for banning it, and they had already prohibited its foreign circulation."

Markington's formulation is the memoir's most devastating epigram on the subject: "The soldiers are not allowed to express their point of view. In wartime the word 'patriotism' means suppression of truth" [038-005]. The sentence converts patriotism — the official language of the war's justification — into its opposite. What the home front calls patriotic feeling is, from the perspective of the trenches, the systematic silencing of those who know what the war actually is.

### The Structural Logic of Silence

Censorship in the memoir operates at multiple levels. There is the official censorship of the press, preventing Markington from publishing soldiers' accounts. There is the self-censorship of officers like Sherston himself, who cannot tell [[aunt-evelyn|Aunt Evelyn]] the truth about the front and instead performs the role of the "happy warrior." And there is the deeper cultural censorship — the refusal of the civilian world to hear what it does not wish to know. When Sherston returns to England on leave, he finds that his experience has rendered him inarticulate in the very society he is supposed to be defending: "I couldn't allow my protest to become a domestic controversy."

### From Censorship to Protest

Sherston's eventual protest — his written statement declaring that the War is being "deliberately prolonged by those who have the power to end it" — is best understood as an act of anti-censorship [041-001]. It is an attempt to break through the institutional silence that Markington described, to say publicly what soldiers could only say to one another. That the protest lands him not in prison but in [[craiglockhart|Craiglockhart]] War Hospital is itself a commentary on how the system managed dissent: not by punishing it openly (which would create martyrs) but by medicalising it.
