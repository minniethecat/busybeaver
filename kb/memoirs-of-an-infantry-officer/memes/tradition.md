## Definition & Context

In Sassoon's memoir, "tradition" operates on multiple levels: the formal traditions of the Regiment (ceremonial, behavioural, sartorial), the public school tradition that shaped the officer class, and the family tradition that bound individuals to a particular code of conduct. The concept is central to understanding the social architecture of the British Army in WWI — and to Sassoon's increasingly ambivalent relationship with that architecture.

## In the Memoir

Tradition appears most explicitly in the figure of the Colonel, described as "an upholder of prewar regimental traditions" who maintains the Saturday Meets of the regimental hunt even as the war grinds on [023-006]. This persistence of peacetime ritual in wartime conditions is presented not with mockery but with a kind of elegiac recognition — the traditions are precious precisely because they cannot survive the war unchanged.

The narrator acknowledges that officers like Ormand had a deeper investment in tradition than he did: "his sense of 'the regimental tradition' was stronger than mine, and in that tradition he was rooted" [023-010]. This admission reveals Sassoon's own ambivalent position — he is half-inside the tradition (by class, by regiment) and half-outside it (by temperament, by his growing anti-war conviction).

The public school tradition is particularly significant. At the front, Shirley is described as "true to the traditions of his class" in his simulated nonchalance in the face of danger [032-005]. The "public school tradition" is what makes young officers "politely introduced" to death — a socialization that inculcates stoic acceptance rather than protest [032-006].

Later, reflecting on the ethos of the pre-war officer, Sassoon asks: "the tradition of the prewar regimental officer" — what did it mean? [042-009]. The question is left partly unanswered, its meaning dissolving under the pressure of mechanized war.

Kendle's death prompts one of the memoir's most pointed reflections: "the framework of his family tradition" had bound him to service, his maternal grandfather having been a general [044-006]. Tradition here becomes not a comfort but a trap — an inherited obligation that sends young men to their deaths.

In the memoir's final movement, Sassoon confronts the tradition directly in an imagined dialogue: is there "anything except the gentlemanly traditions of the Regiment" worth defending? [046-012]. The question hangs unresolved, marking the memoir's terminal ambivalence.

## Sassoon's Perspective

Sassoon's treatment of tradition is characteristically double-edged. He values the aesthetic and communal aspects of regimental tradition — the hunt, the Mess rituals, the code of gentlemanly conduct. But he cannot reconcile these with the mechanical slaughter that the same tradition silently sanctions. The memoir's genius is to hold both attitudes in tension: tradition is simultaneously what the war destroys and what enables the war to continue. The young officers die not despite tradition but because of it — because "the traditions of his class" forbid complaint, protest, or the open acknowledgment of fear.

## Historical Reality

The British Army of 1914–18 was profoundly shaped by regimental traditions, many dating back centuries. The public school ethos of muscular Christianity and stoic service was the explicit moral framework of the officer corps. Sassoon's memoir is one of the most nuanced literary treatments of how this framework collapsed under the weight of industrial warfare — not in a single moment of rupture but in a slow erosion that left the forms intact while hollowing out their meaning.
