# Cowardice

Cowardice is the shadow that falls across every act of courage in [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s memoir — the accusation that the military system held over every soldier, the fate that befell those who could no longer endure, and the charge that the narrator himself would eventually face when he chose protest over obedience.

## Definition & Context

In the British Army of the Great War, cowardice was a capital offense. Soldiers who were judged to have failed in their duty through fear could be court-martialled and, if convicted, sentenced to death by firing squad. Between 1914 and 1918, approximately 306 British and Commonwealth soldiers were executed for cowardice or desertion — a figure that does not include the many more who were convicted but had their sentences commuted. The military justice system operated on the assumption that the threat of execution was necessary to maintain discipline, and the charge of cowardice hung over every soldier as both a legal possibility and a moral shadow. The line between cowardice and the medical condition then called "shell shock" was vague and inconsistently applied [014-013].

## In the Memoir

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] treats cowardice with the same refusal to simplify that he applies to courage. The memoir records specific instances: the man who narrowly escaped "being court-martialled for cowardice" [014-013], the "soldiers who had been shot for cowardice since the end of January" [027-004]. The dates are specific — "since the end of January" — as if the narrator wants the reader to understand that these deaths are not abstractions but events that happened in a particular winter, to particular men, in the course of a particular war.

The memoir's most devastating treatment of cowardice comes through a case the narrator encounters directly: a soldier "condemned to be shot for cowardice. The court had recommended the man to mercy" [030-010]. The court recommended mercy — the military admitted, in effect, that the punishment was disproportionate — but the sentence stood. The narrator records this without explicit comment, but the structure of the passage is itself an indictment: a system that can acknowledge its own injustice and proceed anyway is worse than one that simply errs.

The memoir's culminating treatment of cowardice is personal. When the narrator's superior accuses him of moral failing, the response comes: "Isn't that worse cowardice than being thought cold-footed?" [046-012]. The accusation of cowardice is turned back on the accuser — not the cowardice of fear but the cowardice of refusing to question, of "supporting me" when "me" means the machinery that sends men to their deaths. This is the memoir's great inversion: true cowardice is not the soldier who cannot face the guns but the officer who will not face the truth.

## Sassoon's Perspective

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] knew the charge of cowardice intimately — not because he ever faced it in a courtroom, but because his 1917 protest against the war was widely interpreted as a form of moral cowardice dressed in the language of conscience. The memoir is, among other things, a sustained rebuttal to this interpretation. The narrator argues, implicitly but persistently, that the war has inverted the meanings of courage and cowardice: that it is cowardly to continue participating in a war one knows to be unjust, and that the courage required to protest is of a higher order than the courage required to fight.

This argument is never stated as a thesis. It emerges through incident, through juxtaposition, through the memoir's quiet insistence that the men "shot for cowardice" [027-004] were not cowards but victims — of a system that demanded more than human beings could give, and then punished them for their humanity.

## Historical Reality

The execution of British soldiers for cowardice and desertion during the Great War remained a source of controversy for decades. In 2006, the British government issued a posthumous pardon to all 306 soldiers executed for these offenses during the war, acknowledging that many were suffering from what would now be recognized as post-traumatic stress disorder. The pardon was a belated recognition that the line between cowardice and psychological breakdown was one that the military justice system of 1914–1918 was not equipped to draw.

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s memoir anticipates this recognition. His treatment of cowardice — the soldier "condemned to be shot," the court that "recommended mercy," the officers who enforced a code they could not themselves fully believe — is a moral argument made through narrative rather than polemic, and it is the more powerful for its restraint.
