# Sleep

Sleep in [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s memoir is never merely rest; it is a borderland between the living and the dead, a temporary reprieve from consciousness that the war perpetually threatens to revoke, and one of the few experiences in the trenches that remained stubbornly human and resistant to military discipline.

## Definition & Context

Sleep deprivation was a constant feature of [[trench-warfare|trench warfare]]. Night was the time for working parties, [[barbed-wire|wire]] repair, patrols, and trench raids — the infantryman's day was often inverted, with rest snatched during daylight hours when movement in the trenches was more dangerous. The continuous noise of bombardment made deep sleep nearly impossible close to the front line. Medical officers recognized that chronic sleep deprivation contributed to the condition then called "shell shock," but the practical demands of [[trench-warfare|trench warfare]] left little room for the luxury of uninterrupted rest. A man who had been "without sleep for two nights" [014-001] was considered to be operating under normal conditions rather than exceptional hardship.

## In the Memoir

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] treats sleep as one of the memoir's central motifs, returning to it repeatedly as a register of the war's toll on the human body. The narrator's first night in the trenches establishes the pattern: "[[durley|Durley]] was muttering in his sleep, something rapid and incoherent" [005-008] — sleep as the site where the mind processes what waking consciousness cannot, the "unsleeping strangeness" [005-007] of the trenches invading even the refuge of dreams.

The memoir records an extraordinary range of sleeping postures and conditions. Soldiers sleep "around us in the gloom" [016-005], "on the hillside... in the daylight which made everything ordinary" [016-018], with "the sound of heavy firing" [016-002] as a lullaby. Mounted officers "rode as if asleep" [016-017] — exhaustion so profound that it overrides even the demands of horsemanship. Kendle "fell fast asleep with his head against my shoulder" [014-014] — the intimacy of shared exhaustion, men who have learned to sleep anywhere and in any position because the alternative is not sleeping at all.

The memoir's most striking sleep image is also one of its quietest: "I fell asleep to the sound of full gutters" [012-002]. The domesticity of the image — rain in the gutters, the sound that might accompany any English night — is set against the reality of [[france|the Western Front]]. Sleep, in this rendering, is the last trace of civilian life that the trenches cannot entirely extinguish.

## Sassoon's Perspective

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s treatment of sleep is characteristically dual. On one hand, sleep is a biological necessity, the thing that separates the functional soldier from the broken one. The narrator notes that "most of them had been without sleep for two nights" [014-001] before a major operation — the observation is clinical, almost professional, the officer assessing his men's readiness.

But sleep is also, in the memoir's deeper register, a form of resistance. To sleep is to refuse consciousness of the war, if only for a few hours. The sleeping soldier is the one figure in the memoir who is not performing — not maintaining the officer's composure, not modeling courage, not doing anything other than inhabiting his own exhausted body. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] records these sleeping men with a tenderness that is rare in the memoir, as if sleep were the one condition in which soldiers could be simply human rather than instruments of war.

## Historical Reality

The chronic sleep deprivation of [[trench-warfare|trench warfare]] is well documented in medical and military records of the period. Soldiers on the front line typically got no more than four hours of sleep per night, often interrupted by stand-to duties at dawn and dusk, when the entire garrison manned the firing step in anticipation of attack. The cumulative effects of sleep deprivation — impaired judgment, emotional volatility, hallucinations — were recognized by medical officers but could rarely be addressed, since any reduction in front-line duty meant some other unit would have to cover the gap.

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s memoir captures this reality without ever stating it directly. The sheer variety of his sleeping scenes — men asleep on hillsides, in dugouts, on trains, in the "sleepy sort of place" [015-002] of a rear-area billet — testifies to the universality of exhaustion as a condition of [[trench-warfare|trench warfare]]. Sleep is, in the memoir's implicit argument, what the war most fundamentally steals from its participants.
