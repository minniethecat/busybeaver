## Definition & Context

The salmon in Sassoon's memoir functions as a comic leitmotif and symbol of the absurd collision between civilian domesticity and military life. Purchased at the Army and Navy Stores during a period of leave in London, the fish becomes an obsessive concern for the narrator — a mundane object whose freshness and delivery displace all thoughts of the impending Somme offensive [010-007].

## In the Memoir

The salmon first appears when Sassoon, armed with his Aunt Evelyn's membership ticket, invades the Army and Navy Stores and procures "a superb salmon" alongside brandy, an automatic pistol, and wire-cutters [010-007]. The narrator immediately elevates the fish above his military situation: "The salmon was now my chief concern. I was concerned about its future freshness, for I had overstayed my leave by twenty-four hours" [010-007]. He reasons that "the salmon spelt safety at Battalion Headquarters" — a darkly ironic calculation that a gift of food would placate Colonel Kinjack's displeasure at his late return [010-007].

The salmon's journey continues on the leave boat to France, where "an obliging steward" places the hamper in cold storage, allowing Sassoon's mind to remain "unperturbed" as he watches the Isle of Wight recede [010-008]. On the march from Corbie to Bussy, the narrator "kept to the shady side of the road, for the salmon in its hamper was still my constant care" [010-009] — the fish's preservation taking priority over the approaching realities of trench warfare.

Later, the salmon appears in a contrasting register when Sassoon observes Major Macartney at the Mess. The Major's mind is described as being "often back in Ireland, snipe-shooting and salmon-fishing" — the salmon here shifting from comic prop to symbol of the pre-war leisured life that the war has suspended [023-004].

## Sassoon's Perspective

Sassoon deploys the salmon with deliberate comic timing and self-deprecating irony. The narrator's obsessive concern with a fish's freshness, set against the backdrop of the Somme offensive, creates a tonal dissonance that is characteristic of the memoir's method: the mundane and the catastrophic coexist without resolution. The salmon also serves as a quiet critique of the officer class's privilege — the ability to procure luxury goods at the Army and Navy Stores, the expectation that a gift can smooth over a disciplinary infraction, the whole apparatus of class-based entitlement that the war has not yet dismantled.

## Historical Reality

The Army and Navy Stores was a real London department store catering to military families and the officer class. The detail that Sassoon "overstayed my leave by twenty-four hours" reflects the genuine tension between the relative freedom of leave and the rigid discipline of the front. The salmon's cold-storage accommodation on the leave boat also points to the surprisingly comfortable logistics that existed alongside the privations of war — officers travelled on the same Channel steamers as civilians, with stewards and amenities intact.
