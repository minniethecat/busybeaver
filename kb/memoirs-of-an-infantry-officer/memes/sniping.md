# Sniping

**Sniping** — the deliberate targeting of individual enemy soldiers by concealed marksmen — was one of the defining terrors of trench warfare on the Western Front. In Sassoon's *Memoirs of an Infantry Officer*, the sniper is an invisible presence, a threat that turns every glance over the parapet into an act of reckless courage.

Unlike the impersonal violence of the artillery barrage, sniping is intimate and intentional: a man looking through a telescopic sight, choosing his target, squeezing the trigger. Every exposure above the parapet is a gamble with a marksman's patience.

## The Invisible Enemy

The sniper's power lies in his invisibility. Sassoon never describes a direct confrontation with an enemy sniper; instead, the threat is ambient, woven into the fabric of trench routine. A periscope raised too quickly, a head silhouetted against the sky at dawn, a momentary carelessness on the fire-step — any of these can attract the sniper's bullet.

This invisibility gives sniping a psychological weight disproportionate to the casualties it inflicts. The artillery barrage kills more men, but the sniper kills in a way that feels personal. Every soldier knows that somewhere across no-man's-land, a man with a rifle is watching, waiting, and has all the time in the world.

The memoir records sniping as a constant background condition. "Birdie's language about moonlight and snipers was a masterpiece" — the veteran officer's fury at bright nights that expose patrols to aimed fire. Yet on other nights, fortune favours the wiring party: "there had been no sniping that night, and the machine-gun at Wing Corner had been silent."

## Optics and Expertise

The memoir hints at the technical dimension of sniping — the telescopic sights, the careful observation, the patient stalking that transforms marksmanship into a dark art. A "renowned big-game hunter gave us demonstrations of the art of sniping," Sassoon records.

The civilian expertise of the African safari is translated without irony to the trenches of the Western Front — a colonial hunter's skills repurposed for industrial warfare.

Sassoon himself is no marksman. When discussing weaponry in London, he concedes that "the only object I could be sure of hitting was myself" [010-007], drawing a contrast between the amateur officer and the professional sniper for whom every shot is calculated. Sniping requires a particular temperament: patience, detachment, and an ability to regard human beings as targets. It is the most modern form of combat — mechanised, optical, dispassionate — prefiguring the remote killing technologies of later wars.

## The Parapet as Frontier

The sniper defines the architecture of trench life. The parapet — the raised front wall of the trench — is the boundary between relative safety and mortal exposure. Sassoon records how men learn to move below the parapet line, to use periscopes rather than direct observation, to time their movements to the sniper's rhythms. The trench is not merely a defensive work; it is a habitat shaped by the constant threat of the aimed bullet.

The fire-step, where sentries stand to observe no-man's-land, is the most dangerous place in the trench system. A man on the fire-step at dawn or dusk is gambling his life on the hope that the marksman across the way is looking elsewhere.

The danger is not abstract: "already men were lying wounded and killed by sniping" in the shallow trench, their bodies evidence of the sniper's relentless efficiency.

## The Sniper Killed

One passage in the memoir breaks the sniper's invisibility with shocking clarity. In the aftermath of an attack, Sassoon describes a moment of focused rage: "after blank awareness that he was killed, all feelings tightened and contracted to a single intention — to settle that sniper on the other side of the valley."

The sniper is momentarily made visible, identified, targeted in return. The invisible threat becomes a specific man, a specific hillside, a specific rifle.

The aftermath is anti-climactic. Returning to "the sniping post at the end of the trench," Sassoon meditates "somewhat like a boy who has caught a fish too big to carry home." The image is disconcerting — the enemy sniper reduced to a trophy, the act of killing domesticated into a boyhood memory of fishing. The sniper, so terrifying when invisible, becomes merely a body when seen.

## Sniping and the Moral Imagination

Sassoon's treatment of sniping is characteristically ambivalent. He records the practical reality of the threat without either glorifying or condemning the sniper's craft. The sniper is simply a fact of trench warfare, as inescapable as mud or rain.

Yet the silence around the emotional cost of killing a specific, identified human being is itself a moral statement. There are no triumphal accounts of "getting" an enemy sniper in Sassoon's narrative. The sniper remains at the periphery of vision, a threat acknowledged but never dwelt upon.

In the end, a wounded man's cry cuts through the combat narrative: "Don't go out, Dicky; they snipe like hell!" — the sniper's bullet reducing all strategy to a friend's desperate warning. The sniper kills, the friend calls out, and the war continues.
