**Worgan** is a private in C Company of the Second Battalion, Flintshire Fusiliers — one of the seasoned soldiers whose toughness Sherston relies upon in the tense days before the Somme offensive. Though he appears only briefly in the memoir, his presence illuminates the texture of front-line camaraderie: the unspoken partnership between an officer and the men who do the hardest physical work of war.

## In the Memoir

### The Night Wire-Cutting

Worgan first appears during the night expedition to cut the German wire. Sherston's party, working in darkness under shellfire, dwindles as men are wounded or lose heart. By the first greying of dawn only three remain: Sherston, [[kendle|Kendle]], and Worgan — "slicing away for all they were worth" [007-013]. Worgan's endurance, together with Kendle's youthful cheerfulness, forms the human core of an otherwise anonymous and thankless task. The scene captures the memoir's characteristic register: the officer's voice observing with precise affection the men who make his own limited heroism possible.

### The Daylight Expedition

The following day, when Sherston decides to return to the wire in daylight — a reckless but necessary act — it is Worgan who stays behind with him while the rest of the company moves to the battle-assembly position [012-010]. "It was queer to be in an empty front-line trench on a fine morning," Sherston writes, "with everything quite peaceful after a violent early bombardment. Queerer still to be creeping about in the long grass ... and shearing savagely at the tangles." Worgan's laconic verdict on their work — "we were giving it a proper haircut this journey" — supplies the memoir with one of its rare moments of soldiers' speech: blunt, understated, and darkly comic.

### Characterisation

Worgan is introduced with deliberate brevity: "one of the tough characters of our company" [012-011]. Unlike [[kendle|Kendle]], whose boyish face and jaunty cigarette-smoking are rendered with the detail of portraiture, Worgan remains a figure defined by action rather than description. He is the type of the experienced soldier — competent, uncomplaining, dependable — whose toughness is less a personality trait than a survival strategy. In the memoir's economy of characterisation, Worgan and Kendle form a complementary pair: youth and experience, exuberance and stolidity, the face the war will break and the face that has already learned to endure.

## The Officer-Private Bond

The relationship between Sherston and Worgan is one of the memoir's quiet revelations about military life. There is no sentimentality in their interaction — no speeches about duty or comradeship — only the shared work of cutting wire in a trench that could come under fire at any moment. When Sherston notes that "much depended on my efforts. Worgan stayed behind with me" [012-012], the parallel construction is telling: the officer's efforts and the private's presence are placed on equal syntactic footing, each man's contribution essential to the other's survival. The class hierarchy of the British Army — officer and private, gentleman and working man — is momentarily suspended by the shared danger of the task.

This is the memoir's method in miniature: Sassoon does not preach about the equality of men under fire; he simply records that Worgan stayed behind, and lets the reader draw the conclusion. The officer-private bond, in Sassoon's rendering, is not a matter of ideology but of proximity — two men in a trench, cutting wire, with no one else to rely on.
