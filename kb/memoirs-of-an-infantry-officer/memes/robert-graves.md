# Robert Graves

The poet and fellow officer whose friendship with [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] spanned the pre-war years, the trenches of [[france|the Western Front]], and the crisis of 1917 — fictionalized in the memoir as [[david-cromlech|David Cromlech]], and the man who intervened to prevent [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s court-martial.

## Role & Identity

Robert Graves (1895–1985) was a poet, novelist, and scholar who served as a captain in the [[royal-welch-fusiliers|Royal Welch Fusiliers]] — the same regiment as [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]. The two met in 1915 and formed a close literary friendship that survived the war. Graves was badly wounded on the Somme in 1916 and reported dead in *The Times*, but survived a lung wound.

In the memoir, Graves appears as [[david-cromlech|David Cromlech]], a "big and impulsive" officer whose intelligence sets him apart from the "mechanical" outlook of most fellow officers [016-005]. The fictionalization is thin: [[david-cromlech|Cromlech]] is recognizably Graves.

## In the Memoir

[[david-cromlech|Cromlech]]/Graves first appears at a midnight meeting on the eve of [[bazentin-ridge|Bazentin]] — [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] hears his "desperately cheerful ejaculations" and they talk about [[england|England]] and "the wonderful things we'd do after the war" [016-005]. The scene is elegiac: "We knew that this might be our last meeting" [016-005].

When [[david-cromlech|Cromlech]] is reported killed at High Wood, [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s grief is "stupefying" but deferred: "there was no spare time for personal grief when the Battalion was getting ready to move back to Divisional Rest" [018-002]. The news of his survival — "my friend [[david-cromlech|Cromlech]] had risen again from the dead" — brings rare joy [020-001].

The two reunite at [[craiglockhart|Craiglockhart]], where [[david-cromlech|Cromlech]] plays football "in spite of having had a bit of shell through his right lung" [023-003]. His most consequential moment is in Liverpool, finding [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] in "stubborn apathy": "Thank God you've come!" [046-009].

The Colonel remarked "that young [[david-cromlech|Cromlech]] threw his tongue a hell of a lot too much" but was "agreeably surprised to find that he isn't windy in trenches" [016-008].

## Historical Counterpart

Graves intervened decisively after [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s 1917 protest, persuading the medical board that [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] was suffering from [[shell-shock|shell shock]] rather than political sedition. His own memoir, *Goodbye to All That* (1929), was published a year before [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s — the two books form a dialogue about the war.

The friendship was complex: literary rivalry and mutual admiration coexisted. Graves's fictional counterpart [[david-cromlech|David Cromlech]] is drawn with affectionate exasperation — "a positive expert at putting people's backs up unintentionally" [016-008].

## Significance

Graves represents friendship as survival in the memoir. Where institutions — Army, medical board, press — are hostile to individual truth, the bond between [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] and Graves cuts through. His intervention at the medical board was an act of literary-historical consequence: without it, [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] might have been imprisoned, and this memoir might never have been written.

## Quotations
