# Ration

## Overview

**Rations** in Sassoon's memoir are the food supplies that sustained the infantry battalion — bully beef, bread, tea, bacon, and the tot of rum that was the soldier's daily comfort. The ration system was the logistical backbone of trench life, and the arrival of the rations was one of the few predictable pleasures of the front. For Sassoon, rations appear as a constant background detail — the "dixies of hot tea" [014-008] after an attack, the Quartermaster-sergeant who "ruled the ration-dump" [006-004], and the rum ration that lived under Barton's bed [009-001].

## The Ration Dump

The ration dump was a small empire ruled by the Quartermaster-sergeant: "the Quartermaster-sergeant ruled the ration-dump, and every evening Dottrell arrived with the ration-limbers" [006-004]. The arrival of the rations was an event — the limbers coming up from the Transport Lines with food, water, and mail. The ration dump was the place where the battalion's physical needs were met, the interface between the fighting troops and the supply chain that stretched back to England.

The sound of rations arriving was part of the front-line soundscape: "noise of carts coming along with rations. Occasional bang of our guns" [006-007] — the mundane and the military interleaved, the cart wheels and the artillery in the same auditory frame.

## The Rum Ration

The rum ration was the most cherished of all supplies. It appears in the memoir as an almost sacramental detail: "he came down for the rum ration. The rum-jar lived under Barton's bed" [009-001]. The ritual was precise: "having been poured into some tin receptacle, the rum was carried cautiously upstairs to be tipped into the men's tea-dixies" [009-001]. The care of the transfer — cautiously, avoiding spillage of the precious liquid — is a miniature drama of trench economy.

Rum was distributed before attacks and after exposure — it was both Dutch courage and restorative. Sassoon mentions it without moralizing, presenting the rum ration as a fact of trench existence that everyone accepted. "Dixies of hot tea, and the rum issue, made all the difference to our outlook" [014-008] — the combination of tea and rum, heat and alcohol, was the post-battle restorative that brought the battalion back to life.

## Emergency Rations

Before the raid on Kiel Trench, Sassoon distributes emergency rations: "their emergency rations consisted of two tins of bully beef" [012-007]. The emergency ration was the soldier's last resort — food to sustain him if cut off or stranded in no-man's-land. The detail is practical and grim: two tins of bully beef were all that stood between a man and starvation in the shell-holes.

Bully beef — tinned corned beef — was the universal food of the British Army. It appears throughout the memoir as an unglamorous constant, the taste of army life. Soldiers joked about it, depended on it, and grew sick of it in equal measure.

## Rations as Comfort

After the Somme attack, the rations arrive as comfort: "Dixies of hot tea, and the rations" [014-008]. The hot tea is mentioned first — it was the British soldier's supreme comfort, more important than solid food. Tea was brewed whenever possible, carried forward in dixies (large metal containers), and distributed to men who had been fighting, marching, or standing in water for hours. "Flook was ready with some hot tea" [007-014] when Sassoon returned to his billet — the servant's first duty was the production of tea.

The bivouac fires of the Somme rear area were primarily tea-making stations: "Thin smoke rose from the little bivouac fires which had been used for tea making" [014-003]. The smoke of tea-fires is one of the memoir's gentler images — small domestic acts performed in the shadow of battle.

Sassoon notes wryly that soldiers got fat on their rations: "Soldiers got fat on their good rations at the Front" [019-002]. The claim is partly ironic — the British Army was better fed than the civilian poor at home — but it also reflects the reality that food was one thing the Army did well. The contrast between well-fed soldiers and the horror of their circumstances is one of the memoir's recurring ironies.

## Food as Memory of Home

Food in the memoir is often a vehicle for nostalgia. Sassoon's last dinner before returning to France — "a dinner which included two of my favourite puddings" [010-004] — is a ritual of departure, the domestic table a place of farewell. The meal is followed by cribbage with Aunt Evelyn and the grey parrot brought up from the kitchen: the food, the game, the pet — the whole apparatus of home concentrated into a final evening.

Barton provides the memoir's most explicit contrast between trench food and civilian luxury. "This time three nights ago my wife and I were having dinner at the Café Royal. Upstairs at the Café Royal — best food in London, and as good as ever even now" [009-002]. The Café Royal dinner, remembered from a dugout, is a talisman of the world left behind — the best food in London existing simultaneously with bully beef in a trench.

The battalion's servants competed to provide comfort through food: "they were competing for the favours of their masters" [010-011] — the officers' mess was a small hierarchy of cooks and batmen, the quality of a dinner a matter of regimental pride. "A rich restaurant dinner followed by a mechanical drawing-room comedy" [010-007] was Sassoon's London leave — the food marking the transition from trench to civilization and back again.

## The Ration Party

The ration party was a regular institution — men detailed to carry food forward from the Transport Lines to the front. Dottrell "appeared with the ration-limbers" [006-004] each evening, a reliable presence. The ration party was also a source of news: "Dottrell and the ration-party had returned from their journey with much information" [016-006] — the men who carried the food also carried the rumors and reports from the rear.
