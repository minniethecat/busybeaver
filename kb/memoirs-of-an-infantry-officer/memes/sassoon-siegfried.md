# Siegfried Sassoon

The author-narrator of *Memoirs of an Infantry Officer* — poet, [[royal-welch-fusiliers|Royal Welch Fusiliers]] officer, and the consciousness through which every scene in the memoir is filtered, from the trenches of the Somme to the [[protest|anti-war protest]] of 1917.

## Role & Identity

Siegfried Sassoon (1886–1967) was a poet who enlisted in the British Army at the outbreak of the First World War and served as a lieutenant in the [[royal-welch-fusiliers|Royal Welch Fusiliers]]. By 1917 his war poetry and his public protest against the war's prolongation had made him one of the most controversial figures in the British Army.

In the memoir, Sassoon narrates in the first person but uses the fictional name "George Sherston" — a device that creates distance between the experiencing soldier and the remembering author. The narrative voice shifts between the naive enthusiasm of 1915 and the bitter clarity of 1917, tracking a mind that outgrows the language of patriotism and duty.

## In the Memoir

The narrator's arc moves from unquestioning participation to moral crisis. Early chapters portray a young officer absorbed in the routines of trench life — [[fox-hunting|hunting]], riding, the camaraderie of the mess. The death of friends, particularly [[david-cromlech|David Cromlech]] (reported killed, then miraculously alive), and the carnage of the Somme gradually erode his belief in the war's conduct [018-002][020-001].

The memoir's climactic action is the narrator's protest — a written declaration that the war is "being deliberately prolonged by those who have the power to end it" — and his subsequent removal to [[craiglockhart|Craiglockhart]] under the care of [[w-h-r-rivers|W. H. R. Rivers]] [044-003][046-009]. The protest is both the memoir's political centre and a deeply personal crisis: Sassoon spends his final pre-[[craiglockhart|Craiglockhart]] days in a Liverpool hotel, isolated between the Army that wants to silence him and the public that cannot hear him.

## Historical Counterpart

Sassoon is, of course, the historical author himself. The fictional framing — calling himself "George Sherston" — is the memoir's most significant literary device. It allows Sassoon to treat his younger self as a character: to observe his own courage, vanity, and moral awakening with the detachment of a novelist. The real Sassoon survived the war, continued to publish poetry, and lived until 1967. The memoir, published in 1930, is a work of mature recollection, not immediate testimony.

## Significance

Sassoon's significance in his own memoir is as a test case for the question the book poses: what does a decent man do when his country asks him to kill for a cause he no longer believes in? His answer — protest, then return to his men — is unresolved, and the memoir does not pretend otherwise. The [[courage]] page traces how Sassoon's conception of courage evolves from physical [[courage|bravery]] to moral conviction; his own story is the memoir's longest and most searching illustration of that evolution.

## Others' View of Sassoon

Sassoon the narrator observes everyone; the memoir records, implicitly and explicitly, how others observe him in return:

- **[[david-cromlech|David Cromlech]]'s view**: The older, more worldly friend who treats Sassoon with a mixture of affection and exasperation — admiring his bravery while gently mocking his earnestness. Cromlech's letters, quoted in the memoir, convey the tone of a man who knows Sassoon better than Sassoon knows himself [012-002][015-006].
- **[[w-h-r-rivers|W. H. R. Rivers]]'s view**: The professional observer who diagnoses Sassoon's condition not as cowardice but as an extreme form of the same strain every frontline officer endures. Rivers treats his patient with respect, recognising that Sassoon's protest, however politically inconvenient, is rooted in genuine moral conviction rather than pathology [046-003].
- **[[kinjack|Colonel Kinjack]]'s view**: The commanding officer who sees Sassoon as a capable if somewhat restless young officer — an asset to the battalion when his energies are channelled into action (the trench raid) but a potential problem when his restlessness turns toward questioning the war itself [005-008][009-003].
- **[[barton|Barton]]'s view**: The company commander who sees Sassoon as one of his reliable junior officers — someone to entrust with a raiding party, whose competence he takes for granted even as Sassoon's inner life grows steadily further from the assumptions of military duty [007-004].
- **[[aunt-evelyn|Aunt Evelyn]]'s view**: The civilian who sees the young man she loves, not the soldier — who sends kippers and worries and reads the communiqués, unable to perceive the chasm between her nephew's inner experience and the version of the war reported in the newspapers [007-015][010-001].

## Authorial Technique

Sassoon's self-portrait draws on the full range of his literary skill:

- **The split self**: By creating the "George Sherston" persona, Sassoon achieves a double perspective — the younger man's immediate experience and the older author's retrospective judgment. The tension between these two selves generates the memoir's characteristic irony: the narrator who describes his own courage as vanity, his own patriotism as delusion, without ever quite disowning either [001-001].
- **Understatement as moral force**: Sassoon's method is almost never to rage. His most devastating criticisms of the war are delivered in the mildest tones — a description of a communiqué read over breakfast toast, a quiet record of a friend's depression after leave. The restraint makes the anger more powerful by refusing to discharge it [007-015][009-001].
- **The comic register**: Throughout the memoir, Sassoon uses humour — often self-deprecating — as a structural counterpoint to horror. The kippers from Aunt Evelyn, the farcical episode of Kinjack's salmon, the absurdities of Army bureaucracy: these comic interludes do not dilute the memoir's seriousness but deepen it, by showing the full range of a mind under pressure [003-005][010-008].
- **Absence as presence**: The memoir's most significant characters often exist through their letters rather than direct scenes — [[joe-dottrell|Dottrell]], [[david-cromlech|David Cromlech]], Aunt Evelyn. This technique captures the essential condition of trench warfare: the people who matter most are precisely the ones who are not there. The letters that arrive in the line are the memoir's most reliable vehicles of emotion [020-008][038-018].
