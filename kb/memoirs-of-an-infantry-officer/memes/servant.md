# Servant

A military servant, or batman, was a soldier assigned to an officer to handle domestic duties — kit maintenance, meals, errands — allowing the officer to focus on command responsibilities. In Sassoon's memoir, the servant-officer relationship reveals the class structure of the British Army and the quiet humanity that persisted within it.

## Definition & Context

In the British Army of the Great War, every officer was entitled to a servant (batman) drawn from the ranks. The batman was responsible for the officer's kit, meals, quarters, and personal needs — a direct inheritance from the Victorian class system that structured military life. While officially a military duty, the role inherently mirrored the master-servant relationships of Edwardian civilian society. The batman occupied an ambiguous position: formally a combat soldier, practically a domestic attendant, and often a confidant by proximity.

## In the Memoir

Sassoon's primary servant throughout the narrative is Flook, introduced early in the memoir:

> My servant Flook hoisted up my bulging valise, wiped his red face with his sleeve, and followed me to the roof. "Mind and keep Mr. Sherston well polished up and punctual on parade, Flook!" Flook grinned; and away we went. [002-002]

Flook is a constant, unobtrusive presence — carrying hampers, managing kit, and serving as a touchstone of ordinary loyalty amid the dislocation of war.

The domestic rhythm maintained by servants is captured in billets behind the lines: "the candles were lit, there was a smell of cooking, and the servants were clattering tin plates in the sizzling kitchen" [005-004]. Servants created islands of domestic order within the military machine, their work forming the invisible infrastructure of officer life.

The emotional register of servants shifts with the proximity of battle. Before a major action, Sassoon notes:

> The behaviour of our servants expressed it; they were competing for the favours of a handsome young woman in the farmhouse, and a comedy of primitive courtship was being enacted in the kitchen. Death would be lying in wait for us. [010-011]

This human comedy plays out against the backdrop of impending death — the servants' vitality a counterpoint to what awaits.

Later, convalescing at Nutwood Manor, Sassoon encounters the civilian equivalent: "A reassuring manservant conducted me upstairs. My room was called 'The Clematis Room'" [038-001]. The quiet efficiency of the servant class bridges military and civilian worlds.

The most poignant moment comes when Sassoon is wounded and his servant accompanies him: "My servant went with me, carrying my haversack. He was a quiet clumsy middle-aged man who always did his best and never complained" [034-019]. After their parting, a letter from his servant — thanking him for a photograph and gramophone — prompts Sassoon to reflect:

> I looked at another letter. It was from my servant (to whom I'd sent a photograph of myself and a small gramophone). "Thank you very much for the photo, which is like life itself, and the men in the Company say it is just like him. The gramophone is much enjoyed by all." What could one do about it? Nothing short of stopping the War could alter the inadequacy of kippers and gramophones or sustain my sense of unity with the man who had written that letter. [038-020]

## Sassoon's Perspective

Sassoon's treatment of servants is characteristically understated. He does not sentimentalise the relationship, nor does he critique it in overtly political terms. Instead, he records the servant's presence with quiet respect — Flook's grin, the clumsy middle-aged batman who "never complained," the letter of thanks. The servant becomes a moral witness: someone who serves without pretension, whose loyalty exposes the absurdity of the system that demands it. Sassoon's anti-war consciousness registers the gap between what servants give and what the war gives back.

## Historical Reality

The batman system was universal in the British Army of 1914–1918. Each infantry battalion had an establishment of officers each entitled to a servant. The role was not voluntary — soldiers were assigned — and the batman was expected to accompany his officer into action. In practice, the relationship varied enormously: some officers treated their batmen as personal valets; others, like Sassoon, maintained a more egalitarian soldierly respect. The system persisted through WWII and was only formally abolished in the postwar reforms of the British Army.
