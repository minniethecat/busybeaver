# Inspection

## Overview

**Inspection** was one of the fundamental rituals of British Army life in Sassoon's memoir — a daily performance of order imposed upon the chaos of the Western Front. From rifle inspection at stand-to to the Commander-in-Chief's review of massed horse lines, inspection served to maintain discipline, enforce standards, and assert the presence of military authority even in the most degraded conditions.

## Stand-To and Rifle Inspection

The daily cycle of trench life was punctuated by the twin ceremonies of dawn and dusk stand-to, each accompanied by rifle inspection. Sassoon records the routine: "On my way to the dugout, to fetch my revolver and attend the twilight ceremony of stand-to and rifle inspection" [009-006]. The phrase "twilight ceremony" captures the ritualistic quality — inspection was not merely practical but almost liturgical, a reassuring repetition in a world of uncertainty.

In the aftermath of battle, inspection was one of the few occupations that restored order: "Rifle-cleaning and inspection was the only occupation possible" [032-003]. The men were "blear-eyed" and "fed-up and far from home," but the inspection imposed a minimum standard of military routine.

## The Commander-in-Chief's Inspection

At the Army School, Sassoon witnessed inspection on a grand scale: "Hundreds of light and heavy draft horses were drawn up along a road for an inspection by the Commander-in-Chief (a bodily presence which the infantry mind could not easily imagine)" [003-004]. The parenthetical aside is telling — for the infantry officer in the trenches, the high command was an abstraction, its "bodily presence" almost inconceivable.

## Kit Inspection and the March

Before battalion moves, inspection took on a practical urgency. Sassoon describes his formal inspection of the men before Barton emerged "with bulging pockets, his burly figure hung like a Christmas tree with haversack, water-bottle, revolver, field-glasses, gas-mask, map-case, and other oddments" [012-002]. The comic image of Barton festooned with equipment contrasts with the seriousness of the inspection ritual that preceded it.

On the march, inspection turned to the men's feet — "the most important part of them." Sassoon made his "sympathetic inspection of sores and blisters" [031-023], the adjective "sympathetic" distinguishing the officer who cared for his men from the mere enforcer of regulations. The old soldiers "grinned at me philosophically, puffing their Woodbines" [031-023], while the new recruits "stared up at me with stupid trusting eyes."

## Medical Inspection

In the hospital ward, inspection took a gentler form: "The doctor came along the ward on his cheerful morning inspection" [018-007]. The word "cheerful" transforms the military ritual into something humane — the doctor's inspection was concerned with healing, not discipline.

## The Social Function of Inspection

Beyond its practical purpose, inspection served a social function. It was a moment when officers and men confronted one another formally, when the hierarchy was made visible and reaffirmed. For Sassoon, who was "too easygoing with the men when we were out of the Line" [009-003], the formalities of inspection may have chafed, but he recognized their role in maintaining the structure upon which the battalion depended.
