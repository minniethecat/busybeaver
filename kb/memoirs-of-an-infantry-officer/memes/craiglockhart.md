# Craiglockhart War Hospital

The military hospital near Edinburgh, originally a hydropathic establishment, converted to treat officers suffering from shell shock and [[shell-shock|neurasthenia]] — the institution where [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] was sent after his [[protest|anti-war protest]] and where medical diagnosis became an alternative to court-martial.

## Geography & Context

Craiglockhart is situated on a hill overlooking Edinburgh, in a substantial Victorian building originally constructed in 1880 as a hydropathic hotel. During the war, it was requisitioned by the Royal Army Medical Corps as a Specialist [[hospital|Hospital]] for Officers, specializing in the treatment of war neuroses — what was then called "shell shock" or "[[shell-shock|neurasthenia]]."

The hospital's location — in Edinburgh, far from the front — was itself therapeutic: removal from the war zone was considered part of the cure. But the distance also meant that patients were isolated from the war that had broken them, suspended between front and [[england|home]], neither soldiers nor civilians.

## In the Memoir

Craiglockhart appears in the memoir's final chapters as the institutional space where [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s protest is processed, classified, and neutralized. Instead of facing court-martial for his "Soldier's Declaration," [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] found himself classified as a medical case — "shell-shock" — and packed off to Scotland, a destination that simultaneously acknowledged his suffering and defused his political challenge [045-001]. The hospital is described with a mixture of appreciation for its medical staff (particularly W.H.R. [[w-h-r-rivers|Rivers]]) and ironic awareness of its institutional function: to return officers to the front [045-012].

The hospital is also the setting for the memoir's most famous literary encounter: [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s meeting with Wilfred Owen, a fellow patient who would become one of the great war poets. Owen showed [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] his poems; [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] encouraged him; the literary history that resulted is one of the war's few creative legacies [045-012].

## Military Significance

Craiglockhart's military significance is institutional rather than tactical. It represents the army's evolving response to psychological casualties — a recognition that "shell shock" was a real condition requiring medical treatment, but also a mechanism for managing dissent (as in [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s case) and for returning men to duty.

## Atmosphere & Description

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s Craiglockhart is a place of suspended time. Officers play golf, read, talk — normal activities rendered strange by their context. The hospital's Victorian architecture — designed for health and leisure — becomes a setting for the treatment of industrial warfare's psychological casualties, an irony [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] registers without needing to state.
