The **regiment** in Sassoon's memoir is more than a military unit; it is a social world — a miniature of the English class system transported to the Western Front, complete with its own traditions, hierarchies, and codes of belonging. Throughout *Memoirs of an Infantry Officer*, the regiment functions as both a source of identity and a site of tension: the thing Sherston belongs to, the thing he is proud of, and the thing whose values he ultimately cannot reconcile with what he has seen.

## In the Memoir

### Regimental Badges and the Map of England

The memoir's first extended meditation on the regiment occurs at the Army School, where Sherston attends lectures alongside officers from across the British Army. "Every afternoon at half-past five the School assembled to listen to a lecture," he writes. "Eyeing an audience of about 300 officers and N.C.O.s, I improved my knowledge of regimental badges, which seemed somehow to affect the personality of the wearer. A lion, a lamb, a dragon or an antelope, a crown, a harp, a tiger or a sphinx, these devices differentiated men in more ways than one" [001-006]. The regimental badges become a map of England itself: "the difference between Durham and Devon for instance." The regiment, in this vision, is a form of local identity — county loyalty projected onto the battlefield.

### The Pre-War Regiment

The pre-war regiment, glimpsed through the recollections of older officers like [[barton|Barton]], was a world apart from the mass army of the Western Front. Barton recalls "how he narrowly escaped being reduced to the ranks for 'making a book'" — running an informal betting operation — "when the Battalion was stationed in Ireland before the war" [014-004]. "There were some fine riders in the regiment then," he adds; "they talked and thought about nothing but hunting, racing, and polo." The pre-war regiment was defined by horsemanship, sporting prowess, and a gentleman's code — qualifications rendered irrelevant by industrialised warfare. "It was lucky for some of us," Barton concludes, "that horsemanship wasn't needed for winning the war, since most mounted officers now looked as if they were either rowing a boat or riding a bicycle uphill."

### Regimental Snobbery and the Temporary Officer

The regiment's traditional identity could curdle into exclusion. [[holman|Holman]], a Sandhurst-trained officer, dismisses temporary officers who "wouldn't have been wanted in the Regiment in peacetime" [017-001]. The distinction between Regular and Temporary officers — between those who belonged to the regiment by right and those who merely served in it by necessity — is one of the memoir's quiet social observations. Sherston himself, though a temporary officer, has earned his place through service at the front; but the regimental hierarchy, with its insistence on pre-war credentials, represents the persistence of peacetime values in a war that has rendered them obsolete.

### The Regiment as Belonging

Yet the memoir is not simply a critique of regimental culture. Sherston's own identity is inseparable from his regiment. When he meets anti-war activists in London, he finds he "was unable to dislike being a Flintshire Fusilier" [042-003]. The regiment, for all its limitations, is the community in which he has experienced the most intense solidarity of his life. His protest against the war is made not in spite of his regimental identity but through it — as a soldier speaking on behalf of soldiers, using the authority conferred by his service to denounce what that service has become.

### The Regiment and the Individual

The memoir's treatment of the regiment reflects a larger tension in Sassoon's war writing: the relationship between collective identity and individual conscience. The regiment demands loyalty, conformity, the subordination of private doubt to public duty. Sherston's eventual protest — his "independent action" — is a break with regimental discipline that is also, paradoxically, an affirmation of what the regiment at its best represents: courage, honesty, the refusal to deceive. "I am a soldier," he writes in his statement, "who believes he is acting on behalf of soldiers" [041-003]. The regiment has taught him how to belong; his protest teaches him how to dissent.
