# The Unconservative Weekly

A fictional weekly journal of liberal opinion, edited by the journalist Markington, that appears in the later chapters of [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s memoir as the embodiment of the educated civilian establishment's carefully modulated critique of the war — a critique that the narrator both respects for its intelligence and finds inadequate to the moral urgency of his own position.

## About the Work

The Unconservative Weekly is a satirical invention — a composite of the various liberal and radical periodicals that constituted the intellectual opposition to the war in [[england|Britain]] during 1916–1918. Its name parodies the real "Conservative Weekly" and similar establishment publications, while its editorial stance — critical of the war's conduct but not of the war itself — reflects the position of journals like the *Nation* and the *Manchester Guardian* during the period of [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s protest.

The journal is edited by Markington, a journalist described as possessing "the omniscience of this ably conducted organ" — a phrase whose ironic edge cuts both ways, acknowledging the journal's competence while mocking its pretensions to comprehensive understanding of events it observed from a safe distance [037-002]. Markington himself is portrayed as a figure of the literary-journalistic world that [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]], as a published poet, moved in during his periods of convalescence in [[england|England]].

## In the Memoir

The Unconservative Weekly appears during the London episodes of the memoir's later chapters — the period following [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s wound at Arras, when he was convalescing and beginning to formulate the critique of the war that would culminate in his public protest. The journal functions as a marker of the civilian intellectual milieu into which [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] was being drawn, a world of editorial offices, club lunches, and reasoned argument that stood in stark contrast to the trenches [037-002][040-001].

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s treatment of the journal and its editor is characteristically ambivalent. He lunches with Markington at his club, reads the "editorial paragraphs" with the attention of a fellow writer, and acknowledges the journal's intellectual seriousness. But he also notes that "the columns of the Unconservative Weekly seemed less stimulating than usual" — a moment of deflation that registers the growing gap between civilian political discourse and the experiential knowledge of the front [037-006].

The journal's most significant narrative function is as a potential vehicle for [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s protest. Markington is "the most likely man to help" when [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] considers publishing his anti-war statement — a connection that places the Unconservative Weekly at the threshold between private conviction and public action [040-009]. The journal thus represents the institutional machinery of dissent: the presses, the editorial offices, the networks of influence through which protest must pass to become effective.

## Thematic Significance

The Unconservative Weekly serves the memoir as a symbol of the limits of rational opposition to the war. Its editorials criticise the war's conduct — its "bellicose patriotism" is distinguished from the *[[morning-post|Morning Post]]*'s more strident nationalism — but the criticism remains within the framework of acceptable political discourse [037-002]. The journal represents the best that civilian liberalism could offer, and [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s portrayal suggests that the best was not enough.

This is one of the memoir's most politically sophisticated moves. Rather than simply attacking jingoistic newspapers (a target too easy to be interesting), [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] trains his irony on the more ambiguous figure of the liberal critic — the intelligent, well-meaning civilian whose opposition to the war never quite reaches the point of radical refusal. The Unconservative Weekly is not the enemy; it is, more troublingly, an ally whose limitations illuminate the narrator's own more extreme position.

The journal also functions as a marker of class and education. Its readership is the educated middle and upper classes — the people who run the country, who write the editorials, who debate the war in terms of policy and principle rather than in terms of the bodies that policy and principle consume. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s own class position places him among these readers, and part of the memoir's drama is his progressive alienation from the discursive world they inhabit.
