# Bazentin

The village and ridge in the Somme sector where [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s battalion was deployed during and after the [[bazentin-ridge|Battle of Bazentin Ridge]] in July 1916 — a place that stands in the memoir for the greatest concentration of violence the narrator experienced firsthand, and for the emotional exhaustion that followed it.

## Geography & Context

Bazentin-le-Petit is a village in the Somme département, approximately 7 kilometers east-northeast of Albert. The Bazentin Ridge, a low rise of open ground, was the objective of the British attack of 14 July 1916 — a dawn assault that achieved initial success but bogged down into the attritional fighting characteristic of the Somme. The memoir describes the approach to the ridge across "open ground sloped treeless to Bazentin Ridge, dull green and striped with chalky trenches" [014-003].

The village and ridge had been in German hands since 1914. Their capture was one of the few clear successes of the Somme offensive's middle phase, though like all Somme successes, it came at a price the memoir refuses to quantify — because the price, it implies, was not worth the gain.

## In the Memoir

Bazentin enters the narrative as the destination for Sassoon's battalion after the initial July fighting: "My battalion was engaged in the Battle of Bazentin Ridge" [016-003]. The engagement is recorded in Sassoon's characteristically understated register — "My boredom was combined with an inapprehensive sense of what we were in for" — the casual tone a form of retrospective discipline, the narrator refusing to dramatize what was, in fact, dramatic.

The fighting at Bazentin produces the memoir's heaviest casualty figures: "More than a hundred casualties up at Bazentin" [018-002]. The number is delivered flat, without exclamation, the memoir's narrative strategy being to let the figure speak for itself. Sassoon's own reflection — "How were things going at Bazentin, I wondered. And should I be glad to be out of it?" [016-011] — is one of the memoir's frankest admissions of the infantry officer's conflicted desire to be elsewhere.

The return journey to Bazentin is described with characteristic sensory precision: the battalion, "diminished" by casualties, marches up through the communication trenches as the sound of shelling grows closer, the landscape transitioning from the rear area's relative normality to the devastation of the front.

## Military Significance

Bazentin Ridge was a tactically important feature on the Somme battlefield, its capture opening the way toward High Wood and the German second line. The 14 July attack — often called the Battle of Bazentin Ridge — was one of the most carefully planned British operations of the Somme, employing a dawn attack that caught the Germans by surprise. The initial success, however, could not be exploited, and the fighting settled into the pattern of incremental advance and heavy loss that characterized the entire offensive.

## Atmosphere & Description

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s Bazentin is a place of aftermath. The fighting has moved on, but its evidence is everywhere: the dead unburied, the trenches half-demolished, the "stench of the battlefield" that the memoir's more fastidious passages prefer to leave unnamed. The weather — a Sassoon preoccupation — is hot July, the flies worse than the shelling, the men drinking from water bottles that taste of petrol.
