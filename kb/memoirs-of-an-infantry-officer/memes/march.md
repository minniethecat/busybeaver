# March

## Overview

**March** in Sassoon's memoir refers to the long-distance movement of infantry battalions on foot between billets, camps, and the front line. The march was the default mode of transport for the infantry soldier — a physical ordeal of heat, dust, and fatigue that occupied the interstices between battles. For Sassoon, the march is both a military routine and a sensory experience, its rhythms shaping the memoir's geography of the Western Front.

## The Physical Experience

### Body and Equipment

The march was a bodily experience before it was a military one. Sassoon's attention to sensory detail is sharpest in march passages: "the aspens by the river were shivering and showing the whites of their leaves, and it was goodbye to their cool showery sound when we marched away in our own dust at four o'clock on a glaring bright afternoon" [016-001]. The farewell to the aspens — to the cool, living, green world — is also a farewell to the rear area, the march taking the battalion toward the treeless forward zone.

Sassoon's marching boots were a personal talisman: "I was wearing my best friends, a pair of greased marching boots whose supple strength had never failed to keep the water out" [030-002]. The boots were a soldier's most intimate equipment — the interface between body and road — and Sassoon's affection for them is entirely practical: "how much those boots meant to me can only be understood by persons who have never had to march far in bad weather" [030-002].

### Heat and Fatigue

Heat was the march's constant antagonist. "By midday the march had become tedious; the road was dusty, the sun glared down on us, and I was occupied in preventing exhausted men from falling out" [031-023] — the officer's responsibility was not only to march but to keep his men marching. The Companies marched "with fifty yard intervals between them" [031-024], a special order that stretched the column out along the road.

The physical condition of the men was always visible during a march. After the Somme, Sassoon observed "the trudging figures in full marching order; and how difficult they found it to keep their heads up" [010-009]. The weight of equipment — rifles, packs, ammunition — turned the march into an endurance test even before combat began.

## Weather and Seasons on the March

The memoir's marches span all seasons. The Somme retreat was in summer heat and dust: "a glaring bright afternoon" [016-001] in July. The advance toward Arras began "in cold bright weather" [031-022] and ended in snow at Mercatel [032-002]. The greatcoats "had been left behind and we had nothing but what we stood up in" [032-001] — the march into battle stripped of even the comfort of warm clothing.

Rain transformed the march into a struggle against mud as well as distance. The physical misery of a wet march — boots sodden, packs heavier — was a different order of suffering from the dusty marches of summer. Sassoon does not dwell on wet marches at length, but the condition is implicit in his attention to boots and weather.

## Route Marches and Distances

### Battalion Movements

The battalion moved by foot between every location in the memoir. Marching was the connective tissue of the war experience. Sassoon records marches of various lengths and conditions: "a nine mile march" [016-002] after leaving Heilly, "four easy miles to a village" [018-001] following his return from leave, and the twelve-mile retreat after the Somme: "marched back twelve miles to peace and luxury" [014-023].

### Specific Routes

Sassoon's diary preserves exact details of particular marches. After leaving Heilly, he records "that nine mile march" [016-002] — the precision of distance a soldier's habit. The retreat from the Somme was "twelve miles to peace and safety" [014-023], the relief of reaching the rear measured in miles walked.

The advance toward Arras involved a sequence of marches that Sassoon documents with a diarist's attention to place-names. The battalion marched from Basseux [031-038], through "a monotonous eight-mile stretch of the main road from Amiens to St. Pol" [031-024], and onward toward Doullens and Arras. A local woman told Sassoon "in broken English that troops had been going through for fifteen days, never stopping more than one night and always marching toward Doullens and Arras" [031-023] — the march was not only the battalion's experience but the condition of the entire army in motion.

The march to the Arras front ended in snow: "The snow had stopped when, after marching eight miles, we bivouacked in the dregs of daylight by a sunken road near Mercatel" [032-002]. The eight-mile march in winter conditions, without greatcoats, was a prelude to the battle.

## March Past and Military Ceremony

The ceremonial march past appears at the memoir's opening, when Sassoon's battalion marches past the Army Commander: "Our exertions ended with a march past the Army Commander" [003-007]. The march past is a display of discipline and collective identity, the battalion presenting itself to the authority that will direct its fate.

During the advance toward Arras, a similar ceremony recurs when the battalion marches past General Allenby: "The Battle of Arras, and we who marched past him had an excellent chance of finding glory" [032-003]. The irony is palpable — the march past is a ritual of display, but the men who perform it are marching toward their deaths.

Sassoon also remembers the march past in civilian life: "I remembered the Volunteers whose torchlight march-past had made such a glowing impression on my nursery window" [043-004] — the military spectacle seen through a child's eyes, now hollowed out by experience of the real thing.

## The March to the Front

The march toward the front line carried a particular psychological weight. Sassoon captures the mounting tension of approach: "Stage by stage, we had marched to the life-denying region which from far away had threatened us with the blink and growl of its bombardments" [033-006]. The march transforms the landscape as well as the marcher — the familiar world recedes, replaced by the zone of desolation.

Before the Somme, the battalion "marched a couple of miles to a concentration point between Mametz and Carnoy" [014-001] — the march as approach, the physical movement toward battle. The march was the last interval of normality before the chaos of an attack.

## March and Morale

### Marching Away

The march could deflate or sustain morale depending on context. Marching away from the front produced a different spirit: "I had a feeling that we were marching away to a better land" [031-002] — the march as liberation, the column moving toward rest and safety.

### Discipline and Refusal

But the march could also be punishment: when a soldier refused to march, discipline was absolute. Sassoon records "some humble inarticulate wretch who refused to march" [028-001] — the military's demand on the soldier's body, the refusal to march treated as insubordination that could end in a firing squad.

### The March as Memory

The rhythm of the march also produced moments of aesthetic detachment. Sassoon recalls "that retrospective picture of the Battalion marching at ease along an unfrequented road, at the end of a July afternoon, with Colonel Kinjack riding rather absentmindedly at the head" [018-001] — the battalion as a composition, the march as a living tableau framed by memory.
