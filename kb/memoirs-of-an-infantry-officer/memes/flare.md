# Flare

In trench warfare, flares (also called Very lights) were pyrotechnic devices fired from pistols to illuminate no-man's-land at night, revealing enemy patrols and wiring parties. Their ghostly, intermittent light was one of the defining sensory experiences of the Western Front.

## Military Function

Flares served both practical and psychological purposes. Tactically, they exposed movement in no-man's-land, making night patrols and wiring parties perilous. Psychologically, their rising and sinking light punctuated the darkness with uncertainty — every flare might reveal a soldier caught in the open. Men learned to freeze "stock-still while a flare quivered and sank" [005-007], knowing that movement meant death.

The standard Very light was fired from a brass signal pistol. Different colours carried different meanings — green, red, white — forming a crude signalling system across the line. A succession of coloured flares could indicate a call for artillery support, a request to lengthen or shorten range, or a distress signal from a patrol pinned in no-man's-land. The flare thus served as the nervous system of the night battlefield, carrying messages that telephone wires could not.

## In the Memoir

Flares appear as a recurring motif in Sassoon's night scenes. During his first trench tour, the Western Front is described as "sending up its uneasy flares" [007-004], an image of the entire line as a single restless organism. The silence before the raid is broken by an "occasional flare, never near our craters" that reveals "antagonistic shining of alarmed flares" [007-008]. Sassoon dodges "to and fro" counting the flares [007-008], and laments that a "flare would have helped my searching" for a wounded man [007-009].

Beyond their military function, flares carry a strange beauty in Sassoon's prose. A flare "quivered and sank, silvering" the landscape [005-007]. Later, he remembers the "diffused whiteness of a sinking flare beyond the ridge" [033-006], as if the flares themselves are part of the war's surreal aesthetic.

Sassoon's account of flares also reveals the psychological discipline of trench warfare. Counting flares, tracking their colours, gauging their distance — these were survival skills. A flare too close meant a patrol had been spotted. A flare too far meant the enemy was watching a different sector. The flare-lit night demanded a constant, exhausting vigilance that Sassoon captures in prose that is itself alert and watchful [007-008].

## Sassoon's Perspective

Sassoon treats flares as an almost poetic element of the trench landscape — simultaneously threatening and strangely luminous. They embody the war's fusion of industrial technology and primal fear, turning the night into a theatre of "antagonistic shining."

The flare serves as what might be called an "objective correlative" for the memoir's broader vision: the war as something that intermittently illuminates, momentarily reveals, and then plunges back into darkness. Sassoon never says this explicitly, but his flare imagery carries the weight of the suggestion — that the war itself is a kind of terrible flare, exposing truths about human nature that the pre-war world had kept in comfortable darkness [033-006].
