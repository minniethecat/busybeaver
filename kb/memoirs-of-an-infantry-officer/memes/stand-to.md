# Stand-To

Stand-to was the daily ceremony of heightened alertness observed at dawn and dusk in the trenches — every man at his post on the fire-step, rifle loaded, bayonet fixed, staring across No Man's Land for the attack that might come with the changing light. In Sassoon's memoir, stand-to frames moments of reflection and sensory intensity that define the trench experience.

## Definition & Context

Stand-to (short for "stand to arms") was a universal practice on the Western Front, instituted early in the war when dawn and dusk attacks were most common. Every soldier, from private to battalion commander, was required to be in position and ready for action. The ritual typically lasted an hour — from roughly an hour before sunrise or sunset until full daylight or darkness — and was followed by stand-down, weapon cleaning, and the distribution of rum rations. Though the frequency of genuine dawn attacks diminished as the war progressed, stand-to remained a sacrosanct routine that provided structure to the formless days of trench holding.

## In the Memoir

Sassoon's most vivid stand-to description occurs in the support line near Fricourt:

> Next evening, just before stand-to, I was watching a smouldering sunset and thinking that the sky was one of the redeeming features of the war. Behind the support line where I stood, the shell-pitted ground sloped sombrely into the dusk; the distances were blue and solemn, with a few trees grouped on a ridge, dark against the deep-glowing embers of another day endured. I was looking westward, away from the war, and the evening star twinkled serenely. Guns were grumbling miles away. [009-004]

The passage is characteristically Sassoon — finding beauty in the interstices of military obligation. He looks westward, away from the enemy lines, toward the evening star and the darkening ridge, while distant artillery provides a bass counterpoint to the quiet.

The moment is shattered by a canister shell:

> A canister soon came over quite near enough to shatter my meditations with its unholy crash and cloud of black smoke. A rat scampered across the tin cans and burst sandbags, and trench atmosphere reasserted itself in a smell of chloride of lime. On my way to the dugout, to fetch my revolver and attend the twilight ceremony of stand-to and rifle inspection, I heard the voice of the front-line. [009-006]

The abrupt transition from contemplation to violence mirrors the stand-to ritual itself: a state of poised readiness that could at any moment become actual combat.

## Sassoon's Perspective

Sassoon's treatment of stand-to reveals his dual consciousness as both soldier and poet. The military obligation is acknowledged without resentment, even as his attention drifts to the sky, the evening star, the "redeeming features" of the war. Stand-to provides a framework for reverie — the soldier must be present at his post, but the mind is free to wander. His description of the sunset as "smouldering" carries the double resonance of beauty and destruction, the natural world mirroring the war's consumption.

## Historical Reality

Stand-to remained one of the few constants of trench routine throughout the war. Officers were responsible for ensuring every man was present and equipped; the ritual reinforced discipline and unit cohesion. Veterans' memoirs consistently recall stand-to as among the most memorable experiences of trench life — the peculiar tension of watching the light change over No Man's Land, not knowing whether the day would bring attack or monotony. The practice faded from military doctrine after WWI as warfare became more mobile, but its psychological imprint on the generation that endured it was profound.
