The **home front** in Sassoon's memoir is not merely the geographical opposite of the Western Front but its moral and psychological antithesis. It is the world of civilian England — of Nutwood Manor tea parties, London editorial offices, and Cambridge colleges — that continues its rhythms undisturbed while men die in the trenches. Sassoon's narrator moves between these two worlds with increasing disorientation, and the gap between them becomes one of the memoir's central structural ironies.

## The Two Englands

Sassoon's memoir is built on the contrast between two Englands: the England of the trenches — mud, exhaustion, casual death — and the England of the home front — tea, conversation, the confident ignorance of those who have never heard a shell. The narrator shuttles between them on leave, and each return to the home front deepens his alienation. The people he encounters speak of the war in abstractions that have no referent in the world he has left behind.

## Nutwood Manor

The memoir's most concentrated depiction of the home front occurs during Sherston's stay at Nutwood Manor, his Aunt Evelyn's country house. The manor's "protective hospitality" was "almost bewildering" [038-011] after the trenches — a phrase that captures the fundamental incompatibility of the two worlds. Sassoon records "the persuasive human happiness which Nutwood Manor's hospitality offered me" [038-008] even as he feels himself unable to accept it. The very comfort of the place is an accusation: how can such happiness exist while men are dying?

Here he encounters "pleasant-mannered neighbours who dropped in to tea" whose "blandness" is "incompatible with the notion of 'sacrifice'" [038-014]. A woman tells him that "those who are killed in the War — they help us from up there" [038-013], converting the dead into spiritual propagandists. Lord Asterisk returns from the House of Lords where the war is discussed as policy, while Sherston carries the knowledge of what those policies mean in human terms.

The satire is gentle but devastating. Sassoon never mocks these people; he simply records their conversation and lets the gap between their language and the reality of the front speak for itself. The home front, in his rendering, is not malicious — it is simply, catastrophically, innocent. When he departs, he records that "nevertheless I left Nutwood with gratitude for the kindness I had received" [038-022] — a sentence that holds both genuine appreciation and the quiet despair of a man who knows that kindness, however sincere, cannot bridge the gulf between those who have seen the war and those who have not.

## The Press and Public Opinion

The home front is also the domain of the press — the newspapers and periodicals that shape civilian understanding of the war. Sherston's encounter with the Liberal editor [[markington|Markington]] in London opens a window onto the world of editorial opinion [038-023], where the war is debated in terms of policy and principle while Sherston carries the knowledge of its physical reality. The press circulates the casualty lists that families scan for names, the patriotic editorials that Sassoon's protest would later challenge, and the abstract language — "sacrifice," "honour," "the fallen" — that the memoir systematically dismantles by showing what those words actually describe. The home front, the memoir quietly insists, is not a refuge from the war but its enabling condition — the society that produces the men it consumes.

## The Return

Sherston's final return to England — convalescing after his wound and his protest — brings the memoir's home-front theme to its conclusion. Cambridge, "looking contented enough in the afternoon sunshine, as though the Long Vacation were on" [042-010], continues its rhythms undisturbed. The university that trained the officer class will train the next generation in the same codes, the same assumptions. The home front endures — beautiful, oblivious, and necessary — the world for which the war is supposedly being fought, and which the war has rendered permanently alien to those who fight it.
