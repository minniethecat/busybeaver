# Battalion

## Overview and Identity

The **battalion** was the basic infantry unit of the British Army in the First World War — roughly a thousand men, commanded by a lieutenant-colonel, organized into four companies. In Sassoon's memoir, the battalion is the protagonist's primary social world: the unit he belongs to, the men he serves alongside, the collective entity that moves, fights, rests, and dwindles. The memoir follows the Royal Welch Fusiliers' Second Battalion through the Somme, Arras, and beyond, and the battalion's fate is the narrative's central thread.

### Belonging and Attachment

The battalion was more than a military unit — it was an identity. Sassoon's sense of belonging is tied to the battalion as a specific community with its own history, traditions, and character. When he is posted away from the Second Battalion to join a different unit, he feels "more homeless than ever" [027-002]: "the prospect of joining a strange battalion made me feel more homeless than ever" — the battalion was home, and a strange battalion was exile.

Sassoon's attachment had a personal dimension. "My feeling was much the same as it would have been if I had owned a horse and then been told that someone else was to ride it in a race" [005-005] — the battalion was his, and the thought of another officer taking his place was a proprietary grief. The simile is characteristically Sassoon: the battalion as a possession one could lose, not just a unit one belonged to.

### The Stable Cadre

The battalion's identity was preserved through its officers and senior NCOs — figures like the Quartermaster, the Transport Officer, the Medical Officer — who formed a stable cadre even as the rank and file were killed and replaced. Sassoon's portraits of individual officers are also portraits of the battalion's character.

## The Battalion on the March

### The Column in Motion

The battalion in motion is one of the memoir's recurring images. The unit moved as a single body: "the Battalion at 71. North, and marched a couple of miles to a concentration point" [014-001]. The sight of a battalion on the march — a column of men stretching along a French road — was the visual signature of the war's logistics.

Sassoon captures the battalion at ease in one of the memoir's most painterly paragraphs: "that retrospective picture of the Battalion marching at ease along an unfrequented road, at the end of a July afternoon, with Colonel Kinjack riding rather absentmindedly at the head" [018-001]. The entire battalion is rendered as a composition — the colonel, the column, the July light — a living tableau that memory has preserved.

### The Diminished Column

After the Somme, the battalion is diminished: "the other dead beat and diminished battalion" [016-001] — the physical depletion visible in the shortened column, the empty spaces where men used to march. "Such was their habit, and so it went on" [016-001] captures the battalion's endurance: reduced but still functioning, still marching.

## Battalion Relief

The relief of one battalion by another was the basic rhythm of trench warfare. Sassoon describes it precisely: "Two guides from the outgoing battalion awaited us. We were to relieve them in the front line" [033-002]. The relief was a transfer of responsibility conducted in darkness, the incoming unit taking over the trenches, dugouts, and positions of the outgoing.

"Scarcely a trace remained of the battalion which had bivouacked there" [016-005] — the battalion leaves almost no physical trace, only its ghost in the landscape and its name in the war diary. The relief was the most routine of military operations and also the most existential: one battalion replaced another, and the war continued unchanged.

## The Sister Battalions

The Royal Welch Fusiliers fielded multiple battalions, and Sassoon moved between them. The First Battalion appears as a separate community with its own character: "I'd been there for a few days with the First Battalion last July, before we marched back to the Line in dust and glare" [030-009]. The relationship between the First and Second Battalions was familial — same regiment, same traditions, different men.

The memoir's battalion geography extends beyond Sassoon's immediate unit. "Two battalions had been called out; the Royal Welch were not among them" [014-004] — the battalion defined by what it was spared as much as by what it endured. The reprieve of not being called out was a gift that Sassoon records without sentiment, aware that another battalion had taken the place his might have occupied.

## Battalion and Battle

### The Battalion as Spectator

In battle, the battalion was both an instrument and a victim. Two battalions were called out before the Bazentin Ridge attack: "Two battalions had been called out; the Royal Welch were not among them, but from our position on the ridge we could see the whole battle spread out" [014-004]. The battalion as spectator, watching other battalions fight — a reprieve that carried its own guilt.

### The Shattered Battalion

After a failed attack, the battalion's condition was measured in casualties: "a shattered survivor of a broken battalion which had done all that was asked of it" [024-008]. The language is formulaic — "done all that was asked of it" is the official idiom of commendation — but Sassoon's awareness of the gap between the formula and the reality gives it bitter force.

The battalion could also be sacrificed: "a subsequently sacrificed battalion" [034-006] — Sassoon's parenthetical aside, as matter-of-fact as a footnote, acknowledging that the unit sent over the top after his own would be destroyed.

## The Silent Battalion

After battle, the battalion was silent: "by the morning we were a silent battalion, except for snoring. Some eight hundred men lay sleeping" [014-008]. The silence of exhaustion, the battalion reduced to its most basic animal state — eight hundred men asleep in the sun. It is one of the memoir's most peaceful images, and also one of its most fragile: the battalion restored to humanity only in sleep.

## Battalion Command

### The Remote Colonel

Battalion commanders appear in the memoir as remote figures. Colonel E., who asks Sassoon "Are you stuck to that bloody horse?" [031-024], is glimpsed only briefly — the commanding officer as a voice of authority, occasionally visible, usually absent. The battalion functioned through its company officers and NCOs; the colonel was a presence felt more than seen.

### The Disciplinary Apparatus

The contrast is with the second-in-command: the disciplinary apparatus of the battalion, the officer who enforced the rules. "The Major had him tied to a wheel" [028-001] — the battalion's power over the individual soldier's body, absolute and institutional. The battalion contained multitudes: the paternal commander, the disciplinarian major, the popular Transport Officer, the efficient Quartermaster — a small society in arms.
