The **fire-step** was a raised platform of earth, wood, or sandbags built into the forward wall (parapet) of a front-line trench. It elevated the soldier high enough to observe and fire over the parapet into no-man's-land. In Sassoon's memoir, the fire-step appears as both a physical location and a site of human intimacy — the place where exhausted men sit, doze, and wait.

## In the Memoir

### The Wounded Lance-Corporal

The fire-step appears at moments of intense narrative significance. After the trench raid at Mametz, a wounded lance-corporal sits on the fire-step and delivers one of the memoir's most devastating lines: "I half carried him in and when he was sitting on the fire-step he said, 'Thank God Almighty for this; I've been waiting eighteen months for it and now I can go home'" [007-010]. The fire-step — a piece of military infrastructure — becomes the site of an unexpected benediction: a man grateful for the wound that will send him away from the war.

### The Exhausted Company

During the night before the attack on Quadrangle Trench, the fire-step becomes a place of exhausted communion: "The men were sitting on the rough-hewn fire-step, and soon we were all dozing. Barton's bulky figure nodded beside me, and Kendle fell fast asleep with his head against my shoulder" [013-025]. The "rough-hewn" quality of the fire-step — carved from the earth by the men who use it — contrasts with the tenderness of the scene it hosts: an officer and his men sleeping together in the dark before battle.

### The Glum Officers

After the attack, Sherston finds three officers "sitting on the fire-step with hunched shoulders and glum unenterprising faces" [013-029]. The fire-step here is the architecture of paralysis — the place where men sit when they have exhausted their capacity for initiative. The posture of the officers — hunched, glum — expresses the moral exhaustion that the fire-step witnesses as silently as it witnesses the wounded lance-corporal's gratitude.

## The Fire-Step as Architecture

The fire-step was typically about two feet high and eighteen inches wide — enough for a man to stand on while keeping his head below the parapet when not actively observing. It was constructed of whatever materials were available: wooden duckboards, packed earth, sandbags. In wet weather, the fire-step became slippery and treacherous; in dry weather, it was where men sat to eat, smoke, write letters, and wait. It was, in the most literal sense, the threshold between the trench and no-man's-land, between relative safety and absolute exposure.
