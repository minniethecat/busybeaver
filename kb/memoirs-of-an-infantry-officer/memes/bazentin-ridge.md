# Battle of Bazentin Ridge

The British attack on the German second line at Bazentin Ridge, 14–17 July 1916 — the action that followed the capture of [[mametz-wood|Mametz Wood]] and in which [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s battalion participated while he himself waited in suspense two miles behind the lines.

## Background

Following the capture of the first-line objectives on the Somme ([[mametz|Mametz]], Montauban, and the peripheral woods), the Fourth Army pressed forward against the German second position along Bazentin Ridge, a low chalk rise running from Bazentin-le-Petit to Longueval. The attack began before dawn on 14 July — a rare night assault that achieved initial surprise — and involved the battalions that had already been through [[mametz-wood|Mametz Wood]] and were now being asked to fight again without rest.

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] was not with them. He was one of eight officers held in reserve at the Transport Lines, two miles back, a position of enforced passivity that generates some of the memoir's most introspective writing.

## The Event

The memoir's account of Bazentin Ridge is distinctive in the war-literature canon because it is narrated almost entirely from behind the lines. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] "existed monotonously while the Battalion was engaged in the Battle of Bazentin Ridge" [016-003]. The boredom he describes is not ordinary idleness but the specialized torment of a man who knows his friends are fighting and dying while he reads Thomas Hardy in a tent.

The night brings visitors. Officers from the [[second-battalion|Second Battalion]], "keeping up their courage with the volubility usual among soldiers who knew that they would soon be in an attack" [016-005], arrive at the camp. Among them is [[david-cromlech|Cromlech]], his friend from the previous winter. [[david-cromlech|Cromlech]] would be killed days later at High Wood.

News arrives in fragments. The battalion has taken more than a hundred casualties. [[david-cromlech|Cromlech]] is dead. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] records these facts without elaboration: "This piece of news had stupefied me, but the pain hadn't begun to make itself felt yet" [018-002]. The deferral of grief is itself the memoir's subject — the war allows no time for mourning.

## In the Memoir

Bazentin Ridge occupies Chapters 16–18, but it is present more as atmosphere than as action. The memoir's most vivid Bazentin passage is not about the battle at all: it is about [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] lying alone in his tent with *The Return of the Native* on his knee, his candle guttering on a shell box, wondering "how were things going at Bazentin" [016-011]. The Hardy quotation he records — "The quick-silvery glaze on the rivers and pools vanished; from broad mirrors of light they changed to lustreless sheets of lead" — becomes a metaphor for his own state: a man dimming from active participant to passive observer, waiting for news that can only be bad.

## Casualties & Aftermath

The battalion took more than a hundred casualties at Bazentin [018-002]. Among them was [[david-cromlech|Cromlech]], whose death [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] records as a wound that would not close. The news reached him through the casual brutality of military communication: "Yesterday afternoon I'd heard that [[david-cromlech|Cromlech]] had been killed up at High Wood" [018-002]. The juxtaposition of the personal and the institutional — a friend reduced to a casualty report — is one of the memoir's most characteristic rhetorical gestures.

## Sassoon's Commentary

The memoir's judgment on Bazentin Ridge is inseparable from its form: a battle narrated from absence. By removing himself from the action — or rather, by recording his removal, his boredom, his reading, his fear — [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] creates a space in which the reader experiences the battle as the infantry officer in reserve experiences it: through rumor, through the arrival of the wounded, through the faces of men who have seen what you have not. It is a narrative technique that makes the reader complicit in the memoir's central insight: that war is, for most of its participants, something that happens elsewhere, to other people, and is known only in its echoes.
