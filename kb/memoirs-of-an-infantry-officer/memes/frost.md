# Frost

## Overview

**Frost** in Sassoon's memoir is a double-edged phenomenon — a destroyer of pre-war pleasures and a brutalizer of trench conditions. Before the war, frost meant cancelled fox-hunts; during the war, it meant frozen ground, coke-fume poisoning, and the bitter cold of the winter line. The word marks the distance between the world Sassoon had lost and the world he inhabited as a soldier.

## Frost in Peacetime

Sassoon recalls frost in the context of his pre-war life as a hunting man: "Under such conditions no day could be a bad one, and although more than one Saturday's hunting was stopped by frost, I derived singular consolation from the few hunts I had" [023-007]. The frost here is a frustration, an interruption of sport — and the memory is framed in the retrospective warmth of a man looking back from the trenches. The "singular consolation" of those few hunts is amplified by their scarcity, the frost a minor adversary compared to what would follow.

The pre-war frost is benign: it cancels a day's hunting but leaves the world intact. It belongs to the rhythm of English seasons, the natural order that the war would shatter.

## Frost on the Western Front

The war transforms frost into a lethal condition. Sassoon records that "Our First Battalion, which had been up to its neck in mud in front of Beaumont-Hamel, was now experiencing fifteen degrees of frost while carrying on minor operations connected with straightening the line" [025-006]. The contrast is brutal — the same men who had been "up to their neck in mud" are now frozen. The First Battalion endures the extremes of both mud and frost, the Western Front offering no respite between drowning and freezing.

The frost was not merely uncomfortable but deadly in indirect ways: "Anyhow the frost had been intense, and owing to the rationing of coal in England the issue to the Army had been limited and coke-issues had caused many cases of coke-fume poisoning where the men slept in unventilated dugouts" [030-004]. The chain of causation is bitterly ironic: coal rationing at home leads to coke issues at the front, which leads to men dying of carbon monoxide poisoning in their sleep. The frost is the ultimate cause — a natural phenomenon weaponized by the logistics of total war. Men who sealed their dugouts against the cold sealed in their own deaths.

## Frost and the Language of War

Sassoon's treatment of frost is characteristic of his descriptive method: he rarely dwells on bodily sensation, preferring to trace the chain of consequences that a physical condition sets in motion. Frost is not described as a feeling — cold fingers, numbed feet — but as a cause: of cancelled hunts, of frozen offensives, of coke-fume deaths. This is the memoir's characteristic register, in which physical experience is rendered not through sensory impression but through its effects on action and outcome. The frost is real because of what it prevents and what it causes, not because of what it feels like. In this sense, Sassoon writes about weather as a military historian would — as a factor in operations, not as an occasion for atmospheric description.

## The Poetry of Cold

Sassoon does not linger on the physical sensation of frost in the way he does on mud or rain. Cold appears less frequently as a subject in itself, perhaps because it was less distinctive — mud was the signature misery of the Western Front. But frost appears in the memoir as a condition that sharpens other experiences: the "cold misty dawn" after the retreat from the Somme, the frozen ground that Sassoon's company trudged across in the winter of 1916–17. It is part of the texture of trench life, an adversary as constant and indifferent as the shellfire.
