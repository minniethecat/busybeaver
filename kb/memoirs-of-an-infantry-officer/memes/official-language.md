# Official Language

The bureaucratic and euphemistic discourse of military communications — communiqués, casualty reports, staff memoranda — that [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s memoir relentlessly exposes as a form of organized lying, and against which the memoir's own plain style defines itself as truth-telling.

## Definition & Context

Every military organization generates official language: reports, orders, communiqués, casualty returns. In World War I, this language acquired a distinctive character — abstract, impersonal, and systematically euphemistic. "Hostile trenches were entered without difficulty" meant that men had crawled through mud under machine-gun fire. "Casualties were sustained" meant that men had been killed [007-015].

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s memoir is, at one level, a sustained counter-document — an attempt to write the war in a language adequate to its reality, against the official language that systematically obscures that reality. The memoir's plain style — concrete, sensory, deliberately un-heroic — is a stylistic argument against the communiqué's abstractions.

## In the Memoir

The memoir's key passage on official language comes after the trench raid: "Nothing important had happened on the British Front that night, so we were rewarded by a mention in the G.H.Q. communiqué" [007-015]. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] then quotes the communiqué verbatim: "'At [[mametz|Mametz]] we raided hostile trenches. Our party entered without difficulty and maintained a spirited bombing fight, and finally withdrew at the end of twenty-five minutes.'"

The gap between this official account and [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s own narrative of the same events — two killed, ten wounded, O'Brien left somewhere in the craters, the chaos, the fear, the rain — is the gap the entire memoir exists to expose. The communiqué is not false in its facts; it is false in its meaning. "Spirited bombing fight" is true in the sense that there was a bombing fight and the men were spirited, but it is false in its implication that this was a clean, successful, almost sporting engagement.

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] returns to this theme in Chapter 32, where a communiqué circulated "for instructive purposes" becomes a document of black comedy — the staff's attempt to extract "lessons" from operations whose only lesson is that they are futile [032-007].

## Sassoon's Perspective

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s treatment of official language is the memoir's most radical formal innovation. Rather than merely criticizing the communiqués, he reproduces them — lets them speak in their own voice, and lets their inadequacy be demonstrated by contrast with the surrounding narrative. This is the method of irony at its most devastating: the official text is allowed to condemn itself.

The memoir's plain style is thus not an absence of rhetoric but a rhetorical strategy — a deliberate refusal of the abstractions and euphemisms that make war tolerable to those who do not fight it. To write "two killed and ten wounded" rather than "casualties were sustained" is, in [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s hands, a political act.

## Historical Reality

The British Army's communiqué system was designed to provide a daily summary of operations for press and public consumption. The communiqués were written at G.H.Q. by staff officers with access to unit reports but no direct experience of the fighting they described. The resulting language — "our line was slightly withdrawn," "the situation remains unchanged," "enemy resistance was overcome" — became notorious both during and after the war as a form of institutional dishonesty that [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s memoir helped to expose.
