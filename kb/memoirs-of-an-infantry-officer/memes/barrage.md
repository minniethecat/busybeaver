# Barrage

The artillery barrage — the sustained bombardment that preceded infantry attacks, shaped the soundscape of trench warfare, and redefined the terrain of the Western Front — is one of the memoir's most persistent sensory presences. Sassoon records barrages not as tactical abstractions but as physical experiences: noise that becomes weather, fire that reshapes the earth, rhythm that structures time.

## The Creeping Barrage on the Somme

Sassoon's most detailed account of a barrage comes during the Battle of the Somme, where he watches the bombardment from an observation post. His journal entry records the barrage's progression with precise timing: "7:45. The barrage is now working to the right of Fricourt and beyond" [013-007]. The language is technical but the scene is apocalyptic: "I am staring at a sunlit picture of Hell, and still the breeze shakes the yellow weeds, and the poppies glow under Crawley Ridge where some shells fell a few minutes ago" [013-010]. The barrage does not cancel the natural world — the breeze, the poppies — but coexists with it in surreal juxtaposition. Later, watching the Manchesters advance behind the creeping barrage, he notes: "Barrage going strong to the right of Contalmaison Ridge. Heavy shelling toward Mametz" [013-010]. The barrage is a moving wall of fire, and the infantry must follow it closely — too close and they risk their own shells, too far and the enemy has time to emerge from shelter.

## Barrage as Environment

The barrage is not a discrete event but an ambient condition of trench life. Sassoon describes how "Next day began grey and cheerless; shells screeching overhead, the earth going up in front of the Wood, and twigs falling on my tin hat" [024-004]. The shelling is weather — grey and cheerless — and its physical effects (falling twigs, thrown earth) register as natural phenomena. The memoir rarely distinguishes between specific bombardments; instead, the barrage becomes the continuous bass note of existence at the front, always present, varying only in intensity.

## The Barrage and the Attack

At Fontaine-les-Croiselles, Sassoon records the barrage as a prelude to renewed attack: "At five-thirty next morning our Brigade renewed the attack on Fontaine-les-Croiselles, but we remained in reserve. Enveloped by the din of the bombardment" [032-009]. The word "enveloped" is precise — the barrage surrounds, contains, defines the space in which soldiers exist. Even those in reserve cannot escape it. The barrage is the war's most total sensory claim on the soldier's body.

## The Barrage at Fontaine-les-Croiselles

At the Battle of Arras, Sassoon describes the barrage from a reserve position, watching the landscape "full of gun flashes and drifting smoke" as "a glowering red sun was rising" over the ridge [032-007]. The scene possesses what he calls an "angry beauty" — the aesthetic strangeness of a bombardment observed at a distance, where destruction becomes spectacle. He imagines the men on the other side of the slope, "strained to an intolerable expectancy, until the whistles blew and the barrage crept forward, and they stumbled across the open" [032-007]. The phrase "the barrage crept forward" captures the creeping barrage in a single verb — not moving swiftly, but crawling across the ground, a wall of fire that the infantry must follow closely. His pocket holds an official communiqué about nearby Wancourt Tower, a document whose bureaucratic abstraction ("three unsuccessful bombing attacks were made on the Tower") stands in stark contrast to the human reality he has just imagined [032-007].

## The Aftermath

After combat, the barrage persists in memory and in the hospital ward. In Craiglockhart, Sassoon writes: "I couldn't be free from the War; even this hospital ward was full of it, and every day the oppression increased" [036-009]. The barrage has become internalized — a psychological pressure that continues long after the guns fall silent. What was once external noise has become a condition of consciousness.

## Sassoon's Perspective

Sassoon's treatment of the barrage is characteristically sensory rather than analytical. He does not explain artillery tactics or critique the employment of the creeping barrage. Instead, he renders the experience — the noise, the thrown earth, the surreal coexistence of poppies and shellfire — and trusts the reader to feel what it meant to live inside that sound. The barrage is the war's acoustic signature, and by recording it with such precision, Sassoon preserves a dimension of the Western Front that no strategic analysis can capture.

## The "Enthusiastic Barrage" and Wounded Heroism

Sassoon's final encounter with the barrage comes during the action in which he is wounded. After being shot through the shoulder by a sniper, he experiences a renewed surge of "convulsive energy" and plans one last assault, declaring that "the Stokes-gun officer put up an enthusiastic barrage" [034-017]. His own shellfire dropping short had already knocked out some of his men, and yet his "overstrained nerves had wrought me up to such a pitch of excitement that I was ready for any suicidal exploit" [034-017]. The adjective "enthusiastic" applied to a barrage is characteristically Sassoon — darkly comic, registering the absurd disproportion between the officer's professional language and the destructive reality it names. Only a written order from the Cameronian Colonel halts his impetuosity. It is Sassoon's last barrage of the war, and the episode captures how the barrage, even when it becomes friendly fire, remains the inescapable frame within which all frontline action takes place.
