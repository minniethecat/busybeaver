# Rain

Rain is the dominant weather condition on [[france|the Western Front]] and a constant physical presence in [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s memoir, shaping trench life, military operations, and psychological states.

## Definition & Context

In the context of WWI [[trench-warfare|trench warfare]], rain transformed the battlefield into a waterlogged quagmire. The Western Front's geology — clay soil over chalk in the Somme region, low-lying Flanders fields — meant that even moderate rainfall rendered trenches impassable, collapsed dugouts, and turned shell-holes into drowning pools. Soldiers lived perpetually wet, their feet rotting from trench foot, their equipment waterlogged [003-007]. Rain was not merely weather; it was a tactical factor that could halt offensives and a medical factor that disabled thousands through exposure and trench-related illnesses [009-001].

## In the Memoir

Rain appears at critical moments throughout the narrative, often marking transitions between the relative comfort of billets and the misery of the line. Early in the memoir, [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] describes "a noise like a mad rainstorm, caused by the whizzing of the bullets" at the training ground, conflating natural and military violence [003-007]. During a [[night-operations|night patrol]], "it was raining quietly and persistently," the weather compounding the danger and disorientation of crawling through no-man's-land [007-007].

The memoir treats rain as an almost malevolent actor. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] notes "a little rain made a big difference to life in front-line trenches" [009-001], capturing how weather could turn routine trench-holding into abject misery. The narrator's prewar raincoat becomes a recurring motif — a relic of civilian life pressed into service against industrial warfare: "I was wearing my prewar raincoat; I could feel the pipe and tobacco pouch in its pocket" [007-009].

The cumulative effect of rain in the memoir is to erode the romanticism of war. [[mud|Mud]]-soaked uniforms, flooded dugouts, and the perpetual damp systematically strip away any notion of military glory. Soldiers huddle "in the rain, waiting for the attack" [014-022], the weather as much an adversary as the Germans.

## Sassoon's Perspective

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] treats rain with a characteristic blend of understatement and irony. He rarely complains directly about the weather; instead, he observes its effects with a kind of weary precision that makes the misery self-evident. When he writes of slipping on his "old raincoat" over his uniform [007-002], the adjective "old" carries weight — a civilian garment enduring what no civilian should have to.

The narrator's attitude toward rain reflects his broader anti-pastoral vision. Where Georgian poetry might celebrate English rain as gentle and life-giving, [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s rain is indifferent, corrosive, and — crucially — shared equally by both sides. German and British soldiers alike crouch in waterlogged trenches, united in suffering if in nothing else. This democratic misery is central to the memoir's anti-war argument.

## Historical Reality

The weather on [[france|the Western Front]] was historically exceptional. The summer of 1916 — during the Somme offensive — saw unusually heavy rainfall that turned the battlefield into the iconic mudscape of WWI memory. The winter of 1916–17 was one of the coldest on record. Medical records show that "trench foot" — a fungal infection caused by prolonged exposure to damp and cold — accounted for over 20,000 British casualties in 1915 alone before systematic preventive measures were introduced.

The British Army eventually developed anti-trench-foot protocols (foot inspections, whale-oil applications, dry sock rotations), but enforcement varied by unit and commanding officer. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s memoir, concerned as it is with the infantry officer's experience, captures the gap between official prophylaxis and frontline reality.
