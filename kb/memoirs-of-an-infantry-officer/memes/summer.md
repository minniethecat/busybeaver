# Summer

The warm season on the Western Front, which in Sassoon's memoir serves as a recurring contrast between the natural beauty of the French countryside and the mechanized violence of trench warfare. Summer brought better weather for offensives, making it paradoxically the most lethal season.

## Definition & Context

In the military calendar of WWI, summer was the campaigning season — the period when dry ground and good visibility enabled large-scale offensives. The "Big Push" of summer 1916 (the Battle of the Somme) exemplified this logic. For the infantryman, summer meant long daylight hours under shellfire, heat, dust, and the incongruous presence of birdsong and wildflowers in the devastated landscape.

## In the Memoir

Sassoon opens the memoir with a summer scene: the "fine weather at the outset of summer" with "untroubled notes of the wood-pigeons" [002-008]. He describes horses "shining in their summer coats" looking "a picture of sleek and soldierly prosperity" [003-004]. Yet summer also brought battle: "the 'Big Push' of last summer and autumn had now found a successor" [023-003]. The memoir's most pastoral passages occur in summer — "falling asleep under a summer sky" [016-002] — as do its most violent. Sassoon remembers "summer evenings when I was a little boy" [019-002], using the season to bridge his pre-war English childhood and the French battlefields.

## Sassoon's Perspective

Sassoon uses summer as a device of ironic contrast. The beauty of a summer morning makes the violence of trench life more surreal: "it was queer to be in an empty front-line trench on a fine morning, with the larks singing" [012-012]. Summer heightens the memoir's central tension between the English pastoral tradition and industrial warfare. The poet-soldier is acutely aware of the natural world even as it is being destroyed around him.

## Historical Reality

The Somme offensive was launched on 1 July 1916, at the height of summer. Summer offensives were logistically easier but exposed attacking infantry to longer periods of daylight, increasing casualties. Sassoon's descriptions of summer campaigning conditions — heat, dust, flies, and the smell of unburied dead — are consistent with historical accounts.
