# Blighty Wound

A wound severe enough to require medical evacuation to [[england|Britain]] — "[[england|Blighty]]" in soldier slang — which occupied a unique and morally complex place in the psychology of [[france|the Western Front]]: simultaneously feared, desired, and endlessly discussed as the one wholly legitimate exit from the trenches.

## Definition & Context

"[[england|Blighty]]" was British soldier slang for [[england|home]], derived from the Hindi-Urdu word *bilāyatī* ("foreign," i.e., British), picked up during the colonial era and repurposed in the trenches. A "[[england|Blighty]] wound" or "Blighty one" was a wound that was serious enough to get you sent back to [[england|England]] for treatment, but not so serious as to be permanently disabling or fatal. It was the soldier's golden ticket — an escape from combat that carried no moral penalty [009-002].

The concept was so pervasive that it spawned a dark humor all its own. Soldiers speculated openly about what kind of wound would qualify, and medical officers understood the dynamic well enough to be suspicious of self-inflicted injuries.

## In the Memoir

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] introduces the [[england|Blighty]] wound as a candid admission of the soldier's calculation. "I wondered if I should ever get a [[england|Blighty]] wound," he writes, noting that one of the battalion's best officers has just been hit — and presenting the wound as a kind of lottery, a random distribution of survival [009-002].

The raid on the German trenches in Chapter 7 provides the memoir's first sustained encounter with the realities of wounding: "Two killed and ten wounded was the only result of the raid" [007-012]. The clinical language — "accounted for everyone," "the only result" — is itself a commentary on how wounds become statistics, their horror drained by [[official-language|official report]]ing.

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s own eventual wound is treated with characteristic understatement. He receives it almost as an afterthought, a sniper's bullet through the shoulder that means a hospital bed instead of a trench. The experience is described not as heroic but as a strange mixture of relief and guilt — the relief of escape, the guilt of leaving others behind.

## Sassoon's Perspective

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s treatment of the [[england|Blighty]] wound is subtly subversive. He refuses to romanticize wounding — there is no heroic stoicism, no "tell mother I died game." Instead, he presents the wound as an objective fact: something that happens to you, something you hope for, something you feel guilty about hoping for.

This honest accounting of the soldier's psychology was part of what made [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s memoir controversial. Official accounts emphasized [[courage|bravery]] and sacrifice; [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] emphasized calculation and survival. The [[england|Blighty]] wound, in his telling, strips away the language of honor and reveals the soldier's irreducible desire to live.

## Historical Reality

The medical evacuation system of the British Army — casualty clearing stations, base hospitals, hospital ships, and convalescent hospitals in [[england|Britain]] — was one of the most organized aspects of the war. A soldier wounded in the trenches could be in a London hospital within 24–48 hours.

The term "[[england|Blighty]]" was so deeply embedded in trench culture that it appeared in popular songs ("Take Me Back to Dear Old [[england|Blighty]]"), letters [[england|home]], and official medical records. The desire for a [[england|Blighty]] wound was so well-recognized that medical officers were trained to detect self-inflicted wounds — though [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] notes that even "brave men" sometimes resorted to this course when the strain became unendurable [031-032].
