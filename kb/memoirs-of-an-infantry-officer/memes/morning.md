# Morning

Morning in Sassoon's memoir is a threshold time — the hour of military routine (stand-to, orders, movement), of departures and farewells, and of the occasional intrusion of natural beauty into the war landscape.

## Military Routine

Morning governed the rhythm of trench life. Battalion moves typically began in the morning [012-001], and "Orders were circulated next morning" [012-003] — the administrative machinery of war restarting with each new day. The morning after the bombardment began found Barton and Sassoon with "our bottle" still intact [010-012].

Sassoon practises bayonet-fighting the "next morning" after the Major's lecture [003-001], and the morning after his return from leave sees him dispatched to the other end of the line [006-001].

## Weather and Mood

Sassoon contrasts fine mornings with miserable ones to underscore the war's physical conditions. "Wednesday morning was miserably wet" as junior officers prepare for a move [012-006].

Yet he also records the rare pleasure of "fine mornings when the air smelt so fresh" [003-002], and his arrival at a front-line trench "on a fine morning, with everything quite peaceful" [012-012]. The weather on any given morning could determine the entire character of a day at the front.

## Departures and Farewells

Morning is the time of leave-taking. Sassoon says "goodbye to him next morning" [010-004], and the woman at the billet "contrived to be stoically" composed the "next morning" [010-005]. The journey "behind the sluggish pony next morning" brings "the mellow bells of" a French village [009-006].

These morning farewells carry an understated pathos — the knowledge that the departing officer may not return.

## Morning as Narrative Frame

Morning functions in the memoir as more than a chronological marker; it is a structural device that enables the memoir's distinctive temporal rhythm.

Sassoon repeatedly opens chapters and episodes with morning — "We started next morning" [012-001], "next morning I was sent" [006-001] — creating a diaristic texture that mimics the infantry officer's experience of time: measured not in grand historical arcs but in the succession of dawns, each one bringing orders, movement, or the resumption of routine.

This reliance on morning as a narrative hinge also serves a deeper purpose. The morning after a bombardment or a raid registers continuity — the world still exists, the men still function — and Sassoon's quiet notation of "next morning" after scenes of violence is itself an act of witness. The ordinary has survived the extraordinary.

The contrast between war mornings and peace mornings runs throughout the memoir. Sassoon's recollection of "fine mornings when the air smelt so fresh" [003-002] belongs to a pre-war sensibility, the memory of English countryside mornings that makes the trench mornings — wet, cold, shell-torn — feel like violations of a natural order.

Morning thus becomes the site where the memoir's pastoral nostalgia and its war realism intersect.

## Sassoon's Perspective

Sassoon uses morning as more than a time-marker. It is the hinge between sleep and duty, between the temporary reprieve of night and the demands of war. He registers morning with a diarist's precision, letting the accumulation of mornings build the memoir's temporal texture.
