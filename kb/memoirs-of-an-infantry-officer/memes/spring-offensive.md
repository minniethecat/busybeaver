# Spring Offensive (1918)

The series of German attacks that began on 21 March 1918 and overran the British Fifth Army's positions on the Somme — the war's most dramatic strategic reversal since 1914, and in [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s memoir an event that is simultaneously anticipated as a looming threat, experienced through fragmentary reports from the front, and absorbed as the context for the battalion's final devastating losses.

## Background

By early 1918, the strategic balance on [[france|the Western Front]] had shifted decisively. The collapse of Russia in 1917 freed over fifty German divisions for transfer to the west. The German High Command, under Ludendorff, prepared a series of offensives designed to split the British and French armies, drive the BEF back to the Channel ports, and force a negotiated peace before American manpower could tip the balance [027-004].

The British Army, conversely, was critically weakened. The losses of 1916 and 1917 — on the Somme, at Arras, at Ypres — had drained the infantry of experienced officers and N[[conscientious-objection|CO]]s. The government's reluctance to release reinforcements from [[england|Britain]] left battalions chronically under strength. The memoir registers this weakness indirectly, in its accounts of battalions "almost wiped out" and companies reduced to handfuls of men [040-004].

## The Event

The offensive opened before dawn on 21 March 1918 with a hurricane bombardment of unprecedented intensity, followed by infantry attacks using new infiltration tactics — storm-troopers bypassing strongpoints to penetrate deep into the rear areas. The British Fifth Army, holding the Somme sector over which [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s battalion had fought in 1916, was forced into a fighting retreat that by early April had surrendered virtually all the ground won at such cost two years earlier.

For [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s battalion, the [[second-battalion|Second Battalion]] of the [[royal-welch-fusiliers|Flintshire Fusiliers]], the offensive meant being thrown into the line to stem the German advance. The memoir does not narrate the battalion's action in detail — [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] was not present for the worst of it — but records its consequences through the letters that arrive from [[dottrell|Dottrell]] and through the casualty lists that name [[ormand|Ormand]], Dunning, and others [038-021][041-003].

## In the Memoir

The Spring Offensive functions in the memoir less as a described event than as an anticipated catastrophe. From early 1918 onward, references to "the Spring Offensive" recur as a motif of dread: the men talk of it with grim humour, the officers plan for it, and the narrative's tone darkens as the expected date approaches [023-003][027-012][031-025].

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s own experience of the offensive is characteristically oblique. He is not in the front line when it breaks — he is in [[england|England]], or in transit, or in hospital — and his knowledge of events comes through letters, official [[official-language|communiqué]]s, and the rumours that circulate behind the lines. This mediated experience is, in its way, truer to the war's reality than a set-piece battle narrative would have been: for most participants, the shape of events was only ever partially visible.

When the offensive's consequences arrive in the memoir, they arrive not as tactical narrative but as a list of the dead. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]] records "the deaths of [[ormand|Ormand]] and Dunning and the others" with a compression that is itself the point — the offensive consumed lives faster than the memoir's form can accommodate, and the only honest response is a kind of stunned notation [038-022].

## Casualties & Aftermath

The Spring Offensive cost the British Army approximately 178,000 casualties between March and May 1918. The German army suffered comparable losses, and the offensive's failure to achieve a decisive breakthrough effectively exhausted Germany's last reserves of manpower. By July, the initiative had passed to the Allies, who would launch the counter-offensives that ended the war in November.

For [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s battalion, the offensive was devastating. "Our [[second-battalion|Second Battalion]] had been almost wiped out ten days before," the narrator records — a loss that effectively ends the battalion as the community he had known [040-004]. The personal cost is registered in the letters from [[dottrell|Dottrell]] and in the narrator's struggle to "accept the deaths of [[ormand|Ormand]] and Dunning" as facts rather than absences [038-022].

## Sassoon's Commentary

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s commentary on the Spring Offensive is implicit rather than explicit. He does not analyse strategy or assign blame. Instead, he lets the structure of the narrative do the work: the long anticipation of the offensive, the fragmentary reports of its progress, and the accumulating list of the dead constitute an argument against the war that is all the more powerful for being unspoken.

The memoir's political edge emerges most sharply in the contrast between the language of official [[official-language|communiqué]]s and the reality recorded in private letters. "Viewed broadmindedly, the attack had been a success," [[dottrell|Dottrell]] writes with bitter irony — a sentence that encapsulates the gap between the military's institutional perspective and the human cost that individual soldiers experienced [038-021].
