**Thornton Tyrrell** is the pacifist intellectual to whom Markington directs Sherston when the young officer resolves to make a public protest against the war. Described as "an eminent mathematician, philosopher, and physicist" and "the most uncompromising character I know," Tyrrell represents the tradition of principled dissent — the civilian counterpart to Sherston's inarticulate moral revolt.

## In the Memoir

### The Introduction

When Sherston visits Markington's office to declare his intention to protest the war, Markington counsels caution and recommends a meeting with Tyrrell: "After further reflection he said that the best man for me to consult was Thornton Tyrrell. 'You know him by name, I suppose?' I was compelled to admit that I didn't. Markington handed me Who's Who and began to write a letter while I made myself acquainted with the details of Tyrrell's biographical abridgment, which indicated that he was a pretty tough proposition" [038-019].

Sherston's anxiety about meeting Tyrrell — "I knew no more about the functions of a physicist than a cat in a kitchen" — captures the class and educational gap between the young infantry officer and the Cambridge intellectual. Tyrrell belongs to a world of ideas that Sherston respects but cannot inhabit.

### The Uncompromising Character

Markington's description of Tyrrell as "the most uncompromising character I know" establishes him as a figure of moral seriousness in a memoir otherwise populated by compromisers. Sherston later defends Tyrrell against David's criticism that he is "only a doctrinaire," insisting that "Tyrrell's a jolly fine man and knows a bloody sight more about everything than you do" [038-023]. The defense, couched in Sherston's characteristic colloquialism, registers the impression Tyrrell has made: a man whose intellect and integrity command respect even from those who cannot fully follow his arguments.

## Significance

Tyrrell represents the civilian intellectual tradition that provided the philosophical framework for the war's critics. In a memoir otherwise dominated by military voices, his presence — brief though it is — opens a window onto the world of dissent that existed alongside the war and that Sherston, in his stumbling way, is trying to join. Tyrrell is never depicted directly in the memoir; he exists in the narrative only through Markington's recommendation and Sherston's subsequent invocation of his name. But his absence from the scene is itself significant: the intellectual's influence is exerted through ideas rather than presence, through the moral authority that Sherston invokes even when he cannot articulate the philosophy behind it.
