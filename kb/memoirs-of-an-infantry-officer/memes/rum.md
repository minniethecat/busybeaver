# Rum

The rum ration occupies a distinctive place in [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s memoir as both a practical provision and a ritual that structured the daily rhythm of trench life, providing warmth, courage, and a fleeting reprieve from the war's demands.

## Definition & Context

In the British Army of WWI, the rum ration was a daily issue of dark Navy rum (approximately two tablespoons, or a "tot"), distributed to each soldier, typically at dawn or before an attack. It was officially sanctioned as a medical prophylactic against cold and a morale booster. The rum-jar — a stoneware vessel marked "S.R.D." (Supply Reserve Depot, popularly reinterpreted as "Seldom Reaches Destination" or "Service Rum Diluted") — was a fixture of every company's stores. Distribution was the responsibility of the company sergeant-major, and the ritual of queuing for the ration, particularly in the bitter cold of dawn "stand-to," was one of the few predictable comforts of trench routine [009-001].

## In the Memoir

Rum appears in the memoir primarily as a social lubricant and a marker of trench culture. The rum-jar "lived under [[barton|Barton]]'s bed in the dugout" [009-001], its location suggesting both its importance and its quasi-domestic status — an object that belongs to the men's private world rather than the official military apparatus.

The distribution of rum is described as a moment of levity amid deprivation: soldiers coming "down for the rum ration" [009-001] at the end of a stand-to, the ritual providing a punctuation mark to the night's vigil. The memoir captures the sensory details — the warmth spreading through the chest, the brief loosening of tension — without romanticizing them. Rum is a comfort, but a comfort that acknowledges the misery it temporarily masks.

Later in the memoir, rum becomes a point of conflict between frontline reality and headquarters abstraction. An officer is described as having "a bee in his bonnet about the rum ration" [034-006], attempting to restrict it on temperance grounds. The memoir treats this with quiet contempt — the staff officer's moral crusade revealing his ignorance of what the rum ration actually meant to men who faced death each morning.

## Sassoon's Perspective

[[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s treatment of rum is characteristic of his narrative method: he never mounts an explicit defense of the ration, yet through accumulation of detail makes its importance self-evident. The rum ration is never sentimentalized — no one in the memoir gets drunk or becomes a caricature. It is simply there, part of the furniture of trench existence, like the duckboards and the sandbags.

The narrator's attitude reflects the paternalism of the officer class toward the men's small comforts. There is no suggestion that officers should abstain (they received their own ration), but neither is there any squeamishness about the men's drinking. The rum ration belongs to a pre-temperance world, a Georgian [[england|England]] that took alcohol as a normal part of daily sustenance. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]]'s memoir preserves this attitude without apology, implicitly contrasting it with the civilian moralizing that would later judge the practice.

## Historical Reality

The rum ration was a subject of genuine controversy during the war. Temperance organizations in [[england|Britain]] campaigned against it, arguing that it promoted alcoholism among soldiers. Some commanding officers, particularly those with Nonconformist religious backgrounds, restricted or abolished the ration in their units. Military authorities generally supported the ration on pragmatic grounds — it worked, it was cheap, and the men expected it.

The historical record supports the memoir's implicit argument: front-line soldiers overwhelmingly valued the rum ration, and attempts to abolish it were deeply resented. The controversy reflected the class and cultural divide between the temperance-minded [[england|home]] front and the pragmatic culture of the trenches. [[sassoon-siegfried|Sassoon]], writing from the perspective of the trench officer, comes down firmly on the side of pragmatism.
