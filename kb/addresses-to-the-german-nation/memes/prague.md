## Overview

Prague appears in [[Good-for-Nothing]] as the home of a group of traveling students whom the protagonist encounters. The city is associated with student culture, music, and a vibrant leave-taking scene. It represents one of the way-stations in the wanderer's [[wandering-motif|journey]], a place of youthful energy and musical tradition.

## In the Source Text

The students plan their return journey:

> …we leave Linz, so as to be in Prague in time. (009-012)

The protagonist learns they are students from the city:

> …that they were students from Prague, and I conceived a great resp… (009-015)

Music is central to the students' identity:

> …played when they returned to Prague. There he sat and fingered an… (009-019)

A farewell song is sung to the city:

> …long farewell we give thee, O Prague, for we must leave thee… (009-034)

The students join a festive procession:

> …the drum, the students from Prague in their cloaks played away… (010-022)

They are invited along for the final celebration:

> …and we'll take with us the Prague students, and the Porter! (010-030)

## Significance

Prague in the narrative is not directly visited but is invoked as a place of origin and return. The Prague students function as a mobile piece of the city—carrying its music, student traditions, and festive spirit with them on the road. This reflects the Romantic ideal of the wandering student, for whom the city is a cultural anchor even while traveling. The "long farewell" sung to Prague echoes the broader [[wandering-motif|wandering]] theme of departure and longing.
