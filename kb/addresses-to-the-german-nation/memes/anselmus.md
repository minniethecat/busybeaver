## Overview

**Anselmus** is the protagonist of E.T.A. Hoffmann's fairy tale "The Golden Pot" (Der goldne Topf).

A [[youth-motif|young]] student in Dresden, Anselmus begins the story as an awkward, accident-prone figure whose mishaps mark him as an outcast in bourgeois society.

His life is transformed when he encounters the supernatural realm of [[archivarius-lindhorst|Archivarius Lindhorst]] and falls in [[romantic-love|love]] with [[serpentina|the serpent-maiden]] [[serpentina|Serpentina]].

## Role in The Golden Pot

Anselmus serves as the central consciousness through whom Hoffmann explores the tension between the prosaic bourgeois world and the poetic realm of imagination. His trajectory mirrors the Romantic ideal of the [[romantic-art|art]]ist's journey from mundane existence to transcendent vision.

### Initial Misfortunes

At the story's opening, Anselmus is portrayed as a hapless figure, prone to comic disasters. On Ascension Day, he accidentally knocks over the basket of an apple-woman at the Black Gate, losing his money and drawing the mockery of passersby:

> [021-027] The mishaps of the student Anselmus. Conrector Paulmann's sanitary tobacco pipe.

> [021-029] The student Anselmus, for the young man was no other, felt as if he were crushed to the earth by the dark powers of some malignant destiny.

### Encounter with the Serpent-Maidens

While sitting dejectedly under an elder-tree by the Elbe, Anselmus witnesses a supernatural vision: three golden-green snakes winding through the branches, their movements accompanied by crystalline bell-like sounds. He is transfixed by the gaze of one serpent in p[[romantic-art|art]]icular:

> [021-042] The student Anselmus observed how the three snakes trickled and glided through the leaves and branches.

> [021-081] "For love of thee, thou gentle lovely Serpentina!"

### Copying the Manuscripts

Through the intervention of [[conrector-paulmann|Conrector Paulmann]], Anselmus secures employment as a copyist for the mysterious [[archivarius-lindhorst|Archivarius Lindhorst]]. The work requires him to copy ancient manuscripts with exacting precision—a task that proves to be a test of his spiritual worthiness:

> [021-093] "Herr Anselmus," said Archivarius Lindhorst, "you have indeed fine capacities for the calligraphic art."

> [021-102] These working hours were for Anselmus a continuous mysterious initiation.

### Between Two Worlds

Anselmus finds himself torn between two feminine figures representing opposing worlds: the celestial [[serpentina|Serpentina]], who belongs to the magical realm of Atlantis, and the e[[romantic-art|art]]hly [[veronica|Veronica]], daughter of [[conrector-paulmann|Conrector Paulmann]], who represents domestic bourgeois life:

> [021-130] His soul was turned to the sweet Serpentina and the wonders of Archivarius Lindhorst's fairy kingdom.

### Imprisonment and Liberation

When Anselmus doubts [[serpentina|Serpentina]]'s [[romantic-love|love]]—influenced by the black magic of the witch—he is punished by being imprisoned in a crystal bottle on Lindhorst's shelf. This imprisonment serves as his final trial:

> [021-172] "O Serpentina! Serpentina! save me from this horrible crystal prison!"

His liberation comes through renewed faith and [[romantic-love|love]], after which he is united with [[serpentina|Serpentina]] in the mythical realm of Atlantis.

## Character Significance

Anselmus embodies the Romantic archetype of the **[[romantic-art|art]]ist-hero**—the sensitive soul who must navigate between the demands of ordinary life and the call of creative or spiritual transcendence. His journey from clumsy student to initiated inhabitant of Atlantis allegorizes Hoffmann's vision of the [[romantic-art|art]]ist's vocation.
