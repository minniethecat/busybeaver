## Overview

Rome (*Rom*) is a central location in the [[Good-for-Nothing]] narrative, representing both the geographical destination of the protagonist's [[Journey to Italy]] and a symbolic convergence point where the story's major characters intersect. As a city of art, history, and romance, Rome embodies the [[italy-motif|Italian allure]] that draws the northern Romantic wanderer southward.

## In the Narrative

### Arrival and First Impressions

The protagonist first approaches Rome in Chapter VII, learning that he is "only five or six miles from Rome" and feeling overjoyed at the prospect (007-002). As a child at home, he had heard "wonderful stories of gorgeous Rome" and imagined the city floating among clouds with "wondrous mountains and abysses, around the blue sea, with golden gates and lofty gleaming towers, where angels in shining robes were singing" (007-002). He enters "the famous city of Rome" through a "gorgeous gate-way" at night, with the moon shining "bright as day among the palaces" while the streets lay empty (007-005).

### Search and Convergence

Rome becomes the focal point of a search when the [[The Beautiful Lady|German Countess]] arrives and "made inquiries in every nook and corner of Rome" for the two painters [[painter-lionardo|Lionardo]] and [[guido|Guido]], and a young musician with a fiddle (007-015). The protagonist's friend later confirms that "the Countess is still in Rome" (008-016), setting the stage for their eventual reunion.

### A Philosophical Interlude

In a memorable scene, the painter Herr Eckbrecht delivers a philosophical monologue about the artist's predicament, describing himself with "one leg still in the middle of Rome, in the Piazza del Popolo" and the other leg stretching into eternity (008-017). This dual image captures Rome's significance in the narrative: a real, vibrant city and a symbol of the artist's precarious position between present and future.

### Departure and Memory

Later chapters reveal the dispersal of characters from Rome. The protagonist mentions coming "directly from Rome" (009-008), and the Countess's return "from Rome" is noted (010-026). A lady writes from Rome about the protagonist's departure (009-030). Even after returning north, a character proposes taking "a trip to Italy--to Rome--where lovely fountains are playing" (010-030), reinforcing Rome's enduring appeal as a romantic destination. A later poetic reference compares the transience of worldly glory to how civilizations "sink in ruins like old Rome" (025-168).
