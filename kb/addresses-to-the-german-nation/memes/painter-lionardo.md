## Overview

Painter Lionardo (*Herr Lionardo*) is one of two painters who meet the [[Good-for-Nothing]] protagonist on the road and become his travelling companions. [[valley-motif|Tal]]l, brown, and slender, he is the more irritable and outspoken of the pair, forming a contrasting dynamic with the gentler [[Guido]].

## Role in the Narrative

Lionardo first appears on the road, introducing himself with characteristic directness:

> I am the painter Lionardo, and my friend here is a painter also, called Guido. (003-034)

His physical appearance is described at [[morning-motif|dawn]]:

> Herr Lionardo was tall, brown, and slender, with merry, fiery eyes. (003-035)

Lionardo travels with the protagonist and [[Guido]] in a grand carriage, moving from inn to inn. His temperament is notably more irritable than his companion's:

> Herr Lionardo seemed cross — he had two angry red spots on his cheeks. (003-039)

### The Castle Stay

At the castle where they stop, Lionardo insists on his own way:

> Herr Lionardo meanwhile insisted that I had better put on the clothes intended for him. (004-002)

Throughout the carriage journey, the contrast between the two painters grows:

> I watched from the box, laughing the while at Herr Lionardo, who always seemed to dislike driving in a carriage. (004-004)

### Later Recognition

His name carries weight — when the protagonist mentions his companions to a stranger, the response is immediate:

> "What!" he cried, "the two painters called Guido and Lionardo?" (007-015)

Lionardo reappears in the final joyful reunion scene, where his merry [[romantic-nature|nature]] reasserts itself:

> I saw him clearly — it was merry Herr Lionardo! The little girls now opened the door, and he sprang out. (010-013)

> Hardly had Herr Lionardo finished his speech when the door opened. (010-015)
