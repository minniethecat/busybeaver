## Overview

**Mina** is a key figure in Adelbert von Chamisso's novella "[[peter-schlemihl|Peter Schlemihl]]" (1814).

The daughter of a forest-master, Mina becomes the fiancée of [[peter-schlemihl|Peter Schlemihl]] after he rises to wealth and social prominence. Their [[romantic-love|love]] story forms the emotional core of the tale's middle section.

When [[peter-schlemihl|Schlemihl]]'s secret—that he has sold his shadow to the Devil—is exposed, their engagement is broken, and Mina becomes the central casualty of [[peter-schlemihl|Schlemihl]]'s Faustian bargain.

## The Love Story

Mina first appears as a figure of innocent beauty, the daughter of respectable parents. [[peter-schlemihl|Schlemihl]], now wealthy from the inexhaustible gold provided by the man in grey, courts her successfully:

> [014-011] Mina's happy parents believed their daughter had made the most brilliant match imaginable.

> [014-021] Mina was really an amiable, kind, good child. I loved her with all my soul.

She represents the **domestic happiness** that [[peter-schlemihl|Schlemihl]]'s shadow-wealth seems to promise—a normal life of [[romantic-love|love]], family, and social respectability.

## The Broken Engagement

Mina's world collapses when [[peter-schlemihl|Schlemihl]]'s lack of a shadow is discovered. Her father, the forest-master, recoils from the supernatural horror:

> [015-009] Mina sat there, pale and lovely as on the first day I saw her.

> [015-014] "Yes, I have long known it, my foreboding!" exclaimed Mina. "Yes, I have long known it, that you had no shadow."

Despite her [[romantic-love|love]] for [[peter-schlemihl|Schlemihl]], Mina cannot defy the social and natural order that his shadowlessness violates:

> [015-020] Mina clung, sobbing violently, to my arm.

## Aftermath

After the engagement is broken, Mina's fate is sealed. The man in grey manipulates events to ensure [[peter-schlemihl|Schlemihl]] can never reclaim her:

> [015-043] "To rescue therewith the poor unhappy Mina from the claws of the villain."

> [016-017] "How goes it with Mina?"

The novella's epilogue reveals that Mina eventually married another—a conclusion that [[peter-schlemihl|Schlemihl]] learns with mingled grief and resignation:

> [021-009] The lovely woman was Mina.

> [021-010] Mina was a widow. An unhappy criminal process had cost her her husband's life and her own property.

## Character Significance

Mina embodies the **human cost** of [[peter-schlemihl|Schlemihl]]'s supernatural transaction. She is not a magical being but an ordinary woman whose happiness is destroyed by forces beyond her comprehension. Her suffering gives weight to the tale's moral: that bargains with the diabolical exact a price not only from the bargainer but from all who [[romantic-love|love]] him.
