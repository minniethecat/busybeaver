## Overview

**Kühleborn** is a major antagonist in Friedrich de la Motte Fouqué's fairy-tale novella "[[undine|Undine]]" (1811). He is a water-spirit (elemental), the uncle of [[undine|Undine]], and a powerful, malevolent force who repeatedly intrudes upon the human world.

Unlike [[undine|Undine]], who has acquired a soul through marriage to the k[[romantic-night|night]] [[huldbrand|Huldbrand]], Kühleborn remains a soulless elemental being, incapable of understanding human [[romantic-love|love]] or moral complexity. His interventions, though sometimes framed as protective of [[undine|Undine]], consistently bring terror and destruction.

## Nature and Powers

Kühleborn is described as a "mere elemental mirror of the outward world, without the power of reflecting the world within" (023-014). As a water-spirit, he commands the Danube and its tributaries, and can shapeshift at will—appearing as a tall white man, a spectral figure, a wagoner, or a gigantic wave.

> [023-014] "You already know, my beloved lord, something of my evil uncle, Kühleborn, and you have frequently been displeased at meeting him in the galleries of this castle. He has several times frightened Bertalda into illness. This is because he is devoid of soul, a mere elemental mirror of the outward world, without the power of reflecting the world within."

His power extends through the water network: [[undine|Undine]] explains that he is "at odds with the other water-spirits in the neighborhood" and his "kingdom only recommences further off on the Danube" (023-015).

## Role in Undine

### Haunting Castle Ringstetten

After [[undine|Undine]] seals the fountain—the door through which Kühleborn accesses the castle—with an inscribed stone, his direct intrusions are temporarily blocked. However, he continues to appear in the vaulted galleries, terrifying [[bertalda|Bertalda]]:

> [023-003] The tall white man, in whom Huldbrand recognized only too plainly Uncle Kühleborn, and Bertalda the spectral master of the fountain, often passed before them with a threatening aspect, and especially before Bertalda, on so many occasions that she had several times been made ill with terror.

### The Black Valley

When the p[[romantic-art|art]]y travels into territory where Kühleborn's power is restored, he pursues them through the Black [[valley-motif|Valley]] with ferocious force:

> [024-003] "Malicious Kühleborn!" cried the knight, recovering himself. "Hey, 'tis you, you goblin? There, take your kiss!" And he furiously hurled his sword at the figure. But it vanished like vapor, and a gush of water which wetted him through left the knight in no doubt as to the foe with whom he had been engaged.

> [024-015] The whole valley indeed was suddenly filled with the surging flood, that visibly increased. "It is Kühleborn, the evil water-spirit, who wishes to drown us!" exclaimed the knight.

> [024-018] "It does matter to you, though," said the wagoner, "for I am Kühleborn." So saying, he thrust his distorted face into the wagon with a grin, but the wagon was a wagon no longer, the horses were not horses—all was transformed to foam and vanished in the hissing waves, and even the wagoner himself, rising as a gigantic billow, drew down the vainly struggling horse beneath the waters.

### Final Harassments

During the Journey to Vienna, Kühleborn's teasing tricks on the Danube repeatedly destroy the pleasure of the voyage:

> [025-006] The ungovernable Kühleborn began, undisguisedly, to exhibit his power, which started again at this point. This was indeed manifested in mere teasing tricks, for Undine often rebuked the agitated waves or the contrary winds, and then the violence of the enemy would be immediately submissive; but again the attacks would be renewed, and again Undine's reproofs would become necessary, so that the pleasure of the little party was completely destroyed.

[[huldbrand|Huldbrand]]'s growing resentment toward [[undine|Undine]]—"This comes from like not being linked with like, from a man uniting himself with a mermaid!" (025-006)—is directly fueled by Kühleborn's unceasing disruptions.

## Character Significance

Kühleborn embodies the **inhuman elemental world** against which the human characters must define themselves. As a soulless spirit, he cannot comprehend the moral and emotional complexity of [[romantic-love|love]]—he sees [[undine|Undine]]'s tears as simple sorrow requiring vengeance, without understanding the sweet resemblance between [[romantic-love|love]]'s joys and sorrows (023-014).

His presence serves as a constant reminder of [[undine|Undine]]'s dual [[romantic-nature|nature]]: she belongs to both the human world (through her soul and marriage) and the elemental world (through her birth and kinship with Kühleborn). The tragedy of the tale arises in p[[romantic-art|art]] from [[huldbrand|Huldbrand]]'s inability to accept this duality.
