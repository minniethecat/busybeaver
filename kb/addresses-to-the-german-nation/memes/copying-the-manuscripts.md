## Overview

**Copying the Manuscripts** is the central task and testing-ground in E.T.A. Hoffmann's "The Golden Pot" (Der goldne Topf, 1814). The student [[anselmus|Anselmus]] is employed by the mysterious [[archivarius-lindhorst|Archivarius Lindhorst]] to copy a collection of ancient manuscripts written in Arabic, Coptic, and other exotic scripts. What appears to be mere clerical work is in truth a **spiritual trial**—[[anselmus|Anselmus]]'s success or failure at the copying determines whether he will prove worthy of marriage to [[serpentina|the serpent-maiden]] [[serpentina|Serpentina]] and entry into the poetic realm of Atlantis.

## Context

After [[anselmus|Anselmus]]'s disastrous encounter with the [[the-apple-woman|Apple-Woman]] at the Black Gate and his strange vision of serpent-maidens in an elder-tree by the Elbe, [[conrector-paulmann|Conrector Paulmann]]'s friend [[heerbrand|Registrator Heerbrand]] secures [[anselmus|Anselmus]] a position copying manuscripts for the eccentric [[archivarius-lindhorst|Archivarius Lindhorst]]:

> [021-072] ...he possesses a number of manuscripts, partly Arabic, Coptic, and in strange characters...

> [021-074] ...young people for copying these manuscripts, has at last applied to me...

The task is presented in mundane terms—a paying job for a poor student—but from the moment [[anselmus|Anselmus]] enters Lindhorst's house, the supernatural [[romantic-nature|nature]] of the work becomes apparent:

> [021-081] ...the copying of the Lindhorst manuscripts. That at his very entrance into the house...

[[anselmus|Anselmus]] discovers that the manuscripts he copies are not dead texts but **living narratives** that respond to his touch and reveal their secrets only to the pure of he[[romantic-art|art]]:

> [021-095] ...then at last an Arabic manuscript; and as Anselmus sat down to copy it...

> [021-096] ...the copying of these Arabic manuscripts had prospered in his hands...

His success is rewarded with increasingly vivid visions of [[serpentina|Serpentina]], while his failure—triggered by the witch's curse—plunges him into despair. The manuscripts are thus a **barometer of his spiritual state**:

> [021-107] ...have now copied me a number of manuscripts, rapidly and correctly...

## Significance

### The Test of Poetic Vision

Copying the manuscripts is a Romantic allegory for the [[romantic-art|art]]ist's task: the true poet does not invent but **transcribes**—faithfully copying the divine or natural text that ordinary eyes cannot see. [[anselmus|Anselmus]]'s success depends not on skill but on the purity of his [[romantic-love|love]] for [[serpentina|Serpentina]].

### The Marriage Condition

Lindhorst reveals that [[anselmus|Anselmus]]'s copying is the condition for his marriage to [[serpentina|Serpentina]]—only by proving his fidelity to the task can he prove his worthiness of her:

> [021-102] ...needed to cast his eye upon the manuscript, while copying it all with the deepest attention...

### The Counter-Force

[[the-apple-woman|The Apple-Woman]], agent of the e[[romantic-art|art]]h-spirits opposed to Lindhorst's Salamander clan, attempts to sabotage [[anselmus|Anselmus]]'s copying. Her curse causes the ink to splatter and the manuscripts to resist his pen—externalizing the spiritual warfare being waged over his soul.
