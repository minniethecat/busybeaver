## Overview

**The Black Gate Incident** is the opening scene of E.T.A. Hoffmann's "The Golden Pot" (1814), occurring on Ascension Day at the Black Gate (Schwarzes Tor) of Dresden. The hapless student [[anselmus|Anselmus]], rushing to join the holiday crowd, collides with the basket of an old apple-woman, sending her wares tumbling. The incident seems a trivial urban mishap but proves to be the **supernatural trigger** for the entire narrative—the old woman is a witch in league with the e[[romantic-art|art]]h-spirits, and her curse upon [[anselmus|Anselmus]] binds his fate to the cosmic struggle between [[archivarius-lindhorst|the Salamander]]'s realm and the forces of darkness.

## Context

On the afternoon of Ascension Day, the citizens of Dresden are promenading in holiday spirits when [[anselmus|Anselmus]], already late and flustered, comes running through the Black Gate:

> [021-028] ...Dresden came running through the Black Gate, falling right into a basket of apples and cakes that an old woman was selling there...

The apples scatter, the cakes are trampled, and [[anselmus|Anselmus]]—penniless—is forced to hand over his meager purse as compensation while the crowd mocks him. The old woman's response is not the complaint of a mere market-seller but the curse of a supernatural being:

> [021-030] ...the mocking laughter at the Black Gate. In this mood, he had got to the banks of the Elbe...

The witch's words pursue him: "Yes, run—run to the devil! Into the crystal shall you fall—into the crystal!" This prophecy, dismissed at first, proves to be a precise description of [[anselmus|Anselmus]]'s later imprisonment in a crystal bottle.

## Significance

### The Prosaic Opening into the Fantastic

The Black Gate incident exemplifies Hoffmann's technique of grounding the supernatural in the mundane. The gate is a real Dresden landmark; the apple-woman appears to be an ordinary market-seller; the collision is a comic accident. Only gradually does the reader realize that this has been an encounter with dark supernatural forces.

### The Curse as Plot Engine

The witch's curse—"into the crystal shall you fall"—establishes the stakes and direction of the narrative:

> [021-078] ...it was the Apple-woman of the Black Gate! The pointed teeth gnashed together...

The old woman reappears throughout the story, shape-shifting into various forms:

> [021-186] ...the Apple-wife of the Black Gate stood before him. She grinned...

### The Link Between Worlds

The incident demonstrates Hoffmann's vision of a world where the supernatural is not elsewhere but **here**—lurking in market stalls, accosting students at city gates, woven into the fabric of everyday bourgeois life. The Black Gate is the threshold between the ordinary and the extraordinary.
