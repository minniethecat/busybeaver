## Overview

The Departure from the Mill is the inciting event of the narrative. Dismissed by his father as a "[[Good-for-Nothing]]" who lounges in the sun while others work, the protagonist embraces the call to leave as liberation rather than punishment, setting out into the world with his violin on a [[fountain-motif|spring]] morning.

## The Scene

The dep[[romantic-art|art]]ure occurs at the very opening of the narrative. [[The Mill]], with its steady, industrious rhythm, represents the settled bourgeois life the protagonist rejects. [[spring-motif|Spring]] mirrors his awakening to the world beyond:

> The wheel of my father's mill was once more turning and whirring merrily, the melting snow trickled steadily from the roof, the sparrows chirped and hopped about, as I, taking great delight in the warm sunshine, sat on the door-step and rubbed my eyes to rid them of sleep. (001-001)

The father's rebuke is delivered from the mill's interior, his [[romantic-night|night]]cap awry from labor:

> You Good-for-nothing! There you sit sunning yourself, and stretching yourself till your bones crack, leaving me to do all the work alone. I can keep you here no longer. Spring is at hand. Off with you into the world and earn your own bread! (001-002)

The protagonist's response—joyful acceptance—defines his Romantic temperament:

> "Well," said I, "all right; if I am a Good-for-nothing, I will go forth into the world and make my fortune." In fact, I was very glad to have my father speak thus. (001-003)
