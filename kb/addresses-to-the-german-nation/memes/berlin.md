## Overview

Berlin appears across several of the Romantic narratives in the corpus. It is referenced as the site of an art exhibition in [[Good-for-Nothing]], the location of the university in [[The Golden Pot]], and a place connected to a character's past in [[Peter Schlemihl]]. As the Prussian capital, it represents the world of institutional culture—art academies, universities, and official recognition—in contrast to the pastoral and small-town settings that dominate much of the corpus.

## In the Source Text

An artwork is identified by its Berlin exhibition:

> …in the autumn of 1814 at the Berlin Art-Exposition! (008-007)

A character's coat is traced back to the city:

> …which I had already worn in Berlin, and which, I know not how… (019-003)

Scholarly works are destined for the university:

> …works shall be deposited in the Berlin University. (021-025)

A photographic illustration is credited to a Berlin firm:

> Permission Berlin Photo Co., New York. (021-031)

The Royal Academy building is mentioned:

> …building of the Royal Academy at Berlin. (025-192)

## Significance

Berlin in these narratives functions as a symbol of institutional authority and cultural legitimacy. Unlike [[Vienna]] (a destination of longing) or [[Dresden]] (a site of visionary experience), Berlin is associated with the formal structures of art, scholarship, and official recognition. Its appearances are brief but telling—the city represents the world of academies, exhibitions, and institutional validation that stands apart from the Romantic wanderer's immediate experience.
