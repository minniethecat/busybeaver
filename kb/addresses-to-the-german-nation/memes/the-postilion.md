## Overview

**The Postilion** is a minor but recurring figure in Joseph von Eichendorff's novella "Memoirs of a Good-for-Nothing" (Aus dem Leben eines [[good-for-nothing|Taugenichts]]). He drives the carriage that carries the [[good-for-nothing|Good-for-Nothing]] on his journeys, and his horn signals serve as [[romantic-music|music]]al punctuations marking the rhythm of travel and dep[[romantic-art|art]]ure.

## Role in the Narrative

### The Journey Begins

The postilion first appears at the st[[romantic-art|art]] of the narrator's journey, driving a splendid carriage:

> [004-001] …four horses and a gorgeous postilion, while I, seated high up on the box, flew through the air like a bird.

> [004-003] The postilion blew his horn merrily, and we drove with spirit into the imperial city.

### Impatience and Departure

When the narrator lingers at an inn, the postilion's impatience drives the action forward:

> [004-016] The carriage was ready to start, and the impatient postilion blew his horn fit to burst.

> [004-017] The inn-people stared at me, the postilion cursed, the horses neighed, and I dashed out of the house like one possessed.

### Transformation

Later in the journey, the postilion undergoes a strange metamorphosis that signals the narrator's entry into a more fantastical realm:

> [005-001] In the midst of a fine dinner the postilion wound his horn, and I had to leave all and go on my way.

> [005-004] I shouted to the postilion to ask him where he was going, but he wore no uniform and was not a postilion; he seemed to be growing restless…

The narrator's lingering anxiety—"Still in anxiety lest the postilion should begin to blow his horn" (005-015)—reflects the postilion's symbolic function as a herald of change and movement.

## Character Significance

The postilion functions as a **liminal figure** in the Romantic narrative—a threshold character who facilitates transitions between spaces and episodes. His horn ([[horn-motif|Posthorn]]), a traditional symbol of the postal service and travel, becomes a [[romantic-music|music]]al motif that punctuates the story's rhythm of arrival and dep[[romantic-art|art]]ure.

In the broader context of [[german-romanticism|German Romanticism]], the postilion's horn carries rich poetic associations, famously celebrated in Eichendorff's own poetry and later in Mahler's settings—a lonely, [[sehnsucht|yearning]] sound that evokes wanderlust and the open road.
