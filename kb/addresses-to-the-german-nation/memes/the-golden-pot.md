## Overview

"The Golden Pot" (Der goldne Topf) is a fairy-tale novella by E.T.A. Hoffmann, first published in 1814. Set in [[Dresden]], it tells the story of [[Anselmus]], a clumsy student who becomes caught between two worlds: the bourgeois reality of [[Conrector Paulmann]]'s household and the mythic realm of [[Archivarius Lindhorst]], who is revealed to be a Salamander from the elemental world of Atlantis. The narrative is one of the defining works of [[German Romanticism]], blending the everyday with the supernatural in a manner characteristic of Hoffmann's style.

## Significance

"The Golden Pot" is Hoffmann's most celebrated fairy-tale, representing the high point of the Romantic Kunstmärchen (literary fairy tale). The narrative operates on two planes simultaneously—the everyday world of bourgeois [[Dresden]] and the mythic realm of Atlantis—and the golden pot itself is the object that bridges these worlds. It symbolizes both artistic transcendence and domestic fulfillment, the Romantic ideal of uniting the poetic with the everyday. The story exemplifies the core Romantic conviction that the marvelous is hidden within ordinary reality, accessible through imagination, love, and art.
