## Overview

The Prague Students are three [[youth-motif|young]] scholars—a cornetist, a clarionetist, and a hautboy player—whom the [[Good-for-Nothing]] encounters on a mountain summit overlooking Austria. (009-006) They represent the ideal of the itinerant student-[[romantic-music|music]]ian, combining Latin erudition with a bohemian [[wandering-motif|wandering]] life.

## In the Source Text

### The Encounter

The students appear suddenly from the forest, joining the protagonist's song with their instruments:

> I was standing on the summit of a mountain whence the first view of Austria can be had, and I waved my hat joyfully in the air as I sang the last verse, when suddenly from the forest behind me some fine instrumental music joined in. (009-006)

### The Wandering Philosophy

Their philosophy of travel is [[romantic-art|art]]iculated with precision:

> It's just the delightful part of it that, when we set out early in the morning, and the birds of passage are winging their flight high in the air above us, we do not know what chimney is smoking for us today. (009-017)

They frame [[wandering-motif|wandering]] as a form of study—reading "the vast picture-book which the dear God spreads open before us"—and their Latin erudition validates the protagonist's own path.

### The Narrative Pivot

The cornetist's revelation that his cousin is the porter at the castle creates the pivot that brings the journey full circle:

> "There is a castle not far from Vienna, and in that castle there is a porter, and that porter is my cousin!" (009-020)
