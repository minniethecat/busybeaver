## Overview

The Toll-House is a small building with a red-tiled roof situated near [[The Castle Garden|the castle]], along the high-road. It appears throughout [[Good-for-Nothing]]'s journey and ultimately becomes his settled home, replacing [[The Mill]] as the site of domestic life. It represents the [[heimat|home]] the wanderer finds—or builds—at the end of his Romantic [[wandering-motif|wanderings]].

## In the Source Text

The toll-house is first described early in the narrative as a charming pastoral structure:

> A very pretty little toll-house with a red-tiled roof stood n... (002-001)

The protagonist later returns to it, playing his fiddle:

> …if I were not coming from the toll-house with my fiddle. (006-029)

He sits before it, letting music carry across the landscape:

> …on the bench before the toll-house so that the sound should reac… (007-009)

From a distance he glimpses it as a landmark of home:

> …I could see my little toll-house, and the high-road, and the D… (007-016)

At the journey's end, it reappears as the place where everything comes full circle:

> My toll-house, which I had to pass, was sta… (010-002)

## Significance

The toll-house serves as the narrative's endpoint—the destination that replaces [[The Mill]] as home. Unlike the mill (associated with paternal authority and expulsion), the toll-house is a place the protagonist chooses for himself. It embodies the Romantic ideal of a humble, self-sufficient dwelling integrated into the natural landscape.
