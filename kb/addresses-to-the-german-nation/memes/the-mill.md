## Overview

The Mill is the [[Good-for-Nothing]]'s family [[heimat|home]] and the setting of the narrative's opening scene. A working water mill operated by the protagonist's father, it represents the world of labor, domesticity, and rootedness that the Romantic [[wandering-motif|wanderer]] must leave behind—and to which he is ultimately drawn back, though on different terms.

## In the Source Text

The mill appears in the very first paragraph, introduced through sensory detail:

> The wheel of my father's mill was once more turning and whirring merrily, the melting snow trickled steadily from the roof, the sparrows chirped and hopped about, as I, taking great delight in the warm sunshine, sat on the door-step and rubbed my eyes to rid them of sleep. (001-001)

The father emerges from the mill—"he had been busy in the mill since [[morning-motif|daybreak]], and his [[romantic-night|night]]cap was all awry" (001-001)—and delivers [[departure-from-the-mill|the dismissal]] that launches the [[Departure from the Mill]]. The mill is thus both origin and point of expulsion.

At the narrative's close, the mill reappears in memory as [[good-for-nothing|the wanderer]] returns to find [[The Castle Garden]] toll-house:

> My toll-house, which I had to pass, was standing on the old spot, the tall trees in the castle garden were still murmuring above it. (010-002)

| Lost innocence | A prelapsarian state the protagonist can never fully recover |
