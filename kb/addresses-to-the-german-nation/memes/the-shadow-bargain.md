## Overview

**The Shadow Bargain** is the central inciting event of Adelbert von Chamisso's "[[peter-schlemihl|Peter Schlemihl]]'s Wundersame Geschichte" (The Wonderful History of [[peter-schlemihl|Peter Schlemihl]], 1814), one of the embedded tales within the larger narrative.

The protagonist [[peter-schlemihl|Peter Schlemihl]], tempted by the offer of limitless wealth, agrees to sell his shadow to a mysterious stranger (012-015)—an act that renders him an outcast from human society despite his newfound riches.

The bargain is the archetypal **Faustian transaction** of German Romantic literature: material prosperity in exchange for an essential p[[romantic-art|art]] of the self.

## Context

[[peter-schlemihl|Schlemihl]] encounters the man in gray at a wealthy gathering where his lack of means is conspicuous. The stranger makes an extraordinary proposition:

> [012-015] ...if he repented of the foolish bargain as heartily as I did!...

The bargain is consummated: [[peter-schlemihl|Schlemihl]]'s shadow—which he had considered a trivial thing—is exchanged for a purse that never empties (015-038). The immediate result is wealth beyond measure, but the true cost emerges only gradually as [[peter-schlemihl|Schlemihl]] discovers that a man without a shadow is regarded as monstrous:

> [015-038] "Sir, I have sold you my shadow for this in its place..."

The mysterious stranger later offers additional supernatural compensation (016-016), revealing the full scope of the transaction:

> [016-016] "...I give you also my cap into the bargain."

## Significance

The Shadow Bargain is significant on multiple levels:

### Social Commentary

The shadow represents that which makes a person **socially legible**—the unremarkable, taken-for-granted quality that allows one to walk among others without attracting notice. [[peter-schlemihl|Schlemihl]]'s loss demonstrates that wealth alone cannot buy belonging; without a shadow, even a rich man is an outsider.

### Moral Allegory

The bargain is a warning against trading the essential for the merely desirable. [[peter-schlemihl|Schlemihl]], like Faust, learns that some things cannot be bought back once sold. His subsequent quest for redemption forms the moral arc of the tale.

### Romantic Motif

The shadow bargain p[[romantic-art|art]]icipates in the Romantic fascination with **doppelgängers, doubles, and the divided self**. The shadow is at once [[peter-schlemihl|Schlemihl]] and not-[[peter-schlemihl|Schlemihl]]—his externalized soul, the visible sign of his humanity.
