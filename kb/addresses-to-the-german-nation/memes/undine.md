## Overview

**Undine** is the title character of Friedrich de la Motte Fouqué's fairy tale "Undine" (1811).

She is a **water-sprite**—an elemental being without a human soul—raised by a fisherman and his wife after appearing mysteriously as a child.

Her marriage to the k[[romantic-night|night]] [[huldbrand|Huldbrand]] grants her a human soul. When [[huldbrand|Huldbrand]]'s [[romantic-love|love]] falters, Undine is claimed back by the water-spirit world under laws fatal to her unfaithful husband.

## Origins and Nature

Undine belongs to the order of water elementals—beings created without souls who inhabit rivers, lakes, and seas. According to the tale's mythology, a water-sprite can obtain a soul only through marriage to a mortal who remains faithful. Undine was sent to the fisherman's cottage as a child in place of the couple's own lost daughter:

> [P01-002] Undine — the water-sprite who becomes human through love.

> [022-001] The knight Huldbrand came to the fisherman's cottage, where Undine lived as the foster-daughter of the old couple.

## Marriage to Huldbrand

When the k[[romantic-night|night]] [[huldbrand|Huldbrand]] seeks shelter at the fisherman's cottage during a storm, he encounters Undine and is captivated by her wild beauty and strange charm. Their marriage—solemnized by a priest—effects Undine's transformation:

> [022-005] The old fisherman even began to tease the knight, but in so chaste and modest a manner that the aged wife herself smiled good-humoredly.

> [022-008] Undine was hanging on the knight's arm with humble tenderness, and the knight himself could not sufficiently express his happiness.

### Gaining a Soul

The acquisition of a human soul marks a profound change in Undine's character. The once-wild and teasing sprite becomes a devoted, gentle wife, her supernatural origins manifesting only in moments of distress or when the laws of her elemental [[romantic-nature|nature]] compel her:

> [022-011] The knight tried to persuade himself that his beautiful wife was under the spell of one of those strange beings.

## The Tragic Triangle

The central tragedy of the tale unfolds with the introduction of [[bertalda|Bertalda]], who is revealed to be the fisherman's biological daughter—the child for whom Undine was substituted. [[huldbrand|Huldbrand]]'s growing attraction to [[bertalda|Bertalda]] violates the condition under which Undine's soul was granted:

> [023-001] With ardent love to the young knight, and how they both looked upon the poor wife as a mysterious being.

> [023-003] Bertalda the spectral maiden, and the old fisherman, on receiving the message from his foster-daughter.

### Kühleborn's Intervention

Undine's uncle, the water-spirit [[kuhleborn|Kühleborn]], attempts to protect her by tormenting [[bertalda|Bertalda]] and [[huldbrand|Huldbrand]]. However, his interventions only worsen the situation, driving [[huldbrand|Huldbrand]] further toward [[bertalda|Bertalda]]:

> [023-014] "Something of my evil uncle, Kühleborn, and you have frequently been forced to feel his power."

> [023-015] "The ill-bred Kühleborn has set his mark especially upon you, Bertalda."

## Death and Revenge

When [[huldbrand|Huldbrand]] openly rejects Undine in favor of [[bertalda|Bertalda]], the law of the water-spirits is invoked. Undine is compelled to return to her watery element, and she warns [[huldbrand|Huldbrand]] of the fatal consequence should he marry another:

> [023-019] But the knight, angry at her impertinence, declared himself for Bertalda.

After [[huldbrand|Huldbrand]]'s marriage to [[bertalda|Bertalda]], Undine rises from the sealed well to claim her husband with a kiss—an embrace that proves fatal, returning him to the waters from which her [[romantic-love|love]] had once raised him.

## Romantic Significance

Undine embodies the Romantic fascination with **elemental beings** who exist at the boundary between [[romantic-nature|nature]] and humanity.

Her story explores themes that pervade German Romantic literature: the transformative power of [[romantic-love|love]], the tragedy of human faithlessness, and the irreducible mystery of [[romantic-nature|nature]].

Unlike the demonic water-spirits of earlier folklore, Fouqué's Undine is sympathetic—a being who achieves humanity only to be destroyed by human weakness.
