## Overview

**Bertalda** is a central figure in Friedrich de la Motte Fouqué's fairy tale "[[undine|Undine]]" (1811).

Raised as the foster-daughter of a duke, Bertalda is later revealed to be the biological child of the old fisherman and his wife—the same couple who raised [[undine|Undine]] as their own.

Her rivalry with [[undine|Undine]] for the [[romantic-love|love]] of the k[[romantic-night|night]] [[huldbrand|Huldbrand]] drives the tale toward its tragic conclusion.

## Identity and Origins

Bertalda's discovery of her true parentage creates the tale's central dramatic irony. She was exchanged for [[undine|Undine]] in infancy—[[undine|the water-sprite]] taking her place in the fisherman's cottage while Bertalda was raised in noble privilege:

> [023-001] Bertalda more and more responded to his affection, while the poor Undine was treated with coldness.

> [023-003] Bertalda, especially, thought that she perceived in Undine a rival who stood in her way.

## Rivalry with Undine

Bertalda's treatment of [[undine|Undine]] grows increasingly cruel as she senses [[huldbrand|Huldbrand]]'s weakening attachment to his supernatural wife:

> [023-004] "Lonely I now sit in my cottage, Bertalda is better with you than with me."

> [023-009] Bertalda came running up and called out to Undine with harsh words.

Bertalda represents the **e[[romantic-art|art]]hly, human rival**—beautiful, socially accomplished, but ultimately limited in understanding. Her inability to comprehend the supernatural dimension of [[undine|Undine]]'s [[romantic-nature|nature]] leads her to behave with arrogant cruelty.

## Marriage and Aftermath

After [[undine|Undine]]'s dep[[romantic-art|art]]ure, Bertalda marries [[huldbrand|Huldbrand]]. Their union is doomed from the st[[romantic-art|art]], haunted by the violated laws of the elemental world. On their wedding [[romantic-night|night]], [[undine|Undine]] rises from the sealed well:

> [023-021] His affection for Bertalda burst forth again with vehemence.

> [024-001] How Bertalda returned home with the Knight.

The tale's conclusion leaves Bertalda as a survivor—but one whose happiness was built on the destruction of a being more innocent than herself.

## Character Significance

Bertalda functions as the **shadow figure** to [[undine|Undine]]—the merely human woman whose virtues of beauty, cultivation, and social status prove hollow when measured against [[undine|Undine]]'s supernatural innocence. Her character illustrates the Romantic theme that worldly accomplishment cannot substitute for spiritual authenticity.
