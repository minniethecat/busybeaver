## Overview

**The Old Fisherman** is a supporting character in Friedrich de la Motte Fouqué's fairy-tale novella "[[undine|Undine]]" (1811).

He and his wife are the foster-parents who raise [[undine|the water-nymph]] [[undine|Undine]] after their own daughter is mysteriously lost to the waters (022-005). A pious, simple man living in a cottage on a lake-shore promontory, the Fisherman represents the values of **Christian humility and domestic contentment** against which the elemental world of water-spirits is contrasted.

## Role in Undine

### Foster-Father of Undine

The Fisherman and his wife lost their own daughter under mysterious circumstances, and [[undine|Undine]] appeared as a replacement—a beautiful child who wandered into their cottage from the waters (022-005). They raised her as their own, though her wild, wayward [[romantic-nature|nature]] constantly tested their patience.

> [022-005] The old fisherman even began to tease the knight about his evident admiration for his foster-daughter.

### The Knight's Arrival and the Wedding

When [[huldbrand|Huldbrand]] arrives at the cottage, the Fisherman offers him hospitality (022-010). The k[[romantic-night|night]]'s stay leads to his betrothal to [[undine|Undine]], and [[the-priest|Father Heilmann]] performs the marriage ceremony in the Fisherman's cottage:

> [022-010] …had borne her back to the old fisherman on that first night.

> [022-013] …as he brought me hither to the fisherman, a light-hearted, laughing child…

### Later Years

After [[undine|Undine]] dep[[romantic-art|art]]s from the human world and the Fisherman's wife dies, he becomes a solitary figure (023-003). When [[bertalda|Bertalda]] stays at [[life-at-castle-ringstetten|Castle Ringstetten]], the Fisherman writes:

> [023-003] I have now become a poor old widower, for my dear and faithful wife is dead. However lonely I may be in my cottage, I prefer to remain there rather than go among strangers.

## Character Significance

The Fisherman embodies the **simple, pious life** that Fouqué presents as the moral foundation of the human world.

His cottage—isolated on a wooded promontory, accessible only by boat or a narrow causeway (022-005)—is a liminal space between the human and elemental realms, making it the perfect setting for [[undine|Undine]]'s transition from water-spirit to wife.

His humility and faith stand in quiet contrast to the pride and passion of the aristocratic characters, and his loss of both daughter and wife parallels the tragedy that ultimately engulfs [[life-at-castle-ringstetten|Castle Ringstetten]].
