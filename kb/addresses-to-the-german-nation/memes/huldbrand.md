## Overview

**Huldbrand** is the male protagonist of Friedrich de la Motte Fouqué's fairy tale "[[undine|Undine]]" (1811).

A k[[romantic-night|night]] of noble birth, Huldbrand seeks shelter at a fisherman's cottage during a storm. There he meets [[undine|Undine]], the wild and beautiful foster-daughter of the old couple, and falls in [[romantic-love|love]] with her.

Their marriage, solemnized by a priest, grants [[undine|Undine]] a human soul. But Huldbrand's he[[romantic-art|art]] later turns toward [[bertalda|Bertalda]], violating the condition of [[undine|Undine]]'s humanity and triggering the tragic conclusion.

## The Fisherman's Cottage

Huldbrand's arrival at the fisherman's cottage—cut off from the world by a flooded forest—initiates the tale's central romance:

> [022-001] Huldbrand lay still, absorbed in deep meditation on the singular events of the day.

> [022-010] Huldbrand, full as he was of strange feelings and forebodings, embraced her with the most heartfelt tenderness.

## Marriage and Transformation

Through his marriage to [[undine|Undine]], Huldbrand becomes the agent of her transformation from soulless water-sprite to loving human wife. The priest's blessing seals this change:

> [022-014] Huldbrand embraced her with the most heartfelt emotion.

Yet Huldbrand never fully understands the supernatural laws governing [[undine|Undine]]'s existence. His rational skepticism blinds him to the danger posed by [[kuhleborn|Kühleborn]] and the consequences of his own wavering he[[romantic-art|art]].

## The Turn Toward Bertalda

The arrival of [[bertalda|Bertalda]]—revealed to be the fisherman's biological daughter—introduces the fatal complication. Huldbrand's affection gradually shifts:

> [023-001] Huldbrand's heart began to turn from Undine to Bertalda; how Bertalda more and more responded to his affection.

> [023-016] Huldbrand felt deeply the generosity of his sweet wife.

His failure to remain faithful to [[undine|Undine]] invokes the implacable law of the water-spirits.

## The Fatal Kiss

After rejecting [[undine|Undine]] and marrying [[bertalda|Bertalda]], Huldbrand meets his end when [[undine|Undine]] rises from the sealed family well to claim him with a kiss—an embrace that returns him to the waters:

> [024-002] "Give me a kiss, you enamoured swain!" Huldbrand sprang up with a cry of horror.

> [024-007] "I will never leave you!" cried Huldbrand, vainly endeavoring to restrain his horse.

## Character Significance

Huldbrand embodies the **flawed mortal [[romantic-love|love]]r** of Romantic fairy tales—a figure whose human weakness cannot sustain the supernatural gift of [[romantic-love|love]] he has received. His tragedy lies not in malice but in the limitations of ordinary human constancy when tested against elemental forces.
