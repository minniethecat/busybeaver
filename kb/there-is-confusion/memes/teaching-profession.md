# The Teaching Profession in There Is Confusion

Teaching occupies a distinctive position among the professions portrayed in *There Is Confusion*. Unlike medicine or law — professions requiring capital, credentials, and access to institutions that routinely excluded African Americans — teaching was a career path that the novel's Black characters could realistically pursue. Fauset depicts teaching as simultaneously a form of community service, a marker of middle-class respectability, and a profession with real but bounded possibilities for advancement.

## Isaiah Bye: The Teacher as Community Founder

Isaiah Bye's school on Vine Street represents the novel's fullest portrait of teaching as a vocation. Uncredentialed by any formal institution, Isaiah builds his practice from intellectual passion, community need, and the meager resources available to a freedman in post-Civil War Philadelphia. His pupils include "ex-slaves, freedmen, men like himself born in freedom, but unable through economic pressure to enjoy it except in name."

> "Twenty-five cents an hour he charged them, giving each a meticulous care such as would bring a modern tutor many dollars."

The economics of Isaiah's teaching reveal the paradox of the profession for African American practitioners: immense skill and dedication rewarded at rates that barely permit survival. Isaiah supplements his income through pamphleteering for the A.M.E. Church, through his brother-in-law's secondhand shop, and through a relentless frugality that his neighbors cannot comprehend. That he nevertheless accumulates property — two houses on Pearl Street, a house on Vine Street, a half-interest in a store, a cemetery plot — testifies to the sheer force of his discipline. Teaching alone cannot explain this accumulation; it is teaching combined with ceaseless supplementary labor that makes Isaiah's modest prosperity possible.

## Mrs. Marshall and the Domestic Sphere of Teaching

Joel Marshall's wife, "a schoolteacher" before her marriage, represents a quieter model of the teaching life. Her "precision of language and exactitude in small matters" suggests the disciplinary habits that teaching cultivates, and these habits infuse the Marshall household. Though the novel does not dramatize her classroom, her presence as a former teacher signals the respectability that the profession conferred on African American women in the late nineteenth century.

The contrast between Mrs. Marshall's career and her husband's is instructive. Joel's catering business generates far more wealth than teaching ever could, but teaching carries a cultural capital that commerce cannot match. Joel's recurring lament — that his lack of formal education bars him from "the kind of greatness for which his heart had yearned" — implicitly elevates his wife's world of books and language above his own world of sandwiches and pastries.

## Teaching as a Limited Horizon

For all its dignity, Fauset does not romanticize the teaching profession. Meriwether Bye, the novel's failed medical aspirant, "might have made a good teacher, caterer, minister or storekeeper" — but the listing of teaching alongside catering and storekeeping suggests its place in the occupational hierarchy. Teaching was respectable, essential, and honored within the African American community; it was not, however, the path to the kind of greatness that Isaiah dreamed for his son or that [[joanna-marshall]] demands for herself.

The novel's generational logic suggests that teaching belongs to the first wave of post-Emancipation achievement — Isaiah's generation — while medicine, law, and the arts represent the higher ambitions that become possible for the next generation. Joanna, who knows "almost to the day and measure the amount of time and effort it would take him to become a surgeon, a dentist, a lawyer, an engineer," occupies a world of professional possibility that Isaiah could barely have imagined. Teaching made that world possible, even as it was surpassed by the ambitions it nurtured.
