# Courtship in There Is Confusion

Courtship in *There Is Confusion* is a carefully observed social ritual that reveals the values, constraints, and aspirations of the novel's characters. Fauset depicts romantic pursuit not as private passion but as a public performance shaped by class expectations, gender conventions, and the particular pressures facing African American men and women in the early twentieth century. The novel treats courtship with sociological precision, attending to the rules, rituals, and power dynamics that govern romantic life.

## Gender and Agency in Courtship

The novel registers the gendered asymmetry of romantic pursuit with sociological precision. The narrator offers a general observation that applies across the novel's romantic plots:

> Since American customs of courtship give the girl largely the upper hand, it is the man who must prove himself worthy of her choice. [016-025]

This observation frames the courtship dynamics throughout the novel. Joanna Marshall, with her talent, beauty, and social standing, occupies the position of the pursued. Peter Bye's romantic trajectory is defined by his need to prove himself worthy — a project complicated by his uncertain family history and professional struggles. The burden of proof falls on the man, but the novel is clear-eyed about the costs this imposes on women as well: Joanna's own agency is constrained to the act of choosing among suitors, while her artistic ambitions must compete with courtship's demands.

The novel also registers how courtship pressures intersect with professional ambition. Joanna's concert tours, her dedication to rehearsals, and her single-minded pursuit of the stage all conflict with the expectations of romantic availability, creating a tension that drives significant portions of the plot.

## Courtship and Class Boundaries

Romantic pursuit in the novel is inseparable from class consideration. Courtship serves as a mechanism for reinforcing — or occasionally transgressing — the boundaries of the "better class of colored people." Characters evaluate potential partners not only on personal qualities but on family background, professional prospects, and social standing:

> Peter's rather lackadaisical courtship had not seemed worth taking seriously to Joanna, who was absorbed in her dancing. [029-010]

Peter's "lackadaisical" approach to courtship reflects his uncertain class position. Without the secure professional identity that would establish his standing, his romantic pursuit carries an ambivalence that Joanna, secure in her own status, finds insufficient. The adjective is damning precisely because courtship — in the novel's social world — demands purposeful, deliberate attention; "lackadaisical" pursuit is itself a sign of inadequate class performance.

## Courtship as Competition

Dance and social events become arenas of romantic competition in the novel. At a gathering, the narrator describes the choreography of courtship with almost anthropological detachment:

> Joanna taught them all the barn dance, Peter maneuvered until he got her for a partner, and they danced together for the rest of the evening. [007-048]

The verb "maneuvered" is telling: it suggests strategic action within a social field, where proximity to the desired partner must be engineered through skill and persistence. The dance floor becomes a microcosm of courtship itself — a space where partners are chosen, strategies are tested, and social positioning is made visible.

## Failed Courtships

The novel includes accounts of courtships that fail — narratives within the narrative that illuminate the stakes of romantic pursuit:

> Peter told her about his disastrous courtship of Vera Manning and Peter had been too shy to press his suit. [029-013]

These failed courtships serve as cautionary tales about the consequences of mismatched expectations, professional instability, and the vulnerability that courtship entails. They also highlight the particular precarity of African American romantic life in a society where racial barriers limited the social avenues available for meeting suitable partners, and where professional and economic instability could derail the most promising romantic beginnings.
