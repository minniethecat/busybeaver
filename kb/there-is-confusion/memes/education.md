# Education in There Is Confusion

Education functions in *There Is Confusion* as both a practical instrument of advancement and a symbolic marker of full humanity. For characters born into slavery or its immediate aftermath, literacy and formal schooling represent the most tangible evidence of freedom. Fauset traces education across multiple sites — the night school for freedmen, the segregated public classroom, the professional aspirations discussed around Joel Marshall's dinner table — to show how learning becomes the bedrock upon which ambitions are built.

## Isaiah Bye's Self-Education

Isaiah Bye, the novel's most exemplary figure of self-directed learning, embodies the post-Emancipation hunger for knowledge. Born into slavery, Isaiah teaches himself to read through "wise books borrowed and bought as best he might," mastering geography and history with an intensity that "made his heartbeats go wild." His intellectual curiosity is inseparable from his political consciousness — learning about the French Revolution teaches him "how a whole nation once practically enslaved arose to a fuller, richer life."

> "Who shall say how he increased his slender store of knowledge?"

This question, posed by the narrative voice, acknowledges the mystery of Isaiah's achievement while also insisting on its reality. His learning is not merely personal enrichment; it becomes the foundation for community service. He opens a school for colored youth on Vine Street, teaches ex-slaves and freedmen at night for twenty-five cents an hour, and writes pamphlets for the A.M.E. Church. Education, for Isaiah, is never a private good — it is the mechanism through which a people collectively rise.

## Joel Marshall and the Value of Schooling

[[joel-marshall]]'s relationship to education is defined by lack. His thwarted ambition to attend theological seminary — "his lack of education would be a bar forever between him and the kind of greatness for which his heart had yearned" — becomes the driving force behind his investment in his children's schooling. Joel's wife, a former schoolteacher, brings "precision of language and exactitude in small matters" to the household, reinforcing the value of learning as a domestic practice.

> "Joel believed that all things were possible. 'Nothing in reason,' he used to tell Joanna, 'is impossible.'"

The Marshall children's education is discussed with "the intense seriousness which colored men feel when they speak of their children's futures." College catalogues fill the house; Alexander and Philip debate professional paths; [[joanna-marshall]] absorbs her father's conviction that education is the corridor to achievement. Joanna's own approach to learning — "a fine serious mind, a little slow-moving at first, but working with a splendid precision" — reflects the disciplined, methodical relationship to knowledge that her father values above natural brilliance.

## The Schoolroom as Social Arena

The integrated schoolroom where Joanna and [[peter-bye]] first meet serves as a microcosm of the novel's social world. Here, race, class, and intellect intersect in ways that both mirror and challenge the larger society. Joanna distinguishes herself through academic performance; Peter, despite his evident intelligence, remains "undoubtedly shiftless" in his schoolwork — "Not lazy," Joanna judges, "It's just that he doesn't care; he just doesn't want to be anybody."

Miss Shanley's classroom also reveals the limits of formal education. When the class flounders during a physiology review, it is Peter — self-taught from his father's medical textbooks — who unexpectedly demonstrates mastery, reciting anatomical facts that astonish both teacher and classmates. This moment suggests that learning happens as powerfully outside school as within it, and that the novel's deepest educational relationships are those of transmission between generations: Isaiah to Meriwether, Meriwether to Peter, Joel to his children.
