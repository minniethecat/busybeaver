# Mrs. Reading

Mrs. Reading is a minor figure in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *There Is Confusion*, appearing only in retrospective passages concerning [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]]'s childhood. As [[meriwether-bye|Meriwether Bye]]'s longest-tenured landlady, she occupies a distinctive narrative position: the practical woman who must manage the consequences of [[meriwether-bye|Meriwether Bye]]'s fecklessness, and who treats the young Peter as a kind of human collateral.

## Meriwether Bye's Landlady

Mrs. Reading enters the novel through Peter's memory as he transports his father's belongings — a "small and motley collection" — from her house to [[susan-graves]]'s home:

> The collection consisted of a well-worn extra suit of clothes, another pair of shoes, some underwear in sad need of patching, some books chiefly on physiology and anatomy, the Bye Family Bible — and a roll of bills which the woman said had amounted to a lot of money for a colored man. (Ch. V)

The detail of the roll of bills — money Meriwether had apparently accumulated despite his rootless lifestyle — introduces the central paradox of [[meriwether-bye|Meriwether Bye]]: a man of intellectual and professional capability (the physiology books, the medical aspirations) whose instability makes him an unreliable presence in his son's life.

## The Collateral Child

Mrs. Reading's most vivid narrative function is her pragmatic response to Meriwether's disappearances:

> Meriwether had stayed longest at Mrs. Reading's but as, in later years, he more and more went off on his runs without paying his bills, Mrs. Reading frequently refused to let Peter leave the house until his father's return. "For all I know he may be joinin' his father on the outside and the two of them go off together. Then where'd I be? For you know yourself, Peter, he ain't paid me a cent these last two weeks." (Ch. VI)

The scene is at once comic and cruel: a child held hostage for unpaid rent, a landlady whose suspicion that father and son might "go off together" treats a boy as an extension of his father's unreliability. Peter, who "had gone to school, one might almost say, all over New York, and nowhere for any great length of time," bears the practical cost of his father's rootlessness — a childhood spent in the interstices of other people's patience.

## Critical Significance

Mrs. Reading is not developed as a character in her own right; she exists as a structural feature of [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]]'s backstory. Her role in the novel's social taxonomy is to embody the precariousness of Peter's upbringing — a boy whose claim on shelter was contingent on a father who could not be counted upon to pay his bills.

The juxtaposition with [[susan-graves]] is telling: where Mrs. Reading treats Peter as a liability to be managed, [[susan-graves|Susan Graves]] sees in him a legacy to be preserved. Mrs. Reading's practical wariness, however, is not presented as cruelty. She is a working woman who cannot afford to extend credit indefinitely. Her refusal to let Peter leave is less malice than arithmetic — the calculation of someone for whom an unpaid boarder's son is a risk that must be hedged.
