# Harlem Renaissance Context of There Is Confusion

Jessie Redmon Fauset's *There Is Confusion* (1924) occupies a distinctive position within the Harlem Renaissance, the flourishing of African American arts and letters centered in Harlem during the 1920s. As literary editor of *The Crisis*, the NAACP magazine edited by W.E.B. Du Bois, Fauset was a central figure in the Renaissance's literary infrastructure, and her first novel reflects both the movement's aspirations and its internal debates about representation, class, and artistic purpose.

## Publication History

Published by Boni & Liveright in 1924, *There Is Confusion* was Fauset's first novel, appearing at the height of the Harlem Renaissance's literary productivity. Fauset had already established herself as a pivotal literary gatekeeper through her editorial work at *The Crisis*, where she mentored and published many of the movement's key writers, including Langston Hughes and Jean Toomer. Her own turn to novel-writing represented a shift from facilitating others' voices to asserting her own.

The novel's publication coincided with a period of intense interest in African American literature from white publishing houses, a phenomenon that brought both opportunity and complication. Boni & Liveright, known for taking risks on modernist and unconventional work, provided Fauset with a mainstream platform unavailable to many of her contemporaries.

## The Marshalls in Harlem

The novel is grounded in the specific geography of Harlem during its transformation into the cultural capital of black America. The Marshall family's move from [[fifty-ninth-street]] to a house on One Hundred and Thirty-first Street reflects the northward migration of New York's African American middle class:

> "Papa is thinking of buying a house in One Hundred and Thirty-first Street, in Harlem, you know. So we may move after Sylvia and the others come back from Philadelphia. Papa would still keep his office in Fifty-ninth Street."

[[joel-marshall]]'s possession of "one of the first ten colored men in Harlem to possess an automobile" marks the family's economic ascent and the material texture of Harlem's black bourgeoisie. Joel's catering business, headquartered on Fifty-Ninth Street, connects the downtown commercial world with the uptown residential life, embodying the spatial and economic mobility that defines the novel's vision.

The novel's Harlem is rendered in sensory detail — "the golden haze over Harlem and Morningside Park" of late summer, the "myriad sounds of Harlem; a huckster calling unintelligibly, some school children on their way to P.S. 89," and the post-war delirium when "All Harlem was delirious with excitement" at the return of black soldiers from France. This textured rendering of neighborhood life distinguishes Fauset's novel from more abstract treatments of racial uplift.

## Middle-Class Focus

Unlike many Harlem Renaissance works that emphasized folk culture, vernacular expression, or explicit racial protest, *There Is Confusion* centers on educated, middle-class African Americans pursuing professional careers. [[joanna-marshall]]'s ambition to become a concert singer, and the Marshall family's economic comfort, reflect Fauset's interest in depicting what contemporaries called "the better class" of African American life.

This artistic choice was controversial. Critics including some of Fauset's Harlem Renaissance peers argued that focusing on the educated elite risked obscuring the harsher realities faced by the majority of African Americans. The novel's attention to professional aspiration — Joel's catering business, Joanna's conservatory training, Philip's and Alexander's college catalogues — was read by some as a retreat from racial politics into respectability politics.

Yet Fauset's depiction of middle-class life is not naïve. The novel shows that "like many of the better class of colored people, the Marshalls did not meet with the grosser forms of color prejudice, because they kept away from the places where it might be shown." This strategic self-segregation is presented not as a solution but as a compromise — "bad from the standpoint of development of civic pride and interest" even as it produced "good results along another line."

The novel thus participates in a central Harlem Renaissance debate: whether artistic representation should emphasize racial protest, folk authenticity, or the underexplored interior lives of the black middle class. Fauset's choice of the latter positioned her at odds with more explicitly political writers while opening a space for psychological complexity that subsequent scholarship has come to appreciate.

## Gender and Artistic Ambition

Fauset's focus on a female protagonist pursuing artistic greatness connects *There Is Confusion* to broader concerns within the Renaissance about gender and creative expression. Joanna's declaration "I'll be great, too" echoes the Renaissance's ethos of self-determination, while the obstacles she faces as a black woman in the classical music world foreground the intersection of race and gender.

The novel explicitly frames artistic excellence as a strategy for racial advancement. Joanna's teacher [[madame-caldwell]] tells her that "if there's anything that will break down prejudice it will be equality or perhaps even superiority on the part of colored people in the arts." This formulation — achieving racial progress through individual artistic excellence — captures a distinctive strain of Harlem Renaissance thought that Fauset both represents and complicates.

Joanna's ambition is distinguished by its historical consciousness. She studies African American heroes and develops a comparative framework for understanding their achievements, recognizing "that the bravery and courage exercised by these slave women was a much finer and different thing from that exercised for instance by Florence Nightingale." Her identification with Joan of Arc — "Joan, wonderful Joan with the name almost like mine" — signals her understanding of herself as part of a lineage of exceptional women who transcended circumstance.

## Critical Reception and Legacy

The novel received mixed reviews on publication. Some praised its ambition and psychological depth; others criticized what they saw as its accommodationist focus on middle-class respectability. In the decades following the Harlem Renaissance, Fauset's work fell into relative obscurity as the movement's canon coalesced around more protest-oriented male writers.

Subsequent scholarship, particularly from feminist critics beginning in the 1980s, has reconsidered *There Is Confusion* as a significant exploration of the interior lives of African American women and the psychological costs of racial aspiration. The novel is now recognized as offering a vital counter-narrative to the dominant Harlem Renaissance story — one that takes seriously the experiences of educated black women navigating the competing demands of artistic ambition, racial solidarity, and personal fulfillment.
