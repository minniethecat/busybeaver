# Harry Portor

Harry Portor is a Harvard-educated doctor in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *There Is Confusion*, a steadfast presence in the [[family-in-there-is-confusion|Marshall family]]'s social circle whose unrequited [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|love]] for [[joanna-marshall|Joanna Marshall]] mirrors the romantic misalignments that drive the novel's plot.

## Social Role and Friendship

Harry appears early in the novel as a member of the [[family-in-there-is-confusion|Marshall family]]'s extended social world, arriving at the Friday and Saturday night dances where the younger generation gathers. When [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] teaches a barn dance, Peter strategically installs Harry at the piano: "The night that [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] taught them all the barn dance, Peter maneuvered until he got Harry Portor at the piano." The scene captures Harry's functional role — always present, always helpful, but rarely the center of attention.

He moves easily among the novel's central figures: a companion of [[brian-spencer|Brian Spencer]], a friend of [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]], and an associate of [[alec-marshall|Alec Marshall]]. His Harvard education places him among the novel's professional class, and later, when [[philip-marshall|Philip]] is dying, "Peter and Harry Portor were at his bedside every day" — a testament to the loyalty that defines his character.

## Professional Life

Harry establishes his medical practice in the San Juan district, and his office becomes a crucial narrative location during the crisis of [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie Ellersley]]'s encounter with [[henderson-neal|Henderson Neal]]. When [[mr-simpson|Mr. Simpson]]'s incoherent telephone call summons him, "the doctor promised to come at once and soon the chug-chug of his little Ford rose above the sounds of the noisy street." The detail of the "little Ford" — practical, unglamorous, reliable — suits Harry's position in the novel as a competent professional rather than a romantic hero.

He treats [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] after [[henderson-neal|Neal]]'s assault and later during her nervous breakdown: "They found it hard to quiet her. 'I killed him,' she moaned to Harry Portor, hastily summoned. 'His death is as much due to me as though I had poisoned him.'" Harry's presence at these moments of crisis establishes him as the novel's steady medical anchor.

When [[philip-marshall|Philip]] returns from his sanitarium stay and seeks career advice, Harry's response is notably cool: "The latter in his own office greeted him, he thought, none too cordially, ignored his hand." The moment registers the tension between [[philip-marshall|Philip]]'s past romantic claim on [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] and Harry's protective instincts.

## Unrequited Love for Joanna

Harry's deepest narrative function is as [[joanna-marshall|Joanna Marshall]]'s devoted admirer — a role that places him in unspoken competition with [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]]. The narrator notes that [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] "had been too kind to Harry Portor; of late she had consciously avoided him because she knew only too well what he meant to ask of her the next time they were alone. She hated to hurt him but that seemed inevitable, for her heart held not the slightest fraction of [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|love]] for him."

This romantic asymmetry is triangulated through [[sylvia-marshall|Sylvia]], who "in her protecting eagerness to pretend to Harry Portor that she herself was in need of a sedative and Harry had spoken to [[brian-spencer|Brian]] about it." [[sylvia-marshall|Sylvia]]'s ruse — using Harry's medical expertise as a pretext for a conversation she cannot have directly — captures the emotional indirection that characterizes so many of the novel's relationships.

## Critical Significance

Harry Portor belongs to that group of secondary male characters — alongside [[brian-spencer|Brian Spencer]] and [[alec-marshall|Alec Marshall]] — who represent the novel's vision of respectable African American masculinity. Unlike Peter, he does not drift; unlike [[philip-marshall|Philip]], he does not burn out; unlike [[henderson-neal|Henderson Neal]], he does not prey. His unrequited [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|love]] for [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] makes him a figure of pathos rather than bitterness, and his continued presence in the social circle suggests an emotional maturity that the novel values but does not dramatize. In the economy of *There Is Confusion*, Harry is a good man who simply is not the right man — and the novel's willingness to let that be his fate, without tragedy or resolution, is itself a form of realism.
