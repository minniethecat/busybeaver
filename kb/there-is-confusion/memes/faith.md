# Faith in There Is Confusion

Religious faith operates as a quiet but persistent force in *There Is Confusion*, providing characters with spiritual resources for confronting the novel's intersecting challenges of race, ambition, love, and loss. Fauset treats faith not as a dramatic conversion experience but as a steady, inherited orientation toward life that sustains her characters through adversity.

## Inherited Faith

Faith in the novel is presented as a generational inheritance, passed down alongside family stories, values, and ambitions. The religious convictions that sustained enslaved ancestors through the trauma of bondage have not disappeared in the post-Emancipation generation; they have been transformed into a more settled, institutional form of spirituality centered on the [[church-life|A.M.E. Church]].

This inheritance is less doctrinal than dispositional — a habit of turning toward transcendent meaning when worldly circumstances offer little ground for hope. For characters who face systematic exclusion from the full promise of American life, faith provides an alternative framework for understanding their worth and purpose. Joel Marshall embodies this union of piety and practical ambition:

> He went to work every morning with a prayer on his lips, wanted also to do honestly and faithfully the things that bring great rewards. (001-008)

## Faith and Ambition

The novel explores an implicit tension between religious faith and worldly ambition. [[joanna-marshall]]'s drive for artistic greatness — her declaration "I'll be great, too" — can be read as either an expression of God-given talent or a secular challenge to divine ordering. Fauset does not resolve this tension explicitly, allowing it to operate as an undercurrent in Joanna's character development.

The novel's other characters often frame Joanna's artistic drive in spiritual terms:

> "Joanna has the faith that moves mountains," laughed Peter. (007-033)

This characterization suggests that ambition itself, when pursued with enough conviction, becomes a form of faith — a secular parallel to religious devotion that Fauset neither endorses nor condemns but examines with characteristic nuance.

For [[joel-marshall]], faith and ambition coexist more comfortably. His thwarted greatness finds compensation not only in his daughter's achievements but in a religious frame that gives meaning to sacrifice and deferred fulfillment. The novel suggests that faith — unlike ambition — does not require worldly validation.

## Spiritual Resilience

The novel's most profound treatment of faith appears in moments of loss and disappointment. When characters confront the limits of what effort and talent can achieve against racial barriers, faith offers a language for endurance that secular frameworks of achievement cannot provide. This spiritual resilience is not presented as passivity or otherworldly escapism but as a practical resource for continuing to strive in the face of odds.

Joanna's eventual turn toward prayer in moments of crisis marks a significant development in her character:

> Joanna, who had hardly uttered a prayer outside of "Now I lay me," fell on her knees in the darkness. (021-010)

This scene registers faith not as doctrinal conformity but as a human response to extremity — consistent with the novel's broader treatment of religious belief as a lived resource rather than an abstract system.
