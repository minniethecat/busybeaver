# Isaiah Bye

Isaiah Bye is a pivotal ancestor figure in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *There Is Confusion*. His story, narrated in Chapter 3, traces the transformation of a poor black boy who, wounded by a white boy's casual cruelty, dedicates his life to self-education and the uplift of his people. He represents the post-Emancipation generation that transformed freedom from a legal status into a lived reality of achievement and dignity.

## Family and Origins

Isaiah was the son of [[joshua-bye|Joshua Bye]] and Belle Potter Bye. [[joshua-bye|Joshua]], though born in slavery, had never experienced its "more hideous aspects" — he was "a very little boy when his freedom came to him" (003-007). His father, Ceazer Bye, had fought in the American Revolution, and his mother, [[judy-bye|Judy]], preserved the family memory of that service (003-008).

After Emancipation, [[joshua-bye|Joshua]] continued working for the white [[family-in-there-is-confusion|Bye family]] — Aaron and Dinah Bye — who had been his former owners. He was a "born agriculturist" with "an uncanny knowledge of planting, of grafting, of fertilizing," responsible for making the Bye peach orchards near what is now Bryn Mawr productive (003-010). Though many farmers tried to hire him away, [[joshua-bye|Joshua]] remained with [[aaron-bye|Aaron Bye]] out of a "blind, invincible attachment" inherited from his mother (003-010).

Upon [[joshua-bye|Joshua]]'s [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]] to Belle Potter, the white Byes gave him a large Family Bible "bound in leather and with an Apocrypha," inscribed: "To [[joshua-bye|Joshua]] and Belle Bye from Aaron and Dinah Bye. By their fruits ye shall know them" (003-008). This Bible became a crucial artifact in the [[family-in-there-is-confusion|Bye family]] history and would pass down through generations.

## The Formative Incident

The defining moment of Isaiah's childhood came through his friendship with [[meriwether-bye|Meriwether Bye]], the youngest son of Aaron and Dinah's ten children. The two boys — "black Isaiah and white Meriwether" — were raised together, with Meriwether visiting Isaiah's home at Fourth and Coates Streets (now Fairmount Avenue) as often as Isaiah appeared at the Bye house on Fourth and Spruce (003-012).

When Isaiah showed Meriwether the family Bible inscription, the white boy explained it was the [[family-in-there-is-confusion|Bye family]] motto. Isaiah, who "used to pore absorbedly as a boy over this book with its pictures and long old-fashioned *S*," misunderstood the inscription as referring literally to fruit trees — [[joshua-bye|Joshua]] worked in orchards, after all (003-009, 003-016).

The cruel clarification came on a hot July afternoon. Meriwether, walking home from the brickyard where Isaiah worked, explained the metaphor: "Good trees, good fruit; rich, important people, rich important sons" (003-017). When Isaiah asked what he himself would become, Meriwether — "his own position so secure" — answered with devastating innocence: "Your father and your father's father were slaves. ... So I s'pose that's what you'll be, a good servant" (003-018). He offered: "Isaiah, you can be my coachman. ... I'll point you out to some famous doctor from France and say, 'His father was a good servant to my father, and he's been a good servant to my father's son'" (003-019).

Isaiah's response was immediate and prophetic: "You'll never be able to say that about me" (003-020). He turned and ran, and never again entered the Bye house or orchards.

## Self-Education and Achievement

Meriwether's words became the engine of Isaiah's life. "Although Isaiah met the young fellow many times after that incident, and apparently with friendliness, he never in his heart forgave him" (003-024). He developed "a sturdy independence and an unyielding pride." Unlike many, he refused to be ashamed of his slave ancestry, measuring himself against his forebears with "honest pride. 'See what they were and how far I've gone'" (003-024).

His self-education was remarkable. The novel asks: "Who shall say how he increased his slender store of knowledge? How he learned to read wise books borrowed and bought as best he might? How he learned geography and history that made his heartbeats go wild since it told him of the French Revolution and how a whole nation once practically enslaved arose to a fuller, richer life?" (003-023).

His mother Belle provided crucial support. A natural insurgent who "would have been an insurgent in any walk of life," she told him wisely: "I don't believe that you kin go as fur as you dream. Too many things agin you fur that, boy. But you kin die much further along the road than when you was born. Never forget that" (003-022). This advice saved Isaiah "from the initial mistake of aiming too high and of coming utterly to smash. Yet he accomplished wonders" (003-023).

His resentment toward the white Byes crystallized into a political consciousness. He resented "the assurance with which the white Byes took their wealth and position and power. 'Hoisted themselves on the backs of the black Byes'" (003-025). But his response was constructive, not destructive: "The white Byes had gone far, but the black Byes having now that greatest of all gifts, freedom, would go far, too. They would be leaders of other black men" (003-026).

## The School on Vine Street

The culmination of Isaiah's life was the founding of a school for colored youth on Vine Street in [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia. The novel concludes his story with the assertion: "No name and no figure in colored life in [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia was ever better be[[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|love]]d and more revered than his" (003-026).

## Legacy and Narrative Significance

Isaiah Bye's story serves as the ancestral foundation for the [[family-in-there-is-confusion|Bye family]] line that produces [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]], one of the novel's central characters. His journey from brickyard laborer to revered educator embodies the post-Emancipation African American experience of self-transformation through education and moral will.

The inscription "By their fruits ye shall know them" — originally a marker of the white Byes' paternalistic ownership — becomes, through Isaiah's life, a statement of the black Byes' independent achievement. He reappropriated the motto by producing his own fruits: knowledge, leadership, and generations of free, educated descendants.
