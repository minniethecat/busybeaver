# Alice Talbert

Alice Talbert is a supporting character in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *There Is Confusion*, representing the established colored bourgeoisie of [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia. As the daughter of Lawyer Talbert and his secretary, [[alice-graves|Alice]] moves with assurance through [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia's elite social circles, where her cool treatment of [[maggie-ellersley]] dramatizes the class barriers that complicate the novel's broader themes of racial solidarity and individual aspiration.

## Family and Social Standing

Alice Talbert comes from a prominent [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia family. Her father, Lawyer Talbert, maintains an office at the corner of Fifteenth and Lombard Streets, where [[alice-graves|Alice]] serves as his secretary. The Talbert household on Christian Street hosts the [[family-in-there-is-confusion|Marshall family]] during their visits to [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia, establishing the Talberts as part of the network of respectable colored families that spans both [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia and New York.

The Talbert family is sizable: [[alice-graves|Alice]] has two brothers, one an undertaker "with a disposition far less lugubrious than his calling," and the other a student at the Wharton School of Finance at the University of Pennsylvania, who plans to read law later at Harvard. Two sisters, both engaged to medical students at Howard University, complete the household. John Talbert, [[joanna-marshall|Joanna Marshall]]'s cousin, is a Zeta Gamma man — the equivalent of Phi Beta Kappa — at his school, though he is denied the honor due to racial prejudice.

[[alice-graves|Alice]]'s father is described as sufficiently respected in [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia's colored community that [[henderson-neal|Henderson Neal]] and [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie Ellersley]] seek his legal services for a divorce. The Talberts thus occupy a position of professional prestige, social influence, and economic security — precisely the world that [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie Ellersley]], with her working-class origins and compromised reputation, struggles to enter.

## Appearance and Manner

[[alice-graves|Alice]] is introduced through [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s admiring but intimidated gaze:

> Maggie's landlady introduced her to one girl, a Miss Talbert from Philadelphia, who came up on the piazza one day to inquire for a former boarder. She was brown, not pretty, rather plainly but well dressed, with a beautiful manner. An atmosphere of niceness hung about her [1589-CCC].

The description is revealing in its ambivalence: "not pretty" yet possessed of a "beautiful manner"; plainly dressed but wearing her plainness with assurance. [[alice-graves|Alice]]'s appeal is not physical beauty but social authority — the "atmosphere of niceness" that signals her secure position within the colored elite. For [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]], who has never possessed such security, [[alice-graves|Alice]] becomes both a model and a gatekeeper.

## Encounters with Maggie Ellersley

Alice Talbert's primary narrative function is to embody the social exclusivity that [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie Ellersley]] cannot penetrate. Their encounters form a compressed study in class rejection.

### Atlantic City

The first meeting occurs at [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s Atlantic City boarding house, where [[alice-graves|Alice]] is vacationing with her party. [[alice-graves|Alice]] "asked her to go in bathing, introduced her to a few people, beckoned her over to her table at lunch" [1595-CCC]. This initial friendliness proves temporary; [[alice-graves|Alice]] and her party leave after three days, and [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] is "almost as badly off as ever when she left." [[alice-graves|Alice]]'s kindness is the casual benevolence of the securely placed toward the outsider — extended without cost and withdrawn without thought.

### Christian Street

When [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] encounters [[alice-graves|Alice]] on Christian Street in [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia, the interaction turns colder. [[alice-graves|Alice]] "greeted her kindly but a bit doubtfully, spoke about the weather." When [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]], desperate for social connection, asks if she may visit [[alice-graves|Alice]]'s home, the response is pointed:

> Miss Talbert looked at her a little sadly, exchanged a few more banalities, and went on her assured way [1627-CCC].

That "little sadly" is characteristic of [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]]'s nuanced social observation — [[alice-graves|Alice]] is not cruel but simply unwilling to extend her social capital to someone of [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s uncertain standing. Her sadness acknowledges [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s plight without accepting responsibility for it.

### Lawyer Talbert's Office

When [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] visits Lawyer Talbert's office to pursue divorce proceedings against [[henderson-neal|Henderson Neal]], [[alice-graves|Alice]] is "quite remote on seeing [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]], until she learned that the latter's business was with the lawyer" [2162-CCC]. As her father's secretary, [[alice-graves|Alice]] polices access to professional services while simultaneously guarding the social boundary between the Talberts and the Ellersleys. Only the legitimacy of legal business — not social fellowship — compels [[alice-graves|Alice]] to acknowledge [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]].

### The YMCA Bazaar

At a charity bazaar on Christian Street, [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] attends with [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]] and enjoys Alice Talbert's discomfiture:

> The startled stare of Alice Talbert, who happened to be there that evening, afforded her endless satisfaction [2160-CCC].

For the first time, the power dynamic reverses: [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]], arm in arm with a Bye, can look back at [[alice-graves|Alice]]'s social exclusivity with something approaching contempt. [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] later reflects, "She would show Alice Talbert!" — making [[alice-graves|Alice]] the symbol of everything [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] hopes to overcome through her [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]] to [[peter-bye]].

## Voice of Social Judgment

[[alice-graves|Alice]]'s social exclusivity finds its sharpest expression in her judgment of [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]]'s engagement. She tells [[arabelle-morton|Arabelle Morton]] that she considers "Peter done for, ruined, if he married that gambler's wife. Cousin [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] did well to get rid of him" [2939-CCC]. The remark reveals several dimensions of [[alice-graves|Alice]]'s worldview: her easy equation of [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] with [[henderson-neal|Henderson Neal]]'s gambling ("that gambler's wife"), her sense of proprietorship over [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]]'s social standing, and her invocation of family ties ("Cousin [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]") to authorize her judgment.

[[arabelle-morton|Arabelle Morton]]'s reaction — laughter and a determination to meet [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] — suggests that [[alice-graves|Alice]]'s severity is not universally shared even within her own set. Arabelle, "of as unimpeachable standing as Miss Talbert, though her choice of friends was not so exclusive," represents an alternative model of social authority: one that can afford to be generous precisely because it is secure.

## Relationship with Joanna Marshall

Alice Talbert's connection to [[joanna-marshall]] runs through multiple channels: John Talbert is [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s cousin, the Talberts host the Marshalls in [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia, and [[alice-graves|Alice]] is part of the extended network of colored professional families that the novel maps across two cities. Late in the novel, when [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] is struggling with whether to intervene in [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s engagement to [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]], she plans to visit Alice Talbert in [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia:

> She might go down to Philadelphia to visit Alice Talbert. Yes, she would do that very soon. And then maybe she would see Maggie Ellersley — on the street, or even go and call on her [3085-CCC].

That [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] would use a visit to [[alice-graves|Alice]] as cover for seeing [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] underscores the social distance between the two women: even [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]], a Marshall and a celebrated dancer, feels she must approach [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] obliquely when Alice Talbert's social world is the frame of reference.

## Critical Significance

Alice Talbert belongs to the novel's gallery of women who enforce class boundaries within the colored community. She is not a villain — [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] does not write villains — but a realist's portrait of how social status, once achieved, becomes a resource to be protected. [[alice-graves|Alice]]'s "beautiful manner" and "atmosphere of niceness" are the very qualities that make her exclusivity so effective: she need not be rude to make [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] feel her unworthiness.

[[alice-graves|Alice]]'s function in the novel's social taxonomy is to demonstrate that the barriers [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie Ellersley]] faces are not only racial but also intra-racial. The colored elite of [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia — lawyers, businessmen, doctors — has its own hierarchies, its own gatekeepers, its own mechanisms of exclusion. Alice Talbert, as her father's secretary and a daughter of Christian Street, is one of those gatekeepers. That [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] gives her a "little sadly" look and a family of engaging brothers suggests that the gatekeeping is structural, not personal — [[alice-graves|Alice]], too, is constrained by the class system she enforces.
