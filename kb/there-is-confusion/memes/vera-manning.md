# Vera Manning

Vera Manning is a supporting character in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *There Is Confusion*, notable as one of the novel's most explicit treatments of racial passing. A member of [[joanna-marshall|Joanna Marshall]]'s old [[the-dance-in-there-is-confusion|dancing]]-class and a daughter of a successful Harlem businessman, Vera chooses to pass for white in downtown New York, severing ties with her colored acquaintances and abandoning her romance with [[harley-alexander]]. Her trajectory mirrors and contrasts with [[joanna-marshall]]'s own path — where [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] achieves distinction through talent and persistence, Vera seeks escape through racial disguise, and both women discover the emotional emptiness that can accompany their chosen routes.

## Family Background

Vera Manning comes from a prominent Harlem family. Her father is described as "a remarkably successful businessman," placing the Mannings among the upper echelon of New York's colored society. Along with her sister [[alice-graves|Alice]] Manning, Vera belonged to the same social set as the Marshall children and the daughters of other prominent colored professionals — "a physician, a lawyer, a journalist, a real-estate man among them."

The Manning family's standing in the community is such that [[joanna-marshall|Joanna Marshall]], reflecting on her own lack of social pedigree, groups the Manning sisters with the Marshalls and the Byes as families with genuine social capital:

> Other girls' fathers and mothers were "somebodies." Alice and Vera Manning's father was a remarkably successful businessman, old Joel Marshall was as famous in his way, she guessed, as Delmonico.

## Social Circle and the Dancing-Class

Vera was one of the core members of [[joanna-marshall|Joanna Marshall]]'s [[the-dance-in-there-is-confusion|dancing]]-class group in their youth, alongside [[helena-arnold|Helena Arnold]], [[sylvia-marshall|Sylvia Marshall]], Gertrude Moseley, and Elizabeth Beckett. This social formation serves as a marker of the [[family-in-there-is-confusion|Marshall family]]'s status in Harlem's colored elite — the daughters of doctors, lawyers, and businessmen who participated in the rituals of middle-class respectability.

The [[the-dance-in-there-is-confusion|dancing]]-class functions in the novel as a symbol of the social ambition and cultural aspiration of Harlem's colored bourgeoisie. Vera's membership in it places her squarely within this milieu, making her later decision to pass all the more significant: she is not an outsider seeking entry but an insider choosing exit.

## Racial Passing

Vera's decision to pass for white is the defining feature of her character arc. When [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] encounters her unexpectedly in downtown New York, the narrator notes that "the report in Harlem was that she was passing for white and had no desire to be recognized by her colored acquaintances." Vera herself acknowledges the situation candidly:

> "You see, I'm 'passing' just now — I know you've heard of it — and so I go into any of these places around here, but I never see any colored people."

The physical toll of passing is immediately visible to [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]], who observes that Vera "looked different from the Vera Manning she used to know. Her clothes showed her usual careful, even modish taste, but her face looked hard — 'reckless' — [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] suddenly decided; that was the word." This hardening of Vera's features externalizes the internal cost of living a divided identity.

When [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] asks why Vera chose this path, her answer reveals a profound alienation:

> "Oh, to get away from everybody and everything I'd ever known."

Vera's passing is not driven by economic necessity or professional ambition — she comes from a prosperous family — but by a more existential desire to escape the constraints of racial identity altogether. Yet the escape proves hollow; the novel suggests that cutting oneself off from community leaves an "awful emptiness."

## Relationship with Harley Alexander

Vera's romantic history centers on [[harley-alexander|Harley Alexander]], "a tall personable youth about her own color, a companion of [[harry-portor|Harry Portor]], [[brian-spencer|Brian Spencer]], and to a less degree of [[alec-marshall|Alec Marshall]]." Harley had been "the constant shadow of Vera Manning" during their youth, and the two were expected to marry.

But Vera's ambitions for [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]] — she confesses to [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] that "most of all I wanted, I expected to marry" — were not fulfilled by Harley. Vera describes the end of their romance to [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] with bitter resignation:

> "So that," Vera ended wryly, "was the end of my young romance."

The [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|courtship]]'s failure is recounted later in the novel when Harley, now a soldier en route to France during World War I, tells [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]] about his "disastrous [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|courtship]] of Vera Manning." The adjective "disastrous" suggests that the rupture was painful on both sides, and Harley's subsequent friendship with Peter aboard the troopship — two men talking through their romantic disappointments — forms part of the novel's broader meditation on [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|love]], loss, and the difficulty of sustaining relationships across the pressures of race and ambition.

## Later Appearances

Vera reappears late in the novel at a society gathering celebrating [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s success as a dancer. On this occasion, Vera "grinned at her impishly and announced to the room that she and Miss Marshall were old friends. They had been schoolmates." Her mischievous demeanor and public claim on [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] suggest a woman who has made peace — or at least an accommodation — with her double life:

> "When I was a child," said Vera impudently, "my mother sent me to public school for almost a year. She said she wanted me to be a real democrat."

Vera's brother, [[tom-mason|Tom]] Manning, enters [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s circle during this period of fame and popularity, eventually proposing [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]]. [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] declines, finding him "charming and sympathetic, but he was too white and she did not want a [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]] which would keep the difficulties of color more than ever before her eyes." This subplot extends the Manning family's thematic role — Vera passes to escape race; [[tom-mason|Tom]], almost white, represents the possibility of a [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]] that would erase color; and [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s refusal of him affirms her commitment to living as a colored woman, on her own terms.

## Critical Significance

Vera Manning serves as a foil to [[joanna-marshall|Joanna Marshall]] within the novel's examination of racial identity and ambition. Where [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] pursues greatness within the colored community — becoming a celebrated dancer who is visibly and proudly Negro — Vera pursues escape from that community through racial passing. [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s path leads to professional fulfillment but personal emptiness; Vera's leads to personal freedom but social isolation. Neither woman finds uncomplicated happiness.

[[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] uses Vera's story to critique passing not as a moral failure but as a tragedy of social conditions. Vera does not pass out of shame or self-hatred; she passes because she wants "to get away from everybody and everything I'd ever known." Her father's wealth cannot protect her from the daily indignities of racism — the "awful emptiness" she experiences is the product of a society that makes belonging and dignity mutually exclusive for Black women.

Vera also contributes to the novel's broader social realism. The Mannings — a successful businessman father, two daughters in the [[the-dance-in-there-is-confusion|dancing]]-class set, a son who becomes part of [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s entourage — represent the diversity of experience within a single colored family. Vera's passing is not an isolated phenomenon but one possible response among many to the pressures of American racial hierarchy, and [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] treats it with the same unsentimental clarity she brings to [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s ambition, [[sylvia-marshall|Sylvia]]'s social climbing, and [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s struggle for respectability.
