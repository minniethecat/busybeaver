# Miss Mason

Miss Mason is a minor figure in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *There Is Confusion*, appearing in a single scene as [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie Ellersley]]'s neighbor in the [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia apartment building where she lives with [[henderson-neal]] after their [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]]. Though her role is limited, Miss Mason provides a rare instance in the novel of spontaneous, uncalculated neighborly warmth.

## A Neighbor's Visit

Miss Mason enters when a misdelivered letter brings her to [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s door. Annie, the building's maid, has given her [[henderson-neal|Henderson Neal]]'s letter by mistake:

> "I got your husband's letter by mistake. He's Mr. H. Neal ain't he?" She held out the letter glancing about the room. "You've fixed it up real pretty here. The last roomers here didn't have nothin' — only a bed and trunk and a whisky bottle and cockroaches." (Ch. XIV)

The visit is a small masterclass in neighborly reconnaissance: Miss Mason returns the letter, appraises the apartment, and establishes her credentials as a long-term resident who has seen roomers come and go. Her observation that [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] has "fixed it up real pretty" is casual praise that nevertheless confirms what the reader already suspects — that [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s domestic instincts run toward order and respectability, whatever the limitations of her circumstances.

## Frank Curiosity

Miss Mason's curiosity is open and unembarrassed:

> Miss Mason was frankly curious. "You here alone all day? What do you do while your husband's to work?" "Oh, clean, and sew and — and nap," Maggie laughed a little. "Don't you want to come to see me sometime, now, this afternoon?" (Ch. XIV)

The exchange is notable for what it lacks: there is no social calculation, no class anxiety, no guardedness. [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]], who spends so much of the novel navigating the charged social terrain of the Talberts, the Marshalls, and [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia's colored elite, meets in Miss Mason someone who asks direct questions and extends direct invitations without subtext. Miss Mason "thought she might's well, your room seems bigger'n mine 'cause we've got a piano and you've got a table there. Say, s'pose I was to bring my sewing down, and I could help you even off your skirt" (Ch. XIV).

The proposal — to bring her sewing down and share the work — is the novel's most unadorned image of female companionship. No ambition, no social climbing, no romantic rivalry: just two women with their mending in an apartment on a quiet afternoon.

## The North Pennsylvania Accent

[[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] takes care to note that Miss Mason speaks with "the North Pennsylvania accent which carried with it something very wholesome and grateful." The regional detail is characteristic of [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]]'s attentiveness to the social geography of voice: just as [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s speech marks her class origins and [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s her education, Miss Mason's accent identifies her as a product of a particular regional culture — rural, unpretentious, "wholesome." In a novel preoccupied with the performance of social identity, a voice that carries no aspiration beyond what it already is constitutes a quiet anomaly.

## Critical Significance

Miss Mason's single scene functions as a palate cleanser in the novel's dense social architecture. She is not connected to the Byes or the Marshalls; she has no role in the plot; she does not reappear. Yet her presence registers something essential about [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s married life in [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia: that it contains, alongside the growing misery of her [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]] to [[henderson-neal|Henderson Neal]], ordinary moments of human contact — the neighbor who returns a misdelivered letter and offers to help with the sewing.

In a novel structured around ambition, exclusion, and the long climb toward respectability, Miss Mason represents an alternative mode of social being: one that requires no credentials, performs no exclusions, and asks only whether you'd like company while you hem a skirt.
