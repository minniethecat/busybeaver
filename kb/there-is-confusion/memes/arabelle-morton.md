# Arabelle Morton

Arabelle Morton is a minor but socially significant figure in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *There Is Confusion*. A young woman of "unimpeachable standing" in [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia's colored society, she serves as a social bridge — introducing [[peter-bye]] to the city's respectable circles and later sponsoring [[maggie-ellersley]]'s acceptance among the younger set at a moment when more exclusive figures like [[alice-talbert]] have closed ranks against her.

## Role in Philadelphia Society

Arabelle Morton presides over a welcoming household where [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]] spends his Fridays and Sundays during his [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia years. [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] introduces her through Peter's first visit to her home, when he is struck by the contrast between the Morton household and the ambitious, striving atmosphere he encounters elsewhere:

> Peter spoke of it to Arabelle Morton, the last girl to whose house Louis took him. ... Peter thought so, too. He liked Arabelle already and her friendly shallowness. (Ch. XI)

Peter's regular attendance at Arabelle's home — "Friday and Sunday he spent at Arabelle Morton's or at Lawyer Talbert's on Christian Street" (Ch. XI) — establishes her house as one of the two poles of his [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia social life. Where the Talbert household represents professional aspiration and social ambition, the Morton house offers uncomplicated sociability.

## Character

[[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]]'s portrait of Arabelle is notably free of moral judgment. Her key qualities — "good-natured shallowness, her frank determination not to be a 'highbrow' and her complete social assurance" (Ch. XXVII) — are presented as functional attributes rather than character flaws. Unlike the more exclusive [[alice-talbert]], Arabelle's "choice of friends was not so exclusive," and it is precisely this openness that proves decisive for [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie Ellersley]]'s social fortunes.

Arabelle's social assurance is the product of secure standing. She does not need to prove anything, and this security frees her from the anxious gatekeeping that characterizes [[alice-talbert|Alice Talbert]]'s interactions with those outside her circle. Where [[alice-graves|Alice]] polices the boundary, Arabelle simply ignores it.

## Sponsorship of Maggie Ellersley

Arabelle's most consequential action in the novel is her sponsorship of [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie Ellersley]]. When [[alice-talbert]] declares Peter "done for, ruined, if he married that gambler's wife. Cousin [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] did well to get rid of him," Arabelle takes the opposite course. She laughs off the scandal, expresses a desire to meet "that good-looking Bye boy's" fiancée, and visits [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] in person — inviting her to the Morton house (Ch. XXVII).

Through Arabelle's sponsorship, [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] is "taken up" by the younger set, and "henceforth her lines were comparatively easy." The social validation Arabelle provides — the imprimatur of a young woman whose own standing is beyond question — is precisely what [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] needs to counteract the snubs of the older generation.

## Sister and Family

Arabelle's sister, [[selma-morton|Selma Morton]], is mentioned in connection with a racial incident at her Master's degree graduation ceremony, which Peter recounts as an illustration of Northern racial prejudice (Ch. XXII). The Morton family's educational attainments — [[selma-morton|Selma]]'s graduate degree, the family's secure social position — establish them as firmly within [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia's colored elite, even as Arabelle herself affects a deliberate anti-intellectualism.

## Critical Significance

Arabelle Morton represents an alternative mode of social being in a novel preoccupied with ambition, exclusion, and the long climb toward respectability. Her "friendly shallowness" is not a failing but a functional trait: it enables her to extend welcome without calculation, to sponsor without demanding credentials. In a social world where every invitation carries subtext, Arabelle's uncomplicated hospitality makes her house one of the few spaces in the novel where social acceptance is offered freely — and it is this freedom that changes [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie Ellersley]]'s life.
