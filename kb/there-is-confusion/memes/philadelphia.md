# Philadelphia, Pennsylvania

**Philadelphia** is the gravitational center of [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s *There Is Confusion* (1924). The city's streets, neighborhoods, and social hierarchies shape the lives of the Bye and Marshall families across three generations, from the waning days of slavery through the tumult of [[world-war-i|World War I]].

## A City of Intertwined Histories

The novel opens its historical lens wide, tracing the Bye family's presence in Philadelphia back to the late eighteenth century:

> "As far back as the last decades of the eighteenth century there had been white Byes and black Byes in Philadelphia. The black Byes were known to be the chattels of Aaron and Dinah Bye, Quakers, who with their quiet insistence on man's unconsciousness of his real beliefs freed their slaves and then hired them."

This origin story — Quaker emancipation, followed by continued economic entanglement — establishes Philadelphia as a city where racial intimacy and hierarchy coexist. The white Byes live at Fourth and Spruce Streets; the black Byes at Fourth and Coates (now Fairmount Avenue). The geography of the city maps directly onto its racial geography.

### The Bye School

Philadelphia was also the site of one of the most significant institutions in Black Philadelphia's history — the school founded by the Bye family:

> "No name and no figure in colored life in Philadelphia was ever better beloved and more revered than his."

The school, located on Vine Street, educated generations of Black youth and established the Byes as pillars of Philadelphia's Black community — a legacy that Peter Bye inherits and must reckon with.

## Social Geography

Philadelphia in *There Is Confusion* is not a single community but a landscape of carefully demarcated social zones:

| Neighborhood | Association |
|-------------|------------|
| **Fourth and Coates / Fairmount Avenue** | The historical black Bye residence, working-class roots |
| **Fourth and Spruce** | The white Bye residence, old Quaker wealth |
| **Vine Street** | The Bye school, Black educational aspiration |
| **Girard Avenue** | Merchant-class Black respectability (the Sharples family) |
| **Fifty-Ninth Street** | The Marshall family home, upwardly mobile Black middle class |

The distances between these addresses are modest — a few miles at most — but the social distance is immense. Fauset uses Philadelphia's geography to map the class stratifications within Black life itself.

## A Hub of Black Middle-Class Life

Philadelphia in Fauset's portrayal is a center of Black institutional life — churches, schools, social clubs, and professional networks. The city's Black elite gather at dances, concerts, and benefits, forming a self-conscious community of "the better class of colored people." Joanna Marshall's ambition to become a celebrated performer is fueled by Philadelphia's competitive social scene:

> "Philadelphians were not as a rule as strikingly dressed as the folks, say, from Washington, but they had a better time. They seemed bound by some kind of tie, family, perhaps — which made them one regardless of clothes or accomplishments."

This observation — that Philadelphia's Black community values family ties over superficial display — is one of the novel's most pointed social commentaries.

## Contrast with Other Cities

Philadelphia is constantly measured against other cities in the novel's geography of Black aspiration:

- **Richmond, Virginia**: The Marshall family's Southern origins, representing a more openly hostile racial regime
- **Washington, D.C.**: A city of "strikingly dressed" strivers, where appearance matters more than substance
- **[[new-york-city|New York City]] / [[harlem|Harlem]]**: The emerging center of Black cultural life, where Joanna and Peter eventually make their mark
- **[[baltimore|Baltimore]]**: Another node in the mid-Atlantic Black social network

Philadelphia stands between these poles — older than Harlem, more institutionally established than Washington, more cosmopolitan than Richmond. It is Fauset's representative city of the "old" Black middle class, with its family Bibles, its careful respectability, and its unspoken codes of conduct.

## The City at War

When [[world-war-i|World War I]] arrives, Philadelphia becomes a point of departure and return. Peter Bye ships out from the city; the white [[meriwether-bye|Meriwether Bye]] visits from Bryn Mawr. The war cracks open the city's provincial self-containment, exposing its residents to France, to combat, and to new possibilities for racial solidarity — and disillusionment.

## Themes

| Theme | Expression in Philadelphia |
|-------|---------------------------|
| **Racial Geography** | The city's streets encode centuries of racial hierarchy and intimacy |
| **Class Within Race** | Philadelphia's Black community is internally stratified by neighborhood, profession, and family lineage |
| **Institution Building** | The Bye school, churches, and clubs represent Black self-determination within a segregated city |
| **Historical Memory** | The late-18th-century origins of the Bye family saga root the novel's present in Philadelphia's deep past |
