# Mrs. Larrabee

Mrs. Larrabee is a minor figure in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *There Is Confusion*, appearing as the landlady of the boarding house in West [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia where [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]] lives with his fraternity brothers. Though her presence in the novel is brief, she anchors two emotionally significant moments in Peter's narrative arc.

## The West Philadelphia Boarding House

Mrs. Larrabee presides over the rooms on Sansom Street where Peter and his fraternity brothers lodge during his [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia period. When [[peter-bye]] returns to [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia on a bitter winter day, she greets him with characteristic warmth:

> "They're all out I think," the landlady, Mrs. Larrabee, told him. She gave him a friendly smile. "You can run up, though, and see." She was right, they were out, but the rooms were warm and comfortable. (Ch. XIII)

The description is economical but suggestive: a landlady who smiles at her boarders, who allows them to "run up" even when their friends are absent, whose rooms are "warm and comfortable." In a novel where domestic spaces carry heavy symbolic weight — the Ellersleys' "awful place," the Marshalls' respectable home, the Talberts' Christian Street residence — Mrs. Larrabee's boarding house occupies a middle register: modest but decent, the kind of place where a medical student of limited means can find shelter and community.

## Witness to Joy

Mrs. Larrabee's most vivid narrative moment occurs on New Year's Eve, when Peter receives a letter from [[joanna-marshall]] affirming her [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|love]]:

> His spirits went up, up. "Good night," he called to Mrs. Larrabee. "Happy New Year." It wasn't so cold after all, he thought. Anyway, it wouldn't do him any harm to stretch his legs a bit. (Ch. XV)

The scene is structured around a contrast between interior warmth and exterior cold, between the contained joy of a letter read in a landlady's house and the expansive joy that propels Peter out into the [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia night. Mrs. Larrabee, who receives only a "Good night" and a "Happy New Year," is the unwitting witness to a turning point in Peter's romantic life.

## Peter's Domestic Circumstances

Mrs. Larrabee's boarding house also serves as a measure of Peter's financial precarity. He has moved there after the house inherited by his great-uncle's daughter, Mrs. Boyd, proved unavailable to him: "Of course he couldn't expect anything of her. Her father was only his great-uncle, and she had her own children to look after. He had moved to Mrs. Larrabee's in West [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia, with some of his fraternity brothers" (Ch. XV).

The arrangement — fraternity brothers sharing rooms, the cost of medical books and instruments described as "pure robbery," Peter's confession that "I do what playing I can, but I confess I'm pretty well strapped" — places Mrs. Larrabee's establishment within the novel's careful accounting of the economic pressures on its aspiring characters. She is not merely a landlady but a marker on the map of Peter's material circumstances.

## Critical Significance

Mrs. Larrabee exemplifies [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]]'s technique of investing minor figures with atmospheric significance. She speaks few lines, has no backstory, and exerts no influence on the plot; yet her "friendly smile" and warm rooms establish the texture of Peter's [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia life — a life of fraternity, modest comfort, and quiet respectability that stands in pointed contrast to both the childhood instability he endured under [[mrs-reading]]'s roof and the elite domestic spaces he will later enter through [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]].
