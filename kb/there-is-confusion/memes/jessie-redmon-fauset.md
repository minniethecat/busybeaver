# Jessie Redmon Fauset

Jessie Redmon Fauset (1882–1961) was an African American novelist, poet, essayist, literary editor, and educator. As the literary editor of *The Crisis* — the official magazine of the NAACP — from 1919 to 1926, she played a pivotal role in nurturing and promoting the writers of the Harlem Renaissance. Her four novels, including *There Is Confusion* (1924), explore the lives of educated, middle-class African Americans and the tensions of race, class, and gender in early twentieth-century America.

## Biography

Born on April 27, 1882, in Fredericksville (now Lawnside), New Jersey, Fauset grew up in [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia. She attended the [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia High School for Girls, where she was the only African American student, and graduated as valedictorian. She earned a scholarship to Cornell University, where she was one of the first black women elected to Phi Beta Kappa, graduating in 1905 with a degree in classical languages.

After graduation, Fauset taught French and Latin at the M Street High School (later Dunbar High School) in [[washington-dc|Washington, D.C.]], where she remained until 1919. She earned a master's degree in French from the University of Pennsylvania in 1919.

In 1919, W. E. B. Du Bois invited Fauset to become the literary editor of *The Crisis*. In this role, she discovered, mentored, and published many of the writers who would define the Harlem Renaissance, including Langston Hughes, Countee Cullen, Claude McKay, and Jean Toomer. Hughes later credited her as one of the "midwives" of the movement.

## Literary Career

Fauset published four novels: *There Is Confusion* (1924), *Plum Bun* (1928), *The Chinaberry Tree* (1931), and *Comedy: American Style* (1933). Her fiction focuses on the experiences of the African American middle class — educated, cultured, and aspirational — and explores themes of racial identity, passing, [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]], and the position of women.

*There Is Confusion*, her first novel, tells the intertwined stories of [[joanna-marshall|Joanna Marshall]], an ambitious singer, and [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]], a would-be surgeon, against the backdrop of the Marshall and [[family-in-there-is-confusion|Bye family]] histories. The novel is notable for its exploration of intra-racial class dynamics, its engagement with the concept of the "New Negro," and its insistence on the legitimacy of black middle-class aspiration as a subject for serious fiction.

Fauset also published poetry, short stories, and essays. Her poem "Oriflamme" appears in many anthologies of African American literature. After leaving *The Crisis* in 1926, she returned to teaching and continued to write, though her later novels received less critical attention than her earlier work.

## Significance

Fauset is now recognized as a central, though historically underappreciated, figure of the Harlem Renaissance. Alongside Nella Larsen and Zora [[henderson-neal|Neal]]e Hurston, she is one of the major women novelists of the period. Her work offers a distinctive perspective within the Renaissance — focusing not on folk culture or proletarian struggle but on the educated elite, the "talented tenth," and the particular burdens and opportunities of black middle-class identity.
