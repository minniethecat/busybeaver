# Music in There Is Confusion

Music is the primary vehicle of artistic ambition in *There Is Confusion*, centered on [[joanna-marshall]]'s determination to become a concert singer. Through Joanna's pursuit of musical excellence, Fauset explores music's dual function as personal fulfillment and racial advancement strategy.

## Joanna's Musical Vocation

Joanna Marshall's defining characteristic is her devotion to singing. From childhood, she displays an unusual seriousness about her art, practicing dancing and singing with a discipline that sets her apart from her peers:

> Joanna astonished him. "In my choir—I sing there already! No! Every moment that I can spare from my school work I'm going to spend in taking lessons." (006-033)

Her teacher [[madame-caldwell]] recognizes her talent and tells her that artistic excellence can serve as a weapon against racial prejudice: "if there's anything that will break down prejudice it will be equality or perhaps even superiority on the part of colored people in the arts."

This formulation — achieving racial progress through individual artistic excellence — captures a distinctive strain of Harlem Renaissance thought. Joanna internalizes this mission, seeing her singing not merely as personal expression but as a form of racial service. Her ambition to perform on the concert stage represents both self-realization and a challenge to the racial barriers that confined African American performers to segregated venues and limited repertoire.

## The Dance as Musical Expression

The novel's title is drawn from the world of performance: "There Is Confusion" refers to a dance number that Joanna performs. In a pivotal scene at the [[grant-banquet]], Joanna's dance performance causes a stir, and the aftermath — involving [[peter-bye]]'s jealous reaction — sets in motion much of the novel's romantic plot.

The dance sequence is significant because it represents Joanna's bodily investment in her art. Unlike singing, which can be abstracted from the performer's physical presence, dance makes the body itself the medium of artistic expression. For a novel concerned with the constraints placed on African American bodies, this is a charged choice. Joanna's dance becomes a site where race, gender, and artistic expression converge.

## Classical Music and Racial Boundaries

Fauset situates Joanna's musical ambition within the tradition of European classical music — the concert stage, not the vaudeville theater or the jazz club. This choice reflects the novel's broader investment in portraying middle-class African American culture as equally sophisticated and equally entitled to the "high" cultural tradition.

The novel registers the racial boundaries that constrained this access. Joanna must contend with the reality that concert stages were largely closed to African American performers, regardless of talent. Her determination to breach these barriers through sheer excellence — "I'll be great, too" — captures both the idealism and the burden of the "racial uplift" philosophy that Fauset both represents and complicates.

## Music and Marriage

The novel connects Joanna's musical ambition to its marriage plot. [[peter-bye]]'s discomfort with Joanna's performance career becomes a central romantic conflict. His jealousy at the Grant Banquet, where Joanna's dancing attracts male attention, reveals the tension between artistic self-display and the expectations of respectable womanhood.

Joanna's eventual choice of Peter over a career on the European concert stage represents a complex resolution. Critics have debated whether this represents a retreat from artistic ambition or a redefinition of fulfillment that incorporates love and family alongside creative achievement.
