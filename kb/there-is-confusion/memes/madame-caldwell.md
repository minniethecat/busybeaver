# Madame Caldwell

Madame Caldwell is a minor but pivotal character in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s *There Is Confusion* (1924). A celebrated colored singer, her recognition of ten-year-old [[joanna-marshall]]'s talent during an Easter Sunday church service sets in motion [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s lifelong pursuit of a musical career.

## Background and Perspective

Madame Caldwell's career was forged through hardship. The novel notes she "had won her own laurels through bitter experience in various studios, meeting insult, indifference and unkindness with an unyielding front, which brought her finally consideration, a grudging interest, sometimes a genuine appreciation" (001-012).

Despite her success, she harbors bitterness about the sacrifices required of a Black woman artist in America. She once said, "But no daughter of mine, if she has the voice of an angel shall go through what I have suffered" (001-012). This remark reveals a protective instinct born of personal experience — she would spare others, especially other Black women, the degradations she endured.

## The Easter Discovery

During an Easter Sunday service, Madame Caldwell sings an anthem "among the tall white lilies" at the [[family-in-there-is-confusion|Marshall family]]'s church. Afterward, she dines at the Marshall home, where ten-year-old [[joanna-marshall|Joanna Marshall]] approaches her (001-012).

[[joanna-marshall|Joanna]], with characteristic boldness, declares, "I can sing like you," and proceeds to sing most of the anthem from memory. Her voice — "already free of the shrillness of childhood and beginning to assume that liquid golden quality which so distinguished it later" — astonishes Madame Caldwell (001-012).

## Catalyst Role

Madame Caldwell's recognition of [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s talent serves as the catalyst for the novel's central plot. She seizes [[joel-marshall]]'s arm and urges him: "Get her a teacher, Mr. Marshall. She has a voice in ten thousand. Poor child, how you will have to work!" (001-012).

This moment confirms for [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] and her father that singing could be her path to greatness. Madame Caldwell's verdict carries authority precisely because she knows, from bitter experience, what that path entails — and yet she does not dissuade [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] from pursuing it. Her urging is at once a prophecy and a warning: the voice is extraordinary, but the work will be punishing.

## Critical Significance

Madame Caldwell appears in only a single scene, yet her function in the novel's architecture is essential. She represents the older generation of Black artists who cleared a path through "insult, indifference and unkindness" — and who, despite their scars, can still recognize and champion the talent of the next generation. Her declaration that [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] "has a voice in ten thousand" is the novel's originating judgment, the external validation that transforms a child's self-belief into a family project.

In a novel structured around ambition, Madame Caldwell embodies the cost of artistic achievement for Black Americans. Her presence at the Easter service links [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s secular ambition to the sacred space of the church, suggesting that the talent she recognizes is not merely a skill but a gift — and one that carries obligations beyond personal glory.
