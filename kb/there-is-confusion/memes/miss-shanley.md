# Miss Shanley

Miss Shanley is [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]] and [[joanna-marshall|Joanna Marshall]]'s teacher at the public school on Fifty-second Street in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *There Is Confusion*. A thorough if unimaginative educator, Miss Shanley presides over the classroom where [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]]'s dormant brilliance first surfaces, transforming him from a problem student into an "undiscovered diamond."

## Classroom Role

Miss Shanley is [[joanna-marshall|Joanna Marshall]]'s teacher when [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]], newly arrived in the neighborhood under his aunt [[susan-graves|Miss Susan Graves]]'s care, is placed in her fifth-grade classroom:

> "We'll try him," said the principal doubtfully, "in the fifth grade. I'll take him to Miss Shanley's room" [372-CCC].

Her greeting is described without sentiment: "She greeted Peter without enthusiasm, not because he was colored but because he was clearly a problem." Miss Shanley's pragmatism places her among the novel's unsentimental professionals — teachers who assess children by their classroom performance rather than their personal histories.

## Relationship with Joanna Marshall

Miss Shanley's classroom dynamic revolves around [[joanna-marshall|Joanna Marshall]]'s reliable excellence:

> Joanna clearly stood for something in the class. Peter noted a little enviously the quality of the tone in which Miss Shanley addressed her. To other children she said, "Gertrude, can you tell me about the Articles of Confederation?" Usually she implied a doubt, which Gertrude usually justified. But she was sure of Joanna. The tenseness of her attitude might be seen to relax; her mentality prepared momentarily for a rest. "Joanna will now tell us—" she would announce [378-CCC].

[[joanna-marshall|Joanna]], drilled by her father [[joel-marshall]] in the principle that "final perfection is built on small intermediate perfections," becomes the teacher's dependable anchor — the student Miss Shanley can always call upon when others falter.

## Peter's Breakthrough

The novel's most extended classroom scene centers on Miss Shanley's physiology review, conducted in preparation for important examinations. After "two days to geography, two more to grammar, another to history, one to the rather puzzling consideration of that mysterious study, physiology," Miss Shanley finds herself in despair:

> Poor Miss Shanley was in despair. She could not call on Joanna for everything. Pupil after pupil had failed. Her eye roved over the room and fell on Peter's black head [394-CCC].

When Peter, drawing on the anatomical instruction he received from his father [[meriwether-bye]], delivers an impromptu lecture on the digestive system — sketching the mouth cavity, gullet, pancreatic juice, and thoracic duct — Miss Shanley is stunned:

> Miss Shanley stared at him in amazement. This jewel, this undiscovered diamond! [404-CCC]

Her subsequent declaration — "If all the children would just get their lessons like [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]] and [[joanna-marshall|Joanna Marshall]]" [412-CCC] — pairs the two future protagonists for the first time, establishing the intellectual parity between [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s disciplined study and Peter's inherited genius that will shape their relationship.

## Later Recollection

Late in the novel, when [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] must improvise a tourniquet during a crisis, Miss Shanley's classroom instruction resurfaces:

> Somewhere out of the past floated a memory of Miss Shanley's direction how to make a tourniquet, one of the things Joanna had meant to forget after she grew up. Subconscious memories guided her fingers [3321-CCC].

The moment quietly vindicates Miss Shanley's thoroughness — lessons meant to be forgotten after childhood prove unexpectedly vital in adulthood.

## Critical Significance

Miss Shanley is a minor character who serves a pivotal structural function: her classroom is the site where [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]] and [[joanna-marshall|Joanna Marshall]] first encounter each other as intellectual equals. Her recognition of Peter's ability — however belated — provides the first institutional validation of the [[family-in-there-is-confusion|Bye family]]'s anatomical legacy, and her pairing of "[[peter-bye|Peter Bye]] and [[joanna-marshall|Joanna Marshall]]" as the class's twin exemplars foreshadows the novel's central romance. As a character, she represents the competent, unglamorous professionalism that [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] consistently values throughout the novel.
