# Harley Alexander

Harley Alexander is a supporting character in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *There Is Confusion*. A dentist by profession and a member of the same Harlem social circle as [[harry-portor|Harry Portor]], [[brian-spencer|Brian Spencer]], and [[alec-marshall|Alec Marshall]], Harley appears in two distinct contexts: as [[vera-manning]]'s former suitor in the novel's central chapters, and as [[peter-bye]]'s wartime companion and confidant in the World War I sections.

## Character and Appearance

Harley is introduced through [[joanna-marshall|Joanna Marshall]]'s recollection as "a tall personable youth about her own color, a companion of [[harry-portor|Harry Portor]], [[brian-spencer|Brian Spencer]], and to a less degree of her own brother [[alec-marshall|Alec]]." What [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] "especially remembered was that he had been the constant shadow of [[vera-manning|Vera Manning]]" — a description that establishes both his devotion to Vera and his position on the periphery of the [[family-in-there-is-confusion|Marshall family]]'s inner circle.

Physically, Harley is noted for his soldierly appearance. During the war, [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]] observes him at the training camp in Lathus, France, and notes approvingly that "Alexander, one of the trimmest men in the regiment, was looking unusually shipshape, almost dapper." He responds to Peter's comment with characteristic good humor:

> The other struck him familiarly across the shoulder. "And that ain't all. Say, fellow, there's a band concert tonight right here in this little old square. I'm goin' and I'm goin' to take a lady."

## Courtship of Vera Manning

Harley's most significant personal storyline is his "disastrous [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|courtship]] of [[vera-manning|Vera Manning]]." The [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|courtship]] is described secondhand, first in Vera's conversation with [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] and later in Harley's confession to Peter aboard the troopship. Vera tells [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] that she had "wanted, expected to marry," but the romance with Harley ended in disappointment:

> "So that," Vera ended wryly, "was the end of my young romance."

Harley's own account of the [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|courtship]], shared with Peter during their transatlantic passage, is characterized as "disastrous" — a word choice that suggests the relationship's failure was painful and possibly public. The narrator notes that "Harley had told Peter about his disastrous [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|courtship]] of [[vera-manning|Vera Manning]] and Peter had reluctantly, inevitably returned the confidence." This exchange of romantic confidences binds the two men together in what the narrator describes as "one of those strange intensive friendships between two people, in which each tries to bare his heart to the utmost before the other."

## Wartime Friendship with Peter Bye

Harley's most developed scenes occur during World War I, where he serves as [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]]'s "buddy" — first at Camp Upton, then aboard the troopship crossing the Atlantic, and finally at the training camps in France. Their friendship is forged in the peculiar intimacy of military service, and Harley functions as a pragmatic, good-humored counterweight to Peter's brooding nature.

When Peter confesses his dilemma about [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie Ellersley]] — whether to marry her despite not loving her — Harley offers blunt, clear-sighted advice:

> "I'll be doggone. I suppose Joanna did use to queen it over you, but what'd you go make a doormat of yourself for? She gave you what you were biddin' for. But now as far as this Miss Ellersley's concerned — I can't seem to remember her, Peter — she's got no claim on you that I can see. If she's any sense at all she knows that you came to her on sheer impulse. If you don't love her, don't you marry her. You'll regret it all your life if you do."

This advice — unsentimental, rooted in personal experience — reflects Harley's own history. Having lost [[vera-manning|Vera Manning]], he understands the cost of romantic entanglement entered into without genuine feeling. His counsel is one of the few moments in the novel where a character speaks to Peter with both clarity and compassion about [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|love]].

Harley also serves as a kind of morale officer for Peter, teasing him out of his darker moods. After Peter's dispiriting encounter with [[meriwether-bye|Meriwether Bye]] at La Courtine, the narrator notes: "'Come out of it, ole hoss,' Harley Alexander used to say to him. 'What'd your "grand white" friend do to you?'" Harley's teasing is affectionate, recognizing Peter's complexity while refusing to indulge his self-pity.

## Critical Function

Harley Alexander occupies a modest but coherent role in the novel's social architecture. As a dentist — a profession that represents the respectable middle tier of the colored professional class, below the prestige of medicine but above manual labor — he exemplifies the economic stability available to educated Black men in the early twentieth century.

His failed [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|courtship]] of [[vera-manning|Vera Manning]] provides one of the novel's several examples of romantic disappointment among the younger generation. Like [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] and Peter, like [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] and [[philip-marshall|Philip]], Harley and Vera could not sustain their early attachment — in their case, because Vera chose racial passing over community belonging.

His wartime friendship with Peter allows [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] to show Peter from an outside perspective and to introduce a voice of working-class common sense into Peter's tortured romantic deliberations. Harley is not a major character, but his presence enriches the novel's evocation of male friendship, military camaraderie, and the particular solidarities that form among colored soldiers serving in a segregated army.
