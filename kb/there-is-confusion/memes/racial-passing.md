# Racial Passing in There Is Confusion

Racial passing — the phenomenon of light-skinned African Americans crossing the color line to live as white — is a recurring concern in Jessie Redmon Fauset's fiction. In *There Is Confusion*, passing appears not as a central plot device but as a persistent *possibility* that shadows the novel's light-skinned characters, shaping their choices and self-understanding even when they do not act on it.

## Passing as Possibility

[[joanna-marshall]]'s light skin is remarked upon throughout the novel. Her appearance opens the possibility of passing — a possibility she never pursues but that nonetheless conditions her experience of racial identity. The option to pass, even when declined, functions as a kind of existential background radiation: always present, never entirely ignorable.

Fauset's treatment of this dynamic is psychologically acute. The availability of passing creates a peculiar form of freedom and burden. Joanna can move through certain spaces with less friction than darker-skinned characters, yet this very mobility raises questions about authenticity, solidarity, and self-definition that darker-skinned characters do not face in the same way.

## Passing and Class

The novel connects passing to class in complex ways. The [[joel-marshall|Marshall family]]'s economic comfort and cultural sophistication — what contemporaries called their membership in the "better class" of colored people — positions them closer to the white middle class in habits and aspirations. This class proximity intensifies both the possibility and the temptation of passing.

Yet Fauset resists any simple equation of passing with class aspiration. Her characters' decision to remain identified as African American, despite the advantages passing would confer, represents a form of racial commitment that the novel treats with respect. This is not the racial pride of political protest but the quieter loyalty of communal belonging.

## The Psychology of Refusal

The novel's most distinctive contribution to the literature of passing is its attention to the psychology of *refusing* to pass. Joanna's choice to pursue a career as an African American concert singer, when passing might have opened easier paths, is central to her character. This refusal is presented not as heroic defiance but as an expression of identity so deep it scarcely registers as a choice.

The novel's title — *There Is Confusion* — gestures toward the psychological complexity of this refusal. The "confusion" is not merely external (the chaos of social prejudice) but internal: the difficulty of maintaining a coherent sense of self when the boundaries of identity are socially constructed and permeable.

## Critical Context

Fauset's treatment of passing can be usefully contrasted with Nella Larsen's *Passing* (1929), published five years later. Where Larsen centers the dramatic consequences of crossing the color line, Fauset explores the quieter consequences of *not* crossing it — the daily reaffirmation of racial identity in the face of constant temptation to escape it.
