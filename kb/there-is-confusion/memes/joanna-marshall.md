# Joanna Marshall

Joanna Marshall is the protagonist of [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *There Is Confusion*, a landmark work of the Harlem Renaissance. She is the youngest child of [[joel-marshall|Joel]] and Mrs. Marshall, born into a prosperous African American family in New York City during the early twentieth century.

## Character and Origins

From early childhood, Joanna displays an intense and unusual ambition to be "great" — a trait she inherits directly from her father [[joel-marshall|Joel Marshall]]. She is described physically as having "a fluff of thick, black hair, and solemn, earnest eyes and an infinite capacity for spending long moments in thought" (001-010). Unlike her three older siblings — [[alec-marshall|Alec]] (Alexander), [[philip-marshall|Philip]], and [[sylvia-marshall|Sylvia]] — Joanna is intellectually precocious and deeply introspective.

Her father teaches her to read before she starts school, and she devotes herself to studying historical figures who overcame significant obstacles. Her reading focuses particularly on African American heroes: Frederick Douglass, Denmark Vesey, Nat Turner, Harriet Tubman, [[philip-marshall|Phil]]lis Wheatley, and Sojourner Truth (001-011). [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] presents Joanna as developing an unusually sophisticated historical consciousness for her age, comparing the courage of enslaved women who fought for freedom favorably against that of Florence Nightingale.

The name "Joanna" itself carries significance. The novel notes that Joanna identifies with Joan of Arc — "Joan, wonderful Joan with the name almost like mine" — but restrains herself from adopting the shorter form out of "an innate, almost too meticulous sense of honesty" (001-011). This detail establishes Joanna's characteristic blend of grand aspiration and moral scrupulousness.

## The Singing Voice

At age ten, Joanna reveals her extraordinary vocal talent during an Easter Sunday visit from [[madame-caldwell|Madame Caldwell]], a renowned colored singer. Without accompaniment, the child sings most of the Easter anthem from memory. Her voice is described as "already free of the shrillness of childhood and beginning to assume that liquid golden quality which so distinguished it later" (001-012).

[[madame-caldwell|Madame Caldwell]], who has endured years of hardship and discrimination in pursuing her own musical career, recognizes Joanna's exceptional gift immediately. She seizes [[joel-marshall|Joel Marshall]]'s arm with the urgent counsel: "Get her a teacher, Mr. Marshall. She has a voice in ten thousand" (001-012). This moment crystallizes Joanna's path — singing becomes her "road to glory," transforming an abstract, inchoate ambition into a concrete artistic pursuit.

## Relationship with Her Father

The most significant relationship in Joanna's early life is with her father, [[joel-marshall|Joel Marshall]]. The novel describes their bond as "a secret understanding" that began in Joanna's infancy. She would spend hours in his office, "where she watched him with her solemn eyes," and he, recognizing his own thwarted ambition mirrored in her, devoted himself to nurturing her potential (001-010).

[[joel-marshall|Joel]]'s own story — born a slave, self-made caterer, forever barred from the intellectual greatness he craved by lack of formal education — provides the emotional and psychological foundation for Joanna's drive. The novel makes explicit the generational transmission of ambition: "She was so completely like himself, and he could help her. 'Thank God,' he used to murmur over his books that daily showed an increase in his earnings" (001-011).

## Critical Significance

Joanna Marshall represents [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]]'s contribution to the Harlem Renaissance's exploration of the "New Negro" — educated, ambitious, and determined to claim a place in American cultural life. Unlike protagonists focused on racial protest or folk authenticity, Joanna pursues individual artistic excellence as a form of racial advancement. Her story foregrounds the intersection of race, gender, and artistic ambition, asking whether a black woman in early twentieth-century America can achieve recognition on the basis of talent alone.
