# Legacy of Slavery in There Is Confusion

The legacy of American slavery forms the historical substrate of [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s *[[ch005-there-is-confusion|There Is Confusion]]* (1924), shaping the psychological, economic, and social contours of the Marshall family across three generations.

## The First Generation: Joel's Parents

The novel establishes slavery's ongoing presence through the figure of [[joel-marshall|Joel Marshall]]'s father, "broken and sickened by slavery" who "lingered on and on" before dying (001-003). This father, never named in the text, represents slavery's direct victims — those whose lives were so thoroughly consumed by the institution that they could not participate in the post-Emancipation world.

Joel's mother, "Mammy," survives into the novel's present, providing a living link between the antebellum past and the early-twentieth-century setting. Her perspective on Joel's success — "my son is sure a great man" — reflects the first post-Emancipation generation's measure of achievement: survival, economic independence, community respect (001-003). The gap between her definition of greatness and Joel's reveals the widening horizon of possibility across a single generation.

## Joel Marshall: The Transitional Figure

Joel Marshall embodies the transitional generation — born into slavery but coming of age after Emancipation. His early life illustrates the economic devastation that persisted beyond legal freedom. After his father's death, "the little house should burn down," forcing fifteen-year-old Joel and his mother into domestic service for Harvey Carter (001-004). This trajectory — from slavery to menial service to entrepreneurship — traces the narrow path of post-Emancipation mobility.

The psychological legacy proves more durable than economic circumstances. Joel's "sick despair" — the sense that "he had so much against him" — persists even as his catering business prospers. The novel frames this as a structural condition: "His color, his poverty, meant nothing to his ardent heart; those were nature's limitations, placed deliberately about one, he could dimly see, to try one's strength on" (001-003). Fauset's language here is ambivalent — racial barriers are simultaneously oppressive and character-forging, a tension that runs throughout the novel.

## Education as the Unhealed Wound

The most consequential legacy of slavery in the novel is educational deprivation. Joel's "lack of education" is described as "a bar forever between him and the kind of greatness for which his heart had yearned" (001-004). This deficit is explicitly linked to slavery's systematic denial of literacy to enslaved people.

Joel's response — ensuring [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s education from an early age — represents a deliberate intervention in this legacy. He "had her taught to read after that" (referring to her first articulation of ambition), and provided her with books about African American historical figures (001-011). Education becomes the mechanism through which slavery's legacy is confronted and, potentially, overcome.

## Joanna's Generation

By Joanna's generation, slavery exists as a transmitted memory rather than direct experience. She asks her father, "Didn't colored people ever do anything, Daddy?" — a question that reveals how slavery's erasure of African American achievement requires active historical recovery (001-011).

The novel presents Joanna's artistic ambition as slavery's refutation: where the institution denied African Americans the capacity for cultural production, Joanna claims the highest form of artistic expression. Yet the novel is attentive to the costs of this refutation — the question of whether individual achievement can compensate for collective historical injury remains unresolved.
