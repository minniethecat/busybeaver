# Sylvia Marshall

Sylvia Marshall is [[joanna-marshall|Joanna Marshall]]'s elder sister in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *There Is Confusion*, a significant work of the Harlem Renaissance. She is the second child of [[joel-marshall|Joel]] and Mrs. Marshall, distinguished from her more serious younger sister by her quickness, versatility, physical attractiveness, and vivacious social grace.

## Character and Temperament

Sylvia stands as a deliberate foil to [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] throughout the novel. While [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] is slow, methodical, and obsessively ambitious, Sylvia is described as "thoughtless, quick to jump at conclusions, natively witty and strongly disinclined toward seriousness." Her childhood complaint that "[[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] makes me sick — always thinking of her lessons and how important she's going to be when she's grown-up" captures the fundamental difference between the sisters' temperaments. [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] characterizes the contrast through the metaphor of light: "Sylvia was like a firefly in comparison with [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s steady beaconlike flood of light. Sylvia dashed about, worked as quickly as she thought and produced immediate and usually rather striking results."

Despite their childhood differences, the novel emphasizes that Sylvia and [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] "were destined to be such perfect friends in their maturity." Their relationship deepens considerably after the crisis of Peter's rejection of [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]], when Sylvia becomes her sister's primary emotional support and confidante.

## Physical Appearance and Style

Sylvia possesses a natural glamour that [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] lacks in her youth. She has "a fluff of thick, black hair" worn in waves over her ears, caught up by decorative bows, and is described as having "that peculiar luminosity of appearance" that marks her as a beauty. When [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie Ellersley]] first encounters her at school, Sylvia appears in "a soft dark red cashmere dress made with a wide pleated skirt" with "little patent leather, buttoned shoes with cloth tops, and a big red bow perched butterfly fashion on her dark head." The aura of "well-being and easy self-assurance" that surrounds her becomes a hallmark of all the Marshall children.

Her fashion sensibility extends into adulthood, where she designs [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s flame-colored dressing gown and hatches half-serious plans to open a dressmaking establishment in Paris, with [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] as her mannequin. She has a sharp, witty eye for the sartorial failings of others, including the wealthy Miss Sharples, whose gray suit and brown scarf combination prompts Sylvia to shudder.

## Relationship with Brian Spencer

Sylvia's romantic life follows a straightforward, conventional arc in contrast to [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s and [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s turbulent experiences. [[brian-spencer|Brian Spencer]], a Harvard-trained physician, is devoted to Sylvia from their youth onward; the narrator notes he was "pretty sure to be where Sylvia was" even during their adolescent years. Their [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|courtship]] is presented as natural and effortless — a "simple, easy, jog-trot time" that [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]], reflecting on her own heartbreak, later envies.

[[brian-spencer|Brian]] and Sylvia eventually marry and have at least two children, including a son named Roger. After [[philip-marshall|Philip Marshall]]'s death in the war, Sylvia's children become a source of renewed purpose for the elder Marshalls, with Mrs. Marshall's "long allegiance to Sylvia" intensifying as she lavishes attention on her grandchildren.

## Sylvia as Sister and Confidante

Sylvia's most significant narrative function emerges in the aftermath of [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]]'s [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]] to [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie Ellersley]]. She becomes [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s sole confidante during the period of acute grief and sleeplessness that follows. She intercepts Peter's letter, meets [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] at the train station, gets her "home and finally to bed," and quietly manages the family's knowledge of the crisis to protect her sister. Her protectiveness extends to practical intervention — pretending to [[harry-portor|Harry Portor]] that she herself needs a sedative so [[brian-spencer|Brian Spencer]] can prescribe one for [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] without exposing her condition.

Sylvia also mediates between [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] and the broader social world. Through her, [[brian-spencer|Brian]] learns of [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s unspoken grief, and his analysis of Peter's "complex of color" — delivered in conversation with Sylvia — provides the novel's most explicit articulation of how racial consciousness distorts personal relationships.

## Motherhood and Later Life

After [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] and Peter's eventual reconciliation and [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]], Sylvia's family remains intimately connected with the Marshalls. Her son Roger serves as Peter's "office-boy" in an "impressionistic capacity" and develops a fondness for his new "soldier-uncle." The novel ends with the two sisters and their families interwoven across the house on One Hundred and Thirty-eighth Street, their childhood differences resolved into a deep, comfortable intimacy.

## Critical Significance

Sylvia Marshall embodies the foil-character function in African American domestic fiction of the Harlem Renaissance — the socially graceful, conventionally feminine woman whose path illuminates, by contrast, the protagonist's more unconventional ambition. She represents a viable model of black womanhood that prioritizes [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]], motherhood, and social harmony over individual artistic achievement. Her narrative of fulfilled domesticity, untroubled by the racial and professional obstacles that torment [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] and [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]], functions as a counterweight to the novel's central argument about the costs of exceptional ambition.
