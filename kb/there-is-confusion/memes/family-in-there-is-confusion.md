# Family in There Is Confusion

Family is the deep structure of *There Is Confusion*. [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]] does not write about isolated individuals pursuing happiness — she writes about people embedded in lineages, each generation carrying forward (and struggling against) the legacy of the one before. The Bye family saga, spanning from late-eighteenth-century slavery through early-twentieth-century professional achievement, is the novel's central narrative engine.

## The Bye Family Tree

Fauset constructs a remarkably detailed multi-generational genealogy:

| Generation | Key Figures | Defining Event |
|-----------|------------|----------------|
| **1st (enslaved)** | Aaron & Dinah Bye, Ceazer, Judy | Emancipation by Quaker owners |
| **2nd (freeborn)** | [[isaiah-bye|Isaiah Bye]] & Belle Potter | Rise to property ownership, Pride of lineage |
| **3rd (post-Civil War)** | [[meriwether-bye|Meriwether Bye]] & Alice Graves | Education, failed ambition, drink |
| **4th (modern)** | [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]] & [[joanna-marshall|Joanna Marshall]] | Professional achievement, breaking the cycle |
| **5th (future)** | Baby Meriwether Bye | Redemption of the name |

Each generation's choices constrain and enable the next. Isaiah's pride becomes Meriwether's burden; Meriwether's failure becomes Peter's challenge.

## The Family Bible

The Bye Family Bible is the novel's central material symbol of lineage. Given to Isaiah Bye on the occasion of his marriage, bound in leather and containing an Apocrypha, it bears on its title page "a fine old script" recording the family's origins. When Meriwether Bye dies, this Bible is virtually the only inheritance Peter receives — and as [[miss-susan-graves|Miss Susan Graves]] reflects, it is far more valuable than money:

> "She would have been glad of course if Meriwether Bye had left Peter some money. But of the two she would very much rather have had the Bible with its absolute assurance of the former standing and respectability of the black Byes."

The Bible is proof — of freedom, of marriage, of literacy, of belonging to a named lineage. In a society that denied Black families their history, the Bye Bible is a counter-archive.

## Shared History Across the Color Line

The Bye family story is inseparable from the white Bye family. The two families share a name, a city, and centuries of entangled history:

> "As far back as the last decades of the eighteenth century there had been white Byes and black Byes in Philadelphia."

This shared history is not romanticized. The black Byes were "chattels" of the white Byes. The same name that confers status within Black Philadelphia is also a permanent reminder of enslavement. Peter's eventual inheritance from the white [[meriwether-bye|Meriwether Bye]] — both material and spiritual — represents a kind of reckoning with this history, though not its erasure.

## Family as Social Capital

In Fauset's Philadelphia, family functions as a form of social currency. The Graves family of Gravestown, New Jersey, is "a clan well known to colored people not only in that vicinity, but also throughout Pennsylvania." The Sharples family of Girard Avenue represents merchant-class respectability. To marry well is to ally oneself with a family; to lack family is to lack standing.

[[maggie-ellersley|Maggie Ellersley]]'s poverty is compounded by her lack of family connections. Her tragedy is not just economic but genealogical — she has no lineage to draw upon, no network to fall back on.

## The Dinner Scene

The novel's most vivid depiction of family as community occurs at a social gathering where characters from Philadelphia, Washington, and Baltimore mingle:

> "They were like one big family although they came from Washington, Philadelphia, and Baltimore."

This is "family" in its extended sense — the Black middle class as a kind of kinship network, bound by shared institutions, shared values, and shared vulnerability to racism.

## Themes

| Theme | Expression |
|-------|-----------|
| **Intergenerational Legacy** | Each generation inherits both the achievements and the failures of its predecessors |
| **Family as Archive** | The Bible, the name, the stories — family preserves what official history erases |
| **Racial Entanglement** | The white and black Bye families are bound together across centuries |
| **Family as Social Capital** | In a racist society, family connections are a form of wealth and protection |
