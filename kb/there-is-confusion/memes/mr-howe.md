# Mr. Howe (John Howe)

John Howe, later the Reverend John Howe, is a minor but consequential character in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *There Is Confusion*. As a Lincoln University divinity student who boards with the Ellersley family during [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s adolescence, Howe becomes the instrument of the family's escape from poverty — and a lasting symbol of the decency and generosity that counterbalance the novel's bleaker portraits of selfishness and social exclusion.

## The Boarder and the Illness

Howe first appears as "young John Howe, who was occupying the front room" at the Ellersley boarding house. When he contracts typhoid fever, his circumstances are precarious: he begs [[mrs-ellersley|Mrs. Ellersley]] not to send him to the hospital, and his family in Oklahoma is unreachable. [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]], then fourteen, stays out of school to nurse him:

> The doctor gave her a list of directions which she followed with meticulous care. In that shabby house down in that terrible district John Howe met with an attention, a devotion from the humble woman and her delicate daughter, such as no money could have bought him in the seats of the mighty. (Ch. VIII)

This episode establishes two patterns that recur throughout the novel: [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s capacity for devoted service, and the formation of cross-class alliances through shared hardship. Howe, a Lincoln divinity student "intermittently working his way through college," is himself marginal — neither wealthy nor secure — yet to the Ellersleys he represents a connection to a broader world of education and respectability.

## Enabling the Escape

Howe's most consequential act in the novel is his role in moving the Ellersleys from their "awful place" — the shabby, dangerous neighborhood that threatens [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s development — to a more decent apartment. [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]], with the fierce clarity of adolescent longing, makes her appeal directly:

> "There's only one thing I want, Mr. Howe, and you couldn't give me that."
> "I could try. What is it?"
> "Oh Mr. Howe, if you could just get us out of this awful place, this house, this street! If I could just get to know some decent folks — " (Ch. VIII)

[[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s scheme — that Howe should make his headquarters with them for the year, paying rent that would cover a better apartment — requires her mother's consent. [[mrs-ellersley|Mrs. Ellersley]], "sure of her livelihood, her mere existence here, but doubtful about a great venture," hesitates. Howe's backing proves decisive: "She's right, [[mrs-ellersley|Mrs. Ellersley]], and this is no place for a young girl to grow up." He locates a house, arranges furniture, and mobilizes "plenty of fellows who own trucks" to execute the move (Ch. VIII).

The narrator's judgment is unequivocal: "John Howe, staying for that year in New York, realizing more and more how truly he was indebted to [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] and her mother, took a proprietary interest. He laid the cheap rugs, he set up the cots" (Ch. IX). The word "proprietary" is carefully chosen — Howe's investment in the Ellersleys' welfare carries the weight of moral obligation, not charity.

## The Return as Reverend Howe

Howe reappears late in the novel, transformed. He is now "the Reverend John Howe," a minister, and his return prompts [[mrs-ellersley|Mrs. Ellersley]]'s delighted announcement: "Guess who's here, [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]? But, pshaw, you'd never guess. John Howe, do you remember?" (Ch. XXVII)

His visit, initially intended for "only five minutes," extends to five hours. [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]], now trapped in an unhappy [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]] to [[henderson-neal]], pours out her heart: "her first liking for [[philip-marshall|Philip]], the dreadful affair of [[henderson-neal|Neal]], her present isolation" (Ch. XXVII). The structure of the scene is striking — the man she once nursed through illness and whose help she once sought for physical escape is now the confidant for her spiritual and emotional imprisonment. Howe, "the lean type" of minister, listens without judgment, his presence a reminder that the aspirations [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] articulated as a fourteen-year-old were never foolish, only deferred.

Howe also appears in the novel's later chapters, connected to [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s social world and the network of [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia connections; references to him surface in contexts involving [[joanna-marshall|Joanna Marshall]] and [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]]'s circle (Ch. XXVIII, XXXI).

## Critical Significance

John Howe functions in the novel's moral economy as a figure of genuine reciprocity. Unlike the casual benevolence of [[alice-talbert]] — extended without cost and withdrawn without thought — Howe's investment in the Ellersleys is earned through their prior care of him. His trajectory from sick boarder to Reverend is itself a testament to the values of perseverance and mutual aid that the novel endorses.

That Howe is a divinity student and later a minister is thematically significant: his faith is expressed not in preaching but in practical action — finding a house, arranging furniture, listening to a troubled friend for five hours. [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]], who treats organized religion sparingly in the novel, locates authentic Christian charity in Howe's quiet, consistent decency rather than in any institutional setting.
