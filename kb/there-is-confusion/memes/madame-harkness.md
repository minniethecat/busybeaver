# Madame Harkness

Madame Harkness is a background figure in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *There Is Confusion*, representing the economic possibilities available to African American women through entrepreneurship in the beauty industry. Though she never appears directly as a speaking character, her hair culture business serves as the economic foundation for [[maggie-ellersley]]'s independence, providing [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] with a career, a salary, and a measure of freedom after her disastrous [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]] to [[henderson-neal|Henderson Neal]].

## The Harkness Method

Madame Harkness is introduced through [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie Ellersley]]'s enthusiastic description of her work:

> "You know there's a Madame Harkness who's invented a method of softening hair, and of taking the harshness out of your folks' locks. You know I think there's a big future in it. It ought to mean a lot to us. Everybody wants to be beautiful, and every woman looks better if her hair is soft and manageable."

The "Harkness Method of Hair Culture" represents one of the novel's few depictions of Black entrepreneurship and technical innovation. [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] embeds this detail in [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s early conversations with [[philip-marshall|Philip Marshall]], establishing the hair culture business as a legitimate and promising career path — one that requires study, dedication, and offers genuine economic returns.

## Business Empire

Madame Harkness's business card, which [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] keeps and later encounters in Atlantic City, establishes the scale of the operation:

> **Madame Harkness**
> Hair Culturist
> 270 West 137th Street
> New York City

The card lists branch offices in [[washington-dc|Washington, D.C.]] (1307 U Street, N. W.), Baltimore (1816 Druid Hill Avenue), and [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia (2021 South Street) — addresses in the heart of those cities' African American commercial districts, all on streets that were, in the early twentieth century, synonymous with Black business and culture.

This geographic reach — New York, [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia, Baltimore, [[washington-dc|Washington]] — mirrors the novel's own spatial imagination, tracing the northeastern corridor of Black urban life. The business's expansion suggests that Madame Harkness has built something substantial: a multi-city enterprise serving a real market need.

## Maggie's Employment and Independence

[[maggie-ellersley|Maggie Ellersley]]'s connection to Madame Harkness evolves from student to employee to trusted supervisor. After leaving New York following her [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]] to [[henderson-neal|Henderson Neal]], [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] builds a new life around her work:

> "That Madame Harkness — you know whom I mean? She's made me supervisor of three of her branch stores, here in Philadelphia, Baltimore, and Washington, D.C. I have my little home here, my salary's good. I make more than enough to live on. My mother doesn't have to do anything if she doesn't want to. And above all, I'm practically free."

This passage is one of the novel's most direct statements of female economic independence. [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s salary from Madame Harkness's business enables her to buy a house in New York, support her mother, and achieve a degree of autonomy that [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]] never provided. The word "free" — placed in italics for emphasis — carries particular weight: for a working-class colored woman in the 1910s, economic self-sufficiency is the nearest available form of liberation.

The business continues to thrive; later in the novel, the narrator notes that "Madame Harkness' business had increased considerably in [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia and Baltimore," and [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] is "making a remarkably good living from her connection with Madame Harkness."

## Symbolic Function

Madame Harkness and her business serve several functions within the novel's thematic architecture. First, the hair culture industry itself — with its promise of making Black women's hair "soft and manageable" — participates in the novel's ambivalent treatment of color and beauty standards. [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s own naturally straight hair makes her "fortunate" in not needing such preparations, yet she embraces the business as a means of helping other women achieve beauty.

Second, Madame Harkness represents a specifically female model of entrepreneurship that contrasts with the male-dominated professions pursued by the novel's central men (medicine for [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]] and [[harry-portor|Harry Portor]], real estate for [[alec-marshall|Alec Marshall]] and [[brian-spencer|Brian Spencer]]). The beauty industry offered Black women a rare path to business ownership in the early twentieth century, and [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]]'s inclusion of this detail reflects the historical reality of figures like Madam C. J. Walker, whose hair care empire made her one of America's first self-made female millionaires.

Third, the business functions as a plot mechanism that keeps [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] economically viable and geographically mobile, allowing her to move between [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia, New York, and the narrative's various social worlds without depending on a husband's income.
