# Mr. Hale

Mr. Hale is a supporting character in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *There Is Confusion*, notable as the theater manager who gives [[joanna-marshall]] her decisive professional opportunity. In a novel centrally concerned with the barriers that race and gender erect around Black ambition, Mr. Hale represents the rare white gatekeeper willing to risk commercial failure — and public controversy — on the talent of a colored performer.

## Hiring Joanna Marshall

Mr. Hale first appears when [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] auditions for the production "America," a patriotic dance spectacle. The audition is arranged by Miss Sharples, a white woman who has seen [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] dance and believes in her talent. Mr. Hale's response to [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] is direct and pragmatic, devoid of the patronizing qualifiers that often accompany white assessments of Black artistry:

> The tall dark man, Mr. Hale, came over to her. "You're all right, Miss Marshall, if you're willing, we'll try you. America's got some foolish prejudices, but we'll try her with a sensation, and you'll be all of that. I'll leave you with Miss Sharples and Miss Rosen, our secretary, to make final arrangements, while Francis and I go out to see what we can do about taking on these kids. I suppose you'll need them." [032-109]

His language — "America's got some foolish prejudices" — acknowledges the racial barrier even as he decides to circumvent it. The strategy he articulates is to overwhelm prejudice with spectacle: "we'll try her with a sensation." In this, Mr. Hale proves to be an astute judge of both the theatrical marketplace and the psychology of his audience.

## The Show's Success

Mr. Hale takes [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] on in May, and the production runs through the summer with [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] as its great attraction — though pointedly not as its "acknowledged star." That title belongs to [[miss-ashby]], a white dancer who performs the roles of an Indian and the symbolic figure of America. The racial hierarchy within the production is explicit: [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] is the talent that fills the seats, but [[miss-ashby|Miss Ashby]] — by contract and by custom — occupies the position of prestige:

> Mr. Hale had taken her on in May. The piece ran all summer with Joanna as the great attraction, although not the acknowledged star. Miss Ashby, the girl who danced as an Indian and as an American, was that. From the first she had resented the colored girl's success and had held jealously to all her rights and privileges. [033-009]

## The Unmasking

The climax of Mr. Hale's role in [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s story comes on the night when an "inveterate theatergoer" in the first row insists on an encore, and [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] — performing in a mask that conceals her race — is forced to reveal her identity. The moment is a theatrical crisis and a racial one:

> Mr. Hale, Miss Sharples, Francis, Miss Rosen and Miss Phelps held a hurried consultation behind the scenes. "There's nothing to be done," Hale said, "quick, off with your mask, Miss Marshall." And breathless, somewhat with the air of a man bracing himself, he led Joanna again on the stage. [033-023]

The detail of Mr. Hale leading [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] onto the stage "with the air of a man bracing himself" is telling. He knows that what he is about to do — presenting a colored woman unmasked as the star of a Broadway production — could destroy the show. Yet his judgment proves sound: the audience, won over by [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s talent during the summer run, receives her unmasked appearance with applause rather than protest.

> The very next week Mr. Hale moved the production to Broadway. [033-029]

This terse sentence marks one of the novel's most significant plot turns. [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s Broadway debut, facilitated by Mr. Hale's willingness to take the risk, transforms her from a talented unknown into a celebrated performer whose fame eventually extends to Europe.

## Critical Significance

Mr. Hale functions in the novel as what might be called an "enabling figure" — a character whose principal narrative role is to open doors that the protagonist cannot open alone. He is not developed as a personality (we never learn his first name, his background, or his motivations beyond the professional), but his actions carry disproportionate weight in [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s trajectory.

[[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]]'s treatment of Mr. Hale is notable for its restraint. She does not make him a white savior — his decision to hire [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] is clearly motivated by commercial calculation ("we'll try her with a sensation") rather than racial idealism. Nor does she sentimentalize the employment relationship: [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] remains the unacknowledged star, paid less and credited less than her white counterpart, even as her talent drives the show's success. Mr. Hale, for all his willingness to take a risk, operates firmly within the racial economy of the theater world, and the novel does not pretend otherwise.

The strategic alliance between Mr. Hale (white capital) and [[joanna-marshall|Joanna Marshall]] (Black talent) mirrors, in the theatrical realm, the broader pattern of racial advancement that [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] traces throughout the novel: progress occurs not through revolution but through the slow, pragmatic negotiation of spaces within existing power structures. Mr. Hale is not a friend to racial justice, but he is a businessman who recognizes talent when he sees it — and in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]]'s world, that is often enough to move the story forward.
