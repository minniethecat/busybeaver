# Fifty-Ninth Street, New York

Fifty-Ninth Street in Manhattan is the location of the Marshall family home in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *[[ch005-there-is-confusion|There Is Confusion]]*. The address serves as the spatial marker of the family's successful migration from the slave South to the urban North, and as the domestic setting within which [[joanna-marshall|Joanna Marshall]]'s artistic ambition is nurtured.

## The Marshall Home

The Marshall residence on Fifty-Ninth Street is depicted as a comfortable, well-appointed domestic space that reflects [[joel-marshall|Joel Marshall]]'s economic achievement. The novel notes that "Joel had his office in his residence" (001-010), combining professional and domestic functions in a pattern typical of the entrepreneurial class, where the boundary between work and home was permeable.

The home serves as the setting for key scenes in Joanna's early life. It is where she "used to lie in her bed at night, straight and still with her eyes fixed on the stretch of sky visible even from a house in Fifty-ninth Street and dream dreams" (001-011). The image of the child gazing at the sky from her comfortable room captures the novel's fusion of material security and transcendent aspiration.

## Social Geography

In the early twentieth century, Fifty-Ninth Street formed the southern boundary of Central Park and marked a transitional zone in Manhattan's social geography. The area included both wealthy residential blocks and more modest middle-class housing. The Marshall family's presence at this address signals their economic achievement without claiming elite status — they are comfortably middle class, not wealthy.

This positioning is significant for Fauset's literary project. By placing her African American protagonists in a conventionally respectable Manhattan neighborhood, she challenges the literary expectation that black characters must be either impoverished or exotic. The Marshalls' Fifty-Ninth Street home asserts their participation in the ordinary American narrative of upward mobility.

## Domesticity and Ambition

The Fifty-Ninth Street home is the site of Joanna's intellectual formation. It is here that Joel Marshall taught her to read, provided her with books about African American historical figures, and hosted the Easter dinner at which [[madame-caldwell|Madame Caldwell]] recognized her vocal talent.

The novel presents this domestic space as the infrastructure of ambition: Joanna's drive for greatness is not a rejection of middle-class domesticity but its product. The stable, comfortable home — paid for by Joel's catering business — provides the security from which artistic risk-taking becomes possible.

## Symbolic Contrast

Fifty-Ninth Street stands in implicit contrast with the [[richmond-virginia|Richmond, Virginia]] of Joel's childhood — "the tiny half-story room in that tiny house" from which he dreamed of escape (001-003). The distance between that cramped Virginia room and the Manhattan home with its view of the sky measures the generational arc of the Marshall family: from slavery to service to entrepreneurship to artistic ambition. Fifty-Ninth Street is both arrival point and launching pad.
