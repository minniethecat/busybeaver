# Maggie Ellersley

Maggie Ellersley is a significant secondary character in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *There Is Confusion*. She represents the aspirations and limitations of a young woman of humble origins who seeks to rise above her circumstances through work, [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]], and self-improvement. Her story arc, intertwined with that of the [[family-in-there-is-confusion|Marshall family]], illuminates the class tensions within early twentieth-century African American communities.

## Background and Origins

Maggie comes from a background of deep poverty. Her father died when she was about eight, leaving herself and her mother with nothing (011-001). Her mother supported them by taking in roomers, and the precariousness of this existence — where failing to correctly assess a prospective boarder could jeopardize a week's rent — instilled in Maggie a lifelong skill for reading people and a driving need for security (011-011).

Maggie's mother described the first fourteen years of Maggie's life as a period of "arid" hardship. Despite these circumstances, Maggie graduated from high school alongside [[sylvia-marshall|Sylvia Marshall]], and afterward found employment as a "sort of overseer-bookkeeper" in the catering business of [[joel-marshall|Joel Marshall]], [[sylvia-marshall|Sylvia]]'s father (010-008, 011-012). This position placed her in proximity to the prosperous [[family-in-there-is-confusion|Marshall family]], exposing her to a social world far removed from her own upbringing.

## Character and Social Ambition

Maggie's defining characteristic is her "almost morbid obsession" with securing "decent respectability" through [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]] (011-002). Unlike [[joanna-marshall|Joanna Marshall]], whose ambition is channeled toward artistic greatness, Maggie's aspirations are fundamentally social — she wants to escape the insecurity and stigma of her impoverished origins.

The novel positions Maggie as [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s foil. While [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] pursues individual achievement, Maggie pursues social positioning through [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]]. [[brian-spencer|Brian Spencer]], observing [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s dismissive attitude toward Maggie, calls [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] "an utter snob" and insists Maggie is "as good as any of us" — a judgment the narrative partially endorses while also criticizing [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s class-based condescension (010-037).

Maggie also represents the new economic opportunities available to African American women in the early twentieth century. She is training as a hairdresser under [[madame-harkness|Madame Harkness]], who has developed "a method of softening hair, and of taking the harshness out of your folks' locks" (011-013). Maggie sees a business future in beauty culture: "I think there's a big future in it. It ought to mean a lot to us. Everybody wants to be beautiful" (011-014). Her entrepreneurial spirit reflects the broader economic self-determination themes of the Harlem Renaissance.

## Relationship with Philip Marshall

Maggie's romantic interest in [[philip-marshall|Philip Marshall]] — [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s brilliant older brother — forms the central tension of her storyline. She [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|love]]s him not only for himself but as the embodiment of the respectability and success she craves (011-002). In church, watching [[philip-marshall|Philip]], she notes his "lean, brown, slightly haggard face" with its "already somewhat remote glance of the thinker," though his "firm chin pronounced him no less the man of action" (010-005).

The class barrier between them is made explicit when [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] discovers Maggie's feelings. [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] reacts with fury, exclaiming: "Why, [[philip-marshall|Philip]] — he's going to be somebody great, wonderful, a Garibaldi, a Toussaint! And Maggie, Maggie's just nobody" (010-035). She dismisses Maggie's profession: "What is she taking up? Hair work, straightening hair, salves and shampoos and curling-irons" (010-035). This outburst reveals [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s class prejudice while also underscoring the authentic social distance the novel depicts between the established [[family-in-there-is-confusion|Marshall family]] and the self-made Maggie.

Despite [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s opposition, Maggie and [[philip-marshall|Philip]]'s relationship develops throughout the novel, with Maggie's devotion remaining constant. Her feelings are described as genuine [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|love]], not mere calculation: "She knew now that the one man through whom she wanted to secure that respectability was [[philip-marshall|Philip Marshall]]. She [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|love]]d him" (011-002).

## Friendship with Sylvia Marshall

[[sylvia-marshall|Sylvia Marshall]], [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s more conventional sister, is Maggie's closest friend. Their friendship, begun as schoolmates, persists despite the social gap between the Marshall and Ellersley households. [[sylvia-marshall|Sylvia]]'s acceptance of Maggie contrasts with [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s snobbery and helps to humanize Maggie for the reader.

## Critical Significance

Maggie Ellersley embodies the collision between individual aspiration and social constraint that defines much of African American literature of the period. Her desire to "marry well" is not presented as shallow — it is the only path to security and dignity available to a woman of her background and era.

[[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] uses Maggie to explore intra-racial class dynamics, a recurring concern in her fiction. [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s disdain for Maggie's hairdressing ambitions and her panic at the thought of her brother marrying beneath the family's station reflect the real class anxieties of the African American elite. Yet the novel ultimately treats Maggie with sympathy, presenting her as a legitimate striver whose methods differ from, but are no less valid than, [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s pursuit of artistic glory.
