# Miss Susan Graves

Miss Susan Graves is a significant supporting character in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *There Is Confusion*. As the elder Graves sister, she serves as [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]]'s guardian after the death of his father [[meriwether-bye|Meriwether Bye]], providing the boy with stability, family tradition, and — despite her limited means — a connection to the values of "old [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphian" respectability.

## Family Background and Character

[[susan-graves|Susan Graves]] belonged to "the Graves family of Gravestown, New Jersey, a clan well known to colored people not only in that vicinity, but also throughout Pennsylvania" (005-011). Her identity was fundamentally shaped by this lineage. "Miss Susan Graves had not married for the simple and sufficient reason that in her day there were not enough male Graves to go around. She would as soon have thought of marrying outside her family as a Spanish grandee would have thought of marrying an English cockney" (005-009).

This fierce family pride defined her worldview. "In those days the position of old maid had its decided disadvantages — few people if any gave her the benefit of the doubt that she might have remained single from choice. Yet Miss Susan Graves, in spite of three other offers, soared on family pride above all this and made her career that of housekeeper for the family of a wealthy merchant on Girard Avenue, in [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia" (005-009).

Though not an "old [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphian" herself, Susan "knew something of colored [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia's pride in the possession of family and tradition. She would have been glad of course if [[meriwether-bye|Meriwether Bye]] had left Peter some money. But of the two she would very much rather have had the Bible with its absolute assurance of the former standing and respectability of the black Byes" (005-011).

## Career and New York Years

When the oldest son of Merchant Sharples of Girard Avenue married and relocated to New York, "[[susan-graves|Susan Graves]] went along as housekeeper. And thither [[alice-graves|Alice Graves]] followed shortly to do sewing for that intricate but orderly household" (005-010). In New York, Susan's younger sister [[alice-graves|Alice]] met and married [[meriwether-bye|Meriwether Bye]].

Susan "resigned herself easily to the matter — for Bye belonged to old stock and must, she thought, make good eventually. But she developed a strong dislike for him before his death, and took Peter not only for his mother's sake but also to dispel if possible his father's doubtless harmful influence" (005-010).

## Guardian of Peter Bye

After [[meriwether-bye|Meriwether Bye]]'s death, [[susan-graves|Susan Graves]] became Peter's guardian. "It may seem a cold-blooded thing to say, but the dying of [[meriwether-bye|Meriwether Bye]] was about the best thing he could have done for his son, Peter. Certainly that was what Miss Susan Graves thought as she viewed rather grimly the small and motley collection of belongings which Peter transferred to her home" (005-008).

Peter's possessions included "a well-worn extra suit of clothes, another pair of shoes, some underwear in sad need of patching, some books chiefly on physiology and anatomy, the Bye Family Bible, a little old black testament, and a box of letters. There was also a big railroad map which Peter lugged along under his arm and from which he stubbornly refused to be parted" (005-008).

Susan took her guardianship seriously, both practically and socially. When Peter began spending time at the Marshall household, "Miss Susan Graves did not like this at first, not that she resented Peter's absence from her so much, but he was a Bye and she did not choose to have him associate too much with people whom she did not know. It was no part of her plan for Peter to retrograde into the wreck which Meriwether had become" (007-005). She "made it her business to meet Mrs. Marshall at a church affair" (007-005).

## Relationship with the Marshall Family

When [[susan-graves|Susan Graves]] visited the Marshall home, she was "impressed enough to let Peter continue, though he would have continued without her permission." Like Belle Bye nearly a century earlier, Susan "recognized atmosphere when she saw it. She was poor; Peter was penniless. These were the sort of people her nephew ought to know. She liked [[joel-marshall|Joel]]'s success, his pride, his air of being somebody. She estimated rightly the correctness of the old-fashioned walnut furniture, the heavy curtains, the kidney table in the parlor, the massive silver service and good linen" (007-008).

Her first meeting with [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s mother was characteristically direct:

> "I think," said Miss Graves, eyeing Joanna's mother with her clear, square gaze, "that my boy has spoken to me of you." (007-006)

Mrs. Marshall was initially puzzled, thinking "this was a *Miss* Graves" (007-007).

## Later Wisdom

As [[susan-graves|Susan Graves]] aged, her rigid views softened. "As [[susan-graves|Miss Graves]] grew older, she began to see that her younger sister had had some pretty good views after all, that it did not do to stick to settled views — 'this for me, and that quite other thing for you.' The great things of life were for the taking, it was true, but the result of deliberate planning. One did not simply stumble into success. She had lived too long with 'the best white people' not to find that out" (007-010).

This evolution in her thinking represents one of the novel's quiet arguments: that proximity to excellence and ambition — whether in white or Black households — teaches universal lessons about success and character.
