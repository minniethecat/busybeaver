# Dr. Meriwether Bye

Dr. Meriwether Bye is a background figure in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *There Is Confusion* — the white Quaker physician of the [[family-in-there-is-confusion|Bye family]] whose name echoes across three generations of the black Bye line. He is the youngest son of Aaron and Dinah Bye, the Quaker couple who held the black Byes as slaves before manumitting them in 1780. Though he never appears directly in the novel's present-tense narrative, his existence and his casual cruelty in youth shape the central psychological drama of the [[family-in-there-is-confusion|Bye family]].

## Family and Origins

[[meriwether-bye|Meriwether Bye]] was born into the white branch of the [[family-in-there-is-confusion|Bye family]] of [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia, the youngest of Aaron and Dinah Bye's ten children. His father Aaron was a prosperous peach merchant — the "rich peach-merchant" young Meriwether proudly invokes — who manumitted his slaves in 1780 under Pennsylvania law, a gesture that "allowed the Quakers to salve their consciences without offending their thrifty instincts" (Ch. I). The [[family-in-there-is-confusion|Bye family]]'s wealth and position placed young Meriwether in a world of assumed superiority that he would carry unreflectingly into adulthood.

## Childhood with Isaiah Bye

As a boy, white [[meriwether-bye|Meriwether Bye]] and black [[isaiah-bye]] were companions — a relationship that was, in its early form, apparently one of genuine boyhood friendship. Meriwether "used to come to the Bye house at Fourth and Coates Streets ... as often as [[isaiah-bye|Isaiah]] used to appear at the Bye house at Fourth and Spruce" (Ch. I). It was Meriwether who explained to [[isaiah-bye|Isaiah]] the meaning of the biblical inscription [[aaron-bye|Aaron Bye]] had written in [[joshua-bye|Joshua]]'s testament: "By their fruits ye shall know them."

But the friendship carried within it the poison of the slaveholding order. When [[isaiah-bye|Isaiah]] asked what the inscription meant for people, the young Meriwether answered with what [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] calls "the unconscious cruelty of youth":

> "When it comes to people, it means pretty much the same thing. Now when I grow up, I'm going to be a great doctor ... and I'll be good fruit. That's the way it always is: good trees, good fruit; rich, important people, rich important sons." (Ch. I)

When [[isaiah-bye|Isaiah]] asked what *he* could be, Meriwether replied: "Your father and your father's father were slaves ... So I s'pose that's what you'll be, a good servant. Tell you what, [[isaiah-bye|Isaiah]], you can be my coachman" (Ch. I). He added, with a condescension he mistook for generosity: "I'll point you out to some famous doctor from France and say, 'His father was a good servant to my father, and he's been a good servant to my father's son'" (Ch. I).

[[isaiah-bye|Isaiah]]'s response — "You'll never be able to say that about me" — was the founding moment of his lifelong pride. He "never in his heart forgave him" and developed "a sturdy independence and an unyielding pride" in direct response to Meriwether's words (Ch. I).

## The Naming Legacy

In a gesture of complex pride, [[isaiah-bye|Isaiah]] named his own son [[meriwether-bye]] — "a boy with the same name which old white [[aaron-bye|Aaron Bye]]'s son had borne and with as good chances" (Ch. II). The naming was a declaration: black [[meriwether-bye|Meriwether Bye]] would not be a coachman; he would be a doctor, a man of consequence. The name was both a rebuke to the white boy who had consigned [[isaiah-bye|Isaiah]] to servitude and an act of faith that the future could be different.

[[isaiah-bye|Isaiah]]'s faith was not rewarded. The black [[meriwether-bye|Meriwether Bye]], lacking his father's "solid trait of concentration," drifted through his medical studies without distinction, lost his inherited property through "sickness, gambling, and a hitherto unsuspected penchant for sheer laziness" (Ch. II), and ended his life in shiftless poverty. Years later, the black Meriwether wrote to "Dr. Meriwether Bye telling him who he was and implying he would not disdain a little aid." The letter likely never reached its recipient, who "at that time was traveling in Europe with his tiny grandson" (Ch. II).

The grandson traveling in Europe was the current [[dr-meriwether-bye]] of Bryn Mawr — a third generation of white Bye physicians, carrying the same name, living in "the old Bye house," and unaware of the black namesake whose father had once believed that a shared name might mean shared fortune.

## Critical Significance

Dr. Meriwether Bye exists entirely in the novel's historical backstory, yet his shadow falls across three generations of the black Bye line. His name — given freely to his own descendants, borrowed by [[isaiah-bye|Isaiah]] for his son — becomes one of the novel's central symbols of the entanglement of black and white lives in America. That the black [[meriwether-bye|Meriwether Bye]] fails where the white one succeeds is, for [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]], not a judgment on individual merit but an illustration of how names, however shared, cannot equalize the structural inheritance of slavery and patronage.

The childhood scene between the two boys — white Meriwether dispensing his casual prophecy, black [[isaiah-bye|Isaiah]] receiving it as a wound that would become a creed — is the novel's most compressed dramatization of its central theme: that the accident of birth into one race or another determines, with terrifying precision, the range of life outcomes that can be imagined.
