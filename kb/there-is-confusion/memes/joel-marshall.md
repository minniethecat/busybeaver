# Joel Marshall

Joel Marshall is a central figure in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s *There Is Confusion* (1924), the father of protagonist [[joanna-marshall|Joanna Marshall]] and the emotional and thematic anchor of the novel's exploration of ambition across generations.

## Early Life and Enslavement

Joel Marshall was "born a slave and the son of a slave in [[richmond-virginia|Richmond, Virginia]]" (001-003). His father was "broken and sickened by slavery" and died while Joel was still young. His mother, known in the novel as "Mammy," survived to old age, providing a direct link between slavery, Reconstruction, and the novel's early-twentieth-century present.

From childhood, Joel harbored an intense drive for greatness that set him apart. His mother recalled: "As a little tyke, he was always pesterin' me: 'Mammy, I'll be a great man some day, won't I? Mammy, you're gonna help me to be great?'" (001-003). He studied the lives of Napoleon, Lincoln, and William Lloyd Garrison, and later Frederick Douglass and Toussaint L'Ouverture, seeing in their trajectories a template for his own aspirations.

The novel makes clear that Joel's ambition was not selfish: "If he wanted to be great he also wanted to do honestly and faithfully the things that bring greatness" (001-003). Yet even as a boy, he felt "a sick despair" at the obstacles before him — not only "his color, his poverty" but also a father broken by slavery and a home destroyed by fire.

## The Catering Career

At fifteen, after the death of his father and the burning of the family home, Joel and his mother entered domestic service for Harvey Carter, a wealthy Virginian. There he learned cooking from an expert French chef (001-004).

Joel saved methodically, intending to attend a theological seminary and become a minister — "a man with a great name and a healing tongue." But his mother's prolonged illness forced him to abandon this plan. He drew his savings from the bank, rented "a tiny two and a half room shack," and opened a lunchstand that grew through his culinary skill into a celebrated catering business (001-004).

The turning point came when he catered a banquet for President Ulysses S. Grant during Grant's visit to Richmond. "The Grant incident created a reputation for him in New York and he was shrewd enough to take advantage of it and move there" (001-004). By the time of the novel's main action, Joel is a prosperous businessman, a church steward, and a respected community leader.

## The Unfulfilled Dream

Despite his material success, Joel carries a persistent sense of incompletion. "His lack of education would be a bar forever between him and the kind of greatness for which his heart had yearned" (001-004). His mother never understood this: "To her, her son was already great." The gap between community recognition and his own standard of achievement is the central tragedy of Joel's life.

His [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]] to a former schoolteacher briefly rekindled his intellectual aspirations, but his wife was "fundamentally unambitious," and his first three children — [[alec-marshall|Alec]], [[philip-marshall|Philip]], and [[sylvia-marshall|Sylvia]] — showed no sign of his driving intensity. By forty, he had "given up his old ideas completely and decided to be a good businessman, husband and father; not a bad decision if he had but known it" (001-010).

## Joanna and Redemption

The birth of [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] transforms Joel's resignation into renewed purpose. From infancy, [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] shows his own traits — intensity, ambition, a preference for contemplation. The novel describes how [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] "used to lie in her bed at night, straight and still with her eyes fixed on the stretch of sky visible even from a house in Fifty-ninth Street and dream dreams. 'I'll be great, too,' she told herself" (001-011).

Joel becomes her enabler: teaching her to read, providing her books, taking "her everywhere with him." When [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] declares, "Daddy, you'll help me to be a great woman, somebody you'll be proud of?" the novel records that "Her words made him so happy; they renewed his life" (001-011). In his daughter's ambition, Joel finds the redemption his own life could not provide.
