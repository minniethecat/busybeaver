# Mrs. Terry

Mrs. Terry is a supporting character in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *There Is Confusion*, appearing in the novel's World War I sequences as the colored worker in charge of the American leave-center at Chambéry, in the Savoy region of France. She supervises [[maggie-ellersley]]'s war service and provides the setting for [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s reunion with [[peter-bye]].

## The Chambéry Leave-Center

Mrs. Terry is introduced when [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie Ellersley]], seeking escape from her failed [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]] to [[henderson-neal]], volunteers for overseas war work:

> she was summoned to Chambéry to help Mrs. Terry, the colored worker, in charge of the leave-center in the Savoyard capital [3821-CCC].

The leave-center serves Negro soldiers stationed in France — men who, even as they risk their lives, face the "hateful prejudice that followed and flayed them in the very act of laying down their lives for their country." Mrs. Terry's work places her at the intersection of war service and racial injustice: she ministers to soldiers who have been denied access to recreational facilities in Aix-les-Bains because a white American woman protested what she saw as an approach to "social equality."

The soldiers' affection for both women is evident: "They were pathetically proud of her — and of Mrs. Terry, too — glad to be allowed a sight of her bright face, to exchange a word" [3823-CCC].

## The Reunion Scene

Mrs. Terry's leave-center becomes the stage for [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] and [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]]'s unexpected wartime reunion. When a new detachment of soldiers files into the canteen, [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] finds herself handing a cup of cocoa to Peter, now a tall lieutenant:

> His manner toward Mrs. Terry, whom he had known slightly in New York, seemed by contrast almost effusive [3827-CCC].

This detail — Peter's prior acquaintance with Mrs. Terry from New York — establishes her as part of the extended social network that connects the novel's characters across time and geography. Mrs. Terry's presence provides continuity between the Harlem and [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia worlds of the novel's first half and the European theater of its wartime climax.

## Medical Judgment

Mrs. Terry's experience with gassed soldiers lends authority to her assessment of Peter's condition:

> even Mrs. Terry, who had seen many cases of gassed men, thought he had come out of the hospital too soon [3869-CCC].

Her judgment, offered without drama, underscores the seriousness of Peter's condition and [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s growing concern. As a woman "who had seen many cases," Mrs. Terry represents the accumulated expertise of war workers — knowledge earned through experience rather than formal training.

## Critical Significance

Mrs. Terry is one of several minor characters through whom [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] maps the wartime experiences of African Americans. Her leave-center in Chambéry dramatizes the paradox of the Negro soldier's position: welcomed as a fighting man, segregated as a social being. Within this constrained space, Mrs. Terry creates the conditions for the novel's central romantic reunion — the chaste, careful renewal of feeling between [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] and Peter that unfolds against the backdrop of war.
