# Joshua Bye

Joshua Bye is the foundational ancestor of the [[family-in-there-is-confusion|Bye family]] in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *There Is Confusion*. An enslaved black man freed in 1780, his labor, lineage, and the truth of his paternity constitute one of the novel's central revelations about the entanglement of race, family, and inheritance in American history.

## Freedom and the Bye Family

[[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] establishes Joshua's historical context in the novel's first extended genealogical passage: "As far back as the last decades of the eighteenth century there had been white Byes and black Byes in [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia. The black Byes were known to be the chattels of Aaron and Dinah Bye, Quakers, who without reluctance had set free their slaves, among them black Joshua Bye, the great-grandfather of Peter. This was done in 1780 according to the laws of Pennsylvania, which thus allowed the Quakers to salve their consciences without offending their thrifty instincts."

The passage's irony is deliberate: the Quakers' "thrifty instincts" and "salved consciences" suggest that freedom was granted not from pure principle but in a transaction that preserved white economic interests.

## Economic Foundation of the White Byes

Peter's father Meriwether transmits a family oral tradition that reframes Joshua as the economic engine behind the [[family-in-there-is-confusion|Bye family]]'s prosperity: "The white Byes were Quakers, and see how they left us stranded, though my father told me old black Joshua Bye practically coined them their money." This assertion — that Joshua's labor generated the wealth that the white Byes subsequently withheld from their black relatives — introduces one of the novel's core economic critiques.

## Genealogy and Hidden Parentage

In the novel's climactic genealogical disclosure scene, Peter examines his family Bible: "His lean brown finger traced the lines. 'Joshua Bye, born about 1780'—heavens, that must have been his great-great-grandfather. No, maybe he was just a 'great,' for the black Byes, he remembered hearing his father Meriwether say, lived long and married late."

The crucial revelation comes from an elderly Bye relative who discloses the hidden truth: "black Joshua Bye, was my oldest brother; my father—he was [[aaron-bye|Aaron Bye]]—was his father. Joshua was really his oldest child." This means that [[aaron-bye|Aaron Bye]], the white Quaker who "freed" Joshua, was in fact his biological father — making the [[family-in-there-is-confusion|Bye family]]'s black and white branches literally kin.

Peter's response is shock and anger: "My father told me and told me often that much of [[aaron-bye|Aaron Bye]]'s prosperity had been due to the loyalty and hard work of Joshua Bye. But he never told me that Aaron was his father. And you knew this, have known it—"

## The Erased Name: Ceazer Bye

An additional genealogical mystery surfaces in the family Bible: "A little below a familiar name appeared, Joshua Bye, and above it, evidently written, in the same hand, Ceazer Bye. But through this entry a firm black line was drawn, drawn with a pen that dug down into the thin paper." The struck-through name of Ceazer Bye — presumably an earlier generation or an alternate name for Joshua — represents the violence of erasure that accompanies even the most "benevolent" forms of slavery and manumission.

## Critical Significance

Joshua Bye, despite appearing only in retrospective narration, is one of the novel's most significant figures. His story embodies *There Is Confusion*'s central thesis: that American racial identity is built on intertwined genealogies of exploitation and [[family-in-there-is-confusion|kinship]] that the official record works to suppress. The revelation that [[aaron-bye|Aaron Bye]] both owned and fathered Joshua crystallizes the novel's argument that the "confusion" of its title is not merely psychological but structural — written into family Bibles, property records, and the very surnames that characters carry. Peter's eventual reconciliation with this history, facilitated by [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]], constitutes one of the novel's most important arcs of self-knowledge.
