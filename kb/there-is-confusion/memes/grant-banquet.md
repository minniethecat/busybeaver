# The Grant Banquet

The Grant Banquet is a pivotal backstory event in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s *[[ch005-there-is-confusion|There Is Confusion]]* (1924). Occurring before the novel's main action, it marks the turning point in [[joel-marshall|Joel Marshall]]'s career and serves as the economic catalyst for the Marshall family's migration from [[richmond-virginia|Richmond, Virginia]], to [[new-york-city|New York City]].

## Historical Context

The event takes place during the post-Civil War period when Ulysses S. Grant, the former Union general and sitting president, visited Richmond — the former capital of the Confederacy. The selection of a formerly enslaved African American man to cater a presidential banquet in this context carries historical significance, marking both the transformations of Reconstruction and the limits of those transformations.

## The Event Itself

The novel provides limited detail about the banquet itself, presenting it primarily through the retrospective pride of Joel's mother: "Once you know they had him fix a banquet fur President Grant. Sent all the way to Richmond fur 'im" (001-003). The dialect rendering of Mammy's speech emphasizes the generational and educational distance between Joel, the self-made businessman, and his formerly enslaved mother.

The phrase "sent all the way to Richmond" suggests that Joel's reputation had extended beyond Virginia by this point, or at least that his skills were sufficiently distinctive to warrant special arrangement. The Grant banquet thus serves as both validation of Joel's culinary expertise and amplification of his professional standing.

## Consequences

The most significant consequence was geographical: "The Grant incident created a reputation for him in New York and he was shrewd enough to take advantage of it and move there" (001-004). The move to New York transforms the Marshall family's circumstances and possibilities. Without it, [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s access to musical training, her exposure to the cultural ferment of [[harlem|Harlem]], and the novel's entire plot would be unimaginable.

The banquet also exemplifies Joel's pattern of converting culinary skill into social capital. Unlike the intellectual greatness he craved, catering success came through a domestic art associated with service — cooking. This irony haunts Joel's achievement: he is celebrated for skills that, in the plantation economy, would have been valued as the competence of an enslaved person.

## Symbolic Resonance

The Grant banquet compresses multiple historical ironies. A formerly enslaved man catering for the president who accepted Lee's surrender at Appomattox; an African American achieving national recognition through domestic service; a career-defining moment that simultaneously validates and limits its subject. The event exemplifies Fauset's technique of embedding historical complexity within seemingly straightforward narrative details.
