# Miss Phelps

Miss Phelps is a minor character in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *There Is Confusion*, appearing as a member of the dance performance committee that evaluates [[joanna-marshall]]'s audition. Her spontaneous enthusiasm — the first voice to break the committee's professional reserve — signals [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s triumph.

## Joanna's Audition

Miss Phelps appears in the novel's extended audition scene, when [[joanna-marshall|Joanna Marshall]] performs before a committee assembled by [[vera-manning|Vera Sharples Manning]] to judge her readiness for a public recital. Alongside [[miss-rosen|Miss Rosen]], Miss Phelps is one of two women on the committee:

> the two other women in the room — a Miss Rosen and a Miss Phelps as they turned out to be — bowed also noncommittally but without hostility [3457-CCC].

Her initial reserve — "noncommittally but without hostility" — is the careful neutrality of a gatekeeper who has not yet been persuaded.

## The Decisive Reaction

When [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] performs, Miss Phelps's reserve dissolves. Her exclamation — "Oh, but that's great, that's genius!" [3477-CCC] — is the first explicit endorsement from the committee, and its enthusiasm breaks the professional formality of the occasion. Where [[miss-rosen|Miss Rosen]] is described in terms of function ("our secretary"), Miss Phelps is defined by reaction — she feels and expresses what the committee more cautiously withholds.

Later, when a crisis threatens [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s performance — a white audience member objects to an unmasked colored dancer — Miss Phelps joins the emergency consultation:

> Mr. Hale, Miss Sharples, Francis, Miss Rosen and Miss Phelps held a hurried consultation behind the scenes [3509-CCC].

## Critical Significance

Miss Phelps serves a single but essential narrative purpose: to voice the audience's delighted recognition of [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s talent. Her "that's genius!" is the moment the committee's skepticism converts to advocacy, and it marks the beginning of [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s professional ascent. In the novel's economy of minor characters, Miss Phelps is the committee's heart — the member who allows herself to be moved and, in doing so, moves the others.
