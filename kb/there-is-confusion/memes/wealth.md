# Wealth and Economic Aspiration in There Is Confusion

Wealth and economic aspiration function as a central axis of social measurement in *There Is Confusion*. Fauset traces how successive generations of African American characters pursue financial stability and property ownership as both a practical necessity and a marker of respectability. From [[joel-marshall]]'s rise from poverty to prosperity through the catering trade to Isaiah Bye's accumulation of Philadelphia property, the novel insists that economic achievement is inseparable from the broader project of racial advancement.

## Joel Marshall's Rise from Poverty

Joel Marshall's trajectory from enslaved child to successful New York caterer is the novel's primary success story of economic self-making. Born a slave in Richmond, Virginia, Joel began with nothing — "Forty years ago I was almost a pauper in Richmond" — and built a catering business through relentless work and shrewd business sense. His signature sandwiches and pastries attracted first Black customers, then white ones, and eventually outside orders that carried his reputation across state lines.

> "Look at me today. I spend more on you in a month, Joanna, than my mother and I ever saw in a five-year stretch."

The catering business allows Joel to purchase a home on Fifty-ninth Street and provide his children with education, music lessons, and the material comforts of middle-class life. Yet Joel never regards wealth as an end in itself. His thwarted ambition had been the ministry — a calling he sacrificed to economic necessity — and he channels his unfulfilled desire for greatness into his children, particularly [[joanna-marshall]].

## Property, Respectability, and the Bye Family

The Bye family narrative offers a contrasting economic arc: accumulation followed by dissipation. Isaiah Bye, the son of a freedman, builds an extraordinary portfolio through teaching, tutoring at twenty-five cents an hour, writing pamphlets for the A.M.E. Church, and shrewd investment. By the time his son Meriwether is eight, Isaiah owns two houses on Pearl Street, a house on Vine Street, a half-interest in his brother-in-law's store, and a cemetery plot — a "edifice of respectability and good citizenship" carefully constructed over decades.

> "Who shall say how he increased his slender store of knowledge? How he learned to read wise books borrowed and bought as best he might?"

This edifice collapses in the next generation. [[meriwether-bye]], destined by his father to become a great physician, instead squanders his inheritance "through sickness, gambling, and a hitherto unsuspected penchant for sheer laziness." The loss of the Philadelphia properties represents not merely financial ruin but the dissolution of a family's claim to standing. The Bye Family Bible, passed to [[peter-bye]], becomes the sole remaining token of former respectability.

## Wealth, Class, and Racial Boundaries

Fauset portrays economic class as operating both within and across racial lines. Within the African American community, distinctions of wealth and property ownership create internal hierarchies. The Marshalls' comfortable home with its walnut furniture, silver service, and good linen signals their membership in a respectable middle class. Maggie Ellersley's poverty, by contrast, marks her as an outsider — her "calla-lily charm" cannot survive exposure to the harsh economics of the [[tenderloin-district]].

Across the color line, Fauset registers the absurdity of inherited white wealth. Meriwether Bye (the white Quaker boy) casually assumes his own superiority — "good trees, good fruit; rich, important people, rich important sons" — while being unable to imagine that Isaiah Bye's son might aspire to anything beyond service. The novel insists that Black economic achievement, achieved against structural barriers, represents a more genuine form of greatness than inherited white prosperity.
