# Helena Arnold

Helena Arnold is a dancer who marries Alexander "[[alec-marshall|Alec]]" Marshall, the eldest son of the [[family-in-there-is-confusion|Marshall family]], in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *There Is Confusion*. Though she appears only intermittently, her career and connections embody the novel's preoccupation with the barriers facing African American artists and the role of white patronage in crossing them.

## The Dancer and the Color Line

Helena's artistic biography is first narrated through her encounter with racial exclusion in the world of dance instruction. The European teacher Bertully refuses her as a student: "I'm sorry, Mees, but the white Americans like not to study with the brown Americans. Vair seely, but so. I am a poor man, I must follow the weeshes of my clients!" [[joanna-marshall|Joanna Marshall]], recounting this rebuff, shrugs with practiced resignation — but the incident illustrates the institutional barriers that talented African American performers faced even in the supposedly cosmopolitan setting of New York.

Helena's breakthrough comes not through her own efforts but through the intervention of [[vera-manning|Vera Sharples]], a white patron: "A Miss Sharples — why, they're the people your Aunt Susan works for, aren't they? Your aunt told Miss Sharples about Helena, and Miss Sharples took her, herself, to Bertully." This chain of connection — from Aunt Susan as domestic worker, through Miss Sharples as employer and patron, to Bertully as gatekeeper — maps the precarious pathways by which African American artists accessed training and opportunity in the early twentieth century.

Helena's talent is later validated when Miss Sharples, reflecting on her dance [[concert-in-there-is-confusion|performances]], uses her as a benchmark for authentic African American artistry: "I'd seen Helena Arnold dance, you know, and I'd seen you, and I figured that your way was the right way, because you were colored."

## Ten-Year Courtship

Helena's romantic trajectory with [[alec-marshall|Alec Marshall]] follows an unusually protracted arc. The narrator notes that after "seeing each other constantly and unresponsively for ten years," the pair "suddenly fell completely in [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|love]] on that night of the Pierce Day Nursery dance." The catalyst is never fully explained — [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] seems to suggest that the right moment, the right atmosphere, can transform a long-standing acquaintance into passion in an instant.

[[alec-marshall|Alec]]'s proposal is impulsive: "[[alec-marshall|Sandy]] proved himself an impulsive wooer, for he won Helena's consent and would have married her before [[sylvia-marshall|Sylvia]]'s and [[brian-spencer|Brian]]'s wedding came off." His argument for precedence draws on primogeniture: "Well, I'm the oldest. It's really my place to marry first." Helena, characteristically practical about the material dimensions of [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]], delays to serve as bridesmaid at [[sylvia-marshall|Sylvia]]'s wedding: "Think of all the extra clothes I can get. Besides, I couldn't possibly finish my trousseau before."

Their [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]] in the June following [[sylvia-marshall|Sylvia]]'s wedding marks the beginning of the [[family-in-there-is-confusion|Marshall family]]'s geographic dispersal, as [[alec-marshall|Alec]] and Helena move into their own house.

## Role as Connector

Helena's most consequential function in the plot may be her role as a connector. "Through Helena Arnold and indirectly through [[vera-manning|Vera Sharples]] she obtained the promise of an interview with one of the season's favorites." This pathway — from Helena, through the white patroness Sharples, to a professional opportunity — exemplifies the network of mutual obligation and advocacy that enabled African American performers to advance in a segregated industry.

## Critical Significance

Helena Arnold, like [[alec-marshall|Alec]] among the Marshall siblings, belongs to the novel's secondary register of characters who represent functional, un-dramatic lives. Her successful navigation of the color line in dance, her decade-long patience in [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|love]], and her integration into the [[family-in-there-is-confusion|Marshall family]] through [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]] all model a quieter form of achievement than [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s ambition or [[sylvia-marshall|Sylvia]]'s social grace. She is also one of the few characters in the novel whose artistic career is explicitly addressed, making her a counterpoint to [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] — a dancer who, unlike [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]], seems to have achieved professional recognition without the corresponding emotional turbulence. In a novel that foregrounds the costs of genius, Helena suggests that talent, patience, and the right connections may be a more sustainable path.
