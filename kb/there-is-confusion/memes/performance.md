# Performance and Theater in There Is Confusion

Performance — on stage, in dance, and through artistic production — serves as one of the novel's central metaphors for self-making. Joanna Marshall's career as a dancer and the various theatrical productions that thread through the narrative represent not only professional ambition but the broader project of crafting an identity that transcends racial and social constraints. The novel is deeply interested in what it means to perform — on stage and in life — and in the relationship between artistic and social self-presentation.

## Joanna Marshall's Stage Career

Joanna Marshall's pursuit of a stage career is the novel's most sustained engagement with performance as a vocation. From her childhood love of dance to her eventual appearance in "The Dance of the Nations," Joanna's trajectory dramatizes the tension between artistic calling and the social expectations placed on a young woman of the "better class of colored people."

The novel tracks her dedication with precision:

> She spent her strength by day going to see performances, practicing, inventing new steps, studying the methods of great dancers. [018-003]

This description emphasizes practice, study, and deliberate effort — performance is work, not merely inspiration. Fauset resists the romanticization of artistic talent, instead showing the discipline required to transform natural ability into professional accomplishment. The phrase "spent her strength" acknowledges the physical cost of this dedication.

The professional stakes of Joanna's career are registered in her booking for a concert tour:

> Even the concert tour, a remarkable booking for so young an artist, left her indifferent. [013-006]

The narrator's parenthetical — "a remarkable booking for so young an artist" — establishes the objective terms of Joanna's success even as her subjective experience is one of dissatisfaction. The tension between external achievement and internal emptiness is a recurring note in the novel's treatment of artistic ambition.

## The Dance of the Nations

The culminating performance of the novel is "The Dance of the Nations," a production that Joanna conceives and executes as a representation of global cultures through dance:

> I'm going to do a dance representing all the nations," she announced. "It will be something entirely new. [012-065]

This ambition — to represent "all the nations" through a single dancer's body — makes explicit the novel's interest in performance as a vehicle for transcending particularity. Joanna's multi-national dance is an assertion that her art can encompass more than her specific racial and national identity, even as her position as an African American woman necessarily inflects how that ambition is received.

The production itself draws characters from across the novel's social world:

> People came from Philadelphia and New York to see Joanna in the "Dance of the Nations." And the result was that she was offered a contract. [034-001]

The geographical reach of the audience — "from Philadelphia and New York" — signals the scope of Joanna's achievement and the power of performance to draw disparate communities into a shared experience.

## The Stage as Transformation

Performance in the novel is associated with metamorphosis. When Joanna performs, she becomes something beyond her everyday self:

> Night after night, when the performance was over, she appeared, splendid and glowing, to receive the applause that was her due. [033-002]

The stage offers a space where the limitations of race, class, and gender can be — if not overcome, then at least suspended. In performance, Joanna is judged not by her racial identity but by her artistry, a liberation that the novel treats as genuine but also precarious. The applause that is "her due" suggests a justice that the everyday world denies her.

## Performance and Social Performance

The novel suggests a parallel between theatrical performance and the broader social performance required of its characters. Just as Joanna crafts a persona on stage, the Marshalls and their social circle perform respectability, refinement, and belonging in their daily lives. The novel's attention to dress, manners, speech, and institutional affiliation can be read as an extended meditation on the performative dimensions of class and racial identity.

The small incident of a theater patron holding a coat "during the performance" and wrinkling it "shamelessly" [009-016] captures in miniature the tension between the elevated space of the theater and the mundane, sometimes careless social world that surrounds it. The performance on stage is flawless; the performance of the audience — their manners, their attention — remains imperfectly realized.
