# Alec Marshall

Alexander "Alec" Marshall, known variously as "Alec" and "Sandy" by his siblings, is the eldest son of [[joel-marshall|Joel]] and Mrs. Marshall in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *There Is Confusion*. Unlike his ambitious sister [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] and intellectually driven brother [[philip-marshall|Philip]], Alec represents the "average" American boy who settles into a practical, respectable life in business and family.

## Character and Temperament

[[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] introduces Alec in explicit contrast with his father's thwarted aspirations: "Alec, as he was usually called, did not resemble his father in the least. He was the average baby and the average boy, interested in marbles, in playing hookey, in parachutes, but with no determination to be a dark Napoleon or a Frederick Douglass." This description is not pejorative — rather, Alec's ordinariness is part of what makes the [[family-in-there-is-confusion|Marshall family]] a rounded social portrait. His existence proves that not every child of an aspiring colored family must pursue genius; a good citizen, businessman, husband, and father is also a legitimate outcome.

His siblings describe him in childhood as physically plump: "You were fat, Alec, and your face rose large and round over your collar like a full moon. You had two eyes set away back from your fat cheeks." Yet as an adult, Alec is "tall and well set-up," a "fine gentleman" like his brother [[philip-marshall|Philip]], whom [[joel-marshall|Joel]] has shaped through "teaching as well as by his attitude toward their mother and sisters."

Alec is characterized as "slower, like [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]" — a steadiness that distinguishes him from the mercurial [[philip-marshall|Philip]] and [[sylvia-marshall|Sylvia]]. Where [[philip-marshall|Philip]] is described as "a little like [[sylvia-marshall|Sylvia]], only neither as handsome nor as lithe and quick," Alec shares [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s methodical, unhurried tempo.

## Profession

As an adult, Alec enters the real estate business, a practical profession befitting his temperament. [[brian-spencer|Brian Spencer]], [[sylvia-marshall|Sylvia]]'s future husband, becomes his partner: "[[brian-spencer|Brian]], too, had gone into real estate as Alexander's partner." Alec participates in the conversations about purchasing [[joel-marshall|Joel]] a house in Harlem's One Hundred and Thirty-first Street, the neighborhood's emerging character as a center of African American life in New York.

His church attendance, unlike [[philip-marshall|Philip]]'s politically analytical presence, is pragmatic: "Sandy, now in the real estate business, thought it gave him standing in the community." This instrumental approach to institutional participation reflects his fundamentally uncomplicated relationship to social convention — he does what is expected and benefits from it, without [[philip-marshall|Philip]]'s restless questioning or [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s driving ambition.

## Marriage to Helena Arnold

Alec's [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|courtship]] of [[helena-arnold|Helena Arnold]] follows an unusually protracted but ultimately conventional arc. After "seeing each other constantly and unresponsively for ten years," the two "suddenly fell completely in [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|love]] on that night of the Pierce Day Nursery dance." Alec reveals an impulsive, romantic side that surprises his family: "Sandy proved himself an impulsive wooer, for he won [[helena-arnold|Helena]]'s consent and would have married her before [[sylvia-marshall|Sylvia]]'s and [[brian-spencer|Brian]]'s wedding came off."

His argument for precedence is pragmatic and traditional: "Well, I'm the oldest. It's really my place to marry first." [[helena-arnold|Helena]], aware of the sartorial opportunities involved, delays the wedding to serve as bridesmaid at [[sylvia-marshall|Sylvia]]'s: "Think of all the extra clothes I can get. Besides, I couldn't possibly finish my trousseau before." The two marry the June following [[sylvia-marshall|Sylvia]]'s wedding and move into their own house, an event that contributes to the gradual dispersal of the Marshall household.

After Alec's departure, the family finds adjustment difficult: "Else we'd be lonely, what with Sandy gone for good, and [[philip-marshall|Philip]] and [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] everlastingly 'on the road.'"

## Relationship with Siblings

Among the Marshall siblings, Alec shares the least intimate bond with [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]. The narrator notes that "[[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] and Alec had never been thrown much together" — a contrast to her deep camaraderie with [[philip-marshall|Philip]]. His boyhood experience of Sunday collars and Buster Brown discomfort, recounted with humor in adult reminiscence, suggests a childhood that was conventional rather than transformative.

Yet Alec participates fully in the family's rituals and culture: the Friday and Saturday night dances, the church attendance, the encouragement of [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s talent. He represents the foundational layer of the Marshall enterprise — the son who will manage the practical world while his siblings reach for the exceptional.

## Critical Significance

Alec Marshall's function within the novel's character system is that of the "norm" against which his more extraordinary siblings are measured. His averageness is not a failure; it is a legitimate life choice in a novel that otherwise focuses on exceptional ambition ([[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]), intellectual leadership ([[philip-marshall|Philip]]), and social grace ([[sylvia-marshall|Sylvia]]). Through Alec, [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] argues implicitly that the "New Negro" encompasses not only the artist, the intellectual, and the race leader but also the businessman, the husband, the citizen — those who build communities through steady work and family life rather than through transformative vision. His successful real estate partnership with [[brian-spencer|Brian Spencer]] also suggests the economic infrastructure that made Harlem's black middle class possible.
