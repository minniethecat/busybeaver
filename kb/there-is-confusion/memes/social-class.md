# Social Class in There Is Confusion

Social class operates as a defining axis of identity and aspiration throughout *There Is Confusion*. Fauset depicts a stratified African American community in which the "better class of colored people" — a phrase that recurs through the novel — distinguishes itself through education, profession, cultural refinement, and institutional belonging, even as it navigates the overriding racial hierarchy of early twentieth-century America. The novel's treatment of class is notable for its insistence on internal differentiation: Fauset refuses the flattening assumption that all Black Americans occupied a single social position.

## The "Better Class of Colored People"

The novel's characters are acutely conscious of their position within African American social hierarchy. The Marshall family embodies the "better class" ideal: educated, professionally accomplished, culturally sophisticated, and anchored in the A.M.E. Church. As the narrator observes:

> Like many of the better class of colored people, the Marshalls maintained standards of conduct and appearance that reflected their sense of communal responsibility. [007-023]

This class identity is not merely aspirational but institutional. The phrase "better class" appears throughout the novel as a term of art, marking those who have achieved education, professional standing, and cultural capital. The Marshalls' home, according to the narrator, enjoyed a reputation:

> Their house spread among the better class of transients. All her lodgers were people of refinement, of some standing in the community. [008-052]

Peter Bye, observing the Marshalls from his own more precarious social position, registers the distance between their world and his own:

> Peter noted a little envious look on the faces of some of the boys who had come from homes where there was no tradition. The Byes had tradition, but it was, alas, a white tradition. [006-008]

This observation captures the particular complexity of class in the novel: tradition and lineage matter, but racial identity complicates their transmission. A "white tradition" cannot easily be converted into standing within the African American community.

## Class and Racial Hierarchy

The novel insists on the simultaneity of class distinctions *within* the African American community and the racial barrier that affected all Black Americans regardless of internal class position. Joanna Marshall's father articulates a philosophy of class mobility that is explicitly racialized:

> He told Joanna that no class of men remained static through the ages. He pointed out that the white man had had his centuries of degradation, and the black man was going through his. The thing to do was to hold one's head high and keep on. [002-017]

This double consciousness — class pride within the community, racial subordination without — structures the experiences of every major character in the novel. The narrator explicitly frames the problem:

> It is a system which believes in a superior class and yields blindly to its mandates. [003-028]

The characters must navigate a society that consigns them to an inferior racial category while they simultaneously construct internal hierarchies of achievement and refinement. This tension is not resolvable within the novel's world — it is the permanent condition of African American middle-class life in the Jim Crow era.

## Professional Identity and Social Standing

Profession serves as the primary marker of class position in the novel's world. Medicine, law, education, and the arts are not merely occupations but badges of belonging to the "better class." Peter Bye's pursuit of a medical career, Joanna Marshall's dedication to dance, and Maggie Neal's struggle for economic independence are all mediated through the lens of class aspiration.

Medical education in particular functions as a class marker. Peter's attendance at medical school and his relationship with his classmates reflect the precarious nature of professional identity for African Americans:

> Perhaps by now the class was a bit fed up with cramming. [006-016]

The casual mention of the medical school class — its rhythms, its social dynamics — positions Peter within a professional milieu that carries class meaning independent of race.

The novel registers the precarity of this professional-class identity. Characters who fall short of professional success — or who must work in undignified conditions — experience their situation not only as economic hardship but as class diminishment. Maggie Neal, described at one point as "alone and déclassée," embodies this anxiety [019-016]. The French term "déclassée" is pointed: it suggests not merely poverty but a *fall* from class position, a loss of standing that is social as much as economic.

## Class Anxieties and Boundaries

Beneath the confident performance of class belonging, the novel registers persistent anxiety. Peter Bye's struggle with his family history — the white Byes of Philadelphia — reflects a specifically class-inflected insecurity. His lack of a secure family narrative within the African American community leaves him socially unmoored, unable to claim membership in the "better class" on the same terms as the Marshalls.

The novel also shows how class boundaries are policed through social mechanisms rather than formal rules. Access to certain spaces, events, and social circles requires credentials that are never explicitly stated but universally understood:

> He was soon enrolled in the social calendar, and if he chose to accept it, a new world was open to him. [013-043]

This enrollment in the "social calendar" is presented as a kind of naturalization — the social equivalent of citizenship. It is granted, not claimed, and the novel is attentive to the gatekeeping mechanisms that determine who is enrolled and who is not.
