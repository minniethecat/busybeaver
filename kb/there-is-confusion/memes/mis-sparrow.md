# Mis' Sparrow

Mis' Sparrow is a minor but symbolically weighty character in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *There Is Confusion*. As the cousin of [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie Ellersley]]'s mother, she embodies the grinding poverty, thwarted aspiration, and corrosive resentment that the novel presents as the condition [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s generation must transcend — or be trapped by.

## Physical Description and Circumstances

[[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] introduces Mis' Sparrow with precision: she is "small and spidery," a woman with "a perpetual complaint against the world." She serves as cook at a house on Madison Avenue, where [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s mother, a laundress, joins her on Thursdays and Fridays to wash and iron and gossip. [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]], as a child, comes to these dinner meetings and absorbs the atmosphere of bitterness and envy that surrounds Mis' Sparrow.

Her circumstances, the narrator hastens to add, must be understood in context: "Her jaundiced disposition may be excused, however, when one reflects that hers was a lot which had been hard ever since she could remember. She was poor, she was weak, she was ignorant. Add to that the fact that she was black in a country where color is a crime and you have her 'complex.'"

## The Problem of the "Complex"

[[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]]'s use of the word "complex" — placed in quotation marks — is deliberate and loaded. Mis' Sparrow is presented as a case study in how structural oppression produces psychological deformation:

> "Some people would say she had really done well in one sense with her life. She had attained by her own unaided efforts to a comfortable, even if menial, position, where she had heat, light and enough to eat. They would ask: Considering her beginnings what more could she want? Alas, in that dull soul unknown aspirations stirred, amazing questions took form. 'Why, why, why?' asked Mis' Sparrow in her own peculiar dialect, 'are all the sweetness and light of life showered on some and utterly denied to me?'"

This passage — one of the most philosophically explicit in the novel — establishes Mis' Sparrow as a dramatic counterpoint to the novel's middle-class protagonists. Her "why, why, why?" is the same existential question that drives [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s ambition and [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s desperate social climbing, but spoken by a voice that has no access to the institutions, education, or material resources that might answer it. The narrator's use of "Alas" signals the tragedy: Mis' Sparrow's aspirations are real but her means of realizing them are nonexistent.

## Narrative Function

### Catalyst for Maggie's Ambition

Mis' Sparrow's most direct narrative function is as an inadvertent catalyst for [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie Ellersley]]'s class aspirations. It is during one of these kitchen dinners that Mis' Sparrow recounts the story of [[mrs-proctor|Mrs. Proctor]] — a poor white girl who married into wealth — planting in twelve-year-old [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] the idea that [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]] is the most viable escape route from poverty:

> "She wan't nobuddy. Jes' a little teeny slip of ole white gal. No money, no fambly, no nuthin'."

When [[mrs-ellersley|Mrs. Ellersley]] muses, "Don't it beat all how some people have all the luck? Now if that kind of thing could just happen to my [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]," Mis' Sparrow is "unmoved by the irrelevant allusion to [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]. Where would she get such a chance?" This dismissiveness, born of a lifetime of disappointment, becomes a negative spur to [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s determination.

### Symbol of the Trap

In the novel's later sections, after [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s divorce from [[henderson-neal|Henderson Neal]], Mis' Sparrow reappears as the embodiment of the life [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] is terrified of being confined to forever: "It would mean my death... if he left me alone with nothing in her life but [[madame-harkness|Madame Harkness]] and those two poor old ladies — her mother and Mis' Sparrow — she would die of it." In this moment, Mis' Sparrow represents not just a person but the entire socioeconomic trap from which [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] has spent her life trying to escape.

### The Threat of Violence

Mis' Sparrow figures peripherally in the novel's most violent scene. Her routine departure from the Ellersley house is observed by [[henderson-neal|Henderson Neal]], who has been stalking the household for two weeks: "One by one he had watched them emerge, [[mrs-ellersley|Mrs. Ellersley]] and Mis' Sparrow finally within fifteen minutes of each other." The precision of his surveillance — tracking their movements until he knows the exact window when [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] will be alone — creates a sense of menace that culminates in the attack.

## Relationship with Mrs. Ellersley

As "Cousin Jinny Sparrow," she is [[mrs-ellersley|Mrs. Ellersley]]'s constant companion in later life. The two women are described as "great 'jiners'" who attend "innumerable lodge meetings" together — a detail that places them within the tradition of African American fraternal and mutual aid organizations that provided social infrastructure for working-class black communities. When [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s mother declares "Long's I got Cousin Jinny Sparrow to go around with me I ain't carin'," it reveals the importance of same-class solidarity in sustaining those whom economic ambition has left behind.

## Critical Significance

Mis' Sparrow is one of the few working-class characters in *There Is Confusion* who receives sustained narrative attention. Through her, [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] acknowledges the existence of a black underclass whose suffering is more material than psychological — hunger, ignorance, and poverty rather than the conflicts of ambition and racial identity that define the novel's protagonists. Her small acts of kindness (giving [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] the blue serge dress from [[mrs-proctor|Mrs. Proctor]]) coexist with her resentment and envy, making her the novel's most fully realized portrait of the internal contradictions that poverty produces. She is the warning that drives [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]], the mirror that reflects the cost of failure, and the testament to the fact that "unknown aspirations" stir even in "dull souls" who will never find the means to satisfy them.
