# Joanna's Easter Discovery

The Easter Sunday discovery of [[joanna-marshall|Joanna Marshall]]'s singing talent is the inciting event of [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s *[[ch005-there-is-confusion|There Is Confusion]]* (1924). Occurring when Joanna is ten years old, it transforms her abstract childhood ambition for "greatness" into a concrete artistic path and sets the novel's central plot in motion.

## Setting and Occasion

The event takes place at the Marshall family's church during Easter services. [[madame-caldwell|Madame Caldwell]], "a great colored singer, a beautiful woman," performs an Easter anthem "among the tall white lilies" (001-012). The church setting is significant: African American churches of this period served not only as religious institutions but as cultural spaces where artistic talent could be cultivated, recognized, and celebrated within the community.

The floral description — "tall white lilies" — evokes Easter's associations with resurrection and new beginnings, thematically appropriate for the moment of Joanna's artistic awakening.

## The Performance

After the service, Madame Caldwell dines at the Marshall home on [[fifty-ninth-street|Fifty-Ninth Street]]. Joanna, displaying the directness that characterizes her throughout the novel, approaches the celebrated singer and makes a startling declaration: "I can sing like you, and I can remember the tune of most of that hymn you sang this morning. Listen" (001-012).

Without accompaniment or hesitation, the ten-year-old performs most of the Easter anthem from memory. The novel's description emphasizes the precocious maturity of her voice: it was "already free of the shrillness of childhood and beginning to assume that liquid golden quality which so distinguished it later" (001-012). The phrase "liquid golden quality" is one of Fauset's most striking descriptions, conveying both the sensory richness of Joanna's voice and its promise of future development.

## Madame Caldwell's Response

Madame Caldwell's reaction is complex and revealing. She is first astonished — "She gasped" — then reflective. The novel provides her backstory: "She had won her own laurels through bitter experience in various studios, meeting insult, indifference and unkindness with an unyielding front" (001-012). Her career was forged through suffering, and she had resolved that "no daughter of mine, if she has the voice of an angel shall go through what I have suffered" (001-012).

This bitterness shapes her response to Joanna's talent. Her exclamation — "Poor child, how you will have to work!" — conveys both recognition of Joanna's gift and foreknowledge of the obstacles awaiting a young African American woman in the classical music world. She seizes [[joel-marshall|Joel Marshall]]'s arm with the directive: "Get her a teacher, Mr. Marshall. She has a voice in ten thousand" (001-012).

## Narrative Function

The Easter discovery serves as the novel's inciting incident, converting Joanna's diffuse ambition into specific artistic pursuit. The novel's concluding line for this scene — "Both of them knew at once that the road to glory was stretching out before her" (001-012) — establishes the terms of the narrative that follows. Joanna's "road to glory" will be shaped by the twin recognitions of this Easter Sunday: her exceptional talent and the exceptional challenges she will face in realizing it.
