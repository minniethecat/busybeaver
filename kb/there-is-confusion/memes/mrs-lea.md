# Mrs. Lea

Mrs. Lea is a minor but thematically significant character in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *There Is Confusion*. A wealthy white woman living in a "large low white house" in Bryn Mawr, she appears in two episodes that together frame the novel's treatment of racial prejudice and professional identity. In her first appearance, she casually humiliates [[peter-bye]] by assuming he is a hired entertainer rather than the medical student and gentleman he is; in her second, she is the grieving recipient of war mementos that Peter delivers, creating a moment of shared humanity across the racial divide.

## The Lea Affair

Mrs. Lea first appears when she hires [[tom-mason|Tom Mason]]'s musical ensemble to play for an elaborate evening party at her Bryn Mawr estate. [[tom-mason|Tom]], overwhelmed with engagements, sends [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]] to her home to learn the special arrangements she requires:

> "Tell you what, Bye, we really are cleaning up. I hadn't expected anything like this run of engagements. Now suppose you beat it along to Mrs. Lea's and find out what special arrangements she wants made for the musicians tonight and I'll go on to Mrs. Lawlor and see about tomorrow." [045-009]

Mrs. Lea receives Peter in a small morning-room. [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] sketches her physical appearance with economy and precision, while Peter's reaction reveals his own racialized consciousness:

> Mrs. Lea received Peter in a small morning-room. She was pretty, a genuine blonde, with small delicate features and beautiful fluffy hair. But as Peter did not like fair types, his mind simply registered "washed-out," and took no further stock of her looks. What he did notice was that she was dressed in a lacey, too transparent floating robe, too low in the neck, and too short in the skirt. [045-035]

Mrs. Lea immediately assumes Peter is one of the musicians rather than an intermediary, giving him detailed instructions about the floral arrangements and the musicians' concealment behind palm fronds — "I want the effect of music instead of perfume pouring out of the flowers." When she asks his name, the question is plainly perfunctory.

## Peter's Encounter with Racial Prejudice

The central moment of the scene occurs when Peter corrects Mrs. Lea's assumption, identifying himself as a medical student rather than an entertainer. His response is sharp and dignified:

> Peter felt his skin tightening. "I'm afraid you'll be disappointed. I'm a medical student, not an entertainer. I came here for Mr. Mason, who is very busy. You may be sure I'll give him your instructions. Good day, Mrs. Lea." [045-055]

This brief exchange crystallizes a recurring theme in the novel: the chasm between how Black professionals see themselves and how white society reflexively categorizes them. Peter is training to be a surgeon, on the cusp of a distinguished career, yet Mrs. Lea sees only a colored man who must be there to provide a service. Her assumption is not malicious — she barely registers him at all — but the casualness of the slight makes it, for Peter, all the more galling.

## Catalyst for Peter's Ambition

The encounter with Mrs. Lea proves to be a turning point for Peter. Furious and humiliated, he quits his work with [[tom-mason|Tom Mason]] on the spot and redirects his energies entirely toward his medical career. The episode crystallizes his determination to prove himself:

> Well, he would show her and Isaiah, yes, and Mrs. Lea, too, that there was something to him. But chiefly Joanna. Some day he'd go to her and say, "Joanna, what I am, you made me." [045-105]

The narrative links Peter's encounter with Mrs. Lea to his subsequent romantic urgency with [[joanna-marshall]]: "When she telephoned him on the day on which he had had his interview with Mrs. Lea, she made up her mind to hasten the [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]]" [046-041]. The racial indignity Peter suffers at Mrs. Lea's hands thus becomes an indirect catalyst for the novel's central romance.

## Later Appearance: The War Mementos

Mrs. Lea reappears near the novel's conclusion, in a very different emotional register. After [[meriwether-bye]]'s death in the influenza epidemic of 1918, Peter attempts to return Meriwether's photographs and locket to [[dr-meriwether-bye|Dr. Meriwether Bye]] and to Mrs. Lea, who evidently had some connection to the young soldier:

> He persuaded her to go to Philadelphia, to Bryn Mawr in fact. "I've got to give these pictures and the locket to Dr. Meriwether Bye and to Mrs. Lea. I'm so sorry for them. To think we're alive and have each other —" [039-085]

The elderly Dr. Bye is ill with bronchitis when Peter visits, and he leaves a message and the mementos. He then proceeds to Mrs. Lea's, "just the next station up the line," to deliver the remaining items. The episode is brief but resonant — the woman who once casually demeaned Peter is now a recipient of his quiet, dignified service in a time of collective mourning.

## Critical Significance

Mrs. Lea functions in the novel as a representative of the white upper class whom [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]]'s Black professionals must navigate. She is not a villain — [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] draws her as a pretty, slightly vain society woman, unremarkable in every way except for the racial privilege she wields unthinkingly. Her significance lies precisely in her ordinariness: the racial slight she delivers to Peter requires no malice, only the inertia of a society in which a well-dressed colored man at one's door must be a servant rather than a surgeon-in-training.

Her second appearance complicates this picture. As a grieving recipient of war mementos, she shares with Peter the universal human experience of loss. The juxtaposition of these two encounters — one marked by racial estrangement, the other by shared grief — reflects [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]]'s broader thematic strategy of refusing easy binaries. Mrs. Lea is both a source of racial injury and a fellow mourner, and Peter's willingness to serve her in the latter capacity without bitterness testifies to his own growth and the novel's unsentimental humanism.
