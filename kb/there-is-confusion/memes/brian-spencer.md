# Brian Spencer

Brian Spencer is [[sylvia-marshall|Sylvia Marshall]]'s devoted suitor and eventual husband in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *There Is Confusion*. A college-educated man who enters real estate in partnership with [[alec-marshall|Alec Marshall]], Brian functions in the novel as a voice of steady masculine rationality and, in one crucial passage, as the novel's most penetrating analyst of the psychological effects of racial consciousness on personal relationships.

## Character and Appearance

[[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] introduces Brian physically as "a tall dark boy with fine proud features that looked chiseled." He is first seen at the Marshall household during one of the Friday and Saturday night dances, arriving predictably at [[sylvia-marshall|Sylvia]]'s side: "Brian Spencer came in, who even in those days was pretty sure to be where [[sylvia-marshall|Sylvia]] was." This loyalty and predictability define Brian's character throughout the novel. He is humorous and supportive, applauding [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s [[the-dance-in-there-is-confusion|dancing]] with genuine appreciation: "Can't you see it all just as plainly? Really, Jan, you ought to go on the stage as an impersonator, I don't believe you could be beat."

His relationship with [[sylvia-marshall|Sylvia]] is marked by steadiness rather than drama. [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]] observes their dynamic with envy: "When Brian comes to see [[sylvia-marshall|Sylvia]] she runs to meet him, puts her arms about his neck." Brian's devotion extends to patiently accompanying [[sylvia-marshall|Sylvia]] to church despite finding the experience excruciating: "[[sylvia-marshall|Sylvia]] because she rather liked to please her mother and because it amused her to have Brian Spencer, whom churchgoing bored to the point of agony, obey her wish that he should go."

## Profession and Marriage

Brian partners with [[alec-marshall|Alec Marshall]] in the real estate business, participating in the economic development of Harlem's emerging black middle class. His professional prudence shapes his personal life: after his engagement to [[sylvia-marshall|Sylvia]], "Brian insisted on getting a good start before he married. [[sylvia-marshall|Sylvia]] would have married him the day after they became engaged. But I think Brian's right," [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] reflects. They eventually marry at Easter, and at Mrs. Marshall's insistence, the couple lives with the [[family-in-there-is-confusion|Marshall family]], further integrating Brian into the household.

## Role as Racial Analyst

Brian performs his most significant narrative function halfway through the novel, when he delivers the book's most explicit diagnosis of [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]]'s psychological paralysis — the "complex of color" that causes Peter to break with [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] and drift toward [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie Ellersley]]. Brian, unusually for a character in this novel, articulates a structural and racial explanation for individual behavior:

"Peter had a lot of natural handicaps, he was poor, he had no sense of responsibility, he was never too fond of work unless he had someone to spur him on to it. In addition to that he falls in [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|love]] with a girl who has everything in the world which he lacks, especially comparative ease and overwhelming ambition."

But Brian goes further, identifying the racial dimension that personal psychology alone cannot explain:

"Of course, what [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] doesn't realize is that she's up against the complex of color in Peter's life. It comes to every colored man and every colored woman, too, who has any ambition. Jan will feel it herself one day. Peter's got it worse than most of us because he's got such a terrible 'mad' on white people to start with. But every colored man feels it sooner or later. It gets in the way of his dreams, of his education, of his [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]], of the rearing of his children. The time comes when he thinks, 'I might just as well fall back; there's no use pushing on. A colored man just can't make any headway in this awful country.' Of course, it's a fallacy. And if a fellow sticks it out he finally gets past it, but not before it has worked considerable confusion in his life."

This passage — from which the novel's title is directly drawn — places Brian in the unusual position of being the character who names the book's central thematic concern.

## Later Life

Brian and [[sylvia-marshall|Sylvia]]'s [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]] produces at least two children, including a son named Roger, who grows to be "a tall lieutenant" during the war years. Their domestic life is characterized by [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] (now reflecting on her own turbulent romantic history) as "such a simple, easy, jog-trot time" — a description that carries both mild envy and recognition that Brian's steadiness, unlike Peter's volatility, produces a reliable foundation for family happiness.

## Critical Significance

Brian Spencer represents the novel's model of functional African American masculinity: educated, professionally established, emotionally available, and capable of analyzing the social structures that shape individual lives. His willingness to embrace [[sylvia-marshall|Sylvia]]'s family as his own — living in the Marshall household, deferring [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]] until financially ready, acting as surrogate brother to [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] — marks him as the most thoroughly assimilated male figure in the novel's extended family. His "simple, easy, jog-trot" path with [[sylvia-marshall|Sylvia]] serves as a normative baseline against which the novel measures the exceptional turbulence of the relationships between [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] and Peter and between [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] and [[philip-marshall|Philip]]. In a novel that foregrounds ambition and drama, Brian is the character who demonstrates that stability, intelligence, and quiet devotion constitute a form of heroism in their own right.
