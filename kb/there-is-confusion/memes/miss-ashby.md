# Miss Ashby

Miss Ashby is a minor antagonist in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *There Is Confusion*, functioning as a narrative foil to [[joanna-marshall]] within the theater-world episodes of the novel's second half. As the acknowledged star of the Broadway production "America" — the show that launches [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s career — Miss Ashby personifies the racial hierarchy of the entertainment industry, where white performers enjoy contractual and symbolic precedence even when Black talent drives the show's commercial success.

## Role in "America"

Miss Ashby performs two roles in the patriotic dance spectacle "America": an Indian and the symbolic figure of America itself. These roles, particularly the latter, carry symbolic weight — "America" is supposed to come on last as "a regal, symbolic figure." The casting of a white woman in this role is itself a racial statement about who can represent the nation on stage:

> Sylvia had a suggestion here. "America" was supposed to come on last as a regal, symbolic figure, but Miss Ashby had paid more attention to the dancing than to the symbolism. [033-501]

Miss Ashby's tenure as the star of "America" predates [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s arrival. When [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] is hired by [[mr-hale]] in May, the arrangement is explicitly hierarchical: [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] becomes "the great attraction, although not the acknowledged star. Miss Ashby, the girl who danced as an Indian and as an American, was that." This distinction — between being the audience's favorite and being the contracted star — encapsulates the racial economy that [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] anatomizes throughout the novel.

## Jealousy and Resentment

From the moment [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] joins the production, Miss Ashby resents the colored dancer's success. Her hostility is described as professional rather than explicitly racial, though the two categories are, in the world of the novel, inseparable:

> From the first she had resented the colored girl's success and had held jealously to all her rights and privileges. But the public, surprisingly loyal to this new and original plaything, never varied in the expression of its enjoyment of Joanna. [033-009]

The word "plaything" — presumably reflecting the narrator's ironic ventriloquism of the theater establishment's perspective — captures the commodification at the heart of the situation. The public treats [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] as a novelty, and Miss Ashby resents being displaced by a novelty act. That [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] is in fact a serious artist, not a novelty, is precisely what Miss Ashby cannot acknowledge without undermining her own sense of entitlement to the starring role.

## Departure from the Show

When [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s contract comes up for renewal, Miss Ashby seizes the opportunity to force a confrontation. As [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s popularity continues to grow and the show's success becomes increasingly dependent on her drawing power, Miss Ashby announces her departure — a gambit that appears calculated to force the producers to choose between the two women:

> Now that her changed contract was again about to expire, Miss Ashby announced her inability to remain with the play. [033-011]

Miss Ashby's departure is framed as a principled stand: "I've really been violating my principles in staying this long," she tells [[mr-hale|Mr. Hale]] "with meaning." The meaning, left unspoken, is clear: performing alongside a colored woman, and being eclipsed by her in the eyes of the public, is a violation of the racial order that Miss Ashby inhabits and enforces.

Her exit clears the way for [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s elevation, and the very next week [[mr-hale|Mr. Hale]] moves the production to Broadway with [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] as its undisputed attraction. Miss Ashby's attempt to reassert racial hierarchy thus backfires: rather than preserving her privileged position, it accelerates the colored dancer's ascent.

## Critical Significance

Miss Ashby is a minor character whose narrative function is primarily structural: she represents the barrier that [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] must overcome, and her departure marks one of the novel's key turning points. [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] does not invest her with the psychological depth she grants to characters like [[vera-manning]] or [[maggie-ellersley]] — Miss Ashby is, deliberately, a type rather than a person, the white rival whose professional jealousy is indistinguishable from racial entitlement.

Yet even this relatively flat characterization serves [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]]'s broader artistic project. By making Miss Ashby's resentment professional rather than explicitly bigoted, [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] demonstrates how racial hierarchy operates not through overt hatred but through the ordinary mechanisms of contracts, billing, and "rights and privileges." Miss Ashby never utters a racial epithet; she simply insists on the prerogatives that the system has always guaranteed her — and in doing so, reveals the system's workings more clearly than any explicit racism could.

The contrast between Miss Ashby's reliance on contractual privilege and [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s reliance on talent forms one of the novel's many variations on the theme of earned versus unearned advantage. Miss Ashby, [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] implies, is not a bad dancer, but she is a lazy one — she has "paid more attention to the [[the-dance-in-there-is-confusion|dancing]] than to the symbolism" — while [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] has trained with relentless discipline. The racial economy of the theater protects the former and exploits the latter, and it is only through the intervention of market forces (the public's enthusiasm for [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]) that this economy is temporarily disrupted.
