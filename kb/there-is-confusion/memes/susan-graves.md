# Susan Graves

[[miss-susan-graves|Miss Susan Graves]] is a supporting character in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *There Is Confusion*. She belongs to the Graves family of Gravestown, New Jersey — "a clan well known to colored people not only in that vicinity, but also throughout Pennsylvania" (005-007). Her role in the novel is that of a guardian figure to [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]], providing him with family tradition, stability, and a sense of lineage that his father Meriwether could not offer.

## Family and Background

Susan Graves was a member of the Graves family of Gravestown, New Jersey, a distinguished clan within African American society. Her family pride was so intense that she remained unmarried — not from lack of opportunity, but because "in her day there were not enough male Graves to go around. She would as soon have thought of marrying outside her family as a Spanish grandee would have thought of marrying an English cockney" (005-008).

Despite three offers of [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]], Susan "soared on family pride above all this and made her career that of housekeeper for the family of a wealthy merchant on Girard Avenue, in [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia" (005-008). As the narrator dryly notes: "You must marry a Graves, but obviously you obtained work where you could find it" (005-008).

## Guardianship of Peter Bye

When [[meriwether-bye|Meriwether Bye]] died, Susan Graves took in his young son Peter. The narrator observes that "the dying of [[meriwether-bye|Meriwether Bye]] was about the best thing he could have done for his son, Peter. Certainly that was what [[miss-susan-graves|Miss Susan Graves]] thought" (005-004). She viewed Peter's meager inheritance — including the Bye Family Bible, a small testament, some worn clothing, and a box of letters — with approval. Though she would have preferred that Meriwether had left money, "of the two she would very much rather have had the Bible with its absolute assurance of the former standing and respectability of the black Byes" (005-007).

Susan's plan for Peter was clear: she intended to counteract what she saw as his father's "doubtless harmful influence" (005-010). She was, as the narrator describes her, "not as direct in purpose as Susan" — a woman of firm convictions and practical methods.

## Relationship with the Marshall Family

Initially, Susan resisted Peter's growing attachment to the Marshall household. "She did not choose to have him associate too much with people whom she did not know. It was no part of her plan for Peter to retrograde into the wreck which Meriwether had become" (007-012). She made a deliberate visit to meet Mrs. Marshall at a church affair to assess the family.

Her visit impressed her. "[[miss-susan-graves|Miss Susan]], like Belle Bye nearly a century ago, recognized atmosphere when she saw it" (007-016). She approved of [[joel-marshall|Joel Marshall]]'s success, his pride, "his air of being somebody," and "estimated rightly the correctness of the old-fashioned walnut furniture, the heavy curtains, the kidney table in the parlor, the massive silver service and good linen" (007-016). Satisfied that the Marshalls represented suitable company, she permitted Peter to continue his association with them.

## Character and Significance

Susan Graves represents the theme of family tradition and respectability that runs through *There Is Confusion*. Her fierce attachment to the Graves name mirrors the [[family-in-there-is-confusion|Bye family]]'s own investment in lineage and legacy. Through her guardianship of Peter, she provides a bridge between the [[family-in-there-is-confusion|Bye family]] history and the [[family-in-there-is-confusion|Marshall family]]'s present, ensuring that the values of the past are transmitted to the next generation.
