# Washington, D.C.

**Washington, D.C.** appears in *There Is Confusion* as a foil to [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]] — a city of fashion-conscious strivers whose values differ from Philadelphia's family-centered Black elite. It also represents the geographical horizon of the novel's characters' aspirations, a city where Maggie Ellersley briefly imagines making a new life.

## A Contrast with Philadelphia

Fauset draws the distinction explicitly through a social gathering scene:

> "Philadelphians were not as a rule as strikingly dressed as the folks, say, from Washington, but they had a better time. They seemed bound by some kind of tie, family, perhaps — which made them one regardless of clothes or accomplishments."

This is one of the novel's sharpest sociological observations: Washington invests in *appearance*, Philadelphia in *connection*. The Washington set are "strikingly dressed" — they have mastered the external signs of status. But Philadelphians possess something deeper: "some kind of tie, family, perhaps," that makes their enjoyment genuine rather than performative.

## A Dream of Escape

Washington functions symbolically as a place of fresh starts. Maggie Ellersley, trapped in poverty and social marginalization in Philadelphia, fantasizes about leaving for a city where no one knows her history:

> "When I get through high school I'll come back and marry you. How'd you like to live in a little house — not like this! — in Washington?"

The "little house in Washington" — modest but *elsewhere* — represents the dream of self-reinvention that pervades the novel.

## The Tri-City Network

Washington is one node in a mid-Atlantic triangle that shaped Black middle-class life in the early twentieth century:

| City | Association |
|------|------------|
| **Philadelphia** | Old-family respectability, institutional depth |
| **Washington, D.C.** | Fashion, social striving, aspirational display |
| **Baltimore** | A quieter node, connected to both through family networks |

The characters move among these cities fluidly — for dances, concerts, visits, and courtship — forming what Fauset depicts as a coherent Black social world.

## Themes

| Theme | Expression |
|-------|-----------|
| **Class Signifiers** | Washington's emphasis on dress contrasts with Philadelphia's emphasis on lineage |
| **Geographical Mobility** | The D.C. dream represents the possibility — and limits — of self-reinvention |
