# Marriage in There Is Confusion

Marriage is the crucible in which [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]] tests her characters' deepest values. Across *There Is Confusion*, the question "whom should I marry — and why?" functions as a proxy for larger questions about class aspiration, racial solidarity, gender roles, and the meaning of a well-lived life.

## The Three Couples

Fauset structures the novel around three contrasted marriages and near-marriages:

| Couple | Dynamic | Outcome |
|--------|---------|---------|
| **Joanna Marshall & Peter Bye** | Ambition meets drift; love deferred by mutual growth | Marriage after both achieve distinction |
| **Maggie Ellersley & Harry Portor** | Class anxiety drives a "practical" choice | Brief, disastrous marriage to a man beneath her |
| **Maggie Ellersley & Philip Murray** | Redemption through honest affection | A second-chance marriage built on acceptance |

Each pair illuminates a different dimension of what marriage *means* — and what happens when the wrong reasons prevail.

## Marriage as Ambition

[[joanna-marshall|Joanna Marshall]] refuses to marry a man who has not yet become what she believes he can be. Her love for Peter is real, but it is conditional:

> "Both of us know it, Peter. And it's up to us, to you and me, Peter Bye, to show them we can stick to our last as well as anybody else."

For Joanna, marriage is not an escape from ambition but its culmination. She will not "settle" — a word the novel treats as the death of the soul. Her eventual marriage to Peter, after he has become a distinguished surgeon and she a celebrated performer, represents the novel's ideal: two fully realized individuals choosing each other from strength, not need.

## Marriage as Escape

[[maggie-ellersley|Maggie Ellersley]]'s story is the novel's cautionary tale. Trapped in poverty and social marginalization, she marries Harry Portor for the wrong reason:

> "What was marriage for if not for a change?"

The Portor marriage — brief, miserable, and ending in divorce — dramatizes the danger of treating marriage as a vehicle for social mobility. Maggie's redemption comes through her second marriage to Philip Murray, a union based not on escape but on mutual recognition and respect.

## The Bye Family Marriage History

The novel embeds marriage in a multi-generational frame. The marriages of [[isaiah-bye|Isaiah Bye]] to Belle Potter, of [[meriwether-bye|Meriwether Bye]] to Alice Graves, and finally of Peter to Joanna form a lineage in which each generation must reckon with the choices of its predecessors:

> "a tradition that had become warped, that had gone astray and had carried Peter and Peter Bye's father along in its general wreckage."

Peter's marriage to Joanna is the point at which the "warped" tradition is set right.

## Marriage and Race

Marriage in Fauset's novel is never separable from race. When Maggie briefly considers a white suitor, the possibility is presented not as romance but as racial betrayal. The novel's ideal marriages are *intra*racial but *inter*class — unions that strengthen the Black community by bridging its internal divisions rather than crossing the color line.

## Themes

| Theme | Expression |
|-------|-----------|
| **Love vs. Practicality** | The novel punishes marriages of convenience and rewards marriages of genuine affection |
| **Gender and Ambition** | Joanna's refusal to sacrifice her career for marriage was radical for 1924 |
| **Class Within Race** | Marriage choices reveal and reinforce class divisions within Black communities |
| **Redemption** | Maggie's second marriage offers the novel's clearest vision of grace |
