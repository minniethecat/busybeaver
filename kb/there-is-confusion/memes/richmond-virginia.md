# Richmond, Virginia

Richmond, Virginia serves as the geographical origin of the Marshall family in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s *[[ch005-there-is-confusion|There Is Confusion]]* (1924). It represents the antebellum and Reconstruction South — the landscape of slavery, emancipation, and initial economic struggle from which the Marshall family rises through migration to New York.

## Historical Context

At the time of [[joel-marshall|Joel Marshall]]'s childhood, Richmond was the former capital of the Confederacy, a city deeply marked by the Civil War and the collapse of the slave economy. The novel's references to Richmond situate the Marshall family within this specific historical geography, where the possibilities for African American advancement were limited by the persistence of racial hierarchy even after Emancipation.

## Joel Marshall's Richmond

Joel Marshall was "born a slave and the son of a slave in Richmond, Virginia" (001-003). His childhood unfolded in "the tiny half-story room in that tiny house in Virginia," where he read about Napoleon, Lincoln, and Garrison while dreaming of a greatness that seemed incompatible with his circumstances.

The Richmond of Joel's youth is a city of constrained possibility. After his father's death and the destruction of the family home by fire, fifteen-year-old Joel and his mother entered domestic service for Harvey Carter, "a wealthy Virginian, whose wife entertained on a large scale" (001-004). The trajectory from slavery to service within the same city illustrates how Emancipation did not immediately transform social relations — the same families who had owned enslaved people now employed their former property as servants.

## The Catering Business

Joel's entrepreneurial rise began in Richmond, where he opened "a tiny two and a half room shack in the front room of which he opened a restaurant — really a little lunchstand" (001-004). His clientele grew from fellow African Americans to white customers to outside orders. "In five years' time Joel's catering became known statewide" (001-004).

The catering business represents a form of economic self-determination achievable within the segregated South — a niche that leveraged domestic skills (cooking) into commercial success. [[grant-banquet|The Grant banquet]] — catering for President Ulysses S. Grant during a Richmond visit — provided the reputation that enabled Joel's move north.

## Symbolic Function

Richmond functions in the novel as the site of origin and constraint, contrasted with the possibility represented by New York. It is where Joel's father was destroyed by slavery, where the family home burned, where social structure limited ambition to economic rather than intellectual achievement. The move from Richmond to New York symbolizes the generational arc of African American migration: from the rural and small-city South to the urban North, from the shadow of slavery to the uncertain promise of freedom.
