# Racial Discrimination in There Is Confusion

Racial discrimination is the ambient condition against which all the aspirations and achievements of the characters in *There Is Confusion* must be measured. Fauset depicts discrimination not as spectacular violence but as a pervasive, often petty system of exclusions that shapes professional trajectories, social possibilities, and psychological experience. Her approach is characteristically restrained: discrimination is rendered through accumulated detail rather than melodramatic confrontation, making its presence all the more insidious.

## Early Encounters with Discrimination

The novel traces how awareness of racial discrimination begins early in life. Joanna Marshall's childhood is marked by a formative encounter with racial difference:

> It was not until Joanna went to school and learned something of discrimination that she began to ponder. [001-027]

This moment of recognition — the dawning awareness that the world treats people differently based on race — is presented not as a single traumatic event but as a gradual accretion of knowledge that reshapes a child's understanding of her place in the world. The verb "ponder" captures Fauset's characteristic restraint: discrimination provokes thought and strategy, not merely pain. Joanna does not weep or rage; she ponders, begins the intellectual labor of understanding a society organized around her exclusion.

## Navigating Public Space

The novel's characters develop sophisticated maps of where they will encounter "the least discrimination." The narrator notes with matter-of-fact precision:

> Once Maggie took Annie to the theater. They sat where they met with the least discrimination. [014-042]

This practical knowledge — which theaters, which neighborhoods, which establishments offered relative safety — was an essential form of cultural competence for African Americans in the early twentieth century. The phrase "the least discrimination" is devastating in its precision: it acknowledges that no space was entirely free of discrimination; the question was always one of degree. The sentence's offhand tone — "once" Maggie took Annie — suggests how routine such calculations were.

The cumulative effect of these navigations is captured in a single compressed observation about one character's experience abroad:

> She thought of Frenchwomen and the lack of discrimination in the French cafés. [030-003]

The contrast between American racial regime and European treatment — a recurring theme in African American expatriate writing — registers here as a brief, almost offhand observation whose very brevity underscores how thoroughly discrimination structured daily American life. The freedom of a French café, unremarkable to those who possess it by default, appears to the novel's African American characters as a revelation.

## The Weight of Petty Discrimination

Beyond the structural exclusions of segregation, Fauset attends to the psychological weight of discrimination's minor forms. The narrator describes one character's experience:

> His enthusiasm was dissipated by galling if petty discriminations. [024-005]

The pairing of "galling" and "petty" is central to Fauset's method. These discriminations are minor enough to be dismissed individually — a cold glance, an unserved customer, a seat in the back — yet collectively "galling," wearing down the spirit through relentless accumulation. The novel's attention to these moments reflects its broader project: documenting the interior experience of African American life under Jim Crow, not only its dramatic confrontations but its daily erosions.

## Discrimination and Double Consciousness

The novel's treatment of discrimination is inseparable from the experience of double consciousness — the awareness of seeing oneself through the eyes of a hostile or indifferent white world. Characters in the novel are constantly engaged in acts of self-presentation that anticipate and attempt to manage others' prejudiced perceptions. This psychological labor — the burden of representing one's race through individual conduct — is itself a form of discrimination's cost, exacted invisibly from every encounter.
