# Aaron Bye

Aaron Bye is a foundational figure in the genealogy that opens [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *There Is Confusion*. A white Quaker slaveholder in late eighteenth-century [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia, he and his wife Dinah Bye freed their slaves in 1780 under Pennsylvania's gradual abolition law, setting in motion the intertwined histories of the white and black Bye families that span the novel's multi-generational prologue and shape the identity of its protagonist, [[peter-bye]].

## Historical Role

Aaron Bye occupies a pivotal position in the novel's opening genealogy, which traces the [[family-in-there-is-confusion|Bye family]] from the last decades of the eighteenth century through the early twentieth. The narrator establishes the [[family-in-there-is-confusion|Bye family]]'s dual racial lineage with precision:

> As far back as the last decades of the eighteenth century there had been white Byes and black Byes in Philadelphia. The black Byes were known to be the chattels of Aaron and Dinah Bye, Quakers, who without reluctance had set free their slaves, among them black Joshua Bye, the great-grandfather of Peter. This was done in 1780 according to the laws of Pennsylvania, which thus allowed the Quakers to salve their consciences without offending their thrifty instincts.

The narrator's tone — "allowed the Quakers to salve their consciences without offending their thrifty instincts" — introduces the novel's skeptical approach to white benevolence. Aaron Bye's emancipation of his slaves is portrayed as a gesture that costs him nothing and preserves his social position while perpetuating an economic arrangement that keeps the black Byes in subordinate roles for generations.

## Treatment of the Black Byes

Aaron Bye is described as "unusually good to his slaves," possessing "something of the patriarchal instinct" and liking "to think of himself as ruler over the destiny of many people, his wife's, his children's and more completely that of his slaves." This characterization carries [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]]'s characteristic irony: Aaron's "goodness" is inseparable from his need for control.

His kindness to [[joshua-bye|Joshua Bye]] — teaching him to write, giving him a black testament inscribed "The gift of Aaron Bye" — coexists with a system that denies [[joshua-bye|Joshua]] genuine autonomy. [[joshua-bye|Joshua]] spends his life as Aaron's agricultural laborer, developing "an uncanny knowledge of planting, of grafting, of fertilizing" that makes Aaron's peach orchards flourish while [[joshua-bye|Joshua]] himself receives only "small wages."

The power dynamic is crystallized in the contrast between [[joshua-bye|Joshua]]'s loyalty and Ceazer's defiance. Ceazer, [[judy-bye|Judy]]'s husband, "hated slavery, hated all white people, hated particularly the Mortons, hated ineffably Aaron Bye. He wanted nothing at his hands." When [[joshua-bye|Joshua]] writes "Ceazer Bye" in the testament and asks his father to make his mark, Ceazer draws "a thick broad line through his name with a fury which almost tore the thin page. *He* was no Bye!" Ceazer's rejection of the Bye name — and by extension, of Aaron Bye's paternalistic authority — stands as the novel's most forceful repudiation of the master-slave relationship.

## By Their Fruits

Aaron Bye's inscription in the Family Bible given to [[joshua-bye|Joshua]] and Belle Bye for their wedding — "By their fruits ye shall know them" — becomes a motif that echoes across the novel's generations. Aaron intends the phrase as a statement of Christian virtue, but [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] allows it to accrue irony: the "fruits" of Aaron Bye include not only peach orchards and prosperity but also a system of racial hierarchy that white Byes inherit and black Byes must struggle against.

Young [[meriwether-bye|Meriwether Bye]], Aaron's youngest son, explains the motto to [[isaiah-bye|Isaiah Bye]] with the "unconscious cruelty of youth":

> "When it comes to people, it means pretty much the same thing. Now when I grow up, I'm going to be a great doctor, but nobody will be surprised. They'll all say, 'Of course, he's the son of Aaron Bye, the rich peach-merchant. Good stock there,' and I'll be good fruit. That's the way it always is: good trees, good fruit; rich, important people, rich important sons."

When [[isaiah-bye|Isaiah]] asks what he will be, Meriwether answers without malice: "Your father and your father's father were slaves. ... So I s'pose that's what you'll be, a good servant." This conversation becomes the inciting trauma of [[isaiah-bye|Isaiah Bye]]'s life, driving him to open a school for colored youth, accumulate property, and name his own son [[meriwether-bye|Meriwether Bye]] — a deliberate appropriation of the white family's name and lineage.

## Legacy

Aaron Bye's legacy in the novel is the system of racial hierarchy his emancipation failed to disrupt. The white Byes continue to prosper — his son Meriwether becomes a doctor, his great-grandson another [[dr-meriwether-bye|Dr. Meriwether Bye]] who serves in World War I. The black Byes, despite [[isaiah-bye|Isaiah]]'s achievements, face the accumulated weight of discrimination: [[isaiah-bye|Isaiah]]'s son Meriwether squanders the family property through "sickness, gambling, and a hitherto unsuspected penchant for sheer laziness," leaving [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]] to grow up in poverty, conscious of his distinguished ancestry but with none of its material advantages.

Aaron Bye never appears as a living character in the novel's present-tense narrative; he exists only in the genealogical prologue. Yet his decisions — to free his slaves, to keep them economically dependent, to bestow his name and his Bible and his paternalistic approval — ripple through the generations, shaping the social world that [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]] must navigate as a young colored man in early twentieth-century America.
