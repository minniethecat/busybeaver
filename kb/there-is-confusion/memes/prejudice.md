# Racial Prejudice in There Is Confusion

Racial prejudice is the shaping force against which the characters of *There Is Confusion* define themselves. Unlike protest novels that center dramatic confrontations with racism, Fauset's treatment of prejudice is notable for its attention to the *internal* consequences of living within a system of racial subordination — the psychological accommodations, strategic withdrawals, and redirected ambitions that racial barriers produce.

## The Architecture of Exclusion

The novel depicts prejudice not as isolated acts of individual malice but as a comprehensive structure governing access to opportunity. Professional fields — medicine, music, business — are organized to exclude or marginalize African Americans regardless of individual merit. [[peter-bye]]'s medical career and [[joanna-marshall]]'s concert aspirations both run up against barriers that no amount of personal excellence can fully surmount.

The everyday texture of this exclusion is registered in scenes of refused service and denied access:

> She was the only other colored person in the room and there was no mistaking the unwillingness of the proprietor to serve her. (006-007)

Fauset's attention to the *institutional* character of prejudice distinguishes her novel from more sentimental treatments of race. The barriers Joanna and Peter face are embedded in professional organizations, educational institutions, and social networks — not simply in the prejudices of individual white people.

## Strategic Accommodation

The novel documents what might be called the *strategy of the "better class"*:

> Like many of the better class of colored people, the Marshalls did not meet with the grosser forms of color prejudice, because they kept away from the places where it might be shown. (007-023)

Fauset presents this as a complex and morally ambiguous accommodation. On one hand, strategic withdrawal from hostile spaces protects dignity and enables flourishing within the protected sphere of family, church, and community. On the other hand, Fauset acknowledges this strategy is "bad from the standpoint of development of civic pride and interest" — a recognition that self-protection comes at the cost of full citizenship.

## Psychological Costs

*There Is Confusion* is particularly attentive to prejudice's psychological toll. Characters must manage not only external barriers but internal doubts — about their worth, their entitlement to ambition, their place in American society. [[joel-marshall]]'s thwarted greatness, [[joanna-marshall]]'s anxious striving, and [[peter-bye]]'s oscillation between resentment and aspiration all register the interior costs of living under racial constraint.

The novel's title — drawn from a dance number — gestures toward this psychological dimension. The "confusion" is not only social but existential: the disorientation produced by living in a society that simultaneously demands excellence and denies its possibility.

## Prejudice and Class

Fauset complicates the treatment of prejudice by attending to its intersection with class. The Marshalls' relative economic comfort buffers them from some forms of discrimination while exposing them to others. Joanna's light skin — remarked upon throughout the novel — opens possibilities (including the possibility of [[racial-passing]]) that darker-skinned characters do not share, even as it intensifies certain forms of scrutiny.
