# Henderson Neal

Henderson Neal is a shadowy, morally ambiguous figure in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *There Is Confusion*. A boarder at [[mrs-ellersley|Mrs. Ellersley]]'s house and a professional gambler, he functions in the narrative as a force of chaos and menace in [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie Ellersley]]'s life — representing the "confusion" that the novel's title names.

## Physical Presence and Mystery

[[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] introduces Neal as "a mysterious tall brown figure of a man, twenty years older than [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] and a thousand years older in experience." He captures [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s attention precisely through his opacity: "he caught and not infrequently held her attention. He had lived with them two years, paid his bills regularly, asked no questions and vouchsafed no explanations." When [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] sees a white man on a train who shares his build, she is unsettled: "something about his build made her think of Henderson Neal, her mother's roomer."

His physicality is imposing — when he appears suddenly at the dining room door asking "Was you asleep or—was you crying?", his arrival is framed as an intrusion that demands a response. Later, in the novel's most violent passage, "an iron hand shot out of the darkness of the landing, caught her wrist in an agonizing vise" and [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] "looked up into the raging somber eyes of Henderson Neal."

## The Cardsharp

Neal's moral character is most explicitly delineated by an old man who testifies to his history:

"Even when I was a little boy, Henderson Neal was knowed to be a cardsharp. There wasn't nobody could stand against him. Used to wait for the men on a Saturday night, white and colored. He'd meet 'em in the bar and treat, and then ask 'em in on a little game. And they'd play, till they was cleaned out. Then he'd give 'em another drink, and clap 'em on the back. Perhaps he'd hand 'em back a dollar. 'Better luck next time old man!' And they'd come back the next Saturday night, the poor fools. Some of them blowed their brains out, they got so far back in their debts."

This characterization establishes Neal as predatory and remorseless — a man who has made his living exploiting the weakness of others.

## Marriage to Maggie Ellersley

[[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s decision to marry Neal is presented not as [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|love]] but as desperation. After [[joanna-marshall|Joanna Marshall]]'s interference destroys her relationship with [[philip-marshall|Philip Marshall]], [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] marries Neal in an act of self-destruction that mirrors Neal's victims returning to his card table. [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] recognizes her culpability: "she became more and more overwhelmed with the awfulness of that old unconsidered act of hers, the sending of the letter which had caused [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] to marry Henderson Neal."

[[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s own reflection is bitter: "[[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] had interfered, and then she had married Henderson Neal, a gambler, a *gambler* who had plunged her further back than ever into the obscurity from which she was beginning to emerge." She reflects on what she gave up: "Why, there had been [[philip-marshall|Philip]] once, and then Henderson Neal!—Whereas Peter had been the only [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|love]] of her own life."

The [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]] is marked by fear. [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] confesses to Peter: "I met Henderson, Henderson Neal, uptown Saturday—I didn't mean to tell you—but he glared at me. He made me shiver, I wished you were with me. I'm afraid of him, Peter, I'll never be safe till we're married."

## Death

Neal is killed in a street accident: "A Negro, later identified as Henderson Neal, was killed instantly this afternoon—" [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]], despite having feared him, experiences a nervous collapse: "I killed him," she moans to [[harry-portor|Harry Portor]]. "His death is as much due to me as though I had poisoned him. I did poison his life."

## Critical Significance

Henderson Neal functions in the novel as the embodiment of what the [[family-in-there-is-confusion|Marshall family]] most fears: the destruction that awaits those who lack the protections of education, class aspiration, and family solidarity. His presence in [[mrs-ellersley|Mrs. Ellersley]]'s boarding house is the shadow side of the Marshalls' upward mobility — he is what [[philip-marshall|Philip]] might have become without discipline, what Peter threatens to become through self-indulgence. [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] uses him to dramatize the stakes of the novel's moral economy: without ambition, family, and racial consciousness, the individual is vulnerable to predation by figures like Neal.
