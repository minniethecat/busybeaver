## Overview

World War I (referred to as "the Great War" in the novel) forms a critical historical backdrop in *[[ch005-there-is-confusion|There Is Confusion]]*. The war affects multiple generations of the Bye and Marshall families, from military service to the post-war transformation of African American communities in [[harlem|Harlem]] and [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]]. Fae [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]] uses the war to explore themes of patriotism, racial identity, and the gap between American ideals and the reality faced by Black citizens.

## Family Military Legacy

The novel traces a multi-generational tradition of military service among its African American characters, linking the American Revolution, the Civil War, and World War I as a continuous line of patriotic sacrifice. [[joanna-marshall|Joanna Marshall]] proudly declares her family's lineage:

> "My great-grandfather fought in the Revolution, my uncle fought in the Civil War and my brother is 'over there' now." [028-010]

Similarly, [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]] is reminded by his Aunt Susan of the Bye family's military heritage: "Your great-grandfather fought in the Revolution, Peter, and two of your uncles, my brothers, were in the Civil War. If you enlist you'll only be following their example" [025-026]. The Bye family paid a heavy price in the Civil War — [[meriwether-bye|Meriwether Bye]] recounts that of [[aaron-bye|Aaron Bye]]'s ten sons, all except his father "died in the Civil War" [029-033].

## Meriwether Bye's Sacrifice

Meriwether Bye articulates the most idealistic vision of the war as a redemptive cause. Despite being able to serve in the safer medical corps, he chooses the ranks, driven by a conviction that the war represents a historic opportunity:

> "Don't think I'm a fanatic, Peter. I see this war as the greatest gesture the world has ever made for Freedom. If I can give up my life in this cause I shall feel that I have paid my debt." [029-054]

## The Experience of Black Soldiers

The novel portrays the complicated, often disillusioning experience of African American soldiers in the war. After returning home, Peter describes being embittered by the treatment of Black soldiers:

> "He says the colored soldiers were all sold—fighting for freedom was a farce so far as they were concerned. But France is all right if the white Americans don't get in too much propaganda." [033-019]

The French town of Chambéry became known to thousands of Black Americans because "during the great War it was metamorphosed into a rest center for colored soldiers" [031-001], suggesting that Black soldiers found in France a degree of dignity denied them by their own countrymen.

## Post-War Harlem

The war's end brought celebration and transformation to Harlem:

> "The War was over, the men were coming home. All Harlem was delirious with excitement. Everything conceivable must be done for 'the boys,' for those boys who having fought a double battle in France, one with Germany and one with white America, had yet marvelously, incredibly, returned safely home." [033-001]

The returning soldiers represented all social classes, from Harvard graduates to Alabama farmhands who "had seen Paris before they had seen New York and they blessed the War which had given them a chance to see the great capital" [033-001]. This exposure to European culture, combined with the experience of fighting for democracy abroad while being denied full citizenship at home, contributed to the emerging spirit of the Harlem Renaissance.

## Post-War Racial Climate

The aftermath of the war brought complex changes to race relations. Philadelphia, in particular, "since the War, has changed for the worse in her attitude toward colored people" [034-065], reflecting the broader national pattern in which the hopes for expanded civil rights after Black military service went largely unfulfilled. This disillusionment forms part of the social fabric against which the novel's characters pursue their ambitions.
