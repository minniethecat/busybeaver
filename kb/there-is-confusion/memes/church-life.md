# Church Life in There Is Confusion

The African Methodist Episcopal (A.M.E.) Church serves as a central institutional presence in *There Is Confusion*, providing spiritual sustenance, community cohesion, and social infrastructure for the novel's African American characters. Fauset depicts the church not as a backdrop but as an active force shaping individual lives and collective identity.

## The A.M.E. Church as Institution

The A.M.E. Church, founded by Richard Allen in Philadelphia in 1816, was the first independent Protestant denomination organized by African Americans. By the early twentieth century — the period of *There Is Confusion* — the A.M.E. Church had grown into a national institution with churches, schools, and publishing operations across the country.

In the novel, the church functions as more than a place of worship. It is a site of professional networking, social validation, and community leadership. Joel Marshall's standing illustrates this institutional role:

> His success, his position in the church, in the community at large and his undoubted piety, earned him many friends among both white and colored people. (001-012)

Characters' standing within the church community serves as an index of their broader social position, reflecting Fauset's attention to the institutional architecture of African American middle-class life.

## Religious Community and Social Bonds

The church structures social relationships throughout the novel. It provides a context for courtship, friendship, and mutual support that operates parallel to and intertwined with the secular world of work and art. For characters navigating the double consciousness of African American identity in a segregated society, the church offers a space of full citizenship — a world within a world where dignity is assumed rather than negotiated.

The "better class" of colored people that Fauset depicts is in large part defined by its relationship to the church. Regular attendance, participation in church activities, and reputation within the congregation serve as markers of respectability that distinguish the Marshalls and their social circle.

## Faith and Resilience

Beyond its institutional functions, the novel registers genuine religious faith as a source of personal resilience. Characters facing racial discrimination, professional disappointment, and personal loss draw on religious conviction. The novel treats this faith with respect, neither dismissing it as mere social convention nor elevating it above other forms of meaning-making.

The church's role in sustaining African American communities through slavery, Reconstruction, and Jim Crow forms an implicit historical backdrop. By the early twentieth century, churches like the A.M.E. had accumulated generations of institutional knowledge about survival under hostile conditions — knowledge that Fauset's characters inherit and draw upon.
