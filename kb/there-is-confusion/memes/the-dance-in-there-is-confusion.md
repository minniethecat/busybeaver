# The Dance in There Is Confusion

Dance appears throughout *There Is Confusion* as an index of character, a site of social performance, and a metaphor for the grace under pressure that [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s protagonists must cultivate. Joanna Marshall's gift for dance is her first and most instinctive art — the foundation upon which her later ambitions are built.

## Joanna's Gift

Joanna's dancing is presented as innate, almost miraculous:

> "Joanna was like the spirit of dancing. She had grace, the very poetry of motion, and she could dance any step however intricate if she saw it once."

This is talent in its purest form — untaught, unrehearsed, a gift of the body that transcends training. Unlike her singing, which Joanna pursues through disciplined study, her dancing simply *is*. It represents a form of Black expressiveness that the novel values but also, problematically, must channel into socially acceptable forms.

## Dance and Social Exclusion

Joanna's relationship to dance also reveals her social isolation as a child. Her schoolmates recognize her gift but keep their distance:

> "She wouldn't play 'I Spy' or 'Pussy wants a corner' with you for worlds."

Dancing makes Joanna extraordinary; her extraordinariness makes her solitary. This pattern — talent that elevates but also isolates — recurs throughout Joanna's story.

## The Social Dance

Beyond Joanna's individual gift, dance functions in the novel as the central ritual of Black middle-class social life. Dances, balls, and benefit concerts are where couples meet, rivalries play out, and social hierarchies are enacted and challenged. The dance floor is where class, color, and courtship converge.

For the characters who gather from Philadelphia, Washington, and Baltimore, the dance is simultaneously entertainment, courtship market, and community affirmation. Fauset treats these occasions with ethnographic precision — what people wear, how they dance, who dances with whom — as data about social structure.

## Dance as Resistance

Joanna's own relationship to dance carries a political dimension. When she declares "I dance, too... Not ordinary dancing, you know, but interpretative dancing," she is claiming an art form that had historically been racialized and devalued. To dance *as art* rather than *as entertainment* is to refuse a stereotype.

## Themes

| Theme | Expression |
|-------|-----------|
| **Innate Gift** | Joanna's dancing represents talent that precedes and exceeds training |
| **Social Ritual** | Dances are the primary sites of courtship and community performance |
| **Art vs. Entertainment** | Joanna's insistence on "interpretative" dancing claims artistic seriousness |
| **Isolation of Talent** | Giftedness separates Joanna from her peers even as it defines her |
