# Philip Marshall

Philip Marshall is one of [[joanna-marshall|Joanna Marshall]]'s two older brothers in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *There Is Confusion*. Unlike his more conventional brother [[alec-marshall|Alec]], Philip is intellectually serious from adolescence, studying at Harvard University and aspiring to leadership of his race. His romantic relationship with [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie Ellersley]] — marked by years of miscommunication, missed opportunity, and eventual reconciliation — constitutes one of the novel's most emotionally complex narrative threads.

## Early Life and Intellectual Development

Philip is the third child of [[joel-marshall|Joel]] and Mrs. Marshall. [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] notes that he "resembled" [[alec-marshall|Alec]] and [[sylvia-marshall|Sylvia]] in being less obsessively driven than [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]], yet he is distinguished from his elder brother by genuine intellectual depth. From late adolescence, Philip develops an extraordinary capacity for political and social reflection. [[brian-spencer|Brian Spencer]] observes early that "Philip was the only one who had evinced any interest in the business" of his father's catering — an interest driven not by vocational inclination but by a desire to contribute to the family's financial stability while pursuing his studies.

He attends Harvard University, where he achieves election to Phi Beta Kappa. During summer vacations, his conversations with [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] reveal the scope of his ambition: ""When I graduate — by the way, I think I'll be elected Phi Beta Kappa next year — I've got a scheme, I've got a plan that will work all right. Father will be proud of me, you'll see. And you, too, old girl, you've always been a bright beacon light. You stick to this stage business, you'll win out. There'll be a twin star constellation. 'The well known Marshalls, [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] and Philip.' We'll make the whole world realize what colored people can do.""

## Political Consciousness and Race Leadership

Philip's most distinctive characteristic is his systematic interest in race leadership and mass psychology. During church services, while others follow the liturgy, Philip studies the congregation: "He had a strong desire to sense the social consciousness, for he was trying to learn just what stirred mass feeling and into what channels it could be directed. A minister of the poorest type was an unfailing source of study to him. How would this man sway the people? And what would he ask of them once he had them ready to listen to his will? Philip always dreamed of a leader who should recognize that psychological moment and who would guide a whole race forward to the realization of its steadily increasing strength."

[[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] describes him as having "the somewhat remote glance of the thinker, though his firm chin pronounced him no less the man of action." His lean, brown, slightly haggard face, bent forward in concentration, becomes a familiar image to [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie Ellersley]], who watches him covertly from across the church and reflects that "he looked like a man who would take what he wanted."

His political orientation includes journalism; he eventually edits a magazine, and his friend Barney Kirchner becomes a newspaper manager. His circle includes politically conscious figures who understand the structural obstacles facing African Americans.

## Relationship with Maggie Ellersley

### Early Courtship

Philip's relationship with [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie Ellersley]] is the most protracted and emotionally fraught romance in the novel. They grow close during [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s integration into the Marshall household, where she frequently accompanies Philip on his catering assignments. "He was usually jolly, unsentimental, almost brotherly. [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] took care to follow his lead. But to her great surprise she was beginning to be conscious of a deep affection for him."

Their walks home through quiet streets generate "a curious little sense of intimacy," with Philip laughing "a little nervously" — a shyness that, combined with his intellectual preoccupations, prevents him from recognizing either his own feelings or [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s. [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s internal monologue during this period is heart-wrenching: "If you'd only say one word, Philip. I'd wait forever."

### Missed Connection and the Letter

The turning point comes through [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s well-intentioned but disastrous intervention. Upon discovering [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s engagement to [[henderson-neal|Henderson Neal]], [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] is outraged on Philip's behalf — "Why, Philip — he's going to be somebody great, wonderful, a Garibaldi, a Toussaint! And [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]], [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s just nobody." Her cruel letter to [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]], combined with Philip's own "rather lackadaisical [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|courtship]]," ensures the two miss each other for years. Philip, too slow to realize "until too late what she meant to him," never inquires about [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s divorce and assumes her first [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]] is final.

### War, Reunion, and Marriage

In Des Moines during the war, Philip encounters [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]] and delivers a brutal judgment: "I don't shake hands with any such damned light of [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|love]]" — a remark that haunts Peter and confuses his relationship with both women. Philip's bitterness is fed by ignorance; he does not know of [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s divorce and considers her married to [[henderson-neal|Neal]].

Years later, wounded and recovering in Chambéry, France, Philip is found by [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]], now a Red Cross worker. Their reunion in the chapel of the old Dukes of Savoy becomes the novel's most romantic scene: "For Philip had risen and drawn [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] to him. 'You really mean it, [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]], my [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]! Oh, my little yellow flower, I'll never let you go.'" They marry, and [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] nurses him through his final months.

### Death and Legacy

Philip dies of his war wounds shortly after returning to New York with [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]. His death is "the first serious rent in the fabric of the [[family-in-there-is-confusion|Marshall family]]," precipitating [[joel-marshall|Joel]]'s long reveries, Mrs. Marshall's ritualistic handling of her son's baby clothes, and [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]]'s guilt-ridden confession attempt. Yet Philip's final days are spent in "complete and unbounded satisfaction" in [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s presence — a man who at last found what he had so long deferred. As [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] reflects, "It was what he always wanted to do" — serve his country — though she knows "Philip's real desire envisaged *living* for his country — to save her from something worse than war."

## Critical Significance

Philip Marshall represents the novel's vision of the African American intellectual as race leader — the figure who, through education, political consciousness, and moral seriousness, can guide his people forward. His tragedy is not that he fails in this mission but that he dies before fully realizing it, a casualty of a war fought for a country that denies his full citizenship. His relationship with [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] — two people who [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|love]] each other deeply but spend years missing each other — functions as a parable about the costs of class ambition, poor communication, and social circumstance in African American romantic life. His Phi Beta Kappa intellect, his editorial ambitions, and his deathbed reconciliation with [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] together sketch the arc of a man for whom personal and political fulfillment are tragically interdependent.
