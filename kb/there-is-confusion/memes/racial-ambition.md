# Racial Ambition in There Is Confusion

Racial ambition — the drive for personal greatness pursued against the structural constraints of race — is the central thematic concern of [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *[[ch005-there-is-confusion|There Is Confusion]]*. The theme operates across generations, linking the post-Emancipation struggles of [[joel-marshall|Joel Marshall]] with the artistic aspirations of his daughter [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]].

## Generational Transmission

The novel establishes racial ambition as an inheritance passed from father to daughter. Joel Marshall, born a slave in [[richmond-virginia|Richmond]], felt as a boy "that same impulse to greatness" and studied figures like Napoleon, Lincoln, Frederick Douglass, and Toussaint L'Ouverture. He "knew if he could but burst his bonds he, too, could write his name in glory" (001-003).

This ambition, thwarted in Joel's own life by poverty, family obligation, and lack of formal education, finds its vessel in Joanna. The novel describes her as "a little, living echo out of his own forgotten past" (001-003), making explicit that ambition is not merely personal but genealogical — passed down, refined, and redirected across generations.

## The Historical Imagination

Joanna's ambition is distinguished by its sophisticated historical consciousness. She studies African American heroes — Douglass, Vesey, Turner, Tubman, Wheatley, Truth — and develops a comparative framework for understanding their achievements. The novel notes she understood "that the bravery and courage exercised by these slave women was a much finer and different thing from that exercised for instance by Florence Nightingale" (001-011).

This historical awareness shapes Joanna's self-conception. Her identification with Joan of Arc — "Joan, wonderful Joan with the name almost like mine" — signals her understanding of herself as part of a lineage of exceptional women who transcended circumstance. Yet Fauset's Joanna restrains herself from adopting the shorter name, held back by "an innate, almost too meticulous sense of honesty" (001-011). This detail reveals the tension at the heart of the novel's treatment of ambition: the desire for greatness held in check by moral scrupulousness.

## Material Foundations

Fauset grounds ambition in concrete economic reality. Joel's catering success — moving from a "tiny two and a half room shack" to a comfortable [[fifty-ninth-street|Fifty-Ninth Street]] residence — provides the material basis for Joanna's artistic pursuits. The novel emphasizes Joel's careful financial management: "he used to murmur over his books that daily showed an increase in his earnings" (001-011). Ambition, in Fauset's telling, requires not only drive but infrastructure.

This grounding distinguishes *There Is Confusion* from more romantic treatments of artistic aspiration. Fauset, a middle-class African American intellectual herself, insists that greatness is built on the unglamorous foundation of economic stability and parental sacrifice.

## Critical Context

Within the [[harlem|Harlem]] Renaissance, Fauset's treatment of racial ambition occupies a distinctive position. Unlike works that centered racial protest or folk authenticity, *There Is Confusion* explores the psychology of striving — the interior experience of African Americans who pursued professional and artistic excellence as a form of racial advancement. Joanna's story asks whether individual achievement can serve as collective progress, a question that remains resonant in ongoing debates about representation and racial justice.
