# Tom Mason

Tom Mason is a [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia violinist and orchestra leader in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *There Is Confusion*, whose partnership with [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]] provides the latter with financial independence while simultaneously threatening his higher aspirations of becoming a surgeon.

## The Lucrative Partnership

Tom Mason is introduced through the enthusiastic endorsement of a mutual acquaintance: "Yes. It's Tom Mason, the man I told you about. The very thing for you! He makes barrels of money. Come on, Peter." The recommendation — grounded in money, not artistry — foreshadows the nature of the work Peter will do for Tom: functional, remunerative, and ultimately unsatisfying to a man of Peter's ambitions.

The financial rewards are real. When [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] visits Peter in New York, his appearance testifies to his new prosperity: "Peter was superlative. His financial success with Tom Mason had made it possible for him to indulge in a new outfit which emphasized the distinction of his carriage, set off his handsome face. Several people looked at him on the crowded street." Yet the scene's deeper register is ironic: Peter's success with Tom is precisely what enables him to *look* the part of the man [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] might marry, even as it leads him away from the career that would win her respect.

## The Temptation of Comfort

Peter's own justification for remaining in Tom's orbit reveals the seductive logic of easy money: "Why shouldn't I? There're plenty of pleasanter ways to earn a living. Why should I take any more of their selfish dog-in-the-manger foolishness? I can make all the money I want with Tom Mason. If you aren't satisfied for me to be an accompanist, I could go into partnership with him and we could form and place orchestras."

This speech — delivered to [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] — articulates the novel's central conflict between ambition and accommodation. Peter's phrase "since the world owes me a living" carries the bitterness of a man who has experienced racial exclusion and decided that conventional striving is a fool's game. Tom Mason represents the alternative: a livelihood earned outside the white professional structure, lucrative but without prestige.

## The Break

The arrangement cannot last. Ten months into the partnership, the novel marks the passage of time with an image of Tom at ease: "Ten months later Tom Mason leaned back against the red plush of the car seat and jingled some coins in his pocket." The gesture — casual, proprietary, satisfied — suggests that Tom has found his level and is content.

Peter cannot match this contentment. His break with Tom comes in a moment of revulsion against the work's social dimensions: "You know Mason, Tom Mason on Fifteenth Street? I've been playing for him. But I can't stick it any longer. Tom's all right, but I can't stand his customers. Besides, I've got to get back to work. I'm quitting this minute — see."

Peter announces his decision to [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] with relief: "I've chucked all this foolishness with Tom Mason. My, I bet he's cursing mad. I'm getting down to brass tacks; went back to my classes this morning." [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]], who had her own plans for managing Peter away from Tom's influence, is relieved — though for her own reasons.

## Later Years and Emigration Plans

Tom Mason surfaces one final time in the novel's closing pages, in a letter to Peter. He has "made his pile and he wants to leave the country" — a decision that reflects the novel's ambivalent conclusion about the possibilities for African American success in the United States. His sister refuses to go: "Why should I give up my country? It *is* my country even if my skin is black?" The question, left unanswered, is one of the novel's most pointed: whether economic success is sufficient, or whether belonging requires something more than money can buy.

## Critical Significance

Tom Mason functions in the novel's moral economy as a test that Peter must fail in order to succeed. The money Tom offers is real and the work is honest; the problem is not that playing for Tom is degrading but that it is not *enough* for a man of Peter's gifts. Tom, who is content with his red plush car seats and jingling coins, represents a legitimate life choice that simply is not Peter's. In a novel that values ambition as a moral quality, Tom is neither villain nor hero — he is the baseline against which the protagonists measure their hunger for something more. His final letter, proposing emigration as a solution to American racism, opens a line of inquiry that the novel leaves provocatively unresolved.
