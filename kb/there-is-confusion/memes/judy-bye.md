# Judy Bye

Judy Bye is a foundational figure in the genealogical architecture of [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *There Is Confusion*. An enslaved woman owned by [[aaron-bye|Aaron Bye]], she is the mother of [[joshua-bye|Joshua Bye]] and the grandmother of [[isaiah-bye|Isaiah Bye]], making her the matriarch from whom the novel's entire Bye lineage — including [[peter-bye]] and [[meriwether-bye]] — ultimately descends. Though she appears only in the genealogical flashbacks of the novel's early chapters, her presence reverberates across four generations, and her name, inscribed in the family Bible, becomes one of the novel's most resonant symbols of continuity and identity.

## Physical Description and Character

[[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] gives Judy Bye one of the novel's most striking physical descriptions, investing her with a dignity and depth that set her apart from the stock figures of antebellum fiction:

> She was a tall, straight, steely, black woman with fine inscrutable eyes, a thin-lipped mouth and a large but shapely nose. She bore about her a quality of brooding, of mystery, embodying the attraction which she exercised for many men, white and black. But apparently she knew little of this. [003-218]

The adjectives are carefully chosen. "Steely" suggests an unyielding core beneath the enslaved woman's exterior compliance. "Inscrutable" eyes hint at an interior life that the white family she serves cannot access — a strategic opacity that is itself a form of resistance. The "quality of brooding, of mystery" that attracts men "white and black" positions Judy as a figure of power even within the constraints of her station, though [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]]'s narrator is quick to add that "apparently she knew little of this" — preserving Judy's innocence while acknowledging the sexual dynamics that slavery imposed on Black women's bodies.

## Loyalty to the Bye Family

Judy Bye's defining characteristic within the novel's economy of values is her "inexplicable attachment to the white [[family-in-there-is-confusion|Bye family]]." This loyalty perplexes those around her, particularly the men in her life, and it complicates any simple reading of the master-slave relationship:

> Her only weakness, if such it might be called, was an inexplicable attachment to the white Bye family. She married, a few years before receiving her freedom, a man named Ceazer, a proud, surly, handsome individual, who refused to adopt the surname of his master; he had belonged to white people named Morton. Since even after freedom Judy would not hear to leaving the Bye family, Aaron Bye greatly pleased by this loyalty offered the position of coachman to Ceazer, which the latter, with his customary surliness, accepted. [003-218]

The word "inexplicable" is key. [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] does not pretend to explain Judy's attachment — does not reduce it to gratitude, or Stockholm syndrome, or any of the reductive frames that a simpler novel might deploy. The attachment simply is, a fact of Judy's emotional life as irreducible as Ceazer's surly hatred of all white people. Between these two poles — Judy's loyalty and Ceazer's rage — [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] maps the full spectrum of Black psychological responses to the slave system.

## Ceazer and the Divided Household

Judy's [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]] to Ceazer creates a domestic situation that is at once comic, tragic, and deeply revealing of slavery's contradictions. Ceazer is everything Judy is not: proud where she is accommodating, surly where she is patient, and possessed of a hatred for white people — and for [[aaron-bye|Aaron Bye]] in particular — that is almost pathological:

> His was a strange truculent character; he hated slavery, hated all white people, hated particularly the Mortons, hated ineffably Aaron Bye. He wanted nothing at his hands. Once he knocked down another Negro who referred to him as "Mist' Bye's man." He was no man's man, he assured the stricken narrator, least of all the man of that damn Quaker. [003-220]

Ceazer's hatred extends even to the name "Bye" — he refuses to adopt his master's surname, keeping his own identity intact. Eventually he abandons Judy and [[joshua-bye|Joshua]] altogether, "vanishing finally into legend." His flight is an act of radical self-assertion, but it also leaves Judy to raise their son alone, within the household of the white family she refuses to leave.

## The Testament Inscription

Judy Bye's most enduring legacy in the novel is a physical artifact: her name, written in her own hand, in the family Bible that passes from generation to generation. This single act of literacy — "her single attempt at writing" — becomes the thread connecting the enslaved generation to the free one, the rural plantation past to the urban professional present:

> Underneath her boy's name she fashioned in halting crazy characters her single attempt at writing, her own name, Judy Bye. [003-220]

The description of her handwriting as "halting crazy characters" insists on the material difficulty of her achievement. Judy is not educated; her literacy is self-won, partial, the product of watching her son learn to write and "fashioning" the letters herself. Yet this fragile inscription outlasts her, traveling through [[joshua-bye|Joshua]], [[isaiah-bye|Isaiah]], and finally to Peter, who discovers it in the black testament aboard a troopship bound for France during World War I:

> The two heads so like yet so different bent over the old faded script. On the top of the page in a beautiful clear hand was written Aaron Bye, then underneath in crazy drunken letters, Judy Bye. [036-649]

The contrast between [[aaron-bye|Aaron Bye]]'s "beautiful clear hand" and Judy's "crazy drunken letters" is a visual inscription of the power differential between master and slave, made material in ink and paper. Yet it is Judy's name that Peter fixates on — "I wonder who those two were, Judy and Ceazer" — and the question of his ancestry that the testament provokes.

## Meriwether Bye's Recollection

Late in the novel, the elderly [[meriwether-bye|Meriwether Bye]] (the white Bye patriarch) recalls Judy with a vividness that suggests she left a deep impression even on those who held legal title to her body:

> "You've heard of your grandfather Isaiah, the boy that grew up with me?" Peter nodded. "Well, his father, black Joshua Bye, was my oldest brother; my father — he was Aaron Bye — was his father. Joshua was really his oldest child. His mother was Judy Bye, old Judy Bye, whom I've seen often sitting in Isaiah's house, her eyes straining, straining into the future — perhaps she saw this, who knows?" [041-290]

The image of Judy sitting in her son [[isaiah-bye|Isaiah]]'s house, "her eyes straining, straining into the future," is one of the novel's most haunting images. The elder Meriwether's speculation — "perhaps she saw this, who knows?" — gestures toward the novel's own project: to imagine the interiority of those whose voices history has not preserved. Judy Bye, who left behind only a single line of handwriting, nonetheless becomes, through [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]]'s narrative, a figure of prophetic depth and maternal endurance.

## Critical Significance

Judy Bye is the novel's most fully realized representation of the enslaved generation, and through her, [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] addresses the question that haunts the entire [[family-in-there-is-confusion|Bye family]] saga: what does it mean to be descended from slaves? The Bye men — [[joshua-bye|Joshua]] the loyal servant, [[isaiah-bye|Isaiah]] the proud entrepreneur, Peter the aspiring surgeon, Meriwether the war hero — each negotiate this legacy differently. But Judy, alone among them, embodies the paradox of her generation without resolving it: a woman who [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|love]]d the family that owned her, who chose loyalty when she could have chosen freedom, and whose mysterious interiority resists every attempt to reduce her to a type.

The testament that bears her name functions as the novel's central symbol of lineage and identity. It is a slaveowner's gift — "The gift of [[aaron-bye|Aaron Bye]]" — yet it carries Judy's name as well, the two inscriptions coexisting on the same page, master and slave bound together in ink as they were in life. For Peter, discovering this artifact in the midst of war, the testament becomes a link to an ancestry he barely knows and a reminder that identity is never simply chosen or inherited but is always, in part, a mystery to be investigated.
