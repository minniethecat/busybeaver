# The Medical Profession in There Is Confusion

Medicine occupies a uniquely charged position among the professions depicted in *There Is Confusion*. From Isaiah Bye's dream for his son through Meriwether Bye's failure to [[peter-bye]]'s renewed determination, the aspiration to become a physician functions as a multi-generational thread linking the novel's exploration of race, class, and professional identity. For Fauset's African American characters, medicine represents not merely a career but a claim to full participation in modern civilization.

## Isaiah Bye's Dream

Isaiah Bye, born a slave and freed after the Civil War, identifies medicine as the profession through which his son will achieve the greatness denied to him. A schoolteacher, pamphleteer, and community leader in Philadelphia's Vine Street colored community, Isaiah channels his formidable intelligence into a single, focused ambition: his son Meriwether will become a doctor.

> "Here he was, Meriwether Bye, destined to be a great man, a famous physician."

This ambition is partly personal — a father's hope for his son's advancement — but it is also ideological. By naming his son after the white Bye family's physician son, Isaiah stakes a claim to professional identity as a form of racial equality. If a Bye can be a doctor, then a Black Bye can be one too. The very act of naming encodes a theory of social mobility that the novel's plot will both validate and complicate.

## Meriwether Bye's Failure

[[meriwether-bye]], the bearer of his father's medical ambition, proves incapable of fulfilling it. Described as "a clever enough child though without his father's solid trait of concentration," Meriwether drifts through school, "doing absolutely mediocre work, never failing, never shining, and always rather purposely waiting the day which should bring him to the Medical School." That day never arrives. His father's death, followed by a cascade of poor decisions — gambling, drinking, the gradual liquidation of inherited property — transforms the designated great physician into "just a poor nobody, picking up odd jobs."

> "How," said Meriwether Bye harking back to Sunday-School days, "how are the mighty fallen!"

Meriwether's failure is the novel's most explicit cautionary tale about the gap between inherited ambition and individual capacity. Yet Fauset does not treat him merely as a moral failure. The weight of Isaiah's expectations, the structural barriers facing African American professionals in the late nineteenth century, and the sheer difficulty of escaping generational poverty — all contribute to a portrait of defeat that is tinged with pathos rather than condemnation.

## Peter Bye's Renewal

The medical ambition that Meriwether abandons is unexpectedly revived in his son [[peter-bye]]. Raised on the anatomy and physiology textbooks that were his father's sole remaining connection to the profession, Peter absorbs medical knowledge before he learns to read properly. "Long before he knew his multiplication tables he knew the names of the principal bones of the body and the course of the food."

> "A surgeon," said Peter forgetting his old formula and expressing a resolve which her question had engendered in him just that second.

Peter's declaration to [[joanna-marshall]] that he will become a surgeon represents a pivotal moment of self-definition. Unlike his father, who merely repeated Isaiah's ambition "by rote," Peter arrives at the medical aspiration through his own encounter with the books, his own moment of imagining a future. Joanna's admiration — "But you'll be famous, too — you'll be a wonderful doctor" — seals the commitment, linking Peter's professional ambition to his romantic pursuit of Joanna and embedding medicine within the novel's larger exploration of what it means to become somebody.
