# Mrs. Proctor

Mrs. Proctor is an offstage presence in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *There Is Confusion*, never directly encountered by the reader but rendered vivid through the resentful commentary of [[mis-sparrow|Mis' Sparrow]]. The young bride of "Young Mr. Proctor," son of the white family that employs [[mis-sparrow|Mis' Sparrow]] as a domestic, Mrs. Proctor becomes the vehicle for some of the novel's most pointed reflections on class, [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]], and the arbitrary distribution of good fortune.

## A Cinderella Resented

Mrs. Proctor is introduced through [[mis-sparrow|Mis' Sparrow]]'s aggrieved reportage at the Ellersley supper table:

> At present Mis' Sparrow had fastened a resentful eye on Mrs. Proctor, the bride of the son of the "white folks" for whom she worked. Edmonia the maid had told her about the newcomer, and over the supper table she retailed it to Mrs. Ellersley. "She wan't nobuddy. Jes' a little teeny slip of ole white gal. No money, no fambly, no nuthin'." (Ch. VIII)

The injustice, from [[mis-sparrow|Mis' Sparrow]]'s perspective, is precisely Mrs. Proctor's lack of distinction: she "wan't nobuddy" — a girl without money, family, or beauty — yet she has secured through [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]] what [[mis-sparrow|Mis' Sparrow]], with all her sharp intelligence and long service, has been denied. "Why," [[mis-sparrow|Mis' Sparrow]] demands, "are all the sweetness and light of life showered on some and utterly denied to me?"

The answer, supplied by Edmonia, only sharpens the sting: "Young Mr. Proctor's sister met her in boardin'-school, poorest thing there. Miss Dorothy sorry for her and got her a job in her father's office. She went out with some of the girls from the office and they met Mr. Proctor and he took a fancy to her" (Ch. VIII). The chain of accidents — boarding-school pity, an office job, a chance meeting — exposes the randomness of social ascent. That Mrs. Proctor is white only complicates the dynamic: [[mis-sparrow|Mis' Sparrow]], a black domestic, watches a white girl of no greater native merit ascend into the security she herself cannot reach.

## Maggie's Reflection

Mrs. Proctor's story has its deepest effect on [[maggie-ellersley]], who listens to [[mis-sparrow|Mis' Sparrow]]'s account and is moved to reflection:

> On the night after she had listened to Mis' Sparrow's comments about young Mrs. Proctor, she sat thoughtful a long time. She had sense enough to know that very often these trainmen stayed poor. They made pretty good money — they did, too, in those days — but not enough to save their wives from the washtub. (Ch. VIII)

[[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s thoughts turn immediately to her own prospects: she is being courted by [[henderson-neal|Henderson Neal]], a Pullman porter — a "trainman." Mrs. Proctor's story, meant by [[mis-sparrow|Mis' Sparrow]] as a complaint against cosmic unfairness, becomes for [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] a cautionary tale about the limits of [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]] as a vehicle for class mobility. Not every bride rises.

## The Dress

Mrs. Proctor makes one final, tangible appearance in the novel: as the unwitting donor of [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s school dress. [[mis-sparrow|Mis' Sparrow]], who "in turn had had it from young Mrs. Proctor," passes on a dark blue serge dress trimmed with black braid. [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] "tacked a tiny turnover of white lace in the high collar" and wears it to her first day at the eighth grade (Ch. VIII). The dress is "worn, but it was whole" — a material link in a chain of female gift-giving that runs from the white Proctors through [[mis-sparrow|Mis' Sparrow]] to [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie Ellersley]], compressing the novel's interest in the circulation of goods, kindness, and resentment across class and color lines.

## Critical Significance

Mrs. Proctor never speaks a line in the novel; she exists only as she is seen by others. Yet her narrative function is precise: she is the occasion for [[mis-sparrow|Mis' Sparrow]]'s most sustained philosophical outburst and for [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s most clear-eyed assessment of her own marital prospects. The randomness of Mrs. Proctor's good fortune — the boarding-school pity, the office job, the "fancy" taken by Mr. Proctor — exposes the machinery of class ascent as fundamentally arbitrary, a lesson [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] absorbs without fully understanding its implications for her own life.
