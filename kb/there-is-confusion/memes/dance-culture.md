# Dance Culture in There Is Confusion

Dance pervades *There Is Confusion* as both a form of personal expression and a marker of [[joanna-marshall|Joanna Marshall]]'s artistic ambition. From childhood performances in the Marshall parlor to professional stage productions in New York, dance traces Joanna's trajectory from gifted girl to accomplished artist. Fauset uses dance to explore tensions between individual talent and social constraint, between art as private joy and art as public career.

## Joanna's Natural Gift

Joanna's talent for dance appears early and is presented as innate — a gift that needs no formal training, only opportunity:

> "She had grace, the very poetry of motion, and she could dance any step however intricate if she saw it once." [002-008]

This instinctive ability distinguishes Joanna from her peers. She does not learn dance so much as she already knows it; formal lessons merely refine what nature has given. The narrator frames her dancing as inseparable from her physical being — motion as poetry, the body as instrument.

Joanna's relationship to dance is also shaped by the folk traditions she absorbs as a child:

> "Any sort of folksong or dance, though she did not know them by that name, delighted the colored child." [002-010]

These early encounters with vernacular dance — unnamed, untheorized, absorbed through community life — form the foundation upon which her later professional training builds.

## Dance as Social Ritual

Beyond Joanna's individual talent, dance serves as a central mechanism of social bonding in the novel. The Marshall household becomes a site where dance brings characters together across lines of class, ambition, and romantic tension:

> "The night that Joanna taught them all the barn dance, Peter maneuvered until he got Harry Portor at the piano." [007-048]

These domestic performances are simultaneously entertainment and courtship. When Joanna teaches [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie Ellersley]], [[sylvia-marshall]], [[philip-marshall]], and [[harley-alexander]] to dance, she is also negotiating the social dynamics of her circle — asserting leadership, managing rivalries, and drawing [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]]'s attention.

The dance lesson scene dramatizes these tensions explicitly:

> "Now, Maggie, dance up to one of them. All right, take Philip. You point your finger at somebody and laugh." [007-027]

Here dance instruction doubles as social orchestration. Joanna directs bodies through space, but she is also directing relationships — pairing Maggie with Philip, creating moments of contact and performance that have consequences beyond the dance floor.

## Performance and Cultural Identity

Joanna's dance ambitions carry her beyond Philadelphia parlors and into professional contexts where race and artistry intersect. Her training with Madame Harkness introduces her to European dance forms:

> "Another time she taught her an intricate Spanish dance." [012-064]

The Spanish dance lessons are significant — they signal Joanna's access to a cosmopolitan repertoire that transcends the limitations of her racialized environment. Her declaration to Peter articulates a vision of dance as universal language:

> "I'm going to do a dance representing all the nations, some day." [012-065]

This ambition — to embody "all the nations" — is both artistic and political. In a society that constrains Black women's bodies and careers, Joanna imagines dance as a medium through which she can transcend nationality and race, speaking a language that belongs to no single people.

The climactic dance performance that makes Joanna's professional reputation draws on these years of training and this expansive vision. When she performs on stage, she brings together the folk traditions of her childhood, the technical training of her lessons, and her own fierce determination.

## Dance and Romantic Economy

Dance is also the novel's primary language of courtship. Peter Bye's attraction to Joanna is mediated through watching her dance:

> "The players were absorbed, enraptured with the spirit of the dance and the abandon of the music. Joanna, too, was in a transport." [007-005]

The word "transport" recurs in Fauset's treatment of dance — it is an experience that lifts the dancer and the watcher out of their ordinary selves. For Peter, watching Joanna dance is a form of possession: he sees her at her most fully realized, and he wants her.

The couple's reconciliation, after years of separation and misunderstanding, is prefigured by a dance scene. Across the novel, dance appears at moments of emotional intensity — it is never merely decorative, always functional.
