# The Concert in There Is Confusion

The concert — and the broader world of musical performance it metonymically represents — is the vehicle through which [[joanna-marshall|Joanna Marshall]] pursues her ambition to become "the greatest singer in the world." In [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s novel, the concert stage is simultaneously a site of artistic achievement, professional advancement, and racial representation.

## Joanna's Career

Joanna's path from gifted child to professional performer is traced through specific performances that structure her narrative arc. Her talent is recognized early, and she pursues it with a "fixity of purpose" that the novel admires — and that contrasts sharply with Peter Bye's early drift.

> "Joanna already started on her concert tours, often met him on the 'road.' Sometimes they were booked at the same place for the same night."

The concert tour — "a remarkable booking for one so young" — is the material expression of Joanna's success. It represents mobility (geographical and social), income, and the public recognition she has sought since childhood. Even when personal life disappoints, "the prospect of the days stretched long and dreary before her. Even the concert tour... did not entirely console her," the career remains a source of identity and purpose.

## The Benefit Concert

The benefit concert is a specific subgenre within the novel's performance economy — a charitable event that combines artistic display with community obligation:

> "'Peter's mad,' she laughed as the two moved off, 'because I told him I was going to the benefit concert with you, Brian, and so he couldn't come tonight.'"

Here the concert becomes a site of romantic rivalry — Peter displaced by Brian Spencer, Joanna exercising her prerogative to choose her escort. The benefit concert is thus triply coded: as art, as philanthropy, and as social theater.

## Concert as Public Sphere

For Fauset's characters, the concert world is one of the few arenas where Black performers could achieve national recognition on something approaching their own terms. Joanna's career is modeled on real figures of the period — Black women singers and musicians who built professional careers in the face of segregation and stereotyping.

The concert stage offers something that the "ordinary" life of marriage and domesticity cannot: a public self, a name recognized beyond one's immediate community. When Maggie Ellersley imagines Joanna's future, she envisions precisely this:

> "That's Peter Bye, the distinguished surgeon! His wife is unusual, too, she was Joanna Marshall. You must have heard of her. Why, she sings all over the country!"

## Concert and Courtship

Concerts also function as sites of courtship and social encounter. Peter and Joanna meet and re-meet in the orbit of performance; their professional lives intertwine as they are "booked at the same place for the same night." The concert circuit is, for them, what the dance floor is for others — a space where romance and career converge.
