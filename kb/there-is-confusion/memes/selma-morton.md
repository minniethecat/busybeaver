# Selma Morton

Selma Morton is a minor character in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *There Is Confusion*, referenced in a single but thematically significant passage. She is the sister of [[arabelle-morton|Arabelle Morton]], a member of a respected African American family in [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia whose social standing is described as "of as unimpeachable standing as Miss Talbert" (021-006).

## Education and the Graduation Incident

Selma Morton is introduced through a story told by [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]] to [[joanna-marshall|Joanna Marshall]] in Chapter XVII. Peter, brooding over the racial injustices he has encountered in his pursuit of a medical career, recounts Selma's experience:

> "Arabelle Morton's sister, Selma," Peter went on morosely, "took her Master's degree last year. The candidates sat in alphabetical order. Selma sat in her seat wondering whom the chair on the left of her belonged to — it was vacant. At the last moment a girl came in, a Miss Nelson, who had been in one or two of her classes. Selma knew she was a Southerner. 'Oh, I just can't sit there,' Selma heard her say, not too much under her breath. And some friend of hers went to the Professor in charge of the exercises and he let her change her place, though it threw the whole line out of order." (017-009)

The incident exemplifies the casual, institutionalized racism that educated African Americans faced even in academic settings. A white Southern student's refusal to sit next to Selma — and the professor's acquiescence — underscores the barrier between educational achievement and social acceptance.

## Family and Social Context

Selma belongs to the Morton family of [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia, which occupies a respected position within the city's Black middle class. Her sister [[arabelle-morton|Arabelle Morton]] is described as possessing "good-natured shallowness" and "complete social assurance" (021-006). Arabelle's social circle provides entrée for [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie Ellersley]] after her engagement to [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]], indicating the Mortons' standing within [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia's stratified Black society.

The Morton household serves as a regular social destination for [[peter-bye|Peter Bye]] during his [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia years. He spent "Friday and Sunday at [[arabelle-morton|Arabelle Morton]]'s or at Lawyer Talbert's on Christian Street" (015-003), suggesting the family's home was a hub for young, educated African Americans.

## Thematic Significance

Though Selma Morton appears in only one passage, her story serves a crucial narrative function. Peter recounts her experience to [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]] as part of his argument that racial prejudice makes conventional professional advancement nearly impossible for African Americans, regardless of their qualifications. Selma's Master's degree — a mark of academic excellence — offers no protection against the casual cruelty of a white classmate and the institutional compliance of a professor.

Her story forms part of a pattern of racial grievances that Peter catalogs, culminating in his declaration that he will abandon medicine for the more immediately accessible career of a musician and orchestra organizer.
