# Mr. Simpson

Mr. Simpson is a minor but narratively consequential character in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *There Is Confusion*. A lodger in [[mrs-ellersley|Mrs. Ellersley]]'s boarding house on a [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia street, he is described as a "tousel-headed" man of modest means and limited sophistication. His accidental failure to secure the front door sets in motion one of the novel's most dramatic episodes: the violent intrusion of [[henderson-neal|Henderson Neal]] into the house, which results in an assault on [[maggie-ellersley]] and catalyzes the chain of events leading to her eventual reunion with [[henderson-neal]].

## Role at the Ellersley Boarding House

Mr. Simpson is one of several lodgers who share the Ellersley home, along with [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] and her mother, [[mrs-ellersley]]. The house functions as a microcosm of [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia's working-class colored community, with lodgers coming and going at all hours. Simpson, who "usually left later than this," occupies a room at the head of the stairs — a spatial detail that becomes significant when the crisis unfolds.

On the evening of [[henderson-neal|Neal]]'s attack, Mr. Simpson's routine is disrupted by an unusual errand. Instead of remaining in the house or securing the door behind him as he normally would, he steps out to a saloon around the corner:

> Gradually the house had emptied itself with the exception of Maggie and this tousel-headed Mr. Simpson who usually left later than this. He had not seen Bye come out, but thought it likely the visitor had left in the quarter of an hour he had spent in the saloon around the corner where he had swallowed an unaccustomed dram to fortify his intention. [030-119]

The "unaccustomed dram" proves fateful. With Simpson absent and the front door left unsecured, [[henderson-neal|Henderson Neal]] — drunk, jealous, and bent on violence — enters the house unchallenged and confronts [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] alone.

## The Aftermath of the Attack

Mr. Simpson returns to the house shortly after [[henderson-neal|Neal]]'s violent exit, still unaware of the catastrophe that has unfolded in his absence. His discovery is rendered with a careful attention to his limited, working-class perspective:

> A few moments later Mr. Simpson came rushing up the front steps. He tried the door gingerly and found to his relief that it was not locked. That meant Mrs. Ellersley had not yet returned to chide him for his carelessness. Miss Maggie now was different; she would never carry on, no matter what a fellow did. It would be just as well for him to stop at the room at the head of the stairs and let her know he had returned. [031-007]

The detail of his relief that [[mrs-ellersley|Mrs. Ellersley]] hasn't returned to scold him — and his fond appraisal of [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s gentleness — humanizes a character who could easily have been reduced to a plot device. Simpson is not malicious; he is simply careless, and his carelessness has devastating consequences.

## Remorse and Devotion

Mr. Simpson's response to the disaster he has unwittingly enabled is immediate and total. [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] characterizes his remorse with the same unsentimental clarity she brings to all her minor figures:

> The nervous shock which Harry Portor had predicted from her encounter with Neal followed only too surely, but for another reason. The flesh wound itself had been negligible and she might have recovered without the nervous breakdown, had not Mr. Simpson in an agony of remorse at the danger to which he had so unwittingly exposed her, subjected her again with equally complete unconscious thoroughness to another shock. He was always presenting her with flowers, magazines, and journals, his eyes silently beseeching her forgiveness. For Maggie had never betrayed his share in the disaster and had thus made him her eager servitor forever. [036-011]

The language is richly ironic. Simpson's "agony of remorse" is genuine, but its expression — an endless stream of flowers and magazines — becomes its own form of burden for [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]. His "silently beseeching" eyes and his transformation into her "eager servitor forever" are at once comic and touching. [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] suggests that guilt, like violence, has a way of binding people together in relationships that are not entirely voluntary.

## Role in the Crisis Response

During the immediate aftermath of [[henderson-neal|Neal]]'s attack, Mr. Simpson proves useful despite his limitations. When [[harry-portor]] arrives to treat [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s wounds, Simpson hovers in the background, and it is he who retrieves the knife that [[henderson-neal|Neal]] used:

> "I don't know," she told him faintly. Why had she not thought of this? Now she must keep him out of the sitting-room. Her confusion escaped him, but Mr. Simpson hovering in the background had heard the question and slipping out returned with the knife. [031-047]

He is also dispatched to the druggist by [[harry-portor|Harry Portor]], a small errand that allows him to be useful even as the more serious medical and legal issues are handled by others. On his return, he encounters [[mrs-ellersley|Mrs. Ellersley]] and delivers the news of the attack — the narrative registers only "a shriek cut short by the closing door," leaving the specifics of that painful conversation to the reader's imagination.

## Critical Significance

Mr. Simpson belongs to a class of minor characters [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] uses to ground the novel's melodramatic plot turns in the textures of everyday life. He is not a hero or a villain; he is an ordinary man whose ordinary carelessness — stepping out for a drink, forgetting to lock a door — has extraordinary consequences. His devotion to [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] afterward, half-comic and half-touching, is a study in the strange intimacies that guilt can create.

His presence in the Ellersley boarding house also serves [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]]'s project of social documentation. The Ellersley home, with its shifting population of lodgers, represents the economic precariousness of [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia's colored working class — people who cannot afford private homes, who share space with strangers, whose lives are shaped by the comings and goings of others. Mr. Simpson, the tousel-headed lodger who lingers later than the rest, is one thread in this fabric of interdependence and vulnerability.
