# Mrs. Ellersley

Mrs. Ellersley is the widowed mother of [[maggie-ellersley]] in [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Jessie Redmon Fauset]]'s 1924 novel *There Is Confusion*. Though a minor character in terms of narrative presence, she embodies the working-class colored experience from which [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] struggles to escape, and her relationship with her daughter illuminates the novel's exploration of poverty, ambition, and intergenerational striving.

## Life of Poverty

Mrs. Ellersley is introduced in the novel's eighth chapter as "a spare hardworking woman to whom life had meant nothing but poverty and confusion." A laundress by trade, she spends Thursdays and Fridays of each week washing and ironing, supplementing her meager income by renting out the front room of her tenement apartment to trainmen and occasionally married couples.

The Ellersley home, located in New York's Tenderloin district on Thirty-fifth Street, is described in terms of unrelieved squalor:

> She and Maggie slept and lived in the two wretchedly ventilated rooms, in a perpetual gloom penetrated ever so slightly by a flickering blue flame. A confusion of clothes, obscene old furniture, boxes, stale newspapers was littered about them. For some reason the rooms were everlastingly damp, perhaps because, although rain could get down the air-shaft, the sunlight never could.

[[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s sensory memory of this environment — "a gas-flame burning low and blue" — persists even in her later, more fortunate years, a marker of childhood deprivation that the novel never allows the reader to forget.

Mrs. Ellersley's obsessive frugality — "Saving was Mrs. Ellersley's insistent because necessary fetish" — means that "[[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s tea was always weak, and never sweet enough. The bread — baker's with holes in it, yesterday's, two loaves for five cents — was always stale; the meat usually salt and sometimes tainted." This grinding poverty is presented without sentimentality; it is simply the material condition of Mrs. Ellersley's life, and by extension, [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s.

## Widowhood and Resilience

Mrs. Ellersley is described as "a widow who considered herself fortunate to be one" — a startling phrase that hints at an unhappy [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]] to a man who "had run on the road" as a trainman. Her husband's death, rather than being a loss, appears to have been a liberation from a difficult union. Yet widowhood also means carrying the full economic burden of survival alone, without a man's income to supplement her own.

Despite the harshness of her circumstances, Mrs. Ellersley supports [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s ambitions with "dreary hopefulness." When [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] declares, "I will not always live like this, Ma — I'll get out of it some way," the narrator notes that "her mother, though always scoffing, believed her." This paradoxical combination — habitual skepticism paired with genuine faith in her daughter — captures the emotional complexity of a woman who has been ground down by life but refuses to extinguish her child's hopes.

## Relationship with Maggie

Mrs. Ellersley's most significant function in the novel is as the baseline from which [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] measures her social ascent. [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s early experiences watching her mother wash and iron — "she did not want to wash and iron, to go through the dreary existence which had been her mother's" — fuel her determination to escape through [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]] to a man of means.

The Ellersley women's relationship is characterized by pragmatic cooperation rather than emotional warmth. When [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] devises the scheme to transform their tenement into a boardinghouse — using John Howe's savings to rent a larger apartment and sublet rooms — Mrs. Ellersley is "reinvigorated" by the improvement in their circumstances. She covers a packing-box dressing table with scrim, takes in mending, and gives breakfast to "a few chosen spirits." The boardinghouse represents the peak of Mrs. Ellersley's own social mobility: from laundress in the Tenderloin to landlady in a slightly better neighborhood, she has risen about as far as her circumstances permit.

[[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]], in contrast, sees the boardinghouse as only a way station. Her mother's life is what she is running from, and the novel charts the emotional cost of that flight — the guilt, the distance, the uneasy knowledge that one's own success has been built on a foundation of maternal sacrifice.

## Later Life

In the novel's later chapters, Mrs. Ellersley appears primarily as background — a presence in [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s New York house, a companion to [[mis-sparrow|Mis' Sparrow]] at lodge meetings, a witness to her daughter's romantic crises. When [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] returns home to rest after leaving [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia, her mother chides her gently: "Can't see why you mope so about the house, anyway. Don't believe you've been anywhere since you've been here this trip — 'cept to [[madame-harkness|Madam Harkness]]'."

[[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s bleak assessment of her own life — that without Peter she has "nothing in her life but [[madame-harkness|Madame Harkness]] and those two poor old ladies — her mother and [[mis-sparrow|Mis' Sparrow]]" — acknowledges Mrs. Ellersley's presence while also revealing the emotional distance between mother and daughter. Mrs. Ellersley has given [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]] everything she could; it has not been enough, and both women know it.

## Critical Significance

Mrs. Ellersley represents the economic baseline of the novel's social world — the condition of the colored working poor from which characters like [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]], [[joanna-marshall|Joanna]], and Peter either rise or fall. Unlike the Marshalls, with their walnut furniture and silver service and aspirations to greatness, or the Byes, with their genealogical pride and centuries of [[philip-marshall|Phil]]adelphia history, the Ellersleys have nothing but labor and determination.

[[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]]'s treatment of Mrs. Ellersley is notable for its lack of condescension. She is not a figure of pathos or a sentimentalized emblem of maternal sacrifice. She is a woman who has endured poverty, an unhappy [[marriage-in-there-is-confusion|marriage]], and widowhood, and who has managed, through relentless work and scraping frugality, to give her daughter a chance at something better. That [[maggie-ellersley|Maggie]]'s ambitions carry her into social territory her mother can never enter is neither celebrated nor mourned; it is simply the way of the world [[jessie-redmon-fauset|Fauset]] depicts — a world in which class mobility requires, for those born at the bottom, a kind of orphanhood from one's origins.
