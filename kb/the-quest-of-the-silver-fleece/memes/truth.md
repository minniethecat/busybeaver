# Truth

"The truth is," confesses [[colonel-cresswell|Colonel Cresswell]] at one point (008-027), but the novel's deepest concern is how rarely truth is told in a society organized around [[race|racial]] hierarchy. Truth-telling becomes a political act.

## Half-Truths and Lies

The novel studies the half-truth as a Southern institution. [[zora|Zora]] is described as "full-eyed and telling the half-truth easily" (007-047), and the narrative suggests that the half-truth is more dangerous than the lie because it obscures its own incompleteness. [[mary-taylor|Mary Taylor]]'s New England conscience collides with a community where "lying [[tradition|custom]]ary" has become second nature (007-048).

## The Risk of Truth

"Always—tell—the truth?" [[zora|Zora]] repeats slowly (009-036), and follows with "All the truth?" (009-039). Her questions recognize that complete truth-telling is a luxury the powerless cannot afford. The novel positions truth not as a simple moral choice but as something that must be negotiated against survival.

## Truth as Foundation

Yet the novel also insists that truth is the necessary foundation for any real change. "The masses and [[labor|work]] is the truth one must face" (006-040)—a declaration that links truth to material reality and [[community|collective]] action rather than individual virtue.
