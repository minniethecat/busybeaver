# Shame

Shame in *The Quest of the Silver Fleece* has two faces. It is the emotion imposed by the [[race|racial]] order on black people who are made to feel their subordinate status as personal inadequacy. And it is the moral sentiment that the black community mobilizes to enforce its own norms—the "Have you absolutely no shame?" (015-089) that polices behavior within the group. Du Bois traces how shame circulates, who is made to feel it, and who is exempt.

## The Shame of Poverty

[[zora|Zora]] experiences shame most acutely around her material conditions. [[bles-alwyn|Bles]] "thought her ashamed of" her home in [[the-swamp-land|the swamp]], "and delicately refrained from mentioning it" (005-033). The shame of poverty is not simply about lacking things but about being seen to lack them—about the exposure of one's vulnerability to a gaze that judges. [[zora|Zora]]'s "one great fear was lest her squalid home be seen by those who had seen her amid more luxurious surroundings" (005-038). Shame is social: it requires an audience.

## Shame as Moral Weapon

The novel also depicts shame as a community enforcement mechanism. "[[bles-alwyn|Bles]], I am ashamed to hear you talk so," someone says, invoking shame to correct behavior (004-036). "Have you absolutely no shame?" is a question that assumes shame should exist—that its absence is a moral failing (015-089). In this register, shame is not imposed by the oppressor but generated within the community as a means of maintaining standards.

## The Shame of the Powerful

The novel's most interesting treatment of shame concerns those who should feel it but do not. The white characters who exploit black labor, who manipulate markets, who preach submission—they are notably unashamed. Du Bois suggests that shamelessness is a privilege of power, and that the capacity for shame is a marker of moral seriousness that the powerful have learned to do without.
