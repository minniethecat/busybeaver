# The Firm

The firm in *The Quest of the Silver Fleece* is the basic unit of commercial organization. The novel's firms—Grey and Easterly, the unnamed law offices, the "wholesale houses" (006-064)—are the institutional nodes through which [[wealth|capital]] flows from Northern investors to Southern plantations and back again as profit.

## Grey and Easterly

The most explicitly named firm is "Grey and Easterly, Brokers" (006-003), the Wall Street firm that handles the cotton speculation at the novel's financial center. The firm is a partnership—the ampersand between two names is the basic form of Gilded Age business organization—and its function is to connect [[wealth|capital]] to commodity, investor to market. [[mary-taylor|Mary Taylor]]'s connection to the firm through "her late husband's office" (006-013) shows how firms are inherited as well as built.

## The Firm as Instrument

The firm is presented as a neutral instrument that serves whoever [[power|control]]s it. It can finance the cotton trust or the [[the-school|school]]; it can speculate in cotton futures or invest in industrial development. The firm has no politics, only interests—which means that in a society structured by [[race|racial]] in[[freedom|equality]], the firm reliably serves white interests. Black farmers, black laborers, and black communities have no firms to represent them.

## Partnership and Power

The partnership form of the firm—two names joined by an ampersand—is the novel's image of how power is consolidated through personal connection. The firm is not an abstract corp[[speech|oration]] but a [[love|relationship]] between individuals who trust each other (in the financial sense) and exclude those they do not. The firm's "affirmation" (008-049) of a transaction, its "firmness" (008-062) in negotiation—these are the gestures of economic power that the novel tracks.
