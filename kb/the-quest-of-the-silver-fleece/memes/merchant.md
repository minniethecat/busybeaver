# Merchant

Merchants in *The Quest of the Silver Fleece* occupy a distinct position in the Southern economic hierarchy, standing between the large planters and the laboring class. They are portrayed as a group whose interests align with the plantation elite but whose economic power is secondary and dependent.

## Economic Position

John Taylor, seeking to organize the cotton market, identifies the merchant class as a critical constituency. He calculates that the cotton trade is "controlled by five thousand wholesale merchants and three thousand big landowners," and his strategy depends on winning over "the banks, wholesale merchants, and biggest planters" to his cause (010-114, 010-116). However, this ambition is met with skepticism — Taylor is warned that he "can't get the banks and the big merchants" behind his reform efforts, revealing the merchants' conservative alignment with the status quo (010-124).

## Caldwell and Local Commerce

At the local level, Caldwell represents the small-town merchant whose political views mirror the racial hierarchy. He sighs that "we've got to vote the nigger out" — a sentiment reflecting how the merchant class participated in the disenfranchisement of Black citizens (035-024). Later, Bles Alwyn, attempting to organize Black economic independence, "went to all the chief merchants one after another and received" only resistance, underscoring the merchant class's role as gatekeepers of economic opportunity (036-044).

## Related Concepts

The merchant class functions as a bridge between the [[plantation]] economy and the broader market, including the [[cotton]] trade. Their resistance to change reinforces the economic barriers faced by characters seeking to escape the [[tenant-farming]] system.
