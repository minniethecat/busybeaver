# Law

In *The Quest of the Silver Fleece*, law is not an abstract principle of justice but a concrete instrument of racial and economic power. Du Bois presents the legal system of the post-Reconstruction South as a mechanism that protects white property interests while criminalizing black life. The novel traces law's operation at every level of society, from the spectacle of the courthouse to the quiet manipulation of legislation, revealing a system in which legal authority and economic domination are fused into a single instrument of control.

## The Courthouse as Symbol

The novel's first extended treatment of law comes through the image of the courthouse, which dominates the town square of [[tooms-county|Tooms County]]. Du Bois describes the scene with a visual intensity that collapses the distance between architecture and power:

> The hot, dusty quadrangle stretched in dreary deadness toward the temple of the town, as if doing obeisance to the courthouse. [004-089]

The courthouse is rendered as a "temple"—a word that invests secular authority with religious awe—and the surrounding buildings appear to bow before it. This architectural deference mirrors the social deference that the legal system commands and enforces. From its steps, the machinery of enforcement becomes visible:

> Down the courthouse steps the sheriff, with Winchester on shoulder, was bringing the last prisoner—a curly-headed boy with golden face and big brown frightened eyes. [004-089]

The image is deliberately jarring: a child, described in terms that emphasize beauty and vulnerability, led at gunpoint from the seat of justice. The courthouse is not merely where justice is administered but where force is deployed against the bodies of the powerless.

## Law and Race

The novel makes clear that legal institutions operate differently depending on the color of the accused. Miss Smith's quiet declaration—"What I don't understand, I don't judge" [007-023]—stands as a counterpoint to the rush to judgment that characterizes the legal treatment of black characters. Her restraint is exceptional; the norm is represented by a system in which accusation and conviction are effectively the same act.

When [[zora|Zora]] faces legal jeopardy, the novel notes that "she never thought of appeal to courts, for Colonel Cresswell was Justice of the Peace and his son was bailiff" [017-069]. The fusion of economic power—Cresswell as landowner—and legal authority—Cresswell as Justice of the Peace—means that the law offers no recourse to those whom it is designed to control. The courthouse that dominates the town square is, in effect, the Cresswell house extended into public space.

## Economic Dimensions

Law in the novel also operates through legislation that serves economic interests. When the Cotton Combine's legal department drafts its agenda, the reach of law into economic life becomes explicit:

> Among others was one relating to child labor, another that would effect certain changes in the tariff, and a proposed law providing for a cotton bale of a shape and dimensions different from the customary—the last constituting a particularly clever artifice which, under the guise of convenience in handling, would necessitate the installation of entirely new gin and compress machinery, to be supplied by the trust. [013-001]

This passage reveals law operating at its most insidious: a technical regulation whose stated purpose is administrative convenience but whose actual effect is market concentration. Du Bois thus shows law functioning at two levels—the spectacular violence of the courthouse steps and the quiet violence of legislative manipulation—united by their common service to the interests of [[power|the powerful]].
