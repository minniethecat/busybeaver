# The Color Line

"The problem of the Twentieth Century," Du Bois famously declared in *The Souls of Black Folk* (1903), "is the problem of the color-line." *The Quest of the Silver Fleece* is, in large measure, a novelistic expl[[speech|oration]] of that declaration—a demonstration, in narrative form, of how the color line shapes lives, limits possibilities, and structures the [[society|social world]] of [[tooms-county|Tooms County]].

## The Veil and Double Consciousness

Du Bois's metaphor of "the Veil"—the invisible but impenetrable barrier that separates black and white Americans, through which each sees the other darkly—permeates the novel. White characters move through [[tooms-county|Tooms County]] with a confidence born of not seeing what black characters cannot avoid seeing: the omnipresence of [[race|racial]] hierarchy. The novel dramatizes what Du Bois theorized: that the color line wounds both sides, degrading the humanity of the oppressor even as it crushes the oppressed.

## The Color Line in Daily Life

The novel maps the color line at every scale, from the largest structures of political [[economics|economy]] to the smallest moments of personal interaction. It determines where people may live, what [[labor|work]] they may do, whom they may marry, how they may speak, and whether they may learn to read. No [[love|relationship]] in the novel—not between [[bles-alwyn|Bles]] and [[zora|Zora]], not between [[mary-taylor|Mary Taylor]] and her students, not between [[john-taylor|John Taylor]] and his business partners—is untouched by it.

## Crossing the Line

The novel's most dramatic moments often involve transgressions of the color line—or the threat of such transgression. Characters who cross the line, or who are perceived to have crossed it, face consequences that reveal the violence underlying the [[race|racial]] order. The color line is not merely a [[tradition|custom]] or a prejudice but a regime enforced by law, [[economics|economy]], and the constant possibility of lethal force (007-048).
