---# Class

Class in *The Quest of the Silver Fleece* is never simple. It is crossed, at every point, by race: the poorest white person outranks the most accomplished black person; the white aristocracy derives its status from its ownership of black people; and the very idea of class mobility is withheld from those whom the [[race|racial]] order has fixed in place. Du Bois depicts a society in which class operates through multiple registers—birth, [[economics|wealth]], education, race—and in which these registers are in constant tension.

## The White Aristocracy

The [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]]s represent the "local white aristocracy"—a class defined by [[family|lineage]], land, and the long possession of human property. [[mary-taylor|Mary Taylor]]'s encounter with [[tooms-county|Tooms County]] is mediated through this class: she learns "at length the impossibility of her knowing much about the local white aristocracy" and then "told him all she had heard" (006-084)—the paradox of claiming ignorance and then transmitting the lore captures how aristocracy reproduces itself through the very stories that purport to describe it from outside.

The aristocracy's relation to the [[the-school|school]] is one of condescending patronage. [[harry-cresswell|Harry Cresswell]]'s visit is framed as "an olive branch from the white aristocracy to this [[labor|work]]" (008-062), and [[mary-taylor|Mary Taylor]] argues that "the local white aristocracy, helped by Northern philanthropy, should take charge of such gradual changes" (012-007). The aristocracy offers not partnership but management—a benevolent super[[dream|vision]] that leaves the [[race|racial]] hierarchy intact while claiming the moral [[debt|credit]] for [[philanthropy|reform]].

## Historical Context

The post-Reconstruction South was a society in which class and race were inseparably fused. The destruction of the planter class's [[economics|wealth]] by the Civil War had been followed by its partial reconstitution through sharecropping, tenant farming, and debt [[slavery|peonage]]—systems that preserved the [[race|racial]] hierarchy while adapting its economic form. Northern [[wealth|capital]], personified by figures like [[john-taylor|John Taylor]] and Easterly, introduced a new class logic—market-based, meritocratic in rhetoric, exploitative in practice—that coexisted uneasily with the older Southern aristocracy of birth.

## Two Aristocracies: Birth vs. Talent

The novel stages a conflict between two models of class: the Southern aristocracy of birth and the Northern ideology of talent. [[john-taylor|John Taylor]] "believed in an aristocracy of talent alone, and secretly despised [[colonel-cresswell|Colonel Cresswell]]'s pretensions of birth" (035-071). [[john-taylor|Taylor]]'s meritocratic ideal is, in principle, open to black [[progress|advancement]]: "If a man had ability and push [[john-taylor|Taylor]] was willing and anxious to open the way for him, even though he were black" (035-071).

But even [[john-taylor|Taylor]]'s "aristocracy of talent" has its limits: "The weak and the ignorant of all races he despised and had no patience with them" (035-071). [[john-taylor|Taylor]]'s class ideology is more inclusive than [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]]'s—it admits the possibility of black achievement—but it remains hierarchical. It replaces birth with ability as the criterion of worth but does not question the necessity of hierarchy itself.

## Caste and Color

The novel uses the language of "caste" to describe the Southern class system: "the caste way of thinking in the South, both as applied to poor whites and to Negroes, he simply could not understand" (035-071). Caste, unlike class, is immutable—determined by birth and unalterable by effort. The Southern system is a caste system because it is a [[race|racial]] system: race overrides every other determinant of social position.

[[colonel-cresswell|Colonel Cresswell]], moving against [[zora|Zora]]'s land and livelihood, understands himself as "fighting the battle of his color and caste" (036-054). The conflation is complete: color and caste are one. To defend white supremacy is to defend the class order; to challenge the class order is to challenge white supremacy.

## The Lady and the Gentleman

Class also operates through the language of gentility. [[elspeth|Elspeth]], the black woman who runs the cabin in [[the-swamp-land|the swamp]], is "a lady in the fine old-fashioned aristocracy of the term" in [[zora|Zora]]'s eyes (019-006). The novel's willingness to apply aristocratic language to a black woman living in a [[the-swamp|swamp]] cabin is a quiet but radical act—an insistence that [[pride|dignity]] is not the monopoly of the white upper class.
