# Jealousy

Jealousy in *The Quest of the Silver Fleece* is presented not merely as a private romantic failing but as a force with social consequences. The novel connects jealousy to the broader patterns of possession and [[power|control]] that structure [[love|relationship]]s under the color line.

## Romantic Jealousy

[[caroline|Caroline Wynn]] counsels [[zora|Zora]] bluntly: "you stow all that jealousy and heavy tragedy. Treat [[bles-alwyn|Alwyn]] as an acquaintance" (026-067). The advice is strategic, not moral—jealousy is framed as a tactical error, a display of emotional investment that weakens one's position. The "heavy tragedy" that jealousy produces is self-inflicted damage, not a mark of love's depth.

## Jealousy as Possession

The novel connects jealousy to the possessive instinct that underlies both romantic domination and [[race|racial]] slavery. To be jealous is to treat another person as property. [[miss-wynn|Miss Wynn]]'s worldly advice—to "stow" jealousy—is also implicitly a rejection of the possessiveness that the slave system encoded into intimate relations.

## The Alternative

The novel's treatment of jealousy suggests an alternative ethic: treating others as free agents rather than possessions. [[zora|Zora]] tended the children "jealously; they were her [[hope|Hope]]" (011-045), where jealousy is redirected from romantic rivalry to protective devotion—transformed rather than suppressed.
