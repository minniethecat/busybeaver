# Marriage

"This marriage—or, rather, these marriages—" (020-003). The novel's plural framing of marriage signals its complexity: marriage is never simply a union of two individuals but an entanglement of families, [[economics|economic]] interests, and [[race|racial]] politics.

> This marriage—or, rather, these marriages—were not to his liking. It was a mésalliance of a sort that pricked him tenderly; it savored grossly of bargain and sale. [020-003]

Colonel Cresswell's discomfort with the match—seeing it as "bargain and sale"—reveals how marriage among the Southern elite functioned as an instrument of class consolidation, where romantic choice was subordinated to strategic alliance.

## Love and Ambition

The central marriage plot involves [[Zora]] and [[Bles-Alwyn|Bles]], whose relationship is tested by ambition, [[sacrifice]], and mistrust. Bles "had spoken of their marriage; with twined hands and arms" (015-056)—the physical tenderness of the scene contrasts with the social forces arrayed against their union.

> Today seemed doubly glorious, for Bles had spoken of their marriage; with twined hands and arms, and lips ever and again seeking their mates, they walked the leafy way. [015-056]

As [[Mrs-Vanderpool|Mrs. Vanderpool]] dryly observes, "there is marrying and giving in marriage" (015-058), reducing the sacred institution to a mundane transaction. The tension between love and ambition runs through their courtship, with [[Zora]]'s education and independence creating friction with traditional expectations of wifely submission.

## The Wedding as Spectacle

The novel treats weddings as elaborate social performances. [[Helen-Cresswell|Helen Cresswell]]'s wedding preparations consume multiple chapters, with "the improbability of getting a wedding-gown in time" (020-019) becoming a minor crisis. The [[wedding]] gown itself—a garment that will be worn once—symbolizes the performative excess of upper-class marriage rituals.

> Zora had been in the house but a week when a large express package was received from John Taylor. Its unwrapping brought a cry of pleasure from the ladies. There lay a bolt of silken-like cambric of wondrous beauty. [021-027]

"For the wedding-dress" (021-027) reads one marked package, and the elaborate preparations extend even to "the wedding invitations" (021-028). The wedding is not a private ceremony but a public demonstration of [[wealth]] and status, a theatrical production in which the bride is the reluctant star and the guests are the audience.

## Marriage Across the Color Line

The novel's treatment of marriage cannot be separated from the [[race|racial]] order. Interracial marriage was illegal throughout the South during this period, and the possibility—or impossibility—of marriage across the color line haunts several relationships in the novel. The legal prohibition of interracial marriage was one of the most jealously guarded boundaries of the Jim Crow order, and the novel registers its presence even when the characters do not directly confront it.
