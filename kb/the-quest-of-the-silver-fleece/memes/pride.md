# Pride

Pride in *The Quest of the Silver Fleece* operates across a [[race|racial]] double standard. White pride—[[colonel-cresswell|Colonel Cresswell]]'s "much family pride" (008-003), [[mary-taylor|Mary Taylor]]'s "pride in being emancipated from [[tradition|convention]]" (026-014)—is a recognized social value, a marker of standing and self-respect. Black pride is treated as dangerous, an affront to the [[race|racial]] order that demands deference. The novel tracks how pride functions as both a resource for resistance and a vulnerability to [[greed|exploitation]].

## White Pride and Its Costs

[[colonel-cresswell|Colonel Cresswell]]'s "family pride" is the affective dimension of his class position. It is pride in [[family|lineage]], in land, in "the old [[class|aristocracy]]" that defines his identity. This pride is socially validated—it is what makes him recognizable as a gentleman—but it is also what blinds him to the contradictions of his position. His pride cannot acknowledge that what he is proud of was built on [[greed|exploitation]].

## Black Pride as Resistance

[[zora|Zora]]'s pride operates differently. She "despised" [[the-swamp-land|the swamp]] cabin and the poverty it represented, "but her pride was in arms and her one great fear was lest her squalid home be seen by those who had seen her amid more luxurious surroundings" (005-038). Pride is self-protection—a refusal to be reduced to one's conditions. It is "wounded pride" (007-029) that drives characters to assert their dignity against a world that denies it.

## Pride and Achievement

When [[zora|Zora]] accomplishes something significant, the novel notes that "there was no pride of achievement in her manner" (017-046). The absence of pride marks the seriousness of her purpose: she is not performing for recognition. Pride, properly understood, is not self-congratulation but the steady maintenance of dignity under assault.
