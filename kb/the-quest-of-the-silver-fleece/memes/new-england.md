# New England

New England functions in the novel as both a geographical region and a symbolic counterpoint to the American South. It represents the heritage of Northern reformers, the industrial might of textile mills, the educational institutions that shape characters' worldviews, and a particular moral sensibility that Du Bois both honors and critiques.

## Overview

New England first enters the narrative through character background rather than direct setting. Miss Sarah Smith "represented the older New England of her parents — honest, inscrutable, determined, with a conscience which she worshipped, and utterly unselfish" (003-013). This characterization establishes New England as the source of a particular moral and cultural formation that shapes the novel's schoolteacher protagonist.

The region appears throughout the novel primarily through characters who hail from it, the industrial interests centered there, and the cultural contrast it provides to the Southern setting.

## Geography and Economic Role

New England's economic significance in the novel centers on its textile industry. John Taylor notes that "there are a thousand cotton-mills in this country, half of them in the South, one-fourth in New England, and one-fourth in the Middle States" (010-109). The "great Easterly mills in New England" (005-062) represent the industrial endpoint of the cotton economy that originates in Southern fields like those of the Cresswell plantation.

This economic geography creates a chain of exploitation: Southern Black laborers grow cotton, Southern white planters and the Farmers' League control its distribution, and New England mills process it into finished goods. The region thus participates in — and benefits from — the economic system the novel critiques.

## Cultural Character

Du Bois develops New England's cultural character through recurring motifs. The "New England conscience" appears as a distinctive moral formation — "honest, inscrutable, determined" (003-013) — that drives Miss Smith's educational mission in Alabama and Mary Taylor's sense of duty. Mary "thought of the golden sheen of the cotton, and the cold March winds of New England" (004-007), contrasting the sensuous warmth of the South with the austerity of her Northern origins.

The cultural contrast between North and South also plays out in behavioral differences. When Bles hesitates before answering a question, the narrator notes: "Oh! these people were so slow — now a New England boy would have answered and asked a half-dozen questions in the time" (003-020). The Northern temperament is portrayed as quicker, more direct, but also potentially less patient with the rhythms of Southern life.

Bles Alwyn, educated in the North, brings a New England-influenced perspective back to the South, creating tensions and opportunities that drive key plot developments.

## Thematic Significance

New England serves three primary thematic functions. First, it represents the Northern reform tradition — the abolitionist and educational missionary impulse that established schools for freedpeople across the South after the Civil War. Miss Smith embodies this tradition, bringing "New England conscience" to bear on the problems of Black education in Alabama.

Second, the region represents industrial capitalism — the mills that process Southern cotton and the economic interests that shape national policy. The tension between New England's reformist heritage and its industrial present mirrors the novel's broader critique of American capitalism.

Third, New England provides a cultural foil that helps define Southern distinctiveness. The contrast between Northern efficiency and Southern leisure, between New England conscience and Southern pragmatism, structures the novel's exploration of regional identity in post-Reconstruction America.
