# Caroline Wynn

Caroline Wynn of Washington embodies the intersection of Southern gentility and political calculation. A young white woman of the capital's elite circles, she navigates the complex social geography where race, gender, and power converge—sometimes as an ally, sometimes as an obstacle, always as a force to be reckoned with.

## Character Overview

> Miss Caroline Wynn of Washington had little faith in her escort. Whether or not Caroline Wynn would ever marry him was a perplexing question. [024-001–002]

The novel introduces Caroline through the lens of marriage—the primary index of a woman's power in her world—but immediately signals that her concerns extend beyond the personal into the political. Her "little faith" is not just in her escort but in the entire arrangement of power that surrounds her.

Caroline represents the ambiguous position of elite white women in the racial order. Her protected status as a Southern lady is purchased at the cost of black women's vulnerability, yet her own autonomy is sharply constrained by the same system that elevates her.

## Key Relationships

With [[senator-smith|Senator Smith]], Caroline deploys the arts of feminine influence that her class and race make available. With [[mrs-vanderpool|Mrs. Vanderpool]], she engages in a quiet rivalry that maps the limited terrain on which elite women could compete for power. With [[mary-cresswell|Mary Cresswell]], she shares a social world but not necessarily the same commitments.

## Key Scenes

> Suppose he asked Caroline Wynn to help him in this case? It was delicate but correct. [025-032]

Senator Smith's strategic calculus reveals Caroline's value: she is "delicate but correct"—the perfect instrument for political work that must not look like political work.

> The vertical line in Caroline Wynn's forehead became pronounced. [029-004]

This small physical detail, repeated at moments of stress, registers the cost of Caroline's careful performances. Behind the composed surface, the novel suggests, is a woman who understands far more than she can safely express.

> All this Caroline Wynn thought of, and then smiled. [029-036]

The smile that closes her internal calculations is her signature gesture—the mask that elite white Southern womanhood requires and that Du Bois exposes as a strategic performance.

## Character Arc

Caroline's trajectory traces the education of a Southern white woman in the realities of power. She begins as a product of her class, skilled in its social arts but unreflective about their foundations. By the novel's political climax, she has learned to wield those arts for ends that exceed mere social advancement—though the novel remains characteristically ambivalent about whether she ever fully confronts the racial order that makes her power possible.
