# John Taylor

John Taylor represents the new face of American [[wealth|capital]]ism arriving in the cotton South. Unlike [[colonel-cresswell|Colonel Cresswell]], whose power is rooted in land and [[family|lineage]], John Taylor understands power as flows of [[wealth|capital]], chains of production, and [[power|control]] of markets. His dream of a cotton trust that would dominate every stage from field to factory embodies the transformation of the Southern [[economics|economy]].

## Character Overview

John is [[mary-taylor|Mary Taylor]]'s brother, a young man of business ambition and flexible ethics. He comes to [[alabama|Alabama]] not out of [[philanthropy|reform]]ist zeal but because he sees in cotton an opportunity for [[economics|wealth]] and power. His [[dream|vision]] is vast: a trust that would [[power|control]] "the [[cotton-mill|cotton-mills]] of America," linking Southern agriculture to Northern industry and European markets in a single integrated system (006-048)(006-050).

## The Cotton Trust Vision

John's plan represents a fundamental shift in how power operates in the cotton [[economics|economy]]. [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]]'s power is local, personal, and landed; John's would be national, corporate, and financial. He recognizes that [[power|control]]ling cotton manufacturing is more profitable than merely growing cotton, and his schemes are efforts to capture the value that lies between the field and the finished cloth (006-058)(006-068).

John also sees the "big cotton-land monopoly in sight"—the concentration of land ownership that creates both a business opportunity and a structural barrier for black farmers (006-064).

## Relationship with Mary Taylor

John's [[love|relationship]] with his sister is affectionate but strained by their divergent values. Where [[mary-taylor|Mary]] sees the black community of [[tooms-county|Tooms County]] as people to be educated, John sees them as labor to be managed. His business interests repeatedly conflict with her educational mission.

## The New South vs. the Old

John Taylor and [[colonel-cresswell|Colonel Cresswell]] represent two eras of American [[wealth|capital]]ism: the industrial North and the agrarian South, the corp[[speech|oration]] and the plantation, the speculator and the aristocrat. Their alliance—and their conflicts—dramatize the economic transformation of the post-Reconstruction South and the emerging shape of twentieth-century American [[wealth|capital]]ism.
