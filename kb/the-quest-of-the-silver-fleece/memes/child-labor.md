# Child Labor

The issue of child labor enters the novel through the political plotline centered on Washington, D.C. The proposed Child Labor Bill — and specifically its amendment — becomes a proxy battle between industrial interests (the cotton trust) and progressive reformers (the Civic Club), testing the moral commitments of the novel's characters.

## Definition

In the novel's context, child labor refers specifically to the legislative struggle over federal regulation of child workers in American industry. The "Child Labor Bill" (also called the "Child Labor movement") is a progressive reform measure that attracts the advocacy of the Civic Club and Congressman Todd, while drawing fierce opposition from the cotton trust and its political allies. The bill functions less as a detailed policy proposal than as a moral-litmus test that reveals characters' true allegiances.

## Manifestation in the Novel

### Plot Level

The Child Labor Bill first appears as one of several legislative items that intersect with the cotton trust's interests (013-001). The trust views the amendment process as a strategic opportunity: the "amended Child Labor Bill" must either be passed in a form that protects industrial interests or be killed entirely (028-027).

The bill becomes central to the conflict between Harry Cresswell and his wife Mary. Harry, aligned with the trust, orders Mary to "drop this bill and the Civic Club from now on" (028-070). His demand transforms the bill from a political issue into a personal test of Mary's autonomy and moral development.

Congressman Todd's Civic Club actively campaigns for the bill, issuing circulars and mobilizing reformist energy (028-049). The trust's representative Easterly warns that "it will ruin the plans of the Combine to put this amended Child Labor Bill through" (028-036), revealing how legislative reform threatens concentrated economic power.

### Character Level

- **[[mary-cresswell|Mary Cresswell]]** — Her work on the Child Labor Bill through the Civic Club marks her emergence as an independent moral agent. When Harry demands she abandon the cause, her refusal represents a critical step in her character development.
- **[[harry-cresswell|Harry Cresswell]]** — Harry's opposition to the bill aligns him fully with the trust's interests. His later regret at having "acted too cavalierly in this Civic Club mess" (028-074) suggests a flicker of conscience, though his primary loyalty remains with economic power.
- **[[bles-alwyn|Bles Alwyn]]** — Though less directly involved with the bill, Bles is urged to "take a stand on some of these progressive matters — this bill, or the Child Labor movement" (022-010) as part of his political education in Washington.

### Symbolic Level

The Child Labor Bill functions symbolically as a measure of what the political system values: children's welfare versus industrial profit. The trust's confidence that "nothing short of a popular upheaval can send any Child Labor bill through Congress in six years" (010-109) reveals the deep structural obstacles facing even modest reform. The bill thus symbolizes the gap between American ideals of progress and the reality of captured institutions.

## Historical Context

Child labor was a major reform issue in the Progressive Era (1890s–1920s). The first federal child labor law (the Keating-Owen Act) was passed in 1916 — five years after the novel's publication — and was subsequently struck down by the Supreme Court in 1918. Du Bois's inclusion of the Child Labor Bill in the novel reflects the contemporary political landscape in which reformers were building momentum for federal regulation. The novel accurately captures the role of industrial trusts in opposing such legislation, as well as the strategic use of amendments to weaken or kill reform bills.

## Related Themes

The child labor issue intersects with the novel's broader thematic concerns. Economically, it connects to the cotton industry's exploitation of labor at every level — from Southern Black sharecroppers to Northern factory children. Politically, it illustrates how economic power translates into legislative control. Morally, it serves as a test that separates characters who act on principle from those who accommodate power. Racially, it exists within the novel's complex portrait of progressive reform, where even well-intentioned white organizations like the Civic Club operate within a segregated framework.
