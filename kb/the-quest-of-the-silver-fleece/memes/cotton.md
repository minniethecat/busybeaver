# Cotton

The dominant agricultural commodity of the American South and the economic foundation upon which the world of *The Quest of the Silver Fleece* is built—the "Silver Fleece" of the novel's title itself referring to cotton. In the context of Du Bois's novel, cotton is far more than a crop. It is the economic engine of [[tooms-county|Tooms County]], [[alabama|Alabama]], the commodity that binds black laborers to white landowners in a system barely distinguishable from slavery, and the raw material whose manufacture sparks dreams of industrial empire. Cotton is simultaneously the source of Southern [[economics|wealth]] and the mechanism of [[race|racial]]ized economic [[greed|exploitation]] (006-001).

## Historical Context

At the turn of the twentieth century, cotton was the United States' leading export commodity and the foundation of the Southern [[economics|economy]]. The crop was cultivated predominantly by African American labor under the sharecropping and tenant farming systems that replaced slavery after the Civil War. These systems, while nominally based on contract, often replicated the power dynamics of the plantation through debt [[slavery|peonage]], legal coercion, and [[race|racial]] violence.

Du Bois wrote *The Quest of the Silver Fleece* in 1911, at a time when cotton prices were rising and Northern [[wealth|capital]] was increasingly invested in Southern agriculture and industry. The novel's attention to the economics of cotton—from field to factory to international [[economics|trade]]—reflects Du Bois's broader argument that the "Negro problem" was fundamentally an economic problem rooted in the organization of labor and land.

## Cotton as Economic Foundation

The entire [[economics|economy]] of [[tooms-county|Tooms County]] revolves around cotton. The [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]] plantation and surrounding farms depend on cotton cultivation, with black [[the-tenant-farmers|tenant farmers]] and laborers [[labor|work]]ing the [[field|fields]] under conditions that perpetuate the economic subjugation of the post-Reconstruction era. The cotton market—its prices, its buyers, its speculators—determines the fate of characters across the social spectrum (004-045)(004-085).

The cotton [[economics|trade]] extends beyond farming into [[economics|commerce]] and industry. Cotton warehouses dominate the skyline of the local town, and the chimneys of processing facilities smoke beyond them, marking the first stages of the commodity chain that connects [[alabama|Alabama]] [[field|fields]] to Northern and European [[cotton-mill|mill]]s (004-075)(004-076).

## Scientific and Reform Interest

[[bles-alwyn|Bles Alwyn]] approaches cotton with scientific curiosity, stu[[death|dying]] its cultivation and [[dream|dreaming]] of improving agricultural practices. For him, cotton represents a path to knowledge and self-[[progress|improvement]] through education (003-002)(003-039).

[[mary-taylor|Mary Taylor]], the Northern [[the-school|school]]teacher, studies cotton economics as part of her broader investigation into Southern conditions. She gathers data on production, land ownership, and market dynamics, seeing in cotton the key to understanding—and potentially [[philanthropy|reform]]ing—the region's social structure (004-001)(004-084).

## The Cotton Trust Vision

[[john-taylor|John Taylor]] embodies the industrial ambition that cotton enables. He en[[dream|vision]]s a vast trust that would [[power|control]] cotton manufacturing across America, from raw fiber to finished cloth—a monopoly that would make him one of the most powerful men in the nation. His [[dream|vision]] reveals how cotton, as a commodity, links Southern agriculture to Northern [[wealth|capital]] in a web of mutual dependence and [[greed|exploitation]] (006-048)(006-050)(006-068).

## Cotton as Labor

For characters like [[zora|Zora]], cotton is the reality of physical labor. She picks cotton in the [[field|fields]] to earn money for her education, her hands [[labor|work]]ing the same crop that fills the dreams of speculators and industrialists. The cotton field is where [[love|romance]], hardship, and survival intersect (005-053)(005-059)(007-107).

## Symbolic Significance

The novel's title—*The Quest of the Silver Fleece*—elevates cotton to the level of myth. The "Silver Fleece" invokes both the Golden Fleece of Greek legend (a prize pursued by heroes) and the literal appearance of cotton bolls shining silver-white in the [[alabama|Alabama]] sun. This dual meaning frames cotton as both a precious object of [[ambition|aspiration]] and a source of human suffering.

The imagery of the cotton field recurs throughout the novel as a space of beauty and brutality: "the golden-green of waving cotton [[field|fields]]" that catches the eye of [[bles-alwyn|Bles]], the "bursting bolls" attended by "black attendant earth," the "yellowish cotton looming and swaying" on wagons bound for market (004-007)(006-001)(007-028).
