# The Cotton Trust Scheme

The cotton trust that [[john-taylor|John Taylor]] en[[dream|vision]]s is not yet a functioning organization but a proposal—and the proposal itself is revelatory. It shows how [[wealth|capital]] was reorganizing itself in the early twentieth century and what that reorganization meant for the cotton South.

## Structure and Ambition

The trust would be a vertically integrated monopoly [[power|control]]ling cotton from field to factory: owning the land or securing exclusive supply contracts, operating the gins and [[cotton-mill|mill]]s, managing distribution and sales. It would be headquartered in [[new-york|New York]], financed by Northern [[bank|banks]], and would make [[john-taylor|John Taylor]] one of the [[wealth|riches]]t men in America (006-048)(006-050).

## The Trust as Threat

To [[colonel-cresswell|Colonel Cresswell]], the trust represents a threat to the planter class's traditional [[power|control]] of cotton. Northern [[wealth|capital]] would displace Southern landed [[economics|wealth]] as the dominant force in the cotton [[economics|economy]]. The conflict between [[john-taylor|Taylor]] and [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]] is thus more than personal—it is a conflict between two eras of American [[wealth|capital]]ism (006-058)(006-068).

## The Trust and Labor

For the black laborers who actually grow and pick cotton, the trust promises only intensified [[greed|exploitation]]. Vertical integration means more efficient extraction of value at every stage—and the people at the bottom of the chain would feel the squeeze most acutely.
