# Mary Taylor's Arrival in Alabama

[[mary-taylor|Mary Taylor]]'s journey from New England to [[tooms-county|Tooms County]] is both a physical passage and a psychological crossing. She arrives carrying one set of assumptions about race, education, and the South; she will spend the novel discovering how inadequate those assumptions are.

## The Journey South

Traveling from New England to [[alabama|Alabama]] in the early twentieth century meant crossing not just a geographic distance but a civilizational boundary. The landscape changes, the social codes shift, and the traveler—especially a white Northern woman—enters a world organized by rules she has been taught not to see.

## First Impressions

[[mary-taylor|Mary]]'s first encounters with [[tooms-county|Tooms County]]—its cotton [[field|fields]], its [[race|racial]] hierarchy, its poverty alongside its [[economics|wealth]]—initiate the long process of her political education. She sees enough to know she does not understand what she sees. The novel begins, in this sense, with an arrival that is also a disorientation.
