# Despair

Despair is the emotional ground against which the novel's affirmations must prove themselves. Du Bois does not flinch from depicting the depths to which his characters can fall—the "sickening weariness and panting despair" that overtakes them in [[the-swamp-land|the swamp]]'s darkness (009-001), the "dumb despair" of defeat (014-009), the "perplexed despair" of those who try to help and fail (007-044). [[hope|Hope]], in this novel, is not the absence of despair but the refusal to let it have the last word.

## The Geography of Despair

The [[the-swamp|swamp]], which is also the novel's space of dreams, is simultaneously its geography of despair. "The great roots, twined in ominous shadows" (009-001) evoke a world in which effort is swallowed by an indifferent nature—a metaphor for the [[society|social order]] that seems designed to absorb and nullify every attempt at black [[progress|advancement]]. The [[the-swamp|swamp]]'s doubleness—dream-space and despair-space—is the novel's formal acknowledgment that the conditions that make hope possible are the same conditions that make despair inevitable.

## Despair and Action

The novel's treatment of despair is action-oriented. [[bles-alwyn|Bles]], confronting obstacles, experiences "despairing anger" (012-163)—despair that does not paralyze but fuels resistance. The novel distinguishes between despair that leads to resignation and despair that leads to renewed determination. Its characters are measured by which kind of despair they experience and what they do with it.
