# Escape

"That she could not escape it" (003-012)—the phrase recurs throughout the novel, naming a central tension: the desire to escape a condition and the impossibility of doing so. Escape is both the deepest longing of the novel's characters and, in its fullest sense, something the novel suggests may be unachievable.

> Then there was the fact of their color: it was a fact so insistent, so fatal she almost said at times, that she could not escape it. Theoretically she had always treated it with disdainful ease. [003-012]

## The Geography of Escape

The physical escape from the South—from [[tooms-county|Tooms County]], from the [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]] plantation, from the [[the-swamp|swamp]]—is the most literal form. Characters flee to school, to [[new-york|New York]], to [[washington-dc|Washington]], to any place that promises freedom from the Southern [[race|racial]] order. Yet the novel complicates this geography:

> "Then we must escape her," he said gayly. "See! yonder lies the Silver Fleece spread across the brown back of the world; let's get a bit of it, and hide it here in the swamp, and comb it, and tend it, and make it worth something." [005-082]

[[Bles-Alwyn|Bles]] says this of Elspeth, recognizing that escape is as much about leaving certain people and relationships as it is about leaving places. His words also suggest a counter-intuitive form of escape—not flight away from but purposeful work within, transforming the swamp's raw material into something of value.

## The Impossibility of Full Escape

The novel repeatedly suggests that certain conditions cannot be escaped—they must be confronted, transformed, or endured. Race, in particular, is presented as inescapable: "the fact of the boy's color had quite escaped her" (003-052) is stated only to be corrected—the color line cannot truly be escaped because it is a social fact, not a personal attribute. Even [[Zora]], who rises higher than almost any other black character in the novel, cannot escape the constraints of being a black woman in early twentieth-century America.

## Escape into the Natural World

The novel offers one form of escape that is not flight but immersion: escape into the [[the-swamp|swamp]], into the forest, into the sensory richness of the natural world. When Zora "escaped into the air" (004-088) from an awkward social encounter, nature provides temporary refuge.

> Miss Taylor looked up a little puzzled, and became aware of a battery of eyes and ears. Everybody seemed craning and listening, and she felt a sudden embarrassment and a sense of half-veiled hostility. [004-088]

This form of escape is momentary—a respite rather than a permanent solution—but it recurs throughout the novel as a counterpoint to the more ambitious projects of social escape through [[education]] or migration.
