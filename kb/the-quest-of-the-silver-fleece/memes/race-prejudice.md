# Race Prejudice

**[[race|Race]] prejudice** functions as the invisible architecture of the world Du Bois depicts in *The Quest of the Silver Fleece*. It is not merely individual bigotry but a systemic force that shapes [[education|educational access]], [[debt|economic opportunity]], political participation, and even romantic [[love|relationship]]s. Du Bois, a trained sociologist, embeds [[race|racial]] prejudice in the novel's narrative structure itself—it is the obstacle against which every major character must struggle.

## In the Novel

[[race|Race]] prejudice operates at multiple levels in the narrative. At its most explicit, it appears in the attitudes of white Southern characters. But Du Bois is careful to show that prejudice is not merely a Southern phenomenon:

> "...are not always impervious to race prejudice." [PN=022-062]

This observation, made in the context of Northern philanthropy, reveals Du Bois's critique that [[race|racial]] discrimination is national in scope, infecting even those who profess benevolent intentions toward Black Americans.

## Du Bois's Sociological Framework

Du Bois's portrayal of race prejudice in the novel draws directly from his sociological [[labor|work]], particularly *The Souls of Black Folk* (1903). The concept of "double-consciousness"—the sense of always looking at oneself through the eyes of others—permeates the novel. Characters like [[bles-alwyn|Bles Alwyn]] and [[zora|Zora]] must navigate both the Black community's [[hope|expectation]]s and the white world's judgments.

The novel also anticipates Du Bois's later concept of the "color line" as the defining problem of the twentieth century. In *The Quest of the Silver Fleece*, the color line is drawn through every institution: the [[the-school|school]], [[religion|the church]], the cotton market, and the ballot box.

## The Novel's Argument

Du Bois uses the novel form to make an argument that his sociological essays could not: that race prejudice is not an abstract social problem but a lived experience that shapes individual destinies. The novel traces how prejudice:

1. **Distorts economic relations** — Black farmers cannot get fair prices for their [[the-silver-fleece|cotton]]
2. **Corrupts education** — White philanthropists impose their own agendas on Black [[the-school|school]]s
3. **Poisoned politics** — The [[democratic-party|Democratic Party]] uses race to divide poor whites and Blacks
