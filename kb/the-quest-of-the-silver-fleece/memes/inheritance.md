# Inheritance

In *The Quest of the Silver Fleece*, inheritance is what death transfers—not merely property but power, memory, and moral obligation. The novel asks: what does one generation owe the next, and what does the present owe the past?

## The Meaning of Inheritance

Inheritance in the novel operates on two levels. There is the literal inheritance of land and property—the [[cresswell-plantation|Cresswell plantation]] passes from [[colonel-cresswell|Colonel Cresswell]] to his heirs, and this transfer has material consequences for everyone who lives on that land. And there is the figurative inheritance of history: the legacy of [[slavery]], the accumulated weight of racial injustice, and the cultural traditions that sustain the black community.

## In the Novel

### Land and Property

The [[cresswell-plantation|Cresswell plantation]] is the novel's central site of contested inheritance. Colonel Cresswell's death redistributes power over the land, but the question of who *deserves* to inherit it is never settled. The black families who have worked the land for generations have a moral claim that the law does not recognize. "A week after the funeral of Colonel Cresswell" (038-029), the machinery of legal inheritance continues, indifferent to the moral claims of those who labored.

### The Inheritance of Slavery

The novel treats the legacy of [[slavery]] as an unwanted inheritance that the nation refuses to acknowledge. The characters live in a world shaped by slavery's aftermath—the [[color-line]], the plantation economy, the racial hierarchy—but the white characters, particularly, deny that this inheritance creates any obligation. Du Bois's point is that inheritance is not chosen: one inherits history whether one acknowledges it or not.

### Cultural Inheritance

Against the inheritance of property and injustice, the novel sets the inheritance of culture: the spirituals, the folk wisdom, the communal traditions that [[zora|Zora]] carries from the [[the-swamp|swamp]] to the wider world. This inheritance is not material but it is real—it is what sustains the black community when material resources are denied.

## Moral Dimension

Du Bois presents inheritance as a moral question. What does the present generation owe to past suffering? What does the white South owe to the descendants of the enslaved? What do parents owe to children? The novel does not answer these questions, but it insists that they are the right questions to ask.
