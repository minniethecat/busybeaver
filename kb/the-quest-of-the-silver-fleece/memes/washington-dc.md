# Washington D.C.

Washington D.C. appears in *The Quest of the Silver Fleece* not as a setting for dramatic scenes but as a gravitational center of political power. The decisions made there — about tariffs, about education funding, about the enforcement (or non-enforcement) of federal law in the South — shape the conditions of life in [[tooms-county|Tooms County]] as surely as the weather shapes the cotton crop. The distance between Washington and Tooms County is geographic but the connection is structural.

## Political Center

The federal government's presence in the post-Reconstruction South was complex and contradictory. Federal troops had been withdrawn in 1877 as part of the Compromise that ended Reconstruction, effectively abandoning Black Southerners to the "home rule" of the white Democratic regimes that swiftly enacted disfranchisement and segregation. Yet Washington still held constitutional authority over the states and controlled policies — trade, education funding, legal enforcement — that directly affected the cotton economy.

This contradiction is at the heart of the novel's political vision: Washington is simultaneously the source of potential justice (the constitutional guarantee of equal protection) and the enabler of injustice (through non-enforcement and political compromise).

## The Cotton Tariff

The tariff on cotton and cotton goods was one of the most contentious political issues of the era. Southern planters wanted low tariffs to facilitate cotton exports; Northern manufacturers wanted high tariffs to protect domestic textile industries. The novel's characters have interests on both sides of this debate: [[colonel-cresswell|Colonel Cresswell]] wants access to world markets, while Northern industrialists want protection. Washington is where these competing interests are adjudicated, and the outcomes determine the price of cotton — and therefore the fate of everyone who depends on it in [[alabama|Alabama]].

## Education Policy

Federal education funding — or its absence — is another Washington concern that echoes through Tooms County. The [[the-school|school]] that [[mary-taylor|Mary Taylor]] and [[miss-smith|Miss Smith]] sustain depends ultimately on political decisions made far from Alabama, by men who have never seen a cotton field or met a Black child. The novel's treatment of this theme reflects Du Bois's own political advocacy: he believed that federal investment in Black education was essential to genuine Reconstruction, and that Washington's abandonment of this responsibility after 1877 was a moral and political catastrophe.

## The Political Novel

Washington's structural role in the novel reflects a broader feature of Du Bois's fiction: his determination to write a *political* novel, not merely a regional pastoral. The cotton fields of Tooms County and the hearing rooms of Washington are part of the same system. By keeping Washington offstage but ever-present, Du Bois forces readers to recognize that the "local" events of the novel are shaped by national forces that most novels of the period preferred to ignore.
