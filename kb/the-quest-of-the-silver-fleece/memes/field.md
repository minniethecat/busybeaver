# The Cotton Fields

The cotton fields in *The Quest of the Silver Fleece* stretch "full of dreams, piled high and gold" (001-067) behind [[the-swamp-land|the swamp]]. They are the economic engine of the [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]] plantation and the primary site of black labor—simultaneously a landscape of extraordinary beauty and a theater of systematic [[greed|exploitation]].

## Landscape

Du Bois renders the cotton field in painterly terms. The cotton "covers the field like foam, all misty" (003-042), and the "golden-green of waving cotton fields" (004-007) creates a seductive aesthetic that the novel both celebrates and interrogates. The beauty of the fields is real, but it is produced by labor that the novel exposes. "Boys at [[labor|work]] in a young cotton field" (003-028) are glimpsed in passing—the human cost of the [[preacher|pastor]]al vista.

## Labor

The field is where the novel's economic [[love|relationship]]s become visible. [[zora|Zora]] "appeared in the cotton-field beside [[bles-alwyn|Bles]], and picked" (005-059), demonstrating her competence in the [[labor|work]] that defines black life. But "two white men watching" (003-048) from the edge of the [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]] fields make clear that the field is a surveilled space, where black labor benefits white [[wealth|capital]]. The "white woman in the field" (008-013) disrupts the [[race|racial]] [[economics|economy]], prompting scandal.

## Symbolic Function

The field mediates between [[the-swamp-land|the swamp]] (wild freedom) and the mansion (cultivated [[economics|wealth]]). "Toward the [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]] fields" (009-029) is a directional marker in the novel's moral geography—to move toward the fields is to move toward labor, [[duty|obligation]], and the visible order of the plantation [[economics|economy]]. Yet the fields are also "full of dreams" (001-067), containing the possibility that labor might yield more than subsistence.
