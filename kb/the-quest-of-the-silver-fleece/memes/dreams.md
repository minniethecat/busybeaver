# Dreams

"If you wants to know what life is," Zora tells Bles in the opening chapter, "life is just a lot of dreams" (001-073). The claim, delivered in the childlike dialect of the rural South, contains the novel's most radical assertion: dreams are not escape from reality but its very substance.

## Definition in the Novel

In *The Quest of the Silver Fleece*, dreams operate on several levels. They are the literal dreams of sleeping characters. They are daydreams—the wandering imagination of characters suspended between work and aspiration.

They are also collective dreams: the shared vision of a better life that sustains the black community through hardship. Du Bois refuses to separate these meanings, insisting that the boundary between dreaming and living is porous.

## In the Novel

### Zora's Dreams

[[zora|Zora]] is the novel's primary dreamer. She dreams in the swamp—"I creeps in 'mongst the dreams; they hangs there like big flowers, dripping dew and sugar and blood" (001-073)—and she dreams of the [[the-silver-fleece|Silver Fleece]], the cotton crop that represents both material wealth and spiritual vision. "All the vacation days Bles worked on the farm, and Zora read and dreamed and studied in the wood" (005-059). For Zora, dreaming is a form of work, a way of cultivating the interior life.

### Bles's Dreams

[[bles-alwyn|Bles]] also dreams, though his dreams are more worldly. He dreams of education, of advancement, of a life beyond the limitations of [[tooms-county|Tooms County]]. His dream of planting the [[the-silver-fleece|Silver Fleece]] with Zora—"today Zora was to show him the long dreamed of spot for the planting of the Silver Fleece" (007-095)—marries aspiration to action.

### The Collective Dream

Beyond individual dreamers, the novel evokes a collective dreaming. "Black men and mules had staggered beneath its burden, while deep songs welled in the hearts of men; for the Fleece was goodly and gleaming and soft, and men dreamed of the gold it would buy" (018-001). The cotton field becomes a site of communal dreaming, where labor and longing merge.

### Dreams Deferred

But dreams in the novel are also threatened. [[mary-taylor|Mary Taylor]]'s "dream had been a postgraduate course at Bryn Mawr; but that was out of the question until money was earned" (003-002). The [[the-color-line|color line]] constrains what black people are permitted to dream. [[zora|Zora]] herself experiences the terror of dreams that turn dark: "Slowly Zora's hands fell and her eyes blinked as though waking from some awful dream" (020-052).

## Symbolic Meaning

The swamp is the novel's primary dream-space. It is "full of dreams, piled high and burning" (001-067). Dreams in the swamp are "big flowers, dripping dew and sugar and blood—red, red blood" (001-073). The swamp dreams are generative—they produce the Silver Fleece—but they are also sites of danger and sacrifice. To dream in Du Bois's world is to risk something.

## Historical Context

Du Bois's treatment of dreams reflects the broader African American tradition of "dreaming" freedom—from the spirituals that dreamed of the promised land to the political dreams of Reconstruction. The novel insists that black people's dreams are not escapist fantasy but a form of knowledge: to dream of a better world is to understand that the present world is unjust.
