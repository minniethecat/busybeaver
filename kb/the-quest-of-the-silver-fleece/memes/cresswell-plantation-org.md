# The Cresswell Plantation Enterprise

Beyond its physical existence as land and buildings, the [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]] plantation is an organizational structure—a business enterprise designed to produce cotton at the lowest possible cost by [[power|control]]ling every input: land, labor, [[debt|credit]], and market access.

## Organizational Structure

The plantation is organized hierarchically: the [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]] family at the top (ownership and management), overseers in the middle (super[[dream|vision]] and discipline), and [[the-tenant-farmers|tenant farmers]] and field laborers at the base (production). Each level extracts value from the level below, with the largest share accumulating at the top.

## Labor Management

The plantation's labor system is its organizational core. Tenant farmers are bound by debt and contract; field laborers by wages and necessity. The system is designed to maximize labor extraction while minimizing labor cost—a calculus that treats black [[labor|workers]] as inputs rather than as human beings (004-054).

## The Credit Function

The plantation operates its own internal [[debt|credit]] system through the commissary, advancing supplies to tenants against future harvests at interest rates that ensure perpetual [[debt|indebtedness]]. This [[debt|credit]] function is as central to the plantation's profitability as cotton cultivation itself.

## Historical Basis

The [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]] plantation is modeled on the actual plantation system of the post-Reconstruction Black Belt, which Du Bois studied extensively as a sociologist. The organizational innovations—debt [[slavery|peonage]] replacing chattel slavery, the crop lien system, the furnish—were real historical developments that shaped the lives of [[cotton-mill|mill]]ions of black Southerners.
