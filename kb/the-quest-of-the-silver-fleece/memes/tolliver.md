# Mr. Tolliver

Mr. Tolliver is both planter and politician—a man whose thousand acres of Alabama soil give weight to his words in the state legislature. Du Bois uses him to illustrate how land ownership translated directly into political power in the post-Reconstruction South, and how that power was deployed against the interests of black education.

## Character Overview

> "Cresswell has ten thousand acres and Mr. Tolliver a thousand." [004-083]

The novel's first mention of Tolliver frames him in the only terms that matter in Tooms County: acres. His thousand acres make him a man of consequence, though his holdings are dwarfed by [[colonel-cresswell|Colonel Cresswell]]'s ten thousand. This hierarchy of land—and the power it confers—structures every political decision Tolliver makes.

Tolliver is a functionary of the planter class, not an independent actor. His votes in the legislature are determined not by conviction but by calculation—a responsiveness to the interests of larger landowners that the novel presents as the normal operation of Southern politics.

## Key Relationships

Tolliver's relationship with [[colonel-cresswell|Colonel Cresswell]] defines his political horizon. As a smaller planter, he depends on the Colonel's patronage and dare not oppose him. His relationship with his tenants—black farmers who work his land—is one of economic coercion papered over with paternalist rhetoric.

## Key Scenes

> It was her neighbor, Tolliver—a gaunt, yellow-faced white man with a thin, hard mouth. [012-136]

This physical description—gaunt, yellow-faced, hard-mouthed—is Du Bois's shorthand for the type: the poor white turned middling planter whose resentment of black progress is sharper than the Colonel's aristocratic indifference.

> Tolliver, in a fit of rage, had sold land to the school—a thorn in his flesh ever since. [012-141]

The school's acquisition of Tolliver land is presented as an accident of temper rather than a principled decision. His subsequent regret—"a thorn in his flesh"—drives his political opposition to the school. He is a man whose grudges become policy.

> The transfer of the Tolliver land had not yet been effected. [016-003]

This single line, buried in a chapter of financial maneuvering, reveals the precariousness of the school's position. Tolliver's land, sold in anger, can be unmade by the same emotion. The legal machinery of property transfer is another arena where black institutions are vulnerable to white caprice.

## Character Arc

Tolliver does not change; he is the fixed point against which change is measured. His thousand acres, his legislative seat, his thin hard mouth—these are constants in a landscape where black aspiration is perpetually threatened. He represents not the spectacular evil of the [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswells]] but the banal hostility of the middling white man whose interests align perfectly with white supremacy and who feels no need to examine that alignment.
