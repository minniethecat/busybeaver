# The Tenant Farmers of Tooms County

The tenant farmers of [[tooms-county|Tooms County]] are not a formal organization but a class — a collective defined by their common relationship to land, labor, and debt (004-054). Their condition is the human face of the cotton economy's organizational logic. Living on [[colonel-cresswell|Colonel Cresswell]]'s land in cabins clustered along the plantation's margins, they constitute the labor force that makes the cotton economy possible — and the community whose fate is the novel's deepest moral concern.

## The Tenant System

Under the tenant farming and sharecropping system, Black families worked plots of Cresswell land in exchange for a share of the cotton crop — typically half, minus deductions for supplies advanced during the growing season. The system was presented as a contract between free parties; in practice, the tenant had no bargaining power and no alternative. The crop lien system, in which future harvests were pledged against current debts, created a cycle of indebtedness that was functionally indistinguishable from peonage.

The novel depicts this system not as a relic of slavery but as its successor — a new mechanism for extracting Black labor that was legally distinct from chattel slavery but economically continuous with it. The "free" tenant farmer who owes more at the end of the season than at the beginning is, in Du Bois's analysis, a worker in all but name.

## Collective Condition

The tenant farmers share a common predicament: landlessness, indebtedness, and dependence on the landowner's goodwill. Their individual fates vary — some years are better than others — but the structure within which they operate ensures that the collective outcome is immiseration. The novel treats this shared condition as the basis for a potential collective identity: the tenant farmers are a class *in themselves*, even if they are not yet a class *for themselves*.

The cabins where they live, the fields where they work, and the credit arrangements that bind them constitute a social world parallel to but invisible from the "Big House." The novel insists on making this world visible.

## Resistance and Survival

The tenant farmers are not passive victims. The novel traces the quiet forms of resistance — foot-dragging, deception, mutual aid, the strategic use of ignorance — through which they preserve dignity and survive within an oppressive system. These are not the dramatic gestures of heroic resistance but the daily practices of people who have learned to endure. The novel's attention to these practices is itself a political act: it refuses the stereotype of the passive, contented Black peasant that dominated Southern literature of the period.

The formation of the Farmers' League represents an attempt to transform this latent collective identity into organized collective action — to move from survival to resistance.

## Historical Context

The tenant farming system that Du Bois depicts was the dominant form of agricultural labor in the post-Reconstruction Black Belt. By 1910, more than 75% of Black farmers in Alabama were tenants or sharecroppers. The system was enforced not only by economic necessity but by violence — the threat of lynching, arson, or eviction for any tenant who challenged the landowner's authority. Du Bois's depiction of the tenant farmers is both documentary (an accurate portrait of rural Black life) and polemical (an argument for collective economic organization as the path to liberation).
