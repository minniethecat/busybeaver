# Colonel Cresswell

Colonel Cresswell is the embodiment of the old Southern ruling class—a planter-aristocrat whose power rests on land, [[family|lineage]], and the labor of black tenants. He is not a villain in the melodramatic sense but something more chilling: a man for whom the [[race|racial]] and economic order is simply the natural order of things.

## Character Overview

Cresswell owns the largest plantation in [[tooms-county|Tooms County]] and exercises the [[power|authority]] that land ownership confers in an agrarian [[economics|economy]]. He is described as "a high-bred gentleman of the old [[the-school|school]]," and his manners are those of a man who has never needed to raise his voice because his power is structural, not personal (004-083). He is one of the "leading landowners" whose [[power|control]] of the land is the foundation of the cotton [[economics|economy]] (004-082).

## Power and Paternalism

The Colonel represents a specifically Southern form of white supremacy—paternalistic rather than virulently hostile, confident rather than defensive. He speaks of "his" black tenants with a proprietary affection that masks the brutal economics of the system he [[power|control]]s. His power does not depend on violence (though violence is always available) but on the legal and economic structures that make resistance futile.

## The Cresswell System

The Cresswell plantation is not merely a farm but a system of social organization. The black families who [[labor|work]] Cresswell land are bound by debt, [[tradition|custom]], and the absence of alternatives. The plantation includes not only cotton [[field|fields]] but the entire apparatus of [[power|control]]—the commissary where tenants must buy supplies, the ledger where debts accumulate, the [[power|authority]] that settles disputes and dispenses punishments (004-054).

## Historical Significance

Du Bois uses Cresswell to dramatize the continuity between the slaveholding South and the post-Reconstruction South. The Colonel's grandfather owned slaves; the Colonel owns land and [[power|control]]s labor through debt. The legal form has changed but the structure of domination persists. Cresswell is not a relic of the past but an adaptive figure who has reconstituted planter power under new legal conditions.
