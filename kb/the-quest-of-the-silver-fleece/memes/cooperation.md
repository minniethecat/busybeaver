# Cooperation

**Cooperation** emerges in *The Quest of the Silver Fleece* as a hopeful alternative to the competitive and exploitative economic systems that dominate the post-Reconstruction South.

## In the Novel

Zora becomes the novel's primary advocate for cooperative action. Addressing a gathering of rural families, she speaks with plain eloquence:

> She talked of cooperation and refuges and other efforts; she praised their way of adopting children into their own homes; and then finally she told them of the land she was buying for new tenants and the helping hands she needed. The people were listening breathless to a kind of straightforward talk which they seldom heard and for which they were hungering. [033-079]

This scene captures the essence of Zora's cooperative vision: practical, grounded in community tradition, yet ambitiously forward-looking. She does not lecture — she connects, weaving local practice into a larger strategy.

Bles Alwyn provides the operational backbone. The novel notes that "for the development of her plans she needed Bles Alwyn's constant cooperation" [035-073]. His role as business manager of the school translates Zora's vision into practical operations. Zora recognizes that economic independence must precede any meaningful alliance with white laborers: "Meantime the Negro must strengthen himself and bring to the alliance as much independent economic strength as possible" [035-073].

Their partnership — Zora the visionary, Bles the executor — models the kind of collaborative effort the novel presents as essential for social progress.

## Thematic Significance

Du Bois positions cooperation against the backdrop of an economic system designed to keep African Americans dependent and impoverished. Sharecropping, the crop-lien system, and market manipulation all work to isolate individuals and prevent collective bargaining. The cooperative model represents self-determination and community solidarity — themes central to Du Bois's broader social philosophy.

The novel suggests that individual effort, however noble, is insufficient without collective organization. Zora's cooperative is not charity but mutual aid: every participant contributes and benefits. This vision anticipates Du Bois's later work on economic cooperation as a cornerstone of African American uplift, making the novel not merely a story but a platform for social thought.
