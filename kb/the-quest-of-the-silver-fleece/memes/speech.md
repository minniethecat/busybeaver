# The Speech

The speech in *The Quest of the Silver Fleece* is a public performance that carries economic and political weight. "The speech at the Bethel Literary" (025-021) is reported in the newspapers, establishing the speaker as a figure of public consequence. A good speaker commands "twenty-five dollars a speech" (025-052), transforming oratory into a marketable skill.

## The Bethel Literary Speech

The Bethel Literary speech is a milestone in the novel's political narrative. Someone has "masterly read an account of the speech at the Bethel Literary" (025-021)—the speech is not just delivered but reported, circulated, consumed. It becomes a text as much as an event, its impact multiplied by the newspapers that carry it beyond the room where it was spoken. The "speech was a softened English, purely" (024-019) pronounced, indicating the careful self-presentation required of a black speaker addressing a mixed audience.

## Speech as Commodity

The novel is explicit about the economics of oratory. "Him at twenty-five dollars a speech. If he balks, raise to fifty" (025-052)—the speaker is a hired performer, and the speech is a product whose price can be negotiated upward. This commodification of black eloquence is one of the novel's sharpest ironies: the speech that advocates for [[race|racial]] justice is itself bought and sold in the marketplace.

## The Power of the Spoken Word

Despite its commodification, the speech retains transformative power. "That speech had moved her. If he could think" (024-078)—the speech moves listeners in ways that written argument cannot. When the speaker "fairly burst into speech" (024-073), the physical eruption of language suggests that the speech is not merely a performance but an authentic expression that exceeds the speaker's [[power|control]].
