# Tom Teerswell

Tom Teerswell is a minor but distinctive character in the Washington, D.C. sections of the novel. Handsome, indolent, and cynical, he moves through the capital's social circles as Caroline Wynn's long-time escort, embodying a particular type of urban sophistication that Du Bois contrasts with the earnestness of characters like Bles Alwyn.

## Character Overview

Teerswell is introduced at a Washington social gathering where he is listed among the guests alongside Bles Alwyn and others (022-116). He is described as "handsome and indolent, with indecision in his face and a cynical voice" (022-116). For years, he has served as Caroline Wynn's escort (024-002), placing him at the center of the novel's Washington social world.

Physically, Teerswell is described as having "golden yellow" skin tone in a scene that catalogs the range of complexions among the Black elite at a Washington gathering (024-018). This detail situates him within Du Bois's nuanced treatment of color and class in African American society.

## Key Relationships

- **With [[caroline-wynn]]** — Teerswell has been Miss Wynn's escort "for years" (024-002). When she becomes attracted to Bles Alwyn, Teerswell is "furious and began to plot vengeance" (024-004), revealing his possessiveness and capacity for resentment.
- **With [[bles-alwyn]]** — Teerswell views Alwyn as a rival for Caroline Wynn's attention. His class condescension toward the Southerner is evident when he dismisses Alwyn as "the damned Southern upstart" (026-040).

## Key Scenes

### The Washington Gathering

At a social event, Teerswell is observed interacting with various guests. When Bles Alwyn is elected to a position that Teerswell coveted, his resentment deepens. "Teerswell, who was speaking, affected not to notice them" (024-053) when Alwyn and Miss Wynn arrive together.

### Plotting with Stillings

Teerswell's most active narrative moment comes when he visits [[sam-stillings]] to conspire against Bles Alwyn. "That night Teerswell called on him in his modest lodgings, where over hot whiskey and water they talked" (026-039). During this conversation, he vents his class-based contempt: "The damned Southern upstart... Do you mean to say he's actually slated for the place?" (026-040). Teerswell mixes himself "another stiff dram" (026-042) as he nurses his grievances.

### Confrontation at the Political Meeting

During a heated political discussion, Teerswell advocates for a strategy of division: "Still I insist: divide and conquer" (024-056). His cynical political philosophy contrasts with the idealism of the novel's protagonists.

## Character Arc

Teerswell undergoes little transformation, functioning instead as a foil. His indolence, cynicism, and class-bound resentment highlight by contrast the ambition, sincerity, and upward mobility that Bles Alwyn represents. Where Alwyn rises through merit, Teerswell relies on social connections and inherited position.

## Thematic Significance

Teerswell embodies several themes central to Du Bois's critique of the Black elite in Washington. His color consciousness ("golden yellow"), his class snobbery toward Southerners, and his resentment-driven politics all illustrate the internal divisions that Du Bois saw as weakening African American collective advancement. His character also represents the temptations of cynicism and indolence that the novel's more virtuous characters must resist.
