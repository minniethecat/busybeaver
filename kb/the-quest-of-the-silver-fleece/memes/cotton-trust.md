# Cotton Trust

The cotton trust in *The Quest of the Silver Fleece* is the economic antagonist: the concentrated financial power that controls the cotton market, exploits black labor, and threatens the fragile autonomy that [[zora|Zora]] and [[bles-alwyn|Bles]] seek to build. It is capitalism given a name and a face.

## In the Novel

### The Trust's Design

The cotton trust is first described through the ambitions of financiers who had "a vision of a vast trust of cotton manufacturing covering the land" (006-048). The trust's plan is vertical integration: "a trust to include the raising and manufacturing of cotton in America" (010-110). By controlling both the raw material and its manufacture, the trust aims to "corner the cotton market" and gain "the whip-hand of the industrial world" (010-110).

### The Trust vs. The Farmers

The trust's agenda brings it into direct conflict with the [[farmers-league|Farmers' League]], the cooperative organization that represents black and white cotton farmers. The headline "'League Beats Trust'" (022-006) records a rare victory: "Farmers of South Smash Effort to Bear Market… Send Cotton to Twelve Cents… Common People Triumph." But the victory is temporary. The trust responds by squeezing small mills: "The small mills had not been able to buy cotton when it was low because Cresswell was cornering it in the name of the Farmers' League; now that it was high they could not afford to, and many surrendered to the trust" (035-002).

### Cresswell's Role

[[colonel-cresswell|Colonel Cresswell]] is the trust's local agent. He corners the cotton supply in the name of the League, then uses that position to serve the trust's interests. His manipulation of the cotton exchange (018-054) shows how financial speculation compounds the exploitation of agricultural labor. "It'll knock the cotton trust to hell" (015-013)—the threat to the trust becomes a rallying cry, but the novel registers the trust's resilience.

## Economic Analysis

Du Bois's portrayal of the cotton trust is a work of economic analysis embedded in fiction. The trust exemplifies the concentration of capital that defined the Gilded Age and Progressive Era. By linking the trust to the cotton fields of [[alabama|Alabama]], Du Bois makes visible what most economic commentary of his era ignored: that the trust's profits depend on the exploitation of black agricultural labor.

## Historical Context

The cotton trust fictionalizes the real economic forces that Du Bois analyzed in his sociological work. The early twentieth century saw the rise of industrial trusts and holding companies that consolidated control over entire industries. For the cotton economy, this meant that decisions made in New York boardrooms determined the fate of black sharecroppers in Alabama—a connection the novel makes explicit.
