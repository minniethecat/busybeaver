# The Association

The association in *The Quest of the Silver Fleece* is the organizational form of black civil society. Implied rather than depicted in detail—"a meeting of the trustees" (033-075), the gatherings that accompany [[the-church|church]] and revival—the association is where black communities organize themselves, independent of the white-[[power|control]]led institutions that dominate the novel's visible organizational landscape.

## Voluntary Organization

The association is voluntary where the plantation is coercive. Black Southerners who are compelled to labor in the [[field|fields]], to buy at [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]]'s store, to defer to white [[power|authority]] at every turn, choose to associate with one another in [[the-church|church]], in mutual aid societies, in the informal net[[labor|work]]s of community that the novel gestures toward but does not fully represent. The association is the organizational form of freedom within unfreedom, and its very existence is an act of resistance against a system designed to isolate and control.

## Civil Society

Du Bois is attentive to the infrastructure of black civil society. The "three other collections, a baptism, a meeting of the trustees" (033-075) that accompany the revival are the activities of association—financial, ritual, organizational. These associations are the institutional backbone of a community that the official institutions of Southern society exist to [[power|control]] and exploit. The association is where black people govern themselves, pool resources, and build the collective capacity that the formal economy and political system deny them.

## The Unseen Infrastructure

The novel's focus on the plantation's dominant institutions—the trust, the committee, the firm—leaves the world of black association partly in shadow. But the shadow is itself significant: the associations that are not depicted are the ones that white power does not see and therefore cannot [[power|control]]. From burial societies that ensured dignified death for the poor to fraternal orders like the Prince Hall Masons that provided mutual insurance and political organizing infrastructure, the unseen network of black association was the foundation upon which more visible forms of resistance and uplift were built.

## Historical Context

Black voluntary associations proliferated in the post-Reconstruction South as one of the few institutional spaces not controlled by whites. Mutual aid societies provided sickness and death benefits when white-owned insurance companies refused to cover black customers. Fraternal orders—the Odd Fellows, the Knights of Pythias, the Prince Hall Masons—offered not only material support but training in organizational leadership that produced many of the era's black political leaders. Du Bois himself was deeply involved in this associational life, and his sociological training at Atlanta University included extensive study of black voluntary organizations. The relative invisibility of these associations in the novel reflects not their unimportance but the novel's narrative focus on institutions where racial power was most visibly contested.
