# Family

Family in *The Quest of the Silver Fleece* is at once the most intimate of human bonds and the most powerful mechanism for transmitting [[race|racial]] and class position across generations. Du Bois examines family as it functions for the white [[class|aristocracy]] of the [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]]s, for aspiring black individuals like [[bles-alwyn|Bles]] and [[zora|Zora]], and for the "more than motherless" children left vulnerable by a system that disrupts black kinship. The novel asks: what does it mean to form a family under conditions designed to prevent it?

## Historical Context

Du Bois wrote at a moment when black family structure was a subject of intense political and sociological debate. The legacy of slavery—which had systematically torn black families apart through sale, sexual [[greed|exploitation]], and the denial of legal marriage—continued to shape black family life in the post-Emancipation South. The novel's attention to the fragility and resilience of black kinship reflects Du Bois's insistence that the "Negro problem" was not a problem of black family pathology but of a [[society|social order]] that had never permitted black families to flourish.

## The Cresswell Family: Aristocracy and Its Costs

[[miss-smith|Miss Smith]]'s assessment of the [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]]s is characteristically blunt: "The old man's a fool; the young one a rascal; the girl a ninny." And yet, she adds, "they own us body and soul" (004-006). The paradox of the [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]] family is that its members are individually contemptible but [[community|collective]]ly omnipotent. Family is the vehicle through which land, power, and [[race|racial]] status pass from generation to generation.

The [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]] lineage is narrated as a story of [[greed|accumulation]]: "Old Gen'ral [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]] had niggers and acres no end back dere in Carolina. He brung a part of dem here and here his son, de father of dis [[colonel-cresswell|Colonel Cresswell]], was born" (004-050). The transmission of property—including human property—across generations is what family means in the planter class. [[colonel-cresswell|Colonel Cresswell]] inherits not only land and [[economics|wealth]] but a position in a [[race|racial]] order. [[harry-cresswell|Harry Cresswell]], the third generation, is "well-bred and masterful," his father a man of "much [[pride|family pride]]" (008-003).

## Marriage as Transaction

Marriage in the novel is rarely about love alone. The [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]] marriage to Northern money—"an alliance with Northern [[cotton-mill|mill]]ions"—is "a mésalliance of a sort that pricked him tenderly; it savored grossly of bargain and sale" (020-003). The union of Southern land and Northern [[wealth|capital]] through marriage reproduces the economic order across regional lines while maintaining white supremacy as its shared ground.

[[john-taylor|John Taylor]]'s interest in the [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]] family's marriage prospects is explicitly economic: "To my own family," he asserts, aligning his interests with the [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]] marital alliance (010-170). Family in this register is not a refuge from the market but an extension of it—a vehicle for consolidating [[wealth|capital]] and consolidating power.

## Black Families Under Assault

The novel's black characters navigate family formation under conditions of systematic disruption. [[bles-alwyn|Bles]], at the novel's opening, is already severed from his family of origin. Standing alone in the darkness, he whispers of "the father and old mother, away back yonder beyond the night. They were far away; they would never be as near as once they had been, for he had stepped into the world" (001-002). This exile from the family of origin is the condition of possibility for [[bles-alwyn|Bles]]'s education and his entry into the wider world, but it is also an irreparable loss.

[[zora|Zora]] is "more than motherless" (005-070)—a phrase that captures the particular vulnerability of black children whose kinship net[[labor|work]]s have been shattered by slavery's legacy and its ongoing economic consequences. The [[the-school|school]], under [[miss-smith|Miss Smith]]'s care, attempts to fill the void that disrupted families leave, treating black children "just as you would your own children" (002-016). But this substitution is fragile, dependent on the [[the-school|school]]'s precarious finances and the surrounding white power structure's tolerance.

## The Marriage of Bles and Zora

The novel's central romantic [[love|relationship]]—[[bles-alwyn|Bles]] and [[zora|Zora]]'s—is the site of its deepest meditation on what family might mean for black people in America. Their marriage is simultaneously a personal union and a political act: "with twined hands and arms, and lips ever and again seeking their mates, they walked the leafy way" (015-056). The language is almost spiritual, suggesting that their union transcends the transactional logic that governs [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]] marriages.

Yet the novel does not allow this [[dream|vision]] of marital harmony to remain untroubled. The forces of [[race|racial]] and economic [[greed|exploitation]] that structure [[tooms-county|Tooms County]] intrude upon their [[love|relationship]], testing whether love can survive in a world designed to destroy black intimacy.
