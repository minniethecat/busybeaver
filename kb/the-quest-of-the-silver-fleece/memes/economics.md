# Economics

The economic order of *The Quest of the Silver Fleece* is not a background condition but a foreground subject. Every character's fate is shaped by the cotton economy—who owns the land, who [[labor|work]]s it, who profits from it, and who is crushed by it.

## Cotton as Economic System

The novel treats cotton not as a crop but as an economic system: a net[[labor|work]] of land ownership, [[debt|credit]], labor [[greed|exploitation]], and commodity markets that binds black [[labor|workers]] to white landowners in relations of near-servitude. "Things that give them power and wealth and make them rule" (005-018)—[[zora|Zora]]'s early insight names the connection between economic power and [[race|racial]] domination.

## Wealth and Power

"Are they—wealthy people?" (004-049)—the question, asked with hesitation, acknowledges that wealth in [[tooms-county|Tooms County]] is the measure of all things. [[colonel-cresswell|Colonel Cresswell]]'s [[wealth|fortune]] was built "in such a way as to bring himself wealth" (006-003), a euphemism for [[greed|exploitation]] that the novel proceeds to unpack. The novel shows that economic power and [[politics|political power]] are inseparable: "[[politics|political power]] gave them little economic advantage as long as the threat" of violence remained (035-036).

## Economic Independence

[[zora|Zora]]'s [[the-swamp|swamp]] project pursues "as much independent economic strength as possible" (035-073), recognizing that political rights without economic power are hollow. The novel's [[dream|vision]] of [[freedom|liberation]] is fundamentally economic: freedom requires not just the vote but [[power|control]] over land and labor.
