# Prejudice

Racial prejudice in *The Quest of the Silver Fleece* operates as an invisible but suffocating armor around Southern society. Du Bois depicts it not merely as individual bigotry but as a systemic force that "paralyzed" the region, encasing it in "an armor of prejudice and sectionalisms" (008-061).

## In Social Relations

Prejudice manifests in the daily slights and exclusions faced by Black characters. Bles Alwyn learns to turn "the sly slights and humiliations of prejudice" to his advantage, using them as fuel rather than accepting defeat (022-106). The Cresswell family embodies the entrenched nature of racial bias — John Taylor notes that Colonel Cresswell's "prejudice on this score must still be reckoned with" when dealing with him (019-004).

## As a Political Tool

Prejudice is deliberately weaponized for economic and political ends. The text records how "the flame of racial prejudice was studiously fanned" by those seeking to maintain control over Black labor (036-144). Yet the novel also presents characters who transcend inherited prejudice — Miss Taylor sheds "narrow New England prejudices" through her experiences in the South (026-014), suggesting that prejudice can be unlearned through genuine human contact.

## Related Concepts

Racial prejudice intersects with the economic exploitation of the [[plantation]] system, the politics of [[the-south]], and the struggle for [[education]] as a path to dignity. The novel contrasts prejudice with the ideals of [[forgiveness]] and mutual understanding.
