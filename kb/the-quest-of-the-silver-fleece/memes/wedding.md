# The Double Wedding

The double wedding at [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]] Oaks is the novel's most elaborate social set piece. "Things were arranged for a double wedding at [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]] Oaks" (021-038)—the event marries [[mary-taylor|Mary Taylor]] to [[harry-cresswell|Harry Cresswell]] and [[helen-cresswell|Helen Cresswell]] to [[john-taylor|John]], binding Northern money to Southern land through the ritual of matrimony.

## Preparations

The wedding is defined by its material preparation. The "improbability of getting a wedding-gown in time" (020-019) creates urgency, while the fabric "marked: 'For the wedding-dress'" (021-027) becomes a symbol of the [[economics|wealth]] being consolidated. "The wedding invitations appeared" (021-028), signaling the social reach of the event—this is not a private ceremony but a public declaration of alliance. The detail that the dress was made "not even when the wedding invitations appeared" (021-028) suggests the scale of preparation involved.

## Economic Underpinning

The wedding is an economic transaction dressed in [[love|romance]]. [[mary-taylor|Mary Taylor]] brings Northern [[wealth|capital]]; [[harry-cresswell|Harry Cresswell]] brings Southern land. The "double" nature of the wedding—two couples, two regions, two forms of [[economics|wealth]]—makes explicit what a single wedding might only imply. "Between this and hurrying up a wedding-gown" (020-022) the groom "growled"—his impatience suggesting that the wedding is a business arrangement being expedited.

## Zora's Perspective

[[zora|Zora]] experiences the wedding from a position of exclusion. "[[zora|Zora]], almost forgetting the wedding, stood before the mirror" (021-062)—her self-regard in the mirror momentarily displaces the social event from which she is excluded. Later, "she got out her wedding-dress, and although it hung loosely" (031-088) in a different context, the wedding-dress becomes a motif of longing and loss rather than celebration.
