# Mary Cresswell

Mary Cresswell is a supporting character in *The Quest of the Silver Fleece*, representing the privileged Southern elite's encounter with broader political and social forces. Her arc traces a path from plantation-born confidence through Washington political engagement to a final state of existential isolation.

## Role in the Novel

Mary Cresswell is the daughter of [[Colonel Cresswell]] and sister of [[Harry Cresswell]]. She first appears in Chapter 10 as a fashionable young woman of the Cresswell household, initially portrayed as comfortably dismissive of the educational experiment at the swamp school. "I don't believe it," she remarks easily when told of the school's progress [010-032].

Her character arc moves from the provincial Alabama plantation to Washington, D.C., where she becomes involved with Congressman Todd's Civic Club. There she finds purpose in political advocacy, though her personal life grows increasingly troubled:

> It was recurring flashes of temper like this, together with evidences of dubious company and a growing fondness for liquor, that drove Mary Cresswell more and more to find solace in the work of Congressman Todd's Civic Club. [028-025]

In Washington, she discovers a talent for political influence and social maneuvering. She "listened but a half hour" before grasping the dynamics of political advocacy [027-053], and soon "worked with redoubled energy" on behalf of the Civic Club's causes [031-023]. Her political success brings her into the orbit of power, yet leaves her personally unfulfilled.

By the novel's end, Mary Cresswell has become a Washington hostess of some note, but her story concludes with a moment of existential reckoning. Looking upon her husband's political world, she walks "in another world" [031-122], suggesting a profound alienation from the life she has built. After six months in Washington, she "walked into her Washington hotel" [031-017] a changed woman, and "sped her departing guests with" a practiced smile [031-026] that concealed inner emptiness.

## Social World

Mary Cresswell's social circle in Washington reflects the uneasy alliance between old Southern aristocracy and new Northern political power. Her laugh when confronted with Zora's appearance—"With that hair?" [023-035]—reveals the racial contempt that underpins her class assumptions, even as she cultivates a more cosmopolitan public persona in Washington.

## Key Relationships

- **[[Colonel Cresswell]]** — Her father, the patriarch of the Cresswell plantation whose wealth and power define her social position.
- **[[Harry Cresswell]]** — Her brother, whose own ambitions and moral compromises parallel her trajectory.
- **[[John Taylor]]** — An early acquaintance who is visibly uncomfortable in her presence [010-153], reflecting the class tensions she embodies.
- **Congressman Todd** — The political figure through whose Civic Club she finds an outlet for her energies in Washington.

## Thematic Significance

Mary Cresswell serves as a lens through which Du Bois examines the position of Southern elite women in the post-Reconstruction era. Her journey from plantation privilege to Washington society and eventual disillusionment mirrors the broader displacement of the old Southern aristocracy in the face of modern political and economic forces. Her "fondness for liquor" [028-025] and restless search for purpose suggest the spiritual emptiness underlying the material comfort of her class.

Her story also illustrates the limits of political reform when disconnected from genuine moral conviction. Mary Cresswell's advocacy work, however energetic, serves primarily as an escape from personal unhappiness rather than a principled commitment to change—a distinction Du Bois draws with care.
