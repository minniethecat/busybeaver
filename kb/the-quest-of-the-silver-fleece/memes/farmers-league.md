# Farmers' League

The Farmers' League is a powerful cotton-growers' organization in the American South, functioning as both a cooperative and a vehicle for market manipulation under the leadership of Colonel Cresswell. It represents the intersection of agricultural economics and political power that Du Bois critiques throughout the novel.

## Overview

The Farmers' League first appears in the narrative as a subject of John Taylor's strategic counsel to his son. Taylor describes it as an organization of "big landlords" (006-064) and urges close observation of its methods. By Chapter 10, the League has become the dominant force in the regional cotton economy, with Colonel Cresswell serving as its president (010-118). The organization's stated purpose — protecting Southern cotton-growers — masks its actual function as a vehicle for speculation, market cornering, and the consolidation of wealth among the planter elite.

## Structure and Operations

The League operates through a hierarchical structure centered on Colonel Cresswell's leadership. An executive committee governs its affairs, with meetings held in Montgomery, Alabama (018-051). The organization leases warehouse facilities in strategic Southern trading centers — Savannah, Montgomery, and New Orleans (018-053) — enabling it to physically control cotton supply.

The League's core operational mechanism is market cornering: purchasing and holding cotton to drive up prices (018-016). At its peak, the League "holds three million bales and controls five" (018-062), giving it leverage over both Northern manufacturers and small farmers. The organization also exerts political influence, using its economic weight to shape legislation and resist reform efforts such as the Child Labor Bill.

## Key Members

- **[[colonel-cresswell]]** — President of the Farmers' League, he uses the organization to build personal wealth and political influence while presenting himself as a champion of Southern agricultural interests.
- **[[john-taylor]]** — Strategic advisor who counsels his son to study the League's methods, recognizing its potential as an instrument of power (006-074).

## Narrative Role

The Farmers' League serves multiple functions in the novel's plot. Economically, it demonstrates how Southern agricultural institutions were weaponized against both small farmers and Northern industrial interests. The League's cotton speculation directly affects the fortunes of characters across the social spectrum — from Bles Alwyn and Zora, whose harvested cotton passes through League warehouses (036-056), to the small mill owners crushed by its market manipulations (035-002).

Politically, the League embodies the alliance between Southern economic power and Washington influence. Colonel Cresswell leverages his League presidency to gain access to Congressional circles, while using the same organization to oppose progressive legislation. The newspaper headline "League Beats Trust" (022-006) ironically celebrates a victory that primarily benefits the planter class.

## Historical Context

The Farmers' League reflects real historical organizations of the post-Reconstruction era, when Southern cotton-growers formed cooperatives and associations to exert market power. However, Du Bois's portrayal highlights how such organizations could be captured by elite interests. The tension between the League's rhetoric of protecting farmers and its actual function as a tool of the Cresswell family mirrors broader Progressive Era critiques of concentrated economic power.
