# Politics

Politics in *The Quest of the Silver Fleece* operates on two distinct but interlocking planes: the brutal local politics of [[tooms-county|Tooms County]], where white power is absolute and black participation forbidden, and the transactional national politics of [[washington-dc|Washington]], where black votes are courted as a commodity and black politicians must navigate a system designed to contain and neutralize them. Du Bois, a trained political scientist, embeds in his novel a systematic anatomy of how American politics functions—and malfunctions—along the color line.

## Historical Context

When Du Bois published the novel in 1911, black disfranchisement in the South was nearly total. The last black congressman of the Reconstruction era, George Henry White, had left office in 1901. Booker T. [[washington-dc|Washington]]'s Tuskegee Machine exercised near-monopoly [[power|control]] over black political appointments through its [[love|relationship]] with the Republican Party. The novel's political plot—a black man's rise through party machinery, his eventual disillusionment, and the machinations of white power brokers—reflects Du Bois's deep engagement with and criticism of this political order.

## The Exclusion of Black Southerners

At the local level, politics is simply closed to black people. "Don't let the blacks meddle in politics," says Dr. Boldish, summarizing the white South's categorical position (016-041). This exclusion is not merely a matter of disfranchisement statutes but is enforced by the entire social and economic order. [[colonel-cresswell|Colonel Cresswell]] serves as [[justice|Justice]] of the Peace; his son is bailiff. The legal and political apparatus is unified in white hands, leaving black Southerners without any institutional avenue for redress.

## Black Votes as Political Currency

In the North, the situation is inverted: black votes matter, but only as a commodity to be purchased. As Easterly explains, the Republican Party's electoral strategy depends on manipulating the Northern Negro vote. "There are five hundred thousand or more black voters in pivotal Northern States," a party strategist notes, "and they're in revolt. In a close election the Negroes of [[new-york|New York]], Ohio, Indiana, and Illinois choose the President" (025-011). The solution, as Easterly describes it, is transactional: "We may have to give them a little extra money and a few more minor offices than usual" (022-016).

This commodification of black political power extends to the highest levels. [[mrs-vanderpool|Mrs. Vanderpool]]'s husband's Paris ambassadorship depends on securing Southern Senate votes, and the maneuvering involves leveraging [[race|racial]] loyalties and disloyalties across party lines (031-028). Politics is revealed as a machine in which [[race|racial]] interests are simultaneously central and invisible—the unspoken ground of every calculation.

## The Honest Man in Politics

[[bles-alwyn|Bles Alwyn]] enters this system as what [[mrs-vanderpool|Mrs. Vanderpool]] calls "an honest man in politics, even if he is black" (026-082). Her qualification—"even if he is black"—captures the central dilemma Du Bois explores: can a black man participate in American politics without being corrupted by it, and can he represent black interests without being reduced to a [[race|racial]] token?

[[bles-alwyn|Alwyn]]'s political education tracks the novel's broader argument about [[race|racial]] [[progress|uplift]]. He must learn to operate within a system designed by and for white men while maintaining his [[honor|integrity]] and his connection to the black community whose interests he purports to serve. The campaign against [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]] becomes the testing ground for whether politics can be an instrument of [[race|racial]] justice or merely another venue for [[race|racial]]ized power.

## Party Politics and the Color Line

Du Bois anatomizes both major parties' [[love|relationship]] to black America. [[democratic-party|The Democratic Party]], historically the party of slavery and Reconstruction's overthrow, is represented through [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]]'s ambitions and the Southern political machine. [[bles-alwyn|Bles]] is shocked to learn that anyone "can advise a black man to vote the Democratic ticket" (024-060). Yet the novel also stages a political meeting where a black lawyer makes "[[democratic-party|The Democratic Party]] and the Negro" his theme, offering "cool, carefully reasoned, and plausible" arguments that trouble simple partisan loyalties (024-070).

[[republican-party|The Republican Party]], the party of Lincoln, has its own corruption. Congressman Peter Smith is disciplined by Easterly "for certain socialistic votes in the House" (006-019). The party that claims black loyalty delivers "a little extra money and a few more minor offices" rather than structural change (022-016). Du Bois's analysis prefigures his later political evolution toward independent black political action.

## The Farmers' League and Economic Politics

The novel also explores politics outside the electoral frame[[labor|work]]. [[farmer|The Farmer]]s' League campaign, which [[john-taylor|John Taylor]] and [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]] see as threatened by the Smith [[school|School]]'s demand for immediate payment, represents an attempt at bi[[race|racial]] economic organizing (015-033). Its fragility—the threat that black educational [[ambition|aspiration]] will "ruin" it—reveals how the [[race|racial]] divide is weaponized to prevent cross-[[race|racial]] class solidarity.
