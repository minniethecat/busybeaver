# Progress

"Progress" is one of the most freighted words in *The Quest of the Silver Fleece*. It is the term that politicians invoke when they want to avoid action, that [[philanthropy|reform]]ers use to describe projects that leave existing power structures intact, and that [[mrs-vanderpool|Mrs. Vanderpool]] deploys with cynical clarity when she tells [[bles-alwyn|Bles]]: "Most of these white folk—believe me, boy—are fools and knaves: they don't want truth or progress; they want to keep niggers down" (025-142). The novel asks: progress toward what, for whom, and at whose expense?

## The Cynicism of Progress Talk

[[mrs-vanderpool|Mrs. Vanderpool]]'s assessment is the novel's most devastating. The white people who [[power|control]] politics "don't want truth or progress; they want to keep niggers down" (025-142). Her cynicism is earned through years of observing how the language of progress is deployed to disguise the maintenance of [[race|racial]] hierarchy. [[truth|Truth]] and progress are paired—and both are rejected by a political class that knows exactly what it is doing.

## Progress as Political Evasion

[[senator-smith|Senator Smith]] is confronted by a [[philanthropy|reform]]er who urges him to "take a stand on some of these progressive matters—this bill, or the Child [[labor|Labor]] movement, or Low Tariff legislation" (022-010). The list of "progressive matters" is revealing: child labor, tariff [[philanthropy|reform]]—issues that matter to white constituents but leave the [[race|racial]] order untouched. Progress, in this register, is the set of [[philanthropy|reform]]s that can be pursued without disturbing white supremacy. It is progress for some, stasis for others.

## Class and the Limits of Progress

[[mary-taylor|Mary Taylor]]'s initial reluctance to teach at the [[the-school|school]] is expressed in class terms: "if I teach Negroes I'll scarcely see much of people in my own class" (003-008). Progress, for [[mary-taylor|Mary]], initially means maintaining her class position—the "society of people of your class" that a white benefactor later laments she is deprived of (006-028). The novel exposes how class loyalty constrains even well-intentioned [[philanthropy|reform]]. Progress that requires crossing class lines, that demands solidarity rather than condescension, is progress of a kind that most white characters are unwilling to undertake.

## Miss Smith's Classification

[[miss-smith|Miss Smith]]'s "succinct and acid classification of the county's first family"—"The old man's a fool; the young one a rascal; the girl a ninny"—is immediately qualified: "but they own us body and soul" (004-006). The classification is accurate and useless. Knowing exactly what the [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]]s are does nothing to diminish their power. Progress, the novel suggests, requires not just clear-sighted analysis but the capacity to act on it—and that capacity is precisely what the existing order denies.

## Progress and the School

The [[the-school|school]] itself embodies the paradox of progress. It is an institution dedicated to black advancement, yet it exists at the sufferance of the white power structure that progress would need to transform. [[mrs-vanderpool|Mrs. Vanderpool]]'s ideal of "a set of Negro [[the-school|school]]s where the white teachers could know the [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]]s" captures the double bind: the [[the-school|school]] is tolerated only to the extent that it facilitates rather than challenges cross-[[race|racial]] [[love|relationship]]s on white terms (006-028). Progress that threatens the structure will not be permitted; progress that does not threaten it may not be progress at all.

## Historical Context

The early twentieth century was the high tide of Progressivism in American politics—a movement that achieved significant [[philanthropy|reform]]s in labor law, women's suffrage, and corporate regulation while largely ignoring or actively opposing [[race|racial]] [[freedom|equality]]. Du Bois's skepticism about "progress" reflects his experience of a [[philanthropy|reform]] movement that treated [[race|racial]] justice as, at best, an afterthought. The novel's analysis anticipates the critique that would later be leveled at the New Deal and other progressive achievements: that they were progress for white Americans built on the exclusion of black Americans.
