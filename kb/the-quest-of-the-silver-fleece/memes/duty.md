# Duty

Duty in *The Quest of the Silver Fleece* is the concept through which characters negotiate the demands that the world places on them—and the demands they place on themselves. It operates across [[race|racial]] lines with radically different content: for white characters, duty often means maintaining the existing order; for black characters, it means transforming it. Du Bois uses the idea of duty to explore what people owe to one another, to their communities, and to the future.

## Miss Smith and the Duty of Education

[[miss-smith|Miss Smith]] embodies a duty that is indistinguishable from vocation. Her entire life is organized around the [[the-school|school]] and its mission. She has "done her duty," the narrator tells us—a statement that carries both finality and exhaustion (004-018). Yet the very concept of duty is complicated by the Veil that separates black and white worlds. "The Veil hung thick, sombre, impenetrable" (004-018), and even [[miss-smith|Miss Smith]], for all her commitment, cannot fully see through it. Duty, however strenuous, is no guarantee of understanding.

## Zora's Duty as Mission

[[zora|Zora]]'s sense of duty is the novel's most radical. Confronting the vulnerability of a young black girl, [[zora|Zora]] experiences duty not as obligation imposed from without but as mission arising from within: "She would protect this girl; she would protect all black girls. She would make it possible for these poor beasts of burden to be decent in their toil" (032-031). The language is transformative: "It was her duty, her [[tradition|heritage]]" (032-031). Duty here is not submission to an external code but the realization of a self-chosen purpose—the duty to build "ramparts against cruelty, poverty, and crime" (032-031).

## Mary Taylor's Conflicted Duty

[[mary-taylor|Mary Taylor]]'s [[love|relationship]] to duty is more troubled. She struggles with the contradiction between her genuine desire to help and the [[race|racial]] ideology that limits what she can imagine. Her proposal for "a chain of endowed Negro [[the-school|school]]s under the management of trustees composed of Northern business men and local Southern whites" is, in her mind, "a way of fulfilling the world's duty to this black boy" (012-008). But the duty she en[[dream|vision]]s is paternalistic—a duty exercised over black people rather than a duty to empower them.

The tension between [[mary-taylor|Mary]]'s sense of duty and [[harry-cresswell|Harry Cresswell]]'s world is encapsulated in her observation that "with her folk, hard [[labor|work]] and inflexible duty were of prime importance; they were the rock foundation" (026-015). The values she brings from her Northern upbringing are real but insufficient—they cannot survive contact with a [[society|social order]] where "the depths beneath the gravely inclined head" have "sounded strangely hollow" (026-015).

## Duty and Marriage

The novel also explores how duty constrains personal life. Mrs. [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]], confronting [[bles-alwyn|Bles Alwyn]]'s presence in her home, resolves that "at the slightest indication she must crush him ruthlessly and at the same time point the path of duty. He ought to marry some good girl—not [[zora|Zora]], but someone" (034-048). Duty in this context is the enforcement of [[race|racial]] boundaries under the guise of moral guidance. The "path of duty" leads away from love and toward the reproduction of the existing order.

## Historical Context

The concept of duty held particular resonance in the [[progress|Progress]]ive Era, when Du Bois wrote. Reform movements—[[village|settlement]] houses, temperance, missionary [[labor|work]], education—were saturated with the language of duty and obligation. Du Bois's treatment of the concept is characteristically dialectical: he honors the genuine moral impulse behind duty while exposing how that impulse is captured, distorted, and deployed in defense of [[race|racial]] hierarchy. The duty to "help" black people, in the novel's analysis, is too often a duty to manage them rather than to liberate them.
