# Honor

Honor in *The Quest of the Silver Fleece* is a concept with a double life. Among white Southern gentlemen, it governs commercial dealings with fastidious precision. But in relations with black people, it vanishes—suspended by a [[race|racial]] logic that does not recognize black humanity as entitled to the protections honor affords. Du Bois anatomizes honor as a code that is at once real in its effects and radically incomplete in its application.

## The Code of Southern Honor

[[colonel-cresswell|Colonel Cresswell]] is, by the standards of his class, "an honest man." [[john-taylor|John Taylor]] observes that "in most matters of [[economics|commerce]] between men he was punctilious to a degree almost annoying to [[john-taylor|Taylor]]" (033-015). The code is real; it is observed; it has consequences. [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]] keeps his word to other white men with a rigor that his business partner finds excessive.

## Honor Among Black Characters

The novel also explores honor as it operates within the black community and across the color line. [[bles-alwyn|Bles Alwyn]]'s sense of personal integrity—his refusal to compromise his principles for political advantage—represents an alternative honor code, one grounded not in [[race|racial]] hierarchy but in moral commitment. The courtroom scene, where a black defendant addresses the judge as "Your honor" (036-105), carries the weight of irony: the black speaker observes a code of respect that the white legal system routinely denies to black people.

## The Limits of Honor

Yet "there was one part of the world which his code of honor did not cover, and he saw no incongruity in the omission" (033-015). That excluded territory is the entire sphere of relations with black people. Du Bois explains with sociological precision: "The uninitiated cannot easily picture to himself the mental attitude of a former slaveholder toward property in the hands of a Negro. Such property belonged of right to the master, if the master needed it; and since ridiculous laws safeguarded the property, it was perfectly permissible to circumvent such laws" (033-015).

The result is a system of double bookkeeping. "No Negro starved on the [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]] place, neither did any accumulate property. [[colonel-cresswell|Colonel Cresswell]] saw to both matters" (033-015). Honor, in its [[race|racial]]ized form, guarantees subsistence but forbids [[greed|accumulation]]—a paternalistic minimum and a predatory maximum.

## Honor and Marriage

The [[family|marriage]] market reveals honor's entanglement with class calculation. The [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]]s' neighbors regard "an alliance with Northern [[cotton-mill|mill]]ions an honor for [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]] blood" (020-003). Honor here is measured in [[wealth|capital]]—the honor done to an old name by new money. The [[colonel-cresswell|Colonel]]'s discomfort with the match suggests a residual tension between two orders of value: the aristocratic code that prizes [[family|lineage]] above [[economics|wealth]] and the market logic that converts everything, including family names, into instruments of [[greed|accumulation]].

## The Politics of Honor

When a white ally declares, "I honor the old [[class|aristocracy]] you represent" and pledges to "crush this Yankee and put the niggers in their places" (036-154), honor becomes explicitly what it has always been implicitly: a mechanism of [[race|racial]] solidarity. To honor the old [[class|aristocracy]] is to endorse the [[race|racial]] order that [[class|aristocracy]] created and sustains. Honor, in this register, is the name that violence gives to its own legitimacy.

## Historical Context

The Southern code of honor Du Bois anatomizes has deep roots in the antebellum planter class's self-understanding. The "code of the gentleman" governed dueling, debt, and social [[duty|obligation]] in a society that understood itself as chivalric. Du Bois's contribution is to reveal the [[race|racial]] substructure of this code: its elaborate punctiliousness in white-to-white relations was not despite but because of the absolute power it exercised over black bodies and black property.
