# Tooms County

The rural [[alabama|Alabama]] county that is the primary setting of the novel—a landscape of cotton [[field|fields]], plantations, and the struggling black community at its center.

## Description

Tooms County is the fictional [[alabama|Alabama]] county where all the major events of the novel unfold. Du Bois based it on the Black Belt counties of [[alabama|Alabama]] he had studied as a sociologist—places where cotton was king and black labor built white [[economics|wealth]].

## Key Aspects

- With the [[cotton|Cotton]] Economy: Tooms County is defined by cotton. Every institution, every [[love|relationship]], every [[ambition|aspiration]] is shaped by the rhythms of planting, picking, and selling the white fiber (003-007)(004-045).

## Geography

- The [[land|Land]]scape: The county's geography—the wide black [[field|fields]], [[the-swamp-land|the swamp]], the plantation houses, the tenant cabins—is mapped with sociological precision.

## Social Significance

- The Social Order: Tooms County is a microcosm of the post-Reconstruction South, its [[race|racial]] hierarchy visible in every interaction between black and white.

## Historical Context

Tooms County is not merely backdrop but argument: Du Bois uses the physical and social geography of the county to demonstrate how environment, [[economics|economy]], and race interact to shape human possibility.

## Related Entries

Du Bois's decision to set the novel in a single county reflects his sociological conviction that the local is where large forces become visible.
