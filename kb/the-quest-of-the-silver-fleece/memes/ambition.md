# Ambition

Ambition is the engine of the novel's plot—the force that drives [[bles-alwyn|Bles]] from his family's home, that fuels [[john-taylor|John Taylor]]'s financial scheming, that animates [[zora|Zora]]'s [[dream|vision]] of a transformed community. But ambition is also a site of [[race|racial]] discipline: white characters [[labor|work]] to contain black ambition, to redirect it toward servility, to denounce it as "impossible" and "wild." Du Bois traces how ambition operates differently depending on whose ambition it is.

## Black Ambition and White Containment

The white South's response to black ambition is captured in [[mary-taylor|Mary Taylor]]'s conclusions about the [[the-school|school]]. "She believed it wrong to encourage the ambitions of these children to any great extent; she believed they should be servants and farmers, content to [[labor|work]] under present conditions until those conditions could be changed" (012-007). The logic is circular: black ambition must be suppressed because conditions are not right for its fulfillment, and conditions will not change unless black ambition insists that they must.

[[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]] makes the threat explicit: when black people are "suddenly thrust beyond their natural station, filled with wild ideas and impossible ambitions, it meant terrible danger to Southern white women" (013-014). Ambition in black people is framed as inherently dangerous—not because of anything ambitious black people do, but because ambition itself violates the [[race|racial]] order that assigns black people to fixed, subordinate stations.

## Bles Alwyn's Ambition

[[bles-alwyn|Bles]] embodies the novel's affirmative [[dream|vision]] of black ambition. "Strength and ambition and inarticulate love were fighting within him. He felt, in the dark thousands of his kind about him, a mighty calling to deeds" (011-048). The passage yokes ambition to love and to [[community|collective]] purpose—ambition is not selfish striving but a response to "a mighty calling." [[bles-alwyn|Bles]] "was becoming conscious of the narrowness and straightness of his black world, and red anger flashed in him ever and again as he felt his bonds" (011-048). Ambition and anger are fused: the desire to rise is inseparable from the recognition of what holds him down.

## The School as Crucible of Ambition

[[miss-smith|Miss Smith]]'s [[the-school|school]] is where ambition is kindled. Looking back at years of students, she sees how "some had caught, kindled and flashed to ambition and achievement; some, glowing dimly, had plodded on in a slow, dumb [[religion|faith]]ful [[labor|work]] worth while; and yet others had suddenly exploded, hurtling human fragments to heaven and to hell" (012-004). Ambition is not safe. It can lead to achievement or destruction—the [[the-school|school]] cannot guarantee which. But the alternative to kindling ambition is to leave black children in darkness, which [[miss-smith|Miss Smith]] refuses to do.

## Ambition Redirected

The alternative offered to black ambition is servitude dressed as virtue. "Let your ambition be to serve rather than rule, to be humble followers of the lowly Jesus" (016-022). This is the accommodationist gospel that Du Bois spent his career opposing: ambition is permissible only if it aims downward, only if it embraces subordination, only if it calls itself something other than ambition.

## Historical Context

The containment of black ambition was a central project of Jim Crow. Disfranchisement, segregated education, and employment discrimination were all mechanisms designed to ensure that black ambition would find no institutional support. Booker T. [[washington-dc|Washington]]'s 1895 [[atlanta|Atlanta]] Compromise speech, with its famous admonition to "cast down your bucket where you are," represented the most prominent articulation of the view that black ambition should be redirected toward manual labor and away from political and social [[freedom|equality]]. Du Bois's *The Souls of Black Folk* (1903) was, in significant part, a rebuttal to this view—and the novel extends that rebuttal into fiction.
