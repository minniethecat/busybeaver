# Slavery

In *The Quest of the Silver Fleece*, slavery is not merely a historical institution abolished by the Civil War but a living reality whose structures persist into the post-Emancipation South through debt, peonage, [[tradition|custom]], and violence. Du Bois portrays a world in which the legal abolition of chattel slavery has been followed by economic arrangements that reproduce its essential features—unfree labor, extraction of black [[economics|wealth]], and the constant threat of coercion.

## Historical Context

Du Bois wrote *The Quest of the Silver Fleece* during the nadir of American race relations, when the convict lease system, debt peonage, and sharecropping had effectively re-enslaved much of the black South. The Supreme Court's *Plessy v. Ferguson* (1896) had sanctioned Jim Crow segregation, and lynching reached its peak in the first decade of the twentieth century. The novel's insistence that slavery had not ended but merely changed form was both a literary argument and a political intervention in the debate between Booker T. [[washington-dc|Washington]]'s accommodationism and Du Bois's own demand for full civil and political rights.

## Slavery's Ideological Defense

The novel exposes how the white South justifies the new slavery through a re[[dream|vision]]ist history of the old. At a public gathering, a speaker—echoing the rhetoric of the post-Reconstruction [[forgiveness|reconciliation]] narrative—declares: "Remember that the slavery of your people was not necessarily a crime. It was a [[the-school|school]] of [[labor|work]] and love. It gave you noble friends, like Mr. [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]] here" (016-022). This passage dramatizes Du Bois's critique of the "Lost Cause" mythology that sanitized slavery as a benevolent institution and positioned former slaveholders as the natural guardians of black people.

Even figures who acknowledge the old intimacy between the races can deploy that acknowledgment in defense of the existing order. [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]] himself agrees that "they never were far apart, even in slavery days; both sides were honest and sincere" (013-035)—a claim that obscures the violence and coercion at slavery's core while naturalizing the continued subordination of black labor.

## The Persistence of Slavery under New Names

The novel's most direct treatment of the slavery-as-living-reality theme occurs when a Northern visitor, observing the conditions of black laborers on the [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]] plantation, exclaims: "Why, [[aunt-rachel|Aunt Rachel]], it's slavery!" The old woman does not dispute the characterization. "Yas'm, hit's slavery," she agrees. "I hates it mighty bad, too" (012-044)(012-045). This exchange establishes the novel's central argument: that the post-Reconstruction Southern economic order, while legally distinct from chattel slavery, functions as a system of unfree labor enforced by the same [[race|racial]] hierarchy that sustained the old regime.

Du Bois traces the mechanics of this new slavery through multiple institutional channels. The debt system binds black farmers to white landowners through "complicated contract binding the tenant hand and foot to the landlord" (012-072). The legal system, personified by [[colonel-cresswell|Colonel Cresswell]] as [[justice|Justice]] of the Peace and his son as bailiff, ensures that black people cannot seek redress against white predation (017-069). The educational system, as [[mary-taylor|Mary Taylor]] observes, withholds the training and [[wealth|capital]] that would enable genuine economic independence: "Without intelligence and training and some [[wealth|capital]] it is the wildest nonsense to think you can lead your people out of slavery" (012-089).

## Slavery and Gender

Du Bois pays particular attention to how the new slavery ensnares black women. When [[zora|Zora]] confronts the theft of her cotton and her resulting [[poverty|destitution]], the novel makes explicit the connection between economic powerlessness and the threat of sexual [[greed|exploitation]]: "If [[elspeth|Elspeth]] chose to sign a contract of [[labor|work]] for her tomorrow, it would mean slavery, jail, or hounded running away" (017-069). The passage links economic coercion, legal entrapment, and gendered vulnerability into a single system of domination—what Du Bois elsewhere calls the "double burden" borne by black women.

## The Meaning of Freedom

[[bles-alwyn|Bles Alwyn]], the novel's educated black protagonist, articulates the book's understanding of freedom as something earned through struggle rather than bestowed by law. "I fought for my freedom," he declares. "I know what slavery is; may I forget God when I vote for traitors and slaveholders" (024-062). His statement connects the memory of slavery to the imperative of political action in the present—a rejection of the conciliatory politics urged by white moderates.

## Slavery and the Future

The novel's final word on slavery is not one of despair but of ongoing struggle. As [[bles-alwyn|Bles]] addresses a political gathering, he reminds his audience "of the history of slavery" and points not only to Lincoln but to Charles Sumner and "other white friends" (024-074)—an insistence that the fight against slavery has always been multi[[race|racial]] and must remain so. [[zora|Zora]], planning the [[the-school|school]]'s future, refuses to accept that partial success is enough: "There will still be slavery and oppression all around us. The children will be kept in the cotton [[field|fields]]; the men will be cheated, and the women—" (032-058). Her unfinished sentence points toward a [[dream|vision]] of [[freedom|liberation]] that extends beyond the [[the-school|school]]'s walls, toward the transformation of the entire [[society|social order]].
