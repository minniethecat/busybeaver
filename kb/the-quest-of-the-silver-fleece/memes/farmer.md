# The Farmer

The farmer in *The Quest of the Silver Fleece* is the novel's central economic subject: the black agricultural [[labor|work]]er whose labor produces cotton, [[economics|wealth]], and the [[society|social order]] itself. The novel distinguishes between the tenant farmer—bound by debt and contract—and the free farmer that the [[the-school|school]] project imagines as a future possibility.

## The Tenant Farmer

The tenant farmer exists in a condition the novel presents as neither slavery nor freedom. The [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]] plantation is populated by those "content to [[labor|work]] under present conditions" (012-007)—a contentment imposed by the contract system rather than freely chosen. The farmer's labor sustains the entire economic order, yet the farmer is systematically denied the fruits of that labor.

## Farming as Vocation

Against the degraded figure of the tenant, the novel sets an alternative [[dream|vision]]: the farmer as independent cultivator. The [[the-school|school]] project's ambition involves "our own farm with our own tenants" (032-059), where farming becomes a path to economic self-determination rather than a trap. The novel insists that "any training good enough for a farmer that the Southern farmer" receives (012-089) must include scientific knowledge, not merely the brute labor that the plantation demands.

## The Cotton Economy

The farmer's world is defined by cotton. "When cotton rose, [[the-tenant-farmers|the tenants]] had already sold their cotton" (030-066)—the farmer is always one step behind the market, trapped in a temporal structure of [[greed|exploitation]] where the timing of sale is [[power|control]]led by others.
