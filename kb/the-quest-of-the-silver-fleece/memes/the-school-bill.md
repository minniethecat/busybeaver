# The School Bill Debate

The [[the-school|school]] bill's journey through the state legislature is the novel's most extended political sequence—a lesson in how policy is actually made in the post-Reconstruction South and whose interests it serves.

## The Legislation

The bill concerns funding for Negro education—specifically, the kind of [[the-school|school]] that [[mary-taylor|Mary Taylor]] has established in [[tooms-county|Tooms County]]. On its face, it is a measure to support black [[education|schooling]]. In practice, it becomes a vehicle for competing interests: planters who want a compliant labor force, philanthropists who want [[power|control]], and politicians who want votes (027-053).

## The Political Machinery

Tolliver and his fellow legislators are not ideologues but functionaries. Their votes are determined by the balance of political forces—who has power over them, what interests they must serve. The bill's fate reveals that education policy in the Jim Crow South was never about education.

## Mary Taylor's Education

For [[mary-taylor|Mary Taylor]], the [[the-school|school]] bill debate is a political education. She learns that the [[the-school|school]] she founded does not belong to her or to her students but to the political and economic forces that fund—or defund—it. Her idealism encounters the machinery of power and is transformed by the encounter.
