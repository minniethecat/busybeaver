# The Tenant Cabins

The cabins where black [[the-tenant-farmers|tenant farmers]] and their families live are the most intimate spaces of the cotton [[economics|economy]]—the places where the abstractions of debt, labor, and [[race|racial]] hierarchy become the concrete realities of daily life.

## Living Conditions

The cabins are simple wooden structures, often no more than one or two rooms, with dirt floors or rough planking. They lack the amenities that even the poorest white families take for granted. The physical condition of the cabins reflects the economic condition of their inhabitants: precarious, deteriorating, never quite one's own.

## Family Life

Within these cabins, black families cook, sleep, love, grieve, and raise children. The cabin is the site of the most private dimensions of black life—and also the place where the public structures of the cotton [[economics|economy]] are most intimately felt. A family's security in its cabin depends entirely on the landowner's will.

## The Cabin and the Big House

The spatial [[love|relationship]] between the tenant cabins and the [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]] mansion maps the social [[love|relationship]] between black labor and white power. The cabins are visible from the Big House but separated by distance and landscaping—close enough for surveillance, far enough for the pretense of separation.
