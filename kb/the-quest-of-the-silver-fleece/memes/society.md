# Society

"Society" in *The Quest of the Silver Fleece* names the structured field of social relations within which every character must navigate. It is both an abstract order—the system of race, class, and region that positions people relative to one another—and a concrete experience: the dinners, drawing rooms, and institutions where positions are performed and reinforced. Du Bois examines society as it functions for white people, for black people, and across the color line, attending to the mechanisms of inclusion and exclusion that make society what it is.

## White Society and Its Demands

[[mary-taylor|Mary Taylor]]'s [[love|relationship]] to society is defined by what it excludes. "There's no society—just elementary [[labor|work]]," she protests when considering the teaching position (003-005). The distinction is revealing: society, for [[mary-taylor|Mary]], means the company of people of her own class, the "social prestige" she later acknowledges seeking through her philanthropic [[labor|work]] (007-001). Teaching black children in rural [[alabama|Alabama]] is not society; it is its absence.

The Vanderpool porch scene crystallizes this logic. A white visitor laments that [[mary-taylor|Mary]]'s "[[labor|work]] deprives you of the society of people of your class" and offers her ideal: "a set of Negro [[the-school|school]]s where the white teachers could know the [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]]s" (006-028). Society is that from which [[mary-taylor|Mary]] is excluded by her choice of [[labor|work]]—and the solution is not to challenge society's exclusions but to redesign the [[labor|work]] so that it permits social access. The black children are incidental to this calculation.

## Society Across the Color Line

The novel continually stages encounters across the color line that reveal society's [[race|racial]] architecture. [[mary-taylor|Mary]] [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]], confronting [[bles-alwyn|Bles Alwyn]]'s presence in her home, is horrified by the possibility that he might have "acquired Northern notions of freedom and [[freedom|equality]]" (034-027). Southern society is built on the premise that black people do not have such notions—that they accept their place. When a black person asserts [[freedom|equality]], society's foundation trembles.

## Black Society

Du Bois also depicts black society as a world with its own institutions, hierarchies, and codes. The [[the-church|church]], the [[the-school|school]], the cabin in [[the-swamp-land|the swamp]]—these are sites where black social life is organized on terms that are not wholly determined by white power. The novel's attention to the internal complexity of black society is itself a political argument: black people are not merely the objects of white society's gaze but subjects of their own social world.

## Society and the School

[[miss-smith|Miss Smith]]'s [[the-school|school]] occupies an ambiguous position in the social order. It is a black institution in a white-dominated landscape, a place where the "society of people of your class" is irrelevant and where the [[labor|work]] is everything. [[miss-smith|Miss Smith]]'s impatience with white social codes—her blunt assessment of the [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]]s, her refusal to perform deference—models an alternative relation to society: not withdrawal but refusal of its terms.

## Historical Context

The concept of "society" in the early twentieth-century South was inseparable from the [[race|racial]] etiquette that governed every interaction across the color line. Jim Crow was not only a legal system but a social one—a set of [[hope|expectation]]s about how black and white people should address each other, move through space, and acknowledge or ignore each other's presence. The novel's attention to the micro-dynamics of social interaction—who sits where, who speaks to whom, who is deprived of whose company—reflects Du Bois's understanding that [[race|racial]] hierarchy is reproduced not only through law and economics but through the daily performance of social position.
