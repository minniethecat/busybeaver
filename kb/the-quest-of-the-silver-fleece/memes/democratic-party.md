# The Democratic Party

The **Democratic Party** in *The Quest of the Silver Fleece* represents the political embodiment of the post-Reconstruction Southern [[race|racial]] order. It is the party of the planter class, of white supremacy, and of the systematic disenfranchisement of Black voters. For characters like [[colonel-cresswell|Colonel Cresswell]], the Democratic Party is not merely a political affiliation but an instrument of economic and social [[power|control]].

## The Party of the Solid South

By the period of the novel, the South had become the "Solid South"—a region where Democratic dominance was absolute and Black political participation had been effectively eliminated. The party's power rested on three pillars:

1. **White supremacy** as political ideology
2. **Disenfranchisement** of Black voters through poll taxes, literacy tests, and violence
3. **Economic [[power|control]]** exercised by the planter class over Black labor

## The Party and the Negro Question

The novel records a debate about the Democratic Party's [[love|relationship]] to Black Americans:

> His theme was "The Democratic Party and the Negro." [PN=024-070]

This speech acknowledges that the "Negro question" was a live political issue, even for a party committed to white rule. The speaker's claim—"I know the Democratic Party" (PN=024-074)—suggests insider knowledge of the party's real calculations regarding Black citizens.

## Relationship to the Republican Party

Du Bois presents the two-party system as a mechanism that fails Black Southerners. The [[republican-party|Republican Party]] takes Black votes for granted; the Democratic Party actively [[labor|work]]s to eliminate Black political participation entirely. Between these two parties, Black citizens have no genuine political home:

> ...punish the Republican Party for not protecting them. [PN=025-046]

This formulation captures the tragic structure of Black politics in the era: the party that ought to protect Black rights has failed, and the party that openly opposes Black rights is ascendant.

## The Party as Economic Instrument

For the planter class, the Democratic Party is less an ideological commitment than a practical tool. It [[power|control]]s the local institutions—[[justice|courts]], sheriffs, tax assessors—that enforce the [[debt|debt peonage]] system. Democratic dominance means that Black farmers cannot appeal to the law for protection, because the law is an instrument of the same class that exploits them.

## Historical Context

Du Bois wrote during the nadir of American race relations—the period from roughly 1890 to 1920 when Jim Crow was consolidated, lynching was common, and Black citizenship was hollowed out. The Democratic Party's "Solid South" was the political expression of this regime. Du Bois's critique of the party was not merely partisan but structural: he saw it as the organized expression of a system designed to reduce Black Americans to permanent subordination.
