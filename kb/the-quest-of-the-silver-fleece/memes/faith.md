# Faith

Faith in *The Quest of the Silver Fleece* is less theological than social. It names the trust that characters place in one another and in the possibility of change—a trust repeatedly tested by the realities of the color line.

## Faith in People

[[miss-wynn|Miss Wynn]] "had little [[religion|faith]] in the world and its people" (024-001), and the novel presents this cynicism as both a survival strategy for a black woman in [[washington-dc|Washington]] and a spiritual impoverishment. By contrast, a "brown girl, with boundless [[religion|faith]] in white folks, went to a Judge" and suffered for it (024-097)—the novel refuses to judge whether [[religion|faith]] or skepticism is wiser.

## Faith in the Work

[[mary-taylor|Mary Taylor]]'s "tireless [[religion|faith]]" (P03-006) drives the [[the-school|school]] project forward against all discouragement. Her [[religion|faith]] is not religious but practical: a conviction that "the masses and [[labor|work]] is the truth one must face" (006-040). [[bles-alwyn|Bles]]'s "stanch, unfaltering [[religion|faith]]" (009-026) mirrors hers, and the novel suggests that shared [[religion|faith]] in the [[labor|work]] is what sustains both characters.

## The Limits of Faith

The novel also records [[religion|faith]]'s failure. [[zora|Zora]]'s "strange [[religion|faith]] of theirs" in the community's capacity for self-[[politics|governance]] meets resistance from those "who had little belief" in it (033-089). Faith without power is fragile; the novel does not pretend otherwise.
