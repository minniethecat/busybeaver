# Dreams

"Dreams" is the first word of significance that [[zora|Zora]] speaks, and it is the novel's master trope. When [[bles-alwyn|Bles]] dismisses dreams as "nothing," [[zora|Zora]] insists: "Oh, yes they is! There ain't nothing but dreams—that is, nothing much" (001-065)(001-066). This exchange, occurring in the novel's opening chapter, establishes the tension that will drive the entire narrative: between the hard material realities of [[race|racial]] oppression and the transformative power of vision, imagination, and [[ambition|aspiration]].

## The Swamp as Dream-Space

The [[the-swamp|swamp]] is the novel's geography of dreams. It is where [[zora|Zora]] lives, where she and [[bles-alwyn|Bles]] first meet, and where the "Field of Dreams" that gives the novel one of its central metaphors is located. The [[the-swamp|swamp]] is marginal, uncultivated, beyond the reach of the plantation [[economics|economy]]—and it is precisely this marginality that makes it a space of possibility. Dreams, in Du Bois's rendering, grow in the places that power has not yet fully colonized.

## Dreams and Education

[[miss-smith|Miss Smith]]'s [[the-school|school]] is built on the conviction that dreams matter. Looking out over her institution, she reflects that it carries "the hopes of thousands to come" and that its significance is "big with the [[fate|destiny]] not simply of a county and a State, but of a race—a nation—a world" (002-007). The [[the-school|school]] is a dream made material—buildings, curricula, children [[education|learning]] to count and read. But the novel also insists on the fragility of materialized dreams: the [[the-school|school]] is always one funding crisis away from collapse, always surrounded by "the [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]] domain that lay like a mighty hand around the [[the-school|school]], ready at a word to squeeze its life out" (012-010).

## Dreams Deferred and Destroyed

The novel does not sentimentalize dreams. [[miss-smith|Miss Smith]] has "hoped so desperately during a quarter of a century" for an endowment that never comes (002-009). Dreams deferred for decades can curdle into resignation. The novel tracks what happens when dreams encounter the hard walls of [[race|racial]] reality—when [[bles-alwyn|Bles]]'s political [[ambition|aspiration]]s are blocked, when [[zora|Zora]]'s land is threatened, when the [[the-school|school]]'s survival hangs by a thread.

## The Fate of Dreams

The novel's epigraph—"I am the master of my fate"—is the individualist counterpoint to its collectivist vision of dreams (029-084). The tension between individual mastery and [[community|collective]] transformation is never fully resolved. Dreams can be defeated, deferred, betrayed. But the novel's final word is not defeat. [[zora|Zora]]'s vision, born in [[the-swamp-land|the swamp]] and tested in the world, endures. Dreams, she has told us from the beginning, are not nothing. They are, in some sense, everything.
