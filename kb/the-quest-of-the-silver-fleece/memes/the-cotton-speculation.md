# The Cotton Speculation

[[john-taylor|John Taylor]]'s scheme to [[power|control]] cotton manufacturing represents a new form of power entering the Southern [[economics|economy]]: not landed [[economics|wealth]] but financial [[wealth|capital]], not the plantation but the corp[[speech|oration]], not inheritance but speculation.

## The Scheme

[[john-taylor|John Taylor]], backed by Easterly's [[wealth|capital]] and connections, en[[dream|vision]]s a cotton trust that would [[power|control]] every stage of production from field to finished cloth. The scheme involves buying [[power|control]]ling interests in cotton [[cotton-mill|mill]]s, securing supply contracts with planters like [[colonel-cresswell|Colonel Cresswell]], and using market power to crush competitors (006-048)(006-050)(006-058).

## Old Power Meets New

The speculation brings [[john-taylor|John Taylor]] into alliance—and tension—with [[colonel-cresswell|Colonel Cresswell]]. [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]] represents landed [[economics|wealth]], inherited [[power|authority]], the old order. [[john-taylor|John]] represents financial [[wealth|capital]], speculative energy, the new. Their negotiation over cotton reveals the shifting structure of power in the post-Reconstruction South.

## The Human Cost

The speculation is discussed in offices and over cigars, far from the cotton [[field|fields]]. But its consequences reach the pickers in those [[field|fields]]. When cotton prices are manipulated in [[new-york|New York]] and Boston, it is black farmers in [[alabama|Alabama]] who feel the effects—in lower prices for their crop, in tighter [[debt|credit]], in deeper debt.
