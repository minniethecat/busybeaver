# Sharecropping

Sharecropping in *The Quest of the Silver Fleece* is the economic system that replaces slavery in form but preserves much of its substance. Black farmers [[labor|work]] the cotton [[field|fields]] of white landowners under contracts that bind them "hand and foot to the landlord" (012-072), a system the novel presents as the central economic obstacle to black progress.

## The Tenant System

The [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]] plantation operates through tenant farming. [[zora|Zora]] and [[bles-alwyn|Bles]]'s [[the-school|school]] project involves plans to "buy" tenants away from the [[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]]s and establish them on an independent farm (032-032). The phrase "our own farm with our own tenants" (032-059) captures the project's ambition: to transform tenancy from a trap into a path toward autonomy.

## Debt and Dependency

The novel emphasizes how the crop-lien system traps tenants in permanent debt. The "back debts of [[the-tenant-farmers|the tenants]]—no possible crops could pay" them (033-044), and the entire system "grew on the backs of the black tenants" (018-054). When cotton prices rose, "[[the-tenant-farmers|the tenants]] had already sold their cotton" (030-066), capturing the structural timing of [[greed|exploitation]]: tenants sold at harvest lows while landlords profited from later price increases.

## The Complicated Contract

The "complicated contract binding the tenant hand and foot to the landlord" (012-072) is the legal instrument of this dependency. [[colonel-cresswell|Colonel Cresswell]] resents "further interference with our tenants" (008-010), treating black labor as property whose management is a white prerogative. [[john-taylor|Taylor]]'s question—"Tightening up on [[the-tenant-farmers|the tenants]]?" (018-044)—acknowledges that the squeeze on black farmers is a deliberate, adjustable instrument of [[power|control]].
