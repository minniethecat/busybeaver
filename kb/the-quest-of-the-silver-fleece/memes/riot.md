# The Riot

The riot in *The Quest of the Silver Fleece* is less a single event than a pervasive threat—the possibility of [[race|racial]] violence that haunts the novel's [[society|social order]]. Du Bois evokes "the riot of thirst and the orgies of" violence (012-004) as the dark underside of Southern civilization, the explosion that the system of plantation [[power|control]] is designed to prevent but also perpetually generates.

## The Undercurrent

Riot is not depicted directly but invoked as the ever-present alternative to order. Men with "money in their pockets and riot in their hearts" (020-029) represent the volatile combination of economic grievance and [[race|racial]] animus. The "gathering" (020-029) where such feelings accumulate is the seedbed of mob violence—any crowd can become a riot, any grievance can tip into bloodshed.

## Racial Violence

The novel's treatment of riot is inseparable from its treatment of race. The plantation system is itself a form of organized violence, and the riot is what happens when that organization breaks down. Du Bois implies that the entire [[society|social order]] of [[tooms-county|Tooms County]] rests on the threat of violence—the overseer's whip, the sheriff's gun, the mob's rope—and that "rioting" (022-004) thoughts are not exceptional but structural.

## Containment and Eruption

That the riot never fully materializes as a depicted event is itself significant. The novel is interested in the mechanisms that contain [[race|racial]] violence—[[religion|the church]], the [[the-school|school]], the law—and in the fragility of those mechanisms. "Rioting through it again" (022-004) suggests that the potential for violence circulates continuously, ready to surface when containment fails.
