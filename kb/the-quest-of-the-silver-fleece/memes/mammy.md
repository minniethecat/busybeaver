# Mammy

The figure of the mammy appears in *The Quest of the Silver Fleece* as both a specific character type and a vessel for African American folk tradition. Du Bois presents the mammy figure with a complexity that challenges the simplistic stereotype common in early twentieth-century Southern literature, investing her with ancestral power and cultural authority.

## Zora's Mammy

The most significant mammy figure in the novel is [[zora]]'s maternal caretaker, whose folk wisdom and storytelling shape Zora's worldview. Her mammy transmits a heritage that blends the supernatural with ancestral pride:

> "I'se heard 'em, 'cause my mammy is a witch." [005-002]

This declaration—that the mammy is a witch—is not presented as ignorant superstition but as a source of power and identity. The stories of "a witch for a mammy" [005-009] and "mammy's pappy was a king's son" [005-058] give Zora a sense of ancestral dignity and magical possibility that sustains her through hardship. The claim of royal African lineage, passed through the mammy, offers an alternative genealogy to the degradation imposed by slavery.

> "You see, mammy's pappy was a king's son, and he was brought from Africa and set to work." [005-058]

The mammy figure is also a source of practical protection and clear-eyed wisdom. "Boys, dey is good to de're old mammy, but dey'se high strung" [004-073] captures the affectionate but unsentimental relationship between the Black children and their caretaker. The question "Do you s'pose mammy's the witch?" [005-078] suggests the awe and uncertainty with which children regard this figure of hidden power.

## Beyond the Stereotype

Du Bois complicates the mammy archetype by showing her as a keeper of African-descended spiritual knowledge rather than merely a devoted servant. The figure connects to broader themes of oral tradition, cultural memory, and the hidden power of Black women within the plantation system. When characters invoke "my own mammy" [019-057] or when a child's first appeal is to his "black mammy" [018-057], they reference a relationship that carries emotional weight beyond the economic arrangement of servitude.

The novel also shows how the mammy's authority extends beyond the Black community. Her wisdom and folk knowledge command a reluctant respect even from white characters who would prefer to dismiss her. The mammy's stories—of witches, kings, and falling stars [005-004]—preserve a cultural memory that the plantation system was designed to erase.

## Cultural Memory and Resistance

Through the mammy figure, Du Bois explores how African American oral tradition survived and was transmitted within the constraints of the plantation economy. The mammy is both a product of that economy and a point of resistance to it—her stories maintain connections to African heritage even as her labor sustains the white household. This duality is central to Du Bois's treatment: the mammy is not a figure of simple victimhood or romanticized nobility, but a complex actor navigating the contradictions of her position while preserving a heritage her employers cannot touch.
