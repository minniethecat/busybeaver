# Miss Wynn

A young white woman of Washington's political society, Miss Wynn moves through drawing rooms and Senate offices with the same practiced ease. Her social graces mask a strategic intelligence that makes her an unlikely but effective advocate for Southern educational reform.

## Character Overview

Miss Wynn represents the ambiguous position of white women in the political economy of the post-Reconstruction era. Her power derives entirely from her social connections and her skill at deploying them—she cannot vote or hold office, yet she shapes legislation through the men who depend on her good opinion. Du Bois uses her to illustrate both the possibilities and the limits of elite white female influence.

> "Miss Wynn's air was so quietly assured" [022-041]

Her quiet assurance is her primary weapon—a composure that commands attention in rooms where women were expected to be decorative rather than directive.

## Key Relationships

Miss Wynn's relationship with [[senator-smith|Senator Smith]] is the axis around which her political influence turns. She manages him with a combination of flattery, deference, and unmistakable direction—the arts of power available to women of her class and race. With [[mary-cresswell|Mary Cresswell]] and [[mrs-vanderpool|Mrs. Vanderpool]], she forms part of a network of women who quietly shape the [[the-school|school]]'s fate from behind the scenes.

## Key Scenes

> "My friend, Miss Wynn." The Senator glanced at Miss Wynn and she bowed demurely. [022-046–047]

In the Senate anteroom, Miss Wynn orchestrates the meeting that brings the school bill to Senator Smith's attention. Her performance of deference masks her role as the meeting's true director.

> "Precisely," said Miss Wynn. "And I came to tell you, Senator, that your party needs you—and I need you too." She tapped her boot on the carpet. [022-076–088]

This scene reveals her method: she frames political necessity as personal appeal, making the Senator's cooperation feel like gallantry rather than compliance.

## Character Arc

Miss Wynn does not undergo dramatic transformation—her arc is one of quiet effectiveness. By the novel's political climax, she has proven that a well-placed word in a drawing room can move legislation more decisively than a speech on the Senate floor. Her power, however, remains contingent on the goodwill of white men, and the novel offers no illusions about its ultimate limits.
