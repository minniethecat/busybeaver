# Wealth

Wealth in *The Quest of the Silver Fleece* is a product of extraction. It flows from the cotton [[field|fields]] of the Black Belt, through the trading houses of Wall Street, and into the drawing rooms of Northern philanthropy—everywhere carrying with it the unacknowledged cost of black labor. Du Bois traces [[economics|wealth]]'s circulation through multiple regimes: the planter's landed estate, the speculator's market manipulation, the philanthropist's conscience, and the political machine's patronage net[[labor|work]].

## Historical Context

The period Du Bois depicts—the early twentieth century—was the high point of the first Gilded Age, when vast fortunes were amassed through industrial combination, financial speculation, and resource extraction. The novel's account of how cotton [[economics|wealth]] was generated tracks with historical reality: the cotton [[economics|trade]] was a linchpin of American and British industrial capitalism, and the fortunes it created in [[new-york|New York]] and New England were built directly on the labor of black [[the-tenant-farmers|sharecroppers]] and [[the-tenant-farmers|tenant farmers]] in the South.

## The Origin of Wealth: Black Labor

The novel insists on a single point of origin for the [[economics|wealth]] it depicts. [[cotton|Cotton]], produced by black hands on Southern soil, is the raw material of American prosperity. [[john-taylor|John Taylor]]'s career as a cotton broker is built on "his knowledge of the world's nakedness and of black men's toil"—knowledge he applies "in such a way as to bring himself [[economics|wealth]]" (006-003). The passage anatomizes the supply chain of American capitalism: black men "starved in [[alabama|Alabama]]" producing the cotton, white men "froze in Siberia" lacking it, and men like [[john-taylor|Taylor]] and Grey grew rich in the gap between production and consumption.

This is not, Du Bois emphasizes, an accidental arrangement. [[john-taylor|Taylor]] is "but following the teaching of his highest ideal, lately deceased, Mr. Job Grey," who "had so successfully manipulated the cotton market" that both producer and consumer suffered while he profited (006-003). Wealth is a skill, a practice, a tradition—taught and learned and refined across generations.

## The Planter's Wealth

[[colonel-cresswell|Cresswell]]'s estate—the great oaks, the tended lawn, the flowers—displays what [[miss-smith|Miss Smith]] recognizes as "signs of taste and [[economics|wealth]]." Her immediate, passionate rejoinder—"But it was built on a moan"—condenses the novel's entire political [[economics|economy]] into six words (012-138). The beauty of the plantation is inseparable from the suffering that produced it. Wealth is not innocent; it carries within it the sound of those it has immiserated.

## The Philanthropist's Wealth

[[mrs-grey|Mrs. Grey]]'s fortune exemplifies the novel's treatment of [[economics|wealth]] as morally compromised at its source. "The rich [[mrs-grey|Mrs. Grey]] was at the crisis of her fortunes," having inherited "more [[cotton-mill|mill]]ions than she could easily count" (006-012). Her impulse—to spend her money "in bettering the world right off"—is blocked by Easterly, her husband's former partner, who finds philanthropy "not only foolish but wicked, and, incidentally, distinctly unprofitable to him" (006-012). The passage exposes the structural opposition between [[economics|wealth]] [[greed|accumulation]] and [[economics|wealth]] redistribution, even within a single fortune.

The irony deepens when [[miss-smith|Miss Smith]], desperate for five thousand dollars to buy land for the [[the-school|school]], reflects that the sum she needs is "so little—so little beside what this woman squandered" (002-009). The vast gulf between the philanthropist's surplus and the [[the-school|school]]'s need measures the distance between the two worlds that [[economics|wealth]] connects and divides.

## Wealth and Power

The connection between [[economics|wealth]] and power is made explicit through [[zora|Zora]]'s early observation. "They know things that give them power and [[economics|wealth]] and make them rule," she says of the white world (005-018). The sequence is deliberate: knowledge produces both [[economics|wealth]] and power, and the two together produce rule. [[bles-alwyn|Bles]], confronting the enormity of the system aligned against him, recognizes that he faces "a world of power, opinion, [[economics|wealth]], opportunity"—a world in which he, "the one, must cringe and bear in silence lest the world crash about the ears of his people" (008-070).

## Wealth and the School

The [[the-school|school]]'s [[love|relationship]] to [[economics|wealth]] is necessarily ambivalent. It needs money to survive—the five thousand dollars, the endowment, the land—but the [[economics|wealth]] available to it comes from the same system that produces the poverty it fights. [[mrs-vanderpool|Mrs. Vanderpool]]'s gown leaves "lingering perfume" in the [[the-school|school]]'s attic (002-009), a sensory trace of the world of "[[economics|wealth]] and leisure and some breeding" (006-026) that exists in relation to the [[the-school|school]] as donor to supplicant, not as equal partner in transformation.
