# The Founding of the School

[[mary-taylor|Mary Taylor]]'s arrival in [[tooms-county|Tooms County]] and the establishment of her [[the-school|school]] is the novel's inaugural event—the act that brings education to the black children of the cotton [[field|fields]] and sets in motion the conflicts that will define the narrative.

## Mary Taylor's Arrival

[[mary-taylor|Mary Taylor]] arrives from New England carrying the assumptions of Northern [[philanthropy|reform]]: that education is a universal good, that ignorance is the root of [[race|racial]] in[[freedom|equality]], and that a dedicated teacher can transform a community. Her first encounters with [[tooms-county|Tooms County]] test every one of these assumptions (001-049).

## The First Students

The children who come to the [[the-school|school]]—[[bles-alwyn|Bles Alwyn]] among them—arrive from the cotton [[field|fields]], from the tenant cabins, from [[the-swamp-land|the swamp]]. Each brings a different [[love|relationship]] to [[education|learning]], a different set of hopes and constraints. The [[the-school|school]]'s founding is also the founding of a community.

## Opposition and Support

The white power structure of [[tooms-county|Tooms County]] views the [[the-school|school]] with suspicion. Educated black [[labor|workers]] are harder to [[power|control]]. But some white figures offer tentative support, recognizing that a stable labor force requires a minimum of literacy. The [[the-school|school]] is born into a contested political space from its first day.
