# Thralldom

The system of slavery and human bondage that operates most visibly at Utterbol but extends in various forms throughout the novel's world. Thralldom is the moral and political antithesis of the freedom sought by Ralph and embodied by the Well at the World's End. It represents not merely individual captivity but an entire social order built on the ownership of human beings.

## Overview

Thralldom appears in the narrative as both a specific institution (the war-taken thralls of Utterbol) and a broader metaphor for unfreedom. The word "thrall" — Morris's preferred term, drawn from Old Norse *þræll* — carries medieval and saga connotations that distinguish it from the racialized chattel slavery of the Atlantic world: thralls in Morris's universe are war-captives, debt-bondsmen, and the conquered, their status tied to military fortune rather than inherent identity.

The first explicit mention comes when Ralph witnesses the aftermath of raiding: "captives, some going afoot and some for weariness borne in wains; for all these war-taken thralls were women and women-children; of males there was none" (013-007). This gendering of thralldom — women and children taken, men killed — establishes the system as one of sexual as well as economic exploitation.

## Thralldom at Utterbol

The Lord of Utterbol's domain is the novel's most fully realized slave-state. The system operates through multiple mechanisms:

### The Let-Pass System

Utterbol controls movement through its territory via a passport system that restricts who may enter, leave, or travel through. This bureaucratic apparatus of control complements the direct violence of enslavement — it is thralldom as administration, not merely as force.

### The Thrall Economy

Thralls at Utterbol serve multiple economic functions: domestic labor, agricultural work, and the servicing of the Lord's military apparatus. The "war-taken thralls" (013-007) are the raw material of a continuous cycle: raid → capture → enslave → exploit → raid again. This is not incidental to Utterbol's power but its foundation.

### The Queen's Role

The Queen of Utterbol, described as "a devouring sea of souls" (001-016), presides over the thrall system. Her sorcery and her political power are intertwined: she does not merely rule a slave-state but is the symbolic devourer at its center, consuming the lives of those who fall into her domain.

## Thralldom as Theme

### The Antithesis of the Well

The Well at the World's End offers freedom, renewal, and liberation. Thralldom offers the opposite: captivity, depletion, and the reduction of persons to property. Every character who seeks the Well is fleeing some form of thralldom — Ralph escaping the confinement of Upmeads, Ursula escaping captivity at Utterbol, the Lady of Abundance escaping her sorcerous enslavement.

### The Gendering of Thralldom

Morris's depiction of thralldom is notably gendered. The war-taken thralls are "women and women-children" (013-007); the Lady of Abundance's enslavement by the Sorceress is explicitly gendered torment; Ursula is a thrall at Utterbol when Ralph first encounters her. Thralldom in the novel is a system that particularly targets women, combining economic exploitation with sexual subjection.

### Freedom as the Central Value

The novel's moral universe is organized around the opposition between freedom and thralldom. Just governance — King Peter's Upmeads, the Queen of Goldburg's city — is defined by the absence of thralldom. Tyranny — Gandolf's Utterbol, the Sorceress's wildwood house — is defined by its presence. The quest for the Well is, at its deepest level, a quest for a world without thralls.

## The Burgers' Complicity

The commercial cities of the Burg of the Four Friths, Goldburg, and Whitwall maintain an ambiguous relationship with thralldom. They do not practice it directly — their citizens are free — but they trade with Utterbol and benefit from goods produced by thrall labor. The Burgers' moral unease is captured in their description of the Queen of Utterbol: they fear her sorcery and her "devouring" power but do not refuse to trade with her domain. This economic complicity complicates the novel's moral geography: even the "free" cities are entangled in the thrall economy.
