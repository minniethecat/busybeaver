# The Dale of Lore

The Dale of Lore is a secluded valley deep in the wilderness, known to only a few. It is the place where the Lady of Abundance spent her girlhood as a goatherd under the dominion of a sorceress — and where she first encountered a knight, setting in motion the chain of events that would bring her to the Castle of Abundance.

## Overview

The Dale of Lore enters the narrative in Chapter 28, when the Lady of Abundance tells Ralph the story of her youth. She describes how "my feet had worn a path through the wilderness to the Dale of Lore" [028-013] — a phrase that captures both her isolation and her intimate knowledge of the wild land surrounding it. The Dale is not merely a setting but the stage for the formative events of her life: the encounter with a beautiful knight, her decision to flee the sorceress, and her emergence into womanhood.

## Geography & Description

The Dale of Lore lies in a wilderness region, reached by a path worn through heath and scrub. The Lady describes driving her goats there daily: "on Midsummer Day I went my ways to the dale according to my wont" [028-013]. It is a place of pasture and seclusion, with "the best of the pasture" [028-029] where goats could be tethered safely. An old woman — a "carline" — dwelt there, who was neither the sorceress nor entirely a friend, but who served as a keeper of knowledge and a witness to the Lady's transformation.

The wider wilderness surrounding the Dale contained the sorceress's house, with its "three great books there laid on the lectern" [028-029], and the hut where the Lady kept her goats at night. The Dale itself seems to have been a place of daylight and relative safety, a green island in a wild land.

## Inhabitants

During the Lady's girlhood, the Dale was frequented by three figures: the Lady herself as a young goatherd, the carline who dwelt there, and the unnamed knight who rode across the heath one Midsummer Day [028-013]. The sorceress who held the Lady in thrall did not come to the Dale — it was a place beyond her immediate reach, and it was to the Dale that the Lady fled after slaying her [028-029].

## Role in the Narrative

### The First Encounter

On Midsummer Day, the Lady — then a girl tending her goats — saw "a bright thing coming over the heath" which proved to be "a weaponed man riding a white horse" [028-013]. This knight was the first beautiful young man she had ever seen, and her description of the meeting is suffused with wonder: "the heart leaped within me at his beauty; for though the carline had told me of the loveliness of the sons of men, that was but words and I knew not what they meant" [028-013].

The knight gave her a collar to wear about her neck — a gift that would later mark her — and rode away. The Lady returned "down into the Dale of Lore" [028-023] where the carline examined the collar and foresaw a greater destiny.

### Flight and Liberation

After slaying the sorceress, the Lady made for the Dale: "I drove the goats out of their house and went my way towards the Dale of Lore, and said to myself that the carline would teach me what further to do" [028-029]. She "slept sweetly and softly amongst my goats" there, the Dale serving as her first refuge of freedom.

### A Place of Love

The knight returned to the Dale, and the Lady describes their reunion in one of the novel's most lyrical passages: "we talked together long till the day was gone. Then we kissed and embraced each other in the Dale of Lore, and the darkness of summer seemed but short for our delight" [029-007]. The Dale thus becomes the site of the Lady's first love — and the beginning of her path toward becoming the Lady of Abundance.

### Lingering Memory

Even much later in the narrative, the Dale of Lore remains a reference point. When the Lady — now transformed — meets a woman who seems to know her, the woman asks: "hast thou forgotten the Dale of Lore?" [031-030], a question that links the Lady's present grandeur to her humble origins in that hidden valley.

### Significance

The Dale of Lore is the place of origin for one of the novel's central figures. It represents the hidden, wild places where destinies are formed away from the sight of courts and kingdoms. Its name — "Lore" — suggests knowledge, and indeed it is the place where the Lady first learned of the wider world beyond the wilderness.
