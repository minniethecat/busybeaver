# The Innocent Folk

A peaceful, isolated community dwelling in a fertile valley in the far eastern reaches of the world, beyond Utterness and the harsh lands. They are characterized by their gentleness, their concern for the Well at the World's End, and their practice of sending messengers to seek its location. Their name — "the Innocent Folk" — suggests a people untainted by the political corruption, sorcery, and violence that mark the novel's other societies.

## Overview

The Innocent Folk are introduced in the chapter "Now Come the Messengers of the Innocent Folk" (079-001), when Ralph and Ursula encounter messengers traveling from their land. The messengers — "two young men and an elder" — come with oxen and provisions (079-002), approaching as peaceful travelers rather than warriors or traders.

Ralph and Ursula subsequently travel to the Land of the Innocent Folk (080-001), where they receive hospitality and rest before continuing their journey toward the Well. The Innocent Folk become one of the final sanctuaries on the quest — the last peaceful community before the climactic approach to the Well.

## Nature and Society

The Innocent Folk are explicitly set apart from the novel's other societies. They are not warriors (like the Champions of the Dry Tree), not traders (like the Burgers of the Four Friths), not ruled by a tyrant (like Utterbol), and not organized around a monarchy (like Upmeads). They appear to live in a communal arrangement governed by elders, with messengers sent on behalf of the whole community rather than any individual leader.

The chapter title "They Come to the Land of the Innocent Folk" (080-001) frames their territory as a distinct realm — a "Land" with its own identity — yet no king, lord, or ruler is ever named. This absence of hierarchy is itself a political statement within Morris's world: the Innocent Folk represent a society that has transcended the need for coercive governance.

## The Messengers

The messengers of the Innocent Folk are a recurring motif in the novel's later chapters. They travel outward from their secluded valley to gather intelligence about the Well, suggesting a sophisticated information-gathering network that operates through peaceable means rather than espionage or conquest.

When Ralph and Ursula take their leave, "They would have taken leave of the Innocent Folk in the said vale, but those others must needs go with them a little further, and would not leave them" (088-005). This small detail — the Folk's insistence on accompanying departing guests — illustrates their ethos: hospitality is not transactional but relational, extending beyond the guest's stay into the journey itself.

## Role in the Quest Infrastructure

The Innocent Folk form part of the network of sanctuaries, guides, and waypoints that enable the quest for the Well. In the sequence of helpers: the Carline gives the necklace, the Damsel of Bourton Abbas provides the test of single-heartedness, the Lady of Abundance offers shelter and healing, Ursula becomes companion, the Sage provides lore — the Innocent Folk offer the final communal sanctuary before the Well itself.

Their role is distinct from the Sage's (who provides knowledge) and the Sorceress's (who provides hallowed raiment): the Innocent Folk provide rest, provisions, and the reassurance of human community at the quest's most isolated stage. They are the last reminder of the civilized world before Ralph and Ursula enter the desert that leads to the Well.
