# Hugh

The second son of King Peter of Upmeads, characterized by his father as "a stout rider and lifter, but headstrong and foolhardy, and over bounteous a skinker" (001). Hugh draws the lot to ride north — the direction most associated with danger and the Wood Debateable.

## Overview

Hugh is the warrior brother: physically capable, impulsive, and generous to a fault. His father pairs him with Lancelot Longtongue as squire, noting "where is Hugh, there is trouble and debate" (001) — a squire who combines courtly skill with fighting ability is necessary to match Hugh's temperament.

## Biography

### Origins

Born in Upmeads as King Peter's second son, Hugh grew up alongside his brothers in the small kingdom. He is familiar with the Wood Debateable to the north, having both "ridden therein and ran therefrom valiantly" (001-010). His experience with the perilous northern forest shapes his readiness for adventure.

### Journey / Arc

Hugh draws the lot to ride north with Lancelot Longtongue as his squire. The northern direction carries symbolic weight — it is toward the Wood Debateable where King Peter's father and eldest son died, toward danger and the unknown. Hugh's headstrong nature makes him suited for this perilous path.

## Character Analysis

### Traits & Personality

Hugh is defined by three qualities: physical prowess ("a stout rider and lifter"), impulsiveness ("headstrong and foolhardy"), and excessive generosity ("over bounteous a skinker"). This combination marks him as the most chivalric of the brothers — a knight-errant type whose virtues and flaws are the classic ones of medieval romance.

### Relationships

Hugh's squire, Lancelot Longtongue, is chosen specifically to complement him: "good of seeming and can compass all courtesy, and knoweth logic" yet also "a proper man of his hands" (001). The pairing of a headstrong warrior with a courteous, logical squire mirrors the classic knight-squire dynamic.

### Themes

Hugh represents the martial aspect of the quest — adventure sought through strength of arms. His northward journey toward the Wood Debateable connects him to the family history of battle and loss that shaped King Peter's pacifism.
