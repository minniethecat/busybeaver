# The Trial of the Lady of Abundance

The Trial of the Lady of Abundance is a backstory event in *The Well at the World's End*, in which the Lady — captured during the War of the Tower — was brought before ecclesiastical judges on charges of sorcery for having "beguiled" her husband the King's son. The trial episode crystallises the novel's critique of how accusations of witchcraft served as instruments of political and gendered persecution.

## Background

After the King's forces captured the Lady and her lord during the War of the Tower, the King sought to dispose of her permanently:

> "the king would have had me lie there till I perished, that I might be forgotten utterly; but there were many of the king's knights who murmured at this, and would not forget me" (008-020)

The King's initial impulse — to let her rot in prison until death — reveals a ruler who prefers secret elimination over public process. But the knights' "murmuring" complicated his plan. Their refusal to "forget" the Lady speaks to her charisma and the loyalty she inspired even among the King's own men. Pressure from his own knights forced the King to adopt a more formal mechanism of destruction — a trial that would lend the appearance of law to a predetermined outcome.

The political context is crucial: the King could not simply execute the Lady without alienating his own supporters. The trial was a compromise between his desire to destroy her and the knights' insistence on some semblance of justice.

## The Charge

The specific charge — sorcery for "beguiling" the King's son — is revealing in its logic:

> "so the king being constrained, had me brought forth to be judged by his bishops of sorcery for the beguiling of my lord" (008-020)

The word "beguiling" is doing heavy ideological work. It transforms a consensual marriage — the King's son had chosen the Lady, wedded her "whether others were lief or loth" — into an act of supernatural coercion. The underlying assumption is that no woman could legitimately win a prince's love through her own qualities; therefore her influence must be unnatural, demonic.

This logic recurs throughout medieval and early modern witch trials: a woman's desirability is itself treated as evidence of witchcraft. Morris's use of the term "beguiling" captures the circular reasoning precisely — the Lady is accused of sorcery because the prince loved her, and the prince loved her because of sorcery.

## The Trial

The trial was conducted by bishops — ecclesiastical judges whose presence lent the proceedings a veneer of religious authority:

> "as was like to be, I was brought in guilty of sorcery, and doomed to be burned in the Great Square in three days time" (008-020)

The Lady's phrasing — "as was like to be" — conveys her complete lack of surprise at the verdict. She never entertained the possibility of acquittal. The trial was not a genuine inquiry into guilt or innocence; it was a ritual legitimation of a death sentence already decided.

The venue — "the Great Square" — was chosen for maximum public visibility. Burning was the standard penalty for witchcraft, but the choice of a central public location rather than an out-of-the-way execution ground indicates the King's interest in spectacle. The death was meant to be witnessed, to serve as a warning.

## The Sentence and its Context

The Lady recounts the sentence with a striking absence of self-pity, addressing Ralph's visible distress:

> "Nay, my friend, thou hast no need to look so troubled; for thou seest that I was not burned. This is the selfsame body that was tied to the stake in the market place of the king's city many a year ago" (008-020)

The aside — "this is the selfsame body" — is both reassurance and theological claim. The Lady's body, which has survived the stake and later drunk from the Well, is a testament against the judgment that would have destroyed it. Her matter-of-fact tone ("thou seest that I was not burned") contrasts starkly with the horror of what she describes.

The three-day interval between sentence and execution is a narrative necessity — it provides the window for rescue — but it also reflects a grim reality of medieval justice: condemned prisoners were often given a brief period for confession and repentance before execution.

## The Rescue

The execution was prevented by armed intervention. Knights sympathetic to the Lady and her lord — including a young knight who loved the Lady — gathered forces and confronted the King's host:

> "Then the spears went up and from the rest, and swords were sheathed" (008-022)

The moment of armed confrontation nearly became a second battle. Three ancient knights of the Lineage stepped forward to negotiate:

> "there went forth three ancient knights from out of the king's host and came up to him and spake with him" (008-022)

These three knights — unnamed representatives of the old aristocracy — embody an alternative authority to both the King's political power and the bishops' ecclesiastical power. Their intervention produces the truce:

> "truce had been hanselled them for this time, but on these terms... that in any case the Sorceress should get her gone from those walls" (008-022)

The truce terms are revealing: the Lady must leave the city, but she is not required to die. The compromise — exile rather than execution — satisfies the King's need to be rid of her while respecting the knights' refusal to see her burned. The label "the Sorceress" in the truce terms shows how thoroughly the trial's verdict had been accepted as fact even by those who saved her life.

## Aftermath

The Lady departed the city the same day she was to have been burned:

> "it was three hours before noon that I was to have been burned" (008-022)

The temporal precision — "three hours before noon" — gives the escape its dramatic urgency. She rode out with a company of knights and men-at-arms, not as a fugitive but under the protection of armed escort.

The trial and near-execution had lasting consequences for the Lady. It deepened her bond with the Men of the Dry Tree, who had been among her rescuers. It cemented her lifelong wariness of established authority — both royal and ecclesiastical. And it established her public identity as "the Sorceress," a label she could never fully shed, even among those who loved her.

The trial episode also serves a structural purpose in the novel: it provides the emotional foundation for the Lady's later generosity toward the persecuted. Having been unjustly condemned herself, she extends protection to others who flee unjust power — Ralph among them.

## Thematic Significance

The trial brings together several of the novel's central themes. The alliance of throne and altar against a woman who threatens neither, only inspires inconvenient loyalty, exposes the institutional foundations of misogyny. The knights' rescue — secular, martial authority intervening against ecclesiastical judgment — reflects Morris's medievalist preference for chivalric honour over clerical power. And the Lady's survival, her "selfsame body" still living long after the bishops who condemned her are dust, is a quiet vindication of the natural over the institutional, the lived life over the written verdict.
