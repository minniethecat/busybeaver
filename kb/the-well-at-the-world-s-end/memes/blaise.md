# Blaise

The eldest of the four sons of King Peter of Upmeads, described by his father as "wise and prudent, but no great man of his hands" (001). Blaise draws the lot to ride east, accompanied by the squire Richard the Red.

## Overview

Blaise is introduced in the opening chapter as the oldest brother, having "seen thirty winters" (001-002). His father's characterization of him as wise but not physically formidable establishes the diversity of the four brothers' qualities. Blaise rides east, the direction associated with the rising sun and new beginnings.

## Biography

### Origins

Born in Upmeads as the first son of King Peter, Blaise grew to adulthood in the little kingdom. At thirty winters, he feels the constraint of his father's small domain and joins his brothers in pressing for permission to seek adventure (001).

### Journey / Arc

When King Peter devises the lottery to determine each son's direction, Blaise accepts the outcome with equanimity: "Sir, as for me I say we will do after your commandment, to take what road luck may show us" (001). He rides east with Richard the Red as his squire, toward unknown lands and fortunes.

## Character Analysis

### Traits & Personality

King Peter describes Blaise as "wise and prudent" (001), marking him as the intellectual brother. His measured response to the lottery — accepting without complaint — demonstrates the prudence his father noted. Unlike Hugh's headstrong valor or Gregory's sluggish courtesy, Blaise's defining quality is considered judgment.

### Relationships

Blaise is the first-named of the four brothers and the eldest. His relationship with Ralph is established through contrast: Blaise is the known, proven quantity; Ralph is "young and untried" (001). Richard the Red, a "fierce carle and a doughty" who "knoweth well all feats of arms" (001), complements Blaise's wisdom with martial skill.

### Themes

Blaise represents the measured, prudent approach to adventure — a counterpoint to Ralph's more romantic, impulsive quest. His eastward journey suggests knowledge-seeking (the direction of dawn and learning in medieval symbolism).
