# Swevenham

Swevenham is a small town near [[goldburg|Whitwall]], visible from its eastern walls across the meadows. It holds a unique place in the geography of the quest: here, local memory preserves first-hand lore of the [[the-well-at-the-worlds-end|Well at the World's End]], passed down through generations of townsfolk. It is the birthplace of Richard, the old retainer of [[upmeads|Upmeads]], who returns to Swevenham as [[ralph-of-upmeads|Ralph]]'s agent to gather intelligence about the Well.

## Overview

Swevenham first enters the narrative in Chapter 37, when Richard points across the meadows from Whitwall's Petergate Bar and tells Ralph: "turn about and look straight over the meadows in a line with yonder willow-tree, and tell me what thou seest" (037-010). What Ralph sees, beyond the plain and the river, is Swevenham — the town where Richard was born and where, fifty years earlier, he witnessed the departure of three seekers bound for the Well.

The town's name — "Sweven-ham," the home of dreams or visions — is thematically resonant. It is a place where the dream of the Well lives in oral tradition. Richard's decision to return there alone, to coax tales from old acquaintances without "over much digging and hoeing of the ground" (037-015), acknowledges that Swevenham's knowledge is not written but held in the memory of its elders.

## Geography

Swevenham lies in the fertile plain east of Whitwall, approximately four miles distant (037-014). It is situated across a river — "a fair plain spreading wide, and a river running through it" (037-010) — and is reached by roads crossing the meadows under Whitwall's walls. The town is sixty miles from the Castle of the Waste March, where Richard served after leaving Swevenham in his youth (037-013).

Richard describes Swevenham's inhabitants as "upcountry carles" whose tales "flow forth from them the better if they come without over much digging and hoeing of the ground; that is, without questioning" (037-015). This characterisation suggests a close-knit rural community where knowledge circulates orally and informally.

## In the Narrative

### Richard's Youth (Chapter 37)

Richard's account of his youth in Swevenham provides the town's most significant narrative moment. At eighteen years old, he witnessed three fellow townsfolk — two young men and one young woman — set out to seek the Well. They had acquired detailed lore from:

> An old man, a friend of a holy man, who had been at the Well itself.
>
> (037-012)

Richard never learned whether they returned; their departure spurred his own restlessness, and he left Swevenham shortly afterward to become a man-at-arms (037-013). The existence of a chain of testimony — "a holy man who had been at the Well" → "an old man, a friend of that holy man" → "three seekers" — establishes Swevenham as one of the very few places in the novel where the Well is not a distant legend but a reality attested by living memory.

### The Tales of Swevenham (Chapter 40)

Chapter 40, titled "Of the Tales of Swevenham," marks Richard's return from his solo reconnaissance. Though the tales themselves are not transcribed in full, the chapter title and Richard's report serve as a narrative hinge: what he learns in Swevenham shapes Ralph's next decisions on the road to the Well.

### Richard of Swevenham

Richard's association with the town is so strong that [[clement-chapman|Clement Chapman]] addresses him as "Richard of Swevenham" (038-007), suggesting that his origin there is widely known among the merchants and travellers of the region. The epithet links man and place: Richard's wisdom and his knowledge of the Well are products of Swevenham's oral culture.

## Significance

Swevenham functions in the novel's symbolic geography as a **repository of authentic lore**. In a world filled with rumours, tavern-tales, and deliberate deceptions about the Well, Swevenham stands out as a place where the chain of testimony remains unbroken. The old man who spoke to Richard's three townsfolk was himself "a friend of a holy man, who had been at the Well itself" — a lineage of knowledge only two removes from direct experience.

The town also serves as a counterpoint to the courtly intrigue of Utterbol and the commercial bustle of Whitwall. It is a place of oral tradition, old memory, and quiet persistence — qualities Morris consistently associates with the rural and the ancient.
