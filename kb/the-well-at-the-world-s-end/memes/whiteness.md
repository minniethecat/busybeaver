# Whiteness

A town at the threshold of the great mountains, serving as the gateway to the passes that lead to Goldburg. Its whitewashed timber houses on a long ridge give it its name, gleaming against the dark, bare mountain walls beyond.

## Overview

Whiteness lies some fifty miles from Cheaping Knowe, "close underneath the mountains" [025]. It is the door of the passes whereby men ride to Goldburg. The town is built along a long ridge at the end of a valley, with nothing green behind it — "naught but the huge and bare mountains" [025]. The Lord of Whiteness pays tribute to the Lord of Cheaping Knowe "rather for love of peace than for fear of him; for he was no ill lord, and free men lived well under him" [025].

## Geography & Description

The town's walls and towers are not very high or strong, for the steep hill on which it stands provides natural defense [025]. Unlike Cheaping Knowe, built entirely of stone, the houses of Whiteness are mostly timber, "done over with pargeting and whitened well" — hence the name [025]. Despite its lesser fortifications and size, Whiteness is "more cheerful of aspect than Cheaping Knowe," and its folk at the gates are described as "not so woebegone, and goodly enough" [025]. The great mountains tower behind it, dark, bare, and stony, appearing "very great and terrible" through the gaps in the foothills [025].

## Inhabitants

Free men live under the Lord of Whiteness, who is accounted a fair ruler. The town hosts chapmen and travelers passing between the eastern plain and Goldburg. Clement Chapman's fellowship lodges in the marketplace for five days, hiring additional men-at-arms and servants for the mountain crossing [025]. The Deacon of the Chapmen provides intelligence about travelers passing through [025].

## Role in the Narrative

### Key Events

The Fellowship of Clement Chapman arrives at Whiteness at sunset on the third day from Cheaping Knowe, traversing a land of tillage and pasture where men and women work as thralls — a sight that troubles Ralph [025]. At Whiteness, Ralph seeks news of the damsel: the Deacon of the Chapmen tells him that the wild man did bring her into the market, but then "turned away thence suddenly with her, without bringing her to the stone," and it was most like he intended to sell her to the Lord of Utterbol [025].

Five days the Fellowship abides at Whiteness, during which Clement waged additional men-at-arms, bringing the company to "a goodly company of fourscore and ten" before entering the mountain passes [025].

Upon their return journey, Ralph and Ursula pass through Whiteness, receiving "good entertainment" but tarrying only one night before riding on to Cheaping Knowe [032].

### Significance

Whiteness is the last outpost before the mountain crossing into the perilous eastern lands. It is here that Ralph loses the trail of the damsel, learning she has been taken toward Utterbol. The town marks a crucial turning point — beyond it lies Goldburg, the domain of the Lady of Abundance, and the lands of Utterbol where both peril and the path to the Well await.
