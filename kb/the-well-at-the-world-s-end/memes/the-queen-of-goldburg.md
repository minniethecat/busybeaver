# The Queen of Goldburg

The sole ruler of Goldburg at the time of Ralph's first visit, a young woman of beauty, wisdom, and compassion whose mysterious disappearance leaves an unresolved note of tragedy in the saga.

## Overview

Goldburg was ruled by a Queen — "for a woman ruled in Goldburg" (029-001). She lived in a palace of extraordinary craftsmanship, her innermost chamber "wrought rather by goldsmiths and jewellers than by masons and carvers" (029-005). Clement described her as "young, and so fair, and withal so wise" (029-007), and she proved her compassion by offering Ralph both hospitality and practical aid in his quest.

Her reign was remembered fondly by her people, but it ended in mystery. In the early spring of the year following Ralph's departure from Utterbol, the Queen vanished without trace — leaving behind only speculation and sorrow.

## Biography

### Origins

The Queen's lineage was known "by seeming" (032-014), though some among her people whispered that she was "of the fairy" — a being who "needed neither steed nor chariot to go where she would" (032-014). She ruled Goldburg alone, without a king at her side, commanding the loyalty of her people through wisdom rather than force.

### Ralph's First Visit

Clement brought Ralph to the Queen's presence on the fifth day of their stay in Goldburg. Her chambers impressed even the worldly Clement, but he told Ralph that "the Queen who sat there was the goodliest part thereof" (029-005).

At their first meeting, she questioned Ralph about his lineage, which he told her frankly. She offered him a choice between staying in Goldburg in comfort or continuing his dangerous quest — and when he chose the quest, she did not press but instead pledged her aid. She wrote him a letter of protection to show to the Lord of Utterbol, "which he will heed, if he heedeth aught my goodwill or enmity" (029-015). Though Ralph ultimately chose not to present the letter to the tyrant, he kept it as a token of her kindness.

The Queen's favor was no small thing. Clement observed that she "looked to me the other day as one who would deny thee few of thine askings" (029-020).

### Tragedy and Disappearance

When Ralph returned to Goldburg after drinking from the Well at the World's End, he found the kingdom transformed. A King now ruled where the Queen had been. Redhead told him the news: "There is the King's wedded wife, but her they call not Queen, but Lady." And of the Queen that was: "a Queen sat alone as ruler here a while ago; but whether she died, or what befell her, I know nothing" (032-008).

The innkeeper at the hostel gave Ralph the fuller account. In the early spring, "somewhat more than a year ago," the people awoke to find "the Queen's place was empty" (032-014). A search was mounted but nothing was found. One tale held that the Queen and a waiting-woman slipped away by boat in the March night, sailing downriver through the great wood. At sunrise, "she stood up in the fore part of the boat, and bade him turn the barque toward the shore, and even as the bows ran upon the sand, she leapt out and let the thicket cover her; nor have any of Goldburg seen her since" (032-015).

Those who knew her best believed she "had slain herself, as they thought, for some unhappiness of love. For indeed she had long gone about sad and distraught, though she neither wept, nor would say one word of her sorrow, whatsoever it might be" (032-014).

Ralph, hearing this, was "exceeding sorry" — and recognized with a pang of guilt that "she loved him, and how would it be if she might not outwear her love, or endure the days of Goldburg, and he far away?" (032-016).

## Character Analysis

### Traits & Personality

The Queen combined authority with compassion. Unlike the Lord of Utterbol, who ruled through fear, she governed through goodwill and the willing service of her people. Even her enemies respected her power — the Lord of Utterbol's men acknowledged that her letter carried real weight in their master's domain.

She was also marked by a deep private sorrow. She met Ralph with warmth and aid, yet her face was "worn by sorrow" (029-015) and her women spoke of her long sadness. Whatever grief consumed her, she bore it with dignity: "she neither wept, nor would say one word of her sorrow" (032-014).

### Themes

The Queen of Goldburg embodies the theme of love and loss that runs through Morris's romance. Where Ralph drinks from the Well and finds joyous renewal, the Queen's love for him — unrequited and unspoken — becomes her undoing. She represents the human cost of the quest: those left behind, those who loved and were not loved in return.
