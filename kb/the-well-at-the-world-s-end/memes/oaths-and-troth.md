Oaths and troth — the pledged faith between individuals — form the moral backbone of the world depicted in *The Well at the World's End*. In a landscape without strong centralized law, the sworn word binds knights to their lords, companions to one another, and lovers to their beloveds. The keeping or breaking of oaths is the novel's primary measure of character.

## Overview

The novel's society runs on oaths. The [[the-burg-of-the-four-friths|Burg of the Four Friths]] maintains its ancient custom of slaying enemies "by all the greatest oaths that we might swear, in the days when we first cast off their yoke." The Men of the [[the-dry-tree|Dry Tree]] are bound by a sworn fellowship. [[ralph-of-upmeads|Ralph]] himself is reminded by [[clement]] that his knightly oath compels his actions:

> "in doing as I do, my deed shall be but according to the duty bounden on me by mine oath, when Duke Osmond made me knight." [005-039]

Yet Clement sees through the formal language: "I see thee to be young, and that love and desire bind thee in closer bonds than thy knightly oath." [005-039] This tension between institutional oath and personal bond runs throughout the narrative.

## The Old Shepherd's Oath

Early in his journey, Ralph encounters an old shepherd who makes him swear an oath of mutual aid. The exchange establishes the ritual gravity of oath-making in this world:

> "Thou hast sworn an oath, and it is a good oath, and well sworn. Now if thou dost as thou swearest, words can but little thanks, yet deeds may. Wherefore if ever thou comest back hither, and art in such need that a throng of men may help thee therein; then let light a great fire upon each corner of the topmost wall of Bear Castle, and call to mind this watchword: 'Smite aside the axe, O Bear-father,' and then shalt thou see what shall betide thee for thy goodhap." [269-001]

The shepherd's response — "words can but little thanks, yet deeds may" — captures the novel's pragmatic approach to oaths: they are not mere formalities but actionable pledges with concrete consequences.

## The Dry Tree Oath

The most solemn oath in the narrative belongs to the Men of the Dry Tree. An aged member of the fellowship tells the [[the-lady-of-abundance|Lady of Abundance]]:

> "a band of us of the Dry Tree swore an oath together to seek her till we found her, that we might live and die together: but of that band of one score and one, am I the last one left that seeketh; for the rest are dead, or sick, or departed." [1937-002]

This oath, kept across fifty years and at the cost of twenty lives, exemplifies the novel's highest ideal: a promise so sacred that only death releases it. The elder's sons continue the quest — "these two men, they are my sons whom I have bred in the knowledge of these things" — demonstrating that the oath's binding force transcends a single generation.

## Troth Between Lovers

Morris uses "troth" specifically for the bond between lovers, distinct from the martial oaths of knights. When Ralph asks the Lady "And did the carline keep troth with thee?" she confirms: "She held her troth to me in all wise." [1905-001]

The word carries the weight of both romantic fidelity and legal covenant. Troth-plight — the medieval form of betrothal — appears when Ralph and [[ursula|Ursula]] become "troth-plight lovers," entering a bond as binding as any knightly oath. The distinction between "troth" (personal, intimate) and "oath" (public, institutional) is one the novel consistently maintains.

## The Vow of Deliverance

The Lady of Abundance makes a personal vow that drives significant action:

> "I swore to myself that I would set free the first captive or death-doomed creature that I came across, in honour of my pleasure and delight." [1657-001]

This self-imposed oath leads her to rescue prisoners from the Burg, and later to free a condemned woman — actions that ripple through the plot. The novel treats even private, unwitnessed vows as sacred obligations, suggesting that the truest oaths are those sworn to oneself.

## Ralph's Oath to Deliver Dorothea

When Ralph learns that [[the-damsel-of-bourton-abbas|Dorothea]] has been captured by the [[the-sons-of-the-rope|Sons of the Rope]], he swears to deliver her:

> "he would deliver that friend of his, that fair woman, from captivity; and would bring her back safe and sound to her own folk." [2599-001]

This vow propels him eastward and gives his quest renewed purpose after the death of the Lady of Abundance. The keeping of this oath — Ralph's deliverance of Dorothea — marks his transition from passive quester to active rescuer.

## Oaths as Structural Devices

Morris uses oaths to create dramatic tension. When Ralph's companions from [[swevenham|Swevenham]] swear "that back would they never, save they found the Well and could bear its water to the folk," their disappearance becomes a permanent mystery — the oath explains why they never returned, and their fidelity redeems what might otherwise be read as failure.

The novel's treatment of oaths reflects a world where honor is the only reliable currency. In the absence of courts and constables, the sworn word is the foundation upon which all trust — political, martial, and romantic — is built. The few characters who break their oaths ([[walter-the-black|Walter the Black]], in his false accusation against the Lady) are marked as irredeemable, while those who keep them across impossible odds are the novel's heroes.
