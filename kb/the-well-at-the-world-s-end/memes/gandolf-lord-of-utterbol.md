# Gandolf, Lord of Utterbol

The ruler of Utterbol, a powerful and feared lord whose domain represents the dark counterpoint to the peaceful rule of King Peter. Gandolf holds Ursula as a thrall and his riders patrol the lands near the Sage of Swevenham's dwelling.

## Overview

The Lord of Utterbol governs through fear. His "let-pass" system controls movement through his territory — without his scroll, the road to Utterness is "deadly, to any who is not furnished with a let-pass from the Lord of Utterbol" (029). Morfinn the Minstrel carries such a pass, "a writing charging all men to let pass and aid Morfinn the Minstrel, under pain of falling into the displeasure of Gandolf, Lord of Utterbol" (029).

## Biography

### Origins

Gandolf rules from Utterbol, described as "a very great house in a fair land" with "sundry roofs and many fair chambers" (032). Despite the beauty of his domain, his rule is characterized by thralldom and fear. His nephew serves as his lieutenant, described by Ursula as one "who like his eme was half my lover, half my tyrant" (032).

### Journey / Arc

Gandolf's power extends through his riders and his let-pass system. His men know the dwelling of the Sage of Swevenham, making the approach to the sage perilous for Ralph and Ursula. He represents the institutionalized oppression that stands opposite to the freedom of the quest.

## Character Analysis

### Traits & Personality

Gandolf is defined through the fear he inspires rather than direct narration. His let-pass system reveals a methodical tyrant who controls through bureaucracy as much as force. The description of his nephew — "half my lover, half my tyrant" (032) — suggests a dynasty built on coercion disguised as affection.

### Relationships

Gandolf's relationship with Ursula is that of captor and thrall. His riders hunt those who approach the Sage's dwelling without permission. His power is mediated through agents — riders, his nephew, the let-pass system — creating a pervasive atmosphere of surveillance and control.

### Themes

Gandolf represents institutional tyranny in Morris's political imagination — the opposite of King Peter's consensual rule. Where Peter governs through goodwill, Gandolf governs through writs and riders. His name (echoing Tolkien's Gandalf, though predating it) carries mythic resonance of dark power.
