# Geoffrey

**Geoffrey**, also known as **Geoffrey the Minstrel**, is a knight of the
[[the-knights-of-the-dry-tree|Fellowship of the Dry Tree]] who, in his youth,
was a devoted swain of the [[the-lady-of-abundance|Lady of Abundance]]. His
emotional reunion with her in old age is one of the most affecting scenes in
the later chapters.

## Youth and Devotion

In his youth, Geoffrey was a swain who "had nigh died" for the Lady of Abundance
when she fled with her husband from the old king. He followed her with "kind
service about the woods in the days when I was at my happiest," and became one
of the Dry Tree [032-012]. His devotion spanned decades, and he never forgot
the Lady even as he grew old and raised sons of his own.

## The Reunion

When the Lady returns from the [[the-well-at-the-worlds-end|Well at the World's End]],
she finds Geoffrey as an old man in the wilderness. Transformed by the Well's waters,
she is youthful and unrecognizable. She tests his memory, asking about shared
experiences from their youth — the white hind, Dame Joyce's youngest born, and the
great old oak. Overcome with emotion, Geoffrey weeps:

> "Who art thou? Art thou the daughter of my Lady, even as these are my sons?"
> [032-012]

Upon recognizing her, Geoffrey reminds the Lady of her old promise to return to
the fellowship of the Dry Tree [032-014]. His joy at her return is tempered only
by the weight of the years between them.

## Service in Later Days

Geoffrey informs the Lady that the Land of Abundance is at peace, inhabited by
simple folk who remember her, though "strange tales" are told of her there
[032-020]. He and his sons escort her to the Land of Abundance, where she takes
up dwelling in her castle [032-023].

He is also referenced as a skilled chess player: [[agatha|Agatha]] compares a
strategic military plan to "the chance betwixt Geoffrey the Minstrel and Black
Anselm, when they play at chess together" [060-020] — suggesting his reputation
extended beyond the Dry Tree fellowship.

## Character

Geoffrey embodies unwavering loyalty across a lifetime. His relationship with
the Lady spans from his youth as a devoted swain, through decades of separation,
to a tender reunion in old age. Unlike many knights in the tale who are defined
by combat prowess, Geoffrey is remembered for his constancy and his skill at
chess — a quieter, more enduring form of service.
