# Castle of the Scaur

A great castle-fortress built atop the Scaur, the cliff rising above the town of Hampton-under-Scaur, serving as the stronghold of the Champions of the Dry Tree and later the seat of Ralph of Upmeads's lordship over the surrounding lands.

## Overview

The Castle of the Scaur crowns the height of the Scaur, a great cliff or escarpment overlooking the town of Hampton (032-016). It was originally the stronghold of the Fellowship of the Dry Tree, a band of warrior-champions who built it after leaving the depths of the Wood Perilous. After Ralph of Upmeads' successful quest to the Well at the World's End, the Champions bestowed the castle upon him to hold as his own lordship (106-042). Under Ralph's rule, it became a centre of justice and protection, from which he cleansed the Wood Perilous of robbers and reivers.

## Geography and Strategic Position

The castle sits upon the Scaur, a precipitous cliff formation that gives the location its natural defensive strength. The Scaur rises abruptly from the surrounding countryside, making direct assault nearly impossible. Below it lies the town of Hampton, which came to be called Hampton-under-Scaur after the castle was built (032-016).

The castle commands views of the surrounding countryside, including the lands between Higham and the mountains, making it a strategic military position against the Burg of the Four Friths (106-055). This vantage allowed Ralph, as its lord, to monitor movement across a wide stretch of contested territory.

The location was deliberately chosen — not a hidden fastness like the Dry Tree's original lair in the depths of the Wood Perilous, but a visible, commanding presence on the landscape. The move from forest-hidden camp to clifftop castle marks the Dry Tree's transition from fugitive band to established military power.

## Construction and the Dry Tree

The Champions of the Dry Tree built the castle after their long tenure in the Wood Perilous. The castle's construction represents a significant organizational achievement for a band more associated with guerrilla warfare than with stonework:

> "the Champions of the Dry Tree, who had built the said castle after they had left the depths of the Wood Perilous" (032-016)

The shift from forest camp to stone fortress signals the Dry Tree's evolution from outlaws to a recognized — if still feared — political entity. A castle is a statement of permanence and territorial claim, unlike the transient encampments of the deep wood.

The castle passed from generation to generation among the Champions: "the Fellowship of the Champions of the Dry Tree, which went from father to son" (032-016). This hereditary structure gave the castle institutional continuity across decades, ensuring it would remain a Dry Tree stronghold until Ralph's arrival.

## The Lady's Arrival

One of the castle's most striking scenes occurs when Roger leads Ralph's company to its gates. The Champions, recognizing their old comrades, raise a great shout:

> "they shouted for joy when they beheld the tokens of the Dry Tree on the spears of the newcomers" (106-014)

Inside the great hall, the Lady of Abundance — the woman Ralph loves — is discovered seated upon the throne where the Queen of the Dry Tree once sat:

> "there sat a woman on the throne, clad all in green, and her face was the face of the Lady" (106-004)

This moment is charged with symbolic meaning. The Lady, once a refugee from the War of the Tower, now occupies the seat of power in the castle of her former allies. The green of her raiment connects her to the wildwood and to the natural magic of the Well. Ralph, seeing her enthroned, understands that the quest has led him not merely to a castle but to the woman who has haunted his thoughts since their first meeting at the want-ways of the wood.

## Ralph's Lordship

The Champions offer Ralph the castle as his inheritance, to be held worthily:

> "they gave it to him to hold as his own, and all that pertained thereto, for him and his heirs forever" (106-042)

### The Grant of Lordship

The terms of the grant are significant: Ralph is not merely a castellan or steward; he receives full lordship with hereditary right. The Champions, who have no heirs of their own (their fellowship is martial, not dynastic), choose to pass their fortress to a proven warrior who has drunk from the Well.

Ralph's acceptance of the castle transforms him from wandering quester to settled lord. After the final battles against the Burg of the Four Friths, he becomes formally recognized as Lord of the Castle of the Scaur (113-023). The transition is complete: the youth who left Upmeads seeking adventure now holds a fortress, commands men, and dispenses justice.

## Ralph's Rule and the Cleansing of the Wood

Ralph's lordship was not merely titular. He used the castle as a base for active governance, most notably the campaign to cleanse the Wood Perilous:

> "he held it until his death, and therefrom he cleansed the Wood Perilous of all evil beasts and robbed-men and reivers, so that the said wood became as safe as any highway between the most peaceful of towns" (119-003)

This achievement — transforming the Wood Perilous from a place of terror to a safe thoroughfare — is the culmination of Ralph's character arc. The questing knight becomes the protecting lord. The castle, originally built for warriors, becomes the seat of the peace those warriors made possible.

The detail that the wood became "as safe as any highway" between peaceful towns is Morris's measure of good governance: the test of a lord is whether ordinary folk can travel without fear. Ralph passes this test.

## Architecture and Features

Morris provides less architectural detail for the Castle of the Scaur than for some of his other buildings, but certain features can be inferred from the narrative:

- **The Great Hall**: Contains the throne where the Lady is found seated; large enough to accommodate the assembled Champions and Ralph's company
- **Defensive works**: Built atop a cliff, the natural defenses are formidable; the castle likely includes curtain walls and a gatehouse controlling the sole approach up the Scaur
- **Living quarters**: Sufficient to house the Champions as a permanent garrison and, later, Ralph's household
- **Strategic sightlines**: The castle's elevation provides visibility across the contested lands between Higham and the mountains

## Later History

Ralph held the Castle of the Scaur until his death. The novel does not specify who inherited it afterward, but the hereditary grant suggests it passed to Ralph's descendants or to those designated in his will. The castle's final mention in the narrative is in the context of Ralph's death:

> "he died in peace, and was buried in the fair church of Hampton-under-Scaur" (119-004)

That Ralph chose to be buried in Hampton, the town beneath his castle, rather than in his birthplace of Upmeads, signals how completely the Castle of the Scaur had become his home.

## Significance in the Narrative

The Castle of the Scaur serves three linked functions in the novel's symbolic economy:

1. **The military reward**: It represents the tangible prize of Ralph's quest — not merely the intangible renewal of the Well, but a place and a purpose in the world.

2. **The bridge between quest and settlement**: Ralph's acceptance of the castle marks his transition from the wandering phase of life to the settled phase. The castle is both the end of the quest and the beginning of adult responsibility.

3. **The institutionalization of adventure**: The Dry Tree Champions were once outlaws; Ralph was once a runaway youngest son. The castle transforms both legacies into established order — the fortress that was once a brigand stronghold becomes the seat of a just lord who protects travelers.
