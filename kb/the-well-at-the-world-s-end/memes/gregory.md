# Gregory

Third son of King Peter of Upmeads, sent westward by the drawing of cuts at the Four-want-way.

## Overview

Gregory was one of the four sons of King Peter of Upmeads, brother to Blaise, Hugh, and Ralph. His father described him as "courteous and many worded, but sluggish in deed" — though expressly not a dastard (001-043). He drew the third-longest straw at the Four-want-way, sending him westward with his squire Clement the Black, while his brothers departed north, east, and home.

## Biography

### Origins

Gregory was born at the High House of Upmeads, the third son of King Peter and his queen. He grew up in the little kingdom of Upmeads — a land with "no great merchant city; no mighty castle, or noble abbey of monks" (001-023). Like his brothers, he wearied of the narrow life and longed for adventure in wider lands.

### Departure from Upmeads

On a hot June afternoon, King Peter summoned his four sons and offered them the drawing of cuts to determine their fates. Gregory drew the third-longest straw, which sent him **westward** (001-043). He was assigned Clement the Black as his squire — "a careful carle, and speaketh one word to every ten deeds that he doeth; whether they be done with point and edge, or with the hammer in the smithy" (001-045). The king gave each son a bag of treasure — coined money, gold, rings, and brooches — and the four brothers kissed and embraced before departing (001-048).

### Character

King Peter's assessment of his sons paints Gregory as:

> Blaise is wise and prudent, but no great man of his hands. Hugh is a stout rider and lifter, but headstrong and foolhardy, and over bounteous a skinker; and Gregory is courteous and many worded, but sluggish in deed; though I will not call him a dastard. (001-043)

Gregory's gift was his tongue — he shared with Ralph the quality of being "smooth-tongued." But unlike Hugh's physical prowess or Blaise's prudence, Gregory's defining trait was a certain slowness to action that his father noted but did not condemn.

### Fate

Gregory's fate after departing westward is not directly narrated in the main story. His journey westward with Clement the Black takes him into lands beyond the immediate scope of Ralph's adventures, which follow the northern and eastern roads.
