# Walter the Black

**Walter the Black** is a knight whose treachery toward the
[[the-lady-of-abundance|Lady of Abundance]] set in motion events that shaped
her exile among the [[the-knights-of-the-dry-tree|Dry Tree]]. He later rose to
become an earl and a tyrant, threatening [[whitwall|Whitwall]] and waging war
in the North.

## The Betrayal

Walter was a companion of the Knight of the Sun, the lord of Sunway. When the
Lady of Abundance came to Sunway, Walter desired her for himself. According to
the Lady's account:

> "He would have me wed him, as it were against my will, till I ceased striving
> against him... there befell that treason of Walter the Black, who loved me and
> prayed for my love, and when I gainsaid him, swore by all that was holy, before
> my lord, that it was I who sought his love, and how I had told and taught him
> ways of witchcraft, whereby we might fulfill our love, so that the Baron should
> keep a wife for another man."
> [031-014]

The Knight of the Sun believed his friend's accusation. The Lady was shamed and
cast forth — an exile that ultimately led her to the Dry Tree fellowship and her
eventual transformation at the [[the-well-at-the-worlds-end|Well at the World's End]].

## Wounding and Healing

Later in the narrative, Walter the Black was gravely wounded in combat. The Lady
of Abundance, despite her hatred for him, used her healing arts to save his life.
When she prepared to flee with [[ralph-of-upmeads|Ralph]], she sent a holy man to
continue Walter's treatment:

> "I sent him the dream which came to him drowsing, and bade him come to the
> helping of Walter the Black: for I knew that I should take thy hand and flee
> with thee this morning e'en as I have done: and I would fain have a good leech
> to Walter lest he should die, although I owe him hatred rather than love."
> [031-015]

This act of mercy toward her betrayer reveals the Lady's complex moral character.

## Rise as Earl and Tyrant

Walter survived his wound and rose to power as an earl. By the time of Ralph's
return from his quest, "Earl Walter the Black" was described as "a fair young
man outwardly, but false at heart and a tyrant" [022-018]. He threatened the
free town of Whitwall with war, and [[blaise|Blaise]] and [[richard-the-red|Richard]]
prepared the town's defenses against his expected assault. The war with Walter
the Black was later referenced as a significant conflict in the region [048-010].

## Character

Walter the Black is one of the tale's most morally compromised figures. His false
accusation against the Lady was born of thwarted desire and wounded pride. Unlike
many antagonists in the narrative who are straightforwardly villainous, Walter is
portrayed with a degree of complexity — beloved by the Knight of the Sun as a
friend, yet capable of profound betrayal. His survival and later rise to power
suggest that treachery, in Morris's world, does not always meet with immediate
justice.
