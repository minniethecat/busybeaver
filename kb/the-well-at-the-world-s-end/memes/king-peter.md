# King Peter

The ruler of Upmeads, described as a *regulus* or kinglet, whose small kingdom and peaceful nature form the backdrop from which his four sons depart on their adventures. King Peter is the father of Ralph of Upmeads and his three brothers.

## Overview

King Peter rules "a little land" where he is called a kinglet rather than a king, reflecting the modest scale of his domain (001-002). He is a man of peace, shaped by the loss of his own father and eldest son in battle in the nearby forest (001-010). His decision to let his sons draw lots for their journeys sets the entire narrative in motion.

## Biography

### Origins

King Peter inherited the rule of Upmeads after his father died in battle in the Wood Debateable (001-010). His eldest son also perished in that same forest, cementing Peter's commitment to peace. He rules not through taxation or force but through the goodwill of his people, described as "kindly and of measure" (001-010).

### Journey / Arc

The pivotal moment of King Peter's narrative comes when his four sons weary him with their longing to leave home. Rising from an afternoon nap on a carpet given by the Prior of St. John's by the Bridge, he summons his sons to the High House of Upmeads (001). There he devises the lottery system: "let luck choose for me, and ye shall draw cuts for your roads" (001). He assigns each son a squire and a direction, instructing the one who draws the shortest straw to remain and eventually inherit the throne.

### Fate

King Peter remains at Upmeads through the events of the novel. His ultimate fate — reunion with Ralph — occurs late in the narrative when Ralph returns home.

## Character Analysis

### Traits & Personality

King Peter embodies the virtues of a peaceful ruler: kindness, fairness, and measured judgment. He is "no ill man, but kindly and of measure" (001-010). Unlike the mightier lords surrounding his kingdom — including a bishop described as "a close-fist and a manslayer" — Peter governs through consent rather than coercion. His pacifism is rooted in personal loss, not cowardice.

### Relationships

Peter's relationship with his four sons defines his character. He listens to their restlessness with patience, devises a fair method of deciding their fates, and provides each with an appropriate squire. The lottery system reveals his wisdom: rather than choosing favorites, he trusts to chance and acknowledges each son's distinct qualities — Blaise's wisdom, Hugh's valor, Gregory's courtesy, and Ralph's untested potential (001).

### Themes

King Peter represents the "ordinary world" from which the hero departs — the stable, peaceful homeland that Ralph must leave to find adventure. His rule embodies Morris's ideal of decentralized, consensual governance, contrasting with the tyrannical lords Ralph encounters in his travels.
