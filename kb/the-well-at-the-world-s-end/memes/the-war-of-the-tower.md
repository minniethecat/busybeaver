# The War of the Tower

The War of the Tower is a backstory conflict in *The Well at the World's End*, fought between the old King of the Tower and his son, who had married the Lady of Abundance against his father's wishes. The war forms a crucial part of the Lady's history and explains her long association with the Men of the Dry Tree.

## Origins

The conflict originated from the old King's hostility toward his son's marriage to the Lady of Abundance. The Lady had been found in the wildwood by the King's son, who brought her to his father's kingdom and wedded her regardless of opposition:

> "he was put out of his kingdom and his father's house for the love of her, because other kings and lords hankered after her; whereof befell long and grievous war" (005-030)

The phrase "other kings and lords hankered after her" is key to understanding the conflict's nature. This was not merely a father-son dispute over an unsuitable bride; it was a multi-power struggle over possession of a woman of legendary beauty. The King's objection to the marriage may have been less about filial disobedience than about the political destabilization caused by rival lords who desired the Lady for themselves.

Morris presents the war's origin through the lens of the Book of the Castle of Abundance, whose account the Lady later reveals to be "partly false of intention and ill-will" (008-005). The book's version frames the war as the inevitable consequence of the Lady's beauty — she is the cause, the men who fight over her are merely reacting. The Lady's own account shifts the moral weight: the war was caused by the "hankering" of powerful men, not by her existence.

## Course of the War

The Lady's firsthand account to Ralph reveals that the war was protracted and bitter. The King himself led forces against his son with relentless energy:

> "the king had been as wary as we were privy, and no less speedy than we; and he was a mighty and deft warrior, and he himself followed us on the spur with certain of his best men-at-arms" (008-020)

The King's personal involvement — he is "mighty and deft," commanding his "best men-at-arms" — underscores the intensity of his determination. This was not a war prosecuted by proxies; the King himself took the field against his own son.

The decisive engagement was an ambush at a woodside. The King's forces caught the young couple and their supporters after a period of cat-and-mouse pursuit:

> "they fell upon us as we were passing through a thorny wood... and though we fought valiantly, yet we were everyone slain or hurt, and but few escaped with bare life" (008-020)

The outcome was catastrophic for the Lady's faction. The phrase "everyone slain or hurt" signals near-total destruction. Only "a few" escaped. The Lady and her lord were captured and imprisoned.

## The Castle of Abundance as War Refuge

Throughout the war and its long aftermath, the Castle of Abundance served as the Lady's sanctuary. Her lord had built the castle specifically for her, and its remote location made it a natural stronghold:

> "which we held as our refuge all through the War of the Tower, both before we joined us to you in the wildwood, and afterwards" (008-028)

The castle's dual role — both marital gift and military fortress — reflects the inseparable blending of love and war in the Lady's story. The castle was a place of beauty and art (its illuminated book, its gardens), but its primary function during the war years was defensive.

The timing of the refuge is significant: the Lady and her lord used the castle "both before we joined us to you" — that is, before they allied with the Men of the Dry Tree — "and afterwards." The alliance with the Dry Tree was not the beginning of their resistance but a strategic escalation after the castle alone proved insufficient.

## Alliance with the Dry Tree

The war drove the Lady and her lord into alliance with the Champions of the Dry Tree, the outlaw-warriors of the wildwood. This alliance transformed the conflict: what had begun as a dynastic dispute now involved a disciplined fighting force operating outside the feudal system.

The Dry Tree's motivation for joining the war is not explicitly stated, but can be inferred. The Champions were already enemies of the settled powers; a war against a king aligned with their own interests. Moreover, the Lady's charisma — the same quality that had "beguiled" the King's son — won the loyalty of these hardened warriors. They would later build their fortress at the Castle of the Scaur and, years afterward, offer it to Ralph as the Lady's champion.

## Resolution

After their capture, the Lady was imprisoned and tried for sorcery, while her lord was held in ward. The war was resolved not through continued fighting but through the intervention of the Lineage — the network of allied noble houses — who forced a truce:

> "there went forth three ancient knights from out of the king's host and came up to him and spake with him" (008-022)

The three ancient knights represent an older, more honorable aristocratic order, distinct from the King's personal vendetta. Their intervention shifts the war's resolution from military to diplomatic grounds. The truce terms required the Lady (branded "the Sorceress") and her lord to depart the city and remain at least fifty miles distant:

> "that in any case the Sorceress should get her gone from those walls" (008-022)

The exile was a political compromise: the King was rid of a woman he could not kill outright, and the knights of the Lineage avoided the disgrace of seeing an innocent woman burned. The Lady and her company rode out "that very day before sunset" — the same day she was to have died at the stake.

## Aftermath and Long-Term Consequences

The war's consequences rippled through the entire narrative:

1. **The Lady's exile**: She lived thereafter in the wildwood and at the Castle of Abundance, never returning to the city. This exile created the circumstances in which Ralph first encountered her.

2. **The Dry Tree alliance**: The war cemented the bond between the Lady and the Champions of the Dry Tree, an alliance that would persist for decades and ultimately benefit Ralph when the Champions offered him lordship of the Castle of the Scaur.

3. **The Lady's wariness**: The war — and especially the trial that followed it — gave the Lady a permanent distrust of established authority. When she later encounters Ralph, she is cautious, testing his character before revealing her identity.

4. **The narrative frame**: The war exists entirely as backstory, narrated by the Lady to Ralph in Chapter 8. This narrative structure — a grande dame recounting her own history to a young listener — is one of Morris's most effective uses of embedded storytelling.

## Thematic Significance

The War of the Tower is never directly depicted in the novel's present action, yet its shadow falls across the entire story. It exemplifies the novel's recurring pattern of powerful men using force to control women they desire. The war's origin — a king punishing his son for marrying a woman other men "hankered after" — reveals the possessiveness at the heart of patriarchal power. The Lady is not a political actor in the war; she is its object, a prize to be won or destroyed.

Yet the war's outcome subverts this pattern. The Lady survives. Her rescuers, not her persecutors, shape the future. And when Ralph of Upmeads arrives years later, he inherits the alliances and sanctuaries that the war inadvertently created — the Dry Tree's loyalty, the Castle of Abundance, the network of safe passage through the wildwood. The war, intended to destroy the Lady, instead built the infrastructure of resistance that sustained her and enabled Ralph's quest.
