# The Dale of the Tower

The Dale of the Tower is a fertile valley in the down country east of Utterbol, containing a lord's tower, a homestead, and the meadow called Bull-mead where Ralph of Upmeads took part in a tourney during his captivity. In the novel's closing chapters, it is named as the site of a projected cheaping town that would extend the Lord of Utterbol's power.

## Overview

The Dale of the Tower first appears in Chapter 58, when Captain Otter tells Ralph what lies ahead on their journey: "At midmorn we shall come into a fair dale amidst the downs, where be some houses and a tower of the Lord's, so that that place is called the Dale of the Tower: there shall we abide a while to gather victual, a day or two, or three maybe: so my Lord will hold a tourney there" [058-028]. The Dale thus serves a dual function: as a provisioning stop and as a stage for the martial displays by which the Lord of Utterbol tests and entertains his men.

## Geography & Description

The Dale is described as "a fair dale amidst the downs" [058-028], suggesting a sheltered valley surrounded by rolling chalk hills. It contains a river with water-meadows, a grange or homestead, several cottages, and "a great square tower, with barriers and bailey all dight ready for war" [059-006 area]. The tower, with its war-readiness and the Lord's banner, dominates the valley.

Within the Dale lies a specific meadow called **Bull-mead**, where the tourney takes place. David, another of the Lord's men, tells Ralph: "After we have left Bull-mead in the Dale of the Tower, where tomorrow thou art to run with the spear, it is four days' ride to Utterness; and from Utterness ye may come (if my lord will) unto Utterbol in twelve hours" [058-031]. Bull-mead is thus a named field within the larger Dale, and its association with the tourney makes it a landmark of Ralph's time in captivity.

## Inhabitants

The Dale is home to the Lord's garrison at the tower and the small farming community that supports it — the "some houses" Otter mentions [058-028]. When the Lord of Utterbol is in residence with his lady-wife and retinue, a great pavilion of "snow-white cloth striped with gold and purple" is pitched near the river, and the population swells with men-at-arms [059-006 area].

## Role in the Narrative

### The Tourney at Bull-mead

The tourney held at Bull-mead is Ralph's public test as a fighting man in the Lord of Utterbol's service. Otter frames it as a trial of the "new colt's paces" [058-028], and Ralph, though anxious, proves himself. The event establishes Ralph's martial reputation among the men of Utterbol — a reputation that serves him later in the narrative.

### A Vision of the Future

In the novel's closing chapters, Otter speaks of the Lord's plans for the Dale of the Tower: "it was the Lord's intent that a cheaping town should grow up in the Dale of the Tower, and that the wilderness beyond it should be tilled and builded" [091-006]. He adds that if this vision is realized, the new lord (Ralph, who has by then overthrown the old tyranny) "may become exceedingly mighty, and if he hold on in the way whereas he now is, he shall be well-beloved also" [091-006]. This projected transformation — from a lord's private holding to a thriving market town — represents the civilizing impulse that runs through the novel's closing pages, and ties the Dale's future to Ralph's kingship.

## Significance

The Dale of the Tower is emblematic of the feudal landscape Morris depicts throughout the novel: a fertile valley dominated by a fortified tower, its rhythms governed by the Lord's needs and pleasures. The tourney at Bull-mead, the provisioning stop, and the projected cheaping town together trace an arc from military feudalism toward settled commerce — a microcosm of the novel's larger movement from strife toward peace.
