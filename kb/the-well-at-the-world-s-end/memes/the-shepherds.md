# The Shepherds

A pastoral community dwelling on the grassy downs near Upmeads, the Shepherds represent the rural yeomanry of the novel's world — free folk who live by their flocks, govern themselves through the folkmote, and maintain a fierce independence from lords and kings while retaining loyalty to just rulers.

## Overview

The Shepherds first appear early in Ralph's journey, when he encounters them on the downs near his homeland. An old shepherd offers Ralph hospitality and practical advice, and a younger shepherd named Wat engages in jesting conversation (004-014). Despite their humble station, they show dignity and a clear-eyed understanding of the world: "ye must not misdeem of us that we shepherds of the Downs can do nought but run to ales and feasts" (004-019).

Ralph swears an oath to the shepherds: "if I wax as my will is, and I come to be lord in these lands, I will look to it to do what a good lord should do for the shepherds of the Downs" (004-025). The old shepherd blesses this oath: "Thou hast sworn an oath, and it is a good oath, and well sworn" (004-026). This early exchange establishes the reciprocal relationship between just lordship and loyal folk — a theme that resonates through the entire narrative.

## The Folkmote

The Shepherds' most significant narrative appearance comes in the final movement of the novel, when they hold a folkmote — a communal assembly — to decide on action against the tyrannical Burgers who threaten Upmeads (111-001). The folkmote is one of the novel's few depictions of collective decision-making, contrasting with the top-down rule of kings and lords.

The chapter "The Folkmote of the Shepherds" (ch111) shows the Shepherds exercising self-governance: gathering to debate, reaching consensus, and mustering for collective action. This is a distinct governance model from the consensual monarchy of Upmeads, the tyranny of Utterbol, or the commercial republic of the Burgs — it is direct, communal, and rooted in the land.

## Role in the Campaign to Reclaim Upmeads

When Ralph returns to reclaim Upmeads from the Burgers who have seized it, the Shepherds are among the forces that rally to his cause. Their motivation is not feudal obligation — Ralph is not yet their lord — but a combination of loyalty to the memory of King Peter's just rule and their own interest in resisting Burger expansion onto the downs.

The Shepherds thus embody the novel's ideal of the free rural folk: independent enough to govern themselves through the folkmote, loyal enough to fight for a just cause, and rooted enough in their land to resist both external conquest and internal tyranny.

## Narrative Function

The Shepherds serve a structural purpose in the novel's political economy. They are the baseline against which other governance models are measured: simpler than the Burgers' commercial cities, freer than Utterbol's thralls, more communal than Upmeads's monarchy. Their folkmote represents a form of democracy that predates and underlies the feudal structures built atop it.
