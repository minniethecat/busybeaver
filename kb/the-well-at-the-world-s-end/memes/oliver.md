# Oliver

An ancient warrior of the Wood Perilous, whose respect for Nicholas Longshanks proved decisive for Ralph's freedom.

## Overview

Old Oliver was a veteran warrior in the service of the Knight of the Dry Tree. When Ralph was stopped by the Dry Tree company in the Wood Perilous, Oliver's recognition of Ralph's connection to Nicholas Longshanks — his old friend and occasional foe — helped secure Ralph's safe passage. He later served as a point of trust for Ralph in the perilous lands around the Burg of the Four Friths.

## Biography

### The Encounter in the Wood Perilous

When Ralph was accosted by the Knight of the Dry Tree and his company in the Wood Perilous, the Knight turned to "the ancient man" Oliver to assess the young stranger. Oliver looked Ralph over carefully before speaking:

> "Yea, Sir Aymer," quoth Oliver; "And now meseems I know him better than he knoweth me." (008-568)

Oliver then asked after Nicholas Longshanks, and when Ralph begged him not to reveal his identity, Oliver laughed and agreed — "for the sake of the old days his name shall help thee, young lord" (008-578). He vouched for Ralph's noble birth: "he is of a goodly house and an ancient," and advised the Knight to "let his name alone."

This intervention was decisive. The Knight, respecting Oliver's judgment, offered Ralph safe passage rather than detention.

### Service to the Dry Tree

Oliver rode at the vanguard of the Dry Tree company. When a runner brought urgent news, the Knight immediately called: "Oliver! Oliver! lead on the way thou wottest! Spur! spur, all men!" (008-590). His seniority and reliability made him the natural choice to lead in moments of crisis.

### Ally in the Perilous Lands

Ralph later regarded Oliver as someone "he had to fall back on" in the dangerous lands around the Burg of the Four Friths (009-021). Though their interaction was brief, Oliver's recognition of old bonds — and his willingness to honour them — marked him as a man of loyalty that transcended current allegiances.

## Character Analysis

Oliver represents the old code of honour among warriors in Morris's world. Though aligned with the ambiguous Dry Tree company, his personal bonds — forged in "the old days" with Nicholas Longshanks — override present enmities. He is a figure of memory and continuity: old enough to have both "friends" and "foes" among the scattered men of the wider world, and wise enough to let past friendship guide present judgment.
