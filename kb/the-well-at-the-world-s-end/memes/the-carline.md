# The Carline

A mysterious old woman — a "carline" — who appears to Ralph of Upmeads in the Wood Perilous and gives him a protective necklace. She is the first character to speak of the Well at the World's End in connection with Ralph, setting the quest in motion.

## Overview

The Carline appears in the Wood Perilous, bearing knowledge of the Well and gifts of protection. She gives Ralph a necklace that will serve "as if thou hadst drunk of the Well at the World's End" (003-018), simultaneously revealing the Well's existence and providing a tangible connection to its power. Her role as guide and benefactor is central to the narrative's early development.

## Biography

### Origins

The Carline's origins are obscure. She appears in the wilderness, seemingly from nowhere, bearing deep knowledge of the magical geography of the world. Her age and wisdom mark her as belonging to the old, wild world that exists alongside — and beneath — the civilized kingdoms.

### Journey / Arc

The Carline gives Ralph the protective necklace, explaining that it will guard him as though he had already drunk from the Well (003-018). She tells him of the Well's existence and the path to find it. Her role as a guide is reinforced when she reappears or is referenced at key moments, her knowledge forming the foundation of Ralph's quest.

## Character Analysis

### Traits & Personality

The Carline embodies the archetype of the wise old woman — a figure common in medieval romance and fairy tale. She speaks with authority about magical matters, yet her manner is direct and unadorned. Unlike the Lady of Abundance, she carries no courtly refinement; she is of the wild, practical and mysterious.

### Relationships

The Carline's relationship with Ralph is that of guide to quester. She gives him both physical protection (the necklace) and knowledge (the existence and nature of the Well). She also speaks of the Lady of Abundance with familiarity, suggesting a network of powerful female figures operating across the narrative landscape.

### Themes

The Carline represents the wisdom of the old world — knowledge that exists outside institutions, passed through personal encounter rather than books or churches. Her necklace's power is explicitly contrasted with religious blessing: it serves without being blessed by "pope nor priest" (003-018), reflecting Morris's secular humanist values.
