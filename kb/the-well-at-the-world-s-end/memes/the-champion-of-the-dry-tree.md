The Champions of the Dry Tree — also called the Men of the Dry Tree — are a band of free warriors who make their home at [[hampton-under-scaur|Hampton under Scaur]], dwelling in the Castle of the Scaur. Unlike the feudal lords and their retainers who populate much of the world, the Fellowship is not bound to any kingdom but serves as an independent martial order devoted to a sacred quest.

## Origin and Quest

The Fellowship's defining purpose is rooted in a lost love. As one of their elder members recounts to [[ralph-of-upmeads|Ralph]]:

> "Fifty years ago, when I was yet but a young man, there was a fair woman who was Queen of the Land of the Tower and whom we loved sorely because we had dwelt together with her amidst tribulation in the desert and the wildwood: and we are not of her people, but a fellowship of free men and champions hight the Men of the Dry Tree: and we hoped that she would one day come back and dwell with us and be our Lady and Queen." [1937-001]

When the Queen vanished, a band of twenty-one champions swore an oath to seek her. As the elder explains:

> "Therefore a band of us of the Dry Tree swore an oath together to seek her till we found her, that we might live and die together: but of that band of one score and one, am I the last one left that seeketh; for the rest are dead, or sick, or departed: and indeed I was the youngest of them." [1937-002]

The quest has continued across two generations, with the elder's sons carrying on the search: "But for these two men, they are my sons whom I have bred in the knowledge of these things and in the hope of finding tidings of our Lady and Queen, if it were but the place where her body lieth." [1937-003]

## Reputation

The Champions have an ambiguous reputation in the surrounding territories. [[the-damsel-of-bourton-abbas|The Damsel of Bourton Abbas]] tells Ralph that "at Hampton dwelleth the Fellowship of the Dry Tree, and that those of the fellowship are robbers and reivers." Yet she concedes, "Nevertheless they will perchance be little worse than the others." [682-001]

In the [[the-burg-of-the-four-friths|Burg of the Four Friths]], the townsmen have erected a gallows in the marketplace with four nooses and a sign reading **The Dry Tree** — a symbol of their enmity. [820-001] Those loyal to the Burg speak of "the Devil of the Dry Tree" as their hated foe. [836-001]

Yet the Champions are also capable of mercy. During a raid on a grange, they slay men but spare the innocent, and their tall captain shows skill and restraint with his bow. [1037-001] The Fellowship lives by a code of honor distinct from the political warfare of the settled lands.

## The Lady of the Dry Tree

The Fellowship is associated with a mysterious woman who leads them — a figure of fear to the folk of the Burg, who call her "the Devil of the Dry Tree." [836-001] This woman is later revealed to be the [[the-lady-of-abundance|Lady of Abundance]], whom the Champions revere.

The Champion who speaks with Ralph at Hampton under Scaur refers to "our Lady and Queen" with deep devotion, and when [[roger-of-the-ropewalk|Roger of the Ropewalk]] speaks of the arrangement whereby another woman might sit "in our Lady's seat" at the Castle of the Scaur, he says it "will be hard for me to see another woman sitting in our Lady's seat: yea, to see her sitting there, who hath stolen her luck." [5235-001] This reveals the depth of the Champions' loyalty to their lost Queen.

## Role in the Narrative

The Lady of Abundance directs Ralph toward the Champions, referring to them as "the Fellowship of Champions who dwell on the rock" at Hampton under Scaur, suggesting they will be allies in his quest for [[the-well-at-the-world-s-end|the Well at the World's End]]. [1633-001]

The Champions represent a third way between the tyranny of [[Utterbol]] and the commercial order of the Burg — a fellowship bound by oath and honor rather than fear or profit. In Morris's medieval-inspired world, they embody the ideal of free martial brotherhood, devoted to a higher cause.
