David the Old Man, also called David the Sage, serves as the chief counselor to the [[the-lord-of-utterbol|Lord of Utterbol]]. He is described as "a man somewhat stricken in years, clad in scarlet," who stands at the lord's right hand and advises on matters of captives and household management. [2871-001]

## Role at Utterbol

When [[ralph-of-upmeads|Ralph]] is captured and brought before the Lord of Utterbol, it is David who inspects him and pronounces him suitable. He is tasked by the lord with ensuring Ralph is "in condition when he cometh home to Utterbol," under threat of punishment if the captive deteriorates. [2879-001]

Despite his position of influence, David is ultimately as much a thrall as anyone in Utterbol. The narrative notes:

> "though this man had not been bought for money, he was little better than a thrall of the higher sort, as forsooth were all the Lord's men, saving the best and trustiest of his warriors: and these were men whom the Lord somewhat feared himself: though, on the other hand, he could not but know that they understood how the dread of the Lord of Utterbol was a shield to them, and that if it were to die out amongst men, their own skins were not worth many days' purchase." [2883-001]

His counsel extends only so far as it aligns with the Lord's desires, making him a figure of compromised authority. David "durst counsel otherwise than as the Lord desired to go; unless he thought that it would bring his said Lord, and therefore himself, to very present peril and damage." [2883-002]

## Care of Ralph

David treats Ralph with elaborate courtesy during their journey to [[Utterbol]], attending closely to his comfort. He "smiled on Ralph and led him out unto a tent not far off, and there he made much of him, and bade bring meat and drink and all he needed." [2881-001] At the same time, he warns Ralph not to attempt escape, showing him how close the guards are posted.

During the day's ride, David "stuck close to his side all day, was so sugary sweet with him, and praised and encouraged him so diligently." [2895-001] But Ralph recognizes the underlying menace:

> "David, who stuck close to his side all day, was so sugary sweet with him, and praised and encouraged him so diligently, that Ralph began to have misgivings that all this kindness was but as the flower-garlands wherewith the heathen times men were wont to deck the slaughter-beasts for the blood-offering." [2895-002]

Ralph briefly fears that David's kindness masks heathen sacrificial rites, but he soon dismisses this anxiety, reasoning "how little wisdom there was in running to meet the evil, which might be on the way." [2895-003]

## Conversations on the Road

David serves as a source of lore and cautionary tales during the journey. When Ralph gazes longingly at the distant mountains, David warns him:

> "If all tales be true thou art best this side thereof, whatever thy lot may be. [...] men say that over there are things to be seen which might slay a strong man for pure fear, without stroke of sword or dint of axe." [2899-001, 2901-001]

When Ralph questions whether the tales of the [[the-well-at-the-world-s-end|Well at the World's End]] are true, David recounts the story of the [[the-king-of-goldburg|lord of Goldburg]], who "went over those mountains, and drank of the Well at the World's End." [2905-001] David adds a note of skepticism: "when all is said, 'tis but a tale." [2905-002]

Their most revealing exchange occurs when David asks whether Ralph believes a man might "drink of the Well and recover his youth thereby." [2911-001] When Ralph asks if the Well is nearby, David draws close and says softly: "Nigher? Yea, but belike not so much nigher." [2915-001] — suggesting David knows more than he openly states.

At evening, David visits Ralph's tent, sometimes accompanied by [[captain-otter|Otter]], the captain of the guard. On the night before Ralph's tilting exhibition at Bull-mead in the [[the-dale-of-the-tower|Dale of the Tower]], David reassures him: "if it be seen that thou hast spoken truly, and art more than a little deft at the play, he will be like to make the best of thee, since thou art already paid for." [3111-001]

## Character

David is portrayed as a man of practical wisdom constrained by servitude. He "was so sugary sweet" in manner that Ralph instinctively distrusts him, yet David shows genuine moments of good cheer — when the mountains come into view, he is "joyous of mood" and "looked so merry that Ralph wondered what was toward." [3133-001]

He is a survivor in a brutal court, navigating the whims of a tyrant through skillful service. Though stripped of true autonomy ("little better than a thrall of the higher sort"), David maintains his position through competence and careful deference. His name marks him as an outsider — a Hebrew name in a medieval romance setting — perhaps suggesting wisdom traditions from beyond the chivalric world.
