# Richard of Upmeads

Richard of Upmeads is an old and wise retainer of the [[upmeads|Upmeads]] household, a man-at-arms who serves [[blaise|Blaise]] as his master and holds deep, personal knowledge of the [[the-well-at-the-worlds-end|Well at the World's End]] from his youth in [[swevenham|Swevenham]].

## Overview

Richard first appears in the narrative as the companion assigned to accompany Blaise on his eastward journey from Upmeads (001-021). Though not of noble birth, Richard occupies a unique position in the household: at sixty-eight years of age, he has witnessed the upbringing of all four of King Peter's sons and serves as a trusted elder rather than a mere servant. His narrative function shifts from background retainer to critical guide when he recognizes [[ralph-of-upmeads|Ralph]]'s despair and reveals that he once lived in Swevenham — a town intimately connected to the lore of the Well at the World's End.

## Biography

### Origins

Richard's early life remains partially obscure, but he reveals to Ralph that he was born and raised in Swevenham, a town visible from the walls of Whitwall. At the age of eighteen, he witnessed three fellow townsfolk — two young men and one young woman — set out to seek the Well at the World's End, armed with lore learned from an old man, a friend of a holy man, who had first-hand knowledge of the Well (037-012).

> Three folk of our township, two young men and one young woman, set out thence to seek the said Well: and much lore they had concerning it, which they had learned of an old man, a friend of a holy man, who had been at the Well itself.
>
> (037-012)

### Journey / Arc

After the departure of the three seekers, Richard grew weary of Swevenham. He girded himself with a sword, laid a spear upon his shoulder, and journeyed sixty miles to the Castle of the Waste March, where the Baron took him into service as a man-at-arms (037-013). His subsequent wanderings eventually brought him to the service of [[king-peter|King Peter]] at Upmeads, where he became a fixture of the household and helped raise the king's four sons: [[blaise|Blaise]], [[hugh|Hugh]], Gregory, and [[ralph-of-upmeads|Ralph]].

When King Peter sends his sons out into the world, Richard is assigned to accompany Blaise eastward. He reappears significantly in Whitwall (Chapter 36), where he and Blaise, now prosperous merchants, encounter Ralph. Richard's joy at the reunion is palpable:

> Then up came Richard, and if Blaise were glad, Richard was twice glad, and quoth he: "Said I not, Lord Blaise, that this chick would be the hardest of all to keep under the coop? Welcome to the Highways, Lord Ralph!"
>
> (036-006)

Richard quickly perceives Ralph's sorrow and takes it upon himself to draw out the cause. In a pivotal conversation outside the walls of Whitwall at Petergate Bar, Richard coaxes Ralph to confess that his grief concerns a woman — and then reveals his own crucial knowledge: he once lived in Swevenham and knows the lore of the Well at the World's End (037-009–037-015).

### Fate

Richard's ultimate fate is not shown in the narrative. After revealing his knowledge of Swevenham, he undertakes to return there alone to gather intelligence about the Well, telling Ralph to "keep thy soul in patience" (037-016). His story arc transitions him from retainer to guide-figure, one of several older mentors who point Ralph toward his destiny.

## Character Analysis

### Traits and Personality

Richard is characterised above all by **wisdom tempered by age and experience**. At sixty-eight, he speaks with the authority of one who has "bought the knowledge of them dear enough, and have not forgotten" (037-009). He combines the pragmatism of a seasoned man-at-arms with genuine affection for the young men he helped raise — particularly Ralph, whom he calls "foster son."

His manner is direct, perceptive, and at times wryly humorous. When Ralph looks "woebegone" after a tour of Whitwall, Richard "could scarce withhold his laughter" despite his genuine concern (037-005). This blend of compassion and clear-eyed realism makes him one of the novel's most sympathetic secondary figures.

### Themes

Richard embodies the **wise elder** archetype common in medieval romance — the seasoned retainer whose long memory and varied experience provide crucial knowledge at a narrative turning-point. His connection to Swevenham weaves him into the novel's central mystery: the Well at the World's End, a destination that has claimed seekers for generations. His own choice to leave Swevenham rather than join the quest suggests a pragmatic temperament that stands in contrast to Ralph's romantic yearning — yet he does not discourage Ralph; instead, he becomes an enabler of the quest.
