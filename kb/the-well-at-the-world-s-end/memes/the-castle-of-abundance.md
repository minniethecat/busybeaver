The Castle of Abundance (also called the House of Abundance) is a fair castle standing in a pleasant wilderness east of the [[the-wood-perilous|Wood Perilous]] and south of the [[the-burg-of-the-four-friths|Burg of the Four Friths]]. Built by the King's Son for the [[the-lady-of-abundance|Lady of Abundance]], it serves as a repository of her history — preserved in tapestry and book — and as the place where [[ralph-of-upmeads|Ralph]] first learns the lore of [[the-well-at-the-world-s-end|the Well at the World's End]].

Chapter 18 bears its name: "Of Ralph in the Castle of Abundance" (018-001). The castle is not far from the House of the Sorceress — the Lady and her company depart from there and ride to the Castle of Abundance in a single day (032-023).

## Construction and Purpose

The Lady recounts how "my lord had the Castle of Abundance built up very fairly for me and him to dwell in at whiles; and indeed we had before that dwelt at a little manor house that was there" (1879-001). The King's Son — a figure of noble lineage who found the Lady in the wilderness — "had done it as fair as ye saw it, and had those arras cloths made with the story of my sojourn in the wilderness, even as ye saw them" (1879-002).

The castle replaced a simpler manor house that had stood on the site during the years of war. When peace returned and the land grew prosperous again — "wealth came back to the mighty of the land, and fields flourished and the acres bore increase, and fair houses were builded in the towns" — the King's Son made the Castle of Abundance a fitting dwelling for his lady (1879-001).

## Geography and Setting

The castle lies deep in a pleasant woodland wilderness. The surrounding country is forested — "long and long they rode the woodland, but it was hard on May when they entered it, and it was pleasant therein" (081-004). A hazel copse stands nearby, through which the Lady leads Ralph hand in hand (026-006).

The castle itself is fair and well-appointed, with gardens where Ralph and the Lady walk and talk. The Lady bids Ralph not to tarry: "look, how low the sun is growing!" (034-007). The setting sun over the gardens establishes the dreamlike, suspended quality of Ralph's time there.

## The Arras of the Chamber of Dais

The castle's most remarkable feature is the arras hanging in the Chamber of Dais. As Ralph discovers:

> "On the dais was a throne of carven ivory, and above it a canopy of baudekin of the goodliest fashion, and there was a foot-carpet before it, wrought with beasts and the hunting of the deer. As for the walls of that chamber, they were hung with a marvellous halling of arras, wherein was wrought the greenwood, and there amidst in one place a potherb garden, and a green garth with goats therein, and in that garth a little thatched house." (1237-001)

The arras depicts two women — one old, richly dressed; the other young, poorly clad — shown over and over again in the activities of daily toil: spinning, digging, milking goats. The older woman watches or sits idle while the younger works. "Long he abode in that chamber looking at the arras, and wondering whether the sitter in the ivory throne would be any other than the thrall in the greenwood cot" (1241-001).

The Lady of Abundance later explains the arras's origin: "as to the halling of the Chamber of Dais, I have told thee before how my lord, the King's Son, did do make it in memory of the wilderness wherein he found me, and the life of thralldom from which he brought me" (1963-001).

## The Book of the Well

Within the castle, Ralph discovers a book that tells the story of the Well at the World's End. The old carline who serves as the castle's sole attendant tells him: "there is a book in this house that telleth of it; I know it well by the look of it though I may not read in it" (1191-001).

The book — "fairly written, with many pictures therein" — tells a version of the Lady's story, "part true and part false, and altogether of malice against me," as the Lady herself later says (1963-002). The book describes how "a king's son found her there and brought her to his father's kingdom and wedded her," and how after long wars she returned to the wilderness, was enslaved by a sorceress, and ultimately found the Well (1279-001).

The Lady later reveals she bought this book "of a monk who came our way, and who at first was sore afeared when he found that he had come to my castle" (1963-003).

## Inhabitants

The castle is the domain of the Lady of Abundance alone — she appears to dwell there without a court or household, attended perhaps by unseen servants. She tells Ralph that she foresaw his coming: "I was abiding thy coming at the Castle of Abundance" (025-024). She had instructed Roger of the Ropewalk to guide Ralph to her (025-024).

The "People of Abundance" are mentioned later, when Ralph and Ursula revisit the castle after the Lady's death (103-029). These folk appear to be the broader community of the surrounding land.

## Ralph's Stay

[[roger-of-the-ropewalk|Roger of the Ropewalk]] guides Ralph to the House of Abundance at the Lady's bidding. The castle at the time of Ralph's visit is nearly empty — tended only by the old carline — yet it is well-stocked with arms, victuals, and all the furnishings of a noble dwelling. Ralph finds the guard-chamber hung with armour and weapons "clean and in good order, though there was never a man-at-arms there, nor any soul except the old woman" (1237-002).

The Lady reveals to Ralph that she had foreseen their meeting, though not all that followed: "all this that hath befallen since I set out to meet thee at the Castle of Abundance I foresaw not, any more than I can foresee tomorrow" (033-004). She speaks of a man who "would have me wed him" — the Lord of Utterbol — whom she refused, setting in motion the events that would lead to war and her eventual flight to the Scaur (033-007).

It is here, studying the arras and reading the book, that Ralph's understanding of the quest ahead begins to take shape. The castle stands as a threshold between the familiar world of the west and the unknown lands beyond.

## Return Visit

After completing the quest for the Well, Ralph and [[ursula|Ursula]] return to the Castle of Abundance with the [[the-sage-of-swevenham|Sage of Swevenham]]:

> "They arose betimes on the morrow, and whereas the Sage knew the woodland ways well, they made but a short journey of it to the Castle of Abundance, and came into the little plain but two hours after noon, where saving that the scythe had not yet wended the tall mowing grass in the crofts which the beasts and sheep were not pasturing, all was as on that other tide." (5035-001)

The castle stands unchanged, its crofts and closes still tended by the peaceful folk who dwell there — a sign that the orderly world the Lady built endures. Yet the Lady herself is dead, and the People of Abundance greet the returning travelers with solemn welcome (103-001). Ralph tells them: "true it is that your Lady who is dead loved me, and it is no wonder" (103-029).

## Significance

The Castle of Abundance is Ralph's first encounter with the enchanted, the beautiful, and the sorrowful in equal measure. It represents a paradise briefly found and inevitably lost — a pattern that repeats throughout the narrative. The Lady's foreknowledge and her resigned acceptance of fate give the castle the quality of a place outside ordinary time, where love and loss are intertwined.
