# Hampton under Scaur

A small town in a valley beneath the rocky height of the Scaur, upon which stands the great castle of the Champions of the Dry Tree. The town's peaceful folk live under the heavy hand of their warlike overlords.

## Overview

Hampton under Scaur is known by a double reputation: to some, its folk are "peaceable and friendly" [005]; to others, the Fellowship of the Dry Tree who rule from the castle above are "robbers and reivers" [006]. The truth encompasses both: the townsfolk are harmless, even sheep-like, but they live under "evil lords who are not their true lords, who lay heavy burdens on them and torment them even to the destroying of their lives" [007].

## Geography & Description

The town lies in the lower ground, while the Castle of the Scaur rises upon the height above — "for upon the height of the said Scaur is our castle builded" [016]. The woodland lies nearby: the wood-path from the south leads toward Hampton, and the Wood Perilous itself is within reach. The town's position places it in tension with the Burg of the Four Friths, its rival stronghold [016].

## Inhabitants

The common folk of Hampton are farmers and craftsmen, described by Roger of the Ropewalk as "harmless and innocent as sheep" [007]. Above them rule the Champions of the Dry Tree, a warrior-fellowship who have "left the thick of the wood, and built them a great castle" [016]. The Lady of Abundance serves as their Queen "in a fashion," though she acknowledges another Queen — "a fierce and hard woman, and now no longer young in years" — who holds greater power [017]. The champions take their name from the Dry Tree, a token which some say stands on the hither side of the Wall of the World and others claim lies beyond it [018].

## Role in the Narrative

### Key Events

Ralph first hears of Hampton under Scaur from the Lady he rescues in the Wood Perilous. She bids him go there, describing its folk as peaceable — though the damsel of Bourton Abbas warns him that the Fellowship of the Dry Tree are robbers [005–006]. Roger of the Ropewalk, a man driven from his land at Hampton, tells a darker tale of the tyrants who seized his croft and his brother's wife for their sport [007].

The Lady of Abundance reveals that she was brought to Hampton by the tall champion (brother of the Knight of the Sun) after being cast forth, and there she has dwelt ever since, dwelling "betwixt Hampton and the Castle of Abundance" [017]. She describes her quiet act of freeing prisoners from the Scaur's dungeon, walking them past all gates and wards through her deep knowledge of the castle [017].

### Significance

Hampton under Scaur embodies the ambiguous nature of power in the wildlands. The Champions of the Dry Tree are both oppressors and protectors, feared by some and sought out by questers who mistake their token-name for a guide to the Well. The town's suffering under their rule contrasts with the Lady of Abundance's quiet acts of mercy carried out within their very stronghold.
