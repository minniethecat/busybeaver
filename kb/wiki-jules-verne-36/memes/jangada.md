## Overview

The Jangada is the colossal timber raft on which [[Joam Garral]] and his household voyage eight hundred leagues down the Amazon. More a floating village than a boat, it gives Verne's novel its original French title, *[[eight-hundred-leagues-on-the-amazon|La Jangada]]*, and serves as the mobile stage on which the whole story unfolds. (EHLA-006-005)(EHLA-006-015)

## Design & Operation

The word names a humble local craft before Verne magnifies it:

> rafts ... a triangular sail ... a cabin of mud and straw ... a floating home (EHLA-006-005)

Garral's version is built by felling timber on a giant scale. A whole section of his fazenda's forest is cut away to furnish the hull, the trees carried down to form the raft's body. (EHLA-006-010) The clearing required is vast:

> half a mile square of forest (EHLA-006-015)

The finished vessel is less a raft than a drifting landmass:

> it attained the dimensions of a small island (EHLA-006-015)

Verne underscores the scale by comparison with ordinary river craft:

> larger than a hundred egariteas or vigilingas coupled together (EHLA-006-016)

Upon this platform stand dwellings, a chapel, and store-rooms, the raft carried downstream by the Amazon's own current rather than by engine or sail.

## Role in the Story

The Jangada is the setting as much as the vehicle: the Garral family lives aboard it for the length of the river journey, and the novel's drama of concealed identity and belated justice plays out along its slow descent toward Belém. Its self-contained completeness lets Verne keep his cast together while the geography of the Amazon scrolls past.

## Science & Speculation

Here Verne exaggerates rather than invents. The jangada is a genuine Brazilian raft, and floating timber down the Amazon to market was real practice; Verne simply inflates the type to island scale for narrative grandeur. As with the [[granite-house-lift|Granite House Lift]], the interest is less in novel physics than in the spectacle of ordinary technology pushed to an extraordinary magnitude, a floating community sustained by the river itself.
