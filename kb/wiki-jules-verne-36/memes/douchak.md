## Overview

Douchak is a station on the Transcaspian section of the Grand Transasiatic railway, standing at the six-hundred-and-sixtieth verst of the line. In the Vernean corpus it belongs to *The Adventures of a Special Correspondent*, where it is both a halt on Claudius Bombarnac's journey and the point at which several fateful passengers board the train.

## In the Narrative

The train "extends to the station of Douchak at the six hundred and sixtieth verst, which we reach at six o'clock in the morning," (ASC-008-022) and there the reporter sets off "to look at Douchak with Major Noltitz as my cicerone." (ASC-008-023) The two "did" the town together, (ASC-008-028) though "the stoppage at Douchak had another half hour to last," (ASC-008-060) and afterwards the travellers found "the provisions having come freshly on board at Askhabad and Douchak." (ASC-009-029)

## Geography & Features

Douchak marks where the railway parts from the road it has followed: "it is only beyond Douchak that the line begins to leave it." (ASC-008-021) Its strategic value is considerable, for "this station at Douchak might be the terminus of a line from British India through the Afghan frontier, Kandahar, the Bolan Pass and the Pendjeh oasis, that would unite the two systems." (ASC-008-025) It was here too that the funeral train "took from Douchak the body of the great mandarin Yen Lou coming from Persia to Pekin." (ASC-010-041)

## Connections

Douchak is bound to *The Adventures of a Special Correspondent* as the boarding-point of the story's schemers. It was there that "my lord Faruskiar ... had joined the train at Douchak so as to accompany the treasure to its destination," (ASC-018-006) and there that the reporter noticed "the suspicious-looking Mongols we picked up at Douchak." (ASC-011-017) The distinction is later drawn between the ordinary passengers and "those we picked up at Douchak when we picked up Faruskiar and Ghangir." (ASC-019-003)
