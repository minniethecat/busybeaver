## Overview

Sacramento is the capital of California and the river that shares its name. In the Vernean corpus it marks the western reaches of the transcontinental journey in *Around the World in Eighty Days*.

It appears again as the northern mining hub and legislative seat of California in *Godfrey Morgan*.

## In the Narrative

In *Around the World in Eighty Days*, Sacramento lies near the western end of the Pacific Railroad, whose line runs from Omaha across the continent to the Californian coast. (AWED-026-006) The train leaves Sacramento and, passing the junctions of Roclin, Auburn, and Colfax, climbs into the Sierra Nevada. (AWED-026-015)

The country between San Francisco and Sacramento is described as not very hilly, (AWED-026-014) while on the river itself many-decked steamboats ply the Sacramento and its tributaries, alongside clippers and steamers of every nation. (AWED-025-002)

In *Godfrey Morgan*, Sacramento is named as the centre for the mines of northern California — the rival of Stockton, which serves the mines of the south. (GM-002-037) It is also a seat of government: the millionaire Kolderup wins his place in the Legislative Council of Sacramento. (GM-002-041)

## Geography & Features

Sacramento is presented both as a city of northern California and as a navigable river feeding a network of tributaries. Its steamboat traffic links the inland districts to San Francisco Bay. (AWED-025-002)

The rail line running east from the city carries travellers up the grade of the Sierra Nevada, by way of Roclin, Auburn, and Colfax. (AWED-026-015)

## Connections

Sacramento anchors the Californian leg of *Around the World in Eighty Days*, the point where Phileas Fogg's transcontinental crossing nears the Pacific and the passage home by sea. (AWED-026-014)

As the northern mining capital of *Godfrey Morgan* it stands beside Stockton and San Francisco in Verne's picture of Gold-Rush California, (GM-002-037) tying the mining economy to the state's political life in the Legislative Council. (GM-002-041)
