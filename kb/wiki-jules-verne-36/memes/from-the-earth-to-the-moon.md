## Overview

*From the Earth to the Moon* is Jules Verne's satirical space-adventure, in which the [[Gun Club]] of [[baltimore|Baltimore]] turns its ballistic genius toward the Moon. It opens with the club's founding — "During the Federal war in the United States a new and very influential club was established in the city of [[baltimore|Baltimore]], Maryland" (FEM-001-002).

Its president [[Impey Barbicane]] poses the question that drives the novel: "Is it possible to send a projectile to the moon?" (FEM-004-009). The answer is the casting of the [[Columbiad]], a colossal cannon.

## Plot Summary

With the Civil War over, the artillerists of the [[gun-club|Gun Club]] languish for want of a target, having "singularly surpassed Europeans" in "the science of ballistics" (FEM-001-003).

[[barbicane|Barbicane]] revives them with his lunar proposal, and the club resolves to cast a gigantic cannon in Florida to hurl a projectile at the Moon. The French adventurer [[Michel Ardan]] volunteers to ride inside it, transforming a cannon-shot into a crewed voyage that ends on a note of suspense, continued in *[[Round the Moon]]*.

## Main Characters

- [[Impey Barbicane]] — president of the [[gun-club|Gun Club]] and originator of the Moon-shot
- [[Michel Ardan]] — the French adventurer who volunteers to ride the projectile

## Key Places & Technology

- [[Columbiad]] — the titanic cannon cast in Florida
- [[The Columbiad Launch]] — the firing of the projectile toward the Moon
- [[Gun Club]] — the [[baltimore|Baltimore]] artillery society behind the enterprise

## Themes

The novel fuses genuine astronautical calculation with broad satire of American enterprise and the martial "military instinct" (FEM-001-002) of a nation of gunmakers. Verne turns the machinery of war toward exploration, imagining spaceflight through the lens of nineteenth-century ballistics.
