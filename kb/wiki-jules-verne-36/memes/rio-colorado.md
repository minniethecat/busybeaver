## Overview

The Rio Colorado is a river of Patagonia, one of the two great streams that, with the Rio Negro, cross the thirty-seventh parallel and carry Lord Glenarvan's search across the Pampas in In Search of the Castaways. Besides the other Patagonian rivers, the Rio Colorado and the Rio Negro flow into the sea along deserted solitudes, uninhabited and uninhabitable. (SC-032-011)

It stood among the waters that might have borne the castaways' bottle: here are the Rio Negro and Rio Colorado, and their tributaries intersected by the 37th parallel, any of which might have carried the bottle on its waters. (SC-010-033)

## In the Narrative

The river shaped the party's route from the first council. One counsel was simply to go up the Rio Colorado instead. (SC-008-076) Paganel's plan led through the capital of Araucania and over the Cordilleras, then gently down past the Neuquem and the Rio Colorado on to the Pampas. (SC-010-043)

As they pressed east, the river remained the measure of distance: they were still ninety miles from the point where the Rio Colorado crosses the thirty-seventh parallel, about two days' journey. (SC-016-015) By evening the valley of the Colorado was already sunk in shadow, night drawing her mantle over the eastern slopes of the Andes. (SC-013-015)

## Geography & Features

The Rio Colorado flows into the sea across the empty Patagonian solitudes, paired with the Rio Negro as the region's principal rivers. (SC-032-011) Its tributaries are cut by the 37th parallel, the line the expedition followed. (SC-010-033)

Between the Colorado and the Rio Negro lay a wild country roamed by Indian tribes; it was there that a European was held a slave among them. (SC-016-074)

## Connections

The Rio Colorado is bound to the story of the captive the searchers pursued. The Frenchman was dragged by the cruel Indians as far as the shores of the Colorado, but at length managed to escape. (SC-021-057)

The river's chief role was as one arm of the twofold Patagonian watercourse, forever named beside the Rio Negro, whose parallel crossings framed the whole search along the thirty-seventh degree. (SC-010-033)
