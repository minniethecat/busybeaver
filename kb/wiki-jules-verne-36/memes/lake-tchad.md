# Lake Tchad

## Overview

Lake Tchad is the great Central African lake — the historical Lake Chad — reached by Dr. Ferguson's balloon expedition in *Five Weeks in a Balloon*. It is a central object of the whole enterprise: for one earlier explorer "the principal aim of his journey was to reconnoitre Lake Tchad, from which he was still three hundred and fifty miles distant." (FWB-004-009)

Verne frames the lake within the great geographical project of joining the continent's explored regions, "to explore all the country between the Nile and Lake Tchad." (FWB-005-063)

## In the Narrative

The travellers approach the lake as a longed-for landmark. Ferguson exults, "everything works in our favor: we shall discover Lake Tchad this very day." (FWB-031-004) Their reckoning fixes them "nearly five hundred miles from Lake Tchad, and more than four hundred miles from the western coast of Africa." (FWB-026-014)

Passing "in the midst of rich and verdant landscapes," the *Victoria* at last "reached the southern shore of Lake Tchad" about nine in the morning. (FWB-031-028) Even the character of its water becomes a subject of debate once "we've settled the question as to what kind of water there is in Lake Tchad." (FWB-031-040)

## Geography & Features

The lake lies in a low basin fed by rivers: "the river Shari, which eighty miles farther on rolled its impetuous waters into Lake Tchad, was quite distinctly seen." (FWB-030-020) Its highlands divide watersheds, for "the lofty summits of these mountains separate the valley of the Niger from the basin of Lake Tchad." (FWB-029-048)

Verne stresses how poorly charted it is: "the chart of Lake Tchad is very difficult to trace with exactitude, for it is surrounded by muddy and almost impassable morasses, in which Barth thought that he was doomed to perish." (FWB-031-030) Earlier travellers had reached "Kouka, near Lake Tchad," by the caravan route from Tripoli. (FWB-030-015)
