# The Lima Indian Uprising

## Overview

The Lima Indian Uprising is the great insurrection of *[[The Pearl of Lima]]*, in which the Sambo rouses the Indians, zambos, and chiños of the city to rise at the stroke of five against their Spanish oppressors, only for the revolt to be broken before the president's palace under the fire of the government's cannon (PL-008-011) (PL-008-028).

## What Happens

Through the streets wandered a great number of Indians, zambos, and chiños, who at the secret orders of their chiefs gathered by degrees into the wealthiest quarters of Lima (PL-008-003). A man emerged from a neighbouring alley and came to them—it was the Sambo (PL-008-008), who said, "The Spaniard has returned; he is one of the race which crushes us—woe to him!" (PL-008-009).

Asked when they should strike, he answered, "When five o'clock sounds, and the tocsin from the mountain gives the signal of vengeance" (PL-008-011). Then the Sambo marched with hasty steps to the *chingana* and rejoined the chief of the revolt (PL-008-012).

The sun had begun to sink, the hour when the Limanian aristocracy went in its turn to the Amancaës, their richest equipages shining along the road (PL-008-013).

## Significance

The clock on the cathedral tower suddenly struck five, and a shrill funereal sound vibrated through the air as the tocsin thundered over the crowd (PL-008-014).

An immense cry resounded, and from every square, street, and house issued the Indians, arms in their hands and fury in their eyes (PL-008-015). "Death to the Spaniards! death to the oppressors!" was the watch-word of the rebels (PL-008-016).

Those who tried to return to Lima recoiled before these masses, while the summits of the hills were covered with other enemies, so that all retreat was cut off (PL-008-017). At the head of one column Martin Paz waved the black flag—the flag of independence (PL-008-018).

But the government's soldiers, forewarned of the revolt, were ranged in battle array before the palace of the president, and a frightful fusillade greeted the insurgents at their approach (PL-008-019). As Manangani sprang upon the first steps, the opening ranks of soldiers unmasked two pieces of cannon ready to fire on the assailants (PL-008-021).

## Participants & Setting

- The Sambo, chief spirit of the revolt, who gathers the Indians in the wealthy quarters and fixes the tocsin at five o'clock as the signal of vengeance against the Spaniards (PL-008-008) (PL-008-011).

- Martin Paz, the young Indian who leads a column of insurgents bearing the black flag of independence in the assault on the president's palace (PL-008-018).

- Manangani, the ferocious warrior who springs first upon the palace steps, only to be met by the unmasked cannon (PL-008-021).

- The cannon fired and the grape swept the Indians on the steps; the ensuing flight bore all the consequences of treason, and the soldiers pursued the scattered rebels and killed a great number without mercy (PL-008-028) (PL-008-030).
