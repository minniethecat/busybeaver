## Overview

*Master Zacharius* is Jules Verne's fantastical allegory set in old Geneva, which "lies at the west end of the lake of the same name" (MZ-001-002). In a curious aged house dwells "the old clockmaker, Master Zacharius, whose household consisted of his daughter Gerande, Aubert Thun, his apprentice, and his old servant" Scholastique (MZ-001-004).

It is a tale of a craftsman whose genius curdles into blasphemy.

## Plot Summary

A strange affliction falls upon the town: the watches Zacharius made have "suddenly stopped, and without any apparent reason," and though he takes them apart, "despite his skill, they will not go" (MZ-002-003, MZ-001-029).

The old man believes his own life is bound to them, declaring himself "really the creator of all the watches that I have fashioned," so that "every time that one of these accursed watches stops, I feel my heart cease beating" (MZ-001-047, MZ-001-047).

His apprentice Aubert, who loves Gerande, watches the master consumed until "the pride of science has possessed you," as he labours over "this crystal watch," his intended masterpiece (MZ-002-008, MZ-002-013). Zacharius exalts himself above God: "If God has created eternity, Master Zacharius has created time!" (MZ-003-055).

A monstrous dwarf then haunts the town, muttering "your watches will never go, and Gerande will not wed Aubert" (MZ-003-065). He offers the failing clockmaker the secret of restoring his watches — but only "on the day after that on which you have given me your daughter's hand" (MZ-003-059).

Despoiled and dying, Zacharius vanishes from Geneva, seeking the one clock not yet returned: an iron clock "sold to M. Pittonaccio... sent to his château at Andernatt" (MZ-005-013). Gerande, Aubert, and Scholastique follow him to the ruined castle "in the gorge of the 'Dents-du-Midi'" (MZ-005-018).

There the dwarf reveals himself as "Signor Pittonaccio," reminding the old man, "you have remembered my words, 'Gerande will not wed Aubert'" (MZ-005-068). Against him the hermit insists that Zacharius's soul is "immaterial" and "immortal" (MZ-005-041).

Zacharius winds the clock frantically, reading its impious maxims, until the final hour: as midnight fails to sound, the words appear — "WHO EVER SHALL ATTEMPT TO MAKE HIMSELF THE EQUAL OF GOD, SHALL BE FOR EVER DAMNED!" (MZ-005-114).

"The old clock burst with a noise like thunder"; Pittonaccio seized the escaping spring and "ingulfed himself in the earth," and "Master Zacharius fell backwards. He was dead" (MZ-005-115, MZ-005-117, MZ-005-118). Aubert and Gerande returned to Geneva, dutiful ever after "to redeem by prayer the soul of the castaway" (MZ-005-121).

## Main Characters

- Master Zacharius — the aged Geneva clockmaker who believes his soul lives in his watches
- Gerande — his devoted daughter, whom the dwarf demands in marriage
- Aubert Thun — the apprentice who loves Gerande
- Scholastique — the old servant who defends her master against charges of sorcery
- Pittonaccio — the diabolical dwarf who bargains for Zacharius's soul and Gerande's hand

## Key Places & Technology

- Geneva — the old man's home, where he keeps his shop and his clocks
- The château of Andernatt — the ruined castle in the Dents-du-Midi holding the fatal clock
- The iron clock and the crystal watch — Zacharius's masterworks, in which he fancies his life resides

## Themes

Verne turns clockmaking into a parable of hubris, as a Christian craftsman comes to think he has made himself the master of time, boasting that he "created time" (MZ-003-055). Against the pride of science he sets the hermit's warning that the soul is "immaterial" and "immortal," letting damnation answer the man who would rival God (MZ-005-041).
