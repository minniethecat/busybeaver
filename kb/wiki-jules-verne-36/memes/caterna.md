# Caterna

## Overview

He betrays his origins the moment he speaks, for "his way of expressing himself shows that 'Monsieur Caterna'--if that was his name--was a sailor, or ought to have been one" (ASC-004-046).

The narrator sizes him up at once as a born entertainer: "as a Parisian, Caterna must have been the wag of the forecastle when he was at sea" (ASC-009-050).

## Role in the Story

He and his wife settle into the train with easy good humour, and from their car "I hear the chorus of an operetta in the deep voice of Monsieur Caterna" (ASC-006-018).

His trade is the strolling stage, for he confides "we have made a little money by going about from town to town" as touring players (ASC-009-062).

His theatrical instinct never sleeps, and one evening "the dinner lasted till rather late, and terminated in an unexpected manner by an offer from Caterna to recite a monologue" (ASC-014-052).

Even amid the dervishes of Samarkand "the instinct of the old actor was awakened in Caterna," ever ready to perform (ASC-016-016).

## Character & Traits

Once launched into talk he cannot be stopped: "it was a pleasure to listen to Caterna; he was in steam... and the only thing to do was to let him blow off" (ASC-009-049).

For all his bluster he is a tender husband, and the narrator notes "with what attention Monsieur Caterna looks after his wife" Caroline, encouraging her after her seasickness (ASC-005-042).

His whole manner is that of the stage, explaining "the attitudes, and mobile physiognomy, and demonstrative gestures of Caterna" (ASC-006-063).

## Relationships

- [[Claudius Bombarnac]] — the special correspondent who befriends him and draws out his yarns, asking "you must have been a good deal about the world, Monsieur Caterna?" (ASC-009-053).
- [[Major Noltitz]] — a fellow passenger and confidant, the one companion whose learned jest "was only understood by Major Noltitz," as Caterna turns to his wife nearby (ASC-011-048).
