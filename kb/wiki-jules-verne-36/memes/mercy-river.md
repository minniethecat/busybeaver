## Overview

The Mercy is the principal river of Lincoln Island in The Mysterious Island. The settlers gave the river, which furnished them with drinking water and near which the balloon had thrown them, the name of the Mercy, in true gratitude to Providence. (MI-011-074) Its mouth opened at the cliff between the projection of the shore and the rocks that formed the Chimneys. (MI-019-011)

## In the Narrative

The Mercy framed the settlers' earliest movements: to return to the Chimneys it was enough to cross the plateau and descend to the elbow formed by the first detour of the Mercy. (MI-012-069) By the left bank of the Mercy, Cyrus Harding and his companions arrived at the Chimneys. (MI-012-070)

Later they followed the bank of the Mercy, traversed Prospect Heights, and reached a glade near Lake Grant. (MI-013-028) The river also became a highway: Harding built a flat-bottomed canoe to navigate the Mercy and approach its source, where the water would be shallow. (MI-023-033) At six in the morning the canoe put off and proceeded to the mouth of the Mercy. (MI-025-008)

## Geography & Features

The source of the Mercy lay hidden in the mountain: it was impossible even to follow the course of the Mercy, or to ascertain in what part of the mountain it took its source. (MI-023-055) Towards four o'clock the navigation of the Mercy became exceedingly difficult, for its course was obstructed by aquatic plants and rocks. (MI-025-056)

In winter ice accumulated at the mouth of the Mercy, and it was not long before the whole expanse of Lake Grant was frozen. (MI-021-003) A bed of oysters formed among the rocks at the mouth. (MI-020-027)

## Connections

The Mercy bound together the island's inland districts. On its left bank lay the forest to which the settlers gave the name of Jacamar Wood. (MI-013-033) By following the Mercy through the woods of the Far West, or the way to Lake Grant, the travellers were certain to arrive at Prospect Heights and Union Bay. (MI-012-013)

Granite House stood well placed between the Mercy and Lake Grant, an advantage not to be neglected. (MI-016-012)
