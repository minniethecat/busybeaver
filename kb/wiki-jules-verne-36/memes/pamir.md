## Overview

The Pamir is the great high plateau of Central Asia, commonly called the "Roof of the World," from which radiate the mighty chains of the Thian Shan, Kuen Lun, Kara Korum, Himalaya, and Hindoo Koosh. (ASC-015-033) The piercing of the Pamir joined the Russian railway with the Chinese line running from one frontier of the Celestial Empire to the other. (ASC-016-003)

## In the Narrative

The correspondent looks forward to the crossing: as the train approaches the Pamir the scenery will change, and he waits for this crossing and its varied landscape. (ASC-009-008) By night the line runs through the defiles of the Pamir, where travel is no longer quiet. (ASC-013-010)

Among the gorges the gradients grow so steep as to demand more engine power. (ASC-015-002) Beyond one station the road reaches the frontier dividing Russian Turkestan from the Pamir plateau and the vast territory of the Kara-Khirghizes. (ASC-015-030) At first the train runs down the slopes of the Pamir at great speed. (ASC-015-048)

Even at journey's far end the plateau is remembered: since leaving the Caspian, deserts have succeeded deserts, save when crossing the Pamir. (ASC-022-059)

## Geography & Features

Fed by the snows and rains of the plateau, sluggish waters flow between low clay cliffs and banks of sand. (ASC-010-064) The travellers of the Aryan peoples have all attempted to explore it. (ASC-015-035) Birches and junipers are its principal trees, with tamarisks, sedges, and reeds by the saline pools. (ASC-015-039)

A varied fauna lives on its heights. (ASC-015-040) From the Pamir tableland extends the mighty range of the Himalaya, where rise the highest summits on the face of the globe. (ASC-018-042)
