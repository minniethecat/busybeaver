## Overview

*Godfrey Morgan* is Jules Verne's playful Robinsonade, a castaway tale that is secretly a contrivance. Its hero is Godfrey Morgan, in "his twenty-third year," the orphaned nephew and heir of the fabulously rich William W. Kolderup of San Francisco (GM-003-023, GM-003-022).

Brought up in his uncle's house alongside the goddaughter Phina Hollaney, Godfrey is expected to marry her (GM-003-014, GM-003-025).

## Plot Summary

The story opens with the auction of Spencer Island, which "lies in the west-south-west of the Bay of San Francisco, about 460 miles from the Californian coast" (GM-001-028). Kolderup wins it after a furious bidding duel with his enemy J. R. Taskinar (GM-002-042).

Before settling to marriage, Godfrey craves adventure, dreaming of "a shipwreck or two on a desert island where he could live the life of a Selkirk or a Robinson Crusoe" (GM-003-029). He sails off with his absurd dancing-master, Professor Tartlet, and is duly wrecked on a lonely Pacific island, where the pair endure the trials of castaways and are joined by a rescued native, Carefinotu.

The whole ordeal proves a fiction. Kolderup reveals it was "Spencer Island, only three days' journey from San Francisco, on which I thought it would be a good plan for you to serve your apprenticeship to the Crusoe business," the shipwreck and savages all staged, and Carefinotu really his servant Jup Brass playing Friday (GM-022-020, GM-022-030).

## Main Characters

- Godfrey Morgan — the rich young Californian who longs to be a castaway
- William W. Kolderup — his uncle, who stages the entire desert-island ordeal
- Phina Hollaney — Kolderup's goddaughter, betrothed to Godfrey
- Professor Tartlet — Godfrey's ridiculous dancing-master and companion

## Key Places & Technology

- Spencer Island — the Pacific island bought at auction and used to school Godfrey
- The staged shipwreck — the sham disaster that maroons Godfrey and Tartlet

## Themes

Verne turns the desert-island genre inside out, mocking the romance of the castaway even as he indulges it. Beneath the comedy lies a lesson in maturity, as a pampered youth's craving for hardship is granted, exposed, and gently cured before he is fit to marry.
