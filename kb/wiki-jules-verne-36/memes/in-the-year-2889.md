## Overview

*In the Year 2889* is a futurist short story published under Jules Verne's name, imagining that "the people of this twenty-ninth century live continually in fairyland," so surfeited with marvels that "to them all seems natural" (YEAR-001-001).

It follows a single day of Fritz Napoleon Smith, director of the great newspaper the *Earth Chronicle*, whose spoken edition reaches its subscribers each morning by telephone (YEAR-001-006).

## Plot Summary

The narrative opens on the "Earth Chronicle" building in 253d Avenue at Centropolis, the political center to which power moved from Washington two centuries before (YEAR-001-004, YEAR-001-005). No longer printed but "spoken to subscribers," the paper has grown to 80,000,000 subscribers and made Smith a king of wealth (YEAR-001-006, YEAR-001-007).

Smith awakes "in very bad humor," his wife having left for France eight days earlier (YEAR-001-010). He begins his round through the departments: rebuking a novelist whose story "lacks the elements of truth," then passing the hall of fifteen hundred reporters telephoning the world's news (YEAR-001-012, YEAR-001-013).

In the astronomical department he learns of phototelegrams from Mercury, Venus, and Mars, where "there is a revolution in the Central Empire," while Jupiter still sends no answer (YEAR-001-016, YEAR-001-018, YEAR-001-020).

Among the scientific editors, Cooley and Peer report that the Moon's near face is "positively uninhabited," prompting talk of turning "the moon about-face" (YEAR-001-030, YEAR-001-034, YEAR-001-035).

Passing the hall of atmospheric advertising, whose messages are "reflected from the clouds," Smith reaches the reception chamber of ambassadors (YEAR-001-039, YEAR-001-041). The Russian, "pressed on our eastern frontier by the Chinese," seeks leave to push west (YEAR-001-043).

The British agent begs annulment of the annexation that has made "Great Britain a colony of the United States" (YEAR-001-049, YEAR-001-050).

At noon a table rises through the floor, and by the phonotelephote Smith breakfasts "face to face" with his wife in Paris, their food piped in from the Grand Alimentation Company (YEAR-001-060, YEAR-001-061, YEAR-001-062). Afterward his air-coach carries him to his accumulator works at Niagara, where he collects and sells the falls' long-wasted energy (YEAR-001-068, YEAR-001-069).

Back at his office he hears petitioners: a chemist who would resolve the last three elements and "produce easily all bodies whatever," a projector who moves whole cities, a climate engineer, and the committee proposing to revive Dr. Nathaniel Faithburn from a century of hibernation (YEAR-001-080, YEAR-001-084, YEAR-001-085, YEAR-001-087). Smith advances the chemist $100,000 (YEAR-001-083).

His physician Dr. Wilkins warns that he is "over-worked," then stays to dine (YEAR-001-097, YEAR-001-098). At ten the awakening of Faithburn proceeds, but the "veritable mummy" cannot be revived: "Faithburn was dead, quite certainly dead!" (YEAR-001-114, YEAR-001-115). The tale closes noting that "the history of that one day is the history of 365 days every year" (YEAR-001-118).

## Main Characters

- Fritz Napoleon Smith — director of the *Earth Chronicle*, whose single day the story chronicles
- Mrs. Smith — his wife, in Paris, with whom he speaks by phonotelephote
- Dr. Wilkins — his physician, who warns him against over-work
- William Cooley and Peer — the scientific editors debating Jupiter and the Moon
- Dr. Nathaniel Faithburn — the hibernation experimenter whose resurrection fails

## Key Places & Technology

- The *Earth Chronicle* building in 253d Avenue, Centropolis — the newspaper's vast headquarters (YEAR-001-004)
- The phonotelephote — the device by which Smith sees and speaks with his wife in Paris (YEAR-001-061)
- The accumulator and transformer, and the Niagara works that harness the falls (YEAR-001-069)
- Atmospheric advertising, whose messages are projected upon the clouds (YEAR-001-039)

## Themes

The story extrapolates nineteenth-century invention into a triumphant future, insisting its marvels "rest upon principles which were perfectly familiar to our remote ancestors, but which they disregarded" (YEAR-001-002).

Beneath the optimism run strands of satire: an America so dominant that "the whole of America belongs to the Americans" and Britain is a colony (YEAR-001-052), and a mechanized life whose ceaseless toil leaves even its king "over-worked" (YEAR-001-097).
