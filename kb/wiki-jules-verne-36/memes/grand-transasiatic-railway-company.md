# Grand Transasiatic Railway Company

## Overview

The Grand Transasiatic Railway Company operates the great through line that carries the reporter Claudius Bombarnac — and the entire novel — from the Caspian shore across Central Asia to China. Its train runs "the direct Grand Transasiatic between the European frontier and the capital of the Celestial Empire" (ASC-001-007).

The enterprise is brand new. "This Grand Transasiatic has only been open six weeks and the company is as yet only running two trains a week" (ASC-005-014). Its through service has already transformed travel: the baron uses "the Grand Transasiatic which puts Pekin within a fortnight of the Prussian capital" and so "might halve the old time" (ASC-006-083).

## Role in the Story

The railway is the stage on which the whole book takes place; the narrator sets out to chronicle it, resolving "not to lose a detail of my run on the Grand Transasiatic" (ASC-001-036). The departure itself is an event: "the cars begin to move, and a loud cheer salutes the departure of the Grand Transasiatic express" (ASC-005-050).

The line's geography structures the plot. It "traverses in Turkestan, and the plains of the desert of Gobi" (ASC-002-038), climbs the Pamir plateau with "extraordinary" difficulty (ASC-015-037), and meets at Kachgar, now "a station on the Grand Transasiatic, the junction between the Russian and Chinese lines" (ASC-016-008).

Its safety is a running concern. Major Noltitz warns that beyond the Pamir "the Grand Transasiatic is under Chinese control, and I have not much confiden[ce]" (ASC-008-032), while the mysterious bandit's "presence has ever been reported in that part of Mongolia served by the Grand Transasiatic" (ASC-008-054).

The famous smuggled coffin also travels as company freight, entrusted "to the care of the Grand Transasiatic" (ASC-016-067).

## Members

The company appears through its officials, engineers, and the travellers who depend on it.

- The General Manager — the "general manager of the line" who "ought to keep an eye on" the freight entrusted to the railway (ASC-016-067)

- General Annenkof — the engineer who "in ten months, at a cost of thirty-five thousand roubles... built this bridge, the most important one on the Grand Transasiatic" (ASC-010-066)

- [[Claudius Bombarnac]] — the special correspondent sent to report on the line (ASC-001-036)

- [[Major Noltitz]] — seasoned traveller who has been "over the whole line known as the Grand Transasiatic" five or six times (ASC-006-035)

Passengers grumble at its delays — "whenever it is behind there are new recriminations and menaces against the Grand Transasiatic Company" (ASC-006-017) — and its officials keep to a strict schedule: "The Grand Transasiatic does not wait" (ASC-016-056).

## Activities

The company's business is running the through express and the trade it carries. Major Noltitz points out "the incontestable advantages of the Transasiatic with regard to the trade between Grand Asia and Europe in the security and rapidity of its communicat[ions]" (ASC-011-053).

Its officials handle passengers and freight alike, forwarding goods along the line — a Roumanian woman takes "advantage of this through train on the Grand Transasiatic to get her glass forwarded" (ASC-004-017), and "the officials of the Grand Transasiatic" are pledged to attend to declared cargo (ASC-004-075).

Operations run at speed and at risk. An express runs "at sixty kilometres an hour" (ASC-014-070), though "such a hurricane as sometimes stops the locomotives of the Grand Transasiatic" (ASC-009-002) can halt it. Where the line crosses lawless country, the company has even "had to make terms" with bandit chiefs (ASC-016-045) to keep the trains running.
