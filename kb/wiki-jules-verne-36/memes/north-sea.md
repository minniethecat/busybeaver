## Overview

The North Sea is the shallow arm of the Atlantic lying between Britain and the European mainland. It recurs across many of Verne's voyages — a fishing ground, a schooner's passage, and a cold northern limit measured against far inland exile.

In 1864, during experiments on fishing by electric light in the middle of the North Sea, glass panes less than seven millimetres thick resisted a pressure of sixteen atmospheres. (TTLU-013-033)

## In the Narrative

Bound for Iceland, the schooner doubles the Skaw at the northern point of Denmark, passes the Skager Rack in the night, skirts Norway by Cape Lindness, and enters the North Sea. (JCE-009-015) Off the Norwegian coast the airship "Albatross" keeps her southerly course after leaving Gousta. (RC-014-032)

Before he ventured on the open sea, old Ole Kamp had fished the fiords and robbed the sea-birds' nests, and as a cabin-boy he made a voyage across the North Sea. (TN-004-003) Far to the east, exiles keep their festival in a wretched hut six hundred leagues from the North Sea, in a temperature thirty degrees below zero. (WAI-012-023)

## Geography & Features

Its people are the Flemings, calm and moderate, still to be met between the Scheldt and the North Sea. (DOSE-001-004) Aboard the "Nautilus" its bounty is served up as sugar drawn from the huge fucus plants of the North Sea. (TTLU-010-078) The same waters hosted the early trials of electric-light fishing, whose thin glass panes withstood sixteen atmospheres of pressure. (TTLU-013-033)
