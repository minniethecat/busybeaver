# Hudson's Bay Company

## Overview

The Hudson's Bay Company is the great British fur-trading concern whose commercial ambitions set the whole action of *The Fur Country* in motion. Throughout the novel it is referred to simply as "the Company," a power whose forts, agents, and expeditions dominate the North American north.

Its wealth rests on furs — "the costly furs piled up in the magazines of the Company, which was this year to realise hitherto unheard of profits" (FC-001-019). Its officers are bred to absolute obedience; Lieutenant Hobson is "a child of the Company" (FC-001-024), trained "never questioning" his orders, "a true machine in uniform" serving the Company (FC-001-025).

At its height "the Hudson's Bay Company employed about a million men on its territories" (FC-004-007), its domain "extending over 3,700,000 square miles" (FC-002-023).

## Role in the Story

The novel's central enterprise is the Company's decision to plant a new fort on the very shore of the Arctic Ocean. Captain Craventy explains "a matter of vital interest to the Company" (FC-002-015).

The fur-bearing animals have retreated north, and "the Company is also compelled to seek a more northern centre of operations, for an Act of Parliament has lately greatly reduced its domain" (FC-002-045).

The Company therefore mounts the expedition that forms the book's action: "the Company had collected everything necessary for the expedition" (FC-004-058), attaching "great importance" to "the establishment of a new factory beyond the seventieth parallel" (FC-004-068). Lieutenant Hobson leads it north to found the settlement (FC-008-005).

The Company also shapes the fate of its people. Mrs Paulina Barnett travels under its protection, carrying a "special letter of recommendation" from "the governor of the Company" (FC-001-027), and the astronomer secures "urgent letters of recommendation to the principal agents of the Hudson's Bay Company" (FC-003-058) precisely so as not to "miss the departure of Lieutenant Hobson" (FC-003-060).

## Members

The Company's people in the story range from its governors to its frontier officers and the travellers under its wing.

- [[Jaspar Hobson]] — the lieutenant who leads the Company's expedition to found the new Arctic fort (FC-001-024)

- Captain Craventy — commander of Fort Reliance, who briefs the party on the Company's history and aims (FC-002-016)

- [[Paulina Barnett]] — the celebrated traveller who journeys under the governor's letter of recommendation (FC-001-027)

- The governor of the Company — who authorises Mrs Barnett's passage and the expedition's aims (FC-001-027)

Its founding shareholders were men of high rank: the 1670 licence "numbered several men of high rank amongst its shareholders: the Duke of York, the Duke" and others (FC-002-019). Hobson defines himself entirely by the institution: "what am I but an employé of the Hudson's Bay Company?" (FC-007-023).

## Activities

The Company's business is the fur trade, conducted through a network of forts and factories across the north. Captain Craventy sketches its history: "On the 2nd May, 1670, a licence to trade in furs in the Hudson's Bay Territory was granted to the Company" (FC-002-019). English traders "learned the customs of the country, the ways of the Indians and their system of exchange of goods" (FC-002-020).

Trade with the Indians is transacted by a token system: "the agents of the Company give him as many pieces of wood as he brings skins, and he exchanges these pieces of wood for manufactured articles on the premises" (FC-004-034). The forts serve as depôts; one is "no more than a depôt of provisions" (FC-006-003), and the flag of the Company floats "from the tower of the fort" (FC-008-037).

The Company's fortunes have declined. "Until 1839, madam," says the Captain, "the Company was in a flourishing condition" (FC-002-041), but its profits "for the last twenty years have been decreasing" (FC-002-039).

Its rivalry with the North-west Company ended in 1821, when "a treaty was made" and the old rival was absorbed (FC-002-022). Craventy warns that "the interests of the Company and those of civilisation are antagonistic" (FC-002-047).
