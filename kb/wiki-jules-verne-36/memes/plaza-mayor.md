## Overview

The Plaza-Mayor is the great central square of Lima, that forum of the ancient city of kings, where a great movement of the populace gathered and artisans quit their daily labors to circulate among the crowd. (PL-001-003) So bound up was it with the life of the Peruvian capital that even a whistling cry heard far off in the night recalled the sound once heard upon the Plaza-Mayor. (PL-007-015)

## In the Narrative

A magnificent carriage drawn by four fine horses entered the Plaza-Mayor, bearing a single occupant of proud but sorrowful mien who gazed, without seeming to see them, upon the assembled multitude. (PL-001-015) It was there, the old duenna recalled, that a stranger had come to the young girl's assistance one evening. (PL-003-047)

When the rising came at last, Martin Paz stood at the head of one of the rebel columns, waving the black flag, the flag of independence, while the Indians attacked through the neighboring streets. (PL-008-018) Had he but remained upon the Plaza-Mayor, it was said, he might have conquered; instead the crowd of Indians swelled with all the conquered of the square. (PL-008-048, PL-008-034)

## Geography & Features

At the centre of the Plaza-Mayor played a pretty fountain, near which the young people of Lima were wont to gather. (PL-001-007) On the north side of the square rose the viceroy's palace; before its central portico the mounted guards were stationed, and when the whole Plaza-Mayor was alive with cries and tumult they could scarcely maintain their position. (PL-001-040)

## Connections

The Plaza-Mayor lay at the heart of [[Lima]], and in the palace fronting it the president Gambarra seemed immovable in his power. (PL-005-003) The square gives both its stage and its name to the events of [[The Pearl of Lima]], from the first gathering of the crowd to the waving of the flag of independence. (PL-001-003)
