## Overview

The Ganges is the great sacred river of India, an artery of Phileas Fogg's crossing. Where once one travelled by palanquin or coach, now fast steamboats ply on the Indus and the Ganges, and a railway threads the peninsula. (AWED-010-004) The Great Indian Peninsula Railway, leaving Bombay, runs north-east across the Western Ghauts toward the river country. (AWED-010-005)

## In the Narrative

Fogg's road follows the river through the holy places of India. His elephant descended the spurs of the Vindhias and passed the village of Kallenger on the Cani, one of the branches of the Ganges. (AWED-012-010) At Allahabad, the City of God, the line meets the junction of the two sacred rivers, Ganges and Jumna. (AWED-014-006)

## Geography & Features

Beyond Benares the railway runs a while along the valley of the Ganges. (AWED-014-024) The river carries the dead as well as the living: borne by the Ganges to the high seas were deceased villagers not fully devoured by the vultures. (TTLU-025-040) Geographers rank it with the Indus as a rival of the Oxus of the Ancients. (ASC-010-064)

## Connections

The Ganges waters the plains crossed by the railway from [[Bombay]] through [[Benares]]. (AWED-014-024) Its corpse-laden current reaches the sea where the [[Nautilus]] of [[Twenty Thousand Leagues Under the Sea]] passed. (TTLU-025-040)
