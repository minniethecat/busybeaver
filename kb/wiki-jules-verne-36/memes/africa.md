## Overview

Africa is the vast continent whose unknown interior Jules Verne's first published voyage sets out to unveil. At the outset it is, at length, about to surrender the secret of her vast solitudes to a modern OEdipus. (FWB-002-003) The point of intersection no traveller had yet reached — the very heart of Africa — is where all efforts should now be directed. (FWB-002-004)

Dr. Ferguson's design is the boldest yet: this intrepid discoverer proposes to traverse all Africa from east to west in a balloon. (FWB-002-006)

## In the Narrative

Ferguson intends not to be separated from the balloon until he reaches the western coast of Africa. (FWB-003-066) To secure his landfall, the English Government places a transport at his disposal, and three or four vessels are to cruise off the western coast about the presumed period of his arrival. (FWB-003-075)

At two hundred and forty miles every twelve hours, by travelling day and night, it would take only seven days to cross Africa. (FWB-008-022) When at last the "Victoria" rescues a stranded missionary, Ferguson explains the whole venture in a sentence: they are English travellers trying to cross Africa in a balloon. (FWB-022-045)

## Geography & Features

The travellers pass over one of the most unhealthy regions in Africa (FWB-013-007), yet it often happens there that some of the unhealthiest districts lie close beside others perfectly salubrious. (FWB-013-009) During the equatorial storms it is not rare to count from thirty to thirty-five flashes of lightning per minute. (FWB-016-065)

Geographers had pretended that in the centre of Africa there existed a vast depression, an immense central lake. (FWB-019-023) Kazeh, an important point in Central Africa, is not a city, for in truth there are no cities in the interior. (FWB-015-002) The surrounding Unyamwezy is the country of the Moon, above all the fertile and magnificent garden-spot of Africa. (FWB-015-003)

The elephants of Central Africa are reckoned the finest in the world (FWB-017-068), but toward the north the desert gradually expands, until not another village — not even a few huts — is to be seen. (FWB-024-012)
