# Faruskiar

## Overview

Aboard the Grand Transasiatic train he is first pointed out to the narrator as a commanding presence — "This leading star, I soon learned from Popof, bore the name of Faruskiar" (ASC-008-082).

Bombarnac covets an interview with "his superb lordship Faruskiar," the most magnificent of his fellow travellers (ASC-009-006).

At a frontier station his true station is revealed when a Chinese functionary bows to him: "General Manager, that is what he is, this lord Faruskiar!" (ASC-016-042).

## Role in the Story

His whole splendour is a mask; the narrator later sees him for "this abominable bandit hidden beneath the skin of a manager" (ASC-025-007).

His scheme was cunningly laid — "Faruskiar's scheme--in the execution of which he had executed his rival Ki-Tsang--had been cleverly contrived in utilizing this branch line leading to the unfinished viaduct" (ASC-025-009).

He diverts the train toward disaster to steal the imperial gold: "the rascal who sent us on to the Nanking line, who would have hurled us into the Tjon valley, to walk off with the imperial treasure, is Faruskiar" (ASC-025-044).

Unmasked at last, he stands condemned as "the most consummate bandit who had ever disgraced Central Asia" (ASC-025-052).

## Character & Traits

He earns a false glory during the bandit attack, when "Faruskiar, pistol in one hand, kandijar in the other, has rushed onto them, his eyes gleaming, his lips covered with a slight foam" (ASC-020-029).

His valour dazzles the narrator, who watches him display "extraordinary intrepidity, bearing himself the boldest in the struggle, and when he had exhausted his revolver, using his kandijar like a man who had often faced death" (ASC-020-036).

Yet his defence of the treasure is itself a ruse, for he cut down Ki-Tsang only "to keep it from the chief of the bandits who stopped the train, whose attack would have interfered with his criminal projects" (ASC-024-063).

## Relationships

- [[Major Noltitz]] — the one traveller whose steady suspicion never lets Faruskiar out of sight, keeping "his eye on his lordship Faruskiar" throughout the journey (ASC-013-003).
- Ghangir — his constant confederate, forever at his side "talking together in a low tone" as they plot (ASC-009-013).
- [[Claudius Bombarnac]] — the reporter who lionizes him as a hero before learning the truth of his crimes.

## Appearances

- [[The Adventures of a Special Correspondent]] — as the grandee-turned-bandit whom Bombarnac ironically telegraphs to Europe, prompting the paper's "compliments and respects to the heroic Seigneur Faruskiar" (ASC-027-064).
