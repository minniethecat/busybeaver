# Joam Garral

## Overview

He is master of a great fazenda on the Upper Amazon, for after "the arrival of Joam Garral at the farm at Iquitos the fazenda had become one of the richest establishments on the Upper Amazon" (EHLA-003-018).

He had come to that refuge unencumbered by any known history, since "Brazilian by birth, Joam Garral was without family or fortune" (EHLA-003-013).

His prosperity conceals a second self, for the blackmailer Torres confronts him as "the man who was sentenced to death in this affair of the robbery and murder!" (EHLA-020-005).

Under that name he is a condemned convict who dares not go home "as the fazender, Joam Garral, but as the convict, Joam Dacosta, continually in fear of his extradition" (EHLA-036-019).

## Role in the Story

He undertakes the long descent of the Amazon in secret purpose, "canceling the hateful proceedings of which he had been the victim, so as not to leave on his daughter and son-in-law the weight of the terrible situation" (EHLA-020-101).

He tells the judge plainly that his real motive "was the resolution I had taken to give myself up to the justice of my country" (EHLA-024-044).

His hidden past is turned against him by the villain, who "threatened to denounce Joam Garral as being Joam Dacosta, if he declined to purchase his silence" (EHLA-022-034).

He is taken at last by the police, told "you have also been Joam Dacosta; both names have been borne by the same man--I arrest you!" (EHLA-020-108).

His ordeal ends in vindication, when a deciphered document is read out that "proclaimed so absolutely the innocence of the fazender of Iquitos, and which snatched from the gallows this victim of a frightful judicial mistake" (EHLA-039-013).

## Character & Traits

From the first he strikes others as trustworthy: "The proverbial honesty of the Brazilian merchants and fazenders showed itself in his features, of which straightforwardness was the leading characteristic" (EHLA-003-035).

Confronted with a man who knows of his death sentence, he never wavers: "He had before him a man convicted of the most heinous of crimes, and the man never flinched" (EHLA-020-020).

Offered escape from prison, he refuses on principle, declaring "Joam Dacosta, innocent, will not fly!" (EHLA-037-039).

His composure springs from conviction and faith: "His indomitable energy was due not only to the feeling of his innocence, but to his faith in that God" (EHLA-037-002).

## Relationships

- Yaquita, his wife, remains steadfast under either name; whether "Yaquita Garral or Yaquita Dacosta, he would still find her the devoted wife and brave companion he had ever known her to be" (EHLA-026-003).

- His children are Benito and Minha, who as "the grandchildren of the old Portuguese, became worthy of their grandfather, children worthy of Joam and Yaquita" (EHLA-003-029).

- Manoel Valdez, betrothed to Minha, is embraced as kin, for "Joam would be glad to welcome to his arms the new son whose sterling qualities he recognized and appreciated" (EHLA-004-014).

- Torres, the blackmailer who holds the proof of his innocence, he denounces to his face as "a consummate scoundrel" (EHLA-020-053), meeting the extortion with "the indignant refusal of the father to hand over his daughter to save his honor and his life" (EHLA-037-012).
