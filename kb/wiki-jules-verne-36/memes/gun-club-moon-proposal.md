# Barbicane's Moon Proposal

## Overview

Barbicane's Moon Proposal is the assembly scene that sets *[[from-the-earth-to-the-moon|From the Earth to the Moon]]* in motion. Before a packed hall, all eager to learn the important communication of [[barbicane|President Barbicane]] (FEM-002-002), the [[gun-club|Gun Club]]'s president unveils a scheme to turn idle artillery toward the sky.

## What Happens

As the clock strikes eight, [[barbicane|Barbicane]], as if moved by a spring, jumps up, and a general silence ensues (FEM-002-014). He reminds his colleagues that he had asked himself whether they could "undertake some grand experiment worthy of the nineteenth century" (FEM-002-020), now that peace has left their cannons idle.

He then turns their eyes upward, observing that all the sciences can teach is known about the moon, "but until now no direct communication with it has ever been established" (FEM-002-028). The remedy he proposes is audacious: to cast a cannon vast enough to send a projectile to the Moon itself, and the hall erupts in acclamation.

## Significance

The proposal is the founding act of the entire enterprise: the casting of the [[columbiad|Columbiad]], the manned projectile, and the launch all descend from this single meeting. That [[barbicane|Barbicane]] framed it as a grand experiment worthy of the nineteenth century (FEM-002-020) marks the venture as a triumph of calculation and ambition, and his complaint that no direct communication with the moon had ever been established (FEM-002-028) becomes the problem the whole novel exists to solve.

## Participants & Setting

- Proposed by [[Barbicane]] to the assembled [[Gun Club]].
- Leads to the casting of the [[Columbiad]] and the [[Lunar Projectile]].
- Chronicled in [[From the Earth to the Moon]].
