# Barrow Strait

## Overview

Barrow Strait is the real Arctic passage leading on from Lancaster Strait toward Beechey Island, a route worn by generations of searchers. The plan is plain: "we ought to ascend that strait as far as Barrow Strait, and from there sail to Beechey Island; the same track has been gone over a hundred times." (ACH-013-036)

It forms one arm of the great Arctic crossroads where the *Forward* takes her bearings.

## In the Narrative

The brig follows Franklin's own path: "he passed through Lancaster and Barrow Straits, and arrived at Beechey Island, where he passed the winter of 1845 and '46." (ACH-017-005) The *Forward* does likewise, opening "a passage amongst the ice to get to Beechey Island through Barrow Strait." (ACH-020-002)

The strait is fickle with ice — "sometimes Barrow Strait was closed to one of them, and the next year another found it open." (ACH-014-012) When the ship is beset, it is still the way home: "happily we are near the entrance to Barrow Strait, which will take us back to Baffin's Sea!" (ACH-020-051)

## Geography & Features

The strait is one outlet of the four-way meeting of Arctic seas — "fenced by the outlets of Lancaster Strait, Barrow Strait, Wellington Channel, and Regent's Passage." (ACH-014-010)

Its northern shores were charted in the great searches: McClure "explored the northern coasts of Barrow Strait, but without result." (ACH-014-023)
