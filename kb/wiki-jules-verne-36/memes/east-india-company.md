## Overview

The East India Company is the celebrated English trading corporation that governed British India for a century, "all-powerful from 1756, when the English first gained a foothold on the spot where now stands the city of Madras, down to the time of the great Sepoy insurrection." (AWED-010-003)

By the time [[Phileas Fogg]] crosses the peninsula in *Around the World in Eighty Days*, the Company has passed away, leaving the British possessions in India directly under the control of the Crown. (AWED-010-003)

## Role in the Story

The Company is the historical backdrop to Fogg's Indian journey. Aboard the *Mongolia* travel officials and military officers, some attached to the regular British forces, others commanding the Sepoy troops once raised in the Company's service. (AWED-009-002)

Its legacy shapes the India Fogg hurries through: where once one travelled only by palanquin or coach, a great railway now runs from Bombay to Calcutta across the peninsula it had opened. (AWED-010-004) Among the peoples it left behind are the thrifty Parsees, "the richest native merchants of Bombay." (AWED-010-018)

The Company's reach extended to exploration as well as trade. In *The Mysterious Island*'s account of the search for La Perouse, Dillon returned to Calcutta and "was able to interest the Asiatic Society and the East India Company in his discovery," which fitted out a ship for him. (TTLU-019-045)

## Members

The Company acts through its officials and its officers, the administrators and the commanders of Sepoy regiments who staff British India. No single director is named; it is present as an institution whose servants fill the ships and garrisons that [[Phileas Fogg]] passes on his way around the world.
