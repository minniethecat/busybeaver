# oxyhydric gas

## Overview

Oxyhydric gas is the illuminating gas by which [[Doctor Ox]] proposes to light the sleepy Flemish town of Quiquendone—ostensibly a civic improvement, in truth the cover for a physiological experiment. When the counsellor gropes for its name, the doctor's assistant supplies it: "Oxyhydric gas." (DOSE-003-041)

Promised as the gas that would make Quiquendone "the first town in Flanders to be lighted with" it, the scheme was to be carried entirely at the doctor's own expense. (DOSE-003-040, DOSE-003-039)

## Design & Operation

Rather than the ordinary carburetted hydrogen distilled from coal, the town was to be lit "by the use of a more modern and twenty-fold more brilliant gas, oxyhydric gas, produced by mixing hydrogen and oxygen." (DOSE-004-009)

Doctor Ox obtained the two gases not by costly reagents but "by the direct decomposition of slightly acidulated water, by means of a battery made of new elements, invented by himself." (DOSE-004-010)

An electric current sent through large basins of water split it into its constituents: the oxygen passed off at one end, the hydrogen—"of double the volume of its late associate"—at the other, each led away through separate mains laid beneath the streets. (DOSE-004-010, DOSE-005-026)

## Role in the Story

The doctor "took little account" of the promised lighting; his true design lay elsewhere. (DOSE-004-011) Secretly he saturated first the public buildings, then the houses, and finally the streets of Quiquendone "with pure oxygen, without letting in the least atom of hydrogen." (DOSE-017-002)

The effect was a transformation: "One who lives in an air saturated with oxygen grows excited, frantic, burns!" (DOSE-017-003) The phlegmatic townsfolk fell into passions, quarrels, and warlike fervor—while those who climbed above the heavy oxygen-laden lower air, like the pair atop the belfry, "were themselves again." (DOSE-017-004)

The experiment ended by accident: a communication opened between the oxygen and hydrogen reservoirs, an explosive mixture formed, and a "formidable explosion" abolished the gas-works. (DOSE-015-006, DOSE-015-002) At once Quiquendone "became the peaceable, phlegmatic, and Flemish town it formerly was." (DOSE-016-002)

## Science & Speculation

Oxyhydric gas is the engine of Verne's satirical thought-experiment on temperament and chemistry. The "providential explosion" that ended the "dangerous experiment" leaves the doctor's thesis untested but pointed. (DOSE-017-006)

Verne frames it as an open scientific question: "Are virtue, courage, talent, wit, imagination,—are all these qualities or faculties only a question of oxygen?" (DOSE-017-007) The tale plays real electrolysis and the physiology of oxygen for comedy, hinting—without quite asserting—that human character might be no more than a matter of the air one breathes.
