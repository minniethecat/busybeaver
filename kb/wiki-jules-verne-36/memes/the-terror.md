# The Terror

## Overview

The Terror is the machine built and piloted by [[Robur]] in *[[the-master-of-the-world|Master of the World]]*, a single craft that can move as automobile, boat, submarine, and aircraft. Observers puzzle over "how could an automobile become a boat, and yet more, a submarine?" (MW-008-003).

Its narrator-captive Strock finally examines it at rest: "The machine was as I have said spindle-shaped... The body was of aluminium, the wings of a substance whose nature I could not determine. The body rested on four wheels" (MW-015-026).

## Design & Operation

The Terror combines wheels, screws, and wings in one hull: examination shows "the machine used wheels and turbine screws and wings," though its engine remained a mystery (MW-015-030). In the air "the chief aerial support... was that of the great wings," so that "the theory of the 'heavier than air' flying machine was employed by the inventor" (MW-015-028).

Its motive force is electrical: "the power by which the machine was driven, was neither steam nor gasoline, nor any of those similar liquids so well known by their odor" (MW-013-027).

## Role in the Story

On land the Terror outruns everything: racers judged "this amazing vehicle was going at least one hundred and thirty miles an hour" (MW-004-044). On water Strock finds "the 'Terror' was moving upon some broad liquid surface" (MW-013-007), and beneath it the craft submerges — "we were moving peacefully forward through the ocean's depths" (MW-017-051).

In flight "those powerful wings... beat upon either side at the same time that the screws spun" beneath (MW-017-042). [[robur|Robur]] claims total dominion through his invention: "With it, I hold control of the entire world" (MW-009-009).

## Science & Speculation

Verne treats the Terror as the convergence of the era's competing vehicles rather than pure fantasy; Strock even notes "a sort of fantastic resemblance between the general appearance of this boat and this automobile" (MW-005-055). The story ends by punishing [[robur|Robur]]'s hubris: challenging a storm, "with her wings fallen, her screws broken," the Terror "fell from the height of more than a thousand feet into the ocean beneath" (MW-017-069).
