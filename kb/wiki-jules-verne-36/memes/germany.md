## Overview

Germany is the Central European homeland of Professor Lidenbrock in *A Journey to the Center of the Earth* — a land of learned men, for Germany has not a few professors of this sort. (JCE-001-019) Axel's longing fixes on it from the depths of the earth: had the tempest sent the raft a little more east, they should have passed under Germany, under his beloved town of Hamburg. (JCE-036-010)

## In the Narrative

The professor's discoveries ripple outward — the noise of this discovery was very great, not in France alone, but in England and in Germany. (JCE-038-004) Yet the land remains a fixed point of reference: when the compass seems to fail, Lidenbrock infers that we are not in Germany. (JCE-044-042)

Beyond the underground novel, Germany recurs as a byword for craft and science. Master Zacharius's works were valued in all France and Germany (MZ-001-007), and such serious, practical countries as England, America, and Germany were deeply concerned by the great sea-monster. (TTLU-001-012)

## Geography & Features

It is a confederation of cities famed for skilled work — the old clockmaker commanded high prices in the cities of France, Switzerland, and Germany. (MZ-003-006) It is a cradle of aeronauts: the narrator's passage through the principal cities of Germany had been brilliantly marked by aerostatic ascensions. (VB-001-004)

Even among the comet's scattered survivors, Germany may be said to have a representative. (OC-019-065)
