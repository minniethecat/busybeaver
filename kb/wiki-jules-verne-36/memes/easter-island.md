# Easter Island

## Overview

Easter Island, called by Verne the Isle of Paques, is the remote Pacific landfall that gives the novice captain Dick Sand his one reliable navigational fix during the *Pilgrim*'s storm-driven voyage. Sighting it, he reasons: "It can only be the Isle of Paques; there is no other in these parts." (DSCF-012-083)

Verne supplies its history: "The Isle of Paques--by its true name Vai-Hon--discovered by David in 1686, visited by Cook and Laperouse, is situated 27° south latitude." (DSCF-012-093)

## In the Narrative

The island is the pivot of Dick Sand's dead reckoning. "Mrs. Weldon looked attentively at the Isle of Paques, which only formed an imperceptible point on the chart." (DSCF-012-086)

The young captain treats it as providential: "now that, thanks to the appearance of the Isle of Paques, he knew exactly his position, he had reason to believe" the storm might abate. (DSCF-012-096) From its bearing he plots the run east: "here is the Isle of Paques, that we have left behind in the west... I conclude that we shall reach land in the east." (DSCF-013-047)

## Geography & Features

The island's very isolation makes it a treacherous reference. Sand knows "the 'Pilgrim' had been thrown to the northeast... even by the bearings of the Isle of Paques." (DSCF-014-005)

The fix proves fatally misleading, for the land finally reached "was not the Isle of Paques, whose bearing the novice had taken at sea, but some other island situated exactly to the west of this continent." (DSCF-018-107) The error echoes an older one: sailors "believed they had made the Isle of Paques, when they passed in sight of Tristan d'Acunha." (DSCF-020-062)
