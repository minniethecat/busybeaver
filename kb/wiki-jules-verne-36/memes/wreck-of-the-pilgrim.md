# The Wreck of the Pilgrim

## Overview

The wreck of the Pilgrim is the turning point of *[[Dick Sand: A Captain at Fifteen]]*, in which the boy-captain, his compass secretly sabotaged, drives his storm-battered schooner ashore on a coast he believes to be South America but which is in truth [[Angola]].

After a voyage of more than seventy days the vessel was cast up on a continent (DSCF-014-002); the Pilgrim herself was lost, a valueless carcass whose débris the surf would scatter within hours, yet all aboard reached land safe and sound (DSCF-014-004).

## What Happens

The disaster was sown in secret. During the hurricane the cook Negoro contrived to fall against the binnacle and slip a piece of iron beneath it, then rose and hid the metal before Dick Sand could see (DSCF-012-013).

Once the iron was taken away the needle returned to its true position, but the young novice never knew that his compass had been falsified, and steered his ship far from her reckoning (DSCF-012-019).

At last, on the 6th of April, the long-awaited cry rang out from the bars: "Land before us!" (DSCF-013-074). But before the schooner lay a long band of reefs on which the sea foamed all white (DSCF-013-085).

Seeing no harbour, Dick Sand made his resolve. "We must run aground!" he told Mrs. Weldon; "I am forced to lose her to save you!" (DSCF-013-095).

He poured whale-oil on the surf to smooth it—"Pour the oil—pour!" (DSCF-013-109)—and steered for a channel. Then a shock came: lifted by a formidable wave, the Pilgrim stranded and her masting fell without wounding anyone (DSCF-013-112).

Her hull, staved by the collision, was invaded by the water with extreme violence (DSCF-013-113), and within ten minutes all who had sailed in her had landed at the foot of the cliff (DSCF-013-114).

## Significance

The survivors could not agree what part of the American coast had received them; Dick Sand supposed it to be the shore of Peru, reasoning from the bearings of the Isle of Paques (DSCF-014-005). In fact the falsified compass had carried them to Africa.

Ominously, the coast was deserted—no habitation, hut, or smoke, no mark that human beings ever came there—and the novice remained greatly surprised (DSCF-014-015).

More ominous still, Negoro surveyed the strand and cliff like a man recalling old recollections, as though he knew this country (DSCF-014-022). His silent recognition foreshadows the betrayal that will deliver the castaways to the slave-traders of the interior.

## Participants & Setting

- Dick Sand, the fifteen-year-old novice raised to captain, brings the doomed schooner through the reefs and lands every soul alive, unaware that his own instrument had betrayed him.

- Negoro, the ship's cook and hidden villain, sabotages the compass to wreck the Pilgrim on the coast he secretly knows; with the stranding his service ended and he was now free to go where he pleased (DSCF-014-021).

- Mrs. Weldon and her son Jack, old Tom and his black companions, and the entomologist Cousin Benedict make up the shipwrecked party, saved by Dick Sand's seamanship.

- The stranding place was a narrow beach shut off by a cliff, with the mouth of a little river a quarter of a mile to the north (DSCF-014-008)—a lonely shore that the travellers would soon learn was no part of the Americas at all.
