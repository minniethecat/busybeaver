## Overview

*A Drama in the Air* is Jules Verne's early short story of aeronautics, narrated by a professional balloonist who "arrived at Frankfort-on-the-Maine" to make an ascent during the great September fair (DA-001-001).

Though three citizens had begged to accompany him, at the last moment their courage failed, and he "did not hesitate to ascend alone," replacing their weight with ballast (DA-001-009).

## Plot Summary

As the balloon lifts off, the narrator is thrown down, and rising he finds himself "face to face with an unexpected fellow-voyager,--the pale young man" who had watched his ascents (DA-001-016). The intruder coolly announces he comes "by the right which the impossibility of your getting rid of me confers" (DA-001-020).

At once the stranger begins casting out ballast, for where the narrator wishes to descend, he insists, "Let us begin by first ascending" (DA-001-039). Asked his name, he answers only, "My name is Erostratus or Empedocles, whichever you choose!" (DA-001-072).

As they climb, the stranger pours out a long history of ballooning and its martyrs — Pilâtre des Rosiers, the drowning peril of Blanchard and Jeffries over the Channel, and the fiery death of "Madame Blanchard," who "was killed!" when her fireworks ignited the escaping gas (DA-001-118, DA-001-157).

Recounting disaster after disaster, from Zambecarri to a whole "necrology" of aeronauts who "fell, and was killed," the man grows ever wilder, until the narrator at last "recognized the horrible truth. I was in the presence of a madman!" (DA-001-155, DA-001-159).

The lunatic throws out the last ballast so that "we must have now reached a height of at least nine thousand yards," and blood spurts from the narrator's nose and mouth (DA-001-160). Glorying that "the higher we go, the more glorious will be our death," he moves to cut the car loose to "approach the sun" (DA-001-197, DA-001-203).

In a desperate struggle the narrator "violently repulsed the madman," who "disappeared in space" (DA-001-208, DA-001-211). The over-dilated balloon then bursts, but he clings to the netting and is carried down, saved at the last by the anchor cord catching in a crevice (DA-001-213, DA-001-216).

He wakes "in bed in a peasant's cottage, at Harderwick," his life spared by a miracle, and closes with the hope that this "terrible narrative... not discourage the explorers of the air" (DA-001-217, DA-001-219).

## Main Characters

- The narrator — a professional balloonist who ascends from Frankfort and survives the ordeal
- The madman — the pale intruder, self-named "Erostratus or Empedocles," who seeks a glorious death aloft

## Key Places & Technology

- Frankfort-on-the-Maine — the city from whose Place de la Comédie the balloon ascends
- The balloon — the silk, gutta-percha aerostat whose ballast the madman squanders to soar ever higher

## Themes

Verne turns a lecture on aerostation into a study of monomania, letting the madman's catalogue of ballooning martyrs sound the story's obsession with death aloft. Against his cry that the "martyrs of science" are "canonized by posterity," the narrator's survival affirms prudence over the fatal romance of the infinite (DA-001-161).
