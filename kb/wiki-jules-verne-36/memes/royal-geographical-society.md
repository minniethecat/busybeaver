# Royal Geographical Society

## Overview

The Royal Geographical Society of London is the learned body that sponsors, records, and rewards the great voyages of exploration in Verne's world. Its sessions are grand public occasions: "There was a large audience assembled on the 14th of January, 1862, at the session of the Royal Geographical Society, No. 3 Waterloo Place, London" (FWB-001-002).

The Society is the arbiter of geographical fame. It maintains international sister bodies — Dr. Ferguson could not "claim membership in either of the Royal Geographical Societies of London, Paris, Berlin, Vienna, or St." Petersburg (FWB-001-035) — and its proceedings are the record of record, with speeches moved for "insertion... in 'The Proceedings of the Royal Geographic'" society (FWB-001-025).

## Role in the Story

In *Five Weeks in a Balloon*, the Society is the institution that launches the adventure. Dr. Ferguson receives his applause "in the Royal Society" (FWB-001-041), and "the proposal for this scientific undertaking was officially made... at the rooms of the Royal Geographical Society, and the sum of twenty-five hundred pounds was voted to defray the expenses of the enterprise" (FWB-002-007).

The Society sees the travellers off and welcomes them home. "On the 20th, a grand farewell dinner was given to Dr. Ferguson and Kennedy by the Royal Geographical Society" (FWB-008-009).

On their return it draws up a formal "certificate... in the terms in which it appears to-day, in the archives of the Royal Geographical Society of London" (FWB-044-006), and at "a public meeting of the Royal Geographical Society" Ferguson recounts his aerial journey and wins "the golden medal set apart to reward the most r" remarkable exploration (FWB-044-020).

In *The Adventures of Captain Hatteras*, the Society plays the same authenticating role for the polar expedition. Clawbonny drafts a document "a copy of which is now among the archives of the Royal Geographical Society of London" (ACH-055-055).

Later he makes it "his first care... to request the Royal Geographical Society to receive a communication from him" (ACH-058-018). The narrative of the voyage is "published the next year under the auspices of the Royal Geographical Society" (ACH-058-025).

## Members

The Society's world is peopled by its officers, its explorer-heroes, and the honorary fellows of its sister academies.

- Sir Francis M---- — whose address is "the finest oratorical success that the Royal Geographical Society of London had yet achieved" (FWB-001-009)

- [[Dr. Ferguson]] — the balloonist whose expedition the Society funds and honours (FWB-001-041)

- [[Kennedy]] — Ferguson's companion, feted at the Society's farewell dinner (FWB-008-009)

- [[Dr. Clawbonny]] — chronicler of the Hatteras expedition, who lays his record before the Society (ACH-055-055)

A "gouty old admiral" among the fellows is "completely won over" by Ferguson and moves to publish his speech (FWB-001-025). Related bodies appear too — the Royal Society of Edinburgh, of which one speaker is "an honorary member" (ACH-005-045), and the Geographical Society of Paris, which awards its "annual prize medal" to Burton and Speke (FWB-004-029).

## Activities

The Society's chief activities are funding expeditions, publishing their results, and conferring honours. It votes money for undertakings (FWB-002-007) and dispatches explorers itself: "in 1857, Lieutenants Burton and Speke... were sent by the London Geographical Society to explore the great African lakes" (FWB-004-026).

It regards "as very important the exploration of this lake of which Speke caught a glimpse" (FWB-005-055) and provides its members with "the excellent charts published in the 'Bulletins of the Geographical Society of London'" (FWB-012-069).

Above all it keeps the archive of discovery and awards its medals. It decreed a prize "of the Geographical Society in 1828" (FWB-038-045), and both Ferguson's and Hatteras's narratives pass into its archives, giving the Society the final word on who has earned the fame of exploration.
