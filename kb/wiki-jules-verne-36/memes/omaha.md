## Overview

Omaha is the eastern anchor of the Pacific Railroad and a decisive stage in Phileas Fogg's dash across America. President Lincoln himself fixed the end of the line at Omaha, in Nebraska. (AWED-026-005) From here an uninterrupted metal ribbon runs west to Sacramento and the sea.

## In the Narrative

The town names the great trunk line. The Pacific Railroad is divided into two distinct lines: the Central Pacific, between San Francisco and Ogden, and the Union Pacific, between Ogden and Omaha. (AWED-026-002) Its route is exact — on leaving Omaha, it passes along the left bank of the Platte River as far as the junction of its northern branch. (AWED-026-006)

The plains beyond it are perilous. Between Omaha and the Pacific the railway crosses a territory which is still infested by Indians and wild beasts. (AWED-026-003) When the Sioux storm the train, the engine driver judges it prudent to continue on to Omaha rather than turn back into the danger. (AWED-030-038)

## Geography & Features

Set on the Missouri in Nebraska, Omaha is where five main lines from New York converge and the transcontinental crossing truly begins — the point from which the road climbs the prairies a mile and a half a day toward the Rockies.
