## Overview

The Royal Institution of Edinburgh is the learned society to which the mining engineer [[James Starr]] belongs, a body of scientists and colleagues who deliberate on the questions of the day and correspond through its President.

Starr is counted among its most active members, and the *Edinburgh Review* frequently published clever articles over his signature. (UC-001-006)

## Role in the Story

The Institution frames the mystery of *The Underground City*. When Starr receives the summons that draws him back to the Aberfoyle mines, he writes to Sir W. Elphiston, President of the Royal Institution, excusing himself from being unable to attend the next meeting. (UC-002-006)

The President duly communicates to his colleagues the letter Starr had sent, explaining the engineer's absence. (UC-009-002) When Starr then vanishes underground, it is to the President of the Royal Institution in Edinburgh that any information concerning him is requested to be sent. (UC-009-040)

Elphiston, not only a colleague but a personal friend of Starr's, is informed and asked to direct the search made for the missing engineer. (UC-009-058)

## Members

Its membership is drawn from the scientific men of Scotland. The engineer [[James Starr]] is its most prominent figure in the story, while Sir William Elphiston serves as its President, the officer through whom the Institution's business and its search for Starr are conducted.
