# The Rescue of the Waif of the Cynthia

## Overview

The Rescue of the Waif of the Cynthia is the finding of an infant adrift in a cradle off the Norwegian coast, the foundling event that gives *[[the-waif-of-the-cynthia|The Waif of the Cynthia]]* its title and sets the boy Erik on the search for his true parents.

The child's origin lies in disaster. The doctor learns from Malarius that the boy was not the fisherman's son, but had been cast on their shore by a shipwreck and taken in (WC-002-063).

## What Happens

The fisherman confirms the truth. Asked directly whether the child was his own, Hersebom answered gravely that the boy was not their son by birth (WC-002-064).

He recounts the rescue itself. Fishing near the island at the entrance of the fiord, he rowed out to a white object and found a little wicker cradle, enveloped in a woollen cloth and strongly tied to a buoy, cradling a sleeping infant (WC-002-068).

Only a wreck could explain it, and one clue remained. As the doctor presses, the cradle was attached to a buoy, and it is customary on all vessels to write on the buoy the name of the ship to which it belongs (WC-002-087).

## Significance

The lost ship is at last named. Pressed to remember the articles saved with the child, the family recall the name inscribed on the buoy: 'Cynthia' (WC-002-091).

The rescue defines the family. Though not their son by birth, Katrina holds that the boy is theirs in love and affection, counted with Otto and Vanda among their children (WC-002-065); the poor little one is their child now, brought up with no difference made (WC-002-069).

Years later the meaning lands on Erik. As his father describes the cradle abandoned to the mercy of the waves, rescued by the hardy fisherman and carried to his wife, the young man vividly realises his own history (WC-006-013).

## Participants & Setting

- The infant later named Erik, found adrift in a wicker cradle tied to a buoy off the fiord near Noroe.
- Rescued by the fisherman Hersebom and raised by him and his wife Katrina alongside their children Otto and Vanda.
- The buoy's inscription 'Cynthia' the sole clue to the lost ship; chronicled in *[[the-waif-of-the-cynthia|The Waif of the Cynthia]]*.
