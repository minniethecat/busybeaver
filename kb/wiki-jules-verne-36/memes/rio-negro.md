## Overview

The Rio Negro is the most remarkable of all the tributaries of the Amazon, the great black-water river that joins the main stream at Manaos in Eight Hundred Leagues on the Amazon. (EHLA-018-020) From the frontier of Brazil down to Manaos, where the superb Rio Negro joins it, the Amazon bears the name of the Solimaës. (EHLA-005-023)

The Rio Negro, discovered by the Spaniard Favella in 1645, rises in the very heart of the province of Popayan, on the flanks of the mountains that separate Brazil from New Grenada, and it communicates with the Orinoco. (EHLA-021-003)

## In the Narrative

The river marks a turning point in the voyage of the jangada. Twenty-four hours more and the raft would be off the mouth of the Rio Negro, before the capital of the province of Amazones. (EHLA-018-039) The crew had to work the raft slantingly across the current of the Amazon, here doubled in force by that of the Rio Negro, to make for the embouchure of the tributary. (EHLA-022-053)

The raft was then in the black waters of the Rio Negro, near a high bluff covered with cecropias and palisaded with stiff-stalked reeds. (EHLA-022-057) From there all three landed on the bank of the Rio Negro and started for the town. (EHLA-026-017)

## Geography & Features

It is on the left bank of the Rio Negro that Manaos, the capital of the province, towers above the water. (EHLA-021-002) The town lies some four hundred and twenty leagues from Belem and about ten miles from the embouchure of the Rio Negro. (EHLA-021-001)

The jangada crossed a little above the mouth of the Rio Negro, into the current that carried it to the vast bay opening on the left side of the stream. (EHLA-022-055) On the banks of the Rio Negro there remained only a few half-breeds, Portuguese and natives, where a few years before twenty-four different nations had their homes. (EHLA-005-045)

## Connections

The river gave its name to the settlement at its mouth: Manaos was formerly called Moura, or Barra de Rio Negro. (EHLA-021-006) In the sixteenth century Orellana descended the Rio Negro and arrived on the main river in 1540, venturing without a guide across the unknown district. (EHLA-005-034)

In the age of steam the Rio Negro joined the network of navigable arteries, as steamboats ascended by way of the Tapajoz, the Madeira, the Rio Negro, and the Purus. (EHLA-005-041)
