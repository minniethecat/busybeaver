## Overview

The Arctic Ocean is the northern polar sea whose shores anchor the enterprise of The Fur Country and recur through the polar voyages of The Adventures of Captain Hatteras. When the designation was questioned, one traveller answered that it is called the Arctic Ocean, and that it would be very inconvenient to alter its name. (ACH-039-034)

The Hudson's Bay Company's whole design turned on it: the flight of the fur-bearing animals beyond the polar circle explained the Company's motive in founding a factory on the borders of the Arctic Ocean. (FC-002-044) The question was openly asked what induced the Company to construct a fort on its shores. (FC-002-011)

## In the Narrative

Reaching the ocean was the object of the march. Every one shared the Lieutenant's impatience and resisted fatigue in order to reach the Arctic Ocean with the least possible delay. (FC-010-034) As the party advanced, the Coppermine valley widened sensibly as they neared the Arctic Ocean. (FC-010-031)

For seasoned sailors it was familiar and dreaded ground. Johnson was an old stager in the Arctic Ocean, with nothing to learn in audacity or coolness. (ACH-005-041) Yet even reaching it raised a question: supposing they found it free from ice and easy to navigate, what should they do without a ship? (ACH-046-047)

## Geography & Features

Fort Hope was set high on its rim. The Company had advised Hobson to keep as much as possible above the seventieth parallel and on the shores of the Arctic Ocean. (FC-011-004) Into it drained the great rivers: the Mackenzie and Coppermine flow from south to north and empty themselves into the Arctic Ocean. (FC-007-004)

Its tides were a matter of dispute. The shore west of Cape Bathurst rises but a few inches above the sea, and the tides were said to be very high in the Arctic Ocean, as reported by Parry, Franklin, and the two Rosses. (FC-015-004)

## Connections

The ocean's shores framed the fate of Hobson's colony. Connected with the mainland for ages, the earthquake of the 8th of January dragged the peninsula from its moorings, and it was carried along the Arctic Ocean as a floating island. (FC-024-002)

Its coasts drew hunters and rivals alike. Russian traders travelled all along the coasts of New Cornwall as far as the Arctic Ocean in pursuit of sea-otter skins. (FC-008-022) Hobson sought information about the districts bordering the ocean and bounded by Cape Bathurst. (FC-008-012)
