# Nadia

## Overview

Nadia is a young Livonian girl — "from Riga; she was Livonian, consequently Russian" (MS-007-010) — travelling across a war-torn Siberia to reach her exiled father at Irkutsk. She first gives her name to her fellow traveller with a simple gesture: "'Nadia,' said she, holding out her hand" (MS-008-019).

## Role in the Story

To cross the Tartar lines together, Michael takes her under his false identity: "'Come, Nadia,' answered Michael, 'and make what use you like of your brother Nicholas Korpanoff'" (MS-008-020).

Warned that "there are physical fatigues a woman may be unable to endure" (MS-010-012), she presses on, earning his praise: "'You are a brave girl, Nadia,' replied Michael. 'God Himself would have led you'" (MS-010-045).

Separated and taken captive to Omsk, Nadia is thrown together with Michael's mother, and "rendered to the mother those attentions which she had herself received from the son" (MS-021-075).

After Michael is blinded, their roles reverse and she becomes his eyes: "Michael could now only see with Nadia's eyes" (MS-028-001), his steps kept true by "that trembling little hand which guided him" (MS-025-006).

## Character & Traits

Nadia's endurance is her hallmark: on the laborious road to Irkutsk "Nadia never complained" (MS-025-036), and in scarcity she gave Michael "the lion's share of this scanty meal" (MS-025-033).

Her sympathy is instinctive — it was "an instinctive sympathy" that "first drew Nadia towards" the old prisoner (MS-021-083), of whom she says, "'Brother, sister, mother--he has been all to me!'" (MS-021-094).

## Relationships

- [[Michel Strogoff]] — her companion and pretended brother, whose blind steps are led by "that trembling little hand which guided him" (MS-025-006).
- Marfa Strogoff — Michael's mother, whom Nadia tends in captivity (MS-021-075) and claims as her own: "'I am Marfa's daughter'" (MS-014-038).
