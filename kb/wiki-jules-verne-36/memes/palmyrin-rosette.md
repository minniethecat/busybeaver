# Palmyrin Rosette

## Overview

Palmyrin Rosette is the astronomer swept into space aboard the comet Gallia — Hector Servadac's own former schoolmaster, hailed at their reunion as "my old tutor, Mr. Rosette, in very flesh and blood" (OC-027-014). Servadac warns his companions that "although the professor was always eccentric, and at times very irascible, yet he was real[ly]" a man of worth (OC-027-020).

His prickliness is confirmed by his history: his name had been dropped from an official commission "apparently for no other reason than his personal unpopularity" (OC-029-018).

## Role in the Story

Rosette is the one mind that comprehends the wanderer they ride. Not content "with three positions," he "made ten, twenty, thirty observations" of the comet whenever the fog broke (OC-029-029), and from them proved "able to calculate the date at which the comet would reach its perihelion," overjoyed at his discovery (OC-029-037).

He treats Gallia as his personal possession: with equanimity restored he announces "the diameter, the surface, the volume of my comet are settled" (OC-030-076). His whole science is worked from a hole in the ice from which "projected the case of an astronomer's telescope; it was the opening of Palmyrin Rosette's observatory" (OC-031-036).

## Character & Traits

He is the caricature of the absorbed scholar — quick to fury and quicker to condescend. He cries out "with all the petulant impatience of the old pedagogue" (OC-030-033), and when baffled he "scratched his head in perplexity, glaring round upon his companions as if they were personally responsible for his annoyance" (OC-032-045).

His obsession makes him a hermit of the sky: "Rosette, enthusiast as he was, should be irremovable from his observatory" (OC-034-025), and through the calm Gallian nights "Palmyrin Rosette cared so little to quit his observatory" (OC-034-003).

Servadac, mindful of "Rosette's dislike to visitors" (OC-037-018), learns to leave him be — yet the professor's rapture is genuine, "rejoicing in an approach nearer to Jupiter than any other mortal" (OC-036-008).
