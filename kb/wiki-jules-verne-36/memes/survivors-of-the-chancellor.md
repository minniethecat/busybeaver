## Overview

*The Survivors of the Chancellor* is Jules Verne's grimmest sea story, told as the private journal of the passenger "J. R. Kazallon, of London" (SC2-002-006). It opens at Charleston on "SEPTEMBER 27th, 1869," as the ship leaves the Battery-quay under Captain Huntly (SC2-001-001).

The *Chancellor* is "a fine square-rigged three-master, of 900 tons burden," belonging to the wealthy Liverpool firm of Laird Brothers (SC2-001-003).

## Plot Summary

The vessel sails for Liverpool carrying 1700 bales of cotton (SC2-003-007). A smouldering fire is discovered deep in the cargo, and effective command passes from the failing Captain Huntly to the resolute mate, Robert Curtis (SC2-002-003, SC2-009-005).

Curtis tries to smother the flames by hermetically sealing the ship, but the fire spreads beyond control and the *Chancellor* must be abandoned (SC2-010-005, SC2-024-004). The survivors escape onto a hastily built raft (SC2-028-004).

Adrift for weeks, tormented by hunger and a thirst "more intolerable still," the castaways sink toward drawing lots for cannibalism (SC2-042-002, SC2-044-020). They are saved only when Kazallon finds "the water was fresh" — they have drifted to the mouth of the Amazon, whose current alone is "strong enough to freshen the ocean twenty miles from shore" (SC2-055-020, SC2-056-010).

## Main Characters

- J. R. Kazallon — the London passenger whose journal narrates the disaster
- Robert Curtis — the steadfast mate who takes command of the doomed ship
- Captain John Silas Huntly — the *Chancellor's* nominal commander, who loses his nerve

## Key Places & Technology

- The *Chancellor* — the 900-ton three-master that becomes a burning wreck
- The raft — the makeshift platform on which the survivors drift across the Atlantic

## Themes

Verne strips the romance from the sea and stares at human extremity: fire, shipwreck, thirst, and the slow descent toward drawing lots for human flesh. Against that darkness he sets the discipline of Curtis and the diarist's clear eye, then rescues his survivors by a stroke of geography — the freshwater plume of the Amazon reaching far out to sea.
