## Overview

The Giant Telescope is a colossal reflecting telescope commissioned by the [[Gun Club]] to track the flight of the [[Lunar Projectile]] after it was fired from the [[Columbiad]] toward the Moon. Erected atop Long's Peak in the Rocky Mountains, it was, in the world of *[[from-the-earth-to-the-moon|From the Earth to the Moon]]*, the most powerful astronomical instrument ever built. (FEM-024-022)(FEM-024-024)

## Design & Operation

Verne's instrument is a reflector, not a refractor: the Cambridge Observatory adopted "the system of Herschel" — a large concave mirror gathering light — because a refractor of the required aperture was impractical. (FEM-024-015)

The dimensions dwarf any real contemporary instrument:

> the tube of the new reflector was to be 280 feet long and its mirror 16 feet in diameter (FEM-024-016)

The great mirror was silvered by Foucault's deposition process, the same technique used to give glass mirrors their reflective coat. (FEM-024-014) Raising the tube into position on the mountain was itself a feat of engineering, overcome only with great difficulty. (FEM-024-023)(FEM-024-025)

Its cost and power were stated in round, staggering figures:

> cost more than 400,000 dollars ... magnified objects 48,000 times (FEM-024-024)

## Role in the Story

The telescope existed for a single purpose: to follow the projectile in flight and confirm whether it reached its destination. Stationed at Long's Peak (FEM-024-022), it became the eye through which the launch was verified, and in the sequel it is again from Long's Peak that observers strain to perceive the travellers. (FEM-028-002)(RM-007-048)

## Science & Speculation

Verne grounds the instrument in real optics — Léon Foucault's silvering process and the reflector tradition of William Herschel — and echoes Lord Rosse's "Leviathan of Parsonstown," a 72-inch reflector then the largest on Earth. His 280-foot tube and 16-foot mirror, magnifying 48,000 times, extrapolate far beyond any real telescope of the 1860s, trading engineering plausibility for the narrative need to see a body the size of a projectile against the lunar disc.
