# Back Cup

## Overview

Back Cup is a small, apparently extinct volcanic islet at the western extremity of the Bermuda archipelago, and the hidden stronghold of the pirate Ker Karraje in *Facing the Flag*. Seen from the sea its rounded dome resembles an overturned cup, which "fully justifies the name of Back Cup given to it." (FF-008-015)

Its true nature — a vast hollow cavern reached only from beneath the sea — makes it a perfect criminal refuge, invisible for what it is to any passing ship.

## In the Narrative

The engineer Simon Hart, held prisoner by the pirates, is carried into the islet aboard their submersible tug, which "penetrated to the interior of Back Cup mountain." (FF-008-076) There he makes a first inspection of the vast cavern and realizes the supposed volcano is nothing of the kind. (FF-009-002)

Hart pieces together the truth: "Back Cup is nothing but a lair of pirates, the Count d'Artigas is the leader of the band." (FF-009-040) The Count is in reality Ker Karraje, and Back Cup his refuge — "absolutely safe as it was from any possible chance of discovery." (FF-010-080)

The islet becomes the setting for the novel's climax. Ker Karraje seeks to arm himself with Thomas Roch's [[Fulgurator]], and Hart prays that "Back Cup is only destroyed before Ker Karraje has Roch's fulgurator at his disposal." (FF-012-009) In the end Roch turns the weapon upon the attackers and blows the lair apart.

## Geography & Features

Exteriorly the island is nearly circular, measuring some two hundred and fifty yards in circumference, and enclosing an interior superficies of about six acres. (FF-009-017) It lies at the western extremity of the archipelago, connected to it by a chain of small islets and reefs that cannot easily be approached. (FF-008-034)

Its defining secret lies underwater: exploring the sea-bottom near the Bermudas, the pirates' engineer Serko discovered at the base of the island the tunnel that led into the hollow interior of the mountain. (FF-010-080) Only the electric submersible tug could pass through this flooded passage, sealing the cavern-colony off from the outside world.

For fifty years honest fishing families had lived on the outer east coast in log-cabins and houses of stone, unaware of what the mountain concealed. (FF-008-027)
