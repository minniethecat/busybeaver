# The Recognition of Erik

## Overview

The recognition of Erik is the closing event of *[[The Waif of the Cynthia]]*, in which the foundling Erik—having completed the first circumpolar voyage yet failed to learn the secret of his birth—is at last identified through his published biography by Mr. Durrien of Paris, who writes to claim him as the grandson he had mourned for years (WC-020-032) (WC-021-009).

## What Happens

On the drifting ice-island, the sole man who held Erik's secret, Patrick O'Donoghan, died without revealing it (WC-020-002), and the villain Tudor Brown expired without a word (WC-020-003). Rescued at the cannon-shot of the *Alaska* (WC-020-007), the crew turned homeward.

On the 14th of September the *Alaska* cast anchor before Stockholm (WC-020-023); in seven months and four days the first circumpolar periplus had been accomplished by a navigator only twenty-two years of age (WC-020-024).

Uneasy at the praise heaped upon him, Erik stated frankly that his real motive had been to discover the secret of his birth and of the wreck of the *Cynthia*, and that he had failed (WC-020-032). To a reporter he related his whole story—how he had been picked up at sea tied to a buoy bearing the name *Cynthia* (WC-020-033).

The biographical details, translated into every language, made the tour of Europe and reached Paris, penetrating a modest drawing-room on Varennes Street (WC-020-035). There Mr. Durrien, honorary consul-general of the geographical society (WC-020-038), read the article, was startled, and turned intensely pale (WC-020-039).

## Significance

Two letters bearing the Paris postmark soon reached Erik: the first an invitation from the Geographical Society of France to receive a medal voted to the navigators of the first circumpolar periplus (WC-021-004); the second an envelope stamped with a medallion bearing the letters "E.D." and the motto "Semper idem" (WC-021-005).

The enclosed letter from Mr. Durrien began "My dear child," telling how he had read the biography translated from the Swedish and been overcome, recognising in it Erik's own account of himself (WC-021-008).

If it were all true, Durrien wrote, then Erik was the grandson he had mourned for many years, believed lost forever—as did his broken-hearted daughter, who still wept every day for her only child (WC-021-009).

An accompanying explanation set out the family history: Durrien's daughter Catherine had married the mining engineer George Durrien, and their son was named Emile Henry Georges (WC-021-015).

Six months after the father was killed in the mines, the young widow embarked on the *Cynthia* to rejoin Durrien (WC-021-016), and on the 7th of October 1858 the *Cynthia* was wrecked off the Faroe Islands under circumstances never explained (WC-021-017).

## Participants & Setting

- Erik, the waif of the *Cynthia*, now the twenty-two-year-old captain who completed the first circumpolar voyage and told his true story to the world, still ignorant that it would reveal his origin (WC-020-024) (WC-020-033).

- Mr. Durrien, honorary consul-general of the geographical society, who recognised his lost grandson in Erik's biography and wrote "My dear child" to claim him (WC-020-038) (WC-021-008).

- Patrick O'Donoghan, the Irishman who alone held the secret of Erik's birth and died on the ice without revealing it (WC-020-002).

- Tudor Brown, the villain who had sought to destroy Erik and died without a word (WC-020-003).
