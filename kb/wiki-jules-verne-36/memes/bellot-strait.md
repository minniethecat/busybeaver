# Bellot Strait

## Overview

Bellot Strait is the real, narrow Arctic passage on which the fortunes of Hatteras's voyage turn. The captain will not risk missing it — "Hatteras did not wish to miss the entrance to Bellot Strait, as the only outlet to the Gulf of Boothia on the south." (ACH-015-012)

It is a recent discovery, "a strait the existence of which Sir John Ross did not even guess at during his expedition in 1828." (ACH-015-026)

## In the Narrative

Everything hangs on finding it open: "would he find Bellot Strait open at that epoch, and could he ascend Peel Strait by rounding North Somerset?" (ACH-015-010) As the brig nears, "Hatteras felt his anxiety increase as he neared the strait; the fate of his voyage depended upon it." (ACH-016-002)

Beyond it lie further hopes — "once out of Bellot Strait, we shall be able to go north by Peel Strait or McClintock Channel." (ACH-016-026) A sudden thaw lets the *Forward* through: "the ice-fields were separated; the *Forward* went along boldly." (ACH-016-036)

## Geography & Features

The strait is a slender thread of water: "Bellot Strait is seventeen miles long and a mile wide, and about six or seven fathoms deep." (ACH-016-010)

It runs west of North Somerset, opening the way from the Gulf of Boothia toward the channels that lead north — the very key Hatteras seeks, missed by his predecessors. (ACH-016-002)
