## Overview

Samarkand is an ancient city of Central Asia, standing in the oasis of the Zarafchane in the valley of Sogd. In the Vernean corpus it belongs to *The Adventures of a Special Correspondent*, where it is one of the great halting-places on the Grand Transasiatic railway that carries the reporter Claudius Bombarnac from the Caspian toward China.

## In the Narrative

The narrator counts on the line to break its "monotony" only at the "vast oases of Merv, Bokhara and Samarkand." (ASC-006-008) When the train reaches it, "We had five hours to stop at Samarkand during the day, and that promised something pleasant and several pages of copy." (ASC-012-003) The reporter is captivated: "in this incomparable city of Samarkand scene succeeded scene," (ASC-012-049) and even after the visit he is "tired ... after that long run through Samarkand." (ASC-013-004)

## Geography & Features

Samarkand "is situated in the rich oasis watered by the Zarafchane in the valley of Sogd," (ASC-012-001) an oasis reached where "the windings of an affluent of the Amou-Daria, the Zarafchane" run up toward "the city of Samarkand." (ASC-010-072) Its great square is "a fine quadrilateral, perhaps a little spoiled by the fact that the Russians have paved it and ornamented it with lamps," (ASC-012-019) and its covered market is the "Samarkand bazaar." (ASC-012-031) From here "the distance from Samarkand to Tachkend is three hundred kilometres." (ASC-013-006)

## Connections

Samarkand is bound above all to *The Adventures of a Special Correspondent*, as a chief town of the Grand Transasiatic where the train "will stop a few hours" so the reporter can "do these towns in reporter style." (ASC-005-018) Its history is old and violent: "Burned by the armies of Cyrus in B.C. 329, Samarkand was in part destroyed by Genghis Khan, about 1219," (ASC-012-002) and long after "Samarkand passed under their dominion after the battles of Irdjar and Zera-Buleh." (ASC-007-018)
