# The Return of the Jeune-Hardie

## Overview

The return of the *Jeune-Hardie* is the closing event of *[[A Winter Amid the Ice]]*, in which the polar survivors—having quelled André Vasling's mutiny but lost Jean Cornbutte—wait out the thaw and sail home, reaching Dunkirk on the 16th of August, where the old curé says one mass for Jean Cornbutte's soul and another for the wedding of Louis and Marie (WAI-016-006) (WAI-016-022).

## What Happens

With the mutiny broken, the mortally wounded Herming was carried to a berth (WAI-016-002); but a greater misfortune had overtaken Louis Cornbutte, for his father Jean gave no further sign of life, the poor old sailor broken by disease at last ceasing to live (WAI-016-003).

Louis and Marie fell into a sad despair and knelt weeping in prayer for Jean Cornbutte's soul, while the bodies of Vasling, Aupic, and Jocki were thrown into a hole dug on the coast, soon joined by Herming, who died in the night without repentance (WAI-016-004).

Jean Cornbutte was buried on the coast; he had left his native land to find his son and had died in these terrible regions, his grave dug on an eminence and marked by the sailors with a simple wooden cross (WAI-016-006). From that day the survivors passed through many other trials, but the lemons they found restored them to health (WAI-016-007).

The cold held until the return of the sun (WAI-016-005), and February brought violent tempests and abundant snows before the warmth came sooner than usual (WAI-016-010). The crew had burned the barricading, the bulkheads, and much of the bridge for fuel, so it was time their wintering was over (WAI-016-011).

## Significance

After the equinox the sun remained above the horizon, and the eight months of perpetual daylight began to act upon the ice (WAI-016-012). The lower mass detached itself from the warming water, and early in April the ship reached her natural level (WAI-016-013). The rains of April, extending over the plain, hastened the breaking up (WAI-016-014).

On the 25th of April the ship was put in readiness, her sails found perfectly preserved (WAI-016-017). The May thaw melted the coastal snow (WAI-016-018), and twenty miles off the ice masses floated free towards the Atlantic, opening channels of which Louis Cornbutte wished to profit (WAI-016-019).

On the 21st of May, after a parting visit to his father's grave, Louis at last set out from the bay; for a month the way was full of perils, icebergs blown up with powder and ice-banks cut with saws (WAI-016-020). The *Jeune-Hardie* passed beyond the ice off Jan Mayen Island, nearly a month in emerging from the Polar Sea (WAI-016-021).

On the 16th of August she came in view of Dunkirk; the whole population flocked to the jetty, the sailors were clasped in the arms of their friends, and the old curé received Louis Cornbutte and Marie, saying on the following day two masses—the first for the repose of Jean Cornbutte's soul, the second for the marriage of Louis and Marie (WAI-016-022).

## Participants & Setting

- Louis Cornbutte, master of the *Jeune-Hardie*, who mourns his father, buries him beneath a wooden cross, and brings ship and crew home to Dunkirk to wed Marie (WAI-016-003) (WAI-016-020).

- Marie, Louis's betrothed, who weeps at Jean Cornbutte's bedside and is at last married to Louis by the old curé of Dunkirk (WAI-016-004) (WAI-016-022).

- Jean Cornbutte, the old sailor who left his native land to find his son and died in the polar regions, buried on an eminence by the coast (WAI-016-006).

- Penellan, Pierre Nouquet, Turquiette, and Fidèle Misonne, the sturdy sailors who did the work of ten men through the perilous homeward passage (WAI-016-020).
