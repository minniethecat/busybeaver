## Overview

The Great Eyrie is the mysterious mountain that opens *[[the-master-of-the-world|Master of the World]]*, a crag in the Blue Ridge whose summit hides an unseen secret. The narrative introduces "the crest called the Great Eyrie" with its "huge rounded form" dominating the surrounding country (MW-001-004), a landmark whose very name and nature puzzle the people below.

From the start the mountain is framed as an enigma. The narrator asks why the name Great Eyrie "was originally given this mountain by the people of the surrounding region" (MW-001-005), and the question of what the peak conceals propels the whole investigation.

## In the Narrative

The Great Eyrie's role is to be inaccessible and threatening. Its shape suggests a hidden vessel of fire: "the mount might better have been called a crater" (MW-001-006), and observers wonder whether an old volcano is stirring back to life within it.

Alarming signs issue from the summit. "Smoke had floated above the mountain" (MW-001-008), and pale flames have been seen reflected on the clouds at night, convincing the country folk that the mountain is dangerous. The mystery of these emanations is what draws the authorities — and the narrator — to attempt the climb.

## Geography & Features

The mountain is defined by its impregnable walls. Its steep, rounded rim encloses a hollow like a crater (MW-001-006), and its sheer faces have kept every climber out: it is remarked that "climbers had not previously attempted to ascend to the summit" (MW-001-011), leaving the interior wholly unexplored.

This inaccessibility is the mountain's essential geographic feature. Together with the smoke and flame that rise from within (MW-001-008), the unscalable ramparts make the Great Eyrie a sealed vault — later revealed to shelter the machine of the tale's antagonist rather than any volcanic fire.

## Connections

The Great Eyrie is the hidden lair at the center of the mystery in *[[Master of the World]]*, the inaccessible crater from which the story's investigation proceeds.
