# The Chimneys

## Overview

The Chimneys is the first dwelling used by the five castaways after their balloon strands them on [[Lincoln Island]] in *The Mysterious Island*. It is not a grotto but a pile of enormous rocks, heaped up by "the convulsions of nature" at the mouth of the watercourse above the reach of high tide, of the kind that granite countries call "Chimneys." (MI-004-020)

The name derives from the labyrinth of flue-like passages running through the tumbled granite blocks, which vented smoke like a chimney and gave the improvised home both its shape and its title.

## In the Narrative

The sailor Pencroft judged that by stopping up some of the openings with a mixture of stones and sand, the Chimneys could be rendered habitable. (MI-004-021) With the engineer Cyrus Harding still missing, the shelter "offered a retreat sufficient for the present" for the little band. (MI-006-005)

The Chimneys became the colonists' workshop as much as their home. Its hearth fired the clumsy but precious clay utensils that stocked the settlers' kitchen, baked at high temperature until they served as well as enameled china. (MI-013-043)

When the colonists later discovered the far safer cliff-dwelling of [[Granite House]], they debated their nomenclature, asking whether they should keep the name of the Chimneys for their first encampment. (MI-011-054) The site was retained as a forge and outwork rather than abandoned outright.

## Geography & Features

The Chimneys stands at the mouth of the Mercy, above the reach of the high tide, so that fuel and materials could be floated down the river to its door. (MI-004-057) Directly above it rose the high granite precipice crowned by the plateau to which the settlers gave the name of Prospect Heights. (MI-011-074)

Its pile of rocks was solid enough to shelter the men through violent storms, though loose fragments were sometimes flung onto its upper part. (MI-007-032) The maze of interior passages made it defensible and easy to warm, but its low situation by the shore left it exposed to the sea in a way the later heights were not.
