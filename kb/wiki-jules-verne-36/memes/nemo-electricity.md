## Overview

Nemo's Electricity is the single power source behind the [[Nautilus]] — the light, heat, and motive force of [[captain-nemo|Captain Nemo]]'s submarine, all drawn from the sea itself. When Professor Aronnax marvels that everything aboard runs without coal or contact with the shore, [[captain-nemo|Nemo]] answers with the principle on which his whole independence rests. (TTLU-012-011)(TTLU-012-023)

## Design & Operation

[[captain-nemo|Nemo]] names the force directly:

> There's a powerful, obedient, swift ... force ... This force is electricity (TTLU-012-011)

He anticipates the obvious objection — that ordinary batteries have limited dynamic potential — and dismisses it: his electricity "isn't the run-of-the-mill variety." (TTLU-012-014)(TTLU-012-015) The secret is his fuel. Rather than mining zinc, he extracts sodium from seawater, whose composition he recites (TTLU-012-021), and uses it to charge his cells:

> Mixed with mercury, it forms an amalgam that takes the place of zinc in Bunsen cells ... the sea itself gives me that (TTLU-012-023)

The energy to distill the sodium comes not from the sea but from coal mined beneath it:

> I utilize the heat of coal from the earth (TTLU-012-025)

[[captain-nemo|Nemo]] also alludes to drawing current from the differing temperatures of wires sunk at different depths and to the metallic veins of the ocean floor. (TTLU-012-017)(TTLU-012-019)

## Role in the Story

This electricity is the engine of [[captain-nemo|Nemo]]'s freedom: it severs every dependence on the land and makes the [[nautilus|Nautilus]] a wholly self-contained world. The long exposition to Aronnax is Verne's device for establishing that the submarine's wonders rest on a stated, if generous, physical basis rather than pure fantasy.

## Science & Speculation

Verne builds on real electrochemistry — the Bunsen cell was a genuine 1840s battery, and sodium is truly abundant in seawater. But the scheme quietly begs its own question: extracting sodium and refining it costs more energy than the resulting cells return, and [[captain-nemo|Nemo]] himself concedes he still burns submarine coal to power the process. The passage is a striking early vision of energy self-sufficiency, scientifically dressed yet resting on a perpetual-motion sleight of hand.
