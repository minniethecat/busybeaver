## Overview

Carmen is a colonial town of Patagonia, standing at the mouth of the Rio Negro, and the eastern terminus of the great overland route that Lord Glenarvan's party discussed while crossing the Pampas in In Search of the Castaways. Any notable event would have attracted the notice of the Indian traders who traffic between Tandil and Carmen, at the mouth of the Rio Negro. (SC-021-062)

The town gave its name to that route. When Thalcave named the way, Paganel replied in his best Spanish, "the route from Carmen to Mendoza." (SC-016-019)

## In the Narrative

The name Carmen entered the journey as a question of direction. Thalcave spoke of "the Carmen route," (SC-016-018) and asked whether the travelers were going to Carmen at all. (SC-016-027) He wanted to know whether they were going to Carmen or Mendoza, and was much surprised at the negative reply to both questions. (SC-016-032)

To make the geography plain, Paganel got down on the ground and traced a map on the sand, showing where the lines of latitude and longitude cross and where the two oceans lay, along which the Carmen route led. (SC-016-041)

## Geography & Features

Carmen stands at the mouth of the Rio Negro, the point from which the Indian traders' traffic ran inland toward Tandil. (SC-021-062) From the town the Carmen route stretched westward across the continent to Mendoza, the line along which the party fixed its bearings. (SC-016-019)

The route was a true cross-continental line: Paganel's sand map showed the lines of latitude and longitude and the two oceans it linked. (SC-016-041)

## Connections

The town lent its name to the local naming of the Patagonian peoples. Though Magellan called the natives Patagonians, the colonists of Carmen called them Tehuelches. (SC-009-019)

Carmen served the expedition chiefly as a fixed point of reference, the far end of the Carmen-to-Mendoza road that Thalcave and Paganel weighed when the party chose its course across the Pampas. (SC-016-032)
