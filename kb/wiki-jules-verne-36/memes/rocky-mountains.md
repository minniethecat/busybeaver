# Rocky Mountains

## Overview

The Rocky Mountains are the vast North American range that, in Verne's lunar cycle, hosts the giant telescope through which the flight of the Columbiad's projectile is watched. *From the Earth to the Moon* describes them as "that immense chain which begins at the Straits of Magellan, follows the west coast of South America under the name of the Andes or Cordilleras, crosses the Isthmus of Panama, and runs up the whole of North America to the very shores of the Polar Sea." (FEM-024-020)

## In the Narrative

Because the Gun Club "wished that its telescope, as well as the Columbiad, should be set up in the States of the Union, they were obliged to be content with the Rocky Mountains," raising the instrument on the summit of Long's Peak. (FEM-024-022) In *Round the Moon*, J.T. Maston keeps his watch there: "He was no doubt stationed in the Rocky Mountains at Long's Peak, trying to discover the invisible bullet gravitating in space." (RM-007-048) When the news of the projectile's fate arrives, a dispatch is wired "to the Honourable J.T. Maston, Long's Peak, Rocky Mountains." (RM-021-025)

## Geography & Features

The telescope of the Rocky Mountains "brings the moon to within two leagues only" and "allows them to see objects having nine feet of diameter on her surface." (RM-020-037) The heights are prone to obscuring cloud, "the great tempest which swept away the clouds heaped up on the Rocky Mountains." (RM-020-019) The range is also a barrier to travel: in *Around the World in Eighty Days*, Fogg's train reaches "Bridger Pass, seven thousand five hundred and twenty-four feet above the level of the sea, one of the highest points attained by the track in crossing the Rocky Mountains." (AWED-028-029)
