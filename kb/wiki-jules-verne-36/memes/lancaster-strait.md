# Lancaster Strait

## Overview

Lancaster Strait is the real Arctic passage off the west coast of Baffin's Sea, the historic doorway of the Northwest Passage explorers. When the northern route is closed, it offers the alternative: "if Smith's Strait is closed up from us, Lancaster Strait, on the west coast of Baffin's Sea, is not." (ACH-013-036)

It is hallowed ground of Arctic history, the track of Parry and Franklin before the *Forward*.

## In the Narrative

The brig makes for it and passes within: "the *Forward* was then at the extremity of Lancaster Strait." (ACH-014-006) At its mouth "the crew caught a glimpse of Burney Cape." (ACH-014-003)

Its fame is recited aboard — "in 1819 and 1820, the celebrated Parry passed through Lancaster Straits, and penetrated... as far as Melville Island." (ACH-006-030) And it was Franklin's own last course: "'I hope,' he said, 'to get under way to-night for Lancaster Strait.'" (ACH-017-005)

## Geography & Features

The strait is one arm of a great Arctic crossroads. The *Forward* sits "in the centre of this four-road way... fenced by the outlets of Lancaster Strait, Barrow Strait, Wellington Channel, and Regent's Passage." (ACH-014-010)

To follow it westward is to forgo the North-West passage — "we shall leave to our left the only track that leads to it," Johnson observes of the *Forward*'s true polar aim. (ACH-009-045)
