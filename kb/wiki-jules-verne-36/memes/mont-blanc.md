## Overview

Mont Blanc is the highest mountain in Europe (AMB-001-104), rising above the valley of Chamonix in the Alps. It is the setting of Verne's [[The Ascent of Mont Blanc]], whose narrator "arrived at Chamonix on the 18th of August, 1871, fully decided to make the ascent of Mont Blanc, cost what it might" (AMB-001-002).

Beyond that work its summit serves Verne as a byword for great elevation. In [[From the Earth to the Moon]] its height is set at 14,439 feet against the Himalayas' 26,776 (FEM-024-021), and in [[In Search of the Castaways]] a barometer reading of 11,000 feet is judged "only 910 meters lower than Mont Blanc" (SC-013-014).

## In the Narrative

The ascent is framed as an arduous and storied undertaking. Verne recalls that the first ascent was made by Jacques Balmat with Doctor Paccard (AMB-001-040), and warns plainly that "the ascent of Mont Blanc is a very painful one" (AMB-001-146).

Its dangers are recorded in the mountain's own necrology. Verne notes that "on the 13th of October, 1866, an English traveller and three of his guides were buried under a mass of ice that fell from Mont Blanc" (AMB-001-081). At the climax the narrator stands upon the peak, and "Mont Blanc was under our feet!" (AMB-001-100).

## Geography & Features

The summit is a narrow crest, not a broad dome: "the summit of Mont Blanc forms a ridge from southwest to north-east, two hundred paces long and a yard wide at the culminating point" (AMB-001-111). It crowns a chain of eternal snow, past which the climbers pass "the eternal snows of the Goûter, Mont Maudit, and, lastly, Mont Blanc" (AMB-001-062).

The peak stands as the measure of European altitude in Verne's cosmography. In [[Round the Moon]] it is imagined that "a Selenite landed in Europe on the summit of Mont Blanc, or in Asia on a peak of the Himalayas, would not be precisely at his destination" (RM-007-005), the mountain named as Europe's natural apex.

## Connections

Mont Blanc is the titular peak of [[The Ascent of Mont Blanc]], the culminating summit of the [[Alps]] above the valley of Chamonix. Its height recurs as a standard of elevation across [[From the Earth to the Moon]], [[Round the Moon]], and [[In Search of the Castaways]], where Verne measures other mountains and barometric heights against it.
