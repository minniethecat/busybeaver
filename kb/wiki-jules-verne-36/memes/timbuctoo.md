## Overview

Timbuctoo is the legendary African city that the aeronauts of *[[Five Weeks in a Balloon]]* long to see from the air. Its fame is such that Joe declares "nobody nowadays can think of making the trip to Africa without going to see Timbuctoo" (FWB-037-041), and the doctor sets his course toward it with the cry "Ho, then, for Timbuctoo!" (FWB-037-043).

The city is the "Queen of the Desert, mysterious Timbuctoo, which once, like Athens and Rome, had her schools of learned men, and her professorships of philosophy" (FWB-039-016), a place of storied learning fallen into decay by the time the balloon passes over it.

## In the Narrative

Reaching Timbuctoo is one of the voyage's set-piece goals. The doctor tracks the explorers who came before, recalling how "it was there that Barth crossed the Niger, on his return from Timbuctoo" (FWB-038-029), and how Major Laing was fated "to force his way across the desert of Sahara, penetrate to Timbuctoo, and perish a few miles above it" (FWB-038-041).

The wind nearly cheats them of the sight. An "obstinate northeast wind... bore them away from the latitude of Timbuctoo" (FWB-039-002), yet at last the doctor cries out joyously, "there is the harbor of Timbuctoo, and the city is not five miles from here!" (FWB-039-012), and the Victoria drifts over it.

## Geography & Features

From above the city is unimpressive. Seen as a bird sees it, Timbuctoo is "a collection of billiard-balls and thimbles" (FWB-039-019), a huddle of rounded roofs on the desert's edge, much shrunken from its former size.

Its history is one of conquest and trade. Long "an object generally coveted by all the tribes, since the eleventh century," it "has belonged in succession to the Touaregs, the Sonrayans, the Morocco men" and others (FWB-039-022), and it remains a caravan hub, for the nearby city of "Jenne... supplies all the wants of Timbuctoo" (FWB-040-026). Great camel trains still set out from it, "one hundred and fifty camels... go from Timbuctoo to Tafilet with a load of five hundred pounds upon their backs" (FWB-038-018).
