## Overview

The Black Sea is the great inland sea of southeastern Europe, recurring across Verne's works as a fixed point of geography and trade. At the fair of Nijni-Novgorod its shores supply the market: the products from the shores of the Caspian and the Black Sea were united at this corner of the globe. (MS-006-037)

## In the Narrative

It anchors the Transcaucasian railway in The Adventures of a Special Correspondent. The station at Tiflis is the junction of three lines — the western line ending at Poti on the Black Sea, where the passengers land coming from Europe. (ASC-001-019) Its name serves Verne as a byword: the sands of the Kara Koum are no more black than the waters of the Black Sea. (ASC-006-026)

Elsewhere it measures distance and misnomer. The great eastern section would include the greater part of Europe, from the Black Sea to Sweden. (TT-015-018) A lake's waters, Paganel owns, were no more white than the Black Sea is black. (SC-036-002) The Caspian is not in communication with the Black Sea or the Sea of Aral, being at a much lower level. (RC-013-056)

## Geography & Features

A deep inland sea between Europe and Asia Minor, lower-lying than the Caspian, the Black Sea serves Verne as a landing point for European travellers bound east and as a recurring name for the trade and geography of southern Russia.
