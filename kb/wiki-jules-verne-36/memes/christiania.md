# Christiania

## Overview

Christiania is the Norwegian capital — the city later renamed Oslo — in *Ticket No. 9672*. It is the home of the story's benefactor, introduced with a name that needs no gloss: "Sylvius Hogg, of Christiania--no further recommendation was needed." (TN-009-003) From it the mails, goods, and travellers of the tale set out into the Telemark country.

## In the Narrative

The city is the source of the region's traffic and news. Word comes that "the agent from Christiania has come down with his wagon of wines and provisions," (TN-001-018) and letters arrive by its post, one "that came by way of Christiania." (TN-001-058)

It supplies the comforts of civilisation to the up-country folk; if need be, Siegfrid "would not hesitate to go to the elegant stores of M. Benett, in Christiania, to make her purchases." (TN-005-042) It is also a fountainhead of the tourists who fill the valley, "these last being for the most part students from Christiania." (TN-002-008)

## Geography & Features

Christiania stands at one end of the great arc of southern Norway. Verne fixes its geography against its rival port: "the Telemark composes the broad portion of the immense horn that Norway forms between Bergen and Christiania." (TN-002-004)

In the story's own day the capital was not yet joined to the north by rail, for "at the date of this story, 1862, Norway was not yet traversed by the railroad that now enables one to go from Stockholm to Drontheim, by way of Christiania." (TN-002-006)
