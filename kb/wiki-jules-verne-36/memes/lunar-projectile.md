# The Projectile

## Overview

The projectile is the hollow aluminium shell that carries [[barbicane|Barbicane]], Nicholl, and [[michel-ardan|Michel Ardan]] on their voyage to the Moon after being fired from the [[Columbiad]]. The travellers descend "into the aluminium vehicle" through an opening made in its side (RM-001-003).

The metal is chosen for its lightness: cast in iron the shell "67,440 lbs." would be far heavier, but "cast in aluminium it would be reduced to 19,250" pounds (FEM-007-089). Michel later calls their craft "our aluminium planet" (RM-003-045).

## Design & Operation

Once sealed and lit, "the projectile presented the aspect of a comfortable room with padded walls, furnished with circular divans, the roof of which was in the shape of a dome" (RM-001-007). "Three beds, thick and solidly made, had been placed in the projectile" for the crossing (RM-001-039).

To survive the launch the crew rely on hydraulic buffers: "water-cushions placed between movable partitions elastic enough to protect us" from the shock (RM-001-023). Air is renewed by "the Reiset and Regnault apparatus, destined to produce oxygen" (RM-003-053).

## Role in the Story

The gun fires with colossal force: "the projectile, under the impulsion of 6,000,000,000 litres of gas developed by the deflagration of the [[gun-cotton|pyroxyle]], rose into space" (RM-001-065). Michel notes they are "shut up in a bullet at the bottom of a cannon 900 feet long" over "400,000 lbs. of gun-cotton" (RM-001-056).

In flight the crew manage their mass directly: "by throwing out the water in our movable partitions the projectile was made considerably lighter all at once" (RM-005-010). For the hoped-for landing [[barbicane|Barbicane]] fits "fireworks shut up in little cannons of bored steel, which could be screwed on to the bottom of the projectile" as retro-rockets (RM-009-017).

## Science & Speculation

Verne anticipates weightlessness at the gravitational neutral point, where the travellers float: passing it, [[barbicane|Barbicane]] explains, "the lunar attraction will draw us to the moon" (RM-008-042). The projectile's failure to reach its target is treated as an orbital-mechanics problem, its deviation caused when another body's "attraction was sufficient to have an influence upon our direction" (RM-009-050).
