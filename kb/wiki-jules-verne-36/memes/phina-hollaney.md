# Phina Hollaney

## Overview

Phina is introduced as the ward of the great millionaire, for "Phina Hollaney was the goddaughter of William W. Kolderup" (GM-003-014).

Her marriage to his nephew is a settled design, since "Godfrey Morgan was going to marry Phina Hollaney" (GM-003-025).

## Role in the Story

Rather than hold Godfrey back, she frames his voyage as a rite of passage, "the departure of Godfrey, who, before he gets married, wants to see a little of the world" (GM-003-046).

Her counsel carries the day, for she "pleaded" that they "must let Godfrey go," having "thought it carefully over" (GM-003-054).

## Character & Traits

She is a musician whose feeling escapes at the keyboard, where "Phina's hand sought the key-board and rippled along a series of sinking sevenths, which spoke of a plaintive sadness" (GM-003-021).

At the parting she masters herself, so that "Phina showed herself much the most composed" (GM-005-041).

## Relationships

- Godfrey — her betrothed and future husband, ever in his thoughts, for in exile "his first thought was for Phina, his betrothed, whom he had so stupidly refused to make his wife" (GM-010-009), her likeness kept near, since "Phina's portrait had its allotted place in Godfrey's cabin" (GM-005-035), and once wed she tells him, "with you, my dear husband, I fear nothing from anywhere" (GM-022-115).
- [[William W. Kolderup]] — her godfather, with whom at the reunion "Phina and Uncle Will asked Godfrey to do the honours of his island" (GM-022-063).

## Appearances

- [[Godfrey Morgan]] — the tale in which Godfrey names his desert isle for her, resolving, "I'll call it Phina Island, in memory of her" (GM-010-062); at its close she is the bride who declares, "Phina Island... is quite uninhabitable" (GM-022-112).
