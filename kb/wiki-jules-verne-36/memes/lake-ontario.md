## Overview

Lake Ontario is the lowest of the Great Lakes, appearing across several of Verne's American scenes. Its role is chiefly geographic: the lake empties at its northeastern end into Lake Ontario by means of the Niagara River and its celebrated falls. (MW-011-022)

## In the Narrative

It frames the Niagara descent in The Master of the World. Its length, from Lake Erie to Lake Ontario, is about fifteen leagues. (MW-014-046) It is here that the irresistible rapids of the Niagara River begin, where the river bends sharply to descend toward Lake Ontario. (MW-015-006)

At this end Lake Erie has no other outlet than the Niagara River, by which it empties into Lake Ontario. (MW-013-035)

Verne's flying machines cross it too. The night before, an aerial trumpet had blared its brazen notes through space immediately over that part of Canada between Lake Ontario and Lake Erie. (RC-001-014) The Albatross was then sweeping across the immense expanse of Lake Ontario. (RC-008-075) On its shores, Martin Paz would have been a great chief among the wandering tribes. (PL-002-030)

## Geography & Features

The northeastern terminus of the Erie–Niagara system and the lowest Great Lake, Lake Ontario recurs in Verne as the sink toward which the Niagara plunges and as open water for the Albatross to sweep across.
