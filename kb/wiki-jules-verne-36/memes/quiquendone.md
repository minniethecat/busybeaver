# Quiquendone

## Overview

Quiquendone is the tiny, imaginary Flemish town in which *Doctor Ox's Experiment* takes place. Verne opens by warning the reader that "if you try to find, on any map of Flanders, ancient or modern, the small town of Quiquendone, probably you will not succeed." (DOSE-001-002) Its extraordinary calm makes it the perfect subject for the doctor's chemical experiment on human temperament.

## In the Narrative

The town is governed with sleepy ease by the burgomaster Van Tricasse, who "considered himself the very man to administer the affairs of Quiquendone and its tranquil population." (DOSE-002-018) So peaceful is the place that the post of civil commissary is deemed entirely useless. (DOSE-002-007)

Doctor Ox offers to light the town with his new gas, and its leaders imagine Quiquendone will be the first town in Flanders so illuminated. (DOSE-003-040) In truth the experiment is a covert study: by flooding the streets and halls with oxygen, Ox means to see whether the phlegmatic townsfolk can be roused to passion.

The effect is dramatic. The once-somnolent citizens grow feverish, quarrelsome, and warlike, until an explosion ends the experiment. After the blast, "Quiquendone immediately became the peaceable, phlegmatic, and Flemish town it formerly was." (DOSE-016-002)

## Geography & Features

Quiquendone is a walled Flemish town of narrow streets, Spanish-looking houses, a market, and a burgomaster — so vivid that Verne insists "Quiquendone really exists," despite being on no map. (DOSE-001-002) Its chief ornament is the Stadtholder's Hall, adorned with a full-length portrait of William of Nassau.

Its economy is as gentle as its temper: the principal industry is the manufacture of whipped creams and barley-sugar on a large scale. (DOSE-001-002) Verne even adds, with a smile, that the town "has neither industry nor commerce, and that it does very well without them." (DOSE-001-004) This drowsy stability is precisely what Doctor Ox sets out to disturb.
