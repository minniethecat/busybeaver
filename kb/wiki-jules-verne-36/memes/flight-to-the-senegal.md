# The Flight to the Senegal

## Overview

The flight to the Senegal is the climax of *[[Five Weeks in a Balloon]]*, in which the failing *Victoria*, stripped of everything to stay aloft, is chased across West Africa by the Talabas and delivered at the last moment onto the far bank of the river, at the very edge of [[Senegal]].

The disaster had a plain cause: the gutta-percha of the envelope had softened and melted in the heat, and the hydrogen was escaping through the silk (FWB-041-007). By the final night Doctor Ferguson found by stellar observation that he was scarcely twenty-five miles from Senegal (FWB-042-002).

## What Happens

To keep the sinking balloon in the air, the travellers made their last sacrifice. Ferguson resolved to be rid of the cylinder-chests, the spiral, and the Buntzen battery—nine hundred pounds of the apparatus that had let him dilate his gas (FWB-042-012).

"However serious the decision may appear," he told his friends, "we must sacrifice our apparatus" (FWB-042-019). No sooner was the work done than a fresh peril struck: the forest beneath them burst into flames (FWB-042-039), set, the doctor judged, by Al-Hadji's marabouts (FWB-042-046).

Ferguson severed the anchor-rope with a blow of his hatchet, and the *Victoria*, released, spun a thousand feet upward out of the fire (FWB-042-049). By daylight some thirty mounted men in floating bournouses were following the balloon along the ground (FWB-043-007)—the cruel Talabas, the marabouts of Al-Hadji (FWB-043-009).

As the deflating balloon sank within musket-range, Joe took aim at the foremost horseman and fired, and the Talaba fell headlong (FWB-043-035). Lightened again and again, the *Victoria* made enormous leaps, like a huge gum-elastic ball bounding through the air (FWB-043-049).

At last it ascended above the gulf of the cataracts and was swept across the river (FWB-043-095). As it settled toward the far bank, French soldiers rushed into the water and caught the three aeronauts in their arms just as the *Victoria* fell (FWB-043-098).

## Significance

The empty balloon, borne off by the swift current, plunged headlong over the falls—Joe's "poor Victoria" lost at the very moment of rescue (FWB-043-101).

The soldiers who saved them were an expedition sent by the governor of Senegal (FWB-044-002); as the only witnesses to the journey's end, their officers drew up the certificate, "Done at the Cataracts of Gouina, on the 24th of May, 1862," that attested Ferguson's crossing of Africa (FWB-044-008).

The episode is distinct from the earlier rescue of the missionary over Lake Tchad; here the drama is not deliverance of another but the crew's own escape, and it closes the five-week voyage that the whole novel had been building toward.

## Participants & Setting

- Doctor Ferguson, the expedition's leader, orders each successive sacrifice—the apparatus, the anchor-rope, the last of the ballast—and keeps his nerve through fire and pursuit until the river is crossed.

- Joe, the doctor's devoted servant, does the dangerous work aloft and holds the Talabas at bay with his rifle, felling the foremost rider to buy the balloon a few more leaps.

- Kennedy, the Scottish sportsman, guards his beloved fire-arms to the last and turns them on the horsemen only when the pursuers close within two hundred paces.

- The Talabas, Al-Hadji's marauding marabouts, harry the balloon across a country of burned villages and ruined fields, and the French marines of the Senegal post are the deliverers who pluck the aeronauts from the water at the Cataracts of Gouina.
