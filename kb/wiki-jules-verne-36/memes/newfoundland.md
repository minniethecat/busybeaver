## Overview

Newfoundland is the North Atlantic island whose surrounding banks are among the most storied fishing grounds in Verne's world. In *[[Ticket No. 9672]]* the whole of Hulda's anxiety turns on the distance to them, as she frets over "how far it is from here to the Newfoundland fishing banks" (TN-001-026), where her betrothed has sailed.

The banks are a place of danger and long absence. Ole Kamp's "winter's experience on the fishing banks of Newfoundland had been a severe one" (TN-004-001), and the fear of shipwreck "between Iceland and Newfoundland" (TN-011-078) haunts those who wait at home in Norway.

## In the Narrative

Newfoundland recurs as the far terminus of Atlantic voyages. The fishing fleet sails there each season, for "one week afterward the 'Viking' set sail for the fishing banks of Newfoundland" (TN-004-063), and news travels slowly back, so that "still no Ole, nor did the postman bring any letter from Newfoundland" (TN-005-052).

Its banks are also a hazard to Arctic expeditions. In *[[The Adventures of Captain Hatteras]]* the Forward meets a Danish corvette "making for the bank of Newfoundland" (ACH-006-004), and the island marks the place where the Gulf Stream, "after ascending the eastern coast of America to Newfoundland, inclines to the north-east along the coast of Norway" (ACH-005-042).

## Geography & Features

The island lies athwart the great ocean routes between Europe and America. Ships crossing must reckon with "the seas that lie between Norway and Newfoundland" (TN-011-077), and vessels bound for Europe "follow the same route" past it (TN-011-078), making it a fixed waymark of the North Atlantic.

Its banks are notoriously fog-bound. Phileas Fogg's steamer, crossing homeward, "passed the edge of the Banks of Newfoundland, a dangerous locality; during the winter, especially, there are frequent fogs and heavy gales of wind" (AWED-033-011). The island is also a landfall of the transatlantic cable, "the American cable" running "between Valentia and Newfoundland" (RM-020-005), and it appears in the roll of lands discovered by early navigators (ACH-012-051).
