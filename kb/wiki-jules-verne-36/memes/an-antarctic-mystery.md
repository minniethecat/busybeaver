## Overview

*An Antarctic Mystery* is Jules Verne's polar sequel to Edgar Allan Poe's *Narrative of Arthur Gordon Pym*. It is narrated by the American traveler Mr. Jeorling of Connecticut, stranded at Kerguelen awaiting a ship (AM-001-004, AM-001-019).

Verne opens by inviting doubt: "the following narrative will be received with entire incredulity, but I think it well that the public should be put in possession of the facts" (AM-001-001).

## Plot Summary

At Christmas Harbour, Jeorling takes passage aboard the schooner *Halbrane* under Captain Len Guy (AM-001-039). The captain reveals a personal stake: he is brother to Captain William Guy of the *Jane*, and asks whether Jeorling believes "that my brother William Guy and five of his companions are living?" (AM-008-020).

The *Halbrane* sails south to seek the survivors wrecked at Tsalal, the island of Poe's tale. Jeorling confesses he is "haunted by the figures of Arthur Pym and his companions, lost in Antarctic ice-deserts," and joins the search (AM-008-006). Aboard is the half-breed Dirk Peters, Pym's old companion, still alive.

Pressing beyond the great iceberg-barrier through "a passage found amid a thousand risks," the crew at last recovers "Captain William Guy and his three companions" (AM-026-015, AM-026-007). Dirk Peters dies before the voyage ends, and Jeorling salutes Pym, "the hero whom Edgar Poe has made so famous," who "has shown the way" (AM-026-002, AM-026-031).

## Main Characters

- Mr. Jeorling — the American narrator who sails with the *Halbrane*
- Captain Len Guy — commander of the *Halbrane*, brother of the lost William Guy
- Dirk Peters — Arthur Pym's old companion, who dies on the voyage
- William Guy — captain of the *Jane*, found alive at last with three companions

## Key Places & Technology

- The *Halbrane* — the schooner that carries the search into the Antarctic
- Tsalal — the polar island from Poe's tale where the survivors are sought

## Themes

Verne takes up Poe's unfinished mystery and drives it toward a rational resolution, replacing the supernatural veil with geography, ice, and human endurance. The voyage becomes a meditation on belief and proof, as a skeptic is carried to the very scene he had dismissed as fiction.
