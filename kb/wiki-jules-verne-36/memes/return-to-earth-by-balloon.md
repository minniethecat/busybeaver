# The Return to Earth by Balloon

## Overview

The return to earth by balloon is the climax of *[[Off on a Comet]]*, in which Hector Servadac and his companions abandon the comet [[Gallia]] in a hot-air balloon at the instant of its second approach to the earth, and are carried back to the world they had left.

When the balloon had reached an elevation of about 2,500 yards, a wire-work stove suspended below the casing and filled with lighted hay served to keep the air within at a proper temperature (OC-044-001). They came at last to their senses in Algeria, not far from Mostaganem (OC-045-002).

## What Happens

Not every soul went willingly. Professor Rosette, refusing to quit the nucleus he had studied, was checked only when Servadac bade two sailors take him in their arms and set him down in the car (OC-043-073). Then the cords were cut, and the balloon rose with stately calmness into the air (OC-043-078).

Beneath their feet lay the basin of the [[Gallian Sea]] (OC-044-002), while the great expanse of the terrestrial disc drew rapidly down obliquely towards them (OC-044-004). The aeronauts were being hurried on at a speed of seventy miles a second (OC-044-005).

Even now Rosette kept his eyes fixed upon the abandoned comet, floating about a mile and a half below him (OC-044-013). Chronometer in hand, Procope reckoned the calculated time of impact at two hours, forty-seven minutes, thirty-five seconds—five minutes more and collision must ensue (OC-044-018).

This time the deviation gave no hope that Gallia would merely graze the earth as before; a true collision was certain (OC-044-020). Worse, it seemed only too likely that in the fusion of the two atmospheres the balloon itself would be rent into ribbons (OC-044-022).

Yet they survived the shock. Recovering from their bewilderment, they found themselves scarcely more than a mile from Mostaganem (OC-045-004).

## Significance

At the Mascara gate the first persons Servadac recognized were the two friends who, two years before, had been his seconds in an intended duel (OC-045-007). The quarrel had lost its cause, and the former rivals took each other by the hand, united henceforth in sincere friendship (OC-045-016).

To their lasting perplexity, the shores of the Mediterranean had undergone no change whatever (OC-045-018)—the world went on as though the whole comet-voyage had never been. The episode is the counterpart of the earlier comet-collision that first flung them into space; here the same cosmic accident, reversed, sets them down again.

## Participants & Setting

- Hector Servadac, the French captain, insists on being the last to leave "the ship" and directs the desperate ascent that carries the whole company off the doomed comet.

- Ben Zoof, his devoted orderly, greets the swelling earth by crying out that he can see Montmartre, his beloved Paris hill, long before any feature could truly be made out (OC-044-012).

- Professor Palmyrin Rosette, the astronomer, must be carried bodily aboard, mourning the comet he alone is loath to abandon even as it recedes below.

- Count Timascheff and Lieutenant Procope share the car with the Spaniards and the little girl Nina; the balloon, borne between Gallia and the earth over the Gallian Sea, is their only vessel of deliverance.
