# Strait of Gibraltar

## Overview

The Strait of Gibraltar is the narrow neck of water joining the Mediterranean to the Atlantic, through which the Nautilus makes its swift exit in *Twenty Thousand Leagues Under the Sea*. The narrator marks the crossing precisely: "Departing from the waterways of Greece on the morning of February 16, we cleared the Strait of Gibraltar by sunrise on the 18th." (TTLU-031-004)

## In the Narrative

As the submarine approaches, the sea floor tells a history of disaster: "these Mediterranean depths became more and more cluttered with such gruesome wreckage as the Nautilus drew nearer to the Strait of Gibraltar." (TTLU-031-044) In the small hours "on February 18, near three o'clock in the morning, it hove before the entrance to the Strait of Gibraltar." (TTLU-031-045) Emerging beyond, "the Nautilus took to the high seas." (TTLU-032-004)

## Geography & Features

Verne uses the strait to expound a theory of Mediterranean currents, positing "some lower current that carries the Mediterranean's surplus through the Strait of Gibraltar and into the Atlantic basin." (TTLU-031-046) Its narrowness is a measure of the monstrous: the ancients told of "monsters with mouths as big as a gulf, which were too huge to get through the Strait of Gibraltar." (TTLU-042-047) Ned Land reads the exit as a bid for escape, dreading the day "the Nautilus had emerged from the Strait of Gibraltar and made for that cape in the south." (TTLU-032-010)
