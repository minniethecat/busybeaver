# Gulf of Mexico

## Overview

The Gulf of Mexico is the great warm basin whose waters give birth to the Gulf Stream in *Twenty Thousand Leagues Under the Sea*. The narrator traces the current's origin there: the Gulf Stream, "as it leaves the Gulf of Mexico, is barely different from your blood temperature." (TTLU-043-012)

## In the Narrative

The gulf frames Aronnax's long meditation on the ocean's great river. He describes how the current, "attaining a white heat in the Gulf of Mexico, it heads north up the American coast, advances as far as Newfoundland, swerves away under the thrust of a cold current from the Davis Strait." (TTLU-043-008)

Even the sea-creatures the *Nautilus* meets are placed by their avoidance of it: one "obviously didn't want to frequent the waves of the Gulf of Mexico or the Caribbean Sea." (TTLU-042-002)

## Geography & Features

The gulf is the fountainhead from which the Atlantic's warm current spreads. "Before entering the Gulf of Mexico near latitude 44 degrees north, this current divides into two arms; its chief arm makes for the shores of Ireland and Norway." (TTLU-035-003)

Its mouth is one of the ocean's busiest highways: "the sea was plowed continuously by the many steamers providing service between the Gulf of Mexico and New York or Boston, and it was crossed night and day by little schooners engaged in coastal trade." (TTLU-043-017)
