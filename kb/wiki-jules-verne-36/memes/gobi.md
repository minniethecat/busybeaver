## Overview

The Gobi is the great desert of Central Asia crossed by the Grand Transasiatic railway in The Adventures of a Special Correspondent. Beyond lay the interminable plains of Chinese Turkestan, the immense sandy desert of Gobi, where the monotony of the journey will begin again. (ASC-009-009)

## In the Narrative

The train runs for days across its emptiness. All along is this monotonous desert of Gobi, with the heights of the Humboldt mountains on the right reaching on to the ranges of Nan Chan. (ASC-022-038) This part of the desert of Gobi has little resemblance to the regions of Eastern Turkestan crossed on leaving Kachgar. (ASC-022-039)

The glorious sun gilds the sands of the Gobi as far as could be seen. (ASC-022-010)

It is also a place of danger. Before their eyes stretches the Gobi — barren, desolate and gloomy, according to the reports of Grjimailo, Blanc and Martin. (ASC-019-025) The thicket gives passage to a gang in ambush — some sixty Mongols, nomads of the Gobi. (ASC-020-022)

## Geography & Features

A barren sea of sand between Chinese Turkestan and the ranges of the Nan Chan and Humboldt mountains, the Gobi is the longest and dreariest stage of the Transasiatic line — monotonous by day, haunted by Mongol raiders, and framed on Verne's map by the Lob Nor and the Kara-Koum.
