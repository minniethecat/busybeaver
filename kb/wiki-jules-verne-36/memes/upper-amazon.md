## Overview

The Upper Amazon is the headwater basin of the great river, spanning the Peruvian forests and the western Brazilian frontier where Eight Hundred Leagues on the Amazon opens. Its waters issue from the forests of Peru, which the wandering Torres had just entered when he passed the frontier. (EHLA-001-008)

It was journey's end for a long voyage. One traveller rejoiced at the passage that had led him from the coast of the Atlantic to the Upper Amazon. (EHLA-001-018) Its chief town, Tabatinga, at present the capital of the Upper Amazon, began as a simple Mission founded by the Portuguese Carmelites about 1692. (EHLA-014-030)

## In the Narrative

The basin was settled by the missions. Iquitos, like every collection of huts in the basin of the Upper Amazon, was founded by the missionaries. (EHLA-003-002) It was there that the Garral fortune grew: ten years after Joam Garral's arrival, the fazenda at Iquitos had become one of the richest establishments on the Upper Amazon. (EHLA-003-018)

The region was watched as much as it was settled. When trouble came, an escort was ordered to be ready in the missions of the Upper Amazon. (EHLA-011-064) Into its towns came travellers like Fragoso, barber in ordinary and extraordinary to the tribes of the Upper Amazon. (EHLA-012-061)

## Geography & Features

The forests teemed with life. Of all the prehensile-tailed monkeys that haunt the forests of the Upper Amazon, the guariba is the most eccentric. (EHLA-002-003) The great ficus, in its many kinds, grows all over the Upper Amazon basin. (EHLA-002-044)

Its waters and sands were rich. The tapirs, called antas in Brazil, are already nearly undiscoverable on the banks of the Upper Amazon and its tributaries. (EHLA-007-055) Its sand banks are considered the richest turtle grounds in the basin of the Upper Amazon. (EHLA-016-072)

## Connections

The basin bore the marks of a vanished native world. On the Upper Amazon many Indian tribes have already disappeared, among them the Curicicurus and the Sorimaos. (EHLA-005-044) Along its banks still travelled the wandering barbers, going from village to village to serve the peoples of the river. (EHLA-007-137)

Its remoteness made it a refuge. Penniless and unable to enter Tijuco again, Ortega fled to the districts of the Upper Amazon where the capitaes da mato are found. (EHLA-039-021)
