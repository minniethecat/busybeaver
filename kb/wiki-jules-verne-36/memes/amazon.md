## Overview

The Amazon is the great river of South America and the highway of Eight Hundred Leagues on the Amazon, down which the Garral family's jangada drifts toward Belem. Its bed is deep and vast — Benito turns off so as to reach the middle of the bed of the Amazon, where there was the greatest depression. (EHLA-030-025)

## In the Narrative

The river is a living world. Gymnotuses are plentiful enough both in the Amazon and its tributaries, one of these living coils, about ten feet long, threatening the divers. (EHLA-030-039) The current carries all before it — the carcass of a huge cayman had been swept by the current of the Rio Negro into the bed of the Amazon. (EHLA-030-027)

Its affluents define the geography of the voyage. An important affluent of the Amazon comes from the southwest, and from source to mouth has not a single island nor rapid to check its course. (EHLA-014-008) The dark tributaries teem with life the main channel lacks — the amphibians prefer the black waters of the tributaries of the Amazon. (EHLA-029-029)

## Geography & Features

Draining half a continent from the Andes to the Atlantic, the Amazon and its innumerable affluents form the stage of the whole novel — eight hundred leagues of navigable water past Iquitos, Tabatinga, Manaos, and Belem.
