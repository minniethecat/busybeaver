## Overview

Saville Row is the London street in Burlington Gardens where Mr. Phileas Fogg lived in 1872, at No. 7, the house in which Sheridan died in 1814. (AWED-001-002) There he lived alone, whither none penetrated. (AWED-001-010)

The mansion, though not sumptuous, was exceedingly comfortable. (AWED-001-012) What must have been a very temple of disorder and unrest under the illustrious but dissipated Sheridan was, under Fogg, cosiness, comfort, and method idealised. (AWED-002-011)

## In the Narrative

The house kept the rhythm of its master. At exactly half-past eleven Mr. Fogg would, according to his daily habit, quit Saville Row and repair to the Reform. (AWED-001-013) When the servant Passepartout was engaged, he found himself alone in the house in Saville Row. (AWED-001-027)

From here the wager began. Master and man descended, the street-door was double-locked, and at the end of Saville Row they took a cab and drove rapidly to Charing Cross. (AWED-004-029) At the journey's close, in three minutes Fogg was in Saville Row again. (AWED-037-005)

## Geography & Features

The house measured out Fogg's fortune as much as his hours. Even far away he thought of the gas remorselessly burning at his expense in Saville Row. (AWED-017-014) On his supposed ruin the house stood as if uninhabited through a Sunday, and for the first time he did not set out for his club when the clock struck half-past eleven. (AWED-035-020)

## Connections

The street bound Fogg to the Reform Club he quit each morning and to the wider city. When he returned unlooked-for, the dwellers in Saville Row would have been surprised had they been told Phileas Fogg was home. (AWED-035-002) A room in the house was set apart for Aouda, overwhelmed with grief at her protector's misfortune. (AWED-035-006)
