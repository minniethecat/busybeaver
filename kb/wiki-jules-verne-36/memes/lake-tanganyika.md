# Lake Tanganyika

## Overview

Lake Tanganyika is the real great lake of Central Africa that recurs through the African chapters of *Dick Sand: A Captain at Fifteen*, both as a landmark of exploration history and as a node in the slave traffic the novel condemns. Its lakeside town of Oujiji stands as "the principal market of Lake Tanganyika," (DSCF-028-008) drawing traders from across the region.

The lake anchors the book's geography of the interior.

## In the Narrative

The lake is bound up with the fate of captives driven inland. Their master, a rich Arab trader, means "in a few days" to send them "to Lake Tanganyika, the great thoroughfare for slaves; then, from that point, toward the factories of Zanzibar." (DSCF-028-046) The waterway is thus the grim highway along which the caravans move.

It is equally the stage for the era's great exploring feats, recounted at length in the narrator's digressions.

## Geography & Features

The lake sits at the heart of the country the explorers cross: Livingstone "wished to visit the country comprised between the Nyassa and Lake Tanganyika," (DSCF-032-055) and later took "a northern direction, with the design of reaching the important town of Oujiji, on the Tanganyika." (DSCF-032-056)

It was here that Stanley's search closed: the correspondent "sent in search of Livingstone, had found him on October 30th, 1871, at Oujiji, on Lake Tanganyika." (DSCF-019-039) Livingstone in turn hoped "to gain the sources of the valley of the Nile by ascending the Tanganyika." (DSCF-032-058)
