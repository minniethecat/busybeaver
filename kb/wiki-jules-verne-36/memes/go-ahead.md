# Go-Ahead

## Overview

The Go-Ahead is the enormous dirigible aerostat built by the [[Weldon Institute]] of Philadelphia, the lighter-than-air answer with which the balloonists meant to refute Robur and his heavier-than-air [[Albatross]]. Rejecting the grandiose name Excelsior, its makers called it simply the "Go-Ahead," so that it might justify the name by going ahead at the will of its commander. (RC-003-012)

Built in the Turner yard, the aerostat was the club's central project, and its president Uncle Prudent staked the Institute's whole creed upon it. When he and Phil Evans vanished, the assembly even voted to suspend work on the Go-Ahead in their absence. (RC-021-015)

## Design & Operation

An enormous aerostat reposing in the Turner yard, its strength was proved by highly compressed air, and it well merited its name as the largest balloon yet conceived. (RC-003-010)

It was of elongated form, so as to facilitate its horizontal displacement, and possessed every quality of a good aerostat: dimensions for great height, impermeability for indefinite flight, and solidity to resist the atmosphere. (RC-022-009)

Its lift came from the lightest of gases. Ordinary lighting gas raises about 700 grams per cubic meter, but hydrogen was reckoned far stronger:

> hydrogen possesses an ascensional force estimated at 1,100 grams per cubic meter. (RC-022-011)

At a capacity of 40,000 cubic meters, this gave the craft an ascensional power of some 44,000 kilograms. (RC-022-011)

For propulsion the Institute purchased a patent dynamo-electric machine, nearly ready six weeks before the maiden cruise. (RC-003-013) The mechanical difficulties were not all overcome: endless evenings were spent disputing not the screw's form or size but whether it should be mounted behind, as the Tissandier brothers had done, leaving it impossible to fix the screw in place. (RC-003-014)

## Role in the Story

On the 29th of April, seven months after Uncle Prudent and Phil Evans returned, the aerostat rose from a clearing in Fairmount Park before an immense crowd drawn from neighboring states. (RC-022-002)

It went up in a perfectly vertical line and, under the influence of its rudder, executed every evolution its steersman could command, mounting at last to a height of fourteen thousand feet. (RC-022-023)

The triumph was brief. The [[Albatross]] returned like a huge bird of prey to strike its rival. (RC-023-002) Though smaller than the Go-Ahead, Robur's craft ran it down as the swordfish runs the whale. (RC-023-016) The balloon sought safety by rising, but the aeronef dropped upon it, and the crew dragged Uncle Prudent and Phil Evans by force from the Go-Ahead onto the Albatross. (RC-023-026)

## Science & Speculation

The Go-Ahead is Verne's vehicle for the novel's central debate: lighter-than-air versus heavier-than-air flight. The [[Weldon Institute]] insisted that the true atmospheric vehicle was the aerostat and dismissed the inventions of rival aviators. (RC-022-006)

The book grounds this faith in real ballooning history, measuring the Go-Ahead against Nadar's Géant, John Wise's balloon, and the Giffard balloon of the 1878 Exhibition. (RC-003-011)

Yet the plot decides the argument against them: the frail balloon, for all its record dimensions, is helpless before Robur's screw-lifted machine, and the [[Albatross]] wins the sky. Verne, writing in 1886, sided with powered heavier-than-air flight decades before it was achieved, and the destruction of the Go-Ahead is his verdict on the dirigible balloon. (RC-023-026)
