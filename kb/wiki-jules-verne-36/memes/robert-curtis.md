# Robert Curtis

## Overview

He is the chief officer of the doomed vessel, introduced by the narrator as "this mate, whose name is Robert Curtis, our crew consists of Walter, the lieutenant, the boatswain, and fourteen sailors" (SC2-002-003).

The passengers form a high opinion of him at once: "a very favourable opinion of Robert Curtis, the mate, a man of about thirty years of age, of great muscular power, with a frame and a will that seem ever ready for action" (SC2-004-015).

As the captain's judgement fails, he assumes command outright, declaring "From this time forward, I am captain of this vessel" (SC2-012-030).

## Role in the Story

He leads the long fight against the fire smouldering in the cotton cargo, hoping "that by excluding the air, the fire may be stifled before it ignites the general cargo; he has hermetically closed every accessible aperture" (SC2-010-005).

Amid the wreck he tends the survivors before himself: "His next care was to distribute preserved meat and biscuit amongst the passengers and crew already half fainting with hunger and fatigue" (SC2-016-010).

When the ship must be abandoned he oversees the raft, and "Curtis insisted upon the raft being made with proper care to insure its strength" (SC2-026-004).

He holds authority over the drifting company, telling them: "Here on this raft, just as when we were on board the 'Chancellor,' I consider myself your captain" (SC2-031-002).

When famine drives the castaways toward butchery he forbids it, and "whilst we were alive to prevent it, this butchery should not be permitted, and we rushed forwards simultaneously to snatch the victim from his murderers" (SC2-055-018).

His seamanship at last reads salvation in the water: it is "near the Amazon; no other river has a current strong enough to freshen the ocean twenty miles from shore" (SC2-056-010).

## Character & Traits

His bearing marks him from the first as "a man of energy, thoroughly endowed with the calmness and courage that are indispensable to the true sailor" (SC2-004-016).

He commands without bluster, rallying the men "in his calm clear voice" (SC2-020-012).

His resolve never fails; he tells the narrator: "As long as a plank of the ship remains to stand on, Mr. Kazallon, I shall not give up my hope" (SC2-014-009).

To the bitter end he stands unbroken, for "Curtis was the only man who remained on his feet, but with indomitable pluck he continued to stand on the front of the raft, waiting, watching, hoping" (SC2-051-005).

## Relationships

- Kazallon, the narrator, owes his life to him, avowing "it is to Robert Curtis that I owe my real deliverance, for he it was that prevented me from being carried away by a second heavy wave" (SC2-036-002).

- Captain Huntly is the failing commander he must overrule; the narrator watches "Curtis remonstrating somewhat vehemently with Captain Huntly" (SC2-008-007) before he takes the ship in hand.

- André Letourneur, a young passenger, he saves in the flood, for "Curtis caught young Letourneur again in his arms, and running with him across the flooded deck deposited him safely in the starboard shrouds" (SC2-025-001).

- Owen and the mutinous sailors he faces down unarmed, "walking straight up to Owen, asked him what he wanted" (SC2-038-016).

## Appearances

- [[The Survivors of the Chancellor]] — the account of the burning ship and the raft, in which, as the vessel is abandoned, "Curtis then left the ship; the rope was cut and we went slowly adrift" (SC2-029-013).
