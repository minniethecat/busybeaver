# Fulgurator

## Overview

The Fulgurator is the war-engine invented by the French chemist [[Thomas Roch]]; his "latest invention in war-engines bore the name of Roch's Fulgurator." (FF-001-021) It is a self-propelled explosive of unprecedented power, and its secret becomes the central prize of the novel—coveted by the great powers and finally seized by the pirate [[Ker Karraje]]. (FF-011-016)

Roch would not experiment until the millions at which he valued the weapon had first been paid to him. (FF-001-026) When Europe and America refused his impossible price, the embittered, half-mad inventor became an opening his enemies were determined to exploit.

## Design & Operation

The fulgurator "was a sort of auto-propulsive engine, of peculiar construction, charged with an explosive composed of new substances and which only produced its effect under the action of a deflagrator that was also new." (FF-001-024)

Its power was reputed almost without limit: if Roch were to be credited, "this fulgurator could shatter the terrestrial spheroid at one blow." (FF-011-061) The two elements—the explosive proper and the deflagrator that fired it—had to be compounded from special substances, and it took about thirty-five seconds before their combination was effected. (FF-011-060, FF-013-014)

In action the engines rested in grooved trestles and were given a long trajectory without any gyratory spin, unlike "Inventor Turpin's gyroscopic projectiles." (FF-017-044) Roch poured a few drops of liquid into the rear of each engine to prime it, whereupon the projectiles tore through the air and, boomerang-like, curved back upon the doomed vessel. (FF-017-050, FF-017-054)

Its destructive zone was nonetheless bounded: a ship that passed beyond a radius of some five to six miles was safe, a weakness the attacking squadron would later exploit. (FF-012-067, FF-015-085)

## Role in the Story

Roch offered the fulgurator first to France, then—rebuffed over his price—carried it in turn to Germany, England, and finally the United States, who "attached exceptional importance" to it. (FF-001-024, FF-001-031, FF-001-035) No nation would meet his terms.

Kidnapped by the Count d'Artigas—in reality [[Ker Karraje]]—Roch is carried to the island stronghold of Back Cup, where his captors scheme "to force from him the secret of his fulgurator." (FF-009-010) Once master of the weapon, Ker Karraje could "carry on his crimes with complete impunity." (FF-013-023)

When a warship squadron closes on Back Cup, Roch launches his engines and blows the leading cruiser "to pieces," the blast shaking the whole island to its foundations. (FF-017-055, FF-017-056) But at the sight of the tricolor unfurling over the ships, the inventor understands and draws back, refusing to fire on "a very part of France." (FF-017-072, FF-017-078)

Conscious at last of the crime of lèse-patrie, Roch rushes to the magazine and detonates the stored explosive, destroying Back Cup and himself; Ker Karraje, the pirates, and the secret of the invention all vanish with him. (FF-018-028, FF-018-029)

## Science & Speculation

The Fulgurator is Verne's meditation on the arms race of his age: an "engine that was capable of revolutionizing the condition of both offensive and defensive warfare on land and sea." (FF-001-041) By naming real ordnance—Turpin's gyroscopic shells, the Zalinski pneumatic gun—the narrative grounds Roch's fantastic weapon in the melinite-and-dynamite era of the 1890s. (FF-017-044, FF-017-056)

Yet Verne withholds the weapon's triumph. The title, *Facing the Flag*, names the moment patriotism overrides genius: rather than let his countrymen fall to his own invention, Roch destroys it. The novel's verdict is that no super-weapon is worth the treason of turning it against one's own flag. (FF-017-074)
