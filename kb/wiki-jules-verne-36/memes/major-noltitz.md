# Major Noltitz

## Overview

He is introduced by his profession and rank: "a doctor in the Russian army, and they call him Major Noltitz" (ASC-004-032), one of the small company of through passengers the narrator first notes at table (ASC-004-047).

The correspondent warms to him at once — "I like this Major Noltitz," he confides, "and I hope to make his acquaintance very soon" (ASC-006-039).

## Role in the Story

He becomes the narrator's indispensable escort on the long transit — "my good fortune furnished me with a companion, or I should rather say a guide, in Major Noltitz" (ASC-007-002).

Their friendship is sealed by frank introduction: "I am a Frenchman, Claudius Bombarnac, special correspondent of the Twentieth Century, and you are Major Noltitz of the Russian army" (ASC-007-004), followed by the proposal that they "become more than mere traveling companions" (ASC-007-006).

As guide he unfolds the railway's history, reminding the correspondent "of the facts which had gradually prepared the conquest of Turkestan and its definite incorporation with the Russian" empire (ASC-007-017).

## Character & Traits

He is observant and shrewd, a match for the narrator's own curiosity — he "was as much struck as I was at the behavior of my lord Faruskiar" (ASC-015-070), and keeps a steady watch as danger nears, continuing "to watch Faruskiar and the Mongols" (ASC-020-006).

His judgment proves sound at the crisis, when he names the traitor outright: "the rascal who sent us on to the Nanking line, who would have hurled us into the Tjon valley, to walk off with the imperial treasure, is Faruskiar" (ASC-025-044).

His easy sociability sets the tone of the journey, quietly conversing with the captain among the hardy few "whom the sea has not affected" (ASC-004-087).

- Faruskiar — the imposing fellow-passenger whose treachery he watches and finally exposes.
