## Overview

The Coanza is one of the rivers of Angola in *Dick Sand: A Captain at Fifteen*, flowing into the Atlantic a hundred miles from the cape where the *Pilgrim* was wrecked. (DSCF-025-004) Its banks bore the slave caravans of the Benguela interior on the road inland to Kazounde. (DSCF-026-016)

## In the Narrative

Ten miles from the coastal forest, on the Coanza, a caravan of slaves lay encamped under the Arab Ibn Hamis. (DSCF-020-088) From this camp on the Coanza the convoy set out for Kazounde; eight days on, twenty had fallen by the way, prey to the beasts that prowled behind it. (DSCF-026-014) The camp was the point from which Dick Sand's forced march inland began. (DSCF-026-016)

## Geography & Features

The Coanza, its waters swelled by tributaries, overflowed during a night's storm. (DSCF-025-003) All the country east of the river was only forest for twenty miles together. (DSCF-026-012) Kazounde stands three hundred miles from the mouth of the Coanza. (DSCF-027-004) The river was crossed on the way through the immense forests of the war-devastated provinces. (DSCF-019-038)

## Connections

The Coanza belongs to *Dick Sand: A Captain at Fifteen* as the artery of Alvez's slave country, its right bank later skirted by David Livingstone, who followed it to the confluence of the Lombe and passed by Cassange. (DSCF-032-046) The camp on its banks lay within two hundred and fifty miles of the inland city on the road from Saint Paul de Loanda. (DSCF-025-036)
