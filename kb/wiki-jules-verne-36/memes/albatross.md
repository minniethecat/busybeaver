# Albatross

## Overview

The Albatross is the flying machine that [[Robur]] builds and commands in *[[robur-the-conqueror|Robur the Conqueror]]*. Verne calls it "this flying engine known as the 'Albatross,' which he was guiding through the currents of the atmosphere" (RC-007-007). It is an "aeronef—a name which can be exactly applied to the 'Albatross'" (RC-007-030).

The craft belongs to the "heavier than air" school of flight rather than to the balloons championed by the [[Weldon Institute]]. [[robur|Robur]] trusts entirely to screws: "the simpler his contrivance the better. And the screws... had sufficed for all the needs of his flying machine. One series could hold it suspended in the air, the other could drive it along" (RC-007-022).

## Design & Operation

The Albatross is built around a ship-like platform: "a framework a hundred feet long and twelve wide, a ship's deck in fact, with a projecting prow," its hull "enclosing the engines, stores, and provisions of all sorts" (RC-007-025).

Lift comes from a forest of vertical screws: "Above the deck rose thirty-seven vertical axes... The 'Albatross' might be called a clipper with thirty-seven masts." Each mast bore two horizontal screws, so the ship carried "seventy-four suspensory screws," with "two propelling screws, each with four arms" fore and aft (RC-007-026).

Its power source is electrical and secret. [[robur|Robur]] "employed electricity, that agent which one day will be the soul of the industrial world," drawing on "piles and accumulators" of "extraordinary strength" whose chemistry "[[robur|Robur]] only knew" (RC-007-027).

Even the material is unexpected: the hull no bowie-knife could scratch was "Simply paper!" (RC-007-030). It was "Unsized paper... squeezed in hydraulic presses," forming "a material as hard as steel" and, valuably for a high-flyer, "incombustible" (RC-007-031).

## Role in the Story

Manned by a crew of eight (RC-007-032), the Albatross carries its captives around the globe. Aloft "the two propellers were running very easily and driving the ship at about eleven knots an hour," while "the suspensory screws were rapidly spinning overhead" (RC-008-004).

[[robur|Robur]] treats the machine as a claim to dominion: "with her... I am master of the seventh part of the world... this aerial Icarian sea, which millions of Icarians will one day people" (RC-007-035).

Its redundancy is a point of pride. Asked about parachutes, "No. [[robur|Robur]] did not believe in accidents of that kind," for the screw axes were independent and "if only half were working, the 'Albatross' could still keep afloat in her natural element" (RC-007-034).

## Science & Speculation

Verne sets the Albatross at the end of a long, real catalogue of aviation pioneers, so that "on the top of" the "ladder of aerial locomotion... appeared [[robur-the-conqueror|Robur the Conqueror]]" (RC-007-007).

The craft dramatises the era's "heavier than air" versus balloon debate, and Verne sides with the winged camp: the aeroplane's "disposable weight... increases with the square of the speed. Herein the aeroplane has the advantage over the aerostat" (RC-007-021).

Its paper hull and secret accumulators extrapolate from genuine 1880s materials and battery research. [[robur|Robur]]'s later machine, the [[The Terror]], carries the same electric, multi-mode ambition further.
