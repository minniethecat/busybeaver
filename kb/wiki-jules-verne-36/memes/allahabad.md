## Overview

Allahabad is the town at which the interrupted Indian railway begins again, and the object of one of Fogg's most celebrated improvisations. The whole wager turns on it — only eighty days are possible now that the section between Rothal and Allahabad, on the Great Indian Peninsula Railway, has been opened. (AWED-003-028) Or so the newspapers claim.

## In the Narrative

The line, in fact, is not finished. "There's still a matter of fifty miles to be laid from here to Allahabad, where the line begins again," the conductor announces, stranding the passengers at Kholby. (AWED-011-026) The travellers must fend for themselves — the passengers know that they must provide means of transportation from Kholby to Allahabad. (AWED-011-030)

Fogg's answer is an elephant. "Sir Francis," said Mr. Fogg quietly, "we will, if you please, look about for some means of conveyance to Allahabad." (AWED-011-032) The beast is hired, and it is agreed to carry Sir Francis to Allahabad as well. (AWED-011-050)

## Geography & Features

A junction town on the Great Indian Peninsula Railway near the Ganges, Allahabad marks the western end of a fifty-mile gap in the line — the obstacle that sends Fogg's party across the forest of Bundelcund by elephant.
