## Overview

*The Ascent of Mont Blanc* is a first-person narrative in Jules Verne's collected works recounting a mountaineering expedition. The narrator "arrived at Chamonix on the 18th of August, 1871, fully decided to make the ascent of Mont Blanc, cost what it might" (AMB-001-002).

An earlier attempt in 1869 had failed when bad weather stopped him "beyond the Grands-Mulets" (AMB-001-002).

## Plot Summary

M. Balmat, "chief guide of Chamonix," at first counsels patience, then assigns the guide Edward Ravanel, who has climbed the peak once and is "not anxious to do it again" (AMB-001-005, AMB-001-013). Ravanel recounts a recent disaster in which two Englishmen and their guides were "blown off by the wind" and found frozen, "become blocks of ice" (AMB-001-017).

Won over, Ravanel agrees to guide him but asks to bring "my brother, Ambrose Ravanel, and my cousin, Gaspard Simon" (AMB-001-032). The narrator's friend M. Donatien Levesque resolves to join the climb to the summit, declaring, "You may count on me" (AMB-001-059).

The party crosses the Bossons glacier, threading "yawning and apparently bottomless crevasses" and the perilous seracs, roped together (AMB-001-043). Reaching the hut at the Grands-Mulets, they rest, then set out again at two in the morning by lantern (AMB-001-054, AMB-001-073).

At the Grand-Plateau, "eleven thousand eight hundred feet high," they choose to attack an ice-wall, cutting "more than four hundred steps in this terrible mass" to gain the summit of the Corridor (AMB-001-078, AMB-001-093). Battling the rarefied air, "after two hours of superhuman efforts," at fifteen minutes past noon, "Mont Blanc was under our feet!" (AMB-001-100, AMB-001-102).

From the highest mountain in Europe the view spans "sixty leagues," with a "sea of clouds" below whence peaks emerge like an archipelago (AMB-001-109, AMB-001-110). But descent is the harder task: the Corridor proving impassable, they must go down the same ice-wall, and untie from M. N----'s failing party lest "they slip, they will drag us after them" (AMB-001-117, AMB-001-122).

Near the Petit-Plateau an "immense avalanche of ice" has buried their morning tracks (AMB-001-132). Forced beneath an overhanging ice-mass, they run the deadly passage at their guides' urging and reach safety, regaining the Grands-Mulets by five o'clock (AMB-001-134, AMB-001-139).

Back at Chamonix, English tourists rush to congratulate them, one lady begging, "Let me touch your alpenstocks!" (AMB-001-143, AMB-001-144). The narrator closes by quoting the mountaineer M. Markham Sherwell on the doubtful rewards of the climb (AMB-001-146).

## Main Characters

- The narrator — the determined traveller who ascends Mont Blanc at his second attempt
- Donatien Levesque — his friend, who resolves to press on to the summit
- Edward Ravanel — the reluctant chief guide who leads the climb
- Ambrose Ravanel and Gaspard Simon — Edward's brother and cousin, guides on the expedition
- M. Balmat — the chief guide of Chamonix who authorizes the ascent

## Key Places & Technology

- Chamonix — the alpine town from which the expedition sets out
- The Bossons glacier and the Grands-Mulets — the crevassed route and the hut where climbers shelter
- The Grand-Plateau and the Corridor ice-wall — the high plateau and the steep wall scaled to the summit

## Themes

Verne renders mountaineering as a contest of will against a beautiful and lethal nature, where "the heart, responding 'Excelsior!'" drives an exhausted body upward (AMB-001-100). The narrative dwells on the peril as much as the glory, its litany of frozen victims and falling avalanches shadowing the triumph of reaching Europe's highest summit.
