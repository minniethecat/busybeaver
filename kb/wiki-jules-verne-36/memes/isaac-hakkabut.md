# Isaac Hakkabut

## Overview

He is introduced as a Mediterranean trader of German origin, for "His name was Isaac Hakkabut, and he was a native of Cologne" (OC-019-051).

He owns and dwells aboard a small vessel that doubles as a floating shop, since "Isaac Hakkabut lived almost entirely on board the _Hansa_, as he had named his tartan" (OC-019-051).

Swept into space when the comet Gallia carries off a fragment of Earth, his laden ship keeps him a solitary outsider whose companions propose to "see what kind of a life the misanthrope leads on board the _Hansa_" (OC-031-029).

## Role in the Story

His hold is a lifeline for the stranded colony, packed with "sugar-loaves by hundreds, chests of tea, bags of coffee, hogsheads of tobacco, pipes of wine, casks of brandy, barrels of dried herrings, bales of cotton" (OC-020-024).

Even amid the catastrophe he presses his debts, insisting to the governor that the Spaniards "owe me twenty reals apiece for their passage here; they owe me two hundred reals" (OC-020-038).

He schemes to drain the little world of coin, resolving that "he set his heart on getting all the monetary wealth of Gallia into his possession, and to do this he must sell his goods" (OC-030-010).

Lending out a steelyard, he exacts a usurer's terms — "Deposit in gold money a hundred francs, and twenty francs for the hire" (OC-032-038) — and privately gloats over some "eighteen hundred per cent. interest" (OC-032-078).

His swindling is undone when a coffee-weighing trap exposes his rigged balance and the cry breaks out, "The old reprobate, the rascal has cheated us!" (OC-040-083).

## Character & Traits

The narrative frames him through the antisemitic "usurer" caricature common to its period, dismissing the fate of the moon as no concern "to an old usurer like him" (OC-020-018).

His avarice is written as incurable, for "the desire of gain was too thoroughly ingrained into his hard nature ever to be eradicated" (OC-030-009).

His whole fortune rested on fraud, since from his first venture "his dealings had all been carried on by a system of false weight" (OC-041-001), shorting every customer by a quarter.

He fondles his coin like a lover, drawing "a small piece of gold from his pocket" and pressing "it to his lips" (OC-040-068).

Yet cornered aboard the escape balloon, "the avaricious old man was found to value his life above his money" and gave up his hidden money-girdle at last (OC-043-071).

## Relationships

- [[Hector Servadac]] — the French officer whose protection he relies upon and who must trade with him for supplies, for Servadac "resolved, according to his promise, to apply to the Jew and become a purchaser" (OC-035-007).

- [[Ben Zoof]] — Servadac's orderly, who despises him and never wearies of warning that on returning to the world "he would be prosecuted for using false weights" (OC-041-004).

- [[Palmyrin Rosette]] — the astronomer who regards him with "the greatest repugnance and contempt" (OC-040-025) and, on exposing the false balance, "rushed at the Jew, had seized him by the throat, and was shaking him till he was black in the face" (OC-040-078).

## Appearances

- [[Off on a Comet]] — throughout which he clings to his hoard until, doubly ruined by the loss of his tartan and his fortune, "he disappeared entirely from the scene" (OC-045-021).
