# Off on a Comet — CHAPTER IV. A CONVULSION OF NATURE

[OC-005-001]Whence came it that at that very moment the horizon underwent so strange and sudden a modification, that the eye of the most practiced mariner could not distinguish between sea and sky?

[OC-005-002]Whence came it that the billows raged and rose to a height hitherto unregistered in the records of science?

[OC-005-003]Whence came it that the elements united in one deafening crash; that the earth groaned as though the whole framework of the globe were ruptured; that the waters roared from their innermost depths; that the air shrieked with all the fury of a cyclone?

[OC-005-004]Whence came it that a radiance, intenser than the effulgence of the Northern Lights, overspread the firmament, and momentarily dimmed the splendor of the brightest stars?

[OC-005-005]Whence came it that the Mediterranean, one instant emptied of its waters, was the next flooded with a foaming surge?

[OC-005-006]Whence came it that in the space of a few seconds the moon's disc reached a magnitude as though it were but a tenth part of its ordinary distance from the earth?

[OC-005-007]Whence came it that a new blazing spheroid, hitherto unknown to astronomy, now appeared suddenly in the firmament, though it were but to lose itself immediately behind masses of accumulated cloud?

[OC-005-008]What phenomenon was this that had produced a cataclysm so tremendous in effect upon earth, sky, and sea?

[OC-005-009]Was it possible that a single human being could have survived the convulsion? and if so, could he explain its mystery?
