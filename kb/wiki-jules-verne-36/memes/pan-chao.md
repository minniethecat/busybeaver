# Pan Chao

## Overview

He is marked from the first as a young man of means, for the narrator judges "Pan-Chao belongs to some rich family, for he is accompanied by his doctor" (ASC-006-044).

To the narrator's delight the young traveller proves cultivated in a Western tongue: "and so Pan Chao speaks French" (ASC-008-094).

## Role in the Story

A shared curiosity draws them together, for "Pan Chao knows I am on the staff of the Twentieth Century, and he is apparently as desirous of talking to me as I am of talking to him" (ASC-011-018).

Fluent in the country's speech, "the complaisant Pan-Chao offers to be my interpreter" among the Chinese travellers (ASC-022-061).

When the train is stormed he shows his mettle, and "Pan-Chao also exposed himself bravely, a smile on his lips, gallantly leading on the other Chinese passengers" (ASC-020-033).

He lends his tongue to the ceremonies too, delivering words "which Pan-Chao translated to us, he thanked Faruskiar, complimented him" (ASC-023-046).

## Character & Traits

His years abroad have all but remade him, so that "Pan Chao reminds me more of Paris and France than of Pekin and China" (ASC-017-008).

The narrator's regard is open and warm: "I looked at Pan Chao; I admired him" (ASC-011-036).

He is a man of the modern age, and "Pan Chao seemed to me well posted up in their progress" of science and invention (ASC-011-038).

## Relationships

- [[Claudius Bombarnac]] — the special correspondent who seeks him out, confessing "I want to enter into conversation with Pan Chao" (ASC-009-003).
- [[Major Noltitz]] — a fellow member of the narrator's circle, at whose side "I am near Major Noltitz, who asks young Pan Chao" a question in company (ASC-014-072).

## Appearances

- [[The Adventures of a Special Correspondent]] — as the narrator's most sympathetic number, "young Pan Chao, who replies with very Parisian pleasantry" throughout the journey to Pekin (ASC-014-033).
