## Overview

Nitroglycerine is the liquid explosive that the engineer [[Cyrus Harding]] manufactures from first principles on [[lincoln-island|Lincoln Island]] in *[[the-mysterious-island|The Mysterious Island]]*. Lacking gunpowder, Harding turns to chemistry to obtain a blasting agent capable of splitting the granite of [[granite-house|Granite House]], choosing nitroglycerine over the [[Gun-Cotton]] he could also have prepared. (MI-017-055)(MI-017-058)

## Design & Operation

Harding first produces the reagent acids, distilling azotic (nitric) acid from saltpeter treated with sulphuric acid. (MI-017-050) He then combines the acid with glycerine:

> mixed the azotic acid with the glycerine ... an oily yellow mixture (MI-017-053)

Knowing the danger, he carried out the reaction alone, fearing an explosion. (MI-017-054) The result he announces plainly:

> Here is nitro-glycerine! (MI-017-055)

Its destructive power he estimates against the familiar standard of gunpowder:

> its explosive power is perhaps tenfold that of ordinary powder (MI-017-056)

The compound is treacherously sensitive: it detonates not by flame but by shock, exploding under the blow of a hammer. (MI-017-064)(MI-017-066)(MI-017-067)

## Role in the Story

Harding prepares roughly seven quarts of the liquid (MI-017-063) to blast open a granite outcrop and drain a lake, work no ordinary powder could accomplish against such hard rock. (MI-017-058) When applied, the charge acts powerfully, tearing the granite exactly as the engineer intended. (MI-018-003)

## Science & Speculation

Verne's chemistry is essentially correct: nitroglycerine is genuinely made by nitrating glycerine with a mix of nitric and sulphuric acids, and its shock-sensitivity — detonation by percussion rather than ignition — is its real and defining hazard, the property Alfred Nobel later tamed with dynamite in 1867. The novel's "tenfold ordinary powder" is a fair order-of-magnitude figure. Where the [[Gun-Cotton]] entry shows Verne's interest in nitrocellulose, nitroglycerine demonstrates the same castaway-as-industrial-chemist theme, extrapolated from real 1860s explosives science.
