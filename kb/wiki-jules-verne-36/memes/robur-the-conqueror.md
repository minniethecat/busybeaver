## Overview

*Robur the Conqueror* is Jules Verne's great aviation romance, pitting the visionary engineer [[Robur]] against the balloon-devoted [[Weldon Institute]] of Philadelphia. [[robur|Robur]] champions heavier-than-air flight, staking his creed on his airship the [[Albatross]].

He proclaims that man shall become "master of atmospherical space by apparatus heavier than the air" (RC-004-015) — a doctrine the Institute's members flatly reject.

## Plot Summary

[[robur|Robur]] bursts into the Institute's hall and, mocked, abducts its president and secretary aboard the [[albatross|Albatross]], "a framework a hundred feet long" lifted by dozens of screws and manned by a crew of eight (RC-007-032).

He carries his captives around the world to prove the superiority of the aeroplane over the balloon. The prisoners escape and complete their rival balloon, the *Go-Ahead*, only for [[robur|Robur]] to reappear and vanish — his ambition carried further in the sequel *[[Master of the World]]*, where he builds the machine [[The Terror]].

## Main Characters

- [[Robur]] — the defiant engineer and master of the [[albatross|Albatross]]
- Uncle Prudent — president of the [[weldon-institute|Weldon Institute]] and [[robur|Robur]]'s chief antagonist
- Phil Evans — the Institute's secretary, abducted alongside Prudent

## Key Places & Technology

- [[Albatross]] — [[robur|Robur]]'s heavier-than-air flying machine
- [[Weldon Institute]] — the Philadelphia balloon club [[robur|Robur]] challenges

## Themes

The novel dramatizes the historic contest between "lighter than air" and "heavier than air" flight, and Verne sides emphatically with the winged machine. In [[robur|Robur]] he sketches the ambivalent genius — a benefactor of science whose pride shades into menace, fully realized only in the sequel.
