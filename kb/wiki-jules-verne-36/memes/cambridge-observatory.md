## Overview

The Cambridge Observatory is the astronomical establishment at Cambridge in Massachusetts, the scientific authority consulted by the Baltimore [[Gun Club]] once [[Impey Barbicane]] proposed to fire a projectile at the Moon.

When the club required precise answers on lunar motion and distance, it drew up a note of special questions and addressed it directly to the observatory. (FEM-004-003) Its staff met at once and replied over the signature of the Director. (FEM-004-006)

## Role in the Story

The observatory's reply, its "memorable letter of October 7th," settled the astronomical basis of the whole enterprise, treating the question from a purely astronomical point of view before the mechanical problem could be tackled. (FEM-007-002)

The Director's letter told the members of the Gun Club when and where the shot must be fired, recommending that the gun be aimed perpendicularly to the plane of the horizon, toward the zenith. (FEM-011-002) It laid out all that could be said of the height the Moon attains and the ellipse of her revolution. (FEM-006-011)

The scientific journals reprinted and commented on the letter, and the observatory's authority carried the debate; its answers became the reference every commentator cited. (FEM-006-003)

## Members

The observatory acts through its Director and staff, who put themselves "entirely at their disposition for questions of theoretic astronomy" on the club's behalf. (FEM-004-035) The correspondence runs between the unnamed Director and the Gun Club's president [[Impey Barbicane]], with the club's secretary [[J.T. Maston]] pressing the astronomers for figures.

The Director closes the exchange by summarising the answers to every question the [[Gun Club]] had proposed. (FEM-004-027)
