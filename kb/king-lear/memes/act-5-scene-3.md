# Act 5 Scene 3: The Final Tragedy

The final scene: [[edmund|Edmund]]'s forces are defeated, [[edgar|Edgar]] defeats [[edmund|Edmund]] in single combat, [[goneril|Goneril]] and [[regan|Regan]] die by poison and suicide, [[cordelia|Cordelia]] is hanged, and [[king-lear|Lear]] dies holding her body -- the catastrophic ending that centuries of audiences and critics have found almost unbearable.

## Summary

[[edmund|Edmund]] enters with [[king-lear|Lear]] and [[cordelia|Cordelia]] as prisoners (005-099) and secretly orders the [[captain|Captain]] to hang them in prison. [[duke-of-albany|Albany]], [[goneril|Goneril]], and [[regan|Regan]] arrive (005-144); [[duke-of-albany|Albany]] accuses [[edmund|Edmund]] of treason. A herald summons a champion, and [[edgar|Edgar]] appears in armor, defeats [[edmund|Edmund]] in single combat (005-269). [[edgar|Edgar]] reveals his [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|identity]] and recounts his and [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s story. [[regan|Regan]] falls sick from [[goneril|Goneril]]'s poison; [[goneril|Goneril]] exits and kills her[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]. The dying [[edmund|Edmund]], moved by [[edgar|Edgar]]'s account, attempts to revoke the order to kill [[cordelia|Cordelia]]. The reprieve is too late: [[king-lear|Lear]] enters carrying [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s body (005-358). He howls his grief -- "Howle, howle, howle" (005-382) -- and dies believing she still breathes (005-444). [[duke-of-albany|Albany]] surrenders [[authority-and-power-in-king-lear|rule]] to Kent and [[edgar|Edgar]], who are left to "sustaine" the ruin.

## Structure

### Opening

The scene opens with [[edmund|Edmund]]'s triumph: he enters "with Drumme and Colours, [[king-lear|Lear]], and [[cordelia|Cordelia]], as prisoners" (005-099). [[king-lear|Lear]]'s speech to [[cordelia|Cordelia]] imagines their prison as a paradise removed from the world's cruelty: "Vpon such sacrifices my [[cordelia|Cordelia]], / The Gods themselues throw Incense" (005-121). [[edmund|Edmund]]'s secret order to the [[captain|Captain]] -- to kill them immediately -- extinguishes this hope before it can take root.

### Development

The political resolution unfolds through a series of confrontations. [[duke-of-albany|Albany]] enters with [[goneril|Goneril]] and [[regan|Regan]] (005-144), arrests [[edmund|Edmund]] for treason, and challenges him. A herald calls for a champion; the trumpet sounds (005-269), and [[edgar|Edgar]] appears in armor. [[edgar|Edgar]] defeats [[edmund|Edmund]] in single combat, revealing his [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|identity]]: "My name is [[edgar|Edgar]] and thy Fathers Sonne" (005-295). [[edgar|Edgar]] recounts how he led the blind [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] to Dover, revealed him[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]] at the last, and how [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s heart "Burst smilingly" (005-305) at the revelation.

The sister-plot collapses simultaneously: [[regan|Regan]] sickens from [[goneril|Goneril]]'s poison; [[goneril|Goneril]] exits and dies by her own hand. The dying [[edmund|Edmund]], momentarily moved, confesses the order to kill [[cordelia|Cordelia]]: "I pant for life. Some good I meane to do / Dispight of mine owne [[nature-in-king-lear|Nature]]" (005-343). [[edgar|Edgar]] reveals that [[regan|Regan]] is "by her poyson'd" (005-343). The reprieve for [[cordelia|Cordelia]] is dispatched too late.

### Closing

[[king-lear|Lear]] enters carrying [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s body, and the scene dissolves into pure tragedy. His howl -- "Howle, howle, howle: O you are men of stones" (005-382) -- is one of the most devastating cries in all drama. He searches for signs of life: "Lend me a Looking-glasse. / If that her breath will mist or staine the stone, / Why then she liues" (005-387). A feather stirs -- or [[king-lear|Lear]] imagines it does. He dies believing she lives: "Do you see this? Looke on her? Looke her lips, / Looke there, looke there" (005-444). Kent tries to follow [[king-lear|Lear]] into death: "I haue a iourney sir, shortly to go, / My Master cals me, I must not say no" (005-449). [[duke-of-albany|Albany]] hands authority to Kent and [[edgar|Edgar]], and [[edgar|Edgar]] speaks the play's final, exhausted words: "The oldest hath borne most, we that are yong, / Shall neuer see so much, nor liue so long" (005-453).

## Thematic Connections

The final scene brings all the play's themes to their terrible conclusion. The nothingness motif returns with devastating literalism: [[king-lear|Lear]]'s [[britain|kingdom]], family, and sanity are all gone, and now [[cordelia|Cordelia]] -- "she's gone for euer" (005-384) -- reduces him to pure grief. The justice question is resolved only negatively: the villains die, but so do the innocent. [[king-lear|Lear]]'s delusion that [[cordelia|Cordelia]] breathes (005-444) is the play's final, cruelest ambiguity: is it mercy that he dies believing she lives, or is it the ultimate evidence of his madness and the universe's indifference? The scene asserts no moral order, only the bare fact of survival: [[edgar|Edgar]] and [[duke-of-albany|Albany]] remain, tasked with the impossible work of rebuilding.

## Dramatic Significance

This is the longest and most relentlessly eventful scene in Shakespearean tragedy. It compresses military victory, single combat, poison, suicide, execution, multiple [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|identity]] revelations, and the play's two most important deaths into a single continuous action. The triple death -- [[goneril|Goneril]], [[regan|Regan]], [[cordelia|Cordelia]] -- represents the total collapse of [[king-lear|Lear]]'s family line. The scene's power lies in its refusal to provide redemption: [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s death is needless, [[king-lear|Lear]]'s death is pure grief, and the survivors' language of restoration ("The weight of this sad time we must obey") feels desperately inadequate against the scale of what has been lost.

## Textual Notes

The Folio and Quarto texts diverge significantly in this scene. Q gives the final speech to [[duke-of-albany|Albany]]; F assigns it to [[edgar|Edgar]]. The surviving promptbook tradition follows F, and most modern editions retain [[edgar|Edgar]] as the final speaker. The Folio also includes [[king-lear|Lear]]'s "Looke there, looke there" (005-444), one of the most debated lines in the canon: does [[king-lear|Lear]] see [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s lips move, or is he hallucinating as he dies?
