# Act 1 Scene 5: Lear Sends Kent to Regan

[[king-lear|Lear]] dispatches [[kent-earl-of-kent|Kent]] with letters to [[regan|Regan]] while the [[the-fool|Fool]] sharpens his barbs, and Lear's first explicit fears of madness surface — a turning point that marks the beginning of his psychological dissolution.

## Summary

Lear sends [[kent-earl-of-kent|Kent]] ahead to [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] with letters, commanding him to say nothing to [[regan|Regan]] beyond what she demands from the letter (001-683). [[kent-earl-of-kent|Kent]] vows not to sleep until the letter is delivered. Left alone with the [[the-fool|Fool]], Lear endures a series of biting jests: the Fool warns that Regan will prove as like Goneril "as a Crabbe's like an Apple" (001-687) and that Lear should not have grown old before growing wise. Lear, stung, mutters "I did her wrong" — his first admission of injustice toward [[cordelia|Cordelia]]. He then erupts at "Monster Ingratitude!" and makes his desperate plea: "O let me not be mad, not mad sweet Heaven: keepe me in temper, I would not be mad" (001-687). The Fool closes with a cryptic couplet about maids and departure.

## Dramatic Significance

This short scene — a mere breath between the confrontation with Goneril and the arrival at Regan's — contains one of the play's most important psychological turning points. "I did her wrong" is Lear's first acknowledgment of his injustice to Cordelia, a crack in the defensive armor of the old king's self-righteousness. The admission is muttered, almost to himself, among his complaints about Goneril, as if he cannot bear to dwell on it.

The plea "O let me not be mad" introduces the play's central concern with psychological dissolution. Lear does not say "I am not mad" but "let me not be mad" — a prayer rather than a denial. He knows, at some level, that madness is coming, and his prayer is for preservation, for "temper" (sanity as temperature regulation, the mind kept in its proper climate). The fact that this prayer will not be answered — that the play's trajectory is toward the very madness Lear fears — gives the line its terrible dramatic irony.

The Fool's crab-apple simile — Regan will taste as like Goneril "as a Crabbe's like an Apple" — is one of his most effective metaphors. The crab apple looks like an apple but tastes sour; Regan will look like a loving daughter but prove as bitter as her sister. The Fool's wisdom is always sensory, grounded in taste and touch rather than abstraction, and this groundedness makes his truths all the more devastating.

## Textual Note

The Fool's closing couplet — "She that's a Maid now, and laughs at my departure, / Shall not be a Maid long, vnlesse things be cut shorter" — is one of the most enigmatic passages in the play. Some editors assign it to Lear rather than the Fool. Its bawdy suggestion seems out of place at this moment of gathering darkness, but it may function as a deliberate, jarring release of tension before the play's next descent.
