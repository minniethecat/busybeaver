# Nothing and Nihilism in King Lear

"No thing" is the first substantive exchange of *[[king-lear|King Lear]]* — and its last word, spoken through silence, is the same. Between [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s "Nothing, my Lord" (001-077) and [[king-lear|Lear]]'s fivefold "Never" over her dead body (005-280), the word "nothing" tolls through the play like a bell, accumulating meanings until it names the ground of the play's universe.

## Overview

The word "nothing" and its variants appear over thirty times in *[[king-lear|King Lear]]*. It functions simultaneously as a linguistic tic (the [[the-fool|Fool]]'s mockery), a philosophical principle ([[edmund|Edmund]]'s nihilism), an economic condition ([[edgar|Edgar]] reduced to "nothing"), and an existential abyss ([[king-lear|Lear]]'s final vision of a world without justice or meaning). The play's genius is to refuse resolution: it does not tell us whether "nothing" is the terrifying [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|truth]] beneath all human pretension, or whether [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s "nothing" — her refusal to perform love for political gain — contains a redemptive fullness that the play's busy "somethings" cannot reach.

## Key Manifestations

### The Love Test — Nothing Spoken, Everything Lost

The motif is inaugurated in the play's opening scene. [[king-lear|Lear]] demands that [[cordelia|Cordelia]] speak her love: "What can you say to draw / A third, more opulent than your sisters?" She answers: "Nothing, my Lord" (001-077). [[king-lear|Lear]]'s reply — "[[nothing-will-come-of-nothing|Nothing will come of nothing, speak again]]e" (001-078) — is a proverbial truism that the play will spend five acts demolishing. [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s "nothing" is not emptiness but refusal: she will not convert love into currency, will not perform [[love-in-king-lear|affection]] for political reward. [[king-lear|Lear]] reads her silence as negation; the play suggests it is integrity.

### The Fool's Arithmetic of Nothing

[[the-fool|The Fool]] picks up the word and turns it into a running joke at [[king-lear|Lear]]'s expense. "Can you make no use of nothing, Nuncle?" (001-505). "Thou art an O without a figure. I am better than thou art now; I am a [[the-fool|Fool]], thou art nothing" (001-509). [[the-fool|The Fool]]'s wordplay has a deadly serious undertow: [[king-lear|Lear]], having given away his [[britain|kingdom]], has literally become "nothing" in the political calculus — a king without a crown, a father without authority, a man without a place in the order he shattered.

### Edgar as "Nothing"

[[edgar|Edgar]]'s descent into [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|Poor Tom]] is a reduction to bare existence. "[[edgar|Edgar]] I nothing am" (002-176), he declares as he strips off his clothes and [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|identity]]. On the heath, [[king-lear|Lear]] sees in [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|Poor Tom]] the image of humanity stripped to its essence: "Is man no more than this? ... Thou ow'st the worm no silk, the beast no hide, the sheep no wool, the cat no perfume. Ha! Here's three of us are sophisticated; thou art the thing it[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]. Unaccommodated man is no more but such a poor, bare, forked animal as thou art" (003-340). This is the play's most radical vision: the human [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|being]], stripped of clothing, rank, property, and reason, is "nothing" — and that nothingness is both terrifying and liberating.

### Gloucester's Nihilism and Recovery

[[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s philosophy darkens into nihilism after his blinding. He declares the [[fortune-and-fate-in-king-lear|gods]] indifferent: "As flies to wanton boys are we to the [[fortune-and-fate-in-king-lear|gods]]; / They kill us for their sport" (004-020). His attempted suicide at Dover is the logical end of this vision — if life is meaningless, death is the only rational choice. [[edgar|Edgar]]'s fiction of the miraculous preservation ("Thy life's a miracle" — 004-405) is a necessary lie: it restores [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s will to live, but the play has already shown us a universe in which no such miracle occurs.

### The Final "Never"

The play's catastrophe — [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s death — is the ultimate "nothing." [[king-lear|Lear]]'s fivefold "Never" over her body ("Never, never, never, never, never" — 005-280) is the word "nothing" stretched across five syllables, the negation of all hope, all meaning, all future. Kent's "Is this the promised end?" (005-273) frames the moment as eschatological: the end of the world, or the end of meaning. The play offers no consolation. [[king-lear|Lear]] dies believing [[cordelia|Cordelia]] lives; the audience knows better.

## Dramatic Development

The arc of "nothing" is the arc of the play. **Act 1**: [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s "nothing" sets [[act-5-scene-3|the catastrophe]] in motion; [[edmund|Edmund]]'s forged letter (created from nothing) initiates the subplot. [[the-fool|The Fool]] teaches [[king-lear|Lear]] the grammar of negation. **Act 2**: [[edgar|Edgar]] reduces him[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]] to "nothing" to survive. [[king-lear|Lear]] confronts the possibility that his own [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|identity]] might be "nothing" without his retinue. **Act 3**: The storm strips [[king-lear|Lear]] to "unaccommodated man" — the human [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|being]] as "nothing." The mock trial scene stages justice as farce, exposing the "nothing" at the heart of legal authority. **Act 4**: [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s nihilism ("As flies to wanton boys") is the darkest articulation of cosmic indifference. Yet [[king-lear|Lear]]'s mad speech on the heath — "When we are born, we cry that we are come / To this great stage of fools" (004-575) — converts nothingness into a kind of terrible wisdom. **Act 5**: [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s death is the play's answer to [[king-lear|Lear]]'s opening "Nothing will come of nothing." What comes of [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s "nothing" is not "nothing" but the most devastating "something" the stage had ever shown.

## Critical Perspectives

Samuel Johnson famously could not bear to read [[act-5-scene-3|the final scene]] because [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s death violated [[justice-and-injustice-in-king-lear|poetic justice]]. The Romantics (Lamb, Hazlitt) saw in [[king-lear|Lear]]'s madness a sublime transcendence of the merely rational. More recently, existentialist readings have framed [[king-lear|Lear]] as a precursor to Beckett — a play that stages the confrontation with meaninglessness without flinching. [[the-fool|The Fool]]'s dis[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|appearance]] after Act 3 has been read as the point at which [[king-lear|Lear]] internalizes the "nothing" the [[the-fool|Fool]] represented: he no longer needs an external voice of negation because he has become it.
