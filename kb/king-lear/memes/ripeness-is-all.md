# "Ripeness is all"

As the tragedy hurtles toward its catastrophic conclusion, [[edgar|Edgar]] — disguised, battle-weary, and guiding his blinded father — utters five words of Stoic acceptance that have resonated across four centuries.

## Full Text

> **Edgar.** Away old man, giue me thy hand, away:
> King Lear hath lost, he and his Daughter tane,
> Giue me thy hand: Come on.
> （005-088–005-090）

> **Gloucester.** No further Sir, a man may rot euen heere.
> (005-091)

> **Edgar.** What in ill thoughts againe?
> Men must endure
> Their going hence, euen as their comming hither,
> Ripenesse is all come on.
> （005-092–005-095）

> **Gloucester.** And that's true too.
> (005-096)

## Context

Act 5 Scene 2 is a scene of aftermath. The battle between the British forces (led by [[edmund|Edmund]], [[goneril|Goneril]], and [[regan|Regan]]) and the French invasion (supporting [[king-lear|Lear]]) is over. A brief alarum sounds; [[edgar|Edgar]] enters, having fled the field. He tells [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] the devastating news: [[king-lear|Lear]] has lost, and he and [[cordelia|Cordelia]] are captured. [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]], who has been led through the play's second half by his disguised son, collapses into despair: "No further Sir, a man may rot euen heere."

[[edgar|Edgar]]'s reply — "Men must endure / Their going hence, euen as their comming hither, / Ripenesse is all come on" — is a compressed philosophy of life. We come into the world without our consent, and we must leave it the same way. What matters is not when we die, but whether we are _ripe_ — ready, fully lived, complete — when the moment arrives.(005-095)

## Speaker

**[[edgar|Edgar]]](edgar)**

Legitimate son of [[earl-of-gloucester|the Earl]] of [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] and brother to the villainous [[edmund|Edmund]], [[edgar|Edgar]] has spent most of the play in disguise as "[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|Poor Tom]]" — a mad beggar. He has witnessed his father's blinding, endured [[the-heath|the storm]] on the heath, and led [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] through a mock-suicide at Dover Cliff. By Act 5 Scene 2, [[edgar|Edgar]] has transformed from a naive nobleman into the play's moral centre. His Stoic philosophy — "Ripenesse is all" — is hard-won wisdom, not abstract principle. He has learned to endure through suffering, and now he teaches his father the same lesson.

## Analysis

### Literal Meaning

Human [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|being]]s have no choice about [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|being]] born ("their comming hither") and no choice about dying ("their going hence"). What matters is not the timing or circumstances of death, but whether one is _ripe_ — prepared, complete, at peace — when it comes. "Come on" is both a physical instruction (keep walking, let us leave this place) and a philosophical acceptance (whatever comes, let it come).

### Thematic Significance

"Ripenesse is all" is the play's answer to the question of how to face mortality. Shakespeare contrasts three responses to death and suffering across _[[king-lear|King Lear]]_:

- **[[king-lear|Lear]]** rages against it — "Howle, howle, howle" — and cannot accept [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s death or his own complicity.
- **[[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]** tries to control death through suicide at Dover, only to be tricked into life by [[edgar|Edgar]].
- **[[edgar|Edgar]]** accepts death as part of a natural process: the fruit falls when it is ripe, and human life should be no different.

The metaphor of ripeness draws on the natural imagery that pervades the play — but here the natural world offers not brutality but wisdom. Against [[edmund|Edmund]]'s competitive, Darwinian nature, [[edgar|Edgar]] proposes a nature of organic completeness and acceptance.

### Dramatic Function

This tiny exchange — barely seven lines — is the play's philosophical climax. It comes between the battle's [[order-and-chaos-in-king-lear|chaos]] and the prison scene's transcendent tenderness. [[edgar|Edgar]]'s Stoicism provides a frame through which to understand everything that follows: the reunion of [[king-lear|Lear]] and [[cordelia|Cordelia]], [[edmund|Edmund]]'s death, and the final catastrophe. Without this moment of stillness and wisdom, the play's ending would be unbearable; with it, the ending becomes what it is — not merely tragic but _ripe_.

## Allusions & Echoes

- **[[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s earlier despair** — "As flies to wanton boys are we to the [[fortune-and-fate-in-king-lear|gods]]; they kill us for their sport" — is the position [[edgar|Edgar]] now counters. [[edgar|Edgar]] offers not theological consolation but Stoic acceptance.(003-294)
- **[[edgar|Edgar]]'s own endurance** — The "[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|Poor Tom]]" disguise, [[the-heath|the storm]], the mock-suicide, and the final duel with [[edmund|Edmund]] form a long arc of suffering that culminates in this philosophy. [[edgar|Edgar]] is the character who most fully enacts the principle he states.
- **[[king-lear|Lear]]'s "Come let's away to prison"** speech that follows immediately (005-097 onward) echoes [[edgar|Edgar]]'s acceptance — [[king-lear|Lear]] and [[cordelia|Cordelia]] will "sing like birds i'th' cage" and "take upon's the mystery of things" — the same spirit of embracing what cannot be changed.

## Performance Notes

Directors have staged this moment in strikingly different ways. Trevor Nunn (RSC, 2007, with Ian McKellen as [[king-lear|Lear]]) placed [[edgar|Edgar]] and [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] on a near-empty stage with rubble, emphasizing the intimacy of the father-son bond. Sam Mendes (National Theatre, 2014, with Simon Russell Beale) staged it against the distant noise of battle, the philosophical quiet contrasting with offstage violence. The line's power often depends on the actor playing [[edgar|Edgar]] choosing simplicity over declamation — it is a whisper of wisdom, not a proclamation.
