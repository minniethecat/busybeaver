## Overview

[[king-lear|The King]] of France appears in only one scene, but his [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|role]] is crucial: he is the first character in the play to see clearly. While [[king-lear|Lear]] and [[duke-of-burgundy|Burgundy]] value [[cordelia|Cordelia]] by her dowry, France values her for her[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]] — "Thee and thy virtues here I seize upon" (001-231) — and his willingness to marry her without a dowry is the play's first act of genuine valuation. His recognition of [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s worth when she has been stripped of everything anticipates the play's central concern with what remains when worldly goods are removed.

France's decision to marry [[cordelia|Cordelia]] is also politically significant: it provides [[cordelia|Cordelia]] with an army, and her return to [[britain|Britain]] at the head of French forces sets the play's final catastrophe in motion. France him[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]] is notably absent from the invasion — Shakespeare writes him out, leaving [[cordelia|Cordelia]] to lead alone — and his absence intensifies the play's focus on the daughter's relationship with her father.

## Key Scenes

### The Marriage Decision (001-197)

France's response to [[king-lear|Lear]]'s disinheritance of [[cordelia|Cordelia]] is the play's first act of moral clarity. "This is most strange," he says to [[king-lear|Lear]], "that she whom even but now was your best object, / The argument of your praise, balm of your age, / Should in this trice of time commit a thing / So monstrous" (001-203). His bafflement is the audience's bafflement; his willingness to marry [[cordelia|Cordelia]] anyway — "[[love-in-king-lear|Love]]'s not love / When it is mingled with regards that stand / Aloof from th' entire point" (001-228) — is the play's first assertion that true value is intrinsic, not assigned.

## Notable Speeches

> "Thee and thy virtues here I seize upon" (001-231)

France's declaration to [[cordelia|Cordelia]] is the play's first genuine act of love — a commitment made not in exchange for territory but in recognition of worth. The verb "seize" suggests both passion and possession: France claims [[cordelia|Cordelia]] as a prize, but his prize is her virtue, not her dowry.
