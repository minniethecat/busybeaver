# "Nothing will come of nothing"

In the opening scene of _[[king-lear|King Lear]]_, the aged king demands a public display of [[love-in-king-lear|affection]] from his three daughters as the price of their inheritance. When his youngest and favourite, [[cordelia|Cordelia]], can only say "[[nothing-and-nihilism-in-king-lear|Nothing]], my Lord," [[king-lear|Lear]] utters the line that sets the entire tragedy in motion.

## Full Text

> **Lear.** Nothing will come of nothing, speake againe.
> (001-078)

The preceding exchange:

> **Cordelia.** Nothing my Lord.
> (001-075)

> **Lear.** Nothing?
> (001-076)

> **Cordelia.** Nothing.
> (001-077)

## Context

The scene is [[king-lear|Lear]]'s court, where the King has announced his plan to divide his [[britain|kingdom]] among his three daughters — [[goneril|Goneril]], [[regan|Regan]], and [[cordelia|Cordelia]] — according to how eloquently each professes her love. [[goneril|Goneril]] and [[regan|Regan]] have delivered florid, hyperbolic declarations. [[cordelia|Cordelia]], the youngest, refuses to perform. When [[king-lear|Lear]] asks what she can say "to draw a third more opulent" than her sisters, she responds [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|truth]]fully: she loves him "according to my bond, no more nor lesse."

[[king-lear|Lear]]'s reply — "[[nothing-and-nihilism-in-king-lear|Nothing]] will come of nothing, speake againe" — is both a threat and a plea. He is offering her a second chance. But the deeper irony is that the phrase comes back to haunt him: by the play's end, [[king-lear|Lear]] him[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]] is left with nothing — no [[britain|kingdom]], no authority, no sanity, and ultimately no daughters.(001-078)

## Speaker

**[[king-lear|King Lear]]](king-lear)**

At this moment in the play, [[king-lear|Lear]] is still the absolute monarch — accustomed to command, surrounded by courtiers, and confident that words are equivalent to deeds. His demand for public declarations of love reveals a man who confuses flattery with [[love-in-king-lear|affection]]. He genuinely believes [[cordelia|Cordelia]] can "mend her speech" and produce the words he craves. Her refusal wounds him precisely because she is his favourite. His response moves from bafflement to fury, setting in motion the banishment of the one daughter who truly loves him.

## Analysis

### Literal Meaning

The maxim "Nothing will come of nothing" (_ex nihilo nihil fit_) was a commonplace of Renaissance thought, inherited from classical philosophy via Lucretius and Aristotle. On its surface, [[king-lear|Lear]] means: if you say nothing, you will get nothing. [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s silence has practical consequences — no share of [[britain|the kingdom]].

### Thematic Significance

The line introduces the play's central thematic preoccupation: **nothingness**. The word "nothing" and its cognates reverberate through virtually every scene — from the [[the-fool|Fool]]'s mockery ("thou art nothing")(001-520)to [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s grim reading of [[edmund|Edmund]]'s letter ("[[nothing-and-nihilism-in-king-lear|Nothing]] almost sees miracles"）(002-287)to [[king-lear|Lear]]'s later recognition on the heath that "unaccommodated man" is "no more but such a poor, bare, forked animal." The [[division-of-the-kingdom|love test]] frames a fundamental question: what is the relationship between words and love, between [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|appearance]] and substance? [[king-lear|Lear]]'s tragedy begins when he fails to distinguish between them.

### Dramatic Function

This exchange triggers the entire plot. [[king-lear|Lear]]'s misreading of [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s honesty leads to her disinheritance and exile, the division of [[britain|the kingdom]] between [[goneril|Goneril]] and [[regan|Regan]], and the chain of [[loyalty-and-betrayal-in-king-lear|betrayal]]s that follow. The phrase also establishes [[king-lear|Lear]]'s tragic flaw: his inability to value substance over rhetoric. He demands speech — "speake againe" — when he should have been listening.

## Allusions & Echoes

The "nothing" motif recurs throughout the play with almost obsessive frequency:

- **[[the-fool|The Fool]]** picks up the thread in Act 1, calling [[king-lear|Lear]] "an O without a figure" and taunting him with "thou art nothing"(001-520)— a brutal reminder that [[king-lear|Lear]] has given away his substance and retained only a title.
- **[[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]** reads [[edmund|Edmund]]'s forged letter and remarks "[[nothing-and-nihilism-in-king-lear|Nothing]] almost sees miracles"(002-287), unwittingly setting in motion the parallel plot of filial [[loyalty-and-betrayal-in-king-lear|betrayal]] that mirrors [[king-lear|Lear]]'s own.
- **[[edmund|Edmund]]**, when caught, dismisses the forged letter with "nothing" — a word whose weight the audience now fully understands.

## Performance Notes

The delivery of "Nothing will come of nothing" varies enormously across productions. Ian McKellen (RSC, 2007) delivered it as a barely controlled whisper, suggesting wounded love rather than rage. James Earl Jones (Public Theater, 1973) gave it a thunderous, almost biblical authority. Simon Russell Beale (National Theatre, 2014) emphasized the line's rhythmic incantatory quality, as though [[king-lear|Lear]] were casting a spell. The choice between anger, hurt, and incredulity fundamentally colours the character's trajectory.
