# Blindness and Sight in King Lear

The opposition of **blindness and sight** is one of [[king-lear|King Lear]]'s most pervasive motifs, operating on both literal and metaphorical levels. [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s physical blinding in Act 3 Scene 7 is the play's most brutal enactment of the theme, but [[king-lear|Lear]]'s moral blindness — his inability to distinguish true love from flattery — sets the tragedy in motion from the opening scene.

## Lear's Moral Blindness

Lear begins the play blind to the true natures of his daughters. He mistakes [[goneril|Goneril]] and [[regan|Regan]]'s extravagant declarations of love for genuine affection, while failing to see [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s honest but restrained "Nothing, my lord" as the truest expression of love. [[kent-earl-of-kent|Kent]]'s warning — "See better, Lear" (001-043) — is the play's first explicit call for moral sight, and Lear's refusal to heed it costs him everything.

The [[the-fool|Fool]] functions throughout as Lear's external conscience, repeatedly pointing out the king's failure to see:

> Fathers that wear rags
> Do make their children blind,
> But fathers that bear bags
> Shall see their children kind.
> [002-366]

The Fool's bitter wisdom — that poverty makes fathers invisible to their children — is a lesson Lear learns too late, wandering mad on the heath after being cast out by the daughters he enriched.

## Gloucester's Physical Blinding

The blinding of Gloucester in Act 3 Scene 7 is the play's most shocking scene. [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]] and [[regan|Regan]] gouge out Gloucester's eyes as punishment for his loyalty to Lear. "Out, vile jelly!" Cornwall cries as he stamps out the second eye (003-371). The act is both literal mutilation and symbolic enactment: Gloucester, who was morally blind to [[edmund|Edmund]]'s treachery and [[edgar|Edgar]]'s innocence, is now physically blinded.

The irony is acute. It is only after losing his eyes that Gloucester begins to see clearly:

> I stumbled when I saw. Full oft 'tis seen,
> Our means secure us, and our mere defects
> Prove our commodities.
> [004-253]

Blind Gloucester now perceives moral truth more accurately than he ever did when sighted. He recognises the voice of the mad Lear on the heath and understands, finally, that Edgar was loyal and Edmund treacherous.

## Sight and Insight

Shakespeare develops a paradox that runs through the entire play: those who have physical sight are morally blind, and those who lose sight gain insight. Lear, on the heath, strips away the trappings of kingship and comes to understand the reality of human suffering:

> Poor naked wretches, whereso'er you are,
> That bide the pelting of this pitiless storm,
> How shall your houseless heads and unfed sides,
> Your looped and windowed raggedness, defend you
> From seasons such as these? O, I have ta'en
> Too little care of this.
> [003-274–279]

This is Lear's moment of moral awakening — he sees, for the first time, the suffering of others. His earlier blindness to social reality is replaced by a terrible clarity.

The [[the-fool|Fool]] again provides the sardonic commentary: "All that follow their noses are led by their eyes, but blind men" (002-383). In the inverted world of King Lear, it is the blind who truly see.
