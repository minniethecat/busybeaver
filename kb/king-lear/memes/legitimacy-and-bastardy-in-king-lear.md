**Legitimacy and bastardy** in *[[king-lear|King Lear]]* are not merely questions of birth but of the play's deepest philosophical preoccupation: what makes any authority — parental, political, natural — legitimate? [[edmund|Edmund]]'s rebellion against his status as bastard opens a line of inquiry that the play extends to every institution it examines, from [[authority-and-power-in-king-lear|kingship]] to [[family-and-kinship-in-king-lear|kinship]].

## Edmund's Challenge

[[edmund|Edmund]]'s first soliloquy (Act 1 Scene 2) is the play's manifesto against legitimacy:

> Thou Nature art my Goddesse, to thy Law
> My seruices are bound. Wherefore should I
> Stand in the plague of custome, and permit
> The curiosity of Nations, to depriue me?
> For that I am some twelue, or fourteene Moonshines
> Lag of a Brother? Why Bastard? Wherefore base?
> When my Dimensions are as well compact,
> My minde as generous, and my shape as true
> As honest Madams issue? Why brand they vs
> With Base? With basenes Bastardie? Base, Base?
> (001-312)

The speech is rhetorically overwhelming — a cascade of rhetorical questions that expose legitimacy as a social construction rather than a natural fact. [[edmund|Edmund]]'s argument is that nature, his chosen goddess, makes no distinction between legitimate and illegitimate children; only "the plague of custome" does. The repetition of "Base, Base?" is both accusation and lament.

## Gloucester's Complicity

[[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s treatment of [[edmund|Edmund]] is the play's most damning exhibit against the system [[edmund|Edmund]] attacks. In the opening scene, [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] jokes about [[edmund|Edmund]]'s conception in [[edmund|Edmund]]'s presence:

> … though this Knaue came somthing sawcily to the world before he was sent for:
> yet was his Mother fayre, there was good sport at his making, and the horson
> must be acknowledged.
> (001-021)

This public shaming — delivered with a laugh, as if [[edmund|Edmund]]'s origin were entertainment — provides the emotional fuel for [[edmund|Edmund]]'s later [[loyalty-and-betrayal-in-king-lear|treachery]]. The play suggests that the system of legitimacy creates the villain it then condemns. [[edmund|Edmund]] is not born evil; he is made so by a social order that brands him "base" before he has done anything.

## Nature Versus Custom

The tension between nature and custom runs through the entire play. [[king-lear|Lear]]'s division of [[britain|the kingdom]] — a custom of inheritance — triggers a cascade of unnatural acts: daughters turning against father, sister against sister, brother against brother. [[edmund|Edmund]]'s invocation of "[[nature-in-king-lear|Nature]]" as his goddess is ironic: the nature he worships is a nature of appetite and competition, a nature that the play's actual natural world — [[the-heath|the storm]], the heath, the cliff at Dover — seems to mock.

[[edgar|Edgar]], the legitimate son, survives by becoming "nothing" — [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|Poor Tom]], the naked beggar who is "the thing it [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]e" (003-261). His journey from legitimate heir to outcast to restorer of order dramatises the possibility that legitimacy is earned rather than inherited.

## Bastardy and Sympathy

The play complicates any simple moral equation between birth and virtue. Late in the play, [[edgar|Edgar]] reports that the dying [[edmund|Edmund]] acknowledged [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s [[fortune-and-fate-in-king-lear|fate]] with unexpected compassion:

> … for Glousters bastard Son
> Was kinder to his Father, then my Daughters
> Got 'tweene the lawfull sheets.
> — 004-367 (paraphrased: a report that Edmund showed more kindness than Lear's legitimate daughters)

This reversal — the bastard showing more natural feeling than legitimate children — is the play's final verdict on the [[nothing-and-nihilism-in-king-lear|emptiness]] of legal categories when measured against human conduct. [[goneril|Goneril]] and [[regan|Regan]], born within marriage, are the play's true monsters; [[edmund|Edmund]], for all his villainy, dies acknowledging the [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|truth]].

## The "Legitimate" Word

[[edmund|Edmund]]'s mockery of the word "legitimate" — "fine word: Legitimate" (001-329) — exposes language it[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]] as an instrument of power. To be called legitimate is to be granted rights, status, and [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|identity]]. To be called base is to be denied them. The play's genius is to show that these are words, not facts — and that the order they sustain can be shattered by one man who refuses to accept the name he has been given.

> … Edmond the base
> Shall to'th' Legitimate: I grow, I prosper:
> Now Gods, stand vp for Bastards.
> (001-331)
