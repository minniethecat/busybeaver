# Britain

The kingdom that [[king-lear|King Lear]] [[authority-and-power-in-king-lear|rule]]s and divides among his daughters, setting the entire tragedy in motion. Britain in the play is a pre-Christian, legendary realm where [[loyalty-and-betrayal-in-king-lear|allegiance]], land, and power are inseparable.

## Overview

The play opens with the division of Britain. [[king-lear|Lear]], aged and weary, proposes to "shake all Cares and Businesse from our Age" by partitioning the kingdom among his three daughters [[goneril|Gonerill]], [[regan|Regan]], and [[cordelia|Cordelia]] (001-023-027). This political act — meant to prevent "future strife" — instead triggers civil war and [[order-and-chaos-in-king-lear|chaos]]. Britain quickly fractures into rival spheres of influence controlled by [[duke-of-albany|the Duke]]s of [[duke-of-albany|Albany]] and [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]], between whom "there is diuision" (003-017). By Act 4, a French army has landed to restore order, and by Act 5 the kingdom is convulsed in open battle.

## Description in the Text

Shakespeare evokes Britain as a vast, ancient realm bound by ritual and oath. [[king-lear|Lear]] enters with a ceremonial procession and calls for a map to physically carve the kingdom into three parts (001-023-024). The geography is loosely drawn: [[king-lear|Lear]]'s royal seat, [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s castle, [[duke-of-albany|Albany]]'s palace, [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]]'s residence, and the heath and Dover as marginal spaces.

[[the-fool|The Fool]]'s prophecy darkly names the kingdom "the Realme of Albion" and foresees it falling "to great confusion" when all social order is inverted (003-132). [[edgar|Edgar]], in his mad guise as [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|Poor Tom]], alludes to the folkloric depths of the island when he chants: "I smell the blood of a Brittish man" (003-299).

> "Giue me the Map there. Know, that we haue diuided / In three our Kingdome" (001-024-025)

### The Kingdom as Family

Britain's political geography is indistinguishable from [[king-lear|Lear]]'s family drama. The kingdom is divided as dowries for daughters; rejection of [[cordelia|Cordelia]] means she is cast out of the realm entirely and married to the [[king-of-france|King of France]]. When [[king-lear|Lear]] is turned out by [[goneril|Gonerill]] and [[regan|Regan]], he becomes a homeless king — the kingdom he once commanded is now closed to him. Kent's quiet recognition late in the play — "In your owne kingdome Sir" (004-597) — marks the moment [[king-lear|Lear]] is restored to [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]-awareness and to his realm.

## Dramatic Function

Britain functions as more than a backdrop. The kingdom is [[king-lear|Lear]]'s body politic: his physical and mental disintegration mirrors the realm's collapse into civil war. The map [[king-lear|Lear]] divides in Act 1 is the same kingdom he wanders, mad and homeless, in Act 3. The play moves from the ordered, ceremonial court to the unbounded [[order-and-chaos-in-king-lear|chaos]] of the heath, and finally toward Dover's cliffs — the literal edge of Britain — before the moral order is partially restored and then devastatingly undone.

### Symbolic Meaning

The kingdom represents natural and patriarchal order. [[king-lear|Lear]]'s initial act — dividing it — violates the principle of unified [[authority-and-power-in-king-lear|sovereignty]]. The ensuing fragmentation (court vs. heath, [[duke-of-albany|Albany]] vs. [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]], Britain vs. [[king-of-france|France]]) externalises the rupture in [[king-lear|Lear]]'s family and mind. [[the-fool|The Fool]]'s prophecy that Albion shall "come to great confusion" (003-132) frames the entire action as a vision of national as well as personal apocalypse.

## Real-World References

Shakespeare drew on the legendary history of Leir, an ancient British king recorded in Geoffrey of Monmouth's *Historia Regum Britanniae* (c. 1136) and retold in Holinshed's *Chronicles* (1587). In that tradition, Leir [[authority-and-power-in-king-lear|rule]]d a pre-Roman Britain and founded Leicester. The play's "Albion" is a mythical Britain, but real place-names — Dover, [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]], [[duke-of-albany|Albany]] (Scotland north of the Humber) — anchor the geography in the island Shakespeare's audience knew.
