# Act 2 Scene 2: Kent in the Stocks

In one of the play's most darkly comic scenes, [[kent-earl-of-kent|Kent]] confronts [[oswald|Oswald]] outside [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s castle, and [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]]'s decision to place the King's messenger in the stocks marks a decisive escalation in the abuse of [[king-lear|Lear]]'s authority.

## Summary

[[kent-earl-of-kent|Kent]] meets [[oswald|Oswald]] outside Gloucester's castle and delivers a legendary torrent of insults — "a Knaue, a Rascall, an eater of broken meates, a base, proud, shallow, beggerly, three-suited-hundred pound, filthy woosted-stocking knaue" (002-188). When Oswald cries for help, [[edmund-bastard-son-of-gloucester|Edmund]], [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]], [[regan|Regan]], and [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] enter. Kent refuses to flatter Cornwall, and despite being the King's messenger, Cornwall orders him placed in the stocks. [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] protests that Lear will take this as a personal affront, but Cornwall and [[regan|Regan]] insist. Alone after the others leave, Kent reads a letter from [[cordelia|Cordelia]], taking comfort that she knows of his disguised service to her father.
