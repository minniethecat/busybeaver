# Authority and Power in King Lear

The opening action of *[[king-lear|King Lear]]* is an act of power — the division of a [[britain|kingdom]] — that paradoxically destroys the authority it was meant to preserve. From that moment, the play traces the migration of power from the legitimate king to his illegitimate children, exposing authority as a performance that requires an audience willing to believe.

## Overview

[[king-lear|Lear]] begins the play as an absolute monarch whose word is law. He ends it as "a very foolish fond old man" (004-575), stripped of retinue, reason, and finally his most beloved daughter. Between these poles, *[[king-lear|King Lear]]* conducts a forensic examination of what authority actually consists of. Is it the name of king? The hundred knights? The ability to command obedience? When [[goneril|Goneril]] and [[regan|Regan]] ask why [[king-lear|Lear]] needs even one retainer — "What need one?" (002-400) — they are asking the question the play cannot answer without exposing the [[nothing-and-nihilism-in-king-lear|void]] at authority's center.

## Key Manifestations

### The Emptying-Out of Royal Authority

[[king-lear|Lear]]'s first speech performs sovereignty: "Giue me the Map there. Know, that we haue diuided / In three our Kingdome" (001-023). The map is the emblem of territorial power; the division is an act of absolute will. Yet this very act empties the authority it exercises. [[king-lear|Lear]] intends to "shake all Cares and Businesse from our Age, / Conferring them on yonger strengths" (001-027) while retaining "the name, and all th' addition to a King" (001-134). The play demonstrates that the "name" without the substance is a contradiction [[goneril|Goneril]] and [[regan|Regan]] are quick to exploit.

The hundred knights become the contested symbol of residual authority. [[goneril|Goneril]] reduces them from one hundred to fifty; [[regan|Regan]] to twenty-five; then — "What need one?" (002-400). Each reduction is a test of [[king-lear|Lear]]'s power to command, and each time he fails. The knights are not a military force but a symbolic retinue — the visible sign that [[king-lear|Lear]] is still a king. Stripped of them, he is stripped of the performance that constitutes authority.

### Authority Without Justice — Cornwall and Regan

If [[king-lear|Lear]] represents the evaporation of legitimate authority, [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]] and [[regan|Regan]] represent its brutal perversion. [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]]'s blinding of [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] is an act of power that masquerades as justice: "Though well we may not pass upon his life / Without the form of [[justice-and-injustice-in-king-lear|Justice]], yet our power / Shall do a courtesy to our wrath" (003-385). The explicit acknowledgment that power operates "without the form of [[justice-and-injustice-in-king-lear|Justice]]" marks the play's darkest political [[blindness-and-insight-in-king-lear|insight]] — that authority, once unmoored from legitimacy, is indistinguishable from violence.

### The Image of Authority — Lear's Mad Insight

In his madness on the heath in Act 4, scene 6, [[king-lear|Lear]] delivers the play's most concentrated meditation on authority. Seeing the blinded [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]], he launches into a speech about the hypocrisy of power: "Through tattered clothes small vices do appear; / Robes and furred gowns hide all" (004-379). Authority, [[king-lear|Lear]] now understands, is a costume — a "dog obeyed in office" — that conceals the same human frailty present in the poorest beggar. This is the wisdom that madness grants: the recognition that all [[order-and-chaos-in-king-lear|hierarchy]] is a fiction sustained by violence and performance.

### The Obedience Crisis

The play repeatedly stages moments where obedience is demanded and denied. Kent's refusal to flatter [[king-lear|Lear]] — "I cannot wish the fault undone" (001-011) — is an act of disobedience that proves truer than [[goneril|Goneril]] and [[regan|Regan]]'s lavish professions. [[oswald|Oswald]]'s blind obedience to [[goneril|Goneril]] — "I'll not be struck, my Lord" (001-620) — is the mirror image: obedience without moral discrimination. The play asks whether there can be a form of obedience that is truly virtuous, or whether all obedience to corrupted authority is it[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]] corrupt.

## Dramatic Development

**Act 1** establishes the paradox: [[king-lear|Lear]]'s exercise of absolute authority (dividing [[britain|the kingdom]]) destroys the foundation of that authority. [[edmund|Edmund]]'s soliloquy offers an alternative theory of power grounded in appetite, not legitimacy. **Act 2** develops the crisis through the motif of the knights — the visible emblem of [[king-lear|Lear]]'s remaining authority, progressively stripped. Kent in the stocks is an image of loyal service humiliated by power that no longer recognizes loyalty. **Act 3** turns authority into its opposite: on the heath, [[king-lear|Lear]] is king of nothing; in [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s castle, [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]]'s "justice" is pure violence. The mock trial scene (3.6) is [[king-lear|Lear]]'s attempt to reconstitute authority as moral judgment in a hovel — a deranged but ethically lucid courtroom. **Act 4** reverses the lens: [[king-lear|Lear]], insane and flower-crowned, speaks [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|truth]]s about authority that his sane [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]] could not. "A dog's obeyed in office" (004-379) is the play's bleakest political aphorism. **Act 5** resolves authority through violence: the duel, the poison, the deaths. [[duke-of-albany|Albany]]'s final speech attempts to restore legitimate rule — "All friends shall taste / The wages of their virtue" (005-277) — but the restoration feels hollow in the shadow of [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s body.

## Critical Perspectives

The play's treatment of authority has been read through multiple political lenses. The traditional reading (A.C. Bradley, 1904) sees the restoration of legitimate authority at the play's end, however tragic the cost. New Historicist critics (Greenblatt, 1985) emphasize the play's exposure of authority's theatricality — power is a performance, and when the audience (the hundred knights, the loyal subjects) is withdrawn, the performance collapses. Feminist readings have focused on [[goneril|Goneril]] and [[regan|Regan]] as figures who expose the patriarchal logic of authority by mastering it — and are punished for doing so.
