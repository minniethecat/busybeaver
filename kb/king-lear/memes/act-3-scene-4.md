# Act 3 Scene 4: The Hovel and Unaccommodated Man

In a hovel on [[the-heath|the storm]]-blasted heath, [[king-lear|Lear]] confronts the figure of absolute human destitution -- [[edgar|Edgar]] as [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|Poor Tom]] -- and strips him[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]] down to the essential question: "Is man no more then this?"

## Summary

Continuing from [[the-heath|the storm]], Kent leads [[king-lear|Lear]] and the [[the-fool|Fool]] toward "a Houell" (003-100) for shelter, but [[king-lear|Lear]] hesitates at the threshold, his mind more occupied with his daughters' cruelty than with [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]-preservation. [[the-fool|The Fool]] enters first and recoils from the mad beggar inside. [[edgar|Edgar]] as [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|Poor Tom]] emerges, raving about the "foule Fiend" (003-201) and listing the torments of demonic possession -- eating "the swimming Frog, the Toad, the Tadpole, the Wall-newt" (003-238). [[king-lear|Lear]], [[blindness-and-insight-in-king-lear|seeing]] this creature of absolute destitution, asks the defining question of the play: "Is man no more then this? ... Thou art the thing it [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]e; vnaccommodated man, is no more but such a poore, bare, forked Animall as thou art" (003-223). [[king-lear|Lear]] then tears off his own clothes to become like Tom. [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] arrives with a torch (003-223), warning of a plot against [[king-lear|Lear]]'s life and arranging his transport to Dover, where [[cordelia|Cordelia]] awaits.

## Structure

### Opening

Kent urges [[king-lear|Lear]] to enter the hovel as [[the-heath|the storm]] continues: "Gracious my Lord, hard by here is a Houell, / Some friendship will it lend you 'gainst the Tempest" (003-100). [[king-lear|Lear]], however, delays, his mind still fixed on his daughters' ingratitude and [[the-heath|the storm]] within: "the tempest in my mind / Doth from my sences take all feeling else" (003-165). He prays for the "houselesse pouertie" (003-180), for the first time in his life showing genuine empathy for the dispossessed.

### Development

[[the-fool|The Fool]] enters the hovel and rushes out, terrified by the "spirit" inside. [[edgar|Edgar]] emerges as [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|Poor Tom]], raving: "Away, the foule Fiend followes me" (003-201). He catalogues his torments: the fiend "hath led through Fire, and through Flame, through Foord, and Whirle-Poole, o're Bog, and Quagmire" (003-202). He names his fiends -- "Flibbertigibbet" (003-225), "Smulkin" (003-249) -- and describes his diet of vermin: "Poore Tom, that eates the swimming Frog, the Toad, the Tadpole, the Wall-newt, and the water" (003-238).

[[king-lear|Lear]], riveted by this spectacle, asks "Did'st thou giue all to thy Daughters? And art thou come to this?" (003-202), reading his own story into Tom's madness. The encounter pushes [[king-lear|Lear]] to his most radical act of identification: "Is man no more then this?" He tears off his clothing, declaring that "Off, off you Lendings: Come, vnbutton heere" (003-223), seeking to strip him[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]] down to the same bare humanity as Tom.

### Closing

[[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] enters with a torch (003-223), searching for the King. He is horrified by [[king-lear|Lear]]'s state and by the presence of "the thing it [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]e" -- poor Tom, whom [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] does not yet recognize as his own son [[edgar|Edgar]]. [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] urges Kent to convey [[king-lear|Lear]] to Dover, revealing that "There is a Litter ready, lay him in't, / And driue toward Douer friend, where thou shalt meete / Both welcome, and protection" (003-490). The scene closes on the plan for escape, as [[king-lear|Lear]]'s madness deepens.

## Thematic Connections

The hovel scene is the philosophical heart of the play. The question "Is man no more then this?" (003-223) distills the play's investigation of human nature to its essence: strip away the "lendings" of civilization -- clothing, title, property, language -- and what remains? [[edgar|Edgar]]'s [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|Poor Tom]] is the answer: a trembling body, exposed and vulnerable, haunted by demons. [[king-lear|Lear]]'s stripping of his own clothes (003-223) is his most radical act of [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]-knowledge: to understand what he has become, he must become what Tom is. The scene also deepens the play's treatment of madness: the border between Tom's assumed madness and [[king-lear|Lear]]'s encroaching real madness dissolves, and the hovel becomes a space where the two madmen mirror each other (003-202, 003-238).

## Dramatic Significance

This scene marks [[king-lear|Lear]]'s moral and philosophical turning point. His prayer for the "houselesse pouertie" (003-180) earlier in the scene -- before he even enters the hovel -- shows that [[the-heath|the storm]] has already begun to teach him what [[authority-and-power-in-king-lear|kingship]] had hidden. The encounter with [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|Poor Tom]] completes that education: [[king-lear|Lear]] now knows, viscerally, what he previously ignored. [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s arrival with the torch sets up the parallel tracking of the two old men's [[fortune-and-fate-in-king-lear|fate]]s -- both betrayed, both homeless, both moving toward Dover and toward their appointed revelations.

## Textual Notes

[[edgar|Edgar]]'s [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|Poor Tom]] speeches are rich with allusion to Samuel Harsnett's *A Declaration of Egregious Popish Impostures* (1603), a polemic against Catholic exorcists. The fiend names -- Flibbertigibbet, Smulkin, Modo, Mahu -- are drawn directly from Harsnett's catalogue of demons, suggesting Shakespeare's engagement with contemporary religious controversies about demonic possession and fraud.
