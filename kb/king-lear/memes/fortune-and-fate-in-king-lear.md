# Fortune and Fate in King Lear

Is the suffering in *[[king-lear|King Lear]]* the work of malicious gods, indifferent Fortune, or human choice? The play refuses to answer, but it stages the question with relentless intensity. Characters invoke Fortune's wheel, divine providence, and astrological portents — all in search of a framework that might make their suffering bearable. The play's final gesture is to offer none.

## Overview

The word "Fortune" appears in the play's first scene, when Kent tells [[king-lear|Lear]] he "would not be so treated by Fortune" (001-015). From that moment, the characters of *[[king-lear|King Lear]]* reach for cosmic explanations for their suffering — astrology, the gods, Fortune's wheel — while the play's action suggests that the true cause is simpler and more terrible: human cruelty, operating without supernatural sanction or restraint.

## Key Manifestations

### Fortune's Wheel

Kent, placed in the stocks, invokes Fortune as the governing principle of his fate: "Fortune, good night, smile once more, turn thy wheel" (002-154). The image is medieval — the goddess Fortune turning her wheel, raising men up and casting them down — but Kent's invocation is ironic. He is in the stocks because of [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]]'s tyranny, not cosmic caprice. The play systematically undermines the Fortune model by showing that the reversals characters attribute to fate are almost always the result of identifiable human actions.

[[king-lear|Lear]]'s descent — from king to mad beggar — might seem to exemplify Fortune's wheel, but every step in his fall is traceable: the division of [[britain|the kingdom]], the banishment of [[cordelia|Cordelia]], the alliance of [[goneril|Goneril]] and [[regan|Regan]]. Fortune is the name characters give to consequences they did not foresee.

### Edmund's Rejection of Fate

[[edmund|Edmund]] explicitly rejects astrological determinism in his first soliloquy: "This is the excellent foppery of the world, that when we are sick in fortune — often the surfeit of our own behavior — we make guilty of our disasters the sun, the moon, and the stars" (001-298). For [[edmund|Edmund]], the stars are an excuse; the real authors of human destiny are human wills. His radical [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]-determination — "I should have been that I am, had the maidenliest star in the firmament twinkled on my bastardizing" (001-298) — is the play's most modern voice. Yet [[edmund|Edmund]]'s philosophy of [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]-authorship leads him to betray his father, order [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s death, and die by his brother's sword. The rejection of fate does not guarantee moral action.

### Gloucester's Gods

[[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] is the play's most committed seeker of cosmic meaning. He reads the eclipses as portents: "These late eclipses in the sun and moon portend no good to us" (001-298). After his blinding, his cosmology darkens into the play's most famous indictment of [[justice-and-injustice-in-king-lear|divine justice]]: "As flies to wanton boys are we to the gods; / They kill us for their sport" (004-020). This is not atheism but anti-theism — the gods exist, and they are cruel. [[edgar|Edgar]] counters with a providential reading: "The gods are just, and of our pleasant vices / Make instruments to plague us" (005-162). But [[edgar|Edgar]]'s piety, spoken just before [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s death, sounds less like the play's conclusion than its most painful irony.

### Cordelia's Death and the End of Providence

[[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s death is the play's final argument against any providential reading. She is the most virtuous character in the play; her death is meaningless, random, the result of [[edmund|Edmund]]'s order arriving minutes too late. [[king-lear|Lear]]'s "Why should a dog, a horse, a rat have life, / And thou no breath at all?" (005-280) is a question the play refuses to answer. The silence that follows is the play's theology: if there are gods, they do not speak.

## Dramatic Development

**Act 1**: Characters invoke Fortune and the stars. [[king-lear|Lear]]'s division of [[britain|the kingdom]] is framed as willed choice, not fate. [[edmund|Edmund]]'s soliloquy explicitly rejects astrological determinism. **Act 2**: Kent's "Fortune, good night" invokes the wheel; his continued loyalty suggests the wheel can be resisted, if not reversed. **Act 3**: The storm is the play's central image of cosmic indifference — [[nature-in-king-lear|Nature]] does not care whether [[king-lear|Lear]] is king or beggar. [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s blinding prompts his darkest theological reflections. **Act 4**: [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s "As flies to wanton boys" is the play's bleakest verdict on cosmic justice. [[edgar|Edgar]]'s "The gods are just" sets up a competing reading. **Act 5**: [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s death resolves the contest — not in [[edgar|Edgar]]'s favor. The surviving characters are left with the task of rebuilding meaning in a cosmos that has demonstrated its indifference.

## Critical Perspectives

A.C. Bradley argued that *[[king-lear|King Lear]]* presents a world in which "the gods are just" only if justice is understood as the purification of the soul through suffering — a reading that requires ignoring [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s death. More recent criticism has emphasized the play's refusal of consolation. The tension between pagan Fortune (random, amoral) and Christian Providence (ordered, just) reflects the play's setting in pre-Christian [[britain|Britain]] — a world in which the Christian framework is unavailable, and the old gods offer no comfort.
