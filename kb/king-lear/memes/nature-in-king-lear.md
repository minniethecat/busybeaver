# Nature in King Lear

Nature in *[[king-lear|King Lear]]* is not a single concept but a contested field: it is the divine, hierarchical order that [[king-lear|Lear]] violates by dividing his [[britain|kingdom]]; it is the raw biological impulse [[edmund|Edmund]] invokes to justify his ambition; and it is the indifferent, storm-blasted cosmos that answers human suffering with silence.

## Overview

The word "nature" and its cognates ("unnatural," "natural," "natures") appear more than forty times in *[[king-lear|King Lear]]*, making it one of the most heavily freighted terms in the play. The concept fractures along two axes. Vertically, it names the cosmic order — the Great Chain of Being — that ties king to subject, father to child, and man to God (001-040). Horizontally, it names the animal drives of appetite, [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]-interest, and survival that [[edmund|Edmund]] calls his "Goddesse" (001-312). Between these two poles, the play charts the catastrophic consequences of confusing them — and the terrifying possibility that there is no "Nature" at all, only a storm on a heath.

## Key Manifestations

### Nature as Cosmic Order

[[king-lear|Lear]] opens the play by invoking Nature as the guarantor of [[order-and-chaos-in-king-lear|hierarchy]]. He calls upon "Nature" to justify [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s promised dower: "Where Nature doth with merit challenge" (001-040). The [[division-of-the-kingdom|love test]] it[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]] is a perversion of natural order — a king demanding public performance of private [[love-in-king-lear|affection]]. When [[cordelia|Cordelia]] refuses, [[king-lear|Lear]]'s rage is framed in the language of unnatural rupture: "The barbarous Scythian, / Or he that makes his generation messes / To gorge his appetite" (001-115). The play's central crime — children turning against their father — is measured against a cosmic norm now shattered.

The storm on the heath in Act 3 literalizes this collapse. [[king-lear|Lear]] dares Nature to destroy him: "Blow, winds, and crack your cheeks! Rage, blow!" (003-001). The storm is both a manifestation of Nature's power and a sign that Nature offers no protection to the just. The king who violated natural order by abdicating his throne now confronts a Nature that does not recognize him.

### Nature as Raw Impulse — Edmund's Creed

[[edmund|Edmund]]'s soliloquy in Act 1, scene 2 is the play's most radical statement of a counter-nature: "Thou Nature art my Goddesse, to thy Law / My seruices are bound" (001-312). For [[edmund|Edmund]], "Nature" is not [[order-and-chaos-in-king-lear|hierarchy]] but appetite — the drive of the stronger, the cleverer, the more ambitious to prevail. His illegitimacy is, in his own reasoning, more "natural" than [[edgar|Edgar]]'s lawful birth because it was conceived in "the lusty stealth of Nature" rather than "a dull, stale, tired bed" (001-313).

[[edmund|Edmund]]'s Nature is a philosophy of radical [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]-interest. He manipulates both his father [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] and his brother [[edgar|Edgar]] by exploiting their belief in a moral Nature that [[edmund|Edmund]] him[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]] rejects. His success through the first four acts suggests that his vision of Nature may be the one that actually governs the play's world — until it collapses catastrophically in Act 5.

### Unnatural Children

[[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] explicitly links the breakdown of Nature to the breakdown of family: "These late eclipses in the sun and moon portend no good to us ... [[love-in-king-lear|Love]] cools, friendship falls off, brothers divide. In cities, mutinies; in countries, discord; in palaces, treason; and the bond cracked 'twixt son and father" (001-298). [[goneril|Goneril]] and [[regan|Regan]] are repeatedly called "unnatural" — their cruelty to [[king-lear|Lear]] violates the most fundamental bond in the Elizabethan cosmos. Yet the play complicates this: [[king-lear|Lear]]'s own unnatural act ([[division-of-the-kingdom|abdication]] while retaining the "name" of king) sets [[act-5-scene-3|the catastrophe]] in motion.

### Nature Redemptive — Cordelia and Edgar

Against the predatory Nature of [[edmund|Edmund]] and the cosmic Nature of [[the-heath|the storm]], the play offers glimpses of a healing Nature associated with [[cordelia|Cordelia]]. Her tears are described as "holy water" (004-248), and her reunion with [[king-lear|Lear]] in Act 4, scene 7 carries the imagery of natural restoration. [[edgar|Edgar]], too, leads his blinded father [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] to believe in a miraculous preservation at Dover — a gentle fiction that restores [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s will to live after Nature (in the form of [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]]'s brutality) has destroyed his eyes.

## Dramatic Development

The trajectory of Nature across the five acts traces a descent from ordered [[order-and-chaos-in-king-lear|hierarchy]] to [[order-and-chaos-in-king-lear|chaos]], with only fragmentary redemption. **Act 1** establishes Nature as the normative frame — [[king-lear|Lear]]'s division of [[britain|the kingdom]] is a violation, [[edmund|Edmund]]'s soliloquy a heresy (001-040) (001-312). **Act 2** intensifies the unnatural: [[king-lear|Lear]]'s daughters strip him of his knights, and he appeals to Nature for patience — "You heavens, give me that patience" (002-400). **Act 3** is the rupture. The storm on the heath dramatizes a Nature wholly indifferent to human moral categories — [[king-lear|Lear]], the [[the-fool|Fool]], and [[edgar|Edgar]]-as-Poor-Tom are reduced to "unaccommodated man," "a poor, bare, forked animal" (003-340). **Act 4** offers the play's only sustained image of redemptive Nature: the blinded [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s "I stumbled when I saw" (004-024) and [[king-lear|Lear]]'s flower-decked madness on the heath. **Act 5** returns to the problem: [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s death — the most "unnatural" event in the play — prompts [[king-lear|Lear]]'s final, unbearable question: "Why should a dog, a horse, a rat have life, / And thou no breath at all?" (005-280). Nature, in the end, offers no answer.

## Critical Perspectives

The Elizabethan worldview inherited the concept of a "Great Chain of Being" — a cosmic [[order-and-chaos-in-king-lear|hierarchy]] from God through angels, kings, fathers, to animals and inanimate matter. *[[king-lear|King Lear]]* systematically dismantles this chain. John Danby's influential study *Shakespeare's Doctrine of Nature* (1949) identifies two competing concepts of Nature in the play: the hierarchical, medieval Nature of Hooker and the competitive, Hobbesian Nature of Machiavelli. [[edmund|Edmund]] speaks for the latter; [[king-lear|Lear]]'s tragedy is that he embodies the former but sets in motion the forces that destroy it.
