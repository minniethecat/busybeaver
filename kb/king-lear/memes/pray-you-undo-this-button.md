# "Pray You Undo This Button"

> And my poor fool is hanged. No, no, no life?
> Why should a dog, a horse, a rat have life,
> And thou no breath at all? Thou'lt come no more,
> Never, never, never, never, never.
> Pray you undo this button. Thank you, sir.
> Do you see this? Look on her? Look, her lips,
> Look there, look there.
>
> — King Lear, Act 5 Scene 3 [005-442–005-448]

## Context

These are [[king-lear|King Lear]]'s final words, spoken as he dies over the body of his daughter [[cordelia|Cordelia]]. The scene occurs at the climax of Act 5 Scene 3, after the battle between the French and British forces has been lost, [[edmund|Edmund]] has been mortally wounded by [[edgar|Edgar]], and Edmund's last-minute attempt to spare Cordelia has come too late. Lear enters carrying Cordelia's corpse, and all the play's political resolution — [[duke-of-albany|Albany]]'s plans for restoration, Edgar's new authority — is suspended and rendered meaningless by the image of the old king cradling his dead child.

## Speaker

King Lear, the aging monarch of Britain, has by this point lost everything: his kingdom, his sanity, and all three of his daughters — [[goneril|Goneril]] and [[regan|Regan]] dead by poison and suicide, and [[cordelia|Cordelia]] hanged on Edmund's orders. The king who began the play demanding declarations of love and dividing a kingdom is reduced, in his final moments, to the most elemental of human experiences: the death of a child, and his own death.

## Analysis

### The Intimacy of "Undo This Button"

The request "Pray you undo this button" is one of the most devastatingly intimate moments in Shakespeare. After the cosmic rhetoric of Lear's earlier speeches — the invocations of Nature, the calls for divine justice, the apocalyptic rage on the heath — his final words are domestic, physical, and small. He asks someone to loosen his collar, as if he cannot breathe. The line humanises Lear utterly: he is not a king dying in state but an old man struggling for breath, attended by a small kindness.

### "Never" Repeated Five Times

The fivefold repetition of "never" (005-445) is among the most famous passages in English literature. Each iteration closes a door: Cordelia will never speak, never move, never breathe again. The word's finality is absolute. Unlike earlier moments in the play where Lear imagines a future — "We two alone will sing like birds i' the cage" (005-041) — here he confronts the unalterable fact of death with a word that permits no hope.

### "Look There, Look There"

Lear's dying words — "Look there, look there" (005-448) — are radically ambiguous. Is he seeing Cordelia's lips move, believing her alive in a final delusion? Or is he drawing the onlookers' attention to her dead body, demanding they witness what they have done? Productions have interpreted this moment both ways. The ambiguity is Shakespeare's final gift to the play: it may be a moment of cruel hope or of devastating clarity.

## Performance Notes

The speech presents extraordinary demands for the actor playing Lear. After hours of emotional extremity, the final moments require a drop from rhetorical grandeur to whispered intimacy. Notable performances have included:
- **John Gielgud** (1955): emphasised the tenderness, playing the "button" request as Lear's last gesture of connection
- **Paul Scofield** (1962, Peter Brook production): delivered the lines with exhausted understatement, the "nevers" almost matter-of-fact in their finality
- **Ian McKellen** (2007, RSC): played the final "Look there" as a genuine moment of transcendent vision
