# Division of the Kingdom

[[king-lear|King Lear]] abdicates by dividing [[britain|Britain]] among his daughters through a love test, disinherits [[cordelia|Cordelia]] for her honest silence, and banishes Kent -- a catastrophic misjudgment that triggers every subsequent tragedy.

## Overview

The division of [[britain|the kingdom]] is the inciting event of *[[king-lear|King Lear]]*. [[king-lear|Lear]] declares his "darker purpose" (001-023) to divide [[britain|Britain]] into three parts, awarding the largest share to the daughter who professes the greatest love. [[goneril|Goneril]] and [[regan|Regan]] deliver hyperbolic declarations and are richly rewarded; [[cordelia|Cordelia]] answers "[[nothing-and-nihilism-in-king-lear|Nothing]] my Lord" (001-075) and is stripped of her inheritance. When Kent protests, [[king-lear|Lear]] banishes him. [[king-of-france|France]], recognizing [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s virtue, marries her without dowry (001-257). The sisters privately acknowledge [[king-lear|Lear]]'s poor judgment and plot to manage his authority (001-299).

## Dramatic Context

### Preceding Events

The play opens with [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] introducing his illegitimate son [[edmund|Edmund]] to Kent (001-002), establishing the parallel subplot of filial [[loyalty-and-betrayal-in-king-lear|betrayal]]. A sennet announces the arrival of [[king-lear|Lear]] and his court (001-019). [[king-lear|Lear]] dispatches [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] to attend the Lords of [[king-of-france|France]] and [[duke-of-burgundy|Burgundy]], signaling the political significance of the moment (001-020).

### The Event

[[king-lear|Lear]] announces his intention to "shake all Cares and Businesse from our Age" (001-026), dividing [[britain|the kingdom]] so that "future strife may be preuented now" (001-032). The love test is framed as a condition for receiving their "severall Dowers" (001-031).

[[goneril|Goneril]] speaks first, professing a love "more then word can weild y matter, / Deere then eye-[[blindness-and-insight-in-king-lear|sight]], space, and libertie" (001-042). [[regan|Regan]] matches and exceeds her, claiming [[goneril|Goneril]] "comes too short" (001-059). [[king-lear|Lear]] awards each a third of [[britain|the kingdom]].

[[cordelia|Cordelia]] resolves to "Loue, and be silent" (001-049). When called upon, she says "[[nothing-and-nihilism-in-king-lear|Nothing]] my Lord" (001-075). [[king-lear|Lear]]'s reply -- "[[nothing-and-nihilism-in-king-lear|Nothing]] will come of nothing, speake againe" (001-078) -- introduces the play's central motif. [[cordelia|Cordelia]] explains she loves [[king-lear|Lear]] "According to my bond, no more nor lesse" (001-081). Enraged, [[king-lear|Lear]] disclaims "all my Paternall care" (001-102), declaring her a stranger forever.

Kent intervenes: "be Kent vnmannerly, / When [[king-lear|Lear]] is mad" (001-136), calling the decision "hideous rashnesse" (001-143). [[king-lear|Lear]] banishes Kent: "Thy banisht trunke be found in our Dominions, / The moment is thy death" (001-173).

[[king-of-france|France]], [[blindness-and-insight-in-king-lear|seeing]] [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s virtue, declares: "Fairest [[cordelia|Cordelia]], that art most rich [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|being]] poore, / Most choise forsaken, and most lou'd despis'd, / Thee and thy vertues here I seize vpon" (001-257).

### Immediate Aftermath

[[cordelia|Cordelia]] bids farewell to her sisters, warning "Time shall vnfold what plighted cunning hides" (001-289). [[goneril|Goneril]] and [[regan|Regan]] privately assess [[king-lear|Lear]]'s unstable character: "he hath euer but slenderly knowne him[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]e" (001-299). [[goneril|Goneril]] concludes, "We must do something, and i'th' heate" (001-308), setting in motion the systematic stripping of [[king-lear|Lear]]'s remaining authority.

## Thematic Significance

The event introduces the opposition between [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|appearance]] and [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|reality]]. [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s "I want that glib and oylie Art, / To speake and purpose not" (001-228) establishes her as the moral center of the play, while [[king-lear|Lear]]'s inability to distinguish rhetoric from genuine [[love-in-king-lear|devotion]] reveals his fatal flaw. [[king-lear|Lear]] divests him[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]] of power while retaining its symbols, believing "the name, and all th' addition to a King" (001-126) can survive without "Sway, Reuennew, Execution" (001-127). This catastrophic misunderstanding about the nature of power and love sets every subsequent tragedy in motion.

## Dramatic Function

This event serves as the inciting incident. [[king-lear|Lear]]'s actions dismantle the political order and unleash ambition and cruelty ([[goneril|Goneril]], [[regan|Regan]], [[edmund|Edmund]]) that consume [[britain|the kingdom]]. The event also establishes the structural parallel between the [[king-lear|Lear]] and [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] plots: [[king-lear|Lear]] misjudges [[cordelia|Cordelia]] and trusts [[goneril|Goneril]]/[[regan|Regan]], just as [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] will misjudge [[edgar|Edgar]] and trust [[edmund|Edmund]].

## Notable Stagings

The division scene has been staged as formal court ritual (Trevor Nunn, 2007, RSC), as a political press conference (Sam Mendes, 2014, National Theatre), and as intimate family drama around a table (Richard Eyre, 2018). The map -- "Giue me the Map there" (001-024) -- is a crucial prop, handled with regal authority, childish eagerness, or senile uncertainty.
