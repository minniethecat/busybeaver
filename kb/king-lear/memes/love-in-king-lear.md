# Love in King Lear

The first word of dramatic substance in *[[king-lear|King Lear]]* is "love." [[king-lear|Lear]] demands that his daughters tell him "Which of you shall we say doth love us most" (001-038). The play then spends five acts demonstrating that love cannot be measured, performed on demand, or distinguished from its counterfeits by any test except suffering and time — by which point it is too late.

## Overview

Love in *[[king-lear|King Lear]]* appears in four modes: love as performance ([[goneril|Goneril]] and [[regan|Regan]]'s speeches), love as refusal ([[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s "[[nothing-and-nihilism-in-king-lear|Nothing]]"), love as service (Kent's disguised devotion, the [[the-fool|Fool]]'s constancy), and love as appetite ([[edmund|Edmund]]'s liaisons with [[goneril|Goneril]] and [[regan|Regan]]). The play pits these against each other, and what survives — barely — is love that does not speak its name: [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s return with an army "for love, dear love" (004-243), Kent's following [[king-lear|Lear]] to the grave.

## Key Manifestations

### Love as Performance and as Refusal

The [[division-of-the-kingdom|love test]] in Act 1, scene 1 is the play's originating catastrophe. [[goneril|Goneril]] and [[regan|Regan]] perform love as rhetorical competition: [[goneril|Goneril]] loves [[king-lear|Lear]] "more than word can wield the matter" (001-042); [[regan|Regan]] is "made of that [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]] metal as my sister" (001-056). [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s "I cannot heave / My heart into my mouth" (001-080) is the play's only authentic speech about love, and it costs her everything. The test establishes the play's central [[blindness-and-insight-in-king-lear|insight]]: love that can be performed on demand is not love.

### Love as Service

Kent's entire story is a definition of love as action. Banished for speaking [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|truth]], he returns in disguise to serve [[king-lear|Lear]] — endures the stocks, follows him into [[the-heath|the storm]], and arranges [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s rescue. He asks nothing, receives nothing, and at the play's end refuses [[duke-of-albany|Albany]]'s offer of shared [[authority-and-power-in-king-lear|rule]]: "I have a journey, sir, shortly to go; / My master calls me, I must not say no" (005-289).

### Cordelia's Return

[[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s return in Act 4 is the play's most powerful statement about love. She comes not to conquer but to rescue: "No blown ambition doth our arms incite, / But love, dear love, and our aged father's right" (004-243). The reunion with [[king-lear|Lear]] — "Pray do not mock me: / I am a very foolish fond old man" (004-575) — is the emotional center of the play.
