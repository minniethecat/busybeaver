# "Howl, howl, howl"

The most elemental cry in all of Shakespeare: Lear, holding his dead daughter, commands the world to mourn with him.

## Full Text

> Howle, howle, howle: O you are men of stones. (PN=005-382)

## Context

The battle is lost. Cordelia's forces have been defeated, and she and Lear have been taken prisoner. Edmund has ordered their deaths. Though Edgar mortally wounds Edmund in single combat and a reprieve is sent, it arrives too late. Lear enters, carrying Cordelia's body in his arms. The stage direction that precedes this line — "Enter Lear with Cordelia in his arms" — is one of the most famous images in all drama. The line that follows is not reasoned speech but pure grief made sound (PN=005-378).

The sequence leading to this moment is one of Shakespeare's most devastating structural choices. Just before Lear's entrance, the audience has witnessed Edgar's triumph over Edmund and Albany's moral resolution. Hope has briefly kindled — a reprieve has been sent. Then Lear enters with the body, and the audience understands, a beat before the characters do, that the reprieve arrived too late.

Earlier, the Gentleman had described Cordelia's grief for her father's suffering in terms that prefigure this moment:

> She shooke the holy water from her heauenly eyes, / And clamour moystned her [004-178]

Now the roles are reversed. It is Lear who holds Cordelia, and what moistened Cordelia's eyes — "holy water" — has become Lear's inarticulate howl. The play has traded tears for a sound beyond tears.

## Speaker

**[King Lear](king-lear)**

Lear is no longer a king, nor even the raging, half-mad prophet of the heath. He is simply a father, holding his dead child. His earlier madness has burned away, leaving a clarity that makes the grief sharper. In the lines that follow, he will veer between desperate hope and crushing reality, but this first cry — "Howl, howl, howl" — is grief before language, the sound the body makes when words are inadequate.

Earlier, Lear had told Cordelia in their brief moment of shared captivity:

> We two alone will sing like Birds i'th'Cage... / And take vpon's the mystery of things, / As if we were Gods spies. (PN=005-295)

That vision of shared, peaceful enclosure is brutally annihilated by this entrance. The cage becomes a corpse; the singing becomes a howl.

## Analysis

### Thematic Significance

The triple "howl" breaks the boundary between theatrical speech and primal utterance. By calling the onlookers "men of stones," Lear accuses them — and by extension the audience — of an inhuman stillness in the face of unbearable suffering. The line interrogates what art itself can do in the face of tragedy: can watching a play about grief ever be adequate to grief itself? Shakespeare pushes language to its breaking point and beyond, suggesting that some experiences can only be howled, not spoken.

The accusation of stoniness is particularly pointed. Stone is unfeeling, like the "flint" Regan and Goneril claimed their hearts were made of. But it is also the material of monuments — Lear implicitly contrasts the living, howling body with the statues that will later commemorate Cordelia. A stone memorial is not grief; only the howl is.

### Lear's Desperate Hope

In the lines immediately following the howl, Lear cycles through the stages of denial with terrifying speed. He holds a feather to Cordelia's lips, believing he sees it stir — "This feather stirs, she liues: if it be so, / It is a chance which do's redeeme all sorrowes / That euer I haue felt" [005-385]. He then seems to recognize the finality of death — "I know when one is dead, and when one liues: / She's dead as earth" — only to lapse again into the delusion that Cordelia speaks:

> Cordelia, Cordelia, stay a little. Ha: / What is't thou saist? Her voice was euer soft, / Gentle, and low, an excellent thing in woman [005-396]

The oscillation between recognition and denial — "she's dead" followed by "what is't thou saist?" — is one of the most psychologically acute representations of grief in literature. Lear's mind cannot hold the fact of Cordelia's death; it keeps slipping away from him, and each time he rediscovers it, the wound is fresh.

### Dramatic Function

This moment is the emotional and structural climax of the tragedy. The Folio text gives Lear no further major speeches after this — only fragments, questions, and his final "Neuer, neuer, neuer, neuer, neuer" (PN=005-413). The howl marks the point after which coherent speech is no longer possible. The dramatic weight falls entirely on the actor's physical and vocal performance; the text provides only the raw material.

Kent's stunned response — "Is this the promis'd end?" — immediately follows and amplifies the moment, suggesting that what we are witnessing is nothing less than the end of the world, the apocalypse rendered in the death of one innocent woman. Edgar's reply, "Or image of that horror," keeps the question open: is this the thing itself, or only a representation of it? The meta-theatrical question echoes Lear's own — what can art do, what can a play do, in the face of this?

## Allusions & Echoes

The command to "howl" recalls Lear's earlier address to the storm — "Blow windes, and crack your cheeks" (PN=003-033) — but the register has shifted entirely. There, Lear commanded the cosmos; here, he commands fellow humans to feel. The "men of stones" image also recalls Gloucester's heart, which "burst smilingly" at the moment of recognition with Edgar (PN=005-274): grief petrifies or dissolves, but either way, it exceeds the body's capacity to contain it.

The triple repetition — "howl, howl, howl" — has a structural parallel in Lear's final "Neuer, neuer, neuer, neuer, neuer" (five repetitions). The threefold howl marks the entrance of grief; the fivefold never marks its final, exhausted acceptance. Between them lies the entire arc of Lear's final speech — from rage to hope to despair to silence.

The howl also echoes backward to Lear's first entrance in the play, where he commanded "Attend the Lords of France and Burgundy" with the authority of a king (001-026). The distance from that commanding voice to this inarticulate cry measures the full trajectory of the tragedy.

The play's final word, spoken by Edgar, is "obey" — a return to the language of order and duty. But the howl lingers. It is the sound that no obedience can quiet.

## Performance Notes

The howl is a notorious challenge for actors. Some — like Laurence Olivier (1983) — delivered it as a series of rising animal cries; others, like John Gielgud (1955), made it a whispered, broken sound, almost inaudible. The Folio text provides no punctuation beyond commas and a period; the actor must decide where each howl begins and ends, and whether the "O" that follows continues the cry or begins a new thought. Productions that stage Cordelia's body prominently — center stage, fully visible — heighten the impact; Lear's howl then becomes a sound the audience has been dreading since the tragedy began its final descent.

In Ian McKellen's 2007 RSC production, directed by Trevor Nunn, the howl was preceded by a long, almost unbearable silence — Lear standing motionless with Cordelia in his arms — before the first "howl" erupted as a sound that seemed to come from somewhere outside the actor's body. Critics noted that the silence was as powerful as the cry, if not more so, suggesting that the actor's greatest challenge in this moment is not vocal but physical: to embody the stillness against which the howl becomes meaningful.
