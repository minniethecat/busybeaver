**Act 2 Scene 4** is the scene in which [[king-lear|Lear]]'s authority is systematically dismantled. Arriving at [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s castle expecting refuge, he finds his servant Kent in the stocks — a deliberate humiliation by [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]] and [[regan|Regan]]. The scene that follows is a masterclass in psychological cruelty conducted in the language of reason.

## Summary

[[king-lear|Lear]] discovers Kent in the stocks and rages at [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]] and [[regan|Regan]], who refuse to see him. When they finally appear, [[regan|Regan]] urges [[king-lear|Lear]] to return to [[goneril|Goneril]] and apologise. [[goneril|Goneril]] arrives, and the sisters unite in a campaign of attrition: [[king-lear|Lear]]'s hundred knights are reduced to fifty, then twenty-five, then "What need one?" The question — posed in the name of practical necessity — is really an assault on [[king-lear|Lear]]'s very [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|identity]]. His response, "O reason not the need," is one of the play's most famous speeches, arguing that human [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|being]]s require more than bare subsistence to be human.

The scene ends with [[king-lear|Lear]] storming out into the coming tempest, followed only by the [[the-fool|Fool]] and Kent, as [[regan|Regan]] orders the doors locked behind him.

## Key Moments

- **Kent in the stocks**: A visual emblem of the disrespect [[king-lear|Lear]] now commands
- **The arithmetic of cruelty**: [[regan|Regan]] and [[goneril|Goneril]]'s coordinated reduction of [[king-lear|Lear]]'s knights is a calculated assault on his dignity
- **"O reason not the need"**: [[king-lear|Lear]]'s great speech (002-423) distinguishes between animal necessity and human flourishing

## Significance

This scene completes [[king-lear|Lear]]'s fall from king to beggar. The daughters' cold arithmetic — "What need you fiue and twenty? Ten? Or fiue?" — exposes a world in which human bonds are reduced to calculation. [[king-lear|Lear]]'s flight into [[the-heath|the storm]] is both physical escape and metaphysical rebellion.
