# The Heath

A desolate, open landscape blasted by one of the most violent storms in all of drama. Here [[king-lear|Lear]], stripped of home and power, confronts the elements, his own madness, and the nature of justice.

## Overview

The [[act-3-scene-2|heath scene]]s span Act 3, Scene 1 through Scene 6 (with the storm persisting into [[act-3-scene-4|the hovel scene]]s). Cast out by [[goneril|Gonerill]] and [[regan|Regan]] into a night of unprecedented fury, [[king-lear|Lear]] encounters on the heath the naked [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|truth]] of human vulnerability. The storm strips away all the trappings of [[authority-and-power-in-king-lear|kingship]], exposing the "poore, infirme, weake, and dispis'd old man" beneath (003-060). On the heath, [[king-lear|Lear]] meets [[edgar|Edgar]] disguised as [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|Poor Tom]] — a figure of absolute destitution — and in that meeting achieves a flash of compassion for "houselesse pouertie" (003-180) he had never known as king.

## Description in the Text

The heath is established entirely through dialogue and stage directions. The recurring rubric "Storme still" (003-001, 003-043, 003-153, 003-203) is one of the most evocative stage directions in the Folio, sustaining the tempest as an active dramatic presence across four scenes.

Shakespeare does not describe the heath's physical features; he makes the audience feel it through the storm. Kent calls the night one of "sheets of Fire, such bursts of horrid Thunder, / Such groanes of roaring Winde, and Raine" such as he has "neuer / Remember to haue heard" (003-082-084). [[king-lear|Lear]] him[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]] addresses the storm as a cosmic antagonist, daring the winds to "crack your cheeks" and the thunder to "Strike flat the thicke Rotundity o'th' world" (003-044, 003-050).

The heath has no shelter — Kent points to "a Houell" nearby, but it is a bare, inhospitable structure (003-100). The desolation is moral as well as physical: [[king-lear|Lear]]'s daughters have barred their doors against him on this night.

## Dramatic Function

The heath serves as the anti-court: a space where [[order-and-chaos-in-king-lear|hierarchy]], ceremony, and social [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|identity]] dissolve. [[king-lear|Lear]] entered Act 1 as a king commanding the map of [[britain|Britain]]; on the heath he stands "bare-headed" (003-099), asking "Is man no more then this?" (003-223). His madness mirrors the storm's [[order-and-chaos-in-king-lear|chaos]] — [[king-lear|Lear]] him[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]] calls it "the tempest in my mind" (003-165).

The heath also functions as a crucible of recognition. Only when exposed to the storm does [[king-lear|Lear]] think of the suffering of others: "Poore naked wretches, where so ere you are / That bide the pelting of this pittilesse storme" (003-183-184). His prayer for the "houselesse pouertie" (003-180) marks the moral turning point of the play.

### Symbolic Meaning

The heath embodies the collapse of order — political, familial, cosmic. The storm it[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]] symbolises divine judgment ([[king-lear|Lear]] calls on the "great Goddes" to "Finde out their enemies" (003-087-089)) and the indifference of nature. It is a place of [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|truth]]-telling: [[king-lear|Lear]], the [[the-fool|Fool]], and [[edgar|Edgar]] as [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|Poor Tom]] speak their most unvarnished wisdom here. The heath's barren openness contrasts with the enclosed cruelty of [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s castle, where the blinding occurs behind closed doors.

## Real-World References

Shakespeare's heath is geographically unspecific but resonates with the wild moorlands and heaths of [[britain|Britain]] — desolate, wind-scoured spaces that an Elizabethan audience would recognise. Some productions set it on the heaths of Yorkshire or [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]]. The open-air stage of the Globe would have heightened the imaginative power of the storm for the original audience, as they too sat exposed to the elements.
