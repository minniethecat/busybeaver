**Act 5 Scene 2** is one of the shortest scenes in *King Lear* — barely twenty lines — yet it contains the play's definitive statement on endurance. The battle between the French forces (with Cordelia) and the British (under Edmund and Albany) occurs offstage. The scene shows only its aftermath.

## Summary

Lear and Cordelia cross the stage with drum and colours, then exit to battle. Edgar enters, having left Gloucester under a tree for safety, and returns moments later with the news: "King Lear hath lost, he and his Daughter tane." He urges Gloucester to flee, but Gloucester, in despair, refuses: "a man may rot euen heere." Edgar's response is the play's philosophical heart:

> Men must endure
> Their going hence, euen as their comming hither,
> Ripenesse is all
> — 005-358 (approximate)

## Significance

This scene is the hinge between the battle and the final tragedy in Scene 3. Its brevity and compression — the battle itself is invisible, reduced to alarums offstage — emphasises that martial conflict is not the play's real subject. The real drama is in how characters face defeat. Edgar's "Ripenesse is all" echoes Hamlet's "the readiness is all" and provides the play's most stoic counterweight to Gloucester's despair.
