# "More sinned against than sinning"

King Lear, stripped of his retinue and exposed to the storm, voices a dawning awareness: that he has suffered more wrong than he has inflicted.

## Full Text

> I am a man, more sinn'd against, then sinning. (PN=003-098)

## Context

Lear has just been turned out into the storm by Regan and Cornwall after Goneril's earlier rejection. His hundred knights have been taken from him, and he wanders the heath in a state of mounting fury and grief. This line comes near the end of his first storm speech, after he has called upon the elements to destroy the world. A shift occurs here — from rage at the cosmos to a quieter, more self-aware reflection on his own moral standing (PN=003-090).

The immediate dramatic trigger is Lear's earlier threat to Regan — "I will do such things — / What they are yet I know not, but they shall be / The terrors of the earth" (PN=003-078) — which he now seems to recognize as hollow. The storm strips away his royal pretensions and forces a reckoning with what he has actually done.

## Speaker

**[King Lear](king-lear)**

The aging king, having divided his kingdom between Goneril and Regan, now finds himself betrayed by both daughters. At this moment, Lear oscillates between grandiosity and vulnerability — he is, for the first time, acknowledging that he is not purely a victim but also an agent of harm. The line captures this ambivalence: "sinn'd against" emphasizes his suffering, while "sinning" acknowledges his own culpability.

Earlier in the scene, Lear had commanded the storm to "crack Nature's moulds" and "spill" the seeds of ingratitude (PN=003-008). This line — quiet, almost confessional — marks his retreat from cosmic rage to moral introspection.

## Analysis

### Thematic Significance

This line crystallizes one of the play's central tensions: the difficulty of assigning moral responsibility. Lear asserts his status as more victim than perpetrator, yet the play has already shown his rashness in dividing the kingdom and disinheriting Cordelia. The ambiguity is deliberate — Shakespeare refuses to let the audience settle on a simple verdict (PN=001-170).

The play returns repeatedly to the question of whether suffering is proportionate to fault. Gloucester, blinded for loyalty, echoes Lear's sentiment in his own way:

> As flies to wanton boys, are we to th'Gods; / They kill us for their sport. (PN=004-034)

This darker formulation — that suffering has no moral logic at all — stands as the play's bleakest answer to Lear's implied question. Lear's line, by contrast, still assumes a moral universe where sin and suffering can be compared and weighed.

### Dramatic Function

The line marks a crucial pivot in Lear's arc. His earlier speeches on the heath call for apocalyptic destruction; here, he turns inward. This shift toward self-examination prepares the ground for his later encounter with [[Poor Tom]] (Edgar in disguise) and his famous recognition: "unaccommodated man is no more but such a poor, bare, forked animal" (PN=003-223). The line also establishes a pattern — Lear will oscillate between self-pity and genuine insight for the remainder of the play.

By Act 4, Lear's self-awareness has deepened considerably. Confronting the blinded Gloucester, he acknowledges:

> I am a very foolish fond old man... / I fear I am not in my perfect mind. (PN=004-242)

And in the reconciliation scene with Cordelia:

> If you have poison for me, I will drink it. / I know you do not love me; for your sisters / Have, as I do remember, done me wrong. / You have some cause, they have not. (PN=004-328)

The progression from "more sinn'd against than sinning" — still defensively comparative — to "you have some cause" — a frank admission that Cordelia was wronged — traces Lear's moral education across the play's second half.

## Allusions & Echoes

The idea of sin and retribution runs throughout the play. Edmund's soliloquy on bastardy frames his plotting as revenge against a society that has wronged him (PN=001-305). Gloucester's blinding prompts his realization that he "stumbled when he saw" — that moral blindness preceded physical blindness (PN=004-014). Lear's line anticipates these later reckonings with guilt and suffering.

The language of "sinned against" also resonates with the play's persistent legal and commercial metaphors. Lear frames his moral condition in terms of debt and accounting — more against than owing — a mode of thinking that echoes his disastrous love test, where love was quantified in speech and rewarded in land (PN=001-065).

## Performance Notes

Actors have interpreted this line across a wide spectrum: some emphasize the self-pity ("sinn'd against"), others the dawning moral awareness ("sinning"). Paul Scofield (1962) delivered it quietly, as a private realization; Ian McKellen (2007) made it a defiant challenge to the heavens. The ambiguity of the Folio's comma placement — "more sinn'd against, then sinning" — allows for both readings.
