# "O, reason not the need!"

Spoken by [[king-lear|King Lear]] to his daughters [[goneril|Goneril]] and [[regan|Regan]] in Act 2, Scene 4, this speech marks the moment Lear's authority collapses into raw, existential pleading — the king who once commanded "nothing" as a curse now confronts the terror of having nothing himself.

## Full Text

> O reason not the need: our basest beggars / Are in the poorest thing superfluous. / Allow not Nature, more then Nature needs: / Mans life is cheape as Beastes. Thou art a Lady; / If onely to goe warme were gorgeous, / Why Nature needs not what thou gorgeous wear'st, / Which scarcely keepes thee warme, but for true need: / You Heauens giue me that patience, patience I need. (002-400)

## Context

[[king-lear|Lear]] has arrived at [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s castle after fleeing [[goneril|Goneril]]'s household, only to find [[regan|Regan]] and [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]] already there — and in league with [[goneril|Goneril]]. The two sisters, working in concert, methodically strip away his hundred knights. First [[goneril|Goneril]] asks: "What need you five and twenty? Ten? Or five?" (002-395). [[regan|Regan]] goes further: "What need one?" (002-396). Lear's reply — "O, reason not the need" — is his last coherent philosophical statement before his language fractures into the storm-blasted ravings of Act 3.

## Speaker

**[[king-lear|King Lear]]**

At this moment in the play, Lear has lost almost everything: his kingdom, his authority, [[cordelia|Cordelia]], and now his knights — the last visible sign of his kingship. He is not yet mad, but the speech marks the threshold of his sanity. Its rhetorical clarity — its ability to reason about why he should not have to reason — is what will disappear in the scenes to come.

## Analysis

### Literal Meaning

Lear argues that "need" is the wrong measure for human life. Even the poorest beggar has something beyond bare survival — "the poorest thing superfluous." If human existence were reduced to mere biological necessity — "Nature, more then Nature needs" — then "man's life is cheap as beast's." He points to [[goneril|Goneril]]'s own clothing: her gorgeous garments do not keep her warmer than simple wool would; they exist for a purpose beyond need. In the same way, his knights are not a military necessity — they are the "superfluous" thing that makes his life human. He ends by appealing not to his daughters but to the heavens: "give me that patience, patience I need" — the repetition of "patience" underscoring his desperation.

### Thematic Significance

The speech crystallizes the play's meditation on what it means to be human. Lear's argument anticipates the [[storm-on-the-heath|heath scene]] in Act 3, where he encounters "unaccommodated man" — [[edgar|Poor Tom]] — and asks: "Is man no more than this?" (003-340). The speech draws a line between animal existence (need) and human existence (dignity, ceremony, love) that the play will test to destruction. By Act 4, the same Lear who spoke so eloquently about superfluity will be crowned with wildflowers, his language reduced to fragments.

The speech also resonates with the play's economic language. Cordelia's "I cannot heave my heart into my mouth" was a refusal to quantify love; Lear's "reason not the need" is a refusal to quantify dignity. Both speeches lose. The play's tragic logic insists that what cannot be numbered will be taken away.

### Dramatic Function

This is Lear's last moment of rhetorical mastery. After this speech, the storm breaks — literally and figuratively — and Lear's next major utterance is the elemental howl of "Blow, winds, and crack your cheeks!" (003-001). The speech functions as the hinge between the play's political tragedy (a king who gives away his power) and its philosophical tragedy (a man confronting the void left when all social markers are stripped away).

## Allusions and Echoes

The argument that superfluity defines humanity echoes throughout the play. [[king-lear|Lear]]'s own earlier blindness to Cordelia's truth was a failure to distinguish the superfluous (flattering words) from the necessary (honest love). [[edmund|Edmund]]'s soliloquy in Act 1, Scene 2 makes a related but opposed argument: if social custom is mere superfluity, then why shouldn't "Nature" — raw appetite — rule? The play never settles the question Lear raises; it only strips away every superfluity until what remains, in the final scene, is a father holding his dead daughter, asking why a dog should have life "and thou no breath at all" (005-280).

## Performance Notes

The speech is a formidable test for any actor playing Lear. It must bridge the regal command of the opening scene with the shattered madness of the heath — an actor must show Lear reasoning clearly about the collapse of reason itself. Paul Scofield's 1962 RSC Lear (directed by Peter Brook) delivered the speech with an icy, intellectual clarity that made the subsequent breakdown all the more harrowing. Ian Holm's 1997 Lear at the National Theatre emphasized the speech's vulnerability — a king who knows he is pleading and cannot stop himself.
