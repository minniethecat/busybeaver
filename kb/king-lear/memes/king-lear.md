## Overview

King Lear is the titular protagonist of Shakespeare's tragedy and one of the most demanding [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|role]]s in the classical repertoire. An aging monarch who decides to abdicate his throne and divide his [[britain|kingdom]] among his three daughters based on their professions of love, Lear's decision triggers a cascade of [[loyalty-and-betrayal-in-king-lear|betrayal]], madness, and ultimately tragedy. His journey from arrogant king to mad wanderer to heartbroken father constitutes one of the most powerful character arcs in dramatic literature.

At the play's opening, Lear is an absolute [[authority-and-power-in-king-lear|rule]]r accustomed to unquestioning obedience. He confuses the ceremony of [[authority-and-power-in-king-lear|kingship]] with its substance, believing that he can retain "the name and all th'addition to a King" while divesting actual power. This fundamental misunderstanding of political authority — that one cannot be king without the [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|reality]] of [[authority-and-power-in-king-lear|kingship]] — is the fatal error from which all subsequent catastrophe flows.

The [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|role]] of Lear demands an actor capable of spanning regal authority, towering rage, fractured madness, childlike tenderness, and bottomless grief, often within a single scene. Major actors who have undertaken the [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|role]] include Paul Scofield, Laurence Olivier, Ian Holm, Ian McKellen, Anthony Hopkins, and Glenda Jackson, each bringing distinct interpretations to the character's blend of grandeur and vulnerability.

Lear's significance extends far beyond the stage. He has been read as an allegory of the human condition, a study in the psychology of aging and dementia, a political parable about the nature of authority, and an existential meditation on the possibility of meaning in a universe that offers no guarantees. The character's enduring power lies in his capacity to contain all these readings simultaneously.

## Role in the Play

Lear opens the play by demanding his daughters compete in a love-test to determine their share of [[britain|the kingdom]]: "Meane time we shal expresse our darker purpose. / Giue me the Map there" (001-023). The phrase "darker purpose" hints at the psychological complexity beneath the political ceremony. His plan — to retire while keeping the title of king — reveals a fatal misunderstanding: political authority cannot be separated from its exercise.

When his youngest and most beloved daughter [[cordelia|Cordelia]] refuses to flatter him, answering only that she loves him "according to my bond," Lear disowns her in a fury, proclaiming [[nothing-will-come-of-nothing|"Nothing will come of nothing"]] (001-078). This fatal misjudgment — valuing rhetorical display over authentic [[love-in-king-lear|devotion]] — sets the entire tragedy in motion.

Lear compounds his error by banishing Kent, the one courtier who speaks plainly, shouting "Out of my [[blindness-and-insight-in-king-lear|sight]]" (001-151) when Kent tries to intervene. The banishment of honest counsel is the act of a tyrant, not a king. It demonstrates that Lear's authority, while absolute in form, has become indistinguishable from the demand for total compliance.

Stripped of power by [[goneril|Goneril]] and [[regan|Regan]], who prove to be the true monsters, Lear descends into madness on the heath during a violent storm. There, in his suffering, he gains wisdom he never possessed as king. He challenges the elements themselves — "Blow windes, & crack your cheeks; Rage, blow / You Cataracts, and Hyrricano's spout" (003-044) — and discovers empathy for the poor and dispossessed.

His great speech of resistance — "O reason not the need: our basest Beggers / Are in the poorest thing superfluous" (002-585) — articulates the play's central philosophical question: what does a human [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|being]] need to exist with dignity? The [[blindness-and-insight-in-king-lear|insight]] that even beggars possess something "superfluous" defines human worth as that which exceeds bare necessity.

On the heath, Lear encounters [[edgar|Edgar]] disguised as [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|Poor Tom]], the "vnaccommodated man" who is "the thing it [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]e." Lear's attempt to strip off his own clothes in imitation is both madness and the play's most radical philosophical gesture: the king who once measured his worth in knights and territory now sees that human [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|identity]] stripped to its essence is "a poore, bare, forked Animall."

His reunion with [[cordelia|Cordelia]] offers a brief moment of redemption. Awakening to find her before him, Lear speaks with bewildered tenderness: "I am a very foolish fond old man, / Fourescore and vpward" (004-576). When [[cordelia|Cordelia]] says there is "No cause, no cause" for him to ask forgiveness, the moment represents a grace that Lear, in his earlier demand for performative love, did not deserve.

The play's devastating conclusion sees Lear enter carrying [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s dead body, howling with grief: "Howle, howle, howle: O you are men of stones" (005-382). His final speech — "Why should a Dog, a Horse, a Rat haue life, / And thou no breath at all?" (005-443) — collapses all distinctions of rank and species, reducing existence to the bare question of breath. The fivefold "Neuer, neuer, neuer, neuer, neuer" is among the most devastating repetitions in English poetry. He dies believing he sees life in her lips: "Looke there, looke there."

Lear's dramatic function is unique among Shakespearean tragic heroes. Unlike Hamlet, whose delay stems from intellectual paralysis, or Macbeth, whose crime is ambition, Lear's error is catastrophic misjudgment fueled by emotional need. He demands love as performance, and when he receives the real thing — [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s honest silence — he cannot recognize it. The entire play traces the consequences of that failure of perception, and Lear's journey is fundamentally an education in [[blindness-and-insight-in-king-lear|seeing]]: "See better Lear," Kent pleads early on, and the play's arc is Lear's slow, agonizing acquisition of that better [[blindness-and-insight-in-king-lear|sight]], culminating in his final ambiguous vision of [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s lips.

## Character Arc

**Act 1: The Abdication and Dispossession**

Lear enters in full majesty, commanding the attendance of [[king-of-france|France]] and [[duke-of-burgundy|Burgundy]], dividing his [[britain|kingdom]] with cartographic precision. "Giue me the Map there" — the gesture is imperial, absolute. His plan to "shake all Cares and Businesse from our Age, / Conferring them on yonger strengths, while we / Vnburthen'd crawle toward death" (001-023) imagines a graceful retirement but ignores the political [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|reality]] that power, once given, cannot be retrieved.

The [[division-of-the-kingdom|love test]] is a public ritual designed to confirm what Lear already believes: that [[cordelia|Cordelia]], his favorite, loves him most. When [[cordelia|Cordelia]] refuses to perform, answering "[[nothing-and-nihilism-in-king-lear|Nothing]] my Lord," Lear's rage is not merely anger but wounded love. He had "lou'd her most, and thought to set my rest / On her kind nursery" — the image of Lear as child, [[cordelia|Cordelia]] as nursing mother, inverts the [[nature-in-king-lear|natural order]] and anticipates the play's systematic dismantling of [[order-and-chaos-in-king-lear|hierarchy]].

His response is immediate and absolute: "Let it be so, thy [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|truth]] then be thy dowre" (001-097). He banishes Kent with equal fury — "Come not betweene the Dragon and his wrath" (001-111) — silencing the only voice that speaks [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|truth]] to power. At [[goneril|Goneril]]'s palace, he discovers that his status as retired king means nothing. She complains about his hundred knights — "men of choise, and rarest parts" — treating them as a nuisance rather than the symbols of royal [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|identity]] Lear believes them to be. His curse on her fertility — calling on [[nature-in-king-lear|Nature]] to "drie vp in her the Organs of increase" — is his most violent exercise of patriarchal authority even as his actual power vanishes.

**Act 2: The Arithmetic of Ingratitude**

At [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s castle, Lear confronts his daughters' systematic reduction of his [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|identity]]. [[regan|Regan]] insists he return to [[goneril|Goneril]]; [[goneril|Goneril]] arrives; together they whittle his hundred knights to fifty, to twenty-five, to "What need one?" The reduction is not practical but symbolic — each knight subtracted is a piece of Lear's [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|identity]] removed. He clings to the number as if it were his soul: "O reason not the need: our basest Beggers / Are in the poorest thing superfluous" (002-585).

The speech is Lear's first genuine philosophical reflection. His argument that even beggars possess something "superfluous" — that human dignity resides precisely in what exceeds bare necessity — is one of the profoundest moments in Shakespeare. Yet the speech immediately collapses into incoherent threat: "I will do such things — what they are yet I know not — but they shall be / The terrors of the earth." The movement from philosophy to rage to the edge of madness charts his psychological crisis with terrifying precision. He rushes out into [[the-heath|the storm]], crying "O [[the-fool|Fool]]e, I shall go mad" (002-607), preferring the violence of nature to the violence of his daughters' ingratitude.

**Act 3: The Storm and the Discovery of Man**

On the heath, Lear delivers the apocalyptic address to the elements: "Blow windes, & crack your cheeks; Rage, blow / You Cataracts, and Hyrricano's spout, / Till you haue drench'd our Steeples, drown the Cockes" (003-044). He commands [[the-heath|the storm]] as he once commanded his court, but the imperatives are now futile — nature, unlike courtiers, does not obey. The speech modulates from command to recognition: "I taxe not you, you Elements with vnkindnesse. / I neuer gaue you Kingdome, call'd you Children." [[nature-in-king-lear|Nature]], unlike his daughters, owes him nothing, and in this recognition there is a strange liberation.

In the hovel, Lear encounters [[edgar|Edgar]] disguised as [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|Poor Tom]] — naked, shivering, apparently mad. Lear asks the question that strips civilization to its foundations: "Is man no more then this? ... Thou art the thing it [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]e: vnaccommodated man, is no more but such a poore, bare, forked Animall as thou art." He tears off his clothes in imitation, the king becoming the beggar, the highest becoming the lowest. The mock-trial of [[goneril|Goneril]] and [[regan|Regan]] — conducted with [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|Poor Tom]] and the [[the-fool|Fool]] as judges — is both heartbreaking (Lear's madness) and devastating (Lear's [[blindness-and-insight-in-king-lear|insight]]): he arraigns the absent daughters in a proceeding with no legal force and total moral validity.

His resolve — "No, I will be the patterne of all patience, / I will say nothing" (003-074) — echoes [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s strategy: the man who could not bear her silence now embraces it. [[the-fool|The Fool]]'s dis[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|appearance]] after this scene marks Lear's descent into a solitude so complete that even his [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|truth]]-teller can no longer accompany him.

**Act 4: [[madness-in-king-lear|Madness]], Vision, and Reconciliation**

Lear's encounter with the blinded [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] on the heath produces his most radical philosophical [[blindness-and-insight-in-king-lear|insight]]. "None do's offend, none, I say none, Ile able 'em" (004-379) — a doctrine of universal innocence that is both absurd and profound. If all human [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|being]]s are equally frail, driven by forces beyond their control, then [[justice-and-injustice-in-king-lear|punishment]] is merely power disguised as justice. The speech on the corrupt judge who whips the whore while lusting after her is some of the sharpest social criticism Shakespeare ever wrote.

"Thou must be patient; we came crying hither" (004-384) — his sermon to [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] on the nature of suffering. "When we are borne, we cry that we are come / To this great stage of [[the-fool|Fool]]es." Birth it[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]] is a protest, a cry of objection at [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|being]] brought into a world of suffering. The image of the world as a stage collapses all distinctions — between king and beggar, sanity and madness, tragedy and farce.

Lear's reunion with [[cordelia|Cordelia]] is the play's emotional climax. Awakening from restorative sleep — dressed in fresh garments, the symbols of restored humanity — he sees [[cordelia|Cordelia]] and believes he has died: "You do me wrong to take me out o'th' graue, / Thou art a Soule in blisse, but I am bound / Vpon a wheele of fire." The wheel of fire — eternal, tormenting — is his spiritual [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]-portrait.

His recognition of [[cordelia|Cordelia]] is halting, uncertain, achingly vulnerable: "I am a very foolish fond old man, / Fourescore and vpward" (004-576). The specificity of "Fourescore and vpward, / Not an houre more, nor lesse" is the language of a mind trying to grasp the simplest facts of its own existence. His fear that he is [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|being]] mocked — "Do not laugh at me" — reveals the depth of his damage. And yet he trusts: "I thinke this Lady / To be my childe [[cordelia|Cordelia]]." The reconciliation culminates in an inversion of the [[division-of-the-kingdom|love test]]: Lear kneels before [[cordelia|Cordelia]], and her "No cause, no cause" erases the debt that Lear's guilt demands.

**Act 5: The Final Catastrophe**

Captured by [[edmund|Edmund]]'s forces, Lear achieves a transcendent vision: "We two alone will sing like Birds i'th' Cage: / When thou dost aske me blessing, Ile kneele downe / And aske of thee forgiuenesse" (005-109). King and daughter exchange blessings and forgiveness in a cycle without [[order-and-chaos-in-king-lear|hierarchy]]. "Vpon such sacrifices my [[cordelia|Cordelia]], / The Gods themselues throw Incense" (005-121).

But [[edmund|Edmund]]'s deathbed reprieve arrives too late. Lear enters carrying [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s body — the father carrying the dead child, the king carrying [[britain|the kingdom]]'s last hope. His opening cry — "Howle, howle, howle: O you are men of stones" — is grief so absolute it exceeds language. The scene oscillates between desperate hope — "This feather stirs, she liues" — and the accumulating certainty of death.

His final speech — "Why should a Dog, a Horse, a Rat haue life, / And thou no breath at all? Thou'lt come no more, / Neuer, neuer, neuer, neuer, neuer" (005-443) — collapses all distinctions between species, all hierarchies of [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|being]], reducing existence to the bare, unanswerable question of breath. The sudden domestic detail — "Pray you vndo this Button. Thanke you Sir" — grounds cosmic grief in the body of an old man who cannot breathe. His final words — "Looke there, looke there" — leave permanently ambiguous whether he dies in deluded hope or final vision. The ambiguity is the point: Lear dies on the threshold between meaning and meaninglessness.

### Language and Imagery

Lear's language charts his psychological journey with extraordinary precision. In Act 1, he speaks in the royal "we" and issues imperatives — "Attend," "Giue me," "Know." His sentences are structured, hierarchical, confident. By Act 2, the imperatives become pleas, the confident assertions fracture into questions. By Act 3, his language breaks entirely into fragments, exclamations, wordless howls — the royal "we" has become the solitary "I," then the universal "we" of shared human suffering.

The play's central imagery clusters around several key patterns.

**Sight and blindness**: Lear's demand that Kent "auoid my [[blindness-and-insight-in-king-lear|sight]]" in [[act-1-scene-1|1.1]], his repeated invocations of his own eyes ("Old fond eyes, / Beweepe this cause againe, Ile plucke ye out"), and the terrible irony of his encounter with the literally blinded [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]. Lear's journey is from blindness of perception to clarity of vision — though whether his final vision (of [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s moving lips) is true [[blindness-and-insight-in-king-lear|sight]] or delusion remains the play's unresolvable question.

**Clothing and nakedness**: The king who enters in royal robes ends tearing off his garments on the heath, recognizing that "robes and furr'd gownes hide all." The stripping of clothes is the stripping of social [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|identity]] — Lear discovers that beneath the costume of [[authority-and-power-in-king-lear|kingship]] lies only "a poore, bare, forked Animall." His restoration in Act 4 begins with fresh garments, suggesting that humanity can be re-clothed as well as stripped.

**The storm**: The tempest on the heath is at once a projection of Lear's psychological state, a symbol of cosmic disorder, and a literal weather event. Lear's address to [[the-heath|the storm]] blurs the boundary between inner and outer worlds — the "tempest" in his mind and [[the-heath|the storm]] on the heath become indistinguishable. The storm is also the play's great leveler: in it, king and beggar, sane man and madman, human and animal become indistinguishable.

**Animals**: Lear's language is saturated with animal imagery — dragons, serpents, wolves, tigers, pelicans, dogs, horses, rats. As his world disintegrates, the human descends toward the animal. Yet the play never settles whether "beastliness" belongs to the cruel ([[goneril|Goneril]] and [[regan|Regan]] as "pelican daughters," "Tigers, not daughters") or to the vulnerable (Lear asking "Why should a Dog, a Horse, a Rat haue life?"). The animal is both the threat and the measure of human fragility.

**[[nothing-and-nihilism-in-king-lear|Nothing]]**: [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s "[[nothing-and-nihilism-in-king-lear|Nothing]]" in Act 1 echoes through the entire play, accruing meanings: the [[nothing-and-nihilism-in-king-lear|void]] of disinheritance, the [[nothing-and-nihilism-in-king-lear|emptiness]] of flattery, the [[nothing-and-nihilism-in-king-lear|zero]] of existential negation, and finally — in [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s death — the absolute absence that no language can fill. Lear learns that [[nothing-will-come-of-nothing|"Nothing will come of nothing"]] is not merely a threat but a metaphysical [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|truth]] from which there is no escape.

## Performance History

The [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|role]] of Lear has attracted the greatest actors of every generation.

**Early traditions**: David Garrick restored Shakespeare's text (partially) to the stage in the mid-18th century after decades of Tate's adaptation. [[edmund|Edmund]] Kean's 1820 Lear was praised for its raw emotional power. Henry Irving's 1892 production emphasized spectacle and pictorial staging. John Gielgud played Lear multiple times, first at age 26 (too young, by his own admission) and finally in a celebrated 1955 production designed by Isamu Noguchi.

**Mid-20th century**: Laurence Olivier's 1946 Lear at the Old Vic established the template of the "grand" Lear — titanic rage giving way to monumental grief. But it was Paul Scofield's 1962 RSC Lear, directed by Peter Brook, that redefined the [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|role]] for the modern era. Brook stripped away spectacle and emphasized the play's existential bleakness; Scofield's Lear was not a roaring titan but a stoic, weathered survivor, his voice "like iron crumbling into rust." This production, alongside Kott's absurdist reading, set the dominant tone for late-20th-century interpretation.

**Late 20th century**: Ian Holm's 1997 Lear at the National Theatre (directed by Richard Eyre) used Holm's small physical stature to make the loss of authority palpable — this was a king whose power had always been performative, and when the performance collapsed, nothing was left.

**21st century**: Ian McKellen's 2007 RSC Lear, directed by Trevor Nunn, emphasized tenderness beneath the rage; his reunion with [[cordelia|Cordelia]] was almost unbearably intimate. Glenda Jackson's 2016 Old Vic Lear, performed when Jackson was 80, foregrounded the character's authority as a construct — kingliness as a [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|role]] that cracks under pressure. Anthony Hopkins's 2018 BBC Lear explored dementia as a medical condition, the cognitive decline made harrowingly specific.

The [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|role]] continues to challenge the world's greatest actors. It demands, uniquely in the classical repertoire, the ability to play both god and worm, king and beggar, sage and madman — often within a single scene.

## Critical Perspectives

**Restoration and Eighteenth Century**: Nahum Tate's 1681 adaptation, which gave the play a happy ending with Lear restored to the throne and [[cordelia|Cordelia]] married to [[edgar|Edgar]], dominated the English stage for over 150 years. Tate's revision reflected a neoclassical sensibility that found Shakespeare's catastrophe morally and aesthetically intolerable. Samuel Johnson confessed he was "so shocked by [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s death" that he could scarcely reread [[act-5-scene-3|the final scene]], endorsing Tate's version. Johnson's response registers the sheer emotional violence of the original ending — a violence that eighteenth-century aesthetic norms could not accommodate.

**Romantic Criticism**: Coleridge and Hazlitt saw Lear as a metaphysical drama about the nature of existence it[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]. For Coleridge, Lear's madness was not pathology but heightened perception — the crack through which cosmic [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|truth]] becomes visible. Charles Lamb argued that "the Lear of Shakespeare cannot be acted" because the play's scope exceeds what any stage can contain. For Lamb, the character existed on a plane beyond theatrical representation, accessible only through private reading.

**Bradley and the Redemptive Reading**: A.C. Bradley's *Shakespearean Tragedy* (1904) read Lear as a play about the "greatness of the human soul" revealed through suffering. Bradley emphasized the redemptive dimension of Lear's moral education: the king who begins demanding love as performance ends by receiving love as grace. For Bradley, Lear's death was not meaningless but transcendent — his final vision of [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s lips was a beatific recognition of love's [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|reality]] even in death.

**Marxist and Materialist Readings**: Arnold Kettle and other Marxist critics see Lear's tragedy as the crisis of feudal authority giving way to early modern individualism. In this reading, Lear's demand for absolute love is the reflex of a feudal lord whose world is already dissolving. His confrontation with [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|Poor Tom]] — "vnaccommodated man" — is a confrontation with the material basis of human existence, stripped of ideology and mystification. "Robes and Furr'd gownes hide all" is not madness but materialist critique.

**Feminist Criticism**: Coppelia Kahn reads Lear's tragedy as rooted in his inability to accept dependence on the feminine. His demand for mothering from his daughters coexists with his horror at [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|being]] subject to female authority. The [[division-of-the-kingdom|love test]] is an attempt to extract a declaration of total love from daughters while maintaining patriarchal control — an impossible contradiction that collapses into Lear's curse on female fertility. Kathleen McLuskie argues that the play's treatment of [[goneril|Goneril]] and [[regan|Regan]] as "monstrous" women who refuse their "natural" [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|role]] makes the play a site of patriarchal anxiety rather than universal wisdom.

**Absurdist and Existential Readings**: Jan Kott's *Shakespeare Our Contemporary* (1964) read the play through the lens of the Theater of the Absurd, comparing Lear's heath to Beckett's barren landscapes and the final catastrophe to the universe of *Endgame*. In this reading, Lear is not a Christian tragedy of redemptive suffering but an absurdist drama about the meaninglessness of pain. The question "Why should a Dog, a Horse, a Rat haue life?" receives no answer because, Kott argues, the play knows there is none.

## Themes and Significance

Lear's journey engages several of the play's central thematic concerns.

**[[authority-and-power-in-king-lear|Authority]] and [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|identity]]**: Lear's tragedy begins with his attempt to separate the name of king from the substance of power. The play demonstrates, with relentless logic, that this separation is impossible — to be king is to exercise authority, and to give away authority is to cease to be king. Lear's question "Who is it that can tell me who I am?" (to which the [[the-fool|Fool]] answers "Lear's shadow") is the play's central existential crisis. When social [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|identity]] is stripped away, what remains?

**[[justice-and-injustice-in-king-lear|Justice]] and mercy**: Lear's developing understanding of justice moves from retributive to distributive to something approaching universal mercy. His early curses demand divine vengeance; his later speeches recognize that all human [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|being]]s are equally frail. "None do's offend" is both an acknowledgment of shared human weakness and a radical critique of the very concept of [[justice-and-injustice-in-king-lear|punishment]].

**[[love-in-king-lear|Love]] and obligation**: The [[division-of-the-kingdom|love test]] that opens the play sets up a central question: can love be measured, quantified, demanded? Lear demands absolute love and receives flattery; he rejects bounded love and loses [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|truth]]. By the play's end, he has learned that love cannot be compelled — it can only be given and received as grace.

**[[nature-in-king-lear|Nature]] and civilization**: The play stages a collision between multiple concepts of [[nature-in-king-lear|Nature]] — the hierarchical, ordered [[nature-in-king-lear|Nature]] of Lear and [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]; the amoral, competitive [[nature-in-king-lear|Nature]] of [[edmund|Edmund]]; and the indifferent, destructive [[nature-in-king-lear|Nature]] of [[the-heath|the storm]]. Lear's exposure on the heath forces him to confront the question of what is natural in human [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|being]]s: is it the animal, the social, the familial? The play offers no settled answer.
