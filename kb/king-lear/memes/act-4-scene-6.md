# Act 4 Scene 6: Lear and Gloucester at Dover

The Dover scene: [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s imagined suicide, [[king-lear|Lear]]'s mad entrance "crown'd with ranke Fennitar," and the meeting of the two ruined old men — the most concentrated scene of suffering in the play  .

## Summary

[[edgar|Edgar]] leads his blinded father to what he convinces him are the [[dover|Dover]] cliffs  . In an extraordinary passage of dramatic illusion, [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] attempts suicide by leaping from the "cliff," but [[edgar|Edgar]] has staged the scene on flat ground. [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] survives, and [[edgar|Edgar]], changing his voice, convinces him the gods have miraculously preserved his life. [[king-lear|Lear]] then enters "mad, and crown'd with ranke Fennitar and furrow Weedes," delivering his devastating speeches on justice, hypocrisy, and female sexuality (004-366). When [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] recognizes Lear's voice, the two ruined patriarchs meet — Lear raving about the nature of authority, [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] longing only for death.
