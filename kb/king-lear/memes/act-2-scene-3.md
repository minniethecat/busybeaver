# Act 2 Scene 3: Edgar Becomes Poor Tom

In one of the play's shortest and most pivotal scenes, Edgar strips himself of name, status, and clothing — and becomes Poor Tom, the "unaccommodated man" who will haunt the heath and speak truth through madness. The scene lasts barely twenty lines but creates the figure through whom the play explores its deepest questions about identity, suffering, and what remains when everything is taken away.

## Summary

Alone in the countryside, [[edgar|Edgar]] reflects that he has escaped the hue and cry set after him by [[edmund|Edmund]]'s deception. He realizes he can no longer live as himself — "Edgar I nothing am" (002-320). His solution is radical: he will become a "Bedlam beggar," one of the mad, dispossessed poor who roamed the Jacobean countryside. He describes the role he will play in vivid detail — grime on his face, tangled hair, nakedness, animal sounds — and exits, already transformed.

## Structure

### Opening

[[edgar|Edgar]] enters, alone, having escaped [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s pursuit. "I heard myself proclaimed" (002-315) — he is now an outlaw, his name published as that of a would-be parricide. A hollow tree has offered temporary shelter. The scene's physical setting — the open countryside, a hollow tree — mirrors Edgar's social position: outside the walls of family, property, and identity.

### Development

[[edgar|Edgar]]'s soliloquy lays out his plan with painful clarity: he will "take the basest and most poorest shape / That ever penury in contempt of man / Brought near to beast" (002-316). He enumerates the details of his disguise — knotting his hair, smearing his face with filth, stripping himself near-naked, adopting a madman's vocal tics. The speech moves from desperate decision to almost professional attention to detail: Edgar is not merely hiding but *crafting a role*.

### Closing

The scene ends with the speech's most famous line: "Edgar I nothing am" (002-320). In a play structured around the word "nothing" — Cordelia's "nothing," Lear's "nothing will come of nothing," the Fool's riddling on the theme — Edgar's self-annihilation is both tactical (it will save his life) and philosophical (it enacts the play's central question: what is a human being when stripped of social markers?). He exits, and the next time we see him, he will be Poor Tom, crouching in a hovel while the storm rages.

## Thematic Connections

Edgar's transformation into Poor Tom is the play's most literal enactment of the theme of [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|disguise and identity]]. Unlike [[kent-earl-of-kent|Kent]], who disguises himself only in appearance (changing his clothes but retaining his name, "Caius"), Edgar abandons his entire social identity. He becomes not a different nobleman but a figure from the very bottom of the social order — a mad beggar. In doing so, he becomes the play's most radical truth-teller. As Poor Tom, Edgar will speak to [[king-lear|Lear]] on the heath in a language of fragments, demons, and suffering — a language that Lear, in his own descent into madness, understands.

The scene also establishes the play's concern with what Lear will later call "unaccommodated man" (003-340). Edgar, by choice, enters the condition that Lear will be forced into — homelessness, nakedness, madness — and he enters it early enough to witness Lear's fall from the perspective of one who has already fallen. Their meeting on the heath (Act 3, Scene 4) is the play's central philosophical encounter: the king who has lost everything and the nobleman who has chosen to lose everything, together in the storm.

## Dramatic Significance

Act 2, Scene 3 functions as a hinge between the play's political plot and its philosophical dimension. Edgar's flight from his father's justice is the direct consequence of Edmund's scheming in the previous scene; but Edgar's decision to become Poor Tom opens the door to the heath scenes, the mad trial of Act 3, Scene 6, and the Dover cliff scene — all of which depend on Edgar's disguise. In a play where so much suffering is inflicted, Edgar's transformation is unique: it is suffering *chosen*, and for that reason it becomes the play's instrument of survival, witness, and eventually, partial redemption.

## Textual Notes

This scene does not appear in the Quarto (1608); it is Folio-only. Its brevity and Edgar's soliloquy form have led some scholars to suggest it may have been added for the Folio revision to strengthen Edgar's character arc. In performance, the scene can be cut, but to do so loses the moment when Edgar consciously *chooses* his disguise — a choice that makes his character trajectory through the rest of the play legible as deliberate witness rather than passive suffering.
