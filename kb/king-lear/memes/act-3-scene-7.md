**Act 3 Scene 7** is the climactic scene of violence in *[[king-lear|King Lear]]*. [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]], having aided [[king-lear|Lear]]'s escape to Dover, is brought before [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]] and [[regan|Regan]]. Their interrogation escalates into the play's most notorious act of physical cruelty: [[gloucesters-blinding|the blinding of Gloucester]] on stage.

## Summary

[[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]] sends [[edmund|Edmund]] away to accompany [[goneril|Goneril]] to [[duke-of-albany|Albany]], sparing him the [[blindness-and-insight-in-king-lear|sight]] of his father's [[justice-and-injustice-in-king-lear|punishment]]. [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] is bound and accused of treason. [[regan|Regan]] taunts him, plucking his beard. When [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] defiantly declares he will "see the wings of vengeance" overtake his tormentors, [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]] puts out one eye. [[regan|Regan]] cries "the other too," but a servant intervenes, fatally wounding [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]] before [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|being]] killed by [[regan|Regan]]. [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] is blinded in the second eye but learns from [[regan|Regan]] that [[edmund|Edmund]] betrayed him — the revelation that his legitimate-[[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|seeming]] son was the villain, and that [[edgar|Edgar]], whom he condemned, was innocent.

## Key Moments

- **[[goneril|Goneril]]'s verdict**: "Plucke out his eyes" (003-370) — the elder daughter's cruelty exceeds even [[regan|Regan]]'s
- **The servant's intervention**: A nameless servant, unable to witness the atrocity, attacks [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]] and is killed — one of the play's few moments of moral courage from an unexpected source
- **[[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s revelation**: [[king-lear|Lear]]ning of [[edmund|Edmund]]'s [[loyalty-and-betrayal-in-king-lear|treachery]], [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] cries "O my Folly! Then 'twas my infirmity" — his physical blindness now matched by moral clarity

## Significance

This scene is the structural pivot of the play. [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s blinding mirrors [[king-lear|Lear]]'s madness — both are reductions to a state of helplessness that paradoxically produces [[blindness-and-insight-in-king-lear|insight]]. The scene's unflinching depiction of violence tests the limits of dramatic representation and has been the subject of centuries of critical debate.
