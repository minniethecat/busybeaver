# "As flies to wanton boys"

Blinded and cast out, Gloucester reaches the darkest conclusion in all of Shakespeare: that human suffering has no moral meaning and the gods are merely cruel.

## Full Text

> As flies to wanton Boyes, are we to th'Gods; They kill vs for their sport. (PN=004-046)

## Context

Gloucester has just been horrifically blinded by Cornwall and Regan and expelled from his own castle. Led by an old tenant, he stumbles across the heath and encounters Edgar, his loyal son, now disguised as Poor Tom — though Gloucester does not recognize him. His thoughts turn immediately to suicide: he asks to be led to the cliffs of Dover. It is in this moment of absolute despair that he delivers these words, the most nihilistic lines in the play (PN=004-040).

The torture that precedes this line is among the most brutal in Shakespeare. Cornwall, enraged by Gloucester's loyalty to Lear, declares:

> Vpon these eyes of thine, Ile set my foote. (PN=003-399)

The blinding is enacted onstage in full view of the audience. The physical horror of what Gloucester has endured — his eyes gouged out by a man he considered his guest — gives the metaphysical despair of "As flies to wanton boys" its terrible weight.

## Speaker

**[Earl of Gloucester](earl-of-gloucester)**

Gloucester, like Lear, is a father betrayed by his children — but where Lear rages, Gloucester sinks into despair. His blinding has literalized the moral blindness that led him to trust Edmund and reject Edgar. Now, sightless and hopeless, he voices a vision of the cosmos as an arena of arbitrary cruelty. The line marks the furthest point of his despair before his eventual, tragic redemption through Edgar's ministrations.

Gloucester's earlier credulity — his easy acceptance of Edmund's forged letter, his readiness to believe Edgar a traitor — reflects a man who assumed the world was legible and just. The blinding destroys that assumption utterly, and this line is the record of its destruction.

## Analysis

### Thematic Significance

This line is the play's most direct challenge to the idea of a moral universe. Unlike Lear, who earlier declared himself "more sinn'd against than sinning" (PN=003-098) — still operating within a framework of justice — Gloucester abandons the framework entirely. The gods are not just or unjust; they are indifferent, treating human lives as toys. This stark pessimism reflects the play's deep engagement with the problem of suffering and places King Lear among the most philosophically radical works of the Renaissance.

The simile itself is devastatingly precise. The verb "wanton" carries its older sense of "undisciplined, ungoverned, capricious" — the boys are not evil, merely unsupervised; the cruelty is casual, thoughtless, without malice. The gods, in this vision, do not hate us. They simply do not care.

> I haue no way, and therefore want no eyes. / I stumbled when I saw. Full oft 'tis seene, / Our meanes secure vs, and our meere defects / Proue our Commodities. (PN=004-014)

This preceding speech reveals the paradox of Gloucester's insight: physical blindness brings moral clarity. He "stumbled when he saw" — his sighted judgment led him to trust Edmund and condemn Edgar. Now, blind, he sees the nature of the world more clearly than ever before.

### Dramatic Function

Gloucester's despair functions as a dark mirror to Lear's madness. Where Lear finds insight through madness ("unaccommodated man"), Gloucester finds only the desire for death. The juxtaposition of Gloucester's suicidal despair with the disguised Edgar's determination to save him creates one of the play's most poignant ironies: father and son are inches apart, yet Gloucester cannot see the love that is right beside him. This scene sets up the Dover cliff sequence, where Edgar will stage an elaborate miracle to restore his father's will to live (PN=004-080).

The entire Dover sequence — Edgar's description of the cliff, Gloucester's "fall," Edgar's pretended wonder at his survival — is a direct dramatic response to this line. Edgar must counterfeit a miracle because the real gods, as Gloucester has concluded, offer none.

## Allusions & Echoes

The image of flies and boys returns, transformed, in the play's final scene. Lear, holding the dead Cordelia, cries "Why should a Dog, a Horse, a Rat haue life, / And thou no breath at all?" (PN=005-407) — the same question Gloucester asks here, but rendered even more intimate. Edmund's later confession that "The Wheel is come full circle" (PN=005-301) suggests a universe of consequences, offering a partial counterpoint to Gloucester's nihilism — though Edmund's wheel turns only at the moment of his death.

Gloucester's vision also echoes the Fool's earlier, more oblique skepticism. The Fool's riddles and songs persistently undercut Lear's assumptions about order and justice, but they do so through indirection and wit. Gloucester, stripped of all indirection, speaks the thing plainly.

## Performance Notes

This line is often delivered as a whispered confession rather than a declamation — the words of a broken man, not a philosopher. In the 2016 RSC production, David Troughton's Gloucester spoke it almost inaudibly, to himself, as if discovering the thought in the moment. The understated delivery can be more devastating than rhetorical emphasis: the horror lies in the calm certainty of the despair.
