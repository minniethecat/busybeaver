## Overview

Cordelia is the moral center of [[king-lear|King Lear]] — the daughter who refuses to participate in her father's love-test, choosing honesty over flattery, and who ultimately returns from exile to rescue the father who wronged her. Her virtue and [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|truth]]fulness form the ethical standard against which the play measures all other characters, yet Shakespeare complicates any simple reading of Cordelia as pure symbol by giving her a quiet dignity, a stubborn integrity, and, in the end, a death of shocking [[justice-and-injustice-in-king-lear|injustice]].

Cordelia appears in only four scenes of the play: the [[division-of-the-kingdom|love test]] in Act 1, her return leading the French army in Act 4, the reconciliation with [[king-lear|Lear]] in Act 4, and her capture and death in Act 5. Yet her presence — first as rejected [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|truth]]-teller, then as absent hope, then as returning healer, finally as devastating loss — defines the play's emotional and philosophical arc. She is the character who tells the [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|truth]] and suffers for it, who returns with mercy and is killed for it, and whose death leaves the question of cosmic justice permanently unresolved.

The name Cordelia derives from the Latin "cor" (heart), and she is indeed the heart of the play: the principle of love that [[king-lear|Lear]] fails to recognize, the absence that haunts his madness, the presence that briefly redeems his suffering, and the loss that finally destroys him.

## Role in the Play

Cordelia's story begins and ends the tragedy. In the opening scene, when [[king-lear|Lear]] demands his three daughters compete in expressing their love, Cordelia's aside — "What shall Cordelia speake? Loue, and be silent" (001-049) — establishes her predicament with remarkable economy. She knows what the [[division-of-the-kingdom|love test]] demands, she knows her sisters are performing, and she chooses silence (or minimal speech) as the only honest response. Her second aside deepens the contrast: "Then poore Cordelia, / And yet not so, since I am sure my loue's / More ponderous then my tongue" (001-064). The word "ponderous" suggests weight, substance, gravity — the very qualities that rhetorical performance lacks.

When called upon to speak, Cordelia's answer — "[[nothing-and-nihilism-in-king-lear|Nothing]] my Lord" — becomes the play's philosophical keyword. The word echoes through the entire tragedy, from [[king-lear|Lear]]'s [[nothing-will-come-of-nothing|"Nothing will come of nothing"]] (001-078) to the [[the-fool|Fool]]'s jokes about empty eggshells to the final image of Cordelia's lifeless body. Cordelia's "[[nothing-and-nihilism-in-king-lear|Nothing]]" is not [[nothing-and-nihilism-in-king-lear|emptiness]] but fullness — a love so complete that it cannot be reduced to words exchanged for territory.

Her explanation to [[king-lear|Lear]] is logically impeccable: "Good my Lord, / You haue begot me, bred me, lou'd me. / I returne those duties backe as are right fit, / Obey you, Loue you, and most Honour you." A daughter's love is real but bounded; a wife must divide her love between father and husband. [[king-lear|Lear]], demanding absolutes, cannot hear the [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|truth]]. His response — "So young, and so vntender?" — prompts Cordelia's quiet, devastating correction: "So young my Lord, and true."

Her [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|truth]]fulness enrages [[king-lear|Lear]], who disowns her with the terrible pronouncement "Let it be so, thy [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|truth]] then be thy dowre" (001-097). She is rescued from ruin by the [[king-of-france|King of France]], who values her honesty above a dowry, declaring "Fairest Cordelia, that art most rich [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|being]] poore" (001-257). [[king-of-france|France]]'s recognition of Cordelia's worth precisely at the moment [[king-lear|Lear]] devalues her establishes the play's moral calculus: what the world calls poverty may be the truest wealth. Her farewell to her sisters is both gracious and prophetic: "I know you what you are" (001-277) — a warning they ignore at their peril.

Cordelia disappears from the play until Act 4, when she returns at the head of a French army to rescue her father from the cruelty of [[goneril|Goneril]] and [[regan|Regan]] (004-177). Her return transforms her from abstract principle to active agent of mercy. She arrives not to reclaim her inheritance but to heal her father: "O my deere Father, restauratian hang / Thy medicine on my lippes, and let this kisse / Repaire those violent harmes, that my two Sisters / Haue in thy Reuerence made" (004-538). The image of the kiss as medicine — the word "restauratian" (restoration) — defines Cordelia's redemptive function in the play's symbolic economy.

Her lament over the sleeping [[king-lear|Lear]] reveals the righteous anger beneath her mercy: "Had you not bin their Father, these white flakes / Did challenge pitty of them. Was this a face / To be oppos'd against the iarring windes?" (004-543). The rhetorical questions express a moral outrage the play's cosmos never answers. Her capacity for anger serves only to heighten the mercy she extends: "Mine Enemies dogge, though he had bit me, / Should haue stood that night against my fire" (004-546).

The reunion with [[king-lear|Lear]] is among the most tender scenes in Shakespeare. She kneels before him, and the humbled king kneels to her in return. When [[king-lear|Lear]] says "If you haue poyson for me, I will drinke it: / I know you do not loue me," Cordelia answers with the play's simplest, most powerful line: "No cause, no cause." Forgiveness enacted as erasure — there is no debt to be reckoned, no cause for bitterness.

The play's final cruelty is Cordelia's death. Despite the defeat of [[edmund|Edmund]]'s forces, he has secretly ordered her execution: "To hang Cordelia in the prison, and / To lay the blame vpon her owne dispaire" (005-378). Her final spoken words are characteristically [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]less: "We are not the first, / Who with best meaning haue incurr'd the worst" (005-103). [[king-lear|Lear]] enters with Cordelia dead in his arms, howling with grief (005-382). Her death — the murder of the play's one truly innocent character — makes [[king-lear|King Lear]] perhaps Shakespeare's darkest tragedy. The question "Why should a Dog, a Horse, a Rat haue life, / And thou no breath at all?" (005-443) receives no answer because, the play suggests, there is none.

### Cordelia and the Fool

A persistent critical and theatrical tradition pairs Cordelia with the [[the-fool|Fool]]. The two never appear on stage together — the [[the-fool|Fool]] vanishes after Act 3, and Cordelia returns in Act 4 — and some productions double the [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|role]]s, with the same actor playing both. The logic is compelling: both Cordelia and the [[the-fool|Fool]] are [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|truth]]-tellers, both speak honestly to [[king-lear|Lear]] when others flatter, and both suffer for their honesty. Cordelia's "[[nothing-and-nihilism-in-king-lear|Nothing]]" and the [[the-fool|Fool]]'s bitter jests serve the same function: they puncture [[king-lear|Lear]]'s illusions. When Cordelia returns, the [[the-fool|Fool]] is no longer necessary — [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|truth]] has returned in its most complete form, and the jester's proxy [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|truth]]-telling is redundant.

## Character Arc

**Act 1**: Cordelia refuses the love-test, knowing that true love cannot be reduced to rhetoric. Her aside — "What shall Cordelia speake? Loue, and be silent" (001-049) — establishes her philosophy that genuine [[love-in-king-lear|affection]] does not need public performance. Her answer of "[[nothing-and-nihilism-in-king-lear|Nothing]]" becomes the play's philosophical keyword: not [[nothing-and-nihilism-in-king-lear|emptiness]] but a fullness so complete it exceeds language. Disowned and disinherited — "Let it be so, thy [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|truth]] then be thy dowre" (001-097) — she is taken as Queen of France, leaving her sisters behind with the prophetic warning "I know you what you are" (001-277). [[king-lear|Lear]]'s final words to her — "Thou hast her [[king-of-france|France]], let her be thine" (001-269) — complete the rupture.

**Act 2-3**: Cordelia is absent from [[britain|Britain]], offstage in [[king-of-france|France]]. She appears only through others' words — letters she sends to aid her father, the army she raises, the love that draws her back across the Channel. The [[gentleman-messenger|Gentleman]]'s description of her response to news of [[king-lear|Lear]]'s suffering paints her as an emblem of compassionate grief: she "gave soft Pardon to my horses bit" and wept. Her offstage presence is a beacon of hope in the increasingly dark world of the play, the promise of mercy that the audience clings to as [[king-lear|Lear]] descends into madness.

**Act 4**: Cordelia returns to [[britain|Britain]] leading the French army (004-177). She arrives not for conquest but for rescue: "O my deere Father, restauratian hang / Thy medicine on my lippes, and let this kisse / Repaire those violent harmes" (004-538). The language of medicine, repair, and restoration defines her redemptive function — she is the physician of the soul. Her lament over the sleeping [[king-lear|Lear]] — "Had you not bin their Father, these white flakes / Did challenge pitty of them" (004-543) — reveals her capacity for righteous anger, but the anger serves only to heighten the mercy she extends. Her reunion with [[king-lear|Lear]] is the play's emotional peak: she kneels before him, he kneels to her, and the [[order-and-chaos-in-king-lear|hierarchy]] of the [[division-of-the-kingdom|love test]] is inverted into mutual recognition. When [[king-lear|Lear]] says "If you haue poyson for me, I will drinke it," Cordelia answers "No cause, no cause" — forgiveness as the erasure of debt. The reconciliation scene is the play's only fully realized moment of grace.

**Act 5**: Cordelia and [[king-lear|Lear]] are captured and imprisoned by [[edmund|Edmund]]'s forces. Her response to defeat is characteristically stoic and outward-looking: "We are not the first, / Who with best meaning haue incurr'd the worst" (005-103). Her concern is for [[king-lear|Lear]], not her[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]: "For thee oppressed King I am cast downe, / My [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]e could else out-frowne false [[fortune-and-fate-in-king-lear|Fortune]]s frowne." In their prison cell, [[king-lear|Lear]] imagines an idyllic life together — "We two alone will sing like Birds i'th' Cage" (005-109) — exchanging blessings and forgiveness. "Vpon such sacrifices my Cordelia, / The Gods themselues throw Incense" (005-121). This fragile vision of an eternity of mutual love is immediately destroyed. [[edmund|Edmund]]'s secret order — "To hang Cordelia in the prison, and / To lay the blame vpon her owne dispaire" (005-378) — is carried out. Cordelia is hanged offstage, and [[king-lear|Lear]] carries her body onstage in the play's most devastating image. Her death — innocent, unearned, absolute — leaves the question of cosmic justice permanently unresolved.

### Structural Position in the Play

Cordelia's structural position in the play is unique and carefully calibrated. She opens the tragedy (Act 1, Scene 1 — the [[division-of-the-kingdom|love test]]) and closes it (Act 5, Scene 3 — her death in [[king-lear|Lear]]'s arms). She is absent for the entire middle of the play (Acts 2-3), yet her absence is a felt presence throughout: [[king-lear|Lear]]'s madness circles around her memory, the [[the-fool|Fool]]'s jokes mourn her loss, and the audience's hope for her return sustains the possibility of redemption.

This structural pattern — presence, absence, return, loss — mirrors the play's emotional rhythm. The first movement (Act 1) establishes the wound. The second movement (Acts 2-3) explores the consequences of the wound in a world from which Cordelia has been expelled. The third movement (Act 4) offers the possibility of healing. The fourth movement (Act 5) demonstrates that healing, however real, is not permanent — that the world can and will destroy what love restores.

Cordelia's silence through Acts 2 and 3 is it[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]] a dramatic strategy of extraordinary power. By removing the character who embodies [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|truth]] and love, Shakespeare forces both [[king-lear|Lear]] and the audience to inhabit a world without her — and to recognize, in her absence, what her presence meant. When she returns in Act 4, the emotional release is proportionate to the duration of her absence.

## Key Speeches

Cordelia speaks fewer lines than almost any other character of comparable importance in the Shakespearean canon — fewer than 120 lines across the entire play. This economy of speech is it[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]] a dramatic choice: Cordelia's virtue is enacted through silence and presence, not through rhetoric.

- **"Loue, and be silent"** (001-049): Cordelia's aside during the love-test — a declaration of her philosophy that true love does not need public performance. The verb "speake" is active, social, public; "Loue" and "silent" are private, interior, true. The aside establishes her character with remarkable economy.

- **"Then poore Cordelia"** (001-064): Her second aside, asserting that her love is "more ponderous then my tongue." The word "ponderous" suggests weight, substance, gravity — the qualities that [[goneril|Goneril]] and [[regan|Regan]]'s rhetorical performances lack. "And yet not so" — she is not truly poor, because her love is real.

- **The Truth Speech** (001-049-064): "I cannot heaue / My heart into my mouth: I loue your Maiesty / According to my bond, no more nor lesse." Cordelia's choice of "bond" is deliberate — it means both obligation and connection. Her love is real and binding but does not consume her entire [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|being]]. Her subsequent explanation about dividing love between father and husband is simple, reasonable, and utterly unacceptable to [[king-lear|Lear]].

- **"So young my Lord, and true"** (001-093): When [[king-lear|Lear]] asks "So young, and so vntender?", Cordelia's quiet correction replaces "vntender" with "true," refusing the false opposition [[king-lear|Lear]] has constructed between youth and honesty.

- **Farewell to her sisters** (001-277): Cordelia's parting words to [[goneril|Goneril]] and [[regan|Regan]] — "I know you what you are" — warn them of their duty to [[king-lear|Lear]], a speech that proves tragically prophetic. She leaves not in bitterness but in knowledge.

- **"O my deere Father"** (004-538): The restoration speech upon Cordelia's return. "Restauratian hang / Thy medicine on my lippes, and let this kisse / Repaire those violent harmes, that my two Sisters / Haue in thy Reuerence made." The kiss as medicine, repair as mission — Cordelia as physician of the soul.

- **The Lament** (004-543, 004-546): "Had you not bin their Father, these white flakes / Did challenge pitty of them" and "Mine Enemies dogge, though he had bit me, / Should haue stood that night against my fire." Her grief over [[king-lear|Lear]]'s ravaged state fuses daughterly love with wider human compassion. The rhetorical questions express moral outrage that the play's cosmos never answers.

- **Reunion with [[king-lear|Lear]]** (004-587): The reconciliation scene where Cordelia kneels to her father and absolves him of guilt with the simplest possible words: "No cause, no cause." Forgiveness as the erasure of debt — the play's most powerful and economical line.

- **"We are not the first"** (005-103): Facing death, Cordelia offers stoic consolation rather than defiance or despair: "We are not the first, / Who with best meaning haue incurr'd the worst." Her concern is for [[king-lear|Lear]], not her[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]: "For thee oppressed King I am cast downe." Her final spoken words before [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|being]] led to execution are characteristically [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]less.

### Language and Style

**The weight of brevity**: Cordelia's speeches are characteristically short, direct, and concrete. Where [[goneril|Goneril]] and [[regan|Regan]] deploy elaborate figures and hyperbole ("Beyond all manner of so much"), Cordelia uses simple declarative sentences: "I loue your Maiesty / According to my bond, no more nor lesse." The contrast between rhetorical excess and plain speech is the play's first and most important moral distinction.

**"[[nothing-and-nihilism-in-king-lear|Nothing]]" as rhetoric**: Cordelia's most famous word is also her most misunderstood. Her "[[nothing-and-nihilism-in-king-lear|Nothing]]" is not silence or absence but a refusal to participate in a corrupt speech act. The [[division-of-the-kingdom|love test]] demands that love be performed publicly in exchange for territory; Cordelia's "[[nothing-and-nihilism-in-king-lear|Nothing]]" refuses the exchange. Her subsequent explanation — that she will divide her love between father and husband — is simple, reasonable, and inherently non-theatrical.

**The return to speech**: When Cordelia returns in Act 4, her language has expanded. She speaks in longer, more lyrical sentences, her verse enriched by compassion: "O my deere Father, restauratian hang / Thy medicine on my lippes" (004-538). The word "restauratian" — restoration, healing, renewal — is Cordelia's key term. Her language, once defined by refusal, now enacts the mercy it describes.

**The final silence**: Cordelia's final speech — "We are not the first, / Who with best meaning haue incurr'd the worst" (005-103) — is characteristically stoic and [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]less. After this, she is silent; her death happens offstage, and she returns only as a body. The character who spoke least becomes, in death, the play's most eloquent absence.

## Themes and Significance

**Truth and its cost**: Cordelia embodies the play's central ethical claim: that [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|truth]] has value even when — especially when — it costs everything. Her refusal to participate in the [[division-of-the-kingdom|love test]] is not stubbornness but integrity. The play asks whether such integrity is sustainable in a world that punishes [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|truth]]-tellers, and answers with Cordelia's death.

**[[love-in-king-lear|Love]] beyond language**: Cordelia's claim that her love is "more ponderous then my tongue" challenges the play's initial premise — that love can and should be spoken, measured, and rewarded. Her love for [[king-lear|Lear]] is demonstrated not in words but in action: in her return from [[king-of-france|France]], in her tears over his sleeping body, in her "No cause, no cause" that erases his guilt.

**Mercy and forgiveness**: Cordelia's response to [[king-lear|Lear]]'s guilt is not to demand recompense but to erase the debt entirely. "No cause, no cause" is the play's most powerful articulation of grace — a forgiveness so complete that it refuses even to acknowledge the existence of an injury. This is the love that [[king-lear|Lear]] could not recognize in Act 1, and its expression in Act 4 is the play's answer to the corruption of language that opened the tragedy.

**The problem of suffering**: Cordelia's death is the central theological and philosophical problem of the play. She is the one character who has done nothing to deserve suffering, and yet she suffers the most complete destruction. The play offers no explanation. The question that [[king-lear|Lear]] asks over her body — "Why should a Dog, a Horse, a Rat haue life, / And thou no breath at all?" (005-443) — remains permanently unanswered. Cordelia's death is the supreme instance of what A.C. Bradley called the "waste" that is the essence of tragedy.

## Critical Perspectives

Cordelia has been the subject of extensive critical debate, encompassing theological, feminist, and ethical frameworks.

Early readings cast her as a Christ figure — the innocent who dies to redeem her father, whose love is agape rather than eros, whose kiss brings "restauration" in language that echoes the sacraments. Her return from [[king-of-france|France]] with an army to rescue her suffering father echoes the Harrowing of Hell; her kiss as medicine — "restauratian hang / Thy medicine on my lippes" (004-538) — carries sacramental overtones; her "No cause, no cause" embodies the logic of grace, which erases debt rather than repaying it. However, the play resists straightforward Christian allegory. Cordelia's death is not followed by resurrection but by [[king-lear|Lear]]'s howl and death. The play's closing lines — "The oldest hath borne most, we that are yong, / Shall neuer see so much, nor liue so long" — do not promise redemption or afterlife, only the bitter wisdom of survival. If Cordelia is a Christ figure, she is a Christ in a universe where the resurrection never comes.

A.C. Bradley influentially saw Cordelia as the embodiment of [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|truth]] and love, arguing that her death is the play's ultimate statement about the nature of tragedy in a morally indifferent universe. For Bradley, Cordelia's [[fortune-and-fate-in-king-lear|fate]] demonstrates that "the [[fortune-and-fate-in-king-lear|gods]] are just" only in the bitter sense that they destroy indiscriminately. The sheer [[justice-and-injustice-in-king-lear|injustice]] of Cordelia's death has troubled audiences and critics for centuries. Nahum Tate's 1681 adaptation, which kept Cordelia alive and married her to [[edgar|Edgar]], held the English stage until 1838. Samuel Johnson wrote that he was "so shocked by Cordelia's death" that he could barely endure to read [[act-5-scene-3|the final scene]], endorsing Tate's revision. The restoration of the tragic ending in the nineteenth century — driven by Romantic critics who valued authenticity over moral comfort — marked a turning point in the reception of Shakespeare's darkest vision.

Feminist criticism has complicated the saintly reading, questioning whether Cordelia's silence in Act 1 is passive virtue or something more — a principled refusal to participate in a patriarchal demand that women perform emotion for male approval. Her stubbornness, in this reading, is not theological virtue but political resistance to the commodification of daughters. Coppelia Kahn notes that Cordelia's refusal to speak her love publicly is a refusal of the terms on which the patriarchal economy operates: daughters as property whose value is measured by their declarations. Her return as [[king-of-france|France]]'s invading queen, however, re-entangles her in the very power structures she rejected.

### The Ethics of Cordelia's Silence

Critics have long debated whether Cordelia's silence in the [[division-of-the-kingdom|love test]] is virtuous integrity or dangerous stubbornness. One tradition reads her silence as absolute virtue — the refusal to participate in a corrupt speech act, the insistence that love cannot be performed. In this reading, Cordelia is right, [[king-lear|Lear]] is wrong, and the tragedy flows from [[king-lear|Lear]]'s failure of perception, not from Cordelia's failure of communication.

Another tradition, traceable to William Hazlitt, notes the strain of pride in Cordelia's refusal. She knows what the [[division-of-the-kingdom|love test]] demands; she knows her father's character; she could, perhaps, have found words that were both true and tactful. Her insistence on absolute honesty, in this reading, shares [[king-lear|Lear]]'s absolutism — both father and daughter demand that the world conform to their principles, and the world breaks them both.

The play's genius is that it sustains both readings simultaneously. Cordelia is right, and her rightness destroys her. Truth is a virtue, and [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|truth]]-telling can be lethal. The [[division-of-the-kingdom|love test]] is corrupt, and refusing to participate in it does not prevent the corruption from spreading. These paradoxes are not failures of the play but its deepest [[blindness-and-insight-in-king-lear|insight]]: that virtue and catastrophe can coinhere, that the best actions can produce the worst outcomes, and that morality offers no protection against tragedy.

The question of whether Cordelia's death has meaning — whether the play is Christian, absurdist, or something else — remains the central critical question about [[king-lear|King Lear]]. Productions that emphasize bleakness (Peter Brook's 1962 staging) make her death the final, meaningless cruelty. Productions that find traces of redemption (Trevor Nunn's 2007 RSC production) emphasize the reconciliation scene, suggesting that the love between [[king-lear|Lear]] and Cordelia — however briefly restored — is real and matters, even if the universe provides no guarantee of justice.

## Performance History

Cordelia presents a unique challenge to actors: how to make virtue dramatically compelling. The [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|role]]'s brevity and emotional restraint can, in lesser hands, make Cordelia seem passive or merely symbolic. The most successful performances have found strength in her quietness, dignity in her refusals, and steel beneath the gentleness.

Notable Cordelias have included Peggy Ashcroft (1950, with John Gielgud), whose combination of clarity and warmth set the modern standard; Judi Dench (1976, RSC), who emphasized Cordelia's stubborn integrity; and more recent performances that have foregrounded Cordelia's military leadership in Act 4, resisting the temptation to play her as purely victim. The doubling of Cordelia and the [[the-fool|Fool]] — a practice dating back at least to the early 20th century — has produced some of the most intellectually suggestive productions, making explicit the link between the play's two [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|truth]]-tellers.

### Modern Relevance

Cordelia poses difficult questions about the ethics of [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|truth]]-telling in a world that punishes honesty. Her refusal to participate in the [[division-of-the-kingdom|love test]] is principled — but it also contributes to [[act-5-scene-3|the catastrophe]]. The question of whether she could have spoken differently, could have found a way to be both [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|truth]]ful and tactful, haunts the play and speaks to contemporary dilemmas about how to navigate systems that demand performative compliance.

Her return as military leader in Act 4 complicates reductive readings of Cordelia as purely gentle or passive. She commands an army, invades a country, and does so not for conquest but for rescue — a model of ethical intervention that resonates in contemporary geopolitics. At the same time, her invasion fails, and her death asks whether even the most ethical power is ultimately powerless against the forces of cruelty and [[order-and-chaos-in-king-lear|chaos]].

Cordelia's "No cause, no cause" has become a touchstone for discussions of forgiveness and reconciliation. In an age of public call-out culture and demands for accountability, Cordelia's model of forgiveness — which refuses even to acknowledge the existence of a debt — offers a radical alternative. It is a forgiveness so complete that it erases the injury, and the play asks whether such forgiveness is possible, or sustainable, in a world that will kill the forgiver.
