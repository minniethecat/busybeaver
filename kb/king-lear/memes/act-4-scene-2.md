# Act 4 Scene 2: Goneril and Edmund

[[goneril|Goneril]] welcomes [[edmund|Edmund]] to her palace and gives him a farewell kiss, while [[duke-of-albany|Albany]]'s moral awakening produces one of the most searing marital confrontations in the play. The scene is dominated by two powerful reversals: Goneril's power over her husband evaporates as Albany finds his moral voice, and the news of Cornwall's death transforms the political landscape of the second half of the play.

## Summary

Goneril arrives at her palace with Edmund and expresses surprise that her husband Albany has not met them (004-094). [[oswald|Oswald]] reports that Albany is much changed: he smiled at news of the French landing, called the worst news pleasant, and rebuked Oswald for praising Edmund's loyal service (004-094). Goneril, disgusted by Albany's "cowish terror of his spirit," sends Edmund back to [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]] to hasten his military preparations, giving him a kiss and a favor, promising "A Mistresses command" (004-106). When Albany enters, he denounces her brutally: she is not worth the dust the wind blows in her face, a devil whose proper deformity seems not so horrid in the Fiend as in a woman (004-106). A [[messenger|Messenger]] arrives with news that Cornwall is dead — slain by a servant who opposed the blinding of [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s second eye (004-106). Goneril is torn: one way she likes the news (Regan is now a widow), but she fears that [[regan|Regan]] and Edmund together may "plucke vpon my hatefull life."

## Scene Structure

### Goneril and Edmund (lines 1–28)

The scene opens with Goneril entering her palace alongside Edmund. Her first words — "Welcome my Lord" (004-094) — are addressed not to her husband but to Edmund, establishing at once the perversion of domestic hierarchy that defines the scene. She immediately complains that Albany "neuer seem'd so low" as to neglect meeting her, and Oswald confirms that Albany is "much alternated from his wonted disposition" (004-094). The word "alternated" is significant: Albany has not simply changed mood but has been fundamentally transformed by the moral crisis engulfing his household.

### The Kiss and the Commission (lines 29–58)

Goneril dismisses Albany's moral qualms as "cowish terror of his spirit" and issues Edmund a commission to return to Cornwall and "hasten his musters" (004-106). The military business is a pretext: the real transaction is erotic. Goneril gives Edmund "this kisse" and "this fauour" — tokens of her favor that carry unmistakable sexual implication — and promises "A Mistresses command" in the future. The phrase is layered: "mistress" means both female authority figure and sexual partner, and the "command" encompasses both military orders and erotic summons.

She then makes the transition from innuendo to declaration: "Wear this; spare speech, / Decline your head. This kisse, if it durst speake / Would stretch thy Spirits vp into the ayre" (004-106). She ties the favor around Edmund's neck in a gesture that echoes the [[division-of-the-kingdom|love test]] — once again, public performance replaces private affection. But where Lear's ceremony was political, Goneril's is sexual. And where Lear was deceived by flattery, Goneril is deceived by her own desire.

### Albany's Denunciation (lines 59–90)

Albany enters in a fury. His first words to Goneril — "O Gonerill, / You are not worth the dust which the rude winde / Blowes in your face" (004-106) — are among the most devastating things one character says to another in Shakespeare. The image is precise and degrading: Goneril is worth less than the elemental stuff that the wind picks up at random. Dust, the lowest imaginable substance, has more value than she does.

He continues, building an antithesis between Goneril's outward form and inward nature: "See thy selfe diuell: / Proper deformitie seemes not in the Fiend / So horrid as in woman" (004-106). The word "proper" does double duty — it means both "appropriate" and "belonging to." A devil's deformity is proper to a devil; a woman's is an unnatural horror. Albany is working through the play's obsession with the gulf between appearance and reality, locating in Goneril the most extreme version of the theme: a human form concealing demonic substance.

### Cornwall's Death (lines 91–97)

The Messenger's entrance changes the scene's register from moral confrontation to political calculus. Cornwall is dead — and not just dead, but killed by a servant who "thrill'd" him with a sword while he was gouging out Gloucester's second eye. This is the play's justice operating through mechanisms that have nothing to do with human intention: a nameless servant, moved by the spectacle of Gloucester's suffering, acts where the gods do not.

Goneril's response is chillingly pragmatic. Her soliloquy — "One way I like this well, / But being widdow, and my Gloster with her, / May all the building on my fancie plucke / Vpon my hatefull life" (004-106) — reveals her mind at its clearest: she processes her brother-in-law's murder as a strategic opportunity, then immediately calculates the threat it poses. Regan, newly widowed, will now compete with her for Edmund. The erotic triangle that will destroy all three of them in Act 5 is set up here, in a single soliloquy that takes the audience from political relief to erotic anxiety in four lines.

## Thematic Significance

### Gender and Power

The scene is a study in how power circulates through gender. Goneril wields power through sexual manipulation — the kiss, the favor, the promise — while Albany wields it through moral authority. Both are limited: Goneril's sexual power depends on Edmund's compliance; Albany's moral authority is mocked by Goneril as weakness. The scene refuses to valorize Albany's position (he is ineffectual, arriving too late to prevent harm) or to simply condemn Goneril's (her complaints about Albany's passivity have genuine force).

### The Moral Awakening of Albany

Albany's transformation from passive husband to moral accuser is one of the play's most significant character arcs. In the Quarto text, Albany is weak and ineffectual until this scene; in the Folio, his moral strength develops even further in Act 5. His denunciation of Goneril is the play's most explicit statement that external beauty can conceal internal corruption — a theme that runs through the play but finds its most forceful articulation here.

### Justice by Servant's Hand

The Messenger's report of Cornwall's death — killed by a servant who could not bear to see Gloucester's second eye put out — is the play's sole instance of divine or poetic justice arriving through human action. The servant is unnamed, his motivation simple: he "oppos'd against the act." No god intervenes; no king dispenses justice. A servant, moved by the sight of cruelty, picks up a sword. The play's bleakest statement about the absence of divine justice is answered by its most hopeful statement about human decency.

## Dramatic Function

This scene bridges the blinding of Gloucester ([[act-3-scene-7|Act 3 Scene 7]]) and the reunion of Lear and Cordelia ([[act-4-scene-7|Act 4 Scene 7]]). It serves several crucial dramatic purposes: it advances the Goneril-Edmund-Regan love triangle that will destroy all three; it gives Albany his moral awakening; and it delivers the news of Cornwall's death, which tips the political balance. The scene also provides tonal contrast — after the horror of the blinding and the bleakness of Gloucester's despair, the confrontations here have a different energy: marital warfare, erotic scheming, political calculation. It is villainy without the cosmic dimension, and in that it offers a kind of relief.
