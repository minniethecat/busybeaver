# Edmund's Plot

[[edmund|Edmund]] launches one of Shakespeare's most efficient schemes: a forged letter, a feigned loyalty, and a single soliloquy that crystallizes his rejection of custom, legitimacy, and the social order that brands him "base" for the accident of his birth.
## Dramatic Context


The scene opens immediately after the love test and Cordelia's banishment. As Lear's world fractures, [[edmund|Edmund]] enters alone and delivers his manifesto against legitimacy. He then executes a three-step plot: (1) he shows [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] a forged letter supposedly from [[edgar|Edgar]] proposing parricide; (2) he warns [[edgar|Edgar]] to flee, pretending to protect him; and (3) he wounds himself to convince [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] that [[edgar|Edgar]] attacked him. By scene's end, [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] has disinherited [[edgar|Edgar]] and promised all his lands to [[edmund|Edmund]].

The speed of Edmund's plot is remarkable even by Shakespearean standards. In the space of a single scene, he destroys Edgar's reputation, secures Gloucester's trust, and positions himself as sole heir. No other Shakespearean villain — not Iago, not Richard III — achieves so much with so little: a piece of paper, a few words, and a self-inflicted scratch.

## The Plot

Edmund executes his scheme in three rapid steps, each building on the last:

### The Bastard's Soliloquy
Edmund's soliloquy (001-312 to 001-333) is a radical rejection of the social order that subordinates him to his legitimate brother. He invokes Nature — not the ordered, hierarchical Nature of Lear's worldview but the anarchic, competitive Nature of survival — as his goddess:

> "Thou Nature art my Goddesse, to thy Law / My seruices are bound, wherefore should I / Stand in the plague of custome, and permit / The curiosity of Nations, to depriue me?" (001-312)

His argument is chillingly logical: his "Dimensions are as well compact" as Edgar's, his "minde as generous," his "shape as true / As honest Madams issue" (001-318). The difference between them is merely a social convention, and he refuses to be bound by it. The speech concludes with a triumphant declaration that overturns the moral universe:

> "Now Gods, stand vp for Bastards." (001-333)

The invocation is deliberately blasphemous. Edmund does not pray to a benevolent divine order but demands that the gods endorse his rebellion against legitimacy itself. It is the anti-Cordelia moment — where Cordelia cannot "heave her heart into her mouth," Edmund speaks with irresistible fluency. Where Cordelia submits to truth at the cost of power, Edmund weaponizes language to seize power from lies.

### The Forged Letter
The forged letter is the practical engine of Edmund's scheme. He presents it to [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] with calculated reluctance, pretending to conceal it from his father's view while ensuring that Gloucester's curiosity is inflamed. The letter, supposedly from Edgar, proposes that the brothers "kill the old man" and divide his estate between them:

> This policy, and reverence of Age, makes the world bitter to the best of our times: keepes our Fortunes from vs, till our oldnesse cannot rellish them. I begin to finde an idle and fond bondage, in the oppression of aged tyranny [001-327]

The forged letter is a work of psychological genius. It attributes to Edgar exactly the kind of impatient ambition that Edmund himself feels — projecting his own desires onto his legitimate brother while presenting himself as the loyal son appalled by Edgar's treachery. Gloucester's readiness to believe the letter reveals more about his own anxieties as an aging patriarch than about Edgar's character.

### The Self-Wounding Scene
The third step of Edmund's scheme is the most audacious. He tells Edgar to flee, then cuts his own arm and tells Gloucester that Edgar wounded him while Edmund tried to defend their father. The self-inflicted wound is a physical lie that complements the textual lie of the forged letter:

> Some blood drawne on me would beget opinion / Of my more fierce endeauour. I haue seene drunkards / Doe more then this in sport [002-031]

The casualness of the gesture — "I have seen drunkards do more than this in sport" — underscores Edmund's chilling self-possession. His body becomes another instrument of deception, as disposable as the forged letter.
## Thematic Significance


Edmund's plot mirrors Lear's tragedy in miniature. Both involve an aging father who cannot see the truth (Lear cannot see Cordelia's honesty; Gloucester cannot see Edmund's deceit), a wronged child driven into exile (Cordelia to France; Edgar into Poor Tom), and a deceptive child who exploits paternal blindness (Goneril and Regan; Edmund). The subplot intensifies the play's central question: can love and loyalty survive in a world where legitimacy — of birth, of authority, of language — has lost all meaning?

The parallel is reinforced by verbal echoes. Lear's "nothing will come of nothing" (001-079) is answered by Edmund's "nothing" — the word he writes on the forged letter, which Gloucester reads as Edgar's nothing but which is in fact Edmund's everything, the blank space on which he will write his own destiny.

## Dramatic Function


Edmund's plot serves as the engine of the secondary plot, driving [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s blinding, [[edgar|Edgar]]'s transformation into Poor Tom, and ultimately Edmund's own rise and fall. It also provides the play's sharpest critique of the social structures Lear's tragedy challenges: if a king's authority is a custom, and a father's love can be measured, then why should a bastard be "base" merely by the "plague of custome"?

Edmund's plot also demonstrates the terrifying power of theatrical performance. Edmund is not merely a liar but an actor — he performs loyalty, shock, and concern with a virtuosity that rivals the professional players of Shakespeare's company. His manipulation of Gloucester and Edgar is a demonstration of theater's dark potential: to deceive, to destroy, to make the false indistinguishable from the true.
