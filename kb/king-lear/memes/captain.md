## Overview

The Captain appears in a single sequence of Act 5, entering with [[edmund|Edmund]]'s victorious army and exiting with a death warrant for [[cordelia|Cordelia]]. [[edmund|Edmund]] commands him to take a note and follow [[king-lear|Lear]] and [[cordelia|Cordelia]] to prison: "Take thou this note, go follow them to prison" (005-130). The note instructs the Captain to hang [[cordelia|Cordelia]] and make it appear as suicide — "To lay the blame vpon her owne dispaire, / That she for-did her [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]e" (005-374). The Captain's sole spoken line — "Ile do't my Lord" (005-130) — marks him as the play's final obedient agent before [[act-5-scene-3|the catastrophe]].

## Role & Significance

The Captain embodies the problem of obedient evil in [[king-lear|King Lear]] — the person who follows orders without question and becomes the instrument of tragedy. His one line of dialogue is an unthinking "Ile do't my Lord" to [[edmund|Edmund]]'s command (005-130). Unlike the Servant who rebels against [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]] and dies for it in Act 4 — "Oppos'd against the act" (004-139) — the Captain offers no resistance, no question, no hesitation. Shakespeare gives him just enough presence to be the hand that kills [[cordelia|Cordelia]], and just enough absence to make that hand chillingly anonymous.

## Key Scenes

### The Capture and the Warrant (005-099)

The Captain enters as part of [[edmund|Edmund]]'s victorious procession: "Enter in conquest with Drum and Colours, [[edmund|Edmund]], [[king-lear|Lear]], and [[cordelia|Cordelia]], as prisoners, Souldiers, Captaine" (005-099). [[edmund|Edmund]] orders the prisoners taken away, then calls the Captain aside for a private conference: "Come hither Captaine, hearke" (005-130). He hands over a note with instructions for [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s execution, promising advancement in exchange for obedience: "One step I haue aduanc'd thee, if thou do'st / As this instructs thee, thou dost make thy way / To Noble [[fortune-and-fate-in-king-lear|Fortune]]s" (005-130). [[edmund|Edmund]] adds an implicit threat: "know thou this, that men / Are as the time is; to be tender minded / Do's not become a Sword" (005-130). The Captain's only line — "Ile do't my Lord" — seals [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s [[fortune-and-fate-in-king-lear|fate]].

### The Sword Token Arrives Too Late (005-374)

As [[edgar|Edgar]] confronts the dying [[edmund|Edmund]], he demands a token of reprieve: "Send thy token of repreeue" (005-374). [[edmund|Edmund]] tells him to take his sword and "Giue it the Captaine" (005-374), but immediately reveals the Captain "Hath Commission from thy Wife and me, / To hang [[cordelia|Cordelia]] in the prison, and / To lay the blame vpon her owne dispaire, / That she for-did her [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]e" (005-374). The implication is devastating: the Captain has already carried out the order. The sword token arrives too late, and [[king-lear|Lear]] enters moments later carrying [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s body (005-429).

## Character Arc

The Captain has no arc — he is a function, not a character. Shakespeare's decision to make him a single-scene unnamed figure with one line of dialogue is it[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]] significant: the murder of [[cordelia|Cordelia]] should not be attributed to a developed villain but to the machinery of power, faceless and obedient. The Captain represents the endpoint of the moral trajectory that [[edmund|Edmund]]'s ambition set in motion. He is what [[edmund]] was becoming — but without [[edmund|Edmund]]'s charisma, complexity, or final flicker of conscience.

## Notable Speeches

> "Ile do't my Lord" (005-130)

The Captain's sole line of dialogue. Four words, no question, no hesitation. Contrast with the Servant who rebels against [[duke-of-cornwall]] in Act 4: "Oppos'd against the act" (004-139). The Servant dies for his conscience; the Captain lives to kill for his obedience.
