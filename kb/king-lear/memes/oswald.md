## Overview

Oswald is Goneril's steward — a figure of unctuous servility whose function in the play is to embody everything that Kent despises. Where Kent's service to [[king-lear|Lear]] is founded on love and moral commitment, Oswald's service to [[goneril|Goneril]] is founded on [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]-interest and obedience without conscience. He is the play's most vivid example of corrupted service: a man who carries out his mistress's orders without question, whether those orders involve insulting [[king-lear|Lear]] or delivering letters that arrange murder. His death at [[edgar|Edgar]]'s hands — shot while attempting to kill the blinded [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] — is the play's judgment on a life spent in service to cruelty.

Oswald's most memorable moment is not an action but a reaction: his attempt to deliver [[goneril|Goneril]]'s letter to [[edmund|Edmund]] is intercepted by [[edgar|Edgar]], and the letter reveals [[goneril|Goneril]]'s plot to murder [[duke-of-albany|Albany]]. Oswald dies without delivering his message, and the letter becomes evidence against [[goneril|Goneril]]. In this, Oswald's death serves the play's justice in a way his life never did.

## Key Relationships

- **[[goneril]]** — Oswald's mistress, whose cruelty he enables and whose letters he carries
- **[[kent-earl-of-kent]]** — Oswald's antagonist; Kent attacks him at [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s castle and delivers the play's most elaborate insult catalogue against him
- **[[edmund]]** — The intended recipient of [[goneril|Goneril]]'s letter, which Oswald fails to deliver
- **[[edgar]]** — Oswald's killer; [[edgar|Edgar]] intercepts Oswald attempting to murder [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] and kills him in [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]-defense

## Key Scenes

### The Confrontation with Kent (002-153)

Kent's attack on Oswald at [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s castle produces one of the most virtuosic comedic rants in Shakespeare: "A Knaue, a Rascall, an eater of broken meates, a base, proud, shallow, beggerly, three-suited, hundred pound, filthy woosted-stocking knaue" (002-164). The insult catalogue is both hilarious and morally serious: Oswald represents everything Kent despises about service without loyalty, obedience without conscience.

### The Letter and Death (004-483)

Oswald's attempt to murder the blinded [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] — "A proclaimed prize! Most happy!" (004-489) — and his death at [[edgar|Edgar]]'s hands is his final scene. He dies asking [[edgar|Edgar]] to deliver [[goneril|Goneril]]'s letter to [[edmund|Edmund]], but the letter instead becomes evidence of [[goneril|Goneril]]'s [[loyalty-and-betrayal-in-king-lear|treachery]]. Oswald's death, like his life, serves purposes he did not intend.

## Character Arc

Oswald does not change — he is consistent in his servility from first [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|appearance]] to death. His function is to provide a fixed point of corrupted service against which the play's other servants (Kent, the servant who kills [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]]) can be measured. His death is one of the play's small acts of justice, and the letter he carries becomes one of the instruments of the play's resolution.

## Notable Speeches

> Oswald: "Why what a monstrous Fellow art thou, / Thus to raile on one, that is neither knowne of thee, / Nor knowes thee?" (002-166)

Oswald's baffled response to Kent's assault — he genuinely does not understand why anyone would object to his behavior — reveals the moral vacuum at the center of his character. He has no conception of service as anything other than obedience, and Kent's moral outrage is literally incomprehensible to him.
