# Poor Tom

"Poor Tom" is the disguise persona adopted by Edgar, the legitimate son of the Earl of Gloucester, after he is forced to flee his home due to his half-brother Edmund's plot. As Poor Tom, Edgar feigns the identity of a "Bedlam beggar" — a mentally ill vagrant released from London's Bethlem Hospital — and in doing so becomes a conduit through which the play explores themes of madness, poverty, and the stripping away of social identity [003-193].

## Origins and Adoption

Edgar adopts the Poor Tom disguise after Edmund convinces their father Gloucester that Edgar seeks to murder him. Facing certain death if captured, Edgar determines to disguise himself as the lowest form of outcast he can imagine:

> Whom the foule fiend hath led through Fire, and through Flame, through Sword, and through the waters and the ponds to distroy him to the very conscience of his owne impietie. Who giues any thing to poore Tom? [003-202]

The persona of Poor Tom draws on contemporary Jacobean understandings of the "Tom o' Bedlam" figure — a beggar who feigned or suffered from madness, often wandering the countryside and speaking in a delirious mixture of scripture, folklore, and fragmented memory [001-367]. Edgar's choice of this identity reflects the desperate situation of a noble son reduced to the most abject social state imaginable.

Edgar's soliloquy announcing his transformation reveals the calculated nature of the disguise. He resolves to "take the basest and most poorest shape / That euer penury in contempt of man, / Brought neere to beast" — a deliberate self-reduction to the condition of an animal, grime and all [002-126]. The theatrical self-consciousness of this moment — Edgar describing the role he will play — establishes Poor Tom as a performance within a performance, an acted madness that will provide the foil for [[king-lear|Lear]]'s real descent.

## The Language of Poor Tom

Poor Tom's speech is a distinct verbal register within the play — a jagged, hallucinatory idiom that veers between the grotesque and the biblical. His characteristic exclamation invokes the "foule fiend" as the source of his torments:

> Doe poore Tom some charitie, whom the foule fiend leads through Fire, and through the sharp-Hawthornes, blowes the cold winde, through a thousand hedges, and through as many ditches [003-215]

This language is not random madness. It draws systematically on Samuel Harsnett's *Declaration of Egregious Popish Impostures* (1603), a polemical exposé of fraudulent Catholic exorcisms from which Shakespeare borrowed the names of the devils — Flibbertigibbet, Smulkin, Modo, and Mahu — that populate Poor Tom's imagined cosmology [003-220]. The borrowing gives Edgar's performance an anti-Catholic, satirical charge that would have resonated with Jacobean audiences.

The verbal texture of Poor Tom's ravings combines three registers: the language of demonology (the "foule fiend" and his "five fiends"), the language of bodily suffering (cold, hunger, nakedness), and the language of moral warning (against pride, lust, and theft). This mixture allows Edgar to speak uncomfortable truths from behind the mask of madness — a pattern that mirrors the Fool's licensed speech and Lear's increasingly truth-telling ravings.

## Poor Tom on the Heath

Poor Tom becomes the central figure in the storm scenes of Act 3, where he encounters Lear, Kent, and the Fool on the heath. In this exposed, chaotic setting, Lear — himself descending into madness — immediately identifies with the near-naked beggar and asks the question that cuts to the heart of the play's concern with essential humanity:

> Is Man no more then this? Consider him well. Thou ow'st the Worme no Silke; the Beast, no Hide; the Sheepe, no Wooll; the Cat, no Perfume. Ha? Here's three on's are sophisticated. Thou art the thing it selfe: vnaccommodated man, is no more but such a poore, bare, forked Animall as thou art [003-202]

Lear's recognition — "Thou art the thing it selfe" — transforms Poor Tom from a disguised nobleman into a universal symbol of stripped humanity. The speech is the philosophical center of the play's radical critique of civilization: remove clothing, status, property, and what remains is a "poor, bare, forked animal."

Edgar maintains the Poor Tom persona through the storm, uttering fragmented warnings about the "foule fiend" while Lear strips off his own clothes in sympathetic identification [003-220]. The mad king and the feigned madman create a mirror image of reason and unreason, with Edgar's assumed madness providing a foil for Lear's genuine breakdown. The irony is sharp: Edgar, sane and calculating, must perform the madness that Lear, once powerful and rational, cannot escape.

> Good my Lord, enter here. The Tyrant of this place, where he will keepe vs vntil he sees vs perish [003-250]

Edgar's aside during the storm — "My teares begin to take his part so much, / They marre my counterfetting" — reveals the emotional cost of maintaining the disguise [003-255]. Watching Lear's suffering, the actor behind Poor Tom nearly breaks character, and this near-collapse of the performance deepens the pathos of both figures.

## Dramatic Function

Poor Tom serves multiple dramatic functions. First, the disguise allows Edgar to remain present in the narrative while in hiding, observing events and eventually guiding his blinded father Gloucester [004-090]. Second, Poor Tom's vivid descriptions of the torments of the "foule fiend" create a language of psychological suffering that parallels Lear's mental disintegration. Third, the disguise enables the play's most radical social critique: when Lear encounters the near-naked Edgar, he confronts the reality that beneath all social trappings, humanity is fundamentally vulnerable.

A fourth function emerges in the Dover cliff scene, where Edgar-as-Poor-Tom leads the blinded [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] to a non-existent cliff edge and convinces him he has fallen and miraculously survived. In this episode, Poor Tom transforms from victim to guide, using the resources of theatrical illusion — the "cliff" is a product of Edgar's persuasive description — to save his father from despair [004-090]. The disguise that began as self-protection becomes an instrument of redemption.

> Come on Sir, heere's the place: stand still: how fearefull / And dizie 'tis, to cast ones eyes so low [004-090]

Edgar's description of the non-existent cliff — with its "shrilling shreeke" of crows and choughs, fishermen on the beach appearing "like Mouses," and the "murmuring Surge" far below — is one of the most vivid passages of sensory description in Shakespeare [004-090]. It is also, paradoxically, the moment when Poor Tom's disguise begins to dissolve. Edgar is no longer speaking in the cracked idiom of the Bedlam beggar but in the measured blank verse of a nobleman — the linguistic shift signals his psychological return from the abyss of his assumed identity.

## Social Commentary

Poor Tom also functions as a vehicle for Jacobean social commentary. The Bedlam beggar was a recognizable figure in early modern England — a victim of the inadequacy of poor relief and the brutal treatment of the mentally ill. By placing a nobleman inside this figure, Shakespeare collapses the social distance between high and low, exposing the arbitrariness of status. Edgar's list of the demons that possess Poor Tom — "Flibbertigibbet," "Smulkin," "Modo," and "Mahu" — draws on Samuel Harsnett's *Declaration of Egregious Popish Impostures* (1603), a contemporary exposé of fraudulent exorcisms, lending the persona a satirical edge aimed at religious charlatanism [003-220].

> Keepe thy pen from Lenders Bookes, and defie the foule fiend; yet fiue fiends haue beene in poore Tom at once [003-227]

The list of the five fiends — Obidicut (lust), Hobbididance (darkness), Mahu (stealing), Modo (murder), and Flibbertigibbet (lying) — constitutes a miniature morality play within the larger tragedy, a catalog of sins that Edgar-as-Tom attributes to demonic possession rather than human choice.

## Critical Interpretations

Poor Tom has attracted significant critical attention. Some scholars emphasize the psychological realism of Edgar's performance — the way the disguise allows him to process his own trauma and displacement through the safety of a fictional identity. Others focus on the political dimensions of the figure, reading Poor Tom as a critique of Jacobean policies toward poverty and mental illness. The Harsnett source material has generated a substantial secondary literature on Shakespeare's engagement with contemporary religious controversy through the Poor Tom episodes.

A further interpretive tradition — associated with Jan Kott's influential reading — sees Poor Tom through the lens of the Theater of the Absurd. In this view, Edgar's performance anticipates Beckettian endgame scenarios: a man playing a role so thoroughly that the distinction between self and mask becomes undecidable, set against a landscape of cosmic indifference.
