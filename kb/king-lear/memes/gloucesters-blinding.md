# Gloucester's Blinding

[[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]] and [[regan|Regan]] gouge out [[blindness-and-insight-in-king-lear|Gloucester's eyes]] for the crime of aiding [[king-lear|Lear]], in the most brutal onstage act of violence in Shakespeare's canon. The atrocity provokes a servant to fatally wound [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]], and shatters whatever claim to legitimate authority the villains still possessed.

## Overview

[[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s blinding (Act 3 Scene 7) is the physical realization of the play's blindness/[[blindness-and-insight-in-king-lear|insight]] motif. Having learned that [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] has sent [[king-lear|Lear]] to Dover to meet [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s French army, [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]] and [[regan|Regan]] bind the old earl to a chair and tear out his eyes. A servant, unable to stand by, wounds [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]] fatally before [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|being]] killed by [[regan|Regan]]. The blinded [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]], calling for [[edmund|Edmund]], is cruelly told that [[edmund|Edmund]] betrayed him -- and in that moment achieves the moral clarity his physical [[blindness-and-insight-in-king-lear|sight]] never gave him.

## Dramatic Context

### Preceding Events

[[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]], troubled by the cruelty of [[goneril|Goneril]] and [[regan|Regan]] toward [[king-lear|Lear]], has received news of a French invasion led by [[cordelia|Cordelia]]. Against the explicit orders of [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]] and [[regan|Regan]], [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] secretly arranges [[king-lear|Lear]]'s transport to Dover. [[edmund|Edmund]], seeking advancement, betrays his father to [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]], reporting that [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] has "gone to seeke the King" (003-309). [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]] rewards [[edmund|Edmund]], making him [[earl-of-gloucester|Earl of Gloucester]] and ordering him to "seeke out where thy Father is" (003-309).

### The Event

[[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]], [[regan|Regan]], [[goneril|Goneril]], and [[edmund|Edmund]] enter with servants (003-367). [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]] orders [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] bound: "Come hither sirrah, hold the chaire ... Thou ioynted stoole, this is thy Ladies footstoole" (003-369). [[regan|Regan]] taunts [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] by tearing his beard: "To plucke me by the Beard" (003-401). [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] protests that he is their host: "you are my Guests" (003-397).

When [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] admits he has sent [[king-lear|Lear]] to Dover, [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]] declares: "Vpon these eyes of thine, Ile set my foote" (003-440). [[regan|Regan]] cries "Plucke out his eyes" (003-370), and [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]] matches her: "Plucke out his poore old eyes" (003-429). [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]] gouges out one eye, and [[regan|Regan]] incites him to take the other. [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] calls on [[edmund|Edmund]] for aid, and [[regan|Regan]] reveals the devastating [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|truth]]: "Thou call'st on him that hates thee. It was he / That made the ouerture of thy treasons to vs" (003-462). [[regan|Regan]] then helps complete the blinding.

A servant, witnessing the atrocity, intervenes, saying "Hold your hand, my Lord: / I haue seru'd you euer since I was a Childe: / But better seruice haue I neuer done you, / Then now to bid you hold" (003-469). [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]] and the servant fight; [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]] is mortally wounded. [[regan|Regan]] stabs the servant from behind. [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]], dying, still completes [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s blinding.

### Immediate Aftermath

The blinded [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]], reeling in darkness, cries "O my Follies! then [[edgar|Edgar]] was abus'd, / Kinde Gods, forgiue me that, and prosper him" (003-468). His physical blindness has become the condition for moral vision: he now sees that [[edgar|Edgar]], not [[edmund|Edmund]], was the loyal son. [[regan|Regan]] orders the blinded [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] thrown out to "smell his way to Douer" (003-485). The remaining servants, horrified, resolve to help [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]], fetching "flaxe and whites of egges" (003-493) to tend his wounds.

## Thematic Significance

[[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s blinding is the most literal enactment of the [[blindness-and-insight-in-king-lear|sight]]-blindness paradox that structures the entire play. [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s physical eyes have never helped him see the [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|truth]]: he believed [[edmund|Edmund]]'s forged letter, condemned [[edgar|Edgar]] without investigation, and trusted those who would destroy him. Only when [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]]'s boot grinds out his eyes does [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] see -- "I stumbled when I saw" (004-024), he says later. The blinding also marks the moment when evil overreaches it[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]: [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]]'s death at the hands of his own servant demonstrates that even within the machinery of power, conscience can rebel (003-469).

## Dramatic Function

The blinding is the play's most violent turning point: it shatters the audience's tolerance for the villains, precipitates [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]]'s death, and transforms [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] from a morally confused figure into a tragic subject capable of the profoundest [[blindness-and-insight-in-king-lear|insight]]. The scene also serves as the structural counterpart to [[the-heath|the storm]]: [[king-lear|Lear]]'s suffering is internal and cosmic; [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s is physical and intimate. Both men must be broken before they can see.

## Notable Stagings

The blinding has been staged with a wide range of explicitness. Some productions use stylized lighting effects and [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s screams offstage; others show the act directly, with prosthetic eyes and stage blood. The 2016 Talawa Theatre production set the blinding in a corporate boardroom, with [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]] using a letter opener -- the bureaucratic banality of evil intensified the horror. The servant's intervention is a crucial directorial choice: is it a spontaneous moral eruption, or a premeditated act of resistance?
