# Act 4 Scene 3: Cordelia's Search

Cordelia returns to England with the French army, not for conquest but for love — determined to find and restore her broken father. This brief but luminous scene contrasts Cordelia's selfless purpose with the cold calculation of Goneril and Regan.

## Summary

[[cordelia|Cordelia]] enters with the French forces and describes [[king-lear|Lear]] as "as mad as the vext Sea, singing alowd, Crown'd with ranke Fenitar, and furrow weeds" (004-180). She sends soldiers to search every field for him and appeals to the healing powers of nature to restore his sanity: "All blest Secrets, All you vnpublish'd Vertues of the earth / Spring with my teares; be aydant, and remediate" (004-194). A messenger arrives with news that the British army marches toward them. Cordelia answers that their preparations already stand ready, and she declares her purpose: "No blowne Ambition doth our Armes incite, / But loue, deere loue, and our ag'd Fathers Rite" (004-207).

## Dramatic Significance

This scene functions as both a tonal pivot and a character revelation. After the accumulating horrors of Act 3 — the storm, the blinding, the mock trial — Cordelia's arrival brings an infusion of compassion and purpose. Her description of Lear crowned with weeds rather than gold inverts the play's opening imagery of kingship: the man who demanded flattery and divided his kingdom is now a pitiful figure adorned by nature rather than ceremony. Cordelia's language of healing herbs and tears invokes a regenerative power that stands against the play's darker currents of nihilism and cruelty.

## Textual Notes

In the First Folio (1623), this is Act 4 Scena Tertia (004-176). Modern conflated editions often number this as Act 4 Scene 4, as they insert an additional scene (the Gentleman and Kent conversation) before Cordelia's entrance. The Folio structure — going directly from the Gloucester-Blindness aftermath to Cordelia's search — creates a sharper dramatic contrast between the play's poles of cruelty and love.
