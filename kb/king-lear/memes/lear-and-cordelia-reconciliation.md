# Lear and Cordelia's Reconciliation

> "O my deere Father, restauratian hang / Thy medicine on my lippes, and let this kisse / Repaire those violent harmes" — Cordelia (004-538)

The reunion of Lear and Cordelia is the emotional climax of the play — a scene of almost unbearable tenderness that gathers all the play's themes of love, forgiveness, recognition, and restoration into a single, luminous moment before the darkness closes in.

## Dramatic Context

[[king-lear|Lear]] has been found wandering the fields, crowned with weeds, "as mad as the vext Sea" (004-489). [[cordelia|Cordelia]] has had him brought to her camp, dressed in fresh garments, and laid to sleep. When the scene opens, Lear is carried in asleep in a chair. The stage image — the mad king sleeping while his exiled daughter watches over him — is itself a theatrical reversal of the first scene, where Lear sat enthroned and Cordelia stood accused.

Cordelia's prayer over his sleeping body — her kiss as "medicine" to "Repaire those violent harmes, that my two Sisters / Haue in thy Reuerence made" (004-540) — establishes the scene's key note: this is a moment of restorative love, the antidote to everything Goneril and Regan have done. The word "medicine" is crucial: love in this scene is not sentiment but active healing, a force that undoes damage. Music plays softly throughout, creating what the Gentleman describes as "a sacred Physicke" (004-501).

The presence of [[kent-earl-of-kent|Kent]] — still disguised, watching the reunion he helped bring about — adds a layer of silent witness. The loyal servant who followed Lear into the storm now sees the beginning of the restoration he risked everything to achieve.

## The Awakening

When Lear wakes, he believes he has died and been drawn from the grave:

> "You do me wrong to take me out o'th' graue, / Thou art a Soule in blisse, but I am bound / Vpon a wheele of fire, that mine owne teares / Do scalde, like molten Lead" (004-552)

He mistakes Cordelia for a blessed spirit and himself for a soul in torment. The image of the "wheele of fire" condenses his journey through madness into a single, searing metaphor: he has been broken on the wheel of his own grief. The tears that burn like "molten Lead" are at once physical sensation and spiritual condition — Lear feels his suffering as literal heat, the body and soul indistinguishable.

This is a crucial dramatic moment. Lear's awakening is not immediate but gradual; the audience watches him climb back toward sanity step by step, each new perception a small triumph. The parallel with waking from a dream — or from death — is deliberate. Lear has been in a kind of death, and Cordelia's presence draws him back.

## The Recognition and Cordelia's Response

As Lear slowly recognizes Cordelia, his language shifts from cosmic hallucination to the most intimate register in the play:

> "Pray do not mocke me: / I am a very foolish fond old man, / Fourescore and vpward, not an houre more, nor lesse: / And to deale plainely, / I feare I am not in my perfect mind" (004-568)

This is Lear's first moment of true self-knowledge. The king who demanded flattery now admits he is "a very foolish fond old man." The man who could not recognize love when Cordelia offered it "according to my bond" now fears he is "not in my perfect mind" — and in that fear, paradoxically, his mind is more clear than it has ever been. The phrase "not an houre more, nor lesse" shows Lear's characteristic precision even in his vulnerability: he knows exactly how old he is, and that knowledge is part of his return to himself.

Lear then acknowledges his wrong openly: "I know you do not loue me, for your Sisters / Haue (as I do remember) done me wrong. / You haue some cause, they haue not" (004-571).

Cordelia's reply is the play's most devastating line:

> "And so I am: I am." (004-571) — in response to Lear's fear that she has "some cause" to hate him
>
> "No cause, no cause." (004-573)

In four words, Cordelia undoes the logic of the [[division-of-the-kingdom|love test]] that began the tragedy. Lear demanded quantifiable cause for love — "Which of you shall we say doth loue vs most" (001-041); Cordelia offers love that requires no cause. The echo of her Act 1 "[[nothing-will-come-of-nothing|Nothing]]" is unmistakable: what was once a refusal to perform is now a complete and unconditional gift. The repetition — "No cause, no cause" — has the quality of a blessing, a ritual undoing of the curse Lear's demand for "cause" set in motion.

Critics have noted that Cordelia speaks fewer lines in this scene than Lear, yet the emotional weight rests with her. Her silence in Act 1 becomes her speech in Act 4; both are forms of integrity. She does not need many words because her presence — and the kiss she offered as "medicine" — has already said everything.

## Thematic Significance and Dramatic Function

The reconciliation scene gathers the play's central threads and serves as its emotional fulcrum. Every cruelty of the first three acts is answered by this moment of grace; every horror of the final act is foreshadowed by its fragility.

**Recognition and sight**: Lear's recognition of Cordelia mirrors [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s recognition of [[edgar|Edgar]] on [[the-heath|the heath]] — both fathers, blind in different ways, finally see. The play's double plot converges on this motif: spiritual sight is purchased through physical or psychological affliction.

**Love and "cause"**: The scene is structured as a theological argument about the nature of love. Lear's worldview — love must have a measurable cause, a reason — is answered and dismantled by Cordelia's "No cause." This is the philosophical center of the play: love that requires justification is not love.

**Clothing and identity**: Lear is dressed in "fresh garments" while he sleeps, a deliberate echo of Edgar's change of clothes and Kent's disguise. Throughout the play, clothing signifies identity; Lear's new garments signify his restoration from the naked madness of the heath to human community.

**The shadow of tragedy**: The reconciliation makes Cordelia's death in Act 5 not merely tragic but devastating: the love that has been so painfully restored is destroyed in an instant. Shakespeare places the reunion before the final catastrophe so that the audience feels both the full value of what is lost and the full injustice of its loss.

Structurally, this scene is the calm before the storm — the one moment in the play where love operates without obstruction, where forgiveness is complete, where the world seems capable of healing. That Shakespeare places it in Act 4 Scene 7 rather than the final act is telling. The reconciliation is not the resolution; it is the condition of possibility for a resolution that never comes. The play's tragic structure requires that we see what could have been — Lear and Cordelia restored, a kingdom healed — before we watch it all unravel.

## Critical Perspectives

The scene has been read as a secular resurrection — Lear drawn from the "graue" by Cordelia's love — and as a pastoral interlude, with music, gentleness, and healing after the storm. Feminist critics have emphasized Cordelia's agency: she is not a passive vessel of forgiveness but an active agent of restoration, commanding armies and doctors to create the conditions for Lear's healing. The scene is also the play's most explicit engagement with the Christian paradigm of redemptive love, even as the ending refuses to confirm that paradigm.
