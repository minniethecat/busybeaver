# Act 1 Scene 2: Edmund's Bastard Soliloquy

[[edmund|Edmund]]'s soliloquy inaugurates the parallel subplot of *[[king-lear|King Lear]]*: the bastard son rejects custom, forges a plot against his brother [[edgar|Edgar]], and sets the [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] family on a path toward tragedy.

## Summary

[[edmund|Edmund]] opens with his soliloquy "Thou Nature art my Goddesse" (001-312), rejecting the stigma of bastardy and declaring his allegiance to Nature over custom. He reveals a forged letter ostensibly from [[edgar|Edgar]] proposing they murder their father [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] and divide his lands. When [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] enters troubled by recent events at court, [[edmund|Edmund]] pretends to conceal the letter, then shows it, manipulating his father into believing [[edgar|Edgar]] is treacherous (001-327). [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] exits in rage, and [[edmund|Edmund]] encounters [[edgar|Edgar]], warning him to flee armed, thereby deepening the suspicion against his brother (001-366).

## Key Lines

> "Thou Nature art my Goddesse, to thy Law / My seruices are bound, wherefore should I / Stand in the plague of custome?" (001-312)

> "Legitimate Edgar, I must haue your land" (001-327)

> "Now Gods, stand vp for Bastards." (001-333)

## Dramatic Significance

This scene establishes the Gloucester subplot as a structural mirror to the Lear tragedy. Both involve an aging father (Lear/Gloucester) who fails to see the truth about his children, a wronged child driven into exile (Cordelia/Edgar), and a deceptive child who exploits the father's blindness (Goneril-Reagan/Edmund). The parallel intensifies the play's central concern with the failure of paternal judgment and the collapse of patriarchal authority.

Edmund's invocation of Nature — "Thou Nature art my Goddesse" — is the philosophical antithesis of Lear's worldview. Lear assumes a hierarchical, ordered Nature that legitimates his authority; Edmund's Nature is competitive, amoral, and governed by appetite rather than tradition. The clash between these two conceptions of Nature will drive the tragedy across both plots.

The scene also demonstrates Shakespeare's interest in the power of written texts. Edmund's forged letter is a document that creates reality rather than recording it — a piece of writing that, like the love test itself, uses language to construct a world that fits the desires of its author. In a play profoundly concerned with the relationship between words and truth, Edmund's letter is the ultimate example of language as pure instrument.

## Textual Notes

Edmund's "Now Gods, stand vp for Bastards" (001-333) is one of Shakespeare's most audacious lines. The imperative "stand up" — a command addressed to the gods — inverts the proper relationship between mortal and divine. Edmund does not pray; he instructs. The line's rhetorical force derives from its blasphemous self-confidence: Edmund does not ask for divine aid but demands divine endorsement of his rebellion.

The word "bastard" appears twice in the soliloquy, first as a term of stigma ("base") and then as a term of pride. Edmund's linguistic reclamation of the word parallels his broader project of overturning the categories that subordinate him. He will not accept the name society gives him; he will make the name his banner.
