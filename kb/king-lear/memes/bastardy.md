# Bastardy in King Lear

**Bastardy** — the condition of being born outside lawful marriage — is a central thematic driver in [[king-lear|King Lear]], concentrated in the figure of [[edmund|Edmund]], the illegitimate son of the [[earl-of-gloucester|Earl of Gloucester]]. Through Edmund, Shakespeare explores how social stigma around illegitimacy fuels resentment, ambition, and a radical challenge to the established order.

## Edmund's Bastardy

Edmund is introduced in the play's opening scene as Gloucester's "bastard son," and Gloucester makes crude jokes about his conception before Edmund's face (001-007–020). This public humiliation establishes the social reality of bastardy: Edmund is legally excluded from inheritance, denied the title and lands that belong by right to his legitimate half-brother [[edgar|Edgar]].

Edmund's opening soliloquy in Act 1 Scene 2 is the play's most direct engagement with the theme:

> Thou, Nature, art my goddess; to thy law
> My services are bound. Wherefore should I
> Stand in the plague of custom, and permit
> The curiosity of nations to deprive me,
> For that I am some twelve or fourteen moonshines
> Lag of a brother? Why bastard? Wherefore base?
> [001-321–328]

Edmund here rejects "custom" — the man-made laws that stigmatise illegitimacy — in favour of "Nature," which makes no distinction between legitimate and illegitimate children. His rhetorical question "Why bastard? Wherefore base?" is both a personal protest and a philosophical challenge to the social order.

## Legitimacy and Inheritance

The bastardy theme is inseparable from questions of inheritance and property. As a bastard, Edmund cannot inherit Gloucester's lands or title. His plot against [[edgar|Edgar]] is driven in part by this material grievance. The forged letter he shows Gloucester falsely implicates Edgar in a conspiracy to usurp their father's estate, playing on Gloucester's fears about legitimate succession.

Edmund frames his ambition in explicitly economic terms:

> Legitimate Edgar, I must have your land.
> Our father's love is to the bastard Edmund
> As to the legitimate. Fine word, "legitimate"!
> [001-328–333]

The word "legitimate" is spat out with contempt — a "fine word" that conceals an arbitrary social convention. By the soliloquy's end, Edmund has inverted the hierarchy: "Now, gods, stand up for bastards!" (001-333).

## Natural Order Inverted

Edmund's rebellion against his bastard status mirrors the play's larger theme of natural order disrupted. [[king-lear|Lear]]'s division of the kingdom, [[goneril|Goneril]] and [[regan|Regan]]'s filial ingratitude, and Edmund's usurpation of [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s position all contribute to a world in which "the bond cracked 'twixt son and father" (001-366).

Yet Shakespeare complicates the bastardy theme. Lear himself, in his madness on the heath, echoes Edmund's challenge to legitimacy:

> Gloucester's bastard son
> Was kinder to his father, than my daughters
> Got 'tween the lawful sheets.
> [004-371]

The irony is devastating: the bastard proved more loyal (in his final moments) than Lear's legitimate daughters. The distinction between "bastard" and "legitimate" collapses under the weight of moral action.
