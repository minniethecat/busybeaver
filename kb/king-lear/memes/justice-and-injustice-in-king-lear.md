# Justice and Injustice in King Lear

*[[king-lear|King Lear]]* is, among other things, a trial of the very concept of justice. The play stages formal trials (the [[division-of-the-kingdom|love test]], the mock trial, the duel), informal judgments ([[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s blinding, [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s hanging), and cosmic arraignments ([[king-lear|Lear]]'s indictment of [[the-heath|the storm]], [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s accusation of the [[fortune-and-fate-in-king-lear|gods]]). The verdict, across all jurisdictions, is the same: the innocent suffer, the guilty prosper — until they don't — and no higher court intervenes.

## Overview

The question "Is there any justice in this world?" runs through *[[king-lear|King Lear]]* with escalating urgency. At the play's center is a sequence of scenes — the mock trial (Act 3, scene 6), [[gloucesters-blinding|the blinding of Gloucester]] (Act 3, scene 7) — that juxtapose the impotence of imagined justice against the brutality of real power. The play offers no metaphysical consolation. When [[duke-of-albany|Albany]] says "All friends shall taste / The wages of their virtue, and all foes / The cup of their deservings" (005-277), [[cordelia|Cordelia]] is already dead, and the restoration of political order cannot undo the moral catastrophe that preceded it.

## Key Manifestations

### Divine Justice on Trial

The play's characters repeatedly invoke the [[fortune-and-fate-in-king-lear|gods]] as guarantors of justice — and just as repeatedly find them absent. [[king-lear|Lear]] swears "by Apollo" (001-152) and "by Jupiter" (002-400); [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] reads the heavens for moral signs (001-298). But the [[fortune-and-fate-in-king-lear|gods]] invoked never appear. [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]]'s servant, witnessing the blinding, cries out, "I'll never care what wickedness I do, / If this man come to good" (003-447). When [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]] is killed by that same servant moments later, it can be read as providential justice — or as random violence that happens, this time, to punish the guilty. The play refuses to decide.

[[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s "As flies to wanton boys are we to the [[fortune-and-fate-in-king-lear|gods]]; / They kill us for their sport" (004-020) is the bleakest theological statement in Shakespeare. If the [[fortune-and-fate-in-king-lear|gods]] exist, they are not just — they are cruel, indifferent, or absent. [[edgar|Edgar]]'s counter-claim — "The [[fortune-and-fate-in-king-lear|gods]] are just, and of our pleasant vices / Make instruments to plague us" (005-162) — rings hollow, spoken shortly before [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s wholly undeserved death.

### The Mock Trial — Justice as Farce

In a hovel on the heath, [[king-lear|Lear]] convenes a trial of [[goneril|Goneril]] and [[regan|Regan]]. [[edgar|Edgar]] (as [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|Poor Tom]]), the [[the-fool|Fool]], and Kent serve as the court. The arraignment is deranged but morally lucid: "Arraign her first, 'tis [[goneril|Goneril]] ... here's another whose warped looks proclaim what store her heart is made of" (003-450). The mock trial is the play's most concentrated statement about justice: in a world without a functioning legal system, justice must be performed as madness, in secret, by the powerless. It accomplishes nothing — the "accused" escape — but it preserves the idea of moral judgment when all institutional forms have failed.

### The Blinding of Gloucester — Justice Perverted

[[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]] and [[regan|Regan]]'s blinding of [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] in Act 3, scene 7 is the play's most graphic act of injustice. [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]] explicitly frames it as a form of justice: "Though well we may not pass upon his life / Without the form of Justice, yet our power / Shall do a courtesy to our wrath" (003-385). The distinction between "the form of Justice" and the substance of power is the play's central political [[blindness-and-insight-in-king-lear|insight]]. When authority is violence, the forms of justice become ornaments on brutality.

The servant who intervenes — the play's most striking example of moral courage from an unexpected source — is killed by [[regan|Regan]], but not before fatally wounding [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]]. The scene enacts a microcosm of the play's logic of justice: the one character who acts on moral principle is killed; the villain is punished, but not by any court — by a random act of resistance whose consequence is death for the resistor.

### Poetic Justice Denied

The play systematically denies what eighteenth-century critics would call "poetic justice." [[cordelia|Cordelia]] — the play's most virtuous character — is hanged for no reason. [[king-lear|Lear]] dies believing she lives; the audience is denied even the consolations of his ignorance. [[edmund|Edmund]]'s deathbed attempt at a good deed — "Some good I mean to do, / Despite of mine own nature" (005-218) — comes too late to save [[cordelia|Cordelia]]. The play's final vision, Kent's "Is this the promised end?" (005-273), refuses to name whether "the end" is eschatological justice or simply, terrifyingly, the end.

## Dramatic Development

**Act 1**: [[king-lear|Lear]]'s [[division-of-the-kingdom|love test]] is a parody of justice — a judgment rendered on the basis of performance rather than [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|truth]]. [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s banishment is the play's first and most consequential miscarriage. **Act 2**: Kent in the stocks is an image of loyal service punished; [[edgar|Edgar]]'s flight demonstrates that the innocent cannot rely on institutional protection. **Act 3**: The play's justice sequence — mock trial (3.6) followed by [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s blinding (3.7) — juxtaposes justice as farce with justice as pure violence. The servant's intervention is the play's sole act of effective moral resistance. **Act 4**: [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s [[nothing-and-nihilism-in-king-lear|nihilism]] ("As flies to wanton boys") is the intellectual culmination — the play's bleakest answer to the question of cosmic justice. **Act 5**: The duel between [[edgar|Edgar]] and [[edmund|Edmund]] restores a semblance of providential justice — [[edmund|Edmund]] is punished — but [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s death makes the restoration feel hollow. [[duke-of-albany|Albany]]'s final speech attempts to close the moral arc, but the play's emotional [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|truth]] is [[king-lear|Lear]]'s "Never."

## Critical Perspectives

A.C. Bradley argued that *[[king-lear|King Lear]]* presents the most terrible vision of any Shakespearean tragedy because the suffering is so disproportionate to any fault. The play's refusal to provide poetic justice — [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s death — was so disturbing to eighteenth-century audiences that Nahum Tate's rewritten version (1681) with a happy ending held the stage for 150 years. Modern critics (Dollimore, 1984) have read the play's critique of justice as a materialist argument: justice is a function of power, not a transcendental category. The play's radicalism lies in its willingness to stage the possibility that there is no justice — only power, and the rare, costly acts of resistance that power cannot entirely extinguish.
