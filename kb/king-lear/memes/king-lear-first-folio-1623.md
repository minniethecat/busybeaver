# King Lear (First Folio 1623)

The text of *King Lear* printed in the 1623 First Folio — *Mr. William Shakespeares Comedies, Histories, & Tragedies* — the first collected edition of Shakespeare's plays and the primary source text for this wiki. The Folio text of *Lear* differs substantially from the earlier 1608 Quarto and represents a revised, theatrical version of the play.
## Overview


The First Folio was compiled by Shakespeare's fellow actors John Heminge and Henry Condell, printed by Isaac Jaggard and Edward Blount, and published in 1623, seven years after Shakespeare's death. It contains thirty-six plays, eighteen of which (including *The Tempest*, *Macbeth*, *Antony and Cleopatra*, and *Twelfth Night*) had never been printed before. *King Lear* occupies a position among the tragedies, printed between *Hamlet* and *Othello*.

Without the First Folio, roughly half of Shakespeare's plays — including *King Lear* in its most fully realised form — would have been lost. The Folio is thus not merely a text but an act of preservation: Heminge and Condell's editorial labour saved the plays that define the English dramatic tradition.

## Relevance to King Lear


### Primary Source for the Wiki

Every PN reference in this wiki points to a paragraph in the Folio text. The Folio preserves the play in its most fully realised theatrical form. Its language is generally cleaner and more metrically regular than the Quarto's, reflecting either scribal polishing or Shakespeare's own revisions. The stage directions are fuller and more theatrical, suggesting the text derives from a prompt-book rather than the author's working papers.

### Major Differences from the Quarto

The Folio omits approximately 300 lines present in the Quarto (including some of the mock-trial sequence on the heath and the Doctor's speech in the reunion scene) while adding some 100 lines not found in the Quarto. These additions include: Lear's lines in the mock trial — "Then let them Anatomize Regan: See what breeds about her heart" (003-342) — portions of the servants' dialogue after the blinding; and the crucial shift in the final speech from Albany (Quarto) to Edgar (Folio). The cumulative effect of these changes is to sharpen the play's tragic focus, emphasising Edgar's emergence as the survivor who must "speake what we feele" (005-360) over Albany's more political closing.

### The Acting Version

Most scholars now believe the Folio text represents the play as revised by Shakespeare for performance, probably around 1610. Cuts and additions reflect the practical demands of the King's Men's repertory: tightening pace, redistributing lines among the available actors, and adjusting the ending's political implications in the Jacobean context. This means the Folio is not merely a "collected" text but a distinct artistic revision — a later, more theatrical *King Lear*.

### The Title Page and Its Significance

The Folio's title page — with its famous Droeshout engraving of Shakespeare — presents the plays in three generic categories: Comedies, Histories, and Tragedies. *King Lear* appears among the Tragedies, its title shortened from the Quarto's more expansive *M. William Shak-speare: His True Chronicle Historie of the life and death of King Lear and his three Daughters*. The Folio's title — simply "The Tragedie of King Lear" — signals the play's elevation from "chronicle history" to the genre of tragedy, placing it alongside *Hamlet* and *Macbeth* in the canon of supreme tragic achievement.

The generic reclassification matters: the Quarto frames Lear's story as a historical narrative with a moral (the chronicle form implies "this happened, and here is what it means"). The Folio frames it as a tragedy (the tragic form implies "this happened, and its meaning exceeds any moral we might extract"). The shift from history to tragedy is the shift from explanation to confrontation.

### The Heminge-Condell Connection and the Folio's Authority

The two compilers, John Heminge and Henry Condell, were not merely publishers but Shakespeare's longtime collaborators. Heminge (c. 1556–1630) was a fellow actor and shareholder in the King's Men; he appears in the cast lists of several Shakespeare plays and served as the company's business manager. Condell (c. 1576–1627) was also a King's Men actor and shareholder. Both men had performed in *King Lear* — they knew the play from the inside, as actors who had spoken its lines on stage.

Their preface "To the great Variety of Readers" is a direct appeal: they ask readers to "buy" the plays, arguing that Shakespeare's works — which had been "maimed and deformed" in unauthorized quartos — are now "cured and perfect of their limbes" in the Folio. For *Lear* specifically, this claim carries weight: the Folio's text, whatever its divergences from Q1, was assembled by men who had performed the play and had access to the company's prompt-books. Their editorial authority is not merely antiquarian — it is theatrical.

## Key Passages / Ideas


The Folio's added material often deepens the thematic architecture. For instance, Lear's command on the heath — "Then let them Anatomize Regan: See what breeds about her heart" (003-342) — is unique to F1. It echoes the earlier "Is there any cause in Nature that make these hard-hearts" (003-343), linking anatomical curiosity with moral outrage. These Folio-only lines intensify the play's investigation of nature — what is natural, what is unnatural — that runs from Lear's curse on Gonerill to Edmund's soliloquy on Nature as his "Goddesse" (001-312).

The Folio's closing speech, assigned to Edgar rather than Albany, is perhaps the single most consequential textual variant. Albany is a political figure; Edgar is a survivor who has passed through madness, disguise, and the death of his father. When Edgar speaks — "The oldest hath borne most, we that are yong, / Shall neuer see so much, nor liue so long" (005-360) — the weight is experiential rather than political. The Folio's choice of speaker transforms the play's final gesture from governance to witness.

## Editions & Variants


The First Folio is the basis for most modern conflated editions, which typically use F1 as their control text while importing Q1-only passages (set off typographically or placed in appendices). Early conflated editions include Alexander Pope (1725), Samuel Johnson (1765), and Edmond Malone (1790). The New Variorum edition (1880, revised 2020) meticulously documents all Q1/F1 variants.

Notable modern editions that privilege the Folio include the Arden Third Series (R.A. Foakes, 1997), which treats the two texts as distinct versions and prints both, and the Norton Shakespeare (3rd edition, 2015), which prints Q1 and F1 as separate texts. Stanley Wells and Gary Taylor's *William Shakespeare: The Complete Works* (Oxford, 1986; 2nd edition 2005) broke new ground by printing Q1 and F1 *Lear* as independent texts, arguing that Shakespeare revised the play and that conflating the two versions obscures his artistic development.


The First Folio is also a material artifact of extraordinary cultural significance. Its dimensions (roughly 13 × 8.5 inches, "folio" format meaning each sheet folded once), its typography (set by compositors working from a variety of copy-texts of varying reliability), and its publication context (a speculative venture by a syndicate of stationers) all shape the text it preserves. The Folio's *Lear* is not a clean "author's text" but a collaborative product of Shakespeare, his fellow actors, scribes, compositors, and publishers — a reminder that every text we read is mediated by the material conditions of its production.

The Folio also testifies to the economic logic of early modern publishing. Plays were not considered literature; they were ephemeral entertainment, and their survival into print was the exception rather than the rule. The Folio's publication — an expensive, high-risk venture — represents an early claim for the literary value of drama, a claim that has been retrospectively vindicated by the four centuries of performance, criticism, and adaptation that the Folio enabled.

### The Compositors and Their Habits

Bibliographical scholarship, particularly the work of Charlton Hinman (*The Printing and Proof-Reading of the First Folio of Shakespeare*, 1963), has identified five compositors who set the Folio's pages, designated Compositors A through E. Each had distinctive spelling preferences, typographical habits, and error patterns. The *King Lear* section was set primarily by Compositor B, whose work is characterised by a preference for "doe"/"goe" spellings and a tendency to justify lines by altering punctuation. Compositor E assisted with several pages of the final act. Knowing which compositor set which page matters: some variants between Q1 and F1 may be compositorial rather than authorial, and some Folio-only readings long attributed to Shakespeare may be Compositor B's improvisations.
