# "Unaccommodated man"

Confronted by the sight of Edgar disguised as Poor Tom, Lear delivers one of the play's most radical philosophical moments: a stripping away of all social pretense to reveal what a human being truly is.

## Full Text

> Thou art the thing it selfe; vnaccommodated man, is no more but such a poore, bare, forked Animall as thou art. Off, off you Lendings: Come, vnbutton heere. (PN=003-223)

## Context

Lear has fled into the storm and taken shelter in a hovel, where he encounters Edgar, disguised as the mad beggar Poor Tom. Gloucester has just left to fetch provisions. Lear, already on the edge of madness, sees in Poor Tom's near-nakedness a revelation: that all the trappings of civilization — clothes, status, wealth — are borrowed things that conceal our essential poverty as creatures (PN=003-210).

Lear's own journey to this insight has been prepared by his earlier stripping: first of knights (a hundred reduced to fifty, then to none), then of shelter (thrust into the storm), and finally of sanity. In Poor Tom, he sees the endpoint of this process and recognizes it not as degradation but as truth.

## Speaker

**[King Lear](king-lear)**

By this point, Lear has been stripped of his knights, his authority, and his shelter. He is teetering between sanity and madness. His encounter with Poor Tom triggers a leap of empathy and philosophical insight: he sees himself in the beggar and begins to undress, physically enacting his recognition that "unaccommodated man" — the human being without the protections of society — is the fundamental reality beneath all rank and privilege.

Lear's question to Poor Tom moments earlier reveals the direction of his thinking:

> Did'st thou giue all to thy Daughters? And art thou come to this? (PN=003-216)

He assumes Tom's misery, like his own, originates in filial betrayal. The pathos lies in the partial truth of the assumption: Tom's suffering does originate in Edmund's betrayal, though Lear does not yet know it.

## Analysis

### Thematic Significance

The passage is the play's most concentrated meditation on the theme of appearance versus reality. Lear's insight reverses his earlier errors: where he once mistook flattery for love and ceremony for authority, he now sees that the social world is a garment that can be taken off. The phrase "poor, bare, forked animal" reduces humanity to its biological essentials — a startlingly materialist vision in a play otherwise concerned with kingship and cosmic order. This moment also connects to the [[nature-in-king-lear|nature]] theme: Lear discovers that human nature, stripped of custom, is radically vulnerable (PN=003-223).

The line echoes across the play. Lear's earlier command — "Nothing will come of nothing" (PN=001-092) — was a threat to Cordelia grounded in the logic of property and inheritance. Now, confronting real nothingness in Poor Tom, Lear discovers it is not a threat but the fundamental human condition.

> Is man no more then this? Consider him well. Thou ow'st the Worme no Silke; the Beast, no Hide; the Sheepe, no Wooll; the Cat, no perfume. Ha? Here's three on's are sophisticated. Thou, art the thing it selfe. (PN=003-222)

This extraordinary catalogue of borrowings — silk from the worm, hide from the beast, wool from the sheep — frames all human possessions as theft from nature. The word "sophisticated" here carries its older sense: adulterated, corrupted, removed from the natural state.

### Dramatic Function

Lear's gesture of undressing — "Off, off you Lendings: Come, vnbutton heere" — is one of the play's most powerful physical moments. It literalizes his spiritual and social stripping, and it creates a visual tableau of the king and the beggar as equals. This scene also initiates Lear's turn toward empathy: having spent most of the play focused on his own grievances, he now recognizes another's suffering. The compassion he shows Poor Tom will later flower in his reconciliation with Cordelia (PN=004-323).

The scene's structure reinforces the theme of inversion. The King kneels to the beggar; the sane man learns from the mad; the clothed man strips to match the naked. Each reversal undoes one of the hierarchies the play's opening scenes established, until the entire social order stands exposed as a set of "lendings."

## Allusions & Echoes

The stripping motif recurs throughout the play. Edgar himself, forced into disguise, has already shed his noble identity to become Poor Tom (PN=002-181). Lear's "Off, off you Lendings" echoes his earlier divestiture of kingship in the love test (PN=001-054). The image of man as animal resonates with Edmund's Hobbesian vision of nature as competitive and ruthless (PN=001-305), though Lear reaches a very different conclusion: one of compassion rather than predation.

The word "unaccommodated" itself is significant. It appears nowhere else in Shakespeare and may be his coinage. The root "accommodate" — to fit, to furnish, to supply — makes the negation powerful: to be unaccommodated is to be unfitted for the world, stripped of all that makes life bearable. Lear's discovery is that this unaccommodated state is not a privation but a revelation of the truth.

## Performance Notes

The stripping scene is notoriously demanding. In Peter Brook's landmark 1962 production, Paul Scofield's Lear tore at his clothes with desperate conviction; the moment was shocking rather than symbolic. More recent productions, such as the 2014 National Theatre staging with Simon Russell Beale, have emphasized the tenderness of Lear's gesture toward Poor Tom — the undressing as an act of care rather than madness. The line "Come, vnbutton heere" can be played as a plea for help, a command, or an intimate request; the choice fundamentally shapes the audience's understanding of Lear's mental state.
