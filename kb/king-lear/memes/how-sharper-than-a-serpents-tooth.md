> How sharper then a Serpents tooth it is,
> To haue a thanklesse Childe. Away, away.
> — King Lear, Act 1 Scene 4 (001-606)

## Context

[[king-lear|Lear]] speaks these lines after [[goneril|Goneril]]'s first open act of rebellion: demanding that he reduce his train of knights from one hundred to fifty. The speech that culminates in this image is [[king-lear|Lear]]'s curse upon [[goneril|Goneril]]'s fertility — a prayer to [[nature-in-king-lear|Nature]] to make her barren or, if she must bear children, to make that child a torment to her as she has been to him.

The curse begins with [[king-lear|Lear]] invoking "[[nature-in-king-lear|Nature]]" as a "deere Goddesse" and proceeds through increasingly violent images of sterility and [[justice-and-injustice-in-king-lear|punishment]] before arriving at this single, devastating simile. The serpent's tooth — venomous, hidden, striking from within — captures the essence of ingratitude as [[king-lear|Lear]] experiences it: a poison delivered by one's own flesh.

## Significance

The line distills one of the play's central preoccupations: the asymmetry between parental love and filial obligation. A serpent's tooth injects venom; a thankless child injects a poison that is both psychological ([[king-lear|Lear]]'s madness follows directly) and cosmic (his curse on [[goneril|Goneril]]'s fertility invokes [[nature-in-king-lear|Nature]] it[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]] as instrument of justice).

The word "thanklesse" is deliberate. [[goneril|Goneril]] is not merely cruel — she is ungrateful. [[king-lear|Lear]] has given her "all" (half his [[britain|kingdom]]), and her response is to strip him of dignity, retinue, and finally shelter. The arithmetic of the exchange — [[king-lear|Lear]]'s gift of a [[britain|kingdom]] against [[goneril|Goneril]]'s demand that he reduce his knights — makes ingratitude, not greed, the primary sin.

## Performance and Reception

This line, with its memorable juxtaposition of the domestic ("thanklesse Childe") and the monstrous ("Serpents tooth"), has become one of Shakespeare's most quoted images of parental suffering. The metaphor's power lies in its compression: a serpent's bite is intimate and invisible, delivered in proximity, just as a child's ingratitude wounds from within the family.
