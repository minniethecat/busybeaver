# Kent's Banishment

Kent's banishment occurs in Act 1, Scene 1 of King Lear, immediately following Lear's disinheritance of Cordelia. When the Earl of Kent attempts to intercede on Cordelia's behalf and warn Lear of his folly, Lear — enraged and unwilling to be contradicted — banishes Kent from the kingdom on pain of death. This event establishes Lear's tyrannical temper, demonstrates the peril of honest counsel in his court, and sets in motion Kent's disguised return as the servant Caius, a disguise that persists through most of the play [001-138].

## Dramatic Context

The banishment follows directly from the love test and Lear's division of the kingdom. After [[cordelia|Cordelia]] refuses to participate in the rhetorical competition and is disinherited, Kent — alone among the court — dares to challenge Lear's judgment. His intervention unfolds in a series of escalating challenges, each more direct than the last:

> What would'st thou do old man? Think'st thou that dutie shall haue dread to speake, When power to flatterie bowes? To plainnesse honour's bound, When Maiesty falls to folly [001-138]

Kent's address — "old man" — strips Lear of his royal title in the very act of claiming to serve him. It is an extraordinary breach of decorum, and it signals Kent's understanding that true service requires speaking the truth that power cannot hear. The phrase "when majesty falls to folly" is both diagnosis and prophecy: Lear's majesty is in the act of falling, and Kent is the only one willing to name it.

## Lear's Decree

Lear responds to Kent's plea with escalating fury. He invokes the classical oath "by Apollo" — aligning himself with pagan divinity even as he commits an act of injustice — and draws his sword:

> Come not betweene the Dragon and his wrath. I lou'd her most, and thought to set my rest On her kind nursery. Hence, and auoid my sight: So be my graue my peace, as here I giue Her Fathers heart from her [001-138]

Lear's sentence — banishment within five days, death upon return within ten — mirrors and intensifies his treatment of Cordelia. The symmetry is precise: Lear disinherits Cordelia for refusing to speak flattery, then banishes Kent for speaking truth. Both Kent and Cordelia are punished for honesty, and both will return to Lear in forms that he does not initially recognize: Cordelia as the leader of a French invasion, Kent as the disguised servant Caius [001-177].

Kent's final words before departing are a blessing on Cordelia and the other daughters — "The Gods to their deere shelter take you" — followed by a declaration that he will carry his "old bones" into another land [001-178]. The reference to his age ties Kent's fate to the play's broader meditation on the vulnerability of the elderly, a theme that will expand to encompass [[king-lear|Lear]] and [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]].

## Consequences

Kent's banishment has far-reaching consequences. Rather than accepting exile, Kent immediately resolves to return to Lear's service in disguise, adopting the identity of "Caius." He reflects on his altered state:

> Now banisht Kent, If thou canst serue where thou dost stand condemn'd, So may it come thy Master whom thou lou'st, Shall find thee full of labours [001-420]

This soliloquy reveals the central paradox of Kent's character: banishment frees him to serve more purely than office ever allowed. As Caius, Kent becomes the play's moral witness — present at every crucial moment, protecting Lear, defying Cornwall, following his king onto the heath. His loyalty, tested through banishment and sustained through disguise, stands as one of the play's most powerful affirmations of faithful service [001-364].

The disguise also generates dramatic irony that runs throughout the play. Lear, who banished Kent for speaking truth, now unknowingly depends on the same man for survival. When Kent is placed in the stocks by Cornwall — an insult to Lear's servant and thus to Lear himself — the king's outrage is sharpened by the audience's knowledge that the degraded servant is the earl he wronged [002-132].

## Thematic Significance

Kent's banishment crystallizes several of the play's central concerns. First, it demonstrates the relationship between power and truth: Lear's inability to tolerate honest counsel — his demand that speech serve flattery rather than truth — is the fundamental error from which the tragedy unfolds. Second, it establishes the motif of disguise and unrecognized identity that structures much of the play's action, paralleling Edgar's transformation into [[poor-tom|Poor Tom]] and Cordelia's return at the head of a foreign army.

Third, the banishment introduces the play's preoccupation with the cost of loyalty. Kent pays for his devotion with exile and, later, with physical suffering in the stocks. Yet he never wavers. His willingness to serve where he is condemned defines a model of duty that the play holds up against the self-interest of [[edmund|Edmund]], [[goneril|Goneril]], and [[regan|Regan]] — a loyalty that expects no reward and receives none, except the knowledge of having done right [001-302].
