## Overview

The Duke of Burgundy is [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s other suitor, and his brief [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|appearance]] in Act 1 Scene 1 serves as a foil to the [[king-of-france|King of France]]. Where [[king-of-france|France]] sees [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s virtue and marries her without a dowry, Burgundy sees only a balance sheet and withdraws his suit. His single significant line — "Pardon me, royal sir. Election makes not up in such conditions" (001-197) — reduces the marriage contract to a transaction, and his rejection confirms the play's critique of a world in which love is measured in land and gold.

## Key Relationships

- **[[cordelia]]** — Burgundy's rejected suitor; he values her by her price, not her person
- **[[king-of-france]]** — Burgundy's rival, whose acceptance of [[cordelia|Cordelia]] contrasts with Burgundy's rejection
- **[[king-lear]]** — The king whose withdrawal of [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s dowry determines Burgundy's decision

## Key Scenes

### Rejecting Cordelia (001-197)

Burgundy's response to [[king-lear|Lear]]'s announcement that [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s "price is fallen" (001-189) is immediate and unambiguous: without a dowry, [[cordelia|Cordelia]] has no value to him. His language is the language of commerce — "election," "conditions" — and his refusal exposes the logic of the [[division-of-the-kingdom|love test]] it[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]: [[king-lear|Lear]] asked his daughters to compete for territory with declarations of love, and Burgundy simply makes explicit what the test implicitly assumed.

## Notable Speeches

> "Pardon me, royal sir. Election makes not up in such conditions" (001-197)

The line is Burgundy's only significant speech, and it is enough. "Election" — choice — is constrained by "conditions," and the condition that mattered was always the dowry. Burgundy's honesty, however [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]-interested, exposes the transaction at the heart of the [[division-of-the-kingdom|love test]].
