# "O, reason not the need"

Reduced from king to beggar by the daughters he empowered, [[king-lear|Lear]] delivers one of Shakespeare's most profound meditations on human dignity — an argument that what makes life human is precisely what exceeds mere survival.

## Full Text

> **Lear.** O reason not the need: our basest Beggers
> Are in the poorest thing superfluous.
> Allow not Nature, more then Nature needs:
> Mans life is cheape as Beastes. Thou art a Lady;
> If onely to go warme were gorgeous,
> Why Nature needs not what thou gorgeous wear'st,
> Which scarcely keepes thee warme, but for true need:
> You Heauens, giue me that patience, patience I need,
> You see me heere (you Gods) a poore old man,
> As full of griefe as age, wretched in both,
> If it be you that stirres these Daughters hearts
> Against their Father, foole me not so much,
> To beare it tamely: touch me with Noble anger,
> And let not womens weapons, water drops,
> Staine my mans cheekes. No you vnnaturall Hags,
> I will haue such reuenges on you both,
> That all the world shall- I will do such things,
> What they are yet, I know not, but they shalbe
> The terrors of the earth? you thinke Ile weepe,
> No, Ile not weepe, I haue full cause of weeping.
> （002-585–002-606）

> _Storme and Tempest._
> (002-607)

> But this heart shal break into a hundred thousand flawes
> Or ere Ile weepe; O Foole, I shall go mad.
> （002-608–002-609）

## Context

By this point in Act 2 Scene 4, [[king-lear|Lear]] has fled [[goneril|Goneril]]'s house after she halved his hundred knights and has sought refuge with [[regan|Regan]] — only to find her allied with her sister. Together, the two daughters dismantle [[king-lear|Lear]]'s remaining authority knight by knight: [[goneril|Goneril]] asks why he needs fifty, then twenty-five, then ten, then five. [[regan|Regan]] delivers the final cut: "What need one?"(002-585)

[[king-lear|Lear]]'s speech answers this question — not with a number, but with a philosophical argument. His retinue of knights is not about utility; it is about what it means to be human. The speech is the last moment of coherent authority before [[king-lear|Lear]] plunges into [[the-heath|the storm]] — and into madness.

## Speaker

**[[king-lear|King Lear]]](king-lear)**

This is [[king-lear|Lear]] at his most sympathetic — stripped of [[authority-and-power-in-king-lear|kingship]], betrayed by his daughters, standing at the edge of sanity. The arrogance of the [[division-of-the-kingdom|love test]] has given way to vulnerability and [[blindness-and-insight-in-king-lear|insight]]. For the first time, [[king-lear|Lear]] thinks beyond him[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]: he invokes "our basest Beggers" and argues that even the poorest human [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|being]] deserves something beyond brute necessity. But the speech also reveals the limits of his transformation — he cannot sustain this compassionate vision and lurches almost immediately into curses, threats, and [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]-pitying rage.

## Analysis

### Literal Meaning

"Do not argue with me about what I strictly _need_. Even the poorest beggars possess more than the bare minimum for survival. If you reduce human life to nothing but biological necessity, then human life is worth no more than an animal's. You, [[goneril|Goneril]], are a lady — yet the fine clothes you wear are not purely functional; they scarcely keep you warm. Why do you wear them, if only 'true need' matters?"

[[king-lear|Lear]] draws a sharp distinction between animal existence and human life. Animals simply survive; humans require dignity, beauty, and meaning — things that are, strictly speaking, unnecessary.

### Thematic Significance

The speech confronts one of the play's deepest philosophical questions: what is _natural_? [[goneril|Goneril]] and [[regan|Regan]] have invoked a brutal, Hobbesian version of nature throughout the play — a world of appetite, [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]-interest, and power where the strong devour the weak. [[king-lear|Lear]] counters with a different vision of nature: one in which human [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|being]]s are distinguished from beasts precisely by their capacity for more-than-natural desires and attachments.

This tension between the natural as _brutal_ and the natural as _humane_ runs through the entire play. [[king-lear|Lear]]'s argument — that humanity inheres in the "superfluous" — anticipates his later recognition on the heath, where he sees "poor naked wretches" and acknowledges "O I haue tane too little care of this"(003-187). The speech marks the beginning of [[king-lear|Lear]]'s moral education.

### Dramatic Function

The speech is a dramatic turning point. It is [[king-lear|Lear]]'s last attempt to reason with his daughters — and it fails. Their response is not argument but the literal storm that breaks over the scene. The transition from reasoned plea to impotent rage to [[the-heath|the storm]]'s elemental violence mirrors [[king-lear|Lear]]'s psychological disintegration. The speech also establishes the parallel between [[king-lear|Lear]]'s inner turmoil and the external tempest that dominates Act 3.

## Allusions & Echoes

- [[king-lear|Lear]]'s invocation of "our basest Beggers" prefigures his great speech on the heath in Act 3: "O I haue tane too little care of this"(003-187), where he finally acknowledges his failure to consider the poor while he was king.
- The distinction between human and beast echoes throughout the play — from [[edmund|Edmund]]'s "[[nature-in-king-lear|Nature]]" soliloquy at 001-327 to [[king-lear|Lear]]'s description of "unaccommodated man" as "a poor, bare, forked animal" in Act 3 (003-171).
- [[king-lear|Lear]]'s cry "O [[the-fool|Fool]]e, I shall go mad" at the speech's end directly connects this moment to the madness that follows, and to the [[the-fool|Fool]] him[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]] — whose absence after Act 3 is one of the play's haunting silences.

## Performance Notes

Paul Scofield (Peter Brook, 1962) delivered the speech with a gravelly, exhausted dignity, emphasizing the line "I haue full cause of weeping" as the moment [[king-lear|Lear]]'s [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]-awareness breaks through. Ian Holm (RSC, 1997) played it with increasing hysteria, the logic of the argument crumbling as the speech progressed. Glenda Jackson (Old Vic, 2016) brought a particularly sharp focus to the gendered dimension — "let not womens weapons, water drops, staine my mans cheekes" — highlighting [[king-lear|Lear]]'s toxic masculinity as part of his undoing.
