# "The wheel is come full circle"

Mortally wounded by the brother he wronged, Edmund — the play's supreme manipulator — utters a single line of recognition: his schemes have returned upon him.

## Full Text

> The Wheele is come full circle, I am heere. (PN=005-301)

## Context

Edmund has been challenged by an unknown knight — actually Edgar in disguise — who accuses him of treason. They fight, and Edmund falls. As he lies dying, Edgar reveals his identity and recounts their father Gloucester's suffering. Albany confronts Edmund with Goneril's letter plotting his death. It is in this cascade of revelations that Edmund speaks this line. The "wheel" is the wheel of fortune, a medieval emblem of the rise and fall of fortune, and Edmund — who began the play as the dispossessed bastard scheming his way to power — recognizes that his trajectory has completed its arc (PN=005-295).

The scene leading to the combat is rich with dramatic irony. Edgar, unrecognized, approaches Edmund through the formal channels of chivalric combat:

> Draw thy sword, / That if my speech offend a noble heart, / Thy arme may do thee Iustice. (PN=005-250)

Edmund, who has spent the play subverting every social institution — legitimacy, inheritance, loyalty, filial duty — now finds himself embedded in one: the trial by combat. The form he mocked becomes the instrument of his undoing.

## Speaker

**[Edmund](edmund)**

Edmund is the play's most self-aware villain. From his first soliloquy — "Now gods, stand vp for Bastards!" (PN=001-305) — he has been the architect of his own fate, rejecting the cosmic order that would keep him subordinate. His invocation of the wheel of fortune here is startling, because the wheel belongs to the very order he rejected: it implies a universe in which actions have consequences and fortune turns for all, even those who deny its power. This moment of recognition opens the door to his subsequent, unexpected attempt at goodness — his confession that he has ordered Cordelia's death and his attempt to revoke it (PN=005-315).

Edmund's great soliloquy in Act 1 established his philosophy of self-determination:

> Thou Nature art my Godesse; to thy Law / My seruices are bound. (PN=001-306)

He invoked Nature not as the ordered hierarchy of medieval cosmology but as a force of appetite and competition — the strong over the weak, the clever over the dull. The Wheel of Fortune belongs to the older vision he rejected. That he returns to it at the moment of death is either a surrender or a reconciliation: perhaps the two worldviews are not as opposed as Edmund believed.

## Analysis

### Thematic Significance

The wheel-of-fortune image ties Edmund's arc to the play's broader meditation on justice. Throughout King Lear, characters debate whether the universe is morally ordered. Edmund has been the case against: nature, he argued, is red in tooth and claw, and conventional morality is a fiction imposed by the powerful. His dying words complicate this position. The wheel turns — and Edmund is not exempt. Whether this is cosmic justice or mere narrative symmetry, the play leaves ambiguous.

The wheel operates at multiple scales in the tragedy. Lear falls from kingship to nakedness; Gloucester from comfort to blindness; Edmund from power to death. The symmetry is unmistakable — but is it justice or simply pattern? The play refuses to answer, which is part of its power.

### Dramatic Function

This line is Edmund's anagnorisis — the moment of tragic recognition. It precipitates his conversion: immediately afterward, he confesses, "I pant for life. Some good I meane to do / Dispight of mine owne Nature" (PN=005-315), and reveals that he has ordered Lear and Cordelia's deaths. The confession comes too late to save Cordelia, but it completes Edmund's arc from amoral schemer to something more complex: a villain who dies trying, against his own nature, to do one good thing.

The tragedy of the timing is crucial. Edmund's conversion — genuine, one feels — does not avert the catastrophe but deepens it. The audience watches him try to undo what he set in motion, knowing as he speaks that Cordelia is already dead. The wheel has turned, and even Edmund's belated goodness cannot stop it.

## Allusions & Echoes

The wheel of fortune is a medieval commonplace, but Shakespeare gives it specific weight in King Lear. Lear himself invokes the wheel earlier, in his madness: "Let me wipe it first, it smels of mortality" — referring to his hand, but the word "mortality" evokes the wheel's turning (PN=004-178). Kent, in the stocks, speaks of fortune's wheel (PN=002-234). Edmund's use of the image at the point of death gives the metaphor its final, tragic resonance: the wheel turns for everyone.

Edmund's confession also echoes and inverts Gloucester's despair. Gloucester saw the gods as wanton boys; Edmund, dying, sees a wheel that completes its circle — a structure, however pitiless. The two versions of the cosmos — one random, one patterned — stand side by side in Act 5, unresolved.

## Performance Notes

Edmund's dying line can be delivered as bitter irony, sincere recognition, or something in between. In the 2007 RSC production, the actor spoke it with a kind of wonder — as if Edmund were genuinely surprised to discover that consequence exists. The physical staging matters: Edmund is typically on the ground, bleeding, yet the line has an uprightness to it — "I am heere" is a statement of presence, almost of defiance, even at the moment of defeat.
