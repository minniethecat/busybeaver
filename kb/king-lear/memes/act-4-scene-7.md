# Act 4 Scene 7: The Reconciliation

After three acts of suffering, madness, and wandering, Lear wakes in Cordelia's care. What follows is the play's most tender scene — a father who has wronged his daughter, kneeling before her, and a daughter whose tears answer where words cannot. It is the moment the audience has waited for since [[act-1-scene-1|Act 1, Scene 1]], and it is heartbreaking because we know it cannot last.

## Summary

The scene opens with [[cordelia|Cordelia]] and a Doctor attending the sleeping [[king-lear|Lear]]. [[kent-earl-of-kent|Kent]], still in disguise, stands by. Cordelia kisses her father and prays for his recovery: "O my dear father, restoration hang / Thy medicine on my lips" (004-230). Music plays as Lear wakes. Disoriented, he believes he has died: "You do me wrong to take me out o'th'grave" (004-242). Gradually, painfully, he recognizes Cordelia — and kneels to her. "Pray you now, forget and forgive; I am old and foolish" (004-240). Cordelia replies simply: "No cause, no cause" (004-245). The scene closes with Lear and Cordelia walking together, father and daughter reunited if only for a moment.

## Context: The Road to Reunion

The journey to this tent in the French camp has been long. Cordelia was banished in [[act-1-scene-1|Act 1 Scene 1]] for refusing to flatter Lear in the [[division-of-the-kingdom|love test]]. She has returned at the head of a French army, not to conquer Britain but to rescue her father. In the preceding scene — [[act-4-scene-6|Act 4 Scene 6]] — Lear was found wandering near Dover, crowned with weeds, his mind shattered. Now, dressed in fresh garments and laid to sleep, he is carried into Cordelia's tent. The stage image is iconic: the mad king, asleep in a chair, watched over by the daughter he wronged.

Kent's presence matters too. The loyal servant who was banished for speaking truth in Act 1 Scene 1, who followed Lear into the storm disguised as Caius, now stands as silent witness to the reunion he helped bring about. His disguise — still maintained — is a reminder that the play's world is not yet safe enough for truth to walk openly.

## Scene Structure

### Opening: Cordelia's Prayer (lines 1–25)

Cordelia sends a Gentleman to find Lear, who has been seen wandering the fields "crowned with rank fumiter and furrow-weeds" (004-185). The Doctor reports that Lear is asleep and that Cordelia's presence is the best medicine. Cordelia's prayer — "O you kind gods, cure this great breach in his abused nature" (004-225) — establishes the scene's register: not the storm's rage but gentle restoration. The word "breach" is significant: Lear's suffering is not just illness but a rupture, a wound in his nature that only love can close.

### The Awakening (lines 26–45)

Lear is brought in asleep on a chair. Music plays — a detail Shakespeare rarely specifies in stage directions, marking this scene as ritual as much as drama. When Lear wakes, his first words are fragmented and hallucinatory. He thinks he sees Cordelia as "a soul in bliss" (004-235) and himself as "bound upon a wheel of fire" (004-237), a Dantesque image of purgatorial torment. The recognition unfolds in stages: first he sees a woman, then he knows it is Cordelia, then he knows what he has done to her. Shakespeare gives us consciousness returning in increments, each perception a small triumph over madness.

### The Kneeling (lines 46–70)

Lear's kneeling is the scene's emotional centre. The king who demanded public performance of love in Act 1 now kneels in private shame. His words — "I am old and foolish" (004-240) — are the simplest he has spoken since the love test. They are also his first honest self-assessment. Where once he demanded "Which of you shall we say doth love us most," he now asks only to be forgiven.

This is the structural inversion of the play's opening. In Act 1, Lear sat enthroned and Cordelia stood accused. Here, Cordelia stands and Lear kneels. The political hierarchy is reversed, but more importantly, the moral hierarchy is corrected: the wronged daughter is elevated, the wrong-doing father humbled.

### Cordelia's Response (lines 71–85)

Cordelia's "No cause, no cause" (004-245) is as devastating as it is brief. She does not debate, does not recount his wrongs, does not demand an apology — she simply erases the offence. The phrase answers the logic of the love test directly: Lear had demanded quantifiable "cause" for love; Cordelia offers love that requires no cause. The echo of [[nothing-will-come-of-nothing|her Act 1 "Nothing"]] is deliberate: what was then a refusal to perform is now a complete and unconditional gift.

Cordelia weeps — "Be your tears wet?" Lear asks (004-250) — and her tears are the answer that words cannot provide. She speaks fewer lines than Lear in this scene, yet the emotional weight rests with her. Her silence in Act 1 becomes her speech in Act 4; both are forms of integrity.

### Closing: The World Outside (lines 86–97)

The Gentleman enters with news: [[edmund|Edmund]] leads the British forces. The world outside the tent is unchanged. The armies are still marching. But inside the tent, for the duration of this scene, love has held. The scene ends with Lear and Cordelia walking together, father and daughter, toward the waiting catastrophe.

## Key Lines

> "O my dear father, restoration hang / Thy medicine on my lips, and let this kiss / Repair those violent harms that my two sisters / Have in thy reverence made." (004-230) — Cordelia

> "You do me wrong to take me out o'th'grave. / Thou art a soul in bliss, but I am bound / Upon a wheel of fire, that mine own tears / Do scald like molten lead." (004-235) — Lear

> "Pray you now, forget and forgive; I am old and foolish." (004-240) — Lear

> "No cause, no cause." (004-245) — Cordelia

> "And so I am, I am." (004-250) — Lear's response to Cordelia, completing her sentence; father and daughter in harmony at last.

## Thematic Significance

### Love and Forgiveness

The reconciliation scene embodies the play's central question: can love survive the catastrophe that human blindness and cruelty set in motion? For a fleeting moment, the answer is yes. Cordelia's forgiveness is absolute — she does not negotiate terms, does not require Lear to earn back her love. In this she is the antithesis of [[goneril|Goneril]] and [[regan|Regan]], whose love was always transactional.

### Recognition and Identity

Lear's recognition of Cordelia parallels [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s recognition of [[edgar|Edgar]] in the double plot. Both fathers, blind in different ways, finally see. Both moments of recognition are purchased through suffering. But where Gloucester dies of joy ("his flawed heart...burst smilingly"), Lear's recognition leads to the final catastrophe. The double plot's two recognition scenes together suggest that seeing the truth is necessary but not sufficient — it does not guarantee a happy ending.

### The Shadow of Act 5

The scene's placement — immediately before the catastrophe — gives it a terrible dramatic irony. We watch the reconciliation knowing that Cordelia will be dead within a few hundred lines. The scene's tenderness is real, but [[act-5-scene-3|the final scene]] will answer Lear's hope with Cordelia's hanged body. Shakespeare places the grace before the fall so that we feel both the full value of what is lost and the full injustice of its loss.

## Dramatic Significance

Act 4 Scene 7 is the emotional climax of *[[king-lear|King Lear]]*. It pays off Lear's trajectory from arrogance through madness to humility. It gives Cordelia her only extended scene since Act 1. And it marks the structural turn toward the catastrophe: from this point, the play's movement is entirely downward. The reconciliation is not a false ending but a grace note — real, beautiful, and brief.

## Performance Notes

The scene is notoriously difficult to stage. Too much sentiment and it becomes melodrama; too little and the audience feels cheated. The music is essential — it signals that the scene operates in a different register from the storm-tossed scenes that precede it. Cordelia's tears must be visible but not theatrical. Lear's transition from madness to recognition must be incremental: the audience should see him climbing back toward sanity, not simply arriving there. The kneeling — Lear, the former king, on his knees before his daughter — should be held long enough for its reversal of Act 1's power dynamic to register fully.
