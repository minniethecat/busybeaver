# Act 2 Scene 1: Edmund's Plot Deepens

[[edmund|Edmund]]'s plot against [[edgar|Edgar]] reaches its climax: he wounds himself, convinces [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] of [[edgar|Edgar]]'s treachery, and wins the favor of [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]] and [[regan|Regan]] (002-001).

## Summary

[[edmund|Edmund]] learns from Curan that [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]] and [[regan|Regan]] will arrive at [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s castle that night, and that conflict between the [[duke-of-albany|Albany]] and [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]] factions is brewing (002-001). After [[edgar|Edgar]] enters, [[edmund|Edmund]] tells him to flee, then wounds himself and claims [[edgar|Edgar]] attacked him when he refused to join a plot against their father. [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]], horrified, orders a manhunt for [[edgar|Edgar]]. [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]] and [[regan|Regan]] arrive (002-140), hear Edmund's story, and praise him for his loyalty, enlisting him in their service — setting the stage for the confrontation with Lear in the next scene.

## Dramatic Significance

This scene completes Edmund's three-step plan — forged letter, feigned warning, self-inflicted wound — with theatrical flair. The self-wounding is Edmund's most audacious move: he transforms his own body into a prop in his performance, the blood on his arm a physical lie that corroborates his verbal one. Gloucester's response — "of my land, / Loyall and Naturall Boy, Ile worke the meanes to make thee capable" — is the fulfillment of Edmund's scheme. The bastard is now the heir [002-031].

The arrival of Cornwall and Regan shifts Edmund's allegiance from family drama to political alliance. Cornwall calls him "a trusty and a most ingenious seruice" and Regan speaks of her father's "late differences" with approval — the scene thus connects the Gloucester subplot to the main plot's political crisis. Edmund is no longer just a scheming son; he is now a player in the power struggle between the dukes.

The mention of "differences" between Cornwall and Albany (reported by Curan) is crucial exposition. The kingdom is fragmenting not only along familial lines (Lear vs. his daughters) but along political lines (the dukes at odds). This fragmentation — anticipated in the play's opening image of Lear dividing his kingdom — accelerates in Act 2, and Edmund positions himself at its center.

> Some blood drawne on me would beget opinion / Of my more fierce endeauour [002-031]

Edmund's cool calculation as he wounds himself reveals the Machiavellian intelligence that sets him apart from the play's other villains. Goneril and Regan are cruel by instinct; Edmund is cruel by design. His self-awareness — "I haue seene drunkards / Doe more then this in sport" — transforms the wounding from a desperate act into a knowing piece of theater.
