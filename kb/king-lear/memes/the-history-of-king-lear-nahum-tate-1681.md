# The History of King Lear (Nahum Tate, 1681)

**The History of King Lear** is Nahum Tate's 1681 adaptation of Shakespeare's [[king-lear|King Lear]], which profoundly altered the play's structure and ending. Tate's version — in which [[cordelia|Cordelia]] survives, marries [[edgar|Edgar]], and [[king-lear|Lear]] is restored to his throne — replaced Shakespeare's original on the English stage for over 150 years, from 1681 until William Macready restored Shakespeare's tragic ending in 1838.

## Bibliographic Information

- **Full title**: *The History of King Lear. Acted at the Duke's Theatre. Reviv'd with Alterations. By N. Tate.*
- **Author**: Nahum Tate (1652–1715), Poet Laureate of England
- **Year**: 1681
- **Publisher**: E. Flesher for R. Bentley and M. Magnes, London
- **Type**: Stage adaptation (Restoration tragedy revised as tragicomedy)

## Tate's Major Alterations

Tate made several significant changes to Shakespeare's text:

### The Happy Ending

The most consequential alteration is the ending. In Tate's version, Cordelia does not die. Lear is restored to his throne, and Cordelia marries Edgar. Tate justified this change in his dedication, writing that he found Shakespeare's original ending "too harsh and unpleasing" for Restoration audiences, who preferred poetic justice.

### The Love Plot

Tate introduced a romantic subplot between Cordelia and Edgar, who have no romantic relationship in Shakespeare's original. This addition brought the play into line with Restoration taste for love intrigues and provided a conventional romantic resolution.

### The Fool Removed

Tate entirely cut the [[the-fool|Fool]] from the play, judging the character's bitter wit and enigmatic speeches unsuitable for the refined taste of Restoration theatre. The Fool did not reappear in productions of *King Lear* until the nineteenth century.

### Language Modernised

Tate modernised and smoothed Shakespeare's verse, removing much of the play's roughness and obscurity. While this made the text more accessible to Restoration audiences, later critics have judged that it diminished the poetic power of the original.

## Critical Reception and Legacy

For its first century and a half, Tate's adaptation was simply *King Lear* — the only version known to English theatregoers. Samuel Johnson, writing in 1765, confessed that he was "so shocked by Cordelia's death" in Shakespeare's original that he could not bring himself to reread the final scenes, and he endorsed Tate's version.

The restoration of Shakespeare's original ending by Macready in 1838 marked a turning point in critical taste. Romantic and Victorian critics valorised the tragic power of Shakespeare's conclusion, and Tate's happy ending came to be seen as a betrayal of the play's artistic integrity.

Modern criticism takes a more nuanced view. Tate's adaptation is now studied as a significant document of Restoration theatrical practice and changing taste, rather than simply dismissed as a corruption of Shakespeare. It raises enduring questions about adaptation, fidelity, and the relationship between tragedy and audience expectation.
