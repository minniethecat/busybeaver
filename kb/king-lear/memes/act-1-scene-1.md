# Act 1 Scene 1: Love Test and Banishments

The opening scene of *[[king-lear|King Lear]]* -- the [[division-of-the-kingdom|love test]], [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s "[[nothing-and-nihilism-in-king-lear|Nothing]]," Kent's banishment, and [[king-of-france|France]]'s proposal -- sets in motion every subsequent tragedy in the play.

## Summary

The scene opens with [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] introducing his bastard son [[edmund|Edmund]] to Kent (001-002), establishing the parallel subplot before the main action begins. A sennet announces the entrance of [[king-lear|King Lear]] with his court (001-019). [[king-lear|Lear]] declares his "darker purpose" (001-023): to divide his [[britain|kingdom]] among his three daughters, awarding the largest share to the one who professes the greatest love. [[goneril|Goneril]] and [[regan|Regan]] deliver hyperbolic declarations and receive their portions. [[cordelia|Cordelia]] refuses to "heave my heart into my mouth" (001-079), answering "[[nothing-and-nihilism-in-king-lear|Nothing]] my Lord" (001-075). [[king-lear|Lear]], enraged, disclaims "all my Paternall care" (001-102) and strips her of her inheritance. Kent protests the "hideous rashnesse" (001-143) and is banished on pain of death (001-173). [[king-lear|Lear]] offers [[cordelia|Cordelia]] to [[duke-of-burgundy|Burgundy]] without dowry; [[duke-of-burgundy|Burgundy]] withdraws. [[king-of-france|France]], moved by her virtue, seizes upon [[cordelia|Cordelia]] as his queen (001-257). The scene closes with [[goneril|Goneril]] and [[regan|Regan]] privately assessing [[king-lear|Lear]]'s unstable character (001-299) and resolving to act against him.

## Structure

### Opening

The scene begins with the prose exchange between Kent, [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]], and [[edmund|Edmund]] (001-002), which establishes the [[edmund|Edmund]] subplot and introduces [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s [[blindness-and-insight-in-king-lear|moral blindness]] about his sons. The sennet announces [[king-lear|Lear]]'s entrance (001-019), and the tone shifts to ceremony. [[king-lear|Lear]] immediately dispatches [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] to attend the Lords of [[king-of-france|France]] and [[duke-of-burgundy|Burgundy]] (001-020), signaling the moment's political weight. He then reveals his "darker purpose" (001-023) and calls for the map (001-024).

### Development

The [[division-of-the-kingdom|love test]] unfolds in three rounds. [[goneril|Goneril]] claims a love "more then word can weild y matter, / Deere then eye-[[blindness-and-insight-in-king-lear|sight]], space, and libertie" (001-042). [[regan|Regan]] outdoes her, calling [[goneril|Goneril]]'s profession insufficient (001-059). [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s turn precipitates the crisis: "[[nothing-and-nihilism-in-king-lear|Nothing]] my Lord" (001-075), followed by [[king-lear|Lear]]'s towering rage and disinheritance. Kent's intervention -- "be Kent vnmannerly, / When [[king-lear|Lear]] is mad" (001-136) -- escalates the conflict to banishment (001-173). The arrival of [[king-of-france|France]] and [[duke-of-burgundy|Burgundy]] (001-185) provides a brief formal interlude before [[king-lear|Lear]] strips [[cordelia|Cordelia]] of her dowry and [[duke-of-burgundy|Burgundy]] withdraws. [[king-of-france|France]]'s declaration of love for [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s virtue (001-257) constitutes the scene's one moment of grace, but [[king-lear|Lear]]'s dismissal -- "we / Haue no such Daughter" (001-269) -- extinguishes even that.

### Closing

[[cordelia|Cordelia]] bids farewell to her sisters with the prophetic warning "Time shall vnfold what plighted cunning hides" (001-289). [[goneril|Goneril]] and [[regan|Regan]], left alone, conduct a clinical assessment of [[king-lear|Lear]]: "he hath euer but slenderly knowne him[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]e" (001-299); "The best and soundest of his time hath bin but rash" (001-300). [[goneril|Goneril]]'s concluding line -- "We must do something, and i'th' heate" (001-308) -- sets the conspiracy in motion and closes the scene on a note of cold, practical menace.

## Thematic Connections

The scene introduces the play's central opposition between [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|appearance]] and [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|reality]]. [[king-lear|Lear]] cannot distinguish flattery from love; [[cordelia|Cordelia]]'s "I want that glib and oylie Art, / To speake and purpose not" (001-228) is an indictment not just of her sisters but of a whole courtly culture that values rhetoric over [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|truth]]. The theme of nothingness -- [[nothing-will-come-of-nothing|"Nothing will come of nothing"]] (001-078) -- will reverberate through every subsequent scene. The closing scheming of [[goneril|Goneril]] and [[regan|Regan]] also introduces the theme of calculation and cruelty that drives the play's second movement.

## Dramatic Significance

Act 1 Scene 1 is the longest and most densely packed opening scene in Shakespearean tragedy. It establishes the [[king-lear|Lear]] plot, introduces the [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]] subplot, and contains the inciting incident that sets every other action in the play in motion. [[king-lear|Lear]]'s three errors -- dividing [[britain|the kingdom]], disinheriting [[cordelia|Cordelia]], banishing Kent -- create the power vacuum that [[goneril|Goneril]], [[regan|Regan]], and [[edmund|Edmund]] will exploit. The scene's structure -- court ceremony collapsing into family quarrel, then into private conspiracy -- mirrors the play's larger movement from order to [[order-and-chaos-in-king-lear|chaos]].

## Textual Notes

The Folio text (1623) contains significant differences from the Quarto (1608) in this scene, including the absence of [[king-lear|Lear]]'s "darker purpose" speech from Q. The Folio's version is generally considered more authoritative, and the scene's structure as presented here follows F.
