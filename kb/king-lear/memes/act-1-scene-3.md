# Act 1 Scene 3: Goneril's First Move

[[goneril|Goneril]] begins her campaign to strip [[king-lear|Lear]] of his remaining authority, instructing [[oswald|Oswald]] to neglect the King and his retinue in a calculated display of ingratitude that sets the filial betrayal of *[[king-lear|King Lear]]* in motion.

## Summary

[[goneril|Goneril]] enters with [[oswald|Oswald]] and demands to know whether Lear struck her gentleman for chiding his [[the-fool|Fool]] (001-395). She complains that Lear wrongs her constantly, that his knights grow riotous, and that he upbraids her on every trifle. She decides she will not endure it: when Lear returns from hunting she will claim sickness and refuse to speak with him. She instructs [[oswald|Oswald]] and his fellow servants to put on "weary negligence" and give Lear's knights "colder looks" (001-395). If Lear takes offense, she says, let him go to [[regan|Regan]] — whose mind, she claims, is one with hers. She resolves to write immediately to [[regan|Regan]] to coordinate their strategy.

## Key Lines

> "By day and night, he wrongs me, euery howre / He flashes into one grosse crime, or other, / That sets vs all at ods: Ile not endure it" (001-395)

> "Put on what weary negligence you please, / You and your Fellowes: I'de haue it come to question" (001-395)

## Dramatic Significance

This short scene — one of the briefest in the play — performs crucial dramatic work. It establishes that Lear's abdication has not brought him peace but has placed him in a position of humiliating dependency. The king who commanded the attendance of dukes is now struck by his daughter's servants for defending his Fool.

Equally important, the scene introduces Goneril as a calculating political actor rather than a mere villain. Her complaints have rhetorical force — Lear's knights are "riotous," his demands are constant, his presence unmans her household. Shakespeare gives Goneril enough credibility to make the moral calculus uncomfortable: Lear's behavior is genuinely difficult, even if Goneril's response is disproportionate. The "weary negligence" she orders is a form of passive aggression that stops short of open confrontation — the first increment in a strategy of escalation that will culminate in the storm.

The scene also establishes the alliance between Goneril and Regan. Goneril's letter to her sister — "pray you let vs hit together" — reveals their coordination as a strategic partnership rather than an eruption of individual malice. The sisters are not merely cruel; they are organized.

> When he returns from hunting, I will not speake with him: say I am sicke [001-395]

Goneril's decision to refuse speech — to meet Lear's presence with silence and absence — inverts the play's opening. Where Cordelia's silence was an act of integrity, Goneril's is an act of aggression. The same gesture — refusing to speak — carries opposite moral valences depending on the character who performs it.
