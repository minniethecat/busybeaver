## Overview

Regan is [[king-lear|Lear]]'s middle daughter — less rhetorically flamboyant than [[goneril|Goneril]], less ideologically [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]-conscious than [[edmund|Edmund]], but in some ways more terrifying for her matter-of-fact cruelty. Where [[goneril|Goneril]]'s evil is strategic and [[edmund|Edmund]]'s is philosophical, Regan's is something closer to instinct: she does not justify her cruelty; she simply enacts it. Her [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|role]] in [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s blinding — "One side will mocke another: Th' other too" (003-419) — is the play's single most chilling moment, a casual escalation of violence delivered without hesitation or remorse.

Regan functions as [[goneril|Goneril]]'s darker mirror. Both sisters betray their father; both pursue [[edmund|Edmund]] sexually; both die by poison. But Regan's trajectory is steeper and more compressed. She enters the play as the apparently milder sister — [[king-lear|Lear]] flees to her after [[goneril|Goneril]]'s hostility, expecting better treatment — and reveals her[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]] to be equally ruthless. Her competitive dynamic with [[goneril|Goneril]] — each attempting to outdo the other in cruelty to [[king-lear|Lear]], each pursuing [[edmund|Edmund]] — drives the play's middle acts toward their catastrophic conclusion.

## Role & Significance

Regan's dramatic function is to confirm that [[king-lear|Lear]]'s tragedy is total. [[king-lear|Lear]] flees from [[goneril|Goneril]] to Regan in the desperate hope that he has at least one loving daughter: "Thy tender-hefted [[nature-in-king-lear|Nature]] shall not giue / Thee o're to harshnesse" (002-431). The hope is immediately dashed. Regan matches [[goneril|Goneril]] cruelty for cruelty, and their competition — who can strip [[king-lear|Lear]] of more knights, who can treat him more contemptuously — is the engine of [[king-lear|Lear]]'s descent into madness.

Regan's marriage to [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]] makes her structurally distinct from [[goneril|Goneril]]. Where [[goneril|Goneril]] is the dominant partner in her marriage (openly contemptuous of [[duke-of-albany|Albany]]'s "milky gentlenesse," 004-123), Regan is initially partnered with a husband whose violence matches her own cruelty. [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]]'s death at the hands of a servant — a moment of moral revolt from below — leaves Regan widowed and sets the stage for her fatal rivalry with [[goneril|Goneril]] over [[edmund|Edmund]].

Regan's relationship with [[goneril|Goneril]] is the play's most toxic sibling dynamic. The two sisters begin as allies in cruelty, but their alliance fractures under the pressure of sexual competition. Both desire [[edmund|Edmund]], and Regan's jealousy — she poisons [[goneril|Goneril]], and is her[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]] poisoned — literalizes the play's vision of a world in which family bonds have become vectors of destruction.

## Key Scenes

### The Love Test (001-001)

Regan's performance in the [[division-of-the-kingdom|love test]] is the play's most compressed act of rhetorical fraud. Her speech — "I am made of that [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]e-mettle as my Sister, / And prize me at her worth" (001-055) — is both a declaration of love and a competitive bid: she will not be outdone by [[goneril|Goneril]]. The speech's genius is its apparent modesty (she claims only to equal [[goneril|Goneril]], not exceed her) masking its actual ambition (she demands to be valued equally). The "[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]e-mettle" pun — metal/mettle — suggests both substance and character: Regan claims to be made of the same material as her sister, and that material is base.

### The Refusal of Lear's Knights (002-422)

When [[king-lear|Lear]] arrives at [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s castle seeking refuge from [[goneril|Goneril]]'s hostility, Regan's rejection is more wounding than [[goneril|Goneril]]'s because it follows the logic of "reason." [[goneril|Goneril]] attacks [[king-lear|Lear]]'s knights as disorderly; Regan, more insidiously, asks what need a king has of knights at all: "What need one?" (002-548). The rhetorical question — framed as practical economy rather than cruelty — is the play's most devastating diminishment of [[king-lear|Lear]]. Regan does not rage at her father; she reasons him into nothing.

> "O Sir, you are old, / Nature in you stands on the very Verge / Of his confine: you should be rul'd, and led / By some discretion, that discernes your state / Better then you your selfe" (002-481)

The speech is cruel precisely because it deploys the language of care. Regan does not attack [[king-lear|Lear]]; she diagnoses him — old, enfeebled, in need of guidance — and offers her[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]] as the authority who will provide it. The word "discretion" does the heaviest work: it means both "discernment" and "power to decide," and Regan claims both.

### The Blinding of Gloucester (003-367)

Regan's [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|role]] in [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s blinding is brief but definitive. When [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]] gouges out one of [[blindness-and-insight-in-king-lear|Gloucester's eyes]], a servant intervenes — one of the play's few acts of moral courage from below — and is killed by Regan, who seizes a sword and stabs the servant from behind. Her line as [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]] prepares to blind [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s second eye — "One side will mocke another: Th' other too" (003-419) — is delivered without passion, as a practical observation. The casualness is the horror: Regan treats torture as a matter of aesthetic symmetry.

### The Rivalry with Goneril (005-002)

In Act 5, Regan's jealousy of [[goneril|Goneril]]'s relationship with [[edmund|Edmund]] consumes her. Her interrogation of [[edmund|Edmund]] — "Why is this reasond?" (005-026) — betrays a possessiveness that [[goneril|Goneril]], more strategically, conceals. The sisters' mutual poisoning — [[goneril|Goneril]] poisons Regan, then kills her[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]] — is the logical endpoint of a relationship built entirely on competition. There has never been love between them; there has only been comparison, and comparison breeds destruction.

## Character Arc

Regan's arc is one of revelation rather than change. She begins the play as what she appears to be — a daughter performing love for political advantage — and reveals her[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]], across the five acts, to be worse. There is no moment of [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]-recognition, no gesture toward redemption. Regan dies offstage, poisoned by her own sister, and her death is reported in a single line: "[[goneril|Gonerill]] and Regans bodies brought out" (005-349). The brevity is the judgment: Regan's death, unlike [[edmund|Edmund]]'s, produces no [[blindness-and-insight-in-king-lear|insight]], no recognition, no meaning. She simply ceases to exist.

The trajectory from "I am made of that [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]e-mettle as my Sister" (001-055) to the body brought out in Act 5 traces the collapse of competitive cruelty into mutual annihilation. Regan and [[goneril|Goneril]] spend the play outdoing each other in ruthlessness — who can strip [[king-lear|Lear]] of more knights, who can secure [[edmund|Edmund]] — and the competition consumes them both. The play's moral logic is implicit: cruelty, unchecked by any bond of love or loyalty, is inherently [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]-destructive.

## Notable Speeches

> "I am made of that selfe-mettle as my Sister, / And prize me at her worth" (001-055)

Regan's love-test speech is a masterpiece of competitive flattery. The claim to equality with [[goneril|Goneril]] is actually a claim to superiority — if she is [[goneril|Goneril]]'s equal, she deserves [[goneril|Goneril]]'s reward — and the "[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]]e-mettle" pun suggests both material substance and moral character, neither of which Regan possesses.

> "O Sir, you are old, / Nature in you stands on the very Verge / Of his confine: you should be rul'd, and led" (002-481)

Regan's most devastating speech deploys the language of care to justify cruelty. [[king-lear|Lear]] is old; [[nature-in-king-lear|Nature]]'s hold on him is tenuous; he should be "rul'd" by those who know better. The word "rul'd" does double work: it means both "guided" (the benevolent reading) and "governed" (the actual intent). Regan does not need to attack [[king-lear|Lear]]; she simply redefines him as incompetent and her[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]] as his necessary guardian.

> "One side will mocke another: Th' other too" (003-419)

Spoken as [[duke-of-cornwall|Cornwall]] prepares to gouge out [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s second eye. The line's horror lies in its aesthetic logic: Regan treats symmetrical blinding as a matter of visual balance. "One side will mocke another" — the asymmetry would be displeasing, a mockery of the body's natural pairing. The "joke" is Regan's: she finds the human body's bilateral symmetry amusing, and she will not tolerate [[appearance-vs-reality-in-king-lear|being]] mocked.

## Critical Interpretations

Regan has traditionally been read as [[goneril|Goneril]]'s lesser twin — less intelligent, less articulate, less interesting — but recent criticism has complicated this assessment. Regan's cruelty, while less rhetorically elaborate, is in some ways more terrifying for its directness. [[goneril|Goneril]] justifies her actions ([[king-lear|Lear]]'s knights are disorderly, [[duke-of-albany|Albany]] is weak); Regan simply acts. Her stabbing of the servant who intervenes in [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s blinding is the play's most impulsive act of violence — no calculation, no justification, just the immediate annihilation of an obstacle.

Feminist criticism has explored Regan as a figure who refuses the constraints of daughterhood and wifehood, pursuing power and sexuality with a directness that the play codes as monstrous. Regan's desire for [[edmund|Edmund]] — a desire she does not conceal, does not rationalize — marks her as "unnatural" in the play's terms, but it also marks her as a figure of female agency that exceeds the available categories. The play destroys her for this excess, but the excess it[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]] registers as a kind of rebellion against the limited [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|role]]s available to women in the [[king-lear|Lear]] universe.

Psychoanalytic readings have emphasized the Regan-[[goneril|Goneril]] dyad as a study in sibling rivalry taken to its lethal extreme. The two sisters are mirror images, each defined by comparison with the other, and their mutual destruction is the logical outcome of a relationship in which [[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|identity]] is constituted through competition rather than love. Regan's poisoning by [[goneril|Goneril]] — and [[goneril|Goneril]]'s subsequent suicide — literalizes the death-drive at the heart of their bond.
