# King Lear (Quarto 1608)

The first printed edition of Shakespeare's *[[king-lear|King Lear]]*, published in quarto format in 1608. It presents a text substantially different from the version that appears in the 1623 First Folio — lacking approximately 300 lines and containing some 100 lines not found in the Folio. The Quarto is one of the two foundational witnesses to the play's early textual history [^1].
## Overview


The 1608 Quarto (designated Q1 by bibliographers) was entered in the Stationers' Register on 26 November 1607 and printed by Nathaniel Butter the following year. Its full title is *M. William Shak-speare: His True Chronicle Historie of the life and death of [[king-lear|King Lear]] and his three Daughters. With the unfortunate life of [[edgar|Edgar]], sonne and heire to [[earl-of-gloucester|the Earl]]e of Gloster, and his sullen and assumed humor of Tom of Bedlam*. Several variant states of Q1 exist (twelve copies survive), suggesting the text was corrected during the print run. A second quarto (Q2) appeared in 1619, printed by William Jaggard as part of the False Folio, but it is essentially a reprint of Q1 and has no independent textual authority [^2].

The Quarto's title page frames the play as a "chronicle history" — a genre designation that aligns *Lear* with the popular Elizabethan history-play tradition. This generic framing was deliberate marketing: history plays sold well in the early Jacobean book trade, and the Quarto's title foregrounds the political and dynastic elements of the story. The subtitle's emphasis on "the unfortunate life of Edgar" further signals the Gloucester subplot as a major selling point, treating it not as secondary action but as co-equal tragedy [^3].

The printer Nicholas Okes was a relatively inexperienced stationer at the time of Q1's production; Butter subcontracted the printing to him, and the resulting text shows signs of compositorial inexperience — frequent misreadings, erratic punctuation, and prose passages mistakenly set as verse. Okes would later go on to print other Shakespeare quartos, but the 1608 *Lear* was among his earliest ventures into play-printing. The type used was a worn pica roman, and the compositors appear to have worked from a manuscript (likely Shakespeare's foul papers) that was itself difficult to decipher, compounding errors at every stage of production [^4].

## Relevance to King Lear


### Textual Source

The Quarto text is one of the two primary witnesses to Shakespeare's play, alongside the 1623 First Folio. Since the early 1980s, editors have generally accepted that Q1 and F1 descend from different manuscript sources — Q1 likely from Shakespeare's foul papers (his working draft), and F1 from a prompt-book or a transcript of it that had been annotated by reference to Q1. The Quarto therefore offers a window into an earlier, perhaps more immediate version of the play as Shakespeare originally conceived it [^5].

### Major Differences from the Folio

The Quarto lacks several Folio passages, including [[king-lear|Lear]]'s mock trial of his daughters (a portion of Act 3, Scene 6) and some of the dialogue between the servants after [[earl-of-gloucester|Gloucester]]'s blinding. Conversely, the Quarto preserves some 100 lines not in the Folio, including the mock-trial scene in extended form and the Doctor's speech in the reunion scene. The Folio's ending differs subtly: in the Quarto, [[duke-of-albany|Albany]] speaks the final lines, while in the Folio, [[edgar|Edgar]] does — shifting the political weight of the closing moments [^6].

### The "Two-Text" Controversy

Since the Oxford Shakespeare (1986) printed the Quarto and Folio texts as separate plays, the scholarly consensus holds that they represent distinct versions, each with its own artistic integrity. The Quarto may represent the play as first staged (c. 1605–06), while the Folio reflects revisions made by Shakespeare him[[disguise-and-identity-in-king-lear|self]] for later performances, possibly around 1610. This means the "[[king-lear|Lear]]" we commonly read in modern conflated editions is an editorial construct that has no historical existence [^7].

The two-text theory has profound implications for how we read the play. In the Quarto, the mock-trial scene is longer and more explicit in its political satire, while Lear's madness is presented with more explanatory context. In the Folio, cuts sharpen the tragic focus by removing some of that explanatory material, leaving the audience — like the characters — to confront events without the comfort of explanation. The Quarto offers an *interpreted* Lear; the Folio offers an *unmediated* one [^8].

## Key Passages / Ideas


> The Quarto's title page describes a "True Chronicle Historie" — aligning the play with the popular history-play genre and emphasising the Gloucester subplot ("the unfortunate life of Edgar") as a selling point. This framing was dropped from the Folio, which titles the play simply "The Tragedie of King Lear." [^9]

The Quarto's mock-trial scene is crucial for understanding the play's political radicalism. In it, [[king-lear|Lear]], [[edgar|Edgar]], and the [[the-fool|Fool]] stage an impromptu courtroom on the heath, trying [[goneril|Gonerill]] and [[regan|Regan]] in absentia. The Folio shortens this scene, but the Quarto preserves [[king-lear|Lear]]'s hallucinatory justice in fuller form — an image of a king who has lost his [[britain|kingdom]] but not his instinct to judge [^10].

A Quarto-only passage in the reunion scene (Act 4, Scene 7) gives the Doctor a substantial speech describing Lear's recovery through rest and music. The Folio cuts this speech to a few lines, transforming Lear's awakening from a medical event into a miraculous one. Where the Quarto implies healing is a process, the Folio presents it as revelation — a difference that shapes the entire reunion sequence's emotional architecture [^11].

## Textual History & Reception


Q1 is notoriously corrupt in places — filled with misreadings, mislineation, and prose set as verse — which suggests it was set from Shakespeare's difficult-to-read manuscript. The twelve surviving copies show stop-press corrections, indicating the printer made changes mid-run. Notable variants include the spelling of names, stage directions (some expanded in F1), and the crucial final speech assignment [^12].

### Modern Scholarly Editions

Modern editions that privilege Q1 include the Oxford Shakespeare (1986, edited by Stanley Wells and Gary Taylor), the New Cambridge Shakespeare (Jay Halio, 1992), and the Arden Third Series "Q" edition (1994, edited by R.A. Foakes). Michael Warren's *The Complete King Lear 1608–1623* (1989) — a parallel-text facsimile edition — remains the most accessible resource for direct Q1/F1 comparison. More recently, the New Oxford Shakespeare (2016, edited by Gary Taylor et al.) has reaffirmed the two-text theory with updated textual analysis, arguing that Q1 preserves not only Shakespeare's first draft but also the traces of collaborative revision with an unknown co-author [^13].


### Reception History
Until the twentieth century, the Quarto was regarded as a corrupt or pirated version of the "true" Folio text. Alexander Pope (1725) dismissed its variants as printer's errors; Samuel Johnson (1765) followed Pope in preferring F1 readings. This began to change with W.W. Greg's *The Editorial Problem in Shakespeare* (1942), which demonstrated systematic patterns in Q1/F1 divergence incompatible with random corruption. Greg argued that both texts have authority, and that where they differ, editorial choice — not automatic F1 preference — must govern [^14].

The watershed came in the 1980s with the Oxford Shakespeare's decision to print Q1 and F1 as separate works — "The History of King Lear" (Q1) and "The Tragedy of King Lear" (F1). This editorial decision reflected a new scholarly consensus: Shakespeare revised his own work, and both versions deserve to be read as autonomous artistic statements rather than conflated into a single "ideal" text that never existed in his lifetime [^15].

The Quarto's rehabilitation is itself a parable about textual scholarship: what was once dismissed as corruption proved to be evidence of artistic process. The "bad" Quarto turned out to be the *early* Quarto — and the variations between Q1 and F1, far from diminishing Shakespeare's achievement, reveal him as a working playwright who revised, refined, and reimagined his own work across years of performance history [^16].
