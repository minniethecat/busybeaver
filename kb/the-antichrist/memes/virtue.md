The concept of **virtue** is subjected by Nietzsche to one of his most thoroughgoing revaluations. Where the entire Christian-moral tradition takes virtue as a given — a universal standard of goodness discoverable by reason or revelation — Nietzsche treats virtue as something that must be _created_ by the individual, grounded in the instincts of life, and judged solely by whether it enhances or diminishes the feeling of power. The Christian "virtues" — [[pity]], humility, chastity, obedience — are not virtues at all but symptoms of physiological decline, instruments by which the weak impose their values on the strong.

## Definition and Mechanism

Nietzsche's definitional move on virtue is to strip it of universality and return it to its etymological and psychological root: _virtù_ in the Renaissance sense — efficiency, capacity, power. A virtue, in his analysis, is not something one _obeys_ but something one _invents_ out of personal necessity.

> "Not contentment, but more power; _not_ peace at any price, but war; _not_ virtue, but efficiency (virtue in the Renaissance sense, _virtu_, virtue free of moral acid)." (002-020)

This is the touchstone of Nietzsche's entire treatment of virtue: it must be free of "moral acid" — the corrosive universalizing impulse that dissolves all particular strength into an abstract "good." The mechanism of genuine virtue is self-overcoming, not self-denial; it draws its energy from the will to [[will-to-power|power]], not from submission to an external law.

| Traditional (Christian) Virtue | Nietzschean Revaluation |
|-------------------------------|------------------------|
| Obedience to moral law | Self-legislation from instinct |
| Universal, binding on all | Personal, arising from individual need |
| Denial of the body and passions | Channeling and intensifying natural drives |
| Humility as highest state | Pride in self-mastery as highest state |
| Measured by conformity | Measured by enhancement of life |

## Kant as the Philosopher of Anti-Virtue

Nietzsche reserves particular scorn for Kant's moral philosophy, which he sees as the philosophical codification of Christian virtue stripped of its theological dressing but retaining all its life-denying structure.

> "A virtue must be _our_ invention; it must spring out of _our_ personal need and defence. In every other case it is a source of danger. That which does not belong to our life _menaces_ it; a virtue which has its roots in mere respect for the concept of 'virtue,' as Kant would have it, is pernicious. 'Virtue,' 'duty,' 'good for its own sake,' goodness grounded upon impersonality or a notion of universal validity — these are all chimeras, and in them one finds only an expression of the decay, the last collapse of life, the Chinese spirit of Königsberg." (011-014)

Kant's categorical imperative — act only according to that maxim which you could will to become universal law — is, for Nietzsche, the philosophical equivalent of a suicide note. By demanding that one's action be universalizable, it strips the act of everything personal, everything instinctual, everything that makes it _one's own_. What Kant calls "reason" Nietzsche diagnoses as a moribund, leveling, life-weary instinct disguising itself as philosophy.

## Christian Virtues as Symptoms of Décadence

Nietzsche's critique extends beyond moral philosophy to the specific virtues that Christianity has elevated. Each of the cardinal Christian virtues is unmasked as a manifestation of physiological and psychological decline.

> "Christianity is called the religion of _pity_. — Pity stands in opposition to all the tonic passions that augment the energy of the feeling of aliveness: it is a depressant. A man loses power when he pities." (007-014)

Pity, the supreme Christian virtue, is revealed as a mechanism for transmitting and multiplying suffering rather than alleviating it. It "thwarts the whole law of evolution, which is the law of natural selection" — it preserves what is ripe for destruction, fighting on the side of the botched and the condemned.

> "Mankind has ventured to call pity a virtue (—in every _superior_ moral system it appears as a weakness—); going still further, it has been called _the_ virtue, the source and foundation of all other virtues — but let us always bear in mind that this was from the standpoint of a philosophy that was nihilistic, and upon whose shield _the denial of life_ was inscribed. Schopenhauer was right in this: that by means of pity life is denied, and made _worthy of denial_ — pity is the technic of nihilism." (007-014)

The moral halo around pity conceals its true function: to make life seem gloomy, dubious, and ultimately worthy of denial. What Christianity presents as its highest moral achievement is, in Nietzsche's diagnosis, its most effective instrument of destruction.

## Virtue and the Theology of Decadence

Nietzsche traces the Christian transformation of virtue to a specific historical-psychological process: the revaluation by which the weak, unable to exercise genuine power, redefine powerlessness as virtue. The strong man's virtues — pride, aggression, ambition, courage — are recoded as vices, while the weak man's necessary adaptations — humility, submission, forgiveness — are recoded as the highest virtues.

> "Wherever the will to power begins to decline, in whatever form, there is always an accompanying decline physiologically, a _décadence_. The divinity of this _décadence_, shorn of its masculine virtues and passions, is converted perforce into a god of the physiologically degraded, of the weak. Of course, they do not _call_ themselves the weak; they call themselves 'the good.'" (017-014)

This revaluation of virtues is not an innocent semantic shift — it is a strategy of [[priest|priestly]] power. By convincing the strong that their strength is sinful, the priest gains control over the strong; by convincing the weak that their weakness is virtue, the priest gains the loyalty of the weak. The entire Christian virtue-system is thus exposed as a technology of [[holy-lie|priestly domination]].

## Virtue and the Life-Instinct

Nietzsche's alternative to Christian-Kantian morality is not immorality but a higher, more demanding morality grounded in the life-instinct itself. Genuine virtue is whatever increases the feeling of power, whatever makes life more abundant, whatever sharpens the instincts rather than dulling them.

> "An action prompted by the life-instinct proves that it is a _right_ action by the amount of pleasure that goes with it: and yet that Nihilist, with his bowels of Christian dogmatism, regarded pleasure as an _objection_.... What destroys a man more quickly than to work, think and feel without inner necessity, without any deep personal desire, without pleasure — as a mere automaton of duty? That is the recipe for _décadence_, and no less for idiocy." (011-014)

The life-instinct is the only valid criterion for evaluating a virtue. If a "virtue" makes one weaker, more dependent, less capable of joy — it is not a virtue, regardless of how many millennia of priests and philosophers have called it one. This is [[morality|Nietzsche's revaluation of all values]] in microcosm: the return of judgment from the abstract universal to the concrete, embodied, individual life.
