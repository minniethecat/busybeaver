**Revenge** (_Rache_) is, in Nietzsche's genealogy of Christianity, the founding passion of the church. It is not a peripheral emotion but the _constitutive_ motive that transformed the [[jesus-christ|gospel of Jesus]] — a purely inner state of blessedness, a way of living without resistance or judgment — into a system of cosmic retaliation, eschatological [[judgment]], and priestly power. "It was precisely the most unevangelical of feelings, _revenge_, that now possessed them" (040-001). The entire apparatus of Christian eschatology — the Last Judgment, the separation of sheep and goats, the eternal [[punishment]] of the damned — is, in Nietzsche's reading, the revenge-fantasy of disciples who could not forgive their master's death.

## The Cross: Where Revenge Entered

In §40, Nietzsche narrates the psychological crisis that followed the crucifixion. Jesus' death — "that unexpected and shameful death... which was usually reserved for the _canaille_ only" — confronted the disciples with "the real riddle: '_Who was it? what was it?_'" (040-001). The answer they arrived at — "dominant Judaism, its ruling class" — transformed their understanding of Jesus retroactively:

> From that moment, one found one's self in revolt _against_ the established order, and began to understand Jesus as _in revolt against the established order_. Until then this militant, this nay-saying, nay-doing element in his character had been lacking; what is more, he had appeared to present its opposite. (040-001)

The disciples projected their own reactive emotion backward onto Jesus. His death, which had been the supreme demonstration of his teaching — "the freedom from and superiority to every feeling of _ressentiment_" — was reinterpreted as a _refutation_ requiring counter-action:

> But his disciples were very far from _forgiving_ his death — though to have done so would have accorded with the Gospels in the highest degree; and neither were they prepared to _offer_ themselves, with gentle and serene calmness of heart, for a similar death. (040-001)

## The Revenge Mechanism: Elevation and Separation

Nietzsche identifies a specific psychological mechanism by which revenge operates in the early Christian community. Unable to strike back directly, the disciples transformed their enemy's victim into a unique, unreachable being:

> Their revenge took the form of _elevating_ Jesus in an extravagant fashion, and thus separating him from themselves: just as, in earlier times, the Jews, to revenge themselves upon their enemies, separated themselves from their God, and placed him on a great height. The One God and the Only Son of God: both were products of _ressentiment_. (040-001)

The doctrine of Christ's unique divinity is thus decoded as a _revenge mechanism_: by making Jesus incomparable, his followers made his enemies incomparably guilty. The higher Christ rises, the lower his killers fall. The entire architecture of Christian theology — incarnation, atonement, judgment — is built upon this initial act of psychological vengeance.

## Paul: "The Greatest of All Apostles of Revenge"

If the disciples' revenge was reactive and emotional, [[paul|Paul's]] was systematic and theological. In §45, Nietzsche cites Paul's own words from 1 Corinthians as evidence:

> "Hath not God made foolish the wisdom of this world?... God hath chosen the foolish things of the world to confound the wise; and God hath chosen the weak things of the world to confound the things which are mighty; And base things of the world, and things which are despised, hath God chosen, _yea_, and things which are not, to bring to nought things that are: That no flesh should glory in his presence." (045-001, quoting 1 Cor. 1:20ff.)

Nietzsche's verdict is absolute:

> In order to _understand_ this passage, a first-rate example of the psychology underlying every Chandala-morality, one should read the first part of my "Genealogy of Morals": there, for the first time, the antagonism between a _noble_ morality and a morality born of _ressentiment_ and impotent vengefulness is exhibited. Paul was the greatest of all apostles of revenge. (045-001)

Paul's theology of the cross — God choosing the weak to shame the strong — is the _ressentiment_-morality in its purest theological form. The "wisdom of the world" must be destroyed not because it is false but because it belongs to the strong.

## The Three Forms of Christian Revenge

Nietzsche's analysis distinguishes three distinct but interconnected forms of revenge operating in early Christianity:

| Form | Mechanism | Theological Expression | Historical Agent |
|------|-----------|----------------------|-----------------|
| Reactive revenge | Projection of militancy backward onto Jesus | Jesus as "revolutionary" | The disciples after the cross |
| Elevating revenge | Making the victim unique to maximize enemy guilt | Christ as "Only Son of God" | The early community |
| Systematic revenge | Theological inversion of all natural values | Chandala-morality as doctrine | Paul |

All three share a common structure: the _denial_ of the actual Jesus — who "does not resist; he does not defend his rights; he makes no effort to ward off the most extreme penalty" (035-001) — in favor of a Jesus who _judges_, a God who _punishes_, and a kingdom that _arrives_ to destroy enemies.

## Revenge in the Concept of God

Even before Christianity, Nietzsche detects the revenge-motive in the transformation of the divine. In §16, the healthy national god — a being "who knew... anger, revenge, envy, scorn, cunning, violence" (016-001) — is contrasted with the neutered Christian god. Paradoxically, the god who _includes_ revenge among his attributes is healthier than the god who _denies_ it while enacting it covertly through eschatological judgment.

The Christian god claims to be "good" — all-forgiving, all-loving — but the doctrine of hell reveals the repressed revenge that animates him. "What could be less evangelical than 'recompense,' 'punishment,' and 'sitting in judgment'?" (040-001). The very concepts Jesus' teaching had abolished were reinstated by the disciples' inability to live without them.

## Beyond Revenge: The Evangelical Possibility

Nietzsche's reading of Jesus in §35 gestures toward a mode of existence _beyond_ revenge — a way of dying, and therefore of living, that does not need to retaliate:

> He does not resist; he does not defend his rights; he makes no effort to ward off the most extreme penalty — more, _he invites it_.... And he prays, suffers and loves _with_ those, _in_ those, who do him evil.... _Not_ to defend one's self, _not_ to show anger, _not_ to lay blames.... On the contrary, to submit even to the Evil One — to _love_ him. (035-001)

This is not Christian meekness in the pejorative sense — the submission of the weak who _wish_ to retaliate but cannot. It is the genuine incapacity for enmity that Nietzsche attributes to Jesus' physiological type. Whether this state is _desirable_ — whether it represents an ideal or a pathology — is a question Nietzsche leaves deliberately unresolved. What is clear is that Christianity, as a historical institution, chose the opposite path: it built a world-religion upon the very revenge-impulse its founder had transcended.
