**Sickness** is, in Nietzsche's physiological critique of Christianity, not a metaphor but a _diagnosis_. Christianity is not accidentally associated with illness — it is a religion that _requires_ sickness, _produces_ sickness, and _sanctifies_ sickness as the condition of its own existence. The priestly type has a "vital interest in making mankind sick" (024-001), and the entire system of Christian salvation is, at its deepest level, "a method of producing a _folie circulaire_ upon a soil already prepared for it, which is to say, a soil thoroughly unhealthy" (051-001).

## Christianity Finds Sickness Necessary

Nietzsche's most concentrated analysis of Christianity-as-pathology appears in §51, where he draws an explicit contrast with the Greek valuation of health:

> Christianity finds sickness _necessary_, just as the Greek spirit had need of a superabundance of health — the actual ulterior purpose of the whole system of salvation of the church is to _make_ people ill. And the church itself — doesn't it set up a Catholic lunatic asylum as the ultimate ideal? — The whole earth as a madhouse? (051-001)

The requirement is not metaphorical. Nietzsche identifies a causal chain: the church needs believers who are _physiologically incapable_ of resistance, and therefore cultivates precisely the conditions — guilt, self-loathing, instinct-suppression — that produce nervous exhaustion:

> The sort of religious man that the church _wants_ is a typical _décadent_; the moment at which a religious crisis dominates a people is always marked by epidemics of nervous disorder; the "inner world" of the religious man is so much like the "inner world" of the overstrung and exhausted that it is difficult to distinguish between them. (051-001)

## The Physiological Basis of Salvation Doctrine

In §§29–30, Nietzsche reads the Gospel psychology through a physiological lens. The Saviour's psychological type, he argues, is not a moral phenomenon but a _physiological_ one — a "morbid sensibility" that recoils from all contact with reality:

> We all know that there is a morbid sensibility of the tactile nerves which causes those suffering from it to recoil from every touch, and from every effort to grasp a solid object. Brought to its logical conclusion, such a physiological _habitus_ becomes an instinctive hatred of all reality, a flight into the "intangible," into the "incomprehensible." (029-001)

From this physiological condition, Nietzsche derives the entire structure of Christian doctrine:

> These are the two _physiological realities_ upon and out of which the doctrine of salvation has sprung. I call them a sublime super-development of hedonism upon a thoroughly unsalubrious soil. (030-001)

The "glad tidings" are, in this reading, the symptom-report of a nervous system pushed beyond its tolerance for reality.

## The Priest as Pathological Type

The [[priest]] is not merely a social role but a _physiological type_ — recognizable by specific symptoms of degeneration:

> The complete lack of psychological cleanliness in the priest — revealed by a glance at him — is a phenomenon _resulting_ from _décadence_, — one may observe in hysterical women and in rachitic children how regularly the falsification of instincts, delight in lying for the mere sake of lying, and incapacity for looking straight and walking straight are symptoms of _décadence_. (052-001)

The priest's relationship to truth is determined by his physiology: he is a fraud _because_ he is sick. The instinct to lie is not a choice but a symptom:

> "Faith" means the will to avoid knowing what is true. The pietist, the priest of either sex, is a fraud _because_ he is sick: his instinct _demands_ that the truth shall never be allowed its rights on any point. "Whatever makes for illness is _good_; whatever issues from abundance, from superabundance, from power, is _evil_": so argues the believer. (052-001)

| Healthy Instinct | Sickness-Instinct (Christian) |
|-----------------|------------------------------|
| Seeks truth | Avoids knowing what is true |
| Affirms abundance | Calls abundance "evil" |
| Values power | Values weakness |
| Trusts the body | Teaches misunderstanding of the body |

## The God of the Sick

In §17, Nietzsche traces the historical _degradation_ of the concept of God — a process driven by the instincts of the weak who reshape divinity in their own sick image:

> When everything necessary to _ascending_ life; when all that is strong, courageous, masterful and proud has been eliminated from the concept of a god; when he has sunk step by step to the level of a staff for the weary, a sheet-anchor for the drowning; when he becomes the poor man's god, the sinner's god, the invalid's god _par excellence_, and the attribute of "saviour" or "redeemer" remains as the one essential attribute of divinity — just _what_ is the significance of such a metamorphosis? (017-001)

The Christian God becomes "the democrat among gods" — a god of "all the dark nooks and crevices, of all the noisesome quarters of the world" (017-001). He is pale, weak, _décadent_ — a reflection of those who worship him. The reduction of the divine to a "thing-in-itself" is the final stage of this pathological diminishment.

## Holiness as Symptom

Perhaps Nietzsche's most devastating move is to reinterpret "holiness" — Christianity's supreme value — as a _symptom complex_ of physiological disorder:

> Christianity... teaches misunderstanding of the body! that refuses to rid itself of the superstition about the soul! that makes a "virtue" of insufficient nourishment! that combats health as a sort of enemy, devil, temptation! that persuades itself that it is possible to carry about a "perfect soul" in a cadaver of a body, and that, to this end, had to devise for itself a new concept of "perfection," a pale, sickly, idiotically ecstatic state of existence, so-called "holiness" — a holiness that is itself merely a series of symptoms of an impoverished, enervated and incurably disordered body! (051-001)

Holiness is not transcendence of the body but _symptom of its decay_. The entire ascetic ideal — fasting, vigils, self-mortification — is read as the nervous system's cry of distress, dressed in theological language.

## The Final Condemnation

Nietzsche's concluding judgment in §62 consolidates the sickness theme into a comprehensive accusation:

> Parasitism as the _only_ practice of the church; with its anæmic and "holy" ideals, sucking all the blood, all the love, all the hope out of life; the beyond as the will to deny all reality; the cross as the distinguishing mark of the most subterranean conspiracy ever heard of, — against health, beauty, well-being, intellect, _kindness_ of soul — _against life itself_.... (062-001)

The church is not merely mistaken about health — it is _at war_ with health. Its existence depends on the perpetuation of the very sickness it claims to cure. "It lives by distress; it _creates_ distress to make _itself_ immortal" (062-001).
