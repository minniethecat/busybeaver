**Sacrifice**, in Nietzsche's analysis, undergoes a decisive transformation between pagan antiquity and Christianity. For a healthy, self-affirming people, sacrifice is an act of _gratitude_ — the overflowing of abundance, a gift offered to a god who reflects the people's own strength. "He who is rich will give of his riches; a proud people need a god to whom they can make _sacrifices_" (016-001). Christianity, by contrast, converts sacrifice into an economy of _debt_ — a blood-transaction in which an innocent victim pays the penalty owed by the guilty. The [[crucifixion]] becomes not an exemplary death but a _payment_, and the entire religion is organized around the management of this cosmic accounting.

## Healthy Sacrifice: The Pagan Model

In §16, Nietzsche sketches the pre-Christian logic of sacrifice as part of a healthy religious ecology:

> A nation that still believes in itself holds fast to its own god. In him it does honour to the conditions which enable it to survive, to its virtues — it projects its joy in itself, its feeling of power, into a being to whom one may offer thanks. He who is rich will give of his riches; a proud people need a god to whom they can make _sacrifices_.... Religion, within these limits, is a form of gratitude. (016-001)

Sacrifice here is not propitiation but _expression_ — the outward sign of an inward abundance. The god is not a creditor but a recipient of the people's overflowing strength. This is sacrifice as festival, as the ritualized acknowledgment that life is good and powerful enough to _give away_ some of itself.

## The Abolition of Atonement in Jesus' Teaching

Nietzsche argues in §33 that Jesus himself rejected the entire Jewish framework of sacrifice and atonement:

> He no longer needed any formula or ritual in his relations with God — not even prayer. He had rejected the whole of the Jewish doctrine of repentance and atonement; he _knew_ that it was only by a _way_ of life that one could feel one's self "divine," "blessed," "evangelical," a "child of God." _Not_ by "repentance," _not_ by "prayer and forgiveness" is the way to God: _only the Gospel way_ leads to God — it is _itself_ "God!" (033-001)

The "glad tidings" abolish the logic of sacrifice entirely:

> What the Gospels _abolished_ was the Judaism in the concepts of "sin," "forgiveness of sin," "faith," "salvation through faith" — the whole _ecclesiastical_ dogma of the Jews was denied by the "glad tidings." (033-001)

| Jewish/Christian Framework | Gospel (Jesus') Framework |
|---------------------------|--------------------------|
| Sin as debt requiring payment | No sin-concept; only ways of living |
| Atonement through sacrifice | Transformation through way of life |
| Forgiveness as transaction | Unnecessary — nothing to forgive |
| Faith as belief in atonement | Faith as lived experience |

The paradox is that Christianity — ostensibly built upon Jesus — rebuilt precisely the sacrificial system he had abolished, centering it on his own death reinterpreted as a cosmic atonement.

## The Cross as Counter-Sacrifice

The crucifixion, in Nietzsche's reading of Jesus, was not a sacrifice _for_ anything. It was the consistent conclusion of a way of life that refused resistance:

> The life of the Saviour was simply a carrying out of this way of life — and so was his death. (033-001)

Jesus did not die _in order to_ accomplish something; he died _because_ his way of living made no provision for self-defense. The interpretation of his death as an atoning sacrifice — the "lamb of God who takes away the [[sin]] of the world" — is the first and most consequential act of theological distortion:

> The deep instinct which prompts the Christian how to _live_ so that he will feel that he is "in heaven" and is "immortal," despite many reasons for feeling that he is _not_ "in heaven": this is the only psychological reality in "salvation." — A new way of life, _not_ a new faith. (033-001)

## Paul's Theology of Blood

It was [[paul|Paul]] who most thoroughly reconstructed the sacrificial system around the figure of Jesus. The doctrine of substitutionary atonement — Christ dying _for_ our sins, his blood as the price of [[redemption]] — reinstates, at a cosmic scale, the very logic of debt and payment that Jesus' life had dissolved.

Nietzsche's critique is implicit but devastating: Paul takes Jesus' refusal of the sacrificial economy and makes it _itself_ the ultimate sacrifice. The anti-sacrificial death becomes the sacrifice to end all sacrifices — a theological sleight-of-hand that preserves the form of the old religion while claiming to transcend it.

## The Priest as Manager of the Sacrificial Economy

The [[priest|priestly type]] has a structural interest in maintaining the sacrificial framework. If "salvation" is a way of life accessible to all, the priest has no function. If salvation requires a sacrifice — and access to that sacrifice requires authorized mediation — the priest becomes indispensable. The entire apparatus of sacraments, penance, and sacerdotal authority can be read as the institutional management of a sacrificial economy that Jesus had rendered obsolete.

## Sacrifice and the Revaluation of Values

Nietzsche's critique of Christian sacrifice ultimately points toward his broader project of the revaluation of all values. The Christian sacrificial logic rests on a specific valuation: that the guilty can be made innocent through the suffering of another, that debt can be transferred, that blood has metaphysical purchasing power. Nietzsche's rejection of this logic is not merely theological but _physiological_ — it is the rejection of a mode of thinking that belongs to a declining [[life]]-form, one that has lost the capacity for [[freedom|self-affirmation]] and can only relate to the divine through the medium of guilt and payment.

> Religion, within these limits, is a form of gratitude. A man is grateful for his own existence: to that end he needs a god. (016-001)

To recover sacrifice as gratitude rather than debt is to recover a relationship to life that does not begin from insufficiency.
