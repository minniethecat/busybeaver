# Friedrich Nietzsche

**Friedrich Wilhelm Nietzsche** (October 15, 1844 – August 25, 1900) was a German philosopher, cultural critic, composer, poet, and philologist. His work has exerted a profound influence on modern intellectual history. *The Antichrist* (German: *Der Antichrist*), written in 1888, was one of his final works and represents a concentrated statement of his critique of Christianity and organized religion.

## Life and Career

Nietzsche was born in Röcken, Prussia (now part of Germany), the son of a Lutheran pastor. Both his grandfathers were also Lutheran ministers. He showed early academic promise and was appointed to a chair in classical philology at the University of Basel at the unusually young age of 24.

His early work, *The Birth of Tragedy* (1872), already contained ideas that would later develop into his full critique of Christianity. As H.L. Mencken observes in the Introduction to *The Antichrist*, "You will find some of them, clearly recognizable, in the first book he ever wrote" (P01-007).

## Major Works

Nietzsche's philosophical output accelerated dramatically in the 1880s. After *Thus Spake Zarathustra* (1883–1885), he produced a rapid succession of works:

- *Beyond Good and Evil* (1886)
- *The Genealogy of Morals* (1887) — written in twenty days
- *The Case of Wagner* (1888)
- *The Twilight of the Idols* (1888)
- *The Antichrist* (1888) — completed in September 1888
- *Ecce Homo* (1888) — his autobiographical work

The Introduction notes that "The Antichrist is the last thing that Nietzsche ever wrote" aside from *Ecce Homo*, and "so it may be accepted as a statement of some of his most salient ideas in their final form" (P01-001).

## The Will to Power Project

*The Antichrist* was originally conceived as the first volume of Nietzsche's projected *magnum opus*, *The Will to Power* (*Der Wille zur Macht*). The original four-volume plan was:

1. **The Antichrist**: An Attempt at a Criticism of Christianity
2. **The Free Spirit**: A Criticism of Philosophy as a Nihilistic Movement
3. **The Immoralist**: A Criticism of Morality, the Most Fatal Form of Ignorance
4. **Dionysus**: The Philosophy of Eternal Recurrence

Nietzsche began making notes for this project in 1884, working on it during his travels across Europe — at Nice, Venice, Sils-Maria, Cannobio, Zürich, Genoa, Chur, and Leipzig (P01-003). He revised his plans several times, at one point expanding it to ten volumes, before settling on the four-volume structure. In September 1888 he began work on the first volume, completing it before the end of the month. His health collapsed in December 1888, and he wrote no more thereafter.

## Philosophical Position

Nietzsche's philosophy represents a comprehensive attack on Christianity in all its forms — as a system of practical ethics, as a political code, as metaphysics, and as a gauge of truth. The Introduction summarizes his intellectual project:

> The will to power was his answer to Christianity's affectation of humility and self-sacrifice; eternal recurrence was his mocking criticism of Christian optimism and millennialism; the superman was his candidate for the place of the Christian ideal of the "good" man, prudently abased before the throne of God. (P01-008)

Mencken characterizes Nietzsche as "a Greek born two thousand years too late," whose "dreams were thoroughly Hellenic; his whole manner of thinking was Hellenic" (P01-008). His true intellectual forefather, Mencken suggests, was not Plato but Heraclitus, in whom "one finds the germ of his primary view of the universe — a view, to wit, that sees it, not as moral phenomenon, but as mere aesthetic representation" (P01-008).

## Nietzsche on Christianity

Nietzsche viewed himself as the logical culmination of his Christian heritage turned against itself. He wrote: "In me the Christianity of my forbears reaches its logical conclusion. In me the stern intellectual conscience that Christianity fosters and makes paramount turns *against* Christianity. In me Christianity ... devours itself" (P01-006).

His critique was not merely theological but physiological. For Nietzsche, Christianity represented a systematic denial of the [[will-to-power|Will to Power]], promoting values of humility, pity, and self-sacrifice that he saw as symptoms of [[Décadence]] — a physiological and cultural decline. The weak, he argued, had inverted the natural order of values, calling their own powerlessness "goodness" and the strength of the masterful "evil."

## Later Influence

Despite — or perhaps because of — the controversy his ideas provoked, Nietzsche's influence grew steadily after his death. During World War I, he was simultaneously claimed and denounced by opposing sides, often on the basis of profound misunderstandings of his work. His critique of democracy, Christianity, and traditional morality has continued to provoke intense debate, while his stylistic innovations — compressed, aphoristic, free of academic verbosity — have influenced writers and thinkers across disciplines.
