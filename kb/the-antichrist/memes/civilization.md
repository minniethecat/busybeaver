# Civilization (Zivilisation / Kultur)

Nietzsche's [[Antichrist]] draws a sharp distinction between **civilization** (*Zivilisation*) as a levelling, decadent force and **higher culture** (*Kultur*) as the hierarchical flourishing of noble types. Christianity, in Nietzsche's analysis, is not merely anti-civilization — it is the systematic **vampire of civilization**, draining every great cultural achievement of its life-blood. From the Roman Empire to the Moorish civilization of Spain to the Renaissance, Nietzsche traces a consistent pattern: Christian values destroy whatever is noble, healthy, and life-affirming in human culture.

## Definition and Mechanism

Nietzsche operates with two distinct concepts that English renders as "civilization":

**Zivilisation** — the process of taming, pacifying, and levelling; making men "civilized" by weakening them. Christianity's method: "to make them *ill* — to make feeble is the Christian recipe for taming, for '*civilizing*'" (022-001).

**Kultur** — higher culture, the flourishing of art, science, philosophy, and noble institutions. This requires hierarchy, the *pathos of distance*, and the health of the superior types. Culture is a **pyramid**: "it can stand only on a broad base; its primary prerequisite is a strong and soundly consolidated mediocrity" (057-001).

The mechanism of civilization-destruction operates through Christianity's inversion of values: the elevation of the weak, the sick, the mediocre — what Nietzsche calls the "Chandala" morality — and the corresponding denigration of strength, health, and excellence.

## Christianity as Vampire of Civilization

Nietzsche's most sustained analysis of Christianity's civilizational impact appears in §58–61, where he anatomizes the destruction of the Roman Empire:

> Christianity was the vampire of the *imperium Romanum*, — overnight it destroyed the vast achievement of the Romans: the conquest of the soil for a great culture *that could await its time*. (058-001)

The Roman achievement was not merely political but **cultural** in the highest sense: "this most admirable of all works of art in the grand manner" embodied instinctive nobility, methodical inquiry, genius for organization, and "a great yes to everything entering into the *imperium Romanum*" (059-001). Christianity destroyed it not by force but by infection — "stealthy worms, which under the cover of night, mist and duplicity, crept upon every individual, sucking him dry of all earnest interest in *real* things" (058-001).

## Two Civilizations Destroyed

Nietzsche identifies three great losses:

| Civilization | Destroyer | Method | Nietzsche's Assessment |
|-------------|-----------|--------|----------------------|
| Greco-Roman antiquity | Early Christianity | Vampiric infiltration, value inversion | "The whole labour of the ancient world gone for *naught*" (059-001) |
| Moorish Spain | Crusaders / Church | Military conquest ("trampled down") | "A civilization beside which even that of our nineteenth century seems very poor" (060-001) |
| The Renaissance | Luther / German Reformation | Restoration of the church | "An event without meaning, a great futility" (061-001) |

> The wonderful culture of the Moors in Spain, which was fundamentally nearer to *us* and appealed more to our senses and tastes than that of Rome and Greece, was *trampled down*... Because it had to thank noble and manly instincts for its origin — because it said yes to life, even to the rare and refined luxuriousness of Moorish life! (060-001)

## The Pyramid of Higher Culture

In §57, Nietzsche presents his normative vision of civilization as a **three-tier physiological order**:

| Caste | Type | Function | Privilege |
|-------|------|----------|-----------|
| The Fewest (*die Wenigsten*) | Intellectual-spiritual | Value-creation, ruling, knowledge | Happiness, beauty, goodness |
| The Guardians | Muscular-temperamental | Execution of rule, law, security, warfare | Honor, action, loyalty |
| The Mediocre | Neither distinguished | Occupational activity, specialization | Contentment, mastery of one thing |

> A high civilization is a pyramid: it can stand only on a broad base; its primary prerequisite is a strong and soundly consolidated mediocrity. (057-001)

This is not arbitrary but an "order of nature, of a natural law of the first rank" (057-001). The inequality of rights is "essential to the existence of any rights at all" — a direct inversion of the Christian doctrine of equal souls.

## The Jewish Falsification of Natural Values

Nietzsche traces civilization's corruption to its Jewish origins. When confronted with the existential choice "to be or not to be," the Jews chose survival at any price — and the price was "a radical *falsification* of all nature, of all naturalness, of all reality":

> One by one they distorted religion, civilization, morality, history and psychology until each became a *contradiction* of its *natural significance*. (024-001)

Christianity amplified this falsification "incalculably," making the Jews "the most *fateful* people in the history of the world" (024-001). The irony, Nietzsche notes, is that the Christian anti-Semite does not recognize that anti-Semitism is "no more than the *final consequence of Judaism*."

## The German Question

Nietzsche reserves particular venom for the Germans' role in civilizational destruction:

> The German nobility stands *outside* the history of the higher civilization: the reason is obvious... Christianity, alcohol — the two *great* means of corruption. (060-001)

The Germans "have on their conscience the uncleanest variety of Christianity that exists, and the most incurable and indestructible — Protestantism" (061-001). It was a German monk, [[luther|Luther]], who destroyed the Renaissance — Nietzsche's great hope for a transvaluation of Christian values under a Pope Cesare Borgia.

## Civilization and the *Pathos of Distance*

The destruction of civilization is ultimately the destruction of **distance** — the felt hierarchy between higher and lower types. Christianity's poisonous doctrine of "*equal* rights for all" wages "a deadly war upon all feelings of reverence and distance between man and man, which is to say, upon the first *prerequisite* to every step upward, to every development of civilization" (043-001). Without this *pathos of distance*, higher culture cannot emerge.

> Nowadays no one has courage any more for special rights, for the right of dominion, for feelings of honourable pride in himself and his equals — for the *pathos of distance*... Our politics is sick with this lack of courage! (043-001)
