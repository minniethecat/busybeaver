**Evolution**, in Nietzsche's philosophical engagement with nineteenth-century biology, is both a point of affinity and a target of critique. While Nietzsche shares with Darwin a naturalistic, anti-teleological framework, he rejects the core Darwinian mechanism — the "survival of the fittest" — as inconsistent with observable reality. In its place, Nietzsche proposes an evolutionary model driven not by adaptation but by [[life|will to power]], the internal pressure toward expansion, mastery, and self-overcoming.

## The Critique of Darwin

In §14, Nietzsche directly challenges the Darwinian picture:

> It is _not_ true that the strong survive and the weak perish. On the contrary, the weak — the "botched," the "failures," the "degenerate" — have, under Christianity, survived and proliferated at the expense of the strong. (014-001, paraphrase)

Nietzsche's objection is not biological but _historical_. The triumph of Christianity demonstrates that the weak can defeat the strong through cultural-moral mechanisms — through the invention of values that make strength into "evil" and weakness into "good."

In §7, he links this to the type of "modernity" he diagnoses:

> The whole of mankind, even the best, allow themselves to be deceived by words. (007-001)

The "words" in question are moral concepts — "good," "evil," "pity," "humility" — that have been wielded by the weak as instruments of evolutionary reversal.

## The Strong as the Vulnerable

Nietzsche inverts the Darwinian assumption. It is not the weak who are threatened with extinction, but the strong:

> In every healthy society there are three physiological types, gravitating toward differentiation but mutually conditioning one another... (057-001)

The [[nobility|noble type]] — the "fewest," the most valuable — is also the most fragile. It requires specific conditions to flourish: hierarchy, the pathos of distance, the freedom to create values. When those conditions are destroyed — as Christianity destroyed them — the noble type is the first to perish.

The mediocre type, by contrast, is resilient precisely because it does not aspire to greatness:

> To the mediocre mediocrity is a form of happiness; they have a natural instinct for mastering one thing, for specialization. It would be altogether unworthy of a profound intellect to see anything objectionable in mediocrity in itself. It is, in fact, the _first_ prerequisite to the appearance of the exceptional... (057-001)

## Christianity as Counter-Evolutionary Force

In §51, Nietzsche describes Christianity as a systematic reversal of natural selection:

> Christianity has the rancour of the sick at its very core — the instinct against the _healthy_, against _health_. Everything that is well-constituted, proud, gallant and, above all, beautiful gives offence to its ears and eyes. (051-001)

The Christian formula, as articulated by [[paul|Paul]], is precisely an anti-evolutionary manifesto:

> "And God hath chosen the _weak_ things of the world, the _foolish_ things of the world, the _base_ things of the world, and things which are _despised_": _this_ was the formula; _in hoc signo_ the _décadence_ triumphed. — _God on the cross_ — is man always to miss the frightful inner significance of this symbol? — Everything that suffers, everything that hangs on the cross, is _divine_.... (051-001)

Christianity is an evolutionary catastrophe because it systematically preserves and elevates the types that natural selection would eliminate:

| Darwinian Selection | Christian Inversion |
|--------------------|--------------------|
| Strong survive | Strong are "evil," persecuted |
| Weak perish | Weak are "blessed," elevated |
| Fitness = reproductive success | Fitness = faith, obedience |
| Nature selects for power | Church selects for submission |

The result, Nietzsche argues, is the long-term _degeneration_ of the human type — not biological devolution in a strict sense, but the loss of those qualities (courage, pride, intellectual honesty, self-reverence) that made the highest human achievements possible.

## Cultural Evolution and the Will to Power

Nietzsche does not reject evolution as such, but replaces natural selection with a different motor: the **will to power**. Organisms — and cultures — are not primarily driven to survive and reproduce, but to _expand_, to _dominate_, to _overcome_:

> Wherever I found a living thing, I found will to power. (quoted from _Thus Spake Zarathustra_)

Evolution, in Nietzsche's sense, is not adaptation to an environment but the _imposition_ of form upon an environment. The strong do not adapt — they conquer. The weak adapt — and in adapting, they degenerate.

## The Renaissance: A Lost Evolutionary Opportunity

In §61, Nietzsche presents the Renaissance as the great "what if" of European evolution. The Renaissance was an attempt to re-establish noble values — "the transvaluation of Christian values, — an attempt with all available means, all instincts and all the resources of genius to bring about a triumph of the _opposite_ values, the more _noble_ values" (061-001).

But it failed. Luther, "a German monk... with all the vengeful instincts of an unsuccessful priest in him, raised a rebellion _against_ the Renaissance" (061-001). Christianity, having been nearly overthrown at its capital, was restored. The evolutionary path toward a nobler humanity was blocked.
