The **order of rank** (_Rangordnung_) is one of Nietzsche's most essential and most misunderstood concepts. It is not a political program for restoring aristocracy, nor is it a racial hierarchy. It is the recognition that values themselves exist in a natural hierarchy — that some values enhance life while others diminish it — and that a healthy civilization is one that acknowledges and institutionalizes this hierarchy rather than leveling it in the name of equality.

## The Supreme Law of Life

Nietzsche elevates rank-ordering to a metaphysical principle: it is "the supreme law of life itself" (057-001). This is not an arbitrary preference but a descriptive claim about how life operates. Every living system — biological, psychological, social — organizes itself hierarchically. The attempt to abolish hierarchy is the attempt to abolish life:

> The order of castes, the supreme, the dominating law, is only the sanction of a natural order, of a natural lawfulness of the first rank — over this, no arbitrary will, no "modern idea" has any power. (057-001)

The "modern idea" that all humans are equal is not merely false but _harmful_ — it contradicts the natural order and, in doing so, damages the conditions under which higher types can emerge.

## The Three Physiological Types

In §57, Nietzsche outlines a tripartite natural order grounded in physiology rather than birth:

> In every healthy society there are three physiological types, gravitating toward differentiation but mutually conditioning one another, and each of these has its own hygiene, its own sphere of work, its own special mastery and feeling of perfection. (057-001)

| Type | Characteristic | Function | Hygiene |
|------|---------------|----------|---------|
| The Fewest (intellectual) | Most spiritual, self-mastery, ascetic instinct | Creating values, ruling | Solitude, selective sympathy |
| The Guardians (muscular) | Warriors, executors, noble strength | Enforcement, protection | Obedience, honor |
| The Mediocre (majority) | Specialization, mechanical aptitude, contentment | Handicrafts, commerce, agriculture | Modest contentment |

Each type has "its own hygiene" — its own conditions of health. The error of Christianity (and modernity) is to impose a single morality on all three types, which makes genuine health impossible for any of them. The "highest" type requires different conditions than the "mediocre" — what is virtue for one may be poison for another.

## Christianity as Leveler

Christianity, in Nietzsche's genealogy, is the great anti-hierarchical force in Western history. It begins among the lowest orders — "the underworld of the ancient world" (022-001) — and proceeds to invert all natural hierarchies:

> Christianity has taken the part of all the weak, the low, the botched; it has made an ideal out of antagonism to all the self-preservative instincts of sound life. (005-001)

The Christian "transvaluation of values" is precisely the destruction of rank-ordering. What was low (humility, pity, submission) becomes high; what was high (pride, self-affirmation, power) becomes sin. This is not a correction of unjust hierarchy but the abolition of hierarchy as such:

> The majority became master; democracy, with its Christian instincts, triumphed. (051-001)

## Order and the Holy Lie

One of Nietzsche's most provocative claims is that every stable civilization has relied on a "holy lie" to maintain its order of rank. The Law of Manu, the priestly codes of Egypt, the Platonic noble lie — all are "means to the end of making the concept of 'order of rank' sacred" (057-001). The content of the lie varies, but the function is constant: to render hierarchy invisible to those at the bottom by wrapping it in divine authority.

The Christian innovation is not the holy lie as such but its particular content: the lie of equality before God, which destroys the natural order of rank rather than sanctifying it. Nietzsche calls this "the most fatal and seductive lie that ever existed" (062-001).

## The Order of Values

Rank-ordering applies not only to persons but to values themselves. The "transvaluation of all values" that Nietzsche calls for is not the replacement of one set of values with another but the re-establishment of _value hierarchy_:

> What is good? — All that heightens the feeling of power, the will to power, power itself in man. What is bad? — All that proceeds from weakness. (002-001)

The order of values places life-affirmation above life-denial, strength above weakness, becoming above being. Christian morality, by contrast, places the weak above the strong, the afterlife above this life, pity above self-affirmation. Nietzsche's project is not to invert Christian values (replacing one absolute hierarchy with another) but to restore the natural order that Christianity inverted.

## Order and Culture

A culture's health, for Nietzsche, is measured by the distance between its highest and lowest types — not by the average welfare of its members:

> Every elevation of the type "man" has hitherto been the work of an aristocratic society — and so will it always be: a society which believes in a long scale of rank-ordering and differences of worth between man and man. (057-001)

The modern democratic movement, with its "Christian instincts," seeks to narrow this distance — to level the peaks and fill in the valleys. The result is not universal flourishing but universal mediocrity: a civilization incapable of producing great individuals because it has abolished the conditions under which greatness can emerge.
