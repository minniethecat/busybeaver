**Theology** is, in Nietzsche's vocabulary, not an academic discipline but a _pathology of the intellect_ — the systematic organization of falsehood in service of the [[priest|priestly]] will to power. When Nietzsche declares theologians his "antagonists" in the opening of _The Antichrist_, he is naming a cognitive and instinctual orientation that extends far beyond the clergy: theological thinking is a _habit of mind_ that infects philosophy, morality, and politics.

## The Theological Instinct

Nietzsche identifies the theological instinct as the most pervasive form of intellectual dishonesty in Western civilization:

> Upon this theological instinct I make war: I find the tracks of it everywhere. Whoever has theological blood in his veins is shifty and dishonourable in all things. The pathetic thing that grows out of this condition is called _faith_: in other words, closing one's eyes upon one's self once for all, to avoid suffering the sight of incurable falsehood. (009-001)

The theological instinct is defined by its relationship to [[truth]]: it is not merely indifferent to truth but actively hostile to it. The theologian's "profound instinct of self-preservation stands against truth ever coming into honour in any way, or even getting stated" (009-001). This is not a failure of cognition — it is a _strategy_.

## The Criterion of Truth Inverted

Nietzsche proposes a devastating heuristic:

> Whatever a theologian regards as true _must_ be false: there you have almost a criterion of truth. (009-001)

This is not mere polemic. Nietzsche's reasoning is structural: the theologian's instincts consistently favour whatever is "most damaging to life." When theological influence is felt, "the concepts 'true' and 'false' are forced to change places: whatever is most damaging to life is there called 'true,' and whatever exalts it, intensifies it, approves it, justifies it and makes it triumphant is there called 'false'" (009-001).

| Life-Affirming | Called "False" by Theology |
|----------------|---------------------------|
| Strength | Pride (superbia) |
| Sensuality | Lust (luxuria) |
| Intellect | Impious curiosity |
| Self-assertion | Selfishness |

| Life-Denying | Called "True" by Theology |
|-------------|---------------------------|
| Self-abnegation | Humility |
| Instinct-suppression | Chastity |
| Will-lessness | Obedience |
| World-rejection | Piety |

## Theology as the Ruin of Philosophy

Nietzsche traces the corruption of German philosophy directly to its theological roots:

> Among Germans I am immediately understood when I say that theological blood is the ruin of philosophy. The Protestant pastor is the grandfather of German philosophy; Protestantism itself is its _peccatum originale_. (010-001)

The Kantian system — often celebrated as philosophy's great achievement — is, for Nietzsche, merely "a very artful form of theology" (010-001). Kant's categorical imperative is a secularized Christian commandment; his noumenal world is the theological "beyond" dressed in philosophical language:

> The theological instinct alone took it under protection! — An action prompted by the life-instinct proves that it is a _right_ action by the amount of pleasure that goes with it: and yet that Nihilist, with his bowels of Christian dogmatism, regarded pleasure as an _objection_. (011-001)

## The Theologian's Unfitness for Truth

In perhaps his most sustained analysis of the theological mind, Nietzsche diagnoses a specific cognitive incapacity:

> Another characteristic of the theologian is his _unfitness for philology_. What I here mean by philology is, in a general sense, the art of reading with profit — the capacity for absorbing facts _without_ interpreting them falsely, and _without_ losing caution, patience and subtlety in the effort to understand them. (052-001)

The theologian cannot read — cannot encounter a text, a fact, or a phenomenon without immediately subordinating it to a predetermined interpretive framework. Everything must be made to serve the theological narrative. This is the antithesis of what Nietzsche calls "intellectual conscience" — the discipline of seeing what is actually there.

## Theology and Nihilism

The theological will is, at its deepest level, a _nihilistic will_:

> When theologians, working through the "consciences" of princes (or of peoples —), stretch out their hands for _power_, there is never any doubt as to the fundamental issue: the will to make an end, the _nihilistic_ will exerts that power. (009-001)

Theology serves life only by denying it. Its God is a God of the sick, the weak, the resentful — a "God of the 'great majority,' this democrat among gods" who "remains a god in a corner, a god of all the dark nooks and crevices" (017-001). Theology is the intellectual justification for this diminishment — the "science" that makes the reduction of the divine to a "thing-in-itself" seem like intellectual progress.
