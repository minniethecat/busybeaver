Science (_Wissenschaft_), in Nietzsche's [[will-to-power|The Antichrist]], is far more than a method of inquiry — it is the existential antagonist of the [[christianity|Christian-priestly]] world-order. Where [[faith]] closes the eyes, science opens them. Where the priest invents imaginary causes ("God," "sin," "soul"), science tracks real ones. Nietzsche's analysis in The Antichrist traces Christianity's hostility to science not as an incidental feature but as its _constitutive principle_: the priest _knows_ that science is his mortal enemy and has organized the entire edifice of Christian doctrine to destroy it.

## Science as the Priest's "One Great Danger"

Nietzsche reads the opening chapters of Genesis as a "priest-book _par excellence_," encoding in mythological form the fundamental political problem of the priestly type: knowledge is power, and the man who knows no longer needs a priest.

> Has any one ever clearly understood the celebrated story at the beginning of the Bible—of God's mortal terror of _science_?... No one, in fact, has understood it. This priest-book _par excellence_ opens, as is fitting, with the great inner difficulty of the priest: _he_ faces only one great danger; _ergo_, "God" faces only one great danger. (048-001)

The entire narrative of the Fall is redescribed as an allegory of the priest's war on knowledge. God creates man — and immediately realizes his mistake. Man learns, man thinks, man becomes scientific — and "it is all up with priests and gods when man becomes scientific!"

> _Moral_: science is the forbidden _per se_; it alone is forbidden. Science is the _first_ of sins, the germ of all sins, the _original_ sin. _This is all there is of morality._ —"Thou shall _not_ know":—the rest follows from that. (048-002)

From this primal prohibition, Nietzsche traces the cascade of priestly inventions designed to disable human thought:

| Priestly Invention | Function |
|-------------------|----------|
| **Paradise lost** | Happiness and leisure foster thought → banish man from paradise |
| **Distress and death** | Man must not have time to think |
| **Sickness** | Man weakened by illness cannot pursue knowledge |
| **War** | Man destroying man as "a great disturber of science" |
| **Sin** | The ultimate weapon — destroy man's sense of cause and effect |

## The Invention of "Sin" as an Attack on Causality

Nietzsche's most penetrating insight in this analysis is that "sin" was invented specifically to sever man's relationship with causality — the foundation of all scientific thinking.

> The concept of guilt and punishment, the whole "moral order of the world," was set up _against_ science— _against_ the deliverance of man from priests.... Man must _not_ look outward; he must look inward. He must _not_ look at things shrewdly and cautiously, to learn about them; he must not look at all; he must _suffer_.... And he must suffer so much that he is always in need of the priest.—Away with physicians! _What is needed is a Saviour._ (049-001)

The logic is devastating: by reinterpreting all natural consequences as _moral_ consequences, as rewards and punishments from a supernatural agent, the priest destroys "the whole ground-work of knowledge." When a man no longer understands that fire burns because of natural causality — when he believes it burns because God punishes — he has lost the capacity for science. And the priest _rules_ through this loss.

> When the natural consequences of an act are no longer "natural," but are regarded as produced by the ghostly creations of superstition—by "God," by "spirits," by "souls"—and reckoned as merely "moral" consequences, as rewards, as punishments, as hints, as lessons, then the whole ground-work of knowledge is destroyed— _then the greatest of crimes against humanity has been perpetrated_. (049-001)

## Faith as the Veto on Science

Nietzsche identifies "faith" as the specific mechanism through which Christianity enforces its hostility to science. Faith is not a parallel mode of knowing — it is an _imperative_ against knowing.

> "Faith," as an imperative, vetoes science— _in praxi_, lying at any price.... Paul _well knew_ that lying—that "faith"—was necessary; later on the church borrowed the fact from Paul. (047-001)

The connection between faith and the prohibition on science is intrinsic, not accidental. A religion "which does not touch reality at a single point and which goes to pieces the moment reality asserts its rights at any point" must be "the deadly enemy of the 'wisdom of this world,' which is to say, of _science_."

## Philology and Medicine: The Two Enemies of Superstition

Nietzsche identifies two specific disciplines as the paradigmatic enemies of the Christian-priestly worldview: philology and medicine. These are not arbitrary choices — they represent the two fronts on which science dismantles religion.

> As a matter of fact no man can be a _philologian_ or a physician without being also _Antichrist_. That is to say, as a philologian a man sees _behind_ the "holy books," and as a physician he sees _behind_ the physiological degeneration of the typical Christian. The physician says "incurable"; the philologian says "fraud." (047-001)

The philologian reads scripture as a historical document, revealing its human authorship and textual corruption. The physician reads the Christian body as a clinical case, diagnosing the physiological basis of religious experience. Together they dissolve the two pillars of priestly authority: the sacred text and the "soul."

## Science as Deliverance

Despite the priest's millennia-long war on knowledge, Nietzsche presents science as an irreversible force — knowledge that "prospers in spite of war," that "towers aloft, invading heaven, shadowing the gods." The final resolution of the Genesis allegory is God's despair: "Man has become scientific — _there is no help for it: he must be drowned!_"

But Nietzsche's point is that man was _not_ drowned. Science survived the priest's war. The task of the [[free-spirit|free spirit]], in Nietzsche's vision, is to complete this deliverance — to liberate knowledge from the last vestiges of theological instinct, to restore to man the "sound comprehension of cause and effect" that the priest spent two millennia trying to destroy.
