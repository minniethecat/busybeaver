**Philosophy** (_die Philosophie_) for Nietzsche is a discipline in crisis — corrupted by its historical descent from the priestly type and by its subservience to moral imperatives that it refuses to examine. Genuine philosophy, as Nietzsche conceives it, is not the construction of metaphysical systems or the pursuit of an abstract "truth" but a diagnostic practice: the critical examination of values, the genealogy of concepts, and ultimately the _creation_ of new values. The philosopher must become a physician of culture rather than its apologist. "Philosophizing with a hammer" — Nietzsche's self-description — means striking concepts to hear whether they ring true or hollow.

## The Priestly Inheritance

Nietzsche's most fundamental criticism of philosophy is genealogical: the philosopher is historically descended from the priest, and philosophy has never fully freed itself from this inheritance:

> When one recalls the fact that, among all peoples, the philosopher is no more than a development from the old type of priest, this inheritance from the priest, this fraud upon self, ceases to be remarkable. (012-001)

The philosopher inherits from the priest:
- The claim to determine what is "true" and "not true"
- The belief in a "true world" behind the world of appearance
- The subordination of intellectual inquiry to moral imperatives
- The posture of unworldly authority

This inheritance means that philosophy, even when it presents itself as radical or skeptical, often functions as disguised theology. The "will to truth" that philosophers pride themselves on is, in Nietzsche's analysis, the secularized form of the Christian will to a "true world" — a world of being, stability, and moral order beyond the world of becoming, flux, and value-ambiguity:

> The "true world" and the "world of appearance" — in plain English: the fabricated world and reality... (048-001)

## Kant as Case Study

Kant is Nietzsche's exemplary case of the philosopher as crypto-priest. Kant's critical project appeared to limit reason to make room for faith, but Nietzsche sees it as the exact reverse: Kant limited reason to make room for _morality_, and morality was always the priest's tool:

> Kant tried to give a scientific flavour to this form of corruption, this dearth of intellectual conscience, by calling it "practical reason." He deliberately invented a variety of reasons for use on occasions when it was desirable not to trouble with reason — that is, when morality, when the sublime command "thou shalt," was heard. (012-001)

This is the essence of the priestly operation in philosophy: reason is deployed against itself, undermined at the point where it threatens to expose the non-rational foundations of moral belief. Nietzsche calls this "theological blood" in philosophy:

> Among Germans I am immediately understood when I say that theological blood is the ruin of philosophy. The Protestant pastor is the grandfather of German philosophy; Protestantism itself is its peccatum originale. (010-001)

## The Philosopher as Physician of Culture

Against the priestly model, Nietzsche proposes the philosopher as cultural physician — someone who diagnoses the health or sickness of a civilization by examining its dominant values:

> We Hyperboreans — we are philosophers... we are the doctors here, to be unmerciful here, to wield the knife here — all this is our business, all this is our sort of humanity. (007-001)

The philosopher-physician asks not "Is this belief true?" but "What does this belief do to the person who holds it? Does it enhance life or diminish it? Is it a symptom of ascending or declining vitality?" This is the "diagnostic" function of philosophy, and it requires a set of skills very different from traditional metaphysical reasoning:

| Traditional Philosopher | Nietzschean Philosopher |
|--------------------------|------------------------|
| Seeks timeless truth | Diagnoses historical values |
| Constructs systems | Practices genealogy |
| Serves morality | Questions morality |
| Defends convictions | Suspends convictions |
| Inherits priestly authority | Creates new values |

## The Philosopher as Value-Creator

Nietzsche's most radical redefinition of philosophy is that the philosopher must become a _creator of values_. This goes beyond critique — beyond exposing the errors of previous philosophers — and enters the domain of legislation:

> Genuine philosophers are commanders and legislators: they say, "thus it shall be!" They determine the Wherefore and the Whither of mankind. (011-001)

This is the point at which Nietzsche's conception of philosophy most clearly separates from the academic tradition. The genuine philosopher does not analyze existing values from a neutral standpoint — there is no neutral standpoint. The philosopher _posits_ values, creates new tables of evaluation, and in doing so shapes the future direction of culture.

This is not arbitrary or voluntaristic. The philosopher's value-creation is grounded in:
1. **Physiological insight** — understanding what serves and what harms life
2. **Historical sense** — the genealogical knowledge of how existing values arose
3. **Psychological acuity** — seeing through the self-deceptions of previous philosophers
4. **Artistic form-giving** — the capacity to shape concepts into compelling wholes

## Philosophy and Science

Nietzsche distinguishes philosophy from science while respecting the latter's methodological rigor. Science (_Wissenschaft_) provides the tools of inquiry — the "little, modest, cautious truths" that the free spirit accumulates — but it cannot answer the question of value:

> A scientific head stood on an ape's body, a refined exceptional understanding in a base soul... (047-001)

Science can tell us how things are; it cannot tell us what they are worth. That is the prerogative of philosophy. But philosophy must incorporate the scientific virtues — intellectual conscience, methodological skepticism, suspicion of conviction — if it is to avoid relapsing into priestly dogmatism:

> We free spirits — we are already a "transvaluation of all values"... The most valuable insights are arrived at last; but the most valuable insights are methods. (013-001)

## The Philosopher of the Future

Nietzsche's "philosophers of the future" are figures who combine the intellectual conscience of the scientist, the diagnostic skill of the physician, the form-giving power of the artist, and the value-creating authority of the legislator. They are "free spirits" — not in the sense of being unconstrained (they are highly disciplined) but in the sense of having liberated themselves from the inherited value-systems that constrain lesser minds:

> For the free spirit, the philosopher who must be a free spirit, is a man who thinks otherwise than is expected of him on the basis of his origin, his milieu, his class and profession, or on the basis of the ruling views of his time. (053-001)

This figure does not yet exist — Nietzsche presents himself as its herald and precursor. The genuine philosopher is a promise, not a reality; the history of philosophy so far has been, with a few exceptions, the history of the priest in academic dress.
