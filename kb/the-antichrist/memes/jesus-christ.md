Nietzsche's analysis of [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]] in [[decadence|The Antichrist]] is not a historical investigation but a psychological typology. Nietzsche is not interested in what Jesus "really" said or did — he is interested in _what kind of human being_ could have generated the figure depicted (however mutilated) in the Gospels. The answer he arrives at is radical: Jesus represents the ultimate physiological type of décadence, a being so estranged from reality that he inhabits a purely "inner" world where the [[christianity|Kingdom of God]] is not a future promise but an ever-present psychological state.

## The Psychological Type

For Nietzsche, the key to understanding Jesus lies not in heroism or genius — both concepts he explicitly rejects for this case — but in a specific physiological condition: a morbid hypersensitivity that recoils from all contact with reality.

> What the Gospels make instinctive is precisely the reverse of all heroic struggle, of all taste for conflict: the very incapacity for resistance is here converted into something moral: ("resist not evil!" — the most profound sentence in the Gospels, perhaps the true key to them), to wit, the blessedness of peace, of gentleness, the _inability_ to be an enemy. (029-001)

This incapacity for resistance — what the Gospels call "turning the other cheek" — is, for Nietzsche, a physiological fact before it is a moral teaching. It arises from a nervous system so sensitive that any contact with the external world registers as pain.

## The Symbolist

Nietzsche insists that Jesus was a "great symbolist" who understood all realities as _subjective_ realities only. The concepts of "Son of God," "Father," and "Kingdom of God" were, for Jesus, not theological propositions but psychological experiences.

> The concept of "the Son of God" does not connote a concrete person in history, an isolated and definite individual, but an "eternal" fact, a psychological symbol set free from the concept of time. (034-001)

The "Kingdom of Heaven" is not a place or a future event — it is "a state of the heart" (034-002), an experience of beatitude available at every moment. There is no doctrine of immortality of the soul in Jesus's teaching; death, for him, simply does not exist as a meaningful category.

## The One Christian

Nietzsche makes a startling claim: there was only one Christian, and he died on the cross. Everything that followed — the church, the theology, the [[paul|Pauline]] invention — was the negation of what Jesus lived.

> The very word "Christianity" is a misunderstanding — at bottom there was only one Christian, and he died on the cross. The "Gospels" _died_ on the cross. What, from that moment onward, was called the "Gospels" was the very reverse of what _he_ had lived: "bad tidings," a _Dysangelium_. (039-001)

This is the tragic irony at the heart of Nietzsche's reading: the historical Jesus was the antithesis of everything institutional Christianity became. The cross was not a sacrifice for sins — it was the demonstration of a way of life that refused all resistance, even to death.

## Jesus vs. Paul: The Dysangelium

| Aspect | Jesus (the Evangel) | Paul (the Dysangelist) |
|--------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Core message | Blessedness now, Kingdom within | Salvation through faith, judgment to come |
| Relation to death | Death is absent, unreal | Death on cross as sacrifice for sin |
| Psychology | Instinctive peace, incapacity for enmity | Hatred, will to power, priestly cunning |
| Concept of God | "Father" as felt transformation | God as judge, lawgiver, "reduced to absurdity" |
| Historical effect | A life, a practice | A theology, a church, a priesthood |

The entire structure of Christian theology — original sin, redemption through blood sacrifice, resurrection as historical event, judgment, afterlife — is, in Nietzsche's reading, Paul's invention. Jesus himself taught none of it.

## Legacy

For Nietzsche, Jesus remains a figure of profound psychological interest precisely because he represents a limit case of the human: a being whose total withdrawal from reality into pure interiority reveals both the possibility of a certain kind of "blessedness" and its fatal cost — the complete irrelevance of the external world, of history, of culture, of _life_ itself.
