**Nature** (_die Natur_, _Physis_) in Nietzsche's vocabulary is the immanent, physiological standard against which all valuations are to be measured. It is not "nature" in the Romantic sense — a benevolent, harmonious order — nor in the modern scientific sense — a domain of value-neutral facts. Nature is the totality of life-processes, the economy of instincts, the body's wisdom. What is "against nature" (_widernatürlich_) is what denies, suppresses, or pathologizes these processes. Christianity, in Nietzsche's diagnosis, is the most systematic _widernatürlich_ force in Western history.

## Nature as Evaluative Standard

Nietzsche's naturalism is not the reduction of values to facts but the insistence that valuations must be traced back to the physiological conditions that produce them. An instinct "at fault in everything" is a revolt against nature:

> Instinct at fault in everything and anything, instinct as a revolt against nature, German décadence as a philosophy — that is Kant! (011-001)

Kant's categorical imperative — the demand that one act according to a rule that could be universalized — represents for Nietzsche the complete inversion of natural valuation. Nature does not universalize; it differentiates. Each organism has its own conditions of flourishing. The attempt to impose a single moral law on all is itself "against nature."

The natural is that which affirms and enhances life. The anti-natural is that which negates and diminishes it:

> I call an animal, a species, an individual corrupt, when it loses its instincts, when it chooses, when it prefers, what is injurious to it. (006-001)

This is the core of Nietzsche's naturalistic ethics: corruption is not a theological category but a physiological one — the organism that has lost the instinct for what benefits it.

## Christianity as Anti-Nature

The most sustained polemic in _The Antichrist_ is against Christianity's systematic hostility to nature. Nietzsche charges Christianity with waging war on the very conditions of life:

> Christianity... refuses to rid itself of the superstition about the soul! that makes a "virtue" of insufficient nourishment!... that combats health as a sort of enemy, devil, temptation! that persuades itself that it is possible to carry about a "perfect soul" in a cadaver of a body. (051-001)

The Christian elevation of "soul" over body is not merely a metaphysical error — it is a strategic attack on nature itself. By positing an immaterial self that transcends the body, Christianity can declare war on every natural instinct while claiming to defend the person.

| Domain | Natural (Affirming Life) | Anti-Natural (Christian) |
|--------|-------------------------|------------------------|
| Body | Source of valuation, criterion of health | "Cadaver of a body" (051-001), obstacle to salvation |
| Instinct | Guide to beneficial action | "Revolt against nature" (011-001) |
| Pleasure | Proof of right action | "Objection" to action (011-001) |
| Health | Superabundance, overflowing | Enemy, devil, temptation (051-001) |
| Egoism | Duty, healthy self-interest | Sin, that which must be overcome |

## The Natural Order of Rank

In §57, Nietzsche presents nature — not God, not tradition, not the social contract — as the source of social hierarchy:

> It is not Manu but nature that sets off in one class those who are chiefly intellectual, in another those who are marked by muscular strength and temperament, and in a third those who are distinguished in neither one way or the other. (057-001)

This "natural order" of three physiological types — the intellectual few, the muscular guardians, and the mediocre majority — is presented not as a political prescription but as a _physiological fact_. Each type "has its own hygiene, its own sphere of work, its own special mastery and feeling of perfection" (057-001). Christianity's egalitarianism, by ignoring these natural differentiations, makes genuine health impossible for all types.

## Nature and the Noble

Nietzsche's concept of nobility is grounded in nature. The noble person lives according to natural instincts — pride, courage, self-affirmation — rather than according to the reactive, anti-natural morality of the weak. Noble morality is "nature's" morality; slave morality (Christianity) is morality against nature:

> Everything that is well-constituted, proud, gallant and, above all, beautiful gives offence to [Christianity's] ears and eyes. (051-001)

The Christian instinct is "the rancour of the sick at its very core — the instinct against the healthy, against health" (051-001). This is not merely a moral preference for weakness — it is a physiological revolt of the sick against the healthy, of the anti-natural against the natural.

## Jesus and Nature

Nietzsche's treatment of Jesus presents a complex relationship to the natural. Jesus's "incapacity for resistance" — his refusal to negate, to fight, to be an enemy — is simultaneously natural (a physiological given, not a moral choice) and anti-natural (a recoil from all reality, "a flight into the 'intangible,' into the 'incomprehensible'" (029-001)):

> He speaks only of inner things: "life" or "truth" or "light" is his word for the innermost — in his sight everything else, the whole of reality, all nature, even language, has significance only as sign, as allegory. (032-001)

Jesus's world is one in which "all nature, even language, has significance only as sign" — nature is not denied but rendered transparent, converted into pure symbolism. This is not the Christian war on nature but a pre-conceptual indifference to the natural world that Christianity later transforms into systematic hostility.

## Nature and Science

Nietzsche's naturalism is allied with science not as a body of doctrines but as a method — the patient, cautious, distrustful approach that characterizes the scientific spirit:

> All the methods, all the principles of the scientific spirit of today, were the targets for thousands of years of the most profound contempt. (013-001)

Science, properly understood, is the extension of natural instinct into the domain of knowledge — it is nature becoming conscious of its own methods. The war on science conducted by Christianity is thus a war of the anti-natural against nature's own self-consciousness.
