**Immortality** (personal immortality, the immortal soul) is, in Nietzsche's analysis of Christianity, not a genuine metaphysical truth but a psychologically motivated doctrine whose function is to devalue earthly life and consolidate priestly power. Within the framework of *The Antichrist*, immortality operates at three distinct levels: as a psychological state produced by the Gospel way of life, as a [[priest]]ly instrument of torture and domination, and as a strategic weapon wielded by [[paul|Paul]] to rob "the world" of its value. Nietzsche traces the transformation of immortality from Jesus's lived experience into the doctrinal weapon that wages war on [[nobility]], [[reason]], and [[life]] itself.

## The Psychological Origin: Immortality as a Way of Life

In Nietzsche's reconstruction of the original Gospel, immortality was not a doctrinal promise but a psychological reality produced by a specific mode of living. The early Christian, by practicing non-resistance, refusing judgment, and rejecting the concepts of guilt and punishment, experienced a state of being that felt like "being in heaven" and being "immortal." This was the only genuine "salvation" — not a belief, but a lived condition.

> "The deep instinct which prompts the Christian how to _live_ so that he will feel that he is 'in heaven' and is 'immortal,' despite many reasons for feeling that he is _not_ 'in heaven': this is the only psychological reality in 'salvation.'—A new way of life, _not_ a new faith...." (033-001)

Key to this original conception is that immortality was not "merely promised" nor "bound up with conditions" — it was conceived as the only reality, with everything else being mere signs pointing toward it. The Gospel way of life abolished the distance between God and man, making the practitioner feel divine and blessed through action, not through belief in an [[afterlife]].

## Immortality as Priestly Instrument of Torture

Nietzsche categorizes "the immortality of the soul" alongside "the other world," "the last judgment," and "the [[soul]]" itself as instruments of psychological cruelty. These concepts were not innocent theological errors — they were deliberately crafted systems of torture through which the [[priest]] established and maintained mastery over humanity.

> "We know, our _conscience_ now knows—just _what_ the real value of all those sinister inventions of priest and church has been and _what ends they have served_, with their debasement of humanity to a state of self-pollution, the very sight of which excites loathing,—the concepts 'the other world,' 'the last judgment,' 'the immortality of the soul,' the 'soul' itself: they are all merely so many instruments of torture, systems of cruelty, whereby the priest becomes master and remains master...." (038-001)

The function of the doctrine is structural: by inventing an eternal afterlife in which every action is judged, the priest creates a permanent system of psychological dependency. The believer's attention is diverted from earthly flourishing toward an imaginary beyond, while the priest — as the sole mediator of that beyond — consolidates unchallengeable authority. The doctrine is a lie, and Nietzsche insists that modern conscience knows it to be a lie, yet it persists because it serves the interests of power.

## Paul's Corruption: Personal Immortality as Doctrine

The decisive transformation of immortality from lived psychological state to doctrinal promise occurred through [[paul|Paul]]. In Nietzsche's genealogy, Jesus had abolished the concept of guilt and lived the unity of God and man — this was the "glad tidings." Paul, by contrast, introduced the sacrificial logic of Christ's death as atonement for sin, and with it the doctrine of resurrection and personal immortality as a reward.

> "And at once there sprang from the Gospels the most contemptible of all unfulfillable promises, the _shameless_ doctrine of personal immortality.... Paul even preached it as a _reward_...." (041-001)

This was not an innocent evolution but a deliberate corruption. The doctrine of personal immortality served to "juggle away" the entire concept of blessedness — which in the original Gospel was the whole and only reality — and substitute a state of existence after death. The promise is "unfulfillable" and "shameless" because it cannot be verified, yet it demands absolute allegiance. It turns life into a waiting room for a [[reward]] that never arrives.

## The War on Life and Noble Humanity

The most devastating consequence of the doctrine of personal immortality, in Nietzsche's analysis, is its systematic destruction of the value of earthly existence. By placing "the centre of gravity of life" not in life itself but in "the beyond" — which Nietzsche equates with nothingness — the doctrine robs life of its meaning, its instincts, and its future.

> "When the centre of gravity of life is placed, _not_ in life itself, but in 'the beyond'—in _nothingness_—then one has taken away its centre of gravity altogether. The vast lie of personal immortality destroys all reason, all natural instinct—henceforth, everything in the instincts that is beneficial, that fosters life and that safeguards the future is a cause of suspicion." (043-001)

Nietzsche enumerates the practical consequences: public-spiritedness becomes suspect, pride in ancestry becomes a "temptation," labor and trust and concern for the common welfare become "strayings from the straight path." The single-minded focus on personal salvation atomizes the community; every individual, convinced of their cosmic importance, becomes incapable of genuine collective action.

The doctrine also functions as a weapon of leveling. By granting "immortality" to every person regardless of merit, Christianity wages war on the very preconditions of civilization — reverence, distance, and hierarchy:

> "To allow 'immortality' to every Peter and Paul was the greatest, the most vicious outrage upon _noble_ humanity ever perpetrated." (043-001)

This "poisonous doctrine" of equal rights — which Nietzsche traces directly to the equality of immortal souls — undermines "all feelings of reverence and distance between man and man," which he identifies as "the first _prerequisite_ to every step upward, to every development of civilization." The doctrine thus serves as Christianity's chief weapon against everything "noble, joyous and high-spirited on earth."

## Paul's Strategic Calculus: Immortality as the Death of Life

In Section 58, Nietzsche reveals the strategic logic behind Paul's insistence on immortality. Paul understood — in what Nietzsche calls his "revelation at Damascus" — that the belief in immortality was the necessary instrument for devaluing the Roman world and mobilizing the Chandala (outcaste) resentment against it.

> "This was his revelation at Damascus: he grasped the fact that he _needed_ the belief in immortality in order to rob 'the world' of its value, that the concept of 'hell' would master Rome—that the notion of a 'beyond' is the _death of life_...." (058-001)

The equation is stark: "the notion of a 'beyond' is the death of life." Immortality is not merely a false belief — it is a weapon of cultural destruction. By promising eternal life elsewhere, Paul taught his followers to despise life here. This was the formula that enabled Christianity to act as "the vampire of the _imperium Romanum_," draining the Roman achievement of the earnest interest and instinct for reality that had made it possible.

Nietzsche contrasts Paul's Christianity with Epicurus, noting that "to deny immortality was already a form of genuine _salvation_" — a recovery of life's value and dignity against the subterranean cults of guilt, punishment, and the beyond.

### Nietzsche's Hierarchy of Immortality-Concepts

| Form | Origin | Function | Evaluation |
|------|--------|----------|------------|
| Psychological immortality (Gospel) | Jesus's way of life | Feeling "in heaven" through lived practice | Psychologically real, not doctrinal |
| Personal immortality (doctrine) | Paul's corruption | A reward promised for faith | "The most contemptible of all unfulfillable promises" |
| Immortality of the soul (priestly) | Church institution | Instrument of torture; priestly domination | "Systems of cruelty" |
| Denial of immortality (Epicurean) | Philosophical reason | Recovery of life's value | "A form of genuine _salvation_" |
