**Symbol** is, in Nietzsche's interpretation of Jesus, not a rhetorical ornament but the very _mode of his existence_. Jesus is "this great symbolist" (034-001) for whom "everything else, the whole of reality, all nature, even language, has significance only as sign, as allegory" (032-001). The catastrophic error of the [[paul|Pauline]] church, in this reading, was to take symbols as _facts_ — to literalize psychological states into metaphysical entities, inner "kingdoms" into future apocalypses, and a way of being into a set of doctrinal propositions. "Nothing could be more un-Christian than the _crude ecclesiastical_ notions of God as a _person_, of a 'kingdom of God' that is to come... a disrespect for symbols amounting to _world-historical cynicism_" (034-001).

## Jesus the Symbolist

In §32, Nietzsche presents Jesus as a figure for whom the distinction between "inner" and "outer" has collapsed — everything external is merely material for the expression of inner states:

> He speaks only of inner things: "life" or "truth" or "light" is his word for the innermost — in his sight everything else, the whole of reality, all nature, even language, has significance only as sign, as allegory. (032-001)

This is not esotericism — a secret doctrine hidden behind public symbols. It is the more radical claim that there _is_ no outer doctrine, no set of propositions that stand behind the symbols. The symbols _are_ the communication, and their meaning is experiential, not propositional:

> It is only on the theory that no work is to be taken literally that this anti-realist is able to speak at all. Set down among Hindus he would have made use of the concepts of Sankhya, and among Chinese he would have employed those of Lao-tse — and in neither case would it have made any difference to him. (032-001)

The specific symbolic vocabulary is arbitrary; what matters is the inner state the symbols evoke and communicate. This is a Jesus who stands "outside all religion, all notions of worship, all history, all natural science, all worldly experience, all knowledge, all politics, all psychology, all books, all art" (032-001) — "his 'wisdom' is precisely a _pure ignorance_ of all such things."

## The De-Literalization of Christian Concepts

In §34, Nietzsche applies this hermeneutic systematically to the central concepts of Christianity:

> The concept of "the Son of God" does not connote a concrete person in history, an isolated and definite individual, but an "eternal" fact, a psychological symbol set free from the concept of time. The same thing is true, and in the highest sense, of the _God_ of this typical symbolist, of the "kingdom of God," and of the "sonship of God." (034-001)

Each term is decoded:

| Ecclesiastical Reading (Literal) | Symbolic Reading (Nietzsche's Jesus) |
|----------------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| "Son of God" = second person of Trinity, unique to Jesus | "Entrance into the feeling of general transformation" |
| "Father" = first person of Trinity, transcendent creator | "That feeling itself — the sensation of eternity and perfection" |
| "Kingdom of God" = future eschatological event, coming judgment | "A state of the heart — not something to come 'beyond the world' or 'after death'" |
| "Kingdom of Heaven" = post-mortem reward | "An experience of the heart, everywhere and nowhere" |

> The "kingdom of heaven" is a state of the heart — not something to come "beyond the world" or "after death." The whole idea of natural death is _absent_ from the Gospels: death is not a bridge, not a passing; it is absent because it belongs to a quite different, a merely apparent world, useful only as a symbol. (034-001)

## The Church's "World-Historical Cynicism"

The church's literalism is not, for Nietzsche, a mere hermeneutical mistake. It is an act of interpretative violence — "like thrusting one's fist into the eye (and what an eye!) of the Gospels" (034-001). By converting symbols into dogmas, the church made Christianity into precisely the thing Jesus had transcended: a _religion_ with fixed beliefs, institutional structures, and metaphysical claims.

Nietzsche's own impatience is palpable:

> I am ashamed to remind you of what the church has made of this symbolism: has it not set an Amphitryon story at the threshold of the Christian "faith"? And a dogma of "immaculate conception" for good measure?... _And thereby it has robbed conception of its immaculateness_. (034-001)

The virgin birth is not just factually false — it is _symbolically_ obscene. It takes a symbol of inner transformation and turns it into a biological claim, thereby destroying the very dimension of meaning the symbol was meant to convey.

## Symbol versus Holy Lie

The distinction between Jesus' symbolism and the later church's [[holy-lie|"holy lie"]] is crucial. Jesus, in Nietzsche's reading, does not _assert_ the truth of his symbols as propositions; he simply lives within them. The symbols are transparent — they do not pretend to be anything other than symbols. The priestly "holy lie," by contrast, _insists_ on the literal truth of what it knows to be fictions, because only literal truths can ground institutional authority.

This is why Nietzsche can call Jesus a "free spirit" in §32 — his symbolism frees him from the need to _have_ a fixed doctrine at all. The priest needs dogma; the symbolist needs only life.

## The Psychological Precondition

Nietzsche is careful to ground Jesus' symbolism in a specific _physiological_ condition — "a morbid sensibility of the tactile nerves which causes those suffering from it to recoil from every touch, and from every effort to grasp a solid object" (029-001). The symbolism is not a philosophical choice but the expression of a nervous system for which "reality" is unbearable. Jesus' "inner world" is not a spiritual achievement but a physiological retreat:

> Such a physiological _habitus_ becomes an instinctive hatred of all reality, a flight into the "intangible," into the "incomprehensible"; a distaste for all formulae, for all conceptions of time and space, for everything established — customs, institutions, the church. (029-001)

The paradox is that this retreat from reality is also the condition of Jesus' [[freedom]]. Because he cannot bear the solid world, he is not trapped by any of its forms. His weakness is his liberation.
