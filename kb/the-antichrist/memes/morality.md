Morality, in Nietzsche's [[will-to-power|The Antichrist]], is not a neutral framework for distinguishing right from wrong — it is the primary instrument through which [[decadence|décadence]] is institutionalized, life is denied, and [[christianity|the priest]] seizes power. Nietzsche's critique of morality is not an attack on all valuation but on a specific historical development: the transformation of natural, life-affirming values into an abstract, life-denying "moral order of the world."

## Nietzsche's Diagnostic Method

Nietzsche approaches morality not as a philosopher defending or attacking ethical systems, but as a psychologist and physiologist examining the _effects_ of moral valuations on human health, instinct, and power.

> I have drawn back the curtain from the _rottenness_ of man. This word, in my mouth, is at least free from one suspicion: that it involves a moral accusation against humanity. It is used — and I wish to emphasize the fact again — without any moral significance: and this is so far true that the rottenness I speak of is most apparent to me precisely in those quarters where there has been most aspiration, hitherto, toward "virtue" and "godliness." (006-001)

The key move is methodological: Nietzsche refuses to evaluate morality _in moral terms_. Instead, he asks: what does this morality _do_ to those who practice it? Does it enhance or diminish life? Does it strengthen or weaken instincts?

## The Genealogy of Morality

In The Antichrist, Nietzsche builds on the analysis developed in _On the Genealogy of Morals_, tracing two fundamentally opposed moral systems:

| Morality Type | Origin | Core Value | Effect on Life |
|--------------|--------|-----------|----------------|
| **Noble morality** | Self-affirmation of the strong | Power, pride, self-approval | Enhances life, says Yes |
| **Ressentiment morality** | Reaction of the weak against the strong | Pity, humility, equality | Denies life, says No |

> In my "Genealogy of Morals" I give the first psychological explanation of the concepts underlying those two antithetical things, a _noble_ morality and a _ressentiment_ morality, the second of which is a mere product of the denial of the former. The Judaeo-Christian moral system belongs to the second division, and in every detail. (024-003)

The Judaeo-Christian system, Nietzsche argues, is ressentiment morality perfected into a world-historical force — a system that inverts all natural values and declares life itself guilty.

## The Denaturization of Values

Nietzsche traces the historical process through which natural values were progressively "denaturized" — stripped of their connection to life, instinct, and reality. The history of Israel provides his case study.

> _Morality_ is no longer a reflection of the conditions which make for the sound life and development of the people; it is no longer the primary life-instinct; instead it has become abstract and in opposition to life — a fundamental perversion of the fancy, an "evil eye" on all things. _What_ is Jewish, _what_ is Christian morality? Chance robbed of its innocence; unhappiness polluted with the idea of "sin"; well-being represented as a danger, as a "temptation"; a physiological disorder produced by the canker worm of conscience.... (025-001)

This denaturization operates through four mechanisms:

1. **Invention of "sin"** — natural misfortune is reinterpreted as moral punishment
2. **Invention of a "moral order"** — causal explanation is replaced by reward/punishment logic
3. **Invention of "free will"** — human action is moralized as choice between good and evil
4. **Invention of "the other world"** — life on earth is devalued in favor of an imaginary afterlife

## Morality as a Tool of Power

For Nietzsche, morality is not merely a set of beliefs — it is a technology of power, wielded by the [[priest|priestly type]] to dominate others and to invert natural hierarchies.

> Morality is the best of all devices for leading mankind _by the nose_! — The fact is that the conscious conceit of the chosen here disguises itself as modesty: it is in this way that _they_, the "community," the "good and just," range themselves, once and for always, on one side, the side of "the truth" — and the rest of mankind, "the world," on the other. (044-001)

The priest does not need to argue for his morality — he simply needs to define himself as its embodiment. By identifying "morality" with his own interests, the priest transforms self-interest into righteousness and persecution into virtue.

## Kant and the Philosophical Reinforcement of Morality

Nietzsche reserves particular contempt for Immanuel Kant, whom he sees as the philosophical architect of morality's final, most dangerous form: the categorical imperative.

> A virtue must be _our_ invention; it must spring out of _our_ personal need and defence. In every other case it is a source of danger. That which does not belong to our life _menaces_ it; a virtue which has its roots in mere respect for the concept of "virtue," as Kant would have it, is pernicious. (011-001)

Kant's achievement, in Nietzsche's analysis, was to sever morality entirely from life — to make duty abstract, universal, and impersonal. This "Chinese spirit of Königsberg" represents the terminal stage of moral denaturization: a system in which the life-instinct itself becomes an objection to moral worth.

## Christianity's Fictitious Moral World

The Christian moral system, Nietzsche argues, is built entirely on imaginary foundations that have no contact with reality:

> Under Christianity neither morality nor religion has any point of contact with actuality. It offers purely imaginary _causes_ ("God," "soul," "ego," "spirit," "free will" — or even "unfree"), and purely imaginary _effects_ ("sin," "salvation," "grace," "punishment," "forgiveness of sins"). (015-001)

This "purely fictitious world" falsifies, cheapens, and denies reality — and does so, Nietzsche insists, because it is rooted in _hatred_ of the natural. The man who suffers from reality needs to lie his way out of it, and Christian morality provides the lie.

## Pity as the Central Moral Value

Nietzsche identifies pity (_Mitleid_) as the keystone of Christian morality — and his most devastating critique is reserved for it. Pity is not a virtue but a pathology: it multiplies suffering, preserves what should perish, and acts as a depressant on the life-instinct.

> Mankind has ventured to call pity a virtue (— in every _superior_ moral system it appears as a weakness —); going still further, it has been called _the_ virtue, the source and foundation of all other virtues — but let us always bear in mind that this was from the standpoint of a philosophy that was nihilistic, and upon whose shield _the denial of life_ was inscribed. (007-001)

The elevation of pity to the supreme virtue is the diagnostic sign of nihilism: a morality that sides with the suffering, the weak, and the botched against life itself.

## Nietzsche's Revaluation

Nietzsche's project is not to abolish all morality but to _revalue_ values — to return valuation to its natural foundation in the life-instinct and the will to power. The task is not to become immoral but to create _new_ moralities that affirm rather than deny life.

The formula is simple in principle but radical in implication: every valuation must be tested by asking whether it strengthens or weakens the organism, whether it says Yes or No to life. A morality that fails this test — and Nietzsche argues that Judaeo-Christian morality fails it catastrophically — must be overcome.
