**Master morality** (_Herrenmoral_) is Nietzsche's term for the spontaneous, self-affirming value system of the strong — those who posit values out of their own abundance rather than reactively defining themselves against an enemy. It stands in fundamental opposition to slave morality (_Sklavenmoral_), which originates in ressentiment and defines "good" as the negation of the master's attributes. While Nietzsche rarely uses the exact compound _Herrenmoral_ in _The Antichrist_, the concept pervades his analysis of the psychological type that Christianity displaced.

## The Mechanism of Master Valuation

Master morality begins with self-affirmation: "I am good, therefore what serves me is good." The master does not define himself against an enemy; he defines the enemy as whatever is unlike himself:

> The noble type of man experiences itself as determining values; it does not need approval; it judges, "what is harmful to me is harmful in itself"; it knows itself as that which first accords honor to things; it is value-creating. (Beyond Good and Evil §260)

In _The Antichrist_, Nietzsche traces the historical displacement of master morality by Christian slave morality. The strong, "masterful and proud" attributes of the noble type were systematically eliminated from the concept of God:

> When everything necessary to ascending life; when all that is strong, courageous, masterful and proud has been eliminated from the concept of a god; when he has sunk step by step to the level of a staff for the weary... just what is the significance of such a metamorphosis? what does such a reduction of the godhead imply? (017-001)

The reduction of God from a master-deity to a "staff for the weary" is the theological equivalent of the psychological victory of slave morality over master morality.

## Masters and Slaves: The Psychological Antithesis

Nietzsche's psychological typology distinguishes masters from slaves not by birth or political power but by the structure of their valuation:

| Dimension | Master Morality | Slave Morality (Christian) |
|-----------|----------------|--------------------------|
| Origin of value | Self-affirmation, abundance | Ressentiment, reaction against the other |
| Primary judgment | "I am good" → "You are bad" | "You are evil" → "I am good" |
| Relation to power | Power as natural expression | Power as guilt-inducing |
| Core virtues | Pride, courage, self-reliance | Humility, pity, obedience |
| Relation to body | Body as source of value | Body as source of sin |

The master does not _choose_ to be masterly — he simply is what he is, and his values express his nature. The slave, by contrast, cannot be what he is; he must first negate the master before he can posit himself:

> Whereas all noble morality grows from a triumphant affirmation of itself, slave morality from the outset says No to what is "outside," what is "different," what is "not itself"; and this No is its creative act. (Genealogy of Morals I §10)

## Paul: The Apostle of Slave Morality

In Nietzsche's reading, Paul is the paradigmatic agent of the master-to-slave transvaluation. He takes the subtle, pre-moral type of Jesus and reinterprets it through the lens of ressentiment, converting a psychological type into a theological weapon:

> Paul simply transposed the center of gravity of that whole existence after this existence — in the lie of the "resurrected" Jesus. (042-001)

Paul's genius — a perverse genius from Nietzsche's perspective — was to make slave morality _universally binding_. By declaring that all are sinners before God, he abolishes the distinction between master and slave entirely. Everyone is equally guilty, equally in need of redemption. The moral hierarchy is inverted: the weak are "chosen," the strong are damned:

> "And God hath chosen the weak things of the world, the foolish things of the world, the base things of the world, and things which are despised": this was the formula; in hoc signo the décadence triumphed. (051-001)

## Master Morality and the Order of Rank

Master morality is inseparable from Nietzsche's concept of _Rangordnung_ (rank-ordering). The master does not claim that all should be masters — that would be absurd. The natural order requires different types with different moralities:

> In every healthy society there are three physiological types, gravitating toward differentiation but mutually conditioning one another, and each of these has its own hygiene, its own sphere of work, its own special mastery and feeling of perfection. (057-001)

The master type — "those who are chiefly intellectual" — requires a different "hygiene" than the mediocre or the guardians. What is virtue for the master (self-affirmation, solitude, severity toward oneself) may be poison for the mediocre (who requires external regulation, communal morality, modest contentment). Christianity's universal morality — one law for all — destroys this differentiation and makes genuine health impossible.

## The Modern Vestiges of Master Morality

Nietzsche finds traces of master morality even in the Christianized world. The Renaissance represents a brief resurgence of noble values against Christian slave morality. Figures like Cesare Borgia — whom Nietzsche provocatively praises — embody the master type's indifference to Christian moral categories:

> The Christian values — noble values: it is only we, we free spirits, who have re-established this greatest of all antitheses in values! (037-001)

The free spirit is, in a sense, the heir of the master type under modern conditions — not through political power but through intellectual sovereignty. The free spirit's "transvaluation of all values" (013-001) is the restoration of the master's prerogative: the right to create values rather than receive them.
