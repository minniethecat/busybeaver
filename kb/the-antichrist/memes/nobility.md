**Nobility**, in Nietzsche's philosophical vocabulary, is not a social title but a _physiological-psychological type_ — the human expression of ascending [[life]], characterized by strength, pride, self-reverence, and the capacity to create values rather than receive them. Against the "noble morality" of masters, Nietzsche sets the "slave morality" of [[christianity|Christianity]], which he identifies as the historical triumph of weakness, [[ressentiment]], and the instinct to revenge against everything lofty.

## Noble Morality vs. Ressentiment Morality

In §24, Nietzsche provides his most concentrated definition of the antithesis:

> In my "Genealogy of Morals" I give the first psychological explanation of the concepts underlying those two antithetical things, a _noble_ morality and a _ressentiment_ morality, the second of which is a mere product of the denial of the former. The Judaeo-Christian moral system belongs to the second division, and in every detail. (024-001)

The noble type creates values from within, out of overflowing strength. The ressentiment type defines itself negatively — by saying _No_ to everything that the noble type affirms. Where the noble says "I am good, therefore you are bad," the slave says "You are evil, therefore I am good."

In order to deny "everything representing an _ascending_ evolution of life — that is, to well-being, to power, to beauty, to self-approval — the instincts of _ressentiment_, here become downright genius, had to invent an _other_ world in which the _acceptance of life_ appeared as the most evil and abominable thing imaginable" (024-001).

## The Physiological Basis of Nobility

Nobility is not an idea but a bodily reality. In §51, Nietzsche insists:

> At the time when the sick and rotten Chandala classes in the whole _imperium_ were Christianized, the _contrary type_, the nobility, reached its finest and ripest development. (051-001)

The noble type requires health, not as an abstract ideal, but as the physiological foundation for pride, courage, and the capacity to affirm existence without metaphysical consolation.

## Christianity's War on the Noble

In §21, Nietzsche catalogs the Christian instincts as the systematic opposite of everything noble:

> Christian, again, is all deadly enmity to the rulers of the earth, to the "aristocratic" — along with a sort of secret rivalry with them (— one resigns one's "body" to them; one wants _only_ one's "soul"...). And Christian is all hatred of the intellect, of pride, of courage, of freedom, of intellectual _libertinage_; Christian is all hatred of the senses, of joy in the senses, of joy in general.... (021-001)

In §43, this becomes explicit: Christianity wages "a deadly war upon all feelings of reverence and distance between man and man, which is to say, upon the first _prerequisite_ to every step upward, to every development of civilization" (043-001). The core target is the **pathos of distance** — the noble's instinctive recognition of rank, of the gulf between higher and lower types:

> "The salvation of the soul" — in plain English: "the world revolves around _me_."... The poisonous doctrine, "_equal_ rights for all," has been propagated as a Christian principle: out of the secret nooks and crannies of bad instinct Christianity has waged a deadly war upon all feelings of reverence and distance between man and man... out of the _ressentiment_ of the masses it has forged its chief weapons against _us_, against everything noble, joyous and high-spirited on earth, against our happiness on earth.... (043-001)

## The Code of Manu: Noble Legislation

In §56–57, Nietzsche presents the Hindu Code of Manu as an example of legislation that serves the noble type, contrasting it sharply with the Bible:

> By means of it the _nobles_, the philosophers and the warriors keep the whip-hand over the majority; it is full of noble valuations, it shows a feeling of perfection, an acceptance of life, and triumphant feeling toward self and life — the _sun_ shines upon the whole book. (056-001)

The order of castes — the separation of the intellectual, the warrior, and the mediocre — is not an arbitrary invention but "the ratification of an _order of nature_, of a natural law of the first rank" (057-001):

| Caste | Type | Function | Privilege |
|-------|------|----------|-----------|
| First (fewest) | Intellectual | Rule, create values | Happiness, beauty, goodness |
| Second | Warrior/Guardian | Execute, maintain order | Strength, honor |
| Third (majority) | Mediocre | Occupational activities | Mediocrity as happiness |

> The superior caste — I call it the _fewest_ — has, as the most perfect, the privileges of the few: it stands for happiness, for beauty, for everything good upon earth. Only the most intellectual of men have any right to beauty, to the beautiful; only in them can goodness escape being weakness. (057-001)

Crucially, Nietzsche insists that "the separation of the three types is necessary to the maintenance of society, and to the evolution of higher types, and the highest types — the _inequality_ of rights is essential to the existence of any rights at all. — A right is a privilege" (057-001).

## Noble Figures in Nietzsche's Reading

**Pilate** — the one solitary figure in the New Testament whom Nietzsche considers noble. "To regard a Jewish imbroglio _seriously_ — that was quite beyond him. One Jew more or less — what did it matter?... The noble scorn of a Roman, before whom the word 'truth' was shamelessly mishandled" (046-001).

**Cæsar Borgia** — the lost possibility of the Renaissance. Nietzsche imagines "Cæsar Borgia as pope!... _that_ would have been the sort of triumph that _I_ alone am longing for today —: by it Christianity would have been _swept away_!" (061-001). The Renaissance was "the transvaluation of Christian values, — an attempt with all available means, all instincts and all the resources of genius to bring about a triumph of the _opposite_ values, the more _noble_ values" (061-001).

**The Romans** — "Instinctive nobility, taste, methodical inquiry, genius for organization and administration, faith in and the _will_ to secure the future of man, a great yes to everything entering into the _imperium Romanum_ and palpable to all the senses, a grand style that was beyond mere art, but had become reality, truth, _life_..." (059-001).

## Nobility and the Free Spirit

In §37, Nietzsche declares the task of the [[free-spirit|free spirit]]:

> _Christian_ values — _noble_ values: it is only we, we _free_ spirits, who have re-established this greatest of all antitheses in values!... (037-001)

The free spirit is the modern inheritor of the noble type — one who has the courage to revalue values, to stand outside the Christian-moral interpretation of existence, and to affirm life without transcendental consolations. Nietzsche describes himself and his readers in §1 as "Hyperboreans" who have discovered a happiness "beyond the North, beyond the ice, beyond _death_" — a distinctly noble, self-created happiness.
