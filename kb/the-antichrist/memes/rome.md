For Nietzsche, [[rome|Rome]] is not merely a geographical location — it is the symbolic site where the most decisive value-transformation in Western history occurred: the moment when the slave morality of [[christianity|Christianity]] conquered the noble values of the ancient world. Rome represents both what was lost (the strong, masterful, this-worldly civilization of antiquity) and how it was lost (through the patient, subterranean work of [[decadence|décadence]] and priestly cunning).

## The Conquest That Was a Surrender

Nietzsche's analysis inverts the conventional narrative. Rome did not fall to external barbarians — it was hollowed out from within by Christian décadence. The "strong races of northern Europe" failed to repudiate the Christian god, and this failure proved decisive.

> A curse lies upon them because they were not equal to it; they made illness, decrepitude and contradiction a part of their instincts — and since then they have not managed to _create_ any more gods. (019-001)

The triumph of Christianity in Rome was not a victory of truth over error, but of weakness over strength — the revenge of the weak against the masters, institutionalized as a world religion.

## Rome and Paul

[[paul|Paul]], operating from within the Roman sphere of influence, understood the imperial infrastructure as a vehicle for his new religion. The Roman roads, the Roman administrative system, the Roman _pax_ — all became instruments for the propagation of Christian priestly power. What began as a "Buddhistic peace movement" in Galilee became, through Paul's genius and Rome's infrastructure, a global ecclesiastical empire.

| Location | Nietzsche's Symbolic Meaning |
|----------|---------------------------|
| Galilee / Nazareth | Origin of Jesus's life-practice, the "inner world" |
| Jerusalem | Site of the Jewish priestly tradition, the temple |
| **Rome** | Site of value-transformation, Christianity becomes imperial |
| Northern Europe | The "strong races" that failed to resist Christian décadence |

## The Unfinished Revaluation

Nietzsche's [[decadence|The Antichrist]] is itself a declaration of war against the Roman inheritance — against the two-thousand-year reign of Christian values that began with Paul in the Roman world. The "revaluation of all values" that Nietzsche proposes is, in part, a call to undo what Rome enabled: the institutional triumph of anti-life morality over the life-affirming values of antiquity.
