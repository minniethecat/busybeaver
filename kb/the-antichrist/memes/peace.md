**Peace**, in Nietzsche's critique, is not the natural condition of a healthy [[culture]] but the political expression of decline — a value that arises when a people has lost the will to power, the capacity for struggle, and the [[nobility|pathos of distance]] that distinguishes higher from lower types. Far from being a universal good, peace is the triumph of the Chandala, the victory of the mediocre over everything that aspires upward.

## The Genealogy of Peace as a Value

In §16, Nietzsche traces how the concept of god transforms when a people declines, and peace enters the pantheon of virtues:

> When a nation is on the downward path, when it feels its belief in its own future, its hope of freedom slipping from it, when it begins to see submission as a first necessity and the virtues of submission as measures of self-preservation, then it _must_ overhaul its god. He then becomes a hypocrite, timorous and demure; he counsels "peace of soul," hate-no-more, leniency, "love" of friend and foe. He moralizes endlessly; he creeps into every private virtue; he becomes the god of every man; he becomes a private citizen, a cosmopolitan. (016-001)

Peace of soul, in this genealogy, is not spiritual attainment but political defeat internalized. The god who once represented "everything aggressive and thirsty for power in the soul of a people" becomes "simply _the good god_" — a god for those who can no longer fight.

## Peace as a Weapon of the Weak

The Christian valuation of peace is, for Nietzsche, an instance of [[ressentiment]] — the weak redefining their weakness as virtue. In §21, he catalogs the Christian instincts as systematic hostility to everything strong:

> Christian is all hatred of the intellect, of pride, of courage, of freedom, of intellectual _libertinage_; Christian is all hatred of the senses, of joy in the senses, of joy in general. (021-001)

Peace, in this framework, is not the absence of conflict but the _prohibition_ of the instincts that make conflict creative — pride, courage, the will to distinguish oneself from others. It is the demand that the strong make themselves weak so that the weak may feel safe.

## The Political Consequences of Peace

In §43, Nietzsche draws the political implications of the Christian peace-ideal:

> Nowadays no one has courage any more for special rights, for the right of dominion, for feelings of honourable pride in himself and his equals — for the _pathos of distance_.... Our politics is sick with this lack of courage! — The aristocratic attitude of mind has been undermined by the lie of the equality of souls. (043-001)

The modern democratic state, with its language of equal rights and universal peace, is the secular continuation of Christianity:

> If belief in the "privileges of the majority" makes and _will continue to make_ revolutions — it is Christianity, let us not doubt, and _Christian_ valuations, which convert every revolution into a carnival of blood and crime! Christianity is a revolt of all creatures that creep on the ground against everything that is _lofty_: the gospel of the "lowly" _lowers_. (043-001)

## The Physiology of Peace

Nietzsche's critique of peace operates at the physiological level. Health is not a static condition of tranquility but a dynamic process of growth, struggle, and overcoming:

> Life itself appears to me as an instinct for growth, for survival, for the accumulation of forces, for _power_: whenever the will to power fails there is disaster. (006-001)

Peace, understood as the cessation of struggle, is incompatible with life thus conceived. What Christianity calls "peace" is, physiologically, the exhaustion of the [[decadence|décadent]] — not the stillness of strength but the stillness of depletion.

## Buddhism vs. Christianity on Peace

Nietzsche contrasts Christianity unfavorably with [[buddhism]], which he regards as a more honest and hygienic response to suffering. Buddhism does not moralize — it prescribes:

> He encourages ideas that make for either quiet contentment or good cheer — he finds means to combat ideas of other sorts. He understands good, the state of goodness, as something which promotes health. _Prayer_ is not included, and neither is _asceticism_. (020-001)

Most importantly, Buddhism's peace does not depend on resentment of the healthy:

> His teaching is antagonistic to nothing so much as to revenge, aversion, _ressentiment_ ("enmity never brings an end to enmity": the moving refrain of all Buddhism....) (020-001)

Christian peace, by contrast, is inseparable from the anticipation of [[judgment]] — the secret satisfaction that those who disturbed one's peace will be punished eternally. It is peace purchased by the imagination of vengeance.

## Beyond Peace: The Affirmation of Struggle

Nietzsche's alternative to Christian peace is not mindless violence but the affirmation of struggle as the medium of all genuine achievement. The Hyperborean formula — "a Yea, a Nay, a straight line, a _goal_" (001-001) — rejects both the laziness of perpetual peace and the nihilism of aimless conflict. The free spirit makes war on what is low and sick in order to clear the ground for what is high and healthy. That is not "peace" — but it is the only condition under which anything worth having can grow.
