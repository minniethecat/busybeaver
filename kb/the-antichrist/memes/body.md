The **body** (_der Leib_) is the hidden protagonist of Nietzsche's critique of Christianity. Where Christianity posits an immortal soul housed in a contemptible body, Nietzsche reverses the valuation: the body is the reality, and the "soul" is a fiction produced by a sick body's resentment against itself. Christianity, he charges, "teaches misunderstanding of the body" (051-001) — and this is not a secondary error but the _primary_ one, from which all other Christian falsifications follow.

## The Body as Criterion

Nietzsche's method in _The Antichrist_ is consistently physiological: he reads doctrines, psychological types, and entire religions through the state of the body that produces them. The physiological is not one perspective among many — it is the _fundamental_ one:

> These are the two _physiological realities_ upon and out of which the doctrine of salvation has sprung. (030-001)

What Christianity calls "sin," Nietzsche reads as a symptom of physiological disorder. What it calls "faith," he reads as the cognitive expression of a body too exhausted to face reality:

> "Faith" means the will to avoid knowing what is true. The pietist, the priest of either sex, is a fraud _because_ he is sick: his instinct _demands_ that the truth shall never be allowed its rights on any point. (052-001)

## The Soul as Superstition

Nietzsche identifies the doctrine of the immortal soul as Christianity's foundational error — the lie that makes all other lies possible:

> Christianity... refuses to rid itself of the superstition about the soul! that makes a "virtue" of insufficient nourishment!... that persuades itself that it is possible to carry about a "perfect soul" in a cadaver of a body. (051-001)

The soul-doctrine is not merely false — it is _strategic_. By positing an immaterial self independent of the body, Christianity can declare war on the body without appearing to attack the person. Every instinct, every appetite, every natural impulse becomes a threat to the "soul."

> The vast lie of personal immortality destroys all reason, all natural instinct — henceforth, everything in the instincts that is beneficial, that fosters life and that safeguards the future is a cause of suspicion. (043-001)

## The Physiological Types of §57

Nietzsche's most developed account of the body's role in social organization appears in §57, where he outlines a "natural order" of three physiological types:

> In every healthy society there are three physiological types, gravitating toward differentiation but mutually conditioning one another, and each of these has its own hygiene, its own sphere of work, its own special mastery and feeling of perfection. It is _not_ Manu but nature that sets off in one class those who are chiefly intellectual, in another those who are marked by muscular strength and temperament, and in a third those who are distinguished in neither one way or the other. (057-001)

| Physiological Type | Characteristics | Sphere |
|-------------------|-----------------|--------|
| The Fewest (intellectual) | Most spiritual, self-mastery, asceticism as instinct | Ruling, creating values |
| The Guardians (muscular) | Warriors, executors of law, noble strength | Enforcement, protection |
| The Mediocre (majority) | Specialization, mechanical aptitude, contentment | Handicrafts, commerce, agriculture |

This is not a political theory but a _physiological_ observation: each type has "its own hygiene" — its own conditions of health. Christianity, by asserting the equality of all souls, destroys these physiological distinctions and makes genuine health impossible. The order of rank is "the supreme law of life itself" (057-001).

## Buddhism as Physiological Contrast

Nietzsche's extended comparison of Christianity with Buddhism in §20 is centrally a _physiological_ argument:

> The two physiological facts upon which it grounds itself and upon which it bestows its chief attention are: first, an excessive sensitiveness to sensation, which manifests itself as a refined susceptibility to pain, and _secondly_, an extraordinary spirituality. (020-001)

Unlike Christianity, Buddhism responds to physiological depression with hygienic measures — open air, careful diet, moderation, the avoidance of intoxicating passions. It "understands good, the state of goodness, as something which promotes health" (020-001). Prayer and asceticism are excluded because they would only "increase the excessive sensitiveness above mentioned." Buddhism is "a hundred times as realistic as Christianity" (020-001) precisely because it treats physiological facts as _facts_, not as occasions for moral judgment.

## The Body Against the "Beyond"

Nietzsche's ultimate charge is that Christianity's war on the body is a war on reality itself:

> When the centre of gravity of life is placed, _not_ in life itself, but in "the beyond" — in _nothingness_ — then one has taken away its centre of gravity altogether. (043-001)

To deny the body is to deny the only ground of value that exists. Christianity's "beyond" is not a real place — it is _nothingness_, the absence of the body, elevated to the status of a metaphysical principle. The body, in Nietzsche's counter-doctrine, is not the prison of the soul — it is the _only_ site where value, truth, and life can be measured.
