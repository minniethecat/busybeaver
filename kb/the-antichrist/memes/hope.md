**Hope**, in Nietzsche's anthropology of Christianity, is not a virtue but a psychological instrument of life-denial. Where the Greeks recognized hope as the most malign of evils — remaining behind at the source of all suffering — Christianity elevated it into a theological virtue, alongside [[faith]] and [[love]]. This elevation was not accidental: hope keeps humanity fixed on an imaginary "beyond" and thereby drains the will to transform the actual world.

## The Three Christian Ingenuities

In §23, Nietzsche groups faith, hope, and charity together — not as virtues, but as "ingenuities," psychological tricks by which Christianity secures its hold on suffering humanity:

> Hope, in its stronger forms, is a great deal more powerful _stimulans_ to life than any sort of realized joy can ever be. Man must be sustained in suffering by a hope so high that no conflict with actuality can dash it — so high, indeed, that no fulfilment can _satisfy_ it: a hope reaching out beyond this world. (023-001)

This is the core of Nietzsche's critique: hope is powerful precisely because it can never be fulfilled. An attainable goal would eventually be reached, and the sufferer would be freed. A hope "reaching out beyond this world" — beyond death, beyond nature — ensures permanent dependency. The sufferer is sustained _in_ suffering, not delivered _from_ it:

> Precisely because of this power that hope has of making the suffering hold out, the Greeks regarded it as the evil of evils, as the most _malign_ of evils; it remained behind at the source of all evil. (023-001)

## Hope as the God of the Weak

In §16, Nietzsche traces how a people's god reflects its condition. When a nation is strong, its god expresses power, victory, and self-affirmation. When a nation declines, its god becomes a god of goodness, peace, and hope — a god for those who have lost the capacity for actual triumph:

> True enough, when a nation is on the downward path, when it feels its belief in its own future, its hope of freedom slipping from it, when it begins to see submission as a first necessity and the virtues of submission as measures of self-preservation, then it _must_ overhaul its god. He then becomes a hypocrite, timorous and demure; he counsels "peace of soul," hate-no-more, leniency, "love" of friend and foe. (016-001)

## The Mechanism of Promissory Religion

Christianity operates through a logic of deferred gratification. The "proof by power" — "faith makes blessed: _therefore_ it is true" — is circular: one believes in the hope of blessedness, and the failure of that hope to materialize in this world is itself evidence that one must believe _more_, since only perfect faith unlocks the reward:

> It appears, unless I have been incorrectly informed, that there prevails among Christians a sort of criterion of truth that is called "proof by power." "Faith makes blessed: _therefore_ it is true."... But what of the thing that the priest promises to the believer, the wholly transcendental "beyond" — how is _that_ to be demonstrated? (050-001)

Nietzsche's verdict is blunt:

> Faith makes blessed: _therefore_, it lies. (050-001)

## The Political Economy of Hope

The theological virtue of hope is, for Nietzsche, inseparable from the priestly economy of power. The [[priest]] does not promise deliverance from suffering — he promises a _meaning_ for suffering, payable only after death. This converts hope into a currency that the priest alone can issue and redeem:

> The priest knows of only one great danger: that is science — the sound comprehension of cause and effect... Man must _not_ look outward; he must look inward. He must _not_ look at things shrewdly and cautiously, to learn about them; he must not look at all; he must _suffer_. And he must suffer so much that he is always in need of the priest. (049-001)

Hope, in this scheme, is the interest paid on suffering. The greater the suffering, the greater the hope required — and the deeper the dependence on the priestly intermediary who administers the promise.

## Hope vs. Amor Fati

Against Christian hope, Nietzsche posits a different relation to suffering and the future — what he will later call _amor fati_, the love of fate. The free spirit does not hope for a better world elsewhere; he affirms this world, with all its suffering, as the only world there is. To hope is to defer life; to affirm is to live it. The Hyperborean alternative stated in §7 — "to be the doctors _here_, to be unmerciful _here_, to wield the knife _here_" — is the rejection of hope in favor of action, health, and [[life|life-affirmation]].
