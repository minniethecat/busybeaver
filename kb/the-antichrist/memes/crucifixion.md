The [[crucifixion|death on the cross]] is, for Nietzsche, the pivot point of Western religious history — the event that separates the Evangel (the "glad tidings" of [[jesus-christ]]) from the Dysangelium (the "bad tidings" of [[paul]]). The cross is not a sacrifice, not an atonement, not the cosmic transaction that [[christianity|Christian]] theology claims. It is something simpler and more devastating: the natural and inevitable end of a life-form that had renounced all resistance to reality.

## The Death That Ended Christianity

Nietzsche's most radical claim about the crucifixion is that it was the death _of_ Christianity, not its founding. The "Gospels died on the cross" — meaning that the lived practice of Jesus, his way of being in the world, ended with his death. What arose afterward was its negation.

> The very word "Christianity" is a misunderstanding — at bottom there was only one Christian, and he died on the cross. The "Gospels" _died_ on the cross. What, from that moment onward, was called the "Gospels" was the very reverse of what _he_ had lived. (039-001)

## The Pivot: From Evangel to Dysangelium

| Before the Cross | After the Cross |
|-----------------|-----------------|
| Jesus's life-practice: peace, non-resistance, blessedness now | Paul's theology: sacrifice, resurrection, judgment to come |
| Kingdom of God as present experience | Kingdom of God as future promise |
| Death as unreal, absent from consciousness | Death as cosmic event requiring theological explanation |
| No church, no priesthood, no doctrine | Church, priesthood, theology, canon |

Nietzsche sees Paul's genius precisely in his recognition that the cross could be repurposed. By transforming it from the end of a life into a _sacrifice for sins_, Paul created the psychological mechanism — guilt, redemption, faith — that would power two millennia of priestly domination.

## A Buddhistic Peace Movement

Before Paul's reinterpretation, Nietzsche reads the crucifixion as the end of "a new and thoroughly original effort to found a Buddhistic peace movement" (042-001). Jesus's project was to establish happiness on earth — real happiness, not merely promised — through a radical practice of non-resistance and inner transformation. The cross demonstrated the limit of this practice: total non-resistance to the point of death.

## The Meaning of the Cross

Nietzsche does not sentimentalize the crucifixion. He does not see it as a tragedy or an injustice. It is, for him, the consistent conclusion of a life that had systematically refused all the instincts of self-preservation and self-assertion. The cross demonstrates what happens when the will to power is extinguished — not as metaphor but as lived reality.
