**Humanity**, in Nietzsche's vocabulary, is not a neutral term for the human species but a concept that has been systematically corrupted by [[christianity|Christian]] and modern valuations. Where the noble cultures of antiquity understood humanity as a hierarchy — with the highest types representing its true flourishing — Christianity and its secular successors have redefined "humanity" to mean the aggregate of the mediocre, the sick, and the botched, elevated to a moral principle of equality that wages war on everything exceptional.

## The Rottenness of Humanity

Nietzsche opens his diagnosis in §6 with a deliberately non-moral use of "rottenness":

> It is a painful and tragic spectacle that rises before me: I have drawn back the curtain from the _rottenness_ of man. This word, in my mouth, is at least free from one suspicion: that it involves a moral accusation against humanity. (006-001)

The rottenness is not moral but biological — it is [[decadence]], the loss of the instincts that preserve and enhance life. The "highest values" of humanity — compassion, equality, selflessness — are symptoms of decline:

> I call an animal, a species, an individual corrupt, when it loses its instincts, when it chooses, when it _prefers_, what is injurious to it. (006-001)

> My contention is that all the highest values of humanity have been emptied of this will — that the values of _décadence_, of _nihilism_, now prevail under the holiest names. (006-001)

## Christianity's War on Noble Humanity

The doctrine of personal immortality is, for Nietzsche, the instrument by which Christianity undermined all that was noble in humanity. By declaring every soul equal and eternal, it destroyed the hierarchy of types:

> To allow "immortality" to every Peter and Paul was the greatest, the most vicious outrage upon _noble_ humanity ever perpetrated. (043-001)

This "equality of souls" systematically attacks the [[nobility|pathos of distance]] — the recognition of rank that is the prerequisite for civilization's upward development:

> Christianity has waged a deadly war upon all feelings of reverence and distance between man and man, which is to say, upon the first _prerequisite_ to every step upward, to every development of civilization. (043-001)

## The Priest as Parasite on Humanity

In §38, Nietzsche arrives at his most devastating conclusion about the priest's relationship to humanity:

> We know, our _conscience_ now knows — just _what_ the real value of all those sinister inventions of priest and church has been and _what ends they have served_, with their debasement of humanity to a state of self-pollution, the very sight of which excites loathing — the concepts "the other world," "the last judgment," "the immortality of the soul," the "soul" itself: they are all merely so many instruments of torture, systems of cruelty, whereby the priest becomes master and remains master. (038-001)

The concept of [[sin]] is the central weapon in this war:

> Sin, man's self-desecration _par excellence_, was invented in order to make science, culture, and every elevation and ennobling of man impossible; the priest _rules_ through the invention of sin. (049-001)

By severing the connection between natural cause and natural effect, the priestly system makes humanity dependent on its own parasitic class:

> When the natural consequences of an act are no longer "natural," but are regarded as produced by the ghostly creations of superstition — by "God," by "spirits," by "souls" — then the whole ground-work of knowledge is destroyed — _then the greatest of crimes against humanity has been perpetrated_. (049-001)

## The Free Spirit's Humanity

Against Christian "humanity," Nietzsche sets a different ideal — the humanity of the philosopher, the free spirit, the one who cures rather than pities:

> Nothing is more unhealthy, amid all our unhealthy modernism, than Christian pity. To be the doctors _here_, to be unmerciful _here_, to wield the knife _here_ — all this is _our_ business, all this is _our_ sort of humanity, by this sign we are philosophers, we Hyperboreans! (007-001)

The free spirits perform a revaluation:

> _Christian_ values — _noble_ values: it is only we, we _free_ spirits, who have re-established this greatest of all antitheses in values! (037-001)

## The New Testament as Anti-Humanity

In §46, Nietzsche's reading of the New Testament yields a stark aesthetic-psychological judgment:

> I have searched the New Testament in vain for a single sympathetic touch; nothing is there that is free, kindly, open-hearted or upright. In it humanity does not even make the first step upward — the instinct for _cleanliness_ is lacking. (046-001)

## Christianity as Humanity's Greatest Misfortune

Nietzsche's final judgment in §62 is unsparing:

> Christianity remains to this day the greatest misfortune of humanity. (051-001)

The Christian church operates as a parasite on the life of the species:

> Parasitism as the _only_ practice of the church; with its anaemic and "holy" ideals, sucking all the blood, all the love, all the [[hope]] out of [[life]]; the beyond as the will to deny all reality; the cross as the distinguishing mark of the most subterranean conspiracy ever heard of — against health, beauty, well-being, intellect, _kindness_ of soul — _against life itself_. (062-001)
