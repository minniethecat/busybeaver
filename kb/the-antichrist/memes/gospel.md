The "Gospel" (_Evangelium_, "glad tidings"), in Nietzsche's [[will-to-power|The Antichrist]], undergoes a radical reinterpretation. It is not the message _about_ [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] — his divine sonship, his atoning death, his resurrection — that the later church would preach. It is the message _of_ Jesus: a new way of being in the world, a state of inner beatitude so complete that it abolishes the very categories of sin, guilt, punishment, and reward. Nietzsche's reading of the Gospels is an attempt to recover this "glad tidings" from beneath two millennia of ecclesiastical distortion.

## What the Gospels Abolished

Nietzsche's most concentrated statement on the nature of the Gospel appears in his analysis of Jesus's psychological world. The "glad tidings" consist, above all, in the _abolition_ of the entire apparatus of religious distance between man and God.

> In the whole psychology of the "Gospels" the concepts of guilt and punishment are lacking, and so is that of reward. "Sin," which means anything that puts a distance between God and man, is abolished— _this is precisely the "glad tidings."_ Eternal bliss is not merely promised, nor is it bound up with conditions: it is conceived as the _only_ reality—what remains consists merely of signs useful in speaking of it. (033-001)

The Gospel, on this reading, is not a promise of future salvation but a declaration of _present_ blessedness. Jesus does not say "you will be saved if you believe" — he lives in a state of such complete beatitude that the question of "salvation" no longer arises. This is why Nietzsche insists on the formula: **"a new way of life, _not_ a new faith."**

## The Gospel Way of Life

The "glad tidings" project themselves into a distinctive mode of existence — the "evangelical way of life" — that Nietzsche describes in terms of non-resistance, non-judgment, and radical indifference to social distinctions.

> It is not a "belief" that marks off the Christian; he is distinguished by a different mode of action; he acts _differently_. He offers no resistance, either by word or in his heart, to those who stand against him. He draws no distinction between strangers and countrymen, Jews and Gentiles. He is angry with no one, and he despises no one. He neither appeals to the courts of justice nor heeds their mandates ("Swear not at all"). He never under any circumstances divorces his wife, even when he has proofs of her infidelity. (033-001)

These are not moral rules but expressions of a fundamental psychological state — a state in which the distinction between "self" and "world," "friend" and "enemy," has dissolved into a felt unity. The Gospel way of life is not _achieved_ through effort; it _flows_ from an inner transformation.

## Jesus's Faith vs. the Church's Faith

Nietzsche draws a critical distinction between the "faith" that Jesus embodied and the "faith" that [[paul|Paul]] and the subsequent church would preach. Jesus's faith is pre-dogmatic, pre-ecclesiastical — a "recrudescent childishness of the spirit" that needs no creed, no scripture, no institutional structure.

> What the "glad tidings" tell us is simply that there are no more contradictions; the kingdom of heaven belongs to _children_ ; the faith that is voiced here is no more an embattled faith—it is at hand, it has been from the beginning... A faith of this sort is not furious, it does not denounce, it does not defend itself: it does not come with "the sword"—it does not realize how it will one day set man against man. It does not manifest itself either by miracles, or by rewards and promises, or by "scriptures": it is itself, first and last, its own miracle, its own reward, its own promise, its own "kingdom of God." This faith does not formulate itself—it simply _lives_, and so guards itself against formulae. (032-001)

The contrast with the church's faith could not be sharper: where Jesus's faith is silent, living, and self-sufficient, ecclesiastical faith is noisy, doctrinal, and dependent on promises and threats. The church reduced the Gospel to "an acceptance of truth, to a mere phenomenon of consciousness" — which, Nietzsche insists, is "to formulate the negation of Christianity."

## The Dysangelium: From "Glad Tidings" to "Bad Tidings"

Nietzsche coins the term _Dysangelium_ — "bad tidings" — to describe what happened to the Gospel after the death of Jesus. The original "glad tidings" died on the cross, and what arose in its place was its exact opposite.

> The very word "Christianity" is a misunderstanding—at bottom there was only one Christian, and he died on the cross. The "Gospels" _died_ on the cross. What, from that moment onward, was called the "Gospels" was the very reverse of what _he_ had lived: "bad tidings," a _Dysangelium_. (039-001)

This is Nietzsche's most radical historical claim: Christianity, as a historical religion, is not the continuation of Jesus's Gospel but its _reversal_. Paul, the church, the creeds, the sacraments — all of these belong to the Dysangelium, not the Evangelium.

## The World-Historical Irony of the Church

Nietzsche frames the entire history of Christianity as an instance of "world-historical irony": the church pronounces "holy" precisely what Jesus regarded as beneath him.

> That mankind should be on its knees before the very antithesis of what was the origin, the meaning and the _law_ of the Gospels—that in the concept of the "church" the very things should be pronounced holy that the "bearer of glad tidings" regards as _beneath_ him and _behind_ him—it would be impossible to surpass this as a grand example of _world-historical irony_. (036-001)

The irony operates at every level:

| Jesus's Gospel | The Church's Dysangelium |
|---------------|------------------------|
| A lived state of beatitude | A system of beliefs to be accepted |
| No sin, no guilt, no punishment | Sin, guilt, punishment as central mechanisms |
| No distance between man and God | Priest as mediator between man and God |
| "Kingdom of God" as present reality | "Kingdom of God" as future promise |
| No scripture, no formula | "Holy books," creeds, dogmas |

## The Gospel as Psychological Reality

Nietzsche's reading of the Gospels is, ultimately, a _psychological_ reading. The "glad tidings" are not a theological proposition — they are a description of a possible human state. "To this day _such_ a life is still possible, and for _certain_ men even necessary: genuine, primitive Christianity will remain possible in all ages."

> The deep instinct which prompts the Christian how to _live_ so that he will feel that he is "in heaven" and is "immortal," despite many reasons for feeling that he is _not_ "in heaven": this is the only psychological reality in "salvation."—A new way of life, _not_ a new faith. (033-002)

What Nietzsche recovers from the Gospels is not a religion but a _possibility_ — the possibility of a life lived without resentment, without judgment, without the need to make others suffer for one's own suffering. Whether this possibility is desirable is a separate question; Nietzsche's own answer is complex. But his reading of the "glad tidings" as a psychological reality, rather than a theological claim, is the foundation of everything else he says about Christianity.
