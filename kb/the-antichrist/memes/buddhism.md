**Buddhism** occupies a unique position in Nietzsche's critique of religion. It is, together with [[christianity|Christianity]], one of the two "religions of [[decadence|décadence]]" — both arise from the exhaustion of life, both respond to the problem of suffering — but Nietzsche insists they are separated by "a very remarkable" difference. Where Christianity falsifies reality through moral concepts, promises, and priestly fraud, Buddhism confronts suffering directly, without self-deception. It is, Nietzsche declares, "a hundred times more realistic than Christianity."

## Definition and Mechanism

Nietzsche's definition of Buddhism is inseparable from his definition of Christianity — each illuminates the other by contrast. Both are "nihilistic religions," but their nihilism operates through fundamentally different mechanisms.

> "In my condemnation of Christianity I surely hope I do no injustice to a related religion with an even larger number of believers: I allude to _Buddhism_. Both are to be reckoned among the nihilistic religions — they are both _décadence_ religions — but they are separated from each other in a very remarkable way." (020-014)

The key distinction lies in Buddhism's rejection of moral concepts as explanatory categories. Buddhism does not speak of a "struggle with sin" but, "yielding to reality," of the "struggle with suffering." It has moved beyond the moral interpretation of existence — in Nietzsche's phrase, it is _beyond good and evil_.

| Christianity | Buddhism |
|-------------|----------|
| Struggle with _sin_ | Struggle with _suffering_ |
| Self-deception through moral concepts | Honest confrontation with reality |
| God as person, judge, saviour | Concept of God already disposed of |
| Promises everything, fulfills nothing | Promises nothing, actually fulfills |
| Priestly fraud as operating system | Hygienic measures against depression |
| Mastery over _beasts of prey_ through making ill | Peace for the over-spiritualized through care |

## Buddhism as Physiological-Hygienic Practice

For Nietzsche, Buddhism's superiority over Christianity is rooted in its honest physiological basis. It diagnoses its own condition — excessive sensitivity to sensation, over-spiritualization — and responds with hygienic measures rather than moral exhortations.

> "The two physiological facts upon which it grounds itself and upon which it bestows its chief attention are: first, an excessive sensitiveness to sensation, which manifests itself as a refined susceptibility to pain, and _secondly_, an extraordinary spirituality, a too protracted concern with concepts and logical procedures, under the influence of which the instinct of personality has yielded to a notion of the 'impersonal.'" (020-014)

Buddha's response is pragmatic and this-worldly: a life in the open, moderation in eating, caution with intoxicants, avoidance of worry, cultivation of cheerfulness. "He understands good, the state of goodness, as something which promotes health." There is no prayer, no asceticism for its own sake, no categorical imperative. "It is always possible to leave" — even from a monastery. And crucially: "his teaching is antagonistic to nothing so much as to revenge, aversion, _ressentiment_."

> "Buddhism, I repeat, is a hundred times more austere, more honest, more objective. It no longer has to _justify_ its pains, its susceptibility to suffering, by interpreting these things in terms of sin — it simply says, as it simply thinks, 'I suffer.'" (023-014)

## Christianity vs. Buddhism: Two Types of Décadence

The comparison yields one of Nietzsche's most devastating insights: the difference between a religion that honestly confronts human weakness and one that exploits it. Buddhism arises among the "higher and better educated classes" in a mild climate with customs of "great gentleness and liberality, and _no_ militarism." Christianity, by contrast:

> "When Christianity departed from its native soil, that of the lowest orders, the _underworld_ of the ancient world, and began seeking power among barbarian peoples, it no longer had to deal with _exhausted_ men, but with men still inwardly savage and capable of self-torture — in brief, strong men, but bungled men." (022-014)

Christianity's method for mastering these "beasts of prey" is to make them _ill_ — "to make feeble is the Christian recipe for taming, for 'civilizing.'" Buddhism, aimed at races that have "become kind, gentle and over-spiritualized," offers a "summons that takes them back to peace and cheerfulness." Christianity creates sickness; Buddhism treats it.

> "Buddhism is a religion for peoples in a further state of development, for races that have become kind, gentle and over-spiritualized (—Europe is not yet ripe for it—): it is a summons that takes them back to peace and cheerfulness, to a careful rationing of the spirit, to a certain hardening of the body. Christianity aims at mastering _beasts of prey_; its modus operandi is to make them _ill_ — to make feeble is the Christian recipe for taming, for 'civilizing.'" (022-014)

## Christianity's Borrowings and Buddhism's Honesty

Nietzsche identifies specific techniques that Christianity has appropriated — and debased — from older spiritual traditions. The shrewdness of Christian psychology, its understanding that "it is of very little consequence whether a thing be true or not, so long as it is _believed_ to be true," is an Oriental inheritance:

> "At the bottom of Christianity there are several subtleties that belong to the Orient. In the first place, it knows that it is of very little consequence whether a thing be true or not, so long as it is _believed_ to be true. Truth and _faith_: here we have two wholly distinct worlds of ideas, almost two diametrically _opposite_ worlds — the road to the one and the road to the other lie miles apart." (023-016)

But where Buddhism uses psychological insight for healing, Christianity uses it for control. Faith, hope, and charity — the three Christian theological virtues — Nietzsche renames "the three Christian _ingenuities_." They are devices for making suffering tolerable by anchoring the sufferer in illusion. "Buddhism is in too late a stage of development, too full of positivism, to be shrewd in any such way."

## Jesus as Buddhist: The Lost Possibility

In one of his most provocative reconstructions, Nietzsche reads Jesus himself as a kind of Buddhist — a figure who attempted to found "a Buddhistic peace movement" that would establish "happiness _on earth_ — real, _not_ merely promised." This is the essential difference that Paul's intervention destroyed:

> "One now begins to see just _what_ it was that came to an end with the death on the cross: a new and thoroughly original effort to found a Buddhistic peace movement, and so establish _happiness on earth_ — real, _not_ merely promised. For this remains — as I have already pointed out — the essential difference between the two religions of _décadence_: Buddhism promises nothing, but actually fulfils; Christianity promises everything, but _fulfils nothing_." (042-014)

The tragedy, in Nietzsche's reading, is that [[paul|Paul]] — the "dysangelist," the "genius for hatred" — destroyed this possibility and replaced it with the doctrine of judgment, immortality, and priestly power. The authentic Jesus was closer to Buddha than to the church that bears his name.
