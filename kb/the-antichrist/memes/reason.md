Reason, in Nietzsche's _The Antichrist_, is a contested faculty — valued by Nietzsche as the organ of [[truth|intellectual integrity]] but diagnosed as systematically corrupted by [[theology|theological]] [[instinct]]. The critique is not of reason as such (Nietzsche is no irrationalist) but of a specific **counterfeit of reason** that serves morality and faith while pretending to serve truth. Nietzsche calls this counterfeit "practical reason" and traces it directly to the priestly inheritance in philosophy.

## Practical Reason: The Priestly Fraud

Nietzsche's sharpest attack on the corruption of reason targets Kant's concept of "practical reason" — which Nietzsche reads as a philosophical cover for continuing to believe what one wants to believe:

> In the end, with "German" innocence, Kant tried to give a scientific flavour to this form of corruption, this dearth of intellectual conscience, by calling it "practical reason." He deliberately invented a variety of reasons for use on occasions when it was desirable not to trouble with reason — that is, when morality, when the sublime command "thou shalt," was heard. (012-001)

The argument is precise: Kant invents a second, subordinate "reason" — practical reason — whose function is to _preserve_ moral beliefs that theoretical reason would otherwise undermine. This is not philosophy in the service of truth; it is philosophy in the service of [[morality]], which is to say, in the service of the [[priest]].

## The Genealogy of the Philosopher-Priest

Nietzsche traces the corruption of reason to its historical roots: the philosopher is a descendant of the priest, and philosophy has never fully escaped this inheritance:

> When one recalls the fact that, among all peoples, the philosopher is no more than a development from the old type of priest, this inheritance from the priest, this _fraud upon self_, ceases to be remarkable. When a man feels that he has a divine mission, say to lift up, to save or to liberate mankind — when a man feels the divine spark in his heart and believes that he is the mouthpiece of supernatural imperatives — when such a mission inflames him, it is only natural that he should stand beyond all merely reasonable standards of judgment. (012-001)

This genealogical insight is devastating. What philosophers present as the "demands of reason" are, in Nietzsche's reading, often the disguised demands of the priestly instinct — the will to be an authority, to pronounce upon good and evil, to "save" mankind. The philosopher's "reason" is the priest's "revelation" in secular dress.

## Reason vs. Faith

Nietzsche frames the conflict between reason and [[faith]] as a conflict between two fundamentally opposed orientations toward reality:

> Truth and _faith_: here we have two wholly distinct worlds of ideas, almost two diametrically _opposite_ worlds — the road to the one and the road to the other lie miles apart... But when _faith_ is thus exalted above everything else, it necessarily follows that reason, knowledge and patient inquiry have to be discredited: the road to the truth becomes a forbidden road. (023-001)

| Reason | Faith |
|--------|-------|
| Patient inquiry | Immediate conviction |
| Open to correction | Closed to evidence |
| Skeptical of authority | Submission to authority |
| Seeks to know | Seeks to believe |
| Intellectual conscience | "Closing one's eyes" |

The opposition is absolute: a culture that prioritizes faith _must_ discredit reason, just as a culture that prioritizes reason _must_ question faith. There is no stable compromise — every attempt to "reconcile" faith and reason is, in Nietzsche's view, a disguised surrender of reason.

## Reason and Life

Nietzsche's vitalism extends to his epistemology: reason is not a transcendental faculty but a **tool of [[life]]**. Its health or sickness is measured by its service to life:

> The vast lie of personal immortality destroys all reason, all natural instinct — henceforth, everything in the instincts that is beneficial, that fosters life and that safeguards the future is a cause of suspicion. (043-001)

When reason is separated from life — when it is made to serve anti-life doctrines like personal immortality — it ceases to be genuine reason and becomes its opposite: a rationalization of [[decadence]]. This is Nietzsche's deepest lesson about reason: its value is not intrinsic but instrumental — it is good when it serves life, and it becomes pathological when it serves the denial of life.

## The Intellectual Conscience

Against the corrupted "practical reason" of the philosophical tradition, Nietzsche posits the **intellectual conscience** (_intellektuelles Gewissen_) — the disciplined refusal to accept comfortable falsehoods, the commitment to seeing things as they are:

- **Honesty**: The first virtue of the intellectual conscience — not deceiving oneself
- **Courage**: The willingness to endure truths that are painful or dangerous
- **Patience**: The rejection of premature certainty in favor of sustained inquiry
- **Self-suspicion**: The recognition that one's own beliefs may be products of instinct rather than evidence

The intellectual conscience is what separates the genuine philosopher from the priest in philosopher's clothing. It is, for Nietzsche, the rarest and most valuable quality a human being can possess — and the one most systematically destroyed by two thousand years of Christian training in credulity.
