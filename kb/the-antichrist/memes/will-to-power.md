The [[decadence|Will to Power]] (_Wille zur Macht_) is the fundamental explanatory principle of Nietzsche's mature philosophy.

It is not a metaphysical substance but a dynamic principle: life itself conceived as an instinct for growth, for the accumulation of force, for self-overcoming. Wherever the will to power declines, Nietzsche diagnoses [[decadence]] — the organism that chooses what harms it, the civilization that elevates weakness into virtue.

## The Fundamental Instinct

Nietzsche defines will to power not as a desire for domination over others but as the intrinsic tendency of all living things toward expansion, mastery, and the enhancement of their own capacities.

> Life itself appears to me as an instinct for growth, for survival, for the accumulation of forces, for _power_: whenever the will to power fails there is disaster. My contention is that all the highest values of humanity have been emptied of this will — that the values of _décadence_, of _nihilism_, now prevail under the holiest names. (006-002)

This formulation is crucial: the will to power is not one instinct among others but the underlying _form_ of all healthy instincts. Its failure is not a moral failing but a physiological catastrophe — the organism turns against its own conditions of flourishing.

## Will to Power and Décadence

| Healthy Expression | Décadent Perversion |
|-------------------|-------------------|
| Growth, expansion, accumulation | Decline, contraction, depletion |
| Self-overcoming, mastery | Self-denial, submission |
| Instinct affirmed, trusted | Instinct denied, suspected |
| This-worldly, embodied | Otherworldly, disembodied |
| Strength celebrated as virtue | Weakness elevated as "goodness" |

The entire critique of [[christianity|Christianity]] in The Antichrist can be understood as a case study in what happens when the will to power is systematically denied: the strong are made to feel guilty for their strength, the weak are celebrated for their weakness, and a whole civilization's instincts are turned against life.

## The God of Décadence

Nietzsche traces the historical degradation of the concept of God as a direct expression of declining will to power. From the tribal god of Israel — strong, partial, demanding — to the Christian "god of the good" — pale, universal, and emptied of all life-affirming qualities — the trajectory is one of progressive depletion.

> Wherever the will to power begins to decline, in whatever form, there is always an accompanying decline physiologically, a _décadence_. The divinity of this _décadence_, shorn of its masculine virtues and passions, is converted perforce into a god of the physiologically degraded, of the weak. (017-001)

## Beyond Good and Evil

The will to power is the key to understanding Nietzsche's "immoralism."

If life is will to power, then moral systems are not neutral descriptions of good and evil but expressions of particular configurations of power — some healthy, some décadent. Christian morality, as "slave morality," expresses a declining will to power that takes revenge on the strong by calling them "evil."
