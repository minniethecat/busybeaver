Life (_Leben_) is the **ultimate criterion of value** in Nietzsche's philosophy — the standard against which all moral systems, religions, and cultural forms must be evaluated. In _The Antichrist_, Nietzsche uses "life" not as a biological given but as a normative principle: whatever enhances, intensifies, and affirms life is good; whatever diminishes, denies, or devalues it is [[decadence|décadent]]. This vitalism places Nietzsche in direct opposition to [[christianity]], which he diagnoses as a systematic "No" to life.

## Definition and Mechanism

For Nietzsche, life is not mere survival or bare existence — it is the active drive toward growth, power, and self-overcoming. This is the [[will-to-power]] operating at the biological and psychological level:

> Life itself appears to me as an instinct for growth, for survival, for the accumulation of forces, for _power_: whenever the will to power fails there is disaster. (006-002)

The mechanism is evaluative: life serves as Nietzsche's **meta-criterion** for judging all other values. A morality that says "No" to the instincts, that teaches contempt for the body, that posits a "true world" beyond this one — such a morality is _ipso facto_ condemned by the standard of life. It is not merely wrong; it is _sick_.

## Life Against the "Beyond"

Nietzsche's most condensed argument for life as the sole standard of value appears in his critique of the Christian doctrine of personal immortality. To place the meaning of life _outside_ life is, logically, to drain life of all meaning:

> When the centre of gravity of life is placed, _not_ in life itself, but in "the beyond" — in _nothingness_ — then one has taken away its centre of gravity altogether. The vast lie of personal immortality destroys all reason, all natural instinct — henceforth, everything in the instincts that is beneficial, that fosters life and that safeguards the future is a cause of suspicion. So to live that life no longer has any meaning: _this_ is now the "meaning" of life. (043-001)

This passage illustrates Nietzsche's dialectical method: the Christian promise of "eternal life" is exposed as a _denial_ of actual life. By directing all value toward an imaginary "beyond," Christianity renders the real world — the only world — worthless.

## The Physiological Standard

Nietzsche's vitalism is grounded in the body. Life is not an abstract principle but a physiological reality — the health or sickness of the organism:

| Aspect | Life-Affirming | Life-Denying |
|--------|---------------|--------------|
| Relation to body | Body as source of wisdom | Body as prison of the soul |
| Relation to instinct | Trust in instinct | Suspicion of instinct |
| Relation to suffering | Suffering as stimulus to growth | Suffering as argument against life |
| Relation to joy | Joy as proof of health | Joy as temptation |
| Exemplar | [[will-to-power|The strong, the noble]] | [[priest|The priest]], the décadent |

This physiological grounding means that Nietzsche's evaluations are not "subjective" in the conventional sense — they are anchored in the observable health or sickness of the human organism. A morality that produces weak, resentful, life-weary human beings is objectively — physiologically — false.

## Life and Pity

One of Nietzsche's most controversial applications of the life-standard is his critique of pity (_Mitleid_). Far from being a virtue, pity is a **depressant** that multiplies suffering and preserves what is ripe for destruction:

> Pity thwarts the whole law of evolution, which is the law of natural selection. It preserves whatever is ripe for destruction; it fights on the side of those disinherited and condemned by life; by maintaining life in so many of the botched of all kinds, it gives life itself a gloomy and dubious aspect. (007-001)

This argument is often misunderstood as cruelty. Nietzsche's point is not that suffering is good but that pity, by preserving the weak at the expense of the strong, is _anti-life_ — it prevents the flourishing of what is most vital and promotes the multiplication of misery.

## Life as Affirmation

The ultimate expression of life-affirmation in Nietzsche's thought is the _amor fati_ — the love of fate, the ability to say Yes to existence as a whole, including its suffering and tragedy. This stands in stark contrast to the Christian "No" to earthly existence.

The life-affirming attitude is characterized by:
- **Gratitude for existence**: Not _despite_ suffering but _including_ it
- **Trust in instinct**: The body's wisdom over the mind's abstractions
- **Joy in power**: The pleasure of self-overcoming and growth
- **Acceptance of hierarchy**: Recognition that life produces rank and difference, not equality

For Nietzsche, the [[gospel|original Evangel]] of [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] — before its corruption by [[paul|Pauline]] theology — was itself a practice of life-affirmation: a way of living _now_, in the body, without guilt or resentment. What Christianity became was the precise opposite: a religion of _ressentiment_ against life.
