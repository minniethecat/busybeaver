# Courage (Tapferkeit)

**Courage** (*Tapferkeit*) in Nietzsche's revaluation is not the conventional virtue of facing physical danger but an *epistemic virtue* — the intellectual courage required to face uncomfortable truths, to question one's own deepest convictions, and to live without the consolations of metaphysical certainty. Courage is the precondition of [[honesty]] (*Redlichkeit*): one cannot be intellectually honest without the courage to endure what honesty reveals.

## Definition and Mechanism

Courage appears at the very opening of *The Antichrist* as a defining characteristic of the "Hyperboreans" — Nietzsche's figure for those who have overcome modernity:

> We were brave enough; we spared neither ourselves nor others; but we were a long time finding out *where* to direct our courage. We grew dismal; they called us fatalists. *Our* fate — it was the fulness, the tension, the *storing up* of powers. (001-001)

Courage is initially undirected — a raw capacity, a "fulness" of power seeking an object. The critical turn is learning *where* to direct courage: not at external enemies but at the "virtuous dirtiness of the modern Yea and Nay," at lazy compromises, at the "tolerance and *largeur* of the heart that 'forgives' everything because it 'understands' everything" (001-001). Nietzschean courage is directed *against* the modern virtues themselves.

## Courage as Anti-Christian Virtue

Nietzsche positions courage in direct opposition to the Christian value system. Christianity, in his analysis, *hates* courage because courage is the virtue of those who do not need metaphysical comfort:

> Christian is all deadly enmity to the rulers of the earth, to the "aristocratic" — along with a sort of secret rivalry with them (—one resigns one's "body" to them; one wants *only* one's "soul"...). And Christian is all hatred of the intellect, of pride, of courage, of freedom, of intellectual *libertinage*. (021-001)

Here courage is listed alongside pride, freedom, and intellectual libertinage as what Christianity must destroy. These are the virtues of the *noble* — the type that affirms itself without needing a transcendent guarantor. Christianity hates courage because courageous people do not need priests.

## Courage and Health

Nietzsche's most striking deployment of courage connects it to *health* — specifically, the courage to *affirm* health against a religion that pathologizes it:

> We others, who have the *courage* for health *and* likewise for contempt, — we may well despise a religion that teaches misunderstanding of the body! that refuses to rid itself of the superstition about the soul! that makes a "virtue" of insufficient nourishment! that combats health as a sort of enemy, devil, temptation! (051-001)

"Courage for health" is an unusual formulation — health is not normally thought of as something requiring courage. But in a civilization shaped by Christianity, where health is suspect, the body is despised, and sickness is sanctified, the affirmation of health becomes a *courageous act*. Courage is needed to say Yes to the body, to strength, to well-being — against the millennia of training that say these are temptations.

The paired phrase "courage for health *and likewise* for contempt" is significant: the same courage that affirms health also permits *contempt* for what opposes health. Contempt (*Verachtung*) is not ressentiment — it is the noble affect of looking down from a height, the natural response of the healthy to the sickly.

## Courage and the History of Natural Values

In Nietzsche's genealogy of Jewish religion, courage appears as a marker of the *natural* phase — before the priestly denaturization of values:

> Originally, and above all in the time of the monarchy, Israel maintained the *right* attitude of things, which is to say, the natural attitude. Its Jahveh was an expression of its consciousness of power, its joy in itself, its hopes for itself: to him the Jews looked for victory and salvation... a god who *demands* — in place of a god who helps, who gives counsel, who is at bottom merely a name for every happy inspiration of courage and self-reliance. (025-001)

In the natural phase, the god is a *name for courage* — a projection of the people's own self-reliance. When the priestly denaturization occurs, this god of courage is replaced by a god of *demands* — a moralizing god who requires submission rather than inspiring self-reliance. The loss of courage and the loss of natural religion are the same event.

## The Courage-Doubt-Honesty Triad

Courage, [[doubt]], and [[honesty]] form an interlocking triad of epistemic virtues in Nietzsche's thought:

- **Courage** is the *capacity* — the strength to endure what truth-seeking reveals
- **Doubt** is the *method* — the refusal to close inquiry, the "will to doubt"
- **Honesty** is the *practice* — the actual intellectual conscience in operation

Without courage, doubt collapses into cynicism and honesty becomes cruel self-laceration. Without doubt, courage becomes reckless conviction and honesty becomes self-righteousness. Without honesty, courage and doubt remain merely intellectual postures without practical effect.

This triad is the counter-force to the Christian epistemic-vice triad of [[belief]] (closed certainty), [[conviction]] (resistance to revision), and [[faith]] (willful blindness). The [[war]] that Nietzsche wages is fought with these virtues as weapons.
