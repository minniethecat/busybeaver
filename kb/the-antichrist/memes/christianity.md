[[christianity|Christianity]] is, in Nietzsche's [[decadence|The Antichrist]], not merely a religion among others — it is the supreme institutional expression of life-denial, the organized carrier of décadence that has, for two millennia, taught Western humanity to despise the conditions of its own flourishing. Understood correctly, "Christianity" is a misunderstanding — there was only one Christian, [[jesus-christ]], and he died on the cross. What followed, built by [[paul]], is the institutional negation of everything Jesus lived.

## A Religion That Touches No Reality

Nietzsche's most damning charge is epistemological: Christianity is a system of belief that has no contact with reality at any point, and which collapses the moment reality asserts its rights.

> Such a religion as Christianity, which does not touch reality at a single point and which goes to pieces the moment reality asserts its rights at any point, must be inevitably the deadly enemy of the "wisdom of this world," which is to say, of _science_. (047-001)

This hostility to reality explains Christianity's structural opposition to science, to philology (which sees behind the "holy books"), and to medicine (which sees behind the physiological degeneration of the typical Christian). For Nietzsche, the philologian and the physician are, by the nature of their disciplines, _Antichrist_.

## The Institutional Structure of Décadence

| Dimension | Pre-Christian | Christian Transformation |
|-----------|--------------|------------------------|
| God-concept | Tribal/national deity, strong and proud | Universal "good" god, pale and weak |
| Morality | Noble morality: strength, pride, mastery | Slave morality: pity, humility, obedience |
| Truth | Reality-based, this-worldly | "Faith" as imperative, lying sanctified |
| Psychology | Instincts affirmed, life celebrated | Instincts denied, life slandered |
| Institution | No centralized priesthood | Universal priesthood, global organization |

## The Two Thousand Year Crime

Nietzsche frames Christianity's historical triumph as a civilizational catastrophe: the victory of weakness over strength, of sickness over health, of the "Chandala" morality over noble values. The "strong races of northern Europe" failed to repudiate the Christian god, and have paid for this failure ever since.

> A curse lies upon them because they were not equal to it; they made illness, decrepitude and contradiction a part of their instincts — and since then they have not managed to _create_ any more gods. Two thousand years have come and gone — and not a single new god! (019-001)

## Christianity vs. Buddhism

Nietzsche draws a sharp contrast between the two "religions of décadence" — Christianity and Buddhism — using the comparison to sharpen his critique of the former:

> Christianity promises everything, but _fulfils nothing_. (042-001)

Where Buddhism represents an honest, psychologically sophisticated response to suffering (promising nothing but delivering technique), Christianity represents fraud — promising eternal life, salvation, and cosmic justice while delivering only the psychological mechanisms of priestly control.

## The Total Critique

Nietzsche's attack on Christianity operates on every level simultaneously — historical, psychological, physiological, institutional. It is not a critique of Christian _beliefs_ (which Nietzsche regards as symptoms) but of the Christian _type_: the kind of human being produced by two millennia of anti-life training. Against this, Nietzsche poses the figure of the "Hyperborean" — the philosopher-physician who wields the knife against the accumulated infections of Christian morality.
