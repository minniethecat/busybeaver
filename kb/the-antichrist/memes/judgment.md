The concept of **judgment** — and specifically the **Last Judgment** — occupies a central place in Nietzsche's analysis of how the original teaching of [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] was corrupted by the [[ressentiment]] of his followers. In Nietzsche's reading, the idea of a final cosmic judgment is not part of Jesus' "glad tidings" but a later _revenge fantasy_ — the product of disciples who could not forgive the crucifixion.

## Judgment as Revenge: The Corruption of the Gospel

Nietzsche's most striking claim is that the entire apparatus of judgment — recompense, punishment, the Last Judgment — was introduced into Christianity _after_ the crucifixion, by disciples who could not accept their master's death:

> It was precisely the most unevangelical of feelings, _revenge_, that now possessed them. It seemed impossible that the cause should perish with his death: "recompense" and "judgment" became necessary (—yet what could be less evangelical than "recompense," "punishment," and "sitting in judgment"!). (040-001)

The original Gospel, in Nietzsche's reconstruction, was the _practice_ of a way of life — not a promise of future reward or punishment. Jesus "died as he lived and _taught_ — _not_ to 'save mankind,' but to show mankind how to live" (035-001). The doctrine of the Last Judgment replaces this existential teaching with a crude system of cosmic bookkeeping.

## The Last Judgment as Imaginary Revenge

Nietzsche identifies the psychological mechanism behind the invention of judgment:

| Element | Psychological Function |
|---------|----------------------|
| Future judgment | Defers justice to a time when the powerless will be vindicated |
| Eternal punishment | Inflicts imaginary revenge on enemies who escaped earthly consequences |
| The judge (Christ/God) | Elevates one's own resentment to divine decree |
| "Sitting in judgment" | Reverses the humiliation of being judged by the powerful |

The Last Judgment is the ultimate expression of what Nietzsche calls the "slave revolt in morality": the powerless, unable to enact revenge in this world, invent a _next_ world in which the cosmic order itself executes their vengeance.

## The Imaginary Character of Judgment

Nietzsche places judgment within his broader analysis of Christianity's detachment from reality:

> Under Christianity neither morality nor religion has any point of contact with actuality. It offers purely imaginary _causes_ ("God," "soul," "ego," "spirit," "free will" — or even "unfree"), and purely imaginary _effects_ ("sin," "salvation," "grace," "punishment," "forgiveness of sins"). (015-001)

Judgment belongs to the category of "purely imaginary effects." It is not something that happens in the world — it is a _story_ told about the world, designed to make the suffering of the powerless feel meaningful by imagining the future suffering of the powerful.

## Jesus' Anti-Judgment Teaching

Nietzsche contrasts the disciples' revenge-driven judgment doctrine with Jesus' own practice, which he interprets as the radical _renunciation_ of judgment:

> He does not resist; he does not defend his rights; he takes no steps to ward off the worst that can happen to him — more, he _provokes_ it.... And he prays, suffers and loves with those, _in_ those, who do him evil.... _Not_ to defend one's self, _not_ to show anger, _not_ to lay blame.... On the contrary, to submit even to the Evil One — to _love_ him. (035-001)

This is the anti-ressentiment teaching: the refusal to judge, the refusal to retaliate, the refusal even to distinguish between friend and enemy in a moral sense. The disciples, unable to maintain this extraordinary psychological discipline, reverted to the most primitive religious instinct — the demand that enemies be punished and friends vindicated. They "created a 'god' that met that need" (031-001).

## Judgment in Nietzsche's Final Condemnation

In his concluding verdict on Christianity, Nietzsche lists the Last Judgment among the instruments of priestly torture:

> The concepts "the other world," "the last judgment," "the immortality of the soul," the "soul" itself: they are all merely so many instruments of torture, systems of cruelty, whereby the priest becomes master and remains master. (062-001)

The Last Judgment is the ultimate priestly threat: not only will you be punished, but you will be punished _eternally_, by a cosmic tribunal whose verdict is already known. The [[priest]] alone can tell you how to escape this fate — and the price of escape is submission to priestly authority.
