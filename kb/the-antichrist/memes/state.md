**The State** — and its modern democratic form in particular — is, in Nietzsche's political critique, the secular continuation of Christianity by other means. The Christian doctrine of the equality of souls is the theological ancestor of modern egalitarianism, and the democratic State is its institutional expression. Nietzsche's analysis of the State is inseparable from his critique of Christianity: both are expressions of the _ressentiment_ of the weak organized into a system of power.

## Christianity as Political Dynamite

Nietzsche traces a direct line from the Christian doctrine of soul-equality to modern political revolution:

> The "equality of souls before God" — this fraud, this _pretext_ for the _rancunes_ of all the base-minded — this explosive concept, ending in revolution, the modern idea, and the notion of overthrowing the whole social order — this is _Christian_ dynamite.... (062-001)

The theological claim that every soul is equal before God becomes, when secularized, the political claim that every citizen is equal before the law — and eventually, that every person has equal rights to everything. For Nietzsche, this is not progress but _pathology_:

> The poisonous doctrine, "_equal_ rights for all," has been propagated as a Christian principle: out of the secret nooks and crannies of bad instinct Christianity has waged a deadly war upon all feelings of reverence and distance between man and man, which is to say, upon the first _prerequisite_ to every step upward, to every development of civilization — out of the _ressentiment_ of the masses it has forged its chief weapons against _us_, against everything noble, joyous and high-spirited on earth, against our happiness on earth. (043-001)

## The Sickness of Modern Politics

Nietzsche's diagnosis extends to the psychological condition of modern political life:

> Our politics is sick with this lack of courage! — The aristocratic attitude of mind has been undermined by the lie of the equality of souls; and if belief in the "privileges of the majority" makes and _will continue to make_ revolutions — it is Christianity, let us not doubt, and _Christian_ valuations, which convert every revolution into a carnival of blood and crime! (043-001)

Christianity, he insists, "is a revolt of all creatures that creep on the ground against everything that is _lofty_: the gospel of the 'lowly' _lowers_" (043-001). The political form of this revolt is democracy — the rule of the many over the few, of the mediocre over the exceptional.

## The Destruction of the Imperium Romanum

Nietzsche's primary historical exhibit for Christianity's political destructiveness is the fate of Rome. The _imperium Romanum_ was "the most magnificent form of organization under difficult conditions that has ever been achieved, and compared to which everything before it and after it appears as patchwork, bungling, _dilletantism_" (058-001). Yet Christianity destroyed it:

> Christianity was the vampire of the _imperium Romanum_, — overnight it destroyed the vast achievement of the Romans: the conquest of the soil for a great culture _that could await its time_. (058-001)

The mechanism was not military but psychological: Christianity alienated individuals from the Roman order by redirecting all loyalty and hope to the "beyond":

> These stealthy worms, which under the cover of night, mist and duplicity, crept upon every individual, sucking him dry of all earnest interest in _real_ things, of all instinct for _reality_ — this cowardly, effeminate and sugar-coated gang gradually alienated all "souls," step by step, from that colossal edifice. (058-001)

## The Order of Rank vs. Equality

Against Christian-democratic egalitarianism, Nietzsche posits an "order of rank" (_Rangordnung_) grounded in physiological reality:

> The order of castes, the _order of rank_, simply formulates the supreme law of life itself; the separation of the three types is necessary to the maintenance of society, and to the evolution of higher types, and the highest types — the _inequality_ of rights is essential to the existence of any rights at all. — A right is a privilege. (057-001)

This is not an arbitrary hierarchy but a recognition of natural differences. "In every healthy society there are three physiological types" (057-001) — the intellectual few, the warrior-guardians, and the productive many. Each has its own "hygiene," its own appropriate sphere, its own form of happiness. The modern State, by imposing a single standard of equality on all, makes health impossible for any.

## The Christian and the Anarchist

Nietzsche draws a provocative equivalence:

> The anarchist and the Christian have the same ancestry. (057-001)

> In point of fact, the end for which one lies makes a great difference: whether one preserves thereby or destroys. There is a perfect likeness between Christian and anarchist: their object, their instinct, points only toward destruction. (058-001)

> The Christian and the anarchist: both are _décadents_; both are incapable of any act that is not disintegrating, poisonous, degenerating, _blood-sucking_; both have an instinct of _mortal hatred_ of everything that stands up, and is great, and has durability, and promises life a future. (058-001)

The Christian destroys by directing all value to the beyond; the anarchist destroys by declaring war on all existing institutions. Both are expressions of the same underlying instinct: the hatred of the _well-constituted_ — of whatever is strong, durable, and life-affirming.

## The German Betrayal

Nietzsche reserves special venom for the Germans, whom he blames for destroying Europe's last chance to escape Christianity's political shadow — the Renaissance:

> The Germans have destroyed for Europe the last great harvest of civilization that Europe was ever to reap — the _Renaissance_. Is it understood at last, _will_ it ever be understood, _what_ the Renaissance was? _The transvaluation of Christian values_, — an attempt with all available means, all instincts and all the resources of genius to bring about a triumph of the _opposite_ values, the more _noble_ values.... (061-001)

Luther's Reformation was, in Nietzsche's reading, a rebellion _against_ the Renaissance — a restoration of Christian hegemony at the very moment it might have been overcome:

> Luther saw only the _depravity_ of the papacy at the very moment when the opposite was becoming apparent: the old corruption, the _peccatum originale_, Christianity itself, no longer occupied the papal chair! Instead there was life! Instead there was the triumph of life! (061-001)

The Renaissance — personified in the fantasy of Cesare Borgia as Pope — would have enthroned noble values at the heart of Christianity, effecting a transvaluation from within. Luther's Reformation saved Christianity from this fate and condemned Europe to centuries more of Christian political domination.
