# The Nebuly Coat

## Overview

The [[nebuly-coat|nebuly coat]] — "barry nebuly of six, argent and vert" (002-050) — is the heraldic emblem of the [[the-blandamer-family|Blandamer]] family and the novel's title image. More than a decorative motif, the nebuly coat functions as the gravitational centre around which the novel's themes of inheritance, identity, obsession, and genealogical mystery orbit. From the stained-glass window in [[cullerne-minster|Cullerne Minster]] to the papers that consume [[martin-joliffe|Martin Joliffe]]'s final years, the "sea-green and silver" (011-019) recurs as a visual leitmotif that connects every stratum of the narrative — architectural, genealogical, psychological.

## Heraldic Description

The term "nebuly" derives from the Latin *nebules*, meaning clouded or cloudy — a reference to the wavy, cloud-like line that divides the horizontal stripes of the coat of arms. As the [[lord-blandamer|Lord Blandamer]] explains: "Nebuly, you know, from the Latin *nebules*, cloudy" (002-052). The heraldic blazon — barry nebuly of six, argent and vert — describes six horizontal bars, alternately silver (argent) and green (vert), divided by a wavy (nebuly) line.

The coat appears in multiple locations within the novel's fictional world:

## Symbolic Meanings

### Inheritance and Legitimacy

The nebuly coat is first and foremost a signifier of the Blandamer inheritance. To possess the coat — by birth or by proof — is to possess [[fording|Fording]] and all its lands. The coat's heraldic authority is absolute within the novel's social world, yet its claim to legitimacy is precisely what the plot calls into question. Martin Joliffe's belief that *he* is the rightful bearer of the nebuly coat — "not a Joliffe at all, but a Blandamer" (003-052) — transforms the emblem from a mark of stable inheritance into a site of contested identity.

### Genealogical Obsession

For Martin Joliffe, the nebuly coat becomes an object of consuming obsession. "All these papers are nebuly coat — the sea-green and silver" (011-019), he tells Westray, revealing the lifelong research project that has consumed his reason. The coat has "blighted the very life out of him" (015-030) — the emblem of aristocratic identity becomes, for those who pursue it without possessing it, a destructive force. "The nebuly coat was too much for him" (011-022) stands as both epitaph and warning.

### Appearance and Reality

The novel plays on the heraldic meaning of "nebuly" — cloudy — as a metaphor for the ambiguity that surrounds the Blandamer inheritance. What appears clear in heraldic blazon (argent and vert, six bars) is, in genealogical reality, clouded by uncertain paternity, suppressed truths, and the gap between heraldic certainty and historical contingency. The "nebuly" line that divides the bars is a visual metaphor for the blurred boundary between legitimate and illegitimate, between appearance and reality.

## Narrative Function

The nebuly coat serves at least five distinct narrative functions across the novel:

1. **Title image** — It names the novel and establishes the heraldic/heritage theme from the first encounter
2. **Visual leitmotif** — The "sea-green and silver" recurs across settings (Minster, inn, Fording) and characters' speech, binding disparate narrative strands
3. **Plot mechanism** — Martin Joliffe's genealogical research into the coat generates the novel's central mystery
4. **Psychological symbol** — The coat's effect on those who pursue it (Martin, Miss Euphemia) dramatises the destructive power of genealogical obsession
5. **Thematic nexus** — It connects the novel's concerns with inheritance, class, identity, architecture, and the weight of the past

> "They're gone where they don't wear nebuly coats" (003-056)

This remark — made after Martin Joliffe's death — encapsulates the coat's double-edged symbolic force. It is at once an object of reverence and a burden from which death alone offers release.

## The Coat in the Novel's Structure

The nebuly coat is introduced early — in Chapter 2, when the [[edward-westray|organist]] first draws Westray's attention to the Blandamer aisle in Cullerne Minster (002-050). From that moment, references to the coat accumulate steadily through the novel's middle chapters (005, 008, 010, 011, 012, 013, 015, 016, 018, 019), reaching a visual crescendo in Chapter 21, where the coat "was many times repeated, besides being the motif of the silver border" (021-002) at Fording. This steady accumulation mirrors the genealogical obsession it represents — each mention adds another layer, another claimant, another shade of meaning to an emblem that is, by the novel's end, saturated with significance.
