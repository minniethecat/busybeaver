# Cullerne

## Overview

[[cullerne-town|Cullerne]] is the town in which *The Nebuly Coat* takes place, and it is far more than mere setting. Once a seaport of note — it "figures in history as a seaport of repute" (002-001) — Cullerne has declined into a provincial backwater whose empty streets and abandoned warehouses testify to departed commercial vitality. The town's decay is not scenic but structural: it parallels the attenuation of the [[the-blandamer-family|Blandamer]] line, the deterioration of [[cullerne-minster|Cullerne Minster]], and the broader historical process of English provincial decline that the novel records with melancholy precision.

## Physical Description

### The Old Quarter

The old town, where [[edward-westray|Westray]] and [[mr-sharnall|Mr. Sharnall]] walk on the night of Westray's arrival, is a landscape of abandonment:

> "There wasn't one house in ten with anyone in it now" (003-008)

The deserted streets, the "lines of what had once been a horse-tramway" (003-008) along the abandoned wharves, and the houses "with no glass in the windows" (003-006) compose a visual rhetoric of decay that establishes the novel's tonal register. The old quarter is the Cullerne of memory — a place where prosperity has ebbed, leaving only architectural shells.

### The New Town

Further up the hill lies the newer part of town, where "all we fashionables have moved" (003-008), as Sharnall notes with characteristic sardonicism. The new town's relative prosperity only accentuates the desolation below — the division between old and new Cullerne mapping the novel's broader concern with the uneven distributions of modernity.

### Bellevue Lodge

[[bellevue-lodge|Bellevue Lodge]], the lodging-house kept by [[miss-euphemia-joliffe|Miss Euphemia Joliffe]], occupies the transitional zone between old and new. "Miss Joliffe has christened it Bellevue Lodge" (003-025), the narrator notes — the aspirational name a gentle satirical touch that captures the Joliffes' precarious social position. The lodge is Westray's home throughout the restoration and the site of many of the novel's most intimate scenes.

### The Blandamer Arms

The [[the-blandamer-arms|Blandamer Arms]] inn, with "the green and silver of the nebuly coat itself" (004-037) on its signboard, is Cullerne's principal public house and the novel's primary social arena outside the church. It is here that [[lord-blandamer|Lord Blandamer]] stays, here that the town's collective opinion forms and circulates, and here that the novel's class dynamics are most visible — the publican's deference to the peer, the workmen's talk at the bar, the convergence of all social strata in the shared space of drink and conversation.

## Social Life

Cullerne's social life is structured around three institutions:

| Institution | Function | Key Figures |
|-------------|----------|-------------|
| Cullerne Minster | Religious and social centre | Canon Parkyn, Mr. Sharnall, the congregation |
| The Blandamer Arms | Public house and information exchange | The publican, the townspeople |
| Bellevue Lodge | Lodging-house and domestic sphere | Miss Euphemia Joliffe, Anastasia Joliffe, Westray |

The town's collective memory — preserved in the stories told by its inhabitants — is the medium through which the Blandamer mystery is transmitted. The verger's knowledge of the tower, the organist's bitterness about the restoration, the townspeople's speculation about Lord Blandamer — all compose a distributed archive of local knowledge that the novel draws upon as a kind of vernacular chorus.

## The Chimes of St. Sepulchre

The chimes of Cullerne Minster — to which the novel devotes an entire appendix ("Tunes Played by the Chimes of St. Sepulchre's Church at Cullerne," 026-001) — mark the rhythm of Cullerne life. The chimes are the town's sonic signature, their melodies woven into the fabric of daily existence. This musical dimension of Cullerne's identity — the chimes above, the organ within — gives the town an acoustic presence that complements its visual description and deepens the novel's immersive sense of place.

## Symbolic Significance

Cullerne's decay is the physical manifestation of the novel's central theme: the ebbing of vitality from institutions that once defined English life. The town is what the Blandamer line risks becoming — a hollow shell, its substance departed, its visible forms (the Minster, the abandoned wharves, the empty houses) testifying to a greatness that has receded into memory.

The town also figures the novel's ambivalent relationship to the provincial. Cullerne is at once an object of satirical observation — its social pretensions, its deference to rank — and a repository of authentic communal life. The novel's attitude toward the town, like its attitude toward the restoration project, is suspended between critique and affection.
