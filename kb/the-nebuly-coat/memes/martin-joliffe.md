# Martin Joliffe

## Overview

Martin Joliffe never appears directly in the novel — he is dead before the story begins — yet his presence haunts every page. His obsessive belief that he was not a Joliffe at all but a Blandamer, the rightful heir to Fording, set in motion the investigation that Westray inherits. The townspeople called him "Old Nebuly" and mocked him in the streets, but his tragedy is the novel's emotional core.

## Biography

As a boy, Martin Joliffe attended Cullerne Grammar School, earned a scholarship to Oxford, and "did still better there" (003-052). He seemed destined for success — "just when he seemed starting strong in the race of life, this nebuly coat craze seized him and crept over his mind, like the paralysis that crept over his body later on" (003-052).

His genealogical obsession consumed his life. He amassed "a mass of information" — papers upon papers of research, elaborating and correcting the published Blandamer genealogy (008-032). This "research and jargon about the nebuly coat" became "the staple of his conversation" (008-032). His quest took him across the country and consumed his half-sister's savings: "he was frittering away her savings" (003-056).

The townspeople's mockery was cruel and constant. Boys would bow in the streets and say "Good morning, Lord Blandamer" (003-056). His sister Anastasia "loved her half-brother dearly, and doesn't like his cranks being talked about" (003-056). In the end, as Mr. Sharnall puts it, "Martin's gone where they don't wear nebuly coats" (003-056).

## Relationships

Martin's most significant relationship was with his half-sister **Anastasia Joliffe**, whose savings he consumed and whose love he nevertheless retained. His papers, guarded by **Miss Euphemia Joliffe** after his death, become the documentary evidence that Westray must interpret. His story is first narrated by **Mr. Sharnall**, whose account of Martin's tragedy provides Westray — and the reader — with the essential backstory of the nebuly coat.

## Significance

Martin Joliffe is the novel's sacrificial figure — the person destroyed by the secret that the plot must resolve. His tragedy demonstrates the human cost of the obsession with lineage and inheritance that the novel anatomises. In a story concerned with legitimacy and its discontents, Martin represents the most extreme case: a man whose belief in an alternative identity consumed his actual life.
