# Bell Chimes

## Overview

The chimes of Saint Sepulchre's Church provide the auditory landscape of Cullerne in *The Nebuly Coat*. Unlike the silenced tower bells, which cannot be rung due to structural instability, the chimes play their melodies at regular intervals throughout the day and night, marking the passage of time and punctuating critical narrative moments. Their tunes — "Bermondsey" and others — become leitmotifs woven into the fabric of the story.

## In the Novel

The chimes' melodies are specified with unusual precision. The tune "Bermondsey" in D major, "scored in SATB for chimes in ³⁄₂ time" (024-034), is described as playing clearly at a moment of crisis. The Appendix to the novel catalogues the tunes played by the chimes, listing them with their key signatures and time signatures, lending the fictional chimes a documentary reality characteristic of Falkner's antiquarian sensibility.

The chimes mark domestic rituals and social rhythms. At Miss Euphemia Joliffe's lodging house, the chimes sounding a quarter to ten signal the end of the evening: "Miss Euphemia would say when the chimes sounded a quarter to ten, 'There are the chimes; it is a quarter to ten'" (016-041). They provide a structure of regularity against which the novel's disruptions and revelations are measured.

In moments of emotional intensity, the chimes take on an almost supernatural quality. "The deep and slow melodious chimes of Saint Sepulchre told the hour" (012-074), their measured pace contrasting with human urgency. The chimes begin to play "as clearly as ever" (024-033) at a pivotal moment when the plot's resolution hangs in the balance, their steady rhythm suggesting the persistence of order amid chaos.

The bells themselves embody the tension between presence and silence that pervades Cullerne Minster. The tower bells have been silent for thirty years — "them bells was never rung for thirty year" (002-040) — due to fears that the vibration would collapse the unstable tower. The chimes, however, continue their mechanical melodies, a quieter but persistent voice that cannot be silenced.

## Analysis

The chimes function as a form of mechanical memory — they play on regardless of human events, preserving the patterns of the past in their programmed melodies. Their presence in the novel reflects Falkner's interest in the intersection of the mechanical and the spiritual, the automated and the meaningful. The specific musical details (keys, time signatures, SATB scoring) ground the chimes in technical reality while their emotional effect transcends it.

The contrast between the silenced tower bells and the continuing chimes creates a hierarchy of sound that mirrors the novel's social hierarchy. The great bells — capable of being heard across the town, associated with celebration and communal gathering — are silenced by structural decay. The chimes — more modest, more regular, more domestic in scale — persist. This pattern of grand silence and modest continuity echoes the novel's treatment of the Blandamer legacy: the grand claims are unstable, while the quiet persistence of characters like Miss Euphemia endures.
