# Sea and the Maritime

## Overview

Cullerne "figures in history as a seaport of repute, having sent ships to the Crusades" (002-001), but by the time of the novel its maritime glory has faded. The sea is everywhere present as absence — in the abandoned wharves, in the salt wind that sweeps through the church, and in the sea-green bars of the nebuly coat itself. Falkner uses the maritime setting to establish a world in which the tides of fortune have receded, leaving behind a town haunted by its former prosperity.

## In the Novel

Westray's first experience of Cullerne is mediated through the sea air. As he and the organist leave the minster, "the wind was rising, and had in it a clean salt smell, that contrasted with the close and mouldering atmosphere of the church" (003-001). This sensory contrast between the living sea air and the decaying interior of the church establishes a pattern that runs throughout the novel — the tension between the vital and the moribund.

The abandoned wharves and warehouses along the river testify to Cullerne's maritime decline. Westray and Sharnall walk past "abandoned warehouses, square-fronted, and huddled together like a row of gigantic packing-cases" while "they could hear the gurgle of the current among the mooring-posts, and the flapping of the water against the quay wall" (003-008). The sea that once brought prosperity now only provides a melancholic soundtrack.

The sea-green of the nebuly coat links the Blandamer heraldry directly to the maritime. The "waving sea-green bars" (002-048) of the coat of arms connect aristocratic identity to the element that surrounds Cullerne. The shanty sung in the tavern — "Across the rolling sea" (003-021) — connects the town's working-class culture to the maritime world.

Cullerne Bay itself becomes the site of tragedy. It was there that Martin Joliffe's parents drowned: "his father and mother were drowned — drowned together in Cullerne Bay" (002-045). The sea that gives the town its identity also takes lives, embodying the double nature of nature itself.

## Analysis

The maritime setting is not decorative but structural. Cullerne's decline from prosperous seaport to decaying backwater mirrors the trajectory of the Blandamer family — once powerful, now attenuated. The sea's recession from the town's economic life parallels the receding claims of the legitimate Blandamer line. Falkner, who grew up in Dorchester and knew the English coast intimately, uses the maritime atmosphere to create what might be called an "archaeological" sense of place — a location defined as much by what has been lost as by what remains.

The sea also provides the novel's most consistent source of sensory imagery: the salt wind, the sound of water against the quay, the "clean" air that contrasts with the church's mouldering interior. This sensory grounding prevents the novel's Gothic elements from becoming entirely abstract — the physical world of Cullerne is always present, always tangible.
