# Social Class

## Overview

*The Nebuly Coat* offers a nuanced portrait of England's class system at the turn of the twentieth century. From the aristocratic Lord Blandamer at the apex to the lodging-house keeper Miss Joliffe and the humble workmen of Cullerne, the novel maps a complete social hierarchy. Class is not merely background but active force — it determines access, shapes speech, governs behaviour, and ultimately drives the novel's central conflicts over inheritance and legitimacy.

## In the Novel

The class structure of Cullerne is established through the church itself. The seating arrangements within the minster embody social distinction: certain pews are reserved as "the Fording seats, and kep' for Lord Blandamer's family" (007-035). When a stranger sits in them, the sexton protests until the stranger reveals himself to be Lord Blandamer himself — a revelation that instantly transforms transgression into privilege.

The novel is particularly attentive to the nuances of social position. Westray, as a professional architect, occupies the respectable middle class — educated enough to converse with the Rector and Sir George, yet not of the landed gentry. Mr. Sharnall, the organist, occupies a more precarious position: talented and educated but impoverished, bitter about his dependence on the very class system that has failed him. "If you've got the money, you're everything that's wonderful, and if you haven't, you may go rot," he laments (010-072).

The tavern loafer and the workman represent the lower orders, speaking in dialect that Falkner renders phonetically. Their gossip about the Blandamer family and the nebuly coat circulates through Cullerne's public houses, forming a parallel narrative to the official one preserved in church and manor house.

Lord Blandamer himself embodies the paradox of aristocratic status. Described in the village gossip as a figure of exotic accomplishment — "He could shoot, he could ride, he could play cricket for the county" (008-024) — he is simultaneously distant and omnipresent. His arrival at Cullerne, like his engagement to Anastasia, disrupts the established social order even as it ultimately reinforces it.

## Analysis

Falkner's depiction of class is distinguished by its lack of either sentimentality or polemic. The working-class characters are neither sanctified nor caricatured; the aristocrats are neither demonised nor glamorised. Instead, the novel presents class as a system that everyone understands and navigates, however unequally. The "volte face" of Cullerne society upon Anastasia's engagement is presented not as moral failing but as the system operating exactly as designed.

The novel's architectural metaphor extends to its treatment of class. Just as the central tower's instability threatens the physical church, the class system's rigidity creates structural tensions that threaten the social order. The Blandamer inheritance plot — with its questions of legitimate and illegitimate claims — represents a crack in the edifice of hereditary privilege that the novel explores but does not resolve.
