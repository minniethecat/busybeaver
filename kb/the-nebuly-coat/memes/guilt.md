# Guilt and Conscience

## Overview

Guilt pervades *The Nebuly Coat* as both psychological state and moral proposition. Characters are burdened by what they have done, what they have failed to do, and what they know. The novel maps guilt across social classes — from Miss Euphemia's petty economies of conscience to the mortal weight of Lord Blandamer's secret — creating a moral landscape as stratified as Cullerne's social hierarchy.

## In the Novel

Miss Euphemia Joliffe's guilt is the most domesticated form in the novel. She feels compelled "to free her conscience from the guilt of extravagance" (006-004), treating minor indulgences as matters requiring moral accounting. Her economies of guilt — the careful tracking of small expenditures against an inner ledger of virtue — represent the quotidian face of conscience in a world of limited means.

The organist Mr. Sharnall is "constantly guilty" of the "luxury" of bitterness (006-006), his resentment a moral failing that he simultaneously acknowledges and indulges. His guilt is not for any single act but for a disposition — the sour negativity that has become his character. This diffusion of guilt into temperament rather than action reflects the novel's sophisticated moral psychology.

The novel's most profound guilt attaches to Lord Blandamer and the secret of his identity. The text speaks of "dank dungeons of despair, or guilty mycethmi black as night" (017-005) in describing a state of moral torment that goes beyond ordinary remorse. The Gothic language signals that we have entered the territory of mortal sin — guilt that cannot be expiated through ordinary means.

Miss Anastasia's acknowledgment of her own culpability — "Yes, she was indeed a guilty woman. It was she that had 'killed'" (018-010) — introduces the language of criminal guilt into the domestic sphere. Whether the guilt is real or imagined, the subjective experience of it is powerful enough to reshape identity.

## Analysis

Falkner treats guilt with a complexity that distinguishes *The Nebuly Coat* from more schematic Victorian moralism. Guilt is not simply the consequence of wrongdoing but a condition that can precede and even cause transgression. Characters who believe themselves guilty act in ways that confirm their self-assessment, creating a feedback loop of moral deterioration.

The novel also explores the relationship between knowledge and guilt. Westray's gradual acquisition of dangerous knowledge — about the tower's instability, about Lord Blandamer's secret — implicates him in events he did not cause. To know is, in some measure, to be responsible — a proposition that gives the novel's epistemological structure its moral weight.
