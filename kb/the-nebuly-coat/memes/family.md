# Family and Kinship

## Overview

Every major character in *The Nebuly Coat* is defined by family. Lord Blandamer's identity derives entirely from his lineage; Martin Joliffe's madness is driven by his belief in an alternate family history; Anastasia Joliffe's social transformation is mediated through her half-brother's obsession and her own engagement. Family is not merely background but the primary determinant of identity, opportunity, and fate.

## In the Novel

The Blandamer family represents the apex of the kinship hierarchy — aristocratic, landed, heraldic. Their coat of arms, the nebuly coat, is the visual embodiment of family identity, displayed in the church window, on the gates of Fording, and in the hidden painting at Miss Joliffe's lodging house. Yet the Blandamer line is also attenuated, its legitimate continuity uncertain — a family as structurally precarious as the central tower.

The Joliffe family provides the novel's counterpoint to the Blandamers. Martin Joliffe's belief that he is "not a Joliffe at all, but a Blandamer" (003-052) represents the most extreme form of the kinship anxiety that pervades the novel — the terror of being born into the wrong family. His half-sister Anastasia, by contrast, achieves through marriage what Martin could not through research: legitimate entry into the Blandamer lineage.

Miss Euphemia Joliffe occupies the novel's most complex family position — guardian of her brother's memory, keeper of his papers, aunt to Anastasia. Her fierce protectiveness of family secrets reflects a loyalty that transcends social calculation. She represents a form of kinship that is based not on advantage but on duty and love.

## Analysis

Falkner presents family as both prison and possibility. For Martin Joliffe, the belief in an alternative family history becomes a form of madness that destroys him. For Anastasia, family is the vehicle of social ascent through marriage to Lord Blandamer. The novel suggests that family identity is simultaneously the most real and the most constructed aspect of social existence — a paradox that the Blandamer inheritance plot dramatises with tragic intensity.
