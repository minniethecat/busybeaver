# Decay and Ruin

## Overview

Decay is the governing aesthetic of *The Nebuly Coat*. From the opening description of Cullerne Minster — with its "close and mouldering atmosphere" (003-001) — to the abandoned warehouses along the wharf and the dilapidated state of the organ, the novel's world is one of pervasive decline. Falkner presents decay not as a moral condition but as a material fact, the inevitable fate of all things built by human hands.

## In the Novel

The former church of Saint Sepulchre, now fallen "from its high estate," embodies the melancholy of architectural decline. "It was not ruinous, but Ichabod was written on its walls" (003-023) — the biblical reference to departed glory capturing the sense of a sacred space that has lost its dignity without quite becoming a ruin.

The abandoned quarter of Cullerne through which Westray and Sharnall walk presents urban decay in its most concentrated form. "There wasn't one house in ten with anyone in it now" (003-008), Sharnall explains, describing the flight of the fashionable classes to higher ground. The empty houses, the vanished glass from windows, the "lines of what had once been a horse-tramway" — all testify to a town that has shrunk within its former boundaries.

The organ that Mr. Sharnall plays is itself a ruin in miniature. "The naturals are worn down to the quick, you can see the wood in the gutters of the keys, and the pedal-board's too short and all to pieces" (003-003). The instrument's decay is both material and metaphorical — "the organ's like me — old, neglected, worn-out." The equation of architectural with human decay is made explicit.

The Blandamer family's history is entangled with ruin in a supernatural register. "Church lands brought ruin to their new owner's family" (007-003) — the suggestion that the appropriation of ecclesiastical property carries a curse that works itself out across generations. The dirge played by the wind "in a ruined window" (007-018) sonically embodies the Gothic equation of decay with the uncanny.

## Analysis

Falkner's treatment of decay reflects his deep knowledge of architectural history and preservation. As an antiquary and the author of studies of Oxfordshire and Berkshire churches, he understood decay not as abstract metaphor but as a practical problem of maintenance and restoration. This technical knowledge gives the novel's descriptions of architectural decay their documentary precision.

Yet decay in the novel is never merely architectural. The physical deterioration of Cullerne's buildings mirrors the attenuation of its social fabric — the decline of the Blandamer line, the poverty of the organist, the empty houses of the old town. Falkner presents a world in which everything is gradually subsiding, where entropy is the only reliable constant. The restoration project that brings Westray to Cullerne is an attempt to arrest this process — an attempt whose outcome the novel leaves profoundly ambiguous.
