# Miss Euphemia Joliffe

## Overview

Miss Euphemia Joliffe runs the lodging house at Bellevue Lodge where Westray resides during his time in Cullerne. She is the guardian of her brother Martin's genealogical papers — documents that hold the key to the Blandamer mystery — and the aunt of Anastasia, whose engagement to Lord Blandamer transforms the family's fortunes. Quiet, proper, and fiercely protective of her family's secrets, Miss Euphemia represents the domestic virtues that sustain the novel's moral world.

## Biography

Miss Euphemia's life has been shaped by the tragedy of her brother Martin. His madness and death left her as the keeper of his papers and the guardian of his memory. She guards these documents with intense privacy, treating them as holding "so momentous a secret" (008-042). Her careful economies — tracking every small expenditure against "the guilt of extravagance" (006-004) — reflect a life of genteel poverty maintained through scrupulous self-discipline.

Lord Blandamer's visit to Bellevue Lodge is the most dramatic moment of her life. His presentation of a card bearing only "Lord Blandamer" — "in the most unpretending and unobtrusive style" (008-043) — renders her almost speechless: "Lord Blandamer!" she faltered (008-044). Her subsequent role as chief celebrant of the engagement — discussing "each and every detail" from "the buttons on Lord Blandamer's boots to the engagement-ring on Anastasia's finger" (019-059) — reveals her capacity for vicarious enjoyment of social triumph.

The hidden painting in her house — the picture of the nebuly coat concealed beneath flowers — represents the literal embodiment of the family secret she guards. When Westray finally recognises its significance, the knowledge transforms his understanding of the Blandamer mystery.

## Relationships

Miss Euphemia's loyalty to her brother **Martin Joliffe** transcends his madness and the financial ruin he caused. Her care for her niece **Anastasia** is unwavering, and her pride in the Blandamer engagement is one of the novel's most human moments. With **Westray**, she maintains the formal relationship of landlady to lodger, though her guard gradually lowers as the novel progresses.

## Significance

Miss Euphemia represents the domestic sphere as a site of moral seriousness. Her guardianship of the Blandamer papers — and of the hidden painting — gives her a power within the novel's economy of secrets that her social position would otherwise deny. She embodies the form of duty that is performed without public recognition, sustained by love rather than obligation.

## Key Quotes
