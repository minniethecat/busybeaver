# The Nebuly Coat Window

## Overview

The [[the-nebuly-coat-window|Nebuly Coat Window]] in the south transept of [[cullerne-minster|Cullerne Minster]] is the novel's most significant visual artefact — a medieval stained-glass work depicting the [[the-nebuly-coat|nebuly coat]] in "the sea-green and silver" (018-060) that names the novel and anchors its heraldic symbolism. Attributed to the fictional Abbot Vinnicomb, the window is simultaneously a work of ecclesiastical art, a heraldic document, and — in its threatened state — a symbol of the decay that the [[restoration|restoration]] project seeks to arrest.

## Location and Description

The window occupies the end of the south transept of Cullerne Minster, in the aisle known locally as the Blandamer aisle (002-032). It is first introduced when the organist draws Westray's attention to it:

> "...good painted glass in this end window of the transept" (002-047)

The window depicts the Blandamer arms — "barry nebuly of six, argent and vert" (002-050) — the heraldic device that gives the novel its title. The sea-green and silver glass catches the light in a way that makes the coat of arms appear to shimmer, as if the heraldic bars were moving with the cloud-like undulation that "nebuly" etymologically implies.

The window is repeatedly associated with the evening light that transforms the Minster interior. In a characteristic passage, the "pinnacles of Saint Sepulchre's tower shone as if they had been regilt" (004-032) — the light that passes through the transept window illuminating the entire church with the Blandamer colours.

## Artistic and Heraldic Significance

As a work of medieval craftsmanship, the window represents the integration of heraldic and ecclesiastical art that characterised the great churches of medieval England. The Blandamer arms are not merely decorative but documentary: the window asserts the Blandamer presence in the Minster, recording the family's patronage in the most permanent medium available to medieval culture.

| Aspect | Significance |
|--------|-------------|
| Heraldic | Records the Blandamer arms in their most authoritative form |
| Artistic | Medieval stained glass, attributed to Abbot Vinnicomb |
| Architectural | Anchors the Blandamer aisle, defining the south transept's visual identity |
| Historical | Documents the Blandamer patronage of the Minster across centuries |
| Symbolic | The decay of the window mirrors the decay of the Blandamer presence |

## Decay and Restoration

Like the Minster itself, the window has suffered from centuries of neglect. The restoration project that brings [[edward-westray|Westray]] to Cullerne encompasses the window's repair — a physical mending that parallels the genealogical restoration that [[lord-blandamer|Lord Blandamer]]'s arrival attempts. To restore the window is to restore the visible sign of Blandamer legitimacy; to let it decay is to let the heraldic record fade into illegibility.

The window's precarious state mirrors the ambiguous condition of the Blandamer inheritance itself. Just as the glass may shatter, so may the genealogical claim it represents. The restoration of one becomes, symbolically, the restoration of the other.

## Association with Martin Joliffe

For [[martin-joliffe|Martin Joliffe]], the window is a constant provocation — a visible, public assertion of the Blandamer identity that he believes should be his own. The "sea-green and silver" (018-060) shines down on the organist's daily practice, a reminder of the inheritance denied him. The window thus functions as a silent character in the genealogical drama, its unchanging heraldic assertion standing in stark contrast to the contested, unstable claims of the living.
