# Marriage

## Overview

Marriage in *The Nebuly Coat* operates as both a social institution and a narrative mechanism. The central romance between Lord Blandamer and Anastasia Joliffe provides the emotional arc of the novel while serving as the vehicle through which questions of class, legitimacy, and inheritance are ultimately resolved. Falkner presents marriage not as an escape from social constraints but as the arena in which those constraints are most vividly enacted.

## In the Novel

The marriage plot centers on Anastasia Joliffe, the daughter of a lodging-house keeper, whose engagement to Lord Blandamer scandalises and transforms Cullerne society. The engagement itself was "the secret of the matter" for some time before becoming public (019-058). Before the engagement was announced, Anastasia occupied an ambiguous social position — connected to the Blandamer mystery through her half-brother Martin's obsession, yet excluded from polite society by her family's reduced circumstances.

The announcement of the engagement produces an immediate and unashamed social realignment. As the narrator observes with satirical precision, "The ladies who had contemned a lodging-house keeper's daughter courted the betrothed of a peer. From themselves they did not disguise the motive for this change, they did not even attempt to find an excuse in public. They simply executed their volte face simultaneously and with most commendable regularity" (019-059).

Miss Euphemia Joliffe, Anastasia's aunt, becomes the chief celebrant and narrator of the engagement's details. "Each and every detail was conscientiously considered and enlarged upon, from the buttons on Lord Blandamer's boots to the engagement-ring on Anastasia's finger" (019-059). The ring itself — an emerald surrounded by diamonds in green and white, the colours of the nebuly coat — physically embodies the fusion of romantic love with heraldic inheritance.

Marriage also functions negatively in the novel through its absence or failure. The late Mrs. Blandamer's death left Lord Blandamer without heir or partner, creating the vacuum of legitimacy that Martin Joliffe attempted to fill. Mr. Sharnall's bitter loneliness — "as lonely, and have nothing to look forward to" (003-003) — represents the desolation of life without familial ties. For those beneath the gentry, marriage could be a source of anxiety: Canon Parkyn's early departure from an event because "he had an engagement which would occupy him for an hour" (012-037) reflects how clerical and social obligations intertwined with domestic arrangements.

## Analysis

Falkner's treatment of marriage reflects the Victorian novel's preoccupation with the institution while maintaining an ironic distance. The social climbing that follows Anastasia's engagement is presented with comic clarity, yet the narrator's tone stops short of moral condemnation. The ladies of Cullerne are satirised not for their hypocrisy — which is presented as natural and almost innocent — but for the transparency of their motives.

The marriage between Lord Blandamer and Anastasia also serves a structural function, resolving the novel's competing claims to the Blandamer legacy. Where Martin Joliffe's genealogical obsession led only to madness and death, Anastasia's marriage achieves through love and legitimacy what her half-brother could not through research and litigation. The novel thus suggests that inheritance is ultimately a matter of social recognition rather than documentary proof.
