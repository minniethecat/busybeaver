# Money and Wealth

## Overview

Money flows through *The Nebuly Coat* as an undercurrent beneath every social transaction. The restoration of Cullerne Minster depends upon fundraising and patronage; the Blandamer inheritance represents wealth on an aristocratic scale; the poverty of characters like Mr. Sharnall and the Joliffes shapes their choices and their bitterness. Falkner, writing at the height of the Edwardian era, presents wealth not as a moral failing but as the inescapable medium of social existence.

## In the Novel

The restoration fund provides the novel's most explicit treatment of money as social force. The Rector has been "very successful in collecting money for the restoration" (001-005), establishing the church's dependence on the generosity of patrons. Lord Blandamer's failure to contribute — "I had hoped that Lord Blandamer would have contributed handsomely to the restoration fund" (002-035) — becomes a measure of his ambiguous relationship to Cullerne and its institutions.

The Blandamer fortune itself exerts a gravitational pull on the narrative. Fording, the Blandamer estate with its "stately house" visible "on a clear day from Cullerne" (008-023), represents wealth in its most established form — landed, hereditary, and imposing. The contrast between Fording and the Joliffes' lodging house maps the economic extremes of the novel's world.

Mr. Sharnall gives voice to the novel's most bitter critique of wealth. "No one's thought anything of today, who can't build a church or a museum or a hospital," he rails. "If you've got the money, you're everything that's wonderful, and if you haven't, you may go rot" (010-072). His diatribe captures the transformation of philanthropy into a form of social display, where public benefaction serves private vanity.

The economics of Martin Joliffe's obsession are particularly poignant. His genealogical research consumed not only his own reason but his half-sister's savings: "he was frittering away her savings" (003-056). The quest for the Blandamer inheritance, which promised fabulous wealth, instead produced only poverty and madness.

## Analysis

Falkner treats money with a clear-eyed realism that distinguishes *The Nebuly Coat* from both the moralising tradition that condemns wealth and the sentimental tradition that ignores it. Money is simply the medium through which social life operates — necessary for the restoration of churches, for the maintenance of estates, for the basic dignity of characters like the organist. The novel's critique is directed not at wealth itself but at a society that has made wealth the sole measure of human worth.

The novel also registers the changing nature of wealth at the turn of the century. The Blandamer fortune is old money — landed, heraldic, inherited. But the novel's world also contains new commercial wealth, the professional income of men like Westray, and the precarious respectability of those who, like the Joliffes, have fallen from prosperity. This economic diversity reflects the transitional moment between Victorian and Edwardian social orders.
