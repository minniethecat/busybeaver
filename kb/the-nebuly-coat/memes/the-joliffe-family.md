# The Joliffe Family

## Overview

The [[the-joliffe-family|Joliffe family]] of [[cullerne-town|Cullerne]] occupies the novel's middle social stratum — neither the aristocracy of the [[the-blandamer-family|Blandamers]] nor the rural poor, but the respectable trade and professional class whose lives are irrevocably altered by the Blandamer presence. Comprising [[miss-euphemia-joliffe|Miss Euphemia Joliffe]] (lodging-house keeper), [[martin-joliffe|Martin Joliffe]] (organist and amateur genealogist), and [[anastasia-joliffe|Anastasia Joliffe]] (whose engagement to [[lord-blandamer|Lord Blandamer]] transforms the family's social position), the Joliffes are the novel's most fully realised portrait of a provincial family navigating the treacherous currents of class, inheritance, and identity.

## The Three Joliffes

### Miss Euphemia Joliffe

[[miss-euphemia-joliffe|Miss Euphemia Joliffe]] keeps [[bellevue-lodge|Bellevue Lodge]], the lodging-house where [[edward-westray|Westray]] boards during the restoration. Described with the novel's characteristic blend of sympathy and satirical precision, Miss Euphemia represents the economic precarity of the unmarried gentlewoman. She has kept the lodging-house for fifteen years (003-038), and her life is described as "a precious hard life" (003-047) — harder even than that of the men around her.

Her reaction to Lord Blandamer's calling card — "she faltered, as soon as she could command her voice" (008-044) — is the novel's most concentrated image of the quasi-religious awe that aristocratic identity commands in Cullerne.

### Martin Joliffe

[[martin-joliffe|Martin Joliffe]], the organist of [[cullerne-minster|Cullerne Minster]], is the novel's tragic figure — a man consumed by genealogical obsession. His belief that he is "not a Joliffe at all, but a Blandamer, and rightful heir to Fording" (003-052) drives him to decades of research and, the novel implies, to the edge of madness. The boys of Cullerne call him "Old Nebuly" (003-056) — a nickname that fuses his identity with the [[the-nebuly-coat|heraldic emblem]] of the family he believes to be his own.

> "The nebuly coat was too much for him" (011-022)

This epitaph — delivered after Martin's death — is at once literal (the genealogical research destroyed him) and symbolic: the coat of arms that should signify identity and belonging becomes, for those outside its legitimate line, a force of destruction.

### Anastasia Joliffe

[[anastasia-joliffe|Anastasia Joliffe]] is the novel's Cinderella figure — Miss Euphemia's niece, whose beauty and gentleness attract Lord Blandamer's attention and result in an engagement that reconfigures the social map of Cullerne. Her elevation from Bellevue Lodge to [[fording|Fording]] is the novel's most dramatic instance of social mobility, and the novel treats it with characteristic ambiguity: is it a fairy tale or a form of absorption, the Blandamer line consuming the Joliffe line through marriage?

## Class Position and Social Mobility

The Joliffes embody the novel's exploration of class in provincial England:

| Family Member | Class Position | Trajectory |
|--------------|---------------|------------|
| Miss Euphemia Joliffe | Genteel poverty — lodging-house keeper | Static; economic precarity |
| Martin Joliffe | Professional (organist) with aristocratic claim | Downward; consumed by genealogical obsession |
| Anastasia Joliffe | Lower-middle-class daughter | Upward; engagement to a peer |

The family's internal diversity illustrates the novel's understanding of class as dynamic rather than static — a system in which individuals can rise, fall, or be consumed by the attempt to move at all.

## Relationship to the Blandamers

The Joliffe-Blandamer relationship is the novel's central genealogical knot. Martin Joliffe's belief in his Blandamer descent means that the two families are, in his theory, one — the Joliffes are the true Blandamers, and the present Lord Blandamer is a usurper. Lord Blandamer's engagement to Anastasia can be read as the resolution of this claim through marriage: the Blandamer line absorbs the Joliffe line, neutralising the genealogical threat through social alliance.
