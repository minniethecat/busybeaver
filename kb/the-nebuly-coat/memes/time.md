# Time and Temporality

## Overview

*The Nebuly Coat* is deeply concerned with time — not as abstract philosophy but as lived experience. The chimes of Saint Sepulchre mark the hours with mechanical regularity; the decaying fabric of the church records the passage of centuries; the Blandamer inheritance represents the persistence of medieval arrangements into the modern world. Falkner presents a temporality in which past, present, and future are not distinct realms but interpenetrating layers of a single reality.

## In the Novel

The chimes provide the novel's most insistent temporal marker. They sound the quarters and the hours, punctuating conversation, marking bedtimes, and accompanying moments of crisis. "The deep and slow melodious chimes of Saint Sepulchre told the hour" (012-074) — their pace suggesting a time that is measured, deliberate, outside human urgency.

The church itself embodies what might be called "deep time" — the centuries-long duration of a medieval building. The central tower was built in an era whose engineering practices are now lost; its weakness is the consequence of decisions made by builders long dead. This architectural temporality — in which the past is structurally present in the physical world — is central to the novel's vision.

The thirty-year silence of the tower bells represents a specific durations of suspended time. "Them bells was never rung for thirty year" (002-040) — thirty years during which the town has lived without the sound that once defined its acoustic identity. This silence is itself a measure of decay, a temporal wound in the life of the community.

## Analysis

Falkner's treatment of time reflects the late Victorian and Edwardian preoccupation with historical continuity and rupture. The novel was published in 1903, at a moment when England was acutely conscious of its medieval heritage and its modern transformations. The restoration of Cullerne Minster — an attempt to preserve the medieval past for the modern future — embodies this historical self-consciousness.
