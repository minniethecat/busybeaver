# The Nebuly Coat

## Overview

The Nebuly Coat is the heraldic device of the Blandamer family, described as "barry nebuly of six, argent and vert" — six wavy bars of silver (white) and green (002-050). The term "nebuly" derives from the Latin *nebulum* or *nebulus*, meaning cloud, referring to the wavy, cloud-like outline of the bars that represent cumulus clouds (002-052). As both the novel's title and its central symbolic motif, the nebuly coat weaves together themes of aristocratic inheritance, architectural preservation, genealogical obsession, and the destructive power of fixations upon the past.

## In the Novel

The nebuly coat is introduced in the opening chapters as organist Mr. Sharnall explains the heraldry of Cullerne Minster to the young architect Westray. Sharnall notes that "there have been queer tales told of that shield, and maybe there are queerer yet to be told," observing that it has been "stamped for good or evil on this church, and on this town, for centuries" (002-050).

The coat becomes inseparable from the tragedy of Martin Joliffe, the organist's predecessor. According to Sharnall, Joliffe became convinced he was not a Joliffe at all but a Blandamer and the rightful heir to Fording. This belief — described as a "nebuly coat craze" — "seized him and crept over his mind, like the paralysis that crept over his body later on" (003-052). The townspeople of Cullerne mockingly called him "Old Nebuly" and boys would bow in the streets saying "Good morning, Lord Blandamer" (003-056).

The physical emblem appears throughout the novel's settings. In Cullerne Minster, the sea-green and silver shield shines in the great transept window — Abbot Vinnicomb's window — where "the sea-green and silver of the nebuly coat was many times repeated, beside figuring in chief at the head of the window" (021-002). At Fording, the Blandamer estate, the coat is set in ironwork over the great gates (023-023). In Miss Joliffe's lodging house, a hidden painting bearing the nebuly coat bars conceals a crucial secret about the Blandamer lineage (023-058).

Mr. Sharnall's bitter feelings toward the emblem capture its oppressive presence: "Sunlight and moonlight, it is always there," he cries, wishing all Blandamers "were wrapped up in their nebuly coat for a shroud" (010-072).

## Analysis

The nebuly coat functions as what might be termed a "hereditary MacGuffin" — a symbol whose power lies not in its intrinsic value but in what it drives characters to do. For Martin Joliffe, the coat represents a birthright denied, consuming his reason and his sister's savings in a futile genealogical quest. Westray observes that Joliffe's papers contained "a mass of information" — elaborate corrections to the published Blandamer genealogy, the product of obsessive "research and jargon about the nebuly coat which formed the staple of his conversation" (008-032).

The cloud etymology is thematically significant. Just as clouds are insubstantial and shifting, the claims to the Blandamer inheritance prove elusive and dangerous to grasp. The "wavy outline" of the nebuly bars mirrors the uncertain boundaries between legitimate and illegitimate claims, between appearance and reality, that pervade the novel's mystery plot.

The coat's colours — sea-green and silver — recur as a visual leitmotif. The green and silver appear in the stained glass, in the engagement ring Lord Blandamer gives Anastasia ("green and white being the colours of his lordship's shield, what they call the nebuly coat" (019-059)), and in the very atmosphere of scenes where Blandamer secrets surface. This chromatic repetition binds the novel's visual world to its thematic core.

## Related Concepts

- **Inheritance and Legitimacy** — The nebuly coat is the visual marker of legitimate Blandamer descent, making it the object of Martin Joliffe's genealogical obsession and the silent witness to Lord Blandamer's secret.
- **Architectural Heritage** — The coat appears most prominently in Cullerne Minster's stained glass and stonework, linking family lineage to the physical fabric of the church that Westray is tasked with restoring.
- **Class and Social Ambition** — The coat represents the aristocratic status that characters either possess, claim, or covet, driving the novel's exploration of England's rigid class hierarchy.
- **Obsession and Madness** — As Sharnall's account of Martin Joliffe demonstrates, the nebuly coat becomes a destructive fixation — a symbol that literally "turns heads."
