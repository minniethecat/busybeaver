# Secrecy and Concealment

## Overview

*The Nebuly Coat* is a novel built upon secrets. The identity of Lord Blandamer, the true nature of the codicil, the hidden painting beneath Miss Joliffe's flowers, and the structural weakness of the central tower — all are truths concealed, waiting to be uncovered. Falkner structures the narrative as a series of revelations, each disclosure deepening rather than resolving the mystery, until the final catastrophe brings all hidden things to light.

## In the Novel

The most literal secret in the novel is the hidden painting in Miss Joliffe's lodging house. The picture of the nebuly coat is concealed beneath "the flowers" — a decorative covering that hides the crucial evidence of the Blandamer lineage (023-058). When Westray finally recognises the significance of the moulding and the size of the picture, he realises "that the green wriggling caterpillar was but a bar of the nebuly coat" (023-058). The secret, once revealed, transforms everything.

Miss Euphemia Joliffe guards her brother's papers with fierce privacy, treating them as "so momentous a secret" (008-042). These documents contain Martin Joliffe's genealogical research — the evidence of his lifelong obsession with proving his Blandamer claim. The papers represent knowledge that could destabilise the existing order, and their guardianship by Miss Euphemia gives her a power that her social position would otherwise deny.

The narrative's treatment of secrecy extends to characters' internal lives. Miss Anastasia Joliffe "had unburdened herself of her secret" (019-039) in confiding her engagement, suggesting that secrecy is a burden from which characters seek relief. Yet the novel also recognises that some secrets are maintained not from malice but from shame or self-protection — as when Westray acknowledges that people are "secretly occupied with some matter that fills their whole mind" (016-065) even in company.

The architect Westray himself becomes a keeper of secrets, burdened with knowledge he cannot share. When Lord Blandamer reveals damning information, Westray "longed to cry out, 'For God's sake, do not tell me these things; do not give me this evidence against yourself!'" (023-058). To know a secret is to be implicated in it — a moral proposition that runs through the novel.

## Analysis

Falkner uses secrecy as both theme and narrative technique. The reader, like Westray, is positioned as an outsider who gradually gains access to Cullerne's hidden truths. This narrative strategy creates suspense but also epistemological uncertainty — we can never be sure that we have reached the final secret, that some further revelation does not await.

The novel's treatment of secrecy reflects the Gothic tradition in which hidden crimes and buried pasts eventually surface. Yet Falkner's secrets are notably secular — they concern property, identity, and inheritance rather than supernatural forces. The Gothic machinery of hidden documents and ancestral curses is repurposed to explore the more modern anxieties of legitimacy and social identity.
