# Janaway

## Overview

Janaway represents the novel's working-class voice — a Cullerne labourer whose practical knowledge of the church building and its history provides Westray with information unavailable from official sources. His dialect speech and local perspective offer an alternative to the professional discourse of architects and clergy.

## Biography

Janaway has worked at the church for years and possesses intimate knowledge of its physical condition. His testimony about the bells — "them bells was never rung for thirty year" (002-040) — establishes a crucial timeline. His warning about the tower — "These yere old tower'll never stand it" (002-038) — expresses in dialect what Westray's professional training tells him in technical language.

His relationship with the church authorities is marked by deference mixed with quiet independence. He knows what he knows, regardless of what his social superiors may wish to hear.

## Relationships

Janaway's most significant interaction is with **Westray**, to whom he provides the ground-level knowledge that complements the architect's professional expertise. His warnings about the tower, delivered in the broad dialect that Falkner renders phonetically, carry an authority born of experience rather than education.

## Significance

Janaway embodies the novel's recognition that knowledge is not the exclusive possession of the educated classes. His practical wisdom about the church building speaks to a theme that runs throughout the novel: the importance of listening to those who know things from the ground up, not just from the top down.

## Key Quotes

> "These yere old tower'll never stand it. I see him rock in the great gale" (002-038).

> "Them bells was never rung for thirty year" (002-040).
