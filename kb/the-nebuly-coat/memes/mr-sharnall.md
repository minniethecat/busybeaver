# Mr. Sharnall

## Overview

Mr. Sharnall is the organist of Cullerne Minster — an old, poor, and embittered musician whose sharp tongue and deep knowledge of the town's history make him Westray's indispensable guide. He is the novel's most vivid secondary character, a figure of thwarted talent and corrosive resentment whose rants against wealth and privilege give voice to the novel's social criticism.

## Biography

Sharnall has served as organist at Cullerne Minster for decades, playing an instrument that is itself a ruin — "the naturals are worn down to the quick, you can see the wood in the gutters of the keys" (003-003). His identification with the decaying organ is explicit: "the organ's like me — old, neglected, worn-out. I wish I was dead" (003-003).

He is the keeper of Cullerne's oral history — the story of Martin Joliffe's madness, the legend of the nebuly coat, the gossip about Lord Blandamer. His explanation of the Blandamer arms to Westray — "barry nebuly of six, argent and vert" (002-050) — establishes the novel's central symbol. His bitterness is directed particularly at the wealthy: "If you've got the money, you're everything that's wonderful, and if you haven't, you may go rot" (010-072).

## Relationships

Sharnall's friendship with **Westray** is the novel's most developed relationship. He pilots the young architect through Cullerne's dark streets, shares his knowledge of the church's history, and provides the emotional counterpoint to Westray's professional reserve. Despite his bitterness, Sharnall retains a capacity for genuine connection.

His knowledge of **Martin Joliffe**'s tragedy — "this nebuly coat craze seized him and crept over his mind, like the paralysis that crept over his body later on" (003-052) — makes him the novel's most reliable narrator of the Blandamer mystery's human cost.

## Significance

Sharnall represents the human cost of the social order that the novel anatomises. His poverty, his neglected instrument, his bitter eloquence — all testify to a system that values wealth above talent. He is the novel's conscience, its most honest voice, and its most poignant figure of wasted potential.

## Key Quotes

> "Ah well! the organ's like me — old, neglected, worn-out. I wish I was dead" (003-003).

> "There have been queer tales told of that shield, and maybe there are queerer yet to be told" (002-050).

> "I hate all Blandamers. I wish they were as dead and buried as dodos" (010-072).
