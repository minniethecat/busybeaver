# Fate and Fortune

## Overview

The characters of *The Nebuly Coat* move within a world shaped by forces beyond their control. The Blandamer inheritance was determined generations ago; the central tower's weakness was built into its foundations centuries past; the chimes of Saint Sepulchre mark time with mechanical indifference to human hopes. Fate in the novel is not supernatural decree but the accumulated weight of past decisions pressing upon the present.

## In the Novel

Westray's arrival in Cullerne has the quality of fate — he is sent by his firm, not by choice, yet the commission entangles him in a web of relationships and secrets from which he cannot extricate himself. The novel traces how apparently contingent events — a professional assignment, a chance conversation — become the instruments of an unfolding destiny.

The organist Sharnall is perhaps the character most explicitly marked by fate. He "accepted his fate with less reluctance than he might have been expected to show" (014-054), suggesting a weary resignation to forces he cannot resist. His life has been shaped by economic necessity and social neglect — fates imposed by class rather than by cosmic design.

The novel's epigraph and occasional verse fragments introduce a more explicitly tragic register. The line "Such evil fate in store" (007-005) appears as a kind of choric commentary, reminding the reader that tragedy awaits. Miss Euphemia's "fateful gesture" (009-007) in putting down her tray signals a moment of narrative weight — a small action freighted with consequence.

The central tower itself is the novel's most potent symbol of fated catastrophe. Its structural weakness was built into it from the beginning — a flaw that has waited centuries to manifest. The tower embodies the novel's conception of fate as latent, cumulative, and ultimately irresistible.

## Analysis

Falkner's treatment of fate reflects the transition from Victorian providential thinking to a more modern sense of secular determinism. There is no divine plan in *The Nebuly Coat* — only the impersonal operation of cause and effect across generations. The Blandamer inheritance, like the tower's structural flaw, is a fact of engineering rather than theology.

Yet the novel retains the emotional register of classical tragedy. Characters speak of fate and fortune in language that echoes Shakespeare and Greek drama, even as the plot's mechanisms remain stubbornly material. This tension between tragic affect and secular causation gives the novel its distinctive tone — a Gothic atmosphere without Gothic supernaturalism.
