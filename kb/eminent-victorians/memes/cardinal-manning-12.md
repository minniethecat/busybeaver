[012-001]For the next five years [[john-henry-newman|Newman]], unaided and ignored, struggled

[012-002]desperately, like a man in a bog, with the overmastering difficulties of

[012-003]his task. His mind, whose native haunt was among the far aerial

[012-004]boundaries of fancy and philosophy, was now clamped down under the

[012-005]fetters of petty detail and fed upon the mean diet of compromise and

[012-006]routine. He had to force himself to scrape together money, to write

[012-007]articles for the students' Gazette, to make plans for medical

[012-008]laboratories, to be ingratiating with the City Council; he was obliged

[012-009]to spend months travelling through the remote regions of Ireland in the

[012-010]company of extraordinary ecclesiastics and barbarous squireens. He was a

[012-011]thoroughbred harnessed to a four-wheeled cab--and he knew it.

[012-012]Eventually, he realised something else: he saw that the whole project of

[012-013]a Catholic University had been evolved as a political and ecclesiastical

[012-014]weapon against the Queen's Colleges of Peel, and that was all. As an

[012-015]instrument of education, it was simply laughed at; and he himself had

[012-016]been called in because his name would be a valuable asset in a party

[012-017]game. When he understood that, he resigned his rectorship and returned

[012-018]to the Oratory.

[012-019]But, his tribulations were not yet over. It seemed to be God's will that

[012-020]he should take part in a whole succession of schemes, which, no less

[012-021]than the project of the Irish University, were to end in disillusionment

[012-022]and failure. He was persuaded by Cardinal Wiseman to undertake the

[012-023]editorship of a new English version of the Scriptures, which was to be a

[012-024]monument of Catholic scholarship and an everlasting glory to Mother

[012-025]Church. He made elaborate preparations; he collected subscriptions,

[012-026]engaged contributors, and composed a long and learned prolegomena to the

[012-027]work. It was all useless; Cardinal Wiseman began to think of other

[012-028]things; and the scheme faded imperceptibly into thin air. Then a new

[012-029]task was suggested to him: "The Rambler", a Catholic periodical, had

[012-030]fallen on evil days; would Dr Newman come to the rescue, and accept the

[012-031]editorship? This time he hesitated rather longer than usual; he had

[012-032]burned his fingers so often--he must be specially careful now. 'I did

[012-033]all I could to ascertain God's Will,' he said, and he came to the

[012-034]conclusion that it was his duty to undertake the work. He did so, and

[012-035]after two numbers had appeared, Dr. Ullathorne, the Bishop of

[012-036]Birmingham, called upon him, and gently hinted that he had better leave

[012-037]the paper alone. Its tone was not liked at [[rome|Rome]]; it had contained an

[012-038]article criticising St. Pius V, and, most serious of all, the orthodoxy

[012-039]of one of Newman's own essays had appeared to be doubtful. He resigned,

[012-040]and in the anguish of his heart, determined never to write again. One of

[012-041]his friends asked him why he was publishing nothing. 'Hannibal's

[012-042]elephants,' he replied, 'never could learn the goose-step.'

[012-043]Newman was now an old man--he was sixty-three years of age. What had he

[012-044]to look forward to? A few last years of insignificance and silence. What

[012-045]had he to look back upon? A long chronicle of wasted efforts,

[012-046]disappointed hopes, neglected possibilities, unappreciated powers. And

[012-047]now all his labours had ended by his being accused at Rome of lack of

[012-048]orthodoxy. He could no longer restrain his indignation, and in a letter

[012-049]to one of his lady penitents, he gave vent to the bitterness of his

[012-050]soul. When his Rambler article had been complained of, he said, there

[012-051]had been some talk of calling him to Rome.

[012-052]'Call me to Rome,' he burst out--'what does that mean? It means to sever

[012-053]an old man from his home, to subject him to intercourse with persons

[012-054]whose languages are strange to him--to food and to fashions which are

[012-055]almost starvation on the one hand, and involve restless days and nights

[012-056]on the other--it means to oblige him to dance attendance on Propaganda

[012-057]week after week and month after month--it means his death. (It was the

[012-058]punishment on Dr. Baines, 1840-1, to keep him at the door of Propaganda

[012-059]for a year.)

[012-060]'This is the prospect which I cannot but feel probable, did I say

[012-061]anything which one Bishop in England chose to speak against and report.

[012-062]Others have been killed before me. Lucas went of his own accord

[012-063]indeed--but when he got there, oh!' How much did he, as loyal a son of

[012-064]the Church and the Holy See as ever was, what did he suffer because Dr.

[012-065]Cullen was against him? He wandered (as Dr. Cullen said in a letter he

[012-066]published in a sort of triumph), he wandered from Church to Church

[012-067]without a friend, and hardly got an audience from the Pope. 'And I too

[012-068]should go from St. Philip to Our Lady, and to St. Peter and St. Paul,

[012-069]and to St. Laurence and to St. Cecilia, and, if it happened to me as to

[012-070]Lucas, should come back to die.'

[012-071]Yet, in spite of all, in spite of these exasperations of the flesh,

[012-072]these agitations of the spirit, what was there to regret? Had he not a

[012-073]mysterious consolation which outweighed every grief? Surely, surely, he

[012-074]had.

[012-075]'Unveil, O Lord, and on us shine,

[012-076]In glory and in grace,'

[012-077]he exclaims in a poem written at this time, called 'The Two Worlds':

[012-078]'This gaudy world grows pale before

[012-079]The beauty of Thy face.

[012-080]'Till Thou art seen it seems to he

[012-081]A sort of fairy ground,

[012-082]Where suns unsetting light the sky,

[012-083]And flowers and fruit abound.

[012-084]'But when Thy keener, purer beam

[012-085]Is poured upon our sight,

[012-086]It loses all its power to charm,

[012-087]And what was day is night ...

[012-088]'And thus, when we renounce for Thee

[012-089]Its restless aims and fears,

[012-090]The tender memories of the past,

[012-091]The hopes of coming years,

[012-092]'Poor is our sacrifice, whose eyes

[012-093]Are lighted from above;

[012-094]We offer what we cannot keep,

[012-095]What we have ceased to love.'

[012-096]Such were Newman's thoughts when an unexpected event occurred which

[012-097]produced a profound effect upon his life: Charles Kingsley attacked his

[012-098]good faith, and the good faith of Catholics in general, in a magazine

[012-099]article. Newman protested, and Kingsley rejoined in an irate pamphlet.

[012-100]Newman's reply was the Apologia pro Vita Sua, which he wrote in seven

[012-101]weeks, sometimes working twenty-two hours at a stretch, 'constantly in

[012-102]tears, and constantly crying out with distress'. The success of the

[012-103]book, with its transparent candour, its controversial brilliance, the

[012-104]sweep and passion of its rhetoric, the depth of its personal feeling,

[012-105]was immediate and overwhelming; it was recognised at once as a classic,

[012-106]not only by Catholics, but by the whole English world. From every side

[012-107]expressions of admiration, gratitude, and devotion poured in. It was

[012-108]impossible for one so sensitive as Newman to the opinions of other

[012-109]people to resist the happy influence of such an unlooked-for, such an

[012-110]enormous triumph. The cloud of his dejection began to lift; et l'espoir

[012-111]malgre lui s'est glisse dans son coeur.

[012-112]It was only natural that at such a moment his thoughts should return to

[012-113][[oxford-university|Oxford]]. For some years past proposals had been on foot for establishing

[012-114]there a Hall, under Newman's leadership, for Catholic undergraduates.

[012-115]The scheme had been looked upon with disfavour in Rome, and it had been

[012-116]abandoned; but now a new opportunity presented itself--some land in a

[012-117]suitable position came into the market. Newman, with his reviving

[012-118]spirits, felt that he could not let this chance go by, and bought the

[012-119]land. It was his intention to build there not a Hall, but a Church, and

[012-120]to set on foot a 'House of the Oratory'. What possible objection could

[012-121]there be to such a scheme? He approached the Bishop of Birmingham, who

[012-122]gave his approval; in Rome itself there was no hostile sign. The laity

[012-123]were enthusiastic and subscriptions began to flow in. Was it possible

[012-124]that all was well at last? Was it conceivable that the strange and weary

[012-125]pilgrimage of so many years should end at length in quietude, if not in

[012-126]happiness, where it had begun?

[012-127]It so happened that it was at this very time that Manning was appointed

[012-128]to the See of Westminster. The destinies of the two men, which had run

[012-129]parallel to one another in so strange a fashion and for so many years,

[012-130]were now for a moment suddenly to converge. Newly clothed with all the

[012-131]attributes of ecclesiastical supremacy, Manning found himself face to

[012-132]face with Newman, upon whose brows were glittering the fresh laurels of

[012-133]spiritual victory--the crown of an apostolical life. It was the meeting

[012-134]of the eagle and the dove. What followed showed, more clearly perhaps

[012-135]than any other incident in his career, the stuff that Manning was made

[012-136]of. Power had come to him at last; and he seized it with all the avidity

[012-137]of a born autocrat, whose appetite for supreme dominion had been whetted

[012-138]by long years of enforced abstinence and the hated simulations of

[012-139]submission. He was the ruler of Roman Catholic England, and he would

[012-140]rule. The nature of Newman's influence it was impossible for him to

[012-141]understand, but he saw that it existed; for twenty years he had been

[012-142]unable to escape the unwelcome iterations of that singular, that alien,

[012-143]that rival renown; and now it stood in his path, alone and inexplicable,

[012-144]like a defiant ghost. 'It is remarkably interesting,' he observed

[012-145]coldly, when somebody asked him what he thought of the Apologia: 'it is

[012-146]like listening to the voice of one from the dead.' And such voices, with

[012-147]their sepulchral echoes, are apt to be more dangerous than living ones;

[012-148]they attract too much attention; they must be silenced at all costs. It

[012-149]was the meeting of the eagle and the dove; there was a hovering, a

[012-150]swoop, and then the quick beak and the relentless talons did their work.

[012-151]Even before his accession to the Archbishopric, Manning had scented a

[012-152]peculiar peril in Newman's Oxford scheme, and so soon as he came into

[012-153]power, he privately determined that the author of the Apologia should

[012-154]never be allowed to return to his old University. Nor was there any lack

[012-155]of excellent reasons for such a decision. Oxford was by this time a nest

[012-156]of liberalism; it was no fit place for Catholic youths, and they would

[012-157]inevitably be attracted there by the presence of Father Newman. And

[012-158]then, had not Father Newman's orthodoxy been impugned? Had he not been

[012-159]heard to express opinions of most doubtful propriety upon the question

[012-160]of the Temporal Power? Was it not known that he might almost be said to

[012-161]have an independent mind? An influence? Yes, he had an influence no

[012-162]doubt; but what a fatal kind of influence to which to subject the rising

[012-163]generation of Catholic Englishmen!

[012-164]Such were the reflections which Manning was careful to pour into the

[012-165]receptive car of Monsignor Talbot. That useful priest, at his post of

[012-166]vantage in the Vatican, was more than ever the devoted servant of the

[012-167]new Archbishop. A league, offensive and defensive, had been established

[012-168]between the two friends.

[012-169]'I daresay I shall have many opportunities to serve you in Rome,' wrote

[012-170]Monsignor Talbot modestly, 'and I do not think any support will be

[012-171]useless to you, especially on account of the peculiar character of the

[012-172]Pope, and the spirit which pervades Propaganda; therefore, I wish you to

[012-173]understand that a compact exists between us; if you help me, I shall

[012-174]help you.' And a little later he added, 'I am glad you accept the

[012-175]league. As I have already done for years, I shall support you, and I

[012-176]have a hundred ways of doing so. A word dropped at the proper occasion

[012-177]works wonders.'

[012-178]Perhaps it was hardly necessary to remind his correspondent of that.

[012-179]So far as Newman was concerned, it so fell out that Monsignor Talbot

[012-180]needed no prompting. During the sensation caused by the appearance of

[012-181]the Apologia, it had occurred to him that it would be an excellent plan

[012-182]to secure Newman as a preacher during Lent for the fashionable

[012-183]congregation which attended his church in the Piazza del Popolo; and, he

[012-184]had accordingly written to invite him to Rome. His letter was

[012-185]unfortunately not a tactful one. He assured Newman that he would find in

[012-186]the Piazza del Popolo 'an audience of Protestants more educated than

[012-187]could ever be the case in England', and 'I think myself,' he had added

[012-188]by way of extra inducement, 'that you will derive great benefit from

[012-189]visiting Rome, and showing yourself to the Ecclesiastical Authorities.'

[012-190]Newman smiled grimly at this; he declared to a friend that the letter

[012-191]was 'insolent'; and he could not resist the temptation of using his

[012-192]sharp pen.
