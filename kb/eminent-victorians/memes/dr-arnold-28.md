[028-001]And certainly, if he was to fulfil the prophecy of the Provost of Oriel,

[028-002]the task before him was sufficiently perplexing. The public schools of

[028-003]those days were still virgin forests, untouched by the hand of reform.

[028-004]Keate was still reigning at Eton; and we possess, in the records of his

[028-005]pupils, a picture of the public school education of the early nineteenth

[028-006]century, in its most characteristic state. It was a system of anarchy

[028-007]tempered by despotism. Hundreds of boys, herded together in

[028-008]miscellaneous boarding-houses, or in that grim 'Long Chamber' at whose

[028-009]name in after years aged statesmen and warriors would turn pale, lived,

[028-010]badgered and overawed by the furious incursions of an irascible little

[028-011]old man carrying a bundle of birch-twigs, a life in which licensed

[028-012]barbarism was mingled with the daily and hourly study of the niceties of

[028-013]Ovidian verse. It was a life of freedom and terror, of prosody and

[028-014]rebellion, of interminable floggings and appalling practical jokes.

[028-015]Keate ruled, unaided--for the undermasters were few and of no

[028-016]account--by sheer force of character. But there were times when even

[028-017]that indomitable will was overwhelmed by the flood of lawlessness. Every

[028-018]Sunday afternoon he attempted to read sermons to the whole school

[028-019]assembled; and every Sunday afternoon the whole school assembled shouted

[028-020]him down. The scenes in Chapel were far from edifying; while some

[028-021]antique Fellow doddered in the pulpit, rats would be let loose to scurry

[028-022]among the legs of the exploding boys. But next morning the hand of

[028-023]discipline would reassert itself; and the savage ritual of the

[028-024]whipping-block would remind a batch of whimpering children that, though

[028-025]sins against man and God might be forgiven them, a false quantity could

[028-026]only be expiated in tears and blood.

[028-027]From two sides this system of education was beginning to be assailed by

[028-028]the awakening public opinion of the upper middle classes. On the one

[028-029]hand, there was a desire for a more liberal curriculum; on the other,

[028-030]there was a demand for a higher moral tone. The growing utilitarianism

[028-031]of the age viewed with impatience a course of instruction which excluded

[028-032]every branch of knowledge except classical philology; while its growing

[028-033]respectability was shocked by such a spectacle of disorder and brutality

[028-034]as was afforded by the Eton of Keate. 'The public schools,' said the

[028-035]Rev. Mr. Bowdler, 'are the very seats and nurseries of vice.'

[028-036]Dr. Arnold agreed. He was convinced of the necessity for reform. But it

[028-037]was only natural that to one of his temperament and education it should

[028-038]have been the moral rather than the intellectual side of the question

[028-039]which impressed itself upon his mind. Doubtless it was important to

[028-040]teach boys something more than the bleak rigidities of the ancient

[028-041]tongues; but how much more important to instil into them the elements of

[028-042]character and the principles of conduct! His great object, throughout

[028-043]his career at [[rugby-school|Rugby]], was, as he repeatedly said, to 'make the school a

[028-044]place of really Christian education'. To introduce 'a religious

[028-045]principle into education', was his 'most earnest wish', he wrote to a

[028-046]friend when he first became headmaster; 'but to do this would be to

[028-047]succeed beyond all my hopes; it would be a happiness so great, that, I

[028-048]think, the world would yield me nothing comparable to it'. And he was

[028-049]constantly impressing these sentiments upon his pupils. 'What I have

[028-050]often said before,' he told them, 'I repeat now: what we must look for

[028-051]here is, first, religious and moral principle; secondly, gentlemanly

[028-052]conduct; and thirdly, intellectual ability.'

[028-053]There can be no doubt that Dr. Arnold's point of view was shared by the

[028-054]great mass of English parents. They cared very little for classical

[028-055]scholarship; no doubt they would be pleased to find that their sons were

[028-056]being instructed in history or in French; but their real hopes, their

[028-057]real wishes, were of a very different kind. 'Shall I tell him to mind

[028-058]his work, and say he's sent to school to make himself a good scholar?'

[028-059]meditated old Squire Brown when he was sending off Tom for the first

[028-060]time to Rugby.

[028-061]'Well, but he isn't sent to school for that--at any rate, not for that

[028-062]mainly. I don't care a straw for Greek particles, or the digamma; no

[028-063]more does his mother. What is he sent to school for?... If he'll only

[028-064]turn out a brave, helpful, truth-telling Englishman, and a Christian,

[028-065]that's all I want.'

[028-066]That was all; and it was that that Dr. Arnold set himself to accomplish.

[028-067]But how was he to achieve his end? Was he to improve the character of

[028-068]his pupils by gradually spreading around them an atmosphere of

[028-069]cultivation and intelligence? By bringing them into close and friendly

[028-070]contact with civilised men, and even, perhaps, with civilised women? By

[028-071]introducing into the life of his school all that he could of the humane,

[028-072]enlightened, and progressive elements in the life of the community? On

[028-073]the whole, he thought not. Such considerations left him cold, and he

[028-074]preferred to be guided by the general laws of Providence. It only

[028-075]remained to discover what those general laws were. He consulted the Old

[028-076]Testament, and could doubt no longer. He would apply to his scholars, as

[028-077]he himself explained to them in one of his sermons, 'the principle which

[028-078]seemed to him to have been adopted in the training of the childhood of

[028-079]the human race itself'. He would treat the boys at Rugby as Jehovah had

[028-080]treated the Chosen People: he would found a theocracy; and there should

[028-081]be judges in Israel.

[028-082]For this purpose, the system, prevalent in most of the public schools of

[028-083]the day, by which the elder boys were deputed to keep order in the

[028-084]class-rooms, lay ready to Dr. Arnold's hand. He found the Praepostor a

[028-085]mere disciplinary convenience, and he converted him into an organ of

[028-086]government. Every boy in the Sixth Form became ipso facto a Praepostor,

[028-087]with powers extending over every department of school life; and the

[028-088]Sixth Form as a body was erected into an authority responsible to the

[028-089]headmaster, and to the headmaster alone, for the internal management of

[028-090]the school.

[028-091]This was the means by which Dr. Arnold hoped to turn Rugby into 'a place

[028-092]of really Christian education'. The boys were to work out their own

[028-093]salvation, like the human race. He himself, involved in awful grandeur,

[028-094]ruled remotely, through his chosen instruments, from an inaccessible

[028-095]heaven. Remotely--and yet with an omnipresent force. As the Israelite of

[028-096]old knew that his almighty Lawgiver might at any moment thunder to him

[028-097]from the whirlwind, or appear before his very eyes, the visible

[028-098]embodiment of power or wrath, so the Rugby schoolboy walked in a holy

[028-099]dread of some sudden manifestation of the sweeping gown, the majestic

[028-100]tone, the piercing glance, of Dr. Arnold. Among the lower forms of the

[028-101]school his appearances were rare and transitory, and upon these young

[028-102]children 'the chief impression', we are told, 'was of extreme fear'. The

[028-103]older boys saw more of him, but they did not see much. Outside the Sixth

[028-104]Form, no part of the school came into close intercourse with him; and it

[028-105]would often happen that a boy would leave Rugby without having had any

[028-106]personal communication with him at all.

[028-107]Yet the effect which he produced upon the great mass of his pupils was

[028-108]remarkable. The prestige of his presence and the elevation of his

[028-109]sentiments were things which it was impossible to forget. In class,

[028-110]every line of his countenance, every shade of his manner imprinted

[028-111]themselves indelibly on the minds of the boys who sat under him. One of

[028-112]these, writing long afterwards, has described, in phrases still

[028-113]impregnated with awestruck reverence, the familiar details of the scene:

[028-114]'the glance with which he looked round in the few moments of silence

[028-115]before the lesson began, and which seemed to speak his sense of his own

[028-116]position'--'the attitude in which he stood, turning over the pages of

[028-117]Facciolati's Lexicon, or Pole's synopsis, with his eye fixed upon the

[028-118]boy who was pausing to give an answer'--'the pleased look and the

[028-119]cheerful "thank you", which followed upon a successful translation'--'the

[028-120]fall of his countenance with its deepening severity, the stern elevation

[028-121]of the eyebrows, the sudden "sit down" which followed upon the

[028-122]reverse'--and 'the startling earnestness with which he would check in a

[028-123]moment the slightest approach to levity'.

[028-124]To be rebuked, however mildly, by Dr. Arnold was a Potable experience.

[028-125]One boy could never forget how he drew a distinction between 'mere

[028-126]amusement' and 'such as encroached on the next day's duties', nor the

[028-127]tone of voice with which the Doctor added 'and then it immediately

[028-128]becomes what St. Paul calls REVELLING'. Another remembered to his dying

[028-129]day his reproof of some boys who had behaved badly during prayers.

[028-130]'Nowhere,' said Dr. Arnold, 'nowhere is Satan's work more evidently

[028-131]manifest than in turning holy things to ridicule.' On such occasions, as

[028-132]another of his pupils described it, it was impossible to avoid 'a

[028-133]consciousness almost amounting to solemnity' that, 'when his eye was

[028-134]upon you, he looked into your inmost heart'.

[028-135]With the boys in the Sixth Form, and with them alone, the severe

[028-136]formality of his demeanour was to some degree relaxed. It was his wish,

[028-137]in his relations with the Praepostors, to allow the Master to be

[028-138]occasionally merged in the Friend. From time to time, he chatted with

[028-139]them in a familiar manner; once a term he asked them to dinner; and

[028-140]during the summer holidays he invited them, in rotation, to stay with

[028-141]him in Westmorland.

[028-142]It was obvious that the primitive methods of discipline which had

[028-143]reached their apogee under the dominion of Keate were altogether

[028-144]incompatible with Dr. Arnold's view of the functions of a headmaster and

[028-145]the proper governance of a public school. Clearly, it was not for such

[028-146]as he to demean himself by bellowing and cuffing, by losing his temper

[028-147]once an hour, and by wreaking his vengeance with indiscriminate

[028-148]flagellations. Order must be kept in other ways. The worst boys were

[028-149]publicly expelled; many were silently removed; and, when Dr. Arnold

[028-150]considered that a flogging was necessary, he administered it with

[028-151]gravity. For he had no theoretical objection to corporal punishment. On

[028-152]the contrary, he supported it, as was his wont, by an appeal to general

[028-153]principles. 'There is,' he said, 'an essential inferiority in a boy as

[028-154]compared with a man'; and hence 'where there is no equality the exercise

[028-155]of superiority implied in personal chastisement' inevitably followed.

[028-156]He was particularly disgusted by the view that 'personal correction', as

[028-157]he phrased it, was an insult or a degradation to the boy upon whom it

[028-158]was inflicted; and to accustom young boys to think so appeared to him to

[028-159]be 'positively mischievous'.

[028-160]'At an age,' he wrote, 'when it is almost impossible to find a true,

[028-161]manly sense of the degradation of guilt or faults, where is the wisdom

[028-162]of encouraging a fantastic sense of the degradation of personal

[028-163]correction? What can be more false, or more adverse to the simplicity,

[028-164]sobriety, and humbleness of mind which are the best ornaments of youth,

[028-165]and offer the best promise of a noble manhood?'

[028-166]One had not to look far, he added, for 'the fruits of such a system'. In

[028-167]Paris, during the Revolution of 1830, an officer observed a boy of

[028-168]twelve insulting the soldiers, and

[028-169]'though the action was then raging, merely struck him with the flat part

[028-170]of his sword, as the fit chastisement for boyish impertinence. But the

[028-171]boy had been taught to consider his person sacred, and that a blow was a

[028-172]deadly insult; he therefore followed the officer, and having watched his

[028-173]opportunity, took deliberate aim at him with a pistol and murdered him.'

[028-174]Such were the alarming results of insufficient whipping.

[028-175]Dr. Arnold did not apply this doctrine to the Praepostors, but the boys

[028-176]in the lower parts of the school felt its benefits, with a double force.

[028-177]The Sixth Form was not only excused from chastisement; it was given the

[028-178]right to chastise. The younger children, scourged both by Dr Arnold and

[028-179]by the elder children, were given every opportunity of acquiring the

[028-180]simplicity, sobriety, and humbleness of mind, which are the best

[028-181]ornaments of youth.
