# Littlemore

The rural retreat that became the symbol of Newman's estrangement from the Church of England. In 1842, Newman withdrew to a cottage at Littlemore with a small group of disciples, living a life of prayer, study, and physical austerity that was a novitiate in all but name. It was here that he wrote his *Essay on the Development of Christian Doctrine* and made the final decision to enter the Roman Church.

## Manning at the Door

One of Strachey's most telling scenes takes place at Littlemore. After delivering a sermon at Oxford, Manning "walked out to the neighbouring village of Littlemore, where Newman was now living in retirement with a few chosen disciples, in the hope of being able to give a satisfactory explanation of what he had done. But he was disappointed; for when, after an awkward interval, one of the disciples appeared at the door, he was informed that Mr. Newman was not at home" (006-033). The closed door is Strachey's symbol of the rupture between the two men — and of Manning's position, at that moment, as neither Tractarian nor Catholic, neither Newman's ally nor his rival.
