[002-001]'Watch, my dear Samuel,' wrote the elder Wilberforce to his son, 'watch

[002-002]with jealousy whether you find yourself unduly solicitous about

[002-003]acquitting yourself; whether you are too much chagrined when you fail,

[002-004]or are puffed up by your success. Undue solicitude about popular

[002-005]estimation is a weakness against which all real Christians must guard

[002-006]with the utmost jealous watchfulness. The more you can retain the

[002-007]impression of your being surrounded by a cloud of witnesses of the

[002-008]invisible world, to use the scripture phrase, the more you will be armed

[002-009]against this besetting sin.'

[002-010]But suddenly it seemed as if such a warning could, after all, have very

[002-011]little relevance to Manning; for, on his leaving [[oxford-university|Oxford]], the brimming

[002-012]cup was dashed from his lips. He was already beginning to dream of

[002-013]himself in the House of Commons, the solitary advocate of some great

[002-014]cause whose triumph was to be eventually brought about by his

[002-015]extraordinary efforts, when his father was declared a bankrupt, and all

[002-016]his hopes of a political career came to an end forever.

[002-017]It was at this time that Manning became intimate with a pious lady, the

[002-018]sister of one of his College friends, whom he used to describe as his

[002-019]Spiritual Mother. He made her his confidante; and one day, as they

[002-020]walked together in the shrubbery, he revealed the bitterness of the

[002-021]disappointment into which his father's failure had plunged him. She

[002-022]tried to cheer him, and then she added that there were higher aims open

[002-023]to him which he had not considered. 'What do you mean?' he asked. 'The

[002-024]kingdom of Heaven,' she answered; 'heavenly ambitions are not closed

[002-025]against you.' The young man listened, was silent, and said at last that

[002-026]he did not know but she was right. She suggested reading the Bible

[002-027]together; and they accordingly did so during the whole of that Vacation,

[002-028]every morning after breakfast. Yet, in spite of these devotional

[002-029]exercises, and in spite of a voluminous correspondence on religious

[002-030]subjects with his Spiritual Mother, Manning still continued to indulge

[002-031]in secular hopes. He entered the Colonial Office as a supernumerary

[002-032]clerk, and it was only when the offer of a Merton Fellowship seemed to

[002-033]depend upon his taking orders that his heavenly ambitions began to

[002-034]assume a definite shape. Just then he fell in love with Miss Deffell,

[002-035]whose father would have nothing to say to a young man without prospects,

[002-036]and forbade him the house. It was only too true; what WERE the prospects

[002-037]of a supernumerary clerk in the Colonial Office? Manning went to Oxford

[002-038]and took orders. He was elected to the Merton Fellowship, and obtained

[002-039]through the influence of the Wilberforces a curacy in Sussex. At the

[002-040]last moment he almost drew back. 'I think the whole step has been too

[002-041]precipitate,' he wrote to his brother-in-law. 'I have rather allowed the

[002-042]instance of my friends, and the allurements of an agreeable curacy in

[002-043]many respects, to get the better of my sober judgment.' His vast

[002-044]ambitions, his dreams of public service, of honours, and of power, was

[002-045]all this to end in a little country curacy 'agreeable in many respects'?

[002-046]But there was nothing for it; the deed was done; and the Fates had

[002-047]apparently succeeded very effectively in getting rid of Manning. All he

[002-048]could do was to make the best of a bad business.

[002-049]Accordingly, in the first place, he decided that he had received a call

[002-050]from God 'ad veritatem et ad seipsum'; and, in the second, forgetting

[002-051]Miss Deffell, he married his rector's daughter. Within a few months the

[002-052]rector died, and Manning stepped into his shoes; and at least it could

[002-053]be said that the shoes were not uncomfortable. For the next seven years

[002-054]he fulfilled the functions of a country clergyman. He was energetic and

[002-055]devout; he was polite and handsome; his fame grew in the diocese. At

[002-056]last he began to be spoken of as the probable successor to the old

[002-057]Archdeacon of Chichester. When Mrs. Manning prematurely died, he was at

[002-058]first inconsolable, but he found relief in the distraction of redoubled

[002-059]work. How could he have guessed that one day he would come to number

[002-060]that loss among 'God's special mercies? Yet so it was to be. In after

[002-061]years, the memory of his wife seemed to be blotted from his mind; he

[002-062]never spoke of her; every letter, every record, of his married life he

[002-063]destroyed; and when word was sent to him that her grave was falling into

[002-064]ruin: 'It is best so,' the Cardinal answered, 'let it be. Time effaces

[002-065]all things.' But, when the grave was yet fresh, the young Rector would

[002-066]sit beside it, day after day, writing his sermons.

[002-067]II

[002-068]IN the meantime, a series of events was taking place in another part of

[002-069]England, which was to have a no less profound effect upon Manning's

[002-070]history than the merciful removal of his wife. In the same year in which

[002-071]he took up his Sussex curacy, the [[oxford-movement|Tracts for the Times]] had begun to

[002-072]appear at Oxford. The 'Oxford Movement', in fact, had started on its

[002-073]course. The phrase is still familiar; but its meaning has become

[002-074]somewhat obscured both by the lapse of time and the intrinsic ambiguity

[002-075]of the subjects connected with it. Let us borrow for a moment the wings

[002-076]of Historic Imagination, and, hovering lightly over the Oxford of the

[002-077]thirties, take a rapid bird's-eye view.

[002-078]For many generations the [[church-of-england|Church of England]] had slept the sleep of the

[002-079]...comfortable. The sullen murmurings of dissent, the loud battle-cry of

[002-080]Revolution, had hardly disturbed her slumbers. Portly divines subscribed

[002-081]with a sigh or a smile to the Thirty-nine Articles, sank quietly into

[002-082]easy living, rode gaily to hounds of a morning as gentlemen should, and,

[002-083]as gentlemen should, carried their two bottles of an evening. To be in

[002-084]the Church was in fact simply to pursue one of those professions which

[002-085]Nature and Society had decided were proper to gentlemen and gentlemen

[002-086]alone. The fervours of piety, the zeal of Apostolic charity, the

[002-087]enthusiasm of self-renunciation--these things were all very well in

[002-088]their way and in their place; but their place was certainly not the

[002-089]Church of England. Gentlemen were neither fervid nor zealous, and above

[002-090]all they were not enthusiastic. There were, it was true, occasionally to

[002-091]be found within the Church some strait-laced parsons of the high Tory

[002-092]school who looked back with regret to the days of Laud or talked of the

[002-093]Apostolical Succession; and there were groups of square-toed

[002-094]Evangelicals who were earnest over the Atonement, confessed to a

[002-095]personal love of Jesus Christ, and seemed to have arranged the whole of

[002-096]their lives, down to the minutest details of act and speech, with

[002-097]reference to Eternity. But such extremes were the rare exceptions. The

[002-098]great bulk of the clergy walked calmly along the smooth road of ordinary

[002-099]duty. They kept an eye on the poor of the parish, and they conducted the

[002-100]Sunday Services in a becoming manner; for the rest, they differed

[002-101]neither outwardly nor inwardly from the great bulk of the laity, to whom

[002-102]the Church was a useful organisation for the maintenance of Religion, as

[002-103]by law established.

[002-104]The awakening came at last, however, and it was a rude one. The liberal

[002-105]principles of the French Revolution, checked at first in the terrors of

[002-106]reaction, began to make their way into England. Rationalists lifted up

[002-107]their heads; Bentham and the Mills propounded Utilitarianism; the Reform

[002-108]Bill was passed; and there were rumours abroad of disestablishment. Even

[002-109]Churchmen seemed to have caught the infection. Dr. Whately was so bold

[002-110]as to assert that, in the interpretation of Scripture, different

[002-111]opinions might be permitted upon matters of doubt; and, Dr. Arnold drew

[002-112]up a disquieting scheme for allowing Dissenters into the Church, though

[002-113]it is true that he did not go quite so far as to contemplate the

[002-114]admission of Unitarians.

[002-115]At this time, there was living in a country parish, a young clergyman of

[002-116]the name of John Keble. He had gone to Oxford at the age of fifteen,

[002-117]where, after a successful academic career, he had been made a Fellow of

[002-118]Oriel. He had then returned to his father's parish and taken up the

[002-119]duties of a curate. He had a thorough knowledge of the contents of the

[002-120]Prayer-book, the ways of a Common Room, the conjugations of the Greek

[002-121]Irregular Verbs, and the small jests of a country parsonage; and the

[002-122]defects of his experience in other directions were replaced by a zeal

[002-123]and a piety which were soon to prove themselves equal, and more than

[002-124]equal, to whatever calls might be made upon them. The superabundance of

[002-125]his piety overflowed into verse; and the holy simplicity of the

[002-126]Christian Year carried his name into the remotest lodging-houses of

[002-127]England.

[002-128]As for his zeal, however, it needed another outlet. Looking forth upon

[002-129]the doings of his fellow-men through his rectory windows in

[002-130]Gloucestershire, Keble felt his whole soul shaken with loathing, anger,

[002-131]and dread. Infidelity was stalking through the land; authority was

[002-132]laughed at; the hideous doctrines of Democracy were being openly

[002-133]preached. Worse still, if possible, the Church herself was ignorant and

[002-134]lukewarm; she had forgotten the mysteries of the sacraments, she had

[002-135]lost faith in the Apostolical Succession; she was no longer interested

[002-136]in the Early Fathers; and she submitted herself to the control of a

[002-137]secular legislature, the members of which were not even bound to profess

[002-138]belief in the Atonement. In the face of such enormities what could Keble

[002-139]do? He was ready to do anything, but he was a simple and an unambitious

[002-140]man, and his wrath would in all probability have consumed itself

[002-141]unappeased within him had he not chanced to come into contact, at the

[002-142]critical moment, with a spirit more excitable and daring than his own.

[002-143]Hurrell Froude, one of Keble's pupils, was a clever young man to whom

[002-144]had fallen a rather larger share of self-assurance and intolerance than

[002-145]even clever young men usually possess. What was singular about him,

[002-146]however, was not so much his temper as his tastes. The sort of ardour

[002-147]which impels more normal youths to haunt Music Halls and fall in love

[002-148]with actresses took the form, in Froude's case, of a romantic devotion

[002-149]to the Deity and an intense interest in the state of his own soul. He

[002-150]was obsessed by the ideals of saintliness, and convinced of the supreme

[002-151]importance of not eating too much. He kept a diary in which he recorded

[002-152]his delinquencies, and they were many. 'I cannot say much for myself

[002-153]today,' he writes on September 29th, 1826 (he was twenty-three years

[002-154]old). 'I did not read the Psalms and Second Lesson after breakfast,

[002-155]which I had neglected to do before, though I had plenty of time on my

[002-156]hands. Would have liked to be thought adventurous for a scramble I had

[002-157]at the Devil's Bridge. Looked with greediness to see if there was a

[002-158]goose on the table for dinner; and though what I ate was of the plainest

[002-159]sort, and I took no variety, yet even this was partly the effect of

[002-160]accident, and I certainly rather exceeded in quantity, as I was fuzzy

[002-161]and sleepy after dinner.' 'I allowed myself to be disgusted, with--'s

[002-162]pomposity,' he writes a little later, 'also smiled at an allusion in the

[002-163]Lessons to abstemiousness in eating. I hope not from pride or vanity,

[002-164]but mistrust; it certainly was unintentional.' And again, 'As to my

[002-165]meals, I can say that I was always careful to see that no one else would

[002-166]take a thing before I served myself; and I believe as to the kind of my

[002-167]food, a bit of cold endings of a dab at breakfast, and a scrap of

[002-168]mackerel at dinner, are the only things that diverged from the strict

[002-169]rule of simplicity.' 'I am obliged to confess,' he notes, 'that in my

[002-170]intercourse with the Supreme Being, I am be come more and more

[002-171]sluggish.' And then he exclaims: 'Thine eye trieth my inward parts, and

[002-172]knoweth my thoughts ... Oh that my ways were made so direct that I might

[002-173]keep Thy statutes. I will walk in Thy Commandments when Thou hast set my

[002-174]heart at liberty.'
