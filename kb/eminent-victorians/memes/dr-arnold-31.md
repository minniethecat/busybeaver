[031-001]Once or twice he found time to visit the Continent, and the letters and

[031-002]journals recording in minute detail his reflections and impressions in

[031-003]France or Italy show us that Dr. Arnold preserved, in spite of the

[031-004]distractions of foreign scenes and foreign manners, his accustomed

[031-005]habits of mind. Taking very little interest in works of art, he was

[031-006]occasionally moved by the beauty of natural objects; but his principal

[031-007]preoccupation remained with the moral aspects of things. From this point

[031-008]of view, he found much to reprehend in the conduct of his own

[031-009]countrymen. 'I fear,' he wrote, 'that our countrymen who live abroad are

[031-010]not in the best possible moral state, however much they may do in

[031-011]science or literature.' And this was unfortunate, because 'a thorough

[031-012]English gentleman--Christian, manly, and enlightened--is more, I

[031-013]believe, than Guizot or Sismondi could comprehend; it is a finer

[031-014]specimen of human nature than any other country, I believe, could

[031-015]furnish'. Nevertheless, our travellers would imitate foreign customs

[031-016]without discrimination, 'as in the absurd habit of not eating fish with

[031-017]a knife, borrowed from the French, who do it because they have no knives

[031-018]fit for use'. Places, no less than people, aroused similar reflections.

[031-019]By Pompeii, Dr. Arnold was not particularly impressed.

[031-020]'There is only,' he observed, 'the same sort of interest with which one

[031-021]would see the ruins of Sodom and Gomorrah, but indeed there is less. One

[031-022]is not authorised to ascribe so solemn a character to the destruction of

[031-023]Pompeii.'

[031-024]The lake of Como moved him more profoundly. As he gazed upon the

[031-025]overwhelming beauty around him, he thought of 'moral evil', and was

[031-026]appalled by the contrast. 'May the sense of moral evil', he prayed, 'be

[031-027]as strong in me as my delight in external beauty, for in a deep sense of

[031-028]moral evil, more perhaps than in anything else, abides a saving

[031-029]knowledge of God!'

[031-030]His prayer was answered: Dr. Arnold was never in any danger of losing

[031-031]his sense of moral evil. If the landscapes of Italy only served to

[031-032]remind him of it, how could he forget it among the boys at [[rugby-school|Rugby School]]?

[031-033]The daily sight of so many young creatures in the hands of the Evil One

[031-034]filled him with agitated grief.

[031-035]'When the spring and activity of youth,' he wrote, 'is altogether

[031-036]unsanctified by anything pure and elevated in its desires, it becomes a

[031-037]spectacle that is as dizzying and almost more morally distressing than

[031-038]the shouts and gambols of a set of lunatics.'

[031-039]One thing struck him as particularly strange: 'It is very startling,' he

[031-040]said, 'to see so much of sin combined with so little of sorrow.' The

[031-041]naughtiest boys positively seemed to enjoy themselves most. There were

[031-042]moments when he almost lost faith in his whole system of education, when

[031-043]he began to doubt whether some far more radical reforms than any he had

[031-044]attempted might not be necessary, before the multitude of children under

[031-045]his charge--shouting and gambolling, and yet plunged all the while deep

[031-046]in moral evil--could ever be transformed into a set of Christian

[031-047]gentlemen. But then he remembered his general principles, the conduct of

[031-048]Jehovah with the Chosen People, and the childhood of the human race. No,

[031-049]it was for him to make himself, as one of his pupils afterwards

[031-050]described him, in the words of Bacon, 'kin to God in spirit'; he would

[031-051]rule the school majestically from on high. He would deliver a series of

[031-052]sermons analysing 'the six vices' by which 'great schools were

[031-053]corrupted, and changed from the likeness of God's temple to that of a

[031-054]den of thieves'. He would exhort, he would denounce, he would sweep

[031-055]through the corridors, he would turn the pages of Facciolati's Lexicon

[031-056]more imposingly than ever; and the rest he would leave to the

[031-057]Praepostors in the Sixth Form.

[031-058]Upon the boys in the Sixth Form, indeed, a strange burden would seem to

[031-059]have fallen. Dr. Arnold himself was very well aware of this. 'I cannot

[031-060]deny,' he told them in a sermon, 'that you have an anxious duty--a duty

[031-061]which some might suppose was too heavy for your years'; and every term

[031-062]he pointed out to them, in a short address, the responsibilities of

[031-063]their position, and impressed upon them 'the enormous influence' they

[031-064]possessed 'for good or for evil'. Nevertheless most youths of seventeen,

[031-065]in spite of the warnings of their elders, have a singular trick of

[031-066]carrying moral burdens lightly. The Doctor might preach and look grave;

[031-067]but young Brooke was ready enough to preside at a fight behind the

[031-068]Chapel, though he was in the Sixth, and knew that fighting was against

[031-069]the rules. At their best, it may be supposed that the Praepostors

[031-070]administered a kind of barbaric justice; but they were not always at

[031-071]their best, and the pages of "Tom Brown's Schooldays" show us what was

[031-072]no doubt the normal condition of affairs under Dr. Arnold, when the boys

[031-073]in the Sixth Form were weak or brutal, and the blackguard Flashman, in

[031-074]the intervals of swigging brandy-punch with his boon companions, amused

[031-075]himself by toasting fags before the fire.

[031-076]But there was an exceptional kind of boy, upon whom the high-pitched

[031-077]exhortations of Dr. Arnold produced a very different effect. A minority

[031-078]of susceptible and serious youths fell completely under his sway,

[031-079]responded like wax to the pressure of his influence, and moulded their

[031-080]whole lives with passionate reverence upon the teaching of their adored

[031-081]master. Conspicuous among these was Arthur Clough. Having been sent to

[031-082]Rugby at the age of ten, he quickly entered into every phase of school

[031-083]life, though, we are told, 'a weakness in his ankles prevented him from

[031-084]taking a prominent part in the games of the place'. At the age of

[031-085]sixteen, he was in the Sixth Form, and not merely a Praepostor, but head

[031-086]of the School House. Never did Dr. Arnold have an apter pupil. This

[031-087]earnest adolescent, with the weak ankles and the solemn face, lived

[031-088]entirely with the highest ends in view. He thought of nothing but moral

[031-089]good, moral evil, moral influence, and moral responsibility. Some of his

[031-090]early letters have been preserved, and they reveal both the intensity

[031-091]with which he felt the importance of his own position, and the strange

[031-092]stress of spirit under which he laboured. 'I have been in one continued

[031-093]state of excitement for at least the last three years,' he wrote when he

[031-094]was not yet seventeen, 'and now comes the time of exhaustion.' But he

[031-095]did not allow himself to rest, and a few months later he was writing to

[031-096]a schoolfellow as follows:

[031-097]'I verily believe my whole being is soaked through with the wishing and

[031-098]hoping and striving to do the school good, or rather to keep it up and

[031-099]hinder it from falling in this, I do think, very critical time, so that

[031-100]my cares and affections and conversations, thoughts, words, and deeds

[031-101]look to that in voluntarily. I am afraid you will be inclined to think

[031-102]this "cant" and I am conscious that even one's truest feelings, if very

[031-103]frequently put out in the light, do make a bad and disagreeable

[031-104]appearance; but this, however, is true, and even if I am carrying it too

[031-105]far, I do not think it has made me really forgetful of my personal

[031-106]friends, such as, in particular, Gell and Burbidge and Walrond, and

[031-107]yourself, my dear Simpkinson.'

[031-108]Perhaps it was not surprising that a young man brought up in such an

[031-109]atmosphere, should have fallen a prey at [[oxford-university|Oxford]], to the frenzies of

[031-110]religious controversy; that he should have been driven almost out of his

[031-111]wits by the ratiocinations of W. G. Ward; that he should have lost his

[031-112]faith; that he should have spent the rest of his existence lamenting

[031-113]that loss, both in prose and verse; and that he should have eventually

[031-114]succumbed, conscientiously doing up brown paper parcels for Florence

[031-115]Nightingale.

[031-116]In the earlier years of his headmastership Dr. Arnold had to face a good

[031-117]deal of opposition. His advanced religious views were disliked, and

[031-118]there were many parents to whom his system of school government did not

[031-119]commend itself. But in time this hostility melted away. Succeeding

[031-120]generations of favourite pupils began to spread his fame through the

[031-121]Universities. At Oxford especially, men were profoundly impressed by the

[031-122]pious aims of the boys from Rugby. It was a new thing to see

[031-123]undergraduates going to Chapel more often than they were obliged, and

[031-124]visiting the good poor. Their reverent admiration for Dr. Arnold was no

[031-125]less remarkable. Whenever two of his old pupils met, they joined in his

[031-126]praises; and the sight of his picture had been known to call forth, from

[031-127]one who had not even reached the Sixth, exclamations of rapture lasting

[031-128]for ten minutes and filling with astonishment the young men from other

[031-129]schools who happened to be present.

[031-130]He became a celebrity; he became at last a great man. Rugby prospered;

[031-131]its numbers rose higher than ever before; and, after thirteen years as

[031-132]headmaster, Dr. Arnold began to feel that his work there was

[031-133]accomplished, and that he might look forward either to other labours or,

[031-134]perhaps, to a dignified retirement. But it was not to be.

[031-135]His father had died suddenly at the age of fifty-three from angina

[031-136]pectoris; and he himself was haunted by forebodings of an early death.

[031-137]To be snatched away without a warning, to come in a moment from the

[031-138]seductions of this World to the presence of Eternity--his most ordinary

[031-139]actions, the most casual remarks, served to keep him in remembrance of

[031-140]that dreadful possibility. When one of his little boys clapped his hands

[031-141]at the thought of the approaching holidays, the Doctor gently checked

[031-142]him, and repeated the story of his own early childhood; how his own

[031-143]father had made him read aloud a sermon on the text 'Boast not thyself

[031-144]of tomorrow"; and how, within the week, his father was dead. On the

[031-145]title page of his MS. volume of sermons, he was always careful to write

[031-146]the date of its commencement, leaving a blank for that of its

[031-147]completion. One of his children asked him the meaning of this. 'It is

[031-148]one of the most solemn things I do,' he replied, 'to write the beginning

[031-149]of that sentence, and think that I may perhaps not live to finish it.'

[031-150]It was noticed that in the spring of 1842 such thoughts seemed to be

[031-151]even more frequently in his mind than usual. He was only in his

[031-152]forty-seventh year, but he dwelt darkly on the fragility of human

[031-153]existence. Towards the end of May, he began to keep a diary--a private

[031-154]memorandum of his intimate communings with the Almighty. Here, evening

[031-155]after evening, in the traditional language of religious devotion, he

[031-156]humbled himself before God, prayed for strength and purity, and threw

[031-157]himself upon the mercy of the Most High.

[031-158]'Another day and another month succeed', he wrote on May 31st. 'May God

[031-159]keep my mind and heart fixed on Him, and cleanse me from all sin. I

[031-160]would wish to keep a watch over my tongue, as to vehement speaking and

[031-161]censuring of others ...I would desire to remember my latter end to which

[031-162]I am approaching ... May God keep me in the hour of death, through Jesus

[031-163]Christ; and preserve me from every fear, as well as from presumption.'

[031-164]On June 2nd he wrote, 'Again the day is over and I am going to rest. Oh

[031-165]Lord, preserve me this night, and strengthen me to bear whatever Thou

[031-166]shalt see fit to lay on me, whether pain, sickness, danger, or

[031-167]distress.' On Sunday, June 5th, the reading of the newspaper aroused

[031-168]'painful and solemn' reflections ... 'So much of sin and so much of

[031-169]suffering in the world, as are there displayed, and no one seems able to

[031-170]remedy either. And then the thought of my own private life, so full of

[031-171]comforts, is very startling.' He was puzzled; but he concluded with a

[031-172]prayer: 'May I be kept humble and zealous, and may God give me grace to

[031-173]labour in my generation for the good of my brethren and for His Glory!'
