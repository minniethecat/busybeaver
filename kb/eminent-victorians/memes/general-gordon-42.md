[042-001]It is curious that Gordon himself never understood the part that Mr.

[042-002][[william-ewart-gladstone|Gladstone]] was playing in his destiny. His [[khartoum|Khartoum]] journals put this

[042-003]beyond a doubt. Except for one or two slight and jocular references to

[042-004]Mr. Gladstone's minor idiosyncrasies--the shape of his collars, and his

[042-005]passion for felling trees, Gordon leaves him unnoticed while he lavishes

[042-006]his sardonic humour upon Lord Granville. But in truth Lord Granville was

[042-007]a nonentity. The error shows how dim the realities of England had grown

[042-008]to the watcher in Khartoum. When he looked towards home, the figure that

[042-009]loomed largest upon his vision was--it was only natural that it should

[042-010]have been so the nearest--it was upon Sir Evelyn Baring that he fixed

[042-011]his gaze. For him, Sir Evelyn Baring was the embodiment of England--or

[042-012]rather the embodiment of the English official classes, of English

[042-013]diplomacy, of the English Government with its hesitations, its

[042-014]insincerities, its double-faced schemes. Sir Evelyn Baring, he almost

[042-015]came to think at moments, was the prime mover, the sole contriver, of

[042-016]the whole Sudan imbroglio.

[042-017]In this he was wrong; for Sir Evelyn Baring, of course, was an

[042-018]intermediary, without final responsibility or final power; but Gordon's

[042-019]profound antipathy, his instinctive distrust, were not without their

[042-020]justification. He could never forget that first meeting in Cairo, six

[042-021]years earlier, when the fundamental hostility between the two men had

[042-022]leapt to the surface. 'When oil mixes with water,' he said, 'we will mix

[042-023]together.' Sir Evelyn Baring thought so too; but he did not say so; it

[042-024]was not his way. When he spoke, he felt no temptation to express

[042-025]everything that was in his mind. In all he did, he was cautious,

[042-026]measured, unimpeachably correct. It would be difficult to think of a man

[042-027]more completely the antithesis of Gordon. His temperament, all in

[042-028]monochrome, touched in with cold blues and indecisive greys, was

[042-029]eminently unromantic. He had a steely colourlessness, and a steely

[042-030]pliability, and a steely strength. Endowed beyond most men with the

[042-031]capacity of foresight, he was endowed as very few men have ever been

[042-032]with that staying-power which makes the fruit of foresight attainable.

[042-033]His views were long, and his patience was even longer. He progressed

[042-034]imperceptibly; he constantly withdrew; the art of giving way he

[042-035]practised with the refinement of a virtuoso. But, though the steel

[042-036]recoiled and recoiled, in the end it would spring forward. His life's

[042-037]work had in it an element of paradox. It was passed entirely in the

[042-038]East; and the East meant very little to him; he took no interest in it.

[042-039]It was something to be looked after. It was also a convenient field for

[042-040]the talents of Sir Evelyn Baring. Yet it must not be supposed that he

[042-041]was cynical; perhaps he was not quite great enough for that. He looked

[042-042]forward to a pleasant retirement--a country place--some literary

[042-043]recreations. He had been careful to keep up his classics. His ambition

[042-044]can be stated in a single phrase--it was to become an institution; and

[042-045]he achieved it. No doubt, too, he deserved it. The greatest of poets, in

[042-046]a bitter mood, has described the characteristics of a certain class of

[042-047]persons, whom he did not like. 'They,' he says,

[042-048]'that have power to hurt and will do none,

[042-049]That do not do the things they most do show,

[042-050]Who, moving others, are themselves as stone,

[042-051]Unmoved, cold, and to temptation slow,

[042-052]They rightly do inherit heaven's graces,

[042-053]And husband nature's riches from expense;

[042-054]They are the lords and owners of their faces ...'

[042-055]The words might have been written for Sir Evelyn Baring.

[042-056]Though, as a rule, he found it easy to despise those with whom he came

[042-057]into contact, he could not altogether despise [[general-gordon|General Gordon]]. If he

[042-058]could have, he would have disliked him less. He had gone as far as his

[042-059]caution had allowed him in trying to prevent the fatal appointment; and

[042-060]then, when it had become clear that the Government was insistent, he had

[042-061]yielded with a good grace. For a moment, he had imagined that all might

[042-062]yet be well; that he could impose himself, by the weight of his position

[042-063]and the force of his sagacity, upon his self-willed subordinate; that he

[042-064]could hold him in a leash at the end of the telegraph wire to Khartoum.

[042-065]Very soon he perceived that this was a miscalculation. To his disgust,

[042-066]he found that the telegraph wire, far from being an instrument of

[042-067]official discipline, had been converted by the agile strategist at the

[042-068]other end of it into a means of extending his own personality into the

[042-069]deliberations at Cairo. Every morning Sir Evelyn Baring would find upon

[042-070]his table a great pile of telegrams from Khartoum--twenty or thirty at

[042-071]least; and as the day went on, the pile would grow. When a sufficient

[042-072]number had accumulated he would read them all through, with the greatest

[042-073]care. There upon the table, the whole soul of Gordon lay before him--in

[042-074]its incoherence, its eccentricity, its impulsiveness, its romance; the

[042-075]jokes, the slang, the appeals to the prophet Isaiah, the whirl of

[042-076]contradictory policies--Sir Evelyn Baring did not know which exasperated

[042-077]him most. He would not consider whether, or to what degree, the man was

[042-078]a maniac; no, he would not. A subacid smile was the only comment he

[042-079]allowed himself. His position, indeed, was an extremely difficult one,

[042-080]and all his dexterity would be needed if he was to emerge from it with

[042-081]credit.

[042-082]On one side of him was a veering and vacillating Government; on the

[042-083]other, a frenzied enthusiast. It was his business to interpret to the

[042-084]first the wishes, or rather the inspirations, of the second, and to

[042-085]convey to the second the decisions, or rather the indecisions, of the

[042-086]first. A weaker man would have floated helplessly on the ebb and flow of

[042-087]the Cabinet's wavering policies; a rasher man would have plunged

[042-088]headlong into Gordon's schemes. He did neither; with a singular courage

[042-089]and a singular caution he progressed along a razor-edge. He devoted all

[042-090]his energies to the double task of evolving a reasonable policy out of

[042-091]Gordon's intoxicated telegrams, and of inducing the divided Ministers at

[042-092]home to give their sanction to what he had evolved. He might have

[042-093]succeeded, if he had not had to reckon with yet another irreconcilable;

[042-094]Time was a vital element in the situation, and Time was against him.

[042-095]When the tribes round Khartoum rose, the last hope of a satisfactory

[042-096]solution vanished. He was the first to perceive the altered condition of

[042-097]affairs; long before the Government, long before Gordon himself, he

[042-098]understood that the only remaining question was that of the extrication

[042-099]of the Englishmen from Khartoum. He proposed that a small force should

[042-100]be dispatched at once across the desert from Suakin to Barber, the point

[042-101]on the Nile nearest to the Red Sea, and thence up the river to Gordon;

[042-102]but, after considerable hesitation, the military authorities decided

[042-103]that this was not a practicable plan. Upon that, he foresaw, with

[042-104]perfect lucidity, the inevitable development of events. Sooner or later,

[042-105]it would be absolutely necessary to send a relief expedition to

[042-106]Khartoum; and, from that premise, it followed, without a possibility of

[042-107]doubt, that it was the duty of the Government to do so at once. This he

[042-108]saw quite clearly; but he also saw that the position in the Cabinet had

[042-109]now altered, that Mr. Gladstone had taken the reins into his own hands.

[042-110]And Mr. Gladstone did not wish to send a relief expedition. What was Sir

[042-111]Evelyn Baring to do? Was he to pit his strength against Mr. Gladstone's?

[042-112]To threaten resignation? To stake his whole future upon General Gordon's

[042-113]fate? For a moment he wavered; he seemed to hint that unless the

[042-114]Government sent a message to Khartoum promising a relief expedition

[042-115]before the end of the year, he would be unable to be a party to their

[042-116]acts. The Government refused to send any such message; and he perceived,

[042-117]as he tells us, that 'it was evidently useless to continue the

[042-118]correspondence any further'. After all, what could he do? He was still

[042-119]only a secondary figure; his resignation would be accepted; he would be

[042-120]given a colonial governorship and Gordon would be no nearer safety. But

[042-121]then, could he sit by and witness a horrible catastrophe, without

[042-122]lifting a hand? Of all the odious dilemmas which that man had put him

[042-123]into this, he reflected, was the most odious. He slightly shrugged his

[042-124]shoulders. No; he might have 'power to hurt', but he would 'do none'. He

[042-125]wrote a dispatch--a long, balanced, guarded, grey dispatch, informing

[042-126]the Government that he 'ventured to think' that it was 'a question

[042-127]worthy of consideration whether the naval and military authorities

[042-128]should not take some preliminary steps in the way of preparing boats,

[042-129]etc., so as to be able to move, should the necessity arise'. Then,

[042-130]within a week, before the receipt of the Government's answer, he left

[042-131]Egypt. From the end of April until the beginning of September--during

[042-132]the most momentous period of the whole crisis, he was engaged in London

[042-133]upon a financial conference, while his place was taken in Cairo by a

[042-134]substitute. With a characteristically convenient unobtrusiveness, Sir

[042-135]Evelyn Baring had vanished from the scene.

[042-136]Meanwhile, far to the southward, over the wide-spreading lands watered

[042-137]by the Upper Nile and its tributaries, the power and the glory of him

[042-138]who had once been Mohammed Ahmed were growing still. In the

[042-139]Bahr-el-Ghazal, the last embers of resistance were stamped out with the

[042-140]capture of Lupton Bey, and through the whole of that vast province three

[042-141]times the size of England--every trace of the Egyptian Government was

[042-142]obliterated. Still farther south the same fate was rapidly overtaking

[042-143]Equatoria, where Emir Pasha, withdrawing into the unexplored depths of

[042-144]Central Africa, carried with him the last vestiges of the old order. The

[042-145]Mahdi himself still lingered in his headquarters at El Obeid; but, on

[042-146]the rising of the tribes round Khartoum, he had decided that the time

[042-147]for an offensive movement had come, and had dispatched an arm of 30,000

[042-148]men to lay siege to the city. At the same time, in a long and elaborate

[042-149]proclamation, in which he asserted, with all the elegance of oriental

[042-150]rhetoric, both the sanctity of his mission and the invincibility of his

[042-151]troops, he called upon the inhabitants to surrender. Gordon read aloud

[042-152]the summons to the assembled townspeople; with one voice they declared

[042-153]that they were ready to resist. This was a false Mahdi, they said; God

[042-154]would defend the right; they put their trust in the Governor-General.

[042-155]The most learned Sheikh in the town drew up a theological reply,

[042-156]pointing out that the Mahdi did not fulfil the requirements of the

[042-157]ancient prophets. At his appearance, had the Euphrates dried up and

[042-158]revealed a hill of gold? Had contradiction and difference ceased upon

[042-159]the earth? And, moreover, did not the faithful know that the true Mahdi

[042-160]was born in the year of the Prophet 255, from which it surely followed

[042-161]that he must be now 1,046 years old? And was it not clear to all men

[042-162]that this pretender was not a tenth of that age?

[042-163]These arguments were certainly forcible; but the Mahdi's army was more

[042-164]forcible still. The besieged sallied out to the attack; they were

[042-165]defeated; and the rout that followed was so disgraceful that two of the

[042-166]commanding officers were, by Gordon's orders, executed as traitors. From

[042-167]that moment the regular investment of Khartoum began. The Arab generals

[042-168]decided to starve the town into submission. When, after a few weeks of

[042-169]doubt, it became certain that no British force was on its way from

[042-170]Suakin to smash up the Mahdi, and when, at the end of May, Berber, the

[042-171]last connecting link between Khartoum and the outside world, fell into

[042-172]the hands of the enemy, Gordon set his teeth, and sat down to wait and

[042-173]to hope, as best he might. With unceasing energy he devoted himself to

[042-174]the strengthening of his defences and the organisation of his

[042-175]resources--to the digging of earthworks, the manufacture of ammunition,

[042-176]the collection and the distribution of food. Every day there were

[042-177]sallies and skirmishes; every day his little armoured steamboats paddled

[042-178]up and down the river, scattering death and terror as they went.

[042-179]Whatever the emergency, he was ready with devices and expedients. When

[042-180]the earthworks were still uncompleted he procured hundreds of yards of

[042-181]cotton, which he dyed the colour of earth, and spread out in long,

[042-182]sloping lines, so as to deceive the Arabs, while the real works were

[042-183]being prepared farther back. When a lack of money began to make itself

[042-184]felt, he printed and circulated a paper coinage of his own. To combat

[042-185]the growing discontent and disaffection of the townspeople, he

[042-186]instituted a system of orders and medals; the women were not forgotten;

[042-187]and his popularity redoubled. There was terror in the thought that harm

[042-188]might come to the Governor-General. Awe and reverence followed him;

[042-189]wherever he went he was surrounded by a vigilant and jealous guard, like

[042-190]some precious idol, some mascot of victory. How could he go away? How

[042-191]could he desert his people? It was impossible. It would be, as he

[042-192]himself exclaimed in one of his latest telegrams to Sir Evelyn Baring,

[042-193]'the climax of meanness', even to contemplate such an act. Sir Evelyn

[042-194]Baring thought differently. In his opinion it was General Gordon's plain

[042-195]duty to have come away from Khartoum. To stay involved inevitably a

[042-196]relief expedition--a great expense of treasure and the loss of valuable

[042-197]lives; to come away would merely mean that the inhabitants of Khartoum

[042-198]would be 'taken prisoner by the Mahdi'. So Sir Evelyn Baring put it; but

[042-199]the case was not quite so simple as that. When Berber fell, there had

[042-200]been a massacre lasting for days--an appalling orgy of loot and lust and

[042-201]slaughter; when Khartoum itself was captured, what followed was still

[042-202]more terrible. Decidedly, it was no child's play to be 'taken prisoner

[042-203]by the Mahdi'. And Gordon was actually there, among those people, in

[042-204]closest intercourse with them, responsible, beloved. Yes; no doubt. But

[042-205]was that in truth, his only motive? Did he not wish in reality, by

[042-206]lingering in Khartoum, to force the hand of the Government? To oblige

[042-207]them, whether they would or no, to send an army to smash up the Mahdi?

[042-208]And was that fair? Was THAT his duty? He might protest, with his last

[042-209]breath, that he had 'tried to do his duty'; Sir Evelyn Baring, at any

[042-210]rate, would not agree.
