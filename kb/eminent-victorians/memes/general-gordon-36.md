[036-001]Two days later, he was off for Pekin. 'Every one will say I am mad,'

[036-002]were his last words to Lord William Beresford; 'but you say I am not.'

[036-003]The position in China was critical; war with Russia appeared to be

[036-004]imminent; and Gordon had been appealed to in order to use his influence

[036-005]on the side of peace. He was welcomed by many old friends of former

[036-006]days, among them Li Hung Chang, whose diplomatic views coincided with

[036-007]his own. Li's diplomatic language, however, was less unconventional. In

[036-008]an interview with the Ministers, Gordon's expressions were such that the

[036-009]interpreter shook with terror, upset a cup of tea, and finally refused

[036-010]to translate the dreadful words; upon which Gordon snatched up a

[036-011]dictionary, and, with his finger on the word 'idiocy', showed it to the

[036-012]startled Mandarins. A few weeks later, Li Hung Chang was in power, and

[036-013]peace was assured. Gordon had spent two and a half days in Pekin, and

[036-014]was whirling through China, when a telegram arrived from the home

[036-015]authorities, who viewed his movements with uneasiness, ordering him to

[036-016]return at once to England. 'It did not produce a twitter in me,' he

[036-017]wrote to his sister; 'I died long ago, and it will not make any

[036-018]difference to me; I am prepared to follow the unrolling of the scroll.'

[036-019]The world, perhaps, was not big enough for him; and yet how clearly he

[036-020]recognised that he was 'a poor insect!' 'My heart tells me that, and I

[036-021]am glad of it.'

[036-022]On his return to England, he telegraphed to the Government of the Cape

[036-023]of Good Hope, which had become involved in a war with the Basutos,

[036-024]offering his services; but his telegram received no reply. Just then,

[036-025]Sir Howard Elphinstone was appointed to the command of the Royal

[036-026]Engineers in Mauritius. It was a thankless and insignificant post; and,

[036-027]rather than accept it, Elphinstone was prepared to retire from the

[036-028]Army--unless some other officer could be induced, in return for L800, to

[036-029]act as his substitute. Gordon, who was an old friend, agreed to

[036-030]undertake the work upon one condition: that he should receive nothing

[036-031]from Elphinstone; and accordingly, he spent the next year in that remote

[036-032]and unhealthy island, looking after the barrack repairs and testing the

[036-033]drains.

[036-034]While he was thus engaged, the Cape Government, whose difficulties had

[036-035]been increasing, changed its mind, and early in 1882, begged for

[036-036]Gordon's help. Once more he was involved in great affairs: a new field

[036-037]of action opened before him; and then, in a moment, there was another

[036-038]shift of the kaleidoscope, and again he was thrown upon the world.

[036-039]Within a few weeks, after a violent quarrel with the Cape authorities,

[036-040]his mission had come to an end. What should he do next? To what remote

[036-041]corner or what enormous stage, to what self-sacrificing drudgeries or

[036-042]what resounding exploits, would the hand of God lead him now? He waited,

[036-043]in an odd hesitation. He opened the Bible, but neither the prophecies of

[036-044]Hosea nor the epistles to Timothy gave him any advice. The King of the

[036-045]Belgians asked if he would be willing to go to the Congo. He was

[036-046]perfectly willing; he would go whenever the King of the Belgians sent

[036-047]for him; his services, however, were not required yet. It was at this

[036-048]juncture that he betook himself to Palestine. His studies there were

[036-049]embodied in a correspondence with the Rev. Mr. Barnes, filling over

[036-050]2,000 pages of manuscript--a correspondence which was only put an end to

[036-051]when, at last, the summons from the King of the Belgians came. He

[036-052]hurried back to England; but it was not to the Congo that he was being

[036-053]led by the hand of God.

[036-054]Gordon's last great adventure, like his first, was occasioned by a

[036-055]religious revolt. At the very moment when, apparently forever, he was

[036-056]shaking the dust of Egypt from his feet, Mahommed Ahmed was starting

[036-057]upon his extraordinary career in the Sudan. The time was propitious for

[036-058]revolutions. The effete Egyptian Empire was hovering upon the verge of

[036-059]collapse. The enormous territories of the Sudan were seething with

[036-060]discontent. Gordon's administration had, by its very vigour, only helped

[036-061]to precipitate the inevitable disaster. His attacks upon the

[036-062]slave-trade, his establishment of a government monopoly in ivory, his

[036-063]hostility to the Egyptian officials, had been so many shocks, shaking to

[036-064]its foundations the whole rickety machine. The result of all his efforts

[036-065]had been, on the one hand, to fill the most powerful classes in the

[036-066]community--the dealers in slaves and, ivory--with a hatred of the

[036-067]government, and on the other to awaken among the mass of the inhabitants

[036-068]a new perception of the dishonesty and incompetence of their Egyptian

[036-069]masters. When, after Gordon's removal, the rule of the Pashas once more

[036-070]asserted itself over the Sudan, a general combustion became inevitable:

[036-071]the first spark would set off the blaze. Just then it happened that

[036-072]Mahommed Ahmed, the son of an insignificant priest in Dongola, having

[036-073]quarrelled with the Sheikh from whom he was receiving religious

[036-074]instruction, set up as an independent preacher, with his headquarters at

[036-075]Abba Island, on the Nile, 150 miles above [[khartoum|Khartoum]]. Like Hong-siu-tsuen,

[036-076]he began as a religious reformer, and ended as a rebel king. It was his

[036-077]mission, he declared, to purge the true Faith of its worldliness and

[036-078]corruptions, to lead the followers of the prophet into the paths of

[036-079]chastity, simplicity, and holiness; with the puritanical zeal of a

[036-080]Calvin, be denounced junketings and merrymakings, songs and dances, lewd

[036-081]living and all the delights of the flesh. He fell into trances, he saw

[036-082]visions, he saw the prophet and Jesus, and the Angel Izrail accompanying

[036-083]him and watching over him forever. He prophesied and performed miracles,

[036-084]and his fame spread through the land.

[036-085]There is an ancient tradition in the Mohammedan world, telling of a

[036-086]mysterious being, the last in succession of the twelve holy Imams, who,

[036-087]untouched by death and withdrawn into the recesses of a mountain, was

[036-088]destined, at the appointted hour, to come forth again among men. His

[036-089]title was the Mahdi, the guide; some believed that he would be the

[036-090]forerunner of the Messiah; others believed that he would be Christ

[036-091]himself. Already various Mahdis had made their appearance; several had

[036-092]been highly successful, and two, in medieval times, had founded

[036-093]dynasties in Egypt. But who could tell whether all these were not

[036-094]impostors? Might not the twelfth Imam be still waiting, in mystical

[036-095]concealment, ready to emerge, at any moment, at the bidding of God?

[036-096]There were signs by which the true Mahdi might be recognised--unmistakable

[036-097]signs, if one could but read them aright. He must be of the family of

[036-098]the prophet; he must possess miraculous powers of no common kind; and

[036-099]his person must be overflowing with a peculiar sanctity. The pious

[036-100]dwellers beside those distant waters, where holy men by dint of a

[036-101]constant repetition of one of the ninety-nine names of God, secured the

[036-102]protection of guardian angels, and where groups of devotees, shaking

[036-103]their heads with a violence which would unseat the reason of less

[036-104]athletic worshippers, attained to an extraordinary beatitude, heard with

[036-105]awe of the young preacher whose saintliness was almost more than mortal

[036-106]and whose miracles brought amazement to the mind. Was he not also of the

[036-107]family of the prophet? He himself had said so, and who would disbelieve

[036-108]the holy man? When he appeared in person, every doubt was swept away.

[036-109]There was a strange splendour in his presence, an overpowering passion

[036-110]in the torrent of his speech. Great was the wickedness of the people,

[036-111]and great was their punishment! Surely their miseries were a visible

[036-112]sign of the wrath of the Lord. They had sinned, and the cruel tax

[036-113]gatherers had come among them, and the corrupt governors, and all the

[036-114]oppressions of the Egyptians. Yet these things, 'Too, should have an

[036-115]end. The Lord would raise up his chosen deliverer; the hearts of the

[036-116]people would be purified, and their enemies would be laid low. The

[036-117]accursed Egyptian would be driven from the land. Let the faithful take

[036-118]heart and make ready. How soon might not the long-predestined hour

[036-119]strike, when the twelfth Imam, the guide, the Mahdi, would reveal

[036-120]himself to the world?' In that hour, the righteous 'Would triumph and

[036-121]the guilty be laid low forever.' Such was the teaching of Mohammed

[036-122]Ahmed. A band of enthusiastic disciples gathered round him, eagerly

[036-123]waiting for the revelation which would crown their hopes. At last, the

[036-124]moment came. One evening, at Abba Island, taking aside the foremost of

[036-125]his followers, the Master whispered the portentous news. He was the

[036-126]Mahdi.

[036-127]The Egyptian Governor-General at Khartoum, hearing that a religious

[036-128]movement was afoot, grew disquieted, and dispatched an emissary to Abba

[036-129]Island to summon the impostor to his presence. The emissary was

[036-130]courteously received. Mohammed Ahmed, he said, must come at once to

[036-131]Khartoum. 'Must!' exclaimed the Mahdi, starting to his feet, with a

[036-132]strange look in his eyes. The look was so strange that the emissary

[036-133]thought it advisable to cut short the interview and to return to

[036-134]Khartoum empty-handed. Thereupon, the Governor-General sent 200 soldiers

[036-135]to seize the audacious rebel by force. With his handful of friends, the

[036-136]Mahdi fell upon the soldiers and cut them to pieces. The news spread

[036-137]like wild-fire through the country: the Mahdi had arisen, the Egyptians

[036-138]were destroyed. But it was clear to the little band of enthusiasts at

[036-139]Abba Island that their position on the river was no longer tenable. The

[036-140]Mahdi, deciding upon a second Hegira, retreated south-westward, into the

[036-141]depths of Kordofan.

[036-142]The retreat was a triumphal progress. The country, groaning under alien

[036-143]misgovernment and vibrating with religious excitement, suddenly found in

[036-144]this rebellious prophet a rallying-point, a hero, a deliverer. And now

[036-145]another element was added to the forces of insurrection. The Baggara

[036-146]tribes of Kordofan, cattle-owners and slave-traders, the most warlike

[036-147]and vigorous of the inhabitants of the Sudan, threw in their lot with

[036-148]the Mahdi. Their powerful Emirs, still smarting from the blows of

[036-149]Gordon, saw that the opportunity for revenge had come. A holy war was

[036-150]proclaimed against the Egyptian misbelievers. The followers of the

[036-151]Mahdi, dressed, in token of a new austerity of living, in the 'jibbeh',

[036-152]or white smock of coarse cloth, patched with variously shaped and

[036-153]coloured patches, were rapidly organised into a formidable army. Several

[036-154]attacks from Khartoum were repulsed; and at last, the Mahdi felt strong

[036-155]enough to advance against the enemy. While his lieutenants led

[036-156]detachments into the vast provinces lying to the west and the

[036-157]south--Darfur and Bahr-el-Ghazal--he himself marched upon El Obeid, the

[036-158]capital of Kordofan. It was in vain that reinforcements were hurried

[036-159]from Khartoum to the assistance of the garrison: there was some severe

[036-160]fighting; the town was completely cut off; and, after a six months'

[036-161]siege, it surrendered. A great quantity of guns and ammunition and

[036-162]L100,000 in spices fell into the hands of the Mahdi. He was master of

[036-163]Kordofan: he was at the head of a great army; he was rich; he was

[036-164]worshipped. A dazzling future opened before him. No possibility seemed

[036-165]too remote, no fortune too magnificent. A vision of universal empire

[036-166]hovered before his eyes. Allah, whose servant he was, who had led him

[036-167]thus far, would lead him onward still, to the glorious end.

[036-168]For some months he remained at El Obeid, consolidating his dominion. In

[036-169]a series of circular letters, he described his colloquies with the

[036-170]Almighty and laid down the rule of living which his followers were to

[036-171]pursue. The faithful, under pain of severe punishment, were to return to

[036-172]the ascetic simplicity of ancient times. A criminal code was drawn up,

[036-173]meting out executions, mutilations, and floggings with a barbaric zeal.

[036-174]The blasphemer was to be instantly hanged, the adulterer was to be

[036-175]scourged with whips of rhinoceros hide, the thief was to have his right

[036-176]hand and his left foot hacked off in the marketplace. No more were

[036-177]marriages to be celebrated with pomp and feasting, no more was the

[036-178]youthful warrior to swagger with flowing hair; henceforth, the believer

[036-179]must banquet on dates and milk, and his head must be kept shaved. Minor

[036-180]transgressions were punished by confiscation of property or by

[036-181]imprisonment and chains. But the rhinoceros whip was the favourite

[036-182]instrument of chastisement. Men were flogged for drinking a glass of

[036-183]wine, they were flogged for smoking; if they swore, they received eighty

[036-184]lashes for every expletive; and after eighty lashes it was a common

[036-185]thing to die. Before long, flogging grew to be so everyday an incident

[036-186]that the young men made a game of it, as a test of their endurance of

[036-187]pain.
