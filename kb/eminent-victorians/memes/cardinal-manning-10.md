[010-001]It was a melancholy fate which ordained that the last years of this

[010-002]comfortable, easygoing, innocent old man should be distracted and

[010-003]embittered by the fury of opposing principles and the venom of personal

[010-004]animosities. But so it was. He had fallen into the hands of one who

[010-005]cared very little for the gentle pleasures of repose. Left to himself,

[010-006]Wiseman might have compromised with the Old Catholics and Dr. Errington;

[010-007]but when Manning had once appeared upon the scene, all compromise became

[010-008]impossible. The late Archdeacon of Chichester, who had understood so

[010-009]well and practised with such careful skill the precept of the golden

[010-010]mean so dear to the heart of the [[church-of-england|Church of England]], now, as Provost of

[010-011]Westminster, flung himself into the fray with that unyielding intensity

[010-012]of fervour, that passion for the extreme and the absolute, which is the

[010-013]very lifeblood of the [[catholic-church|Church of Rome]]. Even the redoubtable Dr.

[010-014]Errington, short, thickset, determined, with his `hawk-like expression

[010-015]of face', as a contemporary described him, 'as he looked at you through

[010-016]his blue spectacles', had been known to quail in the presence of his,

[010-017]antagonist, with his tall and graceful figure, his pale ascetic

[010-018]features, his compressed and icy lips, his calm and penetrating gaze. As

[010-019]for the poor Cardinal, he was helpless indeed.

[010-020]Henceforward, there was to be no paltering with that dangerous spirit of

[010-021]independence--was it not almost Gallicanism which possessed the Old

[010-022]Catholic families of England? The supremacy of the Vicar of Christ must

[010-023]be maintained at all hazards. Compared with such an object, what were

[010-024]the claims of personal affection and domestic peace? The Cardinal

[010-025]pleaded in vain; his lifelong friendship with Dr. Errington was plucked

[010-026]up by the roots, and the harmony of his private life was utterly

[010-027]destroyed. His own household was turned against him. His favourite

[010-028]nephew, whom he had placed among the Oblates under Manning's special

[010-029]care, left the congregation and openly joined the party of Dr.

[010-030]Errington. His secretary followed suit; but saddest of all was the case

[010-031]of Monsignor Searle. Monsignor Searle, in the capacity of confidential

[010-032]man of affairs, had dominated over the Cardinal in private for years

[010-033]with the autocratic fidelity of a servant who has grown indispensable.

[010-034]His devotion, in fact, seemed to have taken the form of physical

[010-035]imitation, for he was hardly less gigantic than his master. The two were

[010-036]inseparable; their huge figures loomed together like neighbouring

[010-037]mountains; and on one occasion, meeting them in the street, a gentleman

[010-038]congratulated Wiseman on 'your Eminence's fine son'. Yet now even this

[010-039]companionship was broken up. The relentless Provost here too brought a

[010-040]sword. There were explosions and recriminations. Monsignor Searle,

[010-041]finding that his power was slipping from him, made scenes and protests,

[010-042]and at last was foolish enough to accuse Manning of peculation to his

[010-043]face; after that it was clear that his day was over; he was forced to

[010-044]slink snarling into the background, while the Cardinal shuddered through

[010-045]all his immensity, and wished many times that he were already dead.

[010-046]Yet, he was not altogether without his consolations; Manning took care

[010-047]to see to that. His piercing eye had detected the secret way into the

[010-048]recesses of the Cardinal's heart--had discerned the core of simple faith

[010-049]which underlay that jovial manner and that facile talk. Others were

[010-050]content to laugh and chatter and transact their business; Manning was

[010-051]more artistic. He watched his opportunity, and then, when the moment

[010-052]came, touched with a deft finger the chord of the [[religious-conversion|Conversion]] of England.

[010-053]There was an immediate response, and he struck the same chord again, and

[010-054]yet again. He became the repository of the Cardinal's most intimate

[010-055]aspirations. He alone sympathised and understood. 'If God gives me

[010-056]strength to undertake a great wrestling-match with infidelity,' Wiseman

[010-057]wrote, 'I shall owe it to him.'

[010-058]But what he really found himself undertaking was a wrestling-match with

[010-059]Dr. Errington. The struggle over St. Edmund's College grew more and more

[010-060]acute. There were high words in the Chapter, where Monsignor Searle led

[010-061]the assault against the Provost, and carried a resolution declaring that

[010-062]the Oblates of St. Charles had intruded themselves illegally into the

[010-063]Seminary. The Cardinal quashed the proceedings of the Chapter;

[010-064]whereupon, the Chapter appealed to [[rome|Rome]]. Dr. Errington, carried away by

[010-065]the fury of the controversy, then appeared as the avowed opponent of the

[010-066]Provost and the Cardinal. With his own hand he drew up a document

[010-067]justifying the appeal of the Chapter to Rome by Canon Law and the

[010-068]decrees of the Council of Trent. Wiseman was deeply pained: 'My own

[010-069]co-adjutor,' he exclaimed, 'is acting as solicitor against me in a

[010-070]lawsuit.' There was a rush to Rome, where, for several ensuing years,

[010-071]the hostile English parties were to wage a furious battle in the

[010-072]antechambers of the Vatican. But the dispute over the Oblates now sank

[010-073]into insignificance beside the rage of contention which centred round a

[010-074]new and far more deadly question; for the position of Dr. Errington

[010-075]himself was at stake. The Cardinal, in spite of illness, indolence, and

[010-076]the ties of friendship, had been brought at last to an extraordinary

[010-077]step--he was petitioning the Pope for nothing less than the deprivation

[010-078]and removal of the Archbishop of Trebizond.

[010-079]The precise details of what followed are doubtful. It is only possible

[010-080]to discern with clearness, amid a vast cloud of official documents and

[010-081]unofficial correspondences in English, Italian, and Latin, of Papal

[010-082]decrees and voluminous scritture, of confidential reports of episcopal

[010-083]whispers and the secret agitations of Cardinals, the form of Manning,

[010-084]restless and indomitable, scouring like a stormy petrel the angry ocean

[010-085]of debate. Wiseman, dilatory, unbusinesslike, and infirm, was ready

[010-086]enough to leave the conduct of affairs in his hands. Nor was it long

[010-087]before Manning saw where the key of the whole position lay. As in the

[010-088]old days, at Chichester, he had secured the goodwill of Bishop

[010-089]Shuttleworth by cultivating the friendship of Archdeacon Hare, so now,

[010-090]on this vaster scale of operations, his sagacity led him swiftly and

[010-091]unerringly up the little winding staircase in the Vatican and through

[010-092]the humble door which opened into the cabinet of Monsignor Talbot, the

[010-093]private secretary of the Pope. Monsignor Talbot was a priest who

[010-094]embodied in a singular manner, if not the highest, at least the most

[010-095]persistent traditions of the Roman Curia. He was a master of various

[010-096]arts which the practice of ages has brought to perfection under the

[010-097]friendly shadow of the triple tiara. He could mingle together astuteness

[010-098]and holiness without any difficulty; he could make innuendoes as

[010-099]naturally as an ordinary man makes statements of fact; he could apply

[010-100]flattery with so unsparing a hand that even Princes of the Church found

[010-101]it sufficient; and, on occasion, he could ring the changes of torture on

[010-102]a human soul with a tact which called forth universal approbation. With

[010-103]such accomplishments, it could hardly be expected that Monsignor Talbot

[010-104]should be remarkable either for a delicate sense of conscientiousness or

[010-105]for an extreme refinement of feeling, but then it was not for those

[010-106]qualities that Manning was in search when he went up the winding stair.

[010-107]He was looking for the man who had the ear of Pio Nono; and, on the

[010-108]other side of the low-arched door, he found him. Then he put forth all

[010-109]his efforts; his success was complete; and an alliance began which was

[010-110]destined to have the profoundest effect upon Manning's career, and was

[010-111]only dissolved when, many years later, Monsignor Talbot was

[010-112]unfortunately obliged to exchange his apartment in the Vatican for a

[010-113]private lunatic asylum at Passy.

[010-114]It was determined that the coalition should be ratified by the ruin of

[010-115]Dr. Errington. When the moment of crisis was seen to be approaching,

[010-116]Wiseman was summoned to Rome, where he began to draw up an immense

[010-117]scrittura containing his statement of the case. For months past, the

[010-118]redoubtable energies of the Archbishop of Trebizond had been absorbed in

[010-119]a similar task. Folio was being piled upon folio, when a sudden blow

[010-120]threatened to put an end to the whole proceeding in a summary manner.

[010-121]The Cardinal was seized by violent illness, and appeared to be upon his

[010-122]deathbed. Manning thought for a moment that his labours had been in vain

[010-123]and that all was lost. But the Cardinal recovered; Monsignor Talbot used

[010-124]his influence as he alone knew how; and a papal decree was issued by

[010-125]which Dr. Errington was 'liberated' from the Coadjutorship of

[010-126]Westminster, together with the right of succession to the See.

[010-127]It was a supreme act of authority--a 'colpo di stato di Dominiddio', as

[010-128]the Pope himself said--and the blow to the Old Catholics was

[010-129]correspondingly severe. They found themselves deprived at one fell swoop

[010-130]both of the influence of their most energetic supporter and of the

[010-131]certainty of coming into power at Wiseman's death. And in the meantime,

[010-132]Manning was redoubling his energies at Bayswater. Though his Oblates had

[010-133]been checked over St. Edmund's, there was still no lack of work for them

[010-134]to do. There were missions to be carried on, schools to be managed,

[010-135]funds to be collected. Several new churches were built; a community of

[010-136]most edifying nuns of the Third Order of St. Francis was established;

[010-137]and L30,000, raised from Manning's private resources and from those of

[010-138]his friends, was spent in three years. 'I hate that man,' one of the Old

[010-139]Catholics exclaimed, 'he is such a forward piece.' The words were

[010-140]reported to Manning, who shrugged his shoulders.

[010-141]'Poor man,' he said, 'what is he made of? Does he suppose, in his

[010-142]foolishness, that after working day and night for twenty years in heresy

[010-143]and schism, on becoming a Catholic, I should sit in an easy-chair and

[010-144]fold my hands all the rest of my life?'

[010-145]But his secret thoughts were of a different caste.

[010-146]'I am conscious of a desire,' he wrote in his Diary, 'to be in such a

[010-147]position: (I) as I had in times past; (2) as my present circumstances

[010-148]imply; (3) as my friends think me fit for; and (4) as I feel my own

[010-149]faculties tend to.

[010-150]'But, God being my helper, I will not seek it by the lifting of a finger

[010-151]or the speaking, of a word.'

[010-152]So Manning wrote, and thought, and prayed; but what are words, and

[010-153]thoughts, and even prayers, to the mysterious and relentless powers of

[010-154]circumstance and character? Cardinal Wiseman was slowly dying; the

[010-155]tiller of the Church was slipping from his feeble hand; and Manning was

[010-156]beside him, the one man with the energy, the ability, the courage, and

[010-157]the conviction to steer the ship upon her course. More than that; there

[010-158]was the sinister figure of a Dr. Errington crouching close at hand,

[010-159]ready to seize the helm and make straight--who could doubt it?--for the

[010-160]rocks. In such a situation the voice of self-abnegation must needs grow

[010-161]still and small indeed. Yet it spoke on, for it was one of the paradoxes

[010-162]in Manning's soul that that voice was never silent. Whatever else he

[010-163]was, he was not unscrupulous. Rather, his scruples deepened with his

[010-164]desires; and he could satisfy his most exorbitant ambitions in a

[010-165]profundity of self-abasement. And so now he vowed to Heaven that he

[010-166]would SEEK nothing--no, not by the lifting of a finger or the speaking

[010-167]of a word. But, if something came to him--? He had vowed not to seek; he

[010-168]had not vowed not to take. Might it not be his plain duty to take? Might

[010-169]it not be the will of God?

[010-170]Something, of course, did come to him, though it seemed for a moment

[010-171]that it would elude his grasp. Wiseman died, and there ensued in Rome a

[010-172]crisis of extraordinary intensity. 'Since the creation of the

[010-173]hierarchy,' Monsignor Talbot wrote, it is the greatest moment for the

[010-174]Church that I have yet seen.' It was the duty of the Chapter of

[010-175]Westminster to nominate three candidates for succession to the

[010-176]Archbishopric; they made one last effort, and had the temerity to place

[010-177]upon the list, besides the names of two Old Catholic bishops, that of

[010-178]Dr. Errington. It was a fatal blunder. Pius IX was furious; the Chapter

[010-179]had committed an 'insulta al Papa', he exclaimed, striking his breast

[010-180]three times in his rage. 'It was the Chapter that did it,' said Manning,

[010-181]afterwards; but even after the Chapter's indiscretion, the fatal

[010-182]decision hung in the balance for weeks.
