## Overview

John Keble (1792–1866) was an Anglican priest, Fellow of [[oxford-university|Oriel College]], [[oxford-university|Oxford]], and one of the three founding leaders of the [[oxford-movement|Oxford Movement]]. His sermon on "National Apostasy," preached in the University Church in 1833, is traditionally regarded as the moment the movement began.

> At this time, there was living in a country parish, a young clergyman of the name of John Keble. He had gone to Oxford at the age of fifteen. [002-116]

## The Assize Sermon and the Birth of the Movement

Keble had returned from [[oxford-university|Oxford]] to his father's parish in Gloucestershire, where through his rectory windows he contemplated the state of England with growing alarm. Strachey describes his reaction to the political and religious developments of the 1830s:

> Keble felt his whole soul shaken with loathing, anger, and dread. Infidelity was stalking through the land; authority was laughed at; the hideous doctrines of Democracy were being openly professed. [002-130]

The specific trigger was the proposed suppression of ten Irish bishoprics by a secular legislature — an act Keble saw as a direct assault on the spiritual independence of the Church. His sermon on this "National Apostasy" galvanised the High Church party and launched the Tractarian campaign [002-138].

## The Tractarian Partnership

Keble was a simple and unambitious man, and Strachey suggests his wrath would have consumed itself ineffectually had it not been for the arrival of a more daring spirit — his former pupil Hurrell Froude, and through him, [[john-henry-newman|John Henry Newman]] [002-142]. Together the three formed the intellectual core of the [[oxford-movement|Oxford Movement]], with Keble providing the poetic soul, [[john-henry-newman|Newman]] the dialectical genius, and Pusey the professorial authority.

## After Newman's Departure

When [[john-henry-newman|Newman]] converted to Roman Catholicism in 1845, the [[oxford-movement|Oxford Movement]] suffered a devastating blow. Keble, along with [[edward-pusey|Edward Pusey]], chose a different path. In one of Strachey's most celebrated images:

> Keble and Pusey continued for the rest of their lives to dance in an exemplary manner upon the tight-rope of High Anglicanism. [006-132]

## Literary Legacy

Keble's *The Christian Year* (1827), a collection of poems for the Sundays and holy days of the Anglican calendar, was one of the most popular volumes of devotional verse in the nineteenth century. It went through countless editions and established Keble's reputation as the foremost religious poet of his generation long before the [[oxford-movement|Oxford Movement]] began.
