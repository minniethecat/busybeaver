# First Vatican Council (1870)

The Council that defined Papal Infallibility was, for Manning, the culmination of a lifetime's movement toward absolute authority. Seven hundred bishops gathered in Rome — "the whole world seemed to be gathered together within her walls" — to provide "an adequate remedy to the disorders, intellectual and moral, of Christendom" (013-136). Everyone knew what it meant.

## Manning's Role

Manning "flew to Rome in a whirlwind of Papal enthusiasm" (014-049). His role was that of the Pope's most reliable advocate: organising the ultramontane majority, marginalising the inopportunist minority (Newman, Dupanloup, Dollinger), and ensuring that the definition would be as absolute as possible. The triumph was Manning's as much as Pius IX's.

## Strachey's Treatment

Strachey's account of the Council is a set piece of ironic journalism. He lingers on the incongruities: the Roman aristocracy in their carriages, the Zouaves in St. Peter's colonnade, the "inopportunist" bishops who admitted Infallibility was true but thought it unwise to say so. The definition itself, when it came, was almost anticlimactic — a vote, a proclamation, and the modern world proceeding as before.
