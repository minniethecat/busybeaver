# Administrative Reform

The Crimean War was the great revelation of administrative incompetence in Victorian Britain. The army that defeated Napoleon had become, in forty years of peace, a "gigantic machine for the prevention of effective action" — its supply chains broken, its medical services nonexistent, its officers chosen by purchase rather than competence. Administrative reform was the response: the attempt to replace aristocratic amateurism with professional expertise.

## Nightingale and the War Office

Nightingale's greatest battles were not in the Crimea but in Whitehall. After the war, she "retired" to her sofa and began a campaign of bureaucratic pressure — letters, reports, statistical memoranda, private interviews with ministers — that transformed the Army Medical Department, the Royal Commission on the Health of the Army, and eventually the structure of the War Office itself. Strachey presents her invalidism as the perfect administrative posture: immobile, tireless, impossible to avoid.

Dr. Sutherland, "a sanitary expert, who for more than thirty years acted as her confidential private secretary" (023-185), was the instrument through which she worked. Together they produced reports that were "statistical, sanitary, architectural" — the paperwork of reform (024-055).

## The Northcote-Trevelyan Report

The same impulse toward professionalisation that drove sanitary reform also transformed the civil service. The Northcote-Trevelyan Report (1854) recommended competitive examination for government posts, ending the system of patronage that had filled ministries with gentlemen-amateurs. This was the administrative counterpart of Arnold's educational reforms: both sought to replace birth with merit, influence with competence.
