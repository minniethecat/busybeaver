[026-001]With statesmen and governors at her beck and call, with her hands on a

[026-002]hundred strings, with mighty provinces at her feet, with foreign

[026-003]governments agog for her counsel, building hospitals, training

[026-004]nurses--she still felt that she had not enough to do. She sighed for

[026-005]more worlds to conquer--more, and yet more.

[026-006]She looked about her--what was left? Of course! Philosophy! After the

[026-007]world of action, the world of thought. Having set right the health of

[026-008]the [[british-army|British Army]], she would now do the same good service for the

[026-009]religious convictions of mankind. She had long noticed--with regret--the

[026-010]growing tendency towards free-thinking among artisans. With regret, but

[026-011]not altogether with surprise, the current teaching of Christianity was

[026-012]sadly to seek; nay, Christianity itself was not without its defects. She

[026-013]would rectify these errors. She would correct the mistakes of the

[026-014]Churches; she would point out just where Christianity was wrong; and she

[026-015]would explain to the artisans what the facts of the case really were.

[026-016]Before her departure for the Crimea, she had begun this work; and now,

[026-017]in the intervals of her other labours, she completed it. Her

[026-018]'Suggestions for Thought to the Searchers After Truth Among the Artisans

[026-019]of England' (1860), unravels, in the course of three portly volumes, the

[026-020]difficulties hitherto, curiously enough, unsolved--connected with such

[026-021]matters as Belief in God, the Plan of Creation, the Origin of Evil, the

[026-022]Future Life, Necessity and Free Will, Law, and the Nature of Morality.

[026-023]The Origin of Evil, in particular, held no perplexities for Miss

[026-024]Nightingale. 'We cannot conceive,' she remarks, 'that Omnipotent

[026-025]Righteousness would find satisfaction in solitary existence.' This

[026-026]being, so, the only question remaining to be asked is: 'What beings

[026-027]should we then conceive that God would create?' Now, He cannot create

[026-028]perfect beings, 'since, essentially, perfection is one'; if He did so,

[026-029]He would only be adding to Himself. Thus the conclusion is obvious: He

[026-030]must create imperfect ones. Omnipotent Righteousness, faced by the

[026-031]intolerable impasse of a solitary existence, finds itself bound by the

[026-032]very nature of the cause, to create the hospitals at [[scutari|Scutari]]. Whether

[026-033]this argument would have satisfied the artisans was never discovered,

[026-034]for only a very few copies of the book were printed for private

[026-035]circulation. One copy was sent to Mr. Mill, who acknowledged it in an

[026-036]extremely polite letter. He felt himself obliged, however, to confess

[026-037]that he had not been altogether convinced by [[florence-nightingale|Miss Nightingale]]'s proof of

[026-038]the existence of God. Miss Nightingale was surprised and mortified; she

[026-039]had thought better of Mr. Mill; for surely her proof of the existence of

[026-040]God could hardly be improved upon. 'A law,' she had pointed out,

[026-041]'implies a law-giver.' Now the Universe is full of laws--the law of

[026-042]gravitation, the law of the excluded middle, and many others; hence it

[026-043]follows that the Universe has a law-giver--and what would Mr. Mill be

[026-044]satisfied with, if he was not satisfied with that?

[026-045]Perhaps Mr. Mill might have asked why the argument had not been pushed

[026-046]to its logical conclusion. Clearly, if we are to trust the analogy of

[026-047]human institutions, we must remember that laws are, as a matter of fact,

[026-048]not dispensed by lawgivers, but passed by Act of Parliament. Miss

[026-049]Nightingale, however, with all her experience of public life, never

[026-050]stopped to consider the question whether God might not be a Limited

[026-051]Monarchy. Yet her conception of God was certainly not orthodox. She felt

[026-052]towards Him as she might have felt towards a glorified sanitary

[026-053]engineer; and in some of her speculations she seems hardly to

[026-054]distinguish between the Deity and the Drains. As one turns over these

[026-055]singular pages, one has the impression that Miss Nightingale has got the

[026-056]Almighty too into her clutches, and that, if He is not careful, she will

[026-057]kill Him with overwork.

[026-058]Then, suddenly, in the very midst of the ramifying generalities of her

[026-059]metaphysical disquisitions, there is an unexpected turn and the reader

[026-060]is plunged all at once into something particular, something personal,

[026-061]something impregnated with intense experience--a virulent invective upon

[026-062]the position of women in the upper ranks of society. Forgetful alike of

[026-063]her high argument and of the artisans, the bitter creature rails through

[026-064]a hundred pages of close print at the falsities of family life, the

[026-065]ineptitudes of marriage, the emptinesses of convention, in the spirit of

[026-066]an Ibsen or a Samuel Butler. Her fierce pen, shaking with intimate

[026-067]anger, depicts in biting sentences the fearful fate of an unmarried girl

[026-068]in a wealthy household. It is a cri du coeur; and then, as suddenly, she

[026-069]returns once more to instruct the artisans upon the nature of Omnipotent

[026-070]Righteousness.

[026-071]Her mind was, indeed, better qualified to dissect the concrete and

[026-072]distasteful fruits of actual life than to construct a coherent system of

[026-073]abstract philosophy. In spite of her respect for Law, she was never at

[026-074]home with a generalisation. Thus, though the great achievement of her

[026-075]life lay in the immense impetus which she gave to the scientific

[026-076]treatment of sickness, a true comprehension of the scientific method

[026-077]itself was alien to her spirit. Like most great men of action--perhaps

[026-078]like all--she was simply an empiricist. She believed in what she saw,

[026-079]and she acted accordingly; beyond that she would not go. She had found

[026-080]in Scutari that fresh air and light played an effective part in the

[026-081]prevention of the maladies with which she had to deal; and that was

[026-082]enough for her; she would not inquire further; what were the general

[026-083]principles underlying that fact--or even whether there were any--she

[026-084]refused to consider. Years after the discoveries of Pasteur and Lister,

[026-085]she laughed at what she called the 'germ-fetish'. There was no such

[026-086]thing as 'infection'; she had never seen it, therefore it did not exist.

[026-087]But she had seen the good effects of fresh air; therefore, there could

[026-088]be no doubt about them; and therefore, it was essential that the

[026-089]bedrooms of patients should be well ventilated. Such was her doctrine;

[026-090]and in those days of hermetically scaled windows it was a very valuable

[026-091]one. But it was a purely empirical doctrine, and thus it led to some

[026-092]unfortunate results. When, for instance, her influence in India was at

[026-093]its height, she issued orders that all hospital windows should be

[026-094]invariably kept open. The authorities, who knew what an open window in

[026-095]the hot weather meant, protested, but in vain; Miss Nightingale was

[026-096]incredulous. She knew nothing of the hot weather, but she did know the

[026-097]value of fresh air--from personal experience; the authorities were

[026-098]talking nonsense; and the windows must be kept open all the year round.

[026-099]There was a great outcry from all the doctors in India, but she was

[026-100]firm; and for a moment it seemed possible that her terrible commands

[026-101]would have to be put into execution. Lord Lawrence, however, was

[026-102]Viceroy, and he was able to intimate to Miss Nightingale, with

[026-103]sufficient authority, that himself had decided upon the question, and

[026-104]that his decision must stand, even against her own. Upon that she gave

[026-105]way, but reluctantly and quite unconvinced; she was only puzzled by the

[026-106]unexpected weakness of Lord Lawrence. No doubt, if she had lived today,

[026-107]and if her experience had lain, not among cholera cases at Scutari, but

[026-108]among yellow-fever cases in Panama, she would have declared fresh air a

[026-109]fetish, and would have maintained to her dying day that the only really

[026-110]effective way of dealing with disease was by the destruction of

[026-111]mosquitoes.

[026-112]Yet her mind, so positive, so realistic, so ultra-practical, had its

[026-113]singular revulsions, its mysterious moods of mysticism and of doubt. At

[026-114]times, lying sleepless in the early hours, she fell into long, strange,

[026-115]agonised meditations, and then, seizing a pencil, she would commit to

[026-116]paper the confessions of her soul. The morbid longings of her

[026-117]pre-Crimean days came over her once more; she filled page after page

[026-118]with self-examination, self-criticism, self-surrender. 'Oh Father,' she

[026-119]wrote, 'I submit, I resign myself, I accept with all my heart, this

[026-120]stretching out of Thy hand to save me.... Oh how vain it is, the vanity

[026-121]of vanities, to live in men's thoughts instead of God's!'

[026-122]She was lonely, she was miserable. 'Thou knowest that through all these

[026-123]horrible twenty years, I have been supported by the belief that I was

[026-124]working with Thee who would bring everyone, even our poor nurses, to

[026-125]perfection'--and yet, after all, what was the result? Had not even she

[026-126]been an unprofitable servant? One night, waking suddenly, she saw, in

[026-127]the dim light of the night-lamp, tenebrous shapes upon the wall. The

[026-128]past rushed back upon her. 'Am I she who once stood on that Crimean

[026-129]height?' she wildly asked--'The Lady with a lamp shall stand.... The lamp

[026-130]shows me only my utter shipwreck.'

[026-131]She sought consolation in the writings of the Mystics and in a

[026-132]correspondence with Mr. Jowett. For many years the Master of Balliol

[026-133]acted as her spiritual adviser. He discussed with her in a series of

[026-134]enormous letters the problems of religion and philosophy; he criticised

[026-135]her writings on those subjects with the tactful sympathy of a cleric who

[026-136]was also a man of the world; and he even ventured to attempt at times to

[026-137]instil into her rebellious nature some of his own peculiar suavity. 'I

[026-138]sometimes think,' he told her, 'that you ought seriously to consider how

[026-139]your work may be carried on, not with less energy, but in a calmer

[026-140]spirit. I am not blaming the past ... But I want the peace of God to

[026-141]settle on the future.' He recommended her to spend her time no longer in

[026-142]'conflicts with Government offices', and to take up some literary work.

[026-143]He urged her to 'work out her notion of Divine Perfection', in a series

[026-144]of essays for Frazer's Magazine. She did so; and the result was

[026-145]submitted to Mr. Froude, who pronounced the second essay to be 'even

[026-146]more pregnant than the first. I cannot tell,' he said, 'how sanitary,

[026-147]with disordered intellects, the effects of such papers will be.'

[026-148]Mr. Carlyle, indeed, used different language, and some remarks of his

[026-149]about a lost lamb bleating on the mountains, having been unfortunately

[026-150]repeated to Miss Nightingale, required all Mr. Jowett's suavity to keep

[026-151]the peace. In a letter of fourteen sheets, he turned her attention from

[026-152]this painful topic towards a discussion of Quietism. 'I don't see why,'

[026-153]said the Master of Balliol, 'active life might not become a sort of

[026-154]passive life too.' And then, he added, 'I sometimes fancy there are

[026-155]possibilities of human character much greater than have been realised.'

[026-156]She found such sentiments helpful, underlining them in blue pencil; and,

[026-157]in return, she assisted her friend with a long series of elaborate

[026-158]comments upon the Dialogues of Plato, most of which he embodied in the

[026-159]second edition of his translation. Gradually her interest became more

[026-160]personal; she told him never to work again after midnight, and he obeyed

[026-161]her. Then she helped him to draw up a special form of daily service for

[026-162]the College Chapel, with selections from the Psalms under the heads of

[026-163]'God the Lord, God the judge, God the Father, and God the

[026-164]Friend'--though, indeed, this project was never realised; for the Bishop

[026-165]of [[oxford-university|Oxford]] disallowed the alterations, exercising his legal powers, on

[026-166]the advice of Sir Travers Twiss.

[026-167]Their relations became intimate. 'The spirit of the Twenty-third Psalm

[026-168]and the spirit of the Nineteenth Psalm should be united in our lives,'

[026-169]Mr. Jowett said. Eventually, she asked him to do her a singular favour.

[026-170]Would he, knowing what he did of her religious views, come to London and

[026-171]administer to her the Holy Sacrament? He did not hesitate, and

[026-172]afterwards declared that he would always regard the occasion as a solemn

[026-173]event in his life. He was devoted to her--though the precise nature of

[026-174]his feelings towards her never quite transpired. Her feelings towards

[026-175]him were more mixed. At first, he was 'that great and good man'--'that

[026-176]true saint, Mr. Jowett'; but, as time went on, some gall was mingled

[026-177]with the balm; the acrimony of her nature asserted itself. She felt that

[026-178]she gave more sympathy than she received; she was exhausted, and she was

[026-179]annoyed by his conversation. Her tongue, one day, could not refrain from

[026-180]shooting out at him: 'He comes to me, and he talks to me,' she said, 'as

[026-181]if I were someone else.'
