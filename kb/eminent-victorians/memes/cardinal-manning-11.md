[011-001]'The great point of anxiety with me, wrote Monsignor Talbot to Manning,

[011-002]'is whether a Congregation will be held, or whether the Holy Father will

[011-003]perform a Pontifical act. He himself is doubting. I therefore say mass

[011-004]and pray every morning that he may have the courage to choose for

[011-005]himself, instead of submitting the matter to a Congregation. Although

[011-006]the Cardinals are determined to reject Dr. Errington, nevertheless I am

[011-007]afraid that they should select one of the others. You know very well

[011-008]that Congregations are guided by the documents that are placed before

[011-009]them; it is for this reason that I should prefer the Pope's acting

[011-010]himself.'

[011-011]But the Holy Father himself was doubting. In his indecision, he ordered

[011-012]a month of prayers and masses. The suspense grew and grew. Everything

[011-013]seemed against Manning. The whole English episcopate was opposed to him;

[011-014]he had quarrelled with the Chapter; he was a convert of but few years'

[011-015]standing; even the congregated Cardinals did not venture to suggest the

[011-016]appointment of such a man. But suddenly, the Holy Father's doubts came

[011-017]to an end. He heard a voice--a mysterious inward voice--whispering

[011-018]something in his ear. 'Mettetelo li! Mettetelo li!' the voice repeated,

[011-019]over and over again. Mettetelo li! It was an inspiration; and Pius IX,

[011-020]brushing aside the recommendations of the Chapter and the deliberations

[011-021]of the Cardinals, made Manning, by a Pontifical act, Archbishop of

[011-022]Westminster.

[011-023]Monsignor Talbot's felicity was complete; and he took occasion in

[011-024]conveying his congratulations to his friend, to make some illuminating

[011-025]reflections upon the great event.

[011-026]'MY policy throughout,' he wrote, 'was never to propose you DIRECTLY to

[011-027]the Pope, but, to make others do so, so that both you and I can always

[011-028]say that it was not I who induced the Holy Father to name you--which

[011-029]would lessen the weight of your appointment. This I say, because many

[011-030]have said that your being named was all my doing. I do not say that the

[011-031]Pope did not know that I thought you the only man eligible--as I took

[011-032]care to tell him over and over again what was against all the other

[011-033]candidates--and in consequence, he was almost driven into naming you.

[011-034]After he had named you, the Holy Father said to me, "What a diplomatist

[011-035]you are, to make what you wished come to pass!"

[011-036]'Nevertheless,' concluded Monsignor Talbot, 'I believe your appointment

[011-037]was specially directed by the Holy Ghost.'

[011-038]Manning himself was apparently of the same opinion.

[011-039]'My dear Child,' he wrote to a lady penitent, 'I have in these last

[011-040]three weeks felt as if our Lord had called me by name. Everything else

[011-041]has passed out of my mind. The firm belief that I have long had that the

[011-042]Holy Father is the most supernatural person I have ever seen has given

[011-043]me this feeling more deeply. 'Still, I feel as if I had been brought,

[011-044]contrary to all human wills, by the Divine Will, into an immediate

[011-045]relation to our Divine Lord.'

[011-046]'If indeed,' he wrote to Lady Herbert, 'it were the will of our Divine

[011-047]Lord to lay upon me this heavy burden, He could have done it in no way

[011-048]more strengthening and consoling to me. To receive it from the hands of

[011-049]His Vicar, and from Pius IX, and after long invocation of the Holy

[011-050]Ghost, and not only without human influences, but in spite of manifold

[011-051]aria powerful human opposition, gives me the last strength for such a

[011-052]cross.'

[011-053]VI

[011-054]MANNING'S appointment filled his opponents with alarm. Wrath and

[011-055]vengeance seemed to be hanging over them; what might not be expected

[011-056]from the formidable enemy against whom they had struggled for so long,

[011-057]and who now stood among them armed with archiepiscopal powers and

[011-058]invested with the special confidence of [[rome|Rome]]? Great was their amazement,

[011-059]great was their relief, when they found that their dreaded master

[011-060]breathed nothing but kindness, gentleness, and conciliation. The old

[011-061]scores, they found, were not to be paid off, but to be wiped out. The

[011-062]new archbishop poured forth upon every side all the tact, all the

[011-063]courtesy, all the dignified graces of a Christian magnanimity. It was

[011-064]impossible to withstand such treatment. Bishops who had spent years in

[011-065]thwarting him became his devoted adherents; even the Chapter of

[011-066]Westminster forgot its hatred. Monsignor Talbot was extremely surprised.

[011-067]'Your greatest enemies have entirely come round,' he wrote. 'I received

[011-068]the other day a panegyric of you from Searle. This change of feeling I

[011-069]cannot attribute to anything but the Holy Ghost.' Monsignor Talbot was

[011-070]very fond of the Holy Ghost; but, so far, at any rate as Searle was

[011-071]concerned, there was another explanation. Manning, instead of dismissing

[011-072]Searle from his position of 'oeconomus' in the episcopal household, had

[011-073]kept him on--at an increased salary; and the poor man, who had not

[011-074]scrupled in the days of his pride to call Manning a thief, was now duly

[011-075]grateful.

[011-076]As to Dr. Errington, he gave an example of humility and submission by at

[011-077]once withdrawing into a complete obscurity. For years the Archbishop of

[011-078]Trebizond, the ejected heir to the See of Westminster, laboured as a

[011-079]parish priest in the Isle of Man. He nursed no resentment in his heart,

[011-080]and, after a long and edifying life of peace and silence, he died in

[011-081]1886, a professor of theology at Clifton.

[011-082]It might be supposed that Manning could now feel that his triumph was

[011-083]complete. His position was secure; his power was absolute; his prestige

[011-084]was daily growing. Yet there was something that irked him still. As he

[011-085]cast his eyes over the Roman Catholic community in England, he was aware

[011-086]of one figure which, by virtue of a peculiar eminence, seemed to

[011-087]challenge the supremacy of his own. That figure was [[john-henry-newman|Newman]]'s.

[011-088]Since his conversion, Newman's life had been a long series of

[011-089]misfortunes and disappointments. When he had left the [[church-of-england|Church of England]],

[011-090]he was its most distinguished, its most revered member, whose words,

[011-091]however strange, were listened to with profound attention, and whose

[011-092]opinions, however dubious, were followed in all their fluctuations with

[011-093]an eager and indeed a trembling respect. He entered the [[catholic-church|Church of Rome]],

[011-094]and found himself forthwith an unimportant man. He was received at the

[011-095]Papal Court with a politeness which only faintly concealed a total lack

[011-096]of interest and understanding. His delicate mind, with its refinements,

[011-097]its hesitations, its complexities--his soft, spectacled, [[oxford-university|Oxford]] manner,

[011-098]with its half-effeminate diffidence-such things were ill calculated to

[011-099]impress a throng of busy Cardinals and Bishops, whose days were spent

[011-100]amid the practical details of ecclesiastical organisation, the

[011-101]long-drawn involutions of papal diplomacy, and the delicious bickerings

[011-102]of personal intrigue. And when, at last, he did succeed in making some

[011-103]impression upon these surroundings, it was no better; it was worse. An

[011-104]uneasy suspicion gradually arose; it began to dawn upon the Roman

[011-105]authorities that Dr. Newman was a man of ideas. Was it possible that Dr.

[011-106]Newman did not understand that ideas in Rome were, to say the least of

[011-107]it, out of place? Apparently, he did not--nor was that all; not content

[011-108]with having ideas, he positively seemed anxious to spread them. When

[011-109]that was known, the politeness in high places was seen to be wearing

[011-110]decidedly thin. His Holiness, who on Newman's arrival had graciously

[011-111]expressed the wish to see him 'again and again', now, apparently, was

[011-112]constantly engaged. At first Newman supposed that the growing coolness

[011-113]was the result of misapprehension; his Italian was faulty, Latin was not

[011-114]spoken at Rome, his writings had only appeared in garbled translations.

[011-115]And even Englishmen had sometimes found his arguments difficult to

[011-116]follow. He therefore determined to take the utmost care to make his

[011-117]views quite clear; his opinions upon religious probability, his

[011-118]distinction between demonstrative and circumstantial evidence, his

[011-119]theory of the development of doctrine and the aspects of ideas--these

[011-120]and many other matters, upon which he had written so much, he would now

[011-121]explain in the simplest language. He would show that there was nothing

[011-122]dangerous in what he held, that there was a passage in De Lugo which

[011-123]supported him--that Perrone, by maintaining that the Immaculate

[011-124]Conception could be defined, had implicitly admitted one of his main

[011-125]positions, and that his language about Faith had been confused, quite

[011-126]erroneously, with the fideism of M. Bautain.

[011-127]Cardinal Barnabo, Cardinal Reisach, Cardinal Antonelli, looked at him

[011-128]with their shrewd eyes and hard faces, while he poured into their ears

[011-129]which, as he had already noticed with distress, were large and not too

[011-130]clean--his careful disquisitions; but, it was all in vain--they had

[011-131]clearly never read De Lugo or Perrone, and as for M. Bautain, they had

[011-132]never heard of him. Newman, in despair, fell back upon St. Thomas

[011-133]Aquinas; but, to his horror, he observed that St. Thomas himself did not

[011-134]mean very much to the Cardinals. With a sinking heart, he realised at

[011-135]last the painful truth: it was not the nature of his views, it was his

[011-136]having views at all, that was objectionable. He had hoped to devote the

[011-137]rest of his life to the teaching of Theology; but what sort of Theology

[011-138]could he teach which would be acceptable to such superiors? He left

[011-139]Rome, and settled down in Birmingham as the head of a small community of

[011-140]Oratorians. He did not complain; it was God's will; it was better so. He

[011-141]would watch and pray.

[011-142]But God's will was not quite so simple as that. Was it right, after all,

[011-143]that a man with Newman's intellectual gifts, his devoted ardour, his

[011-144]personal celebrity, should sink away out of sight and use in the dim

[011-145]recesses of the Oratory at Birmingham? If the call were to come to him

[011-146]to take his talent out of the napkin, how could he refuse? And the call

[011-147]did come. A Catholic University was being started in Ireland and Dr.

[011-148]Cullen, the Archbishop of Armagh, begged Newman to become the Rector. At

[011-149]first he hesitated, but when he learned that it was the Holy Father's

[011-150]wish that he should take up the work, he could doubt no longer; the

[011-151]offer was sent from Heaven. The difficulties before him were very great;

[011-152]not only had a new University to be called up out of the void, but the

[011-153]position was complicated by the presence of a rival institution--the

[011-154]undenominational Queen's Colleges, founded by Peel a few years earlier

[011-155]with the object of giving Irish Catholics facilities for University

[011-156]education on the same terms as their fellow-countrymen. Yet Newman had

[011-157]the highest hopes. He dreamt of something greater than a merely Irish

[011-158]University--of a noble and flourishing centre of learning for the

[011-159]Catholics of Ireland and England alike. And why should not his dream

[011-160]come true? 'In the midst of our difficulties, he said, 'I have one

[011-161]ground of hope, just one stay, but, as I think, a sufficient one, which

[011-162]serves me in the stead of all other argument whatever. It is the

[011-163]decision of the Holy See; St. Peter has spoken.'

[011-164]The years that followed showed to what extent it was safe to depend upon

[011-165]St. Peter. Unforeseen obstacles cropped up on every side. Newman's

[011-166]energies were untiring, but so was the inertia of the Irish authorities.

[011-167]On his appointment, he wrote to Dr. Cullen asking that arrangements

[011-168]might be made for his reception in Dublin. Dr. Cullen did not reply.

[011-169]Newman wrote again, but still there was no answer. Weeks passed, months

[011-170]passed, years passed, and not a word, not a sign, came from Dr. Cullen.

[011-171]At last, after dangling for more than two years in the uncertainties and

[011-172]perplexities of so strange a situation, Newman was summoned to Dublin.

[011-173]There he found nothing but disorder and discouragement. The laity took

[011-174]no interest in the scheme; the clergy actively disliked it; Newman's

[011-175]authority was disregarded. He appealed to Cardinal Wiseman, and then at

[011-176]last a ray of hope dawned. The cardinal suggested that a bishopric

[011-177]should be conferred upon him, to give him a status suitable to his

[011-178]position; Dr. Cullen acquiesced, and Pius IX was all compliance.

[011-179]'Manderemo a Newman la crocetta,' he said to Wiseman, smilingly drawing

[011-180]his hands down each side of his neck to his breast, 'lo faremo vescovo

[011-181]di Porfirio, o qualche luogo.' The news spread among Newman's friends,

[011-182]and congratulations began to come in. But the official intimation seemed

[011-183]to be unaccountably delayed; no crocetta came from Rome, and Cardinal

[011-184]Wiseman never again referred to the matter. Newman was left to gather

[011-185]that the secret representations of Dr. Cullen had brought about a change

[011-186]of counsel in high quarters. His pride did not allow him to inquire

[011-187]further; but one of his lady penitents, Miss Giberne, was less discreet.

[011-188]'Holy Father,' she suddenly said to the Pope in an audience one day,

[011-189]'why don't you make Father Newman a bishop?' Upon which the Holy Father

[011-190]looked much confused and took a great deal of snuff.
