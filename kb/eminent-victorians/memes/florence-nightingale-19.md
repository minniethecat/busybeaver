[019-001]And, indeed, the difficulties in her path were great. For not only was

[019-002]it an almost unimaginable thing in those days for a woman of means to

[019-003]make her own way in the world and to live in independence, but the

[019-004]particular profession for which Florence was clearly marked out both by

[019-005]her instincts and her capacities was at that time a peculiarly

[019-006]disreputable one. A 'nurse' meant then a coarse old woman, always

[019-007]ignorant, usually dirty, often brutal, a Mrs. Gamp, in bunched-up sordid

[019-008]garments, tippling at the brandy bottle or indulging in worse

[019-009]irregularities. The nurses in the hospitals were especially notorious

[019-010]for immoral conduct; sobriety was almost unknown among them; and they

[019-011]could hardly be trusted to carry out the simplest medical duties.

[019-012]Certainly, things HAVE changed since those days; and that they have

[019-013]changed is due, far more than to any other human being, to Miss

[019-014]Nightingale herself. It is not to be wondered at that her parents should

[019-015]have shuddered at the notion of their daughter devoting her life to such

[019-016]an occupation. 'It was as if,' she herself said afterwards, 'I had

[019-017]wanted to be a kitchen-maid.' Yet the want, absurd and impracticable as

[019-018]it was, not only remained fixed immovably in her heart, but grew in

[019-019]intensity day by day. Her wretchedness deepened into a morbid

[019-020]melancholy. Everything about her was vile, and she herself, it was

[019-021]clear, to have deserved such misery, was even viler than her

[019-022]surroundings. Yes, she had sinned--'standing before God's judgment

[019-023]seat'. 'No one,' she declared, 'has so grieved the Holy Spirit'; of that

[019-024]she was quite certain. It was in vain that she prayed to be delivered

[019-025]from vanity and hypocrisy, and she could not bear to smile or to be gay,

[019-026]'because she hated God to hear her laugh, as if she had not repented of

[019-027]her sin'.

[019-028]A weaker spirit would have been overwhelmed by the load of such

[019-029]distresses--would have yielded or snapped. But this extraordinary young

[019-030]woman held firm, and fought her way to victory. With an amazing

[019-031]persistency, during the eight years that followed her rebuff over

[019-032]Salisbury Hospital, she struggled and worked and planned. While

[019-033]superficially she was carrying on the life of a brilliant girl in high

[019-034]society, while internally she was a prey to the tortures of regret and

[019-035]of remorse, she yet possessed the energy to collect the knowledge and to

[019-036]undergo the experience which alone could enable her to do what she had

[019-037]determined she would do in the end. In secret she devoured the reports

[019-038]of medical commissions, the pamphlets of sanitary authorities, the

[019-039]histories of hospitals and homes. She spent the intervals of the London

[019-040]season in ragged schools and workhouses. When she went abroad with her

[019-041]family, she used her spare time so well that there was hardly a great

[019-042]hospital in Europe with which she was not acquainted; hardly a great

[019-043]city whose slums she had not passed through. She managed to spend some

[019-044]days in a convent school in [[rome|Rome]], and some weeks as a 'Soeur de Charite'

[019-045]in Paris. Then, while her mother and sister were taking the waters at

[019-046]Carlsbad, she succeeded in slipping off to a nursing institution at

[019-047]Kaiserswerth, where she remained for more than three months. This was

[019-048]the critical event of her life. The experience which she gained as a

[019-049]nurse at Kaiserswerth formed the foundation of all her future action and

[019-050]finally fixed her in her career.

[019-051]But one other trial awaited her. The allurements of the world she had

[019-052]brushed aside with disdain and loathing; she had resisted the subtler

[019-053]temptation which, in her weariness, had sometimes come upon her, of

[019-054]devoting her baffled energies to art or literature; the last ordeal

[019-055]appeared in the shape of a desirable young man. Hitherto, her lovers had

[019-056]been nothing to her but an added burden and a mockery; but now--for a

[019-057]moment--she wavered. A new feeling swept over her--a feeling which she

[019-058]had never known before--which she was never to know again. The most

[019-059]powerful and the profoundest of all the instincts of humanity laid claim

[019-060]upon her. But it rose before her, that instinct, arrayed--how could it

[019-061]be otherwise?--in the inevitable habiliments of a Victorian marriage;

[019-062]and she had the strength to stamp it underfoot.

[019-063]'I have an intellectual nature which requires satisfaction,' she noted,

[019-064]'and that would find it in him. I have a passionate nature which

[019-065]requires satisfaction, and that would find it in him. I have a moral, an

[019-066]active nature which requires satisfaction, and that would not find it in

[019-067]his life. Sometimes I think that I will satisfy my passionate nature at

[019-068]all events....'

[019-069]But no, she knew in her heart that it could not be. 'To be nailed to a

[019-070]continuation and exaggeration of my present life ... to put it out of my

[019-071]power ever to be able to seize the chance of forming for myself a true

[019-072]and rich life'--that would be a suicide. She made her choice, and

[019-073]refused what was at least a certain happiness for a visionary good which

[019-074]might never come to her at all. And so she returned to her old life of

[019-075]waiting and bitterness.

[019-076]'The thoughts and feelings that I have now,' she wrote, 'I can remember

[019-077]since I was six years old. A profession, a trade, a necessary

[019-078]occupation, something to fill and employ all my faculties, I have always

[019-079]felt essential to me, I have always longed for. The first thought I can

[019-080]remember, and the last, was nursing work; and in the absence of this,

[019-081]education work, but more the education of the bad than of the young ...

[019-082]Everything has been tried--foreign travel, kind friends, everything. My

[019-083]God! What is to become of me?'

[019-084]A desirable young man? Dust and ashes! What was there desirable in such

[019-085]a thing as that? 'In my thirty-first year,' she noted in her diary, 'I

[019-086]see nothing desirable but death.'

[019-087]Three more years passed, and then at last the pressure of time told; her

[019-088]family seemed to realise that she was old enough and strong enough to

[019-089]have her way; and she became the superintendent of a charitable nursing

[019-090]home in Harley Street. She had gained her independence, though it was in

[019-091]a meagre sphere enough; and her mother was still not quite resigned:

[019-092]surely Florence might at least spend the summer in the country. At

[019-093]times, indeed, among her intimates, Mrs. Nightingale almost wept. 'We

[019-094]are ducks,' she said with tears in her eyes, 'who have hatched a wild

[019-095]swan.' But the poor lady was wrong; it was not a swan that they had

[019-096]hatched, it was an eagle.

[019-097]II

[019-098]Miss NIGHTINGALE had been a year in her nursing-home in Harley Street,

[019-099]when Fate knocked at the door. The [[crimean-war|Crimean War]] broke out; the battle of

[019-100]the Alma was fought; and the terrible condition of our military

[019-101]hospitals at [[scutari|Scutari]] began to be known in England. It sometimes happens

[019-102]that the plans of Providence are a little difficult to follow, but on

[019-103]this occasion all was plain; there was a perfect coordination of events.

[019-104]For years [[florence-nightingale|Miss Nightingale]] had been getting ready; at last she was

[019-105]prepared--experienced, free, mature, yet still young (she was

[019-106]thirty-four)--desirous to serve, accustomed to command: at that precise

[019-107]moment the desperate need of a great nation came, and she was there to

[019-108]satisfy it. If the war had fallen a few years earlier, she would have

[019-109]lacked the knowledge, perhaps even the power, for such a work; a few

[019-110]years later and she would, no doubt, have been fixed in the routine of

[019-111]some absorbing task, and moreover, she would have been growing old.

[019-112]Nor was it only the coincidence of time that was remarkable. It so fell

[019-113]out that Sidney Herbert was at the [[war-office|War Office]] and in the Cabinet; and

[019-114]Sidney Herbert was an intimate friend of Miss Nightingale's, convinced,

[019-115]from personal experience in charitable work, of her supreme capacity.

[019-116]After such premises, it seems hardly more than a matter of course that

[019-117]her letter, in which she offered her services for the East, and Sidney

[019-118]Herbert's letter, in which he asked for them, should actually have

[019-119]crossed in the post. Thus it all happened, without a hitch. The

[019-120]appointment was made and even Mrs. Nightingale, overawed by the

[019-121]magnitude of the venture, could only approve. A pair of faithful friends

[019-122]offered themselves as personal attendants; thirty-eight nurses were

[019-123]collected; and within a week of the crossing of the letters Miss

[019-124]Nightingale, amid a great burst of popular enthusiasm, left for

[019-125]Constantinople.

[019-126]Among the numerous letters which she received on her departure was one

[019-127]from Dr. Manning, who at that time was working in comparative obscurity

[019-128]as a Catholic priest in Bayswater. 'God will keep you,' he wrote, 'and

[019-129]my prayer for you will be that your one object of worship, Pattern of

[019-130]Imitation, and source of consolation and strength, may be the Sacred

[019-131]Heart of our Divine Lord.'

[019-132]To what extent Dr. Manning's prayer was answered must remain a matter of

[019-133]doubt; but this much is certain: that if ever a prayer was needed, it

[019-134]was needed then for Florence Nightingale. For dark as had been the

[019-135]picture of the state of affairs at Scutari, revealed to the English

[019-136]public in the dispatches of "The Times Correspondent", and in a

[019-137]multitude of private letters, yet the reality turned out to be darker

[019-138]still. What had occurred was, in brief, the complete breakdown of our

[019-139]medical arrangements at the seat of war. The origins of this awful

[019-140]failure were complex and manifold; they stretched back through long

[019-141]years of peace and carelessness in England; they could be traced through

[019-142]endless ramifications of administrative incapacity--from the inherent

[019-143]faults of confused systems, to the petty bunglings of minor officials,

[019-144]from the inevitable ignorance of Cabinet Ministers, to the fatal

[019-145]exactitudes of narrow routine.

[019-146]In the inquiries which followed, it was clearly shown that the evil was

[019-147]in reality that worst of all evils--one which has been caused by nothing

[019-148]in particular and for which no one in particular is to blame. The whole

[019-149]organisation of the war machine was incompetent and out of date. The old

[019-150]Duke had sat for a generation at the Horse Guards repressing innovations

[019-151]with an iron hand. There was an extraordinary overlapping of authorities

[019-152]and an almost incredible shifting of responsibilities to and fro. As for

[019-153]such a notion as the creation and the maintenance of a really adequate

[019-154]medical service for the army--in that atmosphere of aged chaos, how

[019-155]could it have entered anybody's head? Before the war, the easygoing

[019-156]officials at Westminster were naturally persuaded that all was well--or

[019-157]at least as well as could be expected; when someone, for instance,

[019-158]actually had the temerity to suggest the formation of a corps of Army

[019-159]nurses, he was at once laughed out of court. When the war had begun, the

[019-160]gallant British officers in control of affairs had other things to think

[019-161]about than the petty details of medical organisation. Who had bothered

[019-162]with such trifles in the Peninsula? And surely, on that occasion, we had

[019-163]done pretty well. Thus, the most obvious precautions were neglected, and

[019-164]the most necessary preparations were put off from day to day. The

[019-165]principal medical officer of the Army, Dr. Hall, was summoned from India

[019-166]at a moment's notice, and was unable to visit England before taking up

[019-167]his duties at the front. And it was not until after the battle of the

[019-168]Alma, when we had been at war for many months, that we acquired hospital

[019-169]accommodations at Scutari for more than a thousand men. Errors, follies,

[019-170]and vices on the part of individuals there doubtless were; but, in the

[019-171]general reckoning, they were of small account--insignificant symptoms of

[019-172]the deep disease of the body politic--to the enormous calamity of

[019-173]administrative collapse.

[019-174]Miss Nightingale arrived at Scutari--a suburb of Constantinople, on the

[019-175]Asiatic side of the Bosphorus--on November 4th, 1854; it was ten days

[019-176]after the battle of Balaclava, and the day before the battle of

[019-177]Inkerman. The organisation of the hospitals, which had already given way

[019-178]under the stress of the battle of the Alma, was now to be subjected to

[019-179]the further pressure which these two desperate and bloody engagements

[019-180]implied. Great detachments of wounded were already beginning to pour in.

[019-181]The men, after receiving such summary treatment as could be given them

[019-182]at the smaller hospitals in the Crimea itself, were forthwith shipped in

[019-183]batches of 200 across the Black Sea to Scutari. This voyage was in

[019-184]normal times one of four days and a half; but the times were no longer

[019-185]normal, and now the transit often lasted for a fortnight or three weeks.

[019-186]It received, not without reason, the name of the 'middle passage'.

[019-187]Between, and sometimes on the decks, the wounded, the sick, and the

[019-188]dying were crowded--men who had just undergone the amputation of limbs,

[019-189]men in the clutches of fever or of frostbite, men in the last stages of

[019-190]dysentry and cholera--without beds, sometimes without blankets, often

[019-191]hardly clothed. The one or two surgeons on board did what they could;

[019-192]but medical stores were lacking, and the only form of nursing available

[019-193]was that provided by a handful of invalid soldiers who were usually

[019-194]themselves prostrate by the end of the voyage. There was no other food

[019-195]beside the ordinary salt rations of ship diet; and even the water was

[019-196]sometimes so stored that it was out of reach of the weak. For many

[019-197]months, the average of deaths during these voyages was seventy-four in

[019-198]1,000; the corpses were shot out into the waters; and who shall say that

[019-199]they were the most unfortunate? At Scutari, the landing-stage,

[019-200]constructed with all the perverseness of Oriental ingenuity, could only

[019-201]be approached with great difficulty, and, in rough weather, not at all.

[019-202]When it was reached, what remained of the men in the ships had first to

[019-203]be disembarked, and then conveyed up a steep slope of a quarter of a

[019-204]mile to the nearest of the hospitals. The most serious cases might be

[019-205]put upon stretchers--for there were far too few for all; the rest were

[019-206]carried or dragged up the hill by such convalescent soldiers as could be

[019-207]got together, who were not too obviously infirm for the work. At last

[019-208]the journey was accomplished; slowly, one by one, living or dying, the

[019-209]wounded were carried up into the hospital. And in the hospital what did

[019-210]they find?
