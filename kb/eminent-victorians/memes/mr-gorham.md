# Rev. George Cornelius Gorham

The evangelical clergyman whose disputed right to a parish living triggered the Gorham Case — a landmark ecclesiastical controversy that shook the [[church-of-england|Church of England]] and propelled [[cardinal-manning]] towards [[rome|Rome]].

## Life

George Cornelius Gorham was born in 1787, educated at Cambridge, and ordained as a clergyman in the [[church-of-england|Church of England]]. By 1847, he was a respected but controversial figure — an evangelical incumbent whose views on baptismal regeneration were at odds with the High Church party that then dominated the Church's hierarchy.

### The Gorham Case

> "In November, 1847, the Reverend Mr. Gorham was presented by the Lord Chancellor to the living of Brampford Speke, in the Diocese of Exeter." [008-027]

The crisis began when Gorham was presented to a new living in the Diocese of Exeter, whose bishop, [[henry-phillpotts]], was a High Churchman of the strictest sort. The Bishop of Exeter, Henry Phillpotts, suspecting that Gorham held evangelical views on baptism, subjected him to a theological examination. His conclusion was damning: "Mr. Gorham held heretical doctrines on the subject of baptismal regeneration" [008-034]. The Bishop refused to institute Gorham to the living.

Gorham, "thereupon, took proceedings in the Court of Arches" [008-036], beginning a legal battle that would escalate all the way to the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council — the highest court of appeal for ecclesiastical matters.

### The Judgment and Its Aftermath

> "The Gorham judgment became a bit between his teeth." [008-190]

The Privy Council ultimately ruled in Gorham's favour, holding that his views were within the permissible range of Anglican doctrine. The judgment was a devastating blow to the High Church party, which had insisted that the Church's teaching on baptismal regeneration was settled and could not be questioned by a civil court.

For Manning, then an Anglican Archdeacon, the Gorham judgment was a turning point. The spectacle of a secular court determining the doctrine of the [[church-of-england|Church of England]] convinced him that the established Church was fundamentally compromised. "The Gorham Case," Strachey writes, was the catalyst for Manning's conversion — "the bit between his teeth" that drove him towards [[rome|Rome]].

### Later Years

Gorham himself seems to have taken little satisfaction from the controversy. He served quietly at Brampford Speke for the remainder of his life, dying in 1857, while the aftershocks of his case continued to reverberate through the [[church-of-england|Church of England]] and beyond.

## Character and Beliefs

Gorham was, by all accounts, a sincere and unyielding evangelical. His theology was not unusual for his party — it was the timing and the adversarial nature of the Bishop of Exeter that turned a routine examination into a cause célèbre. Strachey treats Gorham not as a protagonist but as a catalyst: the obscure clergyman whose case set in motion events far larger than himself.

## Historical Significance

The Gorham Case was one of the most significant ecclesiastical legal disputes of the Victorian period. It exposed the tensions between the [[church-of-england|Church of England]]'s dual identity as both a spiritual body and an established Church subject to civil law. For the [[oxford-movement|Oxford Movement]], the case was a catastrophe that accelerated the drift of its most prominent figures — Manning above all — towards [[rome|Rome]].
