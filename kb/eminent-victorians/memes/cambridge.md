# Cambridge

The university town that stood, in the imagination of Victorian England, as one of the two great poles of intellectual and ecclesiastical formation. Strachey invokes Cambridge at decisive moments in the lives of both Cardinal Manning and Dr. Arnold — for Manning, as the road not taken toward a comfortable Anglican establishment; for Arnold, as the place where his own Oxford-bred convictions were sharpened by contrast. "Whose cloisters," Strachey writes, "have ever been the natural refuge of those who prefer the established order to the uncomfortable splendours of the truth" [^1].

## In the Narrative

Cambridge enters the Manning chapters early, in a speculative register. Manning's father had been a Cambridge man, and the young Manning himself seemed destined for the university — "who can doubt that" the course of English religious history would have been different had he gone there, Strachey muses, settling into the comfortable Anglicanism of the Cambridge cloisters rather than the Roman certainties of the Oxford Movement [^2]. The passage is pure Stracheyan irony: Cambridge stands for the safe, the moderate, the English — everything Manning, in his fierce pursuit of absolute authority, was not.

### Association with Dr. Arnold

For Dr. Arnold, Cambridge belonged to the world of his pupils rather than his own formation — he was an Oxford man to the bone. Yet Arnold's influence reached Cambridge through his educational writings. When a Cambridge undergraduate wrote to him, "I quite agree" with his views, it was a small sign of the Arnoldian tide lapping at the older university's gates [^3]. The Duke of Cambridge, a royal personage of military rank, appears in the Gordon narrative as a figure of institutional resistance [^4].

## Key Sites in the Narrative

- **The Colleges**: The "cloisters" of Cambridge, which Strachey treats as a metonym for the established Anglican order — comfortable, dignified, and resistant to the fierce certainties that Manning and Newman brought from Oxford [^1]
- **The Senate House**: The administrative heart of the university, where the intellectual conservatism that Arnold opposed found its institutional expression
