# Roman Catholic Church

The institution that defined Manning's later career and provided Strachey with his richest material for ironic observation. The Catholic Church in Victorian England was simultaneously a persecuted minority (emancipated only in 1829), a growing force (swelled by Oxford Movement converts), and a theatre of internal conflict between ultramontanes and liberals.

## Role in the Narrative

The Church appears primarily in Manning's biography as the terminus of his theological journey — the institution whose claims to infallibility satisfied his craving for certainty. Strachey traces the Church's English revival from the Catholic Emancipation Act of 1829 through the restoration of the hierarchy in 1850 (which provoked the anti-Catholic "Papal Aggression" panic) to the triumph of Ultramontanism at the First Vatican Council.

### Under Manning

As Archbishop of Westminster, Manning was the Church's chief English advocate. He built churches, established schools, founded a Catholic university (thwarted by Newman's competing vision), and at the Vatican Council secured the definition of Papal Infallibility. Strachey notes with amusement that Manning's ultramontane Catholicism was, in its centralising logic, an ecclesiastical mirror of the imperial administration he had once hoped to serve as a colonial governor.
