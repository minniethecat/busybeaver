[037-001]With this Spartan ferocity there was mingled the glamour and the mystery

[037-002]of the East. The Mahdi himself, his four Khalifas, and the principal

[037-003]Emirs, masters of sudden riches, surrounded themselves with slaves and

[037-004]women, with trains of horses and asses, with body guards and glittering

[037-005]arms. There were rumours of debaucheries in high places--of the Mahdi,

[037-006]forgetful of his own ordinances, revelling in the recesses of his harem,

[037-007]and quaffing date syrup mixed with ginger out of the silver cups looted

[037-008]from the church of the Christians. But that imposing figure had only to

[037-009]show itself for the tongue of scandal to be stilled. The tall,

[037-010]broad-shouldered, majestic man, with the dark face and black beard and

[037-011]great eyes--who could doubt that he was the embodiment of a superhuman

[037-012]power? Fascination dwelt in every movement, every glance. The eyes,

[037-013]painted with antimony, flashed extraordinary fires; the exquisite smile

[037-014]revealed, beneath the vigorous lips, white upper teeth with a V-shaped

[037-015]space between them--the certain sign of fortune. His turban was folded

[037-016]with faultless art, his jibbeh, speckless, was perfumed with

[037-017]sandal-wood, musk, and attar of roses. He was at once all courtesy and

[037-018]all command. Thousands followed him, thousands prostrated themselves

[037-019]before him; thousands, when he lifted up his voice in solemn worship,

[037-020]knew that the heavens were opened and that they had come near to God.

[037-021]Then all at once the onbeia--the elephant's-tusk trumpet--would give out

[037-022]its enormous sound. The nahas--the brazen wardrums--would summon, with

[037-023]their weird rolling, the whole host to arms. The green flag and the red

[037-024]flag and the black flag would rise over the multitude. The great army

[037-025]would move forward, coloured, glistening, dark, violent, proud,

[037-026]beautiful. The drunkenness, the madness of religion would blaze on every

[037-027]face; and the Mahdi, immovable on his charger, would let the scene grow

[037-028]under his eyes in silence.

[037-029]El Obeid fell in January, 1883. Meanwhile, events of the deepest

[037-030]importance had occurred in Egypt. The rise of Arabi had synchronised

[037-031]with that of the Mahdi. Both movements were nationalist; both were

[037-032]directed against alien rulers who had shown themselves unfit to rule.

[037-033]While the Sudanese were shaking off the yoke of Egypt, the Egyptians

[037-034]themselves grew impatient of their own masters--the Turkish and

[037-035]Circassian Pashas who filled with their incompetence all the high

[037-036]offices of state. The army led by Ahmed Arabi, a Colonel of fellah

[037-037]origin, mutinied, the Khedive gave way, and it seemed as if a new order

[037-038]were about to be established. A new order was indeed upon the point of

[037-039]appearing: but it was of a kind undreamt of in Arabi's philosophy. At

[037-040]the critical moment, the English Government intervened. An English fleet

[037-041]bombarded Alexandria, an English army landed under Lord Wolseley, and

[037-042]defeated Arabi and his supporters at Tel-el-kebir. The rule of the

[037-043]Pashas was nominally restored; but henceforth, in effect, the English

[037-044]were masters of Egypt.

[037-045]Nevertheless, the English themselves were slow to recognise this fact:

[037-046]their Government had intervened unwillingly; the occupation of the

[037-047]country was a merely temporary measure; their army was to be withdrawn

[037-048]as soon as a tolerable administration had been set up. But a tolerable

[037-049]administration, presided over by the Pashas, seemed long in coming, and

[037-050]the English army remained. In the meantime, the Mahdi had entered El

[037-051]Obeid, and his dominion was rapidly spreading over the greater part of

[037-052]the Sudan.

[037-053]Then a terrible catastrophe took place. The Pashas, happy once more in

[037-054]Cairo, pulling the old strings and growing fat over the old flesh-pots,

[037-055]decided to give the world an unmistakable proof of their renewed vigour.

[037-056]They would tolerate the insurrection in the Sudan no longer; they would

[037-057]destroy the Mahdi, reduce his followers to submission, and re-establish

[037-058]their own beneficent rule over the whole country. To this end they

[037-059]collected together an army of 10,000 men, and placed it under the

[037-060]command of Colonel Hicks, a retired English officer. He was ordered to

[037-061]advance and suppress the rebellion. In these proceedings the English

[037-062]Government refused to take any part. Unable, or unwilling, to realise

[037-063]that, so long as there was an English army in Egypt they could not avoid

[037-064]the responsibilities of supreme power, they declared that the domestic

[037-065]policy of the Egyptian administration was no concern of theirs. It was a

[037-066]fatal error--an error which they themselves, before many weeks were

[037-067]over, were to be forced by the hard logic of events to admit. The

[037-068]Pashas, left to their own devices, mismanaged the Hicks expedition to

[037-069]their hearts' content. The miserable troops, swept together from the

[037-070]relics of Arabi's disbanded army, were dispatched to [[khartoum|Khartoum]] in chains.

[037-071]After a month's drilling, they were pronounced to be fit to attack the

[037-072]fanatics of the Sudan. Colonel Hicks was a brave man; urged on by the

[037-073]authorities in Cairo, he shut his eyes to the danger ahead of him, and

[037-074]marched out from Khartoum in the direction of El Obeid at the beginning

[037-075]of September, 1883. Abandoning his communications, he was soon deep in

[037-076]the desolate wastes of Kordofan. As he advanced, his difficulties

[037-077]increased; the guides were treacherous, the troops grew exhausted, the

[037-078]supply of water gave out. He pressed on, and at last, on November 5th,

[037-079]not far from El Obeid, the harassed, fainting, almost desperate army

[037-080]plunged into a vast forest of gumtrees and mimosa scrub. There was a

[037-081]sudden, appalling yell; the Mahdi, with 40,000 of his finest men, sprang

[037-082]from their ambush. The Egyptians were surrounded, and immediately

[037-083]overpowered. It was not a defeat, but an annihilation. Hicks and his

[037-084]European staff were slaughtered; the whole army was slaughtered; 300

[037-085]wounded wretches crept away into the forest.

[037-086]The consequences of this event were felt in every part of the Sudan. To

[037-087]the westward, in Darfur, the Governor, Slatin Pasha, after a prolonged

[037-088]and valiant resistance, was forced to surrender, and the whole province

[037-089]fell into the hands of the rebels. Southwards, in the Bahr-el-Ghazal,

[037-090]Lupton Bey was shut up in a remote stronghold, while the country was

[037-091]overrun. The Mahdi's triumphs were beginning to penetrate even into the

[037-092]tropical regions of Equatoria; the tribes were rising, and Emir Pasha

[037-093]was preparing to retreat towards the Great Lakes. On the East, Osman

[037-094]Digna pushed the insurrection right up to the shores of the Red Sea and

[037-095]laid siege to Suakin. Before the year was over, with the exception of a

[037-096]few isolated and surrounded garrisons, the Mahdi was absolute lord of a

[037-097]territory equal to the combined area of Spain, France, and Germany; and

[037-098]his victorious armies were rapidly closing round Khartoum.

[037-099]When the news of the Hicks disaster reached Cairo, the Pashas calmly

[037-100]announced that they would collect another army of 10,000 men, and again

[037-101]attack the Mahdi; but the English Government understood at last the

[037-102]gravity of the case. They saw that a crisis was upon them, and that they

[037-103]could no longer escape the implications of their position in Egypt. What

[037-104]were they to do? Were they to allow the Egyptians to become more and

[037-105]more deeply involved in a ruinous, perhaps ultimately a fatal, war with

[037-106]the Mahdi? And, if not, what steps were they to take?

[037-107]A small minority of the party then in power in England--the Liberal

[037-108]Party--were anxious to withdraw from Egypt altogether and at once. On

[037-109]the other hand, another and a more influential minority, with

[037-110]representatives in the Cabinet, were in favour of a more active

[037-111]intervention in Egyptian affairs--of the deliberate use of the power of

[037-112]England to give to Egypt internal stability and external security; they

[037-113]were ready, if necessary, to take the field against the Mahdi with

[037-114]English troops. But the great bulk of the party, and the Cabinet, with

[037-115][[william-ewart-gladstone|Mr. Gladstone]] at their head, preferred a middle course. Realising the

[037-116]impracticality of an immediate withdrawal, they were nevertheless

[037-117]determined to remain in Egypt not a moment longer than was necessary,

[037-118]and, in the meantime, to interfere as little as possible in Egyptian

[037-119]affairs.

[037-120]From a campaign in the Sudan conducted by an English army they were

[037-121]altogether averse. If, therefore, the English army was not to be used,

[037-122]and the Egyptian army was not fit to be used against the Mahdi, it

[037-123]followed that any attempt to reconquer the Sudan must be abandoned; the

[037-124]remaining Egyptian troops must be withdrawn, and in future military

[037-125]operations must be limited to those of a strictly defensive kind. Such

[037-126]was the decision of the English Government. Their determination was

[037-127]strengthened by two considerations: in the first place, they saw that

[037-128]the Mahdi's rebellion was largely a nationalist movement, directed

[037-129]against an alien power, and, in the second place, the policy of

[037-130]withdrawal from the Sudan was the policy of their own representative in

[037-131]Egypt, Sir Evelyn Baring, who had lately been appointed Consul-General

[037-132]at Cairo. There was only one serious obstacle in the way--the attitude

[037-133]of the Pashas at the head of the Egyptian Government. The infatuated old

[037-134]men were convinced that they would have better luck next time, that

[037-135]another army and another Hicks would certainly destroy the Mahdi, and

[037-136]that, even if the Mahdi were again victorious, yet another army and yet

[037-137]another Hicks would no doubt be forthcoming, and that THEY would do the

[037-138]trick, or, failing that ... but they refused to consider eventualities

[037-139]any further. In the face of such opposition, the English Government,

[037-140]unwilling as they were to interfere, saw that there was no choice open

[037-141]to them but to exercise pressure. They therefore instructed Sir Evelyn

[037-142]Baring, in the event of the Egyptian Government refusing to withdraw

[037-143]from the Sudan, to insist upon the Khedive's appointing other Ministers

[037-144]who would be willing to do so.

[037-145]Meanwhile, not only the Government, but the public in England were

[037-146]beginning to realise the alarming nature of the Egyptian situation. It

[037-147]was some time before the details of the Hicks expedition were fully

[037-148]known, but when they were, and when the appalling character of the

[037-149]disaster was understood, a thrill of horror ran through the country. The

[037-150]newspapers became full of articles on the Sudan, of personal

[037-151]descriptions of the Mahdi, of agitated letters from colonels and

[037-152]clergymen demanding vengeance, and of serious discussions of future

[037-153]policy in Egypt. Then, at the beginning of the new year, alarming

[037-154]messages began to arrive from Khartoum. Colonel Coetlogon, who was in

[037-155]command of the Egyptian troops, reported a menacing concentration of the

[037-156]enemy. Day by day, hour by hour, affairs grew worse. The Egyptians were

[037-157]obviously outnumbered: they could not maintain themselves in the field;

[037-158]Khartoum was in danger; at any moment, its investment might be complete.

[037-159]And, with Khartoum once cut off from communication with Egypt, what

[037-160]might not happen? Colonel Coetlogon began to calculate how long the city

[037-161]would hold out. Perhaps it could not resist the Mahdi for a month,

[037-162]perhaps for more than a month; but he began to talk of the necessity of

[037-163]a speedy retreat. It was clear that a climax was approaching, and that

[037-164]measures must be taken to forestall it at once. Accordingly, Sir Evelyn

[037-165]Baring, on receipt of final orders from England, presented an ultimatum

[037-166]to the Egyptian Government: the Ministry must either sanction the

[037-167]evacuation of the Sudan, or it must resign. The Ministry was obstinate,

[037-168]and, on January 7th, 1884, it resigned, to be replaced by a more pliable

[037-169]body of Pashas. On the same day, [[general-gordon|General Gordon]] arrived at Southampton.

[037-170]He was over fifty, and he was still, by the world's measurements, an

[037-171]unimportant man. In spite of his achievements, in spite of a certain

[037-172]celebrity--for 'Chinese Gordon' was still occasionally spoken of--he was

[037-173]unrecognised and almost unemployed.

[037-174]He had spent a lifetime in the dubious services of foreign governments,

[037-175]punctuated by futile drudgeries at home; and now, after a long idleness,

[037-176]he had been sent for--to do what?--to look after the Congo for the King

[037-177]of the Belgians. At his age, even if he survived the work and the

[037-178]climate, he could hardly look forward to any subsequent appointment; he

[037-179]would return from the Congo, old and worn out, to a red-brick villa and

[037-180]extinction. Such were General Gordon's prospects on January 7th, 1884.

[037-181]By January 18th, his name was on every tongue, he was the favourite of

[037-182]the nation, he had been declared to be the one living man capable of

[037-183]coping with the perils of the hour; he had been chosen, with unanimous

[037-184]approval, to perform a great task; and he had left England on a mission

[037-185]which was to bring him not only a boundless popularity, but an immortal

[037-186]fame. The circumstances which led to a change so sudden and so

[037-187]remarkable are less easily explained than might have been wished. An

[037-188]ambiguity hangs over them--an ambiguity which the discretion of eminent

[037-189]persons has certainly not diminished. But some of the facts are clear

[037-190]enough.
