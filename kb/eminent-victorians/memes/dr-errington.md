# Dr. George Errington

Catholic Archbishop of Trebizond *in partibus infidelium*, appointed as coadjutor to [[cardinal-wiseman]] but deposed in one of the most dramatic ecclesiastical power struggles of Victorian Catholicism.

## Life

George Errington was born in 1804 and ordained as a Catholic priest. A member of the "Old Catholic" tradition — those English Catholics who had kept the faith through the penal centuries — Errington represented a different strand of Catholicism from the [[oxford-movement|Oxford Movement]] converts like [[cardinal-manning]] and [[john-henry-newman]].

### The Coadjutorship Crisis

> "Not only was Dr. Errington an Old Catholic of the most rigid type, but his character was singularly unyielding." [009-124]

Errington's dramatic appearance in *Eminent Victorians* centres on his appointment as coadjutor to Cardinal Wiseman, Archbishop of Westminster, with the right of succession. This appointment, secured by the Old Catholic party, was a direct threat to Manning's ambitions. Manning, who had set his sights on Westminster, could not afford to see an Old Catholic with an unyielding character sitting in the Archbishop's chair.

The ensuing struggle was, in Strachey's telling, a masterpiece of ecclesiastical politics. Manning, working through his ally [[monsignor-talbot]] in [[rome|Rome]], systematically undermined Errington's position. "The struggle over St. Edmund's College" [010-059] became the flashpoint, as Manning used every tool of influence at his disposal.

### The Deposition

When the moment of crisis arrived, Manning and Talbot struck decisively. Talbot, at his post in the [[rome|Vatican]], "used every effort" [010-123] to secure Errington's removal. The result was that Errington was "liberated" from his coadjutorship [010-125] — a euphemism for deposition that Strachey deploys with characteristic irony.

> "As to Dr. Errington, he gave an example of humility and resignation rare in the annals of the Church." [011-076]

### Later Years

Errington retired from active ecclesiastical life after his deposition. He died in 1886, having witnessed Manning's complete triumph — Manning succeeded Wiseman as Archbishop of Westminster and was eventually created Cardinal.

## Character and Beliefs

Strachey describes Errington as "short, thickset, determined" [010-014] — a physical description that captures his stubborn, unyielding character. He was an Old Catholic by conviction and temperament, suspicious of the Ultramontane enthusiasm of the converts. His tragedy was to be caught between Manning's ambition and the changing currents of Roman politics.

## Relationships

- [[cardinal-manning]]: Errington's adversary, who orchestrated his deposition
- [[cardinal-wiseman]]: The Archbishop to whom Errington was appointed coadjutor
- [[monsignor-talbot]]: Manning's ally in [[rome|Rome]], who executed the deposition
- [[pope-pius-ix]]: The Pope who approved Errington's removal

## Historical Significance

Errington's case illustrates the transformation of English Catholicism in the Victorian period. The Old Catholic tradition he represented — recusant, insular, suspicious of Roman centralization — was being supplanted by the Ultramontane Catholicism of Manning and his allies. Errington's deposition was a turning point in that transition.

## In This Book

Errington appears exclusively in the Manning section (Chapters 1–17). He is a minor figure, but his fate illuminates the central themes of Strachey's portrait of Manning: the uses of power, the alliance between English ambition and Roman intrigue, and the human cost of ecclesiastical politics.
