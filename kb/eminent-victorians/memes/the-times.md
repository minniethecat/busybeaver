# The Times

The Victorian newspaper that Strachey treats as a character in its own right — a [[british-army|military]] supply chain, a [[florence-nightingale|Florence Nightingale]] ally, a political platform for [[general-gordon|General Gordon]], and ultimately a mirror held up to an age of publicity. Founded in 1785, The Times of London had by the mid-nineteenth century become the voice of the British establishment: its columns shaped parliamentary debate in the [[house-of-commons|House of Commons]], its war correspondent dispatches exposed the horrors of the [[crimean-war|Crimean War]], and its editorial campaigns could compel cabinets to act or to fall.

## Role in the Narrative

The Times appears across three of Strachey's four biographies, always at moments of crisis where private conviction meets public opinion. Its presence in the narrative is less that of a journalistic institution and more that of a mediating force between the individual and the state — sometimes enabling reform, sometimes amplifying folly.

### The Crimean War and Florence Nightingale

The most sustained engagement with The Times occurs during Nightingale's mission to Scutari. In the chaos of the Barrack Hospital, where the [[british-army|British Army]]'s medical services had collapsed, The Times emerged as an essential auxiliary. Mr. Macdonald, the paper's special correspondent, arrived simultaneously with Nightingale and brought with him the substantial balance of The Times Fund — money raised from readers to aid sick and wounded soldiers. Macdonald "had the sense to see that the best use he could make of The Times Fund was to put it at the disposal of Miss Nightingale" (020-073). This alliance of journalism and nursing was transformative: laundry boilers, clothing, kitchens, and entire hospital wards were "defrayed from her own funds and that of The Times" (020-164). Strachey wryly notes that officialdom, by contrast, regarded The Times Fund as an affront to departmental propriety. Lord Stratford de Redcliffe, asked how the money should be spent, "answered that there was indeed one object to which it might very well be devoted — the building of an English Protestant Church at Pera" (020-084).

> "I cannot conceive," wrote an eye-witness, "as I now calmly look back on the first three weeks after the arrival of the wounded from Inkerman, how it could have been possible to have avoided a state of things too disastrous to contemplate, had not Miss Nightingale been there, with the means placed at her disposal by Mr. Macdonald." (020-077)

### General Gordon and the Power of the Press

The Times played an even more decisive — and ultimately fatal — role in the Gordon affair. Gordon's public letter to Sir Samuel Baker, elaborating his views on the Sudan, was "intended for publication, and published it was in 'The Times' of January 14th" (038-076). This appearance, on the same day the Government was secretly cabling about Gordon's fate, helped catalyze the decision to send him to Khartoum. Later, after Gordon's appointment had collapsed into siege, The Times became a vector for the popular agitation that demanded a relief expedition. The Baroness Burdett-Coutts "wrote an agitated letter to 'The Times' begging for further subscriptions" (041-031) for Gordon's safety. And at Khartoum itself, Gordon confided his desire for the appointment of Zobeir to Mr. Power, "the English Consul at Khartoum, and the special correspondent of 'The Times'" (040-130) — a disclosure that Strachey implies backfired catastrophically by stiffening parliamentary opposition to the plan.

### Manning and the Established Press

Even in Manning's story, The Times makes its appearance. At a key moment in Manning's ecclesiastical career, "the following statement appeared in 'The Times'" (017-069), demonstrating how even the most autocratic of churchmen could not escape the reach of the press. The newspaper's prominence was such that public figures across every sphere — church, state, army — understood that a Times editorial, a Times letter, or a Times correspondent's dispatch carried the force of an official pronouncement.

## Historical Significance

The Times' role in *Eminent Victorians* illustrates a broader Strachean thesis: that the Victorian age was, in crucial respects, the first media age. Public opinion, channeled through the press, became a force that even the most powerful individuals could neither ignore nor fully control. The Times Fund at Scutari demonstrated that a newspaper could function as an emergency relief organisation more effectively than the [[war-office|War Office]]. Gordon's letter to The Times demonstrated that a single publication could trigger a sequence of decisions leading to a national tragedy. Strachey's irony is sharpest precisely here: the same newspaper that exposed the Crimean disaster and enabled Nightingale's reforms also helped create the conditions for Gordon's destruction.

> Gordon sat at Khartoum "opening The Times and suddenly discovering, to his horror, that the English Government had decided to evacuate the Sudan" (044-081) — a man learning his fate in the morning paper, a politician of empire undone by the thing he had tried to use. Later, haunted by the execution of two Egyptian officers, Gordon was tormented by the thought that "'The Times', perhaps, would" (045-005) publish the damning truth.
