[030-001]He had planned a great work on Church and State, in which he intended to

[030-002]lay bare the causes and to point out the remedies of the evils which

[030-003]afflicted society. Its theme was to be, not the alliance or union, but

[030-004]the absolute identity of the Church and the State; and he felt sure that

[030-005]if only this fundamental truth were fully realised by the public, a

[030-006]general reformation would follow. Unfortunately, however, as time went

[030-007]on, the public seemed to realise it less and less. In spite of his

[030-008]protests, not only were Jews admitted to Parliament, but a Jew was

[030-009]actually appointed a governor of Christ's Hospital; and Scripture was

[030-010]not made an obligatory subject at the London University.

[030-011]There was one point in his theory which was not quite plain to Dr.

[030-012]Arnold. If Church and State were absolutely identical, it became

[030-013]important to decide precisely which classes of persons were to be

[030-014]excluded, owing to their beliefs, from the community. Jews, for

[030-015]instance, were decidedly outside the pale; while Dissenters--so Dr.

[030-016]Arnold argued--were as decidedly within it. But what was the position of

[030-017]the Unitarians? Were they, or were they not, members of the Church of

[030-018]Christ? This was one of those puzzling questions which deepened the

[030-019]frown upon the Doctor's forehead and intensified the pursing of his

[030-020]lips. He thought long and earnestly upon the subject; he wrote elaborate

[030-021]letters on it to various correspondents; but his conclusions remained

[030-022]indefinite. 'My great objection to Unitarianism,' he wrote, 'in its

[030-023]present form in England, is that it makes Christ virtually dead.' Yet he

[030-024]expressed 'a fervent hope that if we could get rid of the Athanasian

[030-025]Creed many good Unitarians would join their fellow Christians in bowing

[030-026]the knee to Him who is Lord both of the dead and the living'. Amid these

[030-027]perplexities, it was disquieting to learn that 'Unitarianism is becoming

[030-028]very prevalent in Boston'. He inquired anxiously as to its 'complexion'

[030-029]there; but received no very illuminating answer. The whole matter

[030-030]continued to be wrapped in a painful obscurity, There were, he believed,

[030-031]Unitarians and Unitarians; and he could say no more.

[030-032]In the meantime, pending the completion of his great work, he occupied

[030-033]himself with putting forward various suggestions of a practical kind. He

[030-034]advocated the restoration of the Order of Deacons, which, he observed,

[030-035]had long been 'quoad the reality, dead; for he believed that 'some plan

[030-036]of this sort might be the small end of the wedge, by which Antichrist

[030-037]might hereafter be burst asunder like the Dragon of Bel's temple'. But

[030-038]the Order of Deacons was never restored, and Dr. Arnold turned his

[030-039]attention elsewhere, urging in a weighty pamphlet the desirabitity of

[030-040]authorising military officers, in congregations where it was impossible

[030-041]to procure the presence of clergy, to administer the Eucharist, as well

[030-042]as Baptism. It was with the object of laying such views as these before

[030-043]the public--'to tell them plainly', as he said, 'the evils that exist,

[030-044]and lead them, if I can, to their causes and remedies'--that he started,

[030-045]in 1831, a weekly newspaper, "The Englishman's Register". The paper was

[030-046]not a success, in spite of the fact that it set out to improve its

[030-047]readers morally and, that it preserved, in every article, an avowedly

[030-048]Christian tone. After a few weeks, and after he had spent upon it more

[030-049]than L200, it came to an end.

[030-050]Altogether, the prospect was decidedly discouraging. After all his

[030-051]efforts, the absolute identity of Church and State remained as

[030-052]unrecognised as ever.

[030-053]'So deep', he was at last obliged to confess, 'is the distinction

[030-054]between the Church and the State seated in our laws, our language, and

[030-055]our very notions, that nothing less than a miraculous interposition of

[030-056]God's Providence seems capable of eradicating it.'

[030-057]Dr. Arnold waited in vain.

[030-058]But, he did not wait in idleness. He attacked the same question from

[030-059]another side: he explored the writings of the Christian Fathers, and

[030-060]began to compose a commentary on the New Testament. In his view, the

[030-061]Scriptures were as fit a subject as any other book for free inquiry and

[030-062]the exercise of the individual judgment, and it was in this spirit that

[030-063]he set about the interpretation of them. He was not afraid of facing

[030-064]apparent difficulties, of admitting inconsistencies, or even errors, in

[030-065]the sacred text. Thus he observed that 'in Chronicles xi, 20 and xiii,

[030-066]2, there is a decided difference in the parentage of Abijah's

[030-067]mother;'--'which', he added, 'is curious on any supposition'. And at one

[030-068]time he had serious doubts as to the authorship of the Epistle to the

[030-069]Hebrews. But he was able, on various problematical points, to suggest

[030-070]interesting solutions.

[030-071]At first, for instance, he could not but be startled by the cessation of

[030-072]miracles in the early Church; but upon consideration, he came to the

[030-073]conclusion that this phenomenon might be 'truly accounted for by the

[030-074]supposition that none but the Apostles ever conferred miraculous powers,

[030-075]and that therefore they ceased of course, after one generation'. Nor did

[030-076]he fail to base his exegesis, whenever possible, upon an appeal to

[030-077]general principles. One of his admirers points out how Dr. Arnold

[030-078]'vindicated God's command to Abraham to sacrifice his son and to the

[030-079]Jews to exterminate the nations of Canaan', by explaining the principles

[030-080]on which these commands were given, and their reference to the moral

[030-081]state of those to whom they were addressed--thereby educing light out of

[030-082]darkness, unravelling the thread of God's religious education of the

[030-083]human race, and holding up God's marvellous counsels to the devout

[030-084]wonder and meditation of the thoughtful believer'.

[030-085]There was one of his friends, however, who did not share this admiration

[030-086]for the Doctor's methods of Scriptural interpretation. W. G. Ward, while

[030-087]still a young man at [[oxford-university|Oxford]], had come under his influence, and had been

[030-088]for some time one of his most enthusiastic disciples. But the star of

[030-089][[john-henry-newman|Newman]] was rising at the University; Ward soon felt the attraction of

[030-090]that magnetic power; and his belief in his old teacher began to waver.

[030-091]It was, in particular, Dr. Arnold's treatment of the Scriptures which

[030-092]filled Ward's argumentative mind, at first with distrust, and at last

[030-093]with positive antagonism. To subject the Bible to free inquiry, to

[030-094]exercise upon it the criticism of the individual judgment--where might

[030-095]not such methods lead? Who could say that they would not end in

[030-096]Socinianism?--nay, in Atheism itself? If the text of Scripture was to be

[030-097]submitted to the searchings of human reason, how could the question of

[030-098]its inspiration escape the same tribunal? And the proofs of revelation,

[030-099]and even of the existence of God? What human faculty was capable of

[030-100]deciding upon such enormous questions? And would not the logical result

[030-101]be a condition of universal doubt?

[030-102]'On a very moderate computation, Ward argued, 'five times the amount of

[030-103]a man's natural life might qualify a person endowed with extraordinary

[030-104]genius to have some faint notion (though even this we doubt) on which

[030-105]side truth lies.' It was not that he had the slightest doubt of Dr.

[030-106]Arnold's orthodoxy--Dr. Arnold, whose piety was universally

[030-107]recognised--Dr. Arnold, who had held up to scorn and execration

[030-108]Strauss's Leben Jesu without reading it. What Ward complained of was the

[030-109]Doctor's lack of logic, not his lack of faith. Could he not see that if

[030-110]he really carried out his own principles to a logical conclusion he

[030-111]would eventually find himself, precisely, in the arms of Strauss? The

[030-112]young man, whose personal friendship remained unshaken, determined upon

[030-113]an interview, and went down to [[rugby-school|Rugby]] primed with first principles,

[030-114]syllogisms, and dilemmas. Finding that the headmaster was busy in

[030-115]school, he spent the afternoon reading novels on the sofa in the

[030-116]drawing-room. When at last, late in the evening, the Doctor returned,

[030-117]tired out with his day's work, Ward fell upon him with all his vigour.

[030-118]The contest was long and furious; it was also entirely inconclusive.

[030-119]When it was over, Ward, with none of his brilliant arguments disposed

[030-120]of, and none of his probing questions satisfactorily answered, returned

[030-121]to the University to plunge headlong into the vortex of the Oxford

[030-122]Movement; and Dr. Arnold, worried, perplexed, and exhausted, went to

[030-123]bed, where he remained for the next thirty-six hours.

[030-124]The Commentary on the New Testament was never finished, and the great

[030-125]work on Church and State itself remained a fragment. Dr. Arnold's active

[030-126]mind was diverted from political and theological speculations to the

[030-127]study of philology, and to historical composition. His Roman History,

[030-128]which he regarded as 'the chief monument of his historical fame', was

[030-129]based partly upon the researches of Niebuhr, and partly upon an aversion

[030-130]to Gibbon.

[030-131]'My highest ambition,' he wrote, 'is to make my history the very reverse

[030-132]of Gibbon in this respect, that whereas the whole spirit of his work,

[030-133]from its low morality, is hostile to religion, without speaking directly

[030-134]against it, so my greatest desire would be, in my History, by its high

[030-135]morals and its general tone, to be of use to the cause without actually

[030-136]bringing it forward.'

[030-137]These efforts were rewarded, in 1841, by the Professorship of Modern

[030-138]History at Oxford. Meanwhile, he was engaged in the study of the

[030-139]Sanskrit and Slavonic languages, bringing out an elaborate edition of

[030-140]Thucydides, and carrying on a voluminous correspondence upon a multitude

[030-141]of topics with a large circle of men of learning. At his death, his

[030-142]published works, composed during such intervals as he could spare from

[030-143]the management of a great public school, filled, besides a large number

[030-144]of pamphlets and articles, no less than seventeen volumes. It was no

[030-145]wonder that Carlyle, after a visit to Rugby, should have characterised

[030-146]Dr. Arnold as a man of 'unhasting, unresting diligence'.

[030-147]Mrs. Arnold, too, no doubt agreed with Carlyle. During the first eight

[030-148]years of their married life, she bore him six children; and four more

[030-149]were to follow. In this large and growing domestic circle his hours of

[030-150]relaxation were spent. There those who had only known him in his

[030-151]professional capacity were surprised to find him displaying the

[030-152]tenderness and jocosity of a parent. The dignified and stern headmaster

[030-153]was actually seen to dandle infants and to caracole upon the hearthrug

[030-154]on all fours. Yet, we are told, 'the sense of his authority as a father

[030-155]was never lost in his playfulness as a companion'. On more serious

[030-156]occasions, the voice of the spiritual teacher sometimes made itself

[030-157]heard. An intimate friend described how 'on a comparison having been

[030-158]made in his family circle, which seemed to place St. Paul above St.

[030-159]John,' the tears rushed to the Doctor's eyes and how, repeating one of

[030-160]the verses from St. John, he begged that the comparison might never

[030-161]again be made. The longer holidays were spent in Westmorland, where,

[030-162]rambling with his offspring among the mountains, gathering wild flowers,

[030-163]and pointing out the beauties of Nature, Dr. Arnold enjoyed, as he

[030-164]himself would often say, 'an almost awful happiness'. Music he did not

[030-165]appreciate, though he occasionally desired his eldest boy, Matthew, to

[030-166]sing him the Confirmation Hymn of Dr. Hinds, to which he had become

[030-167]endeared, owing to its use in Rugby Chapel. But his lack of ear was, he

[030-168]considered, amply recompensed by his love of flowers: 'they are my

[030-169]music,' he declared. Yet, in such a matter, he was careful to refrain

[030-170]from an excess of feeling, such as, in his opinion, marked the famous

[030-171]lines of Wordsworth:

[030-172]'To me the meanest flower that blows can give

[030-173]Thoughts that do often lie too deep for tears.'

[030-174]He found the sentiment morbid. 'Life,' he said, 'is not long enough to

[030-175]take such intense interest in objects in themselves so little.' As for

[030-176]the animal world, his feelings towards it were of a very different cast.

[030-177]'The whole subject,' he said, 'of the brute creation is to me one of

[030-178]such painful mystery, that I dare not approach it.' The Unitarians

[030-179]themselves were a less distressing thought.
