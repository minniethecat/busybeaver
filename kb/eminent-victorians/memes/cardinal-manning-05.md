[005-001]The object of the Tract was to prove that there was nothing in the

[005-002]Thirty-nine Articles incompatible with the creed of the [[catholic-church|Roman Church]].

[005-003][[john-henry-newman|Newman]] pointed out, for instance, that it was generally supposed that

[005-004]the Articles condemned the doctrine of Purgatory; but they did not; they

[005-005]merely condemned the Romish doctrine of Purgatory--and Romish, clearly,

[005-006]was not the same thing as Roman. Hence it followed that believers in the

[005-007]Roman doctrine of Purgatory might subscribe the Articles with a good

[005-008]conscience. Similarly, the Articles condemned 'the sacrifices of

[005-009]masses', but they did not condemn 'the sacrifice of the Mass'. Thus, the

[005-010]Mass might be lawfully celebrated in English Churches. Newman took the

[005-011]trouble to examine the Articles in detail from this point of view, and

[005-012]the conclusion he came to in every case supported his contention in a

[005-013]singular manner.

[005-014]The Tract produced an immense sensation, for it seemed to be a deadly

[005-015]and treacherous blow aimed at the very heart of the [[church-of-england|Church of England]].

[005-016]Deadly it certainly was, but it was not so treacherous as it appeared at

[005-017]first sight. The members of the English Church had ingenuously imagined

[005-018]up to that moment that it was possible to contain, in a frame of words,

[005-019]the subtle essence of their complicated doctrinal system, involving the

[005-020]mysteries of the Eternal and the Infinite on the one hand, and the

[005-021]elaborate adjustments of temporal government on the other. They did not

[005-022]understand that verbal definitions in such a case will only perform

[005-023]their functions so long as there is no dispute about the matters which

[005-024]they are intended to define: that is to say, so long as there is no need

[005-025]for them. For generations this had been the case with the Thirty-nine

[005-026]Articles. Their drift was clear enough; and nobody bothered over their

[005-027]exact meaning. But directly someone found it important to give them a

[005-028]new and untraditional interpretation, it appeared that they were a mass

[005-029]of ambiguity, and might be twisted into meaning very nearly anything

[005-030]that anybody liked. Steady-going churchmen were appalled and outraged

[005-031]when they saw Newman, in Tract No. 90, performing this operation. But,

[005-032]after all, he was only taking the Church of England at its word. And

[005-033]indeed, since Newman showed the way, the operation has become so

[005-034]exceedingly common that the most steady-going churchman hardly raises an

[005-035]eyebrow at it now.

[005-036]At the time, however, Newman's treatment of the Articles seemed to

[005-037]display not only a perverted supersubtlety of intellect, but a temper of

[005-038]mind that was fundamentally dishonest. It was then that he first began

[005-039]to be assailed by those charges of untruthfulness which reached their

[005-040]culmination more than twenty years later in the celebrated controversy

[005-041]with Charles Kingsley, which led to the writing of the Apologia. The

[005-042]controversy was not a very fruitful one, chiefly because Kingsley could

[005-043]no more understand the nature of Newman's intelligence than a subaltern

[005-044]in a line regiment can understand a Brahmin of Benares. Kingsley was a

[005-045]stout Protestant, whose hatred of Popery was, at bottom, simply

[005-046]ethical--an honest, instinctive horror of the practices of priestcraft

[005-047]and the habits of superstition; and it was only natural that he should

[005-048]see in those innumerable delicate distinctions which Newman was

[005-049]perpetually drawing, and which he himself had not only never thought of,

[005-050]but could not even grasp, simply another manifestation of the inherent

[005-051]falsehood of [[rome|Rome]]. But, in reality, no one, in one sense of the word,

[005-052]was more truthful than Newman. The idea of deceit would have been

[005-053]abhorrent to him; and indeed it was owing to his very desire to explain

[005-054]what he had in his mind exactly and completely, with all the refinements

[005-055]of which his subtle brain was capable, that persons such as Kingsley

[005-056]were puzzled into thinking him dishonest. Unfortunately, however, the

[005-057]possibilities of truth and falsehood depend upon other things besides

[005-058]sincerity. A man may be of a scrupulous and impeccable honesty, and yet

[005-059]his respect for the truth--it cannot be denied--may be insufficient. He

[005-060]may be, like the lunatic, the lover, and the poet, 'of imagination all

[005-061]compact'; he may be blessed, or cursed, with one of those 'seething

[005-062]brains', one of those 'shaping fanatasies' that 'apprehend more than

[005-063]cool reason ever comprehends'; he may be by nature incapable of sifting

[005-064]evidence, or by predilection simply indisposed to do so. 'When we were

[005-065]there,' wrote Newman in a letter to a friend after his conversion,

[005-066]describing a visit to Naples, and the miraculous circumstances connected

[005-067]with the liquefaction of St. Januarius's blood,

[005-068]'the feast of St. Gennaro was coming on, and the Jesuits were eager for

[005-069]us to stop--they have the utmost confidence in the miracle--and were the

[005-070]more eager because many Catholics, till they have seen it, doubt it. Our

[005-071]father director here tells us that before he went to Naples he did not

[005-072]believe it. That is, they have vague ideas of natural means,

[005-073]exaggeration, etc., not of course imputing fraud. They say conversions

[005-074]often take place in consequence. It is exposed for the Octave, and the

[005-075]miracle continues--it is not simple liquefaction, but sometimes it

[005-076]swells, sometimes boils, sometimes melts--no one can tell what is going

[005-077]to take place. They say it is quite overcoming-and people cannot help

[005-078]crying to see it. I understand that Sir H. Davy attended everyday, and

[005-079]it was this extreme variety of the phenomenon which convinced him that

[005-080]nothing physical would account for it. Yet there is this remarkable fact

[005-081]that liquefactions of blood are common at Naples--and, unless it is

[005-082]irreverent to the Great Author of [[supernatural-victorian|Miracles]] to be obstinate in the

[005-083]inquiry, the question certainly rises whether there is something in the

[005-084]air. (Mind, I don't believe there is--and, speaking humbly, and without

[005-085]having seen it, think it a true miracle--but I am arguing.) We saw the

[005-086]blood of St Patrizia, half liquid; i.e. liquefying, on her feast day. St

[005-087]John Baptist's blood sometimes liquefies on the 29th of August, and did

[005-088]when we were at Naples, but we had not time to go to the church. We saw

[005-089]the liquid blood of an Oratorian Father; a good man, but not a saint,

[005-090]who died two centuries ago, I think; and we saw the liquid blood of Da

[005-091]Ponte, the great and holy Jesuit, who, I suppose, was almost a saint.

[005-092]But these instances do not account for liquefaction on certain days, if

[005-093]this is the case. But the most strange phenomenon is what happens at

[005-094]Ravello, a village or town above Amalfi. There is the blood of St.

[005-095]Pantaleon. It is in a vessel amid the stonework of the Altar-it is not

[005-096]touched but on his feast in June it liquefies. And more, there is an

[005-097]excommunication against those who bring portions of the True Cross into

[005-098]the Church. Why? Because the blood liquefies, whenever it is brought. A

[005-099]person I know, not knowing the prohibition, brought in a portion, and

[005-100]the Priest suddenly said, who showed the blood, "Who has got the Holy

[005-101]Cross about him?" I tell you what was told me by a grave and religious

[005-102]man. It is a curious coincidence that in telling this to our Father

[005-103]Director here, he said, "Why, we have a portion of St. Pantaleon's blood

[005-104]at the Chiesa Nuova, and it is always liquid."'

[005-105]After leaving Naples, Newman visited Loreto, and inspected the house of

[005-106]the Holy Family, which, as is known to the faithful, was transported

[005-107]thither, in three hops, from Palestine.

[005-108]'I went to Loreto,' he wrote, 'with a simple faith, believing what I

[005-109]still more believed when I saw it. I have no doubt now. If you ask me

[005-110]why I believe it, it is because everyone believes it at Rome; cautious

[005-111]as they are and sceptical about some other things. I have no antecedent

[005-112]difficulty in the matter. He who floated the Ark on the surges of a

[005-113]world-wide sea, and enclosed in it all living things, who has hidden the

[005-114]terrestrial paradise, who said that faith might move mountains, who

[005-115]sustained thousands for forty years in a sterile wilderness, who

[005-116]transported Elias and keeps him hidden till the end, could do this

[005-117]wonder also.'

[005-118]Here, whatever else there may be, there is certainly no trace of a

[005-119]desire to deceive. Could a state of mind, in fact, be revealed with more

[005-120]absolute transparency?

[005-121]When Newman was a child he 'wished that he could believe the Arabian

[005-122]Nights were true'. When he came to be a man, his wish seems to have been

[005-123]granted.

[005-124]Tract No. 90 was officially condemned by the authorities at [[oxford-university|Oxford]], and

[005-125]in the hubbub that followed, the contending parties closed their ranks;

[005-126]henceforward, any compromise between the friends and the enemies of the

[005-127]Movement was impossible. Archdeacon Manning was in too conspicuous a

[005-128]position to be able to remain silent; he was obliged to declare himself,

[005-129]and he did not hesitate. In an archidiaconal charge, delivered within a

[005-130]few months of his appointment, he firmly repudiated the Tractarians. But

[005-131]the repudiation was not deemed sufficient, and a year later he repeated

[005-132]it with greater emphasis. Still, however, the horrid rumours were

[005-133]afloat. The "Record" began to investigate matters, and its vigilance was

[005-134]soon rewarded by an alarming discovery: the sacrament had been

[005-135]administered in Chichester Cathedral on a weekday, and 'Archdeacon

[005-136]Manning, one of the most noted and determined of the Tractarians, had

[005-137]acted a conspicuous part on the occasion'. It was clear that the only

[005-138]way of silencing these malevolent whispers was by some public

[005-139]demonstration whose import nobody could doubt. The annual sermon

[005-140]preached on Guy Fawkes Day before the University of Oxford seemed to

[005-141]offer the very opportunity that Manning required. He seized it; got

[005-142]himself appointed preacher; and delivered from the pulpit of St. Mary's

[005-143]a virulently Protestant harangue. This time there could indeed be no

[005-144]doubt about the matter: Manning had shouted 'No Popery!' in the very

[005-145]citadel of the Movement, and every one, including Newman, recognised

[005-146]that he had finally cut himself off from his old friends. Everyone, that

[005-147]is to say, except the Archdeacon himself. On the day after the sermon,

[005-148]Manning walked out to the neighbouring village of [[littlemore|Littlemore]], where

[005-149]Newman was now living in retirement with a few chosen disciples, in the

[005-150]hope of being able to give a satisfactory explanation of what he had

[005-151]done. But he was disappointed; for when, after an awkward interval, one

[005-152]of the disciples appeared at the door, he was informed that Mr. Newman

[005-153]was not at home.

[005-154]With his retirement to Littlemore, Newman had entered upon the final

[005-155]period of his Anglican career. Even he could no longer help perceiving

[005-156]that the end was now only a matter of time. His progress was hastened in

[005-157]an agitating manner by the indiscreet activity of one of his proselytes,

[005-158]W. G. Ward. a young man who combined an extraordinary aptitude for a

[005-159]priori reasoning with a passionate devotion to Opera Bouffe. It was

[005-160]difficult, in fact, to decide whether the inner nature of Ward was more

[005-161]truly expressing itself when he was firing off some train of scholastic

[005-162]paradoxes on the Eucharist or when he was trilling the airs of Figaro

[005-163]and plunging through the hilarious roulades of the Largo al Factotum.

[005-164]Even Dr. Pusey could not be quite sure, though he was Ward's spiritual

[005-165]director. On one occasion his young penitent came to him, and confessed

[005-166]that a vow which he had taken to abstain from music during Lent was

[005-167]beginning to affect his health. Could Dr. Pusey see his way to releasing

[005-168]him from the vow? The Doctor decided that a little sacred music would

[005-169]not be amiss. Ward was all gratitude, and that night a party was

[005-170]arranged in a friend's rooms. The concert began with the solemn

[005-171]harmonies of Handel, which were followed by the holy strains of the 'Oh

[005-172]Salutaris' of Cherubini. Then came the elevation and the pomp of

[005-173]'Possenti Numi' from the Magic Flute. But, alas! there lies much danger

[005-174]in Mozart. The page was turned and there was the delicious duet between

[005-175]Papageno and Papagena. Flesh and blood could not resist that; then song

[005-176]followed song, the music waxed faster and lighter, until, at last Ward

[005-177]burst into the intoxicating merriment of the Largo al Factotum. When it

[005-178]was over, a faint but persistent knocking made itself heard upon the

[005-179]wall; and it was only then that the company remembered that the rooms

[005-180]next door were Dr. Pusey's.
