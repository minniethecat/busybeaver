[047-001]The news of the catastrophe reached England, and a great outcry arose.

[047-002]The public grief vied with the public indignation. The Queen, in a

[047-003]letter to Miss Gordon, immediately gave vent both to her own sentiments

[047-004]and those of the nation.

[047-005]'HOW shall I write to you,' she exclaimed, 'or how shall I attempt to

[047-006]express WHAT I FEEL! To THINK of your dear, noble, heroic Brother, who

[047-007]served his Country and his Queen so truly, so heroically, with a

[047-008]self-sacrifice so edifying to the World, not having been rescued. That

[047-009]the promises of support were not fulfilled--which I so frequently and

[047-010]constantly pressed on those who asked him to go--is to me GRIEF

[047-011]INEXPRESSIBLE! Indeed, it has made me ill ... Would you express to your

[047-012]other sisters and your elder Brother my true sympathy, and what I do so

[047-013]keenly feel, the STAIN left upon England, for your dear Brother's cruel,

[047-014]though heroic, fate!'

[047-015]In reply, Miss Gordon presented the Queen with her brother's Bible,

[047-016]which was placed in one of the corridors at Windsor, open, on a white

[047-017]satin cushion, and enclosed in a crystal case. In the meanwhile, Gordon

[047-018]was acclaimed in every newspaper as a national martyr; State services

[047-019]were held in his honour at Westminster and St Paul's; L20,000 was voted

[047-020]to his family; and a great sum of money was raised by subscription to

[047-021]endow a charity in his memory. Wrath and execration fell, in particular,

[047-022]upon the head of [[william-ewart-gladstone|Mr. Gladstone]]. He was little better than a murderer; he

[047-023]was a traitor; he was a heartless villain, who had been seen at the play

[047-024]on the very night when Gordon's death was announced. The storm passed;

[047-025]but Mr. Gladstone had soon to cope with a still more serious agitation.

[047-026]The cry was raised on every side that the national honour would be

[047-027]irreparably tarnished if the Mahdi were left in the peaceful possession

[047-028]of [[khartoum|Khartoum]], and that the Expeditionary Force should be at once employed

[047-029]to chastise the false prophet and to conquer the Sudan. But it was in

[047-030]vain that the imperialists clamoured; in vain that Lord Wolseley wrote

[047-031]several dispatches, proving over and over again that to leave the Mahdi

[047-032]unconquered must involve the ruin of Egypt; in vain that Lord Hartington

[047-033]at last discovered that he had come to the same conclusion. The old man

[047-034]stood firm. Just then, a crisis with Russia on the Afghan frontier

[047-035]supervened; and Mr. Gladstone, pointing out that every available soldier

[047-036]might be wanted at any moment for a European war, withdrew Lord Wolseley

[047-037]and his army from Egypt. The Russian crisis disappeared. The Mahdi

[047-038]remained supreme lord of the Sudan.

[047-039]And yet it was not with the Mahdi that the future lay. Before six months

[047-040]were out, in the plenitude of his power, he died, and the Khalifa

[047-041]Abdullahi reigned in his stead. The future lay with Major Kitchener and

[047-042]his Maxim-Nordenfeldt guns. Thirteen years later the Mahdi's empire was

[047-043]abolished forever in the gigantic hecatomb of Omdurman; after which it

[047-044]was thought proper that a religious ceremony in honour of [[general-gordon|General Gordon]]

[047-045]should be held at the palace at Khartoum. The service was conducted by

[047-046]four chaplains--of the Catholic, Anglican, Presbyterian, and Methodist

[047-047]persuasions--and concluded with a performance of 'Abide with Me'--the

[047-048]General's favourite hymn--by a select company of Sudanese buglers. Every

[047-049]one agreed that General Gordon had been avenged at last. Who could doubt

[047-050]it? General Gordon himself, possibly, fluttering, in some remote

[047-051]Nirvana, the pages of a phantasmal Bible, might have ventured on a

[047-052]satirical remark. But General Gordon had always been a contradictious

[047-053]person--even a little off his head, perhaps, though a hero; and besides,

[047-054]he was no longer there to contradict ... At any rate, it had all ended

[047-055]very happily--in a glorious slaughter of 20,000 Arabs, a vast addition

[047-056]to the [[british-imperialism|British Empire]], and a step in the Peerage for Sir Evelyn Baring.
