# Sir Gerald Graham

British general whose victories at Suakin in 1884–1885 highlighted the Government's contradictory Sudan policy — aggressive on the Red Sea coast while [[general-gordon]] was abandoned to his fate at [[khartoum|Khartoum]].

## Life

Gerald Graham was born in 1831 and entered the Royal Engineers, distinguishing himself in the [[crimean-war|Crimean War]] and later campaigns. By 1884, he was a lieutenant-general with a reputation for competence and courage.

### The Suakin Campaign

> "The Government dispatched Sir Gerald Graham with a considerable British force to Suakin." [040-100]

In early 1884, while Gordon was besieged in [[khartoum|Khartoum]], the British Government made one of the most bewildering decisions of the Sudan crisis: they sent Graham with an expeditionary force to Suakin, on the Red Sea coast, hundreds of miles from [[khartoum|Khartoum]]. Graham's mission was to engage the Mahdi's ally Osman Digna and secure the Red Sea littoral.

Graham executed his orders with professional efficiency. "Sir Gerald Graham advanced, and in a series of bloody engagements completely defeated the Mahdi's forces" [040-101]. His victories at El Teb and Tamai were brilliantly conducted — and utterly irrelevant to Gordon's situation.

### The Irony

The Suakin campaign became, in Strachey's telling, an emblem of the Government's incoherence. While Graham was winning victories on the Red Sea, Gordon was dying at [[khartoum|Khartoum]]. Graham's troops could not march the hundreds of miles across the desert to relieve the siege; their victories served only to demonstrate what a properly resourced British force could achieve — if only the Government had the will to deploy one where it mattered.

> "Sir Gerald Graham and his British Army were within a few hundred miles of Khartoum; but they might as well have been at the Antipodes." [040-140]

The campaign also had political consequences. "Sir Gerald Graham's bloody victories awoke [[william-ewart-gladstone|Mr. Gladstone]]" [041-111] — but the prime minister awoke only to the moral horror of what was being done in his name, not to the strategic imperative of saving Gordon.

## Character and Beliefs

Graham was, by all accounts, a capable and courageous officer. He was not responsible for the strategic incoherence of the campaign he was ordered to fight. His role in *Eminent Victorians* is that of the competent professional whose competence was deployed in the wrong place for the wrong reasons.

## In This Book

Graham appears in the Gordon section (Chapters 32–47) as part of the tragic machinery of the Sudan crisis. His victories at Suakin are not celebrated but mourned — they are the victories of a Government that could fight everywhere except where it mattered.
