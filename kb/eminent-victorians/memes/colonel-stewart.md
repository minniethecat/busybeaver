# Colonel J.D.H. Stewart

Colonel John Donald Hamill Stewart was [[general-gordon|General Gordon]]'s second-in-command at [[khartoum|Khartoum]], whose tragic death while attempting to run the blockade became one of the most dramatic episodes in the final section of *Eminent Victorians*.

## Life

John Donald Hamill Stewart was born in 1845 and served as an officer in the [[british-army|British Army]]. In 1884, he was selected to accompany [[general-gordon]] on his mission to the Sudan, serving as second-in-command at [[khartoum|Khartoum]]. He accompanied Gordon on the journey from London, through Cairo, to [[khartoum|Khartoum]] [039-107].

### The Siege of Khartoum

During the siege of [[khartoum|Khartoum]] by the forces of the Mahdi, Stewart kept a detailed diary that later became one of the most important primary sources for understanding what happened inside the besieged city. The diary recorded both the military situation and Gordon's increasingly desperate state of mind.

When it became clear that the city could not hold out indefinitely, Stewart made a fateful decision: he would attempt to run the Mahdi's blockade by steamer down the Nile, carrying Gordon's dispatches and his own diary to the outside world. Gordon agreed reluctantly [044-015].

### Death on the Nile

> "It was terrible: Colonel Stewart and his companions had been killed." [045-044]

The steamer successfully passed through the Mahdi's lines but struck a rock further down the Nile. Stewart and his companions were forced ashore, where they were set upon by Arab tribesmen. All were killed. Stewart's papers — including his invaluable diary — fell into the hands of the Mahdi [045-052], who used them as propaganda, presenting them as evidence that the British had betrayed the Sudanese.

The news of Stewart's death, when it finally reached England, deepened the sense of tragedy surrounding the Gordon mission. Not only was Gordon himself besieged and doomed, but the one man who had escaped [[khartoum|Khartoum]] had been murdered on the river.

## Character and Beliefs

Stewart emerges from the sources as a competent, loyal, and brave officer who found himself in an impossible situation. His diary reveals a man trying to make sense of Gordon's mercurial personality and the increasingly dire military situation. Unlike Gordon, Stewart was a conventional soldier — practical, methodical, and ultimately unable to shape the events that were engulfing him.

## Relationships

- [[general-gordon]]: Stewart's commander, whose personality and decisions dominated the siege
- [[evelyn-baring]]: The consul-general in Cairo with whom Stewart communicated

## Historical Significance

Colonel Stewart's death was one of the many tragedies of the Gordon relief expedition. His diary, captured by the Mahdi and later recovered, provided an essential record of the siege. The controversy over Stewart's death — whether it could have been prevented, who bore responsibility — became part of the larger controversy over the Government's handling of the Sudan crisis.

## In This Book

Stewart appears in the Gordon section (Chapters 32–47). Strachey uses him as a foil to Gordon: the sensible second-in-command whose conventional competence cannot save him from the consequences of Gordon's genius and the Government's vacillation. His death is one of the most affecting moments in the book.
