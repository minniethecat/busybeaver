# Papal Infallibility

The Roman Catholic doctrine affirming that the Pope, when speaking *ex cathedra* (from the chair of St. Peter) on matters of faith or morals, is preserved from the possibility of error by the Holy Spirit. Defined as dogma at the First Vatican Council of 1870, it was championed by Cardinal Manning and treated by Lytton Strachey with characteristic irony as a case study in the psychology of absolute certainty.

## Definition

The dogma holds that the Roman Pontiff, when exercising his office as pastor and teacher of all Christians, possesses that infallibility with which Christ endowed His Church (013-136). The theological chain of reasoning, as Strachey presents it, runs: Christ said to Peter "Thou art Cephas" — Peter became Bishop of Rome — the Pope succeeds Peter — Christ prayed that Peter's faith would not fail — therefore papal pronouncements on faith and morals cannot err (013-136). Strachey notes that this logic was "obvious" to its proponents, and yet caused "searchings of heart" even among the faithful (013-156).

## Context in Victorian Religion

The definition of Papal Infallibility came at a moment when the Papal States were collapsing and the Pope's temporal power was vanishing. The doctrine was, in effect, a spiritual compensation for earthly loss: "Let that doctrine be proclaimed, with the assent of the whole Church, an article of faith, and, in the face of such an affirmation, let the modern world do its worst!" (013-136). A General Council — the first since Trent — was summoned to the Vatican with a programme of providing "an adequate remedy to the disorders, intellectual and moral, of Christendom" (013-136). Everyone knew what it meant.

The opposition came from those who found the timing "inopportune" — the Gallican spirit still lingering among French and German bishops who "secretly hankered, even at this late hour, after some form of constitutional government" within the Church (014-029). Newman was "more than usually upset"; Monseigneur Dupanloup was "disgusted"; and Dr. Dollinger "prepared himself for resistance" (013-136).

## In the Book

### In Cardinal Manning's Narrative

Papal Infallibility is the climax of Manning's Roman career. Having converted to Catholicism and risen to become Archbishop of Westminster, Manning "flew to Rome in a whirlwind of Papal enthusiasm" as the Council approached (014-049). Strachey portrays Manning as the Pope's most reliable agent — Pius IX's appointment of Manning to Westminster was due to "the fact that he was the one man in England upon whose fidelity the Roman Government could absolutely rely" (014-049). The chapter reaches its ironic peak in Manning's relentless advocacy for the doctrine, culminating in the dogmatic definition itself.

Manning's certainty matched the Pope's own: Pius IX remarked, "Before I was Pope, I believed in Papal Infallibility, now I feel it" (014-049). Strachey's treatment emphasizes the psychological mechanism by which conviction manufactures its own evidence.

### Strachey's Critique

Strachey devotes sustained attention to the logical difficulties of the doctrine. He recounts the fourteenth-century case where Pope John XXII and his predecessor Nicholas III issued contradictory bulls on the poverty of Christ — a dilemma that left the entire Papal succession logically vitiated regardless of which Pope was right (014-029). He observes that "Catholic apologists, when they try to tone down those pretensions and to explain them away, forget that it is in their very exorbitance that their fascination lies" (014-029). The doctrine compels not because it satisfies reason, but because "it astounds it."

The core insight Strachey draws from the infallibility controversy concerns the psychology of power: Manning and the ultramontanes sought an absolute authority precisely because the modern world offered none. The dogma was less a theological conclusion than an assertion of will — a defiant proclamation that, in an age of Liberalism, the Church would not compromise.

## Key Figures Associated

- **Pope Pius IX** (1792–1878): The Pope who convened the First Vatican Council and in whose pontificate the dogma was defined. His personal trajectory from liberal sympathies to reactionary absolutism exemplifies the themes Manning's biography explores (013-136).

- **Cardinal Manning** (1808–1892): The doctrine's most zealous English advocate. Manning's advocacy for infallibility completed his transformation from Anglican archdeacon to ultramontane cardinal — a journey Strachey traces with fascinated horror.

- **John Henry Newman** (1801–1890): Though a Catholic convert himself, Newman was among those who considered the definition inopportune. His misgivings about the "extreme party" in Rome presaged his marginalization under Manning's ascendancy.

- **Dr. Ignaz von Dollinger** (1799–1890): The German church historian who led the intellectual opposition to the dogma, refusing to accept the Council's definition and eventually being excommunicated.

## Related Concepts

- **Ultramontanism**: The broader movement toward centralizing Church authority in Rome, of which Papal Infallibility was the capstone doctrine. Manning was the leading English Ultramontane.
- **Gallicanism**: The French tradition of limiting papal authority in favour of national churches — the position the infallibility definition was designed to defeat.
- **Liberalism**: The nineteenth-century political philosophy that the Vatican Council was intended to combat, and which Strachey sees as the historical tide against which Manning's absolutism was a desperate rearguard action.

## Historical Significance

The definition of Papal Infallibility at Vatican I (1870) represented the triumph of ultramontane Catholicism over conciliar and Gallican tendencies that had persisted for centuries. Strachey treats it as the institutional expression of Manning's personal psychology — the same drive toward absolute authority and certainty that had previously led him from Evangelicalism to Tractarianism to Rome. The doctrine would not be exercised again until the definition of the Assumption of Mary in 1950, confirming the very narrow scope that its cautious opponents had always claimed to prefer.
