# Martyrdom and Self-Sacrifice

Gordon's death at Khartoum in 1885 was the most famous Victorian martyrdom — a death that "went straight to the heart of the English people" and cemented Gordon's legend. Strachey's treatment of this death is characteristically double: it was heroic and it was absurd; it was a sacrifice and it was a suicide; Gordon sought it and it was forced upon him.

## Gordon's Death-Wish

Strachey presents Gordon as a man who had been courting death all his life. His postings to dangerous places, his refusal to evacuate Khartoum when he could, his mystical communion with a God who seemed to promise martyrdom — all point to what Strachey sees as a deep psychological drive. "He was a man whose one desire was to lose himself in God," and death in His service was the perfect dissolution of self (032-041).

The relief expedition that arrived "too late" — that haunting Victorian phrase — sealed the narrative. Strachey implies that Gordon chose this ending, consciously or not, because it was the only narrative that could contain his contradictions: the soldier who hated war, the imperialist who despised empire, the Christian who sought not salvation but annihilation.

## Victorian Martyrdom

Gordon's martyrdom was part of a broader Victorian cult of heroic death. From the Christian missionaries whose deaths in Africa and Asia were celebrated in evangelical literature, to the military martyrs of imperial campaigns, to the social martyrs like Nightingale (who "died" to normal life on her sofa), Victorian culture elevated self-sacrifice into the highest virtue — and Strachey's irony exposes the psychological costs of this elevation.
