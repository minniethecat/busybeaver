# John Henry Newman

Strachey's portrait of Newman is a portrait of genius misdirected. Without Newman, "the [[oxford-movement|Oxford Movement]] would have flickered out its little flame unobserved in the Common Room of Oriel" (003-038). Without [[oxford-university|Oxford]], Newman might have been a poet of the first rank. As it was, the University's "air thick with clerical sanctity" (003-055) turned him into something stranger: a theologian who was really an artist, a Catholic convert who was really a Romantic, a cardinal who reached immortality "neither as a thinker nor as a theologian, but as an artist who has embalmed the poignant history of an intensely human spirit in the magical spices of words" (003-062).

## Life

### Oxford and the Movement

A Fellow of [[oxford-university|Oriel College]], Newman was the intellectual engine of the [[oxford-movement|Oxford Movement]]. His sermons at St. Mary's transfigured undergraduates; his *[[oxford-movement|Tracts for the Times]]* (1833–1841) defined Tractarian theology. Strachey describes him as "a child of the Romantic Revival, a creature of emotion and of memory, a dreamer whose secret spirit dwelt apart in delectable mountains" (003-040).

### Conversion

Newman's gradual estrangement from the [[church-of-england|Church of England]] culminated in his secession to [[rome|Rome]] in 1845 — the event that, more than any other, defined the Victorian religious crisis. His retirement to [[littlemore|Littlemore]], his reception by Father Dominic the Passionist, and the publication of his *Essay on the Development of Christian Doctrine* marked the end of the [[oxford-movement|Oxford Movement]] as an Anglican phenomenon.

### Catholic Years and the Apologia

Newman's Catholic career was overshadowed by Manning's ascendancy. The ultramontane Catholicism of the Manning faction distrusted Newman's intellectualism; his plan for a Catholic college at [[oxford-university|Oxford]] was thwarted. But in 1864, provoked by Charles Kingsley's aspersions on his honesty, Newman wrote the *Apologia Pro Vita Sua* — the autobiography that Strachey considers his true masterpiece and the work that secured his literary immortality.

## Strachey's Judgment

Strachey's verdict is double: Newman was wasted on theology. His gifts — psychological acuity, prose style, the capacity to render the motions of the spirit in language of crystalline precision — were those of a novelist, not a dogmatist. The *Apologia* vindicated Newman not as a thinker but as an artist. It is, Strachey implies, the only part of his work that matters.
