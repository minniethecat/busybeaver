# Dublin

Dublin enters *Eminent Victorians* as the scene of one of John Henry Newman's most disheartening ventures: his rectorship of the Catholic University of Ireland. The episode occupies only a few pages in Strachey's biography, but it is presented with the compressed irony that is the book's signature. Newman, summoned to Ireland by the Irish bishops, arrives to find an institution that exists only on paper and an ecclesiastical superior — Archbishop Cullen — who refuses to communicate with him. The Dublin years are a study in the futility of intellect and goodwill deployed against the stone wall of institutional inertia.

## In the Narrative

### The Summons

The Catholic University of Ireland was conceived as a counterweight to the Protestant Trinity College, and the Irish bishops, with the encouragement of Pope Pius IX, resolved to place it under the most distinguished English Catholic convert of the age. Newman, then in his early fifties and living in uneasy retirement in Birmingham, accepted the appointment. He "wrote to Dr. Cullen asking that arrangements might be made for his reception in Dublin," Strachey records. "Dr. Cullen did not reply" [^1].

Newman wrote again. "Weeks passed, months passed, years passed, and not a word, not a sign, came from Dr. Cullen" [^1]. The cruel comedy of the situation — Newman dangling in the void for more than two years, not knowing whether he was wanted or not — is rendered by Strachey with an almost theatrical sense of timing.

### Arrival and Disappointment

"At last, after dangling for more than two years in the uncertainties and perplexities of so strange a situation, Newman was summoned to Dublin" [^2]. What he found there confirmed his worst fears. "There he found nothing but disorder and discouragement. The laity took no interest in the scheme; the clergy actively disliked it" [^2]. The Catholic University, such as it was, had no buildings, no students, no funds, and no constituency.

Newman persevered, delivering the lectures that would become *The Idea of a University* — by general consent one of the great works of nineteenth-century educational thought. But as Strachey makes clear, the intellectual triumph was a practical failure. The university never flourished, and Newman eventually withdrew to Birmingham, leaving behind another of the unrealised projects that, in Strachey's telling, constituted the pattern of his life.

## Key Events

- **1851–1852**: Newman appointed Rector of the proposed Catholic University of Ireland; Dr. Cullen's two-year silence [^1]
- **1854**: Newman finally summoned to Dublin; begins delivering the lectures published as *The Idea of a University* [^2]
- **1858**: Newman effectively withdraws from the Dublin venture, returning to the Oratory at Birmingham
