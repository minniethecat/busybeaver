# Gorham Case (1847–1850)

A landmark ecclesiastical legal case about baptismal regeneration that became, in Strachey's telling, the proximate cause of [[cardinal-manning|Manning]]'s defection from the [[church-of-england|Church of England]]. The case exposed the fundamental contradiction of an established church: a body of lay lawyers, appointed by the Crown, held the legal power to determine what Anglican clergy must believe. For the [[oxford-movement|Oxford Movement]] generation who had spent two decades trying to restore Catholic doctrine to the Church of England, the Gorham judgment proved that the Reformation settlement was not a temporary accident but a permanent constitutional reality.

## Overview

In November 1847, the Reverend George Cornelius Gorham was presented by the Lord Chancellor to the living of Bramford Speke in the diocese of Exeter (008-027). The Bishop of Exeter, Henry Phillpotts, a staunch High Churchman, suspected Gorham of holding Evangelical opinions on the subject of baptismal regeneration (008-030). Phillpotts subjected Gorham to an extraordinary examination: a verbal interrogatory lasting thirty-eight hours, followed by a series of one hundred and forty-nine written questions (008-032). At its conclusion, Phillpotts determined that Gorham held heretical views and refused to institute him to the living (008-035). Gorham sued the Bishop in the Court of Arches, lost, and then appealed to the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council (008-037).

The Privy Council, composed entirely of laymen, delivered its judgment in 1850: it reversed the Court of Arches and upheld Gorham's position, ruling that his views were not contrary to the doctrine of the Church of England as set forth in its Articles, Formularies, and Rubrics (008-100). The decision left every Anglican clergyman at liberty to deny that regeneration invariably takes place at infant baptism — a ruling that still holds good (008-101).

## The Theological Dispute

The doctrinal question at the heart of the case was deceptively narrow: whether sacramental grace in baptism is given absolutely (by the mere act of baptism) or conditionally (requiring worthy reception through faith and repentance).

The High Church party, represented by Bishop Phillpotts, asserted "that the mere act of baptism conferred regeneration upon the recipient and washed away his original sin" (008-056). The Evangelicals, headed by Gorham, replied that "regeneration would not follow unless baptism was *rightly* received" — and that "'rightly'" implied "not merely lawful administration, but worthy reception; worthiness, therefore, is the essence of the sacrament; and worthiness means faith and repentance" (008-062).

Both parties agreed on two propositions: that all infants are born in original sin, and that original sin could be washed away by baptism (008-064). But this created a logical impasse. "How could both these propositions be true," argued Gorham, "if it was also true that faith and repentance were necessary before baptism could come into operation at all? How could an infant in arms be said to be in a state of faith and repentance?" (008-068).

The only resolution lay in the doctrine of prevenient grace. Gorham maintained "that unless God performed an act of prevenient grace by which the infant was endowed with faith and repentance, no act of baptism could be effectual; though to whom, and under what conditions, prevenient grace was given, Mr. Gorham confessed himself unable to decide" (008-074).

Strachey notes that the Church of England's own formularies were ambiguous enough to permit either interpretation. The question was simply "whether or not she excluded Mr. Gorham's" view — and the Privy Council decided that she did not (008-084).

> The judgement still holds good; and to this day, a clergyman of the Church of England is quite at liberty to believe that Regeneration does not invariably take place when an infant is baptised.
> — Section 8 (008-101)

## Key Figures

- **George Cornelius Gorham**: An Evangelical clergyman whose refusal to affirm unconditional baptismal regeneration triggered the case. His examination by Bishop Phillpotts — thirty-eight hours of oral questioning plus 149 written queries — became notorious (008-032).

- **Henry Phillpotts, Bishop of Exeter**: A determined High Churchman who saw Gorham's appointment as an assault on Catholic doctrine within the Established Church. His refusal to institute Gorham set the legal machinery in motion (008-029).

- **Henry Edward Manning**: At the time Archdeacon of Chichester and a leading Tractarian. The Gorham judgment shattered his remaining confidence in the Church of England and precipitated his conversion to Rome in 1851 (008-104).

- **William Ewart Gladstone**: Deeply perturbed by the judgment, he wrote of the need to "rescue and defend the conscience of the Church from the present hideous system" and proposed elaborate schemes of delay and covenant to prevent precipitate action (008-114).

## The Constitutional Crisis

Beneath the theological dispute lay a constitutional question of far greater consequence. An Act passed in 1833 had constituted the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council as the supreme court of appeal for ecclesiastical cases — and this committee was composed entirely of laymen (008-090).

The implication was inescapable: the Royal Supremacy was not a historical relic but an active constitutional reality. A body of lawyers appointed by the Crown possessed "the legal right to formulate the religious doctrine of the Church of England" (008-093). For Tractarians who had spent years constructing a vision of the Church of England as a branch of the universal Catholic Church, independent of the State in spiritual matters, this was a devastating revelation.

> What an escape for my poor soul! Manning is said to have exclaimed when, shortly after his conversion, a mitre was going a-begging. But, in truth, Manning's 'poor soul' had scented nobler quarry. To one of his temperament, how was it possible, when once the choice was plainly put, to hesitate for a moment between the respectable dignity of an English bishop, harnessed by the secular power, with the Gorham judgment as a bit between his teeth, and the illimitable pretensions of the humblest priest of Rome?
> — Section 8 (008-190)

## Impact on Manning

The judgment "fell upon no one with greater violence than upon Manning" (008-104). He had been convinced that the Royal Supremacy was "a mere accident — a temporary usurpation which left the spiritual dominion of the Church essentially untouched" (008-108). Now the reality confronted him: "an Act of Parliament, passed by Jews, Roman Catholics, and Dissenters, was the ultimate authority which decided upon the momentous niceties of the Anglican faith" (008-111).

Manning organised a formal protest, drawing up and signing a declaration with several influential Anglicans (008-116). Gladstone urged delay, proposing a covenant scheme to bind protestors to inaction for a fixed period — a plan Manning dismissed. "Protests and procrastinations, approving Wegg-Prossers and cork-like Lord Feildings — all this was feeding the wind and folly; the time for action had come" (008-130).

Manning's letter to Robert Wilberforce set out three convictions that made continued Anglican ministry impossible: that the Royal Supremacy in ecclesiastical causes violated the Divine Office of the Church; that it had involved the Church of England in schism from the Universal Church; and that it had suspended the spiritual functions of the Church of England (008-136). On April 6th, 1851, Manning was received into the Roman Catholic Church (008-162).

## Significance

The Gorham Case was far more than a dispute over baptismal theology. It demonstrated, conclusively and publicly, that the Church of England was not a self-governing spiritual body but a department of the State — its doctrines subject to determination by secular courts, its clergy bound by Acts of Parliament rather than by Catholic tradition. For the Oxford Movement, the judgment was a final, incontrovertible proof that the Catholic revival within Anglicanism could never be complete. The "flood of converts" to Rome in the years that followed — Manning prominent among them — was the Gorham judgment's direct legacy.

Strachey treats the case as the turning point in Manning's biography: the event that transformed an Anglican archdeacon into a Roman Catholic cardinal, and the moment when the Church of England's fundamental ambiguity — Catholic in aspiration, Erastian in constitution — became impossible to deny.
