[044-001]The relief expedition had begun, and at the same moment a new phase

[044-002]opened at [[khartoum|Khartoum]]. The annual rising of the Nile was now sufficiently

[044-003]advanced to enable one of Gordon's small steamers to pass over the

[044-004]cataracts down to Egypt in safety. He determined to seize the

[044-005]opportunity of laying before the authorities in Cairo and London, and

[044-006]the English public at large, an exact account of his position. A cargo

[044-007]of documents, including Colonel Stewart's Diary of the siege and a

[044-008]personal appeal for assistance addressed by Gordon to all the European

[044-009]powers, was placed on board the Abbas; four other steamers were to

[044-010]accompany her until she was out of danger from attacks by the Mahdi's

[044-011]troops; after which, she was to proceed alone into Egypt. On the evening

[044-012]of September 9th, just as she was about to start, the English and French

[044-013]Consuls asked for permission to go with her--a permission which Gordon,

[044-014]who had long been anxious to provide for their safety, readily granted.

[044-015]Then Colonel Stewart made the same request; and Gordon consented with

[044-016]the same alacrity.

[044-017]Colonel Stewart was the second-in-command at Khartoum; and it seems

[044-018]strange that he should have made a proposal which would leave Gordon in

[044-019]a position of the gravest anxiety without a single European subordinate.

[044-020]But his motives were to be veiled forever in a tragic obscurity. The

[044-021]Abbas and her convoy set out. Henceforward the Governor-General was

[044-022]alone. He had now, definitely and finally, made his decision. Colonel

[044-023]Stewart and his companions had gone, with every prospect of returning

[044-024]unharmed to civilisation. [[william-ewart-gladstone|Mr. Gladstone]]'s belief was justified; so far

[044-025]as Gordon's personal safety was concerned, he might still, at this late

[044-026]hour, have secured it. But he had chosen--he stayed at Khartoum.

[044-027]No sooner were the steamers out of sight than he sat down at his

[044-028]writing-table and began that daily record of his circumstances, his

[044-029]reflections, and his feelings, which reveals to us, with such an

[044-030]authentic exactitude, the final period of his extraordinary destiny. His

[044-031]Journals, sent down the river in batches to await the coming of the

[044-032]relief expedition, and addressed, first to Colonel Stewart, and later to

[044-033]the 'Chief of Staff, Sudan Expeditionary Force', were official

[044-034]documents, intended for publication, though, as Gordon himself was

[044-035]careful to note on the outer covers, they would 'want pruning out'

[044-036]before they were printed. He also wrote, on the envelope of the first

[044-037]section, 'No secrets as far as I am concerned'. A more singular set of

[044-038]state papers was never compiled. Sitting there, in the solitude of his

[044-039]palace, with ruin closing round him, with anxieties on every hand, with

[044-040]doom hanging above his head, he let his pen rush on for hour after hour

[044-041]in an ecstasy of communication, a tireless unburdening of the spirit,

[044-042]where the most trivial incidents of the passing day were mingled

[044-043]pell-mell with philosophical disquisitions; where jests and anger, hopes

[044-044]and terrors, elaborate justifications and cynical confessions, jostled

[044-045]one another in reckless confusion. The impulsive, demonstrative man had

[044-046]nobody to talk to any more, and so he talked instead to the pile of

[044-047]telegraph forms, which, useless now for perplexing Sir Evelyn Baring,

[044-048]served very well--for they were large and blank--as the repositories of

[044-049]his conversation. His tone was not the intimate and religious tone which

[044-050]he would have used with the Rev. Mr. Barnes or his sister Augusta; it

[044-051]was such as must have been habitual with him in his intercourse with old

[044-052]friends or fellow-officers, whose religious views were of a more

[044-053]ordinary caste than his own, but with whom he was on confidential terms.

[044-054]He was anxious to put his case to a select and sympathetic audience--to

[044-055]convince such a man as Lord Wolseley that he was justified in what he

[044-056]had done; and he was sparing in his allusions to the hand of Providence,

[044-057]while those mysterious doubts and piercing introspections, which must

[044-058]have filled him, he almost entirely concealed. He expressed himself, of

[044-059]course, with eccentric ABANDON--it would have been impossible for him to

[044-060]do otherwise; but he was content to indicate his deepest feelings with a

[044-061]fleer. Yet sometimes--as one can imagine happening with him in actual

[044-062]conversation--his utterance took the form of a half-soliloquy, a copious

[044-063]outpouring addressed to himself more than to anyone else, for his own

[044-064]satisfaction. There are passages in the Khartoum Journals which call up

[044-065]in a flash the light, gliding figure, and the blue eyes with the candour

[044-066]of childhood still shining in them; one can almost hear the low voice,

[044-067]the singularly distinct articulation, the persuasive--the

[044-068]self-persuasive--sentences, following each other so unassumingly between

[044-069]the puffs of a cigarette.

[044-070]As he wrote, two preoccupations principally filled his mind. His

[044-071]reflections revolved around the immediate past and the impending future.

[044-072]With an unerring persistency he examined, he excused, he explained, his

[044-073]share in the complicated events which had led to his present situation.

[044-074]He rebutted the charges of imaginary enemies; he laid bare the

[044-075]ineptitude and the faithlessness of the English Government. He poured

[044-076]out his satire upon officials and diplomatists. He drew caricatures, in

[044-077]the margin, of Sir Evelyn Baring, with sentences of shocked pomposity

[044-078]coming out of his mouth. In some passages, which the editor of the

[044-079]Journals preferred to suppress, he covered Lord Granville with his

[044-080]raillery, picturing the Foreign Secretary, lounging away his morning at

[044-081]Walmer Castle, opening The Times and suddenly discovering, to his

[044-082]horror, that Khartoum was still holding out. 'Why, HE SAID DISTINCTLY he

[044-083]could ONLY hold out SIX MONTHS, and that was in March (counts the

[044-084]months). August! why, he ought to have given in! What is to be done?

[044-085]They'll be howling for an expedition.... It is no laughing matter; THAT

[044-086]ABOMINABLE MAHDI! Why on earth does he not guard his roads better? WHAT

[044-087]IS to be done?' Several times in his bitterness he repeats the

[044-088]suggestion that the authorities at home were secretly hoping that the

[044-089]fall of Khartoum would relieve them of their difficulties.

[044-090]'What that Mahdi is about, Lord Granville is made to exclaim in another

[044-091]deleted paragraph, 'I cannot make out. Why does he not put all his guns

[044-092]on the river and stop the route? Eh what? "We will have to go to

[044-093]Khartoum!" Why, it will cost millions, what a wretched business! What!

[044-094]Send Zobeir? Our conscience recoils from THAT; it is elastic, but not

[044-095]equal to that; it is a pact with the Devil.... Do you not think there is

[044-096]any way of getting hold of H I M, in a quiet way?'

[044-097]If a boy at Eton or Harrow, he declared, had acted as the Government had

[044-098]acted, 'I THINK he would be kicked, and I AM SURE he would deserve it'.

[044-099]He was the victim of hypocrites and humbugs. There was 'no sort of

[044-100]parallel to all this in history--except David with Uriah the Hittite';

[044-101]but then 'there was an Eve in the case', and he was not aware that the

[044-102]Government had even that excuse.

[044-103]From the past, he turned to the future, and surveyed, with a disturbed

[044-104]and piercing vision, the possibilities before him. Supposing that the

[044-105]relief expedition arrived, what would be his position? Upon one thing he

[044-106]was determined: whatever happened, he would not play the part of 'the

[044-107]rescued lamb'. He vehemently asserted that the purpose of the expedition

[044-108]could only be the relief of the Sudan garrisons; it was monstrous to

[044-109]imagine that it had been undertaken merely to ensure his personal

[044-110]safety. He refused to believe it. In any case,

[044-111]'I declare POSITIVELY,' he wrote, with passionate underlinings. 'AND

[044-112]ONCE FOR ALL, THAT I WILL NOT LEAVE THE SUDAN UNTIL EVERY ONE WHO WANTS

[044-113]TO GO DOWN IS GIVEN THE CHANCE TO DO SO, UNLESS a government is

[044-114]established which relieves me of the charge; therefore, if any emissary

[044-115]or letter comes up here ordering me to comedown, I WILL NOT OBEY IT, BUT

[044-116]WILL STAY HERE AND FALL WITH THE TOWN, AND RUN ALL RISKS'.

[044-117]This was sheer insubordination, no doubt; but he could not help that; it

[044-118]was not in his nature to be obedient. 'I know if I was chief, I would

[044-119]never employ myself, for I am incorrigible.' Decidedly, he was not

[044-120]afraid to be 'what club men call insubordinate, though, of all

[044-121]insubordinates, the club men are the worst'.

[044-122]As for the government which was to replace him, there were several

[044-123]alternatives: an Egyptian Pasha might succeed him as Governor-General,

[044-124]or Zobeir might be appointed after all, or the whole country might be

[044-125]handed over to the Sultan. His fertile imagination evolved scheme after

[044-126]scheme; and his visions of his own future were equally various. He would

[044-127]withdraw to the Equator; he would be delighted to spend Christmas in

[044-128]Brussels; he would ... at any rate he would never go back to England.

[044-129]That was certain.

[044-130]'I dwell on the joy of never seeing Great Britain again, with its

[044-131]horrid, wearisome dinner-parties and miseries. How we can put up with

[044-132]those things, passes my imagination! It is a perfect bondage ... I would

[044-133]sooner live 'like a Dervish with the Mahdi, than go out to dinner every

[044-134]night in London. I hope, if any English general comes to Khartoum, he

[044-135]will not ask me to dinner. Why men cannot be friends without bringing

[044-136]the wretched stomachs in, is astounding.'

[044-137]But would an English general ever have the opportunity of asking him to

[044-138]dinner in Khartoum? There were moments when terrible misgivings assailed

[044-139]him. He pieced together his scraps of intelligence with feverish

[044-140]exactitude; he calculated times, distances, marches. 'If,' he wrote on

[044-141]October 24th, they do not come before 30th November, the game is up, and

[044-142]Rule Britannia.' Curious premonitions came into his mind. When he heard

[044-143]that the Mahdi was approaching in person, it seemed to be the fulfilment

[044-144]of a destiny, for he had 'always felt we were doomed to come face to

[044-145]face'. What would be the end of it all? 'It is, of course, on the

[044-146]cards,' he noted, 'that Khartoum is taken under the nose of the

[044-147]Expeditionary Force, which will be JUST TOO LATE.' The splendid hawks

[044-148]that swooped about the palace reminded him of a text in the Bible: 'The

[044-149]eye that mocketh at his father and despiseth to obey his mother, the

[044-150]ravens of the valley shall pick it out, and the young eagles shall eat

[044-151]it.' 'I often wonder,' he wrote, 'whether they are destined to pick my

[044-152]eyes, for I fear I was not the best of sons.'

[044-153]So, sitting late into the night, he filled the empty telegraph forms

[044-154]with the agitations of his spirit, overflowing ever more hurriedly, more

[044-155]furiously, with lines of emphasis, and capitals, and exclamation-marks

[044-156]more and more thickly interspersed, so that the signs of his living

[044-157]passion are still visible to the inquirer of today on those thin sheets

[044-158]of mediocre paper and in the torrent of the ink. But he was a man of

[044-159]elastic temperament; he could not remain forever upon the stretch; he

[044-160]sought, and he found, relaxation in extraneous matters--in metaphysical

[044-161]digressions, or in satirical outbursts, or in the small details of his

[044-162]daily life. It amused him to have the Sudanese soldiers brought in and

[044-163]shown their 'black pug faces' in the palace looking-glasses. He watched

[044-164]with a cynical sympathy the impertinence of a turkey-cock that walked in

[044-165]his courtyard. He made friends with a mouse who, 'judging from her

[044-166]swelled-out appearance', was a lady, and came and ate out of his plate.

[044-167]The cranes that flew over Khartoum in their thousands, and with their

[044-168]curious cry, put him in mind of the poems of Schiller, which few ever

[044-169]read, but which he admired highly, though he only knew them in Bulwer's

[044-170]translation. He wrote little disquisitions on Plutarch and purgatory, on

[044-171]the fear of death and on the sixteenth chapter of the Koran. Then the

[044-172]turkey-cock, strutting with 'every feather on end, and all the colours

[044-173]of the rainbow on his neck', attracted him once more, and he filled

[044-174]several pages with his opinions upon the immortality of animals,

[044-175]drifting on to a discussion of man's position in the universe, and the

[044-176]infinite knowledge of God. It was all clear to him. And yet--'what a

[044-177]contradiction, is life! I hate Her Majesty's Government for their

[044-178]leaving the Sudan after having caused all its troubles, yet I believe

[044-179]our Lord rules heaven and earth, so I ought to hate Him, which I

[044-180](sincerely) do not.'
