# British Imperialism

The political, economic, and military system by which Victorian Britain governed a quarter of the world's land surface and population. In Strachey's biography of General Charles Gordon, imperialism is less an ideology than a stage — the arena in which Gordon's peculiar combination of military efficiency, religious mysticism, and moral absolutism played out to its tragic conclusion at Khartoum.

## Scope and Character

By the 1880s, the British Empire encompassed India, large parts of Africa, the Caribbean, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, and strategic points across the Mediterranean and Asia. It was administered through a patchwork of direct rule (India), chartered companies (East Africa, Nigeria), protectorates (Egypt from 1882), and self-governing colonies (Canada, Australia). The ideology of imperialism combined liberal paternalism (the "civilising mission"), evangelical humanitarianism (the abolition of slavery), strategic calculation (the defense of India), and commercial interest (access to markets and raw materials).

## In the Book

### Gordon's Imperial Career

Gordon's life was shaped by the Empire. He served in the Crimean War, commanded the "Ever-Victorious Army" that suppressed the Taiping Rebellion in China (1863–1864), governed the equatorial provinces of the Sudan (1874–1879), and died defending Khartoum against the Mahdist forces in 1885. Strachey presents these episodes not as chapters in the history of imperial administration but as stages in Gordon's spiritual drama — each posting a further renunciation of worldly ambition, each success a step toward the martyrdom he half-consciously sought.

The Sudan campaign reveals the contradictions of liberal imperialism. Gordon was sent to evacuate Egyptian garrisons — a humanitarian mission to rescue stranded soldiers and civilians. But once in Khartoum, he found evacuation impossible and chose instead to stay and fight. Strachey treats the resulting siege as a parable of imperial hubris: a single Englishman, armed with Bible and cane, attempting to hold back history by force of will.

### Strachey's Treatment

Strachey does not mount an explicit anti-imperial argument — that would violate his method of ironic understatement — but his portrait of Gordon undercuts every premise of imperial ideology. Gordon, the empire's agent, was indifferent to commerce, contemptuous of colonial officials, and convinced that Africans were his spiritual equals. The relief expedition that failed to save him — "too late" became the watchword — exposed the incompetence at the heart of imperial administration.

The irony is structural: the Empire required men like Gordon to govern its territories, but men like Gordon were too strange, too unworldly, too morally absolute to serve imperial interests reliably. "He was a man," Strachey writes, "whose one desire was to lose himself in God" (032-041). Such a man could be a soldier, even a hero, but never a reliable imperial servant.

## Key Figures

- **General Charles Gordon** (1833–1885): The central figure of Strachey's fourth biography — soldier, administrator, mystic, and martyr of Khartoum.
- **Sir Evelyn Baring** (1841–1917): British Consul-General in Egypt, whose cautious diplomacy clashed with Gordon's impetuosity.
- **Lord Wolseley** (1833–1913): Commander of the Gordon Relief Expedition, whose arrival at Khartoum came two days too late.
- **Benjamin Disraeli** (1804–1881): Prime Minister whose imperial policies (purchase of Suez Canal shares, proclamation of Victoria as Empress of India) defined the high imperial moment.
