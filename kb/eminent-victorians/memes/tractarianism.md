# Tractarianism

Tractarianism was the theological engine of the [[oxford-movement|Oxford Movement]] — a system of High Church doctrine propagated through the *Tracts for the Times* (1833–1841) that sought to reclaim the Catholic heritage of the [[church-of-england|Church of England]] through the reaffirmation of apostolic succession, sacramental realism, and patristic authority. Its intellectual architect was [[john-henry-newman|John Henry Newman]]; its most politically consequential adherent, [[cardinal-manning|Henry Edward Manning]]. The name derives from the ninety *Tracts* that served as the movement's primary vehicle of dissemination, and its trajectory — from Anglican revival to Roman conversion — constitutes the central religious drama of Strachey's first biography.

## Definition

The term "Tractarianism" refers specifically to the doctrinal system set forth in the *Tracts for the Times*, a series of pamphlets published at Oxford between 1833 and 1841 under Newman's editorship. The Tracts were written, in their own words, "with the hope of rousing members of our Church to comprehend her alarming position ... as a man might give notice of a fire or inundation, to startle all who heard him" (003-145). They addressed doctrines ranging from Baptismal Regeneration and Fasting to the Mysticism of the Early Fathers, but their unifying intention was "to attack the accepted doctrines and practices of the Church of England" (003-151) — specifically, the Erastian subordination of the Church to the State and the Protestant assumption that the Reformation had settled all theological questions.

At its core, Tractarianism rested on three convictions. First, that the Church of England was not a Protestant sect but a branch of the one Holy Catholic Church, its priesthood preserved through an unbroken Apostolic Succession stretching back "through the mystic symbol of the laying on of hands, to the very Godhead" (003-099). Second, that the Christian religion must be taken *au pied de la lettre* — the Creeds meant what they said, the Sacraments were real channels of divine grace, and the supernatural was "everywhere and at all times, a living force" (003-103). Third, that the Thirty-nine Articles, properly interpreted, were compatible with the full cycle of Catholic doctrine — a claim that reached its explosive culmination in Tract No. 90.

## Context in Victorian England

Tractarianism arose against what its founders perceived as the spiritual bankruptcy of the established Church. Strachey paints a memorable picture of the pre-Tractarian clergy: "Portly divines subscribed with a sigh or a smile to the Thirty-nine Articles, sank quietly into easy living, rode gaily to hounds of a morning as gentlemen should, and, as gentlemen should, carried their two bottles of an evening" (002-080). The Church was a department of state, its doctrine a matter of convention rather than conviction. Into this complacency burst the Tractarian insistence that Christianity demanded everything — not comfortable conformity but the wholehearted embrace of mysteries that reason could not penetrate.

The timing was no accident. The Reform Bill of 1832 had demonstrated that Parliament could reshape the Church's relationship to the nation at will; the suppression of ten Irish bishoprics in 1833 confirmed that the secular power felt no obligation to respect ecclesiastical integrity. Keble's assize sermon on "National Apostasy" (July 1833) — conventionally dated as the beginning of the Oxford Movement — took this very event as its text. For the Tractarians, the political crisis was a theological crisis: had the Church of England any existence independent of the State that had created it? Tractarianism was their answer.

Strachey notes the irony that this most ecclesiastical of movements was launched by men who were anything but worldly. Keble looked out from his Gloucestershire rectory and saw "Infidelity stalking through the land" and "the hideous doctrines of Democracy being openly preached" (002-131). Froude's romantic ardour expressed itself not in politics or poetry but in "a romantic devotion to the Deity and an intense interest in the state of his own soul" (002-149). Newman, "a child of the Romantic Revival, a creature of emotion and of memory, a dreamer" (003-040), would have been more at home in the twelfth century than the nineteenth. The movement they created was at once profoundly reactionary and profoundly disruptive.

## The Tracts and Their Doctrine

The *Tracts for the Times* were not academic treatises; they were polemical pamphlets designed to startle and convert. The following table summarises the most consequential of the ninety Tracts:

| Tract | Author | Subject | Significance |
|-------|--------|---------|-------------|
| No. 1 | Newman | The Apostolic Succession | Established the central Tractarian claim: bishops succeed the Apostles by divine institution |
| No. 67–69 | Pusey | Baptismal Regeneration | Provoked the charge of Romanism by insisting on the objective efficacy of sacraments |
| No. 80 | Keble | Mysticism of the Early Fathers | Defended patristic allegorical exegesis through elaborate numerological arguments |
| No. 90 | Newman | The Thirty-nine Articles | Argued the Articles were compatible with Roman doctrine; precipitated the movement's crisis |

Keble's Tract on mysticism exemplified the Tractarian temper: it treated the number five as "the mystical opponent of the visible and sensible universe" (003-169), and argued through ten pages of close print, with references to Aristeas, St. Augustine, St. Jerome, and Dr. Whitby, that the five loaves at the feeding of the multitude were providentially designed (003-170). To the modern reader, such reasoning seems fantastical; to the Tractarians, it was the recovery of a lost mode of Christian thought.

### Tract No. 90 and the Crisis

Tract No. 90, published in 1841, was the movement's Rubicon. Its thesis was simple and devastating: the Thirty-nine Articles, properly interpreted, did not condemn the doctrines of the Roman Catholic Church. Newman examined each Article in turn, drawing minute verbal distinctions — *Romish* was not *Roman*, "sacrifices of masses" was not "the sacrifice of the Mass" (005-003). The Tract "produced an immense sensation, for it seemed to be a deadly and treacherous blow aimed at the very heart of the Church of England" (005-014).

> Newman pointed out, for instance, that it was generally supposed that the Articles condemned the doctrine of Purgatory; but they did not; they merely condemned the *Romish* doctrine of Purgatory — and Romish, clearly, was not the same thing as Roman. Hence it followed that believers in the Roman doctrine of Purgatory might subscribe the Articles with a good conscience. (005-003)

Strachey's judgment is characteristically double-edged. He concedes that Newman was not dishonest — "the idea of deceit would have been abhorrent to him" (005-052) — but also observes that "the possibilities of truth and falsehood depend upon other things besides sincerity" (005-058). The Tract's real effect was to make explicit what had always been implicit in Tractarian logic: that the Church of England, on its own principles, could not justify its separation from Rome.

## In the Book

Tractarianism is the organising theological context of the Manning biography and appears as a counterpoint in the narratives of Thomas Arnold and Arthur Hugh Clough.

### In Cardinal Manning's Narrative

Manning's encounter with Tractarianism transformed his career. When the Tracts reached his Sussex rectory, he was a young clergyman of Evangelical sympathies; within a few years he had "shaken off his early Evangelical convictions, started an active correspondence with Newman, and was soon working for the new cause" (004-080). He collected quotations, translated Optatus for Pusey, published a sermon on Faith that was condemned as "abominable" by Mr. Bowdler (004-087), and conducted secret confessions of ladies who "met him by stealth in his church" (004-093).

The Tractarian phase of Manning's career is the period in which Strachey's irony is at its most delicate. Manning was "one of the most noted and determined of the Tractarians" (005-136) — yet he was also carefully cultivating the friendship of the low-church Archdeacon Hare, reassuring him about the relocation of pews, and positioning himself for preferment. When Tract No. 90 exploded, Manning found himself in "too conspicuous a position to be able to remain silent" (005-127) and publicly repudiated the Tractarians — delivering a "virulently Protestant harangue" from the pulpit of St. Mary's on Guy Fawkes Day (005-143). The man who had been Newman's warm friend now cut himself off from the movement entirely — or so it appeared. Within six years of Newman's conversion, Manning had himself been received into the Roman Catholic Church.

> As time went on, the situation became more complicated. Tractarianism began to arouse the hostility, not only of the evangelical, but of the moderate churchmen, who could not help perceiving in the ever-deepening "catholicism" of the Oxford party, the dread approaches of Rome. (004-109)

### In Dr. Arnold's Narrative

Thomas Arnold of Rugby represented the antithesis of Tractarian theology. Where the Tractarians insisted on the exclusive authority of the Apostolic Succession, Arnold drew up "a disquieting scheme for allowing Dissenters into the Church" (002-112). Where the Tractarians found truth in the arcana of patristic exegesis, Arnold built a practical, moral Christianity of "muscular" virtue. The two visions of the Church of England could not coexist, and Strachey presents their collision as the theological drama that animated the 1830s and 1840s within the University.

|--------|------|----------------------------|
| **John Henry Newman** | Intellectual leader, editor of the Tracts | "A child of the Romantic Revival ... an artist whose subtle senses caught, like a shower in the sunshine, the impalpable rainbow of the immaterial world" (003-040) |
| **John Keble** | Moral inspiration, Tract author | His *Christian Year* "carried his name into the remotest lodging-houses of England" (002-126); he wished England "vastly more superstitious, more bigoted, more gloomy, more fierce in its religion" (003-136) |
| **Edward Bouverie Pusey** | Scholarly authority, Tract author | "A man of wealth and learning, a professor, a canon of Christ Church, who had, it was rumoured, been to Germany" (003-141) |
| **Hurrell Froude** | Combative edge, provocateur | A young man of "self-assurance and intolerance" whose romantic ardour took the form of "a romantic devotion to the Deity" (002-144); said of Cranmer that "he burned well" (003-139) |
| **Henry Edward Manning** | Most consequential adherent | Rose from Tractarian sympathiser to Archdeacon through strategic ambiguity, then followed the movement's logic to Rome |

## The Logic of Conversion

The most consequential feature of Tractarianism was its inherent trajectory toward Rome. The movement began as an attempt to prove that the Church of England was the true Catholic Church in England; it ended by convincing many of its brightest adherents that it was not. Strachey traces this arc with the precision of a logician: the Tractarians discovered that the English Church "bore everywhere upon it the signs of human imperfection; it was the outcome of revolution and of compromise, of the exigencies of politicians and the caprices of princes" (003-084). Once this was admitted, the case for remaining within it rested on increasingly fragile grounds.

Newman spent six years after Tract No. 90 trying "to persuade himself and anyone else who liked to listen to him that the holding of Anglican orders was not inconsistent with a belief in the whole cycle of Roman doctrine as laid down at the Council of Trent" (004-195). But after reading St. Augustine on the Donatists — who had pointed out that the Donatists were heretics because the Bishop of Rome had said so — "the argument was crushing; it rang in Newman's ears for days and nights; and, though he continued to linger on in agony for six years more, he never could discover any reply to it" (004-192).

Manning followed the same path, though with less agony and more calculation. Tractarianism had given his Anglican career its theological substance; when that substance dissolved, he sought a new foundation. The movement that began with Keble's longing for a more superstitious England ended by delivering the future Cardinal Archbishop of Westminster to the Roman obedience.

## Opposition and Controversy

Tractarianism provoked opposition from every quarter of the Church. Evangelical journals like the *Record* "sniffed Popery in every direction" and spoke of Tractarian clergymen as "tainted" (004-113). Moderate churchmen, who had been content to let sleeping doctrines lie, found themselves confronted with questions they had hoped never to answer. Even bishops — "aggressive low churchmen" like Dr. Shuttleworth, who "went so far as to parody the Tractarian fashion of using Saints' days for the dating of letters by writing 'The Palace, washing-day'" (004-144) — took up arms against the movement.

The official condemnation of Tract No. 90 by the Oxford authorities in 1841 marked the point of no return. "In the hubbub that followed, the contending parties closed their ranks; henceforward, any compromise between the friends and the enemies of the Movement was impossible" (005-125). Newman retired to Littlemore; Manning repudiated his old friends from the pulpit of St. Mary's; the movement as an Anglican force was effectively over. Yet its legacy endured. Strachey notes with characteristic dryness that "since Newman showed the way, the operation" — of interpreting the Articles to mean whatever one wished — "has become so exceedingly common that the most steady-going churchman hardly raises an eyebrow at it now" (005-033).
