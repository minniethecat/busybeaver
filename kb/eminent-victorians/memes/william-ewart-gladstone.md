# William Ewart Gladstone

Gladstone appears in *Eminent Victorians* not as a subject but as a recurring presence — Manning's friend and correspondent, Nightingale's political target, Gordon's reluctant employer. He was the great Victorian statesman who embodied the Liberal synthesis Manning found impossible: devout Anglican piety combined with progressive politics, moral intensity without dogmatic certainty.

## Relationship with Manning

Gladstone and Manning were close friends from their [[oxford-university|Oxford]] days. Their correspondence, which continued even after Manning's conversion to [[rome|Rome]], mixed "the details of practical statesmanship with the speculations of a religious thinker" (006-206). When Manning became Archdeacon, Gladstone wrote: "I rejoice on your account personally; but more for the sake of the Church" (006-206). Manning's conversion ended this intimacy, though the letters continued — cooler now, more formal, the record of a friendship that could not survive the confessional divide.

## The Liberal Statesman

As Prime Minister, Gladstone pursued policies that Manning's Ultramontanism directly opposed: the disestablishment of the Irish Church, educational reform that reduced ecclesiastical control, and a foreign policy of non-intervention that Gordon found maddening. Strachey's Gladstone is the foil to Manning's absolutism — the statesman who believed in parliamentary process and the slow work of moral persuasion, set against the cardinal who believed in infallible authority and immediate obedience.
