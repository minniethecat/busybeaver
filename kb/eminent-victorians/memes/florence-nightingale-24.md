[024-001]Two facts soon emerged, and all that followed turned upon them. It

[024-002]became clear, in the first place, that that imposing mass was not

[024-003]immovable, and, in the second, that its movement, when it did move,

[024-004]would be exceeding slow. The Bison was no match for the Lady. It was in

[024-005]vain that he put down his head and planted his feet in the earth; he

[024-006]could not withstand her; the white hand forced him back. But the process

[024-007]was an extraordinarily gradual one. Dr. Andrew Smith and all his War

[024-008]Office phalanx stood behind, blocking the way; the poor Bison groaned

[024-009]inwardly, and cast a wistful eye towards the happy pastures of the Free

[024-010]Church of Scotland; then slowly, with infinite reluctance, step by step,

[024-011]he retreated, disputing every inch of the ground.

[024-012]The first great measure, which, supported as it was by the Queen, the

[024-013]Cabinet, and the united opinion of the country, it was impossible to

[024-014]resist, was the appointment of a Royal Commission to report upon the

[024-015]health of the Army. The question of the composition of the Commission

[024-016]then immediately arose; and it was over this matter that the first

[024-017]hand-to-hand encounter between Lord Panmure and [[florence-nightingale|Miss Nightingale]] took

[024-018]place. They met, and Miss Nightingale was victorious; Sidney Herbert was

[024-019]appointed Chairman; and, in the end, the only member of the Commission

[024-020]opposed to her views was Dr. Andrew Smith. During the interview, Miss

[024-021]Nightingale made an important discovery: she found that 'the Bison was

[024-022]bullyable'--the hide was the hide of a Mexican buffalo, but the spirit

[024-023]was the spirit of an Alderney calf. And there was one thing above all

[024-024]others which the huge creature dreaded--an appeal to public opinion. The

[024-025]faintest hint of such a terrible eventuality made his heart dissolve

[024-026]within him; he would agree to anything--he would cut short his

[024-027]grouse-shooting--he would make a speech in the House of Lords, he would

[024-028]even overrule Dr. Andrew Smith--rather than that. Miss Nightingale held

[024-029]the fearful threat in reserve--she would speak out what she knew; she

[024-030]would publish the truth to the whole world, and let the whole world

[024-031]judge between them. With supreme skill, she kept this sword of Damocles

[024-032]poised above the Bison's head, and more than once she was actually on

[024-033]the point of really dropping it--for his recalcitrancy grew and grew.

[024-034]The personnel of the Commission once determined upon, there was a

[024-035]struggle, which lasted for six months, over the nature of its powers.

[024-036]Was it to be an efficient body, armed with the right of full inquiry and

[024-037]wide examination, or was it to be a polite official contrivance for

[024-038]exonerating Dr. Andrew Smith? The [[war-office|War Office]] phalanx closed its ranks,

[024-039]and fought tooth and nail; but it was defeated: the Bison was bullyable.

[024-040]'Three months from this day,' Miss Nightingale had written at last, 'I

[024-041]publish my experience of the [[crimean-war|Crimean Campaign]], and my suggestions for

[024-042]improvement, unless there has been a fair and tangible pledge by that

[024-043]time for reform.'

[024-044]Who could face that?

[024-045]And, if the need came, she meant to be as good as her word. For she had

[024-046]now determined, whatever might be the fate of the Commission, to draw up

[024-047]her own report upon the questions at issue. The labour involved was

[024-048]enormous; her health was almost desperate; but she did not flinch, and

[024-049]after six months of incredible industry she had put together and written

[024-050]with her own hand her Notes affecting the Health, Efficiency, and

[024-051]Hospital Administration of the [[british-army|British Army]]. This extraordinary

[024-052]composition, filling more than 800 closely printed pages, laying down

[024-053]vast principles of far-reaching reform, discussing the minutest details

[024-054]of a multitude of controversial subjects, containing an enormous mass of

[024-055]information of the most varied kinds--military, statistical, sanitary,

[024-056]architectural--was never given to the public, for the need never came;

[024-057]but it formed the basis of the Report of the Royal Commission; and it

[024-058]remains to this day the leading authority on the medical administration

[024-059]of armies.

[024-060]Before it had been completed, the struggle over the powers of the

[024-061]Commission had been brought to a victorious close. Lord Panmure had

[024-062]given way once more; he had immediately hurried to the Queen to obtain

[024-063]her consent; and only then, when Her Majesty's initials had been

[024-064]irrevocably affixed to the fatal document, did he dare to tell Dr.

[024-065]Andrew Smith what he had done. The Commission met, and another immense

[024-066]load fell upon Miss Nightingale's shoulders. Today she would, of course,

[024-067]have been one of the Commission herself; but at that time the idea of a

[024-068]woman appearing in such a capacity was unheard of; and no one even

[024-069]suggested the possibility of Miss Nightingale's doing so. The result was

[024-070]that she was obliged to remain behind the scenes throughout, to coach

[024-071]Sidney Herbert in private at every important juncture, and to convey to

[024-072]him and to her other friends upon the Commission the vast funds of her

[024-073]expert knowledge--so essential in the examination of witnesses--by means

[024-074]of innumerable consultations, letters, and memoranda. It was even

[024-075]doubtful whether the proprieties would admit of her giving evidence; and

[024-076]at last, as a compromise, her modesty only allowed her to do so in the

[024-077]form of written answers to written questions. At length, the grand

[024-078]affair was finished. The Commission's Report, embodying almost word for

[024-079]word the suggestions of Miss Nightingale, was drawn up by Sidney

[024-080]Herbert. Only one question remained to be answered--would anything,

[024-081]after all, be done? Or would the Royal Commission, like so many other

[024-082]Royal Commissions before and since, turn out to have achieved nothing

[024-083]but the concoction of a very fat bluebook on a very high shelf?

[024-084]And so the last and the deadliest struggle with the Bison began. Six

[024-085]months had been spent in coercing him into granting the Commission

[024-086]effective powers; six more months were occupied by the work of the

[024-087]Commission; and now yet another six were to pass in extorting from him

[024-088]the means whereby the recommendations of the Commission might be

[024-089]actually carried out. But, in the end, the thing was done. Miss

[024-090]Nightingale seemed, indeed, during these months, to be upon the very

[024-091]brink of death. Accompanied by the faithful Aunt Mai, she moved from

[024-092]place to place--to Hampstead, to Highgate, to Derbyshire, to Malvern--in

[024-093]what appeared to be a last desperate effort to find health somewhere;

[024-094]but she carried that with her which made health impossible. Her desire

[024-095]for work could now scarcely be distinguished from mania. At one moment

[024-096]she was writing a 'last letter' to Sidney Herbert; at the next she was

[024-097]offering to go out to India to nurse the sufferers in the Mutiny. When

[024-098]Dr. Sutherland wrote, imploring her to take a holiday, she raved.

[024-099]Rest!--

[024-100]'I am lying without my head, without my claws, and you all peck at me.

[024-101]It is de rigueur, d'obligation, like the saying something to one's hat,

[024-102]when one goes into church, to say to me all that has been said to me 110

[024-103]times a day during the last three months. It is the obbligato on the

[024-104]violin, and the twelve violins all practise it together, like the clocks

[024-105]striking twelve o'clock at night all over London, till I say like Xavier

[024-106]de Maistre, Assez, je sais, je ne le sais que trop. I am not a penitent;

[024-107]but you are like the R.C. confessor, who says what is de rigueur....'

[024-108]Her wits began to turn, and there was no holding her. She worked like a

[024-109]slave in a mine. She began to believe, as she had begun to believe at

[024-110][[scutari|Scutari]], that none of her fellow-workers had their hearts in the

[024-111]business; if they had, why did they not work as she did? She could only

[024-112]see slackness and stupidity around her. Dr. Sutherland, of course, was

[024-113]grotesquely muddle-headed; and Arthur Clough incurably lazy. Even Sidney

[024-114]Herbert ... oh yes, he had simplicity and candour and quickness of

[024-115]perception, no doubt; but he was an eclectic; and what could one hope

[024-116]for from a man who went away to fish in Ireland just when the Bison most

[024-117]needed bullying? As for the Bison himself, he had fled to Scotland where

[024-118]he remained buried for many months. The fate of the vital recommendation

[024-119]in the Commission's Report--the appointment of four Sub-Commissions

[024-120]charged with the duty of determining upon the details of the proposed

[024-121]reforms and of putting them into execution--still hung in the balance.

[024-122]The Bison consented to everything; and then, on a flying visit to

[024-123]London, withdrew his consent and hastily returned to Scotland. Then for

[024-124]many weeks all business was suspended; he had gout--gout in the

[024-125]hands--so that he could not write. 'His gout was always handy,' remarked

[024-126]Miss Nightingale. But eventually it was clear even to the Bison that the

[024-127]game was up, and the inevitable surrender came.

[024-128]There was, however, one point in which he triumphed over Miss

[024-129]Nightingale: the building of Netley Hospital had been begun under his

[024-130]orders, before her return to England. Soon after her arrival she

[024-131]examined the plans, and found that they reproduced all the worst faults

[024-132]of an out-of-date and mischievous system of hospital construction. She

[024-133]therefore urged that the matter should be reconsidered, and in the

[024-134]meantime the building stopped. But the Bison was obdurate; it would be

[024-135]very expensive, and in any case it was too late. Unable to make any

[024-136]impression on him, and convinced of the extreme importance of the

[024-137]question, she determined to appeal to a higher authority. Lord

[024-138]Palmerston was Prime Minister; she had known him from her childhood; he

[024-139]was a near neighbour of her father's in the New Forest. She went down to

[024-140]the New Forest, armed with the plan of the proposed hospital and all the

[024-141]relevant information, stayed the night at Lord Palmerston's house, and

[024-142]convinced him of the necessity of rebuilding Netley.

[024-143]'It seems to me,' Lord Palmerston wrote to Lord Panmure, 'that at Netley

[024-144]all consideration of what would best tend to the comfort and recovery of

[024-145]the patients has been sacrificed to the vanity of the architect, whose

[024-146]sole object has been to make a building which should cut a dash when

[024-147]looked at from the Southampton river ... Pray, therefore, stop all

[024-148]further progress in the work until the matter can be duly considered.'

[024-149]But the Bison was not to be moved by one peremptory letter, even if it

[024-150]was from the Prime Minister. He put forth all his powers of

[024-151]procrastination, Lord Palmerston lost interest in the subject, and so

[024-152]the chief military hospital in England was triumphantly completed on

[024-153]insanitary principles, with unventilated rooms, and with all the

[024-154]patients' windows facing northeast.

[024-155]But now the time had come when the Bison was to trouble and to be

[024-156]troubled no more. A vote in the House of Commons brought about the fall

[024-157]of Lord Palmerston's Government, and, Lord Panmure found himself at

[024-158]liberty to devote the rest of his life to the Free Church of Scotland.

[024-159]After a brief interval, Sidney Herbert became Secretary of State for

[024-160]War. Great was the jubilation in the Nightingale Cabinet: the day of

[024-161]achievement had dawned at last. The next two and a half years (1859-61)

[024-162]saw the introduction of the whole system of reforms for which Miss

[024-163]Nightingale had been struggling so fiercely--reforms which make Sidney

[024-164]Herbert's tenure of power at the War Office an important epoch in the

[024-165]history of the British Army. The four Sub-Commissions, firmly

[024-166]established under the immediate control of the Minister, and urged

[024-167]forward by the relentless perseverance of Miss Nightingale, set to work

[024-168]with a will. The barracks and the hospitals were remodelled; they were

[024-169]properly ventilated and warmed and lighted for the first time; they were

[024-170]given a water supply which actually supplied water, and kitchens where,

[024-171]strange to say, it was possible to cook. Then the great question of the

[024-172]Purveyor--that portentous functionary whose powers and whose lack of

[024-173]powers had weighed like a nightmare upon Scutari--was taken in hand, and

[024-174]new regulations were laid down, accurately defining his responsibilities

[024-175]and his duties. One Sub-Commission reorganised the medical statistics of

[024-176]the Army; another established in spite of the last convulsive efforts of

[024-177]the Department an Army Medical School. Finally, the Army Medical

[024-178]Department itself was completely reorganised; an administrative code was

[024-179]drawn up; and the great and novel principle was established that it was

[024-180]as much a part of the duty of the authorities to look after the

[024-181]soldier's health as to look after his sickness. Besides this, it was at

[024-182]last officially admitted that he had a moral and intellectual side.

[024-183]Coffee-rooms and reading-rooms, gymnasiums and workshops were

[024-184]instituted. A new era did in truth appear to have begun. Already by 1861

[024-185]the mortality in the Army had decreased by one-half since the days of

[024-186]the Crimea. It was no wonder that even vaster possibilities began now to

[024-187]open out before Miss Nightingale. One thing was still needed to complete

[024-188]and to assure her triumphs. The Army Medical Department was indeed

[024-189]reorganised; but the great central machine was still untouched. The War

[024-190]Office itself--! If she could remould that nearer to her heart's

[024-191]desire--there indeed would be a victory! And until that final act was

[024-192]accomplished, how could she be certain that all the rest of her

[024-193]achievements might not, by some capricious turn of Fortune's wheel--a

[024-194]change of Ministry, perhaps, replacing Sidney Herbert by some puppet of

[024-195]the permanent official gang--be swept to limbo in a moment?
