[023-001]For instance, I was called up last night, a dragoon having come express

[023-002]with a telegraphic message in these words, "Lord Panmure to General

[023-003]Simpson--Captain Jarvis has been bitten by a centipede. How is he now?"'

[023-004]General Simpson might have put up with this, though to be sure it did

[023-005]seem 'rather too trifling an affair to call for a dragoon to ride a

[023-006]couple of miles in the dark that he may knock up the Commander of the

[023-007]Army out of the very small allowance of sleep permitted; but what was

[023-008]really more than he could bear was to find 'upon sending in the morning

[023-009]another mounted dragoon to inquire after Captain Jarvis, four miles off,

[023-010]that he never has been bitten at all, but has had a boil, from which he

[023-011]is fast recovering'. But Lord Panmure had troubles of his own. His

[023-012]favourite nephew, Captain Dowbiggin, was at the front, and to one of his

[023-013]telegrams to the Commander-in-Chief the Minister had taken occasion to

[023-014]append the following carefully qualified sentence--'I recommend

[023-015]Dowbiggin to your notice, should you have a vacancy, and if he is fit'.

[023-016]Unfortunately, in those early days, it was left to the discretion of the

[023-017]telegraphist to compress the messages which passed through his hands; so

[023-018]that the result was that Lord Panmure's delicate appeal reached its

[023-019]destination in the laconic form of 'Look after Dowb'. The Headquarters

[023-020]Staff were at first extremely puzzled; they were at last extremely

[023-021]amused. The story spread; and 'Look after Dowb' remained for many years

[023-022]the familiar formula for describing official hints in favour of

[023-023]deserving nephews.

[023-024]And now that all this was over, now that Sebastopol had been, somehow or

[023-025]another, taken; now that peace was, somehow or another, made; now that

[023-026]the troubles of office might surely be expected to be at an end at

[023-027]last--here was [[florence-nightingale|Miss Nightingale]] breaking in upon the scene with her talk

[023-028]about the state of the hospitals and the necessity for sanitary reform.

[023-029]It was most irksome; and Lord Panmure almost began to wish that he was

[023-030]engaged upon some more congenial occupation--discussing, perhaps, the

[023-031]constitution of the Free Church of Scotland--a question in which he was

[023-032]profoundly interested. But no; duty was paramount; and he set himself,

[023-033]with a sigh of resignation, to the task of doing as little of it as he

[023-034]possibly could.

[023-035]'The Bison' his friends called him; and the name fitted both his

[023-036]physical demeanour and his habit of mind. That large low head seemed to

[023-037]have been created for butting rather than for anything else. There he

[023-038]stood, four-square and menacing in the doorway of reform; and it

[023-039]remained to be seen whether, the bulky mass, upon whose solid hide even

[023-040]the barbed arrows of Lord Raglan's scorn had made no mark, would prove

[023-041]amenable to the pressure of Miss Nightingale. Nor was he alone in the

[023-042]doorway. There loomed behind him the whole phalanx of professional

[023-043]conservatism, the stubborn supporters of the out-of-date, the

[023-044]worshippers and the victims of [[war-office|War Office]] routine. Among these it was

[023-045]only natural that Dr. Andrew Smith, the head of the Army Medical

[023-046]Department, should have been pre-eminent--Dr. Andrew Smith, who had

[023-047]assured Miss Nightingale before she left England that 'nothing was

[023-048]wanted at [[scutari|Scutari]]'. Such were her opponents; but she too was not without

[023-049]allies. She had gained the ear of Royalty--which was something; at any

[023-050]moment that she pleased she could gain the ear of the public--which was

[023-051]a great deal. She had a host of admirers and friends; and--to say

[023-052]nothing of her personal qualities--her knowledge, her tenacity, her

[023-053]tact--she possessed, too, one advantage which then, far more even than

[023-054]now, carried an immense weight--she belonged to the highest circle of

[023-055]society. She moved naturally among Peers and Cabinet Ministers--she was

[023-056]one of their own set; and in those days their set was a very narrow one.

[023-057]What kind of attention would such persons have paid to some middle-class

[023-058]woman with whom they were not acquainted, who possessed great experience

[023-059]of Army nursing and had decided views upon hospital reform? They would

[023-060]have politely ignored her; but it was impossible to ignore Flo

[023-061]Nightingale. When she spoke, they were obliged to listen; and, when they

[023-062]had once begun to do that--what might not follow? She knew her power,

[023-063]and she used it. She supported her weightiest minutes with familiar

[023-064]witty little notes. The Bison began to look grave. It might be

[023-065]difficult--it might be damned difficult--to put down one's head against

[023-066]the white hand of a lady ...

[023-067]Of Miss Nightingale's friends, the most important was Sidney Herbert. He

[023-068]was a man upon whom the good fairies seemed to have showered, as he lay

[023-069]in his cradle, all their most enviable goods. Well born, handsome, rich,

[023-070]the master of Wilton--one of those great country-houses, clothed with

[023-071]the glamour of a historic past, which are the peculiar glory of

[023-072]England--he possessed--besides all these advantages: so charming, so

[023-073]lively, so gentle a disposition that no one who had once come near him

[023-074]could ever be his enemy.

[023-075]He was, in fact, a man of whom it was difficult not to say that he was a

[023-076]perfect English gentleman. For his virtues were equal even to his good

[023-077]fortune. He was religious, deeply religious. 'I am more and more

[023-078]convinced every day,' he wrote, when he had been for some years a

[023-079]Cabinet Minister, 'that in politics, as in everything else, nothing can

[023-080]be right which is not in accordance with the spirit of the Gospel.' No

[023-081]one was more unselfish; he was charitable and benevolent to a remarkable

[023-082]degree; and he devoted the whole of his life, with an unwavering

[023-083]conscientiousness, to the public service. With such a character, with

[023-084]such opportunities, what high hopes must have danced before him, what

[023-085]radiant visions of accomplished duties, of ever-increasing usefulness,

[023-086]of beneficent power, of the consciousness of disinterested success! Some

[023-087]of those hopes and visions were, indeed, realised; but, in the end, the

[023-088]career of Sidney Herbert seemed to show that, with all their generosity,

[023-089]there was some gift or other--what was it?--some essential gift--which

[023-090]the good fairies had withheld, and that even the qualities of a perfect

[023-091]English gentleman may be no safeguard against anguish, humiliation, and

[023-092]defeat.

[023-093]That career would certainly have been very different if he had never

[023-094]known Miss Nightingale. The alliance between them which had begun with

[023-095]her appointment to Scutari, which had grown closer and closer while the

[023-096]war lasted, developed, after her return, into one of the most

[023-097]extraordinary friendships. It was the friendship of a man and a woman

[023-098]intimately bound together by their devotion to a public cause; mutual

[023-099]affection, of course, played a part in it, but it was an incidental

[023-100]part; the whole soul of the relationship was a community of work.

[023-101]Perhaps out of England such an intimacy could hardly have existed--an

[023-102]intimacy so utterly untinctured not only by passion itself but by the

[023-103]suspicion of it. For years Sidney Herbert saw Miss Nightingale almost

[023-104]daily, for long hours together, corresponding with her incessantly when

[023-105]they were apart; and the tongue of scandal was silent; and one of the

[023-106]most devoted of her admirers was his wife. But what made the connection

[023-107]still more remarkable was the way in which the parts that were played in

[023-108]it were divided between the two. The man who acts, decides, and

[023-109]achieves; the woman who encourages, applauds, and--from a

[023-110]distance--inspires: the combination is common enough; but Miss

[023-111]Nightingale was neither an Aspasia nor an Egeria. In her case it is

[023-112]almost true to say that the roles were reversed; the qualities of

[023-113]pliancy and sympathy fell to the man, those of command and initiative to

[023-114]the woman.

[023-115]There was one thing only which Miss Nightingale lacked in her equipment

[023-116]for public life; she had not--she never could have--the public power and

[023-117]authority which belonged to the successful politician. That power and

[023-118]authority Sidney Herbert possessed; that fact was obvious, and the

[023-119]conclusions no less so: it was through the man that the woman must work

[023-120]her will. She took hold of him, taught him, shaped him, absorbed him,

[023-121]dominated him through and through. He did not resist--he did not wish to

[023-122]resist; his natural inclination lay along the same path as hers; only

[023-123]that terrific personality swept him forward at her own fierce pace and

[023-124]with her own relentless stride. Swept him--where to? Ah! Why had he ever

[023-125]known Miss Nightingale? If Lord Panmure was a bison, Sidney Herbert, no

[023-126]doubt, was a stag--a comely, gallant creature springing through the

[023-127]forest; but the forest is a dangerous place. One has the image of those

[023-128]wide eyes fascinated suddenly by something feline, something strong;

[023-129]there is a pause; and then the tigress has her claws in the quivering

[023-130]haunches; and then--!

[023-131]Besides Sidney Herbert, she had other friends who, in a more restricted

[023-132]sphere, were hardly less essential to her. If, in her condition of

[023-133]bodily collapse, she were to accomplish what she was determined that she

[023-134]should accomplish, the attentions and the services of others would be

[023-135]absolutely indispensable. Helpers and servers she must have; and

[023-136]accordingly there was soon formed about her a little group of devoted

[023-137]disciples upon whose affections and energies she could implicitly rely.

[023-138]Devoted, indeed, these disciples were, in no ordinary sense of the term;

[023-139]for certainly she was no light taskmistress, and he who set out to be of

[023-140]use to Miss Nightingale was apt to find, before he had gone very far,

[023-141]that he was in truth being made use of in good earnest to the very limit

[023-142]of his endurance and his capacity. Perhaps, even beyond those limits;

[023-143]why not? Was she asking of others more than she was giving herself? Let

[023-144]them look at her lying there pale and breathless on the couch; could it

[023-145]be said that she spared herself? Why, then, should she spare others? And

[023-146]it was not for her own sake that she made these claims. For her own

[023-147]sake, indeed! No! They all knew it! it was for the sake of the work. And

[023-148]so the little band, bound body and soul in that strange servitude,

[023-149]laboured on ungrudgingly.

[023-150]Among the most faithful was her 'Aunt Mai', her father's sister, who

[023-151]from the earliest days had stood beside her, who had helped her to

[023-152]escape from the thraldom of family life, who had been with her at

[023-153]Scutari, and who now acted almost the part of a mother to her, watching

[023-154]over her with infinite care in all the movements and uncertainties which

[023-155]her state of health involved. Another constant attendant was her

[023-156]brother-in-law, Sir Harry Verney, whom she found particularly valuable

[023-157]in parliamentary affairs. Arthur Clough, the poet, also a connection by

[023-158]marriage, she used in other ways. Ever since he had lost his faith at

[023-159]the time of the [[oxford-movement|Oxford Movement]], Clough had passed his life in a

[023-160]condition of considerable uneasiness, which was increased rather than

[023-161]diminished by the practice of poetry. Unable to decide upon the purpose

[023-162]of an existence whose savour had fled together with his belief in the

[023-163]Resurrection, his spirits lowered still further by ill-health, and his

[023-164]income not all that it should be, he had determined to seek the solution

[023-165]of his difficulties in the United States of America. But, even there,

[023-166]the solution was not forthcoming; and, when, a little later, he was

[023-167]offered a post in a government department at home, he accepted it, came

[023-168]to live in London, and immediately fell under the influence of Miss

[023-169]Nightingale. Though the purpose of existence might be still uncertain

[023-170]and its nature still unsavoury, here, at any rate, under the eye of this

[023-171]inspired woman, was something real, something earnest: his only doubt

[023-172]was--could he be of any use? Certainly he could. There were a great

[023-173]number of miscellaneous little jobs which there was nobody handy to do.

[023-174]For instance, when Miss Nightingale was travelling, there were the

[023-175]railway-tickets to be taken; and there were proof-sheets to be

[023-176]corrected; and then there were parcels to be done up in brown paper, and

[023-177]carried to the post. Certainly he could be useful. And so, upon such

[023-178]occupations as these, Arthur Clough was set to work. 'This that I see,

[023-179]is not all,' he comforted himself by reflecting, 'and this that I do is

[023-180]but little; nevertheless it is good, though there is better than it.' As

[023-181]time went on, her 'Cabinet', as she called it, grew larger. Officials

[023-182]with whom her work brought her into touch and who sympathised with her

[023-183]objects, were pressed into her service; and old friends of the Crimean

[023-184]days gathered around her when they returned to England. Among these the

[023-185]most indefatigable was Dr. Sutherland, a sanitary expert, who for more

[023-186]than thirty years acted as her confidential private secretary, and

[023-187]surrendered to her purposes literally the whole of his life. Thus

[023-188]sustained and assisted, thus slaved for and adored, she prepared to

[023-189]beard the Bison.
