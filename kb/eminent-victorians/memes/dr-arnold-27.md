[027-001]V

[027-002]AT one time she had almost decided to end her life in retirement as a

[027-003]patient at St. Thomas's Hospital. But partly owing to the persuasions of

[027-004]Mr. Jowett, she changed her mind; for forty-five years she remained in

[027-005]South Street; and in South Street she died. As old age approached,

[027-006]though her influence with the official world gradually diminished, her

[027-007]activities seemed to remain as intense and widespread as before. When

[027-008]hospitals were to be built, when schemes of sanitary reform were in

[027-009]agitation, when wars broke out, she was still the adviser of all Europe.

[027-010]Still, with a characteristic self-assurance, she watched from her

[027-011]Mayfair bedroom over the welfare of India. Still, with an indefatigable

[027-012]enthusiasm, she pushed forward the work, which, perhaps, was nearer to

[027-013]her heart, more completely her own, than all the rest--the training of

[027-014]nurses. In her moments of deepest depression, when her greatest

[027-015]achievements seemed to lose their lustre, she thought of her nurses, and

[027-016]was comforted. The ways of God, she found, were strange indeed. 'How

[027-017]inefficient I was in the Crimea,' she noted. 'Yet He has raised up from

[027-018]it trained nursing.'

[027-019]At other times, she was better satisfied. Looking back, she was amazed

[027-020]by the enormous change which, since her early days, had come over the

[027-021]whole treatment of illness, the whole conception of public and domestic

[027-022]health--a change in which, she knew, she had played her part. One of her

[027-023]Indian admirers, the Aga Khan, came to visit her. She expatiated on the

[027-024]marvellous advances she had lived to see in the management of

[027-025]hospitals--in drainage, in ventilation, in sanitary work of every kind.

[027-026]There was a pause; and then, 'Do you think you are improving?' asked the

[027-027]Aga Khan. She was a little taken aback, and said, 'What do you mean by

[027-028]"improving"?' He replied, 'Believing more in God.' She saw that he had a

[027-029]view of God which was different from hers. 'A most interesting man,' she

[027-030]noted after the interview; 'but you could never teach him sanitation.'

[027-031]When old age actually came, something curious happened. Destiny, having

[027-032]waited very patiently, played a queer trick on [[florence-nightingale|Miss Nightingale]]. The

[027-033]benevolence and public spirit of that long life had only been equalled

[027-034]by its acerbity. Her virtue had dwelt in hardness, and she had poured

[027-035]forth her unstinted usefulness with a bitter smile upon her lips. And

[027-036]now the sarcastic years brought the proud woman her punishment. She was

[027-037]not to die as she had lived. The sting was to be taken out of her; she

[027-038]was to be made soft; she was to be reduced to compliance and

[027-039]complacency. The change came gradually, but at last it was unmistakable.

[027-040]The terrible commander who had driven Sidney Herbert to his death, to

[027-041]whom Mr. Jowett had applied the words of Homer, amoton memaniia--raging

[027-042]insatiably--now accepted small compliments with gratitude, and indulged

[027-043]in sentimental friendships with young girls. The author of "Notes on

[027-044]Nursing"--that classical compendium of the besetting sins of the

[027-045]sisterhood, drawn up with the detailed acrimony, the vindictive relish,

[027-046]of a Swift--now spent long hours in composing sympathetic Addresses to

[027-047]Probationers, whom she petted and wept over in turn. And, at the same

[027-048]time, there appeared a corresponding alteration in her physical mood.

[027-049]The thin, angular woman, with her haughty eye and her acrid mouth, had

[027-050]vanished; and in her place was the rounded, bulky form of a fat old

[027-051]lady, smiling all day long. Then something else became visible. The

[027-052]brain which had been steeled at [[scutari|Scutari]] was indeed, literally, growing

[027-053]soft. Senility--an ever more and more amiable senility--descended.

[027-054]Towards the end, consciousness itself grew lost in a roseate haze, and

[027-055]melted into nothingness.

[027-056]It was just then, three years before her death, when she was

[027-057]eighty-seven years old (1907), that those in authority bethought them

[027-058]that the opportune moment had come for bestowing a public honour on

[027-059]Florence Nightingale. She was offered the Order of Merit. That Order,

[027-060]whose roll contains, among other distinguished names, those of Sir

[027-061]Lawrence Alma Tadema and Sir Edward Elgar, is remarkable chiefly for the

[027-062]fact that, as its title indicates, it is bestowed because its recipient

[027-063]deserves it, and for no other reason. Miss Nightingale's representatives

[027-064]accepted the honour, and her name, after a lapse of many years, once

[027-065]more appeared in the Press. Congratulations from all sides came pouring

[027-066]in. There was a universal burst of enthusiasm--a final revivification of

[027-067]the ancient myth. Among her other admirers, the German Emperor took this

[027-068]opportunity of expressing his feelings towards her. 'His Majesty,' wrote

[027-069]the German Ambassador, 'having just brought to a close a most enjoyable

[027-070]stay in the beautiful neighbourhood of your old home near Romsey, has

[027-071]commanded me to present you with some flowers as a token of his esteem.'

[027-072]Then, by Royal command, the Order of Merit was brought to South Street,

[027-073]and there was a little ceremony of presentation. Sir Douglas Dawson,

[027-074]after a short speech, stepped forward, and handed the insignia of the

[027-075]Order to Miss Nightingale. Propped up by pillows, she dimly recognised

[027-076]that some compliment was being paid her. 'Too kind--too kind,' she

[027-077]murmured; and she was not ironical.

[027-088]Dr. Arnold

[027-089]IN 1827 the headmastership of [[rugby-school|Rugby School]] fell vacant, and it became

[027-090]necessary for the twelve trustees, noblemen and gentlemen of

[027-091]Warwickshire, to appoint a successor to the post. Reform was in the

[027-092]air--political, social, religious; there was even a feeling abroad that

[027-093]our great public schools were not quite all that they should be, and

[027-094]that some change or other--no one precisely knew what--but some change

[027-095]in the system of their management, was highly desirable. Thus it was

[027-096]natural that when the twelve noblemen and gentlemen, who had determined

[027-097]to be guided entirely by the merits of the candidates, found among the

[027-098]testimonials pouring in upon them a letter from Dr. Hawkins, the Provost

[027-099]of Oriel, predicting that if they elected Mr. Thomas Arnold he would

[027-100]'change the face of education all through the public schools of

[027-101]England', they hesitated no longer; obviously, Mr. Thomas Arnold was

[027-102]their man. He was elected therefore; received, as was fitting, priest's

[027-103]orders; became, as was no less fitting, a Doctor of Divinity; and in

[027-104]August, 1828, took up the duties of his office.

[027-105]All that was known of the previous life of Dr. Arnold seemed to justify

[027-106]the prediction of the Provost of Oriel, and the choice of the Trustees.

[027-107]The son of a respectable Collector of Customs, he had been educated at

[027-108]Winchester and at [[oxford-university|Oxford]], where his industry and piety had given him a

[027-109]conspicuous place among his fellow students. It is true that, as a

[027-110]schoolboy, a certain pompousness in the style of his letters home

[027-111]suggested to the more clear-sighted among his relatives the possibility

[027-112]that young Thomas might grow up into a prig; but, after all, what else

[027-113]could be expected from a child who, at the age of three, had been

[027-114]presented by his father, as a reward for proficiency in his studies,

[027-115]with the twenty-four volumes of Smollett's History of England?

[027-116]His career at Oxford had been a distinguished one, winding up with an

[027-117]Oriel fellowship. It was at about this time that the smooth and

[027-118]satisfactory progress of his life was for a moment interrupted: he began

[027-119]to be troubled by religious doubts. These doubts, as we learn from one

[027-120]of his contemporaries, who afterwards became Mr. Justice Coleridge,

[027-121]'were not low nor rationalistic in their tendency, according to the bad

[027-122]sense of that term; there was no indisposition in him to believe merely

[027-123]because the article transcended his reason, he doubted the proof and the

[027-124]interpretation of the textual authority'.

[027-125]In his perturbation, Arnold consulted Keble, who was at that time one of

[027-126]his closest friends, and a Fellow of the same College.

[027-127]'The subject of these distressing thoughts,' Keble wrote to Coleridge,

[027-128]'is that most awful one, on which all very inquisitive reasoning minds

[027-129]are, I believe, most liable to such temptations--I mean, the doctrine of

[027-130]the blessed Trinity. Do not start, my dear Coleridge; I do not believe

[027-131]that Arnold has any serious scruples of the UNDERSTANDING about it, but

[027-132]it is a defect of his mind that he cannot get rid of a certain feeling

[027-133]of objections.' What was to be done? Keble's advice was peremptory.

[027-134]Arnold was 'bid to pause in his inquiries, to pray earnestly for help

[027-135]and light from above, and turn himself more strongly than ever to the

[027-136]practical duties of a holy life'. He did so, and the result was all that

[027-137]could be wished. He soon found himself blessed with perfect peace of

[027-138]mind, and a settled conviction.

[027-139]One other difficulty, and one only, we hear of at this point in his

[027-140]life. His dislike of early rising amounted, we are told, 'almost to a

[027-141]constitutional infirmity'. This weakness too he overcame, yet not quite

[027-142]so successfully as his doubts upon the doctrine of the Trinity. For in

[027-143]afterlife, the Doctor would often declare 'that early rising continued

[027-144]to be a daily effort to him and that in this instance he never found the

[027-145]truth of the usual rule that all things are made easy by custom.

[027-146]He married young and settled down in the country as a private tutor for

[027-147]youths preparing for the Universities. There he remained for ten

[027-148]years--happy, busy, and sufficiently prosperous. Occupied chiefly with

[027-149]his pupils, he nevertheless devoted much of his energy to wider

[027-150]interests. He delivered a series of sermons in the parish church; and he

[027-151]began to write a History of [[rome|Rome]], in the hope, as he said, that its tone

[027-152]might be such 'that the strictest of what is called the Evangelical

[027-153]party would not object to putting it into the hands of their children'.

[027-154]His views on the religious and political condition of the country began

[027-155]to crystallise. He was alarmed by the 'want of Christian principle in

[027-156]the literature of the day', looking forward anxiously to 'the approach

[027-157]of a greater struggle between good and evil than the world has yet

[027-158]seen'; and, after a serious conversation with Dr. Whately, began to

[027-159]conceive the necessity of considerable alterations in the Church

[027-160]Establishment.

[027-161]All who knew him during these years were profoundly impressed by the

[027-162]earnestness of his religious convictions and feelings, which, as one

[027-163]observer said, 'were ever bursting forth'. It was impossible to

[027-164]disregard his 'deep consciousness of the invisible world' and 'the

[027-165]peculiar feeling of love and adoration which he entertained towards our

[027-166]Lord Jesus Christ'. 'His manner of awful reverence when speaking of God

[027-167]or of the Scriptures' was particularly striking. 'No one could know him

[027-168]even a little,' said another friend, 'and not be struck by his absolute

[027-169]wrestling with evil, so that like St. Paul, he seemed to be battling

[027-170]with the wicked one, and yet with a feeling of God's help on his side.'

[027-171]Such was the man who, at the age of thirty-three, became headmaster of

[027-172]Rugby. His outward appearance was the index of his inward character;

[027-173]everything about him denoted energy, earnestness, and the best

[027-174]intentions. His legs, perhaps, were shorter than they should have been;

[027-175]but the sturdy athletic frame, especially when it was swathed (as it

[027-176]usually was) in the flowing robes of a Doctor of Divinity, was full of

[027-177]an imposing vigour; and his head, set decisively upon the collar, stock,

[027-178]and bands of ecclesiastical tradition, clearly belonged to a person of

[027-179]eminence. The thick, dark clusters of his hair, his bushy eyebrows and

[027-180]curling whiskers, his straight nose and bulky chin, his firm and

[027-181]upward-curving lower lip--all these revealed a temperament of ardour and

[027-182]determination. His eyes were bright and large; they were also obviously

[027-183]honest. And yet--why was it? Was it in the lines of the mouth or the

[027-184]frown on the forehead?--it was hard to say, but it was

[027-185]unmistakable--there was a slightly puzzled look upon the face of Dr.

[027-186]Arnold.
