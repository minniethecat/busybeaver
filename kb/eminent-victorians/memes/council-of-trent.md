# Council of Trent (1545–1563)

The Council of Trent was the Catholic Church's response to the Protestant Reformation — an ecumenical council meeting in three sessions over eighteen years that produced the doctrinal framework shaping Catholicism for four centuries. In Strachey's "Eminent Victorians," Trent appears at critical junctures as a theological touchstone: the authority invoked in disputes, and the doctrinal heritage that the [[first-vatican-council|First Vatican Council]] of 1870 — the first General Council since Trent — was convened to extend (013-141). For [[cardinal-manning|Cardinal Manning]], Trent represented certainty; for the [[catholic-church|Roman Catholic Church]], it was the foundation upon which the edifice of [[papal-infallibility|Papal Infallibility]] was erected.

## Overview

Convoked by Pope Paul III in 1545 amid the Reformation, the Council met at Trent — a city chosen as compromise between Rome and the German lands. The Fathers defined Catholic doctrine against Protestant challenges and reformed Church discipline. Decrees covered Scripture and tradition, original sin, the seven sacraments, transubstantiation, purgatory, and the veneration of saints. On discipline, Trent mandated seminary education, episcopal residence, and prohibited plural benefices.

Strachey invokes the "whole cycle of Roman doctrine as laid down at the Council of Trent" to describe the standard Manning — while still an Anglican archdeacon — sought to reconcile with the Thirty-Nine Articles (004-197). The decrees were the boundary line: accept them and one was Catholic; reject them and one was Protestant; straddle them, as Newman did for six years, and one inhabited "an extraordinary state of mind" (004-197).

## Key Decrees Referenced in the Book

| Decree | Date | Relevance in "Eminent Victorians" |
|---|---|---|
| Scripture and Tradition | 1546 | Affirmed revelation through both Scripture and unwritten Apostolic Tradition — the claim Tractarians invoked against *sola scriptura*, and that Manning accepted as grounds for conversion (004-197) |
| Original Sin | 1546 | Defined baptismal regeneration as remedy for original sin — the doctrine at the heart of the Gorham Case that propelled Manning toward Rome (008-025) |
| Justification | 1547 | Rejected Lutheran *sola fide*, separating Catholic orthodoxy from the Evangelicalism of Manning's youth (004-197) |
| Holy Orders | 1547 | Affirmed all seven sacraments — the canon Dr. Errington cited when he "drew up a document justifying the appeal of the Chapter to Rome by Canon Law and the decrees of the Council of Trent" (010-068) |
| Transubstantiation | 1551 | Defined Christ's real presence in the Eucharist — central to Tractarian sacramentalism that Manning could not relinquish |
| Episcopal Residence & Seminaries | 1563 | Mandated bishops reside in dioceses and established seminaries — the reform Manning embodied as Archbishop of Westminster |

## In Strachey's Narrative

### The Tractarian Impossible Position

Manning's early Anglican career required balancing affirmation of his Church's Catholic character against the Thirty-Nine Articles. Strachey describes him attempting to persuade himself "that the holding of Anglican orders was not inconsistent with a belief in the whole cycle of Roman doctrine as laid down at the Council of Trent" (004-197). Avoiding "the deadly sin of heresy" while "conscientiously remaining" Anglican was impossible — and it drove first Newman, then Manning, toward Rome (004-197).

> "All he could hope to do was to persuade himself and anyone else who liked to listen to him that the holding of Anglican orders was not inconsistent with a belief in the whole cycle of Roman doctrine as laid down at the Council of Trent." (004-197)

### Errington's Appeal

When the Chapter of Westminster appealed to Rome against Cardinal Wiseman, his coadjutor Dr. Errington "drew up a document justifying the appeal of the Chapter to Rome by Canon Law and the decrees of the Council of Trent" (010-068). The Council's canons gave chapters rights of appeal — rights Errington, who resisted ultramontane autocracy, deployed against the faction Manning represented. Wiseman was wounded: "My own co-adjutor is acting as solicitor against me in a lawsuit" (010-068). The irony Strachey savours is that Trent could be invoked *against* the papal absolutism Manning championed.

### The First Council Since Trent

The most consequential appearance: Pius IX and the ultramontanes summoned "a General Council — the first to be held since the Council of Trent more than 300 years before — to the Vatican" to define Infallibility (013-141).

> "Accordingly, a General Council — the first to be held since the Council of Trent more than 300 years before — was summoned to the Vatican, for the purpose, so it was announced, of providing 'an adequate remedy to the disorders, intellectual and moral, of Christendom.' The programme might seem a large one, even for a General Council; but everyone knew what it meant." (013-141)

Strachey uses Trent as the benchmark: Trent defined what Catholics must believe; Vatican I would define who had the ultimate authority to say so (013-145).

## Outcome and Significance

The Council of Trent established Tridentine Catholicism, persisting until Vatican II (1962–1965). Its decrees defined the boundary between Catholicism and Protestantism — one that, for Manning, Newman, and hundreds of Oxford converts, proved irresistible. The Counter-Reformation reshaped liturgy, clerical formation, and Church discipline for centuries.

In Strachey's narrative, Trent is the doctrinal foundation beneath every Catholic claim. Without its definition of Holy Orders, the argument about Anglican orders collapses. Without its affirmation of tradition alongside Scripture, no Oxford Movement appeal to the Fathers gains traction. And without the three-century gap since Trent, Vatican I cannot carry the historical weight Strachey imparts. Trent is the inheritance Manning's ultramontanism presupposed and, in its centralising drive, sought to transcend.
