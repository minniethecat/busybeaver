[008-001]'Forgive my troubling you with this letter, and believe me to be always

[008-002]your faithful, grateful and affectionate daughter,

[008-003]'EMMA RYLE.

[008-004]'P.S. I wish I could see you once more so very much.'

[008-005]How was Manning, a director of souls, and a clergyman of the Church of

[008-006]England, to reply that in sober truth there was very little to choose

[008-007]between the state of mind of Sister Emma, or even of Sister Harriet, and

[008-008]his own? The dilemma was a grievous one: when a soldier finds himself

[008-009]fighting for a cause in which he has lost faith, it is treachery to

[008-010]stop, and it is treachery to go on.

[008-011]At last, in the seclusion of his library, Manning turned in agony to

[008-012]those old writings which had provided [[john-henry-newman|Newman]] with so much instruction

[008-013]and assistance; perhaps the Fathers would do something for him as well.

[008-014]He ransacked the pages of St. Cyprian and St. Cyril; he went through the

[008-015]complete works of St. Optatus and St. Leo; he explored the vast

[008-016]treatises of Tertullian and Justin Martyr. He had a lamp put into his

[008-017]phaeton, so that he might lose no time during his long winter drives.

[008-018]There he sat, searching St. Chrysostom for some mitigation of his

[008-019]anguish, while he sped along between the hedges to distant sufferers, to

[008-020]whom he duly administered the sacraments according to the rites of the

[008-021]English Church. He hurried back to commit to his Diary the analysis of

[008-022]his reflections, and to describe, under the mystic formula of secrecy,

[008-023]the intricate workings of his conscience to Robert Wilberforce. But,

[008-024]alas! he was no Newman; and even the fourteen folios of St. Augustine

[008-025]himself, strange to say, gave him very little help.

[008-026]The final propulsion was to come from an entirely different quarter. In

[008-027]November, 1847, the Reverend Mr. Gorham was presented by the Lord

[008-028]Chancellor to the living of Bramford Speke in the diocese of Exeter. The

[008-029]Bishop, Dr. Phillpotts, was a High Churchman, and he had reason to

[008-030]believe that Mr. Gorham held evangelical opinions; he therefore

[008-031]subjected him to an examination on doctrine, which took the form partly

[008-032]of a verbal interrogatory, lasting thirty-eight hours, and partly of a

[008-033]series of one hundred and forty-nine written questions. At the end of

[008-034]the examination he came to the conclusion that Mr. Gorham held heretical

[008-035]views on the subject of Baptismal Regeneration, and he therefore refused

[008-036]to institute. Mr. Gorham, thereupon, took proceedings against the Bishop

[008-037]in the Court of Arches. He lost his case; and he then appealed to the

[008-038]judicial Committee of the Privy Council.

[008-039]The questions at issue were taken very seriously by a large number of

[008-040]persons. In the first place, there was the question of Baptismal

[008-041]Regeneration itself. This is by no means an easy one to disentangle; but

[008-042]it may be noted that the doctrine of Baptism includes: (1) God's

[008-043]intention, that is to say, His purpose in electing certain persons to

[008-044]eternal life--an abstruse and greatly controverted subject, upon which

[008-045]the [[church-of-england|Church of England]] abstains from strict definition; (2) God's action,

[008-046]whether by means of sacraments or otherwise--concerning which the Church

[008-047]of England maintains the efficacy of sacraments,' but does not formally

[008-048]deny that grace may be given by other means, repentance and faith being

[008-049]present; and (3) the question whether sacramental grace is given

[008-050]instrumentally, by and at the moment of the act of baptism, or in

[008-051]consequence of an act of prevenient grace rendering the receiver

[008-052]worthy--that is to say, whether sacramental grace in baptism is given

[008-053]absolutely or conditionally.

[008-054]It was over this last question that the dispute raged hottest in the

[008-055]Gorham Case. The High Church party, represented by Dr. Phillpotts,

[008-056]asserted that the mere act of baptism conferred regeneration upon the

[008-057]recipient and washed away his original sin. To this the Evangelicals,

[008-058]headed by Mr. Gorham, replied that, according to the Articles,

[008-059]regeneration would not follow unless baptism was RIGHTLY received. What,

[008-060]then, was the meaning of 'rightly'? Clearly it implied not merely lawful

[008-061]administration, but worthy reception; worthiness, therefore, is the

[008-062]essence of the sacrament; and worthiness means faith and repentance.

[008-063]Now, two propositions were accepted by both parties--that all infants

[008-064]are born in original sin, and that original sin could be washed away by

[008-065]baptism. But how could both these propositions be true, argued Mr.

[008-066]Gorham, if it was also true that faith and repentance were necessary

[008-067]before baptism could come into operation at all? How could an infant in

[008-068]arms be said to be in a state of faith and repentance? How, therefore,

[008-069]could its original sin be washed away by baptism? And yet, as every one

[008-070]agreed, washed away it was.

[008-071]The only solution of the difficulty lay in the doctrine of prevenient

[008-072]grace; and Mr. Gorham maintained that unless God performed an act of

[008-073]prevenient grace by which the infant was endowed with faith and

[008-074]repentance, no act of baptism could be effectual; though to whom, and

[008-075]under what conditions, prevenient grace was given, Mr. Gorham confessed

[008-076]himself unable to decide. The light thrown by the Bible upon the whole

[008-077]matter seemed somewhat dubious, for whereas the baptism of St. Peter's

[008-078]disciples at Jerusalem and St. Philip's at Samaria was followed by the

[008-079]gift of the Spirit, in the case of Cornelius the sacrament succeeded the

[008-080]gift. St. Paul also was baptised; and as for the language of St. John

[008-081]iii 5; Rom. vi 3, 4; I Peter iii 21, it admits of more than one

[008-082]interpretation. There could, however, be no doubt that the Church of

[008-083]England assented to Dr. Phillpotts' opinion; the question was whether or

[008-084]not she excluded Mr. Gorham's. If it was decided that she did, it was

[008-085]clear that henceforward, there would be very little peace for

[008-086]Evangelicals within her fold.

[008-087]But there was another issue, even more fundamental than that of

[008-088]Baptismal Regeneration itself, involved in the Gorham trial. An Act

[008-089]passed in 1833 had constituted the Judicial Committee of the Privy

[008-090]Council the supreme court of appeal for such cases; and this Committee

[008-091]was a body composed entirely of laymen. It was thus obvious that the

[008-092]Royal Supremacy was still a fact, and that a collection of lawyers

[008-093]appointed by the Crown had the legal right to formulate the religious

[008-094]doctrine of the Church of England. In 1850 their judgment was delivered;

[008-095]they reversed the decision of the Court of Arches, and upheld the

[008-096]position of Mr. Gorham. Whether his views were theologically correct or

[008-097]not, they said, was not their business; it was their business to decide

[008-098]whether the opinions under consideration were contrary or repugnant to

[008-099]the doctrine of the Church of England as enjoined upon the clergy by its

[008-100]Articles, Formularies, and Rubrics; and they had come to the conclusion

[008-101]that they were not. The judgement still holds good; and to this day, a

[008-102]clergyman of the Church of England is quite at liberty to believe that

[008-103]Regeneration does not invariably take place when an infant is baptised.

[008-104]The blow fell upon no one with greater violence than upon Manning. Not

[008-105]only was the supreme efficacy of the sign of the cross upon a baby's

[008-106]forehead one of his favourite doctrines, but up to that moment he had

[008-107]been convinced that the Royal Supremacy was a mere accident--a temporary

[008-108]usurpation which left the spiritual dominion of the Church essentially

[008-109]untouched. But now the horrid reality rose up before him, crowned and

[008-110]triumphant; it was all too clear that an Act of Parliament, passed by

[008-111]Jews, Roman Catholics, and Dissenters, was the ultimate authority which

[008-112]decided upon the momentous niceties of the Anglican faith. [[william-ewart-gladstone|Mr. Gladstone]]

[008-113]also, was deeply perturbed. It was absolutely necessary, he wrote, to

[008-114]'rescue and defend the conscience of the Church from the present hideous

[008-115]system'. An agitation was set on foot, and several influential

[008-116]Anglicans, with Manning at their head, drew up and signed a formal

[008-117]protest against the Gorham judgment. Mr. Gladstone however, proposed

[008-118]another method of procedure: precipitate action, he declared, must be

[008-119]avoided at all costs, and he elaborated a scheme for securing

[008-120]procrastination, by which a covenant was to bind all those who believed

[008-121]that an article of the creed had been abolished by Act of Parliament to

[008-122]take no steps in any direction, nor to announce their intention of doing

[008-123]so, until a given space of time had elapsed. Mr. Gladstone was hopeful

[008-124]that some good might come of this--though indeed he could not be sure.

[008-125]'Among others,' he wrote to Manning, 'I have consulted Robert

[008-126]Wilberforce and Wegg-Prosser, and they seemed inclined to favour my

[008-127]proposal. It might, perhaps, have kept back Lord Feilding. But he is

[008-128]like a cork.'

[008-129]The proposal was certainly not favoured by Manning. Protests and

[008-130]procrastinations, approving Wegg-Prossers and cork-like Lord

[008-131]Feildings--all this was feeding the wind and folly; the time for action

[008-132]had come.

[008-133]'I can no longer continue,' he wrote to Robert Wilberforce, 'under oath

[008-134]and subscription binding me to the Royal Supremacy in Ecclesiastical

[008-135]causes, being convinced:

[008-136](1) That it is a violation of the Divine Office of the Church.

[008-137](2) That it has involved the Church of England in a separation

[008-138]from the Universal Church, which separation I cannot clear of the

[008-139]character of schism.

[008-140](3) That it has thereby suspended and prevented the functions of

[008-141]the Church of England.'

[008-142]It was in vain that Robert Wilberforce pleaded, in vain that Mr.

[008-143]Gladstone urged upon his mind the significance of John iii 8. ['The wind

[008-144]bloweth where it listeth, and thou hearest the sound thereof, but canst

[008-145]not tell whence it cometh, and whither it goeth; so is everyone that is

[008-146]born of the Spirit.']

[008-147]'I admit,' Mr. Gladstone wrote, 'that the words might in some way be

[008-148]satisfied by supposing our Lord simply to mean "the facts of nature are

[008-149]unintelligible, therefore, be not afraid if revealed truths be likewise

[008-150]beyond the compass of the understanding"; but this seems to me a meagre

[008-151]meaning.'

[008-152]Such considerations could hold him no longer, and Manning executed the

[008-153]resignation of his office and benefice before a public notary. Soon

[008-154]afterwards, in the little Chapel off Buckingham Palace Road, kneeling

[008-155]beside Mr. Gladstone, he worshipped for the last time as an Anglican.

[008-156]Thirty years later the Cardinal told how, just before the Communion

[008-157]service commenced, he turned to his friends with the words:

[008-158]'I can no longer take the Communion in the Church of England.' 'I rose

[008-159]up, and laying my hand on Mr. Gladstone's shoulder, said "Come". It was

[008-160]the parting of the ways. Mr. Gladstone remained; and I went my way. Mr.

[008-161]Gladstone still remains where I left him.'

[008-162]On April 6th, 1851, the final step was taken: Manning was received into

[008-163]the [[catholic-church|Roman Catholic Church]]. Now at last, after the long struggle, his

[008-164]mind was at rest.

[008-165]'I know what you mean,' he wrote to Robert Wilberforce, 'by saying that

[008-166]one sometimes feels as if all this might turn out to be only another

[008-167]"Land of Shadows". I have felt it in time past, but not now. The

[008-168]theologia from Nice to St. Thomas Aquinas, and the undivided unity

[008-169]suffused throughout the world, of which the Cathedra Petri is the

[008-170]centre, is now 1800 years old, and mightier in every power now than

[008-171]ever--in intellect, in science, in separation from the world; and purer

[008-172]too, refined by 300 years of conflict with the modern infidel

[008-173]civilisation--all of this is a fact more solid than the earth.'

[008-174]V.

[008-175]WHEN Manning joined the Church of [[rome|Rome]], he acted under the combined

[008-176]impulse of the two dominating forces in his nature. His preoccupation

[008-177]with the supernatural might, alone, have been satisfied within the fold

[008-178]of the Anglican communion; and so might his preoccupation with

[008-179]himself--the one might have found vent in the elaborations of High

[008-180]Church ritual, and the other in the activities of a bishopric. But the

[008-181]two together could not be quieted so easily. The Church of England is a

[008-182]commodious institution; she is very anxious to please, but somehow or

[008-183]other, she has never managed to supply a happy home to superstitious

[008-184]egotists. 'What an escape for my poor soul!' Manning is said to have

[008-185]exclaimed when, shortly after his conversion, a mitre was going

[008-186]a-begging. But, in truth, Manning's 'poor soul' had scented nobler

[008-187]quarry. To one of his temperament, how was it possible, when once the

[008-188]choice was plainly put, to hesitate for a moment between the respectable

[008-189]dignity of an English bishop, harnessed by the secular power, with the

[008-190]Gorham judgment as a bit between his teeth, and the illimitable

[008-191]pretensions of the humblest priest of Rome?
